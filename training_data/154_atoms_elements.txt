Everything you can see, touch, taste, smell, or feel is made of atoms. The chair you sit on, the air you breathe, the water you drink, the food you eat, the screen you read this on, and the body you inhabit are all composed of atoms. An atom is the smallest unit of a chemical element that retains the properties of that element. There are one hundred and eighteen known elements, each defined by the number of protons in its nucleus, and every substance in the universe, from the simplest gas to the most complex organic molecule, is made from combinations of these elements.

An atom consists of a nucleus containing protons and neutrons, surrounded by a cloud of electrons. The nucleus is incredibly small relative to the atom as a whole. If an atom were the size of a football stadium, the nucleus would be the size of a marble on the fifty-yard line. The vast majority of an atom is empty space. This means that you, and everything around you, are almost entirely empty space. The apparent solidity of matter is an illusion created by the electromagnetic forces between atoms that resist compression.

The number of protons in the nucleus defines the element. Hydrogen has one proton. Helium has two. Carbon has six. Oxygen has eight. Iron has twenty-six. Gold has seventy-nine. Uranium has ninety-two. Elements with more than ninety-two protons do not occur naturally and must be created in particle accelerators. They are typically unstable and exist for only fractions of a second before decaying into lighter elements.

The periodic table, first developed by Dmitri Mendeleev in 1869, organizes the elements by their atomic number and groups them by their chemical properties. Elements in the same column, called a group, have similar chemical behavior because they have the same number of electrons in their outermost shell. The periodic table is one of the most powerful organizing tools in all of science. It predicted the existence and properties of elements that had not yet been discovered, and it provides a framework for understanding the chemical behavior of every element and every compound.

Hydrogen is the simplest and most abundant element in the universe, comprising approximately seventy-five percent of all ordinary matter by mass. It was the first element to form after the Big Bang and is the fuel that powers stars through nuclear fusion. On Earth, hydrogen is rarely found in its pure form. It is almost always combined with other elements, most commonly with oxygen to form water.

Carbon is the element of life. It has the unique ability to form stable bonds with up to four other atoms simultaneously and to create long chains, branches, and rings of bonded atoms, giving it an almost infinite capacity for structural complexity. This versatility makes carbon the backbone of organic chemistry and the basis of all known life. Every protein, every DNA molecule, every fat, every sugar, and every other organic molecule in your body is built on a framework of carbon atoms.

Oxygen is the third most abundant element in the universe and the most abundant element in the Earth's crust by mass. It is essential for aerobic respiration, the process by which cells extract energy from glucose, and it is a component of water, which is essential for all known life. The oxygen in the atmosphere was produced by photosynthetic organisms over billions of years. Before photosynthesis evolved, the Earth's atmosphere contained virtually no oxygen.

Iron is the most abundant element on Earth by mass, constituting approximately thirty-two percent of the planet's total mass, mostly concentrated in the core. Iron is essential for human biology because it is the atom at the center of hemoglobin, the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen from the lungs to every cell in the body. The iron in your blood was forged in the cores of massive stars that exploded as supernovae billions of years ago.

Gold, silver, and platinum are precious metals valued for their beauty, rarity, and resistance to corrosion. Gold has been used as money, jewelry, and a symbol of wealth and power for thousands of years. It is extremely rare: all the gold ever mined in human history would fit in a cube approximately twenty-one meters on each side. Gold is also produced exclusively in extreme astrophysical events: neutron star collisions and certain types of supernovae. Every gold atom on Earth was created in a cosmic cataclysm billions of years ago.

Chemical bonds form when atoms share or transfer electrons. Covalent bonds involve the sharing of electrons between atoms and are the bonds that hold organic molecules together. Ionic bonds involve the transfer of electrons from one atom to another, creating positively and negatively charged ions that attract each other. Metallic bonds involve the sharing of electrons across a lattice of metal atoms, which gives metals their characteristic properties of conductivity, malleability, and luster.

Chemical reactions occur when atoms rearrange their bonds to form new substances. Combustion is a chemical reaction between a fuel and oxygen that produces heat and light. Photosynthesis is a chemical reaction that converts carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen using light energy. Digestion is a series of chemical reactions that break down food into molecules that cells can use for energy and growth. Rusting is a chemical reaction between iron and oxygen that produces iron oxide. Every chemical reaction conserves mass and energy: the atoms that go in are the same atoms that come out, just rearranged into different configurations.

The atoms in your body are ancient. The hydrogen atoms in your body were created in the Big Bang approximately thirteen point eight billion years ago. The carbon, oxygen, nitrogen, calcium, and iron atoms were forged inside stars that lived and died before our solar system was born. You are, in the most literal sense, made of stardust. The atoms that form your body have been part of stars, interstellar clouds, asteroids, oceans, mountains, and countless other organisms before they became part of you. And after you die, those same atoms will be recycled into new forms, participating in the ongoing chemistry of the universe for billions of years to come.
