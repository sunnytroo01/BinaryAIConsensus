 Outokumpu Oyj is a Finnish multinational corporation headquartered in Helsinki, specializing in the production of stainless steel and advanced materials solutions based on a circular economy model. Founded in 1910 after the discovery of copper ore in eastern Finland, the company initially focused on mining and metal processing before pivoting to stainless steel manufacturing in the 1970s through innovations and acquisitions. Today, it emphasizes sustainable production, with over 90% of its stainless steel derived from recycled scrap, resulting in a carbon footprint up to 75% lower than industry averages.
Outokumpu operates production facilities in Finland, Sweden, Germany, the United States, and Mexico, employing approximately 8,700 people across nearly 30 countries. It commands a leading 31% market share in Europe and a 23% share as the second-largest producer in the Americas, supported by world-class assets and a comprehensive product portfolio tailored for industries such as construction, automotive, and energy. The company's historical milestones include developing flash smelting technology in the 1940s for efficient ore processing and launching low-emission products like Circle Green in 2022, which achieves up to 93% lower emissions compared to standard benchmarks.
Recognized for its sustainability efforts, Outokumpu was ranked among the top 50 companies worldwide on Corporate Knights' 2024 list of the global cleanest firms by revenue, reflecting its commitment to 100% recyclable stainless steel and decarbonization initiatives. As a publicly listed entity on the Helsinki Stock Exchange since 1988, it continues to leverage its expertise in metals to meet demand for durable, corrosion-resistant materials essential for modern infrastructure and green technologies.

History

Origins and Mining Foundations (19101950s)

The discovery of a substantial copper ore deposit occurred on March 16, 1910, in Kuusjrvi, eastern Finland, on a hill named Outokumpu, meaning "strange hill," by German geologist Otto Trustedt during geological surveys. Test drilling confirmed the viability of the 4-kilometer-long ore body, prompting the establishment of mining operations as Finland sought to develop its domestic metal resources amid limited industrial base.
Outokumpu Kopparverk was founded in 1914 as a joint state-private enterprise to exploit the deposit, with mining commencing in 1913 alongside an initial smelter near the site. Early operations remained small-scale through the 1910s and 1920s, hampered by World War I disruptions and financial constraints, producing copper via rudimentary smelting before exporting concentrates via a 12-kilometer railway to a lakeside harbor completed in 1918 and linking to the national rail network by 1928. Ownership transferred fully to the Finnish state in 1925, enabling modernization into a mass-production facility by 1928, though the original smelter closed in 1929 as integrated processing plans advanced.
In 1932, the company reorganized as Outokumpu Oy, a limited liability entity with the state holding majority shares, facilitating rapid expansion into an integrated copper production chain during the 1930s. Key infrastructure included the world's largest electric smelting plant at Imatra in 1935 and a copper refinery at Pori, positioning Outokumpu as a major European copper exporter by decade's end. World War II prompted relocation of the Imatra smelter to Harjavalta in 1944 to evade bombings, doubling capacity, while the late 1940s saw the invention of the flash smelting processan autogenous method reducing energy needs and revolutionizing copper extraction efficiency. By 1950, Outokumpu had solidified as one of Europe's premier mining firms, with the Outokumpu mine serving as its foundational asset, yielding high-grade copper ore that supported downstream metalworking and laid groundwork for diversification.

Transition to Steel Production (1960s1990s)

In 1959, Outokumpu discovered significant chromium ore deposits in Kemi, Finland, prompting a strategic pivot toward ferroalloys essential for stainless steel production, as chromium is a primary alloying element providing corrosion resistance. Exploitation of the Kemi deposit began in 1960, followed by the decision in 1964 to invest in ferrochrome smelting after extensive research and development. Construction of the Tornio ferrochrome plant proceeded, with production commencing in 1968 at an initial capacity of 28,000 tonnes per annum, integrating ore from Kemi to supply chromium for downstream steel applications. This marked the company's initial foray into value-added metals processing beyond traditional copper mining, leveraging local resources amid declining ore grades at the original Outokumpu copper site.
The transition accelerated in the 1970s with the establishment of integrated stainless steel facilities. Feasibility studies for stainless steel production had been initiated as early as 1960, but national political considerations sited the mill in Tornio to support regional development, backed by state financial support. The first stainless steel melt occurred in May 1976 at the Tornio plant, utilizing advanced smelting technology and achieving an initial annual capacity of 50,000 tonnes, fully vertically integrated with the adjacent Kemi mine and ferrochrome operations. This facility produced austenitic, ferritic, and martensitic grades, positioning Outokumpu as a European stainless steel producer amid global demand growth for corrosion-resistant materials in industries like construction and chemicals.
During the 1980s and 1990s, Outokumpu expanded stainless steel capabilities while maintaining multi-metal operations, reflecting a gradual de-emphasis on base mining. The company listed on the Helsinki Stock Exchange in 1988 as Outokumpu Oyj, facilitating further investments. Tornio's ferrochrome capacity grew with a second smelting furnace in 1985, and stainless steel output doubled by 1991, driven by profitability and market consolidation pressures. By the mid-1990s, stainless steel constituted a core focus, with the Tornio works evolving into one of Europe's most integrated mills, though the company retained copper and zinc assets until later divestitures. This era solidified Outokumpu's expertise in high-alloy steels, supported by proprietary technologies like flash smelting adapted for ferroalloys.

Global Expansion and Acquisitions (2000s)

In 2000, Outokumpu launched a strategic initiative emphasizing growth in stainless steel through mergers, acquisitions, and divestitures of non-core assets to enhance its competitive position internationally.
The cornerstone of this expansion was the September 2000 merger agreement between Outokumpu's steel division and Avesta Sheffield, culminating in the formation of AvestaPolarit Oyj in 2001 with Outokumpu holding a 55% stake and Corus Group plc 23%; this entity achieved 1.8 million tonnes of annual stainless steel production capacity, ranking it as the global second-largest producer at the time.
The merger integrated Outokumpu's Finnish operations with Avesta Sheffield's facilities in Sweden and the United Kingdom, markedly broadening Outokumpu's European footprint and technological capabilities in specialty stainless steels.
In July 2002, Outokumpu acquired Corus's 23.2% interest in AvestaPolarit for 530 million, elevating its ownership to 87.9% and securing majority control to streamline decision-making and pursue further synergies.
By 2004, Outokumpu committed to a stainless steel-only focus, divesting mining, other metals, and technology segments to allocate capital toward global production and market expansion in this core area.
Supporting acquisitions included the February 2004 purchase of SMI Group's superconductor operations, though this remained peripheral to primary stainless steel activities.
In 2008, Outokumpu bolstered its distribution and processing infrastructure via three targeted buys: SoGePar Group, an Italian stainless steel distributor extending into the UK; Avesta Klippcenter AB, a Swedish slitting and cutting service provider; and unspecified others, enhancing supply chain efficiency across Europe.

Financial Crises, Restructurings, and Recent Developments (2010s2025)

In December 2012, Outokumpu completed its acquisition of ThyssenKrupp's Inoxum stainless steel division for 2.7 billion, assuming substantial debt and becoming the world's largest stainless steel producer by volume. The transaction, however, exposed the company to overcapacity in the European market and antitrust remedies imposed by the European Commission, requiring divestitures such as the Calvert, Alabama facility sold to ArcelorMittal in 2013. These factors, combined with weak stainless steel demand, led to stretched liquidity and restricted debt market access.
The ensuing financial strain manifested in heavy losses throughout 2013, with net debt reaching 3.0 billion by June and underlying EBIT losses including 126 million in the third quarter and 90 million in the fourth. In November 2013, Outokumpu announced a comprehensive deleveraging plan, including the sale of its Terni facility and VDM nickel business back to ThyssenKrupp, aimed at reducing the debt-to-equity ratio by about 30 percentage points from 132%. Concurrently, the company launched cost-saving initiatives such as the P150 program targeting 150 million in reductions and the P300 program for 300 million in working capital cuts by end-2014, alongside broader synergy efforts yielding 199 million in savings.
Restructuring intensified in 20142015 with an October 2013 industrial plan for European operations, including the ramp-down and closure of the Bochum melt shop in 2015 as part of the EMEA program targeting 100 million in savings by 2017. Post-Inoxum merger programs, such as P250 and synergies, achieved 470 million in annual run-rate savings by year-end 2015, enabling industrial stabilization and a stronger footprint in Europe and the Americas. By 2016, under new CEO leadership and a vision to become the best value creator in stainless steel by 2020, Outokumpu had completed core European restructuring, shifting focus to operational efficiency amid recovering markets.
Entering the 2020s, Outokumpu navigated the COVID-19 pandemic effectively, reporting adjusted EBITDA of 78 million in Q4 2020 on stronger market recovery. Its 20202025 strategy delivered on targets, including a 250 million EBITDA run-rate improvement and net debt to EBITDA below 3.0 by mid-2025. In June 2025, the company introduced the EVOLVE strategy to accelerate growth in sustainable stainless steel, high-value products, and the Americas, with updated financial targets emphasizing shareholder value and a revised dividend policy; this included 25 million in cash flow impacts from capability enhancements through 2028. First-half 2025 results showed adjusted EBITDA rising to 75 million, though net result was a 19 million loss, reflecting ongoing market uncertainty but a fortified balance sheet.

Business Operations

Production Sites and Facilities

Outokumpu maintains stainless steel production facilities across Europe and the Americas, with key mills in Finland, Sweden, Germany, the United States, and Mexico. These sites focus on melting, hot and cold rolling, and finishing processes to produce flat products such as coils and sheets. The company's operations emphasize integrated production where possible, utilizing electric arc furnaces (EAF) and recycling over 90% scrap steel.
The flagship facility is the Tornio stainless steel plant in Finland, an integrated operation that includes melting, hot rolling, cold rolling, and ferrochrome production. Stainless steel output began in 1976 at 50,000 tonnes per annum (tpa), expanding to 1.2 million tpa following a 2004 project; cold rolling capacity exceeds 1.25 million tonnes annually after subsequent upgrades. The adjacent Tornio Ferrochrome Works, doubled in 2013, has a capacity of 530,000 tpa. Nearby, the Kemi Mine supplies chromite ore essential for stainless steel alloys.
In Sweden, the Avesta plant produces austenitic, duplex, and martensitic flat products via melting, hot and cold rolling, yielding coils and sheets. The Degerfors site specializes in quarto plate production, while Nyby operations handle specialized stainless steel processing. Germany's Dillenburg and Krefeld facilities support stainless steel manufacturing, including cold rolling and finishing lines.

Products, Technology, and Supply Chain

Outokumpu specializes in flat stainless steel products, including cold-rolled coils, strips, sheets, hot-rolled coils, strips, plates, quarto plates, and precision strips, available in austenitic, ferritic, duplex, and martensitic grades. Austenitic grades, the largest group, offer good to excellent corrosion resistance, formability, and weldability, suited for consumer products, food processing, and cryogenic applications. Ferritic grades provide price stability due to low nickel content and ferromagnetic properties, ideal for general corrosion-resistant uses. Duplex grades combine high strength with superior resistance to stress corrosion cracking, targeting chemical, oil, and gas sectors. Martensitic and precipitation-hardening grades emphasize strength and hardenability for tools and cutlery. Specialized components include welded I-beams, H-beams, hollow-section tubes, and bent profiles up to 13 meters in length for structural load-bearing. Product ranges such as Pro (optimized for machinability), Forta (for high-strength needs), and Circle Green (sustainable variants) address specific performance demands like corrosion resistance and heat tolerance.
The company invests in technology for low-CO metal production, prioritizing chromium materials like enriched ferrochrome and high-purity chromium metal derived from its Kemi mine operations. Under the EVOLVE strategy, Outokumpu develops scalable platforms for advanced alloys and customer-driven innovations in high-value grades to reduce production cyclicality and enhance sustainability. Efforts include smart decarbonization via capital expenditures on electric arc furnaces and hydrogen-based processes, alongside collaborations like the 2025 partnership with Boston Metal for molten oxide electrolysis to improve metals efficiency. Robotics integration, such as autonomous systems for hazardous inspections, supports operational safety and zero-incident goals at facilities.
Outokumpu's supply chain involves over 7,400 global suppliers in 2024, with 64% localized to minimize emissions and risks, divided into general procurement for operations and raw materials procurement for steel inputs like alloys and scrap. Approximately 95% of raw materials derive from recycled steel scrap, enabling circular economy practices that lower primary resource dependency. Responsible sourcing adheres to OECD guidelines, EU conflict minerals regulations, and U.S. Dodd-Frank Act, with supplier assessments via EcoVadis ratings, on-site audits, and due diligence for high-risk materials like molybdenumsecured via a 10-year off-take agreement with Greenland Resources for 8 million pounds annually starting 2025. Transparency initiatives include supplier codes of conduct and impact assessments to ensure low-carbon and ethical compliance across the value chain.

Market Position and Financial Performance

Competitive Landscape and Strategy

Outokumpu operates in the global stainless steel market, characterized by cyclical demand influenced by sectors such as construction, automotive, and energy, with total global production reaching 57 million tonnes in recent years. The company holds a 3.0% global market share but dominates in Europe with approximately 31% of the market, positioning it as the regional leader, while ranking second in the Americas with a 23% share in the USMCA region. Key competitors include integrated producers like Acerinox S.A., Aperam S.A., ArcelorMittal, POSCO, and Nippon Steel Corporation, which vie for share in flat-rolled products and specialty grades amid pressures from raw material costs and trade tensions. 
Market challenges include supply chain disruptions, geopolitical risks, and weak European demand, particularly in automotive and construction, leading to projected stainless steel delivery declines of 5-15% quarter-over-quarter in mid-2025 due to seasonality and pricing pressures. Growth drivers encompass urbanization, population expansion, and demand for durable, low-emission materials, where Outokumpu differentiates through its emphasis on recyclabilityusing up to 95% recycled contentand the lowest industry carbon footprint. 
Outokumpu's strategy from 2020 to 2025 focused on de-risking operations, enhancing cost discipline, and advancing sustainability, achieving a 287 million EBITDA run-rate improvement by 2024 and reducing net debt to EBITDA to 1.1 times, while investing 386 million in capital expenditures. This phase emphasized commercial excellence and lean organization, culminating in progress toward a 42% CO reduction target by 2030 from a 2016 baseline.
In June 2025, Outokumpu announced its EVOLVE strategy for 20262030, aiming to evolve from a core stainless steel producer into a leader in advanced materials and innovative technologies to mitigate cyclicality and capture higher margins. Key pillars include maximizing value in sustainable stainless steel via cost competitiveness and raw material security; expanding high-margin advanced alloys through customer partnerships; and developing low-CO products like chromium metals using assets such as the Kemi mine. Financial targets under EVOLVE encompass maintaining net debt to EBITDA at 1.0 times over the cycle (up to 2.0 times temporarily), delivering a 250 million EBITDA run-rate uplift, and pursuing stable, growing dividends with a minimum 20% internal rate of return on transformative investments. Capital allocation prioritizes operational resilience, decarbonization, and shareholder returns, including potential share buybacks, to strengthen positioning against global rivals.

Historical and Recent Financial Metrics

Outokumpu Oyj's financial performance has been characterized by cyclical fluctuations driven by global stainless steel demand, nickel and alloy surcharges, and strategic expansions, particularly the 2012 acquisition of Inoxum from ThyssenKrupp, which boosted capacity but initially strained balance sheets with high debt. Pre-acquisition revenues hovered around 3-4 billion annually in the late 2000s, reflecting primarily European operations; post-2012 integration, sales surged to 7.0 billion by 2014 amid expanded global footprint, though adjusted EBITDA remained modest at 300-400 million due to integration costs and market downturns. The 2010s saw restructurings, including divestitures and cost cuts, to address debt peaking above 2 billion net in 2013-2015, reducing leverage through asset sales and operational efficiencies.

Sustainability and Environmental Practices

Key Initiatives and Achievements

Outokumpu has aligned its sustainability strategy with the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals and secured approval from the Science Based Targets initiative for its climate targets, becoming the first stainless steel producer to achieve such validation. The company committed to reducing Scope 1 and 2 greenhouse gas emissions by 42% by 2030 from a 2018 baseline, alongside pursuing carbon neutrality by 2050 through measures including increased use of low-carbon electricity and energy efficiency improvements.
A flagship initiative is the development of Outokumpu Circle Green, launched as the world's first near-zero stainless steel product featuring up to 95% recycled content and a carbon footprint reduced by 93% compared to the global average, emitting less than 0.5 tonnes of CO2 per tonne produced. This product supports circular economy principles by utilizing high recycled scrap ratesmaintaining over 90% recycled material content annuallyand advancing slag utilization toward a 90% target by 2030. In 2024, Outokumpu's overall stainless steel production achieved up to 75% lower carbon emissions than the industry average, verified through product-specific carbon footprint disclosures.
Achievements include the carbon neutrality of its Kemi chrome mine, recognized in the International Chromium Development Association's 2025 Sustainability Awards for environmental performance. Outokumpu earned a Leading Company designation at the 2024 ESG Transparency Awards and a Highly Commended award for carbon reduction at the World Sustainability Awards in October 2024. It was also listed among top steel industry performers in the 2023 Sustainability Yearbook by S&P Global. These efforts reflect measurable progress in emissions reductions and resource efficiency, substantiated by third-party validations and annual reporting under European Sustainability Reporting Standards.

Criticisms, Challenges, and Empirical Scrutiny

Outokumpu has faced scrutiny over radiation contamination incidents at its Tornio stainless steel plant in Finland, stemming from the use of recycled scrap metal, which constitutes over 90% of its raw materials for sustainability benefits. In February 2012, four workers were exposed to radioactive americium that entered the melting process via contaminated recycled steel; one received 10 millisieverts of radiation, classified as a level 2 incident involving material safety risk, though no adverse health effects were reported. The company responded by analyzing melt shop samples and reinforcing prohibitions on radioactive elements in scrap. Similarly, in 2018, the plant experienced four such incidents since July, including one on October 12 involving americium from scrap sourced from the Netherlands and Baltics; while no workers were exposed due to protective measures like oxygen masks, the events generated hundreds of tons of radioactive waste, prompting classification by Finland's Radiation and Nuclear Safety Authority (STUK) under the International Nuclear Event Scale (INES). These cases underscore empirical challenges in scrap-based production: detection difficulties for low-level contaminants, supply chain vulnerabilities, and the causal trade-offs of high recycling rates, which reduce virgin ore emissions but introduce localized hazards, despite contractual bans and supplier collaborations implemented by Outokumpu.
In its U.S. operations, Outokumpu Stainless USA encountered an acid spill at the Calvert, Alabama facility, listed as a Superfund removal site by the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), where cleanup actions were completed without necessitating further site assessment or National Priorities List designation. Additionally, a 2012 air pollution violation at the predecessor ThyssenKrupp Stainless USA facility in Alabamaacquired by Outokumpuresulted in a $20,000 penalty from the Alabama Department of Environmental Management, reflecting non-compliance with emission standards during a transitional period. Such incidents highlight operational risks in chemical handling and emissions control at melt shops and processing units, though limited public data on spill volumes or long-term soil/water impacts suggests contained effects, with regulatory oversight ensuring remediation.
Empirically, Outokumpu's sustainability practices have withstood scrutiny in areas like carbon footprint verification through third-party Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs), showing up to 75% lower emissions than the global stainless steel average due to electric arc furnace reliance and scrap utilization, but challenges persist in waste management and incident prevention. Slag reuse targets 90% by 2030, yet current rates lag in some sites amid variable scrap quality, potentially elevating indirect emissions from impurities requiring additional processing. Peer-reviewed analyses of worker cohorts indicate no excess mortality from occupational exposures, attributing lower-than-expected rates to low hexavalent chromium levels (<0.5 g/m median) and health monitoring, countering broader steel industry concerns over particulate and chemical pollutants. Nonetheless, the recurrence of radiation events critiques supply chain vetting, as causal realism demands rigorous preprocessing to mitigate rare but high-stakes risks in scaling circular models, with Outokumpu committing to zero environmental incidents through enhanced detection technologies.

Controversies and Legal Issues

Labor Disputes and Regulatory Violations

In Finland, Outokumpu's Tornio plant has been repeatedly impacted by industrial strikes, including a 2023 action affecting approximately 1,700 employees, including white-collar and upper-level staff, which halted operations. These disruptions extended into 2024 with political strikes against government labor reforms, lasting up to four weeks and causing an estimated 80 million hit to adjusted EBITDA through delayed production and shipments. Company statements emphasize that these strikes targeted national policy rather than firm-specific labor relations.
At its Calvert, Alabama facility, Outokumpu faced U.S. National Labor Relations Board charges for unfair labor practices, including surveillance of employees engaged in union activities and creating an impression of such surveillance, as ruled in a 2017 decision. Earlier wage disputes in Finland, part of broader technology sector actions, involved over 7,000 workers striking for three days in early 2023 amid unresolved collective bargaining.
Regulatory violations have primarily occurred in U.S. operations. In 2025, Outokumpu Stainless USA incurred a $12,975,038 penalty for wage and hour violations under the Fair Labor Standards Act, stemming from failures to compensate for full work time, including donning/doffing activities and improper rounding of clock-ins/outs by 30 minutes. The Eleventh Circuit upheld a default judgment exceeding $13 million in 2024, including overtime wages and liquidated damages, after the company withheld payroll records.
The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission sued Outokumpu in 2015 for race discrimination at Calvert, alleging denial of promotions to Black employees in violation of Title VII; the case highlighted disparate treatment in supervisory roles. A separate 2022 settlement resolved disability discrimination claims, with Outokumpu paying $49,000 to a rejected applicant and agreeing to policy revisions and training. In 2014, the Occupational Safety and Health Administration cited the firm for two serious violations following a fatal contractor incident at Calvert, imposing fines totaling $3,375 for inadequate hazard assessments and training.

Export Practices and International Trade Concerns

Outokumpu, as Europe's leading producer of stainless steel, conducts exports primarily from its facilities in Finland, Sweden, Germany, and the United States, with key markets including the Americas, Asia, and other European countries outside the EU. In 2024, the company's international sales accounted for a significant portion of its 5.9 billion turnover, supported by a diversified supply chain that mitigates regional trade barriers. However, export operations have faced scrutiny over compliance with international regulations, notably in a 2007 Finnish Customs investigation into Outokumpu Stainless Oy's shipments to Russia, which probed allegations of irregularities including potential money laundering tied to export documentation and payments.
The investigation, initiated in March 2007, examined practices such as undervaluation or misrepresentation in export declarations but concluded without substantiating criminal charges against the company; Finnish courts subsequently dismissed all related accusations against Outokumpu and five employees in subsequent rulings, affirming no violation of export control laws. This case highlighted vulnerabilities in stainless steel trade with sanctioned or high-risk markets, though Outokumpu maintained that its practices adhered to prevailing Finnish and EU export standards at the time. No similar investigations have been reported since, and the company has since emphasized enhanced compliance measures amid evolving geopolitical risks.
Broader international trade concerns for Outokumpu's exports stem from escalating tariffs and safeguard measures, particularly U.S. Section 232 duties on steel imports, which reached up to 50% as of July 2025 despite a U.S.-EU agreement reducing tariffs on most goods to 15%. These barriers have disrupted global stainless steel flows, with Outokumpu's own research indicating that one-third of surveyed buyers delayed or paused orders due to tariff uncertainty, prompting the company to advocate for stricter EU safeguards against non-market economy imports, such as reducing quota volumes by up to 50% and imposing higher duties on excess capacity from Asia. Outokumpu has actively participated in anti-dumping actions, filing petitions in 2016 alongside U.S. producers against Chinese stainless steel imports to counter alleged subsidies and below-market pricing, resulting in duties that indirectly bolstered fair competition for its exports.
In response to these pressures, Outokumpu has pursued geographic diversification, including U.S. production at its Calvert, Alabama mill to localize supply and evade import tariffs on European exports, while calling for EU measures like country-specific import limits to prevent circumvention via third countries. Such strategies reflect empirical evidence of overcapacityglobal stainless steel production exceeding demand by 20-30% in recent yearsdriving trade distortions, though Outokumpu's exports remain compliant with WTO rules and have not faced successful anti-dumping findings against them.

Corporate Governance and Organization

Leadership and Management Structure

Outokumpu's corporate governance framework comprises the General Meeting of Shareholders, the Board of Directors, and the executive management under the President and Chief Executive Officer (CEO), in compliance with Finnish laws and the Finnish Corporate Governance Code effective January 1, 2025. The Board of Directors, elected annually by the General Meeting, holds ultimate responsibility for directing the company's strategy, overseeing operations, and ensuring long-term shareholder value. It consists of eight independent members, with Kari Jordan as Chairman and Jyrki Mki-Kala as Vice Chairman; the remaining members are Hilde Merete Aasheim, Heinz Jrg Fuhrmann, Olavi Huhtala, Pivi Luostarinen, Petter Sderstrm, and Julia Woodhouse.
The President and CEO, Kati ter Horst, assumed office on October 1, 2024, and reports to the Board while managing day-to-day operations and preparing strategic decisions for Board approval. ter Horst leads the Leadership Team, a group of senior executives with delegated authority over core business functions, including finance, technology, legal affairs, and regional operations. The Team's composition reflects Outokumpu's divisional structure, emphasizing stainless steel production, ferrochrome, advanced materials, and geographic business areas.
Key Leadership Team members include Marc-Simon Schaar as Chief Financial Officer, Stefan Erdmann as Chief Technology Officer, Matthieu Jehl as President of Business Line Stainless Europe, Juhani Ristaniemi as Executive Vice President and General Counsel, Martti Sassi as President of Business Area Ferrochrome, Rolf Schencking as President of Business Line Advanced Materials, and Johann Steiner as President of Business Area Americas. On October 6, 2025, Steiner, a long-term executive with over a decade at the company, succeeded Tamara Weinert in the Americas role to advance Outokumpu's EVOLVE growth strategy in that region; a successor for Steiner's prior position in Strategy, Sustainability, and People is under recruitment. This structure supports decentralized decision-making while aligning with centralized strategic oversight from the Board and CEO.

Ownership and Shareholder Relations

Outokumpu Oyj's share capital stands at 311,131,873.30, comprising 456,874,448 ordinary shares of 0.68 each, with all shares conferring equal voting and dividend rights. The company holds treasury shares totaling 32,644,616, equivalent to 7.145% of outstanding shares.
As of October 24, 2025, the largest shareholder is Solidium Oy, Finland's state-owned investment company, owning 70,793,208 shares or 14.97% of the total. This stake had decreased from above 15% prior to July 3, 2025. Other major holders include domestic institutional investors such as Varma Mutual Pension Insurance Company (approximately 5.26% or 24,888,403 shares) and Ilmarinen Mutual Pension Insurance Company, alongside the State Pension Fund of Finland. The top 25 shareholders collectively control about 46.94% of the company.
Shareholder relations are governed by a Shareholders' Nomination Board, formed annually from the four largest shareholders as of August 1, plus the board chairman, which proposes board elections and remuneration to the Annual General Meeting. For 2025, representatives hail from Solidium Oy, Varma, Ilmarinen, and the State Pension Fund (via the Social Insurance Institution of Finland). The Investor Relations team, led by figures including Johan Lindh as of September 2025, handles ongoing engagement through financial disclosures, webcasts (e.g., post-Q3 2025 results on October 29), conference calls, and the AGM in Helsinki on April 3, 2025. Management and board shareholdings remain minimal, with updates disclosed post-remuneration and personnel changes.
