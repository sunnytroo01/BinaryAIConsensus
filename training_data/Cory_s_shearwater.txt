Cory's shearwater
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Cory's shearwater (Calonectris borealis), one of three species formerly lumped under the name, is a medium to large seabird in the family Procellariidae, distinguished by its robust build, brownish-gray upperparts, white underparts with a smudged brown chest band, and a yellow bill tipped in black. Measuring 4556 cm in length with a wingspan of 112118 cm and weighing around 950 g, it exhibits a languid, gliding flight typical of shearwaters, with broad wings and slow, powerful wingbeats.
This highly pelagic species inhabits the open Atlantic Ocean year-round, favoring warm waters far from shore, and returns to land only to breed on remote, rocky islands and archipelagos. Its breeding range is restricted to the northeastern Atlantic, primarily the Azores and Madeira archipelagos (over 85% of the population), the Berlengas Islands in Portugal, and the Canary Islands in Spain (about 15%), where it forms large colonies on cliffs, boulder fields, and barren offshore sites. After breeding from March to October, Cory's shearwaters are strongly migratory, dispersing across much of the Atlantic to winter off the coasts of South America and southern Africa, with occasional vagrants reaching the eastern North American seaboard and even further afield.
Breeding pairs occupy burrows in sandy soil, crevices in cliffs, or spaces under boulders, laying a single large white egg that both parents incubate for 5256 days; the chick fledges after 8597 days and is fed primarily at night. The diet consists mainly of squid, fish such as herring and sand lance, and crustaceans, obtained by surface-seizing, shallow plunges, or swimming, often opportunistically near fishing trawlers attracted to offal and discards.
With an estimated global population of 504,000507,000 mature individuals as of 2012 and a vast extent of occurrence spanning 97.6 million km, Cory's shearwater is classified as Least Concern on the IUCN Red List (assessed 2018), though regional populations in Europe numbered 252,000253,000 breeding pairs as of 2012. Key threats include bycatch in longline and gillnet fisheries, predation by invasive species like rats, cats, and mice at nesting sites, light pollution disorienting fledglings, and poaching of chicks for food in the Azores (5001,500 annually as of 2011). Conservation efforts focus on predator control, reducing bycatch, and habitat protection on breeding islands to sustain this adaptable yet vulnerable oceanic wanderer.

Classification

Taxonomy

Cory's shearwater bears the scientific name Calonectris borealis, originally described by American ornithologist Charles B. Cory in 1881 as Puffinus borealis based on a specimen collected off Chatham Island, Massachusetts. The species was initially classified within the genus Puffinus, which encompasses many shearwaters, reflecting early understandings of seabird taxonomy that grouped them based on superficial morphological similarities.
In 1915, ornithologists Gregory M. Mathews and Tom Iredale transferred the species to the newly established genus Calonectris, distinguishing it from Puffinus due to differences in bill structure, plumage patterns, and overall proportions that better aligned it with Mediterranean and Atlantic shearwater forms. For much of the 20th century, C. borealis was treated as a subspecies of the broader Cory's shearwater complex (Calonectris diomedea), with historical synonyms including Puffinus diomedea borealis. This classification persisted until genetic, morphological, and behavioral studies revealed sufficient divergence to warrant full species status.
The formal split from Scopoli's shearwater (Calonectris diomedea) occurred in 2012, supported by mitochondrial DNA analyses showing reciprocally monophyletic clades, along with morphological distinctions in size and underwing plumage, distinct vocalizations, and ecological differences in foraging and migration; this was further recognized by the American Ornithological Society in 2024 for North American checklists. Within the family Procellariidae (petrels and shearwaters), C. borealis is monotypic in its current delimitation and forms a closely related clade with C. diomedea and the Cape Verde shearwater (C. edwardsii), with molecular studies indicating divergence between Atlantic and Mediterranean lineages during the Pleistocene. This phylogenetic separation highlights the role of Pleistocene glaciation in driving isolation among island-breeding seabirds.

Etymology

The common name "Cory's shearwater" honors the American ornithologist Charles B. Cory (18571921), who first described the species in 1881 under the name Puffinus borealis based on a specimen collected off Chatham Island, Massachusetts. This naming reflects Cory's significant contributions to ornithology, including his extensive work on Caribbean and North American birds during his tenure as curator at the Field Museum in Chicago.
The genus name Calonectris derives from Ancient Greek roots: kalos, meaning "beautiful" or "good," combined with nectris (from nktris), meaning "swimmer," alluding to the bird's elegant, gliding flight over ocean surfaces as it "shears" the waves. The specific epithet borealis is Latin for "northern," highlighting the species' breeding distribution in the North Atlantic, particularly around the Azores, Madeira, and Canary Islands, in contrast to its southern relative.
Historically, the closely related Scopoli's shearwater (Calonectris diomedea) was described in 1769 by the Austrian naturalist Giovanni Antonio Scopoli (17231788), who placed it in the genus Procellaria as P. diomedea, recognizing its distinct Mediterranean breeding range. This earlier description influenced later taxonomic splits, with Cory's shearwater elevated to full species status in the 20th century to reflect genetic and morphological differences from Scopoli's shearwater.
In Portuguese-speaking regions, particularly the Azores where the species breeds in large colonies, Cory's shearwater is known as cagarro, a name derived from onomatopoeic imitation of its eerie, nocturnal calls that echo across breeding grounds, evoking sounds like "ca-gar-ro." This cultural reference underscores the bird's prominence in local folklore and conservation efforts, such as the annual SOS Cagarro campaign, which rescues disoriented fledglings and symbolizes Azorean maritime heritage.

Description

Physical characteristics

Cory's shearwater (Calonectris borealis) is a large seabird measuring 4856 cm in length, with a wingspan of 113126 cm and a body mass ranging from 6051,060 g. Sexual dimorphism is present but subtle, with males averaging slightly larger in overall size and exhibiting more pronounced bill dimensions compared to females.
The plumage features warm pale brown upperparts, including a brownish-grey mantle and back with slightly paler upper tail coverts, contrasting with clean white underparts that extend to the undertail coverts. The flight feathers are darker brown, creating subtle contrast on the upperwings, while the underwings show white bases with a dark trailing edge and leading edge along the primaries. The robust build includes long, narrow wings adapted for dynamic soaring and gliding over ocean waves.
The bill is prominent, thick, and yellowish with a darker tip and subterminal markings, often appearing pale pinkish-yellow overall, and features tubular nostrils characteristic of procellariids for excreting excess salt from seawater. The legs and feet are pinkish. The hooked tip of the bill aids in grasping slippery prey such as fish and squid.
Juveniles resemble adults but have a browner head, paler yellowish bill with grayish or dusky subterminal markings rather than black, and more rounded feathers on the back and upperwing coverts. In the field, Cory's shearwater can be distinguished from the Great shearwater (Puffinus gravis) by its yellower bill, warmer and less contrasting brown plumage lacking a dark cap and white collar, and lazier, more lumbering flight with crooked wings. It differs from the smaller Manx shearwater (Puffinus puffinus) by its much larger size, slower and stiffer wingbeats, and overall bulkier appearance.

Vocalizations

Cory's shearwaters produce a variety of vocalizations primarily at breeding colonies, where they are most active vocally during the night. The principal call is a duet vocalization, characterized by harsh, grating sounds often described as moaning or raucous screams, consisting of multiple notes that vary in structure and intensity. These calls serve functions in pair bonding, mate recognition, and territorial defense, with birds responding more strongly to calls from their mates or same-sex individuals than to those from strangers. Males typically emit shorter, harder calls with a stronger terminal tone, while females produce more nasal, lower-pitched bleats, enabling individual and sexual recognition during courtship interactions.
At sea, Cory's shearwaters are generally silent, but they occasionally give low barks or whines, particularly when following boats or in flight near colonies. Juveniles tend to be quieter overall, vocalizing less frequently than adults and lacking the complex duet calls associated with breeding.
Acoustically, Cory's shearwater calls differ from those of the closely related Scopoli's shearwater (Calonectris diomedea), featuring deeper, more nasal tones and faster delivery of notes, which contributed to their taxonomic distinction. Studies utilizing spectrographic analysis reveal that these calls have a frequency range typically between 200 and 1,000 Hz, with modal frequencies increasing during excitation or agonistic encounters. Such recordings have been employed in passive acoustic monitoring to assess colony activity and population density, correlating call rates with nest occupancy.

Distribution and habitat

Breeding grounds

Cory's shearwater primarily breeds on rocky islands in the North Atlantic, with over 85% of the global population concentrated in the Portuguese archipelagos of the Azores and Madeira. The largest single colony is found on Selvagem Grande in the Savage Islands (part of Madeira), supporting an estimated 38,830 breeding pairs as of 2023. Smaller but significant populations occur in the Canary Islands (Spain), accounting for approximately 15% of the global total, and a minor colony exists in the Berlengas archipelago off mainland Portugal with only a few breeding pairs.
These birds nest in colonial groups on steep, barren offshore islands characterized by cliffs, boulder fields, and lava or sandy soils. Preferred sites include burrows excavated in soil, natural crevices in rock faces, spaces under boulders, or caves, often on remote islets that provide protection from terrestrial predators. Nests are typically constructed in these sheltered locations to safeguard the single egg and chick from environmental exposure.
Colonies exhibit high nesting densities in suitable habitats, with birds displaying strong site fidelity by returning to the same burrow or crevice annually. This philopatry contributes to stable colony structures, where pairs reuse established sites over multiple breeding seasons. Recent monitoring efforts, including fixed-plot surveys on Selvagem Grande from 2009 to 2023, indicate a population increase of 1.45% per year in this key colony. In the Azores, populations have remained stable based on assessments through 2012, with ongoing camera-trap surveys in 2023 confirming consistent occupancy in major sites like Corvo Island.

Migration and non-breeding range

Cory's shearwaters undertake long-distance migrations following the breeding season, with adults typically departing their North Atlantic and Mediterranean colonies between late October and early November. They reach their wintering grounds by mid-December after an average journey of about 36 days, and depart these areas in mid-February, returning to breeding sites by mid-March. Juveniles fledge in September or October and initiate their first southward migration shortly thereafter, often around late October, exhibiting more exploratory paths compared to adults.
The migratory routes involve post-breeding dispersal across the Atlantic Ocean, where birds exploit trade winds and ocean currents for efficient gliding flight. From breeding sites, many individuals initially move northward or westward into the North Atlantic before turning south, while others proceed directly southwestward along favorable tailwinds toward subtropical regions. Tracking studies reveal divergence in paths south of the Tropic of Capricorn, with some birds veering toward South American waters and others continuing to southern African coasts. This pelagic lifestyle allows them to cover vast distances while largely avoiding landmasses.
During the non-breeding period, Cory's shearwaters concentrate in the subtropical Atlantic gyre, utilizing nutrient-rich upwelling zones in areas such as the northwest Atlantic off Newfoundland, the Brazilian Current, the Canary Current off West Africa, and the Benguela-Agulhas Current off southern Africa. Geolocation and GPS tracking indicate high individual flexibility, with some birds shifting wintering sites between years by over 7,000 km, including movements between hemispheres or ocean basins. Recent observations show increased presence off the U.S. East Coast and in Atlantic Canada since the 2010s, potentially linked to warming sea surface temperatures altering distribution patterns.

Life history

Breeding

Cory's shearwater (Calonectris borealis) exhibits a highly monogamous mating system, forming long-term pair bonds that can last for many years, with documented cases of pairs breeding together for up to 18 consecutive seasons. These bonds are typically re-established during the pre-laying phase, with both partners sharing incubation and chick-rearing duties equally. Divorce rates are low, averaging around 11% across study periods in the Selvagens Islands, often increasing following reproductive failure, though extra-pair copulations remain rare. Pair formation emphasizes prior familiarity over physical traits, and vocal duets play a key role in mate recognition and nest defense during courtship.
The breeding season commences with adults returning to colonies on islands in the North Atlantic from late February to early March. Mating primarily occurs in April, leading to egg-laying from late May to mid-June, when pairs deposit a single egg per clutch. Incubation lasts 5255 days and is shared by both parents, who alternate shiftsthe male usually takes the initial boutwith hatching occurring from mid-July to early August. Nests consist of burrows or crevices excavated in rocky or sandy terrain on steep coastal cliffs or plateaus.
During the chick-rearing phase, which extends for 90100 days until fledging in late September to October, parents alternate foraging trips of varying lengthsshort stints of 14 days and longer ones up to 10 daysto provision the chick. Chicks experience rapid initial growth, gaining weight steadily in the first 4560 days to reach approximately 140% of adult mass before fledging at a more streamlined size, with linear increases in wing span throughout development. Feeding occurs every 23 days on average, allowing chicks to accumulate fat reserves independently during parental absences. Post-fledging care is minimal, as independent juveniles depart the colony alone to begin their southward migration.

Foraging and diet

Cory's shearwater primarily feeds on small pelagic fish, such as sardines (Sardina pilchardus) and anchovies (5075% by weight or frequency of occurrence in Azorean populations), alongside cephalopods (up to 62% frequency of occurrence) and crustaceans (around 20% frequency). Specific cephalopod families like Histioteuthidae and Ommastrephidae are common, reflecting opportunistic predation on mesopelagic species. The species also scavenges fishery discards and offal from trawlers, supplementing natural prey in areas of human activity.
Foraging occurs mainly at the sea surface through seizing prey during flight or while swimming, with occasional shallow dives reaching up to 20 m to pursue schools. Birds often associate with fishing vessels to exploit disturbed prey and follow mixed-species feeding flocks, enhancing efficiency in patchy resources. Daily energy intake for adults is estimated at around 1,1001,200 kJ, equivalent to 100200 g of prey depending on composition.
As a mid-trophic level predator, Cory's shearwater plays a key role in marine food webs by linking lower trophic prey to higher predators, with diet reflecting local productivity and showing seasonal shifts, with cephalopods more frequent pre-laying and fish increasing during chick-rearing. GPS tracking reveals extensive foraging ranges, with short trips under 150 km during breeding and longer excursions exceeding 1,000 km. These patterns underscore adaptability to dynamic oceanic environments, including brief overlaps with non-breeding migration routes.
Flight adaptations support this lifestyle, featuring slow, floppy wingbeats interspersed with gliding and dynamic soaring to conserve energy over long distances; birds often bow their wings low over waves to scan for prey.

Conservation

Population status

The global breeding population of Cory's shearwater (Calonectris borealis) is estimated at approximately 250,000300,000 pairs, with over 85% concentrated in the Portuguese archipelagos of the Azores and Madeira (including the Selvagem Islands). This equates to roughly 504,000507,000 mature individuals based on assessments from the early 2010s, though more recent site-specific surveys suggest stability or modest growth in key colonies.
Population trends are stable overall at the global scale, with no evidence of significant decline, but regional variations exist. In the Canary Islands, studies on birds attracted to artificial lights have shown mixed trends, with earlier research suggesting possible declines but recent data indicating an increasing trend in light attraction that may not directly reflect population changes. Conversely, populations in the Azores and Selvagem Grande have shown increases due to conservation protections, including predator control and artificial nest provision; for instance, the Selvagem Grande colony, the species' largest, has recovered at an average annual rate of 4.6% since the 1980s and continued growing at 1.45% per year from 2009 to 2023.
The species is classified as Least Concern on the IUCN Red List, assessed in 2018, owing to its large extent of occurrence exceeding 97 million km and failure to meet thresholds for Vulnerable status under criterion B1ab(v), which requires an extent below 20,000 km combined with continuing decline. Monitoring relies on methods such as fixed-plot counts of burrows and acoustic surveys to estimate breeding pairs non-invasively, with recent 2025 studies at major colonies confirming the absence of a global population decline.

Threats and management

Cory's shearwater faces several anthropogenic threats that impact its populations across breeding and foraging ranges. Bycatch in longline fisheries represents a significant mortality source, particularly in the Atlantic but also in the western Mediterranean where it occurs alongside Scopoli's shearwater (Calonectris diomedea), following the 2024 taxonomic split recognizing these as separate species. Light pollution disorients fledglings during their first flights, leading to groundings, collisions, and increased predation risk; in the Canary Islands, rehabilitation programs at centers like Tafira Wildlife Rehabilitation Centre address this by treating affected birds, with ongoing efforts rescuing affected individuals. Invasive predators, including rats (Rattus spp.), cats (Felis catus), and house mice (Mus musculus), prey on eggs and chicks on breeding islands, reducing reproductive success; for instance, cats account for up to 84% of predation events on chicks in some Azorean colonies. Plastic ingestion is widespread, with over 90% of fledglings in the Canary Islands (Tenerife: 94.5%, Gran Canaria: 88.9%, Lanzarote: 81.3%) containing plastics in their stomachs, primarily from fishing debris. Climate change exacerbates these pressures by altering prey distribution and oceanographic conditions, influencing foraging patterns and survival rates through variability in large-scale indices like the Southern Oscillation Index.
Nest predation by invasives causes substantial chick losses, with studies on Terceira Island in the Azores documenting introduced mammals as primary culprits, though exact rates vary by colony accessibility and predator density; historical data indicate 1530% chick mortality attributable to such predation in affected sites. Poaching for food and bait persists in the Azores, where 5001,500 chicks are harvested annually, representing a direct threat to local populations.
Conservation management includes targeted invasive species eradication, such as the successful removal of rabbits (Oryctolagus cuniculus) and house mice from Selvagem Grande Island in 20072008, which has contributed to a 4.6% annual population increase since protections were strengthened in 1977. In the Canary Islands, mitigation for light pollution involves public lighting master plans under the EU LIFE Natura@night project, promoting shielded and reduced-intensity lights to create "dark refuges" for fledglings. Bycatch reduction employs bird-scaring lines and weighted branch lines in longline fisheries, proven effective in decreasing interactions with shearwaters in Mediterranean fleets. EU LIFE-funded initiatives, including migration tracking studies in 2025, support risk mapping and connectivity assessments to inform protections.
Recent recommendations emphasize expanding marine protected areas (MPAs) to safeguard key foraging zones and reduce fisheries overlap, alongside sustained invasive control and pollution mitigation, to ensure long-term population stability.
