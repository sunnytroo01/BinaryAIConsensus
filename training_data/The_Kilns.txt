The Kilns
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Kilns is a red-brick house located in Headington Quarry on the outskirts of Oxford, England, built in 1922 on the site of a former brickworks and featuring a flooded clay pit that forms a pond in its grounds. It is most renowned as the longtime home of the author and scholar C.S. Lewis, who purchased the property in 1930 for 3,300 along with his brother Major Warren Hamilton Lewis and family friend Mrs. Janie King Moore, with the title registered in Mrs. Moore's name. Lewis resided there from October 1930 until his death on November 22, 1963, sharing the house with his brother, Mrs. Moore (until her death in 1951), her daughter Maureen, and later his wife Joy Davidman (from 1957 until her death in 1960).
During Lewis's occupancy, The Kilns served as a creative hub and communal household, housing evacuee children during World War II whose stories inspired elements of The Chronicles of Narnia, the seven-book fantasy series he wrote there between 1949 and 1954. It was also the setting for much of his other major works, including The Screwtape Letters (1942), Mere Christianity (1952), and The Problem of Pain (1940), as well as his scholarly and theological writings composed in the home's modest study overlooking the pond. The house's semi-rural location, about three miles from Oxford city center, reflected Lewis's preference for a quiet environment conducive to reflection and writing, though it faced challenges like structural deterioration by the 1950s due to poor maintenance.
After Lewis's death, his brother Warnie continued living at The Kilns until 1973, when the property was sold after his death. In the 1980s, the California-based C.S. Lewis Foundation acquired the house for 130,000 (without the surrounding land) and undertook extensive restoration to return it to its 1930s appearance, preserving original features like the tiled floors and fireplaces. Today, The Kilns operates as the C.S. Lewis Study Centre, managed by the Foundation since 1993, offering scholars-in-residence programs, summer seminars, and guided tours by appointment to explore Lewis's life and legacy. The property was added to Oxford City Council's Local Heritage Asset Register on May 20, 2025, recognizing its cultural significance, and visitors can access the grounds and pond freely while the house remains a protected site for literary heritage.

History

Construction and Early Ownership

The Kilns was constructed in 1922 as a red-brick bungalow with an added upper storey, situated on the site of a former brickworks in Forest Hill, within the Headington Quarry parish near Oxford. This modest twelve-room brick cottage reflected the functional suburban architecture of early 20th-century Oxford, designed for practical residential use with extensions to enhance livability in the expanding outskirts. The building's simple, extended cottage style prioritized affordability and utility, aligning with the area's transition from industrial to residential development.
The property's eight-acre grounds retained industrial traces from the brickworks, including two clay kilns that inspired the house's name and were repurposed for fuel storage after operations ceased, remaining until their destruction in 1968. A prominent feature was the flooded clay pit, which naturally formed a pond and later became a protected wildlife site managed by the Berkshire, Buckinghamshire and Oxfordshire Wildlife Trust. These elements underscored the site's origins in clay extraction and brick production, contrasting with its emerging role as a private residence set back from Kiln Lane.
Early ownership records indicate that Joseph Thorne resided at The Kilns from 1926 to 1928, as listed in local directories. The property was auctioned on 18 May 1927, following an advertisement in The Times on 28 April 1927, during Thorne's occupancy. It appeared unoccupied in directories for 1929 and 1930, paving the way for its acquisition in 1930.

Lewis Acquisition and Ownership Period

On 6 July 1930, C.S. Lewis and his brother Warren "Warnie" Lewis first viewed The Kilns, an eight-acre property on the outskirts of Oxford, which they jointly purchased later that year for 3,300 along with Janie King Moore, the mother of Lewis's deceased World War I comrade Edward "Paddy" Moore. Although the three contributed to the cost with Moore's estate covering less than halfthe property title was placed solely in Moore's name for legal reasons, reflecting her role as the household matriarch. The Lewis brothers and Moore, along with her daughter Maureen, took up residence on 10 October 1930, marking the beginning of a shared domestic life that would last over three decades.
The household composition evolved through several key changes during the ownership period. Warnie Lewis, upon retiring from the British Army in 1932, joined his brother and the Moores as a permanent resident, contributing to the maintenance of the home. Janie Moore passed away in 1951 after years of declining health, leaving the property to her daughter Maureen while the Lewis brothers continued to live there. In 1957, C.S. Lewis married American writer Joy Davidman, who resided at The Kilns with her two sons until her death from cancer in July 1960; Lewis himself died on 22 November 1963. Warnie remained the sole occupant thereafter, preserving the household until his own death on 9 April 1973.
Under the Lewis stewardship, The Kilns underwent modest modifications to suit the needs of its residents while retaining its original red-brick bungalow structure, which had been built in 1922 on a former brickworks sitewith the pond originating from a flooded clay pit. The existing tennis court, orchard, and greenhouse were maintained as central features of the eight-acre grounds, which the family actively tended despite financial constraints and wartime rationing. Minor interior updates, such as adaptations for Moore's mobility in her later years and basic furnishings for additional residents, were implemented without major structural alterations, preserving the home's modest, twelve-room layout.
Following Warnie's death, Maureen Dunbarnow Lady Dunbarsold the house to the Thirsk family in 1973, while much of the surrounding land was subdivided for development, ending the Lewis family's direct ownership after 43 years.

Post-Lewis Ownership and Foundation Transfer

After the death of Warren Lewis in 1973, the Thirsk family purchased The Kilns from Maureen Dunbar and owned the property until the mid-1980s, during which time they maintained the house while portions of the original grounds continued to be developed for housing, significantly reducing the estate's size.
In 1986, the house was acquired by the C.S. Lewis Foundation for 130,000, though the purchase initially excluded the remaining surrounding land. This acquisition was facilitated by The Kilns Association, a group formed as a limited partnership in 1984 specifically to purchase and preserve the site, which had fallen into disrepair and faced risks of further development or neglect.
The Association transitioned to a charitable company in 1986 but encountered substantial financial and legal challenges in maintaining the property, including ongoing fundraising efforts to prevent misuse. In March 1988, due to resource constraints, it merged with the C.S. Lewis Foundation, transferring full ownership and operational responsibility to the larger organization under the leadership of president Stan Mattson. By 1993, the Foundation had secured complete control over the site, enabling sustained preservation initiatives.

Architecture and Grounds

Building Design and Interior Layout

The Kilns is a modest 12-room red-brick cottage constructed in 1922 as a bungalow on the site of a former brickworks in Risinghurst, Oxford, England. The structure features a ground floor with an added upper storey, incorporating dormer windows that contribute to its compact, functional design. Located at coordinates 51.7568N 1.1900W, the property now fronts Lewis Close, a cul-de-sac developed in the late 1960s that reduced the original grounds.
The interior layout centers around communal and private spaces suited to multi-resident living, with six bedrooms distributed across both floors to accommodate family and guests. Key areas include C.S. Lewis's upstairs study, equipped with a writing desk and extensive bookshelves that formed the core of his scholarly environment. The ground floor houses the drawing roomalso referred to as the common roomfeaturing comfortable seating, book-lined walls, and a large walnut desk, alongside a spacious paneled library used for gatherings. The kitchen serves as a central hub with an original Aga stove, while the dining room offers light-filled views toward the garden.
Construction materials emphasize durability and simplicity, including exposed red brickwork throughout, quarry tile floors on the ground level, and wooden elements in upper areas. Nine fireplaces, originally sealed, were integral to the home's heating and ambiance. The layout evolved from its initial single-storey bungalow form through the addition of the upper storey to support expanded household needs, including guest accommodations, without significant modern alterations until the 1980s restoration, which preserved the 1930s aesthetic using period photographs. This restoration reopened fireplaces, uncovered original floors, and reinstated period furnishings like cabinets and bookcases.

Grounds, Pond, and Landscape Features

The grounds of The Kilns originally spanned eight acres when acquired by C.S. Lewis and his brother Warren in 1930, encompassing a lawn area, a wooded hill, and remnants of the site's prior use as a brickworks. The property's expansive layout provided a rural retreat on the outskirts of Oxford, with direct access from the house to the surrounding landscape via rear doors and garden paths. The land's size was gradually reduced beginning in the late 1960s, when portions were developed for housing, and further after Warren Lewis's death in 1973, when the estate passed to new ownership without all original acreage.
Positioned south of Kiln Lane in the Headington Quarry district, the grounds blend seamlessly with the area's historic quarry terrain, featuring undulating hills and natural woodland that reflect the site's industrial past. The former brickworks left enduring marks, including clay pits and kilns, which shaped the property's topography and character during the Lewis era.
At the heart of the grounds lies the Kiln Pond, a flooded clay pit originating from the brickworks operations, bordered by dense trees. Residents, including C.S. Lewis, frequently used the pond for swimming during warmer months, appreciating its secluded, natural setting amid the wooded surroundings.
Additional landscape elements included a tennis court installed in the 1930s, which served as a recreational space for household members and visitors. The two original brick kilns, which inspired the property's name, stood as prominent features until their demolition in 1968 to accommodate nearby housing development. Much of the remaining wooded grounds now adjoin the C.S. Lewis Nature Reserve, preserving the area's natural integration with the quarry landscape.

Residents and Daily Life

Core Household Members

The core household at The Kilns during the C.S. Lewis era was shaped by a communal living arrangement rooted in Lewis's wartime promise to his friend Edward "Paddy" Moore, made in 1917 while training as soldiers; if one died in battle, the survivor would care for the other's family, and after Paddy's death in 1918, Lewis honored this by supporting his mother Janie Moore and sister Maureen. This setup led to the joint purchase of the property in 1930, with title held solely in Janie Moore's name despite Lewis's financial contributions from his Oxford academic salary, which served as the primary support for the household.
Clive Staples Lewis (18981963), known as "Jack," resided at The Kilns from its acquisition in October 1930 until his death in November 1963, providing intellectual leadership and financial stability to the unconventional family unit while pursuing his scholarly career at Magdalen College, Oxford. His brother, Warren Hamilton Lewis (18951973), nicknamed "Warnie," joined the household in 1932 upon retiring as a major in the British Army after a distinguished military career, taking on practical responsibilities such as property maintenance, gardening, and daily repairs to sustain the aging house and its expansive grounds.
Janie King Moore (d. 1951), often called "Minto," lived at The Kilns from 1930 until her death, acting as a maternal figure to Lewis and influencing the decision to buy the property; she oversaw domestic management and household decisions in the early years, though her role diminished due to declining health and increasing frailty by the late 1940s. Her daughter, Maureen Helen Moore (later Blake and Dunbar, 19061997), resided intermittently at the property during her youth and returns, contributing to family dynamics as a foster sister to Lewis while pursuing her own life, including marriages and a nursing career.
Later additions to the core included Helen Joy Davidman (19151960), an American writer who married Lewis in 1957 and lived at The Kilns with her two sons until her death from cancer in 1960, bringing a new layer of emotional and familial support to the aging brothers. Long-term staff like Alice Hamilton Moore (no relation to Janie Moore), who served as cook and housekeeper from the early 1930s and resided in an on-site bungalow, were integral to the household's operations, handling meals and upkeep alongside Warnie until her death in 1939. This stable yet evolving group occasionally hosted intellectual visitors, such as members of the Inklings, for brief social gatherings that complemented the resident dynamics without altering the core structure.

Visitors, Staff, and Social Activities

The Kilns served as a hub for various staff members who supported the household's daily operations over the decades. Fred Paxford, employed as the gardener and handyman from the 1930s through the 1960s, handled maintenance, driving, and gardening tasks while living in an on-site bungalow; his wry humor and steadfast service reportedly inspired the character of Puddleglum in C.S. Lewis's The Silver Chair. Alice Hamilton Moore, a widow and friend of the Moore family, acted as the cook during the early years of World War II, contributing to meals amid wartime rationing before her death in November 1939. Other helpers, such as Vera Henry, who assisted with cooking a few times weekly, occasionally joined the staff but often faced interpersonal tensions within the household.
Notable visitors included evacuee children during World War II, starting with the first arrivals on September 2, 1939, as part of the initial evacuations from London; the household provided refuge for groups of teenage girls through the war, including during the Blitz, with around 11 girls in total staying in rotations through 1945, including June Flewett from 1943 to January 1945, and they often heard early drafts of Lewis's Narnia stories during garden sessions or walks designed to spark their imaginations. Later, from 1953 onward, Joy Davidman's sons, Douglas and David Gresham, became frequent visitors and eventual residents at The Kilns following their mother's marriage to Lewis in 1956; Douglas, in particular, recalled the home as a place of both familial warmth and challenges amid his brother's mental health struggles. These transient guests contrasted with the core residents, enriching the household's dynamic without permanent integration.
Social activities at The Kilns emphasized communal recreation and intellectual exchange, including weekend gatherings where Lewis invited male Oxford students from Magdalen College for tennis on the property's court or swimming in the pond, often extending invitations to the evacuee girls for mixed participation. Informal evening readings and discussions occurred regularly, with Lewis sharing stories or engaging in lively debates; while the primary Inklings meetingsfeaturing members like J.R.R. Tolkien and Hugo Dysontook place at pubs such as the Eagle and Child, occasional smaller sessions spilled over to the house for continued conversation. Daily routines incorporated Lewis's two- to three-mile walk to Magdalen College, sometimes joined by household members, and wartime precautions like using the on-site air raid shelter during alerts, underscoring the property's role as a social and resilient haven.

Literary and Cultural Significance

Writing and Creative Output at The Kilns

During his residency at The Kilns from 1930 to 1963, C.S. Lewis established the house as a central hub for his prolific literary production, authoring over 30 books and numerous essays that spanned fiction, theology, and literary criticism. This period marked a shift from his earlier cramped rooms at Magdalen College to a more stable domestic environment conducive to sustained creative work. Key among these outputs were his seminal Christian apologetic texts and imaginative narratives, all crafted amid the routines of household life.
Lewis composed all seven volumes of The Chronicles of Narnia between 1949 and 1954, primarily in the upstairs study overlooking the grounds. Earlier, he wrote The Screwtape Letters in 1942 at the same writing desk, a satirical exploration of temptation that became one of his most enduring works. Mere Christianity, published in 1952, evolved from his BBC radio broadcasts of the early 1940s and was refined during his time at the property, serving as a cornerstone of modern Christian thought. His scholarly contributions included The Allegory of Love in 1936, a landmark study of medieval literature completed soon after acquiring the house.
Lewis adhered to a consistent writing routine, dedicating mornings from 9 a.m. to 1 p.m. to composition in the upstairs study, a practice that sustained his output despite academic duties and household responsibilities. This dedicated space facilitated focused sessions away from interruptions. During the 1990s restoration led by the C.S. Lewis Foundation, original manuscriptsincluding drafts related to the Narnia serieswere uncovered in the attic, offering direct artifacts of his creative labor at the site.
Posthumously, insights into Lewis's creative process at The Kilns emerge from his brother Warnie Lewis's diaries, edited and published as Brothers and Friends: The Diaries of Major Warren Hamilton Lewis, which chronicle daily interactions, discussions with the Inklings, and the domestic rhythms that underpinned Jack's productivity. These records highlight how the shared household dynamic provided both stability and intellectual stimulation for Lewis's endeavors.

Inspirations Drawn from the Property

At The Kilns, C.S. Lewis began improvising tales for evacuee children billeted there during World War II, with the first sessions occurring in the autumn of 1939. These wartime guests, fleeing the Blitz in London, numbered a small group totaling around eight over the years, including teenage girls who stayed from late 1939 through 1945, and their experiences directly shaped the evacuation motif central to The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe. One evacuee, June Flewett (later Jill Freud), whose vivacious personality influenced the character of Lucy Pevensie, resided at the property from 1943 to 1945.
Elements of The Kilns itself permeated Lewis's fictional world, with the house serving as the model for Professor Kirke's countryside mansion in The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe, complete with its rambling layout and surrounding grounds. The property's pond, a site for Lewis's leisurely swims and boating with his brother and friends, contributed to the watery, marshy landscapes in the Narnia series, including the fen-like settings around the Marsh-wiggles in The Silver Chair. Additionally, Lewis's long-serving gardener and handyman, Fred Paxford, who tended the estate from 1930 until Lewis's death, inspired the pessimistic yet steadfast character of Puddleglum the Marsh-wiggle in the same novel, capturing Paxford's wry demeanor and rural practicality.
Beyond direct literary echoes, The Kilns represented a haven of domestic stability amid Lewis's complex household dynamics, fostering the creative focus that produced his fantasy masterpieces. The property features prominently in biographies of Lewis, such as George Sayer's Jack: A Life of C.S. Lewis, and in cultural depictions like the 1993 film Shadowlands, which portrays it as the backdrop for his marriage to Joy Davidman. Today, The Kilns draws literary pilgrims seeking to connect with Lewis's world, managed by the C.S. Lewis Foundation for tours and scholarly residence, while underscoring his ties to Oxford's Inklings literary circle through personal gatherings and shared intellectual life; as of 2025, visitor interest has grown following its addition to the Oxford City Council's Local Heritage Asset Register.

Preservation and Current Status

Restoration Projects and Challenges

Following the sale of The Kilns in 1973 after C.S. Lewis's death, the property experienced neglect and deterioration under subsequent private ownership, leading to significant structural decay by the early 1980s.
In 1984, The Kilns Association was established as a limited partnership to acquire and restore the house to its 1930s appearance, addressing issues such as crumbling brickwork and water damage from years of disuse. The group purchased the property in 1986, but faced immediate financial shortages that limited initial repair efforts.
By March 1988, ongoing funding constraints prompted The Kilns Association to transfer ownership to the California-based C.S. Lewis Foundation, which merged its board with the association's to pool resources and sustain the restoration. This merger enabled continued work on essential repairs, though the foundation's efforts were hampered by the property's reduced acreage; much of the original grounds had been subdivided for housing development, including the construction of Lewis Close in 1968, which demolished the site's two historic brick kilns and diminished the landscape's authenticity.
A major volunteer-led restoration project commenced in 1993, involving enthusiasts from the United States and Britain who focused on structural reinforcements to the brickwork, roof, and foundations, aiming to return the house to its mid-20th-century condition during Lewis's residency. These efforts, which continued through 2001, addressed long-term decay while preserving the home's modest Edwardian design, culminating in the completion of renovations that transformed the site into a functional study center. Despite these milestones, challenges persisted, including the loss of original features like the kilns, which could not be reconstructed, and the need to balance preservation with practical use amid limited budgets.
In a recent development enhancing its protected status, The Kilns was added to Oxford City Council's Heritage Asset Register on 20 May 2025, recognizing its cultural significance and providing additional safeguards against future threats to its integrity.

Contemporary Use and Public Access

Since acquiring full control in 1993, the C.S. Lewis Foundation has owned and managed The Kilns, restoring the property and transforming it into an educational hub dedicated to preserving and promoting the legacy of C.S. Lewis. In July 2002, the site was officially dedicated as the C.S. Lewis Study Centre at The Kilns, emphasizing scholarly research, fellowship, and reflection in a setting evocative of Lewis's own life and work.
The Foundation operates several key programs at the property. The Scholars-in-Residence initiative provides year-round opportunities for visiting scholars and advanced graduate students to live and conduct research at The Kilns while affiliated with Oxford University, fostering a small, intentional community focused on Lewis's intellectual and spiritual contributions. Since 2001, the annual C.S. Lewis Summer Seminars have brought small groups of participants to the site for intensive week-long studies led by leading Lewis scholars, combining lectures, discussions, and excursions in Oxford. These programs prioritize academic depth over tourism, with residencies limited to qualified applicants and no public overnight accommodations available.
Public access to The Kilns is structured to balance preservation with educational outreach. Guided interior tours of the house are offered by appointment only on Tuesdays, Thursdays, and Saturdays, with bookings required at least two weeks in advance via email to accommodate scholar schedules and private functions; fees apply, and groups of 15 or more incur special rates. The grounds, integrated with the adjacent C.S. Lewis Nature Reserve managed by the Berks, Bucks and Oxfordshire Wildlife Trust, remain open daily for public visitation, allowing visitors to explore the landscape that inspired Lewis's writings through established paths and trails. Annual events, such as the Summer Seminars and occasional commemorative gatherings honoring Lewis's life milestones, further enhance the site's role as a living memorial.
