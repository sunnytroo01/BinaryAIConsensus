 Luis Palomino (born October 9, 1980) is a Peruvian-born American mixed martial artist and bare-knuckle fighter based in Miami, Florida, renowned for his knockout power and tenure in major combat sports promotions.
In mixed martial arts, Palomino compiled a professional record of 26 wins and 17 losses between 2006 and 2019, securing 15 victories by knockout or technical knockout, and competed in prominent organizations such as Bellator MMA and World Series of Fighting (WSOF). He captured the lightweight championship in the Championship Fighting Alliance (CFA) by defeating Peter Grimes via submission (armbar) on May 6, 2011, though he later lost the title to Luiz Firmino in 2013, and also won the Absolute Championship Berkut (ACB) lightweight title with a second-round TKO over Musa Khamanaev in 2017. Notable bouts include two high-profile lightweight title challenges against Justin Gaethje in WSOF, both ending in losses by TKO in 2015, as well as a win over future UFC champion Jorge Masvidal early in his career.
Transitioning to bare-knuckle boxing, Palomino has achieved greater success in the Bare Knuckle Fighting Championship (BKFC), where he holds a record of 10 wins and 1 loss as of 2025. He made history as the organization's first double champion by winning both the lightweight and welterweight titles, with the lightweight belt secured via first-round knockout over Isaac Vallie-Flagg in 2020 and defended successfully six times thereafter. In March 2025, Palomino claimed the inaugural BKFC King of the Streets championship by defeating Howard Davis via unanimous decision at BKFC 70, solidifying his status as one of the top bare-knuckle fighters pound-for-pound. A black belt in Brazilian jiu-jitsu, Palomino's aggressive striking style and resilience have defined his career across disciplines.

Background

Early life

Luis Vicente Palomino was born on October 9, 1980, in Lima, Peru. Growing up in a modest family amid the socioeconomic challenges of 1980s Peru, Palomino experienced a brief but formative childhood marked by the country's economic instability and urban hardships before his family sought better opportunities abroad. His Peruvian heritage, rooted in resilience and family bonds, would later influence his approach to adversity in combat sports.
At around age 10 or 11, Palomino immigrated to the United States with his family, entering illegally using fake passports in pursuit of a brighter future; they initially settled in California. Raised primarily by his mother alongside an older brother, he faced significant adaptation struggles as an undocumented immigrant, including limited legal work options and the constant threat of deportation. The family later relocated to Miami Beach, Florida, when Palomino was about 14, where he encountered intensified challenges such as rampant gang activity in his high school neighborhood, leading to involvement in street fights and a tough urban environment that tested his resilience.
Palomino's initial exposure to combat sports came shortly after arriving in California, where he began boxing by tagging along with his older brother to a local gym, training for about three years amid the family's adjustment to life in the U.S. Upon moving to Miami, at age 14, a middle school friend introduced him to capoeira, a Brazilian martial art blending dance, acrobatics, and striking; inspired by films like Only the Strong featuring his future mentor Csar Carneiro, Palomino started informal training that emphasized agility and self-defense in a dangerous setting. These early experiences, shaped by his mother's guidance and the harsh realities of immigrant life, laid the groundwork for his later pursuit of structured martial arts training.

Martial arts training

Palomino began his martial arts journey in his early teens amid a challenging upbringing in Miami Beach, Florida, where he navigated gang influences and other hardships. At age 14, he was introduced to capoeira through Csar Carneiro, a Brazilian master who became a pivotal mentor and father figure, guiding him away from street troubles toward disciplined training inspired by films like Only the Strong. Prior to capoeira, Palomino had trained in boxing, which laid the groundwork for his striking foundation.
Around 2006, as he transitioned toward mixed martial arts, Palomino focused on building core skills in wrestling and striking, drawing from his boxing and capoeira backgrounds to develop explosive power. His affiliation with renowned gyms, including Jackson Wink MMA Academy, honed his technical proficiency and contributed to his reputation as a natural-born striker capable of delivering devastating knockoutsevidenced by 15 such finishes in his MMA career.
Palomino later incorporated Brazilian jiu-jitsu into his regimen, training under Daniel Valverde at MMA Masters in Miami, which enhanced his ground game and grappling defense. He progressed to black belt under Valverde, complementing his striking prowess with versatile submission skills. Standing at 5 feet 8 inches and competing primarily in the 145155-pound divisions, Palomino evolved into a well-rounded striker with a solid defensive ground foundation, influenced in part by his Peruvian heritage's emphasis on resilient, aggressive combat approaches.

Mixed martial arts career

Early career

Luis Palomino made his professional mixed martial arts debut on June 24, 2006, in the Absolute Fighting Championship (AFC), a regional promotion based in Florida, where he secured a first-round TKO victory over Louis Pilato via strikes at 1:23. He followed this with another quick finish on August 26, 2006, defeating Mike Soltz by TKO (punches) at just 0:25 of the first round, and then earned a unanimous decision win over Jeremy May on October 21, 2006, to cap a perfect 30 run in the promotion. These early successes showcased Palomino's aggressive striking style, rooted in his capoeira and kickboxing foundation from prior training.
Following his AFC stint, Palomino competed in various smaller regional promotions, compiling additional wins amid a mix of setbacks. On November 18, 2006, he suffered his first professional loss, a first-round TKO to Travis Cox in World Extreme Fighting. He rebounded in 2007 with a first-round TKO over Steve Conley in the Premier Fighting League (PFL) on March 31 and a knockout of Patrick Mikesz at 1:10 of the first round in the Ring of Combat (ROC) on October 6, though he dropped a second-round TKO decision to Mike Bernhard in Premier Xtreme Fighting (PXF) on December 8. In 2008, Palomino notched first-round TKOs against Andrew Carron in the United States Fight League (USFL) on September 13 and Eric Reynolds in G-Force Fights on November 6, followed by a five-round unanimous decision victory over Marc Stevens in USFL on November 29, but he tapped out to a rear-naked choke against John Mahlow in Xtreme Fighting Championships (XFC) on December 5.
By 2009, Palomino continued building his record in regional circuits, including a September 26 loss via rear-naked choke to Jonathan Brookins in G-Force Fights and a third-round TKO win over Rafael Dias in Unconquered on November 20. These bouts took place primarily in Florida-based events like AFC, PFL, USFL, PXF, and Unconquered, alongside New York circuits such as G-Force Fights and ROC, highlighting his activity in the Northeast and Southeast regional MMA scenes. Overall, Palomino progressed to a 94 record by the end of 2009 in these regional promotions, with seven knockouts and TKOs in his nine victories, demonstrating his power-punching approach.
Throughout his early career, Palomino faced challenges as an undocumented immigrant from Peru, balancing rigorous training with odd jobs and family responsibilities to support his household in Miami. Weight cuts to welterweight also proved demanding, requiring disciplined preparation amid his developing professional schedule.

Bellator Fighting Championships

Palomino first entered the Bellator Fighting Championships in 2009 during Season 1 and Season 2. On April 3, 2009, at Bellator 1, he suffered a split decision loss to Estevan Payan after three rounds. He rebounded on May 8, 2009, at Bellator 6 with a first-round TKO victory over Nick Gonzalez via punches at 2:13. Later that year, on June 19, 2009, at Bellator 12, Palomino earned a unanimous decision win over Troy Gerhart after three rounds (29-28, 29-28, 30-27).
Palomino returned for Season 3's lightweight tournament in 2010. In the quarterfinal bout at Bellator 21 on June 10, 2010, he faced Jose Figueroa and secured a unanimous decision victory after three rounds, with scores of 29-28 across the board, advancing to the semifinals and showcasing his durable striking approach.
The tournament concluded for Palomino at Bellator 24 on August 12, 2010, where he met seasoned veteran Yves Edwards in the semifinals. Despite a competitive effort, Palomino dropped a unanimous decision loss after three rounds (29-28, 29-28, 29-28), preventing him from reaching the finals and highlighting the challenges of the single-elimination format against established opponents.
Palomino returned to Bellator in 2011 for a non-tournament featherweight matchup at Bellator 46 on June 25, against Pat Curran. The fight ended abruptly in the first round when Curran submitted him via Peruvian necktie at 3:49, marking Palomino's final appearance in the promotion. Over his 20092011 tenure, Palomino achieved a 33 record, gaining valuable experience in high-stakes tournament bouts that elevated his profile within the lightweight and featherweight divisions.

Championship Fighting Alliance

On February 4, 2010, in a regional G-Force Fights bout, Palomino defeated future UFC champion Jorge Masvidal via split decision after three rounds, a significant early win that boosted his reputation.
Palomino made his Championship Fighting Alliance (CFA) debut on May 6, 2011, defeating Peter Grimes via fourth-round submission (armbar) to capture the promotion's inaugural lightweight title at the event CFA: The Title. His competitive run in the promotion intensified in 2012, beginning with a unanimous decision victory over James Warfield on April 13 at CFA 6: Palomino vs. Warfield, where he controlled the fight with effective striking and grappling to improve his record to 2-0 in CFA. This performance positioned him for a high-profile lightweight title bout against former DREAM and Strikeforce contender Gesias "JZ" Cavalcante on June 30 at CFA 7: Never Give Up.
In the main event at CFA 7, held at the BankUnited Center in Miami, Florida, Palomino secured the CFA Lightweight Championship with a third-round knockout via punches at 1:41, handing Cavalcante his first knockout loss in a career spanning over a decade. The victory highlighted Palomino's aggressive ground-and-pound style, as he overwhelmed Cavalcante with heavy strikes from top position after a scramble. As the new champion, Palomino aimed to defend his belt, but in his first defense on January 19, 2013, at CFA 9: Night of Champions, he dropped a unanimous decision to Luiz Firmino after five rounds, losing the title in a closely contested grappling-heavy fight.
Following the title loss, Palomino rebounded with a dominant first-round knockout (punch) over Robert Washington on May 24 at CFA 11: Kyle vs. Wiuff, again employing his signature ground-and-pound to finish the bout at 3:26. His CFA tenure concluded on October 12, 2013, at CFA 12: Sampo vs. Thao, where he suffered a unanimous decision loss to The Ultimate Fighter winner Efrain Escudero in a three-round non-title affair. Over six fights in CFA from 2011 to 2013, Palomino compiled a 4-2 record, with three knockouts underscoring his finishing power and establishing him as a top regional lightweight.
The CFA championship win and subsequent performances elevated Palomino's profile, leading to a multi-fight contract with the World Series of Fighting in November 2013 and marking a pivotal step toward national-level competition.

World Series of Fighting

Palomino debuted with the World Series of Fighting (WSOF) on January 18, 2014, at WSOF 8 in Temecula, California, where he defeated Jorge Patino by knockout via punches in the second round at 4:20. This victory marked a strong entry into the promotion following his recent lightweight title win in the Championship Fighting Alliance. He built on this momentum with another knockout win, stopping Lewis Gonzalez via knee and punches in the first round at 4:42 in the main event of WSOF 12 on August 9, 2014, in Las Vegas. These early successes showcased Palomino's striking prowess and positioned him as a top contender in the lightweight division.
Earning a shot at the WSOF lightweight championship, Palomino faced undefeated champion Justin Gaethje at WSOF 19 on March 28, 2015, in Phoenix, Arizona. Despite a competitive stand-up battle, Gaethje retained the title via third-round TKO (leg kicks and punches) at 3:57, though the fight earned widespread acclaim for its non-stop action and Palomino's resilient performance. The pair rematched for the title at WSOF 23 on September 18, 2015, also in Phoenix, where Gaethje again prevailed by second-round TKO (punches) at 4:30; this bout was later named Fight of the Year by multiple outlets for highlighting Palomino's peak striking exchanges and durability against elite competition. These title challenges at WSOF 19 and 23 exemplified Palomino's aggressive Muay Thai-based style during his most prominent run in the promotion.
Palomino returned at WSOF 25 on November 20, 2015, in Phoenix, entering an eight-man lightweight tournament to determine the next title challenger. In the quarterfinals, he secured a first-round TKO (punches) victory over Richard Patishnock at 4:55, advancing with his signature knockout power. However, in the semifinals later that night, he fell to Brian Foster by second-round TKO (punches) at 4:19, ending his tournament run.
Palomino's WSOF tenure concluded at WSOF 31 on June 17, 2016, in Las Vegas, with a unanimous decision loss (29-28, 29-28, 29-28) to Sheymon Moraes after three rounds, resulting in an overall 3-4 record in the promotion.

Absolute Championship Berkut and later MMA

In 2017, following his tenure in the World Series of Fighting, Luis Palomino transitioned to the Russian-based Absolute Championship Berkut (ACB) promotion, seeking greater international exposure in Europe. His debut came at ACB 51 on January 13 against Musa Khamanaev, where he secured a second-round TKO victory via punches, marking a strong start in the promotion.
Palomino continued his momentum at ACB 61 in St. Petersburg, Russia, defeating Marcos Vinicius Schmitz by unanimous decision over three rounds, showcasing his striking prowess and grappling defense in a high-profile European event that highlighted his adaptability abroad. However, his ACB run faltered at ACB 67 in Grozny, where he dropped a unanimous decision to Yusuf Raisov in a competitive lightweight bout, followed by another decision loss to Lom-Ali Eskiev at ACB 84 in Bratislava, Slovakia.
The losses extended into 2019, with Palomino suffering a unanimous decision defeat to Apti Bimarzaev at ACA 93 in Saint PetersburgACA being the rebranded successor to ACBand a final unanimous decision setback against Claudio Quintana Nadal at Productora LFN's One Fight Night in Chile on May 4, culminating in a four-fight losing skid. At age 38, Palomino retired from MMA after this final bout, closing his professional record at 26 wins and 17 losses, as he shifted focus to the rising bare-knuckle boxing scene where his knockout power could thrive without the cumulative wear of gloved MMA.

Bare-knuckle boxing career

Debut and early BKFC fights

Luis Palomino entered the Bare Knuckle Fighting Championship (BKFC) in 2020, transitioning from a distinguished mixed martial arts career where he amassed 15 knockout victories. His debut took place on February 15, 2020, at BKFC 10 in Hollywood, Florida, against Elvin Brito. Palomino secured a unanimous decision victory after five rounds, with scores of 49-46, 50-45, and 50-45, earning the Fight of the Night bonus for the intense striking exchange.
In his second bout, Palomino captured the inaugural BKFC lightweight championship on July 24, 2020, at BKFC 11 against Isaac Vallie-Flagg. He ended the fight early with a first-round knockout at 0:45 via punches, showcasing his precision striking honed from years of gloved combat. Palomino's adaptation to bare-knuckle rules emphasized rigorous hand conditioning through rice bucket drills and knuckle push-ups, allowing him to maintain striking accuracy without gloves while minimizing injury risk.
Palomino extended his undefeated streak through 2023, compiling a 9-0 record in BKFC with notable stoppages that highlighted his veteran striker status. At BKFC 34 on December 3, 2022, he defended his lightweight title against Tom Shoaff with a fourth-round TKO at 0:01, overwhelming his opponent with volume punching. Similarly, in a 2023 lightweight title defense at BKFC 45 on June 23 against James Lilley, Palomino dominated en route to a unanimous decision victory (50-45 across all cards), solidifying his position as a top lightweight contender and drawing significant hype for his seamless blend of MMA footwork and bare-knuckle power.

Title achievements and defenses

Luis Palomino solidified his status as a dominant force in bare-knuckle boxing by becoming the first double champion in BKFC history during 2022, holding both the lightweight and welterweight titles simultaneously. He captured the welterweight championship on June 24, 2022, at BKFC 26 in Hollywood, Florida, defeating defending champion Elvin Brito by unanimous decision after five rounds of high-volume striking and effective counterpunching that neutralized Brito's aggressive pressure. This victory marked Palomino's first title in the division and extended his undefeated BKFC record to 7-0, showcasing his adaptability across weight classes with superior footwork and precision.
Palomino's reign as lightweight champion, which he had won in July 2020, continued with multiple successful defenses during this period, demonstrating his defensive prowess and relentless pace. In February 2022, at KnuckleMania 2, he defended the lightweight title against Martin Brown by unanimous decision, outstriking the challenger 187-121 over five rounds with a focus on body shots and clinch control to maintain distance and avoid Brown's power. Later that year, at BKFC 34 on December 3, 2022, Palomino made his fifth lightweight title defense against Tom Shoaff, securing a TKO victory via doctor stoppage due to an eye injury in the fourth round after overwhelming Shoaff with combinations and leg kicks that disrupted his balance.
Entering 2023, Palomino maintained his double-champion status with continued dominance, defending the lightweight title at BKFC 45 on June 23, 2023, against James Lilley by unanimous decision (50-45 on all cards). The fight highlighted Palomino's strategic use of feints and counterstrikes to counter Lilley's volume, landing 212 significant strikes to Lilley's 156 while avoiding takedown attempts. contributing to a total of eight world title victories across both divisions by early 2024. Palomino's undefeated streak reached 9-0 in BKFC, built on his MMA background that emphasized knockout power and endurance, allowing him to outlast opponents in grueling exchanges.
Throughout these achievements, Palomino's fight IQ and physical conditioning were key to his success, as he averaged over 40 significant strikes per round in title bouts while minimizing damage taken, establishing him as the promotion's pound-for-pound king prior to 2024. His early BKFC wins paved the way for this championship phase, where he balanced dual-division responsibilities without relinquishing either belt.

20242025 developments

In February 2024, Palomino suffered his first defeat in bare-knuckle boxing, losing the vacant BKFC welterweight title to Austin Trout by unanimous decision at BKFC 57 in Hollywood, Florida, ending his undefeated streak after nine consecutive victories. The five-round bout saw Trout outland Palomino significantly, landing 99 of 155 punches compared to Palomino's 46 of 162, showcasing Trout's superior boxing precision in a matchup that highlighted Palomino's prior dominance as a two-division champion.
Following the setback, Palomino made a strong return on March 27, 2025, defeating Howard Davis by unanimous decision (49-46, 49-46, 48-47) at BKFC 70 in Hollywood, Florida, to claim the inaugural BKFC King of the Streets Championship. This victory, against a taller and younger opponent with an 8-2 record entering the fight, demonstrated Palomino's enduring striking power and tactical adaptability in an action-packed contest that solidified his status as a resilient veteran.
By mid-2025, Palomino's bare-knuckle record stood at 10-1, reflecting his ability to rebound at age 44 after more than a year away from competition. His quick adaptation to the new championship format underscored a career marked by high-impact knockouts and decisions, even as he navigated the physical demands of the sport later in his career.
Palomino is scheduled for a highly anticipated rematch with Trout on December 5, 2025, at BKFC 85 in Hollywood, Florida, where a win could avenge his only loss and further cement his legacy as one of BKFC's most accomplished fighters across multiple divisions. This bout carries significant implications, potentially positioning Palomino for another title opportunity and highlighting his perseverance in a promotion he helped define through prior defenses.

His most recent fight took place on April 25, 2025, against Jesus Paramo Flores at Boxeo de Gala 21 in the same location, resulting in another technical knockout defeat in the second round at 2:11.

Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu

Luis Palomino began training in Brazilian jiu-jitsu in 2008, integrating the grappling art into his mixed martial arts regimen to develop a more well-rounded skill set alongside his established striking background. Initially guided by coaches at the MMA Masters gym in Miami, Florida, including jiu-jitsu instructor Daniel Valverde, Palomino's early training focused on foundational techniques to complement his aggressive stand-up style. This affiliation with MMA Masters provided a structured environment where he honed ground control and positional awareness, essential for transitioning seamlessly between striking exchanges and grappling scenarios in MMA bouts.
After approximately seven years of consistent practice, Palomino achieved black belt status under Valverde's instruction by 2015, marking a significant milestone in his grappling progression. While Palomino has not competed extensively in Brazilian jiu-jitsu tournaments and lacks major accolades in the discipline, his proficiency is evident in MMA applications, where he has secured at least one submission victory, demonstrating effective use of chokes and joint locks to finish opponents. The art's emphasis on leverage and technique helped balance his striking-heavy approach, allowing him to defend takedowns and capitalize on ground opportunities without relying solely on power.
For Palomino, Brazilian jiu-jitsu held deep personal value beyond technical gains, instilling discipline and mental resilience that shaped his overall martial arts foundation and steered him away from a troubled youth toward structured achievement. The rigorous training regimen fostered patience and strategic thinking, qualities that enhanced his focus during high-stakes competitions and contributed to his longevity in combat sports.

Championships and accomplishments

Mixed martial arts

Luis Palomino achieved significant success in mixed martial arts by capturing one major lightweight championship in the Championship Fighting Alliance (CFA), demonstrating his prowess as a striker in the division.
In 2012, Palomino won the inaugural CFA Lightweight Championship by defeating James Warfield via unanimous decision at CFA 6 on April 13 in Coral Gables, Florida. He successfully defended the title once against Gesias Cavalcante, securing a third-round knockout victory at CFA 7 on June 30, 2012. Palomino lost the belt in his next defense to Luiz Firmino by unanimous decision at CFA 9 on January 19, 2013.
In the World Series of Fighting (WSOF), Palomino earned a lightweight title shot with a first-round knockout of Lewis Gonzalez (knee and punches) at WSOF 12 on August 9, 2014. He challenged for the WSOF Lightweight Championship but lost to champion Justin Gaethje via third-round TKO at WSOF 19 on March 28, 2015, in a bout widely regarded as one of the best fights of the year.
These accomplishments highlight Palomino's multi-promotion success, with the CFA championship underscoring his competitive impact in regional and mid-tier MMA organizations during the early 2010s. No additional official bonuses such as Fight of the Night or Performance of the Night were awarded in his bouts, though his war with Gaethje earned widespread acclaim as a 2015 Fight of the Year candidate.

Bare-knuckle boxing

Luis Palomino established himself as one of the most dominant figures in bare-knuckle boxing through his tenure with the Bare Knuckle Fighting Championship (BKFC), amassing a record of eight world championships across multiple divisions. He became the organization's first double champion in history by simultaneously holding the lightweight and welterweight titles in 2022.
Palomino captured the inaugural BKFC lightweight championship on July 24, 2020, defeating Isaac Vallie-Flagg by first-round knockout at BKFC 11. He went on to make six successful defenses of the title, a BKFC record for consecutive defenses, against opponents including Jim Alers, Tyler Goodjohn, Dat Nguyen, Martin Brown, Tom Shoaff, and James Lilley between November 2020 and June 2023. Palomino vacated the lightweight belt in January 2024 to concentrate on higher weight classes.
On June 24, 2022, at BKFC 26, Palomino won the BKFC welterweight championship via unanimous decision over Elvin Brito, solidifying his status as a double champion. He lost the welterweight title on February 2, 2024, to Austin Trout by unanimous decision at BKFC 57.
In 2025, Palomino added to his accolades by winning the inaugural BKFC King of the Streets championship on March 27 at BKFC 70, defeating Howard Davis by unanimous decision in a five-round bout. This victory earned him Fight of the Night honors.
