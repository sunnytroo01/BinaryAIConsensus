Nosebleed

1x
 A nosebleed, medically termed epistaxis, is the hemorrhage from the blood vessels of the nasal mucosa, most commonly originating from the highly vascular anterior nasal septum known as Kiesselbach's plexus. These bleeds are typically anterior (90% of cases) and self-limiting, but posterior epistaxis, arising from larger vessels in the posterior nasal cavity, can be more severe and life-threatening. Epistaxis affects about 60% of people during their lifetime, with only around 10% requiring medical attention, and is most prevalent in children aged 2-10 and adults over 50.
The condition arises from a rupture of superficial nasal blood vessels, often due to local trauma such as nose picking (particularly common in children, where avoiding it helps prevent recurrence), dry air, or upper respiratory infections, which irritate and weaken the fragile nasal lining containing numerous delicate capillaries close to the surface. In young adults, including 19-year-old females, common causes are similar and include dry air irritating nasal blood vessels, nose picking, forceful nose blowing, allergies, sinus infections, minor injury, and irritants. Systemic factors like hypertension, anticoagulant medications (e.g., warfarin or aspirin), bleeding disorders (e.g., hemophilia or von WilleBrand disease, which can present with recurrent nosebleeds in teenage and young adult females), and environmental irritants such as chemical exposure or cocaine use can exacerbate or trigger episodes. Less common etiologies include nasal tumors, polyps, or hereditary conditions like hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia, while allergies, deviated septum, and overuse of nasal decongestants contribute to recurrent cases.
Symptoms usually involve unilateral bleeding from one nostril, though bilateral flow can occur if blood drains posteriorly into the throat, potentially causing swallowing of blood. Swallowing small amounts of blood from a minor nosebleed is generally harmless and poses no significant health risks, as stomach acid breaks down the blood, which passes through the digestive system. Possible mild effects include temporary stomach irritation or nausea; larger amounts may cause black stools (melena) or vomiting. In severe instances, difficulty breathing may occur. Most episodes are minor and resolve spontaneously or with simple first-aid measures: stay calm, sit upright and lean slightly forward, spit out any blood in the mouth and breathe through the mouth, then pinch the soft lower part of the nose (nasal wings) with the thumb and index finger for 10-15 minutes without releasing the pressure. This position prevents blood from flowing down the throat. Do not lie down, pack the nose with tissue, or apply ice to the head or forehead.
Management escalates based on severity: topical vasoconstrictors like oxymetazoline, chemical or electrical cautery for visible vessels, or nasal packing for persistent bleeding; posterior bleeds may necessitate hospitalization, embolization, or surgical ligation. Prevention strategies include humidifying indoor air, applying petroleum jelly to nasal passages, avoiding nasal irritants, and managing underlying conditions like hypertension or coagulopathies. Individuals should seek immediate medical care for nosebleeds that do not stop after 10-15 minutes of appropriate first aid, occurring after trauma, or in those on blood thinners, as these may signal significant blood loss or underlying pathology. Nosebleeds recurring more than once a week should prompt medical evaluation to identify the underlying cause, even if the bleeding stops easily; particularly in teenage and young adult females, recurrent episodes may indicate a bleeding disorder such as von Willebrand disease.

Signs and symptoms

Presentation

A nosebleed, or epistaxis, typically presents with a sudden onset of bleeding from one or both nostrils, often manifesting as bright red blood due to its arterial origin in the anterior nasal cavity. In most cases, the bleeding is unilateral and visible externally, though posterior bleeds may primarily drain into the nasopharynx, leading to blood appearing in the mouth or throat.
Associated symptoms include postnasal drip, where blood flows backward into the throat, potentially causing a metallic or bad taste in the mouth from swallowed blood. Swallowing small amounts of blood is generally harmless, as stomach acid breaks down the blood, which passes through the digestive system. Larger amounts may cause stomach irritation, nausea, vomiting, or black, tarry stools (melena). Severe episodes may involve difficulty breathing or swallowing if blood accumulates in the airway, along with mild irritation or discomfort at the bleeding site.
The volume and duration of bleeding range from minor spotting that resolves in seconds to profuse hemorrhage lasting minutes to hours, with anterior bleeds generally milder and posterior ones more voluminous. Patients should seek emergency care for heavy bleeding that soaks through tissues within minutes, persists beyond 20 minutes despite pressure, or causes vomiting, dizziness, or respiratory distress from blood ingestion.

Classification

Nosebleeds, or epistaxis, are primarily classified by their anatomical location within the nasal cavity, with anterior epistaxis accounting for approximately 90% of cases and originating from the Kiesselbach's plexus on the anterior nasal septum. This type is typically unilateral, presents with steady bleeding that is visible in the anterior nares, and is easier to control through simple measures such as pinching the nostrils or local cautery. In contrast, posterior epistaxis comprises about 10% of cases and arises from the Woodruff's plexus in the posterior nasal cavity, often involving branches of the sphenopalatine artery. Posterior bleeds are generally more severe, frequently bilateral due to blood draining forward from the back of the nose, and harder to manage, often requiring hospitalization and advanced interventions like nasal packing or embolization.
The severity of epistaxis varies, with most cases being minor and self-limited, while severe cases may involve significant blood loss leading to hemodynamic instability, hospitalization, and potentially requiring transfusion. This assessment helps guide urgency of care, as severe epistaxis can lead to complications like anemia or airway compromise, particularly in vulnerable populations such as the elderly or those with comorbidities.
Epistaxis can also be categorized by recurrence patterns, distinguishing primary epistaxis as idiopathic and recurrent without an identifiable underlying cause, often frequent and self-perpetuating due to local vascular fragility, from secondary epistaxis, which is linked to specific etiologies such as trauma, medications, or systemic diseases and may present as single or recurrent episodes depending on the trigger. This differentiation is crucial for diagnostic workup, as recurrent primary cases may warrant preventive measures, while secondary cases require addressing the root cause.
Special types of epistaxis include those associated with hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia (HHT), a genetic disorder characterized by abnormal blood vessel formation leading to recurrent, often severe nosebleeds in over 90% of affected individuals due to telangiectasias in the nasal mucosa. Iatrogenic epistaxis, another distinct category, occurs as a complication following nasal or sinus procedures, such as functional endoscopic sinus surgery or septoplasty, resulting from vascular injury or pseudoaneurysm formation. These subtypes highlight the need for tailored management strategies beyond standard classifications.

Causes and risk factors

Frequent nosebleeds, particularly those occurring more than once a week, are commonly caused by local factors such as dry air irritating the nasal membranes, nose picking, allergies, colds or sinus infections, and overuse of nasal sprays, as well as systemic factors including blood-thinning medications like aspirin or anticoagulants. Less commonly, they may result from high blood pressure, bleeding disorders, or hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia. Nosebleeds more than once a week typically warrant medical evaluation to identify the underlying cause.

Local causes

Local causes of nosebleed, or epistaxis, primarily involve direct insults to the nasal mucosa and underlying vasculature, often leading to rupture of superficial vessels in Kiesselbach's plexus or other anterior nasal sites. These factors are distinct from systemic conditions and typically result in anterior epistaxis, which accounts for the majority of cases. Common local triggers include mechanical trauma, environmental irritants, structural nasal abnormalities, infections, iatrogenic interventions, and certain habitual behaviors.
Trauma represents one of the most frequent local precipitants of epistaxis, encompassing both intentional and accidental injuries to the nasal lining. Nose picking, also known as epistaxis digitorum, causes mucosal ulceration and vessel damage through repeated digital manipulation, particularly in children and young adults. Facial injuries from blows, falls, or sports can fracture nasal bones or lacerate mucosa, resulting in immediate or delayed bleeding. In pediatric populations, foreign bodies such as toys or food particles inserted into the nostril may erode the mucosa over time, provoking inflammation and hemorrhage.
Environmental factors contribute by compromising the integrity of the nasal mucosa, often through desiccation or irritation. Low humidity, prevalent in arid climates, heated indoor environments, or during winter months, dries the nasal lining, leading to crusting, cracking, and subsequent vessel exposure and rupture. In particular, cold, dry airsuch as in unheated cold rooms at nightcan exacerbate this desiccation, promoting spontaneous epistaxis. Mild morning nosebleeds are especially common during a common cold, caused by the combination of dry air drying and cracking nasal membranes overnight (worsened by heated indoor air), nasal inflammation from the infection, irritation from sneezing and coughing, and forceful nose-blowing, leading to fragile blood vessels bleeding easily upon waking. Although most dry air-related bleeds are anterior, when bleeding originates from posterior nasal sites, blood may drain backward into the pharynx and mouth rather than exiting the nostrils, presenting as bleeding from the mouth or a sensation of blood dripping into the throat, often noticed at night or upon waking. Allergens and irritants, including dust, chemical fumes, or pollutants, can inflame the mucosa in susceptible individuals, exacerbating dryness and fragility.
Structural and pathological nasal conditions predispose individuals to recurrent epistaxis by altering airflow, promoting chronic irritation, or directly invading vessels. Septal deviations or spurs disrupt normal nasal airflow, causing localized dryness and mucosal trauma on the affected side. Nasal polyps, which are benign growths arising from chronic inflammation, can obstruct passages and lead to bleeding upon manipulation or erosion. Tumors, whether benign (e.g., angiofibromas) or malignant (e.g., squamous cell carcinomas), may cause epistaxis through vascular invasion or tissue friability, often presenting with unilateral symptoms. Infections such as acute or chronic sinusitis and rhinitis (bacterial, viral, or allergic) inflame the mucosa, increasing vascular permeability and bleeding risk, particularly during episodes of sneezing or forceful nose blowing.
Iatrogenic causes arise from medical procedures or therapies that inadvertently damage nasal structures. Postoperative bleeding following rhinoplasty or other nasal surgeries occurs due to mucosal lacerations or vessel transection, with risks heightened in the immediate recovery period. Nasotracheal intubation, often used in anesthesia or critical care, can traumatize the nasal septum and mucosa through mechanical friction. Overuse of topical nasal sprays, including decongestants or corticosteroids, may cause rebound congestion, mucosal atrophy, or irritation, predisposing to epistaxis.
Certain habitual behaviors directly erode nasal tissues, leading to chronic or severe epistaxis. Intranasal cocaine use, common in adolescents and adults, causes vasoconstriction followed by mucosal ischemia, ulceration, and septal perforation, resulting in persistent bleeding sites.

Systemic causes

Systemic causes of nosebleed, or epistaxis, encompass conditions and factors that affect coagulation, vascular integrity, or overall hemostasis across the body, increasing susceptibility to bleeding episodes from nasal vessels. These differ from local nasal factors by involving multifocal physiological disruptions, such as impaired clotting mechanisms or elevated vascular pressures that can precipitate or exacerbate epistaxis.
Coagulation disorders represent a primary category of systemic contributors to epistaxis. Hemophilia A and B, hereditary deficiencies in factor VIII and IX respectively, lead to prolonged bleeding after minor trauma, with epistaxis being a frequent manifestation due to deficient clot formation. Von Willebrand disease, the most common inherited bleeding disorder, impairs platelet adhesion and stabilizes factor VIII, often presenting with recurrent epistaxis alongside mucosal bleeding, particularly in teenage and young adult females. Thrombocytopenia, characterized by low platelet counts, disrupts primary hemostasis and heightens epistaxis risk, particularly when counts fall below 50,000/L, as platelets are essential for initial vessel constriction and plug formation. Recurrent nosebleeds in young adult females may warrant evaluation for bleeding disorders such as von Willebrand disease.
Vascular diseases further predispose individuals to epistaxis through altered blood flow dynamics or structural weaknesses. Hypertension elevates intranasal vascular pressure, straining fragile anterior septal vessels and accounting for a significant portion of adult epistaxis cases, though its role may be more associative than directly causative in all instances. Hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia (HHT), an autosomal dominant disorder, causes abnormal arteriovenous malformations and telangiectasias, with recurrent epistaxis emerging as the hallmark symptom in nearly all affected individuals by age 40 due to fragile nasal mucosa vessels.
Medications that interfere with hemostasis substantially elevate epistaxis risk. Anticoagulants such as warfarin inhibit vitamin K-dependent clotting factors, prolonging bleeding time and increasing epistaxis incidence, particularly when combined with other risk factors. Antiplatelet agents like aspirin and clopidogrel, along with nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), impair platelet aggregation, leading to higher rates of mucosal bleeding including epistaxis; for instance, aspirin use is linked to a twofold increase in severe cases requiring intervention.
Systemic illnesses compromising coagulation pathways also contribute. Liver disease reduces synthesis of clotting factors II, VII, IX, and X, alongside thrombocytopenia from splenic sequestration, resulting in epistaxis as part of a broader bleeding diathesis. Renal failure induces uremia, which causes platelet dysfunction through toxin accumulation and anemia, predisposing to epistaxis via impaired primary hemostasis. Vitamin K deficiency, often secondary to malabsorption or antibiotic use, depletes factors II, VII, IX, and X, manifesting as epistaxis in both adults and neonates due to resultant coagulopathy.
Additional factors include physiological states and habits affecting hemostasis. Pregnancy increases blood volume by 40-50% and elevates estrogen levels, promoting nasal mucosa vascularity and congestion, which heightens epistaxis frequency, especially in the third trimester. Alcohol abuse impairs liver function, leading to coagulopathy and thrombocytopenia, while acute intoxication may vasodilate nasal vessels, both contributing to recurrent epistaxis.

Pathophysiology

Vascular anatomy

The nasal vasculature derives its arterial supply from branches of both the internal and external carotid arteries, creating a rich network that supports the organ's functions but also predisposes it to bleeding. The internal carotid contributes via the ophthalmic artery, which gives rise to the anterior and posterior ethmoidal arteries; these supply the superior septum and lateral walls. The external carotid provides the majority of flow through its maxillary artery branchesthe sphenopalatine artery (supplying the posterior septum and lateral walls), greater palatine artery (anterior septum and floor), and ascending pharyngeal artery (posterior wall)as well as the superior labial artery from the facial artery (anterior septum and vestibule).
Anterior bleeding typically arises from Kiesselbach's plexus, also known as Little's area, an anastomotic network on the anteroinferior septum formed by the convergence of the anterior ethmoidal, sphenopalatine, greater palatine, and superior labial arteries. This region accounts for approximately 90% of epistaxis cases and exemplifies the watershed-like vulnerability where terminal branches meet. Posterior bleeding often originates from the area traditionally known as Woodruff's plexus, located along the posterior lateral nasal wall near the inferior turbinate; while commonly described as comprising branches of the sphenopalatine and ascending pharyngeal arteries, recent anatomical studies suggest it may be primarily a venous plexus.
Venous drainage parallels the arterial supply, with interconnecting pathways that include the facial vein for anterior structures, the pterygoid plexus for lateral walls via maxillary veins, and ophthalmic veins for superior ethmoidal regions; these ultimately converge into the internal jugular vein. The valveless nature of these veins and their connections to extracranial and intracranial plexuses, such as the cavernous sinus, render the system susceptible to pressure fluctuations that can exacerbate bleeding.
The nasal mucosa consists of a thin, pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium overlying a vascular submucosa, where capillaries and small vessels lie in close proximity to the surface, facilitating air humidification but increasing susceptibility to erosion and rupture.
Extensive anastomoses between internal and external carotid-derived arteries, particularly within Kiesselbach's and Woodruff's plexuses, form a highly interconnected network that amplifies bleeding potential by allowing retrograde flow and recruitment of collateral vessels during hemorrhage.
In older adults, age-related vascular changes such as atherosclerosis lead to vessel wall thickening, fragility, and reduced elasticity, particularly in the nasal mucosa, heightening epistaxis risk through impaired hemostasis and easier vessel rupture.

Bleeding mechanisms

Nosebleeds, or epistaxis, often initiate through vessel fragility in the nasal mucosa, particularly where the endothelium is thin and susceptible to damage. Endothelial damage from trauma, such as digital manipulation or forceful nose blowing, or from environmental dryness leading to mucosal cracking, exposes the subendothelium and triggers vessel rupture. This fragility is especially pronounced in areas like Kiesselbach's plexus, where superficial vessels are prone to minor insults. Dryness exacerbates this by reducing mucosal hydration, causing epithelial erosion and subsequent bleeding.
Hemodynamic factors contribute significantly to the onset and persistence of bleeding by imposing mechanical stress on vulnerable vessels. Elevated blood pressure increases hydrostatic pressure within nasal capillaries, promoting vessel wall distension and potential rupture, while also prolonging hemorrhage duration through sustained flow. In hypertensive individuals, this can lead to higher shear stress on endothelial surfaces, further weakening vessel integrity and facilitating epistaxis.
Coagulation failure impairs the hemostatic response, allowing initial bleeds to escalate. Deficiencies in platelet adhesion, often due to medications like aspirin or conditions such as von Willebrand disease, hinder the formation of stable platelet plugs at the injury site. Similarly, disruptions in fibrin formation from anticoagulants like warfarin or inherited coagulopathies such as hemophilia prolong bleeding by preventing effective clot stabilization.
Inflammatory responses in the nasal mucosa can precipitate or worsen epistaxis through localized vascular changes. Infections or allergic conditions, such as rhinitis, induce vasodilation and increased vascular permeability via mediators like histamine, which erode mucosal barriers and expose vessels to rupture. This heightened permeability allows plasma extravasation, further irritating tissues and promoting bleeding episodes.
Recurrent epistaxis can establish vicious cycles that progressively weaken local tissues. Repeated minor bleeds cause cumulative endothelial injury and fibrosis, reducing vascular resilience and increasing susceptibility to future hemorrhages, particularly in conditions like hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia. Over time, this chronic trauma fosters a cycle of inflammation and tissue remodeling that sustains the risk of recurrent bleeding.

Diagnosis

Clinical assessment

Clinical assessment of nosebleed, or epistaxis, begins with a thorough history to characterize the episode and identify potential underlying factors. Healthcare providers inquire about the onset (sudden or gradual), duration, frequency of episodes, and laterality (unilateral or bilateral), as well as any inciting events such as trauma, recent nasal manipulation, or environmental exposures. Associated symptoms are evaluated, including headache, facial pain, dizziness, or vomiting, which may indicate significant blood loss or other complications; a history of easy bruising, prolonged bleeding from other sites, or family history of bleeding disorders is also elicited to screen for coagulopathy. Medication use, particularly anticoagulants, antiplatelet agents, or nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, is documented, along with comorbidities like hypertension or liver disease that could contribute to bleeding risk.
The physical examination starts with assessment of vital signs to evaluate hemodynamic stability, as tachycardia or hypotension may signal substantial blood loss from ongoing or recurrent epistaxis. Mental status and airway patency are checked to rule out immediate threats, particularly in cases of profuse bleeding. Nasal inspection follows, ideally with the patient seated upright and forward-leaning to minimize posterior drainage; a bright light source, nasal speculum, and suction are used to clear clots and visualize the nasal cavity, focusing on the anterior septum and Kiesselbach's plexus where most bleeds originate.
Anterior rhinoscopy, performed with a nasal speculum and headlight, allows direct visualization of the bleeding site in approximately 90% of cases, which are anterior in location and often amenable to targeted intervention. Topical vasoconstrictors like epinephrine may be applied to facilitate examination by reducing blood flow, though care is taken to avoid overuse in patients with cardiovascular risks. If bleeding persists despite initial compression, the exam helps differentiate anterior from posterior sources: anterior bleeds typically present as visible flow from one nostril, while posterior bleeds are characterized by occult drainage into the pharynx, bilateral involvement, or high-volume output that is harder to localize.
Red flags during assessment prompt urgent further evaluation, including signs of anemia such as pallor or fatigue from chronic or heavy blood loss, a personal history of coagulopathy, or symptoms suggestive of neoplasms like unilateral nasal obstruction or recurrent unilateral bleeding, facial swelling, or vision blurring. Unilateral repeated nosebleeds accompanied by facial swelling and vision blurring warrant prompt consultation with an ear-nose-throat (ENT) specialist; expected evaluations include nasal endoscopy to visualize the nasal cavity and nasopharynx, CT or MRI imaging to assess sinuses and surrounding structures, and possible biopsy if abnormalities are found; seek emergency care if symptoms worsen suddenly (e.g., increased bleeding, prominent swelling, rapid vision decline). Airway compromise, hemorrhagic shock, or unrelenting profuse bleeding also warrant immediate stabilization and specialist referral.

Diagnostic tests

When initial clinical assessment fails to identify the source or cause of epistaxis, laboratory tests are employed to evaluate for underlying hematologic abnormalities or the need for transfusion support. A complete blood count (CBC) is recommended to assess for anemia due to blood loss, thrombocytopenia, or leukocytosis suggestive of infection or malignancy. Coagulation studies, including prothrombin time (PT), partial thromboplastin time (PTT), and international normalized ratio (INR), are indicated if a bleeding diathesis is suspected, such as in patients on anticoagulants or with liver disease. For severe or ongoing bleeding, blood typing and cross-matching (type/screen) prepare for potential transfusion.
Imaging modalities are reserved for cases where structural lesions or vascular anomalies are suspected, particularly in recurrent or unilateral epistaxis. Computed tomography (CT) scans of the paranasal sinuses and nasal cavity can detect tumors, vascular malformations, or erosive lesions causing hemorrhage, while magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) provides superior soft tissue detail for neoplasms or extensions into adjacent structures. Nasal endoscopy, using flexible or rigid scopes, is a key diagnostic tool for visualizing posterior bleeding sites not accessible by speculum examination alone, allowing direct identification and potential intervention.
In severe recurrent cases refractory to initial management, angiography identifies vascular sources such as pseudoaneurysms or arteriovenous malformations, guiding embolization procedures. If neoplasm is suspected based on unilateral or persistent symptoms, biopsy of suspicious lesions confirms histology, while allergy testing may be considered in atopic patients with inflammatory nasal mucosa contributing to fragility. For hospitalized patients with significant blood loss, serial monitoring of hemoglobin levels tracks hemodynamic stability and transfusion requirements.

Prevention

General measures

Maintaining nasal moisture is a foundational strategy for preventing nosebleeds, particularly in at-risk populations such as the elderly, who experience higher incidence due to factors like reduced clotting efficiency and comorbidities. Using a humidifier in indoor spaces, particularly in the bedroom at night during dry seasons or heated indoor environments, adds moisture to the air and helps preserve the integrity of nasal mucous membranes. Similarly, regular application of saline nasal sprays or drops, 2-3 times daily, keeps nasal passages hydrated without causing irritation.
Avoiding environmental and behavioral irritants further reduces the risk of mucosal fragility leading to bleeding. Quitting smoking is essential, as tobacco smoke dries and inflames nasal tissues, exacerbating vulnerability to epistaxis. Limiting alcohol consumption helps prevent dehydration and blood thinning effects that can promote nosebleeds. Protecting the nose from dry or cold airsuch as by wearing a scarf in winter or avoiding prolonged exposure to heated indoor environmentsmaintains mucosal health.
Proper nasal hygiene practices support long-term prevention by minimizing trauma to delicate blood vessels. Gentle cleaning with saline solutions avoids harsh disruption, while refraining from forceful nose blowing or picking prevents vessel rupture. This is particularly crucial for children, as habitual nose-picking is a common cause of recurrent nosebleeds in pediatric populations; avoiding nose-picking and trimming fingernails to discourage the habit can significantly reduce the risk of recurrence.
Dietary habits contribute to vascular resilience against nosebleeds. Adequate hydration through consistent intake of water or fluids keeps overall body moisture levels sufficient to support nasal lining integrity. Consuming foods rich in vitamin C, such as citrus fruits and vegetables, promotes collagen synthesis essential for strengthening blood vessel walls, thereby reducing bleeding tendencies.
For seasonal management and in certain high-risk contexts, applying a thin layer of petroleum jelly inside the nostrils with a cotton swab, up to three times daily, acts as a barrier against dryness, particularly effective during winter when low humidity prevails. This practice is also commonly adopted by athletes in contact sports, such as boxing, to moisturize the nasal lining and help prevent nosebleeds caused by dryness, impacts, or friction during training and competition, with boxers and coaches reporting its effectiveness in this regard, consistent with medical recommendations for preventing recurrent nosebleeds due to dryness.
In particular, for mild nosebleeds commonly associated with dry air conditions or upper respiratory infections such as the common coldwhere overnight drying of nasal membranes in heated indoor air, combined with nasal inflammation and irritation from sneezing, coughing, or forceful nose-blowingcan make fragile blood vessels prone to bleeding, often upon wakingthese general preventive measures, including running a humidifier in the bedroom, using saline nasal sprays or drops to moisten the nostrils, applying petroleum jelly or nasal gel inside the nostrils, staying well-hydrated, and avoiding forceful nose-blowing or picking, are usually sufficient to prevent recurrence.
There is no single "best" sleeping position universally recommended by authoritative sources specifically to prevent nosebleeds in children. Primary prevention focuses on keeping nasal passages moist (using a cool-mist humidifier, saline sprays, or petroleum jelly) and avoiding nose picking or dry air. However, for children with recurrent nosebleeds, some pediatric and ENT sources suggest sleeping with the head slightly elevated (e.g., using an extra pillow or raising the head of the bed) to reduce venous pressure in the nasal blood vessels and potentially decrease the frequency of nocturnal nosebleeds. Use caution with pillows in young children to avoid suffocation risks; a wedge pillow or bed elevation is preferable.

Targeted interventions

For individuals with recurrent anterior nosebleeds, prophylactic cauterization targets the specific bleeding site to prevent future episodes. Chemical cauterization using silver nitrate is a common outpatient procedure that seals fragile vessels in Kiesselbach's plexus, reducing recurrence rates in suitable cases. Electrical cautery offers a precise alternative, particularly when chemical methods are insufficient, and both approaches are effective for localized anterior epistaxis without underlying structural defects. These interventions are typically reserved for patients experiencing frequent bleeds despite general measures like humidification.
Medications play a role in preventing nosebleeds among those with predisposing factors, such as coagulopathies or vascular fragility. Antifibrinolytics, such as tranexamic acid, are particularly beneficial for patients with conditions like hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia or excessive fibrinolysis, where they stabilize clots and reduce recurrent bleeding episodes by inhibiting plasminogen activation.
Addressing underlying comorbidities is essential for targeted prevention in high-risk groups. Effective blood pressure control through antihypertensive therapy lowers the risk and severity of epistaxis in hypertensive patients, as uncontrolled hypertension exacerbates vessel rupture. Correcting coagulopathies, such as vitamin K deficiency, involves supplementation to normalize clotting factors and prevent spontaneous bleeds; for instance, intravenous or oral vitamin K rapidly restores coagulation in deficient states.
Structural interventions and supportive devices target anatomical issues contributing to recurrent nosebleeds. Septal surgery, such as septoplasty, corrects deviated septa that promote turbulent airflow and mucosal drying, thereby reducing epistaxis frequency in affected individuals. Nasal tampons or absorbent devices may be used for on-field management of epistaxis in sports settings, particularly for anterior bleeds from injuries.
Vaccinations against respiratory infections help prevent secondary causes of nosebleeds. Annual influenza vaccination reduces the incidence of flu-related sinusitis, which can inflame nasal mucosa and trigger epistaxis, particularly in vulnerable populations.

Treatment

Initial management

The initial management of a nosebleed, also known as epistaxis, focuses on simple, non-invasive first-aid measures to control bleeding and prevent complications such as aspiration or excessive blood loss. The patient should be positioned sitting upright with the head tilted slightly forward to minimize blood flow into the throat and reduce the risk of nausea or airway obstruction. This forward lean helps drain blood out of the nostrils rather than allowing it to pool posteriorly.
Direct pressure is the cornerstone of initial control, achieved by using the thumb and index finger to pinch the soft, lower portion of the nose (the nostrils) firmly against the septum for 10 to 20 minutes without interruption while breathing through the mouth. Releasing pressure prematurely to check for cessation can prolong bleeding, so continuous application is essential. Improvised nasal tampons (e.g., tissue or cotton wool) are not recommended for packing the nose due to risks of infection, irritation, and other complications; reliable sources explicitly advise against inserting such materials into the nostrils for first aid. Temporary use of a soaked cotton ball or similar material for applying pressure should be limited to 10-20 minutes. Adjunctive application of a cold compress to the bridge of the nose can promote vasoconstriction and aid in hemostasis. Patients should avoid tilting the head backward, as this directs blood toward the throat and increases aspiration risk, and should minimize swallowing blood when possible to avoid gastric irritation, nausea, or vomiting; however, small amounts of swallowed blood are generally harmless and are broken down by stomach acid, though larger amounts may cause temporary stomach irritation, nausea, or black stools (melena). Additionally, during an active bleed, aspirin or nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs should not be taken, as they can exacerbate bleeding.
If bleeding persists beyond 20 minutes despite these measures, involves significant blood volume (e.g., more than a cup), or recurs more than once a week, immediate medical evaluation is necessary to rule out underlying causes or require advanced interventions such as professional nasal packing (typically left in place for 24-72 hours or up to 3-5 days under medical supervision). In cases of recurrent epistaxis or those occurring in patients on anticoagulants, even brief episodes warrant prompt professional assessment.

Cauterization and packing

Cauterization and packing represent key interventions for managing moderate epistaxis when initial measures fail, targeting the coagulation of visible bleeding vessels or tamponade of the nasal cavity to achieve hemostasis. These procedures are typically performed by healthcare professionals after identifying the bleeding site, often in the anterior nasal septum via clinical assessment. They are indicated for persistent bleeding not controlled by pressure or topical agents, aiming to minimize blood loss while avoiding more invasive options.
Chemical cauterization involves applying agents directly to the visible bleeding point to induce localized tissue necrosis and vessel sealing. Silver nitrate, commonly in a 75% solution, is applied via a stick or probe for 30 to 60 seconds, promoting protein coagulation and eschar formation effective for anterior septal bleeds. Trichloroacetic acid serves as an alternative, particularly when silver nitrate is unavailable, by denaturing proteins and achieving similar hemostatic effects with application limited to under 10 seconds to prevent excessive tissue damage. Both methods carry risks of septal perforation if overused, but they offer outpatient feasibility with high success rates in uncomplicated cases.
Electrical and thermal cauterization provide precise alternatives for deeper or recurrent sites, using energy to coagulate vessels without widespread chemical spread. Radiofrequency ablation delivers controlled heat via low-energy waves, minimizing surrounding tissue injury and suitable for outpatient settings. Laser therapy, such as Nd:YAG or argon lasers, targets telangiectasias or arterioles with photocoagulation, showing superior long-term efficacy in reducing rebleeding compared to some chemical methods, though it requires specialized equipment. These techniques enhance precision under endoscopic guidance, reducing complications like crusting or infection.
Anterior packing is employed when cauterization alone is insufficient, involving insertion of absorbent materials to apply direct pressure and absorb blood. Materials such as Merocel, a compressed polyvinyl acetate sponge, or layered petroleum gauze are placed bilaterally in the nasal vestibule and septum, often impregnated with vasoconstrictors like oxymetazoline or epinephrine to enhance vessel constriction and hemostasis. This method controls 90% of anterior bleeds but can cause discomfort, mucosal ischemia, or infection if prolonged.
For posterior epistaxis, originating from the nasopharynx or Woodruff's plexus, posterior packing uses balloon catheters to isolate and compress the bleeding area. Devices like Foley catheters or dual-balloon systems (e.g., Epistat) are advanced nasally, with the posterior balloon inflated in the nasopharynx and anterior packing added for support, necessitating hospital monitoring for oxygenation and pressure adjustments. This approach halts severe posterior bleeds but risks complications such as sinusitis or aspiration if mismanaged.
Nasal packing, whether anterior or posterior, is typically left in place for 24-72 hours or up to 3-5 days under medical supervision to allow clot stabilization, with removal timed to balance hemostasis against risks like pressure necrosis or toxic shock syndrome. Premature removal increases rebleeding likelihood, while prolonged duration heightens infection potential, guiding clinical decisions based on bleed severity.

Surgical and advanced therapies

Surgical and advanced therapies are indicated for severe, recurrent, or posterior epistaxis that fails to respond to initial management, such as nasal packing. These procedures are typically performed in an operating room setting and target underlying vascular or structural causes to prevent further hemorrhage.
Endoscopic sphenopalatine artery ligation (ESPAL) involves tying off the sphenopalatine artery, a primary blood supply to the posterior nasal cavity, using an endoscope for direct visualization and minimally invasive access through the nose. This procedure is particularly effective for posterior epistaxis, achieving hemorrhage control in approximately 90-98% of cases, with low recurrence rates when performed by experienced surgeons.
Arterial embolization is a radiological intervention that blocks feeding vessels to the nasal mucosa, such as branches of the internal maxillary or sphenopalatine arteries, using catheter-delivered embolic materials like polyvinyl alcohol particles. It is reserved for patients with high anesthetic risk, failed surgical ligation, or intractable bleeding, offering success rates of 87-93% and serving as an alternative to open surgery.
Septoplasty addresses recurrent epistaxis by surgically correcting a deviated nasal septum that contributes to mucosal trauma, dryness, or vascular fragility leading to bleeds. This procedure reshapes the (/page/S septum) to improve airflow and reduce irritation, and is indicated when structural deformity is identified as a contributing factor in persistent cases unresponsive to conservative measures.
For patients with hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia (HHT), laser therapy targets intranasal telangiectasiasdilated, fragile vessels prone to ruptureusing modalities such as argon, Nd:YAG, diode, or potassium titanyl phosphate (KTP) lasers to photocoagulate lesions and reduce bleeding frequency and severity. These treatments achieve symptom improvement in 70-90% of HHT patients, with blue light and diode lasers noted for minimal surrounding tissue damage compared to traditional cauterization.

Pharmacological options

Pharmacological options play an adjunctive role in managing epistaxis, particularly when initial mechanical measures are insufficient, by promoting vasoconstriction, enhancing clot stability, reversing anticoagulation effects, preventing secondary infections, or alleviating associated pain. These interventions are typically employed in clinical settings for active or recurrent bleeding, with selections guided by the bleed's severity, underlying causes, and patient comorbidities. Guidelines emphasize their use alongside compression or packing rather than as standalone treatments, prioritizing agents with rapid onset and minimal systemic absorption to avoid complications like hypertension or thrombosis.
Topical vasoconstrictors, such as epinephrine, are commonly applied via soaked pledgets or sprays to induce local vessel contraction and facilitate hemostasis in anterior epistaxis. A concentration of 1:1000 epinephrine combined with lidocaine is effective for initial control, achieving bleeding cessation in a significant proportion of cases within minutes, though care must be taken to avoid overuse due to potential cardiovascular effects in susceptible patients. Similarly, topical tranexamic acid (TXA), an antifibrinolytic agent, is sprayed or applied directly to the bleeding site to inhibit fibrinolysis and stabilize clots; randomized trials demonstrate it reduces rebleeding rates at 24 hours and one week compared to placebo or standard packing, with a favorable safety profile for short-term use.
For more severe or refractory epistaxis, systemic antifibrinolytics like intravenous tranexamic acid are administered to systemically inhibit plasminogen activation, thereby preventing premature clot breakdown. A single 1-gram dose has shown efficacy in reducing postoperative nasal bleeding following procedures and in emergency settings for persistent epistaxis, particularly in patients with hyperfibrinolysis or coagulopathy, though evidence is stronger for topical over systemic routes in non-surgical cases.
In patients on anticoagulants experiencing significant epistaxis, reversal agents are considered if bleeding is life-threatening or uncontrolled, balancing thrombotic risks. Protamine sulfate rapidly neutralizes unfractionated heparin, while vitamin K or fresh frozen plasma addresses warfarin-induced coagulopathy by restoring vitamin K-dependent factors; for direct oral anticoagulants, specific antidotes like idarucizumab for dabigatran or andexanet alfa for factor Xa inhibitors are preferred when available, as per consensus pathways. Routine reversal is not recommended for mild bleeds, as continuing anticoagulation often suffices with local measures.
Antibiotics are not routinely indicated for anterior nasal packing in uncomplicated epistaxis, as prophylactic systemic use does not significantly reduce infection rates and may promote resistance; however, they are prescribed if the bleed is infection-related (e.g., due to sinusitis) or for posterior packing, where topical options like mupirocin ointment can mitigate toxic shock syndrome risk.
For HHT-associated epistaxis, pomalidomide, an oral thalidomide analog, has demonstrated significant reduction in bleeding severity in clinical trials as of 2024.
Pain management during or after epistaxis episodes favors acetaminophen, which provides analgesia without impairing platelet function or increasing bleeding risk, at doses up to 4 grams daily in adults. Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), including aspirin and ibuprofen, are contraindicated due to their antiplatelet effects, which can exacerbate hemorrhage.

Complications

Immediate risks

Severe epistaxis can result in substantial hemorrhage, potentially leading to hypovolemic shock if blood loss exceeds significant volumes, such as more than 20% of total blood volume, manifesting as tachycardia, hypotension, and organ hypoperfusion. This risk is heightened in vulnerable populations like the elderly or those with comorbidities, though it remains rare in most cases.
Aspiration of blood into the lungs represents another acute hazard, particularly during posterior bleeds where blood drains posteriorly into the pharynx, potentially causing aspiration pneumonia if inhaled. This complication arises from the forceful flow of blood or dislodged clots, exacerbating respiratory distress and requiring prompt airway management.
Acute anemia may develop rapidly from ongoing blood loss, resulting in a sudden drop in hemoglobin levels that induces symptoms such as fatigue, dizziness, pallor, and shortness of breath. In severe instances, this can necessitate transfusion if hemoglobin falls critically low, underscoring the need for immediate hematologic evaluation.
Infection risks emerge from nasal manipulation during treatment, such as packing or cauterization, which can introduce bacteria leading to sinusitis, rhinosinusitis, or rarely toxic shock syndrome, often mitigated by prophylactic antibiotics. Swallowed blood from epistaxis can cause gastrointestinal irritation. Small amounts are generally harmless and pose no significant health risks, as stomach acid breaks down the blood, allowing it to pass through the digestive system. Larger amounts may lead to temporary stomach irritation, nausea, vomiting, or black, tarry stools (melena).
Posterior epistaxis poses a particular threat of airway compromise, as profuse bleeding into the nasopharynx can obstruct breathing, potentially requiring intubation to secure the airway and prevent asphyxiation. This obstruction risk is amplified if packing migrates or if the patient is unable to clear accumulating blood effectively.

Chronic effects

Frequent or recurrent nosebleeds, often stemming from underlying vascular fragility or environmental factors, can lead to iron deficiency anemia through cumulative blood loss, manifesting as fatigue, pallor, and weakness. In conditions like hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia (HHT), where epistaxis is a hallmark symptom, up to 50% of patients develop anemia due to repeated hemorrhages, necessitating iron supplementation or transfusions to restore hemoglobin levels.
Persistent epistaxis contributes to nasal mucosal damage, including chronic crusting and dryness that perpetuates a cycle of irritation and bleeding. Over time, this can result in septal perforation, particularly if complicated by repeated cauterization or self-induced trauma from crust removal, leading to symptoms such as nasal pain, whistling sounds, and increased infection risk. Additionally, recurrent bleeding may exacerbate sinus issues, including chronic rhinosinusitis, by promoting inflammation and obstruction in the paranasal sinuses.
In individuals with recurrent nosebleeds, psychological effects such as anxiety, social phobia, and depression are common, particularly in chronic conditions like HHT, where unpredictable bleeding episodes foster isolation and emotional distress. These impacts arise from the fear of sudden hemorrhages in social settings, contributing to avoidance behaviors and reduced overall well-being.
Recurrent epistaxis is a frequent comorbidity in cases of uncontrolled hypertension, which can heighten bleeding severity and risk of complications like significant blood loss if unmanaged. Nighttime nosebleeds further impair quality of life by disrupting sleep through sudden awakenings and anxiety, leading to daytime fatigue and diminished daily functioning.

Epidemiology

Incidence and prevalence

Nosebleeds, or epistaxis, are a common occurrence, with an estimated lifetime prevalence of 60% in the general population, meaning most individuals experience at least one episode during their lives. Of those affected, estimates vary, with approximately 6-10% seeking medical attention, highlighting that while frequent, severe cases requiring intervention are relatively uncommon.
The annual incidence of epistaxis leading to emergency department visits is approximately 1.7 per 1,000 population in the United States, reflecting a stable overall rate over recent decades. Incidence exhibits a bimodal age distribution, with peaks among children aged 2 to 10 years and adults over 50 years, particularly those 70 to 79 years old, where rates can reach 12 per 1,000 visits. Seasonally, episodes are more frequent during winter months, attributed to low humidity and dry air that irritate nasal mucosa.
Globally, prevalence varies by climate, with higher rates observed in arid regions such as the Middle East, where low humidity exacerbates nasal dryness and vessel fragility. Overall trends in epistaxis incidence remain stable as of 2025, though emergency department visits for anticoagulant-related bleeding increased during 2016-2020 due to rising use of direct oral anticoagulants.

Demographic patterns

Nosebleeds, or epistaxis, display a bimodal age distribution, with notable peaks in pediatric and geriatric populations. In children, particularly those aged 2 to 10 years, epistaxis is prevalent, affecting up to 30% of children under 5 years and over 50% by age 10, often stemming from trauma-related causes such as nose picking or minor nasal injuries; these cases account for approximately 1 in 200 pediatric emergency department visits. In older adults, incidence rises sharply between 60 and 80 years, driven by age-related vascular fragility, where nasal mucosa and blood vessels become thinner and more susceptible to rupture due to natural degenerative changes.
A slight male predominance exists in nosebleed occurrence, with males comprising about 60-70% of cases across studies, yielding a ratio of roughly 1.5:1 to 2:1; this disparity may relate to greater male involvement in outdoor activities and resultant nasal trauma.
Ethnic variations influence nosebleed rates, with higher incidence observed among Caucasians compared to African-Americans, potentially linked to differences in mucosal dryness and environmental factors like climate. Rates tend to be lower in humid regions, where drier aira key irritant leading to vessel crackingis less prevalent. Hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia (HHT), a genetic cause of recurrent epistaxis, affects all ethnic groups equally overall, though specific mutations show higher prevalence in certain European lineages.
Socioeconomic factors may contribute to treatment disparities in epistaxis management, with studies indicating variations in care access and interventions based on income levels. Urban-rural differences also emerge, with urban dwellers facing elevated rates possibly due to air pollution and denser allergen loads, though some analyses find no significant overall gap.
Comorbidities amplify nosebleed susceptibility, with hypertensive individuals experiencing about 47% higher risk of episodes requiring hospital care, owing to chronic vascular strain. Anticoagulant users face similarly elevated risks, with studies indicating up to 50% or greater increase in bleeding events compared to non-users, due to impaired clotting mechanisms.

History and etymology

Historical context

The management of nosebleeds, or epistaxis, has evolved significantly from rudimentary ancient practices to sophisticated modern interventions, reflecting advancements in anatomical understanding and medical technology. In ancient Egypt around 1600 BCE, treatments documented in medical papyri such as the Edwin Smith Papyrus included the use of linen plugs to tamponade bleeding sites in nasal trauma and raw meat applications to promote clotting, marking early recognition of the need for direct pressure and hemostatic agents in nasal hemorrhage. These methods emphasized physical occlusion over systemic explanations, prioritizing immediate cessation of blood loss in an era without refined surgical tools.
By the classical Greek period, Hippocrates (c. 460370 BCE) advanced these approaches through systematic observation, describing cauterization with hot irons to seal bleeding vessels in the nasal cavity, as detailed in the Hippocratic Corpus. This technique, applied via specialized probes to avoid damaging surrounding tissues, represented a shift toward thermal hemostasis and is credited with being among the first instrument-based interventions for epistaxis. In the medieval era, influenced by Galenic humoral theory, nosebleeds were often interpreted as signs of excess blood or phlegm imbalance, leading to bloodletting practicessuch as venesection or cuppingto restore equilibrium, though this sometimes exacerbated bleeding rather than resolving it.
The 19th century brought anatomical precision to epistaxis management, with the identification of the anterior nasal plexuslater termed Kiesselbach's plexusby Wilhelm Kiesselbach in the 1880s, highlighting the septal region's vascular vulnerability as the source of most bleeds. Concurrently, the introduction of cocaine as a topical anesthetic in 1884 by Carl Koller revolutionized nasal procedures, enabling painless vasoconstriction and packing without the risks of general anesthesia, thus facilitating safer interventions for refractory cases.
In the 20th century, the advent of antibiotics in the 1940s dramatically reduced infection complications associated with nasal packing, a common treatment that previously carried risks of sinusitis or toxic shock syndrome due to bacterial colonization. By the 1970s, transcatheter arterial embolization emerged as a targeted therapy for intractable posterior epistaxis, first reported by Sokoloff et al. in 1974, involving selective occlusion of feeding vessels like the sphenopalatine artery to achieve durable hemostasis with minimal invasiveness.
Modern advancements from the 1980s onward incorporated endoscopic techniques, allowing visualization and precise cauterization or ligation of bleeding sites, such as the sphenopalatine artery, with success rates exceeding 90% and reduced morbidity compared to open surgery. In the 2010s, clinical trials validated topical tranexamic acid as an adjunctive antifibrinolytic agent, demonstrating faster bleeding cessation and lower rebleeding rates within 2472 hours when applied intranasally, particularly in anterior epistaxis unresponsive to initial measures.

Terminology origins

The English term "nosebleed" is a compound word derived from "nose" and "bleed," with its earliest recorded use appearing in Middle English texts from the period 11501500, reflecting a literal description of blood flowing from the nose. Earlier forms in Old English, such as "nosblod," similarly combined elements meaning "nose" and "blood," emphasizing the straightforward anatomical reference in Anglo-Saxon language.
The formal medical term "epistaxis" originates from Ancient Greek, combining "epi" (meaning "upon" or "above") and "stazein" (meaning "to drip" or "to let fall in drops"), thus denoting bleeding from the nose as a dripping from above. This term entered English medical literature in the 18th century, with the first recorded instance in 1779. Although the word draws from classical Greek roots, it was applied in Roman medical writing by Aulus Cornelius Celsus in the 1st century CE, who described nasal hemorrhages in his encyclopedic work De Medicina as part of broader discussions on bleeding and surgical interventions.
Synonyms for nosebleed include the formal "epistaxis" and the colloquial "bloody nose," the latter a simple descriptive phrase common in everyday English since at least the early modern period. Across cultures, naming reflects traditional medical views; in Traditional Chinese Medicine, nosebleeds are termed "b n" (), associated with "lung heat" where excessive yang heat disrupts blood flow and causes upward flaming of qi. In Arabic medical terminology, the condition is known as "ruf" (), literally denoting nasal bleeding, a term used in classical Islamic texts for both description and treatment discussions.
The terminology for nosebleed evolved from superstitious interpretations in folklore, where it was often viewed as an omen of death or divine warningsuch as in legends of Attila the Hun choking on a fatal nosebleed on his wedding night, symbolizing impending doomto a clinical descriptor by the 1800s, as medical advancements shifted focus to anatomical causes like vascular fragility. Up to the Middle Ages, European and Middle Eastern texts treated nosebleeds as a natural purification for internal imbalances, but by the 19th century, terms like "epistaxis" became standardized in otorhinolaryngology, emphasizing etiology over mysticism. This transition marked a broader move from humoral and folk explanations to evidence-based nomenclature in Western medicine.
