 Page is a city in Coconino County, northern Arizona, United States, situated on the Arizona-Utah border along the Colorado River, adjacent to the Glen Canyon Dam and Lake Powell in the Glen Canyon National Recreation Area. Founded in 1957 as a planned housing community for workers constructing the Glen Canyon Dam, it was named after John C. Page, a former Commissioner of the U.S. Bureau of Reclamation, and officially incorporated in 1975. With an estimated population of 7,479 as of 2024, Page serves as a gateway to major natural attractions and supports a tourism-driven economy.
The city's development was directly tied to the construction of the Glen Canyon Dam in the 1950s and 1960s, which created Lake Powell, a reservoir known for its 1,960 miles of shorelinelonger than the combined coastlines of the U.S. West Coast statesand access to 96 scenic canyons, including the renowned Rainbow Bridge National Monument. At an elevation of 4,300 feet, Page lies along U.S. Highway 89, approximately 282 miles north of Phoenix and 277 miles northeast of Las Vegas, making it a central hub for visitors exploring the Colorado Plateau's red rock landscapes. The surrounding area borders the Navajo Nation and features dramatic geological formations shaped by the Colorado River.
Page's economy relies heavily on tourism, recreation, and federal government operations, with key industries including arts, entertainment, food services, and accommodations, which employ about 31.1% of the local workforce, followed by education, healthcare, and social assistance at 23.1%. The city attracts over 5 million visitors annually, drawn to iconic sites such as Horseshoe Benda dramatic meander of the Colorado RiverAntelope Canyon, a slot canyon renowned for its light beams and guided Navajo-led tours, and the Glen Canyon Dam, the second-largest arch dam in the United States. Additional draws include proximity to Vermilion Cliffs National Monument, scenic drives to the Grand Canyon's North Rim, Zion and Bryce Canyon National Parks, and Monument Valley, supporting seasonal activities like boating, fishing, rafting, hiking, and events such as rodeos and balloon festivals on Lake Powell.

History

Founding and Early Settlement

Page, Arizona, was established in 1957 by the U.S. Bureau of Reclamation as a planned housing camp to provide accommodations for workers constructing the Glen Canyon Dam and their families. The site was selected for its proximity to the dam location in northeastern Arizona, approximately 15 miles north of Lees Ferry, on land acquired through an exchange with the Navajo Nation involving 24 square miles. This federal initiative addressed the need for centralized housing amid the project's demands, transforming a remote desert mesa into the foundations of a community.
The town was named after John C. Page, who served as Commissioner of the Bureau of Reclamation from 1937 to 1943 and oversaw significant water infrastructure developments during his tenure. Early infrastructure was rudimentary, featuring temporary metal buildings, house trailers, and about 100 concrete block homes, along with a hospital and basic roads to support incoming residents. These modest facilities highlighted the settlement's provisional nature, with unpaved streets and sandy terrain complicating daily life for the first arrivals, who began moving in around Thanksgiving 1957.
Driven by dam-related employment, Page's population expanded rapidly from nothing in 1957 to approximately 3,000 by 1960, comprising mostly construction workers and their dependents. Initial challenges included limited local amenities, leading residents to depend on nearby areas such as Marble Canyon for essential services and provisions during the camp's formative years. Following the Glen Canyon Dam's completion in 1963, the community transitioned toward permanence as the Bureau of Reclamation continued to administer the site until its incorporation in 1975.

Dam Construction Era

The construction of Glen Canyon Dam, authorized by the Colorado River Storage Project Act on April 11, 1956, marked a pivotal era for the development of Page, Arizona, transforming a remote desert area into a bustling worker community. Groundbreaking occurred on October 15, 1956, with the first dynamite blast, initiating site preparation and the excavation of two massive diversion tunnels to redirect the Colorado River around the construction area; these tunnels, each 3,000 feet long and 45 feet in diameter, were completed between 1957 and 1960. Concrete placement began on June 17, 1960, with the first pour at the dam's toe, and the structure reached its full height of 710 feet on September 13, 1963, when the last concrete was placed. Impoundment of water to form Lake Powell commenced on March 13, 1963, after the diversion tunnels were closed, gradually flooding Glen Canyon over the following years. The dam's power plant began generating electricity on September 4, 1964, with all eight generators operational by 1966, and the project was dedicated on September 22, 1966, by Lady Bird Johnson.
The influx of workers during this decade-long project drove rapid infrastructure development in Page, which was established in 1957 as a government housing camp on Manson Mesa to accommodate construction personnel and their families. At its peak in the early 1960s, the project employed over 2,500 workers directly at the site, with the local population swelling to approximately 7,500 people, including support staff and dependents, necessitating hasty construction of essential facilities. The Bureau of Reclamation built approximately 100 concrete block houses, temporary trailer parks, a modern hospital, basic roads, and utilities such as electricity and water systems to support this boomtown environment. Education infrastructure followed suit, with a temporary school serving 200 students operational by early 1959 and permanent elementary and high school buildings completed in 19591960 on South Navajo Drive to meet the needs of workers' children. These developments were designed for functionality rather than permanence, reflecting the transient nature of the construction workforce.
The dam's construction also imposed significant environmental and social costs, particularly on local Indigenous communities and cultural heritage. The impoundment of Lake Powell submerged vast stretches of Glen Canyon, including hundreds of acres of traditional Navajo grazing lands on Manson Mesa and along the Colorado River, displacing several hundred Din families and disrupting their pastoral lifeways, such as sheep-herding reliant on seasonal watering pockets. This displacement was formalized through the Glen CanyonMcCracken Mesa Land Exchange Act of 1958, which swapped 53,000 acres of Navajo landincluding areas needed for the Page townsite and dam reservoirfor equivalent acreage on McCracken Mesa, often deemed inferior for traditional use, leading to loss of access to sacred sites and ceremonial areas like the Colorado-San Juan River confluence. Additionally, the flooding inundated numerous archaeological sites, including Ancestral Puebloan cliff dwellings, petroglyphs, and more than 2,000 documented locations in the region, such as those in White Canyon and Bears Ears, prompting pre-construction salvage efforts by the National Park Service but resulting in the irreversible loss of irreplaceable cultural artifacts dating back centuries.

Incorporation and Modern Growth

Page was incorporated as a town on March 1, 1975, following a vote by residents to establish self-governance after the completion of the Glen Canyon Dam. The community transitioned to city status shortly thereafter, reflecting its growing permanence and administrative needs as a municipal entity. By the late 20th century, the city's population had stabilized around 7,000 residents, supported by a mix of federal land donations and local development that solidified its role as a regional hub.
The closure of the Navajo Generating Station (NGS) on November 18, 2019, represented a pivotal shift in Page's economic landscape, ending decades of reliance on coal-fired power generation that had employed hundreds and contributed significantly to the local economy. The plant's three iconic 775-foot smokestacks were demolished in a controlled explosion on December 18, 2020, symbolizing the transition from industrial dominance to a tourism-focused economy. This decommissioning accelerated Page's pivot toward leveraging its natural attractions, including the ongoing expansion of tourism since Antelope Canyon's designation as a Navajo Tribal Park in 1997, which opened the site to guided public access and boosted visitor numbers.
In recent years, Page has pursued infrastructure enhancements to foster business growth amid this economic evolution. In September 2024, the U.S. Economic Development Administration awarded the city a $5 million grant to improve downtown infrastructure, including utilities and public spaces, aimed at attracting new enterprises and creating jobs in the tourism and service sectors. This funding, matched by local contributions, underscores efforts to diversify beyond legacy industries while capitalizing on the city's proximity to Lake Powell and surrounding parks. As of 2025, prolonged drought and low water levels in Lake Powell have challenged tourism recovery, contributing to reduced visitor spending and highlighting the need for adaptive economic strategies.

Geography

Location and Physical Features

Page is located in northern Coconino County, Arizona, United States, at coordinates 365504N 1112731W, near the Arizona-Utah border and approximately 130 miles north of Flagstaff. The city occupies a total area of 38.33 square miles, with nearly all of it consisting of land. Situated atop Manson Mesa at an elevation of about 4,300 feet, Page lies within the Colorado Plateau, a vast elevated region characterized by layered sedimentary rock formations, mesas, and canyons.
The surrounding physical features include an arid desert landscape shaped by the plateau's geology, with prominent red rock buttes and expansive vistas. Page overlooks Wahweap Bay and is proximate to key natural landmarks, including Lake Powell roughly 6 miles to the north, the Glen Canyon area with its dam, and slot canyons such as Antelope Canyon located about 5 miles southeast. These elements define the city's distinctive terrain and contribute to its prominence in regional outdoor recreation.

Climate

Page, Arizona, features a cold desert climate classified as BWk under the Kppen-Geiger system, characterized by low precipitation and significant temperature fluctuations between seasons. The area receives an average of 6.4 inches of precipitation annually, with the bulkabout 50%falling during the North American Monsoon from July through September, often in the form of intense thunderstorms that provide brief relief from the dryness. Winters are generally dry, contributing to the overall arid conditions that define the region's environmental challenges.
Temperatures in Page vary widely, with summer highs frequently reaching up to 105F during July and August, driven by intense solar heating and low humidity. In contrast, winter lows can dip to around 20F in December and January, occasionally accompanied by light snowfall, though accumulation is minimal. The annual average temperature stands at approximately 58F, reflecting the transitional nature between scorching summers and chilly winters.
These climatic patterns exacerbate drought cycles in the Colorado River Basin, profoundly affecting Lake Powell, whose water levels have remained critically low from 2023 through 2025 due to prolonged shortages and reduced inflows. Such conditions have led to operational adjustments at Glen Canyon Dam and heightened concerns for water management in the region.

Government

Local Administration

Page, Arizona, operates under a council-manager form of government, where the elected city council serves as the legislative body and appoints a professional city manager to oversee daily operations and implement council policies. The city council comprises seven members: a mayor and six councilors, all elected at-large in nonpartisan elections. Councilors serve staggered four-year terms, with three seats up for election every two years, while the mayor serves a two-year term. This structure emphasizes professional administration, with the city manager responsible for coordinating departments, preparing the budget, and ensuring efficient service delivery to residents.
As of November 2025, Steven Kidman serves as mayor, having been elected in November 2024 to address local priorities such as financial stability and community services. The current council includes Vice Mayor Mike Farrow and councilors Kenna Hettinger, David Auge, Tom Preller, Amanda Hammond, and Debi Roundtree, with terms staggered to provide continuity in governance. In 2025, the council initiated a nationwide search for a new city manager through the executive recruiting firm Duffy Group, narrowing the pool to four finalists by October to fill the role amid ongoing administrative needs.
The municipal government includes key departments such as Economic Development, which promotes business growth and tourism; Public Works, responsible for infrastructure maintenance, water distribution, and sewer services; and Community Development, which handles planning, zoning, and building safety to support sustainable urban expansion. These departments operate under the city manager's oversight, with the overall fiscal year 2023-2024 budget totaling approximately $63 million in revenues for general city operations, funding essential services and capital improvements.

Political Representation

Page, Arizona, falls within Arizona's 1st Congressional District following the 2022 redistricting, which remains in effect as of 2025, and Arizona's 6th Legislative District.
In the United States House of Representatives, the district is represented by Republican David Schweikert, who has served since 2011 and focuses on economic and fiscal policies relevant to northern Arizona's rural communities.
At the state level, Legislative District 6 is represented in the Arizona Senate by Democrat Theresa Hatathlie and in the House of Representatives by Democrats Mae Peshlakai and Myron Tsosie, all of whom emphasize issues pertinent to the region's indigenous populations and natural resources.
Legislators from northern Arizona districts like these frequently advocate for water rights settlements and tribal partnerships, as the area depends heavily on Colorado River allocations amid ongoing drought challenges. For instance, in 2024, Arizona reached agreements resolving long-standing water claims with the Navajo, Hopi, and San Juan Southern Paiute tribes, securing infrastructure funding for delivery systems.
Voter turnout in Coconino County, encompassing Page, was 78.29% during the 2024 general election, higher than the statewide average of 63.6% and indicative of robust participation driven by regional concerns. This engagement aligns with key local priorities such as increased funding for tourism development around Glen Canyon National Recreation Area, environmental safeguards for Lake Powell amid fluctuating water levels, and sustainable management of shared water resources.
The substantial Native American population surrounding Page further shapes policy discussions, particularly on tribal water access and economic development.

Demographics

Population Trends

The population of Page, Arizona, experienced steady growth from its founding in the 1950s, reaching approximately 3,000 residents by 1960 amid the boom associated with Glen Canyon Dam construction. This expansion continued through subsequent decades, reflecting the city's role as a hub for energy and tourism development. By the 2020 U.S. Census, the population had risen to 7,440, marking a more than twofold increase over six decades.
Recent years have seen a slight reversal in this trend, with the population declining to an estimated 7,320 in 2023. This dip is attributed to economic shifts following the 2019 closure of the Navajo Generating Station, a major coal-fired power plant that employed hundreds and supported local commerce. The Arizona Office of Economic Opportunity's 2024 estimate places the population at 7,479 as of July 1, 2024, indicating a modest rebound potentially aided by ongoing migration from the nearby Navajo Nation and recovery in tourism.
Looking ahead, population projections suggest continued stabilization around 7,400 residents through 2025, buoyed by recovery in the tourism sector that draws visitors to nearby attractions like Antelope Canyon and Lake Powell. This outlook accounts for balanced inflows from employment opportunities in recreation and services offsetting prior losses in traditional energy jobs.

Racial and Ethnic Composition

Page, Arizona, exhibits a balanced racial and ethnic composition that highlights its unique position bordering the Navajo Nation. Based on 2023 American Community Survey estimates from the U.S. Census Bureau, the population identifies as 44.55% White (non-Hispanic), 43.67% Native American (predominantly Navajo), 3.08% from other races, and 7.7% multiracial. Hispanic or Latino residents of any race comprise approximately 8.9% of the total, adding further diversity to the community's ethnic profile.
The prominent Native American segment stems directly from Page's adjacency to the Navajo Nation, which encompasses over 27,000 square miles across Arizona, Utah, and New Mexico and serves as home to more than 170,000 Navajo people. This geographic and cultural proximity fosters strong ties, with many residents commuting to or from the reservation for work, family, and community activities. In contrast, the foreign-born population remains minimal at 0.743%, reflecting limited international migration and a largely U.S.-born demographic.
Household structures in Page average 2.6 persons per household, about the same as the national average of 2.5, influenced by multigenerational Native American living arrangements common in the region. The poverty rate stands at 11.2% as of 2023, disproportionately impacting Native American households due to historical economic disparities, limited access to resources, and employment challenges tied to reservation proximity.

Economy

Key Industries

The economy of Page, Arizona, underwent a significant transition following the closure of the Navajo Generating Station, a major coal-fired power plant, in November 2019, which had previously contributed approximately $51 million annually to the local economy through direct and indirect impacts. The plant's shutdown led to substantial revenue losses, including an estimated $40 million in property taxes for Coconino County, prompting a pivot toward tourism, service-oriented sectors, and broader economic diversification to mitigate job losses and sustain growth.
In 2023, Page's largest industries by employment reflected this shift, with accommodation and food services employing 856 workers, underscoring the dominance of tourism-related services that leverage the city's proximity to attractions like Lake Powell. Health care and social assistance emerged as another key sector, supporting 476 jobs and providing essential services to the local population amid the region's remote location. Overall employment reached 3,707 individuals, marking a 15.4% increase from the prior year, driven primarily by these service industries.
Economic indicators in Page highlight steady recovery, with the median household income rising to $88,452 in 2023, an 11.2% growth from 2022. The unemployment rate in Coconino County, where Page is primarily located, stood at approximately 5.0% as of August 2025 (latest available), aligning with broader state trends including an annual average of 3.6% in 2024. To further diversify beyond tourism, the city maintains a 102-acre industrial park with highway access and utilities to attract light manufacturing and businesses, supported by incentives such as no municipal property taxes and low utility rates. In September 2024, the U.S. Department of Commerce awarded a $5 million grant to Page for infrastructure improvements aimed at fostering business expansion and job creation, enhancing long-term economic resilience.

Tourism Sector

Tourism serves as a cornerstone of Page's economy, drawing millions of visitors annually to the surrounding natural attractions and supporting local businesses through expenditures on lodging, rentals, and guided experiences. In 2023, Glen Canyon National Recreation Area, which encompasses much of the region's appeal, recorded 5.2 million visitors who collectively spent $540 million in nearby communities, sustaining approximately 6,300 jobs in the local economy. In 2024, visitation fell to 4.7 million, reflecting ongoing environmental challenges. This influx underscores Page's pivotal role in regional tourism, where activities such as houseboat rentals on Lake Powell and guided tours contribute significantly to visitor engagement and revenue generation.
Antelope Canyon, located just outside Page, exemplifies the area's draw for slot canyon enthusiasts, attracting over 1 million visitors each year through mandatory guided tours that highlight its dramatic sandstone formations. These tours not only bolster Page's service sector but also integrate with broader Glen Canyon visitation patterns, amplifying economic multipliers in hospitality and transportation. Statewide, Arizona's tourism industry generated a record $4.2 billion in tax revenue in 2023, with northern Arizona destinations like Page playing a key part via specialized offerings such as houseboat operations and interpretive excursions.
Despite these strengths, Page's tourism sector faced notable challenges in 2025, including a slowdown attributed to persistently low Lake Powell water levels from ongoing drought conditions and pervasive wildfire smoke that deterred outdoor activities. Visitor spending declined as a result, with smoke from regional fires like the Dragon Bravo Fire reducing air quality and visibility during peak summer months, while drought-limited access to lake-based recreation further strained houseboat and boating concessions. Local employers in park concessions adapted by emphasizing alternative land-based tours, though overall economic impacts highlighted the vulnerability of Page's tourism reliance on environmental stability.

Education

Public School System

The Page Unified School District (PUSD) #8 serves K-12 students in Page, Arizona, operating six schools that include two elementary schools (Desert View Elementary Intermediate and Lake View Elementary Primary), Page Middle School, two high schools (Page High School and Manson Mesa High School), and the alternative Sage & Sand Academy. As of the 2023-2024 school year, the district enrolls approximately 2,370 students across these institutions, with a student-teacher ratio of about 19:1. Page High School, the district's primary comprehensive high school serving grades 9-12, has an enrollment of roughly 812 students as of the 2023-2024 school year.
PUSD emphasizes specialized programs to meet diverse student needs, including STEM initiatives such as courses in graphic communications and information technology offered at Page High School. The district also integrates Navajo language, history, and cultural curriculum throughout K-12 to support its significant Native American student population, funded in part through the Indian Education Office for classes, cultural activities, and guest speakers. Additionally, English as a Second Language services are provided using a pull-out model to aid language acquisition.
Due to Page's location near major tourist attractions like Lake Powell, some students residing in certain sections of the city fall under the boundaries of the adjacent Fredonia-Moccasin Unified School District #6, which serves portions of Page and facilitates attendance options for those families. The district's four-year graduation rate stood at 85% for the class of 2023, reflecting progress from prior years but highlighting ongoing challenges in student retention amid the community's high mobility. This transience, linked to the tourism economy, contributes to attendance and achievement issues, as noted in local planning documents.
As one of the largest employers in Page, PUSD supports the local economy with over 300 staff members dedicated to education and operations.

Culture and Attractions

Cultural Heritage

Page, Arizona, exhibits a rich cultural heritage shaped by its proximity to the Navajo Nation, with over 40% of its residents identifying as American Indian and Alaska Native, predominantly affiliated with the Navajo people. This strong connection fosters ongoing cultural exchange, evident in community events that celebrate Navajo traditions. Annual gatherings, such as the Indigenous Peoples Day celebration held each October, feature traditional dances, storytelling, and artisan demonstrations, highlighting the enduring Navajo influence in daily life and community identity.
Cultural centers in Page serve as vital hubs for preserving and sharing Navajo heritage. Red Heritage, a Navajo-owned venue, hosts regular powwow-style events that blend traditional dining with live cultural performances, reinforcing the community's commitment to intergenerational knowledge transmission.
Historic sites further underscore Page's exploration-era legacy. The John Wesley Powell Memorial Museum, established in 1969, houses exhibits on Major John Wesley Powell's 1869 expedition down the Colorado River, including artifacts, photographs, and maps that document the Anglo exploration of the region's canyons and its impact on Native American lands. Media representations have also contributed to the town's cultural narrative; for instance, the 2001 science fiction film Evolution was filmed almost entirely in Page, utilizing local landscapes and facilities to depict extraterrestrial scenarios, which briefly elevated the area's visibility in popular culture. The local newspaper, the Lake Powell Chronicle, founded in 1965, chronicled these developments until its closure in August 2025, serving as a key historical record of the community's evolving heritage amid its ties to the Navajo Nation and Western frontier history; the shutdown, due to its parent company's collapse, has left Page without a dedicated local news outlet.

Major Tourist Sites

Page, Arizona, is renowned for its stunning natural formations and proximity to iconic landmarks that attract outdoor enthusiasts and photographers from around the world. Among the primary draws are the Antelope Canyon slots, consisting of Upper and Lower sections, both accessible only through guided tours led by Navajo guides on Navajo Nation land. Upper Antelope Canyon features a relatively flat, accessible path through dramatic sandstone formations illuminated by shafts of sunlight, with tours typically lasting 60-90 minutes and departing from Page via off-road vehicles. Lower Antelope Canyon, by contrast, involves a steeper hike with ladders and stairs, offering a more adventurous exploration of narrow, sculpted corridors, and tours generally run about an hour.
Horseshoe Bend, a dramatic meander in the Colorado River carved into red rock cliffs, provides a breathtaking overlook just a short drive south of Page, reachable via a 1.5-mile round-trip trail from the parking area. The site drew approximately 1.4 million visitors in 2023, offering panoramic views from a 1,000-foot-high vantage point and contributing significantly to the local tourism economy through entry fees and related spending.
The Carl Hayden Visitor Center at Glen Canyon Dam serves as an educational hub overlooking the dam and Lake Powell, featuring exhibits on the structure's engineering, the Colorado River's ecology, and the reservoir's role in water management, with free admission and daily operations. Complementing this, Lake Powell offers houseboat rentals from marinas near Page, such as Wahweap and Antelope Point, where vessels ranging from 46 to 77 feet accommodate groups of 6 to 15, allowing self-guided exploration of the lake's 2,000-mile shoreline and remote canyons.
For hikers seeking less crowded slot canyons, Wire Pass provides an accessible entry point to Buckskin Gulch, the longest slot canyon in the world, via a 3-4 mile out-and-back trail starting from a trailhead about 40 miles north of Page on U.S. Highway 89. The route begins with an easy sandy wash before narrowing into sculpted sandstone passages, requiring a permit from the Bureau of Land Management for day hikes or multi-day trips.
To preserve these fragile slot canyons from overuse and erosion, tours in Antelope Canyon and similar sites limit group sizes to 15-20 people, enforced by Navajo Nation regulations, with 2025 fees ranging from approximately $50 to $100 per person, including permits and taxes.

Infrastructure

Transportation Networks

Page's transportation infrastructure centers on road, air, and water networks, supporting its role as a gateway to Lake Powell and surrounding attractions. The primary roadway is U.S. Route 89, a major north-south highway that provides the city's main connection to regional destinations. This route links Page to Flagstaff, Arizonaabout 135 miles southwhere travelers can access Interstate 40, and extends northward to Kanab, Utah, roughly 75 miles away, near the Arizona-Utah border. Page has no direct interstate access, relying on U.S. Route 89 for all overland travel, which experiences annual average daily traffic (AADT) volumes ranging from 5,400 to 10,700 vehicles, with peaks during summer tourism seasons.
To address these seasonal surges, which can reach up to 10,000 daily vehicles at key points along U.S. Route 89, the city installed AI-optimized traffic signals at two major intersections in 2025. These systems, featuring AI-based detection for vehicles and pedestrians, integrate with IoT-enabled cabinets for real-time adjustments, improving flow amid extreme desert conditions like heat, dust, and glare. The upgrades enhance safety and efficiency for the millions of annual visitors traversing the route.
Air travel is facilitated by Page Municipal Airport (PGA), a commercial service facility offering scheduled flights to Phoenix Sky Harbor International Airport (PHX) via Contour Airlines, with up to 12 weekly nonstop flights. While service operates year-round, frequency increases during peak tourism periods to accommodate demand. The airport also supports general aviation, including private charters and scenic tours over Lake Powell and nearby canyons, bolstering tourism access.
Water transport options revolve around Lake Powell, accessed primarily through Wahweap Marina, located just south of Page. This facility provides boat rentals, guided tours, and cruises exploring Glen Canyon, with capacities for houseboats up to 75 feet and smaller watercraft. Additional services include shuttle boats to remote areas and towing for larger vessels, though public ferry operations across the lake are currently suspended as of 2025 due to low water levels. These water-based networks depend on utility-supported maintenance for docks and ramps to ensure reliable access during high-use seasons.

Utilities and Public Services

Page, Arizona, relies on Lake Powell as its primary water source, with water pumped to a local treatment plant that utilizes conventional methods and chlorine disinfection to process up to 2 million gallons per day, serving the city's residential and commercial needs. This system, managed by Page Utility Enterprises, ensures reliable supply amid regional Colorado River challenges, though officials have noted the need for backup infrastructure due to fluctuating lake levels.
Electricity for Page is provided through Page Utility Enterprises, which sources wholesale power primarily from federal hydropower resources marketed by the Western Area Power Administration (WAPA) following the 2019 closure of the nearby Navajo Generating Station coal plant. WAPA handles scheduling, balancing, and ancillary services under a full electric service agreement, drawing from facilities like Glen Canyon Dam to maintain grid reliability for the city's approximately 7,200 residents.
Healthcare services are anchored by Page Hospital, a 25-bed critical access facility operated by Banner Health, offering emergency care, outpatient clinics, laboratory services, and specialized treatments for heart and women's health to the local population of over 7,000 and surrounding northern Arizona communities. The hospital addresses primary and urgent needs in a remote area, supporting both residents and visitors near Lake Powell.
Emergency services are handled by the Page Police Department, which employs 25 sworn officers and 21 civilian staff to enforce laws and respond to incidents within city limits. The Page Fire Department provides fire suppression, emergency medical response, and hazardous materials handling, achieving an average response time of 5 minutes and 18 seconds across its calls, including support for major local infrastructure like the Glen Canyon Dam. These agencies collaborate to ensure public safety in a tourism-dependent region.
