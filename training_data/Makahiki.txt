Makahiki
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Makahiki is the ancient Hawaiian New Year festival, a four-month period of peace, harvest, and celebration dedicated to the god Lono, who embodies fertility, agriculture, and prosperity. It traditionally begins around late October or early November with the rising of the Pleiades star cluster (Makalii) at sunset and concludes in late January or February, aligning with the wet season (Hooilo) for rest and rejuvenation. During this time, warfare and heavy labor were strictly kapu (taboo), allowing communities to focus on tribute collection, feasting, and communal bonding.
The festival's historical significance lies in its role as a seasonal marker for agricultural cycles, where offerings (hookupu) of crops, fish, feathers, and other goods were gathered by priests and chiefs to honor Lono and support the alii (chiefs) and kahuna (priests). Key rituals included the hnaip procession, in which priests circled the islands clockwise for 23 days carrying symbolic poles (akua loa and akua poko) to invoke blessings and collect tributes. Activities emphasized play and physical vitality, featuring sports such as boxing, wrestling, surfing, and knane (a checkers-like game), alongside hula dancing, storytelling, and games like ulu maika (Hawaiian bowling). These elements fostered community pride and reflection, with participants offering the best of their harvests as a gesture of abundance and gratitude.
In ancient Hawaii, Makahiki reinforced social order by prohibiting conflict and promoting renewal, ending with a ceremonial mock battle led by the alii impersonating Lono, followed by sacrifices and the dispatch of offerings toward Kahiki (the mythical homeland). The tradition waned under Western influence in the 19th century but experienced a revival during the Native Hawaiian cultural renaissance of the 1980s, continuing today through community events that strengthen cultural identity, family ties, and ancestral connections. Modern observances often incorporate education on Hawaiian values, aligning with natural rhythms and honoring figures like Ke Alii Pauahi in institutional settings such as Kamehameha Schools.

Overview

Definition and Purpose

Makahiki is the ancient Hawaiian harvest festival and New Year celebration, spanning approximately four months and dedicated to the god Lono, who embodies fertility, agriculture, rainfall, and peace. This period, aligned with the rising of the Makalii constellation in late October or early November, marks a seasonal transition emphasizing abundance and renewal. As a time of peace and rest, Makahiki suspended warfare and heavy labors such as fishing and farming, allowing the land and people to rejuvenate while fostering community bonds through shared activities.
The primary purposes of Makahiki include the collection of harvests and tributes, known as hookupu and auhau, which consisted of agricultural products like taro and sweet potatoes, along with crafted goods such as woven mats and bark cloth, offered to Lono and distributed among chiefs and priests to ensure future prosperity. These offerings symbolized gratitude for the year's bounty and invoked blessings for rainfall and fertile soils, promoting spiritual renewal and agricultural success. Community feasting during this season further reinforced social harmony, providing opportunities for rest, reflection, and the strengthening of familial and societal ties amid the abundance.
Within the Hawaiian kapu systema framework of sacred laws governing daily lifeMakahiki represented a temporary relaxation of many prohibitions, such as those against war and intensive work, while enforcing strict reverence for Lono through dedicated rituals and offerings. This selective suspension allowed for rejuvenation without fully dismantling societal order, highlighting Lono's role as a deity of peace who contrasted with the war god K, thereby balancing spiritual and practical needs.

Cultural Significance

Makahiki served as a critical economic mechanism in traditional Hawaiian society, functioning as the primary period for collecting auhau, or taxes, in the form of goods such as foodstuffs, mats, and feathers from the makainana (commoners) to support the alii (chiefs) and ensure equitable resource distribution across communities. These tributes, often presented as hookupu offerings to Lono, reinforced the alii nui's role as stewards of abundance, preventing shortages and sustaining the chiefly class's authority while promoting communal welfare.
On a social level, the season fostered unity among diverse social strata through inclusive communal activities that encouraged participation from all classes, thereby strengthening social cohesion and collective identity. It also played a vital role in preserving Hawaiian genealogy and oral traditions, as families and communities engaged in storytelling, chants, and shared narratives that transmitted ancestral knowledge and cultural values across generations.
Symbolically, Makahiki embodied the cyclical balance in Hawaiian cosmology between K, the god of war and governance, and Lono, the deity of peace, agriculture, and fertility, marking a temporary shift from conflict to harmony that reflected broader principles of abundance and sustainability. This duality, rooted in Lono's mythological journey across the islands, underscored the interconnectedness of human society with natural rhythms, promoting a worldview where prosperity depended on honoring these divine oppositions.
The season profoundly influenced daily life by imposing kapu (prohibitions) on activities like planting, fishing beyond limits, and construction, allowing the land and people to rest and regenerate in observance of Lono's domain. These restrictions cultivated environmental stewardship, as they aligned human practices with seasonal cycles to maintain soil fertility and marine health, ensuring long-term ecological balance essential to Hawaiian survival.

Historical Origins

Mythological Foundations

The central mythological foundation of Makahiki revolves around Lono-i-ka-makahiki, the aspect of the god Lono embodying the wandering deity who arrives from Kahiki, the ancient Polynesian homeland, to bestow peace, fertility, and agricultural abundance following periods of conflict and toil. In Hawaiian oral traditions, Lono is depicted as traveling across the ocean in a great canoe, signaling the end of the warlike season associated with the god K and ushering in a time of rest and renewal for the land and its people. His return coincides with the heliacal rising of the Pleiades (Makalii) at sunset in late October or early November, marking the onset of the wet season (hooilo) and symbolizing the god's circuit around the islands to ensure prosperity and halt warfare.
This myth positions Makahiki as a commemoration of divine voyages and the gifts of fertility, with Lono's journey paralleling broader Polynesian narratives of gods establishing harmony and sustenance for humanity, including feats attributed to demigods like Mui who fished up the islands and introduced techniques for harvesting the sea's bounty. Lono's role emphasizes themes of rejuvenation, as his presence transforms the landscape through rain and growth, contrasting the destructive cycles of battle under K, the god of war and governance. Meanwhile, Kne, the deity of life, creation, and fresh water, complements Lono by providing the underlying vitality that flourishes during this interlude, highlighting Makahiki as a balanced pause in the annual divine order where peace prevails over strife.
Oral traditions further describe Lono's manifestation during Makahiki through the akua loa, a tall staff image representing the god's elongated form and long journeys, adorned with white kapa cloth, fern fronds, and feathers to evoke his ethereal, cloud-like kino lau (physical embodiments). Carried by priests in processions around the island circuits, the akua loa served as a tangible symbol of Lono's benevolent presence, collecting tributes and invoking his blessings for the coming harvest. These narratives, preserved through chants and stories, underscore the festival's spiritual essence as a direct communion with the god who ensures the land's fertility after the rigors of cultivation and conflict.

Archaeological and Historical Evidence

Archaeological evidence for the Makahiki festival includes heiau temples dedicated to the god Lono, often featuring structures and artifacts associated with agricultural rituals and tribute offerings. On Maui, excavations in the Kahikinui District have uncovered heiau sites with coral offerings dated through uranium-series analysis to approximately AD 15501700, interpreted as remnants of ritualized tribute collection during the Makahiki season. Similarly, Pahua Heiau on Oahu, identified as an agricultural heiau ipu o Lono, contains terraces, retaining walls, and mounds constructed in multiple phases during the pre-contact period, with charcoal samples dated to AD 14851665 and later, alongside coral elements linked to ceremonial use for fertility and harvest prosperity. In the Hlawa Valley of Molokai, pre-contact temple complexes from the 17th18th centuries include agricultural shrines oriented toward Lono, featuring offering platforms that align with Makahiki observances for rain and growth.
Historical accounts preserved in Hawaiian oral traditions and chants provide further evidence of Makahiki's antiquity, tracing its practices to the initial Polynesian settlement of the islands around AD 10001200. The Kumulipo creation chant, a genealogical oral history composed and transmitted by alii families, references Lono of the Makahiki and was recited during the festival season to honor fertility and renewal, embedding the observance within narratives of ancestral voyages and societal origins around the time of intensified settlement in the islands. These traditions, documented through 19th-century transcriptions of pre-contact knowledge, describe Makahiki-like rituals of tribute and peace as integral to early chiefly systems established post-migration.
Evidence also indicates regional variations in Makahiki practices across the Hawaiian Islands, shaped by local environmental and political contexts. Archaeological and ethnohistoric data suggest greater emphasis on surfing competitions and water-based games in Maui's coastal districts, possibly tied to Lono's domain over seas and abundance, while Oahu records highlight elaborate canoe processions for tribute delivery along ahupuaa divisions. Pre-contact alii genealogies, such as those outlining the lineage of 15th-century chief Lloa of Hawaii Island, document the festival's role in unifying districts under ruling chiefs through structured tributes and ceremonies, reinforcing its evolution as a tool of political cohesion.

Timing and Structure

Astronomical Indicators

The Makahiki season in ancient Hawaiian culture was primarily initiated by the heliacal rising of the Makalii constellation, known in Western astronomy as the Pleiades star cluster, which typically occurs in late October or early November as viewed from the Hawaiian Islands. This celestial event, visible just after sunset in the eastern sky, symbolized the arrival of the god Lono and heralded the transition to a period of peace and abundance, aligning with the onset of the rainy season and favorable conditions for harvesting. Priests, or kahuna, meticulously observed this rising to determine the precise timing, ensuring the festival's commencement coincided with environmental cues like the first heavy rains. Timing and phases could vary slightly by island due to local astronomical observations and agricultural needs.
The Makahiki season concluded after approximately four lunar months, reflecting the cyclical nature of Hawaiian cosmology, where the Pleiades' visibility arc guided the seasonal shift from renewal to productivity. Astronomical alignments at sites like stone enclosures on Oahu further supported this timing, with orientations toward the Pleiades' rising points dating to the 16th18th centuries.
Makahiki's timing integrated the stellar observations with the Hawaiian lunar calendar, particularly aligning with the months of Ikuw, Welehu, and Makalii, which span roughly November to January and correspond to the rainy season's peak. The Welehu month, characterized by increased rainfall, thunder, and winds, prepared the land for agriculture by replenishing water sources, while the overall period ensured that harvesting and storage activities benefited from this seasonal moisture. This synchronization of celestial, lunar, and meteorological patterns underscored the practical role of astronomy in sustaining Hawaiian agricultural cycles.
Precise observations of these indicators were conducted from elevated lele platforms at heiau temples, where kahuna used unobstructed views to track the horizon and adjust the lunar calendar as needed to maintain alignment with solar and seasonal changes. These platforms, often positioned at eastern or elevated sites, facilitated accurate sightings of the Pleiades' positions relative to the sun and moon, ensuring the festival's timing supported both spiritual and ecological imperatives.

Duration and Phases

The Makahiki season traditionally spanned approximately four lunar months, from late October or early November to February or March, though the exact timing varied by island and local traditions due to differences in observing celestial markers and agricultural cycles. This period aligned with the onset of the rainy season, providing a structured pause in intensive labor and conflict to honor the god Lono.
Preceding the main season was a preparation period known as Kaaoo, during which communities gathered offerings, stored food, and cleaned altars in anticipation of Lono's arrival.
The season proper was marked by three primary phases. The initiation phase, often called Hoomakaikai, began with purification rituals and the introduction of the Lono image, followed by mobilization for tribute collection, where konohiki (land overseers) organized communities to prepare offerings, and waa auhau (tribute canoes) were dispatched to tour districts, gathering hookupu such as crops, fish, and feathers from each ahupuaa to present before the Lono image as it circuited the island clockwise. This phase emphasized communal organization and the symbolic survey of the land's productivity.
Wke formed the core phase, a dedicated period of heightened observance centered on communal gatherings and feasting, where the accumulated tributes were offered at altars and the full weight of Lono's kapu prohibited warfare and heavy labor, allowing focus on renewal and abundance through sports, games, and celebrations.
The final phase, Hoiuli, encompassed closing rituals to formally dismiss Lono, including the disassembly of the deity's image and the launching of a waa auhau laden with offerings adrift toward Kahiki, symbolizing the god's departure and the resumption of normal societal activities under the domain of K. This transition often involved a final feast and the lifting of the overarching kapu, varying slightly by region to align with local heiau ceremonies.

Traditional Festivities

Processions and Tributes

During the Makahiki festival, a central ceremonial element involved processions featuring the akua loa, a symbolic image of the god Lono constructed as a long staff topped with a carving of Lono and featuring a cross-piece near the top adorned with white kapa, pala fern, and feather lei, carried by priests and attendants clockwise around the island. These processions traversed the boundaries of each ahupuaa (land division), sometimes utilizing canoes for coastal routes to transport the image and participants, allowing communities to present tributes without violating kapu restrictions by gathering at designated coastal or inland sites. The alii (chiefs) or their representatives, such as konohiki, oversaw the route to ensure orderly collection and protocol adherence, with the procession spanning approximately 23 days as it visited districts to survey and gather resources.
The symbolism of these processions embodied Lono's annual circuit, representing his journey to bless the land, renew fertility, and promote agricultural abundance for the coming year, as the image's backward-facing gaze during travel signified reflection on the harvest while invoking prosperity. At each ahupuaa stop, local communities offered hookupu (tributes) to the Lono image, which were tallied and dedicated to the god before redistribution among the alii, priests, and court to sustain the festival and reinforce social bonds.
Types of tributes varied by region but typically included essential crops such as kalo (taro), uala (sweet potatoes), ulu (breadfruit), and maia (bananas), along with awa (kava) for ceremonial use, ia (fish), puaa (pigs), and durable goods like feathers for cloaks, woven mats, and kapa cloth, all gathered as first fruits to honor Lono's domain over peace and productivity. These offerings, presented at lele (altars) or directly to the procession, underscored the festival's role in communal reciprocity, with excess stored for redistribution to ensure equitable support across the island.

Sports, Games, and Celebrations

During the Makahiki season, a variety of traditional sports and games were prominently featured, serving as communal activities that fostered physical prowess, skill, and social unity among all social classes, from commoners to alii (chiefs). These events, often held in large tournaments within ahupuaa (land divisions), drew hundreds or thousands of participants and spectators, emphasizing community pride and the development of strength during this period of peace. Key land-based games included ulu maika, where players rolled heavy stone disks toward a target to test accuracy and control; knane, a strategic board game akin to checkers played with black lava rocks and white coral pieces on a carved stone surface; and hkk, a form of wrestling that built endurance and technique. Other popular contests were ihe (spear throwing for precision), hukihuki (tug-of-war to demonstrate collective strength), and mokomoko (bare-knuckle boxing combined with grappling). These activities were open to everyone, allowing makainana (common people) and alii to compete side by side, with alii often participating to inspire and engage their subjects.
Water-based sports added an element of exhilaration and highlighted the Hawaiians' affinity for the ocean, reinforcing bonds through shared challenges and displays of agility. Contests such as hee nalu (board surfing), kaha nalu (body surfing), au (swimming races), luu (diving competitions), and heihei waa (canoe racing) were common, with participants racing across waves or navigating canoes in coordinated teams to showcase balance, speed, and synchronization. These events not only tested individual and group skills but also celebrated the abundance of the sea, aligning with Makahiki's themes of renewal and harmony.
Feasting and entertainment formed the joyful heart of Makahiki celebrations, promoting inclusivity through shared abundance from seasonal tributes. Lavish communal meals featured taro, fish, pigs, and other foods offered to Lono and distributed among the people, accompanied by performances of hula (dance) and mele (chants) that recounted stories of heritage and nature. These gatherings encouraged merriment and storytelling, strengthening social ties in a setting free from the usual kapu (restrictions).
Competitions often included prizes to motivate participants, such as portions of food, crafted goods, or symbols of honor like featherwork items, though the primary rewards were recognition and communal acclaim for champions who might advance to inter-district challenges. This structure of inclusive rivalry, enabled by the season's cessation of warfare, underscored Makahiki's role in building a resilient and cohesive society.

Religious Rituals

The religious rituals of the Makahiki season centered on honoring Lono, the deity of agriculture, fertility, and peace, through structured ceremonies that sought to ensure prosperity and harmony with the natural world. Central to these practices were the hookupu offerings, where communities presented the first fruits of the harvestsuch as taro, sweet potatoes, fish, and woven craftsat dedicated altars or heiau associated with Lono, often positioned at ahupuaa boundaries. These offerings, gathered by konohiki (land overseers), were placed before the image of Lonoikamakahiki, with kahuna (priests) overseeing the presentation through ritual gestures like a nod of acceptance, symbolizing the god's receipt of tribute. Accompanying these acts were fervent prayers recited by the kahuna, invoking Lono to bestow abundant rain, fertile soils, and bountiful crops for the coming year, as documented in traditional accounts emphasizing survival and renewal.
Daily observances throughout the Makahiki period reinforced devotion to Lono through communal chants (oli) and dances (hula), performed in open spaces or near heiau to invoke the god's presence and celebrate themes of rain, growth, and fertility. These performances, led by trained practitioners, served as meditative expressions of gratitude and supplication, often incorporating poetic references to Lono's manifestations in clouds and storms. Strict kapu (taboos) were enforced during this time, prohibiting any harm to symbols of Lono, such as pigs (considered his kino lau or physical embodiments), to maintain the sacred atmosphere and avoid divine retribution; violations could disrupt the season's blessings.
Kahuna played pivotal roles in these rituals, conducting purifications such as the hiuwai (sea bathing) at the season's outset to cleanse participants and the land, preparing all for Lono's influence. They also performed divinations, interpreting natural signs like the behavior of stars or ritual outcomes to predict the year's success and guide communal actions. The season culminated with the launching of the waa o Lono (Lono's canoe), a vessel loaded with offerings from the tributes, set adrift toward Kahiki (the mythical homeland) to symbolize Lono's departure and promise of return, often accompanied by a mock battle led by the alii impersonating Lono and a sacrificial offering of a pig, marking the end of the kapu and transition back to the season of K. This rite integrated elements from the earlier processions, as the image's sea journey echoed its circuit of the island.

Social and Political Role

Peace and Cessation of Warfare

The Makahiki season imposed a strict kapu, or taboo, prohibiting all forms of warfare, including battles, executions, and the use or manufacture of weapons, as the period was placed under the protection of Lono, the god of peace, agriculture, and fertility. This sacred interlude ensured that no conflicts disrupted the communal focus on harvest tributes, rituals, and celebrations. The kapu extended to alii (chiefs) and makainana (commoners) alike, fostering a collective suspension of hostilities that aligned with Lono's domain of renewal and abundance.
Enforcement of this peace kapu was maintained through proclamations by the alii, who declared the onset of Makahiki upon astronomical signs like the rising of the Makalii (Pleiades) constellation, signaling a formal truce across the islands. Violators who engaged in prohibited acts, such as initiating fights or handling weapons, faced severe repercussions under the traditional kapu system, which could include death or exile imposed by the alii. This mechanism reinforced social order, as the kapu was not merely advisory but divinely mandated, with immediate communal oversight ensuring compliance during the festival's active phases.
The cessation of warfare during Makahiki yielded significant societal benefits, allowing war-torn lands to heal and regenerate through fallow periods that promoted soil recovery and natural resource replenishment after the intense agricultural and conflict demands of the preceding months. Communities could redirect energies toward diplomacy and resolution of disputes, strengthening alliances among alii and fostering long-term stability rather than escalation. This enforced peace also enabled physical and spiritual rejuvenation for the people, reducing injuries from combat and permitting rest that bolstered overall health and productivity in the cycles of Hawaiian society.
In contrast to the K-dominated season of warfare and expansion, which emphasized conquest and resource acquisition through conflict, Makahiki served as an annual "peace interlude" dedicated to Lono, balancing the cyclical rhythm of Hawaiian life between aggression and harmony to sustain ecological and social equilibrium. This alternation prevented perpetual strife, integrating peace as an essential counterpoint to the god K's martial pursuits and ensuring the islands' long-term vitality.

Royal and Elite Participation

During the Makahiki season, alii (chiefs) played a central role in the ceremonial circuits that traversed the islands, accompanying the procession of Lono's representatives to collect tributes and maintain order. These circuits, often led by the alii nui (high chief) acting as Lono's deputy, involved traveling through each ahupuaa (land division) where priests carried the akua loa (god image) and communities presented hookupu (offerings) such as pigs, taro, feathers, and other harvest goods at border altars. This process allowed alii to oversee the auhau (taxes) and ensure equitable distribution, while providing a non-violent forum for resolving disputes among districts, as warfare was kapu (taboo) during this period of peace.
Royal births occurring during Makahiki were considered highly auspicious, believed to imbue the child with Lono's blessings of fertility, abundance, and peace. In the Kamehameha lineage, for instance, the chief Lono-i-ka-makahikison of Keawe and an ancestor in the royal genealogywas named by his mother, Lono-ma-i-kanaka, at birth, honoring the god Lono during this propitious season; the name reflected the Makahiki's association with renewal and divine favor. Similarly, Kamehameha I himself was likely born in late 1758, during the stormy Makahiki months around November, amid prophecies and celestial signs like Halley's Comet.
Elite privileges during Makahiki reinforced social hierarchy while fostering unity, with alii enjoying exclusive access to lavish feasts and select games that showcased chiefly prowess. Nobility participated in or oversaw events like hkk (wrestling) and ihe (spear throwing), often reserved for trained athletes under chiefly patronage, alongside grand ahaaina (feasts) featuring abundant foods denied to commoners during the kapu. These activities not only celebrated physical strength but also allowed alii to demonstrate mana (spiritual power), strengthening bonds between elites and makainana (commoners).
Politically, Makahiki circuits offered alii a vital opportunity to assess district loyalties through tribute yields and community participation, enabling the high chief to gauge resource production and resolve potential rivalries without conflict. By redistributing surplus goods equitably after collectionsuch as allocating portions for communal welfarealii maintained authority and promoted island-wide cohesion, transforming the festival into a mechanism for governance and renewal.

Impact of European Contact

Early Encounters

Captain James Cook's expedition arrived at the Hawaiian island of Kauai on January 18, 1778, and later at Hawaii Island in Kealakekua Bay on January 17, 1779, coinciding with the Makahiki season, a period dedicated to the god Lono involving peace, tribute collection, and festivities. Hawaiian observers, interpreting the ships' white sails and Cook's arrival timing as signs of Lono's return in his circuit around the islands, greeted the visitors with elaborate welcoming rituals, including prostrations, offerings of pigs and plantains, and chants invoking Lono. Cook himself noted in his journal the natives' "surprizing" hospitality and their cries of "Orono," a term later linked to Lono, during these initial encounters.
Eyewitness accounts from Cook's crew, including the captain's journal and officer reports, detailed vibrant Makahiki processions on both Kauai and Hawaii Island, where chiefs like Kalanipuu arrived in canoes adorned with feathered cloaks and idols, accompanied by drummers and bearers of taro, breadfruit, and sugar cane under white flags symbolizing peace. On Hawaii Island, tributes flowed generously to the ships, with hogs, vegetables, and woven mats presented as part of the festival's harvest offerings, reflecting the Makahiki's role in redistributing surplus goods. Crew members observed games such as boxing matches with formal judges and standards, as well as surfing displays in Kealakekua Bay where dozens of natives rode waves on wooden boards, all conducted amid the season's non-violent ethos.
Europeans engaged in limited cultural exchanges during these observances, with some crew members joining or watching the sports and games, noting the absence of warfare and the strict kapu enforcing peace, which prohibited conflict and allowed free movement for tribute collection. This temporary cessation of hostilities facilitated open interactions, as Hawaiians shared provisions without fear. The introduction of European metal tools, such as iron nails and daggers, as trade items and occasional tribute gifts subtly shifted traditional exchanges; Hawaiians valued these durable goods highly, incorporating them into offerings and altering the material dynamics of Makahiki tributes from organic goods to include imported iron.

Decline and Suppression

The arrival of American Protestant missionaries in 1820, shortly after the abolition of the kapu system in 1819, marked a pivotal shift in Hawaiian religious practices. These missionaries, including figures like Hiram Bingham, actively condemned traditional Hawaiian spirituality as idolatrous and "pagan," promoting Christianity as the sole path to salvation. This led to widespread prohibitions against rituals honoring gods such as Lono, central to Makahiki observances, and encouraged the desecration of heiau (sacred temples) by both Hawaiians and foreigners, with churches often built on former temple sites to symbolize the triumph of the new faith. By the 1830s, missionary influence had permeated the ali'i (chiefly class), resulting in royal edicts that further suppressed indigenous ceremonies, effectively diminishing Makahiki's role in communal life.
During the Kingdom of Hawai'i era (17951893), Kamehameha I's unification of the islands initially integrated Makahiki into the new political structure, with ho'okupu tributes repurposed as taxes to support the emerging state. However, as Western advisors and governance models gained prominence under subsequent rulers, the festival's scale and autonomy waned; emphasis shifted toward European-style administration, trade, and military organization, sidelining seasonal agrarian and spiritual cycles in favor of year-round economic productivity. This transition accelerated after Kamehameha's death in 1819, as the monarchy increasingly adopted foreign customs, reducing Makahiki from a kingdom-wide event to localized or diminished practices.
The overthrow of the Hawaiian monarchy in 1893 by American business interests, followed by annexation in 1898, intensified the erosion of Makahiki through the rise of the sugar plantation economy. Native Hawaiians faced massive land dispossession via the 1895 Land Act and subsequent leases to foreign corporations, fragmenting communal 'ina (lands) essential for the festival's agricultural and tribute-based rituals. Plantation labor systems imposed relentless work schedules on Native and immigrant workers alike, clashing with Makahiki's mandated period of rest and cessation from toil, which effectively drove observances underground or into obscurity amid economic pressures.
In the 20th century, under U.S. territorial rule (19001959), policies systematically marginalized Hawaiian customs to assimilate the population into American norms. The suppression of the Hawaiian language in schools via the 1896 policy and promotion of English-only education eroded oral traditions tied to Makahiki, while federal oversight prioritized military and economic development over indigenous cultural preservation. This era of direct colonial control culminated in near-total decline of the festival by mid-century, only beginning to abate with the Hawaiian Renaissance of the 1970s, though suppression had already severed generational knowledge transmission.

Modern Observance

Revival Efforts

The revival of Makahiki traditions gained momentum during the Hawaiian Renaissance of the 1970s, a cultural reawakening that emphasized the reclamation and documentation of Native Hawaiian practices amid broader movements for land rights and identity. Scholars and activists like Davianna Pmaikai McGregor contributed significantly through her work with the Protect Kahoolawe Ohana, where she helped organize Makahiki ceremonies on the island of Kahoolawe starting in 1982 to promote healing and cultural reconnection. These annual ceremonies continue as of 2025, marking their 43rd anniversary with an opening from November 2630 at Hakioawa. The Bishop Museum supported these efforts by curating exhibits and educational resources on Hawaiian seasonal observances, including Makahiki, to preserve and disseminate traditional knowledge.
A pivotal milestone came with the 1978 Hawaii State Constitutional Convention, which amended Article XII, Section 7 to reaffirm and protect all rights customarily and traditionally exercised by Native Hawaiians for subsistence, cultural, and religious purposes. This provision explicitly safeguarded practices like Makahiki observances, allowing for their public performance without legal hindrance and fostering a supportive environment for cultural revival. The convention's outcomes, including the establishment of the Office of Hawaiian Affairs, further empowered Native Hawaiian communities to integrate such traditions into contemporary life.
Educational initiatives have embedded Makahiki into school curricula through ina-based learning approaches, which emphasize hands-on connections to land, culture, and sustainability, often featuring annual units on the season's rituals and games. Institutions like Kamehameha Schools exemplify this by incorporating Makahiki festivals into their programs, where students participate in traditional sports, chants, and processions to build cultural pride and physical skills.
Community organizations have played a vital role in sustaining Makahiki elements, with Kamehameha Schools perpetuating chants such as those invoking Lono and games like ulu maika through campus-wide events and teachings.

Contemporary Events and Practices

In contemporary Makahiki observances, urban adaptations have brought the tradition to city settings, particularly in Honolulu, where annual family-friendly gatherings emphasize community reconnection and cultural education. The Urban Makahiki event, held on November 15, 2025, at Aala Park, features music, cultural demonstrations, ina-focused activities, food vendors, and hookupu offerings from 10 a.m. to 2 p.m., organized by the Trust for Public Land in partnership with First Hawaiian Bank and Hawaii Theatre Center to foster a sense of place and healthy relationships with the land. These events revive traditional sports like ulu maika and hukihuki in accessible formats for modern participants.
Island-specific observances highlight localized expressions of Makahiki across Hawaii. On Oahu, beach ceremonies at sites like Kaimana Beach Hotel incorporate harvest gatherings with mele (songs), storytelling, and feasting to honor Lono and promote peace during the season. At Waimea Valley, the November 11, 2025, Makahiki Festival includes an opening ceremony at 9 a.m., followed by cultural activities and the Te Moana Nui Games, a Polynesian sports competition, running until 4:30 p.m. On Hawaii Island, school-based programs integrate Makahiki into education, such as Lauphoehoe Community Public Charter School's December 19, 2025, event featuring sports like volleyball and basketball to teach cultural values alongside physical activity.
Modern adaptations blend Makahiki with sustainability initiatives, including demonstrations of traditional farming practices and eco-tourism experiences that educate on ina care. The Mahiai Makahiki theme in 2025 celebrates food producers through community panels, cultural games, and locally sourced feasts, underscoring regenerative agriculture's role in Hawaiian communities. Eco-tourism elements, such as guided ina reconnection tours during the season, allow visitors to engage with environmental stewardship while experiencing cultural protocols. These practices often fuse with contemporary holidays like Thanksgiving, aligning Makahiki's harvest gratitude with modern feasting traditions.
In 2025, Makahiki highlights include integrations with broader cultural programming, such as ties to HONOLULU Restaurant Week (November 816), where dining promotions coincide with seasonal celebrations of abundance and community. Cultural nights at hotels like Kaimana Beach emphasize peace, non-violence, and ina stewardship through evening events with Hawaiian music and reflections on Lono's principles.
