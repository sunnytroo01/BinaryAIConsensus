 1938 marked a critical escalation in Nazi Germany's territorial expansion and antisemitic policies, setting the stage for World War II. On March 12, German forces entered Austria unopposed, annexing the country into the Third Reich through the Anschluss, a move that unified ethnic Germans under Hitler's control and violated the Treaty of Versailles. This aggression continued with the Munich Agreement on September 30, where Britain, France, and Italy conceded the Sudetenland of Czechoslovakia to Germany in a policy of appeasement. British Prime Minister Neville Chamberlain declared 'peace for our time' upon returning from the talks, ostensibly to preserve peace but ultimately emboldening further Nazi demands. Domestically in Germany, November 910 saw Kristallnacht, a state-orchestrated pogrom involving the destruction of synagogues, Jewish businesses, and homes, with approximately 30,000 Jewish men arrested and sent to concentration camps, signaling intensified persecution.
Scientific advancements included the discovery of nuclear fission in uranium by German chemists Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann in December, a breakthrough with profound implications for energy and weaponry, though its full consequences emerged later. In the United States, the Fair Labor Standards Act established a federal minimum wage of 25 cents per hour, a 44-hour workweek maximum, and protections against child labor, addressing ongoing Depression-era economic hardships. Natural disasters struck with the Great New England Hurricane on September 21, one of the most destructive in U.S. history, causing over 600 deaths and widespread flooding. Culturally, the year featured milestones like the release of Disney's Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs, the debut of Superman in comics, and Orson Welles' War of the Worlds radio broadcast, which sparked public panic. These events underscored 1938's blend of geopolitical peril, innovation, and societal shifts amid global uncertainty.

Science and Technology

Major Discoveries and Inventions

In chemistry, Roy J. Plunkett discovered polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE), later commercialized as Teflon, on April 6, 1938, during experiments at E.I. du Pont de Nemours and Company's Jackson Laboratory in New Jersey. While investigating tetrafluoroethylene gas for refrigerant applications, Plunkett and his assistant observed that a compressed cylinder's contents failed to release as gas; instead, dissection revealed a waxy white solid formed by unintended polymerization, exhibiting exceptional chemical inertness, low friction, and thermal stability.
In physics, Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann reported the discovery of nuclear fission on December 22, 1938, based on experiments conducted at Berlin's Kaiser Wilhelm Institute for Chemistry. Bombarding uranium-235 with neutrons yielded unexpected lighter elements, including barium-139 isotopes, confirmed via chemical separation and radiochemical analysis showing no transuranic products as anticipated by prevailing theory. Lise Meitner and Otto Robert Frisch provided the theoretical interpretation in early 1939, proposing that neutron capture induced asymmetric nucleus splitting into two fragments of roughly equal mass, releasing 2-3 neutrons and approximately 200 million electron volts of energy per event.
In atmospheric science, Guy Stewart Callendar analyzed land surface temperature data from 147 globally distributed stations in his 1938 paper "The Artificial Production of Carbon Dioxide and Its Influence on Temperature," identifying a mean warming of 0.005C per year from the 1880s to the 1930s, totaling about 0.3C over the preceding half-century. He quantified anthropogenic CO2 emissions at roughly 150,000 million metric tons from coal and petroleum combustion, correlating the resulting 6-10% atmospheric CO2 rise with enhanced infrared absorption that could explain 75-100% of the observed temperature increase after accounting for natural variability.

Economy and Society

Global Economic Conditions

The Recession of 193738 in the United States marked a sharp downturn amid the ongoing Great Depression recovery, with real GDP contracting by approximately 3.3% in 1938 following a peak in mid-1937, driven primarily by fiscal austerity measures including cuts in federal spending and Social Security payroll taxes that reduced aggregate demand. Unemployment surged to a peak of around 1920% by early 1938, reflecting declines in industrial production by over 30% from the prior year's levels, as monetary policy contributed through the Federal Reserve's increase in reserve requirements and the Treasury's sterilization of gold inflows, which limited money supply expansion. These policies reversed earlier stimulus effects, illustrating how contractionary fiscal and monetary actions amplified cyclical weaknesses in a fragile economy still scarred by banking failures and debt overhangs.
In contrast, Nazi Germany's economy exhibited robust expansion in 1938, achieving GDP growth of roughly 4% through aggressive deficit-financed rearmament and public works programs that reduced official unemployment from over 6 million in 1933 to near zero by absorbing labor into state-directed industries. This recovery relied on mechanisms like Mefo bills for off-balance-sheet financing of military buildup, which boosted production in armaments and infrastructure while enforcing wage controls and labor conscription to sustain output amid resource shortages and autarkic trade policies. Such command-style interventions prioritized short-term employment gains over market signals, creating distortions like suppressed consumption and reliance on plunder from annexed territories, yet they underscored how sustained fiscal deficits could counteract deflationary pressures in a politically controlled framework.
Globally, 1938 saw persistent trade imbalances exacerbated by protectionist barriers, with world trade volumes still roughly 25% below 1929 peaks due to tariffs like the U.S. Smoot-Hawley Act and retaliatory measures that shifted export surpluses toward deficit countries while hindering multilateral recovery. The U.S. downturn contributed to reduced demand for imports from Europe and primary producers, amplifying contractions in export-dependent economies, though some nations like Japan sustained growth via militarized expansion into Asia, highlighting uneven recovery patterns tied to domestic fiscal impulses over balanced trade equilibria.

Key Policy and Social Changes

In the United States, President Franklin D. Roosevelt signed the Fair Labor Standards Act on June 25, 1938, establishing a national minimum wage of 25 cents per hour, a maximum standard workweek of 44 hours (with overtime pay required beyond that), and prohibitions on most child labor for those under 16 (or 18 in hazardous occupations); the provisions took effect on October 24, 1938, applying initially to industries engaged in interstate commerce.
On January 3, 1938, Roosevelt established the National Foundation for Infantile Paralysisrenamed the March of Dimes in 1938to fund polio research, treatment, and prevention through public appeals, marking a targeted public health initiative that raised over $1.5 million in its inaugural nationwide campaign by year's end via contributions symbolized by dime donations.
In Nazi Germany, anti-Jewish policies intensified with the "Polenaktion" (Polish Action), ordered by Reinhard Heydrich on October 26, 1938; between October 27 and 29, Gestapo forces arrested and deported approximately 17,000 Jews holding Polish citizenshipmany long-term residentsto the Polish border near Zbaszyn, where Polish authorities refused entry, stranding thousands in squalid no-man's-land conditions and foreshadowing further radicalization.

Culture and Entertainment

Film, Music, and Literature

In film, Walt Disney's Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs entered wide release on February 4, 1938, following its premiere the prior December; as the first full-length animated feature in Technicolor, it pioneered color animation on a grand scale and grossed over $8 million worldwide in its initial theatrical run, recouping its $1.5 million production cost many times over and setting box office records until surpassed by Gone with the Wind. Other prominent releases included The Adventures of Robin Hood on May 14, a Technicolor epic starring Errol Flynn that earned acclaim for its lavish production and action choreography, grossing $4 million domestically, and Alfred Hitchcock's The Lady Vanishes in November, a thriller noted for its suspenseful train sequence and espionage plot.
In music, Benny Goodman's orchestra delivered the first jazz performance at Carnegie Hall on January 16, 1938, featuring an integrated ensemble with pianist Teddy Wilson and vibraphonist Lionel Hamptonboth African American musiciansalongside stars like drummer Gene Krupa and trumpeter Harry James; the sold-out concert, with overflow crowds, marked a pivotal moment in elevating swing jazz from dance halls to concert legitimacy, recorded in part and later released to enduring influence. Another highlight was the Carnival of Swing on May 29 at Randall's Island Stadium, a massive outdoor jazz benefit drawing tens of thousands and showcasing multiple big bands in a display of the genre's rising popularity.
Literature saw key publications such as Marjorie Kinnan Rawlings' The Yearling, a coming-of-age tale set in Florida's backwoods that topped bestseller lists and secured the 1939 Pulitzer Prize for its vivid depiction of rural life and human-animal bonds, and A.J. Cronin's The Citadel, a novel critiquing medical corruption that dominated U.S. sales charts with over 100 weeks on lists. Daphne du Maurier's Rebecca, a gothic suspense story of a haunted estate and obsessive love, also emerged late in the year to strong sales and critical notice for its psychological depth. Graham Greene's Brighton Rock explored crime and morality in England's underworld, contributing to the era's trend toward socially observant fiction.
Radio drama intersected with literature on October 30, 1938, when Orson Welles' Mercury Theatre aired a realistic adaptation of H.G. Wells' The War of the Worlds, formatted as interruptive news bulletins reporting a Martian invasion of New Jersey; while an opening announcement clarified its fictional nature, some late-tuning listeners reportedly panicked, fleeing homes or calling authorities, though contemporary newspaper accounts exaggerated the scalelater analyses estimate affected individuals in the thousands amid 6 million listeners, not millions in mass hysteria.

Sports and Other Milestones

The New York Yankees defeated the Chicago Cubs 4 games to 0 to win the 1938 World Series, marking the team's third consecutive championship and extending their dominance in Major League Baseball. Key contributions included home runs from Joe DiMaggio and complete games pitched by Lefty Gomez and Bill Dickey, with the series concluding on October 9 at Yankee Stadium.
In boxing, heavyweight champion Joe Louis defended his title by knocking out Max Schmeling in the first round on June 22 at Yankee Stadium, avenging a 1936 loss and retaining the undisputed heavyweight crown in a bout attended by over 70,000 spectators.
Italy successfully defended their FIFA World Cup title, defeating Hungary 42 in the final on June 19 in Paris, with goals from Silvio Piola (two), Gino Colaussi, and Giuseppe Meazza securing the victory amid a tournament featuring 16 teams and notable upsets like Brazil's elimination.
The National Baseball Hall of Fame conducted balloting that elected Grover Cleveland Alexander, Alexander Cartwright, and Henry Chadwick as new members, adding to the institution's roster ahead of its formal opening the following year.
In American football, the New York Giants won the NFL Championship Game against the Green Bay Packers 2317 in the league's first sudden-death overtime on December 12 at the Polo Grounds, with a last-second field goal by Ward Cuff sealing the victory after a tied regulation period.
Skiing infrastructure expanded in the United States with the opening of new rope tows, including the Lyndon Outing Club's 300-foot tow in Lyndonville, Vermont, on February 5, facilitating greater access to downhill skiing on local slopes.

On November 10, Mustafa Kemal Atatrk, founder and first president of the Republic of Turkey, died at Dolmabahe Palace in Istanbul at age 57 from cirrhosis of the liver.
At least 91 Jews were killed during the anti-Jewish violence of November 910, 1938, in Germany and recently annexed Austria, according to official reports compiled by Nazi authorities and corroborated by postwar historical analysis. These fatalities occurred amid widespread attacks on Jewish individuals, primarily through beatings, shootings, and arson, with victims including men, women, and some children targeted in their homes, synagogues, and streets.
The documented deaths encompassed cases such as those in major cities like Berlin, Vienna, and Munich, where local records noted immediate killings during the riots, though comprehensive individual victim lists remain incomplete due to the chaotic nature of the events and destruction of evidence. Additional deaths in November totaled in the hundreds from injuries sustained that night, including fatal beatings and burns, as well as suicides triggered by the assaults.

December

On December 25, 1938, Karel apek, the Czech playwright and novelist best known for introducing the term "robot" in his 1920 play R.U.R., died in Prague at age 48 from complications of influenza and pneumonia. His death occurred amid rising political tensions in Czechoslovakia following the Munich Agreement, though he had been nominated multiple times for the Nobel Prize in Literature without receiving it.
On December 28, 1938, Florence Lawrence, a pioneering Canadian-American actress often credited as the first film star due to her work in over 300 silent films for Biograph and Edison companies, died by suicide in Beverly Hills, California, at age 52 after ingesting ant poison amid chronic pain from injuries sustained in her career. She had also invented early automotive safety features, including a mechanical turn signal and brake light, though she received no formal recognition for them during her lifetime.
Other deaths that month included Dina Appeldoorn, a Dutch pianist and composer, on December 4 at age 54, and Tamanishiki San'emon, Japan's 32nd yokozuna sumo wrestler, also on December 4. These losses marked the end of a year shadowed by global economic recovery efforts and escalating international conflicts.

Nobel Prizes

Categories and Laureates

The Nobel Prizes for 1938 were awarded across five categories, as established by Alfred Nobel's will, with laureates selected by the respective Swedish and Norwegian institutions based on nominations and deliberations. Each award recognized specific scientific, literary, or humanitarian contributions, with motivations drawn from the prize committees' assessments.
Physics: Enrico Fermi received the Nobel Prize in Physics for his demonstrations of the existence of new radioactive elements produced by neutron irradiation of elements.
Chemistry: Richard Kuhn was awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for his work on carotenoids and vitamins; the prize was formally presented in 1939 after the Nazi German government initially prohibited acceptance.
Physiology or Medicine: Corneille Jean Franois Heymans was granted the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for the discovery of the role of the sinus and aortic mechanisms in the regulation of respiration.
Literature: Pearl S. Buck was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature for her rich and truly epic descriptions of peasant life in China and for her biographical masterpieces.
Peace: The Nansen International Office for Refugees received the Nobel Peace Prize for continuing the humanitarian efforts of Fridtjof Nansen in aiding refugees across Europe.

Historical Assessments

Geopolitical Debates and Appeasement

The Anschluss of Austria on March 12, 1938, prompted limited diplomatic protests from Britain and France but no military response, marking an early instance of appeasement toward Nazi expansionism. British Prime Minister Neville Chamberlain viewed such concessions as pragmatic, arguing that immediate confrontation risked war for which Britain was unprepared, with military assessments indicating deficiencies in air and ground forces. French leaders, similarly cautious, feared the collapse of the alliance with Czechoslovakia would expose their Maginot Line to direct German assault without British support.
Proponents of appeasement contended that territorial concessions, as in the Munich Agreement of September 30, 1938, allowed time for rearmament; Britain's Royal Air Force saw aircraft deliveries rise from 445 in January 1939 to 579 in February, amid a broader program that allocated 21.4 percent of gross national product to defense by year's end. Chamberlain specifically cited Munich as securing "peace for our time" while enabling defensive buildup against potential future aggression. Critics, however, argued from first principles of deterrence that yielding to bluffHitler's threats lacked full mobilization for invasionsignaled weakness, incentivizing further demands by demonstrating that territorial gains could be achieved without war's costs.
The Sudetenland crisis highlighted genuine ethnic tensions, with approximately three million Sudeten Germans reporting discrimination under Czechoslovak rule, including restrictions on cultural autonomy and economic disadvantages since the post-World War I border redraws. Led by Konrad Henlein's Sudeten German Party, these groups agitated for self-determination or union with Germany, culminating in uprisings and martial law declarations by Czech authorities in September 1938.
American isolationism shaped transatlantic debates, with Gallup polls in November 1938 showing 94 percent disapproval of Nazi treatment of Jews yet 72 percent opposition to U.S. intervention in European affairs, reflecting fears of entanglement post-Versailles. This stance reinforced European hesitancy, as U.S. non-involvement limited Allied resolve against Nazi bluff tactics, which had succeeded in Austria without resistance.

Totalitarian Policies and Their Outcomes

In Nazi Germany, totalitarian policies under Adolf Hitler achieved significant short-term economic gains by 1938, reducing official unemployment to under 1% through extensive public works programs and rearmament efforts that absorbed labor into state-directed projects. These measures, including deficit-financed infrastructure like the Autobahn and military buildup, diverted resources toward autarky and preparation for expansion, fostering industrial output growth but enforcing wage controls, labor conscription via the German Labor Front, and suppression of independent unions, thereby curtailing workers' freedoms.
The regime's antisemitic terror peaked with Kristallnacht on November 910, 1938, a state-orchestrated pogrom where SA and SS paramilitaries destroyed over 7,000 Jewish-owned businesses and burned 267 synagogues, while murdering at least 91 Jews and arresting around 30,000 for internment in concentration camps. Authorities manipulated insurance claims by confiscating payouts from Jewish victims and imposing a collective fine of 1 billion Reichsmarks on the Jewish community to cover damages, extracting funds equivalent to about 12% of Germany's 1938 Jewish assets and accelerating emigration under duress. This event exemplified the regime's use of violence to enforce ideological conformity, with censorship and propaganda suppressing dissent and portraying the violence as spontaneous public outrage.
In the Soviet Union, Joseph Stalin's Great Purge continued into 1938 under NKVD chief Nikolai Yezhov, with mass executions and arrests targeting perceived enemies, including military officers and party officials, resulting in hundreds of thousands of deaths as quotas for "repressions" were met through fabricated charges. These operations dismantled independent thought within the bureaucracy and armed forces, prioritizing regime loyalty over competence and yielding short-term consolidation of power but widespread fear and inefficiency.
Japan's imperialist regime advanced its conquest of China in 1938, capturing key cities like Wuhan in the summer offensive, expanding control over eastern territories to secure resources amid ongoing atrocities against civilians. This militarized expansion, driven by the need for raw materials and markets, suppressed domestic opposition through ultranationalist indoctrination and military tribunals, achieving territorial gains at the expense of international isolation and internal regimentation.

Long-term Causal Impacts

The Munich Agreement of September 30, 1938, exemplified appeasement policy, conceding the Sudetenland to Nazi Germany without Czech input, which emboldened Adolf Hitler to occupy the remainder of Czechoslovakia in March 1939 and invade Poland on September 1, 1939, precipitating World War II across Europe. This chain of territorial aggressions, unchecked by Western powers, demonstrated how concessions to totalitarian expansionism eroded deterrence, contributing to a global conflict that claimed over 70 million lives by 1945.
Kristallnacht pogroms on November 910, 1938, destroyed over 7,000 Jewish businesses and 1,400 synagogues while resulting in at least 91 Jewish deaths and the arrest of 30,000 Jewish men for concentration camp internment, marking a shift from legal discrimination to state-orchestrated violence that radicalized Nazi anti-Jewish measures. This escalation facilitated subsequent policies of forced emigration, property confiscation, and ghettoization, with Jewish deportation rates surging post-1938; by 1941, over 100,000 German Jews had been expelled or confined, laying groundwork for the systematic genocide that exterminated six million Jews during the Holocaust.
The December 1938 discovery of nuclear fission by Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann, with theoretical interpretation by Lise Meitner and Otto Frisch, revealed uranium's capacity to split and release energy, prompting migr physicists like Leo Szilard to warn of chain reactions for explosives, culminating in Albert Einstein's August 1939 letter to President Franklin D. Roosevelt that initiated the Manhattan Project. This refugee-driven scientific exodus from Nazi persecutionexemplified by Meitner's flight due to her Jewish ancestryenabled Allied atomic bomb development, deployed in 1945 against Japan, but raised enduring ethical concerns over state coercion in science, as totalitarian regimes like Nazi Germany's dismissal of Jewish researchers risked both innovation loss and weapon proliferation.
The U.S. recession of 19371938, with industrial production dropping 33% and unemployment rising to 19% by mid-1938, stemmed from Federal Reserve credit tightening and reversal of New Deal fiscal stimulus, illustrating risks of premature policy contraction amid fragile recovery from the Great Depression. These events underscored debates on government intervention, as renewed spending post-1938 correlated with output rebound, though full employment awaited wartime mobilization, informing postwar Keynesian emphases on sustained demand management over abrupt austerity. In Nazi Germany, 1938 economic expansion via rearmament and Mefo bills masked deficits exceeding 15 billion Reichsmarks annually against 30 billion in spending, rendering the autarkic model unsustainable without conquest for resources, as suppressed imports and wage controls heightened hyperinflation risks evident in prior Weimar collapses. This deficit-driven path necessitated war by 1939, exposing totalitarian economics' reliance on plunder over productivity for longevity.
