Vince Lombardi Trophy
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Vince Lombardi Trophy is the championship prize awarded annually to the winner of the Super Bowl, the National Football League's (NFL) culminating playoff game that determines the league's season champion. This sterling silver artifact, crafted by Tiffany & Co., depicts a regulation-size football mounted in a kicking position atop a pyramid-like base composed of three concave sides, measuring approximately 21 inches in height and weighing about 7 pounds.
The trophy's design originated in 1966 when NFL Commissioner Pete Rozelle commissioned Tiffany & Co. vice president Oscar Riedener to create it, with Riedener sketching the initial concept on a cocktail napkin during a meeting; the prototype was inspired by the shape of a football combined with elements resembling a Cornflakes box for the base. It was first presented on January 15, 1967, to the Green Bay Packers following their victory in Super Bowl I, then known as the inaugural AFL-NFL World Championship Game, under the original name of the World Professional Football Championship Trophy. Tiffany & Co. has exclusively produced the trophy ever since, with each one taking four months to craft at a manufacturing cost of $50,000, though its insured value is estimated at over $10,000; the design has remained unchanged for nearly six decades.
In 1970, following the death of legendary NFL coach Vince Lombardi from cancer at age 57, the NFL renamed the trophy in his honor to commemorate his profound impact on professional football. Lombardi, who had served as head coach of the Green Bay Packers from 1959 to 1967, led the team to five NFL championships in seven years and secured victories in the first two Super Bowls (I and II), establishing a legacy of disciplined excellence that defined the early era of the modern NFL. The renaming was formalized before Super Bowl V in 1971, and since then, the permanent possession of the trophy has been granted to each Super Bowl-winning team, with miniature sterling silver replicas distributed to players and key personnel at a cost of $1,500 each.

History and Origin

Naming and Dedication

Vince Lombardi, born on June 11, 1913, in Brooklyn, New York, rose to prominence as one of the most influential coaches in American football history. He assumed the role of head coach and general manager for the Green Bay Packers in 1959, transforming the team into a dominant force and leading them to five NFL championships between 1961 and 1967. Under his leadership, the Packers secured victories in Super Bowl I on January 15, 1967, defeating the Kansas City Chiefs 35-10, and Super Bowl II on January 14, 1968, beating the Oakland Raiders 33-14, establishing an unparalleled legacy of success in the early years of the Super Bowl era.
Lombardi's coaching tenure with the Packers ended after the 1967 season. He served as the team's general manager in 1968 before becoming head coach and executive vice president of the Washington Redskins in 1969, but his impact on the sport endured profoundly. Diagnosed with an aggressive form of colon cancer in June 1970, he passed away on September 3, 1970, at the age of 57 in Washington, D.C. In recognition of his contributions to professional football, NFL Commissioner Pete Rozelle announced on September 10, 1970, that the Super Bowl trophypreviously known simply as the "World Championship Game Trophy"would be renamed the Vince Lombardi Trophy in his honor, effective starting with Super Bowl V. This decision honored Lombardi's role in elevating the NFL's profile and his embodiment of competitive excellence, ensuring his name would forever symbolize the pinnacle of achievement in the league.
The first presentation of the renamed Vince Lombardi Trophy occurred on January 17, 1971, following Super Bowl V at the Orange Bowl in Miami, Florida, where the Baltimore Colts defeated the Dallas Cowboys 16-13. Lombardi's widow, Marie Lombardi, personally handed the trophy to Colts owner Carroll Rosenbloom, marking a poignant dedication that underscored the late coach's enduring influence on the game.
Central to Lombardi's coaching philosophy was his emphasis on relentless preparation and the pursuit of victory, encapsulated in the oft-attributed quote, "Winning isn't everything; it's the only thing," which he popularized during his time with the Packers. This mindset not only drove his teams to multiple titles but also infused the Vince Lombardi Trophy with a prestige tied to uncompromising standards of performance and determination, making it the ultimate emblem of NFL supremacy.

Introduction to the Super Bowl

The Vince Lombardi Trophy serves as the championship prize awarded annually to the winner of the Super Bowl, the National Football League's (NFL) premier event that determines the league's supreme champion by pitting the champions of the American Football League (AFL) and the National Football League (NFL) against each other originally, and since the 1970 merger, the champions of the American Football Conference (AFC) and National Football Conference (NFC). This award embodies the pinnacle of professional American football achievement, signifying dominance across the unified NFL structure.
The trophy's inception occurred on January 15, 1967, immediately following Super Bowl I at the Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum, where it was presented to the Green Bay Packers for their decisive 35-10 defeat of the Kansas City Chiefs. Initially, the award bore no personal namesake and was simply designated the "World Professional Football Championship" trophy, reflecting its role in crowning the top team from the competing leagues.
This establishment aligned directly with the 1966 merger between the NFL and the American Football League (AFL), announced on June 8, 1966, which aimed to consolidate the rival circuits into a single entity by 1970 while maintaining separate schedules in the interim. The Super Bowl emerged as the merger's flagship innovationa season-ending clash between the AFL and NFL championsto resolve the unified league's titleholder and foster competitive parity.
Over time, the Super Bowl transitioned from its origins as an inter-league showdown into the modern NFL's singular culminating spectacle, succeeding earlier formats like the NFL's annual championship games that dated back to 1933. Throughout this evolution, the trophy has endured as the enduring emblem of victory, with Vince Lombardi's Green Bay Packers securing the first two editions in 1967 and 1968.

Early Awards and Renaming

The Vince Lombardi Trophy originated as an unnamed award for the inaugural Super Bowls, specifically designed as a sterling silver football by Tiffany & Co. for the NFL's championship game. For Super Bowls I through IV, held between 1967 and 1970, the trophy was inscribed simply with "World Professional Football Championship" on its base, without additional personalization such as Roman numerals denoting the event number. This early version symbolized the merger of the NFL and AFL, with the Green Bay Packers receiving the trophy after defeating the Kansas City Chiefs 35-10 in Super Bowl I on January 15, 1967, and again after a 33-14 victory over the Oakland Raiders in Super Bowl II on January 14, 1968. The New York Jets then claimed it in Super Bowl III on January 12, 1969, upsetting the Baltimore Colts 16-7, followed by the Kansas City Chiefs' 23-7 win over the Minnesota Vikings in Super Bowl IV on January 11, 1970.
Following the death of legendary coach Vince Lombardi on September 3, 1970, from cancer at age 57, the NFL decided to rename the trophy in his honor to commemorate his pivotal role in establishing the Super Bowl's prestige through back-to-back victories with the Packers in the first two games. The renaming occurred in 1970, with the updated inscription "Vince Lombardi Trophy" first appearing on the award presented after Super Bowl V. On January 17, 1971, the Baltimore Colts received the inaugural Vince Lombardi Trophy after defeating the Dallas Cowboys 16-13 in a hard-fought game marked by turnovers and a last-second field goal, marking a transitional moment in the trophy's identity.
Unlike the modern versions, the early trophies for Super Bowls I-IV lacked engravings for Roman numerals on the base, a practice that began with Super Bowl V to distinguish the event from the calendar year and avoid confusion, as the game concludes the prior season's playoffs. From Super Bowl V onward, the base included the "Vince Lombardi Trophy" inscription alongside the NFL shield and the Roman numeral for that edition, standardizing the award's presentation while maintaining Tiffany & Co.'s original football design. This evolution reflected the NFL's growing emphasis on legacy and clarity in its championship symbolism.

Design and Appearance

Physical Specifications

The Vince Lombardi Trophy measures 20.75 inches (52.7 cm) in height and weighs 107.3 ounces (6.7 pounds or 3.0 kg).
It features a regulation-size football cast in sterling silver, mounted upright in a kicking position on a pyramid-like base with three concave sides, all constructed from sterling silver. The football is oriented as if recently kicked, emphasizing motion and triumph in its design.
The base bears engravings of "Vince Lombardi Trophy," the Roman numeral denoting the Super Bowl edition, and the NFL shield. After the Super Bowl, the trophy is returned to its maker for additional hand-engravings, including the winning team's name, the game's date and location, and the final score.
This design has remained unchanged since the trophy's debut in 1967, with Tiffany & Co. producing a new one annually for the NFL champion.

Materials and Craftsmanship

The Vince Lombardi Trophy is constructed entirely from sterling silver, an alloy composed of 92.5% pure silver and 7.5% other metals, primarily copper, which provides durability and a lustrous finish. The football element, designed to replicate a regulation-size NFL ball in kicking position, features meticulously handcrafted details including raised laces and seams for realistic texture. This material choice ensures the trophy's enduring shine and prestige, as sterling silver resists tarnishing when properly maintained.
Exclusively designed and produced by Tiffany & Co. since its inception in 1967 under contract with the NFL, the trophy is handcrafted at the company's Forest Hill manufacturing facility in Cumberland, Rhode Island. The production process involves traditional silversmithing techniques, including repouss for shaping the metal from the reverse side, chasing to refine details, planishing to smooth surfaces with a specialized hammer, and spring hammering for structural integrity. Artisans begin with sheet silver, cutting and forming the base and football components before soldering them together; the football is then hand-cast and engraved with fine details. Each trophy requires approximately four months of production time, encompassing about 72 hours of direct artisan labor spread across multiple stages to achieve precision and quality.
The economic value of the Vince Lombardi Trophy reflects its bespoke craftsmanship, with a reproduction cost estimated at around $50,000 per unit, though the intrinsic material value of the silver is closer to $10,000. This higher production expense accounts for the labor-intensive process and Tiffany & Co.'s expertise, elevating the trophy's insured value beyond its raw components due to its symbolic prestige in NFL history. Winning teams receive only one original trophy, with smaller replicas distributed to players and staff to commemorate the achievement without diluting the exclusivity of the primary award.

Notable Alterations and Incidents

One notable incident involving the Vince Lombardi Trophy occurred in April 2019, shortly after the New England Patriots won Super Bowl LIII. Tight end Rob Gronkowski, while participating in a ceremonial first-pitch event at Fenway Park, used the trophy as an improvised bat to bunt a baseball, resulting in a visible dent on its base. The damage was minor, leaving the trophy otherwise intact, and it was subsequently repaired by Tiffany & Co., the trophy's longtime craftsman, as part of their standard maintenance process for returned Super Bowl awards.
The NFL maintains a policy requiring winning teams to return the trophy to Tiffany & Co. after the season for cleaning, final engraving, and any necessary repairs, ensuring its preservation for permanent possession by the team. This process includes periodic polishing to maintain the sterling silver's luster, though no major design alterations have been made to the trophy since its introduction.
In a more recent development following Super Bowl LIX in February 2025, which the Philadelphia Eagles won 40-22 over the Kansas City Chiefs, the newly crafted Vince Lombardi Trophy was placed on public display at the Pro Football Hall of Fame in Canton, Ohio, in June 2025, featuring standard engravings of the trophy's name, Super Bowl numerals, and the NFL shield on its base. Such displays highlight the trophy's cultural significance, with rare temporary custom engravings occasionally added for special exhibitions at the Hall of Fame.

Presentation and Traditions

Ceremony Protocol

The Vince Lombardi Trophy is presented on the field immediately following the final whistle of the Super Bowl, typically at the 50-yard line, marking the culmination of the game's on-field celebrations. This timing ensures the handover occurs amid the height of the winning team's excitement, with the trophy carried out by attendants as the clock expires.
The presentation is led by the NFL Commissioner, who has been Roger Goodell since 2006, and involves handing the trophy first to the winning team's owner or, at the owner's request, the head coach before it is passed among players and coaches. The Commissioner delivers a brief speech offering congratulations to the victors and highlighting key league achievements from the season. The sequence begins with a confetti drop triggered by the game's end, followed by the winning team forming a huddle at midfield; the owner then accepts the trophy on a stage erected on the field since Super Bowl XXX in 1996, after which players hoist it collectively. The entire ceremony is broadcast live on major networks like CBS, Fox, or NBC, featuring close-up camera shots of the handover and emotional reactions.
The inaugural presentation occurred after Super Bowl I on January 15, 1967, when NFL Commissioner Pete Rozelle handed the then-unnamed trophy to Green Bay Packers head coach Vince Lombardi, an ironic moment given the award's later renaming in his honor following his death in 1970. This set the precedent for the formal protocol that has evolved into a highly anticipated tradition.

Post-Award Handling and Customs

Following the Super Bowl presentation, the Vince Lombardi Trophy is transferred to the winning team's permanent possession, where it remains the property of the organization rather than individual players or staff. The original sterling silver trophy, newly crafted each year by Tiffany & Co., is transported securely back to the team's facilities under NFL oversight, often arriving in a locked case with armed escort to ensure its safety during travel. For instance, after the Philadelphia Eagles' victory in Super Bowl LIX in February 2025, the trophy was delivered to their NovaCare Complex headquarters in South Philadelphia on March 14, 2025, and placed on public display in a dedicated trophy case for fans and former players to view and photograph. Similarly, teams like the New England Patriots house their multiple trophies in on-site halls of fame adjacent to their stadiums, allowing ongoing access for team members and visitors.
A key post-award custom involves victory parades in the team's home city, during which the trophy is prominently carried and displayed by players to celebrate with fans. In Philadelphia's 2018 Super Bowl LII parade following their win over the New England Patriots, quarterback Carson Wentz and other players hoisted the trophy along Broad Street, drawing over 700,000 attendees to the route ending at the Philadelphia Museum of Art. The 2025 parade after Super Bowl LIX echoed this tradition, with MVP Jalen Hurts raising the trophy for cheering crowds during the open-air procession from Lincoln Financial Field to the Art Museum steps, reinforcing communal pride in the championship. Players frequently participate in photo opportunities with the trophy immediately after the game and during these events, but personal ownership is prohibited, as the artifact belongs exclusively to the franchise. To commemorate individual contributions, the NFL distributes smaller sterling silver replicas, measuring approximately 16 inches in height, to players, while teams may commission additional ones for staff.
The trophy also integrates into broader team lore and cultural practices, serving as a tangible emblem in motivational narratives. The Pittsburgh Steelers, for example, referenced their pursuit of a fifth championship as the "One for the Thumb" campaign during the 2005 season, culminating in their Super Bowl XL victory and the addition of another Lombardi Trophy to their collection, which continues to inspire franchise identity. Occasionally, teams loan their trophies to reputable institutions for temporary exhibits, such as the Green Bay Packers lending the original 1967 Vince Lombardi Trophy to the Newark Museum in 2014 for a Tiffany & Co. silver exhibit, or the Pro Football Hall of Fame borrowing official versions for special displays on league history. In cases of damage from handling, the NFL coordinates repairs through Tiffany & Co. to preserve the trophy's integrity.

Significance and Legacy

Symbolism in NFL Culture

The Vince Lombardi Trophy stands as the ultimate emblem of achievement in professional American football, encapsulating the pinnacle of team success and individual excellence within the NFL. Named after the legendary coach who emphasized relentless discipline and preparation, the trophy embodies Lombardi's philosophy that victory stems from unwavering commitment and moral fortitude, often referred to in league narratives as "the prize" that defines a season's culmination.
In broader NFL culture, the trophy permeates media portrayals and advertising, serving as a visual shorthand for triumph and aspiration that fuels fan loyalty and intensifies rivalries across the league. It has appeared prominently in Super Bowl commercials, such as the NFL's 2021 ad recreating Lombardi's likeness via AI to deliver a unifying motivational message amid national challenges, highlighting its role in evoking shared emotional resonance. In pop culture, the trophy inspires depictions of football's high stakes, as seen in films like Any Given Sunday, where the pursuit of championship glorymirroring the quest for the Lombardiunderscores themes of redemption and collective drive, thereby deepening fan devotion to the sport's dramatic narratives.
The trophy also ties into Lombardi's enduring legacy, symbolizing the unity forged during the NFL-AFL merger era, when his Packers victories in the inaugural Super Bowls bridged rival leagues into a cohesive professional landscape. Coaches frequently invoke its significance in motivational contexts, drawing on Lombardi's disciplined ethos to rally players toward excellence, as evidenced by references in pre-game addresses that frame the hardware as a testament to perseverance and teamwork.
As of 2025, the trophy continues to represent resilience in NFL lore, exemplified by the Philadelphia Eagles' dominant 40-22 victory over the Kansas City Chiefs in Super Bowl LIXa rematch avenging their prior defeat and showcasing a comeback through defensive tenacity and strategic renewal.

Teams with the Most Trophies

The Pittsburgh Steelers and New England Patriots share the record for the most Vince Lombardi Trophies, each with six wins as of Super Bowl LIX in 2025. The Steelers achieved their victories in Super Bowls IX (1975), X (1976), XIII (1979), XIV (1980), XL (2006), and XLIII (2009), defining eras of dominance with the "Steel Curtain" defense in the 1970s under coach Chuck Noll and a resurgence in the 2000s led by quarterback Ben Roethlisberger. The Patriots secured theirs in Super Bowls XXXVI (2002), XXXVIII (2004), XXXIX (2005), XLIX (2015), LI (2017), and LIII (2019), fueled by the dynasty orchestrated by quarterback Tom Brady and coach Bill Belichick from the early 2000s through the 2010s, marked by consistent playoff excellence and strategic adaptability.
Following closely are the San Francisco 49ers and Dallas Cowboys, both with five trophies. The 49ers triumphed in Super Bowls XVI (1982), XIX (1985), XXIII (1989), XXIV (1990), and XXIX (1995), establishing a hallmark dynasty in the 1980s and early 1990s under coaches Bill Walsh and George Seifert, emphasizing the West Coast offense and players like Joe Montana and Jerry Rice. The Cowboys won in Super Bowls VI (1972), XII (1978), XXVII (1993), XXVIII (1994), and XXX (1996), with their 1990s runled by quarterback Troy Aikman, running back Emmitt Smith, and wide receiver Michael Irvin under coach Jimmy Johnsonrepresenting one of the NFL's most star-studded eras.
Three teams have claimed four trophies each: the Green Bay Packers, New York Giants, and Kansas City Chiefs. The Philadelphia Eagles have two wins, with their second coming in Super Bowl LIX in 2025. The Packers' wins in Super Bowls I (1967), II (1968), XXXI (1997), and XLV (2011) include the inaugural AFL-NFL championships and a revival under coach Mike Holmgren and quarterback Brett Favre. The Giants succeeded in XXI (1987), XXV (1991), XLII (2008), and XLVI (2012), with upsets like Super Bowl XLII against the undefeated Patriots highlighting their defensive prowess. The Chiefs captured IV (1970), LIV (2020), LVII (2023), and LVIII (2024), building a modern dynasty under coach Andy Reid and quarterback Patrick Mahomes. No team has surpassed six wins, underscoring the rarity of sustained excellence in the NFL, where the Steelers hold an edge with eight total Super Bowl appearances compared to the Patriots' 11, though both exemplify multi-decade impact through coaching stability and roster talent.

Impact on Super Bowl History

The presentation of the Vince Lombardi Trophy following Super Bowl III on January 12, 1969, marked a turning point in NFL history, as the New York Jets' 16-7 upset victory over the Baltimore Colts represented the first AFL win in the championship game, validating the merger between the leagues and paving the way for a unified NFL structure. This underdog triumph, led by quarterback Joe Namath, shifted perceptions of competitive balance and boosted the event's prestige as a true national showdown. Similarly, the trophy's awarding after Super Bowl VI on January 16, 1972, to the Dallas Cowboys for their 24-3 domination of the Miami Dolphins signaled the emergence of NFC supremacy in the post-merger era, with the Cowboys' first championship establishing a dynasty that influenced conference alignments and playoff dynamics for decades.
Trophy presentations have since commemorated key league milestones, including the introduction of the salary cap in 1994, which reshaped roster construction and competitive parity during Super Bowls in the free-agency era, and the NFL's international expansion efforts, such as regular-season games abroad starting in 2007, with discussions of hosting a Super Bowl overseas to further globalize the sport. The renaming of the trophy in 1971following Vince Lombardi's death in September 1970elevated its symbolic status, transforming it from a generic championship award into an emblem of excellence that enhanced the Super Bowl's allure and commercial value.
In terms of records, the Vince Lombardi Trophy has been lifted after some of the most lopsided Super Bowl victories, such as the San Francisco 49ers' 55-10 rout of the Denver Broncos in Super Bowl XXIV on January 28, 1990, which remains the largest margin of victory and underscored offensive firepower in the late 1980s. The trophy's history also reflects shifting eras between defensive and offensive dominance; early Super Bowls like VI emphasized stifling defenses, while later ones, such as XXIV, highlighted high-scoring attacks, with no single approach guaranteeing success but defenses holding a slight edge in championships overall.
The most recent presentation after Super Bowl LIX on February 9, 2025, where the Philadelphia Eagles defeated the (/page/Kansas City Chiefs) 40-22 to thwart a potential three-peat, showcased a defensive masterclass that pressured quarterback Patrick Mahomes into six sacks without excessive blitzing, influencing subsequent strategies by emphasizing trench warfare and coverage schemes to neutralize elite offenses. This outcome, driven by the Eagles' front four, has prompted teams to prioritize interior defensive line investments in the post-2025 offseason, signaling a potential resurgence of ground-and-pound defensive tactics league-wide.
