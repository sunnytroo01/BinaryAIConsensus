American Board of Internal Medicine

 The American Board of Internal Medicine (ABIM) is a physician-led, non-profit organization founded in 1936 to certify internists and subspecialists through examinations that assess knowledge, skills, and professional attitudes essential for high-quality patient care. Its mission centers on enhancing health care quality by establishing and maintaining standards for board certification in internal medicine and 20 subspecialties, including cardiology, oncology, and rheumatology.
ABIM's certification process begins with initial exams following accredited residency or fellowship training, followed by the Maintenance of Certification (MOC) program, which requires periodic assessments, practice improvement activities, and lifelong learning to demonstrate ongoing competence. While proponents argue that these requirements promote evidence-based practice and patient safety, the MOC program has drawn substantial criticism for imposing burdensome, time-consuming obligations with limited proven benefits to clinical outcomes, exacerbating physician burnout, and generating revenue for ABIM without clear justification.
Notable controversies include antitrust lawsuits accusing ABIM of monopolistic practices by tying initial certification to mandatory, fee-based MOC, which plaintiffs claim stifles competition and prioritizes organizational profits over physician autonomy. In response to physician feedback, ABIM has modified MOC elements, such as offering longitudinal assessments and reducing certain exam frequencies, though skepticism persists regarding the program's empirical value and alignment with real-world medical demands.

History

Founding and Early Development

The American Board of Internal Medicine (ABIM) was incorporated on February 28, 1936, as a nonprofit organization dedicated to establishing uniform standards for the certification of physicians specializing in internal medicine. This initiative responded to growing public and professional demands for formalized credentialing amid the expansion of medical specialization in the United States, building on precedents set by earlier specialty boards such as ophthalmology (1917) and otolaryngology (1924). The board was formed through collaboration between the American Medical Association (AMA) and the American College of Physicians (ACP), reflecting a concerted effort to elevate professional competence in internal medicine without governmental mandate.
Organization of the ABIM was finalized on June 15, 1936, with Walter L. Bierring, M.D., elected as its first president. The board's initial focus centered on developing rigorous examination processes to assess physicians' knowledge and clinical acumen in internal medicine, emphasizing voluntary certification as a marker of expertise rather than a licensing requirement. In December 1936, ABIM administered its inaugural Internal Medicine Certification Examination, marking the beginning of systematic credentialing in the field.30749-6/pdf) Early certifications were granted for life, without periodic renewal, to encourage broad participation among qualified internists.
In its formative years through the late 1930s and 1940s, ABIM prioritized standardizing training prerequisites and oral examinations, often involving case-based assessments conducted by expert panels. The board's efforts aligned with the broader formation of the American Board of Medical Specialties (ABMS) in 1933, which provided oversight for emerging certifying entities like ABIM to ensure consistency across disciplines. By certifying hundreds of physicians annually in its first decade, ABIM contributed to professionalizing internal medicine, fostering trust in specialists amid rising healthcare complexity post-Depression and during World War II, though it faced challenges in defining scope amid evolving diagnostic technologies.

Expansion into Subspecialties

The American Board of Internal Medicine (ABIM), founded in 1936 to certify physicians in general internal medicine, began expanding into subspecialty certification in response to the growing complexity and differentiation within the field. This expansion formalized standards for advanced training and expertise in specific areas of internal medicine, enabling board certification beyond foundational internal medicine residency. The first subspecialty examinations were introduced in 1941, targeting cardiovascular disease, gastroenterology, and pulmonary disease, which addressed emerging clinical needs driven by wartime medical advancements and diagnostic progress.
A significant wave of subspecialty certifications followed in the early 1970s, reflecting the maturation of fellowship training programs and the proliferation of specialized knowledge. In 1972, ABIM launched examinations in endocrinology, diabetes and metabolism; hematology; infectious disease; nephrology; and rheumatology. The subsequent year, 1973, saw the addition of medical oncology. These developments aligned with the establishment of dedicated subspecialty training pathways under the Accreditation Council for Graduate Medical Education (ACGME) precursors, ensuring certified physicians demonstrated proficiency through rigorous examinations.
Further expansions occurred sporadically through the late 20th and early 21st centuries, incorporating interdisciplinary and procedure-oriented subspecialties:

1987: Critical Care Medicine (initially with time-limited certificates).

1988: Geriatric Medicine (time-limited certificates).

1992: Clinical Cardiac Electrophysiology.

1993: Sports Medicine (offered until 2022).

1994: Adolescent Medicine.

1999: Interventional Cardiology.

2006: Transplant Hepatology.

2007: Sleep Medicine.

2008: Hospice and Palliative Medicine.

2010: Advanced Heart Failure and Transplant Cardiology; Focused Practice in Hospital Medicine (certificates discontinued after 2023).

This progression marked a shift toward time-limited certifications starting in the late 1980s for select subspecialties, with full implementation across all new certificates by 1990, requiring periodic reassessment to verify ongoing competence. By 2024, ABIM offered certifications in over 20 subspecialties, with planned relaunches in gastroenterology, hematology, and medical oncology set for 2026 to replace prior versions. The expansion has certified tens of thousands of subspecialists, though it has coincided with debates over workforce distribution, as internal medicine trainees increasingly pursued fellowshipsrising from approximately 50% in the 1970s to over 70% by the 2010spotentially contributing to primary care shortages.30134-1/fulltext)

Evolution of Recertification Policies

Originally, ABIM certification was granted as a lifetime credential, with no requirement for periodic renewal or reassessment following initial board examination. This approach prevailed from ABIM's founding in 1936 until 1990, reflecting the era's assumption that once achieved, board-level competence in internal medicine persisted indefinitely without formal verification.30749-6/fulltext)
In 1990, ABIM shifted to time-limited certificates valid for ten years, mandating recertification through a secure examination to demonstrate ongoing knowledge amid rapid advances in medical science.30749-6/fulltext) Physicians certified prior to this date retained lifetime status but faced incentives to participate in voluntary recertification. The policy applied to all new certifications thereafter, with recertification involving a high-stakes, proctored exam administered every decade, alongside attestations of licensure and good standing.
By the early 2000s, recertification evolved into the broader Maintenance of Certification (MOC) framework, incorporating not only periodic knowledge assessment but also modules on practice performance, patient safety, and professionalism to address criticisms that exam-only renewal inadequately ensured comprehensive competence. ABIM formalized MOC as a continuous process, requiring accumulation of points through approved activities, with a traditional ten-year exam as one component.
Significant enhancements occurred in 2014, when ABIM launched an updated MOC program applicable to all diplomates, including those with lifetime certificates, shifting from discrete ten-year cycles to ongoing requirements such as at least one MOC activity every two years and 100 points over five years, alongside public reporting of compliance status.00802-6/fulltext) This change aimed to foster lifelong learning but drew physician backlash for increasing administrative burden without proven links to improved outcomes.
Subsequent refinements included the 2018 introduction of the Longitudinal Knowledge Assessment (LKA), a quarterly online quiz option replacing the traditional exam for certain specialties, allowing feedback and remediation to reduce high-stakes testing. In 2024, ABIM eliminated the biennial activity mandate, streamlining to five-year point accrual and decennial assessment, responding to feedback on flexibility. These iterations reflect ABIM's adaptation to evidence of knowledge decay and practice evolution, though empirical validation of MOC's causal impact on physician performance remains limited.

Organizational Structure and Governance

Board Composition and Leadership

The American Board of Internal Medicine (ABIM) Board of Directors consists primarily of board-certified physicians, with at least 75% required to have current clinical practice experience and 30% possessing ABIM assessment development experience; up to 20% may be non-internists to incorporate broader perspectives. All physician directors must satisfy Maintenance of Certification (MOC) requirements and adhere to ABIM's academic and financial conflict-of-interest policies, ensuring alignment with certification standards while mitigating potential biases in decision-making. The board includes public members, such as patient advocates and interdisciplinary professionals, to reflect diverse stakeholder inputs, comprising a total of approximately 14-16 members as structured in recent governance updates. This composition emphasizes practicing clinicians from internal medicine subspecialties like cardiology, infectious diseases, nephrology, and oncology, alongside non-physician experts in areas such as population health and health information technology.
ABIM's governance has evolved to include non-physicians and non-internists across its Board of Directors, Council, and Specialty Boards, aiming for representation across demographics including age, practice setting, region, race, ethnicity, and subspecialty, while incorporating patient and caregiver perspectives. Specialty Boards, which support the main board in defining certification for subspecialties, typically feature 8-10 members each, blending physicians with public representatives to oversee training requirements and assessment policies. The Board of Directors oversees four standing committeesGovernance, Finance, Audit, Risk & Compliance, and Executiveto handle strategic, fiduciary, and operational duties.
Leadership of the Board is headed by a Chair, currently Robert O. Roswell, MD, a cardiologist with expertise in equity-focused care, who shapes overall strategy and policy for assessments in internal medicine and subspecialties. The President and Chief Executive Officer, Furman S. McDonald, MD, MPH, serves ex officio on the board, directing executive operations including governance evolution and certification program implementation, drawing from his background in graduate medical education. Other key executive roles include Chief Medical Officer Laura L. Sessums, JD, MD, who oversees clinical policy alignment. Recent appointments, such as Kathleen Noonan, JD, effective September 9, 2025, as a non-physician member focused on complex care coalitions, illustrate ongoing efforts to diversify leadership with operational experts from outside traditional medicine.

Funding Model and Financial Operations

The American Board of Internal Medicine (ABIM) operates as a nonprofit organization primarily funded through fees paid by physicians for initial certification examinations, subspecialty certifications, and participation in the Maintenance of Certification (MOC) program. In fiscal year 2024 (ending June 2024), ABIM reported total revenue of $105.8 million, with approximately 92.9% ($98.3 million) derived from program service revenue, predominantly certification and MOC fees, while the remainder included $6.9 million in investment income and minor other sources. ABIM explicitly states it receives no funding from industry sources, relying exclusively on these physician-paid fees to support its certification activities.
Specific fee structures include an annual MOC enrollment fee of $220 for the first certificate and $120 for each additional certificate, with exam fees varying by specialty but contributing to the overall certification revenue stream, which accounted for 34% of total revenue ($36 million) in FY 2024. MOC-related fees formed the largest portion at 59% ($62.4 million), reflecting the program's scale among approximately 200,000 certified internists and subspecialists. Revenue recognition follows accrual-based accounting under GAAP, with significant deferred revenuecomprising much of ABIM's $167 million in liabilitiesarising from prepaid fees that are recognized over time as services (e.g., exams or MOC credits) are provided. This model has enabled ABIM to accumulate substantial assets of $224 million by FY 2024, including investments that generate supplementary income without reliance on external grants or donations.
Financial operations emphasize operational efficiency and transparency, with total expenses of $69.4 million in FY 2024 directed toward core activities such as exam delivery (25%, $17.4 million), question writing (19%, $13.2 million), research and innovations (15%, $10.4 million), and administrative functions like finance, HR, and communications (14% each). Compensation for employees and executives represented a notable portion, with other salaries and wages at $26.4 million (38% of expenses) and executive pay at $5 million (7.2%). ABIM maintains fiscal accountability through annual IRS Form 990 filings, audited consolidated financial statements (including the related ABIM Foundation), and public disclosures of revenue breakdowns, ensuring oversight as a 501(c)(3) entity without profit distribution. These practices support ongoing program development, psychometric analysis, and physician assessment, though the organization's large reserves have drawn scrutiny in broader debates over certification costs.

Certification Processes

Initial Certification Requirements

To qualify for initial certification in internal medicine, candidates must complete accredited predoctoral medical education, followed by 36 calendar months of residency training in an Accreditation Council for Graduate Medical Education (ACGME)-accredited program, or equivalent Canadian accreditation through the Royal College of Physicians and Surgeons of Canada (RCPSC) or College of Family Physicians of Canada (CFPC). For U.S. and Canadian medical school graduates, predoctoral education must be from a school accredited by the Liaison Committee on Medical Education (LCME), Committee on Accreditation of Canadian Medical Schools (CACMS), or American Osteopathic Association (AOA); international medical graduates require an Educational Commission for Foreign Medical Graduates (ECFMG) certificate or comparable Medical Council of Canada (MCC) credentials.
Residency training must encompass at least 30 months dedicated to general internal medicine, subspecialty internal medicine, and emergency medicine, with allowances for up to 3 months of approved electives and up to 3 months of vacation or leave; deficits of 35 days or fewer may be excused upon program director attestation. Of the total training, candidates require 24 months of direct patient responsibility, including at least 6 months during the first postgraduate year (R-1). Clinical competence in proceduressuch as obtaining informed consent, maintaining a sterile field, applying local anesthetics, and performing basic bedside proceduresmust be demonstrated, with attestation from the program director. Up to 12 months of credit may be granted for prior training in another American Board of Medical Specialties (ABMS) specialty or foreign residency, subject to program director petition and a $300 fee.
Candidates must hold a valid, unrestricted, and unchallenged medical license to practice in a U.S. state, territory, or Canada, or in their country of practice if abroad; any restrictions, suspensions, revocations, or voluntary surrenders disqualify eligibility. The program director, who must be ABIM-certified, verifies satisfactory performance across the six ACGME/ABMS core competencies (patient care, medical knowledge, practice-based learning, interpersonal skills, professionalism, and systems-based practice) and overall clinical competence via ABIM's FasTrack system. 
Final eligibility culminates in passing the ABIM Internal Medicine Certification Examination, a computer-based test assessing knowledge, diagnostic reasoning, and clinical judgment in broad internal medicine domains. Board eligibility, valid for up to 7 years post-training completion (or 3 years if training ends before July 1, 2013), expires if the exam is not passed, potentially requiring retraining approved by ABIM.

Subspecialty Certifications

The American Board of Internal Medicine (ABIM) offers certification in approximately 20 subspecialties of internal medicine to verify physicians' advanced expertise in focused clinical domains. Eligibility for initial subspecialty certification mandates current ABIM certification in internal medicine, completion of fellowship training in an Accreditation Council for Graduate Medical Education (ACGME)-accredited program with durations typically ranging from 2 to 3 years (e.g., 3 years for cardiovascular disease, 2 years for infectious disease), demonstration of clinical competence through evaluations by program directors and peers, possession of an unrestricted state medical license, and passing a secure, computer-based examination testing subspecialty-specific knowledge.
Examinations are offered annually or biennially depending on the subspecialty, with content blueprints published by ABIM to outline assessed topics such as disease pathophysiology, diagnostics, therapeutics, and ethical considerations. Some subspecialties, like interventional cardiology, require additional procedural training and volume thresholds (e.g., minimum cases performed under supervision). Combined training pathways exist for certain areas, such as pulmonary disease and critical care medicine, allowing integrated fellowships to meet dual certification requirements within 3 years.

Maintenance of Certification (MOC) Program

The ABIM Maintenance of Certification (MOC) program mandates that certified internists and subspecialists demonstrate ongoing competence through structured activities, replacing the prior model of time-limited certificates with one emphasizing continuous professional engagement since its major redesign in 2014. Diplomates must hold an unrestricted, active medical license at all times and enroll in MOC to retain certification status. The program aligns with the American Board of Medical Specialties (ABMS) framework, incorporating elements of professionalism, self-assessment, cognitive testing, and practice improvement, though ABIM operationalizes these primarily via a points-based system and assessments.
Core requirements include earning 100 MOC points every five years via approved activities, with points allocated across categories such as medical knowledge (e.g., self-assessment modules or CME with assessment), patient safety, practice assessment (e.g., quality improvement projects), and interpersonal communication. At least one MOC activity must contribute to the five-year cycle, but as of October 28, 2024, the prior mandate to complete activities or earn points biennially was eliminated following physician surveys citing administrative burden without proportional benefit. Additionally, diplomates must pass a secure knowledge assessment, traditionally a proctored exam every ten years, though options like the Longitudinal Knowledge Assessment (LKA)quarterly online questions with pass/fail based on performancehave been introduced for select specialties to reduce high-stakes testing frequency. For interventional cardiology, an annual attestation of procedural volume and outcomes reporting is required.
MOC activities are vetted for relevance, with points awarded upon completion and verification through ABIM's Physician Portal, where progress is tracked. An annual participation fee of $410 per certification (as of 2023 updates, with multi-certification discounts) supports program administration, separate from exam fees ranging from $1,200 to $2,300 depending on format and specialty. Empirical data link sustained MOC participation to measurable physician performance gains, including 18.91% lower patient mortality rates and superior adherence to Healthcare Effectiveness Data and Information Set (HEDIS) quality metrics compared to lapsed certificants. Noncompliance results in transition to "Certified, Not Participating in MOC" status, potentially affecting privileges like hospital credentialing, though certification lapses do not directly revoke licensure.

Evidence of Impact and Efficacy

Links to Physician Performance and Patient Outcomes

A 2022 analysis of Medicare claims data from 2008 to 2017 revealed that patients treated by internal medicine physicians who achieved initial ABIM certification experienced a 15.87% relative decrease in 30-day mortality rates compared to those treated by non-certified physicians, after adjusting for patient and hospital factors. This association persisted across various subgroups, suggesting that initial certification may proxy for knowledge and skills contributing to lower mortality risks, though the study emphasized correlational rather than causal inference due to potential confounding by physician selection into certification.
Subsequent research in 2024 examined outcomes for hospitalized Medicare patients under newly trained internal medicine hospitalists from 2013 to 2019, linking higher ABIM initial certification examination scores to improved patient results. Physicians scoring in the top quartile on the exam saw an 8% reduction in the composite risk of seven-day patient mortality or readmission, equivalent to approximately 1.5 fewer adverse events per 100 patients treated. In contrast, residency milestone ratings showed no such association, indicating that standardized exam performance better predicts real-world outcomes than subjective evaluations. These findings, drawn from over 200,000 hospitalizations, imply that certification exams capture cognitive abilities translating to fewer deaths and readmissions, but limitations include reliance on observational data and inability to isolate exam-specific causal effects from overall training quality.
For maintenance of certification (MOC), evidence ties performance on ABIM assessments to outcomes. A study of the Longitudinal Knowledge Assessment (LKA), introduced in 2020 as an MOC alternative to traditional exams, found that internists scoring in the top 25% on quarterly LKA progress reports had patients with an 8% lower seven-day post-discharge mortality rate and reduced readmissions compared to those scoring lower, based on 2021-2022 Medicare data. An earlier 2007 investigation of MOC exam scores among general internists linked higher scores to increased adherence to evidence-based processes of care for elderly Medicare beneficiaries, such as appropriate prescribing and preventive services, though direct outcome metrics like mortality were not the primary focus. Critics, however, argue that while initial certification shows clearer links to performance, MOC's incremental benefits remain under-evidenced in randomized or causal designs, with some analyses highlighting persistent gaps in demonstrating substantial improvements in patient care beyond baseline certification. Overall, empirical associations favor certified physicians delivering superior outcomes, but methodological challenges in disentangling certification from other physician traits underscore the need for ongoing causal research.

Research on Certification Value

Studies have identified associations between American Board of Internal Medicine (ABIM) initial certification and improved patient outcomes, though these findings reflect correlations rather than proven causation. For instance, analysis of Medicare data from 2014 to 2019 showed that patients treated by internists with initial ABIM certification experienced a 15.87% lower mortality rate compared to those treated by non-certified physicians, after adjusting for patient and hospital factors. Similarly, higher scores on ABIM certification examinations among newly trained hospitalists were linked to an 8% reduction in patient mortality and readmissions within 30 days of hospital discharge, based on a 2024 study of over 62,000 hospitalizations. These results suggest that certification exams may capture knowledge predictive of clinical performance, but residency milestone ratings showed no such association, indicating exams as a stronger signal.
Research on maintenance of certification (MOC) yields mixed evidence of value. Internists maintaining ABIM certification for 20 years or more demonstrated higher adherence to Healthcare Effectiveness Data and Information Set (HEDIS) process-of-care measures, such as diabetes and hypertension management, compared to those who did not, per a study funded by ABIM using claims data from 2006 to 2014. Additionally, physicians scoring in the top quartile on ABIM's Longitudinal Knowledge Assessment (LKA) exhibited an 8% lower seven-day readmission rate, according to 2025 ABMS research. However, critics argue that much of the supporting evidence derives from ABIM-sponsored studies, potentially introducing bias, and lacks rigorous randomized controlled trials to establish causality between MOC participation and outcomes.
Overall, while initial certification correlates with measurable benefits in mortality and quality metrics, the incremental value of ongoing MOC remains empirically contested, with sparse high-quality data isolating its effects from selection bias or other physician traits. Independent replication and longitudinal designs beyond process measures are needed to clarify causal impacts.

Controversies and Criticisms

Burdens and Questioned Value of MOC

The Maintenance of Certification (MOC) program imposes significant financial costs on physicians, with an annual fee of $220 for the first certificate and $120 for each additional one, alongside examination and activity fees that result in an average total expense of $23,607 over 10 years for board-certified internal medicine physicians. These costs, combined with preparation time for exams and required activities such as patient safety modules and practice improvement projects, represent an opportunity cost for busy clinicians who must divert hours from patient care or rest. Surveys and physician feedback highlight this as a substantial time burden, with many reporting that MOC requirements exacerbate burnout and contribute to early retirement or exodus from the profession.
Critics, including medical societies like the Infectious Diseases Society of America, have questioned the clinical relevance of MOC elements such as the points system and decennial exams, arguing they add unnecessary layers of administrative hassle without commensurate benefits. In a 2015 New England Journal of Medicine perspective, oncologist Howard West contended that MOC fails to demonstrably enhance physician knowledge or patient care while increasing fees and testing frequency, urging a focus on efficacy over revenue generation. Empirical studies on MOC's impact reveal sparse evidence of improved outcomes; for instance, a systematic review found that clinical benefits were observed in patients of only 39% of participating physicians, with methodological limitations in linking certification maintenance to reduced mortality or readmissions.
Although ABIM promotes MOC as a means to ensure ongoing competence, independent analyses indicate weak causal connections to superior performance metrics, with critics noting that associations in ABIM-funded research often rely on correlative data prone to confounding factors like selection bias among certified physicians. Physicians have voiced frustration over "grandfathered" exemptions for pre-1990 certificants, which underscore perceived inequities and question the program's necessity for lifelong practitioners already subject to continuous medical education and licensure scrutiny. In response to backlash, ABIM acknowledged errors in 2015, admitting the program had become overly burdensome without sufficient validation of its value.

Wikipedia Editing and Narrative Control Incident

In early 2016, amid widespread physician backlash against the American Board of Internal Medicine's (ABIM) Maintenance of Certification (MOC) programincluding accusations of financial opacity and undue financial burdens on practitionersthe ABIM partnered with Consumer Reports to offer a four-week online training series on Wikipedia editing specifically for its staff and affiliates. The sessions, scheduled on January 13, January 20, January 27, and February 3, focused on topics such as Wikipedia's coverage of health information, basic editing protocols, content quality assurance mechanisms, and broader Wikimedia ecosystem dynamics, with an emphasis on sourcing from published materials rather than hands-on technical tutorials. This initiative coincided with heightened scrutiny of ABIM, as detailed in a April 2015 Newsweek expos that documented the organization's expenditure of over $2 million on international board retreats featuring luxury accommodations and entertainment, funded in part by MOC-related revenues exceeding $200 million annually, while critics argued the program lacked empirical evidence linking it to improved patient outcomes.
Physician critics, including a coalition of prominent internists who had publicly revolted against MOC's escalating requirementssuch as mandatory high-stakes exams every 10 years costing up to $4,000 per cycle plus additional practice improvement modulesinterpreted the Wikipedia training as a strategic bid to influence encyclopedic depictions of ABIM's practices and the attendant controversies. Blogs and commentary from MOC opponents, such as cardiologist Wes Fisher, highlighted ABIM's broader pattern of defensive responses to dissent, including legal actions against alleged exam leakers and assertions of proprietary control over certification narratives, suggesting the editing program could enable subtle alterations to articles on certification efficacy or institutional finances without transparent disclosure. Such involvement evoked parallels to Wikipedia's documented challenges with conflict-of-interest editing, where organizational insiders risk introducing bias favoring self-presentation over neutral sourcing, though no specific ABIM-linked edits were publicly adjudicated as violations by Wikimedia oversight at the time. The episode underscored tensions between professional bodies seeking to steward their digital legacy and the encyclopedic mandate for impartiality grounded in verifiable, diverse evidence.

Antitrust and Legal Challenges

In December 2018, four internists filed a class-action antitrust lawsuit against the American Board of Internal Medicine (ABIM) in the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Pennsylvania, alleging that ABIM violated Sections 1 and 2 of the Sherman Act by tying initial board certification to its Maintenance of Certification (MOC) program, thereby monopolizing over 95% of the market for internal medicine recertification and charging inflated fees without evidence of superior quality. The plaintiffs claimed ABIM refused to recognize alternative certifications, such as those from the National Board of Physicians and Surgeons (NBPAS), and imposed burdensome MOC requirements that lacked proven links to improved patient outcomes, projecting compliance costs exceeding $50,000 per physician over a decade. ABIM countered that MOC provides verifiable value in ensuring physician competence and that no illegal tying existed, as initial certification and MOC serve distinct purposes.
On September 30, 2019, the district court dismissed the case with prejudice, ruling that the plaintiffs failed to plead a cognizable antitrust injury, as MOC did not unlawfully condition initial certification and ABIM's market dominance did not constitute illegal monopolization without predatory conduct. The U.S. Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit affirmed the dismissal in February 2021, holding that the plaintiffs lacked antitrust standing due to insufficient evidence of harm from anticompetitive practices and rejecting related RICO claims, as ABIM's policies did not constitute a pattern of racketeering.
Subsequent litigation has persisted, including a 2023 lawsuit by the Association of American Physicians and Surgeons Educational Foundation (AAPS) against ABIM and other boards, alleging antitrust violations through coercive MOC mandates tied to hospital privileges and insurance reimbursements, harming competition and physician autonomy. The U.S. District Court for the Southern District of Texas initially dismissed the claims in 2023 for lack of antitrust injury and standing, but on June 3, 2024, the Fifth Circuit reversed, finding the complaint adequately alleged injury from ABIM's alleged monopoly maintenance via exclusionary practices, remanding for further proceedings on whether such conduct violated the Sherman Act.
Advocacy groups like Practicing Physicians of America have supported multiple class-action antitrust suits since 2018, arguing ABIM's dominance stifles innovation in certification alternatives and imposes unproven, costly barriers, with calls in 2024 for physicians to report ABIM's parent entity, the American Board of Medical Specialties (ABMS), to the Department of Justice for anticompetitive conduct. These challenges highlight ongoing debates over whether ABIM's near-monopolystemming from its foundational role in internal medicine certification since 1936reflects earned market power or barriers that prioritize revenue over evidence-based physician assessment, though courts have variably required proof of consumer harm beyond dissatisfaction with fees or requirements.

Reforms and Recent Adaptations

Post-2015 Changes to MOC

In February 2015, the American Board of Internal Medicine (ABIM) suspended several requirements it had introduced in its 2014 MOC program overhaul, including Practice Assessment, Patient Voice, and Patient Safety components, for at least two years; ABIM stated this pause allowed time to assess their implementation and address physician feedback on unintended burdens. These suspensions followed widespread criticism from internists, who argued the additions lacked evidence of improving patient care and imposed excessive administrative demands without clear value.
ABIM also revised its Internal Medicine MOC examination blueprint starting with the Fall 2015 administration, incorporating practice-based feedback to emphasize current clinical activities over outdated content, as determined through surveys of practicing internists. Subspecialty blueprints received similar updates, with ABIM committing to ongoing physician input on content weighting and relevance.
In July 2015, ABIM discontinued the mandate for subspecialty-certified physicians to maintain active certification in an underlying primary specialty, exempting those scheduled for Fall 2015 exams in foundational areas from the obligation if it solely supported subspecialty status. This change reduced redundant testing for diplomates holding multiple certifications.
ABIM further froze MOC enrollment fees at 2014 levels through at least 2017, extending the cap potentially longer, in recognition of cost-related objections raised by the physician community. By 2016, ABIM expanded collaborative efforts, soliciting subspecialty-specific feedback on exam blueprints to refine question distribution and ensure alignment with real-world practice demands. These adjustments aimed to mitigate criticisms of the program's rigidity, though surveys indicated persistent skepticism among physicians regarding its overall efficacy and resource demands.

2024 Policy Eliminations and Ongoing Responses

In October 2024, the American Board of Internal Medicine (ABIM) eliminated the requirement for physicians to earn Maintenance of Certification (MOC) points every two years to maintain "participating" status in the program. This biennial points threshold, previously set at a minimum number of activities to demonstrate ongoing engagement, had been criticized by physicians as confusing, administratively burdensome, and disconnected from meaningful professional development. The change took effect immediately upon announcement on October 28, 2024, restoring certification eligibility for approximately 12,000 physicians who had lapsed due to non-compliance with the rule.
ABIM attributed the elimination to direct feedback from physicians via surveys, town halls, and advisory committees, emphasizing a commitment to reducing administrative workload while preserving core MOC elements like knowledge assessments and patient safety modules. Officials stated the policy aimed to simplify participation tracking, allowing physicians greater flexibility in completing activities over the full 10-year certification cycle without intermediate penalties. However, critics, including specialty societies like the American Society of Hematology, continue to argue that broader MOC reforms are needed, such as eliminating the overall five-year points mandate or increasing exam time allocations, viewing the 2024 change as a partial concession amid persistent concerns over program value and opportunity costs.
Ongoing responses include ABIM's monitoring of post-elimination participation rates and further solicitations for input through 2025, with no additional policy removals announced as of late 2024. Physician advocacy groups have welcomed the adjustment but persist in pushing for evidence-based validation of MOC's impact on care quality, citing independent analyses questioning its correlation with outcomes. ABIM has indicated potential refinements to activity credits and assessment formats in response to evolving feedback, though core requirements for recertification remain intact.
