 The 2007 Rugby World Cup was the sixth edition of the quadrennial international rugby union tournament organized by World Rugby, held primarily in France from 7 September to 20 October 2007, featuring 20 national teams competing in four pools of five followed by a knockout stage, and concluding with South Africa's 156 defeat of England in the final at the Stade de France in Saint-Denis.
The tournament spanned 48 matches across 13 venues in four countries, with 42 games in ten French cities including Paris, Lyon, and Marseille, plus additional fixtures in Cardiff (Wales), Edinburgh (Scotland), and Dublin (Ireland) to accommodate demand and logistical needs. It attracted a record total attendance of 2,263,223 spectators, averaging 47,150 per match, and reached a global television audience exceeding 4 billion viewers, marking it as the most watched rugby event to date.
South Africa's triumph secured their second World Cup title, following their 1995 victory, and was highlighted by a robust defensive performance throughout the knockout rounds as well as Bryan Habana's eight tries, which levelled a Rugby World Cup record, including a 3713 semi-final win over Argentina and quarter-final dominance against Fiji (3720). England, as defending champions from 2003, staged a remarkable comeback by edging Australia 1210 in the quarter-finals and upsetting hosts France 149 in the semi-finals to reach their second consecutive final. Notable upsets included France's iconic 2018 quarter-final victory over pre-tournament favorites New Zealandoften regarded as one of the biggest upsets in Rugby World Cup historywhile Argentina finished third with a 3410 bronze medal victory over France, showcasing their rising prowess.
The event generated a net surplus of 122.4 million for World Rugby and delivered an estimated 2.1 billion economic boost to France through tourism, infrastructure, and related spending, while promoting rugby's global growth with debuts from teams like Portugal and Georgia. It also featured innovative formats like four-try and losing bonus points in the pool stage to encourage attacking play, though the tournament was characterized by low-scoring, gritty matches in the later stages, with the final producing just one try.

Background

Bids process

The International Rugby Board (IRB) announced the bidding process for the 2007 Rugby World Cup in 2001, releasing the official tender documents on 31 October to invite formal proposals from interested unions.
Two primary bids emerged: a standalone proposal from the French Rugby Federation and a joint bid led by the Rugby Football Union of England, which incorporated venues and support from the Welsh Rugby Union, Scottish Rugby Union, and Irish Rugby Football Union. The English-led bid emphasized cross-border collaboration to leverage existing infrastructure in the British Isles, while the French bid focused on centralized hosting with select international matches.
The IRB evaluated the submissions based on operational criteria such as stadium infrastructure and fan experience, alongside financial aspects including revenue potential and contributions to global rugby development and promotion. Both bids met core requirements, but the process involved an initial rejection of preliminary versions in November 2002, prompting revisions before final consideration.
On 10 April 2003, the IRB Council voted 18-3 in Dublin to award hosting rights to France, citing the bid's stronger alignment with long-term strategic goals for the sport's worldwide growth. The successful French proposal committed to utilizing 10 stadiums across major cities including Paris, Marseille, Lyon, and Toulouse, while allocating 10 of the 48 matches to venues in Wales, Scotland, and Ireland to broaden accessibility; it also incorporated robust security protocols as part of its operational framework to ensure safe event delivery.

Qualification

The qualification process for the 2007 Rugby World Cup granted automatic entry to the eight quarter-finalists from the 2003 edition: Australia, England, France, Ireland, New Zealand, Scotland, South Africa, and Wales. The host nation France was already included in this group. These teams formed the core of the 20-nation field, with the remaining 12 places determined through regional tournaments involving 86 other nations across five confederations.
Qualifying matches commenced in September 2004 with an encounter between Andorra and Norway and spanned over 190 fixtures until June 2007, when Portugal sealed the final spot. The process emphasized regional development, with pathways designed to reward performance in confederation-specific competitions while incorporating a repechage stage for additional opportunities. In Asia, the tournament planned for Colombo, Sri Lanka, in November 2006 was cancelled due to security concerns amid the country's civil unrest, leading to rescheduling and alternative arrangements for the affected matches.
Europe's qualification, involving around 36 teams, was closely tied to the FIRA European Nations Cup 20062007 across multiple divisions. Georgia qualified directly by defeating Portugal in a two-legged playoff (173 in Tbilisi and 1111 in Lisbon), marking their second successive World Cup appearance. Italy and Romania secured spots through strong showings in higher divisions, reflecting their established status in continental play.
In the Americas, four spots were contested via the Pan American Rugby Association's qualification rounds, including round-robin formats and playoffs. Argentina earned progression as Americas 1, while Canada and the United States advanced as Americas 2 and 3 by topping their respective groups in the Americas Rugby Championship. The fourth Americas representative, Uruguay, headed to repechage.
Africa's single direct spot went to Namibia, who overcame a challenging path by defeating Zimbabwe in earlier rounds and then Morocco 278 in the final qualifier in Casablanca. This marked Namibia's third consecutive World Cup qualification.
Asia's pathway featured the Asian Rugby Football Union tournaments across three rounds, with Japan clinching the direct spot by winning key matches against regional rivals like the Arabian Gulf and Korea.
Oceania allocated two additional spots through a series of Pacific qualifiers, including the Pacific Tri-Nations. Fiji and Samoa qualified directly as Oceania 1 and 2 via victories in round-robin play, while Tonga progressed to repechage following competitive results against other island nations in the Pacific Tri-Nations.
The repechage provided two final opportunities, pitting runners-up from the regional processes against each other in separate playoffs. Tonga qualified by defeating Korea 4217 on aggregate in the Oceania-Asia playoff. Portugal emerged victorious with a 2423 aggregate win over Uruguay in the two-legged Europe-Americas playoff (125 in Lisbon and 1218 in Montevideo), becoming the only fully amateur team to qualify and marking their debut at the World Cup.

Hosting and Logistics

Venues

The 2007 Rugby World Cup was primarily hosted in France, utilizing ten stadiums across the country, with additional matches held at the Millennium Stadium in Cardiff, Wales, and Murrayfield in Edinburgh, Scotland, to accommodate the tournament's schedule and international participation. Ireland was initially selected to host matches at Lansdowne Road in Dublin but withdrew due to ongoing stadium redevelopment.
Key venues included the Stade de France in Saint-Denis near Paris, which served as the main stadium and hosted the final, with a capacity of 80,000; the Millennium Stadium in Cardiff, capacity 74,500; Murrayfield in Edinburgh, capacity 67,500; and Stade de Gerland in Lyon, capacity 43,000. Other French venues encompassed Stade Vlodrome in Marseille (60,000), Parc des Princes in Paris (48,000), Stade Flix Bollaert in Lens (41,000), Stade de Toulouse in Toulouse (37,000), Stade de la Beaujoire in Nantes (38,000), Stade Geoffroy-Guichard in Saint-tienne (36,000), Stade Chaban-Delmas in Bordeaux (34,000), and Stade de la Mosson in Montpellier (33,000). These selections leveraged existing infrastructure, many of which had been upgraded following the 1998 FIFA World Cup, ensuring suitability for rugby union matches.
The tournament saw a total attendance of 2.25 million across 48 matches, marking the first time a Rugby World Cup exceeded two million spectators, with an average of 47,000 per match and a 97% capacity utilization rate in France.
Preparations for the venues involved significant investments in renovations and enhancements, focusing on stadium infrastructure to meet international standards, including pitch upgrades for optimal playing conditions, installation of temporary seating to boost capacities where needed, and bolstered security measures to handle large crowds and global events. Economic analyses highlighted these expenditures as key to the tournament's success, with costs directed toward modernizing facilities for both competition and spectator safety.
Venue assignments were strategically distributed to balance geographic spread and logistical demands; for instance, the Pool A opener between France and Georgia took place at the Stade de France, while Pool B matches, including Wales versus Australia, were allocated to the Millennium Stadium, and Pool C fixtures like Scotland versus Japan occurred at Murrayfield.

Tickets and fan zones

Over 2.4 million tickets were made available for the 48 matches of the 2007 Rugby World Cup, hosted primarily in France with additional games in Wales and Scotland. By late July 2007, more than 2 million tickets had been sold, equating to roughly 85% of the total allocation, with final sales reaching a record 2.25 million tickets across the tournament.
Ticket prices were structured in multiple categories to cater to different budgets, with individual match tickets starting at 10 for green category seats in select pool games and rising to 115 or more for premium seating at knockout matches and the final. Hospitality packages, offered through official agents, provided enhanced experiences including premium seating, dining, and exclusive access, with prices reaching up to 1,500 for top-tier options.
Sales were managed through an online platform operated by the International Rugby Board (IRB), divided into three phases beginning in 2006, allowing global access to team and city packages as well as individual match tickets. High-demand fixtures, such as semifinals and the final, utilized a lottery system to fairly distribute remaining tickets after initial allocations. Around 75% of all tickets were purchased by French nationals, reflecting a deliberate emphasis on local engagement.
To enhance fan engagement beyond stadiums, official zones were set up in host cities, equipped with giant screens for live match viewings, entertainment performances, and interactive activities. These areas, valued at 31.8 million in use benefits, drew large crowds and fostered a festive atmosphere, particularly in major venues like Paris.
Accessibility was prioritized through initiatives such as discounted pricing for youth and family groups on select matches, alongside robust anti-scalping efforts by organizers, who pursued legal action against hundreds of resellers profiting from unauthorized sales.

Sponsorship and broadcasting

The 2007 Rugby World Cup featured a diverse array of global and local sponsors that underpinned the tournament's commercial framework. Heineken served as a key global partner, extending its longstanding involvement with the Rugby World Cup that began in 1995 and focusing activation efforts on major markets like France and the UK. Other official global sponsors included Orange, which provided telecommunications support, Emirates as the official airline, Capgemini for technology and consulting services, Vediorbis for recruitment, and Visa for payment solutions. Local French sponsors, such as the national railway operator SNCF, filled top-tier packages to align with the host nation's infrastructure needs, while companies like lectricit de France (EDF) and Peugeot contributed through targeted partnerships, including advertising during UK broadcasts. Sponsorship revenues, alongside other commercial streams, supported a tournament budget that generated a net surplus of 122.4 million, marking a record for the event at the time.
Broadcasting arrangements for the 2007 Rugby World Cup extended to numerous international territories, driving a cumulative global television audience exceeding 4 billion viewers across all matches. In the United Kingdom, ITV held free-to-air rights for all 48 matches, delivering peak viewership of 15.8 million for the final between England and South Africa, the highest-rated single program of the year in that market. France's TF1 acted as the host broadcaster, managing production and holding primary domestic rights in collaboration with its subsidiary TVH. SuperSport in South Africa provided comprehensive coverage, capitalizing on national interest as the Springboks claimed victory. Notable deals included Setanta Sports securing live rights in Ireland, displacing public broadcaster RT from its previous role, while digital options emerged through the IRB's website for select international streams.

Tournament Organization

Participating teams and squads

The 2007 Rugby World Cup featured 20 national teams divided into four pools of five teams each, as determined by the draw held in London on 1 December 2006. Pool A consisted of South Africa, England, Samoa, Tonga, and the United States; Pool B included Australia, Wales, Fiji, Canada, and Japan; Pool C comprised New Zealand, Scotland, Italy, Romania, and Portugal; and Pool D featured France (the hosts), Argentina, Ireland, Georgia, and Namibia.
Under International Rugby Board (IRB) regulations, each team was required to submit a final squad of 30 players by 14 August 2007, with no more than 8 players permitted as replacements in any match. Player eligibility followed IRB rules, requiring individuals to hold citizenship of the nation they represented or to have completed three years of continuous residency in that country without representing another senior national team.
The participating teams reflected rugby's global diversity, with 9 from Europe (England, France, Ireland, Italy, Portugal, Romania, Scotland, Wales, Georgia), 5 from Oceania and the Pacific (Australia, Fiji, New Zealand, Samoa, Tonga), 3 from the Americas (Argentina, Canada, United States), 2 from Africa (Namibia, South Africa), and 1 from Asia (Japan).
Squads emphasized a balance of experienced internationals and emerging talent, typically comprising 16-18 forwards and 12-14 backs to cover the tournament's physical demands. South Africa's squad, captained by hooker John Smit, highlighted a robust forward pack including locks Victor Matfield and Bakkies Botha, alongside dynamic wing Bryan Habana for attacking flair. New Zealand's group, led by flanker Richie McCaw, featured fly-half Dan Carter as a key playmaker and a versatile backline with wings like Joe Rokocoko. England's team, under captain Phil Vickery, relied on forwards like lock Simon Shaw and scrum-half Matt Dawson for leadership, drawing on their 2003 championship experience. Australia's squad, with Stirling Mortlock as captain and veteran scrum-half George Gregan as vice-captain, incorporated speedsters like winger Lote Tuqiri despite some pre-selection injury concerns in the back row. Other notable inclusions across squads were Argentina's fly-half Juan Martn Hernndez for his tactical acumen and Fiji's explosive wing Rupeni Caucaunibuca for offloading prowess.
In preparation, teams conducted intensive training camps and warm-up friendlies to build cohesion and match fitness. For instance, Ireland held a high-altitude camp in Poland to enhance endurance, while England based in Portugal for a 10-day session focused on tactical drills. New Zealand expanded to a 39-player training group before finalizing their squad, playing Tri-Nations matches against Australia and South Africa as key friendlies. Smaller nations like Portugal arranged fixtures against club sides such as London Welsh to gain competitive exposure. These efforts underscored the tournament's emphasis on logistical readiness across diverse team sizes and resources.

Match officials

The International Rugby Board (IRB) selected a panel of 12 international referees from seven countries to officiate the 48 matches of the 2007 Rugby World Cup, drawing from the IRB's Elite Referee Panel. The selection process involved the IRB Match Official Selection Committee reviewing individual performances in international matches during the 20062007 season, ensuring a balance of experience and consistency in application of the laws. This panel represented England (three referees), Ireland and South Africa (two each), New Zealand (two), and one each from Australia, France, and Wales. Their collective experience ranged from seven international matches for debutants like Wayne Barnes and Nigel Owens to over 40 for veterans such as Paul Honiss and Chris White, allowing for assignments across pool and knockout stages.
The referees' roles extended beyond the main official, with each match featuring a primary referee, two assistant referees (touch judges), up to two reserve referees, and a television match official (TMO) responsible for video reviews of tries, conversions, and potential foul play. Typically, six main referees were active per match day to cover the tournament's schedule, supported by a broader pool of 13 touch judges from 10 countries. The TMO system, established in prior World Cups, provided real-time footage analysis to aid decision-making, enhancing accuracy without altering the on-field referee's authority.
Notable appointments included Tony Spreadbury of England for the tournament opener between France and Argentina at the Stade de France on 7 September 2007. In the final on 20 October 2007, Irish referee Alain Rolland took charge of the decisive England versus South Africa clash, marking a career highlight after 26 prior internationals.

Competition format

The 2007 Rugby World Cup featured 20 national teams competing in 48 matches over 44 days, from 7 September to 20 October. The tournament was structured in two main phases: a pool stage followed by a knockout stage, with all matches played under the laws of rugby union as governed by the International Rugby Board (IRB, now World Rugby). This format aimed to balance competitive equity while allowing for a progression of high-stakes encounters, culminating in the final at the Stade de France in Saint-Denis.
The pool stage consisted of four pools (A, B, C, and D), each containing five teams in a round-robin format, resulting in 10 matches per pool and 40 matches overall. Teams earned points as follows: 4 points for a win, 2 points for a draw, and 0 for a loss, with additional bonus points awardedone for scoring at least four tries in a match, and another for losing by seven points or fewer. The top two teams from each pool advanced to the knockout stage based on total points accumulated; in case of ties, tiebreakers were applied in sequence: head-to-head result, points difference, tries difference, total points scored, total tries scored, and finally IRB world rankings as of 1 October 2007. This system encouraged attacking play through try bonuses while rewarding close contests.
The knockout stage involved the eight advancing teams in a single-elimination bracket with no second chances. Quarter-finals were scheduled as follows: winner of Pool A versus runner-up of Pool B, winner of Pool B versus runner-up of Pool A, winner of Pool C versus runner-up of Pool D, and winner of Pool D versus runner-up of Pool C. The four quarter-final winners proceeded to semi-finals (QF1 winner vs. QF2 winner, QF3 winner vs. QF4 winner), with the semi-final winners contesting the final and the losers playing a third-place match. Ties in knockout matches were resolved by 10 minutes of extra time per half, followed by sudden-death extra time if needed, and ultimately a kicking competition if still unresolved. These eight fixtures were hosted across various venues in France, Wales, and Scotland to maximize attendance and atmosphere.
Pool seeding for the 2007 tournament was determined by performance in the 2003 Rugby World Cup, with the four semi-finalistsEngland, Australia, New Zealand, and Franceseeded as the top band (one per pool) to avoid early clashes among elite teams. The next four quarter-finalists from 2003 (Ireland, South Africa, Wales, and Scotland) formed band two and were randomly assigned to pools, followed by random draws for the 12 qualifiers in bands three through five based on regional qualification rankings. This seeding structure, finalized at a draw in Dublin on 12 May 2004, ensured a distributed field of strength across pools.
The outcomes of the 2007 tournament had direct implications for the 2011 Rugby World Cup, as the top three teams from each pool automatically qualified, securing 12 spots for the leading nations and reserving the remaining eight for regional qualifiers. Furthermore, these 12 automatic qualifiers were seeded into the 2011 pools using IRB world rankings as of 30 November 2008, with the eight quarter-finalists (pool winners and runners-up) placed in the top two bands to promote balanced competition in the subsequent event. This policy rewarded strong performances in 2007 while integrating ongoing rankings for fairness.

Pool Stage

Pool A

Pool A featured the defending champions England, hosts' rivals South Africa, Pacific Island powerhouses Samoa and Tonga, and debutants in a competitive sense the United States, who were making their third appearance but seeking their first win in the tournament. The group was marked by South Africa's dominant run, England's resilient recovery after an early setback, and a tight Pacific derby that highlighted regional rivalries. Matches were played across venues in France, including Lens, Paris, Montpellier, Nantes, and Saint-tienne, from 8 September to 30 September 2007.
The pool kicked off with England securing a comfortable 2810 victory over the USA on 8 September at Stade Flix Bollaert in Lens, where tries from Mark Cueto and Iain Balshaw set the tone for the defending champions' opener. South Africa followed with a resounding 597 thrashing of Samoa the next day at Parc des Princes in Paris, showcasing their attacking prowess with nine tries, including two from Bryan Habana. Tonga edged the USA 2515 on 12 September in Montpellier, with Pierre Hola's try proving decisive in a physical encounter. The standout match early on was South Africa's 360 shutout of England on 14 September at Stade de France, a result that stunned observers and featured Percy Montgomery's 14 points via conversions and penalties, underscoring the Springboks' defensive solidity. Tonga then upset Samoa 1915 on 16 September in Montpellier, a gritty Pacific clash where Finau Maka's leadership helped secure a rare victory in the islanders' head-to-head, denying Samoa an early points boost.
Mid-pool action intensified as South Africa narrowly defeated Tonga 3025 on 22 September in Lens, a thriller where the Springboks scored four tries for a bonus point but survived a late Tonga surge led by Kurt Morath's kicking. On the same day, England bounced back with a 4422 win over Samoa in Nantes, powered by Jason Robinson's try and Andy Gomarsall's contributions, earning maximum points. Samoa salvaged a 2521 victory against the USA on 26 September in Saint-tienne, with Alesana Tuilagi's brace providing the edge in a match that represented the USA's closest brush with a win. England sealed their quarter-final spot with a 3620 defeat of Tonga on 28 September at Parc des Princes, overcoming an early deficit through tries from David Strettle and Tom Rees. South Africa closed the pool with a 6415 rout of the USA on 30 September in Montpellier, where Montgomery added 19 points, including a try, and Takudzwa Ngwenya's effort for the Eagles stood out as a highlight of resilience against a superior side.
Key moments included Tonga's upset over Samoa, which kept the Pacific standings competitive and boosted their confidence heading into tougher fixtures, and the USA's spirited performances, particularly Ngwenya's 90-meter try against South Africa, symbolizing the growth of rugby in North America despite no victories. Percy Montgomery emerged as a standout, contributing 48 points in Pool A through his reliable goalkicking, helping South Africa maintain momentum. The pool's intensity was evident in the close margins for third place, with Tonga's two wins securing their elimination but marking their best group performance since 1999.
South Africa topped the pool with four wins and three bonus points for scoring four or more tries in three matches, advancing as group winners. England, despite their loss to South Africa, qualified in second with three victories and two bonus points. Tonga finished third, Samoa fourth, and the USA last, eliminated after four defeats but gaining valuable experience. Both South Africa and England progressed to the quarter-finals, setting up a rematch path later in the tournament.

Knockout Stage

Quarter-finals

The quarter-finals of the 2007 Rugby World Cup took place over two days, 6 and 7 October, at venues across France and Wales, marking the transition from pool play to single-elimination matches where the winners advanced to the semi-finals. The eight teams emerging from the poolsAustralia, England, New Zealand, France, South Africa, Fiji, Argentina, and Scotlandfaced high-stakes encounters characterized by upsets, defensive battles, and dramatic turnarounds.
On 6 October at the Stade Vlodrome in Marseille, England defeated Australia 12-10 in a tense, low-scoring affair that highlighted England's resurgence under Brian Ashton. Jonny Wilkinson kicked four penalties to secure the victory, with no tries scored by either side; England's forward dominance, particularly Andrew Sheridan's destructive scrummaging that disrupted Australia's set-piece, proved decisive in the second half after Australia led 10-6 at halftime. The match, attended by 59,821, was a gritty defensive struggle, underscoring England's tactical discipline against a favored Wallabies side.
Later that evening at the Millennium Stadium in Cardiff, France pulled off a stunning 20-18 upset over heavily favored New Zealand, eliminating the All Blacks in one of the tournament's most memorable games before a passionate crowd of 74,634. New Zealand led 13-3 early through Dan Carter's penalties and a try by Sitiveni Sivivatu, but France mounted a comeback fueled by Sbastien Chabal's powerful carries and a blue wall of defense that frustrated the All Blacks' attack. Key moments included Thierry Dusautoir's try to narrow the gap and Yannick Jauzion's late try, with David Skrela's conversion sealing the win; the atmosphere in Cardiff, with its mix of French and neutral support, amplified the drama of this pragmatic triumph over New Zealand's flair.
The following day, 7 October, saw two more quarter-finals. In Marseille's Stade Vlodrome, South Africa overcame a spirited Fiji 37-20, advancing despite a nervy second half where Fiji briefly leveled the score. South Africa raced to a 23-6 halftime lead with tries from Bryan Habana, Franois Steyn, and Schalk Burger, but Fiji responded with quick tries from Ifereimi Rawaqa and Mosese Luveitasau to tie at 20-20 midway through the second half. Victor Matfield's commanding lineout work and late surges, including tries by Juan Smith and Percy Montgomery, allowed the Springboks to pull away; the 58,000-strong crowd witnessed Fiji's flair nearly cause another upset.
In the evening at the Stade de France in Saint-Denis, Argentina edged Scotland 19-13 in a physical contest attended by 76,866, showcasing the Pumas' growing prowess on the world stage. Gonzalo Longo Ela's try, converted by Felipe Contepomi, gave Argentina the lead, with Contepomi adding three penalties and Juan Martn Hernndez a drop goal; Scotland fought back through Chris Paterson's boot and a try by Chris Cusiter, closing to within six points late on. Argentina's composure in defense and set-piece solidity, led by captain Agustn Pichot, held firm to secure the win.
England, France, South Africa, and Argentina progressed to the semi-finals, setting up intriguing clashes between northern and southern hemisphere powerhouses.

Semi-finals

The semi-finals of the 2007 Rugby World Cup were contested on 13 and 14 October at the Stade de France in Saint-Denis, France, with both matches drawing crowds exceeding 80,000 spectators. The victors advanced to the final on 20 October, while the defeated teams faced off in the third-place match three days later. These encounters featured intense defensive battles and pivotal individual contributions, determining the finalists from Pool A and Pool D qualifiers.
In the first semi-final on 13 October, England defeated host nation France 149 in a tense affair refereed by Jonathan Kaplan. England struck early when Josh Lewsey scored a try in the second minute after a handling error by French full-back Damien Traille, though Jonny Wilkinson missed the conversion, leaving the score at 50. France responded with penalties from fly-half Lionel Beauxis in the 8th and 18th minutes to lead 65 at halftime. Beauxis added another in the 44th minute for a 95 lead. Wilkinson landed a penalty in the 47th minute to make it 89, followed by a drop goal in the 59th minute for 119, and a final penalty in the 75th minute to secure the 149 victory before a partisan crowd of 80,283. Wilkinson's composure under pressure, including the match-winning kicks, echoed his heroics from the 2003 tournament and propelled England to their second consecutive World Cup final.
The second semi-final on 14 October saw South Africa overpower Argentina 3713, also at the Stade de France with approximately 80,000 in attendance and refereed by Steve Walsh. South Africa dominated from the outset, with scrum-half Fourie du Preez intercepting a pass to score the opening try in the 7th minute, converted by full-back Percy Montgomery for a 70 lead. Argentina's Felipe Contepomi replied with a penalty in the 15th minute, but Bryan Habana extended South Africa's advantage with a try in the 32nd minute (converted), followed by lock Danie Rossouw's score just before halftime from a turnover, making it 246 at the break. In the second half, Argentina mounted a brief comeback with a try from centre Manuel Contepomi in the 45th minute, converted by his brother Felipe, narrowing the gap to 2413. However, Montgomery added two penalties (71st and 75th minutes), and Habana sealed the win with his second try in the 76th minute, both converted by Montgomery. Habana's brace highlighted South Africa's attacking prowess, while Montgomery's perfect kicking record (4 conversions and 3 penalties) contributed 21 points, booking South Africa's place in the final.

Third-place match

The third-place match of the 2007 Rugby World Cup pitted the semi-final losers, Argentina and France, against each other on 19 October 2007 at the Parc des Princes in Paris. Argentina, having fallen 3713 to South Africa in the semi-finals, and France, defeated 149 by England, sought to end the tournament on a high note before a crowd of 45,958 spectators.
Argentina dominated the encounter, securing a decisive 3410 victory to claim third place. The Pumas led 173 at halftime after tries from Felipe Contepomi and prop Omar Hasan, both converted by Contepomi, who also added a penalty. In the second half, Argentina extended their lead with further tries from Federico Aramburu, Ignacio Corleto, and a second from Contepomi, though only the first Contepomi try was converted. France managed a late consolation try through fullback Clment Poitrenaud, converted by Lionel Beauxis, following an earlier penalty from Jean-Baptiste Elissalde. Disciplinary issues plagued both sides, with France captain Raphal Ibaez and Argentine flanker Rimas lvarez sin-binned in the first half, followed by Juan Manuel Leguizamon's temporary dismissal later.
Argentina's captain Agustn Pichot provided inspirational leadership throughout, marking what would be his final international appearance as he retired from Test rugby shortly after the tournament. Contepomi's standout performance, including two tries and key points from the tee, underscored Argentina's clinical execution. This result represented Argentina's highest-ever finish at a Rugby World Cup, surpassing their previous best of fifth place in 1999 and 2003, and highlighted the Pumas' emergence as a major force in international rugby.

Final

The final of the 2007 Rugby World Cup took place on 20 October 2007 at the Stade de France in Saint-Denis, near Paris, France, drawing an attendance of 80,430 spectators. South Africa, captained by John Smit, entered the match after a 37-13 semi-final victory over Argentina, while England, led by Phil Vickery, had advanced by defeating the host nation France 14-9 in the other semi-final. The clash pitted the defending champions against the 1995 winners in a highly anticipated rematch, with both teams emphasizing disciplined defense and set-piece play in cold conditions that limited open rugby.
The game unfolded as a low-scoring, attritional battle with no tries scored by either side, relying entirely on penalties for points. England struck first in the 10th minute when fly-half Jonny Wilkinson converted a penalty after South Africa strayed offside, giving the English a 3-0 lead. South Africa's fullback Percy Montgomery responded three times before halftimeat the 16th, 26th, and 37th minutesto put the Springboks ahead 9-3 at the interval, capitalizing on English infringements in the scrum and breakdown. In the second half, Montgomery added another penalty in the 49th minute to extend the lead to 12-3, but England mounted pressure around the hour mark. A potential game-changer came in the 55th minute when winger Mark Cueto appeared to score in the corner after a cross-field kick, only for the try to be disallowed by Irish referee Alain Rolland due to Cueto's foot brushing the touchline. Wilkinson narrowed the gap to 12-6 with a penalty shortly after, but South Africa held firm; centre Franois Steyn sealed the 15-6 victory with a long-range penalty in the 72nd minute.
South Africa's success was anchored by standout performances in the forwards, with captain John Smit providing inspirational leadership and lock Victor Matfield dominating the line-outs to earn the man of the match award for his 12 wins from 14 throws. England's veteran flanker Lawrence Dallaglio marshaled the back row with characteristic tenacity, contributing to a gritty effort that kept the scoreline close despite South Africa's territorial advantage. The Springboks' kicking accuracyfive penalties from six attemptsproved decisive against Wilkinson's two from three.
In the post-match ceremony, South African President Thabo Mbeki presented the Webb Ellis Cup to captain John Smit on the pitch, a moment celebrated by the team as they lifted the trophy together. Former President Nelson Mandela, absent from the final due to health concerns, had earlier conveyed a supportive message to the Springboks, wishing them victory and emphasizing national unity.

Outcomes and Records

Statistics

South Africa achieved a perfect record in the tournament, securing seven wins from seven matches, including victories in the pool stage, quarter-final, semi-final, and final. New Zealand topped the pool stage with four wins from four matches before their quarter-final exit, scoring the highest total points among all teams at 327. South Africa ranked second in points scored with 278, while also conceding the fewest points overall at 62, demonstrating superior defensive performance.

The tournament featured strong individual contributions, with South Africa's Percy Montgomery leading all players in points scored at 105, achieved through 2 tries, 22 conversions, 15 penalties, and no drop goals across seven matches. Bryan Habana of South Africa recorded the most tries with 8, equaling the single-tournament record set by Jonah Lomu in 1999. Man of the Match awards highlighted key performances, such as Victor Matfield's dominant display in the final, where his lineout and tackling prowess helped secure South Africa's victory.
Discipline was relatively controlled, with referees issuing 35 yellow cards across the 48 matches22 to forwards and 13 to backsresulting in an average of less than one per game. Two red cards were shown: one to Namibia's Jacques Burger against South Africa in the pool stage and one to Tonga's Kati Tala'uli against South Africa in the quarter-final. The average number of penalties and free kicks per match stood at 19, a decrease from 24 in the 2003 tournament, reflecting improved adherence to the laws.
The tournament set several records, including a total of 296 tries scored across all matches, averaging about 6.2 per game. The highest-scoring match was New Zealand's 10813 pool-stage win over Portugal, totaling 121 points and featuring 13 tries by the All Blacks. Attendance reached a record 2,263,223 spectators over 48 matches, with an average of 47,150 per game and a 97% capacity fill rate in host venues. The final at Stade de France drew 80,430 fans.
Bryan Habana was named the IRB International Player of the Year for 2007, recognizing his tournament-leading tries and overall impact in South Africa's campaign. The IRB also selected the International Team of the Year as South Africa, honoring their unbeaten run to the title.

Controversies

The 2007 Rugby World Cup was marred by several refereeing controversies, most notably in the quarter-final between New Zealand and France on October 6 in Cardiff. Referee Wayne Barnes, along with his assistant referees and television match official, overlooked a clear forward pass in the build-up to France's second try by Florian Fritz, which proved decisive in the 20-18 upset victory that eliminated the All Blacks. The International Rugby Board (IRB) later acknowledged that Barnes committed three serious errors in the match's final 15 minutes, including failing to spot the forward pass and other infringements, amid widespread criticism from New Zealand media and fans who deemed the officiating substandard. Barnes himself later conceded in 2019 that the pass was indeed forward, reflecting on how the incident motivated his career improvement.
Another high-profile dispute occurred in the final on October 20 between England and South Africa in Paris, where English winger Mark Cueto's potential try in the 55th minute was disallowed by referee Alain Rolland after consultation with the TMO. Rolland ruled Cueto's left foot was in touch before he grounded the ball, but Cueto and several analysts argued that the ball had been placed down in the field of play first, with his foot merely scraping the line afterward. The decision, which would have tied the score at 9-9, contributed to South Africa's 15-6 victory and remains one of the most debated calls in World Cup history, with Cueto stating in 2023 that it "might have changed the game." No conclusive video evidence overturned the call under the era's TMO protocols.
Ireland's campaign drew significant criticism for poor preparation under coach Eddie O'Sullivan, culminating in a premature quarter-final exit after a 14-10 loss to Argentina on September 16. The team struggled with a shaky 32-17 win over Namibia on September 9, marked by handling errors and lack of composure, followed by a narrow 14-10 victory against Georgia on September 15 despite leading 14-0 early. O'Sullivan faced backlash for inadequate warm-up matches and training cohesion, with reports highlighting how continuous sessions without competitive play led to a dismal overall performance, prompting calls for his resignation post-tournament.
Wales' shock 38-34 pool-stage defeat to Fiji on September 29 in Nantes eliminated them from quarter-final contention and sparked controversy over disciplinary lapses that facilitated Fiji's comeback from a 32-10 deficit. Fiji capitalized on yellow cards to their players while Wales failed to maintain control, with critics pointing to poor game management by coach Gareth Jenkins as a key factor in the collapse, ultimately leading to his sacking by the Welsh Rugby Union. The match highlighted broader concerns about Wales' readiness against unpredictable opponents.
Portugal's debut qualification via the repechage playoff against Uruguay in June 2007 (24-23 aggregate) fueled debates about the legitimacy of their spot, given their amateur status and the nation's limited rugby infrastructure compared to established teams. Some critics questioned whether the IRB's qualification pathways adequately vetted emerging nations, though Portugal's historic win was undisputed; however, post-qualification off-field drama ensued when six players were briefly jailed in Montevideo for a celebratory brawl, raising eyebrows about team discipline.
Off-field issues included security concerns during qualifiers, notably the IRB's cancellation of the final Asian tournament scheduled for Colombo, Sri Lanka, from November 18-25, 2006, due to escalating civil unrest and safety risks for teams and officials. The event was relocated to Hong Kong, underscoring the challenges of hosting in volatile regions. On doping, the tournament passed cleanly, with 212 tests conducted yielding no positive results, as confirmed by the IRB and World Anti-Doping Agency.
In response, the IRB conducted post-tournament reviews of referee performances, affirming the overall high standard while addressing specific errors like those in the New Zealand-France match to refine protocols for future events. The board publicly backed its officials amid backlash, emphasizing continuous evaluation and training enhancements.

Legacy

Impact on rugby development

The 2007 Rugby World Cup significantly boosted the growth of rugby in emerging nations, particularly through heightened visibility and subsequent investments. Fiji's unexpected quarter-final appearance, including their upset victory over Wales, marked a milestone that elevated the sport's profile domestically and internationally, leading to increased funding and development programs for Pacific Island nations. Similarly, Georgia's competitive performance, notably their narrow 14-10 loss to Ireland, served as a watershed moment, spurring the growth of youth academies and positioning the country as a leading producer of talent outside Tier 1 nations. In Portugal, the tournament's exposure more than doubled registered players from 3,876 pre-event levels, fostering grassroots expansion and infrastructure improvements. Overall, Rugby World Cup revenues, which generated a record surplus of 122.4 million, funded a 19% global participation increase since 2007, with a substantial portion directed toward Pacific Islands like Fiji, Samoa, and Tonga through targeted development initiatives. These impacts have endured, as evidenced by Fiji reaching the semi-finals of the 2023 Rugby World Cup, Portugal qualifying for their second appearance in 2023, and Georgia's consistent competitiveness in Rugby Europe competitions.
The event also advanced professionalism across the sport by prompting key structural changes in high-profile teams. For New Zealand's All Blacks, their quarter-final exit triggered an independent review that identified deficiencies in preparation and mental conditioning, leading to coaching restructuring under Graham Henry, including the addition of assistants Steve Hansen and Wayne Smith, which contributed to their 2011 World Cup triumph. Argentina's bronze medal finish propelled their elevation to Tier 1 status, culminating in their inclusion in the Rugby Championship from 2012 onward, enhancing competitive depth and professional opportunities for South American players. These shifts underscored the tournament's role in refining coaching methodologies and elevating second-tier nations toward elite competition.
Commercially, the 2007 World Cup set new benchmarks with total revenues exceeding previous editions, inspiring bids for future hosting and sustainable growth. The event's financial success, including a net surplus of 122.4 million, directly influenced New Zealand's successful bid to host the 2011 tournament, providing a model for integrating rugby into national economic strategies. On participation fronts, hosting in France spurred an upsurge in youth and amateur programs beyond traditional strongholds, with licensed female players increasing by 35% post-event, indirectly boosting women's rugby through heightened overall interest and resource allocation. Additionally, the top three teams from each 2007 pool automatically qualified for 2011, with performances directly impacting seeding and pool compositions based on updated world rankings, ensuring continuity and fairness in global competition pathways.

Cultural and social significance

The Springboks' victory in the 2007 Rugby World Cup played a pivotal role in fostering national unity in post-apartheid South Africa, mirroring the reconciliatory impact of the 1995 tournament under Nelson Mandela. Millions of South Africans from diverse racial backgrounds celebrated the 15-6 final win over England, with parades, flag-draped cars, and gatherings in stadiums and homes symbolizing collective joy and togetherness. One fan encapsulated this sentiment: "It's not about race. It's about South Africans being together... a victory to unity." The event transcended rugby's historical association with white Afrikaner culture, promoting a shared national identity amid ongoing transformation efforts.
A key cultural element was the widespread adoption of the "Amabokoboko" chant, a Zulu-derived affectionate term for the Springboks that emphasized inclusivity and bridged linguistic divides. Used in songs, cheers, and media during the tournament, it helped rally support across South Africa's multicultural population, countering debates over the team's emblem and composition. President Thabo Mbeki's attendance at the final and Nelson Mandela's video message further reinforced the victory as a unifying milestone.
As hosts, France experienced a resurgence in national pride despite the team's semi-final exit to England, with the tournament showcasing the country's rugby heritage and organizational prowess. The 42 matches played on French soil drew record crowds, affirming rugby's deep cultural roots and boosting communal spirit even amid on-field disappointments. The quarter-final upset against New Zealand, a 20-18 thriller, became a symbol of resilience, galvanizing fans and evoking patriotic fervor.
Fiji's remarkable run to the quarter-finals, capped by a 38-34 upset over Walestheir first win against the side in nine attemptsignited nationwide celebrations and elevated rugby's status in island culture. Players' exuberant on-field haka-style dances and street parties in Suva highlighted the sport's communal role, inspiring youth participation and reinforcing Fiji's reputation for flamboyant, passionate play. This achievement marked Fiji's first World Cup quarter-final since 1987, fostering a sense of global recognition for Pacific Island rugby traditions.
Iconic moments like New Zealand's haka confronting France's advancing line in the quarter-final captured global media attention, symbolizing cultural clashes and respect between nationsoften likened to the intensity of anthems such as La Marseillaise. The stare-down, led by figures like Sbastien Chabal, became a hallmark of rugby's theatrical heritage. The tournament inspired various media productions, including the official Rugby World Cup 2007 Review DVD chronicling upsets and triumphs, and World Rugby Films' retrospective on the final featuring captain John Smit's insights.
Socially, the event amplified rugby's appeal to broader demographics, with overall viewership reaching over 4 billion globally and contributing to increased female engagement in subsequent years through heightened visibility. The International Rugby Board (IRB) leveraged the platform for anti-racism initiatives, using South Africa's win to underscore rugby's potential for social cohesion while prompting calls to address lingering exclusion in the sport. Upon the Springboks' return, commentators urged rugby authorities to confront "racism demons" by accelerating transformation and inclusivity, reciprocating black South Africans' widespread support.
