 The Muslim Brotherhood (al-Ikhwn al-Muslimn) is a transnational Sunni Islamist organization founded in 1928 in Ismailia, Egypt, by Hassan al-Banna, a schoolteacher seeking to revive Islamic principles amid Western secular influences and colonial rule. Its core ideology emphasizes comprehensive Islamization of societyencompassing personal piety, social welfare, education, and governancethrough gradualist strategies of grassroots activism, charitable works, and political engagement to establish states ruled by Sharia law. The group has built extensive networks of mosques, schools, and hospitals, amassing millions of supporters across the Muslim world while promoting anti-colonial resistance and opposition to secular nationalism.
Historically rooted in Egypt, the Brotherhood expanded rapidly in the 1930s and 1940s, engaging in social reform but also forming a secret apparatus accused of assassinations, including that of Egyptian Prime Minister Mahmoud al-Nuqrashi in 1948, leading to its initial suppression under Gamal Abdel Nasser. It influenced radical thinkers like Sayyid Qutb, whose writings justified takfir (declaring Muslims apostates) and jihad against perceived tyrannical regimes, laying ideological groundwork for later militant groups. After periods of exile and resurgence, the organization capitalized on the 2011 Arab Spring uprisings to win Egypt's 2012 presidential election with Mohamed Morsi, briefly implementing Islamist policies before his 2013 ouster amid mass protests and military intervention.
Globally, the Brotherhood has spawned affiliates and inspired movements in over 70 countries, including Hamas in Gaza, though it officially renounces violence in favor of electoral politics where possible. It faces terrorist designations from governments in Egypt, Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates, Russia, Bahrain, and Syria due to alleged incitement, financing of extremism, and historical militancy, as well as recent U.S. designations of its branches in Lebanon (as a foreign terrorist organization), Egypt, and Jordan (as specially designated global terrorists), and Argentina's designations of its branches in Egypt, Jordan, and Lebanon as terrorist organizations under President Javier Milei, for providing material support to Hamas, contrasting with non-designation of the organization as a whole in most Western countries despite ongoing scrutiny of its networks. It has been implicated in supporting extremist networks and destabilizing activities beyond the Middle East, including Europe, where authorities have raised concerns about radicalization within some affiliated organizations and charities. These designations highlight persistent debates over its dual role as a social provider versus an incubator for Islamist militancy, with empirical evidence from declassified documents and court cases underscoring ties to designated terror groups.

Ideology and Core Objectives

Foundational Principles and Hassan al-Banna's Vision

Hassan al-Banna established the Muslim Brotherhood on March 22, 1928, in Ismailia, Egypt, amid British colonial rule and accelerating Western secular influences that he viewed as eroding traditional Islamic society. His foundational vision sought a comprehensive Islamic revival, positioning Islam not merely as a personal faith but as a total system governing all aspects of lifeincluding politics, economics, education, and social relationsto counter cultural decay and foreign domination. Al-Banna emphasized returning to the Quran and Sunnah as the sole authoritative sources, rejecting secular ideologies and advocating for Sharia implementation as the path to moral and societal renewal.
Central to al-Banna's principles were twenty fundamental tenets of Islam, which underscored its holistic nature: from Tawhid (the oneness of God) and prophethood to the afterlife, alongside obligations like prayer, zakat, and defensive jihad to protect Muslim lands and faith. He promoted tarbiyah (moral and spiritual education) and da'wah (invitation to Islam) as primary tools for individual and communal transformation, starting with self-purification (jihad al-nafs) to build disciplined cadres capable of broader reform. This gradualist approach involved establishing schools, clinics, and youth programs to foster piety and self-reliance, while fusing anti-colonial resistance with pan-Islamic aspirations for eventual unity under a caliphate-like system.
Al-Banna's ideology integrated nationalism with Islam, arguing that true Egyptian and Arab identity required subordination to divine law over imported Western models, which he criticized for promoting individualism, materialism, and moral laxity. He outlined practical goals such as studying the Quran, unifying Muslims around core doctrines, and mobilizing resourcesincluding economic and militaryfor Islamic objectives, while opposing partition of Muslim territories like Palestine. Though al-Banna advocated non-violent propagation initially, he affirmed jihad's role in liberating occupied lands, as seen in his support for resistance against British forces and Zionist settlement. This vision, disseminated through writings like Risalat al-Ta'alim (Message of Teachings), aimed to create a vanguard Muslim community that would incrementally reform society from the ground up, prioritizing internal strength over immediate political confrontation.

Commitment to Sharia Implementation

The Muslim Brotherhood, founded by Hassan al-Banna in 1928, has maintained an unwavering commitment to implementing Sharia law as the comprehensive legal and moral framework for Muslim societies, viewing it as derived directly from the Quran and Sunnah. Al-Banna articulated this vision in his writings and messages, such as the 1947 "Message of the Teachings" addressed to King Farouk, where he demanded the application of Islamic law to address Egypt's moral and political decay, rejecting secular alternatives as insufficient. The organization's motto"Allah is our objective; the Messenger is our leader; the Quran is our constitution; jihad is our path; dying in the way of Allah is our most sublime aspiration"explicitly elevates the Quran, the primary source of Sharia, as the supreme guiding law above man-made systems.
This dedication manifests in a phased strategy emphasizing da'wah (Islamic propagation), social reform, and political activism to gradually establish governance under Sharia, rather than immediate revolution. Al-Banna's framework called for Sharia's application across personal, familial, economic, and political spheres, including hudud punishments and prohibition of usury, as essential to restoring Islamic purity eroded by Western colonialism and modernization. Successors like Sayyid Qutb reinforced this by interpreting Sharia implementation as a divine obligation requiring confrontation with jahiliyyah (pre-Islamic ignorance equated with modern secularism), though the Brotherhood officially distanced itself from his more radical calls for takfir.
In practice, during Mohammed Morsi's presidency from June 2012 to July 2013, the Brotherhood-influenced government advanced Sharia's role by enshrining in Egypt's 2012 constitution that "the principles of Islamic Sharia are the main source of legislation," with Article 4 mandating Al-Azhar University's role in interpreting Sharia rulings. The group reiterated this stance in October 2012, declaring, "We cannot in any way compromise in demanding to apply Sharia," while framing it as compatible with Egypt's national identity and Article 2 of prior constitutions. Despite such efforts, implementation faced resistance, leading to accusations of ambiguity in application, as the constitution's phrasing allowed judicial interpretation that diluted strict Sharia enforcement.
The Brotherhood's global affiliates echo this core objective, adapting tactics to local contexts but consistently prioritizing Sharia supremacy over democratic pluralism, which they regard as a tactical tool rather than an end in itself. This commitment has drawn scrutiny from governments designating the group as terrorist due to links with violence in pursuit of Islamist governance, though the organization maintains its approach is non-violent reformism.

Stances on Jihad, Democracy, and Secularism

The Muslim Brotherhood's ideology, as articulated by founder Hassan al-Banna in 1928, positions jihad as a central pathway to achieving its objectives, encapsulated in the group's longstanding motto: "Allah is our objective; the Prophet is our leader; the Qur'an is our constitution; jihad is our way; dying in the way of Allah is our highest hope." This formulation, drawn from al-Banna's writings and early organizational texts, frames jihad not merely as defensive warfare but as a comprehensive struggleencompassing military, political, and societal dimensionsto establish Islamic governance and resist perceived Western encroachment. Historical actions, including the Brotherhood's "secret apparatus" conducting assassinations against Egyptian officials in the 1940s and support for groups like Hamas, reflect this commitment, though the organization has publicly renounced violence since the 1970s under pressure from regimes, while maintaining ideological endorsement of jihad through affiliated networks.
On democracy, the Brotherhood adopts a pragmatic, instrumental approach, participating in electoral processes to gain power while subordinating democratic mechanisms to Sharia supremacy, viewing them as temporary tools rather than intrinsic values. During Egypt's 20112012 parliamentary elections, the Brotherhood's Freedom and Justice Party secured 47% of seats, leading to Mohamed Morsi's presidency in June 2012, yet its governance emphasized Islamic constitutionalism over pluralistic liberalism, prompting accusations of subverting democratic norms to impose theocratic rule. Scholarly analyses highlight this tension, noting that while the group exhibits procedural democratic behaviorslike campaigning and coalition-buildingduring periods of opportunity, its core doctrine prioritizes divine sovereignty over popular will, rendering full compatibility with secular democracy illusory. Al-Banna's writings and successors like Sayyid Qutb reinforced this by advocating governance derived solely from Islamic texts, rejecting Western models as jahiliyyah (pre-Islamic ignorance).
The Brotherhood's stance on secularism is one of unequivocal rejection, positing Islam as an all-encompassing system that integrates religion, state, and society, with no room for separation of mosque and state. Al-Banna argued that the Qur'an and Sunnah constitute the sole legitimate constitution, criticizing secular nationalism and Western influences for eroding Muslim unity and sovereignty. Qutb, a pivotal ideologue executed in 1966, extended this by denouncing secular societiesboth Muslim and non-Muslimas manifestations of ignorance, insisting that Sharia must govern all aspects of life to rectify moral and political decay. This position has manifested in the group's advocacy for Sharia-based constitutions, as seen in its 2012 Egyptian draft emphasizing Islamic jurisprudence, and opposition to secular reforms, framing them as colonial legacies incompatible with authentic Islamic revival.

Historical Trajectory in Egypt

Establishment and Expansion (19281945)

The Muslim Brotherhood was established in March 1928 by Hassan al-Banna, a schoolteacher and Islamic scholar, in the city of Ismailia, Egypt. Initially formed with six members from the Suez Canal Company's workforce, the organization emerged as a response to perceived moral decay, Western cultural influence, and the collapse of the Ottoman Caliphate, seeking to revive Islamic principles through education, social welfare, and grassroots activism. Al-Banna, influenced by Sufi orders and reformist thinkers like Rashid Rida, positioned the group as a comprehensive Islamic movement rather than a mere political party, emphasizing personal piety alongside societal reform.
By the early 1930s, the Brotherhood expanded rapidly within Egypt, establishing its first official school in 1931 and leveraging al-Banna's teaching networks to recruit students and professionals. Membership grew through local branches focused on Quranic study circles, youth training via the jawwala (rover) squads modeled on Boy Scouts, and charitable initiatives addressing poverty and illiteracy in rural areas. By 1934, the organization had 50 branches across Egypt; this number surged to 300 branches with approximately 50,000 active members by 1938, reflecting effective mobilization amid anti-colonial sentiments against British presence.
The Brotherhood's outreach extended beyond Egypt in the 1930s, with informal networks forming among Arab students and expatriates, laying groundwork for branches in Lebanon (1936) and Syria (1937). A pivotal aspect of its expansion involved advocacy for Palestinian Arabs against Zionist settlement and British mandates, culminating in al-Banna's calls for jihad and the dispatch of volunteers to support the 19361939 Arab Revolt in Palestine. These efforts, including fundraising and propaganda, enhanced the group's regional prestige and recruitment, framing the conflict as a pan-Islamic duty.
Into the early 1940s, internal organizational consolidation included the formation of a secretive apparatus (al-nizam al-sirri) around 1940 to train militants, amid escalating tensions with Egyptian authorities and involvement in anti-British activities during World War II. Despite government scrutiny, the Brotherhood maintained expansion through parallel "family" and "battalion" systems for covert operations and public outreach, reaching an estimated 500,000 sympathizers by 1945 while navigating alliances with Wafd Party nationalists. This period solidified its dual structure of overt social reform and latent militancy, driven by al-Banna's doctrine of gradual Islamization.

Postwar Conflicts, Assassinations, and Suppression (19451970)

Following World War II, the Muslim Brotherhood expanded its paramilitary activities, including participation in the 1948 Arab-Israeli War, where it dispatched thousands of volunteers to combat Israeli forces, framing the conflict as a religious jihad against Jews described as historic enemies of Muslims. The group's secret apparatus, known as the Special Unit or al-Nizam al-Khas, conducted assassinations, including that of Judge Ahmed Khazinder Bey in 1947 and Prime Minister Mahmoud an-Nuqrashi Pasha on December 28, 1948, by Brotherhood member Abdel-Maguid Hassan, in retaliation for Nuqrashi's dissolution of the organization and arrest of its leaders after the 1948 war's failures.
In response, Egyptian authorities assassinated Brotherhood founder Hassan al-Banna on February 12, 1949, amid heightened state efforts to curb the group's violence. Initially supportive of the 1952 Free Officers' coup led by Gamal Abdel Nasser, the Brotherhood soon clashed with the regime over its secular policies and refusal to implement sharia, leading to tensions that culminated in a failed assassination attempt on Nasser by a Brotherhood member on October 26, 1954, during a public speech in Alexandria.
Nasser's government retaliated by declaring the Brotherhood illegal, arresting approximately 4,000 members, and executing six leaders, including Abdel Qader Awdah, in December 1954, initiating a decade of severe suppression involving torture and imprisonment. Prominent ideologue Sayyid Qutb, arrested in 1954 and convicted in 1955 for involvement in the plot, spent nearly a decade in prison, where he authored Milestones (1964), advocating takfir against un-Islamic regimes and influencing radical Islamist thought. Rearrested in 1965 amid allegations of a new insurgency plot, Qutb was executed by hanging on August 29, 1966, after a military tribunal convicted him of conspiring against the state, solidifying his martyrdom status within the Brotherhood.
Under Nasser, the Brotherhood operated clandestinely through study circles and prisons, enduring mass trials and executions that decimated its leadership but preserved its ideological core, with suppression continuing until partial rehabilitation under Anwar Sadat post-1970.

Resurgence under Sadat and Mubarak (19702010)

Following the death of Gamal Abdel Nasser in 1970, Anwar Sadat initiated a policy of reconciliation with the Muslim Brotherhood, releasing thousands of its imprisoned members as part of a broader amnesty for political detainees in the early 1970s. This included key figures previously convicted during Nasser's crackdowns, allowing the group to reorganize under new leadership. Umar al-Tilmisani was appointed General Guide in 1972, succeeding the imprisoned Hasan al-Hudaybi, and under his guidance, the Brotherhood publicly renounced violence in exchange for permission to engage in preaching, social welfare activities, and advocacy. Sadat tolerated this resurgence to counterbalance lingering Nasserist and leftist influences, though the group was never legalized as a political party and operated unofficially.
The Brotherhood capitalized on this opening by expanding its influence through grassroots networks, particularly in universities and professional syndicates such as those for engineers, doctors, and lawyers, where it gained control of several bodies by the late 1970s and 1980s via elections and service provision. It established clinics, schools, and charitable organizations that addressed socioeconomic gaps neglected by the state, fostering loyalty among lower-middle-class Egyptians and students amid economic liberalization and inflation. Membership estimates grew from a few thousand in the early 1970s to hundreds of thousands by the 1980s, though exact figures remain unverified due to the group's secretive structure. Tensions escalated in the late Sadat era over his 1979 Camp David Accords with Israel, leading to arrests of Brotherhood leaders in 1981; Sadat's assassination that October by Egyptian Islamic Jihadan offshoot influenced by ex-Brotherhood radicals like Sayyid Qutbprompted further scrutiny, though the core group distanced itself from the act.
Under Hosni Mubarak, who succeeded Sadat in 1981, the Brotherhood maintained a tolerated but precarious status, focusing on nonviolent gradualism (tadarruj) to build societal influence while avoiding direct confrontation. It entered electoral politics indirectly, forming alliances with secular parties: in 1984, partnering with the Wafd Party to secure eight seats in parliament as independents; in 1987, aligning with the Labor and Liberal parties to win 36 seats. The group boycotted the 1990 elections citing fraud but participated in 2000, gaining 17 seats amid widespread irregularities. Its peak came in the 2005 parliamentary elections, where candidates running as independents captured 88 of 444 seats in the People's Assemblyabout 20%despite pre-election arrests of over 800 members and violent clashes with security forces that killed at least 10. This success alarmed Mubarak's regime, prompting intensified repression, including mass detentions after 2005 and restrictions on syndicate activities, yet the Brotherhood persisted through internal discipline and da'wa (proselytizing) efforts, amassing a base estimated at up to one million sympathizers by 2010.

Arab Spring Victory, Governance, and Overthrow (20112013)

The Muslim Brotherhood initially adopted a cautious stance during the early stages of the 2011 Egyptian uprising against President Hosni Mubarak, providing logistical support rather than leading the protests, which allowed the group to capitalize on the resulting power vacuum without bearing the brunt of the revolution's risks. Following Mubarak's resignation on February 11, 2011, the Brotherhood announced the formation of the Freedom and Justice Party (FJP) on April 30, 2011, which received legal recognition on June 6, 2011, enabling open political participation. In the parliamentary elections held from November 28, 2011, to January 11, 2012, the FJP-led Democratic Alliance for Egypt secured approximately 47% of the seats, with the FJP alone winning 235 out of 498 available seats, outperforming secular and liberal coalitions amid high voter turnout. 
In the presidential election's runoff on June 1617, 2012, Brotherhood candidate Mohamed Morsi defeated Ahmed Shafik, the last prime minister under Mubarak, with 51.73% of the vote to Shafik's 48.27%, marking the first competitive presidential contest in Egypt's history and the Brotherhood's ascension to executive power on June 30, 2012. Morsi's administration immediately faced tensions with the Supreme Council of the Armed Forces (SCAF), which had dissolved the Islamist-dominated parliament on June 14, 2012, following a constitutional court ruling; Morsi countered by reinstating the legislature via decree in July but encountered broader resistance from judicial and military institutions. On November 22, 2012, Morsi issued a constitutional declaration granting himself temporary legislative and immunity powers, which critics, including secular activists and the judiciary, decried as an authoritarian power grab to shield an Islamist-drafted constitution ratified in a December 15, 2012, referendum with 63.8% approval but low 33% turnout. 
During its year in power, the Brotherhood-led government prioritized Islamist policies, including efforts to incorporate Sharia principles into governance and foreign alignments favoring Turkey and Qatar while straining ties with Saudi Arabia and the UAE, but it struggled with economic stagnation, fuel and food shortages, and rising crime, failing to deliver on promises of stability and inclusivity. Internal divisions within the Brotherhood and exclusionary tactics alienated Coptic Christians, liberals, and even some Islamists, exacerbating polarization; for instance, Morsi's November decree sparked nationwide protests, forcing its partial retraction, while accusations of nepotism and Brotherhood infiltration of state institutions fueled perceptions of a "state within a state." Governance shortcomings were compounded by the Brotherhood's inexperience in administration, leading to policy paralysis and a failure to build coalitions, as evidenced by stalled IMF loan negotiations due to subsidy cutbacks and fiscal deficits exceeding 10% of GDP.
Opposition coalesced around the Tamarod (Rebellion) movement, which collected over 22 million signatures demanding Morsi's resignation by early 2013, culminating in mass demonstrations on June 30, 2013the anniversary of Morsi's inaugurationdrawing millions to Tahrir Square and other cities in calls for his ouster amid reports of violence between pro- and anti-Morsi factions that killed at least 16. The military, under Defense Minister Abdel Fattah el-Sisi, issued a 48-hour ultimatum on July 1 for Morsi to meet opposition demands, which he rejected in a defiant speech; on July 3, 2013, the armed forces deposed Morsi, suspended the constitution, and appointed Adly Mansour as interim president, framing the action as a response to popular will and institutional deadlock rather than a traditional coup. The Brotherhood denounced the removal as a coup, mobilizing sit-ins that were later cleared in August 2013 with hundreds killed, marking the end of its brief electoral triumph and the onset of severe repression. 

Sisi Era Crackdown and Ongoing Resistance (2013Present)

Following the military's removal of President Mohamed Morsi on July 3, 2013, led by then-Defense Minister Abdel Fattah el-Sisi, Egyptian authorities launched an extensive crackdown on the Muslim Brotherhood, arresting thousands of its members and supporters. The interim government, backed by the military, targeted Brotherhood leadership and infrastructure, with over 16,000 people detained by late 2013 in connection with protests against the coup. On August 14, 2013, security forces dispersed large sit-ins at Rabaa al-Adawiya and al-Nahda squares in Cairo, resulting in at least 817 deaths according to official figures, though human rights groups estimate over 1,000 fatalities in what became the deadliest single-day incident in Egypt's modern history.
In December 2013, an Egyptian court designated the Muslim Brotherhood a terrorist organization, formalizing its ban and enabling broader legal actions against affiliates. This led to mass trials, with Egyptian courts issuing around 3,000 death sentences since 2014, many against Brotherhood members accused of violence or membership in a banned group, though appeals and international criticism reduced some executions. At least 92 opponents of the regime, including Brotherhood figures, have been executed since 2013, with high-profile cases involving leaders like Mohamed Badie, the Brotherhood's general guide, sentenced to life imprisonment. The crackdown extended to asset seizures, media shutdowns, and infiltration of Brotherhood networks, severely disrupting its domestic operations.
Despite the repression, the Brotherhood demonstrated resilience through underground activities and exile networks. Supporters initially organized protests under the Rabaa symbol, but these were met with swift suppression, leading many leaders to flee to Turkey and Qatar. Turkey hosted exiled Brotherhood figures, allowing conferences and media operations from Istanbul, while Qatar provided initial refuge post-2013 before expelling key leaders in 2014 amid Gulf pressure. In Egypt, fragmented cells persisted with low-level resistance, including sporadic attacks attributed to splinter groups like Hasm or Liwa al-Thawra, though the core Brotherhood publicly renounced violence to preserve its Islamist framework.
As of 2025, the Brotherhood remains outlawed in Egypt, with Sisi's regime maintaining tight control through surveillance and preemptive arrests, yet exiles continue advocacy abroad, adapting strategies amid declining support from former patrons like Turkey amid its own regional shifts. Internal debates over radicalization have surfaced, with some members questioning non-violent gradualism, but the organization has largely survived by decentralizing and leveraging diaspora ties, posing a latent challenge to the regime's stability.

Organizational Framework and Leadership

Hierarchical Structure and Internal Governance

The Muslim Brotherhood maintains a pyramidal hierarchical structure designed for resilience under repression, with authority centralized at the top and extending downward through layered administrative units. At the apex is the General Guide (Murshid 'Amm), who holds supreme executive authority, supervises all institutions, sets strategic direction, and represents the organization externally. The Guidance Bureau (Maktab al-Irshad), comprising approximately 15-16 senior members, assists the General Guide in operational oversight and policy implementation, often nominated by the Guide and approved by consultative bodies.
The Shura Council functions as the primary consultative assembly, consisting of elected representatives from regional branches who deliberate on major decisions, nominate Guidance Bureau members, and ratify the General Guide's appointment, though in practice the Guide wields significant influence over proceedings. Below this national level, the structure divides into administrative sectors such as dawah (propagation), education, political, economic, and security apparatuses, each with specialized sub-organizations coordinated by a Secretariat General.
Local organization builds from the base-level usra, intimate cells of 5-7 members led by a naqib (captain), which convene weekly for religious study, mutual accountability, and tarbiya (ideological indoctrination) to foster discipline and loyalty. Usras aggregate into shu'ba (branches), mantiqa (districts), idara (administrative offices), and qutr (regions), forming a scalable network that ensures grassroots control and rapid mobilization while maintaining secrecy.
Internal governance emphasizes absolute obedience through bay'at (pledges of allegiance) to superiors, rigorous vetting of members via stages from probationary (mu'ayyan) to active (mujahid), and compartmentalized operations to mitigate infiltration risks. This rigid system, instituted by founder Hassan al-Banna in 1928, prioritizes organizational survival and ideological conformity over flexibility, enabling endurance amid state crackdowns but contributing to internal rigidity and factionalism during leadership transitions. This hierarchical model is mirrored in Europe, featuring Shura councils and patterns of familial leadership continuity. Pan-European coordination occurs through the Council of European Muslims (CEM, formerly the Federation of Islamic Organizations in Europe or FIOE), with specialized subsidiaries such as Europe Trust for real estate and investments, the European Council for Fatwa and Research (ECFR) for issuing fatwas, and the Forum of European Muslim Youth and Student Organisations (FEMYSO) for youth mobilization. According to the February 2026 Israeli Ministry report, the Forum of European Muslim Youth and Student Organizations (FEMYSO), founded in 1996 in Brussels with initial cooperation from the World Assembly of Muslim Youth (WAMY), serves as an umbrella for Muslim youth and student groups across Europe. It comprises around 32 member organizations from 22 countries, many as youth chapters of local federations aligned with the Brotherhood network. FEMYSO holds participatory status with the Council of Europe and membership in its Advisory Council on Youth, as well as in the European Students' Union.

Succession of General Guides and Key Influencers

The Muslim Brotherhood's supreme leader, known as the General Guide or Murshid 'Amm, is elected by the Guidance Bureau from senior members, typically serving for life unless removed or incapacitated. This position shapes the organization's strategic direction, ideological emphasis, and responses to political pressures. The succession has historically reflected tensions between reformist pragmatism and ideological purism, influenced by Egypt's authoritarian regimes and internal debates over violence and political engagement.
Hassan al-Banna founded the Brotherhood in 1928 and served as its first General Guide until his assassination by Egyptian secret police on February 12, 1949, amid crackdowns following assassinations of officials by Brotherhood members. Under al-Banna, the group expanded rapidly through grassroots da'wah and social services while opposing British influence and secular nationalism. His successor, Hassan al-Hudaybi, appointed in 1951 after a period of dissolution under Nasser, led until his death in 1973 and emphasized legalism over revolution, authoring Preachers, Not Judges to counter radical interpretations of jihad.
Umar al-Tilmisani succeeded al-Hudaybi in 1973 and guided the Brotherhood through Sadat's era of partial liberalization until his death in 1986, advocating non-violent political participation and reconciliation with the state while navigating arrests. He was followed by Muhammad Hamid Abu al-Nasr (19861996), who continued pragmatic engagement, then Mustafa Mashhur (19962002), who maintained the focus on electoral alliances despite suppression. Ma'mun al-Hudaybi (20022004) briefly led before Muhammad Mahdi Akef (20042010), under whom the Brotherhood gained significant parliamentary seats in 2005. Muhammad Badi' was elected in January 2010, succeeding Akef, but faced imprisonment after the 2013 overthrow of Mohamed Morsi, receiving a life sentence in 2017.
Post-2013 crackdowns fragmented leadership, with Badi' nominally retaining the title from prison as of 2023. Acting guides emerged in exile: Ibrahim Mounir served until his death in 2023, followed by Salah Abdel-Haq as acting General Guide by mid-2025, amid reports of internal splits and a succession crisis involving younger radicals. These disruptions stem from Egypt's designation of the Brotherhood as a terrorist group in 2013 and arrests of top figures, forcing operations from Turkey and Qatar.
Beyond formal guides, key influencers have profoundly shaped ideology. Sayyid Qutb, a leading theorist executed in 1966 for alleged conspiracy, popularized concepts of jahiliyyah (pre-Islamic ignorance applied to modern states) and a vanguard elite for Islamic revolution in works like Milestones, influencing global jihadism despite al-Hudaybi's opposition to his takfir doctrines. Qutb's radicalism diverged from al-Banna's gradualism, inspiring factions favoring confrontation over accommodation, a tension persisting in Brotherhood debates.

Financing and Resource Mobilization

The Muslim Brotherhood sustains its operations primarily through member contributions, including mandatory subscriptions, voluntary donations, and zakat (obligatory alms), which form the core of its financial base. In Egypt, estimates suggest around 500,000 members contributed approximately LE100 million (about $14 million at historical rates) monthly prior to 2013, with dues structured as 8% of individual income or fixed annual fees such as 150-200 Egyptian pounds for affiliates in the Freedom and Justice Party. These funds are collected via local branches and channeled through trusted intermediaries to support da'wah (proselytization), social services, and political activities, with spikes during elections or crises. Post-2013 suppression in Egypt, diaspora communities in Europe and Turkey have continued zakat collection, often routed through affiliated councils like the European Council for Fatwa and Research, which issue fatwas directing funds to Brotherhood-linked institutions rather than external causes.
Business investments and commercial enterprises provide a secondary revenue stream, emphasizing quick-profit sectors like halal food, real estate, and trade to generate liquidity while embedding the organization in local economies. Brotherhood-affiliated businessmen, such as Khairat El-Shater and Safwan Thabet, reportedly donated tens of millions of Egyptian pounds in 2012 from their holdings, contributing to an estimated organizational war chest of 650 million pounds used for Mohamed Morsi's presidential campaign. Egyptian authorities seized over 1,125 companies and 105 schools linked to the group by 2016, indicating extensive pre-crackdown assets valued in billions, alongside offshore entities in Panama and Switzerland for international halal trade, which taps into a global market exceeding $2 trillion annually. In the West, networks involve banks like the defunct Al-Taqwa (initial capital $229 million in 1994) and charities such as Islamic Relief (revenues of 130 million in 2012), which blend legitimate aid with ideological funding. A February 2026 report by the Israeli Ministry of Diaspora Affairs notes that while historical funding for European Muslim Brotherhood networks came from Gulf states, they now rely primarily on Qatar (e.g., Qatar Charity donations to affiliates like the Institut Europen des Sciences Humaines (IESH) and partnerships with Islamic Relief Worldwide) and Turkey (via the Directorate of Religious Affairs (Diyanet) and Turkish Cooperation and Coordination Agency (TIKA) support for aligned mosques). Additional sources include real estate investments via the Europe Trust, membership fees, local donations, and EU grants to youth and education projects (e.g., the Forum of European Muslim Youth and Student Organisations (FEMYSO) received funding for anti-Islamophobia initiatives). The Israeli Ministry's February 2026 report indicates that the Europe Trust, established in the late 1990s (around 19961997) by FIOE/CEM and affiliates (including ECFR, FEMYSO, and IESH), functions as a financial mechanism managing real estate investments (e.g., mosques, community centers, student housing) in countries like the UK, France, Germany, and Belgium. It provides grants to network organizations, with assets including properties in Leeds (UK), Montpellier (France), and Zaandam (Netherlands, via Europe Trust Nederland founded 2006).
State sponsorship from allies like Qatar has supplemented internal resources, particularly during and after periods of adversity. Qatar provided financial transfers, including up to $850,000 secretly to the Brotherhood during Morsi's 2012-2013 presidency, and continues hosting exiled leaders while funding affiliated mosques and media outlets like Al Jazeera, which amplify Brotherhood narratives. Under Morsi's rule, access to state budgets enabled resource allocation for patronage, though this drew scrutiny for diverting public funds to organizational goals. Following the 2013 overthrow, mobilization shifted to clandestine methods, including informal trade, currency exchange offices for laundering, and smuggling commissions (e.g., 5% per operation), alongside diaspora remittances funneled through charities in Switzerland yielding over $5 million annually. These decentralized tactics, leveraging kinship ties and fictitious entities, ensure resilience against seizures, with overall estimates placing Brotherhood-linked assets at $16.7-20 billion globally, generating $500 million yearly from foreign investments.
Resource mobilization extends beyond finances to human and infrastructural assets, coordinated through hierarchical yet adaptable structures that prioritize loyalty and grassroots networks. Members are mobilized for fundraising via social services like schools and hospitals, which double as recruitment and revenue generators, while international branches adapt to local regulations by infiltrating Muslim community organizations for zakat oversight. In Jordan, recent investigations revealed persistent clandestine flows from membership fees and real estate, underscoring the group's capacity to sustain operations regionally despite bans. This model, rooted in Islamist principles of mutual aid, enables long-term endurance but has prompted asset freezes and designations in countries viewing it as a security risk.

Operations in the Arab World and Middle East

The Muslim Brotherhood (MB) has established branches and affiliated organizations throughout the Arab world and Middle East, often functioning through political parties, charitable networks, and oppositional structures while promoting Islamist governance and social reform. These operations typically emphasize da'wah (proselytization), grassroots mobilization, and electoral participation where permitted, but have frequently involved clandestine activities and alliances with militant groups, leading to designations as a terrorist organization by governments in Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates (UAE), Bahrain, and others since 2014. In countries opposing secular or monarchical rule, MB affiliates have pursued power through uprisings or proxies, though successes remain limited outside brief post-Arab Spring periods.
In Jordan, the MB branch was founded in 1945 by Abdel Latif Abou Qura and initially aligned with the Hashemite monarchy against pan-Arabism and communism, providing social services and gaining parliamentary seats via the Islamic Action Front (IAF) party since its legalization in 1989. The IAF, with over 10,000 members, secured up to 23% of parliamentary seats in elections through 2010 but faced declining support amid accusations of foreign ties and internal splits, culminating in a government ban on the MB in April 2025 for alleged subversive activities and refusal to register under new laws.
The Syrian MB, established in 1945 as an affiliate of the Egyptian parent group, grew influential in the 1960s before launching an armed insurgency against the Ba'athist regime from 1976 to 1982, including assassinations and urban bombings that killed hundreds of Alawite officers. The uprising peaked with the February 1982 Hama siege, where regime forces under Hafez al-Assad killed an estimated 10,000 to 40,000 MB members and sympathizers, effectively crushing the group domestically. Banned since, the MB operated in exile, joining the Syrian National Council in 2011 and supporting the civil war opposition, though it holds marginal influence compared to jihadist factions and has been criticized for authoritarian tendencies in proposed governance plans.
In Palestine, the MB established a presence in the 1940s, evolving into the Islamic Center network of mosques and charities by the 1970s under Sheikh Ahmed Yassin, which spawned Hamas as its militant offshoot in December 1987 during the First Intifada. Hamas's 1988 charter explicitly identifies it as the Palestinian MB branch, blending nationalist resistance with calls for Islamic rule over historic Palestine, and it has governed Gaza since seizing control from Fatah in 2007, launching rocket attacks and incursions against Israel, including the October 7, 2023, assault killing over 1,200. The MB's broader Palestinian operations focus on social welfare to build loyalty, though Hamas's armed strategy has overshadowed non-violent efforts.
In Lebanon, the Muslim Brotherhood operates through its official branch, Al-Jamaa al-Islamiyah (the Islamic Group), a Sunni Islamist political organization founded in 1964.
Gulf states exhibit polarized responses: Saudi Arabia initially hosted MB exiles and adopted elements of its thought in the 1960s but cracked down post-1990 Gulf War and designated it a terrorist group in 2014 over fears of ideological competition with Wahhabism; the UAE similarly banned MB-linked Al-Islah in 2013 for alleged infiltration of institutions; Bahrain outlawed it amid 2011 unrest. Qatar, conversely, shelters MB leaders like Yusuf al-Qaradawi and funds affiliates, using them to project influence, while Kuwait permits limited MB activities through the Islamic Constitutional Movement.
In North Africa, Tunisia's Ennahda Movement, founded in 1981 by Rached Ghannouchi as an MB affiliate, won 37% of votes in 2011 post-Jasmine Revolution elections, forming coalitions until 2014 amid economic criticism and terror attacks, later rebranding as "Muslim democratic" and dissolving its executive board in 2016; it faced further crackdowns under President Saied from 2021. Libya's MB operates via the Justice and Construction Party, which gained 17 seats in 2012 constituent assembly elections but fragmented amid civil war factions. Yemen's Al-Islah, an MB offshoot, held ministerial posts under Saleh and participated in post-2011 transitions but allied with jihadists and lost ground after Houthi advances. In Sudan, MB ideology shaped the National Islamic Front's 1989 coup under Omar al-Bashir, implementing sharia until the 2019 overthrow reduced its role.
Beyond Arab states, in Turkey, President Recep Tayyip Erdoan's Justice and Development Party (AKP) has forged ideological and operational ties with the MB since 2000s, hosting Egyptian exiles post-2013, broadcasting MB-aligned media via state-linked outlets, and employing the Rabia saluteoriginating from 2013 Cairo protestsas a solidarity symbol, despite Erdoan's denials of formal membership. This alliance bolsters Erdoan's regional ambitions but strains ties with MB opponents like Saudi Arabia and Egypt.

Presence in Africa, Asia, and Beyond

The Muslim Brotherhood maintains a notable presence in Sudan, where a branch was established in the early 1950s, exerting significant influence on politics through affiliates like the National Islamic Front (NIF), which orchestrated the 1989 Islamist coup under Hassan al-Turabi. This network has persisted amid civil conflict, with Brotherhood-linked groups such as the Sudanese Islamic Movement (SIM) and al-Bara Battalion leveraging instability to advance agendas, including marginalizing ethnic minorities and persecuting non-Muslims. In South Africa, the Brotherhood operates an active branch that serves as a logistical and ideological hub for continental expansion, facilitating recruitment, funding, and outreach to sub-Saharan Muslim communities despite limited direct branches elsewhere. Efforts in other sub-Saharan nations, such as Libya's civil war, have provided opportunities for Brotherhood militias to gain footholds, often backed by Qatar and Turkey against Gulf rivals, though direct organizational control remains tenuous outside Sudan and South Africa.
In Asia, the Brotherhood's ideology has profoundly shaped affiliate movements rather than formal branches, particularly through the Jamaat-e-Islami (JI) network founded by Abul A'la Maududi in 1941, which maintains doctrinal ties and collaborative ties with the Egyptian parent organization across Pakistan, Bangladesh, and India. In Pakistan, JI functions as a vanguard Islamist party promoting sharia governance, with historical alliances including support for mujahideen in Afghanistan and ongoing transnational Islamist solidarity. Bangladesh's JI branch, banned intermittently for war crimes ties, has sheltered South Asian militants and advanced Brotherhood-aligned goals like ummah unity, regaining influence post-2024 political shifts.
Indonesia hosts the most electorally successful Brotherhood-inspired entity in the Prosperous Justice Party (PKS), formed in 1998 from the Justice Party with direct assistance from Egyptian Brotherhood cadres, embedding tarbiyah (ideological indoctrination) methods to pursue gradual Islamization without explicit calls for an Islamic state. PKS has secured parliamentary seats, as in the 2024 elections where it joined coalitions, while advocating conservative Islamic values amid competition with more moderate groups like Nahdlatul Ulama. In Malaysia, while Parti Islam Se-Malaysia (PAS) shares Islamist objectives, its roots lie more in local ulama traditions than direct Brotherhood lineage, though indirect ideological cross-pollination occurs via regional dawah networks. Beyond these, Brotherhood influence wanes in Central and East Asia, limited by state repression in places like Uzbekistan and competition from Salafi strains, with no verified formal branches reported as of 2025.

Establishment in Europe and North America

The Muslim Brotherhood began establishing a presence in Europe during the 1950s, primarily as a refuge for members fleeing repression under Egypt's Gamal Abdel Nasser following crackdowns in the mid-1950s. Said Ramadan, son-in-law of founder Hassan al-Banna and a prominent Brotherhood ideologue, played a central role after fleeing Egypt in 1954 and settling in Geneva, Switzerland, by the late 1950s. In 1961, Ramadan founded the Islamic Center of Geneva, which served as a key European headquarters for coordinating Brotherhood activities among Arab expatriates and students.
In Germany, Ramadan influenced the takeover of the Munich mosque project starting in 1958, transforming it into a Brotherhood outpost and leading to the formation of the Islamic Society of Germany (IGD) by the early 1960s. These efforts focused on organizing Muslim students and workers, establishing mosques, and promoting Islamist education to sustain the movement's ideology amid host-government tolerance for anti-Nasser exiles. By the 1980s, affiliated groups expanded further, such as the Union of Islamic Organizations of France (UOIF, now Musulmans de France), evolving from earlier student associations in the 1960s. In Sweden, the Islamic Association in Sweden (IFiS) was founded in 1981 as an affiliate within the global Muslim Brotherhood network, functioning as its Swedish branch. It maintains a comparable organizational structure featuring an inner circle overseeing a extensive network, and ideologically promotes a political conception of Islam as a sharia-based framework for society. IFiS advances gradual Islamization through da'wah, community enclaves, and lobbying, applying pressure on regimes to foster Islamization without establishing its own political party.
The Brotherhood's network extended to other European countries, including Spain, the Netherlands, Great Britain, and additional Scandinavian states. In Great Britain, the Muslim Association of Britain (MAB), founded in 1997, operates as a key affiliate, engaging in political advocacy, social services, and da'wah while rooted in the Egyptian MB's Islamist ideology emphasizing Sharia implementation over secularization. Similar organizations in Spain and the Netherlands have focused on community building, education, and mosque networks, often adapting strategies of gradual influence through civil society to promote political Islam. These efforts, linked to the MB's foundational principles from Egypt, have provoked controversies and suspicions of infiltration or resistance to integration, particularly in Francewhere UOIF faced scrutiny for Islamist leaningsand Britain, prompting government reviews on compatibility with democratic norms.
According to a February 2026 report by Israel's Ministry for Diaspora Affairs and Combating Antisemitism, the Muslim Brotherhood's European presence, established in the 1950s1960s with migrant communities, evolved into a pan-European network coordinated through the Federation of Islamic Organizations in Europe (FIOE, founded 1989, renamed Council of European Muslims or CEM in 2020). CEM serves as a hub with a Shura Council and executive committee, representing affiliates in countries like France (UOIF/Musulmans de France), UK (MAB), and Germany (IGD), and engaging in advocacy with EU institutions.
Per the Israeli Ministry for Diaspora Affairs February 2026 report, the Federation of Islamic Organizations in Europe (FIOE), established in 1989 and registered initially in Leicester, UK (at Markfield Islamic Centre), relocated its headquarters to Brussels in 2007. In 2020, it formally rebranded as the Council of European Muslims (CEM) while retaining the same organizational framework, leadership patterns, and objectives as a non-profit coordinating shared goals for Muslim communities across Europe.
The Muslim Brotherhood's European operations involve coordinated networks such as the Federation of Islamic Organizations in Europe (FIOE, founded 1989; renamed Council of European Muslims or CEM in 2020) for overall coordination, the European Council for Fatwa and Research (ECFR, founded 1997) for issuing fatwas, the Forum of European Muslim Youth and Student Organisations (FEMYSO, founded 1996) for youth engagement, and the European Institute of Human Sciences (IESH, founded 1990) for imam training. These networks receive funding primarily from Qatar and Turkey. Additionally, some affiliated charities, such as the Union of Good (designated by the US Treasury and Israel in 2008), have been linked to providing indirect material support to Hamas through humanitarian channels.
In North America, Brotherhood-linked activities emerged in the late 1950s through Egyptian and other Arab students and professionals escaping Nasser's purges, laying groundwork for organized networks. The pivotal early entity was the Muslim Students Association (MSA), founded in January 1963 at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign by a group of Brotherhood-affiliated students aiming to unite Muslim campus chapters and propagate Islamist principles. MSA served as an incubator for subsequent organizations, including the Islamic Society of North America (ISNA) in the late 1970s, which addressed community infrastructure like mosques and schools while advancing Brotherhood da'wah (proselytization).
Additional groups solidified the network, such as the Islamic Circle of North America (ICNA) in 1971, focused on social services and ideology dissemination, and the Muslim American Society (MAS) in 1992, explicitly modeled on Brotherhood structures for grassroots mobilization. Pioneers like Jamal Barzinji and Ahmed Totonji, early Brotherhood immigrants, helped establish think tanks such as the International Institute of Islamic Thought in 1980 to intellectualize the movement's goals in an American context. These entities emphasized non-confrontational integration while pursuing long-term influence through education, civic engagement, and alliances, adapting the Brotherhood's Egyptian model to pluralistic societies.

Operational Strategies and Methods

Da'wah, Social Services, and Grassroots Mobilization

The Muslim Brotherhood's da'wah, or propagation of Islam, formed the core of its early activities following its establishment in 1928 by Hassan al-Banna, who viewed education as essential for moral reform (tahdhib) and reviving Islamic society against secular and Western influences. Al-Banna prioritized comprehensive religious instruction integrating Quran, Hadith, sharia, and practical skills, often drawing from holistic models like Montessori, to train individuals as preachers (du'a) and activists committed to establishing an Islamic order.
Early da'wah efforts included formal schools, starting with one in Isma'iliyya in 1931, and informal kutab classes that numbered over 300 by 1935, alongside youth-oriented programs such as the Rover Scouts launched in 1935, which emphasized oratory for preaching, athletic training, and preparation for jihad alongside religious study. These initiatives extended to family-based cells called usras, formalized in 1943 as groups of four to five members under a naqib (captain), which conducted localized preaching, mutual support, and ideological reinforcement to sustain mobilization amid political pressures.
Social services complemented da'wah by addressing material needs neglected by the Egyptian state, beginning in the early 1930s with the provision of clinics, hospitals, schools, and aid programs that offered lower-cost alternatives, such as childbirth services at $175 compared to $875 in public facilities, thereby building allegiance among the poor and lower-middle classes. Notable examples include rapid response to the 1992 Cairo earthquake, where Brotherhood volunteers supplied food, blankets, and medical clinics, demonstrating organizational efficiency and piety in action to enhance grassroots appeal.
Through usras and local branches, these services integrated Islamist indoctrination, creating a parallel welfare network that predated overt political engagement and proved resilient under bans, such as after 1954, by fostering dependency and loyalty that later translated into electoral strength, exemplified by alliances yielding 65 parliamentary seats in 1984. This approach embedded da'wah within everyday assistance, incrementally expanding influence by linking piety, social utility, and long-term societal Islamization.

Political Engagement and Electoral Tactics

The Muslim Brotherhood's political engagement emphasizes participation in electoral processes as a tactical avenue to promote its vision of Islamist governance, often through affiliated parties or independent candidates, while maintaining a non-violent public stance to build legitimacy. This approach leverages grassroots networks established via da'wah and social services to mobilize voters, framing elections as opportunities to incrementally advance Sharia-based reforms rather than immediate revolution. Critics, including analysts from think tanks like the Council on Foreign Relations, argue this reflects a strategic accommodation to democracy as a tool for power acquisition, with ideological commitments to Islamic law potentially overriding liberal democratic norms once in office.
In Egypt, the Brotherhood first contested parliamentary elections in 1984 as independents under authoritarian constraints, gradually increasing representation to 88 seats (approximately 20%) in the 2005 polls amid reported irregularities favoring the ruling party. Following Hosni Mubarak's ouster in the 2011 Arab Spring uprising, the group formed the Freedom and Justice Party (FJP), which secured 235 of 498 seats in the subsequent parliamentary elections from November 2011 to January 2012, capitalizing on organizational strength and voter disillusionment with secular rivals. Mohamed Morsi, the Brotherhood's candidate, won the presidential runoff on June 24, 2012, with 51.73% of the vote against Ahmed Shafik, marking the first democratic transfer of power but leading to governance marked by decrees consolidating authority and pushes for constitutional provisions prioritizing Islamic jurisprudence.
The Brotherhood's tactics in Egypt included selective alliances with non-Islamist groups during transitional phases, voter outreach through welfare programs, and rhetorical commitments to pluralism, though internal documents and statements, such as a 2012 declaration insisting on no compromise in applying Sharia, underscore a phased implementation strategy aligned with founder Hassan al-Banna's gradualist vision. Post-2013 military ouster of Morsi, the group reverted to protests and boycott strategies, rejecting further electoral participation under the Sisi regime as illegitimate.
Regionally, affiliates adopt similar electoral pragmatism. In Jordan, the Islamic Action Front (IAF), the Brotherhood's political arm, participated in elections since the 1980s, peaking with 31 seats in recent parliamentary contests before a 2025 government ban dissolved its legal status amid accusations of undermining state stability. Morocco's Justice and Development Party (PJD), ideologically linked to the Brotherhood, won 107 of 395 seats in 2011 post-Arab Spring elections, forming a coalition government under King Mohammed VI while moderating rhetoric to navigate monarchical constraints. In Tunisia, Ennahda, a Brotherhood-inspired movement, garnered 89 of 217 seats in the October 2011 constituent assembly elections, leading to Islamist-led transitions before shifting toward a more secular-leaning stance under electoral pressures. Outside the Arab world, in Sweden, Muslim Brotherhood-affiliated groups have pursued political influence through alliances, such as the 1999 cooperation agreement between the Muslim Council of Sweden and the Religious Social Democrats (Tro och Solidaritet), a faction of the Social Democratic Party, to enhance Muslim societal participation. These cases illustrate adaptive tactics: contesting polls to gain influence, forming coalitions for governance access, and using parliamentary platforms to advocate Sharia-inspired policies without direct confrontation.

Clandestine Activities, Alliances, and Proxy Groups

The Muslim Brotherhood established the al-Nim al-Kh (Special Apparatus) as a clandestine armed wing between 1938 and 1940, initially to target British colonial forces through sabotage and bombings, such as the 1942 grenade attack on a British officers' club in Cairo. This paramilitary structure expanded into domestic political violence, including the assassination of Egyptian Prime Minister Mahmoud Fahmi al-Nuqrashi Pasha on December 28, 1948, in retaliation for the Brotherhood's dissolution earlier that month, and an attempted assassination of Interior Minister Ibrahim Abdel Hadi on May 5, 1949. Led by figures like Abdel Rahman al-Sindi and Mustafa Mashhur, the apparatus trained thousands in secret camps for guerrilla tactics and participated in the 1948 Arab-Israeli War, deploying irregular fighters alongside Egyptian forces. A failed assassination attempt on President Gamal Abdel Nasser on October 26, 1954, prompted mass arrests and the apparatus's formal disbandment by the late 1950s amid severe repression, though underground networks persisted under subsequent bans.
Following the Egyptian military's ouster of Brotherhood-affiliated President Mohamed Morsi on July 3, 2013, the group reverted to clandestine operations, maintaining secret cells amid a nationwide crackdown that designated it a terrorist organization on December 25, 2013. These cells have been accused by Egyptian authorities of coordinating violent protests, jailbreaks during the 2011 uprising, and attacks on security forces, including the killing of policemen, leading to life sentences for leaders like Mohamed Badie in 2021. Post-2013 analyses indicate a shift toward militancy in some factions, with prison networks fostering radicalization and reevaluation of non-violent strategies, though the Brotherhood publicly disavows terrorism. 
The Brotherhood has formed strategic alliances with state actors like Qatar and Turkey, which provide exile havens, funding, and political cover for its global operations. Qatar has hosted Brotherhood leaders and financed affiliated media and social services since the 1990s, while shielding Hamas operatives; this support intensified after the 2013 coup, prompting a 2017 blockade by Saudi Arabia, Egypt, and others demanding Doha sever ties. Turkey, under President Recep Tayyip Erdogan, has similarly granted refuge to Egyptian exiles, backed Qatar against the blockade, and aligned ideologically with Brotherhood principles, enabling joint influence in regional Islamist networks. These partnerships facilitate resource flows and proxy influence, contrasting with adversarial relations toward Sunni monarchies opposing the group's transnational ambitions.
A primary proxy group is Hamas, founded in December 1987 as the Palestinian branch of the Muslim Brotherhood by Sheikh Ahmed Yassin during the First Intifada, explicitly positioning itself as an ideological extension to counter secular nationalists. Hamas's 1988 charter invokes Brotherhood founder Hassan al-Banna and outlines jihad against Israel to establish an Islamic state, drawing directly from Brotherhood doctrines of resistance and societal Islamization. The group maintains operational autonomy but receives ideological guidance, training influences, and indirect support via Brotherhood networks, with its military wing, the Izz ad-Din al-Qassam Brigades, embodying the clandestine militancy historically associated with the parent organization. While other affiliates exist in over 70 countries, few function as direct proxies for armed activities beyond Hamas, with the Brotherhood often exerting influence through fronts rather than overt control. Indirect support to Hamas has included channeling funds through networks such as the Union of Good, designated by the US Treasury in 2008 and by Israel as a terrorist financing entity supporting Hamas; Interpal, a UK charity with reported Hamas links; and the Al-Aqsa Foundation, banned in Germany in 2002 and by the EU in 2003 for funding Hamas. Funds have continued to be funneled despite sanctions, often via rebranding efforts.

Controversies, Criticisms, and Defensive Responses

Alleged Promotion of Violence and Terrorism Links

The Muslim Brotherhood maintained a clandestine paramilitary wing, known as the Special Apparatus (al-Nim al-Kh), during the 1940s in Egypt, which conducted assassinations targeting government officials deemed obstacles to Islamist goals. This apparatus was responsible for the December 28, 1948, assassination of Egyptian Prime Minister Mahmoud an-Nuqrashi Pasha, prompting the dissolution of the group and the killing of founder Hassan al-Banna in retaliation on February 12, 1949. Such actions reflected the Brotherhood's early endorsement of violence as a legitimate tool for political and religious advancement, diverging from purely non-violent da'wah efforts.
Sayyid Qutb, a leading Brotherhood ideologue imprisoned and executed by Egyptian authorities on August 29, 1966, advanced doctrines in works like Milestones (1964) that justified offensive jihad against "jahili" (pre-Islamic ignorance-like) regimes and societies, including takfir (declaring Muslims apostates) to legitimize violence against them. These ideas, emphasizing hakimiyyah (God's sole sovereignty) over secular rule, profoundly shaped global jihadism, serving as foundational texts for groups like Al-Qaeda and the Islamic State. Qutb's framework provided ideological cover for terrorism by framing resistance to non-Islamic governance as a religious duty, influencing figures such as Ayman al-Zawahiri, who began as a Brotherhood member before co-founding Al-Qaeda.
The Brotherhood's Palestinian affiliate, Hamas, founded on December 14, 1987, during the First Intifada, explicitly positioned itself as an extension of the group in its 1988 covenant, which invoked Brotherhood principles to justify armed struggle against Israel. Hamas's military wing, the Izz ad-Din al-Qassam Brigades, has conducted thousands of attacks, including suicide bombings and rocket barrages, resulting in over 1,000 Israeli civilian deaths since 1993, per Israeli government data. Although Hamas's 2017 charter omitted direct Brotherhood references amid geopolitical shifts, core ideological ties persist, with Hamas leaders affirming shared roots in Islamist resistance. U.S. congressional testimony has highlighted the Brotherhood's role in fostering such affiliates, which number among designated terrorist organizations in over 70 countries. According to Israel's Ministry for Diaspora Affairs February 2026 report, European networks maintain indirect ties to Hamas through charity umbrellas like the Union of Good (designated terrorist by US 2008 and Israel 2008), which includes groups such as Interpal (UK-based, US-designated 2003) and Al-Aqsa Foundation (banned in Germany 2002, EU-listed 20032014). The report describes these as channeling funds under humanitarian pretexts, with rebranding to evade restrictions, though no uniform European designation exists for the broader network. The Union of Good was founded around October 2000 with Yusuf al-Qaradawi as chairman. Its affiliates include Interpal, established in the UK in 1994 as the Palestinian Relief and Development Fund, and the Al-Aqsa Foundation, founded in 1991 in Aachen, Germany. These entities have faced terrorist designations, including the Union of Good by the United States and Israel in 2008, Interpal by the United States in 2003, and the Al-Aqsa Foundation by Germany in 2002 and the European Union in 2003 (with delistings in some cases by 2014).
Brotherhood networks have acted as conduits for radicalization, with members defecting to violent jihadist outfits; for instance, early Al-Qaeda operatives drew from Egyptian Brotherhood cells, bridging gradualist Islamism to takfiri terrorism. Post-2013 Egyptian coup allegations include Brotherhood incitement of riots and clashes that killed over 800 protesters and security personnel on August 14, 2013, though the group officially condemned violence while facing accusations of orchestrating unrest. Critics, including U.S. lawmakers, cite these patterns as evidence of an enduring promotion of militancy, contrasting with the Brotherhood's public disavowals amid designations as a terrorist entity by Egypt, Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Russia since 2013-2014.

Treatment of Minorities, Women, and Apostates

The Muslim Brotherhood's ideological framework, rooted in the writings of founders like Hassan al-Banna and ideologues such as Sayyid Qutb, subordinates non-Muslims to a hierarchical status under Islamic Sharia law, viewing them as dhimmis obligated to pay the jizya tax and accept restrictions on public religious expression in exchange for protection. This extends to Jews, whom affiliated bodies portray through antisemitic lenses; for instance, the European Council for Fatwa and Research (ECFR), chaired until 2018 by Brotherhood spiritual leader Yusuf al-Qaradawi and identified as the group's supreme religious authority in Europe, has issued content normalizing hostility toward Jews and Israel, including fatwas and the 2019 Euro Fatwa App featuring Qaradawi's foreword with negative characterizations of Jews, such as likening Islamic failings to Jewish permissions for theft, leading to its removal for hate speech. Qaradawi's rulings exclude Israeli civilians from conflict protections, deeming Israeli society entirely military and justifying suicide bombings against them as martyrdom operations. This theological supremacism positions non-Muslims as second-class citizens at best, incompatible with full equality in a Brotherhood-governed society. In practice, during the Brotherhood's brief rule in Egypt under President Mohamed Morsi from June 2012 to July 2013, Coptic Christianscomprising about 10% of Egypt's populationfaced escalated sectarian violence, including church burnings and mob attacks, with Brotherhood partisans accusing Copts of political disloyalty for opposing Morsi's Islamist agenda. Human rights reports documented over 50 attacks on Coptic properties in the months following Morsi's ouster in July 2013, often linked to Brotherhood supporters retaliating against perceived alliances between Copts and the post-Morsi government.
Regarding women, the Brotherhood advocates roles confined to domestic spheres and modest public participation aligned with strict Islamic norms, emphasizing veiling, gender segregation, and obedience to male guardians as divinely ordained to preserve societal order. In a March 2013 statement, the Brotherhood condemned a United Nations declaration on women's rights for promoting sexual freedom, equal inheritance, and the right of Muslim women to marry non-Muslims, arguing it would "definitely lead to the complete disintegration of the family" and contradict Sharia principles. While the group has pragmatically supported limited female political involvementsuch as quotas in electionsto expand influence, this is framed as empowering women within Islamic bounds, not liberal equality, and internal structures like the Muslim Sisters section reinforce traditional hierarchies over autonomous rights.
On apostasy, the Brotherhood's commitment to implementing Sharia law aligns with classical Islamic jurisprudence prescribing death for those who renounce Islam after puberty, following a repentance period, as articulated in foundational texts influencing the group; this penalty underscores the view of apostasy as a profound betrayal threatening the ummah's unity. Sayyid Qutb's doctrine of takfir, which declares Muslim societies in jahiliyyah (pre-Islamic ignorance) unless fully Sharia-compliant, extends to labeling individuals or rulers as apostates warranting confrontation, though the Brotherhood has historically avoided broad individual takfir to maintain mass appeal. Critics note that in Brotherhood-influenced contexts, such as proposed constitutions in Egypt, apostasy provisions would curtail religious freedom, treating converts out of Islam as threats to the Islamic state's cohesion rather than affording exit rights.

Accusations of Deception (Taqiyya) and Infiltration

Critics of the Muslim Brotherhood have accused the organization and its affiliates of employing taqiyya-inspired deception, a practice rooted in Islamic jurisprudence permitting dissimulation under duress, to conceal supremacist objectives while presenting a moderate, integrationist image to Western audiences. This alleged strategy involves publicly advocating democratic participation and civil rights while privately pursuing the establishment of Islamic governance through subversion, as evidenced by internal documents uncovered in U.S. federal investigations. Such accusations draw from declassified Brotherhood writings that prioritize "settlement" as a form of "civilization jihad" to undermine host societies from within, rather than overt confrontation.
A pivotal document supporting these claims is the 1991 "Explanatory Memorandum on the General Strategic Goal for the Group in North America," authored by Brotherhood leader Mohamed Akram and seized during a 2004 FBI raid in Virginia. The 18-page internal memo explicitly outlines a long-term infiltration plan, describing the Brotherhood's work in the U.S. as "a kind of grand Jihad in eliminating and destroying the Western civilization from within and 'sabotaging' its miserable house by their hands and the hands of the believers so that it is eliminated" and God's religion is made victorious. It identifies over two dozen U.S.-based organizationssuch as the Islamic Society of North America (ISNA), Muslim Students Association (MSA), and North American Islamic Trust (NAIT)as Brotherhood "friendship societies" or fronts to consolidate influence among Muslim communities and extend reach into non-Muslim institutions. These entities were later implicated in the 2008 Holy Land Foundation trial, where prosecutors presented evidence of a Brotherhood-linked network funneling over $12 million to Hamas, a designated terrorist group, under the guise of charitable aid.
In Europe, similar patterns of alleged entryism have been documented in security assessments. A 2021 French Senate report warned of Brotherhood infiltration into public institutions, including schools and local governments, through ostensibly apolitical associations that promote parallel Islamic norms while exploiting welfare systems and lobbying for accommodations like halal food and gender segregation. German intelligence evaluations, as compiled in a 2023 George Washington University analysis, describe Brotherhood networks using cultural centers and student groups to foster separatism, with leaders issuing dual messages: conciliatory public rhetoric on integration alongside private endorsements of sharia supremacy. For instance, the Federation of Islamic Organizations in Europe (FIOE), tied to Brotherhood ideology, has advocated for "parallel societies" in policy papers while denying Islamist agendas in media appearances.
A 2026 report by Israel's Ministry for Diaspora Affairs and Combating Antisemitism accuses the Muslim Brotherhood of employing dual messaging in Europe, presenting public moderation and integration while internally promoting narratives of victimhood, segregation, and antisemitism. The report highlights infiltration tactics through EU advisory roles, including the Forum of European Muslim Youth and Student Organisations (FEMYSO) and the Council of European Muslims (CEM), which have gained influence in institutions like the Council of Europe. It also details efforts in youth radicalization via FEMYSO's programs, framing them as ideological incubators for Brotherhood-aligned elites.
These infiltration tactics reportedly extend to political and media spheres, where Brotherhood affiliates are accused of forming alliances with leftist groups to amplify narratives of victimhood, thereby deflecting scrutiny. In the UK, a 2015 government review under Prime Minister David Cameron found that organizations like the Muslim Association of Britain (MAB) maintained Brotherhood links while influencing policy on issues like counter-extremism, often by portraying critics as Islamophobic. In Sweden, government inquiries into parallel social structures and Islamist infiltration have raised concerns about Muslim Brotherhood-linked networks influencing political parties, including the Social Democrats (Socialdemokraterna) and the Green Party (Miljpartiet). Detractors, including former Brotherhood members, argue this duplicity aligns with Yusuf al-Qaradawi's endorsements of phased jihadpeaceful when weak, violent when strongallowing tactical moderation without abandoning core goals. Empirical outcomes, such as the growth of no-go zones in Sweden and France correlated with Brotherhood-influenced mosques, are cited as downstream effects of unchecked embedding.

Brotherhood Rebuttals and Victim Narratives

The Muslim Brotherhood has consistently rebutted accusations of promoting violence by asserting commitment to peaceful, democratic processes. In statements following crackdowns, Brotherhood spokespersons have emphasized rejection of violence in favor of "peaceful and constitutional struggle based on persuasion and dialogue." This position was reiterated in 2019 amid renewed bans, with the group declaring it would persist with "moderate and peaceful thinking" irrespective of legal designations. Such rebuttals frame the organization as a non-violent Islamist movement focused on electoral participation and social reform, distancing itself from militant offshoots by claiming ideological independence.
In response to terrorist designations by governments like Egypt, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE, the Brotherhood portrays these as politically motivated smears aimed at suppressing legitimate opposition. Leaders argue that such labels ignore the group's renunciation of arms post-1970s and its participation in democratic elections, as seen in Egypt's 2012 victory. Critics of designations, including some Western analysts, echo this by warning that proscription could push moderates toward extremism, though evidence of Brotherhood tolerance for violence in affiliates like Hamas complicates these defenses. The narrative positions the group as targeted for its Islamist ideology rather than operational threats.
Central to Brotherhood defensive strategies is a victimhood narrative emphasizing historical and contemporary persecution by secular authoritarian regimes. Founded amid British colonial influence, the group invokes suppression under Nasser including executions following the 1948 assassination of Prime Minister Nuqrashi Pashaas martyrdom fueling resilience. Post-2013, after Mohamed Morsi's ouster, this evolved into claims of a military coup denying democratic will, with the Rabaa al-Adawiya sit-in dispersal on August 14, 2013, depicted as a deliberate massacre of peaceful protesters.
The Rabaa events, where security forces cleared encampments resulting in 600-1,000 deaths per varying accounts, became symbolic of Brotherhood suffering, with the four-fingered "Rabia" gesture adopted globally to signify solidarity against oppression. Funerals and commemorations reinforce this storyline, portraying deceased members as martyrs akin to early Islamic figures, while downplaying protester armament or clashes that Egyptian authorities cite as justification. Human rights organizations like Amnesty International and Human Rights Watch have amplified elements of this narrative, reporting systematic killings, though their alignment overlooks Brotherhood incitement documented in trials. This framing sustains internal cohesion and garners sympathy in the West, despite empirical evidence of post-Rabaa violent reprisals by some factions.
Internationally, the victim trope extends to exile communities in Turkey and Qatar, where leaders like Mohammed Badie decry "genocide" against the group to rally support. Figures such as Turkish President Erdogan have endorsed this by adopting the Rabia symbol, signaling geopolitical backing against shared adversaries. This narrative, while rooted in real repression, strategically omits Brotherhood governance failures under Morsisuch as economic mismanagement and minority exclusionsthat precipitated mass protests leading to the 2013 events.

International Status and Geopolitical Implications

Terrorist Designations by States and Organizations

Egypt designated the Muslim Brotherhood a terrorist organization on December 25, 2013, following the military ouster of President Mohamed Morsi, whom the group had supported, amid accusations of the organization's role in violence and subversion. This move banned its activities and led to mass arrests of members. Saudi Arabia followed on March 7, 2014, classifying the Brotherhood alongside jihadist groups active in Syria, reflecting Riyadh's view of it as a threat to monarchical stability. 
The United Arab Emirates designated the Muslim Brotherhood as a terrorist entity on November 15, 2014, including its local affiliate, the Islah group, in a broader list aimed at curbing Islamist opposition. Bahrain aligned with its Gulf neighbors by designating the organization in 2014, citing its links to domestic unrest and transnational Islamist networks. Russia has included the Muslim Brotherhood on its terrorist list, viewing it as part of broader Islamist extremism, with the designation encompassing its affiliates. Syria, under the Assad regime, has long treated the group as terrorist since the 1980s, following its armed uprising crushed in the Hama massacre of 1982.
The Trump administration designated branches of the Muslim Brotherhood in Lebanon, Egypt, and Jordan as terrorist entities on January 13, 2026. Secretary of State Marco Rubio stated that the USA will "eliminate the capabilities & operations of MB chapters that threaten U.S. citizens & our national security." The Departments of State and Treasury listed the Lebanese branch as a foreign terrorist organization and the Egyptian and Jordanian branches as specially designated global terrorists for providing material support to Hamas, which is itself both a foreign terrorist organization and a specially designated global terrorist entity. The Muslim Brotherhood has a longstanding record of perpetrating acts of terror, and we are working aggressively to cut them off from the financial system, U.S. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent stated. This administration will deploy the full scope of its authorities to disrupt, dismantle and defeat terrorist networks wherever they operate in order to keep Americans safe. John Hurley, U.S. Treasury Undersecretary for Terrorism and Financial Intelligence, stated: The Muslim Brotherhood has inspired, nurtured and funded terrorist groups like Hamas, that are direct threats to the safety and security of the American people and our allies. The State Department stated that Al-Jama'a al-Islamiyya (the Lebanese branch of the Muslim Brotherhood) coordinated with Hamas and Hezbollah to activate its armed wing, the al-Fajr Forces, which engaged in rocket attacks against Israel following the Hamas-led October 7, 2023, attacks, and pushed for a more formal alignment with the Hezbollah-Hamas axis. This prompted an Israel Defense Forces preemptive strike against the branchs al-Fajr Force in March 2024. Additionally, the Lebanese Army dismantled a covert military training camp that included Lebanese Muslim Brotherhood and Hamas militants in July 2025. This action aims to disrupt their financial networks and imposes sanctions on the groups and their members.
The entities join other Muslim Brotherhood entities and offshoots that already fell under U.S. sanctions, including Hamas, Palestinian Islamic Jihad, Egyptian Islamic Jihad, Gamaa al-Islamiyya, Harakat Sawad Misr and Liwa al-Thawra.
Argentina designated the Muslim Brotherhood chapters in Lebanon, Egypt, and Jordan as terrorist organizations on January 15, 2026, under President Javier Milei's administration, citing them as a serious threat to national security. The measure, involving the Ministries of Foreign Affairs, Security, and State Intelligence, added these entities to the Public Registry of Persons and Entities Linked to Acts of Terrorism (RePET). It cited official reports documenting involvement in transnational illegal activities, acts of terrorism, public calls for violent extremism, and operational links to groups such as Hamas and Hezbollah, aligning with recent U.S. designations.
The United States designated the Sudanese Muslim Brotherhood (SMB; also known as the Sudanese Islamic Movement), composed of the Sudanese Islamic Movement and its armed wing the al-Baraa Bin Malik Brigade (BBMB), as a Specially Designated Global Terrorist on March 9, 2026, with the intent to designate the group as a Foreign Terrorist Organization. The SMB uses unrestrained violence against civilians to undermine efforts to resolve the conflict in Sudan and advance its violent Islamist ideology. The SMB has contributed upwards of 20,000 fighters to the war in Sudan, many receiving training and other support from Irans Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps. SMBs BBMB fighters have conducted mass executions of civilians in areas they captured, and repeatedly and summarily executed civilians based on race, ethnicity, or perceived affiliation with opposition groups. The Treasury Department designated BBMB in September 2025 pursuant to Executive Order 14098, Imposing Sanctions on Certain Persons Destabilizing Sudan and Undermining the Goal of a Democratic Transition, for its role in Sudans brutal war. This designation blocks all property and interests in property of the Sudanese Muslim Brotherhood that are in the United States or in the possession or control of a U.S. person. U.S. persons are generally prohibited from conducting business with the sanctioned entity. Persons engaging in certain transactions or activities with the Sudanese Muslim Brotherhood may expose themselves to sanctions risk, including secondary sanctions pursuant to counterterrorism authorities.
More recently, Kenya designated the Muslim Brotherhood a terrorist entity on September 19, 2025, under its Prevention of Terrorism Act, alongside Hizb ut-Tahrir, due to concerns over ideological influence on local extremism. No major international organizations, such as the United Nations or European Union, have issued a terrorist designation for the global Muslim Brotherhood, though some member states have imposed national bans or restrictions.

Relations with Hostile and Supportive Governments

The Muslim Brotherhood (MB) maintains symbiotic relationships with select governments that share its Islamist worldview or utilize it as a proxy against rivals, while enduring suppression from regimes perceiving its grassroots mobilization and electoral strategies as existential threats to secular authoritarianism or monarchical stability. Qatar and Turkey stand as primary patrons, providing financial aid, safe haven for exiles, and platforms for MB-affiliated media, enabling the group to sustain operations despite setbacks in Egypt. In contrast, Egypt, Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates (UAE), Bahrain, Syria, and Russia have imposed bans, terrorist designations, or military crackdowns, often coordinating through coalitions like the 2017 Gulf blockade of Qatar explicitly citing MB support as a casus belli. These dynamics reflect causal tensions: supportive states leverage MB ideology to project influence in Sunni-majority arenas, whereas hostile ones prioritize regime security against the group's history of infiltrating institutions and inciting uprisings.
Qatar has extended overt backing to the MB since the Arab Spring, disbursing approximately $7.5 billion in loans and grants to Egypt's MB president Mohammed Morsi between 2012 and 2013, alongside hosting MB leaders and funding affiliated outlets like Al Jazeera, which amplified pro-MB narratives. This support persisted post-2013 Egyptian coup, with Doha sheltering fugitives and tacitly endorsing MB-linked scholars' calls for jihad, despite a 2017 agreement to curtail ties amid the Saudi-UAE-Egypt-Bahrain embargo. Turkey, under President Recep Tayyip Erdoan, has similarly provided refuge to MB exiles since 2013, including figures like Mohammed Badie, while the Justice and Development Party (AKP)'s ideological lineage traces to MB-influenced Turkish Islamists like Necmettin Erbakan. Erdoan has publicly defended MB figures, hosted MB-aligned events, and coordinated with the group against Egyptian President Abdel Fattah el-Sisi, using R4bia gestures symbolizing MB solidarity during rallies as late as 2023. Iran's ties, though strained by Sunni-Shia divides, involve pragmatic convergence: MB interim leader Saleh Abdel Haq pledged support for Tehran in June 2025 amid Israel tensions, and historical ideological overlapssuch as anti-Western stanceshave fostered limited cooperation against mutual adversaries like Sisi's Egypt.
Hostility dominates in Egypt, where Sisi's regime, following the July 2013 military ouster of Morsi, designated the MB a terrorist organization in December 2013, dissolving its Freedom and Justice Party, seizing assets, and arresting over 16,000 members by 2014 amid Rabaa massacre crackdowns that killed hundreds of protesters. Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Bahrain view the MB as a destabilizing force challenging Gulf monarchies' legitimacy, with Riyadh designating it terrorist in 2014 and all three imposing a blockade on Qatar from June 2017 to January 2021 over Doha's MB patronage. Bahrain echoed this in March 2014, condemning MB threats to GCC stability. Syria's Ba'athist regime under the Assads banned the MB after the 1963 coup, culminating in the 1982 Hama massacre that killed 10,000-40,000 during an Islamist uprising led by the group. Russia classified the MB terrorist in 2003, driven by aversion to political Islam's potential to incite domestic separatism, and has backed Sisi while blocking MB participation in Syrian reconciliation processes. These adversarial stances stem from empirical precedents of MB-orchestrated violence, such as assassinations and insurrections, rather than mere ideological divergence, underscoring the group's dual role as both ideological exporter and perceived infiltrator.

Debates in the West Over Engagement Versus Confrontation

In Western policy circles, debates over the Muslim Brotherhood (MB) have centered on whether to pursue engagementthrough dialogue, electoral inclusion, and partnershipsor confrontation, via restrictions, designations, and counter-ideological measures. Proponents of engagement argue that the MB represents a pragmatic Islamist force amenable to moderation via democratic processes, citing its participation in elections in Egypt post-2011 Arab Spring as evidence of potential alignment with pluralistic norms. This view gained traction during the Obama administration, which met with Egyptian President Mohamed Morsi, an MB affiliate, in 2012 to foster stability amid regional transitions. European advocates, including some in the UK, have similarly emphasized outreach to MB-linked civic groups to preempt radicalization, viewing them as counterweights to more violent Salafi-jihadist networks.
Critics of engagement contend that the MB's foundational ideology, rooted in Hassan al-Banna's calls for global Islamic governance and Sayyid Qutb's rejection of secular systems, inherently conflicts with Western liberal democracy, rendering dialogue illusory and enabling infiltration. They highlight MB affiliations with designated terrorist entities like Hamas, whose 1988 charter invokes MB origins, documented funding flows to such groups, and instances such as the attendance of Swedish Social Democratic MP Jamal El-Haj at the Hamas-connected European Palestinians Conference in Malm in May 2023, arguing that non-confrontational policies risk legitimizing supremacist agendas under guises of moderation. In Europe, security assessments from agencies in Germany, Austria, Belgium, and Sweden have flagged MB networks for promoting parallel societies and exerting influence via mosques and charities, prompting calls for bans on entities like the Islamic Community Millennium in Austria (2015) and scrutiny of the Federation of Islamic Organizations in Europe. Similarly, in October 2025, a motion in the Dutch House of Representatives to ban the Muslim Brotherhood and affiliated organizations, proposed by MPs Boon and Wilders, received votes in favor from the PVV, VVD, BBB, JA21, and SGP but failed by two votes.
The confrontation camp has pushed for formal terrorist designations, with U.S. lawmakers introducing bills like the Muslim Brotherhood Terrorist Designation Act in 2017 and reintroducing it in 2025 to mandate review of the group's global affiliates, citing over 70 countries' involvement and ties to violence. Opponents warn that such moves could alienate non-violent MB factions, drive them underground, and hinder intelligence cooperation, as articulated by analysts who note the group's decentralized structure complicates uniform labeling without clear operational ties to attacks in the West. These divisions reflect broader tensions: engagement risks underestimating ideological rigidity, evidenced by MB reversions to authoritarianism in power, while confrontation demands granular evidence to avoid overreach, amid sources like think tanks showing partisan dividesconservative outlets emphasizing threats and progressive ones prioritizing inclusion.
Recent U.S. policy under the Trump administration in 2025 has leaned toward confrontation, with figures like Secretary of State Marco Rubio endorsing designation to disrupt MB financial networks, contrasting earlier hesitations that preserved diplomatic flexibility. In the EU, fragmented approaches persist, with France's 2021 anti-separatism law targeting MB-inspired radicalism without a bloc-wide ban, underscoring how empirical assessments of MB activitiessuch as proselytization over overt politics in diaspora communitiesinfluence outcomes.
