Digital Radio Mondiale
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Digital Radio Mondiale (DRM) is an open international standard for digital audio broadcasting that enables the transmission of high-quality sound and multimedia services across all analogue radio frequency bands, including longwave, mediumwave, shortwave, and VHF (including FM), using a single unified system. Developed by a consortium of broadcasters, manufacturers, and regulators, DRM employs orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing (OFDM) modulation and advanced audio coding like AAC to deliver robust, spectrum-efficient signals suitable for local, regional, national, and international coverage. Unlike band-specific digital systems, DRM's flexibility allows simulcasting with analogue broadcasts during transition periods, ensuring compatibility and gradual migration without disrupting existing infrastructure.
The DRM Consortium was established in 1997 to address the need for digitizing shortwave and mediumwave broadcasting, with the initial system specification published by the European Telecommunications Standards Institute (ETSI) in 2001 as ES 201 980, focusing on frequencies below 30 MHz (DRM30). In 2005, the standard was extended to VHF bands (DRM+), enabling higher data rates up to 186 kbit/s for enhanced audio and services like text, images, and electronic program guides, while maintaining interoperability across modes. Endorsed by the International Telecommunication Union (ITU) in its recommendations for digital sound broadcasting, DRM requires no licensing fees from the consortium, with royalties paid directly to intellectual property holders, promoting widespread adoption.
Key technical features include forward error correction for reliable reception in challenging propagation conditions, such as those common in shortwave, and multiplexed channels for audio (MSC), service description (SDC), and fast access (FAC) to enable quick tuning and synchronization. DRM offers superior audio qualityCD-like stereo with low bit rates of 20-70 kbit/sand robustness against interference, making it ideal for long-distance propagation and areas with limited internet access. Additional capabilities support multilingual broadcasting, traffic information, and emergency alerts, enhancing its utility for public service media.
Adoption of DRM has grown steadily, particularly in regions seeking cost-effective digital upgrades for AM bands. India has implemented DRM on mediumwave since 2012 for domestic services and shortwave for international outreach, with 41 transmitters operational. In Europe, countries like France and Romania use DRM for shortwave international broadcasting, while trials in Africa and Southeast Asia demonstrate its viability for underserved populations. As of 2025, China has formally adopted DRM as a national industry standard (GY/T 423-2025, issued July 29, 2025) for domestic short- and medium-wave broadcasting to accelerate the transition from analogue AM. This milestone, alongside ongoing efforts in India to standardize DRM for VHF Band II, positions the technology as a key enabler for global digital radio equity.

Overview and History

Definition and Purpose

Digital Radio Mondiale (DRM) is an open, non-proprietary digital audio broadcasting system designed primarily for the longwave (LW), medium wave (MW), and shortwave (SW) bands below 30 MHz. It serves as a successor to analog amplitude modulation (AM) broadcasting, enabling the transmission of high-quality digital audio and associated data services within these frequency ranges.
The primary purpose of DRM is to deliver CD-quality audio with up to 20 kHz bandwidth, alongside multimedia data services such as text, images, and electronic program guides, while achieving efficient spectrum utilization. This system aims to revitalize international and regional broadcasting, particularly in areas with limited infrastructure, by supporting robust signal propagation over long distances with reduced transmitter power compared to analog systems. In contrast to traditional analog AM, which is limited to 5-10 kHz audio bandwidth and susceptible to interference, DRM offers superior audio fidelity, enhanced error protection, and the capacity to multiplex multiple audio channels or data streams within a single frequency allocation.
DRM targets listeners in developing regions where affordable digital receivers can bridge access gaps, international broadcasters seeking wide-area coverage via shortwave, and emergency services requiring reliable dissemination of critical information over challenging propagation conditions. Its technical foundation, including orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing (OFDM), ensures resilience against fading and multipath distortion prevalent in lower frequency bands.

Development and Standardization

The Digital Radio Mondiale (DRM) consortium was formed in early 1998 as a non-profit international organization comprising broadcasters, network operators, transmitter and receiver manufacturers, and research institutions to develop and promote an open standard for digital sound broadcasting in the LF, MF, and HF bands below 30 MHz. Founding efforts involved key players such as the BBC Research and Development Department and Deutsche Welle, aiming to create a robust, spectrum-efficient system compatible with existing analog infrastructure.
Early development progressed through collaborative field testing, with the first over-the-air test transmissions conducted in 1999 to evaluate system performance under real-world propagation conditions in the shortwave band. These trials demonstrated the viability of orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing (OFDM) for mitigating ionospheric interference, paving the way for formal standardization. In April 2001, the International Telecommunication Union Radiocommunication Sector (ITU-R) issued Recommendation BS.1514, defining the system parameters for digital sound broadcasting below 30 MHz and endorsing DRM as a global standard for these frequencies. The ITU's role extended to spectrum allocation, ensuring harmonized use of the bands through planning parameters in subsequent reports like BS.2144 (2009).
Standardization advanced with the European Telecommunications Standards Institute (ETSI), which published the initial DRM system specification as TS 101 980 in September 2001, evolving into ES 201 980 by April 2003. A significant update came in June 2004 with version 2.1.1, refining modulation, error protection, and multiplexing for improved robustness. The specification has since been iteratively enhanced, with version 3.2.1 in June 2012 adding support for advanced features like speech services and multi-language applications; HE-AAC for efficient compression was supported from earlier versions around 2004. xHE-AAC was introduced in version 4.1.2 in April 2017 for better low-bitrate performance, enabling up to three services per 9 or 10 kHz channel. The latest version, 4.3.1, was published in November 2023, incorporating further refinements including loudness metadata. Opus compatibility was added in software implementations around this period for low-latency applications, though not as a core standard feature.
International collaboration was bolstered by extensive field trials in the 2000s, coordinated by the DRM consortium and ITU affiliates to validate performance across diverse environments. In Europe, trials by the BBC and others in 20002003 confirmed reliable coverage over 1,000 km via shortwave. India conducted major tests through All India Radio starting in 2007, demonstrating simulcast with analog AM for hybrid reception in urban areas. African trials, such as those in South Africa and Namibia in 20082009, highlighted DRM's effectiveness for long-distance broadcasting in challenging tropical propagation conditions, informing global adoption guidelines. These efforts underscored DRM's potential to revitalize shortwave and medium-wave services with near-FM audio quality and data capabilities.

Key Features

Audio and Data Capabilities

Digital Radio Mondiale (DRM) supports high-quality audio transmission, including stereo and multichannel surround sound configurations. The system employs advanced codecs such as xHE-AAC and AAC with Spectral Band Replication (SBR) and Parametric Stereo (PS), enabling stereo audio at bitrates starting from 12 kbps and surround sound (up to 5.1 channels via MPEG Surround) at higher rates, typically up to 64 kbps for robust delivery in various frequency bands. This allows for FM-like quality on medium and shortwave bands, with enhanced clarity and reduced artifacts compared to analog broadcasting.
For live broadcasting, DRM incorporates low-latency modes, such as Robustness Mode E with tune-in delays of 0.4 seconds for stereo at 32 kbps, facilitating real-time applications like sports events or news. While dynamic range compression is not explicitly defined in core specifications, the codecs inherently manage wide dynamic ranges through scalable bitrate allocation, preserving audio fidelity across varying signal conditions.
DRM's data services complement audio by delivering ancillary multimedia content within the same transmission stream. Key applications include Journaline, a text-based system for structured news, weather, and information delivery at low bitrates around 200 bps, supporting multiple languages and hierarchical navigation. The Electronic Programme Guide (EPG) provides scheduling details, station logos, and program metadata via Service and Programme Information (SPI). For visual content, the Basic Waveform Service (BWS) enables slideshows and images, while Multimedia Object Transfer (MOT) supports file delivery, such as documents or updates.
These services integrate with audio for enhanced user experiences, such as RDS-like traffic announcements or emergency alerts through the Emergency Warning Functionality (EWF), which activates receivers with multilingual audio, text, and Journaline alerts even in standby mode.
In challenging reception environments, DRM ensures robustness through unequal error protection (UEP) for AAC audio streams, with xHE-AAC employing decoder-based error concealment techniques like frame interpolation and fade-in/out, as per the latest ETSI ES 201 980 v4.3.1 (2023). This allows fallback to lower bitrates or protection levels without total signal loss, maintaining partial service continuitysuch as speech-only audio at 8 kbpswhile spectrum efficiency supports multiplexing multiple audio and data streams.

Spectrum Efficiency and Simulcast

Digital Radio Mondiale (DRM) achieves significant spectrum efficiency by enabling the transmission of multiple audio programs and data services within a single frequency channel, offering up to four times the utilization compared to traditional analog AM broadcasting. This optimization allows broadcasters to deliver several independent streamstypically three audio channels plus datawithin the standard 9 kHz raster used for medium and shortwave bands, thereby maximizing the use of limited spectrum resources without requiring additional frequencies.
In simulcast mode, DRM facilitates a smooth transition from analog to digital by allowing simultaneous transmission of analog AM signals in the lower sideband and DRM in the upper sideband of the same channel. This configuration uses a narrow 4.5 kHz bandwidth for the DRM signal in a 9 kHz channel or 5 kHz in a 10 kHz channel, minimizing interference while preserving compatibility with existing analog receivers.
DRM also provides substantial power savings, requiring approximately 4-6 kW (40-60% less power) for coverage equivalent to 10 kW analog systems, due to its efficient digital modulation and reduced peak-to-average power ratio. The implementation of single-frequency networks (SFNs) further enhances reach by synchronizing multiple low-power transmitters on the same frequency, achieving wider coverage with overall lower energy consumptionup to 40-60% less than analog equivalents.
On shortwave bands, DRM leverages ionospheric reflection for long-distance propagation, enabling global coverage ideal for international broadcasting with robust signal penetration over thousands of kilometers. This propagation advantage, combined with spectrum efficiency, supports high-quality audio delivery in challenging environments.

Technical Architecture

Source Coding

Digital Radio Mondiale (DRM) employs source coding to compress audio signals efficiently, enabling high-quality broadcast within limited bandwidth constraints typical of medium-wave (MW), short-wave (SW), and VHF bands. The process focuses on perceptual audio coding, which discards inaudible spectral components to minimize data while preserving listener experience. This is achieved through transform-based techniques that represent audio in the frequency domain, allowing for scalable bitrate adaptation based on channel conditions and service requirements.
The primary audio codec in DRM is xHE-AAC (extended High-Efficiency Advanced Audio Coding), standardized as part of the MPEG-H 3D Audio framework, which supports bitrates ranging from approximately 8 kbit/s to 72 kbit/s below 30 MHz and up to 186 kbit/s above 30 MHz, depending on the robustness mode and spectrum occupancy. For legacy compatibility, AAC (Advanced Audio Coding) with optional Spectral Band Replication (SBR) and Parametric Stereo (PS) is also supported, targeting similar bitrate ranges with granularity of 20 bit/s in modes A-D and 80 bit/s in mode E. xHE-AAC uses the Unified Speech and Audio Coding (USAC) core, combining frequency-domain coding for music and time-domain linear prediction for speech, while AAC relies on the MPEG-4 ER AAC Scalable object type for error-robust transmission. Opus is compatible in some implementations but is not specified in the core ETSI standard.
Encoding in both codecs centers on the Modified Discrete Cosine Transform (MDCT), which converts time-domain audio samples into frequency-domain coefficients for quantization and entropy coding. Frame sizes are structured into super frames: 400 ms duration for robustness modes A-D (below 30 MHz) and 200 ms for mode E (VHF Band III), comprising multiple sub-frames of 960 samples each in AAC or variable lengths in xHE-AAC with a bit reservoir limited to 6,144 bits per channel. At low bitrates (e.g., 18-26 kbit/s), parametric stereo enhances spatial imaging by transmitting a mono core signal plus compact side-chain parameters, rather than full stereo channels, reducing overhead while maintaining perceived width. Sampling rates vary from 12 kHz to 48 kHz, with SBR in AAC extending effective bandwidth at reduced rates.
Bitrate allocation in the multiplex frame is flexible, allowing audio to consume up to the full Main Service Channel (MSC) capacitytypically 95% or morewhile reserving portions for data services, text messaging (e.g., 80 bit/s in modes A-D), and signaling. Modulation and Bandwidth

Digital Radio Mondiale (DRM) utilizes Coded Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplexing (COFDM) as its primary modulation scheme, enabling robust performance against multipath fading and Doppler shifts prevalent in HF and MF broadcasting bands. In COFDM, the data streamderived from source-encoded audio and service informationis mapped onto multiple orthogonal subcarriers, each individually modulated using Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (QAM) with constellations of 4-QAM, 16-QAM, or 64-QAM to balance robustness and spectral efficiency. The number of subcarriers depends on the robustness mode: mode A employs 288 useful subcarriers, mode B 256, mode C 176, and mode D 112, while mode E (DRM+) uses up to 665 active carriers; these allow adaptation to varying channel conditions and bandwidth constraints.
DRM supports several bandwidth modes aligned with international AM channel allocations, optimizing spectrum usage without requiring new frequency plans. Mode A operates in 18 kHz or 20 kHz channels, ideal for shortwave broadcasting with wider spacing. Mode B fits 9 kHz or 10 kHz channels, common in medium wave regions. Mode D accommodates 5 kHz channels for simulcast applications, where the digital signal can occupy the lower sideband alongside an analog AM carrier to facilitate gradual transition. Mode E operates in 100 kHz channels for VHF bands above 30 MHz (DRM+). These modes ensure the occupied bandwidth remains within 90% of the nominal channel width, minimizing adjacent channel interference.
To mitigate inter-symbol interference from multipath propagation, each OFDM symbol includes a cyclic guard interval appended to the useful symbol period. Guard interval durations vary by mode: 2.67 ms for mode A (ratio TG/TU=1/9T_G / T_U = 1/9TG/TU=1/9), 5.33 ms for modes B and C (ratios 1/4 and 4/11, respectively), and 7.33 ms for mode D (ratio 11/14), providing protection against delay spreads typical in terrestrial channels. In shortwave scenarios, where multipath delays can extend to 100 ms, these guard intervals combine with convolutional interleaving across frames to maintain signal integrity without excessive overhead.
The OFDM frame structure consists of a 400 ms duration for modes A through D, comprising 15 to 24 symbols per frame depending on the mode, with three frames forming a superframe for enhanced synchronization. Mode E uses a shorter 100 ms frame with 40 symbols, suited to VHF environments. Channel Coding and Error Protection

Digital Radio Mondiale (DRM) utilizes a concatenated coding scheme for channel coding and error protection, combining an inner convolutional code with an outer Reed-Solomon code to provide robust forward error correction against noise and interference in shortwave environments. The inner code is a convolutional encoder with a mother rate of $ \frac{1}{6} $ and constraint length 7, which is punctured to achieve effective rates based on the required protection level, allowing flexibility in trading redundancy for throughput. The outer Reed-Solomon code, RS(255,239) with $ t=8 $, adds 16 parity bytes per block to correct up to 8 erroneous bytes, specifically targeting burst errors common in multipath propagation.
Interleaving plays a critical role in mitigating the effects of time-varying fading in shortwave bands by redistributing coded bits across time and frequency domains. Time-domain interleaving operates over multiple 400 ms logical frames, with a long interleaving depth of 2.4 seconds (spanning 6 frames) for robustness modes A-D to decorrelate burst errors over extended periods, while frequency interleaving shuffles data cells across the orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing (OFDM) carriers within each frame. This dual interleaving approach ensures that consecutive errors are separated, enabling the error-correcting codes to recover data effectively even under severe channel impairments.
DRM specifies four protection levels (A through D) for the main service channel, each balancing error resilience with useful bitrate by varying the puncturing patterns and thus the effective code rates. Level A offers the highest robustness for poor channel conditions with an effective code rate of approximately 0.4, progressively increasing to about 0.5 for Level B, 0.6 for Level C, and 0.78 for Level D to maximize capacity in better environments. These levels apply to both equal error protection (EEP) and unequal error protection (UEP) modes, with UEP allocating higher protection to more sensitive audio data partitions.
The overall code rate $ R $ is computed as the product of the convolutional code's nominal rate, the puncturing factor, and the Reed-Solomon rate $ \frac{239}{255} $, yielding effective rates tailored to each protection level while targeting a post-decoding bit error rate (BER) below $ 10^{-4} $ for quasi-error-free performance. These mechanisms ensure the coded and interleaved bits are reliably mapped to the modulated OFDM carriers.

Extensions and Variants

DRM30 Mode

DRM30 Mode refers to the configuration of Digital Radio Mondiale (DRM) designed for operation in the broadcast bands below 30 MHz, encompassing long wave (LW), medium wave (MW), and short wave (SW) frequencies. These bands include LW from 30 kHz to 300 kHz, MW from 300 kHz to 3 MHz, and SW from 3 MHz to 30 MHz, enabling wide-area coverage suitable for regional and international broadcasting.
The system employs five robustness modes (A through E), each optimized for specific propagation characteristics and channel conditions in these bands. Mode A is tailored for international shortwave broadcasting with a 20 kHz bandwidth, suitable for Gaussian channels with minor fading. Mode B supports regional medium wave and general-purpose use in 4.5-20 kHz bandwidths, addressing time- and frequency-selective fading with longer delay spreads. Mode C provides enhanced robustness for local shortwave applications in 10-20 kHz bandwidths, handling higher Doppler spreads. Mode D offers high robustness for local medium wave in 10-20 kHz bandwidths, accommodating severe delay and Doppler effects. Mode E is designed for VHF bands above 30 MHz, employing a fixed 100 kHz bandwidth for local and regional use in time- and frequency-selective channels with significant multipath and Doppler effects. For MW (300 kHz-3 MHz), Mode D is applicable, while SW (3-30 MHz) primarily utilizes Modes A, B, and C. These modes leverage orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing (OFDM) to mitigate multipath interference inherent in these frequency ranges. Modes A-D are used for bands below 30 MHz.

DRM30 supports a multiplex capacity of up to three audio channels or one audio channel combined with data services, achieving a gross bit rate of 72 kbps. This configuration operates without hierarchy, utilizing a single-layer structure to simplify transmission and reception. The Fast Access Channel (FAC) facilitates service discovery by transmitting essential parameters such as robustness mode, spectrum occupancy, and interleaver depth at a fixed rate of 2.5 kbps, enabling rapid signal acquisition even in challenging environments.

DRM+ Mode

DRM+ is an extension of the Digital Radio Mondiale (DRM) standard designed specifically for operation in the VHF broadcasting bands above 30 MHz, targeting frequencies from 30 to 174 MHz in Bands I and II to enable local and regional digital radio services as a potential replacement for analog FM broadcasting. It utilizes a fixed bandwidth of 100 kHz, which allows efficient spectrum use within the typical 200 kHz FM channel raster while supporting higher capacity transmissions compared to lower-frequency DRM modes. This configuration facilitates the delivery of multiple audio channels or enhanced audio quality in urban environments with line-of-sight propagation characteristics.
Key adaptations in DRM+ include an increased number of carriers216 in totalarranged in a coded orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing (COFDM) scheme with a carrier spacing of approximately 444 Hz, enabling robust performance against multipath interference common in VHF mobile reception scenarios. The symbol duration is shortened to 2.5 ms (comprising a useful symbol period of 2.25 ms and a guard interval of 0.25 ms), which reduces susceptibility to Doppler shifts and supports faster interleaving for improved reception in vehicular environments. These modifications allow for higher data rates, reaching up to 186 kbps in the main service channel (MSC) using 16-QAM modulation and code rates up to 0.625, sufficient for stereo audio with ancillary data or even low-resolution video services.
DRM+ maintains full compatibility with the core DRM protocol, employing the same source coding options (such as AAC or xHE-AAC codecs, with xHE-AAC added in ETSI ES 201 980 v4.1.1 (2019) for enhanced low-bitrate performance), frame alignment and control (FAC), service description channel (SDC), and MSC structure as the base system, ensuring that receivers can decode signals across frequency bands with minimal hardware differences. However, it is optimized for VHF-specific challenges, including time- and frequency-selective fading, through enhanced pilot cell placement for channel estimation and support for delay diversity techniques to combat flat fading. This allows seamless integration with existing analog FM infrastructure, with recommended frequency offsets of at least 150 kHz to minimize interference during transitional simulcast operations.
In applications, DRM+ is particularly suited for local broadcasting and FM simulcast, where it can provide digital quality audio to metropolitan areas using a fraction of the power required for analog signals, as demonstrated in European field trials. For instance, a trial in Copenhagen, Denmark, that operated from 2021 to 2022 at 86.5 MHz with a 200 kHz allocation (accommodating two 100 kHz DRM+ signals at 500 W ERP) showcased reliable mobile reception across the city, highlighting its potential for efficient spectrum sharing in crowded VHF bands.

Global Deployment and Regulation

Current Adoption and Trials

In July 2025, China's National Radio and Television Administration (NRTA) adopted Digital Radio Mondiale (DRM) as the national industry standard for shortwave (SW) and medium-wave (MW) broadcasting, marking a significant step toward digitalizing its extensive analog radio infrastructure. The standard, titled "Technical Specifications for Medium and Short-Wave Digital Sound Broadcasting," includes guidelines for analog/DRM simulcast operations and aims to support upgrades at provincial transmission stations, with plans to digitize approximately 600 sites within the existing network of over 2,500 MW and 300 SW transmitters nationwide. This adoption aims to enhance domestic coverage and efficiency in large-scale broadcasting.
India has seen rapid integration of DRM, with more than 13 million car receivers equipped for digital radio by the end of 2025, driven by All India Radio's (AIR) ongoing SW and MW transmissions. In October 2025, the Telecom Regulatory Authority of India (TRAI) recommended adopting a single national standard for digital radio in the VHF Band II (FM), emphasizing a technology-neutral approach.
Ongoing trials and operations in Europe include BBC World Service's regular DRM transmissions on SW frequencies targeting global audiences, alongside Germany's shortwave DRM transmissions for international broadcasting. In Asia and Africa, AIR continues SW DRM broadcasts for wide-area coverage, while Indonesia advances its national rollout with policy frameworks from 2023 and 2024, including 5 operational DRM transmitters and 3 under installation as of June 2025, along with emergency warning functionality integrated into the system to achieve broad population reach. Globally, dozens of active DRM stations and transmissions operate, primarily on SW and MW bands, serving diverse regions from Europe to Southeast Asia.
Growth in DRM adoption is propelled by in-car receiver integration, particularly in emerging markets like India and Indonesia, where affordable modules enable seamless upgrades from analog AM systems. Its robustness for emergency broadcasting further supports deployment in areas with unreliable infrastructure, such as parts of Africa and Asia. However, challenges persist in receiver availability, though low-cost modules like the DRM1000 have reduced integration costs and power consumption by up to 80% since their 2024 launch, facilitating broader market entry.

International Regulatory Framework

The International Telecommunication Union (ITU) plays a central role in establishing global standards for Digital Radio Mondiale (DRM) through its Radiocommunication Sector (ITU-R) recommendations. Recommendation ITU-R BS.1615, first adopted in 2001 and updated through 2023, provides planning parameters for digital sound broadcasting below 30 MHz, including coverage predictions, field strengths, and protection criteria essential for DRM implementations in high-frequency (HF) bands. Complementing this, Recommendation ITU-R BS.1114, revised multiple times with the latest in 2011, outlines systems for terrestrial digital sound broadcasting, specifying technical parameters for DRM such as modulation schemes and error correction to ensure interoperability across international borders. Additionally, Article 5 of the ITU Radio Regulations allocates spectrum in HF bands (3-30 MHz) to the broadcasting service on a primary basis worldwide, permitting digital emissions like DRM within these allocations to support international shortwave broadcasting without requiring separate frequency designations.
National regulations have aligned with ITU frameworks to enable DRM deployment. In the United States, the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) amended its rules in 2005 via 47 CFR 73.758 to authorize DRM as the digital modulation standard for HF international broadcasting, specifying system parameters to ensure compatibility with existing analog operations and limiting emissions to prevent interference. In the European Union, the European Conference of Postal and Telecommunications Administrations (CEPT) has facilitated harmonization through reports like ECC Report 117 (2007), which outlines transition strategies for digital broadcasting in medium-wave (MW) and shortwave (SW) bands, including provisions for DRM trials and spectrum sharing to promote cross-border consistency.
International coordination of DRM transmissions is supported by bodies like the High Frequency Coordination Conference (HFCC), which organizes seasonal scheduling for SW broadcasting to minimize interference among global stations. The HFCC incorporates DRM alongside analog signals in its coordination processes, using tools to resolve frequency conflicts and ensure equitable access to HF bands for digital services. Recent updates in Asia, particularly China's adoption of DRM as a national industry standard by the National Radio and Television Administration (NRTA) in July 2025, reflect ongoing regulatory evolution, mandating technical specifications for domestic SW and MW digital broadcasting to enhance coverage and multimedia capabilities.
To safeguard DRM signals, international standards define protection ratios against adjacent-channel interference from analog broadcasting. ITU-R BS.1615 specifies a minimum adjacent-channel protection ratio of 40 dB for DRM reception when interfered with by analog AM signals, ensuring robust performance in mixed analog-digital environments below 30 MHz.
