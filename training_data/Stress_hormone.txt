 The concept of stress hormones originated from the pioneering work of Hungarian-Canadian endocrinologist Hans Selye in the 1930s, who identified the "general adaptation syndrome" through animal experiments, highlighting the role of adrenal hormones in the body's response to stressors.
Stress hormones refer to a class of hormones, primarily cortisol and the catecholamines adrenaline (epinephrine) and noradrenaline (norepinephrine), that are secreted by the adrenal glands in response to perceived stressors to mobilize the body's resources for immediate action. These hormones are integral to the acute stress response, often termed the "fight or flight" mechanism, which enhances survival by redirecting energy to essential functions like increased heart rate, elevated blood pressure, and heightened alertness. Cortisol, the principal glucocorticoid stress hormone, is produced in the adrenal cortex under regulation by the hypothalamus-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, where corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) from the hypothalamus stimulates adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) release from the pituitary, which in turn prompts cortisol synthesis from cholesterol. Meanwhile, adrenaline and noradrenaline are released via the sympathetic nervous system directly from the adrenal medulla, providing rapid physiological changes such as boosted blood glucose levels through gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis to fuel muscles and the brain.
In the short term, these hormones play adaptive roles by suppressing non-essential activities like digestion, reproduction, and immune function to prioritize energy allocation during threats, thereby improving physical performance and cognitive focus. For instance, cortisol increases amino acid availability for glucose production and modulates inflammation by inducing apoptosis in proinflammatory immune cells, while adrenaline accelerates oxygen delivery to tissues. However, chronic elevation of stress hormones, often due to prolonged psychological or environmental stressors, can disrupt homeostasis, leading to adverse health outcomes such as hypertension, impaired immune response, metabolic disorders like insulin resistance, and increased risk of cardiovascular disease, anxiety, and depression. Cortisol follows a circadian rhythm, peaking in the early morning to promote wakefulness and declining at night, but sustained high levels from chronic stress override this pattern, contributing to sleep disturbances and fatigue.
The balance of stress hormones is crucial for overall health, with deficiencies (e.g., in Addison's disease) causing fatigue and hypotension, and excesses (e.g., in Cushing's syndrome) resulting in weight gain, osteoporosis, and muscle weakness, underscoring their broad influence on nearly every organ system including the nervous, cardiovascular, immune, and metabolic pathways. Research highlights the importance of stress management techniques, such as exercise and mindfulness, to mitigate prolonged hormonal activation and prevent associated pathologies.

Introduction and Definition

Definition of Stress Hormones

Stress hormones are chemical messengers secreted by endocrine glands, primarily the adrenal glands, in response to physical, psychological, or environmental stressors that threaten homeostasis. These hormones, such as cortisol and epinephrine, initiate a coordinated physiological response to enable the body to adapt to or confront the stressor, often referred to as the "fight or flight" mechanism.
Their primary functions include mobilizing energy reserves by promoting glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis to elevate blood glucose levels, thereby providing rapid fuel for muscles and the brain. They also enhance cardiovascular performance by increasing heart rate, blood pressure, and cardiac output to improve oxygen delivery throughout the body. Additionally, stress hormones suppress non-essential activities, such as digestion, reproduction, and immune responses, to redirect resources toward immediate survival needs.
Unlike basal hormones, which are released steadily to maintain routine homeostasis, stress hormones are secreted episodicallyeither acutely in bursts during immediate threats or chronically in sustained elevations under prolonged stressallowing the body to return to baseline functioning once the stressor resolves. Evolutionarily, this system developed to enhance survival against short-term dangers, such as predators, by enabling quick adaptive behaviors; however, persistent activation in modern contexts can lead to maladaptive outcomes, including metabolic disruptions and immune suppression.

Historical Development

The scientific understanding of stress hormones originated with the work of Hungarian-Canadian endocrinologist Hans Selye, who in 1936 observed a consistent syndrome in rats exposed to diverse noxious agents, such as toxins and physical trauma, characterized by enlarged adrenal cortices, thymic atrophy, and gastric ulcers. This led Selye to propose the General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS), a triphasic model encompassing the alarm reaction (initial mobilization), resistance (adaptation), and exhaustion (breakdown) stages, laying the groundwork for recognizing nonspecific physiological responses mediated by hormones from the adrenal glands.
Selye expanded on these findings in his influential 1956 book The Stress of Life, which popularized the modern concept of "stress" as a biological response and explicitly linked it to hormonal mechanisms, particularly those involving the pituitary and adrenal systems, influencing subsequent endocrine research.
Advancements in the mid-20th century focused on adrenal gland secretions, with the isolation of cortisol (also known as hydrocortisone) in the 1930s by Edward C. Kendall and its structural elucidation in the 1940s, culminating in clinical demonstrations of its anti-inflammatory effects in 1949 and the 1950 Nobel Prize shared by Kendall, Philip S. Hench, and Tadeus Reichstein for hormones of the adrenal cortex, establishing cortisol's central role in stress-mediated responses.
In the 1970s and 1980s, the integration of psychological stressors with endocrine pathways advanced through research on the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, notably by George P. Chrousos, whose studies elucidated how corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) orchestrates stress responses, bridging behavioral and physiological dimensions in conditions like depression and anxiety.
From the 1990s onward, the focus shifted to chronic stress's pathological implications, propelled by Bruce S. McEwen's 1998 formulation of allostatic load theory, which described the cumulative "wear and tear" on the body from repeated activation of stress mediators like glucocorticoids, linking prolonged hormonal dysregulation to diseases such as cardiovascular disorders and neurodegeneration.

Types of Stress Hormones

Glucocorticoids

Glucocorticoids are a class of steroid hormones primarily produced in the zona fasciculata of the adrenal cortex, with cortisol (also known as hydrocortisone) serving as the predominant glucocorticoid in humans. These hormones play a central role in the body's response to stress by modulating metabolism, immune function, and energy mobilization. Cortisol is released in response to signals from the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis.
Chemically, glucocorticoids are derived from cholesterol through a series of enzymatic conversions, resulting in lipid-soluble molecules that can readily cross cell membranes. Cortisol, specifically, has the molecular formula CHO and features a pregnane skeleton with hydroxyl groups at positions 11, 17, and 21, as well as ketone groups at positions 3 and 20. This structure enables cortisol to bind with high affinity to intracellular glucocorticoid receptors, forming a complex that translocates to the nucleus to regulate gene transcription.
The synthesis of cortisol begins with the transport of cholesterol into the mitochondria of adrenocortical cells, where it is cleaved by the enzyme cholesterol side-chain cleavage (CYP11A1) to form pregnenolone, the precursor for all steroid hormones. Pregnenolone is then converted through a series of hydroxylations and oxidations in the zona fasciculata: it undergoes 17-hydroxylation by CYP17A1 to 17-hydroxypregnenolone, isomerization to 17-hydroxyprogesterone, 21-hydroxylation by CYP21A2 to 11-deoxycortisol, and finally 11-hydroxylation by CYP11B1 to yield cortisol. This multi-step process is tightly regulated by adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) from the pituitary gland, which stimulates the expression of these enzymes.
Under normal conditions, glucocorticoid secretion follows a circadian rhythm orchestrated by the suprachiasmatic nucleus, with cortisol levels peaking shortly after awakening (around 30-45 minutes) and declining throughout the day to reach nadir in the evening. This rhythm helps synchronize metabolic and behavioral processes with the sleep-wake cycle. During stress, however, acute surges in ACTH drive rapid elevations in cortisol that can override this baseline rhythm, ensuring prioritized resource allocation for immediate survival needs.
A key regulatory feature of glucocorticoids is their negative feedback mechanism on the HPA axis, where elevated cortisol levels inhibit the release of corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) from the hypothalamus and ACTH from the anterior pituitary, thereby preventing overproduction and maintaining homeostasis. This feedback occurs primarily through glucocorticoid receptor-mediated suppression of gene expression in these brain regions.

Catecholamines

Catecholamines represent a class of biogenic amines that function as primary rapid-response stress hormones, with epinephrine (adrenaline) and norepinephrine (noradrenaline) being the most prominent members involved in immediate physiological arousal. These hormones are synthesized from the amino acid tyrosine primarily in the chromaffin cells of the adrenal medulla for epinephrine and in postganglionic sympathetic nerve endings for norepinephrine, enabling swift activation during stress.
The molecular structures of these catecholaminesepinephrine (CHNO) and norepinephrine (CHNO)feature a catechol ring with an amine side chain, allowing them to bind selectively to adrenergic receptors divided into alpha (, ) and beta (, , ) subtypes, which mediate diverse effects such as vasoconstriction and increased heart rate.
Biosynthesis follows a sequential enzymatic pathway: tyrosine is first converted to L-3,4-dihydroxyphenylalanine (L-DOPA) by tyrosine hydroxylase, then decarboxylated to dopamine by aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase, hydroxylated to norepinephrine by dopamine -hydroxylase, and finally methylated to epinephrine by phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase (PNMT), an enzyme predominantly expressed in the adrenal medulla.
Once produced, catecholamines are packaged and stored in chromaffin granules within adrenal chromaffin cells or synaptic vesicles in sympathetic neurons, from which they are released in discrete bursts triggered by acute stress to orchestrate the fight-or-flight response via the sympathoadrenal medullary (SAM) system.
Their plasma half-life is exceptionally brief, typically 1-2 minutes for both epinephrine and norepinephrine, due to rapid uptake, metabolism by enzymes like monoamine oxidase and catechol-O-methyltransferase, and renal excretion, which ensures precise temporal control over stress-mediated arousal.

Other Stress-Related Hormones

In addition to the primary stress hormones such as glucocorticoids and catecholamines, several other hormones play supportive roles in modulating the stress response, often by enhancing physiological adaptations or behavioral adjustments. These modulators include vasopressin, growth hormone, corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH), prolactin, and oxytocin, which interact with core stress pathways to fine-tune responses like fluid balance, energy allocation, and emotional regulation. Unlike primary effectors that drive the immediate fight-or-flight reaction or prolonged metabolic shifts, these hormones typically amplify or sustain the actions of glucocorticoids and catecholamines, contributing to overall homeostasis during stress exposure.
Vasopressin, also known as antidiuretic hormone (ADH), is a neuropeptide released from the posterior pituitary gland in response to stress, where it promotes water retention in the kidneys to maintain blood volume and pressure under conditions like dehydration or hemorrhage. During acute stress, vasopressin synergizes with CRH to stimulate adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) secretion from the anterior pituitary, thereby potentiating the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis activation and glucocorticoid release. This dual role in osmoregulation and stress amplification underscores vasopressin's function as a key modulator, particularly in scenarios involving cardiovascular or osmotic challenges.
Growth hormone (GH), secreted by the anterior pituitary, exhibits elevated levels during both physical and psychological stress, facilitating protein synthesis, lipolysis, and glucose mobilization to support tissue repair and energy demands in the face of stressors such as exercise or trauma. In stress contexts, GH release is mediated through mechanisms involving the HPA axis and sympathetic activation, helping to counteract catabolic effects by promoting anabolic processes like muscle maintenance. This response is particularly pronounced in acute stress, where GH aids in rapid metabolic adaptation without directly initiating the core stress cascade.
Corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH), a peptide primarily produced in the hypothalamus, serves as the initiator of the HPA axis by prompting ACTH release, but it also exerts direct extrahypothalamic effects on behavior, including the induction of anxiety-like states and arousal in brain regions such as the amygdala and locus coeruleus. Under stress, central CRH administration mimics anxiogenic responses, enhancing vigilance and fear conditioning independent of peripheral hormone changes. These behavioral influences position CRH as a neuromodulator that bridges endocrine and neural stress components, often amplifying emotional reactivity.
Prolactin, released from the anterior pituitary, shows increased secretion during chronic stress, where it contributes to immune modulation and behavioral adaptations, such as reduced aggression or enhanced parental care, potentially buffering prolonged stressor effects. In contrast, oxytocin, synthesized in the hypothalamus and released from both the posterior pituitary and directly within the brain, plays a minor but protective role in stress attenuation, particularly through social buffering mechanisms that lower cortisol levels during interpersonal interactions. These hormones' roles are more evident in sustained or social stress paradigms, where they help restore balance by counteracting excessive primary stress signaling.
Collectively, these hormones integrate with glucocorticoids to influence immune responses, such as suppressing inflammation during acute stress while promoting recovery in chronic states. Their modulatory nature distinguishes them from primary stress hormones, as they primarily enhance adaptive resilience rather than drive the initial physiological surge.

Physiological Mechanisms

Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) Axis

The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis represents the primary neuroendocrine pathway regulating the body's response to stress through glucocorticoid production. In this cascade, neurons in the paraventricular nucleus (PVN) of the hypothalamus release corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH), which travels via the hypophyseal portal system to stimulate the anterior pituitary gland. The pituitary then secretes adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) into the systemic circulation, prompting the zona fasciculata of the adrenal cortex to synthesize and release cortisol, the principal glucocorticoid in humans. This sequential activation ensures a coordinated endocrine response to maintain homeostasis under stress.
Activation of the HPA axis occurs in response to diverse stressors, integrating neural and humoral signals. Psychological stressors, such as perceived threats or emotional distress, are processed through the amygdala and broader limbic system, which project excitatory inputs to the PVN to initiate CRH release. Physical stressors, including inflammation or hemodynamic changes, engage the axis via circulating cytokines like interleukin-6 (IL-6) and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-) from immune cells, or through baroreceptor-mediated signals in response to alterations in blood pressure. These inputs converge on the hypothalamus, amplifying CRH secretion to mount an adaptive physiological response.
The HPA axis is tightly regulated by a negative feedback loop to prevent overactivation. Circulating cortisol binds to glucocorticoid receptors (GR) and mineralocorticoid receptors (MR) in the hypothalamus and anterior pituitary, suppressing further CRH and ACTH production and thereby terminating the stress response. This ultradian and circadian rhythmicity is orchestrated by the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus, the master circadian clock, which synchronizes cortisol secretion to exhibit a diurnal pattern: levels nadir during sleep and peak approximately 30-45 minutes after awakening, promoting alertness and energy mobilization.
Dysregulation of the HPA axis, particularly hyperactivity, is implicated in various psychopathologies, including major depressive disorder (MDD). In MDD, impaired negative feedback sensitivity leads to elevated baseline cortisol levels, affecting 40-60% of patients and contributing to sustained hypercortisolemia that exacerbates mood symptoms and cognitive deficits.

Sympathoadrenal Medullary (SAM) System

The sympathoadrenal medullary (SAM) system represents the rapid neural-endocrine arm of the acute stress response, integrating the sympathetic nervous system with the adrenal medulla to mobilize catecholamines for immediate physiological adaptation. Upon activation, preganglionic sympathetic fibers innervate the adrenal medulla, stimulating chromaffin cells to secrete epinephrine and norepinephrine directly into the bloodstream, thereby enabling a swift "fight-or-flight" reaction that enhances survival in threatening situations.
The neural pathway of the SAM system originates in the hypothalamus, where stress signals propagate downward through the brainstem and spinal cord to reach preganglionic sympathetic neurons in the intermediolateral cell column of the thoracic and lumbar segments (T1-L2). These neurons extend axons via the splanchnic nerves to synapse directly with chromaffin cells in the adrenal medulla, bypassing traditional ganglionic relays due to the endocrine nature of the response. This direct innervation allows for millisecond-scale activation, contrasting with slower hormonal cascades.
Activation of the SAM system is triggered by immediate threats, such as physical danger or sudden environmental changes, detected through sensory afferents that relay information to the brainstem and hypothalamus. For instance, inputs from baroreceptors, chemoreceptors, or nociceptors converge on hypothalamic nuclei like the paraventricular nucleus, initiating sympathetic outflow within seconds.
The response is amplified through the differential actions of norepinephrine and epinephrine: norepinephrine released from sympathetic nerve endings exerts localized effects at target tissues, such as vasoconstriction in specific vascular beds, while epinephrine from the adrenal medulla provides systemic dissemination, broadly elevating heart rate, blood glucose, and energy availability across the body. This dual mechanism ensures coordinated, widespread mobilization without relying on prolonged diffusion.
Short-term feedback in the SAM system primarily occurs via receptor desensitization and autoregulatory loops, preventing overstimulation during acute episodes. Presynaptic 2-adrenergic receptors on noradrenergic neurons inhibit further catecholamine release through negative autoregulation, while postsynaptic -adrenergic receptor desensitization in target cells rapidly attenuates signaling, maintaining homeostasis without invoking extended hormonal circuits.

Effects of Stress Hormones

Acute Stress Response

The acute stress response represents the body's immediate physiological adaptation to perceived threats, enabling rapid mobilization of resources for survival. This response, often termed the fight-or-flight reaction, is triggered by the release of catecholamines such as epinephrine and norepinephrine from the adrenal medulla, which act swiftly to prepare the organism for action. The sympathoadrenal medullary (SAM) system plays a key role in initiating this cascade, as detailed in the physiological mechanisms of stress.
Central to the fight-or-flight activation is the enhancement of cardiovascular and respiratory functions to optimize oxygen delivery to vital tissues. Catecholamines bind to - and -adrenergic receptors, resulting in increased heart rate, elevated blood pressure, and accelerated respiration, which collectively ensure heightened alertness and physical readiness. Concurrently, energy mobilization occurs through hormonal actions that prioritize fuel availability: epinephrine induces glycogenolysis in the liver and muscles to release glucose into the bloodstream. These processes provide the metabolic surge necessary for immediate exertion.
Sensory and circulatory adjustments further amplify the response's adaptive utility. Pupil dilation, mediated by sympathetic innervation, improves visual acuity for threat detection, and blood flow is redirected from non-essential areas like the digestive system to skeletal muscles and the brain, enhancing motor performance and cognitive processing. Additionally, a temporary boost to innate immunity occurs, with catecholamines facilitating the rapid mobilization of neutrophils into the bloodstream to bolster defenses against potential injury or infection during the stress event. This acute phase typically endures for minutes to hours, subsiding as the stressor is resolved and hormone levels normalize.

Chronic Stress Response

Chronic stress response arises from the prolonged activation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis and sympathoadrenal medullary (SAM) system, leading to sustained elevation of glucocorticoids and catecholamines. This persistent hormonal state imposes an allostatic load, defined as the cumulative wear and tear on the body from repeated or chronic stress responses, which over time exhausts physiological systems and predisposes to dysregulation across multiple organs.
In metabolic adaptations, chronic elevation of cortisol promotes insulin resistance by interfering with insulin signaling pathways in liver, muscle, and adipose tissues, thereby impairing glucose uptake and utilization. This hormonal excess also drives the accumulation of visceral fat through enhanced lipogenesis and reduced lipolysis in abdominal depots, contributing to central obesity and further metabolic imbalance.
Neurologically, excess glucocorticoids induce structural changes in the brain, particularly hippocampal atrophy due to neurotoxic effects on pyramidal neurons and suppression of neurogenesis in the dentate gyrus. This atrophy correlates with impairments in declarative memory and spatial learning, as the hippocampus's glucocorticoid receptors make it particularly vulnerable to prolonged exposure.
Cardiovascular strain from chronic catecholamine release, such as norepinephrine and epinephrine, manifests as persistent hypertension through sustained vasoconstriction and increased cardiac output. These hormones also damage endothelial cells by promoting oxidative stress and inflammation, leading to reduced nitric oxide bioavailability and impaired vascular relaxation.
On the immune front, prolonged stress shifts the response from initial enhancement to suppression, characterized by reduced T-cell proliferation and numbers, particularly cytotoxic and helper T-cells, due to glucocorticoid-mediated apoptosis and cytokine dysregulation. This culminates in a pro-inflammatory state with elevated chronic markers like C-reactive protein, as the adaptive immune system becomes compromised while innate inflammation persists.

Health Implications

Beneficial Effects

Stress hormones, particularly cortisol and epinephrine, play crucial adaptive roles in enhancing survival during acute threats by mobilizing physiological resources for immediate action. Epinephrine, released via the sympathoadrenal system, rapidly increases heart rate, blood pressure, and blood flow to muscles, enabling faster reaction times and heightened physical performance in emergencies such as evading predators or responding to injury. Cortisol complements this by suppressing non-essential functions like digestion and reproduction, contributing to overall resource mobilization, while the acute stress response promotes analgesia through endogenous mechanisms, reducing pain perception to allow continued focus and mobility despite injuries. These mechanisms, integral to the acute stress response, ensure rapid adaptation to life-threatening situations without long-term detriment when stress is brief.
In terms of immune modulation, short-term elevations in stress hormones bolster defenses against infections by enhancing innate and adaptive immune responses. Acute cortisol and epinephrine surges promote the redistribution of immune cells, such as lymphocytes and natural killer cells, to potential sites of infection, increasing cytokine production like IL-6 to combat pathogens. For instance, during viral threats like influenza, this temporary boost strengthens antiviral activity and accelerates recovery, as evidenced by improved vaccine efficacy and reduced infection severity in moderately stressed individuals. Such effects highlight the protective role of stress hormones in facilitating survival through heightened immunological vigilance.
Stress hormones also optimize metabolic efficiency to support energy demands during exertion, preventing energy depletion in critical moments. Cortisol facilitates gluconeogenesis in the liver, converting proteins and fats into glucose to maintain blood sugar levels and avert hypoglycemia, ensuring sustained fuel for brain and muscle function. Epinephrine similarly mobilizes glycogen stores and inhibits insulin release, redirecting resources toward high-priority activities like physical escape or defense. This coordinated metabolic shift enhances endurance and prevents fatigue, underscoring the hormones' role in promoting adaptive performance under duress.
Cognitively, norepinephrine, a key catecholamine stress hormone, sharpens focus and vigilance in high-stakes environments by modulating attention and arousal networks in the brain. During acute stress, norepinephrine release from the locus coeruleus enhances signal-to-noise ratios in neural processing, improving detection of relevant stimuli and decision-making speed. This leads to better performance in tasks requiring sustained attention, such as navigating complex threats, thereby aiding survival through precise, timely responses.
From an evolutionary perspective, moderated stress hormone responses confer advantages by supporting reproduction and social bonding, essential for species propagation. Acute stress interactions with oxytocin, another stress-related hormone, foster pair bonding and parental care, increasing offspring survival rates in challenging environments. This interplay promotes cooperative behaviors and resilience, optimizing fitness by balancing threat response with social cohesion in ancestral settings.

Adverse Effects and Diseases

Chronic elevation of stress hormones such as cortisol and norepinephrine, resulting from prolonged activation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis and sympathoadrenal medullary (SAM) system, is implicated in the pathogenesis of various diseases by promoting inflammation, endothelial dysfunction, and metabolic dysregulation.
In cardiovascular disease, elevated cortisol and norepinephrine levels contribute to atherosclerosis by enhancing vascular inflammation, plaque formation, and instability, thereby increasing the risk of heart attacks and strokes. Chronic psychological stress has been associated with a greater than twofold increased risk for incident myocardial infarction and other cardiovascular events, comparable to traditional risk factors like hypertension.
Regarding metabolic disorders, sustained high cortisol concentrations, akin to those observed in Cushing's syndrome, induce insulin resistance, visceral fat accumulation, and hyperglycemia, elevating the risk of type 2 diabetes and obesity. In Cushing's syndrome, glucose metabolism is impaired in 43% to 84% of cases, underscoring cortisol's role in promoting metabolic syndrome through enhanced lipogenesis and gluconeogenesis.
Chronic stress hormone exposure adversely affects mental health by causing structural changes in the brain, particularly hippocampal atrophy, which impairs memory, emotional regulation, and stress resilience, contributing to anxiety, depression, and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). Hippocampal volume reductions of approximately 10% to 15% are commonly observed in individuals with depression linked to prolonged cortisol elevation, facilitating the persistence of mood disorders. In PTSD, dysregulated cortisol and norepinephrine responses exacerbate fear conditioning and cognitive deficits via altered HPA axis feedback. Additionally, chronic elevation of cortisol in midlife has been associated with increased amyloid-beta deposition in the brain, elevating the risk of Alzheimer's disease, particularly in post-menopausal women (as of 2025).
On the immune front, prolonged cortisol surges suppress innate and adaptive immunity, heightening susceptibility to infections while paradoxically promoting chronic low-grade inflammation that can trigger autoimmune conditions such as rheumatoid arthritis. This immunosuppression increases infection risk by dampening cytokine balance and lymphocyte function, whereas the inflammatory milieu correlates with elevated disease activity in rheumatoid arthritis patients under chronic stress.
Finally, stress hormones like glucocorticoids may foster cancer progression by stimulating angiogenesis and tumor cell proliferation, particularly in solid tumors. Chronic stress-induced glucocorticoids enhance vascular endothelial growth factor expression, supporting tumor neovascularization and metastasis in models of ovarian and breast cancer.

Clinical Aspects

Measurement of Stress Hormones

Stress hormones, particularly cortisol, are commonly measured to evaluate hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis function in clinical and research settings, with methods selected based on the need for invasiveness, timing, and assessment of free versus total hormone levels.
Blood tests provide direct measurement of plasma cortisol and catecholamines like epinephrine, offering precise snapshots of hormone levels. Plasma cortisol is typically assessed via venipuncture, with normal morning levels (6-8 a.m.) ranging from 5 to 25 g/dL, reflecting the diurnal peak. Baseline plasma epinephrine levels in resting individuals are generally 20-50 pg/mL, though values can vary with posture and require careful standardization during collection to avoid artifacts from stress during sampling.
Salivary cortisol testing serves as a non-invasive alternative, capturing unbound (free) cortisol that diffuses into saliva and correlates well with plasma free cortisol. It is particularly useful for diurnal profiling, involving multiple samples (e.g., upon waking, midday, evening, and bedtime) to map the characteristic decline from morning peaks to nocturnal nadirs, facilitating assessment of circadian rhythm disruptions without the need for phlebotomy.
Urine collection over 24 hours quantifies total cortisol excretion and metabolites such as cortisone, providing an integrated measure of daily production that accounts for episodic secretion. Normal ranges for 24-hour urinary free cortisol are typically less than 45-60 g/24 hours in adults, varying slightly by sex and laboratory assay, and this method is valuable for detecting chronic overproduction as in Cushing's syndrome.
Dynamic tests evaluate HPA axis integrity beyond baseline levels. The ACTH stimulation test involves administering synthetic ACTH (cosyntropin) intravenously and measuring cortisol response at 30 and 60 minutes; a peak above 18-20 g/dL confirms normal adrenal function, while subnormal rises indicate insufficiency. Conversely, the dexamethasone suppression test assesses negative feedback by giving low-dose dexamethasone (1 mg) overnight and checking morning cortisol; suppression below 1.8 g/dL signifies intact HPA regulation, with failure to suppress suggesting hypercortisolemia.
Measurement challenges arise from the pulsatile nature of stress hormone release, where cortisol exhibits ultradian rhythms (pulses every 60-90 minutes) superimposed on diurnal variation, leading to high intra-individual variability. Single-point sampling may miss peaks or troughs, so protocols often incorporate multiple time points or integrated collections to improve reliability and reduce false negatives in diagnosing dysregulation.

Disorders and Treatments

Disorders associated with stress hormone dysregulation encompass conditions of hypersecretion and hyposecretion, as well as stress-related pathologies involving the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis. Cushing's syndrome represents a primary hypersecretion disorder characterized by excessive cortisol production, often due to tumors such as pituitary adenomas (Cushing's disease), ectopic ACTH-secreting tumors in organs like the lungs or pancreas, or adrenal adenomas and carcinomas. These tumors stimulate prolonged cortisol elevation, leading to symptoms including weight gain, hypertension, and muscle weakness. Treatment prioritizes surgical removal of the causative tumor, such as transsphenoidal surgery for pituitary adenomas or adrenalectomy for adrenal tumors, which normalizes cortisol levels in many cases. When surgery is not feasible or as adjunct therapy, medications like ketoconazole inhibit cortisol synthesis at the adrenal level, achieving hypercortisolism control in approximately 66% of patients with Cushing's disease.
In contrast, hyposecretion disorders like Addison's disease, or primary adrenal insufficiency, arise from adrenal gland damage due to autoimmune processes, infections, or hemorrhage, resulting in inadequate cortisol and aldosterone production. This leads to symptoms such as fatigue, low blood pressure, and salt craving, with life-threatening adrenal crises possible during stress. Management relies on lifelong hormone replacement, primarily oral hydrocortisone administered 2-3 times daily to mimic physiological cortisol rhythms, with doses adjusted upward (e.g., 50-100 mg/day) during illness or surgery to prevent crisis. Fludrocortisone is often added for mineralocorticoid replacement to maintain electrolyte balance.
Pheochromocytoma is a rare tumor of the adrenal medulla that causes excessive production of catecholamines, leading to episodic or sustained hypertension, headaches, sweating, and palpitations. It can be benign or malignant and is often associated with genetic syndromes like multiple endocrine neoplasia type 2. Diagnosis involves measuring plasma or urinary metanephrines and catecholamines, with imaging for localization. Treatment typically involves surgical resection after preoperative alpha-blockade to control blood pressure, with beta-blockers added if needed for tachycardia.
Stress-related conditions, such as post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), frequently involve HPA axis dysregulation, including elevated corticotropin-releasing hormone and paradoxically low cortisol levels, which contribute to heightened fear responses and symptom persistence. Treatments target both neurochemical and behavioral aspects; selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), such as sertraline or paroxetine, are first-line pharmacotherapies that alleviate core symptoms like hyperarousal and re-experiencing by modulating serotonin and indirectly influencing HPA feedback. Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), particularly trauma-focused variants, effectively restructures maladaptive responses and has shown efficacy in reducing PTSD severity, often in combination with SSRIs for enhanced outcomes. Adjunctive hydrocortisone has demonstrated promise in preventing PTSD onset post-trauma by normalizing acute cortisol responses.
Lifestyle interventions offer non-pharmacological strategies to mitigate chronic stress hormone elevation, particularly cortisol. Regular exercise, such as aerobic activities, attenuates HPA axis hyperactivity, while mindfulness practices like yoga promote cortisol reduction through enhanced parasympathetic tone. A 12-week yoga program (three sessions weekly) yielded a 19% decrease in cortisol levels among stressed individuals, with sustained benefits at six months. Similarly, brief mindfulness body scans post-exercise reduced cortisol by 22%, illustrating dose-dependent effects in the 20-30% range for consistent practice.
For conditions involving catecholamine excess, such as in acute anxiety episodes, beta-blockers like propranolol provide targeted relief by antagonizing beta-adrenergic receptors, thereby countering sympathetic overactivation without sedative effects. Propranolol effectively diminishes physical symptoms of anxiety, including tachycardia and tremors, by competing with catecholamines like epinephrine at receptor sites, and is particularly useful for performance anxiety. Long-term use requires monitoring for cardiovascular effects, but it serves as a valuable adjunct in catecholamine-driven anxiety states.
