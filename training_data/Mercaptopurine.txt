Mercaptopurine
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Mercaptopurine, also known as 6-mercaptopurine (6-MP), is a thiopurine antimetabolite chemotherapy agent primarily used in the maintenance treatment of acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) in both children and adults, often in combination with other antineoplastic drugs. It belongs to the class of purine analogues and functions by disrupting nucleic acid synthesis in rapidly dividing cells, thereby inhibiting cancer cell proliferation while also affecting normal cells, which can lead to significant side effects such as myelosuppression and hepatotoxicity.
Developed in the late 1940s and early 1950s by biochemist Gertrude B. Elion and pharmacologist George H. Hitchings at Burroughs Wellcome as part of rational drug design efforts targeting purine metabolism in leukemia, mercaptopurine represented a breakthrough in antimetabolite therapy. Their work on this compound, along with related drugs, contributed to the discoveries that earned Elion, Hitchings, and James Black the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1988. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved mercaptopurine for medical use in 1953 under the brand name Purinethol, marking it as one of the first effective chemotherapeutic agents for childhood leukemia and significantly improving remission rates.
In clinical practice, mercaptopurine is administered orally at doses typically ranging from 1.5 to 2.5 mg/kg body weight daily, with bioavailability varying widely (5-37%) due to first-pass metabolism in the liver. Its efficacy is influenced by genetic variations in enzymes such as thiopurine S-methyltransferase (TPMT) and NUDT15, necessitating pharmacogenetic testing to guide dosing and reduce the risk of severe toxicity; patients with low or absent enzyme activity require substantial dose reductions. Beyond oncology, mercaptopurine is used off-label as an immunosuppressant for inflammatory bowel diseases like Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis, as well as other autoimmune conditions including rheumatoid arthritis and autoimmune hepatitis. Available in tablet form (Purinethol) and oral suspension (Purixan, approved in 2014), it requires careful monitoring of complete blood counts and liver function, especially during the initial weeks of therapy.

Medical Uses

Indications

Mercaptopurine is approved by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) in both pediatric and adult patients as part of a combination chemotherapy regimen, particularly for maintenance therapy following remission induction to prevent relapse. It is typically administered in conjunction with other agents, such as methotrexate, to enhance efficacy in sustaining long-term remission in ALL patients who have achieved complete response to initial therapy. This role leverages its antimetabolite properties to inhibit purine synthesis in rapidly dividing leukemic cells.
In addition to its oncology applications, mercaptopurine is commonly used off-label for the management of inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), including Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis, where it serves as a steroid-sparing immunosuppressive agent to maintain remission and reduce inflammation in the gastrointestinal tract. Clinical guidelines from the American College of Gastroenterology (ACG) recommend mercaptopurine for moderate-to-severe Crohn's disease in adults when response to corticosteroids or other therapies is inadequate, emphasizing its utility in achieving and sustaining steroid-free clinical remission. Similarly, for ulcerative colitis, it is employed to control chronic active disease and prevent flares, particularly in patients intolerant to or dependent on higher-potency immunomodulators.
Mercaptopurine's inclusion on the World Health Organization (WHO) Model List of Essential Medicines underscores its critical role in treating acute lymphoblastic leukemia, acute promyelocytic leukemia, and Langerhans cell histiocytosis, highlighting its global importance in oncology care. For IBD, while not explicitly listed on the WHO essential medicines roster, its immunosuppressive effects are supported by evidence from major guidelines, such as those from the European Crohn's and Colitis Organisation (ECCO), which endorse thiopurines like mercaptopurine for maintenance therapy in responsive patients.

Dosage and Administration

Mercaptopurine is administered orally in the form of 50 mg tablets or an oral suspension (20 mg/mL). The medication is typically taken once daily as part of a combination regimen. For the oral suspension, the bottle must be shaken vigorously for at least 30 seconds prior to use, and an oral syringe should be employed for accurate measurement.
In the maintenance treatment of acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL), the recommended starting dosage is 1.5 to 2.5 mg/kg (or 50 to 75 mg/m body surface area) once daily. For inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), such as Crohn's disease, the typical dosage is 0.75 to 1.5 mg/kg once daily, with adjustments made based on clinical response and tolerance. Doses should be individualized and rounded to the nearest practical unit, often 25 mg increments for tablets.
Mercaptopurine should be taken consistently either with food or on an empty stomach to maintain steady absorption, and at the same time each day for optimal adherence. If a dose is missed, the next scheduled dose should be taken without doubling up.
In patients with hepatic impairment, the lowest recommended starting dosage should be used, with subsequent adjustments based on clinical monitoring. Dosing in pediatric patients for ALL is established using weight- or body surface area-based calculations similar to adults, though tablets may not be suitable for very young children weighing less than 17 kg due to the 50 mg strength. In adults, the same weight- or surface area-based regimens apply, tailored to the specific indication.

Pharmacology

Structure and Properties

Mercaptopurine, also known as 6-mercaptopurine, is a synthetic thiopurine analog of the purine base hypoxanthine, with the chemical formula CHNS and a molecular weight of 152.18 g/mol.
The molecule consists of a fused imidazole and pyrimidine ring system characteristic of purines, with a sulfur atom substituting the oxygen at the 6-position, predominantly in the thione tautomeric form (3,7-dihydropurine-6-thione); this structural modification allows it to mimic natural purines and exert antimetabolite effects.
As a physical entity, mercaptopurine exists as a yellow, odorless crystalline powder that is practically insoluble in water (less than 1 mg/mL at 25C) and ether, slightly soluble in ethanol, and more soluble in dilute alkaline solutions or hot alcohol.
Mercaptopurine is chemically stable under recommended conditions but sensitive to light, air, and moisture, which can lead to oxidation; it should be stored in a tightly closed container at controlled room temperature (2025C, with excursions permitted to 1530C) in a dry place, protected from light to prevent degradation. Key degradation products include hypoxanthine via desulfuration and oxidized species such as 6-thiouric acid under oxidative conditions.

Mechanism of Action

Mercaptopurine, an antimetabolite prodrug, is metabolized intracellularly to its active form, 6-thioguanine nucleotides (6-TGN), primarily through the action of hypoxanthine-guanine phosphoribosyltransferase (HGPRT), which converts it to thioinosinic acid (T-IMP) and subsequent intermediates. This activation pathway is essential for its cytotoxic and immunosuppressive effects, as 6-TGN accumulates in cells and mediates the drug's therapeutic actions in conditions such as acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) and inflammatory bowel disease (IBD).
The primary mechanisms of mercaptopurine involve disruption of purine metabolism and nucleic acid synthesis. T-IMP and its methylated derivative, methyl-thioinosine monophosphate (MeTIMP), inhibit de novo purine synthesis by blocking the enzyme phosphoribosyl pyrophosphate amidotransferase (also known as glutamine-5'-phosphoribosyl-1-pyrophosphate amidotransferase), the first committed step in the pathway, leading to depletion of purine nucleotides required for DNA and RNA production. Additionally, 6-TGN is incorporated into DNA as deoxythioguanosine triphosphate (dTGTP) and into RNA as thioguanosine triphosphate (TGTP), causing chain termination during replication and transcription, as well as mismatched base pairing that triggers DNA damage and apoptosis.
In its immunosuppressive role, particularly for IBD maintenance therapy, mercaptopurine interferes with T-cell proliferation by converting 6-TGN into 6-thio-GTP, which competitively binds to Rac1a GTPase in the Vav-Rac1 signaling pathwaypreventing its activation in CD4+ T cells and promoting apoptosis through inhibition of NF-B signaling. This selective targeting of activated lymphocytes contributes to reduced inflammation without broadly suppressing all immune functions.
Mercaptopurine exhibits cell cycle specificity, acting primarily in the S-phase by halting DNA synthesis in rapidly dividing cells, such as leukemic blasts in ALL, thereby exerting preferential cytotoxicity against proliferating malignant or immune cells.

Pharmacokinetics

Mercaptopurine is administered orally and exhibits variable absorption, with bioavailability ranging from 5% to 37% primarily due to extensive first-pass metabolism in the liver and gastrointestinal tract. Peak plasma concentrations are typically reached within 2 hours after administration under fasting conditions, though this time may extend to 2.3 hours when taken with food.
The drug is widely distributed throughout the body, with a volume of distribution exceeding total body water, indicating good tissue penetration. Plasma protein binding is low at approximately 19%, and mercaptopurine crosses the blood-brain barrier only minimally, resulting in negligible concentrations in cerebrospinal fluid.
Metabolism occurs predominantly in the liver through two main pathways: thiopurine methyltransferase (TPMT) converts mercaptopurine to the inactive metabolite 6-methylmercaptopurine, while xanthine oxidase (XO) oxidizes it to 6-thiouric acid; additionally, it is further metabolized to the active thioguanine nucleotides (6-TGN) via hypoxanthine-guanine phosphoribosyltransferase. Excretion is primarily renal, with about 46% of the dose recovered in urine as unchanged drug and metabolites within 24 hours, including approximately 7-12% as unchanged mercaptopurine. The elimination half-life of the parent drug is short, ranging from 0.5 to 2 hours, whereas the active metabolite 6-TGN has a much longer half-life of up to 200 hours in erythrocytes.
Several factors influence mercaptopurine's pharmacokinetics, including food intake, which reduces bioavailability and delays absorption, and age-related variations, such as potentially altered metabolism in pediatric versus adult populations. Genetic polymorphisms in enzymes like TPMT can also affect metabolism rates.

Pharmacogenetics

Mercaptopurine's therapeutic response and toxicity profile are significantly influenced by genetic polymorphisms in the thiopurine S-methyltransferase (TPMT) gene, which encodes the enzyme responsible for inactivating mercaptopurine metabolites. Approximately 10% of individuals of Caucasian descent carry one low-activity TPMT allele, such as TPMT*3A, classifying them as intermediate metabolizers, while homozygous poor metabolizers occur in about 0.3% of this population; in contrast, these low-activity variants are present in only about 1% of individuals of Asian descent. These polymorphisms lead to reduced enzyme activity, resulting in elevated levels of active thioguanine nucleotides (6-TGN) and increased risk of severe myelosuppression.
Another key genetic factor is variants in the NUDT15 gene, which inactivate thiopurine metabolites by hydrolyzing them; these variants are particularly prevalent in East Asian populations, where about 21% of individuals are heterozygous and 2% are homozygous poor metabolizers, primarily due to the p.R139C variant. In European and African populations, NUDT15 poor metabolizer status is rare, affecting less than 1%. Carriers of NUDT15 variants experience heightened accumulation of DNA-incorporated thioguanine, predisposing them to early-onset leukopenia and other hematologic toxicities during mercaptopurine therapy.
Clinical guidelines emphasize pre-treatment genotyping for TPMT and NUDT15 to guide dosing and mitigate risks. The Clinical Pharmacogenetics Implementation Consortium (CPIC), in its March 2024 update, recommends starting intermediate metabolizers at 30-80% of the standard dose (e.g., 22.5-60 mg/m/day for mercaptopurine in acute lymphoblastic leukemia) and poor metabolizers at 10% (e.g., 10 mg/m/day) or considering alternative therapies, with close monitoring for toxicity. For patients who are intermediate metabolizers for both TPMT and NUDT15, the starting dose should be reduced to 20-50% of the standard dose. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) advises dose reductions to 10% of the standard 1.5-2.5 mg/kg/day for known homozygous deficiencies and suggests testing in patients experiencing severe or recurrent myelosuppression.
Therapeutic drug monitoring of erythrocyte metabolites complements genotyping by assessing efficacy and toxicity. Levels of 6-TGN, the active metabolite, correlate with immunosuppressive and antileukemic effects, with target ranges typically 235-400 pmol/810^8 erythrocytes for efficacy; elevated 6-TGN (>450 pmol/810^8 erythrocytes) in genetically at-risk patients signals myelosuppression risk. Conversely, high 6-methylmercaptopurine (6-MMP) levels (>5700 pmol/810^8 erythrocytes) indicate preferential shunting to inactive pathways, often linked to hepatotoxicity rather than efficacy, guiding further dose adjustments in pharmacogenetic-guided therapy.

Clinical Safety

Adverse Effects

Mercaptopurine is associated with a range of adverse effects, primarily due to its immunosuppressive and cytotoxic properties, with myelosuppression being the most common and dose-limiting toxicity observed in clinical use. These effects are monitored through regular blood counts and liver function tests in clinical trials and post-marketing surveillance.

Hematologic Effects

The primary hematologic adverse effect of mercaptopurine is myelosuppression, manifesting as leukopenia, thrombocytopenia, and anemia, occurring in approximately 20-30% of patients across clinical trials for acute lymphoblastic leukemia and inflammatory bowel disease (IBD). This dose-limiting toxicity often requires dose adjustments or interruptions, with severe cases reported in 2-15% of patients in post-marketing data, particularly in those with genetic variants in TPMT or NUDT15 that exacerbate myelosuppression.

Gastrointestinal Effects

Gastrointestinal toxicities include nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, affecting 5-20% of patients in clinical trials, with these symptoms often non-dose-dependent and leading to treatment discontinuation in up to 30% of IBD patients based on long-term observational studies.

Hepatic Effects

Hepatotoxicity is a significant concern, presenting as elevated transaminases, cholestasis, or more severe jaundice and hepatic necrosis, with incidences of 10-30% for enzyme elevations reported in clinical trials and post-marketing surveillance for both leukemia and IBD indications. These effects can occur early (within 1-6 months) or with chronic use, leading to nodular regenerative hyperplasia in some cases.

Oncogenic Effects

Long-term use of mercaptopurine carries an increased risk of secondary malignancies, including hepatosplenic T-cell lymphoma, skin cancers, and lymphoproliferative disorders, as evidenced by post-marketing reports and cohort studies in IBD patients, with overall carcinogenicity confirmed in animal models and human data.

Other Effects

Due to its immunosuppressive action, mercaptopurine heightens susceptibility to infections, with rates varying by degree of myelosuppression but reported in <5% of clinical trial participants, often requiring prompt management. Macrophage activation syndrome (MAS), a life-threatening condition involving cytokine storm and multiorgan failure, has been reported postmarketing, particularly in patients with autoimmune or rheumatic diseases; mercaptopurine should be discontinued if MAS is suspected. Pancreatitis is a rare but serious complication, occurring in 1-3% of patients in IBD trials and post-marketing data, typically within weeks of initiation.

Precautions and Contraindications

Mercaptopurine is contraindicated in patients with known hypersensitivity to the drug or any of its components. It is also contraindicated in individuals who have previously exhibited resistance to mercaptopurine or related thiopurines such as thioguanine, as such resistance may limit therapeutic efficacy.
Precautions are essential due to the drug's immunosuppressive effects, which increase susceptibility to infections; patients with active infections should be closely monitored, and treatment initiation may require resolution of such conditions to mitigate risks. In pregnancy, mercaptopurine can cause fetal harm, including increased risks of miscarriage and birth defects, and is not recommended unless the potential benefits justify the risks to the fetus; thiopurines including mercaptopurine have been associated with intrahepatic cholestasis of pregnancy (ICP) in patients with IBD, and the drug should be discontinued if ICP develops. Effective contraception is advised for females of reproductive potential during treatment and for at least six months afterward, and for males for at least three months post-treatment. Breastfeeding should be avoided during therapy and for one week following the last dose, as the drug may cause serious adverse reactions in nursing infants.
Elderly patients require cautious dosing, starting at the lower end of the range, due to potential declines in organ function that may heighten toxicity risks. Live vaccines are contraindicated during treatment owing to immunosuppression, which can lead to diminished immune responses or increased infection risk; vaccination decisions should follow guidelines for immunocompromised individuals.
Monitoring is critical to detect myelosuppression and hepatotoxicity; complete blood counts with differential should be performed weekly during the initial phase of therapy, then at least biweekly thereafter, while liver function tests (including transaminases, alkaline phosphatase, and bilirubin) are recommended monthly. The FDA includes black box warnings for severe myelosuppression, which can be dose-related and life-threatening, necessitating dose adjustments or discontinuation based on blood counts, and for the risk of secondary malignancies, as mercaptopurine is carcinogenic and mutagenic in animal models and humans. Delayed toxicity may occur up to eight weeks after initiation, requiring ongoing vigilance for signs of bone marrow suppression or hepatic injury, including potential veno-occlusive disease of the liver.

Drug Interactions

Mercaptopurine undergoes metabolism primarily via xanthine oxidase and thiopurine methyltransferase (TPMT), pathways that can be inhibited by concurrent medications, leading to elevated levels of active metabolites like 6-thioguanine nucleotides (6-TGN) and increased risk of toxicity such as myelosuppression or hepatotoxicity. These interactions necessitate dose reductions, therapeutic drug monitoring, and avoidance of certain combinations when feasible to maintain efficacy while minimizing adverse effects. Evidence for these interactions derives from pharmacokinetic studies, clinical trials, and case reports documenting altered metabolite profiles and clinical outcomes.
A major interaction occurs with xanthine oxidase inhibitors like allopurinol, which blocks the conversion of mercaptopurine to inactive thiouric acid, resulting in a 3- to 5-fold increase in 6-TGN levels and heightened risk of severe bone marrow suppression. This effect is compounded by potential inhibition of TPMT, further shifting metabolism toward active thioguanine production. To mitigate toxicity, mercaptopurine dosing should be reduced to one-third to one-quarter (25-33%) of the standard dose, with frequent monitoring of complete blood counts and metabolite levels (e.g., targeting 6-TGN above 235 pmol/810^8 RBC for efficacy while keeping 6-methylmercaptopurine below 5,700 pmol/810^8 RBC to avoid hepatotoxicity).
Concurrent administration with other thiopurines, such as azathioprine (a prodrug metabolized to mercaptopurine), leads to additive myelosuppressive effects due to cumulative exposure to active metabolites. This combination is generally avoided, but if unavoidable, it requires substantial dose reductions for both agents and intensive hematologic monitoring to prevent life-threatening leukopenia or pancytopenia.
Aminosalicylates like olsalazine and sulfasalazine, used in inflammatory bowel disease, inhibit TPMT activity, elevating 6-TGN and 6-methylmercaptopurine levels, which enhances the risk of myelosuppression and hepatotoxicity. Clinical reports have documented severe leukopenia and liver enzyme elevations in patients on these combinations, prompting recommendations for TPMT genotyping prior to initiation, dose adjustments (e.g., reducing mercaptopurine by 25-50%), and regular liver function tests alongside blood counts. Concurrent use should be approached cautiously, with alternatives considered if toxicity emerges.
Mercaptopurine may diminish the anticoagulant effects of warfarin, necessitating higher warfarin doses to achieve therapeutic international normalized ratio (INR) levels, as observed in case reports where up to a 25% dose increase was required during mercaptopurine cycles. The mechanism remains unclear but likely involves reduced warfarin absorption or induction of hepatic metabolizing enzymes rather than protein binding displacement. Frequent INR monitoring (e.g., weekly) and dose titration are essential during coadministration to prevent thromboembolic events.
Overall management of these interactions emphasizes individualized dosing based on therapeutic drug monitoring of 6-TGN and 6-methylmercaptopurine levels, alongside clinical parameters like blood counts and liver enzymes. Pharmacokinetic studies support proactive adjustments to balance efficacy and safety, while case reports highlight the need for patient education on reporting symptoms of toxicity promptly.

History

Discovery and Development

Mercaptopurine, a thio analog of the purine base hypoxanthine, was synthesized in 1951 by Gertrude B. Elion and George H. Hitchings at Burroughs Wellcome in Tuckahoe, New York, as part of a systematic program to develop purine antimetabolites that could inhibit nucleic acid synthesis in rapidly dividing cells. Their approach involved rationally designing structural analogs of natural purines to disrupt metabolic pathways essential for microbial and cancer cell growth, building on earlier work with compounds like 2,6-diaminopurine.
Preclinical evaluation revealed mercaptopurine's potent antileukemic activity in mouse models, notably against the L1210 leukemia line, where it significantly prolonged survival in treated animals compared to controls. These studies, conducted by Howard E. Skipper in collaboration with Elion and Hitchings, demonstrated dose-dependent tumor inhibition and established the compound's therapeutic potential, paving the way for human testing.
Early clinical trials began in 1952 at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center under the leadership of Joseph H. Burchenal, with results published in 1953 showing that mercaptopurine induced complete clinical and hematologic remissions in approximately one-third of pediatric patients with acute leukemia, including acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL). Out of 45 children treated, 15 achieved substantial remissions, marking a breakthrough in managing previously intractable childhood leukemia and validating the transition of mercaptopurine from a laboratory antimetabolite to a viable therapeutic agent.
The innovative rational drug design methodology employed by Elion and Hitchings, exemplified by mercaptopurine's development, earned them the 1988 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine, shared with James Black, for discoveries leading to new classes of disease-modifying drugs.

Regulatory Approvals

Mercaptopurine received its initial approval from the United States Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in 1953 as an oral antimetabolite for the maintenance treatment of acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) in adults and children as part of combination chemotherapy regimens. This marked it as the first orally administered antimetabolite approved for cancer therapy, establishing its role in long-term remission induction and maintenance.
In 2014, the FDA approved Purixan, a liquid oral suspension formulation of mercaptopurine (20 mg/mL), specifically to improve dosing accuracy in pediatric patients with ALL who have difficulty swallowing tablets. This approval addressed adherence challenges in younger patients while maintaining bioequivalence to the tablet form. Although mercaptopurine has been used off-label for inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) since clinical studies in the 1980s demonstrated its efficacy in inducing and maintaining remission in Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis, it has not received formal FDA approval for these indications.
Internationally, the European Medicines Agency (EMA) granted marketing authorization for Xaluprine, an oral suspension of mercaptopurine, on March 9, 2012, for the treatment of ALL in adults, adolescents, and children. This formulation was authorized to enhance precise dosing, particularly in pediatric populations, and remains valid across the European Union. Mercaptopurine has been included on the World Health Organization (WHO) Model List of Essential Medicines since the inaugural list in 1977, recognizing its critical role in treating acute lymphoblastic and promyelocytic leukemia in resource-limited settings.
Labeling updates have focused on safety enhancements, including pharmacogenetic considerations. In 2017, the FDA revised the Purixan label to emphasize testing for thiopurine methyltransferase (TPMT) and NUDT15 variants in patients experiencing severe myelosuppression, recommending substantial dose reductions (up to 90%) for those with homozygous deficiencies to mitigate toxicity risks. The same update added warnings for treatment-related malignancies, such as hepatosplenic T-cell lymphoma and skin cancers, particularly in immunosuppressed patients, though these are presented in the warnings section rather than as a boxed warning. Similar pharmacogenetic guidance appears in the 2020 Purinethol label, underscoring the need for genotyping or phenotyping to guide dosing.
As of 2025, mercaptopurine maintains its regulatory approvals without major withdrawals or revocations. The EMA's most recent procedural update for Xaluprine occurred in September 2025, reaffirming its authorization for pediatric ALL treatment without new restrictions. In February 2025, the FDA approved a generic mercaptopurine oral suspension by Hikma Pharmaceuticals, expanding access options for ALL maintenance therapy. Ongoing monitoring continues for secondary malignancy risks, but no pediatric-specific EMA review alterations were reported in 2024.

Society and Culture

Brand Names and Formulations

Mercaptopurine is commercially available under several brand names worldwide, with formulations primarily consisting of oral tablets and liquid suspensions designed for precise dosing, particularly in pediatric patients. In the United States, the primary brand for tablets is Purinethol, which is supplied as 50 mg scored tablets, originally developed by Burroughs Wellcome (now part of GlaxoSmithKline) but currently marketed as generic versions produced by manufacturers such as Hikma Pharmaceuticals, Dr. Reddy's Laboratories, and Stason Pharmaceuticals, though availability is currently limited due to shortages. Purixan, an oral suspension formulation at 20 mg/mL, is approved for use in acute lymphoblastic leukemia maintenance therapy and is manufactured by Nova Laboratories Ltd. for Rare Disease Therapeutics, Inc., offering an alternative to tablets for improved compliance in children who have difficulty swallowing solids.
Internationally, mercaptopurine is marketed under variants of Purinethol, such as Puri-Nethol, by Aspen Pharmacare in regions including Australia, Europe, and parts of Asia, available as 50 mg tablets for similar indications. Xaluprine, another oral solution at 20 mg/mL, is authorized by the European Medicines Agency for pediatric and adult use in acute lymphoblastic leukemia and is produced by Nova Laboratories Ltd., facilitating easier administration in young patients.
Generic versions of mercaptopurine tablets (50 mg) and oral suspensions (20 mg/mL) are widely produced by multiple manufacturers to meet global demand, including Hikma Pharmaceuticals, Dr. Reddy's Laboratories, and Stason Pharmaceuticals, ensuring availability beyond branded products. Some regional brands, such as Ismipur and Leukerin, exist in specific markets like Canada and parts of Europe but may have limited or discontinued availability depending on local regulatory status. These formulations are immediate-release, with liquid options specifically aiding adherence in maintenance therapy for leukemia by allowing accurate weight-based dosing without the need for tablet crushing.

Availability and Legal Status

Mercaptopurine is classified as a prescription-only medication worldwide, requiring a valid prescription from a licensed healthcare provider for dispensing in countries including the United States, European Union member states, and Canada. In India, it falls under Schedule H of the Drugs and Cosmetics Rules, 1945, mandating sale only on prescription with specific labeling and record-keeping requirements by pharmacists. Its potential for misuse is considered low, as it is an antineoplastic agent without significant abuse liability, and it is not scheduled as a controlled substance under international conventions like the UN Convention on Psychotropic Substances.
The drug is widely available as a generic in the United States and European Union, where multiple manufacturers produce tablet formulations, facilitating broad access through standard pharmacy channels. It is included on the World Health Organization's List of Essential Medicines, underscoring its importance for treating conditions like acute lymphoblastic leukemia in resource-limited settings. However, supply disruptions have occurred, including a notable shortage of 50 mg tablets in the US starting in 2019 due to manufacturing issues, which was largely resolved by 2020 through alternative suppliers, though intermittent shortages persist as of 2025.
In the US, generic mercaptopurine tablets cost approximately $50150 per month for typical maintenance doses, depending on quantity, pharmacy, and insurance coverage, making it affordable for most patients. Branded liquid formulations, such as Purixan, are significantly more expensive at around $5001,000 per month, primarily due to limited competition and specialized production for pediatric use.
Access to mercaptopurine remains a challenge in low- and middle-income countries, where it is incorporated into global health initiatives like the WHO's programs for acute lymphoblastic leukemia treatment, aiming to provide affordable chemotherapy regimens. In November 2025, the WHO released target product profiles for child-friendly formulations of mercaptopurine, such as dispersible tablets, to enhance access in low- and middle-income countries through improved stability, palatability, and affordability. Pediatric-specific formulations, such as liquids or dispersible tablets, are particularly limited in these regions, often requiring importation or compounding due to insufficient local manufacturing, which can delay treatment and increase costs.
As of 2025, mercaptopurine faces no major regulatory restrictions globally, with ongoing monitoring of supply chains by the European Medicines Agency through its shortages platform to mitigate potential disruptions. Recent launches, such as Hikma's oral suspension in the US, have helped stabilize availability for specialized needs.
