 Rickets is a childhood disorder characterized by the softening and weakening of bones due to defective mineralization of the growth plates, primarily resulting from vitamin D deficiency, though it can also stem from deficiencies in calcium or phosphate. It typically affects children under the age of three, particularly during periods of rapid growth, and leads to skeletal deformities if untreated.
The most common cause of rickets is nutritional deficiency of vitamin D, which is essential for calcium absorption in the intestines; this can arise from limited sunlight exposure, inadequate dietary intake (such as in exclusively breastfed infants without supplementation), or malabsorption disorders like celiac disease or inflammatory bowel disease. Less frequently, rickets results from genetic mutations affecting vitamin D metabolism (e.g., vitamin D-dependent rickets types 1 and 2) or renal disorders that impair phosphate reabsorption, such as renal osteodystrophy. Risk factors include darker skin pigmentation, residence in high-latitude regions with reduced sunlight, premature birth, and certain medications like anticonvulsants that interfere with vitamin D activation.
Symptoms often manifest between six months and two years of age and include delayed growth, bone pain or tenderness, muscle weakness, and characteristic skeletal changes such as bowed legs, knock-knees, thickened wrists and ankles, and a prominent forehead (frontal bossing). In severe cases, infants may exhibit hypotonia, respiratory issues due to chest deformities, or hypocalcemic seizures. Diagnosis involves blood tests showing low levels of 25-hydroxyvitamin D (<10 ng/mL), elevated alkaline phosphatase, and low calcium or phosphate, alongside radiographic evidence of widened metaphyses and frayed epiphyseal plates.
Treatment for nutritional rickets focuses on vitamin D supplementation (e.g., 2,0005,000 IU daily or 50,000 IU weekly for six weeks) combined with adequate calcium intake, often leading to rapid improvement in symptoms and bone healing. Genetic or hypophosphatemic forms may require phosphate supplements, calcitriol, or specialized therapies. Prevention emphasizes ensuring infants receive 400 IU of vitamin D daily, promoting sunlight exposure of 1015 minutes several times a week, and incorporating fortified foods like milk or fatty fish into the diet.
Epidemiologically, rickets is more prevalent in developing countries, affecting 1070% of children in some regions due to malnutrition and limited sunlight, while in the United States, incidence has risen among high-risk groups like immigrant populations, with incidence rates up to 24.1 per 100,000 in areas like Minnesota in the early 2000s. Untreated rickets can result in permanent deformities, short stature, fractures, or even life-threatening complications like tetany, underscoring the importance of early intervention.

Introduction and Pathophysiology

Definition and Overview

Rickets is a childhood disorder characterized by impaired mineralization of growing bones, leading to softening, weakening, and skeletal deformities such as bowed legs and delayed growth. It primarily affects the epiphyseal growth plates, where defective mineralization of cartilage disrupts the normal transition to hardened bone tissue. This condition manifests as osteomalacia specifically in children, resulting from imbalances in calcium, phosphate, and vitamin D metabolism that hinder proper bone formation.
In normal bone development, mineralization ensures the strength and elongation of bones through coordinated deposition of calcium and phosphate into the bone matrix, a process that rickets interrupts at the growth plate stage. The disorder is confined to periods of active skeletal growth, typically before puberty, and differs from osteomalacia in adults, which involves inadequate mineralization of existing bone osteoid rather than the cartilaginous growth plates. Vitamin D plays a key role in facilitating calcium absorption for this mineralization process.
Rickets was first systematically described in the mid-17th century in Europe, where it was prevalent among urban children, and later recognized as a preventable condition in the early 20th century through discoveries linking it to nutritional factors like sunlight exposure and dietary interventions.

Bone Development Mechanisms

Bone development in children primarily occurs through endochondral ossification, a process that takes place at the growth plates of long bones and enables longitudinal growth. This mechanism begins with the proliferation and hypertrophy of chondrocytes within the epiphyseal growth plate, where these cartilage cells form a template of extracellular matrix rich in type II collagen and proteoglycans. As chondrocytes mature and undergo apoptosis, they signal the invasion of blood vessels and recruitment of osteoblasts and osteoclasts; osteoblasts deposit osteoid (unmineralized bone matrix) while osteoclasts resorb calcified cartilage, facilitating the replacement of cartilage with trabecular bone.
The mineralization of osteoid is crucial for bone strength and requires adequate levels of calcium and phosphate, which are tightly regulated by hormonal pathways. Disruptions in these processes, such as impaired mineralization, lead to the accumulation of unmineralized osteoid characteristic of rickets, resulting in softened bones and skeletal deformities. In normal physiology, osteoblasts promote mineralization by expressing alkaline phosphatase and other enzymes that elevate local phosphate concentrations, while osteoclasts maintain bone remodeling balance.
Vitamin D plays a central role in ensuring sufficient calcium and phosphate for osteoid mineralization through its active form, 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (calcitriol). This hormone enhances intestinal absorption of calcium by upregulating the expression of calcium-binding proteins like calbindin in enterocytes, increasing active transport across the gut epithelium. Additionally, 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D promotes phosphate reabsorption in the kidneys and stimulates phosphate uptake in bone cells, indirectly supporting mineralization; it also influences osteoblast differentiation and function to facilitate matrix deposition.
The metabolism of vitamin D involves sequential hydroxylations to produce its active form. Endogenous vitamin D synthesis starts in the skin, where ultraviolet B radiation converts 7-dehydrocholesterol to cholecalciferol (vitamin D3). In the liver, cholecalciferol is hydroxylated by the enzyme 25-hydroxylase to form 25-hydroxyvitamin D, the major circulating form used to assess vitamin D status. The final activation occurs in the kidneys, where 1-hydroxylase converts 25-hydroxyvitamin D to 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D, a process tightly regulated by parathyroid hormone (PTH) and other factors.
PTH and fibroblast growth factor 23 (FGF23) interact with vitamin D metabolism to maintain phosphate homeostasis. PTH, secreted by the parathyroid glands in response to low serum calcium, stimulates renal 1-hydroxylase activity to increase 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D production, enhancing calcium absorption while promoting phosphate excretion via downregulation of renal phosphate transporters. Conversely, FGF23, primarily produced by osteocytes in bone, suppresses 1-hydroxylase and activates 24-hydroxylase to degrade active vitamin D, while also increasing urinary phosphate excretion by inhibiting phosphate reabsorption in the proximal tubule; this prevents hyperphosphatemia and maintains a balance conducive to bone mineralization. These interactions ensure that disruptions, such as deficiencies in vitamin D activation, impair calcium-phosphate availability and halt proper endochondral ossification.

This sequence underscores the reliance on hepatic and renal function for systemic mineral regulation during growth.

Causes and Risk Factors

Nutritional Deficiencies

Nutritional rickets primarily arises from deficiencies in vitamin D, calcium, or phosphate, which disrupt the normal mineralization of growing bones. These shortfalls can occur through inadequate dietary intake, impaired absorption in the gastrointestinal tract, or reduced endogenous production of vitamin D due to insufficient ultraviolet B (UVB) exposure from sunlight. For instance, malabsorption syndromes such as celiac disease can hinder the uptake of vitamin D and calcium, leading to secondary nutritional rickets even when dietary sources are available.
Vitamin D deficiency is the most common nutritional cause of rickets, as it is essential for intestinal absorption of calcium and phosphate; without sufficient vitamin D, serum levels of these minerals drop, impairing bone mineralization at the growth plate. Exclusive breastfeeding without vitamin D supplementation increases risk in infants, since human breast milk contains low levels of vitamin D, particularly if the mother is deficient. Vegan diets lacking fortified foods or supplements can also precipitate deficiency, as plant-based sources naturally provide minimal vitamin D. Geographic factors, such as residence at high latitudes where UVB radiation is limited during winter months, further reduce cutaneous vitamin D synthesis. Additionally, individuals with darker skin tones, due to higher melanin content that absorbs UVB rays, require longer sunlight exposure to produce adequate vitamin D compared to those with lighter skin.
Calcium deficiency contributes to hypocalcemic rickets, particularly in developing regions where diets are low in dairy products and other calcium-rich foods, leading to reduced serum calcium levels and secondary hyperparathyroidism that mobilizes calcium from bones at the expense of proper mineralization. Phosphate shortfalls, often linked to overall poor nutrition or diets high in inhibitors like phytates, exacerbate this by limiting the availability of phosphate for hydroxyapatite formation in bone matrix. These nutritional imbalances ultimately hinder the deposition of minerals in the osteoid tissue produced by osteoblasts.

Genetic and Other Forms

Genetic forms of rickets encompass a group of inherited disorders that disrupt phosphate homeostasis or vitamin D metabolism, leading to impaired bone mineralization independent of nutritional deficiencies. These conditions are rarer than dietary causes but require specific diagnostic approaches due to their distinct molecular mechanisms.
The most common genetic variant is X-linked hypophosphatemic rickets (XLH), an X-linked dominant disorder caused by inactivating mutations in the PHEX gene on chromosome Xp22.1. These mutations result in elevated levels of fibroblast growth factor 23 (FGF23), a phosphaturic hormone produced by osteocytes, which promotes renal phosphate wasting and suppresses 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D synthesis, leading to hypophosphatemia and rickets. XLH typically presents in early childhood with lower extremity deformities, growth retardation, and dental abnormalities.
Other genetic types include autosomal dominant hypophosphatemic rickets (ADHR), caused by gain-of-function mutations in the FGF23 gene on chromosome 12p13.3 that render the protein resistant to proteolytic cleavage, thereby increasing circulating intact FGF23 levels and inducing phosphate wasting similar to XLH. Additionally, vitamin D-dependent rickets type 1 (VDDR-I) is an autosomal recessive condition due to mutations in the CYP27B1 gene on chromosome 2q37, encoding the 1-alpha-hydroxylase enzyme, which impairs the conversion of 25-hydroxyvitamin D to its active form, 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D, resulting in hypocalcemia and secondary hyperparathyroidism. Vitamin D-dependent rickets type 2 (VDDR-II), also autosomal recessive, arises from mutations in the VDR gene on chromosome 12q13.11, leading to end-organ resistance to 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D and features such as alopecia in some cases.
Oncogenic rickets, or tumor-induced osteomalacia (TIO), represents an acquired form mimicking genetic hypophosphatemias, where benign mesenchymal tumors overproduce FGF23, causing renal phosphate loss and low 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D levels. This paraneoplastic syndrome often manifests in adulthood with progressive bone pain, muscle weakness, and fractures, and resolution typically follows tumor resection.
Rare non-genetic causes include renal tubular disorders such as Fanconi syndrome, a generalized proximal tubule dysfunction that leads to excessive urinary loss of phosphate, bicarbonate, and other solutes, resulting in hypophosphatemic rickets. Certain medications, particularly anticonvulsants like phenytoin and phenobarbital, can also induce rickets by accelerating hepatic metabolism of vitamin D and reducing its activation, thereby disrupting calcium and phosphate balance.

Clinical Presentation

Signs and Symptoms

Rickets manifests in children through a range of early signs related to impaired growth and neuromuscular function. Affected individuals often exhibit delayed growth, characterized by slower-than-expected height and weight gain during infancy and early childhood. Muscle weakness and hypotonia, or reduced muscle tone, are common, leading to motor delays such as late achievement of milestones like sitting, crawling, or walking (beyond 18 months). In infants, softening of the skull bones (craniotabes) may be an early sign.
Skeletal deformities become prominent as the disease progresses, resulting from softened bones unable to support normal weight-bearing and development. These include bowing of the legs (genu varum), knock-knees (genu valgum), widening of the wrists and ankles due to metaphyseal expansion, and a rachitic rosary presenting as bead-like enlargements along the rib junctions. Additional features encompass frontal bossing, where the forehead protrudes, and potential chest deformities such as pigeon breast or Harrison's groove at the lower rib attachments.
Children with rickets frequently experience bone pain and tenderness, particularly in the legs, spine, and pelvis, which may worsen with movement or pressure and manifest as irritability in younger patients. Proximal muscle weakness contributes to a waddling gait and difficulty rising from a seated position. These symptoms stem from deficiencies or disorders in vitamin D, calcium, or phosphate metabolism that disrupt normal bone mineralization.
Dental manifestations include delayed tooth eruption, enamel hypoplasia leading to defective tooth formation, and increased susceptibility to caries, reflecting the systemic impact on mineralization processes.

Complications

Untreated rickets can lead to a range of severe skeletal and extraskeletal complications due to impaired bone mineralization and associated metabolic disturbances. These consequences often persist if the condition is not addressed during childhood, affecting physical development and overall health.
Skeletal deformities represent one of the most prominent long-term effects of rickets. Permanent bowing of the legs, such as genu varum or genu valgum, occurs as softened bones deform under the weight of the growing child. In females, pelvic distortions, including a narrowed or contracted pelvis (known as rickety pelvis), can develop, potentially complicating childbirth in adulthood by obstructing the birth canal. Additionally, untreated rickets frequently results in short stature due to disrupted linear growth at the epiphyseal plates.
The weakened bone structure in rickets increases the susceptibility to pathological fractures, even with minimal trauma. These fractures, often occurring in the limbs, are accompanied by chronic skeletal pain, particularly in the spine, pelvis, and legs, which can significantly impair mobility and quality of life.
Extraskeletal complications arise primarily from hypocalcemia and structural changes. Seizures may occur as a result of low serum calcium levels, manifesting as tetany or convulsions in severe cases. Chest deformities, such as the rachitic rosary or pigeon breast, can restrict thoracic expansion, leading to respiratory issues including recurrent infections and, in extreme cases, respiratory failure. Furthermore, developmental delays, including motor milestones and overall growth retardation, are common due to muscle weakness and hypotonia associated with the disease.
In the long term, unresolved rickets in childhood can progress to osteomalacia in adulthood, where continued defective mineralization of bone matrix leads to persistent softening, pain, and fracture risk.

Diagnosis

Clinical Assessment

The clinical assessment of rickets begins with a detailed history taking to identify potential risk factors and underlying causes. Clinicians evaluate dietary habits, particularly the intake of vitamin D-fortified foods, calcium-rich sources, and the duration of exclusive breastfeeding without supplementation, as prolonged breastfeeding alone can contribute to deficiency in infants. Sunlight exposure is assessed, including the frequency, duration, and protective measures like clothing or sunscreen that may limit cutaneous vitamin D synthesis, with minimal exposure noted in a high proportion of cases. Family history is explored for skeletal disorders, short stature, or consanguinity indicative of genetic forms such as hypophosphatemic rickets. Additional risk factors include prematurity, especially in very low birth weight infants, and darker skin pigmentation, which reduces vitamin D production from UVB radiation.
Physical examination focuses on growth parameters and musculoskeletal findings to detect abnormalities suggestive of rickets. Height, weight, and head circumference are measured to identify growth retardation or failure to thrive, which are common in affected children. Skeletal deformities are assessed, including widening of the wrists and ankles, bowing of the legs (genu varum or valgum), frontal bossing, and the rachitic rosary along the costochondral junctions. Bone tenderness upon palpation and proximal muscle weakness or hypotonia are evaluated, often manifesting as irritability or a waddling gait. These findings build on common symptoms like bone pain and delayed motor milestones observed in clinical presentation.
Age-specific considerations guide the assessment, as manifestations differ between infants and older children. In infants, particularly those under one year, emphasis is placed on early signs such as craniotabes (softening of the skull), delayed fontanelle closure, and hypocalcemic features like tetany or seizures, often linked to maternal vitamin D deficiency or inadequate supplementation. In older children and toddlers, the focus shifts to lower limb deformities that become evident upon weight-bearing, along with disproportionate short stature and subtle proximal myopathy affecting mobility.
Red flags during assessment warrant urgent evaluation to prevent complications. Failure to thrive, evidenced by persistent weight loss or stagnation despite adequate caloric intake, signals severe nutritional or metabolic disruption. Neurological symptoms, including seizures, irritability, or tetany due to hypocalcemia, require immediate intervention, as they may indicate life-threatening electrolyte imbalances or associated conditions like cardiomyopathy.

Laboratory and Imaging Studies

Laboratory studies play a crucial role in confirming the diagnosis of rickets and distinguishing between its various forms, such as nutritional, hypophosphatemic, or vitamin D-dependent types. Blood tests typically reveal elevated levels of alkaline phosphatase (ALP), often exceeding 400 IU/L in hypophosphatemic rickets and surpassing 2000 IU/L in calcipenic forms, reflecting increased bone turnover due to impaired mineralization. Serum phosphate is commonly low across types, resulting from renal losses or inadequate absorption, while 25-hydroxyvitamin D levels below 10 ng/mL indicate deficiency in nutritional rickets. Calcium may be low or normal, particularly in calcipenic rickets where it is often adjusted for serum albumin, and parathyroid hormone (PTH) is elevated in response to hypocalcemia, promoting secondary hyperparathyroidism.
Urine tests are particularly informative for hypophosphatemic rickets, where phosphaturiaexcessive phosphate excretionsignals renal tubular defects, helping to differentiate it from nutritional causes. These assessments, often including fractional excretion of phosphate, guide the evaluation of renal handling of minerals.
Imaging studies provide visual confirmation of skeletal changes. X-rays of the wrists, knees, and long bones characteristically show widened and irregular growth plates, metaphyseal fraying with indistinct margins, cupping or concavity of the metaphysis, splaying of metaphyseal ends, and overall demineralization leading to bowing deformities. Chest X-rays may reveal the rachitic rosary as expansions at the costochondral junctions. Ultrasound can occasionally assess prominence at the costochondral junctions in cases of suspected rachitic rosary, offering a non-ionizing alternative for initial evaluation in young children.
For suspected hereditary forms, genetic testing involves sequencing relevant genes, such as PHEX for X-linked hypophosphatemic rickets or VDR for vitamin D-dependent rickets type 2, to identify causative mutations and confirm the diagnosis.
Bone biopsy, though rarely performed due to its invasiveness, serves as a definitive tool in ambiguous cases, demonstrating excess unmineralized osteoid tissue indicative of defective mineralization.

Management and Treatment

Nutritional and Lifestyle Interventions

The primary approach to managing nutritional rickets involves correcting underlying deficiencies in vitamin D and calcium through targeted dietary modifications and lifestyle adjustments, which form the cornerstone of first-line treatment. These interventions aim to enhance endogenous vitamin D production and improve mineral absorption, leading to normalization of bone mineralization over time.
Dietary changes focus on increasing consumption of vitamin D-rich and fortified foods, alongside calcium sources, to meet recommended daily intakes. Foods naturally high in vitamin D, such as fatty fish like salmon and mackerel, as well as egg yolks, should be incorporated regularly; additionally, vitamin D-fortified items including milk, cereals, and orange juice provide accessible options for children. For calcium, emphasis is placed on dairy products like milk and yogurt, or plant-based alternatives such as leafy greens (e.g., kale, broccoli) and fortified non-dairy milks, targeting at least 500 mg per day for children over 12 months to support bone health. These adjustments address the dietary insufficiencies that contribute to rickets, as referenced in sections on causes.
Safe sunlight exposure is recommended to promote cutaneous vitamin D synthesis, a natural and non-invasive intervention. Guidelines suggest 10 to 15 minutes of midday sun exposure on the face, arms, and legs several times per week, adjusted for skin type (longer for darker skin) and geographic location to avoid sunburn while minimizing skin cancer risk. Protective measures like sunscreen should be applied after this brief period, as excessive exposure is unnecessary and potentially harmful.
For exclusively breastfed infants, who are at higher risk due to low vitamin D content in breast milk, routine supplementation with 400 international units (IU) of vitamin D per day is advised starting soon after birth, in line with American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) recommendations. This prophylactic measure continues until the infant consumes sufficient vitamin D-fortified formula or foods, helping prevent and treat early-onset rickets.
Response to these interventions is monitored through clinical follow-up, assessing growth parameters, symptom resolution (e.g., improved muscle strength and reduced bone pain), and radiographic evidence of healing, typically over 1 to 3 months. Biochemical markers, such as serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D and alkaline phosphatase levels, may also be re-evaluated to confirm adequacy and guide ongoing adjustments.

Medical and Surgical Therapies

Medical therapies for rickets primarily involve targeted pharmacological interventions to address underlying biochemical deficiencies, with treatment tailored to the specific etiology such as nutritional or hypophosphatemic forms. For nutritional rickets, high-dose vitamin D supplementation, typically ergocalciferol at 2,0005,000 IU/day, is administered alongside calcium at 500 mg/day to promote bone mineralization and correct hypocalcemia or hypophosphatemia. These doses are titrated based on clinical response, monitoring serum levels of 25-hydroxyvitamin D, calcium, phosphate, parathyroid hormone, and alkaline phosphatase, with therapy continuing for at least 3 months until radiographic evidence of healing is observed. This approach effectively reverses skeletal deformities in most cases when initiated early.
In hypophosphatemic rickets, conventional management counters the effects of elevated fibroblast growth factor 23 (FGF23), which promotes renal phosphate wasting and impairs vitamin D activation. Oral phosphate salts, dosed at 2060 mg/kg/day of elemental phosphorus in divided doses for children or 7501,600 mg/day for adults, are combined with active vitamin D analogs such as calcitriol (0.50.75 g/day in adults) or alfacalcidol (0.751.5 g/day) to enhance intestinal phosphate absorption and suppress secondary hyperparathyroidism. Doses are adjusted to maintain normal serum phosphate and parathyroid hormone levels while avoiding hypercalciuria or nephrocalcinosis, though long-term adherence can be challenging due to frequent dosing and gastrointestinal side effects.
For genetic forms like X-linked hypophosphatemia, burosumab, a monoclonal antibody that inhibits FGF23, represents a targeted therapy approved by the FDA in 2018 (expanded in 2022) for patients 6 months of age and older. Administered subcutaneously every 4 weeks at doses of 0.8 mg/kg (up to 50 mg) for children under 10 kg or 1 mg/kg (up to 80 mg) for heavier children and adults, it improves phosphate homeostasis, reduces alkaline phosphatase levels, and enhances growth compared to traditional therapies.
Surgical interventions are reserved for refractory or severe deformities not adequately addressed by medical therapy alone, typically performed after biochemical stabilization to minimize perioperative complications. Orthopedic procedures, such as femoral or tibial osteotomies, correct angular deformities like varus or valgus malalignment by realigning the mechanical axis of the lower limbs, often using internal fixation plates. These surgeries improve gait, function, and prevent secondary osteoarthritis, with outcomes optimized in children over 10 years when combined with ongoing medical management.

Prevention

Dietary and Supplementation Strategies

Preventing rickets through dietary strategies focuses on ensuring adequate intake of vitamin D and calcium, the primary nutrients implicated in bone mineralization. The Institute of Medicine (IOM) recommends a daily vitamin D intake of 400 international units (IU) for infants from birth to 12 months and 600 IU for children and adolescents aged 1 to 18 years to support skeletal health and prevent deficiency-related disorders. For calcium, IOM guidelines specify age-specific requirements ranging from 200 mg per day for infants aged 0 to 6 months, increasing to 1,300 mg per day for adolescents aged 9 to 18 years, as insufficient calcium exacerbates the risk of rickets when combined with low vitamin D. These intakes can typically be met through a combination of natural food sources, fortified products, and supplements, with sunlight exposure serving as a complementary source of vitamin D synthesis in the skin.
Food fortification programs play a crucial role in delivering these nutrients to high-risk populations, particularly in regions with limited access to diverse diets. In many countries, including the United States, milk is routinely fortified with vitamin D at levels of 100 IU per 8-ounce serving, significantly contributing to meeting daily requirements and reducing rickets incidence. Breakfast cereals and margarine are also commonly fortified with vitamin D, providing an additional 10-25% of the recommended intake per serving, while some programs target calcium fortification in staples like flour to address dietary gaps in low-income or urban communities.
Supplementation protocols are essential for at-risk groups to bridge potential shortfalls. For preterm infants, who have higher needs due to rapid growth and limited stores, the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) endorses at least 400 IU of vitamin D daily starting soon after birth, with some guidelines suggesting up to 800 IU for very low birth weight infants to prevent rickets. Children with limited sun exposure, such as those in northern latitudes or with cultural practices restricting outdoor activity, should receive 400-600 IU of vitamin D supplements daily, as endorsed by global consensus recommendations, to maintain serum levels sufficient for bone health.
Cultural and dietary adaptations ensure accessibility for diverse populations. For individuals following vegan diets, fortified plant-based milks (e.g., soy or almond milk) provide viable sources of both vitamin D (often 100-120 IU per serving) and calcium (around 300 mg per serving), helping prevent deficiencies that could lead to rickets. Lactose-intolerant individuals can similarly rely on these fortified non-dairy alternatives or calcium-set tofu and leafy greens like kale, combined with vitamin D supplements, to achieve recommended intakes without relying on traditional dairy products.

Public Health Measures

Public health measures to prevent rickets emphasize systemic strategies that address environmental, cultural, and socioeconomic barriers to vitamin D adequacy on a population level. These include educational campaigns promoting safe sunlight exposure, which is essential for cutaneous vitamin D synthesis, particularly in urban settings where indoor lifestyles predominate, among veiled populations with limited skin exposure, and in regions hampered by air pollution or seasonal reductions in UVB radiation that impair vitamin D production. For instance, initiatives in high-latitude or polluted areas like northern India advocate for brief daily outdoor time (10-30 minutes) without sunscreen during midday, balanced against skin cancer risks, to mitigate deficiency in at-risk groups such as children in urban slums.
Food fortification policies represent a cornerstone of rickets prevention by ensuring widespread access to vitamin D through staple foods. In Canada, following the 2022 regulatory update that approximately doubled levels, mandatory fortification requires 2 g (80 IU) of vitamin D per 100 ml of milk and 26 g (1,040 IU) per 100 g of margarine, using either D2 or D3 forms, which has contributed to improved population-level vitamin D status and reduced rickets incidence. In the United Kingdom, fortification remains voluntary but is commonly implemented in fat spreads and breakfast cereals, with ongoing evaluations exploring mandatory expansion to further lower deficiency rates among vulnerable populations. In endemic regions of South Asia, where calcium and vitamin D intakes are often inadequate, public health supplementation campaigns distribute 400 IU daily vitamin D to infants and children through community programs, targeting high-prevalence areas to curb nutritional rickets outbreaks.
Screening programs focus on early detection of vitamin D deficiency in high-risk groups to preempt rickets development, integrating routine serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D testing into primary care for populations such as immigrants, low-income families, dark-skinned individuals, and exclusively breastfed infants. Guidelines recommend screening these groups, defining deficiency as levels below 20 ng/mL (50 nmol/L), to guide timely supplementation and avert skeletal complications, though population-wide screening is not advised due to limited evidence of broad benefits.
On a global scale, the World Health Organization (WHO) promotes micronutrient programs in developing countries to prevent nutritional rickets, advocating universal vitamin D supplementation of 400 IU per day for infants from birth to 12 months, alongside calcium enhancement through fortified foods tailored to local diets. These initiatives, integrated into maternal and child health frameworks, include fortification of staples like milk and flour in regions with high rickets burden, such as parts of Africa and Asia, and emphasize monitoring adherence to reduce prevalence in low-resource settings.

Epidemiology

Global Prevalence

Nutritional rickets, the most common form worldwide, affects millions of children globally, with prevalence estimates ranging from 10% to 70% in developing regions of Africa, the Middle East, and Asia, driven primarily by vitamin D and calcium deficiencies. In low- and middle-income countries, the condition remains a significant public health issue, particularly among children under five years, where undernutrition exacerbates the burden, though exact global figures are challenging due to underreporting and diagnostic limitations. In contrast, developed countries report lower incidence rates, such as 2.9 per 100,000 in Canada and approximately 1.4 per 100,000 under five years in the United Kingdom as of the early 2020s, but cases persist among vulnerable groups. As of 2025, nutritional rickets persists as a public health issue in sunny regions, with prevalence influenced by calcium deficiency alongside vitamin D issues.
Regional variations highlight stark disparities: in the Middle East, prevalence is elevated due to cultural practices like veiling and environmental factors such as air pollution, with vitamin D deficiency rates reaching up to 71.8% in some populations. South Asia experiences high rates linked to vegetarian diets low in vitamin D and calcium, with deficiency prevalence between 40% and 80%. In sub-Saharan Africa, nutritional rickets is prevalent owing to widespread malnutrition and calcium-poor diets, with incidence ranging from 2 to 37 per 1,000 person-years in affected areas.
Historical trends show a marked decline following public health interventions like milk fortification in the 1930s, which reduced cases dramatically in the United States from widespread occurrence to an incidence of 2.2 per 100,000 by the early 1980s. However, recent decades have witnessed a resurgence in developed nations, with U.S. incidence rising to 24.1 per 100,000 by the early 2000s, attributed to factors including increased indoor lifestyles, obesity, and widespread sunscreen use that limit cutaneous vitamin D synthesis.
Underdiagnosis is particularly notable in migrant and refugee populations, where nutritional rickets is more common due to combined risks of dark skin pigmentation, limited sun exposure, and dietary inadequacies, yet often goes unrecognized in high-income host countries. This contributes to higher true prevalence rates than reported statistics suggest, especially in urban poor communities.

Demographic Patterns

Rickets exhibits distinct demographic patterns, with incidence peaking in early childhood, particularly among infants aged 6 to 24 months, when rapid skeletal growth imposes heightened demands for vitamin D and calcium to support bone mineralization. This age group is especially vulnerable due to limited stores of vitamin D accumulated in utero and during early life, compounded by dietary transitions and reduced sunlight exposure in many settings. While a secondary peak can occur in adolescence, the primary burden falls on toddlers, where clinical manifestations like bowing of the legs often emerge by 18 months if deficiency persists.
Ethnic disparities significantly influence rickets risk, with higher rates observed in populations with darker skin pigmentation, such as those of African and South Asian descent, due to melanin reducing cutaneous synthesis of vitamin D from ultraviolet B exposure. In the United Kingdom, for instance, early 2000s data showed incidence among children under 5 years of 95 per 100,000 for African ethnicity, 38 per 100,000 for Asian ethnicity, and only 0.4 per 100,000 for Caucasian children, reflecting a 200- to 250-fold disparity. Black children face a 10-fold greater risk of nutritional rickets compared to other groups under 5 years, while South Asian children experience a 5-fold increase, often exacerbated by cultural practices limiting sun exposure and dietary vitamin D sources. These patterns persist in migrant communities, where environmental factors in higher latitudes further impair vitamin D production.
Socioeconomic status plays a critical role, with poverty, food insecurity, and restricted healthcare access elevating rickets risk, particularly in urban slums and rural areas where malnutrition and overcrowding prevail. In disadvantaged urban areas of Ireland, vitamin D deficiency prevalence is 34% among children, compared to lower rates in affluent areas, with an odds ratio of 2.18 for deficiency in low-socioeconomic settings linked to reduced dietary intake and outdoor activity. Larger household sizes correlate with lower serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D levels, as seen in inner-city Bangladeshi children where households with more members showed significantly reduced vitamin D status due to shared resources and limited nutrition. In Egypt, low family income and poor housing conditions contribute to a 13.1% incidence of rachitic deformities in children aged 2-4 years from low-socioeconomic backgrounds.
Additional risk factors include prematurity, obesity, and exclusive breastfeeding without vitamin D supplementation, which compound vulnerability across demographics. Preterm infants, especially those with birth weights under 1,000 grams, face elevated risk due to mineral deprivation and immature metabolic pathways, with rates up to 40% in unsupplemented cases. Obesity sequesters vitamin D in adipose tissue, reducing circulating levels and increasing deficiency risk, as evidenced in studies where obese children exhibited higher rates of hypovitaminosis D compared to normal-weight peers. Exclusive breastfeeding without supplements heightens susceptibility, as human milk is low in vitamin D, leading to nutritional rickets in unsupplemented infants with inadequate sun exposure.

History

Historical Recognition

The earliest systematic descriptions of rickets emerged in 17th-century England, where the condition was increasingly observed amid rapid urbanization and dietary shifts. In 1645, Daniel Whistler, an English physician, presented a Latin dissertation for his medical degree at Leiden University, titled Inaugural Medical Disputation on the English Disease in Children, Known as Rickets, which provided one of the first detailed clinical accounts of the disease's symptoms, including skeletal deformities and growth impairments in infants and children. This work was followed in 1650 by Francis Glisson's comprehensive treatise De Rachitide (translated as A Treatise of the Rickets), co-authored with physicians George Bate and Edward Browne, which expanded on Whistler's observations with anatomical dissections, pathological descriptions, and treatment suggestions, establishing rickets as a distinct pediatric disorder prevalent in industrial areas.
By the 19th century, researchers began connecting rickets to environmental and nutritional factors exacerbated by industrialization. Polish physician and chemist Jdrzej niadecki, in his 1822 publication O Rachitide (On Rickets), was among the first to hypothesize a role for sunlight deficiency, noting lower rickets rates in rural Polish children exposed to more natural light compared to urban dwellers in Warsaw, where poor ventilation and limited outdoor access prevailed. This insight aligned with broader observations linking the disease to overcrowded cities, inadequate diets lacking fresh foods, and the soot-laden air of industrial England and Europe, where cases surged during the Victorian era.
The 20th century marked pivotal breakthroughs in understanding rickets' etiology and prevention. In 1922, American biochemist Elmer V. McCollum and his team at Johns Hopkins University isolated the antirachitic factor in cod liver oil, designating it as vitamin D after demonstrating its distinct role from vitamin A in preventing bone deformities in experimental rats. Concurrently, clinical trials in post-World War I Vienna, led by British researcher Harriette Chick under the Medical Research Council from 1919 to 1922, tested cod liver oil supplementation on institutionalized infants, showing dramatic reductions in rickets incidence from over 80% in unsupplemented groups to near elimination in treated cohortswhile also confirming sunlight exposure as an effective alternative therapy.
In the modern era, starting from the 1950s, the recognition of genetic forms of rickets expanded the disease's classification beyond nutritional deficiencies. Pioneering work by clinicians like Charles E. Dent and H. Harris in 1956 identified hereditary hypophosphatemic rickets as a distinct entity involving renal phosphate wasting, unresponsive to vitamin D alone, paving the way for targeted therapies. Post-2000 global efforts have focused on eradication through international consensus, including the 2016 Global Consensus Recommendations by the Endocrine Society and partners, which advocate widespread vitamin D fortification, supplementation programs, and surveillance to eliminate nutritional rickets in at-risk populations worldwide.

Etymology and Terminology

The term "rickets" first appeared in print in 1634, recorded in the Annual Bill of Mortality for London as a cause of death among children. Its origins trace to an English dialect, likely from the Dorset region in the 1630s, derived from "wrick" or "wricken," meaning to twist or wrench, reflecting the disease's characteristic bone deformities. An alternative etymology links it to the Old English "wrickken," also implying twisting, while some sources suggest influence from the German "wricken," translating to "twisted." 
In the 17th century, English physician Daniel Whistler used the term in his 1645 Leiden thesis, titled De morbo puerili Anglorum, quem patrio idimate indigenae vocant The Rickets, marking its formal introduction in medical literature as a native English name for the childhood bone disorder. Whistler adopted the Greek-derived "rachitis" for the scientific nomenclature, from rhakhis meaning "spine," to describe the spinal and skeletal distortions observed. This was echoed in Francis Glisson's 1650 treatise De rachitide sive morbo puerili, qui vulgo The Rickets dicitur, where he proposed "Ilrackitis" or a variant of "rachitis" based on the Greek root for spine, emphasizing the disease's effects on vertebral structure. 
Early nomenclature varied culturally; in Germany, it was known as die Englische Krankheit ("the English disease") due to its prevalence in England during the 17th century. Over time, terminology evolved from descriptive phrases like "infantile osteomalacia," which blurred distinctions between childhood rickets and adult bone softening, to more precise classifications in the 20th century. Modern usage differentiates nutritional rickets, often termed "hypovitaminosis D rickets," from hereditary forms such as hypophosphatemic rickets, reflecting advances in understanding etiology.
