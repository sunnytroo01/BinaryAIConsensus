Hexagonal prism
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 A hexagonal prism is a three-dimensional polyhedron consisting of two congruent and parallel hexagonal bases connected by six parallelogram lateral faces. It belongs to the family of prisms, which are defined by their polygonal bases and the height separating them, and is named for the six-sided hexagonal shape of those bases. In total, a hexagonal prism has 8 faces (two hexagons and six parallelograms), 18 edges (12 from the bases and 6 lateral), and 12 vertices.
When the hexagonal bases are regular (equilateral and equiangular) and the lateral faces are rectangles perpendicular to the bases, the figure is known as a right regular hexagonal prism, exhibiting high symmetry. These properties make the hexagonal prism a fundamental shape in geometry, with applications in nature and engineering, such as in cellular structures requiring efficient packing.

Definition and construction

Basic definition

A hexagonal prism is a three-dimensional polyhedron defined as a prism featuring two parallel and congruent hexagonal bases connected by six lateral faces. The bases are hexagons, which may be regularmeaning each has six equal sides and equal interior angles of 120 degreesor irregular, while the lateral faces are rectangles in the case of a right prism or parallelograms in an oblique prism. This structure distinguishes it from other prisms by the six-sided polygonal bases, providing a symmetric foundation for various geometric applications.
In a right hexagonal prism, the lateral edges are perpendicular to the bases, resulting in rectangular lateral faces that may be squares if the height equals the side length of the base hexagon. Conversely, an oblique hexagonal prism has lateral edges that are not perpendicular to the bases, tilting the structure and forming parallelogram-shaped lateral faces while maintaining the parallel bases. These variations allow for flexibility in construction without altering the fundamental prismatic form.
The term "hexagonal" derives from the Greek "hex" meaning six and referring to the six-sided bases, combined with "prism," which originates from the Late Latin "prisma" and Ancient Greek "prsma," meaning "something sawn" or "cleaved," evoking the idea of a solid cut from a larger form with parallel faces. This etymology reflects the historical geometric conceptualization of prisms as extruded polygonal sections.

Construction and variations

A hexagonal prism is constructed by extruding a hexagonal base along a straight line perpendicular to its plane, forming a right prism with rectangular lateral faces perpendicular to the bases. This extrusion process creates two parallel hexagonal bases connected by six rectangular sides. If the extrusion direction is oblique, at an angle to the base plane, the resulting prism has parallelogram lateral faces, distinguishing it from the right variant.
Variations of the hexagonal prism include the uniform form, where the bases are regular hexagons and the lateral faces are squares, ensuring all faces are regular polygons of equal edge length. Finite prisms have a defined height between bases, while infinite prisms extend indefinitely along the extrusion axis, though only finite versions qualify as polyhedra. Extensions such as truncated hexagonal prisms involve cutting off the vertices, replacing them with new faces to create more complex structures.
Practical construction begins with drawing the hexagonal bases using a compass and straightedge: inscribe a regular hexagon in a circle by marking six equidistant points on the circumference with the compass set to the radius, then connect them with the straightedge. The lateral edges are formed by drawing ruled lines connecting corresponding vertices of the two parallel bases. In contemporary applications, hexagonal prisms are built digitally in computer-aided design (CAD) software, where the base is sketched and extruded to specify height and angle precisely.
The foundational concepts of prism construction trace back to classical geometry, with Euclid defining prisms in Book XI of the Elements as solids bounded by parallel planes and parallelograms, and subsequent texts expanding on specific polygonal bases like the hexagon.

Geometric properties

Faces, edges, and vertices

A hexagonal prism is a polyhedron consisting of two parallel hexagonal bases connected by six rectangular lateral faces, resulting in a total of 8 faces: 2 hexagons and 6 rectangles. Each hexagonal base is bounded by 6 edges, while each rectangular lateral face is bounded by 4 edges, though adjacent rectangles share edges along the height of the prism.
The prism features 18 edges in total, comprising 12 edges from the two hexagonal bases (6 per base) and 6 vertical edges connecting corresponding vertices of the bases. It also has 12 vertices, with 6 forming each hexagonal base.
In terms of edge connectivity, the skeleton of the hexagonal prism forms a cubic graph, where each of the 12 vertices has degree 3: two edges connect to adjacent vertices on the same base, and one edge links to the corresponding vertex on the opposite base.
Topologically, the surface of the hexagonal prism is simply connected and orientable, equivalent to a sphere with genus 0, as confirmed by its Euler characteristic = V - E + F = 12 - 18 + 8 = 2, where the formula = 2 - 2g holds for orientable closed surfaces.

Dimensions and formulas

In a regular hexagonal prism, the side length of each regular hexagonal base is denoted by $ a $, and the height of the prism (distance between the two parallel bases) is denoted by $ h $.
Key dimensions of the base include the apothem, which is the perpendicular distance from the center to a side and equals the inradius $ r = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} a $. The circumradius $ R $, or distance from the center to a vertex, is $ R = a $. The distance between two opposite sides (flat-to-flat width) is $ \sqrt{3} a $.
The lateral edges, connecting corresponding vertices of the two bases, each have length $ h $.
For a right regular hexagonal prism, the dihedral angle between a base and an adjacent lateral face is $ 90^\circ $. The dihedral angle between two adjacent lateral faces is $ 120^\circ $.

Volume and surface area

The volume of a hexagonal prism is determined by multiplying the area of one hexagonal base by the perpendicular height $ h $ of the prism. For a regular hexagon with side length $ a $, which can be decomposed into six equilateral triangles, each with area $ \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} a^2 $, resulting in a base area of $ 6 \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} a^2 = \frac{3 \sqrt{3}}{2} a^2 $. Therefore, the volume $ V $ is
V=332a2h,
V = \frac{3 \sqrt{3}}{2} a^2 h,
V=233a2h,
expressed in cubic units consistent with the units of $ a $ and $ h $. This formula applies to both right and oblique hexagonal prisms with regular bases, where the volume remains unchanged for a fixed base area and perpendicular height, as established by Cavalieri's principle equating volumes of solids with equal cross-sectional areas at corresponding heights.
The total surface area $ A $ of a right hexagonal prism with regular bases consists of the areas of the two hexagonal bases plus the lateral surface area from the six rectangular sides. The two bases contribute $ 2 \times \frac{3 \sqrt{3}}{2} a^2 = 3 \sqrt{3} a^2 $, while the lateral area is the perimeter of the base (6a) times the height, yielding $ 6 a h $. Thus, the total surface area is
A=33a2+6ah,
A = 3 \sqrt{3} a^2 + 6 a h,
A=33a2+6ah,
with the lateral surface area alone given by $ A_l = 6 a h $, all in square units. For oblique prisms with regular bases, the base areas remain the same, but the lateral surface area adjusts to the perimeter times the slant height (the length along the lateral edges), which exceeds $ h $ by the obliqueness factor.
These formulas demonstrate scaling properties: the volume scales quadratically with $ a $ and linearly with $ h $ (as $ a^2 h $), while the total surface area scales linearly with both $ a $ and $ h $ in the lateral term but quadratically with $ a $ in the base terms, reflecting the two-dimensional nature of surfaces.

Classification as a polyhedron

Uniform and semiregular properties

A uniform hexagonal prism is a polyhedron with regular hexagonal bases and square lateral faces, making all faces regular polygons of equal edge length and rendering it vertex-transitive under its symmetry group. This configuration ensures that the polyhedron is uniform, as every vertex is surrounded by the same arrangement of faces: one hexagon and two squares.
The uniform hexagonal prism exhibits semiregular properties in the broader sense, where all vertices are congruent and identical in their local geometry, akin to Archimedean solids but classified distinctly as a prism. It can be constructed via the Wythoff symbol $ 2\ 6\ |\ 2 $, which generates its vertices from the reflections of a point in a triangular fundamental domain of the dihedral symmetry group.
If the bases are irregular hexagons or the prism is oblique (with non-perpendicular lateral edges), the resulting figure loses uniformity, as the faces would no longer be regular or the vertices non-equivalent under symmetry.
As a convex polyhedron, the uniform hexagonal prism satisfies Euler's formula, with 12 vertices, 18 edges, and 8 faces yielding $ V - E + F = 12 - 18 + 8 = 2 $.

Relation to prisms and antiprisms

A hexagonal prism is a specific instance of the broader family of n-gonal prisms, where the two parallel bases are regular n-gons and the lateral faces are rectangles connecting corresponding sides of the bases. For n=6, the bases are regular hexagons, resulting in six rectangular lateral faces, distinguishing it from other prisms such as triangular (n=3) or pentagonal (n=5) variants. This family encompasses an infinite set of polyhedra parameterized by n, with the hexagonal prism serving as the case where the base has six sides.
In contrast to prisms, which feature aligned parallel bases joined by rectangular lateral faces, antiprisms involve bases that are rotated relative to each other by an angle of 180/n and connected by triangular lateral faces. A hexagonal antiprism, for example, consists of two regular hexagonal bases linked by 12 equilateral triangles, forming a more compact and twisted structure compared to the straightforward alignment in the hexagonal prism. This rotation introduces higher symmetry in the lateral banding, differentiating antiprisms as a parallel infinite family to prisms.
The uniform hexagonal prism, featuring square lateral faces of equal edge length to the hexagonal bases, qualifies as a uniform polyhedron due to its regular faces and vertex-transitive symmetry, but it is not classified as an Archimedean solid. Archimedean solids exclude the infinite families of prisms and antiprisms, reserving the term for the 13 finite convex uniform polyhedra beyond the Platonic solids; higher prisms like the hexagonal one lack regular vertex figures, with each vertex surrounded by a configuration of one hexagon and two squares rather than an equilateral polygonal figure. Uniform prisms are thus distinct from Johnson solids, which comprise the 92 strictly convex polyhedra with regular faces that are not uniform (i.e., not vertex-transitive).
Prisms form part of the infinite families of uniform polyhedra, and as the height h approaches infinity, they extend into infinite prismatic structures used in geometric modeling and tilings, though the approximation to a cylindrical form more precisely occurs as n tends to infinity for fixed height, blending the polygonal bases into circular ones.

Space-filling and tiling

As a parallelohedron

A parallelohedron is a convex polyhedron that tiles three-dimensional Euclidean space solely through translations, filling it without gaps or overlaps in a face-to-face manner.
In 1885, the Russian crystallographer Evgraf Fedorov enumerated exactly five combinatorial types of such polyhedra, motivated by the study of crystal structures: the parallelepiped (or cuboid), hexagonal prism, rhombic dodecahedron, elongated dodecahedron, and truncated octahedron.
The hexagonal prism qualifies as a parallelohedron because all its faces occur in identical parallel pairsa pair of regular hexagonal bases and three pairs of identical parallelograms for the lateral faces (rectangles in the case of a right prism)and its 18 edges form belts of either four or six mutually parallel edges, adhering to Fedorov's topological conditions for space-filling by translation. This structure arises from the six-fold rotational symmetry of the hexagonal base, which enables the prism to serve as the fundamental domain for certain lattices in crystallography.
The tiling mechanism involves translating copies of the hexagonal prism along its axis to stack layers and, within each layer, shifting them according to the two-dimensional hexagonal lattice generated by the base edges, thereby filling space periodically.

Honeycomb tilings

The hexagonal prismatic honeycomb is a uniform space-filling tessellation of three-dimensional Euclidean space composed entirely of congruent regular hexagonal prisms. It arises as a translational tiling, where copies of the hexagonal prism are placed by vectors in three linearly independent directions, achieving a packing density of 1 without gaps or overlaps. This structure is cell-transitive, allowing any prism to be mapped to any other via the lattice symmetries of the tiling.
As part of the broader family of prismatic honeycombs, the hexagonal prismatic honeycomb extends the uniform hexagonal tiling of the plane into the third dimension via extrusion along a perpendicular axis. These prismatic honeycombs share the property of being generated from 2D uniform tilings and can be compounded with other parallelohedra, such as the cube or rhombic dodecahedron, to form more complex space-filling arrangements that maintain density 1.
The vertex figure of the honeycomb is a triangular prism, corresponding to the product of the triangular vertex figure of the base hexagonal tiling and the interval in the extrusion direction.
Representative examples include infinite layered stacks of hexagonal prisms, where successive layers align base-to-base to fill space periodically along the height, or oblique arrangements tilted relative to the lattice vectors for variant orientations.

Applications

In crystallography and nature

In crystallography, the hexagonal prism is a fundamental form within the hexagonal crystal system, characterized by six rectangular lateral faces connecting two parallel hexagonal bases aligned along a six-fold rotation axis. This structure is prevalent in minerals like quartz (SiO), where the hexagonal prism, denoted as the m-face, forms the primary "walls" of the crystal, often combined with pyramidal or pinacoidal terminations to create habits such as elongated prisms. Similarly, ice Ih, the common form of ice in the atmosphere and on Earth's surface, exhibits hexagonal prismatic habits, with crystals growing as columns or plates bounded by basal {0001} planes and prism {10$\bar{1}$0} faces, reflecting the underlying wurtzite-like lattice.
Natural geological formations also approximate hexagonal prisms through physical processes. Basalt columns, such as those at the Giant's Causeway in Northern Ireland, emerge from the cooling and contraction of basaltic lava flows, resulting in parallel fractures that form prismatic joints predominantly hexagonal in cross-section due to the efficient tessellation of hexagons under thermal stress. These columns can reach heights of tens of meters and widths of 30-60 cm, illustrating how contraction-induced cracking favors six-sided geometries over other polygons for minimizing surface energy.
In atmospheric science, snowflakes originate from a hexagonal prism core, as water vapor deposits onto ice nuclei in clouds, initially forming simple prismatic crystals before branching into more complex dendritic structures under supersaturated conditions. This core geometry arises from the hexagonal symmetry of the ice lattice, with prism facets growing parallel to the c-axis; without vapor diffusion and faceting instabilities, all snow crystals would remain as basic hexagonal prisms.
Biological systems leverage hexagonal packing for efficiency, often approximating prismatic arrangements. In plant cells, chloroplasts exhibit near-hexagonal close packing to optimize light capture and space utilization, as modeled by simulations of polydispersed disks that favor hexagonal lattices for maximal density in rectangular cellular confines. Diatoms, unicellular algae, form silica frustules with hexagonal lattice patterns via directional deposition of silica rods, creating porous structures that mimic prismatic packing for structural stability and nutrient exchange, as observed in species like Coscinodiscus wailesii. Honeybee combs provide a striking example, where cells are constructed as hexagonal prisms to maximize storage volume with minimal wax, evolving from initial cylindrical forms that deform into hexagons under physical forces.
Recent advancements in nanomaterials, as of 2025, utilize hexagonal prism templates derived from zeolites to engineer advanced structures. For instance, zeolite-templated carbons with graphene-like frameworks host platinum nanoparticles, enhancing catalytic activity through ordered hexagonal pores that facilitate uniform metal dispersion. In photocatalytic applications, zeolite templates guide the synthesis of hexagonal prism-shaped CuO@CdS/ZnS heterojunctions, improving hydrogen production efficiency by promoting charge separation in the prism's anisotropic geometry. Additionally, binder-free MXene-zeolite electrodes doped with graphene quantum dots leverage hexagonal prism motifs for supercapacitors, achieving high capacitance via enhanced ion accessibility in the templated pores. These developments exploit the space-filling efficiency of hexagonal prisms to create hierarchical nanomaterials with tailored properties.

In architecture and design

Hexagonal prisms have been employed in architecture for their inherent structural stability, derived from the six-sided symmetry that allows even distribution of loads and efficient use of materials in load-bearing elements such as columns and towers. This geometric form provides superior resistance to compression compared to other polygonal bases, making it suitable for vertical supports in both historical and contemporary designs. Architects often leverage this efficiency to create modular systems that minimize waste while maximizing strength, particularly in high-rise or stacked configurations.
In the 20th century, Buckminster Fuller's Dymaxion House exemplified early modular applications of hexagonal prismatic forms, featuring a hexagonal plan supported by a central aluminum mast with suspended walls for lightweight, prefabricated construction. This design, developed in the 1940s, aimed to produce affordable housing through geometric efficiency, with the prism's shape enabling easy assembly and transport. Renaissance-era polyhedral models, including hexagonal prisms, influenced architectural treatises and decorative motifs, as seen in geometric studies by Leonardo da Vinci that informed structural experiments in vaults and domes.
Modern architecture frequently draws on hexagonal prisms for towers and facades, such as the Giant's Causeway Visitor Centre in Northern Ireland, where stone mullions mimic prismatic basalt columns to integrate the building with its landscape. Similarly, Steven Holl Architects' Centre for Creativity at University College Dublin employs stacked hexagonal prisms to evoke geological formations, using the form for vertical circulation and natural light penetration in a sustainable campus expansion. Other notable examples include the hexagonal terminal at Berlin Tegel Airport, which utilized prismatic modules for efficient spatial organization, and Fort Jefferson in Florida, a 19th-century brick fortress with hexagonal bastions for defensive stability.
In product design, hexagonal prisms appear in furniture for their aesthetic appeal and functional modularity, as in the Prism Noir end table, crafted from solid mango wood with a black-stained hexagonal form that serves as both a surface and sculptural element. Modular seating like HexSlash by Medium2 Studio slices hexagonal prisms to create customizable chairs, emphasizing the shape's versatility in contemporary interiors. Packaging and 3D printing templates also adopt this form for prismatic containers that optimize space and stacking, while designs like the Red Dot Award-winning Seclude bed frame an open hexagonal prism around a mattress for lightweight, airy outdoor use.
