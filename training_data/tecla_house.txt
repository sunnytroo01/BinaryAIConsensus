Tecla house
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Tecla house (TECLA: Technology and Clay) is a prototype residential structure 3D-printed entirely from local raw earth, serving as the world's first eco-sustainable housing model constructed using multiple collaborative 3D printers to extrude clay layers in a process that took approximately 200 hours for the primary shell. Developed by Italian firm Mario Cucinella Architects in partnership with WASP (World's Advanced Saving Project), it was erected in Ravenna, Italy, as a proof-of-concept for scalable, low-impact construction that integrates ancient earthen building techniques with modern additive manufacturing. The design prioritizes a circular economy by sourcing recyclable, carbon-neutral materials from nearby soil, enabling rapid assembly with minimal waste and adaptability to diverse climates via bioclimatic principles that enhance passive thermal regulation. This achievement demonstrates the feasibility of decentralized, resource-efficient housing production, potentially reducing reliance on high-energy industrial processes while maintaining structural integrity through over 350 layered depositions of unfired clay.

History and Development

Origins and Collaboration

The Tecla house project originated in 2019 as a collaborative effort between Italian architecture studio Mario Cucinella Architects (MCA) and 3D printing specialist WASP (World's Advanced Saving Project), aimed at developing an eco-sustainable housing prototype using locally sourced raw earth to minimize environmental impact and transport emissions. The initiative sought to revive traditional pottery construction methods through modern additive manufacturing, creating a "0-km" model where materials are excavated, processed, and printed on-site to promote circular economy principles.
MCA, led by architect Mario Cucinella, provided the conceptual design emphasizing organic forms and human-scale habitability, drawing from vernacular architecture to integrate the structure with its natural surroundings in Massa Lombarda, Italy. WASP handled the engineering, development of printing technology, and on-site construction using two large-scale Crane WASP printers operating collaboratively to extrude clay mixtures in a continuous process. The partnership was further supported by Mapei, a construction materials firm that researched and supplied stabilizing additives for the earth-based composites to ensure structural integrity without synthetic reinforcements.
This collaboration marked a milestone in demonstrating scalable, low-carbon 3D-printed habitats adaptable to diverse climates, with the prototype's printing phase completed by early 2021, validating the feasibility of earth as a primary building material in industrialized processes.

Construction Timeline

The Tecla House project received planning approval from local authorities in May 2019, marking the formal start of construction preparations in Massa Lombarda, Italy. The 3D printing of the primary structure, using multiple large-scale printers operating on local clay mixtures, commenced in September 2019 as part of an iterative process involving material refinement and structural testing.
The printing phase progressed intermittently from September 2019 through late 2020, accumulating approximately 200 hours of active extrusion time to form the 60-square-meter double-shell walls and organic layout. This extension beyond the initial target completion in early 2020 reflected challenges in scaling crane-like printers and optimizing earth-based mixtures for seismic stability and insulation. The core printing was declared complete by WASP in January 2021, producing a self-supporting exoskeleton without formwork or transport of prefabricated elements.
Subsequent phases included installation of roofing, doors, windows, and utility systems, leading to full operational completion as a prototype residence in May 2021. The entire timeline, spanning roughly two years, demonstrated the feasibility of on-site additive manufacturing while highlighting logistical hurdles in achieving rapid deployment for larger-scale housing.

Design and Architecture

Structural Design

The Tecla house features a structural design comprising two interconnected, dome-shaped volumes that form an organic, curvilinear envelope inspired by vernacular earthen architecture. This configuration creates a sinuous form where the domes merge via an uninterrupted sine curve, terminating in circular openings for natural light and ventilation, enhancing load distribution and stability through compressive forces typical of vaulted structures.
The primary load-bearing elements are the 3D-printed earthen walls, constructed layer-by-layer using locally sourced clay mixed with soil, water, and rice husks, resulting in a monolithic, self-supporting shell without traditional formwork or reinforcements. Wall thickness varies from 35 to 50 millimeters, with a ribbed outer texture that optimizes material use while providing thermal mass and seismic resilience, as the undulating profile dissipates stress similar to adobe vaults. The structure spans a total floor area of approximately 60 square meters, with the printing process depositing over 350 layers to achieve the full height of the domes.
Construction employed the Crane WASP printer, a modular gantry system with two synchronized arms capable of printing up to 100 square meters simultaneously, enabling parallel fabrication of the dual domes in under 200 hours of active printing time. This approach ensures geometric precision and minimizes joints, contributing to the structure's integrity.
Bioclimatic engineering integrates passive elements, such as the dome curvature for airflow and solar shading, with the earthen material's high thermal inertia to regulate internal temperatures without mechanical systems, though empirical load testing data remains limited to prototype-scale validations.

Functional Features

The Tecla house employs a modular layout comprising two interconnected circular domes, providing a total living area of approximately 60 square meters. One dome accommodates the living room and kitchen, facilitating communal activities, while the adjacent dome houses the bedroom and bathroom, ensuring separation of private and service spaces. This configuration promotes efficient spatial use within a compact footprint, with built-in furnishings designed to conform to the organic, curved walls for seamless integration.
Structurally, the dwelling features a double-shell envelope printed in layers, with the interstitial cavity incorporating a parametric wavy infill that creates dedicated pockets for rice husks as natural insulation and channels for interstitial ventilation. This design leverages the thermal mass of raw earth to regulate indoor temperatures passively, balancing heat retention in cooler conditions and dissipation in warmer ones.
Bioclimatic elements further enhance functionality, including optimized wall geometries for shading and natural airflow, reducing dependence on mechanical heating, cooling, or lighting systems. The prototype's adaptability to local climates stems from these features, which draw on vernacular earth-building techniques to support year-round habitability without active energy inputs beyond basic solar orientation.

Technology and Materials

3D Printing Technology

The Tecla house was constructed using WASP's Crane WASP system, a modular, gantry-mounted 3D printer engineered for large-scale extrusion of raw earthen materials, with a single-unit build envelope of 8.2 meters in diameter and 3.2 meters in height. For Tecla, the build employed two synchronized Crane WASP units with robotic arms operating in tandem, controlled by custom software that optimizes trajectories, prevents collisions, and enables simultaneous multilayer printing across the 60-square-meter structure.
The printing process extruded a printable mixture of locally sourced raw earth through nozzles at controlled rates, depositing 350 layers each 12 mm thick to form the load-bearing walls and curved domes, consuming 60 cubic meters of material over 200 hours of active printing, equivalent to 150 km of filament path and 7,000 lines of G-code. This approach leverages the printer's ability to process unrefined soil without high-energy binders, distinguishing it from concrete-based systems by minimizing thermal processing and enabling on-site material adaptation to local geologies. The technology supports scalability through modular assembly, allowing multiple units to collaborate on complex geometries while maintaining precision in layer adhesion and structural integrity via parametric design algorithms.

Material Sourcing and Composition

The primary material for Tecla house's 3D-printed structure is raw earth, consisting of locally sourced clay and soil extracted from a nearby riverbed in the Ravenna region of Italy, enabling a short supply chain with minimal transportation emissions. This "km 0" approach prioritizes regional aggregates to reduce the carbon footprint associated with material logistics, aligning with the project's emphasis on sustainability.
The composition of the printable mixture includes local raw earth as the base, combined with water and minimal additives including a small amount of binder for cohesion and durability without compromising the material's biodegradability or recyclability. This formulation yields a low-embodied-energy earthen composite suitable for extrusion via large-scale 3D printers.
Sourcing emphasizes non-industrial, renewable inputs; rice husks are derived from local agricultural waste in Italy's Po Valley, further minimizing processing needs and waste generation, and used for thermal insulation infill. The mixture avoids high-cement content typical in conventional concrete, relying instead on the earth's natural binding via drying and minimal additives, which supports claims of near-zero added CO2 during production. Empirical tests on prototypes confirmed the material's thermal mass and hygroscopic qualities, contributing to passive climate regulation in Mediterranean contexts.

Sustainability and Environmental Claims

Asserted Benefits

The Tecla house is asserted by its developers, WASP and Mario Cucinella Architects, to embody a carbon-neutral construction model through the exclusive use of locally sourced raw earth, eliminating long-distance material transport and thereby minimizing associated emissions. This "0-km" sourcing approach draws from nearby terrain, ensuring the structure's primary materialcomprising over 60 cubic meters of clay-based mixturehas a low embodied carbon footprint compared to industrially produced alternatives like concrete or steel.
The 3D printing process is claimed to drastically cut waste, with precise layer-by-layer deposition (350 layers totaling 4.2 meters in height for the dual-dome structure) avoiding the scraps typical of traditional masonry or prefabrication methods, resulting in a nearly zero-waste production phase. Developers highlight the project's recyclability, positioning Tecla as a prototype for circular housing where the earthen shell can be disassembled and reintegrated into the local soil without environmental degradation.
Further benefits include the material's inherent thermal mass and insulation properties, derived from the clay's natural composition, which proponents say enables passive climate adaptation and reduced energy demands for interior climate control in diverse environments. The off-grid compatible design, incorporating rainwater harvesting and solar potential via its geodesic dome form, is touted to support low operational emissions post-construction.

Empirical Verification and Metrics

Project proponents assert that Tecla's construction achieves a low carbon footprint through the use of a printable mixture primarily comprising local raw earth supplemented by lime and natural fibers, sourced from the immediate vicinity of the build site in Ravenna, Italy, thereby minimizing embodied emissions from material transport. The additive 3D printing process, utilizing two Crane WASP printers over 200 hours for the walls, is claimed to generate near-zero construction waste, contrasting with traditional masonry's typical 10-15% material loss. However, these benefits remain largely qualitative assertions from the collaborating entities (WASP and Mario Cucinella Architects), without published independent life-cycle assessments (LCAs) quantifying total CO2 equivalents or comparing against benchmarks like conventional adobe or concrete structures.
No empirical post-occupancy data on energy performance, such as measured heating/cooling loads or thermal transmittance values, has been released for Tecla, despite its bioclimatic design incorporating passive features like oriented glazing and earthen thermal mass for natural regulation in Mediterranean climates. Analogous LCAs for similar earth-based 3D-printed prototypes (e.g., the TOVA structure in Spain) indicate potential global warming potential reductions of 20-50% over standard construction phases due to localized sourcing and optimized layering, but these do not directly apply to Tecla and rely on modeled rather than measured inputs. The project's sustainability report references general building sector LCA methodologies but provides no Tecla-specific calculations, such as cradle-to-gate emissions or end-of-life recyclability metrics.
Verification challenges stem from the prototype's scale and the nascent state of large-format earth printing; while process efficiencies suggest environmental gainse.g., energy for printing estimated at under 1 kWh per cubic meter of material based on printer specificationslacking standardized testing (e.g., ISO 14040-compliant LCA) precludes robust claims of "nearly zero-emission" status. Independent audits or longitudinal monitoring would be required to substantiate durability-related metrics, such as long-term material degradation under weathering, which could affect lifecycle sustainability. Overall, empirical evidence supports reduced upfront impacts from material locality and waste minimization, but comprehensive metrics confirming superior environmental performance over alternatives are absent.

Reception and Criticisms

Achievements and Praise

The Tecla house achieved pioneering status as the first residential prototype fully 3D-printed using a mixture of locally sourced raw earth, rice husks, and minimal binders (less than 5% of the mixture), demonstrating viability for natural material extrusion at scale. Completed in April 2021 near Ravenna, Italy, by Mario Cucinella Architects in collaboration with WASP, the 60-square-meter structure was erected in under 200 hours using two crane-mounted printers operating in tandem to deposit clay layers collaboratively. This marked a technical breakthrough in multi-printer synchronization for earthen construction, reducing labor and enabling complex organic forms inspired by vernacular architecture.
Project leads hailed Tecla as a proof-of-concept for low-impact housing, with WASP describing it as "the peak of advanced research between matter and technology" and an "unparalleled challenge" in achieving full printing with raw clay for habitable spaces. Architect Mario Cucinella emphasized its integration of bioclimatic design and local materials to minimize environmental footprint, positioning it as a model for replicable, zero-kilometer homes adaptable to diverse climates. Media coverage praised its innovation; It's Nice That quoted collaborator Michele Moretti noting that Tecla proves "a beautiful, healthy, and sustainable home can be built by a machine," while CNN highlighted its potential as a blueprint for affordable, eco-friendly dwellings in resource-scarce areas. 

Challenges and Skepticism

Despite being presented as Italy's first 3D-printed residential structure in 2021, the Tecla house has faced scrutiny over its long-term structural integrity, particularly given the seismic risks in its location near Ravenna, where clay-based walls lack traditional reinforcements like beams or columns. Engineering analyses of similar WASP-printed earthen structures, such as the Gaia prototype, highlight vulnerabilities to earthquakes and high winds, with recommendations for added stabilizing elements to prevent collapse under lateral forces. Tecla's vaulted domes, while innovative, rely on the compressive strength of raw clay mixtures, which historical data on adobe construction shows can degrade through cracking or erosion without chemical stabilizers, raising doubts about durability in humid or variable climates beyond controlled prototypes.
Scalability remains a core challenge, as Tecla's construction required two coordinated 3D printers operating for approximately 200 hours to extrude 60 cubic meters of material, limiting throughput compared to conventional building methods. Critics argue that while the project demonstrates feasibility for bespoke, low-volume eco-habitats, adapting the process for mass production encounters hurdles in material consistency, printer logistics, and regulatory approvals, with no widespread adoption evidenced three years post-completion. Broader assessments of 3D-printed housing note a lack of standardization, potentially inflating costs for non-prototype builds and failing to address socioeconomic barriers like land access and zoning, rendering Tecla more a high-end demonstration than a scalable solution for global housing shortages.
Skepticism also surrounds unverified environmental assertions, such as zero-kilometer material sourcing reducing embodied carbon, as independent metrics on Tecla's full lifecycle emissionsincluding printer energy use and transport of non-local components like photovoltaic panelsremain limited. While proponents claim biodegradability of the clay shell, real-world testing for thermal performance and moisture resistance is pending, with early-stage prototypes like Tecla requiring further empirical validation before claims of superior sustainability can be substantiated over traditional low-impact builds. Academic reviews of 3D-printed earthen projects, including Tecla, emphasize that while additive manufacturing minimizes waste during printing, overall ecological benefits depend on site-specific factors often overlooked in promotional narratives.

Potential Impact and Future Prospects

Scalability and Applications

The TECLA prototype demonstrates potential scalability through the deployment of multiple synchronized 3D printing cranes, enabling the construction of a 60-square-meter structure in approximately 200 hours with an average energy consumption of 6 kilowatts, significantly reducing labor and material waste compared to traditional methods. This multi-arm approach, developed by WASP, allows for collaborative printing that expands printable volume beyond single-extruder limitations, positioning it as a step toward larger-scale earthen habitats. However, scalability remains constrained by the need for site-specific soil testing and processing, as the mixturecomprising 70% local clay, sand, and strawmust achieve precise moisture and stability parameters to prevent structural failure, limiting rapid replication without customized equipment.
Applications of TECLA's technology extend to low-cost, off-grid housing in resource-scarce regions, leveraging locally sourced earth to minimize transport emissions and costs, with the prototype's bioclimatic design incorporating passive cooling via thick earthen walls that regulate internal temperatures without mechanical systems. Proponents envision modular expansions for community-scale settlements, such as printing additional units or utility structures using the same Crane WASP printers, potentially addressing housing shortages in rural or disaster-prone areas by enabling on-site fabrication with minimal skilled labor. Yet, real-world deployment has been limited to prototypes, with no verified large-scale projects as of 2023, underscoring the technology's experimental status and dependence on further validation for seismic resilience and long-term durability in diverse climates.

Ongoing Developments and Limitations

Since its completion in 2021 as a 60-square-meter prototype in Massa Lombarda, Italy, the Tecla project has remained in an experimental phase, with WASP and Mario Cucinella Architects conducting ongoing structural and thermal performance evaluations to assess long-term viability before broader replication. No full-scale commercial deployments of Tecla-inspired homes have been reported as of 2024, though WASP continues advancing related clay-based 3D printing technologies, including demonstrations at events like Formnext 2024 focused on sustainable extrusion methods. These efforts emphasize modular adaptations for diverse climates, but progress is incremental, tied to iterative printer refinements rather than widespread adoption.
Key limitations include unproven durability against environmental stressors, such as seismic activity in regions like Italy, where clay structures require extensive reinforcement to meet building codesTecla's design incorporates internal trusses, yet lacks multi-year field data confirming resilience. Thermal efficiency claims rely on local earth insulation, but independent verification of energy performance in varying conditions remains pending, with prototypes like Tecla still undergoing testing that could reveal gaps in insulation or moisture resistance. Scalability challenges persist, as the construction process, while reducing waste through on-site printing, demands specialized equipment and skilled operators, limiting cost reductions without automation breakthroughs; critics note that such prototypes often cater to niche demonstrations rather than addressing mass housing shortages. Additionally, while the clay mixture is biodegradable, integration of non-earth elements like windows and doors introduces hybrid material complexities, potentially undermining full recyclability assertions without standardized lifecycle assessments.
