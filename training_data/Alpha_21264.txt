 The Alpha 21264, codenamed EV6, is a 64-bit reduced instruction set computing (RISC) microprocessor developed by Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC) and launched by Compaq Computer Corporation in the fourth quarter of 1998. It implements the Alpha instruction set architecture in a superscalar design with out-of-order and speculative execution, featuring a seven-stage integer pipeline and dedicated floating-point units, on-chip 64 KB two-way set-associative instruction and data caches, and clock frequencies of 500 to 600 MHz. Fabricated using a 0.35 m complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) process with approximately 15 million transistors on a die size of about 3 cm, the processor achieved pioneering performance levels, including SPECint95 scores exceeding 30 and SPECfp95 scores over 58 at 600 MHz, while sustaining over 2.4 billion instructions per second.
As the third generation in the Alpha microprocessor familysucceeding the 21064 (EV4) and 21164 (EV5)the 21264 introduced advanced microarchitectural innovations to maximize instruction-level parallelism, including register renaming for up to 80 in-flight instructions, a hierarchical tournament branch predictor with 90100% accuracy, and a quad-issue fetch unit capable of dispatching up to four instructions per cycle to four integer execution units and two floating-point units. Its memory hierarchy emphasized bandwidth, supporting 128-entry instruction and data translation buffers, 32 outstanding loads and stores, and peak data rates of 6.4 GB/s to the on-chip caches, 54 GB/s to an external secondary (L2) cache via a 128-bit wide interface, and over 1.3 GB/s to main memory in STREAM benchmarks. Packaged in a 587-pin pin grid array (PGA) with error-correcting code (ECC) support on a 64-bit system bus, it enabled high-end workstations and servers such as the Compaq AlphaServer DS20 and XP1000, running operating systems like Windows NT, Tru64 UNIX, and Linux through customizable processor abstraction layer (PALcode).
Following DEC's acquisition by Compaq in 1998, the 21264 was produced by licensees including Samsung Electronics and Mitsubishi Electric, with full volume production reaching 600 MHz variants by early 1999 and plans for shrinks to 0.25 m and 0.18 m processes. It represented a pinnacle of Alpha performance before Compaq's decision to phase out the architecture in favor of x86 by 2004, influencing subsequent RISC designs through its emphasis on aggressive out-of-order execution and memory bandwidth optimization.

Overview

Design and release

The Alpha 21264 microprocessor, codenamed EV6, was developed by Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC) in the mid-1990s as the successor to the Alpha 21164, aiming to advance high-performance reduced instruction set computing (RISC) for demanding applications. The project began around 1995, building on the Alpha architecture's foundation established in 1992, with early prototypes discussed publicly at the Microprocessor Forum in October 1996.
The design effort was led by Richard E. Kessler, with significant contributions from engineers including E. J. McLellan and D. A. Webb, focusing on achieving industry-leading clock frequencies and overall system performance to power advanced workstations and servers. Key goals included maintaining binary compatibility with the core Alpha instruction set architecture (ISA) while pushing boundaries in frequency and efficiency to compete in enterprise computing environments.
DEC officially announced the Alpha 21264 in 1996, with the microprocessor launching on October 19, 1998, initially available at clock speeds of 600 MHz. This release occurred shortly after Compaq Computer Corporation acquired DEC in June 1998 for $9.6 billion, marking a pivotal transition that integrated the Alpha program into Compaq's portfolio.
Following the acquisition, Compaq licensed Alpha 21264 production to third parties, including Samsung Electronics in 1996 and IBM Microelectronics, to expand manufacturing capacity and support broader adoption. Initial shipments began in the fourth quarter of 1998, with full production ramping up through a joint venture, Alpha Processor Inc., involving Compaq and Samsung, positioning the chip as a cornerstone for high-end systems running Unix and OpenVMS operating systems in scientific, engineering, and enterprise markets.

Key specifications

The Alpha 21264 microprocessor features 15.2 million transistors fabricated on a 0.35 m CMOS process with six layers of metal, resulting in a die size of 313.96 mm. It employs a four-issue superscalar design with a seven-stage pipeline, achieving a peak execution rate of six instructions per cycle (four integer and two floating-point) and a sustained rate of four instructions per cycle through out-of-order execution.
The processor includes 64 KB on-chip instruction cache (I-cache), which is virtually addressed and two-way set associative, and a 64 KB on-chip data cache (D-cache), which is virtually indexed, physically tagged, two-way set associative, and dual-read-ported to support multiple concurrent accesses. It supports an external backing cache (B-cache) of up to 16 MB connected via a 128-bit bus, providing high-bandwidth access to second-level storage.
The external interface utilizes a 64-bit double-data-rate (DDR) data bus operating under the EV6 protocol for system communication, enabling efficient data transfer between the processor, memory, and peripherals. Power consumption is approximately 70-80 W when running at 600 MHz, with the chip packaged in a 587-pin ceramic interstitial pin grid array (IPGA) for integration into workstations and servers. The Alpha 21264 is fully compatible with the Alpha instruction set architecture (ISA), supporting 64-bit virtual and physical addressing for robust memory management in 64-bit computing environments.

Microarchitecture

Out-of-order execution

The Alpha 21264 implements out-of-order execution to maximize instruction throughput by dynamically scheduling instructions based on data dependencies rather than program order, enabling the processor to tolerate latency from functional units and memory accesses. This mechanism relies on a combination of register renaming, issue queues, and speculative execution to handle up to 80 instructions in flight simultaneously, allowing non-dependent instructions to proceed while stalled ones wait.
Central to the design are the integer and floating-point issue queues, which serve as dynamic schedulers: a 20-entry integer queue (Iqueue) and a 15-entry floating-point queue (Fqueue) hold dispatched instructions awaiting operands. These queues support issuing up to four integer and two floating-point instructions per cycle from ready entries, selected via wakeup logic that broadcasts operand availability from completed operations and select logic that prioritizes older instructions to maintain fairness and avoid starvation. The Ebox and Fbox execute these renamed instructions from the queues, integrating the scheduling framework with the processor's execution pipelines.
Register renaming eliminates false dependencies by mapping the 32 architectural integer and 32 architectural floating-point registers to larger physical files: an 80-entry integer register file, with duplicate copies in two clusters for parallelism, and a 72-entry floating-point register file. This renaming occurs during dispatch using a mapper that assigns physical registers and checkpoints the mapping state in a "map silo" for recovery, supporting speculative execution where instructions proceed under predicted control flow. On mispredictions or exceptions, the checkpointed map is restored via content-addressable memory (CAM) search, flushing incorrect speculative results and resuming from the correct path without architectural state corruption.
The reorder buffer, with capacity for 80 in-flight instructions, ensures in-order retirement by tracking completion status and committing results only after all prior instructions have finished, preserving the illusion of sequential execution. This buffering, combined with the issue queues' dynamic arbitration, allows the Alpha 21264 to achieve high instruction-level parallelism, with the integer queue's bottom-to-top scanning for ready operands exemplifying the select logic's efficiency in handling variable latencies.

Integer unit (Ebox)

The integer unit, known as the Ebox, in the Alpha 21264 microprocessor handles all integer arithmetic, logical operations, shifts, and memory address calculations, enabling high-throughput execution of up to four integer instructions per cycle. It dispatches instructions from the out-of-order queues to its dedicated hardware resources, focusing on efficient processing of load, store, and computational tasks. The Ebox is organized into two symmetric execution clusters, labeled U0 and U1, to maximize parallelism while minimizing resource contention.
Each cluster in the Ebox contains an arithmetic logic unit (ALU) for add, subtract, and logical operations; a shifter for variable and fixed shifts; and a multiplier for integer multiplication, with the multiplier in U1 being fully pipelined to support ongoing operations without stalling the pipeline. The clusters are subdivided into upper and lower sections: the lower sections include logic units and general-purpose adders primarily for address generation in load/store operations, while the upper sections house additional logic, adders, shifters, and the multiplier or multiply-validate-immediate (MVI) unit. This clustered design allows for balanced distribution of workloads, with cross-cluster result forwarding incurring a one-cycle penalty to maintain coherence.
The Ebox features two dedicated load/store units, integrated within the clusters' lower subclusters, which generate effective virtual addresses and manage data movement for memory references. These units support up to two memory operations per cycle, handling address calculations via the adders and ensuring precise data handling without overlapping with other execution resources.
Central to the Ebox is an 80-entry integer register file, replicated across the two clusters for parallel access, consisting of 32 architectural registers, 40 rename registers to support out-of-order execution, and 8 PAL shadow registers for privileged architecture library operations. The register file provides four read ports and six write ports per cluster, enabling rapid operand fetching and result storage while broadcasting up to 24 results via content-addressable memory (CAM) structures for bypassing.
To enhance addressing efficiency, the Ebox supports multiply-validate-immediate (MVI) instructions, such as MAXSB8, MINUB8, and PKWB, which combine multiplication with validation checks for immediate operands, indicated by AMASK bit 8 in the architecture. These instructions are executed in the dedicated MVI unit within the upper subcluster, optimizing common patterns in data processing and motion video applications.
Most integer operations in the Ebox exhibit a three-cycle latency, including loads on data cache hits and specialized instructions like count leading zeros (CTLZ) and count trailing zeros (CTPOP), though simpler ALU operations like adds and logics can complete in one cycle with potential cross-cluster delays. Multiplications incur a seven-cycle latency due to the pipelined multiplier.
The load/store units integrate with a 32-entry load queue (LQ) and a 32-entry store queue (SQ) to enable non-blocking memory operations, allowing up to 64 in-flight references while enforcing ordering through replay traps and translation buffer coordination. This queue structure ensures that dependent loads can proceed without stalling the integer pipeline, contributing to the overall superscalar performance of the Alpha 21264.

Floating-point unit (Fbox)

The floating-point unit (Fbox) in the Alpha 21264 microprocessor provides dedicated hardware for executing floating-point instructions, enabling high-performance scientific and graphics workloads. It consists of two pipelines: one for multiplication operations and the other for addition and subtraction, with the latter also handling division and square root computations. These pipelines are fully integrated into the processor's out-of-order execution framework, allowing speculative dispatch and dynamic scheduling of floating-point instructions alongside integer operations.
The Fbox features a 72-entry floating-point register file, comprising 32 architectural registers visible to the programmer and 40 additional rename registers to support speculative execution and reduce register pressure during out-of-order processing. This design facilitates efficient handling of dependencies without stalling the pipeline for register availability. The register file supports both single-precision and double-precision formats, with all operations adhering to the IEEE 754 standard for precision, rounding, exceptions, NaNs, and infinities. Additionally, the unit includes support for fused multiply-add (FMA) instructions, which combine multiplication and addition in a single operation to improve accuracy and throughput in iterative algorithms.
Operation latencies are tuned for balanced performance: addition, subtraction, and multiplication each require 4 cycles, enabling rapid execution of common floating-point computations. Division incurs a 16-cycle latency, while square root takes 33 cycles, reflecting the computational intensity of these reciprocal operations. The divide and square root pipeline is non-pipelined, processing one instruction at a time to maintain precision, but includes early retirement mechanisms for certain operand patterns to reduce effective latency.
At peak, the Fbox can sustain 4 floating-point operations per cycleachieved by issuing two instructions simultaneously, such as an add and a multiply, or leveraging FMAthrough a 15-entry issue queue that prioritizes ready instructions for dispatch. This capability, combined with the processor's 6-issue width, positions the Alpha 21264 as a strong performer in floating-point-intensive applications, though actual throughput depends on data dependencies and instruction mix.

Branch prediction

The Alpha 21264 implements a tournament branch predictor that combines a local predictor, focusing on per-branch history, with a global predictor, capturing program-wide branch correlations, to minimize control hazards and maximize instruction-level parallelism. The local predictor employs a two-level adaptive structure: a 1,024-entry local history table stores 10-bit shift registers representing the recent outcomes of each branch, indexed by 10 bits of the branch's virtual program counter (VPC), while a secondary 1,024-entry pattern history table uses 3-bit saturating counters to predict direction based on the history pattern. The global predictor consists of a 4,096-entry table of 2-bit saturating counters, indexed by a 12-bit global history register (GHR) that tracks the outcomes of the last 12 branches in the program. A separate 4,096-entry choice predictor, also using 2-bit saturating counters indexed by the GHR, dynamically selects the output from either the local or global predictor based on their relative historical accuracy for the current context.
Complementing the direction predictor, the branch target buffer (BTB) is a 2,048-entry direct-mapped array that stores target addresses, validity bits, and direction prediction bits for conditional branches, enabling rapid resolution of taken branches. The BTB is probed in parallel with the instruction cache using the VPC of a fetched instruction; on a hit for a predicted taken branch, the target VPC is immediately used to redirect fetch, supporting up to one branch resolution per cycle. This structure covers most forward branches and some backward ones, with untaken branches predicted to fall through without BTB intervention.
A mispredicted branch typically incurs an 11-cycle penalty on average, accounting for pipeline flush, history restoration, and refetch from the correct target, though the minimum penalty is 7 cycles for straightforward cases without additional dependencies. This penalty arises because the predictor operates early in the 7-stage pipeline, allowing speculative work to be discarded efficiently but still requiring recovery time.
The branch prediction hardware integrates seamlessly with speculative execution by speculatively updating the GHR and local histories during fetch, while maintaining backup checkpoints in a history silo for rapid restoration on mispredictions, enabling early resolution and continued speculation up to 80 instructions in flight. This design yields prediction accuracies ranging from 90% to over 95% on standard integer and floating-point benchmarks, significantly reducing control stalls.

Memory system

Caches

The Alpha 21264 features separate primary instruction and data caches, each 64 KB in size and organized as two-way set-associative with 64-byte cache lines. The instruction cache (I-cache) employs a virtually addressed indexing scheme and incorporates line and way prediction mechanisms to enhance access speed, achieving prediction accuracies between 85% and 100% while prefetching up to four lines ahead. The data cache (D-cache) is virtually indexed and physically tagged, supporting dual-ported access to enable two independent load or store operations per cycle through double-pumped operation at twice the processor clock frequency. Both caches implement a write-back policy, with the D-cache using read/write allocation to maintain coherence and efficiency during evictions.
These primary caches are designed as non-blocking structures, permitting multiple outstanding misses to tolerate latency from lower levels in the hierarchy; the processor can track up to eight cache misses simultaneously, allowing continued execution of independent instructions while misses are resolved. To manage dirty lines during evictions, the system includes victim buffers: an 8-entry victim data buffer for the D-cache holds displaced blocks pending write-back, and similar buffering supports I-cache operations and probe responses. This buffering reduces contention and improves overall memory subsystem throughput by staging writes without stalling the pipeline.
The external backup cache (B-cache), serving as the unified second-level cache, ranges from 1 MB to 16 MB and is implemented as direct-mapped using synchronous SRAM (SSRAM) components. It connects to the processor via a dedicated 128-bit wide bidirectional data bus, operating at the processor clock frequency (up to 500 MHz in initial implementations) for peak bandwidths exceeding 6 GB/s to support high-performance workloads. In multiprocessor configurations, the B-cache integrates with the EV6 system protocol to maintain coherence, employing a write-invalidate mechanism that supports MOESI (Modified, Owned, Exclusive, Shared, Invalid) states through duplicate tag arrays and an 8-entry probe queue for snooping operations. This design ensures consistent data visibility across nodes while minimizing latency for shared-memory access.

Memory addressing

The Alpha 21264 microprocessor implements a 48-bit virtual address space, providing access to 256 terabytes (TiB) of virtual memory, with the option to configure a 43-bit canonical addressing mode for user space that limits it to 8 TiB. This configurability is controlled via internal processor registers such as VA_CTL or I_CTL, where the VA_48 bit determines the address width, and higher bits are sign-extended accordingly for compatibility and efficiency in different operating environments.
Virtual addresses are translated to a 44-bit physical address space, supporting up to 16 TiB of physical memory, divided equally between memory (PA=0) and I/O (PA=1) regions. This translation occurs through the processor's memory management unit (MMU), which relies on page table lookups managed by privileged architecture library (PALcode) routines during translation buffer misses. The instruction translation buffer (ITB) and data translation buffer (DTB) are each 128-entry fully associative structures supporting round-robin replacement. The physical address bits are protected with error-correcting code (ECC) or parity for reliability in data transfers.
The page table structure is a software-defined, multi-level hierarchy, but typically implemented as two levels for process virtual address spaces, using a virtual page table base (VPTB) register to initiate fetches for page table entries (PTEs). For standard 8 KB pages (2^13 bytes), the virtual page number (VPN) excludes the 13-bit page offset, with indexing into the second-level page table using portions of the VPN bits to locate the appropriate PTE, which maps to the physical page frame. Support for 64 KB pages (2^16 bytes) is provided by adjusting the offset bits, allowing coarser granularity in translations. PTEs are 64 bits wide, containing the physical page number, protection attributes, and validity information.
To alleviate translation lookaside buffer (TLB) pressure in memory-intensive workloads, the Alpha 21264 supports superpages through hardware TLB entries that map larger contiguous regions, including 64 KB, 512 KB, and 4 MB sizes, implemented by grouping multiple 8 KB base pages and using specific bit patterns in the virtual address (e.g., VA=2 for 64 KB). These superpage mappings are enabled via the M_CTL register's SPE field and indicated in PTEs with granularity hints (GH bits), reducing the number of TLB entries needed for large allocated blocks and improving address translation efficiency. While the hardware directly handles up to 4 MB superpages, the architecture permits OS-managed larger superpages (such as 8 MB or 64 MB) via PTE replication across multiple base pages, though this relies on software for fault handling.
Byte ordering in the Alpha 21264 follows the little-endian convention as the native and default mode of the Alpha architecture, where the least significant byte of a multi-byte datum is stored at the lowest address. Big-endian ordering is optionally supported through the B_ENDIAN bit in the VA_CTL register, which inverts the lower address bits based on the access size (byte, word, longword, or quadword), allowing software-configurable endianness for compatibility with diverse data formats without hardware reconfiguration. This flexibility integrates with the load/store units in the Mbox for handling memory operations.

External interface

The Alpha 21264 employs a 64-bit double data rate (DDR) system bus, designated as the EV6 interface, with configurable system bus frequencies (typically 100200 MHz effective), delivering peak bandwidths of 1.6 GB/s or higher depending on the clock ratio and system implementation. This bus facilitates high-speed communication with off-chip memory and I/O subsystems through a bidirectional data path (SysData_L) and unidirectional time-multiplexed address/control lines (SysAddIn_L and SysAddOut_L), incorporating error-correcting code (ECC) via SysCheck_L for data integrity. The EV6 protocol, originally developed by Digital Equipment Corporation and later licensed for broader use, enables split transactionsallowing multiple outstanding requests without blockingand maintains cache coherency through command-based probes and responses.
The processor is packaged in a 587-pin ceramic interstitial pin grid array (IPGA), with pins categorized into clock, power, and signal groups to support the external interface. Clock signals include differential inputs such as ClkIn_H/L for core timing derivation and FrameClk_H/L for system bus synchronization, ensuring phase alignment via an on-chip phase-locked loop (PLL). Power distribution comprises 94 VDD pins at 2.0 V (with one dedicated PLL_VDD at 2.5 V) and 104 VSS ground pins, alongside reference voltages like SysVref for signal integrity. Signal groups primarily encompass the EV6 bus lines for data, address, and control, with additional miscellaneous pins for interrupts (IRQ_H) and reset (Reset_L). Physical implementation

Fabrication

The Alpha 21264 microprocessor was fabricated using a 0.35 m complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) process with six levels of metal interconnect. This process technology enabled high clock frequencies up to 600 MHz while integrating complex out-of-order execution logic. The initial production occurred at Digital Equipment Corporation's (DEC) Hudson fabrication facility in Massachusetts, with tape-out delays pushing volume manufacturing to the second half of 1998.
The die measured 3.1 cm and contained approximately 15.2 million transistors, including significant cache structures and execution units. The large die size contributed to challenges in achieving high yields during the early production phase, though specific yield figures were not publicly detailed; ramp-up proceeded through 1999 as systems entered the market. The processor operated on a 2.2 V core power supply, with 3.3 V for input/output interfaces to support compatibility with external components.
Following Compaq's acquisition of DEC in June 1998, fabrication transitioned to external foundries to scale production, including partnerships with Samsung Electronics for subsequent runs and Intel at the acquired Hudson facility. This shift ensured continued availability of the Alpha 21264 beyond DEC's internal capacity, supporting its integration into Compaq's AlphaServer systems starting in late 1998.

Packaging

The Alpha 21264 microprocessor was primarily packaged in a 587-pin ceramic pin grid array (PGA) using flip-chip bonding technology, which facilitated efficient heat dissipation and electrical connectivity for its high-performance requirements. This packaging included 388 functional signal pins, 104 ground (VSS) pins, 94 power (VDD) pins, and one dedicated 3.3-V pin for the phase-locked loop (PLL). The flip-chip approach connected the die directly to the package substrate via solder bumps, enabling a compact 59.94 mm 59.94 mm footprint while supporting the processor's 3.1 cm die size.
Thermal management was critical due to the processor's power dissipation, rated at approximately 7080 W thermal design power (TDP) for 600 MHz operation at 2.2 V, though maximum power could reach up to 95109 W under peak loads. The package supported removable, application-specific heat sinks of varying types (e.g., Type 1: 80.5 mm 80.5 mm 32.5 mm), with cooling requirements specifying linear airflow of 4001000 ft/min to maintain maximum ambient temperatures (Ta) below 5570C at the heat sink center, depending on the sink type and frequency. These designs ensured reliable operation by preventing junction temperatures from exceeding 100C.
An alternative packaging option was developed by Alpha Processor Inc. (API), which offered the Alpha 21264 in a Slot B module format for compatibility with Intel-like socket systems. This single-edge connector cartridge (SECC) module measured 110 mm 152 mm and integrated the processor core, up to 8 MB of SRAM for secondary caching, and an onboard voltage regulator, enabling easier integration into non-proprietary motherboards like API's UP2000.
Pin assignments were optimized for power integrity and signal bandwidth, with VDD pins distributed across the grid (e.g., locations B2, V4, AP6) to supply the core at 2.12.3 V and the PLL at 3.1353.465 V, while VSS pins (e.g., A1, B1, C1) provided grounding. Signal pins were allocated for key interfaces, including 128 pins for Bcache data (BcData_H), 64 pins for system data (SysData_L), and control lines like BcDataOE_L and SysCheck_L. This layout supported high-bandwidth transfers while minimizing inductance.
Reliability features included error-correcting code (ECC) support on critical buses, such as the 64-bit system data bus with 8 dedicated ECC check bits (SysCheck_L) for single-bit error correction and double-bit detection, and the 144-bit Bcache interface with 16 check bits (BcCheck_H) for similar protection on 64-byte blocks. Parity protection was also implemented on cache tags and internal arrays, with error reporting via registers like DC_STAT to enable scrubbing and machine checks (MCHK) for uncorrectable errors, enhancing overall system robustness.

Variants

Alpha 21264A (EV67)

The Alpha 21264A, codenamed EV67, was the first derivative of the Alpha 21264 microprocessor, serving as a refined implementation with a focus on manufacturing efficiency and minor functional enhancements. Released in late 1999, it employed a shrunken 0.25 m CMOS process technology, reducing the die size to 210 mm compared to the original's larger footprint. Clock speeds ranged from 600 MHz to 833 MHz across its variants, enabling higher performance in compatible systems while preserving the core out-of-order execution capabilities of the base microarchitecture. The EV67 maintained full compatibility with the EV6 bus interface for seamless integration into existing Alpha platforms.
A key addition in the EV67 was support for Cache Initialization (CIX) instructions, including operations like count leading zeros (CTLZ) and maximum signed byte (MAXSB8), designed to expedite cache setup during system boot processes. These extensions addressed initialization bottlenecks in multiprocessor environments, allowing for faster startup without altering the fundamental pipeline or execution units. Power dissipation scaled with frequency, reaching 73 W at 600 MHz and 90 W at 750 MHz under typical loads.
Production of the EV67 was managed by Compaq Computer Corporation, with fabrication outsourced to Samsung Electronics using their CMOS7 process. The processor powered Compaq's AlphaServer DS20 workstations, which targeted high-end scientific and engineering workloads with up to dual-processor configurations and integrated Tsunami chipsets.

Alpha 21264B (EV68A/C)

The Alpha 21264B variants, designated EV68A and EV68C, represented a process shrink to 0.18 m CMOS, enabling higher clock frequencies while retaining the out-of-order execution core architecture of the original 21264 design.
The EV68A, fabricated by Samsung Electronics using aluminum interconnects, featured a die size of approximately 125 mm and operated at clock speeds ranging from 750 MHz to 940 MHz, with power consumption between 60 W and 75 W at 1.7 V. Released in 2000, it included minor optimizations in clock distribution for improved timing margins and basic power management features to support Energy Star compliance in host systems. These processors were deployed in Compaq AlphaServer ES40 multiprocessor systems for enterprise workloads.
The EV68C variant, produced by IBM, introduced copper interconnects to enhance signal integrity and reduce resistance at higher frequencies, achieving a clock speed of 1.25 GHz with power consumption around 75 W. Samples were available in early 2000, and it powered systems like the Compaq AlphaServer DS20 for technical computing applications. Compaq oversaw production of the 21264B family until 2001, after which manufacturing transitioned amid the company's acquisition by HP.

Alpha 21264C (EV68CB)

The Alpha 21264C, codenamed EV68CB, represents a power-optimized evolution of the Alpha 21264 microprocessor, fabricated on a 0.18 m CMOS process with a die size of 120 mm. This variant maintained the core architecture of its predecessors while focusing on efficiency improvements for server applications.
Released in June 2001 by Compaq Computer Corporation, the 21264C supported clock speeds from 1.0 GHz to 1.33 GHz, with a thermal design power (TDP) ranging from 64 W at 1.0 GHz to 80 W at 1.33 GHz. It featured refined voltage regulation, operating at a 1.7 V core supply and 2.5 V for the phase-locked loop (PLL), which contributed to better power management compared to earlier models.
The processor incorporated an enhanced thermal design optimized for dense server deployments, achieving operating temperatures at the heat sink center of 54.6C at 1.0 GHz, 46.8C at 1.25 GHz, and 43.2C at 1.33 GHz under typical loads. This design, combined with sleep mode power consumption below 30 W for Energy Star compliance, enabled reliable performance in multi-processor configurations.
The 21264C was primarily deployed in Compaq's AlphaServer GS series, such as the GS160 and GS320 models, where it powered high-availability enterprise servers supporting up to 32 processors in clustered environments. It retained the EV6 bus protocol from the base 21264 design for system interfacing.
As the final major production variant of the Alpha 21264 family before the broader phase-out of the Alpha architecture in 20032004, the EV68CB marked Compaq's concluding high-volume effort in this lineage.

Alpha 21264D (EV68CD)

The Alpha 21264D, code-named EV68CD, is a faster derivative of the Alpha 21264 microprocessor fabricated by IBM under a licensing agreement with Compaq. This variant was produced in limited volumes around 20012002 and integrated into server offerings following the licensing deal. It inherited the core architecture of the original Alpha 21264, with minor die revisions focused on higher yields and clock stability. Fabricated on a 0.18 m CMOS process, it targeted clock speeds of 1.41.5 GHz and dissipated around 85 W, necessitating enhanced cooling.

Alpha 21264E (EV68E)

The Alpha 21264E, also known as EV68E, was a planned derivative of the Alpha 21264 microprocessor developed by Samsung Electronics in collaboration with Compaq Computer Corporation. Announced on October 10, 2000, at the Microprocessor Forum, it was intended as a high-performance evolution featuring significant architectural enhancements to the cache subsystem. The design aimed to integrate the L2 cache directly on the die, revising the multilevel cache hierarchy for improved bandwidth and reduced latency compared to prior off-chip configurations.
Fabricated on a 0.18 m seven-layer copper CMOS process with a 0.09 m gate length, the EV68E targeted clock speeds of 1.25 GHz, with potential scalability to 1.5 GHz using silicon-on-insulator (SOI) technology in future iterations. Key specifications included a 64 KB instruction cache and 64 KB data cache at L1, alongside a 1.85 MB 14-way set-associative L2 cache operating in double data rate mode at 417 MHz, delivering over 13 GB/s of internal bandwidth. The die measured 16.5 mm 18.0 mm with approximately 140 million transistors, operating at a nominal voltage of 1.65 V and consuming around 70 W at 85C. It maintained a six-way superscalar execution core with four integer pipelines and two floating-point pipelines, positioned as a drop-in replacement for 1.25 GHz versions of the existing 21264 while promising over 30% real-world performance gains through the on-die L2 integration.
Development progressed through 2000 and into 2001 under Compaq's direction, with Samsung leading the fabrication efforts using advanced SOI processes to enable higher frequencies and efficiency. However, the project was cancelled later in 2001 as Compaq shifted strategic focus toward Intel's Itanium architecture, announcing a phase-out of Alpha development by 2004 while committing only to completing the subsequent EV7 generation. No production units of the EV68E were ever manufactured or released to market.

Supporting hardware

Chipsets

The core chipsets designed to interface with the Alpha 21264 and its variants were the 21272 (Tsunami) and 21274 (Typhoon), developed by Digital Equipment Corporation and later Compaq Computer Corporation. These chipsets provided the foundational logic for connecting the processor to memory, I/O, and other system components, utilizing the EV6 bus protocol for CPU communication. The 21272 supported uniprocessor and up to two-way symmetric multiprocessing (SMP) configurations, while the 21274 extended scalability to one- through four-way SMP, enabling balanced multi-processor environments.
Both chipsets integrated a memory controller optimized for error-correcting code (ECC) SDRAM, delivering low-latency access with pipelined operations and support for up to four independent memory arrays. The 21272 accommodated 16 MB to 4 GB of total system memory, whereas the 21274 scaled to a maximum of 32 GB using high-density modules across its arrays, with each array configurable for 16-byte or 32-byte wide buses. Peak memory bandwidth reached up to 3.2 GB/s per memory bus at 100 MHz, aggregating to higher throughput in multi-array setups, such as approximately 6.4 GB/s total with dual 256-bit buses. The controllers handled single-bit error correction and multi-bit error detection, including self-refresh modes for power management, ensuring data integrity in demanding server applications.
Peripheral connectivity was facilitated through up to two 64-bit PCI buses operating at 33 MHz, compliant with PCI Specification Revision 2.1 and supporting synchronous arbitration for multiple masters. These buses allowed for 4 GB of addressable memory space per PCI interface, with five configurable DMA windows for efficient data transfer between I/O devices and system memory. Interrupt handling occurred via the TIGbus interconnect, which polled up to 64 device interrupts, managed masking and prioritization through dedicated registers (DIM and DIR), and routed them to processors via b_irq pins, including support for interval timer interrupts during low-power states.
AMD complemented these with the Irongate family of chipsets, including the AMD-751 (Irongate) and its successor the AMD-761 (Irongate-2), tailored for Alpha 21264 compatibility over the EV6 bus using source-synchronous, packet-based transfers. The AMD-751 supported up to two-way SMP, an integrated 128-bit ECC SDRAM controller for PC-100 modules with capacities up to 4 GB across six DIMM slots, and peak memory bandwidth of 1.6 GB/s at 100 MHz. It featured a 33 MHz 32-bit PCI bus for six masters and AGP 2.0 compliance with 1x/2x modes at up to 133 MHz effective speed for graphics acceleration. The AMD-761 enhanced these capabilities with AGP 4x support and improved memory timings, maintaining similar SMP, PCI, and ECC SDRAM provisions while optimizing for higher-performance Alpha-based workstations. Interrupt management included APIC compatibility and SERR# signaling for error conditions.

Compatible systems

The Alpha 21264 microprocessor and its derivatives were integrated into several major computer systems produced by Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC), later Compaq, and other vendors, primarily serving as high-performance workstations and servers from the late 1990s into the early 2000s.
Among the primary platforms were the DEC/Compaq AlphaServer DS20 and ES40 series, which targeted enterprise computing and scientific workloads. The AlphaServer DS20, introduced in 1998 as a successor to the AlphaServer 1200, supported dual Alpha 21264 (EV6) processors clocked at 500 MHz, 667 MHz, or 833 MHz within a pedestal enclosure, offering configurations with up to 4 GB of memory and multiple PCI slots for expansion. The AlphaServer ES40, launched in 1999, scaled to up to four Alpha 21264 processors at similar speeds, featuring 6 to 10 64-bit PCI expansion slots and memory options from 512 MB to 16 GB (Model 1) or up to 32 GB (Model 2, with power supply limits), making it suitable for mid-range server applications. These systems remained in production until around 2003, supporting a range of configurations for both workstation and server use.
Samsung developed the UP1000 and UP1500 systems around the Alpha 21264, leveraging the Irongate and Irongate-2 chipsets for motherboard implementations. The UP1000, released in the late 1990s, featured single or dual Alpha 21264 processors starting at 600 MHz, with high-bandwidth cache and memory subsystems delivering up to 3.2 GB/s throughput, aimed at technical computing and embedded applications. The UP1500 extended this design with support for higher-speed variants, providing similar architecture for scalable workstation needs.
For enterprise-scale deployments, Hewlett-Packard (HP) incorporated Alpha 21264A (EV67) variants into the AlphaServer GS series, including models like the GS80, GS160, and GS320. These systems employed a modular ccNUMA (cache-coherent non-uniform memory access) architecture with quad building blocks, each housing up to four EV67 processors, to support large-scale clustering for high-availability environments.
Third-party support expanded compatibility through vendors like Alpha Processor Inc. (API), which offered Slot B modules packaging the Alpha 21264 with integrated SRAM up to 8 MB and voltage regulation on a compact PC board (110 152 mm). These modules were compatible with API's UP2000 motherboard, supporting dual-processor configurations for custom builds.
Across these platforms, the Alpha 21264 family supported key operating systems including Tru64 UNIX (formerly Digital UNIX), OpenVMS, and Linux distributions, enabling deployment in mission-critical, real-time, and open-source environments.

Performance and legacy

Benchmarks

The Alpha 21264 microprocessor family exhibited robust performance across key benchmarks, with particular strength in floating-point computations due to its dual-issue floating-point units and high-bandwidth memory subsystem. Early evaluations of the base Alpha 21264 (EV6) operating at 600 MHz yielded SPECint95 rates around 30 and SPECfp95 rates exceeding 58, establishing it as a leader in its era for both integer and floating-point workloads.
Subsequent variants improved these metrics through higher clock speeds. The Alpha 21264A (EV67) at 616 MHz achieved a SPECint95 peak rate of 35.3 and a SPECfp95 peak rate of 56.1 in single-processor configurations. At 700 MHz, the same core delivered SPECint95 peak rates of 39.1 and SPECfp95 peak rates of 68.1, underscoring the family's scalability and floating-point dominance, where FP scores consistently outpaced integer results by 70-100%.
Memory bandwidth performance was equally impressive, with the STREAM benchmark measuring sustained rates of up to 1.3 GB/s for memory-intensive operations, supporting the processor's ability to handle data-parallel workloads efficiently.
In terms of instructions per second, the Alpha 21264 sustained 2.0-2.4 billion instructions per second (MIPS) at 500-600 MHz clock speeds, scaling to higher values in later variants at elevated frequencies; peak theoretical MIPS reached approximately 2400 at 600 MHz based on its four-instruction-per-cycle issue capability.
Power efficiency metrics for the family highlighted balanced operation, with the base 21264 at 600 MHz dissipating around 72 W under typical loads, enabling SPECint95 performance densities of roughly 0.4 per watt while maintaining thermal viability in high-performance systems. Later models like the EV67 at 700 MHz operated within 80-100 W envelopes, preserving competitive efficiency for scientific and technical computing applications.

Comparisons to contemporaries

The Alpha 21264 demonstrated superior floating-point performance compared to the Intel Pentium III Xeon, delivering nearly twice the SPECfp95 score in contemporary benchmarks, though this came at a significantly higher cost, with Alpha processors retailing for $2,296 to $4,368 versus roughly half that for equivalent x86 systems. While the Pentium III led in integer performance with SPECint95_base scores around 35.6 at 733 MHz, the Alpha's strengths in FP workloads made it preferable for scientific and engineering applications, albeit with elevated manufacturing expenses due to its complex 15 million transistor design.
In comparison to the AMD Athlon, the Alpha 21264 offered similar clock scaling potential, reaching up to 1 GHz by 2001, but its deeper out-of-order execution windowreordering up to 80 instructionsprovided an edge in server environments handling irregular workloads, where the Athlon's more balanced but shallower pipeline lagged in sustained throughput. The Athlon benefited directly from the Alpha's EV6 bus architecture, which AMD licensed for its double-data-rate signaling, enabling competitive memory bandwidth but without matching the Alpha's speculative execution depth for enterprise tasks. At comparable frequencies, such as 1 GHz, the Alpha achieved higher SPECint95 scores (around 65) than the Athlon (around 28), underscoring its IPC advantage in server scenarios.
Relative to the MIPS R12000, the Alpha 21264's tournament branch predictor, combining local and global history tables for 90100% accuracy, reduced misprediction penalties more effectively than the R12000's simpler branch target buffer and global predictor approach, which relied on less correlated history tracking and yielded higher stall rates in speculative execution. This advanced prediction mechanism allowed the Alpha to sustain higher instruction throughput in branch-intensive code, contrasting the R12000's more straightforward design optimized for embedded and workstation use.
Key trade-offs for the Alpha 21264 included its elevated power drawtypically 60 W at 500 MHzcompared to the Pentium III's 23 W at 1 GHz or the Athlon's similar efficiency, limiting its appeal in power-sensitive deployments despite raw performance gains. Moreover, the x86 ecosystem's dominance in software availability and backward compatibility overshadowed the Alpha's architectural merits, confining it to niche high-end markets. The Alpha's innovations, particularly its EV6 interconnect and out-of-order techniques, influenced subsequent RISC designs, with AMD's Athlon adopting the bus protocol and elements of its execution model informing evolutions in PowerPC and PA-RISC pipelines for improved scalability.

Historical impact

In 2001, Compaq announced plans to phase out the Alpha processor line, including derivatives of the 21264, by 2004 in favor of Intel's Itanium architecture, marking the end of active development under Compaq's ownership. Following Hewlett-Packard's acquisition of Compaq in 2002, HP continued production of the final Alpha processors, releasing the EV7z in August 2004 as the last in the lineage, while extending sales of Alpha-based systems like AlphaServers through 2006 to support existing customers in niche markets. This shift reflected HP's strategic pivot to Itanium for enterprise computing, effectively concluding the Alpha 21264's production era as part of the broader Alpha discontinuation.
Licensing agreements with Samsung Electronics, initiated in 1996, and IBM Microelectronics, expanded in 2000 for next-generation Alpha processors, allowed limited continuation of production in specialized applications beyond HP's primary efforts. Samsung manufactured variants like the EV67 and EV68 using advanced processes, sustaining availability for embedded and high-end systems until around 2006, while IBM contributed to fabrication for select later models, prolonging the architecture's viability in scientific and embedded niches despite the mainline sunset.
The 21264's advanced out-of-order execution and speculative features influenced subsequent x86 designs, notably through key personnel transitions; for instance, lead architect Jim Keller, who co-designed the 21264, applied these principles to AMD's K8 microarchitecture in the Opteron processor, enhancing 64-bit server performance. Its emphasis on high clock speeds and efficient instruction scheduling also contributed to industry-wide advancements in out-of-order pipelines, seen in Intel's NetBurst architecture for the Pentium 4, though adapted to x86 constraints. In high-performance computing, the 21264 powered AlphaServer clusters for scientific workloads, including simulations and data processing, due to its robust memory bandwidth and 64-bit addressing, maintaining relevance in HPC environments through the early 2000s.
Today, the 21264 holds collector interest among retro computing enthusiasts for its pioneering role in RISC evolution, with preserved systems and chips valued in niche markets for historical demonstrations. Emulation software, such as solutions from Stromasys, enables running legacy Alpha applications like OpenVMS and Tru64 UNIX on modern x86 hardware, preserving software ecosystems for enterprise archival and research purposes.
