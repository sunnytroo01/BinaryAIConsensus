 The Den is an association football stadium in Bermondsey, south-east London, England, serving as the home ground of Millwall Football Club since its completion in 1993. With a capacity of 20,146 all-seated spectators, it was built to comply with post-Hillsborough safety regulations mandating seated accommodations and to address persistent crowd control issues at the club's prior venue, the Old Den in New Cross. The stadium features four covered stands surrounding the pitch, including the North Stand for away supporters, and is located adjacent to South Bermondsey railway station for convenient access. Opened on 4 August 1993 with Millwall's 00 draw against Southend United, The Den has hosted EFL Championship matches and occasional cup ties, recording its highest attendance of 20,093 during a 1994 FA Cup clash against Arsenal. Despite its modern design aimed at curbing hooliganism, the venue retains an intense atmosphere reflective of Millwall's longstanding working-class supporter base in the Docklands area.

History

Origins and the Original Den

Millwall Football Club was established in 1885 as Millwall Rovers by workers from the J.T. Morton Jute Works on the Isle of Dogs, initially playing on makeshift pitches such as the Lord Nelson Ground near a local pub that served as an early clubhouse. The club relocated frequently during its formative years, occupying at least five grounds in the areaincluding Glengall Road, East Ferry Road, and Millwall Parkover the next 25 years, reflecting the instability of amateur football in industrial London at the time.
In 1910, to increase attendances and secure a fixed base amid growing professional ambitions, Millwall moved south of the River Thames to a site on Cold Blow Lane in New Cross, acquiring leasehold rights to undeveloped land previously used for minor sports. This venue, the club's sixth ground overall, was named The Den upon opening and hosted its first match on 22 October 1910, a 32 victory over Bristol City in the Southern League. Designed in part by Scottish architect Archibald Leitch, known for engineering terraced stands at over 500 British stadia including Ibrox and Hampden Park, the initial layout consisted of earthen banks topped with concrete terracing on two sides, supplemented by basic covered areas with pitched roofs over the rear thirds of the ends.
The Den's modest construction prioritized functionality for a working-class supporter base drawn from nearby docks and factories, with capacity initially around 20,000, expandable via temporary stands. It endured as Millwall's home for 83 years, weathering wartime damageincluding substantial bombing during the Blitz that required post-1945 reconstructionswhile fostering the club's reputation for intense matchday atmospheres rooted in its proletarian origins. No single definitive source attributes the name "The Den" beyond its adoption at relocation, though it evoked the site's enclosed, den-like setting amid terraced housing.

Relocation Pressures and the New Den's Construction

Following the Hillsborough disaster in April 1989, which resulted in 97 fatalities, the Taylor Report published in January 1990 recommended that all Football League stadiums in the top two divisions convert to all-seater configurations to enhance safety. Millwall Football Club, then competing in the Football League First Division, faced significant pressures to modernize its aging Old Den ground at Cold Blow Lane, which featured terracing and did not meet emerging safety standards. The club anticipated stricter regulations and initiated plans for relocation to avoid costly retrofitting of the outdated venue, which had hosted matches since 1910.
In response, Millwall opted to construct a new stadium approximately a quarter-mile away at Senegal Fields in Bermondsey, selecting the site for its proximity to the club's traditional fanbase and transport links. The decision prioritized compliance with Taylor Report mandates, including accessible exits and segregated routes from pitch to concourses, making the New Den the first purpose-built all-seater stadium in England post-report. Construction commenced in 1992, funded primarily through club resources and loans, with the project costing 16 million.
The stadium opened on 4 August 1993 with a friendly match against Sporting CP, attended by over 10,000 spectators, marking the end of 83 years at the Old Den where the final competitive fixture occurred on 8 May 1993. Initially designed for a capacity of 25,000 to 30,000 seats, the completed structure was scaled to just over 20,000 to manage costs and phased development potential. This relocation enabled Millwall to maintain its Second Division status under the new safety regime while preserving local community ties.

Operational Milestones and Renovations

The stadium, initially known as the New Den, began construction in May 1992 and was completed by August 1993 at a cost of 16 million, making it the first all-seater stadium constructed in England after the Taylor Report's recommendations following the 1989 Hillsborough disaster.
It opened on 4 August 1993 with a preseason friendly match against Sporting CP of Portugal, attended by approximately 10,500 spectators, signaling Millwall F.C.'s permanent relocation a quarter-mile from the original Den, which had hosted the club's final match on 8 May 1993. The initial capacity was established at just over 20,000 seats, intentionally scaled back from earlier plans of 25,00030,000 to accommodate potential future phased expansions.
The venue achieved its record attendance of 20,093 on 22 October 1994 during a First Division match against Arsenal, which Millwall won 10. Minimal structural changes occurred in the intervening decades due to the stadium's relative youth upon opening, though the prefix "New" was eventually dropped from its name.
In May 2024, Millwall secured a 999-year lease on the site from Lewisham Council, stabilizing long-term operations amid prior redevelopment uncertainties. Post-2024/25 season renovations focused on functionality and fan experience, including installation of over 30 new hand dryers, replacement dugout seating, enhanced CCTV coverage, and upgraded access control systems. Upgraded hospitality lounges opened in September 2025, expanding non-matchday event capabilities while a broader refresh targeted the venue's aging infrastructure. Phased capacity increases to 34,000 remain in planning, integrated with surrounding residential and community developments approved since 2022, but no major expansions have been operationalized as of October 2025.

Stadium Design and Features

Architectural Layout and Stands

The Den employs a compact rectangular layout with four two-tiered stands encircling the pitch, designed to foster an enclosed and intense atmosphere through steeply raked seating that brings spectators close to the action. Opened in 1993 following the Taylor Report's recommendations for all-seater stadiums, the structure features uniform stand heights and no open corners, enhancing acoustic containment for supporter chants. The pitch orientation runs north-south, with dimensions of 112 by 72 yards, positioned tightly against the stands without surrounding tracks.
The North Stand, primarily allocated to away supporters, holds approximately 4,000 spectators across its two tiers and serves as the designated visitor section for most matches. It includes facilities for segregation, with capacities adjusted based on fixture risk assessments, often reduced below full allocation.
Opposite, the Cold Blow Lane Stand occupies the south end, named after Millwall's historic pre-relocation ground, and accommodates home fans with its prominent positioning behind the goal. This stand features executive boxes and family areas, contributing to its role in generating vocal support during matches.
The East Stand, known as the Barry Kitchener Stand in honor of a dedicated longtime supporter, lines the touchline and provides covered seating with good sightlines across the pitch. It includes hospitality options and integrates with the stadium's perimeter fencing for security.
The West Stand, or Dockers Stand, reflects the club's working-class heritage tied to the former docklands workforce and offers similar two-tiered accommodation for home patrons, emphasizing community-oriented seating zones. Zampa's Family Stand section within this area caters to younger fans and families, promoting accessibility.

Capacity, Pitch Specifications, and Facilities

The Den maintains an all-seated capacity of 20,146 spectators across its four stands, configured to comply with post-Hillsborough safety standards requiring seated accommodation. This figure has remained stable since the stadium's completion in 1993, though Millwall announced plans in September 2025 to incrementally expand to 34,000 seats through phased developments including new stands and mixed-use additions.
The pitch measures 106 meters in length by 68 meters in width, extended by six meters in 2015 to enhance playing conditions under then-manager Neil Harris's directive for a larger surface area. The field utilizes a hybrid grass surface incorporating synthetic fibers for durability, without undersoil heating.
Facilities include two-tiered, covered stands providing covered seating throughout, with the North Stand accommodating approximately 4,000 in its upper and lower tiers primarily for home supporters. Player amenities feature recently upgraded dugout seating and enhanced CCTV for security, alongside over 30 new hand dryers installed in August 2025 across concourse areas. The stadium supports non-match events through eight meeting rooms, 32 private hospitality boxes, and renovated lounges suitable for conferences and celebrations, emphasizing compact yet functional spaces. Away fan facilities remain basic, with tight concourses noted for high attendance days.

Usage and Events

League and Domestic Match Attendances

The Den has consistently hosted strong attendances for Millwall's league fixtures in the EFL Championship, reflecting the club's loyal local fanbase and proximity to London rivals, with averages often exceeding 80% of the stadium's 20,146 capacity in recent seasons. Peak crowds occur for derbies against teams like West Ham United or Charlton Athletic, driven by heightened rivalry and accessible transport links.
The record attendance at The Den stands at 20,093, recorded during an FA Cup third-round match against Arsenal on 10 January 1994, which pushed the stadium to near its initial limits shortly after opening. In league play, the highest reported crowd was 17,195 during the 201819 Championship season, underscoring a pattern where cup ties occasionally surpass regular-season figures due to national interest.
League attendances have trended upward since Millwall's promotion to the Championship in 2017, with the 202324 season marking the highest average (16,544) since the 195253 campaign at the old Den, attributed to improved on-pitch results and expanded away allocations boosting overall turnout. This represents a near 4,000 increase from three seasons prior (12,950 average), sustained by consistent mid-table finishes and matches against high-draw opponents like Sunderland or Derby County. For the 202425 Championship season, the average home attendance through 23 fixtures reached 15,490, with standout crowds including 18,021 against Middlesbrough on 16 August 2024 and 17,877 versus Wrexham on 30 August 2024.

Domestic cup attendances at The Den vary significantly by opponent and round, typically lower than league averages for early EFL Cup or FA Cup ties against lower-division sides but swelling for deeper runs or marquee matchups. For instance, the 2004 FA Cup fifth-round replay against Sunderland drew a near-capacity 18,000-plus, contributing to playoff-era highs. Recent examples include reduced turnouts for preliminary rounds, such as under 6,000 for non-league opposition, reflecting fan prioritization of league commitments amid scheduling conflicts and ticket pricing. Overall, cup games reinforce The Den's reputation for selective but intense support, with averages dipping below 10,000 for mismatches while rivalries sustain figures comparable to midweek league fixtures.

International and Non-Football Events

The Den has hosted a select number of international football matches, typically involving national teams in non-competitive or developmental fixtures. These include encounters such as England Women versus Germany Women on 8 March 1998, which ended in a 01 defeat for the hosts, and Ghana versus Senegal on 21 August 2007, concluding in a 11 draw as part of pre-tournament preparations. Such events underscore the stadium's occasional utility for fixtures beyond Millwall's domestic schedule, though they remain infrequent due to the venue's primary association with club football.
In non-football contexts, The Den has served as a venue for combat sports, most notably hosting the WBO world heavyweight title bout between defending champion Michael Bentt and challenger Herbie Hide on 19 March 1994. Bentt, a London native of Jamaican descent returning for a "roots" homecoming fight, was stopped by Hide via second-round knockout after 135 seconds, marking Hide's first world title win at age 23. The event drew attention for its high stakes and Bentt's pre-fight street altercation with Hide, highlighting the stadium's adaptability for boxing undercard promotions alongside the main event.
The venue supports various corporate and community events, including conferences, award ceremonies, and hospitality functions, leveraging its South East London location and facilities like executive suites for up to 500 delegates. Music concerts occur sporadically but rarely feature major artists, limiting the stadium's role in large-scale entertainment compared to specialized arenas. Overall, non-football usage emphasizes practical, localized gatherings rather than blockbuster spectacles, aligning with Millwall FC's operational focus on football revenue and fan-centric programming.

Fan Culture and Atmosphere

Traditions, Chants, and Community Ties

Millwall supporters at The Den are renowned for their defiant chant "No one likes us, we don't care," adapted to the melody of Rod Stewart's "Sailing" and originating in the 1970s as a terrace anthem embracing the club's outsider status amid widespread media scrutiny of fan behavior. This refrain, belted out consistently from the Cold Blow Lane stand, encapsulates a tradition of vocal resilience, with fans sustaining noise levels through rhythmic clapping and coordinated singing that intensifies during matches against rivals like West Ham United.
Annual traditions reinforce historical roots, such as Dockers' Day, which commemorates the club's 1885 founding by workers from J.T. Morton's canning factory on the Isle of Dogs, drawing former players from eras like the 19641967 squad to The Den for celebratory events and matches. Pre-match gatherings in nearby Bermondsey pubs foster camaraderie, while self-organized clean-ups and steward-led entry protocols maintain order without external policing, preserving an authentic, unyielding matchday ritual.
Community ties anchor Millwall to South London, with the Millwall Community Trustoperational since 1985delivering over 100 annual football and multisport programs targeting disadvantaged youth in Lewisham, Southwark, and Kent boroughs. Initiatives like the Forever Lions Club honor retired players through induction ceremonies, while partnerships with local teams such as the Millwall Romans LGBTQ+ squad promote inclusivity amid the club's working-class heritage. These efforts, including mentoring and equality drives, have earned recognition for bridging the club with its docklands origins, countering stereotypes through tangible local engagement.

Reputation for Intensity and Self-Policing

The Den is renowned for its fervent and intimidating atmosphere, where Millwall supporters generate intense pressure on visiting teams through sustained vocal support and chants. Former player Troy Deeney, who competed at venues like Old Trafford and Anfield, described the environment at The Den as superior in hostility and energy compared to those Premier League grounds. Goalkeeper Kasey Keller similarly highlighted the raucous fans and demanding setting as formative in his professional development during his time at the club. This reputation persists despite the stadium's modern design, with observers noting the unique aggression that envelops matches regardless of the scoreline.
Complementing this intensity is a practice of self-policing among Millwall fans, particularly by older or more seasoned supporters who intervene to curb disruptive behavior and maintain order. During Theo Paphitis's tenure as chairman from 1997 to 2005, the club actively promoted self-regulation as a means to reshape its image, leading to arrest and banning order statistics that contradicted prevailing media narratives of unchecked hooliganism. Such internal oversight has contributed to relatively contained incident levels, with Home Office data for the 2024/25 season recording 38 arrests involving Millwall supporters, 14 of which were for violent disorderthe most common offense but indicative of broader league trends rather than exceptional deviance. This approach underscores a causal link between fan-led discipline and the stadium's operational stability, prioritizing empirical outcomes over sensationalized accounts from biased reporting outlets.

Controversies and Incidents

Historical Hooliganism and Media Portrayals

Millwall supporters gained a reputation for organized hooliganism during the 1970s and 1980s, centered around the original Den stadium in Cold Blow Lane, where the club's firm, known as the Bushwackers, engaged in territorial clashes with rival groups. A 1977 BBC Panorama documentary captured undercover footage of Millwall hooligans planning and executing violence, portraying the issue as endemic to the club's fanbase and influencing public perception of English football's disorder.
The most severe incident unfolded on March 13, 1985, at Luton Town's Kenilworth Road during an FA Cup sixth-round tie, when around 1,000 Millwall fans breached barriers, invaded the home end, and sparked riots involving bottles, coins, and direct assaults on police and Luton supporters, injuring over 40 officers and leading to 74 arrests. The Football Association responded by closing The Den to spectators for two matches and imposing a ban on Millwall away fans until the season's end, while the event accelerated calls for all-seater stadiums under the Taylor Report.
Media outlets amplified these events, with The Guardian labeling the 1985 riot a moment when "football died a slow death" due to unchecked supporter aggression, framing Millwall as a symbol of the sport's endemic violence amid broader 1980s hooligan crises like Heysel. Coverage often emphasized Millwall's working-class South London roots and the "No one likes us, we don't care" chant as badges of defiance, yet critics later noted disproportionate focus on the club compared to similar disturbances at other grounds, potentially exaggerating the scale relative to peaceful attendances averaging 10,000-15,000 at The Den.
Subsequent clashes, including a January 1988 FA Cup match against Arsenal where 41 Millwall fans were arrested amid pitch invasions and fighting, sustained the narrative, though hooliganism declined post-Hillsborough with stricter policing and the shift to the New Den in 1993. A 2002 play-off semi-final loss to Birmingham City revived concerns, as approximately 900 Millwall supporters attacked police outside The New Den with missiles, prompting over 100 arrests and 10-year banning orders for many involved. While these portrayals cemented Millwall's image as England's "most hated" club in press and documentaries, empirical data from the period shows hooligan arrests represented a fraction of overall fans, with media emphasis on sensational incidents over systemic factors like overcrowding and alcohol availability across leagues.

Modern Fan Behavior and Resistance to External Narratives

In recent years, Millwall supporters at The Den have exhibited a blend of fervent loyalty and selective defiance, with average Championship match attendances exceeding 12,000 in the 2023-2024 season, underscoring sustained engagement despite the club's mid-table status. The iconic chant "No one likes us, we don't care"regularly belted out across the standsfunctions as a modern emblem of resistance to external stereotypes, reframing historical associations with hooliganism as a point of pride in authenticity and independence from mainstream football culture's expectations. This vocal tradition, adapted from a 1930s music hall song and amplified since the 1970s, persists as a collective assertion of identity, directly challenging media portrayals that often amplify isolated incidents over routine support.
A prominent example of pushback against imposed narratives emerged in December 2020, when fans booed players taking the knee before kickoff against Derby County, prompting the club to suspend the gesture after internal consultations revealed it alienated significant portions of the supporter base. Millwall officials announced the change to avoid division, opting instead for alternative anti-racism initiatives tailored to community needs, such as partnerships with Kick It Out; this move was lambasted by commentators in left-leaning outlets like The Guardian as enabling prejudice, yet empirical fan feedback indicated a preference for substantive local action over gestures perceived as politicized imports from American activism. The episode highlighted causal tensions between fan autonomy and institutional pressures, with subsequent matches seeing unified applause for anti-racism banners as a club-fan compromise.
While overt violence has declined from peak 1980s levelsgovernment data showing football-related arrests dropping club-wide since all-seater stadium mandatesmodern behavior includes persistent issues, evidenced by 82 active banning orders on Millwall fans in the 2023-2024 season (third-highest in English football) and a 15,000 FA fine in June 2025 for crowd misconduct during a match against Plymouth Argyle. Supporters counterbalance this through informal self-policing and club-backed efforts, including the 2025 Fan Advisory Board for direct dialogue on standards and the All 'Wall program promoting inclusivity, which has engaged thousands in anti-discrimination workshops. These mechanisms reflect a pragmatic adaptation: maintaining the ground's reputed intensitypraised for its vocal edge in derbieswhile resisting blanket condemnations by demonstrating internal accountability over externally dictated reforms.

Access and Location

Transport Links and Accessibility

South Bermondsey railway station provides the most direct rail access to The Den, located approximately five minutes' walk away via a dedicated pedestrian walkway. Trains operated by Southeastern depart regularly from London Bridge, with services typically every 15 to 30 minutes on match days. Other nearby stations include Surrey Quays Overground, about 17 minutes' walk distant, and New Cross Gate, though these require longer journeys to the stadium.
Multiple bus routes serve the area, facilitating access without rail. Routes such as the 21, 53, and 172 stop at Old Kent Road; the P12 at Ilderton Road; the 1 and 381 at Rotherhithe New Road; and the 47, 188, and 225 at Surrey Quays. These services connect to central London and surrounding districts, with low-floor buses available for improved accessibility.
Road access is restricted, with no public parking available at the stadium itself and extremely limited spaces nearby, prompting recommendations for public transport use. For disabled supporters, 18 reserved car park spaces exist for home fans and eight for away fans, requiring prior booking through the club. The stadium offers 46 wheelchair viewing positions, including audio commentary and assistance dog facilities, though some approaches like South Bermondsey station involve steps, with alternatives via step-free stations such as Canada Water on the Jubilee line.

Surrounding Neighborhood and Security Measures

The Den is situated in the Bermondsey district of South East London, an area historically associated with working-class communities and industrial activity, particularly the former Surrey Docks that employed many of Millwall FC's early supporters as shipbuilders and dock workers. The immediate surroundings feature a mix of residential estates, light industrial units, and commercial properties along Zampa Road, with proximity to the Surrey Canal path offering green space amid urban development. While parts of Bermondsey have undergone regeneration, the neighborhood around the stadium retains a gritty, post-industrial character, contributing to the club's longstanding ties with local fans who view The Den as a community stronghold.
Matchday security at The Den emphasizes coordinated efforts between club stewards, CCTV surveillance, and Metropolitan Police presence to manage crowd behavior, informed by the club's history of fan-related incidents. Procedures include bag searches at entry points, with fans encouraged to arrive early to facilitate smoother processing and minimize delays. Police visibility is heightened around pubs and routes to the stadium to deter pre- and post-match disturbances, particularly for away supporters who may receive escorted returns to South Bermondsey station. Despite these measures, sporadic violence has occurred, such as clashes during the March 2024 Millwall vs. Birmingham City match that injured officers, underscoring ongoing challenges in high-attendance fixtures.
Millwall FC promotes a "team effort" approach to safety, integrating fan cooperation with formal protocols, including safeguarding policies for vulnerable individuals and 24-hour monitoring via digital video systems. The neighborhood's reputation for intensity is tempered by reports from attendees noting that sensible behavior yields a secure experience, with police operations effectively handling potential risks without routine escalation. This self-policing ethos among loyal supporters aligns with the club's emphasis on community responsibility, though external narratives often amplify past hooliganism over contemporary management successes.

Ownership and Future Developments

Lease Agreements and Ownership Disputes

The New Den stadium is situated on land owned by the freehold of Lewisham Council, with Millwall Football Club holding a lease rather than outright ownership. The original lease agreement dates to 1993, granting the club a 150-year term for the stadium site and the adjacent Lions Centre community facilities. This arrangement stemmed from the club's relocation from their previous ground at The Old Den to the new purpose-built stadium in South Bermondsey, financed through a combination of club funds and public sector support amid post-hooliganism era reforms in English football.
Ownership disputes primarily revolved around surrounding land parcels, where Lewisham Council pursued compulsory purchase orders (CPOs) to enable redevelopment by private developers, such as Renewal, a firm linked to offshore interests. These efforts, peaking between 2016 and 2019, aimed to integrate residential and commercial projects but threatened Millwall's control over expansion opportunities and raised fears of forced relocation, as the club viewed the proposals as prioritizing profit over community and sporting needs. In 2017, controversies intensified with allegations of false claims by a sports foundation tied to Renewal regarding 2 million in Sport England funding, which the developer stood to benefit from via land sales. Millwall contested the CPOs legally and publicly, arguing they undermined the club's long-term viability at the site, while council documents indicated conditional sales of public land that could displace community assets.
Tensions eased in October 2019 when Lewisham Council terminated the disputed land sale agreement, removing immediate relocation threats and allowing Millwall to plan stadium enhancements. Further progress occurred in May 2023 with the formal scrapping of the CPO on adjacent land, affirming the club's tenure. The resolution culminated on May 9, 2024, in a "landmark development agreement" granting Millwall a new 999-year lease, effectively equivalent to freehold security, while permitting controlled development of peripheral land and safeguarding community spaces like the Lions Centre. This deal, negotiated amid prior acrimony, reflects mutual recognition of the stadium's role in local regeneration without ceding council oversight of broader urban planning. No ongoing ownership challenges persist, though the lease structure underscores the council's retained freehold authority over strategic decisions.

Expansion Plans and Long-Term Prospects

In May 2024, Millwall Football Club secured a landmark development agreement with Lewisham Council, granting a 999-year lease extension for The Den and surrounding land, replacing the previous lease with 120 years remaining that imposed development restrictions. This deal, formally approved by the council in September 2025, enables the club to pursue comprehensive redevelopment, including residential housing and commercial facilities adjacent to the stadium, aimed at funding infrastructure upgrades and ensuring financial sustainability.
Central to these plans is a phased expansion of the stadium's capacity from its current 20,146 seats to a maximum of 34,000, involving incremental additions such as upper tiers behind existing stands, without requiring a full rebuild. The strategy, developed in consultation with architects and local authorities, integrates the stadium into a broader regeneration masterplan for the Surrey Canal Area, incorporating facilities for the Millwall Community Trust such as a sports hall, gym, and changing rooms to enhance community engagement.
Long-term prospects hinge on this secured tenure and mixed-use development, which club officials describe as vital for adapting to evolving fan demands and competitive pressures in the English Football League, potentially increasing matchday revenue through higher attendance and ancillary income streams. However, realization depends on securing planning permissions, private investment, and alignment with local housing needs, with initial phases targeting completion within the next decade amid ongoing economic uncertainties in professional football.
