 Saul Aaron Kripke (November 13, 1940 September 15, 2022) was an American philosopher and logician renowned for his pioneering contributions to modal logic, the philosophy of language, and metaphysics, which reshaped analytic philosophy in the late 20th century. Born in Bay Shore, Long Island, New York, to Rabbi Myer Kripke and author Dorothy K. Kripke, he displayed prodigious talent from a young age, teaching himself ancient Hebrew by six and reading all of Shakespeare's works by nine. His family relocated to Omaha, Nebraska, in 1946, where his father served at Beth El Synagogue.
Kripke graduated summa cum laude from Harvard University in 1962 with a degree in mathematics, having already taught graduate-level logic courses at MIT as an undergraduate. He never pursued a formal doctorate, yet his early achievement included publishing "A Completeness Theorem in Modal Logic" at age 18 in 1959, establishing key semantic frameworks for modal systems. His academic career featured positions as a professor at Rockefeller University from 1968 to 1976, McCosh Professor of Philosophy at Princeton University from 1978 to 1998, and, from 2003 until his death, a distinguished professor at the City University of New York Graduate Center, where the Saul Kripke Center was established to archive his manuscripts. Kripke delivered the prestigious John Locke Lectures at Oxford in 1973 and received the Rolf Schock Prize in Logic and Philosophy from the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences in 2001.
Kripke's most influential work, Naming and Necessity (1980), based on lectures delivered at Princeton in 1970, introduced the concepts of rigid designatorsterms like proper names that refer to the same object in all possible worldsand a posteriori necessities, such as the identity of water with HO, challenging descriptivist theories of reference advanced by Frege and Russell. This book revived metaphysical essentialism, arguing that natural kind terms and identities carry necessary truths discoverable empirically, and it has been cited over 10,000 times in philosophical literature. In Wittgenstein on Rules and Private Language (1982), Kripke interpreted Ludwig Wittgenstein's remarks on rule-following as presenting a skeptical paradox about meaning: no fact about a speaker's past usage can definitively determine the correctness of future applications, prompting debates on semantics, intentionality, and normativity.
Throughout his career, Kripke produced relatively few published worksmuch of his output consisted of extemporaneous lectures later transcribedyet his ideas permeated philosophy of mind, logic, and science. Notable among his later publications is Philosophical Troubles: Collected Papers, Volume 1 (2011), compiling essays on truth, existence, and paradox, including his fixed-point theory for self-referential semantics. Kripke died of pancreatic cancer in Plainsboro, New Jersey, leaving behind an estimated 70% of his writings unpublished and a legacy as one of the most original thinkers since Wittgenstein and Quine. He was married to philosopher Margaret Gilbert from 1976 until their divorce.

Early Life and Education

Family Background

Saul Aaron Kripke was born on November 13, 1940, in Bay Shore, New York, to Jewish parents Myer S. Kripke and Dorothy K. Kripke. His father, Myer S. Kripke, served as a rabbi, leading a Conservative congregation in Patchogue, Long Island, before moving the family to Omaha, Nebraska, in 1946, where he became senior rabbi at Beth El Synagogue until 1975. Myer Kripke was known for his deep engagement with Jewish scholarship, including Talmudic studies, and later became a noted philanthropist supporting Jewish education and institutions. His mother, Dorothy K. Kripke (ne Karp), was a teacher of English and Hebrew at high schools in New York City and at Beth El Hebrew School in Omaha, as well as an author of children's books on Judaism.
Kripke was the eldest of three children, with two younger sisters: Madeline Kripke, who amassed one of the world's largest private collections of dictionaries and books on language, and Netta Kripke Stern, a social worker. Both sisters predeceased him.
The Kripke household in Omaha emphasized religious observance and intellectual rigor, fostering an environment rich in Jewish tradition and scholarly discussion. Saul was exposed to Hebrew texts from a young age through his mother's teaching and the family's synagogue involvement, achieving proficiency in reading ancient Hebrew by age six; the home was filled with books on philosophy, religion, science, and logic, encouraging analytical thinking and debate among family members. This upbringing, rooted in Conservative Judaism, shaped his early familiarity with rigorous reasoning and textual interpretation.

Childhood Prodigy

Saul Kripke exhibited remarkable intellectual precocity during his childhood, displaying an innate fascination with mathematics and logic that led him to tackle complex problems independently, without formal instruction. In fourth grade, he discovered algebra on his own, and by the end of grammar school, he had mastered geometry and calculus, prompting his teacher to provide him with advanced books on mathematical theory. This self-directed exploration highlighted his ability to grasp abstract concepts far beyond his years, laying the foundation for his later contributions to logical systems.
At the age of six, Kripke taught himself Ancient Hebrew using a Bible, a feat that underscored his linguistic aptitude and determination. By nine, he had read the complete works of William Shakespeare, demonstrating not only prodigious reading comprehension but also a broad engagement with literature. These accomplishments, achieved in the supportive environment of his family's home in Omaha, Nebraskawhere his father served as a rabbi and his mother wrote educational booksreflected an environment that nurtured his curiosity without structured guidance.
Kripke's early talent extended to formal logic during his teenage years. At 17, while still in high school, he authored "A Completeness Theorem in Modal Logic," a groundbreaking paper that provided a semantic framework for certain modal systems. The work was accepted for publication in the Journal of Symbolic Logic without any revisions, marking a rare instance of an adolescent producing publishable research in advanced logic. This achievement, completed before his high school graduation, solidified his reputation as a prodigy in philosophical logic.

Following his family's relocation to Omaha, Nebraska, in 1946, where his father assumed the role of senior rabbi at Beth El Synagogue, Saul Kripke attended Hebrew school alongside public schools, including Dundee Grade School. His early prodigy status, marked by self-taught proficiency in Ancient Hebrew by age six and advanced mathematical achievements, accelerated his academic trajectory. In the fall of 1958, Kripke entered Harvard College at age 17 to study mathematics and philosophy. His coursework emphasized logic, guided by the influential philosopher Willard Van Orman Quine, whose analytic approach shaped Kripke's foundational work in the field. He completed a bachelor's degree in mathematics summa cum laude in 1962, the only non-honorary degree he would receive.
After graduation, Kripke pursued advanced studies as a member of Harvard's Society of Fellows from 1963 to 1966 and briefly at Oxford University on a Fulbright Scholarship in 19621963. This period allowed him to deepen his research in logic and philosophy without formal doctoral enrollment.

Academic Career

Early Appointments

Kripke's exceptional abilities as a young scholar enabled his rapid entry into academia without pursuing a traditional graduate degree. After graduating from Harvard University in 1962 with a bachelor's degree in mathematics, Kripke served as a Fulbright Scholar (19621963), joined the Harvard Society of Fellows in 1963, and lectured at Harvard from 1966 to 1968, during which time he delivered influential seminars on logical topics. He also lectured at Princeton University in 1965 and 1966.
Throughout the 1960s, Kripke balanced his Harvard commitments with concurrent visiting appointments at other leading institutions, including Yale University and the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), where he offered graduate-level instruction in logic. These roles underscored his growing reputation among peers and facilitated cross-institutional collaborations. In 1968, following his departure from Harvard, Kripke accepted a position at Rockefeller University in New York City as associate professor of philosophy and mathematical logic, with promotion to full professor in 1972; he served there until 1976, focusing on teaching and research in core philosophical domains.
Kripke's trajectory culminated in his appointment as McCosh Professor of Philosophy at Princeton University in 1977, marking a significant escalation in his academic stature and responsibilities. This endowed chair position, which he retained until his retirement in 1998, provided a stable platform for his mature scholarly pursuits.

Major Institutions

In 1968, Kripke joined Rockefeller University in New York City as an associate professor of philosophy, where he advanced to full professor in 1972 and taught logic and philosophy until 1976. This appointment followed his early momentum from junior fellow and lecturer roles at Harvard University, allowing him to develop his ideas in a research-focused environment that emphasized interdisciplinary work in the sciences and humanities. At Rockefeller, Kripke contributed to the institution's tradition of innovative scholarship, delivering lectures that influenced emerging fields in analytic philosophy and logic.
Kripke then moved to Princeton University in 1977, where he served as the McCosh Professor of Philosophy until his retirement in 1998. During his two-decade tenure, Princeton became a hub for his groundbreaking seminars on modal logic and the philosophy of language, attracting scholars and fostering debates that shaped late-20th-century philosophy. His presence elevated the department's international reputation, as he collaborated with figures like Gilbert Harman and supervised graduate work that advanced semantic theories.
In 2003, Kripke was appointed Distinguished Professor of Philosophy (and later also Computer Science) at the Graduate Center of the City University of New York (CUNY), a position he held until his death in 2022. This role allowed him to continue teaching advanced seminars in a urban academic setting, where he published through the Saul Kripke Center established in his honor in 2007 to archive and promote his unpublished works. Throughout his career, Kripke also served as a frequent visiting professor at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem and other global institutions, including Oxford University, UCLA, and Cornell University, extending his influence across continents.
Kripke mentored numerous students during his appointments at Princeton and CUNY, many of whom became prominent philosophers and logicians, contributing to the dissemination of his ideas on reference, necessity, and rule-following. His teaching style, known for its depth and rigor, inspired a generation through direct supervision and public lectures, leaving a lasting impact on academic philosophy programs.

Later Years

Kripke retired from his full-time professorship at Princeton University in 1998 after more than two decades there. In 2003, he joined the Graduate Center of the City University of New York (CUNY) as a distinguished professor of philosophy and computer science, where he continued his academic engagements in a less intensive capacity. These long-term institutional ties at CUNY helped sustain his influence in philosophical circles.
In his later years, Kripke maintained sporadic lecturing, often delivering extemporaneous talks without notes, though his public appearances became increasingly limited. He focused more on private seminars and ongoing philosophical inquiry rather than broad public engagements, reflecting his preference for intimate intellectual discussions. Kripke remained active in philosophy, contributing to works like his 2011 collection Philosophical Troubles, until health challenges intensified.
Kripke was diagnosed with pancreatic cancer and endured a short illness in his final months. He died on September 15, 2022, at Penn Medicine Princeton Medical Center in Plainsboro, New Jersey, at the age of 81. Following his death, the Saul Kripke Center at CUNY, established in 2007 to house and publish his extensive archivesincluding thousands of pages of unpublished manuscripts and recordingscontinued its efforts to preserve and disseminate his scholarly legacy.

Contributions to Logic

Modal Logic

Saul Kripke revolutionized the semantics of modal logic by introducing a framework based on possible worlds, first outlined in his 1959 paper and fully developed in his 1963 works. In this approach, modal statements about necessity and possibility are evaluated relative to a set of possible worlds connected by an accessibility relation, providing a precise model for how modal operators function across different scenarios. This semantics addressed longstanding challenges in interpreting modal logics, such as those involving necessity (\Box) and possibility (\Diamond), by grounding them in relational structures rather than algebraic or set-theoretic abstractions alone.
Central to Kripke's innovation is the accessibility relation RRR between worlds, which defines the scope of modal operators. A formula p\Box pp holds at a world www in a model if and only if ppp holds at every world vvv such that wRvwRvwRv; dually, p\Diamond pp holds at www if ppp holds at some accessible vvv. This relational interpretation allows for varying strengths of modal logics through constraints on RRR, such as reflexivity for the T axiom or transitivity and symmetry for S5. Kripke's framework thus enables a uniform treatment of diverse modal systems while highlighting their structural differences via frame conditions.
Kripke further advanced the field by constructing canonical models for modal logics and establishing completeness theorems. In his 1959 work, he proved completeness for S5 (with quantifiers), showing that every consistent set of formulas has a model. Extending this in 1963, he demonstrated soundness and completeness for normal modal propositional calculi, including S4 (via reflexive-transitive frames) and S5 (via equivalence relations), using canonical constructions where worlds correspond to maximal consistent sets and accessibility reflects logical consequence. These results confirmed that Kripke semantics fully captures the deductive power of these systems. As an extension for polymodal variants, such as in provability logics, Carlson models simplify Kripke-style frames by incorporating multiple accessibility types in a tree-like structure, maintaining completeness while aiding analysis of arithmetic interpretations.
Kripke's semantics has profoundly influenced formal verification, where possible-worlds models underpin tools for checking system properties like safety and liveness in software and hardware. It also shapes philosophy of science by providing rigid structures for analyzing counterfactuals and nomic necessity. In philosophy of language, this framework informs Kripke's later concept of rigid designators, which refer necessarily to the same entity across accessible worlds.

Intuitionistic Logic

In 1965, Saul Kripke extended his semantic framework, originally developed for modal logics, to provide a model-theoretic interpretation of intuitionistic logic in his seminal paper "Semantical Analysis of Intuitionistic Logic I." This work introduced Kripke models consisting of a set of worlds partially ordered by a reflexive and transitive relation , interpreted as a time-like progression where each world represents a stage of knowledge or information. In these models, the truth of atomic propositions is assigned at each world, and once a proposition becomes true at a world, it persists monotonically in all accessible future worlds (i.e., if w w' and the proposition holds at w, it holds at w'). Complex propositions are defined recursively, with conjunction and disjunction behaving classically at each world, while implication and negation reflect the persistent nature of truth, ensuring that a proposition is forced at a world only if it is forced at all future worlds.
Kripke established the soundness and completeness of this semantics for intuitionistic propositional logic, demonstrating that a formula is provable in the intuitionistic system if and only if it is valid in all such models (with completeness holding relative to tree-like frames with a root for the propositional case). For the predicate fragment, the semantics incorporates monotonically expanding domains (D_w D_w' if w w') and relations, yielding soundness and completeness theorems that validate intuitionistic quantifier rules. These results, proven classically but capturing intuitionistic validity, highlight the framework's ability to model the logic's rejection of non-constructive principles.
The Kripke frames bear close connections to other semantic paradigms for intuitionism, such as topological semantics, where the partial order induces a topology on the worlds (with upward-closed sets as open sets) and truth corresponds to membership in open neighborhoods. Algebraically, the models relate to Heyting algebras, where the order structure aligns with the lattice operations of intuitionistic connectives, and prime filters in the algebra correspond to forcing at individual worlds. These links underscore the robustness of Kripke's approach in unifying disparate interpretations of intuitionistic validity.
Kripke's semantics has profound implications for constructive mathematics, as it formalizes the intuitionistic view that truth emerges progressively through evidence, rather than holding timelessly. Central to this is the framework's validation of the rejection of the law of excluded middle (A A), since there can exist worlds where neither A nor its negation is forced, allowing for sentences that are neither provable nor refutable at intermediate stages. This monotonic, evidence-based model thus supports the foundational tenets of intuitionism, emphasizing constructive proofs over classical bivalence.

Philosophy of Language

Naming and Necessity

In 1970, Saul Kripke delivered lectures at Princeton University, which formed the basis of his influential work Naming and Necessity, first published in book form by Harvard University Press in 1980. These lectures fundamentally reshaped debates in the philosophy of language by proposing a direct theory of reference for proper names and certain general terms, emphasizing their role in modal contexts involving necessity and possibility. Kripke's arguments draw on intuitive examples to demonstrate how reference is fixed historically or via causal chains, rather than through contingent speaker associations.
Central to Kripke's critique is the rejection of the descriptivist theory of proper names, originally advanced by Gottlob Frege and Bertrand Russell, which holds that a name's meaning derives from a definite description or cluster of descriptions that uniquely identify its referent. Descriptivism implies that names are synonymous with such descriptions and thus non-rigid, potentially referring to different objects across possible worlds. Kripke counters this by introducing the notion of a rigid designator, defined as an expression that refers to the same object in every possible world in which that object exists, and nothing else in worlds where it does not. He maintains that ordinary proper names function as rigid designators, fixed by an initial "baptism" or reference-fixing event and preserved through a chain of communication, independent of descriptive content supplied by users. This view undermines descriptivism, as it allows names to retain their reference even if associated descriptions fail or change.
Kripke's theory yields the category of necessary a posteriori truths, identities that are metaphysically necessary yet empirically discovered. A paradigmatic example is the statement "Hesperus is Phosphorus," where both names rigidly designate the planet Venus; the identity holds in all possible worlds but was only learned through astronomical observation. Such truths arise because rigid designation ensures that co-referring terms express the same entity modally, while their linkage requires empirical verification. This challenges traditional epistemology, which confined necessity to a priori knowledge.
Kripke also revives Aristotelian essentialism, arguing that individuals possess essential properties that they could not lack without ceasing to be themselves, such as their origin (e.g., the particular sperm and egg from which a person arises) or material constitution (e.g., the atomic structure of a substance like gold). These properties are necessary, holding across all possible worlds, yet often known only a posteriori. For instance, it is essential to Queen Elizabeth II that she be the product of her specific historical parents, even if imagined alternatives seem intuitively possible under descriptivist assumptions.
Finally, Kripke assails the coherence of contingent identity statements involving rigid designators and the descriptivist reliance on descriptions for synonymy. If two rigid designators refer to the same object, their identity cannot be contingent, as it would require the object to exist differently in some possible world, which contradicts rigidity. Similarly, synonymy cannot be established descriptively, since descriptions may contingently apply or fail without affecting reference. These arguments, grounded in the semantics of possible worlds, extend Kripke's earlier work in modal logic to metaphysical conclusions about identity and essence.

Belief and Reference

Kripke argues that standard semantic approaches struggle to resolve these tensions without ad hoc adjustments.
Central to the paper is the "Pierre puzzle," which illustrates a contradiction in belief attribution stemming from disquotational principles. Imagine Pierre, a Frenchman who has never visited London but has seen pictures and read descriptions portraying it as beautiful; he sincerely assents to the French sentence "Londres est jolie" (London is pretty) and thus, by disquotation, believes that London is pretty..pdf) Later, Pierre moves to London (believing it to be a different city called "London," depicted as ugly in his new environment) and, upon learning that "London" refers to the same place as "Londres," sincerely rejects the English sentence "London is pretty." By the same disquotational principle applied to English, he now believes that London is not pretty. This leads to the intuitive but contradictory conclusion that Pierre both believes and does not believe that London is pretty..pdf)
Kripke's puzzle challenges Fregean theories of sense and reference as applied to propositional attitudes, which posit that names contribute a descriptive "sense" to belief contexts to explain failures of substitutivity. Under a Millian viewwhere names are rigid designators contributing only their referent to semantic contentsubstitution of coreferential names in belief reports should preserve truth, yet the Pierre case shows intuitive resistance to such substitutions across linguistic or informational divides..pdf) Kripke contends that Fregean senses fail to capture the causal chains of reference fixation that determine belief contents, leading to paradoxes in attributing attitudes without invoking implausible cognitive distinctions..pdf)
To address the puzzle, Kripke proposes solutions involving "disavowal," where the subject, upon reflection, rejects or qualifies one of the conflicting beliefs to avoid contradiction. For instance, Pierre might disavow his earlier French belief as inapplicable once he identifies "Londres" with "London," or vice versa, depending on contextual priorities..pdf) This approach avoids positing hidden indexical elements or multiple propositions per sentence, instead emphasizing the speaker's reflective stance on their own attitudes. Disavowal highlights the normative pressure on belief ascription, where ordinary language users hesitate to attribute contradictory beliefs even when disquotational evidence suggests otherwise..pdf)

Wittgenstein's Philosophy

Rule-Following Considerations

In his 1982 book Wittgenstein on Rules and Private Language: An Elementary Exposition, Saul Kripke offers a skeptical reading of Ludwig Wittgenstein's Philosophical Investigations, focusing on the problem of how meaning and rule-following are possible. Kripke interprets Wittgenstein as confronting a radical skeptical paradox about linguistic understanding: for any expression, such as the plus sign in arithmetic, there appears to be no determinate fact that fixes its meaning over all possible applications.
The paradox is vividly illustrated through Kripke's example of "quus" or quaddition, a function that coincides with standard addition for pairs of numbers less than 57 but yields 5 for any sum of 57 or larger, such as 68 + 57. Kripke argues that no past behavior, mental state, or disposition of a speakerwhether individual or communalcan conclusively determine that "plus" means addition rather than quus, since any finite history of correct applications (e.g., 2 + 2 = 4) is compatible with both interpretations. Community-wide dispositions fare no better, as they too are limited by finite observations and could align with deviant rules in unforeseen cases.
To resolve this skepticism without lapsing into meaning nihilism, Kripke proposes a "skeptical solution" on Wittgenstein's behalf: there are no semantic facts about what a term means in the traditional sense, but rather facts about what a speaker is justified in asserting regarding its correct use within the communal language game. Meaning thus emerges as a normative notion tied to social practices, where correctness is determined by what the linguistic community would accept as warranted assertions, rather than any hidden essence or interpretation. This approach underscores the insufficiency of solitary rule-following, as an isolated individual lacks the external criteria needed to distinguish genuine adherence from mere coincidence.
Kripke's analysis has profoundly shaped philosophical debates on the normativity of meaning, emphasizing that linguistic rules impose standards of correctness irreducible to descriptive facts about behavior or psychology. It has also bolstered anti-realist positions in semantics, such as those inspired by Michael Dummett, by challenging the idea of meaning as an objective, fact-stating relation independent of communal justification.

Private Language Critique

In his 1982 book Wittgenstein on Rules and Private Language, Saul Kripke offers a detailed interpretation of Ludwig Wittgenstein's private language argument as presented in sections 243271 of Philosophical Investigations. Kripke argues that Wittgenstein demonstrates the impossibility of a private languageone accessible only to its user and based solely on private sensationsbecause it lacks shared criteria for determining the correctness of its use. Without communal norms, there can be no objective way to distinguish between right and wrong applications of terms, rendering the language meaningless in a normative sense.
Central to this interpretation is Wittgenstein's discussion of sensation names, such as "pain," which cannot be adequately defined through private ostensive acts, like pointing inwardly to one's own sensation. Kripke emphasizes that such ostensive definitions fail to establish a stable reference because they rely on unverifiable private experiences that admit no external check; without public norms, the ostension could equally justify any future sensation as "the same" or different, leading to arbitrary usage. This critique underscores that meaning for sensation terms must be grounded in observable, shared practices rather than isolated mental episodes.
Kripke links this argument to broader concerns about rule-following, noting that private rules for using sensation terms are unverifiable, as there is no community to confirm or correct their application, ultimately dooming solipsistic language projects to incoherence. This builds on Wittgenstein's rule-following paradox by highlighting how privacy exacerbates the problem of justification, as no finite set of private checks can guarantee consistent rule adherence.
In response to potential critiques that mental states could provide internal criteria, Kripke stresses Wittgenstein's insistence on behavioral grounding: meaning and correctness are constituted by communal agreement in observable actions, not inscrutable inner processes. This interpretation has faced responses from those who argue it overemphasizes skepticism, but Kripke maintains that it captures Wittgenstein's therapeutic aim of dissolving philosophical confusions about privacy.
Kripke's reading has profoundly influenced philosophy of mind, challenging Cartesian views of private mental content as foundational to language, and epistemology, by shifting emphasis from individual certainty to social justification for knowledge claims about sensations. It has spurred debates on whether mental states can ever be truly private or if all linguistic understanding requires intersubjective validation.

Theory of Truth

Paradox Resolution

In his 1975 lectures, later published as "Outline of a Theory of Truth," Saul Kripke developed a novel approach to resolving semantic paradoxes, particularly the liar paradox. The classic liar sentence, such as "This sentence is false," generates a paradox because assuming it is true implies it is false, and vice versa, leading to an infinite regress or contradiction within standard bivalent logic. Kripke's solution introduces a distinction between grounded and ungrounded sentences to accommodate self-reference without forcing every sentence into a true-or-false dichotomy.
Kripke proposes that truth predicates can be defined through fixed points of monotone operators applied to partial orders over sentences, allowing for a structured evaluation that handles circularity. This framework builds on partial logic, where sentences may lack a truth value rather than being assigned one arbitrarily. Grounded truths are those constructed hierarchically from atomic factssuch as empirical observations like "snow is white"without circular dependence on the truth predicate itself, ensuring their values are determined step by step in a minimal fixed point.
Ungrounded sentences, including those involved in the liar paradox, receive no truth value, creating "gaps" in the assignment rather than contradictions; for instance, a self-referential claim like "(3) is true" referring to itself remains indeterminate. This approach avoids Alfred Tarski's hierarchical stratification of languages, which prohibits self-reference by requiring separate truth predicates for each level, instead permitting a single truth predicate where sentences effectively "seek their own level" based on underlying facts. By tolerating truth-value gaps, Kripke's theory maintains expressive power for natural language while sidestepping paradox.

Semantic Frameworks

Kripke's semantic framework for a theory of truth employs a fixed-point construction to define truth predicates in languages capable of self-reference, avoiding the inconsistencies of fully recursive definitions. The construction begins with an initial empty assignment where the truth predicate $ T $ is undefined for all sentences, establishing a partial interpretation $ (S_1^0, S_2^0) $ with both sets empty. The T-schema is then iteratively applied: for each successor stage $ \alpha + 1 $, the sets $ S_1^{\alpha+1} $ and $ S_2^{\alpha+1} $ are formed by evaluating ground sentencesthose whose truth values depend only on atomic facts and prior assignmentsyielding true sentences in $ S_1^{\alpha+1} $ and false sentences in $ S_2^{\alpha+1} $. This process continues through transfinite ordinals until reaching a minimal fixed point at some ordinal $ \mu $, where $ (S_1^\mu, S_2^\mu) = (S_1^{\mu+1}, S_2^{\mu+1}) $, providing the least partial truth assignment closed under the T-schema.
In the formal language extended with the truth predicate $ T $, atomic sentences of the form $ T(\ulcorner p \urcorner) $ are evaluated directly: $ T(\ulcorner p \urcorner) $ holds if $ p $ is true relative to the current assignment, fails if $ p $ is false, and remains undefined otherwise. For compound sentences, evaluation extends recursively using a three-valued logic, where connectives like negation and conjunction propagate truth values: for instance, $ \neg \phi $ is true if $ \phi $ is false, false if $ \phi $ is true, and undefined if $ \phi $ is undefined. The core of the framework is the monotonic operator $ \tau $, which maps a partial hypothesis $ X $ (a set of sentences assumed true) to the set of all ground sentences that are true relative to $ X $, formally $ \tau(X) = { \phi \mid \phi \text{ is ground and true given } X } $. The minimal fixed point is then the least set $ S $ such that $ S = \tau(S) $, obtained as the intersection of all fixed points or via the iterative limit.
This construction accommodates strengthened liar sentences, such as those asserting their own falsity in a more robust manner, by rendering them ungrounded and thus undefined in the minimal fixed point; their status becomes contingent on empirical or contextual factors that may ground them in further iterations or alternative fixed points. For example, a sentence like "This sentence is not true" remains undefined unless additional assumptions assign it a value in a non-minimal extension.
Kripke outlines variants to handle ungrounded sentences more finely. In the Strong Kleene scheme, undefined sentences receive third values that propagate conservatively through connectives, preserving bivalence for grounded expressions while allowing partiality for paradoxes. Alternatively, supervaluation assigns truth to a sentence if it is true in every total bivalent extension of the partial assignment, falsity if false in all such extensions, and neither otherwise, thus treating ungroundedness as a form of vagueness without introducing a dedicated third value.

Legacy

Kripke Center

The Saul Kripke Center was established in 2007 at the Graduate Center of the City University of New York (CUNY) to preserve, publish, and promote the philosophical and logical work of Saul A. Kripke, who joined the institution as a distinguished professor in 2003. The center serves as the primary repository for Kripke's intellectual legacy, housing an extensive archive that includes unpublished manuscripts, lecture notes from seminars dating back to the 1970s, audio recordings of his lectures and discussions, and correspondence with fellow philosophers. This collection enables scholars to access Kripke's evolving ideas on topics such as modal logic, philosophy of language, and theories of truth, supporting ongoing research into his contributions.
Directed by Yale Weiss, a CUNY Graduate Center alumnus, with Romina Padr Birman serving as Kripke's literary executor and a CUNY Graduate Center alumna, the center emphasizes areas central to his scholarship, including logic, metaphysics, epistemology, and philosophy of language. It actively fosters academic engagement through initiatives like the Kripke Center Fellowships, which provide funding and resources for Ph.D. students in philosophy at the Graduate Center to conduct research on Kripke-related themes. Additionally, the center hosts regular events, such as the annual Young Scholars Series lectures featuring emerging researchers, colloquia with established Kripke scholars, and international conferences; a notable upcoming event is the 2025 conference "Beyond the Outline: A Celebration of Fifty Years of Kripke on Truth," commemorating his seminal Outline of a Theory of Truth.
The center promotes public and scholarly access to its holdings by maintaining an open archive for in-person consultations and offering digital copies of specific materials upon request, while ongoing digitization efforts aim to make recordings and notes more widely available online. These resources not only safeguard Kripke's unpublished works but also facilitate events and publications that highlight his enduring influence, such as edited volumes drawn from the archives.

Awards and Influence

Kripke received the Rolf Schock Prize in Logic and Philosophy in 2001 from the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences, a prestigious award often considered the philosophical equivalent of the Nobel Prize. He was also honored with Princeton University's Behrman Award for distinguished achievement in the humanities in 1988. Additionally, Kripke earned multiple honorary degrees, including a Doctor of Humane Letters from the University of Nebraska in 1977, from Johns Hopkins University in 1997, from the University of Haifa in 1998, and from the University of Pennsylvania in 2005.
Kripke's innovations in modal logic, particularly his possible worlds semantics, revolutionized the field and extended its applications to linguistics and artificial intelligence, providing foundational tools for analyzing necessity, possibility, and counterfactual reasoning. His seminal ideas advanced debates on essentialism about natural kinds and identities, reshaped semantic theories of reference and meaning, and profoundly influenced Wittgenstein studies, especially through skeptical interpretations of rule-following and private language arguments. With over 47,000 citations documented across his body of work, Kripke inspired the development of direct reference theory in the philosophy of language, emphasizing causal-historical chains over descriptive content for proper names and natural kind terms.
Following Kripke's death in 2022, posthumous tributes included widespread memorials in academic journals and institutions, such as the Bulletin of Symbolic Logic's dedicated issue. A major Saul Kripke Memorial Conference was held at the CUNY Graduate Center in May 2023, featuring leading philosophers discussing his legacy, with ongoing conferences and events continuing to explore and extend his contributions to logic and metaphysics.
