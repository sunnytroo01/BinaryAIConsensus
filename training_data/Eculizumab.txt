Eculizumab
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Eculizumab, marketed as Soliris, is a recombinant humanized monoclonal IgG2/4 antibody that specifically binds to complement protein C5, inhibiting its cleavage into C5a and C5b subcomponents and thereby preventing the generation of the proinflammatory anaphylatoxin C5a and the terminal membrane attack complex C5b-9. Approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration in 2007, it is indicated for reducing hemolysis in paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH), inhibiting complement-mediated thrombotic microangiopathy in atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome (aHUS, excluding Shiga toxin E. coli-related cases), treating anti-acetylcholine receptor antibody-positive generalized myasthenia gravis in patients aged 6 years and older, and managing anti-aquaporin-4 antibody-positive neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder in adults. As the first complement C5 inhibitor approved for clinical use, eculizumab has markedly improved outcomes in these rare, life-threatening conditions by targeting dysregulated terminal complement activation, though treatment requires prior meningococcal vaccination and enrollment in a risk evaluation and mitigation strategy due to heightened susceptibility to Neisseria meningitidis infections. Biosimilars such as eculizumab-aagh (Epysqli) and eculizumab-cakn (Bkemv) have since gained approval, expanding access while maintaining comparable efficacy and safety profiles.

History and development

Discovery and early research

The discovery of eculizumab stemmed from foundational research on complement inhibition dating to the late 1980s, when scientists developed BB5.1, the first monoclonal antibody targeting murine complement component C5. This antibody demonstrated in rodent models that blocking C5 cleavage could prevent terminal complement activation, inhibiting the generation of anaphylatoxin C5a and the membrane attack complex (MAC) while sparing upstream complement functions like opsonization.
Alexion Pharmaceuticals advanced this concept into therapeutic development by engineering antibodies specific to human and primate C5, culminating in the humanization of the anti-C5 monoclonal antibody into h5G1.1 (eculizumab), a recombinant IgG2/4 kappa immunoglobulin. Preclinical studies confirmed eculizumab's high-affinity binding to human C5, selectively halting its proteolytic cleavage in vitro and in animal models of complement-mediated lysis, with evidence of reduced proinflammatory and cytolytic effects relevant to diseases like paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH).
Early research emphasized the causal role of dysregulated terminal complement in PNH pathogenesis, where deficiency of glycosylphosphatidylinositol-anchored regulators exposes erythrocytes to MAC-mediated intravascular hemolysis; eculizumab's targeted inhibition was shown to preserve protective opsonophagocytic activity, supporting its potential as a precise therapeutic without broad immunosuppression.

Clinical trials leading to approval

The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved eculizumab on March 16, 2007, for reducing hemolysis in adults with paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH), based primarily on results from the pivotal phase 3 TRIUMPH trial (C04-001). This multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled study enrolled 87 transfusion-dependent adult patients with PNH who had evidence of hemolysis (lactate dehydrogenase levels at least 1.5 times the upper limit of normal). Participants received loading doses of eculizumab (600 mg weekly for 4 weeks) followed by maintenance doses of 900 mg every 14 days, or matching placebo, for 26 weeks.
The primary endpoint was the percentage change in LDH levels from baseline to the average during the trial period, reflecting reduction in intravascular hemolysis. Eculizumab treatment resulted in a mean 87.2% reduction in LDH (to near-normal levels in 96% of patients), compared to a 4.3% increase in the placebo group (P<0.001). Secondary endpoints included transfusion independence, achieved by 51% of eculizumab-treated patients versus 0% in placebo (P<0.0001), and improvement in quality-of-life measures such as fatigue scores. Eculizumab also stabilized hemoglobin levels and reduced major adverse vascular events, though the trial was not powered for thrombotic outcomes.
Supporting data included a phase 2 pilot study (C02) in 11 PNH patients, which demonstrated feasibility and early efficacy with a 12-week open-label regimen, showing LDH reductions of over 75% and decreased transfusion needs. The phase 3 SHEPHERD trial (C04-002), an open-label study in 97 PNH patients (including those transfusion-independent at baseline), provided additional evidence of sustained hemolysis control and safety across broader populations, with LDH normalization in 85% of treated patients after 52 weeks. These trials collectively established eculizumab's efficacy in blocking terminal complement activation via C5 inhibition, addressing the underlying pathophysiology of PNH without increasing infection risks beyond baseline expectations in this vulnerable population. Adverse events were similar between groups, with no new meningococcal infections reported during the blinded phase, though vaccination was required per protocol.

Post-approval expansions and milestones

In 2011, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) expanded eculizumab's approval to include atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome (aHUS) on September 23, encompassing both adult and pediatric patients to inhibit complement-mediated thrombotic microangiopathy. This marked the drug's initial post-approval indication beyond paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH), supported by phase 2 trial data demonstrating reductions in hemolysis and renal impairment.
Subsequent expansions targeted neurological disorders. On October 23, 2017, the FDA approved eculizumab for generalized myasthenia gravis (gMG) in adults positive for anti-acetylcholine receptor antibodies who are refractory to other therapies, based on the phase 3 REGAIN trial showing improved muscle strength scores. On June 27, 2019, approval extended to aquaporin-4 antibody-positive neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder (NMOSD) in adults, the first complement inhibitor for this condition, following the phase 3 PREVENT trial that reduced relapse risk by 94% versus placebo. 
Pediatric applications further broadened access. The European Medicines Agency (EMA) approved eculizumab for refractory gMG in children and adolescents aged 6 years and older on July 27, 2023, addressing a population with limited options. The FDA followed with expanded approval for the same pediatric gMG cohort on March 10, 2025, enabling use in patients aged 6 and older with anti-AChR-positive refractory disease. 
A key regulatory milestone occurred in May 2024 with FDA approval of the first eculizumab biosimilar (eculizumab-aeeb, Bkemv), interchangeable for PNH and aHUS, reflecting the original drug's established safety profile from over 15 years of post-marketing surveillance. These developments underscore eculizumab's role in complement-mediated diseases, with ongoing label updates incorporating real-world evidence on meningococcal risk mitigation via vaccination and prophylaxis.

Medical indications

Primary approved uses

Eculizumab, sold under the brand name Soliris, is approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH), a rare acquired clonal hematopoietic stem cell disorder caused by somatic mutations in the PIGA gene, leading to complement-mediated intravascular hemolysis, bone marrow failure, and increased risk of thrombosis. The initial FDA approval for PNH occurred on March 16, 2007, for adults, with the indication to reduce hemolysis based on phase 3 trials (e.g., Sheffield and TRIUMPH studies) showing mean lactate dehydrogenase reductions of 86-88% and decreased transfusion dependence from 51-54% to 0-8% of patients.
The drug is also approved for atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome (aHUS), a rare systemic thrombotic microangiopathy driven by dysregulation of the alternative complement pathway, resulting in endothelial damage, microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, and acute kidney injury. FDA approval for aHUS was granted on September 23, 2011, initially for adults to inhibit complement-mediated thrombotic microangiopathy, including in patients refractory to plasma therapy, with pediatric approval following on October 16, 2012; pivotal evidence from prospective trials demonstrated normalization of platelet counts in 81% of adults and 60% of children, alongside improvements in renal function in 76% of cases.
These primary indications target chronic complement activation in PNH and aHUS, where eculizumab's blockade of C5 cleavage prevents membrane attack complex formation, addressing the underlying pathophysiology without curing the genetic defects.

Expanded indications and pediatric applications

In 2017, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) expanded eculizumab's indications to include treatment of generalized myasthenia gravis (gMG) in anti-acetylcholine receptor (AChR) antibody-positive adults, based on phase 3 trial data demonstrating significant reductions in myasthenia gravis activities of daily living scores compared to placebo. In June 2019, the FDA further approved eculizumab for adults with anti-aquaporin-4 (AQP4) antibody-positive neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder (NMOSD), supported by the PREVENT trial showing an 89% relative risk reduction in relapse rates versus placebo over a median 14.7 months. 
Pediatric applications have been progressively expanded. For atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome (aHUS), eculizumab is approved for patients aged 2 months and older, with dosing adjusted by body weight; efficacy in children stems from pharmacokinetic and safety data extrapolated from adults, supplemented by pediatric cohort analyses showing sustained platelet normalization and reduced dialysis needs. For paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH), approval covers all ages, including children, following demonstrations of hemolysis control in pediatric subsets of post-marketing studies. In February 2025, the FDA extended approval to pediatric patients aged 6 years and older with anti-AChR antibody-positive gMG, informed by pediatric data from the REGAIN open-label extension trial, which reported improvements in Quantitative Myasthenia Gravis scores and reduced rescue medication use. No pediatric approval exists for NMOSD.

Pharmacology

Mechanism of action

Eculizumab is a recombinant humanized monoclonal immunoglobulin G2/4 kappa antibody that specifically binds with high affinity to the complement protein C5, a key component of the terminal complement pathway in the innate immune system. This binding sterically hinders the proteolytic cleavage of C5 by upstream convertases (C5 convertase from classical, lectin, or alternative pathways), preventing the release of the proinflammatory anaphylatoxin C5a and the initiation of C5b.
By inhibiting C5b generation, eculizumab blocks the subsequent assembly of the membrane attack complex (MAC, or C5b-9), a pore-forming cytolytic structure that inserts into target cell membranes, leading to cell lysis. This terminal inhibition preserves upstream complement functions, such as opsonization via C3b and immune complex clearance via C4b, which are critical for host defense against pathogens. In conditions like paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH), where red blood cells lack glycosylphosphatidylinositol-anchored complement regulators (e.g., CD55 and CD59), eculizumab thereby interrupts complement-mediated intravascular hemolysis without broadly suppressing adaptive immunity.
The drug's action is highly selective for human C5 and does not cross-react with rodent or nonhuman primate homologs, limiting preclinical model utility but confirming its precision in clinical settings. Incomplete inhibition can occur in some patients due to polymorphisms in C5 (e.g., p.Arg885His), which reduce binding affinity and allow persistent low-level MAC formation, potentially contributing to suboptimal responses. Overall, eculizumab's mechanism reduces both lytic damage and C5a-driven inflammation, such as endothelial activation and thrombosis risk in complement dysregulation disorders.

Pharmacokinetics and administration

Eculizumab is administered exclusively via intravenous infusion, with dilution to a final concentration of 5 mg/mL prior to administration; it must not be given as an intravenous push or bolus injection. Infusion durations typically range from 25 to 45 minutes in adults and patients over 12 years, extended to a maximum of 2 hours for those over 12 years and 4 hours for younger pediatric patients if needed. Dosing regimens vary by indication, patient weight, and treatment phase: for paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH) in adults, a loading dose of 600 mg weekly for the first 4 weeks is followed by 900 mg every 14 days; for atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome (aHUS), initial dosing is 900 mg weekly for 4 weeks, then 1200 mg every 14 days, with adjustments for body weight under 40 kg. Supplemental doses are required within 60 minutes after plasmapheresis or plasma exchange sessions to replenish removed drug, scaled to the most recent dose (e.g., 300 mg per session if the prior dose was 300 mg).
As an intravenously administered monoclonal antibody, eculizumab exhibits complete bioavailability with no absorption phase. Its distribution is primarily confined to the vascular compartment, with a steady-state volume of distribution ranging from 5.1 to 7.7 liters, approximating plasma volume due to its large molecular size (~148 kDa). Metabolism occurs via proteolytic catabolism into small peptides and amino acids by the reticuloendothelial system and lysosomal enzymes, without cytochrome P450 involvement; renal excretion is negligible except in cases of heavy proteinuria. 
Elimination follows target-mediated disposition, best described by a two-compartment pharmacokinetic model with nonlinear clearance at low concentrations due to complement C5 binding, transitioning to linear pharmacokinetics at therapeutic levels. The terminal half-life averages 272 hours (approximately 11.3 days), with clearance rates of 1526 mL/hour in adults, unaffected by age across pediatric (from 2 months) to elderly patients (up to 85 years) or mild-to-moderate hepatic/renal impairment. Steady-state concentrations are achieved by approximately day 57 with standard maintenance dosing.

Chemical structure and properties

Eculizumab is a recombinant humanized monoclonal immunoglobulin G2/4 kappa antibody consisting of two heavy chains, each comprising 448 amino acids, and two light chains, each comprising 214 amino acids. The antibody is produced via recombinant DNA technology in murine myeloma (NS0) cell culture and purified using standard bioprocessing techniques, including protein A affinity chromatography and ion exchange chromatography. Its structure features complementarity-determining regions (CDRs) derived from the murine anti-human C5 monoclonal antibody 5G1.1, with the framework regions humanized to reduce immunogenicity while retaining binding affinity to complement protein C5.
The molecular weight of eculizumab is approximately 148 kDa, determined by methods such as size-exclusion chromatography and mass spectrometry, reflecting its glycoprotein nature with N-linked glycosylation sites on the Fc region. Physicochemical analyses confirm high purity (>99%) and stability under refrigerated storage (28C), with formulation in a phosphate-buffered saline solution containing human serum albumin and polysorbate 80 to prevent aggregation and maintain solubility. The isoelectric point (pI) ranges from 7.8 to 8.2, and it exhibits a typical monoclonal antibody hydrodynamic radius of about 56 nm in solution. These properties ensure effective intravenous administration and prolonged serum half-life.

Clinical efficacy

Evidence from pivotal trials

The pivotal trials establishing eculizumab's efficacy for paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH) centered on the phase 3 TRIUMPH study, a multicenter, double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial involving 87 transfusion-dependent adults with PNH (defined as 4 transfusions in the prior year and 10% PNH type III erythrocytes). Patients received eculizumab (n=43) or placebo (n=44) intravenously every two weeks after a loading dose. The co-primary endpoints were hemoglobin stabilization without transfusions and reduction in packed red blood cell units transfused over 26 weeks. Eculizumab achieved hemoglobin stabilization in 49% of patients versus 0% with placebo (P<0.001), with median transfusions of 0 units versus 10 units (P<0.001). Lactate dehydrogenase levels, a marker of hemolysis, decreased by 85.8% in area under the curve compared to placebo (P<0.001). Serious adverse events occurred in 4 eculizumab patients versus 9 placebo patients, with none deemed treatment-related. These results supported FDA approval for PNH in March 2007.
For atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome (aHUS), efficacy was demonstrated in two prospective, open-label phase 2 trials enrolling 37 patients aged 12 years and weighing 40 kg, with progressive thrombotic microangiopathy (TMA) in one cohort (n=17) or chronic kidney disease requiring plasma exchange in the other (n=20). Patients received eculizumab every two weeks following vaccination and antibiotic prophylaxis. Primary endpoints included platelet count normalization and TMA event-free status at week 26. In the progressive TMA cohort, mean platelet counts increased by 73 10/L (P<0.001), with 88% achieving hematologic normalization; 80% in the chronic cohort were TMA event-free at median 62 weeks' follow-up. Secondary renal outcomes showed mean estimated glomerular filtration rate increases of 32 ml/min/1.73 m and 6 ml/min/1.73 m at week 26, respectively, with dialysis discontinued in 4 of 5 dependent patients in the first cohort. No meningococcal infections occurred, though serious adverse events were reported in all progressive TMA patients and half of the chronic cohort, primarily disease-related. These findings underpinned FDA approval for aHUS in September 2011.

Real-world outcomes and long-term data

In paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH), real-world data from the International PNH Registry, encompassing over 14 years of follow-up for more than 2,000 patients, indicate that eculizumab treatment is associated with a 49% relative improvement in overall survival compared to untreated historical cohorts and an approximate 60% reduction in the risk of thrombotic events or major adverse vascular events (TE/MAVE). Korean Health Insurance Review and Assessment Service data from 200 patients treated between 2014 and 2020 further demonstrate sustained control of intravascular hemolysis, with lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) levels normalizing in 85% of cases by 12 months and remaining stable over five years, alongside reduced transfusion requirements and improved hemoglobin levels. However, a subset of patientsup to 30% in some analysesexperience persistent anemia, breakthrough hemolysis, or suboptimal outcomes despite therapy, highlighting incomplete disease control in real-world settings.
For atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome (aHUS), post-approval surveillance and registry data confirm eculizumab's long-term efficacy in preserving renal function, with a Global aHUS Registry analysis of over 1,400 patients showing dialysis independence achieved or maintained in 80-90% of treated cases after two years, and sustained eGFR improvements in non-dialysis-dependent patients over five years. A French real-world study of 100 adults reported complete thrombotic microangiopathy (TMA) response in 71% within three months, with 65% maintaining response at 24 months, though relapse occurred in 15% upon discontinuation. Long-term safety profiles remain consistent with clinical trials, with meningococcal infections occurring at rates below 1% in vaccinated populations, and no new signals of organ toxicity emerging in extended follow-up beyond seven years.
Across indications, discontinuation studies reveal high relapse ratesexceeding 80% within 12 weeks for PNH and 50% for aHUSunderscoring the need for ongoing suppression of complement activity, as evidenced by prospective withdrawal data integrated into real-world registries. Overall, these outcomes affirm eculizumab's role in transforming prognosis from historical untreated mortality rates of 35% at five years in PNH to near-normal survival under treatment, though individual variability necessitates monitoring for incomplete responders.

Safety and risks

Common and serious adverse effects

Common adverse effects of eculizumab, observed in clinical trials across indications such as paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH) and atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome (aHUS), include headache (reported in 44% of PNH patients and 41% in aHUS), nasopharyngitis (23% in PNH and 27% in aHUS), back pain (19% in PNH), nausea (16% in PNH and 23% in aHUS), diarrhea (37% in aHUS), hypertension (33% in aHUS), abdominal pain (19% in aHUS), vomiting (30% in aHUS), cough (23% in aHUS), and pyrexia (21% in aHUS). These effects were generally mild to moderate and occurred more frequently during the initial dosing phase.
Serious adverse effects primarily involve increased susceptibility to infections due to terminal complement inhibition, with meningococcal infections (caused by Neisseria meningitidis) being the most critical, including cases of sepsis and septic shock that have resulted in fatalities even among vaccinated patients. In PNH trials, two cases of meningococcal sepsis occurred in 196 patients, despite vaccination. Other serious risks include infusion-related reactions such as anaphylaxis or hypersensitivity (leading to discontinuation in rare cases, e.g., 0.9% in generalized myasthenia gravis trials), as well as post-marketing reports of gonococcal infections, encapsulated bacterial infections (Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae), and cholestatic liver injury in aHUS patients. In aHUS studies, serious events affected 60% of patients, with infections (24%), hypertension (5%), and renal impairment (5%) prominent. Mitigation requires pre-treatment meningococcal vaccination (at least two weeks prior) and prophylactic antibiotics if needed, with immediate evaluation for signs like fever or rash.

Infection risks and mitigation strategies

Eculizumab inhibits the terminal complement pathway by binding to C5, thereby increasing susceptibility to infections caused by encapsulated bacteria, particularly Neisseria meningitidis, due to impaired bactericidal activity. Patients receiving eculizumab face a 1,000- to 2,000-fold higher risk of invasive meningococcal disease compared to the general population, with an incidence of 2.7 to 3.0 cases per 1,000 person-years and a case-fatality rate of approximately 10% (0.28 deaths per 1,000 person-years). This elevated risk persists even in vaccinated individuals, as eculizumab can override vaccine-induced immunity by preventing complement-mediated lysis of the pathogen. Life-threatening meningococcal sepsis has been reported, sometimes rapidly fatal within hours of symptom onset, prompting a black box warning in the drug's labeling.
Other infections linked to eculizumab include those from Streptococcus pneumoniae and Haemophilus influenzae type b, especially in pediatric patients, though meningococcal infections predominate and account for the majority of serious cases. Real-world data indicate that meningococcal events occur at a rate of about 0.5% per year of treatment, with cumulative risk reaching 5% after 10 years.
Mitigation begins with mandatory vaccination against meningococcal serogroups A, C, Y, W-135 (using quadrivalent conjugate vaccines) and serogroup B (using recombinant protein vaccines) at least two weeks prior to initiating eculizumab, as recommended by the FDA and CDC. For patients unable to vaccinate in advance, the FDA advises two weeks of antibiotic prophylaxis (e.g., penicillin or ciprofloxacin) while awaiting immunity development, though some guidelines, such as those in the UK and France, endorse lifelong prophylaxis due to breakthrough risks. Enrollment in a Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy (REMS) program is required for prescribers and patients, involving education on early infection signs (e.g., fever, headache, stiff neck) and immediate antibiotic access for suspected cases. Ongoing monitoring includes annual revaccination boosters and serological testing in high-risk scenarios, though antibody responses may be suboptimal under eculizumab therapy. Discontinuation of eculizumab for active meningococcal infection is advised, with prompt administration of broad-spectrum antibiotics and supportive care critical for survival.

Controversies and criticisms

Pricing and market access issues

Eculizumab, marketed as Soliris, has been associated with exceptionally high pricing since its launch, with annual treatment costs typically exceeding $500,000 per patient after the initial year. In pharmacoeconomic analyses, the cost at list prices has been estimated at approximately $701,168 annually for ongoing therapy, based on a vial price of $6,742 per 300 mg dose. First-year costs can reach $728,136 due to loading doses and induction requirements. These figures position eculizumab among the most expensive drugs globally, a status attributed to its orphan drug designation for rare conditions like paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH) and atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome (aHUS), which limit the patient population and recoupment of development expenses through high per-patient pricing.
Criticisms of eculizumab's pricing focus on its sustainability and justification, particularly given the small eligible patient poolestimated in the hundreds to low thousands worldwideand the drug's indefinite lifelong administration. Alexion Pharmaceuticals, the original developer, set an initial U.S. annual price of $409,500 in 2007, which has risen over time amid debates over whether such premiums adequately reflect R&D costs or exploit regulatory incentives like market exclusivity. Orphan drug policies, intended to encourage innovation for rare diseases, have enabled prices far exceeding those of non-orphan therapies, leading to concerns about a "pricing bubble" where costs outpace clinical value. In the U.K., the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) approved coverage in 2014 despite incomplete cost transparency from the manufacturer, highlighting tensions between efficacy and fiscal burden on public systems.
Market access barriers stem primarily from these costs, resulting in variable insurance coverage and patient out-of-pocket burdens. In the U.S., private insurers like Aetna require prior authorization and evidence of inadequate response to alternatives, but high deductibles and copays can impose significant financial toxicity. Globally, access is uneven: while government-funded programs cover eculizumab in select high-income countries such as Canada and parts of Europe, many patients in low- and middle-income regions face outright denial due to infrastructure limitations and pricing. Even with biosimilars entering markets post-patent expiration, projected discounts of 30% would maintain annual costs above $350,000, insufficient to broadly resolve access inequities. These dynamics have prompted calls for value-based pricing models tied to outcomes, though implementation remains limited.

Debates on long-term use and discontinuation

The debate surrounding long-term eculizumab use primarily revolves around its indications in paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH), where chronic therapy is generally favored due to sustained efficacy and lack of cumulative toxicity over extended periods, contrasted with atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome (aHUS), where remission in some patients raises questions about indefinite treatment versus monitored discontinuation. In PNH, real-world data indicate that prolonged administration maintains hemolysis control and survival benefits, with a 49% relative improvement in survival and 60% reduction in thrombotic events, without emerging safety signals beyond initial infection risks. However, in aHUS, regulatory guidance often recommends lifelong therapy to prevent thrombotic microangiopathy (TMA) recurrence, yet empirical outcomes show variable relapse rates post-discontinuation, ranging from 20% in clinician-monitored protocols to higher in cases of non-adherence or genetic predisposition to complement dysregulation. 
Discontinuation feasibility has been explored in multicenter studies, particularly for aHUS patients achieving clinical remission, with median off-therapy follow-up exceeding two years in some cohorts; relapse odds decrease by approximately 76% with continued treatment (odds ratio 0.24, 95% CI 0.09-0.62), underscoring a causal link between complement inhibition cessation and TMA rebound, though salvage reinitiation proves effective in most relapses. Genetic profiling influences outcomes, as patients without identifiable complement mutations exhibit lower recurrence risks upon withdrawal, supporting individualized strategies over blanket lifelong use. Conversely, premature cessation in native-kidney aHUS cases has demonstrated safety and cost savings in select trials, with no dialysis dependence in remitters followed for over a year, challenging the default indefinite paradigm amid ongoing infection prophylaxis burdens. Economic analyses further highlight that withdrawal with monitoring dominates lifelong therapy in cost-effectiveness models for aHUS, projecting substantial savings without disproportionate renal deterioration, though these hinge on vigilant surveillance to mitigate relapse.
Critics of prolonged use emphasize persistent immunosuppression risks, including meningococcal susceptibility despite vaccination, which do not abate over time, juxtaposed against evidence that eculizumab's benefit-risk profile strengthens with duration in responsive diseases like neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder (NMOSD), where long-term data affirm relapse prevention without novel toxicities. In generalized myasthenia gravis (gMG), sustained effectiveness persists across classifications, but duration remains empirically guided rather than protocol-driven. Overall, while discontinuation protocols yield low relapse in pediatric aHUS subsets (median 23.6 months off-therapy), adult data reveal higher TMA recurrence, informing a cautious approach: empirical remission plus genetics favor trialed withdrawal under monitoring, whereas high-risk profiles necessitate indefinite therapy to avert end-organ damage. This tension persists absent definitive prospective trials stratifying by etiology, with current evidence privileging sustained inhibition for relapse-prone cases over speculative finite courses.

Regulatory and commercial aspects

Global approvals and legal status

Eculizumab, marketed as Soliris, was first approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) on March 16, 2007, for the treatment of paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH) in adults. Subsequent FDA approvals expanded its indications to include atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome (aHUS) in 2011, refractory generalized myasthenia gravis (gMG) in adults in 2017, and neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder (NMOSD) in anti-aquaporin-4 (AQP4) antibody-positive adults in 2019. In the United States, its legal status includes a Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy (REMS) program, mandated since 2010, requiring prescriber certification, patient enrollment, and meningococcal vaccination to mitigate serious infection risks.
The European Medicines Agency (EMA) authorized eculizumab on June 20, 2007, initially for PNH in adults, with orphan designation granted earlier in 2003. Expansions in the European Union followed for aHUS (orphan designation 2009), gMG (orphan designation 2014), and NMOSD (orphan designation 2019), covering adults and pediatric patients where applicable. EU marketing authorization stipulates prescription-only use under specialist supervision, with contraindications for unresolved Neisseria meningitidis infections and mandatory vaccination plus antibiotic prophylaxis.
Approvals in other jurisdictions include Canada, where Health Canada approved it for PNH in January 2009 and aHUS in 2013; Australia, with initial Therapeutic Goods Administration approval on March 20, 2009; and Japan, where the Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare granted marketing approval for PNH prior to its third-quarter 2010 launch, followed by gMG in 2017 and NMOSD in 2019. More recently, China's National Medical Products Administration approved it for refractory gMG in June 2023 and NMOSD in October 2023.

Biosimilars and market competition

In May 2024, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved Bkemv (eculizumab-aeeb) from Amgen as the first interchangeable biosimilar to Soliris (eculizumab), indicated for paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH) and atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome (aHUS) in adults and children. In July 2024, the FDA approved EPYSQLI (eculizumab-aagh, also known as SB12) from Samsung Bioepis, marketed by Teva Pharmaceuticals, as a second interchangeable biosimilar for the same indications, with commercial availability in the U.S. beginning in April 2025. 
In Europe, the European Medicines Agency (EMA) approved Bekemv (ABP 959) from Amgen in 2023 as a biosimilar to Soliris for PNH and aHUS. Epysqli (SB12) from Samsung Bioepis received EMA approval in May 2023 for these indications, with phase 3 trials demonstrating pharmacokinetic, pharmacodynamic, and efficacy similarity to the reference product across diverse patient populations. These approvals followed abbreviated pathways relying on analytical, nonclinical, and clinical data showing no clinically meaningful differences in safety, purity, or potency compared to Soliris.
The entry of these biosimilars coincides with the expiration of key Soliris patents in 2025, ending nearly two decades of market exclusivity for AstraZeneca's Alexion unit and introducing direct competition in major markets. Soliris generated $2.5 billion in global sales in 2024, down 18% from the prior year, with projections indicating up to 50% pricing declines by 2032 in regions like North America and Europe due to biosimilar uptake and negotiation pressures. Biosimilars have captured an average of 52% market share within five years of U.S. launch in other categories, though adoption for rare disease treatments like eculizumab may lag due to physician familiarity with the originator and ongoing patent litigation alleging improper extensions by AstraZeneca. 
This competition is expected to enhance access for patients with complement-mediated rare diseases by reducing costs, as biosimilars typically launch at 20-30% discounts initially, fostering broader utilization without compromising therapeutic outcomes demonstrated in pivotal studies. However, market dynamics remain influenced by payer preferences for interchangeability designations, which facilitate automatic substitution in some U.S. states, and global variations in reimbursement policies that could temper rapid erosion of the originator's share.

Economic impact and pricing dynamics

Eculizumab, marketed as Soliris by Alexion Pharmaceuticals (acquired by AstraZeneca in 2021), commands an annual wholesale acquisition cost exceeding $500,000 per patient, with estimates ranging from $470,000 to $700,000 depending on indication, body weight, and dosing frequency. This pricing structure, set upon U.S. approval in 2007 for paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria, leverages the drug's orphan designation for ultra-rare conditions, where per-patient costs are justified by manufacturers against small patient populations and high unmet needs, though critics argue it reflects market tolerance rather than proportional value.
The economic impact on healthcare payers is profound, contributing to strained budgets in systems reliant on public or insurance funding for rare diseases. Cost-effectiveness models indicate incremental costs of over $1.5 million per quality-adjusted life year gained versus supportive care alone, rendering it unfavorable under standard thresholds like $50,000$150,000 per QALY in analyses for conditions such as atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome. Lifetime treatment expenses can surpass $5.5 million per patient, exacerbating disparities in access; in lower-resource settings, high costs result in untreated patients or therapy rationing, while U.S. payers negotiate rebates that reduce net prices but still burden formularies. Independent assessments, including by the Institute for Clinical and Economic Review, peg value-based benchmarks at $13,200$19,400 annually for myasthenia gravis, implying the list price exceeds reasonable returns by 9798%.
Pricing dynamics have evolved amid regulatory and competitive pressures, with Soliris revenues peaking above $3 billion annually pre-2020 before declining to $2.5 billion in 2024 due to uptake of longer-acting alternatives like ravulizumab and emerging biosimilars. Patent disputes and antitrust claims allege tactics to delay generics, sustaining elevated prices, while bodies like Canada's Patented Medicine Prices Review Board have mandated repayments for excessive pricing spanning 20092017. These factors underscore a market transition toward cost containment, potentially lowering barriers but challenging the high-margin model that funded initial development for niche indications.

Ongoing research

New therapeutic applications

In March 2025, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) expanded approval of eculizumab to include pediatric patients aged 6 years and older with acetylcholine receptor (AChR) antibody-positive refractory generalized myasthenia gravis (gMG), specifically those inadequately responsive to cholinesterase inhibitors or at least two immunosuppressive therapies. This marks the first complement inhibitor approved for children with gMG, addressing a gap in treatments for this autoimmune neuromuscular disorder characterized by complement-mediated destruction at the neuromuscular junction. The approval was supported by data from a phase 3 open-label study demonstrating improvements in gMG activities of daily living scores and reduced rescue medication use in pediatric participants.
Investigational applications continue in other complement-driven thrombotic microangiopathies, such as Shiga toxin-producing Escherichia coli hemolytic uremic syndrome (STEC-HUS). A phase 3 randomized controlled trial evaluated eculizumab versus placebo in STEC-HUS patients, showing potential reductions in dialysis needs and neurological complications, though efficacy was inconsistent across subgroups and regulatory approval has not followed due to variable outcomes and safety concerns like meningococcal risk. Real-world evidence suggests adjunctive use in atypical HUS variants, but prospective data for broader STEC-HUS adoption remain limited.
Exploratory research has probed eculizumab in immune-mediated glomerular diseases like C3 glomerulopathy (C3G), where distal C5 inhibition aims to curb terminal complement activation, but phase 2 trials indicated only partial proteinuria reduction without halting disease progression, prompting shifts toward proximal inhibitors. These efforts highlight eculizumab's role in probing complement pathways, though sustained efficacy in non-approved indications requires further validation amid risks of infection from systemic blockade.

Studies on treatment optimization and alternatives

Studies have explored personalized dosing strategies for eculizumab to improve efficacy while reducing treatment burden and costs. A 2022 analysis developed a precision-dosing algorithm using pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic (PK/PD) modeling in patients with transplant-associated thrombotic microangiopathy (TA-TMA), demonstrating that individualized adjustments based on free C5 levels could maintain complement inhibition with lower cumulative doses compared to standard regimens. Similarly, model-informed precision dosing in high-risk complement-mediated TMA cases has shown feasibility for tailoring intervals and amounts via therapeutic drug monitoring, potentially minimizing overexposure risks. In paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH), a 2025 study indicated that individualized eculizumab dosing, guided by clinical response and biomarkers, could achieve comparable hemolysis control with up to 30% dose reductions, enhancing patient convenience and economic viability.
For atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome (aHUS), research supports dose optimization through monitoring tools like the C3:CH50 ratio as a composite biomarker for assessing eculizumab activity, enabling adjustments to sustain renal protection without universal high dosing. A 2022 case series reported successful maintenance with reduced eculizumab doses in aHUS patients post-remission, preserving eGFR and reducing infusion frequency while avoiding relapse, though larger trials are needed to validate long-term outcomes. Pediatric studies have further refined regimens; for instance, PK/PD evaluations in hematopoietic stem cell transplant-associated TMA revealed variable clearance necessitating individualized loading and maintenance doses to achieve target inhibition.
Ravulizumab, a longer-acting C5 inhibitor administered every 8 weeks versus eculizumab's biweekly schedule, has demonstrated noninferiority in controlling intravascular hemolysis and lactate dehydrogenase levels in PNH and aHUS patients switching from eculizumab, with comparable safety profiles in phase 3 trials. A 2024 real-world study confirmed sustained hematologic stability and renal function preservation after transitioning to ravulizumab, supporting its role as an optimized alternative for reducing infusion burden. Emerging proximal complement inhibitors like iptacopan (oral Factor B inhibitor) and danicopan (Factor D inhibitor, often added to C5 inhibitors for extravascular hemolysis) have shown additive benefits in phase 3 trials for PNH, offering oral alternatives or adjuncts that address incomplete responses to eculizumab monotherapy. Crovalimab, a monthly subcutaneous C5 inhibitor, exhibited comparable efficacy to eculizumab in PNH trials with improved patient-reported convenience, though direct head-to-head data remain limited. Biosimilars such as SB12 and ABP 959, approved in Europe by 2025, provide cost-effective equivalents with bioequivalence demonstrated in analytical and clinical studies for PNH treatment.
