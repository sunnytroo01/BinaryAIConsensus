 The National Federation of State High School Associations (NFHS) is a nonprofit organization based in Indianapolis, Indiana, that serves as the national leadership body and advocate for education-based interscholastic athletics and fine and performing arts programs across the United States. Founded on May 14, 1920, in Chicago as the Midwest Federation of State High School Athletic Associations by representatives from Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Michigan, and Wisconsin, it expanded nationally and underwent name changes, including to the National Federation of State High School Athletic Associations in 1923, removal of "Athletic" in 1970 to encompass arts programs, and adoption of the NFHS acronym in 1997. Headquartered in Indianapolis since 2000, the NFHS governs through a National Council with one representative per state association and a 12-member Board of Directors, supported by approximately 40 staff members.
Comprising 51 member associationsincluding those from all 50 states and the District of Columbiathe NFHS serves nearly 20,000 high schools and approximately 15 million students, with over 8 million participating in athletics annually as of 202425. Its core mission is "providing leadership for the administration of education-based high school athletics and activities through the writing of playing rules that emphasize health and safety, educational programs that develop leaders, and administrative support to increase participation opportunities and promote sportsmanship." To achieve this, the organization authors standardized playing rules for 17 sports, sanctions interstate competitions, conducts national meetings, and publishes resources such as the magazine High School Today.
Additionally, the NFHS promotes student well-being and leadership development through initiatives like the NFHS Learning Center, which offers online education courses for coaches, administrators, and officials, and the NFHS Network, a streaming platform for high school events. By focusing on amateur, education-centered programs, the NFHS ensures healthy participation, achievement, and sportsmanship, ultimately aiming to equip students for lifelong success.

History

Founding and Early Development

The National Federation of State High School Associations (NFHS) was established on May 14, 1920, during a meeting at the City Club in Chicago, Illinois, convened by L.W. Smith, superintendent of schools in Joliet, Illinois. The organization emerged in response to the chaotic conditions surrounding high school athletics, where interstate competitions were often sponsored by colleges, athletic clubs, or private promoters, leading to inconsistent eligibility rules and exploitation of student-athletes. Initially named the Mid-West Federation of State High School Athletic Associations, it brought together representatives from Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Michigan, and Wisconsin to promote cooperative oversight and standardization.
In its formative years, the NFHS focused on sanctioning interstate events and developing uniform playing rules to ensure fair competition, beginning with sports such as basketball and football. Charter membership was formalized in 1921 with Illinois, Iowa, Michigan, and Wisconsin adopting a constitution, and the organization was renamed the National Federation of State High School Athletic Associations in 1923 as additional states joined. Early leadership under part-time executive directors L.W. Smith (19201927) and C.W. Whitten (19271940) emphasized protecting amateurism by establishing standards that prevented commercialization and upheld educational priorities in school sports. These efforts addressed the post-World War I surge in interscholastic athletics, which had been bolstered by federal promotion of physical education for military preparedness, resulting in rapid but unregulated growth that heightened risks of professionalism and injury.
By the end of its first decade, the NFHS had grown to 28 member state associations, expanding its role in rule-making and event coordination to foster safer, more equitable competitions. In September 1940, H.V. Porter was appointed as the first full-time executive secretary, marking the establishment of a permanent national office in Chicago to centralize operations and support the organization's increasing responsibilities. This period solidified the NFHS's foundational commitment to amateur, education-based athletics amid ongoing challenges like varying state regulations and external pressures on eligibility.

Key Milestones and Expansion

During World War II, the NFHS adapted to wartime challenges by surveying its member associations, revealing that 24 states canceled spring sports in 1943 due to resource shortages and personnel losses. These adaptations highlighted the organization's role in maintaining educational athletics amid national priorities, while membership grew rapidly, reaching 35 state associations by 1940 and expanding to all 50 states plus the District of Columbia by 1969 with the addition of the Texas University Interscholastic League.
In 1970, the NFHS dropped "Athletic" from its name, becoming the National Federation of State High School Associations, to reflect its broadening scope beyond sports to include fine and performing arts activities. This shift aligned with the organization's founding principles of standardizing high school programs nationwide. The following year, 1971, marked the start of the NFHS High School Athletics Participation Survey, establishing systematic research on participation trends and safety data collected annually from member associations.
Key expansions continued in 1982 with the launch of the National High School Hall of Fame, honoring athletes, coaches, officials, administrators, and others for contributions to interscholastic activities, inducting its first class that year. Entering the 2000s, the NFHS embraced digital initiatives, including the 2007 launch of the NFHS Learning Center, an online platform delivering educational courses on coaching, safety, and sportsmanship to millions of users. In 2018-2020, the NFHS celebrated its centennial, marking 100 years of leadership in education-based interscholastic activities. These developments solidified the NFHS as a comprehensive resource for high school activities.

Mission and Purpose

Core Objectives

The National Federation of State High School Associations (NFHS) is dedicated to fostering healthy lifetime participation in interscholastic athletics and activities, emphasizing the development of character, leadership, and essential life skills among participants. By promoting an environment that prioritizes academic achievement, respect, integrity, and inclusion, the NFHS seeks to cultivate positive school and community cultures through safe and enriching experiences. This commitment extends to encouraging sportsmanship as a foundational value, distinguishing high school programs from professional or club sports by integrating them as extensions of education rather than competitive enterprises.
A core objective of the NFHS involves advocating for the principles of amateurism, rigorous eligibility standards, and equitable opportunities for all students, with a particular focus on compliance with Title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972, which prohibits sex-based discrimination in educational programs. Through these efforts, the organization supports fair play, minimizes risks such as injuries, and ensures that participation benefits diverse populations without compromising educational priorities.
Serving more than 19,900 high schools and approximately 12 million students across the United States via its 51 member state associations, the NFHS provides essential policy guidance to maintain an education-based model that enhances student well-being and achievement. This nationwide reach underscores the organization's role in protecting the integrity of high school athletics and fine arts programs, ensuring they remain accessible and developmentally appropriate.

Advocacy and Educational Role

The National Federation of State High School Associations (NFHS) engages in advocacy efforts to influence policy and secure resources for interscholastic athletics, including lobbying state legislatures for funding and supportive legislation that sustains school-based sports programs. Through collaboration with member state associations, the NFHS promotes safeguards for high school athletics by supporting updates to state laws, such as those addressing deficiencies in procedures for sports-related injuries. In particular, the organization has advocated for comprehensive concussion protocols, providing legal perspectives and recommendations that have contributed to the effectiveness of state concussion laws in reducing injury rates through mandated reporting and management strategies. Additionally, NFHS-funded initiatives, including the National High School Sports-Related Injury Surveillance system, have informed responses to mental health concerns by tracking improvements in athlete well-being alongside concussion reductions.
The NFHS fulfills its educational role by producing annual research reports that analyze trends, safety, and diversity in high school sports participation, offering data-driven insights to guide member associations and policymakers. Its High School Athletics Participation Survey, conducted yearly, documents participation growthsuch as the record 8,266,244 participants in the 2024-25 school yearand highlights trends like increases in girls' flag football and wrestling, which underscore efforts toward greater diversity. Safety-focused research through the same surveillance system identifies injury patterns, enabling the development of evidence-based protocols to minimize risks in activities. These reports also address diversity by tracking equitable access, including surges in female and underrepresented group involvement, thereby supporting educational programming that fosters inclusive environments.
In facilitating the transition from high school to college athletics, the NFHS collaborates with organizations like the NCAA to establish clear eligibility standards and certification processes, ensuring student-athletes understand requirements for postsecondary competition. This partnership includes joint criteria for certifying scholastic events, such as June boys' basketball tournaments, to maintain educational integrity while aligning high school experiences with college expectations. Such efforts emphasize preserving the education-based nature of high school sports amid pressures from elite pathways.
The NFHS promotes inclusive practices through targeted initiatives on adaptive sports and gender equity, aiming to expand opportunities for all students in interscholastic activities. It provides resources and guidance for developing school programs for students with disabilities, clarifying institutional responsibilities under laws like the Americans with Disabilities Act to integrate adaptive athletics. On gender equity, the organization advocates for compliance with Title IX, including oversight of booster clubs to prevent disparities in funding and participation between male and female programs, while supporting rights for LGBTQ+ students in sports. These efforts have contributed to increased female participation and broader inclusivity, as evidenced by ongoing campaigns to empower underrepresented groups.

Organizational Structure and Governance

Board of Directors and Leadership

The NFHS Board of Directors serves as the primary governing body, responsible for setting policy and overseeing the organization's strategic direction. It consists of 12 members, including one representative from each of the eight geographic sections and four at-large representatives (one from each paired section grouping: Sections 1 and 4, 2 and 6, 3 and 7, 5 and 8). These members are elected by the NFHS National Council, which comprises one delegate from each of the 51 member state associations.
Board members are elected to four-year terms, with terms staggered such that two section representatives and one at-large representative's term expire annually; no consecutive terms are permitted to ensure rotation. The eight sections provide regional representation across the United States: Section 1 (Northeast), Section 2 (Mideast), Section 3 (South), Section 4 (Central), Section 5 (Midwest), Section 6 (Southwest), Section 7 (West), and Section 8 (Northwest). This structure promotes balanced input from diverse geographic areas in decision-making processes.
As of 2025, the NFHS officers include President Ron Nocetti from the California Interscholastic Federation (Section 7), who was elected to a one-year term, and President-elect Lance Taylor from the Alabama High School Athletic Association. Other board members include Doug Ute (Section 2, term 2025-29), Erich Martens (Section 5, term 2025-29), and Misty Wharton (at-large for Sections 5 and 8, term 2025-29), among others approved for four-year terms. Officers are elected annually by the Board during the NFHS Summer Meeting.
The Board convenes quarterly to conduct business and holds its annual Summer Meeting, typically in late June or early July, for elections and major deliberations. It oversees various standing and special committees focused on rules development for sports, eligibility standards, sports medicine, and financial matters, appointing members to ensure expertise in these areas. Key responsibilities include approving annual budgets, endorsing strategic plans, and authorizing major initiatives such as expansions in digital resources and educational programs. The Board also hires the Executive Director to manage day-to-day operations.

The position was initially part-time, evolving into a full-time role in 1940, with appointments approved by the NFHS Board of Directors. Membership

Member State Associations

The National Federation of State High School Associations (NFHS) comprises 51 primary member state associations, one from each of the 50 U.S. states and the District of Columbia, which serve as the organization's voting members and core governance structure. These associations act as the recognized governing bodies for interscholastic athletics and activities within their jurisdictions, overseeing state championships, eligibility enforcement, and program administration for high schools.
To qualify for membership, a state association must submit an application to the NFHS Board of Directors, provide its constitution and bylaws for review, demonstrate recognition by its state's education authority, and adopt the NFHS Constitution, Bylaws, playing rules, and eligibility standards through a formal resolution; approval requires a two-thirds vote by the NFHS National Council. Once members, these associations implement NFHS guidelines locally by enforcing standardized rules for 17 sports and various activities, while adapting policies to state-specific needs such as safety protocols and competition formats. They also conduct annual participation surveys and sanction interstate events in alignment with NFHS criteria.
Each member association governs a varying number of schools, ranging from a few dozen to over 1,500, reflecting variations in state population and structure; for instance, the University Interscholastic League (UIL) in Texas oversees 1,500 schools (as of 2025), while the Wyoming High School Activities Association manages approximately 70 schools (as of 2025). Focus areas differ across members: 25 associations emphasize athletics exclusively (as of 2020), whereas 26 incorporate fine and performing arts, journalism, robotics, or chess, promoting a broader educational mission (as of 2020). Some, like the California Interscholastic Federation, operate through regional sections to handle large-scale diversity, and 24 allow middle school affiliations to extend programs (as of 2020).
These associations reflect geographic and demographic diversity, serving urban centers like those in New York and California, rural communities in states such as Wyoming and Montana, and mixed suburban environments, while including both public and accredited private schools where state policies permit.

Affiliate Organizations

The National Federation of State High School Associations (NFHS) maintains several affiliate organizations that serve as professional networks for individuals involved in interscholastic athletics and activities, distinct from its primary state-based member associations. These affiliates include the NFHS Coaches Association, NFHS Officials Association, NFHS Music Association, and NFHS Speech, Debate and Theatre Association, each focused on supporting educators and professionals in their respective fields.
Membership in these affiliates is open to high school coaches, officials, music directors and adjudicators, and speech, debate, and theatre directors, offering benefits such as professional development opportunities, networking through state and regional programs, access to NFHS publications and online resources, liability insurance coverage, and discounts on related services like car rentals and equipment. Unlike NFHS member state associations, affiliate members do not hold voting rights in the organization's governance but contribute to its mission by promoting education-based programs in athletics and performing arts. Annual dues vary by affiliate, typically ranging from $20 to $35 for individuals.
The NFHS Coaches Association, for instance, provides resources like certification courses, rule book access, and an awards program to enhance coaching practices across grades 6-12, with individual membership at $35 per year. The NFHS Officials Association similarly supports officiating professionals with training, certification, and guidelines, charging $35 annually for individual membership and serving a community that includes 237,811 registered high school officials nationwide (as of the 2024-25 school year). The NFHS Music Association, at $20 per year, aids music directors and adjudicators through workshops, publications, and leadership development for band, orchestra, and choral programs. Likewise, the NFHS Speech, Debate and Theatre Association, established in 1986 and costing $20 annually, offers 23 specialized publications, regional workshops, and access to The Forensic Educator for program directors.
These affiliate organizations play a key role in shaping national standards by providing feedback and representation on NFHS rules committees, ensuring input from practitioners on updates to sports rules, activity guidelines, and educational initiatives without direct voting authority. This collaborative mechanism helps refine policies for high school activities across the country.

Programs and Services

Rules Development and Publications

The National Federation of State High School Associations (NFHS) develops and maintains playing rules for 18 high school sports, including basketball, football, volleyball, baseball, softball, soccer, flag football, track and field, wrestling, swimming and diving, and others, ensuring uniformity, safety, and fairness across member state associations. The NFHS also administers the Authenticating Mark Program to certify equipment conformity to rules specifications. For soccer balls used in official high school varsity matches, this U.S.-specific standard requires the ball to display the official NFHS authenticating mark and meet criteria such as size 5, circumference of 2728 inches (68.671.1 cm), weight of 1416 oz (396453 g), weather-resistant material, and tests for sphericity and pressure retention, promoting consistency and safety in competitive scholastic play. These rules also extend to non-athletic activities such as music, speech, debate, and theatre, where the NFHS provides standardized guidelines and points of emphasis to promote equitable participation and best practices.
The rules development process is managed through sport-specific rules-writing committees, each composed of experts nominated by state high school associations, including coaches, administrators, and officials, with a chairperson selected from these nominees. These committees convene annually to review proposals for rule changes, drawing input from state associations, coaches, and officials via questionnaires distributed each year for most sports. Proposed modifications are evaluated based on criteria such as participant safety, game integrity, and educational value, then submitted to a central Rules Review Committee for final approval before incorporation into updated rules. In July 2025, the NFHS published the first rules book for flag football, marking it as the 18th sport and the first new addition since boys lacrosse in 2000.
Key publications include the annual NFHS Rules Books, which outline the official playing rules for each sport and activity; accompanying Case Books, which provide detailed interpretations and scenarios to clarify rule applications; and Officials Manuals, offering guidance for enforcement. Additionally, the NFHS produces model policies on topics such as student eligibility, transfer rules, and event sanctioning, detailed in its official handbook to assist state associations in maintaining consistent standards. These materials are distributed in print and digital formats, including through the NFHS Rules App, to support coaches, officials, and administrators nationwide.
In response to extraordinary circumstances, such as the COVID-19 pandemic during the 2020-2021 season, the NFHS issued temporary modifications to existing rules across multiple sports, including protocols for social distancing, elimination of handshakes, and adjusted game formats to minimize health risks while preserving competitive opportunities. These adaptations were developed collaboratively with sports medicine experts and state associations, emphasizing safety without permanent alteration to core rules.

Educational and Media Initiatives

The National Federation of State High School Associations (NFHS) plays a pivotal role in delivering educational resources and media platforms to support interscholastic activities, focusing on professional development for educators, coaches, officials, and students. Through its initiatives, the NFHS emphasizes safety, skill enhancement, and broader access to high school sports and fine arts, ensuring that participants and leaders are equipped with current best practices.
Central to these efforts is the NFHS Learning Center, an online platform launched in 2007 that provides over 100 coursesmore than 70 of which are freetargeted at coaches, officials, administrators, and students. Courses cover critical topics such as concussion recognition, heat illness prevention, strength and conditioning, and Title IX compliance, with the platform having delivered more than 25 million enrollments to date. For instance, the "Fundamentals of Coaching" course, introduced in 2007, has been completed over one million times, underscoring its foundational impact on coaching education.
The NFHS offers structured certification programs to formalize professional growth in interscholastic roles. The NFHS National Coach Certification Program provides a three-level credentialing pathway, beginning with core courses like "Fundamentals of Coaching" and progressing to sport-specific and elective modules that address leadership, ethics, and risk management. Similarly, officials training includes specialized courses such as "Interscholastic Officiating" and sport-specific modules for basketball, football, soccer, and others, designed to improve decision-making and game management skills; these resources support state-level certifications while promoting consistency across associations.
In the media domain, the NFHS Network, established in 2013 through a partnership with PlayOn! Sports, serves as the leading streaming service for high school events, broadcasting live and on-demand content from over 44 member state associations, including regular-season games, playoffs, and state championships across more than 30 sports and activities. The platform has streamed over one million events, generating tens of millions of views annually and enabling global access for families and fans; for example, during the 2013-14 inaugural year, it attracted more than three million unique viewers for 28,000 events.
Complementing these offerings, the NFHS produces podcasts and webinars to foster ongoing dialogue and resource sharing, particularly in fine arts education. "The Stage," the official performing arts podcast, features discussions on music, theatre, speech, debate, and academic competitions, drawing insights from national experts to aid educators. Webinars and additional resources through the Arts Advocacy initiative provide tools for fine arts directors, including wellness guides for musicians and diversity in composition, extending the NFHS's commitment to holistic interscholastic development.

Participation and Impact

High School Sports Participation Statistics

The National Federation of State High School Associations (NFHS) conducts an annual survey to track interscholastic athletics participation, drawing data from its 51 member state associations based on reported competition at the high school level. The 2024-25 survey revealed a record 8,266,244 participants across more than 1,000,000 teams, marking a 2.5% increase from the previous year's total of 8,062,302. This growth reflects sustained recovery from pandemic-related declines, with overall participation rising by over 645,000 students in the three years since 2021-22.
Boys' participation stood at 4,726,648, while girls accounted for 3,539,596 participants, representing 42.8% of the totala slight uptick driven by expansions in female-specific programs. Girls' involvement grew by approximately 1.5% year-over-year, outpacing boys' gains and highlighting progress toward gender equity in school sports. Among inclusive initiatives, Unified Sportswhich pairs students with and without intellectual disabilitiesreached 70,006 participants across 15 sports, a 36% increase from 51,502 the prior year.

Influence on Interscholastic Activities

The National Federation of State High School Associations (NFHS) promotes the holistic benefits of interscholastic activities by emphasizing their role in developing leadership, teamwork, and academic success among students. Participation in these programs fosters essential life skills, such as collaboration and responsibility, which contribute to stronger peer relationships and personal growth. Studies referenced by the NFHS indicate that involved students exhibit higher grade-point averages, improved attendance, and reduced dropout rates compared to non-participants, underscoring the link between activity engagement and educational outcomes. For instance, athletes and fine arts participants demonstrate better performance on standardized tests and are more likely to pursue higher education, highlighting the long-term developmental advantages.
The NFHS has expanded its influence into non-athletic areas, supporting fine arts programs that engage millions of students in music, theatre, and related activities, thereby enriching school culture beyond sports. Approximately 4 million students participate in performing arts nationwide, which the NFHS advocates as integral to comprehensive education-based programs. Additionally, the organization champions adaptive and unified programs designed for students with disabilities, providing tailored opportunities in sports like bocce and track to promote inclusion and physical well-being. These initiatives, including NFHS-developed courses for coaches, ensure that diverse learners can access interscholastic experiences, broadening the scope of activities to encompass artistic and inclusive expressions.
On a national scale, the NFHS standardizes rules and events through its publications and guidelines, which help mitigate inequities by ensuring consistent opportunities across states and promoting fair competition. By developing uniform rules books for over 20 sports and activities, the NFHS enables equitable implementation by member associations, reducing disparities in access and resources. In response to challenges such as declining school enrollments, the NFHS advances inclusive policies that prioritize diversity and equitable access, supporting state efforts to maintain robust programs amid demographic shifts. This approach fosters school cohesion and counters enrollment pressures by emphasizing broad participation.
Looking toward future trends, the NFHS places growing emphasis on mental health within interscholastic activities, offering resources and training to address issues like stress and anxiety among participants. The organization provides educational tools for coaches to support student-athletes' emotional well-being, recognizing activities as outlets for mental resilience. Concurrently, the NFHS is integrating e-sports into high school programs, with over 20 state associations now sponsoring competitions to engage tech-savvy students and expand activity options. These developments reflect the NFHS's commitment to evolving interscholastic offerings in line with contemporary student needs.

Awards and Honors

National High School Hall of Fame

The National High School Hall of Fame was established in 1982 by the National Federation of State High School Associations (NFHS) to recognize and preserve the contributions of individuals to interscholastic sports and performing arts. It honors athletes, coaches, contest officials, administrators, performing arts contributors, and others who have demonstrated extraordinary achievements and exemplified high standards of sportsmanship during their high school careers, with consideration given to their post-high school impact. As of 2025, the Hall includes 540 inductees representing all 51 NFHS member state associations, with approximately 10 to 12 new members selected each year.
The selection process is a two-level system designed to ensure rigorous evaluation. Nominations are submitted annually by November 15 from state associations, NFHS staff, or the high school community using an official form; nominees must be at least 35 years old and demonstrate significant contributions to interscholastic activities. A screening committee of 10 active high school administrators, coaches, officials, and state association staff reviews submissions and advances candidates by a two-thirds vote to the final selection committee, composed of respected leaders in athletics, education, and business, who rate candidates on a 1-5 scale. The process emphasizes impact at the high school level alongside broader accomplishments, with a maximum of 12 inductees approved annually based on category quotas.
Inductees are honored at an annual ceremony held in conjunction with the NFHS Summer Meeting, typically in late June in Indianapolis, Indiana, where a video production highlights their achievements. The Hall recognizes diverse categories, including multi-sport athletes; for example, the 2025 class featured four former multi-sport standouts, such as NFL player Prince Amukamara, who excelled in football, basketball, and track and field at Apollo High School in Arizona.
Notable inductees include legendary basketball figures such as John Wooden, inducted in 1991 for his standout high school career at Martinsville High School in Indiana, where he led the team to state championships in basketball and baseball, and Larry Bird, honored in 1995 for his dominant performance at Spring Valley High School in French Lick, Indiana. These examples illustrate the Hall's focus on individuals whose high school excellence laid the foundation for lasting legacies in sports.

Other Recognition Programs

The National Federation of State High School Associations (NFHS) administers several annual recognition programs that complement its flagship National High School Hall of Fame by honoring ongoing contributions and exemplary conduct in interscholastic activities. These awards, selected primarily through nominations from member state associations, emphasize leadership, service, and positive impact across sports, education, and the arts.
One prominent program is the National High School Coach of the Year Awards, established in 1982 by the NFHS Coaches Association to recognize outstanding coaching in the top 10 boys' and 10 girls' sports. Annually, 20 to 24 coaches are selected based on their teams' performance, professional development efforts, and influence on student-athletes' character and academics, with recipients approved by the NFHS Board of Directors. For the 2023-24 school year, honorees included Tiger LaVerde for football from Ohio and Eric Sanders for girls golf from North Dakota, highlighting coaches who have amassed hundreds of wins while prioritizing holistic student growth.
The NFHS Citation Awards, initiated to acknowledge long-term dedication, are presented yearly to professionals such as athletic directors, coaches, officials, and activity directors who have significantly advanced high school programs at local, state, or national levels. Nominations come from member associations, with selections focusing on sustained service and innovation; typically 8 to 14 individuals receive the award each year. In 2025, 14 leaders were honored, including athletic directors from states like California and Texas for their roles in enhancing equity and participation in interscholastic activities. Similarly, the 2024 class included eight athletic directors recognized for contributions to athletics administration.
For students, the National High School Spirit of Sport Award celebrates exemplary character and sportsmanship, awarded annually since its inception to nine recipientseight sectional winners and one national honoreefrom NFHS member schools. Selected by state associations for demonstrating integrity, respect, and positive influence in athletics or activities, the award underscores the NFHS's commitment to ethical development. Recent national recipients include Jack Punswick from Kansas in 2025 for his courage as a state champion swimmer battling cancer, and Merik Dismang from Kansas in 2024 as a four-sport Unified athlete battling cancer.
In the fine arts domain, the NFHS Outstanding Performing Arts Educator Awards recognize educators for excellence in music, speech/debate, and theatre, with programs dating back to the late 1980s. The Outstanding Music Educator Award, started in 1989, honors band, orchestra, and choral directors for artistic achievement and student mentorship, awarding 25 recipients annually (17 state and 8 sectional). Examples from 2024-25 include Andrew Forster from Maine for his innovative choral programs and Darrin Abren from Kentucky for orchestral leadership. The Outstanding Theatre Educator Award, separated in 2022 from the broader speech/debate category (which began in 1988), similarly selects 11 honorees each year for directing impactful productions that build student confidence and creativity; 2024-25 recipients featured Jan Thompson from Virginia and Monica Turner from Georgia. These awards are nominated by state associations and highlight educators who integrate arts education with life skills development.
