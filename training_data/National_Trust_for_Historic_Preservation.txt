 The National Trust for Historic Preservation is a privately funded nonprofit organization chartered by the United States Congress on October 26, 1949, through legislation signed by President Harry S. Truman, with the purpose of facilitating public participation in the preservation of sites, buildings, and objects of national historical or architectural significance.
Headquartered in Washington, D.C., and supported by approximately 750,000 members, the organization owns and stewards 27 historic sites open to the public, spanning diverse architectural periods and regions, including 18th-century plantations such as Drayton Hall in South Carolina and modernist structures like the Glass House in Connecticut.
Its core activities encompass advocacy for preservation policies, provision of grants and technical assistance through programs like the Preservation Services Fund established in 1969, and initiatives such as the annual America's 11 Most Endangered Historic Places list launched in 1988 to highlight threats and mobilize action.
Significant achievements include contributing to the passage of the National Historic Preservation Act of 1966, which created a national framework for identifying and protecting cultural resources, and developing the Main Street program in 1980 to leverage historic preservation for economic revitalization in small towns and urban neighborhoods.
While focused on conserving physical embodiments of history to foster community connections and heritage tourism, the Trust has navigated controversies, such as advocating measured processes for addressing monuments linked to the Confederacy rather than impromptu removals, reflecting tensions between preservation imperatives and interpretive demands in a polarized cultural landscape.
Under leadership including current President and CEO Carol Quillen since 2024, it continues to expand efforts in education, policy influence, and site management to counter ongoing threats from development and neglect.

Founding and Organizational History

Establishment and Early Objectives (19491965)

The National Trust for Historic Preservation was established on October 26, 1949, when President Harry S. Truman signed congressional legislation granting it a federal charter as a private nonprofit corporation, codified under 16 U.S.C. 468-468d. This charter aimed to advance the objectives of the Historic Sites Act of 1935 by promoting public participation in the preservation of sites, buildings, and objects of national significance, emphasizing voluntary cooperation between private citizens, philanthropists, and government entities rather than direct regulatory mandates. The organization's formation responded to post-World War II pressures, including accelerating urban renewal projects and infrastructure expansions that threatened historic structures across the United States.
Early objectives centered on acquiring, restoring, and administering historic properties to demonstrate model preservation practices and foster grassroots initiatives. Under the leadership of its first chairman, David E. Finley, a former director of the National Gallery of Art with ties to prominent philanthropists like Andrew W. Mellon, the Trust drew from an initial board comprising leaders in the preservation movement, such as George McAneny, Christopher Crittenden, and Ronald Lee, reflecting origins in elite cultural and civic networks. Finley's vision, detailed in his 1965 account History of the National Trust for Historic Preservation, 19471963, prioritized public education and private stewardship to counteract demolitions driven by commercial development and highway construction.
Formative activities included the Trust's first major acquisition in 1951, when it assumed responsibility for Woodlawn Plantation in Fairfax County, Virginia, an 18th-century estate at risk from suburban encroachment. Subsequent efforts targeted other iconic sites, such as Woodrow Wilson House in Washington, D.C., acquired in the early 1960s amid threats from urban renewal, underscoring the organization's role in bridging public interest with private action before the advent of comprehensive federal preservation laws. By 1965, these initiatives had established the Trust as a key advocate for conserving architectural and cultural heritage through non-governmental means, publishing resources like its inaugural magazine in 1952 to broaden awareness.

Growth Amid Legislative Milestones (19661990s)

The passage of the National Historic Preservation Act (NHPA) on October 15, 1966, marked a pivotal expansion for the National Trust for Historic Preservation, establishing a federal framework that included the National Register of Historic Places and Section 106, requiring federal agencies to assess impacts on historic properties before undertaking projects. The Trust advocated for the legislation's provisions and subsequently supported their implementation by providing technical assistance, educating stakeholders on compliance, and participating as an interested party in Section 106 reviews to protect sites from adverse federal actions. This alignment with NHPA facilitated the creation of State Historic Preservation Offices (SHPOs), which the Trust aided through coordination and resource-sharing to build a nationwide preservation network.
In the 1970s, the Trust launched demonstration initiatives tied to economic revitalization, including the 1977 Main Street Project pilots in Galesburg, Illinois; Hot Springs, South Dakota; and Madison, Indiana, aimed at countering downtown decline through adaptive reuse of historic buildings. These efforts preceded the 1979 rural preservation program and were complemented by the opening of the Trust's first field office in San Francisco in 1971, extending its operational reach. Amid the 1976 Bicentennial celebrations, preservation advocates, including the Trust, promoted awareness campaigns emphasizing historic sites' role in national heritage, contributing to heightened public engagement.
By the 1980s, the Trust formalized the National Main Street Center in 1980, scaling the Main Street approach nationally with a "Four Point" strategyfocusing on design, organization, promotion, and economic restructuringto leverage preservation for local economic gains, partnering with states like Colorado and Texas. This period saw further program diversification, including heritage tourism initiatives in 1989, alongside expanded field operations and collaborations with SHPOs, solidifying the Trust's infrastructure for grassroots advocacy and site stewardship across the U.S.

Modern Evolution and Leadership Changes (2000Present)

Under Richard Moe's presidency, which extended through 2009, the National Trust expanded its focus to incorporate broader narratives in preservation efforts, emphasizing economic development through heritage tourism while addressing security concerns for historic sites following the September 11, 2001, attacks. Moe advocated for preservation as a tool for community revitalization, as evidenced by his speeches linking historic sites to sustainable economic strategies. Post-9/11, the organization collaborated with federal agencies on vulnerability assessments for landmarks, integrating physical security enhancements without compromising architectural integrity.
Subsequent leadership, including Paul W. Edmondson's tenure ending in 2022, prioritized digital transformation to broaden public engagement. The National Trust developed a comprehensive digital strategy in partnership with firms like Viget, launching online platforms for virtual tours and interactive mapping of historic sites to reach younger audiences and remote users. This era also saw increased attention to underrepresented histories, culminating in the 2017 establishment of the African American Cultural Heritage Action Fund, which has awarded millions in grants to preserve sites tied to Black American experiences, such as churches and civil rights landmarks.
With Carol Quillen's appointment as the 10th president and CEO in January 2024, the organization intensified responses to contemporary threats like climate change and urban development pressures. In 2024, the Action Fund distributed $3 million to 30 African American historic sites for restoration and planning, targeting structures vulnerable to neglect or demolition. The 2025 edition of America's 11 Most Endangered Historic Places, released on May 7, highlighted climate-impacted locations including Cedar Key, Florida, where rising seas and hurricanes threaten 19th-century wood-frame buildings central to the community's identity.
In October 2025, under Quillen's leadership, the National Trust submitted a formal letter to federal agencies opposing the proposed demolition of the White House East Wing for a new ballroom addition, urging a pause until full preservation reviews could assess impacts on the 1942 structure's historical fabric. This advocacy underscored ongoing efforts to balance modern adaptations with fidelity to original designs, drawing on the organization's statutory review roles under the National Historic Preservation Act.

Mission, Governance, and Funding

Stated Mission and Strategic Priorities

The National Trust for Historic Preservation articulates its core mission as powering a nationwide preservation movement dedicated to saving historic placesencompassing buildings, neighborhoods, landscapes, and cultural traditionsto foster economic vitality, community resilience, and a connection to verifiable historical narratives that enrich contemporary life. Established as a privately funded nonprofit, the organization emphasizes safeguarding sites of empirical cultural significance through non-regulatory means, such as education on adaptive reuse strategies that repurpose historic structures for modern economic uses, thereby demonstrating preservation's role in generating jobs, tourism revenue, and sustainable development without mandating government intervention. 
Strategic priorities center on empowering local communities to identify and protect places of tangible heritage value, prioritizing initiatives that leverage private philanthropy and voluntary partnerships over expansive federal oversight. Key pillars include promoting innovation in preservation techniques that prioritize cost-effective retrofitting and reuse, as evidenced by the organization's advocacy for policies enabling historic tax incentives that have preserved over 500,000 structures since 1976 while yielding billions in economic returns. The Trust also highlights "telling the full American story" through documentation grounded in primary historical records, focusing on diverse sites that reflect documented contributions to national development, such as industrial mills, rural homesteads, and civic landmarks, to underscore preservation's causal links to identity formation and market-driven revitalization rather than interpretive overlays disconnected from evidentiary foundations.
In alignment with its congressional charter of 1949, these priorities stress measurable outcomes, including the protection of over 40 National Trust-owned historic sites that serve as models for private stewardship, while critiquing over-reliance on public funding that could distort priorities away from broadly verifiable heritage toward narrower agendas. This approach underscores a commitment to causal realism in preservation, where sites are valued for their proven roles in economic historysuch as adaptive conversions yielding 20-30% higher property values in rehabilitated districtsover unsubstantiated narratives.

Governance Structure and Key Leadership

The National Trust for Historic Preservation is governed by a Board of Trustees, serving as its primary policy-making body responsible for strategic oversight and direction. Comprising approximately 30 members, including ex-officio representatives such as the U.S. Attorney General and Secretary of the Interior, the board features a diverse array of professionals: historians and preservationists like Alison K. Hoagland, a professor emerita in historic preservation; philanthropists such as Tracy Frist and Christina Lee Brown; and business leaders including Kari Alldredge of McKinsey & Company and Jennifer Skyler of American Express. Trustees are typically elected by the organization's membership for three-year terms, with some initially appointed before standing for election, ensuring alignment with member priorities on preservation accountability.
Complementing the board is the National Trust Council, a supportive body of philanthropic leaders who commit at least $10,000 annually to advance cultural heritage initiatives. While not involved in formal policy decisions, council members provide advisory influence and reflect broader community passion for historic places, with potential overlap through figures like emerita trustees. This structure facilitates member input via board elections and council engagement, promoting internal accountability through elected representation and high-level donor perspectives.
Executive operations are directed by the President and Chief Executive Officer, currently Carol Quillen, the organization's 10th president, who reports to the board and oversees daily administration alongside a senior executive team. Leadership transitions, such as the prior tenure of Paul Edmondson from 2019, have focused on operational efficiency amid the nonprofit's evolution, with board guidance ensuring fiscal prudence in preservation efforts. Annual governance practices include trustee term limits and reporting mechanisms inherent to the board's oversight role, though specific preservation outcome disclosures occur through organizational updates rather than standalone member votes on programs.

Core Preservation Programs

America's 11 Most Endangered Historic Places

The National Trust for Historic Preservation launched its annual list of America's 11 Most Endangered Historic Places in 1988 as a targeted advocacy mechanism to spotlight historic sites facing imminent threats, such as demolition, neglect, development pressures, or natural disasters, thereby mobilizing public and local action to avert loss. The program evaluates sites based on the verifiable severity and immediacy of risks rather than broader symbolic or ideological considerations, prioritizing those where preservation interventions could realistically alter outcomes. Over its 38 years through 2025, the list has identified more than 350 sites across the United States, serving as a catalyst for community-led efforts without direct funding from the National Trust.
Nominations for inclusion are open to any individual or organization, with submissions assessed by National Trust senior staff against specific criteria, including the site's historical significance, evidence of active local protection efforts, the urgency and nature of the threat, feasible solutions, and potential for wider preservation impact, with added focus on underrepresented communities and climate-related vulnerabilities. This process relies on documented evidence of endangerment, such as pending demolition permits or observable deterioration from neglect, to ensure selections reflect tangible causal risks rather than speculative concerns. While public nominations contribute diverse perspectives, final selections are determined internally by experts, avoiding popularity-based voting that could dilute focus on empirical threats.
The 2025 list, announced on May 7, exemplifies this approach with entries like the French Broad and Swannanoa River Corridors in western North Carolina, where Hurricane Helene's flooding in late 2024 exacerbated risks of neglect and incompatible post-disaster development, threatening historic mills, bridges, and cultural landscapes tied to the region's industrial heritage. Other sites on the list face parallel verifiable pressures, underscoring the program's emphasis on sites where threats are acute and data-supported, such as structural decay or regulatory changes enabling demolition.
Empirical assessment of the list's impact reveals a strong track record in influencing preservation, with only a handful of the over 350 listed sites ultimately lost to threats, while dozens have been fully saved and many others advanced toward recovery through heightened awareness and partnerships. These successes have often yielded economic benefits, including revitalization that supports heritage tourism and local revenue generation from preserved structures, though outcomes depend on sustained local implementation rather than the designation alone. Instances of unrescued sites highlight limitations where broader factors, such as insufficient funding or overriding development interests, prevail despite advocacy, demonstrating that while the list effectively signals risks, causal preservation requires aligned incentives beyond publicity.

Advocacy and Policy Influence

Promotion of Historic Tax Credits

The National Trust for Historic Preservation has long advocated for the federal Historic Tax Credit (HTC), a program offering a 20% income tax credit on qualified rehabilitation expenditures (QRE) for certified historic structures used in income-producing activities, enacted in 1976 as part of the Tax Reform Act. The organization promotes the HTC as a key incentive leveraging private investment into preservation, crediting it with spurring over $127 billion in such investments across more than 50,000 properties nationwide since inception, according to U.S. National Park Service data. This leverage typically multiplies each federal tax dollar foregone into several dollars of private rehabilitation spending, though empirical assessments from government reports emphasize the program's role in directing capital toward existing structures rather than unsubsidized new builds.
In economic impact analyses cited by the National Trust, the HTC is associated with substantial job creation, including claims of over 3.2 million positions generated through construction, operations, and related activities, with recent fiscal year data showing 162,000 jobs from $32 billion in rehabilitations in FY2023 alone. However, these figures derive from input-output models that may overstate net gains by not fully accounting for opportunity costs, such as resources diverted from non-historic developments that could yield comparable or higher employment in unsubsidized markets; causal evaluation requires considering counterfactual scenarios where tax revenues retained might fund broader infrastructure or reduce deficits. The program's fiscal burden on the Treasuryequivalent to the credits claimed, roughly 20% of QRErepresents a targeted subsidy estimated in billions annually, potentially distorting property markets by inflating values for historic assets and discouraging demolition or adaptive reuse without cultural mandates.
Amid 2025 congressional deliberations on tax policy, the National Trust intensified efforts for HTC permanence and enhancements, including through the Historic Tax Credit Coalition's June lobby day involving over 70 meetings with lawmakers to urge reforms like restoring one-year credit delivery over the prior five-year ratable schedule. These pushes highlight the Trust's view of private-sector amplification via tax incentives as superior to direct public spending, though critics note that such permanence could entrench market interventions without periodic review of cost-benefit ratios against alternative fiscal tools. The advocacy aligns with broader goals of sustaining investment flows, yet empirical scrutiny reveals variances in regional efficacy, with stronger returns in urban revitalization but potential inefficiencies in low-demand areas where preservation mandates exceed private willingness to pay.

Engagement with Transportation and Infrastructure Laws

The National Trust for Historic Preservation has advocated for strict compliance with Section 106 of the National Historic Preservation Act (NHPA) in federally funded transportation projects, including those under the Intermodal Surface Transportation Efficiency Act (ISTEA) of 1991 and subsequent reauthorizations like the Transportation Equity Act for the 21st Century. This engagement focuses on requiring federal agencies, such as the Department of Transportation (DOT), to assess and mitigate adverse effects on historic properties before approving undertakings like highway expansions or bridge rehabilitations. The organization submits formal comments on proposed regulations, urging integration of preservation reviews early in project planning to balance infrastructure needs with cultural resource protection, as seen in its 2025 response to USDOT's NEPA implementation rules emphasizing Section 106's role in avoiding irreversible losses.
In practice, the Trust has intervened in cases where proposed infrastructure threatened historic districts, often through advocacy that prompts design modifications or legal challenges under Section 106. For instance, it has supported programmatic agreements to streamline reviews for common elements like post-1945 concrete bridges in transportation corridors, allowing projects to proceed while documenting or relocating affected resources, as developed in collaboration with the Federal Highway Administration (FHWA) and Advisory Council on Historic Preservation (ACHP) in 2012. Such efforts have led to outcomes where mitigation preserves significant assets, though federal data on Section 106 resolutions show varied results: many undertakings (over 90% in FHWA reports) conclude with no adverse effect or memorandum of agreement for avoidance, but adverse findings often require redesigns delaying timelines by months to years.
Critics, including infrastructure advocates, contend that the Trust's push for comprehensive Section 106 consultations contributes to cost overruns by extending review periods and necessitating expensive alternatives, with Congressional Research Service analyses noting that preservation requirements under NHPA can impose "unnecessary and costly delays" on projects facing opposition. Empirical estimates link these processes to broader regulatory burdens, where NEPA-integrated Section 106 reviews have inflated infrastructure costs by requiring iterative consultations; for example, some DOT projects have seen delays of 1-2 years per FHWA case studies, amplifying expenses through inflation and opportunity costs, though precise attributions to preservation alone remain debated amid overlapping environmental mandates. The Trust counters that such trade-offs prevent the permanent loss of irreplaceable sites, as evidenced by preserved historic alignments in rail and highway rights-of-way through negotiated exemptions and context studies.

Positions on Public Lands and Federal Oversight

The National Trust for Historic Preservation actively advocates for the protection of historic and cultural resources on federal public lands, collaborating with agencies including the National Park Service and Bureau of Land Management to nominate sites for designation under frameworks like the National Historic Preservation Act. This includes pushing for permanent safeguards against threats to irreplaceable features, such as archaeological sites and landscapes, while emphasizing federal stewardship responsibilities.
The organization has endorsed increased federal funding for maintenance of public lands infrastructure, highlighting deferred upkeep issues that endanger historic assets; for instance, it supported the Great American Outdoors Act of 2020, which allocates up to $9.5 billion over five years for repairs to roads, trails, and buildings in national parks and other federal areas. Such investments address a multibillion-dollar backlog, with the National Park Service estimating over $22 billion in needed work as of 2023, enabling sustained public access and preservation without expanding land use restrictions beyond core historic protections.
Regarding federal oversight, the National Trust has critiqued proposals that prioritize modern alterations over historical integrity, as seen in its October 2025 letter opposing the Trump administration's plan to demolish the White House East Winga structure tied to significant 20th-century historyfor a 90,000-square-foot ballroom addition, urging consultation with preservation experts to avoid irreversible aesthetic and contextual damage. Preservation on public lands yields economic benefits, including $55.6 billion in annual output from national park tourism supporting 415,400 jobs, though federal designations can constrain activities like mining or drilling to prevent degradation of protected features.

NTCIC structures equity investments via limited partnerships with developers, channeling investor capital into historic property rehabs that yield tax benefits while fostering community revitalization; by December 2023, it had supported over $7 billion in total project costs across more than 1,000 initiatives, including urban infill developments and affordable housing conversions. This arm operates autonomously, retaining fees from syndication to fund its activities without drawing on parent resources, thereby enabling scalable financing for preservation-aligned economic development.
National Trust Insurance Services, LLC, established in 2003 as another for-profit subsidiary, provides customized insurance coverage for historic buildings, owners, and related tourism operations, generating revenue through premiums to underwrite preservation risks independently. This entity isolates potential claims liabilities, allowing it to offer products like property damage and liability policies tailored to irreplaceable structures without exposing the National Trust's broader assets.
National Trust Tours, operating as a subsidiary since the 1970s, curates educational travel programs to historic sites worldwide, functioning as a membership perk that produces self-funding income via tour fees to subsidize preservation grants indirectly. Its independent management ensures operational flexibility in itinerary planning and partnerships, contributing experiential revenue streams segregated from the parent entity's programmatic budgeting.

Such districts also exhibit lower vacancy rates due to incentivized maintenance and reinvestment, fostering community stability and cohesion through tangible connections to shared history, which in turn supports long-term neighborhood vitality without relying on preservation as mere stasis.

Criticisms, Controversies, and Limitations

Barriers to Economic Development and Housing

Historic preservation designations advocated by organizations like the National Trust for Historic Preservation impose regulatory hurdles that elevate development costs and constrain housing supply in urban areas. These designations often mandate adherence to strict aesthetic and material standards for new construction or renovations, which can increase project expenses by requiring specialized labor and materials incompatible with modern, cost-efficient building techniques. For instance, in Seattle, landmark preservation reviews have rejected proposals for high-density apartment buildings, such as a 200-unit development near a transit hub that was denied in favor of retaining a surface parking lot, effectively blocking infill opportunities on underutilized land. Similar delays have occurred elsewhere, including an eight-year holdup in San Francisco for converting a low-rise building into an eight-story apartment complex due to preservation oversight. 
Such restrictions contribute to broader housing shortages by limiting the ability to densify existing neighborhoods, where demand for affordable units is acute. Critics argue that by prioritizing the retention of historic facades or structuresoften of marginal architectural significancethese policies deter mixed-use infill projects that could add hundreds of units, as seen in cases where landmarking halted plans for 124- and 63-unit apartments in Seattle, leading to lost opportunities estimated at over 1,000 potential homes in recent years. In Denver, preservationist opposition delayed the adaptive reuse of a 1960s diner into residential and retail space, illustrating how mandates favoring heritage aesthetics over functional redevelopment stifle economic vitality. Empirical analyses indicate these barriers exacerbate supply constraints in growing cities, where restricted redevelopment correlates with elevated rents and displacement of lower-income residents, as preservation effectively caps the housing stock adaptable to current needs.
The trade-offs manifest starkly in economically stagnant or declining districts, where short-term preservation gainssuch as maintaining cultural landmarkscan entrench underutilization by prohibiting density increases or modern economic adaptations. While designations preserve existing older housing stock that might otherwise serve as naturally occurring affordable units, they simultaneously hinder new construction that could revitalize blighted areas through higher-density infill, potentially leading to long-term population outflows and fiscal strain on municipalities. In high-demand markets, this dynamic amplifies affordability crises, as evidenced by studies linking preservation regimes to reduced housing supply and upward pressure on prices, underscoring a causal tension between heritage protection and dynamic urban growth.

Challenges to Property Rights and Local Autonomy

The National Trust for Historic Preservation has pursued litigation and easement programs that impose restrictions on private property owners, limiting their ability to alter, develop, or sell historic structures without external approval. Through its legal advocacy, the organization participates in disputes to enforce preservation standards, often challenging owners' decisions that conflict with national or designated historic criteria. These interventions, while aimed at safeguarding cultural resources, have drawn criticism for overriding individual property liberties, as easements held by the Trust or affiliates bind future owners to perpetual covenants that curtail modifications.
Courts have scrutinized the standing of nonprofits like the National Trust to intervene in private property matters, particularly when seeking to block sales or demolitions absent direct ownership or injury. In historic preservation cases, plaintiffs lacking proprietary interest in the property have faced hurdles demonstrating the requisite personal stake, as rulings emphasize that non-owners cannot assert control over buildings without legal ties. Such challenges highlight tensions in NGO-driven enforcement, where national organizations assert influence over local transactions, potentially delaying or derailing private sales to prioritize preservation agendas over owner autonomy.
Economist Edward Glaeser has critiqued preservation ordinancesfrequently supported by national advocacy groups like the National Trustas mechanisms that stifle urban innovation and encroach on property rights by restricting adaptive reuse and new construction. In his analysis, excessive landmarking elevates preservation over economic dynamism, confining development options and favoring stasis that disadvantages owners seeking viable uses for aging structures. Glaeser argues these regulations, by design, diminish the "option value" of land, compelling owners to forgo profitable alterations in favor of maintenance that yields limited reciprocal economic benefits.
Compliance with preservation mandates imposes measurable burdens on owners, including heightened redevelopment costs from regulatory reviews and diminished flexibility in property disposition. Property owners in designated areas encounter elevated insurance premiums and fewer willing carriers due to perceived risks, exacerbating holding expenses without commensurate incentives for all. Critics contend these national-level impositions undermine local decision-making, advocating decentralized approaches where communities enact tailored ordinances rather than deferring to NGO-led national standards that homogenize priorities and erode site-specific autonomy. Local historic districts, unlike honorary National Register listings, allow jurisdictions to balance preservation with owner rights through ordinances under direct community oversight, offering a counterpoint to centralized interventions.

Specific Disputes and Legal Challenges

In October 2025, the National Trust for Historic Preservation publicly opposed the Trump administration's demolition of the White House East Wing to facilitate construction of a 90,000-square-foot ballroom addition. On October 21, 2025, the organization submitted a letter to the National Capital Planning Commission, National Park Service, and Advisory Council on Historic Preservation, urging an immediate halt to demolition until comprehensive plans were disclosed and subjected to public and expert review, citing risks to the site's historic fabric and procedural exemptions under the National Historic Preservation Act. The administration proceeded with the work, which began earlier that month and was expected to complete the East Wing's full demolition by late October, dismissing preservation concerns as unwarranted given the White House's explicit exemption from standard federal preservation requirements.
The Trust has engaged in numerous legal challenges via amicus curiae briefs supporting litigation against perceived governmental overreaches threatening historic sites, such as a November 2023 brief backing challenges to municipal actions in New York and an August 2024 brief defending Liberty Hall (the Odd Fellows Building) in Washington, D.C., from incompatible alterations. These filings, aimed at enforcing preservation laws like Section 106 of the National Historic Preservation Act, have produced mixed judicial outcomes, with federal courts upholding preservation arguments in some instances but rejecting them in others involving development priorities. Such interventions typically extend dispute resolutions over multiple years, imposing substantial litigation burdens on opposing parties through required procedural reviews and appeals.
