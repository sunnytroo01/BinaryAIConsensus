Sodium dodecyl sulfate
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS), also known as sodium lauryl sulfate, is an anionic surfactant and the sodium salt of a 12-carbon chain organosulfate with the chemical formula CHNaOS and a molecular weight of 288.38 g/mol. It typically appears as a white to off-white crystalline powder, is highly soluble in water, and has a melting point of 204207 C.
As a versatile ionic detergent, SDS is widely employed in biochemical and molecular biology applications due to its ability to disrupt biological membranes, solubilize proteins, and denature them by unfolding their structure and imparting a uniform negative charge based on mass. In particular, it is a key component in sodium dodecyl sulfatepolyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE), where it enables the separation of proteins by size, as well as in cell lysis for DNA and RNA extraction protocols.
Beyond research, SDS serves as a foaming and cleansing agent in numerous consumer and industrial products, including shampoos, toothpastes, mouthwashes, soaps, and household detergents, owing to its effective emulsifying and wetting properties. It is also utilized in pharmaceutical formulations and as a dispersant in various chemical processes. However, SDS can be irritating to skin, eyes, and mucous membranes, necessitating proper handling to avoid toxicity at high concentrations.

Properties

Physical properties

Sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) has the molecular formula CHNaOS and a molecular weight of 288.38 g/mol.
It appears as a white or light yellow crystalline powder and is odorless.
SDS exhibits high solubility in water, reaching up to 200 g/L at 20C, and forms micelles above its critical micelle concentration (CMC) of approximately 8.2 mM at 25C.
The compound has a melting point of 204207C, at which it decomposes, and a density of 1.1 g/cm.
As an anionic surfactant, SDS reduces the surface tension of water to approximately 40 mN/m at the CMC.
SDS demonstrates thermal stability up to around 200C, beyond which thermal degradation occurs.

Chemical properties

Sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS), with the chemical formula CX12HX25NaOX4S\ce{C12H25NaO4S}CX12HX25NaOX4S, features a linear hydrophobic dodecyl chain consisting of 12 carbon atoms attached to a hydrophilic sulfate ester group (OSOX3\ce{-OSO3-}OSOX3) balanced by a sodium counterion. This amphiphilic structure, where the alkyl chain is CHX3(CHX2)X11X\ce{CH3(CH2)11-}CHX3(CHX2)X11X, enables its surfactant properties. The IUPAC name is sodium dodecyl sulfate, though it is commonly referred to as sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS) or simply SDS.
SDS is distinguished from related compounds like sodium laureth sulfate (SLES), which incorporates ethoxylated (polyethylene oxide) chains in the hydrophilic portion, altering its foaming and mildness characteristics. As an anionic surfactant, SDS derives its charge from the sulfate head group, which has a pKa of approximately 1.9, ensuring complete dissociation (OSOX3\ce{-OSO3-}OSOX3 and NaX+\ce{Na+}NaX+) in neutral and alkaline aqueous environments above pH 3. This ionization facilitates electrostatic repulsion between head groups and attraction to water molecules.
In aqueous solutions, SDS exhibits amphiphilic behavior, self-assembling into micelles above its critical micelle concentration (CMC). The mechanism involves the hydrophobic tails aggregating to minimize contact with water, forming a nonpolar core, while the charged sulfate heads form a Stern layer at the surface, stabilized by the counterions in the diffuse electrical double layer. Typical SDS micelles are spherical with an aggregation number of 6070 surfactant molecules and a hydrodynamic radius of about 2.02.2 nm, though these values can vary slightly with temperature and ionic strength.
Regarding reactivity, SDS is susceptible to acid-catalyzed hydrolysis, particularly under strongly acidic conditions (pH < 2), where the ester linkage cleaves to yield dodecanol (lauryl alcohol) and sodium bisulfate or sulfuric acid; this reaction accelerates in micellar solutions due to increased local concentration. In contrast, SDS demonstrates high stability in alkaline media, with no significant decomposition even at elevated temperatures up to 55C in neutral to basic pH. Additionally, its anionic nature leads to incompatibility with divalent cations like CaX2+\ce{Ca^{2+}}CaX2+ and MgX2+\ce{Mg^{2+}}MgX2+, forming insoluble precipitates of calcium or magnesium dodecyl sulfate that reduce solubility and affect performance in hard water.

Production

Industrial synthesis

Sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) is primarily produced on an industrial scale through the sulfation of lauryl alcohol (dodecanol, C12_{12}12H25_{25}25OH) using sulfur trioxide (SO3_33) gas, followed by neutralization with sodium hydroxide (NaOH). This method is favored for its efficiency and ability to handle large volumes, with alternative sulfating agents like chlorosulfonic acid used less commonly due to higher costs and corrosion issues.
Lauryl alcohol, the key raw material, is derived from natural sources such as coconut oil or palm kernel oil through hydrogenation of the corresponding fatty acids, yielding a mixture rich in C12_{12}12 chain length. SO3_33 is generated on-site from sulfuric acid plants via dehydration and distillation, ensuring a steady supply for continuous operations.
The sulfation reaction occurs in a continuous falling-film reactor, where lauryl alcohol flows down as a thin film on the reactor walls while SO3_33 gas is introduced countercurrently, maintaining temperatures between 3050C to minimize side reactions like polymerization or excessive charring. Modern optimizations focus on sustainability, such as improved gas scrubbing to reduce SO3_33 emissions and the use of stabilized SO3_33 to enhance process safety and efficiency.

Laboratory preparation

In laboratory settings, sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) is commonly prepared on a small scale by sulfating lauryl alcohol with concentrated sulfuric acid or oleum, followed by neutralization with sodium hydroxide to achieve high purity suitable for research applications. The sulfation step proceeds via the reaction C_{12}H_{25}OH + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow C_{12}H_{25}OSO_3H + H_2O, producing dodecyl hydrogen sulfate, which is then neutralized: C_{12}H_{25}OSO_3H + NaOH \rightarrow C_{12}H_{25}OSO_3Na + H_2O.
Detailed procedures typically involve dissolving lauryl alcohol (purity 98-99.5%) in a solvent such as chloroform, cooling to 0-10C, and slowly adding the sulfating agent (e.g., chlorosulfonic acid or sulfur trioxide equivalent) with vigorous stirring for 1-2 hours to form the acid sulfate. The mixture is then neutralized with 20-34% NaOH solution at ambient temperature, concentrated under reduced pressure to 60-85% solids, and the product extracted or filtered. Purification is achieved by low-temperature drying (40-100C) and recrystallization from ethanol, yielding 90-99% with purity exceeding 98% (e.g., 99.5% in optimized runs). Analytical verification includes high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) for purity assessment and nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy to confirm structure and absence of impurities.
Due to the corrosive and reactive nature of sulfuric acid, oleum, or sulfur trioxide derivatives, all handling must occur in a well-ventilated fume hood with appropriate personal protective equipment; post-reaction neutralization should be exothermic and monitored to avoid splattering.
Variations for specialized research include isotopically labeled SDS, such as the fully deuterated analog (d_{25}-SDS), prepared by substituting deuterated lauryl alcohol in the standard sulfation-neutralization sequence, enabling applications in NMR spectroscopy for membrane protein studies without altering the core procedure.

Applications

Personal care and cleaning

Sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS), also known as sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS), serves as a primary foaming agent in various personal care and cleaning products, including shampoos, toothpastes, and soaps, typically at concentrations of 12% to enhance lather formation and cleansing efficacy by reducing the surface tension of water. This reduction in surface tension allows SDS to create stable foam that aids in the removal of dirt and oils from skin and hair, making it a staple in rinse-off formulations where effective cleaning without residue is essential. In toothpastes, SDS at 12% w/w acts similarly to promote foaming during brushing, improving the dispersion of active ingredients and overall user experience. Similarly, in some mouthwashes, SLS is added as a surfactant to serve as an emulsifying and surface cleaning agent. It helps reduce surface tension, emulsify ingredients, facilitate the removal of debris and oils from the oral cavity, create foam for better distribution of active ingredients, and enhance the solubility and bioavailability of other components like fluoride or antimicrobials.
In cleaning applications, SDS functions by emulsifying oils and dirt through micelle solubilization, where its amphiphilic structure enables the formation of micelles that encapsulate hydrophobic substances, facilitating their removal in aqueous solutions; this mechanism is particularly valuable in dishwashing liquids and laundry detergents. As an anionic surfactant, SDS is commonly incorporated into these products to suspend particulate matter and break down greasy residues, contributing to thorough cleaning performance. For instance, in laundry detergents, SDS's ability to interact with positively charged dirt and oily stains enhances its detergency, while in dishwashing formulations, it supports grease-cutting and foam stability.
Formulations often combine SDS with milder co-surfactants like cocamidopropyl betaine to reduce potential irritation while maintaining effective cleansing in shampoos, creating a balanced system that boosts foam volume and mildness for sensitive skin. Historically, SDS was introduced in toothpastes during the 1930s, marking a shift toward synthetic surfactants for improved foaming over traditional soap-based alternatives.
SDS demonstrates high wetting efficiency on fabrics, rapidly lowering water-fabric contact angles to promote uniform penetration of cleaning solutions in textile processing and laundry applications. Environmentally, it exhibits strong biodegradability under aerobic conditions, with OECD 301 tests (guidelines 301B and 301D) showing degradation rates exceeding 94% within 28 days, confirming its ready biodegradability and compliance with international standards for sustainable surfactants. As of 2023, the global surfactants market was valued at over $45 billion, with SDS serving as a major component in personal care applications.

Food and cosmetics

Sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) functions as an emulsifier in select food applications, particularly to enhance stability and texture in aerated products. It is commonly incorporated into certain whipped products at concentrations up to 0.5% by weight (e.g., in gelatin for marshmallows), where it acts as a whipping aid to promote foam formation and retention by reducing interfacial tension between air bubbles and the liquid phase. This property allows for improved incorporation of air, resulting in lighter, more stable structures in products like dried egg white solids (limited to 0.1% or 1,000 ppm by weight) and frozen or liquid egg whites (up to 125 ppm).
In cosmetics, SDS contributes to formulation stability beyond cleansing roles, serving as an emulsifier that aids thickening in lotions and creams by stabilizing oil-in-water emulsions, often at concentrations of 0.55%. It also imparts foaming properties to bath products, enhancing user experience through lather generation at effective levels around 0.52%. Specific applications include its use in certain hair conditioners where it supports the deposition of conditioning agents onto the hair shaft. Efficacy studies demonstrate SDS's role in producing stable oil-in-water emulsions with droplet sizes below 1 m (e.g., d32 0.22 m), which resist coalescence and maintain product integrity.
Regulatory approvals affirm SDS's safety in these contexts: the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) classifies it as generally recognized as safe (GRAS) for food use under specified conditions (21 CFR 172.822), permitting up to 0.5% in general emulsified foods and lower limits in whipped and egg products. For cosmetics, concentrations are typically limited to 0.52% in rinse-off products and lower in leave-on products based on safety assessments to minimize irritation potential.

Scientific and laboratory uses

Sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) is widely employed in biochemical laboratories for protein denaturation and separation, most notably in sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE). In this technique, SDS disrupts non-covalent interactions within proteins, including hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions, and van der Waals forces, leading to their unfolding into linear polypeptides. This denaturation process is essential for standardizing protein migration based on size rather than native charge or conformation.
The mechanism of SDS in SDS-PAGE involves the detergent binding to proteins at a ratio of approximately 1.4 grams of SDS per gram of protein, which coats the polypeptide chains with a uniform negative charge proportional to their length. This binding unfolds the proteins into rod-like structures, with roughly one SDS molecule associating with every two amino acids, overwhelming the intrinsic charges of the protein and imparting an electrophoretic mobility inversely related to molecular weight. SDS is incorporated into polyacrylamide gels at concentrations of 0.1% to 1%, facilitating separation in a porous matrix under an electric field.
SDS-PAGE was introduced by Ulrich K. Laemmli in 1970 as a high-resolution method for analyzing protein cleavage during bacteriophage T4 head assembly, and it has since become a cornerstone of molecular biology for estimating protein molecular weights. In this system, proteins migrate through the gel such that their relative mobility (Rf) follows a logarithmic relationship with molecular weight, allowing separation of polypeptides from about 5 to 250 kDa. Common buffer systems, such as Tris-glycine at pH 8.3 with 0.1% SDS, maintain the necessary ionic conditions for consistent electrophoresis.
Beyond electrophoresis, SDS serves as a key component in cell lysis buffers for protein extraction, where its surfactant properties disrupt lipid membranes and release intracellular contents without excessive nucleic acid contamination when used at 1-2% concentrations. It is particularly effective for solubilizing hydrophobic membrane proteins, enabling their isolation and study in denatured form for downstream analyses like immunoblotting. Additionally, SDS is utilized in DNA extraction protocols prior to PCR, aiding in cell lysis and protein denaturation to facilitate nucleic acid release, though its carryover must be minimized to avoid polymerase inhibition.

Pharmaceutical and medical uses

Sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) serves as a key excipient in pharmaceutical formulations, particularly for enhancing the solubility and bioavailability of poorly water-soluble active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) through micelle formation. In oral and topical drug delivery systems, SDS is typically incorporated at concentrations below 1% to solubilize hydrophobic drugs and improve their dissolution rates, thereby facilitating better absorption in the gastrointestinal tract or across the skin. For instance, in transdermal niosomal formulations, SDS at 0.050.40% (w/v) has been shown to increase nanovesicle stability and promote API permeation by altering surface charge and reducing aggregation.
Beyond solubilization, SDS exhibits antimicrobial properties by disrupting bacterial cell membranes, making it a useful adjuvant in oral care products such as mouthwashes. At concentrations of 0.11%, SDS denatures proteins and compromises membrane integrity, leading to bacterial cell leakage and reduced plaque formation, with effects persisting up to 7 hours post-application. In specific medical applications, SDS has been incorporated into nanoparticle coatings for targeted drug delivery; for example, SDS-supported iron silicophosphate nanocomposites enable pH-responsive release of drugs like ondansetron, achieving up to 45% release in acidic gastric conditions while minimizing cytotoxicity.
Efficacy studies highlight SDS's role in enhancing skin permeation, particularly in topical formulations. Post-2020 research using Franz diffusion cells with human cadaver skin demonstrated that SDS promotes surfactant monomer penetration, correlating with increased drug fluxoften by approximately 2-fold compared to controlsthrough interactions that temporarily disrupt the stratum corneum barrier. However, formulation challenges arise from SDS's potential for irritation, which limits its concentration in sensitive applications; prolonged exposure above 0.5% can cause epidermal damage, necessitating combinations with polymers like hydroxypropyl methylcellulose (HPMC) to achieve controlled release while mitigating irritancy.

Safety and environmental considerations

Human health effects

Sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS), also known as sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS), is recognized as an acute skin and eye irritant, particularly at concentrations above 1%. In rabbit models using the Draize test, a 10% aqueous solution of SDS produces moderate skin irritation with primary irritation indices around 4-6, characterized by erythema and edema that typically resolve within 24-48 hours. For ocular exposure, the same 10% concentration yields Draize scores in the moderate range (approximately 20-30), indicating corneal opacity, iritis, and conjunctival chemosis, though recovery occurs without permanent damage in most cases. Oral acute toxicity is moderate, with an LD50 value of approximately 977-1288 mg/kg in rats, leading to gastrointestinal irritation, diarrhea, and mucosal damage upon ingestion.
In oral applications such as toothpaste, SDS at typical concentrations of 1-2% can irritate the oral mucosa, potentially triggering or exacerbating recurrent aphthous stomatitis (canker sores) through desquamation and inflammation of the epithelial lining. Clinical evidence indicates that individuals prone to aphthous ulcers benefit from SLS-free dentifrices, as SLS disrupts the mucosal barrier and prolongs healing time. Regarding halitosis, SDS influences volatile sulfur compounds (VSCs), the primary contributors to bad breath, by inhibiting their production in the oral cavity; studies show that dentifrices containing SLS reduce morning VSC levels regardless of dosage, thereby mitigating halitosis in users. Additionally, SDS may contribute to dry mouth (xerostomia) by stripping the protective salivary mucin layer, reducing saliva retention and exposing tissues to further irritation, which is particularly problematic for those with pre-existing salivary hypofunction.
Chronic exposure to SDS shows no evidence of carcinogenicity, classified by the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) as Group 3 (not classifiable as to its carcinogenicity to humans), with no tumor promotion or genotoxic effects observed in long-term animal studies. However, sensitive individuals may develop allergic contact dermatitis, manifesting as eczematous reactions upon repeated dermal exposure, though SDS is primarily an irritant rather than a true sensitizer. Inhalation poses low risk due to SDS's poor volatility and limited aerosolization in consumer products, with respiratory irritation only occurring at high dust concentrations exceeding 3.9 mg/m. Dermal absorption is minimal, typically less than 1% of applied dose, as confirmed by in vivo penetration studies showing approximately 0.5% systemic uptake under normal use conditions.
A 2022 evaluation by the Australian Department of Health confirms SDS's low systemic toxicity profile, with no observed adverse effects in repeat-dose studies at relevant exposure levels. Toothpaste formulations containing 1-2% SDS are considered safe for most users, though SLS-free alternatives are recommended for those with mucosal sensitivity.
For individuals with oily sensitive skin, sulfate-based soaps containing SDS should be avoided, particularly in weekly cleansing routines, as SDS strips natural lipids from the skin, compromising the epidermal barrier and leading to dryness and increased sensitivity. This barrier disruption can trigger a rebound effect, where the skin overproduces oil in response to the dehydration, potentially exacerbating oiliness and contributing to acne through inflammation and clogged pores. Dermatological advice recommends replacing such products with gentle, non-stripping cleansers, such as those containing salicylic acid to address acne and excess oil, or niacinamide to soothe sensitivity and regulate sebum production.

Environmental impact

Sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) exhibits high biodegradability in environmental conditions, achieving greater than 70% degradation within 28 days as measured by the OECD Test Guideline 301B, classifying it as readily biodegradable. The primary degradation mechanism involves microbial desulfonation, converting SDS to lauryl alcohol, which is subsequently mineralized into carbon dioxide and water by aerobic bacteria. This process occurs efficiently in wastewater treatment systems and natural waters, minimizing long-term persistence.
Aquatic toxicity of SDS is moderate, with acute LC50 values for fish species such as fathead minnows ranging from 10 to 30 mg/L over 96 hours, primarily due to disruption of gill membranes and osmoregulatory functions. In invertebrates like daphnids, SDS impairs gill respiration and mobility at similar concentrations, while chronic exposure inhibits algal growth rates at levels exceeding 1 mg/L, potentially affecting primary productivity in ecosystems. Overall, these effects are concentration-dependent and mitigated by rapid dilution and biodegradation in receiving waters.
The environmental fate of SDS is characterized by moderate adsorption to sediments, with an organic carbon-water partition coefficient (Koc) of approximately 10^3, leading to accumulation in anaerobic sediments rather than widespread mobility. Bioaccumulation is low, reflected by an octanol-water partition coefficient (log Kow) of about 1.6, indicating limited uptake in aquatic organisms. SDS primarily enters aquatic systems via wastewater effluents from household and industrial sources, where detergents account for 1020% of total surfactant loads; river monitoring typically detects concentrations below 0.1 mg/L under normal conditions.
Mitigation strategies focus on enhanced wastewater treatment and formulation improvements, such as prioritizing linear alkyl chain structures in SDS variants, which accelerate biodegradation rates compared to branched analogs.

Regulatory status

Sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS), also known as sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS), is permitted in cosmetics under the European Union's Cosmetics Regulation (EC) No 1223/2009 provided that a safety assessment demonstrates no risk to human health. In the United States, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) includes SDS in its monograph for over-the-counter (OTC) oral care products, permitting concentrations of 0.1% to 5% as an inactive ingredient to support foaming and cleansing without exceeding safe exposure levels.
In the food sector, SDS is designated as food additive INS 487 by the Codex Alimentarius Commission and functions primarily as an emulsifier in select processed foods. The Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee on Food Additives (JECFA) established an acceptable daily intake (ADI) of 01 mg/kg body weight for SDS based on toxicological evaluations, indicating low risk at typical dietary exposure levels. However, it is prohibited in organic food production under standards such as the USDA National Organic Program (NOP), where synthetic surfactants like SDS are excluded to maintain organic integrity.
For industrial applications, SDS is registered under the European Union's REACH Regulation (EC) No 1907/2006 as a high-volume chemical, with annual production and import exceeding 10,000 tonnes, requiring detailed safety data submissions for environmental and health risk assessments. In the United States, it is listed on the Toxic Substances Control Act (TSCA) inventory as an active substance, subjecting it to EPA oversight for manufacturing, import, and use without specific volume-based restrictions beyond general reporting.
Recent regulatory updates include a 2023 review by California's Proposition 65 program, which did not list SDS as a carcinogen or reproductive toxicant following evaluation of exposure data. The International Fragrance Association (IFRA) incorporates SDS considerations in standards for fragrance formulations where it may serve as a solubilizer, recommending concentrations below irritation thresholds in rinse-off products to align with broader cosmetic safety guidelines.
Internationally, the Codex Alimentarius permits SDS (INS 487) as an emulsifier in specific food categories under the General Standard for Food Additives (GSFA), such as certain baked goods and confectionery, at levels not exceeding good manufacturing practice. Nanomaterials in cosmetics must undergo additional safety assessments under Regulation (EC) No 1223/2009, with potential prohibitions if risks to human health are identified.
