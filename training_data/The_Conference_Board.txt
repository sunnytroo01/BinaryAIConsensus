The Conference Board
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Conference Board is an independent, nonpartisan, nonprofit organization founded in 1916 as a member-driven think tank dedicated to delivering fact-based research and insights on economics, business management, and public policy challenges to corporate leaders and society. With a membership exceeding 2,000 companies and over 20,000 participating executives, it emphasizes consensus-driven analysis and peer networking to address real-world issues, producing more than 1,300 publications annually, including over 45 major research reports.
The organization gained prominence for its economic forecasting tools, notably the monthly Consumer Confidence Index, derived from surveys of U.S. consumer attitudes toward spending, inflation, and economic prospects, and the Leading Economic Index, a composite of ten indicators designed to signal turns in the business cycle. These indices, custodied by The Conference Board since the late 1990s, are utilized by policymakers, investors, and businesses for empirical assessments of economic expansions and contractions, reflecting its role in providing data-driven signals amid volatile conditions. Beyond indicators, it hosts conferences, councils, and events that connect executives across industries and geographies, fostering innovative solutions grounded in member input rather than ideological agendas.

History

Founding and Early Development (19161945)

The National Industrial Conference Boardlater renamed The Conference Board in 1970was founded on May 5, 1916, at the Hotel Gramatan in Bronxville, New York, by a group of prominent business leaders, including Magnus Alexander and Frederick W. Fish, amid rising labor unrest and economic pressures on American industry following events like the 1910 Los Angeles Times bombing and ongoing union militancy. The organization emerged as a business-led response to provide research and analysis on industrial relations, countering what founders viewed as biased or incomplete data from government and labor sources, with an initial budget of $100,000 and offices established in Boston to focus on topics such as workers' compensation laws.
Early operations emphasized empirical research to inform corporate decision-making during World War I. In 1917, the Board published its first report, Workmens Compensation Acts, analyzing state laws and their implications for employers. It supported the War Labor Board by recommending a tripartite structure for resolving disputes involving government, business, and labor representatives. By 1918, the Board developed the first U.S. Cost of Living Index, aggregating data on consumer prices to aid wartime corporate planning and wage adjustments.
In the 1920s, the organization relocated to New York City in 1920 and incorporated as a not-for-profit entity, expanding research into labor practices such as the eight-hour workday. Formal incorporation as a private nonprofit followed in 1924, solidifying its independence from specific trade associations that had initially sponsored it. During the Great Depression of the 1930s, the Board endured financial strain through staff reductions and salary cuts while continuing economic analyses; Magnus Alexander was succeeded as director by Virgil Jordan in 1932, who established the Bureau of Economic Audit and Control to systematize data collection on business cycles and fiscal trends.
World War II marked a pivot toward supporting industrial mobilization, with conferences on production efficiency, pricing controls, and resource allocation for defense contractors. In 1944, the Board created the Division of Business Practices to study postwar management techniques, laying groundwork for expanded operations amid demobilization challenges. Throughout this period, the organization's outputs prioritized factual, data-driven insights derived from member company inputs, distinguishing it from government statistics prone to political influence.

Postwar Expansion and Internationalization (19461990s)

Following World War II, The Conference Board intensified its focus on management practices and economic analysis to address postwar reconstruction and business adaptation, building on the Division of Business Practices established in 1944 for systematic research into operational efficiencies. Under president John Sinclair, appointed in 1949, the organization expanded its scope to include studies on production pricing, labor relations, and government-business coordination, capitalizing on the era's economic expansion and demand for data-driven insights.
Domestic growth accelerated in the 1950s, with annual revenues exceeding $1 million by 1951 and a workforce surpassing 200 employees, enabling broader research into consumer behavior and employment trends, such as the Help-Wanted Advertising Index launched that year. This period saw the initiation of international activities through sponsorship of events abroad, culminating in the opening of a Montreal office in 1954 to serve Canadian members and extend North American influence. The first overseas CEO conference in Torquay, United Kingdom, in 1959, followed by one in Versailles, France, signaled early steps toward global peer networking among executives.
The 1960s under president H. Bruce Palmer, starting in 1962, emphasized corporate social responsibility alongside economic tools, including the Consumer Confidence Index introduced in 1967, which tracked public sentiment on spending and employment to forecast downturns. Research influenced U.S. policy, such as a 1965 Treasury Department study advocating accelerated depreciation for businesses, demonstrating the organization's growing advisory role. Internationalization progressed incrementally, with continued CEO gatherings abroad fostering cross-border knowledge exchange.
By the 1970s, European expansion formalized with the Brussels office opening in 1977, supporting councils for regional executives and studies on topics like environmental regulations amid oil shocks and inflation. Domestic indicators proliferated, including the CEO Confidence Index in 1976, while President Gerald Ford addressed members that year on economic stabilization. The 1980s featured internal modernization under presidents James T. Mills from 1981 and Preston Townley from 1988, who reorganized research around competitiveness, technology upgrades, and restructuring amid global trade shifts.
Entering the 1990s, the organization advanced total quality management through annual conferences and assumed responsibility for the U.S. Leading Economic Index in 1996 from the Department of Commerce, solidifying its forecasting authority with composite indicators of 10 economic components. International reach extended to Asia via a 1994 agreement with China and a Hong Kong office in 1998, reflecting adaptation to emerging markets and supply chain globalization. These developments positioned The Conference Board as a key nonpartisan hub for multinational business intelligence by decade's end.

Contemporary Evolution and Challenges (2000sPresent)

In the 2000s, The Conference Board intensified its focus on executive challenges amid technological shifts, launching the annual CEO Challenge survey that identified the internet's rising strategic importance for business competitiveness and efficiency. This period saw sustained production of core economic indicators, including the Leading Economic Index (LEI), which provided early signals of business cycle turns, with independent releases formalized since 1996 but refined through ongoing methodological updates to incorporate global data. Membership grew steadily, supported by research into corporate governance and productivity, as the organization positioned itself as a neutral forum for peer benchmarking amid post-dot-com recovery and pre-financial crisis planning.
The 2010s marked leadership transitions, with Jonathan Spector serving as President and CEO around 2014, emphasizing navigation of economic volatility through expanded analytics on CEO tenurewhich averaged 8.4 years by 2011, down from 10 years in 2000and succession practices. Steve Odland succeeded as President and CEO by the late 2010s, driving digital adaptations with over 500 on-demand webcasts, 100+ podcasts, and annual outputs exceeding 45 major reports, alongside growth to serve more than 2,000 member companies and 20,000 executives across 60 countries. Strategic initiatives broadened into sustainability, corporate philanthropy, and social responsibility, with over $32 million invested in societal programs from 2016 to 2018, including education and inclusion efforts, reflecting corporate demands for integrated business-society metrics.
Entering the 2020s, The Conference Board adapted to crises like the COVID-19 pandemic by enhancing virtual events and resilience-focused research, while signaling recessions via LEI components showing negative growth rates, such as in mid-2025. Challenges included geopolitical risks and economic headwinds, with C-Suite Outlook surveys in 2025 citing trade warsparticularly U.S.-EU-China tensionsand recession probabilities as top executive concerns, affecting 46% of respondents as high-impact issues. As a membership-driven nonprofit, sustaining financial viability amid volatile corporate budgets required prioritizing high-value, data-driven insights, including new premium datasets like the expanded Consumer Confidence Survey launched in October 2025, which tracks personal financial expectations to aid forecasting. These adaptations underscore causal pressures from global fragmentation and technological disruption, compelling empirical, forward-looking analysis over narrative-driven commentary.

Organizational Structure and Operations

Mission, Governance, and Leadership

The Conference Board functions as a nonpartisan, not-for-profit, member-driven think tank, with a mission to deliver trusted insights on emerging business trends, economic forecasts, and management practices to enable organizations to anticipate challenges, optimize performance, and advance societal objectives. This entails convening senior executives for peer exchange, conducting independent research, and disseminating data-driven analyses that inform strategic decision-making across industries and geographies.
Governance is vested in a board of trustees selected from high-level executives, including chairs and CEOs of major global corporations such as Deloitte, McKinsey & Company, and State Farm, who provide oversight of strategic initiatives, financial stewardship, and programmatic priorities. Trustees, numbering around 20-30 active members at any time, meet periodically to guide the organization's direction while upholding its independence from partisan influences. Corporate members exercise indirect input through voting representatives who participate in annual proxies to elect or renew trustees, ensuring alignment with the interests of the 1,000+ member firms.
Leadership is headed by President and Chief Executive Officer Steve Odland, who took office on July 1, 2018, bringing prior experience as CEO of Office Depot and AutoZone, as well as roles in policy and education sectors. The executive team comprises specialized directors overseeing centers for economics, governance, sustainability, and global operations, such as Brian Campbell for U.S. governance research and Matteo Tonello for corporate leadership and finance. This structure supports the delivery of research outputs like the Leading Economic Index, while maintaining operational headquarters at 845 Third Avenue in New York City.

Membership Model and Financial Sustainability

The Conference Board operates as a membership-based non-profit organization, where corporate subscribers pay annual dues for exclusive access to research reports, economic forecasts, executive briefings, peer-to-peer councils, and benchmarking data. Membership is structured in tiers, including Council memberships that assemble confidential groups of around 40 executives for in-depth discussions on strategic issues, and Center memberships providing specialized insights across domains such as economic strategy, governance and sustainability, human capital, marketing, and public policy. These offerings allow members to customize participation based on organizational size and priorities, with benefits encompassing direct expert consultations, influence over research agendas, and participation in global events.
Eligibility targets corporations and executive teams seeking advanced business intelligence, with over 2,000 member organizations reported as of October 2023 and annual engagement involving more than 20,000 executives across programs. Dues are non-refundable and subject to annual adjustments, though specific fee structures are negotiated individually and not publicly disclosed, ensuring alignment with member budgets while funding the organization's peer-driven research model.
Financial sustainability derives primarily from program service revenues, which encompass membership fees, conference registrations, and custom research contracts, comprising 84.4% of the $99.8 million total revenue in fiscal year 2023. Supplementary income includes contributions (8.4%) and investment returns (2.5%), with expenses of $76.5 million yielding a $23.4 million surplus that bolsters reserves for operational continuity and expansion. In 2022, program services generated 86.1% of $87.6 million in revenue against $70.8 million in expenses, producing a $16.8 million surplus; 2021 saw 88% from programs amid $72.6 million revenue and a $10.3 million net gain. This consistent surplus pattern, absent heavy dependence on grants or endowments, underscores the model's viability in supporting independent, member-funded analysis amid economic fluctuations.

Global Network and Regional Centers

The Conference Board operates a global network comprising physical offices and specialized regional centers that deliver region-specific research, convene executive councils, and foster peer-to-peer collaboration among member companies. This structure supports the organization's mission by adapting insights to local economic, strategic, and human capital contexts while drawing on centralized expertise from its New York headquarters. Established progressively since the mid-20th century, the network expanded internationally to address growing multinational membership, with the Brussels office opening in 1977 to serve European executives through regular councils and events.
In North America, the primary hub is the headquarters at 845 Third Avenue in New York City, which coordinates overall operations and houses core research functions. An affiliate relationship exists with The Conference Board of Canada, based in Ottawa, enabling collaborative activities without a direct owned office.
Europe's presence centers on the Brussels office at Chausse de La Hulpe 178, which anchors thematic centers including Economy, Strategy & Finance and Human Capital. These centers produce analyses on regional business cycles, sustainability, and talent management, hosting councils for senior executives to exchange practices amid European regulatory and economic dynamics.
In Asia, offices in Singapore (WeWork 21 Collyer Quay), Hong Kong (14 Taikoo Wan Road, Taikoo Shing), and Beijing (Jin Tong West Road, Chaoyang District) support a China Center and additional foci on economy, strategy, finance, governance, sustainability, and human capital. These facilities facilitate localized forecasting for Asian markets and councils addressing challenges like talent retention in high-growth economies.
The organization extended its network to the Gulf region with the 2018 launch of a dedicated research center, emphasizing economics, strategy, finance, and human capital amid diversification efforts in oil-dependent economies. This initiative includes councils for strategy executives and leadership development, tailored to Gulf-based multinationals navigating transformation and regional integration.

Core Research Outputs

Economic Indicators and Forecasting Tools

The Conference Board publishes a suite of composite economic indexes, including the Leading Economic Index (LEI), Coincident Economic Index (CEI), and Lagging Economic Index (LAG), which collectively signal peaks and troughs in business cycles for the United States and other major economies. The LEI, comprising ten components such as average weekly manufacturing hours, initial unemployment claims, new manufacturing orders, building permits, stock prices, and the spread between long-term and short-term interest rates, anticipates turning points in economic activity by roughly seven months. In August 2025, the U.S. LEI fell 0.5 percent to 98.4 (base 2016=100), after rising 0.1 percent in July, indicating potential softening in growth momentum. These indexes, originally developed by the U.S. Department of Commerce and transferred to The Conference Board in the late 1990s, are updated monthly and serve as core tools for econometric analysis.
The CEI, which measures contemporaneous economic performance, aggregates four indicators: nonfarm payroll employment, personal income excluding transfer payments, industrial production, and manufacturing and trade sales. This index rose 0.1 percent in August 2025, reflecting ongoing expansion despite LEI weakness. Complementing these, the LAG tracks slower-moving variables like unemployment duration, commercial loans, and consumer installment debt to confirm cycle phases. The Conference Board extends similar leading indexes globally, covering 13 economies including China, Japan, and the Euro Area, with data accessible via its Data Central portal for cross-country comparisons.
Consumer-facing indicators include the Consumer Confidence Index (CCI), based on a diffusion index from monthly surveys of 5,000 U.S. households assessing current business and labor conditions as well as short-term expectations for income, business, and employment. The CCI, released on the last Tuesday of each month, dropped 3.6 points to 94.2 (1985=100) in September 2025, signaling diminished optimism amid persistent inflation concerns. Labor market tools such as the Employment Trends Index (ETI), which rose 0.76 percent in the latest report, and the Help Wanted OnLine index, up 1.0 percent, provide real-time insights into hiring trends and job availability.
These indicators underpin The Conference Board's forecasting framework, integrating them into probabilistic models for U.S. and global outlooks, including quarterly GDP projections and long-term estimates through 2039. Recession trackers derived from sustained LEI declinestypically six months of contractionaid in signaling downturn risks, while growth forecasts incorporate baseline and alternative scenarios based on policy and geopolitical variables. The organization's methodology emphasizes empirical diffusion across components, avoiding over-reliance on any single variable, to enhance predictive reliability for policymakers and businesses.

Business Management and Policy Studies

The Conference Board produces research on business management practices, emphasizing human capital, corporate governance, and leadership strategies to inform executive decision-making. Its Human Capital Center delivers data-driven insights on talent management, employee engagement, and workforce analytics, drawing from surveys of member companies to identify best practices for organizational resilience. The organization also publishes the Where to Hire Index, evaluating labor market competitiveness and hiring attractiveness for multinational companies; in the 2026 European edition, Denmark, Switzerland, Ireland, Sweden, and Finland rank highest due to strong talent pools, high productivity, advanced digitalization, flexible labor markets, and robust institutions, with abundant opportunities in sectors like technology, finance, engineering, and biotechnology, while the Netherlands and Germany stand out as strong contenders for their excellent work-life balance, engineering prospects, and expat-friendly environments. In corporate governance, annual reports such as the 2024 edition of Board Practices and Composition analyze trends among Russell 3000 and S&P 500 firms, documenting shifts like increased board diversity and oversight of executive compensation tied to long-term performance metrics.
Key outputs include practical guides like "How Boards Can Get Human Capital Management Right in Five Steps," derived from the 2023 report Brave New World: Creating Value Through HCM, which advises integrating workforce data into board-level strategy to mitigate risks such as talent shortages amid economic volatility. Recent analyses highlight the rising influence of Chief Human Resources Officers in boardrooms, with studies noting that CHROs now contribute to governance on people-related risks, as evidenced by interviews with CEOs and board chairs showing 63% of large firms elevating HR to C-suite strategic roles by 2025.
In policy studies, The Conference Board's Committee for Economic Development (CED) generates nonpartisan recommendations to enhance U.S. economic competitiveness, focusing on fiscal policy, workforce skills, and innovation. CED's solutions briefs, such as the October 2025 publication on maintaining U.S. leadership in science and technology, propose strengthening public investment oversight and expanding public-private partnerships to counter global rivals, based on analyses of federal R&D spending trends. Other outputs include policy backgrounders on health care subsidies, like the examination of Affordable Care Act mechanisms beyond premium tax credits, and statements on federal budget proposals advocating deficit reduction through targeted reforms rather than broad tax hikes. These efforts prioritize market-oriented solutions, with CED trusteesdrawn from business leadersendorsing policies like infrastructure modernization and AI governance to support private-sector growth.

Impact and Influence

Contributions to Economic Analysis and Policy

The Conference Board's Leading Economic Index (LEI), comprising ten forward-looking components such as average weekly manufacturing hours, new orders, and stock prices, provides empirical signals of U.S. business cycle peaks and troughs six to nine months ahead. First developed from U.S. Department of Commerce precursors in the 1930s and refined by The Conference Board since 1995, the LEI has demonstrated reliability in anticipating recessions, with sustained declines preceding every U.S. downturn since World War II. This index informs economic forecasting models used by central banks and governments, enabling proactive adjustments to fiscal and monetary stimuli amid weakening momentum, as seen in its 0.5% drop to 98.4 (2016=100) in August 2025 signaling potential slowdowns.
The organization's Consumer Confidence Index (CCI), derived from a monthly survey of 3,000 U.S. households since February 1967, quantifies short-term outlooks on income, business, and employment, correlating with consumer spending that accounts for roughly 70% of GDP. Rising CCI readings, such as the index's climb amid post-pandemic recovery phases, have guided policy responses by highlighting demand pressures, while dipslike the August 2025 expectations subindex falling below 80flag risks of curtailed expenditure and inflationary moderation. Federal Reserve officials have incorporated CCI data into assessments of household resilience, complementing unemployment and wage metrics for interest rate calibrations.
Via its Committee for Economic Development (CED), established in 1942 as a nonpartisan policy advocate, The Conference Board advances causal analysis of structural issues like fiscal sustainability and trade imbalances, producing reports that shape executive and legislative priorities through CEO-led recommendations. CED's work, including solutions for post-recession growth and supply chain resilience, has influenced U.S. policy debates on tax reform and infrastructure, emphasizing market-driven incentives over regulatory overreach. Globally, extensions of LEI and coincident indexes to over 20 economies support international coordination, as in G20 analyses of synchronized cycles. These outputs prioritize data-driven realism, countering overly optimistic projections from biased institutional forecasts by grounding predictions in verifiable leading signals.

Recognition and Broader Societal Role

The Conference Board has garnered recognition for its internal operations and enduring reliability in economic analysis. In December 2024, it was named one of America's Best Workplaces by Best Companies Group, based on employee feedback data evaluating workplace culture, benefits, and leadership effectiveness. Its economic indicators, such as the Leading Economic Index and Consumer Confidence Index, are widely acknowledged as authoritative benchmarks, cited by policymakers, central banks, and businesses for over a century due to their methodological rigor and predictive value.
Beyond economic forecasting, The Conference Board plays a pivotal role in shaping corporate governance and public policy through its nonpartisan research and convening power. Operating as a nonprofit since 1916, it facilitates peer dialogue among over 2,000 member companies and 20,000 executives, influencing standards in sustainability, corporate philanthropy, and social responsibility. Its Committee for Economic Development (CED), integrated in 2015, has historically contributed to major U.S. policy frameworks, including advocacy for the Marshall Plan in 1947, the Bretton Woods Agreement in 1944, the Employment Act of 1946which established the Council of Economic Advisersand expansions in pre-K education. These efforts underscore its function as a bridge between business insights and societal needs, promoting evidence-based approaches to issues like infrastructure, cybersecurity, and labor markets without partisan alignment.
The organization's societal investments further amplify its impact, with over $32 million allocated to initiatives in education, diversity, inclusion, and sustainable capitalism between 2016 and 2018 alone. By producing annual reports on topics such as corporate citizenship and ESG leadershiphonoring entities like AT&T and Prudential Financial in 2025The Conference Board fosters measurable progress in aligning business performance with broader public goods, including outcome-based philanthropy and employee-driven social programs. This work positions it as a key influencer in global standards for responsible capitalism, where empirical data guides corporate responses to inequality, environmental challenges, and economic opportunity.

Criticisms and Controversies

Questions of Objectivity and Partisanship

The Conference Board maintains that it operates as an independent, non-partisan, and non-profit organization, emphasizing its avoidance of direct involvement in political campaigns, donations to candidates, parties, or political action committees. Its funding model, reliant on membership dues from nearly 2,000 corporations and grants, supports research and peer networks but has prompted questions about potential corporate influence on outputs, particularly in policy-oriented work through subsidiaries like the Committee for Economic Development (CED). CED, described as a nonpartisan policy center, advocates for business-led solutions on issues including fiscal policy, democratic institutions, and economic inequality, generating revenue of approximately $72.6 million in 2021 primarily from such sources.
Critics from conservative perspectives have alleged progressive biases in The Conference Board's qualitative reports and initiatives, citing endorsements of diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) programs, environmental, social, and governance (ESG) frameworks, net-zero emissions targets by 2050, and facilitated race-based workplace discussions following George Floyd's death in 2020. A 2023 analysis questioned its nonpartisanship, pointing to webinars that dismissed anti-ESG activism as marginal "gadflyism" while promoting pro-ESG agendas, potentially aligning with Biden Administration priorities and the World Economic Forum, and highlighting unresponsiveness to opposing viewpoints. Conversely, left-leaning critics, such as the Canadian Union of Public Employees in 2010, faulted a report on public-private partnerships for superficial analysis that favored proponents by relying on biased value-for-money assessments from project agencies while ignoring auditors' general critiques of flawed methodologies and overstated savings.
Despite these concerns, The Conference Board's core economic indicators, such as the Consumer Confidence Index and Leading Economic Index, derive from empirical data collection and have faced no substantiated claims of manipulation, earning broad acceptance across ideological lines for their methodological rigor. Questions of partisanship thus center more on interpretive policy advocacy than quantitative outputs, with source credibility varying: conservative outlets like InfluenceWatch emphasize perceived left-leaning tilts in social initiatives, while union critiques target pro-privatization leanings reflective of its business constituency.

Engagement with Social and Political Issues

The Conference Board conducts extensive research on corporate engagement with social issues, particularly diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) initiatives and environmental, social, and governance (ESG) frameworks, tracking trends such as declining DEI disclosures among S&P 500 companieswhere usage of the "DEI" acronym fell by 68% in 2025 compared to 2024and companies reframing rather than abandoning these programs amid regulatory and shareholder scrutiny. Similarly, surveys indicate that 80% of corporations are adjusting ESG strategies in response to policy shifts, including tariffs and backlash, with nearly half of large U.S. firms reporting direct ESG-related opposition by 2025. These outputs emphasize risk management, advising boards to align political spending with core values and resist involvement in issues unrelated to business operations. 
On political issues, The Conference Board analyzes corporate political activity, noting that 78% of U.S. firms view the environment as "extremely" or "very" challenging due to polarization, stakeholder scrutiny, and social media amplification, with reports urging transparency in lobbying and contributions to mitigate conflicts. It maintains a policy of nonparticipation in campaigns, with no donations to candidates, parties, or PACs, positioning itself as a neutral advisor rather than an advocate. However, it has hosted discussions on media bias, featuring executives who acknowledge institutional left-leaning tendencies in journalism while defending fact-based reporting.
Critics, particularly from conservative outlets, argue that The Conference Board's emphasis on ESG and DEI research reveals a partisan tilt toward progressive priorities, despite its nonpartisan claims, as it has been accused of dismissing anti-ESG activism as marginal "gadflyism" in webinars and aligning with entities like the World Economic Forum. Such perspectives contend that this focus undermines objectivity, prioritizing advocacy for corporate social responsibility frameworks over balanced scrutiny of their empirical efficacy or economic costs, though The Conference Board's data-driven tracking of backlash trendssuch as surging anti-DEI shareholder proposals in 2025suggests responsiveness to evolving corporate realities rather than ideological promotion.

Professional Programs and Awards

Executive Development Initiatives

The Conference Board maintains specialized councils that facilitate executive development through peer networking, benchmarking, and strategic discussions tailored to senior leaders. The Leadership & Executive Development Council convenes executives to explore evolving methodologies for leadership training, including in-person, hybrid, remote, and technology-enabled approaches, while addressing trends like AI-driven change leadership and global scaling of development programs. Membership offers confidential peer exchanges and eligibility for HRCI and SHRM recertification credits, linking leadership strategies to broader organizational human capital frameworks.
Complementing this, the Coaching & Leadership Development Council emphasizes executive coaching practices across varying organizational maturity levels, with a focus on high-potential leader acceleration, team coaching, and ROI measurement for coaching investments. Participants engage in experiential meetings to refine coaching methods and adapt to future needs, benefiting from trusted, confidential dialogues that enhance development efficacy. Similarly, the Learning & Development Executives Council targets succession planning, executive coaching, and leadership pipelines, incorporating metrics to evaluate program impacts on productivity, employee engagement, and retention amid emerging technologies and cultural shifts.
Beyond councils, The Conference Board delivers immersive Leadership Experiences as customizable multi-day programs for executives and teams confronting strategic challenges. On-location offerings, such as the Apollo program drawing from NASA mission insights or the Gold Medal series informed by elite athletics, immerse participants in real-world scenarios to build resilience, innovation, and decision-making skills. Virtual adaptations, like the Adaptive Leader or Resilient Leader experiences, extend accessibility while maintaining focus on perspective shifts and team cohesion; these initiatives can be tailored to organizational priorities, fostering outcomes like enhanced adaptive capabilities in dynamic environments.

Notable Honors and Distinctions

The Conference Board received the Foreign Policy Association (FPA) Medal on June 28, 2024, the organization's highest honor, awarded for its insights on geopolitics and global business challenges; President and CEO Steve Odland accepted the distinction.
In April 2024, The Conference Board was named one of the Best Companies to Work for in New York State by Best Companies Group, a research-driven program evaluating employee satisfaction, workplace culture, and benefits based on confidential surveys and employer questionnaires.
On December 18, 2024, it was recognized as one of America's Best Workplaces by Best Companies Group in partnership with BridgeTower Media, highlighting fulfilling work opportunities, collaborative environments, and contributions to leadership on pressing issues like economic forecasting and corporate governance.
In 2023, Comparably awarded The Conference Board for Best Leadership Teams, Best Company Outlook, and Best Places to Work in New York, derived from verified employee feedback on management effectiveness and organizational vision.
