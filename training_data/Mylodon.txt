Mylodon
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Mylodon is an extinct genus of giant ground sloth belonging to the family Mylodontidae, known primarily from the species Mylodon darwinii, which inhabited southern South America during the Pleistocene epoch from approximately 1.8 million to 10,000 years ago. These massive herbivores, adapted to cold climates in regions ranging from Bolivia to Patagonia, measured about 3 meters in length and weighed between 1,000 and 2,000 kilograms, featuring long claws, thick fur, and a robust build suited for foraging on vegetation in open grasslands and steppes.
The genus was first recognized through fossils collected by Charles Darwin in 1832 during the HMS Beagle voyage at Punta Alta, Argentina, with the species M. darwinii formally named in 1842 by anatomist Richard Owen in honor of Darwin's contributions. Subsequent discoveries, particularly in the late 19th century, yielded exceptionally well-preserved remainsincluding skin, fur, bones, and dungfrom sites like Mylodon Cave (Cueva del Milodn) in southern Chile, providing rare insights into the animal's soft tissues and lifestyle. These finds revealed that Mylodon did not climb trees or burrow like modern sloths but instead roamed terrestrially, using its powerful limbs to pull down branches and access food.
Recent analyses of coprolites and hair samples indicate that Mylodon darwinii was not strictly herbivorous but an opportunistic omnivore, incorporating animal protein into its diet alongside plants, as evidenced by elevated nitrogen isotope ratios in its remains. Pollen studies from preserved dung further suggest it thrived in a mosaic of grassy steppes and shrublands during the Late Glacial period, browsing on a variety of herbaceous plants. Phylogenetically, Mylodon forms a sister group to modern two-fingered sloths (Choloepus), diverging around 22 million years ago, highlighting its deep evolutionary ties to extant xenarthrans.
Mylodon became extinct at the Pleistocene-Holocene boundary, around 10,000 years ago, likely due to a combination of climatic shifts, changing vegetation, and possibly human hunting pressures as megafaunal populations declined across the Americas. The well-preserved cave specimens not only advanced paleontological understanding but also fueled early 20th-century speculation about surviving populations, though radiocarbon dating confirms their definitive extinction. Today, Mylodon serves as a key example of South American megafauna, illustrating the biodiversity lost during the late Quaternary extinction event.

Discovery and Fossil Record

Initial Discovery and Naming

During the second voyage of HMS Beagle from 1831 to 1836, Charles Darwin collected the first known fossils of Mylodon on September 23, 1832, at Punta Alta in Buenos Aires Province, Argentina. These specimens, excavated from coastal cliffs, included a nearly complete lower jaw and other bones that Darwin initially misidentified as remains of the giant ground sloth Megatherium, reflecting his early familiarity with South American megafauna described by French naturalist Georges Cuvier. The discovery marked Darwin's first major find of fossil vertebrates, occurring amid his geological observations influenced by Charles Lyell's Principles of Geology, which emphasized gradual processes over catastrophic events.
Upon returning to England, the specimens were analyzed by anatomist Richard Owen, who formally described and named the genus Mylodon and species M. darwinii in 1840, honoring Darwin as the collector. Owen's description, published in Fossil Mammalia as part of the Zoology of the Voyage of H.M.S. Beagle, was based primarily on the Punta Alta lower jaw, which featured distinctive hypsodont (high-crowned) teeth adapted for grinding vegetationcontrasting with the more peg-like teeth of Megatherium and other sloths, thus justifying a new genus. These molar-like teeth inspired the name Mylodon, from Greek mylos (mill) and odous (tooth), highlighting their role in distinguishing the taxon.
In the early 19th-century context, Darwin's Mylodon fossils contributed to advancing paleontological understanding of South American megafauna extinctions, aligning with Lyell's uniformitarianism published in 18301833, which posited that Earth's history could be explained by ongoing natural processes rather than sudden divine interventions. By demonstrating recent extinctions of large mammals in a region with no modern analogs, the finds supported a gradualist view of faunal turnover, influencing Darwin's later evolutionary ideas and the broader shift from catastrophism in geology.

Major Fossil Localities

The most prominent locality for Mylodon darwinii fossils is Cueva del Milodn in southern Patagonia, Chile, a large cave system within the Bentez conglomerate formation at the ecotone between the humid Andes and the arid Patagonian steppe. Discovered in 1895 by explorer Hermann Eberhardt and systematically excavated starting in 1896 by Otto Nordenskild, the site has yielded numerous well-preserved skeletal elements, including over 100 bones, skin fragments with embedded osteoderms, coprolites, and keratinous claw sheaths, offering exceptional insights into the species' biology and taphonomy due to the cave's cold, dry conditions that favored mummification. Radiocarbon dating of these remains places them in the Late Glacial period, with coprolites calibrated to approximately 15,90015,500 cal BP and bone samples around 12,900 BP, marking a key endpoint for Mylodon near the Pleistocene-Holocene transition.
Additional significant sites occur in southern Patagonia, including Palli Aike Cave and Fell's Cave, both in volcanic lava formations near the Strait of Magellan, Chile. These late Pleistocene cave deposits, part of the Lujanian South American Land Mammal Age, have produced abundant Mylodon bonessuch as those from at least seven individuals at Palli Aikestratigraphically associated with early human lithic tools in layers dated to roughly 11,0008,600 cal BP, highlighting the species' persistence into the terminal Pleistocene. The geological context of these basalt-hosted caves, formed in Pliocene-Pleistocene volcanic fields, underscores Mylodon's adaptation to open steppe and periglacial environments during climatic fluctuations.
In Argentina, early Pleistocene specimens attributed to Mylodon darwinii are documented from formations in Buenos Aires Province, such as the Tres Arroyos area, representing Ensenadan Stage (ca. 2.00.8 Ma) open-air fluvial and eolian deposits that indicate the genus's antiquity and broader initial distribution across the Pampas and Patagonia. These sites, including coastal and inland localities like Punta Alta, yield isolated bones and osteoderms in loessic and sandy sediments, providing stratigraphic evidence for Mylodon's evolution from middle to late Pleistocene faunas and its role in early South American megafaunal assemblages. Collectively, these cave and open-air contexts span the Pleistocene, with the youngest dated material confirming Mylodon's survival until the Pleistocene-Holocene boundary around 12,00010,000 years ago.

Preservation and Notable Specimens

Exceptional preservation of Mylodon darwinii remains, including soft tissues, has been documented primarily from Cueva del Milodn in southern Chile, where dry cave conditions facilitated the mummification of skin and associated structures. In 1895, explorer Hermann Eberhard discovered a large fragment of mummified skin bearing embedded osteoderms and covered with reddish-brown hair, marking one of the earliest finds of such preserved integument in a Pleistocene ground sloth. Subsequent expeditions in the late 19th century, including those led by Otto Nordenskjld in 1896 and Rodolfo Hauthal in 1899, recovered additional skin impressions with osteoderms and hair, which were analyzed for their morphological and histological features, revealing a dermal armor unique among xenarthrans.
Coprolites from Cueva del Milodn, collected during Nordenskjld's 1899 expedition and described in detail by Einar Lnnberg, represent some of the earliest known fossilized dung deposits attributed to Mylodon darwinii. These well-preserved "dung balls" contain plant macrofossils, including fragments of grasses and sedges, providing direct evidence of the animal's presence in the late Pleistocene.
Among the most notable specimens is the "Milodon saddle," a large partial skin patch with attached reddish-brown hair and embedded osteoderms, recovered from Cueva del Milodn and now exhibited at the Museo de La Plata in Buenos Aires, Argentina. This artifact, analyzed since its late 19th-century acquisition, has served as a key reference for studies on mylodontid integument. Additionally, mummified remains from the same site yielded ancient DNA in the 1990s, enabling molecular phylogenetic analyses that confirmed Mylodon darwinii's close affinities within the xenarthran order, particularly linking it to extant sloths.

Taxonomy

Classification and Species

Mylodon is classified within the superorder Xenarthra, order Pilosa, suborder Folivora, and family Mylodontidae, a group of extinct ground sloths characterized by their robust build and adaptation to terrestrial locomotion. This placement distinguishes Mylodon from the closely related family Megatheriidae, which features more complex, bilophodont teeth suited for shearing tough vegetation, whereas Mylodontidae like Mylodon exhibit simpler, cylindrical, open-rooted teeth optimized for grinding and crushing. The family's dentition and skeletal proportions reflect a divergence in feeding ecology and body plan within Folivora, with Mylodontidae showing a more generalized, less specialized morphology compared to the massive, cursorial forms of Megatheriidae.
The genus Mylodon is primarily represented by the type species M. darwinii, formally described by Richard Owen in 1840 based on mandibular remains from the Argentine Pampas. A second species, M. listai, has been proposed for larger Patagonian specimens, with its validity debated in recent analyses; a 2023 comparative study of postcranial elements from Argentine localities, including the tibia, femur, and astragalus, highlighted significant morphological differences in limb proportions and robusticity from M. darwinii, supporting M. listai as distinct but calling for further cranial evidence to confirm species-level separation.
Historically, Mylodon was often synonymized or confused with the congeneric Glossotherium, particularly in early classifications that lumped southern South American mylodontids together regardless of geographic variation. Twentieth-century taxonomic revisions, starting with works by Ameghino and later Kraglievich, resolved much of this confusion by emphasizing Mylodon's restricted southern Patagonian distribution and unique postcranial traits, separating it from the more northerly-ranging Glossotherium species.

Phylogenetic Position

Mylodon's phylogenetic position has been clarified through molecular and morphological analyses, establishing it as a member of the Folivora clade within Xenarthra, closely allied with living sloths. Early molecular evidence from mitochondrial DNA extracted from Mylodon darwinii bones in 1996 demonstrated a closer relationship to the two-toed sloth Choloepus than to the three-toed sloth Bradypus, supporting placement within Folivora rather than a separate ground sloth lineage. This finding challenged prior morphology-based views that emphasized a deep divergence between arboreal and terrestrial sloths.
Subsequent genomic studies in the late 2010s, including mitogenomic and nuclear exon data from Mylodon specimens analyzed at McMaster University's Ancient DNA Centre, reinforced this affinity, initially positioning Mylodon as sister to Choloepus within a mylodontid clade, with divergence estimates around 22 million years ago. Complementary palaeoproteomic analyses of collagen from Mylodon and related fossils further confirmed groupings of Mylodontidae with Choloepus based on shared protein sequences and highlighting convergences in dental and limb morphology that had previously misled morphological phylogenies. A 2019 mitogenomic study expanded this by incorporating additional fossil sloths, depicting Mylodon as part of a South American radiation within Folivora that diverged from the common ancestor of living sloths around 30 million years ago.
However, a 2024 Bayesian total-evidence dating analysis revises this topology, placing Choloepus as the earliest diverging member of Mylodontoidea around 34 million years ago, with Mylodon emerging as an early-diverging taxon within the Mylodontinae subfamily approximately 21 million years ago. Morphological cladistic analyses using parsimony methods have consistently placed Mylodon within the Mylodontidae family, often as a basal or early-diverging member of the Mylodontinae subfamily. These studies, incorporating craniodental and postcranial characters, depict strict consensus trees where Mylodon clusters near the base of Mylodontinae, with North American genera like Paramylodon emerging as derived offshoots via Pliocene migrations across the Isthmus of Panama during the Great American Biotic Interchange. Such phylogenies underscore Mylodon's role as a foundational taxon in the diversification of mylodontid ground sloths.

Description

General Morphology and Size

Mylodon was a robust, quadrupedal ground sloth characterized by a barrel-shaped torso and a short tail, adaptations that supported its terrestrial lifestyle in open habitats across southern South America. Its body plan featured powerful limbs with strong, weight-bearing proportions suited for foraging and movement on the ground, distinguishing it from arboreal sloths.
The overall body length of Mylodon ranged from 3 to 4 meters. Mass estimates, derived from allometric scaling of complete skeletons including femoral dimensions and craniodental measurements, place it between 1000 and 2000 kilograms, making it one of the larger members of the Mylodontidae family. These dimensions reflect a heavily built herbivore capable of supporting its bulk on all fours while occasionally rearing up on hind limbs to browse vegetation.

Cranial and Dental Anatomy

The skull of Mylodon darwinii is elongated and robust, typically measuring 5971.5 cm in total length, with a low braincase confined to the posterior third of the cranium. This structure features a prominent sagittal crest along the parietals, which provides extensive attachment area for the temporalis muscles to facilitate powerful jaw adduction. The overall cranial profile is dolichocephalic, with a narrow rostrum that widens posteriorly, and the braincase exhibits a relatively large cerebellar region relative to the smaller cerebrum, indicative of limited encephalization typical of xenarthrans.
Nasal openings are notably large and expansive, with broad nasal bones forming a wide posterior aperture bounded by the pterygoids and a prominent vertical internarial fossa for cartilage support; this configuration suggests adaptations for enhanced olfaction, potentially aiding in foraging detection over distances.
The dentition of Mylodon darwinii comprises 16 teeth total, with 8 hypsodont, rootless molariforms in each jaw and no incisors or canines. These teeth lack enamel and consist primarily of orthodentine covered by cement, forming cylindrical structures that include anterior caniniform-like forms and posterior molariforms suited for grinding vegetation through continuous eruption and wear. The alveolar series spans approximately 1215 cm, with teeth featuring deep pulp cavities and vascular medullary canals for persistent growth.

Postcranial Skeleton

The postcranial skeleton of Mylodon is characterized by a robust vertebral column that provided structural stability during locomotion. It consists of 7 cervical vertebrae, 16 dorsal vertebrae, 3 lumbar vertebrae, and approximately 20 caudal vertebrae, with extensive fusion in the dorsal and lumbar regions contributing to a stiff trunk adapted for supporting the animal's weight and facilitating deliberate, plantigrade movement.
The forelimbs are notably robust, featuring a short and thick humerus with prominent deltoidal and pectoral ridges for strong muscle attachments, enabling powerful pulling actions. The radius and ulna exhibit deep muscular impressions and lateral displacement of scars, supporting forearm strength for digging or uprooting vegetation, while the manus includes large, curved claws on digits II and III that enhanced prehensile grip and substrate manipulation.
In contrast, the hindlimbs are shorter relative to the forelimbs but powerfully constructed for weight-bearing, with a broad, flattened femur featuring a hemispherical head and strong trochanters for robust muscle leverage, and a massive tibia that ensured stable ground contact during quadrupedal progression.
A 2023 comparative analysis of postcranial remains identified two distinct morphotypes within the genus: M. darwinii from the Pampean region and M. listai from Patagonia, differentiated primarily by pelvic and femoral robusticity, including a smaller intercondylar space and less medial condyle displacement in the femur of M. listai, resulting in straighter legs compared to the more bowed configuration in M. darwinii.

Integument and Soft Tissues

The integument of Mylodon darwinii featured a robust skin structure embedded with numerous small osteoderms, or dermal ossicles, which formed a protective armor against potential predators. These bony plates, varying in shape from polygonal to irregular and measuring 1-5 mm in diameter, were distributed in disorganized patterns or organized rows across the hide, as revealed by radiographic analysis of mummified skin fragments from Cueva del Milodn in southern Chile. This dermal armor is analogous to the ossified integument of modern armadillos (Cingulata), another xenarthran group, suggesting a convergent adaptation for defense in mylodontid ground sloths, though less extensively plated.
Preserved hair from Mylodon specimens indicates a coarse, shaggy coat with uniformly tubular shafts that lacked a medulla and featured a wavy cuticle pattern with irregular scale orientation. The fur exhibited a reddish-brown hue, consistent with gingery tones observed in multiple samples, and individual hairs varied in length from 5 cm on the head and back to over 20 cm on the limbs, contributing to an overall dense pelage. A 2025 biophysical modeling study indicated that fur thicknesses of 10 mm eliminated thermal stress during warmer months at mid-latitudes, while 30-50 mm provided substantial protection against cold stress in Patagonia's Pleistocene climate, with 50 mm offering near-complete yearly insulation at southern latitudes.
Evidence of soft tissues in Mylodon is limited but includes muscle attachment scars on skeletal elements, such as the roughened nasal arch and robust limb bone processes, which suggest anchorage for powerful musculature beneath a thick hide capable of supporting the animal's massive body. Mummified skin samples further confirm the hide's substantial thickness, estimated at several millimeters, with no indications of pigmentation patterns or markings beyond the uniform reddish-brown of the overlying fur.

Distribution and Chronology

Geographic Distribution

Mylodon darwinii occupied a core range in southern South America, spanning from southern Bolivia at approximately 19S latitude to Tierra del Fuego at 53S, including territories in Argentina, Chile, Uruguay, Paraguay, and southern Brazil. This latitudinal extent highlights its adaptation to a broad swath of southern continental environments, from subtropical fringes to subantarctic conditions.
The species favored open grasslands and shrublands, such as those in the Pampas and Patagonia, which expanded and contracted during glacial-interglacial cycles. These habitats provided ample herbaceous vegetation and browse, suited to the sloth's ecological niche in arid to semi-arid steppes and sedge-dominated meadows.
Fossil evidence comes from numerous sites, with concentrations in the Andean foothills and coastal plains across its range. Such distributions underscore Mylodon's presence in ecotonal zones linking highland and lowland biomes.

Temporal Range and Evolution

Mylodon, a genus of mylodontine ground sloth, is known from the Pleistocene epoch in South America (EnsenadanLujanian stages), with fossils dating from the late Early Pleistocene (approximately 0.8 million years ago) to the Early Holocene (around 10,000 years ago), and reaching peak abundance during the Late Pleistocene. This temporal span reflects the broader diversification of mylodontids during a period of significant climatic and vegetational changes in southern South America.
The origins of Mylodon trace back to the evolution of mylodontids in South America, where early members of the family appeared around 5 million years ago during the late Miocene. These sloths diversified in isolation on the continent, with Mylodon emerging as a distinct lineage in the Pleistocene. During the Great American Biotic Interchange approximately 3 million years ago, related mylodontines migrated northward across the emerging Isthmus of Panama, giving rise to genera such as Paramylodon in North America.
Over its evolutionary history, Mylodon exhibited trends toward increasing body size, adaptations likely linked to the expansion of open habitats. These changes underscore Mylodon's adaptation to shifting ecosystems, from forested to more arid, grassy landscapes.

Paleobiology

Diet and Foraging

Mylodon darwinii was primarily a herbivorous grazer, as evidenced by pollen analysis of Late Glacial coprolites from Mylodon Cave in southern Chile, which reveal a diet dominated by grasses (Poaceae) and sedges (Cyperaceae), alongside significant amounts of browse such as Azorella (>25% of pollen) and fruits like Fragaria chiloensis. These findings indicate selective foraging in open tundra-steppe environments, where abrasive vegetation was prevalent, supported by the species' hypsodont (high-crowned) teeth adapted to withstand wear from gritty plant material and soil particles.
Stable isotope analysis of bone collagen, including C and N values from amino acids like phenylalanine and glutamic acid, further suggests opportunistic omnivory in Mylodon, with elevated trophic position indicators (N Glx-Phe of 2.63.1) pointing to occasional consumption of animal protein, such as meat or insects, beyond a purely plant-based diet. This is corroborated by dental microwear studies on related Oligocene mylodontoid sloths, which show orthodentine textures consistent with a mixed diet of low- to moderate-toughness foliage and twigs, potentially including exogenous grit from soil-contaminated plants.
As a low-browser and grazer, Mylodon likely foraged in groups, using its powerful forelimb claws to pull down vegetation from low shrubs or excavate roots in open habitats, with estimated daily intake of approximately 2030 kg of plant matter scaled to its body mass of around 2,000 kg.

Locomotion and Habitat Use

Mylodon primarily employed a quadrupedal gait for locomotion, characterized by a plantigrade posture that supported its massive body weight and facilitated movement across varied terrains. Limb proportions, with relatively robust forelimbs and elongated hindlimbs, indicate adaptations for both weight-bearing stability and occasional bipedal stances, likely used to reach higher vegetation or during foraging activities. These morphological features suggest Mylodon could achieve moderate speeds, potentially up to 20 km/h in short bursts, though sustained travel was likely slower to conserve energy in its open habitats.
The species inhabited mosaic landscapes of open steppes and forest edges in southern South America, particularly the cold, semiarid Patagonian plains, where it exploited grassy plains and transitional zones rather than dense forests. Recent biophysical modeling of its preserved fur indicates that Mylodon possessed thick insulation, with 10-30 mm of dense hair enabling thermal tolerance in subzero winter temperatures and mild summers, without significant heat stress. This adaptation underscores its minimal arboreal behavior, favoring terrestrial navigation over climbing, consistent with its ground-dwelling niche in expansive, windswept environments.
Paleontological evidence from trackways and claw marks preserved in Pleistocene sediments points to behavioral patterns including wallowing in soft soils, inferred from the deep impressions left by its powerful claws.

Interactions with Predators and Parasites

During the Pleistocene, Mylodon darwinii coexisted with large carnivores in southern South America, including the saber-toothed cat Smilodon populator and the short-faced bear Arctotherium, which are inferred to have preyed upon ground sloths based on their ecological roles as apex predators of megafauna. Juveniles were particularly vulnerable, as adult Mylodon darwinii reached masses of up to 2,000 kg, deterring attacks on mature individuals through sheer size.
Direct evidence of predation includes tooth marks on Mylodon darwinii bones, such as puncture wounds on skulls consistent with the canine bites of jaguars (Panthera onca), indicating failed or scavenging attempts, likely targeting subadults in cave environments.
Paleoparasitological analysis of 52 coprolites from Mylodon darwinii at Cueva del Milodn, Chilean Patagonia, identified helminth eggs in 18 samples (34.62% prevalence), using spontaneous sedimentation methods. Nematodes included Calodium sp. (23.08% prevalence, mean intensity of 31 eggs per gram) and Ascaridida gen. sp. (5.77% prevalence); trematodes comprised Digenea gen. sp. (1.92% prevalence); and cestodes encompassed Hymenolepididae gen. sp. (9.62%), Anoplocephalidae gen. sp. (1.92%), and Taeniidae gen. sp. (3.85%). These parasites likely imposed physiological stress, though no direct links to mortality are established.
Defensive adaptations of Mylodon darwinii included thick integument embedded with dermal ossicles (osteoderms), small bony plates forming disorganized, row-like, or mosaic patterns across the skin, as evidenced by mummified specimens from Cueva del Milodn. These structures, observed in thin sections showing mineralized calcium phosphate and collagen, provided passive armor against predator attacks without evidence of active defensive behaviors beyond body size. Multiple Mylodon darwinii remains in cave sites suggest possible gregarious habits that may have enhanced vigilance, though direct behavioral evidence is absent.

Extinction

Timing of Extinction

The extinction of Mylodon darwinii occurred during the late Pleistocene to early Holocene transition, within a window of approximately 12,000 to 10,000 years ago, coinciding with the end of the Last Glacial Maximum. Radiocarbon dating of the latest bones from southern Patagonia indicates survival until around 10.8 ka, with robust dates from sites like Cueva del Milodn supporting this chronology.
Regional variations in the timing of extinction are evident, though data suggest a general last occurrence around 1012 ka cal BP across southern South America, from Bolivia to Patagonia. In southern Patagonia, calibrated radiocarbon dates for Mylodon at Cueva del Milodn extend to approximately 10.2 ka, while earlier dates at Bao Nuevo are around 14.5 ka cal BP.
The discovery of exceptionally preserved Mylodon remains, including skin and dung, in Cueva del Milodn in 1895 sparked 19th-century rumors among explorers and locals of living individuals surviving in remote Patagonian caves. These speculations were ultimately debunked by the absence of any post-10 ka radiocarbon evidence, confirming the species' full extinction by the early Holocene.

Causes and Evidence

The primary cause of Mylodon extinction is attributed to human overhunting following the arrival of Paleoindians in southern South America around 13,000 years ago, with a rapid population decline commencing approximately 12,500 years before present (B.P.) and nearing completion by 11,600 B.P.. A 2025 study analyzing 38 archaeological assemblages from southern South America demonstrates that extinct megafauna, including Mylodon darwinii, dominated human subsistence diets, comprising over 80% of identified specimens by number (NISP) in 13 of 20 pre-extinction sites, based on cut marks, percussion marks, and burning indicative of processing for consumption.. The study further notes cut marks and percussion evidence in 13 of 18 assemblages with extinct megafauna, aligning with optimal foraging models where Mylodon ranked as high-return prey due to its body size and resource predictability, leading to overhunting as human populations expanded.. Direct evidence includes cut marks on Mylodon bones at Fell's Cave, where the species represents a significant portion (77.9% NISP) of faunal remains associated with human tools dated to ~12,90011,200 B.P., and at Piedra Museo (~11,600 calibrated years B.P.), where Mylodon bones exhibit anthropogenic modifications alongside human artifacts, confirming targeted hunting.. These findings align with broader patterns of megafaunal dependence in early human diets across Patagonia, where behavioral associations (e.g., cut marks on 13 of 18 assemblages) underscore overhunting as the dominant driver, coinciding temporally with human arrival but independent of pre-existing population declines..
Secondary factors include climate warming during the post-Antarctic Cold Reversal phase (~13,00011,900 B.P.), which elevated temperatures by up to 4C and facilitated a vegetation shift from open cold grasslands to dense Nothofagus forests around 11,800 B.P., reducing suitable browsing and grazing habitats for Mylodon.. This habitat loss, evident in pollen records from sites like Lago Eberhard and Pantano Dumestre where forest dominance was established by ~11,400 B.P., likely exacerbated vulnerability but occurred after initial human impacts, with Mylodon persisting until ~10,100 B.P. despite the transition.. The role of disease remains debated and unproven, with no direct pathogen evidence identified in Mylodon remains or associated sediments, though hypothetical introductions via human migration have been speculated without substantiation..
Supporting evidence includes stable isotope analyses of latest Mylodon fossils showing elevated N values at sites like Piedra Museo (~11,600 calibrated years B.P.), suggesting nutritional stress or aridity-induced dietary shifts in the terminal Pleistocene, potentially from habitat fragmentation and overhunting pressure.. This temporal overlap with humans, established through radiocarbon dating of co-occurring remains, reinforces anthropogenic causation over purely climatic mechanisms..
