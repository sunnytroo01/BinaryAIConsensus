 A fiber is a long, thin strand or thread of material, typically with a length at least 100 times its diameter, that can be spun into yarn and processed into fabrics or other structures. Fibers are broadly classified as natural, derived from plants (e.g., cotton, flax), animals (e.g., wool, silk), or minerals (e.g., asbestos), or man-made, produced through chemical synthesis (e.g., polyester, nylon) or regeneration from natural polymers (e.g., rayon).
Fibers serve as the fundamental building blocks for textiles, apparel, and industrial applications such as composites, ropes, and filtration materials. Their properties, including strength, flexibility, and durability, determine suitability for various uses.

Overview

Definition and Characteristics

A fiber is defined as a long, thin strand or thread of material characterized by a high aspect ratio, typically greater than 100 (length-to-diameter ratio), which enables it to be spun, woven, or otherwise processed into yarns or fabrics. This elongated structure distinguishes fibers from other forms of matter, such as particles or films, and is fundamental to their utility in applications like textiles and composites.
The English term "fiber" (also spelled "fibre" in British English) refers in this article to material fibers as defined above. It translates to "Faser" in German for the general material sense, with "Ballaststoff" used for dietary fiber and "Glasfaser" for optical fiber. In Albanian, it translates to "fibr", commonly used in technical contexts such as "fibr optike" for optical fiber.
Key characteristics of fibers include tensile strength, which measures their resistance to breaking under tension; flexibility, allowing them to bend repeatedly without fracturing; and elongation, the extent to which they can stretch before breaking. Fibers also exhibit a typical diameter ranging from 1 to 100 micrometers, influencing their fineness and handling properties, and crystallinity, referring to the degree of ordered molecular arrangement that affects overall strength and durability.
Fibers are classified based on origin into natural (derived from plants, animals, or minerals) and man-made (produced through chemical or mechanical processes); by chemical composition into organic (carbon-based polymers) and inorganic (such as glass or metal oxides); and by structure into monofilament (a single continuous strand) or multifilament (multiple strands bundled together). These frameworks provide a basis for understanding fiber behavior and selection in various contexts.
Common fiber forms include staple fibers, which are short lengths (typically under 100 mm) suitable for spinning into yarns, and continuous filaments, which are indefinitely long and can be directly used or converted into multifilament yarns or nonwoven structures through bonding rather than weaving.

Historical Development

The use of natural fibers dates back to prehistoric times, with the earliest known evidence consisting of twisted flax fibers discovered in a cave in the Republic of Georgia, dating to approximately 34,000 years ago. These artifacts, found alongside stone tools and bone implements, indicate early human manipulation of plant materials for cordage or rudimentary textiles. In ancient China, silk production emerged around 2700 BCE, as evidenced by silk fabrics from the Neolithic site of Qianshanyang in Zhejiang Province, marking the beginning of sericulture and the domestication of silkworms for fiber extraction. Similarly, cotton cultivation in the Indus Valley Civilization began around 5000 BCE, with archaeological remains of cotton fibers and seeds unearthed at the Neolithic site of Mehrgarh in present-day Pakistan, demonstrating early agricultural practices for textile purposes.
The Industrial Revolution in the 18th century transformed fiber processing through mechanization, shifting production from artisanal to factory-based systems. In 1764, James Hargreaves invented the spinning jenny, a multi-spindle device that enabled one worker to spin multiple threads simultaneously, dramatically increasing wool and cotton yarn output and paving the way for textile mills. This was followed in 1785 by Edmund Cartwright's power loom, which automated weaving and further accelerated mass production, leading to the proliferation of mills in Britain and later globally, where steam power integrated spinning and weaving into large-scale operations.
The late 19th century saw the advent of man-made fibers, beginning with regenerated cellulose. In 1892, chemists Charles Frederick Cross and Edward John Bevan developed viscose rayon, the first commercial semi-synthetic fiber derived from wood pulp, which was patented and produced on an industrial scale by the early 1900s. Synthetic fibers followed in the 20th century: Wallace Carothers at DuPont synthesized nylon in 1935, introducing the first fully synthetic polyamide fiber for textiles and later military uses. Polyester was invented in 1941 by John Rex Whinfield and James Tennant Dickson at the Calico Printers' Association in England, offering durable, wrinkle-resistant alternatives that quickly gained traction in apparel.
Post-World War II innovations expanded high-performance fibers for specialized applications. In 1958, Roger Bacon at Union Carbide produced the first high-strength carbon fibers through pyrolysis of rayon precursors, enabling lightweight composites for aerospace. Aramids emerged in 1965 when Stephanie Kwolek at DuPont created Kevlar, a para-aramid synthetic fiber renowned for its exceptional tensile strength and heat resistance. In the 2010s, bio-engineered fibers advanced with recombinant production of spider silk proteins in transgenic silkworms and bacteria, yielding materials with superior toughness for biomedical and protective uses.
Key institutional developments included the establishment of the International Wool Textile Organisation (IWTO) in 1931, which promoted standards and research in wool processing amid growing competition from synthetics. The 1970s oil crises, by raising petroleum costs, temporarily slowed synthetic fiber expansion but ultimately reinforced their dominance through established infrastructure, as global textile consumption shifted toward affordable, versatile man-made options over natural fibers. In the 2020s, the focus has shifted towards sustainable man-made fibers, with recycled polyester comprising about 20% of production and growth in bio-based alternatives to address environmental impacts.

Natural Fibers

Vegetable Fibers

Vegetable fibers, derived from various parts of plants, represent a major category of natural fibers valued for their renewability and versatility in textiles and composites. These fibers are primarily classified based on their botanical origin within the plant structure: bast fibers from the phloem or inner bark of stems, leaf fibers from the leaves, and seed fibers from seed pods or capsules.
Bast fibers are extracted from the stems of dicotyledonous plants, where they form supportive bundles in the phloem tissue. Common examples include flax from Linum usitatissimum, hemp from Cannabis sativa, and jute from species of Corchorus. Extraction typically involves rettingsuch as water or dew rettingto separate the fibers from non-fibrous tissues, followed by decortication to remove the outer bark and break the stem. Flax, in particular, has a long historical association with linen production, dating back over 30,000 years to prehistoric dwellings and serving as a primary textile material in ancient Egypt for clothing and sails. Jute, often used in burlap and packaging, sees its production dominated by India and Bangladesh, which together account for over 90% of global output, with India producing approximately 2 million tonnes annually as of 2024.
Leaf fibers are obtained from monocotyledonous plants, specifically the fibrous bundles in leaf tissues. Notable types include sisal from Agave sisalana and abaca from Musa textilis, both part of the Agave and banana families, respectively. These are extracted through mechanical processes like crushing the leaves to release the fibers and scraping away the pulp, yielding long, strong strands suitable for ropes and twine.
Seed fibers originate from the hairy coverings or linings of seed pods. Cotton, derived from species of Gossypium, is the preeminent example, with global production reaching approximately 25.9 million tonnes in the 2024/25 season, far surpassing other vegetable fibers. Kapok, from the seed pods of Ceiba pentandra, provides a softer, fluffy fiber used in insulation and stuffing. These fibers are typically harvested by ginning, which separates the lint from seeds.
In composition, vegetable fibers are predominantly cellulosic, with cellulose content ranging from 60-75% in bast fibers to 80-90% in cotton, accompanied by hemicellulose (10-20%), lignin (2-25%), and pectin as binding agents. This structure contributes to their high moisture absorption, often up to 8-10% under standard conditions, enhancing breathability in applications like apparel. However, the hydrophilic nature also renders them susceptible to microbial degradation in humid environments without chemical treatments like scouring. Their fully biodegradable composition allows breakdown in soil within months to years, depending on conditions, making them environmentally preferable for sustainable uses. Vegetable fibers generally exhibit moderate tensile strength, with cotton around 300-500 MPa, supporting their role in everyday textiles.

Animal Fibers

Animal fibers are natural protein-based materials derived from various animals, primarily through harvesting their hair, fur, or secretions, and are valued for their warmth, durability, and biodegradability in textile applications. These fibers originate from biological structures such as keratin in mammalian hair or fibroin in insect cocoons, providing unique properties like elasticity and insulation that stem from their molecular composition and morphology. Harvesting techniques vary by source, often involving shearing, combing, or reeling to obtain raw fibers without harming the animal, though ethical considerations arise in some practices.
Wool, the most prominent animal fiber, is harvested from sheep through annual shearing of their fleece, which consists primarily of keratin proteins forming over 90% of the fiber's composition. This keratin-based structure includes a crimped morphology that enhances interlocking and insulation by trapping air within the fiber assembly. Global production of wool reaches approximately 2 million tonnes annually in greasy form as of 2023, with major contributors including Australia and China. The crimp, typically featuring 4-5 waves per centimeter in fine wools, contributes to the fiber's resilience and ability to retain shape after processing.
Silk is produced by the larvae of the silkworm moth (Bombyx mori), where it forms cocoons composed mainly of fibroin protein, accounting for 70-80% of the raw material, coated by sericin gum. Harvesting involves boiling cocoons to kill the pupae and reel the continuous filaments, each up to 1,500 meters long, enabling the creation of smooth, strong yarns without spinning. China maintained a monopoly on silk production for nearly 3,000 years until the 6th century CE, when sericulture spread to the Byzantine Empire via trade routes. The triangular cross-section of silk filaments promotes luster by reflecting light at multiple angles, giving the fiber its characteristic sheen.
Other notable animal fibers include cashmere and mohair, both sourced from goats via combing or shearing during seasonal shedding. Cashmere, the fine undercoat of cashmere goats, offers exceptional softness and warmth, while mohair from Angora goats provides luster and strength due to its longer, straighter guard hairs. Leather fibers, derived from animal hides such as those of cattle, are based on collagen proteins that form intertwined bundles, processed through tanning to create durable, non-woven sheets used in textiles and beyond.
Angora wool from rabbits is harvested by plucking or shearing the undercoat, but this practice raises ethical concerns due to reports of painful live-plucking methods that cause skin irritation and stress to the animals. Unique traits of animal fibers include wool's elasticity, allowing up to 50% elongation before breaking, and its excellent thermal insulation from the crimped structure that minimizes heat loss. However, wool is prone to felting from scale overlap during agitation and damage from alkalis, which degrade the keratin proteins. Silk, in contrast, offers high tensile strength but lower elasticity compared to wool.

Mineral Fibers

Mineral fibers refer to naturally occurring inorganic fibers derived from geological sources, primarily silicate minerals formed through metamorphic and hydrothermal processes in rocks such as serpentinites, limestones, and ultramafic formations. These fibers are distinguished by their crystalline structure, high thermal stability, and incombustibility, making them suitable for applications requiring heat resistance, though many pose health risks due to their fine, inhalable nature. Unlike organic natural fibers from plants or animals, mineral fibers lack biological origins and exhibit rigid, brittle characteristics with diameters often below 1 micrometer, contributing to their unique mechanical reinforcement potential in composites.
Asbestos represents the most prominent group of mineral fibers, consisting of six regulated types: chrysotile from the serpentine group and five amphibole varieties (actinolite-tremolite, amosite, anthophyllite, crocidolite, and tremolite-actinolite). These fibers form in metamorphic rocks through hydrothermal alteration of ultramafic or mafic parent rocks, where magnesium- and iron-rich minerals react with silica-rich fluids under elevated temperatures and pressures, typically producing veins or masses with fiber diameters ranging from 0.025 to less than 1 micrometer and lengths up to hundreds of micrometers. Chrysotile, the most abundant form (over 90% of historical production), has the composition MgSiO(OH), featuring a curled sheet structure that imparts flexibility, while amphiboles like crocidolite (NaFeFeSiO(OH)) exhibit straight, needle-like chains for greater rigidity. These silicate minerals demonstrate exceptional heat resistance, with chrysotile stable up to approximately 800C before dehydroxylation and melting around 850C, and amphiboles enduring similar or higher temperatures due to their anhydrous nature.
Historically, asbestos mining peaked in the 1970s, driven by demand for insulation and construction materials, with Canada (primarily Quebec's chrysotile deposits) and Russia (Ural Mountains' amphibole and chrysotile sources) accounting for over 50% of global output, reaching about 5 million tons annually worldwide by 1975. However, widespread recognition of health hazardsparticularly the link to mesothelioma, a rare cancer of the lung liningled to regulatory actions; the European Union implemented a comprehensive ban on asbestos mining and use in 1999, while the United States enacted a partial ban in 1989 targeting specific products like pipe insulation, though it was largely overturned in 1991, resulting in ongoing restrictions. In March 2024, the U.S. EPA finalized a ban on ongoing uses of chrysotile asbestos, but in July 2025, this was delayed by the Fifth Circuit Court for agency reconsideration, maintaining partial prohibitions as of November 2025. In the US, asbestos exposure contributed to approximately 2,500 mesothelioma deaths annually from 1999 to 2020, underscoring the fibers' carcinogenicity from inhalation of durable, biopersistent particles that trigger inflammation and genetic damage. Despite these traits, asbestos fibers are inherently brittle, prone to fracturing, and non-combustible, offering incombustibility up to their thermal limits but at the cost of severe respiratory risks.
Wollastonite, another key mineral fiber, is a calcium inosilicate (CaSiO) formed via contact metamorphism of siliceous limestones or dolomites at temperatures between 400C and 800C, where calcite reacts with quartz or silica under the influence of igneous intrusions, yielding acicular crystals that can be processed into fibers with aspect ratios up to 20:1. Composed primarily of 48% CaO and 51% SiO, with minor iron and magnesium substitutions, wollastonite exhibits low thermal expansion, high whiteness, and stability up to 1,120C, making it thermally superior to many asbestos variants for insulation without the associated health hazards. Its needle-like morphology provides reinforcement similar to asbestos but with reduced brittleness and no documented carcinogenicity, positioning it as a safer geological alternative in ceramics and polymers.

Man-Made Fibers

Regenerated Fibers

Regenerated fibers are semi-synthetic materials produced by chemically processing natural polymers, primarily cellulose or proteins, to form new filaments with tailored properties. These fibers bridge the gap between natural and fully synthetic options, offering enhanced versatility while retaining some inherent biocompatibility from their biological origins. Unlike unmodified natural fibers such as cotton, regenerated variants undergo dissolution and reformation, resulting in smoother textures and improved processability.
Cellulose-based regenerated fibers dominate this category, with viscose rayon and lyocell as primary examples derived from wood pulp. Viscose rayon, the earliest commercial regenerated fiber, was invented in 1891 and entered production in 1905 through the xanthation process, where purified cellulose is treated with sodium hydroxide to form alkali cellulose, then reacted with carbon disulfide (CS2) to create cellulose xanthate, which is dissolved in a dilute sodium hydroxide solution to form viscose dope. This viscous solution is extruded through spinnerets into an acid bath, precipitating and regenerating the cellulose into continuous filaments. Lyocell, developed in the 1990s, employs a more environmentally friendly closed-loop process using N-methylmorpholine N-oxide (NMMO) as a non-toxic solvent to directly dissolve cellulose pulp, followed by dry-jet wet spinning and solvent recovery exceeding 99% efficiency, minimizing waste and chemical emissions. Lenzing AG leads in lyocell innovation under the Tencel brand, driving a sustainability shift from traditional viscose due to reduced environmental impact.
These fibers consist of over 95% regenerated cellulose, exhibiting a composition chemically identical to natural cellulose but with a smoother, more uniform surface that enhances drape and sheen compared to cotton. Variants like modal, a high-tenacity form of viscose, are produced by modifying the viscose process with extended steeping and higher cellulose purity, yielding fibers with superior wet strength retentionup to 85% of dry strengthmaking them suitable for durable textiles. Protein-based regenerated fibers, such as casein derived from milk proteins and azlon from soy or corn zein, were developed in the early 20th century but are now rare due to production challenges and competition from synthetics; casein fibers, prominent in the 1930s1940s, involved dissolving skim milk casein in alkali and extruding into an acidic coagulating bath, offering wool-like softness before declining post-World War II.
Global production of rayon, primarily viscose, was approximately 7.9 million metric tons in 2023, underscoring its scale in the textile industry. Regenerated cellulose fibers boast high absorbency with a moisture regain of about 1113%, excellent dye affinity for vibrant colors, and breathability, though they suffer from reduced wet strengthlosing up to 50% compared to dry conditionswhich necessitates careful handling during processing. These traits position them as eco-conscious alternatives in apparel and hygiene products, emphasizing sustainability through renewable sourcing and recyclability.

Synthetic Fibers

Synthetic fibers are fully artificial organic polymers produced through chemical synthesis from petroleum-derived monomers, distinguishing them from regenerated fibers that modify natural polymers. These fibers are engineered for specific performance characteristics, enabling widespread use in textiles and industrial applications. The global production of synthetic fibers reached approximately 84 million tonnes in 2023, with polyesters dominating the market at around 55% share.
The primary types of synthetic fibers include polyesters, polyamides, acrylics, and olefins. Polyesters, particularly polyethylene terephthalate (PET), are synthesized via polycondensation of terephthalic acid and ethylene glycol, forming ester linkages in long polymer chains. PET accounts for the majority of polyester production, which exceeded 70 million tons globally by the early 2020s, reflecting its post-1950s rise driven by advancements in polymerization techniques. Polyamides, such as nylon 6/6, are produced by the condensation polymerization of hexamethylenediamine and adipic acid, resulting in amide linkages that provide strength and elasticity; nylon played a pivotal role in World War II, replacing silk in parachutes during the 1940s due to its durability and availability. Acrylic fibers are based on polyacrylonitrile, often used in modacrylic blends containing 35-85% acrylonitrile copolymerized with other monomers like vinyl chloride for enhanced flame resistance. Olefin fibers, including polypropylene and polyethylene, are formed through addition polymerization of propylene or ethylene monomers, yielding non-polar, hydrophobic chains suitable for moisture-resistant applications.
Synthesis of these fibers typically involves polymerization to create high-molecular-weight thermoplastics (10,000-100,000 g/mol), followed by extrusion into fibers. For nylon, condensation polymerization eliminates water to form amide bonds, while polypropylene relies on Ziegler-Natta catalyzed addition polymerization to add monomers across double bonds without byproducts. The resulting polymers exhibit semi-crystalline structures with 40-60% crystallinity, where ordered regions contribute to mechanical strength and amorphous areas allow flexibility. Melt-spinning is the dominant processing method, in which polymer pellets are heated to a viscous melt (typically 250-300C), extruded through spinnerets to form filaments, and cooled to solidify, enabling efficient production of continuous fibers for most thermoplastics like PET and polypropylene.
Specialized synthetic fibers like spandex (also known as elastane) are polyurethane-based, synthesized by reacting diisocyanates with polyols to form segmented block copolymers with soft and hard segments, enabling exceptional elasticity of up to 500% stretch and recovery. Synthetic fibers offer unique traits such as high durability and wrinkle resistance due to their strong intermolecular forces and low friction, making them ideal for crease-resistant garments. However, they exhibit low moisture absorptionpolyester regains only 0.4% moisture under standard conditionsleading to reduced breathability compared to natural fibers. Additionally, concerns over microplastic shedding have grown, as washing synthetic textiles releases microfibers into waterways, contributing to environmental pollution estimated at billions of particles annually; emerging regulations, such as the EU's 2025 restrictions on intentional microplastic releases, aim to mitigate this issue. While carbon-based variants exist for high-performance uses, they fall outside the organic polymer category detailed here.

Inorganic Specialty Fibers

Inorganic specialty fibers encompass a range of engineered non-organic materials designed for demanding applications requiring exceptional strength, thermal stability, and durability. These include carbon fibers, glass fibers such as E-glass, ceramic fibers like silicon carbide, metallic fibers, and basalt fibers as a mineral-derived variant. Unlike organic synthetics, these fibers derive their properties from inorganic compositions, enabling use in environments where organic materials would degrade.
Carbon fibers, the most prominent type, are produced primarily from polyacrylonitrile (PAN) or pitch precursors through a multi-stage pyrolysis process. The process begins with oxidation (stabilization) at 200-300C to cross-link the precursor and prevent melting, followed by carbonization at 1000-1500C in an inert atmosphere to remove non-carbon elements, and graphitization at 2000-3000C for high-modulus variants to align graphite crystallites. The resulting fibers consist of 93-99% carbon, exhibiting tensile strengths of 3-7 GPa and Young's moduli of 200-600 GPa, which provide extreme stiffness suitable for aerospace and automotive composites. PAN-based fibers dominate production, accounting for over 90% of output due to their balance of strength and processability. The global carbon fiber market, valued at $5.75 billion in 2024, is projected to reach $10.68 billion by 2030, driven by demand in lightweight structures; Toray Industries holds a leading position with significant market share in advanced materials. However, their high production costs and inherent brittleness limit broader adoption, though they offer superior corrosion resistance in harsh conditions.
Fiberglass, particularly E-glass, is manufactured by melting a silica-alumina-borate composition (approximately 52-56% SiO, 12-16% AlO, and 16-25% CaO) at around 1400C and drawing it into continuous filaments through platinum-rhodium bushings. This amorphous structure yields strong, insulating fibers widely used in reinforcements, with production pioneered by Owens Corning in the 1930s as a safer alternative to asbestos for electrical and thermal insulation applications. Silicon carbide (SiC) fibers, a key ceramic type, are derived from polycarbosilane precursors via spinning, curing, and pyrolysis at 1000-1300C, resulting in beta-SiC crystallites that withstand temperatures up to 1600C, making them ideal for aerospace turbine components. Metallic fibers, such as stainless steel (e.g., 316L grade), are produced by bundle drawing or melt spinning and integrated into textiles for electromagnetic shielding and conductivity, offering durability in corrosive environments. Basalt fibers, a hybrid from natural volcanic rock, involve melting basalt (46-52% SiO, 15-17% AlO) at 1450C and extruding through dies, providing a cost-effective option with good tensile strength and alkali resistance, though still exhibiting the brittleness common to inorganic fibers. Overall, these fibers excel in corrosion resistance and high-temperature performance but face challenges from elevated costs and fragility under impact.

Fiber Properties

Mechanical and Physical Properties

Mechanical properties of fibers primarily encompass their response to tensile loads, including tenacity (specific strength), elongation at break, and initial modulus (stiffness). Tenacity, measured in grams per denier (g/denier) or centinewtons per tex (cN/tex), quantifies the force required to break a fiber per unit linear density, with typical values ranging from 2-5 g/denier for cotton to 8-10 g/denier for nylon. Elongation at break, expressed as a percentage, indicates extensibility and varies widely from 5-10% for cotton to 20-50% for elastomeric fibers like spandex, reflecting their ability to deform before failure. The initial modulus, also in g/denier, measures resistance to initial deformation and spans 50-200 g/denier across fiber types, with higher values denoting greater stiffness essential for load-bearing applications.
Physical attributes further define fiber behavior under mechanical stress. Fiber diameter typically ranges from 10-50 micrometers, influencing flexibility and packing density, while cross-sectional shapes vary: synthetic fibers often exhibit uniform round profiles for smooth processing, whereas natural fibers like wool display irregular kidney-bean shapes that enhance interlocking in yarns. Density, a key indicator of mass efficiency, averages 1.3-1.5 g/cm for most organic fibers, with cotton at 1.54 g/cm and nylon at 1.14 g/cm, affecting overall composite weight. Surface friction, quantified by the coefficient of friction (typically 0.2-0.4 between fibers), governs inter-fiber interactions during spinning and weaving, with lower values in smooth synthetics reducing processing energy compared to scaly natural fibers like wool.
Standardized testing, such as ASTM D3822 for single-fiber tensile properties, evaluates these traits by mounting fibers in grips and applying controlled extension until breakage. This method derives stress (=F/A\sigma = F / A=F/A), where FFF is force and AAA is cross-sectional area, and strain (=L/L0\epsilon = \Delta L / L_0=L/L0), where L\Delta LL is elongation and L0L_0L0 is initial length, enabling computation of tenacity, elongation, and modulus from load-elongation curves.
Comparisons across fiber types highlight trade-offs: natural fibers like silk offer balanced tenacity around 4.5 g/denier with moderate elongation (20-25%), while synthetics such as polyester provide higher tenacity (5-7 g/denier) but lower elongation (10-20%), suiting durable applications. Synthetic fibers generally exhibit superior fatigue resistance under cyclic loading due to uniform structure, outperforming naturals like cotton which degrade faster from microcracks. Specialty fibers like Kevlar achieve record tenacity of 28 g/denier, driven by highly aligned polymer chains.
Processing factors, such as drawing ratio during manufacturing, enhance these properties by promoting molecular alignment, often increasing modulus by 2-3 times in synthetic fibers.

Chemical and Thermal Properties

Fibers exhibit distinct chemical properties that influence their reactivity to environmental factors such as pH, moisture, and dyes. Natural fibers like wool demonstrate sensitivity to alkaline conditions, with degradation occurring above pH 9 due to the hydrolysis of peptide bonds in keratin, leading to fiber dissolution or weakening. In contrast, cotton, a cellulosic fiber, is more vulnerable to acidic environments below pH 2, where strong acids cause hydrolysis of glycosidic bonds, resulting in reduced fiber integrity. Synthetic fibers such as polyester and nylon show greater chemical stability across a broader pH range, though prolonged exposure to extremes can still induce minor degradation.
Moisture regain, the equilibrium moisture content under standard atmospheric conditions (65% relative humidity at 21C), varies significantly between fiber types and affects comfort and dimensional stability. Natural fibers typically exhibit higher moisture regain values of 8-12%, with cotton at approximately 8.5% and wool at 16%, enabling better absorbency but also hygroscopic swelling. Synthetic fibers, however, have low moisture regain, often less than 1%, such as 0.4% for polyester and 4% for nylon, which contributes to their quick-drying properties but lower breathability.
Dye affinity is governed by the chemical structure and surface charge of fibers, determining the type of dyes required for effective coloration. Wool, with its proteinaceous amino groups, has high affinity for ionic acid dyes that form salt linkages at acidic pH. Cotton, bearing hydroxyl groups, bonds well with direct or reactive dyes via hydrogen bonding or covalent reactions, respectively. Polyester requires disperse dyes that dissolve in the fiber matrix through hydrophobic interactions, while nylon accepts acid or disperse dyes due to its amide groups.
Thermal properties of fibers dictate their behavior under heat, including melting, decomposition, and heat transfer characteristics. Many synthetic fibers like nylon melt at around 220C before decomposing, allowing for thermoplastic processing but risking shrinkage in high-heat environments. Cotton undergoes charring and decomposition starting at approximately 300C, without a distinct melting point, as its cellulosic structure pyrolyzes into volatile gases and carbonaceous residue. Polyester melts at about 260C, followed by thermal degradation, whereas aramid fibers like Nomex decompose above 400C without melting, making them suitable for high-temperature applications such as firefighting gear.
Thermal conductivity measures a fiber's ability to conduct heat, with lower values indicating better insulation. Wool exhibits low thermal conductivity of 0.04-0.06 W/mK, trapping air within its crimped structure for warmth, while cotton ranges from 0.05-0.07 W/mK, providing moderate insulation. Polyester has slightly higher conductivity around 0.10 W/mK, facilitating faster heat dissipation but less thermal retention.
The limiting oxygen index (LOI), the minimum oxygen concentration required for sustained combustion, quantifies flame resistance. Cotton has a low LOI of 18-20%, igniting readily in air (21% oxygen), whereas aramids like Nomex achieve an LOI of 28-30%, self-extinguishing in normal atmospheres. Polyester and nylon typically have LOI values around 20-22%, similar to cotton, necessitating treatments for enhanced retardancy.
Degradation mechanisms under chemical and thermal stress further define fiber durability. Polyesters undergo hydrolysis, where water molecules cleave ester linkages, accelerated by heat or bases, leading to chain shortening and loss of tensile strength. Acrylic fibers experience UV-induced chain scission, where ultraviolet radiation breaks carbon-carbon bonds, causing yellowing, embrittlement, and reduced molecular weight. To mitigate flammability, especially in cotton, phosphorus-based additives promote char formation by dehydrating cellulose during pyrolysis, enhancing LOI without releasing toxic gases. Post-2010 regulations have driven the adoption of halogen-free retardants, such as phosphorus-nitrogen compounds, which decompose to form protective intumescent layers on fibers like cotton and polyester.
Fibers also respond to temperature changes via thermal expansion, quantified by the linear coefficient = (1/L)(dL/dT), where L is length and T is temperature. For textile fibers, typically ranges from 5-10 10^{-6} /C, with synthetics like nylon showing values around 8 10^{-6} /C, influencing dimensional stability in varying thermal conditions.

Production and Processing

Extraction and Preparation of Natural Fibers

Natural fibers are extracted and prepared through processes tailored to their biological origins, ensuring the separation of usable fibers from surrounding materials while preserving fiber integrity for subsequent textile processing. Vegetable fibers, derived from plant stems, leaves, or seeds, typically undergo mechanical or biological separation methods to remove non-fibrous components like pectin and lignin.
For bast fibers such as flax, extraction begins with harvesting mature stems, followed by retting to degrade the gummy substances binding the fibers to the plant core. Dew retting involves laying the stems in fields for natural microbial action under moist conditions, typically lasting 7-14 days depending on weather, while enzymatic retting uses controlled pectinase enzymes in a shorter, more uniform process of similar duration to achieve cleaner separation. In contrast, leaf fibers like sisal are extracted via mechanical decortication, where mature leaves are fed into a raspador or similar machine that crushes and scrapes the leaf to strip away fleshy pulp, yielding long, strong fibers with minimal chemical intervention. Seed fibers, exemplified by cotton, are processed through ginning, a mechanical separation invented by Eli Whitney in 1793 that uses saws or cylinders to detach seeds and foreign matter from the lint, dramatically increasing processing efficiency from hand methods.
Animal fibers are harvested directly from living sources, emphasizing gentle handling to avoid damage. Wool is obtained by shearing sheep, typically once annually in temperate regions to collect the fleece when it reaches 10-15 cm length, though some breeds in warmer climates may be shorn up to twice yearly for welfare and growth optimization. Silk production, known as sericulture, involves rearing silkworms to the pupal stage, harvesting cocoons, and boiling them in water to soften the sericin gum, followed by reeling, where the single filament from each cocoon is unwound and combined with those from multiple other cocoons (typically 4-8) and twisted into continuous threads on automated machines.
Preparation of mineral fibers, though less common today, historically involved physical processing of raw rock. Asbestos fibers were extracted by open-pit or underground mining of serpentine deposits, followed by crushing and milling to liberate and grade the fine, needle-like chrysotile or amphibole fibers; production has been banned or significantly reduced in many countries due to health risks, with mining ceasing in the U.S. by 2002; however, it continues globally, with consumption around 1.2 million metric tons as of 2024, mainly in Russia, China, and Kazakhstan. Basalt fibers, a modern eco-alternative, are prepared by crushing and melting natural basalt rock at approximately 1450C, then extruding the molten material through platinum-rhodium bushings to form continuous filaments that solidify into fibers.
Following extraction, natural fibers undergo cleaning and grading to remove impurities and standardize quality. This includes scouring for animal fibers, where wool is washed in hot soapy water or solvents to eliminate lanolin (wool grease comprising 5-25% of greasy weight), dirt, and suint, resulting in a 30-50% overall weight loss but primarily targeting the lanolin content (typically 10-25% of greasy weight) for yield optimization. Vegetable matter and defects, such as leaf fragments in cotton, are minimized to below 2% through pneumatic cleaning and screening, ensuring high-grade lint. Fibers are then formed into bales of 200-250 kg, with moisture content controlled at 6-8% for cotton (ideally below 8% to prevent degradation during storage) and generally 8-12% across natural types to maintain flexibility without fostering microbial growth. Grading assesses length, strength, color, and purity using standards like USDA classes for cotton.
Global cotton ginning capacity supports annual processing of over 25 million metric tons of lint as of the 2024/25 season, with infrastructure in major producers like China and India exceeding production needs by a wide margin to handle peak harvests. Sustainable practices, such as those under the Global Organic Textile Standard (GOTS) introduced in 2006 following development in the early 2000s, certify organic wool by enforcing chemical-free farming, ethical shearing, and eco-friendly scouring, promoting traceability from farm to fiber.

Polymerization and Spinning of Man-Made Fibers

Man-made fibers, including both regenerated and synthetic types, begin with the polymerization of monomers into high-molecular-weight polymers, followed by spinning processes to form continuous filaments. Polymerization techniques vary depending on the fiber type. For synthetic polyesters, such as polyethylene terephthalate (PET), step-growth polymerization is employed, involving the condensation reaction of diols and dicarboxylic acids. This process operates under equilibrium control, where the removal of byproducts like water is essential to drive the reaction forward and achieve high molecular weights. The average degree of polymerization $ n $ is described by the formula $ n = \frac{1}{1-p} $, where $ p $ is the extent of reaction; near-complete conversion ($ p \approx 1 $) is required for practical fiber-forming polymers.
In contrast, acrylic fibers, such as polyacrylonitrile, are synthesized via chain-growth polymerization initiated by free radicals. This method involves the addition of monomers to a growing chain, typically using initiators like peroxides in solution or suspension polymerization, allowing rapid propagation to form long chains suitable for fiber extrusion. Regenerated fibers, like viscose rayon, derive from natural polymers such as cellulose, which are chemically modified (e.g., via xanthation) before spinning, but the core polymer chain remains derived from renewable sources.
Once the polymer is prepared, spinning converts it into filaments through extrusion under controlled conditions. Melt spinning is the most common method for thermoplastics like nylon and polyester, where the polymer is heated to 250300C, extruded through a spinneret to form molten filaments, and cooled in air to solidify. This technique enables high-speed production for fibers used in textiles and industrial applications. Wet spinning is used for regenerated fibers like viscose, where the polymer solution (dope) is extruded into an acid coagulant bath, causing precipitation and filament formation through chemical regeneration. Dry spinning applies to cellulose acetate, extruding the dope into hot air, where solvent evaporation solidifies the filaments without a liquid bath. For advanced applications, electrospinning produces nanofibers by applying high voltages of 1030 kV to a polymer solution, drawing charged jets that solidify into submicron-diameter fibers with high surface area.
Post-spinning operations enhance fiber properties by aligning molecular chains and imparting desired textures. Drawing involves extending the as-spun filaments 25 times their original length, often between heated godets, to orient polymer molecules and improve strength; tenacity increases linearly with draw ratio up to approximately 8x, beyond which defects may form. Texturing, such as false-twist texturing, introduces crimp and bulk by twisting, heating, and untwisting filaments at high speeds, mimicking natural fiber aesthetics for apparel. Finishing treatments, including antistatic coatings, are applied via emulsions to reduce static buildup and improve processability during weaving or knitting.
Historical advancements underscore the scale of these processes. DuPont's pioneering nylon production, launched in 1939 at its Seaford plant with an initial capacity of 4 million pounds (about 1,800 metric tons) per year, rapidly scaled in the 1940s to meet wartime demands, reaching several thousand tons annually by mid-decade through expanded facilities. In the 1990s, gel-spinning emerged as a specialized technique for ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene (UHMWPE) fibers like Dyneema, where the polymer is dissolved in a gel-like state, extruded, and drawn to achieve exceptional strength-to-weight ratios for ballistic and marine applications. These innovations highlight the evolution from laboratory synthesis to industrial-scale manufacturing.

Applications

Textile and Apparel Uses

Fibers play a central role in clothing production, where blends of natural and synthetic varieties optimize comfort, durability, and aesthetics. Cotton-polyester blends, particularly in a 60/40 ratio (cotton to polyester), are widely used for shirts and casual apparel, combining cotton's breathability and softness with polyester's wrinkle resistance and longevity. Wool remains a staple for suits and formal wear due to its natural insulation properties, which trap air within the fiber structure to regulate body temperature and provide warmth without bulk. For luxury garments, 100% mulberry silk is favored in items like ties, offering exceptional luster, smoothness, and hypoallergenic qualities derived from the uniform filaments produced by Bombyx mori silkworms fed on mulberry leaves.
In home textiles, synthetic fibers enhance functionality and longevity. Nylon and polypropylene are commonly blended or used individually in carpets for their inherent stain resistance; nylon provides resilience against wear while polypropylene repels liquids due to its hydrophobic nature, making these fibers ideal for high-traffic areas. Acrylic fibers dominate upholstery applications, prized for their UV fade resistance achieved through solution-dyeing, which embeds color throughout the fiber to prevent degradation from sunlight exposure.
Performance-oriented apparel leverages advanced fiber properties for specialized needs. Moisture-wicking synthetics, such as Coolmax polyester, feature channeled fiber structures that draw sweat away from the skin and promote rapid evaporation, commonly incorporated into sportswear for enhanced comfort during physical activity. Flame-retardant blends like modacrylic and cotton are standard in protective uniforms for firefighters and industrial workers, where modacrylic's inherent char-forming behavior limits flame spread while cotton adds wearability.
The apparel industry heavily relies on polyester, which accounted for approximately 57% of global fiber production in 2023 and 59% in 2024, with a significant portion directed toward fast fashion due to its versatility and cost-effectiveness. Sustainable alternatives are gaining traction; organic cotton, representing about 2.3% of the total cotton market in 2022/23, experienced a decline in production in recent years following earlier growth, driven by consumer demand for eco-friendly textiles.
Blending techniques further tailor fibers for apparel functionality, exemplified by core-spun yarns in stretch jeans, where an elastane core is wrapped by cotton or denim fibers to deliver four-way stretch, recovery, and reduced bagging while maintaining a natural appearance.

Industrial and Composite Applications

In industrial applications, fibers such as carbon, glass, and aramid are extensively used as reinforcements in composite materials, where they are embedded in polymer matrices like epoxy to enhance structural integrity and reduce weight. For instance, in aerospace, the Boeing 787 Dreamliner incorporates composites comprising 50% of its structure by weight, primarily using carbon fiber reinforced polymers for the fuselage and wings, which contributes to a 20% improvement in fuel efficiency compared to previous aluminum-dominated designs. Aramid fibers, notably Kevlar, are valued for their high tensile strengthapproximately five times that of steel on an equal weight basisand are integrated into composites for applications like radial tire reinforcement and bulletproof vests, where they provide superior impact resistance and puncture protection.
Filtration systems leverage synthetic fibers for their durability and fine pore structures. Polypropylene nonwoven fabrics, produced via melt-blowing, form the core of high-efficiency particulate air (HEPA) filters, achieving filtration efficiencies exceeding 99.97% for particles as small as 0.3 microns by capturing dust, pollen, and microbes through electrostatic and mechanical mechanisms. In geotextiles, inorganic fibers like basalt are employed for road reinforcement; self-adhesive basalt fiber geotextiles improve asphalt pavement durability by enhancing interlayer adhesion and reducing cracking under traffic loads, extending service life in highway overlays.
Renewable energy applications highlight the role of carbon fiber composites in wind turbine blades, which can exceed 60 meters in length to maximize energy capture; these blades enable turbines contributing to global wind capacity additions of over 100 GW annually in the 2020s, with carbon reinforcements reducing weight while maintaining stiffness against aerodynamic stresses. In the automotive sector, carbon fiber components are increasingly adopted for weight reduction; targeted use in structural parts can significantly reduce vehicle mass, improving range and handling in electric vehicles, with examples in models from manufacturers like BMW and potential applications in Tesla vehicles.
Processing these fiber-reinforced composites typically involves preparing dry fiber preforms through techniques such as weaving or layup, followed by resin infusion under vacuum to ensure uniform matrix distribution and minimize voids, resulting in high-performance parts for load-bearing applications. Beyond structural roles, metallic fibers blended into textiles provide electromagnetic interference (EMI) shielding; stainless steel or copper fibers woven into fabrics achieve shielding effectiveness of 40-60 dB in the GHz range, protecting sensitive electronics in industrial enclosures.

Environmental and Economic Aspects

Sustainability and Recycling

Fiber production has significant ecological impacts, particularly in terms of resource consumption and emissions. Natural fibers like cotton require substantial water, with approximately 10,000 liters used per kilogram during cultivation, compared to about 125 liters per kilogram for synthetic polyester, which relies more on petroleum-derived processes. Nylon production contributes around 9 kilograms of CO2 emissions per kilogram, exacerbating greenhouse gas outputs in the textile sector. Additionally, synthetic fibers release microplastics during manufacturing, washing, and disposal, with an estimated 0.5 million tons entering oceans annually before 2025, posing long-term threats to marine ecosystems.
Biodegradability varies markedly between natural and synthetic fibers, influencing their end-of-life environmental footprint. Cotton, a natural cellulose-based fiber, typically decomposes in soil within 1 to 5 months under suitable conditions, breaking down into harmless components via microbial action. In contrast, synthetic fibers like polyester can persist for centuries in landfills due to their petroleum origins, though polyethylene terephthalate (PET) can be recycled through glycolysis, which depolymerizes it back to monomers such as terephthalic acid and ethylene glycol for reuse. According to the Ellen MacArthur Foundation's 2017 report, global textile waste reached 92 million tons per year, underscoring the urgency of addressing these durability challenges in a circular economy.
Recycling methods play a crucial role in mitigating these impacts, though each has limitations. Mechanical recycling of cotton involves shredding and re-spinning waste fibers, but results in about 50% loss of tensile strength due to fiber shortening and damage. Chemical recycling for PET achieves higher efficiency, depolymerizing it to dimethyl terephthalate (DMT) and ethylene glycol (EG) with up to 90% monomer recovery, enabling production of high-quality recycled fiber. Emerging bio-based options like polylactic acid (PLA), derived from corn starch, offer compostability in industrial facilities, decomposing within months without residue. In 2025, the EU's revised Waste Framework Directive mandates extended producer responsibility for textiles, requiring producers to finance collection and recycling, and introduces ecodesign standards to enhance durability and recyclability.
Innovations in sustainable alternatives further support circularity, such as mycelium-based leather developed by 2010s startups like Bolt Threads, which grows fungal networks on agricultural waste to create biodegradable, animal-free materials mimicking traditional leather properties. These approaches aim to lessen reliance on resource-intensive fibers while advancing biodegradation and recycling integration across the industry.

Market Trends and Global Production

The global fiber production reached a record 124 million tonnes in 2023, with synthetic fibers accounting for approximately 75% of the total output. China dominates synthetic fiber manufacturing, holding nearly 70% of global production for synthetics and cellulosic fibers combined. Among natural fibers, cotton leads with an estimated 25 million tonnes produced in 2022-2023, primarily from top producers China, India, and the United States.
The fiber industry is valued at around USD 48.7 billion in 2024, with projections estimating growth to USD 63.4 billion by 2030 at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 4%. Key growth drivers include the expanding technical textiles sector, forecasted to rise from USD 247 billion in 2025 to USD 325 billion by 2030 at a CAGR of approximately 5.6%. In contrast, wool production has declined to about 1.2 million tonnes globally in recent years, reflecting reduced demand and competition from synthetics.
Trade dynamics in the fiber market are heavily influenced by oil prices, as synthetic fibers like polyester and nylon derive from petroleum-based feedstocks, leading to price volatility tied to OPEC decisions. Fair trade certifications for organic fibers command a market premium of around 5-10%, incentivizing sustainable sourcing amid rising consumer demand. Asia accounts for over 70% of global fiber manufacturing capacity, underscoring its central role in supply chains.
Emerging trends include the projected rise of recycled fibers to 10% of the market by 2030, driven by circular economy initiatives and regulatory pressures. Supply chain disruptions, such as the 2021 cotton shortages exacerbated by COVID-19 logistics issues, highlighted vulnerabilities in global trade. Post-COVID recovery has boosted output, with global fiber production increasing by about 15% from 2020 lows to 2023 levels.
Major players shape the market, including Invista (a DuPont spin-off), which leads in nylon 6,6 production and has invested over USD 500 million in expansions through 2025. BASF maintains leadership in polyester and polyamide chemicals, supporting overcapacity adjustments in Asia amid post-pandemic demand shifts.
