 William Graham Sumner (October 30, 1840 April 12, 1910) was an American sociologist and economist who held the first professorship in sociology at Yale University, teaching political and social science there from 1872 until his death. A staunch defender of classical liberalism, Sumner championed laissez-faire economics, arguing that free markets and individual liberty, rather than state intervention, drive social progress and adaptation to natural conditions.
Sumner's intellectual contributions emphasized empirical observation of social customs and evolutionary processes, rejecting reformist efforts to engineer society against underlying competitive dynamics. His 1883 essay "The Forgotten Man" critiqued policies benefiting organized interests at the expense of unrepresented taxpayers, highlighting how such measures distort incentives and burden the productive. Often associated with Social Darwinism for applying notions of natural selection to human societiesviewing wealth disparities as outcomes of superior adaptation rather than injusticeSumner maintained that liberty under law allows the capable to thrive without coercive redistribution.
In his landmark 1906 book Folkways, Sumner analyzed how customary practices, or "mores," emerge spontaneously to regulate behavior and sustain group survival, exerting binding force independent of rational deliberation or legislation. This work laid foundational insights for cultural sociology, underscoring that attempts to override entrenched mores through policy often fail due to their deep-rooted, adaptive origins. Sumner's opposition to protectionism, imperialism, and socialism stemmed from a consistent application of these principles, prioritizing unrestricted exchange and skepticism toward elite-driven reforms that ignore causal realities of human action. His ideas, though polarizing amid rising progressive sentiments, influenced generations of thinkers advocating limited government and spontaneous order.

Biography

Early life and education

William Graham Sumner was born on October 30, 1840, in Paterson, New Jersey, to Thomas Sumner, a machinist born on May 6, 1808, in Walton-le-Dale, Lancashire, England, who had immigrated to the United States, and Sarah Graham Sumner, both of working-class English immigrant stock. His father's self-educated background and mechanical trade shaped a household emphasizing frugality and industry, with Sumner's grandmother contributing savings to fund his later education. The family relocated to Hartford, Connecticut, during Sumner's early childhood, where he attended public high school. 
Sumner entered Yale College in 1859, supported by familial thrift and his own preparatory efforts, and graduated with a Bachelor of Arts degree in 1863 amid the disruptions of the Civil War era. At Yale, he pursued a classical curriculum typical of the institution's emphasis on rigorous intellectual discipline, laying groundwork for his interests in language, history, and moral philosophy, though he initially aimed toward an Episcopal ministry career.
Following graduation, Sumner traveled to Europe from 1863 to 1866 to study theology, ancient languages, and scientific methods at the universities of Geneva, Gttingen, and Oxford, deepening his exposure to continental scholarship and Anglican traditions. These years abroad honed his analytical skills and familiarity with European intellectual currents, influencing his later empirical approach to social sciences, before his return to the United States in 1866.

Academic and ecclesiastical career

Sumner was ordained as a deacon in the Episcopal Church on December 28, 1867, following his theological studies in Europe. Shortly thereafter, he relocated to New York City to serve as assistant rector at Calvary Church. He was ordained a priest in July 1869 and continued in pastoral roles, including as rector of the Church of the Holy Communion in Morristown, New Jersey, from 1870 to 1872. During this period, Sumner engaged in Anglican church politics, briefly editing The Living Church and advocating for reforms within the Episcopal establishment.
In 1872, Sumner resigned from the ministry to return to Yale University, where he had previously tutored in classics from 1866 to 1869. He was appointed professor of political economy and social science in September 1872, though his initial formal title was Professor of Greek and Ancient History; he quickly shifted focus to economics, sociology, and related disciplines as part of Yale's "Young Yale" reformist faculty group opposing traditionalist influences. Sumner's courses emphasized empirical analysis of social institutions, free markets, and the limits of state intervention, drawing from classical liberal thinkers and attracting large enrollments that established him as Yale's most dynamic lecturer.
Sumner held the nation's first dedicated professorship in sociology at Yale, introducing systematic teaching of the subject in the United States through lectures on social evolution, customs, and inequality. He remained at Yale for the duration of his career, expanding his influence through administrative roles, public lectures, and prolific scholarship until his death in 1910.

Later years and death

In the early 1900s, Sumner focused on completing his major sociological treatise, publishing Folkways: A Study of the Sociological Importance of Usages, Manners, Customs, Mores, and Morals in 1906, which introduced key concepts such as "folkways" and "mores" to describe societal norms and their evolution. This work solidified his influence in sociology despite ongoing health challenges, including a debilitating stroke in 1907 that impaired his physical condition but did not halt his intellectual output.
Sumner retired from active teaching at Yale University in June 1909 after nearly four decades of service, transitioning to emeritus status amid an ovation from Yale alumni led by President William Howard Taft. He remained engaged in professional activities, serving as president of the American Sociological Society and delivering addresses such as "The Family and Social Change" in 1908 and "Religion and the Mores" in 1909, the latter braved through a snowstorm in New York.
On December 27, 1909described as his third strokeSumner collapsed from apoplexy while presiding at an American Sociological Society meeting in New York City; he was initially taken to the Murray Hill Hotel before transfer to Englewood Hospital in New Jersey. His condition improved temporarily, but a relapse preceded his death on April 12, 1910, at 5:30 p.m., from stroke complications. Sumner was buried in Guilford, Connecticut, in his wife's family plot.

Economic Thought

Principles of laissez-faire economics

William Graham Sumner advocated laissez-faire as a policy of non-interference by the state in economic activities, defining it not as unchecked natural forces but as deliberate restraint to allow rational human effort to unfold without meddlesome disruption. In his view, expressed around 1886, laissez-faire entailed "do not meddle; wait and see," emphasizing empirical observation of outcomes from free individual actions rather than preconceived reforms. This principle stemmed from his belief that social and economic progress arises from voluntary exchanges and competition, unhindered by government, which he saw as prone to favoritism and inefficiency when intervening beyond protecting life, liberty, and property.
Central to Sumner's laissez-faire framework was the rejection of state-enforced redistribution or paternalism, as articulated in his 1883 treatise What Social Classes Owe to Each Other. He contended that no social class inherently owes material support to another, asserting that such obligations, when imposed by law, undermine self-reliance and productive incentives, leading to dependency and stagnation. Instead, Sumner argued for liberty of contract and free market allocation, where resources flow to their most efficient uses through individual choices, fostering capital accumulation essential for societal advancement. He warned that government efforts to "make the world over" through regulation ignored the complexities of economic causation, often exacerbating the very dislocations they aimed to cure, such as those from industrialization in the late 19th century.
Sumner maintained that laissez-faire did not promise utopian equality or happiness but realistically accounted for human diversity in abilities and efforts, yielding superior outcomes via natural selection in economic competition. He criticized altruistic interventions as naive, noting they burdened the "forgotten man"the productive middle classwho bore costs without benefits, while shielding the improvident from consequences. Empirical evidence from historical free enterprise, in his analysis, demonstrated that minimal state involvement maximized wealth creation, as seen in the rapid U.S. economic growth post-Civil War under relatively light regulation. This approach prioritized causal realism: interventions distorted incentives, while non-interference aligned individual pursuits with collective prosperity.

Critiques of protectionism and monetary policy

Sumner critiqued protectionism as a fundamentally inefficient policy that distorted markets by imposing taxes on consumers to subsidize select producers, arguing that it violated principles of economic conservation by promoting waste over productive use of resources. In his 1885 pamphlet Protectionism: The -Ism Which Teaches That Waste Makes Wealth, he contended that protective tariffs compelled the purchase of domestically produced goods at inflated prices, yielding no equivalent value to the buyer while enriching a narrow class of manufacturers at the expense of the broader populace. He likened such tariffs to a hidden tax system lacking democratic consent, where the revenue benefited special interests rather than public goods, exacerbating inequality by shifting burdens from the politically connected to ordinary workers and consumers.
In a series of 1877 lectures compiled as Lectures on the History of Protection in the United States, Sumner traced American tariff policy from its origins, demonstrating its roots in ad hoc political compromises rather than sound economic reasoning, marked by frequent oscillations between high duties and reductions that reflected ignorance of comparative advantage. He emphasized that protectionism ignored the gains from international specialization, where free trade allowed nations to exchange surpluses efficiently, fostering overall wealth creation; instead, it encouraged domestic industries to persist in uncompetitive pursuits, squandering labor and capital that could yield higher returns elsewhere. Sumner viewed this as the most egregious form of taxation abuse in American history, surpassing even colonial-era grievances by institutionalizing favoritism under the guise of national interest.
Turning to monetary policy, Sumner advocated strictly for the gold standard, opposing fiat currencies and bimetallism as mechanisms that enabled inflationary manipulation by governments and banks, leading to economic instability. He supported the post-Civil War Resumption Act of 1875, which aimed to redeem greenbacks in specie by 1879, criticizing the wartime issuance of unbacked paper money as a destructive expedient that eroded purchasing power and rewarded debtors at creditors' expense. In works like A History of American Currency, Sumner analyzed historical episodes of currency debasement, from colonial bills of credit to the greenback era, arguing that deviations from metallic standards invariably produced booms followed by busts, as expanded money supplies fueled fictitious capital without corresponding real output.
Sumner rejected bimetallism's promise of stable value through silver-gold parity, warning that it invited arbitrage and market disruptions, as seen in Gresham's Law where overvalued metals drove out sound ones; he insisted on gold's superiority due to its scarcity, durability, and universal acceptance as a neutral measure of value immune to political tampering. His monetary theory aligned with classical quantity-based explanations, positing that price levels rose proportionally with money supply expansions, rendering credit-driven inflations unsustainable and prone to corrective contractions that punished savers and productive investors. By championing hard money tied to gold, Sumner sought to constrain state overreach, ensuring that currency served exchange rather than fiscal expediency.

Views on social classes and redistribution

In his 1883 treatise What Social Classes Owe to Each Other, William Graham Sumner argued that no social class bears inherent obligations to another beyond upholding mutual liberty and equal rights under law, rejecting claims of entitlement to wealth or aid from productive members of society. He emphasized individual responsibility, stating that "a free man in a free democracy has no duty whatever toward other men of the same rank and standing, except respect, courtesy, and good-will." Sumner viewed social classes as emerging organically from variations in ability, effort, and the division of labor, which foster cooperation and advance overall welfare through free exchange rather than coercion.
Central to Sumner's critique was the "Forgotten Man," the self-reliant laborer and taxpayerlabeled "C" in his schemawho shoulders the burdens of state interventions designed by "A" and "B" (reformers or elites) to assist "X" and "Y" (the dependent or favored). This industrious producer, often overlooked in policy debates, funds redistributive measures through taxation and regulation without reciprocal benefits, inverting natural incentives by punishing thrift and rewarding idleness. Sumner contended that such schemes exploit the Forgotten Man to maintain privileges for others, as "the history of the human race is one long story of attempts by certain persons and classes to obtain control of the power of the State."
Sumner opposed wealth redistribution and socialist proposals for enforced equality, asserting they destroy liberty, pervert capital allocation, and reduce total societal production by diverting resources from efficient uses to inefficient ones. He criticized philanthropy and state aid for fostering dependency, noting that they "divert capital from productive laborers to the inefficient," thereby undermining self-reliance and long-term prosperity. Instead, he advocated laissez-faire as the true doctrine of liberty"minding ones own business"which allows natural inequalities to yield mutual gains through competition and voluntary cooperation, without class antagonism or forced leveling. Sumner warned that egalitarian interventions, by reversing rewards and punishments, inevitably lead to stagnation, as observed in historical patterns where equality pursuits have consistently eroded freedoms.

Sociological Theory

Foundations of empirical sociology

William Graham Sumner pioneered the academic study of sociology in the United States by integrating it into the curriculum at Yale University, where he served as professor of political and social science from 1872 onward. He offered one of the earliest systematic courses in sociology there, emphasizing the discipline's separation from speculative philosophy toward a scientific examination of social structures and processes. Sumner's approach rejected abstract theorizing in favor of inductive methods, drawing on concrete observations of societal phenomena to identify underlying patterns and laws.
In his 1881 essay "Sociology," Sumner delineated the field as an empirical inquiry into the interplay of social forces, analogizing societal development to biological evolution while insisting on verifiable data over normative prescriptions. He argued that sociology must catalog and analyze the concrete elements of social organizationsuch as institutions, customs, and conflictsthrough historical records and comparative analysis, rather than imposing ethical ideals or teleological assumptions. This framework positioned sociology as a descriptive science capable of revealing causal mechanisms in human associations, grounded in the aggregation of particular facts rather than universal deductions.
Sumner's empirical foundations culminated in works like Folkways (1906), where he systematically documented the origins and functions of social usages across cultures using ethnographic and historical evidence, illustrating how habits emerge from trial-and-error adaptations to environmental pressures. He described this process as involving "rude experiment and selection," yielding persistent patterns without conscious design, thus providing a methodological template for sociology as an observational discipline. By prioritizing such data-driven analysis, Sumner established empirical sociology's core principle: social phenomena must be studied as they manifest in reality, free from reformist biases that distort causal understanding.

Folkways, mores, and ethnocentrism

In his 1906 work Folkways: A Study of the Sociological Importance of Usages, Manners, Customs, Mores, and Morals, William Graham Sumner introduced the concepts of folkways and mores as foundational elements of social order, arising unconsciously from collective efforts to meet human needs. Folkways, defined as "the habits of the individual and customs of the society which arise from efforts to satisfy needs," encompass the broad array of traditional practices, including tools, language, and daily usages, that accumulate over time through repetition and group experience without deliberate design. These customs gain authority from tradition and social approbation rather than rational deliberation, exerting a coercive influence on individual behavior to ensure conformity, though violations typically incur only mild disapproval rather than severe sanctions.
Mores emerge as a subset of folkways when the group attaches to them notions of societal welfare, imbuing them with moral and ethical weight: "The mores are the folkways, including the philosophical and ethical generalizations as to societal welfare which are suggested by them, and inherent in them, as they grow." Unlike mere folkways, mores carry imperatives of right and wrong, demanding stricter adherence through social pressure, taboos, or even institutional enforcement, as they are perceived to determine the group's survival and prosperity. Sumner emphasized that mores function as a "dominating force in history," dictating permissible actions and methods while resisting alteration unless life conditions shift dramatically, often perpetuating practices long after their original utility fades. For instance, mores might sanctify customs like infanticide or ritual sacrifice in primitive societies if deemed beneficial, illustrating their relativity to group context rather than universal ethics.
Sumner linked these concepts to ethnocentrism, which he termed "the technical name for this view of things in which one's own group is the center of everything, and all others are scaled and rated with reference to it." This in-group bias, a natural outgrowth of folkways and mores, fosters intense loyalty and exaggeration of one's own customs while engendering contempt or abhorrence toward out-groups' practices, thereby reinforcing social cohesion within the group. Ethnocentrism, Sumner argued, drives the differentiation of groups through language, rituals, and standards, often leading to conflict or isolation, as each society applies its mores to judge outsiders as inferior or barbaric. While adaptive for group preservation in early societal stages, it hinders objective assessment and syncretism in advanced civilizations, underscoring Sumner's view of customs as evolutionary products selected for group fitness rather than abstract rationality.

Political Commentary

Opposition to imperialism

Sumner opposed American imperialism as a fundamental betrayal of the nation's founding principles of liberty and self-government, viewing it as an adoption of the despotic policies that had ruined Spain. In the wake of the Spanish-American War of 1898, he argued that the United States' military victory paradoxically represented a "conquest of the United States by Spain" through the importation of old-world imperial doctrines, including dominion over subject peoples deemed unfit for self-rule, which contradicted the American commitment to equality and individual liberty. He emphasized that true liberty entails "leaving people to live out their own lives in their own way," a principle imperialism inverted by imposing governance on distant populations without their consent.
As a key figure in the Anti-Imperialist League, formed in 1898 to resist territorial expansion, Sumner critiqued the war itself as a hasty partisan maneuver, "precipitated... headlong, without reflection or deliberation," driven by sensationalism rather than deliberate public choice, thus violating republican self-government. In his January 16, 1899, lecture "The Conquest of the United States by Spain," delivered before Yale's Phi Beta Kappa Society, he warned that imperialism would foster militarism"eating up all the products of science and art"along with vast debts, taxation, a swollen bureaucracy, standing army, and navy, ultimately empowering plutocracy and political jobbery at the expense of democratic institutions. Economically, he highlighted the futility of such ventures, citing the $20 million acquisition cost for the Philippines as wasteful expenditure that enriched no one while risking revolts and restrictive colonial trade policies akin to Spain's, which had precipitated that nation's decline.
Sumner's anti-imperialism aligned with his broader laissez-faire advocacy, predating the war; as early as 1896, he decried rising imperialist sentiments for threatening free markets and individual rights by necessitating coercive state power. He framed expansionism as "at war with the best traditions, principles, and interests of the Republic," predicting it would erode the self-reliant virtues forged in the American Revolution against monarchical overreach. By linking imperialism to ethnocentrismthe tendency to judge other societies by one's own standardshe identified it as a root of unnecessary conflict, further underscoring its incompatibility with peaceful commerce and non-intervention.

Analysis of warfare and its costs

William Graham Sumner regarded warfare as a primordial institution arising from intergroup conflicts in primitive societies, where it functioned as a mechanism for group selection and the formation of social cohesion through shared mores. In his essay "War," he contended that while such conflicts may have eliminated weaker societal units in early human history, contributing to the survival of more robust groups, modern warfare rarely achieves equivalent selective benefits and instead imposes disproportionate burdens on civilized nations. He emphasized that war's origins lie in the "we-group" versus "other-group" dynamics, fostering ethnocentrism but perpetuating cycles of violence that hinder long-term societal advancement.
Sumner detailed the human costs of warfare as staggering, involving direct loss of life and the psychological toll on survivors, which disrupts family structures and labor forces essential for economic productivity. Economically, he highlighted war's drain on resources through increased taxation, public debt accumulation, and diversion of capital from industrial development to military apparatus; for instance, he noted that conflicts like the Spanish-American War of 1898 escalated national expenditures without commensurate returns, fostering dependency on state financing that burdens future generations. Socially, warfare expands governmental authority, promoting militarism and hierarchy over egalitarian principles, as seen in his critique of imperialism where conquests lead to administrative overreach and the erosion of individual liberties.
In Folkways (1906), Sumner further analyzed war's role in enforcing societal norms, arguing that it generates intense mores around patriotism and sacrifice but at the expense of innovation and peaceable progress, as resources squandered in destruction could otherwise advance material welfare. He rejected romanticized views of war as a civilizing force, asserting that in advanced societies, it accelerates decline by equalizing nations through mutual exhaustion rather than elevating the victor. Ultimately, Sumner's causal assessment positioned warfare as a net negative for human welfare, incompatible with laissez-faire principles and the peaceful competition of free markets, which he contrasted as superior engines of societal improvement.

The "Forgotten Man" and state intervention

In his 1883 essay "The Forgotten Man," originally delivered as lectures in New Haven on February 8 and 9, William Graham Sumner critiqued social reform efforts by illustrating how they overlooked the burdens imposed on the productive classes. Sumner described a scenario where "A and B" representing philanthropists, legislators, or moral enthusiasts seek to aid "X," the indigent or idle, through measures like poor relief or regulatory schemes, but the true cost falls on "C," the forgotten man: the industrious, self-reliant worker who pays taxes, adheres to contracts, and sustains society without seeking favors. This C, Sumner argued, is "the real productive and earning class," yet reformers ignore him, treating his earnings as a public resource for redistribution.
Sumner contended that state intervention in this vein, such as subsidized welfare or enforced philanthropy, fosters dependency among the aided while punishing the forgotten man's virtues of thrift and foresight. He warned that such policies erode liberty by substituting coercive legislation for voluntary cooperation, ultimately harming societal progress: "The state cannot get a cent for any such purpose without taking it from the productive members of society." Empirical observation, Sumner emphasized, showed that free individuals under equal laws achieve the highest development of independence and energy, whereas interventions create "mixed" institutions blending liberty with compulsion, leading to inefficiency and moral decay.
Expanding on these themes in his 1887 address "State Interference," Sumner expressed a profound prejudice against government overreach, viewing it as a historical pattern of burdening citizens under the guise of protection. He advocated laissez-faire principles, arguing that economic distribution should follow "free contract under the play of natural laws" rather than state dictates, which inevitably favor special interests and expand bureaucracy. Examples included state controls on wages, prices, education, and sanitation, which Sumner likened to failed Roman and medieval systems that stifled innovation and individual agency. For Sumner, such interventions not only mulcted the forgotten man but also risked transforming the state into a tyrannical force, undermining the republican ideal of limited government securing equal rights without meddling in private affairs.

Engagement with Evolutionary Ideas

Sumner's interpretation of social evolution

William Graham Sumner viewed social evolution as an unconscious, adaptive process driven by the emergence and refinement of folkwaysspontaneous habits and customs arising from repeated efforts to satisfy basic human interests such as hunger, security, and reproductionand their elevation into mores, which carry moral authority and societal sanction. These folkways originate without deliberate intent or rational planning, forming through trial and error within groups, where expedient practices persist due to their utility in promoting pleasure and avoiding pain, while ineffective ones fade. Sumner emphasized that this selection operates at the societal level, akin to a sieve filtering outcomes based on collective experience rather than individual genius or state direction, resulting in the gradual buildup of institutions, rights, and norms over generations.
In Folkways (1906), Sumner detailed how mores evolve as folkways gain ethical and philosophical dimensions, reflecting group judgments on welfare and becoming coercive through taboos, rituals, and punishments that enforce conformity. Social evolution proceeds unconsciously as these mores adapt to shifting life conditionssuch as technological advances or environmental changesvia syncretism, where dominant customs prevail and marginalize rivals, often over centuries, as seen in the Inquisition's elimination of dissenters through sustained coercion spanning 10 to 12 generations by the 12th century. This process fosters societal complexity from simple cooperative acts, like language development from mutual needs or money from barter customs, without invoking biological inheritance as the primary mechanism; instead, it relies on cultural persistence and group validation.
Sumner contrasted this organic evolution with rationalist reforms or legislative interventions, arguing that they fail when misaligned with entrenched mores, as "legislation... must be consistent with the mores" to endure, and artificial accelerations disrupt the natural strain of improvement. He advocated laissez-faire policies to preserve this evolutionary dynamic, positing that free competition in markets and ideas allows adaptive institutions to emerge, benefiting society incrementally without privileging classes or overriding customary selection. Unlike biological evolution's focus on survival of the fittest, Sumner's framework avoided normative superiority claims, treating "fitness" as mere compatibility with local conditions rather than inherent virtue, and warned that state meddling, such as redistribution, could retard progress by insulating maladaptive practices. This interpretation underscored causation rooted in unintended consequences of human action, where societal advancement arises from decentralized adaptation, not directed purpose.

Mischaracterizations as a Social Darwinist

William Graham Sumner has frequently been labeled a Social Darwinist by historians and sociologists, a categorization that distorts his nuanced engagement with evolutionary ideas. Social Darwinism, as a term popularized in the late 19th century, generally refers to the extension of Charles Darwin's biological natural selection to human society, positing that competitive struggle among individuals or classes produces social progress and justifies laissez-faire policies by allowing the "fittest" to prevail without interference. This label gained traction among Progressive Era critics who sought to discredit advocates of limited government, equating their resistance to redistribution with endorsement of ruthless elimination of the weak. However, Sumner's writings demonstrate a rejection of direct analogies between biological and social evolution, emphasizing instead cultural and institutional processes over biological determinism.
In his seminal work Folkways: A Study of the Sociological Importance of Usages, Manners, Customs, Mores, and Morals (1906), Sumner outlined social evolution as driven by the accumulation and adaptation of group customs, or mores, which emerge from human needs and experiences rather than intrasocial conflict akin to animal competition. He contended that mores function as societal preservatives, fostering cooperation and stability through unreflective habit rather than selective elimination, and explicitly noted that Darwinian mechanisms of variation and struggle do not straightforwardly translate to the "superorganic" domain of human institutions. This framework critiques simplistic Social Darwinist views by highlighting how mores resist rational reform and evolve incrementally via societal consensus, not individual fitness contests. Sumner's analysis thus prioritizes empirical observation of historical customs over teleological progress narratives, distinguishing his sociology from Herbert Spencer's more rigid evolutionary optimism.
Sumner's opposition to state intervention, as articulated in essays like "The Forgotten Man" (1883), stemmed from concerns over unintended consequences and exploitation of the productive middle class by special interests, not from a belief that welfare defied natural selection. He argued that such policies burden the self-reliant "forgotten man"the average taxpayerwithout addressing root causes of poverty, which he traced to cultural and economic factors rather than inherent unfitness. Far from celebrating inequality as evolutionary destiny, Sumner warned of the risks of concentrated power and advocated voluntary association, aligning his views with classical liberal principles grounded in historical evidence rather than biological metaphor. His anti-imperialist stance, evident in critiques of expansionism as costly folly (e.g., his 1898 opposition to the Spanish-American War), further diverges from Social Darwinist rationales for conquest as civilizational advancement.
The persistence of the Social Darwinist label reflects interpretive biases in mid-20th-century scholarship, where progressive historians conflated Sumner's defense of liberty with endorsement of unmitigated competition, often ignoring his later refinements. Scholarly reassessments, drawing on primary texts, affirm that Sumner viewed social order as emergent from decentralized customs, not mandated by biological law, rendering the characterization a rhetorical oversimplification rather than a faithful representation.

Legacy

Influence on classical liberalism and conservatism

William Graham Sumner's advocacy for laissez-faire economics and limited government positioned him as a pivotal defender of classical liberalism during the late 19th century, emphasizing individual liberty as the exclusive right to employ one's powers for personal welfare without state interference. In works like What Social Classes Owe to Each Other (1883), he argued that social progress arises from voluntary cooperation and market processes rather than coercive redistribution, critiquing proposals for state-sponsored reforms as ultimately harmful to productive classes. This perspective reinforced classical liberal principles of free trade, the gold standard, and opposition to socialism, influencing subsequent economic thought by highlighting the unintended costs of interventionism on ordinary citizens, whom he termed the "forgotten man" in an 1883 essay.
Sumner's sociological framework, particularly his analysis of folkways and mores as organically evolved customs resistant to deliberate reform, provided intellectual ammunition for classical liberal skepticism toward top-down social engineering. By grounding liberty in empirical observation of societal evolution rather than abstract rights, he anticipated mid-20th-century libertarian critiques of welfare states and regulatory expansion, as seen in his warnings against policies that shift burdens from the idle to the industrious. His influence extended to libertarian sociology, where he is regarded as a foundational figure for integrating free-market advocacy with cultural analysis, countering progressive narratives that favored state-directed equality.
In conservative circles, Sumner's emphasis on the stabilizing role of inherited customs and his rejection of utopian interventions aligned with a tradition-valuing worldview, portraying him as a scientific conservative who derived principles from historical patterns rather than ideological fiat. He influenced American conservatism by defending the moral equality of persons against imperialistic or collectivist encroachments, arguing that true republican government requires restrained power to preserve individual agency and social order. This resonated in later conservative thought, particularly in critiques of expansive government that erode customary liberties, as evidenced by his enduring appeal among thinkers wary of elite-driven policy shifts. Despite academic portrayals sometimes framing him through a Social Darwinist lenswhich overemphasizes competition at the expense of his voluntary ethosSumner's legacy bolstered conservative liberalism's case for incremental, evidence-based governance over radical egalitarianism.

Modern scholarly reassessments

In recent scholarship, William Graham Sumner has been reevaluated as distinct from social Darwinism, with philosophers emphasizing that his invocation of "survival of the fittest" described adaptive processes shaped by environmental constraints rather than prescribing cutthroat individualism or laissez-faire as a moral imperative. Matt Zwolinski, in a 2017 essay, argues this mischaracterization overlooks Sumner's rejection of biological determinism and his advocacy for cooperative industrial competition over violence, as illustrated in his anti-imperialist pamphlet The Conquest of the United States by Spain (1898), which condemned militaristic expansion as antithetical to civilized liberty. Zwolinski further contends that Sumner's framework in Folkways (1906) portrayed social norms as organically evolved and resistant to top-down engineering, aligning with descriptive evolutionary sociology rather than normative Darwinian policy prescriptions.
This reassessment draws support from historians like Thomas C. Leonard and George H. Smith, who refute the social Darwinist label by highlighting Sumner's Malthusian influences and critiques of state-enabled exploitation, such as tariffs benefiting plutocrats at the expense of ordinary producers. Sumner's 1883 essay "The Forgotten Man" receives renewed attention as a proto-public-choice analysis, identifying the self-reliant taxpayer as the unintended victim of reformist interventions favoring special interests, with Zwolinski noting its prescience for modern concerns over moral hazard and cronyism in welfare policies.
Political theorists have revisited Sumner's linkage of liberal democracy to strict laissez-faire, positing in a 2010 Review of Politics analysis that he viewed state expansionsvia socialism, plutocratic alliances, or imperialismas eroding the contractual basis of society and accelerating systemic decay through incompatible interventions. The piece argues Sumner's diagnosis reveals inherent liberal vulnerabilities, such as atomized relations and weak value transmission, necessitating a public philosophy beyond Lockean individualism to preserve dignity and development amid democratic pressures.
Sumner's classical-liberal evolutionary theory faces scrutiny for its marginalization in early 20th-century sociology, eclipsed by progressive critics like W.E.B. Du Bois, who deemed it insufficiently activist against private inequalities and racial mores, favoring state remedies over decentralized adaptation. Yet, 2017 reassessments revive it as foundational to Hayekian spontaneous order concepts, underscoring Sumner's anti-imperialismshared unexpectedly with Du Boisand his warnings against folkways' entrenchment justifying unchecked power concentrations. These efforts position Sumner as a prescient critic of progressive liberalism's synthesis with state growth, relevant to debates on institutional evolution and limited government.

Major Writings

Key books and essays

Sumner's seminal sociological work, Folkways: A Study of the Sociological Importance of Usages, Manners, Customs, Mores, and Morals, published in 1906 by Ginn and Company, analyzes how societal customs and norms evolve through adaptation to environmental pressures rather than rational design or legislation. The book posits that "mores"deeply ingrained folkways reinforced by group consensusform the foundation of social order, resisting deliberate reform and shaping moral judgments organically.
In What Social Classes Owe to Each Other (1883, Harper & Brothers), Sumner critiques proposals for state-mandated social welfare, arguing that no class inherently owes another beyond voluntary cooperation and that interventions distort natural incentives for self-improvement. He emphasizes individual effort over collective obligations, warning that efforts to equalize outcomes undermine the productive classes who bear the costs of such policies.
The essay "The Forgotten Man," delivered as a lecture in 1883 and later anthologized, identifies the overlooked taxpayer as the true victim of reformist schemes, who funds benefits for others without consent or recognition. Sumner uses this concept to illustrate how philanthropic and legislative interventions shift burdens onto unrepresented working individuals, prioritizing liberty over engineered equity.
Other notable essays include "Protectionism" (1888), which denounces tariffs as inefficient wealth destruction disguised as national benefit, and selections in posthumous volumes like War and Other Essays (1911, edited by Albert Galloway Keller), compiling critiques of militarism and imperialism. These works reinforce Sumner's consistent advocacy for laissez-faire principles against protectionist and expansionist policies.
