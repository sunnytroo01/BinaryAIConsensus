Eczema vaccinatum
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Eczema vaccinatum is a rare, severe, and potentially fatal complication of smallpox vaccination that occurs when the vaccinia virus disseminates widely in individuals with active or quiescent atopic dermatitis (eczema), leading to a generalized vesiculopustular rash and systemic illness. First described in the late 19th century, it primarily affects those with compromised skin barrier function and impaired innate immunity due to atopic dermatitis, a chronic inflammatory condition affecting over 10% of the population in some regions. 
The condition arises from direct inoculation of the live vaccinia virus via vaccination or, more commonly in modern contexts, through contact transmission from recently vaccinated individuals to susceptible contacts, typically within 30 days when the vaccination site remains infectious. Pathophysiologically, the defective antimicrobial peptide production and altered immune gene expression in atopic skinsuch as downregulation of genes like ORM1 and TLR4facilitate viral replication and spread, distinguishing it from similar eruptions in non-atopic individuals. Historically, during eras of routine smallpox vaccination, the incidence was approximately 10.6 cases per million primary vaccinations, with a case fatality rate of 5%40%; however, since the resumption of targeted U.S. military vaccinations in 2002, only isolated contact-transmitted cases have occurred due to screening protocols. 
Clinically, eczema vaccinatum presents 69 days post-exposure with fever, malaise, lymphadenopathy, and a rapidly evolving rash that begins in eczematous areas and generalizes, featuring monomorphic vesicles, pustules, and umbilicated lesions resembling smallpox or varicella. Diagnosis relies on clinical history of exposure, characteristic rash distribution, and confirmatory tests such as polymerase chain reaction (PCR) for vaccinia virus from lesion swabs or electron microscopy revealing orthopoxvirus particles. It must be differentiated from eczema herpeticum (caused by herpes simplex virus) or other disseminated infections, often requiring urgent isolation until smallpox is excluded. 
Treatment involves immediate supportive care, including hospitalization and infection control, with vaccinia immune globulin intravenous (VIGIV) as the primary specific therapy to neutralize the virus and reduce mortality. In cases of VIGIV unavailability or failure, antivirals such as tecovirimat, cidofovir, or brincidofovir may be used under investigational protocols, alongside topical agents for secondary bacterial superinfection. Prevention centers on contraindicating smallpox vaccination for individuals with atopic dermatitis or their close contacts, promoting skin-to-skin contact avoidance post-vaccination, and considering alternative vaccines like modified vaccinia Ankara (MVA) for high-risk scenarios. 

Background

Definition

Eczema vaccinatum is a rare, severe cutaneous and potentially systemic infection caused by the widespread dissemination of vaccinia virus in individuals with atopic dermatitis following smallpox vaccination or close contact with a vaccinee. This condition represents a life-threatening complication of exposure to the live replicating vaccinia virus used in the smallpox vaccine.
It presents as a disseminated vaccinia infection resembling eczema herpeticum but caused by vaccinia virus (an orthopoxvirus), distinct from generalized vaccinia and other vaccine adverse events such as progressive vaccinia or postvaccinial encephalitis. Unlike generalized vaccinia, which occurs in individuals with normal skin integrity, eczema vaccinatum is uniquely linked to the presence of eczematous skin lesions that enable viral spread.
A key characteristic is the impaired epidermal barrier in atopic dermatitis, which permits extensive viral replication and dissemination from the vaccination site to distant areas of affected skin. Atopic dermatitis is the primary predisposing condition, heightening susceptibility to this dissemination.

Historical Context

Eczema vaccinatum was first described in the medical literature in 1882 by Henry Austin Martin, who reported a rare case of generalized vaccinia eruption in an infant with underlying eczema following exposure to the vaccinia virus from a vaccinated sibling. This early account highlighted the condition's occurrence during the nascent stages of smallpox vaccination campaigns initiated after Edward Jenner's 1796 use of cowpox material, which gave rise to the vaccinia virus employed in subsequent immunizations. The term "eczema vaccinatum" derives from "eczema," referring to the predisposing atopic skin condition, and "vaccinatum," from the vaccinia virus, itself named after the Latin vacca (cow) due to its origins in cowpox. By the early 20th century, additional cases had been documented, with a 1904 review by Blochmann compiling 19 instances, including five fatalities, underscoring its recognition as a serious complication of vaccination in eczematous individuals.
The condition gained formal recognition as a distinct clinical entity in the mid-20th century amid widespread smallpox immunization efforts, particularly as mass vaccination programs expanded globally to combat the disease. Incidence peaked during the 1950s and 1970s, coinciding with intensive campaigns; for example, in the United States, the rate reached 10.6 cases per million primary vaccinations in 1968, while outbreaks were reported in urban areas like New York City in 1947 and the United Kingdom in 1962. These episodes often involved secondary transmission within households, emphasizing the risks to contacts with active or prior eczema during routine and emergency vaccination drives.
Following the World Health Organization's declaration of smallpox eradication in 1980, routine vaccination ceased worldwide, rendering eczema vaccinatum exceedingly rare. However, potential resurgence risks persist due to stored vaccinia virus in military and laboratory stockpiles, as well as limited vaccination programs for bioterrorism preparedness, such as the U.S. initiatives resumed after the 2001 anthrax attacks to safeguard response personnel.

Pathophysiology

Etiology and Risk Factors

Eczema vaccinatum is primarily caused by infection with the live vaccinia virus, an orthopoxvirus used in smallpox vaccines such as Dryvax or the more recent ACAM2000, which can disseminate widely in susceptible individuals following vaccination or contact exposure. This dissemination occurs through autoinoculation from the vaccination site or direct transmission, leading to a severe, potentially life-threatening complication distinct from the localized reaction in healthy vaccinees. Eczema vaccinatum is associated with replication-competent live vaccinia vaccines and does not occur with non-replicating alternatives such as JYNNEOS (modified vaccinia Ankara).
The dominant risk factor is a current or past history of atopic dermatitis, affecting approximately 10-20% of children worldwide, which compromises the skin barrier and immune response, facilitating viral spread irrespective of disease severity or activity at the time of exposure. Genetic defects in the filaggrin (FLG) gene, the most common inherited risk for atopic dermatitis, further heighten susceptibility by impairing epidermal barrier integrity, as demonstrated in models where filaggrin deficiency led to larger lesions, increased satellite lesions, and higher vaccinia viral loads in skin and organs. Young age, particularly infants under 2 years and especially those under 1 year, amplifies risk due to immature immune systems and higher atopic dermatitis prevalence in this group.
Secondary risk factors include other exfoliative or inflammatory skin conditions such as burns, psoriasis, or severe acne, as well as immunosuppression from conditions like HIV, chemotherapy, or high-dose steroids, though atopic dermatitis remains the predominant predisposing factor. Transmission typically occurs via direct physical contact with lesions at the vaccination site or contaminated fomites from recent vaccinees, with historical data indicating that about 50% of cases in the 1960s involved unvaccinated household contacts rather than primary vaccinees. The condition is most severe in first-time vaccinees and close contacts, underscoring the need for screening in vaccination programs.

Pathogenesis

Eczema vaccinatum arises from the interaction between vaccinia virus and the defective skin barrier characteristic of atopic dermatitis. In atopic dermatitis, defects in the epidermal barrier facilitate the penetration and initial replication of vaccinia virus in keratinocytes and Langerhans cells. These structural abnormalities, including reduced ceramide levels and impaired tight junctions, allow the virus to establish infection more readily than in intact skin, where the barrier typically limits viral entry.
The immune dysregulation inherent to atopic dermatitis further exacerbates viral propagation. A Th2-skewed immune response predominates, characterized by elevated cytokines such as IL-4 and IL-13, which suppress antiviral defenses and enhance vaccinia virus replication in epidermal cells. Additionally, reduced expression of antimicrobial peptides, including defensins, impairs the innate immune containment of the virus, preventing effective clearance at the site of inoculation and promoting unchecked local proliferation. This failure in innate immunity contributes to the transition from localized infection to widespread dissemination.
Viral dissemination in eczema vaccinatum occurs primarily through viremia, enabling the spread from the initial inoculation site to ectopic skin lesions via the bloodstream, with potential direct extension in contiguous eczematous areas. This results in multifocal pustular eruptions across the body and, in severe cases, systemic involvement with tropism for organs such as the liver and lungs, leading to complications like hepatitis or pneumonia. Mouse models of atopic dermatitis demonstrate heightened viral titers and satellite lesions in Th2-biased environments, underscoring the role of allergic inflammation in facilitating this hematogenous spread.
Filaggrin deficiency, a common genetic feature in atopic dermatitis, plays a pivotal role by increasing transepidermal water loss and enhancing penetration of allergens and pathogens, including vaccinia virus. This structural protein's absence disrupts the cornified envelope, exacerbating local inflammation and synergizing with allergic responses to promote viral dissemination; studies in filaggrin-deficient models show increased IL-17A production, which further impairs viral control and heightens susceptibility to eczema vaccinatum.

Clinical Features

Signs and Symptoms

Eczema vaccinatum manifests primarily through cutaneous lesions that disseminate widely, often superimposed on areas of preexisting or healed atopic dermatitis. The rash typically consists of monomorphic papules evolving into vesicles and umbilicated pustules, resembling those at the primary vaccination site but occurring in hundreds of lesions across the body. These lesions frequently begin on the face, neck, upper chest, and extremities, such as the hands, and may become confluent in severe cases.
Systemic symptoms accompany the rash, reflecting the widespread viral dissemination and associated inflammatory response. Patients commonly experience high fever, malaise, and tender lymphadenopathy, with affected individuals appearing systemically ill. Signs of secondary bacterial superinfection, such as purulent discharge from lesions, may develop, exacerbating the condition. Other features can include facial and periorbital edema as well as conjunctivitis due to viral spread to ocular tissues.
The condition follows a progression timeline similar to primary vaccinia infection. In secondary cases from contact exposure, symptoms emerge after an incubation period of 5-19 days, with the rash peaking around 7-10 days post-onset, mirroring the evolution at the inoculation site. This timeline underscores the rapid dissemination in susceptible hosts with impaired skin barriers.

Disease Presentation and Complications

Eczema vaccinatum typically presents with an acute onset in individuals with atopic dermatitis who have received the smallpox vaccine or had close contact with a vaccinee, characterized by a disseminated rash that evolves from erythematous macules and papules to vesicles, pustules, and crusted lesions over approximately 2-4 weeks. This progression mirrors the natural course of vaccinia lesions but occurs primarily in areas of preexisting eczema, often accompanied by systemic symptoms such as fever and lymphadenopathy, while initially sparing the vaccination site in secondary cases among contacts.
The condition exhibits variants ranging from localized involvement, limited to eczematous skin regions with minimal systemic effects, to generalized dissemination driven by viremia, which can affect multiple organs and is particularly severe in infants, young children, or those with extensive atopic dermatitis. Localized forms tend to remain confined to the skin, whereas generalized cases reflect broader viral spread and heightened morbidity, especially among first-time vaccinees or unvaccinated household contacts.
Complications arise frequently due to the compromised skin barrier, including secondary bacterial superinfections with pathogens such as Staphylococcus aureus or Streptococcus species, which can progress to sepsis and require antimicrobial therapy. Ocular autoinoculation may lead to keratitis, corneal ulceration, or scarring with potential vision impairment, while autoinoculation to mucosal sites like the genitals is also reported.
Long-term sequelae often include post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation, keloid or pitted scarringparticularly on the face and exposed areasand psychological distress stemming from visible disfigurement and chronic skin changes. These effects can persist despite resolution of the acute infection, contributing to ongoing emotional and social challenges for affected individuals.

Diagnosis

Clinical Assessment

The clinical assessment of suspected eczema vaccinatum begins with a thorough history to establish exposure and risk factors. Clinicians should inquire about recent smallpox vaccination or contact with a recently vaccinated individual, as the condition often arises from autoinoculation or secondary transmission of vaccinia virus. The timeline of rash onset is critical, typically occurring 519 days after exposure. A personal or family history of atopic dermatitis or eczema, regardless of current activity, is a major predisposing factor, as impaired skin barrier function facilitates viral dissemination.
Physical examination focuses on skin inspection for characteristic vaccinia-like lesions disseminated outside the vaccination site, often appearing as vesicular or pustular eruptions superimposed on eczematous areas. The extent of underlying eczema should be evaluated, along with vital signs to detect fever, tachycardia, or other indicators of sepsis. Regional lymphadenopathy and signs of systemic illness, such as malaise, are commonly observed, with patients frequently appearing acutely ill.
Differential diagnosis is essential to rule out mimicking conditions, including eczema herpeticum due to herpes simplex virus, bacterial impetigo, varicella-zoster infection, or drug-induced eruptions, all of which can present with widespread vesicular rashes in atopic individuals. The presence of a clear history of vaccinia exposure strongly favors eczema vaccinatum over these alternatives, though overlap in clinical features necessitates prompt consideration. Secondary bacterial superinfections may also complicate the picture and require distinction.
Severity is gauged by assessing the underlying atopic dermatitis using validated tools such as the SCORAD (SCORing Atopic Dermatitis) index, which quantifies extent and intensity of eczematous involvement. This is supplemented by estimating the number and confluence of vaccinia lesions, as well as evaluating systemic features like fever, lymphadenopathy, or hemodynamic instability to guide urgency of intervention.

Laboratory Confirmation

Laboratory confirmation of eczema vaccinatum primarily involves detecting vaccinia virus in clinical specimens from skin lesions, as this establishes the viral etiology in patients with atopic dermatitis and a history of smallpox vaccination exposure. The most rapid and sensitive method is real-time polymerase chain reaction (PCR) assay targeting vaccinia virus DNA, performed on swabs or fluid from vesicular or pustular lesions; this technique provides results within hours and is available through specialized laboratories like the CDC's Laboratory Response Network. Electron microscopy of lesion scrapings or biopsy tissue can visualize orthopoxvirus particles, confirming the presence of a poxvirus with characteristic brick-shaped morphology, though it cannot distinguish vaccinia from other orthopoxviruses without further testing.
Viral culture remains a confirmatory approach but is slower, typically requiring 27 days; specimens are inoculated into cell lines such as Vero cells or onto the chorioallantoic membrane of embryonated chicken eggs, where cytopathic effects indicative of vaccinia replication are observed. Serologic testing for vaccinia-specific IgM and IgG antibodies via enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) or plaque reduction neutralization test is generally not useful for acute diagnosis due to pre-existing immunity from prior vaccination or exposure in many individuals; it serves better for retrospective confirmation when paired acute and convalescent sera show seroconversion.
Skin biopsy may be performed if the clinical presentation is atypical, allowing histologic examination for viral inclusions or enabling ancillary tests like PCR or electron microscopy to rule out differential diagnoses such as herpes simplex or disseminated zoster. In the United States, all suspected cases of eczema vaccinatum must be reported immediately to the CDC through the Vaccine Adverse Event Reporting System (VAERS) to facilitate public health response and antiviral distribution.

Management

Treatment Modalities

The primary treatment for eczema vaccinatum involves vaccinia immune globulin intravenous (VIGIV), which provides neutralizing antibodies against the vaccinia virus to mitigate dissemination in atopic skin. VIGIV is administered at a dose of 6000 units per kg body weight intravenously as soon as symptoms are identified, with repeat doses considered every 2-3 days based on clinical response until no new lesions appear for 24-48 hours.
Antiviral agents serve as adjunctive or second-line therapy, particularly in cases unresponsive to VIGIV or with evidence of viremia. These antivirals are available through the CDC and should be used in consultation with public health authorities. Tecovirimat (TPOXX), approved by the FDA in 2018 for smallpox and its complications, is given orally at 600 mg twice daily for patients weighing 40 kg to less than 120 kg or 600 mg three times daily for patients weighing 120 kg or more, for 14 days; it inhibits viral envelope formation by targeting the VP37 protein, preventing virion assembly and release. For resistant or severe cases, alternatives include cidofovir at 5 mg/kg intravenously as a single dose, with a second dose 1 week later if clinically indicated, which inhibits viral DNA polymerase, or brincidofovir at 200 mg orally once weekly for two doses (days 1 and 8) in patients weighing 48 kg or more, offering improved tolerability over cidofovir due to enhanced cellular uptake and lower renal toxicity.
Adjunctive therapies address secondary bacterial superinfections, which are common due to disrupted skin barrier function. Systemic antibiotics such as vancomycin are used for methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) infections, guided by culture results and local resistance patterns. Topical antiseptic agents may be applied to lesions to reduce microbial colonization, though their use is largely historical and supportive.
Early initiation of therapy, ideally within 72 hours of symptom onset, significantly reduces disease severity and improves outcomes by limiting viral replication and dissemination. In severe cases with viremia, combination regimens of VIGIV and an antiviral like tecovirimat are recommended to enhance efficacy and prevent progression to multi-organ involvement.

Supportive Care

Supportive care for eczema vaccinatum focuses on maintaining physiological stability, preventing secondary complications, and promoting skin healing in patients experiencing widespread vesicular and pustular lesions due to vaccinia virus dissemination in atopic skin. Patients often require hospitalization, particularly infants and those with extensive involvement, to provide intensive monitoring and intervention for systemic effects such as dehydration and potential organ dysfunction.
Hydration is critical due to fluid losses from fever, poor oral intake, and disrupted dermal barrier function leading to insensible losses. Intravenous fluids are administered to correct dehydration and maintain electrolyte balance, with vigilant monitoring of serum electrolytes to prevent imbalances. In severe cases with vomiting or high-output skin losses, volume repletion follows burn-like protocols to ensure hemodynamic stability. Nutritional support is essential, especially for prolonged illness; enteral or parenteral nutrition may be provided to meet caloric and protein needs, supporting immune recovery and tissue repair with easily digestible, nutrient-rich foods where possible.
Wound management emphasizes gentle handling of lesions to minimize trauma and autoinoculation while controlling secondary bacterial infections. Meticulous skin care, akin to that for burn patients, involves daily cleansing with mild antiseptics, gentle debridement of necrotic tissue if indicated, and application of occlusive or non-stick dressings to protect denuded areas and reduce viral spread. Strict isolation in a negative-pressure room is implemented to prevent transmission, with contact precautions for healthcare providers.
Pain and fever are managed symptomatically to improve comfort and reduce metabolic stress. Acetaminophen is typically used as a first-line antipyretic and analgesic, with opioids reserved for severe pain from extensive lesions or complications like supraglottic edema. Antiemetics may be given as needed to control nausea from systemic illness.
Close monitoring is imperative to detect early signs of deterioration, such as sepsis or multi-organ failure. Daily assessments include vital signs, complete blood count to evaluate for thrombocytopenia or leukocytosis, liver function tests for hepatic involvement, and renal parameters. Infants and young children often require admission to a pediatric intensive care unit for continuous hemodynamic monitoring and potential ventilatory support if respiratory compromise occurs.

Prevention

Vaccination Contraindications

Eczema vaccinatum arises as a severe complication following exposure to live vaccinia virus in individuals with atopic dermatitis, making certain groups ineligible for live-virus smallpox vaccines such as ACAM2000. Absolute contraindications include a history or presence of atopic dermatitis (also known as eczema) in the vaccine recipient or their household contacts, as these conditions heighten the risk of disseminated vaccinia infection. Other active exfoliative skin conditions, such as burns, impetigo, or varicella-zoster infections, similarly preclude vaccination for both the recipient and close contacts due to the potential for viral transmission and subsequent eczema vaccinatum. According to Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices (ACIP) and Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) guidelines, no live vaccinia vaccine should be administered to these at-risk groups in non-emergency settings.
Pre-vaccination screening is essential to identify these risks and prevent inadvertent administration. The ACIP recommends a standardized questionnaire to assess skin conditions: recipients and vaccinators should inquire whether the individual or any household member has ever been diagnosed with eczema or atopic dermatitis, or currently exhibits skin abnormalities like peeling, cracking, blistering, or oozing. If a history of such conditions is reported, consultation with a dermatologist is advised to confirm the diagnosis and evaluate current status before proceeding. This process ensures that only low-risk individuals receive the live vaccine, minimizing the incidence of eczema vaccinatum.
For individuals with contraindications who require protection against orthopoxviruses like smallpox or mpox, non-replicating alternatives such as JYNNEOS (also known as Imvamune or Imvanex) are recommended. JYNNEOS, a live but replication-deficient modified vaccinia Ankara vaccine, poses no risk of eczema vaccinatum even in those with atopic dermatitis, as it cannot disseminate systemically. ACIP endorses JYNNEOS as the preferred option for at-risk populations, including those with skin conditions, providing effective immunity without the hazards of live-virus vaccines.
Special populations warrant additional caution to avoid transmission to vulnerable contacts. Vaccination with ACAM2000 is contraindicated for infants under 1 year, either as recipients or household contacts of vaccinees, due to their immature immune systems and heightened susceptibility to vaccinia complications, including eczema vaccinatum if atopic dermatitis is present. Similarly, pregnant women should not receive live vaccinia vaccines, and potential vaccinees with pregnant household contacts must defer immunization to prevent fetal vaccinia or transmission risks, particularly if the contacts have eczema. In these cases, JYNNEOS serves as a safe alternative when vaccination is indicated.

Contact and Post-Exposure Measures

Contact precautions are essential to prevent transmission of vaccinia virus from recently vaccinated individuals to high-risk contacts, particularly those with eczema or atopic dermatitis who are susceptible to eczema vaccinatum. Vaccinees must keep the vaccination site covered with a bandage consisting of gauze overlaid by a semipermeable dressing, secured with tape, and further covered by clothing to minimize shedding of virus-laden scabs or drainage. This covering should remain in place until the scab separates naturally, typically 14 to 21 days post-vaccination, during which time the site is most contagious from days 4 to 14. Additionally, vaccinees should avoid sharing personal items such as towels, bedding, or clothing that may become contaminated, and any used dressings or scabs must be disposed of in sealed plastic bags to prevent fomite transmission. Strict hand hygiene is required, including washing with soap and warm water or using an alcohol-based hand rub containing more than 60% alcohol after any contact with the vaccination site or contaminated materials.
For individuals with eczema who have been exposed to a recent vaccinee, post-exposure prophylaxis focuses on mitigating potential infection in those contraindicated for vaccination. Vaccinia immune globulin intravenous (VIGIV) may be considered for prophylactic use in such high-risk exposed contacts, administered intravenously at a dose of 6000 Units per kg body weight as soon as possible, ideally within 3 to 4 days of exposure, to potentially prevent or attenuate disease progression. VIGIV is obtained through CDC's Investigational New Drug protocol upon clinician request via state health departments. Exposed individuals should be monitored closely for symptoms of eczema vaccinatum, which typically appear 5 to 19 days post-exposure, including fever, malaise, lymphadenopathy, and disseminated skin lesions.
Public health responses to potential exposures emphasize rapid intervention, particularly in settings like military operations or outbreaks where vaccination occurs. If a vaccinee's lesions are uncovered or precautions are not followed, quarantine may be implemented to limit contact until the site is no longer infectious, and all suspected transmissions must be reported immediately to local or state health authorities for investigation and resource allocation. In outbreak scenarios, enhanced coordination with CDC ensures availability of countermeasures like VIGIV, and healthcare facilities should enforce contact precautions to prevent nosocomial spread.
Education plays a critical role in adherence, with CDC guidelines recommending that vaccinees and their households receive detailed instructions on transmission risks, especially to children with eczema who face heightened severity from contact vaccinia. Families should be informed about recognizing exposure signs and seeking prompt medical evaluation, reinforcing that inadvertent contact can lead to severe outcomes in atopic individuals.

Epidemiology and Prognosis

Incidence and Case Reports

Eczema vaccinatum occurred at rates of approximately 10 cases per million primary smallpox vaccinations during the 1960s U.S. campaigns, based on national surveys that documented 54 cases in 1963 and 60 cases in 1968 among contacts of vaccinees. These figures reflect the widespread routine vaccination efforts at the time, with contact transmission accounting for a significant portion of incidents. Following the cessation of routine smallpox vaccination in the United States in 1972, the incidence declined sharply due to the reduced use of the live vaccinia virus vaccine.
In modern contexts, eczema vaccinatum remains extremely rare, with an estimated incidence of less than 1 case per million vaccinations in limited programs, such as those resumed for bioterrorism preparedness after 2001. A notable example is the 2007 case in the United States involving a 28-month-old child who developed a severe rash covering 50% of his keratinized skin after contact exposure to his father, an active-duty military member recently vaccinated with ACAM2000. This incident, the first confirmed U.S. case since 1988, occurred despite screening protocols and highlighted risks in household settings. In the U.S. military's post-9/11 vaccination program, over 450,000 doses were administered from 2002 to 2003 with no reported cases among vaccinees, though secondary transmissions like the 2007 event underscore ongoing vigilance.
Pre-1980 case reports often involved clusters linked to mass vaccination drives in the United States and Europe, with U.S. surveys documenting 74 eczema vaccinatum cases among 14 million vaccinated individuals in one national effort. Post-2000 reports are sparse and primarily associated with military preparedness programs, with isolated incidents such as the 2007 case and no evidence of 1-2 annual occurrences beyond that single event in available surveillance data; by 2025, no major outbreaks have been recorded, though theoretical risks persist in scenarios involving bioterrorism-related vaccine deployment.
In global contexts, historical incidence was higher in developing countries during the smallpox eradication campaigns of the 1960s and 1970s, where extensive vaccination amid endemic disease led to elevated exposure and reporting of complications like eczema vaccinatum, though exact rates varied due to underreporting and differing surveillance. There is no established link between eczema vaccinatum and the 2022 mpox outbreak, as the latter involved primarily non-replicating vaccines like JYNNEOS, which do not carry the same risk of vaccinia dissemination.

Fatality Rates and Outcomes

Eczema vaccinatum has historically carried a case fatality rate of 1-6% with appropriate treatment, though rates reached up to 30% in untreated cases or among infants under one year of age. Prior to the widespread use of vaccinia immune globulin (VIG) in the 1960s, mortality exceeded 30-40%, but VIG therapy reduced this to approximately 1%.
Prognostic factors favoring better outcomes include early diagnosis and prompt initiation of treatment, limited extent of the rash, and absence of viremia, while extensive underlying eczema or concurrent comorbidities such as immunosuppression worsen prognosis. Infants and young children face heightened risk due to immature immune responses and more severe disease presentation.
Most survivors of eczema vaccinatum achieve full recovery within 4-6 weeks under supportive care and antiviral therapy, though permanent scarring at lesion sites is common. Rare long-term complications may include recurrent skin infections or psychological effects from the illness experience.
Since 2000, fatality rates have declined to less than 1% with the advent of antivirals like tecovirimat and improved VIG protocols, and no deaths have been reported in U.S. cases after 2010 amid limited vaccination programs.
