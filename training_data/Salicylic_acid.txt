Salicylic acid
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Salicylic acid, also known as 2-hydroxybenzoic acid, is a lipophilic monohydroxybenzoic acid and a type of beta hydroxy acid (BHA) with the molecular formula CHO. It is a white, crystalline solid that occurs naturally in the bark of willow trees (Salix species) and leaves of wintergreen plants. Chemically, it features a benzene ring substituted with a hydroxyl group (-OH) and a carboxyl group (-COOH) in the ortho position, making it a key precursor in the synthesis of aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid).
Historically, salicylic acid's medicinal properties trace back to ancient civilizations, where willow bark was used to alleviate pain and fever, as documented by Hippocrates around 400 BCE. The active compound, salicin, was isolated from willow bark in 1829 by French pharmacist Henri Leroux, and Italian chemist Raffaele Piria derived salicylic acid from it in 1838. In 1853, Charles Frdric Gerhardt first synthesized acetylsalicylic acid, but it was German chemist Hermann Kolbe who developed an efficient industrial synthesis of salicylic acid in 1860, paving the way for its widespread use. By the late 19th century, Bayer patented aspirin in 1899, building on salicylic acid's anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antipyretic effects, though salicylic acid itself causes gastric irritation that aspirin mitigates.
Salicylic acid exhibits keratolytic, antifungal, and anti-inflammatory properties, primarily due to its ability to exfoliate the stratum corneum and penetrate lipid-rich skin layers. Physically, it has a melting point of 158159 C, sublimes at 76 C, and shows moderate solubility in water (approximately 2.24 g/L at 25 C) but better solubility in organic solvents like ethanol and ether. In medicine, it is widely used topically at concentrations of 0.56% to treat acne, psoriasis, dandruff, seborrheic dermatitis, warts, and fungal infections by promoting desquamation and reducing inflammation. These percentages are typically weight/volume (w/v), corresponding to grams of salicylic acid per 100 ml of product. Industrially, it serves as a raw material for producing aspirin, dyes, preservatives, and resins. However, it is classified as harmful if swallowed, causes severe eye irritation and can lead to chemical burns on eye contact, and may pose reproductive toxicity risks, necessitating careful handling.

Properties

Structure and formula

Salicylic acid has the molecular formula C7H6O3C_7H_6O_3C7H6O3. Its molecular weight is 138.12 g/mol.
The compound's systematic IUPAC name is 2-hydroxybenzoic acid. It is also known by common synonyms such as o-hydroxybenzoic acid and 2-carboxyphenol.
Structurally, salicylic acid features a benzene ring with a carboxylic acid functional group (COOH-COOHCOOH) attached at position 1 and a hydroxyl group (OH-OHOH) at the adjacent ortho position (position 2), enabling intramolecular hydrogen bonding between the two groups.
Salicylic acid is classified as a beta-hydroxy acid (BHA), where the hydroxyl group is positioned beta to the carboxylic acid relative to the benzene ring.
It acts as the primary precursor to aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid), differing structurally by the addition of an acetyl group (COCH3-COCH_3COCH3) esterified to the hydroxyl oxygen in aspirin.

Physical properties

Salicylic acid is typically observed as an odorless white crystalline powder, fine needles, or fluffy solid, often appearing colorless to light tan.
This compound exhibits a melting point of 158.6 C and a boiling point of 211 C at reduced pressure (20 mmHg), though it tends to sublime at atmospheric pressure above its melting point. Its density is 1.443 g/cm at 20 C.
Salicylic acid demonstrates limited solubility in water, approximately 2 g/L at 20 C, reflecting its polar yet non-ionic nature in neutral conditions; however, it is readily soluble in organic solvents such as ethanol (up to 34.87 wt% at 21 C), acetone, and ethers. The acidity is characterized by pKa values of 2.97 for the carboxylic acid group and 13.6 for the phenolic hydroxyl group, indicating stronger dissociation of the carboxyl proton due to intramolecular hydrogen bonding with the ortho-hydroxy group.
Under standard conditions, salicylic acid remains stable but gradually discolors upon prolonged exposure to sunlight and decomposes thermally above 200 C, with a characteristic decomposition peak observed around 200.8 C in thermogravimetric analysis.
Spectroscopic properties highlight its functional groups: in the infrared (IR) spectrum, key absorption bands include a broad O-H stretch at 25003300 cm due to hydrogen bonding, a C=O stretch at approximately 1665 cm for the carboxylic acid, and aromatic C=C stretches around 1600 cm. The H NMR spectrum features aromatic protons in the 7.08.0 ppm range, with the ortho proton to the carboxyl group deshielded near 7.9 ppm; C NMR shows the carbonyl carbon at about 170175 ppm. Ultraviolet-visible (UV-Vis) absorption occurs at maxima of 210 nm, 234 nm, and 303 nm in ethanol, attributable to * transitions in the benzene ring and n* in the carbonyl.

Production

Biosynthesis

Salicylic acid (SA) is primarily biosynthesized in plants through the isochorismate (IC) pathway, which begins with the conversion of chorismate to isochorismate by the enzyme isochorismate synthase (ICS). In Arabidopsis thaliana, the ICS1 gene encodes the predominant ICS isoform responsible for pathogen-induced SA production, contributing to approximately 90% of ultraviolet light-induced SA accumulation. Isochorismate is then transformed into SA through a two-step process involving PBS3 (which conjugates isochorismate to a glucose ester intermediate) and EPS1 (an amidase that completes the conversion), distinct from the bacterial mechanism.30369-7) This pathway is upregulated in response to pathogen attack, where transcription factors such as NPR1 and WRKY70 enhance ICS1 expression, leading to rapid SA accumulation at infection sites.
An alternative biosynthetic route in plants proceeds via the phenylalanine ammonia-lyase (PAL) pathway, starting from phenylalanine, which PAL converts to trans-cinnamic acid, eventually yielding benzoic acid through -oxidative or non--oxidative steps involving enzymes like AIM1. Benzoic acid is then hydroxylated at the ortho position to form SA, likely by a cytochrome P450 enzyme such as benzoic acid 2-hydroxylase (BA2H). This pathway predominates in species like rice, where PAL6 mutants exhibit a 60% reduction in SA levels, underscoring its significance in monocots. Both pathways can contribute to SA production depending on the plant species and stress conditions, with crosstalk between them modulated by environmental signals.
In natural systems, SA biosynthesis is highly responsive to biotic stresses, resulting in efficient accumulation; for instance, infection by Pseudomonas syringae in soybean leads to a threefold increase in SA levels within hours, enhancing defense without excessive energy expenditure. In bacteria, particularly Pseudomonas species, SA is synthesized from chorismate via ICS and isochorismate pyruvate lyase (IPL) for siderophore production, as encoded by the pmsCEAB gene cluster in Pseudomonas fluorescens. Animals exhibit minimal endogenous SA biosynthesis, with trace levels primarily derived from dietary sources rather than dedicated enzymatic pathways.

Chemical synthesis

The primary industrial method for synthesizing salicylic acid is the Kolbe-Schmitt reaction, which involves the carboxylation of sodium phenoxide with carbon dioxide under elevated temperature and pressure, followed by acidification to yield the product. In this process, phenol is first converted to sodium phenoxide by treatment with sodium hydroxide, and the phenoxide is then heated with CO at approximately 125150 C and 80100 atm for several hours, producing sodium salicylate, which is subsequently acidified with sulfuric or hydrochloric acid. Yields typically reach 7995% depending on optimization, with the ortho-isomer (salicylic acid) predominating due to the directing effect of the phenoxide ion.

Historically, in laboratory settings, salicylic acid was prepared by the oxidation of o-cresol or salicylaldehyde using oxidizing agents such as potassium permanganate (KMnO) in alkaline medium. For instance, salicylaldehyde is oxidized at the aldehyde group to the corresponding carboxylic acid, with the phenolic hydroxyl group remaining intact under controlled conditions. These methods were common before the development of the Kolbe-Schmitt process in the late 19th century.
Modern variants of the Kolbe-Schmitt reaction incorporate catalysts, such as Lewis acids or organic bases, to enable carbonation of phenol under milder conditions, including homogeneous systems in solvents like DMSO at 100 C or suspension-based approaches in toluene, achieving yields exceeding 90% while reducing energy demands.
The key raw material is phenol, primarily produced from petroleum via the cumene process, which involves alkylation of benzene with propylene followed by oxidation. Scaling this synthesis raises environmental considerations, such as reliance on non-renewable petroleum feedstocks, though the incorporation of CO serves as a form of carbon fixation to mitigate greenhouse gas emissions.
Following synthesis, the crude salicylic acid is purified by recrystallization from hot water or ethanol-water mixtures, where it dissolves at elevated temperatures and crystallizes upon cooling, effectively removing impurities like unreacted phenol or isomers.

The ortho positioning of the hydroxyl and carboxylic acid groups in salicylic acid enables strong intramolecular hydrogen bonding, which stabilizes the molecule and influences its reactivity. Consequently, it affects reaction rates in processes like esterification and acetylation by altering nucleophilicity and electrophilicity at these sites.

Derivatives

Salicylates constitute a class of compounds derived from salicylic acid through esterification, salt formation, or other modifications, which typically alter their lipophilicity by shifting the equilibrium between ionized (hydrophilic) and non-ionized (lipophilic) forms in a pH-dependent manner. Esters such as acetyl or methyl derivatives enhance lipophilicity compared to the parent acid, while salts increase water solubility.
A prominent derivative is acetylsalicylic acid (aspirin), formed by acetylation of the phenolic hydroxyl group of salicylic acid with acetic anhydride, typically in the presence of a catalyst like sulfuric acid. Its structure features a benzene ring with an ortho-acetyl ester and a carboxylic acid group (CHO, molecular weight 180.16 g/mol). Aspirin appears as a white crystalline powder and has a melting point of 135140 C.
Methyl salicylate, the methyl ester of salicylic acid's carboxylic group, has the formula CHO (molecular weight 152.15 g/mol) and is a colorless to yellowish liquid with a characteristic wintergreen odor. It exhibits high volatility, with a boiling point of 220224 C, vapor pressure of 0.0975 mmHg at 68 F, and vapor density of 5.24. This compound occurs naturally in plants such as the leaves of Gaultheria procumbens (wintergreen) and the bark of Betula lenta (sweet birch).
Sodium salicylate, the sodium salt of salicylic acid (CHNaO), enhances water solubility significantly, dissolving at up to 1000 g/L at 20 C. In aqueous solution (100 g/L at 20 C), it produces a mildly acidic pH of 6.5, reflecting partial ionization. This salt form appears as white, lustrous crystalline scales or amorphous powder.
Bismuth subsalicylate is a coordination complex of bismuth and salicylic acid (CHBiO, molecular weight 362.09 g/mol), presenting as a white crystalline powder. It is stable in air but sensitive to light and unstable in alkaline solutions, decomposing into a more basic salt; it also decomposes in strong acids and is insoluble in water (<1 mg/mL at 71.1 F).
Salsalate represents a dimeric structural variation, where the phenolic hydroxyl of one salicylic acid molecule esterifies the carboxylic acid of another, yielding 2-(2-hydroxybenzoyloxy)benzoic acid (CHO, molecular weight 258.23 g/mol). This solid derivative has a melting point of 147 C and limited solubility (0.246 g/L in water), with stability characterized by partial hydrolysis to salicylic acid in the small intestine.

Uses

Medical applications

Salicylic acid and its derivative acetylsalicylic acid (aspirin) are widely used in medicine for their analgesic, antipyretic, and anti-inflammatory properties. Aspirin serves as a prodrug that is rapidly hydrolyzed in the body to salicylic acid, which underlies its therapeutic effects in treating mild to moderate pain, fever, and inflammatory conditions such as arthritis. Standard adult dosages for these indications typically range from 325 to 650 mg every 4 to 6 hours as needed, not exceeding 4 grams per day.
As a keratolytic agent, salicylic acid is applied topically to treat skin conditions by softening and dissolving the outer layer of skin. In acne management, it is commonly formulated at 2% concentrations in gels, lotions, pads, or cleansers to unclog pores and reduce blemishes, with application 2 to 3 times daily on affected areas. Popular over-the-counter pads include Stridex Maximum Strength pads (2% salicylic acid, often referred to as the red box), which are widely used and align with some dermatologist recommendations for acne treatment and conditions such as seborrheic dermatitis. Prescription or professional-grade pads such as Gly/Sal 10-2 (containing 2% salicylic acid and 10% glycolic acid) are recommended by some dermatologists for exfoliation, oil control, and acne management in acne-prone skin. For body acne, some dermatologists prefer salicylic acid body washes over pads. Individual results vary depending on skin type, and irritation may occur. Concentrations are typically expressed as weight/volume (w/v) percentages; for example, a 2% salicylic acid product contains 2 grams of salicylic acid per 100 ml of product. It is particularly beneficial for oily, acne-prone skin with large pores, as salicylic acid is a beta-hydroxy acid (BHA) that penetrates sebum-laden pores to dissolve oil and keratinized debris, thereby removing blackheads (open comedones) and closed comedones, and improving skin roughness through exfoliation. For individuals with oily, acne-prone skin, usage is recommended to start 2-3 times weekly to build tolerance and minimize irritation, increasing to 2-3 times daily as tolerated. Salicylic acid cleansers are compatible and complementary with hydrating products such as hyaluronic acid night creams; salicylic acid exfoliates, unclogs pores, and treats acne, while hyaluronic acid attracts and retains moisture to counteract potential dryness and plump the skin. In a typical routine, apply the salicylic acid cleanser first to exfoliate, then follow with a hyaluronic acid night cream to hydrate and seal in moisture. Start slowly with salicylic acid if new to it, and patch test as needed. To support skin health during treatment, a simple and gentle routine is advised: use a salicylic acid or gentle cleanser, apply a non-comedogenic moisturizer (such as one containing hyaluronic acid) to counteract drying effects, and use broad-spectrum sunscreen with SPF 30 or higher daily due to increased photosensitivity; if irritation occurs, reduce application to every other day. For wart removal, higher concentrations of 17% to 40% are used in liquids, plasters, or gels, applied daily after soaking the wart to promote gradual exfoliation.
Low-dose aspirin, providing 75 to 100 mg daily (most commonly 81 mg), exhibits antiplatelet effects that inhibit blood clot formation, making it a key therapy for cardiovascular prevention. It is recommended for secondary prevention in adults with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, where it reduces the risk of recurrent heart attacks and strokes. As of 2022, guidelines advise against initiating it for primary prevention in adults aged 60 years or older due to bleeding risks outweighing benefits in low-risk individuals.
Historically, salicylic acid derivatives were first employed in the 1870s for treating rheumatic fever, with early clinical reports demonstrating rapid relief of symptoms like joint pain and fever. In modern veterinary medicine, salicylic acid is used topically to manage conditions such as digital dermatitis in dairy cows, where keratolytic formulations like pastes or powders promote lesion healing without antibiotics, and for treating papillomas or pyoderma in dogs, cats, and horses via ointments or shampoos.
Common formulations include ointments and creams at 3% to 6% for localized skin disorders, and shampoos at 1.8% to 3% for scalp conditions like dandruff, seborrheic dermatitis, and psoriasis, applied one to two times weekly or as directed. These topical products are designed for direct application to affected areas, with instructions to rinse thoroughly if used on the scalp and to avoid mucous membranes.

Industrial and other applications

Salicylic acid serves as a versatile intermediate in various industrial processes due to its chemical reactivity and preservative properties. Technical-grade salicylic acid is primarily utilized in the production of agrochemicals, dyes, colorants, and rubber compounds. In the dye industry, it acts as a key intermediate for synthesizing azo dyes, contributing to colorfastness and stability in textile applications. Additionally, it functions as a chemical intermediate for rubber vulcanization accelerators, enhancing the durability of synthetic rubber products.
In polymer production, salicylic acid is incorporated into resins, plastics, and adhesives, particularly as a modifier in alkyd resins for surface coatings. It improves flexibility, oxidation resistance, and moldability in these materials, making it suitable for metal chelate resins and developers. For instance, partial replacement of phthalic anhydride with salicylic acid derivatives in alkyd formulations yields resins with enhanced performance in paints and varnishes. It also serves as a catalyst in epoxy-amine reactions for curing agents, accelerating polymerization without compromising mechanical properties.
Beyond manufacturing, salicylic acid finds application as a preservative and antiseptic in cosmetics and food products. In cosmetics, salicylic acid has been employed at concentrations up to 0.5% as a preservative in shampoos, soaps, and other formulations, leveraging its antimicrobial activity to extend shelf life, but the 2025 SCCS opinion concluded it is not safe at that concentration when used as a preservative in all cosmetic products, deeming it safe only in certain categories (e.g., single dermal products except body lotions). In 2025, the SCCS updated safety assessments for children's exposure (ages 3-10), restricting concentrations for various cosmetic uses to mitigate risks (e.g., not safe at 2.0% in leave-on products except body lotion; safe at 0.15%). As an exfoliant in skincare products, its efficacy depends on pH levels, where lower pH enhances penetration and keratolytic effects in non-therapeutic contexts. Historically and in select regions, it has been used as a food preservative and bactericide, though regulatory restrictions limit its current application.
In agriculture, salicylic acid acts as a precursor for fungicides and agrochemicals, supporting the development of products that induce plant resistance to pathogens. Its role in pesticide synthesis underscores its importance in crop protection formulations. Global annual production of salicylic acid exceeds 170,000 metric tons, driven largely by demand in these industrial sectors, with China as the leading producer.
Derivatives such as methyl salicylate are briefly noted for their use in fragrances and flavorings within the spice industry.

Biological roles

Plant hormone functions

Salicylic acid (SA) functions as a key plant hormone involved in regulating defense responses, growth, and development, particularly in response to biotic and abiotic stresses. It acts as a signaling molecule that orchestrates systemic defenses, enabling plants to mount coordinated reactions against pathogens and environmental challenges. Derived primarily from the shikimate pathway via isochorismate synthase in chloroplasts, SA's hormonal roles extend beyond local responses to influence whole-plant physiology.
A primary function of SA is the induction of systemic acquired resistance (SAR), a broad-spectrum defense mechanism activated following local pathogen infection. In SAR, SA accumulation in distal tissues triggers the expression of pathogenesis-related (PR) genes, enhancing resistance to subsequent invasions by bacteria, viruses, and fungi without direct pathogen contact. For instance, in tobacco and Arabidopsis, SA-deficient mutants fail to establish SAR, underscoring its essential role, while grafting experiments confirm that SA must accumulate locally in systemic leaves for effective defense priming. This process involves mobile signals like methyl salicylate (MeSA), which travels through the phloem to uninfected areas, amplifying immune responses.
SA also mediates thermogenesis in certain plants, notably the voodoo lily (Sauromatum guttatum), where it induces heat production in the inflorescence to volatilize attractants for pollinators. During flowering, endogenous SA levels rise dramatically in the appendix of the spadix, activating the alternative respiratory pathway and elevating tissue temperature by up to 30C for several hours. Mass spectrometric analysis identified SA as the natural calorigen, with exogenous application mimicking this heat burst in thermogenic tissues.
SA interacts antagonistically with jasmonic acid (JA) in modulating stress responses, allowing plants to fine-tune defenses against diverse threats. Biotrophic pathogens typically elicit SA-dependent pathways, which suppress JA-mediated responses suited to necrotrophs and herbivores, preventing resource misallocation during infection. This crosstalk, observed in Arabidopsis and tobacco, involves reciprocal inhibition of signaling components like NPR1 for SA and JAZ repressors for JA, ensuring adaptive prioritization of defenses.
During stress, SA accumulates in phloem tissues for long-distance transport, reaching microgram-per-gram levels that correlate with defense activation. Basal SA concentrations in Arabidopsis leaves range from 0.22 to 5 g/g fresh weight, surging 10- to 100-fold upon pathogen challenge, with MeSA facilitating phloem mobility via pH-dependent loading. This targeted accumulation in vascular tissues ensures rapid systemic signaling without excessive local toxicity.
Exogenous SA applications, such as foliar sprays at concentrations of 0.52 mM, enhance crop protection by priming natural defenses against fungal pathogens. For example, 1 mM SA sprays on apples reduce scab incidence by approximately 55%. In field trials with maize, SA treatments demonstrated 4572% suppression of late wilt (Fusarium oxysporum), alongside yield increases of 2747%. SA also induces resistance to powdery mildew in tomatoes through SAR-like responses.
Limited studies have examined the effects of salicylic acid on cannabis plants. Exogenous SA application has been shown to alleviate salinity stress in hemp (Cannabis sativa L.), improving growth parameters, chlorophyll content, photosynthetic efficiency, antioxidant enzyme activity, and reducing oxidative damage. SA also induces defense responses, such as systemic acquired resistance, potentially enhancing resistance to biotic stresses like pathogens. Effects on cannabinoid production or yield under normal conditions are not well-documented in available research.
In home gardening, particularly for houseplants, exogenous salicylic acid is often applied using uncoated aspirin tablets. A typical method involves dissolving one 300-500 mg tablet in 1-4 liters of water and applying the solution by watering the soil or spraying the leaves with a mister, 1-2 times per month. This practice aims to enhance plant resilience to stresses, promote growth, and activate defense mechanisms by leveraging SA's role as a plant hormone.

Dietary sources

Salicylic acid occurs naturally in various plant-based foods, primarily as a result of its role in plant metabolism. The highest concentrations are found in spices and herbs; for example, curry powder contains approximately 2-7 mg per 100 g, while turmeric and mint exhibit levels around 2-5 mg per 100 g. Fruits such as berries (e.g., blackberries at 0.08 mg per 100 g and strawberries at 0.06 mg per 100 g) and oranges (up to 0.2 mg per 100 g) provide moderate amounts, typically ranging from 0.1 to 2 mg per 100 g depending on variety and ripeness. Vegetables like tomatoes (0.1 mg per 100 g) and peppers also contribute, though generally at lower levels of 0.01-0.1 mg per 100 g.
Processed foods and beverages derived from plants can further supply salicylic acid. Tea infusions may contain up to 0.5-1 mg per liter, while certain wines reach similar concentrations of about 1 mg per liter; honey varies but can provide up to 1 mg per kg in some varieties. In Western diets, estimated daily intake from these sources typically ranges from 0.5 to 10 mg, with medians around 3-4 mg based on food frequency assessments, though vegetarians may consume higher amounts approaching 12 mg daily in plant-rich diets.
Concentrations of salicylic acid in produce show variability influenced by growing conditions, with organic crops often exhibiting higher levels than conventional ones due to stress-induced biosynthesis in the absence of synthetic pesticides. For instance, organic vegetable soups have been found to contain nearly six times more salicylic acid (median 0.012 mg per 100 g) compared to non-organic counterparts (median 0.002 mg per 100 g).
Salicylic acid from dietary sources is readily absorbed in the human gut via passive diffusion or monocarboxylic acid transporters, leading to serum concentrations that can overlap with those from low-dose synthetic aspirin (e.g., 75 mg). This bioavailability is comparable to pharmaceutical forms, as the molecule is identical, though food matrix effects may slightly modulate absorption rates without significant differences in overall uptake.
Non-plant sources, such as animal products including meat, fish, dairy, and eggs, contain minimal to negligible amounts of salicylic acid, typically below detectable limits in standard analyses.

Mechanism of action

Pharmacological effects

Salicylic acid exerts its primary pharmacological effects through inhibition of cyclooxygenase enzymes (COX-1 and COX-2), which are key in the synthesis of prostaglandins from arachidonic acid. While salicylic acid itself acts as a reversible competitive inhibitor of COX activity, particularly at higher concentrations, its acetylated derivative acetylsalicylic acid (aspirin) provides an irreversible mechanism via acetylation of a serine residue in the enzyme's active site. This acetylation reduces prostaglandin production, contributing to anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antipyretic actions. The reaction can be represented as:
Acetylsalicylicacid+COX-Ser-OHSalicylicacid+COX-Ser-OCOCH3
\text{Acetylsalicylic acid} + \text{COX-Ser-OH} \rightarrow \text{Salicylic acid} + \text{COX-Ser-OCOCH}_3
Acetylsalicylicacid+COX-Ser-OHSalicylicacid+COX-Ser-OCOCH3
This covalent modification blocks the enzyme's catalytic function, with the released salicylic acid then mediating additional effects.
For topical applications, salicylic acid acts as a keratolytic agent by disrupting the lipid structure in the stratum corneum and lowering the pH of the skin, which increases hydration and swelling of corneocytes, promoting desquamation and exfoliation. This comedolytic effect unclogs pores in acne and reduces scaling in psoriasis, independent of systemic COX inhibition at low concentrations (0.56%).
In the anti-inflammatory pathway, salicylic acid suppresses the nuclear factor kappa B (NF-B) transcription factor, which regulates genes involved in inflammation, such as those encoding cytokines and adhesion molecules. By binding to and inhibiting IB kinase (IKK), salicylic acid prevents the phosphorylation and degradation of IB, thereby retaining NF-B in the cytosol and reducing its translocation to the nucleus. This inhibition occurs at therapeutic concentrations and contributes to decreased production of pro-inflammatory mediators beyond prostaglandin pathways.
Salicylic acid reduces fever by interfering with prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) synthesis in the hypothalamus, where elevated PGE2 levels during infection or inflammation raise the thermoregulatory set point. By inhibiting COX enzymes, salicylic acid lowers PGE2 concentrations in the preoptic area of the hypothalamus, allowing normal heat dissipation mechanisms to restore body temperature. This central antipyretic effect is shared among salicylates and other non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs.
Recent studies post-2020 have highlighted the anticancer potential of salicylic acid, particularly in colorectal cancer prevention. For instance, salicylate treatment induces a DNA damage response in colorectal cancer cells via activation of AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) and inhibition of c-MYC, leading to reduced cell proliferation and enhanced apoptosis. Additionally, salicylic acid derivatives like 5-aminosalicylic acid (5-ASA) have been shown to activate peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma (PPAR), promoting antiproliferative effects in colorectal cell lines and potentially contributing to chemopreventive outcomes.
Pharmacokinetically, salicylic acid is rapidly absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract, achieving peak plasma levels within 1-2 hours after oral administration. It undergoes hepatic metabolism primarily via glycine conjugation to form salicyluric acid and other conjugates, with minor pathways including oxidation to gentisic acid. The plasma half-life is dose-dependent, typically 2-3 hours at low therapeutic doses, though it prolongs to 15-30 hours at higher doses due to saturation of metabolic pathways.

Plant signaling

Salicylic acid (SA) primarily exerts its signaling effects in plants through the NPR1-dependent pathway, where it modulates the activity of the non-expressor of pathogenesis-related genes 1 (NPR1) protein, a central regulator of defense responses. Upon pathogen perception, SA accumulation triggers redox changes that reduce NPR1 oligomers into monomers via thioredoxin-mediated mechanisms, facilitating NPR1's nuclear translocation. In the nucleus, NPR1 interacts with TGA-class transcription factors and forms condensates to activate the expression of pathogenesis-related (PR) genes, such as PR1, which encode antimicrobial proteins essential for systemic acquired resistance. Recent structural studies reveal that SA directly binds to NPR1 in a hydrophobic pocket with relatively low affinity, but more potently to its paralogs NPR3 and NPR4, which act as transcriptional corepressors; this binding relieves NPR3/NPR4-mediated repression of NPR1, enhancing its co-activator function.
SA signaling integrates with reactive oxygen species (ROS) and pattern recognition receptor (PRR) pathways to amplify plant immunity, particularly during pattern-triggered immunity (PTI). PRRs, such as FLS2, detect pathogen-associated molecular patterns and initiate ROS bursts via respiratory burst oxidase homologs (RBOHs), like RBOHD, producing hydrogen peroxide (HO) that acts as a secondary messenger. SA enhances these ROS bursts by upregulating RBOH transcription and maintains redox homeostasis through glutathione modulation, creating a self-amplifying loop where ROS spatially regulates SA accumulation at infection sites. This crosstalk is evident in responses to biotrophic pathogens, where chloroplastic ROS signals via stromules further potentiate SA-dependent gene transcription for hypersensitive response and defense priming.
Feedback loops in SA signaling ensure balanced immune responses, including autoregulation through suppression of isochorismate synthase (ICS1), the primary enzyme for pathogen-induced SA biosynthesis. Once SA levels rise, NPR1 inhibits ICS1 transcription, preventing excessive accumulation that could impose fitness costs on the plant. This negative feedback is complemented by positive loops, such as the EDS1/PAD4 complex promoting ICS1 expression, and involves NAC transcription factors (e.g., ANAC019) that downregulate ICS1 while enhancing SA conjugation for homeostasis.
Recent research from 2020 to 2025 highlights SA's role in epigenetic modifications and adaptation to climate-induced stresses, such as drought and heat. SA influences DNA methylation and histone acetylation via NPR1-dependent pathways, modulating stress-responsive genes like DREB2A and HSP70 to enhance tolerance; for instance, exogenous SA reduces oxidative damage by 42% in drought-stressed crops through upregulated antioxidants. In temperature stress, SA biosynthesis is suppressed at high temperatures but enhanced at low ones, enabling epigenetic priming for resilience against combined abiotic-biotic challenges.
To visualize SA signaling dynamics, researchers employ -glucuronidase (GUS) reporter genes fused to SA-responsive promoters, such as PR1::GUS, in model plants like Arabidopsis thaliana. These transgenics allow quantitative in situ monitoring of signaling activation through fluorogenic substrates like 4-methylumbelliferyl--D-glucuronide, revealing spatial patterns of defense gene induction; for example, SA treatment induces up to 8-fold GUS activity, enabling high-throughput screens for signaling modulators. This assay distinguishes strong activators from inhibitors via fluorescence-based Z-score analysis, providing insights into pathway fidelity without destructive sampling.

Safety

Toxicity and side effects

Salicylic acid exhibits moderate acute toxicity in mammals, with an oral LD50 of 891 mg/kg in rats. High doses exceeding 150 mg/kg can lead to symptoms such as tinnitus, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and hyperventilation, reflecting its impact on metabolic and auditory pathways. In severe cases, acute exposure may progress to confusion, seizures, and respiratory alkalosis due to uncoupling of oxidative phosphorylation.
Chronic exposure to salicylic acid, particularly through derivatives like aspirin, is associated with gastrointestinal adverse effects, including ulcers and bleeding, primarily due to inhibition of cyclooxygenase-1 (COX-1) enzymes that protect the gastric mucosa. This COX-1 inhibition reduces prostaglandin synthesis, impairing mucosal defense against acid and pepsin. Additionally, prolonged use in children during viral infections such as influenza or varicella has been linked to Reye's syndrome, a rare but serious condition involving encephalopathy and hepatic fatty degeneration.
Hypersensitivity reactions to salicylic acid occur in approximately 0.6-2.5% of the general population, manifesting as urticaria, angioedema, or respiratory symptoms in susceptible individuals. These reactions often involve cross-reactivity with other nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) due to shared inhibition of arachidonic acid metabolism pathways.
Salicylic acid eye contact causes severe ocular irritation and chemical burns, primarily affecting the cornea and conjunctiva with epithelial defects, corneal opacity, and potential limbal involvement. Case reports document severe corneal burns from accidental exposure to high concentrations (e.g., 10-20% salicylic acid mistaken for eye drops or lubricants), leading to pain, photophobia, and temporary vision loss. Damage is limited to the anterior segment with no reported penetration through the cornea to cause retina damage, as acid burns induce coagulative necrosis that forms a barrier limiting deeper penetration. Retina damage is associated with alkali (not acid) chemical burns via mechanisms like TNF-alpha inflammation.
Management of salicylic acid overdose focuses on enhancing elimination through urinary alkalinization, typically achieved with intravenous sodium bicarbonate to maintain urine pH above 7.5, which ionizes salicylate and promotes its renal excretion. This approach, combined with supportive care, is critical for moderate to severe toxicity.
In animals, salicylic acid toxicity mirrors human effects at high doses, but plants exhibit lower susceptibility owing to its endogenous role as a signaling molecule in defense responses against pathogens and stressors.

Regulatory aspects

Salicylic acid is affirmed as generally recognized as safe (GRAS) for use as a flavoring agent by the Flavor and Extract Manufacturers Association (FEMA, GRAS 3985), and permitted as an indirect food additive under 21 CFR 175.105. For medicinal applications, the FDA classifies it as generally recognized as safe and effective (GRASE) for over-the-counter (OTC) topical acne treatments at concentrations of 0.52%. It is also GRASE OTC for psoriasis at 1.83% and for warts at 540% in appropriate vehicles. Higher concentrations may require a prescription for other uses.

In the European Union, the European Commission's Scientific Committee on Consumer Safety (SCCS) deems salicylic acid safe as a cosmetic preservative at a maximum concentration of 0.5%, with higher limits permitted for non-preservative uses: up to 3% in rinse-off hair products and 2% in other leave-on products, reduced to 0.5% in sensitive areas such as body lotions, eye makeup, lipsticks, and oral products; it is prohibited in products for children under 3 years and sprayable applications due to inhalation risks.
Under the EU's REACH Regulation (EC) No 1907/2006, salicylic acid is registered without specific environmental emission limits, but its concentrations in cosmetics are capped as noted to mitigate release into waterways; it demonstrates ready biodegradability in aquatic environments, with aerobic half-lives typically ranging from 6 to 34 days.
The U.S. Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) lacks a specific permissible exposure limit (PEL) for salicylic acid, defaulting to the general standard for particulates not otherwise regulated at 15 mg/m (total dust, 8-hour time-weighted average) or 5 mg/m (respirable fraction); the American Conference of Governmental Industrial Hygienists (ACGIH) has no specific threshold limit value (TLV) for salicylic acid, but 5 mg/m (8-hour TWA) is used in some safety data sheets, analogous to acetylsalicylic acid. Industrial handling requires precautions such as local exhaust ventilation, impervious gloves, safety goggles, and respiratory protection to avoid dust inhalation, skin contact, or eye exposure.
Globally, salicylic acid faces no major bans, though restrictions align with regional cosmetic and pharmaceutical guidelines to limit environmental persistence; it is routinely monitored in wastewater effluents from manufacturing and consumer use as a pharmaceutical pollutant of emerging concern, with concentrations often exceeding 1 g/L in treated sewage.
Recent regulatory developments from 2023 to 2025 include the SCCS's June 2023 opinion reaffirming cosmetic safety limits while noting insufficient data for children aged 310, followed by a July 2024 EU call for aggregate exposure data on salicylic esters; in Asia, South Korea's August 2024 guidelines mandated precise labeling for salicylic acid in cosmetics to restrict claims beyond preservation, while Indonesia's October 2025 updates under PerBPOM No. 25 tightened limits in rinse-off products to 2%.

History

Discovery

The use of willow bark (Salix spp.) as a medicinal remedy dates back to ancient civilizations, where it was employed for pain relief and reducing inflammation. In ancient Egypt, around 1500 BCE, the Ebers Papyrus documented willow as an anti-inflammatory agent for treating aches and pains. In ancient Greece during the 5th century BCE, physicians including Hippocrates (c. 460370 BCE) are traditionally credited with prescribing willow bark extracts to alleviate pain, fevers, and inflammatory conditions, though direct evidence from primary sources is lacking. Similarly, in ancient China, willow shoots and related poplar barks were utilized for centuries to manage rheumatic fever, colds, and hemorrhages.
Pre-industrial herbal traditions across Europe and Asia continued these practices, with willow bark infusions commonly prepared as teas or poultices for joint pain, headaches, and fever reduction, reflecting its widespread role in folk medicine before chemical isolation. In 1828, German pharmacologist Johann Andreas Buchner first isolated salicin, the key glycoside responsible for willow's therapeutic effects, from willow bark extracts. This was refined in 1829 by French pharmacist Henri Leroux, who obtained salicin in crystalline form.
In 1838, Italian chemist Raffaele Piria advanced this work by hydrolyzing salicin to yield a more potent acidic compound, which he named salicylic acid after the Latin genus Salix for willow. Piria and fellow Italian chemists, including those at the University of Pisa, characterized it that year as hydroxybenzoic acid, confirming its structure as a derivative of benzoic acid with a hydroxyl group. Natural sources beyond willow were also identified, with meadowsweet (Filipendula ulmaria) recognized for its high salicylic acid content, particularly in its flowers and leaves, which contributed to its use in European herbal remedies for similar analgesic purposes.

Pharmaceutical development

Earlier, in 1853, French chemist Charles Frdric Gerhardt synthesized acetylsalicylic acid from salicylic acid, though not in a pure or practical form. In 1860, German chemist Hermann Kolbe developed a method to synthesize salicylic acid in pure form from phenol and carbon dioxide under pressure, known as the Kolbe-Schmitt reaction, enabling its production independent of natural sources. This breakthrough laid the groundwork for scalable pharmaceutical applications, as prior extractions from willow bark yielded impure compounds with limited therapeutic reliability.
The modern pharmaceutical trajectory of salicylic acid advanced significantly in 1897 when Felix Hoffmann, a chemist at Bayer, acetylated salicylic acid to produce acetylsalicylic acid (ASA), aiming to mitigate the gastric irritation associated with the parent compound. Bayer patented ASA in 1899 and launched it commercially as Aspirin that year, marketing it initially as a powder for pain relief and fever reduction. By the early 20th century, Aspirin achieved rapid commercial success, with Bayer's sales surging due to its efficacy and aggressive global marketing; by the 1910s, it had become one of the company's flagship products, generating substantial revenue amid growing demand for analgesics.
Bayer's U.S. patent expired in 1917 amid World War I seizures of German assets, prompting widespread generic production and democratizing access to ASA worldwide. Aspirin was included on the World Health Organization's Model List of Essential Medicines in 1977 (and remains as of 2023), affirming its historical role in pain management across resource-limited settings.
