William Heard Kilpatrick
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 William Heard Kilpatrick (November 20, 1871 February 13, 1965) was an American educator and philosopher renowned for advancing progressive education principles through his articulation of the "project method," which prioritized student-driven, purposeful activities over rote learning.
A student, colleague, and successor to John Dewey at Teachers College, Columbia University, Kilpatrick joined the faculty in 1909, earned his Ph.D. there in 1912, and taught for over 25 years, shaping curricula and training influential educators in child-centered pedagogy.
His seminal 1918 essay, "The Project Method," synthesized Dewey's experiential learning into a practical framework defining projects as "wholehearted purposeful activity" carried out in a social environment, which gained widespread adoption in schools and influenced vocational and secondary education innovations.
While celebrated for promoting active engagement and democracy in classrooms, Kilpatrick's approach drew criticism for insufficient emphasis on teacher guidance and core knowledge transmission, potentially undermining academic rigor; he himself later reflected critically, stating "I had made a mistake" regarding its overapplication without balanced structure.

Early Life and Education

Childhood and Family Background

William Heard Kilpatrick was born on November 20, 1871, in White Plains, a rural community in Greene County, Georgia, to James Hines Kilpatrick, a Southern Baptist minister, and his second wife, Edna Perrin Heard. As the first child of this marriage, he entered a large blended family; his father, born in 1833, had previously been married to Cornelia Hall, with whom he had several children before her death in 1870, eventually fathering a total of 13 offspring across both unions.
Kilpatrick's father exemplified the era's Southern clerical tradition, serving as pastor of White Plains Baptist Church for over 53 years from his ordination until his death in 1908, instilling in the household a strict Baptist orthodoxy rooted in evangelical piety and community leadership. The family's rural setting amid Georgia's post-Civil War agrarian landscape exposed young Kilpatrick to the rhythms of plantation-era remnants, including agricultural labor and hierarchical social structures, within a context of Confederate heritage and Reconstruction-era tensions that reinforced conservative values like familial duty and religious discipline.
This upbringing, marked by paternal authority and denominational rigorhis father descending from Scottish, Irish, English, and Dutch lineages that had settled in the American Southfostered an initial worldview aligned with traditional Southern Protestantism, though biographical accounts note subtle contrasts arising from his parents' differing temperaments that may have sparked early independent reflection.

Formal Education and Early Influences

Kilpatrick began his higher education in 1888 at Mercer University, his father's alma mater, where he received a Bachelor of Arts degree in mathematics in 1891 and a Master of Arts degree the following year. Following these degrees, he taught mathematics in Georgia public schools and briefly pursued entry into the Baptist ministry, consistent with his upbringing as the son of a Baptist minister in rural Georgia. 
An early intellectual influence came from the writings of George Washington Cable, a Southern author advocating racial equality and social reform, which contributed to Kilpatrick's gradual shift from religious pursuits toward educational and societal improvement. In 1898, seeking further academic development, Kilpatrick enrolled in a summer session at the University of Chicago, studying under John Dewey and encountering pragmatist ideas that would shape his later thinking.
After this exposure, Kilpatrick undertook additional graduate work in mathematics at Johns Hopkins University before returning to formal doctoral studies. He earned his Ph.D. from Columbia University in 1912, supervised by education historian Paul Monroe, completing requirements that solidified his transition to professional scholarship in education.

Professional Career

Initial Teaching Roles

Kilpatrick commenced his professional career in education shortly after graduating from Mercer University in Macon, Georgia, in 1891, securing his initial position in July 1892 as a mathematics instructor at Blakely Institute, a public school encompassing elementary and secondary levels in southwest Georgia. Within his first year, he advanced to the role of principalor co-principaloverseeing the institution's operations and curriculum. This tenure, spanning approximately 1892 to 1895, marked his early immersion in administrative duties within a rural Southern context characterized by limited resources and entrenched traditional practices.
In this capacity, Kilpatrick experimented with nascent child-centered reforms, abolishing corporal punishmentdeclaring he "never whipped one" pupiland eliminating conventional report cards, substituting them instead with verbal evaluations to foster cooperation rather than antagonism between students and educators. These measures, intended to promote intrinsic motivation and relational discipline, provoked significant local opposition from parents and community members accustomed to disciplinary rigor and quantifiable assessments, resulting in controversies that highlighted tensions between innovative pedagogy and prevailing expectations. Despite the backlash, the experience underscored for Kilpatrick the practical hurdles of implementing pupil autonomy in resource-constrained environments, including those shaped by Southern socioeconomic divides.
Following his time at Blakely, Kilpatrick continued in Georgia's public schools through 1897, holding teaching and administrative posts that further tested progressive leanings against institutional norms. In 1897, he returned to Mercer University as a professor of mathematics, where adjunct responsibilities allowed him to refine instructional methods amid academic settings, though still confronting resistance to deviations from rote learning and hierarchical authority. These formative roles exposed him to the rigidities of segregated Southern education systems, including class-based access disparities, which later influenced his broader critiques of inequitable schooling structures.

Leadership at Teachers College, Columbia University

Kilpatrick joined Teachers College, Columbia University, in 1909 as a lecturer in education, advancing to assistant professor from 1911 to 1915, associate professor from 1915 to 1918, and full professor of the philosophy of education thereafter until his retirement in 1937. As a student and close collaborator of John Dewey, who had been a foundational figure at the institution, Kilpatrick assumed a leading interpretive role in progressive educational thought following Dewey's primary focus shifting toward Columbia's philosophy department. His tenure marked a period of expanding influence for Teachers College amid national debates on child-centered pedagogy versus traditional instruction.
Kilpatrick's teaching reached an estimated 35,000 students over nearly three decades, with his final class in 1937 enrolling 622 individuals, many of whom advanced to prominent positions in education administration and reform. Known as the "million dollar professor" for the revenue generated from public admission to his lectures, he contributed institutionally by drawing widespread attendance and enhancing Teachers College's visibility as a hub for progressive ideas. Through large seminars and mentorship, Kilpatrick cultivated networks of educators who disseminated Deweyan principles, fostering collaborations that shaped curriculum development and teacher training programs.
In 1918, Kilpatrick published "The Project Method" as a contribution to the Seventeenth Yearbook of the National Society for the Study of Education, an address that solidified his stature and influenced institutional priorities toward experiential learning frameworks at Teachers College. His administrative involvement, though primarily through professorial leadership rather than formal executive positions, supported the college's growth in enrollment and reputation during the interwar years, even as progressive methods faced scrutiny from traditionalists.

Post-Academia Activities and Retirement

Following his retirement from Teachers College in 1937, Kilpatrick maintained an active role in educational and civic organizations. He served as president of the New York Urban League from 1941 to 1951, focusing on interracial cooperation and community development initiatives. He also chaired American Youth for World Youth from 1946 to 1951, promoting international understanding through youth exchanges and programs. Additionally, Kilpatrick led efforts within the Progressive Education Association and the World Federation of Education Associations, advocating for experiential learning reforms on a global scale.
Kilpatrick continued lecturing at various universities and contributing writings on educational philosophy into his later decades, including reflections at age 85 in 1956 where he reaffirmed his commitment to progressive ideals despite evolving debates. In reassessing his seminal 1918 "Project Method," he later acknowledged a strategic error in presenting it primarily as a philosophical framework rather than a concrete instructional technique, stating after 1927 that he had "made a mistake to marry program to the term" and subsequently shifted to emphasizing "activities" in student learning.
Kilpatrick died on February 13, 1965, at St. Luke's Hospital in New York City after a prolonged illness, at the age of 93. Contemporary obituaries recognized him as a pivotal figure in progressive education and a close associate of John Dewey, underscoring his enduring influence on pedagogical thought.

Educational Philosophy and Methods

Core Principles of Progressive Education

Kilpatrick maintained that education should be regarded as life itself, supplanting the conventional notion of schooling as mere preparation for subsequent existence. This principle underscored a shift toward integrating learning seamlessly into the ongoing experiences of the individual, prioritizing immediate, purposeful engagement over deferred utility.
Central to his framework was the repudiation of rote memorization and imposed curricula, which he viewed as disconnected from natural human impulses and ineffective for genuine comprehension. Instead, Kilpatrick advocated child-driven experiential processes, rooted in pragmatist philosophy, wherein knowledge emerges from active, interest-guided interactions with the environment rather than passive absorption of abstract content. These approaches drew empirical support from observations of child psychology, emphasizing how intrinsic motivations foster deeper retention and adaptive skills compared to externally enforced drills.
Kilpatrick's principles further stressed education's role in cultivating social cooperation and critical thinking, equipping individuals for participation in democratic societies through collaborative problem-solving and ethical reflection. By centering the child's intentions while positioning educators as facilitators rather than directors, this orientation aimed to build character and civic competence attuned to real-world interdependencies, grounded in the causal dynamics of social interaction over isolated instruction.

Development and Definition of the Project Method

Kilpatrick introduced the Project Method in his September 1918 article published in Teachers College Record, framing it as a response to limitations in traditional recitation-based instruction and drawing from emerging practices in vocational education, where hands-on, practical tasks had gained traction since around 1916. He defined a project as "wholehearted purposeful activity in a social situation," emphasizing learner-driven initiatives over imposed lessons to foster intrinsic motivation and real-world relevance. This approach originated partly from vocational training models, which prioritized applied skills like manual production or problem resolution in contextual settings, adapting them for broader school curricula to replace fragmented subject silos with integrated, experiential units.
Central to the method were four sequential phases: purposing, where the learner identifies and commits to a goal; planning, involving collaborative outlining of steps; executing, the active implementation; and judging or appraising outcomes against the original purpose. Kilpatrick outlined four project types to classify applications: problem-solving projects, focused on intellectual inquiry (e.g., investigating natural phenomena); exchange-of-goods projects, centered on economic transactions (e.g., producing and selling items); appreciation projects, aimed at aesthetic or cultural enjoyment (e.g., studying art or music); and manual projects, emphasizing skill-building (e.g., crafting tools or structures). These categories enabled holistic curriculum design, where subjects like mathematics or history emerged organically within the project rather than as discrete drills, marking a pivot toward child-centered education that prioritized student interest and social cooperation.
The method received prompt uptake as a foundational element of progressive pedagogy in the United States, influencing curriculum reforms by promoting projects as unifying curricular structures that integrated knowledge across disciplines. Internationally, it spread to Germany through translations of Kilpatrick's work alongside John Dewey's essays, culminating in a 1931 German publication that introduced the approach amid post-World War I educational exchanges, despite its declining prominence in America by the 1930s. This adaptation positioned the Project Method as a tool for fostering democratic participation via purposeful group endeavors, though its vocational roots underscored an emphasis on practical competencies over abstract theory.

Alignment and Divergences with John Dewey

William Heard Kilpatrick, having studied under John Dewey at Teachers College, Columbia University from 1907 onward, closely aligned with Dewey's pragmatist rejection of traditional, rote-based education in favor of learning through active experience. Both viewed education as a process of democratic socialization, where students engage in real-world problems to develop habits of inquiry and cooperation, as articulated in Dewey's Democracy and Education (1916), which Kilpatrick explicitly drew upon in promoting child-centered curricula. This shared anti-traditionalism emphasized growth via purposeful activity over passive absorption of facts, positioning education as instrumental to societal reconstruction.
Kilpatrick extended Dewey's experiential framework through his 1918 formulation of the "project method," which operationalized learning around students' self-initiated purposes, involving stages of purposing, planning, executing, and appraising outcomes. However, divergences emerged in emphasis: Dewey prioritized structured reflective thinking and collaborative scientific inquiry within social contexts, cautioning against unchecked individualism that could devolve into aimless pursuits, as he critiqued in Experience and Education (1938). In contrast, Kilpatrick's approach placed greater weight on subjective, interest-driven individual projects, interpreting Dewey's ideas through a lens of personal philosophy that sometimes subordinated objective methodology to the child's immediate motivations.
As Dewey's colleague and informal successor at Teachers Collegewhere Kilpatrick taught from 1911 to 1937 and instructed over 35,000 students in philosophy of educationhe amplified Deweyan progressive ideals but shaped their dissemination toward more autonomous, project-centric practices. Dewey acknowledged Kilpatrick's interpretive role yet distanced himself from interpretations that overly fragmented learning from communal and empirical rigor, highlighting a tension between Kilpatrick's motivational focus and Dewey's insistence on disciplined problem-solving.

Political and Social Perspectives

Commitment to Democratic Values

Kilpatrick regarded education as indispensable for the perpetuation of democracy, insisting that schools function as microcosms of democratic society to instill participatory habits in students. He contended that traditional authoritarian classroom hierarchies perpetuated subservience incompatible with self-governing citizenship, advocating instead for cooperative ventures where learners collectively pursue shared purposes. This perspective, articulated in his writings, positioned education not merely as knowledge transmission but as deliberate practice in mutual deliberation and compromise, essential for countering dictatorial tendencies in both pedagogy and polity.
Central to this commitment was Kilpatrick's promotion of teacher-student collaboration, where educators serve as facilitators rather than directors, enabling curriculum adaptation to genuine student interests and societal demands. In Group Education for a Democracy (1940), he outlined how group-based activities foster the interdependence and voluntary association required for democratic cohesion, drawing causal connections between such experiential freedoms and the resilience of free societies against coercion. This framework echoed broader progressive tenets, emphasizing that unconstrained individual agency in learning environments cultivates the initiative vital for informed civic engagement.
Kilpatrick's advocacy extended to flexible schooling structures that prioritize problem-solving over prescribed content, arguing that rigid standardization undermines the adaptive capacities democracy demands in a changing world. He tied these principles to American ideals of liberty and experimentation, warning that educational regimentation erodes the personal responsibility underpinning pluralistic governance. Through lectures and publications, such as those from his tenure at Teachers College, he urged educators to embody democratic ethos in daily practice, thereby forging causal pathways from classroom autonomy to national vitality.

Positions on Race, Pluralism, and Social Reform

Kilpatrick, born in Georgia in 1871 and raised in the post-Reconstruction South, developed views on race that diverged from prevailing segregationist norms, emphasizing education's role in fostering mutual understanding among ethnic groups. Influenced by New South liberalism, he prioritized improving opportunities for Black education even within existing segregated frameworks as a pragmatic step, arguing in 1935 that court actions to equalize Black schools should be a "conditional alternative" pursued only after voluntary reforms failed. By the early 1930s, he explicitly rejected racial segregation when it stemmed from economic and social discrimination, deeming it incompatible with equitable education.
In his advocacy for pluralism, Kilpatrick promoted "cultural democracy" through intercultural education, viewing the recognition of ethnic differenceswithout mandating separate institutional treatmentas essential for democratic cohesion. He chaired the Service Bureau for Intercultural Education until 1951, supporting initiatives that integrated lessons on ethnic relations into curricula to counteract prejudice and promote social harmony. His 1930 essay "Our Educational Task As Illustrated in the Changing South" highlighted cultural rather than strictly racial divides, urging educators to address them via experiential learning to build pluralistic societies.
Kilpatrick critiqued segregation's educational harms in light of progressive theory, asserting in 1956 that it inherently fostered inequality by denying interactive learning environments necessary for democratic development. As an officer in the New York Urban League from its early years through the 1960s, he favored gradualist strategies akin to the League's over aggressive legal confrontations, such as those by the NAACP, prioritizing voluntary social planning for reform. In 1940, he framed such efforts as akin to foundational American democratic experiments, underscoring pluralism's role in averting social conflict without endorsing coercive desegregation mandates.

Criticisms, Controversies, and Empirical Assessments

Early Practical Controversies in Implementation

During his three-year tenure as principal of Anderson Elementary School in Savannah, Georgia, beginning in 1896, Kilpatrick introduced progressive reforms that eliminated traditional pupil punishment and report cards, aiming to foster self-discipline and intrinsic motivation among students. These measures, drawn from emerging child-centered philosophies, elicited immediate backlash from parents and community members, who argued that the absence of formal discipline eroded teacher authority and invited disorder in the classroom, while the lack of graded reports hindered parental oversight of academic performance.
Local educators and school board officials in the conservative Southern context viewed Kilpatrick's approach as impractical for the resource-strapped public schools of the era, where classrooms often included students from varied socioeconomic backgrounds with limited access to materials for self-directed activities. Critics contended that without structured grading or punitive measures, maintaining order in such heterogeneous groups proved challenging, leading to professional disputes that highlighted clashes between innovative experimentation and entrenched expectations for hierarchical control and measurable outcomes.
These early implementations underscored tensions in applying untested methods amid fiscal constraints, as Savannah's public schools operated with minimal funding for supplementary resources, prompting pushback from administrators who prioritized uniformity over individualized projects. The controversies contributed to Kilpatrick's departure from the principalship after three years, reflecting broader resistance to deviations from recitation-based instruction prevalent in late-19th-century Georgia education.

Theoretical Critiques and Accusations of Misapplication

Critics contended that Kilpatrick's interpretation of John Dewey's ideas veered toward an undue emphasis on subjective, child-driven "wholehearted purposeful activity," thereby distorting Dewey's more balanced integration of experience with reflective inquiry and structured subject matter. Dewey himself expressed reservations, observing that a genuine purpose originates from impulse yet requires teacher guidance and cognitive deliberation to avoid superficiality, rather than Kilpatrick's portrayal of projects as largely autonomous endeavors. This shift, detractors argued, subordinated intellectual rigor to emotional engagement, fostering an anti-intellectual ethos that prioritized process over substantive knowledge acquisition.
Traditionalist educators further charged that Kilpatrick's project method exemplified a relativistic undervaluing of canonical content, where curriculum validity hinged on transient student interests rather than enduring disciplinary standards, potentially eroding objective benchmarks for competence. Such approaches, they maintained, echoed broader progressive tendencies toward sentimentalism and vagueness, as Kilpatrick himself later acknowledged risks of "deadness and dullness" yielding to unchecked emotionalism without firm intellectual anchors. In contrast to more structured alternatives like Montessori's prepared environments, which retained sequenced materials for skill-building, Kilpatrick's framework was seen as overly permissive, inviting misapplications that neglected systematic progression in foundational disciplines.
From a causal standpoint grounded in cognitive principles, opponents asserted that Kilpatrick's child-centered projects overlooked the mechanistic demands of learning, wherein novices confront high intrinsic cognitive loads that preclude effective discovery without prior explicit scaffolding and direct modeling of procedures. This reasoning posits that unguided purposeful acts fail to reliably forge neural pathways for abstract reasoning or procedural fluency, as human cognition evolves through hierarchical dependencies rather than isolated experiential pursuits, rendering pure project reliance causally inadequate for robust skill formation. Kilpatrick eventually conceded limitations in his 1918 formulation, reflecting in the late 1920s that he had erred by framing projects more as subjective philosophy than precise instructional technique, which invited interpretive excesses.

Long-Term Effects on Academic Standards and Student Outcomes

Kilpatrick's project method, emphasizing student-driven inquiry over structured content delivery, contributed to a broader shift in American education toward progressive practices that deprioritized rote mastery of facts and skills. Longitudinal data from the National Assessment of Educational Progress (NAEP) reveal stagnation or declines in core academic proficiency since the method's influence peaked in the mid-20th century; for instance, average mathematics scores for 17-year-olds remained flat from 1973 to 2012 before dropping further, with 2022 long-term trend results showing a 7-point decline in math for age-9 students compared to 2020, marking the largest drop in decades. Similarly, reading scores for the same cohort fell 5 points over the same period, reflecting persistent gaps in foundational literacy amid widespread adoption of child-centered approaches.
Empirical comparisons of project-based learning (PBL), a direct extension of Kilpatrick's framework, against traditional instruction yield mixed results on standardized achievement, with meta-analyses indicating short-term gains in motivation and soft skills but limited or negligible long-term advantages in core subjects like math and reading. A 2021 dissertation analyzing PBL versus non-PBL modalities found equivalent outcomes in student achievement, suggesting no causal superiority for progressive methods in sustaining rigorous standards. E.D. Hirsch Jr., critiquing progressive education's anti-intellectual bias toward process over content, argued that such approaches foster knowledge deficits, as evidenced by lower reading comprehension on unfamiliar topics due to inadequate background facts; his analysis links this to broader U.S. achievement gaps, where students in project-heavy systems exhibit shallower factual recall compared to those in knowledge-explicit curricula.
In systems heavily influenced by progressive legacies, such as U.S. public schools emphasizing experiential projects, observable declines in literacy and numeracy align with reduced emphasis on drill and sequential knowledge-building. Recent NAEP data from 2024 indicate high school seniors' math scores at their lowest since 2005 and reading at two-decade lows, coinciding with curricula prioritizing student interest over systematic instructiona pattern Hirsch attributes to romantic progressive tenets that undervalue explicit teaching of domain-specific facts. While some PBL implementations boost immediate engagement and self-efficacy, as noted in qualitative studies, these affective gains rarely translate to superior long-term outcomes in verifiable metrics like international assessments, where traditional, content-focused nations outperform progressive ones. Critics from institutions like the Hoover Institution highlight that progressive educators' reluctance to prioritize empirical results over ideological commitments exacerbates these shortfalls, perpetuating lower standards without causal evidence of broad success.

Legacy and Broader Impact

Influence on Subsequent Educational Reforms

Kilpatrick's Project Method, introduced in 1918, provided a foundational framework for child-centered curriculum innovations in the mid-20th century United States, particularly influencing the open classroom movement of the 1960s and 1970s, which emphasized flexible learning environments and student-driven activities over rigid structures. This approach adapted Kilpatrick's emphasis on purposeful, interest-based projects to promote exploratory learning in multi-age groupings and integrated studies, as seen in experimental schools like those affiliated with the Bank Street College of Education.
The method's core principles of student-initiated, real-world tasks evolved into modern project-based learning (PBL) and inquiry-based pedagogies by the late 20th and early 21st centuries, with direct lineages traced in educational literature as a resurgence of Kilpatrick's ideas under structured implementations. PBL curricula, adopted in various U.S. districts and international programs, incorporate Kilpatrick's typology of projectsranging from problem-solving to appreciative endeavorsoften integrated with standards-aligned assessments, as evidenced in frameworks promoted by organizations like the Buck Institute for Education since the 1990s.
Internationally, Kilpatrick's ideas reached Germany through educational exchanges in the 1920s, including Peter Petersen's 1928 meeting with Kilpatrick at Teachers College and subsequent lectures, leading to the 1935 publication of Der Projekt-Plan, which compiled Kilpatrick's and Dewey's essays despite the Nazi regime's ideological constraints. Petersen adapted the method into the Jena Plan schools, emphasizing group work and child-centered projects, which persisted in limited forms during the Nazi era and contributed to post-World War II democratic reforms in West German pedagogy.
In teacher training, Kilpatrick's tenure at Teachers College from 1909 to 1938 shaped pedagogical practices for over 35,000 educators, embedding project-oriented methods into curricula that prioritized experiential over rote learning, influencing policy debates on balancing creativity with standardized accountability into the 21st century. Despite shifts toward measurable outcomes via policies like the No Child Left Behind Act of 2001, elements of Kilpatrick's approach endure in progressive enclaves, such as charter networks and alternative certification programs favoring inquiry-driven instruction.

Evaluations of Achievements Versus Shortcomings

Kilpatrick's project method, emphasizing purposeful, child-centered activities, demonstrated potential benefits in fostering adaptability and democratic participation among motivated students in controlled settings. Meta-analyses of project-based learning, a direct descendant of Kilpatrick's approach, indicate medium to large positive effects on academic achievement, collaboration, critical thinking, and affective attitudes compared to traditional instruction, particularly when implementation includes clear structure and teacher guidance. These outcomes align with Kilpatrick's aim to cultivate habits of inquiry and social cooperation, as evidenced in early applications where engaged learners showed improved problem-solving in vocational and experiential tasks.
However, shortcomings in the method's vagueness and lack of prescriptive rigor contributed to inconsistent implementation and a shift toward relativism in educational priorities, prioritizing subjective "wholehearted activity" over systematic knowledge acquisition. Kilpatrick himself later acknowledged a mistake in framing the project as a philosophical orientation rather than a concrete instructional technique, admitting this led to misinterpretations and impracticality in classrooms. Critics, including conservative commentators, argue this approach normalized anti-intellectual tendencies by de-emphasizing disciplined content mastery in favor of experiential pluralism, correlating with post-World War II declines in standardized academic performance as progressive methods proliferated without empirical safeguards. Such evaluations highlight systemic biases in academia toward glorifying progressive ideals, often overlooking causal links to eroded standards evident in longitudinal data on literacy and numeracy gaps.
Empirically, while short-term gains in motivation and skills are verifiable for select demographics, broader adoption yielded mixed long-term impacts, with insufficient evidence of sustained improvements in core competencies amid rising relativism in curricular goals. This legacy underscores the need for verifiable metrics prioritizing causal efficacysuch as controlled trials on knowledge retentionover anecdotal endorsements of "life adjustment," revealing a net contribution to educational flexibility at the expense of foundational rigor.
