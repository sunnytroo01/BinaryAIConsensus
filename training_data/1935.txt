 1935 was a year defined by authoritarian consolidations, aggressive expansions, economic interventions amid depression, and environmental catastrophes that exacerbated human suffering. In Nazi Germany, the Nuremberg Laws were promulgated on September 15, stripping Jews of citizenship and prohibiting marriages between Jews and those of "German or related blood," thereby codifying racial hierarchy into law. In Europe and Africa, Fascist Italy invaded Ethiopia on October 3 under Benito Mussolini, using modern warfare including chemical weapons against a League of Nations member, exposing the organization's inability to enforce collective security. In the United States, President Franklin D. Roosevelt signed the Social Security Act on August 14, establishing federal old-age benefits, unemployment insurance, and aid for dependents, funded by payroll taxes as a response to the Great Depression's hardships. The Dust Bowl intensified with the "Black Sunday" storm on April 14, where winds exceeding 60 miles per hour lofted millions of tons of topsoil across the Great Plains, displacing farmers and contributing to widespread migration. In China, the Chinese Communist Red Army completed the Long March by October, a grueling 6,000-mile retreat that preserved the movement and elevated Mao Zedong's leadership through survival against Nationalist encirclement. These events underscored causal chains of policy failures, ideological extremisms, and ecological mismanagement that propelled global instability toward war.

Historical Context

Economic and Social Conditions

In 1935, the global economy exhibited uneven recovery from the Great Depression, with industrial production and GDP beginning to rebound in some regions amid persistent high unemployment and deflationary pressures. In the United States, real gross domestic product grew by 8.9 percent, reflecting modest expansion driven by New Deal spending, though the unemployment rate remained at 20.1 percent, affecting millions of workers. The Supreme Court's invalidation of the National Recovery Administration in May prompted the shift to the Second New Deal, including the Works Progress Administration established in 1935 to provide jobs through public works projects.
Agricultural distress intensified economic hardships, particularly in the U.S. Great Plains during the Dust Bowl era, where prolonged drought and poor farming practices led to severe soil erosion and dust storms that buried farms and homes. A notable "black blizzard" struck Texas on April 14, devastating crops and forcing mass migrations westward, with an estimated 300,000 "Okies" displaced by mid-decade; between 1930 and 1935, nearly 750,000 family farms failed through foreclosure or bankruptcy. The Social Security Act, signed on August 14, 1935, introduced federal old-age pensions, unemployment insurance, and aid for dependents, aiming to provide a safety net against such vulnerabilities, though initial coverage excluded agricultural and domestic workers comprising half the workforce.
Social conditions reflected deepening divisions, with racial and ethnic tensions exacerbated by economic strain. In the U.S., the Harlem riot of March 1920 erupted over rumors of police brutality against a Black youth, resulting in three deaths, over 100 injuries, and widespread property damage, underscoring urban poverty and discrimination in Northern cities. In Germany, Nazi policies reduced unemployment from 34 percent in 1933 to near full employment by 1936 via deficit-financed public works and rearmament, but this recovery suppressed real wages, dismantled unions, and prioritized autarky over sustainable growth. The Nuremberg Laws promulgated on September 15, 1935, legally segregated Jews as racial inferiors, stripping citizenship from those with three or more Jewish grandparents and prohibiting intermarriages, institutionalizing antisemitism amid the regime's economic mobilization.

Geopolitical Tensions

On March 16, 1935, Adolf Hitler publicly announced the reintroduction of conscription in Germany, expanding the army to 36 divisions totaling approximately 550,000 men, and revealed the existence of the Luftwaffe, directly violating the military restrictions imposed by the Treaty of Versailles. This move, framed by German officials as defensive necessity, prompted international condemnation from the League of Nations but elicited no military response from Britain or France, highlighting the weakness of collective security mechanisms.
In response to Germany's actions, Britain, France, and Italy formed the Stresa Front on April 14, 1935, pledging mutual consultation to resist further unilateral changes to the post-World War I European order and uphold the Treaty of Versailles. The agreement, reached during a conference in Stresa, Italy, aimed to deter German expansionism, though underlying divergencesparticularly Italy's imperial ambitionsundermined its cohesion. Tensions persisted as Germany continued covert rearmament efforts, including naval buildup, which culminated in the Anglo-German Naval Agreement of June 18, 1935, allowing Germany to expand its fleet to 35% of British tonnage and effectively sidelining the Stresa pact.
Parallel to European developments, Italian-Ethiopian border clashes, stemming from the Walwal incident of December 5, 1934, escalated into a full crisis, with Italy massing over 500,000 troops in Eritrea and Somaliland by mid-1935 under Benito Mussolini's directive to conquer Ethiopia for resources and prestige. Ethiopia appealed to the League of Nations on January 3, 1935, for arbitration, but Italian demands for territorial concessions and economic dominance went unmet, fostering perceptions of League impotence. These frictions divided potential allies, as France sought to appease Italy with colonial offers in exchange for support against Germany, further straining the Stresa Front.
In Asia, Japan pressured China into the He-Umezu Agreement on June 10, 1935, compelling the Nationalist government to disband anti-Japanese organizations in northern provinces like Hebei and Chahar, effectively ceding influence to Japanese forces and paving the way for further encroachments toward the Second Sino-Japanese War. This unequal treaty underscored Japan's rejection of the Washington Naval Treaty framework and heightened regional instability, though it received less immediate global attention than European and African flashpoints.
The United States, wary of entanglement amid these rising threats, enacted the Neutrality Act on August 31, 1935, prohibiting arms sales and loans to belligerents to avoid repeating World War I experiences, reflecting isolationist sentiment in the face of unchecked aggression abroad. Overall, 1935 marked a year of eroding diplomatic restraints, with authoritarian regimes testing international norms through remilitarization and territorial ambitions, setting the stage for broader conflict.

Events

January

On January 1, the Kingdom of Italy unified its colonies of Tripolitania and Cyrenaica into the colony of Libya, establishing a single administrative entity under Italian control to consolidate imperial holdings in North Africa.
The trial of Bruno Richard Hauptmann for the 1932 kidnapping and murder of Charles Lindbergh Jr. commenced on January 2 in Flemington, New Jersey, drawing intense national attention amid widespread public outrage over the crime, which had prompted federal legislation like the "Lindbergh Law" criminalizing interstate kidnapping. Hauptmann, a German immigrant carpenter, faced circumstantial evidence including wood matches from his attic linking to the ladder used in the abduction; he maintained his innocence throughout the 32-day proceedings.
On January 7, Italian Prime Minister Benito Mussolini and French Foreign Minister Pierre Laval reached an accord in Rome, with Italy committing to respect French interests in the Mediterranean and Europe while France tacitly endorsed potential Italian expansion in Ethiopia, reflecting mutual colonial ambitions but failing to avert Italy's subsequent invasion later that year.
Aviator Amelia Earhart completed the first solo flight from Honolulu, Hawaii, to Oakland, California, on January 11, covering 2,408 miles in 18 hours and 15 minutes aboard her Lockheed Vega, marking a milestone in trans-Pacific aviation amid ongoing advancements in long-distance flight technology.
The Saarland plebiscite occurred on January 13, supervised by the League of Nations, resulting in 90.8% of voters (477,119 out of 525,063 eligible) opting for reunification with Germany over remaining under League administration or joining France, thereby returning the coal-rich territoryceded after World War Ito the German Reich under Adolf Hitler and signaling early erosion of Versailles Treaty constraints.
Former Bolshevik leaders Grigory Zinoviev and Lev Kamenev went on trial in Moscow starting January 15, charged with conspiring in the 1934 assassination of Sergei Kirov; both pleaded guilty under duress in proceedings widely recognized as orchestrated by Joseph Stalin to eliminate political rivals, confessing to moral complicity despite lacking direct involvement, which foreshadowed broader purges.
Japanese forces launched an offensive into China's Chahar Province on January 23, exploiting tensions with Inner Mongolian allies to expand influence in northern China, prompting the Tanggu Truce's effective collapse and escalating Sino-Japanese hostilities that presaged full-scale war.

February

On February 2, Leonarde Keeler administered the first polygraph test admitted as evidence in a United States court during a murder trial in Portage, Wisconsin, marking an early milestone in forensic science despite ongoing debates over the device's reliability.
On February 12, the U.S. Navy rigid airship USS Macon crashed into the Pacific Ocean off Point Sur, California, during a storm after structural failure in its tail; of the 83 crew aboard, 81 were rescued, but two sailors perished, representing the final loss of a U.S. Navy airship from its interwar fleet.
On February 13, Bruno Hauptmann was convicted of first-degree murder in a New Jersey court for the 1932 kidnapping and death of Charles Lindbergh Jr., the infant son of aviator Charles Lindbergh; the trial, drawing massive public attention, resulted in Hauptmann's death sentence, which was carried out in 1936 amid persistent claims of his innocence by some observers.
On February 26, Adolf Hitler signed a decree formally establishing the Luftwaffe as Germany's independent air force within the Wehrmacht, appointing Hermann Gring as its commander-in-chief; this action violated the Treaty of Versailles' prohibition on German military aviation, signaling accelerated Nazi rearmament and contributing to rising European tensions. The Luftwaffe's creation built on clandestine development since 1933, positioning it as a core element of Germany's military resurgence under the regime's expansionist policies.

March

On March 1, 1935, a group of Venizelist military officers loyal to former Prime Minister Eleftherios Venizelos attempted a coup d'tat against the conservative government of Panagis Tsaldaris in Greece, driven by fears of a monarchical restoration following a referendum. The rebellion, led by Nikolaos Plastiras, initially seized control in parts of northern Greece and Crete but failed to gain traction in Athens or most of the mainland, leading to its swift suppression by loyalist forces under General Georgios Kondylis. Martial law was declared, and by early March, the uprising was quelled, resulting in arrests of rebel officers and civilians; the event deepened political divisions but ultimately facilitated the monarchy's return via plebiscite later in the year.
On March 9, 1935, the German government under Adolf Hitler publicly announced the creation of the Luftwaffe as a new national air force, marking an early step in overt rearmament efforts that defied post-World War I restrictions.
The most significant development occurred on March 16, 1935, when Hitler issued a decree reintroducing compulsory military service in Germany, explicitly violating the military clauses of the 1919 Treaty of Versailles, which had limited the German army to 100,000 volunteers without conscription. The announcement expanded the Wehrmacht to 36 divisions totaling approximately 550,000 men and was accompanied by a public military parade in Berlin to demonstrate resolve. This act of defiance elicited protests from Britain, France, and Italy but no immediate military response, signaling the weakness of the Versailles system and emboldening further Nazi expansions.
In the United States, the Harlem riot erupted on March 19, 1935, in New York City's Harlem neighborhood, sparked by a false rumor that a white store owner had beaten and killed a Black boy who had stolen a knife, amid heightened racial tensions exacerbated by the Great Depression's economic hardships on African American communities. The violence, which lasted into March 20, primarily targeted white-owned businesses perceived as exploitative, resulting in three deaths (two Black assailants shot by police and one Black man killed by a gunshot wound), over 100 injuries, 125 arrests, and an estimated $2 million in property damage concentrated on stores rather than individuals. Investigations later confirmed the boy was alive and uninjured, highlighting how rumor amplified underlying grievances over unemployment, discrimination, and police practices, marking this as an early instance of urban racial unrest directed more at economic symbols than personal violence.

April

On April 8, 1935, U.S. President Franklin D. Roosevelt signed the Emergency Relief Appropriation Act into law, allocating $4 billion for emergency relief and public works projects, which laid the groundwork for the Works Progress Administration (WPA) to combat unemployment during the Great Depression. The legislation authorized employment relief for millions through infrastructure development, arts programs, and other initiatives, marking a significant expansion of federal intervention in the economy.
April 14, 1935, saw one of the most severe dust storms of the Dust Bowl era, known as Black Sunday, sweep across the southern Great Plains. The massive front, originating from the northern Great Plains, carried an estimated 300,000 tons of topsoil, reducing visibility to near zero in areas of Oklahoma, Texas, Colorado, and New Mexico, with winds reaching 60 miles per hour. This event, exacerbated by prolonged drought, overplowing of grasslands, and lack of conservation practices, prompted the U.S. government to establish the Soil Conservation Service later that year to address soil erosion. Eyewitness accounts described the sky darkening midday, leading to traffic accidents, livestock suffocation, and heightened public awareness of environmental degradation in the region.
On April 30, 1935, the Nazi regime in Germany issued a decree prohibiting Jews from displaying or carrying the national flag, further institutionalizing discriminatory policies under the Nuremberg Laws framework. This measure reflected escalating antisemitic legislation aimed at excluding Jews from symbols of national identity.

May

On May 1, 1935, construction of the Boulder Dam (later renamed Hoover Dam) on the Colorado River was completed, marking a major engineering achievement that provided hydroelectric power, flood control, and water storage for the southwestern United States.
On May 6, President Franklin D. Roosevelt signed Executive Order 7034, establishing the Works Progress Administration (WPA) as a key New Deal agency to employ millions in public works projects, infrastructure, and arts programs during the Great Depression. The WPA ultimately employed over 8.5 million people by 1943, focusing on roads, buildings, and cultural initiatives while prioritizing the unskilled and disadvantaged.
Also on May 6, King George V and Queen Mary of the United Kingdom celebrated their Silver Jubilee, commemorating 25 years of reign with nationwide festivities, parades in London attended by over a million people, and messages of loyalty from across the British Empire.
On May 13, British archaeologist and military officer T. E. Lawrence, known for his role in the Arab Revolt during World War I, suffered severe injuries in a motorcycle accident near Bovington Camp in Dorset, England; he succumbed to his wounds on June 19.
The Soviet Union's Tupolev ANT-20 Maxim Gorky, the largest aircraft in the world at the time with a wingspan of 53 meters and capacity for 36 passengers, crashed on May 18 over Moscow after colliding with an escort fighter plane during a demonstration flight, killing all 49 aboard including prominent figures in Soviet aviation and propaganda. The incident highlighted risks in early large-scale air operations and led to investigations into air traffic safety protocols.
On May 24, Major League Baseball played its first nighttime game at Crosley Field in Cincinnati, with the Reds defeating the Philadelphia Phillies 2-1 under newly installed floodlights, an innovation to boost attendance during the Depression.
May 25 featured two athletic milestones: Babe Ruth hit the final three home runs of his career for the Boston Braves against the New York Yankees' pitching staff, concluding his MLB record of 714 home runs; and American track athlete Jesse Owens set three world records and tied a fourth in 45 minutes at the Big Ten Championships in Ann Arbor, Michigan, achievements in the 100-yard dash, long jump, and 220-yard hurdles that foreshadowed his 1936 Olympic dominance.
On May 27, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled in A.L.A. Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States that the National Industrial Recovery Act of 1933 was unconstitutional, invalidating its regulatory codes for exceeding federal commerce powers and dealing a setback to New Deal industrial recovery efforts.

June

On June 1, the United Kingdom implemented compulsory driving tests and vehicle registration plates as part of regulations under the Road Traffic Act 1930, marking a significant step in standardizing road safety amid rising automobile use. This measure required new drivers to pass a practical examination, addressing concerns over inexperienced operators contributing to accidents on increasingly congested roads.
June 2 saw American baseball legend Babe Ruth announce his retirement from professional play at age 40, following a disappointing stint with the Boston Braves; his career included 714 home runs, a record that stood until 1974, and he had played his final game the previous day after hitting three home runs against the New York Yankees. That same day, Uruguayan President Gabriel Terra survived an assassination attempt by a former deputy from an opposing political faction, highlighting internal divisions during his authoritarian rule that had suspended the constitution in 1933.
Severe flooding struck central Mexico on June 3, inundating villages near Mexico City such as San Pedro and Actopan, where at least 150 people drowned amid torrential rains that destroyed homes and infrastructure; estimates of the death toll varied, with some reports citing up to 400 fatalities in the affected regions. Concurrently, the French ocean liner SS Normandie completed its maiden voyage from Le Havre to New York in a record time of four days, three hours, and two minutes, capturing the Blue Riband for the fastest Atlantic crossing and demonstrating advancements in transoceanic travel technology.
In the United States, Alcoholics Anonymous was founded on June 10 in Akron, Ohio, when Bill Wilson, a recovering alcoholic, shared his experiences with Dr. Bob Smith, initiating a mutual-aid fellowship based on spiritual and peer-support principles that grew into a global organization aiding millions in addressing alcohol dependency.
Boxing saw notable upsets in mid-June: on June 13, American James J. Braddock, dubbed the "Cinderella Man" for his comeback from welfare during the Great Depression, defeated world heavyweight champion Max Baer by unanimous decision in a 15-round bout at Long Island City Bowl, New York, reclaiming the title and symbolizing resilience amid economic hardship. On June 25, rising American heavyweight Joe Louis knocked out former champion Primo Carnera in the sixth round in New York, solidifying Louis's dominance and drawing record crowds that underscored boxing's popularity as escapist entertainment during the Depression era.

July

July 5 U.S. President Franklin D. Roosevelt signed the National Labor Relations Act, also known as the Wagner Act, into law, establishing the right of workers to organize unions and engage in collective bargaining without employer interference. The legislation created the National Labor Relations Board to oversee union elections and address unfair labor practices, marking a cornerstone of New Deal labor reforms amid the Great Depression.
July 6 Fred Perry of Britain defeated Gottfried von Cramm of Germany 62, 64, 64 to win the Wimbledon men's singles championship, securing his third consecutive title. In the women's singles, Helen Wills Moody claimed her seventh Wimbledon title by beating Helen Jacobs 62, 61, extending her dominance in the event.
July 7 Severe flooding struck Changde in China's Hunan Province after dikes along the Yuan River collapsed, inundating the city under ten feet of water as part of the broader 1935 Yangtze floods that affected millions across central China. The disaster contributed to widespread devastation, with the overall floods causing an estimated 142,000 deaths and displacing over 12 million people due to prolonged heavy rains and inadequate river controls.
July 12 Nazi authorities in Germany classified Romani people as "asocials," initiating a campaign of arrests and detentions targeting this minority group, which foreshadowed escalated persecution under the regime's racial policies.
July 16 The world's first parking meters were installed on the streets of Oklahoma City, charging drivers five cents per hour to regulate parking in the downtown business district and address traffic congestion.
July 17 Cudjoe Lewis, the last known adult survivor of the transatlantic slave trade, died in Mobile, Alabama, at age 94; born in Benin and transported via the Clotilda in 1860, his passing marked the end of direct eyewitness accounts to that era of forced migration.

August

On August 2, the Government of India Act 1935 received royal assent from King George V, establishing a framework for provincial autonomy in British India while reserving key powers like defense and foreign affairs to the viceroy; the act envisioned a federal structure uniting British Indian provinces with princely states, though full implementation was delayed until 1937 due to political opposition from Indian nationalists.
August 14 saw U.S. President Franklin D. Roosevelt sign the Social Security Act into law, creating a federal system of old-age pensions funded by payroll taxes on workers and employers, alongside provisions for unemployment insurance administered by states and aid to dependent children; the legislation aimed to mitigate poverty among the elderly amid the Great Depression, with initial benefits set to begin in 1940 and covering approximately half the workforce initially excluded agricultural and domestic laborers.
August 15 featured a mass rally by the Nazi Party at Berlin's Sportpalast arena, where speakers including Joseph Goebbels promoted antisemitic ideology under banners declaring "The Jews are our Misfortune," reflecting escalating state-sponsored discrimination against Jews in Germany ahead of the Nuremberg Laws later that year; attendance exceeded 15,000, underscoring the regime's mobilization of public fervor against perceived internal enemies.
The Seventh Congress of the Communist International (Comintern) concluded on August 20 after adopting the Popular Front strategy, directing communist parties worldwide to form alliances with social democrats and liberals against fascism rather than pursuing immediate proletarian revolution; this shift, influenced by Soviet foreign policy under Joseph Stalin, prioritized anti-fascist unity in response to rising threats in Europe and Asia, though critics later argued it subordinated class struggle to geopolitical expediency.
August 30 marked President Roosevelt's signing of both the Revenue Act of 1935, which raised top income tax rates to 75% on incomes over $1 million and introduced a 7% tax on corporate undistributed profits to fund New Deal programs, and the Guffey-Snyder Coal Act (Bituminous Coal Conservation Act), which sought to stabilize the coal industry through price controls, labor standards, and a tax on production to enforce collective bargaining; the latter faced immediate legal challenges for exceeding federal commerce powers, leading to its invalidation by the Supreme Court in 1936.

September

On September 2, the Labor Day Hurricane, a Category 5 storm with sustained winds of 185 mph, made landfall in the Florida Keys, becoming the strongest hurricane on record to strike the United States at the time and causing 423 deaths, primarily among World War I veterans working on construction projects. The storm's rapid intensification and direct hit on low-lying areas like Islamorada led to widespread destruction, including the obliteration of entire settlements and the derailing of rescue trains by storm surge.
On September 8, Louisiana Senator Huey Long, a prominent populist politician known for his Share Our Wealth program, was assassinated in Baton Rouge by Dr. Carl Weiss, who was subsequently killed by Long's bodyguards. Long, who had wielded significant influence in Louisiana politics through patronage and legislative control, died two days later on September 10, sparking investigations into possible broader conspiracies, though Weiss acted primarily due to personal grievances against Long's political machine.
On September 15, during the Nazi Party's annual rally in Nuremberg, the Reichstag enacted the Nuremberg Laws, comprising the Reich Citizenship Law, which stripped Jews of German citizenship and classified them as subjects based on ancestry rather than religion, and the Law for the Protection of German Blood and German Honor, which prohibited marriages and extramarital relations between Jews and Germans of "Aryan" descent. These laws formalized racial discrimination, defining Jews as those with three or more Jewish grandparents, and extended restrictions to partial Jewish ancestry, laying the groundwork for further persecution by providing a legal basis for exclusion from public life. Accompanying the racial laws, the Reich Flag Law designated the swastika-emblazoned flag as Germany's national standard, symbolizing the regime's ideological shift.
Throughout September, the Abyssinia Crisis escalated as Italy prepared for invasion, with Ethiopian Emperor Haile Selassie distributing gas masks to civilians amid fears of chemical warfare, reflecting League of Nations impotence in deterring aggression.

October

On October 3, 1935, Italian forces under General Emilio De Bono launched an invasion of Ethiopia from Eritrea, initiating the Second Italo-Ethiopian War. Benito Mussolini, seeking to expand Italian colonial holdings and avenge the 1896 defeat at Adwa, mobilized approximately 500,000 troops, including modern aircraft and chemical weapons, against Ethiopia's less equipped army led by Emperor Haile Selassie. The invasion disregarded warnings from the League of Nations, highlighting the organization's limited enforcement power.
In response, Ethiopia appealed to the League of Nations on October 5, demanding sanctions against Italy as the aggressor. On October 7, the League Council unanimously declared Italy the aggressor, recommending economic sanctions but excluding key resources like oil and prohibiting military intervention. This marked the first application of Article 16 of the League Covenant but proved ineffective, as major powers like Britain and France prioritized appeasement to avoid broader conflict. Italian advances continued, with forces occupying Adigrat by early October.
Meanwhile, in China, the Chinese Communist Red Army, led by Mao Zedong, concluded its grueling Long March on October 20, arriving in Yan'an, Shaanxi Province, after a 6,000-mile retreat from Nationalist encirclement. Of the original 86,000 participants who began the trek in October 1934, only about 4,000-8,000 survivors remained, solidifying Mao's leadership within the Communist Party. The march evaded Chiang Kai-shek's forces and preserved the Communist movement, setting the stage for future guerrilla warfare.

November

On November 3, a referendum in Greece overwhelmingly approved the restoration of the monarchy, with 97.88% of voters supporting the return of King George II after 12 years of exile following the 1923 abolition; George II arrived in Athens the same day to resume the throne amid political instability after a military coup. Also on November 3, Korean athlete Sohn Kee-chung set a new world record in the marathon at 2:26:42 during the Japanese national championships in Tokyo, though he competed under the imposed Japanese name Kitei Son due to Japan's occupation of Korea.
The United States formally recognized the Commonwealth of the Philippines on November 14, transitioning the territory toward self-governance under the Tydings-McDuffie Act of 1934; Manuel L. Quezon was inaugurated as the first president the following day in Manila, with the U.S. retaining control over foreign affairs and defense.
November 18 marked the implementation of economic sanctions by the League of Nations against Italy in response to its invasion of Ethiopia the previous month, including an embargo on key exports like oil, rubber, and metals, though major powers like the U.S. and Britain participated unevenly, highlighting the League's enforcement weaknesses.
Pan American Airways inaugurated the first regular transpacific airmail service on November 22 when the Martin M-130 flying boat China Clipper, piloted by Edwin C. Musick, departed San Francisco Bay for Manila, covering 7,000 miles with stops in Honolulu, Midway, Wake, and Guam over six days; the flight carried 113 pounds of airmail and symbolized advancing commercial aviation amid technological improvements in long-range seaplanes. The China Clipper arrived in Manila on November 29, completing the route and enabling scheduled mail service that later expanded to passengers.
On November 25, Japanese forces under Prince Demchugdongrub established the puppet state of Mengjiang in northern China, encompassing parts of Inner Mongolia and serving as a buffer against Chinese nationalists; this move escalated Japan's expansionism in the region, backed by military occupation despite nominal independence.
In cultural developments, T.S. Eliot's verse drama Murder in the Cathedral, depicting the assassination of Thomas Becket, premiered on November 15 at Canterbury Cathedral, drawing acclaim for its poetic exploration of martyrdom and drawing large audiences during its initial run. The film Mutiny on the Bounty, starring Clark Gable and Charles Laughton, opened in U.S. theaters on November 8, becoming a box-office success and winning the Academy Award for Best Picture the following year for its dramatization of the 1789 naval revolt.

December

On December 9, Walter Liggett, a Minneapolis-based journalist known for exposing political corruption and organized crime, was assassinated in a drive-by shooting as he returned home with his wife; the attack involved submachine gun fire from a passing vehicle, and investigations pointed to links with local mob figures amid his criticisms of Minnesota Governor Floyd B. Olson. Also on December 9, thousands of students marched in Beijing in the pivotal December 9th Movement, protesting Japanese aggression and the Nationalist government's appeasement policies, a key event in China's resistance history as documented by Western journalist Edgar Snow.
The Nobel Prizes were presented on December 10 in Stockholm and Oslo; in Chemistry, Frdric Joliot-Curie and Irne Joliot-Curie received the award for their synthesis of new radioactive elements, marking the first time a parent-daughter pair won Nobels in the same field following Marie Curie's earlier achievements. The Peace Prize went to Carl von Ossietzky, a German journalist imprisoned by the Nazi regime for revealing rearmament violations, a decision that provoked outrage in Germany where it was viewed as interference in internal affairs.
On December 14, Tom Garrigue Masaryk, founding president of Czechoslovakia since 1918, resigned at age 85 due to deteriorating health, paving the way for Edvard Bene to assume the presidency the following day in a smooth transition reflective of the country's democratic stability amid rising European tensions.
In the ongoing Second Italo-Ethiopian War, Italian dictator Benito Mussolini authorized the use of chemical weapons on December 16, including mustard gas, to counter Ethiopian counteroffensives; this escalation, involving aerial bombings, resulted in thousands of casualties and drew international condemnation despite limited enforcement by the League of Nations, highlighting the body's ineffectiveness in curbing aggression.
On December 26, Tang Yu-jen, China's Administrative Vice Minister for Foreign Affairs, was assassinated in Nanjing shortly after arriving home, prompting the government to declare martial law in Shanghai, Nanjing, and Hankou to suppress potential unrest amid factional rivalries and Japanese pressures in the region.

Scientific and Technological Advances

Key Discoveries and Inventions

In seismology, Charles F. Richter of the California Institute of Technology published an instrumental magnitude scale in January 1935, providing a logarithmic measure of earthquake energy release based on seismograph recordings, initially calibrated for southern California events with adjustments for distance and local geology.
In physics, Japanese theorist Hideki Yukawa proposed a meson-exchange model for the strong nuclear force in a February 1935 paper, predicting a massive particle (later identified as the pion) to mediate interactions between protons and neutrons over short ranges, extending quantum field theory analogies from electromagnetism.
Theoretical quantum mechanics faced challenge from the Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen (EPR) paper published May 15, 1935, in Physical Review, where Albert Einstein, Boris Podolsky, and Nathan Rosen argued via a thought experiment involving entangled particles that quantum mechanics' probabilistic predictions implied incomplete descriptions of physical reality, as simultaneous precise measurements of position and momentum at a distance violated locality without "hidden variables."
In medical chemistry, Gerhard Domagk at IG Farben reported in late 1935 the bactericidal effects of Prontosil (a sulfonamide dye) against streptococcal infections in mice and humans, marking the first chemotherapeutic agent effective against systemic bacterial diseases other than syphilis, paving the way for sulfa drug development despite its later reduction to sulfanilamide in vivo.
A foundational radar experiment occurred on February 26, 1935, when Robert Watson-Watt and Arnold Frederic Wilkins at the Radio Research Station detected echoes from a Handley Page Heyford bomber using radio waves from a BBC transmitter over 8 miles (13 km), demonstrating aircraft detection potential and influencing Britain's Chain Home early-warning system.
In polymer chemistry, DuPont researcher Wallace Hume Carothers and his team synthesized the first sample of nylon 6,6a polyamide from adipic acid and hexamethylenediamineon February 28, 1935, yielding strong, elastic fibers suitable for textiles, though commercial production began in 1939 after process scaling.

March

On March 1, 1935, a group of Venizelist military officers loyal to former Prime Minister Eleftherios Venizelos attempted a coup d'tat against the conservative government of Panagis Tsaldaris in Greece, driven by fears of a monarchical restoration following a referendum. The rebellion, led by Nikolaos Plastiras, initially seized control in parts of northern Greece and Crete but failed to gain traction in Athens or most of the mainland, leading to its swift suppression by loyalist forces under General Georgios Kondylis. Martial law was declared, and by early March, the uprising was quelled, resulting in arrests of rebel officers and civilians; the event deepened political divisions but ultimately facilitated the monarchy's return via plebiscite later in the year.
On March 9, 1935, the German government under Adolf Hitler publicly announced the creation of the Luftwaffe as a new national air force, marking an early step in overt rearmament efforts that defied post-World War I restrictions.
The most significant development occurred on March 16, 1935, when Hitler issued a decree reintroducing compulsory military service in Germany, explicitly violating the military clauses of the 1919 Treaty of Versailles, which had limited the German army to 100,000 volunteers without conscription. The announcement expanded the Wehrmacht to 36 divisions totaling approximately 550,000 men and was accompanied by a public military parade in Berlin to demonstrate resolve. This act of defiance elicited protests from Britain, France, and Italy but no immediate military response, signaling the weakness of the Versailles system and emboldening further Nazi expansions.
In the United States, the Harlem riot erupted on March 19, 1935, in New York City's Harlem neighborhood, sparked by a false rumor that a white store owner had beaten and killed a Black boy who had stolen a knife, amid heightened racial tensions exacerbated by the Great Depression's economic hardships on African American communities. The violence, which lasted into March 20, primarily targeted white-owned businesses perceived as exploitative, resulting in three deaths (two Black assailants shot by police and one Black man killed by a gunshot wound), over 100 injuries, 125 arrests, and an estimated $2 million in property damage concentrated on stores rather than individuals. Investigations later confirmed the boy was alive and uninjured, highlighting how rumor amplified underlying grievances over unemployment, discrimination, and police practices, marking this as an early instance of urban racial unrest directed more at economic symbols than personal violence.

April

On April 8, 1935, U.S. President Franklin D. Roosevelt signed the Emergency Relief Appropriation Act into law, allocating $4 billion for emergency relief and public works projects, which laid the groundwork for the Works Progress Administration (WPA) to combat unemployment during the Great Depression. The legislation authorized employment relief for millions through infrastructure development, arts programs, and other initiatives, marking a significant expansion of federal intervention in the economy.
April 14, 1935, saw one of the most severe dust storms of the Dust Bowl era, known as Black Sunday, sweep across the southern Great Plains. The massive front, originating from the northern Great Plains, carried an estimated 300,000 tons of topsoil, reducing visibility to near zero in areas of Oklahoma, Texas, Colorado, and New Mexico, with winds reaching 60 miles per hour. This event, exacerbated by prolonged drought, overplowing of grasslands, and lack of conservation practices, prompted the U.S. government to establish the Soil Conservation Service later that year to address soil erosion. Eyewitness accounts described the sky darkening midday, leading to traffic accidents, livestock suffocation, and heightened public awareness of environmental degradation in the region.
On April 30, 1935, the Nazi regime in Germany issued a decree prohibiting Jews from displaying or carrying the national flag, further institutionalizing discriminatory policies under the Nuremberg Laws framework. This measure reflected escalating antisemitic legislation aimed at excluding Jews from symbols of national identity.

May

On May 1, 1935, construction of the Boulder Dam (later renamed Hoover Dam) on the Colorado River was completed, marking a major engineering achievement that provided hydroelectric power, flood control, and water storage for the southwestern United States.
On May 6, President Franklin D. Roosevelt signed Executive Order 7034, establishing the Works Progress Administration (WPA) as a key New Deal agency to employ millions in public works projects, infrastructure, and arts programs during the Great Depression. The WPA ultimately employed over 8.5 million people by 1943, focusing on roads, buildings, and cultural initiatives while prioritizing the unskilled and disadvantaged.
Also on May 6, King George V and Queen Mary of the United Kingdom celebrated their Silver Jubilee, commemorating 25 years of reign with nationwide festivities, parades in London attended by over a million people, and messages of loyalty from across the British Empire.
On May 13, British archaeologist and military officer T. E. Lawrence, known for his role in the Arab Revolt during World War I, suffered severe injuries in a motorcycle accident near Bovington Camp in Dorset, England; he succumbed to his wounds on June 19.
The Soviet Union's Tupolev ANT-20 Maxim Gorky, the largest aircraft in the world at the time with a wingspan of 53 meters and capacity for 36 passengers, crashed on May 18 over Moscow after colliding with an escort fighter plane during a demonstration flight, killing all 49 aboard including prominent figures in Soviet aviation and propaganda. The incident highlighted risks in early large-scale air operations and led to investigations into air traffic safety protocols.
On May 24, Major League Baseball played its first nighttime game at Crosley Field in Cincinnati, with the Reds defeating the Philadelphia Phillies 2-1 under newly installed floodlights, an innovation to boost attendance during the Depression.
May 25 featured two athletic milestones: Babe Ruth hit the final three home runs of his career for the Boston Braves against the New York Yankees' pitching staff, concluding his MLB record of 714 home runs; and American track athlete Jesse Owens set three world records and tied a fourth in 45 minutes at the Big Ten Championships in Ann Arbor, Michigan, achievements in the 100-yard dash, long jump, and 220-yard hurdles that foreshadowed his 1936 Olympic dominance.
On May 27, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled in A.L.A. Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States that the National Industrial Recovery Act of 1933 was unconstitutional, invalidating its regulatory codes for exceeding federal commerce powers and dealing a setback to New Deal industrial recovery efforts.

June

On June 1, the United Kingdom implemented compulsory driving tests and vehicle registration plates as part of regulations under the Road Traffic Act 1930, marking a significant step in standardizing road safety amid rising automobile use. This measure required new drivers to pass a practical examination, addressing concerns over inexperienced operators contributing to accidents on increasingly congested roads.
June 2 saw American baseball legend Babe Ruth announce his retirement from professional play at age 40, following a disappointing stint with the Boston Braves; his career included 714 home runs, a record that stood until 1974, and he had played his final game the previous day after hitting three home runs against the New York Yankees. That same day, Uruguayan President Gabriel Terra survived an assassination attempt by a former deputy from an opposing political faction, highlighting internal divisions during his authoritarian rule that had suspended the constitution in 1933.
Severe flooding struck central Mexico on June 3, inundating villages near Mexico City such as San Pedro and Actopan, where at least 150 people drowned amid torrential rains that destroyed homes and infrastructure; estimates of the death toll varied, with some reports citing up to 400 fatalities in the affected regions. Concurrently, the French ocean liner SS Normandie completed its maiden voyage from Le Havre to New York in a record time of four days, three hours, and two minutes, capturing the Blue Riband for the fastest Atlantic crossing and demonstrating advancements in transoceanic travel technology.
In the United States, Alcoholics Anonymous was founded on June 10 in Akron, Ohio, when Bill Wilson, a recovering alcoholic, shared his experiences with Dr. Bob Smith, initiating a mutual-aid fellowship based on spiritual and peer-support principles that grew into a global organization aiding millions in addressing alcohol dependency.
Boxing saw notable upsets in mid-June: on June 13, American James J. Braddock, dubbed the "Cinderella Man" for his comeback from welfare during the Great Depression, defeated world heavyweight champion Max Baer by unanimous decision in a 15-round bout at Long Island City Bowl, New York, reclaiming the title and symbolizing resilience amid economic hardship. On June 25, rising American heavyweight Joe Louis knocked out former champion Primo Carnera in the sixth round in New York, solidifying Louis's dominance and drawing record crowds that underscored boxing's popularity as escapist entertainment during the Depression era.

July

July 5 U.S. President Franklin D. Roosevelt signed the National Labor Relations Act, also known as the Wagner Act, into law, establishing the right of workers to organize unions and engage in collective bargaining without employer interference. The legislation created the National Labor Relations Board to oversee union elections and address unfair labor practices, marking a cornerstone of New Deal labor reforms amid the Great Depression.
July 6 Fred Perry of Britain defeated Gottfried von Cramm of Germany 62, 64, 64 to win the Wimbledon men's singles championship, securing his third consecutive title. In the women's singles, Helen Wills Moody claimed her seventh Wimbledon title by beating Helen Jacobs 62, 61, extending her dominance in the event.
July 7 Severe flooding struck Changde in China's Hunan Province after dikes along the Yuan River collapsed, inundating the city under ten feet of water as part of the broader 1935 Yangtze floods that affected millions across central China. The disaster contributed to widespread devastation, with the overall floods causing an estimated 142,000 deaths and displacing over 12 million people due to prolonged heavy rains and inadequate river controls.
July 12 Nazi authorities in Germany classified Romani people as "asocials," initiating a campaign of arrests and detentions targeting this minority group, which foreshadowed escalated persecution under the regime's racial policies.
July 16 The world's first parking meters were installed on the streets of Oklahoma City, charging drivers five cents per hour to regulate parking in the downtown business district and address traffic congestion.
July 17 Cudjoe Lewis, the last known adult survivor of the transatlantic slave trade, died in Mobile, Alabama, at age 94; born in Benin and transported via the Clotilda in 1860, his passing marked the end of direct eyewitness accounts to that era of forced migration.

August

On August 2, the Government of India Act 1935 received royal assent from King George V, establishing a framework for provincial autonomy in British India while reserving key powers like defense and foreign affairs to the viceroy; the act envisioned a federal structure uniting British Indian provinces with princely states, though full implementation was delayed until 1937 due to political opposition from Indian nationalists.
August 14 saw U.S. President Franklin D. Roosevelt sign the Social Security Act into law, creating a federal system of old-age pensions funded by payroll taxes on workers and employers, alongside provisions for unemployment insurance administered by states and aid to dependent children; the legislation aimed to mitigate poverty among the elderly amid the Great Depression, with initial benefits set to begin in 1940 and covering approximately half the workforce initially excluded agricultural and domestic laborers.
August 15 featured a mass rally by the Nazi Party at Berlin's Sportpalast arena, where speakers including Joseph Goebbels promoted antisemitic ideology under banners declaring "The Jews are our Misfortune," reflecting escalating state-sponsored discrimination against Jews in Germany ahead of the Nuremberg Laws later that year; attendance exceeded 15,000, underscoring the regime's mobilization of public fervor against perceived internal enemies.
The Seventh Congress of the Communist International (Comintern) concluded on August 20 after adopting the Popular Front strategy, directing communist parties worldwide to form alliances with social democrats and liberals against fascism rather than pursuing immediate proletarian revolution; this shift, influenced by Soviet foreign policy under Joseph Stalin, prioritized anti-fascist unity in response to rising threats in Europe and Asia, though critics later argued it subordinated class struggle to geopolitical expediency.
August 30 marked President Roosevelt's signing of both the Revenue Act of 1935, which raised top income tax rates to 75% on incomes over $1 million and introduced a 7% tax on corporate undistributed profits to fund New Deal programs, and the Guffey-Snyder Coal Act (Bituminous Coal Conservation Act), which sought to stabilize the coal industry through price controls, labor standards, and a tax on production to enforce collective bargaining; the latter faced immediate legal challenges for exceeding federal commerce powers, leading to its invalidation by the Supreme Court in 1936.

September

On September 2, the Labor Day Hurricane, a Category 5 storm with sustained winds of 185 mph, made landfall in the Florida Keys, becoming the strongest hurricane on record to strike the United States at the time and causing 423 deaths, primarily among World War I veterans working on construction projects. The storm's rapid intensification and direct hit on low-lying areas like Islamorada led to widespread destruction, including the obliteration of entire settlements and the derailing of rescue trains by storm surge.
On September 8, Louisiana Senator Huey Long, a prominent populist politician known for his Share Our Wealth program, was assassinated in Baton Rouge by Dr. Carl Weiss, who was subsequently killed by Long's bodyguards. Long, who had wielded significant influence in Louisiana politics through patronage and legislative control, died two days later on September 10, sparking investigations into possible broader conspiracies, though Weiss acted primarily due to personal grievances against Long's political machine.
On September 15, during the Nazi Party's annual rally in Nuremberg, the Reichstag enacted the Nuremberg Laws, comprising the Reich Citizenship Law, which stripped Jews of German citizenship and classified them as subjects based on ancestry rather than religion, and the Law for the Protection of German Blood and German Honor, which prohibited marriages and extramarital relations between Jews and Germans of "Aryan" descent. These laws formalized racial discrimination, defining Jews as those with three or more Jewish grandparents, and extended restrictions to partial Jewish ancestry, laying the groundwork for further persecution by providing a legal basis for exclusion from public life. Accompanying the racial laws, the Reich Flag Law designated the swastika-emblazoned flag as Germany's national standard, symbolizing the regime's ideological shift.
Throughout September, the Abyssinia Crisis escalated as Italy prepared for invasion, with Ethiopian Emperor Haile Selassie distributing gas masks to civilians amid fears of chemical warfare, reflecting League of Nations impotence in deterring aggression.

October

On October 3, 1935, Italian forces under General Emilio De Bono launched an invasion of Ethiopia from Eritrea, initiating the Second Italo-Ethiopian War. Benito Mussolini, seeking to expand Italian colonial holdings and avenge the 1896 defeat at Adwa, mobilized approximately 500,000 troops, including modern aircraft and chemical weapons, against Ethiopia's less equipped army led by Emperor Haile Selassie. The invasion disregarded warnings from the League of Nations, highlighting the organization's limited enforcement power.
In response, Ethiopia appealed to the League of Nations on October 5, demanding sanctions against Italy as the aggressor. On October 7, the League Council unanimously declared Italy the aggressor, recommending economic sanctions but excluding key resources like oil and prohibiting military intervention. This marked the first application of Article 16 of the League Covenant but proved ineffective, as major powers like Britain and France prioritized appeasement to avoid broader conflict. Italian advances continued, with forces occupying Adigrat by early October.
Meanwhile, in China, the Chinese Communist Red Army, led by Mao Zedong, concluded its grueling Long March on October 20, arriving in Yan'an, Shaanxi Province, after a 6,000-mile retreat from Nationalist encirclement. Of the original 86,000 participants who began the trek in October 1934, only about 4,000-8,000 survivors remained, solidifying Mao's leadership within the Communist Party. The march evaded Chiang Kai-shek's forces and preserved the Communist movement, setting the stage for future guerrilla warfare.

November

On November 3, a referendum in Greece overwhelmingly approved the restoration of the monarchy, with 97.88% of voters supporting the return of King George II after 12 years of exile following the 1923 abolition; George II arrived in Athens the same day to resume the throne amid political instability after a military coup. Also on November 3, Korean athlete Sohn Kee-chung set a new world record in the marathon at 2:26:42 during the Japanese national championships in Tokyo, though he competed under the imposed Japanese name Kitei Son due to Japan's occupation of Korea.
The United States formally recognized the Commonwealth of the Philippines on November 14, transitioning the territory toward self-governance under the Tydings-McDuffie Act of 1934; Manuel L. Quezon was inaugurated as the first president the following day in Manila, with the U.S. retaining control over foreign affairs and defense.
November 18 marked the implementation of economic sanctions by the League of Nations against Italy in response to its invasion of Ethiopia the previous month, including an embargo on key exports like oil, rubber, and metals, though major powers like the U.S. and Britain participated unevenly, highlighting the League's enforcement weaknesses.
Pan American Airways inaugurated the first regular transpacific airmail service on November 22 when the Martin M-130 flying boat China Clipper, piloted by Edwin C. Musick, departed San Francisco Bay for Manila, covering 7,000 miles with stops in Honolulu, Midway, Wake, and Guam over six days; the flight carried 113 pounds of airmail and symbolized advancing commercial aviation amid technological improvements in long-range seaplanes. The China Clipper arrived in Manila on November 29, completing the route and enabling scheduled mail service that later expanded to passengers.
On November 25, Japanese forces under Prince Demchugdongrub established the puppet state of Mengjiang in northern China, encompassing parts of Inner Mongolia and serving as a buffer against Chinese nationalists; this move escalated Japan's expansionism in the region, backed by military occupation despite nominal independence.
In cultural developments, T.S. Eliot's verse drama Murder in the Cathedral, depicting the assassination of Thomas Becket, premiered on November 15 at Canterbury Cathedral, drawing acclaim for its poetic exploration of martyrdom and drawing large audiences during its initial run. The film Mutiny on the Bounty, starring Clark Gable and Charles Laughton, opened in U.S. theaters on November 8, becoming a box-office success and winning the Academy Award for Best Picture the following year for its dramatization of the 1789 naval revolt.

December

On December 9, Walter Liggett, a Minneapolis-based journalist known for exposing political corruption and organized crime, was assassinated in a drive-by shooting as he returned home with his wife; the attack involved submachine gun fire from a passing vehicle, and investigations pointed to links with local mob figures amid his criticisms of Minnesota Governor Floyd B. Olson.
The Nobel Prizes were presented on December 10 in Stockholm and Oslo; in Chemistry, Frdric Joliot-Curie and Irne Joliot-Curie received the award for their synthesis of new radioactive elements, marking the first time a parent-daughter pair won Nobels in the same field following Marie Curie's earlier achievements. The Peace Prize went to Carl von Ossietzky, a German journalist imprisoned by the Nazi regime for revealing rearmament violations, a decision that provoked outrage in Germany where it was viewed as interference in internal affairs.
On December 14, Tom Garrigue Masaryk, founding president of Czechoslovakia since 1918, resigned at age 85 due to deteriorating health, paving the way for Edvard Bene to assume the presidency the following day in a smooth transition reflective of the country's democratic stability amid rising European tensions.
In the ongoing Second Italo-Ethiopian War, Italian dictator Benito Mussolini authorized the use of chemical weapons on December 16, including mustard gas, to counter Ethiopian counteroffensives; this escalation, involving aerial bombings, resulted in thousands of casualties and drew international condemnation despite limited enforcement by the League of Nations, highlighting the body's ineffectiveness in curbing aggression.
On December 26, Tang Yu-jen, China's Administrative Vice Minister for Foreign Affairs, was assassinated in Nanjing shortly after arriving home, prompting the government to declare martial law in Shanghai, Nanjing, and Hankou to suppress potential unrest amid factional rivalries and Japanese pressures in the region.

Deaths

January

On January 1, the Kingdom of Italy unified its colonies of Tripolitania and Cyrenaica into the colony of Libya, establishing a single administrative entity under Italian control to consolidate imperial holdings in North Africa.
The trial of Bruno Richard Hauptmann for the 1932 kidnapping and murder of Charles Lindbergh Jr. commenced on January 2 in Flemington, New Jersey, drawing intense national attention amid widespread public outrage over the crime, which had prompted federal legislation like the "Lindbergh Law" criminalizing interstate kidnapping. Hauptmann, a German immigrant carpenter, faced circumstantial evidence including wood matches from his attic linking to the ladder used in the abduction; he maintained his innocence throughout the 32-day proceedings.
On January 7, Italian Prime Minister Benito Mussolini and French Foreign Minister Pierre Laval reached an accord in Rome, with Italy committing to respect French interests in the Mediterranean and Europe while France tacitly endorsed potential Italian expansion in Ethiopia, reflecting mutual colonial ambitions but failing to avert Italy's subsequent invasion later that year.
Aviator Amelia Earhart completed the first solo flight from Honolulu, Hawaii, to Oakland, California, on January 11, covering 2,408 miles in 18 hours and 15 minutes aboard her Lockheed Vega, marking a milestone in trans-Pacific aviation amid ongoing advancements in long-distance flight technology.
The Saarland plebiscite occurred on January 13, supervised by the League of Nations, resulting in 90.8% of voters (477,119 out of 525,063 eligible) opting for reunification with Germany over remaining under League administration or joining France, thereby returning the coal-rich territoryceded after World War Ito the German Reich under Adolf Hitler and signaling early erosion of Versailles Treaty constraints.
Former Bolshevik leaders Grigory Zinoviev and Lev Kamenev went on trial in Moscow starting January 15, charged with conspiring in the 1934 assassination of Sergei Kirov; both pleaded guilty under duress in proceedings widely recognized as orchestrated by Joseph Stalin to eliminate political rivals, confessing to moral complicity despite lacking direct involvement, which foreshadowed broader purges.
Japanese forces launched an offensive into China's Chahar Province on January 23, exploiting tensions with Inner Mongolian allies to expand influence in northern China, prompting the Tanggu Truce's effective collapse and escalating Sino-Japanese hostilities that presaged full-scale war.

February

On February 2, Leonarde Keeler administered the first polygraph test admitted as evidence in a United States court during a murder trial in Portage, Wisconsin, marking an early milestone in forensic science despite ongoing debates over the device's reliability.
On February 12, the U.S. Navy rigid airship USS Macon crashed into the Pacific Ocean off Point Sur, California, during a storm after structural failure in its tail; of the 83 crew aboard, 81 were rescued, but two sailors perished, representing the final loss of a U.S. Navy airship from its interwar fleet.
On February 13, Bruno Hauptmann was convicted of first-degree murder in a New Jersey court for the 1932 kidnapping and death of Charles Lindbergh Jr., the infant son of aviator Charles Lindbergh; the trial, drawing massive public attention, resulted in Hauptmann's death sentence, which was carried out in 1936 amid persistent claims of his innocence by some observers.
On February 26, Adolf Hitler signed a decree formally establishing the Luftwaffe as Germany's independent air force within the Wehrmacht, appointing Hermann Gring as its commander-in-chief; this action violated the Treaty of Versailles' prohibition on German military aviation, signaling accelerated Nazi rearmament and contributing to rising European tensions. The Luftwaffe's creation built on clandestine development since 1933, positioning it as a core element of Germany's military resurgence under the regime's expansionist policies.

August 30 marked President Roosevelt's signing of both the Revenue Act of 1935, which raised top income tax rates to 75% on incomes over $1 million and introduced a 7% tax on corporate undistributed profits to fund New Deal programs, and the Guffey-Snyder Coal Act (Bituminous Coal Conservation Act), which sought to stabilize the coal industry through price controls, labor standards, and a tax on production to enforce collective bargaining; the latter faced immediate legal challenges for exceeding federal commerce powers, leading to its invalidation by the Supreme Court in 1936.

September

On September 2, the Labor Day Hurricane, a Category 5 storm with sustained winds of 185 mph, made landfall in the Florida Keys, becoming the strongest hurricane on record to strike the United States at the time and causing 423 deaths, primarily among World War I veterans working on construction projects. The storm's rapid intensification and direct hit on low-lying areas like Islamorada led to widespread destruction, including the obliteration of entire settlements and the derailing of rescue trains by storm surge.
On September 8, Louisiana Senator Huey Long, a prominent populist politician known for his Share Our Wealth program, was assassinated in Baton Rouge by Dr. Carl Weiss, who was subsequently killed by Long's bodyguards. Long, who had wielded significant influence in Louisiana politics through patronage and legislative control, died two days later on September 10, sparking investigations into possible broader conspiracies, though Weiss acted primarily due to personal grievances against Long's political machine.
On September 15, during the Nazi Party's annual rally in Nuremberg, the Reichstag enacted the Nuremberg Laws, comprising the Reich Citizenship Law, which stripped Jews of German citizenship and classified them as subjects based on ancestry rather than religion, and the Law for the Protection of German Blood and German Honor, which prohibited marriages and extramarital relations between Jews and Germans of "Aryan" descent. These laws formalized racial discrimination, defining Jews as those with three or more Jewish grandparents, and extended restrictions to partial Jewish ancestry, laying the groundwork for further persecution by providing a legal basis for exclusion from public life. Accompanying the racial laws, the Reich Flag Law designated the swastika-emblazoned flag as Germany's national standard, symbolizing the regime's ideological shift.
Throughout September, the Abyssinia Crisis escalated as Italy prepared for invasion, with Ethiopian Emperor Haile Selassie distributing gas masks to civilians amid fears of chemical warfare, reflecting League of Nations impotence in deterring aggression.

October

On October 3, 1935, Italian forces under General Emilio De Bono launched an invasion of Ethiopia from Eritrea, initiating the Second Italo-Ethiopian War. Benito Mussolini, seeking to expand Italian colonial holdings and avenge the 1896 defeat at Adwa, mobilized approximately 500,000 troops, including modern aircraft and chemical weapons, against Ethiopia's less equipped army led by Emperor Haile Selassie. The invasion disregarded warnings from the League of Nations, highlighting the organization's limited enforcement power.
In response, Ethiopia appealed to the League of Nations on October 5, demanding sanctions against Italy as the aggressor. On October 7, the League Council unanimously declared Italy the aggressor, recommending economic sanctions but excluding key resources like oil and prohibiting military intervention. This marked the first application of Article 16 of the League Covenant but proved ineffective, as major powers like Britain and France prioritized appeasement to avoid broader conflict. Italian advances continued, with forces occupying Adigrat by early October.
Meanwhile, in China, the Chinese Communist Red Army, led by Mao Zedong, concluded its grueling Long March on October 20, arriving in Yan'an, Shaanxi Province, after a 6,000-mile retreat from Nationalist encirclement. Of the original 86,000 participants who began the trek in October 1934, only about 4,000-8,000 survivors remained, solidifying Mao's leadership within the Communist Party. The march evaded Chiang Kai-shek's forces and preserved the Communist movement, setting the stage for future guerrilla warfare.

November

On November 3, a referendum in Greece overwhelmingly approved the restoration of the monarchy, with 97.88% of voters supporting the return of King George II after 12 years of exile following the 1923 abolition; George II arrived in Athens the same day to resume the throne amid political instability after a military coup. Also on November 3, Korean athlete Sohn Kee-chung set a new world record in the marathon at 2:26:42 during the Japanese national championships in Tokyo, though he competed under the imposed Japanese name Kitei Son due to Japan's occupation of Korea.
The United States formally recognized the Commonwealth of the Philippines on November 14, transitioning the territory toward self-governance under the Tydings-McDuffie Act of 1934; Manuel L. Quezon was inaugurated as the first president the following day in Manila, with the U.S. retaining control over foreign affairs and defense.
November 18 marked the implementation of economic sanctions by the League of Nations against Italy in response to its invasion of Ethiopia the previous month, including an embargo on key exports like oil, rubber, and metals, though major powers like the U.S. and Britain participated unevenly, highlighting the League's enforcement weaknesses.
Pan American Airways inaugurated the first regular transpacific airmail service on November 22 when the Martin M-130 flying boat China Clipper, piloted by Edwin C. Musick, departed San Francisco Bay for Manila, covering 7,000 miles with stops in Honolulu, Midway, Wake, and Guam over six days; the flight carried 113 pounds of airmail and symbolized advancing commercial aviation amid technological improvements in long-range seaplanes. The China Clipper arrived in Manila on November 29, completing the route and enabling scheduled mail service that later expanded to passengers.
On November 25, Japanese forces under Prince Demchugdongrub established the puppet state of Mengjiang in northern China, encompassing parts of Inner Mongolia and serving as a buffer against Chinese nationalists; this move escalated Japan's expansionism in the region, backed by military occupation despite nominal independence.
In cultural developments, T.S. Eliot's verse drama Murder in the Cathedral, depicting the assassination of Thomas Becket, premiered on November 15 at Canterbury Cathedral, drawing acclaim for its poetic exploration of martyrdom and drawing large audiences during its initial run. The film Mutiny on the Bounty, starring Clark Gable and Charles Laughton, opened in U.S. theaters on November 8, becoming a box-office success and winning the Academy Award for Best Picture the following year for its dramatization of the 1789 naval revolt.

December

On December 9, Walter Liggett, a Minneapolis-based journalist known for exposing political corruption and organized crime, was assassinated in a drive-by shooting as he returned home with his wife; the attack involved submachine gun fire from a passing vehicle, and investigations pointed to links with local mob figures amid his criticisms of Minnesota Governor Floyd B. Olson.
The Nobel Prizes were presented on December 10 in Stockholm and Oslo; in Chemistry, Frdric Joliot-Curie and Irne Joliot-Curie received the award for their synthesis of new radioactive elements, marking the first time a parent-daughter pair won Nobels in the same field following Marie Curie's earlier achievements. The Peace Prize went to Carl von Ossietzky, a German journalist imprisoned by the Nazi regime for revealing rearmament violations, a decision that provoked outrage in Germany where it was viewed as interference in internal affairs.
On December 14, Tom Garrigue Masaryk, founding president of Czechoslovakia since 1918, resigned at age 85 due to deteriorating health, paving the way for Edvard Bene to assume the presidency the following day in a smooth transition reflective of the country's democratic stability amid rising European tensions.
In the ongoing Second Italo-Ethiopian War, Italian dictator Benito Mussolini authorized the use of chemical weapons on December 16, including mustard gas, to counter Ethiopian counteroffensives; this escalation, involving aerial bombings, resulted in thousands of casualties and drew international condemnation despite limited enforcement by the League of Nations, highlighting the body's ineffectiveness in curbing aggression.
On December 26, Tang Yu-jen, China's Administrative Vice Minister for Foreign Affairs, was assassinated in Nanjing shortly after arriving home, prompting the government to declare martial law in Shanghai, Nanjing, and Hankou to suppress potential unrest amid factional rivalries and Japanese pressures in the region.

Nobel Prizes

Physics

The Nobel Prize in Physics for 1935 was awarded to James Chadwick, a British physicist, "for the discovery of the neutron". Chadwick, born on October 20, 1891, in Cheshire, England, earned his B.Sc. in 1911 and M.Sc. in 1913 from the University of Manchester, where he initially worked under Ernest Rutherford. He later joined the Cavendish Laboratory at the University of Cambridge, conducting experiments that led to his breakthrough in 1932.
Chadwick's discovery stemmed from investigations into radiation produced by bombarding beryllium with alpha particles, building on earlier observations by Walther Bothe and Herbert Becker in 1930 of a highly penetrating radiation from this process. Interpreting the radiation not as gamma rays but as uncharged particles of approximately the mass of a proton, Chadwick demonstrated that these neutrons could eject protons from paraffin wax with energies consistent only with neutral particles of hydrogen-atom mass. This experimental confirmation resolved discrepancies in the proton-electron model of the atomic nucleus proposed earlier and aligned with theoretical predictions by Rutherford in 1920 of a neutral constituent and Werner Heisenberg's 1932 exclusion of electrons from nuclei. The neutron's lack of electric charge allowed it to penetrate atomic nuclei without electrostatic repulsion, enabling direct interactions that charged particles could not achieve.
The neutron's identification as a fundamental building block of matter, alongside protons and electrons, provided a more accurate framework for calculating nuclear masses and atomic weights, supplanting prior models reliant on proton-electron pairs to explain isotopic variations. Its penetrating power revolutionized nuclear physics by facilitating atomic disintegration and subsequent developments in fission, as seen in the splitting of uranium-235 nuclei. Chadwick received the Hughes Medal from the Royal Society in 1932 for this work, preceding the Nobel recognition, and later contributed to wartime nuclear efforts as head of the British Mission in the Manhattan Project from 1943 to 1946.

Chemistry

The Nobel Prize in Chemistry for 1935 was awarded jointly to Irne Joliot-Curie (ne Curie, born September 12, 1897) and Frdric Joliot-Curie (born March 19, 1900), French physicists and chemists, "in recognition of their synthesis of new radioactive elements." Their work demonstrated the artificial production of radioactive isotopes from stable elements, a discovery first reported in early 1934 after experiments bombarding boron, aluminum, and magnesium with alpha particles emitted from polonium sources. These transmutations yielded radioelements that decayed by positron emission, confirming induced radioactivity and distinguishing it from natural decay processes previously observed only in elements like uranium.
The Joliot-Curies, married since 1926, performed their research collaboratively at the Institut du Radium in Paris, building on the legacy of Irne's parents, Pierre and Marie Curie, who had isolated radium and polonium decades earlier. Initially nominated for the 1934 Nobel Prize in Physics, their contributions were deemed more aligned with chemical synthesis of novel elements, leading to the Chemistry award the following year. This advancement enabled the controlled generation of short-lived radioisotopes, foundational for subsequent developments in nuclear reactors, medical tracers, and isotope labeling techniques, though initial applications were limited by the low yields and short half-lives of the produced elements (e.g., phosphorus-30 with a 2.5-minute half-life).
The prize medal and diploma were presented during the Nobel ceremony on December 10, 1935, in Stockholm, with Irne Joliot-Curie becoming the second woman to receive the Chemistry Nobel and the first to do so in the same field as her mother, Marie Curie (1911). The shared award highlighted the Joliot-Curies' empirical approach, relying on precise detection of induced emissions via cloud chambers and ionization measurements, which verified the nuclear reactions without theoretical preconceptions dominating the era's quantum models.

Physiology or Medicine

The Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1935 was awarded to Hans Spemann, a German zoologist and embryologist, "for his discovery of the organizer effect in embryonic development." Spemann, born on 27 June 1869 in ZaberN, Kingdom of Wrttemberg, and who died on 12 September 1941 in Freiburg im Breisgau, Nazi Germany, received the prize for experiments demonstrating that specific embryonic tissues exert inductive influences on adjacent cells, directing their differentiation into organized structures such as the neural axis.
Spemann's key insight stemmed from transplantation studies on amphibian embryos, particularly newts, conducted in the early 1920s. In a landmark 1924 experiment supervised by Spemann and performed by his doctoral student Hilde Mangold, tissue from the dorsal lip of the blastopore in a donor embryo was grafted onto a host embryo's ventral side, resulting in the induction of a secondary neural tube and somitesevidence of an "organizer" region that coordinates morphogenesis rather than cells differentiating autonomously. This organizer effect, identified at the gastrula stage, revealed hierarchical cell interactions where presumptive ectoderm could be redirected to form neural tissue under the influence of underlying mesoderm, overturning prevailing theories of mosaic development.
The discovery established embryonic induction as a fundamental mechanism in developmental biology, influencing subsequent research into signaling pathways like those involving chordin and noggin proteins, later identified as mediators of the organizer's activity. Although Mangold perished in a laboratory accident in 1924 before the work's full implications were recognized, the Nobel Committee credited Spemann for synthesizing decades of constriction, transplantation, and microsurgery techniques that culminated in this paradigm shift. Spemann delivered his Nobel lecture on 11 December 1935 in Stockholm, titled "The Organizer-Effect in Embryonic Development," emphasizing the organizer's role in primary and secondary inductions without invoking speculative molecular details unavailable at the time.

Literature

The Nobel Prize in Literature was not awarded in 1935, as the Swedish Academy concluded that no literary work or author sufficiently met the criteria established by Alfred Nobel's will for conferring "the greatest benefit on mankind" that year. This decision aligned with the Academy's practice of withholding the prize in years when no nominee was deemed worthy, a precedent set in earlier instances such as 1914 and repeated in subsequent wartime periods.
The Academy had received over 30 nominations for the 1935 prize, including prominent figures such as French novelist Roger Martin du Gard, Portuguese poet Antnio Correia de Oliveira, and Venezuelan writer Rufino Blanco Fombona, but none advanced to laureate status. Martin du Gard, for instance, was nominated multiple times in the 1930s for his multi-volume novel cycle Les Thibault, which explored themes of family dynamics and ideological conflict in early 20th-century France, but his recognition came later in 1937.
In lieu of a recipient, one-third of the prize moneyapproximately 141,069 Swedish kronor out of the total 423,206 kronorwas allocated to the Nobel Foundation's main fund for future prizes, while two-thirds was directed to the Literature prize section's special fund to support administrative and reserve purposes. This redistribution ensured the endowment's sustainability without compromising the prize's standards, reflecting the Academy's commitment to selectivity over annual obligation.

Peace

The Nobel Peace Prize for 1935 was awarded to Carl von Ossietzky, a German journalist and pacifist, for his "burning love for freedom of thought and expression and his valuable contribution to the cause of peace" through exposs on Germany's clandestine rearmament in violation of the Treaty of Versailles. Ossietzky, born on October 3, 1889, in Hamburg, edited the pacifist newspaper Die Weltbhne and published articles in 1929 revealing secret military buildup, including the training of pilots and construction of tanks under the guise of civilian projects, which drew international attention to treaty breaches. His work positioned him as a vocal critic of militarism, leading to his arrest by the Nazi regime in 1933 on charges of treason and espionage; he was convicted in November 1931 but remained active until the Nazis seized power.
At the time of the award announcement on November 27, 1936retroactively for 1935, as no prize was given in 1935 initiallyOssietzky was imprisoned in the Esterwegen concentration camp, where he suffered severe health deterioration from tuberculosis and mistreatment. The Norwegian Nobel Committee selected him over other nominees amid growing European tensions, viewing his case as emblematic of resistance to authoritarian suppression of dissent and aggressive nationalism. Nazi authorities refused him permission to travel to Oslo for the ceremony, confining him instead to a Berlin hospital under guard, where he accepted the prize via telegram despite pressure to renounce it.
The decision provoked intense backlash from the German government; Adolf Hitler denounced it as a "bolshevik" plot and decreed that no German could accept a Nobel Prize henceforth, a ban lasting until after World War II. Within Norway, the award stirred domestic controversy, with King Haakon VII absenting himself from the presentation to avoid endorsing what some viewed as overt political intervention against a foreign power. Ossietzky's selection underscored the prize's occasional alignment with human rights advocacy over immediate diplomatic pacification, though critics argued it prioritized symbolic protest over pragmatic peace efforts amid rising global armament. He received the medal and diploma in a private Berlin ceremony but derived no financial benefit, as Nazi officials seized the funds.
Ossietzky died on May 4, 1938, at age 48, from complications of his camp-induced illnesses, shortly after release to police supervision; international observers attributed his demise to deliberate neglect, amplifying the award's role in highlighting early Nazi atrocities. The prize amount was 171,325 Swedish kronor, equivalent to roughly $40,000 at the time, but its conferral on a concentration camp inmate marked a rare instance of the Nobel Committee recognizing individual sacrifice against totalitarian expansionism.
