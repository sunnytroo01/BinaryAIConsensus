Fluid replacement
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Fluid replacement is the medical practice of replenishing bodily fluids lost through mechanisms such as sweating, bleeding, vomiting, diarrhea, or surgical procedures, primarily to restore intravascular volume, maintain electrolyte balance, and support organ perfusion. This intervention is essential in conditions like dehydration, hypovolemic shock, sepsis, or perioperative care, where oral intake is insufficient or contraindicated, and it can be administered via intravenous, oral, or enteral routes depending on the patient's clinical status.
Key aspects of fluid replacement include the selection of appropriate fluid types, guided by the underlying pathophysiology and patient needs. Crystalloids, such as normal saline (0.9% sodium chloride) and lactated Ringer's solution, are the most commonly used due to their ability to distribute across extracellular spaces and effectively expand plasma volume. Colloids, including albumin and synthetic starches like hetastarch, are larger molecules that primarily remain in the intravascular compartment to provide oncotic pressure support, though their use is more selective due to risks like kidney injury. Fluid choice and volume are determined by assessments of volume status, including vital signs, urine output, and laboratory markers like lactate levels, to avoid complications such as fluid overload or electrolyte imbalances.
Administration typically involves intravenous infusion, with initial boluses (e.g., 1-2 liters for adults in hypovolemia) followed by maintenance rates tailored to ongoing losses and requirements, such as 30 mL/kg ideal body weight in septic shock protocols. Monitoring is critical, targeting endpoints like mean arterial pressure above 65 mm Hg, while using dynamic measures to assess fluid responsiveness and prevent adverse effects including pulmonary edema, infection at the IV site, or metabolic acidosis. Effective fluid replacement improves outcomes in critical illnesses by optimizing tissue oxygenation and hemodynamic stability.

Principles and Physiology

Definition and Importance

Fluid replacement therapy is a critical medical intervention designed to replenish bodily fluids and electrolytes lost through mechanisms such as hemorrhage, dehydration, vomiting, diarrhea, or third-space shifts, thereby restoring physiological balance and preventing complications from hypovolemia. This therapy encompasses maintenance fluids to cover routine daily losses, replacement fluids to address deficits from acute illness or injury, and resuscitative fluids for severe volume depletion or shock, with the overarching aim of optimizing organ perfusion and substrate delivery including oxygen and electrolytes.
The practice originated in the early 20th century amid efforts to treat hypovolemic shock during World War I, where initial approaches focused on saline solutions and early blood transfusions to counteract battlefield injuries. It evolved significantly through subsequent conflicts, including World War II, which advanced plasma and whole blood use; by the 1930s, American pediatrician Alexis Hartmann modified Ringer's solution by adding sodium lactate to create Lactated Ringer's, a balanced salt solution that more closely approximated plasma composition and addressed acidosis risks associated with plain saline.
Fluid replacement is vital for averting hypovolemic shock, sustaining tissue perfusion, and preserving renal function, as inadequate volume restoration can lead to multi-organ dysfunction. It plays a pivotal role in inpatient care, with estimates indicating that up to 80% of hospitalized patients receive such therapy, particularly in intensive care settings where over 20% undergo daily resuscitation and more than 30% on admission. The core objectives include restoring intravascular volume, rectifying electrolyte disturbances, and attaining euvolemia to avoid overload, thereby supporting hemodynamic stability without exacerbating conditions like pulmonary edema.

Body Fluid Compartments and Balance

The human body maintains fluid homeostasis through distinct compartments that collectively account for total body water (TBW). In a typical 70-kg adult male, TBW constitutes approximately 60% of body weight, or about 42 liters, with roughly two-thirds (approximately 28 liters) residing in the intracellular fluid (ICF) compartment inside cells, and one-third (approximately 14 liters) in the extracellular fluid (ECF) compartment outside cells. The ECF further subdivides into the intravascular compartment, primarily plasma (about 3 liters), and the interstitial compartment, which includes fluid in tissues and lymph (about 11 liters). These proportions can vary slightly with age, sex, and body composition, but they underscore the ICF's dominance in overall fluid volume.
Fluid distribution between the intravascular and interstitial spaces within the ECF is primarily governed by Starling forces, which balance hydrostatic and oncotic pressures across capillary walls. Hydrostatic pressure drives fluid out of capillaries, while oncotic pressure, mainly from plasma proteins like albumin, pulls fluid back in. At the arterial end of capillaries, hydrostatic forces typically favor filtration into the interstitium, while at the venous end, oncotic forces promote reabsorption, resulting in a slight net filtration balanced by lymphatic drainage.
The kidneys play a central role in maintaining fluid balance by regulating ECF volume through hormonal mechanisms. Antidiuretic hormone (ADH, or vasopressin), released from the posterior pituitary in response to increased plasma osmolality or reduced blood volume, enhances water reabsorption in the collecting ducts via aquaporin-2 channels, concentrating urine and conserving free water. Aldosterone, secreted by the adrenal cortex as part of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) in response to low blood pressure or hyperkalemia, promotes sodium reabsorption in the distal tubules and collecting ducts, indirectly retaining water to expand ECF volume. Conversely, atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP), produced by cardiac atrial cells during volume expansion, inhibits RAAS, promotes natriuresis, and induces vasodilation to reduce ECF volume and blood pressure. Under normal conditions, adults experience daily insensible losses of 5001000 mL through skin evaporation and respiration, plus urinary output of 12 liters, balanced by intake to maintain steady-state hydration.
Imbalances in these compartments arise from disruptions in volume regulation, leading to hypovolemia, a reduction in intravascular volume that impairs tissue perfusion. Absolute hypovolemia occurs with direct losses, such as hemorrhage from bleeding, where ECF volume decreases outright. Relative hypovolemia, by contrast, results from redistribution, as in sepsis-induced vasodilation, where peripheral vessel dilation increases vascular capacitance without proportional volume expansion, effectively mimicking volume depletion. Common signs include compensatory tachycardia to sustain cardiac output and oliguria due to renal hypoperfusion and ADH activation, signaling the need for fluid replacement to restore balance.

Methods of Administration

Oral and Enteral Routes

Oral rehydration therapy (ORT) is a cornerstone method for fluid replacement in cases of dehydration, particularly from diarrheal diseases, and is recommended by the World Health Organization (WHO) for all age groups except those with severe dehydration requiring immediate intravenous intervention. ORS powder is prepared by dissolving one sachet in the volume of clean water specified on the package (commonly 1 liter for standard WHO sachets, or smaller for pediatric variants), stirring until fully dissolved; over-concentration must be avoided to prevent hypernatremia. ORT leverages the sodium-glucose cotransport mechanism in the small intestine, where glucose facilitates active sodium absorption, drawing water osmotically to promote rehydration without overwhelming the gut. The standard low-osmolarity oral rehydration solution (ORS) endorsed by WHO contains 75 mmol/L sodium, 75 mmol/L glucose, 20 mmol/L potassium, 65 mmol/L chloride, and 10 mmol/L citrate, with a total osmolarity of 245 mOsm/L, making it more effective at reducing stool output and vomiting compared to higher-osmolarity formulations. This therapy is administered gradually in volumes tailored to the degree of dehydration, typically 50-100 mL/kg over 3-4 hours for mild to moderate cases, followed by maintenance fluids.
Enteral feeding provides an alternative route for fluid and nutrient delivery when oral intake is insufficient but gastrointestinal function remains intact, often used in patients with malnutrition accompanied by fluid deficits, such as those with chronic illnesses or post-surgical recovery. This method involves administering isotonic or specialized formulas via nasogastric (NG) tubes, nasojejunal tubes, or gastrostomy/jejunostomy devices, allowing for continuous infusion or intermittent boluses to meet daily fluid requirements, which average 25-35 mL/kg in adults depending on clinical status. Formulas are selected based on electrolyte needs, with monitoring to ensure adequate free water supplementation to prevent imbalances, as enteral nutrition alone may not fully address hydration in high-loss states.
Both oral and enteral routes offer physiological advantages, including promotion of normal gut motility, preservation of the intestinal barrier function to reduce translocation of bacteria, and cost-effectiveness compared to invasive methods, with ORT specifically demonstrating high efficacy in resolving mild to moderate dehydration in over 90% of cases when vomiting is controlled. These approaches support mucosal integrity and immune function in the gut, which can be compromised during prolonged fasting or parenteral nutrition.
Contraindications for oral and enteral routes include conditions impairing gastrointestinal absorption or motility, such as ileus, bowel obstruction, severe vomiting, or hemodynamic instability, where alternative vascular access is necessary to avoid complications like aspiration or further dehydration.

Intravenous Route

Intravenous (IV) fluid replacement involves the direct administration of fluids into the vascular system, providing rapid and controlled delivery essential for managing acute conditions such as hypovolemia and shock. This method utilizes catheters inserted into veins to bypass the gastrointestinal tract, ensuring immediate bioavailability and precise volume control, which is particularly valuable in critical care settings where oral intake is compromised or insufficient.
IV access is achieved through peripheral or central venous catheters, selected based on the clinical urgency and required infusion volume. Peripheral IV catheters, commonly placed in sites like the antecubital fossa or dorsal hand veins, are suitable for short-term use and standard fluid delivery, with flow rates typically ranging from 50 to 200 mL/hour under gravity, though large-bore (14-16 gauge) peripheral catheters can achieve up to 1000 mL/hour with pressure assistance. For high-volume or prolonged therapy, central venous catheters, such as those inserted via the subclavian or internal jugular veins, are preferred due to their ability to handle larger flow rates (up to 500-1000 mL/hour or more) and tolerate irritating fluids, though they require more invasive placement.
Delivery systems for IV fluids include gravity drip mechanisms, which rely on hydrostatic pressure for unregulated infusion, and electronic infusion pumps that provide programmable rates for accuracy. Initial setup requires priming the tubing with fluid to expel air and prevent air embolism, a potentially life-threatening complication where air enters the bloodstream.
The primary advantages of the IV route are its ability to circumvent gastrointestinal absorption limitations, enabling faster onset in patients with nausea or ileus, and the capacity for precise titration of fluid volumes and rates via pumps, which supports tailored therapy in dynamic clinical scenarios. Approximately 80% of hospitalized patients receive IV fluid administration, underscoring its prevalence in acute and perioperative care. However, risks include phlebitis, an inflammation of the vein wall occurring in 20-40% of peripheral IV cases, often linked to dwell time and catheter size, with more detailed complications addressed elsewhere. In milder dehydration, oral routes offer a non-invasive alternative when feasible.

Alternative Routes

Alternative routes for fluid replacement encompass methods beyond oral/enteral and intravenous administration, employed in scenarios where standard vascular access is challenging or unavailable. These approaches, including subcutaneous infusion, rectal administration, and intraosseous access, provide viable options for hydration in specific clinical contexts, such as palliative care, emergencies, or resource-constrained environments. While intravenous routes remain the preferred method for rapid fluid delivery due to their direct vascular access, alternative routes offer slower but effective absorption for maintenance needs or when veins are inaccessible.
Subcutaneous fluid administration, known as hypodermoclysis, involves infusing fluids into the subcutaneous tissue using a needle or catheter, typically in sites like the abdomen, thighs, or arms. Absorption occurs slowly through capillary diffusion, at a rate of approximately 1,000-1,500 mL per day per site, making it suitable for gradual rehydration rather than acute resuscitation. This method is particularly ideal for elderly patients with fragile veins, those in palliative care, or individuals with moderate dehydration from conditions like dysphagia or dementia, as it minimizes discomfort and reduces the need for repeated venipuncture. Complications are rare but can include local edema, pain at the infusion site, or infection if hyaluronidase is not used to enhance dispersion.
Rectal fluid administration, historically exemplified by the Murphy dripa method using saline enemas delivered via a rectal tuberelies on absorption through the colonic mucosa. This approach allows for limited volumes of up to 1 L per day, administered intermittently to avoid colonic distension or electrolyte imbalances. It has been used in moderately dehydrated patients when other routes are impractical, such as in historical wartime settings or for pediatric rehydration in low-resource areas, though its efficacy is constrained by variable absorption rates influenced by hypovolemia. Potential drawbacks include patient discomfort, risk of rectal irritation, and incomplete absorption, limiting its routine application.
Intraosseous administration provides rapid access to the vascular system by inserting a needle into the bone marrow cavity, commonly at the proximal tibia or humerus, allowing fluids to enter circulation via the marrow's venous plexus. Indicated for emergencies in pediatrics and adults when intravenous access failssuch as in shock, cardiac arrest, or traumait achieves flow rates comparable to peripheral IV lines and can be established in under one minute with success rates exceeding 90%. This route is especially advantageous in resource-limited settings due to its simplicity, minimal training requirements, and reliability without specialized equipment. Complications may involve extravasation, osteomyelitis, or compartment syndrome if prolonged beyond 24 hours.
Overall, alternative routes account for less than 5% of fluid administrations in clinical practice, reserved for targeted scenarios where their benefitssuch as ease of use and reduced invasivenessoutweigh limitations in speed and volume capacity.

Types of Fluids

Crystalloid Solutions

Crystalloid solutions are aqueous solutions of electrolytes and small molecules that mimic the composition of extracellular fluid, serving as the cornerstone of fluid replacement therapy due to their low cost, ease of production, and ability to rapidly expand intravascular volume. These solutions are classified based on their tonicity relative to plasma: isotonic, hypotonic, or hypertonic, with isotonic variants being most commonly used for initial resuscitation to avoid cellular shifts. They freely distribute across capillary membranes into the extracellular space, providing volume expansion without oncotic pressure, though approximately 25% remains in the intravascular compartment after equilibration.
Common types include normal saline (0.9% NaCl), which contains 154 mmol/L of sodium and chloride ions, making it isotonic but unbalanced in electrolyte composition compared to plasma. Lactated Ringer's solution is a balanced isotonic crystalloid with 130 mmol/L sodium, 4 mmol/L potassium, 1.5 mmol/L calcium, 109 mmol/L chloride, and 28 mmol/L lactate as a bicarbonate precursor to buffer acidosis. In contrast, 5% dextrose in water (D5W) is initially isotonic due to the osmotic effect of glucose but becomes hypotonic after dextrose metabolism, distributing to both intracellular and extracellular compartments for free water replacement.
A key property of crystalloids is their distribution primarily to the extracellular fluid space, which limits sustained intravascular retention unless given in large volumes. Normal saline, in particular, can lead to hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis when administered in volumes exceeding 2 L, as the high chloride load (154 mmol/L) exceeds plasma levels, suppressing bicarbonate and reducing renal blood flow. Balanced crystalloids like Lactated Ringer's mitigate this risk by more closely approximating plasma electrolyte ratios.
The European Society of Intensive Care Medicine (ESICM) 2025 clinical practice guidelines recommend balanced crystalloids over normal saline for resuscitation in critically ill adults to reduce the risk of acute kidney injury (AKI), based on evidence showing lower incidences of hyperchloremia and renal dysfunction with balanced solutions. For initial dosing, an intravenous bolus of 20-30 mL/kg body weight is standard in hypovolemic states, administered rapidly to assess responsiveness. These solutions are highly cost-effective, typically around $1 per liter, facilitating widespread availability in clinical settings.

Colloid Solutions

Colloid solutions are macromolecular intravenous fluids designed to expand plasma volume by maintaining oncotic pressure, thereby promoting greater retention within the intravascular compartment compared to crystalloid solutions. These agents are particularly considered in scenarios requiring sustained hemodynamic stability, such as severe hypovolemia or conditions with impaired capillary integrity, though they are not routinely recommended as first-line therapy. While crystalloids serve as the initial choice for most fluid resuscitation needs due to their safety and availability, colloids may offer advantages in targeted applications where prolonged intravascular persistence is beneficial.
The primary types of colloid solutions include human-derived albumin, synthetic hydroxyethyl starches (HES), and modified gelatins. Albumin is available in concentrations ranging from 5% to 25%, providing a natural colloid that mimics endogenous plasma proteins. HES solutions, such as hetastarch, are artificially produced from starch derivatives to achieve similar oncotic effects. Gelatins, exemplified by products like Gelofusine, consist of succinylated or polygeline forms that are rapidly degradable.
Key properties of these solutions include their ability to remain in the circulation longer than crystalloids, reducing the required infusion volume for equivalent expansion. For instance, infused albumin exhibits an intravascular half-life of approximately 16-20 hours, attributed to its large molecular size and low permeability across capillary walls. In contrast, HES has raised significant safety concerns, particularly regarding renal toxicity, with 2025 reviews confirming associations with acute kidney injury through mechanisms like osmotic nephrosis and impaired renal autoregulation.
Current guidelines reflect these risks and benefits. The European Society of Intensive Care Medicine (ESICM) 2025 clinical practice guideline issues a conditional recommendation against the routine use of HES in critically ill patients due to evidence of increased adverse events, including renal failure. Albumin, however, is preferentially recommended in specific populations, such as those with cirrhosis complicated by spontaneous bacterial peritonitis or sepsis with hypoalbuminemia, where it reduces mortality and prevents renal complications beyond simple volume expansion.
Evidence from large-scale studies supports a nuanced role for colloids. Meta-analyses of randomized trials in hypovolemic patients, including over 10,000 participants, demonstrate no significant reduction in mortality with colloids compared to crystalloids during resuscitation. Nonetheless, in hypoalbuminemia, albumin infusions are beneficial for correcting oncotic deficits and stabilizing hemodynamics, as they directly replenish plasma protein levels and mitigate capillary leak.

Blood and Blood Products

Blood and blood products are essential components of fluid replacement therapy in scenarios involving significant blood loss, where restoration of oxygen-carrying capacity and coagulation factors is critical alongside volume expansion. These products include packed red blood cells (PRBCs), fresh frozen plasma (FFP), and whole blood, which collectively address deficits in cellular and plasma elements during hemorrhagic conditions. PRBCs primarily restore oxygen delivery by providing concentrated erythrocytes, while FFP supplies coagulation factors and proteins, and whole blood offers a balanced replacement of all blood components when rapid, comprehensive resuscitation is needed.
Hemoglobin-based oxygen carriers (HBOCs), such as HBOC-201 (Hemopure), represent an experimental class of acellular blood substitutes designed to mimic red blood cell function by facilitating oxygen transport without the need for donor blood. However, clinical trials for HBOC-201 were halted in 2013 due to concerns over increased mortality and adverse events, including myocardial infarction and hypertension, preventing widespread adoption. Despite ongoing research into safer HBOC formulations, none have achieved regulatory approval for routine clinical use as of 2025.
PRBCs are the cornerstone of blood product therapy, with each unit typically containing erythrocytes suspended in a small volume of plasma, achieving a hematocrit (Hct) of approximately 60%, which effectively boosts systemic oxygen capacity in anemic or hypovolemic patients. In massive transfusion scenarios, where more than 10 units of PRBCs are administered within 24 hours, protocols recommend a balanced 1:1:1 ratio of PRBCs to FFP to platelets to mitigate coagulopathy and maintain hemostasis, as validated by the PROPPR trial showing improved survival in trauma patients with severe bleeding. FFP complements PRBCs by providing essential clotting factors at concentrations similar to normal plasma, while whole blood, less commonly used due to logistical challenges, delivers an integrated mix of cells, plasma, and factors for immediate volume and oxygen restoration in austere settings.
Indications for blood product transfusion center on hemorrhagic shock, where acute blood loss compromises tissue perfusion and oxygenation, necessitating rapid intervention to prevent organ failure. PRBCs are indicated when hemoglobin levels drop below critical thresholds due to bleeding, aiming to improve oxygen delivery without solely relying on volume expansion from non-cellular fluids. However, risks include transfusion-related acute lung injury (TRALI), characterized by non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema occurring within 6 hours of transfusion, and acute hemolytic reactions from ABO incompatibility, which can lead to intravascular hemolysis, renal failure, and shock. These complications underscore the need for rigorous compatibility testing and monitoring during administration.
As of 2025, major guidelines from organizations like the AABB and American Society of Hematology advocate a restrictive transfusion strategy, recommending PRBC administration only when hemoglobin falls below 7 g/dL in most hemodynamically stable patients to minimize risks of circulatory overload, infections, and immune modulation while preserving blood resources. This approach, supported by meta-analyses of randomized trials, has demonstrated equivalent or superior outcomes compared to liberal strategies in non-bleeding critically ill adults, though higher thresholds (e.g., <8-10 g/dL) may apply in acute coronary syndromes.

Clinical Indications

Resuscitation in Shock and Hypovolemia

Resuscitation in shock and hypovolemia involves the rapid administration of intravenous fluids to restore intravascular volume, improve tissue perfusion, and prevent organ dysfunction in patients with life-threatening circulatory compromise. Hypovolemic shock, often resulting from trauma, hemorrhage, or severe dehydration, features absolute volume loss, while distributive shock, such as septic shock, involves relative hypovolemia due to vasodilation and capillary leak. In both cases, aggressive fluid therapy aims to correct hypotension and hypoperfusion, guided by hemodynamic monitoring and response assessment.
The initiation of intravenous fluids for hypotension requires careful assessment rather than automatic administration based solely on a specific blood pressure value. For example, a blood pressure of 90/60 mmHg represents borderline hypotension (often defined as systolic blood pressure <90 mmHg) and does not independently justify fluid administration. Instead, the decision depends on the clinical context, including signs of hypovolemia (e.g., tachycardia >90 bpm, capillary refill time >2 seconds, low urine output), evidence of inadequate tissue perfusion, or specific conditions such as sepsis or trauma. Guidelines from the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) indicate that patients may require fluid resuscitation if systolic blood pressure is <100 mmHg, heart rate >90 bpm, capillary refill >2 seconds, or other signs are present; in such cases, an initial bolus of 500 mL of crystalloid (sodium 130154 mmol/L) is recommended over less than 15 minutes, followed by reassessment using an ABCDE approach.
The standard initial approach recommends an intravenous bolus of at least 30 mL/kg of crystalloid solution, such as balanced crystalloids, administered within the first 3 hours for patients with sepsis-induced hypoperfusion or septic shock. The European Society of Intensive Care Medicine (ESICM) 2025 guidelines similarly suggest up to 30 mL/kg of crystalloids in the initial resuscitation phase for sepsis or septic shock, emphasizing caution to prevent fluid overload. Fluid challenges are performed iteratively, typically infusing 250-500 mL of crystalloid over 15-30 minutes, followed by immediate reassessment of responsiveness using dynamic parameters like stroke volume variation or passive leg raise tests. Response is evaluated through markers such as lactate clearance, with a goal of greater than 10% reduction per 2 hours after resuscitation initiation indicating adequate perfusion improvement.
Evidence from major trials underscores a shift toward balanced strategies. The CLOVERS trial demonstrated no significant difference in 90-day mortality between liberal fluid administration (prioritizing crystalloids before vasopressors) and a restrictive approach (early vasopressors with limited fluids) in patients with sepsis-induced hypotension, with rates of 14.0% and 14.9%, respectively. The ESICM 2025 guidelines advocate limiting early volume expansion to mitigate risks like pulmonary edema, particularly in non-responders. In hypovolemic shock, timely resuscitation with source control, such as hemorrhage management, improves survival by restoring perfusion and reducing multi-organ failure. Overall, responsive patients undergoing early fluid resuscitation experience mortality reductions of 20-30% compared to delayed or inadequate therapy, highlighting the importance of individualized protocols.

Maintenance and Replacement in Dehydration

Maintenance fluid therapy in dehydration aims to provide ongoing hydration that meets basal metabolic requirements while compensating for ongoing losses, preventing further volume depletion in non-acute settings. This approach differs from resuscitation, focusing instead on steady-state replacement to restore and sustain euvolemia over hours to days. Calculations typically account for body weight, with adjustments for specific losses such as urine output, gastrointestinal fluids, or fever-induced increases.
In pediatric patients, the Holliday-Segar formula serves as the standard for estimating maintenance fluid needs, derived from caloric expenditure where 1 mL of water is provided per kcal, equating to approximately 100 mL/kg/day for the first 10 kg of body weight, 50 mL/kg/day for the next 10 kg, and 20 mL/kg/day thereafter. For adults, maintenance requirements are generally 25-30 mL/kg/day, tailored to individual factors like age and renal function, plus additional volumes to replace measurable losses. These estimates cover insensible lossesprimarily through skin and respiration, averaging 500-700 mL/day in adults under normal conditionsand urinary output, which should be monitored to ensure approximately 0.5-1 mL/kg/hour.
Replacement fluids are formulated to match these needs, often using intravenous solutions when oral intake is inadequate, such as in nil per os (NPO) patients. A common regimen for an average adult is 1.5 L of 5% dextrose in 0.45% saline (D5 0.45% NaCl) daily, supplemented with 20 mEq of potassium chloride (KCl) to maintain electrolyte balance and prevent hypokalemia. Adjustments are made for additional losses; for instance, insensible losses increase by about 10% per degree Celsius above 37C due to enhanced evaporation from fever.
Monitoring is essential to guide adjustments and detect imbalances, relying on daily body weights (where a 1 kg change approximates 1 L fluid shift), strict input/output (I/O) balance charting, and clinical signs like mucous membrane turgor. If acute worsening occurs, a fluid bolus may be administered as per resuscitation protocols before resuming maintenance. Laboratory assessments, including serum electrolytes, help confirm euvolemia and prevent complications like hyponatremia. Current guidelines, such as those from the American Academy of Pediatrics, recommend isotonic solutions (e.g., 0.9% saline or balanced crystalloids) for maintenance therapy in children to minimize the risk of hyponatremia, a potentially life-threatening dilutional effect from hypotonic fluids. Similar principles apply to adults, emphasizing isotonic compositions to align with plasma osmolality and reduce adverse events.

Perioperative Fluid Management

Perioperative fluid management encompasses strategies to maintain hemodynamic stability, organ perfusion, and electrolyte balance across the preoperative, intraoperative, and postoperative phases of surgery, tailored to the patient's condition and surgical stress. In the preoperative phase, fluid deficits from fasting are typically minimal due to shortened NPO periodsclear fluids up to 2 hours before surgery in adultsto minimize dehydration risk. When deficits are anticipated, such as in prolonged fasting, they are calculated using maintenance rates like the Holliday-Segar formula (4 mL/kg/hour for the first 10 kg body weight, 2 mL/kg/hour for the next 10 kg, and 1 mL/kg/hour thereafter), multiplied by fasting hours, though routine intravenous replacement is not recommended unless clinical signs of hypovolemia are present.
Intraoperatively, goal-directed fluid therapy (GDFT) is preferred over fixed-volume approaches to optimize stroke volume and avoid both under- and over-resuscitation, using dynamic indices such as stroke volume variation (SVV; typically targeting 10-13% for fluid responsiveness in mechanically ventilated patients under controlled conditions). This involves monitoring via arterial waveform analysis or esophageal Doppler to guide boluses of 250-500 mL crystalloids, aiming for a mildly positive fluid balance of 1-2 L in major elective noncardiac surgery to support tissue oxygenation without excess. Buffered crystalloids (e.g., lactated Ringer's) are the mainstay; colloids are used selectively in cases of severe hypovolemia but are not routinely recommended over crystalloids due to limited evidence of benefit and potential adverse effects.
Postoperatively, a restrictive strategy limits total fluids to less than 2 L per day in most patients to promote recovery and prevent complications like fluid overload, which can prolong ileus and impair wound healing. Enhanced recovery after surgery (ERAS) protocols emphasize this approach, restricting maintenance rates to 1-2 mL/kg/hour while encouraging early oral intake, with dynamic reassessment using lactate levels or passive leg raising tests to guide adjustments. Over-resuscitation risks exacerbating gastrointestinal ileus by increasing bowel edema, particularly in abdominal procedures.
Evidence from large trials supports balanced over extreme strategies; the RELIEF trial (2018), involving over 3,000 patients undergoing major abdominal surgery, found that a restrictive regimen (1.5 mL/kg/hour intraoperative, total 3-4 L/day) did not improve disability-free survival compared to liberal (3 mL/kg/hour, total 5-6 L/day) but was associated with a higher incidence of acute kidney injury (AKI; 8.6% vs. 5.0%), underscoring the need to avoid undue restriction in high-risk cases. Subsequent analyses and guidelines reaffirm that moderately restrictive ERAS-aligned approaches reduce overall complications like pneumonia and ileus by optimizing volume without excess, with meta-analyses showing up to 20-30% relative risk reduction in pulmonary and infectious issues versus liberal regimens in select cohorts. In special scenarios like major abdominal surgery with >20% blood loss, incorporating colloids alongside blood products mitigates hypovolemia-related risks while minimizing crystalloid volumes to curb postoperative ileus.

Fluid Therapy in Acute Kidney Injury

Fluid therapy in acute kidney injury (AKI) requires a cautious approach to optimize renal perfusion while minimizing the risk of exacerbating tubular damage or causing fluid overload. In prerenal AKI, characterized by hypovolemia leading to reduced renal blood flow, early liberal fluid administration is recommended to restore intravascular volume and improve glomerular filtration rate. Conversely, in intrinsic AKI such as acute tubular necrosis (ATN), where renal parenchymal injury predominates, aggressive fluid resuscitation should be avoided to prevent further edema and compartment syndrome in the kidneys. The Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) 2012 clinical practice guideline for AKI emphasizes prompt correction of hypovolemia with isotonic crystalloids in volume-depleted patients, while advising against routine diuretics for prevention or treatment unless volume overload is present. For oliguric AKI, maintenance fluids are typically limited to 0.51 mL/kg/hour to match insensible losses and avoid overload, with adjustments based on urine output and hemodynamic response.
Balanced crystalloid solutions, such as lactated Ringer's or Plasma-Lyte, are preferred over normal saline for fluid resuscitation in AKI due to their lower risk of inducing hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis, which can worsen renal vasoconstriction and acidosis. Large-volume saline administration has been linked to increased AKI incidence and progression compared to balanced solutions in critically ill patients. Post-bolus monitoring is essential; a lack of improvement in serum creatinine (e.g., rise <0.3 mg/dL within 612 hours) or urine output suggests non-responsiveness, prompting discontinuation to prevent overload.
Meta-analyses of randomized trials in sepsis-associated AKI demonstrate that appropriate early fluid resuscitation can reduce AKI progression by approximately 2030% through improved hemodynamics, particularly in volume-responsive cases. However, fluid overload, defined as >10% weight gain from baseline, is associated with worsened outcomes, including higher mortality (odds ratio 1.364.36) and prolonged mechanical ventilation in AKI patients.
In volume-responsive AKI, fluids serve as a temporary bridge to renal replacement therapy (RRT) by stabilizing hemodynamics and allowing time for potential recovery before initiating dialysis, especially when biomarkers like NGAL indicate reversible injury. This strategy integrates fluid challenges with close monitoring of fluid balance to facilitate timely RRT initiation if renal function does not improve.

Administration Strategies

Fluid Bolus and Challenge

A fluid bolus, also known as a fluid challenge, involves the rapid administration of a small volume of intravenous fluid to assess a patient's hemodynamic response and determine fluid responsiveness, particularly in acute settings like shock. This dynamic test helps differentiate between patients who will benefit from further fluid therapythose whose cardiac output increases significantlyand those at risk of overload if fluids are continued unnecessarily. Typically performed in critically ill patients, it guides early resuscitation by evaluating preload dependence without committing to large volumes upfront.
Fluid boluses are not administered automatically solely based on a blood pressure of 90/60 mmHg, as this represents borderline hypotension (often defined as systolic blood pressure <90 mmHg). Initiation depends on clinical context, including signs of hypovolemia (e.g., tachycardia >90 bpm, capillary refill time >2 seconds, low urine output), inadequate perfusion, or specific conditions such as sepsis or trauma. Guidelines, such as those from the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE), recommend assessing for hypovolemia in patients with systolic blood pressure <100 mmHg and administering a 500 mL crystalloid bolus over less than 15 minutes if indicated, followed by reassessment.
The standard protocol for a fluid challenge entails infusing 250-500 mL of crystalloid solution, such as balanced electrolyte solutions, over 10-15 minutes to minimize risks while allowing measurable changes. Response is assessed through methods like the passive leg raise (PLR) maneuver, which simulates a fluid bolus by transiently increasing venous return without actual fluid administration, or by monitoring an increase in stroke volume greater than 10-15%. In mechanically ventilated patients without arrhythmias, stroke volume variation (SVV) below 13% often predicts non-responsiveness, indicating that additional fluids are unlikely to improve hemodynamics. This approach is particularly indicated in undifferentiated shock, where initial vital sign instability requires rapid evaluation to tailor therapy.
According to the European Society of Intensive Care Medicine (ESICM) 2025 guidelines on fluid therapy, fluid challenges are conditionally recommended for early resuscitation in sepsis or septic shock, with an initial total volume limited to up to 30 mL/kg of crystalloids (approximately 2 L for a 70 kg adult) if no hemodynamic improvement is observed after reassessment. Frequent monitoring during and after the challenge is emphasized to optimize outcomes, with evidence graded as very low certainty due to heterogeneous trial data. These guidelines underscore an individualized strategy over fixed protocols to balance resuscitation benefits against potential harms.
Potential risks of fluid boluses include transient fluid overload, which can exacerbate pulmonary edema or impair oxygenation in vulnerable patients, particularly if responsiveness is not confirmed. Administration should cease immediately if no improvement in cardiac output or vital signs occurs, preventing unnecessary exposure to complications like endothelial glycocalyx damage or electrolyte shifts.

Maintenance Fluid Therapy

Maintenance fluid therapy provides continuous, low-rate intravenous fluids to fulfill basal requirements for water, electrolytes, and energy substrates in patients unable to maintain oral intake, such as during acute illness or perioperative NPO status, thereby supporting homeostasis and preventing metabolic derangements like ketosis. This approach differs from bolus administration by focusing on steady replacement of insensible losses rather than acute volume expansion.
The typical regimen for adults involves infusing 1 to 2 mL/kg/hour of an isotonic crystalloid solution, such as lactated Ringer's supplemented with potassium chloride (e.g., 20 mEq/L), to approximate daily needs of 25 to 30 mL/kg of water. Dextrose is often added (e.g., 5% in half-normal saline) to supply calories and prevent catabolism, but in patients with hyperglycemia or uncontrolled diabetes, dextrose-containing fluids like D5W should be avoided or limited to prevent exacerbation of glucose elevation; instead, monitor blood glucose closely and adjust to isotonic saline as needed. Potassium supplementation is essential, typically at 0.2 to 0.3 mEq/kg/hour, unless contraindicated by renal impairment or hyperkalemia.
Core components are calculated to match physiologic losses: approximately 30 mL/kg/day of water, 1 to 2 mmol/kg/day of sodium, and 1 mmol/kg/day of potassium, ensuring balance without excess. These estimates derive from insensible perspiration, urine output, and fecal losses under euvolemic conditions, with adjustments based on serial monitoring of serum electrolytes, urine output, and weight.
In special populations like the elderly, rates should be reduced by about 20% (e.g., to 20 to 25 mL/kg/day) to account for decreased renal concentrating ability, lower muscle mass, and heightened risk of overload leading to pulmonary edema or heart failure. Close monitoring of fluid balance is critical, as elderly patients may tolerate even standard volumes poorly due to comorbidities.
Supporting evidence highlights the preventive role against catabolism, with dextrose provision mitigating starvation ketosis during NPO periods. For pediatric patients (28 days to 18 years), the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) 2018 guidelines strongly endorse isotonic maintenance fluids (e.g., 0.9% saline with 5% dextrose and potassium) over hypotonic solutions, reducing hyponatremia risk by more than 50% (relative risk >2 with hypotonic fluids; number needed to treat: 7.5 for any hyponatremia). This recommendation stems from meta-analyses showing isotonic fluids prevent iatrogenic hyponatremia without increasing hypernatremia or acidosis risks.

De-escalation and Fluid Removal

De-escalation and fluid removal represent the recovery phase of fluid therapy, aimed at eliminating excess volume accumulated during initial resuscitation to restore euvolemia and prevent complications like edema or organ dysfunction. This process typically commences 24-48 hours after the acute resuscitation period, once hemodynamic stability is confirmed and ongoing fluid needs are minimal.
The European Society of Intensive Care Medicine (ESICM) 2025 clinical practice guideline on fluid therapy in adult critically ill patients endorses active fluid removal strategies during the de-escalation phase for those with resolved shock. It provides a conditional recommendation for protocolized diuretic administration over unstructured usual care, based on moderate certainty evidence from 13 randomized controlled trials, to promote safe and effective diuresis. A practical example is the diuretic challenge, involving an initial low dose of furosemide such as 20 mg intravenously, particularly in patients with elevated central venous pressure exceeding 12 mmHg, to evaluate renal responsiveness and tolerance to fluid loss.
Key methods for fluid removal include passive diuresis, achieved by withholding nonessential intravenous fluids and allowing natural excretion, alongside active interventions like loop diuretics for targeted output. In cases of severe fluid overload refractory to diuretics, ultrafiltration via continuous renal replacement therapy may be employed, though the ESICM guideline advises against its routine use absent other indications for renal support, citing low certainty evidence of benefit. The overarching goal is a controlled negative fluid balance of -0.5 to -1 L per day, which has been associated with improved outcomes in sepsis management by mitigating persistent positive balance.
Supporting evidence from the CLASSIC trial, a 2022 multicenter randomized controlled trial involving over 1,500 ICU patients with septic shock, validates a conservative fluid strategy incorporating early de-escalation through restricted administration and diuretic use when appropriate. This approach led to significantly lower cumulative fluid volumes (median 1,798 mL vs. 3,811 mL in the liberal group) and fewer serious adverse events, without increasing 90-day mortality, thereby establishing the safety and feasibility of proactive removal in the ICU setting.
Successful de-escalation requires close assessment of response, with a urine output exceeding 0.5 mL/kg/hour within 2-6 hours post-diuretic challenge indicating adequate renal function and tolerance for ongoing negative balance. This targeted removal, following initial resuscitation efforts, helps transition patients toward recovery while minimizing risks of prolonged overload.

Procedures and Monitoring

Intravenous Access and Administration Procedure

Establishing intravenous (IV) access is a critical procedure for delivering fluid replacement therapy, typically beginning with peripheral venous cannulation unless central access is indicated for large-volume or prolonged administration. Preferred sites for peripheral IV insertion in adults include the veins of the upper extremities, such as the cephalic, basilic, or dorsal metacarpal veins in the forearm or hand, due to lower risks of complications compared to other locations. Lower extremity sites, such as those in the legs or feet, should be avoided in non-emergent cases because they are associated with higher rates of infection, thrombosis, and phlebitis.
The insertion process requires strict aseptic technique to minimize infection risk. Healthcare providers must perform hand hygiene and don clean, non-sterile gloves prior to preparing the site, as sterile gloves are not necessary for routine peripheral IV placement unless the insertion site will be touched after disinfection. The skin at the selected site is cleansed with an alcohol-based solution containing at least 0.5% chlorhexidine gluconate, applied in a back-and-forth motion for at least 30 seconds and allowed to air dry completely to ensure effective antisepsis. A tourniquet is applied proximally to distend the vein, and the catheter is inserted at a 15-30 degree angle using a bevel-up technique, advancing until a flashback of blood is observed, after which the needle is withdrawn and the catheter secured. The catheter is then stabilized with a transparent, semi-permeable dressing that allows visualization of the site while providing a barrier to microbial entry. To confirm patency and clear any residual blood or air, the line is flushed immediately post-insertion with 10 mL of normal saline using a push-pause technique to ensure the catheter lumen is fully cleared. For central venous access, when required for fluid resuscitation, maximal sterile barrier precautions are employed, including a cap, mask, sterile gown, gloves, and full-body drape, along with chlorhexidine skin preparation, in accordance with CDC guidelines for preventing central line-associated bloodstream infections (CLABSI).
Once access is established, fluid administration involves connecting compatible IV tubing to the catheter hub after scrubbing the connection site with an alcohol or chlorhexidine pad for 15 seconds. Infusion rates are calculated based on patient needs, such as maintenance requirements; for example, a standard adult daily fluid goal of 3 L would equate to approximately 125 mL per hour, adjusted for body weight, clinical status, and electrolyte balance. Precision in low-volume or pediatric infusions is achieved using microdrip tubing, which delivers 60 drops per milliliter (gtt/mL), allowing for accurate manual control via the formula: flow rate (gtt/min) = (volume in mL 60 gtt/mL) / time in minutes. Electronic infusion pumps are preferred for most settings to maintain consistent rates and reduce errors, with tubing changed every 96 hours or sooner if compromised.
Ongoing maintenance of the IV site is essential to prevent complications. Peripheral catheters should be routinely assessed every shift for signs of infection or infiltration, including redness, warmth, pain, swelling, or leakage at the insertion site, with immediate removal if present. Routine replacement of peripheral IV catheters is recommended every 72-96 hours in adults to reduce the risk of phlebitis and bloodstream infections, unless clinically indicated earlier; however, recent evidence supports extending dwell time beyond 96 hours in the absence of complications to preserve vein integrity. Dressings are replaced if soiled, loose, or damp, using chlorhexidine-impregnated options where appropriate for added antimicrobial protection, and the site is flushed with 5-10 mL of saline after any intermittent use or blood draw. Adherence to these protocols aligns with updated CDC recommendations, which emphasize evidence-based practices to achieve CLABSI rates below 1 per 1,000 catheter-days in high-compliance settings.

Assessing Fluid Status and Responsiveness

Assessing fluid status involves evaluating a patient's volume status and potential response to fluid administration to guide therapy effectively, preventing both under- and over-resuscitation in conditions like shock and hypovolemia. This process integrates clinical examination, hemodynamic monitoring, and laboratory assessments to determine preload adequacy and predict whether additional fluids will improve cardiac output and tissue perfusion. Accurate assessment is crucial in critical care settings, where inappropriate fluid management can lead to complications such as organ dysfunction.
Clinical examination is essential for identifying signs of hypovolemia and inadequate tissue perfusion that may indicate the need for fluid administration. These include tachycardia (heart rate >90 beats per minute), prolonged capillary refill time (>2 seconds), reduced urine output (<0.5 mL/kg/hour), cold peripheries, or altered mental status. Intravenous fluid therapy should not be initiated automatically for a blood pressure of 90/60 mmHg alone, as this represents borderline hypotension (typically defined as systolic blood pressure <90 mmHg or mean arterial pressure <65 mmHg). Instead, initiation of fluid therapy depends on the overall clinical picture, including the presence of these signs of hypovolemia or inadequate perfusion, or specific conditions such as sepsis or trauma. Guidelines suggest assessing for hypovolemia in patients with systolic blood pressure <100 mmHg and administering a 500 mL crystalloid bolus over less than 15 minutes if indicated, followed by reassessment. In sepsis-induced hypoperfusion or septic shock, at least 30 mL/kg of intravenous crystalloid fluid is recommended within the first 3 hours.
Static measures provide baseline indicators of fluid status but have limitations in predicting responsiveness. Central venous pressure (CVP) is a traditional static parameter, with a target of 8-12 mmHg often used during resuscitation in septic shock to indicate adequate preload, though it does not reliably predict increases in cardiac output with fluids. Urine output serves as another static marker of renal perfusion, with a goal of greater than 0.5 mL/kg/hour in adults during hypovolemic resuscitation to ensure end-organ function is maintained. These measures are simple and widely available but are influenced by factors like cardiac compliance and vasopressor use, reducing their specificity for fluid responsiveness.
Dynamic parameters offer a more reliable assessment by evaluating changes in hemodynamics during mechanical ventilation or maneuvers like passive leg raising, reflecting the heart's position on the Frank-Starling curve. Pulse pressure variation (PPV), calculated from arterial waveform analysis, predicts fluid responsiveness with high accuracy in mechanically ventilated patients without spontaneous breathing; a PPV greater than 13% indicates likely benefit from fluid bolus, with pooled sensitivity of 88% and specificity of 89% in meta-analyses of critically ill patients. Inferior vena cava (IVC) ultrasound assesses respiratory variability, where an IVC collapsibility index exceeding 40% in spontaneously breathing patients may suggest fluid responsiveness, with moderate accuracy (e.g., sensitivity 93%, specificity 71% in one study of shocked patients), though overall reliability is limited compared to mechanically ventilated patients; passive leg raising is often preferred. These dynamic indices outperform static measures by accounting for cyclic preload changes, particularly in controlled ventilation settings with tidal volumes above 8 mL/kg.
Biomarkers complement hemodynamic assessments by signaling tissue hypoperfusion or early organ injury, aiding decisions on fluid therapy initiation or cessation. Elevated lactate levels greater than 2 mmol/L indicate hypoperfusion in sepsis and shock, prompting resuscitation efforts to normalize clearance and improve outcomes, as levels above this threshold correlate with increased mortality risk. Neutrophil gelatinase-associated lipocalin (NGAL), detectable in plasma or urine, serves as an early biomarker for acute kidney injury (AKI), rising 36-48 hours before creatinine elevations and guiding protective fluid strategies in at-risk critically ill patients, with admission levels predicting AKI development at 48 hours (AUC 0.81).
Current evidence from the European Society of Intensive Care Medicine (ESICM) 2025 guidelines strongly favors dynamic parameters over static ones like CVP for assessing fluid responsiveness in circulatory shock, recommending their use in eligible patients to optimize resuscitation and avoid liberal fluid strategies (as of November 2025). Goal-directed fluid therapy incorporating dynamic indices, such as PPV, has been shown in meta-analyses to reduce postoperative morbidity (odds ratio 0.51) and ICU length of stay, thereby minimizing unnecessary fluid administration and associated risks in surgical and critical care contexts.

Complications and Risks

Fluid Overload

Fluid overload refers to the excessive accumulation of fluid in the extracellular space, resulting in tissue edema and potential organ dysfunction, particularly in the context of intravenous fluid therapy during resuscitation or maintenance. This condition arises from a sustained positive fluid balance, often assessed through a sustained positive fluid balance, such as an increase exceeding 5-10% of body weight over several days, which elevates capillary hydrostatic pressure and disrupts the Starling forces governing fluid movement across vascular walls. The increased pressure forces fluid into the interstitium, leading to pulmonary edemawhere fluid accumulates in the alveoli impairing gas exchangeand ascites, characterized by abdominal fluid collection that can compress organs and exacerbate respiratory distress.
Clinical manifestations of fluid overload provide key diagnostic clues for prompt intervention. Patients often exhibit rapid weight gain exceeding 0.5 kg per day due to retained fluid, reflecting overall volume expansion. Physical examination may reveal pulmonary crackles from alveolar fluid buildup, signaling early pulmonary edema, alongside peripheral edema and jugular venous distension. Laboratory assessment frequently shows elevated B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP) levels above 100 pg/mL, a biomarker released in response to ventricular wall stress from volume expansion, aiding in distinguishing overload from other causes of dyspnea.
Several factors predispose patients to fluid overload, emphasizing the need for tailored fluid strategies. Liberal resuscitation approaches, such as administering more than 4 L of fluids per day, heighten risk by overwhelming the body's compensatory mechanisms for fluid excretion. Preexisting cardiac dysfunction impairs forward flow and increases venous pressure, while renal impairment limits natriuresis and diuresis, both amplifying fluid retention and accelerating progression to overload.
Effective management prioritizes restoring euvolemia to mitigate complications, with diuretics forming the cornerstone of therapy to induce a negative fluid balance. Loop diuretics like furosemide are administered to promote urine output, targeting a reduction of 1-2 L per day while monitoring for electrolyte shifts. Concurrently, fluid intake is restricted to approximately 1 mL/kg/hr, aligned with outputs to prevent further accumulation, often guided by daily weights and hemodynamic parameters. Observational and interventional studies demonstrate that early recognition and de-escalation of fluids in overload states are linked to improved outcomes, including lower mortality rates in critically ill populations by avoiding prolonged positive balances.

Electrolyte and Acid-Base Disturbances

In fluid therapy, including for acute kidney injury (AKI) and broader critical care settings such as sepsis or perioperative care, inappropriate selection of fluid type or administration rate can precipitate electrolyte and acid-base disturbances, particularly hyponatremia and hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis. Hyponatremia, defined as serum sodium concentration below 135 mmol/L, commonly arises from the use of hypotonic maintenance fluids, which dilute plasma sodium due to antidiuretic hormone release in stressed patients; the risk of this complication is approximately 5-10% during maintenance therapy in hospitalized adults with AKI. Similarly, administration of 0.9% normal saline can induce hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis through excess chloride load, leading to a reduction in strong ion difference and a pH decrease of about 0.1-0.2 units per liter infused, exacerbating acid-base imbalance in renal impairment.
Prevention of these disturbances emphasizes the choice of balanced crystalloid solutions, such as lactated Ringer's or Plasma-Lyte, over hypotonic or unbalanced saline fluids, as they more closely mimic plasma composition and minimize sodium dilution or chloride excess. The SMART trial (2018) and subsequent reviews up to 2024 support balanced crystalloids reducing risks of AKI and mortality compared to saline in critically ill patients. In high-risk AKI patients, such as those with oliguria or ongoing inflammation, serum sodium levels should be monitored every 6-12 hours to enable early detection and adjustment of therapy.
For treatment of severe hyponatremia (typically <120 mmol/L with neurological symptoms), 3% hypertonic saline is administered intravenously, with a correction rate not exceeding 0.5 mmol/L per hour initially to avoid osmotic demyelination syndrome; a bolus of 100-150 mL over 10-20 minutes may be repeated up to three times for acute seizures, followed by slower infusion targeting 4-8 mmol/L rise in the first 24 hours. A 2023 meta-analysis indicates that isotonic maintenance fluids reduce the risk of severe hyponatremia compared to hypotonic fluids in hospitalized patients, with benefits up to 78% risk reduction observed, supporting their preferential use in critically ill patients including those with AKI.
