Paris in the Twentieth Century
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Paris in the Twentieth Century (French: Paris au XXe sicle) is a science fiction novel by French author Jules Verne. Written in 1863 but first published posthumously in 1994, the work depicts a technologically advanced but culturally stagnant Paris in the year 1960, where commerce and science dominate at the expense of the arts and humanities.
The story centers on Michel Dufrnoy, a young poet who graduates with honors in literature but struggles to find his place in a society that values practical skills over artistic pursuits. After failing to secure employment in his field, Michel faces poverty, rejection in love, and eventual despair, highlighting the dehumanizing effects of industrialization and utilitarianism.
Verne's manuscript was rejected by his publisher Pierre-Jules Hetzel, who deemed it too pessimistic and unrealistic compared to Verne's more optimistic adventure novels. It remained unpublished during his lifetime and was rediscovered in 1989 by Verne's great-grandson Jean Verne in a family safe. The novel's release revealed Verne's prescient visions, including gasoline-powered vehicles, skyscrapers, fax machines, and a global communications network resembling the internet.

Background and Composition

Verne's Early Writing Career

Jules Verne was born on February 8, 1828, in Nantes, France, the eldest of five children in a prosperous bourgeois family; his father was a magistrate and lawyer who expected his son to follow in his professional footsteps. In 1847, Verne's father sent him to Paris to study law at the city's institutions, amid the political turbulence of the July Monarchy's final years. He passed his first-year examinations but, after a brief return to Nantes for further preparation, Verne abandoned legal pursuits in favor of literature, drawn to the vibrant cultural scene of the capital where he had already begun composing dramatic works.
During the 1850s, Verne established himself as an emerging playwright and short story writer, frequenting Paris's literary salons and theaters. In 1849, he met the renowned author Alexandre Dumas through a mutual acquaintance, leading to a collaboration with Dumas's son on the comedy Les Pailles rompues (Broken Straws), which premiered successfully at the Thtre Historique on June 12, 1850. Verne contributed librettos and comic operas to the newly founded Thtre Lyrique, where he served as secretary from 1851 under director Jules Seveste, producing works such as Le Colin-maillard (1853) and Les Compagnons de la Marjolaine (1855) in partnership with composer Aristide Hignard and librettist Michel Carr. He also published short stories in the magazine Muse des familles, including the adventure tale "The First Ships of the Mexican Navy" (1851), the proto-science fiction piece "A Voyage in a Balloon" (1851), and the novella Martin Paz (1852), which showcased his growing interest in blending historical and speculative elements.
Verne's early forays into science-infused narratives were shaped by the rapid technological progress of the Second French Empire (18521870), including expansions in railways, steam navigation, and industrial innovations that fueled his conceptual "Roman de la Science"a vision of literature merging adventure with scientific education. This period marked the genesis of his distinctive style, evident in stories like "Master Zacharius" (1854), a fantasy exploring clockwork mechanics, and polar adventure "A Winter Amid the Ice" (1854). In 1862, a pivotal introduction to publisher Pierre-Jules Hetzel via mutual friend Alfred de Brhat led Verne to submit a ballooning manuscript, which Hetzel revised and published as Five Weeks in a Balloon in January 1863; this debut novel launched the Voyages Extraordinaires series, securing Verne's career as a prolific author of imaginative scientific voyages.

Manuscript Development in 1863

In 1863, shortly after the success of his debut novel Five Weeks in a Balloon, which established his partnership with publisher Pierre-Jules Hetzel, Jules Verne completed and submitted the manuscript of Paris in the Twentieth Century to Hetzel for consideration. This work marked an early foray into speculative fiction for Verne, crafted amid his rising career in scientific romances.
The novel's structure centers on a third-person narrative tracking the struggles of young protagonist Michel Dufrnoy, a recent literature graduate and aspiring poet, as he navigates a mechanized, commerce-dominated Paris set in 1960. Through a series of loosely connected episodes, the story unfolds in a drawing-room style, emphasizing descriptive catalogs of futuristic inventions and societal shifts rather than a tightly plotted adventure, which contrasted with Verne's more action-oriented works.
Verne drew influences from the transformative events of mid-19th-century France, including the Industrial Revolution's rapid mechanization, which he extrapolated into a dehumanizing economic order stifling artistic expression. The ongoing Haussmann renovation of Paris (18531868), commissioned by Napoleon III to modernize the city's infrastructure with wide boulevards and uniform architecture, informed Verne's vision of an orderly yet soulless urban expanse dominated by steel and glass. Emerging technologies of the era, such as widespread gas lighting illuminating Paris streets since the 1820s and experimental internal combustion engines hinting at motorized transport, shaped his predictions of electric illumination, gasoline-powered vehicles, and automated systems.
The manuscript, estimated at around 38,000 words, represented a thematic departure for Verne, veering into pessimism that critiqued the erosion of humanities amid technological progressa stark contrast to the optimistic adventures in his typical oeuvre. This dystopian tone, influenced partly by Edgar Allan Poe's portrayals of industrial alienation, highlighted fears of a future where commerce supplanted culture.

Publication History

Publisher's Rejection

In 1863, shortly after the success of Jules Verne's debut novel Five Weeks in a Balloon under publisher Pierre-Jules Hetzel, Verne submitted the manuscript of Paris in the Twentieth Century for consideration. Hetzel rejected it decisively in a letter dated December 1863, deeming the work unsuitable for publication.
Hetzel's rejection centered on the manuscript's overly pessimistic tone and absence of adventurous elements, which he believed would undermine Verne's emerging reputation as an author of engaging scientific romances. In his letter, Hetzel wrote: "My dear Verne, I would give almost anything not to have to write you today. You have undertaken an impossible task and, like your predecessors in such matters, you have not been able to pull it off well. It is much below the level of your Five Weeks in a Balloon... It is tabloidish, and the topic is ill-chosen... lackluster and lifeless." He further criticized the work's "grandiose dialogues" as "phony" and "tiresome," noting they lacked the heroic vitality expected in adventure literature, and warned: "I am truly sorry to have to tell you this, but I believe that publishing this would be a disaster for your reputation." Hetzel advised Verne to "wait twenty years, and then try it again," emphasizing the novel's depiction of a bleak future as unappealing to contemporary readers: "even if you were a prophet, no one today would believe this prophecy... they simply would not be interested in it."
Verne accepted Hetzel's judgment without protest, complying by shifting his focus to more optimistic and adventure-driven narratives that aligned with the publisher's vision for the Voyages Extraordinaires series. This redirection led directly to works such as Journey to the Center of the Earth, published in 1864, which helped solidify Verne's commercial success.
Following the rejection, the manuscript was archived in Hetzel's files and remained there untouched, with Verne making no further efforts to revise or resubmit it during his lifetime.

Discovery and 1994 Release

The manuscript of Paris in the Twentieth Century, originally submitted to publisher Pierre-Jules Hetzel in 1863 and rejected for its pessimistic tone, was held in the Verne family archives for over a century.
In 1989, Jules Verne's great-grandson Jean Jules-Verne brought the manuscript to public attention while reviewing family documents; although a dramatic story of its discovery in a forgotten safe deposit box was promoted by publisher Hachette upon release, Verne scholars have noted that its existence was previously known and the tale served as a marketing ploy.
Verne expert Piero Gondolo della Riva conducted a thorough authentication in 1989, confirming the text as Verne's unaltered 1863 work through paleographic analysis and comparison with known manuscripts; he later established the definitive edition and contributed its preface. This validation dispelled any doubts about the document's origin, paving the way for its release.
The novel appeared in France as Paris au XXe sicle on September 14, 1994, published by Hachette, marking the first posthumous unveiling of one of Verne's early submissions. The English edition, translated by Richard Howard, followed from Random House in November 1996. The publication sparked widespread media interest, including features on U.S. network news, National Public Radio, and in The New York Times, contributing to its immediate commercial success as a bestseller.

Narrative Content

Plot Overview

Paris in the Twentieth Century, written by Jules Verne in 1863 and set in the year 1960, follows the struggles of Michel Dufrnoy, a 16-year-old orphan and aspiring poet, in a dystopian Paris dominated by commerce, technology, and mechanization. The narrative unfolds in a third-person linear structure across episodic chapters that trace Michel's personal decline against the backdrop of a transformed cityscape featuring towering glass skyscrapers, electric-powered vehicles, and automated educational institutions where students learn via mechanical tutors rather than human instruction. Upon graduating from a highly standardized school system that emphasizes technical proficiency over humanities, Michel receives a dismal job outlook, reflecting the society's disdain for artistic pursuits.
Desperate for employment, Michel secures a low-level clerical role at Le Grand Livre, a vast banking institution symbolizing the era's financial obsession, but his idealistic nature clashes with the rigid, efficiency-driven environment. His unrequited affection for Lucy Richelot, the granddaughter of his rhetoric professor, introduces emotional turmoil, exacerbating his professional setbacks when a misinterpreted gesture leads to his dismissal. Relocated to a nationalized entertainment depot, Michel briefly attempts to adapt by writing utilitarian scripts, but disillusionment drives him to pursue independent poetry, resulting in repeated rejections from publishers who demand only practical, market-oriented content. As winter sets in, Michel's descent into poverty intensifies, marked by starvation and eviction from his modest lodgings.
Wandering the cold, impersonal streets of Parisnow cluttered with automated trams, gas-lit boulevards overshadowed by commercial monoliths, and remnants of 19th-century architecture decaying amid progressMichel embodies profound alienation in a society that prioritizes material advancement over human connection. The plot culminates in his tragic isolation, freezing to death alone after a final, futile search for Lucy, underscoring the dehumanizing toll of unchecked industrialization.

Key Characters and Setting

The novel's protagonist, Michel Dufrnoy, is a 16-year-old graduate specializing in rhetoric and poetry, whose passion for classical literature and the humanities sets him at odds with a society that prioritizes scientific and commercial pursuits. Living with his uncle and aunt, Stanislas and Athnais Boutardin, in a modest household, Michel represents the fading romantic ideal in an era dominated by utilitarianism. His artistic inclinations are nurtured by family members like Uncle Huguenin, who provides a semblance of cultural continuity amid the encroaching mechanization.
Supporting characters include Michel's love interest, Lucy Richelot, the 15-year-old granddaughter of his rhetoric professor, whose blond curls and blue eyes symbolize a fragile remnant of beauty and emotion in the utilitarian world. Michel's cousin, Athanase Boutardin, and his musician friend Quinsonnas, a scribe at the Ledger office, offer companionship but also highlight the struggles of those outside the technological elite. Bureaucratic figures, such as the Director-General of the Central Administration of the Bank of Deposits, embody the impersonal authority of the state-controlled economy, overseeing operations with mechanical efficiency as a high-ranking official.
The story is set in Paris during August 1960, transformed into a sprawling megalopolis powered entirely by electricity generated from turbines in the Seine River, featuring broad asphalt streets filled with internal-combustion engine vehicles and connected to the ocean via a Suez-like canal that turns the city into a major seaport. Transportation includes high-speed pneumatic tube-trains suspended overhead on iron frameworks and underground passenger rail systems, facilitating rapid movement through the overcrowded urban expanse where glass skyscrapers dominate the skyline. Factories and industrial operations extend underground, contributing to a perpetual haze of smoke and fog that envelops the city, lit at night by artificial gas and electric illumination from a giant lighthouse near the site of what would become the Eiffel Tower.
Social divisions are stark, with a rigid class structure separating the wealthy elite in their electrified, automated residencesequipped with anti-burglar alarms and calculating machinesfrom the impoverished working masses relegated to polluted suburbs and decaying nineteenth-century neighborhoods. Cultural landmarks from the 1860s, such as historical monuments, have largely eroded or been overshadowed by utilitarian structures, erasing much of the city's artistic heritage in favor of functional infrastructure like office towers and elevated rail lines. The atmosphere is one of harsh winters and constant chill, exacerbated by industrial emissions, creating a cold, impersonal environment where human warmth struggles against the mechanical pulse of progress.

Themes and Predictions

Technological Forecasts

In Jules Verne's Paris in the Twentieth Century, written in 1863, the author extrapolates from contemporary inventions to envision a range of technologies that would define urban life nearly a century later. These forecasts, set in a futuristic Paris of 1960, demonstrate Verne's keen observation of emerging scientific trends, particularly in communication, computation, and transportation, including gasoline-powered cars clogging streets, skyscrapers, fax machines, an internet-like global communication network, elevators, electric street lighting, calculators, weapons of mass destruction, and electronic music. Among the most accurate predictions are systems for rapid document transmission, described as pneumatic tubes carrying messages and papers across the city, which closely resemble the pneumatic tube networks and early fax machines that became operational in offices and post systems by the mid-20th century. Similarly, Verne anticipates electronic calculators as devices for swift numerical processing in educational and professional settings, aligning with the proliferation of compact electronic calculators in the 1960s, such as those produced by companies like Friden and Hewlett-Packard.
Verne also foresees urban traffic congestion caused by "horseless carriages"gasoline-powered self-propelled vehicles navigating crowded boulevardswhich mirrors the automobile boom of the 1960s, when gasoline-powered cars overwhelmed Paris streets, leading to widespread gridlock and the expansion of road infrastructure. Other prescient elements include towering skyscrapers constructed from glass and steel frameworks, equipped with elevators for vertical mobility; these echo the architectural shift seen in 1960s high-rises like the Tour Maine-Montparnasse in Paris, built with similar materials and reaching unprecedented heights, alongside electric street lighting illuminating urban expanses. Electric-powered vehicles appear as efficient alternatives to traditional transport, prefiguring the experimental electric cars of the era, such as the 1960s prototypes from Renault, though internal combustion engines dominated. Automated banking systems, where mechanical devices handle deposits and withdrawals without human intervention, anticipate the introduction of early automated teller machines (ATMs) in the 1960s, beginning with trials in London and spreading to Europe. Verne further envisions weapons of mass destruction capable of widespread devastation and electronic music generated by mechanical means, reflecting early experiments in sound synthesis that emerged later in the century.
Comparisons to actual 1960s developments highlight the novel's foresight in computing and transit. Verne's depiction of advanced calculating engines parallels the rise of mainframe computers, such as IBM's System/360 series released in 1964, which revolutionized data processing in businesses and governments. Likewise, the underground rail networks described for rapid urban movement align with the expansion of the Paris Mtro, which by the 1960s featured extensive automated signaling and electric trains serving millions daily, while a global communication network akin to the internet enables instantaneous information exchange worldwide.
These predictions stem from Verne's grounding in 1860s innovations, including the electric telegraph, which enabled near-instantaneous messaging over wires and inspired the novel's communication tubes. Early steam engines and experimental internal combustion prototypes, like those demonstrated by tienne Lenoir in 1860, informed visions of mechanized vehicles and automated systems, allowing Verne to project incremental advancements into a mechanized future.

Social and Economic Dystopia

In Jules Verne's Paris in the Twentieth Century, society in 1960 Paris is portrayed as a stark utilitarian dystopia, where the arts and humanities are systematically devalued in favor of engineering, commerce, and technology, reflecting a broader critique of industrialization's impact on human culture. Education prioritizes vocational training in sciences, business, and technology, rendering classical literature and poetry obsolete, with professorships in literature rumored to be suppressed by 1962 and humanities declining amid a shift toward practical, profit-oriented disciplines. Poetry itself shifts to celebrate technological feats rather than nature or emotion, underscoring a cultural shift toward pure functionality in a commerce-dominated society. This devaluation manifests in the protagonist Michel Dufrnoy's struggles as a literature graduate, unable to find employment in a world that dismisses artistic pursuits as impractical.
Economically, the novel depicts a capitalist system dominated by monopolistic corporations that control nearly every aspect of life, from education to media, fostering a landscape of exploitation and inequality. These entities, such as the Academic Credit Union under figures like Baron de Vercampin, enforce a rigid hierarchy where commerce dictates social value, leading to widespread unemployment as automation displaces human labor in factories and offices. Relationships become commodified, with marriage and personal bonds contingent on financial status, turning human connections into transactions within this profit-driven framework. Such elements highlight Verne's prescient critique of unchecked capitalism, where economic efficiency overrides social welfare.
Socially, the narrative critiques the suppression of individualism in favor of conformity, as citizens navigate a mechanized existence devoid of personal expression or emotional depth. Gender roles are rigidly enforced amid industrialization, with women relegated to grueling factory work, eroding traditional family structures and contributing to societal fragmentation. The once-romantic essence of Parisits boulevards, cafes, and bohemian spiritis lost to a homogenized, industrialized urban sprawl, transforming the City of Light into a joyless, uniform expanse marked by overpopulation and speculation. This portrayal foreshadows 20th-century issues of urban alienation, where inhabitants feel isolated in a "deadly capital" overwhelmed by poverty, pollution, and mechanical routine, evoking the existential disconnection of modern megacities.

Critical Reception and Legacy

Initial and Posthumous Reviews

Upon its posthumous publication in 1994, Paris in the Twentieth Century garnered significant attention for its remarkably prescient depictions of future technologies, including fax machines, gas-powered automobiles, elevated rail systems, and electronic calculators, which reviewers highlighted as astonishingly accurate given the novel's 1863 composition. However, critics frequently noted the work's literary shortcomings, such as wooden and stereotypical characters who behave as if trapped in a conventional mid-19th-century romance, complete with melodramatic elements like sudden adultery accusations and tragic deaths from exposure. The narrative's stark, plaintive tone and episodic structure were seen as dyspeptic and depressing, lacking the engaging adventure and optimism that characterized Verne's later successes like Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea.
In contrast to Verne's typical "boys-with-toys" techno-optimism, the novel's outlier status as an early dystopian visionevoking the bleak industrial alienation of a mechanized societywas praised for its historical significance but critiqued for underdeveloped plotting and unsubtle satire that presumed familiarity with 1860s French cultural debates. The English translation by Richard Howard, released in 1996, was commended for fidelity to the original.
Academic responses in the 1990s and 2000s emphasized the novel's immaturity as an early Verne effort, viewing it as structurally weak with thin characterization and a pessimistic worldview that deviated from his publisher's preferred adventurous style, yet valuable as a precursor to dystopian science fiction. Arthur B. Evans, in a 1995 analysis, positioned it within the evolution of the genre, noting its anti-utopian elements as a bold but unpolished experiment in futuristic speculation. Scholars like those in Science Fiction Studies further explored its literary flaws, such as episodic pacing and overt didacticism, while affirming its role in redefining Verne's legacy beyond mere prophecy.
The book achieved immediate commercial success, becoming a bestseller in France upon its 1994 release by Hachette, with hundreds of thousands of copies sold and sparking international interest in Verne's unpublished manuscripts. No major literary awards were conferred, but its discovery and publication revitalized scholarly interest in Verne's early career.

Modern Interpretations

In recent scholarly examinations, Paris in the Twentieth Century has been interpreted as a prescient critique of neoliberal capitalism, emphasizing the novel's portrayal of a society dominated by industrialists and bankers who prioritize profit over human values. Murielle El Hajj's 2022 analysis, "Beyond Utopia: The Dystopian Capitalist Society in Paris in the Twentieth Century (1863)," argues that Verne anticipated key elements of neoliberalism, including the exploitation of workers through mechanized labor and the commodification of education and culture. El Hajj highlights how the novel depicts a utilitarian Paris where vocational training supplants liberal arts, and technological progress fosters environmental degradation and social alienation, drawing parallels to contemporary precarious employment and corporate dominance.
This perspective extends to discussions of automation and job loss in the digital age, where Verne's vision of a tech-driven economy displacing creative professions resonates with AI's impact on labor markets. In their 2020 study published in Ekonomik Yaklam, Zeynep Ademir and Deniz Abukan examine the novel's depiction of science and technology under capitalism as a double-edged sword, leading to widespread unemployment and societal misery despite material advancements. They note Verne's foresight into state-facilitated technicalization of universities and industries, which mirrors modern automation-induced economic disruptions and inequality.
Interpretations also link the novel's environmental motifssuch as a sudden climatic cooling disrupting urban lifeto contemporary climate fiction, portraying Paris as a climate-alienated metropolis. Merve Tabur's 2024 article in Utopian Studies frames the work as an early dystopian response to Haussmann-era urbanism, influencing modern eco-dystopian narratives that critique techno-futurist planning and its ecological fallout in cities facing global warming. Tabur connects this to broader speculative fiction exploring inequality and environmental collapse, underscoring Verne's role in shaping visions of unsustainable urban futures.
