Stars are the fundamental building blocks of the visible universe. They are enormous spheres of hot gas, primarily hydrogen and helium, held together by gravity and powered by nuclear fusion reactions in their cores. Every element heavier than hydrogen and helium was created inside a star. Every atom of carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, iron, gold, and uranium on Earth was forged in the nuclear furnace of a star that lived and died before our solar system existed. Stars are not just distant points of light. They are the engines of cosmic chemistry, the factories that produce the raw materials of planets, oceans, atmospheres, and life itself.

A star is born when a cloud of gas and dust in space, called a nebula, begins to collapse under its own gravity. The collapse may be triggered by the shockwave from a nearby supernova, by the gravitational influence of a passing star, or by the compression of gas as it spirals into a galaxy's spiral arms. As the cloud collapses, it heats up. The denser the core becomes, the hotter it gets. When the core temperature reaches approximately ten million degrees Kelvin, hydrogen nuclei begin to fuse into helium nuclei in a process called nuclear fusion. Each fusion reaction converts a tiny amount of mass into an enormous amount of energy, following Einstein's famous equation E equals mc squared. This energy, released as light and heat, creates an outward pressure that balances the inward pull of gravity, and the star reaches a stable equilibrium. A star is born.

The life of a star is determined by its mass. Massive stars burn hotter and brighter but exhaust their fuel quickly. The most massive stars, those with fifty or more times the mass of our Sun, live only a few million years. Our Sun, a relatively modest star, will live for approximately ten billion years, of which it has already burned through about five billion. The smallest stars, called red dwarfs, burn so slowly that they can last for trillions of years, far longer than the current age of the universe. The vast majority of stars in the Milky Way are red dwarfs.

Our Sun is a middle-aged, medium-sized star classified as a yellow dwarf, or more precisely, a G-type main-sequence star. Its surface temperature is approximately fifty-five hundred degrees Celsius. Its core temperature is approximately fifteen million degrees Celsius. Every second, it converts approximately six hundred million tons of hydrogen into helium through nuclear fusion, releasing energy equivalent to billions of nuclear bombs. The light that reaches Earth from the Sun took approximately eight minutes to travel the ninety-three million miles between us.

When a star like our Sun exhausts the hydrogen in its core, it undergoes dramatic changes. The core contracts and heats up while the outer layers expand enormously, turning the star into a red giant. Our Sun will become a red giant in approximately five billion years, swelling to a size that may engulf the orbits of Mercury, Venus, and possibly Earth. After the red giant phase, the outer layers are expelled into space as a beautiful glowing shell of gas called a planetary nebula, while the core collapses into a white dwarf, a dense, Earth-sized remnant that slowly cools over billions of years.

Massive stars die more spectacularly. When a star with more than eight times the mass of the Sun runs out of fuel, the core collapse happens so rapidly and so violently that it triggers a supernova, one of the most energetic events in the universe. For a few weeks, a single supernova can outshine an entire galaxy of hundreds of billions of stars. The explosion scatters heavy elements into space, enriching the interstellar medium with the raw materials for new stars and planets. The remnant core either becomes a neutron star, an incredibly dense object where the mass of the Sun is compressed into a sphere about ten miles in diameter, or if the original star was massive enough, a black hole, an object so dense that not even light can escape its gravitational pull.

Galaxies are the largest gravitationally bound structures in the universe. They are enormous collections of stars, gas, dust, dark matter, and empty space, held together by gravity. The smallest galaxies contain a few hundred million stars. The largest contain trillions. Our galaxy, the Milky Way, is estimated to contain between one hundred and four hundred billion stars, along with at least one hundred billion planets.

The Milky Way is a barred spiral galaxy approximately one hundred thousand light-years in diameter. Our solar system is located about twenty-six thousand light-years from the center, in one of the galaxy's spiral arms. It takes our solar system approximately two hundred and thirty million years to complete one orbit around the galactic center, a period sometimes called a "galactic year." The Milky Way has completed approximately twenty galactic years since the formation of the solar system.

At the center of the Milky Way, and at the center of most large galaxies, lurks a supermassive black hole. The Milky Way's central black hole, called Sagittarius A star, has a mass of approximately four million times that of our Sun. Despite its enormous mass, it is remarkably quiescent compared to the active galactic nuclei in some other galaxies, where supermassive black holes are actively consuming matter and producing jets of energy that can extend for thousands of light-years.

Galaxies come in several types. Spiral galaxies, like the Milky Way and the Andromeda galaxy, have a flat disk with spiral arms of stars, gas, and dust surrounding a central bulge. Elliptical galaxies are smooth, featureless collections of older stars that range from nearly spherical to highly elongated. Irregular galaxies have no defined shape and are often the result of gravitational interactions or collisions with other galaxies.

Galaxy collisions are common on cosmic timescales. The Milky Way is currently on a collision course with the Andromeda galaxy, our nearest large galactic neighbor at approximately two and a half million light-years away. The two galaxies are approaching each other at approximately seventy miles per second and will begin merging in approximately four and a half billion years. The merger will take approximately two billion years to complete and will result in a new, larger elliptical galaxy that astronomers have tentatively named "Milkomeda." Despite the violence of the collision, the spaces between stars are so vast that very few individual stars will actually collide.

The observable universe contains an estimated two trillion galaxies, organized into a vast cosmic web of galaxy clusters, superclusters, filaments, and voids. The largest structures in the universe are galaxy filaments, vast threads of galaxies stretching across hundreds of millions of light-years, separated by enormous voids where few or no galaxies exist. This web-like structure, revealed by galaxy surveys conducted over the past few decades, is one of the most beautiful and humbling sights in all of science: a cosmic architecture of staggering scale and complexity, organized by gravity over billions of years from the tiny quantum fluctuations that existed in the first moments after the Big Bang.

The study of stars and galaxies is not just an academic exercise. It is the study of our origins. Every atom in your body was once inside a star. The calcium in your bones, the iron in your blood, the phosphorus in your DNA, all of it was synthesized through nuclear reactions in the cores and explosions of stars that died before the Sun was born. You are, in the most literal sense, a product of stellar evolution. Understanding stars and galaxies is understanding the cosmic process that produced you.

When you look up at the night sky and see the faint band of the Milky Way stretching across the darkness, you are looking at the light of hundreds of billions of suns, each one a furnace of creation, each one contributing to the ongoing story of cosmic chemistry that produced planets, oceans, atmospheres, and at least one species of curious creatures who learned to look up, ask questions, and figure out what they were seeing.
