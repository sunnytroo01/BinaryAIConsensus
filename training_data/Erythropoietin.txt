Erythropoietin

1x
 Erythropoietin (EPO), also known as erythropoetin, is a glycoprotein hormone primarily produced by the peritubular cells of the kidney in response to low oxygen levels (hypoxia), serving as the key regulator of erythropoiesis, the process of red blood cell production in the bone marrow. This hormone stimulates the proliferation and differentiation of erythroid progenitor cells, ensuring the daily generation of approximately 200 billion new red blood cells to maintain adequate oxygen delivery throughout the body. Beyond its central role in hematopoiesis, EPO exhibits pleiotropic effects, including cytoprotection in various tissues such as the brain, heart, and retina, where it promotes cell survival under stress conditions like ischemia.
The production of EPO is tightly controlled by hypoxia-inducible factors (HIFs), a family of transcription factors that sense oxygen availability and upregulate the EPO gene in renal interstitial cells when tissue oxygenation is insufficient. In healthy individuals, EPO levels fluctuate to match physiological demands, such as during high-altitude exposure or blood loss, but production decreases in chronic kidney disease, leading to anemia. The hormone binds to its receptor (EPOR), a cytokine receptor expressed mainly on erythroid precursors, activating the JAK2-STAT5 signaling pathway to drive cell survival, proliferation, and maturation.
First hypothesized in the early 20th century as a factor stimulating red blood cell formation, EPO was purified from human urine in the 1970s and its gene cloned in 1984, enabling the development of recombinant human erythropoietin (rHuEPO). Approved by the FDA in 1989 as epoetin alfa (Epogen), rHuEPO revolutionized treatment for anemia associated with chronic kidney disease, chemotherapy-induced anemia, and other conditions like HIV-related anemia, significantly improving patient quality of life by reducing transfusion needs. However, its use has raised concerns due to potential risks, including increased mortality, serious cardiovascular events (such as heart attack, stroke, and heart failure, particularly when hemoglobin levels exceed 1112 g/dL), thrombosis and embolisms, tumor progression and shortened survival in certain cancers (e.g., breast, head and neck, lung, cervical), and rare pure red cell aplasia, as highlighted by FDA black box warnings and EMA guidelines; additional risks include hypertension, headaches, and seizures, prompting careful dosing and monitoring. Additionally, EPO's misuse as a performance-enhancing drug in sports has led to widespread doping scandals and strict anti-doping regulations.

Molecular and Cellular Biology

Chemical Structure

Erythropoietin (EPO) is a glycoprotein hormone composed of a single polypeptide chain containing 165 amino acids in its mature form, derived from a 193-amino-acid precursor after cleavage of a 27-amino-acid signal peptide and a C-terminal arginine residue. The molecular weight of the mature protein is approximately 30.4 kDa, with the carbohydrate component accounting for about 40% of this mass.
The protein features three N-linked glycosylation sites at asparagines 24, 38, and 83, as well as one O-linked glycosylation site at serine 126. These glycosylation patterns are critical for the hormone's stability, proper folding, secretion, and in vivo biological activity; for instance, preventing glycosylation at these sites results in reduced secretion and loss of activity in bioassays.
EPO's tertiary structure includes four antiparallel -helices arranged in a left-handed bundle, stabilized by two intra-chain disulfide bonds: one between cysteine 7 and cysteine 161, and another between cysteine 29 and cysteine 33. These disulfide bridges are essential for maintaining the protein's conformation and facilitating receptor binding.
Recombinant EPO, typically produced in Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells, closely mimics the native human protein's structure but exhibits subtle differences in glycosylation, particularly in sialic acid linkages (predominantly -2,3 in CHO-derived forms versus a mix of -2,3 and -2,6 in native EPO), which can influence pharmacokinetics without altering core structural motifs.

Synthesis Sites

In adults, the primary site of erythropoietin (EPO) synthesis is the peritubular interstitial fibroblasts located in the renal cortex and outer medulla, which account for approximately 90% of total systemic EPO production. These fibroblast-like cells, also known as renal EPO-producing (REP) cells, are strategically positioned adjacent to the renal tubules to sense oxygen levels in the renal microenvironment.
During fetal development, EPO production occurs predominantly in hepatocytes of the liver, serving as the main site throughout early gestation. This hepatic dominance gradually shifts to the kidneys starting in the last third of gestation, with renal production becoming the primary source by late gestation and completing the transition postnatally within about 40 days after birth.
Extrarenal EPO synthesis contributes a minor fraction (about 10%) to total production in adults under normal conditions but can increase under severe anemia or hypoxia. In such scenarios, the liver resumes a more significant role via hepatocytes and perisinusoidal Ito cells, while smaller amounts are produced in the brain (primarily by astrocytes and neurons) and uterus (in an estrogen-dependent manner).
In renal peritubular fibroblasts, EPO synthesis is triggered by hypoxia, which stabilizes hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF) to drive EPO mRNA transcription from the EPO gene. This process results in rapid upregulation of EPO mRNA levels specifically in these interstitial cells, enabling localized response to oxygen deprivation without involvement of other renal cell types.

Regulation of Production

The production of erythropoietin (EPO) is primarily regulated at the transcriptional level in response to hypoxia, the key physiological stimulus that signals reduced oxygen availability in tissues. Hypoxia leads to the stabilization of hypoxia-inducible factors (HIFs), particularly HIF-2 in renal interstitial fibroblasts, which form heterodimers with HIF-1 (also known as ARNT) and translocate to the nucleus to bind hypoxia-responsive elements (HREs) in the EPO gene promoter. While HIF-1 contributes to broader hypoxic responses, HIF-2 is the dominant isoform driving EPO transcription in the kidney, with HIF-1 playing a more auxiliary role in certain contexts. This stabilization occurs because low oxygen levels inhibit the activity of prolyl-4-hydroxylase domain enzymes (PHDs), preventing the hydroxylation and subsequent ubiquitination of HIF- subunits for proteasomal degradation.
The human EPO gene is located on chromosome 7q22 and spans approximately 5 kb, consisting of five exons and four introns. Its transcriptional regulation is mediated by a proximal promoter region and a critical 3' enhancer sequence located about 120 base pairs downstream of the polyadenylation site, which contains multiple HREs that serve as binding sites for HIF dimers. These HREs, first identified in the early 1990s, enable up to a 1,000-fold induction of EPO mRNA under severe hypoxic conditions by facilitating the recruitment of coactivators such as p300/CBP. Under normoxic conditions, feedback inhibition occurs through PHD enzymes (PHD1-3), which use molecular oxygen, iron, and 2-oxoglutarate as cofactors to hydroxylate specific proline residues on HIF-, marking it for degradation by the von Hippel-Lindau (VHL) E3 ubiquitin ligase complex; this ensures tight control and prevents unwarranted EPO synthesis.
Pathophysiological alterations in EPO production reflect disruptions in this oxygen-sensing pathway. In conditions such as anemia or high-altitude exposure, tissue hypoxia triggers a robust increase in EPO levelsup to 1,000-fold in severe casesto stimulate erythropoiesis and restore oxygen-carrying capacity. Conversely, in chronic kidney disease, damage to EPO-producing renal cells often impairs this response, leading to inappropriately low EPO despite anemia, though certain renal pathologies like polycystic kidney disease can result in overproduction and secondary erythrocytosis.
Recent advancements post-2020 have highlighted the therapeutic potential of HIF prolyl hydroxylase inhibitors (HIF-PHIs), small molecules that mimic hypoxia by blocking PHD activity and stabilizing HIF-, thereby enhancing endogenous EPO production in a more physiological manner than direct EPO administration. These agents, such as daprodustat (FDA-approved in 2023) and vadadustat (FDA-approved in 2024), have demonstrated efficacy in clinical trials and are now approved for the treatment of anemia associated with chronic kidney disease by upregulating EPO gene expression while also modulating iron metabolism and inflammation, offering a nuanced approach to replicating natural regulatory mechanisms.

Physiological Functions

Role in Erythropoiesis

Erythropoietin (EPO) plays a central role in erythropoiesis by stimulating the survival, proliferation, and differentiation of erythroid progenitor cells within the bone marrow, ensuring the continuous production of mature red blood cells (RBCs) to maintain oxygen homeostasis. Primarily produced by peritubular fibroblasts in the kidney in response to hypoxia, EPO binds to its receptor on the surface of early erythroid precursors, initiating signaling cascades that prevent apoptosis and promote cell cycle progression. This process is essential for replacing the approximately 200 billion senescent RBCs lost daily in adults.
EPO specifically targets colony-forming unit-erythroid (CFU-E) cells and proerythroblasts, which express high levels of the EPO receptor (EPOR), a homodimeric cytokine receptor complex. At this stage, EPO enhances the viability of these progenitors by inhibiting pro-apoptotic pathways, such as those involving caspases, while upregulating anti-apoptotic proteins like Bcl-xL. Without sufficient EPO, CFU-E and proerythroblast populations undergo rapid apoptosis, leading to ineffective erythropoiesis. This targeted action on late-stage progenitors distinguishes EPO from earlier-acting factors like stem cell factor, which support burst-forming unit-erythroid (BFU-E) development.
In the stages of erythropoiesis, EPO promotes the sequential differentiation from BFU-E to CFU-E, basophilic erythroblasts, polychromatophilic erythroblasts, and orthochromatic normoblasts, culminating in enucleated reticulocytes that mature into circulating RBCs. This progression involves EPO-induced upregulation of globin gene expression and heme biosynthesis, thereby increasing hemoglobin synthesis critical for oxygen transport. EPO's influence peaks during the transition from proerythroblasts to reticulocytes, where it accelerates terminal maturation and hemoglobin accumulation, ensuring functional RBC output.
Under normal physiological conditions, serum EPO levels range from 5 to 25 mU/mL, sufficient to maintain steady-state erythropoiesis and a peripheral RBC count of 4 to 6 million cells per microliter of blood. In response to severe hypoxia, such as in high-altitude exposure or profound anemia, EPO production can increase 100- to 1,000-fold, reaching up to 10,000 mU/mL or higher, to rapidly expand erythroid output and restore oxygen-carrying capacity. This hypoxic induction, mediated by hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF) stabilization in renal cells, exemplifies EPO's role as a feedback regulator of RBC mass.
Quantitatively, EPO accounts for the majority of erythropoietic drive, with its absence or deficiency resulting in severe anemia, as seen in chronic kidney disease where renal EPO production is inappropriately low relative to the degree of anemia, leading to RBC counts often under 3 million/L and hemoglobin levels below 10 g/dL. In such cases, exogenous EPO administration can restore erythropoiesis, increasing hemoglobin by 1-2 g/dL within weeks and highlighting its dominant influence on RBC production.
EPO integrates with other factors in erythropoiesis, notably requiring adequate iron availability for heme incorporation into hemoglobin, as EPO stimulates transferrin receptor expression on erythroid cells to facilitate iron uptake. Vitamins such as B12 and folate support DNA synthesis in proliferating progenitors, complementing EPO's proliferative effects, though EPO itself does not directly regulate their metabolism.

Non-Hematopoietic Effects

Erythropoietin (EPO) exerts neuroprotective effects through the expression of its receptor (EPOR) in various brain regions, including neurons, astrocytes, and microglia, where it activates signaling pathways that reduce apoptosis and inflammation following ischemic or traumatic insults. In preclinical models of stroke, EPO administration has been shown to limit neuronal cell death by inhibiting caspase-3 activation and promoting anti-apoptotic proteins like Bcl-2, while also suppressing pro-inflammatory cytokines such as TNF- and IL-1 via JAK2/STAT5 and NF-B modulation. Similarly, in neurodegeneration models like Parkinson's disease, EPO attenuates dopaminergic neuron loss and oxidative stress, enhancing survival through antioxidant enzyme upregulation, including superoxide dismutase and glutathione peroxidase.
In cardioprotection, EPO safeguards cardiomyocytes against ischemia-reperfusion injury by activating PI3K/Akt and ERK1/2 pathways, which inhibit mitochondrial permeability transition pore opening and reduce infarct size in animal models of myocardial infarction. This protection extends to heart failure scenarios, where EPO improves left ventricular function and reduces fibrosis by promoting angiogenesis and limiting cardiomyocyte apoptosis, as evidenced in rodent studies using hypoxia-inducible factor stabilization. Preclinical data indicate that EPO's cardioprotective effects are independent of its hematopoietic actions, relying instead on tissue-specific EPOR signaling to enhance endothelial nitric oxide synthase activity and vascular repair.
EPO also contributes to tissue repair processes, including wound healing and angiogenesis, by stimulating endothelial cell proliferation and migration through VEGF induction and matrix metalloproteinase activation in skin injury models. In metabolic regulation, particularly in adipose tissue, EPO influences lipid metabolism and insulin sensitivity; for instance, EPO knockout in non-hematopoietic tissues leads to increased fat mass and obesity in mice, while exogenous EPO reduces adipocyte hypertrophy and improves glucose homeostasis via EPOR-mediated suppression of inflammatory adipokines. These effects highlight EPO's role in promoting vascularization and tissue remodeling beyond blood production.
Despite robust preclinical evidence, the clinical translation of EPO's non-hematopoietic effects remains limited, with mixed results in human trials; for example, the EPO-TBI trial demonstrated no reduction in severe neurological dysfunction or mortality after traumatic brain injury, attributing this to potential dosing issues or EPOR downregulation in chronic inflammation. Strong neuroprotective outcomes in animal stroke models have not consistently replicated in phase III trials, where high doses raised thrombosis risks without improving functional recovery. These controversies underscore the need for optimized delivery strategies to harness EPO's tissue-protective potential without hematopoietic side effects.
Recent developments from 2020 to 2025 have explored EPO's neuroprotective role in COVID-19-related neurological damage, where low serum EPO levels correlate with severe outcomes and hypoxemia, and adjuvant EPO therapy showed promise in reducing inflammation and improving survival in case reports of critical patients. Synergies with hypoxia-inducible factor prolyl hydroxylase inhibitors (HIF-PHIs) have emerged, enhancing EPO's non-hematopoietic signaling in metabolic and inflammatory contexts to improve insulin sensitivity without boosting erythropoiesis. Additionally, personalized approaches based on EPO signaling variants, such as EPOR polymorphisms, are gaining traction for tailoring neuroprotective therapies in stroke and neurodegeneration, with genetic profiling predicting response variability in preclinical models.

Pharmacodynamics and Pharmacokinetics

Mechanism of Action

Erythropoietin (EPO) exerts its effects primarily through binding to the homodimeric erythropoietin receptor (EPOR), a type I cytokine receptor predominantly expressed on erythroid progenitor cells. The EPOR consists of two identical subunits, each featuring a cytokine-binding domain, and exhibits high-affinity binding to EPO with a dissociation constant (Kd) of approximately 100 pM. This high affinity ensures sensitive detection of EPO at physiological concentrations, enabling precise regulation of erythropoiesis.
Upon binding, EPO induces conformational changes in the pre-formed EPOR dimer, bringing the intracellular juxtamembrane domains into close proximity and activating the associated Janus kinase 2 (JAK2) enzymes. This leads to trans-phosphorylation and autophosphorylation of JAK2, initiating intracellular signaling cascades. The primary pathway activated is the JAK2-STAT5 signaling axis, where phosphorylated JAK2 recruits and phosphorylates signal transducer and activator of transcription 5 (STAT5), promoting its dimerization, nuclear translocation, and transcription of genes essential for erythroid proliferation. Complementary pathways include the phosphoinositide 3-kinase (PI3K)-Akt route, which supports anti-apoptotic effects, and the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) pathway, which contributes to cell differentiation.
Downstream of these cascades, EPO signaling upregulates anti-apoptotic genes such as Bcl-xL, which inhibits programmed cell death in erythroid progenitors and facilitates their survival during maturation. Pharmacological Properties

Erythropoietin, when administered subcutaneously, exhibits a bioavailability of approximately 20-30%, with peak serum concentrations occurring 5-12 hours after injection. In contrast, intravenous administration achieves 100% bioavailability due to direct entry into the bloodstream. These pharmacokinetic profiles support the use of both routes in clinical settings, though subcutaneous dosing often provides a more sustained release.
The elimination half-life of erythropoietin varies by administration route, ranging from 4-13 hours following intravenous injection and approximately 24 hours after subcutaneous administration. This difference is influenced by the degree of glycosylation, which enhances stability and reduces clearance by protecting against rapid enzymatic breakdown.
Upon administration, erythropoietin distributes primarily to the extravascular space in the bone marrow, its primary site of action on erythroid progenitors, with a volume of distribution of 30-50 mL/kg, consistent with limited extravascular penetration beyond plasma and target tissues.
Metabolism and clearance of erythropoietin primarily occur through receptor-mediated uptake and proteolytic degradation in target cells expressing EPOR, such as erythroid progenitors, with secondary contributions from the liver and kidneys; no active metabolites are produced. Excretion is minimal and not significantly altered by renal function, as the peptide is largely catabolized internally.
Drug interactions with erythropoietin include reduced efficacy in the presence of iron deficiency, which limits hemoglobin response and necessitates iron supplementation to achieve therapeutic targets below 11.5 g/dL (115 g/L), as per current guidelines (as of 2025). Close monitoring of hemoglobin levels is essential to avoid overcorrection and associated risks.

Clinical Applications

Therapeutic Indications

Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs), including recombinant human erythropoietin (epoetin alfa), are approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of anemia associated with chronic kidney disease (CKD) in patients on dialysis and not on dialysis, aiming to elevate hemoglobin levels and decrease reliance on red blood cell transfusions. These agents are also indicated for chemotherapy-induced anemia in patients with non-myeloid malignancies, particularly when the chemotherapy is not intended to be curative, to alleviate symptoms and reduce transfusion needs. Additionally, ESAs are approved for managing anemia in HIV-infected patients receiving zidovudine therapy at doses up to 4200 mg per week, where the anemia is attributed to the antiretroviral treatment rather than the infection itself.
In CKD, ESAs effectively increase hemoglobin concentrations by approximately 1 to 2 g/dL on average and reduce the risk of transfusions (relative risk 0.61, 95% CI 0.490.77) compared to no ESA therapy, based on meta-analyses of randomized controlled trials demonstrating improved erythropoiesis without excessive cardiovascular risks when targeted appropriately. Similar efficacy is observed in chemotherapy settings, where ESAs raise hemoglobin and cut transfusion rates by about 50%, enhancing quality of life during treatment. For zidovudine-treated HIV patients, ESAs have been shown to boost hematocrit by 3 to 4 percentage points and halve transfusion requirements in responsive individuals.
ESAs extend to special populations, including preterm infants with anemia of prematurity, where recombinant erythropoietin is used off-label as an alternative to transfusions by stimulating endogenous red blood cell production, though outcomes vary by gestational age. In patients with myelodysplastic syndromes, ESAs are utilized off-label or in select cases to address symptomatic anemia, particularly when endogenous erythropoietin levels are low, leading to transfusion independence in responsive subsets. Recent expansions from 2020 to 2025 include enhanced recommendations for perioperative anemia management, where preoperative ESA administration, often combined with iron, reduces transfusion needs in major surgeries like cardiac procedures by optimizing hemoglobin preoperatively.
Guidelines from the Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) recommend initiating ESA therapy in CKD patients with hemoglobin levels below 10.0 g/dL after iron optimization to prevent drops below 9.0 g/dL, targeting levels below 11.5 g/dL to minimize risks, with individualization based on symptoms and risks (updated in the 2025 KDIGO guideline). The National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) advises ESAs for chemotherapy-induced anemia in symptomatic patients with hemoglobin below 10 g/dL, provided the treatment intent is palliative and risks are discussed. As an emerging alternative for CKD anemia, hypoxia-inducible factor prolyl hydroxylase inhibitors (HIF-PHIs) like daprodustat offer oral administration and comparable hemoglobin elevation to ESAs; it has been approved in Japan since 2020 for both dialysis-dependent and non-dialysis-dependent patients, received FDA approval in 2023 for dialysis patients but was withdrawn from the US market in 2024, and plans for an EU launch were abandoned in 2023 (as of 2025).

Administration and Formulations

Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs) such as recombinant human erythropoietin are primarily administered via intravenous (IV) or subcutaneous (SC) routes to treat anemia in various clinical settings. The IV route is commonly used in hemodialysis patients for convenience during dialysis sessions, while the SC route offers flexibility for non-dialysis patients and may require lower overall doses due to prolonged absorption. Administration frequency typically ranges from 1 to 3 times weekly, depending on the formulation and patient response, with adjustments made to maintain stable hemoglobin levels.
Key formulations include epoetin alfa, the first recombinant human erythropoietin approved for clinical use, which mimics the native hormone to stimulate red blood cell production. Darbepoetin alfa represents an advanced formulation with hyperglycosylation, resulting in a longer half-life of approximately 21-25 hours compared to epoetin alfa's 4-13 hours, allowing for less frequent dosing. Other long-acting options, such as methoxy polyethylene glycol-epoetin beta (a pegylated form), extend duration further through chemical modification, enabling once-monthly administration in some cases.
Dosing strategies for ESAs are weight-based and tailored to the underlying condition, such as chronic kidney disease (CKD). For CKD patients not on dialysis, initial doses often start at 50-100 units/kg administered IV or SC three times weekly, with titration to achieve a target hemoglobin of 10-11 g/dL and no higher than 11.5 g/dL to minimize cardiovascular risks. In dialysis-dependent CKD, doses may range up to 150 units/kg three times weekly if response is inadequate, with regular monitoring to avoid exceeding the target range.
Biosimilars have expanded access to these therapies, with Retacrit (epoetin alfa-epbx) approved by the FDA in 2018 as the first biosimilar to reference products Epogen and Procrit, demonstrating pharmacokinetic, pharmacodynamic, and clinical equivalence in randomized trials. These biosimilars maintain similar efficacy and safety profiles, supporting their interchangeability in practice under appropriate guidelines.
Recent advances from 2020 to 2025 include the introduction of novel long-acting formulations like efepoetin alfa, an EPO-hybrid Fc fusion protein designed for extended half-life and subcutaneous administration every 4-6 weeks in CKD anemia management. Additionally, in vitro potency assays, such as the TF-1 cell proliferation bioassay, have gained prominence for quality control, offering a more ethical and precise alternative to traditional in vivo methods by measuring receptor binding and cellular response with reduced variability. These developments enhance manufacturing consistency and support the transition to fully in vitro-based potency evaluation in pharmacopeial standards.

Risks and Adverse Effects

Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs) carry significant risks, as highlighted by black box warnings from the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and the European Medicines Agency (EMA). These warnings emphasize the increased risk of death, serious cardiovascular events, and other adverse effects when hemoglobin levels exceed 1112 g/dL.
Serious cardiovascular complications include heart attack, stroke, heart failure, and increased mortality, particularly in patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) when targeting higher hemoglobin levels. Thrombosis and embolisms, such as deep vein thrombosis and pulmonary embolism, are also major risks, especially with high doses or preoperative use. In cancer patients, ESAs have been associated with tumor progression and shortened survival in certain types, including breast, head and neck, non-small cell lung, lymphoid, and cervical cancers.
Pure red cell aplasia (PRCA), a rare but serious condition involving severe anemia due to neutralizing antibodies against erythropoietin, may occur and often requires transfusions or discontinuation of therapy. Other adverse effects include hypertension, headaches, and seizures, which should be monitored closely during treatment. Guidelines recommend using the lowest effective dose to minimize these risks while achieving therapeutic benefits.

Development and Misuse

Historical Development

The concept of a humoral factor regulating erythropoiesis was first proposed in 1906 by French physicians Paul Carnot and Clotilde Deflandre, who observed that injecting serum from anemic rabbits into normal rabbits induced increased red blood cell production, terming the hypothetical substance "hemopoietine." Their experiments suggested a kidney-derived hormone responsive to hypoxia, laying the groundwork for later research despite initial skepticism about the mechanism.
Significant advances occurred in the late 1970s with the purification of human erythropoietin from urine collected from patients with aplastic anemia. In 1977, Takaji Miyake, along with colleagues Ching-Kuang Kung and Eugene Goldwasser at the University of Chicago, isolated approximately 25 milligrams of highly purified erythropoietin from 2,550 liters of urine using techniques including chromatography and gel filtration, achieving homogeneity and enabling amino acid sequencing that revealed a glycoprotein structure. This purification effort was pivotal, as prior attempts had yielded only impure extracts. In 1984, Sylvia Lee-Huang cloned a partial cDNA for the human erythropoietin gene and expressed it in Escherichia coli from renal carcinoma cell mRNA, representing an early step in recombinant production. The full-length cDNA, confirming the mature protein's 166-amino-acid sequence with glycosylation sites, was cloned in 1985 by a team at Amgen led by Fu-Kuen Lin and others.
Recombinant human erythropoietin (rhEPO), specifically epoetin alfa developed by Amgen, received U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approval on June 1, 1989, for treating anemia associated with chronic kidney disease, revolutionizing therapy by providing a safe, scalable alternative to blood transfusions. In the 1990s, clinical applications expanded; the FDA approved rhEPO in 1993 for anemia in cancer patients undergoing chemotherapy, based on trials showing hemoglobin increases and reduced transfusion needs. A major milestone came in 2019 when the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine was awarded to William G. Kaelin Jr., Sir Peter J. Ratcliffe, and Gregg L. Semenza for discovering the hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF) pathway, which transcriptionally regulates erythropoietin production in response to low oxygen levels.
Early concerns about misuse emerged in the 1990s within professional cycling, where rhEPO's ability to boost oxygen-carrying capacity led to suspicions of performance enhancement following its approval, exemplified by increased hematocrit levels among elite riders and reports of related health risks like thrombosis.

Biosimilars and Alternatives

Biosimilars of erythropoietin, such as epoetin alfa and epoetin zeta, have been approved in the European Union since 2007 to provide cost-effective alternatives to reference products like Epogen and Procrit. For instance, Silapo (epoetin zeta) received European Medicines Agency (EMA) approval in 2007 for indications including anemia in chronic kidney disease (CKD) and chemotherapy-induced anemia, demonstrating comparable pharmacokinetics, pharmacodynamics, and efficacy to the originator through crossover trials. Retacrit (epoetin alfa-epbx), another biosimilar, was authorized by the EMA in 2018 and by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in the same year as the first erythropoietin biosimilar in the United States, with post-2020 expansions including mandatory switches by insurers like UnitedHealthcare to reduce costs by up to 20-30% in oncology and nephrology settings.
Long-acting erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs) represent advancements in formulation to extend dosing intervals and improve patient convenience. Darbepoetin alfa (Aranesp), approved by the FDA in 2001, features a modified amino acid sequence that prolongs its half-life to approximately 25 hours compared to 4-13 hours for standard epoetin alfa, allowing administration every 1-2 weeks. Peginesatide (Omontys), a PEGylated synthetic peptide analog of erythropoietin, was approved in 2012 for anemia in dialysis patients due to its monthly dosing potential but was withdrawn in 2013 following post-marketing reports of severe hypersensitivity reactions, highlighting risks associated with novel PEGylation strategies despite initial non-inferiority to epoetin in maintaining hemoglobin levels.
Emerging alternatives to traditional ESAs include hypoxia-inducible factor prolyl hydroxylase inhibitors (HIF-PHIs), which mimic hypoxic conditions to upregulate endogenous erythropoietin production. Daprodustat, an oral HIF-PHI, was approved in Japan in 2020 and in China in 2021 for anemia in CKD patients on dialysis, showing non-inferiority to epoetin alfa in hemoglobin maintenance across phase 3 trials. Vadadustat (Vafseo), another oral HIF-PHI, received FDA approval in 2023 for dialysis-dependent CKD anemia, with European Commission authorization in 2024, based on studies demonstrating comparable efficacy to darbepoetin alfa while offering daily oral administration. Additionally, luspatercept (Reblozyl), a fusion protein that promotes late-stage erythropoiesis, was approved by the FDA in 2019 for transfusion-dependent beta-thalassemia anemia in adults, reducing transfusion burden by at least 33% in 21% of patients versus 4.5% on placebo in the BELIEVE trial, providing an option for ESA-refractory cases.
Biosimilars face challenges including potential immunogenicity, though long-term data indicate similar profiles to originators, with anti-erythropoietin antibody incidence below 1% in post-approval surveillance for products like Retacrit and Silapo. Comparative clinical trials have consistently established non-inferiority, such as a 15-year review of epoetin alfa biosimilar HX575 (Binocrit/Retacrit) confirming equivalent hemoglobin response rates and safety in over 1,000 patients across CKD and oncology indications.
Future directions in erythropoietin therapeutics emphasize gene therapy approaches to achieve sustained endogenous production, such as adeno-associated virus vectors delivering EPO genes in preclinical models for CKD anemia, and oral formulations of EPO analogs to enhance bioavailability beyond current HIF-PHIs. As of November 2025, no major new approvals or regulatory changes have been reported for these emerging therapies.

Doping Applications

Erythropoietin (EPO) is misused in sports primarily to enhance endurance performance by stimulating erythropoiesis, which increases red blood cell mass and hemoglobin concentration, thereby improving oxygen transport to muscles. This results in an elevated maximal oxygen uptake (VO2 max) of approximately 6-12%, allowing athletes to sustain higher intensities during prolonged exercise. The enhanced aerobic capacity provides a competitive edge in endurance disciplines such as cycling, running, and cross-country skiing, where marginal gains in oxygen delivery can translate to significant performance improvements.
The illicit use of EPO gained prominence in the 1990s, particularly in professional cycling, where it was implicated in several high-profile scandals during the Tour de France. Riders reportedly administered recombinant human EPO (rhEPO) to boost stamina, contributing to an era of widespread doping that tainted the event's reputation. In the 2000s, the case of Lance Armstrong exemplified the issue; the seven-time Tour de France winner was stripped of his titles in 2012 after admitting to a systematic doping program that included EPO use from 1999 to 2005, as detailed in the U.S. Anti-Doping Agency's investigation. More recently, in 2019, UFC fighter T.J. Dillashaw tested positive for rhEPO ahead of a bantamweight title fight, leading to a two-year suspension; this case highlighted the shift toward micro-dosing techniques, where small, frequent doses are used to minimize detectable elevations in hematocrit while still conferring benefits.
The International Olympic Committee (IOC) banned EPO in 1990, recognizing its potential for abuse despite the lack of a reliable detection method at the time. A urine-based test using isoelectric focusing to differentiate recombinant from endogenous EPO isoforms was introduced at the 2000 Sydney Olympics, enabling direct detection for the first time. In 2008, the World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA) implemented the Athlete Biological Passport (ABP), a longitudinal monitoring system that tracks hematological biomarkers such as hemoglobin levels and reticulocyte count to flag suspicious variations indicative of doping, even without a positive substance test. Recent advances include refined isoelectric focusing protocols for improved sensitivity to low-dose EPO and multi-biomarker panels that incorporate gene expression markers to detect micro-dosing.
Misuse of EPO carries severe health risks, primarily due to polycythemia-induced hyperviscosity, which elevates the likelihood of thrombosis, stroke, and myocardial infarction. In the late 1980s and early 1990s, at least 18-20 young Belgian and Dutch cyclists died suddenly from heart attacks or strokes, with EPO widely suspected as a contributing factor amid its rising popularity in the sport. These incidents prompted heightened scrutiny and regulatory action, as the drug's effects on blood thickening were exacerbated by dehydration during intense training and competition.
Estimates of EPO and blood doping prevalence in elite endurance sports range from 14-39% based on self-reported surveys and indirect testing data, though direct detection rates remain lower at around 1-2% of analyzed samples due to sophisticated evasion tactics. WADA continues to update its Prohibited List annually, with the 2025 edition maintaining EPO receptor agonists under strict bans and emphasizing ABP enhancements to combat emerging micro-dosing and biosimilar variants.
