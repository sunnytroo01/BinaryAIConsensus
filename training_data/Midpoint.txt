 In geometry, the midpoint of a line segment is the point that divides the segment into two parts of equal length, making it equidistant from both endpoints. This point serves as the center of the segment and is also its centroid, representing the balance point if the segment were considered a one-dimensional mass distribution.
In coordinate geometry, the coordinates of the midpoint between two points (x1,y1)(x_1, y_1)(x1,y1) and (x2,y2)(x_2, y_2)(x2,y2) are calculated using the midpoint formula: (x1+x22,y1+y22)\left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2} \right)(2x1+x2,2y1+y2). This formula arises from averaging the respective coordinates, effectively finding the arithmetic mean in each dimension. It applies to segments in the Euclidean plane and can extend to higher dimensions, such as three-dimensional space, by including a z-coordinate in the average.
The concept of the midpoint is fundamental in various geometric constructions and proofs, including the midpoint theorem and its converse, where the theorem states that the line segment joining the midpoints of two sides is parallel to the third side and half as long, and the converse states that if a line is drawn through the midpoint of one side of a triangle and is parallel to another side, then it bisects the third side. Beyond pure geometry, midpoints appear in analytic geometry for determining coordinates of medians in triangles and in physics for calculating centers of mass in simple systems. In statistics, the term midpoint can refer to the average value of an interval in grouped data, though this usage stems from the same averaging principle.

Definition and Formulas

Basic Definition

In geometry, the midpoint of a line segment AB is defined as the unique point M on the segment such that the length AM equals the length MB, with M positioned between A and B. This division creates two congruent subsegments, ensuring balance and equality along the line.
Conceptually, the midpoint represents a 1:1 ratio division of the segment, where the position of M balances the endpoints A and B. In vector terms, this corresponds to the position vector of M being the arithmetic average of the position vectors of A and B, emphasizing the symmetric distribution of the segment's extent.
The notion of the midpoint traces its origins to Euclidean geometry in Euclid's Elements, composed around 300 BCE, particularly in Book I, Proposition 10, which addresses the bisection of a finite straight line to locate its midpoint. Visually, the midpoint on a straight line illustrates perfect symmetry, appearing as the central point where the segment folds onto itself without overlap, highlighting the inherent equilibrium in linear division.

Coordinate Formula

In coordinate geometry, the midpoint $ M $ of a line segment joining two points $ A(x_1, y_1) $ and $ B(x_2, y_2) $ in the plane is given by the formula
M=(x1+x22,y1+y22).
M = \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2} \right).
M=(2x1+x2,2y1+y2).
This formula arises from the principle of averaging the coordinates, as the midpoint represents the arithmetic mean of the endpoints' positions along each axis, ensuring it lies exactly halfway between them.
To illustrate, consider points $ A(1, 2) $ and $ B(3, 4) $. The midpoint is
M=(1+32,2+42)=(2,3).
M = \left( \frac{1 + 3}{2}, \frac{2 + 4}{2} \right) = (2, 3).
M=(21+3,22+4)=(2,3).
This calculation confirms the point's position at the average.
The formula's validity can be verified using the distance formula, which states the distance between two points $ (x_a, y_a) $ and $ (x_b, y_b) $ is $ \sqrt{(x_b - x_a)^2 + (y_b - y_a)^2} $. Let $ M $ be the proposed midpoint. The distance $ AM $ simplifies to
AM=(x2x12)2+(y2y12)2=12(x2x1)2+(y2y1)2,
AM = \sqrt{\left( \frac{x_2 - x_1}{2} \right)^2 + \left( \frac{y_2 - y_1}{2} \right)^2} = \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2},
AM=(2x2x1)2+(2y2y1)2=21(x2x1)2+(y2y1)2,
and similarly for $ BM $, yielding $ AM = BM $, thus confirming equal distances from $ M $ to the endpoints.
This approach extends to three-dimensional space, where for points $ A(x_1, y_1, z_1) $ and $ B(x_2, y_2, z_2) $, the midpoint is
M=(x1+x22,y1+y22,z1+z22),
M = \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}, \frac{z_1 + z_2}{2} \right),
M=(2x1+x2,2y1+y2,2z1+z2),
by averaging the coordinates across all three axes in an analogous manner.

Vector Formula

In vector geometry, the position vector of the midpoint $ \vec{M} $ of two points with position vectors $ \vec{A} $ and $ \vec{B} $ is given by
M=A+B2.
\vec{M} = \frac{\vec{A} + \vec{B}}{2}.
M=2A+B.
This formula holds in any vector space over a field where division by 2 is defined, such as the real numbers.
This expression derives from the section formula in vector terms, which describes the position vector of a point dividing the segment joining $ \vec{A} $ and $ \vec{B} $ in the ratio $ m:n $ as $ \frac{m \vec{B} + n \vec{A}}{m + n} $. For the midpoint, the ratio is 1:1, reducing to the average. More fundamentally, it arises as a special case of an affine combination, where $ \vec{M} = t \vec{A} + (1 - t) \vec{B} $ with $ t = \frac{1}{2} $, ensuring the coefficients sum to 1 and preserving affine structure.
Key properties include the midpoint serving as the centroid of the two points under equal weighting, equivalent to the center of mass for two equal masses at $ \vec{A} $ and $ \vec{B} $. Additionally, the midpoint is invariant under translation: if both points are shifted by a vector $ \vec{T} $, the new midpoint is $ \frac{(\vec{A} + \vec{T}) + (\vec{B} + \vec{T})}{2} = \vec{M} + \vec{T} $, maintaining its relative position.
In a vector space, the midpoint between vectors $ \vec{u} $ and $ \vec{v} $ is $ \frac{\vec{u} + \vec{v}}{2} $, which applies to displacement calculations; for example, if $ \vec{u} $ and $ \vec{v} $ are position vectors from a reference origin, this average represents the displacement to the midpoint, useful in analyzing symmetric motion or balancing forces in mechanics. This vector approach generalizes the coordinate-based formula to arbitrary dimensions without relying on a specific basis.

Construction Techniques

Compass and Straightedge Method

The compass and straightedge method is a classical technique in Euclidean geometry for constructing the midpoint of a given line segment, relying solely on these two tools to ensure precision without measurement. The compass allows for drawing circles of specified radii, while the straightedge enables the creation of straight lines connecting points or extending lines. This approach is foundational to geometric constructions, as it adheres to the postulates outlined in ancient Greek mathematics.
Historically, the method originates from Euclid's Elements, specifically Book I, Proposition 10, where it is presented as a way to bisect a given finite straight line. Euclid demonstrates that by constructing the perpendicular bisector of the segment, the point of intersection with the original line serves as the midpoint, equidistant from both endpoints. This proposition builds on earlier postulates regarding the use of the compass and straightedge, establishing a rigorous procedure that has influenced geometric practice for over two millennia.
To construct the midpoint of a line segment AB using this method, begin by drawing the segment AB with the straightedge. Place the compass point at A and adjust the radius to the length of AB, then draw a circle (or arc) centered at A that passes through B; repeat the process with the compass centered at B, drawing another circle of the same radius that passes through A. These two circles will intersect at two points, say C and D, which are equidistant from A and B. Use the straightedge to draw the line connecting C and D, forming the perpendicular bisector of AB. The point where this bisector intersects AB is the midpoint M. This construction works because any point on the perpendicular bisector is equidistant from A and B by the properties of circles.
The method assumes the line segment is straight and finite, as defined in Euclidean geometry, and cannot directly apply to curved or infinite lines without additional constructions. In practice, the accuracy of the resulting midpoint depends on the precision of the compass and straightedge used, as small errors in drawing circles or lines can accumulate, leading to deviations from the true midpoint.

Coordinate Geometry Method

The coordinate geometry method for determining the midpoint of a line segment relies on algebraic computation within a Cartesian coordinate system, where endpoints are represented as ordered pairs. To apply this method, assign coordinates to the two endpoints of the segment, denoted as (x1,y1)(x_1, y_1)(x1,y1) and (x2,y2)(x_2, y_2)(x2,y2). The midpoint MMM is then found by averaging these coordinates using the formula:
M=(x1+x22,y1+y22).
M = \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2} \right).
M=(2x1+x2,2y1+y2).
This averaging process yields the precise location of the midpoint, which can be plotted on a graph or incorporated into subsequent geometric calculations.
The procedure begins by identifying the endpoint coordinates, followed by separately adding and dividing the x-values and y-values by 2 to obtain the midpoint pair. Once computed, the result can be visualized by marking the point on the coordinate plane or used directly in analytical tasks. For instance, for a line segment connecting (0,0) and (4,6), the midpoint is calculated as (0+42,0+62)=(2,3)\left( \frac{0 + 4}{2}, \frac{0 + 6}{2} \right) = (2, 3)(20+4,20+6)=(2,3). This approach provides high precision in numerical results, avoiding approximations inherent in graphical constructions, and proves advantageous in applications requiring exact computations, such as positioning elements in computer graphics or structural analysis in engineering. Properties in Euclidean Geometry

In Line Segments

In Euclidean geometry, the midpoint of a line segment is defined as the point that divides the segment into two congruent parts of equal length. This property ensures that the distances from the midpoint to each endpoint are identical, providing a balanced division fundamental to many geometric constructions and proofs.
A key implication of this property concerns the locus of midpoints formed by segments connecting two parallel lines. Specifically, the set of all midpoints of line segments with one endpoint on each of two parallel lines forms a straight line parallel to both and positioned midway between them. This locus arises because the y-coordinate (in a coordinate system aligned with the lines) of each midpoint is consistently the average of the fixed distances of the parallel lines, while the x-coordinates vary freely along the direction of the lines.
Another significant property involves connecting midpoints in polygonal figures. For instance, the midpoints of the sides of any quadrilateral, when joined in order, form a parallelogram known as the Varignon parallelogram. This result highlights how midpoints preserve parallelism and vector relationships even in non-parallelogram quadrilaterals.
In the context of transversals, the segment joining the midpoints of two sides intersected by a transversal exhibits parallelism and proportional length. A brief extension to triangular setups shows that such a segment is parallel to the third side and half its length, underscoring the midpoint's role in similarity and proportion.
Midpoints find practical application in partitioning problems, where they enable equal division of paths or segments, such as determining balanced positions in navigation or coordinate systems. For example, in dividing a route equally between two points, the midpoint serves as the central reference, facilitating symmetric allocations in geometric modeling.

In Triangles

In a triangle, the line segment connecting the midpoints of two sides is known as a midsegment. The midsegment theorem states that this segment is parallel to the third side of the triangle and half as long as that side.
The converse of the midsegment theorem (also known as the midpoint theorem) states: If a line is drawn through the midpoint of one side of a triangle and is parallel to another side, then it bisects the third side.

In Conic Sections

In conic sections, the midpoint of a chord plays a significant role in understanding geometric properties, particularly in circles, ellipses, and hyperbolas. For a circle, the perpendicular from the center to any chord bisects the chord, meaning the midpoint lies on this perpendicular line. This theorem follows from the symmetry of the circle, where the two segments from the center to the chord endpoints form congruent right triangles. Specifically, if the center is at O and the chord is AB with midpoint M, then OM is perpendicular to AB. Additionally, when the chord is a diameter, its midpoint coincides with the center of the circle, as the diameter passes through the center by definition.
In an ellipse, the locus of the midpoints of a family of parallel chords forms a straight line known as a diameter, which passes through the center of the ellipse. For chords parallel to one of the principal axes, such as the minor axis, the midpoints lie along the major axis, reflecting the ellipse's axial symmetry. Regarding focal properties, the midpoints of focal chordschords passing through one of the focitrace a conic section similar to the original but scaled by a factor involving the eccentricity eee.
For a hyperbola x2a2y2b2=1\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1a2x2b2y2=1, the midpoints of chords passing through a focus have a locus that is another hyperbola. This property arises from the parametric representation and the condition that the chord passes through the focus.

In Graph Theory

In graph theory, a midpoint between two vertices u and v in an undirected connected graph G is a vertex w that lies on at least one shortest u-v path and satisfies d_G(u, w) = d_G(w, v) = d_G(u, v)/2, where d_G denotes the shortest-path distance in G and d_G(u, v) is even. This concept extends the geometric midpoint to discrete structures, where shortest paths serve as geodesics, and such w exists only if the distance is even; otherwise, no vertex midpoint is possible, though edge midpoints could be considered in metric graphs with edge lengths.
The midpoint set for a pair (u, v) is the collection of all such vertices w. For a graph G, the overall midpoint set can be defined as the union of midpoint sets over all pairs of vertices at maximum distance (the diameter), capturing vertices central to the graph's longest connections. This set relates to the graph's radius and diameter: the radius r is the minimum eccentricity (maximum distance from a vertex to any other), the diameter d is the maximum eccentricity, and in general graphs, r d 2r. Every central vertex (those with eccentricity r) lies on some diametral path (shortest path of length d), and thus the center is a subset of the midpoints of diametral paths when d is even. In trees, this relation is exact: the center consists precisely of the midpoint(s) of any diametral path, with a single central vertex if d is even (r = d/2) or two adjacent central vertices if d is odd (r = (d+1)/2).
To compute the midpoint set for a specific pair (u, v), perform breadth-first search (BFS) from u to compute all distances and identify d = d_G(u, v); if d is odd, the set is empty. If even, let k = d/2; run BFS from u to find all vertices at distance k from u, then run BFS from v to verify which of those are at distance k from v (automatically ensuring they lie on some shortest path since d_G(u, w) + d_G(w, v) = d). This requires two BFS traversals, running in O(|V| + |E|) time, where |V| is the number of vertices and |E| the number of edges.
Applications of midpoints appear in network analysis, where the midpoint set helps identify balanced locations for facilities or hubs that equidistant from key nodes, minimizing maximum travel in transportation or communication networks. In social networks, midpoint centralitymeasuring the fraction of pairs for which a vertex serves as a midpointhighlights influential nodes bridging distant actors, similar to betweenness but focused on balanced positions; higher midpoint centrality indicates structural importance in global productive service networks. For example, in a path graph P_5 (vertices 1-2-3-4-5), the midpoint set between endpoints 1 and 5 (d=4) is {3}, the unique central vertex. In a cycle graph C_6 (vertices 1-2-3-4-5-6-1), the distance between 1 and 4 is 3 (odd), so the midpoint set is empty; between 1 and 5, d=2 (via 1-6-5), and the midpoint set is {6}. In C_8 (vertices 1-2-3-4-5-6-7-8-1), between 1 and 5 (d=4), the shortest paths are 1-2-3-4-5 and 1-8-7-6-5; the midpoint set is {3,7}.
