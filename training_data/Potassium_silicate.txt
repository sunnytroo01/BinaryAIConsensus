Potassium silicate
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Potassium silicate is the potassium salt of silicic acid, an inorganic compound typically represented by the formula KSiO and belonging to a family of materials with the general composition KO nSiO, where n varies from 1 to 4. It appears as a white, amorphous or crystalline powder that is highly soluble in water, forming clear, alkaline solutions with a pH of 11 to 13, and it is produced industrially by fusing quartz sand (SiO) with potassium carbonate (KCO) at high temperatures (13001500C) followed by dissolution in water. The compound has a melting point exceeding 900C and is stable under normal storage conditions, though it can release silica upon acidification or heating.
In agriculture, potassium silicate serves as a source of both potassium and soluble silicon, applied as a foliar spray or soil amendment to enhance plant growth, improve drought and stress tolerance, and suppress fungal diseases such as powdery mildew and rice blast, as well as certain insects like mites and whiteflies. It is registered by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency as a biochemical pesticide for use on a wide range of crops, including fruits, vegetables, grains, and ornamentals, with application rates typically ranging from 630 to 1260 ppm SiO equivalent, leading to yield increases of 1030% in silica-accumulating plants like rice and sugarcane. Environmentally, it poses low risk when diluted, though undiluted solutions can be toxic to aquatic life due to their alkalinity, and production emits CO as a byproduct.
Industrially, potassium silicate is valued for its adhesive and binding properties, finding applications in the manufacture of welding electrodes, detergents, paints, and protective coatings, where it acts as a deflocculant, sealant, and corrosion inhibitor. Its solutions, known as water glass, polymerize into silicate networks upon drying or curing, enhancing mechanical strength and chemical resistance in products like cements, adhesives, and ceramics. In electrochemical contexts, additives such as calcium hydrogen phosphate can further improve its anticorrosion performance, achieving high charge transfer resistance (up to 940 kcm) for metal protection. Safety considerations include potential skin and eye irritation from dust or concentrated solutions, necessitating protective equipment during handling.

Chemical composition and structure

Molecular forms and nomenclature

Potassium silicate refers to a family of inorganic compounds composed of potassium oxide and silica, with the general formula $ K_2O \cdot n \mathrm{SiO_2} $, where $ n $ represents the molar ratio of silica to potassium oxide and typically ranges from 1 to 4 depending on the specific composition. Common forms include potassium metasilicate ($ K_2SiO_3 $, where $ n=1 )andpotassiumdisilicate() and potassium disilicate ()andpotassiumdisilicate( K_2Si_2O_5 $, where $ n=2 $), though the exact stoichiometry can vary, often extending from $ K_2Si_2O_5 $ to $ K_2Si_3O_7 $. These compounds are typically produced as amorphous solids or solutions, reflecting their glassy nature derived from high-temperature fusion processes.
In nomenclature, potassium silicate is also commonly termed potassium water glass, a historical designation originating from its soluble, glassy appearance in aqueous solutions, which has been recognized since the early manufacture of soluble silicates in the mid-19th century. This name distinguishes it from sodium-based analogs and highlights its use in liquid form. Potassium silicate exists in multiple physical states: as a solid glassy material, a concentrated aqueous solution (often 20-40% solids), or a powdered form obtained by drying and grinding the solid, each suited to different applications while maintaining the core chemical identity.
The silicate modulus, defined as the molar ratio of $ \mathrm{SiO_2} $ to $ K_2O $ (i.e., $ n $ in the general formula), significantly influences the properties of potassium silicate solutions, particularly their viscosity. Higher modulus values, such as 3.3 or above, result in increased solution viscosity due to greater polymerization of silicate anions, which enhances the binding and structural integrity in uses like coatings and adhesives, whereas lower moduli (e.g., around 2.0) yield less viscous, more fluid solutions. This ratio is carefully controlled during production to tailor the compound's behavior without altering its fundamental nomenclature.

Structural characteristics

Potassium silicate displays a polymeric architecture characterized by silicate anions formed from interconnected SiO tetrahedra, where each silicon atom is centrally bonded to four oxygen atoms in a tetrahedral configuration, and these units are linked by K cations to maintain charge neutrality. In its orthosilicate form, KSiO, the structure consists of discrete, isolated SiO tetrahedra, with each tetrahedron coordinated by four K ions, representing the simplest monomeric unit without extended polymerization. By contrast, the metasilicate form, KSiO, features infinite chains of edge-sharing SiO tetrahedra, where each tetrahedron shares two apical oxygen atoms to create repeating (SiO) units, stabilized by K ions positioned between the chains.
In aqueous solutions, potassium silicate exists primarily in hydrated forms, where silicate species polymerize into colloidal particles through the formation of Si-O-Si bridging bonds between tetrahedra, often denoted in Q notation (n = number of bridging oxygens per silicon). This polymerization can be represented briefly by the condensation reaction: n SiO + 2 KOH KOnSiO + (n-1) HO, leading to chain-like or branched oligomers with Si-O-Si linkages and associated K and OH groups.
Insights from X-ray crystallography reveal that potassium silicate can adopt both amorphous (glassy) and crystalline states, with the latter showing ordered arrangements of SiO tetrahedra. In crystalline forms, typical Si-O bond lengths are approximately 1.60 within the tetrahedra, while Si-O-Si bridge angles vary around 140180, influencing the overall chain or sheet flexibility; amorphous variants exhibit similar local tetrahedral geometry but with disordered long-range connectivity.

Physical and chemical properties

Physical properties

Potassium silicate exists in both solid and liquid forms, with the solid typically appearing as a white, odorless powder or colorless amorphous glass that is hygroscopic. The liquid form is a colorless to yellowish viscous solution, often prepared at concentrations of 20-40% by weight.
It exhibits high solubility in water, readily dissolving to form clear, alkaline solutions up to 30% concentration, though it is insoluble in ethanol. The density of these aqueous solutions varies from 1.2 to 1.6 g/cm, depending on the silica-to-potash ratio and concentration. For the solid form, the melting point is approximately 900910C, while solutions freeze below 0C and may precipitate upon cooling.
The viscosity of liquid solutions ranges from 20 to 200 mPas at 20C and increases with higher silicate ratios due to polymeric chain formation. Aqueous solutions have a pH of 11 to 12, and a boiling point near 100C, often accompanied by decomposition. The refractive index for liquids is typically around 1.4 to 1.5.

Chemical properties and reactivity

Potassium silicate exhibits strong alkalinity in aqueous solutions, with pH values typically ranging from 11 to 12, arising from partial hydrolysis that releases hydroxide ions and various silicate species such as monosilicic acid and polysilicates. This hydrolysis equilibrium is dynamic, where the silicate ions (SiO) partially revert to free silica and hydroxide ions, enhancing the solution's basicity without complete dissociation.
When reacted with acids, potassium silicate undergoes neutralization, leading to the precipitation or gelation of silica. A representative reaction is:

Potassium silicate demonstrates good thermal stability, with a melting point exceeding 900C. It maintains structural integrity in inert atmospheres, showing resistance to oxidation under non-reactive conditions.
In addition to acid-base interactions, potassium silicate engages in ion exchange reactions with multivalent metal cations, such as calcium or magnesium, forming insoluble metal silicates that precipitate from solution. The compound is generally redox inert, lacking direct participation in oxidation-reduction processes, though it can serve as a Lewis acid catalyst in specific polymerization reactions, including those for biofuel production from oils.

Synthesis and production

Laboratory synthesis

Potassium silicate can be synthesized in the laboratory through the fusion of silica (SiO) with potassium carbonate (KCO) at elevated temperatures. The reaction proceeds as follows: SiO + KCO KSiO + CO. Typically, stoichiometric mixtures of finely ground silica and potassium carbonate are heated in a platinum or alumina crucible within a furnace at temperatures ranging from 12001500C, often requiring 24 hours to ensure complete reaction and formation of a homogeneous melt. The resulting potassium silicate glass is then quenched by pouring into a mold or onto a cool surface to solidify it rapidly. This method allows for the production of anhydrous potassium silicate with controlled SiO:KO ratios, commonly around 1:1 to 3:1, depending on the initial reactant proportions. Yields are generally high, exceeding 90%, provided the silica source is pure and reactive, such as precipitated silica or quartz sand.
An alternative laboratory approach involves the hydrothermal reaction of potassium hydroxide (KOH) with silica gel or sand in an aqueous solution under pressure, which is particularly useful for obtaining soluble forms with higher SiO:KO ratios. In this process, a 1050% aqueous KOH solution is mixed with a silicon dioxide source in a pressure reactor (autoclave), heated to 150300C (preferably 200250C) at autogenous pressure (approximately 1030 bar), for 15120 minutes. The SiO:KO ratio, typically targeted at 2.75:1 to 4.2:1, is controlled by adjusting the amount of silica added and reaction time; for instance, extending the duration or using tempered quartz can increase the silica content. This method yields a clear potassium silicate solution directly, avoiding the need for initial melting.
Purification in both methods involves dissolving the crude product in hot water if necessary, followed by filtration to remove unreacted silica or impurities, and decantation to settle particulates. The filtrate is then concentrated by evaporation under reduced pressure or gentle heating to obtain either a viscous solution or solid potassium silicate upon complete drying, with laboratory yields often reaching 8595% after these steps.

Commercial production

Potassium silicate was first commercialized in the 19th century in Europe, marking the beginning of its industrial production through high-temperature fusion processes. The global market has since expanded significantly, with annual production estimated at approximately 500,000 tons as of 2025, driven by demand in agriculture, construction, and manufacturing sectors. Major producing regions include China, which dominates as part of the Asia-Pacific market, and the United States, where companies like PQ Corporation operate key facilities. Production costs typically range from $500 to $800 per ton, influenced by raw material prices, energy expenses, and regional supply chains.
The primary commercial method involves the high-temperature reaction of silica sand (SiO) with potassium carbonate (KCO) or potassium hydroxide (KOH) in furnaces at temperatures exceeding 1,000C, producing a molten glass that is subsequently dissolved in water to yield the silicate solution. This furnace route, often utilizing oil-, gas-, or electrically fired furnaces such as tank or rotary kilns, allows for scalable output and control over the SiO:KO molar ratio, typically ranging from 2:1 to 3.3:1 for commercial grades. The process begins with precise mixing of raw materials, followed by fusion to release CO (when using carbonate), quenching of the melt into lumps or granules, and high-pressure dissolution in steam-heated vessels to form concentrated solutions, which are then filtered and adjusted for viscosity and pH.
This production is energy-intensive due to the elevated temperatures required for fusion, contributing to significant operational costs and environmental considerations. Modern efficiencies incorporate sustainable practices, such as utilizing waste silica sources like rice husk ash, which contains up to 90% amorphous silica and reduces reliance on virgin quartz sand while valorizing agricultural byproducts. These innovations lower energy demands and promote circular economy principles in regions with abundant agro-waste, such as Asia. Byproduct management focuses on minimizing CO emissions from carbonate decomposition and optimizing water usage in dissolution stages to enhance overall process sustainability.

Applications

Agricultural and horticultural uses

Potassium silicate serves as a valuable fertilizer in agriculture and horticulture, providing essential potassium and silicon nutrients that strengthen plant cell walls and enhance overall resilience. Silicon, absorbed as silicic acid, deposits in epidermal tissues to form a physical barrier that improves structural integrity, thereby boosting drought tolerance and reducing water loss through transpiration. Studies have demonstrated that foliar applications of potassium silicate can increase yield in silicon-responsive crops; for instance, integration with standard fertilizers led to a 108% boost in grain yield (from 3,184 kg/ha to 6,617 kg/ha) and 115% in biomass yield for bread wheat grown on acidic Nitisols. In rice and sugarcane, silicon supplementation has been shown to improve productivity under stress conditions, with reports indicating enhanced cane yield and sugar quality due to better nutrient uptake and reduced lodging.
Typical application rates for potassium silicate as a fertilizer range from 1-5 kg/ha equivalent in foliar sprays, often diluted to deliver 100-2,000 ppm SiO, applied every 2-4 weeks during active growth stages. This method is particularly effective for crops like vegetables, orchards, and cereals, where it promotes erect leaf posture, improves photosynthesis efficiency, and mitigates abiotic stresses such as salinity and drought. In hydroponic systems, concentrations of 100-250 ppm SiO in nutrient solutions support continuous uptake, leading to healthier root development and greater biomass accumulation.
In disease management, potassium silicate acts as a suppressive agent against fungal pathogens, notably powdery mildew (caused by Podosphaera xanthii or Sphaerotheca fuliginea), by facilitating silica deposition on leaf surfaces that physically impedes spore germination and hyphal penetration. Foliar sprays have reduced powdery mildew severity by up to 35% in cucurbits like cucumber and melon, often enhancing the efficacy of biocontrol agents when combined. The U.S. Department of Agriculture's National Organic Program has approved aqueous potassium silicate for use in organic farming as an allowed substance for plant disease control and soil amendment since its inclusion on the National List following the 2003 Technical Advisory Panel review, with full listing for crop production in 2011. This approval underscores its role in integrated pest management, particularly for organic systems where synthetic fungicides are restricted.
As a soil amendment, potassium silicate helps neutralize acidity in low-pH soils, raising pH levels and decreasing exchangeable aluminum toxicity, which improves nutrient availabilitysuch as a 23% increase in phosphorus (to 16.4 mg/kg)and fosters better root proliferation. In tropical acidic environments, applications of 40 kg/ha silicate combined with nitrogen-phosphorus fertilizers have alleviated soil acidity by 24%, correlating with higher crop yields and reduced cation imbalances. This amendment is especially beneficial for acid-sensitive crops like sugarcane and rice, where it enhances silicon availability without altering soil structure drastically.

Industrial and manufacturing applications

Potassium silicate serves as a versatile binder and adhesive in several industrial processes. In foundry operations, it is employed as a binder in molding sands, where it forms strong bonding bridges upon hardening through methods such as CO process, traditional drying, or microwave treatment, enabling the production of high-strength molds with reduced processing time. In ceramics manufacturing, it acts as a binder for refractory materials, facilitating the formation of durable structures during firing. Additionally, it functions as an adhesive in detergents, enhancing formulation stability and performance as a builder.
In welding electrode production, potassium silicate is a key binder that adheres coatings to the electrode core, providing mechanical strength and stable rheology for uniform application; during welding, it reacts with metals to form insoluble silicates, contributing to slag formation and arc stability. Preferred for low-alloyed steels due to its ease of arc striking and ionization properties, it is often blended with lithium silicate for high-alloy applications.
As a catalyst and additive, potassium silicate aids in controlling viscosity during paint production, where its polymerization influences the rheology and prevents premature gelation. In rubber manufacturing, it is incorporated as a processing additive to improve filler dispersion and compound properties, often in combination with silane coupling agents. It also plays a role in zeolite synthesis for water purification; potassium ions assist in directing the framework formation of SUZ-4 zeolite, reducing the need for organic templates and enabling efficient Pb adsorption from wastewater with high selectivity and reusability over multiple cycles.
Other applications include textile sizing, where potassium silicate acts as an agent to improve fabric strength and fire resistance during processing. In paper production, it serves as a coating and sizing agent, enhancing sheet strength and water resistance. Recent research (as of 2024) has explored potassium silicate as a superionic conductor material in solid-state batteries, potentially enabling higher energy density, faster charging, and improved safety.

Fire protection and coatings

Potassium silicate is widely applied in the impregnation of wood and textiles to create fire-retardant treatments that form intumescent coatings. In wood treatment, solutions of potassium silicate are pressure-impregnated into the material, where they react with acidic curing agents and metal salts to form insoluble metal silicate polymers, enhancing flame resistance and preventing leaching. Upon exposure to heat, these coatings expand, releasing water vapor that dilutes combustible gases and forms a char barrier, significantly reducing flame spread. For textiles such as hemp or cotton fabrics, potassium silicate treatments, often combined with silica or metal salts, improve fire resistance by depositing a protective layer that inhibits ignition and flame propagation, with durability maintained through washing cycles. These applications have been tested under standards like ASTM E84, where silicate-based treatments achieve Class A flame spread ratings (index of 0-25) and low smoke development, ensuring compliance for building materials.
The primary mechanism of potassium silicate's fire protection involves intumescence triggered by heat, leading to dehydration and the formation of a silicon dioxide (SiO) barrier. When heated above 100C, the alkali silicate undergoes an endothermic dehydration process, releasing bound water as vapor that causes the material to swell into a rigid, porous foam structure with low thermal conductivity. This expanded layer insulates the substrate, limiting oxygen access and heat transfer while the residual SiO provides a durable, non-combustible shield. Such properties have been utilized in fireproofing since the late 1800s, initially in mineral-based coatings for surfaces exposed to high temperatures.
In modern applications, potassium silicate features in spray-on intumescent coatings for protecting steel structures in construction, where it is mixed with silicone binders and applied as a thin film (typically 6 mm dry thickness) to delay structural failure during fires. These coatings expand under fire conditions per ISO 834 standards, maintaining steel temperatures below 500C for over 30 minutes, and form a ceramic-like foam post-exposure. Durability tests demonstrate retention of protective properties for decades, with some formulations showing no significant degradation after 20-50 years of environmental exposure due to the inorganic binder's resistance to UV, weathering, and microbial attack.

Safety and environmental aspects

Health and safety hazards

Potassium silicate is corrosive to the skin, eyes, and respiratory tract primarily due to its alkaline nature and high pH, typically ranging from 11 to 12 in solutions. Direct contact with the skin can result in redness, pain, burning sensations, and potential chemical burns or ulceration upon prolonged exposure. Eye contact leads to severe irritation, including redness, tearing, pain, and possible permanent damage such as corneal opacity if not promptly treated. Inhalation of dust, mists, or vapors irritates the respiratory tract, causing symptoms like coughing, sore throat, shortness of breath, and chest tightness; severe exposure may lead to inflammation, pulmonary edema, or spasm of the larynx and bronchi.
Potassium silicate demonstrates low acute oral toxicity, with an LD50 value exceeding 5000 mg/kg in rats, indicating it is not highly poisonous if swallowed in small amounts but can still cause gastrointestinal irritation or burns. Inhalation hazards stem from its irritant properties rather than fibrogenic effects like silicosis, as it consists of amorphous silica; however, chronic exposure to airborne dust may exacerbate respiratory conditions. The Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) permissible exposure limit (PEL) for total dust of particulates not otherwise regulated, applicable to potassium silicate, is 15 mg/m as an 8-hour time-weighted average.
Safe handling of potassium silicate requires the use of personal protective equipment (PPE), including chemical-resistant gloves, safety goggles or face shields, protective clothing, and respiratory protection such as NIOSH-approved masks if dust or mists are present. In the event of exposure, first aid measures include immediately flushing skin or eyes with large amounts of water for at least 15 minutes while removing contaminated clothing, seeking medical attention for persistent irritation; for inhalation, move the affected person to fresh air and provide oxygen or artificial respiration if breathing is difficult; and for ingestion, rinse the mouth and do not induce vomiting, followed by medical evaluation. Storage should occur in cool, dry, well-ventilated areas in tightly sealed containers to prevent gelation, moisture absorption, or reactions with acids and metals.

Environmental impact and sustainability

The production of potassium silicate is energy-intensive, involving high-temperature fusion of silica sand and potassium carbonate at approximately 1,500C, which generates CO2 emissions from the chemical reaction as a byproduct. Estimates for similar silicate processes indicate around 1 ton of CO2 emissions per ton of product, though specific data for potassium silicate vary by facility efficiency. Additionally, the dissolution step to produce liquid forms requires significant water input, contributing to overall resource consumption in manufacturing.
Although it poses low environmental risk when properly diluted, undiluted solutions can be toxic to aquatic life due to their high alkalinity. In environmental applications, potassium silicate offers benefits as an inorganic compound that does not bioaccumulate and integrates naturally into soil without persistent harm to ecosystems. When applied in agriculture, it enhances plant resilience to abiotic stresses such as drought and salinity by strengthening cell walls and improving water use efficiency, thereby supporting adaptation to climate variability. Furthermore, its use as a silicon source can help reduce reliance on synthetic pesticides through improved natural pest resistance in treated crops.
Potassium silicate complies with EU REACH regulations, as evidenced by its registration as silicic acid, potassium salt, ensuring controlled handling and environmental release. Trends toward sustainability include its increasing adoption in green building materials, such as concrete sealers that meet LEED certifications by providing low-VOC, durable protection without contributing to alkali-silica reactions. Waste silicates from industrial processes, like drilling fluids, can be recycled through land application, where they amend soil without adverse effects and potentially enhance nutrient availability.
