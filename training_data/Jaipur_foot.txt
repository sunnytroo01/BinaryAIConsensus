Jaipur foot
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Jaipur Foot is a low-cost, durable prosthetic foot designed primarily for amputees in developing countries, enabling natural barefoot walking, squatting, and movement on uneven terrain using locally available materials like rubber and wood.
Developed in 1968 by orthopedic surgeon Dr. Pramod Karan Sethi and craftsman Ram Chandra Sharma at Sawai Man Singh Hospital in Jaipur, India, it represents a modification of the Solid Ankle Cushioned Heel (SACH) foot to better suit rural, barefoot lifestyles in resource-limited settings. The design incorporates a rubber keel for ankle flexion and torsion, aluminum shanks for support, and later enhancements like ethylene vinyl acetate (EVA) liners for cushioning, allowing users to perform culturally essential activities such as cross-legged sitting without specialized footwear.
Production and distribution are led by the Bhagwan Mahaveer Viklang Sahayata Samiti (BMVSS), a nonprofit organization founded in 1975 in Jaipur, which has fitted over 740,000 limbs worldwide as of 2024, often at no cost through donor funding and mobile camps in 44 countries. The prosthesis costs approximately US$30 to produce, far below the US$12,000 for advanced Western equivalents, and demonstrates longevity of up to 310 years depending on usage.
Globally recognized for its innovation, the Jaipur Foot was featured in Time magazine and has inspired collaborations with institutions like Stanford University, MIT, and the Indian Space Research Organisation for further refinements, including the low-cost Jaipur Knee for above-knee amputees. Its emphasis on affordability, functionality in harsh environments, and cultural adaptability has transformed rehabilitation access for millions, particularly in Asia, Africa, and Latin America, with ongoing fitment camps continuing into 2025.

Overview

Definition and Purpose

The Jaipur Foot is a rubber-based, non-articulated prosthetic foot designed specifically for below-knee amputations, developed in India as a modification of the Solid Ankle Cushioned Heel (SACH) foot by replacing the traditional wooden keel with a microcellular rubber block for enhanced flexibility and durability. This design allows for natural foot movements without mechanical joints, making it suitable for everyday use in diverse environments.
Its primary purpose is to restore affordable mobility to amputees in resource-limited settings, enabling essential daily activities such as walking on uneven or muddy terrain, squatting, sitting cross-legged, and working in rural or agricultural contextsfunctions often incompatible with conventional Western prosthetics that prioritize shoe use and smooth surfaces. By facilitating barefoot operation and cultural practices common in developing regions, the Jaipur Foot bridges gaps in accessibility, promoting independence and social inclusion for users who cannot afford high-cost alternatives.
The device targets primarily below-knee amputees in developing countries across Southeast Asia, South America, and Africa, including landmine victims from conflict zones and low-income individuals in farming communities who require rugged, low-maintenance solutions. In comparison to standard Western prosthetics, which often cost thousands of dollars and emphasize energy return for athletic performance, the Jaipur Foot prioritizes cultural adaptability and cost-effectiveness at around $50 per unit, though it offers less shock absorption during high-impact activities.

Key Features

The Jaipur Foot is renowned for its non-articulated design, which provides multi-axial mobility in dorsiflexion, inversion/eversion, and transverse rotation, mimicking natural foot movement without requiring mechanical joints. This bio-mimetic structure allows users to perform culturally significant activities such as squatting, cross-legged sitting, and navigating uneven terrain, making it particularly suitable for rural and barefoot lifestyles in developing regions.
Its durability stems from the use of vulcanized rubber and microcellular materials, rendering it water-resistant and capable of withstanding harsh conditions like muddy fields or prolonged barefoot use. Under normal conditions, the prosthesis has an average lifespan of 3-5 years, enabling reliable long-term performance without frequent replacements.
Weighing approximately 0.85-1.5 kg for a below-knee assembly, the Jaipur Foot balances robustness with wearability, though its weight can feel substantial during extended activities; cushioning from ethylene vinyl acetate liners enhances comfort for daily tasks like farming or light labor. The design facilitates rapid fittingoften in under a dayusing basic tools and casts, bypassing the need for advanced orthopedic facilities. As of 2025, BMVSS continues to conduct global fitment camps, including in Afghanistan and Trinidad and Tobago.
A related innovation, the Jaipur Knee, complements the foot for above-knee amputees with its polycentric mechanism, originating from the same Bhagwan Mahaveer Viklang Samiti (BMVSS) efforts to create affordable, high-mobility solutions.

History and Development

Origins

In 1966, a team from the World Health Organization visited the Sawai Man Singh Medical College in Jaipur, India, where they observed the challenges faced by local doctors in treating amputees and introduced the Solid Ankle Cushioned Heel (SACH) foot as a potential solution. However, the SACH foot, imported from Germany, proved unsuitable for the cultural and environmental context of Indian users, with an estimated 80% rejection rate due to its rigidity and inability to support activities like squatting, sitting cross-legged, or working barefoot on uneven rural terrain.
These observations led to the conception of a more appropriate prosthetic in 1968, when Dr. Pramod Karan Sethi and his team at Sawai Man Singh Medical College recognized the unmet needs of amputees who required a device enabling natural dorsiflexion and flexibility for daily life in barefoot cultures. The design addressed the SACH foot's limitations by incorporating locally available materials to create a durable, low-cost alternative that allowed users to perform essential activities without discomfort or restriction.
The first prototype of the Jaipur Foot was developed in 1968 as a direct modification of the SACH foot, replacing the wooden keel with a rubber block sourced from automobile tires to enhance flexibility and mimic natural foot movement. This iteration, crafted with input from local artisan Ram Chandra Sharma, marked the initial success in fitting the device to patients, though production remained limited in the early years. The first Jaipur Foot was fitted in 1968, with approximately 50 limbs provided over the next seven years.
To scale production and provide free access to the prosthetic for the underprivileged, the Bhagwan Mahaveer Viklang Sahayata Samiti (BMVSS) was founded in 1975 by Devendra Raj Mehta in Jaipur, inspired by his own near-amputation experience and a commitment to rehabilitation services. In its inaugural year, BMVSS fitted 59 Jaipur Feet, building on the roughly 50 limbs provided in the preceding seven years and establishing a dedicated framework for ongoing manufacturing and distribution.

Key Contributors and Milestones

The development of the Jaipur Foot is primarily attributed to two key innovators: orthopedic surgeon Dr. Pramod Karan Sethi and master craftsman Ram Chandra Sharma. Sethi, working at Sawai Man Singh Hospital in Jaipur, provided the medical expertise to address the needs of amputees in resource-limited settings, while Sharma contributed his skills in rubber molding and sculpture to create a durable, flexible prosthetic using locally available materials like rubber and wood. Sethi passed away in 2008.
A pivotal organizational figure was D.R. Mehta, who founded the Bhagwan Mahaveer Viklang Sahayata Samiti (BMVSS) in 1975 to scale production and distribution of the prosthesis, transforming it from a local innovation into a global initiative that has fitted over 2 million limbs to people worldwide as of 2025.
Major milestones began with the first fittings in 1968, marking the prosthesis's practical debut for everyday use in rural India. In the 1980s, the Jaipur Foot gained international recognition through BMVSS-led fitting camps in 27 countries, particularly aiding landmine victims in conflict zones like Afghanistan, Cambodia, and Vietnam.
During the 1990s, material enhancements emerged from collaborative research with institutions like the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) and Indian Institutes of Technology (IITs), laying groundwork for lighter alternatives to the original rubber design, though full polyurethane implementation followed in the early 2000s. The 2000s saw further advancement with the introduction of the Jaipur Knee, a low-cost polycentric knee joint developed in partnership with Stanford University starting around 2008, enabling affordable above-knee prosthetics at about US$20 per unit and expanding options for complex amputations.
The prosthesis's non-patented status, intentionally maintained by its creators, has facilitated widespread replication and adaptation worldwide, promoting accessibility without intellectual property barriers.

Design and Materials

Components

The Jaipur Foot's core structure comprises a rubber keel that supports toe-to-heel roll-over motion, an ankle block typically constructed from wood or polyurethane for structural support, and a vulcanized outer rubber layer that encases the assembly for protection and cosmetic appearance. The keel, originally a modification replacing the solid wooden keel of the SACH foot, incorporates a sponge rubber block acting as a universal joint between proximal and distal wooden blocks to enable multi-planar mobility. The distal block provides forefoot rigidity, enhancing stability on uneven surfaces through even weight distribution during stance.
Key materials include soles made from scrap tire rubber in the initial design, valued for its affordability and durability in rugged conditions, with modern iterations incorporating high-density polyethylene (HDPE) for select components to improve longevity and reduce environmental impact. The socket, which interfaces directly with the residual limb, is fabricated from polypropylene or HDPE, offering a lightweight, seamless, and skin-colored fit that withstands climatic variations. Nylon cords are embedded within the rubber for reinforcement, while the outer layer uses a combination of hard vulcanized rubber for the tread and softer cushion compounds for comfort.
The design has evolved significantly since its inception in 1968, when it relied on rubber-wood composites for simplicity and local availability. Later iterations introduced polyurethane foams and elastomers, providing lighter weight and better energy return. In the 2000s, HDPE became prominent for sockets and shanks, offering superior impact resistance and a lifespan of 3-4 years under typical use. More recently, in the 2020s, 3D printing has been integrated for socket production through international collaborations, enhancing fitting precision. These material advancements maintain the prosthesis's lightweight design, around 850 grams as of 2016, while prioritizing accessibility with locally sourced inputs.

Functionality

The Jaipur Foot's flexible ankle mechanism, achieved through a rubber block that replaces the traditional wooden keel in SACH designs, enables multi-axial movement including dorsiflexion, inversion, eversion, and axial rotation. This design allows users to perform culturally and occupationally relevant activities such as squatting, cross-legged sitting, kneeling, and climbing uneven terrain or trees, mimicking natural foot biomechanics in low-resource settings. The rubber component compresses during heel strike and provides a degree of energy return through elastic rebound, facilitating smoother gait transitions and reduced effort for walking on varied surfaces compared to rigid alternatives.
Despite these capabilities, the Jaipur Foot has notable limitations in performance relative to advanced prosthetics. Its shock absorption is inferior to carbon fiber energy-storing and returning (ESAR) feet, as the rubber keel dissipates energy through deformation rather than efficient storage, leading to higher ground reaction forces and increased walking effort. It is unsuitable for high-speed running or athletic activities beyond limited community ambulation, as the non-articulated structure lacks the stiffness and propulsion needed for such demands. Additionally, the foot requires periodic replacement after an average lifespan of 3 to 4 years, depending on usage intensity and environmental exposure.
The prosthesis demonstrates strong adaptability for users in rural and developing contexts, being fully waterproof and resistant to dirt, mud, and abrasive conditions, which supports agricultural labor, wading through water, or barefoot use without rapid deterioration. This durability aligns with needs in barefoot cultures, where it can be worn shoeless or with simple footwear, and its universal sizing accommodates diverse body types. Fitting is notably rapid, typically completed in 1 to 2 hours for below-knee amputees using on-site casting and assembly techniques.
In terms of testing, the Jaipur Foot complies with basic requirements of the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) 10328 for structural integrity and fatigue resistance in lower-limb prosthetics, as demonstrated in mechanical evaluations of its rubber components under cyclic loading. It also meets guidelines from the World Health Organization (WHO) and International Society for Prosthetics and Orthotics (ISPO) for category-1 devices suitable for household and limited community mobility in low-income settings. However, it falls short of advanced biomechanical standards for energy efficiency or high-impact activities, prioritizing affordability over elite performance metrics.

Production and Manufacturing

Processes

The fabrication of the Jaipur Foot prosthesis begins with patient assessment, including evaluation of the residual limb's condition, such as joint function, muscle strength, skin integrity, and pain levels, followed by precise measurements of stump length, circumferences, and limb dimensions to ensure proper alignment and fit. A negative cast is then created by applying a moist cast sock to the limb, marking pressure-tolerant areas like the patellar tendon, and wrapping with plaster bandages while applying controlled pressure; once hardened, the cast is removed and filled with plaster of Paris to form a positive mold, which is modified by scooping non-weight-bearing regions and adding build-ups to sensitive areas for comfort.
The socket is thermoformed using high-density polyethylene (HDPE) sheets, heated to approximately 180C for 20 minutes and shaped over the positive mold via suction to create a double-walled structure that provides durability and flexibility; an inner soft insert, made from ethaflex sheets glued into a cone and molded similarly, lines the socket for cushioning, with optional sheepskin at the proximal edge for scarred or sensitive stumps. The prosthetic foot itself is molded through compression techniques, starting with blocks of microcellular rubber (MCR)soft for the forefoot and toes, hard for the heeljoined with adhesives, coated in vulcanized rubber cement, wrapped in cushion rubber and tire cord for reinforcement, assembled in a die, and vulcanized in an autoclave to form a cohesive, flexible unit with embedded nylon cords for strength.
Assembly involves attaching the foot to the socket extension (an HDPE shank molded from pipe sections) using screws and adhesives, followed by alignment checks and addition of a suspension belt for secure fit. Quality control emphasizes on-site adjustments during trial fittings to eliminate pressure points, verify gait stability, and ensure no misalignment, with iterative modifications to the socket and components as needed. Local materials, such as scrap rubber from automobile tires for the foot's MCR components, are incorporated to minimize costs while maintaining functionality.
The entire process is designed for time efficiency, typically completed in 1-3 days per unit, which facilitates camp-based production where patients receive custom fittings on-site without extended waits. Innovations include the shift to modular components, such as standardized HDPE shanks and interchangeable rubber blocks, allowing for faster replication and scalability in resource-limited settings.

Facilities and Capacity

The primary production facility for the Jaipur Foot is the headquarters of the Bhagwan Mahaveer Viklang Sahayata Samiti (BMVSS) in Jaipur, India, which serves as the central hub for manufacturing and rehabilitation services. This main center, located at S.M.S. Hospital, 13-A Guru Nanak Path, Malviya Nagar, includes specialized departments for prosthetic fabrication, advanced physiotherapy, and robotic neuro-physiotherapy. BMVSS operates additional branches across India, including in Mumbai, Indore, Ranchi, Kanpur, New Delhi, Hyderabad, Chennai, Bengaluru, Guwahati, Bharatpur, Kota, Pali-Marwar, Jodhpur, Bikaner, Udaipur, Ambala, Ajmer, Ahmedabad, Patna, Varanasi, Noida, Jamshedpur, Chaibasa, Jabalpur, Srinagar, Khordha, and Pune, totaling 27 centers that support nationwide production and fitting operations.
As of 2024, BMVSS's annual production capacity exceeds 35,000 prosthetic limbs, including approximately 16,000 Jaipur Feet, enabling the rehabilitation of 90,000 to 100,000 individuals each year through the provision of limbs, calipers, wheelchairs, and other assistive devices. This output is achieved through efficient, low-cost manufacturing processes at the Jaipur facility and its branches, ensuring scalability to meet demand in underserved regions. To extend reach into remote areas, BMVSS employs a camp model featuring mobile units that facilitate on-site fabrication and fitting, conducting over 105 such camps annually within India and internationally as of 2024. In 2025, this includes international expansions such as a camp in Kabul, Afghanistan, fitting 75 amputees, and a planned permanent fitment center in Trinidad and Tobago.
Ongoing research and development at BMVSS facilities focus on enhancing the Jaipur Foot's design for lighter materials and greater sustainability, including explorations of composites, biocompatible polymers, and recyclable components like HDPE and nylon to reduce weight from the current 1.1-1.7 kg range while minimizing environmental impact. Collaborations with institutions such as MIT, Stanford, and IIT Madras support innovations in adaptability, such as multifunctional prosthetics and 3D-printed sockets, with 2024 efforts emphasizing local production to lower carbon footprints and improve global scalability.

Distribution and Accessibility

In India

The Jaipur Foot is provided free of charge to eligible individuals in India through the Bhagwan Mahaveer Viklang Sahayata Samiti (BMVSS), which operates dedicated centers and organizes outreach camps across the country. To access this service, beneficiaries must present basic documents such as an Aadhaar Card and a Disability Certificate issued by a recognized authority, ensuring the process is accessible yet verified. This policy enables rapid fitting, often on the same day, supporting immediate rehabilitation for those in need.
BMVSS collaborates with the Ministry of Social Justice and Empowerment, Government of India, under the Assistance to Disabled Persons for Purchase/Fitting of Aids/Appliances (ADIP) Scheme, which provides funding and facilitates rural outreach programs. These partnerships extend the Jaipur Foot's availability to remote areas, where mobile camps address barriers like distance and poverty. The government's support through ADIP has been instrumental in scaling domestic distribution, prioritizing underserved populations.
Since its inception in 1975, BMVSS has benefited over 2.2 million individuals worldwide as of 2025, with the vast majority in India, with a particular emphasis on amputees from landmine incidents, road accidents, and other traumas. These beneficiaries include survivors of conflicts in border regions and everyday accident victims, enabling them to resume active lives in diverse terrains. The program's scale underscores its role in addressing India's high incidence of limb loss due to such causes.
The Jaipur Foot is integrated into India's healthcare ecosystem through collaborations with government hospitals and non-governmental organizations (NGOs), facilitating swift rehabilitation protocols. Under the ADIP framework, BMVSS works alongside public health facilities to fit prosthetics, often incorporating post-fitting therapy for optimal outcomes. NGOs complement this by conducting community-based camps in partnership with state rehabilitation centers, ensuring holistic support beyond fitting. This integration promotes equitable access within the national disability framework.

Global Reach

The Jaipur Foot has achieved significant international distribution through over 110 on-the-spot fitment camps organized by the Bhagwan Mahaveer Viklang Sahayata Samiti (BMVSS) across 44 countries, enabling rapid prosthetic fitting for amputees in diverse regions. These efforts include targeted missions in conflict-affected areas, such as a five-day camp in Kabul, Afghanistan, in June 2025, where 75 amputees received free limbs, and multiple camps in Iraq during 2024, including a major initiative from March to April that restored mobility to thousands. In Africa, camps have been held in countries like Mozambique, with a 2025 event in Maputo funded to fit up to 1,200 beneficiaries. Many of these international camps, totaling 28 in 22 countries, receive funding and logistical support from India's Ministry of External Affairs under the "India for Humanity" program, emphasizing humanitarian aid in developing and post-conflict nations.
Abroad, the Jaipur Foot is priced between $40 and $80 per unit, reflecting its low-cost design using locally available materials, which contrasts sharply with Western prosthetics costing thousands of dollars. To promote sustainability and avoid intellectual property barriers, BMVSS provides training programs for local prosthetists in fabrication techniques, as the technology was intentionally not patented by its inventor, Dr. P.K. Sethi, allowing replication worldwide without licensing fees.
Key collaborations enhance the prosthesis's global dissemination, leveraging its simple, durable construction for resource-limited settings. The Jaipur Foot USA chapter has supported North American initiatives, such as multi-city fitment camps in 2023 funded through community events like Diwali celebrations, which raised resources for prosthetics in underserved areas.
In warzones and refugee contexts, the Jaipur Foot features variations optimized for quick assembly, with its "Rapid Fit Technology" enabling fabrication in as little as 45 minutes and fitting within hours, ideal for mobile camps in areas like Afghanistan and Iraq where immediate mobility is critical for survival and displacement. This adaptability has made it a staple in humanitarian responses, supporting amputees in unstable environments without requiring advanced infrastructure.

Impact and Significance

Social and Economic Effects

The Jaipur Foot has significantly empowered amputees by restoring mobility, enabling them to return to work and reduce dependency on family or social support systems. This prosthetic device allows users to resume labor-intensive activities, such as farming or manual trades, which are common in rural and low-income communities, thereby fostering economic independence. For instance, studies indicate that a substantial portion of recipients regain their previous occupations, with one analysis reporting an 77.9% success rate in occupational reintegration. Additionally, the low production cost of approximately $45 per unitcompared to over $8,000 for comparable Western prostheticsresults in substantial economic savings for individuals and healthcare systems in developing regions, as the device is provided free of charge and has a lifespan of 3-4 years.
The design of the Jaipur Foot aligns closely with cultural practices in South Asia and similar regions, supporting traditional lifestyles by permitting squatting, sitting cross-legged, and walking barefoot on uneven terrainactivities essential for daily routines and religious observances. This cultural compatibility enhances social integration, as users can participate fully in community events and family roles without the limitations imposed by less adaptable prosthetics. Furthermore, the device's role in improving mobility correlates with better mental health outcomes, including reduced despondency and increased confidence, as evidenced by cases where recipients overcame psychological barriers to resume active lives.
In terms of gender and equity, the Jaipur Foot promotes inclusivity by providing free access without discrimination based on gender, caste, creed, religion, or socioeconomic status, thereby addressing historical barriers that often exclude marginalized groups from rehabilitation services. This equitable approach particularly benefits women in labor-intensive roles, such as agriculture or household chores, by enabling them to maintain productivity and economic contributions within their communities.
Despite these benefits, challenges persist, including the need for periodic inspections and eventual replacement after 3-4 years, highlighting the need for sustained support infrastructure to ensure long-term reliability.

Statistics and Case Studies

As of 2025, the Jaipur Foot initiative has provided prosthetic limbs and assistive devices to over 2.2 million beneficiaries worldwide. The program fits approximately 35,000 artificial limbs annually, alongside 22,000 calipers and 40,000 other assistive devices. BMVSS has conducted over 110 international camps in 44 countries.
Long-term rehabilitation efforts reach 90,000 to 100,000 individuals each year, encompassing fittings, training, and follow-up care. User satisfaction with the Jaipur Foot stands at around 85%, with compliance rates exceeding 90% in clinical follow-ups, reflecting high retention and effective daily functionality.
One notable case is that of Indian classical dancer Sudha Chandran, who lost her right leg below the knee in a 1981 bus accident that led to gangrene and amputation. Fitted with a customized Jaipur Foot prosthetic, she resumed her Bharatanatyam training within months and returned to performing, inspiring her biographical film Mayuri in 1986.
In a 2023 mission in Baghdad, Iraq, the Jaipur Foot team, supported by the Indian Embassy, fitted prosthetics to war-affected amputees during a month-long camp, building on prior efforts that have aided 2,517 Iraqis since 2010, many victims of conflict-related injuries.

Recognition

Awards and Honors

The development of the Jaipur Foot earned significant recognition for its key innovators. Dr. Pramod Karan Sethi, the orthopedic surgeon who co-invented the prosthesis, received the Ramon Magsaysay Award for Community Leadership in 1981 from the Ramon Magsaysay Award Foundation, honoring his innovative surgical and rehabilitation efforts for amputees in resource-limited settings. That same year, Sethi was also awarded the Padma Shri by the Government of India for his contributions to medicine and public health. Ram Chandra Sharma, the master craftsman who collaborated with Sethi on the design, was presented with the Diwali-Ben Award in 1999 by the Diwali-Ben Charitable Trust for his role in creating an affordable, functional prosthetic. D. R. Mehta, founder of the Bhagwan Mahaveer Viklang Sahayata Samiti (BMVSS) that scaled the Jaipur Foot's production and distribution, received the Rajasthan Ratna Award in 2013 from the Government of Rajasthan for exemplary social service in disability rehabilitation. In December 2024, Mehta was conferred the Lifetime Achievement Award by the International Human Rights Organization (IHRO) for his lifelong contributions to disability rehabilitation through BMVSS.
The BMVSS organization itself garnered prestigious accolades for advancing the Jaipur Foot's global accessibility. In 1999, BMVSS was honored with the Mahavir Award for Philanthropic Services by the Avya Trust, recognizing its innovative approach to low-cost prosthetics and rehabilitation for the underprivileged. The Jaipur Foot was featured by TIME magazine in 1997 as one of the greatest inventions of the 20th century, praised for its affordability and adaptability in developing regions. Additionally, in 2007, D. R. Mehta accepted the Tech Award for Innovation for the Benefit of Humanity on behalf of BMVSS from The Tech Museum of Innovation in San Jose, California, for the prosthesis's impact on mobility for millions worldwide.
While no major formal awards have been conferred since 2013, the Jaipur Foot's enduring global influence was acknowledged in 2025 by the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP), which highlighted BMVSS's rehabilitation efforts as a model for inclusive development and disability rights in its feature story "Best Foot Forward."

Media and Cultural Mentions

The Jaipur Foot has been prominently featured in popular culture, particularly through the 1986 Hindi film Naache Mayuri, a biopic inspired by the life of dancer Sudha Chandran, who lost her leg in an accident and regained her mobility using the prosthetic, enabling her to resume her career. This portrayal highlighted the transformative potential of the device for amputees pursuing active lifestyles. Additionally, documentaries have showcased the Bhagwan Mahaveer Viklang Sahayata Samiti (BMVSS) camps, including a 2004 BBC production that documented the fitting process and life-changing impact on recipients worldwide.
Media coverage has elevated the Jaipur Foot's profile globally, with Time magazine recognizing it in its 1997 Fall issue as one of the greatest inventions of the 20th century for its affordability and functionality in resource-limited settings. In 2025, United Nations peacekeeping reports noted its use by Indian battalions in treating over 80 landmine victims in Lebanon, emphasizing its role in post-conflict rehabilitation. Reports from 2024 detailed a BMVSS mission in Iraq, where teams fitted prosthetic limbs to war-affected individuals, restoring mobility and dignity amid humanitarian challenges.
As a symbol of frugal innovation in India, the Jaipur Foot exemplifies cost-effective engineering using local materials to address disability in low-income contexts, influencing discussions on sustainable healthcare solutions. It has been referenced in global disability rights forums, including United Nations events in 2025 that praised BMVSS for rehabilitating over 50,000 people across 44 countries through 114 camps.
Recent mentions include a 2023 visit by a U.S. Senate delegation to the BMVSS Jaipur Foot center, where senators observed the production and fitting processes, underscoring international appreciation for its scalable model. In 2024, research papers highlighted its sustainability, focusing on enhancements for durability, affordability, and adaptability to diverse user needs.
