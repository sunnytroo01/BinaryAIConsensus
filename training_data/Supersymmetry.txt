Supersymmetry

1x
 Supersymmetry (SUSY) is a theoretical framework in particle physics that extends the Standard Model by positing a fundamental symmetry relating bosons, particles with integer spin that mediate forces, to fermions, particles with half-integer spin that constitute matter, such that each type of particle has a superpartner of the opposite type. This symmetry transforms fermions into bosons and vice versa, generalizing space-time symmetries of quantum field theory to include these pairings. Proposed independently in the early 1970s by physicists including Yuri Golfand and Evgeny Likhtman, as well as Julius Wess and Bruno Zumino, supersymmetry aims to address key limitations of the Standard Model, such as the hierarchy problem, where radiative corrections would otherwise drive the Higgs boson mass to enormous values incompatible with observations.
One of the primary motivations for supersymmetry is its potential to stabilize the electroweak scale against quantum corrections, as the contributions from bosonic and fermionic loops cancel due to the symmetry, preventing the hierarchy between the electroweak and Planck scales from being destabilized. Additionally, SUSY facilitates grand unification by predicting that the three gauge couplings of the Standard Model converge at high energies within models like the Minimal Supersymmetric Standard Model (MSSM), which doubles the particle spectrum with superpartners such as squarks for quarks and sleptons for leptons. It also provides a natural candidate for dark matter in the form of the lightest supersymmetric particle (LSP), often the neutralino, which remains stable under reasonable assumptions like R-parity conservation and could account for the observed cosmic dark matter density.
In the MSSM, supersymmetry is spontaneously broken at low energies through soft-breaking terms, allowing superpartners to have masses around the TeV scale while evading strict experimental constraints on exact symmetry. This framework incorporates the Higgs mechanism with two Higgs doublets, resolving issues like the absence of a tree-level Higgs mass term and predicting additional Higgs bosons. Supersymmetry has profound implications beyond particle physics, including connections to string theory where it ensures consistency and anomaly cancellation.
Despite its elegance, supersymmetry remains unobserved, with searches at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) excluding many parameter spaces for superpartners below approximately 2 TeV in various models as of 2025, prompting explorations of non-minimal or split SUSY variants. Ongoing experiments continue to probe higher energies and alternative signatures, such as long-lived particles or compressed spectra, to test this cornerstone of beyond-Standard-Model physics.

Core Principles

Boson-Fermion Correspondence

In particle physics, bosons are fundamental particles characterized by integer values of spin, which obey Bose-Einstein statistics, permitting multiple particles to occupy the same quantum state. Fermions, in contrast, possess half-integer spin values and follow Fermi-Dirac statistics, enforcing the Pauli exclusion principle that restricts them to unique quantum states. Supersymmetry establishes a profound correspondence between these two classes by positing a symmetry transformation that interchanges bosons with fermions, thereby linking particles of differing spin statistics within a unified framework.
This interchange manifests through the organization of particles into supermultiplets, irreducible representations where each bosonic state is paired with a fermionic superpartner (and vice versa), ensuring equal masses and related quantum numbers in the case of unbroken supersymmetry. These superpartners, often termed s-particles (e.g., selectrons for electrons), extend the particle spectrum while preserving the symmetry, with the transformation mediated by supersymmetric charges that alter spin by one-half unit.
A primary physical motivation for this boson-fermion correspondence arises in addressing the hierarchy problem, where quantum corrections to the Higgs boson mass would otherwise induce unnaturally large values unless canceled by contributions from superpartners of comparable mass, stabilizing the electroweak scale against Planck-scale effects. Additionally, supersymmetry facilitates the unification of matter fields (fermions) and gauge bosons (force mediators) within grand unified theories, as the symmetry equalizes their contributions to running couplings, promoting convergence at high energies.
Representative examples of this pairing include squarks, the scalar superpartners of quarks, which carry color charge and baryon number; sleptons, scalar partners of leptons such as electrons and neutrinos, inheriting their lepton number and weak interactions; and gauginos, the fermionic superpartners of gauge bosons, which are Majorana fermions transforming under the adjoint representation of the gauge group. This correspondence is mathematically enabled by extensions of Lie algebras to graded structures accommodating both bosonic and fermionic generators.

Graded Symmetry Structure

Supersymmetry extends the framework of ordinary symmetries in physics, which are typically described by Lie groups whose Lie algebras are generated solely by bosonic operators satisfying standard commutation relations. These bosonic generators correspond to continuous transformations such as translations, rotations, and internal gauge symmetries that preserve the structure of spacetime and particle interactions.
To incorporate supersymmetry, the algebraic structure is generalized to a super-Lie algebra, a Z_2-graded Lie algebra comprising even (bosonic) generators and odd (fermionic) generators. The even sector forms a conventional Lie subalgebra, while the odd sector consists of Grassmann-odd elements that generate transformations mixing bosonic and fermionic states. This bifurcation allows the algebra to unify spacetime and statistical symmetries in a single graded framework.90223-6)
The Z_2 grading partitions the algebra into even and odd components, with the supercommutator (graded Lie bracket) preserving the grading: even generators commute with everything in the usual manner, odd generators anticommute among themselves, and mixed brackets yield odd results. This grading structure ensures that the algebraic operations respect the fermionic nature of the odd generators, distinguishing supersymmetry from ungraded symmetries.
In supersymmetry, the graded Lie algebra extends the Poincar group by adjoining odd generators to the even generators of translations, Lorentz transformations, and possibly internal symmetries. These odd generators effectively mix spacetime translations (bosonic) with internal transformations, creating a unified symmetry that acts non-trivially on both coordinates and spinor fields.90223-6)
The closure property of the super-Lie algebra guarantees that repeated applications of even and odd transformations remain within the algebra, maintaining consistency under group multiplication. In quantum theories, this closure facilitates the construction of finite-dimensional irreducible representations, where the spectrum of states is organized into multiplets accommodating both bosonic and fermionic particles.
Unlike global internal symmetries, which act separately on flavor or charge without altering spacetime, supersymmetry functions as a spacetime symmetry that inherently relates particles differing in spin-statistics, thereby linking bosons and fermions within the same representation. This graded structure underpins the physical pairing of bosons and fermions as a direct consequence of the Z_2 extension.

Mathematical Formalism

Historical Development

Origins in Lie Algebras and Early Proposals

The concept of supersymmetry emerged from efforts in the 1960s to unify bosonic and fermionic degrees of freedom in particle physics, particularly within the framework of hadron spectroscopy. In 1966, Hironari Miyazawa proposed a hidden symmetry relating mesons (bosons) to baryons (fermions) through an SU(6|2) supergroup structure, aiming to explain the observed mass degeneracies and interactions among hadrons without invoking quarks explicitly. This early idea, though not framed in modern supersymmetric terms, anticipated the boson-fermion correspondence by treating hadronic states as components of supermultiplets, motivated by the need to resolve discrepancies in strong interaction symmetries beyond SU(3) flavor.
The mathematical foundations of supersymmetry were laid in the early 1970s through the study of Z-graded Lie algebras, also known as super Lie algebras, which extend ordinary Lie algebras by incorporating a grading that distinguishes even (bosonic) and odd (fermionic) elements. These structures were systematically explored by mathematicians such as Victor Kac, who in 1971 began classifying finite-dimensional simple Lie superalgebras using techniques from Z-graded Lie theory, providing the algebraic framework for symmetries mixing bosons and fermions. Graded Lie algebras had conceptual precursors in topology and deformation theory dating back to the 1940s, but their relevance to physics became clear in this period as a tool for Bose-Fermi symmetries.
A pivotal early proposal came in 1971 when Pierre Ramond introduced worldsheet supersymmetry in the context of dual resonance models for fermionic strings, extending the bosonic string theory by including anticommuting worldsheet coordinates to cancel anomalies and incorporate spin-1/2 particles naturally. This marked the first physical application of supersymmetric invariance on the string worldsheet, motivated by the desire to unify open and closed string spectra while addressing the absence of fermions in prior models.
In particle physics, the first explicit four-dimensional supersymmetry algebra was proposed independently in 1971 by Yuri Golfand and Evgeny Likhtman, who extended the Poincar algebra with supercharges that mix bosonic and fermionic generators, violating parity invariance while preserving Lorentz invariance and leading to equal numbers of bosonic and fermionic states. Their work, published in the Soviet Journal of Experimental and Theoretical Physics Letters, laid the groundwork for supersymmetric field theories by demonstrating the consistency of such a graded extension.
Building on these algebraic foundations, Julius Wess and Bruno Zumino constructed the first interacting supersymmetric quantum field theory in four dimensions in 1974, a supersymmetric extension of quantum electrodynamics featuring chiral supermultiplets and gauge invariance under supergauge transformations. This model realized the supersymmetry algebra off-shell through auxiliary fields, enabling renormalizable interactions while maintaining the equality of boson and fermion degrees of freedom, and it highlighted the potential for supersymmetry to resolve ultraviolet divergences in field theories.

Major Theoretical Milestones

In the mid-1970s, following initial proposals for supersymmetry in quantum field theories, significant progress was made in constructing explicit supersymmetric models. Julius Wess and Bruno Zumino introduced the first interacting four-dimensional supersymmetric quantum field theory, known as the Wess-Zumino model, which demonstrated the viability of linearly realized supersymmetry through a chiral superfield comprising a complex scalar and a Majorana spinor. Concurrently, Sergio Ferrara, Wess, and Zumino developed the framework of supergauge multiplets and superfields, enabling the formulation of supersymmetric gauge theories that preserved the symmetry under gauge transformations.90182-3) These advancements laid the groundwork for realistic model building, with Pierre Fayet proposing early supersymmetric extensions of the Standard Model precursors, incorporating spontaneous supersymmetry breaking via the Fayet-Iliopoulos mechanism to generate masses for particles while addressing hierarchy issues.90187-2)
During the 1980s, supersymmetry's role in unifying fundamental interactions gained prominence through its integration with string theory. Michael Green and John Schwarz demonstrated anomaly cancellation in ten-dimensional supersymmetric gauge theories and type I superstring theory, resolving longstanding inconsistencies in perturbative string spectra by showing that gauge, gravitational, and mixed anomalies vanish for specific gauge groups like SO(32) and E8E8.91565-X) This mechanism not only stabilized superstring perturbation theory but also established supersymmetry as essential for consistent quantum gravity formulations, paving the way for heterotic string models that incorporate grand unification.
In the 1990s, non-perturbative aspects of supersymmetric theories were illuminated by Nathan Seiberg and Edward Witten's exact solution to N=2 supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory with SU(2) gauge group, revealing an electric-magnetic duality that exchanges photons with magnetic monopoles and describes confinement and chiral symmetry breaking through a low-energy effective action involving a Riemann surface.90115-5) Extending this to N=2 supersymmetric QCD, they conjectured a strong-weak coupling duality relating the original theory to a dual magnetic description with different gauge group and matter content, providing insights into the infrared behavior and phase structure of these theories.90116-6) These results advanced the understanding of supersymmetry beyond perturbation theory, influencing studies of integrable systems and exact computations in quantum field theory.
The 2000s saw supersymmetry's connections to holography and the multiverse concept deepen. Juan Maldacena's AdS/CFT correspondence, proposed in 1997 and elaborated in subsequent applications, posited a duality between type IIB superstring theory on AdS5S5 and N=4 supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory on its boundary, allowing non-perturbative string dynamics to be probed via weakly coupled supersymmetric gauge theories and vice versa. This framework highlighted supersymmetry's role in preserving conformal invariance and enabled computations of correlation functions and Wilson loops in strongly coupled regimes relevant to quark-gluon plasmas. Simultaneously, the exploration of the string theory landscape revealed a vast array of supersymmetric vacua, estimated at 10^500 or more, arising from flux compactifications on Calabi-Yau manifolds, which accommodate the observed cosmological constant through anthropic selection in an ensemble of metastable states. These developments underscored supersymmetry's potential in addressing fine-tuning problems in cosmology and unifying quantum field theory with gravity.

Applications in Quantum Theories

Supersymmetry in Quantum Field Theory

Supersymmetry in relativistic quantum field theories is realized through transformations that interchange bosons and fermions while preserving the S-matrix elements, ensuring the theory remains invariant under the full supersymmetry algebra. These theories are constructed using superfields, which provide a compact way to encode the multiplet structure and enforce the symmetry at the Lagrangian level. The resulting actions exhibit enhanced ultraviolet properties due to the balancing of bosonic and fermionic degrees of freedom.
The Lagrangian for supersymmetric quantum field theories typically includes kinetic terms for chiral multiplets, expressed in component form as $ |D_\mu \Phi|^2 + \bar{\Phi} \bar{D}^\mu \Phi $, where \Phi represents the scalar field in the multiplet and DD_\muD is the covariant derivative, alongside fermionic kinetic terms and auxiliary fields that complete the off-shell formulation. The interaction sector is governed by the superpotential $ W(\Phi) $, an analytic function of the chiral fields whose integral over half of superspace generates the F-term potential $ V_F = |\partial W / \partial \Phi|^2 $. For vector multiplets, additional D-term contributions $ V_D = \frac{1}{2} D^2 $ arise from gauge interactions, ensuring the full Lagrangian is invariant under supersymmetry transformations. These structures were first explicitly constructed in four dimensions for interacting theories.
Supersymmetry imposes stringent Ward identities that relate correlation functions across the multiplet, leading to equal propagators for superpartners at the same loop order and thus identical mass spectra in the unbroken phase. These identities also underpin the non-renormalization theorems, which protect the superpotential from perturbative corrections beyond tree level, dramatically improving the renormalizability of the theory by restricting higher-order divergences in scalar interactions. Such theorems arise from the holomorphic nature of the superpotential in superspace, ensuring exact results for certain observables even in strongly coupled regimes.
Spontaneous supersymmetry breaking in these theories occurs when the vacuum expectation values of auxiliary fields do not vanish, parameterized by F-terms from the superpotential or D-terms from gauge sectors. In F-term breaking, exemplified by models where the scalar potential has no global minimum at zero, the vacuum acquires a nonzero F-component, generating soft masses for superpartners. D-term breaking, induced by Fayet-Iliopoulos terms in U(1) gauge theories, similarly leads to a nonzero auxiliary D-field vev, often accompanied by Higgs-like mechanisms. In both cases, the breaking is heralded by the emergence of a massless Goldstino, the fermionic Nambu-Goldstone mode that nonlinearly realizes the broken supersymmetry, with its couplings determined by the goldstino equivalence theorem.
A prototypical example of an interacting supersymmetric quantum field theory is the Wess-Zumino model, which features a single chiral superfield with a cubic superpotential $ W(\Phi) = \frac{1}{3} \lambda \Phi^3 $, yielding a scalar potential $ V = |\lambda \phi^2|^2 $ in components, where \phi is the scalar field. This model demonstrates nontrivial dynamics while preserving supersymmetry, with the Ward identities ensuring finite renormalization at all orders and equal two-point functions for the scalar and Majorana fermion propagators. Its simplicity has made it a benchmark for studying perturbative and nonperturbative effects in supersymmetric field theories.

Generalizations and Extensions

Extended Supersymmetry

Extended supersymmetry, or N-extended supersymmetry with N > 1, generalizes the minimal N=1 supersymmetry by introducing multiple sets of supercharges, transforming under an internal SU(N) R-symmetry group that rotates the supercharge index i = 1, ..., N. Unlike the minimal case with a single supercharge Q_ and its conjugate \bar{Q}^{\dot{\alpha}}, the extended algebra features supercharges Q^i_ and \bar{Q}_i^{\dot{\alpha}}, where the index i labels the additional copies, leading to larger supermultiplets that accommodate more fields while preserving the equal number of bosonic and fermionic degrees of freedom. This structure arises naturally in four-dimensional theories and enhances the symmetry, often resulting in improved ultraviolet behavior and richer non-perturbative phenomena.

Supersymmetry in Higher Dimensions

Supersymmetry in higher dimensions extends the framework beyond four spacetime dimensions, allowing for richer structures constrained by the properties of spinors in the Clifford algebra of the Lorentz group SO(1,D-1). The maximal number of supercharges in D dimensions is determined by the dimension of the smallest irreducible spinor representation, which grows as 2^{\lfloor D/2 \rfloor}, but reality conditions like Majorana or Weyl impose limits to avoid higher-spin fields exceeding spin-2 in supergravity multiplets. In particular, the maximum of 32 real supercharges is achieved in D=11, corresponding to a single Majorana spinor of 32 components, beyond which the theory would require spin-3 fields inconsistent with locality.
Prominent examples of supersymmetric theories in higher dimensions include ten-dimensional super Yang-Mills theory, which features 16 supercharges from a Majorana-Weyl spinor and serves as the gauge sector for string theory constructions, and eleven-dimensional supergravity, the maximal supergravity with 32 supercharges that includes a graviton, gravitino, and 3-form field, underpinning M-theory at low energies. These theories are chiral in even dimensions like D=10, where Majorana-Weyl spinors enable anomaly-free formulations by matching bosonic and fermionic contributions to the gravitational and gauge anomalies.
Dimensional reduction via Kaluza-Klein compactification maps higher-dimensional supersymmetry to extended supersymmetry in four dimensions, where the extra dimensions' geometry determines the multiplicity of supercharges and introduces chirality through the decomposition of higher-dimensional spinors into 4D Weyl spinors of definite handedness. For instance, compactifying 11D supergravity on a circle yields N=2 supersymmetry in 10D, while further reductions to 4D produce extended N=8 supersymmetry, preserving the total supercharge count but redistributing it across vector and hypermultiplets. This process highlights how higher-dimensional unification influences 4D phenomenology without altering the underlying Clifford algebra constraints.

Fractional Supersymmetry

Fractional supersymmetry refers to a generalization of supersymmetry in which the symmetry transformation acts on only a subset of the degrees of freedom within the theory, allowing for partial pairings between bosonic and fermionic components rather than complete mappings as in standard cases. For example, in a 1/2 supersymmetric setup, approximately half of the fermionic fields are paired with bosonic fields under the symmetry, while the remaining degrees of freedom transform differently or remain unpaired, often modeled using paragrassmann variables that satisfy higher-order nilpotency conditions such as K=0\theta^K = 0K=0 for integer K>2K > 2K>2. This framework extends beyond traditional integer-extended supersymmetry by incorporating fractional grading structures, enabling the description of systems where full supersymmetric invariance is not required.
The underlying algebras in fractional supersymmetry feature deformed anticommutation relations among the supercharges, such as {Q,Q}P\{Q, Q\} \sim P{Q,Q}P for partial grading, where PPP represents translations, diverging from the standard {Q,Q}=2H\{Q, Q^\dagger\} = 2H{Q,Q}=2H of ordinary supersymmetry. These structures are often formulated within generalized super-Lie algebras or F-fold Lie superalgebras, which accommodate fractional extensions by treating representations as roots of ordinary Lie algebra representations, thus unifying various known examples under a common mathematical basis. Graded algebras serve as an inspiration for these fractional extensions, but the resulting relations break the strict Z_2 grading of conventional supersymmetry.
Applications of fractional supersymmetry appear in condensed matter physics, particularly for modeling partial particle pairings in systems exhibiting anyonic statistics, such as those related to the fractional quantum Hall regime. In mathematical and integrable systems, it facilitates the construction of fractional supersymmetric sine-Gordon models, which are quantum field theories with integrability properties useful for studying non-perturbative effects. These examples demonstrate its role in capturing incomplete symmetries that arise in realistic physical contexts beyond full supersymmetric invariance.
In contrast to standard supersymmetry, fractional versions disrupt the complete Z_2 grading, which can lead to novel vacuum structures with partial degeneracies rather than full boson-fermion degeneracy, and they permit extensions of the Poincar algebra that were previously considered trivial under theorems like Haag-opuszaski-Sohnius, thereby exploring new theoretical possibilities without relying on full symmetry.

Supersymmetry in Particle Physics

Minimal Supersymmetric Standard Model

The Minimal Supersymmetric Standard Model (MSSM) represents the simplest supersymmetric extension of the Standard Model of particle physics, incorporating a minimal set of additional fields to realize supersymmetry while preserving the gauge structure SU(3)_C SU(2)_L U(1)_Y. This framework was first proposed in the context of softly broken supersymmetry within grand unified theories, providing a realistic model that addresses issues such as the hierarchy problem and gauge coupling unification.90522-8) The particle content doubles the Standard Model spectrum by introducing superpartners: for each fermion, a scalar partner (sfermion), and for each gauge boson, a fermionic gaugino partner. Specifically, the matter sector includes three generations of chiral superfields for left-handed quark doublets $ \hat{Q}_i = (\hat{u}_L, \hat{d}_L) $, right-handed up-type antiquarks $ \hat{u}^c_i $, right-handed down-type antiquarks $ \hat{d}^c_i $, left-handed lepton doublets $ \hat{L}_i = (\hat{\nu}_L, \hat{e}_L) $, and right-handed positrons $ \hat{e}^c_i $ (with $ i = 1,2,3 $ for generations), where the scalar components are squarks ($ \tilde{u}, \tilde{d} )andsleptons() and sleptons ()andsleptons( \tilde{\nu}, \tilde{e} $). The Higgs sector features two chiral superfields, $ \hat{H}_u = (\hat{H}_u^+, \hat{H}_u^0) $ and $ \hat{H}_d = (\hat{H}_d^0, \hat{H}_d^-) $, providing scalar partners to the up- and down-type Higgs doublets necessary for anomaly cancellation and Yukawa couplings. The gauge sector includes vector superfields for the Standard Model groups, with fermionic partners known as gauginos: the bino $ \tilde{B} $ for U(1)_Y, the wino $ \tilde{W}^a $ (a Majorana triplet) for SU(2)_L, and the gluino $ \tilde{g} $ (an octet) for SU(3)_C.

This conservation prohibits renormalizable baryon- and lepton-number-violating terms in the superpotential, ensuring proton stability and requiring superpartners to be produced and decay in pairs. Consequently, the lightest supersymmetric particle (LSP) is stable and a prime candidate for cold dark matter, typically the lightest neutralinoa neutral, weakly interacting mixture of bino, wino, and Higgsinos.

Experimental Searches and Constraints

Experimental searches for supersymmetry (SUSY) have primarily focused on the Minimal Supersymmetric Standard Model (MSSM) as a benchmark framework, where superpartners of Standard Model particles are predicted to be produced in high-energy collisions or probed through indirect effects.
At the Large Electron-Positron Collider (LEP), searches for charginosthe fermionic partners of the W bosonexcluded masses below approximately 103.5 GeV at 95% confidence level (CL) in the limit of negligible mass mixing with Higgsinos, based on analyses of events with missing transverse energy and leptons or jets. Similar LEP data constrained slepton masses, excluding smuons and selectrons below 81-95 GeV depending on the scenario, while Higgs sector searches set lower bounds on the lightest supersymmetric Higgs boson mass above 114 GeV, influencing SUSY Higgs phenomenology. These results, from integrated luminosities up to 2.5 fb at center-of-mass energies around 200 GeV, provided early stringent limits on electroweak-scale SUSY particles.
The Tevatron proton-antiproton collider extended these bounds to strongly interacting superpartners, with CDF and D collaborations excluding gluino masses below approximately 350 GeV and squark masses below 380 GeV at 95% CL in simplified models assuming direct decays to quarks and the lightest neutralino, using datasets corresponding to 5-10 fb at s = 1.96 TeV. These limits, derived from multi-jet events with significant missing transverse energy (MET), were particularly sensitive to gluino pair production followed by cascade decays, though they assumed R-parity conservation and decoupled heavier squarks.
The Large Hadron Collider (LHC) has delivered the most powerful constraints through ATLAS and CMS experiments during Runs 1 and 2 (up to 13 TeV and ~140 fb), excluding gluinos up to 2.4 TeV and first- and second-generation squarks up to 2.0 TeV at 95% CL in simplified models where they decay promptly to jets and the lightest neutralino. Third-generation squarks face weaker bounds, with stops excluded below 1.3 TeV in scenarios with light neutralinos, based on searches in final states with b-jets, leptons, and MET. No significant excesses were observed in multi-jet + MET signatures, which are generic for strongly produced SUSY particles, prompting refinements in search strategies for compressed spectra or long-lived particles. As of 2025, Run 3 data (at 13.6 TeV) continue to tighten these limits without discovering signals.
Indirect searches complement collider efforts by probing SUSY contributions to flavor-changing processes and electric dipole moments (EDMs). Measurements of the b s decay rate, consistent with Standard Model predictions to within 10%, constrain squark mixing and chargino-sbottom loops in the MSSM, limiting flavor-violating parameters to below 10 in many scenarios. Similarly, null results from neutron and electron EDM experiments exclude large CP-violating phases in the SUSY-breaking sector, with the current neutron EDM limit of < 1.8 10 ecm implying || 10 radians for gaugino masses around 1 TeV.
Direct dark matter detection experiments, such as XENONnT, set stringent limits on neutralino scattering cross-sections, excluding spin-independent interactions above 10 cm for neutralino masses between 30-100 GeV under assumptions of Higgsino-like or bino-like compositions, based on exposures exceeding 10 tonne-years. These bounds, derived from null observations of nuclear recoils, disfavor low-mass neutralinos as the dominant dark matter component in unconstrained MSSM parameter space.
Non-collider probes from cosmology further restrict SUSY relics, particularly for gravitinos as the lightest supersymmetric particle. Cosmic microwave background (CMB) data from Planck constrain gravitino masses above 20 MeV in thermal production scenarios, as lighter gravitinos would overproduce dark radiation or disrupt Big Bang nucleosynthesis through late decays. These limits arise from spectral distortions and the effective number of neutrino species, N_eff < 3.04 at 95% CL, excluding pure gravitino dark matter in many R-parity violating extensions.

Current Theoretical Status

As of November 2025, following the completion of LHC Run 3 data collection, no evidence for supersymmetric particles has been observed by the ATLAS and CMS collaborations, despite extensive searches analyzing up to approximately 350 fb of proton-proton collision data at 13.6 TeV. However, certain parameter spaces remain viable, particularly those featuring compressed mass spectra where superpartners are nearly degenerate with the lightest supersymmetric particle, leading to low missing transverse energy signatures that challenge detection. For instance, light higgsinos with masses in the 100-200 GeV range continue to evade current bounds due to their weak production cross-sections and soft kinematic features.
Recent 2025 analyses from CMS and ATLAS have further tightened constraints, excluding roughly 90% of the parameter space in the unconstrained Minimal Supersymmetric Standard Model (MSSM) for colored superpartners, with mass limits reaching up to 2.4 TeV for gluinos and 1.3 TeV for third-generation squarks in simplified models. In electroweak sectors, exclusions exceed 800 GeV for higgsino-like and wino-like scenarios, though regions with highly compressed spectra or non-universal gaugino masses persist as allowed. These results underscore the resilience of focused SUSY models while highlighting the exclusion of much of the broader landscape.
In response to these empirical pressures, theoretical developments emphasize natural supersymmetry variants that prioritize low fine-tuning measures, typically below 10 (or < 10 in the Barbieri-Giudice metric), achieved by keeping higgsino masses and third-generation squarks below 1 TeV to address the electroweak hierarchy problem without excessive cancellations. Split supersymmetry models, featuring heavy scalar superpartners (above 10 TeV) but lighter gauginos and higgsinos, reconcile dark matter candidacy with flavor constraints, while mini-split variants push scalars to around 10 GeV, predicting detectable TeV-scale gaugino resonances at future colliders. If supersymmetry remains undiscovered at the High-Luminosity LHC (starting around 2029, with projected sensitivities up to 3-5 TeV for colored particles via 3000 fb), it would undermine its role as a natural hierarchy stabilizer, though SUSY frameworks could still serve as ultraviolet completions for effective theories; alternatives such as composite Higgs models have gained traction for addressing fine-tuning without superpartners.

Applications Beyond Particle Physics

Condensed Matter and Optics

In condensed matter physics, supersymmetry (SUSY) provides a powerful framework for modeling disordered systems, particularly through its application to random matrix theory and Anderson localization. The SUSY method, introduced by Konstantin Efetov in the 1980s, maps the statistical mechanics of disordered metals onto a supersymmetric nonlinear sigma model, enabling exact solutions for correlation functions and transport properties in the metallic phase. This approach reveals universal behaviors in the density of states and level statistics, akin to those in random matrix ensembles, without relying on perturbative expansions. For instance, in quasi-one-dimensional wires, SUSY techniques predict the localization length and conductance fluctuations with high precision, as demonstrated in studies of weakly disordered systems.
SUSY also manifests in partner Hamiltonians for low-dimensional materials like graphene and topological insulators, where the Dirac-like spectrum inspires isospectral constructions. In graphene, these lead to protected zero-energy states. Similarly, in topological insulators, SUSY partner potentials generate robust edge states; for example, on honeycomb and kagome lattices, the Hamiltonians are related via a supercharge operator, yielding flat bands at zero energy that are immune to perturbations within the SUSY algebra. These constructions, rooted in one-dimensional SUSY quantum mechanics, facilitate the design of materials with topologically protected modes.
In optics, SUSY enables the engineering of waveguides and photonic structures with tailored refractive index profiles derived from superpotentials. The seminal 2013 proposal by Miri, Heinrich, El-Ganainy, and Christodoulides introduced SUSY-based optical lattices where partner waveguides share all bound modes except a zero-index-contrast state, allowing unidirectional propagation of a supersymmetric mode free from backscattering. This mode, analogous to a fermionic zero-energy state, propagates robustly through defects or bends in the partner structure, as experimentally verified in integrated silicon waveguides. Applications extend to mode converters and sensors, where SUSY transformations convert higher-order modes into fundamental ones with minimal loss, enhancing light-matter interactions in integrated photonics. Recent experiments as of 2024 have demonstrated perfect excitation of topological states using SUSY waveguides in silicon photonics, showing broad bandwidth operation.
These SUSY implementations in condensed matter and optics yield practical benefits, such as defect-immune waveguiding that improves signal integrity in photonic devices and enhanced localization control in disordered solids, potentially advancing quantum sensing and topological photonics.

Dynamical Systems and Finance

Supersymmetry finds applications in classical and stochastic dynamical systems, where it facilitates the analysis of integrable hierarchies through the construction of Lax pairs. In the case of the N=2 and N=3 supersymmetric Korteweg-de Vries (KdV) equations, Lax operators are formulated to generate infinite hierarchies of integrable equations, enabling the identification of conserved quantities and soliton solutions. These extensions preserve the integrability of the original KdV hierarchy while incorporating fermionic degrees of freedom via superfields.
In chaotic dynamical systems, supersymmetry provides tools for capturing fluctuations around periodic orbits, offering insights into deterministic chaos without relying on statistical assumptions. This approach has been used to analyze the stability of orbits in nonlinear systems.
Graded algebras extend supersymmetry to stochastic dynamical systems, enabling the treatment of noise through fermionic partners. In financial modeling, supersymmetry from quantum mechanics has been adapted since the 1990s to enhance option pricing frameworks, building on Bender-Dunne polynomials for generating isospectral potentials. These methods yield partner Hamiltonians that share spectra with the original Black-Scholes Hamiltonian, except possibly for the ground state, allowing exact solutions for barrier options and extensions to stochastic volatility.
Hedging strategies benefit from superpartners, where the wavefunctions of partner problems provide delta-hedging portfolios that replicate option payoffs under correlated risks. Path integrals incorporating Grassmann variables formalize risk-neutral measures in supersymmetric models, integrating over bosonic stock paths and fermionic volatility fluctuations to compute expectations efficiently. This yields exact pricing for multi-asset options with intertwined risks, outperforming numerical methods in correlated environments.
The primary advantage lies in deriving exact solutions for systems with correlated risks, reducing computational complexity while maintaining analytical tractability.

Role in Unified Theories

Supersymmetry in String Theory

Supersymmetry is integral to the formulation of consistent string theories in ten dimensions, where it ensures anomaly cancellation on the worldsheet and induces spacetime supersymmetry, facilitating the unification of gravity with other forces through dualities and compactifications. In superstring theories, worldsheet supersymmetry balances the central charges of bosonic and fermionic sectors, preventing conformal anomalies that would render the theory inconsistent at quantum levels. This structure arises in both Type IIB and heterotic string theories, where the Gliozzi-Scherk-Olive (GSO) projection selects physical states that realize spacetime supersymmetry from the underlying Ramond-Neveu-Schwarz formalism.
Type IIB superstring theory features closed, oriented strings with (1,1) worldsheet supersymmetry, meaning equal left- and right-moving supersymmetries, leading to an anomaly-free spectrum after the GSO projection imposes chirality on the right-movers while keeping left-movers opposite. This results in N=2 spacetime supersymmetry in ten dimensions, with the theory being self-dual under SL(2,Z) transformations that mix the dilaton and the complexified axion, thus stabilizing perturbative expansions against ultraviolet divergences. Heterotic string theory, introduced as a hybrid of bosonic and superstrings, employs (1,0) worldsheet supersymmetrysupersymmetry only on the right-moving sectorpaired with a bosonic left-moving sector gauged under SO(32) or E8E8, yielding N=1 spacetime supersymmetry; the GSO projection here ensures tachyon-free spectra and anomaly cancellation via the Green-Schwarz mechanism, which relies on supersymmetric couplings to resolve potential ultraviolet inconsistencies in gauge interactions.
The massless spectrum of these superstring theories in ten dimensions consists of the graviton (metric tensor), the gravitino (supersymmetric partner of the graviton), and the dilaton (scalar modulating string coupling), forming a supermultiplet that matches the content of ten-dimensional supergravity and provides a unified description of gravity, Yang-Mills gauge fields, and fermions. To connect to four-dimensional physics, superstrings are compactified on Calabi-Yau manifolds, which preserve some supersymmetry due to their Ricci-flat Khler structure, generating a chiral N=1 supersymmetric spectrum in four dimensions with three generations of matter fields when appropriate topological conditions, such as the Euler characteristic and Hodge numbers, are satisfied. Supersymmetry, as a potential low-energy manifestation in these string theory models, remains undetected in experiments such as LHC searches up to 2026, which have excluded certain parameter spaces but pushed viable scales higher, emphasizing the ongoing and challenging nature of such tests.
Dualities further highlight supersymmetry's unifying role, as seen in the S-duality of Type IIB strings, which extends the Montonen-Olive duality of N=4 super Yang-Mills theory to the full string level, mapping weak-coupling regimes (g_s << 1) to strong-coupling ones (g_s >> 1) and resolving ultraviolet divergences by relating perturbative and non-perturbative sectors through SL(2,Z) invariance. In heterotic strings, T-duality and heterotic-type IIA duality, both protected by supersymmetry, equate theories on circles of inverse radii, demonstrating that seemingly distinct formulations describe the same underlying physics. These dualities collectively point to an eleven-dimensional origin in M-theory, where ten-dimensional supersymmetric strings emerge as limits.
Moduli stabilization in superstring compactifications addresses the issue of flat directions in the potential for size and shape moduli of extra dimensions, which would otherwise destabilize the vacuum; flux compactifications, involving Ramond-Ramond and Neveu-Schwarz fluxes threaded through Calabi-Yau cycles, generate a potential that fixes these moduli while breaking supersymmetry softly through non-perturbative effects like gaugino condensation or D-brane instantons. These constructions are proposed to lead to metastable de Sitter vacua, though their stability and consistency with cosmological observations remain subjects of ongoing debate, particularly in light of the swampland conjectures that constrain possible effective field theories in string theory.

Implications for Quantum Gravity

Supergravity theories arise from promoting global supersymmetry to a local symmetry, which naturally incorporates diffeomorphism invariance as a gauge principle, thereby coupling the supersymmetric matter fields to gravity in a consistent manner. In this framework, the gravitino serves as the superpartner of the graviton, and the resulting supergravity Lagrangians describe the dynamics of spacetime geometry intertwined with fermionic and bosonic fields under local supersymmetry transformations. A particularly significant example is eleven-dimensional supergravity, which emerges as the low-energy effective theory of M-theory, unifying various string theory limits while providing a non-perturbative description of quantum gravity in higher dimensions.
Supersymmetry addresses key ultraviolet (UV) challenges in quantum gravity by canceling quadratic divergences that arise in loop corrections involving gravitons and their superpartners. These cancellations occur because bosonic and fermionic contributions to the graviton self-energy and other diagrams enter with opposite signs, mitigating the hierarchy problem between the Planck scale and lower-energy physics, and suggesting a pathway toward a UV-complete theory of gravity. In supergravity, this mechanism enhances renormalizability in lower dimensions and provides a framework for exploring finite theories, such as N=8 supergravity in four dimensions, where supersymmetry protects against severe divergences up to high loop orders.
One of the most striking applications of supersymmetry in quantum gravity is the microscopic explanation of black hole entropy through the counting of BPS states. In the seminal work by Strominger and Vafa, the Bekenstein-Hawking entropy of a five-dimensional extremal black hole was reproduced by enumerating the degeneracy of supersymmetric BPS string states in type II string theory compactified on a five-dimensional Calabi-Yau manifold, matching the macroscopic area law exactly. This calculation demonstrates how supersymmetry preserves a BPS bound that protects the microstate count from quantum corrections, offering a non-perturbative test of quantum gravity and resolving long-standing puzzles about the statistical origin of black hole thermodynamics.
Supersymmetry plays a central role in holographic dualities, particularly the AdS/CFT correspondence, where supersymmetric conformal field theories on the boundary provide a non-perturbative definition of quantum gravity in anti-de Sitter (AdS) spacetime via type IIB supergravity on AdS_5 S^5. This duality implies that gravitational dynamics, including black hole formation and Hawking radiation, emerge from strongly coupled quantum field theory observables, with supersymmetry ensuring the stability and exact solvability of the dual descriptions. Ongoing efforts as of 2025 explore metastable de Sitter solutions in supergravity that could admit holographic duals with broken supersymmetry, potentially addressing the cosmological constant problem and vacuum stability in quantum gravity.
