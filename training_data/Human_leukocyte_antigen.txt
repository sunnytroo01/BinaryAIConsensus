Human leukocyte antigen
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Human leukocyte antigen (HLA) is a group of highly polymorphic genes within the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) located on the short arm of chromosome 6 (6p21.3), encoding cell-surface glycoproteins that play a central role in the adaptive immune response by presenting peptide antigens to T lymphocytes. These proteins, first identified through alloantibodies in leukocytes, enable the immune system to distinguish self from non-self entities, facilitating immune recognition and response to pathogens.
The HLA system is divided into three main classes: Class I (HLA-A, HLA-B, HLA-C), which are expressed on nearly all nucleated cells and present endogenous antigens to cytotoxic CD8+ T cells; Class II (HLA-DR, HLA-DQ, HLA-DP), primarily expressed on antigen-presenting cells like dendritic cells, macrophages, and B cells, presenting exogenous antigens to helper CD4+ T cells; and Class III, which includes genes for complement components and other immune regulators but does not encode antigen-presenting molecules. The extraordinary genetic diversity of HLA, with thousands of alleles arising from evolutionary pressures, ensures broad population-level protection against infectious diseases while contributing to individual susceptibility variations.
Functionally, HLA molecules bind and display short peptide fragments derived from intracellular or extracellular proteins within specialized grooves, triggering T-cell activation for targeted immune elimination of infected or abnormal cells. This antigen presentation is crucial for immune regulation, T-cell repertoire selection in the thymus, and responses to viruses, bacteria, and tumors. In clinical contexts, HLA typing is essential for matching donors and recipients in organ and bone marrow transplantation to minimize rejection risks, as mismatches can provoke severe graft-versus-host disease.
Beyond transplantation, HLA polymorphisms are strongly associated with autoimmune diseases, such as rheumatoid arthritis (HLA-DR4) and type 1 diabetes (HLA-DQ alleles), as well as infectious disease outcomes, including HIV progression and COVID-19 severity, highlighting their influence on host-pathogen interactions. Advances in HLA genotyping technologies, from serological methods to next-generation sequencing, have improved precision in clinical testing and personalized medicine applications.

Introduction

Definition and Biological Significance

The human leukocyte antigen (HLA) system represents the human counterpart to the major histocompatibility complex (MHC), a cluster of genes that encode highly polymorphic cell-surface proteins crucial for immune system recognition and response. Class I HLA proteins are expressed on nearly all nucleated cells, while Class II HLA proteins are primarily expressed on antigen-presenting cells, playing a pivotal role in distinguishing self from non-self entities by binding and displaying peptide fragments derived from intracellular or extracellular sources. The HLA region is the most polymorphic genetic locus in the human genome, with over 42,000 distinct alleles documented as of 2025, enabling diverse immune responses tailored to environmental pathogens.
HLA molecules function primarily by presenting peptide antigens on the cell surface to T lymphocytes, thereby initiating and regulating adaptive immune responses. Class I HLA molecules display peptides from endogenous sources to cytotoxic CD8+ T cells, while class II molecules present exogenous peptides to helper CD4+ T cells, facilitating targeted immune activation against infected or aberrant cells. This antigen presentation process is fundamental to immune surveillance and response coordination.
Biologically, HLA's significance extends to maintaining immune homeostasis through self/non-self discrimination, which prevents autoimmunity while mounting defenses against foreign invaders. It contributes to immune tolerance by modulating T-cell selection in the thymus and periphery, ensuring non-responsiveness to self-antigens. Furthermore, HLA polymorphisms underlie variable disease susceptibility, influencing outcomes in infections, autoimmunity, and transplantation by shaping antigen recognition efficiency.

Discovery and Historical Context

The discovery of human leukocyte antigens (HLA) began in the mid-20th century amid investigations into transfusion reactions and tissue compatibility. In 1958, French immunologist Jean Dausset identified the first leukocyte antigen, initially termed MAC (now recognized as HLA-A2), by detecting antibodies in the serum of patients who had experienced severe reactions to blood transfusions. This breakthrough highlighted the role of leukocyte surface proteins in immune responses to foreign tissues, laying the groundwork for understanding histocompatibility. Dausset's work, building on earlier observations of leukocyte agglutinins in the 1950s, demonstrated that these antigens were inherited and polymorphic, influencing transfusion outcomes.
Parallel research in animal models accelerated the development of the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) concept. In the 1940s, American geneticist George Snell discovered the H-2 histocompatibility system in mice through studies of tumor transplantation and skin graft rejection, identifying genes that controlled immune recognition of self versus non-self. Snell's findings provided a foundational model for human studies, revealing structural and functional analogies between the mouse H-2 and human HLA systems. For their contributions to MHC discoveryDausset in humans and Snell in mice, alongside Baruj Benacerraf's work on immune response genes the trio shared the 1980 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine, underscoring the MHC's central role in immunity.
Key milestones in the 1960s and 1970s advanced HLA characterization and clinical application. Serological typing techniques emerged in the early 1960s, allowing systematic identification of HLA specificities and enabling the first comprehensive HLA matching for organ transplants; by 1968, studies showed that HLA-matched renal grafts had significantly higher survival rates. In the 1970s, genetic linkages between HLA variants and diseases were established, most notably the strong association between HLA-B27 and ankylosing spondylitis reported in 1973 by independent groups in the United States and United Kingdom. These discoveries emphasized HLA's influence on disease susceptibility and solidified its importance in transplantation success.
The 1980s and 1990s marked a transition from serological to molecular approaches in HLA research. Restriction fragment length polymorphism (RFLP) and polymerase chain reaction (PCR)-based methods enabled DNA-level typing, while the first full sequencing of HLA genes occurred during this period, culminating in the complete 3.6 Mb HLA genomic sequence annotation in 1999. This genomic era was supported by the establishment of the IMGT/HLA database in 1998, a curated repository of official HLA allele sequences that has since incorporated next-generation sequencing (NGS) technologies for higher resolution; as of 2025, it catalogs over 42,000 alleles, reflecting ongoing advances in NGS-driven discovery.

Molecular Structure and Genetics

HLA Genes and Chromosomal Location

The human leukocyte antigen (HLA) complex, also known as the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) in humans, is located on the short arm of chromosome 6 at the cytogenetic band 6p21.3. This genomic region spans approximately 4 megabases (Mb) and contains over 200 genes, making it one of the most gene-dense segments in the human genome with an average of about 43 genes per Mb. The high density of genes in this area contributes to its critical role in immune function, with expression regulated by various promoters and enhancers that influence transcriptional activity across the locus.
The HLA region is organized into three main classes: class I, class II, and class III, with the classical and non-classical HLA genes primarily clustered in the class I and II regions. Classical class I genes include HLA-A, HLA-B, and HLA-C, which are located toward the telomeric end of the region, while classical class II genes such as HLA-DR, HLA-DQ, and HLA-DP are positioned more centromerically. Non-classical class I genes, exemplified by HLA-E, HLA-F, and HLA-G, and non-classical class II genes like HLA-DM and HLA-DO, provide additional diversity and specialized functions within the complex. These loci form a multigene family characterized by extensive sequence homology and the presence of numerous pseudogenes, which arise from gene duplications and account for a significant portion of the region's genetic content.
High levels of linkage disequilibrium (LD) pervade the HLA region, resulting in the inheritance of extended haplotypes that span multiple loci with limited recombination, which complicates fine-mapping of disease associations. Regulatory elements, including promoters, enhancers, and silencers, are interspersed among the coding and non-coding sequences, modulating gene expression in a tissue-specific manner and contributing to the region's overall complexity. Pseudogenes, such as those derived from HLA-A (e.g., HLA-H and HLA-J), further highlight the evolutionary dynamics of duplication and inactivation within this multigene family.
Recent advancements, including data from the 18th International HLA and Immunogenetics Workshop (main meeting 2022, with projects reported through 2024) and related initiatives in 2025, have provided full haplotype-resolved reference assemblies of the MHC region, revealing previously undetected structural variants such as insertions, deletions, and copy number variations that influence allelic diversity and expression. These efforts by the HLA Haplotype Reference Consortium and related initiatives underscore the structural complexity of the locus, enabling more precise genotyping and understanding of haplotype architecture as of 2025.

Class I and Class II HLA Molecules

Human leukocyte antigen (HLA) molecules are divided into class I and class II, each with distinct structural features that enable their roles in immune recognition. These proteins are encoded by genes clustered in the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) on chromosome 6.
HLA class I molecules form a heterodimer consisting of a polymorphic heavy chain ( chain, approximately 45 kDa) non-covalently associated with the invariant light chain -microglobulin (m, approximately 12 kDa). The extracellular portion of the chain folds into three domains: and , which together create a peptide-binding groove formed by two -helices overlying a -sheet platform, and , which interacts with T cell receptors. This groove accommodates peptides typically 8-10 amino acids in length, bound via anchor residues that fit into specific pockets. HLA class I molecules are expressed on the surface of nearly all nucleated cells, providing a broad surveillance mechanism.
In contrast, HLA class II molecules are also heterodimers but composed of two transmembrane glycoprotein chains: an chain (approximately 33-35 kDa) and a chain (approximately 28-30 kDa), both polymorphic. Each chain has two extracellular domains (, , , ), with the and domains forming an open-ended peptide-binding groove capable of binding longer peptides, usually 13-25 amino acids. The and domains are membrane-proximal and contribute to stability. Expression of HLA class II is primarily restricted to professional antigen-presenting cells such as dendritic cells, macrophages, and B cells, though it can be induced on other cell types under inflammatory conditions.
The assembly of both classes occurs in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), where peptide loading is essential for stability and surface trafficking. For HLA class I, the nascent chain associates with calnexin and then m, forming a complex that recruits the peptide-loading machinery, including the transporter associated with antigen processing (TAP), which delivers cytosolic peptides into the ER. High-affinity peptide binding stabilizes the complex, allowing exit from the ER via the Golgi apparatus; empty or low-affinity complexes are often retained or degraded.
HLA class II assembly involves the invariant chain (Ii, or CD74), which trimerizes and binds to three dimers in the ER, occupying the peptide-binding groove with its CLIP region to prevent premature peptide loading and aid trafficking to endosomal compartments. In these acidic vesicles, Ii is proteolytically degraded, and CLIP is exchanged for antigenic peptides, often facilitated by HLA-DM, before the stable complex reaches the cell surface.
A key structural distinction is their interaction with T cells: HLA class I presents to CD8 cytotoxic T cells via recognition involving the domain and CD8, while HLA class II engages CD4 helper T cells through the domain and CD4. The three-dimensional structure of HLA class I was first elucidated in 1987 for HLA-A2, revealing the peptide-binding platform, while class II structures, solved in the early 1990s for HLA-DR1, highlighted the open groove and dimerization potential, advancing studies of allelic polymorphism.

Functions in Immunity

Antigen Presentation Mechanisms

Antigen presentation by human leukocyte antigen (HLA) molecules involves the processing of protein antigens into peptides that bind to HLA in peptide-major histocompatibility complex (pMHC) complexes, which are then displayed on the cell surface for recognition by T cell receptors (TCRs) on T lymphocytes. This process initiates adaptive immune responses by activating CD4+ helper T cells via HLA class II or CD8+ cytotoxic T cells via HLA class I. TCR engagement with pHLA triggers intracellular signaling cascades in T cells, leading to proliferation, differentiation, and effector functions, provided co-stimulatory signals are present, such as those mediated by CD28-B7 interactions. Adhesion molecules like LFA-1 on T cells binding to ICAM-1 on antigen-presenting cells further stabilize the immunological synapse. The CD4 co-receptor binds to non-polymorphic regions of HLA class II, enhancing signaling, while CD8 interacts similarly with HLA class I.
The HLA class I antigen presentation pathway primarily handles endogenous antigens derived from cytosolic proteins, such as those synthesized within the cell or produced during viral infections. These proteins are tagged with ubiquitin and degraded by the 26S proteasome into oligopeptides of 8-11 amino acids. The resulting peptides are translocated from the cytosol into the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) lumen by the ATP-dependent transporter associated with antigen processing (TAP), a heterodimer of TAP1 and TAP2 subunits. In the ER, HLA class I molecules, consisting of a heavy chain, 2-microglobulin, and the peptide-binding groove, assemble with chaperones like calnexin and calreticulin. The peptide loading complex (PLC), which includes tapasin, ERp57, and TAP, facilitates the exchange of low-affinity peptides for high-affinity ones that stabilize the complex. Quality control ensures only stable pHLA class I molecules traffic through the Golgi to the plasma membrane, where they survey for CD8+ T cell recognition. This pathway allows nearly all nucleated cells to signal intracellular threats to the immune system.
In contrast, the HLA class II pathway processes exogenous antigens, such as those from extracellular pathogens or apoptotic cells, which are internalized by endocytosis or phagocytosis into acidified endosomal compartments. Here, antigens are degraded by lysosomal proteases like cathepsins into peptides of 13-25 amino acids. Newly synthesized HLA class II heterodimers in the ER associate with the invariant chain (Ii, or CD74), which occupies the peptide-binding groove via its CLIP (class II-associated invariant chain peptide) region to prevent premature peptide loading and directs the complex to late endosomes or MIICs (MHC class II compartments) via dileucine motifs. In these compartments, Ii is proteolytically cleaved, leaving CLIP bound; HLA-DM, a non-classical MHC molecule, catalyzes CLIP removal and facilitates loading of antigenic peptides that fit the open-ended groove of HLA class II. HLA-DO can modulate this process in certain cells like B lymphocytes. Stable pHLA class II complexes are then transported to the cell surface, primarily on professional antigen-presenting cells like dendritic cells, macrophages, and B cells, for presentation to CD4+ T cells. This pathway ensures coordination of humoral and cellular immunity against extracellular threats.
A key aspect of HLA-mediated presentation in transplantation is alloreactivity, where recipient T cells respond to foreign donor HLA. In direct allorecognition, recipient T cells directly bind intact donor pHLA complexes on donor antigen-presenting cells or graft tissues, mimicking self-antigen presentation but with allogeneic specificity; this pathway dominates early acute rejection due to high precursor frequency of alloreactive T cells. Indirect allorecognition involves recipient antigen-presenting cells internalizing donor HLA or tissue proteins, processing them into peptides, and presenting these via the endogenous class II pathway on recipient HLA to CD4+ T cells, contributing to chronic rejection and helper functions. These dual pathways amplify graft rejection by engaging both cytotoxic and helper arms of the immune response.

Role in Infectious Disease Susceptibility

The human leukocyte antigen (HLA) system plays a critical role in modulating susceptibility to infectious diseases through its influence on antigen presentation, where specific alleles determine the efficiency with which pathogen-derived peptides are bound and presented to T cells. Certain HLA class I alleles, such as HLA-B*57, restrict HIV-1 progression by effectively presenting conserved viral epitopes like those from the Gag protein, leading to robust cytotoxic T lymphocyte responses that control viral replication. This mechanism exemplifies how allelic variation can confer protection by targeting immunodominant regions less prone to escape mutations. Similarly, HLA class II alleles facilitate CD4+ T cell activation against extracellular pathogens, influencing outcomes in bacterial and parasitic infections.
Key examples illustrate these associations across pathogens. HLA-B27 is linked to slower HIV-1 disease progression, as it elicits strong, focused CD8+ T cell responses against the virus, particularly in early infection stages, reducing viral load set points. In malaria, the HLA-B53:01 allele (formerly Bw53) provides protection against severe Plasmodium falciparum infection in West African populations by presenting liver-stage antigens, thereby limiting parasite burden and clinical severity. Conversely, HLA-DRB1*04 is associated with poorer interferon-based treatment responses in patients with chronic hepatitis C virus (HCV) infection, particularly in males, due to suboptimal CD4+ T cell priming.
Population-based studies highlight the broader impact of HLA diversity, including heterozygote advantage, where individuals carrying dissimilar alleles at HLA loci exhibit enhanced pathogen coverage and reduced infection risk compared to homozygotes. For instance, HLA class I heterozygosity confers resistance to HIV-1 acquisition and slower progression in cohort studies, as diverse peptide-binding repertoires broaden immune surveillance against evolving viral variants. Recent genome-wide association studies (GWAS) in 2025 have confirmed HLA associations with COVID-19 outcomes, identifying alleles like those in the HLA-B locus that influence SARS-CoV-2 infection risk and severity in diverse cohorts, independent of exposure levels. Evolutionarily, this polymorphism is maintained by balancing selection driven by pathogens, where selective pressure from historical epidemics favors diverse HLA profiles to ensure population-level resilience against varied threats.

Involvement in Autoimmune Disorders

Human leukocyte antigen (HLA) variants play a central role in autoimmune disorders by disrupting immune self-tolerance, primarily through impaired negative selection of autoreactive T cells in the thymus. Certain HLA alleles fail to efficiently present self-peptides during thymic development, allowing T cells with potential cross-reactivity to self-antigens to escape deletion and enter the periphery, thereby increasing autoimmunity risk. This central tolerance defect is exacerbated by peripheral mechanisms, such as molecular mimicry, where pathogen-derived peptides structurally resemble self-antigens, leading to cross-reactive T cell responses that target host tissues. Additionally, specific HLA molecules can alter the peptide repertoire presented to T cells, promoting cross-reactivity between foreign and self-peptides and initiating autoimmune inflammation.
Well-established associations link particular HLA alleles to increased susceptibility for several autoimmune conditions. For instance, HLA-DR4 is strongly associated with rheumatoid arthritis (RA), present in approximately 70% of patients in Caucasian populations and conferring a relative risk of about 5 compared to non-carriers. In ankylosing spondylitis (AS), HLA-B27 exhibits one of the strongest genetic links, with an odds ratio exceeding 100 for disease development in carriers. Similarly, nearly all individuals with celiac disease express HLA-DQ2 or HLA-DQ8, accounting for over 90% of cases, as these class II molecules preferentially present gluten-derived peptides that trigger CD4+ T cell responses against intestinal tissue.
Quantitative genetic analyses underscore the substantial contribution of HLA to autoimmune risk. In type 1 diabetes, HLA variants account for at least 50% of the genetic risk, with attributable risk fractions highlighting their dominant role in beta-cell autoimmunity. These associations often involve compound haplotypes, such as DR3-DQ2 or DR4-DQ8, which amplify susceptibility through synergistic effects on antigen presentation.
Recent research as of 2024 has revealed epigenetic modifiers at HLA loci that influence gene expression and exacerbate lupus susceptibility. In systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), DNA methylation and histone modifications within the HLA region contribute to heterogeneous disease phenotypes by altering transcriptional regulation of risk alleles like HLA-DR3, integrating genetic predisposition with environmental triggers.

Contributions to Cancer Surveillance

Human leukocyte antigen (HLA) molecules, particularly class I variants, play a pivotal role in cancer surveillance by presenting tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) and neoantigensmutated peptides derived from tumor-specific alterationsto cytotoxic CD8+ T cells, enabling their recognition and destruction of malignant cells. This process relies on the proteasomal degradation of intracellular proteins into peptides, which are then loaded onto HLA class I molecules in the endoplasmic reticulum and transported to the cell surface for T cell receptor (TCR) engagement, triggering perforin- and granzyme-mediated apoptosis in targeted tumor cells. In tumors with high mutational burdens, such as melanoma or lung cancer, neoantigen presentation by diverse HLA alleles enhances the breadth of the anti-tumor T cell response, fostering immune vigilance against emerging oncogenic transformations.
A prominent example of HLA-mediated tumor recognition involves the HLA-A*02:01 allele, which frequently presents epitopes from melanoma-associated antigens (MAGEs), such as MAGE-A3 and MAGE-A4, to elicit cytotoxic T cell responses in melanoma patients. For instance, the MAGE-A4_{230-239} peptide bound to HLA-A*02:01 has been targeted in T cell therapies, demonstrating specific lysis of MAGE-A4-expressing melanoma cells while sparing healthy tissues. However, tumors can evade this surveillance through loss of heterozygosity (LOH) at HLA loci, where selective deletion or mutation of one HLA allele reduces the repertoire of presentable antigens, thereby diminishing T cell recognition and promoting immune escape; this mechanism occurs in approximately 40% of non-small cell lung cancers and correlates with subclonal neoantigen enrichment. Such HLA LOH events, often driven by chromosomal instability, allow tumors to retain viability under immune pressure, as evidenced in pan-cancer analyses where it refines predictions of checkpoint inhibitor efficacy.
Therapeutically, HLA typing has emerged as a critical tool for optimizing cancer immunotherapies, including immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), where specific alleles like HLA-A03 predict enhanced responses by facilitating robust neoantigen presentation and T cell infiltration. Patients with high HLA class I heterozygosity show improved outcomes with ICIs in diverse malignancies, as this diversity broadens antigen coverage and counters escape variants. In chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cell design, HLA restrictions are considered for affinity-enhanced TCR-based constructs, such as those targeting MAGE-A4/HLA-A02:01 complexes, to ensure specificity and minimize off-tumor toxicity in solid tumors. Recent advances, including single-cell RNA sequencing studies from 2024-2025, have revealed widespread HLA class I downregulation in tumor cellsoften reversible via epigenetic mechanismscontributing to immunologically "cold" phenotypes in cancers like adenoid cystic carcinoma and limiting ICI responsiveness. Notably, high HLA class I expression on breast cancer cells correlates with superior disease-free survival, as meta-analyses of over 5,000 patients demonstrate reduced recurrence risk through enhanced cytotoxic T cell engagement.

Impact on Transplant Rejection

Human leukocyte antigen (HLA) mismatches between donor and recipient are a primary driver of transplant rejection, primarily through T cell-mediated allorecognition pathways. In direct allorecognition, recipient T cells directly recognize intact donor HLA molecules on graft cells as foreign, leading to rapid activation of cytotoxic and helper T cells that infiltrate and damage the allograft. This pathway is particularly prominent in acute rejection episodes, where donor HLA class I and II molecules present peptides to recipient T cells without processing. Indirect allorecognition occurs when recipient antigen-presenting cells process and present donor HLA-derived peptides on self-HLA molecules, eliciting a helper T cell response that promotes antibody production and chronic inflammation; this mechanism contributes to both acute and chronic rejection by amplifying adaptive immunity over time.
Transplant rejection manifests in distinct types, each influenced by HLA compatibility. Hyperacute rejection develops within minutes to hours post-transplant due to pre-formed recipient antibodies against donor HLA or ABO antigens, triggering complement activation, endothelial damage, and thrombosis that render the graft non-viable. Acute rejection, occurring days to months after transplantation, is largely T cell-driven and linked to HLA mismatches, with recipient lymphocytes attacking the graft via direct or indirect pathways, often responsive to immunosuppressive therapy. Chronic rejection, emerging months to years later, involves progressive fibrosis and vascular changes, where indirect allorecognition of mismatched HLA peptides sustains low-grade inflammation and antibody-mediated injury.
The degree of HLA matching significantly influences rejection risk and graft survival. In bone marrow transplantation, an 8/8 allele-level match at HLA-A, -B, -C, and -DRB1 loci between unrelated donors and recipients markedly reduces acute graft-versus-host disease and improves overall survival compared to mismatched donors. For kidney transplants, a 6/6 match at HLA-A, -B, and -DR loci is associated with lower rates of acute rejection and better long-term graft function, though high-resolution typing beyond these loci can further optimize outcomes. HLA mismatches substantially elevate graft failure risk; for instance, each additional mismatch can increase the odds of rejection by up to 1.7-fold, while broader incompatibility correlates with 2-fold higher risks in certain cohorts.
Desensitization protocols mitigate rejection in sensitized patients with pre-existing anti-HLA antibodies by reducing antibody titers through plasmapheresis, intravenous immunoglobulin, and rituximab, enabling successful transplantation while targeting HLA-specific responses. Recent advancements in AI-based HLA matching, particularly for unrelated donors, enhance prediction of compatibility and donor availability, leading to improved engraftment rates and reduced rejection in hematopoietic stem cell transplants as of 2024-2025.

Genetic Variability

Allelic Diversity and Polymorphism

The human leukocyte antigen (HLA) system exhibits extraordinary polymorphism, with 42,996 alleles officially recognized across all loci in the latest release of the IPD-IMGT/HLA Database (version 3.62, October 2025). This diversity is particularly pronounced in class I genes, where HLA-B stands out with 10,680 alleles, surpassing other loci such as HLA-A (8,949 alleles) and HLA-C (8,944 alleles). Such extensive allelic variation arises primarily from single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in the coding regions, including both synonymous and nonsynonymous substitutions concentrated in the peptide-binding regions (PBRs) of the 1 and 2 domains for class I molecules, and the 1 and 1 domains for class II. Nonsynonymous mutations predominate in these PBRs, with ratios of nonsynonymous to synonymous substitutions (dN/dS) often exceeding 1, indicating positive selection that enhances peptide-binding specificity. Additionally, sequence feature variant types (SFVTs) provide a framework for classifying these polymorphisms based on their impact on structural features like antigen-binding grooves, revealing patterns of functional divergence beyond simple sequence counts.
Evolutionary forces have shaped this polymorphism through mechanisms such as pathogen-driven balancing selection, which favors heterozygote advantage in presenting diverse microbial peptides, and interlocus gene conversion events that transfer sequence segments between paralogous HLA genes. Recombination hotspots within the HLA region further contribute, generating novel alleles by shuffling segments at rates up to 100 times the genomic average, thereby accelerating diversity in response to selective pressures. These processes maintain high variability, as evidenced by the asymmetric nature of selection where certain allele combinations confer greater fitness against pathogens.
Allele frequencies vary markedly across human ancestries, reflecting historical migrations and local adaptations; for instance, HLA-A*36 alleles are notably enriched in African populations, reaching frequencies of up to 5-10% in some sub-Saharan groups compared to near absence in Europeans or Asians. This ancestry-specific distribution underscores the role of genetic drift and selection in structuring HLA variation. Additionally, HLA allelic diversity displays a clear geographic gradient, with the highest levels observed in sub-Saharan African populations and progressively decreasing with increasing geographic distance from Africa. This pattern is consistent with serial founder effects during human dispersal out of Africa, as well as pathogen-driven balancing selection maintaining greater diversity in regions with higher pathogen richness. Indigenous populations of the Americas exhibit particularly low HLA diversity, largely attributable to historical population bottlenecks during the peopling of the New World.
A key metric of this diversity is the nucleotide diversity () in HLA coding regions, approximately 0.05, which is over 50 times the human genome-wide average of about 0.001.

Nomenclature and Classification Systems

The standardized nomenclature for human leukocyte antigen (HLA) alleles is overseen by the World Health Organization (WHO) Nomenclature Committee for Factors of the HLA System, in collaboration with the International Union of Immunological Societies (IUIS). This system assigns unique identifiers to HLA alleles based on their genomic sequences, ensuring consistency in scientific and clinical communication. The basic format for an allele name begins with the locus (e.g., HLA-A, HLA-B, or HLA-DRB1), followed by an asterisk, a two-digit allele group indicating broad serologic specificity (e.g., 02 for HLA-A02), a colon, and a two-digit protein designation specifying the amino acid sequence of the antigen-binding region (e.g., :01). Subsequent fields, separated by colons, denote synonymous nucleotide changes in non-coding regions (e.g., :01:01), further synonymous variations (e.g., :01:01:02), and optional suffixes for special cases such as expression levels (L for low expression, N for null/no protein, Q for questionable expression, or A for aberrant expression) or structural features.
The nomenclature has evolved significantly since its inception in the 1970s, initially relying on serologic typing that identified broad antigen specificities (serotypes) such as HLA-A1 or HLA-B8 through antibody-based reactions. By the 1980s, cellular typing methods refined these, but the shift to DNA-based sequencing in the early 1990s enabled precise allele-level resolution, leading to the current sequence-derived naming conventions. The WHO committee, established in 1968, meets periodically to review submissions and update the system, incorporating advances like next-generation sequencing to accommodate increasing allelic diversity.
HLA classification distinguishes between serotype-level groupings, which aggregate alleles sharing similar antibody reactivity (e.g., multiple alleles under HLA-A2 serotype), and high-resolution allele-level designations that differentiate based on full or partial DNA sequences. For clinical applications like transplantation matching, simplified groupings such as G-groups and P-groups are used: G-groups cluster alleles encoding identical protein sequences (e.g., HLA-A02:01:01:01G and HLA-A02:01:01:02G share the same protein despite silent mutations), while P-groups focus on identical peptide-binding domains for immunogenicity assessment. These groupings facilitate donor-recipient compatibility evaluations by reducing ambiguity in ambiguous typings.
The IPD-IMGT/HLA Database serves as the official repository for WHO-recognized HLA sequences, tracking nomenclature changes and providing tools for allele querying and alignment. As of IPD-IMGT/HLA release 3.62 (October 2025), updates to the nomenclature include notations for structural variants, such as splice-site alterations affecting expression, as seen in recent committee reports assigning suffixes like N to alleles with confirmed non-functional transcripts. For instance, HLA-A*02:01:01:02G denotes an allele that encodes the identical protein to HLA-A*02:01 but includes synonymous mutations, allowing it to be grouped equivalently for functional purposes.

Common, Well-Documented, and Rare Alleles

HLA alleles are classified into categories based on their observed frequencies in large-scale population databases, such as transplant registries, and the extent of their documentation through multiple independent reports. Common alleles are defined as those with a frequency of at least 1 in 10,000 typed alleles (0.01%), intermediate alleles range from 1 in 100,000 to less than 1 in 10,000 (0.001% to <0.01%), well-documented alleles from 1 in 1,000,000 to less than 1 in 100,000 (0.0001% to <0.001%), and rare alleles below 1 in 1,000,000 (<0.0001%). These categories, established by the World Marrow Donor Association (WMDA), facilitate standardized reporting and improve matching efficiency in clinical settings.
Frequency distributions vary significantly across global populations, but data from projects like the 1000 Genomes provide a representative overview of allelic diversity. For instance, HLA-A02:01 is among the most prevalent, reaching frequencies up to 25% in Caucasian populations, while its global estimate is approximately 13%. Similarly, HLA-B07:02 exhibits frequencies around 10% in Europeans. These common alleles account for the majority of observed genotypes in diverse cohorts, with the top 10 alleles per locus often covering over 70% of the allelic variation in sampled populations. Updates from the IPD-IMGT/HLA Database as of release 3.62 (October 2025) reflect ongoing discoveries, but core common alleles remain stable, as confirmed by recent large-scale genomic resources like gnomAD.

Common and well-documented alleles predominate in donor registries, comprising the majority of matches in transplantation, as they represent over 99% of circulating genotypes in most populations. In contrast, rare alleles, which constitute more than 80% of the 42,996 named alleles in the IPD-IMGT/HLA Database, pose significant challenges for donor selection, particularly in ethnic minorities where unique variants may reach higher local frequencies but remain globally infrequent. This disparity underscores the need for diverse registry representation to mitigate transplant barriers.
A subset of rare alleles includes null alleles, which fail to produce functional HLA molecules due to mutations such as premature stops or splicing defects; for example, HLA-B*08:16N lacks surface expression and has been associated with bare lymphocyte syndrome type I, leading to severe combined immunodeficiency from impaired class I antigen presentation. Over 100 such null alleles are documented across loci, emphasizing their clinical impact despite low prevalence.

HLA Typing and Analysis

Serological and Cellular Typing Methods

Serological typing of human leukocyte antigens (HLA) relies on antibody-based assays to detect cell surface antigens, primarily using the complement-mediated microlymphocytotoxicity technique, which has served as the historical standard for identifying HLA class I and class II serotypes. In this method, isolated peripheral blood lymphocytes from the individual are incubated with a panel of specific alloantisera or monoclonal antibodies directed against known HLA antigens, followed by the addition of complement; cell death, observed via dye exclusion (e.g., trypan blue), indicates a positive reaction and antigen presence, allowing assignment of serotypes such as HLA-A2 or HLA-DR4. The assay typically employs a tray format with multiple wells, each containing a different antiserum, enabling simultaneous testing for multiple specificities, and results are interpreted under a microscope after a 1-2 hour incubation period.
Panel-reactive antibody (PRA) screening complements serological typing by assessing the breadth of pre-formed HLA antibodies in a patient's serum, which is crucial for donor selection in transplantation; serum is reacted against a panel of 50-100 well-characterized HLA-typed lymphocyte cells, and the percentage of reactive cells yields the PRA value, with levels above 50% indicating high sensitization risk. For example, a PRA of 80% for class I HLA suggests reactivity against 80% of potential donors, guiding risk stratification. This serological approach detects broad antigen specificities but cannot distinguish closely related alleles that share epitopes, leading to ambiguities in up to 25% of cases for HLA-DR typing.
Cellular typing methods, such as the mixed lymphocyte reaction (MLR), evaluate functional HLA compatibility by measuring T-cell proliferative responses to allogeneic antigens, focusing primarily on class II mismatches. In the MLR assay, responder peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) from the recipient are co-cultured with irradiated or mitomycin C-treated stimulator PBMCs from the potential donor for 5-7 days; proliferation is quantified by incorporation of tritiated thymidine or via flow cytometry for Ki-67 expression, with low responses indicating compatibility at HLA-DP, -DQ, and -DR loci. This technique assesses alloreactive T-cell activation in vitro, providing a functional correlate to serological mismatches, though it requires 1-2 weeks for results and is less specific due to contributions from minor histocompatibility antigens.
Both serological and cellular methods offer advantages in cost-effectiveness and simplicity, requiring minimal equipment and no advanced molecular infrastructure, making them suitable for resource-limited settings where they remain in use for routine typing and cross-matching. However, their limitations include low resolutionserological typing conflates alleles with identical serotypes, potentially missing clinically relevant variants, while MLR's subjectivity and time intensity have diminished its routine application since the 1990s. The World Health Organization, in collaboration with the IMGT/HLA Nomenclature Committee, continues to update serotype charts through 2025, incorporating novel designations for emerging serological specificities to maintain standardization. These traditional approaches laid the foundation for HLA assessment but have largely transitioned to higher-resolution molecular techniques for precise allele-level identification.

Molecular Sequencing and Phenotyping

Molecular sequencing techniques for human leukocyte antigen (HLA) typing have revolutionized the identification of alleles by directly analyzing DNA sequences, enabling high-resolution genotyping that distinguishes variants at the nucleotide level. Polymerase chain reaction-sequence specific primer (PCR-SSP) typing, also known as sequence-specific typing (SBT), amplifies targeted HLA regions using primers designed to match specific allele sequences, followed by Sanger sequencing for confirmation. This method achieves intermediate to high resolution, typically resolving alleles to the second field (4-digit nomenclature), and is widely used for its cost-effectiveness and reliability in clinical settings. For instance, PCR-SSP can accurately type HLA class I and II loci with error rates below 5% when combined with confirmatory sequencing.
Next-generation sequencing (NGS) has become the gold standard for high-resolution HLA typing, providing comprehensive coverage of exons, introns, and untranslated regions to resolve alleles up to the eighth digit or beyond, including synonymous and non-coding variants that influence expression. Multiplexed NGS panels, such as those amplifying all eight classical HLA loci (HLA-A, -B, -C, -DRB1, -DQA1, -DQB1, -DPA1, -DPB1) in a single reaction, enable simultaneous genotyping of multiple samples with depths exceeding 100x, reducing ambiguity to near zero. Advances in long-read NGS, including third-generation platforms, further allow full-gene sequencing, capturing intronic polymorphisms that serological methods cannot detect. Imputation methods, leveraging single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) data from genome-wide arrays, predict HLA alleles with over 95% accuracy using machine learning models trained on diverse reference panels, offering a scalable alternative for large cohort studies without direct sequencing. Additionally, as of 2025, machine learning algorithms integrated into NGS pipelines, such as those using deep learning for variant calling, have further reduced error rates and improved ambiguity resolution in diverse populations.
HLA phenotyping assesses protein expression and functionality, complementing genotyping by quantifying surface presentation and transcript levels. Flow cytometry uses fluorescently labeled monoclonal antibodies to measure HLA class I and II molecules on cell surfaces, revealing allele-specific expression variations that affect immune recognition; for example, certain HLA-B alleles exhibit up to twofold differences in density on lymphocytes. Quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) evaluates mRNA abundance, with allele-specific primers detecting imbalances in transcription, such as reduced HLA-A expression in some heterozygous individuals due to cis-regulatory elements. These techniques achieve sensitivities down to 10% allelic imbalance and are essential for correlating genotype with phenotype in disease contexts.
As of 2025, high-resolution (4-field) HLA typing is the standard recommended by organizations like the Organ Procurement and Transplantation Network (OPTN) for hematopoietic stem cell transplants and increasingly for solid organ transplants to improve matching at the allele level. NGS-based methods boast error rates under 1%, far surpassing the 10-25% ambiguity rates of serological typing, where cross-reactive antibodies lead to misassignments, such as confusing HLA-B*44 subtypes. This precision has improved transplant success rates by enabling better donor-recipient matching.

Haplotype Determination

Human leukocyte antigen (HLA) haplotypes are defined as the combination of alleles at multiple HLA loci inherited together on the same chromosome 6, forming a contiguous genetic block due to strong linkage disequilibrium (LD) across the region. These haplotypes typically include alleles from class I (HLA-A, -B, -C) and class II (HLA-DR, -DQ, -DP) loci, such as the well-known A1-B8-DR3 configuration. The HLA region's extensive polymorphism results in thousands of distinct haplotypes observed in human populations, with LD blocks preserving segments of ancient genetic diversity that reflect evolutionary history.
HLA haplotypes follow Mendelian inheritance patterns, with each parent transmitting one haplotype to offspring in a codominant manner, allowing unambiguous tracking of parental contributions in families. Recombination within the HLA region is rare, occurring at a rate of approximately 1 centimorgan (cM) across the ~4 megabase span, which minimizes shuffling and maintains haplotype integrity over generations. This low recombination frequency, lower than in many other genomic regions, contributes to the stability of extended haplotypes and their role in disease susceptibility.
Determination of HLA haplotypes primarily relies on family-based segregation analysis, where allele patterns are resolved by examining inheritance across multiple generations in pedigrees to identify non-recombinant blocks. For population-level studies or when family data are unavailable, statistical phasing methods infer haplotypes from unphased genotype data using algorithms that model LD and allele frequencies, such as the Beagle software, which employs a hidden Markov model for accurate phase reconstruction.
Prominent examples include ancestral haplotypes like the 8.1 ancestral haplotype (AH; HLA-A01:01~B08:01DRB1*03:01DQA105:01~DQB102:01), which is the most frequent extended haplotype in Caucasian populations at 5-10% carrier frequency and confers elevated risk for multiple autoimmune disorders, including myositis and celiac disease.
In clinical applications, haplotype determination enables precise risk prediction for HLA-associated diseases within families by identifying shared susceptibility blocks among relatives. As of 2025, advancements in long-read sequencing technologies, such as those integrated into tools like FuFiHLA, provide high-resolution phase determination directly from genomic data, overcoming ambiguities in short-read methods and enhancing accuracy for complex haplotype structures.

Clinical and Evolutionary Applications

HLA Matching in Transplantation and Sibling Donor Selection

HLA matching is a critical process in transplantation to minimize immunological complications by selecting donors whose human leukocyte antigens (HLA) are compatible with the recipient's. In hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT), high-resolution allele-level matching at HLA-A, -B, -C, and -DRB1 loci is standard, as mismatches at these positions significantly impact outcomes. For solid organ transplants, such as kidney or heart, epitope-level matching using tools like eplet analysis assesses functional mismatches beyond alleles, improving long-term graft survival by focusing on immunogenic sites.
Sibling donors are preferred due to higher compatibility likelihood from shared inheritance. Each full sibling has a 25% chance of being a complete 6/6 HLA match at HLA-A, -B, and -DRB1, a 50% chance of a half-match (haploidentical), and a 25% chance of no match, based on Mendelian inheritance of haplotypes. HLA typing methods, such as molecular sequencing, are employed to confirm sibling compatibility prior to selection. For cord blood transplants, greater tolerance to HLA mismatches is observed, allowing successful engraftment with up to two mismatches at allele level, due to the immunological naivety of neonatal T-cells and faster hematopoietic recovery.
Unrelated donor selection relies on large registries like the National Marrow Donor Program (NMDP), which facilitates searches for 8/8 allele matches at HLA-A, -B, -C, and -DRB1. Permissive mismatches, such as those at HLA-C or HLA-DPB1 within specific epitope groups, are increasingly accepted when full matches are unavailable, as they show lower risks compared to nonpermissive ones at HLA-A or -DRB1. These strategies expand access, with NMDP data indicating that including 7/8 or 5/8 matches can cover nearly all patients needing HSCT. As of 2025, programs like Eurotransplant have implemented donor-center virtual crossmatching, enhancing efficiency in kidney allocation.
Virtual crossmatching, using pre-stored HLA and antibody data to predict compatibility, has reduced cold ischemia times and improved allocation efficiency for highly sensitized patients in kidney transplantation, enabling faster organ dispatch without physical testing delays.
Mismatched transplants elevate risks, particularly for graft-versus-host disease (GVHD); a single HLA class I mismatch is associated with a hazard ratio (HR) of approximately 1.5-2.0 for acute GVHD and overall mortality in unrelated HSCT. Post-transplant, chimerism monitoring tracks donor cell engraftment via techniques like short tandem repeat analysis or next-generation sequencing, performed at regular intervals (e.g., days 14, 28, and monthly thereafter) to detect mixed chimerism indicating rejection risk or relapse. Full donor chimerism (>95%) by day 100 correlates with sustained remission in HSCT for malignancies, guiding interventions like donor lymphocyte infusions if needed.

Detection and Role of HLA Antibodies

Human leukocyte antigen (HLA) antibodies form as an immune response to foreign HLA antigens encountered through common sensitizing events, including pregnancy, blood transfusions, and prior organ transplants. During pregnancy, maternal exposure to paternal HLA antigens via fetal cells can induce both IgM and IgG anti-HLA antibodies, with IgG responses persisting longer and posing greater risks in future transplants. Transfusions introduce allogeneic HLA via donor leukocytes, triggering antibody production in up to 30-50% of recipients, while previous transplants often result in donor-specific antibodies (DSA) that complicate re-transplantation.
Detection of HLA antibodies relies on assays that identify specificity and functional potential to inform transplant compatibility. Complement-dependent cytotoxicity (CDC) assays detect complement-activating antibodies by observing donor cell lysis, serving as a functional benchmark for cytotoxic risk but lacking sensitivity for low-level antibodies. Luminex-based single antigen bead (SAB) assays provide higher resolution by binding patient serum to beads coated with specific HLA antigens, quantifying reactivity via mean fluorescence intensity (MFI); values exceeding 1,000 MFI typically indicate clinically relevant antibodies, enabling precise DSA identification.
HLA antibodies, especially DSA, critically influence transplant outcomes by mediating humoral rejection. Pre-existing DSA predict hyperacute rejection through rapid complement activation and vascular thrombosis upon graft reperfusion. Post-transplant DSA development drives chronic antibody-mediated rejection, including allograft vasculopathy, characterized by intimal thickening and fibrosis that accelerates graft failure.
Management strategies for HLA-sensitized patients focus on antibody reduction and risk mitigation to enable transplantation. Plasmapheresis removes circulating antibodies, often combined with intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) to neutralize remaining alloantibodies and suppress B-cell activity, achieving DSA reductions in 60-80% of cases. As of 2025, eplet matching refines donor selection by targeting HLA epitopes likely to elicit antibodies, reducing DSA formation and improving long-term graft survival compared to traditional antigen matching.
Patients with panel reactive antibody (PRA) levels above 50%reflecting broad sensitizationexperience transplant success rates approximately halved relative to unsensitized individuals, due to heightened rejection and prolonged wait times. Acceptable antigen programs address this by designating non-immunogenic HLA mismatches, facilitating allocation for highly sensitized candidates.

Influence on Mate Choice and Evolution

The human leukocyte antigen (HLA) system influences mate choice primarily through olfactory cues, aligning with the MHC-disassortative mating hypothesis that promotes selection of partners with dissimilar HLA profiles to maximize offspring heterozygosity and immune competence. In a landmark experiment, women rated the attractiveness of body odors from T-shirts worn by men for two nights, showing a preference for scents from individuals with MHC types differing from their own by at least one locus, particularly during fertile phases of the menstrual cycle. This suggests that HLA-associated volatile compounds in sweat signal genetic compatibility subconsciously. Twin studies further substantiate the genetic basis of odor perception linked to HLA, as raters could match identical twins' body odors above chance levels, unlike dizygotic twins, indicating heritable components in odor production and detection tied to MHC variation.
Reproductive outcomes reinforce the functional significance of HLA-driven mate preferences. Couples exhibiting higher HLA similarity, especially at class II loci, experience elevated rates of recurrent miscarriage and infertility compared to those with dissimilar profiles, as greater parental sharing reduces fetal heterozygosity and potentially compromises placental tolerance or immune development. For instance, primary infertile pairs share more HLA alleles than expected under random mating, correlating with impaired fertility. Mathematical models of mate choice demonstrate that such similarity elevates offspring infection risk, underscoring how disassortative mating enhances resistance to pathogens through broader antigen presentation in heterozygous progeny.
Evolutionarily, HLA-influenced mate choice sustains the extraordinary polymorphism of these genes by countering genetic drift and promoting diversity advantageous against evolving pathogens. This process operates alongside balancing selection, where heterozygous advantage provides superior immune coverage, and may reflect sexual selection for fitness rather thanor in addition tokin recognition to avoid inbreeding via odor cues. Genomic analyses of ancient DNA indicate HLA allele frequencies have exhibited relative stability over millennia in certain populations, despite demographic shifts, consistent with persistent selective forces from mate preferences and infectious pressures maintaining diversity.
