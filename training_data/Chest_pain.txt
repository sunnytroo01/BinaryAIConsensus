Chest pain
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Chest pain is discomfort or pain felt anywhere along the front of the body between the neck and upper abdomen, often described as a dull ache, sharp stabbing, tightness, pressure, squeezing, or burning sensation that may be constant or intermittent. It represents a common symptom with a broad differential diagnosis, encompassing both benign and life-threatening conditions, and accounts for approximately 5% of emergency department visits.
Symptoms of chest pain can vary widely depending on the underlying cause. Heart-related pain often presents as pressure, tightness, squeezing, or aching in the chest that may radiate to the shoulder, arm, back, neck, jaw, or stomach, and is frequently persistent and accompanied by shortness of breath, fatigue, cold sweats, lightheadedness, rapid heartbeat, or nausea. Notably, in women, central (middle) chest pain or discomfort is the most common symptom of a heart attack, often described as pressure, squeezing, tightness, or discomfort in the center of the chest; however, women may also experience atypical symptoms such as nausea, fatigue, shortness of breath, or pain in the back or jaw. In contrast, non-cardiac causes may include a sour taste in the mouth, difficulty swallowing, pain worsened or improved by position changes or movement, pain exacerbated by deep breathing, coughing, or tenderness upon touch, and prolonged discomfort without radiation. Common non-cardiac causes of central chest pain include gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) with a burning sensation, esophageal spasms, musculoskeletal strain, pulmonary embolism, or anxiety. Trapped gas can cause sharp or intense chest pain, often on the left side, mimicking heart attack symptoms; however, left arm numbness is not typical of gas or indigestion, and the combination of left-sided chest pain with arm numbness is a classic sign of a potential heart attack or other serious cardiac issue requiring immediate emergency medical attention. Mild stinging, tingling, or positional pain often suggests musculoskeletal or gastrointestinal origins, while persistent pressure with associated symptoms such as shortness of breath and cold sweats indicates higher urgency for cardiac causes. However, these characteristics are not definitive, and self-diagnosis is unreliable; any chest pain warrants prompt medical consultation to exclude serious conditions. Visceral pain from internal organs tends to feel dull, deep, or squeezing and may refer to other areas, while somatic pain from chest wall structures is typically sharp, localized, and stabbing.
The distribution of common causes of chest pain can vary by study and population. According to a 1996 study, cardiovascular etiologies such as acute coronary syndrome (including angina due to reduced blood flow to the heart and myocardial infarction ) account for about 31% of cases in acute settings, with other cardiovascular causes including aortic dissection (1%) and pericarditis (4%). Non-cardiovascular causes are also prevalent and include gastrointestinal issues like gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) or esophageal disorders (around 30% of cases), musculoskeletal problems such as costochondritis (28%), pulmonary conditions like pulmonary embolism or pleurisy (2-5%), as well as panic attacks, shingles, or gallbladder and pancreatic disorders.
However, the distribution of causes varies significantly with age. In young adults, such as a 19-year-old, chest tightness or pain is often due to benign causes including anxiety or panic attacks, asthma, acid reflux (GERD), muscle strain from exercise or poor posture, or costochondritis (inflammation of rib cartilage). Less common but serious causes include heart-related issues (e.g., myocarditis, pericarditis), pulmonary embolism, pneumothorax, or aortic dissection. In this age group, anxiety, respiratory conditions, and musculoskeletal issues are more likely than cardiac causes, but any persistent or severe chest tightness warrants prompt medical evaluation to rule out serious conditions, especially if accompanied by shortness of breath, dizziness, sweating, or pain radiating to the arm, neck, or jaw.
Given its potential to indicate serious pathology, chest pain demands urgent evaluation to rule out life-threatening conditions like acute coronary syndrome or aortic dissection, which carry high mortality risks if untreated. Immediate medical attention is advised for any new, unexplained, sudden, severe, or persistent chest pain, especially if accompanied by symptoms like nausea, dizziness, shortness of breath, cold sweats, or other concerning signs; mild chest pain lasting one hour is potentially serious and requires immediate medical evaluation, as it may indicate a heart attack or other urgent condition even if mild. Call emergency services immediately (e.g., 911 in the United States, 112 in Turkey and many other countries) or go to the nearest emergency room; do not ignore it or wait for it to resolve on its own; have someone drive you if possible, or let an ambulance transport you. Do not assume it is only gas if symptoms include left-sided chest pain with arm numbness, chest pressure, arm/jaw/neck pain, shortness of breath, or dizzinesscalling emergency services is recommended rather than attempting self-assessment. Even pain that appears mild, intermittent, or suggestive of non-cardiac causes should prompt medical consultation, as serious conditions can present atypically.

Signs and Symptoms

Characteristics of Pain

Chest pain can vary widely in its presentation, with key characteristics including location, quality, duration, onset and pattern, aggravating and relieving factors, and intensity. These features are essential for clinical assessment, as they help differentiate the sensation from other types of discomfort. Patients often describe the pain using their own words, which may range from vague discomfort to severe agony.
The location of chest pain is typically central or retrosternal, but it may also occur on the left side, right side (particularly in conditions affecting the right lung), or be diffuse across the chest. Bilateral chest pain, affecting both sides of the chest, is more commonly non-cardiac in adults. Common causes include musculoskeletal problems (e.g., muscle strain, costochondritis), gastrointestinal conditions (e.g., GERD or acid reflux), respiratory issues (e.g., pleurisy, pneumonia, or bronchitis), and anxiety/panic attacks. Right-sided chest pain during physical exertion such as running is most commonly caused by benign musculoskeletal issues, such as muscle strain, costochondritis (inflammation of rib cartilage), or chest wall tension from exertion. Other frequent causes include acid reflux (GERD) triggered by exercise or respiratory issues like exercise-induced bronchospasm. Serious but less common causes include pulmonary conditions (e.g., pleurisy, embolism) or, rarely, cardiac problems (though typically central/left-sided). Trapped gas (flatulence) can cause sharp or intense chest pain, particularly on the left side, which may closely mimic symptoms of a heart attack. Cardiac causes such as angina or heart attack are typically central or left-sided but can occasionally present as bilateral; however, bilateral chest pain is more commonly non-cardiac. Any chest pain should be evaluated by a doctor immediately to rule out serious conditions. Central (middle) chest pain, particularly in women, can indicate cardiac issues such as angina or myocardial infarction, often described as pressure, squeezing, tightness, or discomfort in the center of the chest; women commonly experience this as a main symptom of heart attack, though atypical presentations are frequent. Non-cardiac causes of central chest pain include gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD, often a burning sensation), esophageal spasms, musculoskeletal strain, pulmonary embolism, or anxiety. It frequently radiates to the arms (often the left), shoulders, jaw, neck, back, or upper abdomen, extending as far as the umbilicus or ears in some cases. This radiation pattern contributes to the pain's perceived spread beyond the chest itself.
In terms of quality, chest pain is commonly described as pressure-like or squeezing (sometimes viselike or crushing), sharp or stabbing, burning, aching, or heavy. Chest pain suggestive of cardiac origin is often described as pressure-like, squeezing, or heaviness, frequently persistent and associated with exertion or occurring at rest. In contrast, sharp, stabbing, or mild tingling pain that changes with body position or respiration may suggest non-cardiac causes, such as musculoskeletal or pleuritic origins. Less often, it feels like tearing or indigestion with an urge to belch. These descriptors reflect whether the pain is visceral (dull, deep, poorly localized) or somatic (sharp, well-localized). Specific variations include pain that is positional (changing with body movement) or pleuritic (worsening with breathing or coughing). Certain sleeping positions can also influence chest pain depending on the underlying condition. For example, lying flat on the back often worsens sharp pain in pericarditis (which typically improves when sitting up and leaning forward) and burning pain in gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD; often mitigated by elevating the head of the bed 6-8 inches). Sleeping on the affected side may aggravate musculoskeletal pain such as in costochondritis due to added pressure. In addition, sleeping in an awkward or contorted position can cause musculoskeletal strain on the chest wall muscles or intercostal muscles, often leading to sharp or aching chest pain upon waking, and may further aggravate conditions like costochondritis. Sleeping on the left side has been suggested in some cases to increase cardiac pressure, though evidence remains limited and inconclusive. Chest pain when lying down may also relate to conditions like anxiety.
Duration can be brief (less than 5 seconds, often benign), acute (minutes), episodic (minutes to hours, recurring), or chronic (lasting hours to days or longer). Onset may be sudden or gradual, with patterns including exertional triggers, occurrence at rest, nocturnal episodes, or cyclical timing related to activities like eating.
Aggravating factors often include physical exertion, deep breathing, coughing, movement, or positional changes, while relieving factors may involve rest, leaning forward, or certain medications like antacids. Intensity ranges from mild to severe and is commonly assessed on a 0-10 numeric rating scale, where 0 indicates no pain and 10 represents the worst imaginable pain. This scale aids in quantifying the experience for monitoring and comparison.
Certain characteristics and associated symptoms raise concern for serious conditions. Chest pain described as persistent pressure, tightness, or squeezing, often radiating to the arms, neck, or jaw and accompanied by shortness of breath, diaphoresis, or nausea, may indicate cardiac ischemia and requires immediate emergency medical attention. Trapped gas can produce sharp or intense left-sided chest pain that mimics cardiac conditions, but the combination of left-sided chest pain with left arm numbness is atypical for gastrointestinal causes like gas and is a classic indicator of potential cardiac issues, such as a heart attack, necessitating urgent medical evaluation. Women may experience atypical symptoms such as unusual fatigue, nausea, shortness of breath, or pain in the back or jaw, sometimes with minimal or no chest pain. Mild, tingling, or sharp pain that varies with posture or breathing is less likely to be cardiac in origin but still necessitates professional evaluation. Patients should not attempt self-diagnosis of chest pain, as symptoms can overlap significantly between cardiac and non-cardiac causes, and prompt consultation with a healthcare provider is essential for accurate assessment, particularly for new or severe chest pain, as some causes are life-threatening. These positional influences are not diagnostic in isolation, and any concerning chest pain requires prompt medical evaluation.

Associated Symptoms

Chest pain frequently presents alongside various associated symptoms that can assist in clinical evaluation and differential diagnosis. These accompanying features span multiple systems and patterns, reflecting diverse potential etiologies without specifying causes.
Autonomic symptoms such as diaphoresis, nausea, vomiting, dizziness, and syncope often co-occur with chest pain. Diaphoresis, characterized by profuse sweating, is a common autonomic response in acute episodes, particularly in cardiac-related presentations. Nausea and vomiting may accompany the discomfort, particularly in intense presentations. Dizziness or lightheadedness, sometimes progressing to syncope or fainting, signals potential instability in cardiovascular or systemic function. In women with cardiac causes such as heart attack, atypical autonomic symptoms including unusual fatigue may also occur.
Respiratory symptoms include dyspnea, cough, and hemoptysis. Dyspnea, or shortness of breath, is a frequent companion that may worsen with exertion and is commonly associated with cardiac or pulmonary etiologies. Cough, which can be dry or productive, often exacerbates the chest discomfort. Hemoptysis, the coughing up of blood, represents a more alarming respiratory feature associated with certain pulmonary involvements.
Specific combinations of respiratory symptoms may point toward particular etiologies. For example, chest tightness, right-sided or unilateral chest pain, cough, and chills (often accompanying fever) commonly indicate a respiratory condition such as pneumonia (where pain may be localized to the affected lung side, often pleuritic and worsening with breathing or coughing, with productive cough and systemic symptoms like fever and chills), pleurisy (sharp pain worsened by coughing or deep breathing, potentially with cough and fever in infectious cases), pulmonary embolism (sharp pleuritic pain and cough, though chills are less typical), acute bronchitis (chest tightness and cough), or asthma exacerbations (chest tightness and cough, sometimes with wheezing). These symptom clusters require prompt medical evaluation, as some underlying causes are serious and can lead to complications if untreated. Patients should seek professional assessment rather than self-diagnose, given the overlap with other conditions.
Gastrointestinal symptoms encompass dysphagia, heartburn, and abdominal pain. Dysphagia, or difficulty swallowing, may arise in conjunction with chest pain suggestive of esophageal involvement. Heartburn, a burning sensation, and indigestion frequently mimic or overlap with thoracic discomfort. Abdominal pain, sometimes radiating upward, can accompany these features. Trapped gas or bloating in the gastrointestinal tract can cause sharp or intense chest pain, often on the left side, mimicking heart attack symptoms. However, accompanying left arm numbness is not typical of gas or indigestion and instead indicates a potential cardiac emergency, such as a heart attack, requiring immediate emergency medical attention. Do not assume such symptoms are benign; seek urgent care if chest pain is accompanied by arm/jaw/neck discomfort, shortness of breath, or dizziness.
Neurological symptoms such as headache and altered mental status may accompany chest pain in select cases. Headache can occur as a concurrent feature, potentially linked to vascular or hypoxic states. Altered mental status, including confusion or disorientation, is less common but indicates severe underlying compromise, often tied to reduced cerebral perfusion.
Constitutional symptoms include fever, fatigue, and weight loss. Fever may present as a systemic sign alongside chest pain in inflammatory or infectious contexts. Fatigue represents a generalized exhaustion that persists with recurrent episodes. Unintentional weight loss signals a more chronic process when associated with ongoing discomfort.
Certain patterns of symptom occurrence further characterize chest pain presentations. Orthopneashortness of breath that worsens when lying flat due to fluid redistribution in heart failure and other cardiac conditionsand paroxysmal nocturnal dyspneasudden awakenings due to breathlessness at nightoften indicate fluid-related issues in cardiac conditions. Pleuritic patterns, where pain intensifies with coughing, deep breathing, or movement, suggest involvement of pleural or musculoskeletal structures. Radiation of pain or associated numbness to the jaw or arm, particularly the left arm, may accompany these symptoms in ischemic scenarios. Certain associated symptoms, such as shortness of breath, diaphoresis, nausea, and dizziness, when combined with characteristic chest pain features (especially left-sided chest pain with left arm numbness), may suggest cardiac etiology and warrant urgent evaluation; in women, additional atypical symptoms like unusual fatigue or pain in the back or jaw may be present. This combination is atypical for non-cardiac causes like trapped gas and requires immediate medical attention to rule out serious cardiac issues.

Causes

Cardiovascular Causes

Cardiovascular causes account for approximately 5-10% of chest pain presentations in emergency departments, with acute coronary syndrome being the most common etiology requiring urgent intervention. These conditions often involve myocardial ischemia, vascular rupture, or inflammation, leading to distinct pain patterns such as pressure-like or tearing sensations that may radiate to the arms, jaw, or back. Risk factors including hypertension, diabetes, and smoking significantly increase susceptibility, particularly for ischemic events.
Acute coronary syndrome (ACS) encompasses unstable angina, non-ST-elevation myocardial infarction (NSTEMI), and ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI), all characterized by substernal chest pain or discomfort described as crushing, squeezing, or pressure-like, often radiating to the left arm, jaw, or back and lasting more than 20 minutes. This pain may occur at rest or with minimal exertion in unstable angina and is associated with risk factors such as prior myocardial infarction, family history of cardiac disease, smoking, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and diabetes. In contrast, stable angina presents as predictable exertional chest pain or discomfort, which may be mild or cramping in stable cases though less commonly associated with palpitations, that typically resolves within minutes with rest or sublingual nitroglycerin, reflecting chronic coronary artery disease without acute plaque rupture.
Aortic dissection manifests as sudden, severe tearing or ripping chest pain that often radiates to the interscapular back, mimicking other acute thoracic emergencies, with hypertension serving as the primary risk factor in up to 70% of cases. Pericarditis typically causes sharp or cramping, pleuritic chest pain on the left side or behind the breastbone that worsens when lying flat in the supine position during sleep and improves with sitting forward or leaning ahead, commonly following viral infections or autoimmune processes, and may include palpitations, weakness, or fever. Arrhythmias or other heart rhythm issues can manifest as irregular heartbeats causing palpitations with mild chest pain or cramping, often on the left side. Myocarditis, an inflammatory condition of the heart muscle, presents with chest pain accompanied by palpitations, fatigue, fever, and recent viral illness, potentially progressing to arrhythmias or heart failure if untreated.
These presentations highlight the need to differentiate cardiovascular pain from other origins based on quality, radiation, and precipitating factors, with underlying ischemia often linking to reduced myocardial oxygen supply as detailed in cardiovascular mechanisms.

Respiratory Causes

Respiratory causes of chest pain primarily involve conditions affecting the lungs or pleura, often manifesting as sharp, pleuritic pain that worsens with breathing, coughing, or movement, distinguishing them from the more central pressure typical of cardiac origins. These etiologies account for approximately 2-5% of chest pain presentations in emergency department evaluations.
Pulmonary embolism, involving vascular occlusion in the pulmonary arteries, can cause sharp pleuritic chest pain exacerbated by breathing or coughing, often accompanied by cough (which may be bloody), sudden shortness of breath, and occasionally fever; chills are less typical. This occurs particularly in patients with risk factors such as deep vein thrombosis, recent surgery, or immobility.
Pneumonia, an infection of the lung parenchyma, frequently causes pleuritic chest pain, often localized to the affected lung area (such as right-sided chest pain if the right lung is involved), accompanied by high fever, shaking chills, productive cough, and sputum production; it arises from bacterial pathogens like Streptococcus pneumoniae or viral agents following an upper respiratory infection. The pain results from inflammation extending to the pleura.
Acute bronchitis, typically caused by viral infections, commonly presents with persistent cough (productive or dry), chest tightness or discomfort from coughing, shortness of breath, mild fever, and chills; chest pain, when present, is usually mild and related to coughing rather than pleuritic.
Pleuritis, or pleurisy, involves inflammation of the pleural membranes, leading to sharp, stabbing pain that intensifies with inspiration, coughing, or deep breathing; the pain is often unilateral and may be accompanied by cough, fever, or chills in infectious cases. It is commonly associated with autoimmune conditions such as systemic lupus erythematosus or infectious processes like viral pleuritis. The pain may be unilateral and accompanied by a pleural friction rub on auscultation, reflecting the inflamed layers rubbing together.
Pneumothorax occurs when air enters the pleural space, causing lung collapse and sudden, severe sharp chest pain often with acute dyspnea; it can be spontaneous, particularly in tall, thin young adults due to rupture of subpleural blebs, or traumatic from chest injury. Symptoms may include diminished breath sounds on the affected side and tachycardia, emphasizing the need for prompt recognition to prevent tension physiology.
Pulmonary hypertension, characterized by elevated pressure in the pulmonary arteries, can produce exertional chest pain resembling angina due to right ventricular strain and ischemia; it stems from various etiologies including idiopathic, connective tissue diseases, or chronic lung conditions. Associated symptoms include progressive dyspnea and fatigue, with pain typically provoked by physical activity.
Lung cancer may present with chronic chest pain from tumor invasion of the chest wall, pleura, or mediastinum, often coupled with unintentional weight loss, hemoptysis, and persistent cough; primary etiologies include smoking-related non-small cell or small cell carcinomas. The pain is usually dull and progressive, worsening as the malignancy advances.
Exacerbations of asthma or chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) can cause chest tightness or pressure due to bronchospasm and airway inflammation, frequently with wheezing, shortness of breath, and increased mucus production; triggers include allergens, infections, or irritants for asthma, while COPD flares often follow respiratory infections in smokers. The discomfort is typically diffuse and relieved partially by bronchodilators.

Gastrointestinal Causes

Gastrointestinal disorders account for a significant portion of noncardiac chest pain cases, with gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) being the most prevalent etiology, affecting approximately 20% to 60% of such patients. These conditions often produce visceral pain referred to the chest due to shared neural pathways, distinguishing them from cardiac origins by their relation to meals, posture, or swallowing, and frequently accompanied by nausea that occurs more often and may relate to food intake.
Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) manifests as a burning sensation in the chest, commonly known as heartburn, which typically occurs after eating, may be accompanied by nausea, and commonly worsens when lying down or bending over, particularly when lying flat during sleep. This positional worsening is often mitigated by elevating the head of the bed by 6 to 8 inches. This pain arises from the backflow of stomach acid into the esophagus, irritating its lining, and is often relieved by antacids or acid-suppressing medications. In severe cases, GERD can mimic angina due to the central chest location of the discomfort.
Trapped intestinal gas (also known as flatulence or wind) is a common benign gastrointestinal cause of chest pain. It arises when gas accumulates in the stomach, intestines, or left upper colon, often from swallowed air, certain foods, or digestive issues, leading to sharp, intense, or pressure-like pain that frequently localizes to the left side of the chest. This results from diaphragmatic pressure or referred visceral pain and can closely mimic heart attack symptoms. The pain is typically accompanied by abdominal bloating, belching, or flatulence and is relieved by passing gas, burping, or positional changes. Unlike cardiac causes, trapped gas does not usually produce left arm numbness, radiation to the jaw, neck, back, or shoulders, shortness of breath, dizziness, or profuse sweating. Chest pain accompanied by left arm numbness or these other cardiac red flags requires immediate emergency medical attention and should not be assumed to be due to gas alone. Self-diagnosis is inadvisable, and urgent professional evaluation is recommended when concerning symptoms are present.
Gastritis, an inflammation of the stomach lining often due to Helicobacter pylori infection, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, or alcohol, causes burning epigastric pain that may radiate to the chest and is frequently accompanied by nausea and vomiting, particularly related to food intake.
Esophageal spasm involves abnormal, uncoordinated contractions of the esophageal muscles, leading to episodic squeezing or gripping pain in the chest that can closely resemble angina. This pain is often triggered by swallowing hot or cold foods and liquids and may last from minutes to hours, accompanied by difficulty swallowing (dysphagia). Unlike cardiac pain, it lacks exertional triggers and responds variably to treatments like calcium channel blockers or nitrates.
Peptic ulcer disease, frequently associated with Helicobacter pylori infection or nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug use, causes epigastric pain that can radiate upward to the chest, particularly when the stomach is empty or after meals. The pain is typically gnawing or burning and may be alleviated by food or antacids, though complications like perforation can intensify it. H. pylori contributes by damaging the mucosal lining, promoting ulcer formation in the stomach or duodenum.
Biliary colic, resulting from gallstone obstruction of the cystic duct, presents as intermittent right upper quadrant pain that radiates to the chest or back, often following fatty meals. If inflammation develops into acute cholecystitis, the pain becomes constant and severe, potentially mimicking cardiac or pleuritic discomfort due to diaphragmatic irritation. Nausea and vomiting commonly accompany these episodes.
Pancreatitis, an inflammation of the pancreas often triggered by gallstones or alcohol consumption, produces severe, boring epigastric pain that radiates through to the back or chest, worsened by lying supine and improved by leaning forward. Acute cases are associated with profound nausea, vomiting, and systemic symptoms like fever, reflecting pancreatic enzyme autodigestion. Gallstones account for about 40% of acute pancreatitis episodes, while alcohol is implicated in roughly 30%.
Achalasia, characterized by failure of the lower esophageal sphincter to relax and loss of esophageal peristalsis, leads to dysphagia and chest pain from food and liquid retention in the esophagus. The pain is often retrosternal and cramping, exacerbated during meals, and may result in regurgitation of undigested food. This motility disorder affects younger patients more commonly and can cause weight loss due to eating difficulties.

Musculoskeletal Causes

Musculoskeletal causes of chest pain arise from structures in the chest wall, including muscles, bones, cartilage, and nerves, and are typically benign and related to mechanical or inflammatory processes. These conditions often present with localized, reproducible pain that worsens with movement, palpation, or breathing, distinguishing them from more serious visceral origins. Exertion such as running can cause or exacerbate musculoskeletal chest pain, particularly on the right side, due to muscle strain or costochondritis from chest wall tension during repetitive or asymmetric exertion. This represents a common benign cause of right chest pain during running, with pain characteristics as described in the Signs and Symptoms section. They represent the most common non-serious etiology of chest pain, accounting for approximately 25-30% of cases in primary care and emergency settings.
Costochondritis is a frequent musculoskeletal cause, involving inflammation of the costal cartilage at the junctions between the ribs and sternum, typically affecting the second to fifth ribs. The pain is often sharp or aching, localized to the anterior chest, and characterized by point tenderness that is reproducible on palpation, without radiation to the arms. The pain may also be aggravated by certain sleeping positions, such as lying on the affected side, due to pressure on the inflamed cartilage or associated muscle strain during sleep. It commonly follows trauma, repetitive strain, or upper respiratory infections with severe coughing, though the exact etiology remains unclear in many cases.
Muscle strain, or intercostal muscle injury, occurs due to overstretching or tearing from sudden trauma, heavy lifting, prolonged coughing, repetitive exertion during physical activities such as running, or sleeping in awkward or improper positions involving twisting, turning, or contorted lying, which can strain chest wall muscles or intercostal muscles, often leading to pain noticed upon waking. The pain is typically dull and worsens with deep breathing, twisting, or arm movement, but lacks the inflammatory swelling seen in cartilage disorders. This condition is self-limiting in most instances, resolving with rest and avoiding aggravating activities.
Tietze syndrome is a rare variant of costochondritis distinguished by the presence of palpable swelling over the inflamed costochondral junction, most often at the second rib. It causes similar sharp, localized pain with tenderness, but the visible or tactile edema differentiates it, potentially mimicking more serious infections or tumors. Like costochondritis, it is usually idiopathic or post-traumatic and resolves spontaneously within weeks to months.
Herpes zoster (shingles) can produce dermatomal chest pain from reactivation of the varicella-zoster virus in thoracic nerves, often preceding the characteristic vesicular rash by days to weeks. The pain is burning or stabbing, following a band-like distribution along affected ribs, and may involve intercostal muscle tenderness due to secondary myofascial involvement. Early recognition is key, as antiviral treatment can mitigate severity if initiated promptly.
Fibromyalgia contributes to chest pain through widespread musculoskeletal tenderness, including the chest wall, as part of a syndrome characterized by multiple tender points and central pain amplification. The discomfort is diffuse, aching, and non-reproducible by palpation alone, often accompanied by fatigue and sleep disturbances across the body. Chest involvement may overlap with costochondritis-like symptoms but stems from altered pain processing rather than focal inflammation.
Rib fractures or trauma induce acute, sharp chest pain following direct impact, falls, or high-force coughing, localized to the fracture site with crepitus or deformity in severe cases. Pain intensifies with inspiration, coughing, or chest wall pressure, potentially leading to shallow breathing and secondary complications if multiple ribs are involved. Most heal conservatively with pain management and respiratory exercises over 4-6 weeks.

Psychological Causes

Psychological causes of chest pain primarily involve functional disorders where no organic pathology is identified, often manifesting as noncardiac chest pain (NCCP) linked to mental health conditions such as anxiety and mood disorders. These causes account for a significant portion of unexplained chest pain cases, with psychological disturbances identified in 35% to 79% of NCCP patients. This prevalence is notably higher among young females, particularly those under 25 years of age, who report more frequent episodes influenced by emotional distress. Such pain is typically episodic and self-limiting but can lead to substantial healthcare utilization and reduced quality of life due to fear of underlying cardiac issues.
Panic disorder is a prominent psychological contributor, characterized by sudden, intense episodes of chest pain accompanied by hyperventilation and an overwhelming fear of death or impending doom. Chest pain occurs in 22% to over 70% of panic attacks, often mimicking cardiac events and prompting emergency evaluations. These attacks arise from heightened autonomic arousal, leading to sensations of tightness or pressure in the chest without structural heart disease.
Anxiety and chronic stress frequently present as episodic chest tightness, classified under somatic symptom disorder where psychological distress amplifies bodily sensations. Patients experience recurrent, non-specific discomfort triggered by stressors, with generalized anxiety disorder (GAD) co-occurring in up to 30% of NCCP cases. This tightness stems from sustained muscle tension and hyperawareness of cardiopulmonary signals, exacerbating the pain cycle.
Somatization represents another key mechanism, where chest pain serves as a physical manifestation of underlying depression or post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). In depression, somatic symptoms like chest pain affect up to 80% of patients, often alongside fatigue and anxiety. Similarly, PTSD elevates the risk of chest pain ninefold compared to non-affected individuals, driven by trauma-related hyperarousal and dissociation.
Hyperventilation syndrome contributes to chest pain through acute respiratory alkalosis, resulting from rapid breathing that induces tingling, paresthesia, and muscular chest discomfort. This syndrome is present in up to 50% of noncardiac chest pain patients and is often tied to anxiety-provoking situations. The resulting alkalosis alters nerve excitability, producing sharp or constricting pain without cardiac involvement.
Chronic pain syndromes, including functional chest pain, encompass persistent noncardiac, non-gastrointestinal discomfort that defies organic explanation and aligns with psychological overlay. These syndromes account for approximately one-third of NCCP cases, featuring recurrent episodes that impair daily functioning and are modulated by emotional factors. Unlike musculoskeletal issues, this pain lacks physical tenderness and is purely perceptual, often referencing altered pain perception mechanisms detailed elsewhere.

Other Causes

Other causes of chest pain encompass a variety of less common systemic, infectious, and miscellaneous etiologies that do not align with the primary cardiovascular, respiratory, gastrointestinal, musculoskeletal, or psychological categories. These conditions collectively account for fewer than 5% of chest pain presentations in clinical settings, often occurring in specific populations such as those with hematologic disorders or histories of substance use.
In sickle cell disease, chest pain frequently arises from vaso-occlusive crises leading to acute chest syndrome, a condition characterized by pulmonary infiltrates, fever, and respiratory distress due to sickled red blood cells obstructing pulmonary vasculature. This complication manifests as severe, pleuritic pain exacerbated by hypoxemia and inflammation, particularly in patients with underlying anemia.
Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) can produce chest pain through serositis, involving inflammation of the pleura or pericardium, which results in sharp, positional discomfort worsened by breathing or movement. Pleuritis, the most prevalent pulmonary manifestation in SLE, affects up to 40-50% of patients at some point and may present with or without effusion, often alongside systemic symptoms like fatigue.
Shingles, or herpes zoster, may cause pre-eruptive neuralgia presenting as unilateral, burning chest pain in the affected thoracic dermatome before the characteristic rash appears, sometimes mimicking cardiac or pleuritic etiologies. This prodromal phase, lasting 1-10 days, stems from reactivation of the varicella-zoster virus along sensory nerves and can occur without visible skin lesions in zoster sine herpete cases.
Malignancy-related chest pain often derives from metastatic involvement of the chest wall or mediastinal structures, such as tumors invading ribs, pleura, or lymph nodes, leading to localized, persistent aching or sharp pain due to tissue invasion and compression. Common primaries include lung, breast, or lymphoma metastases, with symptoms like weight loss or cough signaling advanced disease.
Drug-induced chest pain, particularly from cocaine or amphetamines, results from coronary vasospasm triggered by sympathomimetic effects, causing transient ischemia and angina-like symptoms without underlying atherosclerosis. Cocaine's blockade of norepinephrine reuptake heightens this risk, while amphetamines similarly elevate catecholamines, often presenting acutely in users with tachycardia or hypertension.
Trauma, including blunt contusions to the chest wall or retained foreign bodies from penetrating injuries, can elicit localized or diffuse pain through muscle strain, rib fractures, or irritation of thoracic structures. Pulmonary contusions from impact may cause hypoxemia-associated pleuritic pain, while foreign bodies like fragments from accidents can lead to chronic discomfort if not removed.

Chest Tightness in Young Adults

Chest tightness in young adults, such as in a 19-year-old, is often attributable to benign causes. Common etiologies include anxiety or panic attacks, asthma, acid reflux (gastroesophageal reflux disease or GERD), muscle strain from exercise or poor posture, and costochondritis (inflammation of the rib cartilage).
In young adults, anxiety, respiratory conditions, and musculoskeletal issues are more likely than cardiac causes to produce chest tightness.
Less common but serious causes include heart-related issues (e.g., myocarditis, pericarditis), pulmonary embolism, pneumothorax, or aortic dissection.
Any persistent or severe chest tightness warrants prompt medical evaluation to rule out serious conditions, especially if accompanied by shortness of breath, dizziness, sweating, or pain radiating to the arm, neck, or jaw.

Pathophysiology

Cardiovascular Mechanisms

Myocardial ischemia, a primary cardiovascular cause of chest pain, arises from an imbalance between myocardial oxygen supply and demand, often due to coronary artery occlusion or spasm. This mismatch leads to rapid depletion of adenosine triphosphate (ATP) stores in cardiomyocytes, shifting metabolism to anaerobic glycolysis and resulting in accumulation of lactic acid and other metabolites. The buildup of these substances, particularly adenosine, stimulates cardiac nociceptors, activating adenosine A1 receptors on afferent nerve endings and generating a sensation of pressure or tightness in the chest. This adenosine hypothesis, supported by observations that exogenous adenosine infusion reproduces anginal pain in susceptible individuals, underscores the role of metabolic byproducts in pain transduction during ischemia.
In aortic dissection, chest pain typically manifests as an abrupt, severe tearing sensation due to a propagating intimal tear that allows blood to dissect into the aortic media, creating a false lumen. This process stretches and separates the aortic wall layers, particularly the media, which is richly innervated and sensitive to mechanical distortion, eliciting intense nociceptive signaling. The propagation of the dissection flap compromises vessel integrity and can extend distally or proximally, amplifying the tearing pain through ongoing wall tension and potential involvement of adjacent structures.
Pericarditis induces chest pain through inflammation of the pericardial sac, where the parietal pericardium's irritationdue to its somatic innervationproduces sharp, pleuritic discomfort that worsens with lying down or inspiration and improves with sitting forward. The inflamed visceral and parietal pericardial layers rub against each other during cardiac motion, generating a characteristic friction rub audible on auscultation in up to 85% of cases, which correlates with heightened pain intensity. This positional and activity-dependent pain reflects the mechanical friction and inflammatory mediators sensitizing local nociceptors.
Cardiac pain signals are transmitted via visceral afferent pathways, primarily through vagal (parasympathetic) and sympathetic nerves. Vagal afferents, originating from nodose ganglia, convey non-nociceptive and some pain signals to the brainstem, while sympathetic afferents from the heart enter the spinal cord at thoracic levels T1-T5 via dorsal root ganglia. These afferents synapse in the dorsal horn, where convergence with somatic inputs from shared dermatomes explains referred pain to the left arm, shoulder, neck, or jaw during ischemic or inflammatory events. This viscerosomatic convergence in the spinal cord amplifies and mislocalizes the pain, mimicking musculoskeletal origins.

Gastrointestinal Mechanisms

Gastrointestinal mechanisms of chest pain primarily arise from dysfunction in the esophagus, where chemical, mechanical, or motility disturbances trigger nociceptive pathways that mimic cardiac pain due to shared neural projections. In gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), incompetence of the lower esophageal sphincter allows gastric acid to reflux into the esophagus, irritating the mucosa and directly activating nociceptors such as acid-sensing ion channels and transient receptor potential channels. This acid exposure stimulates vagal and spinal afferent nerves, leading to burning or squeezing chest pain that can radiate to the back or arms.
Esophageal spasms contribute to chest pain through uncoordinated or hypercontractile esophageal smooth muscle activity, particularly in diffuse esophageal spasm, where simultaneous contractions in the distal esophagus elevate intraluminal pressure and cause intense, episodic pain often described as a vise-like sensation. These spasms disrupt normal peristalsis, resulting in prolonged contractions that stretch the esophageal wall and activate mechanosensitive nociceptors.
Motility disorders like achalasia exacerbate chest pain via impaired esophageal emptying; degeneration of inhibitory neurons in the myenteric plexus prevents relaxation of the lower esophageal sphincter, leading to food retention, progressive esophageal distension, and wall tension that stimulates stretch-sensitive afferents. This distension-induced pain is often nocturnal and retrosternal, contributing to symptoms in up to 50% of patients.
Visceral hypersensitivity amplifies these GI signals through alterations in the brain-gut axis, where central sensitization in the spinal cord and brainstem heightens perception of normal or subthreshold esophageal stimuli, transforming innocuous reflux or distension into severe chest pain. This hypersensitivity involves enhanced activity in vagal and spinal pathways, often seen in functional esophageal disorders.
The perception of esophageal pain as chest pain occurs via referred pain mechanisms, as visceral afferents from the esophagus converge with somatic fibers in thoracic spinal segments T1-T6, leading to mislocalization of the discomfort to the anterior chest wall. Conditions such as peptic ulcers may occasionally refer pain to the chest through similar pathways but are primarily detailed under gastrointestinal causes.

Psychogenic Mechanisms

Psychogenic mechanisms of chest pain encompass neural and psychological processes that heighten or originate pain signals without identifiable structural pathology, often amplifying innocuous visceral or somatic inputs into distressing experiences. Central sensitization is a primary process, involving enhanced neural responsiveness in the central nervous system that lowers pain thresholds and expands pain referral areas. In non-cardiac chest pain (NCCP), patients demonstrate temporal summation of pain and visceral hyperalgesia following sensitization stimuli, such as esophageal acid perfusion, indicating augmented central processing. Stress exacerbates this through heightened activity in limbic structures like the amygdala and insula, which integrate emotional and sensory inputs to intensify pain perception.
Autonomic dysregulation contributes significantly, with sympathetic nervous system overdrive promoting sustained muscle tension in the chest wall and a subjective sense of tightness. This hyperarousal state, common in anxiety, triggers cardiovascular symptoms including chest pain via excessive norepinephrine release and impaired parasympathetic counterbalance. In psychogenic contexts, chronic stress sustains this overdrive, leading to persistent perceptual distortions of thoracic sensations without tissue damage.
The nocebo effect further amplifies psychogenic chest pain, as negative expectations from anxiety transform benign sensations into perceived threats. In NCCP, cardiophobiairrational fear of heart disease despite normal evaluationsintensifies symptom reporting through learned associations and anticipatory distress. Concurrently, neurotransmitter imbalances, particularly involving serotonin and norepinephrine, underpin these effects in panic-prone individuals, where serotonergic dysregulation heightens CO2 sensitivity and noradrenergic surges provoke acute autonomic storms manifesting as chest discomfort.
Somatic focus manifests as an attentional bias toward chest-related bodily signals in anxiety disorders, fostering hypervigilance that escalates neutral sensations into painful ones. This selective attention, mediated by heightened interoceptive awareness, correlates with increased health anxiety and symptom persistence in NCCP, independent of actual amplification. Such mechanisms are especially evident in panic disorder, where recurrent episodes reinforce perceptual distortions.

Diagnostic Approach

History Taking and Physical Examination

The evaluation of chest pain begins with a thorough history taking, which serves as the cornerstone for characterizing the symptom and guiding further assessment. The OPQRST framework is commonly employed to systematically elicit details: Onset (sudden or gradual, associated activity), Provocation/palliation (worsened by exertion, breathing, or position; relieved by rest or antacids), Quality (e.g., pressure-like, sharp, burning), Radiation (to arm, jaw, or back), Severity (on a 0-10 scale), and Time course (duration, frequency). A variant, SOCRATES, expands this to include Site (location), Onset, Character, Radiation, Associated symptoms (e.g., dyspnea, nausea, diaphoresis), Time course, Exacerbating/relieving factors, and Severity, particularly useful for distinguishing cardiac from noncardiac etiologies such as pleuritic pain suggesting pulmonary involvement or postprandial onset indicating gastrointestinal origin. 
Key historical elements include cardiovascular risk factors such as age over 40 years, smoking history, family history of premature coronary disease, hypertension, diabetes, and hyperlipidemia, which elevate suspicion for ischemic causes. Red flags warranting urgent evaluation include associated syncope, unexplained hypotension, or symptoms like sudden severe tearing pain radiating to the back. Associated symptoms such as shortness of breath, vomiting, fever, or leg swelling further refine the differential. The history is essential for narrowing the differential diagnosis and must be integrated with other findings for accuracy. 
The physical examination complements the history by assessing for hemodynamic instability and focal signs. Vital signs are evaluated first, including heart rate (tachycardia >100 bpm suggesting arrhythmia or shock), blood pressure (hypotension <90/60 mmHg or inter-arm difference >20 mmHg indicating possible dissection), respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, and temperature. Cardiac examination involves inspection for jugular venous distension, palpation for thrills or heaves, and auscultation for murmurs, pericardial friction rubs, or extra heart sounds like S3/S4 gallops. 
Pulmonary assessment includes inspection for respiratory distress or asymmetry, palpation for crepitus, and auscultation for wheezes, crackles, or diminished breath sounds suggestive of consolidation or effusion. Abdominal palpation checks for tenderness or rebound, potentially indicating referred pain from gastrointestinal sources. Chest wall palpation tests for reproducibility of pain with pressure, which if present reduces likelihood of visceral causes. Neurological examination screens for focal deficits such as unequal pupils or hemiparesis, which may signal aortic dissection with cerebrovascular involvement. Overall, these bedside techniques help identify red flags and prioritize life-threatening conditions while informing risk stratification. 

Risk Stratification

Risk stratification in chest pain evaluation involves the use of validated clinical scoring systems to categorize patients by urgency, identify those at low risk for major adverse cardiac events (MACE), and guide safe disposition decisions, such as early discharge from the emergency department (ED). These tools integrate elements from patient history, electrocardiogram (ECG) findings, age, cardiovascular risk factors, and biomarkers like troponin to provide a structured risk assessment, often allowing for accelerated protocols that minimize unnecessary hospitalizations while maintaining high sensitivity for detecting acute coronary syndrome (ACS).
The HEART score is a widely adopted tool for ED patients with chest pain, assigning points across five domains: History (0-2 points based on symptom typicality for ACS), ECG (0-2 points for changes like ST depression), Age (0-2 points, with 65 years scoring 2), Risk factors (0-2 points for 3 factors such as diabetes or smoking), and Troponin (0-2 points for elevated levels). A total score of 0-3 indicates low risk (MACE rate of 0.6-1.7% at 6 weeks), 4-6 intermediate risk requiring observation, and 7 high risk warranting urgent intervention. Originally derived in 2008 and prospectively validated in a cohort of 2,440 patients, the HEART score demonstrates strong discriminatory ability with an area under the curve (AUC) of 0.82-0.85 for predicting MACE, including myocardial infarction or revascularization. However, in the 2020 ESC Guidelines for the management of acute coronary syndromes in patients presenting without persistent ST-segment elevation (NSTE-ACS), the HEART score is referenced in a supporting role, including a citation to Ljung et al. (2019) for its combination with high-sensitivity cardiac troponin in rule-out strategies aimed at reducing hospital admissions, but it is not recommended as a primary tool for risk stratification or for determining the timing of invasive strategies.
The Thrombolysis In Myocardial Infarction (TIMI) risk score is specifically designed for patients with suspected ACS presenting with chest pain, incorporating seven binary predictors: age 65 years, 3 CAD risk factors (e.g., hypertension, family history), known coronary artery stenosis 50%, recent aspirin use, 2 angina episodes in 24 hours, ST-segment deviation 0.5 mm, and elevated cardiac markers. Scores range from 0-7, with 0-2 classifying low risk (14-day MACE rate 4.7%), 3-4 intermediate (13%), and 5-7 high (40%). Validated in over 4,000 patients from the TIMI 11B trial, it aids in prognosticating outcomes like death or recurrent ischemia and supports decisions on invasive strategies.
The GRACE (Global Registry of Acute Coronary Events) risk score is the primary recommended tool in the 2020 ESC NSTE-ACS guidelines for prognostic assessment and guiding management decisions in patients with NSTE-ACS. It incorporates variables including age, heart rate, systolic blood pressure, creatinine level, Killip class, cardiac arrest at admission, ST-segment deviation on ECG, and elevated cardiac biomarkers to estimate in-hospital and post-discharge mortality risk. A GRACE score >140 identifies higher-risk patients warranting an early invasive strategy within 24 hours.
The Emergency Department Assessment of Chest Pain Score (EDACS) integrates age, symptom characteristics (e.g., radiation, diaphoresis), ECG findings, and troponin levels to rule out low-risk patients eligible for early discharge. In derivation and validation across 4,252 ED patients, a score 16 combined with a normal ECG and troponin below the 99th percentile identified 42-51% as low risk, with a 30-day MACE sensitivity of 99.3% and specificity of 10-16%. This accelerated diagnostic pathway (ADP) emphasizes symptom onset >3 hours and stable vital signs, enabling safe discharge in select cases without serial testing.
The Vancouver Chest Pain Rule (VCP), also known as the VCPR, targets low-risk identification for patients under 40 years with non-exertional, non-pleuritic chest pain, normal initial ECG, and no history of ischemic symptoms. Validated in a multicenter study of 1,102 patients, it achieves >99% sensitivity for ACS and supports discharge within 2 hours if troponin is normal at presentation and 2 hours, reducing ED resource use for young, low-probability cases.
The ADAPT (Accelerated Diagnostic Protocol to Assess Patients with Chest Pain) combines elements of the TIMI score (1) with negative troponin at 0 and 2 hours, plus normal ECG, to stratify low-risk patients for early ED discharge. In a prospective validation of 1,425 patients, this ADP safely ruled out 30-day MACE in 20% of cases with 99.2% sensitivity, outperforming TIMI alone by incorporating high-sensitivity troponin for faster rule-out.
These scoring systems collectively enhance efficiency in chest pain management, with implementation studies showing reductions in unnecessary hospital admissions of 19-27% for low-risk patients without compromising safety (MACE miss rate <2%). The 2021 AHA/ACC Chest Pain Guideline, updated with 2023 endorsements on chronic coronary disease and the 2025 ACC/AHA/ACEP/NAEMSP/SCAI Guideline for the Management of Patients With Acute Coronary Syndromes, emphasizes integration of high-sensitivity troponin into these tools for optimal risk assessment, recommending their use in protocols to standardize disposition. Additionally, the 2020 ESC Guidelines for NSTE-ACS recommend the GRACE risk score as the main tool for prognosis and guiding the timing of invasive strategy, such as an early invasive approach within 24 hours for a GRACE score >140. In patients with a history of cancer, pretest probability for ACS and PE is elevated, with substantially higher prevalence of myocardial infarction as the cause of chest pain; AHA/ACC guidelines thus recommend rapid assessment including ECG and troponin for those with intermediate or high-risk features, as non-emergency settings are inadequate for initial rule-out.

Diagnostic Tests

Electrocardiography (ECG) is the initial diagnostic test for patients presenting with chest pain, performed within 10 minutes of arrival to detect ischemic changes such as ST-segment elevation, ST depression, or T-wave inversions, which indicate acute coronary syndrome (ACS). Serial ECGs are recommended if the initial tracing is nondiagnostic and symptoms persist, to identify evolving ischemia.
Cardiac biomarkers, particularly high-sensitivity troponin (hs-Tn), are essential for confirming myocardial injury; measurements are obtained at presentation (0 hours) with repeats at 1-3 hours to detect rises or falls indicative of myocardial infarction (MI). The 2023 European Society of Cardiology (ESC) guidelines endorse rapid 0/1-hour or 0/3-hour hs-Tn protocols for efficient rule-out or rule-in of non-ST-elevation ACS in suspected cases, with further refinements in the 2025 ACC/AHA/ACEP/NAEMSP/SCAI Guideline for Acute Coronary Syndromes.
Chest radiography evaluates for noncardiac causes of chest pain, including pneumothorax or widened mediastinum suggestive of aortic dissection. For suspected pulmonary embolism (PE) or aortic dissection in low-risk patients, D-dimer testing precedes computed tomography (CT) angiography to avoid unnecessary imaging; CT angiography is the modality of choice for confirming PE or dissection when indicated.
Echocardiography assesses for wall motion abnormalities indicating ischemia or pericardial effusion as a cause of chest pain. It is particularly useful in acute settings to evaluate structural cardiac issues.
In stable patients with suspected angina, stress testing such as exercise treadmill testing evaluates for inducible ischemia. This is appropriate for low- to intermediate-risk individuals without recent ACS.
For refractory gastrointestinal-related chest pain, upper endoscopy is recommended to identify esophageal disorders like gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) after failure of empirical therapy.

Management

Prehospital and Initial Care

Prehospital care for patients experiencing chest pain, particularly when acute coronary syndrome (ACS) is suspected, begins with immediate activation of emergency medical services (EMS) through a 9-1-1 call to facilitate rapid assessment and transport. EMS protocols prioritize obtaining a 12-lead electrocardiogram (ECG) within 10 minutes of first medical contact to identify ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI) and guide triage. Initial stabilization focuses on symptom relief, risk mitigation, and efficient transport to appropriate facilities, with ongoing monitoring of vital signs and pain levels en route to detect changes in hemodynamic status.
Pharmacologic interventions form the core of EMS management for suspected ACS. Aspirin, at a loading dose of 162-325 mg chewed, should be administered as soon as possible to inhibit platelet aggregation and reduce mortality risk. Sublingual nitroglycerin (0.3-0.4 mg every 5 minutes for up to three doses) is recommended for ongoing ischemic chest pain, provided systolic blood pressure exceeds 90 mmHg and there are no contraindications such as recent phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitor use; it should be avoided in cases of hypotension or used with caution in inferior myocardial infarction due to the risk of precipitating profound hypotension from right ventricular involvement. Supplemental oxygen is reserved for patients with hypoxemia (SpO2 <90%), targeting SpO2 90%, as routine administration in normoxemic individuals may cause harm. For severe, unrelieved pain, morphine sulfate (1-5 mg intravenously, titrated) may be administered cautiously, though its use is limited due to potential interactions with antiplatelet agents and increased risk of adverse outcomes.
Transport decisions emphasize rapidity and capability: patients with suspected STEMI should be directed to a percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI)-capable center, aiming for a first medical contact-to-device time of 90 minutes when feasible. The 2025 ACC/AHA guidelines refine the traditional MONA protocol (morphine, oxygen, nitroglycerin, aspirin) by restricting oxygen to hypoxic cases and advising judicious morphine use, while upholding aspirin and nitroglycerin as foundational elements for prehospital stabilization.

Acute Hospital Management

Upon arrival at the hospital, patients with suspected acute coronary syndrome (ACS) undergo immediate anticoagulation with unfractionated heparin or low-molecular-weight heparin (LMWH) to prevent thrombus propagation, alongside initiation of beta-blockers to reduce myocardial oxygen demand and dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) consisting of aspirin plus a P2Y12 inhibitor such as clopidogrel, prasugrel, or ticagrelor. For ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI), percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) is performed with a door-to-balloon time of less than 90 minutes to restore coronary blood flow. Prehospital-administered aspirin is continued in the hospital setting to maintain its antiplatelet effects.
In cases of suspected pulmonary embolism (PE) causing hemodynamic instability, systemic thrombolysis is administered for massive PE to rapidly dissolve the clot, while anticoagulation with direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) is preferred for stable patients to prevent recurrence. For intermediate-risk PE, close monitoring guides the decision for thrombolysis if deterioration occurs.
Acute aortic dissection requires urgent surgical consultation for type A dissections involving the ascending aorta, with initial medical management using intravenous beta-blockers to reduce heart rate and shear stress on the aortic wall, targeting a heart rate below 60 beats per minute. Blood pressure control with additional agents like vasodilators follows once heart rate is optimized.
For acute pericarditis presenting as chest pain, treatment involves high-dose nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as ibuprofen or aspirin, combined with colchicine to reduce inflammation and recurrence risk, with gastroprotection to mitigate gastrointestinal side effects. Corticosteroids are reserved for contraindications to first-line therapy.
Arrhythmias contributing to chest pain, such as unstable tachycardia, are managed with synchronized cardioversion if the patient is hemodynamically unstable, per advanced cardiovascular life support (ACLS) protocols, to restore sinus rhythm promptly.
Throughout hospitalization, continuous telemetry monitoring detects arrhythmias or ischemic changes, while serial cardiac troponin measurements every 3-6 hours assess for ongoing myocardial injury and guide risk stratification.
Recent 2025 updates in ACS guidelines recommend shortening DAPT duration to 1-3 months for high-bleeding-risk patients undergoing PCI with newer drug-eluting stents, transitioning to monotherapy to minimize bleeding complications without increasing ischemic events.

Outpatient and Follow-up Care

Outpatient and follow-up care for chest pain focuses on managing underlying causes in stable patients, optimizing preventive therapies, and monitoring for recurrence to improve long-term outcomes. For individuals with resolved acute presentations, care transitions to chronic management strategies tailored to the etiology, emphasizing guideline-directed medical therapy and lifestyle interventions.
In patients with stable angina or chronic coronary disease, high-intensity statins are recommended as first-line therapy to lower low-density lipoprotein cholesterol by at least 50%, reducing cardiovascular events such as myocardial infarction and heart failure hospitalization. Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors or angiotensin receptor blockers are indicated for hypertension control and secondary prevention, particularly in those with left ventricular ejection fraction 40%, diabetes, or chronic kidney disease, lowering cardiovascular risk by approximately 20%. Beta-blockers serve as first-line antianginal agents to relieve symptoms and improve ischemic thresholds, especially post-myocardial infarction or with left ventricular dysfunction. Dual antiplatelet therapy with aspirin and a P2Y12 inhibitor is advised for up to 6 months following percutaneous coronary intervention, transitioning to single antiplatelet therapy thereafter to balance ischemic and bleeding risks. Cardiac rehabilitation is strongly encouraged post-myocardial infarction, percutaneous coronary intervention, or coronary artery bypass grafting, yielding reductions in morbidity, mortality, rehospitalization, and improvements in quality of life. These recommendations align with the 2023 AHA/ACC/ACCP/ASPC/NLA/PCNA Guideline for the Management of Patients With Chronic Coronary Disease, which emphasizes secondary prevention post-acute coronary syndrome through optimized guideline-directed medical therapy.
For gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) as a cause of chest pain, an 8-week trial of once-daily proton pump inhibitors (PPIs), such as omeprazole 40 mg administered 30-60 minutes before a meal, is the mainstay for symptom relief and healing of erosive esophagitis. Maintenance therapy should use the lowest effective PPI dose to control symptoms while minimizing long-term risks, with twice-daily dosing reserved for refractory cases. Lifestyle modifications are integral, including weight loss for overweight or obese patients to improve symptoms, elevating the head of the bed by 6-8 inches to reduce nocturnal reflux, avoiding meals within 2-3 hours of bedtime, and eliminating triggers such as fatty foods, caffeine, alcohol, spicy foods, tobacco, and large meals. These approaches are outlined in the American College of Gastroenterology Clinical Guideline for the Diagnosis and Management of Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease.
Musculoskeletal chest pain, such as costochondritis, is managed outpatient with oral or topical nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) like ibuprofen to alleviate inflammation and discomfort, though higher doses require monitoring for gastrointestinal side effects. Physical therapy involving targeted stretching exercises is recommended, particularly for chronic symptoms lasting over 1 year, to reduce pain and improve mobility. Nonpharmacologic measures include rest from aggravating activities and local heat application, with adjuncts like lidocaine patches or acupuncture considered for persistent cases based on clinical experience. These strategies are supported by evidence reviews from the American Academy of Family Physicians.
Psychogenic chest pain related to anxiety or panic disorders warrants selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), such as sertraline or escitalopram, as first-line pharmacotherapy for moderate to severe cases, continued for at least 6-12 months after symptom remission to prevent relapse. Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), delivered in 7-14 hours over 4 months, is a high-intensity psychological intervention that addresses maladaptive thoughts and behaviors contributing to anxiety-driven symptoms. For immediate relief of stress-induced chest tightness, techniques include deep breathing exercises (inhaling through the nose for 4 seconds, holding for 4 seconds, and exhaling through the mouth for 6-8 seconds), drinking water, sitting comfortably while relaxing the shoulders and chest, engaging in calming activities such as light walking or listening to music, and avoiding caffeine to prevent elevation of heart rate. These treatments are recommended by the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence for generalised anxiety disorder and panic disorder, where physical manifestations like chest pain may mimic cardiac issues.
Follow-up involves specialist referrals to cardiology clinics for ongoing risk stratification in coronary artery disease patients or gastroenterology for persistent gastrointestinal symptoms, with repeat stress testing considered for intermediate-risk individuals if prior results are inconclusive or new symptoms emerge, but avoided within 30 days for low-risk cases. Patient education emphasizes maintaining a symptom diary to track triggers and patterns, alongside instructions to seek immediate emergency care for recurrent severe chest pain, worsening symptoms, or associated features like shortness of breath. These elements are integral to the 2021 AHA/ACC/ASE/CHEST/SAEM/SCCT/SCMR Guideline for the Evaluation and Diagnosis of Chest Pain, promoting shared decision-making and risk factor management in outpatient settings.

Epidemiology and Prognosis

Epidemiology

Chest pain is a common presenting complaint in medical settings, accounting for approximately 5% to 10% of all emergency department (ED) visits in the United States, with an estimated 10 million such visits occurring annually as of 2025. In primary care, the annual incidence among adults is about 1% to 2%, reflecting its frequent occurrence across healthcare contexts. Lifetime prevalence in the general population ranges from 20% to 40%, underscoring its broad impact.
Demographically, chest pain presentations increase with age, with incidence peaking between 40 and 60 years, after which rates continue to rise but more gradually due to higher cardiovascular risk in older adults. Gender differences are notable: cardiac etiologies, such as acute coronary syndrome, are more prevalent in males, while females experience higher rates of gastrointestinal and psychological causes, including gastroesophageal reflux and anxiety-related pain.
Geographically, the prevalence of acute coronary syndromea leading serious cause of chest painis higher in Western countries like those in North America and Europe compared to Mediterranean or Asian regions, attributable to lifestyle factors such as high-fat diets, smoking, and physical inactivity. Trends show a decline in cardiac-related mortality from chest pain since 2010, driven by advances including statin therapy for cholesterol management and percutaneous coronary interventions for revascularization. Conversely, there has been an uptick in anxiety-associated chest pain presentations following the COVID-19 pandemic, linked to a 25% global increase in anxiety prevalence during the crisis.
Most chest pain episodes are benign, with musculoskeletal causes comprising around 25% of cases, but approximately 2% to 4% of serious conditions, such as acute myocardial infarction, are missed during initial ED evaluations, highlighting the need for vigilant assessment.

Prognosis

The prognosis of chest pain varies significantly depending on its underlying etiology, with cardiac causes generally carrying higher risks compared to non-cardiac origins. For patients with ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI), a common cardiac cause, in-hospital mortality rates range from 5% to 10%, though timely percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) can reduce this to under 1% in optimal scenarios. Long-term outcomes for myocardial infarction survivors include approximately a 20% rate of major adverse cardiac events over five years, influenced by recurrent ischemia and heart failure. In contrast, non-cardiac chest pain, such as that from musculoskeletal conditions like costochondritis or gastrointestinal issues like gastroesophageal reflux disease, has an excellent prognosis with over 95% resolution following appropriate management, though cases linked to malignancy exhibit more variable outcomes due to the underlying cancer progression.
Several factors modulate prognosis across etiologies, including patient age, comorbidities such as diabetes or prior cardiovascular disease, and delays in seeking or receiving treatment. For instance, treatment delays exceeding two hours in acute myocardial infarction are associated with worsened outcomes, including higher rates of heart failure and mortality. Overall, approximately 90% of chest pain presentations in emergency departments are benign and self-limiting, but missed acute coronary syndrome carries a substantial risk, with a 2- to 3-fold higher 30-day mortality compared to diagnosed cases. This underscores the high emergency department burden from chest pain, as noted in epidemiologic data.
Advancements in care protocols have markedly improved outcomes, with in-hospital and 30-day mortality for acute coronary events declining from around 15% in the 1990s to less than 5% in recent years, attributable to widespread adoption of reperfusion strategies and risk assessment tools. Recent 2024 studies affirm the utility of the HEART score, where low-risk classifications (0-3 points) predict fewer than 1% major adverse cardiac events at 30 days, enabling safer outpatient management.
