Indirect speech
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Indirect speech, also known as reported speech or indirect discourse, is a grammatical construction in language that conveys the substance of a speaker's or writer's words without using their exact phrasing, often through embedding the content in a subordinate clause introduced by a reporting verb such as "say" or "tell." This mechanism adjusts elements like verb tenses, pronouns, and time/place references to fit the reporter's viewpoint and temporal context, distinguishing it from direct speech, which reproduces the original words verbatim within quotation marks. For example, the direct quote "I am tired," she said becomes She said that she was tired in indirect speech, reflecting a backshift in tense from present to past.
In contrast to direct speech, which preserves the original illocutionary force, intonation, and deictic expressions for vividness or authenticitycommon in narratives, journalism, and dialogueindirect speech prioritizes summarization and integration into the reporter's narrative, making it prevalent in formal writing, academic discourse, and everyday reporting. It serves to attribute ideas or statements to a source while maintaining grammatical cohesion, often omitting the optional complementizer "that" in English (e.g., She said she was tired). Key transformations include pronoun shifts (e.g., "I" to "she") to avoid ambiguity and adverbial adjustments (e.g., "now" to "then," "here" to "there") to account for spatial and temporal displacement from the original event.
The rules governing indirect speech vary by sentence type: statements typically backshift tenses (present simple to past simple, past simple to past perfect) unless the reported information remains universally true; questions invert to declarative word order, using "if/whether" for yes/no queries or retaining wh-words for information questions (e.g., "Where are you going?" becomes He asked where I was going); and commands or requests employ infinitive structures (e.g., "Close the door" becomes She told him to close the door). These adaptations ensure semantic fidelity while embedding the report smoothly, though exceptions occur with stative verbs or habitual actions that resist tense shifts.
Cross-linguistically, indirect speech exhibits significant variation, forming a dedicated syntactic domain that may involve complementizers, logophoric pronouns, or evidential markers rather than strict tense backshifting, as seen in languages like English versus those that do not require tense backshifting such as Lango. In some languages, such as Modern Hebrew, direct speech can appear without explicit framing verbs, while others, like Kwaza, use double person marking for perspective blending; this diversity highlights reported speech as a universal yet adaptable tool for representing others' voices, epistemic stance, and modality in communication. Overall, indirect speech facilitates nuanced attribution in discourse, bridging the perspectives of speaker and reporter across genres and cultures.

Fundamentals

Definition and Distinction from Direct Speech

Indirect speech, also known as reported speech, is a grammatical construction in language that allows speakers or writers to convey the substance of another person's utterance, thought, or writing without reproducing their exact words. This form embeds the reported content within a subordinate clause, often introduced by conjunctions such as "that," "if," or "whether," thereby integrating it into the reporter's own discourse. Unlike literal quotation, indirect speech focuses on the meaning rather than the precise phrasing, making it a flexible tool for summarizing or paraphrasing communication.
The primary distinction between indirect speech and direct speech lies in their approach to representation and adaptation. Direct speech employs quotation marks to preserve the original wording, tense, pronouns, and deictic elements (such as "here" or "now") exactly as uttered, mimicking the source's voice and perspectivefor instance, "She said, 'I am tired.'" In contrast, indirect speech rephrases and adjusts these elements to align with the reporter's temporal and spatial context, such as "She said that she was tired," which shifts the perspective to the reporting frame without quotation. This adaptation ensures coherence in the surrounding narrative but sacrifices verbatim fidelity.
This usage built on classical rhetorical concepts like oratio obliqua (indirect discourse) from Latin grammar, but modern linguistics, particularly through works like those of Voloinov and Bakhtin in the early 20th century, emphasized its role in dialogic interaction and context-shifting.
In terms of basic syntactic structure, indirect speech commonly follows a reporting verbsuch as "say," "tell," or "ask"followed by the subordinate clause containing the adapted content, as in "He told me that the meeting had started." This pattern subordinates the reported material, often omitting the introductory "that" in informal contexts, and facilitates smooth integration into larger sentences.

Purpose and Contexts of Use

Indirect speech primarily serves to summarize or integrate another person's words into the reporter's narrative for greater conciseness, allowing the essence of the original utterance to be conveyed without reproducing it verbatim. It also shifts the perspective to that of the reporter, embedding the reported content within the reporter's own syntactic and temporal framework, which facilitates a smoother flow in extended discourse. Additionally, it enables avoidance of direct quoting in narratives where exact wording is less critical than the conveyed meaning or intent.
In journalistic contexts, indirect speech is commonly employed to paraphrase interviews and statements, prioritizing the conveyance of information, truth, and reliability while constructing newsworthiness and framing stories through evidentiality and intertextuality. In fiction, it appears as a stylistic variant known as free indirect discourse, which blends the third-person narrator's voice with a character's internal thoughts to provide intimate access to the character's perspective without shifting to first-person narration. In legal and academic writing, indirect speech attributes ideas or testimony by rephrasing them, maintaining formal flow and integrating sources seamlessly while adhering to standards that favor paraphrasing for clarity and brevity over direct quotes.
Compared to direct speech, indirect speech offers advantages such as reducing repetition in texts heavy with dialogue by eliminating quotation marks and tags, thus streamlining the narrative structure. It also permits seamless integration into the reporter's voice, enhancing cohesion and allowing the reporter to contextualize or interpret the original content without disrupting the overall tone.
It is driven by professional standards that encourage paraphrasing for non-controversial content to ensure objectivity and efficiency, as outlined in guidelines like those from the Associated Press, which recommend indirect constructions for fair summaries while reserving direct quotes for sensitive passages.

Grammatical Transformations

Verb Tense and Aspect Shifts

In indirect speech, the core rule of backshifting, also known as the sequence of tenses, requires that when the reporting verb is in the past tense, the tense of the verb in the reported clause shifts back one step to maintain temporal relations. For instance, a present simple tense becomes past simple ("I eat apples" reported as "She said she ate apples"), while a past simple becomes past perfect ("I ate apples" reported as "She said she had eaten apples"). This adjustment ensures the reported clause aligns with the past perspective of the reporting context.
Aspectual forms follow a parallel logic in backshifting. Progressive aspects shift accordingly, such as present continuous to past continuous ("I am eating" becomes "She said she was eating") or past continuous to past perfect continuous ("I was eating" becomes "She said she had been eating"). Similarly, perfect aspects adjust, with present perfect moving to past perfect ("I have eaten" becomes "She said she had eaten") and past perfect remaining unchanged ("I had eaten" stays "She said she had eaten"). These shifts preserve the ongoing or completed nature of the action relative to the reporting time.
Future tenses and modal verbs also undergo backshifting when the reporting verb is in the past. For example, "I will go" becomes "She said she would go," and "I can help" shifts to "She said she could help." Other modals like may to might and shall to should follow similar patterns, while must typically remains must or changes to had to depending on context. These changes align the modality and futurity with the reporting timeframe.
Exceptions to backshifting occur when the reported content expresses a general truth or universal fact, in which case the original tense is retained regardless of the reporting verb's tense ("The Earth orbits the sun" reported as "Scientists said the Earth orbits the sun"). No backshift applies if the reporting verb is in the present or future tense, as the temporal frame remains current ("He says he is tired" or "He will say he is tired"). Additionally, with reporting verbs in the present perfect like "has said," backshifting is typically avoided, preserving the original tenses to reflect ongoing relevance.

Pronoun, Possessive, and Demonstrative Adjustments

In indirect speech, pronouns undergo systematic adjustments to align the reported content with the reporter's perspective, replacing the original speaker's deictic references with those appropriate to the reporting context. The first-person singular pronoun "I" typically shifts to a third-person form such as "he," "she," or "they," depending on the gender and number of the original speaker as understood by the reporter; for instance, direct speech "I am tired" becomes "She said that she was tired" when reporting a female speaker's statement. Similarly, first-person plural "we" changes to "they" or "we" if the reporter includes themselves in the group, as in "We will go" reported as "They said that they would go." Second-person "you" adjusts based on the addressee's relation to the reporter, often becoming "I" or "me" if the reporter was addressed, or "he/she/they" otherwise; for example, "You should leave" might be reported as "He told me that I should leave."
Possessive pronouns and adjectives follow analogous patterns to maintain referential consistency from the reporter's viewpoint. The possessive "my" shifts to "his," "her," or "their" to match the third-person reference for the original speaker, as in "This is my book" becoming "He said that it was his book." Likewise, "your" changes to "my," "his/her," or "their" depending on the context; if addressing the reporter, it becomes "my," such as "Your idea is good" reported as "She said that my idea was good," whereas for a third party, it shifts to "his/her." These transformations ensure that possession aligns with the shifted personal pronouns, avoiding confusion in the reported narrative.
Demonstrative pronouns and determiners also require adjustment to reflect spatial or contextual distance from the reporter's standpoint, converting proximal forms to distal ones. The near demonstrative "this" typically becomes "that," and plural "these" shifts to "those," as in direct speech "This is important" reported as "He said that that was important," emphasizing the removal from the original utterance's immediacy. Such changes apply to noun phrases as well, like "this book" to "that book," preserving the intended reference without implying current proximity. These adjustments are context-dependent, varying with the reporter's identification with the original speaker or situation, and are often integrated with tense backshifting for complete transformation.
The rules for these shifts, emphasizing perspective realignment, were systematically formalized in influential 20th-century English grammars, such as Quirk et al.'s A Comprehensive Grammar of the English Language (1985), which details their application across various reporting scenarios. Similarly, Huddleston and Pullum's The Cambridge Grammar of the English Language (2002) underscores the deictic nature of these changes, noting their role in adapting reported clauses to the matrix clause's viewpoint.

Time, Place, and Manner Expression Changes

In indirect speech, temporal deictic expressions undergo systematic adjustments to anchor the reported content to the reporter's spatiotemporal frame, rather than the original speaker's. Common transformations include changing "now" to "then," "today" to "that day," "tomorrow" to "the next day," and "yesterday" to "the day before." These shifts maintain referential accuracy by relativizing the time of the reported event to the moment of reporting, preventing temporal disorientation for the audience.
Spatial deictic expressions similarly adapt to reflect any displacement between the original utterance's location and the reporting context. For example, "here" becomes "there," and "this place" shifts to "that place," ensuring the reported location is interpreted relative to the reporter's position. This adjustment is particularly crucial when the reporter is not in the same spatial setting as the original event, as it avoids ambiguity in locative references.
Manner deictics, which indicate how an action is performed, also realign to fit the new deictic center. Expressions like "thus" or "in this way" are typically rendered as "so" or "in that way," preserving the original mode of action while subordinating it to the reporter's viewpoint. These changes extend to other proximal-distal shifts, such as demonstrative adverbs, to ensure cohesive integration of the reported content.
The rationale for these transformations lies in avoiding deictic mismatch, where expressions anchored to the wrong perspective could lead to pragmatic confusion or misinterpretation. This principle, central to deictic theory in pragmatics, was elaborated in the 1970s and 1980s by linguists including Stephen Levinson, who emphasized how such realignments coordinate with pronoun adjustments to achieve a complete shift in the deictic center.

Ambiguities and Linguistic Challenges

Sequence of Tenses and Backshifting Issues

One key challenge in applying the sequence of tenses to indirect speech is over-backshifting, where the tense shift from the original utterance implies a completed or irrelevant action, even when the reported content retains current relevance. For instance, the direct statement "I am tired" becomes "He said he was tired" in indirect speech, suggesting the tiredness was a past condition rather than an ongoing one, which can distort the speaker's intended meaning. This issue arises because backshifting aligns the reported clause with the past orientation of the reporting verb, but it fails to preserve the "intensionally absolute" temporal reference of the original, absolute tenses in direct speech.
The universality of strict backshifting is subject to debate across languages and contexts, with English exhibiting variability where backshifting is optional for statements of timeless truth or ongoing validity, allowing the present tense to be retained to avoid semantic loss. In contrast, languages like French generally require strict backshifting even for general propositions, exhibiting less flexibility than English in maintaining the original tense for timeless truths, though the reporting verb in the present tense avoids any shift. This linguistic variation highlights how rigid application of backshifting rules, as outlined in basic grammatical transformations, does not universally capture nuanced temporal interpretations.
Stylistic challenges further complicate backshifting in literature, where avoiding it preserves the vividness and immediacy of the reported voice, a preference critiqued in 18th-century grammar debates that emphasized clarity over historical tense alignment. Figures like Samuel Johnson, in his grammatical writings, contributed to these discussions by advocating for precise tense usage to reflect natural speech patterns, influencing later views on when backshifting might obscure rather than clarify meaning. Such historical critiques underscore the tension between prescriptive rules and expressive needs in narrative contexts.
To resolve these issues, speakers and writers often employ present-tense reporting verbs (e.g., "says" instead of "said") or adverbials (e.g., "still" or "now") to signal ongoing relevance without full backshifting. Corpus linguistics data reveal significant variability in natural usage, with backshifting obeyed in only about 60-70% of cases in spoken and written English, particularly less frequently for universal truths or recent reports, indicating a pragmatic preference for meaning preservation over strict rules.

Cross-Linguistic Variations in Interpretation

Indirect speech exhibits significant cross-linguistic variations, particularly in non-Indo-European languages, where conventions often diverge from the tense backshifting and pronoun adjustments typical in Indo-European systems, leading to distinct interpretive challenges. In Japanese, a high-context language, indirect speech frequently omits explicit reporting verbs such as "say" or "tell," relying instead on particles like (to) or (tte) to embed the reported content, which can result in ambiguity regarding speaker attribution as the source of the information must be inferred from surrounding discourse or situational cues. This omission-based strategy contrasts with more explicit marking in languages like English and underscores how contextual dependence in East Asian languages can complicate the clear demarcation of reported utterances.
In languages with grammatical evidentiality, such as Turkish, indirect speech integrates markers of information source directly into the verb morphology, altering interpretations of reliability and epistemic commitment. The suffix -mI, for instance, signals hearsay or indirect evidence in reported speech constructions, conveying that the speaker has not personally witnessed the event but learned of it secondhand, which inherently qualifies the utterance's trustworthiness compared to direct evidential forms like -DI. This evidential encoding in Turkic languages thus embeds layers of modality into indirect reports, influencing how hearers assess the veracity of the conveyed information beyond mere propositional content.
African languages like Ewe employ logophoric pronouns to resolve potential ambiguities in indirect speech by distinguishing the perspective of the reported speaker from other referents, a mechanism absent in most European languages. The pronoun y, for example, is obligatorily used in embedded clauses of speech or thought reports to corefer with the matrix subject (the original speaker), thereby avoiding pronoun resolution confusion that arises in non-logophoric systems where standard pronouns might ambiguously refer to either the reporter or the reported individual. This specialized pronominal system facilitates precise attribution in complex narratives, highlighting a typological strategy for maintaining clarity in perspective-shifting contexts.
Modern typological studies since 2000 have illuminated how these variations pose challenges in translation and cross-linguistic comprehension, as indirect speech strategies like contextual omission, evidential marking, and logophoricity lack direct equivalents in dominant translation models oriented toward Indo-European patterns. For instance, surveys of coding strategies reveal that while some languages morphologically distinguish direct from indirect reports, others blend them through pragmatic inference or specialized morphology, complicating the transfer of nuanced speaker attitudes and evidential nuances in multilingual contexts. These insights from typological perspectives emphasize the need for source-sensitive approaches in interpreting and translating reported speech to preserve interpretive fidelity across language families.

Examples Across Languages

English

In English, indirect speech, also known as reported speech, transforms direct quotations into subordinate clauses that integrate the reported content into the reporting narrative, adhering to rules such as tense backshifting and pronoun adjustments as described in grammatical transformations. A simple declarative sentence provides a clear illustration: the direct statement "I am happy," spoken by a person, becomes "She said that she was happy" in indirect form, where the present tense "am" shifts to past "was," the first-person pronoun "I" changes to third-person "she," and there is no alteration to adverbs since none are present in this example.
For questions, indirect speech eliminates the interrogative structure, adopting declarative word order while incorporating interrogative words if applicable. Consider the direct question "Where are you going?": in indirect speech, it reports as "He asked where I was going," removing the subject-verb inversion ("are you") to a statement form ("I was"), with tense backshift from present to past and pronoun adjustment from second-person "you" to first-person "I."
Commands in indirect speech employ infinitive constructions to convey directives without the imperative mood. The direct command "Close the door!" transforms into "She told me to close the door," where the reporting verb "told" pairs with the base form "close" preceded by "to," and the pronoun shifts from implied second-person to explicit "me" based on context.
A literary variant, free indirect speech, merges the narrator's voice with a character's thoughts without explicit reporting verbs or quotation marks, creating an intimate blend as exemplified in Jane Austen's novels, such as the use of free indirect speech in Northanger Abbey to convey Catherine Morland's gothic anticipations at the abbey.

Classical Languages (Greek and Latin)

In ancient Greek, indirect speech primarily employs the accusative and infinitive construction for reported statements, where the subject of the original direct speech appears in the accusative case and the verb is rendered as an infinitive, often reflecting tense adjustments relative to the main verb. For instance, the direct statement "" (I am coming) might be transformed into indirect speech as " " (he said that he was coming), preserving the sense while embedding it within the reporter's clause. Alternatively, indirect statements can use clauses with indicative or optative moods to convey reported content, particularly in narrative contexts. Indirect questions in Greek typically feature the subjunctive mood to indicate deliberation or uncertainty, as in constructions following verbs of asking or wondering, distinguishing them from declarative infinitives.
In Latin, known as oratio obliqua or indirect discourse, reported speech favors the accusative and infinitive for statements, integrating the subject's accusative form with an infinitive verb that aligns aspect but may shift tense based on the governing verb's sequence. A direct statement like "Ego felum video" (I see the cat) becomes "Dixit se felum videre" (He said that he saw the cat), where "se" is the reflexive accusative and "videre" is the present infinitive, often used even for past actions to maintain vivacity. For indirect commands or purposes, ut clauses with the subjunctive are employed, while quod introduces certain reported statements, especially after verbs of perception or declaration, allowing indicative if the content is presented as factual. Mood shifts occur in subordinate clauses within indirect discourse, with subjunctives signaling the reporter's perspective on contingency or hearsay.
These classical models profoundly influenced English grammatical conventions during the Renaissance through the extensive teaching of Latin syntax in schools. A distinctive aspect of Latin oratio obliqua lies in its seamless integration into historiography, as exemplified in Tacitus's Annals, where extended indirect discourses convey speeches and deliberations without breaking narrative flow, enhancing conciseness and authorial control.

Modern Indo-European Languages (Russian, German, Persian)

In Russian, indirect speech typically involves no tense backshifting, preserving the original verb tense from direct speech to maintain the action's aspect and relevance, which aligns with the language's aspectual focus rather than strict temporal sequence. For instance, the direct statement " " (I am going) becomes " , " (He said that he is going) in indirect form, retaining the present tense for an ongoing action. This approach contrasts with languages requiring backshifting, as Russian subordinate clauses introduced by conjunctions like "" (that) integrate the reported content without altering its temporal frame.
German indirect speech employs the subjunctive mood, primarily Konjunktiv I in formal contexts, to mark reported content as non-factual or distant, with optional tense adjustments but no mandatory backshifting like in English; instead, the subjunctive forms preserve the original tense's nuance while adapting to subordinate word order. A common example is the direct "Ich komme" (I am coming) reported as "Er sagte, er kme" (He said he was coming), using Konjunktiv II when Konjunktiv I coincides with the indicative. This modal shift, rather than tense change, distinguishes reported speech from narration, ensuring clarity in embedded clauses. In addition, indirect speech is commonly expressed using subordinate clauses introduced by "dass" (that), often with the indicative mood and no mandatory tense backshifting, particularly in language learning exercises that emphasize the characteristic verb-final word order of German subordinate clauses. Typical examples from such exercises include: direct "Er lernt Deutsch" becomes "Er sagte, dass er Deutsch lernt"; direct "Sie braucht unsere Hilfe" becomes "Sie schreibt, dass sie unsere Hilfe braucht"; and direct "Sie fingen einen groen Fisch am Samstag" becomes "Sie erzhlten uns, dass sie einen groen Fisch am Samstag fingen". These illustrate the preservation of original tense and the placement of the finite verb at the clause's end, complementing the subjunctive constructions in demonstrating flexible reporting strategies in German.
In Persian, indirect speech generally avoids obligatory tense backshifting, keeping the reported verb's tense intact to emphasize aspect over strict chronology, often using the complementizer "ke" (that) for integration; ezfe constructions may link possessive or descriptive elements within the clause but are not central to the reporting mechanism. For example, the direct " " (I am going) is rendered indirectly as " " (He said that he goes), maintaining the present for ongoing relevance without shift. This preservation reflects Persian's flexible tense system in subordinates, prioritizing contextual timelessness.
These languages illustrate Indo-European divergences from English's rigid backshifting, rooted in shared proto-features like aspectual prominence but evolved through branch-specific syntaxRussian and Persian favoring tense retention for vividness, while German uses subjunctive distancingthus filling gaps in cross-linguistic explanations of deictic adaptation.

Indirect Speech in Competitive Examinations

Direct and indirect speech, also known as narration, constitutes a key topic in the English Language & Comprehension section of the SSC Stenographer Grade 'C' & 'D' examination conducted by the Staff Selection Commission (SSC) in India. In the 2024 and 2025 examination cycles, this topic typically featured several questions (often 4-8) requiring conversion of sentences between direct and indirect forms.
Key rules tested include backshifting of tenses (e.g., present perfect to past perfect), changing pronouns (e.g., "my" to "his"), adjusting time/place words (e.g., "tomorrow" to "the next day"), removing quotes, and adding "that" or "if/whether" as needed.
The following examples illustrate common question types:

The manager said, My secretary has resigned. The manager said that his secretary had resigned. (Tense backshift and pronoun change.)

Ramesh said to Suresh, Are you solving this question? Ramesh asked Suresh whether he was solving that question. (Interrogative conversion with "whether" and tense adjustment.)

The teacher said to the boys, Do not talk in the class. The teacher ordered the boys not to talk in the class. (Imperative to indirect with "ordered".)

Rehan said to Mallika, "The Somnath Temple is in Prabhas Patan in Gujarat." Rehan told Mallika that the Somnath Temple was in Prabhas Patan in Gujarat. (Universal truth but tense backshift applied in exam context.)

In examination contexts, tense backshift may be applied strictly even to statements of universal truths to assess mastery of the rules, though standard linguistic usage often retains original tenses for timeless propositions.
