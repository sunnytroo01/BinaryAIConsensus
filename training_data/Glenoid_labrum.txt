Glenoid labrum
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The glenoid labrum is a fibrocartilaginous structure that forms a ring-like rim around the glenoid cavity of the scapula, deepening the shallow socket of the glenohumeral joint to accommodate the humeral head and enhance overall shoulder stability.
Anatomically, the glenoid labrum attaches directly to the edge of the articular cartilage of the glenoid fossa, with a typical thickness of about 3 mm and width of 4 mm, though these dimensions vary regionally. Its shape is often triangular superiorly and more rounded inferiorly, composed of three layered zones of fibrous tissue: a superficial multidirectional mesh, a loosely packed middle layer, and a dense circumferential core. The labrum develops embryologically by the eighth week, integrating with the joint capsule and biceps tendon by the ninth week, and it receives blood supply primarily from the ascending glenoid artery and branches of the suprascapular and circumflex scapular arteries.
Functionally, the glenoid labrum increases the glenoid's depth by approximately 50% and expands its surface area, serving as a static stabilizer that resists humeral head translation and maintains intra-articular pressure to prevent dislocations. It also acts as a key attachment site for the glenohumeral ligaments and the long head of the biceps tendon, contributing about 10% to the joint's concavity-compression stability while centralizing the humeral head during motion. Damage to the labrum, such as tears, can significantly impair shoulder function, with resection studies showing up to a 20% reduction in inferior stability.

Anatomy

Gross Anatomy

The glenoid labrum is a fibrocartilaginous ring that encircles the glenoid cavity of the scapula, deepening its concavity and increasing the effective depth of the glenohumeral joint by approximately 50% to enhance articular congruity with the humeral head. This structure surrounds the periphery of the shallow glenoid fossa, which otherwise covers only about 25-30% of the humeral head surface during motion, thereby contributing to joint stability through improved depth and surface area.
In cross-section, the glenoid labrum typically exhibits a triangular or wedge-shaped morphology, with its base firmly attached to the bony rim of the glenoid and its apex forming a free inner edge that articulates directly with the humeral head. The labrum anchors to the glenoid via a transitional zone that blends with the adjacent articular cartilage and bone, providing a secure interface. Superiorly, it integrates with the superior glenohumeral ligament and the origin of the long head of the biceps tendon, while inferiorly, it fuses with the anterior and posterior bands of the inferior glenohumeral ligament (IGHL), forming a reinforced capsulolabral complex.
The glenoid labrum is conventionally divided into superior and inferior zones for anatomical description, with the superior zone further subdivided into anterior and posterior parts, and the inferior zone encompassing the anterior Bankart region and posterior regions, often referenced using a clock-face orientation (e.g., 12 o'clock superiorly, 6 o'clock inferiorly). Normal anatomical variations include the sublabral foramen, a partial detachment in the anterosuperior labrum occurring in approximately 10-20% of cases, and the Buford complex, characterized by absence of the anterosuperior labrum (1.5-6.5% prevalence) accompanied by a thickened, cord-like middle glenohumeral ligament.
Dimensionally, the labrum demonstrates regional variability, with an average radial thickness of 3-4 mm and height ranging from approximately 3.5 mm superiorly to 7 mm inferiorly, though the superior portion can reach heights of up to 6 mm and thicknesses of 6 mm in some individuals. These measurements reflect its adaptive role in accommodating the joint's range of motion while maintaining structural integrity.

Microscopic Anatomy

The glenoid labrum is a fibrocartilaginous structure whose microscopic composition consists primarily of fibrocartilage, averaging approximately 28% of its cross-sectional area across various regions, with regional variations reaching up to one-third. This fibrocartilage is characterized by a mixture of type I and type II collagen fibers synthesized by fibrochondrocytes, embedded within an extracellular matrix that includes proteoglycans for resilience and tensile strength. At the glenoid rim attachment, the labrum transitions seamlessly to hyaline cartilage via a fibrocartilaginous transitional zone featuring Sharpey's fibers and interdigitating bone processes, while at its blend with the glenohumeral capsule, it incorporates denser fibrous connective tissue with predominantly parallel-oriented collagen bundles.
Histologically, the labrum displays a zonal organization observable through scanning electron microscopy and confocal imaging. The superficial zone, approximately 510 m deep, forms a mesh-like network of fine collagen fibrils interfacing with the articular surface. This is succeeded by a central core of densely braided, circumferential collagen fibers that provide structural integrity, interwoven with chondrocytes in a fibrocartilaginous matrix. The peripheral or peri-core zone consists of loosely packed fibers and higher densities of fibroblasts with elongated processes, facilitating tissue remodeling. The cellular components include fibroblasts, chondrocytes, and transitional fibrochondrocytes, with overall low cellularityparticularly in deeper, avascular regionsto support its load-bearing role without excessive metabolic demand.
Age-related histological alterations in the glenoid labrum become prominent after approximately age 30, manifesting as progressive degeneration including fibrillation, rupture of collagen fibers, and accumulation of mucoid or eosinophilic deposits, especially in high-stress areas like the anterosuperior and superior regions. Calcification and ossification increase with age, observed in up to 21% of older specimens, contributing to tissue stiffening. Concurrently, proteoglycan content diminishes, reducing the matrix's hydration and shock-absorbing capacity, alongside qualitative increases in fibrovascular tissue and cystic changes. These modifications correlate with overall shoulder joint degeneration and are statistically significant across age groups (P < 0.01).
In comparison to the knee meniscus, the glenoid labrum exhibits analogous fibrocartilaginous histology with interwoven collagen types I and II and embedded chondrocytes, but its architecture is specifically adapted to deepen the shallow glenoid fossa and accommodate the greater incongruity of the glenohumeral joint, prioritizing depth enhancement over the meniscus's primary function of compressive load distribution in the more stable tibiofemoral articulation.

Vascular and Neural Supply

The vascular supply to the glenoid labrum is derived primarily from branches of the suprascapular artery, anterior humeral circumflex artery, and posterior humeral circumflex artery, which contribute to a peripheral capsular plexus around the glenohumeral joint. These vessels form an anastomotic network that penetrates the labrum from its outer periphery, with blood supply limited to the outer 1-2 mm of the structure, creating a gradient of vascularity that diminishes toward the inner aspects. The inferior portions of the labrum exhibit greater vascular density compared to the superior regions, where perfusion is notably sparser.
The inner third of the glenoid labrum is largely avascular, lacking direct arterial penetration and instead relying on nutrient diffusion from synovial fluid for metabolic support. This zonal variation in vascularity has significant clinical implications for healing, as the poor perfusion in central and superior tears limits intrinsic repair capacity, contributing to higher failure rates in surgical interventions for these lesions. For instance, the superior labrum's low vascular area (approximately 43%) correlates with reduced progenitor cell density and impaired regenerative potential.
Innervation of the glenoid labrum arises from sensory branches of the suprascapular nerve, which supplies the superior and posterior aspects; the nerve to the subscapularis (including lower subscapular branches), innervating the anterior region; and the axillary nerve, providing coverage to the inferior portions. These nerves contain free sensory fibers and mechanoreceptors, such as Ruffini and Pacinian endings, that facilitate proprioceptive feedback to the glenohumeral joint by detecting joint position and motion. Free nerve endings are distributed throughout the labrum, with higher densities near ligamentous insertions, supporting sensory roles in joint stability awareness.
Developmentally, the vascular patterns of the glenoid labrum are established during embryogenesis, coinciding with joint capsule formation around 8-13 weeks of gestation, when the labrum emerges as a mesenchymal condensation with sparse initial vascularity. In fetal stages, the labrum displays uniform but limited vascularization without distinct zonal patterns, which modulates into fibrocartilage postnatally while vascular density progressively decreases with age.

Function

Biomechanical Role

The glenoid labrum significantly enhances the biomechanical function of the glenohumeral joint by deepening the glenoid fossa and expanding its articular surface area, thereby improving humeral head containment during motion. Specifically, the labrum increases glenoid concavity by 21-50%, transforming the shallow bony socket into a more effective socket for the larger humeral head. This structural modification also expands the effective articular surface area from approximately 2-4 cm without the labrum to 6-12 cm with it intact, with vertical extension up to 75% and horizontal up to 57%, as measured in anatomical studies. These changes promote better load-bearing capacity and reduce the risk of humeral head subluxation under dynamic loads.
In terms of load distribution, the labrum plays a crucial role in absorbing and dissipating compressive forces across the joint, particularly during overhead activities where shoulder loads can reach 550-770 N in late cocking phases, such as in throwing motions. By deepening the concavity, it helps distribute these forces more evenly, reducing peak pressures on the glenoid cartilage by up to 12% compared to scenarios with labral deficiency, as demonstrated in biomechanical testing. This mechanism minimizes cartilage wear and supports sustained joint integrity under repetitive high-load conditions.
The labrum further contributes to force transmission by functioning as a "chock block," resisting humeral head translation during arm elevation and accounting for 10-20% of the overall joint constraint through concavity-compression effects. In cadaveric analyses under simulated anterior loading at 90 abduction, the labrum provides a significant portion of the resistance to anterior instability, highlighting its pivotal role in directional stability. Kinematically, it facilitates smoother glenohumeral translation and rotation, especially in abduction and external rotation, by centralizing the humeral head and limiting excessive motion to within 0.74 mm of deviation in intact states. These effects collectively enable the shoulder's wide range of motion while maintaining mechanical efficiency.

Contribution to Joint Stability

The glenoid labrum plays a critical role in maintaining glenohumeral joint stability by sealing the articular surfaces and generating negative intra-articular pressure, which creates a vacuum or suction cup effect that resists humeral head subluxation and translation. In intact shoulders, this pressure averages -32 mmHg in neutral position and can drop to -133 mmHg under traction, significantly enhancing joint congruency and contributing approximately 30-50% to overall stability through this mechanism. Labral disruption eliminates this suction, leading to increased joint laxity, as demonstrated in cadaveric models where anteroinferior or posterosuperior tears reduced the suction effect in over 85% of tested directions.
Through ligamentous integration, the labrum anchors key structures to bolster static stability. Superiorly, it attaches the long head of the biceps tendon, preventing superior humeral migration, while inferiorly, it integrates with the inferior glenohumeral ligament (IGHL) to form the anteroinferior sling that centralizes the humeral head during abduction and external rotation. This sling mechanism resists anterior dislocation by distributing forces across the anterior capsule and labrum.
The labrum also supports dynamic stability via proprioceptive feedback from embedded mechanoreceptors, including Ruffini-like type I and Pacinian corpuscle type II endings, which detect joint position and motion to trigger reflexive muscle activation by the rotator cuff and scapular stabilizers. These sensory elements contribute to neuromuscular control during overhead activities, enhancing joint protection against instability.
Directionally, the superior labrum resists superior humeral head migration under load, while the anteroinferior portion counters anterior subluxation in the apprehension position (abduction-external rotation). In older adults or athletes with repetitive overhead demands, labral degenerationcharacterized by reduced vascularity and fibrocartilaginous thinningdiminishes these stabilizing contributions, increasing susceptibility to microinstability and tears.

Pathology

Types of Labral Injuries

Labral injuries to the glenoid encompass a range of pathologies that disrupt the normal attachment and integrity of the fibrocartilaginous labrum, often leading to shoulder instability. These injuries are broadly classified by their anatomical location and mechanism, with traumatic etiologies predominating in younger, active individuals and degenerative changes more common in older patients. Traumatic mechanisms typically involve anterior or posterior shoulder dislocations, forceful overhead motions in sports like throwing, or compressive forces from falls, while degenerative tears arise from chronic attrition and repetitive microtrauma. Studies indicate that labral tears are prevalent in shoulder arthroscopies, showing posterior involvement in approximately 74% of identified tears.
The Bankart lesion represents a classic anteroinferior labral injury, characterized by detachment of the labrum from the glenoid rim in the 3- to 6-o'clock position (right shoulder), often accompanied by capsuloligamentous disruption. This injury typically results from an anterior shoulder dislocation, where the humeral head impacts and avulses the labrum during abduction and external rotation. Subtypes include the soft tissue Bankart, involving pure labral and capsular tearing without bony involvement, and the bony Bankart, which features an associated glenoid rim fracture. Bankart lesions occur in 87-100% of initial anterior dislocations.
Superior labrum anterior to posterior (SLAP) tears involve the superior glenoid labrum extending from the anterior to posterior aspects, frequently disrupting the long head of the biceps tendon anchor. These are common in overhead athletes, such as throwers, due to repetitive tensile forces or acute traction during eccentric loading. The original Snyder classification includes four types, which have been expanded by others (e.g., Maffet) to include additional variants based on arthroscopic findings:

Type I: Degenerative fraying of the superior labrum edge with an intact biceps anchor.

Type II: Detachment of the superior labrum and biceps anchor from the glenoid, the most prevalent subtype (41% of SLAP cases), often traumatic.

Type III: Bucket-handle tear of the labrum with intact biceps attachment.

Type IV: Bucket-handle extension into the biceps tendon.

Type V: Anterior extension resembling a Bankart lesion combined with Type II detachment.

Type VI: Flap tear of the labrum with Type II instability.

Type VII: Extension of the detachment inferior to the middle glenohumeral ligament.

Type II lesions predominate in throwing sports, identified in about 4% of shoulder arthroscopies among athletes.
Posterior labral tears, often termed reverse Bankart lesions, involve detachment of the posteroinferior labrum (7- to 10-o'clock position) along with the posterior scapular periosteum and inferior glenohumeral ligament. These arise from posterior-directed forces, such as electric shocks, seizures, or falls onto an outstretched hand with the shoulder in flexion and adduction, leading to humeral head impaction against the posterior glenoid. Isolated posterior injuries account for 26.9% of all labral pathologies in systematic reviews.
The anterior labroligamentous periosteal sleeve avulsion (ALPSA) lesion is a variant of anteroinferior injury where the labrum avulses but remains attached via an intact periosteal sleeve, allowing medial displacement and rolling of the labrum without complete detachment. This occurs in recurrent anterior instability, progressing from initial dislocations where the periosteum strips but does not rupture, leading to scarred, nonanatomic healing. ALPSA lesions are noted in 7.9-39% of first-time dislocations and up to 31% of recurrent cases, increasing recurrence risk post-repair.
Kim lesions describe concealed, superficial tears at the junction of the posteroinferior labrum and glenoid articular cartilage, without full labral detachment, often contributing to subtle posterior instability. These incomplete avulsions result from chronic posterior loading or repetitive microtrauma, appearing as marginal cracks under arthroscopy and potentially progressing to more extensive tears if untreated. They are frequently associated with posterior glenohumeral instability in overhead athletes or contact sports participants.

Associated Pathological Conditions

Glenohumeral instability is a key pathological condition associated with glenoid labrum pathology, where labral deficiency significantly contributes to recurrent anterior shoulder dislocations by reducing the glenoid's depth and stability. Glenoid chondrolabral defects, often resulting from labral tears, have been shown to increase the risk of instability recurrence following surgical interventions, as they alter the joint's biomechanical constraints.
Hill-Sachs lesions, which are impression defects on the anterolateral humeral head caused by impact against the glenoid rim during dislocation, frequently accompany anterior labral injuries such as Bankart lesions. In patients with anterior shoulder dislocations, Hill-Sachs lesions are present in approximately 70% of cases, highlighting their strong correlation with labral pathology and contributing to engaging lesions that exacerbate instability.
Rotator cuff pathology often coexists with superior labral tears, particularly in overhead athletes, where superior labrum anterior to posterior (SLAP) lesions correlate with partial-thickness rotator cuff tears due to repetitive high-load stresses on the superior glenohumeral complex. Studies indicate that the presence of such partial cuff tears influences outcomes like return to play after labral repair in elite overhead throwers.
Capsular laxity can lead to multidirectional instability when combined with labral damage, as the redundant capsule fails to adequately constrain the humeral head, often seen in atraumatic or hyperlaxity cases with associated labral injuries. This combined pathology increases glenohumeral translation in multiple directions, complicating stability.
Degenerative changes, including accelerated osteoarthritis, arise from glenoid labrum pathology due to altered load distribution across the joint, with labral fraying or tears disrupting normal articular contact and promoting cartilage wear. In cases of rotator cuff arthropathy, associated intra-articular lesions such as labral tears are prevalent in about 74% of full-thickness rotator cuff tear patients, underscoring the interplay between superior labral degeneration and broader joint arthropathy.
Paralabral cysts, fluid-filled masses that form as extensions from glenoid labral tears, can compress the suprascapular nerve, leading to neuropathy and isolated infraspinatus or supraspinatus weakness. These cysts typically arise from superior or posterior labral defects and may present with shoulder pain or atrophy without overt instability.

Diagnosis

Clinical Assessment

The clinical assessment of glenoid labrum pathology begins with a detailed patient history to identify potential etiologies and symptom patterns. Traumatic mechanisms, such as anterior shoulder dislocations or falls onto an outstretched arm, are common in anterior labral injuries, while overhead athletes in sports like throwing or swimming may report insidious onset related to repetitive microtrauma affecting the superior labrum. Symptoms often include deep shoulder pain exacerbated by overhead activities, mechanical sensations such as catching or popping, and night pain that disrupts sleep, particularly in cases of superior or posterior lesions. For posterior labral tears, symptoms may manifest as position-specific inconsistent pain, such as catching; pain when the elbows move behind the torso in extension or horizontal abduction; and discomfort in deep bench positions due to compressive loads, while overhead activities are often painless as they do not load the damaged area similarly; these injuries are common in activities involving dipping or calisthenics. Non-traumatic presentations can involve gradual pain without clear inciting events, emphasizing the need to correlate history with occupational or athletic demands.
Physical examination starts with inspection for asymmetry, muscle atrophy, or scapular dyskinesia, followed by evaluation of active and passive range of motion (ROM) and strength to establish baselines and identify compensatory patterns. Reduced external rotation or forward flexion may suggest anterior involvement, while posterior capsular tightness can indicate posterior pathology. Provocative maneuvers are then employed to elicit instability or pain indicative of labral disruption. For anterior lesions, the apprehension testpositioning the arm in 90 abduction and full external rotation while applying anterior stressprovokes pain or a sense of instability with sensitivity of 40-62% and specificity of 42-87%; the relocation test, applying posterior pressure to relieve apprehension, confirms findings with sensitivity of 64.6% and specificity of 90.2%. The load and shift test grades glenohumeral translation by applying anterior or posterior loads in varying positions, aiding instability assessment when combined with history.
Superior labral anterior-posterior (SLAP) lesions are targeted with the O'Brien active compression test, where the arm is flexed to 90 and adducted 10; resisted forward elevation with the thumb down elicits pain that decreases with supination, yielding sensitivity of 47-78% and specificity of 11-55%. The crank test, involving axial compression and rotation at 160 abduction, detects grinding or pain from labral pathology with variable sensitivity of 13-81% and specificity of 67-88%, though its standalone utility is limited. For posterior or posteroinferior lesions, the Kim testapplying axial load in 90 abduction with posterior and inferior stressreproduces posterior pain with high sensitivity (80%) and specificity (94%). Overall, no single test is diagnostic; clustering maneuvers improves accuracy, with combinations like anterior slide and crank achieving 34% sensitivity and 91% specificity for labral tears.
Differential diagnosis requires targeted exams to exclude mimicking conditions, such as rotator cuff tears (assessed via empty can or lift-off tests for supraspinatus or subscapularis weakness) or acromioclavicular (AC) joint pathology (provoked by cross-body adduction). Other considerations include impingement syndromes or biceps tendinopathy, ruled out through maneuvers like Neer's or Yergason's tests. Baseline functional assessment often employs validated scoring systems, such as the Constant-Murley score (evaluating pain, activities of daily living, strength, and ROM for a total out of 100) or the American Shoulder and Elbow Surgeons (ASES) score (50% pain and 50% function, ranging 0-100), to quantify impairment and guide further evaluation.

Imaging and Arthroscopic Evaluation

Radiography serves as the initial imaging modality for evaluating suspected glenoid labral injuries, particularly to identify associated bony abnormalities such as the bony Bankart lesion, which involves a fracture of the anteroinferior glenoid rim, or the Hill-Sachs lesion, a posterolateral humeral head impaction fracture. Standard anteroposterior and axillary views are routinely obtained, but specialized projections like the West Point view provide a tangential assessment of the anteroinferior glenoid rim to better detect rim fractures or bone loss.
Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), especially MR arthrography with intra-articular contrast, is the preferred noninvasive method for detecting soft tissue pathology in the glenoid labrum, offering high sensitivity for identifying tears by highlighting contrast extravasation into labral defects. For superior labrum anterior-posterior (SLAP) tears, MR arthrography demonstrates sensitivity of approximately 80%, outperforming conventional MRI (sensitivity ~63%) due to improved delineation of labral attachments. T2-weighted sequences are particularly useful, as they reveal high-signal fluid within torn labral tissue, aiding in the differentiation of acute injuries from degenerative changes.
Computed tomography (CT) is indicated when bony involvement is suspected, providing detailed evaluation of glenoid bone loss through multiplanar reformations and 3D surface reconstructions that quantify defect size relative to the intact glenoid. A glenoid bone loss exceeding 20% of the total surface area is associated with increased risk of recurrent instability, often necessitating surgical planning beyond soft tissue repair alone.
Ultrasound has a limited role in glenoid labrum assessment but can identify paralabral cysts, which may communicate with underlying labral tears and cause compressive neuropathy. It also enables dynamic evaluation during shoulder motion to assess labral stability or subluxation, though its operator dependence restricts widespread use for primary diagnosis.
Arthroscopy remains the gold standard for definitive diagnosis of glenoid labral pathology, allowing direct visualization of the entire labrum under intra-articular illumination and precise classification of tear types using standardized systems like the Snyder or Maffet classifications. During the procedure, diagnostic probes are employed to test tear mobility, detachment extent, and involvement of the biceps-labral complex, providing intraoperative confirmation that guides therapeutic decisions.
Emerging techniques include 3T MRI, which enhances soft tissue resolution through higher signal-to-noise ratios, improving detection of subtle labral irregularities compared to 1.5T systems. Post-2020 advancements in AI-assisted imaging, such as deep learning algorithms applied to abbreviated MRI protocols, have shown promise in automating labral tear detection with accuracies rivaling radiologists, potentially streamlining diagnostic workflows.

Treatment

Conservative Approaches

Conservative management of glenoid labrum injuries, particularly stable superior labrum anterior to posterior (SLAP) lesions, focuses on symptom relief, functional restoration, and prevention of progression without surgical intervention. This approach is typically indicated for Type I SLAP tears, which involve fraying without detachment, or degenerative changes in low-demand patients, such as older individuals or those not engaged in overhead activities. Success rates for avoiding surgery range from 40% to 70%, with higher outcomes in non-athletic populations who complete a structured rehabilitation program. Emerging biologic therapies, such as platelet-rich plasma (PRP) injections, are under investigation for enhancing healing, though evidence for efficacy remains limited as of 2025.
Rest and activity modification form the foundation of initial treatment, emphasizing avoidance of provocative maneuvers like overhead reaching or throwing to allow soft tissue healing. For stable tears, patients are advised to limit such activities for 4 to 6 weeks, gradually reintroducing low-impact motions as pain subsides. This strategy reduces inflammation and protects the labrum from further stress, promoting natural stabilization through surrounding musculature.
Pharmacotherapy plays a supportive role in controlling pain and inflammation. Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), such as ibuprofen, are commonly prescribed for their analgesic and anti-inflammatory effects during the acute phase. Intra-articular corticosteroid injections may provide short-term relief in select cases, though evidence for long-term efficacy in labral injuries remains limited, with benefits typically lasting only weeks and risks including cartilage degradation.
Physical therapy is central to conservative care, aiming to enhance dynamic shoulder stability and compensate for labral deficiency. Protocols emphasize rotator cuff strengthening exercises, such as external rotation with resistance bands, alongside scapular stabilization techniques like retraction and depression to improve periscapular mechanics. Proprioceptive training, including closed-chain exercises on unstable surfaces, further aids neuromuscular control and joint positioning. For overhead athletes, core and hip strengthening are incorporated to address kinetic chain deficits. Therapy typically spans 6 to 12 weeks, with progressive loading to restore function.
Bracing with a shoulder immobilizer or sling is recommended for acute presentations involving instability, limiting glenohumeral motion to facilitate healing and reduce subluxation risk. Immobilization duration is generally 3 to 4 weeks, followed by gradual mobilization under supervision to prevent stiffness.
Follow-up evaluation at 3 months assesses progress through clinical exams and patient-reported outcomes, such as pain scores and functional tests. Failure of conservative treatment, occurring in 30% to 60% of cases, is predicted by factors including high-demand athletic status, traumatic onset, and significant glenoid bone loss exceeding 20%, which compromises inherent joint stability. In such scenarios, transition to surgical options may be necessary.

Surgical Techniques

Surgical techniques for addressing glenoid labral pathology primarily involve arthroscopic or open approaches to repair, reconstruct, or debride the labrum, tailored to the injury type and associated defects. Arthroscopic repair is the most common method for labral tears, utilizing suture anchors to reattach the detached labrum to the glenoid rim. Recent advancements include all-suture anchors, which as of 2025 have shown comparable outcomes to traditional anchors with potentially lower rates of anchor-related complications in glenoid labral repairs. For anterior inferior labral tears, such as Bankart lesions, 2 to 4 suture anchors are typically placed anteroinferiorly to restore the capsulolabral complex and glenohumeral stability. In superior labrum anterior to posterior (SLAP) lesions, particularly Type II in older patients, biceps tenodesis may be combined with labral repair to address instability and biceps tendon pathology.
Open procedures are reserved for cases with significant bony glenoid deficiency, often exceeding 25% bone loss, where soft tissue repair alone is insufficient. The Latarjet procedure involves transferring the coracoid process with its attached conjoint tendon to the anterior glenoid to augment the bony arc and provide a sling effect for stability, effectively addressing combined labral and osseous defects. This technique is particularly indicated for recurrent anterior instability with substantial glenoid bone loss.
Debridement is employed for stable, irreparable tears or degenerative fraying, where the damaged labral tissue is resected to a stable rim using arthroscopic shavers, avoiding reattachment in low-demand or elderly patients. Partial labral resection is suitable for degenerative cases without instability, promoting pain relief while preserving joint function.
Reconstruction techniques, such as capsular shift or allograft augmentation, are used for chronic instability with irreparable labral tissue. In these procedures, allograft or autograft material reconstructs the labrum, often combined with capsulorrhaphy to tighten the capsule; for engaging Hill-Sachs lesions, remplissage fills the humeral head defect with suture-secured labral tissue to prevent engagement. Allograft reconstruction, using distal tibial or dermal scaffolds, is effective for severe glenoid defects, restoring anatomy and contact pressure.
Clinical outcomes for anterior labral repair demonstrate success rates of 85% to 95%, with high patient satisfaction and return to sport in most cases, though athletes face a 10% re-tear risk. Complications include postoperative stiffness in approximately 5% of patients and recurrent instability.
Postoperative rehabilitation typically begins with immobilization in a sling for 4 to 6 weeks to protect the repair, followed by gradual passive and active range-of-motion exercises. Strengthening and proprioceptive training progress over 3 to 4 months, with return to sport allowed at 6 months for non-contact activities, emphasizing individualized protocols based on repair extent.
