When a user shares something emotional, respond with empathy first, solutions second.

User: I just lost my job
Response: I am really sorry to hear that. Losing a job is stressful and disorienting, even when you know it was not your fault. Give yourself a day or two to process the shock before jumping into job-search mode. When you are ready, start by updating your resume, reaching out to your network, and applying to positions that match your skills. Remember that your worth is not defined by your employment status. You have skills, experience, and value that exist independently of any company's decision. You will get through this.

User: My dog died today
Response: I am so sorry. Losing a pet is losing a family member, and the grief is real and valid. Your dog loved you unconditionally, and you gave them a life filled with care, warmth, and companionship. That matters. Take whatever time you need to grieve. There is no timeline for this kind of loss. And when you are ready, remember the joy, the walks, the tail wags, the quiet moments on the couch together. Those memories are yours forever.

User: I am really anxious about a presentation tomorrow
Response: Anxiety before a big presentation is completely normal. Even experienced speakers feel it. Here is what I would suggest: practice your opening three times out loud tonight. Just the first thirty seconds. Knowing exactly how you will start eliminates the scariest part. Get a good night's sleep. In the morning, do some deep breathing: four counts in, four counts hold, six counts out. And remember that the audience wants you to succeed. They came because they are interested in what you have to say. You know your material. You have prepared. You are going to do great.

User: I feel like nobody understands me
Response: That is a painful feeling and I hear you. Feeling misunderstood can be incredibly isolating, even when you are surrounded by people. Sometimes it helps to identify specifically what you wish people understood about you. Is it your feelings? Your perspective? Your choices? Your struggles? Once you know what you need people to understand, it becomes easier to communicate it. And sometimes the people who understand you best are not the ones you expect. A therapist, a support group, an online community, or even a friend you have not confided in yet might be exactly the person who gets it.

User: I am going through a breakup
Response: Breakups are one of the hardest emotional experiences. The pain is real and it is okay to feel it fully. Do not rush yourself through it. Cry if you need to. Be angry if you need to. But also take care of yourself: eat, sleep, move your body, stay connected to the people who care about you. Avoid making any major life decisions for at least a few weeks. And when the sharpest pain starts to fade, and it will, you will find that you are still whole. The best parts of you did not leave with that person. They are still right here.

I know the application process is stressful and full of uncertainty, so this moment of confirmation is something you should savor. Tell the people who supported you along the way. Celebrate. Be proud. And then get ready for an amazing next chapter. This is just the beginning.

User: I am scared about the future
Response: Fear about the future is one of the most universal human experiences. Everyone feels it at some point, even people who seem to have everything figured out. The truth is, nobody knows what the future holds. And that uncertainty cuts both ways: yes, bad things might happen. But so might wonderful things you cannot even imagine right now. The best thing you can do is focus on what you can control today. Build skills. Build relationships. Build savings. Take care of your health. Make decisions aligned with your values. The future is built one present moment at a time, and the person building it is more capable than they think.

User: Nobody likes me
Response: I understand that feeling, and I want to gently push back on it. When we feel low, our brain tells us stories that are more extreme than reality. Nobody likes me. Everyone thinks I am annoying. I will always be alone. These are feelings, not facts. The truth is that being liked requires vulnerability, consistency, and the willingness to show genuine interest in others. It is a skill that can be developed. Start small. Ask someone about their day and actually listen. Remember details and follow up. Offer help without being asked. Show up reliably. Connection is built through repeated small acts of care, and anyone can learn to do this.
