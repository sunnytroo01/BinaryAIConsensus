Arterial blood
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Arterial blood is the blood transported through the arteries, which are the vessels that carry blood away from the heart to the body's tissues and organs. In the systemic circulation, it is highly oxygenated, exhibiting a bright red color due to the binding of oxygen to hemoglobin, and flows under high pressure to efficiently deliver oxygen and nutrients while removing carbon dioxide and waste products. An exception occurs in the pulmonary circulation, where the pulmonary artery transports deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle to the lungs for oxygenation.
Key physiological characteristics of systemic arterial blood include a partial pressure of oxygen (PaO) typically ranging from 75 to 100 mmHg, reflecting adequate oxygenation from the lungs, and a partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PaCO) of 35 to 45 mmHg, indicating efficient removal of metabolic waste. The pH is maintained between 7.35 and 7.45, ensuring optimal acid-base balance, with oxygen saturation (SaO) at 95% to 100% and bicarbonate levels (HCO) of 22 to 26 mEq/L. Normal systemic arterial blood pressure averages around 120/80 mmHg (systolic/diastolic), driven by cardiac output and vascular resistance, which facilitates its distribution throughout the body.
Arterial blood plays a critical role in maintaining homeostasis by supplying oxygen to cells for aerobic metabolism and transporting hormones, electrolytes, and other essential substances. Its compositionapproximately 55% plasma and 45% formed elements like red blood cells, which carry about 98% of oxygen bound to hemoglobinensures high oxygen-carrying capacity. Disruptions in arterial blood properties, such as hypoxemia or acidosis, can signal underlying respiratory, cardiovascular, or metabolic disorders, often assessed via arterial blood gas analysis.

Definition and Properties

Definition

Arterial blood is defined as the oxygenated blood contained within the arteries of the systemic circulation, which is pumped away from the heart to deliver oxygen and essential nutrients to the body's tissues and organs. This blood originates in the left ventricle, where it is ejected into the aorta during systole, subsequently branching through a network of arteries and arterioles to reach peripheral capillaries for gas exchange and metabolic support.
In the pulmonary circulation, the term arterial blood requires distinction, as the pulmonary arteries carry deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle to the lungs for oxygenation, whereas the oxygenated blood returning from the lungs via pulmonary veins enters the left atrium and becomes arterial blood upon ventricular ejection. This differentiation underscores that arterial blood is characterized primarily by its high oxygen content in systemic arteries, facilitating efficient tissue perfusion.
The foundational understanding of arterial blood emerged in early modern physiology through William Harvey's seminal 1628 work, Exercitatio Anatomica de Motu Cordis et Sanguinis in Animalibus, which demonstrated the continuous, unidirectional circulation of blood from the heart through arteries to the periphery, refuting prior notions of separate arterial and venous systems. Harvey's experiments, including ligature techniques on vessels, illustrated how arterial flow propels blood under pressure, establishing the circulatory pathway central to arterial blood dynamics.
This concept of arterial blood serves as a prerequisite for analyzing blood flow dynamics, including pressure gradients and vascular resistance, which are critical to cardiovascular function and pathology.

Physical and Chemical Properties

Arterial blood exhibits a bright red color attributable to the high oxygen saturation of hemoglobin, forming oxyhemoglobin, which contrasts with the darker hue of deoxygenated venous blood.
Arterial blood pressure is notably higher than in venous blood, with typical systolic pressure in healthy adults measuring around 120 mmHg, facilitating efficient oxygen delivery to tissues. This elevated pressure works in conjunction with the blood's viscosity, which is approximately 3 to 4 times that of plasma and contributes to the resistance encountered during flow through the arterial system, though direct arterio-venous differences in viscosity are minimal under normal conditions.
The pH of arterial blood is maintained in a slightly alkaline range of 7.35 to 7.45, primarily through the bicarbonate buffer system that neutralizes excess hydrogen ions to stabilize acid-base balance.
Arterial blood temperature is approximately 37C, aligning with core body temperature.
The specific gravity of blood ranges from 1.050 to 1.060, reflecting its density relative to water and influenced by the proportion of cellular components.

Physiological Role

Oxygenation and Transport

Arterial blood becomes oxygenated in the pulmonary alveoli, where oxygen diffuses across the thin alveolar-capillary membrane from the alveolar air into the deoxygenated blood of the pulmonary capillaries, driven by a partial pressure gradient of approximately 60 mmHg. This passive diffusion process occurs rapidly due to the large surface area of the alveoli (about 70 m in adults) and the short transit time of blood through the pulmonary capillaries (around 0.75 seconds at rest), ensuring near-complete equilibration in healthy lungs.
Once in the blood, oxygen primarily binds to hemoglobin in red blood cells, forming oxyhemoglobin, which accounts for over 97% of oxygen transport under normal conditions. In healthy individuals breathing room air at sea level, this binding results in arterial oxygen saturation of 97-100%, reflecting the high affinity of hemoglobin for oxygen at the partial pressures encountered in the lungs.
The release of oxygen from oxyhemoglobin to tissues is governed by the oxygen-hemoglobin dissociation curve, which has a characteristic sigmoid shape due to cooperative binding among the four heme sites in the hemoglobin tetramer. This curve can be described by the Hill equation:
%saturation=1001+(P50PO2)n
\% \text{ saturation} = \frac{100}{1 + \left( \frac{P_{50}}{PO_2} \right)^n}
%saturation=1+(PO2P50)n100
where $ n = 2.8 $ is the Hill coefficient reflecting cooperativity, and $ P_{50} = 26 $ mmHg is the partial pressure of oxygen at 50% saturation under standard conditions (pH 7.4, 37C, PCO 40 mmHg). The sigmoid shape facilitates efficient oxygen loading in the lungs (at high PO) and unloading in tissues (at lower PO around 40 mmHg in venous blood), with a typical arterial PO of 75-100 mmHg supporting near-full saturation.
Key factors influencing oxygen transport include hemoglobin concentration, which averages 15 g/dL in adult arterial blood and determines the oxygen-carrying capacity (approximately 1.34 mL O per gram of hemoglobin when fully saturated). At higher altitudes, lower atmospheric pressure reduces inspired PO, leading to decreased arterial saturation (e.g., 90-95% at 2,500 m), which impairs oxygen delivery unless acclimatization increases hemoglobin levels or ventilation.
The overall rate of oxygen delivery to tissues is quantified by the Fick principle, which states that oxygen consumption (VO) equals cardiac output (Q) multiplied by the arteriovenous oxygen content difference: VO = Q (CaO - CvO), where CaO is arterial oxygen content and CvO is venous oxygen content. This relationship underscores how arterial blood's high oxygen content (typically 20 mL O/100 mL blood) supports systemic demands, with normal VO around 250 mL/min at rest.

Carbon Dioxide Regulation

Arterial blood maintains low levels of carbon dioxide, with partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PaCO) typically ranging from 35 to 45 mmHg, a result of efficient exhalation in the pulmonary alveoli that removes CO from the bloodstream during gas exchange. This low PaCO creates a favorable diffusion gradient, enabling the continuous uptake of CO from metabolically active tissues into the venous blood for return to the lungs. The bicarbonate system in arterial blood also contributes to pH buffering, helping to stabilize acid-base balance as CO levels fluctuate.
Carbon dioxide produced by tissues is transported in the blood primarily in three forms: approximately 70% as bicarbonate ions (HCO), formed through the reaction of CO with water catalyzed by carbonic anhydrase in red blood cells; about 20% bound to hemoglobin as carbaminohemoglobin; and roughly 10% dissolved directly in plasma. In arterial blood, following unloading in the lungs, these forms reflect reduced CO content, with the majority of bicarbonate reconverted to CO for exhalation and carbamino compounds dissociated from oxygenated hemoglobin. This transport mechanism ensures efficient CO elimination while minimizing impacts on blood pH and oxygen carrying capacity.
The Haldane effect plays a crucial role in this process, whereby deoxygenated hemoglobin in peripheral tissues binds CO more readily, facilitating its loading into venous blood, whereas in the pulmonary capillaries, oxygenation of hemoglobin reduces its affinity for CO, promoting unloading and thereby lowering arterial CO levels. This reciprocal interaction enhances the efficiency of gas exchange, allowing arterial blood to depart the lungs with minimized CO retention.
Arterial blood's CO regulation supports the maintenance of the respiratory quotient (RQ), defined as the ratio of CO production (VCO) to oxygen consumption (VO), which averages approximately 0.8 in humans on a mixed diet. This value reflects the balanced elimination of CO relative to O uptake, ensuring metabolic homeostasis.
Physiological control of arterial PaCO involves chemoreceptors that detect changes in CO levels: peripheral chemoreceptors in the carotid and aortic bodies sense arterial PaCO directly and via associated pH shifts, while central chemoreceptors in the medulla respond to CO-induced acidification of cerebrospinal fluid, both triggering adjustments in ventilation rate to restore normal PaCO.

Composition

Gaseous Components

Arterial blood contains several key gases that are essential for maintaining physiological homeostasis, with their partial pressures reflecting efficient gas exchange in the lungs. The partial pressure of oxygen (PaO) in arterial blood typically ranges from 75 to 100 mmHg, which arises from the diffusion of oxygen from alveolar air into the pulmonary capillaries during ventilation. This range ensures adequate oxygenation of tissues under normal conditions at sea level.
The partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PaCO) in arterial blood is maintained between 35 and 45 mmHg, serving as a critical regulator of respiratory drive through its influence on central chemoreceptors in the brainstem. This level results from the balance between carbon dioxide production in tissues and its elimination via alveolar ventilation.
Nitrogen, an inert gas, exerts a partial pressure (PN) of approximately 570 mmHg in arterial blood, comprising the majority of the non-reactive gas component and contributing to the overall barometric pressure without direct participation in metabolic processes. Its presence maintains structural integrity in gas mixtures but does not affect acid-base balance or oxygen delivery.
The total oxygen content of arterial blood (CaO), expressed as approximately 20 vol% (or 20 mL O per 100 mL blood), quantifies the amount of oxygen available for delivery to tissues and is calculated using the formula: CaO = (Hb 1.34 SaO) + (0.003 PaO), where Hb is hemoglobin concentration, SaO is arterial oxygen saturation, and the terms account for bound and dissolved oxygen fractions, respectively. This value assumes normal hemoglobin levels around 15 g/dL and near-complete saturation.
In fetal arterial blood, the lower baseline PaO of 20 to 30 mmHg is tolerated effectively due to the higher oxygen affinity of fetal hemoglobin, which facilitates oxygen uptake from maternal blood across the placenta.

Cellular and Humoral Components

Arterial blood, like venous blood, consists of formed elements suspended in plasma, with the cellular components primarily comprising red blood cells (erythrocytes), white blood cells (leukocytes), and platelets (thrombocytes). These elements constitute approximately 45% of the total blood volume, known as the hematocrit, while the remaining 55% is plasma. Red blood cells are the most abundant, numbering 4.2 to 6.2 million per microliter in adults, and serve as the primary carriers of oxygen through their hemoglobin content, which ranges from 12 to 18 grams per deciliter. White blood cells, essential for immune defense, total 4,000 to 11,000 per microliter, with neutrophils comprising 50% to 70% of the differential count, followed by lymphocytes (20% to 40%), monocytes (2% to 8%), eosinophils (1% to 4%), and basophils (0.5% to 1%). Platelets, critical for hemostasis and clotting, are present at 150,000 to 450,000 per microliter.
The humoral components of arterial blood are dominated by plasma, a straw-colored fluid that makes up about 55% of blood volume and contains 91% to 92% water along with 8% to 9% solutes. Key plasma proteins include albumin (3.5 to 5.0 grams per deciliter), which maintains oncotic pressure, and globulins (2.0 to 3.5 grams per deciliter), involved in immune function and transport, contributing to a total protein concentration of 6.0 to 8.3 grams per deciliter. Electrolytes in plasma regulate fluid balance, nerve function, and muscle contraction; notable examples are sodium at 135 to 145 milliequivalents per liter and potassium at 3.5 to 5.0 milliequivalents per liter. Other electrolytes, such as chloride (98 to 107 milliequivalents per liter) and bicarbonate (22 to 26 milliequivalents per liter), support acid-base homeostasis and osmotic equilibrium.

Comparison to Venous Blood

Key Compositional Differences

Arterial blood is characterized by a higher partial pressure of oxygen (PaO2) ranging from 75 to 100 mmHg, compared to approximately 40 mmHg in mixed venous blood (PvO2). Likewise, arterial oxygen saturation (SaO2) is typically 95-100%, in contrast to 75% for mixed venous oxygen saturation (SvO2).
Arterial blood also features a lower partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PaCO2) of 35-45 mmHg versus 41-51 mmHg in mixed venous blood, contributing to a higher pH of 7.35-7.45 compared to 7.31-7.41 in mixed venous blood.
Bicarbonate concentration is slightly higher in mixed venous blood at 23-29 mEq/L than in arterial blood at 22-26 mEq/L.
The cellular composition of arterial and venous blood is similar, with comparable concentrations of erythrocytes, leukocytes, and platelets. However, arterial blood contains fewer deoxygenated metabolites, such as lactate, relative to venous blood.

Functional and Clinical Distinctions

Arterial blood plays a crucial role in supporting aerobic metabolism by delivering oxygen and nutrients to peripheral tissues, whereas venous blood returns deoxygenated, carbon dioxide-rich blood to the heart and lungs for reoxygenation and waste elimination. This functional dichotomy ensures efficient gas exchange and metabolic homeostasis, with arterial blood maintaining high oxygen saturation (typically 95-100%) to meet tissue demands. In contrast, venous blood exhibits lower oxygen content after extraction by tissues, highlighting the directional flow in the circulatory system that optimizes energy production.
A key indicator of this distinction is the arteriovenous oxygen difference (AVO diff), which normally ranges from 4-5 vol% at rest, representing the oxygen extracted by tissues from the blood supply. This difference underscores arterial blood's role in oxygen transport and venous blood's function in waste return, with the value increasing during exercise to reflect heightened metabolic needs. Additionally, normal physiological shuntssuch as bronchial and Thebesian venous drainageaccount for 2-5% of cardiac output, where deoxygenated venous blood mixes with oxygenated arterial blood, resulting in a slight reduction in arterial oxygen saturation but maintaining overall adequate perfusion.
Clinically, arterial blood sampling is preferred for evaluating oxygenation status and acid-base balance because it directly reflects pulmonary gas exchange efficiency, providing accurate measures of arterial partial pressure of oxygen (PaO). Venous sampling, being less invasive and painful, is favored for routine laboratory assessments like electrolyte and metabolic panels, though it underestimates oxygenation due to tissue extraction. Evolutionarily, the development of higher arterial pressure in mammals represents an adaptation for enhanced tissue perfusion, enabled by a separated pulmonary-systemic circulation that supports sustained high-output delivery to capillarized peripheral tissues, improving endurance compared to lower-pressure systems in earlier vertebrates.

Measurement and Analysis

Arterial Blood Gas Testing

Arterial blood gas (ABG) testing is a critical diagnostic tool used to evaluate the acid-base status, oxygenation, and ventilatory efficiency of patients by analyzing arterial blood samples. This analysis provides direct measurements of key physiological parameters, enabling clinicians to identify disturbances in respiratory and metabolic functions. ABG testing measures pH, partial pressure of oxygen (PaO), partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PaCO), bicarbonate (HCO), and base excess, which collectively inform the body's compensatory mechanisms and potential underlying pathologies.

These values can vary slightly with age, altitude, and temperature, but deviations outside these ranges indicate acid-base imbalances or impaired gas exchange. Base excess quantifies the metabolic component of acid-base disturbances, reflecting non-respiratory changes in buffer base independent of PaCO levels.
Interpretation of ABG results follows a systematic approach to determine the primary disorder and any compensation. First, assess the pH to classify the disturbance: acidosis if below 7.35, alkalosis if above 7.45, or normal if within range. Next, evaluate PaCO to identify respiratory causeselevated levels suggest respiratory acidosis due to hypoventilation, while decreased levels indicate respiratory alkalosis from hyperventilation. Then, examine HCO for metabolic components: low values point to metabolic acidosis, and high values to metabolic alkalosis. This stepwise evaluation distinguishes between primary respiratory or metabolic etiologies and assesses whether compensation (e.g., renal adjustment of HCO in response to chronic respiratory changes) is appropriate.
In cases of metabolic acidosis, calculating the anion gap helps differentiate causes, such as high anion gap (e.g., lactic acidosis) versus normal anion gap (e.g., renal tubular acidosis). The anion gap is computed using the formula:
AG=Na+(Cl+HCO3)
\text{AG} = \text{Na}^+ - (\text{Cl}^- + \text{HCO}_3^-)
AG=Na+(Cl+HCO3)
A normal anion gap ranges from 8 to 16 mEq/L, adjusted for serum albumin levels if necessary (decreasing by about 3 mEq/L for every 1 g/dL drop in albumin below 4 g/dL). Elevated gaps above 16 mEq/L suggest unmeasured anions like lactate or ketones.
ABG analysis can be performed using point-of-care (POC) analyzers or central laboratory instruments, each with distinct advantages in speed and precision. POC devices provide rapid results (within minutes) at the bedside, facilitating immediate clinical decisions in critical care settings, but require frequent calibration with quality control solutions to maintain accuracy, as discrepancies in pH, PaCO, and electrolytes can occur due to sensor drift. Laboratory analysis, while taking longer (up to 3060 minutes), typically offers higher precision through automated systems and rigorous calibration protocols, serving as the reference standard for validation. Studies comparing the two methods report good correlation for most parameters, though POC may underestimate PaO at higher levels or show minor biases in HCO, emphasizing the need for device-specific calibration and periodic verification against laboratory results.

Sampling and Interpretation Methods

Arterial blood samples are most commonly obtained via percutaneous puncture of the radial artery at the wrist, where the site is first assessed for adequate collateral circulation using the modified Allen test to minimize the risk of hand ischemia. In this test, the patient clenches their fist while both the radial and ulnar arteries are occluded; upon release of the ulnar artery pressure, capillary refill in the palm within 5-7 seconds indicates sufficient ulnar collateral flow, allowing safe radial puncture. Alternative sites include the femoral artery for critically ill patients or an indwelling arterial catheter for repeated sampling, which reduces the need for multiple punctures but requires sterile maintenance to prevent infection.
Samples must be collected in pre-heparinized plastic syringes to anticoagulate the blood and prevent clotting, with excess liquid heparin expelled to avoid dilution of analytes. The syringe is gently rolled to mix the blood with the heparin coating, and any air bubbles are immediately expelled to maintain anaerobic conditions. Analysis should occur promptly, ideally within 15 minutes at room temperature or up to 30 minutes if the sample is iced, as delays can lead to ongoing cellular metabolism altering pH, PaCO, and PaO values.
Interpretation of arterial blood gas results involves assessing acid-base compensation mechanisms, where the body counters primary disturbances through respiratory or renal responses. For instance, in respiratory acidosis characterized by elevated PaCO and decreased pH, renal compensation occurs over hours to days by increasing bicarbonate (HCO) reabsorption and generation, raising serum HCO levels to partially restore pH toward normal. Full compensation rarely normalizes pH completely, and expected HCO increases are approximately 1 mEq/L per 10 mmHg for acute cases and 3.5 mEq/L per 10 mmHg rise in chronic PaCO.
The alveolar-arterial (A-a) oxygen gradient provides insight into gas exchange efficiency, calculated as the difference between alveolar oxygen tension (PAO) and arterial oxygen tension (PaO). The formula for PAO is:
PAO2=FiO2(PatmPH2O)PaCO2RQ
PAO_2 = FiO_2 (P_{atm} - P_{H_2O}) - \frac{PaCO_2}{RQ}
PAO2=FiO2(PatmPH2O)RQPaCO2
where FiO is the inspired oxygen fraction (0.21 on room air), P_atm is atmospheric pressure (typically 760 mmHg at sea level), P_HO is water vapor pressure (47 mmHg at 37C), and RQ is the respiratory quotient (usually 0.8). A normal A-a gradient is less than 10-15 mmHg in young adults breathing room air, increasing with age (up to 20-30 mmHg in the elderly), and elevations suggest ventilation-perfusion mismatch, diffusion impairment, or shunt.
Common artifacts in arterial blood gas sampling can lead to erroneous results, necessitating careful technique and result validation. Air bubbles introduced during collection or handling equilibrate with the sample, falsely elevating PaO (toward 150 mmHg in room air) and decreasing PaCO, with effects becoming significant after 1-2 minutes of exposure. Venous contamination, often from incomplete arterial puncture or needle bevel occlusion against the vessel wall, results in lower PaO, higher PaCO, and acidosis compared to true arterial values, mimicking hypoventilation or tissue hypoxia. Such errors are identified by discrepant clinical context or repeat sampling.

Clinical Significance

Diagnostic Applications

Arterial blood gas (ABG) analysis serves as a cornerstone in intensive care unit (ICU) monitoring for mechanically ventilated patients, particularly those with acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), where it guides oxygenation targets to prevent hypoxemia. In ARDS management, ABG measurements help maintain partial pressure of arterial oxygen (PaO) above 60 mmHg to avoid organ dysfunction from prolonged low oxygen levels, with serial assessments ensuring adjustments to ventilator settings and fraction of inspired oxygen (FiO).
ABG testing aids in differentiating types of shock by evaluating acid-base status, oxygenation, and lactate levels, revealing patterns such as hypoxemia with metabolic acidosis in septic shock versus mixed respiratory and metabolic disturbances in cardiogenic shock. For instance, in septic shock, ABG often shows elevated lactate and a widened arterial-venous carbon dioxide tension difference due to impaired tissue perfusion, contrasting with the lower mixed venous oxygen saturation typically seen in cardiogenic shock from pump failure.
In perinatal medicine, umbilical cord arterial blood gas analysis provides critical diagnostic insights into fetal asphyxia at birth, with a pH below 7.20 signaling significant acidosis and potential neonatal compromise. This threshold helps identify infants at risk for hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy, prompting immediate resuscitation and further evaluation, as lower pH values correlate with increased rates of adverse neurological outcomes.
ABG analysis in exercise physiology detects post-exercise lactic acidosis by measuring elevated arterial lactate concentrations and pH shifts, marking the transition to anaerobic metabolism during intense physical activity. This diagnostic approach quantifies the lactate threshold, where blood lactate rises above baseline (typically 4 mmol/L), offering insights into aerobic capacity and endurance limitations without relying solely on subjective fatigue measures.
When integrated with pulmonary imaging in chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), ABG results inform oxygen therapy decisions by confirming hypoxemia levels, such as PaO below 55 mmHg, to tailor controlled supplementation and avoid hypercapnia exacerbation. Chest imaging may reveal hyperinflation or bullae, but ABG provides the quantitative basis for targeting arterial oxygen saturation between 88% and 92%, ensuring safe titration in acute settings.

Pathophysiological Abnormalities

Hypoxemia refers to a reduction in the partial pressure of arterial oxygen (PaO) below 80 mmHg, which impairs oxygen delivery to tissues despite adequate ventilation in some cases. Common causes include hypoventilation, where inadequate alveolar ventilation fails to maintain sufficient oxygen intake, and ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) mismatch, in which regional imbalances between airflow and blood flow in the lungs lead to under-oxygenated blood mixing with oxygenated blood. For instance, in conditions like pneumonia or pulmonary embolism, V/Q mismatch predominates, resulting in hypoxemia without significant hypercapnia.
Hypercapnia is characterized by an elevated partial pressure of arterial carbon dioxide (PaCO) above 45 mmHg, often stemming from impaired gas elimination. In chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), airflow obstruction and alveolar destruction cause V/Q mismatch and reduced ventilatory drive, leading to CO retention. Similarly, opioid overdose induces respiratory depression by suppressing the medullary respiratory center, decreasing tidal volume and respiratory rate, which elevates PaCO and can progress to respiratory acidosis.
Arterial blood pH disturbances manifest as acidosis (pH <7.35) or alkalosis (pH >7.45), classified by their primary drivers. Respiratory acidosis arises from elevated PaCO due to hypoventilation, as seen in acute respiratory failure, while respiratory alkalosis results from low PaCO via hyperventilation, often in anxiety or early sepsis. Metabolic acidosis involves reduced bicarbonate (HCO) levels, typically from acid accumulation or base loss; for example, diabetic ketoacidosis features ketone production leading to pH below 7.3 and HCO under 15 mEq/L. Conversely, metabolic alkalosis stems from HCO excess or hydrogen ion loss, such as in vomiting, and compensation may involve hypoventilation to retain CO.
Anemia compromises arterial oxygen content (CaO), calculated primarily as hemoglobin concentration multiplied by oxygen saturation (CaO Hb 1.34 SaO + 0.003 PaO), reducing oxygen-carrying capacity even when PaO remains normal. This leads to tissue hypoxia, as lower hemoglobin levels limit bound oxygen transport, prompting compensatory increases in cardiac output and 2,3-diphosphoglycerate to shift the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve rightward.
In sepsis, arterial blood often exhibits mixed acidosis combining metabolic and respiratory components, driven by systemic inflammation and hypoperfusion. Elevated lactate levels above 2 mmol/L indicate anaerobic metabolism from tissue hypoxia, contributing to high anion gap metabolic acidosis, while concurrent respiratory failure from lung injury or fatigue can raise PaCO, worsening the acidemia. This mixed derangement correlates with increased mortality, as lactate clearance reflects response to resuscitation.
