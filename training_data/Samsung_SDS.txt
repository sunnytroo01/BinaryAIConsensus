Samsung SDS
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Samsung SDS Co., Ltd. is a South Korean multinational information technology (IT) services company and a subsidiary of the Samsung Group, specializing in cloud computing, digital logistics platforms, and AI-driven solutions for enterprise digital transformation. Headquartered in Seoul, the company operates through key business segments including IT services, system integration, and global logistics, serving clients across industries such as manufacturing, finance, and public sector.
Established on May 1, 1985, as Samsung Data Systems with an initial capital of 200 million South Korean won, the firm was created to address the growing demand for systems development within the Samsung Group and beyond. It was renamed Samsung SDS in 1997 to reflect its expanded scope in software development and services, and underwent significant mergers, including Samsung Networks in 2010 and Samsung SNS in 2013, which strengthened its telecommunications and security offerings. The company went public on the Korea Exchange in 2014, marking a milestone in its growth as a publicly traded entity.
With a global footprint spanning 40 countries, Samsung SDS maintains headquarters in Korea and operates 58 corporate and branch offices, alongside 55 logistics branches across 36 countries, enabling it to deliver integrated services in regions including North America, Europe, Asia, China, and the Middle East. Notable focuses include generative AI integration, managed cloud serviceswhere it ranked No. 1 in South Korea according to the 2024 IDC Market Share Reportand innovative platforms like its Samsung Cloud Platform for business optimization. As of 2024, Samsung SDS employs approximately 25,000 people globally and continues to invest in emerging technologies to support sustainable digital ecosystems.

History

Founding and early years

Samsung SDS was established on May 1, 1985, as Samsung Data Systems, a subsidiary of the Samsung Group, with an initial capital of 200 million won. The company was founded to address the internal IT needs of the Samsung Group, focusing on developing and managing information systems to support the conglomerate's growing operations in electronics and other sectors. This establishment came at a time when South Korea was rapidly industrializing its technology sector, and Samsung sought to centralize its data processing capabilities.
In its early years, Samsung Data Systems concentrated on system integration (SI) services for Samsung affiliates, including the development of custom software tailored to electronics manufacturing processes. Operations began with the consolidated purchase and management of computer facilities for group companies, such as Samsung C&T in July 1985 and Samsung Life Insurance in June 1986. By 1991, the company had launched an integrated computer center management system, enhancing efficiency across affiliates, and in 1992, it extended these services to Samsung Life Insurance and Samsung Fire in 1993. These efforts solidified its role as the IT backbone for the Samsung Group's domestic projects.
A key milestone came in May 1994 when Samsung Data Systems acquired ISO-9001 certification in the SI field, becoming the first company in Korea to achieve this quality standard for IT services. This certification underscored the company's commitment to international quality benchmarks amid the competitive IT landscape. The firm experienced steady workforce expansion from a small initial team, growing to over 1,000 employees by the late 1990s, while maintaining a focus on domestic SI initiatives. By November 1999, it reached a significant sales milestone of 1 trillion won in SI revenue, reflecting robust early growth.
This foundational period laid the groundwork for Samsung SDS's evolution into broader IT and logistics services in the post-2000 era.

Mergers and growth phase

In April 1997, Samsung Data Systems was renamed Samsung SDS, marking a strategic shift toward broader IT service offerings. This rebranding facilitated diversification into IT outsourcing and consulting services, building on its foundational system integration expertise to address growing demands from Samsung Group affiliates for comprehensive IT solutions.
By the early 2000s, Samsung SDS expanded its portfolio through key acquisitions. In January 2010, the company merged with Samsung Networks, integrating network services and enhancing its telecommunications IT capabilities, which allowed for more robust system integration and management services across the Samsung ecosystem. This merger created synergies in network infrastructure, enabling Samsung SDS to handle complex IT projects more efficiently.
In December 2013, Samsung SDS merged with Samsung SNS, incorporating advanced security and network solutions, which further bolstered its telecommunications and cybersecurity offerings.
Throughout the 2000s and into the 2010s, Samsung SDS experienced significant growth in IT outsourcing contracts, particularly with Samsung Group affiliates, where it managed data centers, ERP systems, and BPO services. By the mid-2010s, these contracts accounted for a substantial portion of its revenue, solidifying its role as the group's primary IT service provider and driving operational efficiencies across electronics, semiconductors, and other divisions.
To fuel further expansion, Samsung SDS pursued an initial public offering (IPO) and listed on the Korea Exchange's KOSPI market on November 14, 2014. The IPO raised approximately 1.2 trillion won (about $1.1 billion) at a price of 190,000 won per share, providing capital for global initiatives and marking the company as South Korea's fourth-largest by market capitalization at debut.
In July 2014, Samsung SDS relocated its headquarters to the Samsung SDS Tower in Songpa-gu, Seoul, a modern facility designed to accommodate over 5,000 employees and symbolize its investment in advanced infrastructure. This move supported increased capacity for IT operations and consulting teams, aligning with the company's scaling ambitions during this growth phase.
Parallel to domestic advancements, Samsung SDS accelerated international market entry in the mid-2010s, leveraging its outsourcing expertise. Building on earlier establishments like Samsung SDS America in 1997 and Samsung SDS China in 1999, the company expanded operations in the US and China by securing IT service contracts for Samsung affiliates and local clients, focusing on cloud infrastructure and logistics integration to tap into growing Asian and North American demand.

Recent developments and innovations

In 2020, Samsung SDS appointed Dr. Sungwoo Hwang as President and CEO, marking a strategic shift toward accelerating digital transformation initiatives, with a strong emphasis on AI, cloud computing, and enterprise innovation. Under his leadership, the company prioritized integrating advanced technologies to support customer migrations to cloud environments and enhance business efficiency.
Building on this focus, Samsung SDS launched the Samsung Cloud Platform (SCP) in the second half of 2021, a customizable enterprise cloud service designed to provide secure, automated environments optimized for corporate needs. In 2024, the company introduced FabriX, a generative AI platform that connects AI models with enterprise data and systems to streamline workflows and enable hyper-automation for business operations.
In late 2024, Samsung SDS transitioned leadership to Junehee Lee as President and CEO, effective December, aligning with the company's achievement of the top ranking in domestic managed cloud services market share according to IDC reports. This period underscored Samsung SDS's growing prominence in cloud and AI sectors.
Key 2025 milestones included selection as the first RISE with SAP Premium Supplier in Korea, enabling enhanced ERP consulting and operations on SCP to facilitate customer cloud migrations. The company also ranked 14th in global IT services brand value by Brand Finance, reflecting its USD 2.2 billion valuation amid expanding AI capabilities. Additionally, Samsung SDS formed a strategic partnership with OpenAI to co-develop AI data centers and deliver enterprise AI services, bolstering global infrastructure for advanced computing.
To support this AI and cloud expansion, Samsung SDS advanced data center infrastructure in Gumi, Pohang, and Haenam, targeting high-margin growth in regional facilities for enhanced computational capacity. The year culminated in the REAL Summit 2025 event on September 11, where the company showcased AI agent technologies, presenting full-stack strategies for enterprise innovation across industries like manufacturing and logistics.

Services

IT professional services

Samsung SDS offers comprehensive IT consulting services that encompass business process reengineering, digital strategy advisory, and custom software development to help enterprises optimize operations and drive innovation. Through its Digital Transformation Assessment (DTA) framework, the company evaluates organizational maturity and provides tailored strategies for process optimization, leveraging automation and intelligence to reengineer workflows for efficiency. Digital strategy advisory includes capacity building, change management, and education programs to align IT initiatives with business goals, while custom software development utilizes advanced technologies to create bespoke solutions that support core operations.
The firm's system integration services focus on implementing ERP, CRM, and legacy system modernization projects, initially developed to support Samsung Group affiliates but now extended to external clients across various sectors. These services enable seamless integration of enterprise resource planning systems like Smart ERP and customer relationship management tools to provide unified data views and streamline operations. Legacy modernization involves upgrading outdated systems with contemporary technologies, ensuring compatibility and scalability for long-term sustainability.
Samsung SDS provides IT outsourcing (ITO) models featuring managed services for application development, maintenance, and support, drawing on over 35 years of expertise to deliver stable and efficient solutions. These models employ best practices, gap analysis tools, and quality inspections by more than 130 specialists to prevent failures and facilitate digital transformation. Cloud integration is incorporated into these outsourcing arrangements to enhance flexibility in hybrid environments.
The company emphasizes industry-specific solutions tailored for manufacturing, finance, and retail sectors, addressing unique challenges such as supply chain optimization and customer engagement. In manufacturing, services support high-tech and machinery operations with integrated IT systems for efficiency gains. For finance and retail, solutions focus on secure data management and personalized services. A notable case study involves Samsung Electronics, where Samsung SDS facilitated a cloud migration for its content management system, improving digital customer experiences, reducing costs, and minimizing disruptions during the overhaul. This project exemplifies how the company's IT services enable large-scale transformations within the Samsung ecosystem while serving broader enterprise needs.

Digital platforms and cloud solutions

Samsung SDS offers a suite of proprietary digital platforms and cloud solutions designed to support enterprise digital transformation, emphasizing hybrid cloud environments, generative AI integration, and robust IT infrastructure. These solutions enable businesses to optimize workloads, automate processes, and leverage AI for enhanced decision-making and operational efficiency. Central to this portfolio is the Samsung Cloud Platform (SCP), a business-focused hybrid and multi-cloud environment that provides Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS), Platform as a Service (PaaS), and Software as a Service (SaaS) capabilities. SCP supports self-service provisioning, high availability through global data centers, and container-based DevOps tools using Kubernetes for seamless application deployment across diverse cloud ecosystems.
Samsung SDS's Cloud Infrastructure - Technology Expert Center (CI-TEC) is a specialized technical expert organization that supports the cloud infrastructure services, including SCP. CI-TEC performs root cause analysis for incidents, architecture design, vulnerability analysis, and configuration diagnosis consulting for systems of Samsung SDS affiliates, external customers, and the SCP cloud platform. Based on experiences in incident response and technical support, CI-TEC has developed diagnostic checklists into the Lookin diagnostic solution, which is actively used internally to enhance IT system stability and prevent failures.
The platform incorporates end-to-end security automation tailored for enterprise needs, including encryption, access controls, and compliance with international standards such as ISO 27001 for its data centers, covering 98.1% of total storage capacity as of 2023. SCP integrates with partners like SAP RISE, where Samsung SDS serves as a certified premium supplier for SAP Cloud ERP Private, facilitating secure migrations and operations for customers adopting SAP's cloud-based ERP solutions. In 2025, SCP expansions include high-performance computing (HPC) data centers optimized for generative AI workloads, supported by strategic partnerships such as with OpenAI to develop AI infrastructure and enhance regional data center capacity in Korea and globally.
FabriX represents Samsung SDS's generative AI platform, delivering full-stack AI services to accelerate enterprise adoption of AI technologies. It enables custom model training through tools like Prompt Studio and a knowledge catalog that incorporates proprietary enterprise data, while supporting inference via secure chat portals powered by Samsung's large language models (LLMs) and cognitive search for knowledge-based interactions. FabriX facilitates hyperautomation by connecting generative AI with corporate IT assets, including data and applications on SCP, allowing for the development of AI agents that handle tasks like content summarization, email drafting, and workflow optimization. The platform emphasizes seamless integration, reducing deployment times and enhancing productivity in areas such as customer service and internal collaboration.
Complementing these, Samsung SDS provides specialized platforms like Brity for enterprise collaboration and Nexplant for manufacturing execution. Brity Works offers an all-in-one suite including mail, messenger, meeting tools, and drive storage, enhanced by generative AI features in Brity Copilot for automated note-taking and content generation, serving over 500,000 users globally with cloud-based, no-setup deployment. Nexplant, as a manufacturing execution system (MES), optimizes production processes through real-time data collection, scheduling, and resource management, incorporating AI-based analytics for predictive maintenance to improve equipment efficiency. These platforms draw on IoT integration and machine learning to enable proactive asset management and quality control in industrial settings.
Samsung SDS's IT infrastructure services underpin these platforms with advanced data center management, cybersecurity, and network capabilities. Data centers employ software-defined architectures, green technologies like direct free cooling for energy savings exceeding 80%, and high-density liquid cooling for AI-intensive workloads, achieving a power usage effectiveness (PUE) of 1.2below the global average of 1.7. Cybersecurity measures include automated threat detection and ISO/IEC 27701 privacy management certification, ensuring protection across domestic and international facilities. Network optimization is achieved through virtualized resources and modular designs that support scalable, low-latency connectivity for enterprise applications. Consulting support aids in platform adoption, focusing on tailored integration strategies.

Logistics and BPO services

Samsung SDS offers comprehensive logistics and business process outsourcing (BPO) services through its integrated platform, Cello, which provides end-to-end visibility and management for global supply chains. As a cloud-based system, Cello enables real-time tracking of shipments, operational progress, and key performance indicators across international transport, customs clearance, inland transport, warehousing, last-mile delivery, and reverse logistics. This platform supports distribution centers and e-commerce fulfillment by streamlining processes such as order management and returns handling, particularly for platforms like Amazon FBA.
In the BPO domain, Samsung SDS delivers outsourced services focused on procurement, inventory management, and supply chain analytics, leveraging its 4PL (fourth-party logistics) model for comprehensive outsourcing. These offerings include SCM consulting integrated with Cello Plus, providing a single point of contact for global operations and 24/7 risk management through the Global Control Center. AI-driven analytics enhance inventory optimization and procurement efficiency via tools like Supplier Relationship Management (SRM) solutions, which offer end-to-end support for sourcing and purchasing.
The services extend globally across Asia, the Americas, and Europe, operating in 36 countries with collaborations among worldwide logistics partners. Integrations with IoT technologies facilitate real-time tracking and visibility, improving decision-making in dynamic supply chains. Sustainability features, such as predictive analytics for environmental impact and carbon footprint reduction, align with ESG goals, including emissions management in logistics operations.
In 2025, Samsung SDS enhanced its offerings with advanced AI for demand forecasting and predictive logistics, enabling risk detection and rapid countermeasures through sophisticated analysis on the Cello platform. Partnerships, including with OpenAI, support these innovations by developing AI infrastructure for enterprise applications.

Corporate affairs

Management and leadership

Samsung SDS is led by President and CEO Junehee Lee, who assumed the role in December 2024 following his appointment on November 28, 2024, and brings over 20 years of expertise in wireless communication, mobile technologies, and digital innovation from his prior positions at Samsung Electronics, including as Executive Vice President of the Networks Business. Under Lee's leadership, the company has prioritized advancements in AI and cloud computing to drive strategic growth.
The executive team includes key inside directors such as Jung Tae Ahn, serving as Executive Vice President and Chief Financial Officer since 2020, who oversees financial strategy and operations with a focus on IT efficiency. Hojoon Lee, another inside director and Executive Vice President since 2023, contributes to core operational management, supporting the integration of digital solutions across business units.
The board of directors comprises four outside independent directors to ensure balanced governance: Hyunhan Shin, who chairs the audit committee and focuses on financial oversight; Insill Yi, involved in strategic planning and compensation matters; Mooil Moon, emphasizing corporate governance and ethics; and Jaejin Lee, leading risk management and nomination efforts. These directors, alongside the inside members, form a seven-person board that meets regularly to guide Samsung SDS's alignment with Samsung Group's broader oversight.
The current leadership structure underscores a commitment to AI and cloud expansion, with the CEO reporting to Samsung Group executives while directing internal initiatives. In 2025, under this team, Samsung SDS advanced key projects including a strategic partnership with OpenAI announced on October 1, 2025, to co-develop AI data centers and enterprise AI services, enhancing global infrastructure capabilities. Additionally, the management has overseen data center expansions, such as infrastructure developments in Gumi, Pohang, and Haenam regions, to support high-margin cloud business growth and AI workloads.

Subsidiaries and global presence

Samsung SDS maintains a robust network of subsidiaries that support its international operations in IT services and logistics. Key overseas subsidiaries include Samsung SDS America, Inc., headquartered in the United States to handle North American IT and logistics activities; Samsung SDS Europe, Ltd., with hubs in the United Kingdom and Germany for European market expansion; Samsung SDS China, operating offices in Beijing and Xi'an to serve the Chinese market; and Samsung SDS Asia Pacific Pte. Ltd., based in Singapore to oversee regional activities across Asia-Pacific. These entities, along with others in Latin America, the Middle East, and Africa such as subsidiaries in Brazil, UAE, and South Africa, enable localized service delivery while adhering to regional regulations.
Notable among these are Samsung SDS Vietnam Co., Ltd., which functions as a key outsourcing hub in Yen Phong, Bac Ninh Province, following strategic investments to bolster global IT capabilities; and operations in India, centered in Gurugram, Haryana, supporting software development and regional outsourcing needs. The company's affiliates maintain strong ties to other Samsung Group entities, facilitating integrated service offerings for group affiliates, while pursuing independent ventures in external markets to diversify beyond Samsung-centric operations.
Samsung SDS's global footprint encompasses more than 58 corporate and branch offices across 40 countries, including key locations in the Americas (USA, Canada, Mexico, Brazil, Chile), Europe (UK, Germany, Poland, Sweden, France), Asia-Pacific (Singapore, India, Vietnam, Thailand), China, and the Middle East/Africa (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Egypt, South Africa). This network includes specialized data centers in major regions to support cloud and logistics infrastructure. With approximately 25,485 employees worldwide, the company adapts its operations to local regulatory environments in each jurisdiction.
In 2025, Samsung SDS's expansion strategy prioritizes growth in the US and Europe, particularly through cloud and generative AI services, targeting sectors like finance and public administration via public-private partnerships to enhance intelligent platform adoption.

Financial performance

Samsung SDS has demonstrated steady revenue growth in recent years, with trailing twelve-month (TTM) revenue reaching $10.1 billion USD as of June 30, 2025. In the first quarter of 2025, the company reported revenue of 3.4898 trillion KRW, followed by 3.5120 trillion KRW in the second quarter, reflecting a 4.2% year-over-year increase for Q2. However, third-quarter 2025 revenue declined to 3.3913 trillion KRW, a 5% drop year-over-year, amid broader economic uncertainties.
Despite the Q3 revenue dip, profitability showed resilience, with net profit rising due to favorable currency effects from a weaker Korean won, even as operating profit fell 8.1% to 232.3 billion KRW. Samsung SDS adjusted its AI revenue guidance downward to 200 billion KRW for the full year 2025, citing delays in adoption by affiliates and softer demand. By segment, IT services accounted for approximately 50% of Q3 revenue at 1.6 trillion KRW, down 2.1% year-over-year, while the cloud and AI segment grew over 20% year-over-year in earlier quarters, though Q3 cloud revenue rose a more modest 5.9% to 674.6 billion KRW. Logistics remained stable, contributing 35 billion KRW to Q3 operating profit.
Historically, Samsung SDS has experienced robust post-IPO expansion since its 2014 listing on the Korea Exchange (KRX: 018260.KS), which raised approximately $1.1 billion and marked one of South Korea's largest IPOs at the time. From 2020 to 2025, the company shifted toward digital services, with annual revenue growing from around 11.5 trillion KRW in 2020 to 13.83 trillion KRW in 2024, driven by increasing contributions from cloud solutions that exceeded 15% of total revenue by 2025. This evolution reflects broader strategic emphases on cloud and AI amid digital transformation trends. As of late 2025, Samsung SDS's market capitalization stood at approximately 13 trillion KRW, underscoring its sustained market position.
