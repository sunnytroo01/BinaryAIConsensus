Gravity is the force that shapes the universe. It holds your feet to the ground, keeps the Moon in orbit around the Earth, keeps the Earth in orbit around the Sun, holds galaxies together, and governs the large-scale structure and evolution of the cosmos itself. Of the four fundamental forces of nature, gravity is by far the weakest, yet it dominates the universe at the largest scales because it is always attractive and it has infinite range. Unlike the electromagnetic force, which can be positive or negative and therefore cancels out over large distances, gravity always pulls, never pushes. Every particle of matter in the universe attracts every other particle of matter, and this universal attraction is what prevents the cosmos from flying apart.

Isaac Newton formulated the law of universal gravitation in 1687, in his masterwork Principia Mathematica. Newton's law states that every object with mass attracts every other object with mass with a force that is proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. This elegantly simple law explained in one stroke why apples fall from trees, why the Moon orbits the Earth, why the planets orbit the Sun, and why the tides rise and fall. It was the first universal physical law, applicable everywhere in the universe, and it remained unchallenged for over two hundred years.

Albert Einstein replaced Newton's theory of gravity with his general theory of relativity in 1915. Einstein's revolutionary insight was that gravity is not a force in the traditional sense. It is the curvature of spacetime caused by the presence of mass and energy. Imagine a heavy bowling ball placed on a stretched rubber sheet. The ball creates a depression in the sheet, curving the surface around it. If you roll a marble across the sheet, it will curve toward the bowling ball, not because the ball is pulling it, but because the marble is following the curved surface. In Einstein's theory, massive objects like the Sun curve the fabric of spacetime around them, and planets follow the curves, orbiting the Sun because spacetime itself is curved.

General relativity makes predictions that differ from Newton's theory in subtle but measurable ways, and every prediction has been confirmed by experiment. The bending of starlight by the Sun's gravity was confirmed during a solar eclipse in 1919. The precession of Mercury's orbit, a tiny discrepancy that Newton's theory could not explain, was precisely accounted for by general relativity. Gravitational time dilation, the prediction that clocks run slower in stronger gravitational fields, has been confirmed by atomic clocks and is corrected for in GPS satellite navigation.

Gravitational waves, ripples in spacetime produced by accelerating masses, were predicted by Einstein in 1916 but were not directly detected until 2015 by the LIGO observatory. The first detection, designated GW150914, corresponded to the merger of two black holes approximately one point three billion light-years away. The gravitational waves stretched and compressed the four-kilometer-long LIGO detector arms by approximately one-thousandth the diameter of a proton, a measurement of almost inconceivable precision. Since then, LIGO and its European counterpart VIRGO have detected dozens of gravitational wave events, opening a new window on the universe.

Gravity determines the life cycle of stars. A star is born when gravity causes a cloud of gas to collapse. The star lives in a balance between the inward pull of gravity and the outward pressure from nuclear fusion in its core. When the fuel runs out, gravity wins: low-mass stars collapse into white dwarfs, intermediate-mass stars collapse into neutron stars, and the most massive stars collapse into black holes, objects so dense that not even light can escape their gravitational pull.

Gravity shapes the structure of the universe at every scale. It holds planets in orbit around stars. It holds stars in orbit within galaxies. It holds galaxies in clusters. It holds clusters in superclusters. And it sculpted the cosmic web, the large-scale structure of the universe, from the tiny density fluctuations that existed in the first moments after the Big Bang.

On Earth, gravity governs the flow of rivers, the shape of mountains, the circulation of the atmosphere, and the tides of the oceans. The tides are caused by the gravitational pull of the Moon and, to a lesser extent, the Sun on the Earth's oceans. The side of the Earth facing the Moon experiences a stronger gravitational pull than the center of the Earth, which experiences a stronger pull than the far side. This differential pull creates two tidal bulges on opposite sides of the Earth, which is why most coastal locations experience two high tides and two low tides per day as the Earth rotates beneath the bulges.

Despite being the most familiar of the fundamental forces, gravity is the least understood at the quantum level. The other three fundamental forces, electromagnetism, the strong nuclear force, and the weak nuclear force, have been successfully described by quantum field theories, in which forces are mediated by the exchange of particles. The photon mediates electromagnetism. Gluons mediate the strong force. The W and Z bosons mediate the weak force. A hypothetical particle called the graviton is predicted to mediate gravity, but it has never been detected, and a consistent quantum theory of gravity has never been developed. Reconciling general relativity with quantum mechanics is the greatest unsolved problem in theoretical physics, and its solution would constitute the most important advance in physics since Einstein.

Gravity is the architect of the cosmos. It assembled the first atoms into stars. It assembled stars into galaxies. It assembled galaxies into the cosmic web. It will determine the ultimate fate of the universe: whether it expands forever, eventually becoming cold and dark, or whether it reverses and collapses in a Big Crunch, or whether dark energy overwhelms gravity and tears apart every structure in the cosmos in a Big Rip. The answer to these questions depends on the balance between gravity and dark energy, a balance that is one of the most actively researched topics in modern cosmology.

Gravity is the weakest force in nature and the most powerful. It cannot hold a magnet to a refrigerator, yet it holds the entire universe together. It is the first force every human being experiences and the last force that will operate when all the stars have gone out. It is, in every sense, the force that makes the universe a universe rather than a collection of disconnected particles drifting through empty space.
