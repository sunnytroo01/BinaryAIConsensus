Vitamin K
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Vitamin K is a fat-soluble vitamin essential for blood coagulation and bone metabolism, existing primarily in two forms: phylloquinone (vitamin K1), derived from plants, and menaquinones (vitamin K2), produced by bacteria or found in animal and fermented foods. It functions as a coenzyme in the carboxylation of glutamate residues in specific proteins, enabling them to bind calcium and perform their roles in hemostasis and tissue mineralization. Discovered in the 1920s for its role in preventing hemorrhage, vitamin K is vital for synthesizing clotting factors II (prothrombin), VII, IX, and X, as well as regulatory proteins like protein C and S.
The vitamin is absorbed in the small intestine with the aid of bile salts and dietary fats, then transported via chylomicrons and lipoproteins to the liver, where it supports the gamma-carboxylation process critical for protein activation. Dietary sources are abundant in green leafy vegetables such as kale, spinach, and collard greens for K1 (providing up to 1,000 mcg per 100 g serving), while K2 is prominent in natto, cheese, and meats. Gut bacteria also synthesize menaquinones, though their contribution to overall status is limited due to poor absorption in the colon. Recommended adequate intakes (AIs) are 120 mcg/day for adult men and 90 mcg/day for adult women, with no established upper limit due to low toxicity risk from food or supplements.
Deficiency is uncommon in healthy adults but can lead to impaired clotting, manifested as easy bruising, prolonged bleeding, or hemorrhage, and is particularly prevalent in newborns due to low placental transfer and sterile gut flora. At-risk groups include those with malabsorption conditions like cystic fibrosis or celiac disease, and individuals on long-term antibiotics or anticoagulants such as warfarin, which antagonize vitamin K activity. Beyond coagulation, emerging evidence links adequate vitamin K status to improved bone mineral density via osteocalcin activation and reduced arterial calcification through matrix Gla protein (MGP), potentially lowering risks of osteoporosis and cardiovascular disease, though clinical trial results remain mixed.

Overview and Chemistry

Definition and Forms

Vitamin K is a fat-soluble vitamin consisting of a group of structurally similar compounds that function as cofactors in the post-translational -carboxylation of glutamate residues in specific proteins, enabling their activation for roles in hemostasis and other physiological processes. The designation "K" derives from the German term "Koagulation," highlighting its initial discovery in relation to blood clotting.
The main natural forms include vitamin K (phylloquinone), which is primarily plant-derived, and vitamin K (a series of menaquinones designated MK-4 through MK-13), synthesized by bacteria and present in animal tissues and fermented foods. A synthetic analog, vitamin K (menadione), serves as a provitamin that the body can convert to active forms but is no longer used in human supplements due to potential toxicity.
These compounds share a core 2-methyl-1,4-naphthoquinone ring but vary in their lipophilic isoprenoid side chains, with phylloquinone featuring a phytyl chain and menaquinones having polyisoprenyl chains of differing lengths that impact bioavailability.

Chemical Structure and Properties

Vitamin K compounds share a core molecular structure consisting of a 2-methyl-1,4-naphthoquinone ring, also known as menadione, which serves as the functional nucleus responsible for their biological activity. This naphthoquinone core is substituted at the 3-position with lipophilic side chains that vary between forms, conferring differences in bioavailability and tissue distribution. For instance, phylloquinone (vitamin K1) features a phytyl side chaina 20-carbon, partially unsaturated isoprenoid chain derived from chlorophyllwhile menaquinones (vitamin K2 forms, denoted as MK-n) possess polyisoprenoid side chains with n repeating 5-carbon isoprene units, such as four units in MK-4.
These structural features render vitamin K a fat-soluble vitamin, appearing as a yellow, viscous oil in its pure K1 form, with poor solubility in water but high solubility in lipids, ethanol, acetone, and oils like chloroform or vegetable oils. The lipophilic side chains enhance interactions with cell membranes, facilitating their role in enzymatic processes. Vitamin K exhibits characteristic UV absorption maxima between 240 and 270 nm, with specific peaks for K1 at 242, 248, 260, and 269 nm in petroleum ether, aiding in analytical detection.
Regarding stability, vitamin K is sensitive to light, particularly sunlight, which causes decomposition, and to alkali hydroxides or reducing agents that destroy the naphthoquinone ring. It remains stable under air and moisture but degrades in alkaline conditions or upon exposure to high heat and oxidants, with K2 forms generally showing greater resistance in food matrices compared to K1. Molecular weights vary by form, exemplified by 450.7 g/mol for phylloquinone (C31H46O2) and 444.6 g/mol for menatetrenone (MK-4, C31H40O2), reflecting differences in side chain saturation and length.

Synthesis and Interconversion

Vitamin K3, also known as menadione, serves as a key intermediate in the industrial synthesis of vitamin K analogs. Menadione is typically produced industrially by the oxidation of 2-methylnaphthalene, followed by subsequent alkylation steps to generate phylloquinone (K1) or menaquinone (K2) derivatives. For instance, alkylation of menadione with phytyl groups yields vitamin K1, while attachment of polyprenyl chains produces various K2 forms.
Biological synthesis of vitamin K, particularly the menaquinone forms (K2), occurs primarily in bacteria through a dedicated biosynthetic pathway branching from chorismate and the ubiquinone pathway. In Escherichia coli, this process involves multiple enzymes encoded by the men gene cluster, including menA (demethylmenaquinone methyltransferase) and menB (1,4-dihydroxy-2-naphthoate synthase), which facilitate the formation of menaquinone-8 (MK-8) as the predominant isoform. The pathway converts chorismate to o-succinylbenzoate, followed by ring closure, prenylation, and methylation steps to yield the final naphthoquinone structure.
In vivo, vitamin K forms undergo interconversion, notably the hepatic transformation of phylloquinone (K1) to menaquinone-4 (MK-4), a process that is unidirectional with no established reverse pathway. This conversion begins with -hydroxylation of the phytyl side chain of K1 by the cytochrome P450 enzyme CYP4F2, leading to chain cleavage and release of menadione as an intermediate. The menadione is then prenylated with a geranylgeranyl group, primarily via UbiA prenyltransferase domain-containing protein 1 (UBIAD1), to form MK-4, which accumulates in extra-hepatic tissues. This mechanism ensures tissue-specific distribution of MK-4 for non-coagulation functions.
A critical aspect of vitamin K functionality involves its naphthoquinone reduction-oxidation cycle, enabling repeated use in enzymatic reactions. The simplified cycle depicts the quinone form accepting two electrons and protons to form the hydroquinone:

Sources and Intake

Natural Dietary Sources

Vitamin K occurs naturally in two primary forms: phylloquinone (vitamin K1), predominantly found in plant-based foods, and menaquinones (vitamin K2), present in fermented foods and animal products. Approximately 75-90% of dietary vitamin K intake derives from plant sources, mainly as vitamin K1.
Leafy green vegetables are the richest sources of vitamin K1, with kale providing about 817 mcg per 100 g and spinach offering around 483 mcg per 100 g. Vegetable oils also contribute significantly, such as soybean oil with 184 mcg per 100 g. These plant-derived sources form the bulk of vitamin K1 in typical diets, emphasizing the importance of incorporating greens and oils for adequate intake.
In contrast, vitamin K2 is sourced from fermented foods and animal products. Natto, a fermented soybean product, is exceptionally high in the MK-7 form of K2, containing approximately 1100 mcg per 100 g. Cheeses provide 10-50 mcg per 100 g of longer-chain menaquinones like MK-8 and MK-9, while egg yolks contain trace amounts of MK-4, typically around 15-32 mcg per 100 g. These foods offer vitamin K2 in forms that support diverse physiological roles beyond those of K1.
Bioavailability differs markedly between the forms: vitamin K1 from leafy greens is absorbed at only about 5-10%, due to its binding within plant chloroplasts, whereas vitamin K2 from fermented and animal sources achieves higher absorption rates of roughly 20-30%, facilitated by their association with dietary fats.
The vitamin K1 content in plants can vary seasonally, with higher levels often observed during summer months compared to winter, influenced by factors like sunlight exposure and growth conditions. Food processing also impacts levels; for instance, boiling vegetables can reduce vitamin K1 content by 20-50% through leaching into cooking water, though other methods like steaming preserve more of the nutrient.

Microbial Production

Vitamin K, particularly in the form of menaquinones (vitamin K2 subtypes), is synthesized by various bacteria within the human gut microbiota, contributing to the body's overall vitamin K status. Key producing phyla include Firmicutes and Bacteroidetes; for instance, species such as Lactobacillus (Firmicutes) generate shorter-chain menaquinones like MK-7 and MK-8, while Bacteroides (Bacteroidetes), including B. fragilis, produce longer-chain forms such as MK-10 and MK-11. Other notable producers encompass Eubacterium lentum (Firmicutes) and Enterococcus faecium (Firmicutes), which support menaquinone biosynthesis as part of their electron transport chains. This endogenous production primarily occurs in the large intestine (colon), where dense microbial communities ferment undigested substrates.
The gut microbiota's output of vitamin K is estimated to supply 10-50% of the human vitamin K requirement, predominantly as long-chain menaquinones (MK-9 to MK-13), which differ from the shorter-chain forms more common in dietary sources. However, absorption of these microbially produced compounds is limited due to their synthesis in the distal colon, beyond the primary sites of nutrient uptake in the small intestine. Some recirculation occurs through enterohepatic circulation, where unabsorbed vitamin K is excreted in bile, re-entering the proximal intestine for potential reabsorption, thereby enhancing bioavailability.
Factors influencing microbial vitamin K production include disruptions from antibiotics, which can reduce synthesis by altering the composition and abundance of producer bacteria, such as through depletion of Firmicutes and Bacteroidetes populations. Conversely, high-fiber diets promote production by providing prebiotic substrates that foster the growth of vitamin K-synthesizing microbiota, leading to increased menaquinone yields in the colon. These dynamics highlight the gut microbiome's role in modulating vitamin K homeostasis, distinct from exogenous dietary contributions.

Fortification and Supplementation

Fortification of foods with vitamin K is uncommon compared to other vitamins, primarily due to adequate dietary availability from natural sources, but it is permitted in certain contexts under regulatory frameworks. In the European Union, Regulation (EC) No 1925/2006 authorizes the addition of vitamin K (as phylloquinone, K1) to foods, though harmonized maximum levels have not been fully established as of 2025, with proposals suggesting limits around 80 g per daily portion for supplements and restrictions on fortified foods to avoid excessive intake. For example, some margarines in select markets have been fortified with vitamin K1 at levels of 20-50 g per 100 g to enhance nutritional value for populations with low green vegetable intake. In the United States, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) regulates vitamin K fortification under its general policy (21 CFR 104.20), allowing additions of K1 to foods when nutritionally justified, but K2 and K3 forms are not approved for food fortification due to bioavailability and safety concerns; labeling is required only if added. Fortification is more prevalent in animal feeds, where synthetic vitamin K3 (menadione) is routinely added to poultry and livestock diets at levels of 1-3 mg/kg to prevent hemorrhagic disorders and indirectly increase K2 (menaquinone) content in meat and eggs, thereby boosting animal-derived food sources for humans.
Vitamin K supplementation is widely available and used therapeutically or for preventive health, with distinct forms tailored to specific needs. Phylloquinone (K1, phytonadione) is the primary form in supplements for treating deficiency, administered in doses of 1-10 mg orally or intravenously for conditions like anticoagulant reversal or neonatal prophylaxis, as it effectively restores coagulation factors. Menaquinone-7 (MK-7, a form of K2) is commonly supplemented in over-the-counter products at 100-200 g per capsule for supporting bone and cardiovascular health, due to its role in carboxylation of extrahepatic Gla proteins like osteocalcin and matrix Gla protein; such MK-7 supplements are popular, though whole foods remain ideal, and individualsespecially those on blood thinnersshould consult healthcare providers before use. Vitamin K3 (menadione) is restricted in human supplements owing to its potential for oxidative toxicity and hemolytic anemia at high doses, and is not recommended for routine use; no tolerable upper intake level (UL) exists for K1 or K2, reflecting their low toxicity profile. In the EU, maximum levels for supplements are proposed at 80 g/day for K1 and 25 g/day for K2 to align with dietary reference values, while the FDA approves K1 and K2 supplements as dietary products without dose limits but requires premarket notification for structure/function claims.
Bioavailability varies significantly among supplement forms, influencing their efficacy and duration of action. MK-7 exhibits superior pharmacokinetics, with a plasma half-life of approximately 72 hours, allowing sustained tissue uptake and carboxylation compared to K1's shorter half-life of 1-2 hours, which leads to rapid clearance primarily by the liver. This extended persistence makes MK-7 preferable for long-term supplementation targeting bone mineralization, as evidenced by higher serum levels over 48-144 hours post-dose in clinical studies.

Absorption and Physiology

Gastrointestinal Absorption

Vitamin K, a fat-soluble vitamin, is primarily absorbed in the small intestine, where dietary forms such as phylloquinone (vitamin K1) and menaquinones (vitamin K2) are solubilized by bile salts and pancreatic enzymes to form mixed micelles. These micelles facilitate the transport of vitamin K across the unstirred water layer of the intestinal mucosa, enabling uptake by enterocytes mainly in the jejunum and ileum through passive diffusion or involvement of the Niemann-Pick C1-like 1 (NPC1L1) transporter. Once inside the enterocytes, vitamin K is esterified and incorporated into chylomicrons, which are then secreted into the lymphatic system for delivery to the bloodstream and liver.
The efficiency of gastrointestinal absorption varies by form and dietary context, with phylloquinone from plant sources typically achieving 5-20% bioavailability due to its binding in food matrices like chloroplasts, while menaquinones generally exhibit higher bioavailability than phylloquinone from plant sources, with longer-chain forms like MK-7 showing particularly efficient absorption. Absorption is markedly enhanced by concomitant dietary fats, as lipids promote micelle formation; for instance, adding fat to meals can significantly increase uptake compared to fat-free conditions. Pancreatic enzymes play a crucial role by hydrolyzing dietary lipids to aid micelle assembly, and deficiencies in these enzymes, such as in pancreatic insufficiency, impair the process.
Conditions reducing bile availability, such as cholestasis or biliary obstruction, severely compromise absorption by limiting micelle formation, increasing deficiency risks for fat-soluble vitamins including vitamin K. Additionally, enterohepatic recirculation contributes to vitamin K conservation, whereby a portion of absorbed vitamin K is excreted into bile and reabsorbed in the ileum, helping maintain circulating levels between meals, particularly for bacterially produced menaquinones.

Metabolism and Tissue Distribution

Following absorption in the small intestine via mixed micelles, vitamin K is incorporated into chylomicrons and transported through the lymphatic system to the bloodstream, where it is redistributed primarily via very low-density lipoproteins (VLDL) and low-density lipoproteins (LDL).
In the liver, vitamin K participates in an epoxidation-reduction cycle essential for its recycling. The vitamin K epoxide reductase complex subunit 1 (VKORC1) enzyme catalyzes the reduction of vitamin K 2,3-epoxide to vitamin K quinone and subsequently to the active hydroquinone form. This cycle maintains the availability of reduced vitamin K for physiological processes, with VKORC1 being the primary enzyme responsible in hepatic tissue. Extrahepatically, cytochrome P450 enzymes, particularly CYP4F2 and CYP4F11, mediate the catabolism of vitamin K through -hydroxylation, leading to the formation of hydroxylated metabolites that are further oxidized and excreted, primarily in urine and bile.
Phylloquinone (vitamin K1) exhibits a short plasma half-life of approximately 12 hours, reflecting rapid hepatic uptake and utilization, whereas longer-chain menaquinones like menaquinone-7 (MK-7) have extended half-lives of up to 3 days or more, allowing prolonged circulation. Phylloquinone is preferentially taken up by the liver, comprising the majority of hepatic vitamin K stores, while menaquinones such as MK-4 and MK-7 are directed to extrahepatic tissues, including bones and arterial walls.
Unlike other fat-soluble vitamins, vitamin K lacks substantial long-term body reserves; the total body pool is estimated at 17194 g (mean approximately 88 g), with a rapid turnover rate of about 1.5 days, necessitating consistent dietary intake to maintain adequate levels.

Biochemical Functions

Role in Coagulation and Gla Proteins

Vitamin K plays a central role in blood coagulation by serving as a cofactor for the post-translational modification of specific glutamate residues in vitamin K-dependent proteins, enabling their calcium-binding capacity and subsequent activation in the hemostatic process. This modification, known as gamma-carboxylation, converts glutamate (Glu) to gamma-carboxyglutamate (Gla), which is essential for the procoagulant factors II (prothrombin), VII, IX, and X, as well as the anticoagulant proteins C and S. The reaction is catalyzed by the enzyme gamma-glutamyl carboxylase (GGCX), an integral membrane protein in the endoplasmic reticulum that requires the reduced form of vitamin K (hydroquinone, KH), carbon dioxide (CO), and oxygen (O) as substrates.
The carboxylation mechanism proceeds via a processive reaction where GGCX sequentially modifies multiple Glu residues within the N-terminal Gla domain of these proteins, ensuring efficient activation. For instance, human prothrombin contains 10 Gla residues, while protein C has 9, allowing these proteins to bind calcium ions and phospholipid membranes on activated platelets, which is critical for assembling the coagulation complexes. This process oxidizes vitamin K hydroquinone to its epoxide form (KO), which must be recycled to sustain carboxylation.
The vitamin K cycle maintains the pool of reduced vitamin K through the action of vitamin K epoxide reductase complex subunit 1 (VKORC1), which reduces the epoxide back to the quinone and then to the hydroquinone using electrons from cellular reductants. This recycling is integral to the endoplasmic reticulum membrane and links directly to the broader metabolism of vitamin K in tissues like the liver, where coagulation factors are primarily synthesized. Mutations in VKORC1, such as missense variants, disrupt this reduction step, leading to warfarin resistance in patients requiring higher doses of the anticoagulant to inhibit VKORC1 activity and impair carboxylation.

Functions in Bone and Cardiovascular Health

Vitamin K plays a crucial role in bone health by facilitating the carboxylation of osteocalcin, a non-collagenous protein synthesized by osteoblasts that constitutes about 10-20% of the non-collagenous bone protein. Through its action as a cofactor for -glutamyl carboxylase, vitamin K converts specific glutamic acid residues in osteocalcin to -carboxyglutamic acid (Gla), enabling the protein to bind calcium and hydroxyapatite crystals, which is essential for proper bone mineralization and matrix stabilization. Undercarboxylated osteocalcin (ucOC), resulting from vitamin K deficiency, serves as a reliable biomarker for assessing vitamin K status in bone, with elevated levels associated with reduced bone mineral density and increased fracture risk in observational studies of postmenopausal women and older adults.
In cardiovascular health, vitamin K-dependent carboxylation activates matrix Gla protein (MGP), a potent inhibitor of vascular calcification expressed in vascular smooth muscle cells and chondrocytes. Carboxylated MGP binds to calcium deposits, preventing their accumulation in arterial walls and inhibiting the transdifferentiation of smooth muscle cells into osteoblast-like cells, thereby reducing arterial stiffness and the progression of atherosclerosis. Inactive, undercarboxylated MGP (ucMGP) fails to exert this protective effect, contributing to medial and intimal calcification observed in conditions like chronic kidney disease and aging.
Supplementation with menaquinone-7 (MK-7), a form of vitamin K2, has been shown to enhance the carboxylation of both osteocalcin and MGP, leading to measurable improvements in their active forms. In randomized controlled trials, daily doses of 180-360 g MK-7 for 3-12 months increased carboxylated osteocalcin levels and reduced ucOC by up to 60%, while boosting carboxylated MGP by approximately 70% and decreasing desphospho-uncarboxylated MGP (dp-ucMGP) by 50-86%, depending on dosage. These changes correlate with reduced arterial stiffness, as evidenced by a 7-10% decrease in pulse wave velocity in postmenopausal women. This effect of MK-7 synergizes with vitamin D, which enhances intestinal calcium absorption; together, vitamin D and K2 (particularly MK-7) optimize calcium utilization by directing it to bone via carboxylated osteocalcin for improved mineralization and preventing vascular deposition through carboxylated MGP, thereby providing combined benefits for bone density and cardiovascular health.
Elevated dp-ucMGP levels exceeding 500 pmol/L indicate functional vitamin K insufficiency and are linked to a 2- to 3-fold increased risk of cardiovascular disease events and mortality in cohort studies of adults with and without chronic conditions, independent of traditional risk factors like hypertension and diabetes.

Roles in Plants and Bacteria

In plants and cyanobacteria, phylloquinone (vitamin K1) functions as the secondary electron acceptor A1 within photosystem I (PSI), facilitating forward electron transfer during photosynthesis. Upon excitation of the primary donor P700 to P700*, an electron is rapidly transferred to the primary acceptor A0 (a chlorophyll a molecule), and then to phylloquinone at the A1 site within approximately 30 ps for the A-branch, with the subsequent transfer to Fx occurring on a timescale of about 200 ns. This phylloquinone-mediated step is crucial for efficient electron transport to the terminal iron-sulfur clusters (Fx, Fa, Fb) and ultimately to ferredoxin, preventing charge recombination and supporting the reduction of NADP+. Disruption of phylloquinone biosynthesis impairs this function; for instance, Arabidopsis thaliana mutants lacking the menA gene (essential for the final step in phylloquinone synthesis) exhibit severely reduced PSI activity, lower chlorophyll content, and impaired photosynthetic efficiency, rendering them seedling-lethal under normal light conditions.
In bacteria, menaquinones (forms of vitamin K2) serve as membrane-soluble electron carriers in the respiratory chain, particularly in anaerobic or microaerophilic conditions, shuttling electrons from dehydrogenases to terminal reductases. For example, in Escherichia coli, menaquinone-8 (MK-8) transfers electrons to nitrate reductase A (NarGHI), enabling nitrate respiration as an alternative to oxygen-dependent pathways when oxygen is limited. Menaquinones are indispensable for many Gram-positive bacteria and anaerobically respiring Gram-negative species, where they facilitate two-electron transfers in quinone pools, supporting energy conservation via proton translocation across the membrane. Numerous bacterial species, including those in genera like Bacillus, Lactobacillus, and Enterobacteriaceae, synthesize menaquinones with varying isoprenoid chain lengths (e.g., MK-7 to MK-9) tailored to their respiratory needs.

Deficiency and Recommendations

Risk Factors and Symptoms

Vitamin K deficiency primarily arises from factors impairing its absorption, synthesis, or utilization, with newborns being particularly vulnerable due to limited placental transfer of the vitamin, low maternal stores, and a sterile gut at birth that prevents bacterial production of menaquinones. This results in minimal hepatic reserves in newborns compared to substantially higher levels in adults, heightening the risk of early-onset vitamin K deficiency bleeding (VKDB) within the first 24 hours if exposed to maternal medications like anticonvulsants. In adults, malabsorption syndromes such as celiac disease disrupt fat-soluble vitamin uptake in the small intestine, leading to deficiency in up to 21% of affected individuals, often manifesting as coagulopathy due to impaired bile salt function. Prolonged antibiotic therapy, especially broad-spectrum agents like cephalosporins, can induce deficiency by eradicating vitamin K-producing gut microbiota, as demonstrated in cases where hypoprothrombinemia developed after weeks of treatment despite adequate dietary intake. Similarly, warfarin use antagonizes vitamin K by inhibiting its epoxide reductase (VKORC1), depleting functional reserves and necessitating consistent intake to avoid excessive anticoagulation.
Clinical symptoms of vitamin K deficiency center on impaired coagulation, presenting as hemorrhagic disease characterized by ecchymoses, petechiae, and gastrointestinal bleeding, particularly in the classic VKDB form occurring 1-7 days post-birth. Laboratory findings include prolonged prothrombin time (PT) and international normalized ratio (INR), reflecting reduced activity of vitamin K-dependent clotting factors II, VII, IX, and X, with PT often exceeding 20 seconds in severe cases. In infants, rare but critical manifestations involve neurological complications such as intracranial hemorrhage, affecting up to 50% of late-onset VKDB cases (2-12 weeks) and carrying a 20% mortality risk if untreated.
Prevalence of overt vitamin K deficiency remains low at 1-2% in healthy adults, owing to dietary sources and enterohepatic recirculation, but subclinical insufficiency is more common in the elderly, where up to 42% exhibit low status linked to reduced intake and absorption efficiency. Among older adults, low vitamin K intake below recommended levels affects over 60% of men aged 70 and above, correlating with increased frailty and comorbidity risks. Genetic factors, such as the VKORC1 -1639G>A polymorphism, further elevate sensitivity to vitamin K antagonists like warfarin in 20-30% of Asian populations, where the A allele frequency reaches 22-38%, necessitating lower dosing to prevent over-anticoagulation.

Dietary Recommendations

Dietary recommendations for vitamin K are established as Adequate Intake (AI) levels rather than Recommended Dietary Allowances (RDAs), due to challenges in determining precise requirements beyond maintaining coagulation. In the United States, the AI for adults is 120 mcg per day for men and 90 mcg per day for women, with all forms expressed as phylloquinone (vitamin K1) equivalents. There is no separate RDA for vitamin K2 (menaquinones); recommendations encompass overall vitamin K intake at 90120 mcg per day for adults. These levels are based on the intake needed to ensure normal blood coagulation in healthy individuals. Deficiency affecting blood coagulation is rare in healthy individuals consuming a varied diet.
Recommendations vary by life stage to account for growth and physiological demands. For infants aged 0-6 months, the AI is 2 mcg per day, increasing to 2.5 mcg for 7-12 months; children aged 1-3 years require 30 mcg, 4-8 years 55 mcg, and 9-13 years 60 mcg; for adolescents aged 14-18 years, 75 mcg per day for both males and females; for adults aged 19 years and older, 120 mcg for males and 90 mcg for females, including during pregnancy and lactation without additional increases. There is no Tolerable Upper Intake Level (UL) established for vitamin K, reflecting its low toxicity profile even at higher intakes from food or supplements.
In the European Union, the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) sets an Adequate Intake (AI) of 1 mcg per kg body weight per day for all age and sex groups, equating to approximately 70 mcg per day for average adults, including pregnant and lactating women. In Germany, the Deutsche Gesellschaft fr Ernhrung (DGE) recommends 6080 g per day for adults as an example of international guidelines. Recent post-2020 research has highlighted the potential benefits of vitamin K2 (menaquinone-7, or MK-7) for bone health in adults over 50, with studies suggesting intakes of 45 mcg per day may support bone mineral density, though official guidelines continue to emphasize total vitamin K from dietary sources. As of 2025, official guidelines have not changed, though a 2024 study suggested medium-level intake (65-130 mcg/day) may reduce osteoporosis risk in those over 50.

Diagnosis and Assessment

Diagnosis of vitamin K deficiency typically involves a combination of clinical laboratory tests and biomarkers that assess the functional and nutritional status of vitamin K, focusing on its role in gamma-carboxylation of dependent proteins. Prothrombin time (PT) is a common initial screening test, where prolongation greater than 1.5 times the upper limit of normal, in the presence of normal platelet count and fibrinogen levels, suggests vitamin K deficiency due to impaired synthesis of functional clotting factors II, VII, IX, and X. More severe cases may show an international normalized ratio (INR) exceeding 3.5, which is highly indicative when other causes are excluded.
A more sensitive marker for subclinical deficiency is undercarboxylated prothrombin, also known as protein induced by vitamin K absence-II (PIVKA-II), measured via enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA). Elevated PIVKA-II levels, typically above 2 ng/mL, reflect inadequate carboxylation before overt coagulation changes occur and are particularly useful in at-risk populations like newborns. Plasma vitamin K concentrations provide direct assessment of nutritional status, with normal phylloquinone (vitamin K1) levels ranging from 0.2 to 1.0 ng/mL in individuals consuming adequate dietary amounts; values below this range indicate insufficiency.
For bone health evaluation, the percentage of undercarboxylated osteocalcin (%ucOC) serves as a biomarker, where levels exceeding 20% undercarboxylated osteocalcin signal suboptimal vitamin K status, as carboxylation is essential for its mineralization function. Advanced methods include liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) for precise quantification of menaquinones (vitamin K2 forms) in plasma, enabling differentiation of phylloquinone and menaquinone subtypes with low limits of detection suitable for clinical research.
Functional assays measuring activity levels of vitamin K-dependent clotting factors, such as factors II, VII, IX, and X, offer confirmatory evidence; reduced activity (e.g., below 50% of normal for factor VII, the shortest-lived) correlates with deficiency and helps distinguish it from other coagulopathies. In newborns, PIVKA-II is preferred over PT for early detection of vitamin K insufficiency, as it identifies abnormalities preceding coagulation prolongation and is recommended in guidelines for prophylaxis monitoring.

Medical Uses

Newborn Prophylaxis

Newborns are at risk for vitamin K deficiency bleeding (VKDB) due to limited placental transfer of vitamin K, resulting in plasma levels of vitamin K-dependent clotting factors that are approximately 20-50% of adult values, combined with a sterile gut lacking bacteria that synthesize vitamin K2 and low concentrations in breast milk. Without prophylaxis, the incidence of classical VKDB can reach up to 1.7% (about 1 in 60 births), while late VKDB occurs in 4-7 per 100,000 births.
The standard protocol involves administering a single intramuscular (IM) dose of 0.5-1 mg phylloquinone (vitamin K1) in the thigh within 6 hours of birth, which reduces the risk of late VKDB by 81 times compared to no prophylaxis, nearly eliminates classical VKDB (reducing incidence from up to 1.7% or ~1 in 60 to <1 in 100,000), and provides near-complete protection against late forms. The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) reaffirms this IM approach as the preferred method in its ongoing guidelines, originally established in 1961, supported in the 2022 clinical report, and reaffirmed through 2025.
For parents who decline IM administration, oral prophylaxis is an alternative but less reliable option, typically involving three doses of 2 mg phylloquinone eachat birth, 1 week, and 1 month of age (total 2 mg)though regimens may vary slightly (e.g., at 2-4 weeks and 6-8 weeks). Oral regimens achieve 70-90% efficacy in preventing late VKDB but carry a higher residual risk (up to 1.8 per 100,000) compared to IM, particularly for late-onset cases (4-12 weeks), due to variable absorption and compliance. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) and AAP emphasize IM as the gold standard to ensure protection against all forms of VKDB.

Anticoagulant Management

Vitamin K plays a critical role in the management of anticoagulant therapy with warfarin, a vitamin K antagonist commonly used to prevent thromboembolic events. Warfarin exerts its anticoagulant effect by inhibiting the enzyme vitamin K epoxide reductase complex subunit 1 (VKORC1), which is essential for recycling vitamin K to its active hydroquinone form required for the gamma-carboxylation of coagulation factors II, VII, IX, and X. This inhibition substantially reduces the availability of active vitamin K by approximately 90%, leading to decreased synthesis of functional clotting factors and prolongation of the international normalized ratio (INR). The therapeutic efficacy and safety of warfarin are monitored through regular INR assessments, with a target range of 2.0 to 3.0 for most indications to balance anticoagulation benefits against bleeding risks.
Genetic variations in the VKORC1 gene significantly influence warfarin dosing requirements, accounting for about 30% of the interindividual variability in dose needs. Approximately 30% of patients carry VKORC1 polymorphisms, such as the -1639G>A variant, which can reduce sensitivity to warfarin and necessitate a 20-50% lower dose to achieve the target INR, particularly in individuals with the AA genotype. These variants alter VKORC1 enzyme activity, making it less susceptible to inhibition by warfarin, thus requiring dose adjustments based on pharmacogenetic testing to optimize therapy and minimize adverse events.
In cases of excessive anticoagulation, vitamin K administration is used to reverse warfarin's effects. For asymptomatic patients with minor INR elevations (e.g., 4.5-10.0), low-dose oral phytonadione (1-2.5 mg) is recommended to normalize the INR without causing prolonged warfarin resistance. For active bleeding or urgent reversal, intravenous phytonadione (5-10 mg) is administered, with partial effects typically onsetting within 1-2 hours and full normalization occurring over 6-24 hours, often combined with prothrombin complex concentrates for rapid factor replacement.
Current guidelines emphasize consistent dietary vitamin K intake to maintain stable INR control during warfarin therapy. The 2023 ACC/AHA/ACCP/HRS guidelines advise patients to avoid significant fluctuations in vitamin K consumption, recommending a stable daily intake of around 90-150 mcg to prevent warfarin resistance, which can occur with high intake (>150 mcg/day) leading to subtherapeutic anticoagulation. Excessive vitamin K can counteract warfarin's inhibition of VKORC1, necessitating higher doses and increasing the risk of thrombotic complications.

Poisoning and Overdose Treatment

Poisoning from vitamin K antagonists, particularly long-acting superwarfarins such as brodifacoum found in rodenticides, primarily occurs through accidental or intentional ingestion and leads to coagulopathy by inhibiting vitamin K epoxide reductase complex subunit 1 (VKORC1), disrupting the synthesis of vitamin K-dependent clotting factors. In the United States, approximately 10,000 exposures to superwarfarins are reported annually to poison control centers, with most cases being non-fatal when treated promptly, though coagulopathy develops in 1-5% of accidental exposures. Brodifacoum, the most common superwarfarin, has a prolonged half-life of 20-100 days, necessitating extended therapy compared to shorter-acting antagonists like warfarin.
Treatment focuses on reversing coagulopathy and managing bleeding, starting with discontinuation of the antagonist and administration of high-dose vitamin K1 (phytonadione) to restore clotting factor production. For patients with elevated international normalized ratio (INR) but no bleeding, oral vitamin K1 at 50-100 mg daily is typically initiated, with intravenous (IV) dosing of 10-50 mg used if gastrointestinal absorption is unreliable or for faster onset. In cases of active bleeding or urgent reversal needs, guidelines recommend 4-factor prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC) at a dose of 25-50 units/kg IV, combined with high-dose IV vitamin K1 (5-10 mg) and fresh frozen plasma (FFP) if PCC is unavailable, to rapidly replenish clotting factors. The American Society of Hematology and other expert consensus emphasize PCC over FFP alone for its faster action and lower volume requirements in life-threatening hemorrhage.
Therapy duration for superwarfarin poisoning often extends 2-6 months or longer, guided by serial monitoring of INR or prothrombin time (PT) every 1-2 weeks, with plasma brodifacoum levels measured if available to assess clearance. Once INR normalizes (<1.5), vitamin K1 dosing may be tapered gradually to prevent rebound coagulopathy, and patients require close follow-up to confirm sustained resolution. Supportive care includes blood product transfusions for severe hemorrhage and hospitalization for high-risk cases, with overall prognosis excellent when intervention is timely.

Safety Profile

Side Effects and Toxicity

Vitamin K is generally regarded as safe with a low potential for toxicity, and no tolerable upper intake level (UL) has been established due to the absence of adverse effects observed at high intakes from food or supplements. The National Institutes of Health Office of Dietary Supplements notes that typical supplemental doses of phylloquinone (vitamin K1) up to 10 mg orally or 45 mg/day of menaquinone-4 (vitamin K2) have not been associated with toxicity in clinical studies. The European Food Safety Authority has not established a tolerable upper intake level for vitamin K due to lack of evidence of adverse effects, affirming general safety of supplemental intakes.
Adverse effects are primarily linked to parenteral administration rather than oral intake. Intravenous vitamin K1 has been associated with rare anaphylactoid reactions, occurring at an incidence of approximately 3 per 10,000 doses, attributed to the solubilizing agent Cremophor EL rather than the vitamin itself. These reactions can manifest as flushing, hypotension, or cardiovascular collapse. Oral doses exceeding 1 mg may occasionally cause mild gastrointestinal symptoms such as nausea or facial flushing, though such effects are uncommon and typically resolve without intervention. In neonates, high-dose intravenous vitamin K1 (e.g., 2530 mg) has been linked to hyperbilirubinemia and hemolytic anemia, particularly in premature infants, but standard prophylactic doses of 0.51 mg do not pose this risk.
Excess vitamin K intake carries theoretical risks of interfering with anticoagulant therapy like warfarin by enhancing clotting factor activity, necessitating monitoring of international normalized ratio (INR) levels in affected patients. However, no evidence supports oxidative stress or other cellular damage from phylloquinone or menaquinone intakes below 10 mg/day; in fact, vitamin K exhibits antioxidant properties in some contexts. Vitamin K3 (menadione), a synthetic form, is hepatotoxic at doses exceeding 5 mg/kg body weight, leading to liver damage, and has been banned by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration for use in human supplements due to these risks.

Drug Interactions

Vitamin K interacts with several classes of medications, primarily by influencing coagulation pathways or altering its own absorption and metabolism, which can affect drug efficacy and safety. The most significant interactions occur with oral anticoagulants like warfarin, where vitamin K antagonizes the drug's inhibitory effect on vitamin K-dependent clotting factors. High dietary intake of vitamin K, such as from green leafy vegetables providing approximately 250 mcg daily, can decrease warfarin sensitivity and increase international normalized ratio (INR) variability, potentially requiring dose adjustments to maintain therapeutic anticoagulation. To mitigate this, patients on warfarin are advised to maintain consistent daily vitamin K intake rather than restricting it, as fluctuations pose greater risks to INR stability. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration advises patients on warfarin to adhere to consistent vitamin K intake to avoid INR instability, emphasizing education on dietary sources.
Certain antibiotics, particularly second- and third-generation cephalosporins like cefotetan, can exacerbate vitamin K deficiency by inhibiting vitamin K epoxide reductase complex subunit 1 (VKORC1), the enzyme targeted by warfarin and essential for vitamin K recycling. This inhibition disrupts the vitamin K cycle, leading to reduced carboxylation of clotting factors and synergistic hypoprothrombinemia, especially in patients with marginal vitamin K status or concurrent warfarin use. Prolonged broad-spectrum antibiotic therapy may further contribute to deficiency by altering gut microbiota that produce vitamin K2.
Other medications affect vitamin K absorption in the gastrointestinal tract. Orlistat, a lipase inhibitor used for weight management, reduces dietary fat absorption by about 30%, thereby decreasing the bioavailability of fat-soluble vitamin K and potentially potentiating the anticoagulant effects of warfarin through elevated INR. Similarly, cholestyramine, a bile acid sequestrant for hypercholesterolemia, binds vitamin K in the gut, impairing its absorption and increasing the risk of deficiency-related bleeding, particularly with chronic use.
Additionally, St. John's wort, an herbal supplement, induces cytochrome P450 enzymes (including CYP3A4 and CYP2C9), accelerating the metabolism of warfarin and reducing its plasma levels, thereby decreasing its anticoagulant activity and increasing thrombosis risk.

History and Research

Discovery and Historical Development

In 1929, Danish biochemist Henrik Dam observed that chicks fed diets extracted to remove sterols developed subcutaneous and muscular hemorrhages, along with prolonged blood clotting times, despite the absence of dietary fats; this led to the identification of an anti-hemorrhagic factor in certain fats, later recognized as a fat-soluble vitamin essential for coagulation. Dam's experiments, conducted at the University of Copenhagen's Biochemical Institute from 1928 to 1930, demonstrated that the factor was not cholesterol but a distinct nutrient, prompting further investigations into its role in preventing hemorrhagic diathesis in animals.
The factor was named vitamin K in 1935, with the "K" derived from the Danish and German word for coagulation ("koagulation"); in 1939, Edward A. Doisy and his team at Saint Louis University isolated vitamin K1 (phylloquinone) as a yellow oil from alfalfa meal, elucidating its structure as 2-methyl-3-phytyl-1,4-naphthoquinone. Concurrently, Doisy's group isolated vitamin K2 (menaquinone-4) from putrefied fish meal in 19391940, revealing it as a distinct form produced by bacterial action, while Louis F. Fieser synthesized menadione (vitamin K3), a water-soluble analog lacking the isoprenoid side chain, also in 1939. These advances enabled clinical applications and earned Dam and Doisy the 1943 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for discovering vitamin K and its role in blood coagulation.
Subsequent milestones deepened understanding of vitamin K's biochemical mechanisms; in the 1970s, researchers including Johan Stenflo identified -carboxyglutamic acid (Gla), a vitamin K-dependent post-translational modification of glutamic acid residues in prothrombin and other proteins, essential for calcium binding and biological activity. The vitamin K epoxide reductase complex subunit 1 (VKORC1) gene, critical for recycling vitamin K in its reduced form, was cloned in 2004 by independent teams led by Tong Li and Stefan Rost, revealing genetic variations influencing anticoagulant sensitivity. In the 2020s, studies have linked the gut microbiome to vitamin K2 production, highlighting how bacterial menaquinone synthesis contributes to host vitamin K status and potentially influences health outcomes beyond coagulation.

Current Research Areas

Recent randomized controlled trials (RCTs) have investigated the role of menaquinone-7 (MK-7), a form of vitamin K2, in osteoporosis management, particularly among postmenopausal women. In a three-year RCT involving 244 healthy postmenopausal women, daily supplementation with 180 mcg of MK-7 was associated with reduced bone loss and improved carboxylation of osteocalcin, a vitamin K-dependent protein essential for bone mineralization. A 2025 meta-analysis of RCTs further confirmed that vitamin K supplementation, including MK-7, enhances bone mineral density in middle-aged and older adults, with more pronounced effects in women, potentially reducing fracture risk through improved bone quality. These findings build on earlier evidence, highlighting MK-7's potential to address postmenopausal bone fragility by activating Gla proteins in bone tissue.
In cardiovascular research, vitamin K2 supplementation has shown promise in mitigating arterial stiffness and coronary artery calcification (CAC). Clinical trials indicate that vitamin K2 slows CAC progression, with some studies reporting reductions in calcification scores through inhibition of vascular smooth muscle cell mineralization. Desphospho-uncarboxylated Matrix Gla Protein (dp-ucMGP), an inactive form of the vitamin K-dependent protein Matrix Gla Protein, serves as a biomarker for vascular health, with elevated levels predicting increased cardiovascular risk; vitamin K2 supplementation reduces dp-ucMGP levels, supporting its role in preventing arterial calcification. A 2024 study on combined vitamin K2 and vitamin D supplementation demonstrated beneficial effects on CAC progression in at-risk populations, underscoring the need for larger trials to quantify long-term outcomes.
Emerging research explores vitamin K's broader therapeutic applications, including gut microbiome modulation. A 2025 analysis of NHANES data indicated associations between vitamin K intake and reduced inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) risk in certain subgroups. In oncology, vitamin K exhibits anticancer potential through inhibition of vitamin K epoxide reductase (VKOR), a key enzyme in the vitamin K cycle; downregulation of VKORC1L1 activates ferroptosis pathways, suppressing tumor growth in models of pancreatic and prostate cancers. A 2024 study demonstrated that a vitamin K precursor induces oxidative stress in prostate cancer cells, inhibiting proliferation via PI3K/AKT signaling disruption. Regarding infectious diseases, retrospective analyses link vitamin K deficiency to worsened COVID-19 outcomes, including coagulopathy and thrombosis, with low status correlating to higher disease severity and a synergistic effect with vitamin D deficiency on mortality risk.
Ongoing studies emphasize distinctions among vitamin K2 subtypes, particularly longer-chain menaquinones produced in fermented foods. Research indicates that microbial fermentation can produce various menaquinones, with subtypes like MK-7 in natto prompting investigations into their production and health impacts. These efforts address prior gaps in understanding subtype-specific roles, particularly in microbiome interactions and chronic disease prevention.
