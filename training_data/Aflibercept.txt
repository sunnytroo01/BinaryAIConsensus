Aflibercept
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Aflibercept is a recombinant fusion protein engineered as a soluble decoy receptor, comprising the second immunoglobulin domain of human vascular endothelial growth factor receptor 1 (VEGFR1) and the third domain of VEGFR2 fused to the Fc portion of human immunoglobulin G1 (IgG1), which binds and neutralizes vascular endothelial growth factor A (VEGF-A), VEGF-B, and placental growth factor (PlGF) with higher affinity than their natural receptors, thereby inhibiting VEGF-mediated angiogenesis, vascular permeability, and endothelial cell proliferation.
Developed by Regeneron Pharmaceuticals and marketed intravitrealy as Eylea (2 mg or 8 mg formulations), aflibercept received initial U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approval in 2011 for neovascular (wet) age-related macular degeneration (AMD) following demonstration of noninferior visual acuity gains compared to ranibizumab in the phase 3 VIEW trials, with subsequent approvals for diabetic macular edema (DME), macular edema secondary to retinal vein occlusion (RVO), myopic choroidal neovascularization, diabetic retinopathy, and retinopathy of prematurity based on trials such as VISTA, VIVID, and PHOTON showing sustained improvements in best-corrected visual acuity and reduction in central retinal thickness. The 8 mg high-dose variant (Eylea HD), approved in 2023, enables extended dosing intervals up to every 12-16 weeks for wet AMD and DME, as evidenced by the PULSAR and PHOTON2 trials, which reported comparable efficacy to the 2 mg dose with potentially fewer injections while maintaining safety profiles akin to standard anti-VEGF therapies, though risks include endophthalmitis, retinal detachment, and increased intraocular pressure from intravitreal administration.
Intravenously as Zaltrap, aflibercept is approved in combination with FOLFIRI for metastatic colorectal cancer refractory to oxaliplatin-containing therapy, where the VELOUR trial showed a modest overall survival benefit of 1.4 months over placebo, though its use has been limited by toxicities such as hypertension, proteinuria, and arterial thromboembolic events, reflecting broader challenges in systemic VEGF inhibition. Biosimilars, including interchangeable versions approved by the FDA since 2024 and 2 mg formulations available for NHS use in England as of February 2026, have demonstrated pharmacokinetic equivalence and similar efficacy in neovascular AMD and other indications, potentially enhancing accessibility in multiple healthcare systems amid ongoing debates over anti-VEGF treatment burdens like injection frequency and real-world durability.

Clinical Applications

Approved Indications

Aflibercept, formulated for intravitreal injection and marketed as Eylea, received initial FDA approval on November 18, 2011, for the treatment of neovascular (wet) age-related macular degeneration (AMD) in adults. Subsequent approvals expanded its indications to include macular edema following retinal vein occlusion (RVO) on October 14, 2014; diabetic macular edema (DME) on March 23, 2015; diabetic retinopathy (DR) in patients with DME on May 10, 2019; and retinopathy of prematurity (ROP) in preterm infants on February 8, 2023. These approvals are based on clinical trials demonstrating efficacy in inhibiting vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) to reduce retinal neovascularization and edema.
In oncology, aflibercept is approved by the FDA as ziv-aflibercept (Zaltrap), a systemic formulation, in combination with 5-fluorouracil, leucovorin, and irinotecan (FOLFIRI) for the treatment of metastatic colorectal cancer (mCRC) that is resistant to or has progressed following an oxaliplatin-containing regimen; this approval was granted on August 3, 2012.
The European Medicines Agency (EMA) has approved Eylea for similar ophthalmic indications as the FDA, including wet AMD, macular edema secondary to RVO, and DME in adults, plus visual impairment due to myopic choroidal neovascularization (mCNV) since August 2017 and ROP in preterm infants since February 2022. No oncology indication is approved under Eylea in the EU, where Zaltrap holds the mCRC approval.

Efficacy Evidence from Trials

In the phase 3 VIEW1 and VIEW2 trials evaluating intravitreal aflibercept for neovascular age-related macular degeneration, aflibercept 2 mg dosed monthly or every 2 months after three initial monthly doses met the primary endpoint of noninferiority to monthly ranibizumab 0.5 mg, with mean best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA) gains of 8.1 to 10.9 letters from baseline at 52 weeks across treatment arms. At 96 weeks, aflibercept 2 mg every 8 weeks after loading achieved comparable BCVA improvements to monthly ranibizumab (+7.9 to +9.4 letters versus +8.9 to +9.7 letters), requiring on average 5 fewer injections over two years. Approximately 95% of patients in both aflibercept and ranibizumab groups maintained vision (losing fewer than 15 letters), with similar reductions in central retinal thickness.
For diabetic macular edema, the phase 3 VIVID and VISTA trials demonstrated aflibercept 2 mg's superiority over macular laser photocoagulation, with mean BCVA gains of 10.5 to 12.5 letters at 52 weeks for aflibercept every 4 or 8 weeks versus 0.2 to 1.2 letters for laser (P<0.01). At 148 weeks, sustained gains were observed (+11.7 to +12.8 letters for aflibercept versus +5.0 to +9.8 letters for laser), alongside greater reductions in central subfield thickness (approximately 200-250 m versus 100-150 m). Aflibercept also improved diabetic retinopathy severity scores in post-hoc analyses, with 32-40% of eyes showing 2-step improvement at 100 weeks compared to 15-23% with laser.

Comparative Effectiveness

In neovascular age-related macular degeneration, phase 3 VIEW 1 and VIEW 2 trials established non-inferiority of intravitreal aflibercept (2 mg monthly or every 2 months after three initial monthly doses) to ranibizumab (0.5 mg monthly) for mean best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA) improvement at 52 weeks, with gains of approximately 8-10 Early Treatment Diabetic Retinopathy Study (ETDRS) letters in both arms across diverse populations (VIEW 1: North/South America, Asia; VIEW 2: Europe, Asia, Australia). Safety profiles were comparable, with low rates of ocular and systemic adverse events.
For diabetic macular edema, the DRCR Retina Network Protocol T trial (n=660 eyes) demonstrated superior BCVA gains with aflibercept (2 mg every 4 weeks after five initial monthly doses) over bevacizumab (1.25 mg) and ranibizumab (0.3 mg) at 1 year, particularly in subgroups with baseline BCVA of 20/50 or worse (mean improvement: +18.0 letters for aflibercept vs. +13.3 for ranibizumab and +10.0 for bevacizumab ). This advantage persisted at 2 years in eyes starting with poorer vision, though overall differences narrowed with protocol-specified dosing adjustments. Bevacizumab, used off-label intravitreally despite FDA approval only for systemic oncology, showed inferior central subfield thickness reductions compared to aflibercept.

In macular edema due to retinal vein occlusion, randomized trials and comparative analyses indicate aflibercept yields BCVA improvements similar to ranibizumab, with no significant differences in mean vision change at 6-12 months, though direct head-to-head data remain limited and often derived from network meta-analyses. Network meta-analyses across retinal indications affirm overall comparable efficacy among anti-VEGF agents, with aflibercept showing potential edges in anatomical endpoints like pigment epithelial detachment regression or fluid resolution due to its decoy receptor design binding VEGF-A, VEGF-B, and placental growth factor. Aflibercept's longer intravitreal half-life supports extended dosing intervals (e.g., every 8-12 weeks post-loading) versus monthly regimens for ranibizumab, potentially reducing injection burden without compromising outcomes in responders.

Pharmacology and Administration

Mechanism of Action

Aflibercept is a recombinant, dimeric fusion protein comprising the second immunoglobulin-like domain of human vascular endothelial growth factor receptor 1 (VEGFR1) and the third immunoglobulin-like domain of human VEGFR2, each fused to the Fc portion of human immunoglobulin G1 (IgG1). This structure yields a glycoprotein with a protein core molecular weight of approximately 97 kDa, including about 15% carbohydrate by weight, enabling high-affinity ligand binding and prolonged serum half-life via Fc-mediated recycling.
As a soluble decoy receptor, aflibercept sequesters vascular endothelial growth factor-A (VEGF-A), VEGF-B, and placental growth factor (PlGF) with dissociation constants in the subpicomolar to picomolar range, exceeding the affinities of native VEGFRs and other anti-VEGF agents like ranibizumab (Kd 46 pM for VEGF-A) and bevacizumab (Kd 1.6 nM). By forming stable complexes with these ligands in a 1:1 stoichiometry via its dual-domain arms, aflibercept prevents their dimerization and binding to endogenous VEGFR1 and VEGFR2 on endothelial cells.
This inhibition disrupts VEGF signaling cascades, particularly blocking VEGF-A-induced autophosphorylation and dimerization of VEGFR2, which halts downstream activation of tyrosine kinases and pathways such as PI3K/Akt and MAPK/ERK that promote endothelial cell proliferation, migration, survival, and vascular permeability. Consequently, aflibercept suppresses pathological angiogenesis, neovascularization, and vascular leakage in conditions driven by VEGF overexpression, including retinal disorders and solid tumors. Unlike monoclonal antibodies targeting only VEGF-A isoforms, aflibercept's broader ligand specificity, including PlGF, may potentiate anti-angiogenic effects by countering compensatory angiogenesis pathways.

Pharmacokinetics and Dosing

Aflibercept is administered exclusively via intravitreal injection to achieve localized therapeutic concentrations within the vitreous humor and retina, with minimal systemic exposure due to its high molecular weight and binding affinity for vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF). Following intravitreal administration of 2 mg, peak concentrations in the aqueous humor reach approximately 122 mg/L, while systemic plasma levels of free aflibercept remain low, typically below the limit of quantification in most patients. The intraocular half-life of free aflibercept in humans is estimated at 11 days in aqueous humor, though vitreous half-life data from rabbit models indicate approximately 3.92 days, reflecting species differences in ocular clearance.
Distribution of aflibercept is primarily confined to the eye, where it binds endogenous VEGF-A, VEGF-B, and placental growth factor (PlGF) with high affinity, forming stable complexes that inhibit angiogenic signaling. Systemic distribution, when detectable, yields a volume of approximately 7 L following intravenous administration in non-ocular studies, but intravitreal dosing limits plasma penetration. Metabolism occurs via proteolysis into small peptides and amino acids, alongside target-mediated elimination through VEGF binding, which sequesters the drug and reduces free circulating levels. Excretion involves renal clearance of proteolytic fragments, with the terminal plasma half-life of free aflibercept estimated at 5 to 6 days post-intravitreal injection.
Dosing regimens for aflibercept (as Eylea) vary by indication but follow an initial loading phase of monthly injections to suppress VEGF activity, transitioning to extended intervals based on clinical response and anatomical improvements observed via optical coherence tomography. For neovascular age-related macular degeneration, the recommended dose is 2 mg (0.05 mL) intravitreal every 4 weeks for the first 3 doses (12 weeks), followed by 2 mg every 8 weeks; after 12 months of stable response, some patients may extend to every 12 weeks, though this is less effective than 8-week maintenance. In diabetic macular edema, dosing initiates with 2 mg monthly for 5 injections, then every 8 weeks or as guided by physician assessment. A higher-dose formulation (Eylea HD, 8 mg) supports extended intervals up to 12-16 weeks after initial loading, approved for wet AMD and diabetic retinopathy to reduce injection frequency while maintaining efficacy. All administrations require aseptic technique by qualified ophthalmologists, with no adjustments for renal or hepatic impairment due to predominant local action.

Formulation and Composition

Aflibercept is a recombinant fusion protein consisting of the second immunoglobulin (Ig) domain of human vascular endothelial growth factor receptor 1 (VEGFR1) and the third Ig domain of human VEGFR2, fused to the Fc portion of human immunoglobulin G1 (IgG1). This structure forms a soluble decoy receptor that binds vascular endothelial growth factors (VEGFs) with high affinity. The protein is produced via recombinant DNA technology in Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells, resulting in a dimeric glycoprotein with a core protein molecular weight of 97 kilodaltons (kDa) and total molecular weight of approximately 115 kDa, including about 15% glycosylation. 
In its primary ophthalmic formulation, marketed as Eylea, aflibercept is supplied as a sterile, preservative-free, clear, colorless to pale yellow aqueous solution at a concentration of 40 mg/mL for intravitreal injection. Each single-use glass vial or pre-filled syringe delivers 0.05 mL containing 2 mg of aflibercept, with excipients including 10 mM sodium phosphate buffer, 40 mM sodium chloride, 5% (w/v) sucrose, and 0.03% (w/v) polysorbate 20, adjusted to pH 6.2. A higher-dose variant, Eylea HD, provides 8 mg (0.07 mL of 114.3 mg/mL solution) in a similar buffered formulation for extended dosing intervals.
For intravenous use in oncology, as in Zaltrap, aflibercept is formulated at 25 mg/mL in a sterile solution containing sucrose, monosodium phosphate monohydrate, disodium phosphate heptahydrate, polysorbate 20, and water for injection, with no preservatives, supplied in 4 mL or 8 mL single-use vials (100 mg or 200 mg doses) at pH 6.2. These formulations ensure stability and bioavailability suited to their respective routes of administration, with the ophthalmic version optimized for low-volume intravitreal delivery to minimize intraocular pressure changes.

Safety Profile

Adverse Effects

The most common adverse reactions associated with intravitreal aflibercept, reported in 5% of patients across pooled clinical trials (n=2980), are ocular and include conjunctival hemorrhage (25-31%), eye pain (4-13%), cataract (7-19%), vitreous detachment, vitreous floaters, and increased intraocular pressure. In the VIEW1 and VIEW2 trials for neovascular age-related macular degeneration (n=1824 patients over 96 weeks), conjunctival hemorrhage occurred in 25-27% of aflibercept-treated patients, eye pain in 9-10%, and cataract in 7-13%. Similarly, in the VIVID and VISTA trials for diabetic macular edema (n=578 patients over 100 weeks), conjunctival hemorrhage rates reached 28-31% and cataract 8-19%.
Serious adverse reactions, occurring in <1% of patients, include hypersensitivity, retinal tear, and endophthalmitis, with injection-procedure-related events such as endophthalmitis or retinal detachment reported in <0.1% of intravitreal injections. Postmarketing surveillance has identified retinal vasculitis, with or without occlusion, at rates of 0.6 and 0.2 per million injections, respectively. Increased intraocular pressure may necessitate glaucoma management, and patients require monitoring before and after each injection, as transient elevations can occur.
Non-ocular adverse events are less frequent but include arterial thromboembolic events, observed at 3.3% in diabetic macular edema trials over 52 weeks, though rates are comparable to those in anti-VEGF comparator arms or untreated controls in class-wide studies. In trials for conditions like branch retinal vein occlusion (VIBRANT, n=91) and retinopathy of prematurity (BUTTERFLEYE/FIREFLEYE, n=168), additional events included retinal detachment (5-6%) and conjunctival hemorrhage (5-9% to 20%). No new safety signals have emerged with higher-dose formulations (e.g., 8 mg) compared to the standard 2 mg dose in phase 3 evaluations.

Contraindications and Warnings

Aflibercept intravitreal injection is contraindicated in patients with known hypersensitivity to aflibercept or any excipients in the formulation, as hypersensitivity reactions may manifest as severe intraocular inflammation. It is also contraindicated in the presence of ocular or periocular infections, which could exacerbate infectious risks during intravitreal administration. Active intraocular inflammation represents an additional contraindication for intravitreal use, due to potential worsening of inflammatory processes.
For intravenous ziv-aflibercept (used in oncology), no absolute contraindications are specified in the prescribing information, though use is cautioned in patients with recent severe hemorrhage.
Key warnings for intravitreal aflibercept include the risk of endophthalmitis and retinal detachments associated with intravitreal injections, necessitating strict aseptic technique and patient monitoring for symptoms such as eye pain or vision changes. Acute increases in intraocular pressure may occur shortly after injection, requiring measurement of IOP and potential management with medications in affected patients. Systemic exposure carries warnings for arterial thromboembolic events, such as stroke or myocardial infarction, particularly in patients with cardiovascular risk factors; venous thromboembolic events have also been observed.
Intravenous ziv-aflibercept warnings emphasize an increased incidence of severe hemorrhage, including gastrointestinal and intracranial events, sometimes fatal, with rates up to 3% for severe cases in clinical trials. Other critical risks include gastrointestinal perforation (1-2% incidence), impaired wound healing (discontinue at least 4 weeks prior to elective surgery), severe hypertension requiring monitoring and antihypertensive therapy, and proteinuria/nephrotic syndrome, with discontinuation recommended if nephrotic syndrome develops. Arterial thromboembolic events occur at higher rates compared to controls, mandating caution in patients with recent events.

Drug Interactions

No dedicated pharmacokinetic drug-drug interaction studies have been conducted for aflibercept in either its intravitreal or intravenous formulations.
For the intravenous formulation (ziv-aflibercept), population pharmacokinetic analyses and cross-study comparisons with concomitant chemotherapies used in approved regimens, such as irinotecan and its active metabolite SN-38 or fluorouracil, revealed no clinically important pharmacokinetic interactions. Following intravitreal administration, systemic exposure to aflibercept is minimal, rendering systemic pharmacokinetic interactions negligible.
Pharmacodynamic interactions remain a consideration due to aflibercept's VEGF inhibition, which impairs angiogenesis and hemostasis. Concomitant use with anticoagulants (e.g., warfarin), antiplatelet agents (e.g., aspirin, clopidogrel), or nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs may potentiate the risk of hemorrhage, including severe gastrointestinal or intracranial bleeding observed in clinical trials of the intravenous form, where hemorrhagic events occurred in up to 3% of patients. Similarly, combination with other anti-VEGF agents or anti-angiogenic therapies could exacerbate risks of hypertension, proteinuria, or impaired wound healing, though specific interaction data are limited to class effects rather than direct studies. Monitoring for additive toxicity is recommended in polypharmacy settings, particularly in oncology where ziv-aflibercept is combined with cytotoxic agents. No alcohol/food interactions or significant interactions with CYP450 substrates have been identified.

Development and Regulatory History

Preclinical and Early Development

Aflibercept, a recombinant fusion protein also known as VEGF Trap, was engineered by Regeneron Pharmaceuticals in the early 2000s using proprietary trap technology that fuses extracellular ligand-binding domains from VEGF receptors to the Fc region of human immunoglobulin G1. Specifically, it incorporates domain 2 from VEGFR1 and domain 3 from VEGFR2, enabling it to act as a soluble decoy receptor that binds VEGF-A isoforms, VEGF-B, and placental growth factor (PlGF) with dissociation constants as low as 0.5 pMapproximately 100-fold higher affinity than the native VEGFR1 receptor. This design sequesters these ligands, preventing their interaction with endothelial cell receptors and thereby inhibiting angiogenesis, vascular permeability, and associated pathologies.
Preclinical investigations, initiated around 20022003, demonstrated aflibercept's efficacy in multiple animal models. In oncology-focused studies, systemic VEGF Trap suppressed tumor growth and metastasis in xenograft models by blocking VEGF-mediated neovascularization. For ocular indications, the intravitreal formulation (VEGF Trap-Eye) was optimized through iterative modifications, such as truncating heparin-binding domains to neutralize negative charge and enhance intraocular half-life from hours to days. In laser-induced choroidal neovascularization models in rabbits and monkeyspathophysiologically akin to wet age-related macular degeneration (AMD)VEGF Trap-Eye dose-dependently reduced lesion size by over 50% and vascular leakage, outperforming unmodified traps. Similar results were observed in rodent oxygen-induced retinopathy models, where it regressed retinal neovascularization without evident toxicity in repeat-dose toxicology studies supporting investigational new drug applications.
Early development transitioned to human trials in the mid-2000s, with phase 1 studies of systemic VEGF Trap for solid tumors commencing by 2003 to evaluate safety and pharmacokinetics. Ocular adaptation accelerated thereafter; preclinical data supported intravitreal dosing tolerability, leading to phase 1/2 trials for wet AMD by 20052006, which confirmed biological activity via reduced macular thickness and stabilized vision in small cohorts. Regeneron filed for phase 2 initiation in early 2006, dosing up to 2 mg intravitreally, with results showing sustained VEGF suppression and anatomic improvements that informed subsequent phase 3 protocols. Parallel oncology efforts advanced aflibercept (as ziv-aflibercept) into phase 2 by 2005, highlighting its broad anti-angiogenic potential before regulatory focus shifted toward ophthalmic approval in 2011.

Key Clinical Trials and Approvals

Aflibercept received its initial U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approval on November 18, 2011, for the treatment of neovascular (wet) age-related macular degeneration (AMD), supported by the parallel phase 3 VIEW1 and VIEW2 trials involving over 2,400 patients. These double-masked, active-controlled studies compared intravitreal aflibercept 2 mg dosed monthly or every 8 weeks (after three initial monthly doses) against monthly ranibizumab 0.5 mg, with the primary endpoint of non-inferiority in mean best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA) change from baseline at 52 weeks. Both trials met this endpoint, showing mean BCVA improvements of 8.1 to 10.9 letters across aflibercept arms, comparable to ranibizumab's 8.1-10.7 letters, while the every-8-weeks regimen required approximately 5 fewer injections over two years without compromising efficacy. At 96 weeks, 95% of aflibercept-treated patients maintained vision, with sustained anatomic improvements including reduced central retinal thickness by 130-150 m.
For diabetic macular edema (DME), FDA approval came on March 23, 2015, following the VISTA and VIVID phase 3 trials, which enrolled 872 patients and compared aflibercept 2 mg monthly or every 8 weeks (after five initial monthly doses) to macular laser photocoagulation. Both studies demonstrated superiority over laser, with mean BCVA gains of 12.5 letters (VISTA) and 10.5-10.7 letters (VIVID) at 52 weeks for aflibercept versus 0.2-1.2 letters for laser; at 148 weeks, gains were sustained at 11.7-11.8 letters versus 6.3-6.6 letters. Anatomic outcomes included greater reductions in central subfield thickness, with aflibercept achieving dry retinas in higher proportions. These results supported later expansion on May 13, 2019, to include diabetic retinopathy in patients with DME.
Approval for macular edema secondary to retinal vein occlusion (RVO) occurred in October 2014 for central RVO (CRVO) and March 2015 for branch RVO (BRVO), primarily based on the COPERNICUS and GALILEO trials for CRVO (n=217), which showed aflibercept 2 mg monthly yielding mean BCVA gains of 16.5-17.3 letters at 24 weeks versus -1.8 to 1.8 letters for sham, with 56.1-60.2% gaining 15 letters. For BRVO, supportive data from a 52-week open-label study (n=30) reported mean BCVA improvements of 17.4 letters and 79% gaining 15 letters with 2 mg aflibercept every 4-8 weeks. Subsequent approvals include the 8 mg high-dose formulation (Eylea HD) in August 2023 for wet AMD and DME, backed by PULSAR and PHOTON trials demonstrating non-inferiority to 2 mg with extended dosing up to 16-24 weeks. The European Medicines Agency granted initial approval in 2012 for wet AMD, with subsequent expansions mirroring FDA indications.

Brand Names and Manufacturers

Aflibercept is commercially available under the brand name Eylea (aflibercept injection 2 mg for intravitreal use) for treating conditions such as wet age-related macular degeneration, diabetic macular edema, and retinal vein occlusion; it is developed and manufactured by Regeneron Pharmaceuticals, Inc., which retains exclusive U.S. rights, while Bayer AG holds licensing and marketing rights outside the U.S., where the companies share profits equally. A higher-dose formulation, Eylea HD (aflibercept injection 8 mg), approved by the FDA on August 17, 2023, follows the same development and commercialization structure, enabling extended dosing intervals up to every 16 or 24 weeks.
For intravenous administration in metastatic colorectal cancer, aflibercept is marketed as Zaltrap (ziv-aflibercept injection), co-developed by Regeneron Pharmaceuticals, Inc., and Sanofi; Sanofi handles manufacturing and co-commercialization in the U.S., with Regeneron providing the biologic under a collaboration agreement established prior to its FDA approval on August 3, 2012. Biosimilars such as Yesafili (aflibercept-jbvf) and Opuviz (aflibercept-yszy), approved by the FDA in May 2024, are produced by Biocon Biologics and Samsung Bioepis, respectively, but reference the Eylea originator without altering the primary brand structures.

Commercial and Societal Impact

Legal Status and Patents

Aflibercept is approved for medical use in the United States as a prescription biologic, with initial FDA approval granted on November 18, 2011, for intravitreal treatment of neovascular age-related macular degeneration in adults. Subsequent FDA approvals expanded indications to include macular edema following retinal vein occlusion (2012), diabetic macular edema (2014), diabetic retinopathy (2019), and retinopathy of prematurity (2023). In the European Union, the European Medicines Agency granted marketing authorization on November 22, 2012, for similar ophthalmic indications, with the approval valid across all EU member states. Regulatory approvals have also been obtained in Japan, Canada, Australia, and other markets, positioning aflibercept as a controlled prescription medication requiring physician administration via intravitreal injection.
Regeneron Pharmaceuticals holds a portfolio of patents protecting aflibercept, including composition-of-matter claims that expired in the United States in 2023, followed by the end of 12-year regulatory exclusivity on May 18, 2024. Secondary patents, such as those covering formulations and manufacturing processes, extend protection through June 2027 in the US and EU, with additional patents listed as late as 2039-2040. These have formed the basis for Biologics Price Competition and Innovation Act (BPCIA) litigations against biosimilar entrants, including injunctions upheld by the Federal Circuit to block premature launches.
Patent enforcement has delayed biosimilar market entry despite FDA approvals for versions like Enzeevu (Sandoz) in May 2024; a September 2025 settlement with Regeneron allows US launch no earlier than Q4 2026. Similar resolutions with Celltrion (October 2025) and Formycon permit launches from December 2026, while disputes with Amgen and others continue over alleged infringement of patents expiring beyond 2030. In the EU, biosimilars such as Mynzepli (Alvotech/Advanz) received approval in August 2025 but await patent expiry around 2027 for commercialization. These protections, enforced amid claims of up to 46 infringed patents, underscore Regeneron's strategy to maintain exclusivity despite core patent lapses.

Biosimilars Development

Development of biosimilars for aflibercept, the active ingredient in Regeneron's Eylea, accelerated following the expiration of key European patents in 2022 and U.S. exclusivity periods around 20232024, prompting multiple pharmaceutical companies to pursue regulatory approvals through abbreviated pathways demonstrating similarity in structure, function, and clinical performance. Biosimilar candidates underwent physicochemical characterization, non-clinical testing for binding affinity to vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) receptors, and confirmatory clinical trials, often focusing on pharmacokinetic equivalence and efficacy in neovascular age-related macular degeneration (nAMD) models rather than large-scale superiority studies.
Key developers include Biocon Biologics, which received U.S. FDA approval for Yesafili (aflibercept-kzzy) on May 21, 2024, marking an early entry into the ophthalmology market with indications matching the reference product for retinal diseases. Sandoz followed with FDA approval of Enzeevu (aflibercept-abzv) on December 8, 2024, after resolving patent disputes with Regeneron via settlement, enabling U.S. launch post-licensing date. Celltrion's Eydenzelt (aflibercept-boav) gained FDA approval on October 9, 2025, supported by trials confirming comparable visual acuity outcomes and safety profiles in patients with macular degeneration. In Europe, the EMA approved six aflibercept biosimilars by February 2025, including Alteogen's Eyluxvi (ALT-L9) on September 17, 2025, for wet AMD and other VEGF-mediated conditions, and Advanz Pharma's Mynzepli in August 2025 following positive CHMP opinion.

The originator Eylea remains available, though biosimilars are preferred where appropriate.

Economics and Market Dynamics

Aflibercept, marketed primarily as Eylea by Regeneron Pharmaceuticals in collaboration with Bayer, generated approximately $6 billion in U.S. net product sales for Eylea and Eylea HD combined in 2024, reflecting its dominant position in the anti-vascular endothelial growth factor (anti-VEGF) segment for retinal diseases. Globally, the aflibercept market was valued at around $8.79 billion in 2024, driven by demand for treatments in wet age-related macular degeneration (AMD), diabetic macular edema (DME), and retinal vein occlusion (RVO). In the first quarter of 2025, U.S. sales reached $1 billion, while international sales (primarily through Bayer) totaled $857.8 million, underscoring sustained revenue despite emerging competition.
Pricing for intravitreal injections of Eylea remains high, with U.S. list prices exceeding $1,800 per dose, contributing to its substantial gross margins for Regeneron, though actual net prices are lower after rebates and discounts. In Europe, prices are comparable but subject to national reimbursement negotiations, such as full coverage in France for AMD and DME indications as of early 2025. This pricing structure supports aflibercept's market leadership, where it holds the largest share among ophthalmology anti-VEGF agents, outpacing ranibizumab (Lucentis) and off-label bevacizumab (Avastin), the latter costing under $50 per dose but lacking formal approval for ocular use.
Market dynamics are shifting due to U.S. patent exclusivity expiration on May 17, 2024, enabling biosimilar entry and projected to erode originator sales, with the aflibercept biosimilars market expected to grow from $1.68 billion in 2025 to $3.7 billion by 2034 at a 9.14% CAGR. Regeneron's launch of Eylea HD (8 mg dose) in 2023, generating $504 million in the first half of 2024, aims to counter this by offering extended dosing intervals, potentially preserving pricing power amid biosimilar pressure and competition from lower-cost alternatives. Overall, aflibercept's economics benefit from high barriers to entry in biologics manufacturing and physician familiarity, though long-term revenue growth may moderate as biosimilars capture share in cost-sensitive markets.
In the United Kingdom, NHS England has implemented switches to biosimilar aflibercept 2 mg as part of its framework for best-value biological medicines. As of February 2026, multiple biosimilars including Vgenfli, Eydenzelt, Yesafili, and Afqlir are available for NHS use in England under regional frameworks and are licensed for all indications of the originator Eylea except retinopathy of prematurity. Switches from Eylea to these biosimilars began in late 2025, with biosimilars preferred to achieve cost savings while the originator remains available; clinical evidence from phase 3 studies supports similar efficacy, safety, and immunogenicity. This real-world adoption exemplifies cost-reduction strategies in public healthcare systems through biosimilar utilization, aligning with NHS England's priorities to optimize resources and reinvest savings into patient care.

Ongoing Research

Emerging Indications

In 2023, intravitreal aflibercept received U.S. Food and Drug Administration approval as the first pharmacologic treatment for retinopathy of prematurity (ROP) in preterm infants with zone I, stage 3+, or zone II, stage 3+ ROP involving the posterior retina, based on results from the phase 3 BUTTERFLEYE trial (NCT04004208). The randomized, controlled study enrolled 118 infants and compared a single 0.4 mg dose of aflibercept to laser photocoagulation, demonstrating superior structural and functional outcomes with aflibercept: treatment failure occurred in 4 of 28 eyes (14.3%) in the aflibercept group versus 16 of 39 eyes (45.9%) in the laser group at week 52 (difference, -32.1%; 95% CI, -50.5 to -13.7; P = .001). Two-year follow-up data confirmed sustained benefits, with aflibercept showing lower rates of unfavorable structural outcomes (26.8% vs 53.8%) and visual acuity worse than 20/200 (4.5% vs 20.5%), alongside a comparable safety profile despite concerns over potential systemic VEGF suppression in preterm infants.
Ongoing research includes observational studies evaluating real-world use of aflibercept in ROP, focusing on recurrence rates, neurodevelopmental impacts, and long-term ocular outcomes in diverse preterm populations. For instance, comparative trials have explored aflibercept against ranibizumab, reporting lower recurrence rates (14.3% vs 28.6% at 6 months) and extended reactivation-free intervals with aflibercept in zone II ROP, though larger datasets are needed to confirm durability. Investigational applications extend to adjunctive roles in neovascular glaucoma, where aflibercept has shown preliminary reductions in iris and angle neovascularization when combined with anti-glaucoma therapies, but randomized phase 3 evidence remains lacking for approval.
In oncology, limited phase 3 activity persists beyond approved metastatic colorectal cancer use, with historical trials like VELOUR confirming modest overall survival benefits (13.50 vs 12.06 months; HR 0.817; P = .0032) in bevacizumab-refractory patients, but no new indications have advanced to late-stage validation amid challenges in broader VEGF inhibition efficacy. Early explorations in other VEGF-driven conditions, such as corneal neovascularization, report anatomical improvements but await confirmatory trials for clinical adoption.

Higher-Dose Formulations

EYLEA HD (aflibercept) injection 8 mg represents a higher-dose intravitreal formulation of aflibercept, containing twice the active ingredient (8 mg in 0.07 mL) compared to the standard 2 mg dose, enabling potentially longer treatment intervals for neovascular age-related macular degeneration (nAMD), diabetic macular edema (DME), and diabetic retinopathy (DR). This concentrated version leverages the same fusion protein mechanism but at elevated dosing to sustain VEGF inhibition over extended periods, aiming to reduce injection frequency and treatment burden.
Phase 2 trials such as CANDELA (NCT04429503), involving 106 treatment-naive nAMD patients, evaluated 8 mg aflibercept versus 2 mg, showing comparable best-corrected visual acuity gains (mean +6.3 to +9.8 letters at week 44) and central subfield thickness reductions, with a safety profile consistent with the lower dose and support for 12- to 16-week intervals. Similarly, the PHOTON trial (NCT04126317) in DME demonstrated non-inferiority in visual acuity improvement and anatomic outcomes, allowing up to 16-week dosing after loading, with adverse events like intraocular inflammation occurring at rates akin to standard aflibercept (e.g., 0.9% incidence of ocular serious events).02577-1/abstract) These studies, conducted by Regeneron, informed the formulation's advancement, prioritizing durability without compromising efficacy.
The U.S. Food and Drug Administration approved EYLEA HD on August 18, 2023, for wAMD, DME, and DR in adults, recommending initial monthly doses for three months followed by every 8 to 16 weeks based on response. European Medicines Agency approval followed in 2024 for similar indications with extended intervals. However, in April 2025, the FDA rejected Regeneron's supplemental application for intervals exceeding 16 weeks across indications, issuing a complete response letter that affirmed the existing safety and efficacy data but required additional evidence for longer extensions, without identifying new risks. Pre-filled syringe delivery of the 8 mg dose is anticipated by mid-2025 pending manufacturing approvals.
Real-world applications and ongoing phase 3 trials, such as PULSAR and PHOTON extensions, continue to assess long-term durability, with preliminary data indicating sustained anatomic stability and reduced injection needs in subsets of patients, though individual variability in disease activity necessitates personalized regimens. No significant increases in systemic or ocular adverse events, including endophthalmitis (rates <0.1%) or vascular events, have been linked to the higher dose beyond those observed with 2 mg aflibercept.

Long-Term Outcomes and Real-World Data

In prospective observational studies of treatment-nave neovascular age-related macular degeneration (nAMD) patients treated with intravitreal aflibercept using modified treat-and-extend regimens, visual acuity gains were maintained over 5 years, with a mean improvement of +5.6 ETDRS letters from baseline (56.1 letters), and 53% of eyes achieving >70 letters acuity. Mean total injections totaled 21.6 over this period, decreasing from ~5.7 in year 1 to ~4.2 in year 5, with 20% of eyes requiring no injections by year 5 due to disease inactivity. However, anatomic changes included macular atrophy in 60% of eyes and subretinal fibrosis in 46%, alongside complete fluid resolution in 53%.
Larger retrospective real-world analyses of anti-VEGF therapies, encompassing aflibercept, reveal more modest long-term visual outcomes in nAMD, with mean changes of +3.1 letters at 1 year shifting to -0.2 letters at 3 years and -2.2 letters at 5 years among treatment-nave patients receiving a mean of 32 injections by year 5. In a Dutch real-world cohort, 5-year mean visual acuity reached 64.9 ETDRS letters with bevacizumab as first-line (switching to aflibercept or ranibizumab in 70.9% of cases), outperforming a reference group at 62.6 letters, attributed to higher treatment intensity (14.5 more injections overall).
For diabetic macular edema (DME), 24-month real-world data from the UK DRAKO study demonstrated aflibercept's effectiveness in improving visual acuity and reducing macular thickness, despite injection frequencies lower than in pivotal trials (mean ~4-5 annually after loading). Similar patterns hold for retinal vein occlusion-related macular edema, where long-term stabilization occurs but with limited gains beyond 3 years across anti-VEGF agents including aflibercept.
Safety profiles in real-world settings remain favorable, with a 36-month Japanese prospective surveillance of 3,872 aflibercept-treated AMD patients reporting ocular adverse drug reactions in 2.07% (primarily intraocular inflammation and hypertension) and non-ocular in 0.72%, alongside arterial thromboembolic events in 0.39%. Persistence challenges persist, however, with 38.3% of nAMD patients nonpersistent for 6 months and 19.4% for >12 months after 5 years of aflibercept therapy. These findings underscore that while aflibercept sustains vision against disease progression, outcomes vary with treatment adherence and regimen intensity, often diverging from controlled trial results due to real-world factors like visit non-compliance.
