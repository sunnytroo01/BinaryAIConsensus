 Jessica Hecht (born June 28, 1965) is an American actress and singer noted for her versatile work across stage, television, and film.
Hecht, raised in Bloomfield, Connecticut, after early years in Princeton, New Jersey, trained at New York University's Tisch School of the Arts, earning a B.F.A. in drama. Her breakthrough in television came with recurring roles such as Susan Bunch, the partner of Ross Geller's ex-wife Carol, in the sitcom Friends (19952004), and Gretchen Schwartz, a former associate of Walter White, in Breaking Bad (20082013). She later portrayed Carol Mannheim, a corporate executive, in the series The Boys (20192020).
On Broadway, Hecht has earned acclaim for dramatic roles, including nominations for the Tony Award for Best Featured Actress in a Play for A View from the Bridge (2010) and Best Actress in a Play for Summer, 1976 (2023), alongside appearances in revivals such as The Price (2017) opposite Mark Ruffalo and Fiddler on the Roof (2016). Off-Broadway, she received an Obie Award and Outer Critics Circle Award for Admissions (2018). Her film credits include supporting parts in Sideways (2004) and Whatever Works (2009), underscoring her range in ensemble casts.

Early Life and Education

Family Background and Childhood

Jessica Hecht was born on June 28, 1965, in Princeton, New Jersey. At age three, she relocated with her parents and sister to Bloomfield, Connecticut, where she spent much of her childhood.
Her father, Richard Hecht, worked as a physicist, while her mother, Lenore Hecht, was a psychotherapist. The couple later divorced, after which Lenore remarried psychiatrist Howard Iger, forming a blended family that included Hecht, her biological sister Elizabeth, and Iger's two children from a previous marriage.
Hecht grew up in a culturally Jewish household, though her father held secular socialist views. Despite this, she pursued religious observance, briefly attending an Orthodox synagogue before holding her bat mitzvah at the Conservative Tikvoh Chodoshah synagogue (now B'nai Tikvah-Shalom) in Bloomfield.

Academic and Artistic Training

Hecht briefly attended Connecticut College in New London, Connecticut, for approximately one and a half years, where she studied under an acting instructor influenced by the Stanislavski system. She then transferred to the Tisch School of the Arts at New York University, graduating in 1987 with a Bachelor of Fine Arts in drama. 
During her time at Tisch, Hecht trained under renowned acting teacher Stella Adler, whose approach emphasized imagination, psychological insight, and textual analysis over strict emotional recall. Hecht has credited Adler's instruction with profoundly shaping her technique, particularly in developing a disciplined yet intuitive process for character interpretation in classical and contemporary roles. This formal conservatory education provided the foundational skills that launched her professional theater career in New York.

Career

Early Theater and Off-Broadway Work

Hecht's entry into professional theater followed her graduation from the Tisch School of the Arts at New York University, where she trained in acting. Her off-Broadway debut occurred in 1990 in a revival of Shakespeare's Othello mounted by Theatre for a New Audience, in which she portrayed Bianca, a role described as small but pivotal to the production's dynamics. That same year, she took on the role of Bianca in a production of The Taming of the Shrew.
Throughout the early 1990s, Hecht accumulated credits in multiple off-Broadway plays, building experience in New York City's intimate theater scene. She also participated in 12 seasons at the Williamstown Theatre Festival, a prominent regional summer venue in Massachusetts known for developing talent through new and classic works. These engagements, often involving ensemble roles in Shakespearean and contemporary pieces, provided foundational opportunities to refine her technique amid peers and directors, prior to her transition to larger stages.

Television Roles and Breakthroughs

Hecht's early television appearances in the 1990s included guest spots on series such as Seinfeld (1993) and Law & Order: Special Victims Unit (2000), alongside a lead role as Janeane Percy-Parker in the short-lived sitcom Holding the Baby (1998).
A pivotal breakthrough arrived with her recurring portrayal of Susan Bunch on Friends (19942004), where she depicted the lesbian partner of Ross Geller's ex-wife, Carol Willick (Jane Sibbett), in one of the earliest sustained representations of a same-sex couple on prime-time network television. Hecht featured in seven episodes across seasons 2 through 8, central to arcs involving the artificial insemination and co-parenting of their son, Ben Geller, which highlighted tensions with Ross (David Schwimmer) while normalizing queer family dynamics. She had initially auditioned for the principal role of Monica Geller but was cast as Susan, a decision she later described as fortuitous given the demands of lead status on the ensemble show.
Hecht continued building her screen profile with the role of Gretchen Schwartz on Breaking Bad (20082013), appearing in three episodes of season 5 as Walter White's (Bryan Cranston) former college sweetheart and business partner, whose shared history with Elliott Schwartz (Adam Godley) at Gray Matter Technologies underscored themes of regret and lost potential in the series' narrative. This performance drew acclaim for its layered portrayal of moral ambiguity and unresolved past ties.
Later television work encompassed recurring turns as Jennifer on Bored to Death (20092011), opposite Jason Schwartzman and Ted Danson, and a guest role on Person of Interest (2013). In more recent projects, she played outreach director Carol Mannheim in season 2 of The Boys (2020), satirizing cult-like organizations within the show's superhero critique, and Karen Wheeler, the mother of protagonist Ryan Hayes, in the Netflix series Special (20192021), addressing autism and identity through a grounded familial lens. Additional credits include Emily Dickinson's sister-in-law Susan Gilbert in Dickinson (20192021) and supporting parts in Red Oaks (20152017).

Film Appearances

Jessica Hecht has primarily pursued a career in theater and television, with film appearances consisting mostly of supporting roles in independent dramas, comedies, and biopics. Her cinematic work often features nuanced portrayals of maternal figures, professionals, or historical personages, reflecting her stage-honed subtlety.

Broadway and Major Stage Productions

Jessica Hecht made her Broadway debut in The Last Night of Ballyhoo in 1997, portraying Lala Levy in the comedy by Alfred Uhry, which ran from February 27, 1997, to June 28, 1998. Her performance in this production marked an early highlight in her stage career, contributing to the play's success during its extended run.
Subsequent Broadway appearances included revivals of Arthur Miller's works, such as After the Fall in 2004, where she played Louise from July 29 to September 12. In 2005, Hecht took on the roles of Portia and ensemble member in a production of Shakespeare's Julius Caesar, running April 3 to June 12. She revisited Neil Simon's Brighton Beach Memoirs in 2009 as Blanche Morton, a limited revival from October 25 to November 1.
Hecht received her first Tony Award nomination for Best Performance by an Actress in a Leading Role in a Play for her portrayal of Beatrice Carbone in the 2010 revival of Arthur Miller's A View from the Bridge, which opened January 24 and closed April 4, co-starring Liev Schreiber and Scarlett Johansson. In 2012, she starred as Veta Louise Simmons opposite Jim Parsons in the Roundabout Theatre Company's revival of Mary Chase's Harvey, from June 14 to August 5, earning a Drama League Award nomination. The following year, she appeared as Julie Bascov in Richard Greenberg's The Assembled Parties (April 17 to July 28, 2013), another Drama League nominee. 
In musical theater, Hecht played Golde in the 2015-2016 revival of Fiddler on the Roof, directed by Bartlett Sher, from December 20, 2015, to December 31, 2016. She returned to Arthur Miller with The Price in 2017, embodying Esther Franz alongside Mark Ruffalo, Tony Shalhoub, and Danny DeVito, in a Roundabout production that ran March 16 to May 14. 
More recently, Hecht starred as Alice in the Manhattan Theatre Club's premiere of Summer, 1976 (April 25 to June 18, 2023), earning a Tony nomination for her role opposite Laura Linney. In Eureka Day (December 16, 2024, to February 16, 2025), she portrayed Suzanne in Jonathan Spector's comedy about parental debates over vaccination.

Recent Stage and Screen Projects

In December 2024, Hecht starred in the Broadway production of Eureka Day at the Hudson Theatre, portraying a character in Jonathan Spector's satirical play about parental debates over vaccinations at a private school. The limited run extended to February 16, 2025, earning Hecht a 2025 Tony Award nomination for Best Performance by an Actress in a Featured Role in a Play.
Hecht is scheduled to premiere A Mother, an adaptation of Bertolt Brecht's Mother Courage and Her Children co-conceived and written by Neena Beber, on April 7, 2025, at the Baryshnikov Arts Center in New York City, featuring original songs and choreography. In this solo performance, Hecht embodies the titular role, reimagining the anti-war narrative for contemporary audiences.
On screen, Hecht appeared as Lisa in the 2025 film Eleanor the Great, a drama directed by Scott Beck and Bryan Woods. She also featured in The Atlantic City Story (2020), a crime thriller, and reprised roles in television series such as Tokyo Vice (2022, Season 1) as Samantha. Additionally, Hecht has portrayed Carol Mannheim in recurring episodes of The Boys across recent seasons, contributing to the Amazon Prime series' exploration of superhero corruption.

Awards and Recognition

Theater Accolades

Jessica Hecht has earned three Tony Award nominations for her Broadway performances, recognizing her versatility in both leading and featured roles. In 2010, she was nominated for Best Performance by an Actress in a Featured Role in a Play for portraying Catherine in the revival of Arthur Miller's A View from the Bridge at the Cort Theatre. In 2023, Hecht received a nomination for Best Performance by an Actress in a Leading Role in a Play for her role as Alice in Summer, 1976 at the Samuel J. Friedman Theatre. She garnered her third Tony nomination in 2025 for Best Performance by an Actress in a Featured Role in a Play as Suzanne in Eureka Day at the Laura Pels Theatre.
Off-Broadway, Hecht won the Obie Award for Distinguished Performance by an Actress in 2018 for her role as Sherri Rosenberg in Joshua Harmon's Admissions at Lincoln Center Theater's Mitzi E. Newhouse Theater, a production that also featured her co-star Ben Edelman receiving the same honor. She was nominated for the Outer Critics Circle Award for Outstanding Actress in a Play that same year for Admissions. Additionally, in 2013, Hecht received a Drama League Award nomination for Distinguished Performance for her work as Faye Kreisler in Richard Greenberg's The Assembled Parties on Broadway.

Television and Film Honors

Hecht received a nomination for the Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Actress in a Short Form Comedy or Drama Series in 2019 for her role as the mother of the protagonist in the Netflix series Special. This marked her sole documented nomination from the Academy of Television Arts & Sciences for screen work, recognizing her performance in the series produced by Stage 13 and That's Wonderful Productions. No major film awards or additional television honors, such as Golden Globe or Screen Actors Guild recognitions, have been recorded for her roles in projects like Breaking Bad or Friends.

Personal Life

Marriage and Family

Jessica Hecht married television and film director Adam Bernstein on July 2, 1995. The couple relocated from a loft in Hell's Kitchen to a home in Manhattan's Upper West Side around 2016, prioritizing family space amid their professional commitments.
Hecht and Bernstein have two children: a daughter, Stella Rose Bernstein, and a son, Carlo Bernstein. As of 2023, the children were in their early twenties, having been raised in a secular household despite Hecht's Jewish heritage; Bernstein was raised by a Catholic mother. Hecht has spoken publicly about balancing her acting career with motherhood, noting the challenges of maintaining family stability during demanding roles.

Professional Mentorship and Teaching

Jessica Hecht instructs scene study classes at the HB Studio in New York City, targeting actors seeking to refine their performance techniques through practical exercises. These ongoing sessions draw on her experience in theater and television to guide participants in interpreting roles and scripts.
She also facilitates specialized workshops at HB Studio, including the two-session Scene Study: Script Analysis program, aimed at professional performers aiming to enhance their analytical skills and maintain craft proficiency. Hecht extends her teaching to the HB Playwrights Studio, where she mentors actors in script interpretation and character development.
At Williams College, Hecht has repeatedly taught Winter Study courses, such as the January 2024 "A Filmmaker's Workshop," involving 14 students in producing short films with input from visiting industry professionals. In these programs, she fosters mentorship by connecting students with her network of Hollywood contacts for career advice and practical feedback.
Hecht has led master classes elsewhere, including acting sessions at the Tom Todoroff Studio, where she imparts insights from her Tony-nominated and Obie-winning stage work. Her teaching emphasizes listening, script engagement, and professional resilience, informed by collaborations with playwrights like Arthur Miller.

Reception and Controversies

Critical Acclaim for Performances

Jessica Hecht has garnered significant critical praise for her Broadway performances, evidenced by three Tony Award nominations. Her role as Alice in David Auburn's Summer, 1976 (opened April 25, 2023) opposite Laura Linney was lauded for its emotional authenticity and subtlety; Variety highlighted the duo's "stellar performances" in the affecting two-hander exploring female friendship. Entertainment Weekly described Hecht's work as commanding, complementing Linney's steely intensity with nuanced vulnerability. The New York Post called Hecht and Linney a "perfect pair," praising the production's warmth amid revelations of deception. This performance earned her a 2023 Tony nomination for Best Actress in a Play.
In the 2025 revival of Jonathan Spector's Eureka Day, Hecht's portrayal of Suzanne, a vocal anti-vaccination advocate in a private school debate, secured her third Tony nomination for Best Featured Actress in a Play. Critics noted the role's intensity, with her delivery of a key monologue on vaccine skepticism drawing attention for its fervor and conviction. The production itself won the 2025 Tony for Best Revival of a Play. Hecht's earlier Tony nod came for Beatrice in the 2010 Broadway production of Arthur Miller's A View from the Bridge.
On television, Hecht received an Emmy nomination for Outstanding Guest Actress in a Comedy Series for her role as Karen in the Netflix series Special (20192021). Her dramatic turns, such as Gretchen Schwartz in Breaking Bad, have been recognized for adding depth to ensemble casts, though theater remains the primary venue for her most celebrated work.

Debates Over Key Roles, Including Friends

Jessica Hecht's portrayal of Susan Bunch, the partner of Carol Willick on Friends, sparked debates primarily centered on the show's depiction of same-sex relationships in the mid-1990s, a period when such representations were rare and often contentious in mainstream network television. Susan, introduced in season 1 as Carol's girlfriend and co-parent to Ross Geller's son Ben, featured in storylines that highlighted lesbian co-parenting and, notably, the couple's wedding in the season 2 episode "The One with the Lesbian Wedding," aired on March 18, 1996. This episode marked the first depiction of a lesbian wedding on primetime U.S. television, prompting network executives at NBC to express unease over potential backlash from affiliates and advertisers wary of alienating conservative audiences.
The wedding scene itself avoided a kiss between Carol (played by Jane Sibbett) and Susan to mitigate controversy, yet two U.S. cities declined to air the episode, citing concerns over content deemed inappropriate for family viewing. Sibbett later described preparing to "battle" producers to preserve the storyline's integrity, emphasizing its groundbreaking nature amid pushback from standards and practices departments. Hecht, reflecting on the role in interviews, expressed no personal regret over the surrounding debates, noting the character's progressive elements while acknowledging familial tensions; her father reportedly refused to watch episodes featuring Susan, opting instead for Bible study groups during airings to avoid exposure to the "historic lesbian role."
Among fans and critics, Susan's character drew mixed reactions, with some online discussions labeling her as antagonisticparticularly in interactions with Ross over Ben's upbringing and surnameportraying her as overly snarky or demanding despite not being the biological mother. Hecht has countered such views by praising the writers for treating the lesbian storyline earnestly, without "poking fun at being gay," and highlighted its role in normalizing same-sex relationships on screen at a time when public opinion on gay marriage lagged significantly behind later cultural shifts. Hecht also revealed auditioning for the lead role of Monica Geller but losing it partly due to being perceived as a "bigger size than the rest" of the cast, fueling retrospective discussions on Hollywood's body standards and casting biases in the 1990s.
Beyond Friends, Hecht's stage roles, such as in Arthur Miller revivals or Admissionswhich examined racial diversity quotas in elite institutionshave elicited commentary on performative liberalism in theater, though without widespread controversy; critics noted her nuanced performances but debated broader institutional trends in casting for ideological alignment rather than merit. Hecht has maintained that her career choices prioritized artistic depth over public disputes, often returning to theater post-Friends to avoid the scrutiny of high-profile TV controversies.

Television

Hecht's breakthrough television role came in the NBC sitcom Friends (19942004), where she portrayed Susan Bunch, the same-sex partner of Carol Willick and co-parent to Ross Geller's son Ben, appearing in 15 episodes across seasons 2 through 8. The character was introduced in the episode "The One with the Lesbian Wedding" on March 18, 1996, and contributed to the series' exploration of non-traditional family dynamics.
In the AMC series Breaking Bad (20082013), Hecht played Gretchen Schwartz, Walter White's former romantic partner and co-founder of Gray Matter Technologies with Elliott Schwartz, appearing in the season 1 episode "...and the Bag's in the River" (aired February 22, 2009) and the season 5 episode "Granite State" (aired September 29, 2013). The role highlighted backstory elements of White's pre-cancer life and resentment toward his former associates.
Hecht portrayed Carol Mannheim, the outreach director for the fictional Church of the Collective, in four episodes of season 2 of Amazon Prime Video's The Boys (2019present), which aired from September 4 to October 9, 2020. Her character interacted with The Deep during his involvement with the cult-like organization, blending manipulative persuasion with satirical commentary on celebrity spirituality.
From 2019 to 2021, she starred as Karen Hayes, the mother of the lead character Ryan, in the Netflix comedy-drama Special, appearing in both seasons (8 episodes total) and earning a Primetime Emmy nomination for Outstanding Guest Actress in a Comedy Series in 2020 for her work in season 1. The series, adapted from Ryan O'Connell's memoir, addressed themes of disability and identity through family relationships.
More recently, Hecht has appeared in HBO Max's Tokyo Vice (2022present) as Willa Adelstein, the mother of journalist Jake Adelstein, across multiple episodes starting in season 1. She also featured in Showtime's Super Pumped: The Battle for Uber (2022) and had guest roles in HBO's Succession, Apple's Dickinson, and CBS's The Good Wife.

Stage

Jessica Hecht has built a prominent career in theater, with over a dozen Broadway appearances spanning revivals of classic plays and original works, earning three Tony Award nominations for her performances. Her stage roles often feature complex, emotionally layered characters in dramas and comedies by playwrights such as Arthur Miller, Neil Simon, and Mary Beth Fisher. Off-Broadway, she has received recognition including an Outer Critics Circle Award nomination for Stage Kiss (2014) as Sara.

Other Media

Hecht has narrated audiobooks, primarily short fiction by Stephen King. Her solo narration of King's novella A Good Marriage, released on September 30, 2014, spans 3 hours and 33 minutes and centers on a woman's discovery of her husband's dark secret. She provided the voice for the protagonist in Big Driver, a story from King's oeuvre about a mystery writer's vengeful ordeal, with listeners noting her conveyance of emotional depth in the role. Hecht co-narrated the collection Full Dark, No Stars (2010) alongside Craig Wasson, covering four interconnected tales of moral ambiguity, with a total runtime of 14 hours and 55 minutes. These performances highlight her versatility in audio formats beyond visual media.
