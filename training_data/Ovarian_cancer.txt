 Ovarian cancer is a disease where abnormal cells grow in a woman's ovaries (small organs in the lower abdomen that help make eggs and babies). It is rare in children and mostly affects adult women. Ovarian cancer is a malignant neoplasm that originates in the tissues of the ovary, one of a pair of female reproductive glands responsible for producing eggs and hormones such as estrogen and progesterone. It is the eighth most common cancer among women worldwide, with approximately 324,600 new cases diagnosed globally in 2022, and it accounts for about 3% of all cancers in women. In the United States, an estimated 20,890 women will be diagnosed with ovarian cancer in 2025, and around 12,730 will die from it, reflecting its status as the fifth leading cause of cancer death among women. The lifetime risk for a woman developing ovarian cancer is about 1 in 91, with the disease most commonly occurring after menopause and rarely before age 40.
The majority of ovarian cancersabout 90%are epithelial ovarian cancers, which develop from the cells covering the outer surface of the ovary; these are often grouped with similar malignancies of the fallopian tubes and primary peritoneal cavity due to shared characteristics and origins. Less common types include germ cell tumors (arising from egg-producing cells) and stromal tumors (from hormone-producing cells), which together make up roughly 10% of cases and tend to affect younger women. Many epithelial cancers are high-grade serous carcinomas, the most aggressive subtype, and recent research suggests that a significant portion may actually begin in the fallopian tubes rather than the ovaries themselves.
Symptoms of ovarian cancer are often subtle and nonspecific in early stages, leading to diagnosis at advanced stages in about 75% of cases, which contributes to poorer outcomes. Common signs include persistent bloating, pelvic or abdominal pain, difficulty eating or feeling full quickly, urinary urgency or frequency, and unexplained fatigue or weight changes; these typically worsen over time and may mimic less serious conditions like irritable bowel syndrome. Key risk factors encompass advancing age (with peak incidence in the 60s), a family history of ovarian, breast, or colorectal cancer, inherited genetic mutations such as BRCA1 or BRCA2 (increasing risk up to 44% lifetime), obesity, endometriosis, and postmenopausal hormone replacement therapy without opposed estrogen. Protective factors include pregnancy, oral contraceptive use (reducing risk by 30-50% with long-term use), breastfeeding, tubal ligation, and hysterectomy.
Diagnosis typically involves a combination of pelvic examinations, imaging tests such as transvaginal ultrasound or CT scans, blood tests measuring the tumor marker CA-125 (elevated in about 80% of advanced cases but less reliable in early stages), and surgical biopsy for confirmation. Staging ranges from I (confined to one or both ovaries) to IV (distant metastasis), with most cases diagnosed at stage III or IV. Treatment is multidisciplinary and primarily involves cytoreductive surgery to remove as much tumor as possible, followed by platinum-based chemotherapy (e.g., carboplatin plus paclitaxel), which achieves response rates of 70-80% in advanced disease. Targeted therapies, such as PARP inhibitors (e.g., olaparib) for patients with BRCA mutations, and emerging immunotherapies like checkpoint inhibitors, are increasingly used, particularly for recurrent or platinum-resistant cases.
The overall five-year relative survival rate for ovarian cancer is approximately 51%, but it varies significantly by stage: 92% for localized disease, dropping to approximately 32% for distant spread. Advances in genetic testing, minimally invasive surgeries, and personalized medicine have improved outcomes in recent years, though early detection remains challenging due to the lack of effective screening methods for the general population.

Clinical Presentation

Signs and Symptoms

Ovarian cancer frequently manifests with nonspecific symptoms that are often absent early on and subtle when they appear, resembling digestive issues and attributed to gastrointestinal or urinary tract disorders, leading to delayed recognition. Main symptoms include persistent abdominal bloating; ongoing lower abdominal or pelvic pain; feeling full after eating small amounts (early satiety); frequent or urgent urination; changes in bowel habits such as random gas, constipation, or diarrhea; chronic fatigue; back pain; and unusual vaginal bleeding, especially after menopause. These symptoms alone do not confirm cancer and can indicate other conditions; consult a doctor if they persist for weeks, appear suddenly together, or prompt medical attention.
As the disease advances, symptoms become more pronounced due to tumor growth and metastasis. Later manifestations often involve ascites, causing abdominal distension and discomfort; unintentional weight loss; constipation or changes in bowel habits; back pain from tumor pressure on surrounding structures; and shortness of breath resulting from pleural effusion. These developments reflect progression to higher stages, where symptoms correlate with disease extent and may influence staging assessments.
Rarely, ovarian cancer is associated with paraneoplastic syndromes, which are immune-mediated conditions triggered by the tumor. Notable examples include dermatomyositis, characterized by skin rash and muscle weakness, and subacute cerebellar degeneration, leading to ataxia and coordination difficulties.
The progression of symptoms in ovarian cancer is typically insidious during early stages, with vague complaints that escalate rapidly in advanced disease as complications like ascites or effusions accumulate. This nonspecific presentation contributes to significant diagnostic delays, with an average lag of approximately 6 to 8 months from symptom onset to diagnosis as of recent studies (20222023).

Presentation in Children

Ovarian cancer primarily affects adult women and is exceedingly rare in children, accounting for less than 1% of all childhood malignancies, with the majority of cases involving germ cell tumors and sex cord-stromal tumors rather than the epithelial types predominant in adults. These tumors typically occur in girls under 20 years of age, with an estimated incidence of ovarian neoplasms around 2.6 per 100,000 in prepubertal females, though malignant forms are even less common. Unlike adult ovarian cancer, pediatric cases often present at earlier stages, contributing to better prognoses when detected promptly.
The clinical presentation in pediatric patients frequently includes an abdominal mass, which may be palpable and cause distension or discomfort, alongside acute abdominal pain often resulting from tumor torsion or rupture. Hormone-secreting tumors, such as granulosa cell tumors within the sex cord-stromal category, can lead to unique endocrine manifestations like precocious puberty, characterized by early breast development, vaginal bleeding, or rapid growth in girls as young as 29 years. In adolescents, yolk sac tumorsa subtype of germ cell tumorsmay elevate serum alpha-fetoprotein levels, serving as a key diagnostic marker, and these are most prevalent between ages 10 and 20 years, aligning with the peak incidence of ovarian germ cell tumors in this age group. Acute pain episodes are particularly common in complicated cases, mimicking emergencies like appendicitis due to right-sided involvement.
Diagnosis poses significant challenges in children, as symptoms such as abdominal pain and mass are nonspecific and frequently misattributed to benign ovarian cysts, gastrointestinal issues, or acute appendicitis, leading to delays in appropriate imaging and intervention. Since the early 2000s, advancements in fertility-preserving surgical approaches, including unilateral salpingo-oophorectomy for early-stage disease, have markedly improved outcomes by balancing oncologic control with reproductive potential, achieving recurrence rates as low as 4.5% and mortality under 5% in germ cell tumor series.

Risk Factors and Etiology

Genetic and Hormonal Risks

Ovarian cancer has a significant hereditary component, with up to 25% of cases attributed to inherited genetic mutations, primarily in genes involved in DNA repair pathways. The most well-established genetic risk factors are pathogenic variants in the BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes, which are associated with hereditary breast and ovarian cancer (HBOC) syndrome and account for the majority of hereditary ovarian cancers. These mutations follow an autosomal dominant inheritance pattern, meaning a single altered copy of the gene in each cell increases cancer susceptibility, with incomplete penetrance. Women carrying a BRCA1 mutation face a lifetime risk of ovarian cancer estimated at 39-46%, while those with a BRCA2 mutation have a 12-20% risk, compared to the general population risk of about 1.3%.
Beyond BRCA1 and BRCA2, other genes contribute to ovarian cancer predisposition, though less frequently. Lynch syndrome, also known as hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer (HNPCC), arises from mutations in mismatch repair genes such as MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, and PMS2, and is linked to a 3-12% lifetime risk of ovarian cancer, often of the endometrioid subtype. Rare variants in RAD51C, RAD51D, and BRIP1 genes, which also play roles in homologous recombination repair, confer elevated risks; for instance, BRIP1 mutations are associated with a 6-13% lifetime ovarian cancer risk. These genetic factors highlight the importance of multigene panel testing in high-risk individuals to identify actionable variants.
Hormonal influences represent another key category of non-modifiable risk factors, primarily through prolonged exposure to endogenous estrogen or disruptions in ovulatory cycles. Early menarche (before age 12) and late menopause (after age 50) increase lifetime ovulations and estrogen exposure, each associated with a modest elevation in ovarian cancer risk, with relative risks of approximately 1.2-1.5 compared to average ages. Nulliparity, or never having given birth, similarly heightens risk by avoiding the protective interruptions of pregnancy on ovulation, with nulliparous women facing up to a 1.5-fold increased odds compared to those with multiple pregnancies. Postmenopausal hormone replacement therapy (HRT), particularly estrogen-only regimens used for five or more years, further amplifies this risk, with studies showing a 1.2-2.0-fold increase, though combined estrogen-progestin formulations appear less hazardous.
Endometriosis, a condition involving the growth of endometrial-like tissue outside the uterus, is linked to a 2-3-fold increased risk of developing ovarian cancer, particularly clear cell and endometrioid subtypes. This association is thought to stem from the retrograde menstruation theory, proposed by Sampson in 1927, whereby viable endometrial cells flow backward through the fallopian tubes during menstruation, implanting on ovarian surfaces and undergoing chronic inflammation, genetic instability, and malignant transformation over time. Supporting evidence includes shared molecular alterations, such as ARID1A mutations, between endometriotic lesions and associated tumors.
Family history serves as a critical indicator for genetic risk assessment and testing eligibility. Guidelines recommend genetic counseling and testing for individuals with a personal history of ovarian cancer, regardless of age or family history, as well as those with a first-degree relative diagnosed with ovarian, breast (especially triple-negative at young age), or related cancers. Specific criteria include having two or more first-degree relatives with ovarian or breast cancer, one diagnosed before age 50, or Ashkenazi Jewish ancestry with suggestive family patterns, prompting evaluation for BRCA1/2 and other panel genes. Early identification through these criteria enables risk-reducing strategies tailored to mutation status.

Lifestyle and Environmental Factors

Lifestyle factors, including diet, body weight, and tobacco use, contribute to ovarian cancer risk through modifiable behaviors that influence hormonal and inflammatory pathways. While the evidence is derived from epidemiological studies, these associations are generally modest compared to genetic risks, emphasizing the potential for prevention through behavioral changes. Environmental exposures, such as occupational hazards, further compound these risks in specific populations.
Dietary patterns play a role in ovarian cancer development, with high intake of total, animal, and saturated fats linked to elevated risk. A systematic review of cohort and case-control studies found that higher consumption of these fats, particularly from dairy and meat sources, was associated with increased ovarian cancer incidence in five of nine evaluated studies. Conversely, low consumption of fruits and vegetables, which are rich in antioxidants and fiber, correlates with higher risk, as protective effects from these foods mitigate oxidative stress and inflammation when intake is adequate.
Obesity, defined as a body mass index (BMI) greater than 30 kg/m, is associated with a 1.2- to 1.5-fold increased risk of ovarian cancer, primarily through elevated levels of insulin and estrogen that promote cell proliferation. Excess adiposity leads to higher circulating estrogen from aromatization in fat tissue and insulin resistance, both of which foster an environment conducive to tumorigenesis. Meta-analyses confirm this dose-response relationship, with obese women facing approximately 30% higher risk than those with normal BMI.
Tobacco smoking modestly elevates the risk of mucinous subtype ovarian cancer, with current smokers experiencing up to a twofold increase compared to never smokers. This association is subtype-specific, as smoking does not significantly impact other histological types like serous or endometrioid. In contrast, alcohol consumption shows minimal overall effect on ovarian cancer risk, with meta-analyses reporting no consistent association across studies.
Genital use of talc-based powders for hygiene purposes is weakly linked to ovarian cancer, with pooled analyses of case-control studies indicating a 24% to 32% increased risk among ever-users. This may occur through particle migration to the ovaries, potentially causing inflammation, though the absolute risk remains small and the evidence is primarily from observational data.
Occupational environmental exposures, including asbestos and pesticides, heighten ovarian cancer risk in affected workers. Asbestos exposure is associated with increased incidence, as fibers can translocate to the ovaries via the reproductive tract, with meta-analyses supporting a causal link classified by the International Agency for Research on Cancer. Agricultural workers face approximately 1.5-fold higher risk due to pesticide exposure, as evidenced by cohort studies like the Agricultural Health Study, which identified elevated odds for specific organochlorine insecticides.
Recent post-2020 research has highlighted emerging links between endocrine-disrupting chemicals like bisphenol A (BPA), found in plastics, and ovarian cancer risk. Studies demonstrate that BPA exposure modulates gene expression in ovarian cells, promoting proliferation via estrogenic mimicry and oxidative stress, with epidemiological data showing statistical associations in exposed populations.

Protective Factors

Several reproductive factors have been identified as protective against ovarian cancer, primarily through their influence on ovulation and hormonal exposure. Multiparity, or having multiple full-term pregnancies, is associated with a reduced risk, with each additional pregnancy linked to approximately a 10-20% decrease in incidence. Breastfeeding further contributes to this protection; women who breastfeed for 6-12 months cumulatively experience about a 20% lower risk compared to those who do not breastfeed. Similarly, long-term use of oral contraceptives provides substantial risk reduction, with 7 or more years of use associated with a 30-50% decrease in ovarian cancer incidence among the general population.
Procedures such as tubal ligation and hysterectomy also lower ovarian cancer risk by an estimated 30-40%, likely by preventing the ascent of carcinogenic agents from the lower genital tract to the ovaries and fallopian tubes. These effects are observed across histological subtypes, though they may be more pronounced for non-serous tumors.
Emerging evidence suggests that regular use of aspirin and other nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) may reduce the risk of epithelial ovarian cancer by 10-20%, attributed to their anti-inflammatory properties that mitigate chronic ovarian inflammation. This association is supported by meta-analyses showing a 23% risk reduction with low-dose aspirin use, particularly for invasive epithelial types.
Population-based studies indicate that these protective effects are amplified in high-risk groups, such as women carrying BRCA1 or BRCA2 mutations. For instance, oral contraceptive use is linked to up to a 44% risk reduction in BRCA carriers, while breastfeeding confers a 23% decrease in ovarian cancer incidence among this population. Parity similarly offers stronger protection in BRCA mutation carriers compared to the general population.
The underlying mechanism for many of these reproductive protections aligns with the incessant ovulation theory, which posits that repeated ovulation causes cumulative trauma to the ovarian surface epithelium, promoting malignant transformation over time. Factors like pregnancy, breastfeeding, and oral contraceptives suppress ovulation, thereby reducing the lifetime number of ovulatory cycles and associated epithelial damage. This theory, first proposed in 1971, explains the consistent inverse association between ovulatory suppression and ovarian cancer risk.

Pathophysiology

Tumor Development Mechanisms

Ovarian cancer arises through complex biological processes involving genetic, epigenetic, and environmental factors that drive malignant transformation of ovarian or tubal epithelial cells. One longstanding theory, the incessant ovulation hypothesis, proposes that repeated cycles of ovulation throughout a woman's reproductive life lead to cumulative trauma on the ovarian surface epithelium. During each ovulatory event, follicular rupture causes surface cell damage, followed by repair processes that may induce DNA damage, inflammation, and genetic instability, ultimately contributing to neoplastic progression. This hypothesis is supported by epidemiological evidence showing that factors suppressing ovulation, such as oral contraceptive use or pregnancy, reduce ovarian cancer risk by up to 50%.
Emerging evidence challenges the purely ovarian origin of many tumors, particularly high-grade serous carcinomas, which comprise about 70% of epithelial ovarian cancers. Instead, these tumors frequently originate in the fallopian tube epithelium, especially the fimbriated end, where serous tubal intraepithelial carcinomas (STICs) serve as precursor lesions. STICs exhibit early neoplastic changes, including p53 accumulation and genomic instability, and share clonal mutations with concurrent ovarian tumors, indicating that malignant cells may implant on the ovary after tubal initiation. This tubal origin model explains the rapid progression and peritoneal spread characteristic of serous carcinomas.
At the molecular level, key pathways underpin tumor initiation and growth. Mutations in the TP53 tumor suppressor gene occur in approximately 96% of high-grade serous ovarian cancers, typically early in pathogenesis, leading to loss of cell cycle control and genomic instability that promotes clonal expansion. Dysregulation of the PI3K/AKT signaling pathway is also prevalent, activated through mutations in PIK3CA (in about 12% of cases) or loss of PTEN, resulting in enhanced cell survival, proliferation, and resistance to apoptosis. These alterations often co-occur with TP53 mutations, amplifying oncogenic signaling.
Chronic inflammation plays a pivotal role in ovarian tumorigenesis by creating a tumor-permissive microenvironment. Repeated ovulation or pelvic inflammatory conditions can induce persistent inflammatory responses in the ovarian or tubal epithelium, releasing cytokines and growth factors that foster DNA damage and angiogenesis. This inflammatory milieu promotes vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) expression, enabling new blood vessel formation to support tumor expansion and metastasis. Studies link chronic pelvic inflammation to increased risk of epithelial ovarian cancer, particularly high-grade serous subtypes.
Tumor progression often follows a stepwise model, beginning with benign epithelial inclusion cysts formed from invaginated ovarian surface cells during ovulation. These cysts may accumulate genetic alterations, evolving into borderline tumors with atypical proliferation but limited invasiveness, and eventually progressing to invasive carcinoma through additional mutations enabling stromal invasion and dissemination. This continuum is evident in mucinous and endometrioid subtypes, where shared molecular profiles across stages support a precursor-to-malignant sequence, though serous tumors may bypass borderline phases due to rapid genomic instability.

Histological Subtypes

Ovarian cancers are primarily classified into histological subtypes based on the presumed cell of origin and microscopic features, as outlined in the World Health Organization (WHO) 2020 classification of female genital tumors. This system emphasizes both morphological characteristics and molecular alterations to distinguish benign, borderline (low malignant potential), and malignant neoplasms, with a particular focus on integrating genomic profiling for epithelial carcinomas to refine prognostication and therapeutic targeting.
The majority of ovarian malignancies, approximately 90%, are epithelial in origin, arising from the surface epithelium or modifications thereof. Within this category, high-grade serous carcinoma represents the most common and aggressive subtype, accounting for about 70% of all ovarian cancers; it is characterized by rapid growth, frequent TP53 mutations, and BRCA-associated genomic instability. Low-grade serous carcinoma, comprising roughly 5%, exhibits slower progression and BRAF or KRAS mutations, often evolving from serous borderline tumors. Other epithelial subtypes include mucinous carcinoma (3-5%), which forms gelatinous cysts and is linked to KRAS alterations; endometrioid carcinoma (10%), resembling uterine endometrial cancer with potential ARID1A mutations; and clear cell carcinoma (10%), notable for its clear cytoplasm and association with endometriosis, frequently harboring PIK3CA mutations. The WHO 2020 update highlights molecular subtyping, such as distinguishing high-grade serous tumors based on homologous recombination deficiency, to guide precision medicine approaches.
Germ cell tumors constitute about 5% of ovarian cancers and predominantly affect younger patients, typically adolescents and women under 30. These neoplasms derive from primordial germ cells and include dysgerminoma (analogous to testicular seminoma, with KIT mutations), yolk sac tumor (producing alpha-fetoprotein and showing Schiller-Duval bodies), and immature teratoma (containing elements from multiple germ layers with potential for malignant transformation). They are generally chemosensitive and have favorable outcomes when detected early.
Sex cord-stromal tumors account for 2-5% of cases and originate from ovarian stromal or sex cord cells, often presenting with hormonal effects. Granulosa cell tumors, the most frequent subtype, are estrogen-secreting and associated with FOXL2 mutations, leading to symptoms like endometrial hyperplasia; Sertoli-Leydig cell tumors produce androgens, causing virilization, and may harbor DICER1 mutations. These tumors tend to occur in perimenopausal women and have a variable malignant potential.
Rare subtypes, such as small cell carcinoma of the ovary (hypercalcemic type, often in young women with SMARCA4 mutations) and ovarian sarcomas (e.g., leiomyosarcoma or fibrosarcoma, <1%), represent less than 2% of cases and are managed as distinct entities due to their aggressive behavior. Borderline tumors, primarily serous (60%) or mucinous (30-40%), exhibit atypical epithelial proliferation without stromal invasion and comprise 10-15% of epithelial ovarian neoplasms; they carry a low risk of progression to invasive carcinoma (5-10%) and excellent prognosis. Seromucinous borderline tumors are recognized as a rare subtype in the WHO 2020 classification, while seromucinous carcinomas have been reclassified as variants of endometrioid carcinoma.

Diagnosis

Initial Assessment and Risk Evaluation

The initial assessment of suspected ovarian cancer begins with a thorough medical history to identify potential risk factors and symptom patterns. Clinicians inquire about the duration and nature of symptoms such as persistent bloating, pelvic pain, or early satiety, which may indicate underlying pathology. Family history is critically evaluated for ovarian, breast, or colorectal cancers, as well as known genetic predispositions. Reproductive milestones, including age at menarche, parity, use of oral contraceptives, and menopausal status, are documented to assess hormonal influences on risk.
Physical examination follows, focusing on detecting palpable abnormalities. A comprehensive pelvic exam includes bimanual palpation to assess for ovarian enlargement or tenderness, alongside rectovaginal examination to evaluate deeper structures. Abdominal palpation is performed to identify masses, organomegaly, or ascites, which presents as fluid wave or shifting dullness. These findings help differentiate benign from potentially malignant conditions, though sensitivity for early-stage disease is limited.
To stratify risk, validated scoring tools integrate clinical, biomarker, and imaging data. The Risk of Malignancy Index (RMI) combines serum CA-125 levels, ultrasound findings (scored via morphological features like multilocularity or solid areas), and menopausal status (premenopausal score 1, postmenopausal score 3); a score exceeding 200 indicates high risk and prompts specialist referral. The Risk of Ovarian Malignancy Algorithm (ROMA) incorporates CA-125, human epididymis protein 4 (HE4), and menopausal status to calculate malignancy probability, with thresholds of 11.4% for premenopausal and 29.9% for postmenopausal women signaling elevated risk. The International Ovarian Tumor Analysis (IOTA) simple rules use ultrasound characteristics to classify adnexal masses: benign features include unilocular cysts with smooth walls, while malignant indicators encompass irregular solid components or ascites, aiding in distinguishing simple from complex cysts without advanced imaging.
Referral to a gynecologic oncologist is recommended based on guideline criteria to ensure timely intervention. The National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) advises urgent suspected cancer pathway referral for women with ascites, a palpable pelvic or abdominal mass, or persistent symptoms unexplained by other causes. The American Cancer Society (ACS) and American Academy of Family Physicians (AAFP) endorse referral for elevated risk scores, complex adnexal findings on initial exam, or CA-125 levels above 35 U/mL in postmenopausal women, emphasizing multidisciplinary evaluation for optimal outcomes.

Imaging and Biomarker Testing

Transvaginal ultrasound serves as the primary imaging modality for the initial evaluation of suspected ovarian masses, offering detailed assessment of tumor morphology including the presence of solid areas, thick septations greater than 3 mm, papillary projections, and irregular cyst walls, which are indicative of malignancy. Color Doppler imaging enhances this evaluation by detecting increased vascularity and abnormal blood flow patterns within the mass, such as low-resistance arterial signals, which correlate with malignant potential. These features help differentiate benign from malignant lesions, though simple cysts or unilocular cysts with thin septations typically suggest benignity.
Computed tomography (CT) and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) are essential for further characterization and staging of ovarian cancer, particularly in detecting peritoneal metastases, lymph node involvement, and quantifying ascites. Contrast-enhanced CT of the abdomen, pelvis, and chest is the preferred initial modality for preoperative staging due to its ability to identify distant spread and guide surgical planning. MRI provides superior soft-tissue contrast for evaluating complex adnexal masses when ultrasound findings are equivocal, aiding in the assessment of tumor invasion into adjacent structures.
Biomarker testing plays a crucial role in supporting imaging findings for ovarian cancer diagnosis. Cancer antigen 125 (CA-125) is the most widely used serum biomarker, elevated in approximately 80% of advanced serous ovarian cancers but less sensitive (around 50%) in early-stage or mucinous subtypes. Human epididymis protein 4 (HE4) offers higher specificity than CA-125, particularly for distinguishing malignant from benign pelvic masses, with levels rising earlier in high-grade serous carcinomas. Multimarker panels, such as the Risk of Ovarian Malignancy Algorithm (ROMA) combining CA-125, HE4, and menopausal status, improve diagnostic accuracy and are incorporated into risk assessment models.
Positron emission tomography-computed tomography (PET-CT) using 18F-fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG) is particularly valuable for detecting ovarian cancer recurrence, demonstrating high uptake in high-grade tumors and identifying metabolically active lesions not visible on conventional imaging. It aids in localizing sites of disease in patients with rising CA-125 levels post-treatment, with sensitivity exceeding 90% for recurrent lesions greater than 1 cm.
Despite their utility, imaging and biomarkers have limitations that can lead to diagnostic challenges. CA-125 levels may be falsely elevated in benign conditions such as endometriosis, where values can reach up to 100 U/mL, reducing specificity in premenopausal women. Ultrasound can miss small or deep lesions, while CT and MRI may understage early peritoneal disease, necessitating integration with clinical and pathological correlation for accurate diagnosis.

Pathological Confirmation

Pathological confirmation of ovarian cancer requires tissue sampling and microscopic examination to verify malignancy, as non-invasive methods cannot provide definitive diagnosis. Biopsy techniques are selected based on clinical context, balancing diagnostic yield with risks such as tumor dissemination. The most common approach is surgical biopsy during exploratory laparotomy or laparoscopy, where tissue is obtained directly from the ovarian mass or peritoneal deposits.
Fine-needle aspiration (FNA) is rarely performed for suspected ovarian cancer due to the high risk of tumor seeding along the needle tract, which can lead to peritoneal spread and complicate staging. Core needle biopsy, often image-guided by ultrasound or CT, offers a minimally invasive alternative for preoperative diagnosis in select cases, providing adequate tissue for histopathological analysis with low complication rates and high accuracy in distinguishing benign from malignant lesions. Intraoperative frozen section analysis during surgery is frequently employed to guide immediate decision-making, such as the extent of resection; it involves rapid freezing and sectioning of fresh tissue for microscopic review, achieving diagnostic accuracy of 85-95% for ovarian tumors, though it may underdiagnose borderline lesions.
Once obtained, tissue undergoes histopathological examination to assess cellular architecture, atypia, and invasion. Pathologists evaluate features such as nuclear pleomorphism, mitotic activity, and stromal invasion to confirm carcinoma. Immunohistochemistry (IHC) enhances specificity; for instance, WT1 positivity supports high-grade serous carcinoma, while PAX8 expression is characteristic of epithelial ovarian tumors, aiding differentiation from mimics like mesothelioma or breast metastases. Cytoreductive assessment during pathology review informs surgical outcomes by evaluating residual disease post-resection.
Molecular testing on tumor tissue is integral for therapeutic guidance. BRCA1/2 mutation status and homologous recombination deficiency (HRD) are assessed via next-generation sequencing to identify candidates for PARP inhibitors, with HRD present in up to 50% of high-grade serous cases. Mismatch repair (MMR) protein immunohistochemistry screens for Lynch syndrome-associated deficiencies, prompting germline confirmation in endometrioid subtypes.
Diagnostic pitfalls include distinguishing borderline tumors from invasive carcinomas, as the former lack stromal invasion but may show micropapillary patterns mimicking malignancy on frozen section; up to 25% of borderline cases are initially overdiagnosed as invasive. Differentiating primary ovarian tumors from metastases (e.g., from gastrointestinal tract) is challenging in mucinous subtypes, requiring IHC panels like CK7/CK20 to avoid misclassification.
Post-2015 advances have integrated molecular profiling with pathology, as per ESGO-ESMO-ESP consensus, reclassifying some high-grade serous cases based on genomic subtypes (e.g., BRCA-mutated) and incorporating HRD scores for refined prognostication and therapy selection. This approach, building on TCGA data, emphasizes combined histomorphologic and genomic evaluation for personalized management.

Staging and Grading

Staging of ovarian cancer determines the extent of disease spread and guides treatment decisions, primarily using the surgical-pathological system established by the International Federation of Gynecology and Obstetrics (FIGO). The 2025 FIGO system classifies tumors into stages I through IV, with substages reflecting specific features such as capsule integrity and surface involvement; it refines Stage III for better prognostic stratification, particularly emphasizing isolated retroperitoneal lymph node involvement under IIIA1 without intraperitoneal dissemination. Stage I indicates tumor confined to the ovaries or fallopian tubes, subdivided as follows: IA for tumor limited to one ovary or fallopian tube with an intact capsule and no surface involvement or malignant cells in ascites or peritoneal washings; IB for involvement of both ovaries or fallopian tubes under similar conditions; and IC for cases with surgical spill, preoperative capsule rupture, surface tumor, or malignant cells in ascites or washings, further divided into IC(i) for intraoperative rupture, IC(ii) for preoperative rupture or surface involvement, and IC(iii) for positive cytology. Stage II denotes pelvic extension below the pelvic brim, with IIA for involvement of the uterus or fallopian tubes without positive cytology and IIB for extension to other pelvic tissues. Stage III encompasses peritoneal spread outside the pelvis or retroperitoneal lymph node metastasis, with IIIA1 for positive retroperitoneal lymph nodes only (IIIA1(i) metastasis 10 mm in greatest dimension, IIIA1(ii) >10 mm); IIIA2 for microscopic extrapelvic peritoneal involvement positive retroperitoneal lymph nodes; IIIB for macroscopic peritoneal metastasis 2 cm beyond the pelvis lymph node involvement; and IIIC for macroscopic peritoneal metastasis >2 cm beyond the pelvis lymph node involvement. Stage IV represents distant metastasis, subdivided into IVA for pleural effusion with positive cytology and IVB for parenchymal organ or extra-abdominal nodal involvement.
The American Joint Committee on Cancer (AJCC) TNM classification aligns closely with the FIGO system, categorizing primary tumor (T), regional lymph nodes (N), and distant metastasis (M). In the TNM framework, T1 corresponds to FIGO stage I (tumor limited to ovaries or fallopian tubes, with T1a for one structure intact, T1b for both intact, and T1c for rupture or surface involvement); T2 to stage II (pelvic extension, T2a for adnexal/uterine spread, T2b for other pelvic sites); T3 to stage III (peritoneal or nodal spread, with T3a1 for small nodal metastasis, T3a2 for 2 cm peritoneal deposits, and T3b for 2-10 cm peritoneal involvement); and T4 to stage IV (abdominal wall or distant extension). Nodal status is N0 for no regional metastasis and N1 for positive pelvic or para-aortic nodes (N1i 10 mm, N1ii >10 mm), while M0 indicates no distant spread and M1 (M1a pleural, M1b extra-abdominal) denotes stage IV. This system facilitates international consistency in reporting disease extent.
Grading assesses tumor aggressiveness based on histological features, varying by subtype. For high-grade serous carcinoma, the predominant epithelial type, grading emphasizes marked nuclear atypia, prominent nucleoli, and high mitotic activity exceeding 12 mitoses per 10 high-power fields, distinguishing it from low-grade serous tumors with milder atypia and fewer mitoses. Endometrioid carcinomas, comprising about 10% of cases, use a three-tier FIGO architectural grading: grade 1 for well-differentiated tumors with >95% glandular architecture and <5% solid nonsquamous growth; grade 2 for 5-50% solid growth; and grade 3 for >50% solid growth with significant nuclear atypia. These criteria help predict behavior, as higher grades correlate with more aggressive disease.
The 2025 FIGO revisions, building on the 2014 system, include fallopian tube and primary peritoneal cancers alongside ovarian primaries, further refine stage III substages for prognostic stratification of peritoneal and nodal involvement based on evolving molecular studies on serous tubal intraepithelial carcinoma, and emphasize size-based classification of isolated retroperitoneal lymph nodes under IIIA1. Prognostically, stage significantly influences outcomes; for example, the 5-year relative survival rate for localized stage I disease approaches 92% (based on cases diagnosed 2015-2021), while distant stage IV drops to 31%. These rates underscore the importance of early detection, though most cases present at advanced stages.

Prevention and Screening

Preventive Measures

Preventive measures for ovarian cancer primarily target individuals at elevated risk, such as those with BRCA1 or BRCA2 pathogenic variants, through surgical and pharmacological interventions designed to mitigate disease onset. Risk-reducing salpingo-oophorectomy (RRSO), the removal of both fallopian tubes and ovaries, is the most effective strategy for high-risk women, recommended by the National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) between ages 35-40 for BRCA1 carriers and 40-45 for BRCA2 carriers once childbearing is complete. This procedure reduces ovarian cancer risk by 80-96% in BRCA mutation carriers.
For women at average risk undergoing pelvic surgery, such as hysterectomy or tubal ligation, opportunistic salpingectomyremoval of the fallopian tubesoffers a low-risk opportunity to decrease ovarian cancer incidence. The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) endorses discussing opportunistic salpingectomy as a preventive measure during these procedures, citing evidence from population studies showing up to a 65% risk reduction.
Chemopreventive options, including oral contraceptives, are advised for high-risk individuals to further lower ovarian cancer susceptibility. In BRCA1 mutation carriers, prolonged oral contraceptive use is associated with a significant, long-term reduction in ovarian cancer risk, with overall evidence indicating 30-50% protection across high-risk populations. NCCN and ACOG guidelines support their use in genetic carriers prior to surgical risk reduction, balancing benefits against potential breast cancer risks.
Emerging data on anti-inflammatory agents like low-dose aspirin suggest a modest preventive role, though primarily from observational studies rather than definitive trials. Regular low-dose aspirin use (100 mg daily) has been linked to a 10-23% reduction in ovarian cancer risk in women under 70, potentially through inflammation suppression, but the ASPREE randomized trial in older adults showed no overall cancer incidence benefit and highlighted bleeding risks.
Post-2020 guidelines from NCCN and ACOG emphasize fertility preservation in younger genetic carriers, recommending options like interval salpingectomy with delayed oophorectomy to allow childbearing while addressing tubal origins of many ovarian cancers. These approaches complement natural protective factors, such as multiparity and breastfeeding, which are associated with reduced risk but detailed separately.

Screening Approaches

Ovarian cancer screening in asymptomatic populations primarily involves multimodal approaches combining transvaginal ultrasound (TVUS) and serum CA-125 antigen testing, though these methods have not demonstrated a mortality benefit in large-scale trials. The Prostate, Lung, Colorectal, and Ovarian (PLCO) Cancer Screening Trial, involving over 78,000 women, evaluated annual screening with TVUS and CA-125 compared to usual care; initial results published in 2011 and extended follow-up through 2016 showed no reduction in ovarian cancer mortality, with a risk ratio of 1.06 (95% CI 0.87-1.30) in the screened arm. Similarly, the UK Collaborative Trial of Ovarian Cancer Screening (UKCTOCS), which enrolled more than 200,000 postmenopausal women, tested two strategies: annual TVUS alone and multimodal screening using the Risk of Ovarian Cancer Algorithm (ROCA)a method employing serial CA-125 measurements to calculate risk and trigger TVUS only when thresholds are exceededagainst no screening. The 2021 long-term follow-up (median 16.3 years) reported no significant mortality reduction in either arm, with an ovarian cancer mortality rate ratio of 0.98 (95% CI 0.85-1.12) for the multimodal group compared to controls.00731-5/fulltext) Both trials highlighted high false-positive rates, exceeding 80% in initial screens, leading to unnecessary interventions such as diagnostic surgeries.
The ROCA approach aims to improve specificity by monitoring longitudinal CA-125 trends rather than single thresholds, potentially reducing overdiagnosis while maintaining sensitivity for early detection. In UKCTOCS, the multimodal arm achieved a 47.2% increase in stage I diagnoses and a 24.5% decrease in stage IV cases compared to controls, representing a notable stage shift toward earlier disease without translating to survival gains. Despite this, the overall lack of mortality benefit has led major organizations, including the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force, to recommend against routine screening in average-risk women due to the potential harms from false positives.
For high-risk individuals, such as those with BRCA1 or BRCA2 mutations, guidelines recommend more intensive surveillance starting at an earlier age. The National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) advises annual TVUS and CA-125 testing beginning at age 30-35 years for BRCA carriers, alongside consideration of risk-reducing salpingo-oophorectomy, although evidence for screening efficacy remains limited and primarily based on expert consensus rather than randomized data.
Emerging strategies focus on non-invasive blood-based tests leveraging DNA methylation patterns for improved early detection. For instance, OvaPrint, a cell-free DNA methylation liquid biopsy, has shown promise in distinguishing high-grade serous ovarian carcinoma from benign pelvic masses with 91% accuracy in preliminary studies, and similar methylation assays approved for monitoring recurrence (such as those detecting tumor-specific methylation changes) are being adapted for screening applications in asymptomatic high-risk groups. These approaches aim to address the limitations of traditional methods by enhancing specificity and enabling earlier intervention, though prospective trials are ongoing to validate their impact on mortality.

Screening Limitations

Ovarian cancer's low incidence poses a fundamental challenge to effective population-based screening. The lifetime risk for women developing the disease is approximately 1 in 91, reflecting its relative rarity compared to more common cancers. This low prevalence means that even tests with high sensitivity and specificity yield a low positive predictive value, necessitating screening a large number of women to identify cases. In the UK Collaborative Trial of Ovarian Cancer Screening (UKCTOCS), an interim analysis estimated that 641 women would need to be screened annually for 14 years to prevent one ovarian cancer death using multimodal screening (CA-125 with transvaginal ultrasound referral).01224-6/fulltext) However, the trial's final long-term results demonstrated no statistically significant mortality reduction, underscoring the impracticality of achieving meaningful benefits at scale.00731-5/fulltext)
A major limitation of screening is the high rate of false-positive results, which can lead to significant patient harm. In large randomized trials like the Prostate, Lung, Colorectal, and Ovarian (PLCO) Cancer Screening Trial, cumulative false-positive rates reached approximately 10% over multiple screening rounds with CA-125 and transvaginal ultrasound. These false positives often prompt further invasive evaluations, including surgeries; in the PLCO trial, approximately 33% of women with false-positive results underwent major surgical procedures, such as oophorectomy. Complications from these unnecessary surgeries are notable, with serious adverse eventsincluding infections, bleeding, and cardiorespiratory issuesoccurring in 5-15% of cases, as reported in UKCTOCS and PLCO analyses.01224-6/fulltext)
Overdiagnosis represents another critical drawback, where screening detects slow-growing or indolent lesions that would not have caused harm during a woman's lifetime. Borderline ovarian tumors, which are often identified through screening, exemplify this issue; these tumors have an excellent prognosis with five-year survival rates exceeding 90% but may lead to overtreatment, including fertility-impairing surgeries.01224-6/fulltext) In UKCTOCS, a substantial proportion of screen-detected cases were borderline tumors, contributing to unnecessary interventions without improving overall survival.00731-5/fulltext)
Screening for ovarian cancer is also not cost-effective, particularly given the lack of proven mortality benefits. Economic models based on trial data indicate incremental cost-effectiveness ratios exceeding $100,000 per quality-adjusted life-year (QALY) gained for strategies like multimodal screening, far above commonly accepted thresholds of $50,000-$100,000 per QALY in high-income settings. These costs encompass repeated testing, diagnostic follow-ups, and surgical interventions, amplifying the economic burden without commensurate health gains.
Equity concerns further limit the viability of routine screening, especially in low-resource settings where access to even basic diagnostic and surgical care is restricted. In low- and middle-income countries, ovarian cancer outcomes are poorer due to delayed diagnosis and inadequate infrastructure, making widespread screening infeasible and potentially exacerbating disparities. Major health organizations, including the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force and the World Health Organization in their broader cancer control guidelines, recommend against population-based screening for ovarian cancer in average-risk women, citing insufficient evidence of benefit alongside these harms and inequities.

Management

Surgical Interventions

Surgical interventions form the cornerstone of treatment for ovarian cancer, particularly for epithelial subtypes, where the goal is to achieve maximal tumor cytoreduction while incorporating diagnostic staging to guide subsequent therapy. Primary cytoreductive surgery, also known as primary debulking surgery, is recommended as the initial approach for patients with stage I to III disease who are medically fit, involving removal of visible tumors to improve outcomes. This procedure typically encompasses total abdominal hysterectomy, bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy (removal of the uterus, both ovaries, and fallopian tubes), infracolic omentectomy (resection of the omentum to eliminate potential metastatic sites), and systematic pelvic and para-aortic lymphadenectomy to assess lymph node involvement. The primary objective is optimal cytoreduction, defined as leaving no residual disease or nodules smaller than 1 cm in diameter, which has been associated with improved progression-free and overall survival compared to suboptimal debulking.
Comprehensive staging is integral to primary surgery and is performed via exploratory laparotomy to evaluate the extent of disease spread, even in apparent early-stage cases. This includes obtaining peritoneal washings for cytologic examination from the pelvis, paracolic gutters, and diaphragm to detect microscopic malignant cells, as well as multiple peritoneal biopsies from all quadrants and suspicious areas to identify occult metastases that may upstage the disease. In up to 30% of clinically early-stage cases, such staging reveals unrecognized advanced disease, influencing adjuvant treatment decisions.
For patients with advanced stage III or IV disease where upfront primary cytoreduction is not feasible due to extensive tumor burden, poor performance status, or comorbidities, interval debulking surgery is employed after 3 to 4 cycles of neoadjuvant chemotherapy to shrink the tumor and facilitate resection. This approach aims to achieve the same cytoreductive goals as primary surgery, with evidence from randomized trials showing comparable survival benefits when optimal debulking (residual disease <1 cm) is attained, though it is generally better tolerated with reduced perioperative morbidity.
In select young women with early-stage (FIGO stage IA or IC grade 1-2) epithelial ovarian cancer confined to one ovary and a strong desire for future fertility, fertility-sparing surgery may be offered, preserving the uterus and contralateral ovary and fallopian tube alongside comprehensive staging procedures such as omentectomy and lymphadenectomy. This conservative approach, typically involving unilateral salpingo-oophorectomy, has demonstrated oncologic safety with recurrence rates and survival outcomes equivalent to radical surgery in favorable histology cases, allowing potential childbearing post-treatment.
Since the early 2010s, minimally invasive techniques, including laparoscopy, have gained adoption for staging and treatment of early-stage ovarian cancer, offering benefits such as reduced hospital stay, lower blood loss, and faster recovery compared to open laparotomy. These procedures mirror open surgery components but use small incisions and a camera for visualization; however, conversion to open laparotomy occurs in 3% to 15% of cases, often due to adhesions, obesity, or unexpected extensive disease. Laparoscopy is not routinely recommended for advanced disease cytoreduction due to challenges in achieving complete resection, though it may be used diagnostically or in interval settings post-chemotherapy.

Systemic Therapies

Systemic therapies for ovarian cancer primarily encompass chemotherapy and targeted agents, which are used to treat microscopic and metastatic disease, often in combination with surgical interventions such as neoadjuvant chemotherapy prior to debulking. The standard first-line chemotherapy regimen involves platinum-based agents, typically carboplatin combined with paclitaxel, administered intravenously over six cycles every three weeks. This combination has demonstrated efficacy in improving progression-free survival in patients with advanced epithelial ovarian cancer.
For patients who achieve optimal debulking surgery, intraperitoneal (IP) chemotherapy may be considered as an alternative to intravenous administration, delivering higher drug concentrations directly to the peritoneal cavity where ovarian cancer often spreads. IP regimens, such as cisplatin with paclitaxel, have been associated with prolonged overall survival in select cases of stage III disease with no residual tumor after surgery. However, IP therapy requires careful patient selection due to its technical demands and potential for increased local toxicity.
Platinum sensitivity is a key prognostic factor in ovarian cancer, defined as a treatment-free interval exceeding six months following platinum-based therapy, indicating responsive disease. In contrast, platinum-resistant disease recurs within six months and is associated with poorer outcomes, prompting alternative regimens such as weekly paclitaxel or gemcitabine-based combinations. These options aim to manage refractory cases while minimizing cumulative toxicity from repeated platinum exposure.
Targeted therapies have revolutionized maintenance treatment post-chemotherapy, particularly poly (ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP) inhibitors such as olaparib or niraparib for patients with BRCA1/2 mutations or homologous recombination deficiency (HRD). Olaparib and niraparib are approved for first-line maintenance in advanced ovarian cancer; the NCCN Guidelines (version 3.2025) recommend niraparib for BRCA wild-type or HRD-positive patients who did not receive bevacizumab frontline, significantly extending progression-free survival by exploiting DNA repair vulnerabilities in HRD-positive tumors. This approach is most effective in BRCA-mutated or HRD tumors, where response rates can exceed those in homologous recombination-proficient cases.
Bevacizumab, an anti-vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) monoclonal antibody, is incorporated into first-line therapy for advanced-stage ovarian cancer to inhibit tumor angiogenesis. When added to carboplatin and paclitaxel followed by maintenance bevacizumab, it improves progression-free survival in stage III or IV disease, though overall survival benefits are modest. Its use is particularly beneficial in high-risk patients with suboptimal cytoreduction.
Alternative regimens, such as dose-dense weekly paclitaxel combined with carboplatin, have shown potential benefits in specific subgroups, including non-BRCA mutated patients, based on findings from the GOG-262 trial. This schedule, delivering paclitaxel at 80 mg/m weekly, may enhance efficacy by increasing drug exposure while potentially reducing severe toxicity compared to every-three-week dosing. The trial demonstrated improved progression-free survival in the absence of bevacizumab, supporting its consideration in select frontline settings.
Common toxicities from these systemic therapies include peripheral neuropathy, affecting 20-30% of patients receiving taxane-based regimens like paclitaxel, and myelosuppression, which can lead to neutropenia and anemia. Neuropathy often manifests as sensory symptoms and may persist post-treatment, while myelosuppression is managed with granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF) to prevent febrile neutropenia and support dose intensity. Prophylactic G-CSF use has been shown to reduce hospitalization rates and maintain treatment adherence in myelosuppressive regimens.

Radiation and Hormonal Options

Radiation therapy plays a limited role in the management of ovarian cancer, primarily reserved for specific subtypes and palliative settings due to the efficacy of surgical and systemic approaches in most cases. For germ cell tumors, particularly dysgerminomas, whole pelvic radiation is occasionally employed as adjuvant therapy following surgery or for recurrent disease, typically at doses of 20 to 30 Gy, though its use remains rare given the sensitivity of these tumors to chemotherapy. This modality targets residual microscopic disease but is not standard for epithelial ovarian cancers, where whole abdominal irradiation has largely been abandoned in favor of more targeted treatments.
In palliative contexts, radiation therapy is utilized to alleviate symptoms from symptomatic metastases, such as pain from bony involvement, bleeding, or bowel obstruction in recurrent or persistent epithelial ovarian cancer. Palliative regimens, often delivered as external beam radiation at doses like 25 Gy in five fractions, achieve symptom relief in most patients within one month, with durable responses exceeding three months in many cases. These approaches provide localized control without the systemic toxicity of chemotherapy, making them suitable for patients with platinum-resistant disease or those seeking symptom management.
Hormonal therapies target estrogen receptor (ER)- or progesterone receptor (PR)-positive subtypes, offering a low-toxicity option for low-grade endometrioid, serous, or granulosa cell tumors. Tamoxifen, a selective estrogen receptor modulator, and aromatase inhibitors such as letrozole or anastrozole are commonly used in these ER/PR-positive cases, particularly for recurrent or advanced disease, with objective response rates ranging from 10% to 20%. These agents disrupt hormone-driven tumor growth, providing disease stabilization for months to years in responsive patients, though they are less effective in high-grade serous carcinomas.
Immunotherapies, specifically immune checkpoint inhibitors, are emerging for ovarian cancer subsets with microsatellite instability-high (MSI-H) or mismatch repair-deficient (dMMR) features, which occur in approximately 5% to 10% of cases. Pembrolizumab, a PD-1 inhibitor, is approved under tumor-agnostic indications for advanced MSI-H/dMMR solid tumors, including ovarian cancer, based on data from trials like KEYNOTE-100 showing objective response rates (ORR) of around 30% to 40% in this subgroup, compared to 8% overall in recurrent epithelial ovarian cancer. Dostarlimab, another PD-1 inhibitor, received FDA accelerated approval in 2021 for adult patients with dMMR recurrent or advanced solid tumors that have progressed on prior treatment, including ovarian cancer, based on the GARNET trial. The 2025 FIRST/ENGOT-OV44 phase 3 trial demonstrated improved progression-free survival when dostarlimab was added to standard chemotherapy and niraparib maintenance in frontline advanced ovarian cancer, though without overall survival benefit, supporting further evaluation in this setting. Ongoing trials of checkpoint inhibitors, such as PD-1/PD-L1 blockers, report ORRs of 10% to 15% in broader platinum-resistant populations, with enhanced activity when combined with anti-angiogenics or PARP inhibitors.
For recurrent low-grade subtypes, combinations of hormonal agents with targeted therapies, such as BRAF inhibitors, are being explored and show synergistic effects, with response rates up to 25% in trials for low-grade serous ovarian cancer. In May 2025, the FDA granted accelerated approval to the combination of avutometinib and defactinib for adult patients with KRAS-mutated recurrent low-grade serous ovarian cancer, based on the RAMP 201 trial showing an objective response rate of 30.8%. These multimodal strategies aim to overcome resistance while minimizing toxicity in hormonally sensitive, recurrent disease.

Supportive and Palliative Care

Supportive and palliative care for ovarian cancer emphasizes holistic management to alleviate symptoms, enhance quality of life, and provide emotional support throughout the disease trajectory, particularly in advanced or recurrent stages. This approach integrates multidisciplinary interventions to address physical, nutritional, psychological, and end-of-life needs, often alongside disease-directed therapies.
Symptom control is a cornerstone of palliative care, targeting common complications such as ascites and bowel obstruction. For malignant ascites, which causes abdominal distension and discomfort, therapeutic paracentesis provides rapid relief by draining excess fluid; procedures are typically repeated every 2-4 weeks depending on reaccumulation rates, with studies showing paracentesis intervals extending from 14 to 28 days in ovarian cancer patients. In cases of bowel obstruction, palliative self-expanding metallic stents offer a less invasive option to restore luminal patency, achieving clinical success in approximately 77% of patients with malignant colorectal obstruction from gynecologic cancers, thereby reducing the need for surgery and improving symptom relief.
Nutritional support addresses cachexia, a prevalent issue in ovarian cancer characterized by progressive weight loss and muscle wasting despite adequate intake. When oral nutrition is insufficient, enteral feeding via tube is recommended as the preferred method to maintain nutritional status, prevent further deterioration, and support overall well-being in malnourished patients.
Psychosocial care focuses on mitigating the emotional toll of the disease, including counseling for fertility loss and changes in body image. Fertility preservation counseling is essential for reproductive-age women, as treatments like chemotherapy and surgery can lead to premature ovarian failure; structured psychological interviews help patients process grief and decision-making related to reproductive health. Body image concerns, often exacerbated by surgical scars, ostomies, or hair loss, are addressed through targeted support to rebuild self-esteem and intimacy. Participation in support groups provides peer connection, reducing isolation and offering practical coping strategies for those affected by ovarian cancer.
Psychosocial support extends to family members, particularly children of patients with ovarian cancer. When explaining the diagnosis to children, parents should use simple, honest, and age-appropriate language. Reassurances should include that the cancer is not contagious, is not the child's fault, and that the child is loved and will be cared for. For young children, explanations can be straightforward, such as: "Some cells in Mommy's tummy got sick and are growing too much, so doctors give special medicine to help her feel better." For older children, it can be explained that the ovaries are small organs in the lower abdomen that help make eggs and support reproduction, and that treatments like medicine or surgery aim to remove or control the abnormal cells. Explanations should be tailored to the child's age and maturity, questions should be encouraged, and normal routines maintained to provide stability.
In recurrent disease, palliative chemotherapy with single agents, such as weekly paclitaxel or topotecan, prioritizes quality-of-life preservation over aggressive tumor control, effectively palliating symptoms like pain and fatigue while minimizing toxicity in platinum-resistant cases.
Hospice integration facilitates dignified end-of-life care, with referral typically occurring when curative intent shifts to comfort-focused management; median survival following this transition in advanced ovarian cancer is approximately 3-6 months.

Prognosis

Survival Outcomes

The overall 5-year relative survival rate for ovarian cancer is approximately 52%, based on data from the Surveillance, Epidemiology, and End Results (SEER) program for cases diagnosed between 2015 and 2021. Stage-specific survival rates vary significantly, with early detection leading to markedly better outcomes; for localized disease confined to one or both ovaries, the 5-year relative survival rate is 92%, while it drops to 71% for regional disease (spread to nearby structures or lymph nodes within the pelvis or abdomen), and 32% for distant disease (distant metastasis, corresponding to stage IV). These figures reflect population-based data primarily from high-resource settings and underscore the importance of stage at diagnosis in determining prognosis. For distant-stage invasive epithelial ovarian cancer (stage IV), the 5-year relative survival rate is approximately 32%. Malignant pleural effusion typically indicates stage IVA, which may have a slightly better outlook than stage IVB (parenchymal metastases) in select cases with optimal treatment, with some studies reporting median overall survival of approximately 24 months for stage IVA compared to shorter survival in stage IVB, though overall prognosis for stage IV remains poor and advanced.
In contrast, survival without chemotherapy or in untreated cases is significantly poorer. For example, in a population-based study of women aged over 65 years with stage III/IV ovarian cancer, median overall survival was 1.7 months with no treatment and 2.2 months with surgery only (no chemotherapy). Specific data for stage IV disease with malignant pleural effusion without chemotherapy are limited, but untreated advanced ovarian cancer generally results in short survival, typically around 1-3 months, due to rapid disease progression and complications such as effusion-related symptoms.
Survival outcomes have improved over time, with the 5-year relative survival rate rising from 36% for cases diagnosed in 1975 to 52% by 2021, attributable to advancements in surgical techniques, chemotherapy regimens, and targeted therapies such as PARP inhibitors. Geographic variations highlight disparities in access to care; in the United States, the rate stands at approximately 52%, similar to many European countries where it ranges from 40% to 50%, whereas in low- and middle-income countries, it is often below 30% due to limited diagnostic and treatment resources.
For patients with advanced-stage ovarian cancer (regional and distant, which comprise the majority of diagnoses), median overall survival with optimal debulking surgery followed by platinum-based chemotherapy is 3 to 5 years. In pediatric cases, which are rare and often involve germ cell tumors rather than epithelial types, 5-year survival exceeds 90% with multimodal therapy including surgery, chemotherapy, and sometimes radiation.

Prognostic Indicators

Prognostic indicators in ovarian cancer encompass a range of clinical and molecular factors that influence patient outcomes, including overall survival (OS) and response to therapy. These factors help stratify risk and guide clinical decision-making beyond traditional staging.
The extent of residual disease following cytoreductive surgery is a key surgical prognostic indicator. Achievement of optimal debulking, defined as residual disease less than 1 cm, significantly improves OS compared to suboptimal cytoreduction with larger residuals. For instance, patients with small-volume residual disease (1 cm) have a median OS of approximately 54 months, versus 24-26 months for those with larger residuals (>1 cm), representing an improvement of 20-30 months.
Molecular factors, particularly germline mutations in BRCA1 or BRCA2 genes, are associated with favorable prognosis in ovarian cancer. These mutations confer improved response to platinum-based chemotherapy and PARP inhibitors, leading to enhanced OS. Patients with BRCA1/2 mutations exhibit a median OS advantage of about 12-23 months over non-mutated cases, with hazard ratios for OS around 0.67 indicating reduced mortality risk.
Age and performance status are critical clinical prognosticators. Women over 65 years of age experience substantially worse survival outcomes, with median OS often halved compared to younger patients due to comorbidities and treatment tolerability issues. This effect is particularly pronounced in elderly patients (70 years) with comorbidities such as heart failure and chronic kidney disease (CKD), where frailty and reduced tolerance to aggressive treatments (e.g., optimal debulking surgery and chemotherapy) significantly worsen prognosis. Patients with cardiovascular comorbidities have a median overall survival of approximately 25 months compared to around 48 months in those without such comorbidities. High comorbidity burden, as assessed by indices such as the Charlson Comorbidity Index, is an independent predictor of poorer survival in older patients. Similarly, Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) performance status impacts prognosis markedly; patients with ECOG 0-1 (fully active or restricted in physically strenuous activity) have median OS exceeding 90 months, whereas those with ECOG 3-4 (severely disabled or bedridden) face significantly shorter survival, often under 30 months.
Histological subtype serves as an important prognostic determinant, with variations in aggressiveness and chemosensitivity. High-grade serous carcinomas generally have better 5-year OS rates of around 40-50% compared to mucinous or clear cell subtypes, which are linked to poorer outcomes with 5-year survival rates of 30-40% or lower, reflecting resistance to standard therapies.
Biomarkers such as pre-treatment CA-125 levels provide additional prognostic insight. Lower pre-treatment CA-125 concentrations (e.g., <35 U/mL) are associated with better response to treatment and improved survival, while elevated levels correlate with advanced disease and reduced OS.

Recurrence Patterns

Ovarian cancer recurrence is common following initial treatment, with approximately 70-80% of patients with advanced epithelial disease experiencing relapse. Most recurrences occur within the first 2 years after completing primary therapy. The timing of recurrence relative to prior platinum-based chemotherapy defines sensitivity: platinum-sensitive disease recurs more than 6 months after the last platinum dose, while platinum-resistant disease recurs within 6 months, influencing subsequent treatment options.
The peritoneum is the most frequent site of recurrence, accounting for about 60-70% of cases due to the cancer's propensity for intraperitoneal spread. Retroperitoneal lymph nodes represent approximately 20% of recurrences, often involving pelvic or para-aortic regions. Distant metastases, such as to the liver or pleura, comprise the remaining 20%, typically via hematogenous dissemination.
Detection of recurrence relies on surveillance strategies that include clinical examinations and optional CA-125 monitoring every 2-4 months during the first 2 years post-treatment, with computed tomography (CT) scans recommended only if CA-125 levels rise or symptoms emerge. Elevated CA-125, often doubling from baseline, prompts confirmatory imaging to confirm relapse before initiating further intervention.
Recurrence patterns vary by histological subtype. High-grade serous ovarian cancer, the most common type, tends to recur early, with 20-30% of cases relapsing within 6 months of initial therapy due to aggressive biology. In contrast, low-grade serous carcinoma exhibits a more indolent course, with recurrences often occurring later and at lower rates, reflecting its distinct molecular profile.
Recent 2024 analyses of maintenance poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP) inhibitor therapy demonstrate a reduction in recurrence risk by approximately 50% in patients with homologous recombination deficiency (HRD), based on progression-free survival benefits in frontline and recurrent settings, contributing to improved overall prognosis in these subgroups.

Epidemiology

Global Incidence and Trends

Ovarian cancer ranks as the eighth most common cancer among women globally, with an estimated 324,603 new cases diagnosed in 2022. In the same year, the disease accounted for 206,956 deaths worldwide, yielding an incidence-to-mortality ratio of approximately 1.6:1 and underscoring its poor prognosis relative to other female cancers. Age-standardized incidence rates vary substantially by region, ranging from low levels in parts of Africa and Asia (around 4-6 per 100,000 women) to higher rates in Northern Europe and North America (up to 10-12 per 100,000), reflecting differences in reproductive histories and healthcare access.
Over recent decades, temporal trends in ovarian cancer incidence have diverged by economic development level. In high-income countries, rates have declined largely attributable to widespread use of oral contraceptiveswhich reduce risk by 30-50% with long-term useand increased rates of hysterectomy, which incidentally lower ovarian exposure to hormonal influences. Conversely, incidence is rising in low- and middle-income regions of Asia and Africa, driven by population growth, aging demographics, and shifts toward Westernized lifestyles that may elevate risk through factors like delayed childbearing. The disease predominantly affects older women, with peak incidence occurring between ages 60 and 70; cases in women under 40 represent less than 1% of all diagnoses, often involving germ cell rather than epithelial subtypes.
Projections indicate that global age-standardized incidence rates will remain relatively stable through 2040, though absolute case numbers are expected to rise to around 428,000 annually due to population expansion and aging. Accompanying this stability in overall rates, there are emerging shifts in histological subtype distribution, with a noted increase in the proportion of endometrioid ovarian cancers, potentially linked to rising endometriosis prevalence and improved diagnostic classification.

Demographic Disparities

Ovarian cancer incidence and outcomes exhibit significant disparities across ethnic groups. Among Ashkenazi Jewish women, the prevalence of founder mutations in BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes is notably higher, with approximately 2% of the population carrying these mutations, leading to an elevated lifetime risk of ovarian cancer ranging from 11% to 44% for carriers. Consequently, 29% to 41% of ovarian cancer cases in this population are attributable to these hereditary mutations. In contrast, Black women experience worse survival rates compared to White women; the 5-year survival rate is 41% for Black women versus 49% for White women, even after adjustment for stage at diagnosis. These disparities persist despite similar stage distributions at presentation and are influenced by factors such as access to care and treatment variations.
Geographic variations in incidence rates are pronounced, with higher burdens in developed regions. Age-standardized incidence rates reach up to 11.4 per 100,000 women in Central and Eastern Europe and approximately 10.3 per 100,000 in the United States, reflecting patterns in Northern Europe and North America. In contrast, rates are substantially lower in Asia, often around 5 per 100,000 or less in countries like China, attributed to differences in reproductive histories, hormonal exposures, and genetic factors. These regional differences highlight the role of environmental and lifestyle influences in disease distribution.
Age is a critical demographic factor, with the majority of cases occurring in older women. Approximately 60% of ovarian cancer diagnoses happen in women over 65 years, and incidence rises rapidly after age 50, peaking in the 70s. However, underdiagnosis is common in the elderly, as general practitioners are less likely to recognize and refer older patients presenting with symptoms suggestive of ovarian cancer, potentially due to attribution of vague symptoms to age-related conditions. This contributes to later-stage diagnoses and poorer outcomes in this group.
Socioeconomic status further exacerbates disparities, particularly through reduced access to screening and timely care. Women in low socioeconomic status (SES) areas face barriers to early detection, resulting in a 10% to 20% higher mortality risk compared to those in higher SES groups, based on meta-analyses of treatment receipt and survival data.

Special Populations

Ovarian Cancer in Pregnancy

Ovarian cancer complicating pregnancy is a rare occurrence, with an estimated incidence of 1 in 18,000 pregnancies. The majority of cases involve epithelial ovarian cancers, though germ cell tumors are disproportionately represented relative to non-pregnant populations due to the younger age of pregnant patients.
Diagnosis in pregnant patients requires careful consideration to minimize risks to the fetus. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is the preferred imaging modality over computed tomography (CT) to avoid ionizing radiation exposure. Tumor marker CA-125 is less reliable during pregnancy, as levels can be physiologically elevated by decidual and granulosa cells, often reaching up to 200 U/mL without indicating malignancy. The majority of cases are diagnosed at stage I, owing to early detection via routine prenatal ultrasounds and the younger age of patients, resulting in favorable prognosis compared to non-pregnant cases.
Management strategies prioritize both maternal oncologic outcomes and fetal well-being, often involving a multidisciplinary team of obstetricians, oncologists, and surgeons. Surgical intervention, such as cystectomy or staging laparotomy, is generally recommended in the second trimester to reduce risks of spontaneous abortion or preterm labor associated with first-trimester procedures, with fertility-sparing approaches favored when possible to preserve reproductive potential. Chemotherapy, typically with carboplatin combined with paclitaxel, is considered safe after the first trimester (post-14 weeks gestation), as the organogenesis period has passed, and it can be administered until 3-4 weeks before delivery to allow drug clearance.
Maternal outcomes in pregnant patients with ovarian cancer are comparable to those in non-pregnant women when the disease is properly staged and treated according to gestational age. Fetal risks, including congenital malformations from second- or third-trimester chemotherapy, remain low at less than 5%, aligning closely with background population rates.
Current guidelines, including those from the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) in 2021, emphasize a multidisciplinary approach to care, recommending observation or delay of treatment for early-stage disease if feasible, while advocating prompt intervention for advanced cases to optimize maternal survival without undue fetal compromise.

Veterinary Ovarian Cancer

Ovarian cancer in veterinary medicine primarily affects intact female animals, with dogs being the most commonly studied species due to higher reported incidence compared to other domestic animals. In dogs, ovarian tumors account for approximately 0.5% to 1.2% of all canine neoplasms overall, but this rises to 6.25% among intact bitches. These tumors are exceedingly rare in cats, comprising 0.7% to 3.6% of all feline tumors. Histologically, canine ovarian tumors are classified into three main categories: epithelial tumors (40% to 50% of cases, including adenocarcinomas), sex cord-stromal tumors (about 34%, such as granulosa cell tumors), and germ cell tumors (less common, including dysgerminomas and teratomas). In cats, sex cord-stromal tumors predominate, while epithelial types are very uncommon.
Clinical signs of ovarian cancer in dogs and cats often manifest late due to the abdominal location of the ovaries, including abdominal distension from ascites or mass effect, lethargy, weight loss, and reproductive abnormalities such as persistent estrus, anestrus, or pyometra. In intact females, spaying via ovariohysterectomy effectively prevents nearly all cases of ovarian neoplasia, as these tumors do not develop in the absence of ovarian tissue, though small remnants post-surgery can rarely lead to tumor formation. Breeds like English Bulldogs, German Shepherds, and Yorkshire Terriers may show predisposition, but overall risk is tied to intact status rather than breed-specific factors in most reports.
Canine models of ovarian cancer provide valuable translational insights for human research, particularly due to shared genetic pathways such as BRCA1 and BRCA2 mutations, which are implicated in both canine and human ovarian tumorigenesis and enable studies on targeted therapies.
Treatment typically involves surgical ovariohysterectomy as the cornerstone, often followed by adjuvant chemotherapy for malignant or metastatic cases; carboplatin is a commonly used platinum-based agent, administered intravenously at 300 mg/m every 3 weeks. With surgery alone for benign or early-stage tumors, survival can extend several years, while malignant cases treated with chemotherapy yield median survival times of 1 to 3 years, depending on metastasis and effusion presence.
In wildlife, ovarian cancer has been documented in elephants, though overall cancer incidence remains remarkably low (less than 5% in necropsied individuals), attributed to the species' multiple copies of the TP53 gene (up to 20 retrogenes), which enhance p53 protein function and apoptosis in response to DNA damage, providing broad tumor suppression including against reproductive cancers.

Research Advances

Biomarker and Screening Innovations

Ongoing research into biomarkers and screening for ovarian cancer emphasizes non-invasive methods to enable earlier detection, potentially improving survival rates by identifying the disease before symptoms manifest. Liquid biopsies, which analyze circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) in blood samples, have emerged as a promising tool, particularly for detecting TP53 mutations prevalent in over 90% of high-grade serous ovarian carcinomas. Studies have reported sensitivities of 70-90% for ctDNA-based detection of TP53 mutations in advanced-stage disease, allowing for monitoring of tumor burden and genetic alterations without invasive procedures.
Multi-omics approaches integrate proteomics, genomics, and other data layers to enhance diagnostic accuracy through biomarker panels. The Ova1 test, approved by the FDA in 2009, combines five serum protein markersprealbumin, transferrin, CA-125 II, apolipoprotein A1, and transthyretinto assess the risk of malignancy in women with pelvic masses, achieving a sensitivity of 99% for epithelial ovarian cancer when used preoperatively. Similarly, the Overa panel, an evolution incorporating HE4, CA-125, and three additional biomarkers (e.g., IGFBP-2, leptin, osteopontin), demonstrates improved specificity over single-marker tests, with negative predictive values around 99% in clinical evaluations for distinguishing benign from malignant masses. These panels are particularly valuable in triaging patients for surgical intervention.
Artificial intelligence (AI) integration with imaging modalities, such as ultrasound, is advancing risk stratification for adnexal lesions. The International Ovarian Tumor Analysis (IOTA) group's machine learning models, including the Simple Rules and Assessment of Different Neoplasias in the adneXa (ADNEX) model, use ultrasound features like lesion morphology and vascularity to predict malignancy, with reported specificities up to 95% in prospective validation studies across diverse populations. These AI-enhanced tools outperform traditional subjective assessments by radiologists, reducing unnecessary surgeries for benign conditions.30052-7/fulltext)
Promising clinical trials are evaluating multi-cancer early detection platforms tailored for ovarian cancer. The GRAIL Galleri test, which employs methylation-based analysis of cell-free DNA to detect multiple cancers, completed a phase 3 trial in 2024 with an ovarian cancer arm showing 83.1% overall sensitivity (50% for stage I, 80% for stage II, 87.1% for stage III, and 94.7% for stage IV) and a specificity of 99.5%, though ongoing analyses focus on its utility in high-risk screening populations. To address persistent challenges like high false-positive rates in screening, longitudinal algorithms that track biomarker trends over timesuch as serial CA-125 monitoring combined with AI pattern recognitionare being developed to improve positive predictive values, potentially lowering overdiagnosis in asymptomatic women.

Novel Therapeutic Developments

Antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) represent a significant advancement in targeted therapy for ovarian cancer, particularly in platinum-resistant cases. Mirvetuximab soravtansine-gynx (Elahere), an ADC targeting folate receptor alpha (FR), received accelerated FDA approval on November 14, 2022, for adult patients with FR-high, platinum-resistant epithelial ovarian, fallopian tube, or primary peritoneal cancer who have received one to three prior systemic treatment regimens. This approval was based on the phase II SORAYA trial, which demonstrated an objective response rate (ORR) of 31.7% in 106 evaluable patients, with a median duration of response of 6.9 months and median progression-free survival (PFS) of 4.3 months. Full FDA approval followed in March 2024, supported by confirmatory phase III MIRASOL trial data showing a 32% reduction in death risk compared to chemotherapy.
Chimeric antigen receptor T-cell (CAR-T) therapies targeting MUC16 (also known as CA-125) are emerging in early-phase trials for recurrent or refractory ovarian cancer. In a phase I trial evaluating Muc16CD-specific CAR-T cells, the therapy was well tolerated with evidence of persistence in the blood of patients with recurrent platinum-resistant disease, and preclinical models confirmed tumor infiltration and antitumor activity against MUC16-positive cells.00110-3) Updated 2025 data from ongoing phase I studies highlight CAR-T cells' ability to infiltrate ovarian tumors; for example, the Roswell Park trial targets MUC16 and demonstrates cytotoxicity in heterogeneous tumor environments, while the Stanford trial targets B7-H3 and shows persistence and antitumor activity in recurrent platinum-resistant cases. These approaches address challenges like antigen shedding by incorporating dual-targeting strategies, such as tandem CAR-T cells against MUC16 and mesothelin, which enhance infiltration and reduce escape in preclinical ovarian cancer models.
Combination therapies integrating PARP inhibitors with immunotherapy have shown promise in enhancing response durability. The MOONSTONE (GOG-3032) phase II trial evaluated niraparib plus dostarlimab in patients with recurrent platinum-resistant ovarian cancer, reporting an ORR of 22.9% and disease control rate of 52.1%, with manageable toxicity including anemia and nausea. Building on this, the phase III FIRST/ENGOT-OV44 trial in first-line advanced ovarian cancer demonstrated that adding dostarlimab to platinum-based chemotherapy followed by niraparib maintenance improved median PFS to 20.6 months compared to 19.2 months with niraparib alone, without significant safety interactions. These combinations leverage synthetic lethality from PARP inhibition and immune checkpoint blockade to target DNA repair-deficient tumors, particularly those with homologous recombination deficiency.
Updates in anti-angiogenic therapies focus on bevacizumab combinations to extend maintenance regimens. The phase II OVARIO trial assessed niraparib plus bevacizumab as first-line maintenance in advanced ovarian cancer, achieving a 6-month PFS rate of 98% in HRD-positive patients and demonstrating synergistic vascular normalization with PARP inhibition to improve tumor perfusion and immune access.00881-9/fulltext) Recent real-world data from 2023-2025 confirm that bevacizumab added to chemotherapy in recurrent settings improves PFS by approximately 3-4 months and ORR by 10-15% in diverse populations, including those with BRCA mutations, though benefits are modest in heavily pretreated patients. Combinations with novel agents like mirvetuximab soravtansine have also yielded promising preliminary results in platinum-resistant disease, with enhanced response rates across FR expression levels.
Subtype-specific therapies, such as MEK inhibitors for low-grade serous ovarian cancer (LGSOC), target MAPK pathway aberrations prevalent in this histology. The SOLAR phase Ib trial of selumetinib in RAS-mutant ovarian cancer, including a LGSOC subset, reported an ORR of 44% and clinical benefit rate of 77% in nine LGSOC patients, highlighting efficacy in KRAS-altered tumors.01311-2/fulltext) Broader phase II/III data from the LOGS trial with trametinib versus standard chemotherapy showed an ORR of 26% with the MEK inhibitor, compared to 14% with chemotherapy, establishing it as a chemotherapy-free option for recurrent LGSOC with improved PFS (13 months versus 7.2 months).02175-9/fulltext) These agents exploit constitutive ERK signaling in LGSOC, offering durable responses in BRAF/RAS-mutated cases while minimizing toxicity associated with cytotoxic regimens.

Clinical Trial Landscape

The clinical trial landscape for ovarian cancer in 2025 encompasses a diverse array of studies spanning phases I through III, with a strong emphasis on maintenance therapies, targeted agents, and emerging immunotherapies for advanced and recurrent disease. As of late 2025, over 200 active or recruiting trials are listed on ClinicalTrials.gov for ovarian cancer, reflecting ongoing efforts to address unmet needs in frontline and relapsed settings. Major phase III trials have shaped this landscape, including PAOLA-1, which demonstrated that maintenance olaparib combined with bevacizumab extended median progression-free survival (PFS) to 37.2 months in patients with newly diagnosed advanced ovarian cancer and homologous recombination deficiency (HRD)-positive tumors, compared to 17.7 months with bevacizumab alone. Similarly, the PRIMA trial established niraparib as an effective first-line maintenance therapy, prolonging median PFS to 21.6 months versus 12.5 months with placebo in patients with newly diagnosed advanced ovarian cancer, regardless of biomarker status.
A significant portion of current trials targets recurrent or platinum-resistant ovarian cancer, where options remain limited, with examples including studies on hypermutated tumors responsive to immunotherapy. For instance, a phase II trial of nivolumab in patients with mismatch repair-deficient (dMMR) or hypermutated advanced ovarian cancer reported an objective response rate of 31.4%, highlighting potential for immune checkpoint inhibitors in this subgroup. Emerging areas also include vaccine therapies, such as the Vigil (gemogenovatucel-T) immunotherapy, a personalized vaccine targeting neoantigens in advanced ovarian cancer; phase II/III trials have shown tolerability and clinical benefit, including improved recurrence-free survival in BRCA-wildtype patients when used as frontline maintenance. Additionally, microbiome modulation is gaining traction, with preclinical and early-phase studies exploring gut microbiota influences on therapy response, such as associations between microbiome profiles and PARP inhibitor efficacy, and interventions like dietary mannose to enhance immunotherapy by reprogramming the tumor microenvironment.
Despite these advances, challenges persist in trial conduct, including patient accrual difficulties due to the disease's rarity and rapid progression, which can limit enrollment in multi-arm studies. Endpoint harmonization also poses issues, as progression-free survival (PFS) often serves as the primary measure in late-phase trials but may not consistently correlate with overall survival (OS), complicating regulatory approvals and cross-trial comparisons. Funding has supported this robust pipeline, with the National Cancer Institute (NCI) providing grants for clinical research through programs like the Specialized Programs of Research Excellence (SPORE), and the Ovarian Cancer Research Program (OCRP) allocating $45 million in FY2024 for patient-centered trials. Post-2020, there has been a notable surge in immunotherapy-focused arms, driven by evolving frontline data from trials like KEYNOTE and FIRST, integrating checkpoint inhibitors with standard therapies to overcome resistance. Future directions emphasize combination strategies and biomarker-driven enrollment to improve outcomes in high-risk populations.
