 Passions is an American daytime soap opera created and initially head-written by James E. Reilly that premiered on NBC on July 5, 1999, and concluded its nine-season run on August 7, 2008, after moving to DirecTV's The 101 Network in 2007. Set in the fictional New England town of Harmony, the series centered on the lives of interconnected families, including the powerful Cranes and the working-class Russells, weaving traditional soap opera tropes of romance, betrayal, and class conflict with overt supernatural elements such as witchcraft, demons, curses, and interdimensional travel.
The show's distinctive blend of gothic fantasy and melodramatic storytelling distinguished it from contemporaries like General Hospital or The Young and the Restless, attracting a niche audience intrigued by plotlines involving a 300-year-old witch, a doll possessed by an evil spirit, and apocalyptic prophecies, though it often drew criticism for its outlandish twists and campy execution. Despite consistently low overall Nielsen ratings that led to its cancellation by NBC in January 2007 due to insufficient broad viewership, Passions performed strongly in key younger demographics, particularly among women aged 18-34, which facilitated its brief cable continuation.
Reilly's background in revitalizing Days of Our Lives through sensational story arcs informed Passions' bold narrative risks, earning the series multiple Daytime Emmy nominations for writing and production, as well as accolades like the Imagen Award for Best Daytime Drama in 2004, though it never achieved widespread critical acclaim or sustained network success. Controversies arose from its polarizing supernatural focus, which some viewers and critics deemed incompatible with daytime television's established realism, contributing to mixed reviews and its eventual demise amid shifting industry priorities toward reality programming.

Premise and Setting

Town of Harmony and Key Families

The fictional town of Harmony serves as the primary setting for the television series Passions, depicted as a small community in New England where everyday social tensions unfold among its residents. Launched on July 5, 1999, the series establishes Harmony as a locale blending routine community life with underlying familial power struggles that drive narrative conflicts. At the core of these dynamics is the stark class divide between the elite Crane family, who wield significant economic and political influence over the town, and lower socioeconomic groups such as the working-class Lopez-Fitzgeralds. 
The Cranes maintain their dominance from an imposing mansion perched atop Raven Hill, a location symbolizing their insulated wealth and control, with the estate situated at 14 Raven Hill Road. This architectural emblem of elitism underscores the family's role as the town's de facto rulers, often positioning them in opposition to less privileged households through inherited rivalries and resource disparities. In contrast, the Lopez-Fitzgeralds embody the struggles of immigrant-descended laborers, frequently clashing with the Cranes over employment opportunities and social mobility, reflecting empirical patterns of class antagonism observed in small-town America.
Complementing these polarities, the African American Russell family navigates parallel economic hardships while engaging in community ties that highlight ethnic and class intersections within Harmony. The white Bennett family adds another layer, often mediating or allying variably amid these divides, as the town's social fabric in the 1999 premiere setup prioritizes such relational hierarchies to ground interpersonal dramas in verifiable causal mechanisms of status and kinship. These key families' entrenched positions foster ongoing feuds, providing a realistic scaffold for the series' examination of power imbalances despite occasional departures into less conventional territory.

Creation and Production

Development by James E. Reilly

James E. Reilly, who had served twice as head writer on Days of Our Lives and received a Daytime Emmy Award for Outstanding Drama Series Writing for his work there, was selected by NBC to create a new daytime soap opera following the network's decision to cancel the 35-year-old series Another World. NBC announced Another World's cancellation on April 12, 1999, with its final episode airing on June 25, 1999; Passions was greenlit to occupy the 12:30 p.m. ET slot starting July 5, 1999. 
Reilly conceived Passions as a departure from traditional soap operas by centering it on four core families in the fictional New England town of Harmony, incorporating supernatural elements such as witches, dolls with mystical powers, and paranormal visions to drive the narrative. This structure stemmed from Reilly's recognition that daytime soaps were declining among younger demographics, prompting an intentional fusion of romance, family drama, and fantasy to broaden appeal beyond the genre's aging core audience of older women.
Pre-premiere promotion emphasized the show's "fresh, hip" qualities and targeted marketing toward younger viewers through supernatural hooks, including early teasers of curses and otherworldly interference in interpersonal conflicts. Upon debut, however, the fantastical components sparked debate, with outlets labeling them as bizarre gimmicks potentially undermining the soap's credibility rather than revitalizing interest in the format. Reilly defended the approach as essential for capturing a vivid, innovative vision of Harmony that traditional realism could not achieve.

Filming and Technical Details

Passions was primarily filmed at CBS Studio Center in Studio City, California, following an initial relocation from other facilities to accommodate its production needs. Limited on-location shoots supplemented studio work, including sequences in Oxnard and Burbank, California; Camden, Maine; and Paris, France, with the latter used for early episodes such as the premiere taping on July 5, 1999.
The series adhered to a conventional daytime soap opera taping schedule, producing episodes on weekdays to maintain a five-day-per-week broadcast pace during its NBC run from 1999 to 2007. After transitioning to DirecTV's The 101 Network in September 2007, production shifted to three episodes per week through March 2008, reflecting adjusted operational demands. Final NBC taping occurred on August 15, 2007, with overall production wrapping on March 28, 2008.
Technical execution for supernatural elements relied on practical effects and basic visual enhancements suitable to daytime television constraints, including depictions of witchcraft, zombies, and other fantastical occurrences integrated into studio sets. Budget limitations, estimated at around $765,000 weekly as of 2005, influenced crew efficiency and minimized elaborate post-production, prioritizing live-tape methods with Outpost Farm Productions and NBC as key entities. The DirecTV phase further constrained resources, impacting episode volume but sustaining core studio-based filming until cancellation.

Broadcast History

NBC Run (19992007)

Passions premiered on NBC on July 5, 1999, occupying the 2:00 p.m. Eastern Time slot in the network's daytime lineup following the cancellation of Another World. The series launched amid competition from entrenched daytime dramas on rival networks, such as ABC's General Hospital.
The program encountered periodic preemptions due to network priorities like live sports coverage and breaking news events, which disrupted regular airing in various markets. NBC nonetheless enforced clearance of its time slot across affiliates and refrained from major repositioning, reflecting a strategic tolerance shaped by the show's empirical draw in key viewer segments.
Scheduling stabilized notably from 2003 through 2007, enabling uninterrupted weekday broadcasts that aligned with production cycles prior to NBC's pivot toward expanded morning programming. This period underscored the network's commitment to the format amid broader daytime shifts, without further slot alterations.

DirecTV Continuation (20072008)

Following NBC's cancellation, Passions transitioned to DirecTV's The 101 Network, an original programming channel aimed at subscriber-exclusive content to expand the satellite provider's niche offerings. The deal, announced in April 2007, committed DirecTV to absorbing all production costs for a ninth season of 52 episodes airing Monday through Thursday, totaling nearly $40 million, as a strategy to differentiate its service through targeted, ad-light original series. New episodes premiered on September 17, 2007, providing the series with operational continuity while restricting access to DirecTV subscribers only, which curtailed broader visibility compared to broadcast television.
The arrangement enabled enhanced creative flexibility, including fewer commercials that extended episode runtime to an average of 41 minutes versus 37 on NBC, allowing for denser storytelling without network-imposed content constraints. Principal cast members, including key actors from NBC Universal Television Studio contracts, re-signed to support the shift, ensuring production stability under the satellite platform's model of cost-controlled originals. However, in December 2007, DirecTV opted not to renew beyond the initial commitment, fulfilling the 52 episodes as a finite extension rather than indefinite investment, reflecting a business calculus prioritizing short-term subscriber retention over sustained high-cost production.
The series concluded its DirecTV run with the final episode on August 7, 2008, marking the end of nine seasons amid the platform's experiment in exclusive, niche-targeted programming that ultimately proved insufficient for long-term viability due to its walled-garden distribution limiting audience acquisition. This phase underscored causal trade-offs in media economics: while the deal preserved the show's fantastical elements without broadcast censorship pressures, subscriber-only delivery inherently scaled back promotional reach and potential revenue streams from syndication or wider syndication.

U.S. Viewership Trends

Passions premiered on July 5, 1999, drawing 2.96 million viewers for its debut episode. The series' first season averaged a 1.9 household Nielsen rating, placing it below established competitors such as All My Children, which ranked second overall among soaps that year behind The Young and the Restless. Despite the modest launch, Passions achieved its highest viewership in the 20012003 period, averaging weekly household ratings of 2.1 to 2.3, equivalent to roughly 2.4 million viewers.
Viewership began a steady decline thereafter, with the series averaging a 1.5 household rating (approximately 1.9 million viewers) in its later NBC seasons. By the final NBC episode on September 7, 2007, ratings had fallen to a 1.3 household share, corresponding to 1.68 million viewers. The show's overall Nielsen performance remained consistently low compared to contemporaries; for instance, in the 19992000 season, Passions trailed All My Children by several rating points in household metrics.
Throughout its run, Passions ranked highest among soaps in key younger demographics, particularly females aged 1217, even as total viewership eroded. This trend underscored a niche appeal amid broader commercial underperformance, with household ratings never exceeding the mid-2s and dipping below 2 million viewers annually by 2006.

Factors Contributing to Low Ratings

Passions' viewership declined steadily from its debut, averaging a 1.5 household rating (approximately 1.9 million viewers weekly) by 20062007, far below the thresholds needed for network sustainability in daytime television. This attrition stemmed primarily from content choices that prioritized supernatural gimmickssuch as witches casting curses, zombies rising from graves, and a sentient doll named Timmy engaging in adventuresover the interpersonal relational depth central to the soap opera genre's appeal to its core audience of homemakers and older women. These elements, while drawing initial curiosity, eroded long-term retention by diluting emotional investment in family dynamics and romances with fantastical resolutions that undermined narrative stakes and character consistency.
Compounding this was inconsistent pacing, where episodes featured protracted buildup to supernatural climaxes followed by illogical plot closures, failing to deliver the serialized continuity viewers expected for daily habit formation. High production costs associated with special effects for these sequences further strained budgets without commensurate rating gains, exacerbating financial unviability. Externally, the mid-2000s rise of reality televisiongenres like Survivor (debuting 2000) and The Bachelor (2002)intensified competition by offering cheaper, faster-paced alternatives that captured shifting daytime preferences for unscripted immediacy over scripted fantasy, contributing to broader soap genre erosion.

Content and Style

Supernatural and Fantastical Elements

Passions distinguished itself among daytime serials by incorporating supernatural phenomena as integral causal agents in its narrative structure, enabling plot developments through magical interventions rather than exclusively through human motivations or happenstance. Foremost was Tabitha Lenox, a centuries-old witch residing in Harmony, whose spells and curses directly precipitated crises among the town's elite families, such as hexes targeting the Cranes to sow discord and downfall. Her enchanted doll, Timmy, functioned as an autonomous accomplice, wielding agency in schemes like sabotage or reconnaissance, thereby injecting unpredictability and whimsy into interpersonal rivalries. These elements, operational from the series' July 5, 1999, premiere, allowed for resolutions and escalations unbound by realistic constraints, such as reanimations via dark rituals that revived antagonists as zombies to pursue vendettas.
In contrast to traditional soap operas, which emphasized empirical family dynamics, hidden parentage, and psychological betrayalsexemplified by long-running series like Days of Our Lives maintaining verisimilitude through human-scale conflictsPassions leveraged the supernatural for overt causality, where incantations or artifacts reliably triggered events like possessions or apparitions to propel romantic entanglements and power struggles. Voodoo rituals, invoked by select characters for retribution, exemplified this by materializing tangible threats, such as dolls pinned to inflict pain on targets, bypassing protracted scheming for immediate, visually striking consequences. This approach, while diverging from genre norms, facilitated serialized momentum by attributing anomalies to witchcraft rather than improbable coincidences, fostering a hybrid of melodrama and fantasy.
Fan accounts and retrospective analyses highlight the appeal of these components, particularly Tabitha and Timmy's dynamic, which garnered dedicated followings for their comedic yet pivotal disruptions, contributing to the show's cult status among younger viewers drawn to its eccentricity over rote realism. Enthusiasm for such integrations persisted post-cancellation, with admirers citing the duo's antics as highlights that sustained engagement amid the series' nine-year run ending in 2008.

Major Storylines and Themes

The central narrative of Passions revolved around the entrenched feud between the affluent Crane family, who wielded economic and political control over the fictional town of Harmony, and the working-class Lopez-Fitzgerald family, whose grievances stemmed from perceived historical injustices including wrongful imprisonment and family separations dating back decades. This rivalry, emblematic of class-based ambition and betrayal, propelled early storylines, such as the 1999 premiere's setup where police officer Luis Lopez-Fitzgerald's hatred for the Cranes clashed with his budding romance with Sheridan Crane, Julian Crane's daughter, fostering themes of fated unions thwarted by familial loyalty and deceit. Similarly, maid Theresa Lopez-Fitzgerald's obsessive pursuit of Ethan Crane, initially a one-sided infatuation from her teenage years, evolved into a protracted love triangle with Ethan's fiance Gwen Hotchkiss, highlighting relentless ambition to transcend social barriers through romantic conquest and repeated acts of deception, including forged documents and hidden pregnancies.
As the series progressed into the early 2000s, family secrets intensified the themes of betrayal and inescapable fate, with revelations like the true parentage of characters tying disparate bloodlines in unexpected ways and fueling cycles of vengeance; for instance, the 2002 birth of Theresa's son Little Ethan amid custody battles underscored how personal ambitions often led to profound losses. By mid-decade, plots escalated from interpersonal realism to broader causal disruptions, including a 2005 tsunami that demolished Harmony and symbolized fate's capricious intervention in human schemes, displacing families and exposing buried resentments.
Later arcs culminated in extreme twists reinforcing causal realism in plot pivots, such as the 2007 incest revelation involving Chad Harris-Crane's affair, which shattered alliances and amplified betrayal's long-term consequences within the Crane lineage, and the 2008 murder of patriarch Alistair Crane, resolving power struggles through violent ambition's backlash. These developments, while building on initial feuds and romances, portrayed fate not as predestination but as the aggregate outcome of individual choices marked by greed and infidelity, with the series finale on August 7, 2008, tying loose ends through mass weddings that affirmed enduring romantic bonds amid pervasive discord.

Reception

Critical Assessments

Critical reception to Passions was mixed, with early reviews decrying its execution flaws and plot absurdities while later retrospectives occasionally praised its campy innovation in blending supernatural elements with soap tropes. Entertainment Weekly's 1999 critique labeled the series "astonishingly cretinous," assigning it an F+ grade for lacking suspenseful pacing, abrupt scene transitions, and overreliance on gimmicks like a doll character speaking in third person and a witch's exaggerated declarations such as "Their souls will be mine!" The review highlighted plot absurdities, including exploiting Princess Diana's death via a character's Paris tunnel crash and rote amnesia twists, arguing these undermined dramatic coherence.
The show's debut ratings of 2.1 reflected viewer disinterest, which critics like those at the Sun-Sentinel attributed to unarousing content failing to sustain engagement despite supernatural hooks. Overacting was a frequent target, with BuzzFeed's 2013 analysis noting the series was "over-acted" with lines like "I love your father and he loves me! I will never divorce him! Never!" amid soft lighting, rendering it comically tragic but structurally incoherent. Vice's 2017 retrospective acknowledged pacing slowness"so little happens in each episode"as evidence of flawed execution, even as it cataloged excesses like zombies, tsunamis, and magic as emblematic of unchecked gimmickry.
Some 2000s commentary offered tempered praise for innovation, with TV Guide's Michael Logan in 2001 describing Passions as generating "buzz" akin to General Hospital's Luke and Laura era due to its hip, hellish departures from soap norms. The New York Times echoed this by likening it to "David Lynch" surrealism or "the Twin Peaks of daytime," crediting its bold weirdness for standing out, though persistent low Nielsen ratingsoften below 3.0signaled that such elements prioritized novelty over coherent drama, contributing to its 2008 cancellation.

Audience and Fan Responses

Despite consistently low overall Nielsen ratings that contributed to its 2008 cancellation, Passions cultivated a niche cult following among viewers drawn to its blend of soap opera tropes and supernatural escapism, particularly younger demographics seeking over-the-top entertainment. Early in its run, the series ranked as the most-watched daytime soap among 12- to 17-year-olds, appealing to those who valued its fantastical elements over conventional realism. This polarization manifested in fan preferences: escapism enthusiasts praised its unhinged plots and character quirks, such as the doll Timmy, for providing pure diversion, while traditional soap audiences often dismissed it as too bizarre for sustained engagement.
Post-cancellation, fans voiced strong emotional attachment through letters and online expressions of outrage and sadness, though organized campaigns to revive the show proved unsuccessful amid its relocation to DirecTV and eventual end. Dedicated online communities, including Facebook groups and Reddit threads, have sustained nostalgia, where enthusiasts revisit episodes and celebrate memorable arcs like witch Tabitha's antics, preserving the series' legacy outside mainstream viewership. In 2024, the 25th anniversary of its July 5, 1999 premiere sparked renewed fan buzz via social media posts and retrospective articles highlighting its enduring appeal as a "cult classic" for those who embraced its eccentricity.

Controversies

Outlandish Plot Criticisms

Soap opera traditionalists faulted Passions for incorporating outlandish supernatural elements that eroded narrative logic, such as witches summoning demons, possessed teenagers, and living dolls intervening in human affairs, which frequently bypassed realistic cause-and-effect chains in favor of arbitrary magical interventions. These devices, while central to the series from its 1999 premiere, were seen by genre purists as incompatible with soap opera conventions emphasizing interpersonal drama and consequence-driven plotting, leading many to dismiss the show outright as unserious.
A prime example involved the protracted feud between Charity Standish, positioned as an agent of benevolent supernatural forces, and Tabitha Lenox, a centuries-old witch serving dark powers; this arc, intended to underscore a cosmic battle of good versus evil, stalled without resolution when Charity exited Harmony abruptly, abandoning the conflict's stakes and highlighting inconsistencies in the show's supernatural hierarchy. Such unresolved threads contributed to broader critiques of plot stagnation, where fantastical escalationszombies, time portals, and prophetic visionsdiluted dramatic tension by rendering mortal perils inconsequential, as magical resets negated prior causal developments. Viewer analyses documented over a dozen major plot holes tied to these elements, including inconsistent rules for witchcraft and demonic possessions that contradicted earlier episodes.
The series' reliance on such mechanics paralleled a measurable viewer exodus, with Nielsen household ratings declining from an initial average of around 2.5 in 1999 to 1.5 (roughly 1.9 million viewers) by 2007, prompting NBC's cancellation announcement on January 15, 2007. The subsequent DirecTV-run finale on August 7, 2008, compressed resolutions for dozens of lingering arcs into a single episode, from eternal curses to familial secrets, resulting in abrupt denouements that critics and audiences decried as logically strained and thematically incoherent, further underscoring the narrative breakdowns inherent in the show's fantastical framework.

Boundary-Pushing Storylines

One of the most contentious arcs in Passions involved Vincent Clarkson, revealed in 2007 as the secret son of Julian Crane and Eve Russell, who was intersex and engaged in multiple incestuous relationships. Vincent, portrayed as a bisexual serial killer, raped his mother Eve and maintained a sexual relationship with his half-brother Chad Harris-Crane, unbeknownst to both that they were related as half-uncles and nephews through Alistair Crane. This storyline extended to Vincent's impregnation by his father Julian in 2008, marking a daytime television first for a male-presenting character carrying a pregnancy resulting from father-son incest.
The pregnancy revelation intertwined with supernatural elements, including zombie resurrections, as Vincent's schemes involved manipulating the undead and other fantastical threats in Harmony, culminating in erratic behavior like harassment during medical crises. Critics and viewers condemned the arc for its grotesque handling of intersex and queer identities, portraying Vincent's bisexuality as tied to villainy, rape, and familial violation without nuance or positive representation. The down-low affair between Vincent and Chad drew specific backlash from advocacy groups, who argued it reinforced harmful stereotypes of closeted homosexuality leading to violence, referencing the prior murder of lesbian character Rae Thomas in Simone Russell's coming-out storyline.
Earlier queer plots amplified the show's boundary-pushing approach, such as Simone Russell's 2005 lesbian relationship with Rae, which ended in Rae's strangulationlater tied to Vincent's crimesand was faulted for sensationalizing same-sex attraction amid the series' youth-oriented supernatural marketing. These elements, including Vincent's outlandish assaults and the 2008 pregnancy, correlated with declining viewership, as the insensitivity alienated audiences without boosting ratings, contributing to executive concerns over advertiser pullouts and network tolerance.

The series spawned no video games, spin-offs, or other official media extensions beyond print adaptations, underscoring restrained ancillary market development. Fan-driven items, such as custom T-shirts, dominate online marketplaces, but lack endorsement from the production entities. This pattern aligns with Passions' niche appeal, prioritizing on-air boundary-pushing over robust merchandising ecosystems seen in prime-time franchises.

Cancellation and Legacy

End of the Series

The series aired its final episode on August 7, 2008, after DirecTV opted not to renew its contract, a decision announced on December 10, 2007, despite an initial eight-week production extension. Production wrapped on March 28, 2008, with the studio unable to secure another broadcaster amid declining viewership metrics documented in prior ratings analyses. Over its nine-year run, Passions produced 2,231 episodes, concluding with a resolution to key arcs including the Theresa-Ethan love triangle and supernatural elements in Harmony.
Cast members reacted to the abrupt termination with a mix of emotion and frustration over unresolved or hastily concluded storylines. Actress McKenzie Westmore, who portrayed Sheridan Crane, later described the finale as "weirdly" unsatisfying, particularly for the separation of the central Luis-Sheridan pairing despite their super-couple status. On-set farewells were tearful, with tributes to departed actors like Josh Ryan Evans (Timmy Lenox), but the sudden shutdown left several narrative threads, such as lingering family vendettas, without full closure.

Cultural Impact and Retrospective Views

Passions achieved a niche cult following among viewers appreciative of its campy, over-the-top supernatural elements, with fans continuing to share episodes and clips online more than a decade after its 2008 conclusion. Retrospective analyses in the 2010s highlighted its "unhinged" appeal, portraying the series as a bizarre fusion of soap opera tropes with fantasy horror, including witches, zombies, and possessed dolls, which contrasted sharply with the genre's dominant focus on interpersonal family dramas. However, this eccentricity did not translate to broader cultural permeation or emulation, as evidenced by the absence of subsequent daytime soaps adopting similar heavy supernatural integration; the genre's remaining entries, such as Days of Our Lives, adhered to realistic narratives centered on romance and lineage conflicts.
Critics and commentators in the 2010s and 2020s have noted Passions' failure to catalyze a lasting shift toward fantasy-driven storytelling in soaps, akin to earlier experiments like Dark Shadows (19661971) and Port Charles (19972003), which also incorporated paranormal plots but ultimately folded without altering the format's core emphasis on mundane relational realism. This persistence of family-oriented, empirically grounded drama over speculative elements underscores a viewer preference for causal narratives rooted in human behavior rather than contrived otherworldly interventions, with no major network reviving supernatural soaps post-Passions. While praised for innovation in outlets like Vice for its "weirdest" plots involving tsunamis and magic amid class and romantic strife, the series' legacy remains confined to ironic appreciation rather than transformative influence, reflecting the daytime genre's resistance to sustained genre-bending amid declining viewership.

Reboot Discussions Post-2008

Following news in late 2024 of potential revival plans for All My Children as a Lifetime holiday movie, several Passions alumni voiced support for rebooting their series in a similar limited format, such as a feature film. SoapHub consulted cast members including Mary-Kelly, who described Passions as "well ahead of its time" and argued its blend of supernatural elements and family drama could resonate today, potentially drawing new viewers through streaming platforms. Another alum emphasized cast eagerness, stating, "I think a reboot movie would not only be a great idea, I would love to participate and I think the cast would be eager as well," while noting the show's prescient mix of genres predated modern hits like supernatural procedurals.
In January 2024, actress Lindsay Hartley, who played Cara Fitzgerald, speculated that Passions arrived "a hair too early" for mainstream acceptance of its paranormal themes but predicted it "would explode" in a revival, citing evolved audience tastes for fantasy in television. This sentiment echoed broader cast reflections, with no opposition reported among interviewed alumni to exploring a movie or miniseries continuation.
Speculation intensified in August 2025 when James Hyde (Fancy's father, Vincent), Juliet Mills (Tabitha), and other co-stars reunited for a video teaser posted on social media, captioned to "stir up some trouble" and hinting at "great things coming." Fans interpreted this as possible groundwork for a project, drawing parallels to successful soap revivals like the short-lived Prospect Park continuations of All My Children and One Life to Live in 2013, though those faced production and rights hurdles.
Despite enthusiasm, no formal announcements or developments emerged from NBCUniversal, the rights holder, as of October 2025, mirroring stalled revivals of other soaps where intellectual property complexities and fragmented legacy audiencesnow predominantly over 50pose commercial risks. Cast sentiments remain positive but unheeded by studios, with barriers including the need for substantial investment amid declining linear TV viewership for daytime dramas.
