 Card-Pitt was the temporary merger of the Chicago Cardinals and Pittsburgh Steelers, two National Football League (NFL) franchises, for the 1944 season amid severe player shortages caused by World War II enlistments. The team, co-owned by Art Rooney of the Steelers and Charles Bidwill of the Cardinals, was co-coached by Walt Kiesling and Phil Handler, and it competed in the NFL's Western Division with home games split between Pittsburgh's Forbes Field and Chicago's Comiskey Park. Compiling a winless 010 record, Card-Pitt was outscored 328108 overall, threw a league-record 41 interceptions, and finished last in the division, marking one of the worst performances in NFL history at the time.
The merger was necessitated by the NFL's expansion to 11 teams in 1944, following the addition of the Boston Yanks, which left both the Cardinals and Steelers unable to field full rosters due to wartime military drafts. Approved by NFL Commissioner Elmer Layden on April 22, 1944, the union followed a similar 1943 merger between the Steelers and Philadelphia Eagles known as Phil-Pitt, reflecting the league's broader efforts to survive the war. Training camp was held in Waukesha, Wisconsin, where the combined roster of about 40 players adopted the single-wing and T formations offensively, though quarterback injuries forced a mid-season shift to the Notre Dame Box.
Despite the talent pool, including running back John Grigas (who rushed for 610 yards before quitting mid-season) and lineman Chet Bulger, internal conflicts plagued the team, such as a player strike over fines and a brawl during a 4220 loss to Washington on October 29. The season's closest contest was a 3028 defeat to the Cleveland Rams in the opener, but subsequent blowoutsincluding a 497 rout by the Chicago Bearshighlighted the squad's disarray, with an average margin of defeat exceeding 20 points per game. Although the team posted a 1716 exhibition win over the New York Giants, its regular-season futility earned it the derisive nickname "Car-Pitts" from fans and media.
Following the season's end on December 3, 1944, with a 497 loss to the Chicago Bears, the franchises immediately separated, resuming independent operations in 1945 as the war wound down and player availability improved. Several Card-Pitt participants, such as Bulger (who later played in the Cardinals' 1947 NFL championship) and Vince Banonis (a future Hall of Fame contributor), went on to notable careers, underscoring the merger's role in preserving talent during a precarious era for professional football. The episode remains a unique footnote in NFL history, illustrating the league's adaptability amid national crisis.

Background

World War II and NFL Challenges

The United States' entry into World War II following the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor on December 7, 1941, profoundly disrupted professional sports, including the National Football League (NFL). As the nation mobilized for total war, thousands of young men, including many athletes, enlisted or were drafted into military service, leading to widespread player shortages across the league. By 1943, approximately 638 NFL players were serving in the armed forces, contributing to a broader tally of more than 1,000 league personnel who ultimately served during the conflict. This exodus not only depleted talent but also strained team operations, as gas rationing, travel restrictions, and shifting public priorities caused attendance to dwindle significantly, with average crowds dropping amid economic pressures and the national focus on the war effort.
In response to these challenges, the NFL implemented adaptive measures to sustain play. Rosters were reduced from the pre-war standard of around 33 players to as few as 28 in 1943 and 1944, forcing teams to rely on a mix of veterans, rookies, and even retired players who returned to fill gaps. The league also relaxed substitution rules temporarily, allowing unlimited substitutions during dead balls to accommodate smaller squads and prevent further injuries. Additionally, to bolster manpower, teams signed replacement players from military bases and service academies, while the NFL organized exhibition games against all-star military units to maintain visibility and support war bond sales, which generated millions in contributions by 1942. These strategies reflected the league's determination to continue operations as a morale booster, as emphasized by Commissioner Elmer Layden, who viewed football as essential recreation amid wartime stress.
The escalating instability peaked in 1943, as the ongoing warmarked by major events like the Allied invasion of Normandy on D-Day in June 1944correlated with acute NFL vulnerabilities. Teams such as the Chicago Cardinals and Pittsburgh Steelers encountered these same pressures, highlighting the widespread threat to the NFL's viability.

Formation

Merger Negotiations

Following the dissolution of the Pittsburgh Steelers' temporary merger with the Philadelphia Eagles at the end of the 1943 season, discussions for a new partnership began in the spring of 1944 amid ongoing World War II challenges that had depleted rosters and strained finances across the league. Steelers owner Art Rooney and Chicago Cardinals owner Charles W. Bidwill initiated talks, driven by the need to consolidate resources and ensure both franchises could field competitive teams without folding. NFL Commissioner Elmer Layden played a central role in facilitating these negotiations, emphasizing the merger's necessity to address scheduling imbalances after the Cleveland Rams and Boston Yanks rejoined the league, resulting in an odd number of 11 teams.
At the NFL's annual owners' meetings in April 1944, Rooney explored potential mergers with teams like the Cleveland Rams, Brooklyn Tigers, or Boston Yanks, but these proposals were rejected due to logistical and competitive concerns. Bidwill and Rooney then proposed combining the Steelers and Cardinals, which received league approval on April 21, 1944, for a one-year arrangement. Key deliberations focused on logistical details, including an agreement to alternate home games between Pittsburgh's Forbes Field and Chicago's Comiskey Park, with Rooney insisting on at least half the home schedule in Pittsburgh to protect local interests; the final split allocated three games in Pittsburgh and two in Chicago. The merger also involved sharing draft picks and rosters from both teams' 1943 selections, aiming to pool limited talent amid military enlistments.
Strategically, the negotiations underscored the broader effort to sustain the NFL's viability during wartime, as both franchises faced severe player shortages and financial pressures that threatened their survival. By merging, Bidwill and Rooney sought to combine scouting efforts and talent from recent drafts, preserving two franchises for postwar resumption while avoiding the precedent of outright dissolution. Challenges arose from other owners' hesitations over setting a merger precedent that could destabilize the league structure, though Layden's advocacy helped secure consensus. Internal disagreements on profit distribution were resolved with a 50-50 split, ensuring equitable revenue sharing from ticket sales and broadcasts despite the geographic divide.

Aftermath

Immediate Dissolution

Following the conclusion of the 1944 NFL season on December 3, with a 49-7 defeat to the Chicago Bears at Forbes Field, the temporary Card-Pitt merger dissolved effective immediately, as it had been structured as a one-year wartime measure to address player shortages. On December 18, 1944, Pittsburgh Steelers owner Art Rooney and Chicago Cardinals general manager Arch Wolfe formally announced the separation, allowing Rooney and Cardinals owner Charles Bidwill to reclaim full operational control of their respective franchises.
The dissolution was automatic per the original one-year agreement. Player rosters were divided by returning athletes to their original teams, with shared equipment and resources returned to each organization; any outstanding financial obligations from the joint operation were settled to clear debts and enable independent preparations for the 1945 season.
Players expressed widespread relief at the split, citing the grueling travel and poor on-field resultsthe team's 0-10 recordas key factors in the merger's failure. Media coverage labeled it a "failed experiment," emphasizing the logistical strains and lack of cohesion between the distant franchises, though both the Steelers and Cardinals proceeded to field separate teams in 1945.

Long-Term Legacy

The Card-Pitt merger stands as one of two temporary team consolidations in the modern NFL era, the other being the Phil-Pitt merger between the Steelers and Philadelphia Eagles in 1943, serving as a unique wartime expedient that underscored the league's vulnerability to external crises but also its commitment to continuity. Formed amid severe player shortages due to World War II enlistments, the arrangement helped preserve franchise viability in an 11-team league, preventing potential foldings that could have destabilized operations. Postwar, as military service obligations eased and the NFL expanded with new markets like the Los Angeles Rams in 1946, no similar mergers occurred, reflecting improved financial and roster stability that propelled the league toward its postwar boom.
For the involved franchises, Card-Pitt marked a low point that indirectly shaped their trajectories. The Pittsburgh Steelers, under owner Art Rooney, endured the merger's failures but rebounded after the war through strategic hires like coach Jock Sutherland in 1946, laying groundwork for eventual dominance with multiple Super Bowl titles in the 1970s. The Chicago Cardinals, already grappling with financial woes and poor attendance in the 1940sexacerbated by the merger's 0-10 debaclefaced ongoing instability that contributed to their relocation to St. Louis in 1960, where they sought better market support amid persistent revenue challenges.
Several Card-Pitt players parlayed their experiences into longer, impactful careers, exemplifying resilience amid adversity. Lineman Chet Bulger, who anchored the offensive line in 1944, later became a key contributor to the Cardinals' 1947 NFL championship team, earning All-Pro honors. Similarly, end Vince Banonis transitioned to a standout role with the Detroit Lions in the 1950s, helping secure three NFL titles (one with the Cardinals in 1947 and two with the Lions in 1952 and 1953) and later induction into the College Football Hall of Fame. These trajectories highlight how the merger, despite its futility, served as a proving ground for enduring talents.
The "Card-Pitt" or "Car-Pitt" nickname has persisted in NFL lore as a symbol of wartime grit and comedic futility, frequently invoked in historical accounts to illustrate the league's adaptability. Derided by sportswriters as the "Carpets" for their porous defense, the team endures in trivia and retrospectives, such as those tied to Super Bowl matchups between the Cardinals and Steelers, evoking themes of perseverance through shared hardship.
In contemporary discussions, Card-Pitt is often referenced as a historical parallel during crises, including the COVID-19 pandemic, where league officials navigated player absences and logistical hurdles without resorting to mergers, crediting postwar reforms for enhanced resilience. While no revival proposals have emerged, the episode informs analyses of NFL expansions and contingency planning, reinforcing its status as a pivotal anomaly in league evolution.
