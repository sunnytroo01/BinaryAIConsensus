The Else
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Else is the twelfth studio album by the American alternative rock band They Might Be Giants, consisting of principal members John Flansburgh and John Linnell, released digitally on May 15, 2007, and on CD on July 10, 2007, by the independent label Idlewild Recordings. The album marks a return to the band's adult-oriented rock material following a series of children's albums, featuring 13 original tracks characterized by their signature quirky lyrics, eclectic instrumentation, and pop sensibilities.
Produced collaboratively by They Might Be Giants, the Dust Brothers (known for work with Beck and the Beastie Boys), and engineer Pat Dillett, The Else was recorded at various studios in New York and Los Angeles. The album's artwork was created by Canadian artist Marcel Dzama, whose distinctive drawings of costumed figures and surreal scenes adorn the cover and liner notes, contributing to its whimsical yet slightly eerie aesthetic. Notable tracks include the lead single "I'm Impressed," a tense power-pop song about persuasion and doubt, and "The Mesopotamians," a humorous ode to ancient history imagined as a modern road trip among historical figures.
Upon release, The Else debuted at number 118 on the Billboard 200 chart and received generally positive reviews for recapturing the band's early energy while exploring mature themes like politics and personal anxiety, though some critics noted inconsistencies in production style. It has since been reissued on vinyl in 2023, maintaining a cult following among fans for its blend of clever wordplay and melodic hooks.

Background

Development

The Else marked They Might Be Giants' return to adult-oriented material as their twelfth studio album and the first such project following the children's album Here Come the ABCs in 2005, with the band anticipating a sequel, Here Come the 123s, in 2008. After a period focused on educational recordings, the duo of John Flansburgh and John Linnell decided to pivot back to their alternative rock foundations, seeking a creative rejuvenation that allowed exploration of more complex, mature content. This shift emphasized reconnecting with longtime adult listeners through songs addressing personal emotions and political commentary, such as dictatorship and heartache, rather than the whimsical simplicity of kids' tunes. Initial concepts for the record began to take shape in 2005 and 2006, as Flansburgh and Linnell drew on their established collaborative dynamic to infuse the project with renewed energy and thematic depth.

Production team

The primary producers for The Else were the Dust Brothers, the production duo of John King and Michael Simpson, who brought their expertise in sample-heavy, innovative sound design to the project. Renowned for their groundbreaking work on Beastie Boys' Paul's Boutique (1989) and Beck's Odelay (1996), the Dust Brothers contributed by providing rigorous scrutiny to the tracks, slowing the band's typically rapid workflow to refine grooves and enhance rhythmic tightness, resulting in a more polished overall sound.
Serving as co-producer and engineer, Pat Dillett handled mixing duties across multiple tracks and provided additional production support, drawing on his experience with artists like David Byrne to challenge and elevate the band's arrangements. Dillett, who had previously collaborated with They Might Be Giants on albums such as Factory Showroom (1996), played a key role in integrating electronic elements while maintaining sonic clarity.
Band members John Flansburgh and John Linnell exerted significant production input, overseeing the creative direction and ensuring the album preserved the group's signature quirkiness amid the external influences. Flansburgh, for instance, layered in extensive guitar parts, such as the 19 overdubs on "I'm Impressed," to expand the sonic palette. This team assembly was deliberately chosen to fuse They Might Be Giants' experimental, offbeat style with a sleek, electronic-infused rock production that set a new benchmark for the band's output.

Recording and songwriting

Studio sessions

The recording sessions for The Else took place from late 2006 to early 2007, spanning a period that allowed the band to develop material amid their ongoing touring and other projects.
Primary recording occurred at Kampo Studios and Avatar Studios in Manhattan, with additional sessions at Loho Studio (also in Manhattan), Collier Bros. Studio in Brooklyn, Soy Bomb Studio in Sullivan County, New York, and The Boat in Los Angeles.
The technical approach emphasized multi-tracking to capture live band energy, as seen in tracks like "I'm Impressed" which featured up to 19 guitar layers, while incorporating electronic elements through the contributions of co-producers the Dust Brothers.
Challenges arose from coordinating sessions across multiple East Coast and West Coast locations, requiring efforts to maintain a cohesive sound, compounded by the Dust Brothers' slower, more scrutinizing production style that contrasted with the band's typically rapid workflow.

Collaborative process

The songwriting for The Else was primarily handled by John Flansburgh and John Linnell, the band's core duo, who alternated as lead writers on individual tracks while frequently exchanging ideas to refine lyrics and melodies. This collaborative dynamic between the two Johns emphasized an iterative approach, where initial concepts or musical fragments were developed into full songs through back-and-forth revisions, often starting with a lyrical hook or rhythmic idea before completing the structure.
External influences played a notable role in specific tracks, such as "Withered Hope," which featured co-writing contributions from the Dust Brothers (John King and Michael Simpson), who helped shape its arrangement alongside original ideas from Flansburgh and Linnell. The Dust Brothers also provided drum loops that inspired several songs, including "Withered Hope," "Upside Down Frown," and a post-recording treatment for "Take Out the Trash." The process often involved creating rough demos or pre-existing recordings that were then layered and refined, incorporating challenges like the one faced by Linnell on WNYC's The Next Big Thing radio program, where he was prompted by lexicographer Erin McKean to compose around obscure dictionary words including "contrecoup," "craniosophic," and "limerent," resulting in the song "Contrecoup."
Band members provided essential input on arrangements, with drummer Marty Beller, guitarist Dan Miller, and bassist Danny Weinkauf contributing to the evolution of tracks from simple acoustic sketches into fuller band productions. Their involvement helped transition early ideasoften captured in home demosinto cohesive ensemble pieces during sessions split between New York and Los Angeles. This group dynamic fostered a sense of shared ownership, allowing rhythmic and harmonic elements to be tested and adjusted collectively before final integration with producer Pat Dillett and the Dust Brothers.

Musical style and composition

Genre and instrumentation

The Else is primarily classified as an indie rock and power pop album, incorporating elements of alternative rock and geek rock with experimental production flourishes. Critics noted its return to the band's adult-oriented sound after a series of children's albums, blending taut post-punk influences reminiscent of early new wave acts like Devo and the dB's. The album's style emphasizes catchy, hook-driven melodies with a mix of upbeat anthems and introspective ballads, diverging from the more whimsical, accordion-heavy aesthetic of prior works like Flood.
Instrumentation on The Else features a core rock setup of guitars, bass, and drums, augmented by brass sections and occasional guitar solos, but notably omits the accordion that had been a signature element in They Might Be Giants' sound. The Dust Brothers' involvement introduced electronic beats and subtle synth textures, contributing to a polished, full-band production with nervous, stiff rhythms and fuzz-bass backbeats on tracks like "Take Out the Trash." This collaboration with producers Michael Simpson and John King, alongside longtime engineer Pat Dillett, resulted in a denser, more layered sonic palette compared to the band's earlier lo-fi experiments.
Spanning 13 tracks with a total runtime of 38:12, the album balances energetic power pop numbers such as "I'm Impressed"a Linnell-led anthem with driving percussionand slower, ballad-like pieces like "Withered Hope," which showcases Flansburgh's melodic introspection. These structural choices reflect influences from the band's 1990s output, merging concise song structures with modern electronic enhancements for a cohesive yet varied listening experience.

Lyrical themes

The lyrical themes of The Else prominently feature political satire, drawing on the socio-political climate of the mid-2000s, particularly the Iraq War. In "I'm Impressed," John Linnell incorporates references to military recruitment and societal pressure, reflecting the "horrible" atmosphere of the post-invasion era and what he described as a "shitty government." Similarly, "The Shadow Government" employs satirical commentary on paranoia and hidden power structures, inspired by Vice President Dick Cheney's secretive wartime rhetoric, blending absurdity with critique of governmental opacity.
Personal relationships emerge as another key theme, often filtered through the band's characteristic wit and emotional nuance. "Take Out the Trash," penned by John Flansburgh, delivers biting lyrics on domestic strife and jealousy, portraying a plea to end a troubled romance with raw, confrontational energy. In contrast, Linnell's "Contrecoup" reimagines romantic longing through the lens of traumatic brain injury terminology, using medical jargon to explore vulnerability and disconnection in a poignant yet humorous manner.
The album's tone fuses poignancy, humor, and social critique, exemplified by Flansburgh's energetic, shout-like delivery in tracks like "The Shadow Government" and Linnell's more introspective style in "I'm Impressed," creating a dynamic interplay that underscores the material's emotional depth. Unique elements include historical allusions, such as the upbeat "The Mesopotamians," which nods to ancient Iraq amid contemporary war commentary, and intricate wordplay, as in "Contrecoup"'s deployment of obscure terms like "contrecoup" to layer meaning. These short, punchy songsmost under three minutescohere into a unified arc, balancing levity with underlying tension to evoke a sense of wry resilience.

Release and promotion

Formats and distribution

The Else was initially released digitally on May 15, 2007, through the iTunes Store, allowing fans early access to the full album prior to its physical rollout.
The standard physical edition followed on July 10, 2007, as a compact disc issued by Idlewild Recordings and distributed by Zo Records; initial pressings included a bonus disc titled Cast Your Pod to the Wind, featuring 23 tracks of rare and podcast-exclusive material. An Australian CD edition was released on July 7, 2007.
A vinyl edition, in a gatefold sleeve, was made available on September 11, 2007, also through Idlewild Recordings, catering to collectors seeking the album in analog format. The album was reissued on vinyl in 2023 by Idlewild Recordings.
The album's artwork, created by artist Marcel Dzama, featured surreal imagery such as costumed figures in wooded settings, aligning with the record's whimsical and eccentric themes.

Marketing efforts

The band produced official music videos for four tracks from The Else to promote the album, blending animated and live-action aesthetics. The video for "I'm Impressed," directed by Rob Shaw with animation by Bent Image Lab, features the band performing in a stylized, futuristic Roman circus setting with live-action elements overlaid by digital effects. "With the Dark" employs stop-motion animation by Hine Mizushima, depicting a giant squid interacting with the Johns in a surreal underwater narrative. For "The Shadow Government," the Los Angeles-based Colourmovie created an animated sequence illustrating paranoid conspiracy themes through whimsical, hand-drawn visuals. "The Mesopotamians," directed by David Cowles, uses colorful animated characters representing historical figures in a playful, Monkees-inspired adventure.
Idlewild Recordings, the band's independent label, handled distribution with a strategic digital-first approach, offering The Else exclusively via iTunes download on May 15, 2007, two months before the physical CD release on July 10. This push emphasized direct-to-fan accessibility, aligning with the label's focus on the band's core audience through online platforms and limited physical editions.
Live promotion centered on a North American tour in summer 2007, followed by additional dates into 2008, where setlists prominently featured The Else tracks like "I'm Impressed" and "The Shadow Government" alongside classics to showcase the album's material. Fan engagement was enhanced via the band's official podcast, which included episodes announcing tour dates and sharing live recordings from shows.
The band's ongoing podcast series incorporated behind-the-scenes content for The Else, such as audio previews, production anecdotes, and rarities tied to the album's creation, fostering deeper listener interaction during the promotional cycle.

Commercial performance

Chart achievements

The Else achieved its highest charting position at number 9 on the Billboard Top Digital Albums chart in 2007, marking a strong digital debut for They Might Be Giants' independent release. This placement reflected the album's early availability on iTunes, which preceded the physical CD release by nearly two months and contributed to boosted initial streams and downloads among the band's dedicated fanbase.
It peaked at number 118 on the Billboard 200, underscoring its niche appeal within alternative and indie circles despite critical praise. It garnered minor placements on specialized charts, including a brief appearance on the CMJ Radio 200 and rankings in independent and alternative airplay metrics, where tracks like "The Mesopotamians" received moderate college radio rotation. Overall, these chart performances highlighted the album's success in digital and underground markets rather than broad commercial dominance.

Sales figures

The Else achieved modest initial sales upon its July 2007 release, primarily appealing to They Might Be Giants' dedicated cult fanbase rather than broader audiences. It debuted at number 118 on the Billboard 200 chart and spent only two weeks there, reflecting limited commercial traction in its early months.
No RIAA certification has been issued to date, underscoring its niche performance absent mainstream breakthroughs.
In the long term, The Else maintained steady digital sales following its iTunes debut in May 2007, bolstered by ongoing streaming availability. A vinyl resurgence emerged in the 2010s, highlighted by a 2023 reissue on colored 180-gram pressing, which catered to collectors and renewed interest among fans.
These patterns were influenced by its release on the independent Idlewild label, which constrained widespread promotional efforts, though integrations with the band's podcastsuch as tracks from sessions compiled in the 2006 podcast collection Cast Your Pod to the Windsupported gradual growth within niche communities.

Critical reception

Contemporary reviews

Upon its release in 2007, The Else garnered generally favorable reviews from music critics, who appreciated the album's return to the band's rock roots following their forays into children's music. The album holds a Metacritic score of 73 out of 100, based on 17 reviews, reflecting a consensus of positive reception with some mixed sentiments.
Many reviewers highlighted the album's cohesive sound and infectious hooks as standout elements. Pitchfork awarded it a 6.8 out of 10, praising its tauter, power-pop-infused tracks that evoked the band's early days, such as the nervous energy in "I'm Impressed" and the clever wordplay in "The Shadow Government." AllMusic gave it 4 out of 5 stars, commending its quirky pop songs and welcome return to adult-oriented material. This return to form was a recurring theme, with critics noting how co-producers the Dust Brothers helped deliver a polished yet energetic sound reminiscent of They Might Be Giants' '90s peak.
Criticisms centered on the band's occasional over-reliance on whimsical gimmicks and the album's relative brevity, clocking in at under 40 minutes. Pitchfork faulted the uneven balance caused by excessive musical humor, citing tracks like "Take Out the Trash" for veering into unintended novelty territory. Treble Zine delivered a mixed assessment, arguing that the processed production overshadowed the duo's natural charm and made some early songs feel underdeveloped, contributing to a sense of shortfall compared to past highs like Flood.
Among the more enthusiastic responses, IGN rated the album 8.5 out of 10, describing They Might Be Giants as "quirky as ever" while lauding its inventive energy and replay value. PopMatters, scoring it 7 out of 10, praised the emotional depth in its fine songwriting and inventive arrangements, particularly in how tracks like "Climbing the Walls" blended poignancy with pop accessibility.

Later evaluations

In the years following its release, The Else has garnered growing appreciation among fans and critics for its role as a transitional work in They Might Be Giants' catalog, bridging the band's earlier adult-oriented rock efforts like The Spine (2004) with their burgeoning children's music phase, including Here Come the ABCs (2005) and Here Come the 123s (2008). Band members John Linnell and John Flansburgh reflected on this in a 2015 retrospective, with Linnell praising the Dust Brothers' production for its meticulous grooves and expressing personal concern over replicating the album's quality in subsequent releases, while Flansburgh highlighted its paranoid thematic undertones amid the Iraq War era.
Fan reception has solidified The Else as an underrated gem, often hailed in online discussions for its strong hooks, inventive songwriting, and cohesive rock sound that stands apart from the band's more whimsical output. On aggregate review platforms, it holds a user score of 78 out of 100 based on over 120 ratings as of 2025, with reviewers frequently citing it as a personal favorite for tracks like "I'm Impressed" and its overall replay value. This contrasts with its contemporary Metacritic score of 73 out of 100, underscoring a hindsight appreciation for its experimental edge.
The album's legacy lies in paving the way for They Might Be Giants' more experimental adult works in the 2010s, such as Join Us (2011), by blending polished production with thematic depth on political unease and absurdity, influences that echoed in later songcraft. After a vinyl reissue in 2023, The Else maintains steady streaming presence on platforms like Spotify and Bandcamp, where it has been digitally available since at least 2020, contributing to its enduring accessibility.

Bonus disc contents

The bonus disc, titled Cast Your Pod to the Wind, was included exclusively with the first pressing of the CD edition of The Else, released on July 10, 2007, by Idlewild/Zo Records. It compiles 23 tracks sourced largely from the They Might Be Giants podcast, offering high-fidelity remasters of fan-favorite segments alongside new rarities, live recordings, and previously unreleased studio material. This collection highlights the band's experimental side, featuring short vignettes, humorous sketches, and alternate takes that contrast the polished production of the main album. It was later released as a standalone digital album in July 2009.
The disc's purpose is to extend the album experience by providing deeper access to the group's creative output from their podcast series, which often showcased informal, unrefined compositions and improvisations predating full production. Examples include the unreleased track "Brain Problem Situation," a studio recording exploring quirky lyrical themes, and a live rendition of "Metal Detector" that captures raw performance energy absent from the studio version on the standard album. Other highlights encompass brief interstitial pieces like "Why Did You Grow A Beard?" and "My Other Phone Is A Boom Car," which reflect the podcast's playful, stream-of-consciousness style. Overall, it serves as a companion piece emphasizing the band's iterative process through demos, alternates, and unused ideas.
With a total runtime of 40:47, the disc balances longer compositions with succinct clips, creating a dynamic listening experience that underscores They Might Be Giants' multimedia approach to music distribution in the mid-2000s.
