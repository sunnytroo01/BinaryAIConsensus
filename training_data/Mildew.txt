Mildew
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Mildew is a superficial, often whitish or powdery fungal growth that develops on living plants, organic matter, or damp surfaces in humid environments, caused by various species of fungi or fungus-like organisms that thrive under conditions of high moisture and moderate temperatures.
The term encompasses several distinct types, including powdery mildew, which is produced by obligate parasitic true fungi in the order Erysiphales (such as Blumeria graminis on cereals or Podosphaera xanthii on cucurbits), appearing as discrete white spots that coalesce into a flour-like coating on leaves, stems, and fruits; downy mildew, caused by oomycete pathogens like Plasmopara viticola on grapes, featuring grayish-white sporulation primarily on the undersides of leaves under cool, wet conditions; and indoor or surface mildew, often involving saprophytic fungi such as Aureobasidium pullulans that form thin, flat, pigmented films on painted wood, fabrics, or walls in areas with poor ventilation and condensation. These organisms reproduce via sporesconidia for powdery mildews, sporangia for downy mildewsthat spread by wind, water splash, or air currents, with many species requiring living host tissue for growth while others persist as resting structures like oospores or cleistothecia during unfavorable conditions.
Mildew significantly impacts agriculture and built environments, causing substantial crop yield losses (e.g., 1520% in mango production due to powdery mildew) through reduced photosynthesis, premature defoliation, and fruit deformation, while indoor growth discolors surfaces and degrades materials without posing direct health risks beyond potential allergenicity from spores. Effective management relies on cultural practices like improving air circulation, reducing relative humidity below 60%, and applying fungicides such as sulfur for powdery mildew or copper-based compounds for downy types, though resistance and environmental regulations influence control strategies.

Definition and Classification

Definition

Mildew is a collective term referring to certain fungi and fungus-like organisms that form thin, often white, grayish, or pigmented coatings on surfaces, often resembling a powdery or fuzzy film. These coatings primarily arise from two major groups: powdery mildews, caused by ascomycete fungi in the order Erysiphales, which produce superficial mycelium and spores on host plants; and downy mildews, caused by oomycete pathogens in the order Peronosporales, which generate sporangia on the undersides of leaves.
The term "mildew" originated in Old English as "meledeaw" or "mildaw," meaning "honeydew," a reference to the sweet, sticky substance secreted by aphids on plants, which was once thought to form like dew. By the mid-13th century, it began to denote minute parasitic fungi affecting plants, and by the 16th century, its usage had evolved to encompass superficial fungal growths more broadly, distinguishing them from other plant afflictions.
A key distinction of mildew from related fungi like mold lies in its growth pattern: mildew typically forms superficial, non-penetrating coatings on living or dead organic matter under humid conditions, whereas molds often invade deeper into substrates, causing more structural damage.

Types

Mildew encompasses several distinct types, primarily differentiated by their causative organisms and visible morphologies, which reflect underlying taxonomic variations. The two most prominent categories are powdery mildew and downy mildew, caused by true fungi and oomycete pathogens, respectively, while other forms like sooty mold and slime molds exhibit mildew-like appearances but differ in etiology and biology.
Powdery mildew refers to diseases caused by obligate biotrophic ascomycete fungi in the order Erysiphales, such as Blumeria graminis, which primarily infects cereal crops like wheat and barley. These pathogens produce a characteristic white, powdery coating of conidia on the surfaces of leaves, stems, and fruits, resulting from superficial mycelial growth that does not penetrate host tissues deeply. This type affects over 10,000 species of angiosperms across diverse families, making it one of the most widespread plant pathogens globally.
In contrast, downy mildew arises from oomycete pathogens in the phylum Oomycotastramenopile organisms distinct from true fungisuch as Plasmopara viticola, the causal agent of grapevine downy mildew. Morphologically, it manifests as purplish-gray, fuzzy sporangia primarily on the undersides of leaves, often accompanied by angular yellow lesions on the upper surfaces during humid conditions. This pathogen has been a major threat to viticulture since its introduction to Europe in the mid-19th century, analogous to the oomycete-driven Irish potato famine caused by Phytophthora infestans.
Surface or indoor mildew refers to superficial growths caused by saprophytic fungi such as Aureobasidium pullulans and Cladosporium spp., which form thin, flat, often pigmented (e.g., black or pink) films on damp indoor surfaces like painted walls, wood, or fabrics in humid, poorly ventilated areas. Unlike plant-pathogenic mildews, these are not obligate parasites and thrive on non-living organic matter, contributing to material discoloration and degradation.
Other mildew-like conditions include sooty mold, which involves dark fungal growths such as species in the genus Capnodium that form a black, sooty residue on plant surfaces by colonizing honeydew excreted by sap-feeding insects like aphids and scale. Unlike true mildews, sooty mold is not a direct plant pathogen but a secondary epiphyte that reduces photosynthesis by covering foliage. Slime molds, classified in the kingdom Protista as myxomycetes, appear as non-fungal, slime-like masses in damp, organic-rich environments such as mulch or turf, occasionally mimicking mildew on plants but deriving nutrition from decaying matter and microorganisms rather than living hosts.

Biology

Structure and Characteristics

Mildew fungi are characterized by a filamentous body structure composed of hyphae, which are thread-like filaments that interconnect to form a mycelium, the vegetative body of the organism. In powdery mildews, belonging to the Ascomycota phylum, the hyphae are typically septate, meaning they are divided by cross-walls, and they produce asexual spores known as conidia in chains at the tips of specialized hyphae called conidiophores. Downy mildews, classified as oomycetes rather than true fungi, possess coenocytic hyphae lacking septa and generate sporangia that release motile zoospores for dispersal.
Macroscopically, powdery mildews display a superficial, non-penetrating growth pattern where the mycelium forms a white, powdery coating on the host surface without invading deeper tissues, though haustoriaspecialized absorbing structuresextend into epidermal cells to extract nutrients. In downy mildews, the mycelium grows internally within host tissues, with haustoria that penetrate and invade host cells more extensively to facilitate nutrient uptake, often resulting in a fuzzy, downy appearance on the underside of leaves. These morphological differences distinguish the two major types of mildew, with powdery forms exhibiting ascomycete traits and downy forms showing oomycete features (as detailed in Types).
At the genetic and cellular level, powdery mildews as ascomycetes predominantly maintain a haploid phase throughout their vegetative growth, with a brief diploid stage occurring only during sexual reproduction in asci. Oomycetes in downy mildews, however, feature a diploid dominant vegetative phase, with meiosis occurring in the sexual structures to produce haploid gametes. A key biochemical distinction lies in cell wall composition: true fungal mildews like powdery types have walls primarily of chitin, a polymer of N-acetylglucosamine, providing structural rigidity, whereas oomycete cell walls in downy mildews are mainly composed of cellulose, similar to plant cells.

Life Cycle

The life cycle of powdery mildew fungi, belonging to the order Erysiphales, encompasses distinct dormant, dispersal, infection, and reproductive stages that facilitate survival and proliferation on host plants. These obligate biotrophic pathogens overwinter primarily as cleistotheciasmall, black fruiting bodies containing ascosporeson plant debris, bark, stems, or dormant buds of perennial hosts, ensuring persistence through unfavorable conditions. In some cases, mycelial strands or infected buds also serve as overwintering sites.
With the onset of spring and warming temperatures, cleistothecia rupture to release ascospores, which are wind-dispersed to susceptible host surfaces, initiating primary infections. These ascospores germinate rapidly under moderate temperatures (typically 6080F) and relative humidity levels that avoid free water, forming germ tubes that develop appressoriaspecialized structures enabling penetration of the host epidermis without requiring wounds. Once inside, the fungus forms haustoria to colonize living plant tissues, deriving nutrients while maintaining host viability for sustained growth.
The infection process transitions into prolific asexual reproduction during favorable growing conditions, where conidia (asexual spores) are produced in chains on surface mycelium and dispersed by wind currents, particularly in dry daytime weather following humid nights. These conidia germinate similarly to ascospores, perpetuating secondary infections and enabling the cycle to complete in 510 days under optimal humidity and temperature, often resulting in multiple overlapping generations within a single season. Sexual reproduction, culminating in cleistothecia formation later in the season, generates ascospores that promote genetic diversity, aiding adaptation to environmental pressures and host resistance.
The life cycle of downy mildews, caused by oomycete pathogens, involves both sexual and asexual phases adapted to cool, moist conditions. These pathogens overwinter primarily as thick-walled oosporessexual resting sporesformed in infected plant tissues or debris, which can survive in soil or on hosts for years. In spring or during wet periods, oospores germinate to produce sporangia, which serve as primary inoculum; these sporangia can germinate directly via a germ tube or, in the presence of free water, release motile zoospores that swim short distances to infect nearby host tissues, typically penetrating through stomata under temperatures of 5070F (1021C).
Following infection, the coenocytic mycelium grows intercellularly within the host, forming haustoria to absorb nutrients and producing conidiophores that emerge from stomata to bear sporangia on the lower leaf surfaces during high humidity. These sporangia enable secondary infections, with the cycle completing in 310 days depending on environmental conditions, leading to epidemics in prolonged wet weather. Sexual reproduction occurs when antheridia fertilize oogonia within host tissues, producing oospores that enhance survival and genetic variability.

Environmental Influences

Growth Conditions

Mildew growth is primarily influenced by abiotic factors such as temperature and humidity, which vary slightly between powdery and downy types. For most mildew species, optimal temperatures range from 15 to 25C (59 to 77F), though powdery mildews often thrive at slightly higher ranges of 20 to 30C (68 to 86F), particularly during warm days followed by cool nights that elevate relative humidity. Relative humidity above 85% is essential for development across both types, with downy mildews requiring prolonged leaf wetness or free water droplets for spore germination and infection, while powdery mildews can infect dry foliage but benefit from high nighttime humidity for sporulation.
Light exposure plays a critical role in mildew proliferation, with powdery mildews favoring shaded, low-light environments where reduced UV radiation allows spore germination and hyphal growth to proceed uninhibited. In contrast, ultraviolet light exposure significantly inhibits spore germination for both powdery and downy mildews by damaging fungal structures.
Soil and air conditions further modulate mildew risk. Poor ventilation and dense planting exacerbate growth by trapping moisture, elevating local humidity, and limiting airflow, which concentrates spores and prolongs wet periods on plant surfaces.

Habitats

Mildew fungi, particularly powdery mildew species in the order Erysiphales, commonly inhabit natural settings such as forests and grasslands, where they infect wild plants including trees, shrubs, and herbaceous species like oaks, lilacs, and turfgrasses. These environments provide suitable substrates for spore germination and spread on leaf surfaces, often in shaded or densely vegetated areas.
In agricultural fields, mildew has been a persistent issue since ancient times, affecting crops like vineyards and cereals; historical records, including biblical references in Deuteronomy 28:22 and Amos 4:9, describe mildew as a destructive force on grains and orchards, leading to famines. Powdery mildew on grapes and wheat, for instance, thrives in cultivated monocultures, facilitating rapid epidemics during growing seasons.
Indoor environments favor mildew growth in areas with poor airflow and elevated humidity, such as bathrooms, basements, and storage spaces for textiles and leather, where spores colonize damp surfaces like walls, fabrics, and organic materials. These settings mimic moist natural conditions, allowing surface moldsoften termed mildewto proliferate on household substrates.
Mildew exhibits a cosmopolitan global distribution, occurring worldwide but causing frequent epidemics in temperate zones with moderate temperatures and humidity that support spore dispersal. Climate change is expanding its range, with increasing incidence in tropical regions due to warmer, more humid conditions that enhance suitable habitats for species like rubber tree powdery mildew.

Effects

On Plants

Mildew infections, particularly powdery and downy types, manifest on plants through distinct pathological symptoms that impair photosynthesis and overall vigor. Powdery mildew typically presents as white to gray powdery spots or mats on the upper surfaces of leaves, stems, and buds, often leading to chlorosis, yellowing, and premature defoliation as infected tissues distort and die. In contrast, downy mildew begins with small, water-soaked, chlorotic spots on leaf undersides that progress to angular yellow-green lesions and eventual necrosis, causing stunted growth and leaf drop. These symptoms reduce the plant's ability to produce energy, resulting in weakened structures and aesthetic damage from the visible fungal growth in powdery cases or tissue death in downy infections.
Mildew pathogens exhibit high host specificity, infecting a vast array of plants including over 10,000 species worldwide, with numerous agricultural crops such as cucurbits (e.g., cucumbers and squash for downy mildew) and roses (for powdery mildew) among the primary targets. Economic consequences are severe, with untreated fields experiencing yield losses of up to 45% in cereals from powdery mildew and significant reductions in fruit quality and quantity for cucurbits due to downy mildew-induced defoliation. In viticulture, both powdery and downy mildews pose ongoing threats to grape production, weakening berry skins, reducing photosynthesis, and causing substantial harvest shortfalls if unmanaged.
Historically, downy mildew analogs like the oomycete Phytophthora infestans triggered the Irish Potato Famine from 1845 to 1852, devastating potato crops across Europe and leading to widespread starvation and emigration. This event underscores the potential for mildew-related pathogens to cause catastrophic agricultural and societal impacts, a risk that persists in modern cropping systems vulnerable to similar foliar diseases.

On Humans and Materials

Mildew exposure poses notable health risks to humans, primarily through the inhalation of spores that trigger allergic reactions and exacerbate respiratory conditions. Common symptoms include sneezing, runny nose, watery eyes, coughing, wheezing, and skin irritation, particularly in individuals with pre-existing allergies or asthma. In sensitive populations, such as those with asthma, mildew spores can provoke acute attacks, leading to shortness of breath and chest tightness. These effects are well-documented in indoor environments where dampness promotes mildew growth, with dampness problems reported in at least 20% of buildings in Europe and North America, and up to 50% in U.S. homes, impacting occupants in these environments.
While most health issues stem from allergic responses, rare cases involve mycotoxin production by certain indoor fungi in persistently damp buildings, such as Stachybotrys chartarum (a mold often found in water-damaged areas alongside mildew), potentially causing more severe toxicity such as fatigue, neurological symptoms, or immune suppression. Mycotoxins, toxic metabolites from such fungi, are not commonly produced but can aerosolize in poorly ventilated spaces, contributing to chronic inflammation or hypersensitivity pneumonitis in vulnerable individuals. However, such severe outcomes are uncommon, with the majority of exposures resulting in irritant rather than toxic effects.
Beyond direct health impacts, mildew contributes to material degradation in human environments, causing discoloration, structural weakening, and aesthetic damage to various substrates. On fabrics and textiles, mildew enzymes break down cellulose and protein fibers, leading to permanent staining, musty odors, and reduced tensile strength, as seen in clothing, upholstery, and historical artifacts. Paper and cardboard suffer similar enzymatic digestion, resulting in foxing stains, embrittlement, and loss of integrity in books, documents, and packaging. Wood surfaces, including furniture and building elements, experience surface pitting, darkening, and diminished load-bearing capacity over time due to mycelial penetration.
This pervasive growth in indoor settings also plays a key role in sick building syndrome (SBS), a condition characterized by non-specific symptoms like headaches, fatigue, and concentration difficulties among occupants of mold-contaminated structures. Mildew-related volatile organic compounds and spores degrade indoor air quality, amplifying SBS in buildings with chronic moisture issues.
Economically, indoor mildew imposes substantial remediation burdens, with the global mold remediation service market valued at approximately USD 1.23 billion in 2023 and projected to grow due to rising incidences of water damage from climate events. In the United States alone, average professional cleanup costs range from $1,373 to $3,325 per incident, underscoring the scale of expenses for households and property owners addressing discoloration, health hazards, and material repairs.

Prevention and Management

Agricultural Methods

Agricultural methods for controlling mildew, particularly powdery mildew, in crop production rely on integrated pest management (IPM) approaches that combine cultural, chemical, and biological strategies to minimize disease impact while promoting sustainable farming practices. IPM emphasizes monitoring disease levels, using economic thresholds to guide interventions, and integrating multiple tactics to reduce reliance on any single method, thereby delaying resistance development in fungal populations.
Cultural practices form the foundation of mildew prevention by altering the crop environment to discourage pathogen establishment. Crop rotation with non-host plants interrupts the disease cycle, reducing inoculum buildup in soil and crop residues over multiple seasons. Planting resistant varieties is a key strategy; for instance, since the 2010s, gene-edited wheat lines targeting susceptibility genes like TaMLO via CRISPR/Cas9 have demonstrated enhanced resistance to powdery mildew without introducing foreign DNA. Pruning to improve airflow within the canopy lowers humidity and leaf wetness duration, critical factors for spore germination, and is particularly effective in dense crops like grapes and cucurbits.
Chemical controls target active infections when cultural measures are insufficient, with fungicide selection and timing optimized to maximize efficacy and minimize environmental impact. Elemental sulfur, one of the oldest fungicides documented since 1000 BC, has been used since the mid-19th century to control powdery mildew on crops like grapes and remains widely used due to its low cost and broad-spectrum activity against ascomycete fungi. Systemic azoles, such as tebuconazole, provide protective and curative action by inhibiting ergosterol biosynthesis in fungal membranes and are effective against powdery mildew in cereals and vegetables. Application timing is guided by disease forecasting models, such as the POMICS simulation for cucurbits, which uses weather data like temperature and leaf wetness to predict infection risk and recommend sprays during the pathogen's latent period. As of 2025, new fungicides with novel modes of action continue to be introduced for cucurbit powdery mildew control.
Biological agents offer environmentally friendly alternatives, especially in organic systems, by leveraging natural antagonism against mildew pathogens. Beneficial microbes like Bacillus subtilis colonize plant surfaces and produce antibiotics such as surfactin, which inhibit conidial germination and mycelial growth of powdery mildew fungi, achieving up to 91% disease suppression in greenhouse trials on wheat. These biopesticides, often formulated as sprays, support organic certifications under standards like OMRI (Organic Materials Review Institute), enabling sustainable farming by integrating with cultural practices to maintain soil health and biodiversity.

Household Strategies

Preventing mildew in households begins with controlling indoor moisture levels, as high relative humidity promotes fungal growth on surfaces. Dehumidifiers are effective tools for maintaining relative humidity below 50%, ideally between 30% and 50%, which inhibits mildew development in damp areas like basements and bathrooms. Ventilation fans, particularly exhaust fans in bathrooms, should be used during and after showers to remove excess moisture and reduce humidity buildup, preventing condensation on tiles and fixtures. Additionally, ultraviolet (UV) lamps, especially those emitting UV-C light, can sterilize surfaces by disrupting the DNA of mildew spores, offering a non-chemical option for high-risk areas like air ducts or shower enclosures.
For removing existing mildew, householders can address small infestations on non-porous surfaces using natural or mild chemical solutions. A vinegar solution, consisting of undiluted white vinegar applied directly and left to sit for an hour before scrubbing, effectively kills mildew on tiles, glass, and countertops without harsh residues. For stubborn spots on similar surfaces, such as black mildew on shower grout, a diluted bleach solution (one part bleach to 10 parts water, or a stronger 1:4 ratio for heavy cases) can be sprayed or applied to the affected areas and left to sit for 10-15 minutes without drying, followed by vigorous scrubbing with a stiff brush, thorough rinsing, and complete drying to avoid damaging materials or releasing fumes. Alternatively, for more adherent growth, a paste made from three parts baking soda to one part bleach can be applied, allowed to sit for 15 minutes, scrubbed, and then rinsed after additional sitting time if needed. Bleach kills mold roots but may discolor grout over time, so test in an inconspicuous area first and ensure proper ventilation. Commercial options, such as Tilex Mold & Mildew Remover or Clorox Bleach Gel for targeted application, provide effective alternatives without scrubbing in some cases. However, for infestations exceeding 10 square feet, professional remediation is recommended to ensure safe containment and complete eradication, as DIY methods may not address hidden growth or structural issues.
To prevent mildew development on clothing and fabrics, wet laundry should be removed promptly from the washer after the cycle ends. Wet laundry can typically sit in the washer for 8 to 12 hours before mildewing or developing a musty odor, depending on factors like temperature, humidity, and ventilation. Beyond this timeframe, bacteria and mold growth increase, often requiring a rewash. Overnight (about 8-10 hours) is generally acceptable, but sooner is better to prevent issues.
Handling wet clothing that has developed a sour mildew smell involves prompt action to prevent further growth and odor persistence. First, move the clothing to a dry area and sort into washable and delicate items. For washable items, wash in the hottest safe water using detergent plus 1-2 cups of white vinegar or baking soda, followed by an extra rinse cycle, and dry thoroughly in a hot dryer or sunlight. For stubborn items, pre-soak in hot water with vinegar for 30-60 minutes before washing. Delicate items can be sprayed with a 50/50 vinegar-water mix and air-dried. Rewash if the smell persists, and discard heavily molded items showing black or green spots.
Modern household tools have enhanced mildew management since the early 2000s, incorporating antimicrobial technologies into everyday materials. Paints with built-in antimicrobial agents, such as those approved for mildew resistance under EPA guidelines, provide long-lasting protection on walls and ceilings by inhibiting fungal adhesion and growth. Similarly, fabrics treated with antimicrobial additives, like silver or zinc-based compounds, resist mildew in upholstery and curtains, maintaining efficacy through multiple washes. Hygrometers, affordable digital devices for real-time humidity monitoring, allow homeowners to track conditions and adjust dehumidifiers or ventilation proactively, keeping levels below thresholds that favor mildew.
