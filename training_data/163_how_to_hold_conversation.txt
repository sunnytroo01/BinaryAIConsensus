Here is how to hold a conversation that people actually enjoy and remember.

The number one rule is to be genuinely interested in the other person. Not pretending to be interested. Not performing interest. Actually being curious about who they are, what they think, and what their life is like. If you approach every conversation with the mindset "I wonder what I can learn from this person," you will never run out of things to talk about and the other person will feel valued.

Start with a warm opening. Not "How are you" on autopilot. Something that shows you are present. "It is really good to see you. How have things been since we last talked?" "I have been looking forward to this conversation. What have you been working on?" "You look like you are having a good day. What is going right?"

Ask open-ended questions. Questions that start with what, how, and why produce richer answers than questions that start with do, did, or is. "What do you enjoy most about your work?" opens a conversation. "Do you like your job?" closes it with a yes or no. "How did you get into photography?" invites a story. "Are you a photographer?" invites a one-word answer.

Listen actively. This means making eye contact, nodding, reacting with facial expressions, and giving verbal cues like "really?" and "tell me more" and "that is fascinating." It means putting your phone away entirely, not face-down on the table, but in your pocket or bag where it cannot distract you. It means resisting the urge to formulate your response while the other person is still talking. You cannot listen and plan your next sentence at the same time. Choose listening.

Follow up on what they said. The most powerful conversational move is to pick up on a specific detail from the other person's last statement and ask about it. They say: "I just got back from two weeks in Japan." Bad response: "Cool. I have always wanted to go there." Better response: "Two weeks! What was the highlight? Was there a moment where you felt like you were experiencing something you could not get anywhere else?" The specificity of your follow-up proves that you were actually listening, and it invites a deeper, more personal response.

Share your own experiences in response, not as a way to take over the conversation but as a way to create connection. When the other person shares something, relate to it with your own experience at roughly the same depth. They tell you about a challenging work project. You share a time when you faced something similar. They tell you about a family tradition. You share one of yours. This reciprocity creates the feeling of mutual exchange that makes conversation satisfying for both people.

Use the person's name occasionally. People respond to hearing their own name. It creates a sense of intimacy and attention. "That is a really interesting point, Sarah." "I had not thought of it that way, Marcus." Do not overdo it, two or three times in a conversation is plenty, but using someone's name at natural moments deepens the connection.

Read the energy. If the other person is energetic and enthusiastic, match that energy. If they are quiet and reflective, dial yours down. If they seem eager to talk about something, let them. If they seem uncomfortable with a topic, move on. Conversation is a dance, and the best conversationalists adjust their rhythm to match their partner.

Know how to handle silences. Not every silence needs to be filled. A brief pause after someone shares something meaningful is not awkward. It is respectful. It shows you are processing what they said rather than rushing to respond. If the silence stretches and feels uncomfortable, you can break it with a simple "That really makes me think" or by introducing a new topic: "On a completely different note, have you seen anything good lately?"

Tell good stories. The best stories have a clear setup, a complication, and a resolution. They have specific details that make the listener feel like they were there. They have an emotional point: they are funny, surprising, moving, or thought-provoking. And they are an appropriate length for the context. A thirty-second anecdote at a networking event. A five-minute story over dinner. The worst sin of storytelling is going on too long. Edit your stories ruthlessly. Cut everything that does not serve the point.

End conversations gracefully. Do not just trail off or look at your phone. Signal that the conversation is wrapping up with warmth and intention. "This has been such a great conversation. I am really glad we got to talk." "I need to head out, but I want to continue this. Can I get your number?" "Thank you for sharing that with me. It means a lot." A good ending leaves the other person feeling valued and eager to talk again.

The secret to being a great conversationalist is not being the most interesting person in the room. It is being the most interested. The person who asks the best questions, listens with genuine attention, and makes the other person feel heard and appreciated will always be remembered as someone wonderful to talk to, even if they said very little about themselves.
