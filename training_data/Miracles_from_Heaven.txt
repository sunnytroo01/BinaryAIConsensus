Miracles from Heaven

 Miracles from Heaven is a 2015 Christian memoir written by Christy Wilson Beam, detailing the true story of her daughter Annabel Beam, who at age five was diagnosed with two rare and incurable digestive disorderspseudo-obstruction motility disorder and antral hypomotility disorderthat caused severe abdominal pain, vomiting, and frequent hospitalizations, rendering her unable to lead a normal life. In December 2011, while climbing a 30-foot-tall cottonwood tree on the family's Texas ranch, nine-year-old Annabel fell headfirst into a hollow trunk, remaining trapped for nearly five hours in a near-fetal position before being rescued by emergency services; remarkably, she sustained no broken bones or serious injuries from the fall. Following the accident, Annabel experienced a complete and inexplicable recovery, with her symptoms vanishing entirelyno further pain, medications, or hospital visits requiredleaving her doctors, including specialists at Boston Children's Hospital, baffled as tests confirmed she was fully asymptomatic. Annabel attributed her healing to a near-death experience in which she visited heaven and met Jesus, who assured her she would be healed and return to her family.
The book, published by FaithWords (an imprint of Hachette Book Group), became a New York Times bestseller and emphasizes themes of faith, perseverance, and divine intervention amid the Beam family's struggles. It was adapted into a 2016 American Christian drama film of the same name, directed by Patricia Riggen and produced by DeVon Franklin for Affirm Films and Walden Media in association with Sony Pictures. Starring Jennifer Garner as Christy Beam, Kylie Rogers as Annabel, and Martin Henderson as her father Kevin, the movie portrays the family's emotional journey, including travels to seek treatment and the profound impact of the miracle on their faith. Released on March 16, 2016, the film received positive reviews for its heartfelt storytelling and performances, grossed over $73 million at the box office on a $13 million budget, and earned a PG rating from the Motion Picture Association of America for its family-friendly content.

Background and development

True story and book

The true story behind Miracles from Heaven centers on the Beam family from Burleson, Texas, whose experiences from 2007 to 2012 inspired the film's faith-based narrative. Christy Beam, a mother of three daughtersAbbie, Annabel (often called Anna), and Adelynnalong with her husband Kevin, faced profound challenges when their middle daughter, Annabel, began exhibiting severe digestive issues around age four (late 2006). By early 2008, Annabel's condition worsened dramatically; on March 20, 2008, she awoke in intense abdominal pain, leading to emergency medical evaluation that revealed a life-threatening intestinal blockage requiring immediate surgery.
Following the surgery, Annabel was diagnosed with two rare and incurable disorders: pseudo-obstruction motility disorder, which prevented proper intestinal movement and food processing, and antral hypomotility disorder, affecting stomach function. These conditions caused chronic pain, vomiting, malnutrition, and repeated hospitalizations, necessitating feeding tubes and experimental treatments that offered little relief over the next several years. In January 2009, desperate for answers, Christy and Annabel traveled to Boston Children's Hospital, where pediatric gastroenterologist Dr. Samuel Nurko, director of the Center for Motility and Functional Gastrointestinal Disorders, assumed her care and implemented a regimen to manage her symptoms, though a full cure remained elusive. During their time in Boston, the Beams formed a supportive friendship with local resident Angela Cimino, who provided emotional encouragement and helped them navigate the city.
Annabel's ordeal culminated on December 30, 2011, when, at age nine, she climbed a hollow cottonwood tree during a family outing and fell approximately 30 feet headfirst into its trunk, landing on her head. Rescue teams took nearly five hours to extract her using specialized equipment, yet Annabel emerged with only minor bruises and no spinal or neurological damage, defying medical expectations of paralysis or fatality. Astonishingly, within days, her digestive disorders vanished entirely; tests confirmed normal function, allowing her to eat solid foods without pain or medication for the first time in yearsa recovery verified by Dr. Nurko and other physicians as medically inexplicable. Annabel later recounted a near-death experience during the fall, describing a vision of heaven where she met Jesus and an angel, who assured her she would return to Earth healed to fulfill a purpose.
The Beams' story gained wider attention through Christy's 2015 memoir, Miracles from Heaven: A Little Girl, Her Journey to Heaven, and Her Amazing Story of Healing, published by Hachette Books on April 14, 2015. The book chronicles the family's emotional and spiritual trials, emphasizing their deepening Christian faith amid despair and the integration of prayer with medical intervention. Christy details Annabel's symptoms, treatments, the Boston journey, the tree accident, and the subsequent healing, framing it as a testament to divine intervention while acknowledging the role of healthcare professionals like Dr. Nurko. By the book's publication, Annabel had maintained full health for over three years without ongoing treatment, and the family continued advocating for hope in faith-based communities, highlighting how the miracle strengthened their resolve to support others facing illness. As of 2025, Annabel had graduated magna cum laude from Hardin-Simmons University and remained healthy. The memoir served as the primary source for the 2016 film adaptation, which dramatizes these events in a condensed timeline.

Development

In November 2014, Sony Pictures Entertainment acquired the film rights to Christy Beam's faith-based memoir Miracles from Heaven, with producers T.D. Jakes, Joe Roth, and DeVon Franklin attached to the project through their prior collaboration on the successful Heaven Is for Real (2014). The production, handled by Sony's Affirm Films division, was budgeted at $13 million and aimed to craft an inspirational family drama rooted in themes of perseverance and faith without overt proselytizing. Screenwriter Randy Brown adapted the book into a script that was finalized by early 2015, emphasizing the emotional journey of the Beam family while maintaining fidelity to the source material; no significant revisions to the screenplay were reported during pre-production.
On April 8, 2015, Patricia Riggen was hired to direct, bringing her experience from the survival drama The 33 (2015) to helm this intimate, character-driven story. Casting began shortly after, with Jennifer Garner announced on April 30, 2015, to portray Christy Beam, the resilient mother at the story's center. By mid-July 2015, the principal cast was assembled, including Kylie Rogers as Anna Beam, Martin Henderson as Kevin Beam, Queen Latifah as Angela Cimino, and Eugenio Derbez as Dr. Nurko, rounding out the ensemble to capture the family's dynamics and supporting figures.
Pre-production efforts included location scouting in Georgia to authentically recreate the Texas setting of the Beam family's life, selecting sites like a Covington farmhouse to stand in for their rural home and ensuring visual consistency with the memoir's Midwestern vibe. This phase focused on assembling a creative team attuned to the project's faith-based ethos, with Franklin noting the intent to appeal broadly by highlighting universal themes of hope and community support alongside the miraculous elements.

Synopsis

Plot

The film is based on the true story recounted in Christy Beam's 2015 memoir of the same name. In Burleson, Texas, the Beam family enjoys a close-knit life centered on faith and community, with mother Christy (Jennifer Garner), veterinarian father Kevin (Martin Henderson), and their three daughters: eldest Abbie (Brighton Sharbino), middle child Anna (Kylie Rogers), and youngest Adelynn (Courtney Fansler). This idyllic existence shatters when 10-year-old Anna suddenly falls ill with severe abdominal pain and uncontrollable vomiting, resulting in a diagnosis of a rare, incurable digestive disorder that renders her unable to process food properly.
Desperate to save her daughter, Christy embarks on an exhaustive search for treatment, shuttling Anna to specialists across the country and ultimately to Boston's renowned pediatric gastroenterologist Dr. Samuel Nurko (Eugenio Derbez). Anna endures grueling procedures, including the insertion of a feeding tube, while the family's finances strain under mounting medical bills and travel costs, forcing Kevin to work longer hours. Christy's once-unwavering faith begins to falter as she grows resentful toward God for allowing Anna's suffering, leading her to withdraw from church and clash with judgmental parishioners who question the family's trials. Amid these hardships, the Beams find solace in Angela (Queen Latifah), a warm-hearted Boston waitress who befriends them during hospital visits and offers unwavering emotional support, helping Christy navigate her doubts.
As Anna's condition worsens and her depression deepens, she joins Abbie in climbing a towering cottonwood tree on their property to escape her pain. A branch gives way, sending Anna plummeting 30 feet headfirst into the tree's hollow trunk, where she remains trapped and unconscious for hours until firefighters rescue her. Miraculously, scans reveal no broken bones or internal injuries beyond superficial scrapes. In the hospital, Anna awakens and recounts a vivid near-death experience: she ascended to heaven, a serene realm of meadows and clouds, where she met Jesus, felt enveloped in love without pain, and received assurance that her suffering would end before being sent back to her family.
The next day, Anna's symptoms vanish entirely; she devours a full meal without distress, baffling Dr. Nurko and his team, who confirm through tests that her digestive disorder has inexplicably resolved, defying all medical precedent. This healing restores Christy's faith, prompting her return to church and reconciliation with her community, while the family grows closer, embracing perseverance and divine grace. The story culminates in a reaffirmation of miracles as evidence of God's presence, underscored by poignant dialogue and evocative visuals of heavenly visions, and closes with a cameo appearance by the real Beam family sharing their testimony.

Cast

The principal cast of Miracles from Heaven (2016) is led by Jennifer Garner, who portrays Christy Beam, the devoted mother navigating her daughter's illness, a role for which she was the producers' first choice due to her established presence in family dramas. Kylie Rogers plays Anna Beam, the afflicted daughter at the center of the story. Martin Henderson stars as Kevin Beam, the supportive father. Eugenio Derbez appears as Dr. Samuel Nurko, the Boston-based specialist, marking a notable dramatic role for the Mexican actor in an English-language film portraying a Latino physician. Queen Latifah rounds out the key ensemble as Angela Bradford, a friend and advocate for the family.
Supporting roles include John Carroll Lynch as Reverend Scott, the family's pastor; Brighton Sharbino as Abbie Beam, the eldest daughter; and Courtney Fansler as Adelynn Beam, the youngest. The child actors, including Rogers, Sharbino, and Fansler, were selected to bring authenticity to the portrayal of family dynamics amid illness and recovery, contributing to the film's inspirational tone. The real-life Dr. Samuel Nurko, on whom Derbez's character is based, makes a cameo appearance.

Production

Filming

Principal photography for Miracles from Heaven commenced in July 2015 in Atlanta, Georgia, which served as a stand-in for the film's Texas and Boston settings. The shoot spanned approximately 40 days, with the main unit completing principal photography on August 31, 2015. Pre-production planning, including location scouting and set preparation, facilitated this timely start following the acquisition of the book rights.
Filming took place at various Atlanta-area sites to capture the story's domestic and institutional elements, including the Georgia Aquarium for medical and hospital sequences, North Decatur Presbyterian Church in Decatur for religious gatherings, and a residential property in Covington for the Beam family home scenes. Production designer David R. Sandefur oversaw the creation of sets evoking 2000s Texas suburbia, such as the family's modest backyard and interiors, to ground the narrative in everyday authenticity. Editor Emma E. Hickox managed on-set dailies to streamline the workflow during the intensive schedule.
The production encountered logistical hurdles in depicting sensitive medical procedures involving young performers, requiring careful direction to maintain emotional realism without distressing the child actors portraying Anna Beam and her sisters. Public interest surged from celebrity sightings, notably on August 2, 2015, when stars Jennifer Garner, Queen Latifah, and Kylie Rogers were photographed at the Georgia Aquarium, sparking early media coverage.
Technically, the film was captured digitally on Arri Alexa cameras by cinematographer Checco Varese, who utilized warm, soft lighting schemes to underscore the themes of faith and hope throughout the visuals.

Soundtrack

The film Miracles from Heaven features an original score composed by Carlo Siliotto, which provides the primary musical foundation for its emotional and spiritual narrative. The soundtrack album, titled Miracles from Heaven (Original Motion Picture Soundtrack), was released on March 11, 2016, by Madison Gate Records under exclusive license to Sony Music Masterworks. It consists of 30 instrumental tracks spanning approximately 49 minutes, including cues such as "Heaven" (3:04), "A Happy Family" (2:52), "Loss of Faith" (2:15), and "The Miracle" (3:42), designed to evoke themes of hope, struggle, and redemption throughout the story.
In addition to the score, the film incorporates several licensed songs blending Christian contemporary and pop elements to heighten its inspirational tone. Key tracks include "Soul on Fire" by Third Day, which serves as the opening credits song and is performed live during a church worship scene; "Your Words" (featuring Harvest) by Third Day; "Collide" (acoustic version) by Howie Day; "Right Where I Belong" by Clayton Anderson; and "Here Comes the Sun" (written by George Harrison and performed by The Beatles, with a runtime of 3:06). These selections, totaling around five prominent songs, underscore pivotal moments such as family bonding and the protagonist's miraculous healing, reinforcing the film's message of faith and uplift without a dedicated original score composer for non-instrumental elements.
The Christian rock band Third Day appears on-screen as the performing church band in relevant scenes, integrating their music directly into the production to enhance authenticity and emotional resonance. While no separate compilation album of these licensed tracks was released, the overall musical choices rely heavily on them for thematic emphasis on hope and spiritual renewal.

Release

Theatrical release

Miracles from Heaven had its world premiere on February 21, 2016, in Dallas, Texas, tying into the film's hometown connection to the Beam family. The film was distributed theatrically in the United States by Sony Pictures Releasing, with a wide release on March 16, 2016.
The marketing campaign highlighted the film's miracle theme through trailers that showcased the extraordinary events surrounding Anna Beam's recovery, positioning it as an inspirational faith-based drama. Partnerships with churches and Christian media organizations were established to promote the movie, including campaign kits featuring video clips for sermon series and community outreach. The film received a PG rating from the MPAA for thematic material, including accident and medical images.
Internationally, the film saw limited theatrical rollouts in over 20 territories, beginning with select markets shortly after the U.S. debut. In the United Kingdom, it opened on June 10, 2016. Promotion included red carpet events with cast members such as Jennifer Garner and Kylie Rogers at premieres in Dallas and Los Angeles, along with appearances tied to faith-oriented discussions, though no major controversies arose during the rollout.

Home media

Miracles from Heaven was released digitally on June 21, 2016, and on DVD, Blu-ray, and 4K UHD formats on July 12, 2016, by Sony Pictures Home Entertainment.
The home media editions included special features such as the behind-the-scenes documentary "Miracles Abound: The Making of Miracles from Heaven," which explores the true story; the featurette "Accounts From Annabel" featuring interviews with the real Beam family; audio commentary with director Patricia Riggen and screenwriter Randy Brown; deleted scenes; and a faith-based discussion guide designed for church groups to facilitate biblically grounded conversations on themes from the film.
In sales performance, the release debuted at number 2 on the Nielsen VideoScan home video chart for the week ending July 17, 2016, with 263,438 DVD units and 78,451 Blu-ray units sold, trailing only The Divergent Series: Allegiant. Domestic video sales ultimately totaled an estimated $21.3 million.
The film was available for streaming on Netflix from 2016 to 2018; as of 2025, it streams on Prime Video, Netflix, and faith-based services such as Pure Flix.

Reception

Box office

Miracles from Heaven was produced on a budget of $13 million. The film grossed $61.7 million in North America and $12.3 million internationally, for a worldwide total of $74.0 million.
It opened in 3,047 theaters on March 16, 2016, earning $14.8 million over its five-day debut weekend (March 1620), placing third at the North American box office behind Zootopia ($23.0 million) and The Divergent Series: Allegiant ($18.7 million). The film achieved a strong per-screen average of $4,861, particularly in faith-oriented regions with high family attendance.
During its domestic run, Miracles from Heaven demonstrated solid word-of-mouth among family and faith-based audiences, dropping 35% in its second weekend to $9.7 million. The film's midweek release timing allowed it to capitalize on spring break periods, contributing to sustained performance over several weeks.
Internationally, performance was modest overall, with a minimal opening of $29,123 in the United Kingdom. It fared better in select faith markets, such as Brazil, where it earned $752,487.
The film proved highly profitable, returning over five times its budget and marking a success in the Christian film genre, similar to Heaven Is for Real, which grossed $91.4 million domestically on a $12 million budget.

Critical response

The film received mixed reviews from critics, with aggregate scores reflecting a divide between its inspirational appeal and perceived formulaic elements. On Rotten Tomatoes, Miracles from Heaven holds a 45% approval rating based on 91 reviews; the site's consensus describes it as "Miracles from Heaven makes the most out of an outstanding performance from Jennifer Garner, but it isn't quite enough to keep this faith-based drama from preaching to the choir." Metacritic assigns it a score of 44 out of 100 based on 20 critics, indicating "mixed or average" reviews, with 35% positive, 40% mixed, and 25% negative assessments. In contrast, audiences awarded it an A+ CinemaScore, highlighting strong resonance with general viewers.
Critics frequently praised Jennifer Garner's lead performance as Christy Beam, noting her ability to convey raw anguish, determination, and emotional depth in portraying a mother's crisis of faith and relentless advocacy. The film's depiction of family dynamics and emotional toll of illness also drew acclaim for its heartfelt, relatable scenes that effectively capture parental vulnerability and solidarity. Variety highlighted its uplifting message and integration of faith elements, such as the child's heavenly vision, as central to its inspirational intent, making it a strong entry in the faith-based genre.
However, many reviews criticized the narrative for its predictability and heavy reliance on sentimentality, with the miracle resolution feeling manipulative and overly simplistic. The medical and dramatic aspects were seen as lacking depth, resembling a formulaic "disease-of-the-week" story without sufficient complexity or visual flair. RogerEbert.com gave it 2 out of 4 stars, commending the intimate direction but faulting weaker visual effects and elements like the subplot with a supportive waitress as somewhat facile despite their basis in real events. Opinions on the child actors' performances were mixed, with some noting strong emotional delivery amid the intensity of illness scenes, while others felt the young leads were overshadowed by the adult-driven drama.
Faith-based outlets offered higher praise, emphasizing the film's alignment with Christian values such as perseverance, community support, and God's sovereignty in suffering and healing. Plugged In commended its human portrayal of faith struggles, restoration through miracles, and family sacrifices, rating it suitable with light cautions for teens and adults due to its positive thematic focus. This reception underscores a broader split, where mainstream critics viewed it as earnest but conventional, while religious audiences appreciated its affirming spiritual message.

Accolades

Miracles from Heaven received recognition primarily in faith-based and family-oriented award categories, earning two wins and five nominations overall.
At the 2016 Teen Choice Awards, the film won the Choice Movie: Drama award, with Jennifer Garner nominated for Choice Movie Actress: Drama.
In 2017, it was nominated for Favorite Dramatic Movie at the People's Choice Awards.
The film also secured a win for Best Movie for Families at the 2017 MovieGuide Awards, while Garner received a nomination for the Grace Award for Most Inspiring Performance in Movies.
It earned a nomination for the Epiphany Prize for Most Inspiring Movie at the same ceremony.
The film received no nominations from major awards bodies such as the Academy Awards or Golden Globe Awards.
In Christian media, Miracles from Heaven was awarded the Faith-Friendly Seal for ages 12-plus and five Doves by the Dove Foundation, indicating suitability for family viewing.
