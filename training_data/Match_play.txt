Match play

 Match play is a scoring format in golf in which players or teams compete head-to-head to win individual holes rather than tallying total strokes over an entire round, with a hole won by the side completing it in the fewest strokes and a match concluded when one side leads by more holes than remain to be played. Unlike stroke play, which focuses on cumulative scores across all holes, match play emphasizes direct confrontation and strategy on a hole-by-hole basis, allowing for concessions of strokes or holes to speed up play and influence outcomes. This format, governed by the Rules of Golf established by the United States Golf Association (USGA) and The R&A, does not require scorecards and permits players to agree on certain actions, such as resolving disputes, though penalties must still be applied.
Historically, match play served as the original competitive structure in golf, dating back to the sport's early development in Scotland, and it dominated professional tournaments for much of the 20th century. The PGA Championship, one of golf's major events, was contested exclusively in match play from its inception in 1916won by Jim Barnes at Siwanoy Country Clubthrough 1957, when Lionel Hebert claimed the final title in that format by defeating Dow Finsterwald 2&1 at Miami Valley Golf Club. The event then transitioned to stroke play in 1958 to align with the growing popularity of that format in major championships and to accommodate larger fields and television broadcasting. Earlier precedents include the News of the World Match Play tournament, first held in 1903 in England and won by figures like J.H. Taylor and James Braid, which ran until 1950 and helped establish match play as a prestigious professional contest.
In modern golf, match play thrives in team competitions and select individual events, fostering intense rivalries and dramatic moments due to its knockout-style progression. The Ryder Cup, contested biennially since 1927 between teams from the United States and Europe, exemplifies this with its mix of foursomes, fourball, and singles matches, drawing massive global audiences for its high-stakes, hole-by-hole battles. Similar formats appear in the Presidents Cup (U.S. versus International teams since 1994), Solheim Cup (women's U.S. versus Europe since 1990), and Walker Cup (amateur U.S. versus Great Britain and Ireland since 1922), all of which highlight match play's role in international and amateur golf. On the professional tour, the former WGC-Dell Technologies Match Play (discontinued after 2023), along with portions of the NCAA Division I Championships and U.S. Amateur, continue to showcase the format's enduring appeal for its emphasis on mental toughness and tactical playas of 2025, with no annual individual professional match play events on the PGA Tour schedule. Handicap adjustments in club-level match play further democratize the game, enabling players of varying abilities to compete evenly by allocating strokes on the most difficult holes.

Introduction

Definition and Basics

Match play is a fundamental scoring format in golf where two players or teams compete directly against each other in a head-to-head contest, with the outcome determined on a hole-by-hole basis rather than by cumulative strokes over the entire round. In this system, the player or team with the lowest number of strokes on a given hole wins that hole, while a tie results in the hole being halved, awarding no net advantage to either side. Golf matches are typically played over 18 holes on a standard course, though shorter formats exist, and the overall winner is the side that secures the majority of holes or reaches an insurmountable lead before the round concludes.
The basic mechanics emphasize direct competition on a hole-by-hole basis. A match ends when one side leads by more holes than remain to be playedfor instance, leading by five holes with four leftor after all holes are completed if neither has an outright majority. This format fosters strategic play, as players focus on outperforming their opponent on each hole independently, contrasting with stroke play, the other primary golf scoring method that tallies total strokes across the round.
Key terminology in match play includes descriptors for the match's progress: a player leading by one or more holes is said to be "up," while trailing is "down," and an even score is "all square." Common formats extend beyond individual singles matches to team variations, such as foursomeswhere partners alternate shots on one ballor fourball, where each player uses their own ball and the team's score is the better of the two on each hole. These elements highlight match play's emphasis on rivalry and tactical decision-making within golf's structured 18-hole framework.

Comparison to Stroke Play

Match play and stroke play represent the two primary formats of competitive golf, each with distinct scoring mechanisms that fundamentally shape the game's dynamics. In stroke play, competitors tally the total number of strokes taken across all holes in a round or tournament, with the player or team achieving the lowest cumulative score declared the winner. Conversely, match play focuses on a head-to-head contest where the outcome of each individual hole determines the point allocation, and the match concludes when one side secures more holes won than remain to be played, often allowing for early concessions if a lead becomes insurmountable. This hole-by-hole structure in match play eliminates the need for comprehensive scorekeeping beyond the holes, unlike stroke play, which requires precise tracking of every stroke to compare against the entire field.
The gameplay implications of these formats diverge significantly, influencing strategic decisions on the course. Match play encourages aggressive tactics because a player need only outperform their opponent on the current hole to claim the point, fostering risk-taking without the long-term penalty of a high total score; a poor shot affects only that hole's result, enabling recovery in subsequent ones. In contrast, stroke play demands consistency and caution to minimize overall strokes, as every error compounds into the final tally, rewarding methodical play over bold maneuvers. This difference also extends to rule enforcement: penalties in match play typically result in loss of the hole to protect the integrity of the direct matchup, while stroke play imposes additional strokes to safeguard the competition's broader fairness.
Stroke play predominates in individual professional tournaments, such as majors like The Open Championship, where large fields compete over multiple rounds to identify the lowest overall scorer. Match play, however, thrives in team-based or knockout events, exemplified by the Ryder Cup, where its format heightens rivalry through decisive hole outcomes. Regarding advantages, match play offers a faster pace and heightened spectator engagement, as matches can end abruptly with concessions and focus on interpersonal drama rather than prolonged accumulation. Its disadvantage lies in potentially overlooking comprehensive skill assessment, as a single strong round may not reflect sustained performance. Stroke play, while more time-intensive and less thrilling for observers due to its emphasis on totals, provides a fairer evaluation of overall proficiency across diverse fields, making it ideal for ranking purposes.

History

Origins in Scotland

Match play, the hole-by-hole competition format central to early golf, emerged in 18th-century Scotland as the predominant way golfers contested rounds. The Royal Burgess Golfing Society of Edinburgh, founded in 1735 and recognized as the world's oldest surviving golf club, conducted its initial activities through such challenges among members at Bruntsfield Links, where players vied directly on individual holes rather than aggregating total strokes. This society exemplified the grassroots nature of the game, with records indicating organized play that emphasized direct confrontations between participants.
The format's roots lay in golf's cultural milieu, where informal wagers and personal challenges fostered a duel-like social dynamic among players, often nobility and locals alike. These encounters, common on Scotland's coastal linksland, prioritized outscoring an opponent hole by hole, reflecting the game's evolution from casual pastimes to structured rivalries without the need for overall score tallies. Betting restrictions imposed by early clubs, such as limits on wager amounts by the Honourable Company of Edinburgh Golfers in the mid-18th century, underscore how integral these stakes were to motivating match play participation.
St. Andrews, home to the Old Course, played a pivotal role in formalizing match play through its Society of St. Andrews Golfers, established in 1754, which adopted the 13 Articles of the Gentlemen Golfers of Leith from 1744 for club competitions. These rules codified match play elements, such as penalties resulting in loss of the hole for infractions like striking the wrong ball, establishing it as the default format for contests before stroke play gained traction in the 19th century.
A key milestone came in the 1760s when the Society, renamed the Royal and Ancient Golf Club of St. Andrews in 1834 but operating under its foundational structure since 1754, refined these regulations to govern broader club events, including the inaugural Silver Club challenge. This period solidified match play's emphasis on equitable, opponent-focused play, with rules addressing hazards and disputes tailored to hole-by-hole outcomes, ensuring the format's enduring prominence in Scottish golf traditions.

Evolution and Adoption in Modern Competitions

In the 19th and early 20th centuries, while stroke play gained prominence in major open tournamentssuch as the inaugural Open Championship in 1860, which adopted a medal play format over 36 holesmatch play continued to dominate national championships and club competitions. For instance, the United States Golf Association's inaugural U.S. Amateur Championship in 1895 employed a match play format, emphasizing head-to-head competition that rewarded strategic play over cumulative scoring. This persistence reflected match play's roots in informal Scottish club matches, where it fostered intense rivalries and accommodated varying player abilities through emerging handicap systems.
The introduction of standardized handicaps in the early 20th century further bolstered match play's appeal for amateur equity. In 1911, the USGA implemented the first nationwide handicap system, allowing players of differing skill levels to compete fairly by allocating strokes based on hole difficulty, which was particularly suited to match play's hole-by-hole nature. Professionally, match play was central to events like the PGA Championship, which used the format exclusively from its inception in 1916 until 1957, culminating in 36-hole matches that highlighted dramatic finishes. The tournament's shift to stroke play in 1958 was driven by the need to handle larger fields and align with television broadcasting demands, marking a broader trend toward stroke play in professional majors.
Match play's global adoption accelerated through international team events, beginning with the Ryder Cup in 1927, which pitted a United Kingdom team against the United States in a match play format consisting of foursomes and singles over two days. This competition, initially limited to 12 points across 36-hole matches, expanded to include Europe in 1979 and solidified match play's role in fostering national pride and bilateral rivalries. A notable professional revival occurred with the launch of the World Match Play Championship in 1964 at Wentworth Club, sponsored by Piccadilly and later Volvo, which invited top global players for knockout-style contests and ran annually until 2014, emphasizing elite head-to-head battles.
As of 2025, match play has seen a mixed trajectory in professional circuits, with discontinuations like the WGC-Dell Technologies Match Play, which concluded after its 2023 edition at Austin Country Club due to scheduling shifts and the PGA Tour's evolving calendar. However, it endures strongly in team formats, such as the biennial Ryder Cup and Presidents Cup, where match play's dramatic, spectator-friendly structure continues to drive popularity, while limited-field invitational events occasionally revive the format to highlight star matchups.

Scoring System

Standard Hole-by-Hole Scoring

In match play, scoring is conducted on a hole-by-hole basis, where the player or side that completes a hole in the fewest strokes wins that hole, while overall stroke totals across the round are irrelevant. If both competitors complete the hole in the same number of strokes, the hole is halved (tied), and neither wins it, ensuring the focus remains on relative performance per hole rather than cumulative scoring.
Progress in a match is tracked using notation that indicates the lead in holes (expressed as "up" or "down") and the number of holes remaining to play, such as "3 up with 5 to play," meaning one player leads by three holes after 10 holes completed. A match can conclude before all holes are played if one player's lead equals or exceeds the number of remaining holes, for example, a "4 and 3" result where the winner leads by four holes with three holes left, securing victory without proceeding further.
Standard matches consist of 18 holes, though shortened formats like 9-hole contests are common in casual or time-constrained play, with the overall winner determined by winning more holes than the opponentfor example, 10 holes to 8 in a full 18-hole match played to completion, assuming no halved holes. Players may concede a hole or the entire match at any point, typically verbally or by an action like picking up the ball, a practice often employed in lopsided contests to expedite play, and such concessions are irrevocable once made.

Match Outcomes and Tie Resolution

In match play, a contest concludes when one player or team secures a lead greater than the number of holes remaining, rendering further play unnecessary. For instance, in an 18-hole match, victory is achieved by winning at least 10 holes before all are completed, often expressed in the notation "X and Y," where X represents the margin of holes won and Y the holes still unplayed, such as a 3-and-2 win indicating a three-hole lead with two holes left.
If the match remains all squaretied in holes wonafter the scheduled holes, the result is typically a halved match, awarding a draw unless the competition's terms specify otherwise. In knockout formats, such as individual championships, ties are resolved through sudden-death playoffs, where play extends hole-by-hole starting from a predetermined hole until one side wins a hole, determining the victor.
Tournament-specific rules often adapt these outcomes to suit event structures. In team competitions like the Ryder Cup, halved individual matches award half a point to each team, contributing to the overall aggregate score across all matches, with no sudden-death playoffs for tied singles. For the 2025 Ryder Cup at Bethpage Black, this format persisted, meaning an overall 14-14 tie would have resulted in the defending champion (Europe) retaining the cup without a playoff, though Europe ultimately won 1513.

Handicaps

Handicap Adjustments for Equity

In match play under the World Handicap System (WHS), handicaps are adjusted to promote equity between players of differing skill levels by allowing the higher-handicap player additional strokes on specific holes. The course handicap, derived from a player's Handicap Index adjusted for the course's difficulty via its Course Rating and Slope Rating, serves as the basis for these adjustments. For individual match play, the recommended handicap allowance is 100%, meaning the playing handicap equals the full course handicap. The player with the lower playing handicap competes at scratch (zero strokes), while the opponent receives strokes equal to the difference between their playing handicaps; for example, a 10-stroke difference results in the higher-handicap player receiving 10 strokes total.
This system ensures the higher-handicap player has a fair chance to win individual holes, aligning with match play's focus on hole-by-hole competition rather than cumulative scoring. Unlike stroke play, where the full handicap is subtracted from the total score to determine net results, match play applies the stroke allowance directly to net scoring on each hole, distributing the strokes to the most difficult holes as indicated by the course's stroke index on the scorecard. This full allocation of the handicap difference based on hole difficulty maintains competitive balance without partial reductions, fostering equity in amateur and club-level matches.
Handicap adjustments apply exclusively to net scoring in matches involving players with handicaps, and they are not used in scratch competitions where all participants play at zero handicap, as is standard in professional events. Committees may further modify playing handicaps if a player's Handicap Index does not accurately reflect their current ability, but such interventions are rare and must adhere to WHS guidelines. These limitations preserve the integrity of high-level play while emphasizing the system's role in accessible, equitable amateur golf.

Stroke Allocation on Holes

In match play, handicap strokes are allocated to the player with the higher Course Handicap, with the number of strokes equal to the difference between the two players' handicaps. These strokes are assigned to the most difficult holes on the course, as determined by the official stroke index established by the course committee or governing body, where stroke index 1 designates the hardest hole and 18 the easiest. The allocation follows a structured method under the World Handicap System (WHS), dividing the 18 holes into six triads (groups of three holes) to distribute strokes evenly across the front and back nines, prioritizing the lowest indices (hardest holes) while avoiding consecutive low-index holes at the start or end of each nine for fairness in match play dynamics.
For instance, in a match between Player A with a Course Handicap of 5 and Player B with a Course Handicap of 15, the difference is 10 strokes, which Player B receives on the holes with stroke indices 1 through 10. Player B deducts one stroke from their score on each of those holes before comparing it to Player A's score to determine the hole winner, ensuring equitable competition by effectively lowering Player B's target score on the course's toughest holes. This process applies the full handicap difference for each individual match, regardless of prior results.
In multi-round events, such as knockout tournaments, the full handicap difference is recalculated and applied anew for each match based on the players' current Course Handicaps, which may incorporate adjustments for course rating and slope if playing different courses or tees. If the event spans multiple rounds on the same course, the same stroke index allocation is used consistently unless the committee specifies otherwise.
Common errors in stroke allocation include assigning strokes to easier holes (higher indices) instead of the hardest, which undermines equity, or failing to agree on the course's official stroke index before teeing off, leading to disputes. To prevent this, players should verify the stroke index on the scorecard or with the committee and mark allocations transparently during play.

Tournaments

Professional Events

Professional match play events in golf primarily feature high-stakes team competitions among top players, with the format emphasizing head-to-head battles across various match types like foursomes, four-balls, and singles. These tournaments often culminate in bracket-style knockouts or progress from group stages to finals, drawing massive television audiences and generating significant economic impact through sponsorships and viewership.
The Ryder Cup stands as the premier biennial team event, pitting 12 players from the United States against 12 from Europe in a contest for 28 points over three days at Bethpage Black in Farmingdale, New York, September 26-28, 2025. Matches include eight foursomes and eight four-ball sessions on Friday and Saturday, followed by 12 singles on Sunday, with the first team to 14.5 points claiming victory; ties result in the Cup remaining with the previous winner. Europe won the 2025 Ryder Cup 15-13. While no direct purse is awarded to teams, each U.S. player received $500,000 in 2025, with $300,000 directed to charities of their choice, creating an equivalent value exceeding $12 million across both teams when including European stipends. The 2025 edition highlighted stars like world No. 1 Scottie Scheffler (USA) and Rory McIlroy (Europe), who combined for multiple standout performances amid intense rivalry.
The Presidents Cup, held biennially in even-numbered years, mirrors the Ryder Cup's structure as a match-play showdown between a U.S. team and an International squad (excluding Europeans), awarding 30 points through foursomes, four-balls, and singles sessions at Medinah Country Club in Chicago from September 22-27, 2026. First contested in 1994, it requires 15.5 points for outright victory, with ties leading to shared honors, and features no prize money but substantial charitable contributions from proceeds. Its format fosters dramatic knockouts, similar to the Ryder Cup, and attracts top non-European talents like those from Australia and South Africa.
For women professionals, the Solheim Cup serves as the equivalent biennial team competition between the U.S. and Europe, utilizing a 28-match format identical to the Ryder Cupeight foursomes, eight four-balls over two days, and 12 singles on the final dayheld in 2024 at Robert Trent Jones Golf Club in Gainesville, Virginia, where the U.S. prevailed 15.5-12.5. The next edition is scheduled for September 11-13, 2026, at Bernardus Golf in Cromvoirt, Netherlands, continuing the event's tradition since 1990 of promoting match-play intensity among elite LPGA players. Like its counterparts, it offers no player purses but drives significant television exposure and fan engagement.
Historically, individual professional match play gained prominence through the PGA Championship, which employed a pure match-play format from 1916 to 1957, featuring qualifiers followed by bracket knockouts culminating in a 36-hole final. This era produced icons like Walter Hagen, who won 11 titles, before the tournament shifted to stroke play in 1958 to better suit television and broader appeal. More recently, the World Golf Championships-Match Play, launched in 1999 and rebranded multiple times, ran annually from 2000 to 2023 at venues like Austin Country Club, using a group-stage round-robin leading to 18-hole knockout brackets among 64 top-ranked players, with winners earning Official World Golf Ranking points and purses up to $18 million in its final year before discontinuation due to PGA Tour schedule restructuring. As of 2025, the PGA Tour features no standalone individual match-play event, leaving team formats like the Ryder Cup as the format's professional pinnacle.

Amateur and Team Competitions

Amateur match play competitions emphasize skill development and accessibility, often incorporating handicaps to level the playing field for participants of varying abilities. Major events like the U.S. Amateur Championship, organized annually by the United States Golf Association (USGA) since 1895, begin with two rounds of 36-hole stroke play qualifying, after which the top 64 players advance to single-elimination match play brackets consisting of 18-hole matches in the early rounds and 36-hole matches in the quarterfinals, semifinals, and final. Similarly, the British Amateur Championship, run by The R&A since its inception in 1885, follows a comparable structure with 36 holes of stroke play to select the top 64 for match play progression. These prestigious individual tournaments highlight emerging talent without monetary prizes, fostering a tradition of head-to-head competition.
At the state and regional levels, amateur match play events adapt the format for broader participation, frequently using net scoring based on handicaps to ensure equity. The Northern California Golf Association (NCGA) Match Play Challenge for 2025 offers both individual and four-ball divisions in a single-elimination net match play format, with registration from December 2024 to April 2025 and rounds spanning April to November across various courses. In Colorado, the Colorado Golf Association (CGA) conducts match play qualifiers in May 2025, such as the event on May 28 at Cheyenne Shadows Golf Club, to seed participants for its annual Match Play Championship. These qualifiers typically involve stroke play to determine bracket seeding, underscoring the hybrid structure common in amateur circuits.
Team competitions in match play extend the format to collective play, promoting camaraderie among national or regional squads. The Walker Cup, a biennial event since 1922 pitting the United States against Great Britain and Ireland's top male amateurs, features matches over three days totaling 26 points: eight foursomes, eight four-balls, and ten singles. The United States won the 2025 Walker Cup 17-9 at Cypress Point Club September 5-7. Its women's counterpart, the Curtis Cup, established in 1932, employs a similar setup with three foursomes and three four-balls on the first two days, followed by eight singles on the final day, totaling 20 points. Regionally, the North Dakota Golf Association (NDGA) Best Ball Match Play Championship in 2025, held August 9-10 at Rose Creek Golf Course in Fargo, includes open and net divisions for two-person teams in a best-ball match play format after qualifying rounds.
Club-level and sectional team events further integrate handicaps into match play for inclusive competition. The Wisconsin PGA's (WPGA) Four-Ball Match Play Championship runs from late April to mid-October 2025 across various facilities, allowing teams to qualify through stroke play before entering net match play brackets. This emphasis on net scoring accommodates diverse skill levels, distinguishing amateur and team match play from professional formats by prioritizing participation and development over pure elite performance.

Strategy

Offensive Tactics

In match play golf, offensive tactics emphasize proactive, risk-tolerant approaches designed to capture holes decisively rather than merely minimizing errors, contrasting with the conservative consistency often prioritized in stroke play. Players focus on generating lower scores than their opponent on each hole, which incentivizes bold decisions to achieve birdies or eagles, as a pareven a well-executed onemay result in a halved hole or loss if the adversary outperforms it. This hole-by-hole dynamic allows for greater aggression, such as attempting difficult recoveries or long approach shots, to pressure the opponent and build momentum.
Central to these tactics are risk-reward decisions, where golfers weigh the potential for superior scoring against the possibility of bogeys or worse. A classic example is attempting to reach par-5 greens in two shots, even from challenging lies, to position for eagle opportunities; while this carries hazards like water or rough, the upside of winning the hole outright can outweigh the downside in a format where only the hole outcome matters. Top instructors recommend maintaining an offensive posture by selecting aggressive lines that align with a player's strengths, such as favoring natural ball flight paths for second shots, particularly when trailing in the match. This approach transforms par-5s from conservative three-shot holes into strategic battlegrounds for separation.
Effective course management in offensive play involves positioning the ball for maximum scoring potential, such as using the driver more assertively off the tee on reachable par-5s or drivable par-4s to create eagle chances early in the round and establish a lead. On subsequent holes, players might opt for aggressive approaches to tucked pins if the tee shot sets up favorably, prioritizing birdie hunts over safe pars to force concessions or errors from the opponent. Tiger Woods exemplified this mindset during the 2019 WGC-Dell Technologies Match Play, stating the need to "put the hammer down and be aggressive" to overpower competitors, adjusting based on the match's flow rather than rigid conservatism.
Such tactics are most advantageously applied early in matches to seize psychological advantages through quick leads, or when behind to mount comebacks via calculated risks on scoring holes. Phil Mickelson's high-risk style in Ryder Cup match play, where he consistently attacks pins and pursues birdies on par-5s, illustrates this application, as seen in his aggressive partnerships that aimed to dominate individual holes through bold shot-making. However, over-aggression is tempered late in the match when leading, to preserve the advantage without unnecessary gambles.

Defensive and Psychological Approaches

In match play, defensive strategies emphasize conservative shot selection to safeguard a lead, particularly on challenging holes where aggressive plays could lead to bogeys or worse. When ahead, players often lay up short of hazards to guarantee pars, prioritizing error avoidance over birdie pursuits that might jeopardize the advantage. This approach shifts focus to steady course management, such as aiming for fairways and larger green targets, allowing the leader to halve holes or secure wins without unnecessary risks.
Psychological tactics play a crucial role in disrupting opponents and maintaining mental control. Leaders can set the match's pace by winning early holes, forcing the trailing player to react rather than dictate play, as professional Jon Rahm has described: "In match play, its important to take the lead and be the one who sets the pace so you dont have to react to what he is doing." Conceding short putts strategically speeds up the game, preserves momentum, and applies subtle pressure by denying the opponent chances to build confidence on the greens. Silence or deliberate pacing can further unsettle aggressive foes, while adaptabilitysuch as exploiting an opponent's weaknessesenhances the defensive edge without direct confrontation.
Managing a multi-hole lead involves routine, low-risk shots to close out the match efficiently, avoiding overconfidence that invites comebacks. Trailing players, desperate to close the gap, may attempt bolder shots, creating opportunities for the leader to capitalize on induced errors through patient positioning. In team formats like the Ryder Cup, pairings are selected to bolster mental resilience, pairing complementary styles that foster mutual support and quicker recovery from setbacks, as evidenced by Europe's 2025 victory (1513), where captain Luke Donald highlighted his squads ability to keep their nerve under huge pressure.
A classic example of methodical defensive play is Jack Nicklaus' approach in the 1970s, where his conservative course management and focus on par preservation helped secure leads in high-stakes matches, including Ryder Cup contributions that emphasized smart, unhurried decisions over flashy risks.

Variations

Individual Alternative Formats

In match play, several alternative formats adapt the core head-to-head competition for individual players, often incorporating elements of stroke play, multi-player scoring, or escalating stakes to enhance excitement while maintaining a focus on hole-by-hole outcomes. These variations allow solo competitors to engage in non-traditional matchups, such as against multiple opponents or through hybrid scoring systems, differing from standard pairwise contests by emphasizing cumulative points, monetary incentives, or knockout structures based on total strokes.
The skins game modifies match play by assigning a fixed value, typically monetary, to each hole as a "skin," with the player achieving the lowest score on a hole claiming that skin; if all players tie, the skin carries over to the next hole, potentially accumulating multiple skins and increasing the value until a winner emerges. This format promotes aggressive play due to the escalating stakes and has been a staple in casual betting games for decades, gaining widespread popularity through the professional exhibition event known as the Skins Game, which ran annually from 1983 to 2008 and featured top PGA Tour players competing for large purses. The format saw a relaunch in 2024 as an unofficial PGA Tour event in partnership with Pro Shop and Propagate Content.
Medal match play integrates stroke play scoring into a match play bracket, where competitors post total medal (stroke) scores over fixed segments, such as nine holes, to determine advancement in knockout rounds rather than relying solely on holes won. A prominent example was the Belgian Knockout on the DP World Tour, held in 2018 and 2019 as a hybrid event where the initial two days consisted of 36 holes of stroke play to qualify and seed the top 64 players, followed by nine-hole medal match play knockouts that culminated in a final; since 2020, the event (now the Soudal Open) has been contested in standard 72-hole stroke play. This format revived elements of the historic Belgian Open, first held in 1910 and contested annually in various iterations since. The structure ensured no lead was insurmountable, as every matchup resolved via aggregate strokes, blending endurance from stroke play with the intensity of elimination.
Irish match play extends individual competition to three or more players by awarding points based on relative performance per hole: a player earns two points for the outright lowest score (beating all others) or one point for each opponent beaten in case of ties for the lead. This point-accumulation system allows solo players to compete simultaneously against the field without pairwise pairings, fostering rivalries across the group and rewarding consistency over the round; it is particularly suited to casual settings among friends, though less formalized than standard match play.
Rowan match play provides a dynamic singles format for three or more individual players, starting with all competitors vying on each hole until one secures an outright win, after which that victor faces the remaining players sequentially in one-on-one sub-matches until another outright winner emerges, repeating the process to progressively eliminate or re-challenge participants. This rotational elimination structure adapts match play for odd-sized groups, ensuring continuous individual competition without fixed teams, and is historically noted for its use in informal or historical club events rather than major tournaments.

Team-Based Match Play Forms

Team-based match play formats in golf involve multiple players competing as a side against another side, with scoring determined by the collective performance of the team on each hole or over the match. These formats emphasize collaboration and strategic pairing, often adjusting handicaps to ensure equity between teams. Common in professional and amateur competitions, they differ from individual play by integrating partners' shots or scores into a unified team outcome.
Foursomes, also known as alternate shot, is a pairs format where two partners on each team share a single ball, alternating strokes throughout the round. One player typically tees off on odd-numbered holes and the other on even-numbered holes, fostering reliance on complementary strengths in driving, approach, and putting. The team's score per hole is the total strokes taken with that ball, and the side with the lower score wins the hole in match play. This format is prevalent in major events like the Ryder Cup, where it accounts for half the sessions. For handicap adjustments, each partner's course handicap is reduced to 50% and combined to form the team's handicap; the difference between teams' combined handicaps is then halved to determine stroke concessions.
Fourball, often called better ball, features two partners per team, each playing their own ball from tee to green on every hole. The team's score for each hole is the lower of the two partners' scores, rewarding the stronger performance while allowing recovery opportunities if one player struggles. In match play, the team with the better (lower) team score wins the hole; if tied, the other partner may complete their hole for potential future impact, but the tie stands. This format is a staple in competitions such as the Presidents Cup, promoting aggressive play and individual accountability within the team. Handicap allowances are calculated by first adjusting all four players' course handicaps relative to the lowest among them (setting it to zero), then applying 90% to each adjusted value; the resulting difference between teams dictates strokes received by the higher-handicap side.
Best-ball match play extends the fourball concept to larger teams, typically of three or four players, where each player plays their own ball on every hole, and the team's score for the hole is the lowest individual score among team members. This format rewards the strongest performances while providing multiple scoring opportunities and is used in amateur team events like portions of the Walker Cup for balanced involvement. Handicap adjustments are applied based on the team's overall handicap, often using 100% of each player's course handicap with strokes allocated to the higher-handicap team on the most difficult holes.
Aggregate team play compiles scores from multiple individual or pairs matches into a cumulative total, determining the overall winner by the team with the most holes or points won across sessions, often spanning days. This rare format in pure match play emphasizes endurance and depth, as seen in select national championships where teams accumulate results from successive pairings. Handicap concessions are applied per underlying match (e.g., via foursomes or fourball rules), with the aggregate outcome reflecting net team equity without additional overarching adjustments.
