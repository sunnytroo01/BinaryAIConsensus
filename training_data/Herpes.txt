 Herpes, also known as herpes simplex, is a common and contagious viral infection caused by the herpes simplex virus (HSV), a member of the Herpesviridae family, which primarily affects humans. The virus exists in two main types: HSV-1, which typically causes oral herpes (cold sores around the mouth), and HSV-2, which is more commonly associated with genital herpes, though either type can infect either site. Transmission occurs mainly through direct skin-to-skin contact, including oral-to-oral, genital-to-genital, or oral-to-genital contact, often during asymptomatic shedding when no visible sores are present. Once contracted, HSV establishes lifelong latency in sensory nerve ganglia, periodically reactivating to cause recurrent outbreaks of painful blisters or ulcers filled with fluid, which eventually crust over and heal without scarring.
Globally, HSV infections are highly prevalent, with an estimated 3.8 billion people under the age of 5064% of the world's population in that age groupinfected with HSV-1 as of 2020 estimates. For HSV-2, around 520 million people aged 1549 years, or 13% worldwide, are affected, making it a leading cause of genital ulcer disease. Many infections remain asymptomatic or unrecognized, but initial outbreaks can include flu-like symptoms such as fever, swollen lymph nodes, and body aches, particularly in primary infections. Complications, though rare in healthy individuals, may include neonatal herpes in newborns exposed during birth, which has a high mortality rate if untreated, or ocular herpes leading to vision impairment.
There is no cure for herpes, as the virus persists lifelong, but antiviral medications like acyclovir, valacyclovir, and famciclovir can shorten outbreak duration, reduce severity, and lower transmission risk when taken daily as suppressive therapy. Prevention strategies emphasize consistent condom use, avoiding sexual contact during outbreaks, and limiting partners, though these do not fully eliminate risk due to asymptomatic shedding. Ongoing research focuses on developing vaccines and improved therapies to address the public health burden of HSV infections.

Background

Etymology

The term "herpes" originates from the Ancient Greek word herps (), derived from the verb herpein (), meaning "to creep" or "to crawl," which alluded to the spreading or serpentine progression of the characteristic skin lesions observed in affected individuals.
This nomenclature first appeared in medical literature around 400 BCE, when the Greek physician Hippocrates described "herpes" in his Corpus Hippocraticum to denote creeping cutaneous eruptions, such as those spreading from the groin toward the flank, distinguishing them from other ulcerative conditions based on their migratory pattern.
By the 18th and 19th centuries, medical terminology evolved, leading to the adoption of "herpes simplex," with simplex from Latin meaning "simple" or "uncomplicated," referring to the more straightforward creeping rash.
In the 20th century, as virological research advanced, colloquial English terms emerged tied to the identification of herpes simplex virus types: "cold sores" or "fever blisters" for recurrent oral lesions primarily caused by HSV-1, and "genital herpes" for anogenital infections mainly associated with HSV-2, with these distinctions formalized following the serological differentiation of the virus types in the 1960s.

History

The evolutionary origins of herpes simplex virus type 1 (HSV-1) trace back approximately 5,000 years to the Bronze Age, when a major strain likely emerged and spread across Eurasia, possibly facilitated by the cultural practice of romantic kissing that originated in the region and disseminated westward with migrations.
In the 18th century, French physician Jean Astruc provided the first detailed clinical description of genital herpes, termed "venereal herpes," in his 1736 treatise De Morbis Venereis, distinguishing it as a distinct venereal disease.
The 20th century marked significant virological progress; in 1919, researcher Carl Lowenstein isolated HSV from human vesicular fluid by serial passage in rabbit corneas, confirming its viral etiology and transmissibility. In the 1940s, T. F. McNair Scott and colleagues demonstrated latent infection in rabbit trigeminal ganglia, showing that the virus could persist without symptoms after initial replication, a key insight into its recurrent nature. Differentiation of HSV-1 (primarily oral) from HSV-2 (primarily genital) occurred in the 1960s through serological assays and biological properties, such as antigenicity and host range, enabling targeted diagnostics.
A pivotal therapeutic milestone came in the 1970s with the development of acyclovir by Gertrude B. Elion and colleagues at Burroughs Wellcome, the first antiviral selectively inhibiting HSV DNA polymerase, revolutionizing treatment and earning Elion the 1988 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine. From the 1980s onward, the World Health Organization increasingly recognized herpesviruses as a major global health concern, particularly due to their role in facilitating HIV transmission and the rising prevalence of genital infections amid the AIDS epidemic.
Recent ancient DNA analyses, including a 2022 study sequencing HSV-1 genomes from medieval European dental pulp, have corroborated the Bronze Age origins of modern strains, revealing genetic continuity from prehistoric Europe and underscoring the virus's deep co-evolution with human populations.

Overview and Classification

Definition and Types

Herpes simplex refers to infections caused by the herpes simplex viruses (HSV-1 and HSV-2), members of the Herpesviridae familya group of enveloped, double-stranded DNA viruses that establish lifelong latent infections in host cells after primary infection. These viruses are characterized by their ability to remain dormant in the body for extended periods, reactivating under certain conditions such as stress or immunosuppression.
The Herpesviridae family is divided into three subfamiliesAlphaherpesvirinae, Betaherpesvirinae, and Gammaherpesvirinaebased on differences in genomic structure, replication strategies, and tissue tropism. Alphaherpesviruses, including HSV-1, HSV-2, and varicella-zoster virus (VZV), are neurotropic and establish latency primarily in sensory neurons. Betaherpesviruses, such as cytomegalovirus (CMV), human herpesvirus 6 (HHV-6), and HHV-7, exhibit systemic infection patterns and latency in a variety of cell types, including monocytes and epithelial cells. Gammaherpesviruses, comprising Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) and HHV-8, are lymphotropic, targeting B lymphocytes and other immune cells for latency.
Eight distinct herpesviruses infect humans, known as human herpesviruses 1 through 8 (HHV-1 to HHV-8). HHV-1 (HSV-1) typically causes oral herpes, manifesting as cold sores, while HHV-2 (HSV-2) is primarily responsible for genital herpes, presenting with sores in the genital area. HSV-1 and HSV-2 represent the most common causes of clinically symptomatic herpes infections globally.

Viral Structure

Herpesviruses possess linear double-stranded DNA genomes ranging from 120 to 240 kilobase pairs (kbp) in length, which encode approximately 70 to 100 genes depending on the specific virus. These genomes are characterized by a bipartite organization consisting of a unique long (U_L) region and a unique short (U_S) region, with the U_S region flanked by large inverted repeats (TR_S and IR_S) that permit isomerization through inversion of the U_S segment, resulting in four possible genomic isomers. In contrast, genomes of gammaherpesviruses like Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) lack these inversions and instead feature a single unique sequence with internal direct repeats and terminal direct repeats at the ends. For example, the herpes simplex virus (HSV) genome is approximately 152 kbp, while the EBV genome is larger at about 172 kbp, highlighting size variations across subfamilies.
The mature herpesvirus virion is enveloped and consists of a nucleocapsid core surrounded by a tegument layer and an outer lipid envelope derived from host cell membranes. The nucleocapsid features an icosahedral capsid approximately 125-130 nm in diameter with T=16 symmetry, composed of four major structural proteins including VP5 (major capsomere protein), VP19C, VP23, and VP26 in alphaherpesviruses like HSV-1. The tegument, a protein-rich matrix comprising up to 40% of the virion's mass, contains over 20 virally encoded proteins that are asymmetrically distributed and essential for early stages of infection. The outermost lipid envelope, studded with glycoproteins, has a total virion diameter of about 220 nm and includes key entry mediators such as glycoprotein B (gB) for membrane fusion, gC for initial attachment, gD for receptor binding, and the gH/gL complex for fusion execution.
Among the virally encoded proteins, immediate-early regulators play a critical role in gene expression control; in HSV, these include ICP0 (a transactivator that promotes viral gene transcription), ICP4 (a major transcriptional regulator), and ICP27 (involved in RNA processing and export). For DNA replication, the virus relies on enzymes such as the UL5-encoded helicase subunit of the helicase-primase complex, which unwinds the DNA double helix during genome synthesis. The enveloped structure of the virion contributes to the virus's ability to establish lifelong latent infections, particularly in neuronal cells for alphaherpesviruses like HSV.

Clinical Manifestations

Signs and Symptoms

Herpes simplex virus (HSV) infections typically manifest after an incubation period of 2 to 12 days following exposure. Primary infections often present with systemic symptoms including fever, malaise, and regional lymphadenopathy, which can be particularly pronounced in first-time episodes.
The hallmark lesions of HSV infections are painful, clustered vesicles that arise on an erythematous base, progressing to pustules, erosions, and shallow ulcers before crusting and healing over 2 to 6 weeks. These lesions are highly contagious during the vesicular and ulcerative stages. For HSV-1, primary oral infections commonly cause gingivostomatitis, characterized by widespread intraoral ulcers and painful cold sores on the lips or perioral area. In contrast, HSV-2 primarily affects the genital region, leading to vulvovaginitis in women and penile ulcers in men, often accompanied by dysuria and local tenderness.
Many HSV infections (70-90%) remain asymptomatic throughout their course, though subclinical viral shedding occurs frequently, facilitating transmission without visible lesions. A prodromal phase, marked by tingling, burning, or itching at the site, may precede outbreaks by hours to days in symptomatic individuals. Rarely, HSV can cause systemic complications such as encephalitis or meningitis, presenting with severe headache, fever, altered mental status, and seizures.

Specific Infections

Herpetic whitlow is an HSV infection of the fingers or thumbs, often resulting from autoinoculation or direct contact with infected saliva or genital secretions. It presents as painful vesicles or pustules on the distal digit, accompanied by swelling, erythema, and tenderness, typically resolving in 2-4 weeks but prone to recurrence.
Herpes gladiatorum, also known as wrestlers' herpes, is a cutaneous HSV-1 infection acquired through skin-to-skin contact in contact sports. It manifests as clustered vesicles and ulcers on the head, neck, or trunk, often with fever and lymphadenopathy in primary cases, and is highly contagious during outbreaks.
Eczema herpeticum is a disseminated HSV infection in individuals with atopic dermatitis or other skin barrier defects, leading to widespread, painful erosions and vesicles that can become secondarily bacterially infected. It requires prompt antiviral treatment to prevent complications like sepsis or scarring.
Ocular herpes due to herpes simplex virus (HSV) manifests as keratitis, the most common corneal infection in developed countries, typically affecting the epithelium or stroma and presenting with acute eye pain, photophobia, tearing, blurred vision, and conjunctival injection. Epithelial keratitis involves dendritic ulcers that can progress to stromal involvement with corneal edema, neovascularization, and scarring, potentially causing permanent vision loss if recurrent or untreated; HSV-1 is responsible for 95% of cases, often triggered by prior oral herpes exposure.

Associated Conditions

Herpes simplex virus type 1 (HSV-1) reactivation in the geniculate ganglion of the facial nerve has been implicated as a cause of Bell's palsy, leading to acute unilateral facial paralysis. Studies have detected HSV-1 DNA in endoneural fluid from up to 79% of Bell's palsy patients during surgical decompression, supporting viral reactivation as an etiologic factor in approximately 30-50% of cases.
HSV-1 infection has been associated with an increased risk of Alzheimer's disease, with HSV-1 DNA frequently detected in amyloid plaques within the brains of affected individuals. Meta-analyses and prospective cohort studies indicate that seropositivity to HSV-1 approximately doubles the risk of developing dementia in elderly populations, particularly when combined with genetic factors like the APOE-4 allele.
Neonatal herpes, primarily caused by vertical transmission of HSV-2 from mother to infant during delivery, can result in severe central nervous system involvement such as encephalitis. Untreated neonatal HSV encephalitis carries a mortality rate of about 50%, with survivors often experiencing profound neurological impairments.

Pathophysiology

Infection and Latency

Herpes simplex virus (HSV), primarily HSV-1 and HSV-2, initiates infection by targeting mucosal surfaces or abraded skin, where viral envelope glycoproteins facilitate entry into host cells. The process involves glycoprotein D (gD) binding to cellular receptors such as nectin-1 or herpesvirus entry mediator (HVEM), triggering a cascade that activates glycoproteins B (gB), H (gH), and L (gL) to mediate fusion of the viral envelope with the host cell membrane. This pH-independent fusion allows the viral capsid to be released into the cytoplasm, marking the establishment of primary infection.
Following entry at peripheral sites, HSV spreads via retrograde axonal transport along sensory neurons to establish latency in the corresponding ganglia. For HSV-1, typically infecting oral mucosa, the virus travels to the trigeminal ganglion, while HSV-2, associated with genital infection, targets the sacral ganglia. This transport occurs within microtubules, powered by dynein motors, delivering the capsid to the neuronal nucleus over hours to days. During primary infection, any potential viremia is rapidly contained by innate immune responses, including type I interferons and natural killer cells, limiting systemic dissemination and favoring neuronal tropism.
In the neuronal nucleus, the viral genome establishes latency as an episomal, circular DNA molecule that does not integrate into the host genome, ceasing lytic replication to evade detection. Host chromatin remodeling factors and viral latency-associated transcripts (LATs), such as those from HSV, promote epigenetic silencing of lytic genes through histone modifications and non-coding RNAs, maintaining the genome in a quiescent state. This neuronal reservoir ensures lifelong persistence, as neurons exhibit low or absent major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class I expression, rendering them resistant to cytotoxic T-cell surveillance and allowing the latent virus to persist without triggering adaptive immunity. In contrast to recurrent episodes driven by reactivation, primary infection resolves with this dormant phase, distinguishing the initial limited viral spread from later asymptomatic shedding.

Reactivation Mechanisms

Reactivation of herpes simplex virus (HSV) from latency occurs primarily in sensory ganglia, where the virus resides in a quiescent state following initial infection. Various environmental and physiological triggers can initiate this process by disrupting the balance between viral latency and lytic replication. Common triggers include ultraviolet (UV) light exposure, which induces reactivation in skin and mucosal sites, particularly for HSV-1 in trigeminal or sacral ganglia. Psychological and physical stress activates the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis, leading to glucocorticoid release that promotes viral gene expression and reactivation. Immunosuppression, such as in transplant recipients or those with HIV, diminishes host defenses and increases reactivation frequency. Hormonal fluctuations, including those during menstruation or pregnancy, can also precipitate outbreaks by influencing immune modulation and viral transcription.
At the molecular level, reactivation involves derepression of the latent viral genome through chromatin remodeling. During latency, the HSV genome is associated with repressive histone marks like H3K9me3, which silence viral genes; triggers lead to histone modifications, such as phosphorylation at H3S10, and eviction of insulator proteins like CTCF, allowing access to viral promoters. The JNK signaling pathway plays a critical role, activated by upstream kinases like DLK in response to nerve growth factor deprivation or stress, culminating in the expression of immediate-early genes. A key event is the activation of the viral transactivator VP16 (also known as -TIF), which forms a complex with host factors Oct-1 and HCF-1 to drive immediate-early gene transcription, marking the transition to lytic replication.
Following reactivation, virus travels anterograde along axons to epithelial sites, resulting in shedding. For HSV-2, asymptomatic shedding occurs on approximately 10-20% of days in infected individuals, facilitating transmission without overt symptoms. HSV evades immune detection during this phase through reduced surveillance by CD8+ T cells, which normally patrol latently infected neurons to prevent reactivation; waning or exhausted CD8+ responses allow viral escape and recurrence.

Diagnosis

Clinical Evaluation

The clinical evaluation of suspected herpes infections begins with a detailed patient history to identify risk factors and patterns suggestive of herpes simplex virus (HSV) involvement. Prodromal symptoms, such as localized pain, tingling, or burning at the site of future lesions, often precede outbreaks by hours to days in genital HSV, aiding in early suspicion. A thorough sexual and exposure history is essential for anogenital HSV, including recent unprotected sexual contact, partner's herpes status, and timing relative to pregnancy. Recurrence frequency is assessed to differentiate primary from recurrent episodes, as HSV-2 recurrences are more common (typically 4-6 per year initially) than HSV-1, influencing management decisions.
Physical examination focuses on lesion characteristics and distribution to support clinical suspicion. Typical HSV lesions present as clustered vesicles or shallow ulcers on an erythematous base, often painful and self-limited. Location is key: orolabial for HSV-1, anogenital for HSV-2. A bedside Tzanck smear, prepared by scraping the lesion base and staining, can reveal multinucleated giant cells, providing rapid presumptive evidence of herpetic infection, though it lacks viral specificity.
Severity is graded based on extent and patient factors: mild cases involve localized skin lesions without systemic involvement; moderate cases include regional lymphadenopathy or systemic symptoms like fever; severe cases feature dissemination in immunocompromised individuals, with risks of visceral or neurological complications requiring urgent intervention.
In special cases, evaluation adapts to vulnerable populations. For neonatal HSV, assessment distinguishes skin, eye, and mouth (SEM) diseasemanifesting as vesicular skin lesions or conjunctivitis in 45% of casesfrom central nervous system (CNS) involvement (30%, with seizures or lethargy) or disseminated disease (25%, with multiorgan failure like hepatomegaly), typically presenting within the first 3 weeks of life. Ocular herpes requires slit-lamp examination to detect pathognomonic dendritic ulcersbranching, fluorescein-staining epithelial defectsalong with reduced corneal sensation and anterior chamber inflammation, often in a unilateral pattern.

Laboratory Testing

Laboratory testing plays a crucial role in confirming herpes simplex virus (HSV) infections, particularly when clinical suspicion arises from active lesions or systemic involvement. These tests detect either the virus directly or the host immune response, with methods varying in sensitivity, specificity, and turnaround time depending on the sample type and clinical context. Viral culture, polymerase chain reaction (PCR), serology, direct fluorescent antibody (DFA) staining, and biopsy represent established approaches, while recent advances include multiplex PCR panels for broader herpesvirus detection.
Viral culture remains the traditional gold standard for diagnosing active HSV lesions, involving the inoculation of lesion swabs onto cell lines to observe cytopathic effects. It offers high specificity but moderate sensitivity of 70-90% for vesicular or ulcerative lesions, decreasing significantly as lesions heal or in recurrent episodes due to intermittent viral shedding. This method is particularly useful for confirming HSV in neonatal infections or when antiviral susceptibility testing is needed.
PCR, a nucleic acid amplification test, has become the preferred method for its superior sensitivity and ability to detect low viral loads, quantifying DNA from HSV-1 or HSV-2 in samples like swabs, blood, or cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). For HSV encephalitis, PCR on CSF demonstrates 95% sensitivity and near-100% specificity, making it the diagnostic standard over culture, which has less than 10% sensitivity in such cases. Real-time PCR variants allow rapid results within hours and subtyping to distinguish HSV-1 from HSV-2.
Serologic testing detects antibodies against HSV, with IgM indicating acute infection and IgG signifying past exposure or latency. Type-specific assays, such as glycoprotein G-based enzyme immunoassays or the gold standard Western blot, differentiate HSV-1 from HSV-2 with sensitivities of 90-98% and specificities of 93-100% after seroconversion, which may take 2-12 weeks post-infection. These tests are valuable for asymptomatic individuals or when lesions are absent but exposure history suggests infection.
Other rapid methods include DFA immunofluorescence, which uses monoclonal antibodies to detect HSV antigens in lesion scrapings at the bedside, offering 70-90% sensitivity and 90-100% specificity with results in 1-2 hours, though it requires confirmatory culture for negatives. For atypical or visceral presentations, such as esophagitis or encephalitis, tissue biopsy with histopathology or immunohistochemistry can confirm HSV involvement, showing characteristic multinucleated giant cells with 80-90% sensitivity in fresh specimens.
Advances in 2025 include multiplex PCR panels that simultaneously detect multiple herpesviruses (e.g., HSV, cytomegalovirus) in CSF or mucosal samples, enhancing diagnostic efficiency for central nervous system infections with overall sensitivities exceeding 90% for targeted viruses. These panels facilitate rapid syndromic testing in clinical settings like encephalitis or uveitis. Ongoing research also focuses on improving point-of-care diagnostic assays for direct HSV detection to enable faster, accessible testing in outpatient and resource-limited settings.

Differential Diagnosis

The differential diagnosis of herpes simplex virus (HSV) infections encompasses various conditions that present with similar mucocutaneous or neurological manifestations, requiring careful clinical evaluation to distinguish them based on lesion characteristics, distribution, and associated symptoms.
In cases of suspected genital herpes, primary syphilis must be differentiated, as the syphilitic chancre typically appears as a single, painless, indurated ulcer with clean borders, often accompanied by regional lymphadenopathy but without the vesicular prodrome or multiple lesions seen in HSV. Chancroid, caused by Haemophilus ducreyi, presents with painful, ragged-edged ulcers that may have purulent exudate and prominent inguinal buboes, contrasting with the grouped, painful vesicles or shallow ulcers of genital HSV that evolve from a vesicular stage. Aphthous ulcers, while more common orally, can occasionally mimic genital lesions as shallow, painful erosions without vesicles or viral prodrome, but they lack the clustered distribution and recurrent nature typical of HSV and are not sexually transmitted.
For oral herpes, aphthous stomatitis is a key mimic, featuring recurrent, painful, round ulcers on non-keratinized mucosa without preceding vesicles or an erythematous base, and it is self-limiting without viral shedding. Hand-foot-and-mouth disease, caused by coxsackievirus, produces small vesicles or ulcers in the anterior mouth along with a characteristic maculopapular or vesicular rash on the hands and feet, particularly in children, differing from the localized perioral or intraoral grouped vesicles of HSV-1.
Skin rashes resembling herpes may include impetigo, a bacterial infection often due to Staphylococcus aureus or Streptococcus pyogenes, which forms honey-crusted erosions without the initial vesicular phase or clustering of HSV lesions. Contact dermatitis typically manifests as pruritic, erythematous patches or plaques in areas of allergen exposure, lacking vesicles and showing improvement with avoidance rather than the painful, grouped vesicles on an erythematous base characteristic of herpes.
Neurological presentations of HSV, such as meningitis, overlap with other causes of aseptic meningitis or facial nerve involvement, but are distinguished by positive CSF PCR for HSV and the presence of mucocutaneous lesions. Lyme disease, caused by Borrelia burgdorferi from tick bites, can cause bilateral or unilateral facial palsy with possible systemic symptoms like fever or rash (erythema migrans), but lacks vesicular lesions and is linked to endemic exposure rather than HSV's viral markers.
Key differentiators for HSV include the classic appearance of grouped vesicles on an erythematous base that progress to shallow ulcers, often with prodromal tingling or pain, and confirmation via positive polymerase chain reaction (PCR) or viral culture from lesion swabs, which are highly specific for HSV while negative in mimics.

Prevention

Vaccination and Prophylaxis

No vaccines are currently approved for herpes simplex virus (HSV-1 or HSV-2), despite ongoing research into prophylactic and therapeutic candidates to address the high global burden of genital and oral herpes. Promising developments include Moderna's mRNA-1608, a trivalent mRNA-based therapeutic vaccine targeting HSV-2 glycoproteins, which completed its Phase 1/2 trial in 2025 to evaluate safety, immunogenicity, and proof-of-concept efficacy in adults with recurrent genital herpes; it has advanced to Phase 2 as of November 2025, with interim safety data presented in October 2025. Another candidate, RVx-201 from Rational Vaccines, is a live-attenuated HSV-2 vaccine designed to reduce symptomatic recurrences through targeted mutations in the ICP0 protein; it has advanced to observational clinical studies in the UK under an Innovation Passport designation, with preclinical data showing significant reduction in lesion days in animal models.
Prophylactic antiviral therapy serves as a key non-vaccine strategy to mitigate HSV transmission and asymptomatic shedding in infected individuals. Daily suppressive therapy with valacyclovir at 500 mg reduces HSV-2 genital shedding by approximately 50% compared to placebo, as demonstrated in randomized controlled trials measuring viral detection via PCR. In discordant heterosexual couples, this regimen further decreases the risk of HSV-2 transmission to uninfected partners by 48%, highlighting its role in preventing spread alongside behavioral measures like condom use.

Behavioral and Barrier Methods

Behavioral and barrier methods play a crucial role in reducing the transmission of herpes simplex virus (HSV), particularly HSV-2, which primarily causes genital herpes, and HSV-1, which can cause oral or genital infections. These non-pharmacological strategies emphasize avoiding direct contact with infected areas and promoting safe practices during sexual activity or close personal interactions. While no method guarantees complete prevention, consistent application can significantly lower risk.
Consistent and correct use of male latex condoms during vaginal or anal sex reduces the risk of HSV-2 acquisition by approximately 30% compared to non-use. For oral-genital contact, dental damsthin latex or polyurethane sheets placed over the genital areaprovide a barrier to prevent transmission of HSV from the mouth to genitals or vice versa. These barriers are most effective when used every time and cover all potentially infected skin, though they do not protect uncovered areas.
Abstaining from sexual activity, including oral sex, during active outbreaks or prodromal symptoms (such as tingling or itching) is a key recommendation to avoid transmission, as viral shedding is highest during these periods. Individuals should also refrain from skin-to-skin contact with lesions until they have fully healed.
In serodiscordant couples, where one partner is HSV-positive and the other is negative, open disclosure of status facilitates informed decision-making and mutual testing to confirm serostatus. Combining behavioral strategies like condom use with suppressive antiviral therapy for the infected partner further reduces transmission risk by about 48% overall.
General hygiene practices help prevent HSV-1 transmission, which often occurs through oral contact. Thorough handwashing after touching sores or potentially contaminated surfaces minimizes spread, and avoiding sharing personal items like razors, utensils, or towels that may contact saliva reduces the chance of indirect transmission.
Public health education is essential, as most HSV transmissionsestimated at 70-80%occur during asymptomatic shedding when no visible symptoms are present. Awareness campaigns emphasize that consistent barrier use and communication are vital even in the absence of outbreaks, promoting broader adoption of these methods to curb community spread.

Management in Pregnancy

Management of herpes simplex virus (HSV) infection during pregnancy prioritizes preventing neonatal transmission, which occurs primarily during vaginal delivery when maternal lesions are present. Guidelines from major health organizations recommend targeted approaches rather than universal screening or intervention, focusing on women with known HSV history or symptoms to balance maternal and fetal risks.
Routine serologic screening for HSV is not recommended for asymptomatic pregnant individuals, as it does not improve outcomes and may lead to unnecessary anxiety or interventions. However, third-trimester type-specific HSV serology is advised for those with a personal history of genital herpes, recurrent outbreaks, or exposure to an infected partner, allowing for informed counseling on transmission risks. For women identified with HSV-2 infection, suppressive antiviral therapy with acyclovir (400 mg orally three times daily) initiated at 36 weeks of gestation significantly reduces clinical recurrences at term and the subsequent need for cesarean delivery by approximately 75%. This approach is safe, with no increased risk of birth defects observed in large registries, and is particularly beneficial for those with prior outbreaks.
Delivery mode decisions are guided by the presence of active genital lesions or prodromal symptoms at labor onset. Vaginal delivery is preferred for women with a history of recurrent HSV but no active lesions, as the transmission risk is low (less than 1%). In contrast, cesarean section is recommended for those with active primary or recurrent outbreaks, reducing neonatal HSV transmission risk by about 80% compared to vaginal birth, especially when membranes have been ruptured for less than 4-6 hours.
Neonates exposed to maternal HSV at birth, particularly from primary maternal infection, require prompt evaluation regardless of visible lesions on the infant. High-dose intravenous acyclovir (20 mg/kg every 8 hours for 14-21 days, depending on disease extent) is standard treatment for suspected or confirmed neonatal HSV, dramatically improving survival; untreated disseminated or central nervous system disease carries a mortality rate of up to 50%. Long-term suppressive oral acyclovir is recommended post-discharge for survivors to prevent recurrence.
As of 2025, World Health Organization guidelines underscore the importance of type-specific IgG testing in pregnancy for precise HSV-1 and HSV-2 differentiation, aiding in risk stratification and management decisions for at-risk women.

Treatment

Antiviral Medications

Antiviral medications for herpes simplex virus (HSV) infections primarily consist of nucleoside analogs, which are the cornerstone of systemic therapy for both HSV-1 and HSV-2. These agents, including acyclovir, valacyclovir, and famciclovir, target active viral replication and are administered orally or intravenously depending on the severity of the infection.
The mechanism of action for these nucleoside analogs involves selective activation within infected cells. Acyclovir, for instance, is phosphorylated initially by the viral thymidine kinase to form acyclovir monophosphate, which is then further phosphorylated by cellular kinases to acyclovir triphosphate. This active form competitively inhibits the viral DNA polymerase, preventing chain elongation during viral DNA synthesis and incorporating into the growing DNA chain to cause termination. Valacyclovir and famciclovir function similarly after conversion to their active forms (acyclovir and penciclovir, respectively), with valacyclovir offering improved oral bioavailability due to its prodrug nature, allowing less frequent dosing.
Indications for these medications include treatment of initial episodes, recurrent outbreaks, and suppressive therapy to reduce frequency and asymptomatic shedding. For initial genital herpes, standard regimens are acyclovir 400 mg orally three times daily for 7-10 days, valacyclovir 1 g orally twice daily for 7-10 days, or famciclovir 250 mg orally three times daily for 7-10 days. Recurrent episodes are typically treated with shorter courses: acyclovir 400 mg three times daily for 3-5 days, valacyclovir 500 mg twice daily for 3 days, or famciclovir 125 mg twice daily for 5 days. Suppressive therapy, often for 6-12 months or longer in frequent recurrences, uses acyclovir 400 mg twice daily, valacyclovir 500 mg once or twice daily, or famciclovir 250 mg twice daily to decrease outbreak frequency by 70-80% and transmission risk.
Resistance to nucleoside analogs occurs rarely in immunocompetent individuals but affects approximately 5% of immunocompromised patients, primarily due to mutations in the viral thymidine kinase gene that impair drug phosphorylation. In such cases, alternative agents like foscarnet, a pyrophosphate analog that directly inhibits viral DNA polymerase without requiring phosphorylation, are used, typically at doses of 40-60 mg/kg intravenously every 8 hours, though with risks of nephrotoxicity. Pritelivir, a helicase-primase inhibitor, has shown superior efficacy to foscarnet in a Phase 3 trial for acyclovir-resistant HSV in immunocompromised patients, with topline results reported in October 2025, and is under consideration for regulatory approval.
As of 2025, emerging therapies like ABI-5366, a helicase-primase inhibitor developed by Assembly Biosciences, show promise for more convenient dosing. Phase 1b trial results demonstrated a 94% reduction in HSV-2 shedding rate with monthly subcutaneous administration, alongside a 98% reduction in high viral load shedding, supporting its potential for suppressive therapy in recurrent genital herpes.

Topical and Supportive Therapies

Topical therapies for herpes simplex virus (HSV) infections primarily target localized symptoms on the skin or mucous membranes, offering relief from outbreaks of oral or genital herpes without systemic absorption. Acyclovir 5% cream, applied five times daily at the first sign of symptoms, is a standard treatment for recurrent herpes labialis (cold sores caused by HSV-1), shortening healing time by approximately one day compared to placebo. This formulation accelerates lesion resolution by inhibiting viral replication at the site of application, with studies showing it reduces the duration of symptoms in both initial and recurrent episodes. Similarly, docosanol 10% cream, an over-the-counter option for HSV-1 cold sores, shortens healing time by about 18 hours when applied at the prodrome stage, by preventing viral entry into host cells through disruption of the viral envelope.
Supportive measures focus on alleviating pain and discomfort associated with HSV lesions. Topical lidocaine gel (5%) provides localized analgesia for painful herpetic eruptions, reducing neural transmission of pain signals in acute outbreaks, though it is more commonly studied for herpes zoster-related pain. Oral nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), such as ibuprofen, offer systemic pain relief and help manage inflammation during outbreaks, complementing topical approaches. For genital herpes, warm sitz bathssoaking the affected area in shallow, lukewarm water several times dailycan soothe irritation, reduce discomfort during urination, and promote hygiene without promoting bacterial superinfection.
Wound care practices emphasize gentle management to facilitate healing of HSV-induced lesions. Lesions should be washed softly with soap and water, then patted dry to maintain a moist-free environment that accelerates epithelial repair, while avoiding bandages to allow air exposure. Irritants like harsh soaps or tight clothing should be avoided to prevent exacerbation, and antipyretics such as acetaminophen can address any associated fever in severe cases.
Adjunctive therapies provide additional symptom control, though evidence varies. Zinc oxide cream, applied topically to lesions, offers a modest protective barrier and may slightly shorten symptom duration in oral herpes by providing anti-inflammatory effects. Oral lysine supplements (typically 1 g/day) have limited evidence for preventing or treating HSV recurrences, with studies showing no significant benefit at standard doses without dietary arginine restriction.

Alternative Medicine

Alternative medicine approaches to managing herpes simplex virus (HSV) infections focus on herbal remedies, nutritional supplements, and other non-pharmacological interventions, though evidence varies in strength and most lack robust clinical validation. These methods are often used adjunctively and should not replace standard antiviral therapies.
Topical application of lemon balm (Melissa officinalis) extract has shown promise in reducing the duration of herpes labialis outbreaks. In a double-blind, placebo-controlled trial involving 66 patients with recurrent oral herpes, lemon balm cream applied four times daily significantly shortened healing time by about one day compared to placebo, with reduced blistering and pain reported. Echinacea (Echinacea purpurea) extracts exhibit antiviral effects against HSV-1 in vitro by inhibiting viral replication, potentially supporting immune function, but clinical trials in humans yield mixed results, with limited evidence for reducing outbreak frequency or severity.
Nutritional supplements like L-lysine have been investigated for prophylaxis against recurrences. Daily oral doses of 1 g of L-lysine monohydrochloride reduced herpes simplex outbreaks by approximately 40% in one randomized trial of 52 patients over six months, compared to placebo, possibly due to its antagonism of arginine, which HSV requires for replication. Vitamins C and E may aid lesion healing; topical vitamin E oil applied to HSV lesions in small observational studies decreased pain and accelerated resolution by promoting skin repair, while high-dose intravenous vitamin C (e.g., 7.5 g daily) shortened healing time in herpes-related cases by up to 57% in limited trials, though primarily studied in herpes zoster.
Homeopathic remedies, such as Rhus toxicodendron dilutions, have been used for herpes symptoms based on anecdotal and observational data suggesting reduced outbreak intensity, but randomized controlled trials show no strong efficacy beyond placebo effects. Acupuncture for pain relief in HSV outbreaks relies largely on anecdotal reports of symptom alleviation through nerve modulation, with scant clinical evidence specific to herpes simplex and most studies addressing herpes zoster neuralgia instead.
Patients are advised to avoid unproven "cures" marketed for permanent HSV eradication, as none exist and such claims lack scientific support; additionally, herbal supplements like St. John's wort can interact with antiviral drugs by inducing liver enzymes, potentially reducing their effectiveness. Consultation with healthcare providers is essential to prevent adverse interactions.
As of 2025, emerging research on probiotics highlights their potential for microbiome modulation to curb HSV shedding. Extracellular vesicles from Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG, containing muramyl dipeptide, inhibited HSV-2 infection in vitro and reduced viral titers in mouse models by activating the NOD2-IFN-I immune pathway, suggesting a role in lowering asymptomatic transmission.

Prognosis

Short-Term Outcomes

The primary episode of herpes simplex virus (HSV) infection typically resolves within 2 to 4 weeks when treated with antiviral medications such as acyclovir, valacyclovir, or famciclovir, which accelerate lesion healing and reduce the duration of symptoms compared to untreated cases. Following primary infection, approximately 80% to 98% of individuals develop detectable type-specific IgG antibodies to HSV within 2 to 12 weeks, confirming seroconversion and lifelong immunity against reinfection with the same type.
Recurrent episodes of HSV-2 genital herpes occur at an average rate of 4 to 5 times per year in the first year after initial infection, with frequency declining to about 1 to 2 episodes per year over time due to adaptive immune responses. Viral shedding often occurs asymptomatically, without noticeable lesions, on approximately 15-30% of days in individuals with HSV-2 genital infections, though symptomatic outbreaks still shorten in duration and severity with episodic antiviral therapy.
Antiviral treatments for HSV shorten outbreak duration by 1 to 2 days and reduce symptom severity, including pain and lesion size, when initiated within 72 hours of onset, though they do not eliminate the risk of future recurrences.
Factors influencing recurrence risk include younger age, which is associated with higher initial episode frequency before natural decline, and female sex, which correlates with more frequent and severe genital HSV-2 recurrences due to anatomical and immunological differences.

Long-Term Complications

Herpes simplex virus (HSV) encephalitis represents a severe neurological complication, with mortality rates reduced to approximately 20-30% through antiviral treatment such as acyclovir, though survivors often experience long-term sequelae including cognitive impairment, epilepsy, and motor deficits. These outcomes stem from the virus's propensity to cause widespread brain inflammation and necrosis, particularly in the temporal lobes, leading to persistent neurological disability in up to 50-70% of cases. In immunocompetent individuals, long-term complications from HSV are rare, with most experiencing only occasional recurrences that decrease over time.
In reproductive health, HSV-2 infection triples the risk of HIV acquisition by disrupting mucosal barriers and enhancing viral entry, with meta-analyses reporting adjusted relative risks of approximately 3.0 for women and 2.7 for men. Furthermore, HSV-2 co-infection with high-risk human papillomavirus (HPV) may elevate cervical cancer risk through immune suppression and synergistic effects on cervical epithelial cells, though direct causality remains debated.
Observational studies have suggested a potential association between HSV-1 infection and accelerated Alzheimer's disease progression, with higher HSV seropositivity observed in affected individuals, but Mendelian randomization analyses indicate no proven causal link, attributing findings possibly to confounding factors like age or comorbidities.
Recurrent herpes infections, particularly HSV-2, impose significant quality-of-life burdens, with chronic neuropathic pain persisting in some cases and psychological distress, including depression and anxiety, reported in about 25-30% of individuals experiencing frequent recurrences. These effects often arise from the unpredictable nature of outbreaks and associated stigma, exacerbating emotional strain beyond physical symptoms.

Epidemiology

Global Prevalence

Herpes simplex virus type 1 (HSV-1) is the most prevalent herpesvirus globally, with an estimated 3.8 billion people under age 50representing 64% of this age groupinfected worldwide (2020 estimates). This seroprevalence exceeds 90% in many low- and lower-middle-income countries, particularly in Africa and Southeast Asia, where early childhood oral transmission is common.
HSV-2, which primarily causes genital herpes, affects approximately 520 million people aged 1549 years, or 13% of this demographic, based on 2020 estimates that remain stable in recent modeling. Overall, genital herpes infectionsencompassing both HSV-1 and HSV-2impact 846 million individuals in the same age group, equating to about 1 in 5 adults globally. Of these, HSV-2 accounts for the majority of recurrent genital cases, while genital HSV-1 prevalence stands at around 376 million (10.2%).
Recent trends indicate a shift in genital herpes epidemiology, with HSV-1 increasingly responsible for primary genital infections, particularly in high-income regions, while HSV-2 prevalence remains stable or slightly declining. Annually, there are about 42 million new genital herpes cases worldwide, contributing to 205 million episodes of genital ulcer disease. Disparities are pronounced in low-income regions, where overall herpesvirus burdens are elevated due to limited access to diagnostics and care; for HSV-2 specifically, prevalence is roughly twice as high in women (17%) compared to men (10%).

Transmission and Risk Factors

Herpes simplex virus (HSV) types 1 and 2 are primarily transmitted through direct skin-to-skin or mucosal contact, most commonly during sexual activity such as vaginal, anal, or oral sex with an infected individual. HSV-1 is frequently associated with oral-genital transmission, while HSV-2 is more often linked to genital-genital contact, though both types can infect either site. Vertical transmission from mother to neonate can occur during childbirth, particularly if the mother has active genital lesions at delivery, leading to neonatal herpes in approximately 1 in 3,000 to 20,000 live births in high-income countries.
A significant proportion of HSV transmissionsestimated at 70% or moreoccur asymptomatically through viral shedding from the skin or mucous membranes without visible lesions. This shedding can happen intermittently in infected individuals, even years after initial infection, facilitating unnoticed spread during close contact.
Key risk factors for HSV-2 acquisition include having multiple sexual partners, with odds ratios ranging from 2 to 5 compared to those with fewer partners, reflecting increased exposure opportunities. Co-infection with HIV substantially elevates the risk of HSV acquisition, approximately threefold, due to impaired immune responses that facilitate viral entry and establishment. Incidence of HSV infections peaks during adolescence and young adulthood, particularly among sexually active individuals aged 15-24, when sexual debut and partner networks expand.
Protective factors include male circumcision, which reduces HSV-2 acquisition risk by about 30% in heterosexual men by removing susceptible foreskin tissue. Being male is also associated with a lower prevalence of oral HSV-1 infections compared to females, possibly due to differences in immune responses or exposure patterns.
In recent years, including data up to 2025, genital HSV-1 infections have risen, particularly among youth, driven by increased oral-genital contact and declining childhood oral HSV-1 exposure, leaving more individuals susceptible to genital acquisition via oral sex.

Society and Culture

Stigma and Public Perception

Genital herpes is often stigmatized as a marker of moral failing, primarily due to its association with sexual transmission, leading individuals to internalize feelings of shame, guilt, and promiscuity. This perception portrays those affected as irresponsible or dirty, exacerbating emotional distress and hindering open discussions about the condition. Approximately half of individuals with herpes report fearing disclosure in romantic relationships, citing concerns over rejection or relationship dissolution as primary barriers.
Common myths perpetuate this stigma, such as the belief that herpes afflicts only promiscuous individuals, overlooking the fact that HSV-1 often originates from non-sexual childhood exposures like kissing or sharing utensils. Another widespread misconception is that herpes is curable, ignoring its lifelong nature despite effective management options, which fuels anxiety and misinformation.
Media portrayals have historically amplified these fears by depicting herpes as a debilitating, life-altering affliction, as seen in 1980s films and television that emphasized isolation and despair, thereby heightening public anxiety. Recent efforts, such as the 2025 New Zealand Herpes Foundation campaign "The Best Place in the World to Have Herpes," have aimed to counter this by using humor and education to normalize the condition and reduce associated shame.
Gender disparities intensify the stigma, with women often facing greater blame and scrutiny for herpes infections, linked to societal expectations around sexual purity and resulting in more severe self-stigmatization. This intersects with broader STI stigmas, including that of HIV, where shared associations with sexual behavior compound feelings of moral judgment and vulnerability.
Globally, stigma is more pronounced in conservative cultures, where cultural and religious taboos around sexuality discourage reporting and seeking care, leading to significant underreporting of cases. In such contexts, fear of social ostracism results in delayed diagnosis and reduced public health interventions.

Support Groups and Advocacy

In the United States, the American Sexual Health Association (ASHA) operates the Herpes Resource Center, established in 1979 to offer support networks, educational resources, and a national hotline for individuals with genital herpes. ASHA's initiatives integrate with Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) guidelines, which recommend counseling resources to address the chronic aspects of herpes simplex virus (HSV) infection and facilitate partner communication.
In the United Kingdom, the British Association for Sexual Health and HIV (BASHH) provides clinical guidelines on herpes management, including psychosocial support recommendations, while the Herpes Viruses Association (HVA) offers a dedicated helpline (0345 123 2305) for confidential advice, information, and emotional support to those affected by genital herpes.
Patient-led networks such as Herpes Cure Advocacy, an international nonprofit, connect affected individuals worldwide to foster community and push for improved diagnostics and treatments.
Key services provided by these groups include counseling to manage emotional impacts like anxiety from stigma, disclosure tools to guide conversations with partners, and partner notification programs that promote anonymous alerts and testing referrals. These efforts have demonstrably reduced feelings of isolation among people with HSV by creating peer support environments where experiences can be shared. Additionally, advocacy from organizations like Herpes Cure Advocacy has influenced policy, contributing to the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) recognition of HSV as a priority in its 2025 STI National Strategic Plan addendum, which supports enhanced funding for prevention and vaccine development.

Research

Vaccine Development

Developing an effective vaccine against herpes simplex virus (HSV) has proven challenging due to the virus's ability to establish latency in sensory neurons, evading both innate and adaptive immune responses and leading to lifelong recurrent infections. This latency complicates the achievement of sterilizing immunity, as the virus can reactivate periodically without productive replication detectable by the immune system. Past efforts highlight these hurdles; for instance, GlaxoSmithKline's Simplirix (gD2/AS04 adjuvant), a prophylactic subunit vaccine, failed to meet its primary endpoint of preventing genital herpes in a Phase III trial in women in 2010, showing no overall efficacy despite initial promise in earlier studies. More recently, GSK discontinued its therapeutic HSV vaccine candidate GSK3943104 in September 2024 after it failed to achieve the primary efficacy endpoint in a Phase I/II proof-of-concept study, underscoring ongoing difficulties in eliciting durable protection.
Promising candidates are advancing in clinical and preclinical stages. Moderna's mRNA-1608, a trivalent mRNA vaccine targeting HSV-2 glycoproteins gC2, gD2, and gE2 to induce both humoral and cellular immunity, entered Phase I/II trials in September 2023 and completed in June 2025, with results pending as of November 2025; preclinical data showed up to 100% reduction in genital disease in animal models. In the preclinical space, Rational Vaccines' RVx201, a live-attenuated HSV-2 vaccine engineered to limit neuroinvasion, has demonstrated safety and robust T-cell responses in guinea pig models, reducing lesion severity and viral shedding compared to subunit vaccines. These approaches aim to address latency by promoting tissue-resident memory T cells capable of controlling reactivation.
Insights from varicella-zoster virus (VZV) vaccine development offer valuable lessons for HSV, particularly the success of Shingrix, a recombinant subunit vaccine approved in 2017 that targets glycoprotein E (gE) with an AS01B adjuvant to elicit strong T-cell responses and over 90% efficacy against shingles in older adults. This strategy highlights the potential of adjuvanted glycoprotein-based vaccines to overcome age-related immune decline, a concept being adapted for HSV to enhance mucosal and cellular immunity. Emerging trials, such as preclinical nanobody-based approaches targeting prefusion glycoprotein B, showed potent neutralization of both HSV-1 and HSV-2 in 2025 studies, paving the way for Phase I evaluation with goals of at least 50% reduction in transmission, building on benchmarks where prior candidates achieved partial shedding reductions. However, key barriers persist, including unclear correlates of protectionsuch as optimal antibody neutralization titers or T-cell subsetsand the need for vaccines to induce robust mucosal immunity at entry sites like the genital tract to prevent initial infection and latency establishment.

Novel Therapies and Biomarkers

Assembly Biosciences' ABI-5366, a novel helicase-primase inhibitor designed as a helical peptide mimetic, demonstrated a 94% reduction in genital HSV-2 shedding in an interim Phase 1b clinical trial conducted in 2025, with weekly oral dosing up to 350 mg showing good tolerability in HSV-2 seropositive participants. This long-acting oral candidate supports potential monthly regimens, advancing toward Phase 2 studies in mid-2026 to evaluate longer-term suppression of recurrent genital herpes.
In parallel, bispecific nanobodies targeting non-overlapping epitopes on HSV glycoprotein D have emerged as potent entry inhibitors, with a fusion construct (Nb14-32-Fc) exhibiting high-affinity neutralization of both HSV-1 and HSV-2 in preclinical models, preventing receptor binding and gD-gH/gL interactions to block viral fusion. Published in May 2025, this approach highlights nanobodies' potential for prophylactic or therapeutic use against mucosal HSV infections, particularly in immunocompromised individuals.
Gene editing strategies using CRISPR/Cas systems aim to excise or inactivate latent HSV genomes from neuronal reservoirs, with Excision BioTherapeutics' EBT-104 targeting HSV-1 genes ICP0 and ICP27 in preclinical studies presented in May 2025, achieving significant viral reduction without off-target effects in animal models. Supported by 2025 research grants, these preclinical efforts focus on adeno-associated virus delivery for durable latency reversal, potentially offering a curative option beyond suppressive antivirals.
Long-acting injectables represent another frontier, exemplified by United BioPharma's UB-621, a monoclonal antibody administered subcutaneously every three months, which neutralizes HSV-1 and HSV-2 entry in Phase 2 trials as of 2025, reducing lesion recurrence rates in recurrent genital herpes patients. Additionally, microbiome modulation strategies, such as promoting Lactobacillus dominance in the vaginal microbiota, have shown preclinical promise in decreasing HSV-2 infectivity and shedding by enhancing epithelial barriers and innate immune responses, with ongoing studies exploring probiotic interventions.
Emerging biomarkers include plasma HSV DNA levels, which serve as a predictive indicator of reactivation risk, correlating with subclinical shedding episodes and disease severity in longitudinal cohort studies. Similarly, miRNA profiles, such as miR-146a upregulated during HSV-1 infection, have been associated with neuroinflammation in brain cells and potential contributions to Alzheimer's disease pathogenesis.
These advances align with the NIH Strategic Plan for Herpes Simplex Virus Research (20232028), which prioritizes novel therapeutics and biomarkers through enhanced funding for latency-targeted interventions and predictive diagnostics to address unmet needs in HSV management.
