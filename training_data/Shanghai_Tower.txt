Shanghai Tower
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Shanghai Tower is a 632-meter-tall (2,073 ft) supertall skyscraper located in the Lujiazui financial district of Pudong, Shanghai, China, serving as the tallest building in the country and the third-tallest in the world as of 2025. Designed by the international architecture firm Gensler with principal architects Jun Xia and Marshall Strabala, the tower features a distinctive nine-sided, spiraling form that twists 120 degrees from base to pinnacle to reduce wind loads by 24 percent, topped out in August 2013 and structurally completed in 2015. The 128-story structure, developed by the Shanghai Tower Construction & Development Co., Ltd., encompasses 5 underground levels and rises to an occupied height of 583.4 meters, housing a mixed-use complex that includes Class-A office spaces, the luxury J Hotel Shanghai on floors 84 to 110, retail areas, cultural venues, and the Top of Shanghai Observatory on the 118th floor offering panoramic city views. Construction began in November 2008 under structural engineering by Thornton Tomasetti, incorporating 106 high-speed elevators reaching up to 20.5 meters per second and innovative sky bridges connecting nine vertical zones to foster community and efficiency.
A hallmark of sustainable architecture, the tower earned LEED Platinum certification and China's Three-Star Green Building rating through features like a double-skin glass curtain wall for natural ventilation, 270 on-site wind turbines generating up to 10 percent of its energy needs, rainwater harvesting systems, and extensive vertical gardens that enhance biodiversity and occupant well-being. Officially opened to the public in February 2016, the Shanghai Tower symbolizes Shanghai's rapid urbanization and global ambitions, accommodating up to 16,000 people daily while integrating advanced seismic-resistant engineering to withstand earthquakes up to magnitude 9. In 2025, it received the Council on Tall Buildings and Urban Habitat's (CTBUH) 10-Year Award, recognizing its enduring impact on skyscraper design and urban sustainability.

Location and Context

Site Description

The Shanghai Tower is located at 311409N 1213011E in the Lujiazui Financial District of Pudong, Shanghai, China, anchoring the city's modern skyline on the east bank of the Huangpu River. Rising to a height of 632 meters (2,073 feet), the megatall structure encompasses 128 stories above ground, with an additional 5 levels below grade, making it China's tallest building and a prominent feature in the region's vertical urban landscape. Its design integrates seamlessly with the surrounding high-density environment, contributing to Lujiazui's role as a global financial hub.
The tower's footprint occupies a 3.0-hectare site, approximately 30,370 square meters (7.5 acres), which includes landscaped public spaces and connections to adjacent infrastructure such as metro lines and pedestrian pathways. This compact base supports the tower's spiraling form, which tapers upward to minimize wind loads while maximizing usable interior space across its mixed-use program of offices, retail, hotel, and cultural facilities.
Positioned in immediate proximity to the 421-meter Jin Mao Tower to the north and the 492-meter Shanghai World Financial Center to the east, the Shanghai Tower completes the Lujiazui supertall trio, a cluster of three adjacent skyscrapers that symbolize Shanghai's rapid economic ascent since the 1990s. These neighboring icons, completed in 1999 and 2008 respectively, share a unified plaza level that enhances pedestrian connectivity and visual harmony within the district.
At its core, the tower employs a reinforced concrete central core, measuring roughly 30 meters square with walls up to 1.2 meters thick, paired with steel perimeter supercolumns that form a composite megaframe for lateral stability against typhoons and earthquakes common to the region. This material combinationhigh-strength concrete for the core and structural steel for outriggers, belt trusses, and column encasementsoptimizes load distribution while reducing overall steel usage by about 25% compared to conventional supertall designs.

Urban Integration

The Shanghai Tower is strategically positioned in the Lujiazui financial district of Pudong, Shanghai, as the culminating element of the iconic "Three Towers" complex, which also includes the Jin Mao Tower and the Shanghai World Financial Center. This supertall cluster, developed to symbolize Shanghai's emergence as a global financial powerhouse, anchors the Lujiazui Finance and Trade Zone, a former agricultural area transformed since the 1980s into China's premier business hub. The towers are interconnected via underground walkways and parking facilities, fostering seamless pedestrian flow and reinforcing the district's role as an integrated urban node.
At its base, the tower integrates extensive indoor and outdoor public spaces, including retail areas, cultural venues, an exhibition center, and landscaped gardens that draw inspiration from traditional Chinese designs, such as sunken gardens with bamboo islands and water features. These elements create vibrant community areas for events, leisure, and social interaction, covering a significant portion of the 30,370-square-meter site with green space comprising about 33% of the footprint. This base-level design enhances accessibility and vitality, blending commercial activity with natural respite to support the surrounding urban fabric.
Standing at 632 meters, the Shanghai Tower ranks as the third-tallest building in the world as of 2025, following the Burj Khalifa and Merdeka 118, and significantly elevates Pudong's skyline, underscoring the region's status as a leading international financial center. Its spiraling form not only provides a visual landmark but also contributes to Shanghai's urban development by promoting high-density, mixed-use growth in Lujiazui.
The tower's connectivity to the city's transportation network further bolsters its urban integration, with direct adjacency to Lujiazui Station on Shanghai Metro Lines 2 and 14. Visitors and occupants can access the site via Exit 6 from Line 2 or Exit 9A from Line 14, enabling efficient links to key areas like Pudong International Airport and the historic Bund across the Huangpu River. This proximity to rapid transit supports sustainable mobility and accommodates the high volume of daily commuters in the district.

History

Planning and Funding

The Shanghai Tower project was initiated in 2006 as the third and final phase of the Lujiazui supertall skyscraper development in Shanghai's Pudong district, following the Jin Mao Tower and Shanghai World Financial Center. This planning effort aligned with broader urban strategies to establish Lujiazui as a global financial hub, with early models dating back to 1993 envisioning a trio of iconic high-rises.
Development was led by the Shanghai Tower Construction & Development Co., Ltd., established in 2007 as a joint venture consortium. Key stakeholders included Shanghai Chengtou Corporation (a municipal investment arm), Lujiazui Finance & Trade Zone Development Co., Ltd., and Shanghai Construction Group Co., Ltd., all state-owned entities contributing expertise in finance, urban planning, and construction. The consortium's registered capital totaled RMB 5.4 billion, enabling coordinated oversight from conceptualization through completion.
The project carried an estimated total cost of US$2.4 billion (approximately CNY 15.7 billion), financed primarily through equity from the consortium's state-owned shareholders, bank loans, and support from the Shanghai municipal government. This funding structure reflected China's emphasis on public-private partnerships for landmark infrastructure, with no external private investors dominating the backing.
In 2008, the architectural design was awarded to the international firm Gensler following an invited international competition that included prominent rivals such as Skidmore, Owings & Merrill (SOM), Kohn Pedersen Fox (KPF), and Foster + Partners. Gensler's proposal, selected for its innovative integration of sustainability and urban context, marked a pivotal step in formalizing the tower's conceptual framework.

Design and Construction

The design and construction of the Shanghai Tower involved a collaborative effort among leading international engineering firms, with Thornton Tomasetti serving as the structural engineer responsible for the tower's innovative load-bearing systems, and Cosentini Associates handling the mechanical, electrical, and plumbing (MEP) engineering to integrate complex systems within the twisting form. Construction commenced with groundbreaking on November 29, 2008, marking the start of a multi-year process to erect the 632-meter structure on Shanghai's soft delta soils. The project reached a key milestone with topping out on August 3, 2013, when the core and primary structural elements were completed, followed by full completion in 2015, allowing for subsequent interior and exterior finishing.
A primary construction challenge was implementing the tower's signature 120-degree twist over its height, which required precise engineering to maintain structural integrity against wind and seismic forces while minimizing material use. The core, a composite concrete and steel element measuring 27 meters by 27 meters, was built using a repetitive slip-form technique, enabling continuous vertical progression floor by floor at a rate of about 4 meters per day to accommodate the helical geometry without interruptions. This method, combined with outrigger trusses and mega-columns, distributed loads effectively and reduced the building's wind exposure by 24% compared to a rectilinear design.
The project employed a peak workforce of approximately 4,000 workers, coordinated by the Shanghai Construction Group to manage the demanding schedule and high-altitude operations. Safety was prioritized through rigorous training, supervision, and specialized equipment for tasks like erecting cantilevered steel at elevations exceeding 300 meters, resulting in no major accidents reported during the core construction phase. These measures ensured efficient progress amid the tower's scale, with over 61,000 cubic meters of concrete poured into the foundation and core.

Completion and Operations

The Shanghai Tower achieved partial occupancy in late 2015, with initial tenants beginning to move into office spaces following the completion of core construction. The official opening of the observation deck and key public facilities occurred in July 2016, marking the tower's initial entry into operational use despite ongoing regulatory hurdles.
Early operations faced significant challenges, particularly with the advanced elevator system, which required extensive testing and approvals to meet stringent fire safety regulations for supertall structures. These issues, combined with the building's complex architecture, delayed full tenant occupancy and comprehensive operations until 2017, when the tower finally received clearance for unrestricted use.
By 2025, the tower has hosted its annual Towerrunning Challenge, with the 2025 edition scheduled for November 2223, attracting international athletes to climb 119 floors and 3,398 steps in the world's tallest such event. Ongoing maintenance includes minor upgrades integrating AI-powered sensors and IoT systems for enhanced energy optimization and safety monitoring across facilities. Occupancy has stabilized for premium office spaces, reflecting robust post-COVID recovery and sustained demand in Shanghai's financial district.

Architecture and Design

Structural Features

The Shanghai Tower features a distinctive twisted form that rotates 120 degrees from base to spire, creating a spiraling silhouette designed to minimize wind resistance in Shanghai's typhoon-prone environment. This aerodynamic shape, refined through extensive wind tunnel testing, reduces overall wind loads on the structure by 24 percent compared to a conventional prismatic tower.
The tower employs a double-skin facade system consisting of two concentric layers of glass enclosing nine cylindrical indoor zones stacked vertically. The outer layer uses 26 mm laminated low-iron glass panels with low-e coatings, while the inner layer features 30 mm insulated glass units, creating atriums that facilitate natural ventilation through passive convection and updraft. These atriums, unconditioned in their upper portions, integrate public gardens and reduce thermal loads on the building envelope.
Structurally, the tower relies on a central reinforced concrete core measuring 90 by 90 feet, surrounded by a composite steel-concrete perimeter frame with eight super columns. Stability is enhanced by eight two-story-deep curved steel outrigger trusses that connect the core to the perimeter columns at mechanical floors, distributing lateral forces from wind and earthquakes. This innovative design, combined with the twisted form, resulted in significant weight savings, reducing required structural steel and concrete by amounts that saved approximately US$58 million in material costs.

Vertical Transportation System

The vertical transportation system of the Shanghai Tower, engineered by Mitsubishi Electric, comprises 106 elevators that facilitate efficient movement across the building's 128 stories, incorporating regenerative drive technology to recover energy during descent and reduce overall consumption by up to 30%. These elevators feature active roller guides and advanced noise-reduction systems, ensuring smooth and quiet rides even at high speeds, while ceiling displays in shuttle units provide dynamic visual experiences for passengers heading to the observation decks.
Central to the system's design are three ultra-high-speed elevators reaching 20.5 m/s (74 km/h; 46 mph), which previously held the Guinness World Record for the fastest elevator until 2017 and enable a trip from the ground to the 119th floor in approximately 55 seconds. These express elevators primarily serve the upper zones, connecting directly to sky lobbies that act as transfer hubs for zoned access, thereby minimizing cross-traffic and optimizing vertical flow in the supertall structure.
The tower employs a multi-tiered sky lobby configuration at floors 22, 37, 52, and 68, where passengers disembark from ground-level express lifts and proceed via local elevators to their destinations within dedicated vertical neighborhoods of 12 to 15 floors each. This setup, combined with destination dispatch control systems that assign passengers to optimal cars based on floor selection, significantly cuts average wait times to under 15 seconds and enhances capacity during peak hours.
Overall, the system supports up to 16,000 people daily, integrating seamlessly with the tower's mixed-use functions from offices to hospitality and public amenities, while prioritizing safety features like earthquake-resistant damping and emergency evacuation protocols.

Sustainability Initiatives

The Shanghai Tower exemplifies advanced sustainability in supertall architecture through integrated environmental design strategies. It earned LEED Platinum certification for Core and Shell in December 2015, marking it as the highest-rated building of its height in China at the time and demonstrating leadership in energy efficiency, water conservation, and material use. The tower also holds China's Green Building Three-Star rating, the nation's top accolade for ecological performance.
Central to its energy initiatives is a double-skin glass facade that creates an insulating air cushion, enabling natural ventilation and reducing reliance on artificial cooling systems by regulating internal temperatures. Rainwater harvested from the tower's spiraling parapet is repurposed for the heating, ventilation, and air conditioning systems, minimizing freshwater demand. Complementing these, 270 vertical-axis wind turbines installed near the summit generate renewable electricity equivalent to approximately 10% of the building's needs, harnessing Shanghai's prevailing winds.
Water conservation efforts include a greywater recycling system that reuses 25% of wastewater for toilet flushing and landscape irrigation, while the overall design incorporates low-flow fixtures to further limit consumption. Nine indoor zones feature sky gardens and vertical greenery, providing ecological buffers that improve air quality, support biodiversity, and offer passive thermal regulation through evapotranspiration.
These features collectively achieve a 21% reduction in energy consumption relative to traditional supertall buildings, substantially lowering the structure's operational carbon footprint and setting a benchmark for urban sustainability. The tower's twisted silhouette aids these efforts by channeling winds more efficiently, which reduces aerodynamic loads and enhances the performance of integrated renewable systems.

Facilities and Usage

Floor Plans and Layout

The Shanghai Tower's layout is structured around nine vertical zones, creating a self-contained vertical city that integrates diverse functions while optimizing space and energy use through its twisted form and double-skin facade. This design divides the 128-story structure into distinct levels for public access, commercial activity, professional workspaces, hospitality, and cultural experiences, with mechanical floors interspersed to support operations. The overall floor plate tapers gradually from a base diameter of about 82 meters to narrower profiles at the apex, enclosing approximately 380,000 square meters of usable space above grade.
The lower floors (1-7) form the podium base, dedicated to retail outlets, a conference center, and the grand public lobby, which together span roughly 38 meters in height and include basement levels (B1-B2) for additional support facilities like parking and exhibitions. This area, known as Shanghai Essence, features high-end boutiques, dining venues, and event spaces designed to draw pedestrians and integrate with the surrounding Lujiazui district, acting as a vibrant entry point to the tower.
Mid-level floors (8-50 and 57-94) are allocated primarily to Class-A office spaces, accommodating corporate tenants in a series of stacked cylindrical sections with integrated sky gardens for natural ventilation and wellness. Sky lobbies at floors 8, 50, and 94 serve as key transfer hubs, equipped with amenities such as lounges, fitness areas, and green atriums that enhance user experience and support zoned elevator systems for efficient vertical circulation. These office zones emphasize flexible, open-plan layouts with views enhanced by the building's spiraling curtain wall.

The hotel spans floors 86-98 with 165 spacious rooms and suites averaging 61-380 square meters, including spas, restaurants, and ballrooms, positioned to capitalize on elevated vistas. Observation platforms at floors 118, 119, and 121 provide tiered viewing experiences, from standard decks to VIP lounges, accessible via the world's fastest elevators reaching speeds of 20.5 meters per second.
The pinnacle floors (126-128) are reserved for cultural facilities and the structural spire, culminating in the SKY632 Art Space on floors 125-126the world's highest immersive cultural venue at 583 metersfeaturing multimedia installations and a tuned mass damper exhibit. The tuning fork spire, extending to 632 meters, not only defines the tower's iconic silhouette but also aids wind resistance through its aerodynamic integration.

Tenants and Amenities

The Shanghai Tower accommodates a variety of major tenants across its office spaces, which span floors 8 to 97 and house companies from over 15 countries. Prominent financial firms include Ant Financial, the largest tenant occupying three and a half floors since 2017, alongside Alibaba, PIMCO, and BNP Paribas Cardif, reflecting the building's appeal to global business operations in finance and technology. Insurance companies such as Lloyd's of London have also secured office space, contributing to a diverse tenant mix that saw foreign firms leasing 41% of the available area as of 2022.
The tower's upper levels, from floors 86 to 98, are occupied by the J Hotel Shanghai Tower, a 165-room luxury property that opened in 2021 and holds the distinction of being the world's highest hotel by elevation above ground. Guest rooms and suites range from 61 to 380 square meters, with amenities including a 160-square-meter indoor swimming pool on the 84th floor featuring panoramic city views and a 130-square-meter viewing deck. The hotel emphasizes high-end hospitality with facilities like a spa and fitness center, catering to both leisure and business travelers seeking elevated experiences.
Public amenities enhance the tower's role as a visitor destination, including the "Top of Shanghai" observation decks on the 118th floor at 546 meters and the 121st floor at 562 meters, providing 360-degree vistas of the city skyline via the world's fastest elevators reaching speeds of 20.5 meters per second. These views are superior to those from the Oriental Pearl Tower due to the Shanghai Tower's greater height of 632 meters, offering higher, more expansive 360-degree panoramas with clearer visibility, broader scope, and less obstruction compared to the Oriental Pearl Tower's 468-meter height and 342-meter highest observation deck. Dining options are available at Heavenly Jin, a fine-dining restaurant on the 120th floor serving Huaiyang cuisine at 556 meters, recognized by Guinness World Records as one of the highest such establishments. The tower hosts annual events like the Shanghai Tower Towerrunning Challenge, with the 2025 edition on November 22-23 featuring climbers ascending 119 floors and 3,398 stairs over 552 meters, drawing international participants for its extreme vertical race.
Visitor access to the observation decks requires timed tickets purchased in advance, with operations daily from 8:30 a.m. to 10:00 p.m. and last entry at 9:30 p.m., alongside capacity limits to ensure safety and flow for up to several thousand guests per day during peak periods. These measures, combined with security screenings, provide controlled yet immersive public experiences distinct from the tower's private office and hotel zones.

Significance and Impact

Awards and Recognition

The Shanghai Tower has received numerous accolades for its innovative design, sustainability, and engineering excellence since its completion. In 2016, it was named the Best Tall Building Worldwide by the Council on Tall Buildings and Urban Habitat (CTBUH), recognizing its overall architectural and functional superiority among global high-rises.
In 2015, the tower earned the Emporis Skyscraper Award, selected from over 300 completed buildings taller than 100 meters worldwide for its elegant twisting form and energy-efficient design. For sustainability, it achieved LEED Platinum certification from the U.S. Green Building Council, making it the tallest and largest such certified building globally at the time, due to its integrated green technologies like rainwater harvesting and high-performance glazing.
Further recognition came in 2016 with the inaugural American Architecture Prize for its category, honoring the Gensler-designed structure as a landmark of contemporary skyscraper innovation. In 2025, CTBUH awarded it the 10-Year Award, celebrating its enduring performance and influence on tall building practices a decade after opening.
As of 2025, the Shanghai Tower remains China's tallest building at 632 meters, a record held since its 2015 completion, and ranks third worldwide behind the Burj Khalifa and Merdeka 118, as featured in annual lists of supertall structures.

Economic and Cultural Role

The Shanghai Tower significantly contributes to Shanghai's economy by attracting millions of visitors annually to its observation decks and amenities, generating substantial tourism revenue and supporting the local financial district. Located in the Lujiazui financial hub, the tower provides office, retail, and hospitality spaces that accommodate up to 16,000 people daily, fostering job creation and economic activity in the surrounding area. This presence has helped boost Lujiazui's commercial vitality, drawing international businesses and enhancing the district's role as a key driver of Shanghai's GDP growth.
Culturally, the Shanghai Tower stands as a prominent symbol of China's rapid modernization and urban ambition, embodying the nation's shift toward innovative, high-density development in the 21st century. Its iconic twisted form has even inspired advancements in materials science, such as the 2025 development of the first synthetic dynamic helical polymer, which draws on the tower's spiraling architecture to create adaptive, sustainable structures. By integrating public spaces and green features, the tower promotes a sense of community and progress, reinforcing Shanghai's identity as the "Oriental Pearl" of Asiaa blend of historical charm and futuristic skyline.
As a tourism magnet, the tower's high-altitude observation decks offer panoramic views that draw global visitors, contributing to Shanghai's record tourism revenues exceeding 576 billion yuan in 2024 while elevating the city's international appeal. On a broader scale, the Shanghai Tower serves as a case study in supertall sustainable design, influencing skyscraper projects across Asia and the Middle East through its energy-efficient double-skin facade and wind-resistant form, which prioritize environmental integration in dense urban environments. This global resonance underscores its role in advancing eco-conscious high-rise architecture worldwide.
