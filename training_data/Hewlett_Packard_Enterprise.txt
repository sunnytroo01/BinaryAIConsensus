 Hewlett Packard Enterprise Company (HPE) is an American multinational enterprise technology firm that designs, manufactures, and sells servers, storage, networking hardware, software, and related services focused on business and institutional customers. Formed on November 1, 2015, through the division of the original Hewlett-Packard Company, HPE inherited the enterprise infrastructure operations, while the consumer-oriented personal computing and printing divisions became HP Inc.
Headquartered in Spring, Texas, under President and CEO Antonio Neri, HPE prioritizes edge-to-cloud architectures, hybrid cloud platforms, artificial intelligence integration, and high-performance computing to enable data capture, analysis, and action for enterprise clients. The company's core offerings encompass compute systems like ProLiant servers, storage solutions including Nimble and 3PAR arrays, networking via Aruba and the recently acquired Juniper portfolios, and software for IT operations management and security.
HPE has distinguished itself through targeted acquisitions that bolster its capabilities in AI, networking, and supercomputing, including the 2015 purchase of Aruba Networks for wireless expertise, the 2019 acquisition of Cray Inc. for exascale computing leadership, and the 2025 completion of the Juniper Networks deal, which doubled its networking revenue and expanded cloud-native AI offerings. These moves have positioned HPE as a key provider of transformative infrastructure amid demands for scalable, secure data processing in hybrid environments.

Origins and Corporate Identity

Historical roots in Hewlett-Packard

Hewlett-Packard Company was established on January 1, 1939, by engineers William R. "Bill" Hewlett and David "Dave" Packard in a rented garage at 367 Addison Avenue in Palo Alto, California, with an initial capital investment of $538. The partners' first commercial product, the HP 200A resistance-capacity audio oscillator, was developed in 1938 and sold to Walt Disney Studios for use in synchronizing sound effects for the film Fantasia, demonstrating early innovation in electronic test and measurement equipment. This garage operation laid the engineering foundation for what became a cornerstone of Silicon Valley's technology ecosystem, emphasizing practical problem-solving and product reliability over speculative ventures.
Initially focused on precision instruments for scientific and industrial applications, Hewlett-Packard expanded into computing during the 1960s amid growing demand for automated data handling. Following the 1964 acquisition of Diode-Test Instrument Company (DSI), the firm introduced the HP 2116A minicomputer in 1966, marking its initial foray into programmable systems for scientific and engineering computations. By 1972, Hewlett-Packard launched the HP 3000 Series, its first computer explicitly designed for commercial data processing and multi-user business environments, which supported the emerging needs of enterprise operations through features like virtual memory and database management capabilities.
Through the 1980s and 1990s, as the personal computer market exploded, Hewlett-Packard sustained heavy investment in enterprise-grade hardware, developing scalable servers and workstations that prioritized reliability for mission-critical workloads over consumer-oriented PCs. Milestones included the introduction of RISC-based Precision Architecture servers in the mid-1980s and subsequent advancements in Unix-compatible systems, which enabled robust networking and data center deployments. The 2002 acquisition of Compaq Computer Corporation further strengthened this enterprise orientation by integrating high-volume server technologies, allowing Hewlett-Packard to capture significant market share in x86-based enterprise computing amid competition from specialized server vendors. These developments underscored the company's causal contribution to U.S. technological leadership, as sustained R&D in hardware architectures facilitated scalable infrastructure that underpinned global business digitization without relying on government subsidies or protectionism.

Naming and branding evolution

The name "Hewlett-Packard" originated from its founders, William Hewlett and David Packard, who established the company in 1939 after a coin toss determined the order: Hewlett won, resulting in "Hewlett-Packard" rather than "Packard-Hewlett." The "HP" abbreviation derived directly from this naming convention and became synonymous with the company's identity over decades.
In October 2014, Hewlett-Packard announced its separation into two entities to sharpen focus on distinct markets: the enterprise technology business, spun off as Hewlett Packard Enterprise (HPE), and the personal systems and printing operations, renamed HP Inc. The split, approved by the board in October 2015 and effective November 1, 2015, assigned the full "Hewlett Packard" name to the enterprise arm to evoke the original company's legacy in innovation while differentiating it from HP Inc.'s consumer-oriented branding, thereby reducing market confusion between enterprise solutions and personal computing products.
Under CEO Meg Whitman, HPE's initial 2015 branding emphasized enterprise agility, featuring a logo with a green rectangular element symbolizing growth opportunities and sustainability, integrated with the established HP wordmark to signal continuity amid transformation. This design restored the spelled-out "Hewlett Packard" in corporate identity, moving away from the abbreviated "HP" dominance to underscore a specialized enterprise ethos distinct from consumer perceptions.
In June 2025, under CEO Antonio Neri, HPE refreshed its brand with a bold new identity to reflect advances in AI, cloud, and networking, including an updated logo for its 10th anniversary. The refresh updated typography and colors while retaining the signature green in the "E" to align with an AI-focused future, aiming to project boldness and innovation without severing historical ties. This evolution prioritized visual clarity for enterprise clients, reinforcing HPE's positioning as a hybrid IT and edge-to-cloud provider separate from HP Inc.'s retail image.

Historical Development

Spin-off from HP Inc. (2015)

On November 1, 2015, Hewlett-Packard Company completed a tax-free spin-off of its enterprise products, services, and technology business into Hewlett Packard Enterprise (HPE), separating it from the personal systems and printing operations that became HP Inc. The transaction distributed one share of HPE common stock to each holder of HP common stock as of the record date of October 21, 2015, with HPE shares beginning regular-way trading on the New York Stock Exchange under the ticker symbol "HPE" on November 2, 2015. Under the leadership of CEO Meg Whitman, who transitioned to lead HPE, the split valued the new enterprise entity at approximately $50 billion, aligning with the broader HP market capitalization division into two standalone companies each exceeding $50 billion.
The primary motivations for the separation stemmed from HP's multiyear turnaround strategy initiated by Whitman in 2012, aimed at addressing declining performance in the personal computer market and enabling sharper focus on higher-margin enterprise segments such as servers, storage, networking, and IT services. By divesting the commoditized PC and printing businesses into HP Inc., HPE could prioritize infrastructure solutions for corporate clients, streamline operations, and pursue targeted investments amid competitive pressures from cloud computing shifts and activist shareholder demands for value unlocking. The restructuring was positioned as a means to enhance agility, with HPE inheriting HP's enterprise revenue base of roughly $52 billion annually while shedding lower-growth consumer-facing operations.
In the immediate aftermath, HPE reported fiscal 2015 fourth-quarter segment revenue of $14.1 billion, reflecting a 4% year-over-year decline in reported terms but a 3% increase in constant currency, indicating transitional stability despite one-time separation costs estimated in the hundreds of millions. Market reception included initial skepticism over execution risks and integration expenses, yet the spin-off facilitated HPE's independent capital allocation, setting the stage for subsequent divestitures of non-core assets to further concentrate on core enterprise hardware and hybrid IT offerings.

Strategic expansions and challenges (2016-2022)

Following the 2015 spin-off, Hewlett Packard Enterprise (HPE) pursued a strategy of divesting non-core assets to streamline operations around its enterprise hardware and services segments. In September 2017, HPE completed the separation and merger of its software business with Micro Focus International plc, in a transaction valued at approximately $8.8 billion, which included a $2.5 billion cash payment to HPE and resulted in HPE shareholders owning 50.1% of the combined entity. This divestiture reduced HPE's exposure to lower-margin software maintenance revenues and allowed refocus on high-performance computing (HPC), storage, and networking infrastructure, contributing to a revenue decline from $30.4 billion in fiscal year 2016 (ended October 31, 2016) to $28.9 billion in fiscal 2017 as non-core segments were shed.
In November 2017, HPE announced the transition of CEO leadership from Meg Whitman to Antonio Neri, effective February 1, 2018, positioning Neriwho had served as executive vice president of HPE's Enterprise Groupto emphasize hybrid IT and edge-to-cloud transformations amid competitive pressures from cloud-native rivals like Amazon Web Services and Dell Technologies. Under Neri, HPE accelerated portfolio rationalization and innovation in core areas, with fiscal 2018 revenue rebounding to $30.9 billion, driven by server and storage demand, though profitability faced margin compression from pricing competition and R&D investments exceeding $1.4 billion annually.
Strategic expansions targeted high-growth niches like HPC and edge computing to address data-intensive workloads. A pivotal move was the May 2019 announcement of HPE's $1.3 billion acquisition of Cray Inc., a supercomputing specialist, completed on September 25, 2019, which integrated Cray's expertise in scalable systems to bolster HPE's position in government and research contracts, including exascale computing initiatives. This acquisition enhanced HPE's capabilities for AI and simulation-driven applications at the edge, aligning with market shifts toward distributed computing, though it coincided with fiscal 2019 revenue stabilization at $29.1 billion amid broader enterprise spending caution.
The COVID-19 pandemic from 2020 introduced severe supply chain disruptions, exacerbating component shortages and logistics delays, particularly for semiconductors and assembly in Asia. HPE's fiscal 2020 revenue fell to $27.8 billion, with second-quarter shipments hampered by factory closures and port backlogs, prompting adaptations like accelerated remote work infrastructure demand and inventory prioritization for critical sectors. By fiscal 2021 and 2022, revenue held steady at approximately $27.8 billion and $28.5 billion, respectively, as HPE pivoted to as-a-service models like GreenLake for recurring revenue stability and mitigated shortages through supplier diversification, though gross margins dipped below 25% due to elevated freight and input costs. These challenges underscored vulnerabilities in globalized manufacturing but also catalyzed efficiency gains, such as reduced operational footprints and emphasis on software-defined infrastructure over hardware commoditization.

AI-driven growth and Juniper integration (2023-2025)

In 2023, Hewlett Packard Enterprise intensified its strategic emphasis on artificial intelligence infrastructure, aligning with surging enterprise demand for hybrid cloud and AI workloads, as demonstrated through key announcements and product launches at annual HPE Discover events. These gatherings, including the 2025 Las Vegas edition held June 23-26, showcased HPE's vision for integrated AI-native systems spanning edge-to-cloud environments, with demonstrations of advancements in secure networking and autonomous management tools like GreenLake Intelligence. This pivot contributed to robust empirical growth, particularly in server revenues fueled by AI systems demand.
By the third quarter of fiscal year 2025 (ended August 31, 2025), HPE reported record AI systems revenue of $1.6 billion shipped, supporting large-scale deployments such as NVIDIA GB200 platforms and driving the server segment to $4.94 billion in revenue, a 16% year-over-year increase. Overall quarterly revenue reached $9.1 billion, up 19% from the prior year, with AI and hybrid cloud solutions accounting for much of the expansion amid a pipeline reportedly multiples of the $3.7 billion backlog.
To bolster its networking capabilities for AI-driven architectures, HPE announced the acquisition of Juniper Networks in January 2024 for $14 billion in an all-cash deal valued at $40 per share. Despite U.S. Department of Justice antitrust scrutiny and potential state-level challenges, the transaction settled via divestitures and closed on July 2, 2025, integrating Juniper's Mist AI platform to create a comprehensive cloud-native networking portfolio optimized for AI workloads. This move addressed gaps in high-performance, AI-optimized networking, with early post-closure contributions evident in Q3 networking revenue of $1.7 billion.

Governance and Leadership

Executive team and key figures

Antonio Neri has served as president and chief executive officer of Hewlett Packard Enterprise since February 2018, succeeding Meg Whitman. Neri, who joined Hewlett-Packard in 1995 after studying engineering at Argentina's National Technological University, began his career in customer support and sales roles in Amsterdam before advancing through engineering and business leadership positions. Under his leadership, HPE has prioritized an edge-to-cloud strategy, integrating AI, hybrid cloud, and networking capabilities to drive innovation and customer value, resulting in scaled recurring revenue growth amid market challenges. This approach emphasizes operational efficiency and shareholder returns through targeted investments in AI-native solutions rather than broad expansion.
Marie Myers serves as executive vice president and chief financial officer, appointed in January 2024 following her role as CFO at HP Inc. Myers oversees HPE's financial operations, including budgeting, investor relations, and the integration of AI tools to enhance finance functions and support enterprise-wide AI adoption. Her strategic decisions have focused on aligning financial resources with HPE's AI transformation initiatives, piloting agentic AI applications to streamline processes.
Other pivotal executives include John Schultz, executive vice president and chief operating and legal officer, who manages operations, legal affairs, and administrative functions to ensure compliance and efficiency in HPE's global activities. Fidelma Russo, as chief technology officer, directs technical strategy, particularly in advancing AI and edge computing innovations aligned with Neri's vision. These leaders collectively steer HPE toward data-driven decision-making, prioritizing verifiable performance metrics like AI revenue acceleration over bureaucratic growth.

Board composition and shareholder dynamics

The Board of Directors of Hewlett Packard Enterprise comprises 11 members as of the 2025 proxy statement, with a majority of independent directors possessing extensive experience in technology, finance, and corporate governance. Chairwoman Patricia F. Russo, who assumed the role in November 2015 following the company's spin-off from Hewlett-Packard, leads the board alongside figures such as Raymond J. Lane, Charles H. Noski, and Robert Calderoni, the latter appointed in July 2025 to chair the newly formed Strategy Committee. The board operates through standing committees including Audit, which oversees financial reporting and risk management; Finance and Investment, focused on capital allocation; and the Strategy Committee, established in 2025 to evaluate long-term value creation initiatives.
Institutional investors dominate HPE's shareholder base, with Vanguard Group holding approximately 12.93% of shares outstanding as of mid-2025, followed by State Street Corporation at 5.6%. Insiders control about 0.31% of equity, reflecting limited direct executive ownership. Shareholder dynamics have featured activism, notably Elliott Management's April 2025 disclosure of a $1.5 billion stake, which prompted negotiations and a July 2025 cooperation agreement resulting in board additions and the Strategy Committee's creation to enhance accountability and strategic oversight. Earlier post-spin-off tensions in 2015-2016 involved proxy contests over governance amid the separation from HP Inc., underscoring ongoing pressures for board responsiveness to investor demands for value unlocking.
HPE's governance framework supports shareholder returns through a consistent dividend policy and repurchase programs, with the board declaring a quarterly dividend of $0.13 per share in September 2025, payable October 17, and announcing a 10% increase for fiscal 2026 alongside an additional $3 billion authorization for buybacks to deploy free cash flow. This approach aligns with oversight effectiveness, as evidenced by the company's return on equity of 17.7% for the fiscal 2025 third quarter ended July 31, 2025, a 0.3 percentage point improvement year-over-year, signaling disciplined capital stewardship under board supervision.

Business Operations

Core operating segments

Hewlett Packard Enterprise structures its operations across core segments including Server, Hybrid Cloud, Networking, and Financial Services, which collectively generate the bulk of its revenue through diversified offerings in enterprise IT infrastructure. The Server segment, centered on compute resources such as standard servers and high-performance computing systems optimized for AI workloads, produced $4.9 billion in revenue during the third quarter of fiscal year 2025 (ended July 31, 2025). This segment's performance reflects heightened demand for scalable processing capabilities amid expanding AI deployments.
The Networking segment, rebranded from Intelligent Edge in fiscal 2025 and augmented by the integration of Juniper Networks following its acquisition closure in the third quarter, generated $1.7 billion in revenue for the same period, accounting for nearly 50% of non-GAAP operating profit. Juniper's incorporation has yielded operational synergies, particularly in data center switching and routing, enhancing HPE's competitive positioning in enterprise and campus networking without altering segment reporting boundaries.
Hybrid Cloud, incorporating storage and related services, contributed $1.5 billion in third-quarter revenue, marking an 11% year-over-year increase driven by as-a-service consumption models. Financial Services complements these by offering leasing and asset management to facilitate customer acquisitions across segments, with revenue volumes correlating directly to capital equipment sales in Server and Hybrid Cloud. These segments underpin HPE's operational diversity, with Server and Networking comprising over 70% of quarterly revenue in the reported period.

Global footprint and supply chain

Hewlett Packard Enterprise maintains a global operational presence with offices in 287 cities across more than 100 countries, spanning the Americas, Europe, Middle East, Africa, and Asia-Pacific regions. Key manufacturing facilities include the Chippewa Falls campus in Wisconsin, United States, designated as a Global Center of Excellence for high-performance computing production since 2021, and a dedicated high-performance computing and AI factory in Kutn Hora, Czech Republic, under construction starting in 2023. These sites support assembly and customization of servers, storage, and networking hardware, complementing assembly operations in Asia to balance cost efficiencies with regional proximity to customers.
The company's supply chain is heavily integrated with Asian manufacturing hubs, particularly for components like semiconductors, where Taiwan holds over 90% market share in advanced nodes, creating exposure to geopolitical risks and disruptions. Shortages in the early 2020s, exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic and U.S.-China trade tensions, delayed HPE's hardware deliveries and underscored dependencies on Taiwanese foundries like TSMC, which produce critical chips for enterprise servers and edge devices. In response, HPE has diversified by expanding U.S. and European production capacities, aiming to shorten lead times through localized assembly and reduce vulnerability to trans-Pacific shipping delays or tariffs.
HPE employs approximately 61,000 people worldwide as of fiscal year 2024, distributed across R&D, manufacturing, and sales functions to support its edge-to-cloud operations. To address talent gaps in a competitive tech labor market, the company shifted to skills-based hiring in 2025, prioritizing demonstrated competencies over formal degrees to broaden recruitment pools and enhance workforce adaptability for AI and hybrid cloud demands. This approach, implemented amid ongoing supply chain pressures, facilitates faster integration of specialized skills in logistics and engineering, contributing to operational resilience without expanding headcount.

Products and Solutions

Hardware offerings (servers, storage, networking)

Hewlett Packard Enterprise's server portfolio centers on the ProLiant DL series, designed for high-performance computing (HPC) and artificial intelligence (AI) workloads in enterprise data centers. The HPE ProLiant DL380 Gen11, a 2U rack server, supports 4th and 5th Generation Intel Xeon Scalable processors with up to 64 cores per socket, up to 8 TB of DDR5 memory, and configurations including 8 SFF, 24 SFF, 8 LFF, 12 LFF, or 12 EDSFF chassis options for scalable storage density. These servers enable modular expansion in data centers, evolving from earlier HP-era rack designs to support denser, liquid-cooled options like the ProLiant XD685, which integrates NVIDIA H200 or Blackwell GPUs alongside AMD MI300X for AI training and inference at data center scale. Performance benchmarks show Gen11 models delivering up to 60% gains in workload efficiency over prior generations through enhanced processor power and memory bandwidth up to 5600 MT/s.
HPE's storage hardware includes all-flash arrays from the Nimble and 3PAR lines, optimized for enterprise reliability and low-latency access in hybrid environments. Nimble Storage arrays feature dual-controller designs with 10Gb Ethernet connectivity, enabling individual or clustered management for data reduction and predictive analytics at the hardware level, achieving latency reductions exceeding 96% in ERP applications compared to legacy systems. The HPE 3PAR 8450 and 9450 models provide tier-1 features such as thin provisioning and autonomic replication, supporting up to superfast IOPS for SAP HANA databases in scalable arrays that integrate with data-in-place upgrades to minimize disruption while enhancing capacity by up to 40%. These arrays emphasize hardware-level efficiency for block and file storage, with Remote Copy capabilities for disaster recovery across homogeneous or heterogeneous setups.
In networking hardware, HPE offers switches under the Aruba and Juniper brands, with the July 2025 completion of the Juniper Networks acquisition enabling integrated wired and wireless portfolio enhancements for AI-native operations. Aruba switches provide campus and data center connectivity with high-density port configurations, while Juniper's EX series Ethernet switches support scalable fabrics for enterprise backbones, now unified under HPE's go-forward branding for cross-pollination of hardware capabilities. Post-acquisition, these switches incorporate hardware optimizations for Mist AI-driven management, delivering verifiable throughput in multi-gigabit environments suitable for edge-to-cloud data flows, though specific benchmarks emphasize combined portfolio density over standalone metrics. This evolution shifts from legacy HP networking to modular, disaggregated designs that scale for AI workloads in distributed data centers.

Software, services, and GreenLake platform

Hewlett Packard Enterprise's cloud computing strategy focuses on hybrid cloud through the HPE GreenLake platform, providing as-a-service solutions that integrate on-premises, private, and public clouds with strong AI capabilities for edge-to-cloud environments. HPE GreenLake delivers a hybrid cloud platform-as-a-service model that provides IT infrastructure consumption on a pay-per-use basis, enabling organizations to scale resources dynamically while maintaining control over data sovereignty and avoiding large upfront investments. The platform supports integration with third-party public clouds such as AWS and Azure, facilitating workload portability and hybrid environments that optimize costs by matching consumption to actual demand rather than fixed capacity.
Key software components include HPE InfoSight, an AI-powered operations platform that applies machine learning to infrastructure data across servers, storage, and networking, predicting failures and automating resolutions to minimize downtime. InfoSight processes telemetry from millions of sensors to deliver proactive insights, resolving up to 86% of issues autonomously and supporting self-optimizing data centers in hybrid setups.
HPE's professional services encompass advisory consulting, deployment support, and managed operations tailored to GreenLake implementations, including assessments for cloud migration, architecture design, and ongoing optimization to align IT with business outcomes. These services assist in integrating GreenLake with existing ecosystems, providing expertise in areas like governance, compliance, and cost management to accelerate time-to-value.
GreenLake's annualized recurring revenue (ARR) exceeded $2 billion as of October 2025, serving more than 44,000 customers amid rising demand for flexible, edge-to-cloud services, with rapid growth including a 46% year-over-year increase as of Q1 2025. In the second quarter of fiscal year 2025 (ended April 2025), ARR grew 47% year-over-year to over $2.2 billion, with customer count reaching approximately 42,000, underscoring efficiencies from metered usage that lower total cost of ownership by up to 30-50% relative to perpetual licensing models in comparable deployments; this growth reflects significant AI momentum, including $1.6 billion in new orders and $8.3 billion cumulatively through strong NVIDIA partnerships, 11% year-over-year hybrid cloud revenue growth, improved margins, and leadership in enterprise AI, HPC, and edge computing via turnkey solutions and the subscription model shift.

AI, edge, and hybrid cloud innovations

HPE Ezmeral Container Platform integrates open-source Kubernetes orchestration with dedicated AI and machine learning workflows, allowing enterprises to containerize data science pipelines for model training, deployment, and inference directly on clustered infrastructure. Introduced in versions supporting AI/ML interfaces since 2020 and enhanced through 2025, it enables multi-tenant Kubernetes environments that scale for both cloud-native apps and AI workloads, reducing dependency on siloed tools by unifying container management across hybrid setups.
In edge computing, HPE deploys Edgeline Converged Edge Systems and ProLiant servers optimized for IoT and 5G deployments, processing data at the source to minimize latency for applications like real-time analytics in retail and industrial settings. These solutions, integrated with Aruba Networking for AI-powered edge orchestration, support private 5G networks and cellular connectivity, enabling robust coverage in operational environments without relying on centralized clouds; for instance, partnerships like Infosys-HPE deliver flexible private 5G for demanding IoT use cases as of early 2025.
HPE's hybrid cloud innovations bridge on-premises sovereignty with public cloud scalability via composable architectures that avoid full vendor lock-in, allowing workload portability and data control through integrated management layers. This approach counters the risks of public-only models by maintaining private infrastructure for sensitive operations while leveraging burst capacity elsewhere, as evidenced in unified platforms launched in May 2025 for MSPs and enterprises.
Advancements in 2025 include AI factory blueprints co-engineered with NVIDIA, featuring Blackwell GPUs in systems like HPE Compute XD690 for agentic and generative AI, available from October 2025, which prioritize secure, power-efficient scaling in private environments to foster enterprise autonomy over hyperscaler dependencies. HPE Private Cloud AI, validated against NVIDIA's Enterprise AI Factory design, supports full-stack deployments for physical AI models, driving $1.6 billion in AI systems revenue by Q3 fiscal 2025 through turnkey infrastructure that emphasizes verifiable performance and data residency.

Mergers, Acquisitions, and Divestitures

Major acquisitions including Juniper Networks

Hewlett Packard Enterprise has completed 31 acquisitions as of October 2025, strategically targeting enhancements in high-performance computing, networking, and edge-to-cloud capabilities. These moves have aimed to integrate specialized technologies that address data-intensive workloads and AI-driven infrastructure demands, with post-acquisition synergies contributing to revenue growth in core segments like networking and supercomputing.
The most significant acquisition was Juniper Networks, completed on July 2, 2025, for approximately $14 billion in an all-cash deal at $40 per share. This transaction, announced in January 2024, combined HPE's Aruba networking portfolio with Juniper's AI-native routing and switching technologies to create a comprehensive cloud-native, AI-optimized networking solution, positioning HPE to compete in enterprise AI infrastructure despite initial U.S. Department of Justice delays resolved via settlement. HPE anticipates at least $600 million in cost and operational synergies over three years from the deal, with one-third reinvested into AI-native innovations to drive networking revenue expansion.
Earlier, HPE acquired Cray Inc. on September 25, 2019, for $1.3 billion, bolstering its high-performance computing (HPC) offerings with Cray's expertise in exascale supercomputers and data analytics systems. The deal integrated Cray's Shasta architecture into HPE's portfolio, enabling scalable solutions for AI training and scientific simulations, and strengthening HPE's position in government and research contracts requiring extreme computational power.
In 2020, HPE purchased Silver Peak Systems for $925 million, finalized on September 21, to advance its software-defined wide area network (SD-WAN) capabilities within the Aruba edge services platform. This acquisition merged Silver Peak's Unity EdgeConnect with Aruba's wired and wireless technologies, facilitating secure, high-performance connectivity for hybrid cloud environments and accelerating HPE's edge-to-cloud strategy amid rising demand for remote work infrastructure.

Divestitures and strategic restructurings

In September 2017, Hewlett Packard Enterprise completed the divestiture of its non-core software business through a spin-off and merger with Micro Focus International, valued at approximately $8.8 billion, which included assets such as BSM, ITOM, and Data Center Automation. HPE initially retained a 51% ownership stake in the combined entity, allowing it to monetize underperforming segments while refocusing on core infrastructure offerings like servers, storage, and networking. This transaction reduced operational complexity and freed capital for investments in enterprise IT hardware and emerging technologies.
Subsequent strategic divestitures included the agreement in June 2025 to sell HPE's global Instant On campus and branch wireless LAN business as part of regulatory approvals, aiming to eliminate overlapping assets and sharpen competitive positioning in enterprise networking. These moves aligned with HPE's broader strategy to exit low-margin or non-strategic units, prioritizing scalable solutions in hybrid cloud and edge computing.
To enhance efficiency and concentrate on high-growth domains such as AI-driven infrastructure, HPE undertook significant restructurings, including a March 2025 workforce reduction of about 2,500 employeesroughly 5% of its total headcounttargeted at streamlining operations amid supply chain pressures and market shifts in AI servers. Earlier efforts in 2023 involved organizational realignments to cut redundant roles and accelerate innovation in edge-to-cloud architectures, yielding cost savings that supported reallocation toward R&D in AI and intelligent edge platforms. These initiatives improved resource allocation, enabling HPE to pursue higher-margin activities without diluting focus on core competencies.

Financial Performance and Market Position

Revenue trends, profitability, and key metrics

Following its 2015 spin-off from Hewlett-Packard Company, Hewlett Packard Enterprise's annual revenue initially stood at approximately $30.3 billion for fiscal year 2016 before stabilizing in the $27-30 billion range through fiscal 2024, reflecting challenges in legacy enterprise hardware amid competition and shifts to cloud services. Fiscal 2024 revenue totaled $29.1 billion, with trailing twelve-month revenue as of July 31, 2025, reaching $33.075 billion, a 13.97% year-over-year increase attributable to emerging demand in high-margin areas. This trend reversed prior stagnation, where revenue growth averaged low single digits or declined due to softening in traditional storage and on-premises infrastructure sales.
Fiscal 2025 marked accelerated revenue expansion, driven by AI systems demand offsetting legacy segment weaknesses such as storage softness. Third-quarter revenue (ended July 31, 2025) hit $9.14 billion, up 18% year-over-year and surpassing analyst estimates of $8.53 billion, fueled by AI server shipments and networking strength post-Juniper Networks acquisition closure. Net new AI orders exceeded $2 billion for the quarter, contributing to a record AI backlog of $3.7 billion and sovereign AI deals up over 200% year-over-year. HPE projects full fiscal 2025 revenue growth of 14-16% in constant currency, signaling sustained momentum from AI but vulnerability to delays in order fulfillment or macroeconomic pressures on non-AI products.
Profitability metrics have shown gradual improvement amid revenue upticks, though margins remain pressured by acquisition-related integration costs and R&D investments. Non-GAAP operating margins expanded in fiscal 2025's third quarter, supporting GAAP diluted net earnings per share of $0.44, above the $0.43 consensus estimate. Net profit margin stood at 3.44% trailing twelve months, below the five-year average due to historical legacy declines, while return on equity (ROE) registered 5.06% as of October 2025, reflecting efficient capital deployment in AI but moderated by debt from deals like Juniper. HPE anticipates operating profit growth in the 11-17% compound annual growth rate range through fiscal 2027, predicated on AI revenue scaling and cost controls, though execution risks persist from supply chain constraints in high-demand compute.

Following the completion of its $14 billion acquisition of Juniper Networks on July 2, 2025, HPE shares reached a record high of $26.44 on October 8, 2025, reflecting initial market optimism about expanded networking capabilities. However, shares declined approximately 10% on October 16, 2025, after the company issued fiscal year 2026 earnings per share (EPS) guidance of $2.20 to $2.40, below analyst consensus estimates, amid concerns over slower networking growth post-acquisition, with some analysts viewing HPE's post-Juniper trajectory as a "show-me" story requiring demonstration of integration success and profitability improvements. The stock exhibits elevated volatility, with a five-year beta of 1.46 relative to the broader market, influenced by cyclical demand in enterprise hardware and services.
HPE maintains a regular quarterly dividend of $0.13 per share, equating to an annual payout of $0.52 and a yield of approximately 2.21% as of September 2025. The board increased the dividend by 10% in October 2025, signaling confidence in sustained cash flows despite integration challenges from the Juniper deal. Complementing dividends, HPE has pursued share repurchases, authorizing an additional $3 billion in October 2025, bringing total capacity to support ongoing buybacks that have historically boosted EPS by reducing outstanding shares. Since fiscal 2017, the company has returned over $12 billion to shareholders through dividends and repurchases, representing 120% of free cash flow generated in that period.
Cumulative total shareholder returns for HPE have trailed the S&P 500 over the long term, with a 10-year underperformance noted amid shifts in technology spending cycles. Competitive positioning and industry benchmarks

Hewlett Packard Enterprise competes as a major player in the server market alongside Dell Technologies, Lenovo, and IBM, with an approximate 13% global market share in early 2025 amid surging AI demand that drove overall server revenue growth of 91% year-over-year in Q4 2024. Dell maintains leadership at around 19%, while HPE has achieved notable revenue surges, including 29% growth in servers, positioning it strongly in AI-optimized systems projected to reach $252 billion in market value by the end of 2025.
In networking, HPE's Aruba division held roughly 15.9% share in enterprise segments like Ethernet switches and Wi-Fi as of late 2024, trailing Cisco's dominant 35-46% across wired and wireless infrastructure. The July 2025 acquisition of Juniper Networks doubled HPE's networking revenue base and tripled its total addressable market to approximately $135 billion, enabling better competition with Cisco through enhanced AI-native and cloud-optimized portfolios that yielded 54% year-over-year networking revenue growth in Q3 2025.
HPE's hybrid cloud strategy via GreenLake provides a key differentiator against public cloud giants like AWS, which holds 31% of the overall cloud infrastructure market but prioritizes off-premises services over seamless on-premises integration. HPE positions as a leader in hybrid cloud and enterprise AI/HPC, serving over 41,000 enterprises via GreenLake and excelling in on-premises/hybrid solutions, though it trails public cloud giants in overall cloud market share. This approach targets enterprises preferring hybrid models for data sovereignty and latency-sensitive workloads at the edge, where pure-cloud providers encounter adoption barriers. Weaknesses of the strategy encompass intense competition from hyperscalers (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) and rivals (Dell, Cisco), GPU supply constraints, acquisition integration risks (e.g., Juniper), and vulnerability to rapid tech changes and macroeconomic factors.

Controversies, Criticisms, and Legal Challenges

Autonomy acquisition fallout and fraud litigation

In October 2011, Hewlett-Packard acquired the UK-based software company Autonomy Corporation for approximately $11.1 billion in cash, a deal intended to bolster HP's enterprise software capabilities. Shortly after the acquisition, HP discovered accounting irregularities at Autonomy, leading to allegations that the company had inflated its revenues through improper practices, including the resale of hardware at a discount to resellers while recognizing the full resale value as software revenue, and backdated contracts to meet quarterly targets. These methods, HP claimed, created a misleading picture of Autonomy's financial health, contributing to an overvaluation.
On November 20, 2012, HP announced an $8.8 billion writedown on the Autonomy acquisition, with about $5 billion attributed directly to "serious accounting improprieties, misrepresentation and disclosure failures" at Autonomy, marking one of the largest impairment charges in corporate history and exacerbating HP's financial and leadership turmoil at the time. The writedown prompted lawsuits from HP against Autonomy's former executives, including founder Mike Lynch and finance director Sushovan Hussain, accusing them of orchestrating the fraud to inflate the company's value ahead of the sale.
In a UK civil trial concluding in January 2022, the High Court ruled that Lynch and Hussain had engaged in fraudulent misrepresentation, finding that Autonomy's practices, such as artificial revenue inflation via hardware deals and reseller transactions, deceived HP into overpaying; the court described the misconduct as directed toward meeting revenue targets but noted it did not extend to all aspects of the business. However, in a parallel US criminal trial, Lynch was acquitted on all 15 counts of wire fraud and conspiracy in June 2024 by a San Francisco federal jury, which found insufficient evidence of intent to defraud under criminal standards, despite the civil findings in the UK. Separately, Autonomy's auditor Deloitte faced scrutiny for failing to challenge questionable hardware transactions and reseller deals, resulting in a record 15 million fine from the UK's Financial Reporting Council in September 2020 for "serious and serial failures" in its audits from 2009 to 2010, including loss of objectivity and inadequate testing of revenue recognition.
The litigation culminated in a July 2025 UK High Court quantum judgment awarding Hewlett Packard Enterprise (HPE), as HP's successor entity holding the Autonomy assets, approximately 702 million (equivalent to about $940 million) in damages from Lynch's estatefollowing his death in August 2024and Hussain, representing the portion of losses directly attributable to the fraud after deductions for what HP would have paid absent the misrepresentations; this fell short of HP's initial $5 billion claim but affirmed substantial harm from inadequate due diligence on Autonomy's accounts. The case highlighted systemic risks in high-value tech M&A, including overreliance on audited financials without deeper forensic review of revenue streams like hardware resales, and exposed lapses in both target company auditing and acquirer verification processes.

Internal practices disputes (sales, compensation, pay equity)

In 2017, Hewlett Packard Enterprise faced significant internal disruptions in its sales compensation processes due to failures in the "MyComp" software system used to track commissions, resulting in miscalculations for numerous salespeople, with some individuals reporting shortfalls of $40,000 to $50,000. The system inaccuracies stemmed from inadequate handling of complex sales data post the 2015 corporate split from HP Inc., leading to delayed and erroneous payouts that frustrated employees and prompted HPE to issue public apologies while scrambling to implement fixes. These issues contributed to broader critiques of high-pressure sales quotas, where aggressive targets incentivized performance but were undermined by administrative errors, though no evidence emerged of intentional quota manipulation by HPE itself.
The compensation disputes escalated into litigation, culminating in a 2019 class-action settlement where HPE, alongside HP Inc., agreed to pay $25 million to approximately 2,000 current and former employees alleging faulty commission calculations from the problematic tracking systems. The settlement, which did not include an admission of liability, addressed claims dating back to the post-split transition period and highlighted tensions between quota-driven incentivesintended to reward high achieversand operational breakdowns that eroded trust in payout reliability.
On pay equity, HPE encountered allegations in a 2019 California lawsuit filed by two former female employees, who claimed the company systematically underpaid women compared to similarly situated male counterparts in roles such as sales and technical positions, potentially violating state equal pay laws. The suit further accused HPE of discouraging pay discussions among employees, though internal data accessed by plaintiffs suggested disparities in base and incentive pay. In 2022, HPE settled the case for $8.5 million without admitting wrongdoing, distributing payments to affected female employees in California; subsequent company audits and disclosures indicated that adjusted pay gaps were minimal after accounting for factors like performance and tenure, with no findings of intentional systemic bias. These resolutions underscore quota pressures as a common incentive structure rather than evidence of discriminatory practices, as settlements focused on procedural remedies over liability admissions.

Regulatory hurdles and antitrust issues

In January 2025, the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ) filed a lawsuit to block Hewlett Packard Enterprise's (HPE) proposed $14 billion acquisition of Juniper Networks, primarily citing anticompetitive effects in the enterprise wireless local area network (WLAN) market. The DOJ argued that the merger would eliminate direct rivalry between HPE and Juniper in providing WLAN solutions, potentially leading to higher prices, reduced innovation, and fewer choices for customers, as the companies had competed aggressively on features like AI-driven networking. 
HPE and Juniper strongly opposed the DOJ's action, asserting that the acquisition would enhance competition against dominant players like Cisco and foreign rivals such as Huawei, thereby accelerating innovation in AI-native networking without substantially harming consumers. They committed to litigating the case, warning that blocking the deal could stifle technological advancement in a rapidly evolving sector where scale is essential for R&D investment. Post-merger market share estimates for HPE and Juniper in broader enterprise WLAN segments were projected at 22-26%, below the 2023 Merger Guidelines' presumptive threshold of 30%, suggesting the DOJ's concerns overstated concentration risks.
The challenge was resolved through a settlement announced on June 28, 2025, just before a scheduled trial, requiring HPE to divest its Instant On WLAN business for campus and branch networks and license the source code for Juniper's Mist AI software to independent competitors. This allowed the acquisition to close on July 2, 2025, enabling HPE to integrate Juniper's portfolio and pursue enhanced AI-driven offerings, which company statements positioned as strengthening global competitiveness.
Broader regulatory scrutiny in wireless mergers highlights risks of overreach, as seen in Federal Communications Commission (FCC) settlements for deals like T-Mobile's acquisition of Sprint, where extensive conditions were imposed despite evidence of post-merger efficiencies in network expansion and 5G deployment. Such interventions can reflect capture dynamics, where entrenched interests influence outcomes to preserve status quo market structures at the expense of dynamic competition and innovation. In the HPE-Juniper case, the swift settlement and subsequent closure empirically demonstrated that targeted remedies could address concerns without derailing pro-competitive consolidations.

Broader critiques of corporate strategy

Hewlett Packard Enterprise's strategy has drawn criticism for emphasizing acquisitions over sustained organic growth, potentially exposing the firm to integration risks and diluting focus on internal innovation. The company's acquisition history, including the $14 billion purchase of Juniper Networks in 2025, has been scrutinized by Democratic attorneys general who argued that antitrust approvals under the prior administration overlooked competitive harms in networking markets. While such deals enable rapid capability expansion in areas like AI infrastructure, outcomes reveal mixed results, with organic server revenue growth noted in quarters like July 2025 but overshadowed by broader execution challenges prompting activist intervention. Defenders, including management, contend that acquisitions complement organic efforts by accelerating market entry, as evidenced by revenue upticks in hybrid cloud segments despite headwinds.
The firm's 2025 workforce reductions, targeting 5% of its approximately 61,000 employees or 2,500 positions over 12-18 months, have been framed as efficiency measures to generate $350 million in annual savings amid macroeconomic pressures and tariff uncertainties. Management positions these cuts as necessary for reallocating resources toward high-growth areas like AI, aligning with merit-based reforms that prioritize skills over headcount in competitive tech environments. Critics, including labor advocates, counter that such actions erode institutional knowledge and morale, potentially hindering adaptability in volatile markets, though empirical post-layoff performance shows stabilized operations without immediate revenue collapse.
HPE's transition to an as-a-service model through GreenLake has faced scrutiny for eroding margins, with server operating profits falling to 5.9% in Q2 2025roughly half prior levelsdue to hardware pricing commoditization and unfavorable product mixes. Adjusted gross margins also declined to 31.8% in recent quarters, below expectations, raising questions about fiduciary oversight in pursuing lower-margin recurring revenue over traditional hardware sales. Activist investors like Elliott Management have amplified these concerns, securing a 2025 board agreement for a strategy committee to evaluate business units and enhance returns, underscoring perceived lapses in balancing growth with profitability. Yet, the model's expansion has yielded recurring revenue scaling, defending its viability by matching enterprise demands for consumption-based IT amid cloud shifts.

Sustainability and Societal Impact

Environmental initiatives and carbon metrics

Hewlett Packard Enterprise tracks greenhouse gas emissions across Scope 1 (direct), Scope 2 (indirect from purchased energy), and Scope 3 (value chain) categories to inform its decarbonization strategy. The company has set science-based targets for net-zero emissions by 2040, including a 70% reduction in Scope 1 and 2 emissions and a 42% reduction in Scope 3 emissions by 2030, measured against a 2020 baseline.
In its 2024 Living Progress Report, released June 9, 2025, HPE reported a 13% year-over-year reduction in overall carbon footprint and a 15% decrease in carbon intensity (emissions relative to revenue), while absolute emissions remained just 2% above the 2020 baseline despite 12% net revenue growth. Operational emissions, primarily Scope 1 and 2, fell 34% from the 2020 baseline, driven by expanded renewable electricity procurement, which reached over 50% of total usage in 2024surpassing the 2025 target a year early. Absolute emissions rose 8% in 2023, attributed to business expansion and shifts in product composition that increased energy demands.
Data centers, key contributors to Scope 2 and 3 emissions due to their energy-intensive operations, are targeted for efficiency improvements to offset growth in computing needs. HPE's fanless direct liquid cooling technology cuts cooling power requirements by 37% and reduces data center floor space by half, while ProLiant Gen12 servers deliver up to 87% annual power savings compared to prior generations. The company's systems earned 118 entries on the Green500 list of energy-efficient supercomputers in 2024, with eight in the top 15, demonstrating performance-per-watt gains that lower carbon intensity amid rising technological demands. These initiatives highlight efficiency-driven reductions counterbalancing absolute increases from sector expansion.

Social responsibility efforts

Hewlett Packard Enterprise released its third annual Human Rights Report on May 30, 2025, outlining advancements in safeguarding rights across operations and the supply chain, guided by its Global Human Rights Policy aligned with the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights. The document details due diligence evaluations incorporating geographic risks, ethical considerations, and stakeholder engagement to mitigate impacts on individuals, including expanded supply chain risk assessments that identify high-risk areas and remediation actions.
In talent acquisition, HPE implemented skills-based hiring in early 2025, emphasizing verified competencies over degree credentials to access a wider candidate pool and reduce barriers tied to formal education, which has historically favored certain demographics. This approach aims to enhance merit-driven selection, with internal metrics tracking improved hire quality and retention, though long-term diversity outcomes depend on consistent application beyond entry levels.
DEI initiatives at HPE, led by Chief Diversity Officer Aisha Washington, include data-driven tracking of representation and equity metrics, as highlighted in the company's 2024 Living Progress Report released June 9, 2025, which positions DEI alongside human rights as a core social impact pillar. CEO Antonio Neri has reaffirmed commitment to these programs, contrasting with peers curtailing similar efforts, citing progress in employee resource groups and inclusion training. Yet, self-reported data reveals persistent gaps, such as underrepresented groups comprising under 30% of senior leadership as of fiscal 2024, prompting critiques that such initiatives risk prioritizing demographic targets over performance, potentially yielding performative rather than substantive inclusion absent rigorous, independent efficacy audits.
Supplier oversight integrates social protections via mandatory audits and contractual adherence to HPE's Supplier Social and Environmental Responsibility Requirements, covering labor rights and ethical sourcing for over 1,000 direct suppliers as of 2025, with remediation rates exceeding 90% for identified violations in high-risk regions. These metrics demonstrate value chain advancements, but reliance on self-disclosed compliance raises questions about verification depth, as third-party validations cover only a fraction of the extended network. Overall, while HPE reports year-over-year gains in training reach (e.g., 95% employee coverage on human rights) and audit coverage, the absence of external benchmarks limits claims of transformative impact, underscoring tensions between reported achievements and demands for evidence-based outcomes in DEI and rights protections.

Balanced assessment of ESG claims versus business realities

Hewlett Packard Enterprise has pursued ESG initiatives, including a 13% year-over-year reduction in overall carbon footprint and a 34% drop in operational emissions from the 2020 baseline, as detailed in its 2024 Living Progress Report. The company claims these efforts, such as deploying energy-efficient servers yielding up to 87% annual power savings, align with business benefits like cost reductions in power and cooling. However, ESG reporting and compliance impose substantial overhead; tech firms often incur annual costs exceeding $500,000 for external audits and data collection alone, diverting resources from core operations like R&D in hardware and edge computing.
Empirical analyses reveal neutral to negative correlations between ESG emphasis and stock performance in tech sectors amid market volatility from 2023 to 2025, with initiatives sometimes hindering raw returns due to regulatory and disclosure burdens. For heavily regulated or disclosure-heavy firms, ESG policies have been shown to inhibit enterprise value by increasing operational complexity without commensurate financial uplift. While HPE reported 12% net revenue growth in 2024 alongside ESG progress, its stock delivered only a 14% total shareholder return over longer terms, underperforming broader AI-driven tech surges and highlighting limited ESG-driven alpha in competitive, capital-intensive markets.
From a causal standpoint, sustained profitability underpins genuine long-term sustainability, enabling reinvestment in efficient technologies rather than performative metrics; HPE's explicit shift toward cost optimization for profitability in 2025 underscores this, as unprofitable operations cannot viably fund emissions reductions or innovation. Overemphasis on non-financial ESG targets risks eroding competitive edges in high-margin areas like AI infrastructure, where empirical evidence prioritizes operational excellence over symbolic reporting. Mainstream endorsements of ESG often overlook these trade-offs, favoring narrative alignment over verifiable shareholder value in data-centric industries.
