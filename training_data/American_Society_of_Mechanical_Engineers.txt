 The American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME) is a not-for-profit professional organization founded on February 16, 1880, in New York City by leading industrialists including Alexander Lyman Holley, Henry R. Worthington, and John Edson Sweet, to provide a forum for mechanical engineers to address the challenges of rapid industrialization and mechanization. With a mission to "advance engineering for the benefit of humanity," ASME has grown into a global network focused on promoting the art, science, and practice of multidisciplinary engineering and allied sciences.
As of 2024, ASME has over 75,000 members, including more than 19,000 students, in over 130 countries, offering benefits such as access to technical resources, professional development, and networking opportunities through local sections and divisions. The organization is renowned for developing and maintaining consensus-based codes and standards that ensure safety and reliability in engineering applications; its first standard, the "Code for the Conduct of Trials of Steam Boilers," was issued in 1884, followed by the landmark Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code (BPVC) in 1914, which became the first comprehensive rules for the design, construction, inspection, and repair of boilers and pressure vessels.
Beyond standards, ASME drives innovation through extensive publications, including over 30 peer-reviewed journals and magazines like Mechanical Engineering; annual conferences and events exceeding 40 in number; and continuing education programs with more than 200 technical courses and master classes. The society also engages in research, advocacy, and recognition efforts, such as designating historic mechanical engineering landmarks and awarding prestigious honors to advance technical knowledge and public safety worldwide.

History

Founding

The American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME) was established on February 16, 1880, in New York City by a group of approximately 30 leading engineers who gathered at the editorial offices of American Machinist for the inaugural meeting. This founding responded to the explosive growth of industrialization in the United States during the late 19th century, particularly the widespread adoption of steam power technologies that powered factories, railroads, and ships. The era's rapid mechanization brought significant engineering challenges, including inconsistent manufacturing practices and a lack of standardized components, which hindered efficiency and innovation.
A primary motivation was the urgent need to mitigate safety risks, as frequent boiler explosionsoften resulting from poor design, inadequate materials, or operational errorsclaimed numerous lives and threatened public confidence in mechanical engineering. Engineers recognized that professional collaboration was essential to share technical knowledge, develop best practices, and foster advancements in areas like machine tools and pressure vessels. Key figures in the founding included Alexander Lyman Holley, a pioneering steelmaker and machine tool expert who chaired the first meeting; John E. Sweet, an inventor and educator who helped organize the preliminary discussions; Henry Rossiter Worthington, a pump manufacturer; and Robert Henry Thurston, a prominent academic and the society's first president, serving from 1880 to 1882.
Initial meetings emphasized practical problem-solving, with discussions centered on standardizing tools, machine parts, and work methods to address the era's engineering hurdles and promote safer, more reliable systems. The society's formal chartering occurred on April 7, 1880, at Stevens Institute of Technology in Hoboken, New Jersey, where early activities and headquarters were based, reflecting the close ties between ASME and academic institutions dedicated to mechanical engineering education. These foundational efforts laid the groundwork for ASME's role in advancing professional standards, though detailed standardization initiatives emerged later.

Key Milestones

In 1914, ASME issued the first edition of its Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code (BPVC), a landmark response to escalating industrial accidents involving steam boilers and pressure vessels, establishing comprehensive rules for their design, construction, inspection, and safety to protect workers and the public. This 114-page document was published in 1915, marking the society's shift toward proactive standardization amid rapid industrialization.
During World War I, ASME experienced significant growth in its technical committees and membership as engineers mobilized for national defense, exemplified by the 1918 formation of a Special Technical Committee on Screw Threads to address wartime production challenges in munitions and machinery. In World War II, the society further expanded its influence through accelerated standardization efforts, including 1942 revisions to pressure-temperature ratings for steel pipe flanges that conserved critical materials for military applications like aircraft and naval vessels, while the BPVC saw heightened adoption to support the surge in pressure vessel manufacturing for the war effort.
The post-war era brought ASME's international expansion in the 1950s and 1960s, with the establishment of sections in Europe and Asia to foster global collaboration among mechanical engineers, alongside the BPVC's adoption in countries like Canada (as early as 1938) and broader conformity assessment programs reaching worldwide by 1972. This period reflected ASME's growing role in addressing transnational engineering challenges, such as nuclear power standards under Section III of the BPVC, first published in 1963.
ASME marked its centennial in 1980 with a series of commemorative events and the publication of A Centennial History of the American Society of Mechanical Engineers, 1880-1980, which highlighted the organization's evolution and reaffirmed its commitment to professional ethics amid technological advancements.
In the 2020s, ASME underwent a digital transformation accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic, pivoting to virtual events that not only maintained but increased participationsuch as E-Fest Digital in 2020while enhancing online resources for standards access and professional development. By 2025, the society updated its membership outreach initiatives, including the Professional Section Recognition Awards announced in September to honor exemplary engagement and programming, and expanded media tools like the Integrated Media Planner to bolster global member connections.

Organization and Governance

Structure

The American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME) maintains its headquarters at Two Park Avenue in New York City, New York, serving as the central hub for its administrative, publishing, and standards development activities. This location coordinates the society's global operations, including oversight of technical programs and member services. In addition to the New York headquarters, ASME operates regional offices in Washington, D.C., at 1828 L Street N.W., Suite 406, which focuses on government relations and public policy advocacy, and maintains other U.S. facilities such as a service center in Livingston, New Jersey, a Connecticut office in Stamford, and an office in Houston, Texas, supporting the International Petroleum Technology Institute. Internationally, ASME has branches in Beijing, China, and Gurgaon, India, to facilitate regional engagement, standards adoption, and educational initiatives in those markets. Although specific details on an Atlanta office are not prominently documented in current public records, ASME's presence in the southeastern U.S. is supported through active local sections and event hosting in the region.
ASME is governed by a Board of Governors, elected from its membership, which holds ultimate authority over the society's strategic direction, financial management, and policy decisions. This board oversees a decentralized structure that includes 37 technical divisions organized around specialized engineering fields, such as the Bioengineering Division, which advances research in biological systems and medical devices, and the Fluids Engineering Division, focused on fluid dynamics, multiphase flows, and related applications. These divisions foster technical exchange through conferences, journals, and committees, enabling members to contribute to cutting-edge advancements in mechanical engineering subdisciplines. Complementing the technical divisions are over 600 local sections worldwide, encompassing both professional and student chapters, which provide grassroots networking, professional development events, and community outreach at regional and institutional levels.
As a non-profit organization classified under Section 501(c)(3) of the U.S. Internal Revenue Code, ASME operates to promote the art, science, and practice of mechanical engineering without distributing profits to private individuals. Its primary revenue streams include membership dues, which support access to resources for over 75,000 members as of 2024; sales of standards and codes, a cornerstone of its mission generating significant income through licensing and distribution; and fees from events such as conferences, continuing education courses, and certification programs. These sources ensure financial sustainability while funding initiatives in education, research, and public welfare.
Central to ASME's operational framework are several key standing committees that address core functions. The Standards Committee, encompassing various subcommittees and boards like the Board on Standardization and Testing, develops and maintains over 600 consensus-based codes and standards used globally in industries such as pressure technology and manufacturing. The Ethics Committee, reporting to the Committee of Past Presidents, enforces the ASME Code of Ethics, investigates violations of professional conduct, and provides interpretive guidance to ensure integrity in engineering practice. Similarly, the Education Committee coordinates programs to enhance engineering curricula, supports faculty development, and administers scholarships and accreditation efforts to elevate mechanical engineering education worldwide. These committees, composed of volunteer experts, integrate with the Board of Governors to uphold ASME's commitment to ethical, innovative, and impactful engineering. Leadership roles within this structure, such as those filled by the president and executive director, are elected to guide day-to-day implementation.

Leadership

The leadership of the American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME) is structured to ensure effective governance through elected and appointed roles that guide the organization's strategic direction and operations. The president serves as the chief elected officer, elected annually by ASME members for a one-year term, providing visionary leadership and representing the society in external affairs. As of 2025, Dr. Lester K. Su, a professor of mechanical engineering at Stanford University, holds the position of the 144th president, succeeding Susan Ipri-Brown whose term ended in June 2025.
The executive director/CEO manages the society's daily operations, staff, and implementation of board policies, reporting to the Board of Governors. Thomas Costabile, P.E., has served in this role since 2018, overseeing administrative functions, financial management, and program execution to advance ASME's mission in mechanical engineering.
The Board of Governors constitutes ASME's primary governing body, comprising 12 voting members elected from the membership, including the president and past presidents, along with the executive director/CEO as a non-voting ex-officio member; it also includes sector representatives to ensure diverse input on technical, professional, and public policy matters. Board members serve staggered three-year terms, fostering continuity and expertise in decision-making.
ASME's leadership rotates across sectors to reflect the breadth of mechanical engineering, drawing presidents and governors from academia (e.g., Lester K. Su), industry (e.g., Susan Ipri-Brown, formerly at Southwest Research Institute), and occasionally government-affiliated roles, promoting balanced perspectives on innovation, standards, and professional development.

Standards Development

Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code

The ASME Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code (BPVC) originated from efforts to address frequent boiler explosions in the early 20th century, which posed significant risks to public safety. The BPVC was developed in response to numerous boiler failures, with the committee formed in 1911 following incidents like the 1905 Grover Shoe Factory explosion in Massachusetts that killed 58 people, leading to uniform standards for boiler construction. The first edition was published in 1914, initially focusing on rules for power boilers, and marked a pivotal shift toward regulated engineering practices to prevent failures.
Today, the BPVC comprises 33 volumes organized into 12 main sections, updated biennially to incorporate advancements in technology and safety requirements, with the 2025 edition (released July 1, 2025) reflecting revisions based on industry feedback and research, including updates to Section X for advanced composite materials and code cases for additive manufacturing qualification. It establishes comprehensive rules for the design, fabrication, inspection, testing, and certification of boilers and pressure vessels operating at pressures exceeding 15 psig (103 kPa), ensuring structural integrity under various conditions. Adopted or referenced in over 100 countries, including all U.S. states and Canadian provinces, the code serves as a global benchmark for regulatory compliance in industries such as power generation, petrochemicals, and manufacturing.
Among its key components, Section I provides rules for the construction of power boilers, specifying requirements for materials, fabrication, and installation to withstand high-temperature and high-pressure environments. Section VIII, dedicated to pressure vessels, outlines design criteria including allowable stresses, safety factors typically set at 3.5 for tensile strength in Division 1, and nondestructive examination methods to detect defects. Section II details material specifications, covering ferrous and nonferrous alloys with precise chemical and mechanical property requirements to ensure reliability. These elements collectively mitigate risks by mandating conservative safety margins and rigorous quality controls.
The BPVC's implementation has historically transformed safety outcomes, dramatically reducing boiler explosions and related fatalities in its initial decades by standardizing practices that addressed common failure modes like material weaknesses and improper design. Ongoing revisions continue to adapt to emerging technologies, such as incorporating rules for composite materials in pressure vessel applications to accommodate lightweight, corrosion-resistant alternatives in modern industries like aerospace and renewable energy.

Other Standardization Efforts

Beyond the Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code, ASME develops more than 600 codes and standards addressing a wide array of mechanical engineering disciplines, including elevator and escalator safety, piping systems, and construction equipment. For instance, ASME A17.1 establishes safety requirements for elevators and escalators, covering design, installation, operation, and maintenance to protect public welfare. Similarly, the B31 series of codes governs piping systems for various applications, such as process piping and power plants, ensuring integrity under pressure and environmental stresses.
The development of these standards follows a consensus-based process managed by volunteer technical committees composed of industry experts, regulators, and academics, with procedures accredited by the American National Standards Institute (ANSI) to ensure openness, balance, and due process. This involves drafting, public review, and revision cycles to incorporate stakeholder input, resulting in documents that reflect best practices for safety and performance.
Among these efforts, ASME's Performance Test Codes (PTCs) stand out, comprising 48 specialized codes that provide standardized procedures for measuring the efficiency and performance of equipment and systems in categories like power production, combustion, heat transfer, and fluid handling. These codes guide testing protocols for items such as gas turbines and boilers, enabling accurate acceptance tests and operational verifications. Additionally, in response to emerging technologies, ASME incorporated requirements for additive manufacturing into its construction codes and product standards with the 2025 editions, including code cases for qualification and inspection to support safe integration in industries like aerospace and energy.
ASME standards achieve global adoption, with acceptance in over 100 countries and influence on international norms through U.S. technical advisory groups to the International Organization for Standardization (ISO), where ASME holds secretariats for several committees on topics like fasteners and pipelines. To promote compliance, ASME offers certification programs, such as the Conformity Assessment program, which verifies that manufacturers' products and quality systems meet specific standards, enhancing reliability worldwide.

Publications

Peer-Reviewed Journals

The American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME) maintains a robust program of peer-reviewed journals that disseminate cutting-edge research in mechanical engineering and related fields. The program originated in 1880 with the publication of the inaugural volume of Transactions of the ASME, which served as the society's primary outlet for technical papers and has since evolved into a suite of specialized journals hosted on the ASME Digital Collection platform. Today, ASME publishes 34 journals, encompassing hybrid and fully open access models, to advance scholarly contributions across disciplines such as design, manufacturing, energy, and biomechanics.
Key journals include the Journal of Mechanical Design, which focuses on innovative design methodologies, optimization techniques, and product development processes to support the design community. The ASME Journal of Heat and Mass Transfer (renamed in 2023 from Journal of Heat Transfer) addresses fundamental and applied research in thermal energy transport, including convection, radiation, and conduction phenomena critical to engineering applications. Similarly, the Journal of Pressure Vessel Technology emphasizes advancements in design, fabrication, inspection, and operation of pressure vessels and piping systems, contributing to safety and reliability standards in industrial settings. These and other titles, such as the Journal of Applied Mechanics and Journal of Manufacturing Science and Engineering, represent ASME's commitment to high-impact, archival research that influences engineering practice worldwide.
All ASME journals undergo a rigorous peer-review process, involving expert evaluation to ensure scholarly rigor, originality, and technical accuracy. Impact factors for these journals typically range from approximately 2.0 to 8.0, with notable examples like Applied Mechanics Reviews achieving 7.921 in 2016 and the ASME Journal of Heat and Mass Transfer at 2.11 in 2024, reflecting their influence in the field. Annually, the program produces more than 2,000 peer-reviewed articles, providing essential resources for engineers and researchers. Since 2020, authors have had the option to publish open access in hybrid journals for an article processing charge, alongside the fully open access ASME Open Journal of Engineering, enhancing accessibility to this research.

Awards and Honors

Major Society Awards

The American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME) confers several prestigious society awards to honor exceptional contributions to mechanical engineering, emphasizing lifetime achievements, innovation, and public benefit. These awards recognize individuals whose work advances engineering practice, research, or education in ways that broadly impact society.
The ASME Medal, established in 1920, represents the society's highest accolade and is bestowed for eminently distinguished engineering achievement, including broad contributions to the advancement of engineering science, technology, or practice. Criteria focus on sustained excellence that demonstrates significant influence on the profession and public welfare, often through groundbreaking innovations or leadership. Notable recipients include Tim Lieuwen in 2025, acknowledged for his pioneering leadership in clean energy technologies, propulsion systems, and engineering policy. Historically, trailblazers like Kate Gleason, the first woman elected as a full ASME member in 1919, exemplified the innovative spirit these awards celebrate.
The Holley Medal, another key honor, is awarded for outstanding and unique engineering acts that deliver noteworthy and timely public benefits, such as transformative solutions to pressing challenges. Selection criteria prioritize ingenuity and direct societal impact over routine accomplishments. In 2025, Baba Kalyani received the medal for his exceptional engineering leadership and global contributions to advanced manufacturing and sustainable technologies.
To promote gender diversity, the Kate Gleason Award, established in 2011 and named for the aforementioned pioneer, specifically honors distinguished female leaders for entrepreneurial success or lifetime engineering achievements. Criteria highlight professional impact, mentorship, and barriers overcome in male-dominated fields. The 2025 recipient, Karen A. Thole, was recognized for her influential research in turbine cooling and transformative educational initiatives.
Nominations for these awards occur annually via ASME's online system, requiring detailed documentation of the candidate's qualifications, supported by references from peers familiar with their work. Submissions undergo rigorous peer review by specialized committees; for the ASME Medal, the Committee on Honors forwards recommendations to the Board of Governors for approval.

Fellows Program

The ASME Fellows Program recognizes outstanding engineering achievements and significant contributions to the mechanical engineering profession by conferring the Fellow grade of membership, the highest elected level within the society. To qualify, candidates must be corporate members of ASME with at least 10 years of active engineering practice and 10 years of active corporate membership, excluding student years, and demonstrate substantial accomplishments in areas such as innovation, leadership, or public service.
Nominations for Fellow status are submitted by ASME members or existing Fellows and reviewed by the ASME Committee of Past Presidents, with selections made quarterly. The nomination process requires a detailed application, including up to 10 publications or achievements, and letters from at least three sponsors (two of whom must be Fellows or members) plus one from the nominator. Deadlines occur on March 1, June 1, September 1, and December 1 each year, ensuring ongoing opportunities for recognition.
Fellows are honored in categories that highlight diverse contributions, including engineering design and product development, research and development, education and workforce development, standards and certification, and broader professional leadership. For instance, recognition in education may emphasize innovative programs or outreach to underrepresented groups, while standards contributions focus on advancing codes for public safety. This structure underscores the program's emphasis on technical leadership and societal impact.
Newly elected Fellows receive a certificate, a Fellow badge, and a lapel pin, along with opportunities for leadership roles within ASME committees and recognition at society events. As of 2025, over 3,000 members have attained Fellow status, representing less than 5% of ASME's total membership and reflecting the program's selectivity and prestige. Following ASME's broader diversity, equity, and inclusion initiatives launched in 2021, efforts have included targeted outreach to encourage nominations from underrepresented professionals.

Legal and Ethical Issues

One of the most significant legal challenges faced by the American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME) occurred in 1982, when the U.S. Supreme Court ruled in American Society of Mechanical Engineers, Inc. v. Hydrolevel Corp. (456 U.S. 556) that ASME was civilly liable under the Sherman Antitrust Act for violations committed by members of its Boiler and Pressure Vessel Committee subcommittee. The case stemmed from the subcommittee's issuance of an interpretation of the Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code that deemed Hydrolevel Corporation's innovative low-water fuel cutoff device noncompliant, benefiting a rival manufacturer with ties to the subcommittee members who acted with apparent authority from ASME. The Court affirmed that nonprofit organizations like ASME could be held accountable for such agent misconduct, leading to a $4.75 million settlement in favor of Hydrolevel and underscoring the antitrust risks inherent in volunteer-driven standards processes.
In the aftermath of the Hydrolevel decision, ASME implemented substantial reforms to its governance and procedures, including revisions to its Code of Ethics to explicitly address conflicts of interest, impartiality in standards interpretation, and adherence to antitrust laws. These changes mandated that volunteers in standards development sign antitrust compliance statements and undergo mandatory training on antitrust guidelines, particularly for codes, standards formulation, and conformity assessments, to prevent misuse of the society's authority. The updated ethics framework emphasizes engineers' responsibility to uphold public safety and avoid actions that could restrain trade, reflecting ASME's commitment to self-regulation post-litigation.
More recently, in 2025, ASME actively opposed H.R. 4072, the Pro Codes Act, alongside a broad coalition of standards development organizations, contending that the bill's provisions for compulsory licensing of copyrighted standards would undermine the voluntary consensus process, erode incentives for innovation, and compromise U.S. competitiveness in global markets. ASME Executive Director and CEO Tom Costabile highlighted the legislation's potential to harm public safety by disrupting access to reliable technical standards. Similarly, ASME faced industry backlash in February 2025 over a proposed revision to Section VIII of the Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code, which would have eliminated exemptions for small-diameter vessels and low-pressure designs commonly used in HVACR applications; after advocacy from groups like the Heating, Air-Conditioning & Refrigeration Distributors International (HARDI), ASME withdrew the change to avoid imposing excessive regulatory burdens on manufacturers and installers.
ASME has also advanced ethical advocacy in emerging areas, issuing position statements in 2025 that mandate disclosure and accountability for artificial intelligence use in society publications and external content, while prohibiting generative AI in original research to preserve integrity and prevent bias or misinformation. On sustainability, ASME established a dedicated Committee on Sustainability in 2023 to embed ethical principles into engineering practices addressing climate change, ensuring that standards and initiatives promote equitable, environmentally responsible innovation without compromising safety or fairness.
