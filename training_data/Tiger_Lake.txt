Tiger Lake
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Tiger Lake is the codename for Intel's 11th-generation Core mobile processor family, consisting of system-on-chip (SoC) designs targeted at thin-and-light laptops and other portable computing devices. Launched on September 2, 2020, these processors are built on the Willow Cove CPU microarchitecturea successor to Sunny Covefabricated using Intel's third-generation 10 nm process technology, known as SuperFin, which enhances transistor density and power efficiency. The lineup includes variants such as the low-power UP3 class (12-28 W TDP) for ultrabooks and the higher-performance H-series (35-65 W TDP) introduced in 2021 for gaming and workstation laptops, offering up to 8 cores and clock speeds exceeding 4.5 GHz.
A defining feature of Tiger Lake is its integrated Intel Iris Xe graphics based on the Xe-LP architecture, supporting up to 96 execution units (EUs) for improved visual performanceup to 2.7 times faster than previous-generation integrated graphics in productivity and creative workloads. The processors deliver generational performance gains, including up to 23% faster single-threaded and 19% faster multi-threaded workloads compared to prior 10 nm designs, enabled by architectural enhancements like increased instructions per cycle (IPC) and higher memory bandwidth of up to 86 GB/s via LPDDR4x-4266 or DDR4-3200 support. Additionally, Tiger Lake incorporates advanced I/O capabilities, such as native Thunderbolt 4 and USB4 support for up to 40 Gbps connectivity, PCIe 4.0 lanes, and Wi-Fi 6 integration, facilitating faster data transfer and peripheral expansion in mobile platforms.
Tiger Lake also advances on-device AI acceleration through the second-generation Gaussian & Neural Accelerator (GNA) 2.0, which reduces CPU utilization for always-on inferencing tasks like voice recognition by approximately 20%, and supports DL Boost instructions for machine learning workloads. These processors power devices certified under the Intel Evo platform badge, emphasizing responsiveness, battery life exceeding 9 hours in video playback, and instant wake from sleep. Overall, Tiger Lake marked a significant step in Intel's mobile computing roadmap, bridging high performance with efficiency for consumer and commercial ultraportables until succeeded by Alder Lake in 2022.

Overview

Development and Release

Intel first previewed its Tiger Lake processors, the code name for its 11th-generation Core mobile processor family, at the Consumer Electronics Show (CES) 2020 on January 6, 2020. Tiger Lake was developed as the direct successor to the Ice Lake processors, continuing Intel's 10nm process technology while introducing optimizations for mobile computing. Engineering samples of Tiger Lake were distributed to original equipment manufacturers (OEMs) beginning in the second quarter of 2020 to support early platform development and testing.
The official launch occurred on September 2, 2020, focusing on mobile variants designed for thin-and-light laptops and ultrabooks. The H-series variants for higher-performance laptops were launched in May 2021. These processors incorporated the new Willow Cove CPU core design to enhance performance efficiency. Intel positioned Tiger Lake to compete directly with AMD's Ryzen 4000 series mobile chips, emphasizing gains in graphics and AI capabilities for ultraportable devices.
Initial consumer availability arrived in late 2020 through laptops from OEMs including Dell, HP, and Lenovo. Production and shipments extended into 2021, particularly for embedded and industrial applications via the Tiger Lake-UP3 platform. No major revisions to the Tiger Lake lineup were introduced by 2025, with graphics driver support shifting to legacy status that year.

Manufacturing Process

Tiger Lake processors are fabricated on Intel's 10nm SuperFin manufacturing process, which evolved from the 10nm node used in the prior Ice Lake generation by incorporating redesigns to the FinFET transistors and introducing SuperMIM metal-insulator-metal capacitors. These changes enable higher transistor density, reduced interconnect resistance by up to 30%, and overall improvements in power efficiency and performance. As a result, SuperFin delivers the largest intra-node performance gain in Intel's history, with estimates of 15-20% better performance per watt compared to the standard 10nm process.
Unlike later Intel nodes, SuperFin relies on deep ultraviolet (DUV) lithography rather than extreme ultraviolet (EUV), achieving enhanced yields and efficiency through optimized transistor barriers and capacitor structures. This fabrication approach supports the integration of Willow Cove CPU cores and Xe-LP integrated graphics on a monolithic die, tailored for mobile applications. The die size for quad-core variants measures approximately 144 mm, balancing compute density with thermal constraints.
Power delivery in Tiger Lake is configured for low-power mobile use, with U-series processors operating in 12-28 W envelopes and H-series variants scaling from 35 W up to 65 W to accommodate higher performance demands. No process node shrinks or major refinements were applied to Tiger Lake after its 2020 debut, positioning it as a legacy technology by 2025 amid competitors' adoption of denser 7 nm and 5 nm nodes from foundries like TSMC.

Architecture and Features

CPU Microarchitecture

The Tiger Lake processors employ the Willow Cove microarchitecture as their CPU core design, serving as the direct successor to the Sunny Cove cores found in the previous-generation Ice Lake family. Willow Cove maintains a 4-wide instruction decode stage, enabling the processing of up to four instructions per cycle, paired with a sophisticated out-of-order execution engine that dynamically reorders instructions to maximize pipeline utilization and reduce stalls. This design emphasizes efficiency in mobile environments, building on Sunny Cove's foundation while introducing refinements for better power-performance balance on the 10nm SuperFin process node.
Mobile variants of Tiger Lake integrate up to four Willow Cove cores, supporting eight threads through Intel Hyper-Threading technology, which allows each core to handle two simultaneous threads for improved multithreaded workload efficiency. The cache hierarchy per core includes a 32 KB L1 instruction cache and a 48 KB L1 data cache for low-latency access to frequently used data and code, a dedicated 1.25 MB L2 cache to buffer larger working sets, and a shared 12 MB L3 cache across the quad-core configuration to facilitate inter-core data sharing. These caches are non-inclusive, with the larger L2 and L3 structures contributing to reduced latency compared to prior designs. Clock speeds vary by model and power configuration, with base frequencies ranging from 1.2 GHz to 2.6 GHz and single-core turbo boosts reaching up to 4.8 GHz in high-end i7 configurations, enabling bursty performance for demanding tasks.
Willow Cove delivers approximately 19% higher instructions per cycle (IPC) on average over Sunny Cove, driven by optimizations in the execution pipeline and cache subsystem that enhance overall throughput without proportional power increases. Key features include support for AVX-512 vector instructions, though limited in mobile implementations due to thermal constraints, allowing for accelerated vector processing in compatible workloads. The microarchitecture also incorporates Intel Deep Learning Boost (DL Boost), an extension to AVX-512 that provides dedicated hardware acceleration for AI inferencing tasks such as matrix multiplications, reducing CPU utilization for neural network operations. Additionally, improvements to the branch prediction unit enhance accuracy and reduce misprediction penalties, contributing to smoother control flow in complex code paths. Willow Cove integrates with the on-die Xe-LP GPU to enable heterogeneous computing scenarios, where CPU and graphics resources can collaborate on parallel tasks.

Integrated Graphics

The integrated graphics in Tiger Lake processors utilize the Intel Xe-LP (Gen 12) architecture, delivering enhanced rendering capabilities through up to 96 execution units (EUs). This design supports DirectX 12 Ultimate, enabling features such as variable rate shading and limited hardware-accelerated ray tracing for improved visual effects in compatible applications. Clock speeds reach up to 1.35 GHz in high-end configurations, yielding approximately 2.1 TFLOPS of single-precision floating-point (FP32) performance, which facilitates smoother graphics rendering compared to prior generations.
The media engine integrates advanced video processing, including hardware-accelerated AV1 decoding for efficient playback of next-generation video streams and Quick Sync Video technology for H.265/HEVC encoding, supporting high-quality video workflows at reduced power consumption. Display outputs accommodate up to four 4K@60Hz HDR displays or one 8K@60Hz display via eDP 1.4b and DP 1.4 interfaces, enabling versatile multi-monitor setups for productivity and media consumption.
Compared to the previous Iris Plus graphics in 10th-generation processors, Xe-LP offers significant power efficiency improvements, achieving up to twice the graphics performance within similar thermal envelopes, which supports light gaming and content creation tasks without dedicated GPUs. Processor variants scale the iGPU from 24 EUs in entry-level SKUs (branded as Intel UHD Graphics) to 96 EUs in premium models (branded as Intel Iris Xe Graphics for 80+ EUs), allowing tailored performance across mobile and embedded applications. Additionally, the architecture supports CPU offloading for AI tasks through oneAPI, enhancing compute versatility.

Image Processing Unit

The sixth-generation Intel Image Processing Unit (IPU6), integrated into Tiger Lake processors, serves as a dedicated co-processor for handling image and video pipelines, distinct from general-purpose graphics rendering. It accelerates image signal processing (ISP) tasks, including real-time noise reduction and computer vision operations, enabling efficient preprocessing for AI workloads on edge devices such as laptops and embedded systems. This design supports power-optimized, always-on features like background blur during video calls, reducing CPU overhead by offloading sensor data handling.
IPU6 facilitates multi-camera support for up to six sensors simultaneously, processing 4K video at 30 frames per second natively and up to 90 frames per second architecturally, alongside still images up to 27 megapixels (42 megapixels architectural capability). Key features include hardware-accelerated face detection, low-light enhancements through advanced ISP algorithms, and noise suppression that leverages the integrated Gaussian & Neural Accelerator (GNA) 2.0 to cut CPU utilization by approximately 20% in relevant workloads. These capabilities are optimized for edge AI applications, where IPU6 preprocesses raw camera data before feeding it into neural networks.
Tiger Lake's IPU6 integrates with the OpenVINO toolkit, Intel's software framework for optimizing AI inference on heterogeneous hardware, allowing efficient deployment of computer vision models like those for object recognition. When combined with the CPU's Deep Learning Boost and GPU resources, the platform delivers up to 4 TOPS of AI performance, with IPU6 focusing on input preprocessing to maintain low latency and power draw in scenarios such as multi-stream video analysis. It also briefly interfaces with the Xe integrated graphics for hybrid workloads involving post-processed image data.

Input/Output Capabilities

Tiger Lake system-on-chips (SoCs) provide advanced input/output (I/O) interfaces designed for high-bandwidth connectivity in mobile and embedded platforms, emphasizing compatibility with modern peripherals and displays. The architecture integrates support for high-speed standards directly into the processor or via the associated platform controller hub (PCH), enabling efficient data transfer for storage, networking, and external devices.
A key feature is the native integration of Thunderbolt 4, the first such implementation in Intel client processors, offering up to 40 Gbps bidirectional bandwidth over USB-C connectors. This support includes PCIe 4.0 tunneling for external GPUs and storage, along with full USB4 compatibility for versatile peripheral connections, such as daisy-chaining up to six devices. Thunderbolt 4 also enables power delivery up to 100 W and multi-display output, enhancing docking capabilities for laptops.
For wireless connectivity, Tiger Lake utilizes the CNVi 2.0 interface, which integrates Wi-Fi 6 (802.11ax) and Bluetooth 5.1 support through compatible modules like the Intel Wi-Fi 6 AX201. This setup delivers up to 2.4 Gbps theoretical throughput on 2x2 MIMO configurations across 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz bands, with improved efficiency in dense environments via orthogonal frequency-division multiple access (OFDMA) and target wake time features. Bluetooth 5.1 adds enhanced direction finding and improved connection stability for peripherals.
PCIe 4.0 lane allocation varies by series: low-power U-series processors provide up to 4 lanes directly from the CPU (configurable as x4 for NVMe storage), supplemented by up to 24 PCIe 3.0 lanes from the PCH for additional expansion. In contrast, high-performance H-series variants offer up to 20 PCIe 4.0 lanes from the CPU, supporting configurations like x16 for discrete GPUs and x4 for high-speed storage, enabling faster data access with double the bandwidth per lane compared to PCIe 3.0.
USB connectivity includes support for USB 3.2 Gen 2x2, delivering 20 Gbps speeds via the PCH's flexible I/O lanes, alongside backward compatibility for USB 3.2 Gen 2 (10 Gbps), Gen 1 (5 Gbps), and USB 2.0 ports. This allows for multiple high-speed ports, often up to 10 or more depending on platform configuration, facilitating fast charging and data transfer for external drives and hubs.
Display outputs are handled through the integrated Intel Xe graphics, supporting DisplayPort 1.4 with up to 8K resolution at 60 Hz or 4K at 120 Hz via high bit rate 3 (HBR3) and Display Stream Compression (DSC). HDMI 2.0b integration provides 4K at 60 Hz support with HDCP 2.2 for protected content, configurable through embedded DisplayPort (eDP) or USB-C alt-mode for multi-monitor setups.
Tiger Lake lacks a native 5G modem, requiring external WWAN modules connected via M.2 slots or USB for cellular connectivity in mobile designs.

Hardware Telemetry

Tiger Lake processors incorporate Intel Platform Monitoring Technology (PMT), a standardized framework for accessing hardware monitoring capabilities that enables real-time collection of performance data, including power consumption, thermal conditions, and resource utilization metrics. This telemetry system supports continuous read-only data streams and hardware sampling to facilitate diagnostics and optimization without interrupting system operation.
The platform integrates with Intel Converged Security and Management Engine (CSME) version 15.0, which provides remote management capabilities and facilitates over-the-air firmware updates to maintain system integrity and performance. CSME 15.0 enhances hardware diagnostics by enabling secure remote access to telemetry data for administrators.
Security features in Tiger Lake include a hardware root-of-trust established through the Management Engine, which verifies platform integrity during boot and runtime. Intel Trusted Execution Technology (TXT) further bolsters this by creating a measured launch environment that protects against software-based attacks, ensuring authenticated code execution in a trusted space. These mechanisms support confidential computing by isolating sensitive operations from potential compromises in the operating system or hypervisor.
Telemetry data from PMT informs dynamic voltage and frequency scaling, including support for Intel Turbo Boost Max Technology 3.0 in select variants, allowing individual cores to reach higher frequencies based on thermal and power headroom for improved single-threaded performance. Privacy controls require user opt-in for sharing telemetry data with Intel, ensuring that local monitoring remains active while external transmission is consensual to protect user data.
Firmware updates for Tiger Lake continued through 2023, addressing vulnerabilities such as Spectre and Meltdown variants through microcode patches that mitigate transient execution attacks without requiring full hardware redesigns. These updates are delivered via the Management Engine to enhance overall platform security.

Processor Variants

Low-Power Mobile Processors (U-Series)

The Tiger Lake U-series processors, part of Intel's 11th-generation Core lineup, are designed for low-power mobile applications, targeting ultrabooks and thin-and-light laptops with configurable thermal design power (TDP) ranges of 9-15 W. These processors emphasize efficiency and portability, featuring the Willow Cove CPU cores and integrated Intel Iris Xe graphics, while supporting advanced connectivity like Thunderbolt 4 and Wi-Fi 6. They were introduced in September 2020 to deliver improved battery life and performance for everyday productivity and light creative workloads in premium consumer devices.
Key models in the U-series include the entry-level Intel Core i3-1115G4, which offers 2 cores and 4 threads with a maximum turbo frequency of 4.10 GHz and 6 MB of Intel Smart Cache, paired with Intel UHD Graphics featuring 48 execution units for basic graphics tasks. Stepping up, the mid-range Intel Core i5-1135G7 provides 4 cores and 8 threads, a maximum turbo frequency of 4.20 GHz, 8 MB cache, and Intel Iris Xe Graphics with 80 execution units, enabling smoother multitasking and casual gaming. The top-tier Intel Core i7-1165G7 matches the i5's core and thread count but boosts to a 4.70 GHz maximum turbo frequency with 12 MB cache and Iris Xe Graphics equipped with 96 execution units for enhanced graphics performance in demanding applications.
The U-series is divided into UP4 configurations at 9-15 W base TDP for ultra-portable, fanless designs and UP3 at up to 28 W configurable TDP for slightly thicker laptops requiring more sustained performance, allowing manufacturers flexibility in balancing power efficiency and output for thin-and-light form factors. Embedded variants, such as the Intel Core i5-1145G7E, adapt these designs for industrial and IoT applications with a fixed 15 W TDP, 4 cores and 8 threads, 4.10 GHz maximum turbo, and 8 MB cache, supporting extended reliability in non-consumer environments.
These processors dominated the premium laptop market from 2020 to 2022, powering devices like Dell XPS and Lenovo ThinkPad series with their balance of performance and efficiency, before being phased out by early 2024 in favor of Alder Lake successors offering hybrid architectures.

High-Performance Mobile Processors (H-Series)

The Tiger Lake H-series processors target high-performance mobile applications, such as gaming and workstation laptops, with thermal design power (TDP) ratings of 28-45W. These processors leverage Intel's 10nm SuperFin manufacturing process and Willow Cove CPU cores to deliver enhanced multi-threaded performance for demanding workloads. Released in the first quarter of 2021, the lineup includes models like the Core i5-11400H and Core i7-11800H, which emphasize core count and turbo frequencies suitable for content creation and gaming when paired with discrete graphics.
The Core i5-11400H features 6 cores and 12 threads, with a maximum turbo frequency of 4.5 GHz and 12 MB of Intel Smart Cache, making it suitable for mid-range high-performance laptops. Its integrated Intel UHD Graphics includes 16 execution units (EUs), operating at up to 1.45 GHz, providing basic visual output support. In contrast, the Core i7-11800H offers 8 cores and 16 threads, a maximum turbo frequency of 4.6 GHz, and 24 MB of Intel Smart Cache. This model integrates Intel UHD Graphics with 32 EUs at up to 1.45 GHz, enabling better integrated graphics performance for lighter tasks. Both processors support configurable power profiles, with a base PL1 of 45W and turbo power (PL2) up to 65W, allowing OEMs to tune for sustained high loads in gaming rigs.
The H35 series extends the H lineup with 35W variants optimized for slim high-end laptops, balancing power efficiency and performance for ultraportable gaming. A representative model is the Core i7-11370H, with 4 cores and 8 threads, a maximum turbo frequency of 4.8 GHz, and 12 MB of Intel Smart Cache. It features Intel Iris Xe Graphics with 96 EUs at up to 1.35 GHz, delivering significantly stronger integrated graphics capabilities compared to standard H-series models. These processors support discrete GPUs through up to 20 PCIe 4.0 lanes, configurable as x8 for GPU connectivity, enabling high-bandwidth links for external graphics in devices like the Dell XPS 15. The H-series remained in use for gaming and workstation laptops through 2023, appearing in systems such as Dell XPS 15 models and various gaming rigs before transitioning to newer generations.

Embedded and Desktop Processors

The Tiger Lake platform includes specialized low-power variants tailored for embedded and fanless applications, such as low-power U-series processors designed for ultra-portable devices like tablets. The Intel Core i7-1185G7, a quad-core, eight-thread processor with a configurable TDP of 12W, supports fanless designs by operating efficiently within thermal constraints of compact form factors.
Embedded variants of Tiger Lake, particularly the UP3-class processors, extend the architecture to industrial and IoT applications, offering enhanced reliability for long-term deployments. These include options like the Intel Celeron 6305E and similar low-end configurations optimized for edge computing, with power envelopes around 15W to balance performance and efficiency in space-constrained systems. Intel initiated the discontinuation process for Tiger Lake processors in June 2023, with last orders in October 2023 and final shipments in January 2024, though embedded variants may remain available through partners as of 2025.
Desktop implementations of Tiger Lake are rare and targeted at integrated systems like all-in-one PCs, represented by the B-series processors. The Intel Core i9-11900KB, an eight-core, 16-thread part with a 65W TDP, exemplifies this niche, providing desktop-level performance through BGA packaging for OEM custom builds rather than traditional socketed designs.
Embedded Tiger Lake variants benefit from extended longevity in industrial applications. Some configurations incorporate in-band ECC (IBECC) memory support, enabling error correction with non-ECC DDR4 or LPDDR4x modules to enhance data integrity in mission-critical environments. Hardware telemetry features facilitate remote management in these deployments, allowing monitoring of system health without physical access.
These processors find application in digital signage for high-resolution content rendering, medical devices for imaging and diagnostics, and automotive systems through post-2020 expansions into infotainment and edge processing.
