 The Sun is the star at the center of the Solar System, a G2V spectral class main-sequence starcommonly known as a yellow dwarfcomposed primarily of hydrogen (92.1%) and helium (7.8%), with trace amounts of heavier elements, and powered by nuclear fusion in its core where temperatures reach 15 million degrees Celsius. It formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from a collapsing molecular cloud and is currently in the stable middle phase of its life cycle, expected to expand into a red giant in about 5 billion years before shedding its outer layers and becoming a white dwarf.
With an equatorial diameter of about 1.392 million kilometersroughly 109 times that of Earththe Sun's immense mass of 1.989 10 kilograms accounts for over 99.8% of the total mass in the Solar System, gravitationally binding eight planets, dwarf planets, asteroids, comets, and other objects in their orbits. Its mean distance from Earth is 149.6 million kilometers (1 astronomical unit), and it rotates differentially, with an equatorial period of 26.8 days compared to 36 days at the poles. The Sun's photosphere, its visible "surface," maintains an average temperature of 5,500C, emitting light across the electromagnetic spectrum, while its corona extends millions of kilometers into space at temperatures up to 2 million degrees Celsius.
As the primary source of energy for the Solar System, the Sun drives Earth's climate, weather patterns, ocean currents, and seasons through solar radiation and influences phenomena like auroras via interactions with Earth's magnetic field and the solar winda stream of charged particles emanating from the corona at speeds around 400 km/s. Its luminosity of 3.83 10 watts powers photosynthesis and sustains life on Earth, while dynamic features such as sunspots, solar flares, and coronal mass ejections occur in an approximately 11-year magnetic activity cycle, affecting space weather and satellite operations. Ongoing NASA missions, including the Parker Solar Probe, continue to study these processes to better understand the Sun's influence on the heliosphere and beyond.

Naming and Cultural Significance

Etymology

The English word "sun" originates from Old English sunne, a feminine noun denoting the celestial body, from Proto-Germanic *sunn. This derives from the Proto-Indo-European root *swel- ("the sun"), with a variant *s(u)wen-. The word passed through Middle English as sonne and remains "sun" in Modern English.
Cognates of this root appear in other Indo-European languages. Latin sol ("sun" or "sunlight") derives from *swel-, yielding French soleil (from Vulgar Latin diminutive *soliculus). Ancient Greek hlios (), denoting the sun and its personified deity, comes from *shwel-. Sanskrit srya, the name of the sun god and the celestial body, derives from *sryos, a derivative of *swel-.
In astronomical usage, "Sun" is capitalized as a proper noun to denote our star as distinct from others, a convention standardized after the adoption of the heliocentric model. The International Astronomical Union recommends this capitalization to treat it as a unique identifier within the solar system. These linguistic forms often carry mythological significance, as with Helios and Srya.

Religious and Mythological Aspects

The Sun has occupied a prominent place in religious and mythological traditions worldwide, often personified as a deity symbolizing creation, vitality, and divine power.
In ancient Egyptian mythology, Ra was revered as the supreme sun god and creator. He emerged from the primordial waters of Nun to form the world, sailing across the sky in a solar barque by day and navigating the underworld by night to maintain cosmic order and renewal.
In several traditions, the Sun was depicted as a chariot-driving deity: Helios in Greek mythology, a youthful god with fiery horses; Surya in Hinduism, one of the Adityas, with seven horses bestowing health, prosperity, and enlightenment; and Sl in Norse mythology, a goddess traversing the sky.
In Aztec cosmology, Tonatiuh was the fifth and current sun god, demanding human sacrifices to sustain his movement and prevent cosmic destruction.
In Japanese Shinto tradition, Amaterasu is the sun goddess and mythical ancestress of the Imperial House.
Rituals and festivals frequently celebrated the Sun's life-giving force and seasonal rhythms. Many ancient cultures marked solstices with ceremonies, such as alignments at megalithic sites to invoke blessings for fertility and harvest.
In the Inca Empire, the Inti Raymi festival honored Inti, the sun god and ruler's ancestor, during the winter solstice with processions, offerings of gold and llamas, and ritual dances at Sacsayhuamn to ensure the Sun's rebirth and agricultural abundance.
In Abrahamic religions, sacred texts present the Sun as a divine sign: the Bible calls it the "Sun of Righteousness" arising with healing (Malachi 4:2), while the Quran describes it as a radiant lamp and prohibits prostration to the sun or moon, directing worship to Allah the Creator (Quran 71:16, 41:37).
Symbolically, the Sun appears in creation myths, cycles of renewal, and eschatological narratives as a force of origin, rebirth, and judgment. In Egyptian lore, Ra's emergence from chaos established the Sun as the primordial creative power. In Aztec mythology, the Legend of the Five Suns portrayed successive solar eras ending in destruction and rebirth, mirroring cyclical life. In biblical eschatology, the Sun's darkening signals divine intervention, as in Revelation 6:12 where it turns black as sackcloth, heralding apocalyptic change.

Physical Characteristics

Size and Mass

The Sun has an equatorial radius of 695,700 km, approximately 109 times Earth's radius. This yields a diameter of about 1,391,400 km, roughly 10 times that of Jupiter.
Its mass is 1.989 10^{30} kg, equivalent to about 333,000 Earth masses and comprising 99.86% of the Solar System's total mass.
The mean density is 1.408 g/cm, less than a quarter of Earth's due to its gaseous composition despite high internal pressures.
Surface gravitational acceleration is 274 m/s, approximately 28 times Earth's.
The volume is 1.412 10^{18} km, enough to contain about 1.3 million Earths.

Rotation and Age

The Sun exhibits differential rotation, with equatorial regions completing a sidereal rotation in about 25 days and polar regions taking about 35 days. This variation arises from the Sun's gaseous composition, which allows different latitudes to rotate at different rates.
The Sun is approximately 4.6 billion years old, an estimate based primarily on radiometric dating of meteorites that record the Solar System's formation. Helioseismology supports this age by confirming evolutionary models that match observed solar oscillation frequencies.
The Sun's angular momentum, derived from its rotation, powers the dynamo process that generates and sustains its global magnetic field through interactions between rotation and convection. Relative to other stars, the Sun rotates slowly for its mass and age; younger Sun-like stars typically have periods of only a few days, while older ones rotate more slowly due to angular momentum loss through stellar winds over their lifetimes.

Chemical Composition

Elemental Makeup

The Sun's elemental composition is determined primarily from spectroscopic observations of its photosphere, the visible surface layer. Convective mixing makes this layer representative of the Sun's overall composition.
Hydrogen dominates, comprising approximately 74.38% of the mass and 92.4% of the atoms by number. Helium accounts for 24.23% by mass and 7.6% by number. Together, these two elements constitute over 98.6% of the Sun's mass, reflecting the primordial composition of the solar nebula.
Heavier elements, termed "metals" in astrophysics, make up the remaining ~1.4% by mass, with major contributors including oxygen (~0.58%), carbon (~0.26%), and neon (~0.17%).

Isotopic Variations

The deuterium-to-hydrogen (D/H) ratio in the protosolar nebula, preserved in the Sun's composition, is approximately 2.0 10^{-5}, reflecting primordial abundances from Big Bang nucleosynthesis. This value is inferred from Jupiter's atmosphere as a proxy, since deuterium is rapidly destroyed in stellar interiors. The ratio's consistency across solar system bodies highlights its value as a tracer of early universe conditions, unaltered in the Sun's outer layers.
The helium-3 to helium-4 (He/He) ratio in the photosphere and solar wind is about 4.5 10^{-4}, elevated from the protosolar value of roughly 1.7 10^{-4}. This increase results from He production in proton-proton chain fusion reactions in the solar core, followed by outward mixing of processed material over the Sun's lifetime. Solar wind measurements confirm the current surface abundance and constrain standard solar models.
The carbon-12 to carbon-13 (C/C) ratio in the photosphere is 9093, slightly higher than the terrestrial ratio of 89. This difference arises from gravitational diffusion, radiative acceleration, and convective mixing that differentially transport isotopes from the interior to the surface. Spectroscopic observations of molecular lines provide precise constraints.
These isotopic ratios influence the mean molecular weight gradient and opacity, affecting the sound speed profile and convective zone depth in solar models. Helioseismology data, including global oscillation frequencies, impose tight constraints on these compositions. Discrepancies exist, such as the need for a surface helium abundance Y 0.25higher than predicted by diffusion modelsto match observed p-mode frequencies. Resolving these improves understanding of element settling and transport processes in the Sun.

Internal Structure

Core

The core of the Sun is its central region, extending from the center to approximately 20-25% of the solar radius, or about 150,000 to 175,000 kilometers. This densely packed zone reaches temperatures of around 15 million Kelvin and densities of about 150 grams per cubic centimeter, conditions extreme enough to enable sustained nuclear fusion. These properties, inferred from solar models and helioseismology, make the core the powerhouse of the Sun, where over 99% of its energy is produced.
The primary energy source in the core is the proton-proton chain (pp-chain), a series of nuclear fusion reactions that converts hydrogen into helium. In this process, the Sun fuses approximately 620 million metric tons of hydrogen into helium every second, releasing vast amounts of energy while converting only about 0.7% of the hydrogen's mass into other forms. The net reaction of the pp-chain is:

4\, ^{1}\text{H} \rightarrow \, ^{4}\text{He} + 2\text{e}^{+} + 2\nu_{e} + \text{energy (26.7 MeV)}

This equation represents the overall transformation, where four protons (hydrogen nuclei) fuse to form one helium-4 nucleus, two positrons, two electron neutrinos, and energy equivalent to 26.7 mega-electronvolts per reaction, primarily in the form of gamma rays and kinetic energy. The pp-chain dominates in stars like the Sun due to their relatively low core temperatures, accounting for nearly all fusion energy generation.
A key byproduct of the pp-chain is the production of electron neutrinos, which stream outward from the core at nearly the speed of light. Observations in the late 20th century revealed fewer solar neutrinos than predicted by standard solar models, posing the "solar neutrino problem." This discrepancy was resolved by the discovery of neutrino oscillations, in which electron neutrinos transform into muon or tau neutrinos en route to Earth, as confirmed by experiments like Super-Kamiokande and the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory. These findings not only validated solar fusion models but also established that neutrinos have mass. The energy from core fusion is subsequently transported to the Sun's outer layers via radiative and convective processes.

Radiative and Convective Zones

The radiative zone extends from approximately 0.25 to 0.7 solar radii (175,000 to 490,000 km from the center). In this layer, energy generated by nuclear fusion in the core is transported outward via radiative diffusion. High-energy photons (primarily gamma rays and X-rays) travel through a random walk, scattering repeatedly off ions and electrons in the dense plasma because of high opacity from ionized hydrogen and metals. The short mean free path (on the order of millimeters) results in billions of absorptions and re-emissions, so a typical photon takes an estimated 170,000 years to cross the zone. Temperatures decrease from roughly 7 million K at the base to 2 million K at the top, while density drops from 20 g/cm to 0.2 g/cm. The subadiabatic temperature gradient, sustained by opacity due to bound-free and free-free transitions, keeps the layer stable against convection.
Above the radiative zone lies the convective zone, extending from approximately 0.7 to 1.0 solar radii (roughly 200,000 km below the surface to the photosphere). In this region, increased opacity from heavier elements such as carbon and oxygen traps heat, causing the temperature gradient to exceed the adiabatic value and initiate convection. Hot, less dense plasma rises in convection cells, while cooler material sinks, efficiently carrying energy outward at speeds up to several meters per second. These motions produce the granulation patterns observed on the solar surface. Temperatures fall from about 2 million K at the base to roughly 5,700 K at the top, with the gradient closely matching the adiabatic value for near-neutral stability.
The tachocline forms a thin transition layer between the radiative and convective zones, approximately 0.05 solar radii thick (~35,000 km) at the base of the convective zone. This shear layer exhibits strong radial differential rotation as the nearly solid-body rotation of the radiative interior gives way to the differential rotation of the convective envelope. The resulting shear drives the solar dynamo by generating and amplifying magnetic fields through magnetohydrodynamic instabilities. Helioseismic observations confirm the tachocline's thinness and its latitudinal variations, which play a key role in the 11-year solar cycle.

Solar Atmosphere

Photosphere

The photosphere is the visible "surface" of the Sun, the opaque layer from which most of the Sun's emitted light escapes into space. It defines the apparent solar disk and radius of approximately 696,000 km.
This relatively thin layer ranges from about 100 to 400 km in thickness, comprising less than 0.1% of the Sun's total radius.
The effective temperature is around 5,778 K, with typical surface values between 5,500 and 6,000 K. Energy generated in the core and transported outward emerges here as visible radiation, primarily blackbody emission peaking in the green-yellow wavelengths.
A prominent feature is granulation, arising from convection in the underlying zone. Hot plasma rises in bright granules roughly 1,000 km in diameter, while cooler material descends in darker intergranular lanes. Granules persist for 8 to 20 minutes, with central temperatures 200300 K higher than the lanes and upward flows of 12 km/s, producing a dynamic mottled pattern across the solar disk.
Limb darkening manifests as a gradual decrease in brightness toward the solar limb, where intensity drops to about 3040% of the central value in visible light. This occurs because lines of sight near the limb pass obliquely through higher, cooler layers, in contrast to the deeper, hotter regions sampled at disk center.
Sunspots appear as temporary dark patches on the photosphere, resulting from strong magnetic fields that inhibit convection and lower temperatures to around 3,7004,200 K in the umbra compared to the surrounding ~5,700 K. Their prevalence increases during solar maximum in the 11-year solar cycle.

Chromosphere and Corona

The chromosphere forms the lower portion of the Sun's upper atmosphere, extending above the photosphere roughly 2,000 to 10,000 km due to its dynamic, filamentary nature. Temperatures rise irregularly from about 4,000 K near the base to over 20,000 K at the top, producing strong atomic excitation and emission lines across this relatively thin layer.
Prominent features include spiculestransient, jet-like plasma structures rising up to 10,000 km at speeds of 1030 km/sand prominences (filaments when viewed against the disk), denser and cooler plasma loops suspended by magnetic fields, often arching hundreds of thousands of kilometers and persisting for days to weeks. These structures reflect the layer's turbulent dynamics, driven by convection and magnetic activity.
The corona, the outermost atmospheric layer, extends millions of kilometers outward, gradually transitioning into the interplanetary medium. Plasma temperatures reach 13 million K, with densities around 10^{-15} g/cm in inner regions. Notable structures include coronal holesdark areas of open magnetic field lines associated with faster solar wind outflowsand bright streamers aligned with closed magnetic loops tracing the heliospheric current sheet.
A major unsolved question in solar physics is the coronal heating problem: temperatures rise sharply despite declining density, requiring non-thermal energy sources to offset radiative losses. Proposed mechanisms include dissipation of magnetohydrodynamic waves (such as Alfvn waves) and ubiquitous small-scale magnetic reconnections, or nanoflares, each contributing a fraction of a large flare's energy. NASA's Parker Solar Probe, with approaches as close as approximately 3.9 million miles to the Sun, has delivered in-situ observations of plasma waves and magnetic structures supporting these processes, which supply excess energy of about 200 W/m in the low corona without relying solely on conduction.
Observing the chromosphere and corona is challenging due to the photosphere's overwhelming brightness. The chromosphere appears pinkish during total solar eclipses in hydrogen-alpha emission at 656 nm, while the corona is visible in white light during eclipses or through coronagraphs. Space missions like the Solar Dynamics Observatory routinely image the corona in extreme ultraviolet and X-rays, revealing its hot, structured plasma.

Extended Solar Environment

Heliosphere

The heliosphere is a vast, comet-shaped bubble of solar magnetic field and plasma that envelops the Solar System and shields it from most galactic cosmic rays and the local interstellar medium (LISM). It is compressed on the leading edge facing the interstellar flow and elongated into a tail on the opposite side.
The solar wind carries the Sun's rotating magnetic field outward, forming the heliospheric current sheet (HCS)a thin, wavy surface separating sectors of opposite magnetic polarity, resembling an extended solar magnetic equator. Surrounding the HCS are polar lobes of single polarity (northern and southern) that extend along the heliosphere's axis, contributing to its bipolar configuration.
The heliosphere's outer boundaries were observed in situ by NASA's Voyager spacecraft. The termination shock occurs where the solar wind decelerates from supersonic to subsonic speeds upon interacting with the LISM, at an average distance of about 90 AU. Voyager 1 crossed it at 94 AU in 2004, and Voyager 2 at 84 AU in 2007, revealing asymmetry. Beyond lies the heliosheath, a turbulent layer of compressed plasma extending to the heliopausethe boundary where solar influence ends and interstellar plasma dominatesat roughly 120 AU. Voyager 1 reached the heliopause at 121 AU in 2012, and Voyager 2 at 119 AU in 2018. These positions vary with solar cycle fluctuations and interstellar pressures.
Interaction with the LISM compresses the sunward side, producing a bow wave ahead of the termination shock. The interstellar magnetic field (~0.5 nT) drapes around the heliopause, enhancing asymmetry and influencing plasma flows. Pickup ions from ionized interstellar neutrals dominate thermal pressures in the heliosheath, slowing the solar wind by up to 20% by 80 AU. Galactic cosmic rays experience reduced flux within the heliosphere due to magnetic scattering. Voyager data reveal a foreshock region of accelerated particles ahead of the termination shock, extending tens of AU with energies from keV to MeV.
Missions including Voyager, the Interstellar Boundary Explorer (IBEX), and the Interstellar Mapping and Acceleration Probe (IMAP)launched on September 24, 2025continue to refine understanding of the heliosphere's outer regions. IBEX has mapped a narrow "ribbon" of enhanced energetic neutral atom (ENA) emissions, about 20 wide, encircling the sky beyond the heliopause. Analyses link its temporal variations to solar wind dynamics and suggest origins in secondary ENA production outside the heliosphere. IBEX observations also show the heliotail as two distinct lobes (port and starboard), broader and flatter than expected, tilted by the interstellar magnetic field, and filled with slow solar wind at low latitudes. Voyager measurements from the heliosheath and beyond reveal unexpected low-speed flows near the heliopause, corroborating these remote observations.

Solar Wind

The solar wind is a continuous stream of charged particlesprimarily plasmaemanating from the Sun's corona and expanding outward into interplanetary space. It originates mainly from coronal holes, where open magnetic field lines allow plasma to escape the Sun's gravitational pull. At Earth's distance (1 AU), the solar wind typically has a density of about 5 particles per cubic centimeter.
Its composition is dominated by protons (95% of ions), with 4% alpha particles (helium nuclei), trace heavier ions, and electrons to maintain charge neutrality. Speed ranges from 300 to 800 km/s, averaging around 400 km/s. Fast wind (>500 km/s) is steadier, cooler, and less dense, originating from coronal holes; slow wind (~400 km/s) is more variable, hotter, and denser, emerging from equatorial regions near the streamer belt.
As the solar wind expands radially, the Sun's 27-day rotation (as viewed from Earth) winds the embedded magnetic field lines into the Parker spiral, an Archimedean spiral pattern in which field lines trail outward. Interactions between fast and slow streams create corotating interaction regions (CIRs), where faster plasma compresses slower plasma ahead, forming boundaries with enhanced density and magnetic field strength that persist over multiple solar rotations. The solar wind ultimately shapes the heliosphere, the bubble of solar-influenced space surrounding the solar system.

Energy Output

Solar Radiation

The Sun's total luminosity, representing the total radiant energy emitted per second across all wavelengths, is measured at 3.828 10^{26} watts. This immense energy output originates from nuclear fusion processes in the core and propagates outward through the radiative and convective zones before escaping as electromagnetic radiation. At Earth's mean orbital distance of approximately 1 astronomical unit (AU), this luminosity results in the solar constant, or total solar irradiance (TSI), of 1,361.6 0.3 W/m as measured by the Total and Spectral Solar Irradiance Sensor (TSIS-1) during the 2019 solar minimum. These values establish the scale of solar energy flux available to power Earth's climate system.
The Sun's radiation can be approximated as that of a blackbody, with an effective temperature of 5,772 K derived from integrating the observed spectral irradiance to match the total luminosity. This effective temperature characterizes the surface from which the Sun would radiate the same total energy if it were a perfect blackbody emitter. Solar irradiance exhibits small variations tied to the 11-year solar cycle, with total solar irradiance fluctuating by about 0.1% between solar minimum and maximum, corresponding to roughly 1 W/m change at Earth. These cyclic changes are driven by magnetic activity, including sunspots that temporarily reduce output in visible wavelengths but are offset by facular brightenings. Shorter-term variations occur in ultraviolet (UV) and X-ray bands, where irradiance can vary by factors of 210 over days due to flares and active regions, though these contribute minimally (<0.01%) to the total energy flux. Such modulations influence stratospheric chemistry and ionospheric dynamics but have limited direct impact on global surface temperatures.
The Sun's energy is distributed across the electromagnetic spectrum, with approximately 8% in ultraviolet wavelengths (<400 nm), 44% in the visible range (400700 nm), and 48% in the infrared (>700 nm) for the extraterrestrial spectrum. This breakdown reflects the blackbody peak near 500 nm in the green-yellow visible light, essential for photosynthesis, while infrared dominates the thermal component and UV drives photochemical reactions in Earth's upper atmosphere. Measurements from space-based spectrometers confirm this partitioning, with the visible and near-infrared together comprising over 90% of the total irradiance.

Luminosity and Spectrum

The Sun's absolute bolometric magnitude is 4.74, representing its intrinsic brightness integrated over all wavelengths when viewed from a standard distance of 10 parsecs. This value is defined by the International Astronomical Union (IAU) zero point for bolometric magnitudes, corresponding to a total luminosity of 3.828 10 W. From Earth, at an average distance of 1 astronomical unit, the Sun's apparent visual magnitude is -26.74, making it by far the brightest object in the nighttime sky and dominating the daytime illumination.
The Sun's spectrum features a continuous distribution of radiation that closely follows Planck's law for blackbody thermal emission, with an effective temperature of approximately 5772 K. Superimposed on this continuum are thousands of dark absorption lines, known as Fraunhofer lines, which interrupt the otherwise smooth emission profile. These lines, first cataloged in detail by Joseph von Fraunhofer in the early 19th century, include prominent examples such as the H-alpha line at 656.3 nm from hydrogen. They arise from the absorption of specific wavelengths by cooler gas in the Sun's outer atmosphere (photosphere and chromosphere), where atoms and ions selectively remove photons at discrete energies matching their electronic transitions.
In stellar classification, the Sun is designated as a G2V star, indicating a main-sequence dwarf of spectral type G2 (yellowish-white) with luminosity class V (dwarf). This places it among G-type stars, which constitute about 7.6% of main-sequence stars in the solar neighborhood and are characterized by surface temperatures between 5200 K and 6000 K. As a G2V star, the Sun is currently in the stable hydrogen-fusion phase of its evolution on the main sequence, a stage it has occupied for about 4.6 billion years.

Magnetic Phenomena

Sunspots and Cycles

Sunspots are temporary dark regions on the Sun's photosphere, appearing cooler than surrounding areas with umbral temperatures of 35004500 K. They consist of a dark central umbra, where strong magnetic fields suppress convection, surrounded by a lighter penumbra with weaker fields permitting some heat transport. Sunspots typically form in bipolar groups following Joy's law: the leading spot (in the direction of rotation) lies closer to the equator, with tilt increasing with latitude due to the Coriolis force on rising magnetic flux tubes.
Solar magnetic activity follows an approximately 11-year solar cycle, during which sunspot numbers rise and fall, corresponding to a 22-year magnetic cycle with periodic reversals of global field polarity. Hale's polarity rules state that leading spots in bipolar groups have opposite polarities in each hemisphere, with the pattern reversing every 11-year cycle. The butterfly diagram plots sunspot latitudes over time, revealing equatorward migration of activity bands from about 35 latitude at cycle onset toward the equator at maximum, driven by the dynamo process.
Historical grand solar minima, such as the Maunder minimum (16451715), featured dramatically reduced sunspot activity, likely from suppressed dynamo action.
Solar Cycle 25 began in December 2019. Initial predictions estimated a peak smoothed sunspot number of 115 in July 2025, but updated assessments indicated the maximum phase occurred around October 2024 with smoothed values of 137173, potentially extending into 2025. Activity remained high into 2025, with a daily sunspot number reaching at least 299 on August 8, 2025the highest in over 20 years.
These cycles arise from the - dynamo model: the effect from helical turbulence generates poloidal fields from toroidal, while the effect from differential rotation shears toroidal fields from poloidal, producing oscillatory regeneration of the magnetic field. The cycle period results from dynamo wave propagation, balancing generation rates against magnetic diffusion to yield approximately 11 years.

Flares and Coronal Mass Ejections

Solar flares are sudden, intense bursts of radiation from the Sun's atmosphere, primarily observed as peaks in X-ray emission. They occur when twisted and stressed magnetic fields in the corona undergo magnetic reconnection, where oppositely directed field lines break and reconnect, rapidly converting stored magnetic energy into thermal and kinetic energy, accelerated particles, and electromagnetic radiation across the spectrum from radio waves to gamma rays.
The Geostationary Operational Environmental Satellite (GOES) system classifies flares by peak X-ray flux in the 18 wavelength band: A (weakest, ~10^{-8} W/m), B, C, M, and X (strongest, 10^{-4} W/m), with each class ten times more intense than the previous and subclasses 19 within AM (X-class has no upper limit). The strongest recorded flare was an X28 in 2003, releasing approximately 10^{32} erg; typical flares range from 10^{24} to 10^{32} erg.
Coronal mass ejections (CMEs) are massive expulsions of magnetized plasma from the corona, often triggered by the same magnetic reconnection processes as flares. They eject up to ~10^{12} kg of material at speeds from 250 km/s to over 3,000 km/s (typically 4002,000 km/s), forming expanding clouds threaded by intense magnetic fields.
CMEs are classified by apparent angular width from Earth: partial (narrow), partial halo (>120 but <360), and full halo (360, often Earth-directed). They commonly originate from active regions with sunspots or from erupting prominences due to magnetic flux reconfiguration.
Flares and CMEs peak during solar maximum, with flare rates up to 20 per day and ~23 CMEs per day. CME kinetic energy ranges from 10^{28} to 10^{32} erg, comparable to large flares. In early November 2025, active region AR 14274 produced multiple X- and M-class flares accompanied by full-halo CMEs, reflecting elevated solar activity.
NASA's Parker Solar Probe, launched in 2018, has provided close-range observations, approaching within 3.8 million miles of the Sun. It has revealed switchbacksabrupt reversals in the solar wind's magnetic fieldlikely from near-Sun reconnection, contributing to coronal heating. In July 2025, Parker imaged nascent CMEs, advancing models of eruption dynamics.

Evolutionary History

Formation and Pre-Main Sequence

The Sun formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from the gravitational collapse of a dense fragment within a giant molecular cloud composed mainly of hydrogen and helium with trace heavier elements. This collapse was triggered by Jeans instability, in which local density fluctuations exceeded thermal pressure support, leading to fragmentation and contraction.
Conservation of angular momentum caused the collapsing fragment to rotate faster and flatten into a disk. Most of the mass (about 99.8%) concentrated at the center to form the protosolar core, while the remaining material coalesced into a protoplanetary disk.
The young Sun then entered the T Tauri phase for a few million years, transitioning from an embedded protostar to a pre-main-sequence star surrounded by its protoplanetary disk. Intense accretion from the disk onto the stellar surface, combined with powerful magnetic-driven stellar winds and coronal heating, expelled much of the remaining gas and dust envelope. These outflows dispersed the natal material and shaped the early solar environment, while the disk provided material for planet formation.
During pre-main-sequence evolution, the Sun followed the Hayashi tracka nearly vertical path on the Hertzsprung-Russell diagramas a fully convective protostar contracted under gravity. This released Kelvin-Helmholtz energy from gravitational potential, gradually decreasing surface temperature and luminosity over tens of millions of years. Contraction also reduced the moment of inertia, causing significant spin-up compared to the present rotation rate.
Core temperatures eventually reached about 10 million Kelvin, igniting hydrogen fusion and marking the start of the main-sequence phase.
Key evidence comes from calcium-aluminum-rich inclusions (CAIs) in primitive meteoritesthe oldest solid materials in the Solar Systemdated to 4,567.3 0.2 million years ago using uranium-lead isotope chronometry. This aligns closely with the inferred formation age of the Sun.
Additionally, the Sun's surface lithium abundance is depleted by a factor of about 140 relative to meteoritic values. This depletion results from deep convective mixing during the fully convective pre-main-sequence phase, which transported lithium to hotter interior layers where it was destroyed, consistent with theoretical models for solar-mass stars.

Main Sequence and Future Phases

The Sun is currently in its main sequence phase, fusing hydrogen into helium in its core via the proton-proton chain. This phase, the longest in the life cycle of a star of its mass, began approximately 4.6 billion years ago and is expected to last a total of about 10 billion years. As a G2V-type star, it occupies the main sequence on the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram, where core fusion balances gravitational contraction to maintain stable luminosity and temperature.
Roughly halfway through this phase, the core retains about 70% of its original hydrogen, with the rest converted to helium, gradually increasing core density and temperature. This sustains the Sun's current size, effective temperature of about 5,500 K, and luminosity, ensuring long-term stability for the inner Solar System. Core hydrogen exhaustion is projected in approximately 5 billion years, ending the main sequence.
After core hydrogen depletion, the core contracts and heats while hydrogen fusion ignites in a surrounding shell, causing the outer layers to expand dramatically into a red giant phase beginning in about 5 billion years. The Sun's radius will reach roughly Earth's current orbital distance (about 1 AU), potentially engulfing Mercury, Venus, and possibly Earth. Its surface will cool to red hues while luminosity increases by thousands of times.
In the dense, degenerate core, accumulated helium will ignite in a sudden helium flasha rapid fusion event producing carbon and oxygen via the triple-alpha processtemporarily stabilizing the star on the horizontal branch for about 100 million years of core helium fusion.
As core helium depletes, further shell burning will cause expansion onto the asymptotic giant branch, accompanied by intense mass loss through stellar winds that eject the outer envelope as a planetary nebula. This will expose the hot core and leave a white dwarf remnant of about half the Sun's original mass, composed primarily of carbon and oxygen, which will cool over trillions of years without further fusion. The Sun will shed 3050% of its mass during these late stages.

Position and Motion

Within the Solar System

The Sun comprises approximately 99.86% of the Solar System's total mass, gravitationally binding all bodies from Mercury to the distant Oort Cloud. This central mass places the system's barycenterthe common center of massvery close to the Sun's geometric center, typically within a few solar radii. Perturbations from Jupiter, the most massive planet, cause slight wobbling; at maximum, the barycenter extends beyond the photosphere by about 7% of the Sun's radius when Jupiter is at opposition.
The Sun's gravity governs planetary orbits, which follow Kepler's three laws: planets trace elliptical paths with the Sun at one focus, sweep equal areas in equal times, and have orbital periods squared proportional to the cubes of their semi-major axes. These laws, later explained by Newton's law of universal gravitation, approximate two-body motion because the Sun's mass vastly exceeds the combined mass of the planets.
The Sun's influence extends to its Hill sphere, roughly 1 parsec (about 3.26 light-years) in radius, where its gravity dominates over tidal forces from the Milky Way. This boundary encloses the Oort Cloud and marks the Solar System's outer limit.
This structure formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a collapsing molecular cloud, with the Sun accreting nearly all available mass and the remaining material coalescing into a protoplanetary disk that gave rise to the planets in stable orbits.
Observations of exoplanetary systems around Sun-like (G-type) stars show that roughly half may host rocky planets in habitable zones, indicating that mass-dominant central stars commonly support multi-planet systems similar to our own.

Galactic Orbit and Neighborhood

The Sun orbits the center of the Milky Way galaxy at an average distance of approximately 8.2 kiloparsecs (about 27,000 light-years), traveling at a speed of roughly 230 kilometers per second relative to the galactic center. This motion completes one full revolution, known as a galactic year, in about 230 million years.
The solar system resides within the Local Bubble, a low-density cavity in the interstellar medium spanning roughly 1,000 light-years across, sculpted by the explosions of approximately 15 supernovae over the past 10 to 20 million years. This hot, tenuous region, with gas temperatures exceeding 1 million Kelvin, surrounds the Sun and influences its local environment, including potential perturbations to the Oort cloud from supernova shock waves that could eject small particles or alter orbits of distant cometary bodies.
Positioned in the Orion Arm (also called the Orion Spur), a minor spiral feature between the Sagittarius and Perseus arms, the Sun's immediate stellar neighborhood includes the nearest star, Proxima Centauri, at 4.24 light-years away, part of the Alpha Centauri system. Within 50 light-years, there are approximately 1,500 known stars, predominantly low-mass red dwarfs, forming a sparse but dynamic local cluster.
As the Sun traverses the galaxy, its passages through denser spiral arms expose the solar system to elevated cosmic ray fluxes from increased supernova activity and star formation, potentially modulating interstellar radiation levels over tens of millions of years. The heliosphere, the Sun's protective bubble of solar wind, interacts with the diffuse gas in the Local Bubble, shaping the influx of interstellar particles.

Historical Observations

Ancient and Pre-Modern Understanding

Ancient civilizations developed empirical knowledge of the Sun through systematic observations, often blending astronomy with philosophical, religious, and practical concerns. Babylonian astronomers recorded solar eclipses on clay tablets from at least 747 BCE, using these to predict events and interpret omens.
In ancient Greece, Anaxagoras (c. 500428 BCE) correctly explained solar eclipses as the Moon passing between Earth and Sun and identified the Moon as a reflector of sunlight rather than a luminous body. Around 280 BCE, Aristarchus of Samos proposed a heliocentric model in which Earth and the planets orbit the Sun, with Earth also rotating on its axis to explain day and night. This view remained largely overshadowed by geocentric models. In contrast, Claudius Ptolemy's Almagest (c. 150 CE) formalized a geocentric system with the Sun and planets revolving around a stationary Earth, using epicycles to match observations. It dominated Western astronomy for over a millennium.
Nicolaus Copernicus revived heliocentrism in his 1543 work De revolutionibus orbium coelestium, arguing that Earth orbits the Sun annually, which accounts for planetary retrograde motion and seasonal changes. The model was simpler than Ptolemy's but met resistance due to conflicts with Aristotelian physics and religious teachings.
Telescopic observations in the early 17th century revealed the Sun's dynamic nature. In 1610, Galileo Galilei used a projected image to safely observe sunspotsdark, transient features on the solar surfacechallenging the Aristotelian view of the heavens as perfect and immutable.
Debates over sunspots followed among European astronomers. In 1611, David Fabricius and his son Johannes published the first account asserting that the spots were solar phenomena rather than transiting stars. Christoph Scheiner, observing independently from 1611, initially interpreted them as small satellites orbiting the Sun but later accepted their solar origin through detailed drawings and analysis, leading to a published dispute with Galileo over their nature and implications for celestial perfection.
In 1672, Isaac Newton demonstrated through prism experiments that white sunlight consists of a spectrum of colors, providing key insights into the Sun as a source of composite light.
Non-Western cultures also developed sophisticated solar observations. The ancient Maya created interlocking calendars, including the 365-day haab' aligned with the solar year, which enabled precise tracking of solstices, equinoxes, and agricultural cycles through architectural alignments such as those at Chichn Itz. In China, astronomers recorded solar phenomena as early as 800 BCE, including sunspots described as "black spots" in historical annals and possible flare-related aurora-like events such as "red vapors" noted in 139 BCE, offering the earliest systematic naked-eye evidence of solar variability.

Modern Scientific Developments

In the early 19th century, Joseph von Fraunhofer systematically studied dark absorption lines in the solar spectrum. He independently rediscovered them in 1814 and mapped over 500 lines with precise wavelength measurements using his spectroscope. These lines, later named Fraunhofer lines, provided the first detailed spectral map of sunlight and established the basis for quantitative solar spectroscopy.
In 1859, Gustav Kirchhoff and Robert Bunsen showed that these lines arise from atomic absorption by elements such as hydrogen and sodium in the Sun's cooler outer layers. The dark lines matched bright emission lines produced by the same elements in laboratory flames, confirming spectroscopy as a tool for remote chemical analysis of celestial bodies.
Systematic sunspot observations revealed periodic cycles. In 1843, Samuel Heinrich Schwabe announced an approximately 10-year cycle in sunspot numbers based on 17 years of daily records from 1826 to 1843. Extended monitoring later refined the period to about 11 years.
In 1913, George Ellery Hale discovered magnetic fields in sunspots using the Zeeman effect at Mount Wilson Observatory. He measured fields of several thousand gauss and formulated Hale's polarity law: sunspots in each hemisphere show opposite magnetic polarities during a cycle, with reversal between cycles. This connected sunspots to solar magnetism and accounted for the 22-year magnetic cycle.
Theoretical models of the solar interior advanced in the 1910s. In 1916, Karl Schwarzschild applied general relativity to develop the first exact solution for a spherically symmetric, incompressible fluid star under gravitational equilibrium. This model predicted density and pressure profiles in stellar interiors.
Helioseismology originated in the 1960s. In 1962, Robert Leighton, Robert Noyes, and George Simon detected global solar oscillations through Doppler shifts in spectral lines, revealing 5-minute velocity variations. These p-mode acoustic waves propagate through the interior, enabling inferences of density, temperature, and rotation profiles.
Solar neutrino detection began in 1968 at the Homestake Mine, where Raymond Davis Jr. used a chlorine-based detector to capture electron neutrinos from core proton-proton fusion. The measured flux was about one-third the value predicted by standard solar models, creating the solar neutrino problem. In 2001, the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory resolved this discrepancy by detecting all neutrino flavors. The total ^8B neutrino flux matched predictions, while electron neutrinos comprised only about one-third, confirming flavor oscillations en route to Earth and validating the Sun's core as a pp-chain fusion reactor.

Space-Based Missions

Space-based missions have revolutionized our understanding of the Sun by providing continuous, high-resolution observations free from Earth's atmospheric interference. These spacecraft, often joint efforts between agencies like NASA and ESA, employ advanced instruments to probe the solar interior, atmosphere, and heliosphere, revealing phenomena such as coronal mass ejections (CMEs) and the origins of the solar wind. Key missions since the 1990s have built upon ground-based observations to deliver unprecedented data on solar dynamics and their impacts on space weather.
The Solar and Heliospheric Observatory (SOHO), launched in 1995 as a collaboration between NASA and ESA, remains a cornerstone for solar monitoring. Positioned at the Sun-Earth L1 Lagrange point, SOHO's Large Angle and Spectrometric Coronagraph (LASCO) has imaged over 30,000 CMEs since its inception, enabling detailed studies of their structure, speed, and evolution as they propagate through the corona. Additionally, instruments like the Global Oscillation at Low Frequency (GOLF) and the Michelson Doppler Imager (MDI) have advanced helioseismology by analyzing solar oscillations to map the Sun's internal structure, including its convection zone and rotation patterns. SOHO's longevity, extended multiple times, continues to provide real-time alerts for space weather events affecting Earth.
Launched in 2010, NASA's Solar Dynamics Observatory (SDO) offers unparalleled high-resolution imaging of the Sun's atmosphere across multiple wavelengths. Its Atmospheric Imaging Assembly (AIA) captures solar activity at resolutions up to 0.5 arcseconds every 12 seconds, revealing fine-scale structures in flares, prominences, and coronal loops. The Helioseismic and Magnetic Imager (HMI) on SDO maps the Sun's magnetic field with vector magnetograms, tracking the evolution of sunspots and active regions to model magnetic reconnection processes. By 2025, SDO's decade-plus dataset has been instrumental in correlating magnetic field changes with solar eruptions, enhancing predictions of geomagnetic storms.
NASA's Parker Solar Probe, launched in 2018, has achieved the closest approaches to the Sun of any spacecraft, reaching perihelia of about 3.8 million miles (equivalent to roughly 8.5 solar radii) during its 24th orbit in June 2025. Equipped with instruments like the Wide-field Imager for Solar Probe (WISPR), it directly samples the solar corona and wind, providing in-situ measurements that trace the acceleration and heating mechanisms of the solar wind streams. Parker has also confirmed the existence of a dust-free zone within approximately 0.2 AU of the Sun, where solar heating vaporizes interplanetary dust, reshaping models of the inner heliosphere.
The Solar Orbiter, a joint ESA-NASA mission launched in 2020, uniquely observes the Sun from high inclinations, achieving polar views for the first time in June 2025. Its suite of remote-sensing and in-situ instruments, including the Polarimetric and Helioseismic Imager (PHI), measures plasma and magnetic fields close to the Sun, linking surface activity to heliospheric structures. These observations have yielded insights into the solar dynamo, revealing how polar magnetic fields reverse and drive the 11-year cycle through differential rotation.
India's Aditya-L1, launched by ISRO in September 2023, represents the nation's first dedicated solar mission and is positioned at the Sun-Earth L1 point for uninterrupted observations. The Visible Emission Line Coronagraph (VELC) instrument focuses on the chromosphere and corona, imaging dynamic events like spicules and prominences in visible and UV light to study mass and energy transport in the solar atmosphere. By November 2025, Aditya-L1's data has complemented international efforts by providing multi-wavelength views of chromospheric heating mechanisms.

Direct Observation and Effects

Safe Viewing Methods

Direct observation of the Sun risks severe eye damage from intense ultraviolet (UV) and visible light, which can cause solar retinopathy and permanent vision loss without immediate pain. Unaided viewing is safe only during the brief totality phase of a total solar eclipse, when the Moon fully obscures the Sun's disk. At all other timesincluding partial and annular eclipseseven momentary exposure must be avoided.
Safe direct viewing requires filters meeting the ISO 12312-2 standard, such as eclipse glasses or handheld solar viewers, which block over 99.999% of visible light and nearly all UV and infrared radiation. Filters must be undamaged and purchased from reputable manufacturers; ordinary sunglasses, household glass, exposed film, or unverified materials are dangerous and inadequate. For indirect viewing, pinhole projectors provide a simple, filter-free method: sunlight passes through a small hole (in an index card, colander, or foil-covered box) onto a shaded surface, forming an image of the Sun's disk that can be enlarged or viewed collectively.
Telescopes and binoculars require full-aperture solar filters (such as Mylar or neutral density) mounted securely at the front; eyepiece filters are unsafe, as they can overheat and shatter. During solar eclipses, use certified filters throughout partial phases and remove them only during confirmed totality. Brief phenomena such as Baily's beads and the diamond ring effect, visible just before and after totality, must also be observed through proper filters due to intense light. Smartphone apps from NASA or astronomical societies offer precise local timings to guide safe viewing transitions.
Advanced observations use hydrogen-alpha (H-alpha) filters tuned to 656.3 nm to view the chromosphere, including prominences and filaments. These require dedicated solar telescopes with energy-rejection pre-filters to block excess UV and infrared radiation; double-stacked etalons improve contrast. Equipment must come from certified suppliers to ensure safety.

Visual Phenomena and Impacts

The Sun produces a variety of striking visual phenomena observable from Earth, primarily through the interaction of its light with atmospheric particles. Solar halos, appearing as luminous rings encircling the Sun, form when sunlight refracts through hexagonal ice crystals suspended in high-altitude cirrus clouds, bending the light by approximately 22 degrees to create the common 22-degree halo. Parhelia, commonly known as sun dogs, manifest as bright spots on either side of the Sun, resulting from the same refraction process in plate-shaped ice crystals aligned horizontally, often accompanied by colorful arcs or tails. Rainbows arise from the dispersion and internal reflection of sunlight within spherical water droplets in the atmosphere, separating white light into its spectral colors and forming an arc opposite the Sun's position. Crepuscular rays, or "God rays," occur when sunlight streams through gaps in clouds or between atmospheric layers, with the rays appearing to converge due to perspective, enhanced by scattering of light by air molecules and particles.
These solar emissions also exert profound impacts on Earth's magnetosphere and atmosphere. Auroras, the vibrant polar lights, are triggered when charged particles from coronal mass ejections (CMEs)often following solar flaresinteract with Earth's magnetic field and collide with atmospheric gases, exciting them to emit light in greens, reds, and purples primarily near the poles. The Van Allen radiation belts, two doughnut-shaped zones of energetic particles trapped by Earth's magnetic field, are dynamically influenced by solar activity; the outer belt, composed largely of solar-origin electrons and protons, expands and intensifies during periods of high solar wind from CMEs, posing risks to satellites and astronauts. Solar irradiance, the total energy output from the Sun reaching Earth, modulates global climate by driving atmospheric and oceanic circulations; variations in total solar irradiance, though small (about 0.1% over an 11-year cycle), contribute to long-term temperature fluctuations and patterns like the El Nio-Southern Oscillation.
Space weather events stemming from solar activity can disrupt technological systems on Earth. Geomagnetic storms, induced by CMEs interacting with Earth's magnetosphere, are quantified by the Kp index, a global measure of magnetic field disturbances ranging from 0 (calm) to 9 (extreme), with storms at Kp 5 causing widespread effects. These storms can induce geomagnetically induced currents in power grids, leading to blackouts, as seen in the March 1989 Quebec event where a severe storm caused a nine-hour power failure affecting six million people due to transformer overloads. Satellites experience disruptions from enhanced radiation and drag in the swollen upper atmosphere during such storms, potentially shortening mission lifespans or causing communication failures.
Biologically, sunlight is essential for human health but carries risks from its ultraviolet (UV) components. UVB radiation from the Sun penetrates the skin to convert 7-dehydrocholesterol into previtamin D3, which isomerizes to vitamin D3, supporting calcium absorption and bone health; adequate exposure (about 10-30 minutes midday several times a week) suffices for most people without supplementation. However, excessive UV exposure increases skin cancer risk, with non-melanoma types like basal and squamous cell carcinomas linked to cumulative UVB doses, while melanoma correlates with intense, intermittent exposures. The UV index, a scale from 0 to 11+ forecasting erythemally weighted UV irradiance, guides protective measures; indices above 3 indicate moderate risk, prompting shade-seeking and sunscreen use to mitigate DNA damage in skin cells.
