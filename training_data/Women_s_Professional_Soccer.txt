Women's Professional Soccer

1x
 Women's Professional Soccer (WPS) was a professional women's soccer league in the United States that operated from 2009 to 2011. It succeeded the Women's United Soccer Association (WUSA), which ran from 2001 to 2003 and folded due to financial difficulties despite averaging around 8,000 spectators per match in its early seasons.
Established in 2008 by the United States Soccer Federation in partnership with Division I universities and independent investors, WPS aimed to provide a sustainable platform for top female soccer players following the success of the U.S. women's national team at the 1999 FIFA Women's World Cup. The league launched its inaugural season in 2009 with seven teams: Boston Soccers, Chicago Red Stars, FC Gold Pride, Los Angeles Sol, New Jersey Sky Blue, Saint Louis Athletica, and Washington Freedom. These franchises competed in a 10-game regular season plus playoffs, featuring international stars such as Marta, Kelly Smith, and Hope Solo alongside U.S. national team players.
WPS averaged about 5,000 attendees per match in 2009, with the championship final drawing over 7,000 fans when Sky Blue FC defeated the Los Angeles Sol. The league expanded to eight teams in 2010 but faced increasing financial challenges, including sponsorship shortfalls and economic recession impacts. By 2011, several teams folded mid-season, leading to WPS's suspension of operations on February 7, 2012, after cumulative losses exceeded $30 million.
Despite its short lifespan, WPS played a key role in advancing professional opportunities for women in soccer, paving the way for successors like the National Women's Soccer League (NWSL), founded in 2012.

History

Formation and Planning

The collapse of the Women's United Soccer Association (WUSA) in September 2003 left a significant void in professional women's soccer in the United States, as the league suspended operations after three seasons due to financial losses exceeding $100 million, driven by insufficient sponsorship and declining attendance. This closure occurred just days before the 2003 FIFA Women's World Cup, highlighting the precarious financial foundation of the sport despite growing popularity from the U.S. women's national team's successes. For the next several years, no top-tier professional league existed, prompting efforts to revive structured professional play amid increased interest following the 2004 Athens Olympics.
In response to this gap, the Women's Soccer Initiative, Inc. (WSII), a non-profit organization, was established in November 2004 with support from the WUSA Players Union and the United States Soccer Federation (USSF). WSII aimed to promote and support professional opportunities for women soccer players by facilitating the creation of a new league, conducting market research, and building stakeholder partnerships. Funded initially by modest grants totaling around $100,000 from the USSF, the organization focused on long-term viability through strategic planning rather than immediate launch.
Key planning milestones unfolded over the following years, including securing initial investors and selecting franchises. By February 2007, six ownership groups from major U.S. citiesrepresenting markets such as Los Angeles, Dallas, and Chicagosigned letters of intent to join the new league, committing to operational and financial responsibilities. This was expanded in April 2007 when two additional groups from New York/New Jersey and Boston joined, forming the basis for an initial eight-franchise structure, though plans initially targeted eight markets. On September 4, 2007, the league was formally announced under the temporary name Women's Soccer LLC, marking the culmination of WSII's preparatory efforts and collaboration with the USSF for developmental support. These steps addressed prior leagues' shortcomings by emphasizing sustainable investment and market selection, though delays pushed the inaugural season to 2009.

Launch and Inaugural Season

The Women's Professional Soccer (WPS) league officially launched its inaugural season on March 29, 2009, with the opening match featuring the Los Angeles Sol hosting the Washington Freedom at The Home Depot Center in Carson, California. The Sol secured a 2-0 victory, with goals from Aleshia Ocasio and Marta, drawing an announced crowd of 14,832 fans and marking a promising start to professional women's soccer in the United States following years of planning and anticipation.
The 2009 season consisted of seven teams: the Boston Breakers, Chicago Red Stars, FC Gold Pride, Los Angeles Sol, Saint Louis Athletica, Sky Blue FC, and Washington Freedom. Each team played a 20-game regular season schedule, evenly split between home and away matches, culminating in a postseason playoff involving the top four teams in a single-elimination format: a first-round match, a super semifinal, and a championship final. The regular season ran from late March to early August, showcasing high-level competition with international stars like Marta of the Sol and Abby Wambach of the Washington Freedom.
In the playoffs, the Los Angeles Sol, who finished first in the regular season with a 12-3-5 record, advanced to the final after defeating the Saint Louis Athletica in the super semifinal. However, Sky Blue FC, the fourth-seeded team that had barely qualified for the postseason, staged an upset run, defeating the Washington Freedom in the first round and the Athletica earlier before edging the Sol 1-0 in the championship match on August 22, 2009, at The Home Depot Center to claim the inaugural WPS title. Heather O'Reilly scored the lone goal for Sky Blue FC in the 114th minute of extra time, securing the victory before 7,218 spectators.
The season highlighted standout individual performances, particularly from Marta, who led the league with 10 goals and earned the Most Valuable Player award for her pivotal role in the Sol's regular-season dominance. Overall, WPS drew a total attendance of 323,878 across 70 regular-season games, averaging 4,618 fans per match, with notable highs including 16,089 for a Washington Freedom home game against Sky Blue FC. These figures underscored growing interest in women's professional soccer, though challenges in building sustained fan engagement emerged as key themes from the debut year.

Expansion and Challenges

Following the inaugural 2009 season, Women's Professional Soccer (WPS) sought to stabilize and grow its footprint by adding two expansion teams for 2010, the Atlanta Beat and Philadelphia Independence, to offset the folding of the Los Angeles Sol in January 2010 due to chronic underfunding and failure to attract expected crowds despite featuring high-profile players like Marta. This kept the league at seven teams at the start of the 2010 season: Atlanta Beat, Boston Breakers, Chicago Red Stars, FC Gold Pride, Philadelphia Independence, Saint Louis Athletica, and Washington Freedom. However, expansion efforts stalled as no further franchises materialized amid economic uncertainties, and the league operated without net growth.
The 2010 season was marked by early operational setbacks when the Saint Louis Athletica abruptly folded in May after just four home games, citing a severe funding crisis that left the club unable to cover payroll and operational expenses despite ranking last in attendance with an average of about 2,300 fans per match. This reduced the league to six teams for the remainder of the year, forcing a reshuffled schedule and highlighting vulnerabilities in team ownership stability. FC Gold Pride emerged as champions, defeating the Philadelphia Independence 4-0 in the WPS Championship on September 26 at Toyota Park in Bridgeview, Illinois, but the club folded shortly afterward in November due to unsustainable financial losses exceeding $2 million.
Entering 2011, WPS launched with six teams after the addition of the expansion Western New York Flash, offset by the prior year's folds and the relocation of the Washington Freedom to Florida as magicJack; the teams were Atlanta Beat, Boston Breakers, magicJack, Philadelphia Independence, Sky Blue FC, and Western New York Flash. The Western New York Flash claimed the championship, beating the Philadelphia Independence 1-0 in sudden-death extra time on August 27 at Sahlen's Stadium in Rochester, New York, before a crowd of 10,461 that set a WPS final attendance record. Interest surged following the United States' runner-up finish in the 2011 FIFA Women's World Cup, with league attendance roughly doubling from an average of 2,881 per game before the tournament to 5,126 afterward, excluding playoffs, as returning national team stars like Abby Wambach and Hope Solo drew larger crowds to showcase their skills.
Despite this temporary boost, WPS grappled with persistent challenges, including rising operational costs from a bloated central front office and uneven revenue streams, as teams averaged just 3,518 fans per regular-season game overalla slight decline from 2010while inconsistent sponsorships failed to cover deficits amid a weak economic climate. Playoff matches saw higher turnout, averaging around 5,000 spectators, but fluctuations underscored broader issues like limited marketing budgets focused on grassroots efforts rather than national campaigns. These pressures contributed to the formation of the WPS Players Union in early 2011 to address labor concerns amid the instability.

Decline and Dissolution

On January 30, 2012, Women's Professional Soccer (WPS) announced the suspension of its 2012 season, citing ongoing internal conflicts, including a costly legal dispute with former franchise owner Dan Borislow, as well as broader financial instability that made operations unsustainable. The league's board of governors voted to halt play in hopes of restructuring and resuming in 2013, but the decision came amid mounting pressures from unpaid obligations and team owner frustrations.
Despite attempts at reorganization, WPS officially folded on May 18, 2012, after just three completed seasons (20092011), with the board permanently suspending all operations and dissolving the league. The closure followed failed negotiations to resolve the Borislow litigation and secure stable funding, leaving the league unable to continue without further investment.
Key contributing factors included significant financial losses, with individual team owners reporting deficits in the millions of dollars annuallysuch as one owner's $2 million loss in 2010 and projected $1 million loss in 2011exacerbated by the lack of major television deals and the impacts of the ongoing economic recession. WPS relied on limited broadcasts through Fox Soccer Channel, which drew modest viewership (over 100,000 for select 2009 games), but without lucrative national partnerships, revenue remained insufficient to offset operational costs. The recession, starting in 2008, further strained sponsorship and attendance, reducing the league from seven teams in 2009 to six by 2011.
In the immediate aftermath, WPS players faced dispersal, with many U.S. national team members left without domestic clubs and turning to overseas leagues in Europe and Asia for employment. The U.S. Soccer Federation provided transitional support by facilitating player placements and contributing to the groundwork for a successor league, helping to mitigate the abrupt end for top talents.

Organization and Governance

Business Model and Operations

Women's Professional Soccer (WPS) operated on a grassroots business model designed to build sustainable growth from the local level, prioritizing community engagement and regional support in the absence of significant national media revenue. The league launched without a television rights deal, relying instead on limited local broadcasts and a modest media budget to promote matches. This approach emphasized securing sponsorships from businesses in team markets, fostering direct ties between franchises and their communities to offset operational costs.
Team budgets were constrained to approximately $2.5 million annually per franchise, reflecting the league's cautious financial strategy amid uncertain revenue streams. This cap encompassed player salaries, with an inaugural salary cap of $565,000 per team to control spending, and extended to staffing, travel, and facility expenses. The model aimed to keep operations lean while investing in on-field quality to attract fans and sponsors, though it proved insufficient to weather economic pressures.
WPS collaborated with Major League Soccer (MLS) to share resources, including marketing support and access to facilities, which helped reduce costs for some franchises. For instance, teams like the Philadelphia Independence utilized MLS venues for home games, leveraging existing infrastructure to host professional matches. This partnership provided operational efficiencies, allowing WPS to focus on player development without building standalone stadiums from scratch.
The league maintained a centralized operational structure, with administrative functions coordinated from its headquarters to standardize scheduling and compliance across franchises. Seasons typically ran from spring to summer, with regular seasons of 20 games in 2009, 24 games in 2010, and 18 games in 2011 per team, culminating in playoffs and a championship final. To balance rosters, teams were limited to four international player slots, prioritizing domestic talent while incorporating global stars to elevate competition. Revenue primarily derived from ticket sales, which averaged around 3,500 attendees per game in later seasons, supplemented by merchandise and corporate sponsorships such as the multi-year deal with Puma as the official kit provider.

Labor Relations

The Women's Professional Soccer Players Union (WPSPU) was established in September 2010 as an independent labor organization to represent and advocate for the league's players, ensuring their professional treatment and input in league decisions. Led by executive director Jennifer Hitchon, the union aimed to address key workplace issues in the nascent league, including fair compensation and support structures amid financial uncertainties.
The union's primary objectives centered on negotiating a collective bargaining agreement (CBA) to improve salaries, which averaged approximately $27,000 in 2010 for most players, with top U.S. national team members earning around $54,000; it also sought enhanced health insurance, pension plans, and per diems to cover travel and living expenses during the season. These efforts were driven by players' desires to professionalize the league, as articulated by union representatives who emphasized the need for sustainable wages and benefits to retain talent. In the 2010 offseason, amid discussions of potential pay cuts and roster reductions following the folding of FC Gold Pride, the union pushed back against owner proposals to lower costs, highlighting disputes over compensation and working conditions without formal strike action but through organized advocacy and grievance filings.
The WPSPU's activities extended to addressing broader labor challenges, such as advocating for safer and more equitable team environments during instances of owner misconduct, including a 2011 grievance against magicJack owner Dan Borislow for creating a hostile workplace. Despite these initiatives, the union ceased operations with the league's dissolution in 2012 due to financial insolvency. Its work, including the push for CBAs and player protections, influenced labor standards in successor leagues like the National Women's Soccer League, where former WPS organizers applied lessons from the union's short tenure to establish stronger player associations.

Leadership Structure

The leadership of Women's Professional Soccer (WPS) was headed by a commissioner who served as the primary executive, overseeing the league's operations from its planning stages through its active seasons. Tonya Antonucci was appointed as the inaugural commissioner on September 4, 2007, and held the position until October 1, 2010, during which she managed the league's launch in 2009 and facilitated early expansion to seven teams. Under her tenure, Antonucci secured provisional Division 1 sanctioning from the U.S. Soccer Federation in February 2008, enabling the league's professional status.
Following Antonucci's departure, Anne-Marie Eileraas assumed the role of chief executive officer (CEO) on October 1, 2010, and served until September 18, 2011, with a focus on restructuring the league amid operational difficulties. Eileraas, who had prior experience in sports marketing, aimed to stabilize finances and team structures during her brief term. Jennifer O'Sullivan then took over as CEO on September 26, 2011, guiding the league through its final season in 2012 until operations were suspended in February 2012. As a sports attorney, O'Sullivan handled the wind-down process, including negotiations with the U.S. Soccer Federation for conditional sanctioning to operate a reduced five-team league.
The commissioner and CEO roles encompassed key responsibilities such as approving new franchises, negotiating sponsorship agreements, and coordinating with the league's board of governors, which consisted of team owners including chairman T. Fitz Johnson of the Atlanta Beat. The board provided strategic oversight, while the U.S. Soccer Federation maintained significant influence through its sanctioning authority and representatives involved in approval processes for league operations and Division 1 status.
Leadership transitions were frequent and driven by mounting financial pressures, including sponsorship shortfalls and operational costs, leading to Antonucci's exit after the 2010 season, Eileraas's resignation in 2011, and O'Sullivan's oversight of the league's suspension without a successor appointed. No permanent CEO was established following the 2012 shutdown, as WPS ceased operations entirely.

Teams and Competition

Franchise List and Locations

Women's Professional Soccer (WPS) launched in 2009 with seven inaugural franchises spread across the United States, aiming to establish a professional platform for women's soccer in key markets. These teams were the Boston Breakers based in Boston, Massachusetts; Chicago Red Stars in Chicago, Illinois; FC Gold Pride in Santa Clara, California; Los Angeles Sol in Los Angeles, California; Sky Blue FC in Piscataway, New Jersey; Saint Louis Athletica in Fenton, Missouri; and Washington Freedom in Boyds, Maryland.
Prior to the 2010 season, the league experienced its first contractions, with the Los Angeles Sol folding due to failed ownership negotiations and the Saint Louis Athletica ceasing operations after the inaugural year. To offset these losses, WPS added the Atlanta Beat in Atlanta, Georgia, and the Philadelphia Independence in Chester, Pennsylvania, maintaining a total of seven teams for 2010: the Atlanta Beat, Boston Breakers, Chicago Red Stars, FC Gold Pride, Philadelphia Independence, Sky Blue FC, and Washington Freedom. However, challenges persisted, as the Saint Louis Athletica had already folded earlier, and the FC Gold Pride disbanded shortly after winning the 2010 championship owing to financial insolvency. The Chicago Red Stars also suspended operations after the 2010 season due to insufficient funding.
For its final 2011 season, WPS operated with six franchises following further adjustments: the Atlanta Beat in Atlanta; Boston Breakers continued in Boston; the Washington Freedom moved to Boca Raton, Florida, and rebranded as magicJack; Philadelphia Independence stayed in Chester; Sky Blue FC in Piscataway; and the newly added Western New York Flash in Rochester, New York. This configuration reflected ongoing efforts to stabilize the league amid ownership shifts and relocations.
The league's dissolution in May 2012 led to the end of most franchises, with the Atlanta Beat, FC Gold Pride, Los Angeles Sol, magicJack, Philadelphia Independence, and Saint Louis Athletica fully dissolving without direct successors. However, four WPS teams transitioned into the newly formed National Women's Soccer League (NWSL) in 2013: the Boston Breakers, Chicago Red Stars (revived), Sky Blue FC, and Western New York Flash, providing continuity for players and some organizational elements.

Player Acquisition and Drafts

The Women's Professional Soccer (WPS) league assembled its initial rosters through a structured allocation process for U.S. national team players, followed by targeted drafts for domestic and international talent. On September 16, 2008, shortly after the U.S. women's national team's gold medal win at the Beijing Olympics, WPS conducted its player allocation draft in New York City, distributing 21 players from the national team pool equally among the league's seven founding franchisesthree players per teamto form the core of each squad. This allocation prioritized star power and experience, with players expressing optimism about the league's improved management and marketing compared to its predecessor, the Women's United Soccer Association.
To build out the rosters beyond the allocated national team members, WPS implemented annual drafts starting in late 2008. The inaugural WPS College Draft, held on January 16, 2009, at the National Soccer Coaches Association of America convention in St. Louis, Missouri, selected 70 playersprimarily recent college graduates and undrafted domestic talentacross 10 rounds to fill remaining U.S.-eligible spots. Complementing this, the International Draft allowed teams to secure negotiating rights for non-U.S. players; the first such draft occurred on September 24, 2008, with each of the seven teams selecting four international players to initiate contract discussions. Subsequent college and international drafts followed annually, enabling ongoing roster refreshes while protecting allocated players' rights.
WPS roster rules emphasized a balance of domestic development and global appeal, requiring each team to carry 14 to 18 U.S.-eligible players while permitting up to four international slots to accommodate foreign signings. These constraints, combined with a league-wide salary capinitially set at $225,000 per team in 2009influenced draft strategies, as franchises prioritized cost-effective selections to stay within budget while building competitive depth. The cap, which rose modestly in later seasons, encouraged teams to allocate resources toward high-impact international stars and promising college talents rather than overextending on mid-tier domestic free agents.
Notable examples underscored the drafts' role in attracting elite talent. In the 2009 International Draft, Brazilian superstar Marta was selected third overall by the Los Angeles Sol and signed a three-year contract in January 2009, bringing her FIFA World Player of the Year pedigree to boost the league's visibility. By 2011, amid growing financial uncertainty that foreshadowed the league's eventual suspension, the College Draft still produced highlights, such as Western New York Flash selecting forward Alex Morgan first overall on January 14, a move that highlighted the continued promise of U.S. collegiate soccer despite the league's precarious position.

Media and Popularity

Broadcasting and Coverage

The broadcasting landscape for Women's Professional Soccer (WPS) during its tenure from 2009 to 2011 was characterized by modest national exposure through targeted partnerships, supplemented by regional and digital efforts to build visibility. The league secured a foundational three-year broadcast agreement with Fox Soccer Channel in October 2008, which provided for a national Sunday night game of the week, covering a minimum of 20 regular-season matches annually starting in 2009. This deal positioned Fox Soccer as the primary national broadcaster, airing over 64 live Sunday games across the league's three seasons, with 17 regular-season matches televised in 2011 alone. To expand reach, WPS partnered with Comcast SportsNet in June 2009 for additional regional broadcasts, including four live games on selected networks that year.
Local coverage played a key role in community engagement, with regional sports networks handling home-team broadcasts to complement the national slate. These efforts contributed to dozens of games being televised annually across national and local platforms, though exact totals varied by season based on team markets and production availability. Playoff matches, such as the 2011 Super Semifinal and Championship, received elevated production and airtime on Fox Sports Net in high definition, enhancing the league's postseason profile.
Promotional initiatives focused on digital accessibility and highlights to amplify interest beyond live telecasts. While live streaming was not available during WPS's run, the league's official website offered video recaps and select highlights to engage online audiences. Coverage challenges persisted, particularly in securing broad national exposure prior to the 2011 FIFA Women's World Cup, where average viewership on Fox Soccer hovered around 30,000 per broadcast in the league's later seasons. Post-World Cup, television ratings rose by 18.5 percent, reflecting a temporary surge in interest tied to the U.S. national team's success.

Attendance and Fan Metrics

In its inaugural 2009 season, Women's Professional Soccer (WPS) recorded an average regular season attendance of 4,684 fans per game across 70 matches involving seven teams, totaling 323,878 spectators. Playoff attendance was higher, averaging approximately 5,717 fans per game for the two postseason contests, including 4,217 for the Super Semifinal between the Washington Freedom and Sky Blue FC and 7,218 for the WPS Championship won by Sky Blue FC against the Los Angeles Sol. These figures aligned with the league's modest projections of 4,000 to 6,000 attendees per match, reflecting early interest in professional women's soccer amid growing youth participation.
Attendance dipped in 2010 to an average of 3,601 fans per game across 80 regular season matches with eight teams, a 23% decline from the previous year amid economic challenges and uneven market penetration. The league rebounded slightly in 2011, averaging 3,518 fans per game in the regular season with six teams, though still down 24.9% from 2009; playoff games drew stronger crowds, averaging around 6,268 fans across the Super Semifinal and Championship. A league-record 15,404 fans attended the Western New York Flash's match against magicJack on July 20, 2011, at Sahlen's Stadium, boosted by the recent U.S. women's national team performance in the FIFA Women's World Cup.
WPS fans were primarily families and youth soccer enthusiasts, with the league targeting the broader New England soccer community of over 200,000 registered youth players, as well as casual sports followers and female business owners. Attendance growth, particularly the post-World Cup surge in 2011 where averages rose to 5,164 for select matches, was closely tied to the U.S. women's national team's international success, which heightened visibility for domestic professional play.
Beyond gate figures, engagement metrics showed promise in digital spaces, with WPS amassing 233,400 Twitter followers by mid-2011, supporting broader fan interaction during the league's brief run. Media coverage, including increased broadcasting, contributed to these attendance spikes by amplifying interest in key games.

Awards and Legacy

Individual and Team Honors

Women's Professional Soccer (WPS) recognized outstanding individual and team performances through a series of annual awards presented at the end of each season from 2009 to 2011. These honors highlighted excellence in various categories, with selections emphasizing contributions to team success, statistical achievements, and overall impact on the league. The awards served to celebrate the league's top talents during its brief existence, fostering recognition for players and teams in a growing professional landscape.
The Michelle Akers Player of the Year Award, named after the U.S. soccer legend, was the league's premier individual honor for the most valuable player. It was awarded based on criteria including goals, assists, leadership, and game-changing performances. Brazilian forward Marta dominated early, winning in 2009 with the Los Angeles Sol for her pivotal role in the team's regular-season success, and repeating in 2010 with FC Gold Pride after scoring 19 goals and providing five assists across 24 matches. In 2011, Spain's Veronica Boquete of the Philadelphia Independence claimed the award, recognized for her midfield creativity and contributions to her team's playoff run. Marta stands out as a two-time recipient, underscoring her status as one of the league's most influential stars.
The Golden Boot award went to the regular season's leading goal scorer, a statistical honor that spotlighted offensive prowess. Marta secured this accolade three consecutive years: in 2009 with 10 goals for the Los Angeles Sol, in 2010 with a league-high 19 goals for FC Gold Pride, and in 2011 with 10 goals for Western New York Flash. These wins highlighted her scoring consistency and ability to elevate her teams' attacks. The Rookie of the Year award, introduced in 2010 by U.S. Soccer, honored the top first-year professional; New Zealand defender Ali Riley won in 2010 for her defensive stability with FC Gold Pride, while U.S. forward Christen Press earned it in 2011 with magicJack after a breakout season that included key goals and assists.
Team honors included the Regular Season Shield, awarded to the squad with the best overall record, and the WPS Championship title, determined by the playoff final. The Los Angeles Sol claimed the 2009 Shield with a strong campaign, though Sky Blue FC upset them to win the championship. FC Gold Pride swept both in 2010, finishing atop the standings and defeating Philadelphia Independence 4-0 in the final. Western New York Flash achieved the same double in 2011, topping the regular season before beating Philadelphia 1-0 for the title. These accomplishments reflected the competitive balance and rising quality within WPS franchises.
Awards were selected through a voting process involving league coaches, media members, and players, who evaluated nominees based on performance metrics like goals and assists for offensive honors, shutouts and tackles for defensive ones, and broader impact for MVP criteria. WPS presented 7 to 8 categories annually, including Player of the Year, Golden Boot, Rookie of the Year (from 2010), Coach of the Year, Goalkeeper of the Year, Defender of the Year, and Sportswoman of the Year, ensuring comprehensive recognition across positions and roles.

Influence and Successors

The Women's Professional Soccer (WPS) league, despite its short lifespan from 2009 to 2011, played a pivotal role in demonstrating the market potential for professional women's soccer in the United States, particularly in the wake of the U.S. Women's National Team's strong performance at the 2011 FIFA Women's World Cup. By attracting significant attendance surgessuch as overflow crowds at Western New York Flash gamesand leveraging early social media engagement on platforms like Twitter to build fan communities, WPS highlighted a viable audience for the sport beyond the prior Women's United Soccer Association (WUSA). This visibility and momentum directly influenced the formation of the National Women's Soccer League (NWSL) in 2013, which was launched as a more sustainable successor backed by the U.S. Soccer Federation, Major League Soccer, and initial investments from the Canadian Soccer Association and Mexican Football Federation to allocate and fund top players.
Numerous WPS alumni seamlessly transitioned to the NWSL, bringing experience and star power that helped establish the new league's credibility, with players like Abby Wambachwho competed for the Washington Freedom and magicJack in WPS before leading the Western New York Flash in the NWSLserving as key figures in this continuity. While exact counts vary, dozens of former WPS players, including international talents like Marta and Christine Sinclair, contributed to early NWSL rosters, ensuring a talent pipeline that stabilized the league from its inception. Lessons from WPS's financial challenges, such as team foldings and unsustainable franchise costs, informed NWSL strategies like drastically reduced operating budgetsinitially about one-third of WPS levels, around $1 million per teamand the eventual adoption of collective bargaining agreements starting in 2020 to promote player welfare and revenue sharing.
WPS's broader legacy extended to inspiring increased youth participation in girls' soccer, aligning with the post-2011 World Cup surge that saw high school girls' sports involvement reach a record 3.3 million participants by 2013-14, fostering a deeper talent pool for future professionals. Critiques of WPS's independent franchise model, which led to ownership instability and legal disputes, prompted the NWSL to adopt a single-entity structure for centralized control and risk mitigation, evolving into a hybrid approach that has supported growth. As of 2025, the NWSL thrives with 14 teams, bolstered by expanded media dealsincluding a $240 million four-year agreement across ESPN, CBS, and othersdelivering widespread broadcasts and sponsorships that underscore WPS's foundational, if imperfect, contributions.
Historians and analysts now regard WPS as a critical bridge between the WUSA's collapse in 2003 and the NWSL's endurance, providing essential on-field competition and off-field learnings without any attempts at revival, as its innovations are instead commemorated in narratives of women's soccer evolution.
