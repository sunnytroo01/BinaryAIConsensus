 Huainan is a prefecture-level city in the central-northern part of Anhui Province, People's Republic of China, situated on the banks of the Huaihe River in the hinterland of the Yangtze River Delta. With a population of 3,033,528 as of the 2020 census, it functions as a major industrial hub renowned as the "energy capital of China" and the "industrial granary of East China," primarily due to its extensive coal mining operations and role as a national new energy base. The city administers five districts, two counties, and one national comprehensive experimental zone, supporting a diverse economy that includes electric power generation, chemicals, and emerging sustainable industries.
The region's history traces back thousands of years, with ancient roots in the state of Chu during the Warring States period and later as part of the Han Dynasty's Kingdom of Huainan, named after Liu An, the king who authored the influential Taoist text Huainanzi over 2,000 years ago. This text introduced concepts like the 24 solar terms and is legendarily linked to the invention of tofu, underscoring Huainan's cultural legacy in philosophy, agriculture, and cuisine. The area, historically known as Shouzhou, was the site of the pivotal Feishui Battle in 383 AD, where Eastern Jin forces achieved a famous underdog victory against Former Qin, an event immortalized in Chinese history textbooks and idioms such as "a fleeing army's suspicion of danger at the slightest sound." The modern city emerged in the mid-20th century, rapidly expanding from a minor town called Tianjia'an into a key mining center following the large-scale exploitation and discovery of new coal deposits after the establishment of the People's Republic of China in 1949.
Today, Huainan balances its coal-dependent economy with efforts toward ecological transformation, including urban rehabilitation projects and development of renewable energy sources to address environmental challenges from mining. It features a "2+4" industrial park platform, encompassing one national development zone, one high-tech zone, and four provincial areas, fostering infrastructure like standardized factories to attract investment. Notable cultural and natural sites include Bagong Mountain, tied to ancient scholars and the Huainanzi, and Jiaogang Lake, a scenic area promoting tourism and film production.

History

Ancient origins

The Huainan region has yielded significant paleontological evidence dating back to the early Neoproterozoic era, with the discovery of organic-walled microfossils in the Liulaobei Formation pushing understandings of regional geology to approximately 8001000 million years ago. These microfossils, first reported in studies stemming from explorations in the late 1970s, include diverse eukaryotic forms that illuminate early life evolution in the southern Xuhuai Basin during the Tonian Period (1000720 Ma). The findings, analyzed through biostratigraphic methods, reveal a complex microbial ecosystem predating the Cambrian Explosion and highlight Huainan's role in ancient sedimentary deposits formed amid the breakup of the supercontinent Rodinia.
During the Warring States Period (475221 BC), the area encompassing modern Huainan served as a key political center for the state of Chu, with Shouchun (present-day Shou County, near Huainan) established as its capital around 318 BC following the conquest of the Yue state. This relocation strengthened Chu's strategic position along the Huai River, facilitating defense against northern rivals like Qin and enabling expansion into central China. Archaeological evidence, including the Wuwangdun tomb complex excavated in Huainan, underscores Shouchun's status as a fortified hub of Chu governance, culture, and military power until Qin's conquest in 223 BC. The region's fertile plains and river access supported Chu's agricultural economy and bronze production, contributing to its status as one of the seven major warring states.
The name "Huainan" originates from the Han dynasty's Principality of Huainan, established in 202 BC by Emperor Gaozu (Liu Bang) as a vassal state to reward loyal generals after unifying China. The title was first granted to Ying Bu, a former Qin convict and Chu general who defected to Han during the Chu-Han Contention, assigning him control over territories south of the Huai River (Huai nan, meaning "south of the Huai"). This principality, centered in Lu (modern Lu'an, adjacent to Huainan), encompassed much of modern Anhui's northern plains and served as a buffer against potential rebellions. Ying Bu's enfeoffment reflected Han's strategy of dividing power among merit-based kings, though he rebelled in 196 BC and was defeated, leading to the title's reassignment to Liu Bang's kin.
A prominent legend attributes the invention of tofu to Liu An, Prince of Huainan (c. 179122 BC), who ruled the principality from 164 BC and is said to have developed the process around that time while experimenting with soybeans on Bagong Mountain (Bagongshan). According to tradition, Liu An, seeking an elixir of immortality with his eight scholarly companions (the "Eight Immortals" inspiring the mountain's name), accidentally curdled soybean milk using gypsum or nigari, creating the first bean curd to nourish his ailing mother. This story, rooted in Han-era texts like the Huainanzia philosophical compendium edited by Liu Anforms the historical basis for the annual Bagongshan Tofu Festival in Huainan, which celebrates the region's culinary heritage through traditional tofu-making demonstrations and reenactments dating back to local folklore preserved in Anhui provincial records. While archaeological evidence for tofu's origins remains elusive, the legend underscores Huainan's early innovations in soybean processing, influencing Chinese cuisine for over two millennia.
In 383 AD, the Battle of the Feishui River near modern Huainan marked a pivotal clash between the Eastern Jin dynasty and the invading Former Qin empire, where Jin general Xie An's forces of about 80,000 decisively routed Fu Jian's numerically superior army of over 800,000 through clever psychological tactics. Fu Jian, overconfident after unifying northern China, advanced along the Feishui (modern Fei River) but hesitated due to supply issues and terrain; Xie Xuan exploited this by requesting safe passage for a feigned retreat, then launched a surprise counterattack amid Qin's disorganized ranks, exacerbated by a rumor of Jin reinforcements that triggered mass panic and desertion. The victory, achieved with minimal casualties on the Jin side, halted Qin's southward expansion, preserved Eastern Jin's control over the Huai River valley, and accelerated the Former Qin's collapse into the Sixteen Kingdoms period, entrenching north-south division in China for centuries.

Modern development

Prior to the 20th century, the area now known as Huainan was a minor town centered around Tianjia'an, serving primarily agricultural purposes under the administration of Shouxian. The discovery of significant coal deposits in the early 20th century transformed the region, sparking rapid urbanization as mining operations began in earnest during the 1930s under Japanese occupation. This industrial shift laid the foundation for Huainan's emergence as a key energy hub, with coal extraction driving population growth and infrastructure development.
Following the establishment of the People's Republic of China in 1949, Huainan was designated a prefecture-level city, marking a pivotal post-liberation reorganization that emphasized state-led industrialization. During the planned economy era, the government prioritized coal mining expansion, discovering additional deposits and mechanizing deep pits to support national energy needs. By the 1970s, Huainan had become the center of one of China's most important coalfields, fueling thermal power generation and related industries. Coal production reached approximately 40 million tons in 2006, underscoring the city's critical role in the national economy.
The intense mining activity, however, led to severe environmental challenges, including widespread land subsidence, water pollution from mine discharges, and methane emissions, affecting urban livability and ecosystems. In response, Huainan launched eco-city initiatives starting in 2013, focusing on sustainable restoration of subsidence areas, resource recycling, and green urban planning to mitigate these impacts. A key collaboration came that year when the Natural Resources Defense Council (NRDC) signed a Letter of Intent with the Huainan municipal government to support eco-city development, including investments of 10 billion RMB by 2020 for ecological restoration projects. These efforts continued into the 2020s, with international support such as the World Bank's Huainan Mining Area Rehabilitation Project (approved 2018), which funded subsidence area remediation, wetland restoration, and infrastructure improvements covering over 100 km as of 2023.
Amid these transformations, cultural events highlighted Huainan's heritage to promote modern tourism and local identity. The 17th China Tofu Cultural Festival, held from September 1517, 2010, exemplified this by linking the city's ancient tofu legendwhere Liu An is said to have invented tofu during the Han Dynastyto contemporary promotion through exhibitions and bean product showcases. This annual event, ongoing for nearly two decades by the early 2010s, bolstered eco-tourism as part of broader sustainable development efforts.

Geography

Location and terrain

Huainan is a prefecture-level city situated in north-central Anhui Province, People's Republic of China, at approximately 3237 N latitude and 11700 E longitude, positioned on the alluvial plain south of the Huai River. The city covers a total land area of 5,533 square kilometers, of which the urban area spans about 1,500 square kilometers. This positioning places Huainan within the broader Huai River Basin, contributing to its role in regional water systems.
The terrain of Huainan predominantly consists of flat alluvial plains formed by Huai River sediments, with average elevations ranging from 15 to 50 meters above sea level, gradually sloping southeastward. These low-lying plains are bordered to the south by low mountains, including the Bagongshan range, which rises to higher elevations and provides a natural demarcation from surrounding hilly areas. The Huai River influences the local hydrology, serving as the primary drainage feature and shaping the plain's fertile, sediment-rich soils.
Prominent natural features include Gaotang Lake, located to the east of the urban center, which forms part of the region's wetland ecosystem and supports local biodiversity. Further south, the Bagongshan District encompasses the Bagongshan National Geopark, renowned for its karst landscapes featuring, peaks, and forested hills that highlight the area's geological diversity. These karst formations contribute to Huainan's ecological profile, including habitats near paleontological sites that underscore its geological heritage. The flat plains have historically aided accessibility for resource extraction, such as coal mining.

Climate

Huainan experiences a humid subtropical climate (Kppen Cfa), characterized by distinct seasonal variations and significant monsoon influences. According to long-term records from 1991 to 2020, the city has an average annual temperature of approximately 16C and annual precipitation totaling about 1,009 mm, with the majority falling during the summer months.
Summers are hot and humid, peaking in July with an average temperature of 28C, while winters are cold and relatively dry, with January averaging around 2C. Extreme temperatures recorded since 1981 include highs exceeding 40C during summer heatwaves and lows dropping below -10C in winter cold snaps. The Huai River, flowing through the region, contributes to elevated humidity levels and frequent fog, particularly in winter, exacerbating composite pollution events. Annual sunshine hours average approximately 2,200, providing ample solar exposure despite cloudy periods in the rainy season.
In recent decades, climate change has led to increasing variability in temperature and precipitation patterns, with more intense rain events heightening flood risks along the Huai River basin. Projections indicate that extreme precipitation and associated flooding may become more frequent and severe in the coming years.

Government and administration

Administrative divisions

Huainan, a prefecture-level city in northern Anhui Province, is administratively divided into eight county-level units: five districtsTianjia'an District, Panji District, Xiejiaji District, Bagongshan District, and Datong Districtand three other units consisting of two counties, Fengtai County and Shouxian County, as well as the Maoji Social and Economic Development Experimental Zone. The municipal seat is located in Tianjia'an District, which serves as the political, economic, and cultural center of the city.
These county-level divisions are further subdivided into 90 township-level administrative units, comprising 19 subdistricts, 59 towns, and 12 townships. This structure supports local governance, with each unit managing public services, land use, and community development tailored to urban or rural characteristics.
Among the counties, Shouxian (also known as Shou County) holds significant historical importance as an ancient county dating back to the Warring States Period, featuring well-preserved city walls from the Song Dynasty that enclose the old town and reflect its role as a former capital of the Chu Kingdom. In contrast, Fengtai County has undergone substantial urbanization, with much of its area integrated into the broader Huainan urban agglomeration, facilitating industrial and residential expansion.
As of 2025, the city's leadership includes Ren Zefeng as the Communist Party of China (CPC) Secretary of Huainan and Zhang Zhiqiang as the Mayor, overseeing the coordination of these divisions to promote regional development.

Special economic zones

Huainan has established several special economic zones to diversify its economy beyond traditional resource extraction, with a primary focus on high-technology and sustainable development. The city's industrial development follows a "2+4" park platform, consisting of one national development zone, one national high-tech zone, and four provincial-level development areas.
The Huainan High-Tech Industrial Development Zone, founded in May 2010 and elevated to national status in February 2018, serves as the flagship initiative to foster innovation in sectors such as big data, new energy, biomedicine, and advanced equipment manufacturing. This zone, covering 5.64 square kilometers across two regions and with a built-up area of 13 square kilometers as of 2022, aims to drive the city's transformation from a coal-dependent economy to a modern industrial hub; as of 2019, it hosted approximately 1,300 enterprises and supported over 100,000 residents, with the population exceeding 200,000 by 2022.
Complementing this are provincial-level zones, including the Bagongshan Economic Development Zone in the Bagongshan District and the Huainan Economic and Technological Development Zone, which integrate with national strategies such as the Yangtze River Economic Belt. The other provincial areas include the Panji Economic Development Zone, Datong Industrial Park, and Shouxian Economic Development Zone. These zones position Huainan within the Yangtze River Economic Belt's framework, leveraging its location in the hinterland of the Yangtze River Delta to enhance regional connectivity and sustainable growth. Policies in these areas emphasize incentives for high-tech firms, including tax preferences, research and development subsidies, and streamlined administrative services, while promoting eco-friendly practices as part of Huainan's eco-city remodeling efforts launched in 2013.
Key achievements include the zones' role in cultural-economic initiatives, such as hosting the annual China Tofu Cultural Festival, which celebrates Huainan's historical ties to tofu invention and attracts tourism to stimulate local commerce and innovation in food processing industries. The High-Tech Zone, for instance, ranked 19th among 162 provincial-level development zones in 2017, underscoring its contributions to economic diversification and environmental sustainability.

Demographics

Population trends

Huainan's population has exhibited steady growth since its establishment as a prefecture-level city in 1949, reflecting broader industrialization patterns in north-central Anhui province. The first national census in 1953 recorded a total population of 286,941 inhabitants. By the 2000 census, this had expanded significantly to 2,040,649 residents, marking an increase driven by post-liberation development. The 2010 census further reported 2,334,000 inhabitants, while the 2020 census tallied 3,033,528 total inhabitants, demonstrating a decadal growth rate of approximately 30% over the previous decade.

Ethnic composition

Huainan is predominantly composed of the Han Chinese ethnic group, which accounts for over 99% of the population, aligning with the demographic profile of Anhui Province as a whole. The 2020 national census recorded Huainan's total population at 3,033,528, with ethnic minorities forming a negligible proportion citywide. Small communities of other ethnic groups, such as the Hui, exist primarily in rural areas; for instance, in Lichong Hui Ethnic Township within Fengtai County, ethnic minorities constitute about 40% of the local population, predominantly Hui individuals.
The primary language spoken in Huainan is Standard Mandarin, supplemented by the local Jianghuai Mandarin dialect, which belongs to the Lower Yangtze Mandarin group and features distinct phonetic influences typical of northern Anhui. No significant minority languages are present, reflecting the Han-dominated demographic structure.
Socially, Huainan exhibits a clear urban-rural divide, with urban districts hosting a more diverse mix of residents due to historical influxes of migrant workers during the coal mining expansion in the mid-20th century; these workers, largely Han Chinese from surrounding provinces, have integrated into the majority population. The city's identity is symbolized by the Chinese rose (Rosa chinensis) as its official flower, representing harmony and cultural cohesion among inhabitants.

Economy

Resource-based industries

Huainan's economy has historically been dominated by coal mining, which serves as the cornerstone of its resource-based industries. The Huainan Mining Group, a state-owned enterprise, operates numerous underground mines across the city's coalfields, leveraging vast reserves estimated at over 44 billion tons. Annual coal production reached 71.2 million tons by 2013 and 79.84 million tons in 2018, reflecting the scale of operations in this key mining hub. The group contributes significantly to China's national coal supply, which totaled 4.66 billion tons in 2023, supporting energy demands in eastern provinces.
Closely tied to coal extraction, thermal power generation forms another pillar of Huainan's industrial base, with the city hosting multiple large-scale coal-fired plants. Facilities such as the Huainan Fengtai power station, with a capacity of 2,520 MW, and the Datang Huainan Luohe Power Plant, at 2,500 MW, underscore the region's role in electricity production. Huainan is regarded as the cradle of China's power industry, owing to its pioneering efforts in coal-based electricity generation dating back to the mid-20th century.
Before the rise of heavy industry in the 20th century, the Huai River profoundly shaped Huainan's early resource economy by fostering agriculture and fisheries in its fertile basin. The river valley supported mixed farming systems, including rice and millet cultivation, as well as traditional fish farming practices that sustained local communities for millennia.
Intensive mining has, however, brought substantial environmental challenges, including widespread land subsidenceexacerbated by groundwater extraction and underground collapsesand pollution of surface waters from coal processing runoff. These issues have led to ecosystem degradation and displacement in mining-affected areas, prompting ongoing remediation efforts.

Emerging sectors

Huainan has pursued economic diversification beyond its traditional resource-based industries, with the gross domestic product reaching 160.16 billion yuan in 2023 and increasing to 171.6 billion yuan in 2024, reflecting a growth rate of approximately 7.1 percent. Per capita GDP stood at 53,005 yuan in 2023, underscoring efforts to expand non-coal sectors amid broader provincial development goals. This expansion aligns with Anhui Province's overall GDP growth of 5.8 percent in 2024, emphasizing sustainable transitions in resource-dependent regions like Huainan.
The Huainan High-Tech Industrial Development Zone, established in 2010 and elevated to national status in 2018, drives advancements in electronics and new energy sectors. Key initiatives include State Grid-led new energy storage projects launched in 2024, recognized as national demonstration efforts, alongside manufacturing of lithium-ion and sodium-ion batteries by local firms such as Huainan Pengchengtianxia Electronic Technology Co., Ltd. In 2025, Huainan operationalized the world's largest floating solar farm on a flooded coal mine site, generating 40 MW of clean energy and exemplifying the city's renewable transition. The zone focuses on pillar industries like big data, biomedicine, and advanced equipment, fostering innovation to reduce reliance on extractive activities.
The cultural economy has gained prominence through the promotion of Huainan's tofu industry, recognized as the birthplace of Chinese tofu near Bagongshan. Annual events like the Chinese Bean-Curd Cultural Festival, held for over two decades, highlight traditional techniques and attract visitors, contributing to the service sector's expansion. Tourism tied to cultural landmarks and tofu-themed attractions has bolstered local services, with innovative dishes and festivals driving economic vitality in 2024 and 2025.
Sustainability initiatives, including eco-city projects initiated over a decade ago, aim to mitigate environmental impacts from historical coal dependency while targeting 5-6 percent annual growth in line with provincial objectives. These efforts feature tailored indicator systems for resource conservation and urban greening, supporting the shift toward low-carbon development in Huainan's mining areas.

Transportation

Rail infrastructure

Huainan's rail infrastructure primarily revolves around the FuyangHuainan railway, a key line connecting the city to Fuyang in northern Anhui Province and facilitating both freight and passenger transport. This railway underwent significant electrification and capacity upgrades between 2010 and 2011, involving the supply of over 188,000 sets of rail fasteners to enhance efficiency and reliability under the oversight of the Shanghai Railway Bureau.
The city's network integrates into China's national high-speed rail system through the HefeiBengbu high-speed railway, which serves Huainan East Railway Station in Datong District and forms part of the broader BeijingFuzhou corridor, providing proximity and connectivity to the BeijingShanghai high-speed railway via interlinked routes. This line enables high-speed services to Hefei, with travel times reduced to under an hour, and onward links to Nanjing through the integrated network. Huainan South Railway Station in Tianjia'an District lies on the ShangqiuHangzhou high-speed railway, supporting express services to major eastern cities including Hangzhou and further enhancing regional accessibility.
Historically, Huainan's railways were developed in the mid-20th century to support the region's burgeoning coal industry, with branch lines constructed during the First Five-Year Plan (19531957) to link mines directly to main stations and transport output to industrial centers like Shanghai, aided by Soviet technical assistance for modernization. Originally focused on coal freight, which became a priority through the 1950s following post-1949 development, the infrastructure has seen recent upgrades prioritizing passenger traffic amid declining coal reliance. The Huainan Railway Station in Tianjia'an District handles conventional services on the Huainan railway extending to Wuhu on the Yangtze River, complementing high-speed options. Daily high-speed and conventional services across these stations support the mobility needs of Huainan's metropolitan population, connecting commuters and travelers to provincial and national hubs.

Road and water networks

Huainan's road network includes key provincial highways such as S225, which runs through the city and connects to surrounding areas, supporting both local and regional traffic flow. The highway features a bridge over the Huai River east of the city center, specifically the Kongli Huaihe River Bridge in Panji District, which links the south and north banks and enhances cross-river accessibility for vehicles. Additionally, the Huainan section of the Huainan-Tongcheng Expressway is under construction as of 2025, aiming to improve inter-city connectivity within Anhui Province.
Air travel to Huainan is facilitated through nearby airports, including Hefei Xinqiao International Airport (HFE), approximately 146 km southwest, connected by high-speed rail and expressways.
The city's water networks leverage the Huai River for inland navigation, primarily transporting bulk goods like coal and industrial materials to other inland regions and coastal ports, contributing to Huainan's resource-based economy. Ferry services operate near the city center to connect the rural north bank with urban areas on the south bank, providing essential links for passengers and light cargo where bridges are limited.
Recent urban transport updates in Huainan focus on intelligent transportation systems (ITS) to manage congestion, including smart traffic light systems that use real-time data for signal optimization at key intersections. These systems integrate traffic monitoring, emission control, and adaptive signal controls, reducing average travel times and supporting sustainable mobility as part of the city's ongoing ITS construction. The ITS also encompasses citizen travel services, such as integrated platforms for route planning and safety management.
Local bus networks in Huainan provide daily urban and suburban connectivity, with routes managed under the ITS framework for improved efficiency. Cycling infrastructure supports short-distance travel through designated lanes in urban areas, promoting non-motorized options amid broader Chinese trends in slow-traffic systems. Real-time apps, similar to those in nearby cities like Ji'an, influence Huainan's public transport by enabling live bus tracking and route optimization via platforms like Baidu Maps and Alipay.

Education

Higher education institutions

Huainan hosts several prominent higher education institutions that contribute significantly to regional development in engineering, education, and vocational training. The primary universities include Anhui University of Science and Technology (AUST), Huainan Normal University, and Huainan Union University, collectively enrolling over 76,000 students as of 2024 and offering diverse programs aligned with local industries such as mining and energy.
Anhui University of Science and Technology, established in 1945 as one of the earliest engineering colleges in Anhui Province, specializes in mining engineering and related fields, making it a key institution for the region's coal-based economy. It offers more than 60 undergraduate and graduate programs across engineering, science, management, and humanities, with a strong emphasis on safety science, civil engineering, and resource utilization; the university has produced over 100,000 graduates who support global economic and social development. As of 2024, AUST enrolls over 33,000 full-time undergraduates and more than 6,000 postgraduates, including international students, and holds provincial key disciplines in mining engineering and safety engineering.
Huainan Normal University, originally founded in 1958 as Huainan Normal College and re-established in 1978 after a suspension, focuses on undergraduate education in teacher training, sciences, and liberal arts, preparing professionals for educational and regional needs. The university comprises 15 schools and departments, delivering 55 undergraduate programs in areas such as mathematics, Chinese language and literature, physics, and education; it also maintains two provincial-level key disciplines in education and physics. With an enrollment of over 20,000 full-time students as of 2025, the institution emphasizes comprehensive teacher development and has integrated former colleges like Huainan Education College since 1999 to broaden its scope.
Huainan Union University, established in September 1984 as a comprehensive higher vocational college under the sponsorship of the CPC Huainan Municipal Committee and local government, prioritizes practical skills training in energy, mechanics, and applied technologies to meet industrial demands. It provides 43 vocational majors across 12 categories, including mechanical manufacturing, electrical engineering, and energy-related fields, alongside 10 undergraduate programs in open education; these programs equip students for roles in Huainan's resource industries. The university serves over 23,000 students as of 2025, with more than 13,000 in higher vocational programs, fostering contributions to local economic sectors through hands-on education and adult continuing programs.

Secondary and vocational schools

Huainans secondary education system is governed by the Anhui Provincial Department of Education, which provides oversight for curriculum standards, teacher training, and resource allocation across the prefecture's high schools and vocational institutions. Local education bureaus in districts like Tianjia'an, Fengtai, and Shouxian implement these policies, emphasizing preparation for technical careers in mining and emerging technologies, reflecting the city's coal-dependent economy. Enrollment in senior high schools has risen with urbanization, reaching over 90% gross enrollment rate province-wide by 2020, driven by expanded access in urbanizing areas like Huainan.
Key high schools include Huainan No.1 High School, established in 1946 and designated an Anhui Provincial Demonstration High School in 2001, known for its strong performance in national competitions and honors such as National Defense Education Demonstration School. Huainan No.2 High School, another prominent institution, focuses on comprehensive academic preparation and is certified for international teaching programs. Huainan No.4 and No.5 High Schools serve as vital local centers, contributing to the city's educational landscape with emphasis on STEM subjects aligned with regional industries. In outlying districts, Fengtai No.1 High School and Shouxian No.1 High School stand out as top performers, often ranking highly in provincial assessments for academic excellence.
Vocational education is anchored by Huainan Vocational Technical College, originally founded in April 1951 as part of the Huainan Coal Industry College and evolving through stages including the Huainan Mining Bureau Cadre School before its current form in 2000. The college features specialized departments in energy engineering and mechanical and electrical engineering, alongside training in coal mine safety, supporting over 90 off-campus bases in major mining groups across Anhui. It operates as a provincial continuing education base and coal mine safety training center, preparing students for roles in mining operations and technical maintenance.
These institutions achieve notable success in the gaokao, with top high schools like Huainan No.1 recording high admission rates to STEM programs at provincial universities, including pathways to Anhui University of Science and Technology. Vocational programs align with this by offering certifications that facilitate direct entry into tech and energy sectors, bolstering Huainan's workforce development.

Culture and landmarks

Cultural traditions

Huainan's cultural traditions are deeply rooted in its historical ties to the ancient Chu state and its agricultural heritage, particularly the production and celebration of tofu. The city is widely recognized as the birthplace of tofu, attributed to the legendary Prince Liu An of the Han Dynasty, who experimented with soybean alchemy on Mount Bagong. This legacy is vividly preserved through the annual China Tofu Cultural Festival, established in 2010 and held every September in Shouxian County, which draws thousands of visitors for tofu-making demonstrations, culinary competitions, and massive tofu sculptures weighing up to 19 tons. The event not only showcases over 100 varieties of tofu dishes but also promotes traditional techniques passed down through generations, blending folklore with gastronomic innovation.
Local customs in Huainan reflect the broader Hui cuisine tradition of Anhui Province, emphasizing fresh, seasonal ingredients and subtle flavors achieved through braising, stewing, and steaming methods. Tofu features prominently in dishes like Huainan beef soup and fermented tofu banquets, often incorporating wild herbs and river fish to evoke the region's mountainous and Huai River landscapes. Community gatherings frequently center on these meals, fostering social bonds during harvest seasons, while tofu feasts symbolize prosperity and communal harmony in rural festivals.
Folklore from the Chu state era, when Huainan lay within its cultural sphere, enriches local storytelling traditions, as compiled in the seminal Huainanzi text attributed to Liu An's court. These narratives include mythological tales of immortals, nature spirits, and moral parables drawn from Chu cosmology, often recited during family gatherings or village performances to impart lessons on harmony with the environment. Stories of ancient battles, such as the Eastern Jin's improbable victory over a larger invading force at the Fei River in 383 CE near present-day Huainan, inspire motifs of resilience and underdog triumphs in oral histories and local theater.
In modern expressions, Huainan's eco-city initiatives integrate these traditions into programs promoting sustainable living, such as community workshops on eco-friendly tofu production and green planting drives that echo ancient agricultural rites. These efforts, part of the city's broader environmental strategy since the early 2000s, encourage residents to adopt low-carbon lifestyles through cultural events that highlight tofu's plant-based nutrition and biodiversity conservation in the Huai River basin.

Historical sites

Huainan boasts several significant historical sites that reflect its rich geological, architectural, and cultural heritage, preserved through national efforts and drawing substantial tourist interest.
The Bagongshan National Geopark, located in the Bagongshan District, features striking plateau karst landforms, including caves, canyons, and unique rock formations shaped over millions of years. It is renowned as the legendary birthplace of tofu, tied to the Han Dynasty Prince Liu An, who is said to have invented the foodstuff through alchemical experiments on the mountain. The park also preserves the Huainan biota, including Tonian-period discoidal and worm-like fossils dating to approximately 950720 million years ago, offering insights into early multicellular life. As a designated national geopark since the early 2000s,
The Shouxian Ancient City Wall in Shouxian County stands as the most complete surviving Song Dynasty (9601279) city wall in Anhui Province, with its brick-and-stone structure encircling the historic core of ancient Shouchun. The site incorporates remnants from the Warring States Period (475221 BCE), when Shouchun served as the capital of the Chu state after its relocation in 241 BCE, underscoring its strategic importance. Positioned near the Feishui River, the wall overlooks the location of the ancient Battle of Feishui, enhancing its historical resonance as a preserved defensive landmark.
Other notable sites include the Piano House, a contemporary architectural landmark completed in 2007 in the Shannan District, designed as a giant grand piano with a violin leaning against it to symbolize musical harmony and urban development. The Shouzhou Kiln site in Datong District represents one of China's five famed Tang Dynasty (618907) kilns, celebrated for its celadon porcelain production using local clay resources, with excavated remains highlighting ancient ceramic techniques. The Chunshenjun Cemetery in Shouxian County houses the tomb of Huang Xie, Lord Chunshena prominent Warring States Chu minister and one of the Four Lords of the realmpreserved as a key archaeological relic of pre-Qin elite burial practices. Wolongshan Scenic Area in Xiejiaji District encompasses mountainous terrain with ancient temples and trails, serving as a preserved natural and cultural retreat. Similarly, Shangyao Forest Park in the eastern suburbs integrates dense woodlands with historical elements, including the Fourth Red Army Cultural Museum, dedicated to revolutionary history and ecological preservation.
These sites benefit from ongoing preservation efforts, such as national heritage designations and restoration projects, which support their role in tourism while occasionally hosting cultural festivals to highlight local traditions.
