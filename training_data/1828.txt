1828
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 1828 was a year of pivotal geopolitical shifts, electoral realignments in the United States, and breakthroughs in chemistry that undermined prevailing scientific doctrines. The Treaty of Turkmenchay, signed on February 22 between the Russian Empire and Qajar Persia, ended the Russo-Persian War (18261828) and compelled Persia to cede the khanates of Erivan, Nakhchivan, and Talysh, along with significant indemnities, facilitating Russian dominance in the South Caucasus. In America, Andrew Jackson's victory over incumbent John Quincy Adams in the presidential election represented a triumph of populist mobilization over elite establishment, establishing the Democratic Party as a dominant force and initiating policies like the Tariff of Abominations, which protected Northern industry but exacerbated sectional tensions. The outbreak of the Russo-Turkish War in May, precipitated by Ottoman suppression of the Greek independence movement, saw Russian forces capture key fortresses such as Akhaltsikhe and advance toward the Balkans, reshaping Ottoman territorial control. German chemist Friedrich Whler synthesized urea from ammonium cyanate, demonstrating that organic compounds could be produced from inorganic precursors and eroding the vitalist paradigm that posited a life force unique to living organisms. Culturally, the year witnessed the births of pioneering novelist Jules Verne on February 8 and philosopher-author Leo Tolstoy on September 9, alongside the deaths of Romantic composer Franz Schubert on November 19 and painter Francisco Goya on April 16.

Events

JanuaryMarch

On January 4, Jean-Baptiste Gay, vicomte de Martignac, was appointed Prime Minister of France, succeeding Joseph de Villle amid efforts to moderate the ultra-royalist policies of the Bourbon Restoration.
On February 21, the first issue of the Cherokee Phoenix was published in New Echota, Georgia, marking the debut of the initial newspaper printed in a Native American language using the Cherokee syllabary developed by Sequoyah.
On February 22, the Treaty of Turkmenchay was signed between the Russian Empire and Qajar Persia, concluding the Russo-Persian War of 18261828; Persia ceded the khanates of Erivan and Nakhchivan, along with other territories south of the Aras River, to Russia and agreed to pay a 10 million ruble indemnity, while granting Russia exclusive navigation rights on the Caspian Sea and protections for Russian subjects in Persia.
In March, German chemist Friedrich Whler achieved the synthesis of urea from ammonium cyanate, an inorganic precursor, providing empirical evidence against the vitalist doctrine that organic compounds could only be produced by living organisms.
On March 24, the Pennsylvania General Assembly incorporated the Philadelphia and Columbia Railroad, authorizing construction of an 82-mile line from Philadelphia to Columbia as part of the state's Main Line of Public Works to compete with the Erie Canal.

AprilJune

On May 19, United States President John Quincy Adams signed the Tariff of 1828 into law, imposing protective duties averaging nearly 50% on imported manufactured goods such as woolens, iron, and hemp to shield emerging Northern industries from British competition. The legislation raised duties on raw materials like wool and hemp to 50% while applying even higher ratesup to 61%on finished products, aiming to foster domestic manufacturing amid post-War of 1812 economic recovery.
The Russo-Turkish War commenced on April 26 when Tsar Nicholas I declared war on the Ottoman Empire, motivated in part by Ottoman suppression of the Greek War of Independence and violations of the Akkerman Convention. Russian forces under Field Marshal Peter Wittgenstein rapidly occupied the Danubian Principalities of Wallachia and Moldavia in April and May, securing these territories with minimal resistance due to local Phanariote support. By late June, Russian troops crossed the Danube River into Bulgaria, establishing bridgeheads at key points like Silistra and advancing toward Varna, though logistical challenges from Danube flooding slowed broader offensives.
In the Caucasus theater, Russian General Ivan Paskevich launched operations from Erivan, capturing Ottoman outposts and initiating sieges that pressured Turkish supply lines, complementing the Balkan front's momentum. These early advances exploited Ottoman disarray, with Russian armies numbering over 100,000 troops facing fragmented Ottoman defenses, setting the stage for deeper penetrations later in the year.

JulySeptember

In July 1828, Russian forces operating in the Caucasus during the Russo-Turkish War captured the Ottoman fortress of Kars, advancing their position against Turkish defenses in the region. Concurrently, Russian troops under Prince Menshikov initiated the siege of Varna on the Black Sea coast, a key Ottoman stronghold that would fall after prolonged bombardment and assaults. These operations followed the main Russian army's crossing of the Danube River earlier in the summer, enabling deeper incursions into Ottoman Bulgaria and escalating the conflict's scope.
By August, Russian detachments in the southern theater captured Akhaltsikhe in Georgia after a multi-week siege, inflicting heavy casualties on Ottoman forces and securing control over strategic passes in the region. This victory, part of broader Caucasian campaigns, disrupted Ottoman supply lines and bolstered Russian momentum amid logistical challenges from disease and terrain.
In the United States, the Baltimore and Ohio Railroad broke ground on July 4, 1828, in Baltimore, Maryland, with Charles Carrollthe last surviving signer of the Declaration of Independencelaying the first stone in a ceremony attended by civic leaders. This event initiated the construction of the nation's first common-carrier railroad, intended to link Baltimore to the Ohio River via strap-iron rails on granite ties, addressing competitive pressures from emerging canals and fostering early industrial expansion. Progress included initial grading and track-laying experiments, though full operations would require years of engineering innovations.

OctoberDecember

In October and November, Russian forces in the Russo-Turkish War maintained offensives in the Balkans, consolidating gains after earlier crossings of the Danube and pressuring Ottoman defenses near Silistra and in Wallachia, while Caucasian armies under Ivan Paskevich secured positions following the capture of Anapa. The Imperial Russian Navy, under Admiral Login Geiden, continued blockades and operations in the Black Sea to support land advances amid harsh weather conditions.
Voting in the United States presidential election proceeded variably by state from October 31 to December 2, with most occurring in early November, as electors were chosen through legislative or popular methods depending on state constitutions. On November 19, Austrian composer Franz Schubert died in Vienna at age 31, likely from typhus following prolonged illness, leaving a legacy of over 1,000 works including symphonies and lieder composed in his brief career.
On December 3, Andrew Jackson's victory in the presidential election was confirmed by electoral vote tallies, securing 178 electors from 15 states compared to incumbent John Quincy Adams's 83 from 7 states, primarily in New England; Jackson also garnered 647,231 popular votes (55.97%) against Adams's 508,308 (43.95%), with minor votes for others. Jackson prevailed in states including Pennsylvania (28,000+ votes), Virginia, and Tennessee, reflecting strong support in the South and West, while Adams carried Massachusetts, Connecticut, and Rhode Island. Modern electoral maps highlight this as the election marking the birth of the Democratic Party.
On December 22, Rachel Jackson, wife of president-elect Andrew Jackson, died suddenly at their Hermitage plantation near Nashville, Tennessee, at age 61 from a heart attack manifesting as severe pain in the left shoulder and arm. Her death occurred weeks before the inauguration, prompting Jackson to mourn deeply during the transition period.

Date unknown

German chemist Friedrich Whler synthesized urea, an organic compound, from the inorganic ammonium cyanate by heating a solution of the latter, yielding crystals identical to those obtained from natural sources. This achievement refuted vitalism's core tenet that organic molecules required a vital force from living organisms for their formation, establishing that such substances could arise through chemical processes alone. Whler's work, detailed in correspondence with Jns Jacob Berzelius, shifted organic chemistry toward mechanistic explanations grounded in empirical synthesis.
Hungarian physicist and inventor nyos Jedlik developed an electromagnetic self-rotor, an early prototype of the electric motor, featuring a commutator and electromagnets that enabled sustained rotation powered by a battery. Unlike prior devices reliant on permanent magnets, Jedlik's innovation used only electromagnets, demonstrating principles of continuous current reversal essential to modern motors. This apparatus, constructed during experiments spanning late 1827 into 1828, advanced electromagnetic technology and foreshadowed applications in electrical machinery.

Controversies and Debates

United States Presidential Election Campaign

The 1828 United States presidential election campaign arose from unresolved grievances over the 1824 contest, in which Andrew Jackson obtained the largest share of popular votes (about 42 percent) and electoral votes (99) but fell short of a majority, prompting the House of Representatives to elect John Quincy Adams with pivotal support from Henry Clay, who received Adams's appointment as Secretary of State shortly thereafter. Jackson's allies immediately decried this as a "corrupt bargain," interpreting Clay's influence in the House votedespite no direct evidence of quid pro quoas a subversion of democratic principles by political insiders, a charge that resonated amid broader distrust of centralized power.
Jackson's strategy emphasized restoring government to the people through anti-corruption reforms and popular sovereignty, appealing to agrarian and frontier interests wary of federal overreach, while Adams defended his administration's infrastructure initiatives and patronage system as bulwarks of national progress. By 1828, suffrage expansions in over a dozen states had dismantled property requirements for white male voters, doubling the eligible electorate in some regions and enabling Jackson's organizers to mobilize previously disenfranchised groups via rallies, hickory pole symbols, and partisan presses. This contrasted with Adams's more restrained approach, reliant on established networks but vulnerable to portrayals of elitism, as turnout escalated to roughly 57 percent of eligible voters amid unprecedented participation.
Both campaigns deployed virulent personal assaults, with Adams supporters issuing "Coffin Handbills" that cataloged Jackson's alleged atrocities, including the summary execution of six militiamen for mutiny during the 1812 war without formal trials, framing him as a bloodthirsty authoritarian unfit for office. Jackson forces retaliated by amplifying corrupt bargain accusations and charging Adams with fiscal improprieties, such as diverting public appropriations for luxury items like a billiard table and chess set for the White House, portraying the incumbent as emblematic of aristocratic excess.
The resultsJackson securing 178 electoral votes and 56 percent of the popular vote (642,553 ballots) against Adams's 83 votes and 44 percent (500,897)reflected decisive Southern and Western majorities, with Jackson carrying nearly every state beyond New England and Kentucky, evidencing a voter mandate against perceived Northeastern elite consolidation as the proximate cause of his victory over institutional inertia.

Tariff of 1828 and Sectional Divisions

The Tariff of 1828, enacted on May 19, 1828, imposed high protective duties on imported manufactured goods and raw materials to shield emerging American industries from British competition following the vulnerabilities exposed by the War of 1812. Average ad valorem rates on dutiable imports rose to approximately 45-50 percent, with specific levies reaching up to 60 percent on items such as woolens, iron, hemp, and flax, significantly elevating costs for these goods compared to the lower rates of prior tariffs like the 1816 and 1824 acts. 
Northern manufacturers and industrial interests, concentrated in states like Pennsylvania and New England, strongly supported the measure as essential for fostering domestic production and achieving economic self-sufficiency, arguing that protectionism countered foreign dumping and subsidized infant industries unable to compete on price alone. Customs revenue from duties increased from about $21 million in fiscal year 1827 to over $33 million by 1830, providing empirical evidence of heightened import barriers while generating federal funds, though at the expense of higher consumer prices in non-industrial regions.
Southern agricultural exporters, reliant on cotton, tobacco, and rice sales to Britain in exchange for manufactured imports, vehemently opposed the tariff, viewing it as an unconstitutional transfer of wealth from their export-driven economy to Northern factories, exacerbating dependencies on slavery-based plantation systems that produced raw commodities rather than finished goods. In December 1828, Vice President John C. Calhoun anonymously authored the South Carolina Exposition and Protest, contending that the tariff violated the Constitution's intent for uniform commerce regulation and asserting states' rights to nullify federal laws deemed oppressive within their borders, a doctrine rooted in compact theory of the Union.
These divisions highlighted causal tensions between protectionist policies promoting industrial diversificationevidenced by gradual Northern manufacturing growth amid reduced foreign relianceand free-market critiques emphasizing retaliatory risks and inefficient resource allocation, with Southern protests framing the tariff not merely as economic policy but as sectional overreach favoring one region's interests over the nation's balanced development. Pre-tariff trade data showed U.S. imports of British manufactures exceeding $20 million annually in the mid-1820s, while post-enactment shifts indicated curtailed volumes but sustained Southern export values initially, underscoring the tariff's role in redirecting economic pressures southward without immediate nullification enforcement.

Births

JanuaryJune

On January 10, Nicolas Louis Franois de Neufchteau, a French statesman, poet, and agronomist who had served as Minister of the Interior (17971799) and as a member of the Directory (1799), died in Paris at the age of 77. His contributions to agricultural science and administrative reforms during the French Revolution underscored the era's emphasis on rational governance and economic improvement.
January 13 marked the death of Theodore Foster, an American lawyer and Federalist politician who represented Rhode Island in the U.S. Senate from 1790 to 1803, at age 75 in Providence. Foster's tenure involved early debates on constitutional matters, reflecting the consolidation of federal authority post-ratification.
DeWitt Clinton, the American political leader pivotal to the planning and completion of the Erie Canalwhich connected the Hudson River to Lake Erie and spurred economic integration of the Midwest with Atlantic marketsdied suddenly on February 11 in Albany, New York, at age 58. As former mayor of New York City and multiple-term governor, his advocacy for internal improvements demonstrated the causal link between infrastructure investment and regional development, though his ambitions also fueled partisan rivalries.
On April 16, Francisco Goya, the Spanish painter and printmaker renowned for series like Los Desastres de la Guerra that empirically documented the Peninsular War's atrocities and critiqued absolutist folly through works such as The Third of May 1808, succumbed to cerebral thrombosis in Bordeaux, France, at age 82. Exiled after Ferdinand VII's restoration, Goya's later "Black Paintings" and lithographs advanced expressive realism, influencing subsequent artists by prioritizing unvarnished observation over idealization and highlighting the human cost of political violence.

JulyDecember

On September 22, Shaka, founder of the Zulu Kingdom in southern Africa, was assassinated by his half-brothers Dingane and Mhlangana at Dukuza, following a period of erratic behavior after the death of his mother Nandi, which had destabilized the empire he had expanded through military reforms including the iklwa spear and bull-horn formation tactics.
Franz Schubert, the Austrian composer known for over 600 lieder, nine symphonies, and chamber works produced during a brief but prolific career, died on November 19 in Vienna at age 31 from typhoid fever, contracted amid a weakened immune system possibly exacerbated by prior syphilis; his final months involved intense composition, including the String Quintet in C, completed just weeks before.
Rachel Jackson, wife of U.S. president-elect Andrew Jackson, died on December 22 at their Hermitage plantation near Nashville, Tennessee, at age 61 from a heart attack manifesting as severe left-shoulder pain and thoracic oppression, occurring as she prepared for the move to Washington amid the stresses of the contentious 1828 election campaign; Jackson later attributed her decline partly to public accusations of adultery leveled against her during his prior marriage dissolution.
