A volcano is an opening in the Earth's crust through which molten rock, gases, and ash escape from beneath the surface. Volcanoes are among the most powerful and awe-inspiring natural phenomena on Earth, capable of reshaping landscapes in hours, altering global climate for years, and extinguishing entire civilizations in a single eruption. They are also among the most creative forces on the planet, building new islands from the ocean floor, enriching soils with minerals that support lush vegetation, and contributing to the atmospheric chemistry that has made Earth habitable for billions of years.

The word volcano comes from Vulcan, the Roman god of fire and the forge. Ancient cultures around the world developed myths to explain volcanic eruptions, attributing them to the anger of gods, the breathing of underground monsters, or the forging of divine weapons. The scientific understanding of volcanoes, which emerged gradually over centuries, reveals a reality that is in many ways more remarkable than any myth: volcanoes are windows into the interior of the Earth, allowing us to observe directly the processes that shape our planet from within.

Volcanoes form at three types of geological settings, each producing a distinctive type of volcanic activity.

Subduction zone volcanoes form where one tectonic plate is being pushed beneath another. As the subducting plate descends into the mantle, it is heated and releases water into the overlying mantle rock. This water lowers the melting point of the rock, causing it to partially melt and produce magma. The magma, being less dense than the surrounding rock, rises through the crust and eventually erupts at the surface. The result is typically a stratovolcano, also called a composite volcano: a steep-sided, cone-shaped mountain built from alternating layers of lava flows, volcanic ash, and pyroclastic debris. Mount Fuji in Japan, Mount Rainier in Washington State, Mount Vesuvius in Italy, and Mount Pinatubo in the Philippines are all stratovolcanoes formed at subduction zones.

Divergent boundary volcanoes form where tectonic plates are moving apart. As the plates separate, hot rock from the mantle rises to fill the gap, partially melting as the pressure decreases. This produces basaltic magma that erupts relatively gently, flowing across the surface in rivers and sheets of lava. The mid-ocean ridges are the most extensive volcanic features on Earth, producing new oceanic crust continuously along a chain of underwater volcanoes stretching over forty thousand miles. Iceland, which sits on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, is one of the few places where a mid-ocean ridge rises above sea level, making it one of the most volcanically active places on Earth.

Hotspot volcanoes form over plumes of unusually hot rock rising from deep within the mantle, independent of plate boundaries. As a tectonic plate moves over a stationary hotspot, a chain of volcanoes is produced, with the oldest volcanoes at one end and the youngest at the other. The Hawaiian Islands are the most famous example: the Big Island of Hawaii, which sits directly over the hotspot, has active volcanoes, while the islands to the northwest are progressively older, smaller, and more eroded because the Pacific Plate has carried them away from the hotspot over millions of years. The Yellowstone supervolcano in Wyoming sits over another hotspot, and the trail of ancient calderas stretching across the Snake River Plain to the southwest traces the movement of the North American Plate over the Yellowstone hotspot over the past sixteen million years.

Volcanic eruptions vary enormously in their intensity, from gentle lava flows that can be safely observed from a short distance to cataclysmic explosions that can be heard thousands of miles away and affect global climate for years.

The most powerful eruptions in recorded history include the eruption of Mount Tambora in Indonesia in 1815, which ejected approximately thirty-six cubic miles of material into the atmosphere and caused the "Year Without a Summer" in 1816, when global temperatures dropped by an average of about one degree Celsius, crop failures devastated Europe and North America, and famine killed tens of thousands. The eruption of Krakatoa in Indonesia in 1883 produced the loudest sound in recorded history, heard nearly three thousand miles away, and generated tsunamis that killed over thirty-six thousand people. The eruption of Mount Pinatubo in the Philippines in 1991, the largest eruption of the twentieth century, injected approximately twenty million tons of sulfur dioxide into the stratosphere, causing global temperatures to drop by approximately half a degree Celsius for the following two years.

The eruption of Mount Vesuvius in 79 AD destroyed the Roman cities of Pompeii and Herculaneum, burying them under meters of volcanic ash and preserving them in extraordinary detail for nearly two thousand years. The excavation of these cities, beginning in the eighteenth century, provided an unparalleled window into everyday life in the Roman Empire. Bread was found in ovens. Meals were found on tables. Graffiti was found on walls. And the casts of human bodies, preserved in the poses they held at the moment of death, are among the most haunting archaeological discoveries ever made.

Lava is magma that has reached the surface. Its behavior depends primarily on its silica content and temperature. Basaltic lava, which has low silica content and high temperature, is fluid and flows easily, producing gentle eruptions and broad, shield-shaped volcanoes like those in Hawaii. Rhyolitic lava, which has high silica content and lower temperature, is thick and viscous, often blocking the volcanic vent and building up pressure that is eventually released in a violent explosion.

Pyroclastic flows are the most lethal volcanic hazard. They are avalanches of superheated gas, ash, and rock fragments that race down the slopes of a volcano at speeds of up to four hundred and fifty miles per hour at temperatures of up to one thousand three hundred degrees Fahrenheit. Nothing in their path survives. The pyroclastic flows from the eruption of Mount Pelee in Martinique in 1902 killed approximately thirty thousand people in the city of Saint-Pierre in less than two minutes, leaving only two survivors in the entire city.

Lahars are volcanic mudflows composed of water and volcanic debris that can travel at speeds of up to forty miles per hour and bury entire valleys under dozens of feet of mud. When the Colombian volcano Nevado del Ruiz erupted in 1985, the heat melted glaciers on the summit, producing lahars that traveled over thirty miles down river valleys and buried the town of Armero, killing approximately twenty-three thousand of its twenty-nine thousand inhabitants.

Supervolcanoes are volcanic systems capable of eruptions thousands of times more powerful than ordinary eruptions. The Yellowstone caldera last erupted approximately six hundred and forty thousand years ago, ejecting approximately two hundred and forty cubic miles of material, enough to cover the entire continental United States in a layer of ash several inches thick. The Toba eruption in Indonesia approximately seventy-four thousand years ago may have reduced the global human population to as few as several thousand individuals, creating a genetic bottleneck that is still visible in human DNA. A future Yellowstone eruption, while not imminent, would be the most catastrophic natural disaster in recorded history.

Despite their destructive power, volcanoes are essential to the habitability of Earth. Volcanic outgassing over billions of years created the atmosphere and the oceans. Volcanic minerals enrich soils, making volcanic regions among the most agriculturally productive on Earth. Volcanic heat drives geothermal energy systems that provide clean electricity to millions of people. And the carbon dioxide released by volcanoes, balanced by the carbon dioxide absorbed by the weathering of volcanic rocks, helps regulate Earth's climate through a natural thermostat that has operated for billions of years.

Approximately eight hundred million people live within sixty miles of an active volcano. Monitoring and predicting volcanic eruptions is a critical scientific and humanitarian endeavor. Modern volcanology uses seismometers to detect the earthquakes caused by magma moving underground, GPS to detect the swelling of a volcano as magma accumulates beneath it, gas sensors to measure changes in volcanic gas emissions, satellite radar to detect ground deformation from space, and thermal cameras to detect changes in surface temperature. These tools cannot predict the exact timing of an eruption, but they can often provide days to weeks of warning, enough time to evacuate populations from the danger zone.

Volcanoes remind us that the Earth is alive. Beneath the thin crust we build our cities on, the planet is a churning, molten, dynamic body that has been reshaping its surface for over four billion years and will continue to do so long after human civilization has run its course. We live on the skin of a living world, and the volcanoes are its breath.
