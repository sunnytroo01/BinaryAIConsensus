 Hanan Daoud Mikhael Ashrawi (born 8 October 1946) is a Palestinian academic, politician, and activist of Christian heritage who gained prominence as the official spokesperson for the Palestinian delegation during the 1991 Madrid Conference and the ensuing bilateral negotiations with Israel leading to the Oslo Accords.
Ashrawi, the daughter of a physician and raised in a middle-class family, pursued studies in English literature, earning bachelor's and master's degrees from the American University of Beirut before completing a PhD in comparative and medieval literature at the University of Virginia in 1977.
Returning to the West Bank, she joined the faculty of Birzeit University as a professor of English and later served as dean of the Faculty of Arts, while also engaging in student activism and women's rights advocacy amid the Israeli occupation.
In 1996, she was appointed Minister of Higher Education and Research in the newly formed Palestinian Authority but resigned within months to protest corruption and mismanagement within the leadership.
Elected as an independent to the Palestinian Legislative Council that same year, Ashrawi later aligned with the centrist Third Way faction and held positions on the PLO Executive Committee until her resignation in 2020, citing a lack of internal democracy and accountability.
Throughout her career, she has founded organizations like the MIFTAH think tank to promote Palestinian-Israeli dialogue and human rights, though her public statements equating Israeli actions with historical atrocities and questioning Jewish ties to the land have sparked accusations of historical revisionism and bias from critics.

Early Life and Family Background

Birth and Upbringing

Hanan Ashrawi was born on October 8, 1946, in Nablus, Mandatory Palestine, to Daoud Mikhail Ashrawi, a physician, and Wadi'a As'ad Mikhail, an ophthalmic nurse. The family originated from Ramallah but resided in Nablus at the time of her birth due to her father's medical practice there.
As the youngest of five daughters in a middle-class Palestinian Christian household, Ashrawi was raised primarily in Ramallah after the family relocated there. Her upbringing occurred in a community with a substantial Christian population, where her father's reputation for providing medical aid without refusal shaped the home environment.
From an early age, Ashrawi was exposed to political influences through her father, an early Palestinian nationalist who participated in independence movements and endured multiple arrests by Jordanian authorities prior to 1967. This familial emphasis on activism and public service informed her formative years amid the socio-political tensions of the West Bank.

Impact of the 1948 Arab-Israeli War

Hanan Ashrawi was born on October 8, 1946, in Nablus to Daoud Mikhail, a physician and early Palestinian nationalist, and Wadi'a As'ad, as the youngest of five daughters in a Christian Palestinian family. Her father's professional duties had placed the family in Nablus at the time of her birth, though their roots traced to Tiberias in northern Palestine. Prior to the war's escalation, the family relocated to Tiberias, where they resided amid rising tensions following the UN Partition Plan of November 1947.
The 1948 Arab-Israeli War, triggered by Israel's declaration of independence on May 14, 1948, and subsequent invasions by Arab states, led to widespread Palestinian displacement known as the Nakba. Ashrawi's family, like an estimated 700,000 other Palestinians, fled Tiberias as fighting intensified in the Galilee region, with Arab forces withdrawing and irregular combat displacing local populations. At approximately 18 months old, Ashrawi experienced this upheaval indirectly through her parents' decisions, as the family first sought temporary refuge in Amman, Jordan, crossing into Transjordan amid the chaos of refugee flows.
By late 1948 or early 1949, following the armistice agreements that left Tiberias under Israeli control, the family resettled in Ramallah, her father's ancestral hometown in the West Bank, then annexed by Jordan. This relocation marked the end of their ties to pre-war Palestine's coastal and northern areas, instilling in the young Ashrawi an environment steeped in narratives of loss and resistance, as her father pursued political activism that later contributed to founding the Palestine Liberation Organization in 1964. The war's outcome thus profoundly shaped her formative years in Ramallah under Jordanian rule, fostering a context of refugee integration challenges and enduring family emphasis on Palestinian identity and self-determination.

Education and Academic Career

University Education

Ashrawi enrolled at the American University of Beirut in 1964 to study English literature. She earned both a Bachelor of Arts and a Master of Arts in English literature from that institution, completing her master's degree around 1969.
Following her time at AUB, Ashrawi moved to the United States in 1969 to pursue doctoral studies at the University of Virginia. She obtained a PhD in medieval and comparative literature from UVA, with her dissertation defense occurring during the 19811982 academic year after an interruption in her studies due to regional political developments. Her academic focus during this period emphasized literary analysis, aligning with her later scholarly work in comparative literature.

Teaching Roles and Scholarly Contributions

Ashrawi joined the faculty of Birzeit University in 1973 following completion of her Ph.D. in medieval English literature from the University of Virginia, serving as a professor until 1995. She founded the university's Department of English and chaired it from its inception until 1978, resuming the chairmanship from 1981 to 1984. In 1986, she was appointed dean of the Faculty of Arts, a role she maintained through 1995 while continuing to teach English literature and comparative literature courses. During her tenure, she also headed the university's Legal Aid Committee, supporting student legal needs amid the Israeli occupation.
Her scholarly contributions centered on Palestinian literature and criticism, reflecting her expertise in English and comparative literary analysis. Ashrawi authored Contemporary Palestinian Literature under Occupation, a work presented at the 1976 Association of Palestinian Writers conference, examining literary responses to occupation. She also published The Modern Palestinian Short Story: An Introduction to Practical Criticism, applying critical methodologies to emerging Palestinian narrative forms. These publications, along with articles on Palestinian cultural and political themes, established her as an early academic voice on literature produced under constraint, emphasizing analytical skills for textual interpretation.

Activism and Organizational Founding

Establishment of Key Institutions

In 1974, Hanan Ashrawi founded the Legal Aid Committee (also known as the Committee for Legal Aid and Human Rights) at Birzeit University to provide legal support and advocacy for students, faculty, and staff targeted by Israeli military actions, including arbitrary arrests, detentions without trial, and repeated university closures imposed as punitive measures against political activism. The committee operated as an independent body within the university, documenting human rights abuses, coordinating defense efforts in military courts, and raising awareness of occupation-related violations, thereby laying groundwork for institutionalized Palestinian responses to systemic legal pressures. Ashrawi served as its head from inception through the mid-1970s and beyond, extending its activities into a broader Human Rights Action Project that addressed extrajudicial practices and university autonomy issues.
Building on this foundation, Ashrawi spearheaded the creation of the Palestinian Independent Commission for Citizens' Rights (PICCR), later evolving into the Independent Commission for Human Rights (ICHR), in early 1994 amid the transitional period following the Oslo Accords. Established as a non-governmental entity to oversee human rights compliance in Palestinian-administered areas, the commission investigated abuses by Israeli forces as well as emerging internal violations under the nascent Palestinian Authority, with Ashrawi appointed as its first commissioner-general until 1995. This initiative filled a vacuum left by PLO structures lacking independent oversight mechanisms, emphasizing accountability through field monitoring, legal interventions, and public reporting on over 1,000 cases in its initial years.
In December 1998, Ashrawi established MIFTAH (the Palestinian Initiative for the Promotion of Global Dialogue and Democracy), a policy research and advocacy organization headquartered in Jerusalem, designed to foster transparent governance, economic analysis, and international engagement on Palestinian issues. With an initial focus on post-Oslo democratic deficits, MIFTAH produced reports on institutional reforms, women's rights, and conflict resolution, drawing funding from international donors while maintaining operational independence; Ashrawi has chaired its board since inception, overseeing expansions into digital advocacy and coalition-building with over 50 partner entities by the early 2000s. These efforts collectively advanced civil society infrastructure, prioritizing empirical monitoring over partisan alignment, though critics have noted selective emphases in reporting aligned with nationalist priorities.

Involvement in the First Intifada

During the First Intifada, which began on December 9, 1987, following a traffic incident in Gaza that escalated into widespread Palestinian protests against Israeli occupation, Hanan Ashrawi emerged as a key figure in the uprising's political coordination. In 1988, she joined the Intifada Political Committee, an advisory body linked to the clandestine Unified National Leadership of the Uprising (UNLU), and served on its Diplomatic Committee through 1993, focusing on outreach and representation efforts. This role positioned her to advocate for Palestinian positions internationally, including breaking from traditional resistance norms by engaging publicly with Israelis, which she argued did not legitimize the occupation but highlighted Palestinian grievances.
Ashrawi's activities emphasized non-violent political mobilization and media engagement over direct confrontation. On April 4, 1988, she participated in a televised debate on ABC's Nightline against Israeli spokesperson Ehud Goln, marking one of her earliest high-profile appearances as an unofficial spokesperson for the Palestinian cause during the uprising; this event propelled her into broader visibility, with her articulate English-language advocacy contrasting the era's predominant focus on local stone-throwing and boycotts. Entrusted by PLO leadership in exile, she coordinated communications and helped amplify the Intifada's demands for self-determination, including through women's committees that organized community support networks amid the unrest. 
Her involvement extended to scholarly analysis of the Intifada's dynamics, as evidenced by her contributions to discussions on its political strategy, emphasizing grassroots organization over armed struggle at a time when the UNLU issued leaflets directing both civil disobediencesuch as strikes and tax resistanceand sporadic violence. Ashrawi's diplomatic efforts laid groundwork for later negotiations, though the Intifada itself resulted in over 1,000 Palestinian deaths and hundreds of Israeli casualties by its 1993 conclusion, according to contemporaneous reports.

Roles in Palestinian Diplomacy and Negotiations

Participation in Madrid and Washington Talks

Hanan Ashrawi served as the official spokesperson for the Palestinian delegation at the Madrid Peace Conference, convened from October 30 to November 1, 1991, under U.S. and Soviet co-sponsorship. As a Jerusalem resident, she was barred from official delegate status by Jordanian restrictions on East Jerusalem participation and instead contributed through the steering committee alongside leaders like Faisal Husseini. Her role involved articulating Palestinian positions publicly, emphasizing demands for an end to Israeli occupation and recognition of self-determination, while navigating the joint Jordanian-Palestinian framework that avoided direct PLO representation.
The Madrid talks produced no binding agreements but established procedural precedents for bilateral negotiations, prompting the relocation of Palestinian-Israeli discussions to Washington, D.C., starting December 4, 1991. Ashrawi retained her spokesperson position in these multilateral follow-ups, briefing media on Palestinian insistence for symmetry in talks, interim self-governance, and halting settlement expansion. She advocated for U.S. involvement in direct bilaterals to address power imbalances, as evidenced by the delegation's December 1992 request for American observers amid stalled progress on core issues like borders and refugees. Despite procedural gains, the Washington rounds yielded limited substantive advances, with Ashrawi critiquing Israeli inflexibility and U.S. mediation biases in subsequent reflections.

Critiques of the Oslo Accords Process

Ashrawi voiced strong reservations about the Oslo Accords from their inception in 1993, arguing to PLO leader Yasser Arafat that the process prioritized administrative functionality over genuine self-determination and would enable Israel to reorganize the occupation without relinquishing control. She warned Arafat specifically that "Israel will promise you an inch and not give you a millimeter," predicting that the accords' ambiguities would allow Israel to maintain dominance while shifting security responsibilities onto Palestinians. Ashrawi objected to the mutual recognition exchange, in which the PLO affirmed Israel's right to exist but received only Israel's acknowledgment of the PLO as a representative body, without any reciprocal commitment to Palestinian statehood.
A core structural critique centered on the accords' interim framework, which deferred final-status issues like borders, settlements, Jerusalem, and refugees to unspecified future talks without enforceable parameters or international monitoring mechanisms to curb Israeli settlement expansion. This setup, Ashrawi contended, imposed an asymmetrical burden by requiring Palestinians to ensure Israeli security in fragmented territories (designated Areas A, B, and C) while Israel retained overarching control, effectively subcontracting occupation management to the nascent Palestinian Authority. In her view, the absence of accountability allowed Israel to systematically expand settlementsdoubling their population from approximately 110,000 in 1993 to over 400,000 by 2000and erode Palestinian territorial contiguity without consequence.
Ashrawi identified the handling of Jerusalem as a particularly egregious omission, noting that the accords postponed its status, leaving an estimated 300,000 Palestinian residents under exclusive Israeli jurisdiction without protections or services, resulting in land confiscations that reduced Palestinian holdings to 12-13% of the area and the revocation of thousands of residency IDs by 2015. She contrasted this with the earlier Madrid-Washington talks, where her delegation had insisted on addressing Jerusalem, human rights, and statehood upfront rather than deferring them.
In a 2013 analysis, Ashrawi attributed the accords' overall failure to five built-in flaws: secretive bilateral negotiations that excluded public scrutiny and international involvement; negotiation without legal advisors, leading to unbalanced terms; an emphasis on peripheral administrative reforms over core territorial sovereignty; the division of Palestinian communities into isolated enclaves, creating internal refugee-like conditions; and protracted timelines without binding deadlines, permitting "temporary" Israeli measuressuch as resource appropriation and settlement growthto solidify into permanent facts on the ground. She described the process as "fatally flawed" for lacking enforcement tools and relying on goodwill from an Israeli leadership intent on preserving occupation under the guise of autonomy, ultimately dismantling prospects for Palestinian sovereignty.

Political Positions within Palestinian Institutions

Membership in the Palestinian Legislative Council

Ashrawi was elected to the Palestinian Legislative Council in the January 20, 1996, Palestinian general elections, securing one of the seats allocated to the Jerusalem district as an independent candidate. The election, the first for the newly established 88-seat body under the Oslo Accords framework, resulted in Fatah-affiliated candidates winning a majority of 54 seats, with independents and smaller factions filling the remainder. As a member, she chaired the PLC's Political Committee, which oversaw matters related to foreign affairs, negotiations, and institutional oversight. 
In the January 25, 2006, legislative elections for the expanded 132-seat PLC, Ashrawi ran on the Third Way ticketa centrist list formed by her and former Finance Minister Salam Fayyad as an alternative to Fatah and Hamasand was re-elected to represent Jerusalem. The Third Way secured two seats amid Hamas's overall victory of 74 seats, which led to a political crisis, government collapse, and eventual Fatah-Hamas territorial split in 2007. Post-2006, the PLC ceased effective functioning due to the schism, with pro-PA members like Ashrawi participating in limited Ramallah-based sessions lacking full quorum, while Hamas controlled Gaza's parallel institutions.
Throughout her tenure, Ashrawi focused on legislative reform, serving on the PLC Reform Committee and contributing to subcommittees addressing governance and civil society input into Palestinian Authority structures. She publicly critiqued inefficiencies in Palestinian political processes, including in 2005 briefings where she discussed electoral preparations and institutional challenges. Her membership effectively lapsed with the prolonged suspension of PLC activities after 2007, though she remained nominally affiliated until stepping back from broader Palestinian leadership roles around 20202021, announcing in February 2021 that she would not participate in prospective elections to mentor younger figures.

Election to the PLO Executive Committee

In August 2009, the Palestine National Council (PNC), the legislative body of the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO), convened to elect new members to the PLO Executive Committee, filling six vacancies created by deaths since the previous PNC plenary, including that of Yasser Arafat. Hanan Ashrawi, running as an independent candidate affiliated with the Third Way faction, secured one of the seats in a competitive election alongside Ahmad Qurei (Abu Ala), marking a rare instance of non-Fatah dominance in the committee's composition at the time.
The election on August 27, 2009, in Ramallah resulted in Ashrawi becoming the first woman ever elected to the PLO Executive Committee, the organization's highest executive organ responsible for implementing PNC decisions and overseeing diplomatic and operational affairs. This breakthrough highlighted shifting dynamics within Palestinian politics, where independents and reform-oriented figures gained ground amid criticisms of entrenched leadership under Mahmoud Abbas. Following her election, Ashrawi was appointed to head the committee's Department of Culture and Information, a role she held during her tenure.
Ashrawi was re-elected to the PLO Executive Committee in 2018, further solidifying her position until her resignation in 2020 amid internal PLO disputes. Her initial 2009 victory underscored her long-standing advocacy for Palestinian statehood and reform, though it occurred within a PLO framework often critiqued for its aging structure and limited accountability to broader Palestinian constituencies.

Controversies and Public Statements

Positions on Israel and Jewish Historical Claims

Ashrawi has consistently rejected the notion of exclusive Jewish historical or indigenous claims to the land of Israel/Palestine as a basis for statehood, framing Zionism as a modern colonial enterprise rather than a fulfillment of ancient ties. In aligning with Palestinian Authority narratives, she has echoed denials of key Jewish historical sites, such as the existence of ancient Jewish Temples in Jerusalem, portraying such claims as lacking evidentiary support and serving political ends to displace Palestinians. This stance dismisses archaeological findings, including inscriptions and structures from the First and Second Temple periods documented in sites like the City of David, as instrumentalized for territorial expansion rather than reflective of continuous Jewish presence predating Christianity and Islam by over a millennium.
Regarding Jewish peoplehood and homeland rights, Ashrawi has argued against Israel's self-definition as the state of the Jewish people, contending that such recognition entrenches ethnic supremacy and nullifies Palestinian rights within historic Palestine. In a 2014 interview, she stated that acknowledging Israel as a "Jewish state" would sideline the indigenous Arab population's claims, emphasizing instead a shared civic framework without privileging Jewish historical narratives. She has proposed that a future Palestinian state could accommodate Jews as citizens on equal terms but rejects any framework implying a unique Jewish right of return or sovereignty derived from biblical or historical indigeneity.
Ashrawi has further undermined Jewish historical claims by disputing the refugee status of approximately 850,000 Jews expelled or compelled to flee Arab and Muslim countries between 1948 and the 1970s, asserting their departure was voluntary and orchestrated by Zionist agencies rather than driven by pogroms, asset seizures, and official expulsionsas evidenced by events like the 1941 Farhud in Iraq (killing hundreds) and mass denationalizations in Egypt and Yemen. In a 2012 Huffington Post article, she wrote: "The emigration of Jews was a voluntary act... influenced by factors of pressure and temptation by Zionist movements," labeling assertions of their refugee plight as "deception and delusion" to equate with Palestinian displacements and bolster Israel's legitimacy. This position implicitly severs modern Jewish immigration from any restorative historical continuity, recasting it as ideological migration to seize "ancestral" Palestinian lands.

Responses to Palestinian Violence and Terrorism

In June 2002, during the Second Intifada, Hanan Ashrawi signed an "Urgent Appeal to Stop Suicide Bombings" published as a full-page advertisement in Palestinian newspapers, alongside figures such as Edward Said and Sari Nusseibeh. The statement, endorsed by over 50 intellectuals and activists, urged Palestinians to halt attacks inside Israeli cities, arguing that suicide bombings "deepen the hatred and widen the gap between the two peoples," provide Israel with pretexts for military incursions, and undermine Palestinian national interests by eroding international sympathy. By that point, Palestinian militants had conducted approximately 71 suicide bombings since September 2000, killing over 250 Israelis, mostly civilians.
The appeal framed cessation primarily in pragmatic terms, highlighting strategic harm to the Palestinian cause rather than issuing an absolute moral or religious prohibition against targeting civilians. It acknowledged internal Palestinian debate, with some signers facing backlash from factions viewing the bombings as a necessary "balance of terror" against Israeli operations. Ashrawi later described such appeals as efforts to prioritize self-preservation and long-term viability over short-term retaliation, amid a conflict that had seen thousands of Palestinian casualties from Israeli responses.
Earlier in the intifada, Ashrawi's statements often distinguished between legitimate resistance against military targets and unacceptable terrorism, advocating attacks on soldiers and settlers in occupied territories while critiquing indiscriminate violence. In November 2000, she stated, "We have to target soldiers and settlers. We have to put an end to this occupation," positioning such actions as defensive responses to settlement expansion and military presence in the West Bank and Gaza. Following specific suicide bombings, such as one in Jerusalem on April 12, 2002, that killed eight, her public reaction focused on Israeli Prime Minister Ariel Sharon's "lethal policies" as the root issue requiring intervention, rather than a direct denunciation of the attack itself.
Ashrawi has condemned terrorism outside the Israeli-Palestinian context unequivocally, including the September 11, 2001, al-Qaeda attacks, which she called "the worst terrorist attack in the history of humanity" and a "crime" against all people. She rejected Osama bin Laden's invocation of the Palestinian cause to justify such acts, asserting that the issue "is not up for grabs." In broader terms, she has characterized Palestinian violence as born of "desperation" under occupation, arguing that moral judgments on tactics like suicide bombings depend on their context within asymmetric conflict, though she signed statements affirming that intifada actions must avoid targeting non-combatants to retain legitimacy.

Views Following the October 7, 2023, Hamas Attack

Ashrawi framed the October 7, 2023, Hamas attack on Israelwhich resulted in the deaths of about 1,200 people, mostly civilians, and the abduction of over 250 hostagesas "an act of resistance against the occupation army that surrounds Gaza and regularly bombs and kills civilians." In an NPR interview shortly after the attack, she likened the incursion to "prisoners breaking out of their prison," attributing it to decades of Israeli oppression, including settlement expansion, home demolitions, and restrictions on Palestinian movement. She stated that Hamas had repeatedly warned Israel to halt such policies, positioning the assault as a desperate outburst from an "oppress people beyond endurance."
While expressing general opposition to civilian deaths"I certainly dont want to see any civilian killed"Ashrawi did not explicitly condemn the targeting of Israeli noncombatants by Hamas militants, who documented acts including killings at the Nova music festival and kibbutzim. Instead, she emphasized Palestinian grievances predating the attack, noting that 2023 had already been the deadliest year for Palestinians in the West Bank absent Hamas involvement, with systematic Israeli actions amounting to "terror."
Regarding Israel's subsequent military campaign in Gaza, which by late October 2023 had killed over 7,000 Palestinians according to Gaza health authorities, Ashrawi accused Israel of conducting "slaughter, massacres, ethnic cleansing, genocide," including the eradication of entire families (731 reported cases) and strikes on hospitals, schools, and refugee camps using U.S.-supplied weapons. She described the response as disproportionate and self-defeating, arguing in multiple interviews that it would radicalize more Palestinians, boost Hamas recruitment, and undermine the Palestinian Authority and PLO rather than eliminate the group. Ashrawi rejected U.S. portrayals of Hamas as akin to ISIS, viewing it instead as a legitimate representative of at least 30% of Palestinians with political, social, and governance roles beyond its military wing. She called the U.S. Israel's "partner in crime" for enabling the operations.
In later statements, such as a December 2023 interview, Ashrawi reiterated claims of Israeli genocide in Gaza, tying the conflict's escalation to Netanyahu's government policies and predicting no feasible eradication of Hamas through force alone. She advocated for international intervention to halt the violence, warning that the attack's aftermath would entrench narratives labeling all Palestinians as terrorists.

Awards, Appointments, and Later Activities

Notable Honors and Recognitions

Ashrawi received the Olof Palme Prize in 2002 from the Olof Palme Memorial Fund, which cited her "consistent and fearless fight over the years for Palestinian rights and a Middle East peace based on international law and respect for human rights."
In 2003, she was awarded the Sydney Peace Prize by the University of Sydney's Centre for Peace and Conflict Studies, recognizing her four-decade commitment to human rights, democracy, non-violence, and the Middle East peace process.
Ashrawi was granted the Mahatma Gandhi International Award for Peace and Reconciliation in 2005 by the Gandhi Development Trust in Durban, South Africa, for her contributions to reconciliation efforts.
In 2016, the French government bestowed upon her the Chevalier de la Lgion d'Honneur, a distinction for her work in international affairs and human rights.
She has been awarded eleven honorary doctorates from universities in the United States, Canada, Europe, and the Arab world, including a Doctor of Civil Laws from Saint Mary's University in Halifax, Canada, on May 12, 2000; a Doctor of Humane Letters from the American University in Cairo in spring 2003; and a Legum Doctor from the University of Bath in the 1990s.

Resignations and Post-2022 Developments

In December 2020, Ashrawi resigned from the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO) Executive Committee, stating that the Palestinian political system required "renewal and reinvigoration" to address stagnation, lack of internal democracy, and insufficient opportunities for women and youth in leadership roles. She emphasized the need for comprehensive reforms rather than symbolic gestures, denying reports that her departure protested the Palestinian Authority's resumption of security coordination with Israel. Palestinian Authority President Mahmoud Abbas accepted the resignation in early 2021. Ashrawi had served on the committee since her election in 2009, the first woman to hold the position.
Following her PLO resignation, Ashrawi continued advocacy through MIFTAH, the Palestinian Initiative for Global Dialogue and Democracy, which she founded in 1998 to promote civil society engagement and policy dialogue.
On September 3, 2022, Birzeit University's Board of Trustees elected Ashrawi as its chairperson, with the role taking effect on August 15, 2023, succeeding Hanna Nasir and marking the first time a woman held the position. In this capacity, she has focused on sustaining academic operations amid Israeli restrictions and supporting Palestinian higher education.
Post-2022, Ashrawi has maintained a public profile through speeches critiquing Israeli policies, U.S. involvement in the region, and internal Palestinian governance challenges, including calls for PLO revitalization and accountability in aid distribution during the Gaza conflict. In May 2025, she addressed the need for political will to enforce territorial sovereignty and end occupation-related violations. She has also participated in international forums, such as a September 2025 discussion on women's roles in resistance movements.

Personal Life and Ideology

Family and Personal Relationships

Hanan Ashrawi was born Hanan Daud Mikhail on October 8, 1946, in Nablus, into a prominent Christian family as the youngest of five daughters. Her father, Daud Mikhail, was a wealthy physician and surgeon known for his atheistic views despite a Christian upbringing, along with feminist leanings that influenced her worldview. Her mother worked as a nurse, and the family adhered to Anglican traditions, though extended relatives included Catholic nuns, Greek Orthodox members, and a Baptist uncle. One of her sisters married a Muslim, an uncommon interfaith union at the time that highlighted the family's relatively progressive stance within their community. Her sisters were named Huda, Muna, Abla, and Nadia.
On August 8, 1975, Ashrawi married Emile Ashrawi, a Jerusalem-born Christian who was five years her junior and from a less affluent background; he worked as a photographer for the United Nations, filmmaker, artist, and theater director involved in establishing the Bilalin theater troupe. The couple resided in the West Bank, where Emile supported family logistics, such as school pickups for their children during Ashrawi's frequent travels. Their marriage has endured without public reports of separation.
Ashrawi and her husband have two daughters, Zeina and Amal, born within six years of their wedding. Zeina Ashrawi Hutchison has engaged in advocacy for Palestinian rights, drawing on her upbringing in Ramallah amid her mother's activism. Little public detail exists on Amal's professional life, though both daughters were raised in a politically active household balancing Ashrawi's commitments with family stability.

Religious and Ideological Perspectives

Ashrawi was born on October 8, 1946, into a middle-class Christian family in Nablus, then part of the British Mandate of Palestine. Her father, Daoud Mikhail, a physician, held atheist views despite his Christian upbringing, while her mother, Wadi'a Ass'ad Mikhail, worked as an ophthalmic nurse. Ashrawi has affirmed her own Christian identity, describing herself as a Palestinian Christian descended from the region's earliest Christian communities and noting that "Jesus Christ was born in my country." This heritage positions her as a minority figure in the predominantly Muslim Palestinian context, where Christians have historically comprised a small but influential segment of society.
Ideologically, Ashrawi advocates secularism, emphasizing the separation of religion from state affairs. She has stated, "I do not look at people on the basis of their religion. I believe in the separation between religion and the state," and supports a "secular, democratic state" for Palestinians. Her early political affiliations reflected leftist influences, including alignment with the Democratic Front for the Liberation of Palestine (DFLP), a Marxist-Leninist faction within the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO), shaped by her father's exposure to socialist ideals. As a feminist, she has prioritized women's rights and political empowerment, drawing from her father's progressive stance on gender equality to promote greater female participation in Palestinian governance. These positions underscore a commitment to pluralistic nationalism over religious or theocratic governance.

Published Works and Intellectual Output

Key Books and Anthologies

Hanan Ashrawi authored This Side of Peace: A Personal Account, published in 1995 by Simon & Schuster, which chronicles her experiences as a spokesperson for the Palestinian delegation during the Madrid Conference and early Oslo negotiations, emphasizing internal Palestinian dynamics and challenges in the peace process from 1991 to 1993.
Earlier in her academic career, Ashrawi wrote The Modern Palestinian Short Story: An Introduction to Practical Criticism, a scholarly examination applying critical methods to Palestinian prose fiction, reflecting her background as a literature professor at Birzeit University.
Ashrawi edited the Anthology of Palestinian Literature, compiling selections of poetry, prose, and short stories to showcase evolving Palestinian literary traditions amid political upheaval. This work underscores her contributions to preserving and analyzing Palestinian cultural output before her shift to political activism.

Articles and Speeches

Ashrawi has contributed numerous op-eds and analytical pieces to international media outlets, emphasizing Palestinian perspectives on the conflict with Israel, statehood aspirations, and criticisms of settlement expansion and occupation policies. Through her organization MIFTAH, founded in 1998, she has published a series of opinion articles addressing issues such as women's rights, peace negotiations, and responses to normalization agreements between Israel and Arab states. For example, in an October 2012 op-ed in Haaretz titled "Israel's grand illusion on Palestinian UN recognition," she contended that Israeli threats against Palestinian UN membership bids were counterproductive and ignored the legitimacy of Palestinian self-determination claims under international law. Similarly, her June 5, 2017, New York Times piece "How the 1967 War Came Home to Me" detailed the personal toll of the Six-Day War on her family and community, framing the subsequent occupation as a source of enduring oppression affecting millions of Palestinians.
In September 2021, Ashrawi co-authored an opinion piece in The Guardian with former Algerian diplomat Lakhdar Brahimi, urging the United Nations to investigate Israel's governance over Palestinians as an "unequal regime of control" akin to apartheid structures, drawing parallels to South Africa's historical system while calling for accountability through international mechanisms. Her writings frequently attribute stalled peace efforts to Israeli intransigence, such as settlement construction and rejection of 1967 borders, though critics from pro-Israel organizations have characterized her arguments as omitting Palestinian agency in violence and negotiations.
Ashrawi has delivered speeches at global forums, including United Nations events and conferences, where she advocates for Palestinian rights and critiques power imbalances in the peace process. At the August 2001 World Conference against Racism in Durban, South Africa, she addressed the ongoing Palestinian "Nakba" (catastrophe), describing the population as held "hostage" to displacement and military control, and linking it to broader racial justice themes. She has spoken repeatedly at UN Committee on the Exercise of the Inalienable Rights of the Palestinian People meetings, such as in 2017 and 2024 sessions, highlighting decades of injustice, the failure of Oslo Accords outcomes, and the need for youth involvement in resistance and diplomacy. In a 2012 International Peace Institute address, she warned of a "one minute to midnight" crisis in negotiations, stressing the urgency of addressing core issues like refugees and Jerusalem without preconditions favoring Israel. Her oratory often employs historical narratives and appeals to international law, positioning Palestinian claims as rooted in UN resolutions and human rights standards.
