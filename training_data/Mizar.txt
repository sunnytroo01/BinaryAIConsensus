Mizar

1x
 Mizar ( Ursae Majoris) is a prominent multiple star system in the constellation Ursa Major, marking the middle star in the handle of the Big Dipper asterism and visible to the naked eye across much of Earth. It consists of a close visual binary pair, Mizar A and Mizar B, each of which is itself a spectroscopic binary, forming a quadruple system approximately 83 light-years distant, with a total luminosity about 60 times that of the Sun. Nearby lies the fainter companion star Alcor (80 Ursae Majoris), magnitude 4.0 and separated by 11.8 arcminutes, which is also a binary, making the entire Mizar-Alcor association a gravitationally bound sextuple system known since antiquity as a test of visual acuity, often called the "Horse and Rider."
Discovered as the first visual binary in 1650 by Italian astronomer Giovanni Battista Riccioli using a telescope, Mizar revolutionized early stellar astronomy by demonstrating that some "single" stars are resolvable pairs, with Mizar A and B orbiting each other every 20,000 years at a separation of 380 astronomical units. In 1889, Mizar A became the first spectroscopic binary identified by Edward C. Pickering through Doppler shifts in its spectral lines, revealing an inner pair of A2IV and A3V stars orbiting every 20.5 days; similarly, Mizar B's components were confirmed in 1908 as a spectroscopic binary with a 176-day period. Alcor's faint red dwarf companion was imaged in 2009 using advanced techniques, solidifying the system's complexity.
As a member of the Ursa Major Moving Groupa cluster of stars sharing common motion and origin about 500 million years agoMizar's primary components are hot A-type main-sequence stars with surface temperatures around 9,000 K, masses of 2.4 and 2.7 solar masses, and radii 2.4 times the Sun's, contributing to its bluish-white hue and brightness ranking it as the 70th brightest star in the night sky. This system holds cultural significance in various traditions, including Arabic lore where it symbolized a rider on a horse, and it continues to serve as an accessible target for amateur astronomers to explore stellar multiplicity and evolution.

Nomenclature

Etymology

The name Mizar derives from the Arabic term mi'zar (), meaning "apron," "girdle," or "waist-cloth," reflecting its perceived position as a band or wrapper around the "waist" or tail of the Great Bear in the constellation Ursa Major. In medieval Arabic astronomy, particularly in Abd al-Rahman al-Sufi's Kitab suwar al-kawakib al-thabita (Book of Fixed Stars, ca. 964 CE), the star is cataloged under the name Al-Maraqq, signifying "the loins" or "flank," emphasizing its anatomical analogy within the bear figure.
This Arabic nomenclature entered Western astronomy through translations of Islamic astronomical treatises during the medieval period, with al-Sufi's comprehensive star catalog playing a pivotal role in preserving and disseminating indigenous Arabic names alongside Ptolemaic traditions. The term "Mizar" was formalized in European star lists by the 16th century, as documented by scholars like Joseph Justus Scaliger, who adopted it despite occasional confusions with nearby stars such as Merak (Beta Ursae Majoris).
Across cultures, the name exhibits variations in spelling and pronunciation, such as Mizat or Mirza in early Arabic transliterations, and it has been rendered phonetically as /mazr/ in English. In traditional Indian astronomy, Mizar is identified as Vasistha (or Vashishtha), one of the Saptarishi (seven sages) personifying the stars of Ursa Major, symbolizing wisdom and marital fidelity when paired with its companion Alcor as Arundhati.

Designations

Mizar bears the Bayer designation Ursae Majoris (Zeta Ursae Majoris), assigned by the German astronomer Johann Bayer in his 1603 star atlas Uranometria, where Greek letters were used to label the brightest stars in each constellation in approximate order of magnitude.
It also has the Flamsteed designation 79 Ursae Majoris, from English Astronomer Royal John Flamsteed's Historia Coelestis Britannica published in 1725, which numbered stars in each constellation from west to east in order of right ascension.
In the Henry Draper Catalogue, compiled in the early 20th century, the primary component Mizar A is designated HD 116656, while the secondary Mizar B is HD 116657; correspondingly, in the Harvard Revised Catalogue (Bright Star Catalogue), they are HR 5054 and HR 5055, respectively.
The Hipparcos satellite astrometry catalog assigns the system the identifier HIP 65378.
Due to its status as a visual binary, the components are formally subdivided as Ursae Majoris (Mizar A) and Ursae Majoris (Mizar B).

Observational history

Early astronomical records

Mizar, designated as Ursae Majoris, was first systematically recorded in ancient Greek astronomy as one of the stars comprising the tail of the Great Bear in the constellation Ursa Major. Claudius Ptolemy included it in his Almagest (c. 150 CE), cataloging 27 fixed stars for Ursa Major and assigning Mizar a magnitude of 2, highlighting its prominence in the northern sky. This entry positioned Mizar as the middle of the three stars in the bear's tail, contributing to the constellation's recognizable outline visible to the naked eye.
During the Islamic Golden Age, Mizar's location within the (/page/Big Dipper) asterism drew attention from scholars who integrated Greek knowledge with local observations and cultural interpretations. Al-Biruni (9731048 CE), in his astronomical writings such as Al-Qanun al-Mas'udi, devoted sections to the seven stars of the (/page/Big Dipper), noting their significance in various traditions, including Indian and Bedouin lore where they represented anchorites or a funeral procession with Mizar as a key mourner star. These records emphasized Mizar's fixed position and brightness, aiding in timekeeping and navigation in the Islamic world.
In the European Renaissance, Danish astronomer Tycho Brahe advanced stellar documentation through precise naked-eye observations at his Uraniborg observatory. His 1598 star catalog, comprising 1004 entries, listed Mizar (as the 13th star in Ursa Major) with a magnitude of 2 and detailed ecliptic coordinates, improving upon Ptolemy's positions by a factor of ten in accuracy and confirming its role as a second-magnitude star in the Dipper's handle. Brahe's work, published posthumously in 1602, provided foundational data for subsequent astronomers studying stellar configurations.

Discovery of multiplicity

The visual multiplicity of Mizar was first discovered in 1650 by Italian astronomer Giovanni Battista Riccioli, who resolved it into two distinct componentsMizar A and the fainter Mizar Busing an early telescope, marking it as one of the earliest confirmed double stars. This observation built on tentative earlier reports but established Mizar's duplicity through reliable telescopic evidence, with the components separated by approximately 14 arcseconds.
In 1889, American astronomer Edward C. Pickering at Harvard College Observatory identified Mizar A as a spectroscopic binary by detecting periodic Doppler shifts in its spectral lines, revealing two orbiting stars too close to resolve visually; this was the first spectroscopic binary ever discovered, revolutionizing the study of unseen stellar companions. The short orbital period of about 20.5 days indicated a tight binary system within Mizar A, expanding the understanding of multiple star configurations beyond mere visual pairs.
The quadruple nature of the Mizar system was confirmed in 1908 when astronomers Edwin B. Frost and Oliver C. Lee at Yerkes Observatory observed variable radial velocities in the spectrum of Mizar B, proving it too is a spectroscopic binary with an orbital period of roughly 171 days. This discovery transformed Mizar from a double into a quadruple system, highlighting the prevalence of hierarchical multiples among bright stars.
Subsequent 20th-century interferometric observations further refined the separations and dynamics of Mizar's components, overcoming limitations of visual and spectroscopic methods; notably, in 1995, the Mark III Stellar Interferometer resolved the individual stars of Mizar A (Aa and Ab) at a separation of about 0.01 arcseconds, providing precise angular measurements and confirming their visual binary status. These studies, building on Michelson's early interferometry principles, enabled direct imaging of sub-arcsecond features and contributed to accurate orbital modeling of the system.

Position and visibility

Celestial coordinates

Mizar's position on the celestial sphere is defined by its equatorial coordinates in the J2000.0 epoch, with a right ascension of 13 23 55. and declination of +54 55 31.. These coordinates place it as the fourth star in the handle of the Big Dipper asterism.
In the galactic coordinate system, Mizar has a longitude of 113.11 and latitude of +61.58, positioning it in the direction of the Ursa Major constellation relative to the Milky Way's plane.
The star exhibits proper motion across the sky, as determined from astrometric observations by the Gaia mission in Data Release 3, with components of approximately +119 mas/yr in right ascension (accounting for the cosine of declination) and -26 mas/yr in declination at epoch J2016.0. This motion reflects Mizar's transverse velocity as part of the Ursa Major Moving Group.
Equatorial coordinates are subject to change due to the precession of Earth's rotational axis, a slow wobble with a period of about 25,772 years that shifts the reference equinox. For instance, in the B1950.0 epoch, Mizar's coordinates were right ascension 13 21 54. and declination +55 11 09., demonstrating a drift of roughly 2 minutes in right ascension and 14 arcminutes in declination over the 50-year interval between epochs. To compute positions for other epochs, astronomers apply precession matrices derived from models such as those in the International Astronomical Union standards.

Role in Ursa Major

Mizar serves as a key star in the constellation Ursa Major, forming the middle segment of the Big Dipper asterism's handle. Positioned between Alioth ( Ursae Majoris) at one end and Alkaid ( Ursae Majoris) at the other, it defines the curved "bend" that gives the handle its distinctive shape, making the asterism easily recognizable against the northern sky.
With an apparent magnitude of 2.23, Mizar stands out as one of the brighter stars in Ursa Major, ensuring its prominence in the night sky. It remains visible throughout the year from most of the Northern Hemisphere, appearing circumpolarnever setting below the horizonfrom latitudes above approximately 35N due to its declination of about +55.
Optimal viewing conditions for Mizar occur during spring evenings in mid-northern latitudes, when the Big Dipper rises high overhead after sunset, offering clear sightlines away from light pollution and horizon obstructions. The asterism's role extends to practical navigation, where the Big Dipper's overall orientation helps locate Polaris, the North Star, by aligning the bowl's outer stars as pointers roughly five times their separation distance.
Historically, Mizar's placement in the Big Dipper has aided asterism identification in star charts dating back to ancient astronomers, such as those in Ptolemy's Almagest, where the pattern served as a reference for mapping Ursa Major and nearby circumpolar features. This enduring utility has made it a foundational element in celestial navigation and observational astronomy across cultures.

Stellar system

System overview

Mizar constitutes a hierarchical quadruple star system within the constellation Ursa Major, comprising two spectroscopic binaries arranged in a wide visual pair. The primary component, Mizar A ( Ursae Majoris), is a double-lined spectroscopic binary consisting of two closely orbiting A-type main-sequence stars, while Mizar B ( Ursae Majoris) serves as its visual companion, originally perceived as single but later identified as another spectroscopic binary through radial velocity variations. This configuration forms a "double-double" architecture, with the inner binaries orbiting each other on timescales of days to months and the outer pair separated by approximately 14 arcseconds.
The system lies at a distance of 82.8 light-years (25.4 parsecs) from the Solar System, based on the Gaia DR3 parallax of 39.36 0.30 mas. Its combined apparent visual magnitude is 2.23, rendering it prominent to the naked eye as the fourth-brightest star in Ursa Major, while the absolute magnitude is approximately +0.2, reflecting its intrinsic luminosity at a standardized distance of 10 parsecs.
Mizar is a member of the Ursa Major Moving Group, a loose association of stars sharing common space motion and an estimated age of around 414 million years, indicative of its relatively youthful status among stellar aggregates. This kinship underscores the system's dynamical coherence with nearby Ursa Major stars, though detailed orbital parameters of the quadruple arrangement remain subjects of ongoing observation.

 Ursae Majoris (Mizar A)

 Ursae Majoris, known as Mizar A, is the brighter visual component of the Mizar multiple star system and constitutes a close spectroscopic binary pair composed of Mizar Aa and Mizar Ab. Both stars are A-type main-sequence dwarfs, with Mizar Aa classified as spectral type A2IV and Mizar Ab as A3V. These chemically peculiar Ap stars exhibit anomalous abundances of elements such as silicon, strontium, and rare earths, which are linked to the presence of strong, oblique magnetic fields that segregate chemical species in their atmospheres. Mizar A was the first spectroscopic binary discovered, identified in 1889 through the doubling of spectral lines due to the Doppler shift from the stars' orbital motion.
The components orbit each other with a period of approximately 20.5 days, resulting in a very small visual angular separation of 0.01 arcseconds, resolved only through interferometric observations. Mizar Aa has an estimated mass of 3.2 solar masses (M), while Mizar Ab is slightly less massive at about 2.0 M; both have radii of 2.4 solar radii (R), consistent with their main-sequence evolutionary stage. As rapid rotators, with projected rotational velocities (v sin i) of roughly 30-40 km/s, the stars' magnetic fieldsdetected through Zeeman splitting in their spectrainteract with their rotation to produce the observed peculiarities.
This binary pair provides key insights into the dynamics of close stellar systems, serving as a benchmark for spectroscopic binary studies since its discovery. The distance to the system, approximately 83 light-years, places Mizar A at about 25 parsecs from Earth, allowing detailed characterization of its properties.

 Ursae Majoris (Mizar B)

 Ursae Majoris, commonly known as Mizar B, is the fainter visual companion to Mizar A in the Mizar system, classified as a main-sequence star of spectral type A5V with an apparent visual magnitude of 3.95. This places it among the brighter A-type stars visible to the naked eye as part of the Big Dipper's handle, though it requires a small telescope to resolve from Mizar A at a separation of 14.4 arcseconds.
Mizar B has an estimated mass of about 1.8 solar masses (M) and a radius of approximately 1.9 solar radii (R), consistent with its evolutionary stage as a hydrogen-fusing dwarf on the main sequence. Its surface temperature, inferred from the spectral type, is around 8,200 K, giving it a white appearance with a subtle yellowish tint compared to the bluer Mizar A.
Visually, Mizar B appears as a single star, but spectroscopic observations reveal it to be a binary system with a faint, unresolved companion. Radial velocity variations indicate an orbital period of approximately 175 days for this inner pair, with the companion likely being a low-mass star too dim to detect directly in the visible spectrum. This multiplicity contributes to the overall complexity of the Mizar system without altering its apparent simplicity to the unaided eye.

Physical properties

Spectral classification

Mizar Aa is classified as an A2IV subgiant star with an effective temperature of approximately 9,200 K. Its spectrum is characterized by strong helium lines and prominent Balmer hydrogen absorption lines, which are hallmarks of early A-type stars transitioning toward the subgiant phase. These features indicate a hot atmosphere where ionization states favor enhanced visibility of these lines.
In contrast, Mizar Ab is a main-sequence A3V star with an effective temperature of about 8,600 K. The spectrum shows similar metallic lines to Aa but with reduced intensity in certain features, reflecting its less evolved state and stable main-sequence position. The double-lined nature of the Mizar A binary allows for disentangling these spectra, revealing subtle differences in line strengths between the components.
Mizar B is classified as a peculiar A5VA7V main-sequence star (kA1h(eA)mA7IV-V) with an effective temperature around 7,600 K. Its spectrum exhibits variable line profiles, attributed to rapid rotation that broadens and distorts absorption features, particularly in the metallic lines. The overall metallicity of the Mizar system is slightly subsolar, with -0.1, showing no major chemical peculiarities beyond the rotational effects on line profiles.

Mizar-Alcor pair

Optical companionship

Mizar appears closely paired with the nearby star Alcor (80 Ursae Majoris), forming a prominent naked-eye double in the handle of the Big Dipper asterism. Alcor has an apparent visual magnitude of 4.0 and is separated from Mizar by 11.8 arcminutes, equivalent to about 0.2 degrees on the sky. This separation allows the pair to be resolved by observers with normal visual acuity of around 1 arcminute, though the test historically emphasized detecting the fainter Alcor against the brighter Mizar.
The Mizar-Alcor pair has long served as a rudimentary test of eyesight in ancient cultures. Among ancient Arabs, it was known as the "Arab Eye Test," where the ability to distinguish Alcor from Mizar indicated good vision, correlating roughly with 20/20 Snellen acuity. Greeks also recognized the pair in their constellations and used it similarly to assess visual sharpness for tasks like warfare. This perceptual challenge arose not from the angular separation, which is relatively wide, but from Alcor's moderate brightness requiring clear night skies and keen perception to spot it distinctly beside Mizar.
Both stars belong to the Ursa Major Moving Group, a collection of stars sharing a common origin and proper motion across the sky. This membership ensures that their relative positions remain stable over human timescales and even millennia, preserving the apparent companionship observed since antiquity.
In folklore, the duo is often depicted as "the horse and rider," with Mizar as the horse and Alcor as its rider, a motif rooted in Arabic astronomical traditions. Variants in Bedouin lore sometimes portray them as a "camel and rider," symbolizing companionship in desert narratives. This imagery underscores the pair's enduring role in cultural storytelling, blending visual astronomy with mythic interpretation.

Astronomical distinction

Alcor, formally known as 80 Ursae Majoris, is classified as an A5V main-sequence star with a mass of approximately 1.8 solar masses (M) and lies at a distance of about 81 light-years from Earth, closely matching the distance of the Mizar system. Unlike Mizar, which is a hot A-type star in a tight quadruple configuration, Alcor's physical properties reflect its independent evolution as a younger, hydrogen-fusing dwarf in the Ursa Major Moving Group, a loose association of stars sharing similar space velocities but not forming bound clusters. Alcor itself is a binary system comprising the primary A5V star (Alcor A) and a faint M3.5V red dwarf companion (Alcor B) separated by about 1 arcsecond.
While their shared proper motion and moving group membership suggest possible gravitational association, the lack of close binding between Alcor and Mizar is evident from their projected separation of roughly 0.3 light-years, calculated from the 11.8 arcminute angular distance and their common parallax measurements. Additionally, the relative proper motion difference of approximately 0.01 arcseconds per year between the systems demonstrates that they are not orbiting a common center of mass, as a bound pair would exhibit synchronized motion over such a wide separation. Data from the Gaia mission's DR3 (2022) further confirm this independence, showing a separation of approximately 0.4 light-years and concordant overall velocities consistent with moving group membership but distinct trajectories that preclude a close binary relationship. These faint companions, with magnitudes exceeding 19, orbit at wider separations of several arcseconds and contribute to Alcor's complexity without altering its distinction from the more compact Mizar hierarchy. Gaia's DR3 astrometric data refines the orbit of Alcor B, reinforcing that Alcor operates as an unbound neighbor to Mizar despite their visual proximity and shared dynamical history in the Ursa Major stream.

Cultural impact

Mythological references

In Greek mythology, Mizar forms part of the constellation Ursa Major, known as the Great Bear, which represents the nymph Callisto, a companion of the goddess Artemis who was transformed into a bear by Zeus to protect her from Hera's wrath after Zeus seduced her. The handle of the Big Dipper asterism, where Mizar is located, symbolizes the bear's tail in this tale, emphasizing the constellation's elongated form as depicted in ancient sources.
In Arabic folklore, Mizar derives its name from "al-mi'zar," meaning "apron," "girdle," or "loin-cloth," referring to its position in the bear's figure as a garment or covering. It is often paired with the nearby star Alcor, forming the "horse and rider" motif, where Mizar represents the horse and Alcor the rider, a visual association used in ancient Arab astronomy to test eyesight and evoke tales of companionship in the night sky.
In Chinese astronomy, Mizar is identified as "Yuheng," the fifth star in the Bei Dou or Northern Dipper asterism within Ursa Major, serving as a pivotal point in imperial divination practices where the Dipper's orientation was observed to predict auspicious events, governance, and cosmic harmony. This role underscored Yuheng's significance in Daoist cosmology, linking it to themes of balance and fate among ancient Chinese astronomers.
Native American stories feature variations on Mizar's mythological role, particularly among the Mi'kmaq people, where it represents Chickadee, one of seven bird hunters pursuing the Great Bear across the sky, with Alcor as its cooking pot, symbolizing the eternal chase of seasons and renewal. In some tribal traditions, Mizar contributes to broader narratives of celestial hunters or elements of sacred wheels, illustrating interconnected life cycles and natural order.

Namesakes and popular culture

In military contexts, the name Mizar has been applied to naval vessels during World War II. The USS Mizar (AF-12), originally a United Fruit Company cargo and passenger liner named Quirigua built in 1932, was acquired by the U.S. Navy in 1941, converted into a stores ship, and commissioned on June 14, 1943. It served in both the Atlantic and Pacific theaters, supplying provisions to bases and ships until decommissioning on April 1, 1946.
Scientifically, Mizar refers to a formal proof verification system in mathematics. Developed by Andrzej Trybulec in the 1970s at the University of Biaystok, the Mizar system allows mathematicians to write proofs in a structured, declarative language based on Tarski-Grothendieck set theory, which are then automatically checked for correctness by a computer program. It includes the Mizar Mathematical Library (MML), a vast repository containing over 1,400 articles with more than 65,000 formalized theorems as of 2023 (growing to 1,493 articles by May 2025), facilitating interactive proof development and reuse.
In popular culture, Mizar appears as a navigational reference and planetary system in science fiction. In the Star Trek universe, the Mizar system is depicted as an inhabited location in the Alpha Quadrant, home to the pacifistic Mizarian species on Mizar II; the Mizarians are featured in the 1989 episode "The Ensigns of Command" of Star Trek: The Next Generation, where their reluctance to resist invasion highlights themes of non-violence. The star system is also included in the video game Elite Dangerous, modeled as a quaternary system based on the real astronomical configuration, allowing players to explore its planets and stations since the game's 2014 release.
Additionally, Mizar names an optics brand inspired by the star's historical use as an eyesight test alongside Alcor. Mizar Optical Instruments, a Japanese company established in the mid-20th century, produces telescopes, binoculars, spotting scopes, and other viewing devices, evoking the ancient naked-eye challenge of resolving the Mizar-Alcor pair to assess visual acuity.
