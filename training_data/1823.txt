1823
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 1823 marked a significant juncture in transatlantic relations with the proclamation of the Monroe Doctrine by U.S. President James Monroe in his December 2 address to Congress, which warned European powers against new colonizations or interventions in the independent nations of the Western Hemisphere, thereby asserting American primacy in regional affairs. In exploration, British navigator James Weddell achieved a then-record southern latitude of 7415 S on February 20 during a sealing voyage, penetrating what became known as the Weddell Sea amid unusually ice-free conditions. Europe witnessed the French Army of the Pyrenees, dubbed the Hundred Thousand Sons of Saint Louis, cross into Spain on April 7 to quash constitutionalist forces and reinstate absolutist King Ferdinand VII, culminating in the capture of key fortresses like Trocadero.
The year also featured cultural milestones, including the premiere of Gioachino Rossini's opera Semiramide in Venice on February 3, and the final expulsion of Portuguese loyalists from Bahia, solidifying Brazil's independence. Among notable deaths were English physician Edward Jenner, pioneer of smallpox vaccination through cowpox inoculation, who succumbed to a stroke on January 26 at age 73; French Marshal Louis-Nicolas Davout, renowned for tactical prowess at Austerlitz and Auerstedt, on June 1 from tuberculosis; and explorer Giovanni Battista Belzoni in August while seeking ancient manuscripts in Benin. Births included British naturalist Alfred Russel Wallace, co-developer of evolutionary theory by natural selection, on January 8, and Ottoman Sultan Abdlmecid I on April 23, who later enacted Tanzimat reforms. These events underscored 1823's blend of diplomatic assertions, exploratory feats, monarchical restorations, and transitions in scientific and imperial legacies.

Events

JanuaryMarch

On January 3, Stephen F. Austin received a grant of one league of land on the Brazos River in Mexican Texas from the Coahuila y Tejas state legislature, authorizing him to settle 300 families as colonists.
On January 27, U.S. President James Monroe nominated and the Senate confirmed the first American diplomatic ministers to independent South American nations, including Caesar Rodney as minister to Buenos Aires (United Provinces of the Ro de la Plata, now Argentina), Condy Raguet to Brazil, and John M. Forbes to Buenos Aires as charg d'affaires, marking formal U.S. recognition of their sovereignty following independence from Spain.
On February 3, Gioachino Rossini's opera Semiramide premiered at the Teatro La Fenice in Venice under the Austrian Kingdom of Lombardy-Venetia, with libretto by Gaetano Rossi based on Voltaire's tragedy Semiramis, featuring Isabella Colbran in the title role and marking Rossini's last opera seria.
On February 20, British navigator James Weddell, aboard the brig Jane and cutter Beaufoy, reached a southern latitude of 7415 S in an open sea, surpassing prior records set by James Cook and naming the expanse the "Open Sea" amid favorable conditions unusual for the season.
On February 28, the U.S. Supreme Court issued its decision in Johnson v. M'Intosh, holding that Native American tribes held occupancy rights to lands but that fee simple title vested in the discovering European sovereign, invalidating private purchases from tribes without government extinguishment based on longstanding international precedents of conquest and discovery.
On March 11, the first normal school in the United States, Concord Academy in Concord, Vermont, opened to train teachers, established by Samuel Read Hall to professionalize education instruction.

AprilJune

On April 7, the French Army of the Hundred Thousand Sons of Saint Louis, comprising around 60,000 troops under the command of the Duke of Angoulme, crossed the Bidasoa River into Spain, marking the start of the military intervention authorized by the Congress of Verona to quell liberal constitutionalist forces and reinstate absolute monarchy under King Ferdinand VII. This action followed Ferdinand's imprisonment by Spanish liberals during the Trienio Liberal (18201823), a period of revolutionary governance that had dismantled absolutist structures, prompting European monarchies to view the unrest as a threat to stability.
Three days later, on April 10, French forces commenced the Siege of Pamplona, a fortified city held by constitutionalist troops, initiating a prolonged blockade that aimed to isolate and compel surrender of key liberal strongholds in northern Spain. The operation reflected the campaign's strategy of rapid advances combined with sieges to dismantle revolutionary defenses without widespread devastation, prioritizing restoration over conquest.
In Mexican Texas, empresario Stephen F. Austin, operating under his 1821 colonization contract, actively surveyed and prepared lands for Anglo-American settlement during this quarter, laying groundwork for contractual grants that would formalize pioneer claims and encourage migration into the sparsely populated frontier. These efforts, continuing from initial surveys, positioned Austin's colonyencompassing modern Austin Countyas the primary vector for organized territorial expansion, with land titles beginning issuance in coordination with Mexican authorities to promote agricultural development and loyalty to the Mexican government.

JulySeptember

On July 2, 1823, the Portuguese garrison in Salvador, Bahia, surrendered to Brazilian forces after a prolonged siege, marking the final expulsion of loyalist troops from the province and solidifying Brazilian control amid the War of Independence. This outcome followed naval blockades and land campaigns led by figures such as Lord Cochrane, resulting in the evacuation of approximately 10,000 Portuguese soldiers and securing Bahia's allegiance to the Empire of Brazil under Pedro I, thereby reducing internal fragmentation risks in the nascent empire.
The preceding day, July 1, 1823, saw the United Provinces of Central America declare formal independence from Mexico, following Emperor Agustn de Iturbide's abdication announced on March 19, 1823, establishing a provisional federation across former Spanish captaincies-general including Guatemala, El Salvador, Honduras, Nicaragua, and Costa Rica. This act stemmed from local assemblies rejecting integration into the Mexican Empire, reflecting empirical divergences in regional elites' preferences for republican governance over monarchical centralization, though it initiated a period of constitutional assembly and federal stabilization efforts amid ongoing factional disputes.
In late August 1823, American frontiersman Hugh Glass, scouting for game during a fur-trapping expedition in the unclaimed territories of present-day South Dakota, encountered and killed a grizzly bear defending its cubs, only to sustain grievous wounds from the animal's counterattack. Left for dead by companions Andrew Henry and John Fitzgerald due to the expedition's precarious position post-Arikara conflicts, Glass regained consciousness amid exposed ribs, lacerated flesh, and infection risks, then crawled roughly 200 miles over six weeks to Fort Kiowa, subsisting on berries, roots, and scavenged buffalo carcasses while evading further threats. This incident underscored the lethal hazards of westward expansion in ungoverned plains, where small trapping parties faced acute vulnerabilities from wildlife and isolation without institutional support, contributing to the empirical tally of high mortality ratesestimated at over 50% for such venturesin early 19th-century American frontier commerce.

OctoberDecember

On October 9, the remaining royalist garrison at Puerto Cabello surrendered to republican forces, effectively ending organized Spanish military resistance in Venezuela.
On December 2, President James Monroe delivered his 1823 State of the Union Address (seventh annual message) to the 18th United States Congress, declaring that the Western Hemisphere's American continents should not be regarded as subjects for future colonization by European powers and that any attempt to extend their political systems to independent American nations would be considered a manifestation of unfriendly disposition toward the United States.

Undated

Joseph Smith reported that, beginning in 1823, he received multiple visitations from an angelic being identified as Moroni, who informed him of the existence of buried golden plates containing an ancient religious record and sacred artifacts, located on a hill near his family's farm in Manchester, New York. According to Smith's later accounts, Moroni provided detailed descriptions of the plates' contents, which chronicled the history and teachings of ancient American peoples, and instructed Smith to retrieve them only after spiritual preparation, emphasizing their divine origin and purpose for translation into English. These reported events marked the initiation of Smith's involvement with what would become the Book of Mormon, though contemporary corroboration beyond Smith's testimony remains absent from independent records.

The Monroe Doctrine

Proclamation and Immediate Context

President James Monroe proclaimed the doctrine on December 2, 1823, during his Seventh Annual Message to Congress, warning European powers against further colonization or intervention in the Western Hemisphere. The message's relevant passages, primarily drafted by Secretary of State John Quincy Adams, emphasized that the American continents were closed to new European colonization and that any attempt to extend monarchical systems there would be viewed as a threat to U.S. peace and safety. Adams, skeptical of aligning with Britain, convinced Monroe to issue a unilateral U.S. statement rather than a joint declaration.
The proclamation responded to immediate diplomatic pressures, including British Foreign Secretary George Canning's August 1823 proposal for a joint Anglo-American declaration opposing European recolonization of Latin America, which Monroe initially considered after consultations with former presidents Thomas Jefferson and James Madison. Canning's overture stemmed from Britain's commercial interests in the newly independent republics, but U.S. leaders opted for independence in policy articulation to assert national sovereignty.
Surrounding threats included the post-Napoleonic Holy Alliancecomprising Russia, Austria, Prussia, and supported by Franceof potential intervention to restore Spanish rule in Latin America, following successful independence movements led by Simn Bolvar and others since 1810. A key empirical trigger was France's April 7, 1823, invasion of Spain with 95,000 troops under the Duke of Angoulme, authorized by the Holy Alliance at the Verona Congress to suppress liberal revolts and reinstate absolutist King Ferdinand VII, raising fears of a similar monarchical intervention across the Atlantic. This action exemplified the Alliance's commitment to counter-revolutionary principles, prompting U.S. vigilance against analogous moves in the Americas.

Core Principles and Causal Foundations

The Monroe Doctrine articulated a policy opposing the establishment of new European colonies in the Western Hemisphere, declaring that the American continents, having achieved and maintained independence, were no longer subjects for future colonization by European powers. This principle stemmed from the recognition that such colonization would disrupt the established sovereignty of newly independent states, potentially reigniting conflicts akin to those that had led to the successful rebellions against Spanish and Portuguese rule between 1810 and 1822. Causally, the doctrine viewed monarchical expansionismevident in the Holy Alliance's interventions in Europeas incompatible with the self-determination of republican governments, positing that external recolonization would inevitably threaten regional stability and the security of the United States by altering the balance of power through forced subjugation rather than consensual governance.
Central to the doctrine was the prohibition against European interference in the affairs of independent American nations, framing any attempt to oppress or control their destiny as an unfriendly act toward the United States. This non-interference tenet rested on the empirical observation that the independence of states like Mexico, Argentina, and Chile had been secured through prolonged struggles, creating a verifiable status quo of sovereign governance that warranted preservation to avoid cascading instability from renewed European dominion. From a causal standpoint, intervention by powers such as Russia or the Holy Alliance could propagate absolutist systems, undermining the republican experiments in the Americas and exposing the U.S. to direct threats, as proximity would amplify the risks of spillover conflicts or blockades compared to distant European entanglements. The doctrine thus prioritized the territorial and political integrity post-independence as a pragmatic barrier against revanchist policies, without implying U.S. authority to enforce compliance unilaterally.
In reciprocity, the United States pledged abstention from European internal affairs and respect for existing colonies, establishing mutual non-intervention as a foundational safeguard. This bilateral restraint acknowledged the causal inefficacy of U.S. involvement in Old World disputes, given America's limited military capacity in 1823 and the historical lesson that entangling alliances had embroiled prior powers in protracted wars without altering core dynastic outcomes. By delineating separate spheresthe Americas insulated from European colonization and Europe free from American meddlingthe doctrine aimed to perpetuate peace through compartmentalized sovereignty, where each hemisphere's governments could evolve independently without the provocation of cross-continental hegemony or subversion. This framework did not authorize U.S. dominance over Latin America but instead reinforced a hands-off equilibrium, grounded in the principle that stable self-rule, once empirically demonstrated, should not be overturned by external powers seeking to impose alien systems.

Contemporary Reactions

British Foreign Secretary George Canning endorsed the principles of the Monroe Doctrine shortly after its proclamation on December 2, 1823, aligning them with Britain's established policy of non-intervention in the Americas to protect expanding commercial ties with the newly independent states. In dispatches from late 1823, Canning highlighted the doctrine's compatibility with British interests, particularly in preventing Spanish or French recolonization that could disrupt trade routes and markets; he had earlier proposed a joint Anglo-American declaration on October 16, 1823, to U.S. Minister Richard Rush, which was declined by Secretary of State John Quincy Adams to avoid implying U.S. dependence on British naval power. Canning's private correspondence, including notes to Ambassador Stratford Canning, framed the U.S. stance as a welcome reinforcement of mutual opposition to Holy Alliance ambitions, though he critiqued its separation of non-colonization from non-interference as doctrinally imprecise.
Latin American republican leaders received news of the doctrine with measured approval in early 1824 dispatches and letters, perceiving it as diplomatic leverage against reconquest threats from European powers, despite the absence of U.S. commitments to direct military aid. Officials in states like Mexico and Colombia expressed optimism in correspondence to U.S. envoys, viewing Monroe's warnings as a practical deterrent grounded in the post-Napoleonic balance of power, which prioritized empirical stability over ideological solidarity; however, skepticism persisted regarding U.S. resolve, given its recent demobilization after the War of 1812 and focus on domestic expansion. This reaction underscored a pragmatic assessment: the doctrine bolstered hemispheric legitimacy without altering the republics' primary reliance on internal consolidation and British commerce for survival.
Continental European monarchies, including Austria, Prussia, and Russia within the Holy Alliance, derided the doctrine in diplomatic exchanges and private memoranda as presumptuous bluster from a militarily feeble republic, emphasizing the U.S.'s scant resourcesa standing army of under 10,000 men and a navy dwarfed by Britain'sas rendering enforcement implausible against coalition forces. Austrian Chancellor Klemens von Metternich conveyed outrage in a December 1823 dispatch to allies, decrying Monroe's message as an insolent challenge to sovereign prerogatives and a symptom of republican hubris, while prioritizing European interventions like France's invasion of Spain over distant American pretensions. Russian Tsar Alexander I similarly dismissed it in early 1824 instructions to envoys, focusing instead on Pacific claims but recognizing the doctrine's lack of coercive teeth amid post-war exhaustion. These responses highlighted stark power disparities, with European courts viewing the proclamation as rhetorical posturing rather than a credible barrier to dynastic restorations.

Historical Interpretations and Debates

Historians interpret the Monroe Doctrine primarily as a defensive policy aimed at deterring European recolonization of independent American states, rooted in the geopolitical reality of a militarily weak United States reliant on British naval supremacy for enforcement rather than its own capabilities. The original proclamation emphasized mutual non-interferenceprohibiting European powers from extending their systems into the hemisphere while affirming U.S. abstention from European affairsserving as a bulwark for sovereign republics against monarchical restoration efforts post-Napoleonic Wars, without endorsing U.S. territorial expansionism. This reading privileges the doctrine's causal foundation in preserving the status quo of decolonized independence, countering anachronistic projections that retroactively frame it as a license for American hegemony.
Debates persist over its enforceability, with empirical evidence from the 19th century highlighting limited U.S. military action and reliance on symbolic deterrence. For instance, during the French intervention in Mexico (18621867), the U.S. issued diplomatic protests but refrained from direct enforcement amid its Civil War, allowing European powers to test hemispheric boundaries without immediate reprisal; similarly, initial U.S. inaction on Spanish control of Cuba underscored the doctrine's aspirational rather than operational nature against existing colonial holdings. Conservative scholars argue this symbolic posture effectively deterred overt recolonization by signaling alignment with British free-trade interests, fostering stable, independent markets in Latin America against absolutist threats, as evidenced by the absence of major European reconquests post-1823. In contrast, progressive critiques highlight perceived hypocrisy in U.S. continental expansions, such as the 1845 Texas annexation, portraying the doctrine as a veil for manifest destiny; however, these actions targeted unsettled or disputed territories rather than sovereign states, aligning with the principle's focus on preventing new European encroachments rather than prohibiting all hemispheric change.
Evolving interpretations reveal tensions between the doctrine's anti-colonial origins and later expansions, such as the Roosevelt Corollary (1904), which repurposed it for U.S. interventions to preempt European debt collections, diverging from Monroe's non-interventionist intent. Right-leaning analyses emphasize its role in shielding nascent republics from socialist or monarchist ideologies, promoting self-determination and economic liberty over imperial subjugation, while left-leaning views decry it as proto-imperialist for enabling U.S. dominance; yet, the original text's explicit rejection of colonization and evidence of non-enforcement in crises like the 1830s Texas recognition by Europe substantiate a narrower, reciprocal non-interference framework, not a blueprint for unilateral U.S. control. This distinction underscores how post-19th-century misapplications, often by expansionist administrations, obscured the doctrine's foundational realism in a multipolar world where U.S. power was nascent and deterrence depended on alliances rather than coercion.

Births

JanuaryJune

!
Edward Jenner, the English physician who pioneered vaccination against smallpox, died on 26 January 1823 in Berkeley, Gloucestershire, at the age of 73 from a stroke. In 1796, Jenner empirically tested the folk observation that milkmaids exposed to cowpox appeared immune to smallpox by inoculating an 8-year-old boy, James Phipps, with cowpox pus and later exposing him to smallpox variolous matter, confirming cross-immunity without disease development; this causal mechanism of inducing immunity via a milder related pathogen underpinned the vaccine's efficacy, averting millions of deaths and enabling smallpox's eradication.
Ann Radcliffe, influential English Gothic novelist, died on 7 February 1823 in London at age 58, likely from asthma or a respiratory ailment. Her novels, including The Romance of the Forest (1791) and The Mysteries of Udolpho (1794), established conventions of the Gothic genre such as remote castles, supernatural hints resolved rationally, and heroines navigating peril through sensibility and virtue, achieving commercial success with Udolpho selling over 1,000 copies in its first year.
Louis-Nicolas Davout, Duke of Auerstedt and Napoleonic marshal, died on 1 June 1823 in Paris at age 53 from pulmonary tuberculosis. As war minister under the restored Bourbon monarchy from 1815, Davout reformed army administration by standardizing logistics and discipline, drawing on his prior record of undefeated corps commands, notably at Auerstedt where his 27,000 men repelled 63,000 Prussians through coordinated artillery and infantry squares, inflicting 13,000 casualties while suffering 7,000.

JulyDecember

Lazare Carnot, a French engineer, mathematician, and politician instrumental in organizing the French Revolutionary armiesearning the moniker "Organizer of Victory" through efficient mobilization and strategic planningdied in exile on August 2, 1823, in Magdeburg, Prussia, following the Bourbon Restoration's suppression of revolutionary figures.
Pope Pius VII (Barnaba Chiaramonti), who as pontiff from 1800 pragmatically navigated the Napoleonic upheavalsincluding negotiating the 1801 Concordat to restore Church influence in France amid secularization and occupation, while later excommunicating Napoleon for annexing papal territoriesdied on August 20, 1823, in Rome, succumbing to complications from a thigh fracture sustained in a fall on July 6. His diplomatic balancing preserved the papacy's institutional continuity despite temporal losses, prioritizing long-term ecclesiastical autonomy over immediate confrontation.
David Ricardo, a British political economist of Sephardic Jewish descent who amassed wealth as a stockbroker before entering politics, advanced classical economics through deductive reasoning, notably formulating the labor theory of valuepositing that commodity prices derive from embodied laborand championing free trade via comparative advantage, arguing nations benefit from specializing in lower-opportunity-cost goods regardless of absolute efficiencies. He died suddenly on September 11, 1823, at age 51, at Gatcombe Park, Gloucestershire, from an acute ear infection. Ricardo's parliamentary advocacy influenced Britain's 1846 repeal of the Corn Laws, grounding policy in empirical observation of market dynamics over mercantilist restrictions.
Giovanni Battista Belzoni, an Italian strongman-turned-explorer who pioneered modern Egyptology by mechanically extracting obelisks, sarcophagi, and statuessuch as the colossal bust of Ramesses II now in the British Museumfrom sites like Thebes and Abu Simbel, thereby enabling European study of ancient artifactsdied on December 3, 1823, aged 45, from dysentery at Gwato (near modern Ughoton, Nigeria) during an expedition to trace the Niger River's source. His physical prowess and ingenuity facilitated the removal of massive relics previously deemed immovable, though his methods involved forceful interventions that later drew ethical scrutiny absent at the time.
