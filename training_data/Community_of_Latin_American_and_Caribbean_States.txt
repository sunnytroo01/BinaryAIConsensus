 The Community of Latin American and Caribbean States (CELAC) is a regional intergovernmental forum uniting 33 sovereign countries from Latin America and the Caribbean, excluding the United States and Canada, established on 23 December 2011 in Caracas, Venezuela, to advance political dialogue, economic cooperation, and social integration among members. It emerged as the successor to the Rio Group and the Summit of Latin America and the Caribbean on Integration and Development (CALC), operating without a permanent secretariat and relying on rotating pro tempore presidencies and biennial summits to coordinate positions on regional and global issues. CELAC's foundational declaration emphasizes consensus-based decision-making to promote sustainable development, peace, and enhanced representation in international forums, positioning the bloc as an autonomous voice for the hemisphere's southern nations. While it has facilitated dialogues with external partners, such as the European Union through periodic summits yielding joint declarations on trade and climate, CELAC's effectiveness has been constrained by deep ideological divides among members, including stark differences between democratic and authoritarian-leaning governments, often resulting in stalled initiatives and failure to address internal crises like electoral disputes or human rights deteriorations in countries such as Venezuela and Nicaragua. These fractures underscore CELAC's role more as a symbolic counterweight to U.S.-influenced bodies like the Organization of American States than as a robust engine for tangible regional integration, with limited concrete achievements in areas like infrastructure or trade liberalization amid persistent political polarization.

History

Origins in Regional Initiatives (20082010)

The Rio Group, established in 1986 as a mechanism for political consultation among Latin American and Caribbean nations independent of United States influence, served as a primary precursor to CELAC during this period. In December 2008, Cuba was formally admitted as a full member during an extraordinary summit in Salvador, Bahia, Brazil, following foreign ministers' approval in Zacatecas, Mexico, marking the inclusion of all 33 Latin American and Caribbean states in a regional forum and symbolizing a push for broader hemispheric unity excluding North America.
Concurrently, Brazil under President Luiz Incio Lula da Silva initiated the Latin American and Caribbean Summit on Integration and Development (CALC) to foster exclusive regional dialogue on integration without participation from the United States or Canada. The inaugural CALC summit occurred on December 1617, 2008, in Costa do Saupe, Bahia, Brazil, convening heads of state and government from 32 nations and producing commitments to advance political, economic, social, and cultural cooperation. This event represented the first such gathering of all regional leaders, emphasizing autonomy from external powers and laying groundwork for institutionalized integration.
These initiatives converged in early 2010 amid efforts to consolidate fragmented regional mechanisms. On February 2223, 2010, the Latin American and Caribbean Unity Summit, hosted in Playa del Carmen, Riviera Maya, Mexico, brought together leaders from the Rio Group and CALC, resulting in the Declaration of Unity of Latin America and the Caribbean, which proposed merging the two bodies into a new entitythe Community of Latin American and Caribbean States (CELAC)to serve as a unified platform for dialogue and decision-making on regional issues. The declaration underscored commitments to sovereignty, non-intervention, and collective advancement, explicitly excluding North American states to prioritize intra-regional coordination. In July 2010, Venezuelan President Hugo Chvez and Chilean President Sebastin Piera were appointed co-chairs to draft CELAC's foundational documents, bridging these origins toward formal establishment.

Founding and Consolidation (20112012)

The Community of Latin American and Caribbean States (CELAC) was formally established at the inaugural summit held in Caracas, Venezuela, on December 23, 2011, where heads of state or government from all 33 Latin American and Caribbean nations convened to sign the Declaration of Caracas. This gathering, hosted by Venezuelan President Hugo Chvez, marked the culmination of prior regional efforts, including the 2010 Mexican initiative for a Latin American and Caribbean Community on Equality and Diversity (CELACD) and the Rio Group, positioning CELAC as their successor to foster political, economic, and social integration independent of North American influence. The declaration emphasized consensus-based decision-making, regional autonomy, and cooperation on issues like poverty reduction and sustainable development, explicitly excluding the United States and Canada to create a unified hemispheric forum distinct from the Organization of American States (OAS).
Following the founding, initial consolidation occurred in early 2012 under Chile's pro tempore presidency, which assumed leadership immediately after the Caracas summit and focused on operationalizing the new body's structure. On January 9, 2012, foreign ministers from the CELAC troikacomprising Chile, Venezuela, and Cubamet in Santiago to designate national coordinators for priority areas such as political dialogue, social cohesion, and external relations, laying groundwork for future summits and thematic forums. This meeting addressed mandates from the Caracas Declaration, including coordination of regional synergies and preparation for international engagements, reflecting efforts to institutionalize CELAC amid diverse ideological alignments among members. Chile's role during 2012 involved launching working groups and advancing the implementation of integration agendas, though challenges persisted due to the forum's loose, non-binding framework.
By mid-2012, CELAC had begun external outreach, exemplified by the troika's meeting with India's foreign minister on August 7 to explore cooperation, signaling the group's intent to diversify partnerships beyond traditional Western ties. These steps consolidated CELAC's foundational principles of sovereignty and multilateralism, with 33 members representing over 600 million people, though substantive achievements remained limited to dialogue mechanisms rather than enforceable treaties. The period underscored the initiative's origins in countering perceived OAS dominance, driven by progressive governments, yet it faced skepticism regarding long-term efficacy given varying national priorities.

Evolution Through Summits (2013Present)

The second CELAC Summit of Heads of State and Government, held in Santiago, Chile, on January 2728, 2013, under Chile's pro tempore presidency, emphasized deepening regional political dialogue and integration while excluding external powers from hemispheric affairs. This gathering preceded and informed the inaugural EU-CELAC Summit on January 2627, 2013, in the same city, where leaders issued the Santiago Declaration committing to bi-regional cooperation on trade, sustainable development, and democratic governance.
The third summit convened in Havana, Cuba, on January 2829, 2014, producing the Havana Declaration, which reaffirmed CELAC's foundational principles from the Rio Group and CALC, and declared Latin America and the Caribbean a "Zone of Peace" dedicated to non-intervention, nuclear disarmament, and peaceful dispute resolution. Leaders also endorsed special declarations on issues like the Malvinas Islands sovereignty and eradication of fascism, underscoring the bloc's focus on historical grievances and multilateralism.
Subsequent meetings advanced sectoral priorities: the 2015 summit in San Jos, Costa Rica, formalized the CELAC Food Security, Nutrition, and Eradication of Hunger Plan, aiming to coordinate agricultural policies and reduce dependency on external aid. The 2016 summit in Quito, Ecuador, prioritized science, technology, and innovation as drivers of integration, while the 2017 summit in Punta Cana, Dominican Republic, addressed education and youth employment. These early post-founding gatherings institutionalized rotating pro tempore presidencies and foreign ministers' meetings, though progress remained declarative due to consensus-based decision-making.
From 2018 to 2020, CELAC experienced a hiatus in heads-of-state summits amid internal divisions, including Brazil's withdrawal under President Jair Bolsonaro, who viewed the organization as ideologically misaligned, and lack of consensus over Venezuela's political and humanitarian crisis, which fractured unity between leftist and center-right governments. 
Reactivation occurred under Mexico's 20202021 pro tempore presidency, culminating in the sixth summit in Mexico City on September 18, 2021, which approved revised statutes to streamline operations and refocused on post-COVID-19 recovery, digital transformation, and enhanced ties with partners like China via the China-CELAC Forum.
The seventh summit, hosted by Argentina in Buenos Aires on January 24, 2023, marked Brazil's return under President Luiz Incio Lula da Silva, bolstering attendance and yielding declarations on strengthening multilateral institutions, rejecting unilateral sanctions, and promoting energy transition and gender equality in line with regional consensus.
Under Honduras's 2024 pro tempore presidency, the ninth summit assembled 33 member representatives in Tegucigalpa on April 8, 2025, addressing migration, climate resilience, and economic cooperation despite ongoing ideological tensions, with outcomes emphasizing CELAC's role as a platform for South-South dialogue independent of U.S.-led initiatives. Overall, these summits have evolved CELAC from a nascent forum into a persistent, if intermittent, mechanism for political coordination, prioritizing autonomy and issue-specific agendas over deep institutional integration.

Objectives and Principles

Stated Aims of Integration and Autonomy

The Community of Latin American and Caribbean States (CELAC) was founded on December 3, 2011, through the Caracas Declaration, which articulates its core objectives as fostering deeper political, economic, social, and cultural integration across the region to advance social welfare, improve quality of life, and achieve sustainable development with greater equity. This declaration positions CELAC as the primary forum for channeling inter-regional relations, emphasizing unity in diversity while renewing mechanisms for dialogue and political concertation inherited from predecessor groups like the Rio Group and the Summit of the Americas. The subsequent Procedures for the Organic Functioning of CELAC, adopted in 2012, further specify commitments to building a common space that strengthens cooperation, complementarity, and development initiatives among the 33 member states.
Integration efforts under CELAC prioritize consensus-based decision-making to harmonize national policies on key issues, including poverty eradication, infrastructure connectivity, and trade facilitation, without supranational authority that could infringe on state sovereignty. For instance, the organization promotes joint programs in areas like food security, energy cooperation, and disaster risk reduction, as outlined in summit declarations such as the Havana Declaration of January 2014, which reaffirms national plans for growth and social progress aligned with regional goals. These aims extend to cultural exchanges and educational initiatives to reinforce shared Latin American and Caribbean identity, while economic integration focuses on reducing dependencies through intra-regional partnerships rather than external alignments.
Autonomy features prominently in CELAC's principles, rooted in the sovereign equality of states, non-intervention in internal affairs, and rejection of threats or use of force, as enshrined in the UN Charter and regional precedents. By deliberately excluding the United States and Canadaunlike the Organization of American StatesCELAC aims to enable independent regional positioning on global matters, serving as a unified voice for Latin America and the Caribbean in international forums to safeguard self-determination and counterbalance external influences. This structure underscores a commitment to multilateralism on the region's terms, with rotating pro tempore presidencies ensuring no single state dominates, thereby preserving collective autonomy in pursuing development agendas free from hemispheric asymmetries.

Underlying Geopolitical Motivations

The establishment of the Community of Latin American and Caribbean States (CELAC) in 2011 stemmed from a concerted effort by regional leaders, particularly Venezuelan President Hugo Chvez and Brazilian President Luiz Incio Lula da Silva, to forge a bloc excluding the United States and Canada, thereby circumventing the perceived dominance of the Organization of American States (OAS). This initiative merged prior mechanisms like the Rio Group and the Summit of Latin America and the Caribbean on Integration and Development (CALC), formalized at the inaugural summit in Caracas on December 23, 2011, with 33 member states participating. The exclusion of North American powers was explicitly designed to enable autonomous political dialogue and coordination, free from what proponents viewed as Washington's veto power in hemispheric affairs, as evidenced by OAS resolutions often aligning with U.S. foreign policy priorities.
At its core, CELAC's geopolitical rationale reflected a push for multipolar regionalism, allowing Latin American and Caribbean nations to assert sovereignty against historical U.S. interventions, such as the 2009 Honduran constitutional crisis where OAS suspension was criticized as ineffective by Chvez and allies. Chvez framed the bloc as the culmination of Simn Bolvar's 19th-century vision for a united, independent Latin America, declaring it an "achievement 200 years in the making" during the founding summit. This motivation was intertwined with ideological alignment among left-leaning governments, enabling collective resistance to U.S. sanctions on members like Cuba and Venezuela, while opening avenues for economic ties with non-Western powers, including China, whose loans and investments surged in the region post-2011.
Critics, including U.S. officials, have characterized CELAC as a vehicle for anti-American coordination, particularly under Venezuelan and Cuban leadership in its early years, which prioritized shielding authoritarian-leaning regimes from democratic accountability mechanisms present in the OAS. Empirical data supports this dynamic: CELAC summits have issued declarations opposing U.S. policies, such as the 2019 Lima Group on Venezuela, without enforcing internal consensus on human rights or electoral integrity. Nonetheless, the bloc's structure inherently favors causal independence from hemispheric power imbalances, prioritizing intra-regional tradereaching $1.2 trillion annually by 2020and diplomatic leverage in global forums like the UN, though implementation has been hampered by ideological divergences among members.

Organizational Structure

Forums and Decision-Making Bodies

The Community of Latin American and Caribbean States (CELAC) lacks a permanent secretariat or robust institutional framework, with decision-making centered on consensus among member states rather than formal voting mechanisms. This approach, established at the 2011 Caracas Summit of Heads of State and Government, requires the absence of objection for agreements, enabling flexibility but often resulting in stalled progress on divisive issues due to the need for unanimity. The summits and preparatory meetings serve as the core forums, prioritizing dialogue over binding enforcement, which reflects CELAC's design as a loose political concertation mechanism rather than a supranational entity.
The Summit of Heads of State and Government constitutes the highest decision-making body, convening irregularlytypically every one to two yearsto adopt declarations, action plans, and foundational rules. For example, the inaugural summit in Caracas on December 23, 2011, formalized CELAC's consensus-based governance and integration principles, uniting 33 nations without external powers like the United States or Canada. Subsequent summits, such as the seventh in Buenos Aires on August 9, 2023, have addressed regional priorities like food security and multilateralism, though outcomes remain non-binding and dependent on voluntary implementation. These gatherings enforce prior decisions informally through reaffirmed commitments, underscoring the forum's role in symbolic unity over operational depth.
Ministerial Meetings of Foreign Affairs provide intermediate-level coordination, preparing agendas for heads-of-state summits and advancing political consensus on foreign policy. Held periodically, these sessionssuch as the second meeting in Havana in 2014focus on thematic dialogues, including South-South cooperation and responses to global challenges, representing the forum's executive authority short of summit-level approval. They facilitate preparatory work but operate within the consensus constraint, limiting resolutions to areas of broad agreement.
Meetings of National Coordinators form the operational backbone, convening frequently (often twice annually or more) to harmonize positions, draft documents, and delegate national-level consensus ahead of higher forums. Numbered sequentially, these sessionsreaching the 50th meeting hosted by Saint Vincent and the Grenadines on February 1415, 2024, and the 31st chaired by Mexico in 2019handle technical preparations and enforce summit mandates through ongoing dialogue. For instance, the February 2025 meeting in Honduras addressed preparatory coordination for upcoming summits, illustrating their role in sustaining momentum despite the absence of permanent staff. This tier ensures continuity but highlights vulnerabilities to shifts in pro tempore leadership, as coordinators' effectiveness ties closely to the rotating host's diplomatic capacity.

Leadership and Rotating Presidency

The pro tempore presidency constitutes the principal leadership mechanism of the Community of Latin American and Caribbean States (CELAC), rotating annually among its 33 member states to ensure equitable representation and prevent dominance by any single nation. This position, held by the head of state or government of the presiding country, carries responsibilities including representing CELAC in international forums, coordinating preparatory meetings, and presiding over the biennial summit of heads of state and government, where key declarations and agendas are adopted by consensus. The term begins and ends at these summits, typically held every two years, with the presidency facilitating interim ministerial consultations to advance regional dialogue on issues such as economic integration and sustainable development.
The rotation lacks codified rules in CELAC's foundational Montevideo Consensus of 2010, instead relying on negotiated agreements among members at summits, which has led to variability in sequencing without adherence to strict alphabetical, subregional, or tenure-based criteria. This flexibility underscores CELAC's minimalist institutional design, prioritizing political coordination over permanent bureaucracy, though it can result in agenda shifts aligned with the presiding state's prioritiessuch as Cuba's emphasis on anti-imperialist rhetoric during its 2013 term or Argentina's focus on multilateral trade in 2022. To promote continuity, the Troikacomprising the current pro tempore president, the immediate past president, and the incoming presidentcollaborates on ongoing initiatives and crisis response, as evidenced in joint statements addressing regional challenges like the COVID-19 pandemic.
As of October 2025, Colombia holds the pro tempore presidency under President Gustavo Petro, who assumed the role following the handover at the VII CELAC Summit in Buenos Aires in March 2023 from Saint Vincent and the Grenadines; Petro's tenure includes hosting the IV EU-CELAC Summit in Santa Marta on November 910, 2025, to advance strategic partnerships on trade, climate, and migration. Prior holders include Argentina (2022, under President Alberto Fernndez), which prioritized South-South cooperation, and Honduras (projected successor post-2025, though subject to summit confirmation). The presidency's effectiveness depends on the host's diplomatic capacity and domestic stability, with smaller states like Saint Vincent demonstrating influence through focused agendas on small island vulnerabilities despite limited resources.

Membership

Composition of Member States

The Community of Latin American and Caribbean States (CELAC) comprises 33 sovereign member states, representing all independent nations across Latin America and the Caribbean, deliberately excluding the United States and Canada to foster regional autonomy from North American influence. This composition was established at CELAC's founding in 2011, merging prior initiatives like the Rio Group and the Summit of Latin America and the Caribbean on Integration and Development (CALC), and has remained stable without withdrawals or additions as of 2025.
Membership spans South America (12 countries), Central America and Mexico (8 countries), and the Caribbean (13 countries), covering a combined population of approximately 658 million people and a land area of 20.41 million square kilometers. The full list of members is as follows:

Inclusion Criteria and Potential Exclusions

Membership in the Community of Latin American and Caribbean States (CELAC) is limited to the 33 sovereign, independent nations geographically situated in Latin America and the Caribbean, encompassing countries from Mexico southward through Central and South America, as well as independent island states in the Caribbean Sea and adjacent waters. This criterion was established at CELAC's founding via the Declaration of Caracas on December 3, 2011, which united prior mechanisms like the Rio Group and the Latin American and Caribbean Community on Security and Development (CALC) to include all regional sovereign states without additional ideological, economic, or governance prerequisites.
Non-sovereign territories and dependencies are excluded, such as Puerto Rico (a U.S. commonwealth), the U.S. Virgin Islands, French Guiana, Martinique, and Guadeloupe (French overseas departments), as well as the Falkland Islands (under British administration, though sovereignty is disputed by Argentina). Similarly, the United States and Canada are deliberately omitted due to their North American location and historical roles as external powers, reflecting CELAC's aim for intra-regional autonomy separate from hemispheric forums like the Organization of American States (OAS). Cuba's inclusion, despite its 1962 withdrawal from the OAS, underscores the absence of democratic conditionality, prioritizing geographic completeness over political alignment.
No formal process for admitting new members exists beyond this geographic-sovereignty threshold, as the bloc was designed as exhaustive from inception, with all eligible states participating since 2011; potential future inclusions would require consensus but have not arisen, given the fixed roster of independent nations in the defined area. Exclusions remain structural rather than punitive, with no recorded suspensions or expulsions, though internal divergences on issues like Venezuela's governance have tested unity without altering membership.

Key Activities and Summits

Structure of Summits and Ministerial Meetings

The structure of CELAC summits follows a hierarchical preparatory process coordinated by the pro tempore presidency, which holds office for one year and assumes responsibility for convening and hosting events. Preparations typically commence with meetings of national coordinators, appointed by member states to manage technical dialogues, agenda formulation, and consensus-building on draft documents; these sessions occur multiple times annually, often twice before key gatherings, to align positions at the working level.
Subsequent to coordinators' deliberations, foreign ministers convene in a dedicated meeting to review and refine proposed texts, negotiate political priorities, and advance substantive outcomes, as observed in preparations for the 9th CELAC Summit where this step preceded the heads-of-state assembly. The culminating event is the summit of heads of state and government, held irregularly but aiming for biennial cadencesuch as the 9th Summit in Honduras on April 89, 2025where final declarations are adopted by consensus, focusing on regional integration, development, and external relations without enforceable mechanisms.
Beyond foreign affairs, CELAC convenes specialized ministerial meetings on thematic issues to foster cooperation in sectors like environment, science, and trade; for instance, the Ministerial Meeting on Environment and Climate Change occurred on May 5, 2023, in San Jose, Costa Rica, addressing sustainable development priorities among environment ministers. These forums operate under the pro tempore presidency's guidance, emphasizing dialogue over supranational authority, with outcomes feeding into summit agendas or standalone action plans. All proceedings prioritize consensus decision-making, reflecting CELAC's non-binding, consultative framework.

Notable Outcomes and Declarations

The Second Summit of Heads of State and Government of CELAC, held in Havana, Cuba, on January 2829, 2014, produced the Havana Declaration, which proclaimed Latin America and the Caribbean as a Zone of Peace, pledging members to resolve disputes exclusively through peaceful means, reject the threat or use of force, and promote nuclear disarmament while condemning foreign military interventions. This declaration also committed to advancing regional integration, eradicating poverty and inequality, and strengthening multilateralism through bodies like the United Nations.
The Seventh Summit, convened in Buenos Aires, Argentina, on January 24, 2023, yielded the 111-point Buenos Aires Declaration, which reaffirmed CELAC's dedication to political, economic, social, and cultural unity among member states, emphasizing self-determination, non-intervention, and collective responses to global issues such as climate change, food security, and multilateral reform. It highlighted the need for increased international financing for sustainable development and regional infrastructure, while underscoring cooperation in health, education, and disaster risk management. A separate special declaration from the same summit condemned terrorism in all forms and pledged enhanced collaboration against transnational threats.
Joint declarations from CELAC's interactions with external partners have also marked key outcomes, such as the 2023 EU-CELAC Summit declaration, which outlined cooperation on multilateralism, global peace and security (including condemnation of Russia's invasion of Ukraine), climate action, trade, digital transition, and sustainable development, while pledging 45 billion in EU investments for the region over the following years. In April 2025, a high-level CELAC meeting in Tegucigalpa, Honduras, adopted the Declaration of Tegucigalpa, urging greater unity amid geopolitical tensions, including opposition to unilateral sanctions and promotion of equitable global governance.
These declarations have consistently prioritized regional autonomy and South-South cooperation, though implementation has varied due to differing national priorities among members.

External Relations

Interactions with Non-Regional Powers

CELAC maintains a strategic partnership with the European Union, formalized through periodic summits that facilitate dialogue on shared interests including trade, investment, and sustainable development. The inaugural EU-CELAC summit convened in Santiago, Chile, on January 26-27, 2013, establishing bi-regional cooperation mechanisms. Subsequent gatherings, such as the third summit in Brussels, Belgium, on July 17, 2023, emphasized revitalizing ties amid global challenges, with pledges for increased EU investment in Latin America and the Caribbean to counterbalance influences from China and Russia. The partners adopted a 2023-2025 roadmap prioritizing economic integration, green transition, and multilateralism, with the fourth summit scheduled for Santa Marta, Colombia, on November 9-10, 2025.
CELAC's engagement with China centers on the China-CELAC Forum, launched in January 2015 as a platform for multilateral cooperation in economic, infrastructural, and technological domains. This mechanism has facilitated ministerial meetings, including the fourth on May 13, 2025, in Beijing, where participants endorsed a 2025-2027 joint action plan focusing on solidarity, development, and innovation, encompassing areas like green energy and space cooperation. China's investments through this forum have supported infrastructure projects across member states, with trade volumes between China and CELAC countries reaching substantial levels by 2024, though coordination remains challenged by diverse national priorities.
Interactions with the United States lack a dedicated CELAC-wide forum, reflecting the organization's origins as a hemisphere-exclusive body designed to promote regional autonomy outside U.S.-led structures like the Organization of American States. Relations occur primarily through bilateral channels or ad hoc diplomatic engagements rather than collective mechanisms, underscoring CELAC's emphasis on independence from North American influence.
Russia's outreach to CELAC manifests indirectly via bilateral partnerships with individual members, particularly in energy and defense sectors, without a formalized group-level dialogue comparable to those with the EU or China. Such engagements have raised concerns in Western analyses regarding potential alignment with authoritarian influences, though CELAC has not pursued a unified institutional response.

Tensions with the Organization of American States

The Community of Latin American and Caribbean States (CELAC) was established in 2011 as a regional forum excluding the United States and Canada, which has fostered perceptions of it as a counterweight to the Organization of American States (OAS), an organization including those North American powers and often viewed by CELAC proponents as overly influenced by U.S. interests. This structural difference has led to ideological clashes, with CELAC emphasizing principles of sovereignty and non-intervention, contrasting the OAS's charter commitments to democracy, human rights, and collective security, which have resulted in OAS resolutions criticizing electoral irregularities and authoritarian practices in member states like Venezuela and Nicaragua.
Tensions escalated notably during the September 2021 CELAC summit hosted by Mexico, where President Andrs Manuel Lpez Obrador declared the OAS "obsolete" and proposed transforming CELAC into a replacement mechanism, arguing it better respected national sovereignty amid criticisms of OAS interventions in domestic politics. This proposal aligned with long-standing grievances from left-leaning governments against OAS Secretary General Luis Almagro's re-election and his office's reports on democratic backsliding, such as the 2017 Venezuelan constituent assembly and Nicaraguan crackdowns on opposition, which CELAC summits have largely sidestepped to prioritize unity. However, the initiative faced resistance from CELAC members like Uruguay and Chile, who affirmed the OAS's role in hemispheric dialogue, underscoring internal divisions that prevented any formal replacement.
Further strains emerged in responses to regional crises, where CELAC's consensus-based avoidance of condemnationevident in its 2023 Buenos Aires summit declarations omitting direct rebuke of Venezuelan President Nicols Maduro's disputed 2018 electioncontrasted OAS efforts like the 2019 invocation of the Democratic Charter against Venezuela, leading to accusations that CELAC provides diplomatic cover for authoritarian alignments. These divergences reflect causal tensions rooted in CELAC's design to insulate Latin American and Caribbean states from external pressures, often at the expense of uniform democratic standards enforced by the OAS, though CELAC leaders deny intent to supplant it entirely.

Achievements and Initiatives

Areas of Successful Cooperation

The CELAC Plan for Food Security, Nutrition, and the Eradication of Hunger, initially adopted in 2015 and extended toward 2030, represents a key area of cooperation, emphasizing regional coordination on sustainable agriculture with support from the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO). Implementation in member states has yielded measurable results, such as in the Dominican Republic where, starting December 2018, pilot initiatives under the plan achieved a 117% increase in milk production over two years alongside a 19% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions through improved pasture management and carbon sequestration. In Uruguay's Livestock and Climate Project (2020-2021), 61 farms covering 35,000 hectares saw beef productivity rise by 6%, production costs drop by 6% per hectare, and emissions per unit fall by 27%. Ecuador's Climate-Smart Livestock Project (2016-2020) benefited over 40,000 hectares on 165 farms, boosting farmer incomes by 15%, productivity by 12%, and reducing milk emission intensity by 43% compared to national averages.
Health coordination emerged as another success during the COVID-19 crisis, with CELAC's virtual ministerial meetings commencing March 26, 2020, establishing a Network of Experts that convened multiple times (January 30, February 19, and April 16, 2020) to exchange research and response strategies. This facilitated Mexico and Argentina's coordination, via the Carlos Slim Foundation, for AstraZeneca vaccine production accessible to CELAC members, culminating in a UN General Assembly resolution on April 21, 2020, backed by 179 sponsors to ensure equitable global access to medical supplies and equipment. Complementary efforts included April 2020 agriculture ministerial meetings to safeguard food production and distribution amid disruptions, supporting vulnerable populations estimated at 230.9 million at risk of poverty.
In education (SDG 4), CELAC's July 5, 2020, university-ministerial meeting advanced distance learning frameworks to address pandemic-related disruptions, enabling continuity for approximately 95% of primary school-aged children across the region through shared technological and policy solutions. These initiatives, while reliant on national execution, demonstrate CELAC's role in fostering multilateral dialogue and alignment on Sustainable Development Goals, though outcomes remain uneven due to varying member capacities.

Specific Policy and Development Projects

CELAC has initiated or facilitated several development projects primarily through external partnerships, focusing on science, technology, infrastructure, health, and rural economies, though implementation often relies on member states or collaborators rather than a centralized CELAC mechanism. One prominent example is the Building Europe Link to Latin America (BELLA) project, launched under the EU-CELAC science and technology roadmap, which established a transatlantic optic fiber cable to enhance high-speed internet connectivity between Europe and Latin American research institutions, operationalized by 2018 to support data-intensive scientific collaboration.
In energy and resource efficiency, the EU-CELAC funded the COOSW project, a transnational initiative developing low-cost devices to reduce energy and water consumption in coastal facilities across participating CELAC countries, aiming to promote sustainable development in small-scale operations. Complementing this, the EU-CELAC Working Group on Innovation has driven joint calls for research, including the 2025 STI call, to bolster bi-regional innovation capacities in areas like digital transformation and green technologies.
With China, CELAC endorsed a 2025 Joint Action Plan for Cooperation in Key Areas (2025-2027), encompassing infrastructure projects, trade enhancement, and green energy initiatives, such as potential solar and wind collaborations highlighted in forums like the Fourth China-CELAC Ministerial Meeting, where $9 billion in credit lines were pledged for development financing. This includes commitments for 3,500 government scholarships and 10,000 training spots in China for CELAC members through 2027, targeting capacity-building in technical fields.
In health policy, CELAC supported a 2021 CEPAL-proposed plan for regional self-sufficiency, outlining actions like joint procurement of medical supplies and technology transfer to mitigate dependencies exposed by the COVID-19 pandemic, though progress has varied by country. Rural development efforts include FAO-CELAC collaborations, such as a 2024 exchange mission to China for adopting digital tools in agriculture, aimed at eradicating rural hunger and fostering economic opportunities in underdeveloped areas. Additionally, the CELAC 2020 Planning Agenda sought to integrate subregional mechanisms for economic and social development, emphasizing coordinated policies on poverty reduction and integration. These projects underscore CELAC's role in facilitating dialogue and frameworks, with tangible outcomes often materializing via bilateral or multilateral funding rather than autonomous execution.

Criticisms and Challenges

Institutional Limitations and Lack of Enforcement

The Community of Latin American and Caribbean States (CELAC) possesses no permanent secretariat, headquarters, or supranational institutions, relying instead on a rotating pro tempore presidency supported by a troika comprising the current, previous, and incoming presidents to coordinate activities. This lightweight, forum-like structure, established without a binding treaty or charter, prioritizes dialogue over operational capacity, resulting in inconsistent follow-through on agendas due to annual leadership turnover and dependence on national foreign ministries for administrative support.
Decision-making within CELAC occurs exclusively by consensus among its 33 member states, yielding non-binding declarations, plans, and recommendations rather than enforceable agreements or legal obligations. For example, the CELAC Plan for Food Security, Nutrition, and Eradication of Hunger outlines national guidelines but explicitly states that its provisions are non-binding, leaving implementation to voluntary state action without oversight or compulsion. Similarly, summit outcomes, such as the 2014 Havana Declaration's commitments to regional integration, lack mechanisms to verify or mandate compliance, allowing divergent national priorities to undermine collective goals.
The absence of enforcement toolssuch as sanctions, arbitration bodies, or conditionality clausesrenders CELAC ineffective in addressing non-compliance, particularly in politically sensitive areas like democratic backsliding or human rights violations. Critics note that this institutional fragility, compounded by the inclusion of states with varying commitments to rule-of-law principles, has led to symbolic gestures without tangible enforcement, as seen in repeated failures to resolve intra-regional disputes or hold members accountable to shared declarations on peace and sovereignty. Without dedicated funding instruments or technical agencies to monitor adherence, CELAC's outputs often remain aspirational, limiting its role to convening rather than constraining state behavior.

Ideological Divisions and Authoritarian Alignments

The Community of Latin American and Caribbean States (CELAC) encompasses a diverse ideological spectrum, ranging from market-oriented democracies to socialist governments with authoritarian tendencies, which has frequently impeded consensus on core issues like democracy and human rights. Founding members included left-leaning leaders such as Venezuela's Hugo Chvez and Brazil's Luiz Incio Lula da Silva, who emphasized regional autonomy from U.S. influence, but subsequent shifts in national leadershipsuch as right-wing administrations in Brazil under Jair Bolsonaro (20192023), Argentina under Mauricio Macri (20152019), and Colombia under Ivn Duque (20182022)exposed fractures, with these governments often abstaining from or criticizing CELAC initiatives perceived as overly accommodating to leftist agendas.
These divisions manifest in summit outcomes and voting patterns, where ideological alignment determines participation and declarations. For instance, at the 2022 Buenos Aires summit, broad goals on integration were adopted, but underlying splits prevented deeper commitments, reflecting persistent left-right tensions. Right-leaning states have boycotted or withdrawn, as Brazil did in January 2020, with Foreign Minister Ernesto Arajo stating that CELAC had devolved into a "stage" for authoritarian states like Venezuela, Cuba, and Nicaragua, yielding no substantive results. Similarly, in April 2025, a minority of members aligned with U.S. positions on geopolitical issues, fracturing purported unity and highlighting how ideological minorities can derail collective action.
CELAC's structure, requiring consensus without binding enforcement, has enabled alignments with authoritarian regimes, particularly Cuba, Venezuela, and Nicaragua, by shielding them from regional condemnation. Critics argue this provides diplomatic cover, as seen in CELAC's reluctance to address Venezuela's 2019 political crisis or Nicaragua's 20182023 crackdowns, with left-leaning majorities blocking human rights-focused resolutions. Cuba assumed the pro tempore presidency in 2013, prioritizing implementation of declarations that emphasized "integration in diversity" over democratic clauses, which were adopted in 2011 but rarely enforced. In 2023, Cuba, Nicaragua, Bolivia, and El Salvador abstained from a declaration upholding democratic values, underscoring how authoritarian-aligned states leverage the forum to counter external pressures, such as OAS scrutiny, while right-leaning members like those in the Dominican Republic attempted exclusions in 2025 summits but faced resistance.
This pattern stems from CELAC's origins as an alternative to U.S.-led bodies, fostering non-interventionist norms that prioritize sovereignty over accountability, often at the expense of empirical standards for governance. Authoritarian regimes exploit this, as evidenced by Venezuela's Nicols Maduro invoking CELAC in 2025 for sovereignty conferences amid domestic unrest, without member challenges to his legitimacy despite documented electoral irregularities. Such alignments have drawn bipartisan criticism, including from EU-CELAC dialogues in 2023, where LAC divisions on human rights were evident, reinforcing perceptions that CELAC amplifies authoritarian resilience rather than regional democratic convergence.

Failures in Regional Crisis Response

CELAC's consensus-based decision-making and absence of enforcement mechanisms have hindered its ability to address acute regional crises, particularly those involving human rights violations or political instability in member states. Unlike the Organization of American States, which can invoke democratic charters, CELAC lacks binding provisions to suspend or sanction members, allowing ideological solidarityoften among left-leaning governmentsto override collective action against authoritarian practices. This structural weakness has been evident in responses to Venezuela's protracted collapse, Nicaragua's 2018 crackdown, and Haiti's ongoing security breakdown, where declarations of dialogue rarely translate to tangible intervention.
In Venezuela, the deepening humanitarian and political crisismarked by hyperinflation exceeding 1 million percent in 2018, widespread food shortages, and over 7 million citizens fleeing by 2024exposed CELAC's ineffectiveness. Despite calls for mediation under Mexico's 2020 pro tempore presidency, the bloc avoided condemning Nicols Maduro's regime or supporting democratic transitions, such as Juan Guaid's 2019 interim presidency claim recognized by multiple nations. Instead, CELAC prioritized non-interference, providing a de facto diplomatic shield that critics attribute to alignment with Maduro's allies, preventing unified pressure for elections or aid corridors.
Nicaragua's 2018 protests against social security reforms triggered a brutal government response, resulting in at least 325 deaths, thousands injured, and mass arbitrary detentions, as documented by human rights monitors. CELAC issued no substantive condemnation or fact-finding mission, contrasting with the Inter-American Commission's on-site visit and reports of crimes against humanity. Even in 2023, amid ongoing repressionincluding the stripping of nationality from 317 criticsCELAC's summits featured isolated calls for prisoner releases but no collective mechanism to enforce accountability, underscoring the bloc's tolerance for member-state abuses when ideological affinities prevail.
Haiti's spiraling violence, with armed groups controlling over 80 percent of Port-au-Prince by 2024 and displacing 700,000 internally, further highlighted CELAC's coordination failures. Despite emergency meetings, the bloc produced no joint humanitarian plan or migrant support framework, neglecting vulnerable flows through the Darin Gapover 200,000 Haitians in 2023 aloneand relying on ad hoc national responses rather than regional solidarity. This inaction, amid a collapsed state and unmet UN appeals for $674 million in aid, reflects CELAC's prioritization of summit declarations over practical assistance, exacerbating refugee vulnerabilities without addressing root governance breakdowns.

Impact and Effectiveness

Economic and Political Outcomes

The Community of Latin American and Caribbean States (CELAC) has produced limited measurable economic outcomes since its inception in 2011, primarily due to its status as a non-binding political forum without supranational institutions or enforceable trade agreements. Intra-regional trade in Latin America and the Caribbean remains low at approximately 19.2% of total exports, with projections indicating a decline to 13% in 2024 amid stagnant growth and persistent barriers such as high tariffs and policy fragmentation. Regional GDP growth has averaged below 2% annually in recent years, with no attributable acceleration linked to CELAC initiatives, contrasting with more integrated blocs like the European Union where intra-bloc trade exceeds 50%. Efforts such as the CELAC Plan for Food Security, Nutrition, and the Eradication of Hunger focus on supply chain improvements but lack implementation data demonstrating poverty reduction or productivity gains beyond national-level programs.
CELAC's engagements with external partners, including the European Union and China, have yielded symbolic declarations rather than transformative economic results. The 2023 EU-CELAC Summit launched the Global Gateway Investment Agenda, promising infrastructure funding, yet follow-through has been hampered by mismatched priorities and limited commitments, with EU-LAC trade flows showing no significant uptick post-summit. Similarly, China-CELAC forums have facilitated credit lines totaling billions, but these primarily support commodity exports and debt accumulation without fostering diversified regional value chains or reducing dependency on external markets. Overall, CELAC's economic impact remains marginal, as ideological variances and consensus-based decision-making preclude the deep integration needed for sustained growth in a region marked by inequality where the top 10% earn 12 times the bottom 10%.
Politically, CELAC has enabled a platform for collective bargaining with global powers, asserting regional autonomy outside the Organization of American States framework, but its effectiveness is constrained by internal divisions and absence of enforcement. It has hosted summits condemning perceived U.S. interventions, such as in Venezuela, yet failed to produce unified resolutions, with member states splitting along left-right linesleft-leaning governments defending the Maduro regime while others abstained or opposed. The 2023 Buenos Aires Summit urged dialogue in Venezuela but yielded no verifiable progress toward free elections, as the crisis persisted with over 270 political prisoners and mass humanitarian needs unmet.
CELAC's political outcomes include bolstering multipolar diplomacy, as seen in coordinated outreach to China for political party exchanges and infrastructure loans, countering traditional hemispheric alignments. However, lacking treaty-based authority, it has not resolved core challenges like corruption or democratic backsliding, with assessments noting ineffective problem-solving and stalled convergence on global positions. Recent pro-integration rhetoric under rotating presidencies, such as Colombia's 2025 push, highlights aspirational goals but underscores ongoing hurdles from authoritarian alignments and governance variances, rendering CELAC more a declarative body than a decisive political force.

Comparative Evaluation Against Other Integration Efforts

The Community of Latin American and Caribbean States (CELAC), established on December 3, 2011, in Caracas, Venezuela, distinguishes itself from the Organization of American States (OAS) by excluding the United States and Canada, seeking to promote regional autonomy and dialogue among its 33 member states without perceived Northern Hemisphere dominance. In contrast, the OAS, founded in 1948 with 35 members across the Americas, maintains a more robust institutional framework, including a permanent secretary general and the Inter-American Democratic Charter adopted in 2001, which authorizes collective action to restore democracy in cases of rupture. This enables the OAS to conduct election monitoring and human rights interventions, activities where CELAC lacks equivalent mechanisms, rendering it primarily a consultative forum reliant on rotating presidencies and consensus, which often stalls progress amid ideological divides.
CELAC's effectiveness is further undermined by its absence of supranational authority or enforcement tools, differing sharply from sub-regional economic integrations like MERCOSUR, launched via the 1991 Treaty of Asuncin to form a common market among Argentina, Brazil, Paraguay, and Uruguay (with Bolivia joining as a full member in 2015). While MERCOSUR has developed institutions such as a parliamentary body, its integration remains incomplete due to persistent internal barriers and protectionism, yet it has facilitated some trade growth and dispute resolution absent in CELAC's political scope. The Pacific Alliance, formed in 2011 by Chile, Colombia, Mexico, and Peru, exemplifies deeper economic commitment through tariff elimination on 92% of goods by 2017 and provisions for labor mobility, yielding intra-bloc trade increases of over 10% annually in its early yearsoutcomes CELAC has not replicated in economic domains.
Compared to the Andean Community, established in 1969 with a customs union framework among Bolivia, Colombia, Ecuador, and Peru, CELAC's pan-regional ambition amplifies membership overlap and fragmentation, diluting focus; the Andean group, despite waning influence, once achieved common external tariffs and judicial integration via the Andean Court of Justice, mechanisms CELAC forgoes. Structural causes of this disparity include external influences like China's bilateral engagementsevident in the 2015 CELAC-China Forumand regional powers' disengagement, such as Brazil's reduced funding for overlapping bodies post-2018. Consequently, CELAC functions more as a symbolic platform for summit declarations than a driver of binding cooperation, contrasting with specialized blocs' targeted advancements, though its exclusion of extra-hemispheric powers aligns with autonomy goals critiqued in OAS contexts for U.S. leverage.
