Counterexamples and Limitations

Non-Preserving Maps

A map between rings qualifies as a homomorphism only if it preserves both addition and multiplication (as well as the multiplicative identity in unital rings). Functions that satisfy one condition but fail the other illustrate common failures in this regard.

Categorical Framework

The Category of Rings

In category theory, the category of rings, commonly denoted Ring\mathbf{Ring}Ring, has as its objects all ringstypically unital rings equipped with a multiplicative identityand as its morphisms all ring homomorphisms that preserve both addition and multiplication, including the identity element. This structure ensures that Ring\mathbf{Ring}Ring satisfies the axioms of a category: the composition of two ring homomorphisms :RS\phi: R \to S:RS and :ST\psi: S \to T:ST yields another ring homomorphism :RT\psi \circ \phi: R \to T:RT, since addition and multiplication are preserved componentwise, and the identity morphism idR:RR\mathrm{id}_R: R \to RidR:RR is the identity map, which clearly preserves the ring operations.
A key feature of Ring\mathbf{Ring}Ring is the forgetful functor U:RingAbU: \mathbf{Ring} \to \mathbf{Ab}U:RingAb to the category of abelian groups, which maps each ring to its underlying additive abelian group and each ring homomorphism to the corresponding group homomorphism, discarding the multiplicative structure. This functor is faithful and highlights the additive aspect of rings, but Ring\mathbf{Ring}Ring itself is not an abelian category, as evidenced by the presence of non-split exact sequences that do not behave as in abelian categoriesfor instance, certain short exact sequences in the additive sense fail to split in the ring context due to the interaction with ideals and quotients.
Variants of Ring\mathbf{Ring}Ring address specific conventions: the category CommRing\mathbf{CommRing}CommRing (or CRing\mathbf{CRing}CRing) restricts objects to commutative rings (with unity) and morphisms to homomorphisms preserving commutativity, while Rng\mathbf{Rng}Rng considers non-unital rings (or "rngs") without requiring a multiplicative identity, allowing homomorphisms that need not preserve a unit. These variants maintain the compositional and identity properties but adapt to the absence of unity, providing a framework for studying ring structures without the unital assumption.

Special Morphisms in Ring Theory

In ring theory, an endomorphism of a ring RRR is a ring homomorphism :RR\phi: R \to R:RR. The set of all endomorphisms of RRR, denoted End(R)\operatorname{End}(R)End(R), forms a ring under pointwise addition and composition as multiplication.
An automorphism of a ring RRR is a bijective endomorphism :RR\phi: R \to R:RR whose inverse is also a ring homomorphism. The set of all automorphisms of RRR, denoted Aut(R)\operatorname{Aut}(R)Aut(R), forms a group under composition. Automorphisms preserve all ring-theoretic invariants, such as the ideal structure and characteristic of the ring.
A ring isomorphism is a bijective ring homomorphism :RS\phi: R \to S:RS whose inverse 1:SR\phi^{-1}: S \to R1:SR is also a ring homomorphism./16:_Rings/16.05:_Ring_Homomorphisms_and_Ideals) Two rings are isomorphic if there exists a ring isomorphism between them, meaning they are structurally identical up to relabeling of elements.
In the category of rings, a monomorphism is a ring homomorphism that is left-cancellative: if :RS\phi: R \to S:RS is a monomorphism and 1,2:ST\psi_1, \psi_2: S \to T1,2:ST satisfy 1=2\psi_1 \circ \phi = \psi_2 \circ \phi1=2, then 1=2\psi_1 = \psi_21=2. For ring homomorphisms, monomorphisms are precisely the injective homomorphisms.
An epimorphism in the category of rings is a ring homomorphism that is right-cancellative: if :RS\phi: R \to S:RS is an epimorphism and 1,2:TS\psi_1, \psi_2: T \to S1,2:TS satisfy 1=2\phi \circ \psi_1 = \phi \circ \psi_21=2, then 1=2\psi_1 = \psi_21=2. Unlike in the category of sets, epimorphisms of rings are not necessarily surjective; for example, the inclusion map ZQ\mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Q}ZQ is an epimorphism because any ring homomorphism from Q\mathbb{Q}Q to another ring is uniquely determined by its restriction to Z\mathbb{Z}Z, but it is not surjective. In the subcategory of commutative rings, non-surjective epimorphisms such as ZQ\mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Q}ZQ also exist, highlighting that surjectivity does not always coincide with the categorical notion of epimorphism.
