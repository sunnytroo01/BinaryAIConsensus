Here is how to speak in public without dying of fear, and how to actually be good at it.

The fear of public speaking is the most common phobia in the world. More people fear speaking in front of a group than fear death, spiders, heights, or flying. This fear is irrational in the sense that speaking in front of people poses no physical danger, but it is completely rational in evolutionary terms: being judged and potentially rejected by a group was a survival threat for our ancestors. Your brain treats public speaking as a physical danger because rejection from the group once meant death.

The cure is not to eliminate the fear. It is to learn to perform despite it. Every great speaker feels nervous before they speak. The difference between a great speaker and a terrified one is not the absence of fear but the presence of preparation.

Preparation is everything. Know your material cold. Not word for word, because memorized speeches sound robotic, but concept by concept. You should be able to give your talk without any notes, in any order, because you understand the ideas deeply enough that you do not need to remember specific sentences. You just need to remember the ideas and the words will come.

Structure your talk clearly. Tell them what you are going to tell them. Tell them. Tell them what you told them. This classic structure works because repetition aids comprehension. The opening previews the journey. The body delivers the content. The closing reinforces the key takeaways.

Start with a hook. You have about thirty seconds to capture the audience's attention. Start with a surprising fact, a provocative question, a short story, a bold claim, or a striking image. Do not start with "Today I am going to talk about..." Start with something that makes the audience lean forward.

Use stories. Human brains are wired for narrative. A well-told story is remembered ten times better than a set of bullet points. For every key point in your talk, have a story that illustrates it. The story should be specific, personal, and emotionally resonant. "Last Tuesday, I was standing in the checkout line at the grocery store when..." is more engaging than "Research shows that..."

Speak to individuals, not to the crowd. Pick one person in the audience and talk to them for a sentence or two. Then move your eyes to another person. Then another. This technique makes the audience feel personally engaged and it calms your nerves because you are having a series of one-on-one conversations rather than performing for a crowd.

Pause. Pauses are the most underused tool in public speaking. A pause after a key point lets it sink in. A pause before an important statement builds anticipation. A pause when you lose your train of thought looks deliberate and thoughtful rather than confused. The silence feels longer to you than it does to the audience. Use it.

Use your body. Stand with your feet shoulder-width apart. Keep your weight evenly distributed. Use natural hand gestures to emphasize points. Move purposefully around the stage, not pacing nervously. Make your physical presence match the energy of your words.

Use your voice. Vary your speed: slow down for important points, speed up for exciting ones. Vary your volume: get quieter for intimate moments, louder for passionate ones. Vary your pitch: monotone puts people to sleep. The most engaging speakers sound like they are having a passionate conversation, not reading a textbook.

End strong. Do not trail off or mumble "so yeah, that is basically it." Deliver your final sentence with conviction and confidence. Then pause. Then say "Thank you." The last thing you say is the first thing the audience will remember.

Practice out loud at least five times. Not in your head. Out loud, standing up, at full volume, with gestures. Time yourself. Record yourself on your phone and watch it back. The first run-through will be rough. The fifth will be smooth. The gap between those two is the difference between a mediocre talk and a great one.

The single best piece of advice for public speaking: the audience wants you to succeed. They did not come to watch you fail. They came because they are interested in your topic and they want to learn something. They are on your side. Remember that, and the fear becomes manageable.
