 The Economic Policy Institute (EPI) is a Washington, D.C.-based nonprofit think tank founded in 1986 by economists including Jeff Faux to prioritize the economic interests of low- and middle-income workers in policy debates through research and analysis. EPI's mission centers on using empirical data to address issues like wage stagnation, rising inequality, and weak worker bargaining power, often advocating for interventions such as higher minimum wages, expanded union rights, and critiques of globalization's effects on labor. While self-described as nonpartisan, EPI receives substantial funding from labor unions and progressive foundations, contributing to its left-leaning orientation and prompting accusations of selective emphasis in its institutional economics framework that favors redistributive policies over market-driven alternatives. The organization has produced influential reports on topics including the gender pay gap and automation's labor impacts, though its analyses are sometimes contested for prioritizing normative goals aligned with organized labor over unvarnished causal assessments of policy outcomes.

Founding and Early History

Establishment and Initial Objectives (1986)

The Economic Policy Institute (EPI) was established in 1986 as a nonprofit think tank in Washington, D.C., by a group of economists including Jeff Faux, who served as its founding president, Lester Thurow, Barry Bluestone, Robert Reich, Robert Kuttner, and former U.S. Labor Secretary Ray Marshall. The organization was created amid the Reagan administration's deregulation efforts, weakening of unionssuch as the 1981 firing of striking air traffic controllersand a broader shift toward free-market policies that prioritized corporate interests over labor. Founders sought to counter the dominance of right-wing think tanks like the Heritage Foundation by building an "intellectual infrastructure" for progressive economic analysis, as noted by Robert Reich in reflections on the mid-1980s liberal discourse.
EPI's formation responded to emerging economic challenges, including deindustrialization through offshoring, wage stagnation, and declining union bargaining power, which disproportionately affected working- and middle-class Americans. These trends, exacerbated by neoliberal policies emphasizing deregulation and globalization, were seen as eroding broad-based prosperity and widening inequality, prompting the institute to prioritize data-driven scrutiny of such orthodoxies. Initial funding drew from labor unions and progressive foundations, reflecting early alignments with worker advocacy groups while establishing EPI as a 501(c)(3) nonprofit claiming nonpartisan status to focus on empirical research rather than partisan advocacy.
The institute's core objectives centered on broadening economic policy debates to incorporate the perspectives and needs of low- and middle-income workers, using rigorous analysis to challenge prevailing free-market assumptions. Early efforts emphasized institutional economics approaches to highlight labor market dynamics, support for union protections, wage standards, and strategic industrial policies aimed at reversing deindustrialization's harms. By producing studies on topics like declining manufacturing jobs and productivity-wage gaps, EPI positioned itself as a counterweight to neoliberal consensus, advocating evidence-based alternatives that prioritized worker outcomes over unfettered markets.

Key Developments Through the 1990s and 2000s

In the 1990s, the Economic Policy Institute intensified its scrutiny of trade liberalization policies, particularly the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA), which it opposed from 1992 to 1994 on grounds that it would lead to significant U.S. job losses by shifting manufacturing to lower-wage Mexico. In 1991, EPI hired trade economist Thea Lee to conduct analyses supporting these critiques, including projections of displacement in sectors like autos and electronics. The institute collaborated with trinational labor coalitions to highlight wage suppression effects, drawing on empirical data from industry wage differentials and employment shifts. Concurrently, EPI expanded its research scope through ongoing iterations of the State of Working America series; the 1994 edition introduced a seminal chart illustrating the divergence between productivity growth and hourly compensation since 1979, attributing stagnation to policy failures in bargaining power rather than skill shortages. In 1995, it launched the Wheres the Money Gone? studies on state school funding disparities, and by 1997, established the Economic Analysis and Research Network (EARN) to foster state-level economic research partnerships, enhancing EPI's regional influence.
Entering the 2000s, EPI continued trade critiques, opposing Permanent Normal Trade Relations (PNTR) with China in 2000 through economist Robert Scott's models estimating over 800,000 U.S. job losses from import surges in manufacturing. A key institutional shift occurred in 2002 with the transition from founding president Jeff Faux to Lawrence Mishel, who prioritized wage dynamics and hired Josh Bivens to strengthen macroeconomic analysis. Under Mishel, EPI released the Agenda for Shared Prosperity in 2006-2007, advocating policies to reverse income polarization based on data showing median wages failing to keep pace with productivity gains of 20% from 2000 to 2007. 
The 2008 financial crisis prompted EPI to produce reports documenting weak employment recovery and persistent wage stagnation, with nonfarm payrolls growing only 6 million jobs from 2000 to 2008 compared to 23 million in the prior decade, exacerbating household income declines. EPI advocated fiscal stimulus over austerity, citing empirical evidence from the era's output gaps and arguing that underinvestment in demand-side measures prolonged the downturn. In 2008, it founded the Program on Race, Ethnicity, and the Economy (PREE) to quantify disparities, such as Black-white wage gaps widening post-recession. These efforts coincided with staff and budget growth, diversifying beyond union funding while maintaining focus on labor market data to counter narratives of inevitable globalization benefits.

Organizational Structure and Operations

Leadership and Key Personnel

The Economic Policy Institute was established in 1986 by Jeff Faux, a labor economist who served as its founding president until 2002, alongside other economists and policy experts including Barry Bluestone, Lester Thurow, Ray Marshall (former U.S. Secretary of Labor under President Carter), Robert Reich (future U.S. Secretary of Labor under President Clinton), and Robert Kuttner. These founders, many with ties to labor unions and Democratic administrations, aimed to create a research organization focused on economic policies benefiting working families, drawing from academic and policy backgrounds critical of prevailing free-market orthodoxies.
Lawrence Mishel succeeded Faux as president from 2002 to 2017, having joined EPI in 1987 as its first research director; his tenure emphasized empirical analysis of wage stagnation and labor market trends, informed by his prior work at the Joint Economic Committee of the U.S. Congress. Heidi Shierholz became EPI's president in 2021, following roles as policy director at EPI from 2017 to 2021 and chief economist at the U.S. Department of Labor from 2014 to 2017 under the Obama administration; she previously worked at EPI from 2007 to 2014, specializing in labor economics with a Ph.D. from the University of Michigan.
Josh Bivens serves as EPI's chief economist, a position he has held since joining the organization in 2002, with research expertise in macroeconomics, inequality, and public investment; his work often aligns with progressive critiques of fiscal austerity and trade policies. The leadership's affiliations with labor organizations and Democratic policy circles underscore EPI's orientation toward advocating for policies that prioritize wage growth and worker protections over unfettered globalization.

Affiliated Initiatives and Programs

The Economic Policy Institute maintains several specialized programs focused on targeted economic issues, including the Program on Race, Ethnicity, and the Economy (PREE), established in 2008 to examine intersections of race, ethnicity, class, and economic outcomes in the United States. PREE conducts analyses aimed at advancing policies for racial and ethnic equity, such as reducing wage disparities and promoting union representation among minority workers.
EPI coordinates the Economic Analysis and Research Network (EARN), a collaborative consortium of 57 organizations operating across 45 states and the District of Columbia, which supports state-level economic research and advocacy on labor standards, worker bargaining power, and equity initiatives. EARN facilitates localized policy efforts through grants, such as $400,000 allocated to five state partnerships over two years for projects enhancing economic justice at the regional level.
Supporting these efforts, EPI develops practical data tools like the Family Budget Calculator, which estimates the income required for a modest standard of living across 618 U.S. communities, factoring in costs for housing, food, transportation, child care, health care, taxes, and other necessities for various family configurations. The tool, updated as of January 2024, enables comparisons to federal poverty thresholds and informs regional adequacy assessments without prescribing policy outcomes.
EPI also engages early-career researchers through internships, providing opportunities for substantive contributions to economic analysis while building institutional capacity for ongoing projects. These positions emphasize rigorous data handling and policy-oriented tasks, aligning with EPI's broader operational arms distinct from its primary research dissemination.

Mission, Ideology, and Research Methodology

Stated Mission and Ideological Orientation

The Economic Policy Institute (EPI) describes its mission as using economic research to advance policy changes that enhance worker power, provide economic security for families, and promote racial and gender equity, with a vision of an economy where all workers share equally in national prosperity. Founded in 1986, EPI positions itself as countering forces of rising inequality, stagnant wages, and weak labor standards by prioritizing the interests of low- and middle-income working families in policy debates. It emphasizes an intersectional approach incorporating class, race, ethnicity, and gender dynamics to address these issues.
EPI claims nonpartisan status as a nonprofit think tank, yet its orientation aligns with progressive economic perspectives rooted in institutional economics, which highlight how institutional power structuresparticularly unchecked corporate influenceshape outcomes like wage suppression and inequality. This framework critiques market fundamentalism by focusing on causal relationships between bargaining power imbalances and economic disparities, rather than prioritizing efficiency through deregulation or free-market mechanisms as seen in conservative counterparts like the Heritage Foundation. Independent assessments consistently rate EPI as left-leaning due to its advocacy for redistributive policies and labor empowerment.
The institute's close affiliations with the labor movement, including 14% of funding from unions and staff unionization under the Nonprofit Professional Employees Union, underscore this orientation, distinguishing it from ideologically neutral or right-leaning analyses that emphasize individual incentives over collective bargaining. While EPI maintains it applies rigorous economic tools without partisan allegiance, its emphasis on bolstering union access and countering corporate dominance reveals priors favoring structural interventions to rebalance power, potentially at odds with empirical neutrality in evaluating market-driven growth.

Approaches to Economic Analysis and Data Selection

The Economic Policy Institute (EPI) relies on government datasets such as the Current Population Survey's Outgoing Rotation Group files for hourly wage measurements in its analyses of labor market trends, adjusting these figures for inflation via the CPI-U-RS and addressing top-coding through Pareto imputation methods to estimate higher earners' wages. This approach excludes overtime, tips, commissions, and non-wage benefits like health insurance or pensions, focusing narrowly on base wages and salaries to highlight trends in hourly pay rates. While EPI separately tracks total compensation incorporating benefits via Bureau of Labor Statistics Employer Costs for Employee Compensation data, its prominent wage stagnation narratives prioritize the wages-only series, which critics contend understates overall remuneration growthbenefits' share of private-sector compensation rose from 19.6% in 2000 to 31.6% in 2023potentially amplifying claims of disconnect from productivity advances without fully contextualizing comprehensive employee costs.
In productivity-wage divergence assessments, EPI contrasts real hourly wages (deflated by consumer prices) against net productivity (deflated by the GDP deflator), a methodological choice that widens the apparent gap since the GDP deflator typically grows slower than CPI, overstating decoupling by several percentage points over decades-long periods. EPI researchers acknowledge the deflator discrepancy but maintain its effect is minor, yet independent analyses indicate it contributes substantially to the narrative of suppressed worker gains relative to output per hour, which increased 62% from 1979 to 2023 while EPI's preferred wage measure rose only 15%. This selective indexing and exclusion of total compensation elements aligns with EPI's emphasis on policy-driven wage suppression, though it risks overlooking causal factors like benefit shifts and measurement inconsistencies in cross-series comparisons.
For executive compensation ratios, EPI derives median worker pay from CPS annual earnings and pairs it with realized CEO pay from Execucomp, yielding figures like 281-to-1 in 2024, without standardizing for firm size, industry performance, or incentive alignments tied to shareholder returns. Critics observe that such framing omits value creation metricsCEO pay correlates with total shareholder returns over time, with top executives managing enterprises generating trillions in market valueand aggregates disparate roles, potentially framing disparities normatively to advocate redistribution absent evidence of systemic misalignment from market forces.
EPI employs input-output models to quantify trade's employment effects, linking import-driven output reductions to job displacements across supply chains, as in estimates of 3.7 million U.S. jobs lost to the China trade deficit from 2001 to 2018. These static frameworks assume fixed production coefficients and trace gross impacts but underweight general equilibrium dynamics, such as resource reallocation to expanding sectors, lower consumer prices enhancing purchasing power (equivalent to a 1-2% real income boost from China trade liberalization), or offsetting export gains, resulting in net job loss figures that exceed economy-wide evidence of trade's overall positive employment contributions. Empirical studies incorporating broader adjustments find trade with China yielded net U.S. employment and wage gains, underscoring how EPI's model selectivity prioritizes visible manufacturing displacements over diffuse benefits.

Policy Positions and Proposals

Labor Market and Wage Policies

The Economic Policy Institute has advocated for raising the federal minimum wage to $15 per hour by 2025, estimating that such a policy under the Raise the Wage Act of 2021 would increase earnings for approximately 32 million low-wage workers while lifting 3.7 million out of poverty, based on their analyses of Census and labor data. EPI researchers contend that empirical evidence from prior state-level increases shows minimal disemployment effects, citing studies of U.S. minimum wage hikes over decades that find employment elasticities near zero for low-wage sectors. More recently, EPI supported escalating the target to $17 by 2030, projecting impacts on 22 million workers or 15% of the U.S. workforce, with claims of net wage gains outweighing any job reductions.
EPI has also pushed for public-sector wage reforms to address pay disparities, highlighting that in 2022, about one-third of state and local government employees earned under $20 per hour, with over 15% below $15, disproportionately affecting Black and Hispanic workers. Their proposals emphasize equity adjustments tied to private-sector benchmarks, arguing that underpayment erodes recruitment and retention in essential services like education and public safety.
On unionization, EPI opposes right-to-work (RTW) laws, asserting they undermine collective bargaining by allowing non-union workers to benefit without dues, leading to 3-5% lower wages for all workers in RTW states based on their reviews of state-level data. The institute endorses NLRB reforms, such as those under President Biden's appointees in 2024, to expedite union elections, penalize employer interference, and protect striking workers, viewing these as essential to reversing declining union density from 20% in 1983 to 10% in 2024. EPI supports expanding sector-wide bargaining models, akin to those in the PRO Act, to cover entire industries and counter fragmented employer resistance, claiming this boosts bargaining power and wage floors without broad disemployment.
However, Congressional Budget Office projections contradict EPI's minimal-disemployment claims, estimating a $15 minimum wage by 2025 would eliminate 1.4 million jobs on average, with low-wage and less-experienced workers facing the highest risks of unemployment or reduced hours, potentially pushing some family incomes below poverty thresholds. State-level empirical analyses reveal mixed outcomes: while some increases correlate with wage gains, others, such as in Seattle's $15 policy, show teen employment drops of 6-9% and hours reductions for low-skill workers, indicating trade-offs where aggregate gains mask harms to marginal employees. For RTW laws, while unionization falls 4 percentage points post-adoption per NBER estimates, overall wage effects remain debated, with some studies finding no net employment boost but reduced worker leverage in non-union settings. EPI's selective emphasis on pro-union data aligns with its labor-affiliated funding, potentially overlooking causal evidence from dynamic employment models showing minimum wages compress hiring in competitive low-skill markets.

Trade, Globalization, and Industrial Policy

The Economic Policy Institute has consistently critiqued globalization and free trade agreements for contributing to substantial manufacturing job losses in the United States, attributing these primarily to growing trade deficits rather than productivity improvements. Between 2000 and 2014, EPI estimates that trade deficits in manufactured goods displaced approximately 3.6 million jobs prior to the Great Recession, with total manufacturing employment declining from 17.3 million to 12.3 million jobs over that period. A significant portion of these losses, EPI argues, stemmed from the U.S.-China trade imbalance following China's 2001 entry into the World Trade Organization, which resulted in 3.2 million U.S. jobs displaced between 2001 and 2013, 2.4 million in manufacturing sectors such as electronics and apparel. EPI emphasizes unfair practices like China's currency manipulation and subsidies as causal factors amplifying these effects, disproportionately harming non-college-educated workers without adequate policy mitigation.
In response, EPI advocates protectionist-leaning measures including tariffs targeted at unfair trade practices, enforcement of "Buy American" procurement rules, and renegotiated trade pacts prioritizing worker protections over unfettered market access. The institute supports industrial policies such as government subsidies for reshoring supply chains, infrastructure investments, and clean energy initiatives to create high-wage manufacturing jobs, estimating that combining trade rebalancing with $250 billion annual infrastructure spending and climate-related programs could generate up to 17 million jobs. In the 2020s, EPI has intensified calls for policies countering Chinese competition through strategic industrial support, framing these as essential for national security and economic resilience rather than mere protectionism. Regarding the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA), EPI influenced the inclusion of stronger labor provisions by advocating for enforceable union rights and wage floors in Mexico, though it deems the overall deal insufficient to reverse NAFTA-era job losses.
Critics of EPI's positions contend that they underemphasize comparative advantage principles, where trade specialization yields net efficiency gains despite sectoral disruptions, and overlook empirical evidence of broader offsets such as lower consumer prices and service-sector expansion. Peer-reviewed studies, including those by David Autor, Gordon Hanson, and David Dorn, corroborate the "China shock's" realitydocumenting persistent manufacturing employment declines of about 1 million jobs by 2011 and limited worker reallocation to services due to skill mismatches and geographic frictionsbut find no aggregate evidence that protectionist remedies like tariffs fully restore lost positions without inducing retaliation or cost increases. For instance, analyses of tariff implementations show retaliatory measures raising input prices for U.S. exporters and adding $1,277 annually to average household costs, potentially exacerbating inflation without proportional job gains in protected industries. EPI's reliance on deficit-based job displacement models has faced scrutiny for conflating correlation with causation, as overall U.S. GDP and service employment grew amid trade expansion, suggesting policy failures in adjustment assistance rather than trade itself as the core issue.

Inequality and Fiscal Policy

The Economic Policy Institute (EPI) has highlighted widening income inequality through analyses of wage distribution data, noting that the top 1% of earners captured 14.6% of total U.S. wages in 2021, more than double the 7.3% share in 1979, while the bottom 90% received 58.6%. EPI attributes much of this trend to policy shifts favoring high earners and advocates for redistributive fiscal measures, including progressive taxation with higher effective rates on top incomes and opposition to cuts for high-income households, which it argues fail to deliver broad economic gains and instead deepen stagnation for lower earners. 
EPI supports expanded social spending via universal programs, such as enhancements to the Child Tax Credit, which it credits with driving child poverty down to a record low of 5.2% in 2021 from 9.7% in 2020 through the American Rescue Plan's temporary expansion. The institute also endorses taxing concentrated wealth, emphasizing policies to address excessive accumulation among the top 0.1% and rejecting claims that such levies inherently slow growth, while critiquing extensions of the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act as disproportionately benefiting the rich and corporations, thereby inflating the fiscal gap to 3.3% of GDP and necessitating cuts to programs like Medicaid. 
Countervailing evidence from longitudinal studies indicates that income inequality does not preclude intergenerational mobility; for children born in the 1980s, absolute mobility ratesdefined as exceeding parental incomestood at approximately 50%, reflecting persistent opportunities amid rising Gini coefficients around 0.43-0.44 in recent decades. Fiscal models incorporating dynamic effects, such as behavioral responses to marginal rates, suggest trade-offs from steeply progressive structures: empirical estimates show a 1% of GDP tax increase reducing real GDP by 2-3% over time due to diminished incentives for work, investment, and innovation. High marginal rates have been linked to distorted price signals and lower capital formation, potentially offsetting redistributional aims with slower overall growth, as evidenced in panel data across countries where tax hikes persistently lowered GDP per capita. 

Publications, Influence, and Empirical Impact

Major Research Outputs and Dissemination

The Economic Policy Institute (EPI) produces flagship publications such as The State of Working America, an ongoing series initiated in 1988 that analyzes trends in family incomes, wages, jobs, unemployment, wealth, and mobility using government data sources. The series includes periodic book editions, with the 12th edition published in 2012, alongside annual updates like the 2021 wage analysis during the COVID-19 pandemic. EPI also issues annual reports on CEO compensation, tracking realized pay at top U.S. firms relative to typical workers; for instance, the 2024 report documented average CEO pay at $22.98 million, equating to a 281-to-1 ratio with average worker compensation. 
EPI disseminates its research through diverse channels, including detailed reports, policy briefs, and working papers available on its website, alongside blogs and op-eds that provide timely commentary on economic data releases such as jobs reports and GDP figures. Researchers contribute congressional testimony on labor issues, including minimum wage proposals and worker protections, with examples covering state-level policies like New Jersey's ABC test for employee classification. The institute further extends reach via multimedia, such as the State of Working America podcast, which features discussions on worker voices in policy debates.
Interactive tools and data libraries form a core dissemination mechanism, enabling public access to inequality metrics, wage trends, and economic indicators; the EPI Data Library compiles historical series from government sources for researchers and media. Tools like the Family Budget Calculator allow users to estimate living costs by location and family size, supporting outreach on wage adequacy. These outputs, numbering in the dozens annually across reports and analyses, are hosted on epi.org and referenced in media coverage of labor market developments.

Documented Policy Influences and Outcomes

The Economic Policy Institute (EPI) has contributed to policy discussions on minimum wage adjustments during the Obama administration, providing analyses that supported proposed federal increases from $7.25 to $9.00 in 2013 and $10.10 by 2015. EPI economists, including Jason Furman, outlined methodologies estimating limited job losses from such hikes, influencing White House briefings and congressional testimony on the potential benefits for 28 million workers. However, these efforts coincided with stalled federal legislation, though they informed state-level actions where minimum wages rose in 18 states by 2014, yielding average wage gains of 10-15% for affected workers without uniform employment spikes.
Under the Biden administration, EPI's advocacy for worker protections in trade policy aligned with the "worker-centered" framework, emphasizing labor rights in agreements like the USMCA revisions. Former EPI president Thea Lee, appointed to a key labor role in 2021, helped shape this agenda, which incorporated EPI-researched provisions on union rights and wage standards, contributing to tariff adjustments on Chinese imports affecting 300,000 U.S. jobs in manufacturing sectors. Outcomes include stabilized import competition in steel and autos, with BLS data showing 1.2% manufacturing employment growth from 2021-2023, though broader trade deficits persisted at $1 trillion annually.
EPI research has informed state living wage ordinances, such as early analyses of Baltimore's 1994 law, which raised contractor wages to $6.10 per hour plus benefits, prompting similar policies in over 140 cities by 2006 and correlating with 5-10% poverty reductions among covered workers per local evaluations. In gig economy debates, EPI testimony and reports from 2018 onward argued against independent contractor status for platforms like Uber, influencing NLRB discussions and California's AB5 law in 2019, which reclassified 1 million workers, boosting wage compliance but sparking legal challenges and platform exits.
EPI outputs have been invoked in dozens of congressional hearings on labor issues, with experts testifying on minimum wages, trade, and gig classification since the 1990s, including multiple appearances before House Education and Senate HELP committees. Empirical outcomes of influenced policies reveal wage upliftse.g., 15% gains for low-wage earners post-state hikesbut uneven employment effects, with NBER studies documenting 1-2% job reductions for youth and low-skill groups amid minimum wage rises, and BLS data indicating 1.3 million workers still at or below federal minimum in 2023, concentrated in services. These results underscore challenges in attributing causality, as confounding factors like labor market tightness often drive gains independently of EPI-backed reforms.

Funding and Financial Transparency

Primary Funding Sources

The Economic Policy Institute derives the majority of its revenue from private contributions and grants, which comprised 98.8% of total income in 2023 ($15,968,002 out of $16,155,263), with similar dominance in prior years: 98.6% in 2021 ($16,234,706 out of $16,459,436) and 98.2% in 2022 ($7,981,595 out of $8,123,876). These funds originate from foundations, labor unions, individuals, and corporations, with no government grants reported in IRS Form 990 filings.
EPI states that foundation grants form the largest share of its funding base, followed by contributions from labor unions such as AFL-CIO affiliates and the National Education Association. Foundations including the Ford Foundation have provided ongoing support through multi-year grants for research initiatives. Historical analysis shows foundations and unions collectively accounted for 79% of EPI's funding from 2013 to 2016, reflecting a pattern of heavy reliance on these aligned donors.
The remaining revenue streams, including individual donations and minor program service fees, constitute less than 2% annually, underscoring the nonprofit's dependence on large institutional gifts. Pre-2010 funding leaned more heavily on union sources tied to EPI's origins in labor advocacy, but post-Great Recession diversification increased foundation involvement while maintaining stable overall shares in recent 990 filings (20212023). Total annual revenue has fluctuated between $8 million and $16 million during this period, driven by variable grant cycles.

Financial Trends and Donor Influence Concerns

The Economic Policy Institute (EPI) reported net assets of approximately $13.8 million as of the end of its most recent fiscal year documented in public filings, reflecting steady growth from prior years when total assets stood at around $8.8 million in 2019. This expansion occurred alongside revenues exceeding $16 million in recent periods, outpacing expenses of about $12 million, though EPI has navigated operational challenges including staff expansions and research investments that occasionally pressured margins. Public financial disclosures via IRS Form 990 filings indicate a robust balance sheet, with total assets surpassing $18 million, supported by diversified inflows rather than reliance on endowments or high-risk investments.
EPI maintains public donor lists for contributions over $500, aggregated by category without individual amounts to protect privacy, revealing that foundations provide the largest share (nearly two-thirds of funding), followed by labor unions (about one-fifth). Critics, including analyses from conservative-leaning watchdogs, have raised concerns that substantial union supportsuch as from AFL-CIO affiliatescorrelates with EPI's consistent advocacy for pro-labor policies, potentially incentivizing outputs aligned with funders' bargaining priorities over neutral empiricism. Similarly, grants from foundations focused on inequality, like the Open Society Foundations, coincide with EPI's emphasis on wage stagnation and wealth gaps, prompting questions about whether thematic alignment influences research prioritization.
EPI counters such concerns by emphasizing its disclosure policies and asserting editorial independence, stating that no donors exert veto power over specific research or publications, with funding diversity mitigating single-source sway. Empirical reviews of outputs show thematic consistency with broad progressive economics but no documented instances of suppressed contrary findings, though skeptics argue the absence of vetoes does not preclude subtler agenda-setting through grant conditions tied to inequality or labor themes. These dynamics underscore ongoing debates over non-profit think tank autonomy, where transparency exists but causal inference on influence remains indirect absent internal grant audits.

Criticisms, Controversies, and Counterarguments

Allegations of Methodological Bias

The Economic Policy Institute (EPI) has been rated as left-leaning by multiple media bias evaluators, with Media Bias/Fact Check citing its affiliation with labor unions and advocacy for redistributive policies as contributing to a selective emphasis on data supporting progressive economic interventions. Ad Fontes Media similarly scores EPI as skewing left with moderate reliability concerns arising from opinion-heavy analysis. AllSides assigns a "Left" rating, noting EPI's consistent framing of economic issues through a lens favoring worker protections and skepticism toward market-driven outcomes. These assessments reflect broader critiques from economists that EPI's research prioritizes normative goals, such as highlighting bargaining power imbalances, over neutral empirical rigor.
Critics, including academic economists, have accused EPI of methodological flaws like cherry-picking data series that amplify wage stagnation narratives while omitting contextual factors, such as total compensation including non-wage benefits and government transfers. For instance, EPI's analyses of the productivity-pay divergence often rely on pre-tax hourly wages for typical workers, which understate overall compensation growth when benefits like health insurance and transfers are incorporated, leading to claims of selective measurement to underscore inequality driven by institutional power dynamics rather than market forces. Economists have noted similar omissions in trade and wage studies, where EPI emphasizes negative impacts on low-skilled workers without fully accounting for offsetting gains in consumer prices or overall employment effects, aligning with a preconceived causal emphasis on employer power over technological or global factors.
In response, EPI maintains that its methodological choices reflect a commitment to causal realism in labor markets, where empirical data must incorporate the structural effects of unequal bargaining power between workers and employers, rather than bias toward ideological framing. The institute's defenders argue that critiques overlook how standard economic metrics, like those ignoring power asymmetries, fail to capture real-world wage suppression mechanisms, positioning EPI's approach as a corrective to mainstream models that underemphasize institutional influences. Nonetheless, independent analyses suggest these defenses do not fully address reproducibility concerns, as EPI's data selections often diverge from consensus benchmarks used in peer-reviewed journals.

Debates Over Specific Economic Claims

The Economic Policy Institute has argued that increases in the U.S. federal minimum wage, such as to $15 per hour, would not lead to significant job losses among low-wage workers, citing studies showing minimal or zero disemployment effects. However, meta-analyses of minimum wage research, including those by David Neumark, indicate negative employment effects, particularly for low-skilled teens and young adults, with elasticities around -0.1 to -0.2 for a 10% wage hike, driven by reduced hiring and hours in competitive labor markets where firms face binding constraints on low-productivity workers. These findings align with first-principles expectations of price floors distorting labor demand, as evidenced by state-level variations where higher minimums correlate with 1-2% drops in teen employment rates.
EPI reports have attributed substantial U.S. manufacturing job lossesestimated at over 5 million since 2000to trade deficits and agreements like NAFTA, portraying globalization as a primary causal factor without net reallocation benefits. Critics contend this overstates trade's role by assuming one-for-one import displacement of jobs while underweighting export creation and sectoral shifts, such as automation and productivity gains absorbing 80-90% of manufacturing declines per Bureau of Labor Statistics data. Empirical models incorporating worker reallocation show trade-exposed areas experiencing temporary dislocations but long-term wage adjustments via mobility, not permanent net losses, as incentives drive labor toward higher-productivity sectors.
EPI analyses claim public-sector workers, including teachers, face a 10-20% wage penalty relative to private counterparts with similar education, advocating higher pay to address undercompensation. Counteranalyses adjusting for total compensationincorporating pensions, health benefits, and job securityreveal public employees earn 10-25% premiums, with state/local workers' defined-benefit plans alone adding 15-20% effective value over private 401(k)s, per Census and BLS data on fringes exceeding 40% of pay in government vs. 30% private. This discrepancy arises from EPI's focus on cash wages, neglecting non-wage trade-offs like lower layoff risks that incentivize acceptance of base-pay gaps.
In discussions of inequality, EPI emphasizes static snapshots of wage and wealth stocks, such as top 1% shares rising 171% since 1979, to argue for redistribution without fully accounting for income flows or behavioral responses. Critics highlight that such measures overlook dynamic mobilityU.S. quintile transitions averaging 40-50% over decadesand incentive effects, where high marginal tax rates (e.g., 70% effective on added income) reduce labor supply and investment by 0.5-1% per percentage point, per elasticity estimates, leading to slower growth than EPI's demand-focused models predict. These trade-offs underscore causal realism: redistribution alters effort and capital allocation, potentially compressing flows more than stocks suggest.

Responses to Right-Leaning Critiques

The Economic Policy Institute has rebutted right-leaning critiques by asserting that such objections often prioritize ideological commitments to unfettered markets over empirical evidence from peer-reviewed studies. For example, in defending minimum wage increases, EPI researchers, including Ben Zipperer and Arindrajit Dube, reviewed dozens of studies and concluded that the majority find little or no employment loss, attributing contrary findings to weaker research designs that fail to account for regional economic variations or long-term adjustments. EPI argues that critics, such as those from the American Enterprise Institute (AEI), overlook this body of evidence in favor of theoretical models assuming perfect labor market competition, which do not align with observed data from U.S. state-level implementations since the 1990s.
Right-leaning analysts, including those at AEI and the Heritage Foundation, counter that EPI's analyses systematically understate dynamic effects, such as reduced incentives for business expansion or automation in response to wage mandates, leading to overlooked disemployment among low-skilled youthevidenced by historical federal hikes correlating with 1-2% employment drops in affected demographics per some econometric models. These critics also contend that EPI ignores entrepreneurship disincentives, where stringent labor protections advocated by the institute raise hiring costs and deter small business formation, as shown in cross-state data linking higher regulatory burdens to 5-10% lower startup rates in labor-intensive sectors.
On unionization, EPI responds to productivity critiques by highlighting aggregate benefits, such as higher wages (10-20% premium for union members) and improved community outcomes in high-union-density states, without evidence of net economic drag when controlling for industry mix. Conservative rebuttals, however, point to firm-level studies indicating unions correlate with 10-15% lower productivity growth due to rigid work rules and higher operational costs, as critiqued in analyses of EPI's pay-productivity gap claims that allegedly conflate causation with correlation by excluding managerial compensation adjustments. 
Empirical evidence on these debates reveals context-dependency: while early work like Card and Krueger's 1994 New Jersey studyoften cited by EPIfound no disemployment from a 1992 minimum wage hike, broader meta-analyses of over 100 U.S. and international cases since 2000 indicate small negative employment effects (0.1-0.3% per 10% wage increase) in competitive low-wage sectors, though negligible in monopsonistic markets with tight labor supply. Similarly, union wage premiums hold in panel data, but productivity trade-offs emerge in manufacturing, underscoring that policy outcomes hinge on implementation scale and economic conditions rather than universal ideological priors.

Recent Activities and Developments

Post-2020 Research Priorities

In response to the COVID-19 pandemic, the Economic Policy Institute directed research toward the vulnerabilities of essential workers, documenting their exposure to health risks, low median wages, and underrepresentation in unions across key sectors. A May 2020 analysis classified 12 industries as essential, encompassing over 55 million workers, with many earning below the national median wage of $19.61 per hour and union coverage rates as low as 4.5% in some areas like food and agriculture services. This work underscored how essential roles, disproportionately held by women, Black, and Hispanic workers, lacked remote work options, amplifying inequities in job security and compensation during lockdowns.
From 2021 to 2023, EPI's priorities expanded to pandemic recovery dynamics, including the labor market effects of supply-chain disruptions, which the institute linked to inflationary pressures from 2021 onward while advocating for worker-centered resilience measures like enhanced bargaining rights to mitigate volatility. Research intensified on antitrust enforcement in labor markets to combat monopsony power, with reports citing employer concentration as a factor suppressing wages by up to 20% in concentrated regions, building on empirical studies of non-compete clauses and hiring collusion. Concurrently, analyses of racial wage disparities highlighted persistent gaps, such as Black workers earning 23.4% less hourly than white counterparts by mid-2023, attributing outcomes to structural power imbalances rather than skill differences alone, and calling for policies like sector-specific bargaining to address them.
EPI maintained methodological emphasis on employer-worker power asymmetries, applying this framework to emerging gig and AI-driven economies, where algorithms were critiqued for enabling opaque wage-setting and reducing collective leverage without regulatory offsets like union access. This approach framed technological shifts not as neutral productivity boosters but as amplifiers of inequality unless countered by institutional reforms prioritizing worker organization over market deregulation.

2024-2025 Reports and Positions

In September 2024, the Economic Policy Institute released an analysis of CEO compensation trends, reporting that average realized CEO pay at the top 350 U.S. firms reached $22.98 million, a 5.9% increase from the prior year, resulting in a CEO-to-typical-worker pay ratio of 281-to-1. This update extended EPI's long-standing data series, which shows CEO compensation rising by over 1,200% since 1978 in nominal terms, compared to roughly 18% growth for typical workers, a disparity EPI attributes primarily to expanded stock-based awards rather than operational performance. The report emphasized stagnant worker wages amid corporate profit growth, advocating for policy reforms like higher corporate taxes on executive pay excesses.
EPI also supported the Raise the Wage Act of 2025, projecting that phasing the federal minimum wage to $17 by 2030 would raise pay for approximately 22.2 million workers, or 15% of the U.S. workforce, with annual wage gains totaling over $86 billion. The institute's modeling assumed no significant job losses, drawing on historical minimum wage hikes, though critics have contested such projections for underestimating employment effects in low-wage sectors.
Shifting to 2025 outputs, EPI warned of a shrinking U.S.-born labor force, projecting stagnation or decline without sustained net immigration averaging 1.2 million annually through 2035, as forecasted by the Congressional Budget Office; the institute argued this demographic trend would hinder GDP growth absent immigrant inflows bolstering prime-age participation rates. In parallel, EPI critiqued extensions of the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, asserting they would exacerbate fiscal deficits and inequality by disproportionately benefiting high earners and corporations, potentially forcing cuts to social programs or higher taxes on lower-income households.
On trade policy, EPI maintained that tariffs alone fail to raise wages or secure jobs, contrasting them with unionization's proven effects, while acknowledging limited protective roles for tariffs in import-competing industries but warning of retaliatory risks and consumer costs under broad implementations. The organization aligned with elements of Biden-era industrial policies like the CHIPS Act for domestic manufacturing revival but critiqued insufficient labor standards and enforcement, urging stronger worker protections to prevent wage suppression.
EPI launched a series of policy briefs in 2025 outlining state-level strategies to counter federal rollbacks on worker rights, highlighting victories such as minimum wage hikes in over 20 states and enhanced wage theft penalties, while recommending expansions in unemployment insurance eligibility and opposition to preemption laws limiting local standards. These resources positioned states as bulwarks against perceived national erosion of protections, including Project 2025 proposals to weaken overtime rules and suitable work criteria.
