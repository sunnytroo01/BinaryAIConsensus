Mountains are among the most dramatic and awe-inspiring features on the Earth's surface. They rise above the surrounding landscape as monuments to the immense geological forces that created them, and they have shaped human history, culture, and ecology in ways that are difficult to overstate. Mountains influence weather patterns, direct the flow of rivers, create barriers between civilizations, harbor unique ecosystems, provide fresh water to billions of people, and inspire a sense of wonder and insignificance in everyone who stands at their base and looks up.

Mountains form through several geological processes, all of which involve the movement and deformation of the Earth's crust over millions of years.

Fold mountains are the most common type and are formed when tectonic plates collide. When two continental plates converge, neither can be subducted beneath the other because continental crust is too buoyant. Instead, the crust crumples, folds, and thickens, pushing rock upward to form mountain ranges. The Himalayas, the tallest mountain range on Earth, formed when the Indian plate collided with the Eurasian plate approximately fifty million years ago. The collision is still ongoing, and the Himalayas continue to rise at a rate of approximately five millimeters per year. Mount Everest, the tallest mountain above sea level at twenty-nine thousand and thirty-two feet, grows slightly taller each year as a result.

The Alps in Europe, the Andes in South America, the Rockies in North America, and the Urals in Russia are all fold mountain ranges formed by the collision or convergence of tectonic plates. The Appalachian Mountains in eastern North America are ancient fold mountains that were once as tall as the Himalayas but have been worn down by hundreds of millions of years of erosion to their current modest heights.

Volcanic mountains form when magma from the Earth's interior erupts at the surface and accumulates into a cone-shaped structure. Mount Fuji in Japan, Mount Kilimanjaro in Tanzania, Mount Rainier in Washington State, and Mount Etna in Sicily are all volcanic mountains. The tallest mountain on Earth, measured from its base on the ocean floor, is Mauna Kea in Hawaii, a volcanic mountain that rises approximately thirty-three thousand five hundred feet from the seafloor, of which only thirteen thousand eight hundred feet are above sea level.

Fault-block mountains form when large blocks of rock are tilted or uplifted along fault lines. The Sierra Nevada range in California and the Teton Range in Wyoming are examples of fault-block mountains, where one side of a fault has been pushed upward while the other side has dropped down.

Mountains create their own weather. As moist air is forced upward by a mountain barrier, it cools, and the moisture condenses and falls as precipitation on the windward side of the mountain. By the time the air descends on the leeward side, it has lost most of its moisture, creating a rain shadow, a dry area that receives significantly less precipitation than the windward side. The Atacama Desert in Chile, one of the driest places on Earth, lies in the rain shadow of the Andes.

Mountain ecosystems are organized in vertical zones, each with its own characteristic climate, vegetation, and wildlife. At the base, conditions are similar to the surrounding lowlands. As elevation increases, temperature decreases by approximately three to five degrees Fahrenheit for every thousand feet of altitude gain. The tree line marks the elevation above which conditions are too cold and windy for trees to grow, typically between eight thousand and twelve thousand feet depending on latitude. Above the tree line is the alpine zone, characterized by grasses, wildflowers, and low shrubs adapted to cold temperatures, strong winds, and intense ultraviolet radiation. Above the alpine zone is the permanent snow and ice zone, where only the hardiest lichens, bacteria, and insects survive.

Mountains are critical freshwater sources. They are often called the water towers of the world because they capture precipitation as snow and ice, store it through the winter, and release it gradually as melt water during the spring and summer. Approximately half of the world's population depends on water that originates in mountain regions. The Himalayas alone feed ten of the largest rivers in Asia, including the Ganges, the Indus, the Yangtze, and the Mekong, providing water to approximately two billion people.

The cultural significance of mountains spans every civilization in human history. Mountains have been considered the dwelling places of gods in traditions ranging from Greek mythology, where the gods lived on Mount Olympus, to Hinduism, where Mount Meru is the center of the universe, to Christianity, where Moses received the Ten Commandments on Mount Sinai and Jesus delivered his most famous sermon on a mountain. Mountains symbolize aspiration, challenge, endurance, and transcendence in virtually every culture that has encountered them.

Mountaineering, the sport of climbing mountains, has produced some of the most celebrated achievements and most tragic failures in the history of human exploration. The first confirmed ascent of Mont Blanc, the tallest peak in the Alps, occurred in 1786. The first ascent of the Matterhorn in 1865 ended in triumph and tragedy when four of the seven climbers fell to their deaths during the descent. The first ascent of Mount Everest by Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay on May 29, 1953, was one of the defining achievements of the twentieth century. Since then, over six thousand people have reached the summit of Everest, and over three hundred have died trying.

Mountains are changing. Climate change is causing glaciers to retreat on every continent, reducing the snowpack that feeds rivers, shifting the elevation of vegetation zones upward, and threatening the survival of species adapted to cold, high-altitude environments. The glaciers of the European Alps have lost approximately half their volume since 1900. The glaciers of the Himalayas, which provide water to a significant portion of the world's population, are retreating at an accelerating rate, raising concerns about future water security in some of the most densely populated regions on Earth.

A mountain is time made visible. Every layer of rock, every fold, every fault, every eroded valley tells a story that spans millions or billions of years. When you look at a mountain, you are looking at the Earth's autobiography, written in stone, sculpted by water, ice, and wind, and still being composed, one millimeter at a time, as the tectonic plates continue their slow, relentless dance.
