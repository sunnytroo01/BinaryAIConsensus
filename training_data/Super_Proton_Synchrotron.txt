Super Proton Synchrotron
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS) is a circular particle accelerator at CERN, measuring nearly 7 kilometres in circumference and serving as the second-largest machine in the laboratory's accelerator complex. Operational since 1976, it accelerates protons, antiprotons, electrons, positrons, and heavy ions such as sulphur and oxygen nuclei to energies up to 450 GeV, functioning primarily as an injector for the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) while also supporting fixed-target experiments like NA61/SHINE, NA62, and COMPASS.
Commissioned by the CERN Council in February 1971, the SPS was the first of CERN's giant underground rings and the first cross-border accelerator, spanning France and Switzerland. Construction of its tunnel, completed on 31 July 1974 using a Robbins tunnel-boring machine, marked a significant engineering feat. The accelerator achieved its first circulating beam on 3 May 1976 and reached 400 GeV for protons by 17 June 1976, surpassing its original design energy of 300 GeV. Equipped with 1317 conventional electromagnetsincluding 744 dipolesit rapidly became CERN's workhorse for particle physics research.
Throughout its history, the SPS has played a pivotal role in major discoveries, including the 1983 detection of the W and Z bosons during proton-antiproton collisions, which earned Carlo Rubbia and Simon van der Meer the Nobel Prize in Physics. In 1986, it pioneered heavy-ion collisions to investigate quark-gluon plasma, simulating conditions of the early universe. More recently, the SPS has contributed to studies of proton structure, matter-antimatter asymmetry, exotic matter, and accelerator physics advancements, such as observing resonances in four dimensions in collaboration with GSI in 2024. As of 2025, it continues to beam particles into the LHC and other experiments, ensuring CERN's ongoing high-energy physics program.

Design and Specifications

Location and Layout

The Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS) is situated at the European Organization for Nuclear Research (CERN) laboratory, located in the foothills of the Jura Mountains near Geneva, Switzerland. Its tunnel straddles the border between Switzerland and France, marking it as CERN's first major cross-border accelerator project. This positioning allows integration within the broader Geneva region while minimizing surface disruption in the surrounding countryside.
The SPS occupies a circular tunnel with a circumference of 6.9 kilometers (approximately 7 km), buried at an average depth of 40 meters underground. The tunnel was excavated using a conventional boring method in the local molasse sandstone rock, rather than surface-level techniques, to preserve the landscape; construction of the tunnel ring was completed on 31 July 1974. Access to the underground structure is provided via six vertical shafts distributed around the circumference, facilitating maintenance and installation of equipment. The tunnel's design supports the housing of over 1,300 conventional electromagnets and associated beamline components in a stable subsurface environment.
As the final pre-accelerator in CERN's chain, the SPS receives proton or ion beams from the Proton Synchrotron (PS) for further acceleration before injecting them into the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) or directing them to fixed-target experiments such as NA61/SHINE, NA62, and COMPASS. This layout positions the SPS as a critical link in the accelerator complex, bridging lower-energy machines with high-energy operations. Surface infrastructure includes key buildings such as the SPS control roomequipped with early capacitive touchscreens and a distributed multi-computer system for monitoringand power supply facilities that manage the electrical demands of the underground magnets and RF systems. These surface elements, along with access points connected to the shafts, ensure operational oversight and safety from above ground.

Technical Parameters

The Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS) achieves a maximum proton energy of 450 GeV, representing an upgrade from its original design specification of 300 GeV (increased to 400 GeV during construction) to support higher-energy operations, including as an injector for the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). This energy capability is enabled by a robust magnet lattice housed within a 7 km circumference tunnel, where the spatial arrangement facilitates efficient beam circulation.
The SPS employs 1,317 conventional room-temperature electromagnets to guide and focus the beam, comprising 744 dipole magnets responsible for bending the proton trajectory around the ring and additional quadrupoles for dynamic focusing to maintain beam stability. These magnets operate with a maximum field strength of approximately 1.8 T in the dipoles, ensuring precise control over the high-energy beam path.
Beam acceleration is provided by a radiofrequency (RF) system featuring four cavities (two of four sections and two of five sections) operating at 200 MHz, which deliver the necessary voltage for energy gain across the synchrotron's cycle; this configuration was enhanced during upgrades to accommodate increased beam intensities. Complementing this, the vacuum system sustains an ultra-high vacuum on the order of 10^{-9} to 10^{-10} mbar throughout the beam pipe, reducing interactions with residual gas molecules and preserving beam quality by minimizing scattering losses.
In LHC injector mode, the SPS supports beam intensities up to 1.7 \times 10^{11} protons per bunch, allowing for the accumulation and transfer of high-brightness proton batches to downstream accelerators while managing emittance growth and collective effects.

Acceleration Process

The Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS) accelerates protons and other particles using the fundamental principles of synchrotron operation, where charged particles are maintained in a stable orbit within a circular vacuum chamber by a combination of bending magnets and focusing quadrupoles. Particles are injected into the SPS at an energy of 26 GeV/c from the upstream Proton Synchrotron (PS) via a dedicated transfer line (TT10), entering the ring at a momentum close to relativistic speeds. Once captured, the beam circulates in the 6.9 km circumference ring, guided by 744 dipole magnets that provide the necessary centripetal force.
Acceleration proceeds by synchronously ramping the magnetic field strength in the dipoles from approximately 0.53 T at injection to a maximum of 1.84 T at the top energy of 450 GeV/c, ensuring the orbital radius remains constant as particle momentum increases. Concurrently, radiofrequency (RF) cavities operating at 200 MHz deliver accelerating electric fields through traveling-wave structures, with voltages adjusted to match the required energy gain per turntypically up to several MV depending on the beam mode. This coordinated ramping of magnetic field and RF frequency prevents beam loss due to orbit shifts or phase slippage, achieving the final energy in a controlled manner.
For proton operation, the injection scheme relies on multi-turn charge exchange of H ions in the PS Booster to build intensity before transfer to the PS and then single-turn injection into the SPS, allowing accumulation of multiple batches (up to 34 per ring filling) to reach luminosities suitable for downstream use. Throughout the acceleration process, beam gymnastics are essential to optimize beam quality. Bunch rotation is performed using RF phase manipulations to shorten bunch lengths and preserve longitudinal emittance, particularly during transfer from the PS's 40 MHz RF to the SPS's 200 MHz system, ensuring efficient bucket-to-bucket matching. Transverse emittance is controlled via the quadrupole magnet lattice, which provides strong focusing to counteract beam divergence, while at high energies, synchrotron radiation offers limited damping effectsmore pronounced in lepton modes but contributing to natural emittance reduction for protons over multiple turns.
The full acceleration ramp, including injection, energy increase, and flat-top stabilization, typically spans 7.5 seconds in LHC injector mode, though cycle times extend to 1020 seconds or more depending on operational requirements such as batch composition or fixed-target extractions.

History

Construction Phase

The concept for a high-energy proton synchrotron designed to reach 400 GeV (initially proposed at 300 GeV in 1964) was put to the CERN Council as part of efforts to advance Europe's particle physics capabilities beyond the existing Proton Synchrotron. After years of debate over site selection and design, including initial plans for a separate laboratory, the project was revised to integrate with the existing CERN site near Geneva. In February 1971, eleven CERN member states approved the construction of the Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS), with an estimated budget of 1,150 million Swiss francs at 1970 prices, spread over an eight-year program.
The project was led by John Adams, who was appointed Director-General of CERN Laboratory II in 1971, with responsibility for the SPS. Construction officially began on 19 February 1971, involving extensive civil engineering to create a 6.9 km circular tunnel at an average depth of 40 meters beneath the Jura foothills and Lake Geneva. The effort required excavating rock, managed through two vertical shafts and a tunnel-boring machine that completed the ring on 31 July 1974.
Significant challenges arose during the build, particularly geological instability in the Jura foothills, which complicated tunneling and required careful reinforcement to ensure structural integrity. Additionally, integrating the new accelerator with the existing Proton Synchrotron tunnel demanded precise alignment and shared infrastructure, adding complexity to the layout. Despite these hurdles, the project progressed efficiently, with magnet installation commencing in 1975; over 1,300 conventional electromagnets, including 744 dipoles, were fitted into the tunnel to guide and focus the proton beams.

Commissioning and Early Milestones

The commissioning of the Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS) began with the successful injection and initial circulation of a proton beam on 3 May 1976, marking the first full traversal of its 7 km circumference without acceleration. By early June, testing had progressed to beam extraction at 100 GeV, demonstrating the machine's basic functionality ahead of full acceleration trials. On 17 June 1976, during an official CERN Council session, the SPS achieved its design energy of 400 GeV for the first time, with protons accelerated and circulated at this peak, solidifying its operational readiness.
This milestone was quickly overshadowed internationally, as Fermilab's Main Ring accelerator reached 500 GeV just weeks earlier on 14 May 1976, briefly claiming the record for highest-energy proton synchrotron. The event spurred CERN to implement minor performance enhancements to the SPS, enabling it to exceed 400 GeV in subsequent years and reach up to 450 GeV by the early 1980s, thereby restoring its competitive edge in high-energy acceleration.
Following commissioning, the SPS transitioned to physics operations with fixed-target proton runs commencing in January 1977, delivering beams to experimental areas such as the North Area for high-energy particle studies. Early performance achieved circulating beam intensities of approximately 2 10^{13} protons per pulse at a repetition rate of about 9.6 seconds, supporting initial experiments on muon and pion production while prioritizing beam stability and extraction efficiency.
In preparation for proton-antiproton collider operations, the SPS underwent significant modifications between 1980 and 1981, including adaptations for counter-rotating beam storage and acceleration. Central to this effort was the commissioning of the Antiproton Accumulator (AA) ring in July 1980, which employed stochastic coolinga technique pioneered at CERN to reduce the emittance of low-intensity antiproton beams produced from proton target interactions. This cooling method allowed accumulation of up to about 10^{11} antiprotons after approximately 24 hours of operation in the AA, enabling their transfer to the SPS for acceleration to 300-310 GeV and paving the way for collider physics by late 1981.

Operational Phases

Proton-Antiproton Collider Era

In 1981, the Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS) was converted from a fixed-target proton accelerator into the world's first high-energy proton-antiproton collider, known as the SppS, to probe electroweak interactions at unprecedented energies. This transformation involved installing new beam lines from the Proton Synchrotron (PS) to the SPS for 26 GeV/c injection, upgrading the vacuum system to ultra-high levels of approximately 10910^{-9}109 torr, and adding low-beta insertions at interaction points to focus beams. The collider operated with the UA1 and UA2 detectors positioned at interaction points 4 and 5, enabling the collection of collision data at a center-of-mass energy of 630 GeV, following initial runs at 546 GeV limited by magnet heating constraints.
Antiprotons were produced by directing a 26 GeV proton beam from the PS onto an iridium target, generating approximately 71067 \times 10^67106 antiprotons per cycle with a momentum of 3.5 GeV/c and momentum spread of p/p=1.5%\Delta p/p = 1.5\%p/p=1.5%. These antiprotons were then transferred to the Antiproton Accumulator (AA) ring, a dedicated storage ring where stochastic cooling reduced their phase space by a factor of at least 10810^8108, allowing accumulation over hours or days to build up dense stacks of up to 101110^{11}1011 antiprotons for transfer to the SPS. Protons were accelerated directly in the SPS to 315 GeV/c, while antiprotons were injected from the AA, and the counter-rotating beams were brought into collision using electrostatic separators to avoid unwanted crossings.
The collider's major physics runs began with initial collisions in 1981, but the 1983 run marked a significant milestone, collecting substantial data at 546 GeV that paved the way for key electroweak discoveries. Luminosity performance improved progressively, reaching up to 1029cm2s110^{29} \ \mathrm{cm}^{-2} \ \mathrm{s}^{-1}1029cm2s1 during early operations with 3-6 bunches per beam, supported by automated controls that achieved 51% efficiency in later years. In 1987, the introduction of the Antiproton Collector (ACOL) ring enhanced antiproton production rates to about 71077 \times 10^77107 per pulse, boosting luminosity further.
Operations continued until 1990, with the SPS proton-antiproton collider era concluding in 1991 after a decade of service, having delivered a total integrated luminosity of 4.2 pb1^{-1}1 across all runs. This period established the SPS as a pioneering facility for hadron colliders, demonstrating scalable beam storage and cooling techniques essential for future accelerators.

Fixed-Target and Neutrino Operations

Following the conclusion of the proton-antiproton collider operations in the early 1990s, the Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS) transitioned back to fixed-target mode, resuming beam delivery to experiments with significantly enhanced intensities compared to pre-collider levels. This shift allowed for a renewed focus on high-energy proton beams extracted via resonant slow extraction techniques, enabling diverse studies in particle and nuclear physics.
Proton beams from the SPS, accelerated to energies of 400-450 GeV, are directed to stationary targets in the North Area experimental hall at CERN's Prevessin site, producing secondary particles such as pions, kaons, and muons for downstream analyses. These beams, delivered in long spills lasting several seconds, support a wide array of fixed-target experiments probing hadron structure, rare decays, and exotic particles. For instance, the North Area has hosted investigations into light dark matter and lepton flavor violation using high-intensity proton interactions with fixed targets. Over decades of operation, the SPS has provided more than 10^{19} protons on target cumulatively for these programs, establishing it as a cornerstone for fixed-target research.
A key aspect of the SPS's fixed-target legacy involves neutrino production, where proton beams strike targets to generate focused neutrino beams for oscillation studies. The CERN Neutrino to Gran Sasso (CNGS) facility, operational from 2008 to 2012, utilized 400 GeV protons from the SPS to produce a muon neutrino beam directed 732 km to the OPERA experiment at the Gran Sasso National Laboratory in Italy. This setup delivered a total of 17.97 \times 10^{19} protons on target, enabling the first direct observation of tau neutrino appearance from muon neutrino oscillations and confirming neutrino mixing parameters. Earlier, in the 1990s, the SPS's West Area Neutrino Facility (WANF) wide-band beam supported the CHORUS and NOMAD experiments, which searched for \nu_\mu \to \nu_\tau oscillations using high-resolution tracking and emulsion techniques on neutrino interactions in iron and drift chamber targets, respectively. These efforts, running from 1994 to 1998, set stringent limits on oscillation parameters in the short-baseline regime and advanced detector technologies for neutrino physics.

Role in Modern Particle Physics

LHC Injector Functions

Since its commissioning as the final pre-accelerator in the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) injector chain in 2008, the Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS) has served as the primary injector for delivering proton beams to the LHC, enabling high-energy collisions in the collider. On 10 September 2008, the first proton beam was successfully transferred from the SPS to the LHC, marking the start of its operational role in this capacity. This function transformed the SPS from its earlier roles in fixed-target experiments and proton-antiproton collisions into a dedicated beam preparation stage for the LHC, where protons are accelerated and structured to match the collider's requirements.
In proton mode, the SPS receives bunches from the Proton Synchrotron (PS) at an energy of 26 GeV/c and accelerates them to 450 GeV/c over a cycle of approximately 21.8 seconds. Upon reaching 450 GeV/c, the beams are extracted from the SPS and transported to the LHC via two dedicated transfer lines: TI 2 for clockwise injection and TI 8 for anticlockwise injection, each about 3 km long. These lines ensure precise beam alignment and minimal losses during the transfer process.
Beam injection into the SPS occurs in batches, with each batch consisting of 72 proton bunches spaced by 25 ns, delivered sequentially from the PS over multiple turns during a 10.8-second injection plateau. To preserve low emittance and high brightness essential for LHC performance, phase space painting is employed during this multi-turn injection, allowing controlled filling of the transverse phase space to minimize emittance growth and beam losses. Typically, three or four such batches are injected per SPS cycle, building up the total beam intensity before acceleration and extraction.
The SPS also supports heavy-ion operations for the LHC by accelerating lead ions (Pb^{82+}) from the PS, reaching energies up to 177 GeV/nucleon before transfer to the LHC via the same TI 2 and TI 8 lines. This capability has been crucial for periodic heavy-ion runs, such as those in 2024 and 2025, where lead-ion beams are injected to enable collisions probing quark-gluon plasma formation. The 2025 lead-ion run commenced on 15 November 2025.
Overall, the SPS's injector functions have been pivotal in achieving the LHC's record proton-proton collision energy of 13.6 TeV per beam pair, with the SPS providing the structured 450 GeV/c beams that the LHC further accelerates to 6.8 TeV per beam. This setup continues to support ongoing Run 3 operations as of 2025, including high-luminosity proton runs.

Current Experiments and Beam Usage

The Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS) supports several fixed-target experiments in CERN's North Area, utilizing high-energy beams to probe fundamental particle interactions. The COMPASS experiment investigates the internal structure of protons and neutrons, as well as hadron spectroscopy, by directing muon and hadron beams onto liquid hydrogen or other targets to study spin-dependent phenomena and quark-gluon dynamics. In 2025, COMPASS continued data collection and analysis, including measurements of transversity distributions and light meson spectra from recent runs. Similarly, the NA62 experiment focuses on rare kaon decays, such as K++K^+ \to \pi^+ \nu \bar{\nu}K++, to test lepton flavor universality and search for new physics beyond the Standard Model, achieving the world's largest dataset of charged kaon decays through 2024 operations with plans for further data-taking in 2025. The NA61/SHINE experiment examines hadron production in proton-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus collisions to model cosmic-ray interactions and search for the QCD critical point, conducting runs with various beam species in 2025 to refine strong interaction phase diagram studies.
SPS beam operations in 2025 encompassed proton, muon, and ion modes, with the accelerator delivering slow-extracted beams to fixed-target setups and fast-extracted bunches for injection into other facilities. Annual operation totaled approximately 200 days, including commissioning, physics runs, and machine development, with a schedule featuring proton beams from March onward and ion campaigns in summer. Specific 2025 highlights included proton-oxygen ion runs from late June to early July, providing oxygen beams at energies up to 158 GeV/nucleon for heavy-ion studies. Muon beams, generated from pion decays, supported precision measurements in COMPASS, while ion beamssuch as oxygen and leadwere allocated for about four weeks to North Area experiments like NA61/SHINE.
The AWAKE experiment integrates SPS proton beams to drive plasma wakefield acceleration, creating high-gradient electric fields in plasma cells to test novel acceleration techniques for future colliders. In 2025, AWAKE conducted its second run from May to early June, following accelerator upgrades, and achieved stable proton-driven wakefields with bunches up to 400 GeV, advancing electron acceleration to multi-GeV energies over short distances.
Recent SPS operations set intensity records, with proton pulses reaching a total of 4.2 10^{13} protons per cycle in 20242025, enabling higher luminosity for downstream experiments and injectors while mitigating beam instabilities through improved scraping and RF systems. 2025 marked another record-breaking year for proton performance in the LHC injector chain. These advancements supported enhanced data rates in fixed-target setups, with extracted intensities exceeding 10^{13} protons per spill for North Area usage.

Scientific Achievements

Key Particle Discoveries

The Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS) played a pivotal role in the discovery of the W and Z bosons, the carriers of the weak nuclear force, through its conversion into a proton-antiproton collider in the early 1980s. The UA1 experiment, led by Carlo Rubbia, and the UA2 experiment detected the W boson in data collected at a center-of-mass energy of 540 GeV, with the discovery announced at a CERN press conference on January 25, 1983. The Z boson was subsequently observed by UA1 on April 30, 1983, confirming the electroweak theory proposed by Sheldon Glashow, Abdus Salam, and Steven Weinberg. These findings provided direct evidence for the mechanism of the weak interaction, a cornerstone of the Standard Model of particle physics.
The discoveries earned Carlo Rubbia and Simon van der Meer the 1984 Nobel Prize in Physics for their decisive contributions to the project, with Rubbia spearheading the experimental effort and van der Meer developing stochastic cooling to achieve the necessary beam intensities. The SPS collider operations enabled the production of sufficient high-energy collisions to observe these rare events, marking a breakthrough in understanding fundamental forces.
In the 1990s and early 2000s, the SPS supported fixed-target neutrino experiments NOMAD and CHORUS, which searched for muon neutrino to tau neutrino oscillations using beams from the SPS wide-band neutrino facility. Running from 1995 to 1998, NOMAD analyzed over 1.3 million neutrino interactions and set stringent limits on oscillation parameters, excluding certain regions previously suggested by other experiments and supporting the emerging picture of neutrino mixing. Similarly, CHORUS, operational from 1995 to 1997, provided complementary bounds on tau neutrino appearance, contributing to the accumulation of evidence for neutrino oscillations that culminated in later confirmations. These results helped refine models of neutrino masses and mixing angles within the Standard Model extensions.
Since 2012, while the SPS has not yielded discoveries of new particles, it has continued to enable significant scientific advances through fixed-target experiments and as an injector for the Large Hadron Collider (LHC), accelerating protons to 450 GeV for beam delivery that enabled the 2012 observation of the Higgs boson by ATLAS and CMS experiments. For instance, the NA62 experiment, using high-intensity proton beams from the SPS, reported the first observation of the ultra-rare decay K in September 2024, with a branching ratio consistent with Standard Model predictions and providing a precise test of flavor physics. This role ensures the high-intensity beams required for LHC data validating the Higgs mechanism, integral to electroweak symmetry breaking, and supports ongoing precision measurements in kaon physics.

Contributions to Accelerator Technology

The Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS) played a pivotal role in the development of stochastic cooling, a technique invented by Simon van der Meer in the early 1970s at CERN to reduce the energy spread and angular divergence of charged particle beams. This method employs a feedback system with pickup electrodes to detect particle positions and kicker magnets to apply corrective kicks, allowing individual particles to be damped over multiple turns in the accelerator. Initially tested at the Intersecting Storage Rings (ISR), stochastic cooling was essential for accumulating and cooling antiproton beams in the SPS during the 1980s, enabling the high-luminosity proton-antiproton collisions that led to the discovery of the W and Z bosons. By compressing beams into denser configurations, this innovation dramatically increased interaction rates and set a precedent for high-intensity operations in subsequent colliders worldwide.
From 1999 onward, the SPS served as a primary testbed for studying electron cloud effects, where low-energy electrons generated by beam-induced multipacting accumulate and cause beam instabilities, emittance growth, and pressure riseschallenges critical for high-intensity proton beams. Mitigation strategies developed at the SPS included the installation of solenoids in field-free regions to confine electrons near chamber walls and prevent cloud buildup, as well as the application of low-secondary-electron-yield (SEY) coatings. Amorphous carbon coatings, deposited via a hollow cathode plasma process, achieved SEY values around 1 without requiring in-situ baking, fully suppressing electron clouds for LHC-type beams in coated SPS dipole chambers and monitors after over a year of operation. These techniques, refined through extensive SPS experiments starting in 2007, directly informed LHC beam screen designs, including non-evaporable getter coatings in warm sections and sawtooth-patterned surfaces in cold arcs, ensuring stable 25-ns bunch spacing operations.
The SPS also advanced long-baseline neutrino beam production through the CERN Neutrinos to Gran Sasso (CNGS) project, operational from 2008 to 2012, where it accelerated protons to 400 GeV/c and directed them onto a graphite target to generate pions and kaons. These secondary particles were focused by horn and reflector magnetic lenses into a nearly parallel beam, decaying in a 1-km evacuated tunnel to produce a muon neutrino-dominated flux over a 732 km baseline to the Gran Sasso laboratory. An innovative downstream decay channel and hadron absorber minimized backgrounds, while precise beam instrumentation ensured neutrino energy spectra peaked at around 17 GeV for optimal oscillation sensitivity. This setup enabled the OPERA experiment to observe tau neutrino appearance, confirming muon-to-tau oscillations and providing key measurements of mixing parameters that advanced global neutrino physics.
As a vital injector for the Large Hadron Collider (LHC), the SPS functions as a testbed for beam instability diagnostics, replicating high-intensity conditions to predict and mitigate issues like transverse mode coupling and orbit distortions before LHC injection. Recent studies at the SPS have utilized beam position monitors to observe four-dimensional resonancescoupling transverse and longitudinal motionsrevealing "ghost" effects that influence particle trajectories over thousands of turns. In 2024 experiments, these diagnostics quantified resonance impacts on beam quality, informing nonlinear dynamics models for LHC stability at 450 GeV injection energies. Such advancements, including electro-optic techniques for intra-bunch monitoring, enhance predictive simulations and feedback systems, ensuring reliable beam transfer and minimizing emittance dilution in the LHC chain.

Upgrades and Future Developments

High-Luminosity LHC Preparations

The LHC Injectors Upgrade (LIU) project, initiated to enhance the performance of CERN's accelerator chain, includes significant modifications to the Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS) to support the High-Luminosity LHC (HL-LHC) by increasing beam brightness to a target of $ 2.5 \times 10^{11} $ protons per bunch. This upgrade addresses the need for higher intensity and lower emittance beams, enabling the HL-LHC to achieve up to 10 times the luminosity of its predecessor starting around 2030, thereby facilitating extended data collection for precision measurements. The LIU efforts on the SPS focus on optimizing proton acceleration and extraction at 450 GeV, ensuring reliable delivery to the LHC.
Key hardware enhancements include the installation of new radiofrequency (RF) amplifiers to improve beam stability and longitudinal control during acceleration, alongside upgrades to the beam dump system capable of handling 450 GeV extractions without excessive losses. Additionally, the integration of LINAC4 as the new low-energy injector, operational since 2021, provides higher injection energy into the Proton Synchrotron Booster, reducing space charge effects and supporting the overall brightness improvements in the SPS. These modifications were implemented during Long Shutdown 2 (2018-2022) and subsequent technical stops, with beam tests confirming performance close to targets, such as reaching $ 2.3 \times 10^{11} $ protons per bunch in early 2025.
The LIU project unfolds in phases, with Phase 1 (2016-2021) concentrating on intensity upgrades across the injector chain, including initial SPS RF enhancements and LINAC4 commissioning to double proton bunch populations. Phase 2 (2024-2026) targets brightness optimization in the SPS through advanced beam gymnastics and further RF tuning, with ongoing tests scheduled until summer 2026 to validate HL-LHC requirements. As of November 2025, during LHC Run 3, the upgraded SPS has undergone operational testing with high-intensity beams, reporting no major issues and successfully contributing to stable LHC operations.

Integration with Emerging Facilities

The Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS) plays a pivotal role in CERN's emerging facilities by providing high-energy proton beams for advanced acceleration R&D and future collider infrastructure. In the Advanced Proton Driven Plasma Wakefield Acceleration Experiment (AWAKE), the SPS delivers 400 GeV/c proton bunches to seed plasma wakefields, enabling the acceleration of electron bunches to multi-GeV energies over short distances. Initial tests with SPS beams commenced in 2016, demonstrating wakefield generation, while the second phase, starting in 2021, focuses on electron injection, beam quality preservation, and scalability for high-energy physics applications. Recent upgrades, initiated in 2025 ahead of schedule, enhance the SPS beamline integration to support more precise plasma interactions, with full experimental integration targeted for 2029 to achieve sustained electron acceleration up to 10 GeV.
Beyond AWAKE, the SPS serves as a critical testbed for injector studies related to the Future Circular Collider (FCC), a proposed 100 km circumference machine aiming for 100 TeV center-of-mass energy in hadron collisions. Engineering assessments evaluate the SPSpotentially upgraded to superconducting magnetsas a pre-accelerator, either directly injecting beams into the FCC ring or via an intermediate booster, to validate high-intensity proton handling and energy scaling. These studies, ongoing as of 2025, including the CERN Council's review in November 2025, which confirmed the technical feasibility of the FCC and called for further studies on implementation and funding, leverage the SPS's existing infrastructure to simulate FCC beam parameters, including luminosity targets exceeding 10^35 cm^-2 s^-1, informing the collider's feasibility.
The SPS also supports neutrino physics through upgrades to the NA61/SHINE experiment, enhancing fixed-target operations and providing precise hadron production measurements essential for neutrino beam simulations in experiments such as T2K and NA62. The SHiNE enhancements, including improved beamlines and detectors for hadron production measurements, will enable precise neutrino flux simulations. Although full implementation is slated for the 2030s following CERN's Long Shutdown 3, preliminary data from SPS beams already contribute to global neutrino analyses, extending the experiment's reach into the next decade.
In line with CERN's 2040 strategic vision, updated in 2025, the SPS is positioned for feasibility studies on muon colliders, where its proton beams could drive pion production targets to generate muons for acceleration. This integration explores compact, high-energy lepton colliders reaching 10 TeV, complementing hadron-based machines like the FCC, with SPS tests assessing beam power requirements up to 4 MW for muon cooling and injection.
