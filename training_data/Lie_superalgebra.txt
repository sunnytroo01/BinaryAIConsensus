Lie superalgebras generalize classical Lie algebras by incorporating a grading that distinguishes even and odd elements, reflecting fermionic (odd) and bosonic (even) symmetries. They possess analogous structures such as solvable radicals and semisimple quotients, but the Killing form can be degenerate even for simple cases, complicating the theory compared to ordinary Lie algebras. Finite-dimensional simple Lie superalgebras over the complex numbers were fully classified by V. G. Kac in 1977, yielding 13 families: five classical series A(m,n), B(m,n), C(n+1), D(m,n), and D(2,1;); exceptional ones F(4) and G(3); and four Cartan-type series W(n), S(n), H(n), and exceptional P(n) for specific n.
In theoretical physics, Lie superalgebras underpin supersymmetry (SUSY), extending the Poincar Lie algebra by adding odd generators (supercharges) that mix bosonic and fermionic degrees of freedom, unifying spacetime symmetries with internal symmetries in particle physics models. For instance, the N-extended Poincar superalgebra includes even parts from translations and Lorentz transformations, with odd parts in spinor representations satisfying anticommutation relations like {Q,Q}(m)Pm\{Q_\alpha, Q_\beta\} \propto (\gamma^m)_{\alpha\beta} P_m{Q,Q}(m)Pm, enabling representations where bosons and fermions appear in the same multiplet with spins differing by 1/2. This framework is central to superstring theory, supergravity, and extensions like fractional supersymmetry via F-fold Lie superalgebras.

Examples

Finite-Dimensional Examples

Finite-dimensional Lie superalgebras include several classical families, often denoted by series A(m,n), B(m,n), C(n), and D(m,n), which correspond to the basic simple Lie superalgebras over the complex numbers. These are constructed as subalgebras of matrix Lie superalgebras preserving certain nondegenerate bilinear forms, and they are labeled by Kac-Dynkin diagrams that extend the ordinary Lie algebra diagrams with additional odd roots to account for the Z-grading.
The orthosymplectic superalgebras osp(m|2n) form one of the primary families, defined as the Lie subsuperalgebra of gl(m|2n) consisting of endomorphisms that preserve a nondegenerate supersymmetric bilinear form on the underlying superspace V = ^{m|2n}. This bilinear form is even and symmetric on the even part and skew-symmetric on the odd part, with the explicit matrix representation being block-diagonal involving identity and anti-identity blocks adjusted for the grading. The total dimension of osp(m|2n) is \frac{m(m-1)}{2} + 2mn + 2n^2 + n, comprising even elements from so(m) sp(2n) and odd elements transforming as the tensor product of the defining representations. These superalgebras realize the B(m,n) and D(m,n) series for m 0, n 1 (with adjustments for small values), and the C(n) series corresponds to osp(2|2n) for n 1.
The special linear superalgebras sl(m|n) are another key family, defined as the kernel of the supertrace map in gl(m|n), i.e., sl(m|n) = { X \in \mathfrak{gl}(m|n) \mid \mathrm{str}(X) = 0 }, where the supertrace is the trace on the even block minus the trace on the odd block. This adjustment accounts for the superdeterminant, ensuring the bracket satisfies the super Jacobi identity. For m n, sl(m|n) is simple and realizes the A(m-1,n-1) series (with m > n 1), while for m = n, the center is nontrivial, and the quotient psl(n|n) = sl(n|n)/ I (where I is the identity) is simple but "strange" due to atypical representation properties. The dimension of sl(m|n) is (m+n)^2 - 1.
The periplectic superalgebras pe(n) constitute a family of strange Lie superalgebras, defined as the subsuperalgebra of gl(n|n) preserving an odd symmetric bilinear form on V = ^{n|n}, with elements of the form \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ C & -A^t \end{pmatrix} where B is symmetric, C is skew-symmetric, and no supertrace condition is imposed. This odd form leads to an odd bracket on the odd part, distinguishing pe(n) from classical families. They arise in the classification for n 2 and exhibit unique representation theory, including indecomposable modules not seen in basic superalgebras. The dimension of pe(n) is 2n^2.
Other notable finite-dimensional simple Lie superalgebras include the exceptional basic ones: D(2,1;) with dimension (8|8) for 0, -1; F(4) with dimension (24|16); and G(3) with dimension (17|14). The Cartan-type series comprise W(n) (vector fields on the odd line), S(n) and \tilde{S}(n) (special derivations), and H(n) (Hamiltonian vector fields), all for n 2, in addition to the periplectic P(n) = pe(n). These complete Kac's classification of finite-dimensional simple Lie superalgebras over .

Their natural filtrations by degree are invariant under automorphisms, ensuring structural stability over fields of characteristic zero or positive. These algebras underpin integrable hierarchies and supersymmetric mechanics in infinite dimensions.

Classification

Finite-Dimensional Simple Lie Superalgebras

In 1977, Victor Kac provided a complete classification of finite-dimensional simple Lie superalgebras over an algebraically closed field of characteristic zero. This classification consists of five infinite families of classical Lie superalgebrasdenoted A(m,n), B(m,n), C(n+1), D(m,n), and D(2,1;) for integers m, n 0 with appropriate restrictions to ensure simplicityand two exceptional cases G(3) and F(4). Additionally, there are two "strange" series P(n) and Q(n) for n 2, which do not fit neatly into the classical framework due to their atypical root systems. These superalgebras are defined abstractly via their root systems relative to a Cartan subalgebra, with the even part forming a reductive Lie algebra and the odd part consisting of root spaces corresponding to odd roots.
The classification relies on generalized Dynkin-Kac diagrams, which extend the Dynkin diagrams of ordinary Lie algebras to account for the Z-grading. These diagrams represent the simple roots of the super root system, distinguishing even roots (associated with even simple roots, depicted as usual nodes) from odd roots (marked by gray or crossed nodes to indicate fermionic generators). The Cartan matrix entries reflect the super bracket relations, with off-diagonal entries adjusted for the parity: even-even and odd-odd pairs follow bosonic commutation, while even-odd and odd-even pairs involve anticommutation. The Weyl group of the super root system is generated by reflections across even hyperplanes (which are involutions, as in the Lie algebra case) and odd hyperplanes (where reflections square to -1, altering the group action and introducing non-standard invariance properties). This structure allows for the realization of the classified superalgebras as contragredient Lie superalgebras, constructed from their root data.
A key aspect of the classification involves the choice of Borel subalgebra, which must be compatible with the root system to ensure simplicity. Distinguished Borel subalgebras are those in which exactly one simple root is odd, leading to a unique (up to conjugation) such subalgebra for each simple Lie superalgebra in the list; this choice preserves simplicity by avoiding nilpotent ideals in the odd part. In contrast, non-distinguished Borel subalgebras, which have multiple odd simple roots, may yield non-simple extensions or deformations, and are not used in the classification of simple cases. The simplicity of the superalgebras is thus tied to this distinguished grading, ensuring that the superalgebra has no nontrivial ideals.
Unlike the classification of finite-dimensional simple Lie algebras via the Cartan-Killing form, which exhaustively captures all semisimple cases through invariant bilinear forms, no such complete analog exists for Lie superalgebras. The super Killing form is often degenerate on the odd subspace, preventing a direct extension of the Killing-Cartan criterion, and certain potential diagrams do not yield simple superalgebrasfor instance, the case B(0,n) is absent from the list as it results in a non-simple structure. This incompleteness highlights the subtler interplay of even and odd components in the super setting.

Infinite-Dimensional Simple Lin. Compact Lie Superalgebras

A linearly compact topological vector space over an algebraically closed field kkk of characteristic zero is a Hausdorff space such that every family of closed subspaces with the finite intersection property has nonempty intersection. This topology is dual to the discrete topology on countably infinite-dimensional vector spaces over kkk, and a prototypical example is the space k(/page/t_1,_\dots,_t_r) of formal power series in rrr variables, equipped with neighborhoods of zero given by ideals generated by sufficiently high powers of the variables. A Lie superalgebra equipped with such a topology is called linearly compact if the underlying topological vector space is linearly compact and the Lie bracket is continuous.
The simple infinite-dimensional linearly compact Lie superalgebras over kkk were classified by V. G. Kac in 1998. This classification extends the finite-dimensional theory by identifying infinite families of such superalgebras, analogous to the Cartan series for Lie algebras, along with a finite number of exceptional cases. Unlike the finite-dimensional setting, where all simple Lie superalgebras over algebraically closed fields of characteristic zero are known, the linearly compact case captures only a specific subcategory of infinite-dimensional simples, realized typically as algebras of formal vector fields or differential operators on superspaces.
The main infinite families in Kac's classification are the Cartan-type superalgebras, defined on the superspace kmnk^{m|n}kmn with formal coordinates. The series W(mn)W(m|n)W(mn) consists of all supersymmetric vector fields, forming the general linear Lie superalgebra in this context; these are generated by derivations and exhibit the full structure of formal supersymmetric differential operators. Subseries include S(mn)S(m|n)S(mn) for (m,n)(2,2)(m,n) \neq (2,2)(m,n)=(2,2), comprising the divergence-free vector fields that preserve a supersymmetric volume form, providing a special linear analog. The Hamiltonian series H(2mn)H(2m|n)H(2mn) arises from vector fields preserving an even supersymmetric symplectic form, while the odd Hamiltonian series HO(mn)HO(m|n)HO(mn) for m2m \geq 2m2 preserves an odd symplectic structure, capturing supersymmetric extensions relevant to odd-dimensional settings. Additionally, contact-type series appear in odd total dimensions, completing the infinite families.
Kac's classification also includes five exceptional simple infinite-dimensional linearly compact Lie superalgebras: E(16)E(1|6)E(16), E(36)E(3|6)E(36), E(38)E(3|8)E(38), E(44)E(4|4)E(44), and E(510)E(5|10)E(510), which do not fit into the Cartan-type patterns but share similar formal realizations. Specific families within this framework include extensions related to the finite-dimensional simple Lie superalgebra osp(12)\mathfrak{osp}(1|2)osp(12), such as derivations extending its action on formal modules, and super W-algebras generalizing higher-spin symmetries in supersymmetric contexts. Notable examples are the Neveu-Schwarz and Ramond series, which realize superconformal structures: the Neveu-Schwarz superalgebra features half-integer modes for odd generators, while the Ramond includes integer modes, both embedding as subsuperalgebras of W(01)W(0|1)W(01) or related series and playing key roles in two-dimensional supersymmetric field theories.
All simple infinite-dimensional linearly compact Lie superalgebras in this classification admit a Z\mathbb{Z}Z-grading with finite-dimensional homogeneous components, typically induced by the degree in formal variables, ensuring the topology aligns with the algebraic structure. This grading reflects their primitive nature, generated in low degrees, and distinguishes them from broader classes of infinite-dimensional simple Lie superalgebras, such as affine or Kac-Moody types, for which no complete classification exists.

Involutions

The universal enveloping algebra U(g)U(\mathfrak{g})U(g) of a Lie superalgebra g\mathfrak{g}g is the quotient of the tensor superalgebra T(g)T(\mathfrak{g})T(g) by the two-sided super ideal generated by the supercommutators xy(1)xyyxx \otimes y - (-1)^{|x||y|} y \otimes x - xy(1)xyyx for homogeneous elements x,ygx, y \in \mathfrak{g}x,yg, where | \cdot | denotes the grading parity. This construction endows U(g)U(\mathfrak{g})U(g) with a canonical structure of an associative superalgebra, and the canonical inclusion gU(g)\mathfrak{g} \hookrightarrow U(\mathfrak{g})gU(g) realizes g\mathfrak{g}g as the Lie superalgebra of primitive elements. The universal property states that for any associative superalgebra AAA and a superalgebra homomorphism :gA\phi: \mathfrak{g} \to A:gA satisfying ()=(x)(y)(1)xy(y)(x)\phi() = \phi(x)\phi(y) - (-1)^{|x||y|} \phi(y)\phi(x)()=(x)(y)(1)xy(y)(x), there exists a unique superalgebra homomorphism :U(g)A\overline{\phi}: U(\mathfrak{g}) \to A:U(g)A extending \phi. This property establishes an adjunction between the category of Lie superalgebras and the category of associative superalgebras, where the forgetful functor from associative superalgebras to Lie superalgebras (sending an algebra to its supercommutator subalgebra) has UUU as its left adjoint, mediated by symmetrization maps that project onto symmetric and skew-symmetric components adjusted for super signs.
Alternatively, Lie superalgebras can be viewed as algebras over the supercommutative Hopf algebra governing primitive elements in the enveloping algebra, capturing their coalgebraic structure functorially. The category of representations of a Lie superalgebra g\mathfrak{g}g, denoted gsMod\mathfrak{g}\mathrm{-sMod}gsMod, consists of super vector spaces M=M0M1M = M_{\bar{0}} \oplus M_{\bar{1}}M=M0M1 equipped with an even action morphism :gEnd(M)\rho: \mathfrak{g} \to \mathrm{End}(M):gEnd(M) such that even elements of g\mathfrak{g}g act by even endomorphisms (preserving grading) and odd elements act by odd endomorphisms (interchanging grading components), with the action compatible with the super bracket via the Leibniz rule. In this category, irreducible representations satisfy a super version of Schur's lemma: for a finite-dimensional irreducible representation over an algebraically closed field of characteristic zero, the supercommutant Endg(M)\mathrm{End}_{\mathfrak{g}}(M)Endg(M) is either the base field acting evenly or consists of the scalars plus multiples of an odd endomorphism aaa with a2=ida^2 = \mathrm{id}a2=id, distinguishing it from the classical case.
