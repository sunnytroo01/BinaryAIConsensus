Chymosin

 Chymosin, also known as rennin, is an aspartic protease enzyme (EC 3.4.23.4) that specifically hydrolyzes the Phe105-Met106 bond in the -casein protein of milk, destabilizing casein micelles and initiating the coagulation process essential for cheese production. It is naturally synthesized as an inactive precursor, prochymosin, by the chief cells in the abomasum (fourth stomach compartment) of neonatal ruminants such as calves, lambs, and kids, where it aids in curdling milk to facilitate prolonged digestion in the acidic gastric environment.
Structurally, chymosin consists of a single polypeptide chain of 323 amino acids, folding into a -sheet-dominated structure typical of aspartic peptidases, with two aspartic acid residues in the active site facilitating catalysis at an optimal pH of 4.55.0 and temperature around 4050C. Crystal structures of bovine and camel chymosins reveal high similarity, though camel chymosin exhibits greater flexibility in its substrate-binding cleft due to the absence of an N-terminal -strand, contributing to its enhanced milk-clotting efficiency on bovine milk. The enzyme exists in two main isoformsA and Bdiffering by a single amino acid at position 286 (aspartic acid in A, glycine in B), with natural calf rennet typically containing about 40% A and 60% B.
In its biological role, chymosin secretion peaks shortly after birth in ruminants, promoting the formation of a milk curd that slows gastric emptying and enhances nutrient absorption, though its production declines rapidly post-weaning; non-ruminants like humans and horses lack functional chymosin due to gene mutations. Industrially, chymosin has been pivotal in cheesemaking since ancient times, traditionally extracted from calf stomachs as rennet, but now predominantly produced via recombinant DNA technology in microorganisms such as Aspergillus niger, Kluyveromyces lactis, or Pichia pastoris expressing the prochymosin gene from bovine, goat, or camel sources. Recombinant forms, which comprise over 90% of global chymosin supply, offer consistent purity, high specificity (with clotting-to-proteolysis ratios around 0.91.2), and scalability, enabling efficient coagulation of cow, goat, sheep, and even camel milk while reducing reliance on animal slaughter.
The enzyme's safety for food use has been affirmed by international bodies, with no specified acceptable daily intake due to its long history of consumption and lack of genotoxicity or adverse effects in studies up to 13.2 mg/kg body weight per day in rats. Variations in chymosin from different species, such as goat chymosin showing 38% higher activity on goat milk compared to calf chymosin, allow tailored applications in specialty dairy processing, influencing cheese yield (e.g., 522 g from 3 L goat milk) and texture. Ongoing research explores chymosin's thermostability and specificity enhancements through genetic engineering to optimize performance across diverse milk types and fermentation conditions.

Biological Role and Occurrence

Natural Sources

Chymosin, also known as rennin, is primarily sourced from the abomasum, the fourth compartment of the stomach in young ruminants, where it plays a key role in neonatal milk digestion. In pre-ruminant calves (Bos taurus) under 30 days old, it constitutes over 90% of the milk-clotting activity in the abomasal mucosa, with high levels maintained by a milk diet before declining upon weaning. It is secreted as an inactive precursor, prochymosin, by chief cells and some mucous neck cells in the gastric glands of the abomasum.
Chymosin is also present in the abomasa of other young ruminants, including goats (Capra hircus), sheep (Ovis aries), and lambs, though activity levels vary by species and age. In goat and lamb rennets, chymosin accounts for a significant but generally lower proportion of total proteolytic activity compared to calf rennet, with yields influenced by diet and weaning stage; for instance, goat chymosin exhibits species-specific efficiency in clotting goat milk. These variations reflect adaptations to neonatal digestion across ruminant species.
While true chymosin is animal-derived, microbial sources produce analogous aspartyl proteases used as rennet substitutes, such as mucorpepsin from the fungus Rhizomucor miehei, which shares structural similarities but differs in specificity and is not identical to mammalian chymosin. No significant plant-based true chymosin analogs exist, though certain plant proteases mimic clotting functions.
Evolutionarily, chymosin represents a specialized aspartyl protease adapted for neonatal digestion in mammals, particularly ruminants, where it facilitates milk curdling to enhance nutrient absorption during the pre-ruminant phase; its expression is maintained by milk feeding and diminishes post-weaning, with losses observed in non-ruminant lineages like primates, where the chymosin gene has become a non-functional pseudogene. This distribution underscores its role in early mammalian development across species reliant on milk-based nutrition.

Physiological Function

Chymosin is secreted as an inactive proenzyme known as prochymosin (also called prorennin) by the gastric chief cells in the stomachs of young mammals, particularly neonates of ruminants such as calves, where its production peaks in the first few days after birth before gradually declining. This zymogen form prevents premature activity during synthesis and storage within the chief cells. Upon release into the gastric lumen, prochymosin undergoes autocatalytic activation at low pH levels, typically around 2-4, facilitated by the acidic environment created by gastric hydrochloric acid; this cleavage removes a propeptide segment, yielding the active enzyme.
The primary physiological function of chymosin in neonates is the selective hydrolysis of the Phe105Met106 bond in kappa-casein, a key component of milk casein micelles. This cleavage destabilizes the micelles, causing the release of a soluble glycomacropeptide and the aggregation of remaining caseins into an insoluble curd in the presence of calcium. By forming this semisolid curd, chymosin promotes the retention of milk in the stomach, allowing sufficient time for further enzymatic breakdown of proteins and preventing rapid passage into the intestines.
In neonates, this curdling mechanism offers several benefits, including slowed gastric emptying to enhance overall nutrient absorption, improved digestion of milk proteins by retaining them in the optimal proteolytic environment, and reduced loss of valuable whey proteins that might otherwise pass undigested. These adaptations are crucial for milk-dependent young mammals, ensuring efficient extraction of calories and amino acids from their primary food source. A transient chymosin-like activity has also been observed in the gastric fluid of some human infants, aiding similar milk clotting before it diminishes by around day 11 postpartum, after which pepsin assumes dominance.
Deficiency or impaired activation of chymosin, such as in conditions of achlorhydria (complete absence of gastric acid) or hypochlorhydria (reduced acid secretion), disrupts the low-pH environment necessary for prochymosin conversion, leading to inadequate milk curdling and compromised protein digestion in affected neonates. In historical contexts, such impairments in human infants contributed to challenges with early cow's milk-based formulas, which often failed to curdle properly without sufficient gastric acidity, resulting in rapid transit, malabsorption, and nutritional challenges observed in the early 20th century.
Compared to adults, where pepsin predominates as the main gastric protease with broader substrate specificity, chymosin exhibits higher specificity for kappa-casein in young calves and other neonates, reflecting an adaptation for milk-based diets; post-weaning, chymosin levels drop sharply as pepsin activity rises to handle diverse solid foods.

Structure and Biochemistry

Molecular Composition

Chymosin is classified as an aspartic endopeptidase with the enzyme commission number EC 3.4.23.4. In its mature bovine form, it comprises a single polypeptide chain of 323 amino acids, yielding a molecular weight of approximately 35.6 kDa. The enzyme is synthesized as an inactive precursor, prochymosin, which undergoes proteolytic activation to produce the mature form.
The bovine prochymosin gene spans about 10.5 kb and is organized into 9 exons separated by 8 introns. This genomic structure supports the production of the precursor protein in the abomasal mucosa of young ruminants.
The three-dimensional structure of bovine chymosin is bilobal, consisting of N-terminal and C-terminal domains that form a cleft containing the active site. Key catalytic residues in the active site are Asp32 and Asp215, which facilitate acid-base catalysis typical of aspartic proteases. The crystal structure of recombinant bovine chymosin was determined at 2.3 resolution in 1991 (PDB ID: 1CMS), revealing a compact fold with a central -sheet in each lobe.
Post-translational modifications in bovine chymosin include three disulfide bondsCys45Cys50, Cys206Cys210, and Cys250Cys283that contribute to structural stability and proper folding. Unlike some recombinant variants or orthologs from other species, natural bovine chymosin is not N-linked glycosylated.
Chymosin exhibits high sequence homology among ruminants, with 8590% amino acid identity to orthologs in species such as camels and yaks, reflecting conserved functional domains despite variations in enzymatic properties. This homology is lower in non-ruminant mammals, such as pigs (around 80% identity).

Activation Process

Chymosin is synthesized and secreted by the abomasum of neonatal ruminants as the inactive zymogen prochymosin, a single polypeptide chain comprising 365 amino acid residues, of which the N-terminal 42-residue propeptide occludes the active site to prevent premature enzymatic activity.
The activation of prochymosin to mature chymosin proceeds via an autocatalytic mechanism initiated under acidic conditions (pH 2-5), involving limited proteolysis that cleaves the peptide bond between Phe26 and Leu27 in the propeptide, thereby excising the inhibitory segment and liberating the active enzyme.
This process exhibits strong pH dependence, with optimal autocatalytic activation occurring at pH 3.5 and completing within 30-60 minutes at 37C; rates diminish significantly above pH 6, where the zymogen remains largely inactive.
Cleavage of the propeptide induces key structural rearrangements, including exposure of the catalytic aspartate dyad within the active site cleft and a conformational reorientation of the bilobal structure that enhances the enzyme's capacity for substrate binding.

Enzymatic Mechanism

Catalyzed Reaction

Chymosin, an aspartic protease, specifically catalyzes the hydrolysis of the Phe105-Met106 peptide bond in the C-terminal region of bovine -casein, a key glycoprotein stabilizing casein micelles in milk. This cleavage separates -casein into two fragments: the N-terminal para--casein, which remains associated with the micelle and exposes hydrophobic regions, and the soluble C-terminal glycomacropeptide (CMP), also known as caseinomacropeptide. The reaction proceeds via nucleophilic attack by a water molecule activated by the enzyme's catalytic aspartate residues, without altering the stereochemistry of the substrate or products.
The overall reaction can be summarized by the equation:

This hydrolysis destabilizes the steric and electrostatic barriers provided by the hydrophilic CMP "hairs" on the micelle surface, setting the stage for subsequent micelle aggregation.
Optimal conditions for the reaction align with those in milk coagulation processes, occurring efficiently at pH 5.56.5 and temperatures of 4045C, where the enzyme exhibits peak activity on micellar substrates. Calcium ions (Ca2+) play a crucial role in enhancing chymosin activity by increasing the solubility of colloidal calcium phosphate in micelles at these pH levels, thereby facilitating enzyme access to -casein and stabilizing the post-hydrolysis structure through charge neutralization and bridge formation between para--casein molecules.
The catalyzed process unfolds in two distinct phases: an initial enzymatic phase dominated by the specific hydrolysis of -casein (requiring 6590% cleavage for onset), followed by a non-enzymatic aggregation phase driven by hydrophobic interactions and Ca2+-mediated crosslinking of the exposed para--casein regions, leading to micelle gelation. Under optimal conditions, the stoichiometry reflects high catalytic efficiency, with a turnover number (kcat) of approximately 70 s1 for bovine chymosin acting on -casein, enabling roughly 4,200 substrate molecules to be cleaved per enzyme molecule per minute.

Substrate Specificity

Chymosin exhibits a high degree of substrate specificity as an aspartic protease, preferentially cleaving peptide bonds where the P1 position is occupied by aromatic amino acids such as phenylalanine (Phe) or tyrosine (Tyr), particularly when the P1' position features hydrophobic residues like methionine (Met) or leucine (Leu). This preference is exemplified by its primary action on the Phe105-Met106 bond in bovine -casein, which facilitates the destabilization of casein micelles during milk coagulation.
The enzyme demonstrates exceptional selectivity for -casein, with over 95% of its proteolytic activity directed toward this substrate compared to - and -caseins under typical cheese-making conditions (pH around 6.5). This exclusivity arises from kinetic efficiencies where the catalytic constant over Michaelis constant (kcat/Km) for -casein is approximately 1400 M s, versus 20.6 M s for -casein and 1.8 M s for s1-casein, resulting in a milk-clotting to general proteolytic activity ratio exceeding 20:1. Kinetic parameters for -casein hydrolysis include a Km of 39 M and kcat of 2540 s, reflecting efficient binding and turnover at neutral pH with minimal off-target proteolysis.
Chymosin is potently inhibited by pepstatin A, a competitive inhibitor that binds the active site with a Ki of approximately 2.2 10^{-7} M at pH 6.0, effectively blocking catalysis. Activity is also diminished at high pH (>7), where the aspartic residues in the active site are deprotonated, reducing catalytic efficiency, while inhibitors of serine proteases, such as PMSF, show no effect due to chymosin's distinct mechanism.
In comparison to pepsin, chymosin displays narrower substrate specificity, with a stricter preference for Glu at the P1' position in -casein-derived peptides, whereas pepsin accommodates a broader range of hydrophobic or aromatic residues at this site. This refined selectivity, evolved for the specialized role in neonatal digestion of milk proteins, minimizes non-specific degradation and enhances its utility in targeted applications like cheese production.

Production Methods

Traditional Extraction

Traditional extraction of chymosin relies on sourcing the enzyme from the abomasum, or fourth stomach, of young calves, where it naturally occurs as an inactive precursor known as prochymosin. The primary source material consists of dried calf stomachs, commonly referred to as rennet, which contain approximately 0.5-1% chymosin by weight.
The extraction process begins with the collection and preparation of abomasa from unweaned, milk-fed calves, typically under 1 month old (ideally less than 2 weeks) to ensure high chymosin content. The stomachs are washed to remove impurities, salted for preservation and to aid enzyme release, and dried to facilitate storage and handling. They are then minced into small pieces to increase surface area. Enzymes are extracted by soaking the minced tissue in a 5-10% sodium chloride solution, often combined with an acid such as hydrochloric acid to adjust the pH to 2-5, which activates prochymosin to its active form, chymosin. This acidic extraction step is typically conducted at 35-40C for several hours. The mixture is subsequently filtered or centrifuged to separate the liquid extract from solid residues, neutralized to a pH of around 5.1-5.4, and standardized for consistent activity, sometimes with added preservatives like sodium benzoate.
The resulting extract achieves a typical strength of about 1:15,000, meaning 1 gram of the preparation can clot 15,000 times its weight in milk, with a purity of approximately 80% chymosin, the remainder primarily consisting of pepsin. Standardization of the extract is measured in milk-clotting units (MCU), where 1 MCU is defined as the amount of enzyme that clots 1 mL of milk in 40 minutes at 35C.
Despite its effectiveness, traditional extraction faces several challenges. Variability in enzyme activity arises from factors such as the animal's age, diet, and post-mortem handling, with younger, milk-fed calves yielding higher chymosin proportions (up to 90%) compared to older animals. Ethical concerns arise from the use of abomasa as a byproduct of young calf slaughter in the veal industry, contributing to broader animal welfare debates. Additionally, supply is seasonal and limited by the availability of suitable calf abomasa, leading to inconsistencies and higher costs.

Recombinant Production

The development of recombinant chymosin began in the early 1980s when the cDNA encoding bovine prochymosin was cloned from calf abomasum mRNA and expressed in Escherichia coli as a proof-of-concept for microbial production. This breakthrough enabled the synthesis of active chymosin without relying on animal tissues, marking a pivotal advancement in biotechnology for food enzymes. Commercial fermentation-produced chymosin (FPC), the first genetically engineered food enzyme produced via recombinant DNA technology in microorganisms, was first approved by the FDA and launched in 1990 by Pfizer, with subsequent production scaled up by companies such as Chr. Hansen using fungal hosts.
For industrial production, fungal and yeast hosts like Aspergillus niger and Kluyveromyces lactis are preferred over bacteria due to their superior secretion capabilities and ability to add glycosylation, which enhances enzyme yield, stability, and thermostability during fermentation. The production process involves inserting the optimized prochymosin genederived from bovine or other mammalian sourcesinto an expression vector under strong constitutive or inducible promoters, followed by transformation into the host organism. The genetically modified strains are then cultured in large-scale submerged fermentation bioreactors under controlled conditions of pH, temperature, and nutrient supply to promote high-yield expression and secretion of prochymosin into the culture medium.
Post-fermentation, the enzyme is activated by acid treatment to convert prochymosin to mature chymosin, then purified using techniques such as expanded bed adsorption or ion-exchange chromatography to achieve greater than 90% purity on a protein basis, ensuring removal of host cell contaminants and impurities. Yields can exceed 1 gram per liter in optimized fungal systems, supporting efficient large-scale manufacturing.
Recombinant chymosin offers several advantages over traditional sources, including consistent milk-clotting activity standardized at strengths like 1:15,000 (where 1 part enzyme coagulates 15,000 parts milk), which ensures uniform cheese texture and yield across batches. It is certified kosher and halal, as it contains no animal-derived components beyond the gene sequence, and reduces the need for calf slaughter, aligning with ethical and sustainability goals. By the 2020s, FPC accounted for over 90% of global cheese production rennet, driven by its cost-effectiveness and reliability.
Regulatory approval underscores its safety: the FDA affirmed chymosin from E. coli K-12 as generally recognized as safe (GRAS) in 1990, the first such designation for a recombinant food enzyme. The European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) has similarly approved multiple GMO production strains, including those in A. niger and K. lactis, confirming no safety concerns from genetic modifications or residual DNA after purification.

Applications and Uses

Role in Cheese Production

Chymosin plays a central role in cheese production by catalyzing the coagulation of milk, a process essential for forming the curd that becomes cheese. In cheesemaking, chymosin is typically added to pasteurized milk after acidification by starter cultures, at concentrations of 0.001-0.005% (equivalent to 20-40 mL per 1000 L of milk, or 20-50 international milk clotting units per liter). This addition initiates clotting within 30-60 minutes at temperatures of 30-32C, where chymosin specifically cleaves the -casein molecule, destabilizing casein micelles and allowing them to aggregate into a gel network. The resulting rennet-induced curd achieves a yield of 80-90% of the milk's protein and fat content, providing the foundation for cheese structure.
The effects of chymosin extend beyond initial coagulation to influence cheese texture and flavor development. Higher calcium levels in the milk enhance curd firmness by promoting stronger micelle aggregation, leading to a more robust texture in the final product. During aging, residual chymosin activity contributes to controlled proteolysis, breaking down caseins into peptides and amino acids that develop characteristic flavors, such as the nutty notes in aged varieties. This enzymatic action is particularly vital in rennet-coagulated cheeses like Cheddar and Gouda, where precise chymosin dosing ensures optimal curd syneresis and moisture control.
Dosage requirements vary between traditional animal-derived rennet and recombinant chymosin, with the latter being more potent due to its higher purity (nearly 100% chymosin versus 80-90% in calf rennet, which includes pepsin). Recombinant forms thus require lower amountsoften 20-30% lessto achieve equivalent clotting, minimizing variability and improving consistency in industrial production. Over-dosing chymosin can lead to excessive non-specific hydrolysis, resulting in bitter flavors from unwanted peptide accumulation during ripening. To counter this, chymosin works in synergy with starter cultures, which provide acidification to optimize enzyme activity and enhance overall cheese quality through balanced fermentation.
In the United States, approximately 90% of cheese production utilizes fermentation-produced chymosin (FPC), a recombinant form developed through genetic engineering. Although produced using genetically modified microorganisms, the purified FPC enzyme does not require GMO labeling under current US FDA regulations because it contains no foreign DNA in the final product.
Globally, chymosin-based rennets are used in over 70% of cheese production, predominantly for rennet-coagulated varieties that account for the majority of the world's 25 million tons of annual output, underscoring its indispensable role in the dairy industry.

Emerging and Alternative Applications

Beyond its established role in dairy processing, chymosin has been explored for medical applications, particularly in addressing gastrointestinal disorders associated with impaired milk digestion. A 2009 patent describes the use of oral chymosin supplements to prevent and treat conditions such as infantile colic, heartburn, gastro-esophageal reflux (GER), and gastro-esophageal reflux disease (GERD) by facilitating the hydrolysis of caseins in low-acid environments, which is beneficial for infants with developing digestive systems. This approach leverages chymosin's specificity for -casein cleavage to mimic natural gastric proteolysis, potentially aiding in cases of hypochlorhydria where stomach acid production is insufficient for effective protein breakdown.
In pharmaceutical research, chymosin serves as a structural model for designing inhibitors of aspartic proteases due to its homology with therapeutically relevant enzymes. As a well-characterized member of the aspartic protease family, chymosin's three-dimensional structure has informed the development of inhibitors targeting enzymes that share catalytic mechanisms and active site features with mammalian aspartic proteases like chymosin, pepsin, and renin.
Chymosin also finds utility in research contexts, including protein engineering and biosensor development. Site-directed mutagenesis studies have modified chymosin's active site and surface loops to alter its pH optimum and stability, providing a model for engineering aspartic proteases with enhanced properties for industrial use. Additionally, biosensors have been developed to detect chymosin levels in rennet products using fluorescence assays that monitor its hydrolysis of peptide substrates, aiding in quality control for cheese manufacturing.
In alternative industries, chymosin's adoption is constrained but includes niche uses in food processing beyond cheese. For instance, small additions of chymosin have been tested to stabilize yogurt from non-bovine milks like camel milk by promoting controlled casein aggregation without full coagulation, improving texture and yield. In cosmetics, its proteolytic activity could theoretically aid protein restructuring in formulations, but practical implementation is minimal due to the enzyme's narrow substrate specificity.
Key challenges hinder broader applications of chymosin, primarily its limited stability outside acidic environments. Optimal activity occurs at pH 3.55.5, with rapid inactivation at neutral or higher pH, necessitating formulation strategies like immobilization or polymer stabilization for non-dairy uses. Furthermore, competition from more versatile microbial proteases, such as those from Rhizomucor miehei, restricts its expansion into diverse fields, as these alternatives offer greater thermostability and broader substrate ranges. Its high specificity for -casein, while advantageous in targeted roles, limits general proteolytic utility compared to less selective enzymes.

Historical Development

Early Discovery

The use of rennet, the natural source of chymosin from the stomachs of young ruminants, in cheese production has ancient origins, with the earliest unequivocal archaeological evidence coming from Neolithic sites in Poland dating to around 5500 BCE. Pottery sieves containing lipid residues of milk fats, analyzed through organic residue analysis, indicate that early Europeans processed milk into cheese using coagulants like rennet to separate curds from whey. This practice likely arose accidentally when milk was transported in animal stomachs, leading to coagulation, and spread across Europe and the Near East with the domestication of sheep and goats around 8000 BCE.
In medieval times, rennet's role was more explicitly recognized in scholarly texts, particularly in the Islamic world, where it was described as an extract from calf or lamb stomachs for curdling milk. The 10th-century Arabic cookbook Kitab al-Tabikh by Ibn Sayyar al-Warraq includes recipes for various cheeses that imply the use of animal rennet, reflecting its established application in Abbasid cuisine and medicine. Similarly, the physician al-Razi (Rhazes, d. 925 CE) discussed rennet in his pharmacological writings on dairy products and digestion, terming it a key agent for cheese formation in treatments for stomach ailments.
Scientific interest in chymosin emerged in the 19th century, with the first attempts at isolating the active enzyme from calf stomachs credited to French pharmacist Jean-Baptiste Deschamps around 1840, who named it "chymosine" after its gastric juice-like properties. Commercial standardization followed in 1874 when Danish chemist Christian Hansen developed a method to extract and powder rennet, enabling consistent use in cheese production and marking the enzyme's transition from folk remedy to industrial tool. Early biochemical studies in the late 19th and early 20th centuries revealed its protease nature, with research showing optimal activity for milk coagulation at pH around 6.36.7, with the enzyme's catalytic peak at 4.55.5, as demonstrated in experiments on enzyme kinetics during the 1890s and 1930s.
Further advancements in the mid-20th century clarified chymosin's mechanism, including its classification as an aspartic protease similar to pepsin, based on crystallization and purification techniques applied to gastric enzymes. The enzyme's zymogen form, prochymosin, was isolated and shown to activate at low pH in the stomach, a discovery building on earlier observations of pH-dependent activity. To avoid confusion with the pancreatic enzyme chymotrypsin, the name "chymosin" was formally adopted in the 1960s by researchers like Birgitte Foltmann, who sequenced and characterized it in seminal papers.

Modern Advancements

The development of recombinant chymosin began in the 1980s with the cloning of the bovine prochymosin gene and its expression in Escherichia coli, leading to Pfizer's commercialization of fermentation-produced chymosin (FPC) using genetically modified E. coli K-12 as the first genetically engineered food product affirmed by the FDA as Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) on March 23, 1990. This innovation addressed supply limitations of traditional rennet, significantly reducing reliance on animal-derived sources, and paved the way for microbial production in fungi and yeasts, transforming the industry. In the past decade, significant progress in chymosin research has centered on recombinant expression systems to enhance yield, purity, and specificity, addressing the limitations of traditional extraction methods. Advances in genetic engineering have enabled the production of chymosin variants from non-bovine sources, such as goats and camels, using microbial hosts like Pichia pastoris (now classified as Komagataella phaffii) and Kluyveromyces lactis. For instance, recombinant goat chymosin expressed in P. pastoris achieved a yield of 148,000 units/L, demonstrating superior milk-clotting activity in goat milk (8,727 U/mg) compared to recombinant calf chymosin (5,385 U/mg), with optimal activity at pH 4.55.0 and 60C. Similarly, camel-derived fermentation-produced chymosin (FPC) has shown faster coagulation times and higher curd-firming rates in bovine milk than bovine rennet or standard FPC, making it a promising alternative for industrial cheese production.
Efforts to optimize expression have shifted toward methanol-free constitutive promoters in K. phaffii, avoiding the toxicity and regulatory issues associated with inducible methanol-based systems like P_AOX1. Comparative studies of promoters such as P_TEF, P_GAP, and P_PGK revealed that P_TEF at 28C yielded up to 4,729 IMCU/L of active bovine chymosin after 48 hours, with specific activity reaching 807.76 IMCU/mg post-purificationcomparable to commercial standards in thermostability and -casein specificity. Systems biology approaches in K. lactis have further boosted recombinant calf chymosin production by fine-tuning fermentation parameters, including nutrient sources and pH, resulting in higher enzyme titers to meet global cheese demand without relying on animal slaughter.
These innovations extend to biochemical engineering for improved enzyme properties, such as reduced proteolytic activity to minimize bitterness in aged cheeses. Recombinant goat chymosin exhibited 28% lower non-specific proteolysis than its calf counterpart, enhancing cheese texture and yield in small-ruminant dairies. Microbial milk-clotting enzymes, including engineered chymosin analogs from fungi like Rhizomucor miehei, have been refined for better milk-clotting-to-proteolytic (MCA/PA) ratios, optimizing coagulation efficiency and organoleptic outcomes in diverse cheese varieties. Overall, these developments have increased the adoption of bioengineered chymosin to over 70% of global cheese production as of 2021, promoting sustainability and ethical sourcing while enabling tailored applications in specialty dairy products.
