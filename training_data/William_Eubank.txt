 William Eubank (born November 15, 1982) is an American director, screenwriter, and cinematographer recognized for his work in science fiction, horror, and action genres. His feature directorial debut, the avant-garde science fiction film Love (2011), premiered at the Sundance Film Festival and earned him the City of Athens Award for Best Director at the Athens International Film Festival. Eubank gained wider recognition with The Signal (2014), a sci-fi thriller that he co-wrote with his brother Carlyle Eubank and David Frigerio, and which premiered at Sundance before receiving a limited theatrical release. Subsequent directorial credits include the underwater horror Underwater (2020) starring Kristen Stewart, the found-footage horror Paranormal Activity: Next of Kin (2021), and the military action thriller Land of Bad (2024) featuring Russell Crowe and Liam Hemsworth.
Born in Holyoke, Massachusetts, Eubank developed an early passion for filmmaking, influenced by classics like The Twilight Zone, Stanley Kubrick's films, and low-budget sci-fi such as Pi and Moon. He began his career as a self-taught filmmaker, starting at age 18 by directing commercials for brands like Mikasa and Adidas, as well as music videos. Eubank worked as a camera technician at Panavision during and after college, where he contributed to projects during the transition to digital filmmaking, including second-unit work on films like Broken City (2013). This hands-on experience shaped his distinctive visual style, emphasizing camera-driven storytelling, precise blocking, and collaboration with cinematographers to define a film's aesthetic in preproduction.
Throughout his career, Eubank has balanced independent projects with studio assignments, often blending conceptual narratives with practical effects and grounded visuals. His films frequently explore themes of isolation, technology, and human resilience, drawing from his background in cinematography to create immersive, tension-filled environments. As of 2025, Eubank continues to develop new projects, including the thriller The Epiphany (2025) starring Sylvester Stallone and the action film Archangel (2026) starring Jim Caviezel, maintaining his reputation for innovative, genre-bending cinema.

Early life

Family background and influences

William Eubank was born on November 15, 1982. He grew up in the Santa Ynez Valley, a rural ranching community just north of Santa Barbara, California, where he spent his childhood surrounded by horses and a close-knit family environment. Eubank's early years were marked by a strong emphasis on creativity and outdoor activities.
Eubank's mother, a children's book author and illustrator, played a pivotal role in nurturing his artistic inclinations from a young age. Her work exposed him to illustration and storytelling, fostering a deep appreciation for visual arts that would later inform his filmmaking style. This familial dynamic encouraged Eubank to explore drawing and creative expression, laying the groundwork for his interest in narrative forms.
A key influence during his childhood was his grandfather, who had attended the U.S. Naval Academy at Annapolis and served as a cinematographer in the U.S. Navy. Eubank was captivated by tales of his grandfather's naval adventures, including operating cameras in challenging environments, which ignited his fascination with both military service and the technical craft of filmmaking. This inspiration led Eubank to initially aspire to join the Navy, much like his grandfather, but he ultimately pivoted toward cinema after recognizing his stronger passion for directing and visual storytelling. His grandfather's career highlighted the power of capturing real-world experiences on film, profoundly shaping Eubank's early worldview and career ambitions.

Education and early interests

William Eubank considered attending the United States Naval Academy in Annapolis but ultimately decided against it, opting instead to pursue a career in the arts due to his growing disinterest in subjects like mathematics and his burgeoning passion for filmmaking.
Although largely self-taught, Eubank attended a summer camp at the New York Film Academy at age 16, spent two years at UCLA without gaining admission to its film school, and briefly attended the Brooks Institute of Photography before dropping out. He honed his skills by breaking down filmmaking techniques methodically, such as testing in-camera effects and designing prosthetics with video cameras, often documenting his processes in detailed journals for storyboarding and pre-planning.
From a young age, Eubank developed a strong interest in cinematography, particularly inspired by science fiction narratives and practical effects that emphasized conceptual storytelling over large budgets. His early curiosity was sparked in part by tales from his grandfather, a U.S. Navy cinematographer who shared stories of operating cameras during flights. This influence, combined with admiration for works like The Twilight Zone and films by directors such as Stanley Kubrick and David Lynch, fueled his rejection of a military path in favor of visual experimentation in sci-fi genres.

Professional career

Early work at Panavision

William Eubank began his professional career in the early 2000s at Panavision's Woodland Hills facility in Los Angeles, starting as an intern after dropping out of the Brooks Institute of Photography and later advancing to roles as a camera technician, prep technician, and digital imaging technician (DIT). He held these positions for approximately six to eight years, immersing himself in the operational side of the film industry during its shift from analog to digital technologies.
Through daily hands-on tasks, Eubank acquired in-depth knowledge of camera systems, lenses, and related equipment, including early digital models like the Sony F900 and tools such as AJA cards and Blackmagic capture cards. He often described this tenure as his practical "film school," where he supplemented formal classes with real-world experience in prepping gear for productions and troubleshooting on set. This technical foundation honed his understanding of cinematography fundamentals, such as framing, aspect ratios, and the enduring value of high-quality optics amid rapid technological changes.
Eubank's position at Panavision provided valuable networking opportunities with seasoned professionals, including executives like Bob Harvey, whom he credited as a mentor and "film godfather," and colleagues such as David Dotson, fostering connections that influenced his later career path. These interactions offered insights from industry veterans and expanded his professional contacts, paving the way for future collaborations.
A key aspect of his time there was access to equipment for personal endeavors; Eubank frequently borrowed cameras, lenses, and even a techno crane from Panavision and colleagues on weekends, allowing him to experiment with short films, fake commercials, and music videos. In one instance, he retained an F900 camera at home for several months to refine his skills. This practice served as a crucial bridge from technical support to independent filmmaking, enabling him to apply his growing expertise in creative projects.

Love (2011)

Love (2011) marked William Eubank's directorial debut, originating as an experimental short film concept tied to the album Love by the alternative rock band Angels & Airwaves before evolving into a feature-length science fiction drama. Self-financed on a modest budget of around $500,000 primarily through the band's resources, the production spanned four years, with two dedicated to constructing practical sets on Eubank's parents' ranch in California's Santa Ynez Valley using inexpensive materials like MDF board, insulation, and Velcro. Shot on 35mm film to achieve a cinematic texture despite the low budget, the film emphasized minimal CGI and relied on innovative, DIY techniques for its space station interiors and Earth views captured via external projections.
Eubank took on multiple roles, including writer, director, cinematographer, and production designer, while collaborating closely with his brother Carlyle on visual effects and set fabrication to realize the film's isolated, melancholic atmosphere. This hands-on approach, honed from Eubank's prior technical experience, allowed for a visually striking depiction of solitude in space, blending practical effects with the band's score to underscore themes of isolation and human connection. The production's ingenuity extended to filming Civil War-era sequences in the family's backyard, transforming everyday spaces into immersive environments.
The film premiered on February 2, 2011, at the Santa Barbara International Film Festival, where Eubank earned a nomination for the Panavision Spirit Award for Independent Cinema, recognizing his resourceful filmmaking. It screened at additional festivals including Fantasia and Fantastic Fest, highlighting its festival circuit appeal. Following its premiere, Love received a limited theatrical rollout, including a one-night broadcast to approximately 500 U.S. theaters on August 10, 2011, before a DVD release bundled with the deluxe edition of Angels & Airwaves' album.
Critics lauded Love for its ambitious narrative exploring psychological depth and existential themes, as well as Eubank's sophisticated visual style that evoked comparisons to 2001: A Space Odyssey and Solaris despite the constraints. Reviews highlighted the film's impressive aesthetics achieved through practical ingenuity, with one noting its "undeniably impressive" visuals via "a bevy of DIY tricks." However, reception was mixed, with an aggregate score of 60% on Rotten Tomatoes from 15 reviews, some critiquing its abstract storytelling and scientific liberties. The film's festival exposure and cult following among sci-fi enthusiasts underscored Eubank's early promise as a visionary director.

The Signal (2014)

The Signal is a science fiction thriller directed by William Eubank, marking his second feature film after Love (2011). Co-written by Eubank with his brother Carlyle Eubank and David Frigerio, the script draws inspiration from classic sci-fi like The Twilight Zone and films by Stanley Kubrick and David Lynch, exploring themes of technology, deception, and human-alien integration through a narrative about three college students lured by a mysterious hacker signal during a road trip, leading to a disorienting abduction and experimentation scenario.
Production began after the script's development during the editing of Eubank's debut film, with principal photography commencing in May 2013 over a tight 28-29 day schedule in New Mexico, utilizing locations in Albuquerque, Los Lunas, and Taos, including a full shutdown of the Taos Bridge for key scenes. The film was produced by Low Spark Films and Automatik, with a modest budget of $4 million, allowing Eubank to employ resourceful techniques such as borrowing equipment from Panavision, his former employer. Challenges during shooting included extreme weather like sandstorms and rain, which the crew navigated within 12-hour days to capture the film's desert isolation and escalating tension.
The cast features Brenton Thwaites as the protagonist Nic, a brilliant but conflicted MIT student; Olivia Cooke as his girlfriend Haley; and Beau Knapp as their friend Jonah, all portraying hackers drawn into the conspiracy. Laurence Fishburne joins in a pivotal supporting role as the enigmatic Dr. Wallace Damon, a government agent overseeing the mysterious events, bringing gravitas to the thriller's interrogative sequences. Eubank blended practical effects, including prosthetics from Legacy Effects for body horror elements, with digital visual effects by Spin VFX to create the film's mind-bending transitions and otherworldly environments, enhancing the theme of blurred realities without relying on a high budget. Cinematography techniques honed from Eubank's earlier work at Panavision contributed to the film's striking 2.40:1 widescreen visuals, emphasizing atmospheric dread and visual puzzles.
The film premiered on January 22, 2014, in the Park City at Midnight section of the Sundance Film Festival, where it generated buzz for its ambitious style despite its indie constraints. Focus Features handled domestic distribution, releasing The Signal theatrically in limited release on June 13, 2014, followed by wider international rollout. It earned approximately $600,000 domestically and $2.7 million worldwide, underperforming at the box office but gaining a cult following for its inventive premise.
Reception was mixed, with critics praising the film's visual flair and Eubank's direction but often critiquing the convoluted plot twists and underdeveloped characters that undermined the thematic depth. On Rotten Tomatoes, it holds a 60% approval rating based on 87 reviews, with consensus noting its "stylish visuals" amid a "silly" narrative. Metacritic scores it at 54/100 from 34 critics, highlighting the strong cinematography and effects while faulting the story's reliance on surprises over coherence. Reviewers like Glenn Kenny of RogerEbert.com commended its "impressive" low-budget execution and potential for Eubank's future work, though others, such as Variety's Justin Lowe, described it as a "head trip better appreciated for the journey than the destination." Overall, The Signal showcased Eubank's evolution toward more commercial sci-fi, blending genre tropes with personal stylistic touches to explore paranoia in a tech-driven world.

Underwater (2020)

Underwater is a science fiction horror film directed by William Eubank, developed at 20th Century Fox through Chernin Entertainment after acquiring Brian Duffield's spec script in October 2015. The screenplay was later credited to Duffield and Adam Cozad, with Duffield also receiving story credit, focusing on a crew of deep-sea researchers facing monstrous threats following a catastrophic earthquake at an underwater drilling facility. Eubank, drawn to the script's fast-paced tension during his own project pitches, signed on to direct, leveraging his prior experience with visual effects from films like The Signal to helm the production.
Principal casting included Kristen Stewart as mechanical engineer Norah Price, Vincent Cassel as veteran captain Lucien, and John Gallagher Jr. as engineer Liam Smith, alongside T.J. Miller, Jessica Henwick, and Mamoudou Athie. Filming occurred primarily in New Orleans, Louisiana, starting in March 2017, utilizing studio tanks for underwater sequences and "dry-for-wet" techniques on dark soundstages to simulate the oppressive aquatic environment with smoke, volumetric lighting, and practical suits weighing 80 to 200 pounds. These methods, overseen by water safety expert Jim Pearson, emphasized realism in the confined, high-pressure setting, though the process challenged the cast, particularly Stewart, who has expressed discomfort with water.
The film premiered in theaters on January 10, 2020, with a budget estimated at $5065 million, but grossed only $40.9 million worldwide, marking an underperformance amid early 2020's competitive release slate. Though not delayed by the COVID-19 pandemic, its theatrical run was curtailed by the ensuing lockdowns, leading to subsequent availability on streaming platforms like Hulu, where it gained a cult following for its genre thrills.
Critics delivered mixed reviews, praising the film's tense, claustrophobic atmosphere and inventive creature designs inspired by H.P. Lovecraft's Cthulhu mythos, while noting occasional narrative muddiness in the murky visuals. Eubank highlighted the practical water effects as key to immersion, explaining that post-production refinements to bubbles and fluid dynamics over a year enhanced the horror without relying solely on CGI, creating a visceral sense of dread in the deep-sea chaos.

Paranormal Activity: Next of Kin (2021)

Paranormal Activity: Next of Kin is a 2021 found-footage supernatural horror film directed by William Eubank as the seventh installment in the Paranormal Activity franchise. Written by Christopher Landon and produced by Jason Blum and Oren Peli, the story follows aspiring filmmaker Margot, who travels to a secluded Amish community to learn about her biological mother, only to uncover demonic forces tied to her family's past.
Eubank was approached for the project due to his experience with tense, atmospheric horror in Underwater, emphasizing practical scares and immersive camerawork in the found-footage style. The cast includes Emily Bader as Margot, alongside Roland Buck III, Dan Lippert, and Henry Ayers-Brown, with filming taking place in upstate New York to capture the isolated, rural setting. Production focused on authentic Amish representations while building suspense through subtle supernatural elements, avoiding over-reliance on jump scares.
Released directly to Paramount+ on October 29, 2021, the film bypassed theaters amid the ongoing COVID-19 pandemic. It received mixed reviews, with critics noting its fresh take on the franchise's lore but criticizing formulaic elements and pacing issues. On Rotten Tomatoes, it holds a 32% approval rating from 47 reviews, while audience scores were higher at 45%. Despite divided reception, it contributed to Eubank's growing reputation in horror, blending modern found-footage techniques with cult-like community dread.

Land of Bad (2024)

Land of Bad is a 2024 American action thriller directed and co-written by William Eubank, who collaborated on the screenplay with David Frigerio. The film was produced by Volition Media Partners and Broken Open Pictures, operating on a mid-budget scale with an estimated production cost of A$25 million (approximately $16.5 million USD). This scope allowed for practical filming and effects emphasizing military realism in a survival scenario.
The cast featured Liam Hemsworth in the lead role as a Delta Force soldier, Russell Crowe as an Air Force drone pilot, and Luke Hemsworth in a key supporting part, alongside Milo Ventimiglia and Ricky Whittle. Principal photography occurred in South East Queensland, Australia, including the Gold Coast region, where lush forests and urban areas were used to simulate the Philippines setting. Production wrapped in late 2022, leveraging local incentives and facilities for an efficient shoot.
Distributed by The Avenue Entertainment, Land of Bad received a wide theatrical release in the United States on February 16, 2024. The film premiered to audiences seeking high-stakes action, aligning with Eubank's established style of tense, technology-driven thrillers.
Contemporary reception highlighted the film's strengths in action choreography and its portrayal of drone warfare operations. Critics commended the exciting set pieces and visceral combat sequences, which effectively captured the intensity of remote aerial support in ground missions. Reviewers noted Crowe's commanding performance as the drone operator, adding depth to the theme of modern warfare's detachment. On Rotten Tomatoes, it holds a 66% approval rating from critics and 93% from audiences, praised as a solid entry in the survival thriller genre despite formulaic elements.
At the box office, Land of Bad earned $4.6 million domestically and $2 million internationally, for a worldwide total of approximately $6.6 million. This performance reflected its niche appeal in the action market, bolstered by streaming availability on platforms like Netflix later in 2024.

Upcoming projects

Following the success of Land of Bad, William Eubank has two action-oriented projects slated for 2026 release.
Archangel is an action thriller directed by Eubank for Mandalay Pictures and Sony Pictures, with production handled by David Zelon and William Sherak. Jim Caviezel stars as a former Green Beret veteran and experimental weapons designer living in rural Wyoming, who must defend his community from a powerful corporation seeking to seize their land. The film, written by Chris Papasadero and Randall Wallace, is currently in pre-production as of November 2025 and is scheduled for theatrical release on November 6, 2026. Supporting cast includes Olivia Thirlby, Garret Dillahunt, and Shea Whigham.
Eubank's other announced project, The Last Druid, is a historical action film also targeting a 2026 release, with principal photography set to begin in 2025. Russell Crowe leads as a peaceful Celtic elder in ancient Caledonia who is compelled to lead a defense against the Roman Empire after an emperor uncovers a hidden Druid stronghold in the mountains. The screenplay is by Phil Gawthorne and Carlyle Eubank, marking a reunion with Crowe from Land of Bad. Amazon MGM Studios has acquired international distribution rights, including for the UK market.

Filmography

As director

William Eubank made his feature film directorial debut with Love (2011), a science fiction drama for which he also served as cinematographer. He followed this with The Signal (2014), a sci-fi thriller. Eubank also served as cinematographer on his debut feature Love (2011).
In 2020, he helmed the science fiction action thriller Underwater, starring Kristen Stewart. He directed the horror film Paranormal Activity: Next of Kin (2021), expanding the found-footage franchise. His most recent released feature is Land of Bad (2024), an action war film featuring Russell Crowe and Liam Hemsworth.
Upcoming features include the action thriller Archangel (November 6, 2026), starring Jim Caviezel, and The Last Druid (November 12, 2026), a historical action film with Russell Crowe.
Prior to his features, Eubank directed early short films, including contained sci-fi projects such as a spaceship-set story he built himself. As of 2025, he has no major television directing credits.

As cinematographer

William Eubank's work as a cinematographer draws heavily from his technical expertise gained during his time at Panavision, where he served as a digital imaging technician on various commercials and feature films, honing his skills in camera operation and visual composition.
His first major credited cinematography role came with the 2010 action thriller Caught in the Crossfire, directed by Brian A. Miller, where Eubank captured the film's tense urban sequences using dynamic close-ups and location shooting in Grand Rapids, Michigan, to heighten the narrative's intensity.
Eubank's breakthrough as cinematographer occurred on his directorial debut Love (2011), a science fiction drama set aboard the International Space Station, where he personally managed all visual elements, including the design and lighting of confined interior sets to convey isolation and psychological depth through immersive anamorphic framing.
Following Love, Eubank served as the sole cinematographer on Crave (2012), a psychological thriller directed by Charles de Lauzirika, employing naturalistic color palettes and strategic location work to underscore the protagonist's obsessive fantasies, with a focus on intimate, voyeuristic shots that enhanced the film's unsettling tone.
After Crave, Eubank transitioned primarily to directing, limiting further cinematography credits, though his visual style continued to reflect the precision and innovation from his behind-the-camera beginnings.

As screenwriter

William Eubank's screenwriting career debuted with the 2011 science fiction film Love, for which he received sole screenplay credit, marking his entry into narrative filmmaking alongside his roles as director and cinematographer. The script centers on an astronaut's psychological descent into isolation aboard a derelict space station, drawing from Eubank's interest in introspective, character-driven stories.
In 2014, Eubank co-wrote the screenplay for The Signal, collaborating with his brother Carlyle Eubank and David Frigerio, blending elements of science fiction thriller with psychological mystery. This original script, developed over several years, originated from conceptual ideas shared among the writers during their early careers in film production. The trio's joint effort emphasized narrative twists and technological intrigue, reflecting Eubank's collaborative approach to building layered, genre-bending plots.
Eubank reunited with David Frigerio for the 2024 action thriller Land of Bad, where they shared screenplay credit for an original story involving military operations and high-stakes survival. This partnership built on their prior work, incorporating real-time tension and tactical elements inspired by modern warfare scenarios. Across these projects, Eubank's writing consistently explores human resilience amid technological and existential challenges.
