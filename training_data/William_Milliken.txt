 William Grawn Milliken (March 26, 1922 October 18, 2019) was an American Republican politician who served as the 44th governor of Michigan from 1969 to 1983, holding the longest tenure of any governor in the state's history.
Born in Traverse City, Michigan, Milliken graduated from Yale University and served as a bomber pilot in the U.S. Army Air Forces during World War II, flying 50 combat missions over Europe and earning the Distinguished Flying Cross, Air Medal with three oak leaf clusters, and Purple Heart after surviving two crash landings.
Prior to the governorship, he represented the 27th district in the Michigan Senate from 1961 to 1966 and was elected lieutenant governor in 1964, ascending to governor upon George Romney's resignation to run for president.
Reelected in 1970, 1974, and 1978, Milliken's administration emphasized environmental protection, including the enactment of the Michigan Environmental Protection Act of 1970 and initiatives to reduce phosphates in Lake Erie, establishing Michigan as a leader in conservation policy.
A moderate Republican noted for bipartisanship and civility, he signed landmark civil rights legislation, advocated for state aid to Detroit amid deindustrialization and racial tensions, and navigated fiscal crises in the auto sector through pragmatic reforms, though he faced criticism for approving the controversial 650 Lifer Law mandating life sentences without parole for certain violent crimes.

Early Life and Background

Family and Upbringing

William Grawn Milliken was born on March 26, 1922, in Traverse City, Michigan, the eldest child of James Thacker Milliken, a local businessman and politician, and Hildegarde Grawn Milliken, a community activist. His father owned and operated department stores in Traverse City and Petoskey, fostering a family environment centered on commerce and civic engagement, while also serving as mayor of Traverse City and as a Michigan state senator from 1941 to 1950. Milliken's mother broke local barriers as the first woman elected to public office in Traverse City, joining the school board and promoting educational initiatives.
The family resided in northern Lower Michigan, where Milliken grew up immersed in the region's natural landscape of lakes, forests, and cherry orchards, which cultivated his lifelong appreciation for outdoor recreation and conservation. He had at least one sibling, a younger sister named Ruth, born in 1923, within a household that emphasized public service and Republican values, influenced by his father's bipartisan legislative record and the area's entrepreneurial spirit. The Millikens instilled principles of fiscal responsibility through the demands of running family enterprises, alongside a moderate political outlook shaped by pragmatic governance rather than ideological extremes. This upbringing in a politically active, business-oriented family in rural Traverse City provided Milliken with early exposure to community leadership and environmental stewardship.

Education and Military Service

Milliken enrolled at Yale University in 1940 following his graduation from Traverse City Senior High School. His undergraduate studies were interrupted in 1942 when he volunteered for service in the U.S. Army Air Forces during World War II.
As a staff sergeant and waist gunner, Milliken flew 50 combat missions aboard B-24 Liberator heavy bombers in the European and Mediterranean theaters. He survived two crash landings and sustained injuries that earned him the Purple Heart, along with the Air Medal with three oak leaf clusters. Milliken was honorably discharged in September 1945.
Returning to Yale after the war, Milliken completed his Bachelor of Arts degree in 1946. His military service and academic training fostered a disciplined perspective grounded in historical context, influencing his subsequent approach to leadership and decision-making.

Business Career

Pre-Political Ventures

After graduating from Yale University in 1947, William Milliken returned to Traverse City, Michigan, to manage the family-owned J.W. Milliken, Inc., a chain of department stores founded by his grandfather in 1889 as a clothing and dry goods retailer. The business operated stores in Traverse City, Manistee, and other northern Michigan locations, serving as a key retail anchor in the region's downtown areas during the post-World War II economic expansion. Milliken navigated retail challenges, including competition from emerging national chains and fluctuating consumer demand in a rural economy reliant on agriculture and seasonal tourism, which demanded adaptive inventory and customer-focused operations to sustain profitability.
In addition to store operations, Milliken engaged in local economic development through civic roles, serving as president of the Traverse City Chamber of Commerce and chairman of the Traverse City Planning Commission for six years in the 1950s. These positions involved promoting business growth amid Traverse City's reliance on cherry farming and emerging tourism, fostering resilience against market volatility by advocating for infrastructure improvements and community initiatives that supported small-scale capitalism. His efforts in these networks, including affiliations with Rotary and local banking boards, built practical experience in balancing private enterprise with public coordination, paving a path into Republican political circles without direct electoral involvement at the time. The family stores thrived under this stewardship until sold in 1983, reflecting sustained outcomes from hands-on management in a competitive landscape.

Political Rise

State Senate Service

Milliken was elected to the Michigan State Senate in 1960, representing the 27th District encompassing northern Michigan counties such as Emmet, Antrim, and Charlevoix. He served from 1961 to 1964, during which time he focused on issues pertinent to his rural, tourism-dependent constituency. As a legislator, Milliken supported measures to promote tourism and agriculture in northern Michigan, reflecting the region's economy centered on seasonal visitors, cherry orchards, and related farming activities.
In the final two years of his Senate tenure (19631964), Milliken ascended to the role of majority floor leader, where he helped steer Republican priorities through the chamber. He advocated for fiscal restraint, aligning with the party's emphasis on balanced state budgets amid post-World War II economic growth. His legislative style emphasized pragmatic governance over partisan rigidity.
Milliken's positions distinguished him as a moderate within the Republican caucus, particularly through bipartisan efforts on civil rights. He opposed attempts to repeal Rule 9 of the Detroit Real Estate Brokers Association, a regulation barring discriminatory practices in housing sales, thereby defending fair housing protections against conservative pushback. This cross-aisle collaboration underscored his willingness to prioritize policy outcomes over ideological purity, earning him early recognition for bridging divides in a polarized era.

Gubernatorial Elections

William Milliken ascended to the governorship on January 22, 1969, following the resignation of George Romney, who left office to serve as U.S. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development in the Nixon administration. As lieutenant governor since 1965, Milliken succeeded Romney without an election, inheriting a state facing post-1967 riot recovery and economic pressures from the automotive sector.
In the 1970 gubernatorial election held on November 3, Milliken sought a full four-year term, campaigning on continuity with Romney's moderate Republican policies amid concerns over industrial slowdowns and fiscal stability in Michigan's auto-reliant economy. He defeated Democratic nominee Sander Levin, securing 1,339,047 votes (50.41%) to Levin's 1,294,638 (48.74%), a margin of 44,409 votes that underscored Michigan's politically divided electorate with strong Democratic urban support offset by Republican suburban and rural strength.
Milliken won re-election on November 5, 1974, against Levin in a rematch, again emphasizing steady governance during national recession impacts on manufacturing. Despite a broader Democratic wave in midterm congressional races, he prevailed with 1,356,865 votes (51.07%) to Levin's 1,242,247 (46.75%), by 114,618 votes, reflecting persistent electoral competitiveness in the state.
The 1978 election on November 7 saw Milliken secure a third term against Democrat William B. Fitzgerald, benefiting from incumbency and a less polarized national environment, with 1,628,485 votes (56.80%) to Fitzgerald's approximately 1,200,000, achieving his widest margin and extending Republican hold on the office amid stabilizing auto industry conditions. These victories, particularly the narrow 1970 and 1974 outcomes, highlighted Michigan's status as a swing state with balanced partisan divisions.

Governorship (19691983)

First Term: Transition and Initial Challenges

William Milliken assumed the governorship of Michigan on January 22, 1969, following George Romney's resignation to become U.S. Secretary of Housing and Urban Development under President Richard Nixon. As lieutenant governor since 1965, Milliken facilitated a smooth transition, inheriting and continuing Romney's moderate Republican policies emphasizing fiscal responsibility, education reform, and urban revitalization amid the state's industrial economy.
Early in his tenure, Milliken confronted the persistent aftermath of the 1967 Detroit riot, which had resulted in 43 deaths, over 1,000 injuries, and widespread property damage, underscoring racial tensions and urban decay. To bridge the divide between state government and Detroit, he established a gubernatorial office in the city on February 18, 1969, signaling commitment to addressing underlying socioeconomic issues affecting Black communities without escalating confrontations.
A pivotal initial challenge emerged in 1970 with the 67-day United Auto Workers strike against General Motors, idling approximately 180,000 workers and intensifying unemployment during the national recession of 19691970, which strained Michigan's auto-dependent economy. Milliken's administration navigated the labor dispute through mediation efforts, avoiding heavy-handed intervention and demonstrating crisis management capabilities, while pursuing budgetary efficiencies to maintain state operations without resorting to severe cuts.
Milliken assembled a bipartisan administrative team prioritizing expertise and consensus-building over ideological rigidity, introducing state revenue-sharing programs to bolster municipal finances in cities like Detroit and fostering cross-party appointments to enhance governance effectiveness in a divided political landscape.

Economic and Fiscal Policies

During the early 1970s economic downturns, including the impacts of the 1970 General Motors strike that idled thousands of workers and strained state revenues, Governor Milliken prioritized budget balancing through targeted tax adjustments rather than deficit spending. In 1971, he supported legislation raising the state's personal income tax rate from 2.6 percent to 3.9 percent, effective August 1, to address fiscal shortfalls amid rising unemployment and declining auto sector output. This measure, part of a broader package, aimed to stabilize state finances without immediate deep cuts to services, though critics argued it burdened residents during industrial decline.
Milliken advocated for infrastructure investment to support economic recovery, securing gas tax increases on two occasions to fund roads and transit critical to Michigan's manufacturing logistics. In 1972, the legislature approved a 2-cent-per-gallon hike, effective February 1973, diverting a portion to mass transit while bolstering highway maintenance. A second increase followed in 1978, adding another 2 cents per gallon alongside vehicle registration fees, generating $168.5 million annually for transportation amid ongoing auto industry volatility. These hikes reflected his view that reliable infrastructure was essential for goods movement in a state dependent on production and export, even as they drew opposition from fiscal conservatives.
Facing repeated recessions tied to the auto sector's fortunes, such as the 1974-75 oil crisis and subsequent job losses, Milliken pursued diversification to reduce overreliance on manufacturing while defending industry interests. He established mechanisms like the Michigan Economic Development Authority to promote job preservation and new opportunities beyond autos, culminating in Public Act 326 of 1982, which formalized state efforts to attract diverse investments. During the 1970 GM strike, which exacerbated economic distress ahead of his reelection, Milliken managed state responses to mitigate broader fallout without direct bailouts, emphasizing negotiation over intervention. Critics, including some Republicans, faulted him for resisting more aggressive spending reductions, as seen in his 1980 proposal for targeted budget trims amid slumps, but he maintained a "rainy day" fund post-1975 to buffer future volatility.

Environmental and Conservation Efforts

Milliken's administration emphasized protection of Michigan's wetlands, water quality, and waste management through targeted legislation and regulatory reforms. In 1976, he endorsed the state's beverage container deposit law, approved by voters as Initiative Petition 1, which mandated a 10-cent refundable deposit on carbonated soft drinks, beer, and other beverages to curb roadside litter and boost recycling rates. The measure succeeded empirically, diverting billions of containers from landfills and reducing visible litter by promoting return rates that exceeded 90% in early years, thereby conserving resources and minimizing environmental degradation from disposable packaging.
In 1979, Milliken signed the Goemaere-Anderson Wetland Protection Act (Public Act 203), establishing state-level safeguards against the filling, dredging, or alteration of wetlands without permits, addressing losses from development and agriculture. This law, enacted with bipartisan backing, preserved thousands of acres of critical habitats that filter water, prevent flooding, and support biodiversity, countering pressures from expanding land use in a state with over 11,000 square miles of wetlands.
Milliken reorganized the Department of Natural Resources in 1970 via executive order, merging fragmented environmental and conservation functions to streamline oversight of forests, fisheries, and water bodies. This consolidation enabled stricter phosphorus limits in detergentsenforced despite legal challenges from manufacturerswhich curbed nutrient pollution and algal blooms in the Great Lakes, as evidenced by measurable reductions in affected waters like western Lake Erie and Lake Huron shortly after implementation.
While these initiatives preserved public access to natural amenities and mitigated industrial impacts on ecosystems, they drew opposition from business sectors, including detergent producers and resource extractors, who argued the regulations raised operational costs and hindered logging and mining activities without proportional economic benefits. Milliken's approach balanced conservation gainssuch as sustained Great Lakes usability for recreation and fisheriesagainst such critiques, prioritizing long-term resource integrity over short-term industrial expansion.

Social Policies and Civil Rights

During his governorship, Milliken signed the Elliott-Larsen Civil Rights Act into law on January 13, 1977, which prohibited discrimination in employment, housing, public accommodations, and education based on religion, race, color, national origin, sex, height, weight, or marital status; the act took effect on March 31, 1977. As a moderate Republican, Milliken's support for this expansion of anti-discrimination protections aligned with his social liberalism but drew criticism from conservatives for broadening the scope beyond traditional categories like race and religion to include personal characteristics such as weight and height.
Milliken vetoed multiple legislative attempts to restrict abortion access, including bills in 1978 and 1980 that sought to ban state-funded abortions through Medicaid, arguing that such measures disproportionately affected low-income women. From 1978 to 1982, he issued 11 vetoes blocking prohibitions on Medicaid payments for abortions, reflecting his consistent pro-choice stance despite opposition from social conservatives within his party. He also backed ratification of the Equal Rights Amendment, with Michigan approving it on May 22, 1975, under his administration, and signed a 1972 law granting women equal access to public accommodations. 
In education policy, Milliken prioritized reforms to address inequities tied to property tax reliance for school funding, proposing in 1971 to eliminate property taxes as the primary source and replace them with increases in personal income and value-added taxes to promote equal per-pupil funding across districts. These efforts aimed to reduce disparities in educational opportunities influenced by local wealth variations, though they faced resistance amid strong teacher union advocacy for maintaining funding levels.

Handling of Crises and Unrest

Milliken assumed office in January 1969, less than two years after the July 1967 Detroit riot, which had left the city in economic and social disarray with over 40 deaths and widespread property damage. To address the urban crisis, he championed the adoption of a state revenue-sharing program in 1971, which directed funds to struggling municipalities like Detroit to support local services without federal dependency, marking a pragmatic increase in state aid amid ongoing racial tensions. Rather than endorsing expansive reparative measures, Milliken's administration emphasized restoring order and stability, aligning with his moderate Republican approach to prevent further unrest while investing in housing and education to mitigate underlying grievances.
During labor disputes, such as the 67-day United Auto Workers strike against General Motors starting September 1970, which idled over 180,000 workers and exacerbated unemployment in Michigan's auto-dependent economy, Milliken acted as crisis manager without direct intervention in negotiations, focusing instead on broader economic stabilization to limit spillover effects. The strike tested state resources amid campus unrest over the Vietnam War, yet Milliken navigated these without invoking emergency powers for labor violence, prioritizing dialogue and fiscal preparedness over escalation.
In response to the 19731974 Arab oil embargo and subsequent energy shortages through the late 1970s, Milliken defended the auto industry's jobs and output, implementing conservation measures under the Emergency Powers of Governor Act, including traffic restrictions to manage fuel scarcity without heavy reliance on federal mandates. These efforts complemented his environmental priorities but centered on practical state-level adaptations to sustain manufacturing amid recessionary pressures from high energy costs.
Facing fiscal strains prelude to the 1980s downturn, including auto sector recessions, Milliken managed debt through targeted revenue measures, such as proposing a 1971 income tax increase to 3.6 percent to avert deficits and a 1982 temporary 21.7 percent hike alongside cigarette tax rises, balancing budgets during the deepest economic crisis since the Depression without broad spending cuts that could ignite further unrest. 

Post-Governorship Activities

Advocacy and Public Engagement

Following his retirement from the governorship in January 1983, Milliken sustained his environmental advocacy through involvement in regional conservation efforts. He helped establish the Center for Great Lakes, an organization that coordinated interstate cooperation on water management, leading to the Great Lakes Charter signed on June 18, 1985, by the governors of Michigan, Illinois, Indiana, Minnesota, New York, Ohio, Pennsylvania, and Wisconsin. The charter committed signatories to principles of sustainable use, pollution prevention, and coordinated policy for the Great Lakes basin, addressing threats like diversion and degradation based on hydrological data indicating the lakes' vulnerability as 20% of the world's surface freshwater.
Milliken also co-chaired the bipartisan Michigan Land Use Leadership Council in 1997 with former Democratic Attorney General Frank J. Kelley. Comprising 30 members from government, business, agriculture, and conservation sectors, the council analyzed land use patternsrevealing sprawl's encroachment on farmland and wetlands at rates exceeding 100,000 acres annually in Michiganand recommended policies for growth boundaries, incentives for clustered development, and enhanced state oversight to balance economic expansion with habitat preservation.
In his public engagements, Milliken prioritized issue-specific, cross-aisle collaboration over electoral partisanship, emphasizing Michigan-centric challenges like resource sustainability where empirical assessments of ecological and fiscal impacts informed his positions.

Political Philosophy and Views

Core Principles

Milliken's foundational beliefs centered on fiscal discipline and individual accountability, drawing from his Yale University education in political science and his early involvement in family enterprises in northern Michigan. He viewed balanced budgets as essential to sustainable governance, prioritizing expenditure restraint over expansive spending; in 1975, for instance, he reduced the state budget by $127.3 million amid economic pressures, reflecting a commitment to fiscal realism that avoided both deficits and undue austerity on core public needs. This principle extended to promoting self-reliance, where government facilitated opportunity but did not supplant personal initiative, as seen in his advocacy for equitable economic development tied to private sector vitality.
Environmental stewardship formed another pillar, approached through pragmatic resource management rather than regulatory excess. Influenced by Michigan's natural landscapes, Milliken treated conservation as a duty to preserve usable assets for future productivity, enacting policies like the 1970 Michigan Environmental Protection Act to enable citizen enforcement against pollution while balancing industrial needs. His framework emphasized empirical assessment of resource limits over speculative crises, fostering laws that protected waterways and landssuch as the 1975 Inland Lakes and Streams Actwithout crippling economic activity, aligning with a realist view of ecosystems as foundational to long-term prosperity.
In matters of personal liberty, Milliken upheld limits on state intrusion into private decisions, framing civil rights as protections against coercive authority. His pro-choice position stemmed from opposition to government-imposed barriers on individual autonomy, particularly those disproportionately affecting the disadvantaged; he vetoed 11 legislative attempts between 1975 and 1982 to bar Medicaid funding for abortions, arguing such measures denied low-income women equivalent options available to others and represented unwarranted overreach into medical and familial spheres. This stance reflected a broader principle of minimal interference in consensual adult choices, tempered by support for reasonable regulations to safeguard public order.

Relations with the Republican Party

Milliken aligned with the moderate, or Rockefeller, wing of the Republican Party during his governorship, emphasizing pragmatic governance over ideological purity, which positioned him as a counterweight to the emerging conservative ascendancy led by figures like Ronald Reagan. This affiliation drew internal party tensions, particularly as the national GOP shifted rightward in the 1970s, though Milliken faced no significant primary challenges, securing re-elections in 1970, 1974, and 1978 with substantial margins that underscored the viability of centrism in Michigan. His approach prioritized bipartisan cooperation on issues like environmental protection and fiscal restraint, often clashing with hardline conservatives who viewed such flexibility as insufficiently orthodox.
In post-governorship years, Milliken endorsed moderate Republicans such as John McCain in the 2008 presidential primary, reflecting his preference for candidates who bridged divides rather than exacerbated them. He critiqued George W. Bush's tactics as "pandering to the extreme right wing," arguing they fueled polarization and uncivil discourse within the party. By 2016, amid the GOP's populist turn under Donald Trump, Milliken distanced himself sharply, endorsing Democrat Hillary Clinton and declaring that principle superseded party loyalty, a stance he framed as rejecting divisive rhetoric that undermined Republican traditions of statesmanship.
Accusations of being a "RINO" (Republican In Name Only)a label increasingly applied to moderates in the modern partyechoed critiques Milliken encountered earlier, yet he countered such claims by citing his record of electoral dominance and policy achievements, which sustained Republican control in Michigan for over a decade without alienating core voters. This defense highlighted his belief that moderation, not extremism, had historically delivered wins, contrasting with the party's later embrace of populist insurgencies that he saw as eroding broad appeal.

Legacy and Assessments

Achievements and Positive Impacts

William Milliken's 14-year governorship from 1969 to 1983, the longest in Michigan history, fostered policy continuity amid economic volatility, allowing for consistent implementation of reforms that outlasted short-term political cycles. This extended tenure enabled sustained focus on long-term state priorities, contributing to enduring institutional stability in areas like fiscal management and resource conservation.
His establishment of a budget stabilization fund provided a mechanism to buffer against revenue shortfalls during recessions, including the 1970s downturn triggered by the General Motors strike and oil embargoes, as well as the early 1980s economic slump. This fund supported prudent fiscal navigation, averting deeper deficits and enabling targeted aid such as revenue-sharing programs that bolstered distressed cities like Detroit.
Milliken's bipartisan governance model exemplified effective collaboration in a politically divided state, where Democratic majorities often controlled the legislature; this approach facilitated passage of landmark legislation and crisis responses, setting a precedent for bridging partisan divides to achieve legislative successes.
The Michigan Environmental Protection Act of 1970, enacted under his leadership, instituted citizen standing to sue for environmental harms, yielding long-term preservation of natural assets and influencing national conservation standards. This framework has underpinned Michigan's robust wetland protections, contributing to the safeguarding of millions of acres of ecologically vital habitats against development pressures.

Criticisms and Conservative Perspectives

Conservative critics have faulted William Milliken's fiscal policies for expanding state government spending at a rate far outpacing economic growth, thereby undermining traditional Republican principles of limited government and fiscal restraint. During his tenure from 1969 to 1983, Michigan's general fund spending increased by 340 percent after adjusting for inflation, compared to a 150 percent rise in personal income, with detractors arguing this disparity reflected insufficient tax relief and contributed to long-term fiscal dependency rather than self-reliance.
Milliken's tax policies drew particular ire for multiple increases that conservatives viewed as betraying fiscal conservatism. In 1971, he proposed raising the state income tax rate from 2.6 percent to 3.6 percent for individuals and from 5.6 percent to 7.8 percent for corporations to address budget shortfalls, a move that prioritized spending continuity over cuts. Later, in 1982 amid recessionary pressures, his administration implemented a temporary 21.7 percent surcharge on the income tax, alongside hikes in cigarette and other levies, which opponents contended entrenched higher burdens without structural reforms to curb expenditures.
On social issues, Milliken's liberal stances alienated the emerging conservative base of the Republican Party, accelerating its rightward ideological shift in the 1970s and 1980s. His support for abortion rights, including opposition to restrictive legislation, and endorsement of the Equal Rights Amendment positioned him as an outlier among Republicans, fostering perceptions that moderate governors like him failed to defend traditional values and instead enabled progressive encroachments. This divergence contributed to intra-party tensions, as conservatives argued such positions eroded the GOP's cohesive opposition to cultural liberalism, paving the way for a more ideologically rigid party under figures like Ronald Reagan.
From a conservative standpoint, Milliken's environmental regulations, while pioneering, imposed undue burdens on Michigan's industrial base without commensurate economic benefits, particularly in the auto sector. The 1970 Michigan Environmental Protection Act, signed by Milliken, shifted evidentiary burdens in pollution lawsuits to defendants and empowered citizen suits, which critics contended stifled business operations through protracted litigation and compliance costs amid the state's manufacturing decline. Moderation in resisting these measures, conservatives maintain, allowed regulatory excesses that prioritized ecological goals over job preservation, ultimately facilitating left-leaning overreach in policy without robust counterbalancing from Republican leadership.

Personal Life

Family and Residences

William Milliken married Helen Wallbank in 1945; she served as Michigan's first lady from 1969 to 1983 and was a prominent advocate for the arts, establishing the Michigan Council for the Arts and chairing the traveling cultural exhibit Artrain. Helen Milliken also supported women's rights initiatives, including campaigning for abortion access and the Equal Rights Amendment, while providing steadfast personal support to her husband amid the pressures of his long governorship.
The couple had two children: a son, William G. Milliken Jr., who resided in Ann Arbor and maintained a private life outside public office, and a daughter, Elaine Wallbank Milliken, who pursued a career as a lawyer and feminist activist before her death from cancer in 1993. Both children largely avoided political prominence, reflecting the family's preference for discretion during and after Milliken's tenure.
Milliken was born and raised in Traverse City, Michigan, in a family home at 502 Washington Street, and later constructed his own residence there around 1959, to which he consistently returned even while serving as governor. During his 14 years in office, he declined to occupy the state's official governor's residence in Lansingrecently donated to Michiganand instead opted for more modest, self-managed living arrangements that aligned with his personal ethos of independence and restraint. This Traverse City property, emblematic of his roots in northern Michigan's modest commercial heritage, remained his primary base until his death in 2019.

Death and Memorials

Final Years and Passing

Following the death of his wife, Helen Milliken, from ovarian cancer on November 16, 2012, at their Traverse City home, William Milliken continued to reside there in the house he had built approximately 60 years earlier.
In his later years, Milliken's public engagements diminished as he approached his late 90s, reflecting the constraints of advanced age while maintaining a low-profile presence in the community where he was born. He died peacefully at that same residence on October 18, 2019, at 5:30 p.m., at the age of 97.

Honors and Tributes

In recognition of his military service, Milliken was inducted into the Michigan Military and Veterans Hall of Honor, acknowledging his enlistment in the U.S. Army Air Forces during World War II and receipt of honors including the Purple Heart.
Posthumously, following his death on October 18, 2019, the Michigan Legislature adopted Senate Concurrent Resolution No. 16 as a formal memorial, praising his tenure as the state's longest-serving governor and his embodiment of public service integrity. State lawmakers further issued a resolution of tribute describing him as "one of the most respected political figures" in Michigan history for his moderate Republican leadership.
Tributes emphasized bipartisan acclaim for his problem-solving and ethical governance, with commentators noting his ability to unite parties across ideological lines during economic and environmental challenges. The Michigan Environmental Council awarded him its 2020 Milliken Award posthumously for lifelong stewardship, reflecting the enduring impact of statutes like the 1970 Michigan Environmental Protection Act, which he signed and which empowered citizen lawsuits against polluters.
Earlier, in 2011, Milliken and his wife Helen received the Legacy Award from the Michigan Legacy Art Park for their leadership in conservation and public service. The Michigan Political History Society also honored him in a special tribute event, underscoring his status among the state's esteemed political leaders.
