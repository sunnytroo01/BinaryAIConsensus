 Lanxess AG is a leading German specialty chemicals company headquartered in Cologne, Germany, specializing in the development, manufacturing, and marketing of chemical intermediates, additives, specialty chemicals, plastics, and consumer protection products. Established on January 31, 2005, through a spin-off from Bayer AG, Lanxess has grown into a global enterprise with approximately 12,300 employees across 32 countries as of December 31, 2024, and reported sales of 6.4 billion in 2024.
The company's operations are organized into three main segments: Advanced Intermediates, which produces high-quality industrial intermediates for applications in agriculture, pharmaceuticals, and automotive industries; Specialty Additives, encompassing plastic additives, lubricants, rubber chemicals, and colorants used in manufacturing processes; and Consumer Protection, focusing on preservatives, disinfectants, and other products that safeguard health and the environment. Lanxess emphasizes innovation and sustainability, with a commitment to achieving climate neutrality by 2040, while serving diverse markets including automotive, construction, agriculture, and consumer goods.
Tracing its roots to Bayer's founding in 1863, Lanxess has evolved through strategic acquisitions and adaptations to market demands, positioning itself as a resilient player in the specialty chemicals sector amid global transitions toward sustainable practices. The company maintains a diversified customer base and flexible asset structures to ensure competitiveness, with its shares traded on the Frankfurt Stock Exchange under the ticker LXS.

Company Profile

Founding and Operations

Lanxess was established on July 1, 2004, through a spin-off from Bayer AG, marking the creation of an independent entity focused on chemicals and polymers; its shares were officially listed on the Frankfurt Stock Exchange on January 31, 2005. This formation allowed the company to operate autonomously, emphasizing agility and innovation in the specialty chemicals sector from its inception.
The company's headquarters are located in Cologne, Germany, following a relocation from Leverkusen in 2013 to the LANXESS Tower at Kennedyplatz 1, which centralized global management and supported operational efficiency. This move underscored Lanxess's commitment to modernizing its infrastructure while maintaining proximity to key European markets and talent pools.
As a leading specialty chemicals company, Lanxess concentrates on the development, manufacturing, and marketing of chemical intermediates, additives, and consumer protection products, serving diverse industries through innovative and sustainable offerings. Employing approximately 12,000 people across 32 countries as of 2024, the company maintains a portfolio of over 2,000 products tailored for global applications in areas like materials science and hygiene. Under the slogan "Energizing Chemistry," Lanxess pursues a mission centered on delivering sustainable solutions that advance nutrition, mobility, health, safe living, and climate protection.

Financial Overview

In fiscal year 2024, Lanxess reported revenue of 6.366 billion, representing a 5.2% decline from 6.714 billion in 2023, primarily due to lower selling prices amid market pressures. The company's EBITDA pre exceptionals rose 19.9% to 614 million from 512 million the prior year, driven by cost savings from the FORWARD! action plan and improved capacity utilization, achieving a margin of 9.6% compared to 7.6% in 2023. Net income attributable to Lanxess AG stockholders, however, resulted in a loss of 177 million, a sharp downturn from a 443 million profit in 2023, reflecting exceptional items and operational challenges despite EBITDA gains.
Profitability trends in 2024-2025 showed mixed progress amid persistent market headwinds, with EBITDA improvements signaling operational resilience but net results pressured by weak demand in key sectors. In the first nine months of 2025, Lanxess generated 80 million in net cash inflow from operating activities, down from 156 million in the same period of 2024. Third-quarter 2025 earnings highlighted these challenges, with sales falling 16.3% to 1.338 billion and a net loss of 77 million, compared to a 1 million profit in Q3 2024, attributed to volume declines and geopolitical uncertainties.
Lanxess shares (LXS) have been traded on the Prime Standard segment of the Frankfurt Stock Exchange since January 31, 2005, and are currently included in the MDAX index. The stock was part of the DAX index from September 24, 2012, to September 21, 2015. The company is also recognized for sustainability, maintaining inclusion in the Dow Jones Sustainability Indices (DJSI World and Europe) as of September 2025 and the FTSE4Good Index.
Post-2024 initiatives focused on enhancing financial stability through debt reduction and cash flow optimization, with net financial debt lowered 4.7% to 2.381 billion from 2.498 billion at year-end 2023, supported by 188 million in free cash flow. The FORWARD! program targets 150 million in annual cost reductions by 2025, while proceeds from divestitures, such as the Urethane Systems sale, are earmarked for further deleveraging, including repayment of a 500 million bond maturing in May 2025. These efforts aim to sustain liquidity reserves exceeding 2.2 billion and position the company for improved performance despite ongoing market volatility.

Global Presence

Lanxess maintains a significant global footprint, operating in 32 countries with approximately 50 production sites across Europe, North America, Asia, and Latin America. The company's headquarters are located in Cologne, Germany, serving as the central hub for its international activities. This extensive network enables Lanxess to produce and distribute specialty chemicals efficiently, supporting its role as a key player in the global chemicals industry.
In Europe, Lanxess has a particularly strong presence, with major production facilities in Germany, including the CHEMPARK in Leverkusen, which hosts critical operations for advanced intermediates and engineering materials. Other European sites include those in the Netherlands (e.g., Emmen and Port of Rotterdam), Belgium (Kallo), and France (Port Jrme), focusing on areas such as rubber chemicals and engineering plastics. In North America, the company operates around 20 production sites, primarily in the United States across 13 states and in Canada's Ontario province, with notable facilities like the Kalama site in Washington for specialty additives and the El Dorado complex in Arkansas for inorganic pigments. Latin America features operations in Brazil (e.g., Descalvado for leather chemicals) and Argentina (e.g., Merlo for natural minerals), enhancing proximity to regional markets. In Asia, production occurs in countries such as China (Qingdao), India (Jhagadia), and Singapore, where a technical center supports material protection products. These sites in emerging markets underscore Lanxess's strategy to expand in high-growth areas.
Lanxess's sales are diversified across global markets, serving diverse industries including agrochemicals, automotive, construction, pharmaceuticals, and consumer goods. The company supports this reach through a global network of 13 technical competence centers for research and development, located in key regions such as Leverkusen (Germany), Pittsburgh (USA), and Shanghai (China), which drive innovation in sustainable specialty chemicals. This infrastructure ensures that Lanxess can address customer needs in sectors like mobility, nutrition, and health on a worldwide scale, fostering long-term partnerships with international clients.

Historical Development

Origins and Spin-off from Bayer

Lanxess traces its origins to the chemical divisions of Bayer AG, which was founded in 1863 as a dyestuffs manufacturer and expanded into inorganic and organic chemicals over the subsequent decades. By the late 20th century, these divisions encompassed a broad portfolio of commodity and specialty chemicals, including intermediates and polymers, forming the foundational businesses that would later become Lanxess.
In November 2003, Bayer AG announced its decision to spin off its non-core chemicals and polymers businesses to refocus on pharmaceuticals, crop science, and high-tech materials, thereby streamlining its operations amid competitive pressures in the chemical sector. The spin-off aimed to consolidate Bayer's inorganic and organic chemicals assets into a separate entity, Lanxess, which was named on March 18, 2004, deriving from "lancer" (to advance) and "success." Bayer shareholders approved the plan on November 17, 2004, with the process executed under the German Transformation Act through asset transfers from Bayer Chemicals AG and Bayer MaterialScience AG.
Lanxess Deutschland GmbH was legally incorporated on September 30, 2004, with operations commencing economically on July 1, 2004, and the full spin-off taking legal effect on January 28, 2005, upon registration in the Commercial Register. The company achieved independence with its initial public offering on the Frankfurt Stock Exchange on January 31, 2005, where Bayer shareholders received one Lanxess share for every 10 Bayer shares held. At launch, Lanxess employed approximately 19,000 people worldwide and reported sales of 7.2 billion in its first full year of 2005, primarily focused on four segments: chemical intermediates, performance chemicals, engineering plastics, and performance rubber.
Immediately following the spin-off, Lanxess encountered early challenges from market volatility in commodity chemicals, including fluctuating raw material prices and currency effects that pressured margins in its polymers and intermediates businesses. These conditions contributed to an initial net loss, prompting a focus on cost optimization and portfolio streamlining to stabilize operations. Following the spin-off, Lanxess embarked on its path of independent development.

Early Growth and Restructuring

Following its spin-off from Bayer in 2005, Lanxess focused on stabilizing its core operations in performance polymers and advanced intermediates, which formed the backbone of its three main segments: Performance Polymers, Advanced Intermediates, and Performance Chemicals. During this period, the company navigated economic challenges, including the 2008-2009 financial crisis, by investing in global production capacity and optimizing its supply chain to enhance efficiency in these volatile markets. A significant milestone came in September 2012, when Lanxess was admitted to Germany's DAX 30 index, reflecting its growing market capitalization and recognition as a leading specialty chemicals firm.
In 2013, Lanxess relocated its global headquarters from Leverkusen to the newly renovated LANXESS Tower at Kennedyplatz 1 in Cologne's Deutz district, completing the move on September 3 with approximately 1,000 employees transitioning to the site. This strategic shift centralized corporate functions, including research, finance, and management, to better steer international operations and foster innovation in a more dynamic urban environment. The relocation did not impact production facilities, which remained distributed globally, but it underscored Lanxess's commitment to agile global leadership.
To streamline its portfolio, Lanxess pursued divestitures of non-core units, such as the sale of its Paper business unit to the Finnish Kemira Group on April 1, 2006, which generated annual sales of about 260 million and allowed reallocation of resources to higher-value areas. This effort continued into 2013, when the company announced plans to explore strategic options for underperforming activities, including the Perlon-Monofil subsidiary and certain rubber chemicals lines, with combined sales of roughly 500 million, aiming to reduce complexity and improve margins. These moves were part of a broader optimization strategy to exit low-margin operations and concentrate on technology-driven segments.
Facing pressures from commodity market volatility and raw material price fluctuations between 2005 and 2015, Lanxess strategically refocused on higher-margin specialty products, shifting away from cyclical bulk chemicals toward premium solutions in areas like engineering plastics and fine chemicals. This transition involved reducing exposure to low single-digit margin businesses and emphasizing innovation in value-added applications, such as automotive and agrochemical intermediates, to build resilience against economic downturns.
Key milestones during this era included expansions in additives and intermediates, such as the 2011 acquisition of Syngenta's material protection business to bolster biocides capabilities and the opening of an ion exchange resins plant in Jhagadia, India, in December 2012 to serve growing water treatment demands. Lanxess also achieved recognition in sustainability indices, joining the Dow Jones Sustainability Index in September 2011 and the FTSE4Good Index in March 2011, highlighting its progress in environmental and social governance practices. These developments positioned the company for sustained growth in specialty markets by 2015.

Major Acquisitions and Divestitures

Since 2016, Lanxess has pursued a strategic portfolio transformation through targeted acquisitions and divestitures, shifting focus from commodity chemicals toward high-margin specialty segments such as additives, biocides, and preservatives. This approach has involved acquiring complementary businesses to strengthen market positions and divesting non-core assets, particularly in polymers, to reduce debt and enhance resilience. By 2025, these activities enabled Lanxess to exit polymers entirely, concentrating on specialties that offer higher returns and sustainable growth.
A pivotal acquisition was Chemtura Corporation in April 2017 for an enterprise value of EUR 2.4 billion, which integrated leading flame retardants and lubricant additives into Lanxess's portfolio. The deal added approximately 2,500 employees and 20 production sites across 11 countries, significantly expanding Lanxess's North American footprint and creating a global leader in polymer additives.
In February 2020, Lanxess acquired Itibanyl Produtos Especiais Ltda. (IPEL), a Brazilian biocide manufacturer, completing the transaction on February 3 after antitrust approvals. This move bolstered Lanxess's biocide offerings in Latin America, enhancing its capabilities in active ingredients and formulations for industrial applications.
Lanxess further expanded its additives business with the acquisition of Emerald Kalama Chemical in August 2021 for an enterprise value of USD 1.075 billion (EUR 900 million). The target generated USD 425 million in sales and USD 90 million in EBITDA in 2020, primarily from plastic additives like benzophenones and hindered phenols, solidifying Lanxess's position in the consumer protection segment.
The July 2022 acquisition of International Flavors & Fragrances Inc.'s (IFF) microbial control unit for USD 1.3 billion marked another key step, making Lanxess one of the world's largest suppliers of biocides and preservatives. This transaction integrated advanced technologies for water treatment, personal care, and industrial preservation, aligning with Lanxess's emphasis on resilient, high-growth areas.
On the divestiture front, Lanxess sold its Urethane Systems business to UBE Corporation in April 2025, effective April 1, divesting a unit with annual sales of around EUR 250 million. This transaction eliminated Lanxess's remaining polymer exposure, allowing reallocation of resources to core specialties.
In September 2025, Lanxess announced the sale of its 40.94% stake in the Envalior joint venture to partner Advent International, effective April 1, 2026, for a base price of approximately EUR 1.2 billion. Envalior, formed in April 2023 as a joint venture with Advent International combining Lanxess's engineering materials business and DSM Engineering Materials, represented Lanxess's remaining involvement in high-performance polymers. The proceeds will primarily reduce net financial debt, supporting Lanxess's deleveraging strategy and financial stability amid market challenges.

Business Operations

Advanced Intermediates Segment

The Advanced Intermediates segment represents Lanxess's business unit, specializing in the production of high-quality chemical intermediates essential for various industrial applications. This segment operates through two primary business units: Advanced Industrial Intermediates, which focuses on organic and inorganic precursors, and Inorganic Pigments, which supplies specialized color pigments. As a key pillar of Lanxess's B2B supply chain, it emphasizes integrated production processes to deliver reliable, high-purity materials to downstream industries worldwide.
Key products in this segment include aniline derivatives, such as high-purity aniline used as a foundational building block for further synthesis; chlorine derivatives, notably phosphorus chlorides employed in chemical reactions; salt derivatives like anhydrite (calcium sulfate) for binding applications; and organophosphorus compounds, including phosphites, phosphorus oxides, and polyphosphoric acids for specialized processing. These products are manufactured with a focus on efficiency and sustainability, supporting the creation of innovative end-products in demanding sectors. Inorganic Pigments further contributes iron oxide and chrome oxide pigments under brands like Bayferrox and Colortherm, offering over 100 color shades for durable applications.
The segment's outputs find critical applications in agrochemicals for crop protection agents, pharmaceuticals as intermediates for active ingredients, automotive coatings for enhanced durability and color, and fine chemicals for precision manufacturing processes. For instance, aniline derivatives serve as precursors in polyurethane foams and dyes, while organophosphorus compounds act as extraction agents and defoamers in metal processing and solvents. This diverse portfolio underscores the segment's role in enabling advanced material innovations across B2B markets.
In fiscal year 2024, the Advanced Intermediates segment contributed sales of EUR 1.804 billion, accounting for approximately 28 percent of Lanxess's total group sales of EUR 6.366 billion and highlighting its substantial economic impact within the company. Production is centered at major facilities, including sites in Leverkusen and Krefeld-Uerdingen in Germany for core chemical synthesis, the Antwerp-Kallo plant in Belgium for integrated intermediates, and operations in the United States to support North American demand. These locations leverage Lanxess's global network to ensure supply chain resilience and proximity to key customers.

Specialty Additives Segment

The Specialty Additives segment of Lanxess focuses on developing, producing, and distributing additives that enhance the performance, durability, and safety of polymers and specialty chemical products for industrial processors. This segment integrates three key business unitsRhein Chemie for rubber chemicals, Polymer Additives for flame retardants and plastic enhancers, and Lubricant Additives for synthetic base oils and performance improversto deliver solutions that address material challenges across diverse manufacturing applications.
Key products in this segment include lubricant additives such as the Additin series for extreme pressure protection and oxidation resistance, flame retardants like Emerald Innovation and Firemaster (bromine- and phosphorus-based), plastic additives including plasticizers (e.g., Mesamoll) and hydrolysis stabilizers (e.g., Stabaxol), rubber chemicals for processing aids in tire and seal production, and colorants such as Macrolex for vibrant, stable pigmentation in plastics. These offerings serve critical end-use industries, including automotive for tire reinforcement and engine lubricants, construction for flame-retardant insulation and roofing materials, electronics for cable and circuit board protection, and energy sectors for turbine fluids and high-temperature hydraulic systems. For instance, flame retardants ensure compliance with safety standards in vehicle interiors and electronic housings, while lubricant additives extend equipment life in power generation and metalworking processes.
Innovations in the segment have been bolstered by the 2017 acquisition of Chemtura, which established Lanxess as a leading global supplier of flame retardants and lubricant additives, and the 2021 acquisition of Emerald Kalama Chemical, which expanded capabilities in high-performance plasticizers and industrial additives. Post-integration efforts emphasize sustainable, high-performance solutions, such as the eco-friendly variant of Additin RC 2515 for extreme pressure applications and EU Ecolabel-approved synthetic esters for motor oils, aligning with demands for reduced environmental impact in automotive and energy uses. Lanxess holds a major global market position in flame retardants and lubricant technologies, serving diverse end-markets with over 1,000 products across its units and leveraging regulatory expertise to maintain leadership in safety-critical applications.

Consumer Protection Segment

The Consumer Protection segment of Lanxess specializes in developing and marketing specialty chemicals designed to protect human health, consumer products, and environments from microbial contamination and degradation. This segment operates through four business unitsFlavors & Fragrances, Material Protection Products, Liquid Purification Technologies, and Saltigoleveraging strong brands, innovative technologies, and a global sales network to deliver high-quality solutions. These offerings focus on preventing the growth of bacteria, fungi, and other microorganisms in various end-use applications, ensuring product safety and longevity while aligning with broader sustainability goals within the company.
Key products in this segment include biocides, preservatives, and microbial control agents that inhibit microbial proliferation, as well as material protection solutions that safeguard against deterioration in polymers, coatings, and textiles. For instance, preservatives derived from bio-based raw materials, such as those sourced from vegetable oils, provide eco-friendly alternatives for formulation stability without compromising efficacy. These chemicals are formulated to meet stringent performance standards, with examples like active ingredients for disinfection and odor control in household and personal care items.
The segment's products find applications across diverse industries, including personal care where preservatives ensure the safety of cosmetics, shampoos, and deodorants by preventing microbial spoilage; food and feed preservation through antimicrobials that extend shelf life; water treatment for potable and process water purification; and paints and coatings to inhibit mold and mildew growth. In personal care formulations, for example, these solutions are used in both leave-on products like creams and rinse-off items such as body washes, maintaining efficacy across pH ranges and temperatures.
Growth in the Consumer Protection segment has been driven by strategic acquisitions that enhance capabilities in sustainable and high-margin biocides. In 2022, Lanxess acquired the microbial control business unit of International Flavors & Fragrances Inc. (IFF), bolstering its portfolio in disinfection, personal care, and material protection with annual sales of approximately 300 million at the time. Earlier, in 2020, the acquisition of Brazilian biocide manufacturer Itibanyl Produtos Especiais Ltda. (IPEL) expanded production capacity for water treatment and personal care biocides in Latin America, supporting regional market penetration. These moves have positioned the segment as a leader in eco-friendly solutions amid rising demand for sustainable preservatives.
Regulatory compliance is a cornerstone of the segment, with products adhering to global standards such as the European Union's Biocidal Products Regulation (BPR), which mandates rigorous evaluation for safety and environmental impact. Lanxess maintains dedicated regulatory support teams to secure approvals for ingredients and formulations, ensuring all biocides undergo local registrations and meet criteria for minimal human health and ecological risks. This focus enables the development of alternatives to traditional preservatives, such as those proven non-migratory in applications like leather preservation, thereby addressing consumer safety concerns.

Leadership and Governance

Board of Management

The Board of Management of LANXESS AG, the executive body responsible for the company's operational leadership, consists of four members as of 2025. This team directs the strategic direction of the specialty chemicals company, focusing on growth in high-margin segments while navigating global market challenges.
Matthias Zachert serves as Chairman of the Board of Management and Chief Executive Officer, a position he has held since April 1, 2014. His contract was extended in 2023 for five years, running until March 31, 2029. Zachert brings extensive experience in finance and the chemicals sector; he previously served as Chief Financial Officer of LANXESS from 2004 to 2011, where he established the global finance organization, and later as CFO at Merck KGaA from 2011 to 2014. In his current role, he oversees corporate communications, corporate development, legal and compliance, as well as the Flavors & Fragrances, Liquid Purification Technologies, and Material Protection Products business units, which form key parts of the Consumer Protection segment.
Oliver Stratmann is the Chief Financial Officer, appointed effective September 1, 2023. He manages financial strategy, including accounting, corporate controlling, mergers and acquisitions, tax and trade compliance, treasury, and investor relations, with a focus on sustainable financing for major deals. Stratmann joined LANXESS in 2004 from Celanese AG, where he handled controlling and investor relations, and has led the company's treasury and investor relations since 2015. His contract extends until August 31, 2026, and on November 12, 2025, he was reappointed for a further five-year term ending August 31, 2031.
Frederique van Baarle has been a member of the Board of Management and Labor Director since April 1, 2021, also serving as President and CEO of LANXESS Corporation for the Americas region since October 1, 2023. She is responsible for human resources and the Lubricant Additives, Polymer Additives, and Rhein Chemie business units, which contribute to the Specialty Additives segment. Van Baarle joined LANXESS in 2011 after a career at Royal DSM, advancing through roles in marketing, supply chain, engineering plastics, and global procurement before heading the High Performance Materials business unit in 2020. Her contract runs until March 31, 2026.
Dr. Hubert Fink joined the Board of Management on October 1, 2015, overseeing global procurement and logistics, production, technology, safety and environment, and the Asia-Pacific region. He leads the Advanced Intermediates segment, including the Advanced Industrial Intermediates, Saltigo, and Inorganic Pigments business units. Fink, who holds a doctorate in process engineering from RWTH Aachen University, began his career at Bayer AG in 1988 and headed the Advanced Industrial Intermediates business unit at LANXESS since 2011. His contract is valid until September 30, 2026.
Collectively, the Board of Management provides direct oversight of LANXESS's three core business segmentsAdvanced Intermediates, Specialty Additives, and Consumer Protectionwhile emphasizing strategic initiatives in mergers and acquisitions, operational efficiency, and sustainability integration across the value chain. Under Zachert's leadership, the board has maintained stability amid transformations, including portfolio realignments and responses to market volatility, positioning the company for long-term resilience.

Supervisory Board and Corporate Structure

The Supervisory Board of LANXESS AG consists of 12 members, equally divided between six shareholder representatives and six employee representatives, in accordance with the German Codetermination Act of 1976. As of 2025, the board is chaired by Dr. Rainier van Roessel, with Birgit Biermann serving as vice chairwoman. The shareholder representatives include Hans van Bylen, Dr. Heike Hanagarth, Pamela Knapp, Lawrence A. Rosen, and Dr. Hans-Joachim Mller, while the employee representatives comprise Birgit Biermann, Armando Dente, Dr. Carsten Hesse, Cem Gner, Iris Schmitz, and Manuela Strauch.
The Supervisory Board's primary functions include advising the Board of Management on strategic matters, appointing and dismissing its members, and overseeing the company's compliance, risk management, and overall business performance. It meets regularly to review reports from the Board of Management and ensures alignment with corporate objectives, including sustainability and governance standards.
To enhance its oversight, the Supervisory Board has established several committees: the Executive Committee, which prepares decisions on Board of Management compensation; the Audit Committee, responsible for financial reporting and auditing; the Nomination Committee, which handles proposals for executive appointments; and the Mediation Committee under Section 27(3) of the German Codetermination Act, focused on resolving deadlocks in board decisions. These committees provide specialized input and report back to the full board.
LANXESS AG operates as a German stock corporation (Aktiengesellschaft) under a two-tier governance model, with the Supervisory Board providing oversight and the Board of Management handling executive responsibilities. The company's three business segmentsAdvanced Intermediates, Specialty Additives, and Consumer Protectionreport directly to the Board of Management, ensuring streamlined operational decision-making.
As a publicly traded company listed on the Frankfurt Stock Exchange, LANXESS features diverse shareholder ownership, with institutional investors holding significant stakes and influencing governance through annual meetings and voting rights. The governance framework emphasizes environmental, social, and governance (ESG) principles, including board diversity targets such as at least 30% female representation, which the Supervisory Board met with five women members as of 2025.

Sustainability and Challenges

Sustainability Goals and Initiatives

Lanxess has committed to achieving climate neutrality for Scope 1 and 2 emissions by 2040 and net-zero emissions across its value chain by 2050, with specific targets including a 42% reduction in absolute Scope 1 and 2 greenhouse gas emissions by 2030 from a 2021 baseline. This ambition aligns with science-based targets validated by the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi), emphasizing reductions in direct emissions and purchased energy while addressing Scope 3 emissions through supplier engagement and product lifecycle improvements.
The company advances sustainability through a growing portfolio of eco-friendly products, such as bio-based additives for polymers and lubricants derived from renewable raw materials like vegetable oils. Lanxess also promotes circular economy principles in plastics and chemicals via chemical recycling processes that convert waste plastics into raw materials, alongside initiatives to increase recycling rates to 60% by 2030 in line with European industry commitments. These efforts extend to business segments, including sustainable biocides in the Consumer Protection area that utilize bio-based preservatives for household and industrial applications.
Lanxess publishes an annual Sustainability Report that details progress on environmental, social, and governance (ESG) metrics, ensuring alignment with the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) and the European Union's Green Deal objectives for a climate-neutral economy. The company invests in research and development for low-carbon technologies, such as carbon footprint calculation tools for products and processes to support climate-neutral alternatives. Additionally, biodiversity protection measures at production sites include assessments to avoid impacts on critical ecosystems and Key Biodiversity Areas.
Key achievements include consistent inclusion in the Dow Jones Sustainability Indices (DJSI), where Lanxess ranked fourth in the DJSI World for chemicals in 2024, excelling in climate strategy and water management. Progress in water stewardship is evidenced by the global LANXESS Water Program, which enhances resource efficiency and risk assessments at water-stressed sites, earning an A- rating from CDP in 2025. Supply chain transparency is bolstered through supplier audits and initiatives like the Responsible Raw Materials Initiative, promoting sustainability compliance across upstream partners.

Environmental Controversies and Compliance

In September 2025, the Southwest Clean Air Agency imposed a $2.7 million civil penalty on Lanxess Corporation for significant air quality violations at its Kalama, Washington, chemical plant, stemming from underreporting emissions of toluene and other volatile organic compounds (VOCs) from 2018 to 2023. This followed a prior fine of $112,500 issued by the same agency in 2023 for exceeding toluene emission limits and underreporting emissions during 20182021. The violations involved excess hazardous air pollutant emissions exceeding permit limits by over tenfold in multiple years, prompting enhanced regulatory oversight.
From 2019 to 2023, Lanxess faced over $1 million in environmental penalties across at least 12 U.S. cases, including water pollution, air quality breaches, and hazardous waste issues, as documented in the Violation Tracker database. These liabilities trace back to historical operations, with Lanxess inheriting air pollution violations from its Bayer originssuch as a 2000 case involving excess emissionsand from the 2011 acquisition of Chemtura, which brought forward additional legacy site contaminations.
In October 2025, the Washington State Department of Ecology issued a $4,000 penalty to Lanxess for violating its water permit by discharging inadequately treated water in the first half of 2025.
At its Elmira site near Waterloo, Canada, Lanxess has addressed groundwater contamination concerns originating from prior operators like Uniroyal, with ongoing remediation efforts including a $4.3 million system expansion in 2025 and cumulative investments of tens of millions since 2017 to treat affected aquifers via pump-and-treat methods. These measures, supported by provisions for containment and cleanup, aim to resolve legacy pollutants like chlorinated solvents in the municipal aquifer.
In response to such issues, Lanxess allocated 167 million in provisions as of December 31, 2024, for environmental remediation, including contaminated site cleanup, landfill recultivation, and water protection, down from previous years due to utilization and adjustments. The company has since implemented enhanced monitoring, compliance programs, and routine inspections at affected facilities to mitigate future risks post-fines.
Lanxess operates under key regulatory frameworks, including the European Union's REACH for chemical registration and risk management, the U.S. Toxic Substances Control Act (TSCA) for substance oversight, and local emissions standards enforced by agencies like the EPA and SWCAA, with dedicated product stewardship teams ensuring adherence.
