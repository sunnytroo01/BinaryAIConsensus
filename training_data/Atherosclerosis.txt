Atherosclerosis

1x
 Atherosclerosis is a chronic inflammatory disease of the arteries in which plaquescomposed of lipids, cholesterol, calcium, and cellular debrisaccumulate within the arterial walls, leading to narrowing, hardening, and reduced elasticity of the blood vessels. The term "atherosclerosis" was coined by German pathologist Felix Marchand in 1904 to describe the gruel-like (athero) hardening (sclerosis) of arteries. This progressive condition typically begins in childhood or adolescence and advances over decades, often remaining asymptomatic until significant obstruction occurs, at which point it can impair blood flow and trigger serious complications. As the underlying pathology for many cardiovascular diseases, atherosclerosis is a leading cause of death worldwide, accounting for approximately one-third of all deaths in developed countries as of 2023, primarily through its role in coronary artery disease, stroke, and peripheral artery disease.
The development of atherosclerosis initiates with endothelial injury or dysfunction in the arterial lining, often due to factors such as high blood pressure, turbulent blood flow at arterial branch points, or exposure to toxins like those in cigarette smoke. This damage allows low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol to infiltrate the vessel wall, where it becomes oxidized and triggers an inflammatory response involving monocytes, macrophages, and T-cells, ultimately forming fatty streaks that evolve into fibrous plaques. Inflammation is integral to this process; current scientific consensus holds that excess LDL cholesterol does not cause arterial plaque without inflammation, as no established mechanism supports plaque development independent of inflammation. Advanced plaques may rupture, leading to thrombus formation that acutely blocks arteries and causes events like myocardial infarction or ischemic stroke.
Key risk factors for atherosclerosis include modifiable elements such as dyslipidemia (elevated LDL and low HDL cholesterol), hypertension, smoking, diabetes mellitus, obesity, and sedentary lifestyle, alongside non-modifiable factors like age, male sex (until menopause in women), and genetic predispositions such as familial hypercholesterolemia. Symptoms, when present, depend on the affected arteries: coronary involvement may cause angina or heart attacks, cerebrovascular disease can lead to transient ischemic attacks or strokes, and peripheral artery disease often manifests as leg pain during exertion (claudication). Early detection through lipid profiling, imaging, and risk assessment is crucial, as lifestyle modifications, pharmacotherapy (e.g., statins), and interventions like angioplasty can mitigate progression and reduce morbidity. Moreover, intensive lifestyle interventions incorporating sustained healthy eatingsuch as low-fat vegetarian, Mediterranean, or low-carbohydrate dietsalong with exercise, stress management, and other supportive measures, have demonstrated partial regression of atherosclerotic plaques in clinical trials. In the Lifestyle Heart Trial, a low-fat (10% calories from fat) whole-foods vegetarian diet combined with exercise, stress management, and psychosocial support resulted in a 4.5% relative improvement in coronary artery stenosis after 1 year and 7.9% after 5 years. Similarly, the 2-year DIRECT-Carotid study observed approximately 5% regression in carotid artery vessel wall volume across healthy diets (Mediterranean, low-carbohydrate, or low-fat), primarily driven by weight loss and blood pressure reduction. These observations stem from multimodal interventions where dietary changes and weight loss play central roles. However, recent 2024-2025 evidence indicates that physical activity or exercise alone does not reverse atherosclerosis or cause regression of atherosclerotic plaques. Instead, high levels of physical activity are associated with higher coronary artery calcification (CAC) scores, which may reflect more stable, calcified plaques less prone to rupture, supporting a role in plaque stabilization. Physical activity also improves endothelial function, contributing to overall cardiovascular benefits and potentially slowing atherosclerosis progression, but not reversal. However, full reversal is not always possible, particularly for advanced disease, and requires consistent long-term adherence along with multiple factors; prevention and risk reduction remain primary emphases.

Overview

Definition and Characteristics

Atherosclerosis is defined as a chronic inflammatory disease characterized by the accumulation of lipids, particularly low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol, within the arterial walls, triggering an immune response that leads to the formation of plaques and progressive thickening of the intima layer. This process results in atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD), which is a leading cause of death in developed countries, contributing to approximately 30-40% of all mortality as of 2024 through its role in ischemic heart disease, stroke, and peripheral vascular disease. Unlike acute vascular events, atherosclerosis develops gradually over decades, often beginning in childhood and accelerating with age and risk factors such as hyperlipidemia.
Key pathological features include endothelial dysfunction, where injury to the arterial liningoften from hemodynamic stress or toxins like tobacco smokeimpairs nitric oxide production and promotes monocyte adhesion and infiltration. Lipid-laden foam cells form as macrophages engulf oxidized LDL via scavenger receptors, creating early fatty streaks that evolve into advanced plaques with a necrotic core of cellular debris and cholesterol crystals. A fibrous cap, composed of smooth muscle cells and collagen, overlies the plaque, but its thinning due to inflammatory proteases can lead to lumen narrowing and reduced blood flow, compromising oxygen delivery to distal tissues. These plaques are heterogeneous, ranging from stable, calcified lesions to vulnerable ones prone to rupture.
Atherosclerosis is a specific subtype of arteriosclerosis, which broadly refers to the hardening and loss of elasticity in arterial walls from various causes; while arteriosclerosis encompasses all such changes, atherosclerosis distinctly involves intimal plaque buildup driven by lipids and inflammation, rather than isolated medial calcification seen in conditions like chronic kidney disease. Medial calcification, by contrast, deposits calcium in the arterial media without significant plaque formation or luminal encroachment from intimal lesions. This distinction is crucial, as atherosclerotic plaques directly contribute to thrombotic complications, whereas medial changes primarily affect vessel compliance.
The disease predominantly affects large and medium-sized elastic and muscular arteries, including the coronary arteries supplying the heart, carotid arteries to the brain, and peripheral arteries in the legs and arms, often at sites of turbulent flow such as bifurcations. These locations experience shear stress that exacerbates endothelial injury, facilitating plaque initiation and progression.

Historical Context

Evidence of atherosclerosis dates back thousands of years, with modern imaging techniques revealing arterial plaques in ancient human remains. In 2013, the Horus Study used computed tomography (CT) scans to examine 137 mummified individuals from ancient Egypt, Nubia, Peru, and the Aleutian Islands, dating from approximately 1580 BCE to 525 CE, and identified probable or definite atherosclerosis in 34% of cases, including mummified elites from Egypt's New Kingdom period (circa 15801150 BCE). These findings, which included calcified arterial lesions in the aorta and extremities, suggest that the disease was present in pre-modern populations, potentially linked to dietary and lifestyle factors even then.
The 18th and 19th centuries marked significant progress in recognizing and describing arterial degeneration through autopsy studies. In 1755, Swiss anatomist Albrecht von Haller introduced the term "atheroma" to describe gruel-like deposits in arteries, drawing from Greek roots to characterize the fatty, porridge-like substance observed in vessel walls. By 1833, French pathologist Jean-Frdric Lobstein coined "arteriosclerosis" in his treatise Trait d'Anatomie Pathologique, referring to the hardening and calcification of arteries, a term that encompassed various forms of vascular stiffening. In 1856, German pathologist Rudolf Virchow advanced the understanding by emphasizing inflammation's central role in atherogenesis, proposing that endothelial injury and leukocyte infiltration initiated plaque formation, a concept that challenged earlier views of passive degeneration and laid the foundation for modern pathophysiology.
Early 20th-century research provided experimental and diagnostic breakthroughs. In 1913, Russian pathologist Nikolai Anitschkow and colleague S.S. Chalatow fed rabbits a cholesterol-rich diet, inducing atherosclerotic plaques identical to those in humans and establishing hypercholesterolemia as a causal factor, thus pioneering the lipid hypothesis. The development of angiography in the 1920s, first demonstrated by Portuguese neurologist Egas Moniz in 1927 using iodinated contrast and X-rays to visualize cerebral vessels, extended to coronary arteries by the 1930s, enabling non-invasive assessment of arterial narrowing and transforming clinical diagnosis. The term "atherosclerosis" itself, combining "athere" (gruel) and "sklerosis" (hardening), was refined by German pathologist Felix Marchand in 1904 to specifically denote the lipid-driven subtype of arterial disease, distinguishing it from broader arteriosclerosis. Subsequent milestones, such as the Framingham Heart Study initiated in 1948, further elucidated key risk factors like hypertension and smoking, shaping preventive strategies.

Clinical Presentation

Signs and Symptoms

Atherosclerosis often progresses asymptomatically for many years, with most individuals remaining unaware of the condition until significant arterial narrowing occurs. Symptoms typically do not manifest until the artery is narrowed by more than 70%, at which point blood flow is substantially restricted.
When symptoms emerge, they depend on the location and severity of the arterial blockages. In coronary arteries, common presentations include angina, characterized by chest pain or pressure during physical exertion or stress, and myocardial infarction, which involves severe, prolonged chest pain often radiating to the arms, neck, or jaw. Cerebral artery involvement may lead to transient ischemic attacks (TIAs), marked by temporary episodes of weakness, numbness, or vision changes, or full strokes, presenting with sudden weakness on one side of the body, speech difficulties, or confusion. Peripheral arterial disease results in intermittent claudication, a cramping pain in the legs or buttocks during walking that resolves with rest, progressing to critical limb ischemia with rest pain, ulcers, or tissue loss in advanced cases.
Acute symptoms can arise suddenly from plaque rupture or thrombosis. Stable angina causes predictable chest discomfort triggered by activity, whereas unstable angina involves unpredictable, worsening pain at rest, signaling imminent risk of infarction. In strokes due to cerebral atherosclerosis, acute signs include abrupt onset of facial droop, arm weakness, or slurred speech.
Non-specific symptoms may serve as early indicators, particularly in systemic involvement. Fatigue and shortness of breath can occur with reduced cardiac output from coronary atherosclerosis, while erectile dysfunction in men often reflects early peripheral vascular insufficiency due to impaired penile blood flow. These signs highlight the need for vigilance, as they may precede more overt complications like infarction.

Associated Complications

Atherosclerosis, through the development of unstable plaques, predisposes individuals to acute thrombotic events that manifest as life-threatening cardiovascular complications. One of the primary outcomes is myocardial infarction, occurring when atherosclerotic plaque rupture or erosion triggers thrombus formation, occluding coronary arteries and causing ischemia in the myocardium. Approximately 75% of myocardial infarctions result from such plaque disruptions. Sudden cardiac death, often the first clinical manifestation of coronary atherosclerosis, accounts for over 370,000 deaths annually in the United States alone, frequently due to ventricular arrhythmias triggered by ischemic events.
Cerebrovascular complications arise from atherosclerosis in carotid or intracranial arteries, leading to ischemic stroke via embolism or thrombosis. Ischemic stroke, the most common type, represents about 87% of all strokes and is a leading cause of disability and death worldwide, with roughly 795,000 cases reported annually in the US. Repeated ischemic events can culminate in multi-infarct dementia, a form of vascular dementia characterized by cumulative cognitive decline from multiple small infarcts, affecting up to 25-30% of ischemic stroke survivors.
Peripheral artery disease, resulting from atherosclerotic narrowing of lower extremity arteries, can progress to critical limb ischemia, culminating in gangrene and necessitating amputation in severe cases. In the United States, peripheral artery disease affects approximately 10 to 12 million individuals and increases the risk of amputation by up to 10-fold in advanced stages. Renal artery stenosis due to atherosclerosis impairs kidney perfusion, contributing to secondary hypertension and chronic kidney disease, with progression to end-stage renal failure in untreated cases.
Systemic effects extend to other vascular beds, where atherosclerosis weakens arterial walls, promoting aortic aneurysm formation and rupturea condition with high mortality rates exceeding 80% if untreated. Mesenteric ischemia, caused by occlusive plaques in intestinal arteries, leads to acute bowel infarction and is associated with mortality rates of 50-70% due to delayed diagnosis. Overall, atherosclerosis underlies approximately 50% of cardiovascular deaths globally, driving the majority of ischemic heart disease and stroke events that account for 85% of cardiovascular mortality.

Risk Factors

Modifiable Risk Factors

Modifiable risk factors for atherosclerosis encompass lifestyle and environmental elements that individuals can alter to mitigate disease progression. These factors contribute to endothelial dysfunction and plaque formation through mechanisms such as inflammation, oxidative stress, and lipid accumulation, which interact with the initial stages of atherogenesis. Addressing them is crucial, as they account for a substantial portion of preventable cardiovascular events.
Smoking is a primary modifiable risk factor, exerting direct toxic effects on the vascular endothelium by promoting oxidative damage and impairing nitric oxide production, which accelerates atherosclerotic plaque development. The risk exhibits a dose-response relationship, with smokers facing 2-4 times the likelihood of developing coronary heart disease compared to non-smokers due to enhanced endothelial injury and thrombosis.
Hypertension imposes mechanical shear stress on arterial walls, disrupting endothelial integrity and facilitating lipid infiltration and inflammatory cell adhesion, key early events in plaque initiation. Current guidelines recommend maintaining blood pressure below 130/80 mmHg in at-risk individuals to reduce this stress and lower atherosclerosis progression.
Dyslipidemia, particularly elevated low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL-C) levels above 100 mg/dL, drives cholesterol deposition in arterial walls, promoting foam cell formation and plaque buildup over time, leading to subclinical atherosclerosis; even mild elevations (e.g., 151 mg/dL) can initiate early plaque formation, increasing long-term cardiovascular risk if unaddressed. Conversely, low high-density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL-C) levels fail to adequately remove excess cholesterol from vessels, diminishing its protective role against atherogenesis.
Diabetes mellitus exacerbates atherosclerosis through chronic hyperglycemia, which induces advanced glycation end-products that trigger vascular inflammation and endothelial dysfunction. This glycemic environment accelerates plaque instability by enhancing oxidative stress and prothrombotic states in the arterial wall.
Obesity, especially visceral adiposity, releases pro-inflammatory adipokines such as leptin and resistin, which promote systemic inflammation and endothelial activation, fostering atherosclerotic lesion growth. A sedentary lifestyle compounds this risk, with prolonged inactivity associated with 1.5-2 times higher odds of cardiovascular disease due to reduced endothelial function and altered lipid metabolism.
Dietary patterns high in saturated and trans fats elevate LDL-C and can increase the number of apoB-containing lipoproteins, enhancing the retention of LDL in the arterial intima where retained LDL becomes oxidized, triggering an inflammatory response with macrophage uptake forming foam cells and contributing to plaque buildup. However, the overall link between saturated fat intake and heart disease risk remains inconsistent, with recent meta-analyses of randomized controlled trials showing no significant reduction in cardiovascular events or mortality from restricting saturated fats, depending on dietary context. Low fiber intake further heightens risk by impairing cholesterol excretion and exacerbating dyslipidemia, underscoring the protective benefits of fiber-rich diets in modulating atherogenic processes.

Non-Modifiable Risk Factors

Age is a primary non-modifiable risk factor for atherosclerosis, with the risk increasing exponentially after approximately 45 years in men and 55 years in women, coinciding with progressive arterial stiffening and accumulation of vascular damage over time. This age-related escalation is attributed to cumulative exposure to hemodynamic stress and oxidative damage, leading to endothelial dysfunction and plaque formation.
Sex differences significantly influence atherosclerosis susceptibility, with men exhibiting higher risk before menopause due to the absence of estrogen's protective effects, while postmenopausal women experience a convergence in risk as estrogen levels decline. Estrogen in premenopausal women promotes vasodilation and inhibits inflammatory processes in the vasculature, reducing plaque development; its loss post-menopause accelerates atherosclerosis progression.
Family history and genetics contribute substantially to atherosclerosis risk, with heritability estimates ranging from 40% to 60% based on twin and family studies. Specific genetic variants, such as those in the APOE gene (particularly the 4 allele), increase susceptibility by altering lipid metabolism and promoting lipid deposition in arterial walls. A family history of premature cardiovascular disease in first-degree relatives independently elevates individual risk by 1.5- to 2-fold.
Racial and ethnic backgrounds also play a role in atherosclerosis predisposition, with South Asians facing a heightened burden of premature atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease compared to other groups, partly due to genetic factors influencing lipid profiles and insulin resistance. African Americans experience higher rates of coronary heart disease events and mortality (approximately 20-30% increased compared to whites), linked to a combination of genetic predispositions, higher prevalence of traditional risk factors, and socioeconomic factors, despite similar prevalence of coronary atherosclerosis as evidenced by comparable coronary artery calcium scores. These disparities highlight the interplay of inherent biological traits with environmental influences in disease manifestation.

Emerging Risk Factors

Emerging risk factors for atherosclerosis encompass less established contributors that have gained attention through recent research, including markers of chronic inflammation, environmental exposures, and disruptions in physiological homeostasis. These factors often interact with traditional pathways, such as endothelial dysfunction and plaque formation, to accelerate disease progression.
Chronic inflammation, as indicated by elevated levels of C-reactive protein (CRP), serves as an independent predictor of atherosclerosis development and progression. High-sensitivity CRP levels exceeding 3 mg/L are associated with substantially increased vascular risk, reflecting systemic inflammation that promotes endothelial activation and plaque instability beyond conventional lipid profiles. This marker's predictive value has been demonstrated in large cohort studies, where hs-CRP independently forecasted the rate of increase in carotid intima-media thickness, a key measure of subclinical atherosclerosis. Similarly, elevated CRP correlates with the extent of coronary atherosclerosis in dyslipidemic patients, highlighting its role in linking inflammation to plaque burden.
Exposure to fine particulate matter (PM2.5) from air pollution represents an environmental risk factor that exacerbates atherosclerosis through oxidative stress mechanisms. Long-term PM2.5 exposure is linked to a 10-20% increased risk of cardiovascular events, including those driven by atherosclerotic progression, as ultrafine particles penetrate vascular walls to induce reactive oxygen species and endothelial inflammation. Reviews of epidemiological data confirm that both short- and long-term PM2.5 inhalation promotes plaque formation and instability by upregulating pro-inflammatory cytokines and impairing vasodilation. These effects are particularly pronounced in urban populations, where sustained exposure correlates with accelerated carotid atherosclerosis.
Sleep disorders, particularly obstructive sleep apnea (OSA), contribute to atherosclerosis via intermittent hypoxia and resultant oxidative stress. OSA induces repeated episodes of oxygen desaturation, which heighten cardiovascular risk by 30-50% in affected individuals, independent of obesity or hypertension, through enhanced endothelial dysfunction and platelet activation. Animal models demonstrate that chronic intermittent hypoxia directly accelerates atherosclerotic lesion development in arterial walls, mirroring human pathology. This risk ties into broader pathophysiology by amplifying inflammatory signaling in plaque-prone regions.
Chronic psychosocial stress elevates atherosclerosis susceptibility by dysregulating cortisol and sympathetic nervous system activity. Prolonged stress activates the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis, leading to sustained cortisol release that fosters vascular inflammation and lipid oxidation, thereby promoting plaque buildup. Sympathetic overactivation under stress conditions further contributes by increasing blood pressure and shear stress on arterial endothelium, accelerating intimal thickening. Prospective studies link these neuroendocrine changes to a heightened incidence of coronary events, underscoring stress as a modifiable yet underrecognized driver.
Gut microbiome dysbiosis generates pro-atherogenic metabolites like trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO), particularly from red meat consumption. Dietary L-carnitine and choline in red meat are metabolized by gut bacteria into trimethylamine, which the liver converts to TMAO, enhancing foam cell formation and platelet hyperreactivity to drive atherosclerosis. Elevated TMAO levels independently predict major adverse cardiovascular events, with dysbiosis amplifying this effect through impaired microbial diversity and increased gut permeability. Human and animal studies confirm TMAO's causal role, as its suppression via antibiotics reduces plaque progression.
Post-2020 research has identified Long COVID as a potential accelerator of atherosclerosis, primarily through persistent endothelial inflammation. Studies from 2023 show that SARS-CoV-2 infection induces lasting endotheliopathy, with viral spike protein triggering inflammatory cascades that damage vascular integrity and promote thrombotic plaque evolution. This endothelial barrier disruption correlates with accelerated subclinical atherosclerosis in recovered patients, linking post-acute sequelae to heightened cardiovascular vulnerability.

Pathophysiology

Disease Initiation

Atherosclerosis is initiated by endothelial injury, a process central to the response-to-injury hypothesis originally proposed by Russell Ross in the 1970s, which posits that damage to the arterial endothelium triggers a cascade of cellular events leading to lesion formation. This hypothesis, later refined to emphasize chronic inflammation rather than acute denudation, highlights how subtle endothelial dysfunctionwithout complete loss of the endothelial layerpromotes atherogenesis by altering vascular permeability and promoting leukocyte adhesion. Endothelial injury arises from traditional risk factors such as hypercholesterolemia, which elevates circulating low-density lipoprotein (LDL) levels, promoting plaque buildup in arteries over time and leading to subclinical atherosclerosis; even mild elevations (e.g., 151 mg/dL) can initiate early plaque formation, increasing long-term cardiovascular risk if unaddressedrecent NIH-supported research using cryo-electron microscopy has revealed the molecular structure of apolipoprotein B100 on LDL binding to the LDL receptor (LDLR), elucidating how impaired clearance, particularly due to mutations in familial hypercholesterolemia, contributes to LDL accumulation and subsequent infiltration into arterial walls, exacerbating atherosclerosisand impairs endothelial nitric oxide production, hypertension, which induces mechanical stress and oxidative damage, and smoking, which generates reactive oxygen species (ROS) that reduce vasodilatory capacity and increase pro-thrombotic tendencies.
Hemodynamic factors, particularly disturbed shear stress, further predispose specific arterial sites to initiation. Low or oscillatory shear stress at arterial bifurcations and curvatures disrupts endothelial alignment and function, upregulating pro-inflammatory genes and enhancing permeability to lipoproteins, thereby creating a focal environment for lesion onset. In these regions, the endothelium expresses increased levels of adhesion molecules, facilitating the initial attachment of circulating monocytes. Following injury, LDL particles infiltrate the subendothelial space and become retained by binding to proteoglycans. Diets high in saturated fats can increase the hepatic production of apoB-containing lipoproteins, leading to elevated numbers of LDL particles and thereby enhancing the potential for their retention in the arterial intima; subsequent oxidation by ROS or enzymes like myeloperoxidase modifies retained LDL into oxidized LDL (oxLDL), which is highly atherogenic. Current scientific consensus holds that excess LDL cholesterol does not cause artery plaque without inflammation. Excess LDL contributes to atherosclerosis by being retained in the arterial wall, where it undergoes modification (e.g., oxidation). This modified LDL triggers an inflammatory response that is essential for plaque formation, including endothelial activation, monocyte recruitment, macrophage foam cell formation, and progression to advanced plaques. Inflammation is integral to the process; no established mechanism supports plaque development independent of inflammation. Macrophages, derived from recruited monocytes, avidly uptake oxLDL via scavenger receptors (e.g., CD36, SR-A), leading to cholesterol ester accumulation and the formation of foam cellsthe hallmark of early fatty streaks.
The inflammatory cascade amplifies these early events through monocyte recruitment and cytokine signaling. Endothelial cells express vascular cell adhesion molecule-1 (VCAM-1) and intercellular adhesion molecule-1 (ICAM-1) in response to injury signals, enabling monocyte rolling, firm adhesion, and transmigration into the intima; VCAM-1 plays a predominant role in this selective recruitment of inflammatory monocytes. Once in the intima, these monocytes differentiate into macrophages, which release pro-inflammatory cytokines such as interleukin-1 (IL-1) and tumor necrosis factor- (TNF-), perpetuating endothelial activation and further monocyte influx while promoting oxLDL uptake. This self-sustaining inflammatory loop, integrated into the updated response-to-injury framework, underscores how initial endothelial perturbation evolves into chronic vascular inflammation.

Plaque Development and Components

Atherosclerotic plaques develop progressively from fatty streaks to advanced fibroatheromas, characterized by a complex, multilayered structure that includes a lipid-rich necrotic core, a protective fibrous cap, and transitional shoulder regions. The necrotic core forms through the accumulation of extracellular lipids, cellular debris, and apoptotic remnants, primarily derived from modified low-density lipoprotein (LDL) particles that undergo oxidation and aggregation following their initial retention in the arterial intima. This core expands as foam cellslipid-laden macrophages and vascular smooth muscle cells (VSMCs)undergo apoptosis, releasing cholesterol and other contents that contribute to the acellular, necrotic material. The fibrous cap overlies the necrotic core and consists mainly of VSMCs embedded in a collagen-rich extracellular matrix, providing structural integrity to the plaque; it is thickest in stable lesions but can vary in composition, with type I collagen predominating. Shoulder regions, located at the lateral edges of eccentric plaques, represent areas of transition between the cap and the arterial wall, often containing fewer cells and more proteoglycans, which may influence local plaque dynamics.
Key cellular components drive plaque maturation and maintenance. Foam cells are central, originating from monocyte-derived macrophages that engulf oxidized LDL via scavenger receptors, becoming engorged with cholesteryl esters and forming lipid droplets; VSMCs also contribute significantly to foam cell populations, comprising up to 50% or more in some plaques through similar lipid uptake mechanisms. VSMCs migrate from the media into the intima, proliferate, and switch from a contractile to a synthetic phenotype, producing extracellular matrix proteins while incorporating lipids. T-lymphocytes, particularly CD4+ T helper 1 cells, infiltrate the plaque and exacerbate inflammation by secreting cytokines such as interferon-, which activates macrophages and promotes VSMC proliferation.
Lipid components within the plaque amplify inflammatory processes. Cholesterol crystals precipitate from free cholesterol in the necrotic core, acting as danger signals that activate the NLRP3 inflammasome in macrophages, leading to interleukin-1 release and sustained inflammation. Oxidized phospholipids, generated from modified LDL, further contribute by stimulating endothelial dysfunction and immune cell recruitment, fostering a proinflammatory milieu that sustains plaque growth.
The extracellular matrix plays a crucial role in plaque architecture and lipid retention. Proteoglycans, such as biglycan and decorin, bind apoB-containing lipoproteins like LDL, trapping them in the subendothelial space and promoting their modification, which is essential for foam cell formation and plaque initiation. These interactions enhance LDL aggregation and oxidation, perpetuating the cycle of lipid accumulation.
Calcification emerges as plaques mature, involving a spectrum from microcalcification to macrocalcification. Microcalcifications arise as small calcium deposits around apoptotic cells and matrix vesicles in the necrotic core or fibrous cap, often linked to osteogenic differentiation of VSMCs and macrophages. These evolve into larger macrocalcifications through coalescence, forming sheet-like or nodular structures that resemble bone tissue, potentially stabilizing the plaque by increasing its rigidity; in advanced cases, bone-like formations with marrow elements can occur, though rarely. This process is regulated by inflammatory signals and matrix remodeling, with microcalcifications initially associated with active disease progression.

Plaque Progression and Instability

Atherosclerotic plaques progress through distinct stages, beginning with fatty streaksearly accumulations of lipid-laden macrophages in the arterial intimaand advancing to more complex fibroatheromas characterized by a fibrous cap overlying a lipid-rich necrotic core. This evolution involves smooth muscle cell migration and proliferation, extracellular matrix deposition, and continued lipid accumulation, transforming the lesion into a fibroatheroma that can occupy a significant portion of the vessel wall. Neovascularization, the formation of new microvessels from the vasa vasorum, emerges prominently in advanced plaques, promoting intraplaque hemorrhage by facilitating red blood cell extravasation and accelerating lesion growth through increased inflammatory cell influx.
As plaques enlarge, they cause luminal stenosis, with narrowing exceeding 50% often leading to downstream ischemia due to reduced blood flow and oxygen delivery to perfused tissues. In response, the body may develop collateral vesselsalternative arterial pathways that bypass the stenotic segmentto partially compensate for the hypoperfusion, though this adaptation is typically insufficient in acute settings.
Plaque instability arises from structural vulnerabilities, including a thin fibrous cap measuring less than 65 m in thickness, which is prone to mechanical failure under hemodynamic stress. A large necrotic core, comprising more than 40% of the plaque area and filled with cellular debris and lipids, further compromises stability, while dense macrophage infiltration weakens the cap by releasing proinflammatory cytokines and proteases.
Rupture of the fibrous cap exposes the thrombogenic necrotic core to circulating blood, triggering platelet activation and thrombus formation that can acutely occlude the vessel. Matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs), particularly MMP-2 and MMP-9 secreted by macrophages, degrade the extracellular matrix of the cap, thinning it and heightening rupture risk.
Certain conditions accelerate plaque progression, leading to rapid occlusion; in diabetes, hyperglycemia and insulin resistance promote oxidative stress and endothelial dysfunction, fostering faster lipid accumulation and inflammation within plaques. Similarly, post-injury scenarios, such as endothelial denudation from mechanical trauma, initiate accelerated atherosclerosis by exposing subendothelial layers to blood components, resulting in swift smooth muscle proliferation and thrombus-mediated narrowing.

Diagnosis

Clinical Evaluation

Clinical evaluation of suspected atherosclerosis begins with a detailed history taking to identify symptom onset and screen for risk factors. Patients are questioned about the timing, duration, and characteristics of symptoms such as exertional chest pain or leg claudication, which may indicate ischemic events due to arterial narrowing. Risk factor screening includes family history of premature cardiovascular disease, smoking history quantified in pack-years, hypertension, diabetes, and obesity, as these contribute to disease susceptibility.
The physical examination focuses on signs of vascular compromise and lipid abnormalities. Auscultation for bruits over the carotid and femoral arteries detects turbulent flow from stenotic lesions, while palpation for pulse deficits in peripheral arteries suggests occlusion or reduced flow. Xanthomas, particularly tendon xanthomas on the Achilles or extensor tendons, serve as clinical indicators of underlying dyslipidemia predisposing to atherosclerosis. These findings help localize potential sites of plaque buildup and guide further assessment.
Risk stratification employs validated tools to estimate 10-year cardiovascular disease risk and inform management urgency. The Framingham Risk Score integrates age, sex, total cholesterol, HDL cholesterol, blood pressure, diabetes status, and smoking to predict hard coronary events or overall CVD risk in primary prevention settings. For European populations, the SCORE (Systematic COronary Risk Evaluation) calculator assesses fatal CVD risk based on similar factors, with updates like SCORE2 incorporating non-fatal events for broader applicability. Symptoms such as angina guide this evaluation by elevating pre-test probability.
In acute presentations potentially linked to atherosclerotic complications, the ABCDE approach structures the initial assessment, prioritizing airway and breathing to stabilize the patient before addressing circulation deficits from ischemia, though chronic evaluations shift emphasis to comprehensive risk factor profiling.
Differential diagnosis requires distinguishing atherosclerosis from mimics like vasculitis or embolism, which may present with similar ischemic symptoms but differ in systemic features or acute onset; for instance, vasculitis often involves inflammatory markers or multi-organ involvement, while embolism suggests a proximal cardiac source.

Diagnostic Tests and Imaging

Diagnosis of atherosclerosis relies on a combination of laboratory tests and imaging modalities to assess risk, detect plaque presence, and evaluate disease extent. Blood tests form the cornerstone of initial evaluation by identifying key biochemical markers associated with atherosclerotic processes. A lipid panel measures levels of low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol, high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol, and triglycerides, with elevated LDL (>130 mg/dL) and triglycerides (>150 mg/dL) indicating increased risk, while low HDL (<40 mg/dL in men, <50 mg/dL in women) correlates with plaque progression. Glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) testing screens for diabetes, as levels 6.5% are linked to accelerated atherosclerosis due to hyperglycemia-induced endothelial damage. High-sensitivity C-reactive protein (hs-CRP) quantifies systemic inflammation, with values >2 mg/L signaling heightened cardiovascular risk from inflammatory plaque instability.
Non-invasive imaging techniques provide early, accessible detection without procedural risks. Carotid intima-media thickness (IMT) ultrasound measures the thickness of the arterial wall in the common carotid artery, where IMT >0.9 mm suggests subclinical atherosclerosis and predicts coronary events. The ankle-brachial index (ABI) assesses peripheral artery disease by comparing systolic blood pressures in the arms and ankles, with ABI <0.9 indicating obstructive atherosclerosis and >1.4 suggesting vessel stiffness.
Advanced imaging offers detailed characterization of plaque burden and composition. Computed tomography (CT) angiography, including coronary artery calcium scoring via the Agatston method, quantifies calcified plaques, where scores >100 denote moderate plaque burden and >400 indicate high plaque burden associated with elevated risk for major adverse cardiovascular events. Detection of high plaque burden on such imaging modalities should prompt detailed discussion with a cardiologist regarding the extent of disease, risk implications, treatment recommendations (such as plaque-stabilizing medications and lifestyle changes), need for additional tests, potential interventions, monitoring strategies, and symptoms requiring immediate attention (see Treatment). Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) evaluates plaque components such as lipid cores and fibrous caps, aiding in the identification of vulnerable lesions through high-resolution vessel wall imaging.
Invasive procedures are reserved for symptomatic patients or high-risk cases to precisely delineate stenosis and plaque morphology. Coronary angiography visualizes luminal narrowing, categorizing stenosis severity as mild (<50%), moderate (50-70%), or severe (>70%), guiding potential interventions. Intravascular ultrasound (IVUS) provides cross-sectional views of plaque volume and composition during catheterization, revealing thin-cap fibroatheromas indicative of rupture risk. Optical coherence tomography (OCT) offers superior resolution (10-20 m) for assessing cap thickness and macrophage infiltration, with caps <65 m identifying high-risk plaques.
In the 2020s, artificial intelligence (AI) has enhanced diagnostic precision, particularly in imaging analysis. AI algorithms integrated into CT and ultrasound tools automate plaque detection and risk stratification. As of 2025, the FDA has cleared AI systems for coronary computed tomography angiography (CCTA) plaque analysis, such as HeartFlow Plaque Analysis, which quantifies plaque volume and type with 95% agreement to invasive intravascular ultrasound, enabling improved identification of high-risk features like lipid-rich plaques for earlier intervention.

Prevention

Lifestyle Interventions

Lifestyle interventions form the cornerstone of preventing atherosclerosis progression and potentially inducing partial regression of established plaques by targeting modifiable risk factors such as dyslipidemia, hypertension, and inflammation through behavioral changes. These strategies, supported by major clinical guidelines, emphasize sustainable habits that improve endothelial function, reduce plaque formation and burden, and enhance overall cardiovascular resilience. Evidence from large-scale trials demonstrates that comprehensive lifestyle modifications can substantially lower the incidence of atherosclerotic events, with benefits from individual components such as diet and exercise reducing risk by approximately 30%.
Dietary guidelines prioritize patterns rich in anti-inflammatory and cardioprotective nutrients to mitigate atherosclerosis. The Mediterranean diet, characterized by high intake of omega-3 fatty acids from fish, fruits, vegetables, nuts, and olive oil, has been shown to reduce major cardiovascular events by approximately 30% in high-risk individuals. This diet limits saturated fats to less than 7% of total daily calories to lower low-density lipoprotein cholesterol levels and plaque buildup. Additionally, sodium intake should be restricted to under 2,300 mg per dayideally 1,500 mg for those with hypertensionto decrease blood pressure and vascular stiffness, key drivers of endothelial dysfunction.
Intensive lifestyle modifications that include sustained healthy eating can partially reverse some effects of poor diet on atherosclerosis, particularly plaque buildup, although complete reversal is rare, especially for advanced disease, and requires consistent long-term adherence combined with other factors such as exercise. In the Lifestyle Heart Trial, a very low-fat (approximately 10% of calories from fat) whole-foods vegetarian diet combined with aerobic exercise, stress management, and group psychosocial support resulted in regression of coronary atherosclerosis, with an average 4.5% relative improvement in percent diameter stenosis after 1 year and a 7.9% relative improvement after 5 years. Similarly, the 2-year DIRECT-Carotid study demonstrated approximately 5% regression in carotid artery vessel wall volume across healthy dietary patterns (low-fat, Mediterranean, and low-carbohydrate), an effect primarily driven by weight loss and reductions in blood pressure. These findings support the role of lifestyle interventions not only in prevention but also in mitigating established atherosclerotic disease.
Regular physical activity is essential for atherosclerosis prevention, promoting vasodilation, improved lipid profiles, and reduced inflammation. Guidelines recommend at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise, such as brisk walking, which can decrease cardiovascular risk by about 30% by enhancing arterial compliance and lowering oxidative stress. Complementing this, resistance training 2-3 days per week helps preserve muscle mass, particularly in older adults, and further reduces blood pressure and insulin resistance, contributing to plaque stabilization. Recent evidence from 2024-2025 indicates that physical activity does not cause regression or reversal of atherosclerotic plaques. However, high levels of physical activity are associated with higher coronary artery calcification (CAC) scores, which may reflect more stable, calcified plaques less prone to rupture, supporting a role in plaque stabilization. Physical activity also improves endothelial function, slows atherosclerosis progression, and provides overall cardiovascular benefits.
Smoking cessation is one of the most impactful interventions, as tobacco use accelerates atherosclerosis through endothelial injury and thrombosis. Quitting leads to an immediate halving of coronary heart disease risk within 1-2 years, with benefits accruing further over time. Effective aids include nicotine replacement therapy, which doubles quit rates by alleviating withdrawal, and varenicline, a partial nicotine agonist that reduces cravings and achieves higher abstinence rates than placebo.
Weight management targets a body mass index (BMI) of 18.5-24.9 to minimize visceral fat accumulation, which promotes systemic inflammation and atherogenesis. For individuals with severe obesity (BMI 40), bariatric surgery can reduce cardiovascular risk by 40-50% through substantial and sustained weight loss, improving metabolic parameters like glycemia and hypertension.
Adequate sleep hygiene, aiming for 7-9 hours per night, helps mitigate emerging risks like disrupted circadian rhythms that exacerbate inflammation and endothelial dysfunction in atherosclerosis. Short or long sleep durations increase cardiovascular event risk by 20-30%, while consistent quality sleep supports vascular repair and blood pressure regulation.

Pharmacological Strategies

Pharmacological strategies for preventing atherosclerosis target modifiable risk factors such as dyslipidemia, hypertension, and thrombotic tendencies in high-risk individuals, encompassing both primary prevention in those without established disease and secondary prevention following an initial event. These approaches aim to stabilize plaques, reduce lipid accumulation, and mitigate inflammatory processes that contribute to lesion progression. Evidence from randomized controlled trials and guidelines supports their use in populations with elevated 10-year atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD) risk, often integrated with risk assessment tools like the PREVENT equations.
Statins, or HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors, represent the cornerstone of lipid-lowering therapy for atherosclerosis prevention by substantially reducing low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL-C) levels, which is central to plaque formation. For instance, moderate-intensity statins such as atorvastatin at 20-40 mg daily typically achieve LDL-C reductions of 30-50%, thereby slowing plaque progression and reducing cardiovascular events in both primary and secondary settings. Beyond lipid modulation, statins exhibit pleiotropic effects, including anti-inflammatory actions that decrease endothelial dysfunction and stabilize atherosclerotic plaques, independent of cholesterol lowering. These benefits are particularly pronounced in high-risk groups, such as those with diabetes or prior events, where statins have demonstrated up to 25-35% relative risk reductions in major vascular events.
Antihypertensive agents are essential for blood pressure control to prevent shear stress-induced endothelial injury and plaque rupture in atherosclerosis-prone individuals. Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors, like lisinopril, and angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs) are preferred first-line options, as they not only lower blood pressure but also offer cardioprotective effects by reducing vascular inflammation and improving endothelial function. In primary prevention, these agents have shown a 20-30% reduction in cardiovascular events among hypertensive patients at risk for ASCVD. For secondary prevention post-event, beta-blockers are commonly added to manage heart rate and myocardial oxygen demand, further mitigating ischemic risks.
Antiplatelet therapy plays a key role in secondary prevention by inhibiting thrombus formation on ruptured plaques. Low-dose aspirin at 81 mg daily is recommended for individuals with established ASCVD, reducing the risk of recurrent myocardial infarction or stroke by approximately 20% without excessive bleeding in most cases. In patients undergoing coronary stent placement, dual antiplatelet therapy with aspirin plus clopidogrel is standard, as clopidogrela P2Y12 inhibitorenhances platelet inhibition and lowers stent thrombosis risk by 20-30% during the initial months post-procedure.
Emerging pharmacological options address refractory cases or specific high-risk subgroups. Proprotein convertase subtilisin/kexin type 9 (PCSK9) inhibitors, such as evolocumab, are indicated for patients with familial hypercholesterolemia who do not achieve adequate LDL-C control with statins, achieving additional reductions of 50-60% and slowing atherosclerosis progression in genetic dyslipidemias. In individuals with diabetesa major atherosclerosis acceleratorsodium-glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors provide cardiovascular benefits beyond glycemic control, including a 14-20% reduction in major adverse cardiovascular events through anti-inflammatory and hemodynamic effects. Glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) receptor agonists, such as semaglutide, have also shown cardiovascular benefits in obese individuals without diabetes, reducing major adverse cardiovascular events by approximately 20%.
Major guidelines, including the 2018 AHA/ACC cholesterol management recommendations (with reaffirmations in subsequent updates), endorse initiating moderate- to high-intensity statin therapy for adults aged 40-75 years with a 10-year ASCVD risk greater than 7.5%, prioritizing those with diabetes or LDL-C levels above 190 mg/dL. These strategies should be personalized based on risk-benefit assessments, with ongoing monitoring for adherence and side effects to optimize long-term prevention.
While pharmacological agents such as statins are effective for slowing atherosclerosis progression, stabilizing plaques, and in some cases inducing regression, no over-the-counter supplement is proven to reliably reverse arterial plaque. Plaque regression is primarily achieved through intensive medical treatments like high-intensity statins or prescription medications (e.g., high-dose purified EPA as icosapent ethyl). Some preliminary or small studies suggest potential benefits in slowing progression or limited regression with substances like aged garlic extract, omega-3 fatty acids, or tricaprin (in specific rare conditions), but evidence is insufficient for general use in reversing plaque. For further details on plaque regression, see the Treatment section.

Treatment

Medical Therapies

Medical therapies for established atherosclerosis primarily aim to stabilize plaques, reduce inflammation, and mitigate thrombotic risks through targeted pharmacological interventions. These approaches are recommended for patients with symptomatic or advanced disease to prevent major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE), such as myocardial infarction or stroke. Guidelines emphasize a multimodal strategy, integrating lipid management, antithrombotic agents, and control of comorbidities like diabetes, based on evidence from large-scale clinical trials. Atherosclerosis progression can be halted, and some plaque regression is possible with aggressive lipid-lowering (e.g., statins, PCSK9 inhibitors) in combination with intensive lifestyle interventions such as strict diet, exercise, stress management, and blood pressure control; for instance, the Lifestyle Heart Trial demonstrated partial regression of coronary atherosclerosis, with a 4.5% relative improvement in percent diameter stenosis after 1 year and a 7.9% relative improvement after 5 years, through a comprehensive program including a low-fat vegetarian diet, exercise, stress management, and support. Similarly, the DIRECT-Carotid study reported approximately 5% regression in carotid vessel wall volume after 2 years across healthy diets (Mediterranean, low-carbohydrate, or low-fat), primarily driven by weight loss and blood pressure reduction. Recent evidence from 20242025 indicates that physical activity and exercise do not induce regression or reversal of atherosclerotic plaques but support plaque stabilization, with high levels of physical activity associated with higher coronary artery calcification (CAC) scores that may reflect more stable, calcified plaques less prone to rupture, alongside improvements in endothelial function that contribute to slowing progression. Complete reversal of advanced plaque is rare and not reliably achievable.
No over-the-counter supplement is proven to reliably reverse arterial plaque. Plaque regression is primarily achieved through aggressive medical treatments, including high-intensity statins, PCSK9 inhibitors, and certain prescription medications such as high-dose purified EPA (icosapent ethyl). While some preliminary or small-scale studies have suggested potential benefits in slowing progression or achieving limited regression with substances such as aged garlic extract, omega-3 fatty acids, or tricaprin (particularly in rare conditions such as triglyceride deposit cardiomyovasculopathy), the evidence is currently insufficient to support their general use for reversing atherosclerosis.
Intensive lipid-lowering therapy forms the cornerstone of management, focusing on aggressive reduction of low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL-C) to levels below 55 mg/dL in patients with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD). High-intensity statins, such as atorvastatin 40-80 mg daily or rosuvastatin 20-40 mg daily, are first-line agents, demonstrating a 20-30% relative reduction in MACE through plaque stabilization and regression in trials like IMPROVE-IT and FOURIER. If LDL-C remains above target levels (e.g., 55 mg/dL) on maximal statin therapy, ezetimibe 10 mg daily is added, further lowering LDL-C by 15-25% and reducing cardiovascular events by an additional 6% in post-acute coronary syndrome (ACS) patients, as shown in the IMPROVE-IT trial. If LDL-C remains above target on maximal statin + ezetimibe, add a PCSK9 inhibitor (e.g., evolocumab 140 mg subcutaneously every 2 weeks) or inclisiran 284 mg subcutaneously after initial dose, which further reduce LDL-C by 50-60% and MACE by ~20%, as shown in the FOURIER and ORION-4 trials. For statin-intolerant individuals, bempedoic acid 180 mg daily serves as an alternative, inhibiting ATP citrate lyase to reduce LDL-C by approximately 18-25% and MACE by 13% in the CLEAR Outcomes trial, particularly benefiting those with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD). The 2025 ESC/EAS guidelines endorse this stepwise intensification to achieve LDL-C targets and attenuate atherosclerosis progression.
In patients with atherosclerosis and comorbid atrial fibrillation (AF), anticoagulation is essential to prevent thromboembolic events. Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs), such as apixaban 5 mg twice daily, are preferred over warfarin due to superior efficacy and safety profiles, reducing stroke risk by 20-30% with lower rates of intracranial hemorrhage in trials like ARISTOTLE. The 2025 guidelines recommend DOACs for non-valvular AF in those with stable atherosclerotic disease, balancing ischemic and bleeding risks through tools like the CHA2DS2-VASc score.
For individuals with diabetes and atherosclerosis, stringent glycemic control reduces cardiovascular complications. Metformin, at doses of 500-2000 mg daily, is the preferred first-line agent, associated with a 15-20% reduction in all-cause mortality and MACE in observational data from the UK Prospective Diabetes Study (UKPDS) follow-up, likely through improvements in endothelial function and insulin sensitivity. Glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) receptor agonists, such as semaglutide 1 mg weekly or liraglutide 1.8 mg daily, offer additional benefits, lowering MACE by 15-20% in diabetic patients with established ASCVD, as evidenced by the LEADER and SUSTAIN-6 trials, via mechanisms including weight loss and anti-atherosclerotic effects. Sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 (SGLT2) inhibitors, such as empagliflozin 10 mg daily, are also recommended, reducing MACE by 14% in patients with type 2 diabetes and ASCVD, as shown in the EMPA-REG OUTCOME trial, through cardioprotective effects including reduced heart failure hospitalizations. These agents are prioritized in guidelines for diabetics with high cardiovascular risk.
Anti-inflammatory therapies target residual inflammatory risk in atherosclerosis. Low-dose colchicine 0.5 mg daily reduces MACE by 31% in patients with stable ASCVD, as demonstrated in the 2023 LoDoCo2 trial, by inhibiting neutrophil activity and inflammasome signaling without significant increase in adverse events. This is supported by the COLCOT trial in post-MI patients, confirming efficacy for secondary prevention. Additionally, interleukin-1 (IL-1) inhibitors, such as canakinumab, have demonstrated reductions in cardiovascular events. In the CANTOS trial, canakinumab reduced major cardiovascular events by 15-20% in patients with persistent elevation of high-sensitivity C-reactive protein (hsCRP) levels. The FDA approved this regimen in 2023 for reducing CV events in established ASCVD.
Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) is standard post-ACS to prevent stent thrombosis and recurrent ischemia. Aspirin 81 mg daily combined with ticagrelor 90 mg twice daily for 12 months reduces MACE by 16% compared to clopidogrel in the PLATO trial, with guidelines recommending this regimen for ACS patients undergoing percutaneous coronary intervention. Duration may extend beyond 12 months in select low-bleeding-risk cases, per 2025 ACC/AHA updates.

Surgical and Procedural Interventions

Percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) is a minimally invasive procedure used to treat severe coronary atherosclerosis by restoring blood flow in narrowed or blocked arteries. It typically involves balloon angioplasty to dilate the vessel, often followed by placement of a drug-eluting stent to prevent re-narrowing. This approach is particularly effective for single-vessel disease, achieving angiographic success rates exceeding 90% in uncomplicated cases, as demonstrated in large randomized trials evaluating stable coronary artery disease. Drug-eluting stents have significantly reduced restenosis rates compared to bare-metal stents, with contemporary guidelines recommending their use in most PCI procedures for atherosclerotic lesions to improve long-term patency and reduce the need for repeat interventions.
Coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) serves as a surgical option for patients with multi-vessel coronary atherosclerosis or left main disease, where multiple grafts are used to reroute blood flow around blockages. The internal mammary artery is the preferred conduit due to its superior long-term patency, with rates of 85% to 95% at 10 years, outperforming saphenous vein grafts which exhibit higher occlusion rates over time. CABG is recommended by guidelines for complex multi-vessel disease, particularly in patients with diabetes, as it provides durable revascularization and improved survival compared to PCI in such scenarios.
Carotid endarterectomy involves surgical removal of atherosclerotic plaque from the carotid artery to prevent stroke in patients with symptomatic high-grade stenosis. For individuals with greater than 70% stenosis, this procedure reduces the two-year risk of ipsilateral stroke by approximately 65% relative to medical therapy alone, based on landmark trial data showing a drop from 26% to 9% absolute risk. It is indicated primarily for symptomatic patients, with perioperative risks of stroke or death around 6%, but offering substantial long-term benefits in stroke prevention.
Peripheral interventions address atherosclerosis in the lower extremities, particularly in peripheral artery disease (PAD), using techniques such as atherectomy to debulk plaque or bypass surgery to create alternative flow paths. Endovascular approaches, including angioplasty and stenting, are often preferred for their lower initial morbidity compared to open bypass, though bypass may yield higher one-year patency in complex femoropopliteal lesions. Guidelines emphasize a tailored strategy, with endovascular therapy suitable for claudication and critical limb ischemia in select anatomies, while open procedures are reserved for extensive disease or endovascular failure.
Aortic procedures target atherosclerotic aneurysms, with endovascular aneurysm repair (EVAR) emerging as the standard for abdominal aortic aneurysms due to reduced perioperative mortality versus open repair. EVAR involves deploying a stent graft via catheter to exclude the aneurysm sac, achieving lower 30-day mortality rates (around 1-2%) and faster recovery, though long-term surveillance is required to monitor for endoleaks. It is recommended for aneurysms greater than 5.5 cm in diameter, with guidelines prioritizing it in patients with suitable anatomy to minimize operative risks.

Questions to Ask Your Cardiologist

Patients with atherosclerosis and high plaque burden, often indicated by a high coronary artery calcium score or extensive plaque on imaging, may benefit from preparing specific questions to discuss with their cardiologist. These questions help clarify personal risk, treatment options, diagnostic needs, lifestyle priorities, procedural considerations, monitoring approaches, and warning signs:

What is the extent of my arterial narrowing/blockage, and how does my high plaque burden affect my risk of heart attack, stroke, or other events?

What treatments do you recommend to stabilize plaque, slow progression, or reduce risk (e.g., statins, other medications, lifestyle changes)?

Are additional tests needed (e.g., stress test, angiogram) to assess ischemia or progression?

What specific diet, exercise, or lifestyle changes should I prioritize to manage this?

Do I need procedures like stenting, bypass, or other interventions?

How will we monitor my condition and know if treatment is working?

Are there symptoms I should watch for and report immediately?

These questions align with patient engagement recommendations for managing coronary artery disease and related conditions.

Epidemiology

Global Burden and Prevalence

Atherosclerosis underlies the majority of cardiovascular diseases (CVDs), affecting an estimated 439 million people globally with clinical manifestations of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD), including 239 million with ischemic heart disease (IHD), 77.8 million with ischemic stroke, and 122 million with peripheral artery disease (PAD) in 2023, according to the Global Burden of Disease (GBD) Study 2023. These figures reflect a 1.4-fold increase in overall CVD prevalence since 1990, driven by population aging and growth, though age-standardized rates have declined in high-income regions. While subclinical atherosclerosis is widespread even in younger adults, clinical disease predominantly emerges later in life, contributing to ASCVD as the leading cause of morbidity worldwide.
The global mortality burden from atherosclerosis-related conditions is profound, accounting for approximately 19.2 million CVD deaths in 2023, representing about one-third of all global deaths, with 85% of these due to heart attacks and strokes stemming from atherosclerotic processes. Specifically, IHD caused 8.91 million deaths and ischemic stroke 3.28 million in the same year, per GBD 2023 estimates. In terms of disability, atherosclerotic diseases resulted in 437 million disability-adjusted life years (DALYs) lost globally in 2023, a rise from 320 million in 1990, underscoring the condition's impact on healthy life expectancy.
Prevalence and burden exhibit marked age and sex patterns, with disease occurrence rising exponentially after age 50 and exceeding 50% in individuals over 65 years across vascular beds like coronary and carotid arteries. Males experience higher rates earlier in life due to factors such as greater exposure to smoking and dyslipidemia, maintaining predominance until ages 75 and beyond, after which postmenopausal hormonal changes lead to comparable or higher rates in women.
Regionally, the highest age-standardized DALY rates for atherosclerotic conditions occur in Eastern Europe and Central Asia, where IHD age-standardized prevalence rates exceed 5% in high-risk populations like those in Ukraine and Estonia. In contrast, rates are lower in high-income Asia Pacific regions, but Asia overall is witnessing a surge in ASCVD prevalence due to rapid urbanization, dietary shifts, and increasing obesity, with projections indicating a near doubling of cases by 2050. These variations highlight the interplay of socioeconomic transitions and risk factors in driving global disparities.

Trends and Disparities

In high-income countries, age-standardized cardiovascular disease (CVD) mortality rates have declined substantially since 1990, with median reductions exceeding 50% observed between 1990 and 2021, largely attributable to effective prevention strategies including smoking cessation programs, improved hypertension management, and statin use. This downward trend reflects broader public health advancements, such as dietary guidelines and widespread screening, which have curbed the progression of atherosclerosis-related conditions like ischemic heart disease.
Conversely, in low- and middle-income countries, the burden of atherosclerosis is projected to rise significantly, with global CVD cases expected to increase from 523 million in 2019 to higher levels by 2030, driven by aging populations, urbanization, and rising obesity rates in these regions. Forecasts indicate that low sociodemographic index (SDI) areas, particularly in Oceania and Central Asia, will experience the most pronounced escalations in CVD incidence and mortality, potentially doubling the disease burden in vulnerable subgroups by the end of the decade.
Socioeconomic disparities exacerbate atherosclerosis prevalence, with individuals in low socioeconomic status (SES) groups facing approximately 1.5 times higher risk of CVD events compared to higher SES counterparts, often linked to limited access to preventive care and higher exposure to environmental stressors. Racial inequities are evident in the United States, where Black Americans experience about 20% higher age-adjusted rates of heart disease and up to 40% higher stroke incidence than White Americans, influenced by systemic barriers including healthcare access and chronic stress. Gender trends show a narrowing disparity post-menopause, as women's CVD risk accelerates due to estrogen decline, approaching or surpassing men's rates in older age groups, compounded by social determinants like unequal access to cardiovascular screening.
The COVID-19 pandemic has further intensified these dynamics, with 2025 studies revealing accelerated atherosclerosis progression in survivors, including up to 15-20% faster vascular aging and increased carotid intima-media thickness, particularly among women and those with severe infections. This post-acute effect underscores the need for targeted monitoring in disparity-affected populations to mitigate long-term atherosclerotic complications.

Societal Impact

Economic Consequences

Atherosclerosis imposes a profound economic burden on healthcare systems and societies worldwide, primarily through its role as the underlying pathology in cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) such as coronary artery disease and stroke. Direct costs encompass healthcare expenditures including diagnostics, treatments, and hospitalizations, while indirect costs arise from lost productivity due to morbidity, disability, and premature mortality. These costs strain public health budgets and national economies, particularly in low- and middle-income countries where access to care is limited.
Global direct and indirect costs for CVDs were estimated at $863 billion in 2010, with projections indicating a rise to approximately $1 trillion annually by 2030, driven by increasing prevalence and aging populations. In the United States, direct healthcare costs for CVDs reached $393 billion in 2020, of which hospitalizations accounted for a substantial portionaround 27% or $108 billion in cardiovascular-related admissions in 2021highlighting the role of acute events in escalating expenditures. For instance, percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) procedures for atherosclerosis-related blockages average about $20,000 per case in the US, while coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) can exceed $100,000, reflecting the high expense of invasive revascularization therapies.
Indirect costs further amplify the burden, with global estimates for lost wages and productivity due to CVD-related disability and death contributing roughly $500 billion annually, based on extrapolations from national data and economic models. In the US, these indirect costs totaled $234 billion in 2020, primarily from premature mortality and reduced workforce participation. The widespread availability of generic statins has mitigated some long-term expenses, as these medications for cholesterol management now cost under $10 per month per patient, reducing overall pharmacotherapy outlays compared to branded alternatives.
Investments in prevention offer significant returns; lifestyle interventions targeting risk factors like smoking, diet, and physical inactivity yield a return on investment of $3 to $15 for every dollar spent, through averted events and healthcare savings realized within 1-2 years. Looking ahead to 2025 and beyond, the economic burden is projected to escalate to $1.5 trillion globally by mid-century, with aging populations in Asiawhere crude CVD mortality is expected to increase 91% from 2025 to 2050driving much of the rise due to heightened demand for care in rapidly urbanizing regions.

Research Frontiers

Recent genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified over 250 genetic loci associated with coronary artery disease (CAD), a primary manifestation of atherosclerosis, through meta-analyses involving more than one million individuals. These loci implicate key pathways including lipid metabolism, inflammation, and vascular remodeling, with examples such as the PCSK9 gene variants (e.g., rs11206510) linked to moderate reductions in low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL-C) levels and decreased CAD risk. In 2024 studies, variants in genes like GUCY1A3 have been highlighted for their role in endothelial function and plaque stability.
CRISPR-based gene editing has emerged as a promising approach for monogenic forms of atherosclerosis, particularly familial hypercholesterolemia (FH) driven by PCSK9 mutations. Preclinical applications of CRISPR-Cas9 targeting PCSK9 in hepatic cells have demonstrated durable LDL-C reductions by up to 60% in animal models, paving the way for clinical translation. By 2025, base editing trials for heterozygous FH, such as those using adenine base editors to downregulate PCSK9, have shown preliminary reductions in LDL-C levels in early cohorts, validating the approach for preventing atherosclerotic progression.
The 2017 CANTOS trial established the role of inflammation targeting in atherosclerosis by demonstrating that canakinumab, an IL-1 inhibitor, reduced recurrent cardiovascular events by 15% in patients with prior myocardial infarction and elevated hs-CRP, independent of lipid lowering. This landmark study, involving over 10,000 participants, highlighted a 37% reduction in hs-CRP with the 150 mg dose over 3.7 years of follow-up. Building on this, ongoing trials of IL-6 inhibitors like ziltivekimab in 2024-2025 have shown significant reductions in inflammatory biomarkers such as hs-CRP and fibrinogen in high-risk atherosclerotic patients, with phase 3 studies (e.g., ZEUS, NCT05021835) evaluating cardiovascular event reduction. Subgroup analyses from CANTOS further indicate that genetic variants influencing IL-6 pathways enhance therapeutic responses, guiding personalized anti-inflammatory strategies.
Research on the microbiome and its metabolites has illuminated the gut-artery axis as a modulator of atherosclerosis, with trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO) emerging as a key pro-atherogenic compound derived from dietary choline metabolism by gut bacteria. Fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) trials, such as the ongoing NCT04410003 study, have demonstrated potential to reduce TMAO levels and alter microbiota composition in patients with metabolic syndrome and atherosclerosis, though large-scale cardiovascular outcomes remain pending. In 2025 investigations, disruptions in the gut-artery axisvia metabolites like lipopolysaccharide (LPS) and short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs)have been linked to endothelial dysfunction and plaque inflammation, with preclinical FMT from healthy donors reducing plaque burden in mouse models by restoring microbial balance. Dietary interventions mimicking FMT effects, such as the Mediterranean diet in the PREDIMED trial, have correlated with lower TMAO and fewer cardiovascular events in over 7,000 at-risk individuals.
Regenerative therapies are advancing toward vascular smooth muscle cell (VSMC) replacement to stabilize plaques, with mesenchymal stem cell (MSC) therapy showing promise in preclinical models by differentiating into contractile VSMCs and reducing neointimal hyperplasia. In atherosclerosis models, MSCs have replaced synthetic VSMCs in plaques, decreasing oxidative stress and adhesion molecules while promoting endothelial repair. Complementing this, nanotechnology enables targeted plaque imaging and drug delivery; for instance, ultrasmall superparamagnetic iron oxide nanoparticles enhance MRI detection of inflamed plaques by targeting P-selectin-expressing endothelium. Polymeric nanoparticles loaded with anti-inflammatory agents like rapamycin have achieved controlled release at lesion sites, reducing plaque size by up to 40% in rabbit models without systemic toxicity.
Artificial intelligence (AI) and big data integration are revolutionizing atherosclerosis prediction, with machine learning (ML) models achieving up to 95% accuracy in forecasting plaque rupture using multi-omics datasets. These models incorporate genomics, proteomics, and imaging data to identify instability markers, such as immune cell infiltration in unstable plaques, outperforming traditional risk scores in external validations. In 2024 applications, deep learning algorithms on coronary CT angiography have quantified neovascularization and inflammation with high precision, enabling early intervention. Multi-omics integration via ML further refines risk stratification, highlighting proteins like APOA and inflammatory pathways as predictors of progression.
Animal models have evolved beyond traditional rabbit paradigms to humanized mice, providing insights into human-specific atherosclerosis mechanisms. In 2024, humanized ApoB100/LDLR-knockout mice engrafted with human CD34+ hematopoietic stem cells exhibited accelerated plaque formation mimicking familial hypercholesterolemia, allowing testing of therapies like anti-aggregant peptides that reduced lipid burden by stabilizing LDL. These models, including NSG-Apoe-/- variants, better replicate human immune responses and lipoprotein profiles compared to standard mice or rabbits. Zoonotic perspectives from non-human primates and pigs have revealed conserved inflammatory pathways, informing translational research on plaque vulnerability across species.
