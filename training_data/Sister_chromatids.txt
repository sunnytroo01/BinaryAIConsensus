Sister chromatids
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Sister chromatids are the two identical copies of a replicated chromosome, formed during DNA replication in the S phase of the cell cycle and joined at a constricted region known as the centromere. These structures ensure the faithful distribution of genetic material to daughter cells by maintaining cohesion until the appropriate stage of cell division.
The formation of sister chromatids begins when the DNA double helix is unwound and each strand serves as a template for synthesizing a complementary strand, resulting in two genetically identical molecules that remain physically linked. This cohesion is mediated by a multi-subunit protein complex called cohesin, which encircles the DNA and stabilizes the paired chromatids throughout the cell cycle until separation is triggered. In mitosis, sister chromatids align at the metaphase plate, and during anaphase, spindle fibers attach to protein structures called kinetochores on the centromere, pulling the chromatids to opposite poles of the dividing cell to produce two genetically identical daughter cells. This process is crucial for maintaining genomic stability and preventing aneuploidy, a condition associated with diseases such as cancer.
In meiosis, the first division (meiosis I) involves the separation of homologous chromosomes rather than sister chromatids, but the second division (meiosis II) mirrors mitosis by segregating sister chromatids to ensure haploid gametes receive one copy of each chromosome. Cohesin dynamics differ between these divisions: while centromeric cohesin persists until meiosis II to support proper alignment, arm cohesin is removed earlier to allow homologous pairing and recombination. Disruptions in sister chromatid cohesion can lead to errors in chromosome segregation, contributing to infertility, developmental disorders, and tumorigenesis.

Definition and Characteristics

Definition

Chromosomes are threadlike structures composed of DNA and proteins that carry genetic information from one cell generation to the next. Sister chromatids are the two identical copies of a replicated chromosome, formed during the S phase of the cell cycle when DNA replication occurs. These copies remain closely associated and attached to each other at a specialized region known as the centromere.
A pair of sister chromatids constitutes a chromosome dyad, which is the duplicated form of the chromosome observed during cell division. Upon separation at the centromere during mitosis or meiosis, each sister chromatid is regarded as an individual chromosome, ensuring accurate distribution of genetic material to daughter cells. This process maintains genetic stability across cell divisions.

Structural Features

Sister chromatids represent the duplicated form of a chromosome, each comprising a single, linear DNA double helix molecule that serves as the genetic template. This DNA, with a diameter of approximately 2 nm, is extensively packaged within the nucleus to fit the cellular constraints, achieving a compaction ratio of about 10,000-fold in mitotic chromosomes. The packaging begins at the molecular level, where the DNA wraps around histone proteins to form the fundamental repeating unit of chromatin known as the nucleosome.
The nucleosome core particle consists of an octamer of canonical histonestwo molecules each of H2A, H2B, H3, and H4around which roughly 147 base pairs of DNA are wound in approximately 1.65 left-handed superhelical turns. Adjacent nucleosomes are connected by short stretches of linker DNA, typically 2060 base pairs long, with the linker histone H1 binding to these regions to facilitate further compaction into a 30-nm chromatin fiber through models such as the solenoid or zigzag configuration. These fibers then organize into higher-order looped structures and scaffolds, enabling the elongated arms of the chromatids and contributing to the overall cylindrical shape of mitotic chromosomes, where chromatin exhibits a polymer-like melt organization without consistent 30-nm fibers in some species.
Under light microscopy, particularly during metaphase when chromosomes are maximally condensed, sister chromatids appear as two parallel, rod-like arms of equal length extending symmetrically from a central constriction, giving replicated chromosomes their distinctive X-shaped morphology. This visibility is enhanced by stains such as Giemsa, revealing the chromatids' separation except at the centromere. The centromere functions as the attachment point between the sister chromatids, characterized by a specialized chromatin domain enriched in the histone H3 variant CENP-A, which nucleates the assembly of over 100 kinetochore proteins. These kinetochore components, including the inner kinetochore proteins like CENP-C and the outer KMN network, provide the initial binding sites for spindle microtubules, occupying a defined region at the centromere surface.

Formation and Cohesion

DNA Replication Process

DNA replication, which generates sister chromatids, occurs during the S phase of the cell cycle's interphase, immediately following the G1 phase. In eukaryotic cells, this phase ensures that the genetic material is precisely duplicated to support subsequent cell division, with the process tightly regulated to maintain genomic integrity.
The replication mechanism is semi-conservative, meaning each parental DNA strand serves as a template for the synthesis of a new complementary strand, resulting in two identical DNA molecules per original chromosome. In eukaryotes, this is primarily carried out by DNA polymerases and , which extend the new strands by adding nucleotides in a 5' to 3' direction using deoxynucleoside triphosphates. The process initiates at multiple replication origins along the chromosome, where origin recognition complexes bind and trigger "origin firing" to unwind the DNA double helix.
Key enzymatic steps involve several proteins working in concert at the replication fork. DNA helicases, such as MCM2-7 complexes, unwind the double-stranded DNA to expose single strands, creating a Y-shaped fork that progresses bidirectionally from each origin. Primase synthesizes short RNA primers to provide a starting point for DNA polymerases, enabling continuous synthesis on the leading strand and discontinuous synthesis of Okazaki fragments on the lagging strand. DNA ligase then seals the nicks between Okazaki fragments on the lagging strand by forming phosphodiester bonds, completing the new strands.
As a result of this replication, each original chromosome now comprises two identical sister chromatids, each containing one of the semi-conservative DNA duplexes, poised for cohesion establishment shortly thereafter.

Establishment of Cohesion

Sister chromatid cohesion is established during S phase of the cell cycle, coinciding with DNA replication, to ensure that the newly synthesized sister chromatids are physically linked from the moment of their generation. This timing is critical, as cohesion cannot be effectively imposed after replication has completed; experimental depletion of key cohesion factors like Scc1p during S phase leads to loss of cell viability as DNA replication finishes, whereas blocking replication with hydroxyurea preserves viability by preventing the need for cohesion establishment. Studies in budding yeast have shown that cohesin complexes, preloaded onto chromatin prior to S phase, are dynamically repositioned by the advancing replication fork and stabilized into a cohesive configuration specifically during this phase.
The process involves ring-shaped protein complexes, such as cohesin, that encircle and tether the two daughter DNA molecules along their entire length, with particularly robust cohesion at centromeres and along chromosome arms to facilitate accurate pairing. During replication, the replisome pushes cohesin complexes ahead of the fork until convergence at termination sites, where the disassembly of the CMG helicase allows cohesin to persist and establish stable linkages between nascent sisters; single-molecule imaging in yeast cells has visualized this fork-dependent relocation and retention. Cohesin acetylation, mediated by enzymes like Ctf7/Eco1 on subunits such as Smc3, is essential for this conversion to a pairing-competent state, counteracting anti-establishment factors like WAPL that would otherwise release cohesin.
This establishment of cohesion is vital for promoting bipolar attachment of kinetochores to the mitotic spindle and preventing segregation errors that could lead to aneuploidy and cell death. Mutations disrupting Smc3 acetylation, such as K112R/K113R substitutions, result in lethal cohesion defects and underscore the replication-coupled nature of the process. By ensuring faithful sister pairing only between replication products, this mechanism minimizes misalignment risks during chromosome segregation.

Functions in Cell Division

Role in Mitosis

Sister chromatids play a central role in mitosis by ensuring the accurate duplication and equitable distribution of genetic material to two daughter cells, thereby maintaining genomic stability across cell generations. Formed during the S phase of the cell cycle, these identical copies of each chromosome remain paired until their separation, which is orchestrated through the mitotic phases to produce genetically identical progeny. This process is essential for somatic cell proliferation in multicellular organisms, preventing the accumulation of genetic errors that could lead to cellular dysfunction.
During prophase, the chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes, each consisting of two sister chromatids joined at the centromere, while the mitotic spindle apparatus begins to assemble from microtubules nucleated at the centrosomes. In prometaphase, the nuclear envelope breaks down, allowing the spindle microtubules to interact with kinetochoresspecialized protein structures assembled on the centromeres of each sister chromatid. By metaphase, the chromosomes align at the equatorial metaphase plate, with the kinetochores of sister chromatids bi-orienting to attach to microtubules emanating from opposite spindle poles; this bipolar attachment generates tension across the centromere, which is sensed to stabilize the connections and ensure proper alignment. The equal tension from opposing microtubule pulls confirms correct attachment, activating the spindle assembly checkpoint to delay anaphase until all chromosomes are properly oriented.
In anaphase, the cohesin complexes that hold the sister chromatids together are cleaved by the protease separase, allowing the chromatids to separate and migrate to opposite poles as individual chromosomes, pulled by the shortening of kinetochore microtubules. This separation is triggered once the spindle checkpoint is satisfied, ensuring that each chromatid moves toward its designated pole under the force of microtubule depolymerization. As a result of this precise segregation, telophase and cytokinesis yield two daughter cells, each receiving one complete set of 46 chromosomes in humans, thus preserving the diploid chromosome number and genetic identity.
Errors in sister chromatid segregation, such as nondisjunctionwhere both chromatids fail to separate and move to the same daughter cellcan lead to aneuploidy, an abnormal chromosome number that disrupts cellular function and is implicated in conditions like cancer and developmental disorders. Mitotic nondisjunction arises from defects in kinetochore-microtubule attachments or premature/incomplete cohesin removal, resulting in one daughter cell gaining an extra chromosome and the other losing one, thereby compromising genomic integrity.

Role in Meiosis

In meiosis, sister chromatids play a crucial role in achieving the reductional division that produces haploid gametes, differing from their function in mitosis by supporting the segregation of homologous chromosomes in the first division while ensuring their own cohesion until the second. During meiosis I, the reductional phase, homologous chromosome pairs align and separate, but sister chromatids within each chromosome remain tightly paired due to persistent cohesin complexes along their lengths, including at centromeres. This cohesion allows monopolar attachment of sister kinetochores to spindle microtubules, directing both sisters to the same daughter cell and facilitating the halving of chromosome number.
In meiosis II, the equational phase, sister chromatids separate akin to mitotic anaphase, with bipolar kinetochore attachments pulling them to opposite poles, resulting in four haploid cells each containing one chromatid per chromosome. Cohesin along chromosome arms is largely removed during anaphase I to enable homologous separation, but centromeric protectionmediated by proteins like shugoshinpreserves cohesion until meiosis II, where deprotection allows full dissolution and chromatid segregation. This two-step release ensures accurate distribution without premature sister separation.
Sister chromatids also support meiotic recombination by providing a local template for double-strand break repair, though crossovers typically occur between non-sister (homologous) chromatids to promote genetic diversity and chiasma formation, which reinforces homolog pairing. Inter-sister recombination is minimized through mechanisms like biased strand invasion toward homologs, preventing interference with reductional segregation. This facilitation of homologous exchanges, rather than sister-specific ones, is essential for proper chromosome pairing and disjunction.

Molecular Mechanisms

Cohesin Complex

The cohesin complex is a multi-subunit protein assembly that mediates sister chromatid cohesion by topologically encircling replicated DNA strands. Its core composition consists of two structural maintenance of chromosomes (SMC) proteins, Smc1 and Smc3, which form elongated rod-like structures with ATPase heads and a central hinge domain, along with the kleisin subunit Scc1 (also known as Rad21 or Mcd1) that bridges the Smc1 and Smc3 heads to create a ring-like architecture approximately 35-50 nm in diameter. Additionally, the Scc3 subunit (known as stromal antigen or Stag/SA1/SA2 in vertebrates) associates with Scc1 to stabilize the complex and regulate its interactions.
The primary function of cohesin involves the topological entrapment of DNA within its ring structure, established shortly after DNA replication during S phase to hold sister chromatids together until anaphase. This encircling mechanism is facilitated by the loader complex comprising Nipbl (the ortholog of yeast Scc2) and Mau2 (ortholog of Scc4), which uses ATP hydrolysis to open the Smc hinge domain and promote initial association with chromatin, enabling the ring to capture both sister DNAs. Beyond cohesion, cohesin variants exhibit additional roles in chromatin organization, such as facilitating long-range DNA looping that supports gene regulation; for instance, in vertebrates, cohesin collaborates with CTCF to form enhancer-promoter interactions and insulators at loci like H19/Igf2.
Cohesin is highly conserved across eukaryotes, from budding yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) to humans, reflecting its fundamental role in chromosome dynamics, though ancillary functions like CTCF-mediated looping appear more pronounced in higher organisms. In yeast, the core subunits Smc1, Smc3, Scc1, and Scc3 suffice for basic cohesion, while vertebrate cohesin incorporates paralogs such as Smc1/ and Stag1/2/3 to diversify regulatory interactions. This evolutionary persistence underscores cohesin's ancient origin, with bacterial SMC homologs suggesting even deeper roots in DNA compaction mechanisms.

Separation and Segregation

The separation of sister chromatids is initiated at the onset of anaphase through a tightly regulated proteolytic process that dissolves the cohesion holding them together. The primary trigger for this event is the activation of the anaphase-promoting complex/cyclosome (APC/C), a ubiquitin ligase that, in complex with its co-activator Cdc20, targets securin for polyubiquitination and subsequent proteasomal degradation. Securin acts as an inhibitory chaperone for separase, a cysteine protease; its degradation relieves this inhibition, allowing separase to become active. This mechanism ensures that sister chromatid separation does not occur prematurely, coordinating it with the completion of metaphase alignment.
Once activated, separase cleaves the Scc1 (also known as Rad21 or kleisin) subunit of the cohesin complex, which forms a ring-like structure encircling the sister chromatids. This cleavage opens the cohesin ring, thereby releasing the physical linkage between the sisters and permitting their disjunction. In vertebrates, the process is similarly dependent on separase-mediated proteolysis of the kleisin subunit, confirming its conserved role across eukaryotes. The specificity of separase for Scc1 ensures targeted disassembly of cohesion without broadly disrupting other cellular structures.
Following separation, the segregation of sister chromatids to opposite spindle poles is driven by the depolymerization of kinetochore microtubules. As these microtubules shorten primarily at the kinetochore ends during anaphase A, they generate pulling forces that move the chromatids poleward along stationary microtubule tracks. This depolymerization-powered movement, observed in vitro and in vivo, relies on the energy from tubulin dimer disassembly rather than motor proteins alone, ensuring efficient partitioning.
To prevent errors, the spindle assembly checkpoint (SAC) serves as a safeguard, inhibiting APC/C activity until all kinetochores achieve bipolar microtubule attachment and proper alignment at the metaphase plate. Key SAC components, such as Mad2 and BubR1, form a mitotic checkpoint complex that sequesters Cdc20, delaying securin degradation and separase activation until alignment is verified. This checkpoint mechanism minimizes the risk of aneuploidy by enforcing bi-orientation before chromatid separation proceeds.

Biological Significance

DNA Repair

Sister chromatids serve as the preferred templates for DNA repair following double-strand breaks (DSBs) due to their physical proximity after replication and their identical sequence, which minimizes the risk of allelic loss or errors compared to using the homologous chromosome. This preference is particularly evident in S and G2 phases of the cell cycle, where both sister chromatids are available. Studies indicate that the sister chromatid is utilized approximately 100 times more frequently than the homologous chromosome for homologous recombination (HR) repair of DSBs.
The repair process involves sister-directed homologous recombination, where the broken DNA end invades the identical sequence on the sister chromatid, facilitated by proteins such as Rad51, which forms a nucleoprotein filament to search for and pair with the homologous template. This mechanism ensures accurate restoration of the original sequence without introducing mutations, as the sister provides an exact copy produced during DNA replication. In mitosis, this pathway is a primary means of repairing replication-associated DSBs, promoting genome stability.
Such inter-sister repair events are common during mitotic repair but occur at notable frequencies in meiosis as well; in budding yeast, up to one-third of all recombination events during meiosis involve the sister chromatid. Evidence from model organisms, particularly Saccharomyces cerevisiae, demonstrates that disrupting sister chromatid cohesionvia cohesin mutantsleads to reduced DSB repair efficiency, as shown by increased hypersensitivity to ionizing radiation and impaired post-replicative repair. For instance, cohesin-deficient yeast cells exhibit defective HR at DSB sites, underscoring the essential role of sister chromatids in efficient repair.

Implications in Disease

Errors in sister chromatid segregation, particularly nondisjunction, result in aneuploidy, where cells gain or lose whole chromosomes, contributing to congenital disorders such as Down syndrome (trisomy 21). Nondisjunction occurs when sister chromatids fail to separate properly during mitosis or meiosis, leading both to migrate to one daughter cell and causing chromosomal imbalance. In maternal meiosis I or II, this mechanism accounts for approximately 95% of Down syndrome cases, often linked to advanced maternal age and reduced recombination on chromosome 21.
Mutations in cohesin complex genes disrupt sister chromatid cohesion, leading to developmental disorders like Cornelia de Lange syndrome (CdLS), a multisystem condition characterized by growth retardation, intellectual disability, and distinctive facial features. Approximately 65% of CdLS cases involve mutations in NIPBL, the cohesin loader, or core subunits like SMC1A and SMC3, which impair cohesin function and cause cohesion defects. These mutations result in genome-wide dysregulation of gene expression, exacerbating the syndrome's phenotypic severity. In contrast, cohesin overexpression, such as of SMC1A or RAD21 subunits, promotes colorectal cancer progression by enhancing cell proliferation and survival.
Faulty sister chromatid segregation drives genomic instability in cancer by inducing chromosomal instability (CIN), a hallmark that generates aneuploidy and structural rearrangements, fostering tumor evolution and metastasis. Errors in cohesion and segregation, often due to cohesin dysregulation, lead to merotelic attachments or lagging chromosomes, amplifying mutation rates and enabling oncogene activation. Additionally, cohesin overexpression contributes to chemotherapy resistance; for instance, elevated RAD21 levels in KRAS-mutant colorectal cancers correlate with poor response to chemoradiotherapy by enhancing DNA repair pathways that utilize sister chromatids for homologous recombination, allowing tumor cells to survive genotoxic stress.
Targeting sister chromatid dynamics offers therapeutic promise in cancer treatment, with inhibitors of cohesin or separase (the protease that cleaves cohesin to enable segregation) showing potential to exploit CIN vulnerabilities. Small-molecule separase inhibitors like sepins selectively induce apoptosis in separase-overexpressing cancers, such as breast tumors, by preventing cohesion release and mitotic progression, while sparing normal cells. Cohesin inhibitors, including those disrupting SMC1A function, synergize with agents like bevacizumab in colorectal cancer models, reducing tumor growth by amplifying genomic instability in malignant cells.
