 The Sydney Cricket Ground (SCG) is a historic sports stadium in Sydney, New South Wales, Australia, originally established in 1848 as the Garrison Ground for military and cricket use. Owned by the Government of New South Wales and operated by the Sydney Cricket and Sports Ground Trust, it primarily hosts cricket matches for the New South Wales Blues and Sydney Sixers teams.
Following major renovations, including the 2014 addition of the MA Noble, Don Bradman, and Dally Messenger Stand, the SCG's seating capacity stands at 48,000, enabling it to accommodate large crowds for international fixtures. The venue has hosted 113 Test matches since the first in 188283 between Australia and England, establishing it as a cornerstone of Australian cricket with a reputation for batsman-friendly pitches and high-scoring encounters.
Renowned for its role in Ashes series and annual New Year's Test, the SCG has witnessed pivotal moments such as Shane Warne's debut and final Test appearances, alongside consistent attendance for domestic and limited-overs internationals. Its enduring significance stems from over 170 years of continuous sporting activity, blending heritage structures like the Members' Pavilion with modern facilities to support elite competition.

History

Origins and early development (18481900)

The site of the Sydney Cricket Ground originated as a soldiers' recreational field granted in 18511852 to Colonel Henry Bloomfield, commander of Victoria Barracks, encompassing 25 acres of land behind the barracks in Paddington, initially designated the Garrison Ground for military cricket and other sports. The first recorded cricket match occurred there on 16 February 1854, when the Garrison Club defeated the Royal Victoria Club, marking the ground's early role as a venue for colonial sporting activities amid Sydney's growing interest in cricket following informal games on sites like Hyde Park since the 1820s.
By the 1870s, civilian cricket bodies sought greater control, with the New South Wales Cricket Association securing permission in 1875 to use and redevelop the Garrison Ground at a cost of 3,000, including leveling and fencing improvements; it was then renamed the Association Cricket Ground. The ground was formally dedicated in August 1876 by Governor Sir Hercules Robinson, with initial trustees appointed to oversee management, and the first organized match under the new name took place on 25 October 1877 between the New South Wales Government Printing Office and the Audit Office teams.
Infrastructure expanded rapidly: the Members' Pavilion was erected in 1878, coinciding with the inaugural first-class match between New South Wales and Victoria, while a grandstand opened in 1881 to accommodate growing crowds. The venue hosted its first international Test match on 1721 February 1882, where Australia defeated England by five wickets in a contest pivotal to the early Ashes rivalry, drawing significant attendance and solidifying the ground's status. In 1894, it was officially renamed the Sydney Cricket Ground, reflecting its prominence as New South Wales' primary cricket venue by the century's end, though it also accommodated rugby and other events.

Expansion in the 20th century

In the early 1900s, the Sydney Cricket Ground underwent modest expansions, including extensions to the existing Members' Stand in 1903 to accommodate growing crowds for cricket and other sports. These additions reflected increasing demand following Australia's federation and the venue's role in international fixtures, though major structural changes were limited until the interwar period.
The most ambitious expansion plan emerged in the 1930s under the Sydney Cricket Ground Trust, led by former Australian captain Montague Alfred "M.A." Noble, envisioning a comprehensive redevelopment to modernize the venue amid rising attendance for Test matches and the 1938 British Empire Games. Construction began in 1934 with a major building campaign, culminating in the erection of the new Members' Stand (later renamed the M.A. Noble Stand) in 1936, which replaced the earlier Northern Stand and featured a design extending westward from a central clock tower. This stand represented the only completed element of the grand scheme, as economic constraints from the Great Depression and the onset of World War II halted further progress, leaving the project unfinished.
Postwar recovery spurred additional developments in the 1970s and 1980s, driven by the need to support professional cricket, rugby league, and emerging day-night formats. The Bradman Stand opened in 1974 at a cost of $2 million, providing covered seating on the western side and honoring Sir Donald Bradman's legacy. In 1978, six floodlight towers were installed for $1.2 million, enabling the venue's first day-night match on 28 November and extending usability beyond daylight hours. Subsequent stands included the Brewongle Stand in 1980 ($8.9 million), the Pat Hills Stand (renamed Bill OReilly Stand in 1988) in 1984, the Hill Stand (later Doug Walters Stand) in 1985, and the Churchill Stand in 1986, collectively transitioning much of the terraced Hill area toward seated accommodations and boosting overall infrastructure for spectator safety and comfort. These enhancements increased seated capacity while adapting to televised sports and higher standards, though total attendance figures declined from prewar peaks due to the shift from standing room.

Renovations and modernization (2000present)

In 2008, the Victor Trumper Stand was constructed, replacing the Hill and Doug Walters Stand to improve public facilities, increase undercover seating, and enhance spectator access.
From March 2012 to January 2014, a major redevelopment replaced the MA Noble, Don Bradman, and Messenger Stands with a new four-level pavilion stand, completed ahead of the 2014 Ashes Test series. This $197 million project incorporated heritage elements such as the original scoreboard, etched entry doors, and ornate columns, while introducing seamless indoor-outdoor public spaces for bars and dining, modern technology for spectator engagement, and upgraded services meeting environmental sustainability targets with reused central systems. Key improvements included 85% undercover seating with no obstructed views, Australia's largest video screen at the time, enhanced catering, corporate, media, and Australian rules football facilities, and a roof terrace, contributing to a gross floor area of 33,700 square meters.
Subsequent master planning in 2012 outlined further upgrades to the Brewongle, Churchill, and O'Reilly Stands for improved spectator, corporate, and media amenities, though these remain in proposal stages as of 2025.
As of April 2025, Venues NSW initiated a review of a proposed $700 million overhaul to address ageing infrastructure, potentially increasing capacity beyond the current limits by replacing additional stands and modernizing facilities amid competition from newer venues. These plans emphasize fan experience enhancements but face scrutiny over costs and necessity given the ground's occasional full utilization.

Facilities and Infrastructure

Location, layout, and stands

The Sydney Cricket Ground (SCG) is located at Driver Avenue, Moore Park, in the Moore Park suburb of Sydney, New South Wales, Australia, approximately 4 kilometres south of the Sydney central business district and adjacent to the Entertainment Quarter precinct featuring bars, restaurants, and entertainment venues.
The venue's layout centres on an oval playing field oriented north-south, with the field boundaries measuring approximately 80 metres along the straight and 6668 metres square, making it one of the smaller ovals among major Australian Test cricket grounds and favouring batsmen on the shorter sides. The overall arena footprint spans about 155 metres by 136 metres, fully enclosed by tiered seating without a roof, supplemented by floodlights and a large video screen on the eastern side.
Seating is arranged in a continuous oval around the field, divided into named stands honouring cricketing figures. The eastern grandstand, rebuilt in 20132014, integrates the M.A. Noble Stand, Bradman Stand, and Dally Messenger Stand into a single modern structure providing corporate boxes, media facilities, and premium viewing, with the Bradman and Messenger sections accommodating general patrons and offering proximity to the playing surface. On the western side, the heritage-listed Members' Stand (built in stages from 1896) and adjacent Lady Members' Stand preserve traditional architecture, including Victorian-era elements, and primarily serve club members with reserved seating facing eastward toward the city skyline. The northern and southern ends feature additional terraced seating, such as the Hill area for casual spectators, ensuring 360-degree access while maintaining sightlines optimized for cricket and rugby codes.

Pitch characteristics and maintenance

The Sydney Cricket Ground (SCG) features a fixed pitch constructed with Bulli soil sourced from Bulli, New South Wales, distinguishing it from venues using drop-in pitches. This traditional in-ground setup, shared only with the Gabba among major Australian Test venues, allows for customized preparation but resists multi-sport wear from events like rugby. The pitch adheres to standard cricket dimensions: 22 yards (20.12 meters) in length between the stumps and 10 feet (3.05 meters) in width.
Pitch characteristics at the SCG traditionally favor spin bowling, particularly in later innings, due to the clay-rich Bulli soil's ability to deteriorate and offer grip under dry conditions. However, persistent wet weather, common in Sydney's summer, often prevents cracking and turn, resulting in flatter surfaces that assist pace early and seam movement moderately. Curator Adam Lewis has noted efforts to enhance spin potential through targeted preparation, though rainfall remains the primary limiter to achieving consistent turn.
Maintenance involves standard cricket pitch protocols overseen by the SCG grounds staff, including daily brushing, mowing to uniform height, scarifying for aeration, controlled irrigation, and heavy rolling to consolidate the surface. Advanced soil testing ensures optimal moisture and nutrient levels, supporting turf health amid heavy usage. Preparation for major matches, such as Tests, emphasizes gradual drying to promote even wear, with covers used to protect against dew or rain while preserving playability. The fixed nature necessitates ongoing repairs between events, including top-dressing with compatible soil blends to maintain integrity.

Capacity, attendance, and safety features

The Sydney Cricket Ground has a seating capacity of 48,000 spectators, achieved after the 2014 opening of the MA Noble, Don Bradman, and Dally Messenger Stand, which replaced older infrastructure with fully seated areas. This figure reflects conversions from standing room to permanent seating across most of the venue, prioritizing spectator comfort and visibility while limiting overcrowding compared to pre-20th-century configurations that accommodated larger crowds via temporary stands.
Attendance records at the SCG vary by era and event type, with historical peaks exceeding modern capacity due to standing patrons. The all-time single-day record for a cricket match stands at 58,446, set on 15 December 1928 during an Australia-England Test. In the seated era, the highest Day 1 attendance for a Test match was 47,566, recorded on 3 January 2025 during the fifth India-Australia Test of the 2024-25 Border-Gavaskar Trophy, surpassing a 50-year-old benchmark and approaching full utilization amid high demand. Average attendances for major events, such as international Tests, typically range from 30,000 to 45,000, influenced by ticket sales, weather, and match significance, with recent India tours driving spikes due to bilateral rivalry.
Safety features at the SCG emphasize perimeter security, crowd management, and emergency protocols managed by Venues NSW. A high-tech control center, operational since 2017, integrates facial recognition across CCTV feeds to identify and exclude banned individuals, linking to a database of prior offenders for proactive intervention. Entry protocols prohibit intoxicated or disorderly persons, alcohol, drugs, large bags, and weapons, with bag checks and encouragement for clear, small bags to expedite screening and reduce concealed risks. Accessibility enhancements include dedicated zones for patrons with disabilities, ensuring compliance with evacuation standards, while broader measures like contractor tracking via digital systems and real-time foot traffic monitoring mitigate overcrowding hazards during peak events.

Governance and Management

Sydney Cricket Ground Trust

The Sydney Cricket and Sports Ground Trust was a statutory authority of the New South Wales Government charged with managing the Sydney Cricket Ground (SCG) and the adjacent Sydney Sports Ground, later redeveloped as Allianz Stadium. Established through the amalgamation of the two venues in 1951, the Trust's modern governance framework was formalized by the Sydney Cricket and Sports Ground Act 1978, which vested control of the grounds and empowered the body to maintain facilities, schedule events, and generate revenue for upkeep. Under the Act, the Trust comprised a chairperson, deputy chairperson, and up to nine other members appointed by the Minister for Sport (typically for terms of up to five years), alongside two members elected by SCG members to represent their interests.
The Trust's primary responsibilities included venue operations, capital improvements, event hosting across cricket, rugby, and other sports, and financial self-sufficiency through ticket sales, sponsorships, and hospitality. It oversaw significant renovations, such as the redevelopment of stands and infrastructure to enhance capacity and safety, while preserving heritage elements like the Members' Stand. By the 2010s, the Trust managed annual budgets exceeding tens of millions, funding maintenance without ongoing government subsidies, though it faced challenges from rising costs and event disruptions.
In June 2020, the New South Wales Government announced the merger of the SCG Trust with other venue operators, including the Sydney Olympic Park Trust and Stadium Australia, into a single entity called Venues NSW, effective 1 December 2020. This restructuring aimed to streamline management of state-owned stadiums, centralize procurement, and improve efficiency amid post-COVID fiscal pressures, transferring the SCG's assets and operations to Venues NSW while retaining the Trust's legacy branding in some contexts. The elected member positions were discontinued post-merger, with governance shifting to a broader board under Venues NSW.

Tributes, statues, and memorials

The Sydney Cricket Ground (SCG) hosts a series of life-sized bronze sculptures commissioned under the Basil Sellers Sports Sculptures Project, funded by Australian businessman Basil Sellers to honor athletes who achieved prominence at the venue across cricket, rugby, and other codes. These works, sculpted primarily by New South Wales artists Cathy Weiszmann and Terence Plowright, number at least 11 as of 2023 and form a popular public attraction in the SCG precinct, depicting figures in dynamic action poses to evoke their historical contributions. 
Among the cricket-focused tributes, a statue of former Australian captain and commentator Richie Benaud was unveiled on 4 January 2008, capturing him in a captain's pose. A sculpture of pioneering fast bowler Fred Spofforth followed on 5 January 2009, commemorating his role in early Test cricket victories. Stan McCabe's statue, erected in January 2010, immortalizes the batsman's elegant strokeplay during the 1930s Bodyline series. In a landmark addition, Belinda Clark became the first female cricketer worldwide to receive such an honor with her statue unveiled on 5 January 2023, recognizing her leadership in Australia's 1997 and 2005 World Cup wins and over 100 international centuries. A distinctive non-player tribute is the statue of "Yabba" (Stephen Harold Gascoigne, 18781942), a legendary hill spectator and heckler, unveiled as a symbol of fan passion and unveiled around 2001 to represent all SCG patrons.
The SCG's Walk of Honour features additional commemorative plaques for cricket luminaries, including Sir Donald Bradman for his record 452 not out in a 1930 Sheffield Shield match at the ground. Similar plaques honor Arthur Morris (19222015), post-war opener and long-serving SCG trustee; Bill O'Reilly, the leg-spinner after whom a stand is named; and Doug Walters, known for his aggressive batting in the 1970s. These elements collectively preserve the venue's multi-generational sporting legacy without dedicated war or non-athletic memorials noted in primary records.

Cricket

Pitch behavior and preparation

The Sydney Cricket Ground pitch utilizes Bulli black clay soil, known for its high clay content that supports firm initial bounce and pace while deteriorating to aid spin later in matches.
Preparation, managed by curator Adam Lewis and team, follows standard Australian methods adapted for the venue's clay base: the surface is groomed, mowed to 8-10 mm using Legend couch grass, watered judiciously to control moisture, worked to expose clay layers, sealed, and rolled heavily for consolidation before play.
In Test cricket, the pitch generally provides true bounce and even pace on day one, with potential seam movement in seaming conditions, favoring batsmen in first innings averaging over 350 runs historically.
Subsequent days see cracking from sun exposure and wear, increasing variable bounce and turn for spinners, as the clay grips the ball, leading to higher wicket tallies post-day three.
Efforts since the 2010s have targeted balanced deterioration rather than flatness, evident in the 2024-25 Australia-India Test where bowlers took 26 wickets in two days, earning an ICC 'satisfactory' rating for early assistance.
Across 113 Tests, outcomes show 47 wins batting first, 43 chasing, and 23 draws, underscoring the pitch's shift from batsman dominance to contestable results when prepared for wear.

Notable matches and events

The Sydney Cricket Ground hosted its inaugural Test match on 17 February 1882, when Australia defeated England by five wickets in a timeless encounter that drew significant crowds and marked the venue's entry into international cricket history. In Ashes contests, which have been played at the SCG since 1882, standout moments include the 194647 series where Don Bradman and Sid Barnes forged a then-world-record 405-run fifth-wicket partnership, helping Australia to an innings victory and contributing to a 30 series whitewash. Rugby Codes

Rugby league history and events

The Sydney Cricket Ground has hosted rugby league matches since 1911, when the code was first played there during early interstate fixtures against Queensland, with Dally Messenger scoring 72 points across a three-match series. It served as a primary venue for New South Wales Rugby Football League (NSWRFL) premiership games and grand finals for much of the 20th century, accommodating thousands of spectators until the shift to purpose-built stadiums like the Sydney Football Stadium in the late 1980s.
The ground established attendance benchmarks for the sport, including a world record for an international rugby league match on 14 June 1932, when 70,204 fans watched Australia defeat England 86 in the first Ashes test. This figure surpassed prior domestic highs and highlighted the venue's capacity to draw massive crowds for high-stakes Tests, with subsequent Ashes series games in 1958 (68,777 attendance) and others reinforcing its role in international play. Additional Tests against New Zealand, such as the 1978 Trans-Tasman series (Australia wins in Games 1 and 3), further cemented the SCG's legacy in representative rugby league.
NSWRFL grand finals were routinely held at the SCG from the league's inception through 1987, the last of which saw Manly-Warringah defeat Canberra 188 before a crowd of approximately 36,000. A standout event was the 1965 decider on 18 September, where St. George triumphed over South Sydney 128 amid an official attendance of 78,056exceeding the ground's nominal 70,000 capacity and setting a then-Australian record for club rugby league, though estimates suggest unofficial overflows contributed to the figure. Other notable finals included St. George's 1966 victory over Balmain and earlier triumphs like 1956's St. George win, underscoring the venue's centrality to premiership deciders until infrastructure demands prompted relocation.

Rugby union matches

The Sydney Cricket Ground has hosted rugby union Test matches for the Australia national team, known as the Wallabies, since the sport's early international era in the country. It served as the venue for Australia's inaugural Test on 24 June 1899 against the British Isles touring team, a 133 victory attended by approximately 6,000 spectators that marked the Wallabies' first win on the international stage. Through the early 20th century, the ground regularly accommodated home Tests against touring sides from New Zealand, South Africa, and the British Isles, contributing to its status as a historic rugby venue in Sydney before larger modern stadiums assumed primacy.
Significant mid-century matches underscored the SCG's role in high-stakes rugby. The 1965 first Test against South Africa drew a then-record crowd of nearly 46,000, highlighting the venue's capacity for major international fixtures despite Australia's eventual series loss. In 1966, the opening Test of the series versus the British Lions ended in an 118 defeat for Australia before 42,303 fans, with the match remembered for its physical intensity and the Lions' narrow edge in a low-scoring affair. Test rugby largely shifted away from the SCG after the 1980s, with Allianz Stadium becoming the preferred Sydney venue, though the ground retained relevance for domestic play. Australian Rules Football and Other Codes

Sydney Swans and AFL games

The Sydney Cricket Ground (SCG) became the primary home venue for the Sydney Swans upon the club's relocation from South Melbourne in 1982, marking a pivotal shift in Australian football's expansion to New South Wales. The Swans' inaugural match at the venue occurred on 28 March 1982 against Melbourne, resulting in a 29-point victory attended by 15,764 spectators, the largest crowd for an Australian rules football game in Sydney at the time. Since then, the SCG has hosted the majority of the Swans' home-and-away and finals matches, contributing to the code's growth in a region historically dominated by rugby league, with average annual AFL attendances exceeding 38,000 in recent seasons such as 2024's 420,226 over 11 games.
The venue's oval configuration for AFL provides a capacity of approximately 48,000, including standing areas, though fixed seating totals around 43,500 following redevelopments that enhanced sightlines and amenities for football spectators. Record attendances at the SCG for Swans games include 46,100 against Geelong in Round 22, 1997, and 46,323 during the 2017 elimination final versus Essendon, reflecting peaks in local interest amid the club's competitive eras. The Swans have achieved significant milestones at the ground, such as their highest team score of 211 points against Melbourne on 2 April 1994, underscoring the SCG's role in fostering high-scoring, expansive play styles favored by the venue's dimensions and surface conditions.
Notable SCG-hosted Swans victories include the 2014 Round 13 thriller against Port Adelaide, where Sydney overcame a late surge to secure a top-ladder position-defining win, and the 2013 Round 8 draw with Fremantle, one of only two such results in the 2010s decade. The ground has also featured in multiple finals, such as the 2006 qualifying final win over Brisbane Lions, contributing to the Swans' path to that year's premiership. These events, supported by venue upgrades for AFL-specific needs like electronic scoreboards installed in 1983, have solidified the SCG's status as a key AFL outpost despite occasional use of larger stadia like Accor Stadium for derbies.

Other football variants

The Sydney Cricket Ground has hosted association football matches on rare occasions, primarily as a temporary venue when dedicated soccer stadiums were unavailable or for special events. These fixtures underscore the ground's versatility but highlight its unsuitability for regular play due to the oval shape and turf optimized for cricket and oval-ball codes, which can lead to surface damage and suboptimal pitch conditions for round-ball sports.
A notable modern example occurred on 27 October 2018, when the SCG accommodated the A-League Men's Sydney Derby between Sydney FC and Western Sydney Wanderers, drawing a crowd of approximately 35,000 spectators. This match, which Sydney FC won 30, represented the first association football game at the venue in 33 years, coinciding with the debut of a new video scoreboard and serving as a one-off relocation amid renovations at Allianz Stadium.
Historically, the ground hosted early association football demonstrations, including an 1872 exhibition between England and Scotland representatives, though such events were sporadic and predated organized leagues in Australia. No regular soccer tenancy has developed, with professional clubs favoring rectangular stadiums like Allianz Stadium for better alignment with FIFA standards on field dimensions and sightlines.
Other variants, such as Gaelic football or gridiron (American football), have not been documented as having been played at the SCG, reflecting the venue's primary alignment with cricket, rugby codes, and Australian rules football rather than European or Irish-derived ball games.

Controversies and Incidents

Historical disputes and riots

The Sydney Riot of 1879 occurred during a cricket match between New South Wales and an English touring team led by Lord Harris at the Association Ground (now the Sydney Cricket Ground) on 8 February 1879. The incident began when Australian batsman Billy Murdoch was given out leg before wicket by English umpire George Coulthard, a decision widely contested by the home crowd as erroneous and biased toward the visitors. This sparked outrage among spectators, leading to approximately 2,000 fans invading the pitch, pelting the English players with bottles and debris, and assaulting team members, including bowler Tommy Kendall who was struck on the head.
The unrest was fueled by underlying tensions over umpiring impartiality and allegations of gambling influence, with Lord Harris later attributing the pitch invasion to bookmakers in the stands who stood to lose from New South Wales' strong position at 64 for 3. Edmund Barton, a local umpire and future first Prime Minister of Australia, struggled to maintain order amid the chaos, while Coulthard faced direct threats. Play was abandoned for the day, and the match was ultimately cancelled, marking one of the earliest major crowd disturbances in international cricket history.
Contemporary accounts, such as those in Wisden Cricketers' Almanack, condemned the event as a "deplorably disgraceful affair" involving a "rough and excited mob," highlighting how colonial rivalries and perceived favoritism toward English officials escalated minor disputes into violence. The riot underscored early challenges in crowd control at the venue and contributed to ongoing debates about neutral umpiring in Anglo-Australian contests, though no formal charges resulted from the assaults.
In 1971, during a rugby union match between New South Wales and the touring South African Springboks on 10 July, anti-apartheid protesters clashed violently with police outside the ground, resulting in over 140 arrests amid firebombings and baton charges. While not directly disrupting play on the field, the disturbances reflected broader political tensions infiltrating sports events at the SCG, with demonstrators targeting the apartheid regime's team selection. Police intervention quelled the unrest, but it highlighted the venue's role in hosting matches that intersected with international controversies.

Modern crowd and pitch controversies

In the 202425 Border-Gavaskar Trophy, the Sydney Cricket Ground pitch for the fifth Test match between Australia and India, held from January 3 to 7, 2025, drew widespread criticism for its excessive grass cover and green tint, which favored fast bowlers disproportionately and resulted in a match played at an unusually frenetic pace with rapid wicket falls. Former Australian captain Michael Clarke described it as the "worst" SCG pitch he had seen, while India's Sunil Gavaskar highlighted the unusual preparation despite noting a lack of similar complaints from past Indian players. The International Cricket Council rated the pitch "satisfactory" post-match, a verdict contested by some observers given the imbalance that saw Australia declare at 439 and India struggle to 185 in their first innings.
The multi-sport use of the SCG has repeatedly raised safety concerns over pitch and turf conditions. During an Australian Football League match on June 27, 2025, between the Sydney Swans and Western Bulldogs, players experienced frequent slipping on a slick surface deemed "beyond unacceptable" and hazardous, prompting criticism from Fox Footy analyst Jonathan Brown and Swans coach Dean Cox, who linked it to wear from a prior Andrea Bocelli concert. Similar issues arose in a 2019 A-League soccer match, where Melbourne Victory coach Kevin Muscat condemned the surface as "unsafe" after an injury to Terry Antonis, attributing it to inadequate preparation for the code.
Crowd behavior at the SCG has sparked controversies, particularly during high-profile international cricket matches involving India. On January 34, 2025, during the same Australia-India Test, sections of the Australian crowd taunted Indian supporters with chants of "Where's your visa?", igniting accusations of racism and prompting backlash in Indian media, though Australian officials downplayed it as isolated banter amid broader scrutiny of spectator conduct in the series. A prior incident occurred in January 2021 during another India Test, where the Indian team lodged a formal complaint after players Jasprit Bumrah and Mohammed Siraj reported hearing racist abuse from spectators, audible due to limited attendance of around 10,000 under COVID-19 restrictions.
Other crowd disturbances include physical altercations, such as a brawl among AFL fans during the Sydney Swans' loss to Port Adelaide on April 20, 2025, which unfolded in the stands and highlighted ongoing issues with alcohol-fueled aggression at the venue. In January 2022, during the Ashes Test against England, one spectator received a lifetime ban from the SCG following involvement in a stands brawl on day three. While celebratory pitch invasions, such as thousands of fans storming the field after Lance Franklin's 1,000th AFL goal on March 25, 2022, have occurred without major repercussions, they contravene modern ground regulations designed to prevent disruptions.
