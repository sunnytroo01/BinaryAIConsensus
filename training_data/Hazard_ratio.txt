Hazard ratio
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The hazard ratio (HR) is a statistical measure used in survival analysis to quantify the relative risk of an event occurring in one group compared to another over time, representing the ratio of their respective hazard rates under the assumption of proportional hazards. It is particularly valuable for analyzing time-to-event data, such as time until death, disease progression, or treatment failure, in fields like clinical trials, epidemiology, and biostatistics.
The underlying hazard function, denoted as (t), describes the instantaneous rate at which events occur for a subject or population at time t, conditional on having survived up to that point; formally, it is the probability density function divided by the survival function, (t) = f(t)/S(t). In practice, the HR compares two such functions, (t)/(t), often between treatment and control groups, providing a summary of how a factor like a drug or exposure modifies the event risk.
Central to the estimation of HRs is the Cox proportional hazards model, developed by David R. Cox in 1972, which models the log-hazard as log((tX))=log(0(t))+X\log(\lambda(t|X)) = \log(\lambda_0(t)) + \beta' Xlog((tX))=log(0(t))+X, where 0(t)\lambda_0(t)0(t) is the unspecified baseline hazard, XXX represents covariates, and \beta are regression coefficients; the HR for a unit increase in a covariate is then exp()\exp(\beta)exp(). This semi-parametric approach enables flexible analysis of censored data without assuming a specific distribution for survival times.
Interpretation of an HR is straightforward yet nuanced: an HR of 1 indicates no difference in event risk between groups, an HR less than 1 suggests a reduced hazard (e.g., prolonged survival in the treatment group), and an HR greater than 1 implies an elevated hazard; confidence intervals around the HR assess statistical significance, with values excluding 1 supporting a meaningful effect. The model's key assumption is that of proportional hazards, meaning the ratio of hazards remains constant across time, which can be tested using methods like Schoenfeld residuals. Violations of this assumption may require extensions, such as time-dependent covariates.
In clinical trials, HRs are routinely reported for primary endpoints like overall survival, offering a concise way to communicate treatment efficacy, though they do not directly indicate absolute risk reduction or median survival differences. Their widespread adoption stems from the model's robustness and interpretability, making it a cornerstone of evidence-based medicine.

Fundamentals

Definition

In survival analysis, the hazard function represents the instantaneous rate at which an event occurs for an individual or group, conditional on having survived up to a specific time $ t $. This function captures the risk of the eventsuch as death, disease recurrence, or failurehappening in an infinitesimally small interval immediately following $ t $, given no prior occurrence. It provides a dynamic measure of risk that evolves over time, distinguishing it from static probabilities in other statistical contexts.
The hazard ratio (HR) is defined as the ratio of the hazard functions for two distinct groups or conditions, often comparing an exposed or treatment group to a reference or control group. For instance, in clinical trials, it quantifies the relative hazard of an adverse event between patients receiving a new therapy versus standard care. This ratio offers a way to assess how one factor modifies the instantaneous risk compared to another, enabling comparisons across time-varying risks without assuming the events occur at fixed points.
Intuitively, an HR greater than 1 indicates that the event risk is higher in the numerator group (e.g., treatment) relative to the denominator group (e.g., control) at any given time; an HR less than 1 suggests a reduced risk; and an HR of 1 implies equivalent risks between the groups. For example, an HR of 0.7 would mean the treatment group experiences about 30% lower hazard of the event compared to the control group throughout the study period. This interpretation highlights the HR's role in summarizing relative effects in time-to-event data.
The concept of the hazard ratio emerged in the 1970s within survival analysis, building on earlier epidemiological notions of relative risk ratios but adapted to handle censored and time-dependent data. It was formalized by David Cox in his seminal work on regression models for life tables, which laid the foundation for analyzing how covariates influence event hazards over time.

Mathematical Derivation

The hazard function, denoted $ h(t) $, quantifies the instantaneous rate of occurrence of the event of interest at time $ t $, conditional on survival up to that time; it is formally defined as
h(t)=limt0+P(tT<t+tTt)t, h(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0^+} \frac{P(t \leq T < t + \Delta t \mid T \geq t)}{\Delta t}, h(t)=t0+limtP(tT<t+tTt),
where $ T $ represents the time to event.
To compare risks between two populations, such as treatment and control groups, the hazard ratio at time $ t $ is derived as the simple ratio of their respective hazard functions:
HR(t)=h1(t)h2(t), \mathrm{HR}(t) = \frac{h_1(t)}{h_2(t)}, HR(t)=h2(t)h1(t),
where $ h_1(t) $ and $ h_2(t) $ denote the hazards for the two groups. This ratio measures the relative hazard, with $ \mathrm{HR}(t) > 1 $ indicating a higher instantaneous risk in the first group compared to the second.
In regression modeling, the logarithm of the hazard ratio facilitates additive effects of covariates; specifically, $ \log(\mathrm{HR}(t)) = \log(h_1(t)) - \log(h_2(t)) $, allowing linear combinations of predictors to influence the log-hazard scale.
Under the proportional hazards framework, the hazard for an individual with covariates $ \mathbf{x} $ takes the multiplicative form
h(tx)=h0(t)exp(x), h(t \mid \mathbf{x}) = h_0(t) \exp(\mathbf{x}^\top \boldsymbol{\beta}), h(tx)=h0(t)exp(x),
where $ h_0(t) $ is the baseline hazard function (for $ \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0} $) and $ \boldsymbol{\beta} $ are the regression coefficients; consequently, the hazard ratio relative to the baseline is $ \mathrm{HR}(t \mid \mathbf{x}) = \exp(\mathbf{x}^\top \boldsymbol{\beta}) $, independent of $ t $.

Core Assumptions and Models

Proportional Hazards Assumption

The proportional hazards (PH) assumption is a foundational requirement in survival analysis models, stating that the ratio of the instantaneous hazard rates between two populations remains constant over time. Formally, for two hazard functions $ h_1(t) $ and $ h_0(t) $ at time $ t $, the assumption implies $ h_1(t) = \mathrm{HR} \times h_0(t) $, where HR denotes the constant hazard ratio independent of $ t $. This means the effect of covariates on the hazard is multiplicative and does not vary with time, as originally formulated in the semi-parametric regression framework for life-table data.
A key implication of the PH assumption is that it permits the decomposition of the hazard function into a time-invariant baseline hazard and covariate-specific effects, facilitating the estimation of relative risks without specifying the full form of the baseline hazard. This separation enhances model flexibility, allowing focus on the log-linear effects of covariates while treating the baseline as a non-parametric nuisance function. Without this assumption, the hazard ratio would vary temporally, complicating direct comparisons of risk across groups.
To assess the validity of the PH assumption, several graphical and statistical methods are employed. Graphical diagnostics include log-log survival plots, where the logarithm of the negative log-survival function is plotted against the logarithm of time for different covariate levels; parallel curves indicate proportionality, as deviations suggest time-varying effects. Another approach involves plotting scaled Schoenfeld residuals against time; under the PH assumption, these residuals should show no systematic trend, with a correlation test yielding a non-significant p-value. Additionally, incorporating time-dependent covariatessuch as interactions between covariates and functions of timeinto the model allows testing for significance; a non-zero coefficient for the interaction term signals violation of proportionality.
Violation of the PH assumption can result in biased estimates of the hazard ratio, as the model averages effects over time in a way that misrepresents true temporal dynamics, potentially leading to incorrect inferences about covariate impacts. For instance, simulations demonstrate that non-proportional hazards inflate standard errors and distort effect sizes, particularly in datasets with substantial follow-up variation.

Cox Proportional Hazards Model

The Cox proportional hazards model, introduced by British statistician David Cox in 1972, represents a seminal semi-parametric approach in survival analysis that enables estimation of hazard ratios without specifying the form of the baseline hazard function, serving as an alternative to fully parametric models that assume a particular distribution for survival times.
Under the model's framework, which builds on the proportional hazards assumption that the ratio of hazards between individuals remains constant over time, the hazard function for an individual at time $ t $ given covariates $ X $ is expressed as
h(tX)=h0(t)exp(X),
h(t \mid X) = h_0(t) \exp(\beta' X),
h(tX)=h0(t)exp(X),
where $ h_0(t) $ denotes the unspecified baseline hazard function, $ \beta $ is the vector of regression coefficients, and the hazard ratio for a unit increase in a covariate $ X_j $ is given by $ \exp(\beta_j) $.
Estimation of the coefficients $ \beta $ in the Cox model relies on the partial likelihood function, which focuses solely on the relative contributions of the covariates by marginalizing over the baseline hazard $ h_0(t) $ and treating it as a nuisance parameter, thereby avoiding its explicit estimation. The partial log-likelihood is constructed from the ordered failure times and the risk sets at each event time, maximizing it to obtain $ \hat{\beta} $ via numerical methods.
This model's key advantages include its non-parametric treatment of the baseline hazard, which allows flexibility in handling diverse survival distributions; its inherent accommodation of right-censored data common in observational and experimental studies; and its capacity to incorporate multiple covariates, including time-dependent ones, while providing robust inference for hazard ratios.

Interpretation

Hazard Ratio in Survival Contexts

In survival analysis, the hazard ratio (HR) provides a measure of how the hazard rates between two groups relate over time, directly influencing the survival probabilities under the proportional hazards (PH) assumption. Specifically, if the baseline survival function for a reference group is $ S_0(t) $, the survival function for a comparison group with a constant HR is given by $ S_1(t) = ^{\mathrm{HR}} $. This relationship implies that the survival probability in the comparison group is raised (if HR < 1) or lowered (if HR > 1) to the power of the HR at every time point $ t $, reflecting a consistent multiplicative effect on the instantaneous risk without altering the shape of the underlying survival curve.
For instance, an HR of 2 indicates that the hazard in group 1 is twice that of group 2 at all times, leading to lower survival probabilities in group 1 according to $ S_1(t) = ^{\mathrm{HR}} $ across the follow-up period. This can be visualized in Kaplan-Meier (KM) survival curves, where under PH, the log(-log(S(t))) plots against log(t) for the two groups are parallel, indicating a constant HR. The HR thus connects non-parametrically estimated KM curves to parametric interpretations of risk, allowing researchers to quantify time-dependent differences in event occurrence.
A key diagnostic for the PH assumption underlying this interpretation is the plot of the log-cumulative hazard functions against log-time, where parallel curves for different groups confirm a constant HR. Non-parallelism suggests time-varying hazards, violating the assumption and requiring alternative models. Unlike the odds ratio, which applies to binary outcomes and compares the odds of an event occurring versus not occurring at a fixed point, the HR is inherently suited to time-to-event data, capturing the dynamic rate of events over continuous time rather than a static snapshot.

Relation to Treatment Effects and Endpoints

In randomized clinical trials, the hazard ratio (HR) serves as a key measure of treatment efficacy by comparing the rates of adverse events, such as death or disease progression, between the treatment and control arms. It quantifies the relative risk of the event occurring in one group versus the other over time, providing insight into how a therapy modifies the instantaneous risk throughout the study period.
For time-to-event endpoints, HR is applied distinctly to overall survival (OS), which assesses the time from randomization to death from any cause, and progression-free survival (PFS), which measures the time to disease progression or death. An HR less than 1 for OS indicates a reduction in mortality risk with the treatment, while for PFS, it reflects improved disease control by delaying progression. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) recommends the HR from a Cox proportional hazards model as the primary summary measure for these endpoints in oncology trials.
Clinically, an HR of 0.7 implies a 30% reduction in the hazard rate for the event in the treatment group compared to the control, assuming the proportional hazards assumption holds, which can translate to extended median survival timesfor instance, under an exponential distribution assumption, if the control arm median is 10 months, the treatment arm median would be approximately 10 / 0.7 14.3 months. Note that this median survival extension is exact for exponentially distributed survival times but serves as an approximation otherwise. This interpretation links the relative hazard reduction to practical benefits in patient outcomes, emphasizing the therapy's potential to prolong life or delay disease worsening.
Since the 1990s, both the FDA and the European Medicines Agency (EMA) have increasingly relied on HR for OS and PFS in approving oncology drugs, as these endpoints became central to demonstrating clinical benefit in pivotal trials for therapies targeting solid tumors and hematologic malignancies. The EMA's guidelines similarly endorse estimating HR to quantify treatment effects on PFS and OS in confirmatory studies.

Estimation and Inference

Parameter Estimation Methods

The primary method for estimating the parameters of the Cox proportional hazards model, which yields the hazard ratio as the exponential of the coefficient , is maximum partial likelihood estimation. This approach maximizes the partial likelihood function, derived from the conditional probability of observing the event ordering given the covariates, by solving the score equations set to zero: the first derivative of the log partial likelihood with respect to equals zero. The resulting estimator is asymptotically efficient and normally distributed under standard conditions, providing consistent estimates of the log hazard ratios without specifying the baseline hazard function.
When multiple events occur at the same time (ties), the exact partial likelihood computation becomes complex due to the discrete nature of ties, necessitating approximations to maintain computational feasibility. The Breslow approximation simplifies the risk set denominator by treating all tied events as occurring simultaneously, effectively ignoring the order within ties, which works well for small numbers of ties but can bias estimates when ties are frequent. In contrast, the Efron approximation iteratively adjusts the risk set by subtracting a fraction of the tied events from the denominator for each successive event within the tie group, offering greater accuracy and reduced bias, particularly in smaller samples or with heavy censoring, and is often preferred in practice.
These estimation techniques are widely implemented in statistical software for practical application. In R, the survival package's coxph function performs maximum partial likelihood estimation and defaults to the Efron approximation for ties, supporting both fixed and time-dependent covariates. SAS's PHREG procedure similarly maximizes the partial likelihood, allowing users to specify Breslow, Efron, or exact partial likelihood methods for handling ties via the TIES option. In Python, the lifelines library's CoxPHFitter class implements the same estimation framework, with options for Breslow or Efron tie handling and integration with pandas for data input.

Standard reporting practices in publications emphasize presenting the HR alongside its 95% confidence interval to convey both the effect size and precision, as recommended by guidelines for observational studies and clinical trials to facilitate interpretation and comparison across studies.

Applications and Examples

Use in Clinical Trials

In clinical trials, the hazard ratio (HR) plays a pivotal role in trial design, particularly in determining sample size requirements under the proportional hazards assumption. Sample size calculations for time-to-event endpoints rely on an anticipated HR to ensure sufficient power to detect a clinically meaningful treatment effect, often specifying the number of events rather than total participants to account for censoring. For instance, Schoenfeld's formula approximates the required total number of events DDD as D=(Z1/2+Z1)2p(1p)2D = \frac{(Z_{1-\alpha/2} + Z_{1-\beta})^2}{p (1-p) \left^2}D=p(1p)2(Z1/2+Z1)2, where ppp is the proportion of subjects in the treatment group (e.g., 0.5 for balanced allocation), and ZZZ values correspond to type I error \alpha and power 11-\beta1; this method is widely used for two-arm trials assuming exponential survival distributions. To detect a 20% reduction in hazard (HR = 0.80) with 80% power and two-sided =0.05\alpha = 0.05=0.05 in a balanced trial, approximately 631 events are needed, translating to hundreds of participants depending on accrual and follow-up duration.
A concrete example of HR application is the KEYNOTE-024 phase 3 trial, which evaluated first-line pembrolizumab versus platinum-based chemotherapy in patients with advanced non-small-cell lung cancer and PD-L1 tumor proportion score 50%. The trial demonstrated a significant OS benefit for pembrolizumab, with a median OS of 26.3 months versus 13.4 months (HR 0.62, 95% CI 0.48-0.81), indicating approximately a 38% reduction in the risk of death compared to chemotherapy. This HR informed the trial's primary endpoint analysis using the Cox model and supported regulatory approval by quantifying the treatment effect on survival. Five-year follow-up confirmed sustained benefit, with OS rates of 31.9% versus 16.3% (HR 0.62, 95% CI 0.48-0.81).
HRs are also integral to interim analyses in ongoing trials, where they guide futility stopping rules to halt unpromising studies early and conserve resources. Futility boundaries are often set based on conditional power calculations: if the observed HR at interim exceeds a predefined threshold (e.g., HR > 0.90, suggesting minimal benefit), the trial may stop for futility, assuming proportional hazards hold. For example, in oncology trials, group sequential designs like O'Brien-Fleming use HR-based monitoring to assess if the projected final HR is unlikely to reach statistical significance, with futility thresholds calibrated to control overall type I error.
In subgroup analyses, HRs help assess heterogeneity of treatment effects across patient characteristics, such as age, biomarker status, or disease stage, to identify populations with differential benefits or risks. Forest plots of subgroup HRs, along with tests for interaction (e.g., HR heterogeneity p-value), evaluate whether the overall HR varies significantly; for instance, in trials like KEYNOTE-024, subgroup HRs for OS showed variation across ECOG performance status without significant interaction. Such analyses inform personalized medicine but require caution against multiplicity and overfitting, prioritizing pre-specified subgroups to avoid false positives.

Use in Epidemiology

In epidemiology, the hazard ratio (HR) is a key measure for evaluating the association between exposures and time-to-event outcomes in observational studies, particularly prospective cohort designs that track participants to observe disease incidence while accounting for censoring and competing risks. Unlike randomized trials, these studies often involve non-experimental data where confounding factors like age, socioeconomic status, and comorbidities must be adjusted for to estimate unbiased HRs. The Cox proportional hazards model is commonly applied in this context to adjust for such confounders, enabling researchers to quantify how exposures influence hazard rates over time.
A prominent application of HR in cohort studies is assessing the risk of lung cancer attributable to smoking. In the Nurses' Health Study, a large prospective cohort of women followed since 1976, current smokers had a hazard ratio of 13.44 (95% CI: 10.8016.75) for lung cancer incidence compared to never smokers, after adjusting for age, diet, and other lifestyle factors, demonstrating the profound dose-dependent impact of tobacco exposure. Similar findings from other cohorts, such as the Cancer Prevention Study II, report HRs around 4.9 for current smokers versus never smokers, underscoring smoking's role as a major modifiable risk factor in population-level cancer epidemiology.
The Women's Health Initiative, an observational component of a larger study involving postmenopausal women, provides another illustrative example of HR use in assessing treatment-related risks. In this cohort analysis, combined estrogen plus progestin hormone therapy was associated with a hazard ratio of 1.24 (95% CI: 1.001.54) for coronary heart disease events compared to placebo, after adjustments for baseline cardiovascular risk factors, highlighting potential adverse effects in older populations.
Epidemiologic HR models often incorporate time-varying covariates to handle dynamic confounders, such as changes in smoking status or medication use over follow-up, which enhances the accuracy of risk estimates in long-term studies. For instance, in Cox models applied to cohort data, these covariates allow for updating exposure levels at each event time, providing more realistic assessments of evolving risk factors like intermittent exposures.
Hazard ratios derived from epidemiologic studies have significant public health implications, informing policy decisions on interventions like vaccination programs. In observational cohort studies of COVID-19 vaccine effectiveness, such as those using UK primary care data, adjusted HRs for severe outcomes (e.g., hospitalization) ranged from 0.05 to 0.20 among vaccinated versus unvaccinated individuals, translating to effectiveness estimates of 8095% and supporting rapid policy shifts toward widespread immunization mandates. These findings from real-world data have guided global health strategies, emphasizing the value of HRs in bridging research to actionable public health measures.

Limitations and Extensions

Violations of Assumptions

One common violation of the proportional hazards assumption in the Cox model occurs when the hazard ratio varies over time, known as time-dependent effects, where the influence of a covariate on the hazard changes across follow-up periods, such as stronger treatment benefits early on that diminish later. This non-constancy can arise from evolving biological mechanisms, delayed treatment responses, or external factors altering risk profiles over time.
To detect such violations, analysts often introduce interaction terms between the covariate and a function of time, such as covariate time or covariate log(time), into the model; a statistically significant interaction indicates non-proportionality. Graphical diagnostics, like plots of scaled Schoenfeld residuals against time, can also reveal patterns suggesting time-varying effects, while formal tests of the proportional hazards assumption provide statistical confirmation.
When the proportional hazards assumption is violated due to time-dependent effects, the estimated hazard ratio may average the effect across time, leading to over- or underestimation of the true impact and potentially incorrect inferences about treatment efficacy or risk factors. For instance, if an early protective effect wanes, the overall hazard ratio might mask initial benefits or exaggerate long-term risks, biasing decisions in clinical or epidemiological settings.
A notable case study illustrating time-dependent effects appears in HIV-1 vaccine efficacy trials, such as the HVTN 505 study, where cumulative incidence curves showed a trend toward waning vaccine efficacy over time. In this context, diagnostic tests indicated no significant violation of the proportional hazards assumption, yet the observed waning highlights how time-dependent effects can influence interpretation of preventive benefits in AIDS-related research.

Alternative Approaches

When the proportional hazards assumption underlying the hazard ratio does not hold, parametric survival models offer a structured alternative by fully specifying the form of the baseline hazard function, enabling more precise predictions of survival distributions under specific distributional assumptions. The exponential model assumes a constant hazard rate over time, which is suitable for scenarios where the risk of the event remains unchanging, such as in certain infectious disease transmissions or component failures in reliability engineering. This model's simplicity facilitates straightforward maximum likelihood estimation of parameters, including the baseline hazard , where the survival function is given by S(t) = exp(-t).
The Weibull model extends this by incorporating a shape parameter that allows the hazard to be constant (when shape=1, reducing to exponential), monotonically increasing, or decreasing, making it versatile for modeling aging or wear-out processes in survival data. For instance, in cancer studies, the Weibull distribution has been applied to capture accelerating hazards over time, providing estimates of both the scale and shape parameters through parametric regression. This full parameterization contrasts with semi-parametric approaches by yielding the entire survival curve, though it requires correct specification of the distribution to avoid bias. Seminal applications in survival analysis trace back to the Weibull distribution's introduction in reliability contexts, adapted for biomedical data in subsequent statistical frameworks.
Time-dependent hazard ratio models address violations of the constant proportionality assumption by incorporating interactions between covariates and time directly into the Cox framework, allowing the hazard ratio to vary dynamically. This extension, proposed through the inclusion of time-varying covariates or coefficients, enables modeling of effects that wane or intensify, such as treatment efficacy diminishing post-initial response in clinical settings. Estimation proceeds via partial likelihood maximization, with the hazard at time t expressed as (t|X) = (t) exp((t)X), where (t) captures the time interaction. A foundational development emphasized careful handling of internal versus external time-dependent variables to prevent bias from induced dependencies. This approach maintains the Cox model's flexibility while relaxing the proportionality constraint, as demonstrated in analyses of longitudinal data where covariate effects evolve.
As complements to the hazard ratio, measures like the restricted mean survival time (RMST) and number needed to treat (NNT) provide interpretable summaries of treatment effects without relying on proportional hazards, focusing instead on average survival duration or clinical impact. RMST quantifies the expected survival time up to a predefined horizon , calculated as the integral of the survival function from 0 to , offering a robust alternative when hazards cross or diverge non-proportionally. In randomized trials, the difference in RMST between arms directly estimates additional life gained, with inference via non-parametric bootstrapping or inverse probability weighting, particularly useful in oncology where long-term tails affect means. Its adoption has grown for its model-free nature and alignment with patient-centered outcomes.
Similarly, NNT for survival outcomes extends the binary-event concept by computing the number of patients required to treat to avert one event by a specific time, derived from the absolute risk reduction in cumulative incidence or survival probabilities. For time-to-event data, it is often calculated at fixed points using Kaplan-Meier estimates, yielding time-specific values like NNT at 5 years to prevent one death. This metric enhances clinical decision-making by translating hazard-based results into actionable numbers, though it requires specifying the follow-up duration to account for censoring. Its application in cardiovascular trials has highlighted varying treatment benefits over time, complementing hazard ratios by emphasizing absolute rather than relative effects.
Accelerated failure time (AFT) models present a direct alternative to hazard-based approaches by parameterizing the effect of covariates on the log-survival time scale, assuming that covariates accelerate or decelerate the time to event by a constant factor. The model posits log(T) = + 'X + , where T is survival time, the intercept, the acceleration parameters, and a distribution-specific error, leading to interpretable ratios of expected survival times between groups. Common distributions include log-normal or log-logistic for flexible shapes, with estimation via maximum likelihood, offering advantages in settings where time compression (e.g., disease progression) is more intuitive than hazard ratios. AFT models have proven particularly valuable in labor economics and medical durations, providing consistent estimates even under non-proportional hazards, though they demand distributional assumptions akin to parametric hazards.
