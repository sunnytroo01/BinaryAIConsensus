Hepatitis B
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Hepatitis B is a potentially life-threatening liver infection caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV), a partially double-stranded DNA virus belonging to the Hepadnaviridae family that primarily replicates in hepatocytes. The virus can cause both acute (short-term) and chronic (lifelong) infections, with acute cases often resolving spontaneously in adults but chronic infections developing in approximately 90% of infected infants and 30% of children under 5 years old, leading to progressive liver damage, cirrhosis, hepatocellular carcinoma, and an estimated 1.1 million deaths annually worldwide. HBV is highly infectious and remains viable on environmental surfaces for at least 7 days, with an average incubation period of 6090 days before symptoms or detectable liver enzyme elevations appear. A safe and effective vaccine provides long-term protection, making hepatitis B preventable, and antiviral treatments can manage chronic cases to reduce complications, though no cure exists.
Globally, hepatitis B represents a major public health challenge, with 254 million people living with chronic infection as of 2022 and approximately 1.2 million new infections occurring each year, predominantly in the Western Pacific and African regions. In the United States, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) estimates around 640,000 adults have chronic hepatitis B, with acute cases numbering about 14,400 in 2023, though underreporting means actual figures are higher; incidence is highest among non-Hispanic Asian/Pacific Islander and Black populations. Transmission occurs primarily through perinatal exposure from mother to child, percutaneous or mucosal contact with infected blood or bodily fluids (such as during unprotected sex, sharing needles, or unsafe medical procedures), and less commonly through horizontal transmission in early childhood via close household contact. The virus does not spread through casual contact such as hugging, sneezing, sharing food, or sweaty skin contact.
Acute hepatitis B infections are frequently asymptomatic or present with nonspecific symptoms such as fatigue, fever, loss of appetite, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, dark urine, and jaundice, typically appearing 60150 days post-exposure and resolving within 6 months in over 95% of immunocompetent adults. In contrast, chronic hepatitis B often remains clinically silent for decades but can progress to severe outcomes, including liver fibrosis, cirrhosis in 1525% of cases, and primary liver cancer, which accounts for the majority of HBV-related deaths. Diagnosis relies on serological markers, including hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg) for active infection, anti-HBs for immunity, and anti-HBc for past or current exposure, with routine screening recommended for all adults, pregnant individuals, and high-risk groups.
Prevention strategies emphasize universal vaccination, which the World Health Organization (WHO) recommends initiating within 24 hours of birth followed by 23 additional doses, achieving over 95% efficacy in preventing infection and transmission. In the U.S., the CDC advises vaccination for all infants, unvaccinated children and adolescents, adults aged 1959, and high-risk older adults, contributing to a 90% decline in acute cases since the 1990s. Additional measures include antiviral prophylaxis for infants of infected mothers, safe injection practices, blood screening, and promoting condom use. For chronic infections, management involves nucleoside/nucleotide analogs like tenofovir or entecavir to suppress viral replication and regular monitoring of liver function and cancer screening, though treatment is typically lifelong and focuses on preventing progression rather than eradication. Ongoing WHO goals aim to reduce new infections by 90% and deaths by 65% by 2030 through expanded access to testing, care, and vaccines.

Clinical Presentation

Signs and Symptoms

Hepatitis B infection manifests through a range of clinical signs and symptoms that vary depending on whether the infection is acute or chronic, with many cases remaining subclinical. In acute infection, symptoms typically emerge 1-4 months after exposure during the incubation period, which averages around 90 days but can range from 45 to 180 days.
Common symptoms of acute hepatitis B include fatigue, jaundice (yellowing of the skin and eyes), dark urine, abdominal pain, nausea, fever, joint pain, loss of appetite, and clay-colored stools. These signs often indicate liver inflammation and impaired bilirubin processing, with jaundice and related discoloration serving as key progression indicators in symptomatic cases. Most acute infections resolve spontaneously within weeks to months, though symptoms can persist up to 6 months in some individuals.
In chronic hepatitis B, the majority of infected individualsparticularly those infected in infancy or early childhoodremain asymptomatic for years or even decades, with no overt evidence of liver injury. When symptoms do occur, they are often mild and nonspecific, such as persistent fatigue or right upper quadrant discomfort due to low-grade liver inflammation. Extrahepatic manifestations may also arise, including polyarteritis nodosa, a systemic vasculitis affecting small- and medium-sized arteries that complicates approximately 1-5% of chronic cases. These symptoms underscore the variable clinical course of the infection, where subtle signs can precede more severe hepatic complications if undetected.

Acute Versus Chronic Infection

Hepatitis B virus (HBV) infection is classified as acute when it is self-limiting and resolves spontaneously, typically within 6 months of onset, or chronic when the infection persists beyond this period. In immunocompetent adults, acute HBV infection is self-limiting in 90-95% of cases, with viral clearance occurring through an effective immune response. In contrast, chronic infection develops in approximately 90% of neonates infected perinatally and in 30-50% of children infected between ages 1 and 5 years, reflecting an immature or tolerant immune system that fails to eliminate the virus.
The progression from acute to chronic infection is primarily influenced by the age at which infection occurs, with younger individuals at significantly higher risk due to less robust immune responses. Immunocompromised states, such as in HIV co-infection or during immunosuppressive therapy, further increase the likelihood of chronicity by impairing viral clearance. Additionally, HBV genotype plays a role, as certain genotypes (e.g., genotype C) are associated with higher rates of persistence and more severe outcomes compared to others like genotype A.
Resolution of acute infection is marked by seroconversion, where hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg) becomes undetectable and antibodies to HBsAg (anti-HBs) appear in the serum, indicating protective immunity and viral clearance. In chronic cases, HBsAg persists, often for decades, without seroconversion to anti-HBs unless therapeutic interventions are applied.

Etiology

Transmission

Hepatitis B virus (HBV) is primarily transmitted through exposure to infected blood or other body fluids, such as semen and vaginal secretions, via percutaneous, mucosal, or nonintact skin routes. This bloodborne pathogen is highly infectious, with transmission efficiency depending on the viral load in the source material and the nature of exposure.
Bloodborne transmission occurs most commonly through percutaneous injuries, including sharing contaminated needles or syringes among people who inject drugs, needlestick injuries in healthcare settings, and procedures like tattooing or piercing with unsterilized equipment. Perinatal transmission, from an infected mother to her child during birth, is a major route in regions with high endemicity, accounting for a significant proportion of chronic infections worldwide. In such cases, the risk is particularly elevated if the mother is hepatitis B e antigen (HBeAg)-positive, indicating high viral replication.
Sexual transmission of HBV happens through contact with infected semen or vaginal fluids, with HBV transmitting 50100 times more easily than HIV via sexual contact, especially among unvaccinated individuals; risks are higher with unprotected sex, multiple partners, anal sex, or among men who have sex with men (MSM). Single-exposure probabilities are significantly elevated over those for HIV, though not uniformly quantified. Vaccination provides nearly 100% protection against sexual transmission. This mode is the most common way HBV spreads among adults in low-endemicity areas like the United States.
Horizontal transmission can occur within households or close-contact settings through sharing personal items contaminated with infected blood, such as razors, toothbrushes, or nail clippers, especially if open wounds are present. This route is more prevalent among young children in high-endemicity regions, often through minor skin breaks or mucosal exposures.
In child daycare settings, there is a low but non-zero risk of HBV transmission through human bites that draw blood. Transmission requires the biter to be infected with HBV, the bite to break the skin (percutaneous exposure), and the introduction of infected blood or saliva into the wound of a susceptible (unvaccinated or non-immune) child. Such transmission is rare in daycare environments, as bites that break the skin occur in only about 2% of biting incidents, and most children are vaccinated against HBV, which provides effective protection. If a bite breaks the skin, medical evaluation for post-exposure prophylaxis is recommended if the bitten child is unvaccinated.
HBV is not transmitted through casual contact, such as hugging, kissing, or sharing eating utensils, nor through sweaty skin contact; nor by food, water, sneezing, coughing, or insect vectors like mosquitoes. Sweat contains low quantities of infectious HBV and is not an efficient vehicle for transmission unless contaminated with blood.
Individuals with HBV are most infectious during the acute phase of infection, when viral loads are highest, typically 1-2 months before and after symptom onset if symptoms occur. Chronic carriers remain infectious long-term, particularly those with high HBV DNA levels or HBeAg positivity, posing ongoing transmission risks through any of the above routes. The virus can survive on environmental surfaces for at least 7 days, facilitating indirect transmission via contaminated objects.

Viral Structure and Genome

Hepatitis B virus (HBV) belongs to the family Hepadnaviridae, genus Orthohepadnavirus, and species Hepatitis B virus, classified as an enveloped DNA virus with a tropism for hepatocytes. The virion, known as the Dane particle, measures approximately 42 nm in diameter and consists of an outer lipid envelope surrounding an icosahedral nucleocapsid. The envelope is derived from host cell membranes and incorporates three surface proteinshepatitis B surface antigens (HBsAg)namely the small (S), middle (M, including pre-S2 domain), and large (L, including pre-S1 and pre-S2 domains) proteins, which mediate viral attachment and entry. The nucleocapsid, formed by 180 or 240 dimers of the core antigen (HBcAg), encloses the viral genome and the multifunctional polymerase protein, exhibiting T=3 or T=4 icosahedral symmetry.
The HBV genome is a partially double-stranded, relaxed-circular DNA (rcDNA) molecule of approximately 3.2 kb (ranging from 3,182 to 3,248 nucleotides depending on genotype), featuring a full-length minus strand and an incomplete plus strand with a cohesive overlap region. This compact genome contains four overlapping open reading frames (ORFs): the precore/core ORF (C) encoding the core protein and the secreted e antigen (HBeAg); the polymerase ORF (P) producing a large multifunctional protein with reverse transcriptase, DNA polymerase, and RNase H activities; the surface ORF (S) generating the three HBsAg forms through alternative translation initiation; and the X ORF encoding the regulatory HBx protein involved in transcriptional activation. The genome's organization includes regulatory elements such as enhancers (EnhI and EnhII), promoters, and direct repeats (DR1 and DR2), enabling efficient transcription of multiple mRNAs from a single template, with the pregenomic RNA (pgRNA) serving as both the message for protein synthesis and the template for replication.
HBV replication occurs via reverse transcription of the pgRNA within immature nucleocapsids in the cytoplasm, a hallmark of hepadnaviruses. Upon hepatocyte entry, the rcDNA is transported to the nucleus, repaired by host enzymes into covalently closed circular DNA (cccDNA), which establishes a stable minichromosome-like template for viral transcription by cellular RNA polymerase II. The pgRNA is then encapsidated with polymerase, initiating reverse transcription to regenerate rcDNA for packaging into new virions, while subgenomic mRNAs direct envelope protein synthesis; this cycle allows persistent infection due to the long-lived cccDNA pool.

Pathogenesis

Hepatitis B virus (HBV) initiates infection by attaching to hepatocytes through the sodium taurocholate co-transporting polypeptide (NTCP) receptor, which facilitates viral entry via endocytosis and subsequent release of the nucleocapsid into the cytoplasm. Once inside, the relaxed circular DNA (rcDNA) genome is transported to the nucleus, where host enzymes convert it into covalently closed circular DNA (cccDNA), forming a stable minichromosome that serves as the primary template for viral transcription and the key reservoir for persistent infection. This cccDNA structure ensures long-term viral persistence, as it resists complete elimination by current therapies.
The HBV lifecycle proceeds with transcription of cccDNA by host RNA polymerase II to produce multiple viral RNAs, including the 3.5 kb pregenomic RNA (pgRNA) and subgenomic mRNAs for envelope and X proteins. These RNAs are exported to the cytoplasm for translation into core antigen, polymerase, surface antigens, and X protein, with pgRNA also serving as the template for reverse transcription. Nucleocapsids assemble around pgRNA, where reverse transcription generates new rcDNA; these are then enveloped by surface antigens in the endoplasmic reticulum and secreted as infectious virions, alongside abundant non-infectious subviral particles (22-nm spheres and filaments) composed of hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg). Subviral particles, vastly outnumbering virions, contribute to immune distraction without advancing replication.
Chronic HBV infection progresses through distinct phases reflecting host-virus dynamics. The immune-tolerant phase features high HBV DNA levels (20,000 IU/mL), HBeAg positivity, and normal alanine aminotransferase (ALT), with minimal liver inflammation due to ineffective immune recognition. This transitions to the immune-clearance phase, marked by elevated ALT, fluctuating HBV DNA, and active immune efforts to eliminate infected cells, often leading to HBeAg seroconversion. Resolution may occur, but many enter the inactive carrier phase, characterized by HBeAg negativity, anti-HBe positivity, low or undetectable HBV DNA, normal ALT, and reduced viral activity with minimal disease progression.
Chronicity arises primarily from perinatal or early childhood transmission, where immature immune systems in young hosts fail to mount effective T-cell responses, resulting in over 90% chronic infection rates in neonates compared to 5-10% in adults. HBV evades detection as a "stealth virus," inducing negligible innate immune responses such as minimal interferon or cytokine production during early infection, allowing unchecked replication before adaptive immunity develops. Additional tactics include HBeAg-mediated T-cell tolerance and secretion of regulatory factors that dampen antiviral signaling, promoting persistence.

Genotypes and Serotypes

The hepatitis B virus (HBV) exhibits significant genetic diversity, classified into 10 major genotypes (AJ) based on an intergenotypic nucleotide sequence divergence exceeding 8% across the full genome. These genotypes display distinct geographic distributions, reflecting historical migration patterns and transmission dynamics. For instance, genotype A predominates in sub-Saharan Africa, northern Europe, and the Americas; genotype B is prevalent in Southeast Asia, China, and Japan; genotype C circulates mainly in East and Southeast Asia as well as the Pacific Islands; genotype D has the broadest global spread, especially in the Mediterranean basin, Middle East, and South Asia; genotype E is confined largely to West and Central Africa; genotype F is indigenous to Central and South America; genotypes G and H are rare and primarily found in the United States and Central America, respectively; genotype I occurs in Southeast Asia, particularly Vietnam and Laos; and genotype J has been identified in the Ryukyu Islands of Japan.
Clinical outcomes vary by genotype, influencing disease progression and management. Genotype D is associated with a higher risk of cirrhosis and acute liver failure compared to other genotypes. In contrast, genotype A is linked to chronic hepatitis B and demonstrates superior responsiveness to interferon therapy. Genotype C correlates with more severe liver disease, including delayed HBeAg seroconversion and elevated hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) rates, with subgenotype C2 posing a greater HCC risk than C1. Additionally, genotype F1 exhibits higher HCC incidence relative to genotypes A, B, and D.
HBV serotypes are defined by antigenic variations in the hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg), yielding four principal serotypes: ayw, ayr, adw, and adr. These serotypes align with specific genotypesfor example, ayw is typical of genotypes B, D, and E, while adr predominates in genotype Cand show regional patterns, such as adw in Africa and Europe, adr in Asia, ayw in the Mediterranean and Africa, and ayr in East Asia. Clinically, serotypes impact immune recognition, with mutations in HBsAg (particularly in adw and ayw) enabling vaccine escape variants that reduce the efficacy of preventive measures.
Beyond major genotypes, HBV features over 30 subgenotypes (e.g., A1A3, B1B9, C1C15, D1D9) and recombinant forms, such as A/D hybrids in northern India and D/C (D9) in eastern India, which are increasingly detected due to advanced sequencing. These variants exhibit unique geographic nichessubgenotype A2 in Europe and North America, B2 in Chinaand carry clinical significance, including higher frequencies of immune escape mutations in subgenotypes like A2, B1, C6C15, D4, and F2, potentially complicating serological diagnostics and antiviral therapy responses.

Pathophysiology

Mechanisms of Liver Injury

Hepatitis B virus (HBV) is generally considered non-cytopathic, meaning it does not directly kill infected hepatocytes; however, certain viral proteins contribute to liver injury through indirect cytotoxic effects. The HBx protein, a key regulatory component of HBV, induces oxidative stress by disrupting mitochondrial function and generating reactive oxygen species (ROS) during viral replication, leading to DNA damage and cellular dysfunction in hepatocytes. This oxidative stress is exacerbated by endoplasmic reticulum stress triggered by high levels of viral antigens, such as HBsAg, which accumulate in the hepatocyte cytoplasm.
The primary mechanism of liver injury in HBV infection is immune-mediated, with HBV-specific CD8+ T cells playing a central role in targeting and lysing infected hepatocytes. These cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) recognize viral antigens presented on the hepatocyte surface via major histocompatibility complex class I molecules, leading to direct cell death through perforin-granzyme pathways. Additionally, the activation of CTLs triggers a cytokine storm, characterized by the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines like IFN- and TNF-, which recruit non-specific inflammatory cells such as neutrophils and macrophages, amplifying hepatocyte damage and contributing to acute flares in chronic infection.
Apoptosis of infected hepatocytes is a critical pathway in HBV-induced liver injury, mediated by death receptor signaling. The Fas/FasL pathway is prominently involved, where Fas ligand expressed on activated CD8+ T cells binds to Fas receptors on hepatocytes, initiating caspase-dependent apoptosis. Similarly, TNF- produced by immune cells binds to TNF receptors on hepatocytes, activating the extrinsic apoptosis pathway, with HBx enhancing sensitivity to this signal by upregulating TNF receptor expression.
Chronic inflammation from ongoing immune responses initiates fibrosis by activating hepatic stellate cells (HSCs), which transform into myofibroblast-like cells upon exposure to cytokines such as TGF- released during HBV infection. Activated HSCs then secrete excessive extracellular matrix components, including collagen, leading to deposition and scarring in the liver parenchyma; additionally, integration of HBV DNA into the host genome contributes to genomic instability and promotes oncogenesis, a process that can progress if viral persistence continues.

Immune Response and Persistence

The innate immune response to hepatitis B virus (HBV) infection plays a crucial role in limiting initial viral spread, primarily through natural killer (NK) cells and interferons. NK cells, which constitute 40-50% of liver lymphocytes, exert cytotoxic effects and produce interferon-gamma (IFN-) to inhibit HBV replication, often peaking during the early phases of acute infection. However, in chronic cases, NK cell function is impaired, with reduced expression of activating receptors like NKG2D and 2B4 due to transforming growth factor-beta 1 (TGF-1) and upregulation of inhibitory markers such as NKG2A and TIM-3, allowing viral persistence. Interferons, including type I (IFN-/) and type III (IFN-), further restrict HBV by downregulating viral mRNA and pre-genomic RNA transcription, though HBV evades robust activation through mechanisms like its polymerase inhibiting interferon regulatory factor 3 (IRF3) and mitochondrial antiviral-signaling protein (MAVS).
Adaptive immunity involves both humoral and cellular components that aim to control HBV but often fail in establishing chronic infection. Humoral responses center on B cells producing anti-hepatitis B surface antigen (anti-HBs) antibodies, which neutralize circulating viral particles and prevent reinfection, with efficacy enhanced by interleukin-21 (IL-21) from T follicular helper (Tfh) cells and chemokines like CXCL13 from Kupffer cells. Cellular immunity, driven by CD8+ T cells, clears infected hepatocytes via cytolytic and non-cytolytic mechanisms, such as IFN--mediated inhibition of viral replication; CD4+ T cells provide helper support, with early robust responses predicting viral clearance. In chronic HBV, however, CD8+ T cells undergo exhaustion, including unique dysfunction beyond exhaustion, characterized by diminished proliferation, cytokine production (e.g., IL-2, tumor necrosis factor-alpha , IFN-), and cytotoxicity due to persistent antigen exposure.
Mechanisms of HBV persistence primarily involve immune evasion and suppression, leading to T-cell dysfunction and tolerance, as well as the stability of covalently closed circular DNA (cccDNA) in hepatocytes, which serves as a reservoir for viral replication and reactivation. Upregulation of programmed death-1 (PD-1) on exhausted CD8+ T cells and its ligand PD-L1 on hepatocytes and antigen-presenting cells inhibits antiviral activity, with higher PD-1 expression correlating to elevated HBV DNA levels and reduced T-cell function; blockade of PD-1/PD-L1 restores proliferative and cytolytic responses in vitro. Regulatory T cells (Tregs), particularly CD4+CD25+Foxp3+ subsets, expand in chronic HBV and suppress effector T cells and NK cells through cytokines like IL-10 and TGF-, as well as CTLA-4-mediated inhibition, fostering an immunosuppressive liver microenvironment that promotes viral tolerance. Additionally, maternal HBeAg transmission can induce early immune tolerance by polarizing Kupffer cells toward an M2 phenotype, further dampening responses in infected offspring.
Serological markers reflect these immunological dynamics, particularly in the transition from tolerance to control. The HBeAg-positive tolerance phase features high viral loads, elevated HBeAg levels, and weak T-cell responses, indicative of immune unresponsiveness rather than true tolerance, often with minimal liver inflammation. Anti-HBe seroconversion, marked by HBeAg loss and anti-HBe antibody appearance, signals a shift toward immune activation, typically following hepatitis flares driven by enhanced cytotoxic T lymphocyte (CTL) activity and reduced viral replication, though it does not always eliminate the virus.

Diagnosis

Serological and Molecular Tests

Diagnosis of Hepatitis B relies on serological and molecular tests to detect viral antigens, antibodies, and nucleic acids, enabling identification of acute, chronic, or resolved infections. Serological tests primarily assess immune responses and viral presence through blood markers, while molecular tests quantify viral load and identify genetic variants. These methods are essential for confirming infection status, guiding clinical decisions, and screening at-risk populations.

Serological Markers

The primary serological markers for Hepatitis B virus (HBV) include hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg), antibody to hepatitis B core antigen (anti-HBc), hepatitis B e antigen (HBeAg), antibody to HBeAg (anti-HBe), and antibody to HBsAg (anti-HBs). Quantitative HBsAg (qHBsAg) levels are also used to define disease phases and assess hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) risk, particularly in HBeAg-negative chronic infection where levels >1,000 IU/mL indicate higher risk. HBsAg is the first detectable marker of active infection, appearing 1-10 weeks after exposure and indicating current HBV presence in acute or chronic cases. Anti-HBc signifies past or current exposure to HBV; its total form (IgG + IgM) persists lifelong, while the IgM subclass specifically denotes recent infection within the past 6 months. HBeAg reflects high viral replication and infectivity during active phases, typically in early acute or immune-tolerant chronic infection, whereas anti-HBe indicates reduced replication and lower transmissibility. Finally, anti-HBs denotes immunity from vaccination or resolved infection, appearing after HBsAg clearance.
Interpretation of these markers often involves a standard three-test panel recommended by health authorities: HBsAg, total anti-HBc, and anti-HBs, which distinguishes susceptible, infected, or immune states. IgM anti-HBc is additionally used to distinguish acute from chronic infection when HBsAg is positive. As of 2025-2026, interpretation guidelines from CDC, AASLD/IDSA, and Hepatitis B Online remain consistent with established patterns, emphasizing the triple panel and the role of IgM anti-HBc. Chronic infection is defined by persistent HBsAg for more than 6 months, often with total anti-HBc positivity and IgM anti-HBc negativity, with variable HBeAg status depending on replication phase. Isolated anti-HBc requires further evaluation, such as HBV DNA testing, to rule out occult infection or other causes per CDC 2025 guidance.

Molecular Tests

Molecular diagnostics, particularly polymerase chain reaction (PCR)-based assays for HBV DNA, provide direct evidence of viral replication and are crucial for confirming infection in serologically ambiguous cases, such as occult HBV (where HBsAg is negative but HBV DNA persists). Quantitative real-time PCR measures viral load in international units per milliliter (IU/mL), aiding in distinguishing active from inactive chronic infection; levels above 2,000 IU/mL often indicate high replication needing intervention. Qualitative PCR detects low-level viremia, essential for blood donor screening and identifying occult infections. Emerging molecular biomarkers include hepatitis B core-related antigen (HBcrAg), which predicts HCC risk (e.g., >2.9 log10 U/mL in HBeAg-negative patients) and monitors treatment response, and HBV RNA, used to assess ongoing replication and relapse risk post-therapy, with lower levels associated with better outcomes.
Genotyping via PCR and sequencing identifies HBV genotypes (A through J), which influence disease progression, treatment response, and vaccine escape; for instance, genotype C is associated with higher hepatocellular carcinoma risk in some populations. These tests are performed on plasma or serum and follow standardized protocols from organizations like the World Health Organization.

Limitations

Serological tests face challenges including the serological window period (occurring after HBsAg clearance during resolution of acute infection but before anti-HBs appearance, typically weeks to months later), during which isolated anti-HBc positivity (HBsAg negative, anti-HBs negative, total anti-HBc positive) may lead to indeterminate results. Isolated anti-HBc can also result from other causes such as occult HBV infection, resolved infection with waning anti-HBs levels, false-positive results, chronic infection with variant strains evading HBsAg detection, or passive antibody transfer. In these scenarios, further evaluation with HBV DNA testing is recommended to clarify the diagnosis, detect active viral replication, and guide management, per CDC 2025 guidance. HBV mutants, such as those affecting the S gene, can evade HBsAg detection in some chronic cases, leading to occult HBV infection, necessitating molecular confirmation with HBV DNA PCR to avoid missed diagnoses. Assay sensitivity varies, with some rapid tests showing lower detection limits for low-level antigens, and cross-reactivity with other hepatitis viruses is rare but possible.

Clinical Evaluation and Imaging

Clinical evaluation of hepatitis B begins with a detailed medical history to identify risk factors for infection, such as unprotected sexual contact, intravenous drug use, occupational exposure among healthcare workers, or residence in endemic areas, which guide suspicion for acute or chronic disease. Family history of hepatitis B or liver disease is also assessed to evaluate potential vertical transmission or household spread. In acute infection, patients may report prodromal symptoms like fatigue, nausea, and low-grade fever, while chronic cases are often asymptomatic until advanced stages.
Physical examination focuses on signs of liver involvement and chronic liver disease. Hepatomegaly, tenderness over the liver, and splenomegaly may be present in acute hepatitis B, reflecting inflammation and congestion. In chronic infection, findings such as jaundice, ascites, palmar erythema, spider angiomata, and gynecomastia indicate decompensated cirrhosis. Ascites, detected by shifting dullness or fluid wave, signals portal hypertension, a common complication in advanced chronic hepatitis B. These exam findings, combined with serological confirmation of hepatitis B virus markers, help differentiate acute from chronic infection.
Liver function tests provide essential insights into hepatic injury patterns. In acute hepatitis B, alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST) levels typically rise markedly, often exceeding 1,000 IU/L with ALT greater than AST, reflecting hepatocellular necrosis. Total bilirubin is frequently elevated, contributing to jaundice, while alkaline phosphatase remains normal or mildly increased. In chronic hepatitis B, elevations are milder, usually less than five times the upper limit of normal, and may fluctuate; persistently normal levels do not exclude significant disease. Albumin levels and prothrombin time assess synthetic function, with hypoalbuminemia (<3.5 g/dL) and prolonged prothrombin time emerging in advanced fibrosis or cirrhosis due to impaired hepatocyte production.
Imaging modalities are crucial for visualizing liver morphology and detecting complications without invasive procedures. Abdominal ultrasound serves as the initial imaging tool, screening for chronic hepatitis B patients at risk for hepatocellular carcinoma; it identifies hepatomegaly, steatosis (fatty infiltration), and indirect signs of fibrosis such as coarsened echotexture or nodular contours. Transient elastography, or FibroScan, quantifies liver stiffness in kilopascals to stage fibrosis non-invasively, with values >7 kPa suggesting significant fibrosis and >12 kPa indicating cirrhosis in hepatitis B. For suspected masses, computed tomography (CT) or magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) with contrast enhances characterization, distinguishing benign lesions from hepatocellular carcinoma based on enhancement patterns.
Liver biopsy is indicated in chronic hepatitis B when non-invasive assessments are inconclusive or to precisely stage disease for treatment decisions. It provides histological evaluation of fibrosis using the Metavir scoring system, where fibrosis is graded from F0 (no fibrosis) to F4 (cirrhosis), helping identify patients with moderate (F2) or advanced (F3-F4) disease who may benefit from antiviral therapy. Biopsy is particularly useful in cases of discordant non-invasive test results or to rule out coexisting conditions like steatohepatitis.

Prevention

The primary and most effective prevention for hepatitis B is vaccination, administered as a three-dose series that provides lifelong protection in most recipients who achieve seroprotection. Complementary behavioral measures to avoid transmission include consistent condom use during sexual activity, not sharing sharp tools such as razors or needles, and adhering to safe injection and hygiene practices.

Vaccination Strategies

The hepatitis B vaccine consists primarily of recombinant hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg), a protein derived from yeast cells that mimics the virus's outer envelope to stimulate an immune response without causing infection. Licensed single-antigen vaccines in the United States include Engerix-B, Recombivax HB, Heplisav-B, and PreHevbrio, with Engerix-B and Recombivax HB containing 1020 mcg of HBsAg per dose depending on age and formulation, Heplisav-B containing 20 mcg per dose in a 2-dose adult regimen, and PreHevbrio containing 10 mcg of each of three HBV surface antigens in a 3-dose series. These vaccines are plasma-derived-free, ensuring safety from blood-borne contaminants, and are administered intramuscularly.
The standard vaccination schedule for most individuals is a three-dose series given at 0, 1, and 6 months, which provides optimal seroprotection by achieving protective antibody levels (anti-HBs 10 mIU/mL) in the majority of recipients. Alternative accelerated schedules, such as 0, 1, 2, and 12 months or the 2-dose Heplisav-B regimen (0 and 1 month) for adults aged 18 and older, may be used for certain populations like travelers or those needing rapid protection, but the 016 month regimen remains the cornerstone for routine immunization. For infants, the first dose is recommended within 24 hours of birth to prevent perinatal transmission.
The vaccine demonstrates high efficacy, conferring protection against hepatitis B virus infection in over 95% of healthy infants, children, and young adults who complete the series. In healthy adults, efficacy ranges from 80% to 95%, with lower rates observed in immunocompromised individuals, such as those with HIV or undergoing dialysis, where response rates may drop to 5070%. Protection is long-lasting, persisting for at least 20 years in most recipients and likely lifelong, as booster doses are not routinely required for those with initial seroprotection.
Major health organizations recommend universal vaccination of all infants at birth as the primary strategy to eliminate hepatitis B transmission, followed by catch-up vaccination for unvaccinated children and adolescents up to age 19. For adults, routine vaccination is advised for those aged 1959, with catch-up for older unvaccinated adults, particularly high-risk groups including healthcare workers, people who inject drugs, men who have sex with men, and household contacts of infected individuals. These recommendations aim to achieve population-level immunity and reduce chronic infection rates. As of 2025, global hepatitis B vaccination coverage stands at approximately 85% for the 3-dose series (2022 data), supporting WHO goals to reach 90% by 2030 with no major changes to core strategies following the 2024 guidelines.

Post-Exposure Prophylaxis

Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) for hepatitis B virus (HBV) infection is a critical intervention aimed at preventing transmission following known or suspected exposure, particularly in high-risk scenarios such as percutaneous injuries, sexual contact, or perinatal transmission from infected mothers. This approach combines passive immunity through hepatitis B immune globulin (HBIG) with active immunization via the hepatitis B vaccine to provide rapid and sustained protection. HBIG, derived from plasma of donors with high levels of anti-HBs antibodies, neutralizes circulating HBV particles immediately after administration, while the vaccine stimulates the recipient's immune system to produce protective antibodies over time.
Although rare, HBV transmission can occur through human bites that draw blood, particularly in child daycare settings where the biter is infected with HBV, the bite breaks the skin (percutaneous exposure), and infected blood or saliva enters the wound of a susceptible (unvaccinated or non-immune) child. Such transmission is low-risk, as skin-breaking bites occur in only about 2% of bite incidents, and widespread vaccination among children provides effective protection. If a bite breaks the skin, prompt medical evaluation is recommended, and post-exposure prophylaxis should be initiated for the bitten child if unvaccinated or non-immune, following standard PEP protocols for percutaneous exposures, including assessment of the source's HBsAg status.
For unvaccinated or incompletely vaccinated individuals exposed to blood or body fluids from an HBsAg-positive source, a single dose of HBIG (0.06 mL/kg intramuscularly) should be administered as soon as possible, ideally within 24 hours of exposure, at a site separate from the vaccine injection. The hepatitis B vaccine series should be initiated concurrently, with the first dose given within 24 hours to maximize efficacy; the full three-dose series is then completed according to standard schedules (or 2-dose for Heplisav-B if appropriate). For individuals who refuse vaccination, a second dose of HBIG should be given 1 month after the first. In cases of high-risk occupational exposures like needlestick injuries from HBsAg-positive sources, this combined regimen (one HBIG dose plus vaccine for susceptible personnel) is recommended; known vaccine non-responders receive two HBIG doses separated by 1 month without vaccination. For sexual exposures, HBIG can be given up to 14 days post-exposure, and for perinatal or other mucosal exposures, prophylaxis remains effective if initiated within 7 days.
The efficacy of PEP is well-established, particularly in preventing perinatal transmission. HBIG alone reduces the risk of chronic HBV infection in neonates born to HBsAg-positive mothers by approximately 70-75%, while the hepatitis B vaccine alone achieves about 75% protection; the combined regimen increases efficacy to 85-95%. In occupational settings, prompt administration of HBIG and vaccine has been shown to prevent infection in over 90% of susceptible individuals following percutaneous exposure to HBsAg-positive blood. Serologic testing of the exposed person 1-2 months post-vaccination confirms an adequate response (anti-HBs 10 mIU/mL), guiding any need for additional doses.
Determination of the source patient's HBsAg status is essential to tailor PEP decisions. If the source is HBsAg-positive, full prophylaxis is indicated for susceptible exposed individuals; if negative, no PEP is required. For sources with unknown status, such as in anonymous sexual encounters, testing should be attempted urgently, but prophylaxis should not be delayed if high-risk exposure is suspectedinitiating the vaccine series alone is advised pending results. A comprehensive source panel including HBsAg, anti-HBs, and total anti-HBc helps clarify acute, chronic, or resolved infection status to inform risk assessment.

Infection Control Measures

Infection control measures for hepatitis B virus (HBV) emphasize screening high-risk populations and implementing safe practices to interrupt transmission via percutaneous, mucosal, or perinatal exposure. These strategies complement broader prevention efforts by targeting potential sources of infection in healthcare, community, and household settings.
Screening of blood donors is a cornerstone of infection control, with the World Health Organization (WHO) recommending testing of all blood donations for HBV surface antigen (HBsAg) to prevent transfusion-related transmission, which has been largely eliminated in screened systems. For pregnant women, universal HBsAg screening during each pregnancypreferably in the first trimesteris advised by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), regardless of prior vaccination or testing history, to identify carriers and enable timely interventions that reduce mother-to-child transmission rates to under 5%. Healthcare workers, as part of universal adult screening recommendations, undergo triple-panel testing (HBsAg, anti-HBs, and total anti-HBc) at least once in their lifetime, particularly those with occupational exposure risks, to ensure they are immune or managed appropriately to avoid iatrogenic spread.
Safe practices in healthcare and community settings further mitigate HBV spread. Universal precautions, as outlined by the CDC, require treating all blood and certain body fluids (e.g., semen, vaginal secretions) as potentially infectious, mandating the use of gloves, gowns, masks, and eye protection during procedures involving sharps or splashes, along with safe injection practices to prevent needlestick injuries. Sterilization of equipment is critical; reusable medical devices must be decontaminated via autoclaving or chemical methods, while single-use sharps should be disposed of in puncture-resistant containers without recapping or bending to eliminate contamination risks. In community contexts, education on safe sexsuch as consistent condom use during sexual activityreduces sexual transmission, which accounts for a significant portion of adult infections, while promoting avoidance of shared personal items like razors or toothbrushes and handwashing after blood exposure helps prevent household spread.
Perinatal prevention specifically targets high maternal viral loads to avert vertical transmission. For HBsAg-positive pregnant women with HBV DNA levels exceeding 200,000 IU/mL, the CDC and WHO endorse antiviral therapy, such as tenofovir disoproxil fumarate starting from week 28 of gestation through delivery (and potentially 3 months postpartum), which has demonstrated efficacy in reducing transmission rates by over 90% when combined with neonatal prophylaxis.
The 2024 WHO guidelines introduce simplified testing algorithms tailored for low-resource settings, recommending point-of-care (POC) assays for HBsAg and reflex HBV DNA testing to streamline diagnosis and eligibility assessment, thereby expanding access to care and prophylaxis without requiring advanced laboratory infrastructure. These updates also broaden antiviral prophylaxis criteria for pregnant women, aiming to boost global birth-dose vaccination coverage and address barriers in implementation across diverse settings.

Management

Acute Infection Treatment

The management of acute hepatitis B virus (HBV) infection primarily involves supportive care, as the majority of cases in immunocompetent adults resolve spontaneously without specific antiviral intervention. Patients are advised to rest, maintain adequate hydration, consume a balanced diet, and avoid hepatotoxic substances such as alcohol and unnecessary medications to support liver recovery. Hospitalization is reserved for severe cases, including those with dehydration, coagulopathy, or encephalopathy, while fulminant hepatitis Boccurring in less than 1% of acute infectionsrequires intensive care unit admission due to the high risk of liver failure and mortality exceeding 60% without prompt management.
Antiviral therapy is rarely indicated for uncomplicated acute HBV but is recommended for severe or fulminant cases to inhibit viral replication and potentially reduce mortality. Preferred agents include oral nucleoside/nucleotide analogues such as entecavir or tenofovir disoproxil fumarate, initiated as soon as severe hepatic impairment is evident, with treatment typically continued until clinical resolution. These therapies have demonstrated efficacy in bridging patients to recovery or liver transplantation in fulminant scenarios, though evidence from randomized trials remains limited due to the rarity of such cases.
Monitoring during acute infection focuses on assessing liver function and viral clearance to confirm resolution and rule out progression to chronicity. Serial measurements of alanine aminotransferase (ALT) levels track normalization, typically occurring within weeks to months in resolving cases, while hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg) seroclearance within 6 months indicates acute self-limited infection in over 95% of immunocompetent adults. In immunosuppressed patients, such as those on chemotherapy or with HIV, acute HBV carries a higher risk of severity and chronicity, necessitating earlier antiviral intervention and closer surveillance to prevent fulminant progression.

Chronic Infection Therapy

Therapy for chronic hepatitis B virus (HBV) infection primarily aims to achieve sustained viral suppression, prevent liver disease progression, and, in ideal cases, induce HBeAg or HBsAg seroconversion toward a functional cure. Current guidelines emphasize long-term suppression using oral nucleos(t)ide analogs, with interferon-based therapy reserved for select patients due to its finite duration and potential for immune-mediated clearance. Emerging investigational approaches, such as small interfering RNA (siRNA) therapeutics, target HBsAg reduction to facilitate functional cure.
The 2024 World Health Organization (WHO) guidelines have expanded treatment eligibility to simplify care and increase access, recommending antiviral therapy for all adults and adolescents aged 12 years or older with cirrhosis (e.g., APRI >1.0 or liver stiffness measurement >12.5 kPa) or significant fibrosis (F2; e.g., APRI >0.5 or liver stiffness >7.0 kPa), irrespective of alanine aminotransferase (ALT) levels or HBV DNA concentrations. Additional criteria include HBV DNA >2,000 IU/mL with persistently elevated ALT (> upper limit of normal on two occasions 6-12 months apart), coinfections (e.g., HIV, HDV), immunosuppression, family history of cirrhosis or hepatocellular carcinoma, or extrahepatic manifestations. This expansion aims to treat at least 50% of HBsAg-positive individuals, up from 8-20% under prior thresholds.
First-line therapies consist of high-potency nucleos(t)ide analogs with a low risk of resistance: entecavir (0.5 mg daily for nucleoside-naive patients) and tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (300 mg daily). Tenofovir alafenamide (25 mg daily) is preferred over disoproxil in patients with renal impairment (eGFR <60 mL/min) or bone disease due to reduced renal and bone toxicity. These agents achieve >95% virological suppression within 1 year in treatment-naive patients and are safe for indefinite use, including in pregnancy for tenofovir disoproxil when HBV DNA 200,000 IU/mL.
Pegylated interferon-alpha (e.g., 180 g weekly for 48 weeks) offers finite-duration therapy with higher rates of HBeAg seroconversion (20-30%) and potential HBsAg loss (3-7%) compared to nucleos(t)ide analogs alone, particularly in HBeAg-positive patients with genotype A or high ALT levels (>2x ULN). However, it is contraindicated in decompensated cirrhosis, psychiatric disorders, or cytopenias, and the 2024 WHO guidelines do not recommend it due to tolerability issues and limited efficacy in broader populations. American Association for the Study of Liver Diseases (AASLD) guidelines endorse it as an alternative first-line option in select immune-active cases without contraindications.
The 2025 AASLD/IDSA guidelines update treatment recommendations, including for HBsAg-positive persons in immune-tolerant or indeterminate phases and criteria for antiviral therapy withdrawal, aligning with expanded eligibility while emphasizing evidence-based surveillance.
Emerging strategies pursue functional cure (sustained HBsAg loss off-therapy) through siRNA agents like JNJ-73763989 (formerly JNJ-3989), a subcutaneously administered liver-targeted siRNA that degrades HBV transcripts, reducing HBsAg by up to 2.5 log IU/mL when combined with nucleos(t)ide analogs. Phase II trials (e.g., RIVER and MARCH) demonstrate HBsAg seroconversion in 10-19% of patients after 24-48 weeks, often with pegylated interferon add-on, though long-term durability requires further validation.

Monitoring and Antiviral Resistance

For patients on long-term antiviral therapy for chronic hepatitis B (HBV), monitoring protocols aim to assess virological suppression, biochemical response, serological changes, and the risk of hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC). The 2025 American Association for the Study of Liver Diseases (AASLD)/Infectious Diseases Society of America (IDSA) guidelines recommend HBV DNA levels be measured every 3-6 months initially to confirm viral suppression (targeting undetectable levels <20 IU/mL), transitioning to every 6-12 months once achieved, with adjustments based on phase of infection and risk factors. Alanine aminotransferase (ALT) levels are evaluated every 3-6 months to monitor liver inflammation, with normalization indicating effective therapy. In HBeAg-positive patients, HBeAg status is assessed every 6-12 months to detect seroconversion, a key milestone for potential treatment de-escalation. The European Association for the Study of the Liver (EASL) 2025 guidelines align closely, recommending HBV DNA and ALT monitoring every 3-6 months initially, transitioning to every 6-12 months post-response, and HBeAg evaluation annually or with significant changes in ALT or HBV DNA.
HCC surveillance remains essential for treated patients, particularly those with cirrhosis or high-risk features, involving abdominal ultrasound every 6 months, with alpha-fetoprotein (AFP) as an optional adjunct based on risk stratification tools like PAGE-B score >10. The 2025 AASLD/IDSA guidelines expand surveillance recommendations for HBsAg-positive persons with coinfections (e.g., HCV, HDV, HIV) and post-HBsAg loss. These protocols build on markers used for therapy initiation, such as baseline HBV DNA and ALT thresholds, to guide ongoing adjustments. Non-adherence to monitoring can lead to undetected flares or progression, underscoring the need for regular follow-up in specialized care settings.
Antiviral resistance complicates long-term management of chronic HBV, with patterns varying by nucleos(t)ide analogue. Lamivudine resistance is high, reaching up to 76% after 5 years of therapy, primarily due to mutations in the YMDD motif (tyrosine-methionine-aspartate-aspartate) of the HBV polymerase, such as rtM204V/I. In contrast, entecavir and tenofovir exhibit low resistance rates (<1% in nucleoside-naive patients), though entecavir resistance can emerge in lamivudine-experienced cases via additional mutations like rtT184, rtS202, or rtM250 alongside YMDD variants. Genotyping of the HBV reverse transcriptase/polymerase region is recommended upon virological breakthrough (persistent HBV DNA >200 IU/mL after initial suppression) to identify specific mutants and inform salvage therapy.
Management of resistance prioritizes switching to a non-cross-resistant agent, with tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF) or tenofovir alafenamide (TAF) as first-line options for lamivudine-resistant cases, achieving viral suppression in over 90% of patients without need for combination therapy in most scenarios. Combination regimens, such as tenofovir plus entecavir, are rarely required except in multidrug-resistant strains or decompensated cirrhosis, due to tenofovir's high barrier to resistance.

Prognosis and Complications

Short-Term and Long-Term Outcomes

In acute hepatitis B virus (HBV) infection among adults, approximately 95% of cases resolve spontaneously with complete recovery and clearance of hepatitis B surface antigen within six months. However, a small proportionapproximately 1% (ranging from 0.5% to 2%) of acute infectionsprogress to fulminant hepatitis, which carries a high mortality rate of 60-80% without liver transplantation. These severe outcomes are more common in certain high-risk groups, such as those with underlying immunosuppression or co-infections, though overall acute mortality remains low at less than 1%.
For chronic HBV infection, 15% to 25% of affected individuals progress to cirrhosis over 20 to 30 years, driven by persistent viral replication and liver inflammation. Among those who develop cirrhosis due to HBV, 20% to 30% are at risk of progressing to hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC), particularly if viral suppression is inadequate. This progression underscores the importance of monitoring, as untreated chronic disease significantly elevates the lifetime risk of liver-related complications.
Survival outcomes vary by disease phase. In inactive HBV carrierscharacterized by low viral load and normal liver enzymesmedian survival exceeds 20 years, with long-term studies showing stability in the vast majority over 15 to 18 years of follow-up and low rates of reactivation or decompensation. In contrast, patients with active chronic HBV, marked by elevated alanine aminotransferase and high HBV DNA levels, experience reduced survival, with liver-related mortality increasing in advanced cases due to faster progression to end-stage liver disease.
Several factors influence the rate of disease progression in chronic HBV. Older age at infection increases the likelihood of chronicity and complications, while younger age (e.g., perinatal acquisition) heightens long-term risks. HBV genotypes, such as C and D, are associated with more aggressive liver damage and higher cirrhosis rates compared to genotypes A and B. Additionally, alcohol consumption acts as a cofactor, accelerating fibrosis and elevating HCC risk by 2- to 6-fold in heavy drinkers with HBV.

Cirrhosis and Hepatocellular Carcinoma

Chronic hepatitis B virus (HBV) infection can lead to cirrhosis through progressive liver fibrosis, characterized by the accumulation of extracellular matrix proteins that distort hepatic architecture. This process is driven by persistent viral replication and a dysregulated immune response, resulting in ongoing necroinflammation and activation of hepatic stellate cells, which promote fibrogenesis. Over time, advanced fibrosis culminates in cirrhosis, where regenerative nodules form amid bands of scar tissue, impairing liver function.
In patients with HBV-related cirrhosis, portal hypertension develops due to increased intrahepatic resistance and splanchnic vasodilation, leading to complications such as esophageal varices, which can rupture and cause life-threatening bleeding, and ascites, resulting from hypoalbuminemia and sodium retention. These manifestations signify decompensated cirrhosis, with portal hypertension exacerbating the risk of hepatic decompensation. Management focuses on mitigating these sequelae through beta-blockers for variceal prevention and diuretics for ascites control, alongside addressing the underlying HBV infection.
Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) poses a significant risk in HBV-related cirrhosis, with an annual incidence of 2-5% among affected individuals. HBV contributes to oncogenesis through direct mechanisms, including viral DNA integration into the host genome, which disrupts tumor suppressor genes like TERT and promotes proto-oncogene activation, thereby driving malignant transformation even in the absence of active inflammation. This integration occurs early in infection and persists, amplifying carcinogenic potential in cirrhotic livers.
Screening for HCC is recommended for all patients with HBV-related cirrhosis, initiated at the time of diagnosis, according to the American Association for the Study of Liver Diseases (AASLD) guidelines. This involves biannual abdominal ultrasound surveillance, with or without serum alpha-fetoprotein (AFP) measurement, to detect early-stage tumors amenable to curative interventions. High-risk features, such as elevated HBV DNA levels or family history of HCC, may warrant more intensive monitoring.
Prevention of cirrhosis progression and HCC development relies on antiviral therapy with nucleos(t)ide analogs, such as entecavir or tenofovir, which suppress HBV replication and reduce HCC risk by 50-70% in cirrhotic patients by halting fibrosis advancement and mitigating oncogenic drivers. For those with decompensated cirrhosis, orthotopic liver transplantation offers a curative option, provided HBV is controlled perioperatively with antivirals and hepatitis B immunoglobulin to prevent graft reinfection. Post-transplant outcomes have improved significantly with these strategies, achieving long-term survival rates comparable to non-HBV recipients.

Reactivation Risks

Hepatitis B virus (HBV) reactivation refers to the re-emergence of viral replication in individuals with previously resolved or suppressed infection, often leading to acute hepatitis flares that can result in liver failure or death. This phenomenon is particularly concerning in patients undergoing immunosuppressive therapies, where the virus can reactivate from a dormant state in hepatocytes. Reactivation is defined by a significant increase in HBV DNA levels (typically >1 log10 IU/mL) accompanied by elevated alanine aminotransferase (ALT) levels, and it occurs in both HBsAg-positive chronic carriers and those with resolved infection.
Key triggers for HBV reactivation include immunosuppressive treatments such as chemotherapy for malignancies, biologic agents like rituximab for autoimmune diseases or lymphomas, and high-dose corticosteroids. For instance, rituximab, a monoclonal antibody targeting CD20 on B cells, profoundly increases reactivation risk by depleting humoral immunity essential for HBV control, with rates up to 20-50% in HBsAg-negative, anti-HBc-positive patients without prophylaxis. High baseline HBV viral load prior to immunosuppression further amplifies this risk, as residual virus in the liver reservoir can rapidly replicate upon immune suppression. Other triggers encompass organ transplantation and HIV therapy interruptions, though the highest incidences are linked to B-cell depleting agents.
Risk stratification is crucial for identifying vulnerable patients and involves serological and molecular markers. Individuals with resolved HBV infection, characterized by anti-HBc positivity and HBsAg negativity, face intermediate reactivation risk (3-20% depending on therapy intensity), while those with occult HBV infection (HBsAg-negative but HBV DNA-positive in liver or serum) exhibit higher susceptibility due to persistent low-level viremia. Chronic HBsAg carriers have the highest risk (up to 70% without prophylaxis during intense immunosuppression), stratified further by viral load and genotype. Guidelines recommend assessing HBV status via HBsAg, anti-HBc, and HBV DNA testing before initiating immunosuppression to categorize patients into low, moderate, or high-risk groups.
Prevention strategies emphasize pre-emptive antiviral therapy and routine screening. For high-risk patients, such as those receiving rituximab or anthracycline-based chemotherapy, entecavir or tenofovir is recommended for at least 12 months post-therapy due to their potency and low resistance rates, reducing reactivation incidence by over 80%. In contrast, lamivudine may suffice for short-term, low-intensity immunosuppression like brief corticosteroid courses, though its higher resistance potential limits long-term use. Universal screening for HBV markers prior to immunosuppressive therapy, followed by vaccination for susceptible individuals, forms the cornerstone of prevention, as endorsed by major hepatology societies.
Management of reactivation requires prompt intervention to mitigate hepatic injury. Upon detection of rising HBV DNA or ALT flares (e.g., >5x upper limit of normal), immediate initiation of nucleoside/nucleotide analogs like entecavir (0.5 mg daily) or tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (300 mg daily) is essential, often continued indefinitely in chronic cases. Close monitoring with weekly ALT and HBV DNA assays during high-risk periods allows early detection, while supportive care addresses symptoms like jaundice or coagulopathy. In severe cases with liver failure, hospitalization and potential liver transplantation evaluation are warranted, underscoring the need for multidisciplinary coordination.

Epidemiology

Global Prevalence and Burden

Hepatitis B remains a major global health challenge, with an estimated 254 million people living with chronic infection worldwide in 2022. This equates to a global prevalence of approximately 3.2% among adults. Each year, around 1.2 million new infections occur, contributing to the ongoing disease burden.
The distribution of chronic hepatitis B varies significantly by region, with the highest burdens concentrated in low- and middle-income areas. The WHO Western Pacific Region accounts for the largest number of cases, with 97 million people chronically infected, followed by the African Region at 65 million and the South-East Asia Region at 61 million. In contrast, the European Region and Region of the Americas report lower figures, at 11 million and 5 million chronic cases, respectively. These disparities highlight the uneven impact across continents, driven by differences in vaccination coverage and healthcare access.
The disease imposes a substantial health burden, resulting in an estimated 1.1 million deaths in 2022, the majority attributable to cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma. This positions hepatitis B as a leading cause of liver-related mortality globally. Despite these figures, trends indicate some progress: vaccination efforts have reduced chronic infection rates among children under 5 years to less than 1% in 2019, down from around 5% in the pre-vaccine era. Overall prevalence has shown a modest decline of about 7% from 2015 to 2019, though absolute numbers remain high, and implementation gaps in low-income settings continue to hinder further reductions.

Transmission Patterns and Risk Groups

Hepatitis B virus (HBV) transmission exhibits distinct patterns influenced by regional endemicity and socioeconomic factors. In highly endemic regions such as sub-Saharan Africa and East Asia, perinatal transmission from mother to child predominates, accounting for the majority of chronic infections due to the high viral loads often present in carrier mothers during delivery. Conversely, in low-prevalence areas like North America and Western Europe, transmission occurs primarily through sexual contact among high-risk behavioral groups or horizontal spread in close household settings, reflecting adult-onset infections with lower chronicity rates.
Certain demographic and behavioral populations face elevated risks of HBV acquisition and progression to chronicity. Infants born to hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg)-positive mothers are particularly vulnerable, with approximately 90% of infected neonates developing chronic infection if untreated, underscoring the critical window for intervention at birth. Injection drug users (IDUs) represent a key high-risk group due to shared needles facilitating bloodborne spread, while men who have sex with men (MSM) experience heightened exposure through sexual networks, especially in low-endemic settings. Healthcare workers are also at increased risk from occupational exposures like needlestick injuries, necessitating routine vaccination and post-exposure prophylaxis. Additionally, individuals in endemic regions, including migrants from high-prevalence areas, bear disproportionate burdens due to early-life exposures.
Co-infections with human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) or hepatitis C virus (HCV) exacerbate HBV transmission dynamics through synergistic effects on viral replication and immune response. In HIV/HBV co-infected individuals, the risk of mother-to-child HBV transmission increases approximately 2.5-fold, driven by HIV-induced immunosuppression that promotes HBV replication.
Surveillance data highlight evolving trends among people who inject drugs (PWID), a major risk group for HBV. The U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) 2025 Viral Hepatitis National Progress Report indicates that the reported rate of new HBV infections among PWID aged 1840 years in 2023 was 40% below the 2025 national target of 1.0 cases per 100,000 population, attributable to expanded harm reduction strategies such as syringe services programs and substance use disorder treatment access. These interventions have contributed to a 25% or greater reduction in infection rates since 2015 baselines, demonstrating the efficacy of targeted public health measures in curbing bloodborne transmission.

History

Discovery and Early Research

In 1965, Baruch Blumberg and his colleagues at the National Institutes of Health identified an unusual antigen in the serum of an Australian Aboriginal patient while studying serum protein polymorphisms among diverse populations; this "Australia antigen" (Au antigen) was later recognized as the hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg), marking the first specific marker for hepatitis B virus (HBV) infection. The discovery arose serendipitously during investigations into why certain individuals developed antibodies to foreign serum proteins after multiple transfusions, revealing that Au antigen was associated with acute hepatitis cases among transfused patients. This breakthrough enabled serological detection of HBV carriers, transforming understanding of post-transfusion hepatitis, previously known as serum hepatitis.
In 1970, David Dane and colleagues visualized the complete HBV virion, termed the Dane particle, using electron microscopy on serum from patients positive for Australia antigen; this 42-nm double-shelled particle, containing a 27-nm core, confirmed HBV as a distinct virus rather than just a soluble antigen. The observation of these intact virions alongside smaller 22-nm spherical and tubular forms clarified the morphology of HBV and supported its classification as a member of the Hepadnaviridae family.
During the 1970s, large-scale serum surveys established a strong epidemiological link between chronic HBV infection and primary liver cancer (hepatocellular carcinoma, HCC); for instance, a prospective study of 7,498 Japanese men in Hawaii found that those positive for HBsAg had a 39-fold increased risk of developing HCC over 13 years compared to seronegative individuals. Similar surveys in high-prevalence areas like Taiwan in the mid-1970s, led by Richard Beasley, demonstrated that HBV carriers faced a relative risk of HCC exceeding 100 times that of non-carriers, highlighting vertical transmission and chronic carriage as key drivers of oncogenesis.
Key milestones in early HBV research included the development of sensitive radioimmunoassay (RIA) tests for HBsAg in the early 1970s, with widespread blood bank screening implemented by 1971, reducing transfusion-related transmissions by approximately 25%. In 1979, Pierre Charnay and colleagues achieved the first molecular cloning of the HBV genome (subtype ayw) in Escherichia coli, enabling full nucleotide sequencing and paving the way for genetic analyses of viral replication. These advances culminated in the 1976 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine awarded to Blumberg (shared with Daniel Carleton Gajdusek) for the discovery of the Australia antigen and its causative role in hepatitis B.

Vaccine Development and Milestones

The development of the hepatitis B vaccine began with the plasma-derived formulation, which was first licensed in the United States in 1981 as Heptavax-B by Merck, following purification of the hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg) from plasma of asymptomatic carriers. This vaccine proved safe and effective but raised concerns over potential transmission of blood-borne pathogens like HIV, leading to its replacement by recombinant DNA technology-based vaccines. The first recombinant hepatitis B vaccine, Recombivax HB, was approved by the FDA in 1986, produced using yeast cells engineered to express HBsAg, thereby eliminating risks associated with human plasma. A second recombinant vaccine, Engerix-B, followed in 1989, marking a pivotal shift toward safer, scalable production methods that facilitated global distribution.
Key milestones in vaccine implementation include the World Health Organization's (WHO) 1992 recommendation for universal infant immunization against hepatitis B in all countries, aiming to integrate the vaccine into routine childhood schedules to curb perinatal and early childhood transmission. This policy, building on earlier high-risk group targeting from the 1980s, significantly boosted coverage, with global three-dose infant vaccination rates rising from about 30% in 2000 to 85% by 2019. More recent advancements address chronic hepatitis B management; in 2024, WHO updated its guidelines to simplify treatment criteria, expanding eligibility for antiviral therapy among adults and adolescents while recommending prophylaxis for pregnant women to prevent mother-to-child transmission. These updates emphasize point-of-care diagnostics and decentralized service delivery to improve access in low-resource settings.
Therapeutic innovations continue to evolve, with the TherVacB vaccinea heterologous protein prime/MVA boost candidate designed to achieve functional cure in chronic hepatitis B patientsentering its phase 1b/2a clinical trial in June 2025 across sites in Europe and Africa. This trial evaluates safety, tolerability, and immunogenicity in patients with chronic infection, representing a potential breakthrough beyond preventive vaccination. Concurrently, small interfering RNA (siRNA)-based therapies, such as imdusiran, are advancing toward approval; a phase 2b trial for this HBsAg-targeting agent in HBeAg-negative patients is pending regulatory clearance, with data expected to inform combination regimens for sustained viral suppression.
In 2016, WHO established ambitious elimination targets under its Global Health Sector Strategy on Viral Hepatitis, aiming for a 90% reduction in new chronic hepatitis B infections and a 65% decrease in mortality by 2030 compared to 2015 levels. An interim milestone for 2025 includes reducing HBsAg prevalence in children under five years to less than 0.5%, a goal now achieved in approximately 75% of profiled countries through high vaccination coverage and birth-dose administration. These efforts underscore the vaccine's role in shifting hepatitis B from a high-burden disease toward potential global control.

Society and Culture

Public Awareness and Stigma

Stigma surrounding hepatitis B often originates from its association with high-risk behaviors such as unprotected sex, intravenous drug use, and perinatal transmission in endemic regions, leading to moral judgments and social ostracism. This perception has resulted in widespread discrimination, including employment barriers where individuals with chronic hepatitis B face hiring biases or job loss due to unfounded fears of transmission in workplaces. Similarly, travel and immigration restrictions in several countries, particularly affecting migrants from high-prevalence areas in Asia and Africa, exacerbate isolation and perpetuate stereotypes linking the virus to promiscuity or deviance.
Common misconceptions about hepatitis B transmission further fuel stigma, with many believing it spreads through casual contact like hugging, sharing utensils, or coughing, despite evidence showing it requires direct exposure to infected blood, semen, or other body fluids. Public health efforts emphasize that the virus is not casually transmissible and highlight manageable aspects, such as effective antiviral treatments that suppress replication and reduce liver damage, though no cure exists for chronic cases. These clarifications aim to counter fears, but persistent myths continue to hinder open discussions in communities.
Awareness campaigns have played a crucial role in addressing stigma and promoting education. World Hepatitis Day, observed annually on July 28 since its establishment by the World Health Organization in 2010, focuses on raising global visibility for viral hepatitis, including hepatitis B, through events that debunk myths and encourage testing. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) supports initiatives like Hepatitis Awareness Month in May and the Hepatitis B United coalition, which provide resources, webinars, and community outreach to foster understanding and reduce discrimination. The Hepatitis B Foundation (HBF) runs storytelling projects such as #justB, featuring personal narratives from affected individuals to humanize the experience and challenge stereotypes in multicultural settings.
The impacts of stigma are profound, particularly in endemic communities where fear of judgment leads to delayed testing and diagnosis, allowing the virus to progress undetected to severe outcomes like cirrhosis. Individuals often experience mental health burdens, including anxiety, depression, and social withdrawal, as rejection from family or peers compounds the emotional toll of living with a chronic condition. In high-prevalence regions like parts of Asia and sub-Saharan Africa, these effects create barriers to care-seeking and perpetuate cycles of silence, underscoring the need for targeted anti-stigma interventions.

Global Elimination Efforts

In 2016, the World Health Organization (WHO) adopted the Global Health Sector Strategy on viral hepatitis, setting ambitious targets for eliminating hepatitis B as a public health threat by 2030, including a 90% reduction in new chronic infections and a 65% reduction in mortality compared to 2015 levels. These goals emphasize high vaccination coverage, expanded testing, and equitable access to treatment, with progress tracked through biennial reports on service coverage.
The WHO's 2024 Global Hepatitis Report highlighted uneven advancement, with global coverage of the third dose of hepatitis B vaccine (HepB3) reaching 85% among children, though birth-dose vaccination lagged at 46%, falling short of the 90% target for both. This report underscored the need for intensified efforts in low- and middle-income countries, where only 3% of people living with chronic hepatitis B received treatment, far below the 80% goal.
National programs have played a pivotal role in advancing these targets. In China, a nationwide policy mandates a three-dose hepatitis B vaccine series for infants at birth, 1 month, and 6 months, achieving over 99% coverage among newborns since 2012 and significantly reducing chronic infection rates from 9.8% in 1992 to under 1% in recent cohorts. In Africa, WHO-supported birth-dose initiatives aim to close coverage gaps, with partnerships in countries like Zimbabwe and through the African Union's efforts introducing universal newborn vaccination to prevent mother-to-child transmission, though regional uptake remains at about 18%.
Despite these strides, challenges persist in achieving global elimination. Limited access to services in remote and rural areas hinders timely vaccination and screening, as seen in Pacific island nations where logistical barriers delay interventions. Funding gaps, with only 10% of required resources mobilized by 2024, exacerbate inequities, while shortages in diagnostics impede early detection in resource-limited settings.
By 2025, promising updates include 73% of profiled countries meeting the WHO interim target of less than 0.5% hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg) prevalence among children under five, driven by scaled vaccination programs. New tools, such as WHO-prequalified point-of-care tests for simultaneous detection of hepatitis B, HIV, and syphilis, are enhancing rapid diagnosis in decentralized settings, facilitating earlier linkage to care.
