Carbonyl sulfide
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Carbonyl sulfide (COS) is a simple, one-carbon compound consisting of a central carbon atom double-bonded to both an oxygen atom and a sulfur atom, with the molecular formula COS and a molecular weight of 60.08 g/mol. It exists as a colorless, flammable gas at standard temperature and pressure, exhibiting a distinct sulfide odor, though pure samples are odorless. With a boiling point of -50 C and a melting point of -138.8 C, it has a density of 2.456 g/L and limited solubility in water (1,220 mg/L at 25 C).
As the most abundant sulfur-containing gas in Earth's atmosphere, carbonyl sulfide maintains a global concentration of approximately 0.5 parts per billion (ppb), or 430570 parts per trillion (ppt), influencing atmospheric chemistry and serving as a proxy for carbonic anhydrase activity in ecosystems. Its primary natural sources include oceanic emissions, soil microbes, volcanoes, and biomass burning, accounting for about two-thirds of total emissions (2.73.5 Tg S/year), while anthropogenic contributions from oil refining, coal processing, and chemical industries make up the remainder. Sinks involve rapid uptake by plants, soils, and marine organisms via enzymatic hydrolysis to carbon dioxide (CO) and hydrogen sulfide (HS), as well as atmospheric oxidation by hydroxyl radicals, resulting in a lifetime of 25 years.
Industrially, carbonyl sulfide is produced via reactions such as carbon monoxide with sulfur vapor or hydrolysis of carbon disulfide, with U.S. production estimated at 120 million pounds annually as of 2019. It functions as a key intermediate in synthesizing organic sulfur compounds, including thiocarbamate herbicides and aliphatic polyureas, and has been used as a grain fumigant, though this application is limited in some regions due to toxicity concerns. In chemical biology, COS acts as a substrate for carbonic anhydrase enzymes, potentially serving as a sulfide transporter or gasotransmitter, with detection in human breath, blood, and tissues linking it to biomarkers for conditions like cystic fibrosis and liver disease.
Carbonyl sulfide poses significant health and safety hazards, being highly toxic by inhalation with an LC50 of 1,070 ppm for 4 hours in rats, causing narcotic effects, respiratory irritation, and central nervous system depression at low concentrations. It is flammable within a 1229% volume range in air and reacts with strong oxidants to release toxic gases like hydrogen sulfide upon heating. Occupational exposure limits include a threshold limit value (TLV) of 5 ppm for an 8-hour time-weighted average, and it is classified under NFPA ratings as highly toxic (health: 3), severely flammable (fire: 4), and unstable (instability: 1). Environmentally, its persistence and mobility in soil (Koc: 1) and water (half-life ~2 hours in rivers) contribute to its role in sulfur cycling, though industrial releases are regulated to minimize ecological impacts.

Properties

Physical properties

Carbonyl sulfide (COS) has the chemical formula O=C=S and exists as a linear molecule with a molecular weight of 60.075 g/mol. It appears as a colorless gas under standard conditions.
The compound has a melting point of -138.8 C and a boiling point of -50.2 C. Its density as a gas is 2.48 g/L at 25 C and 1 atm. Impure samples of carbonyl sulfide exhibit an unpleasant sulfide-like odor, detectable at concentrations as low as 0.055 ppm, while pure samples are odorless.

Carbonyl sulfide is flammable, with explosive limits ranging from 12% to 29% by volume in air and an autoignition temperature of 252 C.

Molecular structure

Carbonyl sulfide, with the chemical formula O=C=S, is a linear triatomic molecule in its ground state. The central carbon atom adopts sp hybridization, leading to a bond angle of 180 between the oxygen, carbon, and sulfur atoms. Equilibrium bond lengths have been experimentally determined as 1.160 for the C=O bond and 1.560 for the C=S bond.
The bonding in OCS features cumulative double bonds, often represented as O=C=S in the Lewis structure, with contributions from resonance forms such as OCS and O=CS that account for the polarity. This electronic arrangement results in a dipole moment of 0.715 D, oriented from the sulfur end toward the oxygen. Quantum chemical calculations reveal a HOMO-LUMO energy gap that underscores the molecule's kinetic stability toward dissociation.
Spectroscopically, OCS exhibits characteristic vibrational modes due to its linear geometry and permanent dipole. The asymmetric stretching mode (3\nu_33) produces a strong IR absorption at approximately 2068 cm1^{-1}1, while the symmetric stretching mode (1\nu_11) at around 860 cm1^{-1}1 is primarily Raman active. The bending mode (2\nu_22) appears at about 520 cm1^{-1}1. These frequencies shift in isotopic variants, such as 13^{13}13C-OCS or 34^{34}34S-OCS, enabling precise studies in atmospheric and laboratory spectroscopy.
Structurally analogous to carbon dioxide (CO2_22), OCS differs by substitution of one oxygen with sulfur, which introduces asymmetry and the observed dipole moment; in excited electronic states, this leads to slight bending distortions not seen in the symmetric CO2_22.

Occurrence

Natural sources

Carbonyl sulfide (COS) is emitted from various biogenic sources in the environment. In oceans, phytoplankton and bacterial degradation of dimethyl sulfide contribute significantly to atmospheric COS, with global emission estimates of 840 Gg S/year (range 6481,032 Gg S/year). Soils release COS through microbial activity, particularly in anoxic conditions, with emissions from such soils estimated at approximately 100 Gg S/year. Wetlands, driven by anaerobic microbial processes, also serve as sources, contributing to the overall terrestrial biogenic flux.
Geological sources include volcanic emissions and hydrothermal vents. Global volcanic contributions are relatively small at 2542 Gg S/year. Biomass burning from wildfires adds to natural emissions, accounting for about 70116 Gg S/year globally. Overall, natural sources contribute approximately 1,0001,300 Gg S/year to the global COS budget. Recent research suggests a missing source of approximately 400500 Gg S/year, possibly from underestimated oceanic or terrestrial emissions in the tropics.
Vegetation acts as a major sink for atmospheric COS, with plants absorbing it through stomata in a manner analogous to CO uptake during photosynthesis. Global plant uptake fluxes are estimated at 4001,360 Gg S/year.
Atmospheric COS occurs at trace levels, with a global average concentration of approximately 0.5 ppb; concentrations can reach up to 10 ppb in regions near active volcanoes. Ice core records indicate stable pre-industrial levels around 400 ppt as of 1850, reflecting balanced natural sources and sinks prior to significant anthropogenic influences.

Atmospheric distribution

Carbonyl sulfide (COS) exhibits a global mean concentration of approximately 480 ppt (parts per trillion) in the troposphere, with values typically ranging from 420 to 540 ppt depending on location and season. Concentrations are slightly higher in the Southern Hemisphere, often by 2050 ppt compared to the Northern Hemisphere, primarily due to enhanced oceanic emissions in southern latitudes.
The atmospheric lifetime of COS is estimated at 35 years, allowing for relatively uniform mixing throughout the troposphere. Major sinks include oxidation by hydroxyl (OH) radicals in the troposphere, which accounts for about 3040% of removal; uptake by terrestrial vegetation, serving as a proxy for gross primary production (GPP) due to shared enzymatic pathways with CO; and photolysis in the stratosphere, contributing to sulfate aerosol formation. In the stratosphere, COS concentrations can enrich to around 600 ppt in the lower regions before significant photolytic loss occurs higher up.
COS is well-mixed in the troposphere on interhemispheric scales but shows seasonal variations, with higher concentrations in winter (up to 50 ppt above annual means in the Northern Hemisphere) due to reduced vegetation uptake during dormant periods, and lower levels in summer linked to peak photosynthetic activity. These cycles mirror those of CO, enabling the use of COS/CO ratios as a tracer for monitoring global photosynthesis and the terrestrial carbon cycle.
Historical reconstructions from Antarctic firn air indicate stable pre-1950 COS levels around 410 ppt, remaining around 480500 ppt through the early 2000s and into the 2020s.
Atmospheric COS is measured primarily via gas chromatography coupled with sulfur chemiluminescence detection (GC-SCD), which provides high precision for flask and in-situ samples at ppt levels. Satellite observations, such as those from the Michelson Interferometer for Passive Atmospheric Sounding (MIPAS), offer global vertical profiles and support budget inversions.

Production

Industrial methods

Carbonyl sulfide (COS) is primarily produced industrially as a byproduct of sulfur recovery and desulfurization processes in the petrochemical sector. The Claus process, widely employed for converting hydrogen sulfide from natural gas sweetening and refinery off-gases into elemental sulfur, generates COS through side reactions such as the interaction of carbon monoxide with sulfur species or hydrogen sulfide with carbon dioxide. Although the process is optimized to minimize COS formation, residual amounts are recovered via absorption techniques to prevent atmospheric release or interference with downstream operations.
Another significant source is the production of carbon disulfide (CS2), where COS emerges as a byproduct during the high-temperature reaction of charcoal with sulfur vapor. This method contributes substantially to overall COS output, particularly in facilities manufacturing CS2 for viscose rayon and other chemicals. Additionally, COS forms during hydrodesulfurization of crude oil in refineries, appearing as an impurity in syngas and fuel gas streams from processes like coal gasification and partial oxidation. Global anthropogenic production of COS is estimated at approximately 1.3 million metric tons per year, with much of it originating from these refinery and desulfurization activities.
A dedicated synthesis route involves the direct reaction of carbon monoxide with elemental sulfur to form COS, typically catalyzed at 200400 C. While less common than byproduct routes, this method has been scaled for specific applications, often using catalysts like alkaline earth compounds to enhance selectivity.
Purification of COS from process streams, such as syngas mixtures, commonly employs distillation or cryogenic separation, leveraging differences in boiling points to achieve high purity. Production volumes have expanded since the 1970s alongside the growth of global oil refining capacity, particularly in regions with extensive hydrocarbon processing. As a low-cost byproduct, COS supply is dominated by major refineries in the Middle East and the United States, where economic viability stems from its incidental generation rather than intentional manufacturing.

Laboratory synthesis

Carbonyl sulfide was first synthesized in the laboratory by Hungarian chemist Carl von Than in 1867 through the acid hydrolysis of potassium thiocyanate with dilute sulfuric acid, marking the initial characterization of the compound as distinct from mixtures of carbon dioxide and hydrogen sulfide. This method involves the reaction:
KSCN+2HX2SOX4+HX2OKHSOX4+NHX4HSOX4+COS
\ce{KSCN + 2H2SO4 + H2O -> KHSO4 + NH4HSO4 + COS}

The resulting gas mixture requires purification to isolate COS, typically by fractional distillation or absorption of byproducts.
A classic laboratory preparation utilizes the reaction of phosgene with hydrogen sulfide or sodium sulfide, yielding COS along with hydrogen chloride. The balanced equation for the reaction with hydrogen sulfide is:
COClX2+HX2SCOS+2HCl
\ce{COCl2 + H2S -> COS + 2HCl}
COClX2+HX2SCOS+2HCl
This approach, documented in early chemical literature, allows for controlled generation of small quantities of COS under anhydrous conditions to minimize side reactions. Another traditional route involves the reaction of carbon monosulfide (CS), generated in situ from carbon disulfide pyrolysis or photolysis, with carbon monoxide, facilitating the formation of COS through sulfur-oxygen exchange.
Modern laboratory routes include laser photolysis of carbon disulfide (CS) at wavelengths such as 193 nm using an ArF excimer laser, which fragments CS into CS radicals that can subsequently react with oxidants or carbon monoxide to form COS for spectroscopic studies. Additionally, heating carbon monoxide with elemental sulfur in sealed tubes at elevated temperatures (above 500 C) in the presence of catalysts like alkaline earth metal compounds promotes the direct formation of COS via:
CO+18SX8COS
\ce{CO + 1/8 S8 -> COS}
CO+81SX8COS
Yields in this method can reach up to 70% under optimized conditions.
Due to the high toxicity of COS, comparable to hydrogen cyanide, laboratory syntheses are typically performed in situ within reaction vessels or flow systems for immediate use in experiments, with gas handling under fume hoods and monitoring to ensure concentrations remain below 10 ppm. Reported yields for these small-scale preparations generally range from 50% to 80%, depending on purification efficiency and reaction scale.

Reactions

Hydrolysis and aqueous reactions

Carbonyl sulfide (COS) undergoes hydrolysis in aqueous solutions to produce carbon dioxide (CO) and hydrogen sulfide (HS), according to the reaction:
COS+H2OCO2+H2S
\text{COS} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{CO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{S}
COS+H2OCO2+H2S
This process is slow under neutral conditions but is significantly accelerated in the presence of bases or amines. At 25 C and pH 7, the uncatalyzed hydrolysis follows pseudo-first-order kinetics with a rate constant of approximately 3.7 10^{-4} s^{-1}, corresponding to a half-life of about 31 minutes for dissolved COS. The reaction exhibits strong pH dependence, with rates increasing by orders of magnitude in alkaline environments due to catalysis by hydroxide ions (OH); for instance, in solutions with NaOH, the half-life can decrease to minutes. Amines, such as monoethanolamine (MEA), further catalyze the hydrolysis, enhancing removal rates in industrial aqueous systems.
The mechanism involves nucleophilic addition of water (or OH in basic conditions) to the electrophilic carbon of the C=S bond, forming a transient hydrogen thiocarbonate intermediate (HOCS), followed by proton transfer steps that cleave the molecule into CO and HS (which protonates to HS). This pathway is supported by kinetic studies showing first-order dependence on COS concentration and zero- to negative-order dependence on water in some catalyzed systems. The pH sensitivity arises from the role of OH in facilitating the initial nucleophilic attack, making the reaction negligible in acidic media but rapid under basic conditions.
COS exhibits considerable stability in neutral aqueous environments, with the aforementioned half-life indicating slow decomposition without catalysts; however, in natural waters like seawater (typically pH 8.1), the effective lifetime shortens to hours or days due to mild alkalinity and temperature variations, though overall persistence is longer owing to limited solubility (1.22 g/L at 25 C). In strongly alkaline solutions, such as 0.1 M NaOH, hydrolysis completes in under 10 minutes at ambient temperature.
Isotopic labeling with O and S has been employed to elucidate reaction pathways, revealing fractionation effects that confirm the nucleophilic mechanism and distinguish hydrolysis from other sinks like photolysis; for example, S enrichment in residual COS during hydrolysis supports rate-limiting C-S bond cleavage. These studies highlight oxygen incorporation from water into CO products, aiding mechanistic validation.
In environmental contexts, aqueous hydrolysis serves as a key atmospheric sink for COS, primarily through uptake and decomposition in ocean surface waters, accounting for approximately 10-20% of global removal and influencing the tropospheric lifetime of about 2-5 years. This process modulates stratospheric sulfur loading by converting long-lived COS into shorter-lived products.

Reactions with metals and nucleophiles

Carbonyl sulfide (COS) acts as a ligand in coordination chemistry with transition metals, binding through its sulfur atom in an -S mode or in an fashion involving both the carbon and sulfur atoms. For instance, reactions with iron and ruthenium complexes yield dicarbonyl(-carbonyl sulfide)bis(phosphine) complexes, where COS coordinates side-on to the metal center. Similarly, rhodium complexes such as (-CH)Rh(PPh) react with COS to form (-CH)Rh(-COS)(PPh), demonstrating the ligand's ability to engage in -backbonding akin to carbon monoxide but with sulfur-mediated interactions.
In insertion reactions, COS inserts into metal-hydride bonds of transition metal complexes, forming monothioformate or related species. A representative example involves the reaction of RhH(PPh) with COS, which proceeds via formal insertion of sulfur to yield trans-mercaptocarbonylbis(triphenylphosphine)rhodium(I), Rh(SH)(CO)(PPh). This process is also observed with iron(II)-hydride complexes, where COS inserts into the Fe-H bond at 300 K to produce -thioformyl or hydrido-thiocarbonyl intermediates. Such insertions are common in rhodium and palladium catalysis, facilitating transformations in organometallic systems.
Nucleophilic attack on COS typically occurs at the electrophilic carbon or sulfur, particularly with amines and thiols. Primary amines react with COS to form ammonium salts of monothiocarbamic acids, such as RNH S-C(O)NHR, which can further cyclize or serve as precursors to thiocarbamates. For example, benzylamine yields a stable monothiocarbamic salt upon treatment with COS. Alkanolamines exhibit similar reactivity, with kinetics showing second-order dependence on amine concentration, leading to thiocarbamate formation via addition to the C=S bond. Thiols add to COS under basic conditions to generate dithiocarbamates or thioesters, though these reactions often require catalysts to enhance selectivity.
COS undergoes reduction over heterogeneous catalysts, such as sulfided molybdenum or alumina-supported systems, where hydrogenation with H produces methanethiol (CHSH). This process involves sequential steps including COS dissociation and methyl group formation from surface intermediates, achieving high selectivity at temperatures around 615 K. In oxidative environments, such as flames, COS is oxidized to sulfur dioxide (SO) via radical pathways involving O atoms, contributing to sulfur oxide emissions in combustion processes.
In synthetic contexts, COS serves as a thio-carbonyl source in nickel-catalyzed carbonylations, enabling the formation of thioester equivalents from aryl or alkyl halides. For instance, aryl iodides react with COS in the presence of Ni(0) complexes to insert into the C-Ni bond, yielding cyclic nickel thiobenzoates that liberate thioacids upon hydrolysis. These reactions highlight COS's utility as a sulfur-transfer reagent in transition-metal-mediated thiocarbonylations.

Applications

Organic synthesis

Carbonyl sulfide (COS) serves as a versatile C1 building block in organic synthesis, particularly for incorporating sulfur-containing functional groups under milder conditions than traditional reagents like phosgene. One key application is the formation of thiocarbonates, where COS reacts with alcohols in the presence of a base to yield O-thiocarbamates, such as ROC(O)SH from ROH. This reaction proceeds at ambient temperatures and provides a direct route to these intermediates, which are useful in further transformations for pharmaceuticals and agrochemicals.
In carbonylation reactions, COS acts as a safer alternative to phosgene for generating polyurethane precursors. For instance, the reaction of COS with diamines leads to polythiocarbamates via step-growth polyaddition, often incorporating diacrylates to form degradable polymers with desirable mechanical properties. This approach avoids the toxicity associated with phosgene while enabling the synthesis of sulfur-analogous polyurethanes, as demonstrated in recent enzyme-degradable systems yielding polymers with molecular weights up to several thousand Da.
COS also facilitates isothiocyanate production through initial thiocarbamate formation followed by dehydration. Primary amines react with COS under basic catalysis to give RNH-C(O)-SH intermediates, which eliminate water to afford RN=C=S with yields exceeding 85% under optimized conditions, such as moderate heating in solvent-free setups. This method, reported since the mid-20th century and refined in later works, offers a one-carbon sulfur source superior to CO/S combinations due to its stability and reduced handling risks.
Recent advances highlight COS in photocatalyzed C-S bond formations, particularly post-2010 developments enabling thioester synthesis. For example, photochemically activated COS elimination from thioacids derived from amino acids promotes radical-mediated decarboxylation, forging C-S bonds in complex molecules under visible light without metal catalysts. These innovations leverage COS's role as a mild sulfur donor, expanding its utility in sustainable organic transformations from 1980s foundational methods to modern photoredox strategies.

Fumigation and other uses

Carbonyl sulfide (COS) has been developed as a fumigant for stored grains, serving as a potential alternative to methyl bromide, which is being phased out due to its ozone-depleting properties, and phosphine, amid concerns over resistance development in pests. Developed and patented by CSIRO Australia in 1992, COS demonstrates effective control of stored-product insects such as Sitophilus oryzae and Rhyzopertha dominica across all life stages at concentrations of 2025 g/m for 15 days under field conditions at 1025C, achieving complete mortality in trials on wheat and other commodities. Laboratory assessments indicate LC50 values around 732 ppm for 24 hours at 20C against certain insect species, highlighting its toxicity to pests at relatively low exposure levels. Compared to phosphine, COS offers superior penetration into grain bulks, achieving even distribution within one day, and faster desorption post-fumigation, with residual levels below 0.1 ppm after 24 hours of aeration, reducing residue concerns.
In agricultural applications beyond grain storage, COS shows promise for soil fumigation to control nematodes, with patent evaluations confirming its effectiveness in removing these pests from soil matrices. Studies from the 1990s and early 2000s, including those referenced in international assessments, support its potential for pre-plant soil treatments, though field-scale adoption remains limited as of 2025.
Emerging research leverages COS flux measurements as a proxy for gross primary productivity (GPP) in ecosystems, enabling multi-scale constraints on carbon cycling through atmospheric observations that mirror photosynthetic CO uptake. This approach has been applied in boreal forests, agricultural fields, and urban green spaces to partition biogenic CO signals, with models optimizing COS uptake to refine GPP estimates at regional to global scales. However, these applications remain primarily in scientific research and are not yet widely commercialized for operational use by 2025.
Regulatory oversight by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) lists COS as an intermediate in the production of thiocarbamate herbicides and pesticides, with its use restricted due to toxicity profiles that limit broad pesticidal registration. While not fully registered as a standalone fumigant in regions like California, its intermediate status supports controlled applications in agriculture.

Health and environmental effects

Toxicity and health hazards

Carbonyl sulfide (COS) is acutely toxic via inhalation, with an LC50 value of 1070 ppm for a 4-hour exposure in rats, indicating moderate lethality at elevated concentrations. High-level exposure causes central nervous system (CNS) depression and respiratory failure, primarily due to its rapid metabolism in vivo to hydrogen sulfide (H2S), a potent inhibitor of cellular respiration. In animal models, acute exposures above 1000 ppm lead to convulsions, hypoactivity, ataxia, lethargy, and death, with no morphological changes observed in lungs at lower repeated doses of 54 ppm over 7 weeks in rabbits.
Symptoms of COS exposure in humans are not well-documented due to limited direct studies, but inferred effects from its metabolic products include irritation of the respiratory tract, eyes, and skin, as well as nausea, vomiting, weakness, and muscle cramps at high concentrations. The odor threshold is approximately 0.055 ppm, though rapid olfactory adaptation can mask detection at higher levels. In animals, sublethal exposures produce hypothermia, head tilt, and tremors at concentrations as low as 30 ppm over intermediate durations (6 hours/day, 10 weeks). At around 500 ppm, rats exhibit severe motor impairment and neurotoxicity, potentially leading to unconsciousness.
Chronic exposure to COS may result in narcosis, persistent respiratory irritation, and neurotoxicity, with animal studies demonstrating decreased motor activity, grip strength, and brain lesions (e.g., necrosis in the cortex and thalamus) at 400600 ppm over 12 exposures. Potential reproductive effects include reduced pregnancy rates in rats at 182 ppm for 11 weeks. Occupational exposure guidelines include an ACGIH threshold limit value (TLV) of 5 ppm as an 8-hour time-weighted average (TWA), while OSHA and NIOSH have not established permissible exposure limits (PELs), recommended exposure limits (RELs), or immediately dangerous to life or health (IDLH) values specifically for COS.
COS is metabolized primarily through enzymatic hydrolysis catalyzed by carbonic anhydrase in the blood and tissues, yielding H2S and CO2; the H2S then inhibits cytochrome c oxidase in mitochondria, disrupting oxidative phosphorylation and causing hypoxic damage particularly in the CNS. Alternative pathways involve conversion to thiosulfate or direct oxidation to CO2 via mixed-function oxidases, with effects manifesting at concentrations as low as 200 ppm in the CNS.
In cases of exposure, first aid measures include immediately removing the individual to fresh air, providing oxygen therapy if breathing is difficult, and seeking medical attention; for liquefied COS contact, warm frostbitten areas and flush skin or eyes with water for at least 15 minutes. COS is typically stored and handled as a liquefied gas under pressure to prevent vaporization and ensure safe transport.

Environmental role and impact

Carbonyl sulfide (COS) plays a significant role in the stratosphere by serving as the primary precursor to sulfate aerosols during periods of low volcanic activity. Upon transport from the troposphere, COS undergoes photodissociation and oxidation to sulfur dioxide (SO), which further reacts to form sulfuric acid and contributes to aerosol formation. These aerosols influence climate by scattering incoming solar radiation, exerting a cooling effect with a direct radiative forcing of approximately 0.003 W m from the anthropogenic fraction. Model simulations indicate that COS accounts for about 70% of the stratospheric aerosol sulfur burden above 20 km, making it the dominant non-volcanic source of stratospheric sulfur.
In the biosphere, COS acts as a proxy for gross primary productivity (GPP) because its uptake by plants parallels CO fixation through shared diffusion pathways and enzymatic reactions involving carbonic anhydrase and RuBisCO. Vegetation dominates the global COS sink, with plant uptake fluxes used to estimate terrestrial GPP, providing an independent constraint on carbon cycle models. For instance, observed COS drawdown by ecosystems corresponds to an equivalent of roughly 1.5 PgC yr in photosynthetic carbon fixation, highlighting its utility in quantifying biosphere-atmosphere carbon exchange.
Industrial emissions of COS elevate local atmospheric concentrations, with oxidation products like SO serving as precursors to acid rain and contributing to regional air quality degradation. Globally, anthropogenic sources account for approximately 20% of the total COS flux, primarily from fossil fuel processing, aluminum production, and rayon manufacturing. The primary sinks for COS are terrestrial uptake by soils and vegetation, which together remove the majority of atmospheric COS through hydrolysis and enzymatic degradation, with oceans playing a minor role. COS has no significant ozone depletion potential (ODP 0), distinguishing it from regulated halocarbons.
Atmospheric COS levels are indirectly monitored under the Montreal Protocol framework as part of broader stratospheric sulfur and ozone assessments, though COS itself is not a controlled substance. Concentrations have remained around 0.5 ppb as of 2025, following a period of increase but with a recent slight decline observed since the mid-2010s.
