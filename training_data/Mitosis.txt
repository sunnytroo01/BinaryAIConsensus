Mitosis

1x
 Mitosis is a fundamental process of cell division in eukaryotic organisms, whereby a single parent cell divides to produce two genetically identical daughter cells, each containing the same number and type of chromosomes as the original cell. This mechanism ensures the maintenance of genetic stability and is crucial for growth, tissue repair in multicellular organisms, and asexual reproduction in both unicellular and multicellular organisms.
Mitosis occurs as part of the cell cycle's M phase, following interphase, which includes the G1 (gap 1), S (synthesis), and G2 (gap 2) phases where the cell grows, replicates its DNA, and prepares for division. In human somatic cells, for instance, the 46 chromosomes (23 pairs) are duplicated during the S phase to yield 92 chromatids, ensuring each daughter cell receives a complete set. The process is tightly regulated by checkpointssuch as the G1/S, G2/M, and metaphase/anaphase transitionsto verify DNA integrity and proper chromosome alignment, preventing errors that could lead to conditions like cancer.
The mitotic process itself unfolds in five distinct stages: prophase, prometaphase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase, culminating in cytokinesis. In prophase, chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes, the mitotic spindle begins to form from microtubules, and centrosomes migrate to opposite poles of the cell. During prometaphase, the nuclear envelope fragments, allowing spindle microtubules to attach to kinetochores on the chromosomes. In metaphase, chromosomes align at the cell's equatorial plane, known as the metaphase plate, with each kinetochore connected to microtubules from opposite poles. Anaphase follows, as sister chromatids separate and are pulled toward opposite poles by shortening microtubules. Finally, in telophase, nuclear envelopes reassemble around the segregated chromosomes, which decondense, and the spindle disassembles, paving the way for cytokinesis to divide the cytoplasm and complete the formation of two daughter cells.
Beyond its role in normal cellular function, mitosis is significant in pathology and medicine; disruptions, such as nondisjunction leading to aneuploidy, are implicated in diseases including Turner syndrome and tumorigenesis. In cancer cells, unchecked mitosis drives uncontrolled proliferation, making it a target for therapies like microtubule-stabilizing drugs (e.g., taxanes). Overall, mitosis exemplifies the precision of eukaryotic cell division, balancing fidelity in genetic transmission with adaptability for organismal needs.

Overview

Definition and Process

Mitosis is a fundamental process of cell division in eukaryotic organisms, characterized by the equitable distribution of replicated chromosomes from a single parent nucleus into two daughter nuclei, ensuring genetic continuity. This mechanism allows a diploid cell to produce two genetically identical diploid daughter cells, each containing the full complement of chromosomes, which is essential for growth, tissue repair, and asexual reproduction in multicellular organisms.
The process begins with chromosome duplication during the S phase of interphase, the preparatory stage preceding mitosis proper, where the DNA content doubles to create sister chromatids. As mitosis initiates, the nuclear envelope breaks down, enabling the formation of the mitotic spindlea microtubule-based structure that organizes and pulls the chromosomes. The chromosomes then align at the cell's equatorial plane, followed by the separation of sister chromatids to opposite poles, driven by spindle shortening and elongation. Finally, new nuclear envelopes reform around the segregated chromosomes, completing the division of the nucleus into two identical sets. Cytokinesis, the subsequent division of the cytoplasm, typically follows to yield two separate daughter cells.
Unlike binary fission in prokaryotes, which involves simple DNA replication and cytoplasmic partitioning without a nucleus or spindle apparatus, mitosis requires a complex eukaryotic machinery to manage the larger genome and ensure precise segregation. In contrast to meiosis, which involves two divisions to produce four haploid gametes with genetic variation for sexual reproduction, mitosis maintains the diploid chromosome number and genetic uniformity across daughter cells.

Biological Significance

Mitosis plays a pivotal role in the growth and development of multicellular eukaryotic organisms by enabling the production of genetically identical daughter cells that contribute to the formation of tissues and organs during embryogenesis. Through repeated mitotic divisions, a single fertilized zygote proliferates to generate the billions of cells required for organismal development, ensuring coordinated expansion and differentiation of body structures. In adult multicellular organisms, mitosis supports ongoing growth by replenishing cells lost to normal turnover or damage. Additionally, it facilitates tissue repair, as seen in wound healing where epithelial cells at the injury site undergo mitosis to regenerate the protective barrier and restore tissue integrity.
In unicellular eukaryotes, such as yeasts and protists, mitosis is the primary mechanism for asexual reproduction, allowing a single cell to divide into two genetically identical offspring that can rapidly populate favorable environments. This process, often integrated with binary fission, ensures population expansion without the need for gamete fusion. Certain multicellular organisms, including some plants like strawberries through runners and invertebrates such as hydra via budding, also rely on mitosis for asexual propagation, producing clones that maintain the parental genotype and enable efficient colonization of habitats.
A core function of mitosis across all eukaryotes is to preserve genetic continuity by accurately duplicating and segregating chromosomes, thereby producing daughter cells with the same diploid chromosome complement as the parent cell. This fidelity is crucial for maintaining genomic stability, as precise chromosome distribution during the mitotic phases prevents aneuploidyabnormal chromosome numbers that could disrupt cellular functionin normal somatic cells. In adult organisms, mitosis in stem cell populations sustains tissue homeostasis by generating replacement cells that uphold long-term genetic integrity without introducing variations.

History

Early Observations

In the mid-19th century, Robert Remak conducted pioneering microscopic observations of cell division in animal tissues, demonstrating that new cells arise through the binary fission of pre-existing cells rather than spontaneous generation. Working primarily with embryonic and pathological tissues, Remak's detailed examinations in 1852 provided the first convincing evidence against the prevailing theory of free cell formation, emphasizing division as the sole mechanism of cellular reproduction in animals.
Building on such foundational insights, Walther Flemming advanced the understanding of nuclear division in 1879 through his studies of epithelial cells from salamander larvae. Using improved staining techniques, Flemming identified thread-like structures he termed "chromatin" within the nucleus, observing their condensation into distinct threads and subsequent longitudinal splitting during division to ensure equal distribution to daughter cells. In his 1882 publication Zellsubstanz, Kern und Zelltheilung, Flemming coined the term "mitosis" (from the Greek mitos, meaning "thread") to describe this process and provided a comprehensive description of chromosomal behavior.
Concurrently in the 1870s, Eduard Strasburger extended these discoveries to plant cells, providing comprehensive descriptions of nuclear division in species such as Trillium and Lilium. In his 1876 work ber Zellbildung und Zelltheilung, Strasburger documented the formation and behavior of thread-like nuclear elements during plant cell division, affirming that new nuclei originate solely from the division of existing onesa principle integral to the evolving cell theory. His findings paralleled animal studies, underscoring the universality of this process across eukaryotes.
Early microscopic investigations of cell division faced significant technical hurdles, including optical aberrations like chromatic distortion and spherical aberration in lenses, which blurred fine nuclear details and limited resolution to observe dynamic processes in real time. Additionally, the lack of effective stains until the late 19th century made chromatin threads and splitting events difficult to visualize distinctly from other cellular phenomena, such as karyolysis or simple nuclear breakdown. These challenges delayed precise differentiation of mitotic events from artifacts or unrelated divisions until advancements in achromatic optics and aniline dyes enabled clearer imagery.

Key Developments and Scientists

In 1888, Theodor Boveri published detailed observations on mitosis in sea urchin eggs, describing the behavior of chromosomes and the division of centrosomes during cell division. Building on these early insights, his experiments in the early 1900s proposed that chromosomes are distinct entities responsible for heredity, thereby linking the mitotic process to the transmission of genetic traits across generations.
At the turn of the 20th century, Edmund Beecher Wilson advanced these ideas in his seminal 1900 textbook The Cell in Development and Inheritance, which synthesized cytology by integrating mitosis with chromosome behavior, cellular organelles, and evolutionary development. Wilson's comprehensive treatment refined Boveri's concepts, emphasizing the role of mitotic division in organismal growth and reproduction, and became a cornerstone reference for cytologists.
In the 1930s, the alkaloid colchicine emerged as a pivotal tool for mitosis research when studies revealed its ability to disrupt spindle formation and arrest cells at metaphase without halting other cellular processes. Pioneered by researchers like Albert Levan and Pierre Dustin, colchicine's application enabled precise analysis of chromosome segregation and polyploidy induction in plants and animals, transforming experimental approaches to mitotic studies.
From the 1980s onward, fluorescence microscopy revolutionized the visualization of mitotic dynamics, allowing real-time imaging of microtubule assembly and protein interactions in living cells. Techniques such as epi-fluorescence and confocal microscopy, applied to labeled tubulins and kinetochores, uncovered previously inaccessible details of spindle formation and error correction, driving modern quantitative models of mitosis.

Cell Cycle Integration

Interphase Preparation

Interphase constitutes the extended preparatory period of the cell cycle, encompassing the G1, S, and G2 phases, during which the cell grows, replicates its DNA, and assembles necessary components for the subsequent mitotic division, without any actual partitioning of the nucleus or cytoplasm. This phase typically accounts for approximately 90-95% of the total cell cycle duration in eukaryotic cells, often spanning 12-24 hours in mammalian cells proliferating under optimal conditions, though this varies widely by cell type and organism. Interphase ensures the cell achieves sufficient size, genetic fidelity, and structural readiness before entering mitosis.
The G1 phase (first gap phase) is characterized by cellular growth, including the synthesis of proteins and organelles such as ribosomes and mitochondria, which support the cell's increasing metabolic demands. At the G1/S checkpoint, the cell assesses its size, nutrient availability, and DNA integrity, halting progression if damage is detected to allow repair or trigger apoptosis if irreparable.
During the S phase (synthesis phase), DNA replication occurs semi-conservatively, duplicating the entire genome to produce identical sister chromatids joined at centromeres, ensuring each daughter cell receives a complete set. Concurrently, new histone proteins are synthesized and assembled onto the nascent DNA strands to maintain chromatin structure and epigenetic marks.
In the G2 phase (second gap phase), the cell undergoes further growth and prepares the cytoskeleton by maturing centrosomes, which recruit pericentriolar material to enhance microtubule nucleation capacity for impending spindle formation. The G2/M checkpoint verifies successful DNA replication and repairs any replication errors, preventing entry into mitosis with genomic instability. Progression from G2 to mitosis is primarily regulated by cyclin-dependent kinases, such as CDK1 activated by cyclin B, which phosphorylate targets to initiate mitotic events.

Overall Mitotic Sequence

Mitosis represents the mitotic (M) phase of the eukaryotic cell cycle, following interphase during which the cell grows and duplicates its DNA in preparation for division. The overall sequence begins after interphase and proceeds through prophase, prometaphase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase, and concludes with cytokinesis, resulting in two genetically identical daughter cells. In typical eukaryotic cells, such as those in mammals, this entire mitotic process lasts approximately 1 hour, though it can extend to 1-2 hours depending on cell type and conditions.
Key transitions mark the progression through these stages to ensure accurate chromosome segregation. The transition from prophase to prometaphase occurs with the breakdown of the nuclear envelope, allowing microtubules of the forming mitotic spindle to access and interact with the condensed chromosomes. Later, at metaphase, the spindle assembly checkpoint halts progression to anaphase until all chromosomes are properly aligned and attached to the spindle from opposite poles, preventing errors in distribution.
Standard diagrams of mitosis illustrate these changes by depicting chromosome positions: initially scattered during prophase, congressing to the metaphase plate, separating in anaphase, and clustering at poles in telophase before cytokinesis divides the cytoplasm.

Mitotic Phases

Typical high-quality labeled diagrams of mitosis in animal cells commonly illustrate the process including interphase and cytokinesis, often using the simplified PMAT mnemonic (Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase). These diagrams highlight distinctive animal cell features such as centrioles (within centrosomes) and asters (radial microtubule arrays), which are absent in plant cells where centrosomes do not contain centrioles and cytokinesis occurs via cell plate formation rather than a cleavage furrow. Such diagrams generally depict:

Interphase: Cell growth and DNA replication; the nucleus remains intact with diffuse chromatin, and centrioles are present.

Prophase: Chromatin condenses into compact chromosomes; centrioles migrate to opposite poles, organizing the mitotic spindle and asters; the nucleolus disassembles and the nuclear envelope begins to break down.

Metaphase: Chromosomes align at the equatorial metaphase plate, attached to spindle fibers via kinetochores.

Anaphase: Sister chromatids separate and move toward opposite spindle poles.

Telophase: Chromosomes decondense; nuclear envelopes reform around each set of chromosomes.

Cytokinesis: The cytoplasm divides via a cleavage furrow in animal cells.

While many educational diagrams incorporate nuclear envelope breakdown and initial spindle-chromosome attachments within prophase, this article distinguishes a separate prometaphase stage following nuclear envelope breakdown for greater precision in describing the dynamic interactions leading to chromosome congression.

Prophase

Prophase represents the initial stage of mitosis, during which the cell undergoes significant reorganization to prepare for chromosome segregation. In this phase, the duplicated chromosomes, which were synthesized during the preceding S phase of interphase, begin to condense into compact, visible structures known as chromatids. This condensation is primarily mediated by the condensin complexes, large protein assemblies that bind to DNA and facilitate the coiling and looping of chromatin fibers, reducing the overall length of chromosomes by thousands of times compared to their interphase state.80233-0) The process ensures that chromosomes can be accurately aligned and separated later in mitosis, with condensin II acting early within the intact nucleus to initiate axial shortening, while condensin I contributes more prominently after nuclear envelope breakdown.00303-9)
Concurrent with chromosome condensation, the centrosomes, which duplicated during interphase, separate and migrate toward opposite poles of the cell. This migration is driven by microtubule-based motors and establishes the bipolar axis for the mitotic spindle. As the centrosomes move apart, they organize radial arrays of microtubules known as asters, which help position the spindle and interact with the cell cortex to orient the division plane.00538-1) Throughout prophase, the nuclear envelope remains intact, confining these early mitotic events to the nucleus and maintaining spatial separation from cytoplasmic components. Additionally, the nucleolus disassembles progressively, dispersing its ribosomal components into the nucleoplasm and cytoplasm to allow access to the ribosomal DNA loci on chromosomes for segregation.
In plant cells, prophase is distinguished by the formation of the preprophase band (PPB), a transient cortical array of microtubules that encircles the nucleus and marks the future plane of cell division. The PPB, which appears in late G2 and persists into prophase, recruits motor proteins and stabilizes the cortical division site through interactions with the plasma membrane, ensuring precise cytokinesis in the absence of a centralized centrosome.00460-7) These events in prophase culminate in the cell's readiness for the transition to prometaphase, where the nuclear envelope will disintegrate to enable spindle-chromosome interactions.

Prometaphase

Prometaphase follows the chromosome condensation that occurs during prophase and is defined by the disassembly of the nuclear envelope, which fragments into vesicles and permits direct interaction between the mitotic spindle and the chromosomes. This nuclear envelope breakdown (NEBD) is triggered by phosphorylation of nuclear lamins and pore complexes by cyclin-dependent kinase 1 (CDK1) and other kinases, dispersing the barrier that previously confined chromosomes to the nucleus. As a result, the condensed chromosomes become accessible to microtubules emanating from the centrosomes, marking the onset of spindle-chromosome interactions in open mitosis, as observed in most animal cells.
The core event of prometaphase involves the capture of kinetochoresprotein structures assembled at centromeresby dynamic microtubules through a stochastic search-and-capture mechanism. Microtubules from opposite spindle poles probe the cytoplasmic space until their plus ends bind to kinetochores, often forming initial lateral attachments that transition to stable end-on connections via kinetochore-associated motors like dynein and kinesin-7 (CENP-E). Captured chromosomes then undergo congression, where they are bi-oriented and pulled toward the forming metaphase plate at the cell's equator, driven by microtubule depolymerization and motor proteins; however, this movement is erratic, with chromosomes frequently detaching and reattaching due to attachment errors. This process ensures bipolar attachment, where sister kinetochores connect to microtubules from opposite poles, a prerequisite for accurate segregation.
During congression, chromosomes display oscillatory behavior, alternating between poleward and anti-poleward movements as attachments stabilize or destabilize, reflecting the dynamic tension at kinetochores. Error correction is primarily regulated by the chromosomal passenger complex (CPC), including Aurora B kinase, which phosphorylates kinetochore proteins to destabilize improper attachments, such as syntelic (both kinetochores to the same pole) or merotelic (one kinetochore to both poles) orientations, thereby promoting the formation of correct amphitelic attachments. Spatial separation of Aurora B from properly tensioned kinetochores reduces its inhibitory activity, allowing stabilization via recruitment of stabilizing factors like the KMN network.
Prometaphase is a highly dynamic and variable phase, typically lasting 15-20 minutes in human somatic cells, during which the majority of kinetochore-microtubule attachments are established before progression to metaphase. This brevity underscores its role as a transitional stage, where failures in attachment or error correction can activate the spindle assembly checkpoint to delay anaphase onset.

Metaphase

In metaphase, chromosomes achieve precise alignment at the spindle's equatorial plate, known as the metaphase plate, through balanced tensions exerted on sister kinetochores by microtubules from opposite spindle poles. This bi-orientation stabilizes end-on attachments, where tension separates Aurora B kinase from its outer kinetochore substrates, thereby reducing phosphorylation and promoting attachment stability. The oscillatory movements of chromosomes during this phase, with speeds around 1.46 m/min, arise from microtubule depolymerization at the leading kinetochore and polymerization at the trailing one, ensuring congression to the plate.
The bipolar spindle is fully formed by this stage, comprising kinetochore fibersbundles of 2030 microtubules per kinetochore that attach end-on to kinetochoresand astral microtubules that extend from centrosomes to the cell cortex, aiding in spindle positioning and dynamics. These components, nucleated from centrosomes and captured laterally before transitioning to end-on attachments via motors like cytoplasmic dynein and CENP-E, generate the pulling forces necessary for alignment. Building on the initial chaotic kinetochore captures from prometaphase, this configuration ensures all chromosomes are properly oriented before progression.
The spindle assembly checkpoint (SAC) enforces this alignment by halting anaphase onset until every kinetochore is properly attached, preventing chromosome missegregation. Unattached kinetochores recruit Mad2, which, in a Mad1-bound complex, captures Cdc20 to form part of the mitotic checkpoint complex (MCC), inhibiting the anaphase-promoting complex/cyclosome (APC/C). BubR1 synergizes with Mad2 by binding Cdc20 with higher affinity via its KEN boxes, further blocking APC/C activation and securin degradation, thus delaying sister chromatid separation. Once all kinetochores achieve bipolar attachment and tension, SAC signaling silences, disassembling the MCC and permitting mitotic progression.
Experimental induction of metaphase arrest, such as with low concentrations of nocodazolea microtubule-depolymerizing agentdemonstrates the SAC's role by disrupting kinetochore-microtubule attachments without fully eliminating spindle microtubules, leading to prolonged alignment attempts and checkpoint activation. In such assays, cells remain arrested at metaphase with aberrant chromosome configurations until drug removal allows recovery, underscoring the checkpoint's sensitivity to attachment errors.

Anaphase

Anaphase is initiated upon satisfaction of the spindle assembly checkpoint, which ensures proper bipolar attachment of chromosomes to the mitotic spindle. This phase marks the abrupt separation of sister chromatids, allowing their migration to opposite spindle poles and ensuring equitable distribution of genetic material to daughter cells. The process is highly coordinated and rapid, typically lasting only 2-3 minutes in mammalian cells, making it the shortest visible stage of mitosis.
The onset of anaphase hinges on the cleavage of cohesin complexes that hold sister chromatids together. The anaphase-promoting complex/cyclosome (APC/C), activated by its co-activator Cdc20, ubiquitinates securin, targeting it for proteasomal degradation. This degradation liberates separase, a cysteine protease, which then cleaves the kleisin subunit (such as Scc1/Rad21) of the cohesin ring, dissolving arm and centromeric cohesion. Without this precise cleavage, chromatids remain linked, preventing segregation.
Anaphase proceeds in two overlapping substages: Anaphase A and Anaphase B. During Anaphase A, chromosomes move toward the spindle poles through shortening of kinetochore-to-pole microtubules (k-fibers), driven primarily by microtubule depolymerization at the kinetochore and pole ends, coupled with poleward flux. This kinetochore-mediated shortening generates the force for chromatid poleward motion, with speeds reaching up to 3-5 m/min in vertebrate cells. In Anaphase B, the spindle poles separate further, elongating the spindle via two mechanisms: astral microtubules exert pulling forces on the poles through interactions with the cell cortex, often mediated by dynein motors; and the central spindle pushes the poles apart by sliding antiparallel interpolar microtubules, powered by kinesin-5 and kinesin-6 family motors. These combined actions ensure complete chromatid segregation before nuclear reformation.

Telophase

Telophase follows anaphase, during which sister chromatids have separated and migrated to opposite poles of the cell.
As telophase begins, the chromatids arrive at the spindle poles and start to decondense, transitioning from compact chromosomes back into extended chromatin threads, which allows for the resumption of normal nuclear functions. This decondensation is facilitated by the decline in cyclin-dependent kinase 1 (CDK1) activity, enabling phosphatases to reverse mitotic phosphorylations and promote chromatin relaxation.
Simultaneously, the nuclear envelope reassembles around each set of decondensing chromosomes at the poles, reforming two distinct daughter nuclei and reestablishing compartmentalization between the nucleoplasm and cytoplasm. This process involves the re-association of inner nuclear membrane proteins and lamins with the chromatin, followed by membrane fusion to enclose the nuclear contents. Nucleoli also re-form within each nucleus, marking the restoration of ribosomal RNA synthesis sites.
The mitotic spindle disassembles during telophase as microtubules depolymerize, with interpolar microtubules persisting briefly before breaking down completely. The centrosomes at the spindle poles transition into interphase microtubule-organizing centers, initiating the formation of the cytoplasmic microtubule network required for the subsequent cell cycle phase.
Telophase concludes the nuclear division of mitosis, leading to the transition into G1 phase of interphase, where cells can resume growth and prepare for DNA replication, often with telophase events overlapping temporally with the onset of cytokinesis.
Online educational crossword puzzles (crucigramas) on the phases of mitosis are available for biology reinforcement workshops (talleres de refuerzo). They typically include terms like profase (chromosome condensation, nuclear envelope breakdown), metafase (chromosomes align at equatorial plate), anafase (sister chromatids separate to poles), telofase (nuclear envelopes reform, chromosomes decondense), and related concepts such as citocinesis, ciclo celular, and cromosomas.

Cytokinesis

Animal Cells

In animal cells, as typically illustrated in high-quality labeled diagrams of mitosis stages (including cytokinesis), cytokinesis completes the division process by partitioning the cytoplasm through the formation of a cleavage furrow at the cell equator. This furrow arises from the assembly and contraction of an actomyosin contractile ring, positioned and guided by the central spindlea bundle of overlapping antiparallel microtubules that forms during anaphase. The central spindle recruits signaling molecules to the overlying cortex, ensuring precise equatorial placement of the ring and initiating cortical flows that concentrate actin and myosin components.
The contractile ring's assembly and ingression are primarily orchestrated by RhoA GTPase signaling. Activated RhoA, localized to the equatorial cortex via the guanine nucleotide exchange factor ECT2 (recruited by centralspindlin on the central spindle), engages downstream effectors including formins (e.g., mDia1) for actin filament nucleation and polymerization, as well as Rho-associated kinase (ROCK) to phosphorylate and activate myosin II for force generation. This coordinated actomyosin contraction drives furrow ingression, narrowing the cell diameter progressively until the daughter cells are nearly separated, with the process beginning in late anaphase and overlapping telophase following nuclear envelope reformation.
As ingression nears completion, the contractile ring disassembles, giving way to midbody formation within the intercellular bridgea transient structure of densely packed microtubules stabilized by proteins such as PRC1 and MKLP1. Abscission, the final membrane scission event that cleanly separates the daughter cells, is executed by the endosomal sorting complex required for transport (ESCRT-III), which assembles at the midbody to constrict and sever the bridge after microtubule depolymerization and actin clearance. In mammalian cells, such as HeLa, the full cytokinesis sequence from furrow initiation to abscission typically spans 1020 minutes, maintained consistently across cell sizes due to structural memory in the contractile ring.

Plant Cells

In plant cells, cytokinesis occurs through the formation of a cell plate, a transient structure that develops into the new cell wall separating the daughter cells. This process integrates with the end of telophase, where the phragmoplast begins to assemble between the reforming nuclei. Unlike the contractile mechanisms in animal cells, plant cytokinesis relies on the expansive growth of the cell plate outward from the cell's equator to accommodate the rigid cell wall.
The cell plate forms from Golgi-derived vesicles that accumulate and fuse at the division site, depositing polysaccharides and membrane components to build the nascent wall. These vesicles are transported along microtubules to the equatorial plane, where they undergo homotypic fusion to create a flattened, fenestrated structure that expands centrifugally. This vesicle fusion is essential for establishing the biochemical composition of the new wall, including callose and cellulose precursors.81060-0)
Central to this process is the phragmoplast, a plant-specific array of antiparallel microtubules oriented perpendicular to the division plane. The phragmoplast directs the delivery and fusion of vesicles by providing tracks for motor proteins, such as kinesins, which move cargo toward the leading edge of the expanding cell plate. Microtubule polymerization at the phragmoplast's periphery sustains its expansion, ensuring the cell plate reaches the parental walls. Actin filaments within the phragmoplast also contribute to vesicle positioning, though microtubules predominate in guiding the overall assembly.
The position of the division plane in plant cells is predetermined during preprophase by the preprophase band (PPB), a cortical ring of microtubules that encircles the nucleus and marks the future site of cell plate insertion. The PPB recruits proteins and modifies the cortical cytoskeleton, establishing a division zone that persists as a phragmosome after the band disassembles, thereby guiding phragmoplast orientation during cytokinesis. This spatial control ensures orderly tissue development in walled cells.
Post-division, the cell plate matures into a functional cell wall through the deposition of additional matrix materials and the activity of enzymes that replace temporary components like callose with cellulose. This expansive mechanism allows plant cells to divide without the flexibility afforded to animal cells, supporting sustained growth in multicellular structures.

Function

Growth and Tissue Maintenance

Mitosis plays a crucial role in embryonic development through the process of cleavage, where rapid successive divisions of the zygote produce a multicellular blastula without an increase in overall embryo size. These mitotic divisions partition the zygote's cytoplasm into smaller blastomeres, establishing the foundational cell layers for further differentiation while preserving genetic uniformity across daughter cells. In vertebrates, cleavage typically involves holoblastic divisions that are exceptionally fast, occurring every 10-30 minutes in early stages, driven by shortened cell cycles lacking full G1 and G2 phases.
In adult multicellular organisms, mitosis ensures ongoing tissue renewal by replacing senescent or damaged cells, particularly in high-turnover epithelia. For instance, the human skin epidermis undergoes continuous regeneration, with basal layer keratinocytes dividing mitotically to replenish the stratified layers, resulting in a complete turnover approximately every 28 days. This basal mitosis maintains barrier function against environmental stressors, balancing proliferation with differentiation to sustain epidermal thickness and integrity throughout life.
Mitosis also facilitates tissue repair following injury, notably through the proliferation of fibroblasts in the dermal layer during wound healing. In the proliferative phase of wound repair, fibroblasts undergo mitotic divisions to populate the granulation tissue, synthesizing extracellular matrix components like collagen that restore structural support. This controlled expansion of fibroblasts via mitosis is essential for closing wounds and preventing excessive scarring, with proliferation rates peaking 3-7 days post-injury under the influence of growth factors such as platelet-derived growth factor.
Stem cell niches within tissues regulate mitotic rates to achieve homeostasis, ensuring a precise balance between cell production and loss. These specialized microenvironments, such as the bulge region in hair follicles or the crypts in intestinal epithelium, provide signaling cues like Wnt and Notch that modulate stem cell division, directing symmetric divisions for expansion or asymmetric ones for differentiation. By fine-tuning mitotic frequency, niches prevent overproliferation that could lead to hyperplasia while supporting steady-state maintenance across diverse organs.

Asexual Reproduction

Mitosis plays a central role in asexual reproduction by enabling the production of genetically identical offspring without the involvement of gametes, facilitating propagation in both unicellular and multicellular organisms. In unicellular eukaryotes, this process typically occurs through binary fission or budding, where mitotic division ensures the equitable distribution of replicated chromosomes to daughter cells, resulting in two complete individuals from one parent. This mechanism supports exponential population growth under favorable conditions, as each division directly contributes to increasing the number of organisms.
In organisms like the amoeba, asexual reproduction proceeds via binary fission, during which the nucleus undergoes mitosis to produce two identical nuclei, followed by cytoplasmic division to yield two daughter cells. Similarly, yeast cells reproduce asexually through budding, where a small outgrowth forms on the parent cell, and mitotic division within the parent nucleus allocates genetic material to the bud, which then separates as a new cell. In ciliates such as Paramecium, binary fission involves mitotic division of the micronucleus to maintain genetic fidelity, while the macronucleus divides amitotically; this process divides one cell transversely into two genetically identical offspring when nutrients are abundant.
Among multicellular organisms, starfish exemplify asexual reproduction through fragmentation, where a severed arm containing part of the central disc regenerates an entire new individual via repeated mitotic divisions in regenerative cells. In plants, vegetative propagation occurs through structures like runners or bulbs, driven by mitotic activity in meristematic tissues that produce genetically identical clones, such as new shoots and roots from stem cuttings./30:_Plant_Form_and_Physiology/30.11:Plant_Development-_Meristems) These processes maintain the diploid chromosome number characteristic of somatic mitosis, preserving the parental genome across generations.
The primary advantages of mitosis-based asexual reproduction include its rapidity, as it bypasses the need for meiosis and gamete fusion, allowing organisms to quickly colonize environments or recover from damage without energy-intensive pairing. This efficiency is particularly evident in unicellular contexts, where a single cell can produce millions of descendants in hours under optimal conditions, and in multicellular cases like plant propagation, where clones ensure uniform traits for agricultural or ecological success.

Variations

Alternative Forms Across Organisms

Mitosis exhibits significant variations across eukaryotic organisms, adapted to their cellular architectures and life cycles. In most animal cells, mitosis is described as open, characterized by the breakdown of the nuclear envelope during prophase, allowing the spindle apparatus to form in a common cytoplasmic space and interact directly with chromosomes. This disassembly facilitates chromosome segregation but requires subsequent reformation of the envelope in telophase. In contrast, closed mitosis, prevalent in yeasts and other fungi, maintains an intact nuclear envelope throughout the process, with the spindle assembling within the nucleus to segregate chromosomes without envelope disruption. This form confines mitotic events to the nuclear compartment, preventing mixing of nuclear and cytoplasmic contents.
Dinoflagellates display a highly specialized form of closed mitosis, often termed dinomitosis, where the nuclear envelope remains intact and chromosomes stay permanently condensed and attached to its inner surface. Lacking conventional centrioles, these organisms organize a bipolar spindle using acentriolar microtubule-organizing centers (MTOCs) located in the cytoplasm; the spindle microtubules traverse the nuclear envelope via dedicated channels or tunnels without entering the nucleoplasm. Chromosome attachment occurs through membrane-embedded kinetochores, enabling end-on connections to the external spindle, which pulls sister chromatids apart while the envelope maintains nuclear integrity. This acentriolar, extranuclear spindle arrangement supports efficient segregation in their unique dinokaryotic nuclei.
Filamentous fungi, such as those in Ascomycota and Basidiomycota, adapt mitosis within elongated hyphae through septation, forming cross-walls that compartmentalize the multinucleate structure post-division. After synchronous or asynchronous nuclear mitoses in the hyphal tip, septa assemble via a centrifugal process involving chitin deposition and protein complexes like septins, creating porous barriers that permit cytoplasmic continuity while defining cellular units. This septation wave, often delayed relative to mitosis, supports hyphal extension and damage response, with pores allowing nuclear migration and resource sharing across compartments. In contrast to complete cell separation in yeasts, this partial division maintains the coenocytic nature essential for fungal growth and invasion.

Errors and Aberrations

Mitotic errors, particularly those occurring during anaphase, can lead to chromosome missegregation and genetic instability. One common aberration is aneuploidy, which arises from lagging chromosomes that fail to properly congress to the metaphase plate or from nondisjunction events where sister chromatids do not separate correctly. Lagging chromosomes, often due to merotelic kinetochore-microtubule attachments, can result in chromosomes being left behind during anaphase, leading to their exclusion from daughter nuclei and formation of micronuclei. Nondisjunction, conversely, produces daughter cells with unequal chromosome numbers, such as one gaining an extra chromosome while the other loses it. These errors are prevalent in early embryonic divisions and somatic cells under stress, contributing to mosaic aneuploidy.
In cancer cells, multipolar spindles represent another critical aberration, often stemming from centrosome amplification. Extra centrosomes can organize into more than two spindle poles, disrupting bipolar attachment and causing severe chromosome missegregation. This leads to unequal distribution of chromosomes, with cells inheriting random subsets that promote aneuploidy and genomic heterogeneity. Such multipolar divisions are exacerbated by factors like replication stress or centriole over-elongation, which are common in tumors and drive ongoing chromosomal instability (CIN). Unlike transient multipolar intermediates in normal cells that resolve into bipolar spindles, persistent multipolarity in cancer amplifies missegregation rates.
Failures in the spindle assembly checkpoint (SAC) further compound these issues by allowing cells to proceed through mitosis despite attachment errors. SAC defects, such as weakened signaling from components like Mad2, permit premature anaphase onset, resulting in high rates of chromosome missegregation and CIN. This chromosomal instability manifests as ongoing aneuploidy and structural rearrangements, a hallmark of many cancers. In contrast to intact checkpoints that halt progression until proper alignment, SAC impairments accelerate error propagation.
The consequences of these aberrations are profound, linking to both developmental disorders and oncogenesis. Aneuploidy from nondisjunction, for instance, causes trisomy 21 (Down syndrome), where an extra chromosome 21 disrupts embryonic development, leading to intellectual disability and congenital heart defects. In cancer, CIN-driven aneuploidy fosters tumor progression by enabling rapid adaptation through gene dosage imbalances, immune evasion, and metastasis. While some aneuploid states impose proteotoxic stress that limits proliferation, cancer cells often tolerate or exploit them for evolutionary advantage. Overall, these errors underscore mitosis's vulnerability to instability, with cascading effects on organismal health.

Regulation and Molecular Mechanisms

Checkpoints and Control

Mitosis is tightly regulated by multiple checkpoints that ensure the fidelity of chromosome segregation and prevent propagation of errors. These checkpoints monitor key cellular processes, halting progression if abnormalities are detected, thereby maintaining genomic integrity. The primary checkpoints occur at the G2/M transition, during metaphase via the spindle assembly checkpoint (SAC), and a less stringent mechanism during late cytokinesis involving abscission delay. Underlying these are oscillatory dynamics driven by cyclin-dependent kinase 1 (CDK1) and its regulatory partner cyclin B, which orchestrate entry and exit from mitosis.
The G2/M checkpoint assesses the completion of DNA replication and the presence of DNA damage before allowing entry into mitosis. This surveillance is primarily mediated by the ATM (ataxia-telangiectasia mutated) and ATR (ATM- and Rad3-related) kinases, which detect double-strand breaks and replication stress, respectively. Upon activation, ATM and ATR phosphorylate downstream targets such as Chk1 and Chk2 kinases, leading to inhibition of CDC25 phosphatases and maintenance of inhibitory phosphorylation on CDK1. This prevents premature CDK1 activation, arresting the cell in G2 phase to facilitate DNA repair.
At the metaphase stage, the spindle assembly checkpoint (SAC) ensures proper bi-orientation of chromosomes on the mitotic spindle before anaphase onset. The SAC monitors kinetochore-microtubule attachments and tension; unattached kinetochores generate a "wait-anaphase" signal through recruitment of Mad1, Mad2, Bub1, BubR1, and Bub3 proteins. This signal forms the mitotic checkpoint complex (MCC), which binds and inhibits the anaphase-promoting complex/cyclosome (APC/C), preventing ubiquitination and degradation of securin and cyclin B. Once all chromosomes achieve stable bi-orientation, the SAC is silenced, allowing APC/C activation and progression to anaphase.
Unlike earlier checkpoints, there is no strict telophase checkpoint, but a regulatory mechanism delays abscissionthe final severing of the intercellular bridge during cytokinesisif chromatin bridges or entangled chromosomes are present. This abscission checkpoint, often termed the "NoCut" pathway, is triggered by Aurora B kinase at the midbody, which phosphorylates ESCRT-III components to inhibit membrane scission. The delay provides time for resolution of entanglements, reducing the risk of chromosome breakage or cytokinesis failure.
Central to these checkpoints are cell cycle oscillators governed by CDK1-cyclin B dynamics. Entry into mitosis requires activation of the CDK1-cyclin B complex (mitosis-promoting factor, MPF) through dephosphorylation of inhibitory sites on CDK1, triggered after G2/M checkpoint satisfaction. This activation drives prophase events like nuclear envelope breakdown. For mitotic exit, ubiquitin-mediated degradation of cyclin B by APC/C inactivates CDK1, promoting dephosphorylation of substrates and cytokinesis. These oscillations ensure timed progression through mitosis phases.

Key Proteins and Pathways

Mitosis relies on a suite of specialized proteins and pathways to orchestrate chromosome segregation and spindle dynamics. Among the microtubule-based motors, kinesins and dyneins play pivotal roles in generating forces for spindle assembly and chromosome movement. Kinesin-5 family members, such as Eg5, form homotetrameric structures that cross-link and slide antiparallel microtubules apart, thereby promoting centrosome separation and establishing bipolar spindle bipolarity during prometaphase. This outward force generation by Eg5 is essential for spindle elongation and maintenance, with its inhibition leading to monopolar spindles and mitotic arrest. In contrast, cytoplasmic dynein, a minus-end-directed motor, contributes to poleward chromosome movement by pulling kinetochores toward spindle poles, particularly during prometaphase when initial attachments form. Dynein achieves this through its localization at kinetochores via adaptor proteins like dynactin, facilitating the initial congression of chromosomes to the metaphase plate.
Chromosome architecture during mitosis is maintained by multi-subunit complexes that compact and tether DNA. The condensin complex, a conserved pentameric ATPase comprising two structural maintenance of chromosomes (SMC) subunits (SMC2 and SMC4) and three non-SMC elements, drives mitotic chromosome condensation by looping and compacting chromatin fibers. In vertebrates, two isoforms exist: condensin I, which acts post-nuclear envelope breakdown to resolve sister chromatid axes, and condensin II, which initiates axial shortening in prophase within the intact nucleus. Complementing condensin, the cohesin complexa ring-shaped structure formed by SMC1, SMC3, SCC1 (RAD21), and SCC3 (SA1 or SA2)holds sister chromatids together from S phase through metaphase, ensuring bipolar attachment and proper segregation. Cohesin's entrapment of sister DNAs prevents premature separation, with its core ring topology stabilized by kleisin and hook proteins until anaphase.
Progression through mitosis is tightly regulated by ubiquitin-mediated degradation pathways, prominently the anaphase-promoting complex/cyclosome (APC/C). This E3 ubiquitin ligase, activated sequentially by co-activators CDC20 and CDH1, targets securin and cyclin B for proteasomal degradation, thereby unleashing separase to cleave cohesin and initiating anaphase. The APC/C pathway ensures timely inactivation of the cyclin B-CDK1 complex, which drives mitotic entry, while securin degradation removes inhibition of separase, coordinating chromatid disjunction with spindle elongation. Error correction at kinetochores involves Aurora kinases, particularly Aurora B, which phosphorylates kinetochore proteins like NDC80 to destabilize improper microtubule attachments, promoting reorientation toward amphitelic biorientation. Aurora B's activity, concentrated in the chromosomal passenger complex, senses tension across kinetochores and selectively releases syntelic or merotelic errors, with its inhibition causing aneuploidy.

Related Processes

Comparison to Meiosis

Mitosis and meiosis are both forms of eukaryotic cell division that ensure accurate chromosome segregation, but they serve distinct biological purposes. Mitosis primarily facilitates growth, tissue maintenance, and asexual reproduction by producing two genetically identical diploid daughter cells from a single diploid parent cell. In contrast, meiosis generates gametes for sexual reproduction, yielding four genetically diverse haploid daughter cells from one diploid parent, thereby halving the chromosome number to maintain ploidy across generations.
The outcomes of these processes reflect their roles: mitosis results in two cells each with the same 2n chromosome complement and identical DNA content as the parent, preserving genetic stability. Meiosis, however, produces four n cells with recombined chromosomes, promoting genetic variation through independent assortment and crossing over. This reductional division in meiosis I, followed by an equational division in meiosis II without intervening DNA replication, contrasts with mitosis's single equational division.
Mechanistically, both processes share core elements, including the mitotic spindle apparatus composed of microtubules for chromosome alignment and segregation, as well as checkpoints like the spindle-assembly checkpoint to prevent errors. However, meiosis introduces unique features absent in mitosis, such as synapsis during prophase I, where homologous chromosomes pair via the synaptonemal complex, enabling crossing over and chiasma formation for genetic recombination. Mitosis lacks this homologous pairing, with sister chromatids separating without recombination.
Evolutionarily, meiosis is thought to have arisen from modifications to an ancestral mitotic pathway, incorporating mechanisms like specialized cohesins and monopolar kinetochore attachments to achieve reductional segregation and diversity. This adaptation likely enhanced genetic variability in early eukaryotes, distinguishing sexual from asexual reproduction.

Mitotic Recombination and Cell Rounding

Mitotic recombination is a rare process in which homologous chromosomes exchange genetic material during the mitotic cell cycle, primarily serving as a mechanism for repairing DNA double-strand breaks (DSBs) in somatic cells. This event typically initiates in the G2 phase, after DNA replication has produced sister chromatids that can act as templates for accurate repair via homologous recombination (HR). The process begins with the resection of DSB ends to generate single-stranded DNA (ssDNA), which is coated by RPA and then invaded by Rad51 filaments to form a displacement loop (D-loop) with the homologous sequence. Resolution of joint molecules, such as Holliday junctions, often favors non-crossover outcomes to preserve genome stability, though crossovers can occur and lead to loss of heterozygosity (LOH), where one allele is replaced by the other across a chromosomal segment. In yeast, such as Saccharomyces cerevisiae, mitotic recombination is frequently studied using DSB-inducing assays, revealing its role in gene conversion and LOH detection for mapping mutations, with Rad52 foci assembling at repair sites containing hundreds to thousands of molecules. In mammalian cells, BRCA2 facilitates Rad51 loading onto ssDNA, and ionizing radiation-induced DSBs (typically 50100 per cell) highlight HR's contribution to suppressing LOH-associated tumorigenesis. Overall, mitotic recombination maintains genomic integrity by enabling precise DSB repair, though excessive LOH can promote cancer in heterozygous contexts.
Cell rounding represents another specialized morphological adaptation during mitosis, where adherent animal cells transition from a flattened interphase shape to a spherical form, primarily in prometaphase, to facilitate proper spindle assembly and chromosome segregation. This shape change involves rapid reorganization of the actin cytoskeleton, increasing cortical tension through enhanced actin filament polymerization and crosslinking, driven by formins like Diaphanous-1 (Dia1) and the Arp2/3 complex. Central to this is the activation of RhoA GTPase by the guanine nucleotide exchange factor Ect2, triggered by cyclin-dependent kinase 1 (Cdk1), which recruits myosin II to the cortex and elevates intracellular hydrostatic pressure, enabling cells to round against substrate confinement. Protein kinase C (PKC) isoforms, such as PKC, contribute by modulating cortical contractility and responding to mechanical cues, ensuring timely progression through mitosis. In mammalian epithelial tissues, like MDCK or MCF10A cells, rounding maintains tissue architecture by orienting the mitotic spindle parallel to the plane of the epithelium, preventing misalignment that could disrupt monolayer integrity during division. For instance, in intestinal epithelia, coordinated rounding supports villus morphogenesis by coupling cell division to tissue folding. Thus, mitotic cell rounding not only provides spatial constraints for spindle positioning but also preserves multicellular organization.

Evolution

Origins in Eukaryotes

The evolutionary origins of mitosis are closely tied to the emergence of eukaryotic cells through endosymbiosis, a process hypothesized to have occurred approximately 2 billion years ago when an archaeal host engulfed an alphaproteobacterium that became the mitochondrion. This symbiotic integration necessitated coordinated division mechanisms, as mitochondrial fission had to synchronize with host cell division to maintain organelle distribution across generations, laying the groundwork for the more complex mitotic processes that evolved alongside the nucleus. The transition from prokaryotic binary fission to mitosis likely addressed challenges in segregating larger, linear chromosomes within a membrane-bound nucleus, driven by selective pressures from increased cellular complexity and phagotrophy.
In the last eukaryotic common ancestor (LECA), mitosis is reconstructed as closed orthomitosis, characterized by an intact nuclear envelope throughout division and the formation of an intranuclear spindle for chromosome segregation. Recent studies in opisthokonts, close relatives of animals, suggest that open mitosisfeaturing nuclear envelope breakdownmay have been ancestral in the Holozoa (animal lineage) and potentially in LECA, highlighting ongoing debate in the field. This ancestral state, inferred from comparative genomics and ultrastructural analyses across eukaryotic lineages, involved acentriolar microtubule-organizing centers and RanGTP gradients to assemble the spindle within the nucleus, ensuring precise partitioning without nuclear envelope breakdown. Phylogenetic reconstructions indicate that this closed form predated later variations like open mitosis in animals and plants, reflecting an early adaptation to compartmentalized genomes in a potentially syncytial LECA.
Evidence for this primitive mitotic machinery persists in extant protists, particularly apicomplexans, which exhibit closed mitosis with intranuclear spindles organized from centriolar plaques embedded in the nuclear envelope. In species like Toxoplasma gondii, the spindle forms through polar openings in the envelope, facilitating chromosome alignment and segregation while maintaining nuclear integrity, a configuration considered a relic of the ancestral eukaryotic state rather than a derived trait. These features highlight how early mitotic innovations, such as envelope-associated microtubule nucleation, enabled reliable genome distribution in the absence of advanced centrosomes.
Prior to mitosis, prokaryotic ancestors, including archaea, relied on simpler chromosome segregation mechanisms during binary fission, such as partition systems (parA/parB) or SegA/SegB-mediated DNA translocation without spindles or nuclear envelopes. These parS site-binding proteins and walking mechanisms facilitated basic genome partitioning in circular chromosomes, providing a foundational scaffold from which eukaryotic mitosis elaborated through tubulin diversification and cytoskeletal coevolution. This pre-mitotic fission underscores the profound innovations in eukaryotes, where endosymbiosis and nuclear evolution transformed rudimentary segregation into a highly orchestrated process.

Conservation and Adaptations

Mitosis exhibits remarkable conservation across eukaryotic lineages, with core mechanisms essential for accurate chromosome segregation preserved since the last eukaryotic common ancestor (LECA). The mitotic spindle, composed of microtubules assembled from - and -tubulin proteins, is a universal feature that attaches to chromosomes and facilitates their separation during anaphase; genomic analyses indicate that these tubulin genes were present in LECA, enabling the formation of a functional spindle apparatus in all eukaryotes. Similarly, kinetochoresmultiprotein complexes that assemble on centromeric DNA to mediate microtubule attachmentsare structurally and functionally conserved, as evidenced by shared components like the KMN network observed in diverse taxa from algae to mammals. The anaphase-promoting complex/cyclosome (APC/C), an E3 ubiquitin ligase pathway, is another highly conserved element that drives the degradation of securin and cyclins to trigger anaphase; this multi-subunit machinery, including core subunits like Apc1 and Cdc16, operates via co-activators Cdc20 and Cdh1 in virtually all eukaryotes examined.
Despite this conservation, mitosis displays lineage-specific adaptations tailored to cellular architecture and environmental constraints. In fungi, which possess rigid cell walls, closed mitosis predominates, wherein the nuclear envelope remains intact throughout the process to prevent mechanical disruption during spindle elongation; this mode, characterized in species like Saccharomyces cerevisiae and Schizosaccharomyces pombe, involves intranuclear microtubule organization and partial envelope remodeling without breakdown. In contrast, many insects, particularly those with holocentric chromosomes such as in the order Hemiptera, feature diffuse kinetochores distributed along the entire chromosome length rather than localized at a single centromere; this adaptation allows flexible attachment points to the spindle, accommodating chromosome rearrangements and enhancing segregation fidelity in species with extensive chromosomal variation.
The evolutionary persistence of these conserved mitotic elements has significant implications for therapeutic interventions. For instance, taxanes like paclitaxel stabilize microtubules in the spindle, disrupting dynamics and inducing mitotic arrest; their efficacy in treating diverse cancers stems from targeting the universally conserved tubulin polymerization process, which is nearly identical across eukaryotic species. This conservation underscores the spindle's role as a robust, ancient machinery, with adaptations like closed mitosis or diffuse kinetochores representing evolutionary fine-tuning rather than wholesale reinvention.
