 De Morgan's laws are a pair of equivalence rules in propositional logic and set theory that relate the negation of compound statements or operations to their dual forms. Specifically, in logic, the negation of the conjunction of two propositions P and Q is equivalent to the disjunction of their individual negations, written as (P Q) P Q, while the negation of their disjunction is equivalent to the conjunction of the negations, (P Q) P Q. In set theory, the laws state that the complement of the intersection of two sets A and B equals the union of their complements, (A B)c = Ac Bc, and the complement of their union equals the intersection of the complements, (A B)c = Ac Bc.
Named after Augustus De Morgan (18061871), a prominent British mathematician and logician, these laws were formally introduced in his seminal 1847 work Formal Logic; or, the Calculus of Inference, Necessary and Probable, where he developed them as part of an effort to quantify and systematize deductive reasoning. Although equivalent ideas appeared in earlier philosophical texts by figures such as Aristotle and William of Ockham, De Morgan's version provided a rigorous algebraic foundation, influencing the development of symbolic logic.
The laws hold profound significance across mathematics and related fields, serving as cornerstones of Boolean algebra by enabling the transformation of logical expressionssuch as converting AND gates to OR gates under negationwhich is vital for simplifying circuits in digital electronics and computer design. They also illustrate duality in lattice theory and probability, where negations mirror unions and intersections, and extend to more advanced structures like modal logic and database query optimization. Their proofs, often via truth tables in logic or Venn diagrams in sets, underscore their tautological nature, ensuring universal applicability in formal systems.

Overview

General statement

De Morgan's laws are two key principles in logic that explain how negation distributes over the operations of conjunction (logical "and") and disjunction (logical "or"). These laws assert that the negation of a conjunction of two propositions is logically equivalent to the disjunction of their individual negations, and conversely, the negation of a disjunction is logically equivalent to the conjunction of their negations. This distribution property allows for the transformation of complex logical expressions by pushing negation inward through "and" and "or" operators.
In intuitive, everyday language, De Morgan's first law can be phrased as: the negation of "both A and B are true" is equivalent to "either A is false or B is false (or both)." The second law states: the negation of "A or B is true" is equivalent to "both A is false and B is false." These phrasings capture the essence of how denying a combined condition breaks it into separate denials, providing a foundational tool for reasoning about negated statements.

Importance and applications

De Morgan's laws play a fundamental role in simplifying logical expressions by allowing the transformation of negations involving conjunctions and disjunctions, which facilitates clearer reasoning and duality in logical structures. This duality principle enables mathematicians and logicians to derive dual statements from existing theorems, enhancing proof techniques across various branches of mathematics, such as set theory and algebra, where complementary operations can be interchanged to reveal symmetric properties.
In computer engineering, these laws are essential for optimizing digital circuit designs, as they permit the equivalence of complex gate configurationssuch as converting AND-OR networks to NAND or NOR implementationsreducing hardware complexity and improving efficiency in integrated circuits. Similarly, in database systems, De Morgan's laws support query optimization by rewriting conditional predicates, enabling relational algebra transformations that minimize computational overhead during data retrieval and processing. Their influence extends to the foundations of modern computing, bridging 19th-century logical advancements to the development of Boolean-based systems that underpin contemporary processors and algorithms.
Everyday applications highlight their practical relevance; for instance, search engines apply these laws to handle negation in queries, efficiently excluding unwanted results by reformulating complex search conditions involving "not both" criteria. In safety-critical systems, such as fault-tolerant engineering designs, the laws ensure reliable logic for scenarios like ensuring not both components fail, where the condition (P Q) (neither fails) is equivalent to P Q (at least one works).

Formal statements

Propositional logic

In classical propositional logic, De Morgan's laws provide two fundamental equivalences involving negation and the binary connectives of conjunction and disjunction. The first law states that the negation of a conjunction is logically equivalent to the disjunction of the negations: (PQ)PQ\neg (P \land Q) \equiv \neg P \lor \neg Q(PQ)PQ, where PPP and QQQ are atomic propositions, \neg denotes negation, \land denotes conjunction (logical AND), and \lor denotes disjunction (logical OR). The second law states that the negation of a disjunction is logically equivalent to the conjunction of the negations: (PQ)PQ\neg (P \lor Q) \equiv \neg P \land \neg Q(PQ)PQ. These equivalences hold because the two sides of each formula have identical truth values across all possible assignments of truth values (true or false) to PPP and QQQ, making each a tautology when expressed as a biconditional.
The logical equivalence means that substituting one side for the other in any larger formula preserves the overall truth value, allowing De Morgan's laws to be applied recursively to complex expressions by replacing negated subformulas. For instance, in a formula like (P(QR))\neg (P \land (Q \lor R))(P(QR)), the inner conjunction can first be transformed using the first law, then further applications can push the negation deeper into the structure until it applies only to atomic propositions. This substitution property is essential for simplifying or normalizing logical expressions in proof systems and automated reasoning.

Set theory

In set theory, De Morgan's laws express the relationship between the complements of unions and intersections of sets. These laws are logically equivalent to their propositional counterparts but apply to geometric and membership-based relations among subsets of a universal set $ U $.
Consider two sets $ A $ and $ B $ that are subsets of a universal set $ U $. The complement of a set $ A $, denoted $ A^c $ or $ \overline{A} $, consists of all elements in $ U $ that are not in $ A $. De Morgan's first law states that the complement of the union of $ A $ and $ B $ is the intersection of their complements:
(AB)c=AcBc
(A \cup B)^c = A^c \cap B^c
(AB)c=AcBc
This means that an element is outside both $ A $ and $ B $ if and only if it is outside $ A $ or outside $ B $. The second law states that the complement of the intersection is the union of the complements:
(AB)c=AcBc
(A \cap B)^c = A^c \cup B^c
(AB)c=AcBc
Here, an element is outside at least one of $ A $ or $ B $ if and only if it is outside both.
These laws can be illustrated using Venn diagrams for two sets. In a Venn diagram, overlapping circles represent $ A $ and $ B $ within the rectangle of $ U $. The shaded region for $ (A \cup B)^c $ is the area outside both circles, which matches the unshaded overlap of the complements (regions only in $ A^c $ and only in $ B^c $, excluding their union). Similarly, for $ (A \cap B)^c $, the shaded region covers everything except the lens-shaped intersection, equivalent to the union of the exclusive parts of the complements.
The laws extend to infinite collections of sets indexed by an arbitrary set $ I $. For a family $ {A_i \mid i \in I} $ of subsets of $ U $, the complement of the union is the intersection of the complements:

\left( \bigcap_{i \in I} A_i \right)^c = \bigcup_{i \in I} A_i^c

This holds for both countable and uncountable index sets, preserving the duality under complementation. The universal set $ U $ ensures complements are well-defined, as elements outside $ U $ are irrelevant.

History

Origins in logic

The roots of the principles underlying De Morgan's laws trace back to ancient Greek logic, particularly Aristotle's development of indirect inference, or reductio ad absurdum, which relies on assuming the negation of a proposition to derive a contradiction, thereby affirming the original claim through the interplay of affirmation and denial. In this framework, Aristotle treated negation as a fundamental operation that inverts predications, enabling proofs by contradiction without explicitly formalizing distribution over compound statements, yet laying the groundwork for later understandings of negation's scope.
Medieval scholastic logicians, building on Aristotelian foundations, further explored negation's behavior through commentaries and expansions of term logic. Boethius, in his translations and commentaries on Aristotle's De Interpretatione, recognized negation as a denial that applies to the predicate, distinguishing it from affirmation and noting its role in oppositional relations such as contraries and contradictories, which implicitly addressed how negation interacts with combined terms in simple propositions. This recognition influenced subsequent scholastic treatments, where negation's distribution was examined in the context of categorical propositions, though still confined to syllogistic forms rather than propositional compounds.
In the Arabic logical tradition, thinkers like Avicenna advanced these ideas by analyzing logical oppositions and equivalences in conditional and disjunctive propositions. Avicenna's work on syllogistics implied the first De Morgan lawnot (p and q) equivalent to (not p or not q)through derivations involving negation of conjunctions and their transformation into disjunctions of negations, as seen in his treatment of implications and contraries. He emphasized distribution in predicates and the non-contrariety of certain negated compounds, expanding Aristotle's square of oppositions to include modal and temporal aspects, which facilitated a deeper grasp of negation's distributive properties in relational inferences.
By the early 19th century, these historical threads converged in efforts to expand traditional syllogistics toward more general forms. Medieval and Renaissance logicians, such as those following Buridan's insolubilia or the modal syllogisms of Averroes, had already broadened syllogistic rules to handle negations in hypothetical and relational contexts, introducing equivalences that prefigured propositional transformations. George Boole's 1847 The Mathematical Analysis of Logic marked a pivotal precursor by algebraizing logical operations, treating classes and their complements symbolically in a way that implicitly invoked negation's distribution over unions and intersections, bridging term-based syllogistics to modern symbolic notation. This transition enabled the explicit formulation of such laws in the emerging propositional framework, with De Morgan later building directly on these foundations to formalize them in relational terms.

Naming and publication

Augustus De Morgan first systematically presented the laws that would later bear his name in his 1847 book Formal Logic: Or, The Calculus of Inference, Necessary and Probable, published in London by Taylor and Walton. In Chapter V, on compound names and the logic of contraries, he articulated the duality between conjunction and disjunction under negation, stating that "the contrary of PQR is p,q,r; that of P,Q,R is pqr," where lowercase denotes negation, effectively formalizing the equivalences (P Q) P Q and (P Q) P Q. This presentation marked a key advancement in applying algebraic methods to propositional structures, building on his earlier papers in the Transactions of the Cambridge Philosophical Society.
Although De Morgan's formulation provided a rigorous, symbolic treatment, the underlying equivalences were recognized in earlier logical traditions, including Stoic philosophy and the works of William of Ockham in the 14th century, where verbal expressions akin to the laws appear in discussions of negation and disjunction. De Morgan's contribution lay in their systematic integration into a calculus of inference, resolving ambiguities in syllogistic logic through class semantics. He did not claim originality for the ideas themselves but emphasized their mathematical utility in his correspondence and subsequent writings, such as his 1858 essay "On the Syllogism."
The term "De Morgan's laws" emerged posthumously in the late 19th century, following his death in 1871, as logicians like Charles Sanders Peirce and others referenced his work in developing symbolic logic. An alternative designation, "De Morgan duality," highlights their role in the broader principle of duality in Boolean structures, a connection De Morgan himself explored.
De Morgan's motivation for these laws stemmed from paradoxes in probability theory and traditional logic, particularly the need to clarify inferences involving negation in probabilistic syllogisms and to extend Aristotelian logic beyond simple categorical forms. His work addressed issues like the "paradoxes of material implication" by providing tools for precise handling of compound propositions, influencing the transition to modern mathematical logic.

Semantically, De Morgan's laws arise because negation inverts truth values, and distributing negation over conjunction or disjunction preserves the overall semantics through exhaustive consideration of truth assignments. For instance, (PQ)\neg (P \land Q)(PQ) holds precisely when it is not the case that both PPP and QQQ are true, meaning at least one is false, which aligns with PQ\neg P \lor \neg QPQ.
This equivalence can be unpacked via step-by-step case analysis of the four possible truth assignments for PPP and QQQ:

If PPP is true and QQQ is true, then PQP \land QPQ is true, so (PQ)\neg (P \land Q)(PQ) is false; meanwhile, P\neg PP is false and Q\neg QQ is false, so PQ\neg P \lor \neg QPQ is false.

If PPP is true and QQQ is false, then PQP \land QPQ is false, so (PQ)\neg (P \land Q)(PQ) is true; P\neg PP is false but Q\neg QQ is true, so PQ\neg P \lor \neg QPQ is true.

If PPP is false and QQQ is true, then PQP \land QPQ is false, so (PQ)\neg (P \land Q)(PQ) is true; P\neg PP is true and Q\neg QQ is false, so PQ\neg P \lor \neg QPQ is true.

If PPP is false and QQQ is false, then PQP \land QPQ is false, so (PQ)\neg (P \land Q)(PQ) is true; both P\neg PP and Q\neg QQ are true, so PQ\neg P \lor \neg QPQ is true.

The cases match, establishing the equivalence; the dual law follows analogously by considering when disjunction fails (both false).
In axiomatic systems for propositional logic, such as Hilbert-style calculi, De Morgan's laws can be derived from basic axioms including double negation elimination (PP\neg \neg P \equiv PPP) and rules like modus ponens, though a full syntactic proof involves multiple steps of substitution and inference.

Duality in Boolean structures

In Boolean algebras, the duality principle asserts that any theorem remains valid if the operations of conjunction () and disjunction () are interchanged, the constants 0 and 1 are swapped, and complements are applied throughout the expression. This mapping preserves the structure of the algebra because De Morgan's laws ensure that the complement operation interacts symmetrically with and , effectively transforming one into the other under negation. Specifically, the duality theorem states that if an equation holds in a Boolean algebra, then its dualobtained by replacing with , with , 0 with 1, and 1 with 0, while complementing all variablesalso holds.
A concrete example illustrates this: the distributive law $ x \vee (y \wedge z) = (x \vee y) \wedge (x \vee z) $ has the dual $ x \wedge (y \vee z) = (x \wedge y) \vee (x \wedge z) $, which follows directly from applying De Morgan's laws to complement the original expression and swap operations. This duality extends the utility of De Morgan's laws beyond direct negation equivalences, enabling the derivation of symmetric properties in Boolean structures without separate proofs.
In broader lattice-theoretic contexts, partial dualities hold where De Morgan's laws apply selectively. For instance, in Heyting algebras, which generalize Boolean algebras by replacing classical complements with implication-based pseudo-complements, the dual De Morgan law $ \neg (a \vee b) = \neg a \wedge \neg b $ is always valid, supporting a restricted duality between intuitionistic and dual-intuitionistic logics. Similarly, orthocomplemented latticesbounded lattices with an orthocomplement operation satisfying De Morgan's lawsexhibit duality through order-reversing involutions, where the orthocomplement maps suprema to infima and vice versa.

Extensions to other logics

Predicate and modal logic

In predicate logic, De Morgan's laws extend to the interaction between negation and quantifiers, providing rules for negating quantified statements. The negation of a universal quantification is equivalent to an existential quantification of the negation: xP(x)xP(x)\neg \forall x \, P(x) \equiv \exists x \, \neg P(x)xP(x)xP(x). This means that "it is not the case that for every xxx, P(x)P(x)P(x) holds" is logically equivalent to "there exists some xxx such that P(x)P(x)P(x) does not hold." Similarly, the negation of an existential quantification is equivalent to a universal quantification of the negation: xP(x)xP(x)\neg \exists x \, P(x) \equiv \forall x \, \neg P(x)xP(x)xP(x). These equivalences, often called the quantifier negation rules or De Morgan's laws for quantifiers, allow negation to be distributed inward past the quantifiers, simplifying the manipulation of logical expressions in first-order theories.
These rules hold in classical predicate logic due to the bivalent semantics where statements are either true or false, enabling the interchange of quantifiers with negation without altering truth conditions. For instance, the statement "It is not true that every student passed the exam" (xPassed(x)\neg \forall x \, \text{Passed}(x)xPassed(x)) translates to "There exists a student who did not pass the exam" (xPassed(x)\exists x \, \neg \text{Passed}(x)xPassed(x)). Such transformations are essential for proving equivalences and resolving negations in automated theorem proving and formal verification.
In modal logic, De Morgan's laws manifest as dualities between the necessity operator \Box (meaning "necessarily" or true in all accessible worlds) and the possibility operator \Diamond (meaning "possibly" or true in some accessible world) under Kripke semantics. Specifically, \neg \Box \phi \equiv \Diamond \neg \phi, so "it is not necessary that \phi" is equivalent to "it is possible that not \phi." The dual form is \neg \Diamond \phi \equiv \Box \neg \phi. These equivalences arise because \Diamond is defined as the dual of \Box via negation: =\Diamond \phi = \neg \Box \neg \phi=, ensuring the laws hold tautologically in the semantics where truth is evaluated relative to possible worlds connected by an accessibility relation RRR.
While these dualities are valid in the basic modal logic K and its extensions, they interact with the properties of the accessibility relation in non-classical systems, such as reflexive or transitive frames, which can impose additional constraints on modal validity without undermining the core equivalences. For example, the English sentence "It is not necessary that all A are B" (x(A(x)B(x))\neg \Box \forall x (A(x) \to B(x))x(A(x)B(x))) can be rewritten using the laws as "It is possible that there exists an A that is not B" (x(A(x)B(x))\Diamond \exists x (A(x) \land \neg B(x))x(A(x)B(x))), illustrating how negation pushes through both modalities and quantifiers in quantified modal logic. These principles extend the propositional De Morgan's laws to richer expressive settings involving necessity and possibility.

Intuitionistic logic

In intuitionistic logic, De Morgan's laws do not hold in full equivalence as they do in classical logic, reflecting the rejection of the law of excluded middle and the constructive nature of proofs. Specifically, the law (P Q) P Q is valid in both directions: (P Q) implies P Q, since a proof of (P Q) provides methods to derive contradictions from assumptions of P or Q separately, yielding P and Q; conversely, P Q implies (P Q), as assuming P Q allows elimination to reach a contradiction using the proofs of P and Q.
In contrast, the dual law (P Q) P Q holds only partially: the direction P Q (P Q) is intuitionistically valid, since assuming P Q and P Q leads to a contradiction via disjunction elimination on the negations. However, the converse (P Q) P Q fails without the law of excluded middle, as a proof that P Q leads to contradiction does not constructively determine whether P or Q holds individually; counterexamples in Kripke models show scenarios where no future verifies both P and Q, yet futures exist for P alone and Q alone separately.
This behavior arises in the semantics of intuitionistic logic, where negation is defined via implication to falsehood: A A . In corresponding Heyting algebras, the algebraic models for intuitionistic propositional logic, negation satisfies (a b) = a b fully, but only a b (a b) (the partial direction above), with equality requiring additional classical axioms to make the algebra Boolean.
These modifications have significant implications in computer science, particularly in type theory and proof assistants like Coq, which implement intuitionistic logic to ensure constructive proofs. In Coq, the full classical De Morgan laws require importing axioms such as weak excluded middle, while intuitionistic versions limit reasoning to the valid directions, enforcing explicit constructions in program extraction and verification; for instance, libraries separate classical facts to avoid non-constructive assumptions in certified software.

Applications

Computer engineering

In digital circuit design, De Morgan's laws facilitate the simplification of Boolean expressions, enabling the implementation of complex logic using universal gates such as NAND and NOR, which are preferred for their efficiency in fabrication. For instance, the law (AB)=AB\neg (A \land B) = \neg A \lor \neg B(AB)=AB demonstrates that a two-input AND gate followed by an inverter can be directly replaced by a single NAND gate, eliminating the need for a separate NOT gate and thereby reducing component count. Similarly, (AB)=AB\neg (A \lor B) = \neg A \land \neg B(AB)=AB allows an OR gate with inverter to be realized as a NOR gate. This equivalence is crucial in very-large-scale integration (VLSI) where NAND and NOR gates are easier to manufacture and often form the basis of standard cell libraries.
Hardware description languages (HDLs) like Verilog and VHDL leverage De Morgan's laws during the synthesis process to optimize Boolean expressions for gate-level implementation, ensuring minimal hardware resources in field-programmable gate arrays (FPGAs) or application-specific integrated circuits (ASICs). Designers rewrite expressions using these laws to favor NAND or NOR primitives, which synthesis tools then map to efficient netlists, avoiding suboptimal AND/OR configurations that may increase area or power consumption. For example, in Verilog, an expression like ~(a & b) can be dualized to (~a | ~b) and synthesized as a NOR-based structure, streamlining the resulting hardware.
A practical application is the optimization of a full adder circuit, which computes the sum and carry for three binary inputs (A, B, and carry-in Cin). The standard carry-out expression C=AB+(AB)CinC = AB + (A \oplus B)C_{in}C=AB+(AB)Cin can be dualized using De Morgan's laws to an equivalent form suitable for all-NAND implementation: by inverting inputs and outputs appropriately, the circuit transforms into a network of NAND gates only, such as using (AB+(AB)Cin)=(AB)((AB)Cin)\neg(AB + (A \oplus B)C_{in}) = \neg(AB) \land \neg((A \oplus B)C_{in})(AB+(AB)Cin)=(AB)((AB)Cin) and further simplifying the XOR term. This NAND-only realization typically requires 9 NAND gates instead of mixed gates with inverters, reducing overall gate count by up to 20% in basic designs.
Such optimizations yield significant performance benefits in VLSI design, including fewer gates that lower interconnect complexity and capacitance, thereby reducing propagation delay by 10-30% in critical paths compared to unoptimized AND/OR implementations. Additionally, minimized circuits consume less power and occupy smaller silicon area, which is essential for high-density chips. These advantages stem directly from the duality principle enabled by De Morgan's laws, allowing designers to select gate types that align with process technology strengths.

Engineering fields

In signal processing, De Morgan's laws enable the simplification of logical expressions involving negations in Boolean signal flows, particularly for optimizing filter designs where the complement of union or intersection operations on signal events is required. This is evident in binary morphological operations, such as erosion and dilation in digital image processing, where the duality principle derived from De Morgan's laws relates the complement of a dilation to an erosion of complements, facilitating efficient negation of filter conditions for tasks like edge detection or noise suppression. Such simplifications are crucial for fault detection algorithms, where transforming complex logical conditions reduces computational overhead in real-time signal analysis.
In control systems engineering, De Morgan's laws are instrumental in relay logic and programmable logic controller (PLC) programming, allowing engineers to redesign safety interlocks by converting negated conjunctions or disjunctions into equivalent forms. For example, the safety condition "not both sensors fail," expressed as the negation of the AND of two failure signals ((A B)), simplifies via De Morgan's first law to (A B), enabling implementation with OR logic instead of nested negations and improving system reliability in industrial automation. This transformation ensures fail-safe operations, as seen in ladder logic diagrams where inverted outputs are optimized to prevent hazardous states in machinery control.
Reliability engineering leverages De Morgan's laws to model system failures through set complements, particularly when computing the probability of unions of failure events in non-series-parallel configurations. By deducing minimal cut sets (failure combinations) from minimal path sets (success combinations) using these laws, engineers can express system unreliability as the complement of reliability, streamlining probabilistic assessments. For instance, in fault tree analysis, the laws facilitate pushing negations through OR and AND gates to identify critical failure paths without exhaustive enumeration.
In power systems engineering, De Morgan's laws support dualizing logical conditions in circuit breaker protection schemes, as demonstrated in seismic reliability analyses of large networks. Here, Boolean simplification via the laws decomposes the system into exclusive safe and failed paths, allowing recursive evaluation of joint probabilities under multivariate conditions to quantify outage risks from events like earthquakes. This approach ensures robust logic for breaker tripping, where negated unions of fault signals are transformed to intersections of complements, enhancing grid stability.

Text searching and information retrieval

In text searching and information retrieval systems, De Morgan's laws facilitate the efficient processing of negated Boolean queries by allowing the redistribution of negation operators. For instance, a query such as "not (A and B)"where A and B represent search terms or document setscan be rewritten as "(not A) or (not B)", enabling the system to leverage precomputed inverted indexes for individual terms rather than evaluating complex nested conditions directly. This transformation, grounded in the set-theoretic interpretation of documents as elements in a universe, optimizes retrieval by converting conjunctions under negation into disjunctions of complements, which are faster to compute using union operations on posting lists.
Boolean search operators in tools like Google Advanced Search or SQL queries commonly apply these laws to simplify complex negations. In Google, a query like NOT (cat AND dog) is internally equivalent to (NOT cat) OR (NOT dog), allowing the search engine to exclude documents containing both terms by unioning the complements of their respective posting lists, thus avoiding exhaustive scans. Similarly, in SQL, query optimizers use De Morgan's laws during predicate normalization to eliminate NOT operators; for example, rewriting WHERE NOT (condition1 AND condition2) as WHERE NOT condition1 OR NOT condition2, which can reduce the need for temporary tables or full table scans by pushing selections earlier in the execution plan. This rewriting is part of standard algebraic optimization techniques in relational database management systems.
Inverted indexes, the core data structure in modern search engines, benefit significantly from De Morgan's laws by distributing negations across terms for scalable retrieval. In an inverted index, each term maps to a sorted list of document identifiers (postings); applying the laws to push negations inwardsuch as transforming NOT (A OR B) to (NOT A) AND (NOT B)converts disjunctive negations into intersections of complement lists, which can be efficiently merged using algorithms like galloping search with O(n log(L/)) time complexity, where n is the number of terms, the intersection size, and L the collection size. This avoids computing the full universe of documents for each negation, reducing I/O costs in large-scale systems.
A practical example of database query optimization using De Morgan rewriting occurs in relational queries involving joins and selections. Consider a SQL query selecting employees not in both the sales and marketing departments: SELECT * FROM Employees WHERE NOT (Dept = 'Sales' AND Dept = 'Marketing'), which is malformed but illustrates the intent; rewriting via De Morgan as SELECT * FROM Employees WHERE Dept != 'Sales' OR Dept != 'Marketing' allows the optimizer to apply index scans on individual department attributes separately, potentially eliminating unnecessary joins or cartesian products and reducing execution time from O(N^2) to O(N) in indexed scenarios. Such optimizations are routinely applied in systems like PostgreSQL or Oracle to minimize computational overhead.
