Adrien-Marie Legendre

1x
 Adrien-Marie Legendre (17521833) was a prominent French mathematician whose work significantly advanced number theory, celestial mechanics, and mathematical analysis, including pioneering the method of least squares for data fitting and developing key concepts in elliptic integrals and functions. Born into a wealthy family in Paris on 18 September 1752, Legendre received an elite education in mathematics and physics at the Collge Mazarin, where he defended a thesis in 1770 at age 18.
Legendre began his career teaching mathematics alongside Pierre-Simon Laplace at the cole Militaire from 1775 to 1780, and he gained early recognition by winning the Berlin Academy prize in 1782 for his research on projectile trajectories. In 1783, he was elected as an adjoint to the Acadmie Royale des Sciences, later becoming a member of the Institut National des Sciences et des Arts in 1795 and serving in its geometry section from 1803. During the French Revolution, Legendre lost much of his family's fortune in 1793 but contributed to national efforts, including the metric system committee in 1791 and the Cadastre land survey project from 1792 to 1801.
Among his most influential contributions, Legendre introduced Legendre polynomials in his 1784 memoir Recherches sur la figure des plantes, which addressed planetary shapes and gravitational attraction. In 1805, he published the first formal description of the method of least squares in Nouvelles mthodes pour la dtermination des orbites des comtes, a technique for minimizing errors in astronomical observations that remains foundational in statistics and data analysis. In number theory, Legendre stated the law of quadratic reciprocity in his 1785 work Recherches d'analyse indtermine and attempted a proof, though the complete proof was provided by Carl Friedrich Gauss; Legendre incorporated Gauss's result in the 1808 edition of Thorie des nombres, where he also estimated the distribution of prime numbers. His 1794 textbook lments de gomtrie became a standard Euclidean geometry text, widely translated and reprinted, and included proofs of the irrationality of and . Legendre further advanced analysis through his multi-volume works on integral calculus (18111819) and elliptic functions (18251832), laying groundwork for later developments by Niels Henrik Abel and Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi. He also devised the Legendre symbol to study quadratic residues.
In his later years, Legendre faced financial hardship after his government pension was revoked in 1824 due to political disagreements, leading to poverty despite his earlier wealth. He died in Paris on 10 January 1833, honored during his lifetime with election to the Royal Society of London in 1787. Legendre's legacy endures in fields like approximation theory and astronomy, with concepts such as Legendre's equation and Legendre transformation bearing his name.

Early Life and Education

Birth and Family

Adrien-Marie Legendre was born on September 18, 1752, in Paris, France, into a prosperous bourgeois family. Although some historical accounts suggest he may have been born in Toulouse and relocated to Paris with his family at a young age, Paris is widely accepted as his birthplace.
Legendre's family background provided significant financial stability, allowing him to pursue intellectual interests without the pressures of employment in his early years. His father, from a line of affluent individuals, supported the household through unspecified means that ensured a comfortable lifestyle typical of 18th-century Parisian bourgeoisie. Details on his mother and siblings remain sparse in historical records, with no confirmed names or numbers documented, but the family's resources clearly fostered an environment geared toward education and cultural refinement.
Little is known about Legendre's early childhood, but the stability of his upbringing contrasted with the turbulent socio-political climate of pre-Revolutionary France, enabling focused personal development in a nurturing setting.

Academic Training

Adrien-Marie Legendre, born into a prosperous Parisian family, received his formal education at the prestigious Collge Mazarin, where family resources enabled access to a rigorous curriculum in humanities, sciences, mathematics, and physics.
Under the guidance of notable professors, including the Abb Marie, who taught mathematics, Legendre developed a strong foundation in analytical subjects during his studies at the institution. In 1770, at the age of 18, he defended his theses in mathematics and physics, which primarily outlined a research plan rather than presenting completed findings, marking the culmination of his academic training.
This education exposed Legendre to key concepts in Newtonian mechanics and calculus, equipping him with the analytical tools essential for his subsequent pursuits in astronomy and applied mathematics. His early collaboration with Abb Marie, contributing articles to the latter's Trait de mcanique published in 1774, further demonstrated the practical application of these foundational principles shortly after graduation.

Professional Career

Academy Roles and Astronomical Work

Legendre's mathematical and astronomical talents were recognized early in his career, culminating in his election to the French Academy of Sciences on March 30, 1783, at the age of 31, where he was appointed as an adjoint mcanicien in the astronomy section, replacing Pierre-Simon Laplace who had been promoted. This prestigious position followed the high praise from Laplace's report on Legendre's January 1783 memoir concerning the gravitational attractions of ellipsoids, which demonstrated his proficiency in celestial mechanics. His academic training at the Collge Mazarin in Paris had equipped him with the rigorous computational skills essential for such astronomical analyses.
In addition to his Academy role, Legendre contributed to observational and computational astronomy, including work on the Anglo-French Survey (17841790) to measure the distance between the Paris Observatory and the Royal Greenwich Observatory using triangulation. From 1781, he was tasked with computing planetary tables for the Connaissance des Temps, the official French astronomical almanac used by navigators and astronomers, a responsibility that underscored his expertise in ephemerides and orbital predictions. These duties involved meticulous calculations to ensure accurate predictions of celestial positions, reflecting the practical demands of 18th-century astronomy.
Legendre's key contributions to astronomy included detailed calculations of planetary perturbations, particularly those affecting the orbits of Jupiter and Saturn, which advanced the understanding of mutual gravitational influences among the outer planets. In 1782, he published a memoir on the trajectories of projectiles in resistant media, which earned him a prize from the Berlin Academy of Sciences. He collaborated closely with contemporaries such as Joseph-Jrme de Lalande, the director of the Paris Observatory, on various astronomical projects, including refinements to star catalogs and ephemerides. By 1786, Legendre had produced an influential memoir on the libration of the Moon, exploring the oscillatory motion that causes slight variations in the visible lunar surface, thereby contributing to more precise lunar theories.

Geodetic Surveys and Revolutionary Involvement

In 1784, Legendre published his memoir Recherches sur la figure des plantes, which explored the gravitational attraction of ellipsoidal bodies and contributed to early geodetic theory by modeling planetary shapes as oblate spheroids, laying groundwork for precise Earth measurements. This work built on Newtonian principles and influenced subsequent efforts to determine the Earth's figure through gravitational analysis.
During the 1790s, Legendre played a key role in France's metric system reform, serving on a committee established in 1791 to standardize weights and measures based on natural units. He contributed to triangulation surveys essential for defining the meter as one ten-millionth of the Earth's meridian quadrant from the North Pole to the equator. Specifically, Legendre collaborated with Jean-Baptiste Joseph Delambre on analyzing data from the meridian arc measurement expedition from Dunkirk to Barcelona (17921799), led by Delambre in the north and Pierre Mchain in the south; this project provided the empirical basis for the meter's length, finalized in 1799 as a platinum prototype. His computations helped reconcile observational discrepancies, advancing geodetic accuracy despite wartime disruptions.
Amid the French Revolution, Legendre navigated intense political turmoil, surviving purges that targeted many intellectuals while the Academy of Sciences was dissolved in 1793. Under the Directory and later Napoleon, Legendre continued geodetic contributions, including the Cadastre project (17921801) for land surveying. He also served as an examiner for the cole Polytechnique, mentoring future engineers.

Mathematical Contributions

Number Theory Advancements

Adrien-Marie Legendre's early contributions to number theory focused on Diophantine equations and quadratic forms, detailed in his 1785 memoir Recherches d'analyse indtermine. Legendre extended Leonhard Euler's partial results on quadratic reciprocity in his 1785 memoir, proving the law for specific cases involving primes congruent to 3 modulo 4. Carl Friedrich Gauss provided the first rigorous proof in 1801 using Gauss sums, highlighting flaws in Legendre's approach while acknowledging its foundational role; Legendre subsequently incorporated Gauss's method with full attribution in the 1808 edition of Thorie des nombres, fostering a collaborative advancement in the field.

This conjecture anticipated Dirichlet's theorem, influencing proofs of infinite primes in specific residue classes by leveraging quadratic reciprocity properties.

Elliptic Integrals and Functions

Legendre's pioneering work on elliptic integrals began with a memoir published in 1786 in the Mmoires de l'Acadmie Royale des Sciences, where he addressed the problem of determining the period of a simple pendulum for large amplitudes, leading to the first systematic treatment of these integrals as arising from the arc length of ellipses. In this paper, titled "Mmoire sur les intgrations par arcs d'ellipses," he demonstrated how the pendulum's motion involves integrals that cannot be expressed in elementary functions, marking a shift from ad hoc computations to a unified analytical framework. A companion 1786 memoir further explored comparisons among elliptic arcs, solidifying their role in mechanical problems.

Method of Least Squares

Adrien-Marie Legendre developed the method of least squares as a technique for reducing astronomical observations to determine the most probable values amid measurement errors, publishing it in 1805 as an appendix titled "Sur la Mthode des moindres quarrs" in his work Nouvelles mthodes pour la dtermination des orbites des comtes. This algebraic procedure addressed the challenge of overdetermined systems, where more observations exist than parameters to estimate, by providing a systematic way to fit models to data. Legendre's innovation stemmed from his practical needs in celestial mechanics, marking a foundational advancement in data analysis that influenced subsequent developments in statistics and computation.
The core principle of the method is to minimize the sum of the squared residuals between observed values and the model's predictions, ensuring that the fitted parameters yield the smallest overall deviation. Legendre applied the method primarily to fitting planetary and cometary orbits from telescopic observations, where multiple sightings provided redundant data for estimating orbital elements like semi-major axis and eccentricity. He also extended its use to geodetic surveys, such as the French meridian measurement, treating triangulation points as a system to find the "center of gravity" via least squares adjustment. Although Carl Friedrich Gauss had privately employed a similar approach since 1795 for asteroid orbit calculations, Legendre's 1805 publication was the first public exposition, prompting Gauss to reveal his earlier work in 1809 and credit Legendre accordingly.
Legendre's formulation was inherently algebraic but laid groundwork for extensions to non-linear problems, as seen in his orbital fittings requiring iterative solutions for transcendental equations. Later probabilistic interpretations, building on ideas from Laplace, justified the method under assumptions of normally distributed errors, though Legendre's original presentation predated such statistical framing.

Geometry and Other Analytical Works

In 1794, Adrien-Marie Legendre published lments de gomtrie, a seminal textbook on Euclidean geometry that rivaled Euclid's Elements in scope and pedagogical impact, while placing a strong emphasis on logical rigor and complete proofs for all propositions. The work rearranged classical propositions for clarity and efficiency, omitting some of Euclid's more cumbersome arguments to prioritize deductive precision, and it served as the dominant elementary geometry text in Europe and beyond for nearly a century. Within this volume, Legendre also included an appendix with a straightforward proof, based on continued fraction expansions, establishing the irrationality of , building on Johann Lambert's earlier result; this was the first such proof for , further solidifying the number's non-algebraic nature. He conjectured therein that is not the root of any algebraic equation with rational coefficients, anticipating later transcendence proofs.
Legendre's contributions to spherical geometry arose from his geodetic research, particularly in the 1787 Mmoire sur les oprations trigonomtriques, dont les rsultats dpendent de la figure de la Terre, where he formulated key theorems on spherical triangles to address measurement challenges in Earth surveys. His theorem on spherical triangles provides corrections to treat small spherical triangles as plane ones, with the angles of the spherical triangle exceeding those of the plane triangle with the same sides by approximately the spherical excess divided by three, enabling precise computations of spherical excess (the excess of the angle sum over , proportional to the triangle's area), which was crucial for determining geodesicsthe shortest paths on spherical surfacesin practical triangulation for mapping. These results streamlined geodetic calculations by reducing reliance on exhaustive numerical tables, influencing survey methods for decades.

Later Life and Legacy

Personal Challenges and Death

Legendre married Marguerite-Claudine Couhin in 1793, amid the turmoil of the French Revolution that also led to the loss of his inherited fortune. His wife played a key role in helping him reorganize his finances, enabling a modest family life marked by mutual support during ongoing difficulties.
Following Napoleon's fall in 1815, Legendre's financial security eroded as political changes affected his positions. In 1824, he lost his pension from the cole Militaire after refusing to vote for the government's preferred candidate at the Institut National, forcing him to rely on teaching duties and modest stipends from bodies like the Bureau des Longitudes for survival. This culminated in poverty during his later years, contrasting the relative career stability he had enjoyed even through the Revolution.
Legendre's health declined in his final years, exacerbated by poverty, as he endured a long and painful illness. He received assistance from academic colleagues who valued his contributions, helping to alleviate some hardships.
He died on January 10, 1833, in Paris at the age of 80. Legendre was buried in the Cimetire d'Auteuil alongside his wife, who outlived him until 1856 and preserved his personal belongings as a memorial to his life and work.

Major Publications

Adrien-Marie Legendre produced a substantial body of work, including over 80 papers and several seminal books that advanced fields such as geometry, number theory, astronomy, and analysis. His publications often originated from his roles in the French Academy of Sciences, where he contributed numerous memoirs to the Histoire de l'Acadmie royale des sciences volumes covering the years 1783 to 1800, addressing topics like gravitational attraction and indeterminate analysis. These works, alongside his monographs, established him as a foundational figure in 19th-century mathematics, with many achieving widespread adoption in education and research.
One of Legendre's earliest and most enduring publications was lments de gomtrie (1794), a comprehensive textbook that restructured Euclidean geometry for modern audiences by simplifying axioms, omitting the parallel postulate from initial presentation, and incorporating trigonometric applications. It included innovative proofs, such as the irrationality of (using integrals) and the irrationality of , and became a standard educational text in France and beyond, remaining in use for nearly a century across multiple editions and translations.
In number theory, Legendre's Essai sur la thorie des nombres (1798), expanded in subsequent editions of 1808 and 1830 under the title Thorie des nombres, provided the first systematic book-length treatment of the subject. It covered Diophantine equations, properties of quadratic forms, the law of quadratic reciprocity (presented as a conjecture later proved by Gauss), and early approximations for the distribution of prime numbers, such as (n) n / (ln n - 1.08366), which approximated the prime number theorem before its formal proof. The work drew on Euler's ideas while introducing Legendre's own refinements, influencing later developments in analytic number theory.
Legendre's Nouvelles mthodes pour la dtermination des orbites des comtes (1805) marked a pivotal advancement in astronomy and statistics by formalizing the method of least squares for fitting observational data to orbital paths. The book applied this technique to compute comet orbits from minimal observations, assuming parabolic trajectories, and demonstrated its superiority for error minimization in celestial mechanics, laying groundwork for its adoption in geodesy and beyond.
His extensive explorations of elliptic integrals appeared across multiple volumes, notably in Exercices du calcul intgral (1811, 1817, 1819), which examined integrals, beta and gamma functions, and their mechanical applications, and culminated in the three-volume Trait des fonctions elliptiques (1825, 1826, 1830). These works offered a detailed classification and transformation of elliptic functions, integrating results from Euler and others, though they were soon supplemented by more elegant formulations from Abel and Jacobi. Together, they provided essential tools for mathematical physics until the mid-19th century.

Honors and Misattributions

Legendre was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1789 for his contributions to celestial mechanics. In 1795, he became one of the six founding members of the mathematics section of the newly reconstituted Acadmie des Sciences, which was incorporated into the Institut de France. He was also elected as an International Honorary Member of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences in 1832. In 1831, Legendre was appointed an officer of the Lgion d'Honneur in recognition of his scientific achievements.
Several mathematical concepts bear Legendre's name, reflecting his enduring impact on the field. The Legendre polynomials, introduced in his 1782 work on gravitational attraction, are a system of orthogonal polynomials fundamental to solving partial differential equations in spherical coordinates. The Legendre symbol (a/p)(a/p)(a/p), defined by Legendre in 1798, is a multiplicative function that determines whether a number aaa is a quadratic residue modulo an odd prime ppp, playing a key role in the law of quadratic reciprocity.
Posthumously, Legendre's legacy has been honored through various namings and recognitions. A lunar impact crater near the Moon's eastern limb, approximately 78 kilometers in diameter, was named Legendre in 1935 by the International Astronomical Union. In Paris, Rue Legendre in the 17th arrondissement commemorates him, alongside Passage Legendre. His work, particularly the polynomials, continues to influence modern physics, including quantum mechanics, where they form the basis for angular momentum eigenfunctions in central potential problems.
A notable misattribution in Legendre's biographical representation involves his portrait. For over a century, a 19th-century engraving depicting French revolutionary politician Louis Legendre (17521797) was widely circulated and reproduced as an image of the mathematician Adrien-Marie Legendre, beginning around 1900 due to the similarity in names and birth years. This error persisted in textbooks, encyclopedias, and online sources until 2005, when art historians identified the engraving as Louis Legendre based on historical records and stylistic analysis. In 2008, the only known authentic portrait of Adrien-Marie Legendrea caricature by Julien-Lopold Boilly from 1820, depicting him as a member of the Institut de Francewas rediscovered in the institute's library, providing the first verified visual representation from his lifetime.
