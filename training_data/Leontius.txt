 Leontius (Greek: , Lentios; c. 660 705) was Byzantine emperor from 695 to 698, known for his military prowess and brief rule amid the empire's turbulent seventh-century crises. Of Isaurian origin, he rose as an efficient general under emperors Constantine IV and Justinian II, leading successful campaigns against Arab forces in Armenia and Georgia in 686 that secured a favorable treaty with Caliph Abd al-Malik, including shared revenues from key territories and increased tribute. Appointed strategos of the Anatolikon theme, Leontius suffered a major defeat at the Battle of Sebastopolis in 692, leading to his imprisonment by Justinian II, whose harsh fiscal policies and military failures had alienated the populace. In 695, released and made strategos of Hellas, he spearheaded a rebellion backed by the Blue faction and Patriarch Callinicus, deposing Justinianwho was mutilated and exiledand ascending the throne under the regnal name Leo. His reign sought moderation, limiting army deployments and easing some persecutions, but faltered with the permanent Arab conquest of Carthage in 697698 despite a temporary recapture, culminating in his overthrow by fleet commander Apsimar (Tiberius III) amid a siege and plague. Mutilated and confined to a monastery, Leontius was publicly paraded and beheaded in 705 upon Justinian II's restoration, epitomizing the era's cycle of usurpations and reprisals.

Early Life and Military Career

Origins and Family Background

Leontius was born circa 660 in Isauria, a mountainous and historically rebellious region in central Asia Minor encompassing parts of modern-day Turkey's Taurus Mountains, inhabited by a population known for its martial traditions and partial Hellenization by the late 7th century. Contemporary prosopographical analysis identifies him ethnically as Isaurian, reflecting the region's demographic profile amid ongoing Byzantine efforts to integrate its hardy, semi-autonomous communities.
Historical chronicles offer minimal insight into his family background, parentage, or upbringing, with primary sources such as Theophanes the Confessor focusing primarily on his later military career rather than personal origins. This scarcity likely stems from the turbulent political context of the Twenty Years' Anarchy (695717), during which records of non-imperial lineages were often neglected or lost. Some scholarly interpretations suggest possible Armenian influences in his ancestry, given the prevalence of Armenian settlements and naming conventions in Isauria following earlier migrations and Byzantine resettlements, though direct evidence remains absent.

Service Under Constantine IV and Justinian II

Leontius received his initial high-level appointment under Emperor Constantine IV (r. 668685), who named him patrikios and strategos of the Anatolic Theme, the empire's premier military district responsible for defending Asia Minor's eastern frontiers against Arab incursions. This role positioned him as one of the Byzantine army's senior commanders during a period of ongoing Umayyad pressure following the failed Arab siege of Constantinople in 674678.
Following Constantine IV's death, Leontius continued in imperial service under Justinian II (r. 685695). In 686, Justinian dispatched him to Armenia and Iberia (modern Georgia) to counter Arab raiding parties. Leontius's forces routed the invaders and pressed into Azerbaijan and Albania, amassing substantial plunder while reportedly exhibiting harsh treatment of prisoners, which bolstered his martial reputation. These victories compelled Caliph Abd al-Malik (r. 685705) to renegotiate the truce, conceding shared tax revenues from Armenia, Iberia, and Cyprus to Byzantium alongside an augmented annual tribute payment.
The peace lapsed in 692 amid renewed Umayyad offensives, prompting Justinian to entrust Leontius with another expedition against Muhammad ibn Marwan's army. At the Battle of Sebastopolis (near modern Sivas, Turkey), a massive desertion by Slavic mercenariesnumbering up to 30,000within the Byzantine ranks turned the engagement into a rout, inflicting severe losses on imperial forces. Justinian II, attributing the disaster to Leontius's command, ordered his arrest and confinement in a Constantinopolitan prison, effectively sidelining him from active duty.

Ascension to the Throne

Context of Justinian II's Tyranny

Justinian II's first reign, from 685 to 695, initially featured diplomatic and military initiatives, including a truce with the Umayyad Caliphate in 685 and subsequent Balkan campaigns that subdued Slavic tribes and resettled 30,000 of them as soldiers. However, these efforts strained imperial finances, prompting aggressive fiscal policies that alienated the populace and military. To fund ongoing wars, monumental constructions, and personal luxuries, Justinian imposed burdensome taxes across the themes, targeting both elites and commoners, which exacerbated economic hardship amid recurrent Arab incursions. 
The emperor's autocratic governance further fueled resentment through systematic persecution and terror. He revived the use of informers to root out suspected disloyalty, leading to arbitrary arrests, confiscations of property, and executions of senators, officials, and even the patriarch Kallinikos I, whom he deposed and blinded in 693 for opposing his religious impositions favoring Monothelitism. Justinian also ordered the slaughter of Slavic defectors and targeted Manichaeans and non-Chalcedonian Christians, employing mutilation and exile as tools of control, practices that echoed but intensified Byzantine traditions of political punishment. These measures, chronicled negatively by Theophanes Confessor as a shift to "evil courses" under corrupt advisors, eroded elite support and military cohesion.
Military reversals compounded the domestic crisis. After abrogating the truce in 692 to assert dominance, Byzantine forces suffered heavy defeats, including the loss of 20,000 troops at the Battle of Sebastopolis and subsequent Arab advances that captured key fortresses by 695. The failure of a naval expedition against Carthage in 695, resulting in the city's fall to Arab forces, triggered mutiny among the fleet and army, who viewed Justinian's demands for scapegoats and increased levies as intolerable. This convergence of fiscal oppression, punitive governance, and battlefield setbacks created the conditions for rebellion, as troops and citizens in Constantinople rallied against the emperor's rule.

Coup and Proclamation as Emperor

In 695, following military defeats against the Arabs and growing popular discontent with Justinian II's harsh taxation and arbitrary rule, Leontiusrecently released from prison after the 692 disaster at Sebastopolis and appointed strategos of the Theme of Hellasled a rebellion in Constantinople. He garnered support from the Blue faction of the Hippodrome and Patriarch Callinicus I, who opposed the emperor's policies.
The coup unfolded swiftly toward the end of 695, with Leontius and his allies seizing Justinian II in the palace. The deposed emperor underwent ritual mutilation: his nose and tongue were slit, rendering him unfit for rule under Byzantine custom, before being exiled to Cherson in Crimea. This act, drawn from historical precedents like the treatment of previous rulers, symbolized the rejection of Justinian's legitimacy.
Immediately after the deposition, Leontius was acclaimed as emperor by the Constantinopolitan populace, the military, and key factions in a public ceremony, marking the formal transfer of power. Primary accounts, such as those in Theophanes' Chronographia and Nicephorus' Chronographikon syntomon, record the event as a popular uprising against tyranny, though details vary on the exact sequence. Leontius adopted the regnal name Leo and initiated his brief reign amid ongoing threats from Arab forces.

Imperial Reign

Administrative and Fiscal Reforms

Leontius's brief reign (695698) occurred amid ongoing military pressures and the aftermath of Justinian II's unpopular policies, limiting opportunities for extensive administrative restructuring. Primary Byzantine chronicles, such as those drawing from Theophanes, emphasize his defensive consolidation over innovative governance, with no explicit records of systemic administrative overhauls. He restricted large-scale army deployments, permitting minor border incursions without reprisal to conserve manpower and resources strained by prior campaigns.
Fiscal policy under Leontius appears to have prioritized stability rather than reform, as the empire grappled with losses in North Africa and Armenia. No contemporary sources detail tax adjustments, expenditure cuts, or monetary innovations attributable to his rule, though his issuance of solidi maintained standard imperial iconography and weight, suggesting continuity in minting practices amid fiscal constraints. This approach reflected a pragmatic effort to avoid Justinian II's fiscal excesses, which had alienated elites and fueled the 695 coup, but lacked the transformative measures seen in later Isaurian emperors.
Historians note that Leontius's administration leaned on established thematic structures for revenue collection, focusing resources on fortifications like those in Anatolia rather than bureaucratic reorganization. The absence of documented reforms may stem from the era's chroniclers' bias toward military narratives, undervaluing routine fiscal management in a period of contraction.

Military Engagements and Defeats

Leontius pursued a policy of military restraint during his brief reign, prioritizing internal consolidation over offensive operations against the expanding Umayyad Caliphate under Abd al-Malik, despite ongoing Arab incursions into Byzantine territories. This approach stemmed from the empire's recent defeats, including the Battle of Sebastopolis in 692, and aimed to avoid further strain on depleted resources, but it allowed Arab forces to intensify pressure on North Africa.
In 697, Umayyad governor Hasan ibn al-Nu'man captured Carthage, the last major Byzantine stronghold in Africa, exploiting Leontius's hesitancy to dispatch reinforcements promptly. To counter this loss, Leontius organized a naval expedition comprising approximately 40,000 men under the command of John the Patrician, with the droungarios of the fleet Tiberius Apsimarus as second-in-command. The force initially recaptured the harbor and much of the city, but Hasan returned with reinforcements, defeating the Byzantines in subsequent engagements and forcing their evacuation to Crete by early 698.
The expedition's failure, marked by heavy losses and the permanent forfeiture of Carthagewhose walls were razed and aqueducts destroyedexacerbated military discontent. Returning officers, fearing severe punishment for the debacle, mutinied against John and proclaimed Apsimarus emperor as Tiberius III, who then sailed to Constantinople and besieged the capital. The city fell in 698 after internal betrayal and plague outbreaks weakened defenses, directly linking the African defeat to Leontius's deposition. Byzantine chroniclers such as Theophanes and Nicephorus attribute the outcome to strategic missteps and inadequate support, underscoring the reign's vulnerability to Arab advances.

Religious Policies and Monothelitism

Leontius' religious policies during his brief reign (695698) adhered to the dyothelite orthodoxy established by the Sixth Ecumenical Council (680681), which had definitively condemned Monothelitismthe doctrine positing a single will in Christas heretical, thereby affirming two wills in alignment with Chalcedonian Christology. No evidence indicates that Leontius sought to rehabilitate or promote Monothelitism, a position previously advanced by emperors such as Heraclius and Constans II but rejected under Constantine IV, Justinian II's father. Instead, continuity with prior ecclesiastical decisions prevailed, as Leontius retained Patriarch Callinicus I (r. 693705), who had endorsed the coup against Justinian II and upheld the council's rulings without notable doctrinal shifts.
The Quinisext Council of 692, convened by Justinian II to address disciplinary canons omitted from the Sixth Council, remained in effect under Leontius, with its canons emphasizing clerical discipline and liturgical uniformity rather than reopening Christological debates. Absent any recorded synods, edicts, or persecutions tied to Monothelite sympathizers during his rule, Leontius appears to have prioritized military consolidation over theological innovation, reflecting a pragmatic approach amid ongoing Arab threats. Primary sources like Theophanes the Confessor's Chronographia focus on Leontius' political and martial failures, omitting ecclesiastical initiatives, which suggests religion played a subordinate role in his governance. This stability delayed any Monothelite resurgence until the Armenian emperor Philippicus Bardanes (r. 711713), highlighting Leontius' alignment with prevailing imperial orthodoxy.

Deposition and Immediate Aftermath

Rebellion Led by Tiberius III

In 697, amid the ongoing Arab conquest of Byzantine North Africa, Emperor Leontius dispatched a naval expedition under John the Patrician to recapture Carthage, which had fallen to Umayyad forces led by Hasan ibn al-Nu'man. The campaign ended in catastrophe, as Arab reinforcements decisively defeated the Byzantine fleet, capturing John and compelling the survivors to retreat toward Crete.
Anticipating severe punishment from Leontius for the debacle, the disgruntled sailors and officers mutinied, executing John the Patrician to preempt reprisals. They then acclaimed Apsimar, the drungarios (commander) of the fleeta mid-ranking Isaurian or Germanic officeras emperor, granting him the throne name Tiberius III to evoke legitimacy from past rulers. This rebellion, rooted in military failure and fear of imperial retribution rather than ideological opposition, marked the inception of the Twenty Years' Anarchy's internal power struggles.
Tiberius III consolidated support among the fleet, rallying approximately 1,200 ships and troops disillusioned with Leontius's Arab war setbacks. Sailing directly to Constantinople, the rebels blockaded the city and leveraged their naval dominance to pressure the capital's defenders, who faced depleted resources after recent defeats. The siege concluded swiftly in late 698, with Constantinople's populace and garrison yielding to avoid prolonged starvation and bombardment; Leontius surrendered without a pitched battle.

Surrender of Constantinople

In 698, following the failure of the Byzantine expedition to retake Carthage from Arab forces, naval officers under the command of John the Patrician revolted while wintering in Crete, proclaiming the drungary of the Cibyrrhaeot fleet, Apsimar, as emperor under the regnal name Tiberius III. Tiberius assembled a fleet and sailed for Constantinople, initiating a siege against the capital then under Leontius' control.
The city, already weakened by a outbreak of plague, faced several months of blockade and pressure from the rebel forces. Supporters of the Green faction within Constantinople betrayed the defenders by opening the gates, enabling Tiberius' troops to enter and seize control. This internal treachery facilitated the swift capitulation, marking the effective surrender of the city without prolonged street fighting.
Leontius was captured during the takeover, subjected to mutilation by having his nose slita traditional Byzantine punishment symbolizing depositionand confined to the monastery of Psamathion. Tiberius III thus consolidated his rule, sparing Leontius' life at that juncture but ending his three-year reign amid widespread discontent over military reverses.

Final Years and Execution

Imprisonment Under Justinian II's Restoration

Following his restoration, Justinian II entered Constantinople on 21 August 705 after besieging the city with Bulgar assistance and deposing Tiberius III. Leontius, who had been tonsured and confined to the Psamathion monastery near the capital since his own overthrow in 698, was soon located by imperial agents. He was seized, placed in chains, and conveyed to Constantinople for detention under Justinian's authority.
Leontius remained imprisoned in the city for several months, held alongside Tiberius III as Justinian consolidated power and exacted retribution against rivals. This period of captivity, spanning from late 705 to early 706, reflected Justinian's policy of detaining former emperors prior to public reckoning, as recorded in Byzantine chronicles. Primary accounts by Theophanes Confessor and Patriarch Nikephoros describe the conditions as punitive, with Leontius awaiting judgment amid heightened security in the imperial prison system. No records indicate formal charges during this interlude, emphasizing the detention's role as a prelude to ritual humiliation rather than judicial process.
The imprisonment underscored Justinian's vengeful approach, informed by his own prior mutilation and exile under Leontius, though chroniclers note the emperor's restraint in delaying execution to affirm legitimacy before the populace. Leontius, already disfigured from his deposition, endured this confinement without reported resistance or appeals, consistent with monastic tonsure's disqualifying effect on imperial claims.

Trial and Death

Following the successful restoration of Justinian II to the Byzantine throne in December 705, Leontius was located in the monastery of Psamathion near Constantinople, where he had been confined since his deposition by Tiberius III in 698. Justinian ordered his arrest and imprisonment alongside Tiberius III, whom he had captured earlier while the former emperor attempted to flee to Bithynia.
On February 15, 706, Leontius and Tiberius III were paraded in chains through the streets of Constantinople to the Hippodrome, where Justinian II awaited them on a platform. The two deposed emperors were compelled to prostrate themselves at the feet of the restored ruler, whose feet rested upon stone blocks inscribed with their names as a symbolic act of dominance and public degradation before the assembled populace. No formal judicial trial is recorded in contemporary accounts; the proceedings served primarily as a vengeful spectacle to legitimize Justinian's rule and deter potential usurpers.
After the humiliation in the Hippodrome, Leontius and Tiberius were conveyed to the nearby Kynegion amphitheater and beheaded by executioners. Their bodies were initially discarded into the sea but were subsequently retrieved by sympathetic monks and buried in an honorable monastic tomb. This execution, drawn from chronicles such as that of Theophanes the Confessor, underscores Justinian's ruthless consolidation of power amid the instability of the Twenty Years' Anarchy, though Byzantine sources like Theophanes exhibit a critical bias against Justinian's perceived tyranny.

Legacy and Historical Evaluation

Assessments in Byzantine Sources

The principal Byzantine assessments of Leontius derive from the Chronographia of Theophanes the Confessor (d. 818) and the Breviarium of Patriarch Nikephoros I (d. 828), both of whom relied on earlier, now-lost annals and military dispatches for the late seventh century. These sources present Leontius as a seasoned Isaurian strategos whose usurpation in November 695 capitalized on widespread revulsion toward Justinian II's fiscal exactions and purges, with the Hippodrome factions and Opsikion troops acclaiming him after storming the palace. Theophanes explicitly notes Leontius' restraint in mutilating Justinian's nose and tonguestandard disqualification from rulewhile sparing his life "out of love for his father Constantine," reflecting a portrayal of Leontius as pragmatically merciful rather than vengeful.
Theophanes frames Leontius' three-year tenure (AM 61866188) as a period of escalating military calamity, including the dispatch of a fleet under John the Patrician that failed to retake Arab-held Carthage in 696/7, resulting in the permanent loss of the Exarchate of Africa, and a major eastern campaign in 697/8 that ended in defeat near Sebaste (Armenia) against Maslama ibn Abd al-Malik, with Leontius himself fleeing the field. These reverses, Theophanes implies, stemmed from strategic overreach and internal discord, culminating in the Opsikion fleet's rebellion under Apsimar (Tiberius III) in 698, who besieged and captured Constantinople after Leontius' failed defense. Nikephoros echoes this sequence in his concise history, attributing Leontius' pre-usurpation imprisonment (692695) to the Sebastopolis debaclewhere 20,000 troops, including 12,000 Slavs, deserted to the Arabsand similarly underscores the reign's brevity amid Arab incursions that reduced imperial revenues and territory.
Neither chronicler lavishes praise on Leontius' administrative or religious initiatives, such as convening the Quinisext (Trullan) Council in 692 to codify disciplinary canons, which Theophanes records factually but notes alienated Rome over clauses on clerical celibacy and icons. Theophanes, an iconophile writing amid iconoclastic strife, integrates Leontius into a providential narrative of Heraclian decline, where defeats signal divine disfavor for prior sins like Justinian's tyranny, though Leontius escapes the vituperation directed at mutilated or heretical rulers. Nikephoros, more secular in tone, focuses on factual chronology, portraying Leontius as a link in the chain of short-lived usurpers (695711) that stabilized only under Leo III. Later compilers like John Skylitzes (10th century) largely reproduce these views without embellishment, viewing Leontius' era as emblematic of thematic army volatility and Arab pressure eroding central authority.

Modern Scholarly Interpretations

Modern historians characterize Leontios' brief rule (695698) as inaugurating the "Twenty Years' Anarchy," a phase of recurrent coups and thematic army revolts amid escalating Arab invasions and territorial losses, including the fall of Carthage in 697698. Warren Treadgold interprets his accession as a popular uprising against Justinian II's heavy taxation and perceived mismanagement, driven by the Blues faction and opportunistic military elements, rather than a purely aristocratic plot. John Haldon views Leontios' military campaigns, such as the failed Anatolian expeditions against the Umayyads, as symptomatic of the empire's structural vulnerabilities, including overreliance on Slavic mercenaries who defected en masse at Sebastopolis in 692, exacerbating the breakdown of central command. These analyses emphasize causal factors like fiscal exhaustion from constant warfareByzantine revenues had declined by approximately 30% since the 660sand the empowerment of provincial generals, who leveraged battlefield prestige for imperial bids.
Scholars debate Leontios' religious stance, with some primary sources linking him to monothelitism due to his continuity of Justinian II's policies, though the Quinisext Council (692), convened under his auspices, affirmed Chalcedonian dyothelitism by endorsing two energies and wills in Christ, anathematizing earlier compromises. Treadgold argues this council represented an attempt to consolidate eastern ecclesiastical authority independently of Rome, rejecting papal canons on clerical marriage and icon veneration, which foreshadowed East-West schisms but stabilized internal orthodoxy temporarily. Haldon cautions against overemphasizing theological motivations, attributing Leontios' support for the council to pragmatic alliance-building with monastic factions amid military crises, rather than doctrinal zeal, as evidenced by the absence of monothelite endorsements in conciliar acts. Recent numismatic studies, analyzing over 500 solidi from his mints, reveal consistent weight standards (4.5 grams gold) and iconography blending Heraclian busts with stepped crosses, indicating administrative continuity despite political flux, per analyses by Philip Grierson.
Overall, Leontios is appraised as a competent but unlucky interim rulera skilled Isaurian strategos whose prior successes (e.g., Armenian campaigns under Constantine IV) could not avert systemic collapse. Historians like Treadgold estimate his reign reduced active field armies by 10,00015,000 through attrition, accelerating the shift to defensive themata, while crediting him with averting total Arab overrunning of Anatolia via opportunistic raids. This evaluation contrasts with Byzantine chroniclers' moralistic condemnations, prioritizing empirical metrics of territorial retention (e.g., holding core themes intact until 698) over hagiographic biases in sources like Theophanes, whose anti-Heraclian slant modern critics attribute to ninth-century Isaurian dynasty propaganda.

Numismatic and Artistic Representations

!
The primary numismatic representations of Leontius, Byzantine emperor from 695 to 698, are found on gold solidi minted in Constantinople, weighing approximately 4.5 grams and maintaining the standard Heraclian weight and purity of about 95% gold. These coins feature on the obverse a facing bust of Leontius, depicted as a bearded man wearing a crown with pendants, loros (imperial robe), and holding an akakia (staff) in his right hand and a globus cruciger (orb with cross) in his left, inscribed with legends such as D LEO N PE AV (Dominus Noster Leontius Perpetuus Augustus). The reverse typically displays a cross potent on three steps, sometimes accompanied by a figure of Victory standing left holding a wreath and globus cruciger, with CONOB (Constantinople Obryzum, indicating the mint and purity) in the exergue.
Leontius also issued bronze denominations, including decanummia from Constantinople dated to regnal year 1 (695/6), showing a crowned bust facing in loros holding a globus cruciger on the obverse and a large numeral I with a cross above on the reverse. These coins reflect continuity in Byzantine iconography from the preceding Heraclian dynasty, emphasizing imperial authority through standardized portraiture and Christian symbolism, though Leontius' brief reign limited the variety and volume of issues.
Beyond numismatics, no surviving contemporary artistic representations of Leontius, such as mosaics, frescoes, or sculptures, are known, likely owing to the iconoclastic tendencies emerging in the late 7th century and the political instability of his usurpation, which curtailed monumental imperial art production focused on him. His coin portraits thus serve as the chief visual record, portraying a militaristic ruler consistent with his Isaurian military background, without the individualized naturalism seen in later Byzantine portraiture.
