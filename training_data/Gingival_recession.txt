Gingival recession
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Gingival recession, commonly referred to as receding gums, is a dental condition in which the gum tissue surrounding the teeth pulls away from the tooth surface, exposing the roots and potentially leading to increased tooth sensitivity, higher risk of decay, and aesthetic concerns. This progressive loss of gingival tissue is often a result of periodontal changes and can affect individuals of any age, though it becomes more prevalent with age, impacting approximately 88% of people over 65. While it cannot be fully reversed, early detection and management can halt its progression and prevent complications such as bone loss or tooth mobility.
The primary causes of gingival recession include chronic periodontal disease, which leads to inflammation and destruction of supporting tissues, as well as mechanical factors like aggressive toothbrushing or trauma from oral piercings. Other contributing factors encompass plaque and tartar buildup, smoking, genetic predispositions, and anatomical issues such as thin gingival biotype or abnormal tooth positioning. Predisposing elements like alveolar bone dehiscence or high frenal attachments can exacerbate the condition, particularly in cases influenced by orthodontic movements that may thin buccal bone. Periodontitis, a severe form of gum infection, often initiates the process by forming deep pockets between gums and teeth, resulting in tissue detachment and recession over time.
Symptoms of gingival recession typically manifest as heightened sensitivity to hot, cold, or sweet stimuli due to exposed dentin, along with visible lengthening of teeth and possible discomfort during brushing or flossing. In advanced stages, it may cause pain along the gum line, bad breath, or even loose teeth if bone support is compromised. The condition can present in localized forms, such as V-shaped defects from trauma, or generalized horizontal retraction linked to widespread periodontal disease.
Treatment approaches for gingival recession range from non-surgical interventions like improved oral hygiene, scaling and root planing to remove plaque, antimicrobial rinses, professional application of fluoride varnishes and desensitizing agents to alleviate dentin hypersensitivity, composite resin bonding to camouflage exposed roots and make them less noticeable and more comfortable, and hyaluronic acid gels as adjuncts to promote tissue regeneration and reduce inflammation, to surgical options such as gum grafting to cover exposed roots. Composite resin bonding is a minimally invasive non-surgical option suitable for mild cases, providing immediate coverage of exposed roots to reduce sensitivity and improve aesthetics. Gum grafting is considered the most predictable and long-lasting treatment, particularly for moderate to severe recession, as it restores natural gum tissue and offers better long-term protection against further recession and decay. Orthodontic therapy can play a preventive role by correcting tooth alignment and enhancing gingival thickness, though it requires careful monitoring to avoid worsening recession in susceptible individuals. Preventive measures, including gentle brushing techniques and regular dental check-ups, are essential to mitigate risk factors and maintain gingival health.

Overview

Definition and Anatomy

Gingival recession is defined as the displacement of the gingival margin in an apical direction relative to the cementoenamel junction, resulting in exposure of the root surface and denudation of the underlying cementum or dentin. This condition exposes previously protected tooth structures, potentially leading to sensitivity and aesthetic concerns due to the loss of soft tissue coverage.
The normal gingiva consists of several key components that provide protection and support to the teeth. The free gingiva forms a collar-like band of tissue around the neck of each tooth, extending from the gingival margin to the base of the gingival sulcus, which is typically 1 to 3 mm deep and lined by the junctional epithelium acting as a barrier against microbial invasion. Adjacent to the free gingiva is the attached gingiva, a firm, immobile band of keratinized mucosa that adheres to the underlying periosteum and extends to the mucogingival junction, where it transitions to the more flexible, non-keratinized alveolar mucosa. The interdental papilla, a triangular projection of gingiva between adjacent teeth, fills the embrasures and helps prevent food impaction while maintaining seal integrity. Keratinized gingiva, including the attached portion, features a stratified squamous epithelium with a protective keratin layer, contrasting with the non-keratinized alveolar mucosa, which lacks this tough surface and is more susceptible to trauma.
Supporting the gingiva are essential periodontal structures that maintain its position and overall stability. The periodontal ligament (PDL) is a connective tissue layer that anchors the tooth's cementum to the alveolar bone, absorbing occlusal forces and facilitating gingival attachment through its collagen fibers. Alveolar bone, the specialized bone surrounding the tooth roots, provides the structural foundation for gingival support by housing the PDL and contributing to the crestal height that underlies the attached gingiva. Cementum, a mineralized layer covering the root surface, serves as the attachment site for PDL fibers and protects the dentin beneath, ensuring the integrity of the gingival margin's position relative to the crown.
Gingival recession primarily manifests as marginal recession, the most common form, where the gingival margin shifts apically at the tooth's faciolingual or lingual surfaces, exposing the root without necessarily involving interproximal areas. In contrast, generalized recession affects multiple teeth across the dentition, often involving both marginal and interproximal sites, leading to widespread root exposure.

Epidemiology

Gingival recession is a widespread oral health condition, affecting a substantial portion of the global population. A systematic review and meta-analysis of 15 studies involving 37,460 participants estimated the overall pooled prevalence at 84.92% for at least 1 mm of root surface exposure (as of 2022), with high heterogeneity across studies indicating variations in measurement thresholds and populations. A more recent 2025 meta-analysis of 22 studies reported a pooled prevalence of 81.1% (95% CI: 73.986.7%) for 1 mm recession. This high prevalence underscores the condition's significant public health impact, as it exposes root surfaces to potential sensitivity, caries, and aesthetic concerns.
Prevalence increases with age, becoming particularly common in adults over 40 years, where up to 88% of individuals aged 65 and older exhibit at least one site with recession of 1 mm or more. In contrast, approximately 50% of adults aged 18 to 64 show similar exposure. Males experience higher rates than females, particularly after age 30, often attributed to behavioral factors such as more aggressive toothbrushing and higher tobacco use.
Regional variations in prevalence are notable, with higher incidence observed in populations from developing countries, where poor oral hygiene practices and elevated tobacco consumption contribute to greater exposure rates compared to developed regions. For instance, studies report prevalence ranging from 22% to 100% worldwide, with elevated figures in areas like parts of Asia and Africa linked to socioeconomic factors affecting oral health access.
Gingival recession is closely linked to the broader prevalence of periodontal diseases, serving as a common clinical manifestation in affected individuals without implying direct causality in this context. Systematic analyses confirm its association with plaque accumulation and gingival inflammation, which are hallmarks of periodontal conditions globally.

Etiology

Predisposing Factors

Gingival recession susceptibility is influenced by genetic and hereditary factors, particularly through variations in gingival biotype and familial patterns of periodontal disease. Individuals with a thin gingival biotype, characterized by delicate, translucent tissues and a highly scalloped architecture, exhibit increased vulnerability to recession due to reduced tissue thickness and resilience against inflammatory or mechanical stresses. This biotype often correlates with genetic predispositions that impair collagen synthesis or immune responses, leading to higher rates of attachment loss. Additionally, a family history of aggressive periodontitis elevates risk, as inherited traits such as exaggerated inflammatory responses to plaque bacteria contribute to chronic gingival breakdown.
Anatomical predispositions further heighten the risk by creating structural vulnerabilities in the periodontium. Aberrant frenum attachments, such as a high or tight labial frenum, exert tensile forces on the gingival margin, promoting apical displacement of tissues over time. A shallow vestibular depth or inadequate width of attached gingiva limits the protective mucosal barrier, facilitating recession progression. Alveolar bone defects, including dehiscences (vertical bone loss exposing root surfaces) and fenestrations (window-like openings in the bone), serve as inherent weaknesses that predispose overlying gingiva to recession, especially in areas of thin bone coverage.
Systemic conditions that compromise connective tissue integrity are significant predisposing elements. Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, particularly the periodontal type (pEDS), features genetic mutations affecting collagen formation, resulting in fragile gingiva prone to early recession and lack of attached tissue. Diabetes mellitus exacerbates susceptibility by impairing wound healing and promoting hyperglycemia-induced inflammation, which accelerates periodontal tissue destruction and recession in poorly controlled cases.
Age-related changes contribute to baseline vulnerability through progressive gingival atrophy. As individuals age, natural remodeling and cumulative wear lead to thinning of the gingival epithelium and connective tissue, increasing recession prevalenceobserved in approximately 88% of those over 65. This atrophy reduces the supportive framework around teeth, establishing a foundation for further recession without external trauma.

Precipitating Factors

Periodontal diseases represent a primary precipitating factor for gingival recession, as plaque-induced inflammation from gingivitis or periodontitis leads to progressive loss of the periodontal attachment apparatus, including the gingival margin. In chronic periodontitis, bacterial accumulation erodes the supporting tissues, resulting in apical migration of the junctional epithelium and exposure of root surfaces.
Traumatic oral habits, such as overaggressive toothbrushing with a horizontal scrubbing technique, cause mechanical abrasion of the gingival tissues, particularly in areas with thin biotypes, leading to denudation of the root. Improper flossing techniques that traumatize the interdental papillae or the presence of oral piercings, like lip or tongue studs, can similarly induce localized recession through repeated friction and pressure on the marginal gingiva. These habits often exacerbate recession in sites already vulnerable due to predisposing anatomical factors, such as prominent roots.
Tobacco smoking and chewing impair gingival healing by reducing blood flow and promoting vasoconstriction, which facilitates attachment loss and increases the severity of recession in affected individuals. Smokeless tobacco, in particular, causes direct chemical irritation and nicotine-induced fibrosis, contributing to localized gingival retraction around the site of placement.
Iatrogenic factors from dental interventions, including orthodontic tooth movement that applies excessive forces or positions teeth beyond the alveolar housing, can precipitate recession by stretching or fenestrating the overlying gingiva. Restorative procedures, such as ill-fitting crowns or subgingival margins that induce chronic inflammation, further promote marginal tissue breakdown and apical shift. Additionally, severe nutritional deficiencies like scurvy, resulting from vitamin C inadequacy, weaken collagen synthesis in periodontal ligaments and gingival connective tissue, leading to hemorrhagic and recessional changes.

Clinical Presentation

Symptoms and Signs

Gingival recession manifests clinically through the apical displacement of the marginal gingiva, exposing the root surface and resulting in an elongated appearance of the affected teeth. This visible sign often presents as a V- or U-shaped defect, with root exposure that may appear yellowish or darker than the enamel-covered crown, altering the natural gingival contour. Interdental spaces may widen due to recession of the gingival papillae, creating noticeable "black triangles" between teeth, particularly in anterior regions.
Patients frequently report dentin hypersensitivity as a primary symptom, characterized by sharp, transient pain triggered by thermal stimuli (such as cold air or hot foods), chemical agents (like acidic or sweet substances), or tactile contact (during brushing or flossing). Tooth sensitivity extends to general discomfort near the gum line, exacerbated by oral hygiene practices or eating. Aesthetic concerns are common, especially when recession affects visible anterior teeth, leading to self-consciousness about the uneven smile line or exposed roots.
The condition typically progresses gradually and may remain asymptomatic in early stages, with patients unaware until root exposure becomes pronounced. In advanced cases, tooth mobility can emerge as supporting bone and periodontal structures are compromised. Gingival inflammation or bleeding often accompanies recession due to plaque retention on exposed roots, facilitating bacterial accumulation.
Complications arising from these presentations include an elevated risk of root caries, as the demineralized cementum is more susceptible to decay than enamel, with prevalence rates reaching up to 42% in older adults. Halitosis may develop from bacterial overgrowth on exposed roots or secondary infections in the gingival sulcus.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis of gingival recession primarily relies on clinical examination to identify and quantify the extent of gingival tissue loss. During a routine dental visit, the clinician performs a visual inspection of the gingival tissues to detect apical displacement of the gingival margin, exposing the root surface. This is followed by tactile assessment using a calibrated periodontal probe to measure key parameters. The recession depth is determined as the vertical distance from the cementoenamel junction (CEJ) to the gingival margin, recorded to the nearest millimeter at multiple sites around the tooth, typically the mid-buccal and mid-lingual aspects. Probing is conducted with gentle force (approximately 0.20-0.25 N) to avoid tissue trauma, though inflammation may lead to slight overestimation due to probe penetration into the junctional epithelium.
Additional measurements include recession width, assessed at the widest horizontal extent of the defect, and the probing attachment level (PAL), calculated as the sum of probing depth and recession depth to evaluate cumulative periodontal tissue loss. These metrics provide a comprehensive assessment of the defect's dimensions and severity. In this context, the Miller classification may be applied to gauge the interproximal bone loss and root coverage potential, categorizing recessions into classes I-IV based on clinical features observed during probing, though full classification details are beyond initial diagnostic evaluation. Indices such as bleeding on probing (BOP) are also recorded, with >30% BOP indicating potential active disease progression associated with recession. Reproducibility of these measurements is generally high, with 95% agreement within 1 mm among examiners.
Adjunctive diagnostics complement clinical findings when recession suggests underlying pathology. Periapical or panoramic radiographs are used to evaluate alveolar bone loss, identifying vertical defects or fenestrations that may contribute to recession etiology; these are indicated if probing reveals attachment loss exceeding 4 mm. Pulp vitality testing, such as electric pulp testing or thermal methods, is performed if root exposure raises concern for pulpal involvement, particularly in cases with dentin hypersensitivity or suspected endodontic compromise, as periodontal disease can alter pulpal responses. Advanced imaging like cone-beam computed tomography (CBCT) may be employed for precise CEJ localization in complex cases, though it is reserved due to radiation exposure.
Differential diagnosis involves distinguishing true gingival recession from other conditions presenting with root exposure or tissue alterations. Gingival abrasion, often from aggressive toothbrushing, manifests as localized V-shaped notches on the root without gingival margin displacement, unlike the broader apical shift in recession. Erosion, caused by chemical dissolution from acidic exposures, typically affects enamel and dentin surfaces smoothly without associated gingival loss. Physiological aging-related changes present as gradual, symmetric recession without inflammation or trauma history, contrasting with pathological recession linked to periodontal disease or mechanical factors. Clinical history, lesion morphology, and response to probing help differentiate these, ensuring accurate identification of recession-specific pathology.

Classification

Miller's Classification

Miller's classification system for gingival recession, introduced in 1985 by periodontist Peter D. Miller Jr., provides a framework for categorizing marginal tissue recession based on its extent relative to the mucogingival junction (MGJ) and the presence of interdental bone or soft tissue loss, aiding in periodontal surgery planning and prognosis assessment. This system divides recessions into four classes, emphasizing clinical evaluation to predict the potential for complete root coverage during treatment.
The classification is defined as follows:

Class I: Marginal tissue recession that does not extend to or beyond the MGJ, with no loss of bone or soft tissue in the interdental area; this allows for straightforward clinical distinction without radiographic confirmation.

Class II: Marginal tissue recession that extends to or beyond the MGJ, but with no loss of bone or soft tissue in the interdental area.

Class III: Marginal tissue recession that extends to or beyond the MGJ, with loss of bone or soft tissue in the interdental area, but where the interdental recession does not reach the MGJ (partial interdental fill remains).

Class IV: Marginal tissue recession that extends to or beyond the MGJ, with severe loss of bone and soft tissue in the interdental area, where the interdental recession fully reaches or exceeds the MGJ (no interdental fill); this represents the most advanced form with extensive attachment loss.

One key advantage of Miller's system is its simplicity, relying primarily on visible clinical landmarks like the MGJ and interdental papilla height, which facilitates quick categorization during examinations. Additionally, it offers prognostic value for surgical outcomes: complete root coverage is anticipated in Classes I and II due to intact interdental support, partial coverage in Class III, and minimal to no coverage in Class IV.
Despite its widespread adoption, the classification has notable limitations, including its inability to accommodate recessions on the palatal surfaces of maxillary teeth, where no distinct MGJ exists, and its exclusive focus on clinical extent without quantifying interdental bone loss depth. It also fails to classify certain cases, such as recessions not extending beyond the MGJ but accompanied by interdental bone loss, rendering the system non-exhaustive for all recession morphologies.

Modified Systems

In response to limitations in the original Miller's classification, such as its focus primarily on marginal tissue recession without adequate consideration for interdental attachment loss or palatal involvement, subsequent modifications have been developed to enhance clinical applicability and prognostic accuracy. One notable system is the Kumar and Masamatti classification proposed in 2013, which expands the framework to encompass both buccal/lingual and palatal recessions by categorizing them based on the position of the interdental papilla relative to the cemento-enamel junction (CEJ) and the extent of soft tissue loss. This system divides recessions into three main classes for buccal/lingual sites (reflecting no interdental loss in Class I, partial interdental loss in Class II, and more severe interdental loss in Class III) with subclasses (A/B denoting gingival margin position relative to the MGJ and presence of attached gingiva, varying by class), and introduces a parallel PR (palatal recession) scheme with similar I-III classes where A/B subclasses are based on recession depth (3 mm or >3 mm from CEJ). By incorporating multi-tooth and palatal recessionsareas not addressed in earlier systemsthis classification facilitates more comprehensive assessment for treatment planning, particularly in complex cases involving lingual surfaces.
Building on similar principles, the Cairo et al. classification from 2011 introduces recession types (RT1, RT2, RT3) specifically tailored to predict root coverage outcomes during grafting procedures, determined by the interproximal clinical attachment level relative to the buccal recession. RT1 denotes recession without interproximal attachment loss (most favorable for complete root coverage), RT2 indicates interproximal loss at the same level as the buccal defect (partial coverage expected), and RT3 signifies interproximal loss extending beyond the buccal defect (least predictable). This system improves upon predecessors by emphasizing vertical components of recession and interdental bone loss, enabling clinicians to better forecast surgical success rates, with studies showing RT1 defects achieving up to 100% root coverage in many cases compared to lower probabilities in RT3.
Further refinements appear in the 2018 World Workshop on the Classification of Periodontal and Peri-Implant Diseases and Conditions, which integrates Cairo's RT grading with additional parameters like gingival phenotype (thick or thin, influencing recession progression and treatment response) and root step (grade 0: no step; 1: non-carious step <1 mm deep; 2: non-carious step 1 mm deep or with caries/restoration). These inclusions address both vertical and horizontal dimensions of recession, providing a multidimensional tool that outperforms the original Miller system in prognostic precision for grafting outcomesevidenced by higher inter-examiner reliability and correlation with long-term stability in clinical trials. The 2018 classification, endorsed by the American Academy of Periodontology, remains the current standard for routine diagnostic use as of 2024.

Management

Prevention

Preventing gingival recession involves adopting modifiable behaviors and early interventions that address risk factors such as poor oral hygiene and traumatic habits, which can exacerbate underlying predisposing elements like thin gingival biotype.
Gingival recession cannot be reversed with home remedies; receded gums do not grow back naturally, and professional dental treatment such as gum grafting is often required for restoration. Home practices can help prevent further recession, reduce inflammation, and support oral health, but are supportive measures only. Consult a dentist for proper diagnosis and treatment, as untreated recession can lead to tooth loss.
Effective oral hygiene practices are foundational to minimizing gingival recession. Brushing twice daily with a soft or ultra-soft toothbrush and low-abrasive fluoride toothpaste reduces plaque accumulation and mechanical trauma to the gingival tissues, thereby preventing inflammation and recession progression. Additional supportive home practices include saltwater rinses (1 tsp salt in warm water, rinse 2-3 times daily), oil pulling with coconut oil (swish 20 minutes daily), applying aloe vera gel to gums, and drinking green tea. These practices are supportive only and not curative. Daily interdental cleaning using floss or interdental brushes removes plaque from areas inaccessible to brushing, further supporting gingival health. Regular professional cleanings every 3 to 6 months help maintain these practices and detect early signs of recession, as evidenced by longitudinal studies showing slower recession progression in individuals with consistent oral hygiene.
Lifestyle modifications play a critical role in prevention. Smoking cessation is essential, as tobacco use impairs gingival blood flow and healing, directly contributing to recession; quitting restores periodontal health and reduces incidence. A balanced diet supports periodontal health by aiding collagen synthesis and reducing oxidative stress in gingival tissues. Avoiding aggressive habits, such as oral piercings or excessive fingernail biting, prevents direct trauma to the gingiva.
Early orthodontic and periodontal care can mitigate recession in susceptible individuals. Frenectomy is recommended for aberrant frenal attachments that pull on the gingival margin, preventing recession development by eliminating muscle tension. In patients with thin gingival biotype, monitoring during orthodontic treatment and timely interventions like frenectomy help preserve attachment levels.
Public health measures emphasize education on these preventive strategies to reduce recession incidence population-wide. Longitudinal studies demonstrate that community programs promoting gentle oral hygiene and smoking cessation lower periodontal disease prevalence, including recession.

Non-Surgical Approaches

Non-surgical approaches to gingival recession primarily aim to address underlying etiologic factors, alleviate symptoms such as dentin hypersensitivity, and stabilize or partially reverse mild defects without invasive procedures. These methods are particularly suitable for early-stage recession, where progression can be halted through conservative interventions focused on oral hygiene optimization and inflammation control. By targeting plaque accumulation, traumatic habits, and associated irritants, non-surgical management can reduce gingival inflammation and promote tissue stability, often serving as the first-line treatment for Miller Class I defects.
Behavior modification plays a central role in non-surgical management, emphasizing the adoption of gentle brushing techniques to minimize further tissue trauma. The modified Bass technique, which involves placing the toothbrush at a 45-degree angle to the gingival margin with short, light strokes, effectively removes supragingival and subgingival plaque while avoiding abrasion of exposed roots. Patients are instructed to use soft or ultra-soft bristles and low-abrasive toothpastes to prevent exacerbation of recession. 
Pharmacological options complement hygiene practices by controlling inflammation and addressing hypersensitivity. Chlorhexidine gluconate rinses (0.12% concentration, used twice daily for up to two weeks) reduce plaque buildup and gingival bleeding, thereby mitigating inflammatory responses that contribute to recession progression. For symptom relief, desensitizing agents such as potassium nitrate toothpastes or fluoride varnishes are applied to occlude dentinal tubules and promote remineralization, often providing noticeable reduction in sensitivity within weeks. Professional dental products applied by dentists include fluoride varnishes to protect exposed roots and reduce dentin hypersensitivity, desensitizers to block nerve transmission and alleviate sensitivity, and hyaluronic acid gels to promote tissue regeneration, reduce inflammation, and improve healing outcomes, often as adjuncts to non-surgical or surgical periodontal care. 
Removal of subgingival irritants through scaling and root planing is a key non-surgical intervention, involving meticulous debridement to eliminate plaque and calculus from root surfaces. This procedure smooths irregular root contours, facilitating reattachment of gingival fibers and reducing pocket depths associated with recession. 
Adjunctive therapies include orthodontic correction for malpositioned teeth, which can reposition roots within the alveolar bone and allow for coronal migration of the gingival margin. In young patients, monitoring may reveal spontaneous partial root coverage, as alveolar bone growth can naturally reduce recession depth over time. 
In some dental practices, particularly in Russia and Eastern Europe, physiotherapeutic methods are employed as adjunctive treatments for gingival recession. These include gingival massage, laser therapy, electrophoresis, UV therapy, electrotherapy, and vacuum therapy, which aim to improve blood circulation, reduce inflammation, strengthen tissues, and promote regeneration. A scientific study in rats has shown that gingival massage enhances microcirculation in aging gums by increasing blood flow and blood vessel density. However, these physiotherapeutic methods are not primary or curative treatments; standard non-surgical approaches focus on oral hygiene, scaling and root planing, antimicrobial rinses, fluoride varnishes, desensitizing agents, and hyaluronic acid gels, with surgery recommended for severe cases.
These approaches are indicated for Miller Class I recessions, where no loss of interdental bone support exists, and are effective in stabilizing defects through combined hygiene and debridement efforts. Outcomes are enhanced when patient compliance is high, though complete root coverage is less predictable compared to surgical methods, emphasizing the need for regular follow-up to monitor progression. 

Surgical Techniques

Surgical techniques for gingival recession aim to achieve root coverage, augment keratinized tissue, and restore periodontal health through invasive procedures, particularly for defects classified as Miller Class II to IV where non-surgical methods are insufficient. These approaches include pedicle grafts utilizing adjacent vascularized tissue and free grafts harvested from donor sites, often combined with flaps for optimal outcomes. Alternatives such as acellular collagen matrices are emerging as substitutes to autografts, offering reduced donor site morbidity while achieving comparable root coverage (70-80% in recent studies as of 2025).
Gingival grafting procedures are foundational, with the free gingival graft (FGG) involving harvest of epithelium and underlying connective tissue, typically from the palate, to augment keratinized tissue in areas of recession. Indicated for Miller Class II defects with inadequate attached gingiva, the FGG is placed over a prepared recipient site following root planing and secured with sutures, providing 40-70% root coverage in Class I and II cases. The connective tissue graft (CTG), specifically the subepithelial variant, is considered the gold standard for root coverage, harvesting de-epithelialized connective tissue from the palate and positioning it beneath a coronally advanced flap for dual blood supply and enhanced esthetics. This technique yields 80-90% root coverage success rates, with significant gains in clinical attachment and keratinized tissue width at 6-12 months post-surgery.
Pedicle grafts leverage adjacent mucosa for vascularized coverage without donor site morbidity. Rotated or laterally positioned flaps involve elevating a partial- or full-thickness pedicle from neighboring tissue and advancing it over the recession defect, suitable for narrow, isolated Miller Class I or II recessions with sufficient donor keratinized gingiva. These provide predictable root coverage through maintained blood supply, often achieving complete coverage when combined with connective tissue augmentation.
Growth-factor enhanced techniques promote regeneration by stimulating cellular activity. Platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF), combined with beta-tricalcium phosphate (-TCP), is applied to the root surface under a flap, enhancing periodontal ligament reformation and yielding approximately 88% root coverage in multiple adjacent defects at 6 months. Enamel matrix derivatives, such as Emdogain, mimic natural tooth development proteins and are used adjunctively with CTG or coronally advanced flaps to improve angiogenesis and tissue healing, resulting in 86% root coverage compared to 66% without the derivative.
Post-operative care emphasizes wound stabilization and infection prevention, with initial healing occurring over 2-4 weeks through epithelialization and revascularization. Patients are advised to follow a soft diet, avoid trauma to the site, and use prescribed antimicrobials, with potential need for minor touch-up procedures in 10-20% of cases for optimal esthetic results in Class II-IV recessions.

Comparison of Gum Grafting and Composite Bonding

Gum grafting and composite bonding are common treatments for gum recession, which exposes tooth roots leading to sensitivity, aesthetics issues, and risk of decay or further recession.
Gum grafting (surgical procedure using tissue grafts to cover exposed roots):

Pros: Restores natural gum tissue, provides long-term/permanent coverage, reduces sensitivity, protects against further recession and disease, natural appearance.

Cons: Invasive surgery, painful with recovery time (swelling, soft diet for weeks), expensive ($800$2000+ per area), not suitable for all patients, potential complications like bleeding or need for additional procedures.

Composite bonding (non-surgical application of tooth-colored resin to cover roots):

Pros: Minimally invasive, quick (one visit), affordable, immediate results, reduces sensitivity, no downtime or anesthesia needed.

Cons: Temporary (lasts 510 years, requires replacement), does not regenerate gum tissue or address underlying causes, may contribute to plaque buildup or further issues if improperly placed, less effective for severe recession.

Gum grafting is preferred for moderate to severe recession or when structural restoration is needed, while bonding suits mild cases focused on cosmetics/sensitivity or when surgery is avoided. Consult a dentist for personalized advice based on recession severity and oral health.
