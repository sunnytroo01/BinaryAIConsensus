Scarlet fever
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Scarlet fever, also known as scarlatina, is a contagious bacterial infection caused by toxin-producing group A Streptococcus (Streptococcus pyogenes) bacteria, which typically develops as a complication of strep throat and is characterized by a bright red rash, high fever, and sore throat. The rash and other distinctive symptoms result from an erythrogenic toxin released by the bacteria. It most commonly affects children between the ages of 5 and 15, though cases can occur in individuals of any age, and is rare in children younger than 3 years. The infection spreads through respiratory droplets from coughing or sneezing, or via direct contact with infected sores on the skin, with an incubation period of 2 to 5 days.
Early symptoms of scarlet fever often mimic those of strep throat, including a sudden fever of 101F (38.3C) or higher, chills, headache, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and a red, sore throat with swollen tonsils sometimes covered in white or yellow pus. Within 1 to 2 days, a distinctive rash emerges, appearing as fine, red, sandpaper-like bumps that start on the neck, underarms, and groin before spreading across the chest, trunk, arms, and legs; the rash is brighter in skin folds such as the elbows and groin, and there may be a pale ring around the mouth. Other hallmark signs include flushed red cheeks, a "strawberry tongue" (initially coated in white before becoming red and bumpy), and swollen lymph nodes in the neck; tiny red spots (petechiae) may appear on the roof of the mouth. The rash typically fades within 7 days, but skin peeling may occur on the hands, feet, and groin for several weeks afterward.
Diagnosis of scarlet fever involves a physical examination to identify the rash and throat signs, followed by a rapid antigen detection test (RADT) or throat culture to confirm the presence of group A Streptococcus bacteria; if the RADT is negative in children, a follow-up culture is recommended as the gold standard. Treatment consists of a full 10-day course of antibiotics, such as penicillin or amoxicillin, which reduces symptom duration, prevents transmission, and lowers the risk of complications; for those with penicillin allergies, alternatives like cephalexin, clindamycin, or azithromycin are used. Supportive care includes rest, hydration, over-the-counter pain relievers like acetaminophen or ibuprofen for fever and discomfort, and saltwater gargles for throat relief in older children and adults. With prompt antibiotic treatment, most people recover fully within a week, and individuals may return to school or work after 12 to 24 hours of therapy once fever-free.
Although complications are rare with treatment, untreated scarlet fever can lead to suppurative issues like abscesses or peritonsillar infections, or nonsuppurative conditions such as acute rheumatic fever, poststreptococcal glomerulonephritis, or pediatric autoimmune neuropsychiatric disorders associated with streptococcal infections (PANDAS). Prevention focuses on good hygiene practices, including frequent handwashing, covering the mouth and nose when coughing or sneezing, and avoiding close contact with infected individuals; there is currently no vaccine available for scarlet fever. Scarlet fever occurs worldwide but is more prevalent in temperate climates during winter and early spring, with recent surveillance indicating seasonal increases in cases, including notable rises in group A Streptococcus infections among children in Europe and other regions post-2022.

Clinical presentation

Initial symptoms

Scarlet fever typically follows an incubation period of 2 to 5 days after exposure to group A Streptococcus (Streptococcus pyogenes).
The disease presents with a sudden onset of high fever, usually ranging from 38C to 40C, accompanied by chills and general malaise, which often develop within 1 to 2 days following the incubation period. A prominent sore throat due to pharyngitis is an early hallmark, causing significant difficulty swallowing and frequently associated with headache, nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain. Tender cervical lymphadenopathy, manifesting as swollen and painful lymph nodes in the neck, serves as an important early indicator of streptococcal involvement.
This condition predominantly affects children aged 5 to 15 years, with cases being rare in adults. These systemic symptoms prompt initial medical attention and may precede the emergence of more distinctive features.

Characteristic rash

The characteristic rash of scarlet fever is a fine, blanching, erythematous maculopapular eruption with a distinctive "sandpaper-like" texture due to its rough, pinpoint quality. It typically emerges 24 to 48 hours after the onset of initial symptoms, such as fever and sore throat, and begins on the trunk, neck, chest, underarms, and groin before spreading to the extremities within 1 to 2 days. The rash spares the palms and soles initially and blanches under pressure, distinguishing it from non-blanching eruptions.
Key features include accentuated linear streaks of hyperpigmentation, known as Pastia's lines, in flexural skin folds such as the elbows, armpits, and groin, where the rash appears brighter red. The face often shows diffuse flushing with a notable circumoral pallor, creating a pale ring around the mouth against the otherwise reddened cheeks.
The rash generally fades within 3 to 7 days, transitioning from bright red to a yellowish hue, after which desquamation occurs, starting on the face and trunk before progressing to the extremities, with peeling that can last up to several weeks. This skin manifestation arises from the action of erythrogenic toxins (also called pyrogenic exotoxins A, B, or C), superantigens produced by lysogenized strains of Streptococcus pyogenes that trigger widespread T-cell activation and cytokine release, leading to the diffuse capillary dilation and hypersensitivity reaction responsible for the eruption.

Oral and throat findings

Scarlet fever typically presents with exudative tonsillopharyngitis, characterized by red, swollen tonsils often covered in white or yellowish patches or a pseudomembranous exudate. The pharyngeal mucosa appears beefy red and inflamed, with a swollen uvula, and patients commonly experience severe throat pain and difficulty swallowing. Tender cervical lymphadenopathy accompanies these findings, contributing to the diagnostic picture alongside the characteristic rash and fever.
A hallmark oral finding is the "strawberry tongue," where the tongue initially develops a white or yellowish coating that obscures the papillae. Within 12 days, this coating peels away, revealing enlarged, red, and prominent papillae against a bright red background, resembling the surface of a strawberry. This progression is attributed to the erythrogenic toxin produced by group A Streptococcus, which causes capillary dilation and mucosal hyperemia.
Petechiae may appear on the soft palate and uvula, resulting from toxin-induced capillary fragility and increased vascular permeability in the oral mucosa. The fine, erythematous rash associated with scarlet fever can also involve the oral mucosa, presenting as a blanching, sandpaper-like eruption.
These oral and throat manifestations usually emerge 2448 hours after the onset of initial symptoms, such as sore throat and fever, aiding in clinical differentiation from other pharyngitides.

Complications

Scarlet fever, if untreated, can lead to a range of acute suppurative complications resulting from the local or hematogenous spread of group A Streptococcus (GAS). These include peritonsillar abscess, which involves pus collection near the tonsils leading to severe throat pain and difficulty swallowing; otitis media, an ear infection causing pain and potential hearing issues; sinusitis, inflammation of the sinuses with facial pain and congestion; and bacteremia, where bacteria enter the bloodstream, risking further dissemination to organs like the lungs or heart.
Post-infectious immune-mediated complications arise weeks after the initial infection due to an aberrant immune response to GAS antigens. Acute rheumatic fever (ARF) occurs in approximately 0.3-3% of untreated cases in endemic areas and manifests with carditis (inflammation of the heart valves and muscle, potentially leading to lifelong damage), migratory polyarthritis (painful joint inflammation), and other systemic features like chorea or subcutaneous nodules. Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis (PSGN) presents with hematuria (blood in urine), edema (swelling due to fluid retention), and hypertension from kidney inflammation, typically developing 1-2 weeks after pharyngitis-associated scarlet fever.
Rare but severe outcomes stem from invasive GAS infections complicating scarlet fever, including streptococcal toxic shock syndrome (STSS), characterized by rapid-onset fever, hypotension, and multi-organ failure due to superantigen toxins, and necrotizing fasciitis, a rapidly progressive tissue destruction requiring urgent surgical debridement. These invasive complications have high mortality rates, up to 30-70% for STSS and necrotizing fasciitis, even with aggressive treatment.
Key risk factors for developing complications include delayed or absent antibiotic treatment, which allows bacterial persistence and immune sensitization; young age, particularly in children aged 5-15 years who are most susceptible to GAS infections; and immunocompromised states, such as underlying chronic diseases or immunosuppression, which heighten the risk of invasive disease. Early antibiotic therapy significantly reduces the incidence of these sequelae by eradicating the infection promptly.

Etiology and pathogenesis

Causative organism

Scarlet fever is caused by Streptococcus pyogenes, also known as group A beta-hemolytic Streptococcus (GAS), a Gram-positive coccus that grows in chains and is exclusively pathogenic to humans. This bacterium is a common colonizer of the human throat and skin, capable of causing a range of infections from mild pharyngitis to severe invasive diseases.
Only specific strains of S. pyogenes that produce erythrogenic (pyrogenic) exotoxins, primarily SpeA and SpeC, result in the characteristic rash of scarlet fever. These toxins are encoded by genes carried on temperate bacteriophages integrated into the bacterial genome through lysogeny, enabling toxin production upon phage induction. For instance, the speA gene originates from bacteriophage T12, and its expression in lysogenic strains triggers the erythematous rash by damaging capillary endothelium.
Transmission of scarlet fever occurs primarily through respiratory droplets from infected individuals or direct contact with contaminated surfaces, with asymptomatic carriers playing a significant role in spread. Asymptomatic pharyngeal carriage of GAS affects 5-15% of children, facilitating ongoing community transmission without overt symptoms.
Key virulence factors of S. pyogenes include the surface M protein, which promotes adherence to host cells and evades phagocytosis by mimicking human proteins, and streptolysins O and S, oxygen-labile and oxygen-stable hemolysins, respectively, that lyse host cells and contribute to tissue damage. These factors enhance bacterial survival and invasion, though scarlet fever specifically requires exotoxin production.
Not all S. pyogenes infections lead to scarlet fever; only toxin-producing strains cause the syndrome, accounting for approximately 10% of GAS pharyngitis cases in children.

Pathogenic mechanisms

Scarlet fever arises from pharyngeal colonization by toxin-producing strains of Streptococcus pyogenes (group A Streptococcus, GAS), where the bacteria adhere to the mucosal epithelium of the throat using surface proteins such as M protein and fibronectin-binding proteins. Once established, these strains release erythrogenic toxins, primarily streptococcal pyrogenic exotoxins (SPEs) like SpeA, SpeC, and streptococcal superantigen A (SSA), which are encoded by bacteriophage genes integrated into the bacterial genome. These toxins function as superantigens by binding directly to major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class II molecules on antigen-presenting cells and the T-cell receptor (TCR) -chain outside the peptide-binding groove, leading to non-specific activation of up to 20-30% of T cells rather than the typical 0.01% in conventional antigen presentation.
This massive T-cell activation triggers a cytokine storm, characterized by excessive release of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-), interleukin-1 (IL-1), IL-2, IL-6, and interferon-gamma (IFN-), which drive systemic inflammation, fever, and vasodilation. The cytokines induce capillary dilation and increased vascular permeability, contributing to the characteristic scarlatiniform rash through a delayed-type hypersensitivity reaction in individuals previously sensitized to GAS antigens. Unlike non-toxigenic GAS strains that cause uncomplicated streptococcal pharyngitis without rash, the presence of erythrogenic toxins specifically elicits this diffuse erythema by promoting endothelial cell activation and leukocyte recruitment to the skin.
The toxins disseminate systemically via hematogenous spread from the pharyngeal site or direct extension through lymphatic channels, allowing diffusion to distant tissues including the skin, where they provoke the rash originating on the trunk and spreading peripherally. Host factors modulate disease severity; genetic susceptibility linked to specific human leukocyte antigen (HLA) class II alleles, such as HLA-DQA1*01:02, enhances superantigen binding affinity, increasing T-cell activation and cytokine production in response to SpeA. Impaired immunity, as seen in immunocompromised individuals or those with underlying conditions, exacerbates the inflammatory response, while prior exposure to non-toxigenic GAS may prime the immune system, heightening reactivity to erythrogenic toxins upon reinfection.

Diagnosis

Clinical assessment

Clinical assessment of suspected scarlet fever begins with a thorough history to identify risk factors and symptom timeline. Healthcare providers inquire about recent exposure to individuals with streptococcal infections, such as through close contact in schools or daycare settings, which increases transmission risk particularly among children. Symptoms typically include sudden onset of fever exceeding 38C, sore throat, and malaise, with the rash appearing 24-48 hours after initial signs.
During the physical examination, providers evaluate for signs of group A Streptococcus (GAS) pharyngitis using an adaptation of the Centor criteria, which includes fever, tonsillar exudates, tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy, and absence of cough; scores of 3 or higher suggest a high likelihood of GAS infection. The presence of the characteristic erythematous, sandpaper-like rash starting on the trunk and spreading to extremities, along with strawberry tongueinitially coated and then revealing red, enlarged papillaestrongly supports the diagnosis when combined with pharyngitis. Additional findings such as circumoral pallor and Pastia's lines in skin folds further aid identification.
Suspicion is higher in children aged 5-15 years, where the classic presentation is most common, whereas adults may exhibit atypical features, such as milder or irregularly distributed rash. In children, the combination of pharyngitis and the scarlatiniform rash is strongly suggestive of scarlet fever, offering approximately 90-95% specificity when Centor criteria are met.
Healthcare providers should heighten suspicion during seasonal peaks in winter and spring in temperate climates, when GAS infections are more prevalent. If clinical findings are equivocal, laboratory confirmation via throat swab is recommended.

Laboratory confirmation

Laboratory confirmation of scarlet fever requires microbiological evidence of Group A Streptococcus (GAS) infection, as the disease manifests from toxin-producing strains causing pharyngitis and the characteristic rash. Testing typically begins with throat swabs upon clinical suspicion, focusing on direct detection of the pathogen or its products to verify streptococcal etiology. Supportive laboratory findings from blood analysis further corroborate the inflammatory response.
The rapid antigen detection test (RADT) using a throat swab is a point-of-care method that identifies GAS antigens within minutes. It offers a sensitivity of 70-90% and specificity exceeding 95%, with a positive result directly confirming GAS as the cause. Negative RADT results, particularly in children, necessitate follow-up culture due to potential false negatives.
Throat culture remains the gold standard for definitive diagnosis, involving inoculation of a swab sample onto blood agar medium. Beta-hemolytic colonies of GAS typically grow within 24-48 hours and can be confirmed via latex agglutination or other identification methods. This test achieves a sensitivity of 90-95% when performed correctly, making it essential for resolving inconclusive rapid tests.
Anti-streptolysin O (ASO) titer measurement provides retrospective serological confirmation by detecting antibodies to streptolysin O, a GAS exotoxin. Titers elevate in approximately 80% of cases 1-3 weeks post-infection, peaking at 3-6 weeks, and are particularly useful when acute samples are unavailable or for linking to complications.
Molecular tests, such as polymerase chain reaction (PCR), offer high sensitivity for detecting GAS DNA or erythrogenic toxin genes like speA, which are responsible for the rash. These assays are rapid and increasingly applied in outbreak settings or for toxin profiling, with sensitivities often surpassing 95%.
Blood tests support confirmation by revealing systemic inflammation, including leukocytosis (typically 12,000-16,000/L) with neutrophilia and elevated acute-phase reactants such as C-reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR). These markers help gauge disease severity but are not specific to GAS.

Differential diagnosis

Scarlet fever, characterized by a sandpaper-like rash, pharyngitis, and strawberry tongue due to group A Streptococcus (GAS) infection, must be differentiated from other conditions presenting with fever, sore throat, and rash to ensure accurate diagnosis and appropriate management. A negative rapid antigen detection test (RADT) or throat culture for GAS shifts suspicion toward alternative etiologies, as these tests confirm streptococcal involvement in scarlet fever.

Infectious Mimics

Viral exanthems commonly mimic scarlet fever, including measles (rubeola), which features a maculopapular rash starting on the face and Koplik spots in the mouth, often accompanied by cough, coryza, and conjunctivitis absent in scarlet fever. Rubella presents with a similar rash but includes posterior auricular lymphadenopathy and milder symptoms, while enteroviral infections like Coxsackievirus may cause hand-foot-mouth disease with vesicular lesions and diarrhea, distinguishing them from the diffuse erythroderma of scarlet fever. Other viral causes, such as Epstein-Barr virus mononucleosis or parvovirus B19, can produce pharyngitis and rash but typically include fatigue, splenomegaly, or slapped-cheek appearance, respectively, and lack GAS positivity on testing.
Kawasaki disease shares features like strawberry tongue, conjunctivitis, and rash but is differentiated by prolonged fever exceeding five days, bilateral nonexudative conjunctivitis, extremity edema, and coronary artery risks, often requiring echocardiography for confirmation. Bacterial alternatives include staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome (SSSS), which causes more widespread blistering and epidermal cleavage with a positive Nikolsky sign, contrasting the non-blistering sandpaper texture of scarlet fever rash. Toxic shock syndrome (TSS), whether staphylococcal or streptococcal, involves severe hypotension, multi-organ dysfunction, and diffuse erythroderma but lacks the circumoral pallor and Pastia lines typical of scarlet fever. Arcanobacterium haemolyticum pharyngitis may produce a scarlatiniform rash but is rarer and confirmed via specific cultures.

Non-Infectious Mimics

Drug eruptions, such as those from amoxicillin, can imitate the rash and pharyngitis of scarlet fever, particularly in patients treated empirically for presumed streptococcal infection, but are identified by a history of recent medication exposure and resolution upon discontinuation, with negative GAS tests. Juvenile idiopathic arthritis may present with evanescent salmon-colored rash and fever but includes joint involvement and lacks pharyngeal findings or streptococcal confirmation. Allergic reactions more broadly feature urticarial or morbilliform patterns without the fine desquamation or tongue changes seen in scarlet fever.
Distinguishing scarlet fever relies on the combination of clinical features like the sandpaper rash sparing the perioral area, Pastia lines in skin folds, and positive GAS diagnostics, whereas mimics often show divergent rash distributions (e.g., facial onset in measles) or additional systemic signs (e.g., hypotension in TSS). In cases of diagnostic uncertainty, serology for viral causes or skin biopsy may rarely be employed, but initial exclusion via RADT or culture is prioritized.

Management

Antibiotic treatment

Antibiotic treatment is the cornerstone of scarlet fever management, targeting the underlying Group A Streptococcus infection to eradicate the pathogen and prevent suppurative and nonsuppurative complications. Penicillin remains the first-line therapy due to its efficacy, narrow spectrum, and low resistance rates among streptococci. For children, oral penicillin V is administered at 250 mg two to three times daily for 10 days, while adults receive 500 mg two to three times daily for the same duration. Amoxicillin offers an alternative with simpler dosing50 mg/kg once daily (maximum 1,000 mg) for children or 500 mg every 12 hours for adultsfor 10 days, providing equivalent efficacy. In cases of poor oral compliance or vomiting, a single intramuscular dose of benzathine penicillin G (600,000 units for patients under 27 kg or 1.2 million units for those 27 kg or heavier) is recommended as it ensures adherence and achieves similar outcomes.
For patients with non-anaphylactic penicillin allergy, oral cephalosporins such as cephalexin (25-50 mg/kg per day in two to four divided doses, maximum 4 g/day for 10 days) are suitable alternatives, though cross-reactivity must be considered. In true anaphylactic penicillin allergy, macrolides like erythromycin (40 mg/kg per day in three to four divided doses, maximum 1 g/day for 10 days) or clindamycin (20 mg/kg per day in three divided doses, maximum 600 mg/day for 10 days) are used, despite rising resistance rates in some regions that may necessitate susceptibility testing. The treatment goal is to eradicate the bacteria within 24 hours of initiation, with symptoms typically resolving within 48 hours; routine test-of-cure cultures are not recommended unless symptoms recur or complications arise.
Appropriate antibiotic therapy reduces the risk of acute rheumatic fever by approximately 70%, highlighting the importance of prompt administration within 9 days of symptom onset. This regimen also mitigates other complications like glomerulonephritis, underscoring penicillin's role in altering disease progression when initiated early.

Supportive measures

Supportive measures for scarlet fever focus on alleviating symptoms, maintaining comfort, and preventing dehydration during the acute phase of the illness. Patients are advised to ensure adequate rest to support recovery, as fatigue is common with the accompanying fever and malaise. Encouraging frequent intake of fluids, such as water or cool liquids, helps prevent dehydration and soothes the irritated throat, while a soft diet including items like soups, yogurts, or ice pops eases swallowing for those with severe pharyngitis. Over-the-counter analgesics such as acetaminophen or ibuprofen can effectively reduce fever and relieve throat pain, with appropriate dosing based on age and weight; aspirin should be avoided in children due to the risk of Reye's syndrome.
Throat care involves gentle interventions to minimize discomfort from the associated streptococcal pharyngitis. Gargling with warm saltwater solution (approximately 1/4 teaspoon of salt in 8 ounces of water) several times a day can reduce inflammation and pain in older children and adults capable of gargling safely. Soothing remedies like honey (not recommended for children under 12 months) or throat lozenges may provide additional relief, and exposure to irritants such as tobacco smoke should be avoided to prevent exacerbation of symptoms.
Management of the characteristic rash emphasizes symptom relief and monitoring for secondary issues. Calamine lotion or 1% hydrocortisone cream can be applied to itchy areas to alleviate pruritus, while oral antihistamines may be used if needed under medical guidance. During the desquamation phase following rash resolution, emollients such as petroleum jelly help maintain skin integrity, and the affected areas should be kept clean and monitored for signs of secondary bacterial infection, such as increased redness or pus, prompting prompt medical evaluation. Oatmeal baths may also provide soothing relief for widespread irritation.
To prevent transmission, isolation measures are essential during the contagious period. Affected individuals, particularly children, should remain at home from school, work, or daycare until they are fever-free and have completed at least 24 hours of antibiotic therapy, reducing the risk of spreading group A Streptococcus to close contacts. Good hygiene practices, including frequent handwashing and avoiding sharing personal items like utensils or towels, further support containment efforts.
Hospitalization is rarely required for uncomplicated scarlet fever but may be indicated in cases of severe dehydration, inability to swallow due to intense throat pain (odynophagia), or other acute issues necessitating intravenous hydration and supportive care. Close monitoring in a clinical setting ensures timely intervention if symptoms worsen despite initial management.

Handling resistance and complications

Antibiotic resistance in Streptococcus pyogenes, the causative agent of scarlet fever, remains low for penicillin, which continues as the first-line treatment, but macrolide resistance has risen significantly in recent years, reaching rates of up to 11.6% for erythromycin in some European studies and over 90% in regions like Shanghai, China. In cases of treatment failure, particularly where macrolide alternatives are considered due to allergy or regional resistance patterns, clinicians may switch to clindamycin or administer higher-dose penicillin to address potential toxin-mediated persistence, though penicillin susceptibility is nearly universal.
Management of scarlet fever complications focuses on targeted interventions for poststreptococcal sequelae. For acute rheumatic fever, anti-inflammatory agents such as high-dose aspirin (75-100 mg/kg/day divided every 6 hours) are used to control arthritis and mild carditis, while corticosteroids like prednisone (2 mg/kg/day) are reserved for severe cardiac involvement to reduce inflammation and prevent valvular damage. Poststreptococcal glomerulonephritis is primarily managed supportively with fluid and electrolyte monitoring, antihypertensive therapy if needed, and dialysis in cases of acute kidney injury to support renal recovery, as antibiotics do not alter the immune-mediated course once established.
Follow-up care after scarlet fever focuses on ensuring resolution of acute symptoms and completion of antibiotic therapy. For patients with diagnosed or suspected complications, targeted testing is recommended: echocardiography to screen for rheumatic carditis in those with acute rheumatic fever, even without overt symptoms, to identify valvular abnormalities and guide secondary prophylaxis; and serial urine tests for hematuria and proteinuria to monitor for poststreptococcal glomerulonephritis, with persistent abnormalities warranting nephrology referral.
In 2025, global health authorities, including the World Health Organization's antibiotic resistance surveillance efforts, have underscored the need for enhanced monitoring of resistant S. pyogenes strains during outbreaks to inform targeted interventions and prevent further spread of multidrug-resistant clones like emm12.

Prevention

Infection control strategies

Infection control strategies for scarlet fever emphasize individual and household practices to interrupt the transmission of group A Streptococcus (GAS), primarily through respiratory droplets and direct contact. Hand hygiene is a cornerstone measure, with frequent washing using soap and water for at least 20 seconds recommended to remove bacteria from hands, particularly after coughing, sneezing, or handling contaminated items. Respiratory etiquette, such as covering the mouth and nose with a tissue during coughing or sneezing and promptly disposing of used tissues, further reduces droplet spread in close settings like households. These practices are especially critical during the contagious period, which lasts until approximately 24 hours after initiating antibiotic therapy.
Case isolation prevents community transmission by restricting contact with infected individuals. Patients with scarlet fever should be excluded from school, work, or daycare until they are afebrile and have received at least 24 hours of antibiotic treatment, at which point infectivity significantly decreases. This duration aligns with guidelines from health authorities to ensure the resolution of acute symptoms and bacterial shedding, minimizing risks in crowded environments.
For close contacts, such as household members or siblings, routine antibiotic prophylaxis is not recommended for all exposures due to the low risk of secondary cases in most scenarios. However, prophylaxis with antibiotics like penicillin may be advised for high-risk individuals (e.g., those with immunosuppression) or during outbreaks to curb potential spread. Close monitoring of contacts for symptoms like sore throat or fever is essential, with prompt testing and treatment if illness develops.
Household cleaning plays a key role in eliminating environmental reservoirs of GAS. Surfaces frequently touched, such as doorknobs, toys, and countertops, should be disinfected regularly using a 1:49 to 1:99 dilution of household bleach or EPA-approved disinfectants effective against bacteria. Contaminated items like bedding, clothing, and utensils must be washed in hot water with detergent, and sharing of personal items should be avoided during active infection to prevent fomite transmission. These measures help maintain hygiene in shared living spaces until the patient is no longer contagious.
Carrier treatment targets asymptomatic individuals who harbor GAS in the throat, estimated to occur in 5-15% of the population, particularly in recurrent household cases. In such situations, screening via throat culture or rapid antigen testing is recommended for family members, with antibiotics (e.g., a 10-day course of penicillin) offered to confirmed carriers to reduce ongoing transmission risks. This approach is reserved for scenarios with repeated infections, as routine eradication in carriers is often unnecessary and may not always succeed due to persistent colonization.

Public health measures

Public health measures for scarlet fever primarily focus on surveillance, education, research into prevention, and stewardship to curb transmission and resistance at a population level. In many countries, scarlet fever is designated as a notifiable disease, requiring healthcare providers to report confirmed cases to national health authorities for timely outbreak detection and response. For instance, in the United Kingdom, cases must be reported to the UK Health Security Agency (UKHSA) within specified timeframes, enabling coordinated public health actions such as contact tracing and cluster investigations. In the United States, while scarlet fever itself is not nationally notifiable through the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), many states mandate reporting of group A Streptococcus (GAS) infections, including scarlet fever, to local health departments, which feed into broader GAS surveillance systems like the Active Bacterial Core surveillance (ABCs) for monitoring trends. These systems facilitate early detection of outbreaks, particularly in community settings, by aggregating data on incidence and geographic distribution to inform targeted interventions.
School-based programs play a crucial role in preventing scarlet fever spread among children, who are the primary affected group. These initiatives often include education on personal hygiene practices, such as frequent handwashing and covering coughs, integrated into school curricula to promote awareness and reduce transmission risks. In response to clusters or outbreaks, schools collaborate with public health teams for rapid antigen detection testing of symptomatic students, allowing for prompt identification and isolation of cases to limit further spread. Such programs emphasize exclusion policies for ill children until treated, alongside enhanced cleaning protocols in shared spaces, contributing to lower incidence in educational environments.
No licensed vaccine exists for scarlet fever or GAS infections as of 2025, but research into vaccine candidates targeting key bacterial components is advancing. Promising approaches include M-protein-based vaccines, which aim to elicit immunity against the surface protein variants responsible for GAS evasion of the host immune system; for example, the 30-valent M-protein vaccine StreptAnova has completed phase I trials demonstrating immunogenicity and safety in adults. Toxin-based candidates, such as those incorporating epitopes from the SpyCEP protein that inhibits neutrophil recruitment, are also in early-stage development, with phase I trials underway for multivalent peptide vaccines combining M-protein and toxin targets to broaden protection. These efforts, supported by organizations like the Wellcome Trust and the Coalition for Epidemic Preparedness Innovations, focus on overcoming challenges like antigenic diversity to achieve a broadly effective vaccine.
Antibiotic stewardship programs are essential to mitigate the risk of antimicrobial resistance in GAS, which remains low but requires vigilant management given the reliance on penicillin as first-line therapy. Guidelines from bodies like the Infectious Diseases Society of America (IDSA) recommend confirming GAS infection via testing before prescribing antibiotics for pharyngitis-associated scarlet fever, avoiding unnecessary use in viral cases that mimic symptoms. Public health campaigns promote appropriate dosing durationstypically 10 days of penicillinto ensure bacterial eradication and reduce selective pressure for resistance, with monitoring through national surveillance to track any emerging patterns. These measures align with broader efforts to preserve antibiotic efficacy for scarlet fever treatment worldwide.
Globally, the World Health Organization (WHO) emphasizes integrated surveillance for streptococcal diseases in low-resource settings to address disparities in scarlet fever burden. Through initiatives like the Global Antimicrobial Resistance Surveillance System (GLASS), WHO supports sentinel hospital-based monitoring of GAS infections, including non-invasive forms like scarlet fever, to generate data for policy-making and resource allocation. In resource-limited areas, these programs prioritize simplified diagnostic protocols and community-level reporting to detect outbreaks early, integrating scarlet fever surveillance with other neglected tropical diseases to enhance efficiency and coverage. Such efforts aim to reduce morbidity from complications like rheumatic heart disease, which disproportionately affect low-income populations.

Epidemiology

Disease distribution

Scarlet fever is primarily endemic in temperate climate regions, including parts of Europe, North America, and Asia, where it exhibits higher incidence rates compared to tropical areas, where cases are rare due to environmental and transmission factors. The disease's distribution aligns with patterns of group A Streptococcus (GAS) pharyngitis, which predominates in cooler, developed settings with robust surveillance systems.
Demographically, scarlet fever predominantly affects children, with approximately 80% of cases occurring in those aged 5 to 15 years, reflecting the age group most exposed in school and community environments. The attack rate among susceptible children exposed to GAS is estimated at 10-15%, highlighting the role of prior immunity in limiting spread. Adults and younger children under 5 years experience lower rates, though outbreaks can occur in any age group under favorable conditions.
Seasonally, the disease shows marked variation, with peaks typically in winter and early spring in temperate zones, driven by increased indoor crowding that facilitates respiratory droplet transmission. This pattern is consistent across affected regions, such as the United Kingdom and China, where incidence rises during colder months.
Socioeconomic factors significantly influence distribution, with higher rates observed in low-income communities and overcrowded settings like households, schools, and daycare centers, where close contact amplifies transmission. Urban areas generally report greater incidence than rural ones due to population density and mobility, exacerbating vulnerability in resource-limited environments.
The global burden of scarlet fever remains challenging to quantify precisely due to underreporting, particularly in developing regions with limited surveillance, but it contributes to the broader GAS disease load estimated at over 600 million pharyngitis cases annually worldwide. Recent data indicate persistent low-level endemicity in high-income countries alongside resurgences in Asia, underscoring ongoing public health monitoring needs.

Recent trends and outbreaks

Since 2014, scarlet fever has experienced a notable resurgence in several countries, particularly in the United Kingdom, where annual notifications in England rose from approximately 4,700 cases in 2013 to over 15,000 in 2014 and peaked at around 30,768 in the 2017-2018 season. This increase has been associated with the emergence of new group A Streptococcus (GAS) strains, such as the M1UK lineage, which produces higher levels of toxins and has spread internationally since around 2010. Similar trends were observed in Asia, with China reporting a sharp rise starting in 2011, reaching a peak incidence of 5.68 cases per 100,000 population in 2018.
The COVID-19 pandemic temporarily disrupted these patterns, leading to a significant decline in cases due to lockdowns and reduced social mixing; for instance, notifications in England dropped to historic lows in 2020-2021, and China saw only about 16,564 cases in 2022 following earlier reductions. However, a rebound occurred post-restrictions, with England recording 27,486 notifications in the 2022 season alone and over 55,000 across the 2022-2023 season. In the United States, where scarlet fever is not nationally notifiable, invasive GAS infections increased substantially to an estimated 14,000-28,000 annual cases in recent years (as of 2023 data), reflecting broader GAS activity surges; non-invasive infections, including those potentially manifesting as scarlet fever, are estimated at several million annually, though specific scarlet fever cases are not tracked. Key contributing factors include reduced population immunity from limited exposure during lockdowns (often termed "immunity debt"), the persistence of virulent strains like M1UK, emerging antibiotic resistance to macrolides in regions such as China, and climatic influences that may enhance seasonality, such as temperature and humidity variations strengthening post-2020 associations.
As of November 2025, trends show stabilization in parts of Europe, with England's 2024-2025 season exhibiting scarlet fever rates in line with expected seasonal patterns (GP consultation peak of 1.14 per 100,000 in February 2025), while Asia reports continued rises, including a 152% increase in South Korea through mid-2025 (3,809 cases as of May 2025). Post-pandemic shifts have also been observed, with increased incidence proportions among older children (ages 8-15) in countries like South Korea and Taiwan during 2024-2025 compared to pre-2020 levels. No major global pandemics have emerged, but heightened surveillance is emphasized due to a parallel uptick in invasive GAS co-infections, which have risen alongside scarlet fever cases in affected regions.

History

Major epidemics

In the 19th century, scarlet fever caused annual epidemics across Europe and the United States, particularly affecting children under 10 years old, with case fatality rates ranging from 20% to 30%. In England and Wales, the disease accounted for approximately 10,000 deaths per year by mid-century, primarily among children aged 1 to 5. Severe waves in the 1840s devastated urban centers; for instance, in London, epidemics in 18391840 and 1848 resulted in death rates up to twice the annual average, with nearly 20,000 fatalities nationwide in 1840 alone. By 1840, scarlet fever had become the leading infectious cause of childhood death in the U.S., Great Britain, and much of Europe, exacerbated by poor sanitation and overcrowding in industrializing cities.
Following a period of declining severity in the late 19th century, the disease reached a global peak in the 1920s, with severe outbreaks characterized by a "malignant" form featuring rapid progression, septic complications, and high fatality rates in affected cases. In the United States, incidence among children peaked dramatically during this period, with rates of approximately 150 cases per 100,000 population in some years, though higher among children. These epidemics strained healthcare systems, as the virulent strains caused widespread pharyngitis, rash, and toxic shock-like symptoms, contributing to scarlet fever's status as a top childhood killer before antitoxin development in the mid-1920s.
Post-1940s, the introduction of sulfonamide antibiotics in the late 1930s and penicillin in the 1940s dramatically reduced scarlet fever mortality from around 15% to less than 1%, marking the end of its era as a major public health threat. The last significant U.S. outbreaks occurred in the 1950s, after which cases became sporadic and rarely fatal due to widespread antibiotic use and improved hygiene.
These epidemics imposed profound socioeconomic burdens, including frequent school closures to curb transmission, overwhelming orphanages with the deaths of thousands of children annually, and prompting reforms in public sanitation and infection control. In Europe and the U.S., the high child mortality fueled advocacy for better urban planning and medical infrastructure, influencing early 20th-century public health policies.
Regional variations highlighted urbanization's role; in Asia, such as 1880s Japan during rapid industrialization, scarlet fever followed similar patterns of explosive outbreaks in crowded cities, mirroring European experiences but adapted to local demographics and delayed medical responses.

Key discoveries and tests

In the mid-19th century, Friedrich Henle advanced the understanding of scarlet fever by proposing a theory of contagion in his 1840 work Von den Miasmen und Kontagien, where he described infectious diseases, including those like scarlet fever, as potentially caused by living agents transmissible between individuals, laying early groundwork for germ theory. During the Pasteur-Koch era in the late 19th century, broader bacteriological research solidified the microbial etiology of many infections, though the specific bacterial link to scarlet fever awaited confirmation in the 20th century through experimental isolation of Streptococcus pyogenes strains.
A major breakthrough occurred in 1923 when bacteriologists George F. Dick and Gladys H. Dick isolated the erythrogenic toxin produced by certain hemolytic Streptococcus strains, demonstrating that this toxin was responsible for the characteristic rash and symptoms of scarlet fever, which enabled the development of serum antitoxin therapy to neutralize the toxin and reduce severity. Building on this, in 1924 the Dicks introduced the Dick test, an intradermal injection of diluted erythrogenic toxin to assess susceptibility: a positive reaction (redness and inflammation at the site) indicated lack of prior immunity, while a negative reaction suggested antitoxin presence from previous exposure; the test was widely used for screening and immunization decisions until the 1950s, when antibiotics diminished its necessity.
The advent of antibiotics revolutionized scarlet fever management, with sulfonamides introduced in the 1930s proving effective against Streptococcus pyogenes infections and contributing to a dramatic 52% decline in scarlet fever mortality rates in affected populations. By the 1940s, penicillin further transformed prognosis, rapidly eradicating the bacteria and preventing complications, shifting scarlet fever from a potentially fatal disease to one with excellent outcomes when treated promptly.
In the 1980s, research revealed that the genes encoding key scarlet fever toxins, such as streptococcal pyrogenic exotoxin A (SpeA), are carried by bacteriophages integrated into the Streptococcus pyogenes genome, explaining variability in toxin production and disease severity across strains through phage-mediated lysogenic conversion. More recently, in the 2020s, whole-genome sequencing of epidemic scarlet fever strains has enabled detailed phylogenetic analysis, identifying dominant clones like emm12 and M1T1 variants with enhanced virulence factors, informing outbreak tracking and potential vaccine targets.
