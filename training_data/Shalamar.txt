Shalamar

1x
 Shalamar is an American R&B, soul, and disco vocal group formed in 1977 by Soul Train creator and host Don Cornelius and booking agent Dick Griffey as a manufactured act emerging from the iconic television program. Featuring the classic lineup of vocalists Howard Hewett, Jeffrey Daniel, and Jody Watleytwo of whom were star dancers on Soul Trainthe group blended smooth harmonies with infectious dance rhythms, achieving international acclaim in the late 1970s and early 1980s. Known for pioneering elements of street dance like body-popping in the UK, Shalamar sold over 25 million records worldwide and earned more than 100 gold, silver, and platinum awards.
The group's debut single, the Motown medley "Uptown Festival" (1977), marked their entry into the music scene, produced by Griffey and released on his Soul Train Records label. Their breakthrough came with albums like Disco Gardens (1978) and Big Fun (1979), but true stardom arrived with Three for Love (1980), their first platinum album. Follow-up hits from Friends (1982) and Heartbreak (1984), including "A Night to Remember," "There It Is," and the ballad "Friends," solidified their status as versatile hitmakers, topping R&B charts and crossing over to pop audiences in the US and UK. Jeffrey Daniel's innovative choreography, including teaching Michael Jackson the moonwalk, further elevated their cultural impact.
Following internal tensions, Watley left in 1984 for a successful solo career, and the group underwent lineup changes before disbanding in the late 1980s. Shalamar reformed in 2001 with Hewett, Daniel, and Carolyn Griffey (daughter of Dick Griffey), continuing to tour and perform their catalog with a live band, including celebrations of milestones like the 40th anniversary of the Friends album in 2022. Active into the 2020s with tours including performances in Nigeria and the US in 2025, they remain a cornerstone of R&B and disco legacy, influencing generations of artists with their stylish sound and energetic performances.

Formation and Early Career

Origins and Creation

Shalamar was formed in 1977 by Dick Griffey, the booking agent for the popular television program Soul Train, and Don Cornelius, the show's creator and host, with the aim of creating a disco-oriented act that would leverage the visibility and talent of the program's dancers. The group emerged as a strategic vehicle to transition Soul Train's performers from on-screen appearances to a recording and performing entity, capitalizing on the mid-1970s surge in disco music. Griffey, who had previously managed talent for the show, collaborated closely with Cornelius to assemble the act, drawing directly from the energetic dance routines and cultural influence of Soul Train.
The initial concept for Shalamar emphasized a fusion of R&B, soul, and disco elements, designed to appeal to the show's young, urban audience while showcasing synchronized choreography that mirrored Soul Train's iconic dance lines. This blend was intended to create infectious, dancefloor-ready tracks that extended the television format into the music industry, with the group serving as an extension of the Soul Train brand. Early development involved session musicians and vocalists to prototype the sound, but the focus remained on integrating live performers familiar to viewers.
Key to the group's early recruitment were dancers Jeffrey Daniel and Jody Watley, both prominent figures on Soul Train who had gained recognition through their appearances in the show's "Soul Train Scramble Band" segments and dance lines. Cornelius personally selected Daniel and Watley for their dynamic stage presence and popularity among fans, positioning them as the visual and performative core of Shalamar to bridge the gap between TV stardom and musical success. Their involvement marked a pivotal step in transforming the act from a conceptual project into a cohesive performing unit.
Shalamar's signing to Soul Train Records, the label co-founded by Griffey and Cornelius, facilitated their debut release and signified a deliberate shift from mere television exposure to established recording artists. This move allowed the group to produce material tailored for both radio and club play, setting the stage for lineup evolutions that would define their later sound.

Debut Album and Initial Lineup

Shalamar's debut album, Uptown Festival, was released in 1977 on Soul Train Records, marking the group's entry into the disco scene as a studio project assembled by Soul Train producer Dick Griffey. The record primarily featured covers of popular disco and Motown tracks, with the title track serving as a medley of ten classics such as "I Want You Back" by The Jackson 5 and "Dancing Machine" by the same group, performed over a continuous disco beat. Recorded largely by session musicians and vocalists, including producers Mike Lewis and Laurin Rinder from the group El Coco, the album lacked a fixed performing lineup at this stage and was conceived as a quick cash-in on the era's dance trends.
The lead single, "Uptown Festival (Part 1)," provided Shalamar's first taste of chart success, peaking at No. 10 on the Billboard Hot R&B Singles chart in May 1977 and No. 25 on the Billboard Hot 100. This modest breakthrough established the group's disco roots but highlighted their reliance on session talent, as core members were not yet fully integrated into the recordings. The album itself climbed to No. 48 on the Billboard 200, reflecting limited commercial impact amid the saturated late-1970s dance market.
By 1978, Shalamar transitioned to the newly formed SOLAR Records for their follow-up album, Disco Gardens, which introduced more original material while retaining disco influences in tracks like "Take That to the Bank" and the instrumental title cut. This effort solidified the initial performing lineup of lead vocalist Gerald Brown, alongside Soul Train dancers Jeffrey Daniel and Jody Watley, who contributed vocals and choreography. However, the album underperformed commercially compared to its predecessor, failing to produce significant hits and underscoring the group's evolving identity.
Early challenges plagued Shalamar's development, including lineup instability stemming from Daniel and Watley's ongoing commitments as dancers on the Soul Train television program, which restricted their availability for full-time recording and promotion. Limited label support from Soul Train Records further hampered cohesion, prompting the shift to SOLAR for greater resources. These hurdles delayed the group's stabilization but laid the groundwork for their later disco explorations.

Classic Lineup and Peak Success

Lineup Stabilization

Following the release of their 1978 album Disco Gardens, which featured vocalist Gerald Brown alongside Jeffrey Daniel and Jody Watley, Shalamar underwent a pivotal lineup change when Brown departed due to payment disputes. Howard Hewett joined as the new lead vocalist in 1979, solidifying the group's classic trio configuration of Hewett, Daniel, and Watley. This formation marked a turning point, blending Hewett's soulful tenor with Daniel's dynamic tenor and Watley's alto, creating a balanced vocal harmony that defined Shalamar's signature sound.
In 1979, Shalamar transitioned to SOLAR Records, the label founded by Dick Griffey in 1977 as an evolution of the earlier Soul Train Records partnership with Don Cornelius. This move provided the group with enhanced creative autonomy under Griffey's guidance, shifting their emphasis from pure disco toward a more soul-infused R&B style that aligned with emerging trends. During early performances with the new lineup, dancer Jermaine Stewart contributed as a backing performer, adding visual flair through his Soul Train-honed choreography, though he was not part of the core vocal trio.
The debut SOLAR single, a cover of "Take Me to the River" released in 1979 from the album Big Fun, exemplified this stylistic evolution with its groove-oriented R&B arrangement produced by Leon Sylvers III. Within the trio, Hewett's prominent lead vocals drove the emotional core of tracks, while Daniel provided production and arrangement input that enhanced the group's polished presentation. Watley, meanwhile, began emerging as a songwriter, co-penning songs like "Full of Fire" that infused personal lyricism into Shalamar's repertoire. This collaborative dynamic propelled their breakthrough hit "The Second Time Around," setting the stage for sustained success.

Major Hits and Albums

Shalamar achieved their commercial breakthrough with the 1979 album Big Fun, which peaked at number 23 on the Billboard 200 and number 4 on the Top R&B/Hip-Hop Albums chart. The title track "The Second Time Around" became the group's first major crossover success, reaching number 1 on the Hot R&B/Hip-Hop Songs chart and number 8 on the Billboard Hot 100. This upbeat disco-soul track, emphasizing romantic reconciliation, showcased the classic lineup's vocal interplay and helped certify the album gold in the United States.
The follow-up album Three for Love (1980) continued their momentum, peaking at number 8 on the R&B chart and blending funk-driven uptempo songs with smoother ballads. The lead single "Make That Move" topped out at number 6 on the R&B chart and number 60 on the Hot 100, urging bold romantic action over a groovy bassline and layered harmonies. This release solidified Shalamar's reputation for infectious, dance-oriented R&B that appealed to both club and radio audiences.
In 1982, Friends marked the pinnacle of their peak era, topping the R&B Albums chart and reaching number 35 on the Billboard 200 while earning gold certification. Produced by Leon Sylvers III, the album featured the hits "A Night to Remember," which peaked at number 8 on the R&B chart and number 44 on the Hot 100, and "There It Is," hitting number 4 on the R&B chart. These tracks captured nightlife romance through shimmering synths and falsetto leads, with "Friends" itself climbing to number 1 on the R&B singles chart.
Subsequent releases extended this success amid emerging lineup tensions. Heartbreak (1984) peaked at number 32 on the R&B chart, incorporating more pop-oriented production while maintaining the group's soulful core. The 1983 album The Look, produced by Sylvers, reached number 13 on the R&B chart and featured "Dead Giveaway," which hit number 10 on the R&B chart and number 22 on the Hot 100. This single's synth-funk edge highlighted themes of deception in love, earning a Grammy nomination for Best R&B Performance by a Duo or Group with Vocals.
Shalamar's production during this period, largely helmed by Leon Sylvers III, emphasized tight vocal harmonies, danceable bass grooves, and lyrical explorations of love and urban nightlife, blending disco remnants with emerging electro-R&B elements. Sylvers' arrangements featured prominent basslines and intricate horn sections, creating a polished sound that influenced the genre's evolution.
The group's international appeal surged in the UK, where they notched three Top 10 albums in the 1980s, including Friends at number 7 and Greatest Hits at number 4, alongside multiple Top 5 singles like "A Night to Remember" and "There It Is." This breakthrough expanded their global fanbase and cemented their status as R&B innovators.

Lineup Changes and Later Years

Key Departures

Jody Watley and Jeffrey Daniel departed from Shalamar in 1983 amid internal conflicts, including tensions over creative control, limited songwriting input, and lack of royalty payments from SOLAR Records. Watley, frustrated after contributing to only two co-written songs in eight years, pursued a successful solo career, achieving hits such as "Don't You Want Me," which peaked at number 6 on the Billboard Hot 100 in 1987. These tensions reportedly involved group leader Howard Hewett and issues of vocal prominence.
Howard Hewett left Shalamar in 1985 to focus on his solo career in R&B and gospel music, signing with Elektra Records and releasing his debut album I Commit to Love the following year. He was temporarily replaced by Sidney Justin, who joined the group for subsequent recordings and tours. Hewett's departure further destabilized the lineup, as he had been the primary lead vocalist since 1979.
Daniel's exit in 1983 was followed by his relocation to the United Kingdom and pursuit of solo opportunities, including hosting the British version of Soul Train that premiered on Channel 4 in April 1985.
These key departures marked a significant shift for Shalamar, resulting in a loss of the classic trio's chemistry and a pivot toward a more pop- and rock-influenced sound under SOLAR Records' direction, which alienated some fans of their earlier R&B and disco roots. U.S. sales declined notably, with the post-departure album Heartbreak (1984), featuring new members Delisa Davis and Micki Free, peaking at number 90 on the Billboard 200 and number 32 on the R&B charta sharp drop from the platinum success of prior releases like Three for Love (1980). The resulting lineup instability contributed to the group's Circumstantial Evidence (1987) and Wake Up (1990) albums, which received mixed reception and limited commercial success in the U.S. market. Royalty disputes with SOLAR, affecting both early original members like Nate Brown and later ex-members, exacerbated internal conflicts and financial strains dating back to the early 1980s.

Post-1980s Albums and Activity

Following the departure of key members in the mid-1980s, Shalamar released their ninth studio album, Circumstantial Evidence, in 1987 on SOLAR Records with a lineup featuring Delisa Davis on vocals, Micki Free on guitar and vocals, and Sydney Justin on vocals. The album included a remix of the earlier hit "Love's Train" but marked a commercial downturn for the group, achieving limited chart performance.
The group's tenth and final SOLAR album, Wake Up, arrived in 1990 with the same lineup and shifted toward contemporary R&B sounds, incorporating new jack swing production elements in an effort to align with evolving market trends. Like its predecessor, it achieved limited commercial success and failed to chart significantly.
SOLAR Records' financial woes, culminating in its closure in the late 1990s, exacerbated ongoing royalty disputes for Shalamar, stemming from earlier label mismanagement and distribution deals that left artists underpaid despite prior hits. These issues strained group cohesion, contributing to reduced output as the label's catalog was sold to EMI.
Throughout the 1990s, Shalamar maintained sporadic activity without releasing new studio albums, relying instead on compilation releases such as The Very Best Of Shalamar and European tours that drew on the group's enduring popularity there, often with rotating personnel including reunions of former members like Howard Hewett and Jeffrey Daniel. Efforts to revive interest included remixes of classics like "A Night to Remember," such as a 1991 U.S. version, amid the label transition.

Reunions and Legacy

Modern Reunions

In 2005, the incarnation of Shalamar featuring Howard Hewett, Jeffrey Daniel, and Carolyn Griffey appeared on the UK ITV series Hit Me, Baby, One More Time, where the lineup performed their classic hit "A Night to Remember" and reached the grand final.
This incarnation of Shalamar, featuring Hewett, Daniel, and Griffey, embarked on tours across Europe and the UK from 2008 to 2010, delivering performances of their signature soul and R&B repertoire to enthusiastic audiences amid renewed interest in 1980s music.
In 2022, Hewett and Daniel marked the 40th anniversary of Shalamar's album Friends with promotional activities and interviews, highlighting the enduring bond between the original members while Griffey continued as the third vocalist in live settings; however, Jody Watley, citing her focus on solo projects, declined involvement in any full classic lineup reunion. The group launched a dedicated Friends 40th Anniversary Tour in 2023, performing the entire album live for the first time.
From 2023 to 2025, Shalamar maintained an active schedule of European tours with Hewett, Daniel, and Griffey, including dates in the UK such as the Greatest Hits Tour stop at Brighton's Centre in June 2024 and a performance at TivoliVredenburg in Utrecht, Netherlands, in June 2025, sustaining the group's international presence.
These efforts faced challenges, including Hewett's 2023 hospitalization for appendicitis during a U.S. tour date, which required emergency surgery and briefly disrupted schedules, as well as logistical hurdles stemming from Watley's commitments to her ongoing solo career and aversion to revisiting group dynamics.

Cultural Impact and Awards

Shalamar's fusion of disco, soul, and R&B significantly shaped the trajectory of dance-oriented music, pioneering a smooth transition from 1970s disco grooves to the polished pop-R&B sound of the 1980s. Their upbeat, harmony-driven tracks emphasized infectious rhythms and emotional delivery, influencing subsequent soul acts like DeBarge and The Whispers, whose similar blend of funk and balladry echoed Shalamar's accessible style.
The group's choreography further amplified their cultural footprint, particularly through Jeffrey Daniel's innovative moves that blended popping, locking, and the backslidea precursor to the moonwalk. Performed on UK television in 1982, these routines not only energized Shalamar's live shows but also impacted the burgeoning MTV era; Daniel taught the technique to Michael Jackson, who popularized it in "Billie Jean," setting a standard for synchronized, high-energy music videos that defined 1980s visual pop culture.
Shalamar's songs have endured in media through sampling and soundtracks, cementing their role in hip-hop and film. The track "A Night to Remember" (1982), a staple of their peak era, was sampled by Mase featuring Blackstreet in "Get Ready" (1997), illustrating how Shalamar's melodies provided foundational elements for rap production and cross-genre fusion.
In 2023, Shalamar received an Award Stone on the UK Music Walk of Fame, recognizing their global legacy. Their music continues to inspire tribute performances, including high-profile nods in 2024 events celebrating 1980s soul icons.

Personnel

Core and Classic Members

Jeffrey Daniel, born August 24, 1955, in Los Angeles, California, is an American dancer, singer-songwriter, choreographer, and producer who served as a founding member and key performer in Shalamar's classic lineup from 1977 to 1983. Initially gaining prominence as a dancer on the television program Soul Train in the mid-1970s, Daniel's dynamic stage presence and innovative choreography became central to Shalamar's visual appeal and live performances. He contributed vocals and was instrumental in developing the group's signature dance routines for hits like "A Night to Remember" and "The Second Time Around," blending R&B with energetic, synchronized movements that influenced global pop culture. After leaving Shalamar, Daniel pursued opportunities in the UK, including production work and collaborations that extended his influence in dance and music.
Jody Watley, born January 30, 1959, in Chicago, Illinois, is a singer, songwriter, record producer, and dancer who joined Shalamar as a core member in 1977, rising from her early fame as one of the most recognized dancers on Soul Train starting at age 14. As a vocalist in the classic trio, Watley provided rich harmonies and background vocals that complemented the group's soulful sound, while her fashion-forward style and poised stage visuals helped define Shalamar's polished image during their peak years. Her contributions extended to co-writing tracks and enhancing the band's crossover appeal in both R&B and pop markets. Following her departure in 1983, Watley launched a Grammy-winning solo career, highlighted by the 1987 hit "Looking for a New Love," which earned her the Grammy Award for Best New Artist.
Howard Hewett, born October 1, 1955, in Akron, Ohio, is an R&B and gospel singer-songwriter who joined Shalamar in 1978 as the lead vocalist, solidifying the classic trio alongside Daniel and Watley until 1985. Raised in a musical family with deep gospel rootshis mother promoted major gospel acts like James ClevelandHewett brought emotive, soul-stirring delivery to Shalamar's ballads and uptempo tracks, such as "Make That Move" and "There It Is," which showcased his tenor range and emotional depth. His gospel-influenced phrasing added authenticity and intensity to the group's sound, contributing to their international success. After Shalamar, Hewett released successful solo albums, including I Commit to Love in 1986, which featured the R&B chart-topping title track, and later incorporated his faith through ministry and gospel recordings.
In Shalamar's defining 19791983 era, the trio's interplay was essential: Hewett's powerful leads anchored the romantic and dance-oriented songs, Watley's layered harmonies and stylish visuals provided balance and allure, and Daniel's high-energy performances and choreography infused the music with infectious vitality, creating a cohesive act that sold over 25 million records worldwide.

Additional and Touring Members

Gary Mumford served as the lead vocalist for Shalamar's debut single and album Uptown Festival (1977), recording the vocals as a session singer and forming the initial performing trio with Jeffrey Daniel and Jody Watley before being replaced in 1978.
Gerald Brown served as Shalamar's lead vocalist from 1978 to 1979, performing live for the debut single and contributing vocals to the follow-up Disco Gardens (1978), including the single "Take That to the Bank." A former dancer on Soul Train and member of the Soul Train Gang, Brown later provided session vocals for Shalamar projects after departing the group.
Jermaine Stewart served as a background dancer and occasional backing vocalist for Shalamar from 1978 to 1983, supporting live performances during the classic lineup era alongside his Soul Train dancing background. He transitioned to a solo career, achieving success with the 1986 hit "We Don't Have to Take Our Clothes Off," which reached number five on the Billboard Hot 100.
Sidney Justin joined Shalamar as lead vocalist from 1986 to 1991, replacing Howard Hewett and appearing on the albums Circumstantial Evidence (1987) and Wake Up (1990), both released on SOLAR Records. A former NFL player turned singer and keyboardist, Justin contributed to the group's late-1980s touring efforts before the band's hiatus.
Carolyn Griffey has been Shalamar's lead female vocalist since 2001, participating in reunions and modern tours with core members Howard Hewett and Jeffrey Daniel, including performances of classics like "A Night to Remember." As the daughter of Shalamar founder Dick Griffey, she brings a familial legacy to the group and has helped sustain its international touring presence into the 2020s.
Among touring and session contributors, guitarist and vocalist Micki Free was a key addition from 1983 to 1991, providing instrumentation and vocals on albums like The Look (1983, touring support) through Wake Up (1990), and joining live lineups with Delisa Davis and Sidney Justin. Delisa Davis served as female lead vocalist from 1984 to 1990, replacing Jody Watley and performing on tours and albums including Heartbreak promotions and Circumstantial Evidence. Leon Sylvers III contributed as a session bassist and producer on several Shalamar recordings, including the platinum-certified The Look (1983) and earlier hits under his family production banner at SOLAR.

Discography

Studio Albums

Shalamar's debut studio album, Uptown Festival, was released in 1977 by Fantasy Records and peaked at No. 48 on the Billboard R&B chart.
The follow-up, Disco Gardens, came out in 1978 on SOLAR Records and peaked at No. 52 on the Billboard R&B chart.
In 1979, the group released The Second Time Around on SOLAR Records, which peaked at No. 15 on the Billboard R&B chart.
Big Fun, released in 1980 on SOLAR, peaked at No. 4 on the R&B chart and was certified Gold by the RIAA.
Also in 1980, Three for Love was released on SOLAR and peaked at No. 40 on the Billboard 200 and No. 8 on the R&B chart, earning Gold certification from the RIAA.
In 1981, Go for It appeared on SOLAR, peaking at No. 115 on the Billboard 200 and No. 18 on the R&B chart.
The 1982 release Friends on SOLAR peaked at No. 1 on the R&B chart and received Gold certification from the RIAA.
Heartbreak was issued by SOLAR in 1984 and peaked at No. 32 on the R&B chart.
The Look followed in 1983 on SOLAR, attaining a peak of No. 13 on the R&B chart.
After a hiatus, Shalamar returned with Circumstantial Evidence in 1987 via SOLAR, which reached No. 29 on the R&B chart.
The final studio album, Wake Up, appeared in 1990 on SOLAR but failed to enter the charts.
No original studio albums were released by Shalamar after 1990, with subsequent output limited to compilations and live recordings.

Notable Singles and Chart Performance

Shalamar achieved their first chart success with the 1977 single "Uptown Festival," which peaked at No. 25 on the R&B chart.
The group's breakthrough came in 1979 with "The Second Time Around," topping the R&B chart at No. 1 and reaching No. 8 on the Hot 100, while earning Gold certification from the RIAA for sales exceeding 500,000 units.
In 1980, "Make That Move" continued their momentum, hitting No. 1 on the R&B chart and No. 66 on the Hot 100.
The 1982 single "A Night to Remember" marked a high point, peaking at No. 8 on the R&B chart, No. 44 on the Hot 100, and No. 5 on the UK Singles Chart, and has since been widely sampled in over 45 songs across genres.
That same year, "Friends" also topped the R&B chart at No. 1 and reached No. 33 on the Hot 100.
Shalamar's 1983 releases included "There It Is," which peaked at No. 2 on the R&B chart and No. 55 on the Hot 100, and "Dead Giveaway," attaining No. 10 on the R&B chart and No. 22 on the Hot 100.
A 1987 remix of "Love's Train" charted at No. 48 on the R&B chart.
Overall, Shalamar amassed 10 Top 10 singles on the R&B chart, with significant international success in the UKwhere they scored four Top 10 hitsand across Europe.
