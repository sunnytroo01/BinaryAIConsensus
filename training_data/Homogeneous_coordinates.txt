 Homogeneous coordinates, also known as projective coordinates, are a mathematical system for representing points and lines in projective geometry, where a point in an n-dimensional space is expressed as an (n+1)-tuple of real or complex numbers defined up to nonzero scalar multiplication, such as (x1:x2::xn+1)(x_1 : x_2 : \dots : x_{n+1})(x1:x2::xn+1). This representation extends the affine structure of Euclidean space by incorporating points at infinity, allowing parallel lines to intersect at a common ideal point on the line at infinity, thus unifying affine and projective transformations under linear algebra. Introduced by August Ferdinand Mbius in his 1827 treatise Der barycentrische Calcul, homogeneous coordinates originated from barycentric methods for mass-point geometry and were later formalized in the context of projective geometry by figures such as Jean-Victor Poncelet and Julius Plcker in the early 19th century.
In projective space Pn\mathbb{P}^nPn, homogeneous coordinates define points as equivalence classes of nonzero vectors in Rn+1\mathbb{R}^{n+1}Rn+1 or Cn+1\mathbb{C}^{n+1}Cn+1, where two vectors represent the same point if one is a scalar multiple of the other. This scale invariance is a core property, enabling the embedding of the Euclidean plane into the projective plane P2\mathbb{P}^2P2 via coordinates (x:y:1)(x : y : 1)(x:y:1) for finite points and (x:y:0)(x : y : 0)(x:y:0) for points at infinity, which correspond to directions rather than positions. Projective transformations, represented as invertible linear maps on these coordinates, preserve incidence relations (such as collinearity) and the cross-ratio, a fundamental invariant that generalizes the Euclidean distance ratio for four collinear points. Duality in projective geometry further equates points and hyperplanes, with a point (x1::xn+1)(x_1 : \dots : x_{n+1})(x1::xn+1) dual to the hyperplane defined by the equation x1X1++xn+1Xn+1=0x_1 X_1 + \dots + x_{n+1} X_{n+1} = 0x1X1++xn+1Xn+1=0.
The advantages of homogeneous coordinates lie in their computational efficiency and generality, as they linearize operations like translation and perspective projectiontasks that require nonlinear formulas in Cartesian coordinatesthrough simple matrix multiplications. For instance, in 3D space, a point (X:Y:Z:W)(X : Y : Z : W)(X:Y:Z:W) dehomogenizes to (X/W,Y/W,Z/W)(X/W, Y/W, Z/W)(X/W,Y/W,Z/W) when W0W \neq 0W=0, but accommodates vanishing perspective when W=0W = 0W=0. This framework eliminates special cases in geometric algorithms, such as handling parallel intersections or clipping in rendering.
Homogeneous coordinates have broad applications across mathematics and engineering, particularly in computer graphics for modeling perspective projections and affine transformations in 3D-to-2D rendering pipelines. In computer vision, they facilitate multi-camera calibration and epipolar geometry by naturally handling image planes at infinity. Other uses include robotics for kinematic modeling, computer-aided design (CAD) for surface representations, and algebraic geometry for studying conics and quadrics via homogeneous polynomials. Their enduring influence stems from providing a rigorous, coordinate-based foundation for projective geometry, bridging classical synthetic methods with modern computational techniques.

Fundamentals

Introduction

The primary motivation for homogeneous coordinates arises from the need to extend Euclidean geometry to the projective plane, where parallel lines can intersect at points at infinity, resolving issues in perspective drawing and geometric transformations. In the Euclidean plane, parallel lines never meet, but in projective geometry, this limitation is overcome by adjoining an infinite line, unifying concepts like intersections and projections under a single algebraic framework.

Computer Graphics and Vision

In computer graphics, homogeneous coordinates facilitate clipping and perspective division by representing points in four dimensions as (x,y,z,w)(x, y, z, w)(x,y,z,w), where the www-coordinate encodes depth information for projective transformations. During the rendering pipeline, vertices are transformed into clip space using a projection matrix, and clipping occurs against the canonical view volume defined by wx,y,zw-w \leq x, y, z \leq wwx,y,zw, ensuring efficient culling of geometry outside the frustum before perspective division normalizes coordinates by dividing x,y,zx, y, zx,y,z by www to obtain normalized device coordinates (NDC). This approach avoids nonlinear operations during clipping, as the homogeneous representation linearizes perspective effects.
Viewport transformations in graphics pipelines, such as those in OpenGL, employ 4x4 projection matrices to map 3D world coordinates to 2D screen space, incorporating perspective or orthographic projections via homogeneous coordinates. For instance, the OpenGL perspective projection matrix scales and shears coordinates to fit the near and far clipping planes, producing a www-component that varies with depth for realistic foreshortening. These matrices handle the full transformation chainfrom model-view to clip spaceenabling hardware-accelerated rendering on GPUs.
A key advantage of homogeneous coordinates in both graphics and vision is their ability to unify translations, rotations, and projections through 4x4 matrix multiplications, simplifying affine and projective operations that would otherwise require separate handling in Cartesian coordinates. This matrix-based framework supports efficient composition of transformations, such as camera movements and scene scaling, without coordinate system switches.
In computer vision, homogeneous coordinates enable the fundamental matrix to model epipolar geometry, which relates corresponding points between two images from different viewpoints by enforcing the epipolar constraint. The fundamental matrix FFF satisfies $ \mathbf{x}'^T F \mathbf{x} = 0 $ for matching points x\mathbf{x}x and x\mathbf{x}'x in homogeneous form, capturing relative camera pose and intrinsic parameters to constrain stereo correspondence searches along epipolar lines. This representation is essential for tasks like structure-from-motion and 3D reconstruction.
For example, in ray tracing, intersections between rays and primitives like triangles are solved algebraically using homogeneous lines, where a ray is parameterized as a point at infinity joined to an origin, allowing dual representations of points and planes for robust geometric queries without special cases for parallel rays. This method computes barycentric coordinates in projective space for efficient hit testing.
