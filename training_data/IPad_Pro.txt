 The iPad Pro is a line of high-performance tablet computers developed and marketed by Apple Inc., positioned as the premium offering in the iPad family for professional, creative, and productivity-focused users, featuring larger displays, powerful processors, and advanced input accessories like the Apple Pencil and Magic Keyboard. First announced on September 9, 2015, the initial 12.9-inch model introduced a groundbreaking Retina display with 5.6 million pixels, the A9X chip for desktop-class performance, four speakers for immersive audio, and support for the optional Smart Keyboard and first-generation Apple Pencil, starting at $799 for the 32GB Wi-Fi version.
Subsequent generations expanded the lineup with additional screen sizes and cutting-edge technologies, evolving from A-series chips to Apple's custom silicon for enhanced graphics, AI capabilities, and efficiency. In 2017, the second generation added a 10.5-inch option alongside an updated 12.9-inch model, both powered by the A10X Fusion chip and featuring ProMotion technology for a 120Hz refresh rate on the world's most advanced display at the time. The 2018 third generation shifted to an all-screen design with slimmer bezels, Face ID, and the A12X Bionic chip, available in 11-inch and 12.9-inch sizes starting at $799. Further updates in 2020 brought the A12Z Bionic chip, a LiDAR scanner for augmented reality applications, and studio-quality microphones to the fourth generation. The fifth and sixth generations in 2021 and 2022, respectively, marked the transition to M-series chips with the M1 and M2, delivering up to 50% faster CPU performance than prior models and enabling pro apps like Final Cut Pro and Logic Pro for iPad.
The seventh generation, announced in May 2024, introduced the M4 chipthe first Apple silicon designed specifically for iPadwith a new Ultra Retina XDR display using tandem OLED technology for superior contrast and brightness up to 1,600 nits for HDR content, available in 11-inch and 13-inch models starting at $999 and $1,299. The latest eighth generation, unveiled on October 15, 2025, features the even more powerful M5 chip with a 9-core CPU and 10-core GPU incorporating a third-generation ray-tracing engine for up to 1.5 times faster 3D rendering compared to the M4, up to 3.5 times the AI performance of the M4, Wi-Fi 7 connectivity, a next-generation C1X 5G modem for 50% faster cellular speeds, all-day battery life with 50% charge in about 30 minutes, and continued support for the Apple Pencil Pro and Magic Keyboard, positioning it as Apple's thinnest and most advanced iPad yet, available in 11-inch and 13-inch sizes, with the 11-inch Wi-Fi model priced at $999 for 256GB storage and $1,199 for 512GB storage as of March 2026 (both configurations include 12GB RAM and the M5 chip with a 9-core CPU and 10-core GPU, while 1 TB and 2 TB models feature 16 GB RAM and 10-core CPU for enhanced performance in demanding professional applications including 4K/8K video editing in Final Cut Pro, large Logic Pro projects, and multilayer Photoshop/Illustrator files), and the 13-inch model starting at $1,299. The M5 enables smooth gameplay at maximum settings with high frame rates in demanding open-world titles such as Genshin Impact and Wuthering Waves, supported by excellent thermal management and efficient battery usage during extended sessions. Compared to the iPad mini 7 (A17 Pro chip), which runs these games well at high settings with stable performance in shorter sessions but may experience occasional frame drops, stuttering, or throttling during prolonged play due to its less powerful GPU and 60Hz display, the iPad Pro's superior sustained GPU performance and 120Hz ProMotion display make it the clear choice for intensive gaming. Apple reserves ProMotionan adaptive refresh rate technology reaching up to 120 Hzexclusively for iPad Pro models as a product differentiation strategy to segment the lineup, even though other models such as the iPad Air with M3 chip and entry-level iPad with A16 chip feature powerful processors capable of supporting higher refresh rates but retain 60 Hz displays to maintain clear tier distinctions.
Throughout its history, the iPad Pro has emphasized versatility for tasks ranging from digital art and video editing to portable computing, running iPadOS with features like Stage Manager for multitasking and integration with Apple Intelligence for on-device AI processing.

History

First generation (2015)

First-generation iPad Pro 12.9-inch with attached Smart Keyboard during reviewThe first-generation iPad Pro was announced by Apple on September 9, 2015, during a special event at the Bill Graham Civic Auditorium in San Francisco, marking the introduction of Apple's largest tablet to date. It launched exclusively in a 12.9-inch model on November 11, 2015, available in silver, space gray, and gold finishes, with storage options of 32 GB, 128 GB, or 256 GB. The device was positioned as a professional-grade tablet capable of bridging the gap between mobile computing and laptop productivity, emphasizing its expansive screen for multitasking and creative workflows.
At its core, the iPad Pro was powered by the A9X chip, a 64-bit processor with an integrated graphics core that delivered desktop-class performance for graphics-intensive applications. It featured 4 GB of RAM, enabling smooth handling of multiple apps simultaneously through advanced Multi-Touch gestures, including support for split-view multitasking introduced in iOS 9. The 12.9-inch Retina display offered a resolution of 2732 by 2048 pixels at 264 pixels per inch, with full lamination for reduced parallax, an anti-reflective coating to minimize glare, and LED backlighting for vibrant colors and wide viewing angles.
Speaker array and rear camera on the 2015 iPad ProAudio enhancements included a four-speaker system that automatically optimized sound output for landscape or portrait orientation, providing immersive stereo playback with greater volume and clarity compared to prior iPads. The device introduced the Smart Connector, a magnetic three-pin interface on the side that enabled seamless power and data connectivity for compatible accessories without pairing or charging. It was offered in Wi-Fi-only and Wi-Fi + Cellular variants, with the latter supporting global LTE bands for mobile connectivity. Pricing began at $799 for the 32 GB Wi-Fi model, rising to $1,079 for the 128 GB Wi-Fi + Cellular configuration, reflecting its premium hardware and professional focus.

Second generation (2017)

The refreshed 12.9-inch iPad Pro (2nd generation, 2017) in space grayThe second-generation iPad Pro lineup, comprising a new 10.5-inch model and a refreshed 12.9-inch variant, was announced by Apple on June 5, 2017, and released on June 13, 2017. This iteration built upon the Smart Connector introduced in the first generation, enabling compatibility with the Apple Smart Keyboard.
Both models featured the A10X Fusion chip, a 64-bit hexa-core processor with an embedded M10 motion coprocessor and 4 GB of RAM, clocked at up to 2.38 GHz for enhanced performance in graphics-intensive tasks. A key innovation was the introduction of ProMotion display technology, delivering a 120 Hz refresh rate for smoother scrolling and responsiveness across both screen sizes.
The 10.5-inch iPad Pro (2017 model), introduced as part of the second-generation lineupThe 10.5-inch model boasted a Retina display with a 2224-by-1668 resolution at 264 pixels per inch, incorporating wide color (P3) gamut for improved color accuracy and the debut of True Tone technology, which adjusts screen warmth based on ambient lighting. The design emphasized portability, measuring just 6.1 mm thick and weighing 469 grams for the Wi-Fi version, making it more compact than the larger sibling while retaining professional-grade features like four-speaker audio.
Cameras included a 12 MP rear iSight camera supporting 4K video at 30 fps with optical image stabilization, and a 7 MP front FaceTime HD camera capable of 1080p video recording. Battery life reached up to 10 hours of Wi-Fi usage or video playback. Pricing started at $649 for the 64 GB Wi-Fi 10.5-inch model, with the refreshed 12.9-inch beginning at $799 for the same storage configuration.

Third generation (2018)

Comparison showing the all-screen design change in the third-generation iPad Pro (right) compared to a previous model (left)The third-generation iPad Pro was announced on October 30, 2018, and released on November 7, 2018, available in 11-inch and 12.9-inch models, with the previous 10.5-inch size discontinued. This iteration featured an all-screen design with slimmer bezels that reduced the overall device size by approximately 25 percent compared to prior models, while maintaining the same display areas for both sizes.
The displays adopted Liquid Retina technology, with the 11-inch model offering a resolution of 2388 by 1668 pixels at 264 ppi and the 12.9-inch at 2732 by 2048 pixels at 264 ppi; both supported 120 Hz ProMotion for smoother scrolling and True Tone for adaptive color temperature. Powered by the A12X Bionic chip, which includes an 8-core CPU, 7-core GPU, and Neural Engine, the device came equipped with 6 GB of RAM in higher-capacity configurations to enable enhanced multitasking and support for desktop-class applications. It continued the multi-speaker audio system from previous generations for immersive sound output.
Back of the 2018 iPad Pro featuring the USB-C connector (replacing Lightning) and 12 MP rear cameraA key change was the replacement of the Lightning port with USB-C, which supports data transfer speeds up to 10 Gbps via USB 3.1 Gen 2 and enables connectivity to external displays and peripherals. Security was upgraded with the introduction of Face ID through a TrueDepth camera system on the front, allowing secure biometric unlocking and Animoji features. The rear camera remained a 12 MP sensor with an /1.8 aperture and added Smart HDR processing for improved dynamic range in photos.
Pricing began at $799 for the 11-inch Wi-Fi model with 64 GB storage, positioning the iPad Pro as a versatile replacement for desktop computers in professional workflows.

Fourth generation (2020)

Fourth-generation iPad Pro (2020) with Magic Keyboard and Apple Pencil in useThe fourth-generation iPad Pro was announced on March 18, 2020, and released on March 25, 2020, preserving the existing 11-inch and 12.9-inch form factors from the prior model. This update served as a minor refresh, introducing hardware enhancements for augmented reality (AR) and professional workflows while maintaining the device's positioning as a versatile tool for remote work amid the emerging COVID-19 pandemic.
Back of the fourth-generation iPad Pro (2020) showing the dual rear cameras and LiDAR ScannerAt its core, the device featured the A12Z Bionic chip, an octa-core system-on-chip that built upon the A12X from the previous generation by enabling an active 8-core GPU alongside the existing 4 high-performance and 4 high-efficiency CPU cores. It included 6 GB of RAM and benefited from an enhanced thermal design, allowing for higher peak performance and better sustained operation during intensive tasks compared to its predecessor. The displays remained unchanged, utilizing Liquid Retina panels with ProMotion technology for up to 120 Hz refresh rates, True Tone, and wide color support. A key addition was the rear-mounted LiDAR Scanner, capable of measuring distances to objects up to 5 meters away in both indoor and outdoor environments to improve AR depth sensing and application performance.
The camera system saw refinements with a dual rear setup comprising a 12 MP wide-angle lens (/1.8 aperture) and a 10 MP ultra-wide lens (/2.4 aperture, 125 field of view), supporting 4K video recording at up to 60 fps along with features like Smart HDR and document scanning. The front-facing 12 MP TrueDepth camera continued to enable Face ID authentication, as introduced in the third generation. Connectivity included USB-C with Thunderbolt support for charging and peripherals, while Cellular variants offered Gigabit-class LTE rather than 5G. The quad-speaker audio system provided immersive sound with automatic orientation adjustment, laying groundwork for spatial audio capabilities in subsequent iPadOS updates. Pricing started at $799 for the 11-inch Wi-Fi model with 128 GB storage.

Fifth generation (2021)

The fifth-generation iPad Pro marked Apple's transition to its custom silicon processors for the tablet line, introducing the M1 chip across both available screen sizes. Announced on April 20, 2021, during a virtual "Spring Loaded" event, the device became available for preorder starting April 30 and shipped worldwide beginning May 21, 2021. It retained the 11-inch and 12.9-inch form factors from prior generations, offered in silver and space gray finishes, and built upon the USB-C connectivity introduced in earlier models by upgrading to a Thunderbolt/USB 4 port supporting data transfer speeds up to 40 Gbps.
Fifth-generation 12.9-inch iPad Pro in silver featuring Liquid Retina XDR displayAt the core of the fifth-generation iPad Pro is the Apple M1 chip, featuring an 8-core CPU with four performance cores and four efficiency cores, an 8-core GPU, and a 16-core Neural Engine capable of 11 trillion operations per second. Unified memory configurations include 8 GB for models with 128 GB, 256 GB, or 512 GB storage, and 16 GB for those with 1 TB or 2 TB storage options. The 11-inch model uses a Liquid Retina display with a 2388-by-1668-pixel resolution at 264 pixels per inch, supporting 120 Hz ProMotion refresh rates, P3 wide color, and True Tone technology. In contrast, the 12.9-inch variant introduces a mini-LED backlit Liquid Retina XDR display with a 2732-by-2048-pixel resolution at 264 pixels per inch, achieving 1000 nits brightness for full-screen SDR content and up to 1600 nits peak for HDR, alongside a 1,000,000:1 contrast ratio. Both displays measure approximately 11 inches and 12.9 inches diagonally, respectively, with rounded corners and anti-reflective coatings.
Fifth-generation iPad Pro demonstrating Center Stage during FaceTime callAdditional hardware enhancements include a 12 MP Ultra Wide front-facing camera with Center Stage, an AI-powered feature that automatically adjusts the frame to keep users centered during video calls using the device's Neural Engine. The rear camera system comprises a 12 MP Wide camera with an f/1.8 aperture, 5x digital zoom, and Smart HDR 3, paired with a LiDAR Scanner for augmented reality applications. Connectivity options feature 5G support, including mmWave bands for ultra-fast speeds up to 4 Gbps in the United States on cellular models, alongside Wi-Fi 6 and eSIM compatibility. Storage capacities range from 128 GB to 2 TB, with the base 11-inch Wi-Fi model priced at $799 and the 12.9-inch at $1,099.
Apple positioned the fifth-generation iPad Pro as a professional tool, emphasizing its M1-powered performance for demanding workflows such as video editing in Final Cut Pro for iPad, 3D rendering, and augmented reality development, enabling tasks previously limited to desktop computers. The device's capabilities were demonstrated with high-fidelity HDR content creation and support for accessories like the Magic Keyboard, making it suitable for creators handling complex projects on a mobile platform.

Sixth generation (2022)

Apple announced the sixth-generation iPad Pro on October 18, 2022, and released it on October 26, 2022. The lineup retained the 11-inch and 12.9-inch form factors, with the 11-inch model measuring 9.74 inches by 7.02 inches by 0.23 inches (247.6 mm by 178.5 mm by 5.9 mm) and weighing 1.03 pounds (466 grams) for Wi-Fi models, while the 12.9-inch model measured 11.04 inches by 8.46 inches by 0.25 inches (280.6 mm by 214.9 mm by 6.4 mm) and weighed 1.50 pounds (682 grams). Starting prices were $799 for the 11-inch Wi-Fi model with 128 GB storage and $1,099 for the 12.9-inch equivalent.
The primary upgrade was the integration of Apple's M2 chip, featuring an 8-core CPU with 4 performance cores and 4 efficiency cores, a 10-core GPU, and a 16-core Neural Engine capable of 15.8 trillion operations per second. Built on a second-generation 5-nanometer process with 20 billion transistors25 percent more than the M1's 16 billionthe M2 offered up to 15 percent faster CPU performance and 35 percent faster GPU performance compared to the previous generation. Unified memory configurations reached up to 16 GB on models with 1 TB or 2 TB storage (8 GB on lower capacities), enabling enhanced multitasking and machine learning tasks.
Apple Pencil in use on the sixth-generation iPad Pro, demonstrating the Liquid Retina displayDisplay technology remained consistent with the fifth generation, featuring a Liquid Retina panel on the 11-inch model (2388-by-1668 resolution at 264 ppi, 600 nits brightness, ProMotion 120 Hz refresh rate) and a Liquid Retina XDR mini-LED display on the 12.9-inch model (2732-by-2048 resolution at 264 ppi, up to 1600 nits peak HDR brightness). Connectivity saw improvements with Wi-Fi 6E support for speeds up to 2.4 Gb/s and Bluetooth 5.3; all cellular models included 5G with both sub-6 GHz and mmWave bands for faster mobile data.
Professional image editing with Apple Pencil on the 2022 iPad Pro with Magic Keyboard attachedThe sixth-generation iPad Pro emphasized media production capabilities, with hardware-accelerated ProRes video recording up to 4K at 30 fps directly on the device (limited to 1080p at 30 fps on 128 GB models) and support for external ProRes recording up to 4K at 60 fps via USB-C. The LiDAR Scanner was retained for augmented reality applications and enhanced depth sensing in photography. These features, combined with the M2's processing power, facilitated professional workflows in video editing and color-accurate HDR content creation via the display's Reference Mode.

Seventh generation (2024)

The seventh-generation iPad Pro, along with an updated iPad Air featuring the M2 chip, was announced on May 7, 2024, and released on May 15, 2024, marking a significant redesign with the introduction of 11-inch and 13-inch modelsthe latter renaming the previous 12.9-inch variant. Available in silver and space black finishes, these models continue support for 5G connectivity with eSIM, building on the cellular capabilities from the prior generation.
The device features Apple's M4 chip, built on second-generation 3-nanometer technology, with configurations of a 9-core or 10-core CPU (depending on storage), a 10-core GPU, and a 16-core Neural Engine capable of 38 trillion operations per second. Models with 256 GB or 512 GB storage include 8 GB of unified memory, while 1 TB and 2 TB variants upgrade to 16 GB; the GPU supports hardware-accelerated ray tracing for enhanced graphics rendering. This chip delivers up to 1.5 times the CPU performance of the M2 in the previous generation, emphasizing efficiency for professional workflows.
The Ultra Retina XDR display debuts tandem OLED technology, providing superior contrast and color accuracy compared to prior LCD and mini-LED panels. The 11-inch model offers a 2420-by-1668-pixel resolution at 264 pixels per inch (ppi), while the 13-inch reaches 2752-by-2064 pixels at the same density; both support ProMotion with adaptive refresh rates from 1 Hz to 120 Hz, 1000 nits full-screen brightness, and up to 1600 nits peak for HDR content. A nano-texture glass option, available on 1 TB and 2 TB models, reduces glare for creative professionals.
The 13-inch seventh-generation iPad Pro in space black, showing its ultra-thin aluminum enclosure and rear camera layoutAt 5.3 mm thick for the 11-inch and 5.1 mm for the 13-inch, this iPad Pro is Apple's thinnest product to date, weighing 444 grams and 579 grams respectively. The enclosure uses a 100% recycled aluminum frame, incorporating recycled rare earth elements, gold plating, and tin soldering to support Apple's carbon-neutral goals by 2030.
Cameras include a 12 MP Wide rear sensor with adaptive True Tone flash for improved low-light photography and document scanning, paired with a 12 MP Ultra Wide front camera oriented in landscape for Center Stage video calls. Connectivity features USB-C with Thunderbolt/USB 4 support at 40 Gb/s speeds, Wi-Fi 6E, and Bluetooth 5.3. Storage starts at 256 GB and scales to 2 TB, with Wi-Fi models priced from $999 for the 11-inch 256 GB configuration and $1,299 for the 13-inch equivalent (cellular variants add $200); the concurrently announced iPad Air started at $599 for the 11-inch Wi-Fi model and $799 for the 13-inch Wi-Fi model. Apple maintained these official starting MSRPs without reductions until the next model superseded them, with occasional discounts offered by third-party retailers.
Packaging for the 2024 iPad Pro and the newly introduced Apple Pencil Pro with its distinctive designAlongside the iPad Pro, Apple introduced the Apple Pencil Pro, featuring a squeeze sensor for tool switching, haptic feedback for tactile confirmation, a built-in gyroscope for precise control, and Find My integration for location tracking, attaching magnetically to the device for charging.
No new iPad Pro models were released in 2023. In March 2025, Apple updated the iPad Air line with the M3 chip, maintaining the same starting prices as in 2024. The official starting MSRPs for the iPad Pro remained unchanged until the eighth-generation model superseded them in October 2025, with occasional third-party discounts available.

Design

Form factors and dimensions

11-inch and 12.9-inch iPad Pro models compared from the rear, illustrating size differences in earlier generationsThe iPad Pro line debuted in 2015 with a single 12.9-inch form factor designed for professional workflows, measuring 12.0 x 8.68 x 0.27 inches and weighing 1.57 pounds for the Wi-Fi model. In 2017, Apple expanded the lineup to include a 10.5-inch variant alongside an updated 12.9-inch model, introducing slimmer profiles with the 10.5-inch at 9.8 x 6.8 x 0.24 inches and 1.03 pounds (Wi-Fi). The 2018 redesign marked a pivotal shift to dual 11-inch and 12.9-inch sizes, reducing bezels by over 40% compared to prior generations to maximize screen real estate without a home button, while maintaining similar overall footprints at approximately 9.74 x 7.02 x 0.23 inches (1.03 pounds Wi-Fi) for the 11-inch and 11.04 x 8.46 x 0.23 inches (1.39 pounds Wi-Fi) for the 12.9-inch. Subsequent iterations through 2022 refined these dimensions minimally, with the 2021 12.9-inch model slightly thicker at 0.25 inches due to mini-LED display integration. The 2024 seventh generation transitioned the larger model to 13 inches at 11.09 x 8.48 x 0.20 inches (1.28 pounds Wi-Fi), alongside an updated 11-inch at 9.83 x 6.99 x 0.21 inches (0.98 pounds Wi-Fi), emphasizing thinner, lighter aluminum unibodies; the 11-inch model weighs 444 grams (Wi-Fi), enhancing portability for on-the-go use, while the 13-inch weighs 579 grams but includes a larger 38.99 Wh battery versus the 11-inch's 31.29 Wh, enabling longer runtime under heavy loads despite the increased screen size and weight. The 2025 eighth generation retained these form factors with no dimensional changes, continuing the trend toward portability.

Display technologies

The iPad Pro has featured advanced display technologies since its introduction, evolving from high-resolution Retina panels to cutting-edge OLED and mini-LED systems that emphasize brightness, contrast, and color accuracy. The first-generation model in 2015 introduced a Retina display with fully laminated construction and an anti-reflective coating to enhance clarity and reduce reflections. This design integrated the touch layer, digitizer, and LCD into a single stack, minimizing air gaps for a more immersive viewing experience. Subsequent generations built on this foundation, incorporating wider color gamuts and adaptive features to support professional creative workflows.
HDR content displayed on the iPad Pro OLED screen, demonstrating peak brightness and contrastIn 2017, the second-generation iPad Pro debuted ProMotion technology, a 120Hz refresh rate that delivers smoother scrolling, animations, and responsiveness compared to standard 60Hz displays. Apple reserves ProMotion (adaptive 120Hz refresh rate) exclusively for iPad Pro models as a product differentiation strategy to encourage purchases of the higher-end Pro lineup, despite the technology being feasible on lower-tier models with powerful chips (e.g., iPad Air models with M3 remain at 60Hz, as do entry-level iPads with A16 through 2026 models). This maintains clear segmentation between iPad tiers. This was paired with True Tone, which adjusts the display's white balance based on ambient lighting to reduce eye strain, and a P3 wide color gamut for more vibrant and accurate colors in photo and video editing. The third generation in 2018 transitioned to Liquid Retina displays, which use rounded corners and a laminated design to approximate edge-to-edge visuals while maintaining the 264 pixels-per-inch resolution standard across models, such as 23881668 for the 11-inch variant. These panels also added support for Dolby Vision HDR, enabling dynamic range up to 1,000,000:1 for richer highlights and shadows in compatible content.
The tandem OLED Ultra Retina XDR display on the 2024 iPad Pro demonstrating infinite contrast and color vibrancyThe fifth generation in 2021 marked a significant leap with the introduction of Liquid Retina XDR on the 12.9-inch model, utilizing mini-LED backlighting with over 10,000 mini-LEDs organized into more than 2,500 local dimming zones to achieve full-screen brightness of 1,000 nits and peak HDR brightness of 1,600 nits. This technology provides superior contrast and black levels, approaching OLED performance while avoiding burn-in risks, and maintains P3 wide color support. By the seventh generation in 2024, iPad Pro displays shifted to tandem OLED for both 11-inch (24201668 resolution) and 13-inch (27522064 resolution) models under the Ultra Retina XDR branding, utilizing tandem OLED technology that provides perfect blacks, infinite contrast, higher brightness, and more vibrant colors, with 1,000 nits sustained SDR brightness and 1,600 nits peak for HDR content. ProMotion evolved to variable refresh rates from 10Hz to 120Hz for better power efficiency, while retaining True Tone and Dolby Vision compatibility.
Anti-reflective coatings have been standard since the first generation, with reflectivity as low as 1.8% to improve visibility in bright environments. Starting in 2024, an optional nano-texture glass finish became available on higher-storage models, etching the surface at a nanometer scale to scatter ambient light and further minimize glare without compromising image fidelity. The eighth-generation model in 2025 features the identical Ultra Retina XDR tandem OLED displays as the prior generation, with ProMotion 120Hz refresh rate, 1000-1600 nits brightness, and P3 wide color gamut, ensuring consistent high-fidelity visuals across the lineup. The ProMotion 120Hz adaptive refresh rate contributes to smoother gameplay, reduced motion blur, and better responsiveness in demanding games like Genshin Impact and Wuthering Waves compared to the 60Hz displays on non-Pro models such as the iPad Air (M3), entry-level iPad, and iPad mini 7, enhancing the overall gaming experience alongside the M5's GPU power. These display innovations, including low-latency touch response, complement stylus input for precise creative tasks like digital illustration.

Build materials and durability

The iPad Pro utilizes a precision-machined unibody enclosure constructed from high-strength aluminum, which has been the standard design since its introduction in 2015, offering a balance of rigidity, lightness, and thermal efficiency. Beginning with the fifth generation in 2021, the enclosure incorporates 100% recycled aluminum, comprising over 50% of the device's total recycled content by mass, which reduces the environmental impact of material sourcing without compromising structural integrity. This recycled alloy is certified and processed to maintain the same premium quality as virgin materials, contributing to Apple's broader sustainability goals.
While the iPad Pro lacks an official IP rating for water or dust resistance, its display features a fingerprint-resistant oleophobic coating on the glass surface to repel oils and smudges, enhancing usability and ease of cleaning. The front glass provides inherent scratch resistance through its hardened composition, equivalent in performance to advanced tempered glass protections, though Apple recommends avoiding abrasive materials to preserve longevity.
Ultra-thin side view of the 2024 iPad Pro from durability testingIn terms of form factor advancements, the 2024 11-inch model achieves a thickness of 5.3 mm at its slimmest point, with the 13-inch variant at 5.1 mm, enabled by optimized internal component layouts that reduce overall weightsuch as 444 grams for the Wi-Fi 11-inch modelwhile preserving durability. Independent durability assessments, including bend and drop tests from heights up to 4 feet (1.2 meters), demonstrate that recent models withstand moderate impacts without catastrophic failure, though minor dents or screen cracks can occur on unprotected edges. For water exposure, the device is not officially rated but shows splash tolerance in controlled tests, surviving brief contact with liquids if dried promptly, underscoring the value of compatible protective covers for enhanced ruggedness.
On the environmental front, iPad Pro models from the sixth generation (2022) onward achieve carbon footprint reductions of up to 37% compared to baselines through material efficiencies and renewable manufacturing, aligning with Apple's 2030 goal for carbon-neutral products across the lifecycle. Additionally, all magnets, including those in the speakers, utilize 100% recycled rare earth elements, accounting for nearly all rare earth usage in the device and minimizing mining demands. These efforts result in over 30% total recycled content by mass in current models, with certifications like ENERGY STAR for energy efficiency.

Hardware

Processors and graphics

The iPad Pro's processors have evolved significantly since its debut, transitioning from custom ARM-based A-series chips to Apple's in-house silicon with the M-series, enabling desktop-class performance in a tablet form factor. The first-generation model featured the A9X chip, a dual-core processor clocked at up to 2.26 GHz with a 12-cluster PowerVR Series 7XT GT7600 GPU, marking a substantial leap over prior iPad chips by delivering 1.8 times the CPU performance and twice the graphics performance of the A8X.
Internal logic board of the 2018 iPad Pro with A12X Bionic chip exposed during teardownSubsequent generations built on this foundation with more cores and efficiency improvements. The second-generation iPad Pro introduced the A10X Fusion, a hexa-core design (three high-performance Hurricane cores at 2.38 GHz and three efficiency Zephyr cores) paired with a 12-core PowerVR GPU, which offered up to 40% faster CPU performance and 64% better graphics than the A9X, while maintaining 4 GB of unified LPDDR4 memory. The third generation shifted to the A12X Bionic, an octa-core processor (four Vortex performance cores at 2.5 GHz and four Tempest efficiency cores) with a seven-core Apple-designed GPU and an eight-core Neural Engine capable of 5 trillion operations per second (TOPS), providing 35% faster single-core CPU and 90% faster multi-core performance compared to the A10X, with unified memory options of 4 GB or 6 GB.
Video editing in LumaFusion on the M1-powered iPad Pro (2021)The fourth generation refined this architecture with the A12Z Bionic, which retained the same octa-core CPU configuration but upgraded to an eight-core GPU for enhanced graphics rendering, particularly in 4K video editing and augmented reality tasks, while increasing unified memory to 6 GB across all models. This progression culminated in the fifth generation's adoption of the M1 chip, an eight-core CPU (four Firestorm performance cores at 3.2 GHz and four Icestorm efficiency cores) with an eight-core GPU and 16-core Neural Engine delivering 11 TOPS, unified memory starting at 8 GB (up to 16 GB for higher storage), and support for hardware-accelerated machine learning tasks, achieving up to 1.7 times the CPU performance of the A12Z.
The sixth generation's M2 chip advanced further with an eight-core CPU (four performance cores at up to 3.49 GHz and four efficiency cores), a ten-core GPU supporting Metal 3 for advanced graphics effects like dynamic lighting in games, and a 16-core Neural Engine at 15.8 TOPS, paired with 8 GB or 16 GB unified memory and 100 GB/s bandwidth for sustained multitasking without active cooling. The seventh generation introduced the M4, featuring a ten-core CPU variant (four performance and six efficiency cores at up to 4.4 GHz in higher configurations; 9-core with three performance cores in base) with a ten-core GPU enabling hardware-accelerated ray tracing for realistic rendering in pro applications, a 16-core Neural Engine at 38 TOPS for AI workloads, and unified memory of 8 GB (base models) to 16 GB, delivering approximately 1.5 times the CPU performance and 4 times the rendering speed of the M2; it also incorporated a vapor chamber cooling system to maintain peak efficiency during prolonged use. The eighth generation's M5 chip builds on this with a ten-core CPU (four performance and six efficiency cores, up to 4.4 GHz; 9-core in base models with 256 GB and 512 GB storage configurations) paired with 12 GB unified memory, while 1 TB and 2 TB storage models feature a 10-core CPU with 16 GB unified memory, a ten-core GPU with ray tracing support, and an enhanced 16-core Neural Engine delivering up to 3.5 times the AI performance of the M4 for machine learning acceleration, with 153 GB/s bandwidth to handle complex graphics-intensive tasks like real-time 3D modeling without thermal throttling.
The 16 GB unified memory configuration provides improved performance over the 12 GB variant in memory-intensive professional workflows, such as 4K/8K video editing in Final Cut Pro, handling large Logic Pro projects, or managing complex multilayer files in Photoshop and Illustrator. This additional RAM reduces application reloading and supports running more applications simultaneously for enhanced multitasking.
The M5's ten-core GPU incorporates a third-generation ray tracing engine, delivering up to 1.5 times faster 3D rendering with ray tracing compared to the M4 (approximately 50% better performance in supported graphics tasks). In real-world gaming, the iPad Pro with M5 chip significantly outperforms the iPad mini 7 (equipped with the A17 Pro chip) in demanding titles such as Genshin Impact and Wuthering Waves. The M5 enables smooth gameplay at maximum settings, high frame rates, excellent thermal management, and efficient battery use without noticeable throttling during extended sessions. In contrast, the iPad mini 7 runs these games well at high settings with stable performance in shorter sessions but may experience occasional frame drops, stuttering, or throttling during prolonged play due to its less powerful GPU and 60 Hz display. The iPad Pro's superior sustained GPU performance and 120 Hz ProMotion display make it the preferred choice for intensive gaming.

Cameras, sensors, and input

Rear camera array and LiDAR Scanner on the 2020 iPad ProThe iPad Pro's camera system has evolved to support professional-grade imaging and augmented reality applications, with rear configurations varying by generation. The first three generations featured a single rear camera (8 MP in first and second, 12 MP Wide in third). Starting with the fourth generation (2020), the rear featured a dual-camera setup including a 12 MP Wide camera with an /1.8 aperture and a 10 MP Ultra Wide camera with an /2.4 aperture and 125 field of view, enabling 2x optical zoom out and 4K video recording at up to 60 fps, including ProRes formats for high-quality post-production. This setup, paired with a LiDAR Scanner introduced in the fourth generation and continued through subsequent models, facilitated depth sensing for enhanced low-light autofocus and AR experiences. The fifth and sixth generations (2021 and 2022) retained this dual rear camera array and LiDAR integration, maintaining support for 4K ProRes video capture, with the sixth upgrading the Ultra Wide to 12 MP. The seventh generation (2024) retained the dual rear camera array (12 MP Wide and 12 MP Ultra Wide) with LiDAR. However, the eighth (2025) generation streamlined the rear to a single 12 MP Wide camera with an /1.8 aperture, digital zoom up to 5x, and Adaptive True Tone flash, while preserving LiDAR for depth mapping; the Ultra Wide lens was omitted to prioritize a thinner design, though 4K ProRes video capabilities persisted.
The front-facing camera utilizes a TrueDepth system for secure authentication and video calls, evolving into a landscape-oriented design for better usability in horizontal modes. From the fifth generation (2021) onward, it incorporated a 12 MP Ultra Wide camera with an /2.4 aperture, supporting Center Stage for automatic framing in video calls and 1080p HD recording at up to 60 fps. This configuration, enabled by the TrueDepth camera introduced in 2018 and refined across models, powers Face ID biometric recognition using infrared dot projection and flood illuminator for secure unlocking and payments. The sixth through eighth generations maintained this 12 MP Ultra Wide front camera with Center Stage and TrueDepth/Face ID support, ensuring consistent video quality with features like Smart HDR. Face ID serves as the primary biometric method since the third generation (2018), with no Touch ID on iPad Pro models.
Sensors in the iPad Pro provide essential environmental and motion data, with a core set consistent since the early generations and augmented for advanced functionality. All models from the fifth generation (2021) include a three-axis gyroscope for orientation detection, an accelerometer for motion tracking, a barometer for altitude sensing, and ambient light sensors for automatic brightness adjustment. The LiDAR Scanner, present since 2020 and standard through the eighth generation (2025), uses time-of-flight technology to measure distances up to 5 meters, enabling precise depth mapping for AR applications and improved computational photography in low light. Audio input is handled by four studio-quality microphones with directional beamforming, reducing background noise for clearer recordings and calls across all recent generations. Audio output features four speakers for enhanced stereo sound quality, compared to two speakers in iPad Air models. Since the iPad Pro lacks a 3.5 mm headphone jack, USB-C to 3.5 mm adapters provide compatibility for legacy wired audio devices. Apple's official USB-C to 3.5 mm Headphone Jack Adapter supports audio connectivity only and does not include power passthrough for simultaneous charging. Third-party USB-C adapters and multi-port hubs with 3.5 mm audio jacks and Power Delivery (PD) support are available and compatible, enabling users to connect wired headphones while charging the iPad Pro at the same time.
Input mechanisms emphasize intuitive and versatile interaction, building on the device's foundational multi-touch capabilities. The Retina display supports multi-touch gestures for navigation and control, a feature standard since the first iPad Pro in 2015. The Smart Connector, introduced in 2015 and retained through the eighth generation, enables magnetic attachment and power/data passthrough for compatible accessories like keyboards. Connectivity includes USB-C with Thunderbolt support from the fourth generation (2020) onward, allowing high-speed data transfer and external display output. For biometric input, Face ID serves as the primary method from 2018. Computational photography enhancements, such as Deep Fusion for detail-rich images in varied lighting, are integrated into the camera hardware pipeline from the fifth generation.

USB-C port assembly for charging and connectivity in iPad Pro modelsCharging is handled via the USB-C port, primarily using Power Delivery (PD) protocol for optimal speeds, with no support for wireless charging in any iPad Pro model. When using USB-A chargers with USB-A to USB-C cables, iPad Pro models charge at approximately 10-12W, as they recognize these as high-current dedicated charging ports via proprietary signaling (up to 2.4A at ~5V, with minor voltage compensation for drops), but lack PD negotiation for higher voltages, resulting in lower real-world input due to cable resistance and inefficiencies. Early generations, such as the 2022 lineup, support up to 30W input for standard charging, reaching full capacity in about 2.5 hours with a compatible adapter. The 2024 and 2025 models introduce fast-charging capabilities, achieving up to 50% charge in around 30 minutes when paired with a 60W or higher USB-C PD adapter, though Apple includes only a 20W adapter in the box for basic needs. Practical charging speeds for the 13-inch models peak at 35-40W under USB PD conditions using compatible PD chargers (e.g., 9V at 3-4A or 15V at 2.5A) and USB-C to USB-C cables, with the 38.99 Wh battery benefiting from 85-90% efficient DC-DC conversion, reducing total charge time to under 2 hours, but higher-wattage options like 140W remain unconfirmed and unsupported.
Wireless connectivity has evolved across generations to support faster and more efficient networking. All models include Wi-Fi with MIMO technology and Bluetooth for peripherals, alongside optional cellular connectivity via eSIM only, with no physical SIM tray since the first generation. The 2022 models feature Wi-Fi 6 (802.11ax) with 2x2 MIMO and Bluetooth 5.3, paired with 5G sub-6 GHz and mmWave (in U.S. models) cellular using a Qualcomm modem for Gigabit LTE fallback. The 2024 generation upgrades to Wi-Fi 6E for extended 6 GHz band access and retains Bluetooth 5.3, while cellular remains 5G sub-6 GHz with 4x4 MIMO. The 2025 models advance to faster Wi-Fi 7 via Apple's N1 chip for multi-gigabit speeds and lower latency, Bluetooth 5.4 for improved range and power efficiency, and the next-generation proprietary C1X cellular modem, which enables up to 50% faster 5G data rates and 30% better energy efficiency compared to prior Qualcomm-based systems.
The iPad Pro relies on a single USB-C port for all wired connectivity, eliminating the 3.5 mm headphone jack since the 2015 models. This port supports Thunderbolt 3 in the 2022 generation (up to 40 Gbps bidirectional transfer) and full USB 4 in 2024 and 2025 models, enabling compatibility with Thunderbolt 3/4 accessories, USB 3 devices at 10 Gbps, and DisplayPort output. It allows connection to external storage drives for file transfer and backup, as well as high-resolution displaysup to 6K at 60 Hz or 4K at 120 Hz in the latest generationsvia direct USB-C or adapters. The port also powers compatible accessories like external GPUs or hubs while charging the iPad simultaneously.

iPadOS integration

Customized Home Screen on the 2024 iPad Pro demonstrating iPadOS widget placement and icon flexibilityiPadOS, introduced as a distinct operating system in 2019 with iPadOS 13, was specifically designed to leverage the iPad Pro's advanced hardware, starting with enhanced multitasking capabilities such as multi-window support for apps like Safari, allowing users to open multiple instances of the same application simultaneously. This evolution continued through subsequent versions, culminating in iPadOS 18 released in 2024, which introduced greater Home Screen customization options including resizable app icons, tinting, and flexible widget placement to better utilize the iPad Pro's expansive display real estate. These updates prioritize the device's powerful processors and large screens, enabling more desktop-like workflows without compromising touch-based navigation.
iPad Pro extended to an external display in a productivity setupHardware-specific optimizations in iPadOS further tailor the experience to the iPad Pro's capabilities, notably through Stage Manager introduced in iPadOS 16, which supports external display connectivity for extending the workspace beyond the built-in screen. Security in iPadOS is deeply integrated with the iPad Pro's M-series chips, utilizing the Secure Enclave coprocessor to handle encryption keys for full-device data protection, ensuring that user files remain inaccessible even if the device is compromised. This hardware-backed encryption, akin to FileVault on macOS, protects against unauthorized access without requiring separate activation. App sideloading, previously restricted to maintain ecosystem security, became available in the European Union starting with iPadOS 18 in September 2024, in compliance with Digital Markets Act regulations, allowing third-party app stores while preserving core safeguards.

These tools ensure the iPad Pro's power is inclusive, adapting to diverse user needs without additional hardware.

Update and support lifecycle

Apple provides iPad Pro models with a minimum of 5 to 7 years of major iPadOS updates from their initial release, ensuring compatibility with new operating system versions during that period. For instance, the first-generation iPad Pro, released in 2015, received major updates up to iPadOS 16 in 2022, spanning approximately seven years. This policy aligns with Apple's broader approach to software longevity for its tablet lineup, prioritizing devices with more advanced hardware for extended support.
Beyond major version updates, Apple continues to deliver security patches to older iPad Pro models for an additional period, often 1 to 2 years or more after the end of major support, to address critical vulnerabilities. The first-generation 12.9-inch iPad Pro, for example, received security updates such as iPadOS 16.7.12 as late as September 2025, even though it no longer supported major iPadOS releases beyond version 16. Models equipped with M1 chips or later, introduced starting in 2021, are positioned for the longest support windows due to the uniformity and efficiency of Apple's custom silicon architecture, including the eighth-generation iPad Pro released in 2025, which ensures extended compatibility with the latest iPadOS features such as Apple Intelligence integration.
iPad Pro devices typically achieve feature parity with the latest iPadOS releases ahead of other iPad lines, receiving full access to new capabilities upon launch. However, certain advanced features, such as Apple Intelligence introduced in iPadOS 18.1, require at least an M1 chip and are unavailable on A12X devices, excluding older Pro models from AI-driven functionalities like enhanced Siri processing and on-device image generation.
However, older models including the third-generation iPad Pro (2018) with the A12X chip continue to receive the latest iPadOS updates and remain capable of supporting professional applications such as digital drawing in Procreate, video editing in LumaFusion, and multitasking features, although performance may vary compared to newer M-series models and some advanced features may have limitations.
Hardware constraints, including processor architecture and RAM capacity, further influence eligibility for future features; for example, models with less than 8 GB of RAM cannot support Apple Intelligence workloads.
End-of-life for major software support on iPad Pro models is determined by hardware limitations, with A9X-powered devicessuch as the 9.7-inch and first-generation 12.9-inch variantsceasing to receive major updates after iPadOS 16 due to insufficient performance for subsequent versions. These models, released in 2015 and 2016, exemplify how chip generation and memory constraints can cap feature adoption, preventing compatibility with resource-intensive updates.
Under Apple's classification policy, iPad Pro products reach "vintage" status when Apple has stopped distributing them for sale more than 5 but less than 7 years prior, limiting hardware service to authorized providers where parts are available. They become "obsolete" after 7 years, at which point all hardware service is discontinued globally, except where required by law. As of November 2025, the 9.7-inch iPad Pro and first-generation 12.9-inch iPad Pro are classified as obsolete, while the 10.5-inch and second-generation 12.9-inch models hold vintage status. This timeline is calculated from the last date of sale in each country or region.

Across iPad Pro generations, reviewers consistently highlight superior build quality and display innovation as key strengths, with outlets like WIRED and PCMag commending the premium aluminum construction and color-accurate screens that excel in creative and media tasks. Common criticisms focus on iPadOS limitations compared to macOS, such as restricted file management and multitasking, which The Verge and Ars Technica argue prevent it from fully replacing laptops for power users. Accessory dependency, including costly keyboards and styluses, is another frequent drawback, as noted in multiple CNET and Verge analyses that stress the need for add-ons to unlock productivity potential.
Review aggregates from sites like PCMag and The Verge place recent generations in the 85-95 range out of 100, with emphasis on appeal to professional users in design, video editing, and illustration who benefit from the hardware's power.
Reviews of the M5 iPad Pro, published in late 2025, highlight its enhanced AI processing speeds, with WIRED noting faster image generation in apps like Draw Things compared to the M4, alongside improved efficiency for battery-intensive tasks. Macworld awarded 5 out of 5 for the chip's AI and graphics boosts, though Ars Technica described upgrades as incrementalaround 10-15% in benchmarkspositioning it as a refined option for pro users rather than a revolutionary leap.

Commercial performance

The iPad Pro line has been a key driver of Apple's tablet sales, with cumulative iPad shipments exceeding 600 million units worldwide by the end of 2023. The Pro models have consistently captured around 40-45% of total iPad sales in recent years, reflecting their appeal to professional and creative users. In the second quarter of 2024, Apple shipped 12.3 million iPads globally, a year-over-year increase of 18.2%, largely propelled by demand for the updated iPad Pro with M4 chip.
Apple's pricing strategy positions the iPad Pro as a premium product, starting at higher price points that preserve strong gross marginsoften above 40% for the tablet categorywhile differentiating it from lower-end competitors. This approach was reinforced with the October 2025 launch of the M5-powered iPad Pro. The global tablet market saw approximately 9% year-over-year growth in the second quarter of 2025, according to industry trackers. In Q3 2025, worldwide tablet shipments grew 5.2% year-over-year to 38 million units, with Apple shipping 13.2 million iPads.
In the premium tablet segment, Apple commands over 50% global market share as of late 2025, outpacing rivals like Samsung's Galaxy Tab S series, which holds about 25-30% in high-end shipments. This dominance stems from the iPad Pro's ecosystem integration and performance advantages, though Samsung provides competitive alternatives in Android-based premium devices.
The iPad Pro contributed nearly $27 billion to Apple's fiscal 2024 revenue, representing over 7% of the company's total hardware and services income and underscoring its role in sustaining profitability amid broader tablet market fluctuations. Education pricing discounts, offering up to 10% off for students and institutions, have notably increased adoption in academic settings, with the Pro models popular for professional-grade tasks in higher education.
Regionally, the iPad Pro performs strongly in the United States and Europe, where it accounts for a significant portion of premium tablet sales driven by professional and creative professionals, supported by Apple's 42% revenue share from the Americas. In contrast, penetration in emerging markets remains slower, limited by the device's higher cost relative to local purchasing power, despite gains in areas like India and Southeast Asia.

Cultural and industry impact

The iPad Pro has repositioned tablets as professional-grade tools within creative industries. In film production, professionals utilize it as a reference monitor during post-production, enabled by the Reference Mode in iPadOS 16, which delivers precise color accuracy for on-set grading and review. Its support for Apple ProRes codec facilitates high-quality video capture and real-time editing, allowing filmmakers to transition seamlessly from pre-production scoutingusing the large Retina display for location photos and team collaborationto on-set monitoring and post workflows. In graphic design, Adobe's native apps like Illustrator integrate directly with the iPad Pro, enabling vector-based creation of logos, illustrations, and layouts using the Apple Pencil for natural drawing, with projects syncing effortlessly to desktop via Creative Cloud for collaborative workflows.
Within education, the iPad Pro supports widespread adoption in schools through programs providing devices for digital textbooks and interactive curricula, reducing reliance on print materials while enabling personalized learning environments. Built-in accessibility features, such as VoiceOver for screen reading, AssistiveTouch for motor challenges, and Live Text for cognitive support, promote inclusivity by accommodating students with visual, hearing, mobility, or learning disabilities, thereby broadening educational access.
The iPad Pro has driven industry-wide advancements in tablet technology, notably by pioneering ProMotion with 120Hz adaptive refresh rates in 2017, which set a benchmark for fluid scrolling and responsiveness that competitors like Samsung and Microsoft later incorporated into their premium models. The 2024 model's tandem OLED display further elevated standards for contrast and color in mobile screens, compelling rivals to accelerate OLED adoption and high-refresh-rate implementations. Its modular design, enhanced by accessories like the Magic Keyboard, has influenced hybrid devices such as the Microsoft Surface Pro, intensifying competition in the 2-in-1 category and blurring distinctions between tablets and laptops.
Culturally, the iPad Pro has empowered artists to produce and disseminate digital works, as evidenced by Apple's initiatives showcasing illustrators creating NBA portraits, urban sketches, and immersive installations directly on the device. It has fueled public discourse and memes debating its viability as a laptop replacement, with tech analyses highlighting iPadOS enhancements that challenge traditional computing paradigms. The 2024 "Crush" advertisement, which symbolized compressing creative tools into the slim device, provoked backlash from artists and media for seemingly undervaluing physical artistry, underscoring broader tensions between innovation and tradition.
As a cornerstone of Apple's professional lineup, the iPad Pro's legacy encompasses its role in shaping an interconnected ecosystem, where shared silicon like the M-series chips enables fluid transitions between devices, directly informing the Apple Vision Pro's AR interfaces and creative applications.

Model variants

9.7-inch iPad Pro, a discontinued variant from 2016 with rose gold finishThe iPad Pro line has offered various configurations since its introduction, with storage options evolving from 32 GB in early models to up to 2 TB in recent generations, alongside choices for Wi-Fi-only or cellular connectivity. Initial models, such as the first-generation 12.9-inch iPad Pro released in 2015, provided 32 GB, 128 GB, and 256 GB storage capacities, available in Wi-Fi-only or Wi-Fi + Cellular variants that supported nano-SIM cards. The brief 9.7-inch model from 2016 mirrored these storage tiers and connectivity options but was discontinued in June 2017 following the launch of larger successors.
11-inch and 12.9-inch iPad Pro (3rd generation, 2018) in silver and space graySubsequent generations expanded storage and refined connectivity, incorporating eSIM support alongside nano-SIM for cellular models starting around 2018. The second-generation models in 2017 (10.5-inch and 12.9-inch) offered 64 GB, 256 GB, and 512 GB storage, with the 10.5-inch variant discontinued after the 2018 release of the third-generation lineup. From the third generation onward (2018present), configurations typically include Wi-Fi-only and Wi-Fi + Cellular options, with cellular models using eSIM exclusively in later years for global roaming flexibility. Colors have varied across releases, with early models featuring Silver, Space Gray, Gold (20152017), and Rose Gold (2017), transitioning to standard Silver and Space Gray pairings, and more recently Space Black introduced in 2024.
Apple provides special editions through education pricing bundles, offering discounted configurations for students and educators, often including bundled accessories like the Apple Pencil. Refurbished options are available directly from Apple, featuring certified pre-owned units across various past configurations with a one-year warranty, such as 128 GB to 1 TB storage in Silver or Space Gray for third- through sixth-generation models.
As of November 2025, the current eighth-generation iPad Pro (M5 chip) is available in 11-inch and 13-inch sizes, starting at 256 GB storage and scaling to 512 GB, 1 TB, and 2 TB, with Wi-Fi-only or Wi-Fi + Cellular connectivity. The model name displayed in Settings > General > About for the English version of the iPad Pro 11-inch (M5) is "iPad Pro 11-inch (M5)"; the Model Number initially shows the order number (e.g., MVxxxLL/A), and tapping it reveals the hardware identifier, such as A3357 for Wi-Fi models, A3358 for Wi-Fi + Cellular, and A3359 for Wi-Fi + Cellular models in China mainland. These models come in Silver and Space Black, and include an optional nano-texture glass display on 1 TB and 2 TB configurations for reduced glare.
