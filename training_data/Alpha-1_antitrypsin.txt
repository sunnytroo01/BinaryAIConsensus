 Alpha-1 antitrypsin (AAT), also known as alpha-1 proteinase inhibitor, is a 52 kDa glycoprotein belonging to the serpin superfamily of serine protease inhibitors, primarily synthesized in hepatocytes and secreted into the bloodstream where it circulates at concentrations of 0.92 g/L in healthy adults. Its core physiological function is to inhibit neutrophil elastase and other serine proteases released by inflammatory cells, thereby protecting the elastic fibers in the lower respiratory tract from proteolytic degradation during inflammation or infection. Encoded by the SERPINA1 gene on chromosome 14q32.13, AAT features a characteristic structure comprising nine -helices, three -sheets, and a reactive center loop that acts as a "molecular mousetrap" to irreversibly bind and neutralize target proteases. Beyond its antiprotease role, AAT exhibits multifaceted activities, including modulation of inflammation, immune responses, apoptosis, and proteostasis, positioning it as an acute-phase reactant that increases during systemic inflammation.
Mutations in the SERPINA1 gene cause alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency (AATD), an autosomal codominant disorder characterized by reduced circulating AAT levels or production of dysfunctional variants, leading to unchecked protease activity and tissue damage. The most common pathogenic alleles are Z (Glu342Lys) and S (Glu264Val), with the ZZ genotype resulting in severe deficiency (serum levels <11 mol/L or ~20% of normal) and high risk for disease manifestation. AATD affects approximately 1 in 1,500 to 3,500 individuals of European ancestry, though it is underdiagnosed, particularly among those with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD); prevalence is lower in Asian and African populations. In the lungs, deficiency predisposes to early-onset panacinar emphysema, dyspnea, wheezing, and recurrent infections, typically appearing between ages 2550, exacerbated by tobacco smoke. Hepatic involvement arises from polymerized misfolded AAT retention in hepatocytes, causing neonatal jaundice in ~10% of cases, cirrhosis in ~15% of adults, and increased hepatocellular carcinoma risk.
Diagnosis of AATD involves measuring serum AAT levels, followed by genotyping or phenotyping via isoelectric focusing to identify variants, with screening recommended for individuals with unexplained COPD, emphysema before age 45, or liver disease. Management focuses on supportive care, including smoking cessation, bronchodilators, and vaccinations to prevent respiratory infections; augmentation therapy with intravenous purified AAT (e.g., Prolastin) is FDA-approved for severe lung disease to maintain protective levels. For advanced liver failure, orthotopic liver transplantation is curative, as it restores normal AAT production; emerging therapies target gene correction or polymer clearance. Despite its rarity, AATD underscores the critical balance of protease-antiprotease activity in organ homeostasis and highlights ongoing research into AAT's broader therapeutic potential in inflammatory and metabolic disorders.

Nomenclature and Genetics

Nomenclature

Alpha-1 antitrypsin, also known as alpha-1-proteinase inhibitor, is the recommended name for this serine protease inhibitor protein encoded by the SERPINA1 gene. This nomenclature reflects its primary function in inhibiting proteases such as elastase and trypsin, distinguishing it from other plasma proteins. In systematic biochemical classification, it is designated as serpin peptidase inhibitor, clade A (alpha-1 antiproteinase, antitrypsin), member 1 (SERPINA1), highlighting its membership in the serpin superfamily of proteins that undergo conformational changes to inhibit target enzymes.
Historically, the protein was first isolated in 1955 and referred to as serum trypsin inhibitor due to its observed inhibition of trypsin activity in plasma. This early designation, sometimes abbreviated as STI in older literature, emphasized its antiprotease properties before its broader role was understood. Over time, the name evolved to alpha-1-antitrypsin to specify its position in the alpha-1 globulin fraction of serum protein electrophoresis and its specific inhibition of trypsin-like enzymes. Alternative names, such as alpha-1-antiproteinase or alpha-1 protease inhibitor, are also used interchangeably in clinical and research contexts to underscore its inhibitory mechanism against various serine proteases.
Common abbreviations for the protein include A1AT and AAT, which are widely adopted in medical literature and diagnostic testing to denote alpha-1 antitrypsin. Within the alpha-1 globulin fraction of plasma proteins, alpha-1 antitrypsin constitutes the predominant component, comprising about 90% of this electrophoretic band, in contrast to other alpha-1 globulins like alpha-1-acid glycoprotein (also known as orosomucoid), which is an acute-phase reactant with distinct immunomodulatory functions unrelated to protease inhibition. This distinction is crucial for interpreting serum protein electrophoresis patterns, where alpha-1 antitrypsin levels specifically influence the intensity of the alpha-1 band.

Genetics

The alpha-1 antitrypsin protein is encoded by the SERPINA1 gene, located on the long arm of chromosome 14 at position 14q32.13. This gene spans approximately 14 kb and consists of seven exons, including four coding exons (II-V) and three untranslated exons (Ia, Ib, Ic) in the 5' region, separated by six introns.
Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency follows an autosomal codominant inheritance pattern, meaning that both alleles are expressed, and the phenotype reflects the combination of variants present. The protease inhibitor (Pi) system classifies these phenotypes based on electrophoretic mobility, with over 50 alleles identified, though a few predominate in clinical contexts.
The most common normal allele is PiM, which produces typical serum levels of alpha-1 antitrypsin (approximately 1-2 g/L). The PiZ allele, responsible for severe deficiency, and the PiS allele, associated with mild deficiency, are the primary pathogenic variants. In populations of European descent, the PiZ allele frequency ranges from 1% to 4%, with higher rates (up to 4%) in northern Europeans, while the PiS allele frequency is approximately 56% in southern Europe, with peaks up to 14% in Iberian populations. These alleles are less prevalent in non-Caucasian populations, with PiZ frequencies below 0.1% in Asian and African groups.
The PiZ allele arises from a single point mutation in exon V of SERPINA1, substituting glutamic acid for lysine at position 342 (Glu342Lys; rs28929474), which promotes abnormal protein polymerization and retention in hepatocytes. The PiS allele results from a Glu264Val substitution (rs17580) in exon III. Homozygous PiZZ genotypes, which cause severe deficiency, occur in approximately 1 in 2,500 to 3,500 individuals of European ancestry worldwide. Recent epidemiological data indicate significant underdiagnosis; for instance, a 2025 Norwegian study estimated diagnosed prevalence at 10.7 per 100,000, far below the expected genetic rate of about 62 per 100,000 based on allele frequencies. Similarly, among over 12 million U.S. Veterans screened through 2025, only 3,088 severe cases were identified despite low testing rates (about 1.5%), underscoring underdiagnosis in high-risk groups. Carrier rates for PiZ (heterozygotes) are estimated at 3-4% in northern European populations.

Molecular Structure and Function

Structure

Alpha-1 antitrypsin (AAT) is a ~52 kDa glycoprotein composed of a single polypeptide chain of 394 amino acids. Its primary structure includes a reactive center loop (RCL) at the C-terminus, featuring a P1-P1' cleavage site between Met358 and Ser359, which is critical for its structural dynamics.
The tertiary structure of AAT belongs to the serpin superfamily, characterized by a characteristic fold consisting of three -sheets (A, B, and C), nine -helices (A through I), and a mobile RCL protruding from the top of the molecule. Beta-sheet A forms the core scaffold, with the RCL held above it in a metastable conformation that can undergo insertion into the sheet upon activation. Crystal structures of the native form, such as PDB entry 3NE4 at 1.8 resolution, reveal the intact, uncleaved state with the RCL exposed, while structures of the cleaved form, like PDB entry 1D5S, show the RCL fully inserted into -sheet A as the fourth strand, resulting in a stable six-stranded sheet.
Post-translational modifications of AAT include N-linked glycosylation at three asparagine residues: Asn46, Asn83, and Asn247. These glycan attachments, which account for approximately 12-15% of the protein's mass, enhance thermodynamic stability by shielding hydrophobic regions and reducing susceptibility to thermal and chemical denaturation.
In pathological variants like the Z allele (Glu342Lys), AAT forms loop-sheet polymers through intermolecular insertion of the RCL of one molecule as the fourth strand into the partially opened -sheet A of another. The Glu342Lys mutation at the base of the RCL (position P14) disrupts a key salt bridge with nearby residues, increasing the mobility of strand 5A and facilitating -sheet A expansion to accommodate the incoming loop; this is stabilized by hydrogen bonding between the inserted RCL backbone and acceptor strands in sheet A, as well as hydrophobic interactions involving residues like Phe352 and surrounding -strands. Such polymerization leads to intracellular retention and aggregation.

Function

Alpha-1 antitrypsin (AAT) serves as the archetypal member of the serpin superfamily, primarily functioning to irreversibly inhibit serine proteases through a suicide substrate mechanism. This inhibition targets neutrophil elastase (NE), a key enzyme released by activated neutrophils during inflammation. The reactive center loop (RCL) of AAT acts as a pseudosubstrate, presenting a methionine residue (P1 position) that mimics the protease's natural substrate; upon cleavage by NE, the RCL inserts into -sheet A of the serpin core, translocating and distorting the covalently bound protease to inactivate it permanently. This process induces a conformational shift in AAT from a metastable stressed state to a stable relaxed state, ensuring efficient protease neutralization.
The kinetics of AAT inhibition follow a two-step mechanism involving initial non-covalent Michaelis complex formation followed by rapid acylation and loop insertion, characterized by a second-order association rate constant. For NE, this rate is approximately $ 6.5 \times 10^{7} , \mathrm{M}^{-1} \mathrm{s}^{-1} $, reflecting AAT's high specificity and potency in protecting tissues from proteolytic damage. AAT also inhibits other neutrophil-derived serine proteases, such as proteinase 3 (with $ k_{\mathrm{assoc}} \approx 8.1 \times 10^{6} , \mathrm{M}^{-1} \mathrm{s}^{-1} $) and cathepsin G (with $ k_{\mathrm{assoc}} \approx 4.1 \times 10^{5} , \mathrm{M}^{-1} \mathrm{s}^{-1} $), contributing to a broader regulation of inflammatory protease activity.
In addition to direct protease inhibition, AAT exerts anti-inflammatory effects independent of its antiprotease activity, including modulation of NF-B signaling pathways to suppress pro-inflammatory cytokine production in immune cells. It promotes wound healing by enhancing re-epithelialization through regulation of epithelial cell migration and proliferation at injury sites. AAT further regulates apoptosis by inhibiting caspase activation and protecting various cell types, such as -cells and bronchial epithelial cells, from programmed cell death during stress.
AAT is predominantly synthesized in hepatocytes at a rate of approximately 34 mg/kg body weight per day and secreted into the bloodstream, achieving normal plasma concentrations of 12 g/L (or 2052 M). Circulating AAT is distributed to peripheral tissues, including the lungs and liver, via receptor-mediated uptake, where it accumulates locally to maintain protease-antiprotease balance. Physiologically, this balance safeguards elastin fibers in pulmonary alveoli and connective tissues from degradation by neutrophil proteases during routine immune responses, preventing excessive tissue remodeling.

Pathophysiology and Clinical Significance

Deficiency Mechanisms

Alpha-1 antitrypsin (AAT) deficiency arises from mutations in the SERPINA1 gene that impair the production, secretion, or function of the AAT protein, a key serine protease inhibitor. Over 100 variants of the SERPINA1 gene have been identified, but the most clinically significant severe deficiencies arise from the PiZ allele (Glu342Lys substitution), which accounts for the majority of severe cases and leads to misfolding of the AAT protein during biosynthesis in hepatocytes, and null alleles that prevent any AAT production. Rare dysfunctional variants, such as PiBaghdad (Ala336Pro), allow secretion of AAT but result in a protein with reduced inhibitory activity.
In PiZ deficiency, the misfolded Z variant AAT is retained in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) of hepatocytes, where it undergoes aberrant polymerization into ordered chain-like aggregates via a loop-sheet interaction mechanism. This polymerization triggers the unfolded protein response (UPR), a cellular stress pathway that attempts to restore ER homeostasis but can lead to apoptosis if unresolved, and activates autophagy as a degradative pathway to clear the accumulated polymers. Only 10-15% of Z AAT is secreted into the plasma in homozygous PiZZ individuals, with the remainder degraded by the proteasome or retained intracellularly. For the PiBaghdad variant, AAT is secreted but the monomer exhibits only 37% activity compared to wild-type, requiring 2.7-fold excess for equivalent protease inhibition and conferring 85-95% loss of function against neutrophil elastase (NE). Null alleles, such as PiNull, prevent any AAT synthesis or cause rapid intracellular degradation, yielding undetectable plasma levels and conferring high risk for lung disease without liver involvement.
The deficiency disrupts the protease-antiprotease balance primarily in the lungs, where low circulating AAT levels fail to neutralize NE released by neutrophils during inflammation, leading to unchecked proteolytic degradation of alveolar extracellular matrix and progressive tissue destruction. In the liver, the toxic gain-of-function from polymer accumulation induces hepatocyte stress, inflammation, and fibrosis through ER overload and impaired autophagy, though not all Pi*ZZ individuals develop liver pathology due to incomplete penetrance. Additionally, in smokers, oxidative stress from cigarette smoke inactivates the limited available AAT by methionyl residue oxidation, further exacerbating NE activity and accelerating lung damage.
Plasma AAT levels in PiZZ individuals are typically 10-15% of normal (approximately 0.2 g/L or 3-6 M), compared to the normal range of 1.0-2.8 g/L (20-53 M). A circulating AAT concentration below 11 M is considered the threshold for increased risk of lung emphysema, as it insufficiently protects against proteolytic injury. Environmental factors like smoking markedly worsen outcomes by hastening emphysema onset in deficient individuals, while polygenic modifiers, such as variants in autophagy-related genes (e.g., MTMR12 and FAM134A), influence the penetrance and severity of liver disease in PiZ carriers.

Associated Diseases

Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency (AATD) predominantly manifests as early-onset panacinar emphysema in the lungs, characterized by destruction of alveolar walls and accelerated decline in forced expiratory volume in one second (FEV1). In individuals with the severe PI*ZZ genotype, a high proportion develop emphysema, with onset typically occurring between 30 and 50 years, even among never-smokers; radiographic emphysema is present in approximately 90% of smokers and 65% of nonsmokers.
Liver disease associated with AATD includes neonatal cholestasis, affecting about 17% of PIZZ infants, with 3% progressing to severe fibrosis, liver failure, or death by age 18. In adults with PIZZ, the risk of cirrhosis is markedly elevatedup to 20 times higher than the general populationwith a prevalence of around 7-10% in cohorts, often presenting at an average age of 61; this genotype also carries an increased risk of hepatocellular carcinoma, observed in about 2% over long-term follow-up.
Other conditions linked to AATD encompass rare extrapulmonary manifestations such as necrotizing panniculitis, which presents as ulcerative skin lesions due to unchecked neutrophilic activity, and systemic vasculitis, including aneurysms. Additionally, rare associations exist with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) and anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic antibody (ANCA)-associated vasculitis, potentially exacerbated by deficient protease inhibition.
Epidemiologically, severe AATD (PI*ZZ) has a global incidence of 1 in 3,000 to 5,000 live births, affecting over 235,000 individuals worldwide, with the highest prevalence in populations of European ancestry (about 50% of cases in Europe). As of 2025, underdiagnosis rates remain alarmingly high at approximately 90% in the US and Europe, driven by low clinician awareness and testing barriers, which exacerbate ethnic disparities as the condition is rarer and less recognized in non-Caucasian groups.
Risk factors for disease progression in AATD involve critical genotype-environment interactions, where smoking multiplies the risk of lung disease up to 20-fold in PI*ZZ individuals by hastening FEV1 decline (e.g., 316 mL/year in smokers versus 52-86 mL/year overall). Onset differs by organ and genotype: pediatric presentations are more common for liver cholestasis in infants, while adult-onset predominates for emphysema, further influenced by factors like occupational exposures and male sex.

Diagnosis

Laboratory Analysis

Laboratory analysis of alpha-1 antitrypsin (AAT) primarily involves quantifying protein levels and assessing functional activity in clinical samples to diagnose deficiency and guide management. Protein quantification is typically performed using nephelometry or turbidimetry, which measure AAT concentration in serum or plasma through light scattering from antigen-antibody complexes. Normal serum AAT levels range from 0.9 to 2.0 g/L, with values below 0.8 g/L suggesting possible deficiency. For phenotyping, isoelectric focusing (IEF) electrophoresis serves as the gold standard method, separating AAT variants based on their isoelectric points to identify alleles such as M, S, or Z.
Functional activity is evaluated through elastase inhibition assays, which quantify AAT's capacity to inhibit neutrophil elastase, distinguishing between antigenic (total protein) and functional levels. Serum or plasma is the preferred sample type for these assays, as they provide stable AAT measurements, though dried blood spots can be used with nephelometry for low-volume screening. A key pitfall is AAT's role as an acute-phase reactant, where levels can elevate up to twofold during inflammation or infection, potentially masking deficiency; thus, testing should avoid acute illness periods.
Screening protocols follow guidelines from the American Thoracic Society (ATS) and European Respiratory Society (ERS), recommending AAT testing in at-risk populations such as individuals with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) diagnosed before age 45, unexplained liver disease, or family history of AAT deficiency. Interpretation of results correlates AAT levels with clinical severity; for example, the Z phenotype (Pi*ZZ) typically yields serum levels around 20% of normal, associated with increased risk of emphysema and reduced forced expiratory volume in 1 second (FEV1). Lower AAT levels in deficient individuals show a negative correlation with FEV1, reflecting greater lung function impairment. Abnormal protein or activity results warrant brief reference to genetic testing for confirmation, as detailed elsewhere.

Genetic Testing

Genetic testing for alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency (AATD) primarily involves molecular analysis of the SERPINA1 gene to identify pathogenic variants responsible for reduced or dysfunctional alpha-1 antitrypsin (AAT) protein levels. Common methods include polymerase chain reaction (PCR)-based sequencing of the SERPINA1 exons, which allows for comprehensive detection of mutations across the gene's coding regions, particularly recommended as the initial test in individuals with high clinical suspicion. Targeted genotyping, often via PCR-restriction fragment length polymorphism or allele-specific amplification, focuses on prevalent alleles such as PIZ (Glu342Lys) and PIS (Glu264Val), which account for the majority of severe deficiency cases in populations of European descent and offer high sensitivity and specificity exceeding 99% for these variants. For rare or novel variants, next-generation sequencing (NGS) of the SERPINA1 gene or targeted gene panels is employed to expand detection beyond common alleles, enabling identification of over 100 known pathogenic variants.
Testing is indicated for confirmatory diagnosis following abnormal serum AAT levels or protein phenotyping suggestive of deficiency, as detailed in laboratory analysis protocols. Family screening is recommended for first-degree relatives of probands, including siblings, parents, and offspring, through cascade testing programs that systematically identify at-risk family members. In clinical practice, indications extend to individuals with unexplained chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) at diagnosis, adult-onset asthma with persistent airflow obstruction, or cryptogenic liver disease, where genetic testing refines risk stratification beyond protein-based assays.
Genetic counseling is integral to testing, providing risk assessment for inheritance patterns in autosomal codominant SERPINA1 disorders; for example, offspring of two PIMZ carriers face a 25% chance of inheriting the PIZZ genotype, associated with severe deficiency. Counseling addresses ethical considerations, such as potential psychological impacts like anxiety in asymptomatic individuals and implications for insurability, though protections like the Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (GINA) in the United States mitigate some concerns. Prenatal or preimplantation testing is generally not recommended due to variable disease penetrance and expressivity, emphasizing informed consent and discussion of lifestyle modifications over predictive outcomes.
Limitations of genetic testing include incomplete detection of rare or null alleles not covered by targeted panels, potentially missing up to 5% of cases, and reduced cost-effectiveness in low-prevalence settings, where initial phenotyping is preferred per 2025 guidelines to triage candidates. According to the Canadian Thoracic Society (CTS) 2025 clinical practice guideline, while DNA sequencing is favored for high-suspicion cases, serum AAT level measurement followed by sequencing if levels are low (<1.2 g/L) balances accuracy and resource use in moderate-risk scenarios.
Population screening for AATD is not routinely advocated due to its low prevalence (approximately 1 in 2000-5000 in high-risk populations) and the lack of curative interventions altering carrier status, but targeted screening is endorsed in high-risk groups such as those with early-onset emphysema or family history. Debates center on expanding cascade testing in relatives to improve early detection, balanced against the psychosocial burdens of identifying presymptomatic carriers, with guidelines from the American Thoracic Society/European Respiratory Society (ATS/ERS) and CTS recommending discussion of benefits in adolescents and adults from affected families.

Treatment

Augmentation Therapy

Augmentation therapy involves the intravenous administration of exogenous alpha-1 antitrypsin (AAT) derived from human plasma to raise serum and lung levels in individuals with severe AAT deficiency, aiming to inhibit neutrophil elastase and slow emphysema progression. Approved products include Prolastin-C, Zemaira, and Glassia, all pasteurized and purified from human plasma, administered intravenously in the United States; in the European Union, Respreeza is also available as a plasma-derived formulation. These therapies are indicated for chronic maintenance in adults with documented AAT deficiency and evidence of emphysema, but they provide no benefit for liver disease associated with the condition.
The standard dosing regimen is 60 mg/kg body weight administered weekly via intravenous infusion over approximately 30-45 minutes, targeting trough serum AAT levels above 11 M to protect lung tissue. This frequency accounts for AAT's half-life of about 3-5 days, ensuring sustained anti-elastase activity in the lower respiratory tract. Aerosolized AAT delivery has been explored in clinical studies for direct lung targeting with potentially lower doses, but it remains investigational with limited adoption due to challenges in achieving uniform deposition and long-term efficacy data.
Efficacy data from the RAPID trial, a randomized, placebo-controlled study of 180 patients with severe AAT deficiency, demonstrated that weekly 60 mg/kg infusions slowed the annual loss of lung density on computed tomography by 0.8 g/L compared to placebo (p=0.03), indicating preservation of parenchymal tissue and reduced emphysema progression. Observational registry studies, including the National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute Registry, have shown a slower rate of forced expiratory volume in 1 second (FEV1) decline with augmentation therapy (e.g., -53 mL/year versus -75 mL/year in untreated Pi*ZZ individuals, p=0.02), particularly in non-smokers with moderate airflow obstruction. However, randomized trials like RAPID found no significant impact on FEV1 decline overall, though post-hoc analyses suggest benefits in subgroups without smoking history.
Safety profiles are favorable, with low immunogenicity due to the human-derived nature of the products; adverse events are typically mild, such as headache or infusion-site reactions, occurring in less than 5% of administrations. Patients with IgA deficiency require screening and monitoring, as they face a risk of anaphylactic reactions from trace IgA in plasma-derived preparations. No transmission of blood-borne viruses has been reported since implementation of donor screening and pasteurization. Annual costs for therapy approximate $100,000-$130,000 per patient, primarily driven by the infused product, though real-world analyses indicate overall medical expenditures are substantially higher among users due to comorbidities.
According to guidelines from the Alpha-1 Foundation and the Canadian Thoracic Society (as of 2025), augmentation therapy is recommended for adults with Pi*ZZ or null phenotypes, serum AAT levels below 11 M, and moderate-to-severe lung disease evidenced by FEV1 of 30-65% predicted, after confirmation of airflow obstruction via spirometry. Therapy should be initiated in non-smokers or ex-smokers without recent tobacco exposure, with ongoing monitoring of FEV1 and serum AAT levels to assess response. For advanced liver failure in AATD, orthotopic liver transplantation is the curative standard, as it restores normal AAT production from donor hepatocytes.

Emerging Therapies

Emerging therapies for alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency (AATD) emphasize curative strategies targeting the underlying genetic defect in the SERPINA1 gene, particularly the Pi*Z mutation, with a surge in investigational approaches since 2024. These include RNA interference (RNAi) therapeutics, base editing, CRISPR-based gene editing, RNA editing, small-molecule correctors, and regenerative cell therapies, aiming to restore functional AAT production in the liver and lungs while mitigating polymer accumulation. Clinical advancements in 2025 have demonstrated proof-of-concept for genetic corrections, shifting focus from lifelong protein replacement to potential one-time treatments.
RNAi therapies represent a leading class of gene-silencing approaches delivered via subcutaneous injection to reduce mutant Z-AAT production in the liver. Belcesiran, an investigational GalXC RNAi conjugate, is in phase 2 (ESTRELLA trial, NCT04764448), evaluating safety, tolerability, pharmacokinetics, and pharmacodynamics in adults with PiZZ AATD-associated liver disease. Similarly, fazirsiran, another siRNA therapeutic, advanced in the phase 2 SEQUOIA trial (NCT03945292), with publications from 2025 confirming up to 94% reduction in serum Z-AAT levels and improved liver histology in PiZZ patients with fibrosis, supporting its progression toward phase 3 for liver disease management.
Base editing technologies offer precise single-nucleotide corrections to convert the Z allele to the functional M allele. Beam Therapeutics' BEAM-302, an AAV-delivered base editor, reported positive phase 1/2 data in March 2025, achieving the first clinical genetic correction of the Pi*Z mutation in AATD patients; initial results from low-dose cohorts showed up to 30% editing efficiency in hepatocytes, leading to normalized AAT levels in serum and reduced liver polymers, with durable expression observed at six months post-infusion and no severe adverse events. Additional April 2025 data confirmed lung delivery feasibility, addressing dual organ pathology.
CRISPR-based editing is advancing toward clinical translation for permanent SERPINA1 correction. CRISPR Therapeutics' CTX460, utilizing the novel SyNTase platform for simultaneous nicking and template integration, demonstrated over 90% in vivo mRNA correction in preclinical PiZ mouse models in October 2025, restoring wild-type AAT secretion in liver and reducing polymer burden without detectable off-target edits; the program is slated for phase 1 entry in mid-2026. RNA editing approaches provide transient, reversible corrections by altering AAT mRNA to evade the Z mutation's effects. Wave Life Sciences' WVE-006, an ADAR-mediated RNA editor, showed durable restoration of functional AAT production in the phase 1/2 RestorAATion-2 trial in September 2025, with up to 50% wild-type AAT in PiZZ patients' serum after single dosing, highlighting potential for non-permanent therapy in early disease stages.
Small-molecule therapies target protein misfolding and degradation pathways. Sanofi's efdoralprin alfa, a recombinant AAT variant designed for enhanced stability, met all primary and key secondary endpoints in the phase 2 ElevAATe trial in October 2025, demonstrating superior lung function preservation compared to standard augmentation in emphysema patients, with a 25% reduction in annual FEV1 decline and favorable tolerability profile. Autophagy inducers, such as TFEB activators or LRRK2 inhibitors, are in preclinical stages to enhance clearance of hepatic Z-AAT polymers; 2025 studies showed LRRK2 modulation increased autophagosome formation, reducing polymer load by 40% in hepatocyte models without toxicity.
Regenerative approaches include stem cell-derived AAT-producing cells. Induced pluripotent stem cell (iPSC)-derived hepatocytes from AATD patients have shown promise in preclinical models, secreting functional AAT and engrafting in liver to alleviate polymer accumulation, with 2025 regenerative therapy reviews emphasizing their role in personalized transplantation. As of April 2025, over 30 active clinical trials investigate AATD therapies, with 18 recruiting participants across phases. Challenges include ensuring long-term durability of genetic edits, minimizing off-target effects in non-hepatic tissues, and optimizing delivery for lung involvement, yet these innovations hold curative potential, potentially eliminating the need for chronic interventions.

History

Discovery and Early Research

In 1963, Swedish researchers Carl-Bertil Laurell and Sten Eriksson identified alpha-1 antitrypsin (AAT) deficiency through routine serum protein electrophoresis, noting the absence or marked reduction of the alpha-1 globulin band in five patients with emphysema, which led to the recognition of this condition as an inherited disorder predisposing to early-onset lung disease. Their seminal observation, based on screening over 1,500 samples, highlighted the link between low AAT levels and panacinar emphysema, prompting further investigation into its genetic basis.
During the 1960s and 1970s, studies established the genetic underpinnings of AAT deficiency. In 1969, Howard Sharp and colleagues described periodic acid-Schiff-positive diastase-resistant inclusions in hepatocytes of children with cirrhosis, confirming AAT deficiency as a cause of liver disease and demonstrating its autosomal codominant inheritance. Concurrently, Magne K. Fagerhol developed the protease inhibitor (Pi) phenotyping system in 19681969, using acid starch gel electrophoresis to classify AAT variants by mobility; the normal PiM allele produces adequate AAT, while PiZ results in severe deficiency (1015% of normal levels) due to protein misfolding and retention in the liver. Family studies further validated the system's utility, identifying over a dozen alleles and emphasizing Pi*ZZ homozygotes' high risk for both pulmonary and hepatic manifestations.
The 1980s marked advances in molecular characterization and initial treatments. The human SERPINA1 gene, encoding AAT, was cloned and its cDNA fully sequenced in 1984, revealing the protein's structure and facilitating genetic analysis. The PiZ mutationa single nucleotide substitution causing a glutamic acid to lysine change at residue 342was precisely identified through protein and DNA sequencing, explaining the variant's polymerization and impaired secretion. Early therapeutic efforts focused on replacement, with intravenous plasma-derived AAT infusions trialed in the early 1980s to raise serum levels and mitigate lung damage; these culminated in the U.S. Food and Drug Administration's approval of Prolastin, the first purified human AAT concentrate, in 1987 for chronic augmentation in PiZZ individuals with emphysema. A key milestone came in 1989 when the American Thoracic Society issued guidelines affirming AAT deficiency as a genetic disease, recommending screening for at-risk patients and augmentation therapy to preserve lung function.

Recent Developments

In the 2000s, significant progress was made in establishing the efficacy of augmentation therapy through the RAPID trial, a multicenter, double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled study conducted from 2006 to 2011 involving 180 patients with severe alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency (AATD). The trial demonstrated that weekly intravenous infusions of alpha-1 proteinase inhibitor (A1PI) at 60 mg/kg slowed the decline in lung density by 21% compared to placebo, providing radiographic evidence of preserved lung tissue in emphysema patients. Concurrently, efforts advanced recombinant AAT production, with early developments in the late 2000s exploring transgenic animal models and yeast expression systems to create scalable, pathogen-free alternatives to plasma-derived therapies, though challenges in glycosylation and yield persisted.
The 2010s saw preclinical advancements in gene therapy using adeno-associated virus (AAV) vectors to deliver functional SERPINA1 genes, with studies in mouse and nonhuman primate models achieving sustained hepatic expression of normal AAT protein for up to 12 months, highlighting potential for long-term correction of AATD lung manifestations. Expanded allele screening initiatives gained traction, incorporating targeted genotyping for rare variants beyond PIZ and PIS in high-risk populations like COPD patients, improving detection rates through integration into routine clinical guidelines from organizations such as the American Thoracic Society. A key milestone was the 2015 European Medicines Agency approval of Respreeza, a plasma-derived A1PI concentrate, for maintenance treatment in adults with severe AATD and emphysema, expanding access to augmentation therapy across the European Union.
In the 2020s, underdiagnosis remained a critical challenge, with estimates indicating that approximately 90% of AATD cases in the US and Europe go undetected due to nonspecific symptoms mimicking common COPD. Recent epidemiology updates, such as a 2025 Norwegian nationwide cohort study, revealed a diagnosed incidence of 1.4 per 100,000 person-years (95% CI: 1.31.5; 20082019) and a prevalence of 10.7 per 100,000 (95% CI: 9.811.5) as of December 31, 2019, underscoring regional variations and the need for broader screening in Northern European populations where PI*ZZ allele frequency approaches 1 in 4,500. Awareness campaigns intensified, including the 2025 American Thoracic Society President's Symposium, which addressed diagnostic gaps, patient needs, and pathogenesis to promote targeted testing in at-risk groups like early-onset emphysema patients.
From 2024 to 2025, breakthroughs in gene editing emerged, with Beam Therapeutics' BEAM-302, a base-editing therapy using lipid nanoparticles to correct the PI*Z mutation in hepatocytes, demonstrating clinical proof-of-concept in a phase 1/2 trial; initial data from nine patients showed restored normal AAT protein production and reduced mutant aggregates without serious adverse events. Preclinical CRISPR-based approaches, such as CRISPR Therapeutics' CTX460 utilizing novel SyNTase technology, achieved >90% mRNA correction and near-saturating editing in hepatocytes in disease models, offering promise for single-dose correction of SERPINA1 mutations in AATD lung and liver disease models. A 2025 systematic review highlighted AI applications in AATD, including machine learning for screening in COPD, emphysema progression prediction from CT scans, and clinical subgroup identification.
Societally, the Alpha-1 Foundation's research registries have profoundly impacted AATD management by enrolling 4,231 participants as of FY 2025 since 1997, enabling longitudinal studies that informed FDA approvals and global guidelines while facilitating recruitment for clinical trials. Global screening initiatives, endorsed by bodies like the Global Initiative for Chronic Obstructive Lung Disease and the World Health Organization, advocate universal testing for all COPD patients, resulting in increased diagnosis rates through integrated programs in Europe and North America.
