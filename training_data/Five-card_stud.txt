 Five-card stud is a variant of stud poker in which each player is dealt one face-down hole card and four face-up cards, with betting rounds occurring after the initial two cards and following each subsequent up card, aiming to form the best five-card poker hand or force opponents to fold.
Poker originated in the United States during the early 19th century from earlier vying games like the French Poque, with its earliest documented forms appearing in New Orleans gambling saloons around 18101825 using a 20-card deck limited to four players. By the 1830s, poker transitioned to a standard 52-card deck to allow more participants and incorporate hand rankings like the flush. Five-card stud specifically evolved as a non-draw variant, with the first written reference to stud poker, including the five-card version, appearing in The American Hoyle in 1864, associating it with frontier regions such as Ohio, Indiana, and Illinois. It gained prominence during the American Civil War (18611865), where it was played by soldiers on both Union and Confederate sides as a simple form of poker.
In gameplay, players typically post antes to seed the pot, followed by the dealer distributing one hole card and one door card (face-up) to each; the player with the lowest door card must post a bring-in bet, initiating the first betting round in a fixed-limit structure where smaller bets apply early and larger ones later. Three more face-up cards are then dealt one at a timeknown as third, fourth, and fifth streetswith betting proceeding clockwise after each, starting with the player showing the best visible hand from the second round onward; the hand concludes at showdown among remaining players, where the best combination of the five cards wins the pot. Standard poker hand rankings apply, from high card to royal flush, and the game supports 28 players, though it is often played heads-up or with small tables.
Historically popular in the late 19th and early 20th centuriesespecially on Mississippi riverboats and in Western saloonsfive-card stud was once the dominant poker form but declined in favor of community card games like Texas Hold'em by the mid-20th century, partly due to its reliance on visible cards limiting bluffing opportunities. It featured prominently in early World Series of Poker (WSOP) events from 1970 to 1974 as a bracelet-awarding preliminary, with winners including notable players like Bill Boyd in 1972, before being dropped amid waning interest. Today, it persists in home games and select casinos, valued for its straightforward structure and emphasis on reading opponents' exposed cards, though variants like Seven-card stud have largely supplanted it in professional play.

Rules and Gameplay

Objective and Setup

In five-card stud, the objective is for players to form the best possible five-card poker hand using one face-down hole card and four face-up cards, or to force all opponents to fold through betting, thereby winning the pot at showdown. Standard poker hand rankings apply, with the highest-ranking hand prevailing.
The game utilizes a standard 52-card deck with no jokers, accommodating 2 to 8 players ideally, though up to 10 is possible in some settings. To initiate the hand and seed the pot, each player posts a small ante, typically 10-20% of the small betting limitfor instance, $0.50 in a $5/$10 limit game.
In home or non-casino games, a dealer button designates the nominal dealer position, which rotates clockwise after each hand to determine the order of play and dealing. Some house rules substitute or supplement antes with blinds posted by the two players to the left of the dealer, or a bring-in bet required from the player receiving the lowest-ranking exposed card in the first round.

Dealing Procedure

In five-card stud poker, the dealing procedure begins with the first street, where the dealer distributes one face-down card (known as the hole card) to each player, starting with the player to the dealer's immediate left and proceeding clockwise. Following this, the dealer deals one face-up card (the door card) to each player in the same rotation, completing the initial two cards per player. This process uses a standard 52-card deck, with no jokers, and assumes all players have posted the required antes prior to the deal.
After the first betting round, the dealer burns the top card of the deck face down to prevent any potential advantage from predictable card order. On the second street, the dealer then deals one additional face-up card to each remaining player, again starting to the left and moving clockwise. This burning step is repeated before the third street, where another face-up card is dealt to each active player, and before the fourth street, which delivers the final face-up card to complete each player's five-card hand (one hole card and four exposed cards). The dealer is responsible for ensuring accurate distribution, announcing any notable exposed cards such as pairs, and maintaining the integrity of the deck throughout.
If a duplicate cardmeaning the exact same rank and suit as one already dealt face upis erroneously exposed during any up-card deal, it is treated as a dead card and set aside, with the next card from the deck dealt in its place to the affected player; if the error affects multiple players or cannot be corrected without compromising fairness, the hand is declared a misdeal and redealt from the start. Such irregularities are rare but underscore the dealer's duty to verify the deck's completeness before play begins.

Betting Rounds

In five-card stud, betting occurs in four rounds, one after each upcard is dealt, following the initial hole card and door card distribution. All players typically post a small ante before the deal to seed the pot, with the amount often set at one-tenth or one-fifth of the small bet in limit games.
The first betting round begins after the door cards are revealed, with the player holding the lowest-ranking upcard required to post the bring-in, which is usually half the small bet amountfor example, $1 in a $2/$4 limit game. This player then has the option to complete the bet to the full small bet size, after which action proceeds clockwise around the table, allowing players to fold, call, or raise in increments of the small bet. Raises are typically limited to three per round in fixed-limit structures, though the bring-in round may cap at four total bets if completed early.
Subsequent betting rounds follow the dealing of the second, third, and fourth upcards. On the second street, the small bet limit applies unless any player has a pair or better showing, in which case the big bet limit is used for the entire round; betting starts with the player showing the highest-ranking hand (ties broken by suit: clubs lowest, diamonds, hearts, spades highest), who has the option to check, bet, fold, call, or raise. On the third and fourth streets, the big bet limit always applies, again starting with the high hand player.
Five-card stud is most commonly played as a fixed-limit game, where bet sizes are predeterminedfor instance, $1/$2 meaning $1 small bets on early streets and $2 big bets on later onesbut pot-limit and no-limit variants exist, allowing raises up to the pot size or any amount, respectively, though these are less traditional and rarer in practice. After the final betting round on fourth street, if two or more players remain, a showdown occurs where hole cards are revealed, and the player with the highest-ranking five-card poker hand wins the entire pot; ties result in a split, with any odd chips awarded to the player closest to the dealer's left.

Hand Rankings and Winning

In five-card stud, players form their hands using one private hole card and four exposed upcards, with no shared community cards contributing to the final evaluation. The standard poker hand rankings determine the strength of each player's five-card combination, ranked from highest to lowest as follows: royal flush (ten through ace of the same suit), straight flush (five consecutive cards of the same suit), four of a kind (four cards of the same rank), full house (three of a kind plus a pair), flush (five cards of the same suit), straight (five consecutive cards of mixed suits), three of a kind (three cards of the same rank), two pair (two separate pairs), one pair (two cards of the same rank), and high card (no other combination, ranked by the highest card).
Tiebreakers resolve hands of equal rank by comparing the specific cards involved; for instance, in one pair, the highest-ranking pair wins, and if tied, the highest kicker (unpaired card) determines the winner, continuing with subsequent kickers if needed. For straights and flushes, the highest card in the sequence or suit breaks ties. Suits play no role in ranking or tiebreaking unless specified by house rules, as all suits are considered equal in standard play.
At showdown, after the final betting round on fifth street, remaining players reveal their hole cards, and the player with the highest-ranking hand according to the above hierarchy wins the entire main pot. If multiple players are all-in with unequal chip stacks during betting, side pots are created from excess contributions beyond the smallest all-in amount; the all-in player competes only for the main pot, while active players vie for side pots, ensuring fair distribution based on participation.

History

Origins in the 19th Century

Five-card stud, the earliest form of stud poker, emerged during the American Civil War (18611865) as a popular pastime among soldiers on both Union and Confederate sides, providing diversion amid the hardships of camp life. The game likely developed in the Midwest, around regions such as Ohio, Indiana, and Illinois, where it was played informally before gaining wider recognition.
Stud poker evolved from earlier European vying games, including the French poquewhich contributed the name "poker" and the core mechanic of betting on hand strength without drawsand the English brag, which introduced elements of comparison and bluffing. Unlike draw poker, which allowed players to discard and replace cards for hand improvement and had been documented as early as 1850, stud poker dispensed with discards entirely, emphasizing the fixed cards dealt and the visible information from opponents' upcards to drive betting decisions.
The first written rules for stud poker appeared in 1864 in The American Hoyle, or Gentleman's Hand-Book of Games by William Brisbane Dick, published by Dick & Fitzgerald in New York, marking its formal entry into American gaming literature. In this version, known initially as "stud-horse" poker, each player received one hole card face down and four cards face up, with betting rounds following the dealing of each upcard, establishing the structure of one down and four up as the standard for five-card stud.
Following the war's end in 1865, returning veterans spread the game westward, where it took root in riverboat casinos along the Mississippi and in frontier saloons, transitioning from military encampments to commercial gambling venues and solidifying its place in American card play culture.

Peak Popularity and Decline

Five-card stud achieved widespread popularity in the United States from the 1870s through the late 1940s, particularly as a staple in Wild West saloons and frontier gambling scenes where stud variants dominated over draw poker due to their straightforward play without discards. The game reflected the era's rugged individualism and high-stakes betting culture, spreading via riverboats, mining camps, and cattle towns, where it was favored for its balance of skill and chance in non-community card formats. By the mid-20th century, it had become one of the most played poker variants among men, second only to draw in overall card game appeal.
A notable high point came in 1949, when legendary gambler Johnny Moss engaged in a marathon heads-up match against Nick "The Greek" Dandolos in Las Vegas, reportedly culminating in a record pot of around $500,000 during play that emphasized five-card stud as the prominent variant. This event underscored the game's enduring draw in high-roller circles, even as seven-card stud, which had emerged post-Civil War, offered more strategic depth and betting opportunities with its additional cards.
Post-World War II, five-card stud's prominence waned as seven-card stud gained traction for its increased action and hand-building complexity, drawing players seeking more dynamic gameplay. By the 1960s, the rise of community card games like Texas hold'em further eclipsed it, with hold'em's shared board enabling larger fields and television appeal; consequently, five-card stud became rare in casinos by the 1970s, relegated to occasional private settings. It was featured as a bracelet event in the early World Series of Poker from 1970 to 1974, with winners including Bill Boyd in 1972, before being discontinued.
The game persisted in cultural depictions, such as the 1966 film Kaleidoscope, where five-card stud features prominently in a high-society gambling sequence, highlighting its lingering association with sophisticated risk-taking. Niche revivals occurred in 1980s home games, where it appealed to players nostalgic for simpler, non-community formats amid the hold'em boom, though it remained confined to informal play rather than mainstream venues.

Strategy

Starting Hands and Position

In five-card stud, selecting strong starting hands is crucial for long-term profitability, as players receive one hole card and four upcards over the dealing streets, limiting opportunities for draws compared to community card games. The strongest starters typically include high pairs such as aces through jacks, which provide immediate strength and pair potential without relying on improvement. Suited high cards, like ace-king suited or broadway connectors (e.g., king-queen suited), offer versatility for flush or straight draws while maintaining top-pair potential. Conversely, low unsuited single cards or disconnected low holdings should be avoided, as they lack equity against typical opponent ranges and exposed cards.
Position plays a diminished role in five-card stud relative to games like Texas Hold'em, owing to the exposure of upcards that dynamically determine betting order after the initial street. On the second street, the player with the lowest upcard must post the bring-in and act first, but from the third street onward, the player showing the highest upcard initiates the action, allowing those with weaker visible cards to observe and react. This structure makes acting last on early streets particularly advantageous, as it provides additional information from opponents' exposed cards and actions before committing further. However, the visibility of board cards reduces the overall positional edge, emphasizing hand strength over seat location.
Effective play requires stringent folding thresholds on the first street to preserve chips for premium situations, with experienced players folding most dealt hands that lack pairing potential or high-card draw equity. If holding the low upcard, completing the bring-in becomes viable only with a paired hole card or strong drawing hand; otherwise, folding prevents unnecessary investment against likely superior holdings. This selective approach minimizes variance in limit structures, where pot odds rarely justify speculative calls with marginal starters.
Bankroll management in five-card stud, typically played as a limit game, follows conservative guidelines to withstand downswings from incomplete information and multi-street decisions, recommending 200-300 big bets for the stakes level. For a $10/$20 limit game (big bet $20), this equates to a $4,000-$6,000 roll, accounting for antes and frequent small bets that erode stacks over time. Adhering to this ensures sustainability without overextending, allowing focus on strategic edges like hand selection rather than survival.

Reading Board Cards and Opponents

In five-card stud, reading the board involves closely tracking the exposed upcards of all players to assess potential hand strengths and available draws. Players must monitor live cardsthose still in play on opponents' boardsto evaluate threats like possible straights, flushes, or pairs, while accounting for dead cards that have been folded and mucked. For instance, if three hearts are visible among the upcards, the likelihood of a flush draw succeeding diminishes significantly, as fewer hearts remain in the deck for completion. This process requires calculating outs accurately; with a standard 52-card deck and multiple players, known cards (up to 24 in a six-handed game by the final round) reduce the pool of unknowns, making precise odds estimation essential for decisions on continuing draws.
Tracking dead cards is crucial for refining these assessments, as folded upcards remove possibilities from opponents' ranges and alter drawing probabilities. When a player folds, their exposed card becomes dead, and in later rounds, multiple folds can accumulate significant dead woodsuch as five dead cards after early betting rounds in a multi-way potwhich narrows flush or straight outs dramatically. For example, if four spades have been mucked early, an opponent drawing to a spade flush faces steeper odds, allowing the observant player to bet more aggressively knowing the draw is less viable. Failing to track these dead cards often leads to overestimating one's own or opponents' drawing chances, a common error that results in unprofitable calls on marginal hands.
Reading opponents' upcards extends beyond the current board to infer their likely hole cards and overall hand ranges. A paired board, such as an opponent showing two kings among their upcards, signals potential trips if their door card (first upcard) matches, prompting caution unless one's own hand clearly dominates the visible strength. Conversely, an ace showing as an upcard makes it unlikely that the opponent holds another ace in the hole, tightening their range to draws or lower pairs. Players should compare their combined up and down cards against these visible holdings; if an opponent's unpaired high cards (e.g., ace-queen) outrank one's own, folding is typically advisable unless pot odds are favorable. This inference relies on hand rankings, where higher visible cards often indicate superior potential.
Effective opponent tracking also involves noting mucked upcards from folds to build a mental map of removed cards and adjust ranges dynamically. For example, if a king is folded early and an opponent later shows a king up, they are less likely to have paired it for two pair, enabling more confident raises with a strong holding like concealed kings and sevens. Memory techniques aid this process: categorize cards by suit and rank mentally from the first round, scanning all upcards sequentially (e.g., noting 2h, Ks, 3c across the table) to commit the board state to memory without disrupting play. Practicing offline with simulated boards hones this skill, ensuring real-game recall of dead and live cards to avoid errors like ignoring mucked suits that weaken apparent draws. Overlooking these mucks can lead to misjudging board textures, such as assuming a flush threat exists when dead cards have neutralized it.

Betting and Bluffing Tactics

In five-card stud, value betting focuses on extracting maximum value from strong hands while accounting for the game's partial information disclosure. Players typically bet or raise with top pair or better on fourth and fifth streets to build the pot against weaker holdings, as the limit structure allows controlled aggression without risking the entire stack. Slowplaying premium hands like three of a kind or better is uncommon, as exposed board cards often alert opponents to potential strength, enabling them to fold marginal hands correctly and reducing overall value.
Bluffing in five-card stud is constrained by the visibility of up cards, which makes pure bluffs less viable compared to games with more hidden information, emphasizing selective aggression over frequent deception. Semi-bluffs with drawing hands, such as suited connectors or high-card draws, are most effective on third street to contest the bring-in and antes, leveraging fold equity while maintaining showdown value if called. Pure bluffs become rare after fourth street, as readable boards allow opponents to assess threats accurately and call more confidently with medium strength. Stealing the bring-in with premium unpaired high cards like an ace or king is a standard tactic to build pots early without showdown.
Pot odds play a central role in calling decisions, requiring players to evaluate equity against the cost of continuation bets across the four rounds. A general guideline is to call if implied equity exceeds the offered odds, such as requiring more than approximately 25% equity for a small bet into a pot offering 3:1, while folding draws with reduced outs due to dead cards on the board. For instance, a hand with five outs to improve has about a 12.5% chance on the next street, necessitating favorable pot odds (better than 7:1) to justify calling; otherwise, marginal draws should be abandoned to avoid negative expected value.
Strategic adjustments in five-card stud depend on table dynamics and hand strength to optimize profitability. In short-handed games with fewer opponents, players tighten their calling ranges to counter increased variance and stealing attempts, focusing on premium starting hands to maintain edge. With medium-strength holdings like middle pair, pot control through checking or flat-calling prevents bloating the pot against potential draws or superior hands, preserving equity without overcommitting. Against passive tables, loosen aggression to steal more frequently; versus loose opponents, tighten to exploit their wide ranges with value bets.

Variants

High-Low Split

In the high-low split variant of five-card stud, the dealing procedure remains identical to the standard game, with each player receiving one card face down followed by four cards face up in successive betting rounds. This structure preserves the core stud mechanics while introducing the dual-objective pot split.
The pot is divided equally between the best high hand, ranked according to standard poker hand hierarchies, and the best qualifying low hand, provided a low hand qualifies; if no low qualifies, the entire pot goes to the high hand winner. Low hands are evaluated using an eight-or-better qualification, where the hand must consist of five unpaired cards ranked eight or lower, with aces low and straights and flushes not counting against the low (the best low being A-2-3-4-5). In many home games, players declare for high, low, or both after the final betting roundeither sequentially starting from the last bettor or simultaneously by tapping or signalingto determine eligibility, with penalties such as disqualification for incorrect declarations; ties within high or low portions are split equally, and any odd chip typically awards to the high hand.
Strategic play emphasizes pursuing "scoops," where a single player wins both the high and low portions of the pot with versatile starting hands that can develop in either direction, such as those containing low cards with high potential like aces or suited connectors. Players must avoid "stuck in the middle" handsthose with moderate strength that neither compete strongly for high nor qualify viably for lowas these often result in splitting the pot without a full share, reducing overall profitability.
This variant enjoys popularity primarily in informal home games, where the declaration mechanic adds social interaction, though it sees limited action in modern casinos due to the rise of more communal poker forms like hold'em.

Canadian Stud and Similar

Canadian Stud, also known as Scandinavian Stud or Sk, is a variant of five-card stud that gained popularity in Canada and parts of Europe, with Sk specifically becoming a staple in Finland under local rules. The game traces its roots to Northern America, where it was introduced to Finland in the early 20th century by lumberjacks, sailors, and other returnees, achieving its height of popularity during the 1960s and 1970s.
The dealing follows the standard five-card stud procedure: each player receives one face-down hole card and one face-up door card, followed by a betting round; three more face-up cards are then dealt one at a time with betting after each. This configuration provides the typical mix of hidden and visible information in stud poker. What distinguishes Sk is its unique hand rankings: a four-card straight ranks above a pair but below two pairs, and a four-card flush ranks above the four-card straight but below two pairs, while all other rankings remain standard.

Two Down, Three Up

In the two down, three up variant of five-card stud, players receive a total of two hole cards face down and three cards face up. The dealing procedure typically begins with one card dealt face down to each player, followed by a betting round. Subsequent rounds deal one face up card to each player, with betting after each of the three up cards. The final card is then dealt face down to each player, initiating the last betting round before showdown. This structure results in three betting rounds after the up cards, adjusting the pace compared to variants with four up cards.
The play follows standard stud poker betting conventions, where the player with the lowest exposed card often initiates betting on the first round, and action proceeds clockwise with options to check, bet, call, raise, or fold. With three up cards visible before the final hidden card, players can assess draws and strengths more openly during early and middle rounds, though the second hole card introduces uncertainty in the final betting. At showdown, all five cards form the best poker hand, ranked by standard high poker hierarchy from royal flush to high card.
This variant emphasizes high-only hands with no low or split pot options, keeping the focus on forming the strongest possible combination. The exposure of three up cards early in the hand allows for greater visibility of potential draws, such as straights or flushes, compared to more concealed variants, which can accelerate decision-making in casual settings. It is commonly adapted for home games due to its straightforward structure and fewer visible cards overall than traditional one-down-four-up dealing.

Three Down, Two Up

In the three down, two up variant of five-card stud, also referred to as 3-2 stud, the dealer first distributes three cards face down to each player as the hole cards. A betting round follows this initial deal, typically starting with the player to the left of the dealer since no visible cards determine a bring-in. The dealer then deals the first face-up card to each remaining player in rotation, prompting a second betting round that begins with the player showing the lowest up card. Finally, the second face-up card is dealt to each player, leading to the third and final betting round before showdown. This sequence emphasizes intervening bets after each up card, creating a total of three betting opportunities.
This variant differs from the standard five-card stud format of one down and four up cards by providing only two visible board cards per player, which limits the information available for assessing draws or completed hands. As a result, bluffs become more challenging, as opponents have fewer cues from exposed cards to gauge strength or weakness, shifting focus to psychological play and hidden card value. The heavy reliance on the three private hole cards makes strong starting combinations essential from the outset. In some regions, particularly in casual or club settings, the game is adapted to high-low split rules, where the pot splits between the best high hand and the best qualifying low hand (often eight-or-better).
As a modification of the traditional five-card stud, which traces its origins to 19th-century American saloons and military play, the three down, two up version gained traction in U.S. poker clubs during the 20th century as a faster-paced alternative with fewer exposed cards.
The winner is the player holding the best five-card poker hand constructed from their three hole cards and two up cards, evaluated using standard hand rankings from royal flush down to high card.

One Down, Three Up, One Down

In the One Down, Three Up, One Down variant of five-card stud, also referred to as 1-3-1 stud, the dealing sequence begins with each player receiving a single hole card face down, providing initial private information while the game unfolds. This is followed by the first face-up card dealt to every player, initiating the first betting round, where the player with the lowest exposed card typically brings in a forced bet to start the action. Subsequent face-up cardsthe second and thirdare then dealt one at a time, with a full betting round after each to allow players to build the pot based on visible progress toward strong hands.
The distinctive element occurs after the third up card: instead of a final visible card as in standard five-card stud, each remaining player receives a second hole card face down, concealed from opponents, followed by one final betting round. This structure mirrors the betting progression of traditional five-card stud but culminates in a showdown where players reveal all cards to form the best five-card poker hand, with no community cards involved. The final hidden card creates a late twist, forcing players to wager on incomplete visible information and introducing greater uncertainty about opponents' potential completions.
This dealing pattern builds escalating tension throughout the hand, as early rounds emphasize reading exposed cards for draws and pairs, while the concluding hidden card shifts strategy toward bluffing, value betting on strong partial boards, or folding marginal holdings amid heightened risk. Although uncommon in professional or casino settings due to its deviation from the more straightforward visibility of standard stud, the variant appears in select digital platforms and home games, where it appeals to players seeking varied stud experiences.

Mexican Stud and Wild Card Versions

Mexican Stud, also known as Stud Loco or Mexican Poker, is a roll-your-own variant of five-card stud that emphasizes player choice in revealing cards and frequently incorporates wild cards to heighten unpredictability and variance. The game is typically played with 3 to 10 players using a standard 52-card deck, though some casino versions employ a stripped deck of 41 cards by removing all 8s, 9s, and 10s while including a joker as a designated wild card. This setup, common in Southern California poker rooms, alters hand rankings and introduces the joker as fully wild for high handsable to represent any rank or suitwhile treating it as a high card for low hands in hi-lo formats.
The dealing follows a modified stud structure: Each player receives two cards face down and selects one to turn face up, with betting beginning from the player showing the lowest upcard (aces low). A third card is then dealt face down, and each player chooses one of their remaining face-down cards to reveal, followed by a betting round. This process repeats for the fourth and fifth cards, resulting in each player having four face-up cards and one face-down hole card at showdown. Betting rounds occur after each revelation, mirroring standard five-card stud intervals but with the added strategy of deciding which card to expose based on its potential value. In wild card iterations, any wild cards heldsuch as the jokerenhance hand strength by substituting for needed ranks or suits, potentially elevating a simple pair to a full house or straight flush.
A notable sub-variant, Shifting Sands, builds on Mexican Stud by designating the rank of each player's first revealed upcard (and all other cards of that rank in their hand) as wild exclusively for that player. This creates a "rotating" wild rank dynamic, as each participant effectively has a unique wild suit based on their initial choice, forcing careful consideration when selecting the first card to turn over. The mechanic amplifies chaos, as a low upcard like a 2, 3, or 4 could become wild, allowing it to mimic any rank or suit to complete superior combinations.
Originating from games along the U.S.-Mexico border and gaining traction in California casinos during the mid-20th century, Mexican Stud's wild card elements contribute to its high variance, where even weak starting hands can evolve into monsters through wild substitutions. Strategically, players must recalibrate hand reads to factor in wild potential, prioritizing bets on exposed wild-eligible cards while bluffing more aggressively against opponents showing possible wild ranks, as the unpredictability demands looser ranges and faster folds on non-improving boards.

Telesina and Other Regional Forms

Telesina is an Italian variant of five-card stud poker that emphasizes visible cards and strategic betting based on exposed hands. The game uses a stripped deck, with the number of cards adjusted according to the number of players to ensure sufficient cards for dealing; for instance, with four players, a 40-card deck from 5 to ace is employed, while six players use a 32-card deck from 7 to ace. Each player antes a fixed amount before the deal, and the game supports 2 to 8 players.
The dealing begins with one card face down to each player, followed by one face up, initiating the first betting round starting with the player showing the highest up card. Subsequent rounds involve dealing three more face-up cards one at a time, with betting after each, where the opener is the player with the best visible hand (considering pairs, three of a kind, or four of a kind shown). At showdown, players reveal their hole card alongside the four up cards to form the best five-card poker hand. Hand rankings follow standard poker hierarchy, with the notable exception that a flush outranks a full house, and suits have relative values (clubs lowest, spades highest) to break ties in identical hands. The highest-ranking hand wins the pot.
Regional variations of Telesina introduce distinct twists while retaining the core stud structure. In Telesina con vela, popular in some Italian circles, a face-down community card known as "la vela" is dealt to the center at the beginning and revealed after the final betting round, allowing all players to incorporate it into their hand for a potential six-card total from which the best five are selected. Another form, Telesina con una carta coperta, deals all cards face up except the first and last, with players choosing which hidden card to reveal before the final bet, adding an element of choice. Telesina with a joker designates one rank (often nines) as wild via a joker card, elevating certain combinations like "pokerissimo" (four of a kind plus the wild joker) above even straight flushes. These adaptations enhance replayability and are common in casual and tournament settings.
Teresina serves as an alternate name for Telesina in some contexts, particularly in Brazilian and South American play, where it may incorporate local house rules such as the optional use of a joker as wild to suit regional preferences. The game originated in the 20th century as an adaptation of American five-card stud, gaining traction in Europe through Italian poker traditions. It remains a favored social game in private clubs, casinos, and home gatherings across Italy and parts of Europe, valued for its blend of luck, observation, and bluffing without community cards dominating the action.

Asian Five-Card Stud

Asian Five-Card Stud is a variant of five-card stud poker played with a stripped deck consisting of 32 cards, specifically excluding all 2s, 3s, 4s, 5s, and 6s to leave only ranks 7 through ace in each suit. This modification heightens the likelihood of premium hands like straights and flushes, altering strategic dynamics compared to traditional poker. The game is played among multiple players, with the goal of forming the best five-card hand from the cards dealt individually to each player.
The dealing procedure mirrors standard five-card stud: each player receives one card face down (the hole card) and one card face up, followed by a betting round based on the exposed card. Subsequent rounds deal three more face-up cards to each player, one at a time, with betting after each. At showdown, players reveal their hands, and the highest-ranking poker hand wins the pot. Betting structures can vary, often featuring no-limit formats in high-stakes settings.
This variant emerged in the 1990s within Asian casino environments, where it quickly gained traction for its blend of individual card play and accelerated hand development from the reduced deck. It appeals to players by combining the personal card revelation of stud poker with hand frequencies more akin to community card games like Texas Hold'em, fostering strategic depth in markets such as Macau. The game's popularity in East Asia stems from its suitability for no-limit play, drawing professional players and contributing to substantial winnings in regional tournaments.
