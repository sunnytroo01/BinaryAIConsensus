Industrial Policy Resolution of 1956

1x
 The Industrial Policy Resolution of 1956 (IPR 1956) was a foundational policy statement adopted by the Parliament of India on 30 April 1956, delineating the government's strategy for industrial development in a mixed economy by prioritizing public sector control over the "commanding heights" of the economy, including heavy and basic industries, to foster a socialist pattern of society.
The resolution classified industries into three schedules: Schedule A reserved 17 strategic sectorssuch as arms and ammunition, atomic energy, railways, iron and steel, and heavy machineryexclusively for state ownership and development; Schedule B identified 12 industries, including aluminum and fertilizers, where the state would take the initiative with private sector supplementation allowed; and Schedule C encompassed remaining consumer goods and light industries for private enterprise, subject to licensing under the Industrial (Development and Regulation) Act of 1951.
Its core objectives encompassed accelerating industrialization via emphasis on capital-intensive heavy industries (aligned with the Mahalanobis model), promoting self-reliance through import substitution, ensuring balanced regional growth to mitigate disparities, generating employment via cottage and small-scale sectors, and curbing monopolies to distribute economic power more equitably.
While the policy spurred early diversification and manufacturing expansionoutpacing colonial-era rates in sectors like metals, machinery, and chemicalsits rigid licensing regime and public sector dominance institutionalized the "License Raj," fostering bureaucratic red tape, excess capacity, technological stagnation, and rent-seeking, which empirically constrained productivity, competition, and overall GDP growth to a subdued 3-4% annual average (the "Hindu rate") through the 1970s and 1980s, until partial dismantling via 1991 liberalization unleashed higher growth potential.

Historical and Ideological Foundations

Pre-Independence Industrial Landscape

Prior to British colonization, India maintained a significant artisanal manufacturing base, particularly in textiles, which constituted about 25% of global industrial output around 1750. Handloom production dominated, with exports of cotton goods, silk, and metalware supporting a trade surplus that drew European interest. This sector relied on decentralized, skilled labor in villages, fostering self-sufficiency in consumer goods like yarns, threads, and processed foods such as sugar and oils.
Colonial policies from the mid-18th century onward induced de-industrialization by imposing one-sided tariffshigh duties on Indian exports to Britain while allowing cheap British machine-made textiles to flood local markets, decimating handloom weavers. India's share of world manufacturing exports fell from roughly 27% in the late 18th century to 2% by the early 19th, as East India Company monopolies prioritized raw material extraction like cotton and indigo over local processing. The economy shifted toward agrarian exports, with industry comprising a minor GDP sharemining and manufacturing hovered at 4-5% around 1910while per capita income stagnated amid population growth and recurrent famines exacerbated by export-focused land revenue systems. British investments favored infrastructure like railways (over 40,000 miles by 1947) primarily for resource evacuation to ports, not integrated industrial growth.
Modern factory-based industry emerged modestly from the 1850s, concentrated in enclaves under foreign or elite Indian capital. The cotton textile sector led, with the first mill established in Bombay in 1854, growing to over 400 mills by 1947 and employing around 800,000 workers, though it remained import-dependent for machinery and focused on yarn rather than finished cloth. Jute milling, vital for British sack demand, clustered in Bengal from 1855, with British firms dominating 70 mills by independence, processing raw jute exports from the region. Iron and steel production began late, exemplified by Tata Iron and Steel Company (TISCO) in Jamshedpur starting operations in 1912 after setup in 1907, marking India's initial foray into heavy industry with an output of 100,000 tons annually by the 1930s. Other sectors like tea plantations (post-1830s) and coal mining expanded under British enterprise, but capital goods industries were negligible, leaving India reliant on imports for machinery and technology.
By 1947, the industrial base was underdeveloped relative to population needs, with factory output growing at 4-5% annually from 1900 but contributing only about 15-17% to national income, overshadowed by agriculture's 50% share. Discriminatory policies, including limited protectionism until the 1920s and wartime disruptions, constrained indigenous entrepreneurship, while the "drain of wealth" via tribute and unequal trade siphoned surpluses to Britain, hindering reinvestment. This legacy of extractive orientation and technological lag set the stage for post-independence efforts to build self-reliant heavy industry.

Post-Independence Shift to Planned Economy and Socialism

Upon achieving independence on August 15, 1947, India faced an economy marked by heavy agrarian dependence, with industry contributing less than 15% to national income and per capita income stagnant at around 250 rupees annually under colonial rule. Prime Minister Jawaharlal Nehru, drawing from his exposure to Fabian socialism and Soviet planning models, advocated for state-led development to achieve self-sufficiency, reduce inequality, and accelerate industrialization, rejecting pure laissez-faire in favor of a mixed economy where public ownership would dominate strategic sectors.
The Industrial Policy Resolution issued on April 6, 1948, formalized this orientation by categorizing industries into state monopolies (such as arms, atomic energy, and railways), mixed public-private domains, and areas open to private initiative under government oversight, aiming to harmonize industrial growth with social objectives like employment generation and regional balance. This policy emphasized planned production targets and state intervention to prevent private monopolies, reflecting Nehru's view that unregulated capitalism exacerbated colonial-era disparities.
Centralized planning machinery emerged with the creation of the Planning Commission on March 15, 1950, tasked with assessing resources and formulating development strategies, leading to the First Five-Year Plan (19511956) that allocated 44.6% of investments to agriculture and irrigation while initiating public sector projects in power and transport. Influenced by the Soviet Gosplan but adapted to India's democratic context, the plan targeted 2.1% annual growth, prioritizing public savings mobilization over private enterprise to build foundational infrastructure.
The ideological commitment deepened at the Indian National Congress's Avadi session in January 1955, where a resolution endorsed a "socialist pattern of society" entailing public control over material resources' distribution to meet common needs, thereby reinforcing state dominance in heavy industries and foreshadowing the comprehensive framework of the 1956 resolution. This evolution represented a causal pivot from pre-independence incrementalism to directive economics, driven by the perceived need to counterbalance inherited underdevelopment through bureaucratic allocation rather than market signals.

Objectives and Theoretical Framework

Stated Goals for Industrialization

The Industrial Policy Resolution of 1956 outlined the establishment of a socialistic pattern of society as the overarching objective of India's social and economic policy, with the state assuming a predominant and direct responsibility for guiding industrial development toward rapid economic growth. This framework emphasized accelerating the pace of industrialization to build a self-reliant economy, prioritizing the expansion of heavy industries and machine-making sectors to form the foundation for sustained production capacity and technological advancement. The resolution explicitly aimed to boost the process of industrialization by fostering state-led initiatives in critical areas, thereby reducing external dependencies and enabling domestic capability in core manufacturing.
Beyond growth acceleration, the stated goals included mitigating economic inequalities through the prevention of monopolies, concentration of wealth, and income disparities, while addressing regional imbalances by directing industrial investments toward underdeveloped areas. The policy sought to expand the public sector's dominance in strategic industries to command the "heights" of the economy, ensuring that private enterprise supplemented rather than supplanted state efforts in key domains. Complementary aims involved promoting cooperative sectors and small-scale industries to generate employment and decentralize economic activity, with the state regulating private initiatives via licensing to align them with national priorities.
These objectives reflected a deliberate shift toward planned economic intervention, where industrialization served not only productive expansion but also broader societal restructuring, including the provision of worker amenities and incentives for backward regions to equalize development opportunities. The resolution positioned the government as the primary arbiter of industrial allocation, categorizing sectors to enforce public control in atomic energy, defense, heavy machinery, and basic inputs like steel, while allowing regulated private participation elsewhere.

Mahalanobis Model and Emphasis on Heavy Industry

The Mahalanobis model, developed by Indian statistician Prasanta Chandra Mahalanobis, provided the analytical framework for India's Second Five-Year Plan (19561961), which directly informed the Industrial Policy Resolution of 1956. This model, often characterized as a heavy-industry-biased strategy, posited that rapid economic growth required prioritizing investments in capital goods and producer industriessuch as steel, machinery, and heavy chemicalsover consumer goods to expand the economy's productive capacity and achieve self-reliance. By focusing on intermediate inputs, the approach aimed to generate a virtuous cycle of reinvestment, where output from heavy sectors would fuel further industrialization without excessive dependence on imports or private sector volatility.
In operational terms, the model divided the economy into sectors emphasizing machine-building and basic industries, allocating roughly 20% of the Second Plan's total outlay of approximately Rs. 4,800 crore to heavy industry development, a sharp pivot from the agriculture-focused First Plan. This emphasis reflected Mahalanobis's mathematical formulation, which used input-output relationships to argue that a higher investment rate in capital-intensive sectors would yield exponential growth rates, targeting 5% annual GDP increase through state-directed resource mobilization. The resolution translated this into policy by designating 17 key industries, including iron and steel, non-ferrous metals, and heavy electrical equipment, for exclusive or dominant public sector control, ensuring alignment with the model's causal priority on building an industrial base capable of sustaining long-term expansion.
The strategy's rationale drew from observations of Soviet industrialization successes, adapting them to India's context by assuming closed-economy dynamics and high savings rates to finance public investments, though it implicitly downplayed agricultural bottlenecks and market signals in favor of planned allocation. Empirical projections in the model suggested that neglecting consumer goods temporarily would prevent resource diversion and accelerate technological deepening, a logic that justified the resolution's mixed-economy structure where private enterprise was subordinated in strategic heavy sectors to avert oligopolistic inefficiencies.

Policy Provisions and Sectoral Allocation

Schedule A: Exclusive State Domain

Schedule A of the Industrial Policy Resolution of 1956 reserved 17 industries for exclusive development and control by the State, stipulating that their future expansion, establishment of new units, and overall management would fall under government monopoly to prioritize national interests, prevent private monopolies in essential sectors, and align with the socialist pattern of society. These sectors were selected due to their foundational role in economic infrastructure, strategic importance for defense and security, and potential for natural monopolies that could undermine public welfare if left to private hands. The resolution emphasized that while some existing private enterprises in these areas might continue operations under regulation, no new private investments would be permitted, ensuring state dominance to facilitate coordinated planning under the Second Five-Year Plan.
The industries listed in Schedule A included:

Arms and ammunition and allied items of defense equipment

Atomic energy

Iron and steel

Heavy castings and forgings of iron and steel

Heavy plant and machinery required for iron and steel production, mining, machine tool manufacture, and other specified basic industries

Heavy electrical plant, including large hydraulic and steam turbines

Coal and lignite

Mineral oils

Mining of iron ore, manganese ore, chrome ore, gypsum, sulphur, gold, and diamonds

Mining and processing of copper, lead, zinc, tin, molybdenum, and wolfram

Minerals specified in the Atomic Energy (Control of Production and Use) Order, 1953

Air transport

Railway transport

Shipbuilding

Manufacture of telephones, telephone cables, telegraph equipment, and wireless apparatus (excluding radio receiving sets)

Generation and distribution of electricity

This classification reflected the government's intent to command the "commanding heights" of the economy, drawing from the Mahalanobis framework's focus on heavy industry investment to achieve self-reliance, though it later contributed to inefficiencies by curtailing competition and innovation in these domains. By 1956, the policy formalized state ownership in these areas to mitigate risks of foreign dominance or domestic concentration, as evidenced by pre-existing private stakes in sectors like iron and steel that were targeted for gradual public acquisition.

Schedule B: State-Led Development with Private Participation

Schedule B of the Industrial Policy Resolution of 1956 designated 12 industries for development primarily led by the state, with private sector participation permitted to supplement government initiatives. Adopted on April 30, 1956, this category targeted sectors vital to economic expansion but not warranting full public monopoly, such as aluminium production, machine tools, ferroalloys and special steels, essential drugs and pharmaceuticals, synthetic rubber, chemical fertilizers, and transport services including sea, air, and road operations. The policy stipulated that the state would initiate new undertakings, especially where private investment lagged due to high capital requirements or technological barriers, to accelerate capacity building and ensure alignment with national planning objectives.
Private involvement in Schedule B industries was conditional on obtaining licenses under the Industries (Development and Regulation) Act, 1951, which empowered the government to regulate entry, capacity, and operations to prevent monopolistic tendencies and direct resources toward priority areas. This mixed approach reflected the resolution's aim to leverage state control for strategic direction while harnessing private efficiency for incremental growth, with provisions for the state to progressively assume dominant ownership if private contributions proved inadequate. For instance, in aluminium, the government established facilities like those under National Aluminium Company precursors, while allowing licensed private expansions. The framework emphasized cooperative development, where private units were expected to operate under state oversight, including technology transfer mandates and pricing controls, to support the broader socialist pattern of society outlined in the resolution.
This structure contrasted with Schedule A by permitting private entry, albeit regulated, and sought to mitigate risks of underinvestment in capital-intensive fields through public pioneering. Empirical assessments of the era indicate that state-led entry in Schedule B spurred initial output in sectors like fertilizers, where public plants reached capacities exceeding 1 million tons annually by the early 1960s, complemented by private units under licensing quotas. However, the policy's insistence on state initiative often resulted in administrative bottlenecks, as licensing approvals averaged delays of 1-2 years per project, constraining private supplementation. Overall, Schedule B embodied a pragmatic extension of public sector dominance into mixed domains, prioritizing causal linkages between heavy intermediation and downstream growth over unfettered markets.

Schedule C: Regulated Private Initiative

Schedule C of the Industrial Policy Resolution, adopted by the Government of India on April 6, 1956, comprised all industries not enumerated in Schedules A or B, thereby designating them for development primarily through private sector initiative. This residual category encompassed a broad array of lighter and consumer-oriented industries, such as textiles, food processing, and small-scale manufacturing, where private enterprise was expected to drive expansion and innovation. The resolution emphasized that the future development of these sectors would generally be left to the "initiative and enterprise of the private sector," with the state providing supportive infrastructure like power, transport, and fiscal incentives to facilitate growth.
Despite this reliance on private participation, operations in Schedule C industries were subject to regulatory controls to align with national economic objectives, including balanced regional development and prevention of concentration of economic power. Private undertakings required licensing under the Industries (Development and Regulation) Act, 1951, which mandated government approval for establishing new units, substantial expansions, or manufacturing changes, ensuring that private activities conformed to the broader socialist pattern of society outlined in the policy. The state reserved the right to intervene directly if private progress proved inadequate or if planning imperatives demanded public involvement, such as through establishing state-owned units or offering financial assistance via equity participation, particularly to encourage cooperative enterprises.
This framework sought to leverage private capital and managerial efficiency for non-critical sectors while safeguarding public interest against potential excesses like monopolistic practices or inefficient resource allocation. By subjecting even these areas to licensing and oversight, the policy institutionalized a mixed economy approach, where private initiative operated within boundaries defined by state directives, though it did not specify numerical targets or detailed lists for Schedule C, reflecting its catch-all nature.

Implementation and Administrative Mechanisms

Establishment of Industrial Licensing Regime

The Industrial Policy Resolution of 1956 formalized the industrial licensing regime as a central mechanism for regulating private sector investment, extending the licensing powers enshrined in the Industries (Development and Regulation) Act, 1951 (IDRA). Under Section 11 of the IDRA, which became effective on May 8, 1952, no new industrial undertaking could be established, and no substantial expansion or diversification of existing units was permitted without a license from the Central Government. The 1956 resolution operationalized this by requiring licenses for private initiatives in industries under Schedules B and C, ensuring that all such activities aligned with national development priorities outlined in the Second Five-Year Plan. Licenses were granted only if the additional production was deemed essential for economic needs, thereby preventing overcapacity and directing resources toward planned targets.
Administratively, the regime vested authority in the Central Government, primarily through the Ministry of Commerce and Industry (later Industry and Supply), which coordinated applications via technical appraisal bodies like the Directorate General of Technical Development. Prospective entrepreneurs submitted detailed proposals specifying capacity, location, technology, and financing, subject to scrutiny for technical feasibility, economic viability, and compliance with objectives such as regional balance and prevention of monopolistic concentration. Inter-ministerial committees, including representatives from planning, finance, and technical ministries, reviewed applications to enforce policy consistency, with final approval resting with the Cabinet or designated ministers. Incentives like preferential licensing and subsidized infrastructure (e.g., power and water) were extended to units in backward regions to promote equitable growth.
The licensing process emphasized state control over private enterprise to foster a "socialist pattern of society," mandating that licenses incorporate conditions on output, pricing, and export obligations where relevant. By 1956, this regime had already processed initial applications from the post-1951 period, but the resolution expanded its scope by integrating it with the Mahalanobis growth model, prioritizing capital goods over consumer industries. Empirical data from early implementations showed delays averaging 6-12 months per application due to bureaucratic layers, though proponents argued it curbed speculative investments. Over time, the system evolved to include periodic reviews, but its foundational establishment in 1956 marked the entrenchment of discretionary government oversight in industrial allocation.

Expansion of Public Sector Undertakings

The Industrial Policy Resolution of 1956 marked a pivotal expansion of public sector undertakings (PSUs) by reserving 17 critical industries exclusively for state development under Schedule A, including atomic energy, arms and ammunition, iron and steel, heavy machinery, coal mining, and mineral oils, thereby directing substantial government resources toward establishing or scaling state-owned enterprises in capital-intensive sectors. This strategic allocation aimed to secure the "commanding heights" of the economy, with public investment prioritized to build industrial capacity where private initiative was deemed insufficient due to scale, risk, or national security imperatives. Implementation began immediately, aligning with the Second Five-Year Plan (19561961), which allocated over 20% of total plan outlay to large-scale industries dominated by PSUs.
Key establishments followed swiftly. The Oil and Natural Gas Corporation (ONGC) was incorporated on August 14, 1956, as a statutory body to monopolize hydrocarbon exploration and production, leading to discoveries that increased India's crude oil output from negligible levels to supporting domestic needs in subsequent decades. In steel production, the government expanded Hindustan Steel Limited (formed in 1954) to oversee three integrated public-sector steel plants: Bhilai Steel Plant (commissioned March 1959 with Soviet assistance, initial capacity 0.5 million tonnes annually), Durgapur Steel Plant (1959, British collaboration), and Rourkela Steel Plant (1959, German aid), collectively raising national steel capacity from 1.3 million tonnes in 1956 to over 3 million tonnes by 1961. Bharat Heavy Electricals Limited (BHEL) emerged in 1964 through the merger of state-owned electrical equipment firms dating to the late 1950s, focusing on power generation and transmission machinery to support heavy industry.
This phase saw rapid proliferation of PSUs, with central government enterprises growing from around five in 1951 to dozens by the mid-1960s, fueled by budgetary allocations and foreign aid tied to public projects. Investments in PSUs averaged annual growth rates exceeding 8% from 19601971, encompassing sectors like coal (via National Coal Development Corporation expansions) and machine tools, though initial output shares remained modest at about 3% of factory net output by 1961. Administrative mechanisms, including the Industries (Development and Regulation) Act of 1951, enforced monopoly through licensing, preventing private entry and channeling resources into state-led ventures despite emerging inefficiencies in project execution, such as delays in steel plant ramp-ups. By the 1970s, this foundation had scaled to over 100 major PSUs, embodying the policy's vision of state dominance in foundational industries.

Economic Impacts and Performance Metrics

Initial Industrial Capacity Building

The Industrial Policy Resolution of 1956, aligned with the Second Five-Year Plan (1956-1961), prioritized public sector investments in heavy industries to establish foundational manufacturing capabilities in a nascent economy previously reliant on imports for capital goods. This approach facilitated the creation of integrated steel plants at Bhilai (with Soviet assistance, commencing operations in 1959), Rourkela (with German collaboration, starting in 1959), and Durgapur (with British aid, operational by 1962), which collectively aimed to boost crude steel output from under 2 million tonnes annually pre-plan to support self-reliance in basic inputs. These initiatives, under entities like Hindustan Steel Limited, marked the state's direct role in scaling up metallurgical capacity, with plan allocations directing approximately 20% of the Rs. 4,800 crore outlay toward iron and steel, heavy chemicals, and machine-building sectors.
Industrial output expanded notably in the plan period, with the index of industrial production registering an average annual growth of around 7%, outpacing overall GDP growth of 4.1% against a targeted 4.5%. Coal production rose to meet energy demands for new facilities, while railway infrastructure enhancements supported logistics for heavy industry; these developments laid the groundwork for downstream manufacturing in engineering and construction. Public sector undertakings proliferated, embodying Schedule A monopolies in atomic energy and defense-related production, thereby addressing critical gaps in technological and infrastructural capacity that private entities, constrained by capital and scale, could not fill independently.
Despite shortfalls in achieving the full 25% national income target (actual growth approximated 20%), the policy's emphasis on capital-intensive sectors enabled India to transition from agrarian dominance toward an industrial base, with steel capacity additions exceeding 1 million tonnes by plan-end and fostering ancillary industries like cement and power generation. This phase demonstrated the efficacy of state-led import substitution in building initial scale, though efficiency metrics remained nascent amid foreign aid dependencies and planning rigidities.

Long-Term Constraints on Growth and Productivity

The Industrial Policy Resolution of 1956, by reserving key sectors for state control and mandating extensive licensing for private expansion, engendered barriers to firm entry and scale-up that persisted for decades, curtailing competition and dynamic resource reallocation essential for productivity gains. Empirical analyses indicate that these regulations distorted industrial allocation, with licensing delays and capacity restrictions preventing efficient firms from growing while protecting incumbents, resulting in total factor productivity (TFP) growth averaging near zero during the 1960s and remaining subdued through the 1980s. This regulatory framework, often termed the License Raj, required approvals from multiple government agenciesup to 80 in some casesfostering rent-seeking behaviors where resources were diverted toward lobbying rather than innovation or cost reduction.
Public sector undertakings (PSUs), prioritized under the policy's Schedules A and B, exhibited chronic underperformance, with average capacity utilization hovering below 70% in heavy industries by the 1970s and labor productivity lagging international benchmarks due to overstaffing and soft budget constraints. The emphasis on capital-intensive heavy industry, aligned with the Mahalanobis model, mismatched India's abundant labor supply, channeling investments away from labor-absorbing sectors and contributing to jobless growth patterns. Consequently, overall GDP growth stagnated at the so-called Hindu rate of approximately 3.5% annually from the 1950s to the 1980s, with industrial output expansion driven primarily by input accumulation rather than efficiency improvements.
These constraints manifested in technological lag, as protected domestic producers faced minimal pressure to adopt foreign innovations or process upgrades, leading to persistent quality deficits in manufactured goods and export underperformance relative to East Asian comparators. Import substitution strategies embedded in the policy insulated inefficient units from global competition, exacerbating X-inefficiency and raising unit costs, which in turn constrained downstream productivity in consumer-facing industries. By the late 1980s, studies attributed much of the economy's productivity shortfall to these institutional rigidities, with TFP contributions to growth minimal compared to post-1991 liberalization eras where deregulation spurred a marked uptick.

Criticisms from Economic and Empirical Perspectives

Origins and Effects of the License Raj

The License Raj, a system of pervasive industrial licensing and regulatory controls, originated with the Industries (Development and Regulation) Act of 1951, which mandated government approval via licenses for establishing new industrial units, substantial expansions of existing capacity, or shifts to new product lines in declared industries. The Industrial Policy Resolution of 1956 formalized and expanded this framework by classifying industries into three schedulesSchedule A for state monopolies, Schedule B for mixed state-private development, and Schedule C for regulated private enterprisewhile requiring licenses for private sector entry, capacity augmentation, and diversification to enforce resource allocation, prevent private monopolies, and direct investment toward national priorities like balanced regional growth and heavy industry. This policy, aligned with a socialist vision of state-directed development, centralized discretionary powers in bureaucratic agencies, creating a labyrinth of approvals that encompassed over 800 items by the 1980s and extended to imports, foreign exchange, and pricing.
The system's effects manifested in chronic inefficiencies and economic stagnation, contributing to India's "Hindu rate of growth" of approximately 3.5% annual GDP increase from the 1950s to the 1980s, far below potential and comparative market economies. Licensing distorted resource allocation by favoring incumbent large firmsoften through favoritism and circumventionwhile stifling new entrants and small-scale units; the Industrial Licensing Policy Inquiry Committee (Dutt Committee) of 1969 documented how the regime concentrated production in a few houses, failed to disperse industry geographically, and enabled corruption through bribable officials who allocated scarce licenses. Empirical analyses confirm negative impacts on firm dynamics, with firms established during the 19561990 licensing era exhibiting slower growth tied to age and reduced productivity due to barriers on expansion and innovation. 
Delicensing experiments in the 1980s and comprehensive dismantling post-1991 revealed causal harms: states with delicensed industries saw 17% higher output growth, increased firm entry, and elevated investment, particularly where labor regulations were flexible, underscoring how licensing suppressed competition and efficiency. Rent-seeking proliferated, as entrepreneurs invested in lobbying and bribes rather than productive capacity, fostering underutilized plants (average capacity utilization below 70% in many sectors) and black markets for inputs. Overall, the regime entrenched a low-equilibrium trap, prioritizing administrative control over market signals and yielding suboptimal industrial output growth averaging 5.7% annually from 19511990, hampered by misaligned incentives and bureaucratic delays averaging years for approvals.

Bureaucratic Failures, Corruption, and Rent-Seeking

The industrial licensing system formalized under the Industrial Policy Resolution of 1956 vested extensive discretionary powers in bureaucrats, leading to protracted delays and administrative bottlenecks that stifled industrial expansion. Processing a single license application could take 2-5 years on average, with firms facing arbitrary rejections or modifications based on opaque criteria, resulting in widespread underutilization of approved capacitiesoften hovering below 70% in key sectors like steel and chemicals by the 1960s. These failures stemmed from overburdened ministries lacking technical expertise, compounded by frequent policy revisions that rendered prior approvals obsolete, as documented in contemporaneous government reviews.
Corruption permeated the regime as firms navigated this labyrinthine bureaucracy, routinely offering bribes to officials for priority processing or to circumvent capacity limits. The proliferation of licenses under the 1956 framework created fertile ground for such practices, with economists attributing systemic graft to the need for discretionary approvals in import permits, capacity expansions, and project clearances; by the 1970s, estimates suggested bribes constituted 10-20% of project costs in regulated industries. High-profile cases, such as the 1957 Mundhra scandal involving undue influence on Life Insurance Corporation investments in failing companies linked to industrial licenses, exemplified how political-bureaucratic collusion favored connected entities, eroding public trust and resource allocation efficiency.
Rent-seeking flourished as private actors diverted substantial resources toward influencing policy outcomes rather than enhancing productivity, with large business houses expending efforts on lobbying to secure monopolistic licenses while blocking competitors. Anne Krueger's seminal analysis of India's import-competing regime, directly tied to the licensing controls of 1956, quantified how such activities consumed up to 7-10% of GDP in deadweight losses through unproductive expenditures on permits and quotas. Empirical studies confirm that this distortion reduced firm entry rates by over 50% in licensed sectors compared to unregulated ones, fostering oligopolistic structures where incumbents prioritized regulatory capture over innovation, as evidenced by stagnant total factor productivity growth averaging under 1% annually from 1960-1980.

Comparative Stagnation Relative to Market Alternatives

Under the Industrial Policy Resolution of 1956, India's economy experienced the so-called "Hindu rate of growth," averaging approximately 3.5% annual GDP expansion from the 1950s to the 1980s, with per capita GDP growth lagging at around 1.5%. This subdued performance stemmed from the policy's emphasis on import-substituting industrialization, extensive public sector dominance, and the licensing regime, which restricted private investment, fostered inefficiencies, and limited technological diffusion. In contrast, market-oriented alternatives pursued by East Asian economies, such as South Korea, delivered markedly superior outcomes through export promotion, selective incentives without pervasive controls, and openness to foreign competition.
South Korea, starting with a GDP per capita roughly comparable to India's in 1960 (approximately $84 versus $79 in current USD), achieved average annual GDP growth of over 8% from 1960 to 1990, elevating per capita GDP to about $6,500 by 1990. This trajectory reflected policies prioritizing private enterprise, rapid capital accumulation, and integration into global markets, rather than the inward-focused barriers of India's License Raj, which economists attribute to suppressing productivity and entrepreneurship. Similarly, other East Asian "tiger" economies like Taiwan and Singapore recorded sustained growth rates exceeding 7%, outpacing India's by factors of 2-3 times, as their frameworks allowed for dynamic resource allocation unhindered by bureaucratic approvals and capacity reservations.
Empirical comparisons underscore the causal role of regulatory constraints: India's industrial output growth decelerated to under 5% annually by the 1970s, hampered by rent-seeking and capacity underutilization, while East Asia's averaged 10-12% in manufacturing due to competitive pressures and innovation incentives. Studies highlight that had India adopted less interventionist approaches earlieremphasizing trade liberalization and reduced state monopoliesits growth could have mirrored East Asia's, potentially doubling per capita income by 1990 based on counterfactual models adjusting for policy distortions. The post-1991 liberalization, dismantling key 1956-era controls, accelerated India's GDP growth to 6-7%, providing retrospective evidence of untapped potential under market alternatives and validating critiques of the original policy's stagnation-inducing design.

Reforms, Reversal, and Enduring Lessons

Dismantling via 1991 Liberalization

The 1991 economic crisis, characterized by foreign exchange reserves covering just two weeks of imports as of June 1991, compelled the Indian government to undertake sweeping reforms under Prime Minister P. V. Narasimha Rao and Finance Minister Manmohan Singh. These measures directly targeted the restrictive framework of the 1956 Industrial Policy Resolution, which had imposed comprehensive industrial licensing and public sector reservations to enforce a socialist pattern of development.
On July 24, 1991, the government issued the Statement on Industrial Policy, which abolished licensing requirements for nearly all manufacturing sectors, retaining them only for 18 industries linked to security, strategic, or environmental risksdown from a broad regime that had mandated approvals for most private investments since 1956. This de-licensing applied to 82 additional industries beyond partial relaxations in the 1980s, effectively eliminating the bureaucratic "License Raj" that had constrained private sector entry and expansion.
The policy also curtailed public sector exclusivity by reducing the reserved list from 17 industries under the 1956 resolutionencompassing arms, atomic energy, heavy industries, and coalto eight core strategic areas, including defense production, atomic energy, and mineral oils. Private and foreign firms gained entry into previously monopolized domains, with automatic approval for foreign equity up to 51% in 34 high-priority sectors, reversing the 1956 emphasis on state dominance in "commanding heights" of the economy.
Further dismantling included provisions for disinvestment of minority stakes in select public sector undertakings and the dilution of the Monopolies and Restrictive Trade Practices Act's stringent controls on large firms, fostering competition over administrative allocation. These reforms, influenced partly by IMF conditionalities amid the crisis bailout but driven by domestic recognition of stagnation, marked the decisive reversal of the 1956 policy's dirigiste model, prioritizing market signals over licensing quotas. By 1993, licensing persisted in only three sectors, solidifying the shift away from pervasive controls.

Post-Reform Growth Contrasts and Policy Reevaluations

Following the 1991 liberalization, India's economic growth accelerated markedly compared to the preceding decades dominated by the 1956 Industrial Policy Resolution's framework of extensive public sector reservations and licensing controls. From 1950 to 1990, GDP growth averaged approximately 3.5% annually, a period often termed the "Hindu rate of growth," constrained by bureaucratic inefficiencies and restricted private enterprise that limited capital allocation and technological adoption. In contrast, post-1991 reforms, which dismantled much of the licensing regime and reduced public sector monopolies from 17 industries to three, propelled average annual GDP growth to around 6% from 1991 to 2011, with peaks exceeding 8% in the mid-2000s, driven by expanded foreign direct investment, export surges, and productivity gains in manufacturing.
Industrial output and productivity metrics further highlighted these contrasts. Pre-reform manufacturing growth stagnated due to capacity underutilization and import substitution barriers, with total factor productivity barely advancing. Post-liberalization, firm-level data showed manufacturing productivity rising by 10-15% in the initial years, as reduced trade barriers and delicensing enabled competition, technology imports, and efficiency improvements, particularly in sectors like chemicals and machinery previously reserved for the state. Export growth rates nearly doubled post-1991 relative to the prior era, underscoring how the 1956 policy's inward-oriented controls had suppressed global integration and scale economies.
These empirical divergences prompted widespread policy reevaluations, with economists attributing pre-1991 stagnation to the 1956 resolution's overreliance on state-directed investment, which fostered rent-seeking and misallocated resources away from high-return private initiatives. Analyses post-reforms emphasized that partial pro-business shifts in the 1980s initiated modest acceleration, but the comprehensive 1991 dismantling of controlsreversing the resolution's core tenetsunleashed sustained dynamism by prioritizing market signals over bureaucratic fiat. Official assessments and academic reviews, including those from international bodies, concluded that the dirigiste model failed to adapt to global competitive pressures, contrasting sharply with faster-growing peers like South Korea, and validated liberalization as a causal driver of India's transition to a middle-income trajectory.
