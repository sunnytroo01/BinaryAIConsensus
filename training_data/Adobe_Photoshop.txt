 Adobe Photoshop is a proprietary raster graphics editor developed and published by Adobe Inc., serving as the industry standard for digital image manipulation, graphic design, and photo retouching since its initial release in 1990. It enables users to create, edit, and enhance photographs, illustrations, and composite images using advanced tools for layering, masking, and color correction, making it essential for professionals in advertising, film, web design, and fine art.
Developed by brothers Thomas and John Knoll, Photoshop originated in 1987 as a simple grayscale image display program called Display on a Macintosh Plus, which evolved into a full-fledged editing tool after Adobe licensed and further developed it. Key early milestones include its use in visual effects for films like The Abyss (1989) and Terminator 2 (1991), the introduction of layers in version 3.0 (1994) that revolutionized non-destructive editing, and the addition of adjustment layers and blend modes in 1995. By 1998, version 5.0's history panel and layer effects propelled its mainstream adoption, while later innovations like the healing brush (2002), content-aware fill (2010), and machine learning-powered select subject (2018) expanded its capabilities. In 2019, it launched on iPad, providing professional photo editing features optimized for touch interfaces, including layers, masks, brushes, retouching, and AI-powered Generative Fill, while supporting seamless workflows across devices but not serving as a full desktop replacement, followed by a web version in 2023 and integration of generative AI via Adobe Firefly for features like Generative Fill and Generative Upscale.
Available across desktop (Windows and macOS), web, and mobile platforms as part of Adobe's Creative Cloud subscription service, Photoshop offers subscription pricing starting at $22.99 per month for individual use. Its core features encompass image adjustments for tonal and color corrections, retouching tools to remove blemishes and objects, generative AI for expanding or filling image areas, and painting/drawing capabilities with brushes, shapes, and type tools. Over its 35-year history, Photoshop has earned recognition such as a Sci-Tech Award from the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences in 2018 and remains pivotal in creative industries, powering everything from magazine layouts to Hollywood blockbusters.

History

Origins and Creation

The development of Photoshop originated in 1987 when Thomas Knoll, a PhD student in computer vision at the University of Michigan, created a simple program named "Display" to address the limitations of his Macintosh Plus in rendering grayscale images on its monochrome screen. This initiative stemmed from frustrations in his research, where existing software failed to properly visualize the 256 shades of gray needed for image analysis, prompting him to write custom routines for image loading, display, and basic manipulation. The program was initially coded in Pascal, a language suited to the Macintosh's environment, and ran on hardware with minimal resources, such as the 1 MB RAM standard in the Macintosh Plus.
In 1988, Thomas's brother John Knoll, a visual effects supervisor at Industrial Light & Magic, joined the project and significantly expanded its capabilities into a full image editing tool, renaming it "ImagePro" temporarily before settling on Photoshop. John contributed key features for raster-based pixel-level editing, including basic adjustments for brightness, contrast, and color balance, aimed at replicating analog darkroom techniques digitally to assist professional photographers who sought more precise control over image processing without physical chemicals and prints. John Knoll famously used the image "Jennifer in Paradise," a color photo of his then-girlfriend, in early demonstrations to highlight the program's color editing capabilities. These enhancements were tested on an Apple Macintosh II, which supported color display and up to 5 MB of RAM in its configuration, allowing for more complex operations than the original grayscale viewer.
This demonstration marked a pivotal step, drawing initial interest from hardware vendors and paving the way for further collaboration, including Adobe's eventual involvement in its commercialization.

Adobe Acquisition and Early Commercialization

In 1988, following initial short-term licensing deals with scanner manufacturer Barneyscan and other hardware companies that sought to bundle the software, Adobe Systems opted instead to secure broader distribution rights from the Knoll brothers. Adobe licensed the software in September 1988 after a demonstration by John Knoll, with the formal agreement finalized in April 1989; this arrangement included royalties paid to the Knolls for each copy sold.
The program, originally named ImagePro, was renamed Photoshop in 1988 to avoid trademark conflicts with an existing graphics application of the same name. Before its standalone launch, approximately 200 copies of an early version (0.65) were distributed as the branded add-on "Barneyscan XP" with Barneyscan's film scanners, providing initial exposure to professional users in imaging workflows.
Adobe released Photoshop 1.0 on February 19, 1990, exclusively for the Apple Macintosh platform, at a list price of $895. Initial sales were modest but promising for a specialized tool, reflecting Adobe's expectations of a few hundred licenses per month among early adopters.
Marketing for the initial release focused on desktop publishing and graphic design professionals, emphasizing Photoshop's compatibility with Adobe's PostScript page description language to streamline high-quality print production and raster-to-vector workflows. A key boost came in 1990 through built-in support for Encapsulated PostScript (EPS) formats, enabling seamless integration with Aldus PageMaker and accelerating adoption in professional layout environments.

Features

User Interface and Core Tools

Adobe Photoshop's user interface has evolved significantly since its early versions, transitioning from modal dialogs that required sequential user interactions to a more flexible system of tabbed panels and customizable workspaces in modern releases. This shift allows users to dock, group, and rearrange panels such as Layers and Adjustments for efficient workflows tailored to specific tasks like photo retouching or graphic design. A notable update in Photoshop CS6 (2012) introduced a dark UI theme, featuring a charcoal-gray background to reduce eye strain and enhance focus on image content during extended editing sessions.
To facilitate quick adjustments to workspace visibility, Photoshop provides several keyboard shortcuts for managing panels, screen modes, and zoom levels:

Tab: Show or hide all panels, including the Tools panel and Options bar.

Shift + Tab: Show or hide all panels except the Tools panel and Options bar.

F: Cycle through screen modes (Standard, Full Screen with Menu Bar, Full Screen Mode).

Ctrl + 0 (Cmd + 0 on macOS): Fit the image to the screen.

Ctrl + + / Ctrl + - (Cmd + + / Cmd + - on macOS): Zoom in or out.

These shortcuts enable users to efficiently control interface elements and focus on the canvas. For a complete list of default shortcuts or to customize them, access Edit > Keyboard Shortcuts (Alt + Shift + Ctrl + K on Windows / Option + Shift + Cmd + K on macOS).
The core tools in Photoshop form the foundation for image manipulation, starting with selection tools that enable precise isolation of areas for editing. The Marquee tools create rectangular or elliptical selections, the Lasso tools allow freehand or polygonal paths, and the Magic Wand tool selects pixels based on color similarity for pixel-based selections. The Quick Selection Tool offers an alternative by allowing users to brush over areas for automatic edge detection. Complementary to these, the Crop tool trims images to desired compositions: select the Crop tool from the toolbar (shortcut C) to display adjustable crop borders; drag corner or edge handles to adjust boundaries; drag inside the border to reposition the image; optionally set aspect ratio, resolution, or presets like 1:1 or 16:9 in the Options bar; and press Enter (Windows) or Return (macOS) to apply the crop. This process remains consistent in Photoshop 2024 and 2025, with features like Content-Aware Fill available for smart expansion or fill. The Slice tool divides images into sections for web optimization, and the Move tool facilitates layer repositioning and alignment within the document.
To stretch an image non-proportionally (resize without maintaining aspect ratio), use the Free Transform command:

Select the image layer in the Layers panel.

Go to Edit > Free Transform (Ctrl+T on Windows / Cmd+T on macOS).

In the options bar, unlink the Maintain Aspect Ratio icon (chain link) to enable independent scaling.

Drag the side handles to stretch horizontally or vertically, or enter separate values in the Width (W) and Height (H) fields.

Press Enter to apply.

This directly stretches pixels, which may introduce distortion. For intelligent stretching that attempts to preserve important content while compressing less significant areas, use Edit > Content-Aware Scale (if available in your version).
A practical example of selection tools in use is making black bars or bands in an image transparent (such as letterboxing or other solid black areas). If the image is on a locked Background layer, users double-click the layer in the Layers panel to convert it to a regular layer. Using the Magic Wand Tool with Tolerance adjusted (typically 1032), they click on the black areas to select them based on color similarity. Alternatively, the Quick Selection Tool can brush over the areas for more complex or irregular shapes. The selection can be refined if necessary using Select > Modify > Expand or the Select and Mask feature for better edge accuracy. Pressing Delete removes the selected areas to create transparency, or users can apply Layer > Layer Mask > Hide Selection for non-destructive editing. For cases where the black is not pure #000000, the Color Range command (Select > Color Range) provides more effective selection. The resulting file should be saved in PNG format to preserve the transparency.
For creative input, the Brush and Pencil tools support painting and drawing with customizable opacity, flow, and shape dynamics. These tools integrate pressure sensitivity when used with Wacom tablets, enabling variable stroke width and intensity based on stylus pressure for natural artistic effects. The History panel complements these by providing non-destructive undo capabilities, tracking up to 1,000 steps (default 50, configurable in preferences) to revert edits without permanent changes.
Non-destructive editing is further enabled through adjustment layers, which apply modifications like tonal and color corrections without altering the original pixels. The Levels adjustment targets shadows, midtones, and highlights to balance exposure; Curves offers precise tonal mapping via a histogram-visualized graph for adjusting contrast and color; and Hue/Saturation shifts color properties such as vibrancy and tint across specific ranges.
A key concept in maintaining editability is Smart Objects, which embed vector graphics, raw files, or external references into layers without rasterization, preserving scalability and allowing repeated transformations like scaling or filtering without quality loss. Modern AI enhancements can refine these manual selections for greater accuracy in complex scenarios.

Advanced Editing and AI Capabilities

Adobe Photoshop's advanced editing capabilities extend beyond basic tools to enable sophisticated, non-destructive image manipulation through layer-based compositing. This technique allows users to stack multiple layers of images, adjustments, and effects while preserving the original data for iterative refinements. Central to this process are blending modes, which control how pixels from one layer interact with those beneath it, with a total of 27 modes available, including Multiply for darkening by multiplying color values and Overlay for blending based on luminosity and color to enhance contrast. Layer masks, grayscale thumbnails attached to layers, enable selective visibility by painting black to hide or white to reveal content, facilitating precise composites without permanent alterations. Clipping masks further refine this by constraining the visible area of a layer to the opaque regions of the layer below, ideal for applying adjustments like color corrections to specific shapes non-destructively.
For repairing, removing, and intelligently resizing elements, Photoshop offers intelligent tools that analyze surrounding pixels or image content to maintain visual coherence. Introduced in 2010, Content-Aware Fill uses algorithmic sampling from nearby areas to seamlessly replace selected objects or gaps, such as removing a person from a crowd by synthesizing realistic backgrounds; however, it may produce gray results when there is insufficient or uniform surrounding content for the tool to sample from, defaulting to an averaged color that appears gray if those pixels are uniform (e.g., large gray areas, sky, or blank layer regions), often occurring if the green sampling overlay in the Content-Aware Fill workspace excludes varied details or if sampling is limited to a layer without adequate content. Complementing this, the Spot Healing Brush tool automatically detects and blends textures from adjacent pixels to repair imperfections like blemishes or scratches, ensuring smooth, artifact-free results with minimal user input. Additionally, Content-Aware Scale (Edit > Content-Aware Scale) applies similar content-aware technology to resize or stretch images non-proportionally while preserving important content and protecting key details from distortion. Users can deselect Maintain Aspect Ratio in the options bar to enable independent width and height adjustments, drag bounding box handles or enter specific values, and use the Protect option with an alpha channel or Protect Skin Tones to safeguard specific areas. This tool is available in current versions of Photoshop.
Recent advancements in Photoshop version 26 (2025 release) integrate generative AI powered by Adobe's Firefly model to automate complex edits via natural language prompts. Generative Fill allows users to expand, remove, or modify image elementsincluding precise editing of date text by selecting the area and prompting for removal or replacement with new contentsuch as adding scenery or erasing distractionsby generating photorealistic content that matches the original style and context, leveraging cloud-based diffusion models for high-fidelity outputs; however, it is limited to 8-bit images and does not support 16-bit or 32-bit modes. No single dedicated AI tool exists specifically for batch replacement of date text in images, but Photoshop supports batch processing of such edits via Actions or Image Processor scripts; alternatively, for batch removal of timestamps, PicWish AI Watermark Remover employs AI inpainting to process multiple photos. AI-generated images can be edited in a Photoshop-like manner using Generative Fill by opening the image, selecting the area to modify with tools such as Lasso or Quick Selection, accessing Generative Fill from the Contextual Task Bar or Edit menu, entering a text prompt (or leaving it blank for removal), generating variations, and applying the selected result as a new layer that preserves the surrounding image context. Similarly, Generative Upscale enhances low-resolution images by increasing dimensions up to four times while preserving details and reducing artifacts, drawing on AI upscaling algorithms to add realistic textures without pixelation.
Neural Filters provide one-click AI-driven enhancements, streamlining tasks like portrait retouching and artistic transformations. Examples include the Smart Portrait filter, which smooths skin tones and adjusts facial features non-destructively; Style Transfer, which applies artistic styles from reference images, such as mimicking oil paintings; Landscape Mixer for blending landscape elements; and Colorize for adding hues to monochrome images; all using pre-trained neural networks for quick previews and refinements. Although Adobe Photoshop does not have a specific category or official list titled "creative filters," it offers numerous filters for creative and artistic effects through the Filter Gallery, which includes categories like Artistic, Brush Strokes, Sketch, Stylize, and Texture for painterly, hand-drawn, and textured results, with examples such as Colored Pencil, Dry Brush, Watercolor, and Poster Edges. These complement the AI-based Neural Filters for diverse artistic manipulations, as detailed in the comprehensive filter effects reference. The Remove Tool, updated in 2025, enables precise isolation and deletion of subjects like wires or background figures through AI detection and generative inpainting, processed via cloud computation for optimal accuracy.
A notable 2025 addition is the Harmonize feature, released in July, which automatically matches lighting, color, and shadows across multiple layers to create cohesive composites, powered by Firefly's image model for realistic blending in scenarios like product photography or scene integration. Photoshop is the preferred tool for precisely inserting a product into an A+ mockup, offering advanced control over layers, masking, lighting, shadows, and compositing. Adobe Firefly's AI features, such as Generative Fill and Harmonize, are integrated into Photoshop to enable seamless object insertion, blending, and realistic adjustments. Standalone Firefly excels at quick generation of mockups or concepts but lacks the precision needed for professional A+ content, often requiring refinement in Photoshop.
Adobe Sensei, Photoshop's AI framework, further supports these capabilities, as seen in the Select Subject command, which employs machine learning to automatically outline primary elements in images for efficient masking and editing workflows.
For users seeking a free alternative to Photoshop's editing capabilities, Photopea (photopea.com) offers an online editor with layers, brushes, and AI features like Magic Replace for inpainting-style edits on AI-generated images.

Technical Aspects

File Formats

Adobe Photoshop's native file format is the Photoshop Document (PSD), which serves as the default for saving and editing projects while preserving advanced features such as layers, layer masks, vector masks, adjustment layers, and alpha channels. The PSD format utilizes Run-Length Encoding (RLE) compression for image data to enhance efficiency without loss of quality, and it supports up to 56 channels including spot colors and alpha transparency. However, PSD files are limited to a maximum size of 2 GB and document dimensions of 30,000 by 30,000 pixels, making it suitable for standard workflows but insufficient for very large or high-resolution compositions. When a document exceeds these PSD limits, the Save As dialog restricts available formats to those supporting larger dimensions and sizes, such as PSB, TIFF, and PDF, even if the current file is in PSD format. This ensures compatibility while prompting users to select appropriate formats for oversized projects.
For handling larger projects, Photoshop employs the Large Document Format (PSB), an extension of PSD designed for files exceeding 2 GB or dimensions beyond 30,000 pixels, with support up to 300,000 pixels in width or height and theoretically up to 4 exabytes in size. The PSB format maintains all PSD capabilities, including layers and masks, ensuring seamless compatibility for professional applications like panoramic imaging or billboard design. This format is essential for high-resolution work where preserving editability is critical, though it remains exclusive to Photoshop for full feature support.
Photoshop also supports a range of import and export formats to facilitate interoperability with other applications and media types. The JPEG format is commonly used for web and compressed distribution, offering lossy compression in 8-bit or 12-bit depths for RGB and CMYK color modes, though it discards transparency and can introduce artifacts with higher compression levels. For print-quality output, the TIFF format provides lossless storage with options like LZW compression, supporting multiple color modes and layers in some cases, while enabling large file handling via BigTIFF for sizes over 4 GB. The PNG format excels in scenarios requiring transparency, such as preserving alpha channels after removing unwanted areas (for example, black bars) through selection tools and masking or deletion techniques, delivering lossless compression for 8-bit or 16-bit images in RGB or grayscale, without the jagged edges associated with GIF.
In the 2025 release, Photoshop introduced native support for AVIF, allowing users to open, edit, and save images in this format, which provides superior compression efficiencyoften up to 50% smaller file sizes than JPEG at equivalent visual qualityalong with support for high dynamic range (HDR) and wider color depths. Additionally, HEIF/HEIC support has been enhanced for compatibility with Apple devices, enabling import and export of HDR-enabled images on macOS 10.13 and later, though Canon-specific variants remain unsupported.
Photoshop's Save for Web (Legacy) feature facilitates optimization and export of images to web-friendly formats like JPEG and PNG. On Mac, this saves only the optimized web version without overwriting the original document such as PSD; the document remains open, requiring separate saving of the original via File > Save (Command + S) or File > Save As (Command + Shift + S) if changes were made.
A key limitation of the PSD format is that it does not embed fonts, requiring the recipient's system to have the same fonts installed for accurate text rendering; otherwise, substitutions occur. Similarly, vector data imported from Adobe Illustrator is not preserved as editable vectors in PSD unless placed as a smart object, which maintains scalability but requires the original AI file for full editing; flattening rasterizes the content irreversibly. These constraints emphasize PSD's raster-oriented nature, where layer support enhances compositing but demands careful management for cross-application workflows.

Plugins and Extensions

Adobe extensions for Photoshop are primarily accessed through the Creative Cloud Marketplace, a centralized platform within the Creative Cloud desktop application that allows users to discover, install, and manage add-ons developed by Adobe and its partners. These extensions enhance core functionalities such as color management and typography without requiring manual file placement. For instance, Adobe Color enables users to generate and apply color palettes directly within Photoshop workflows, facilitating consistent design schemes across projects. Similarly, Adobe Fontsformerly known as Typekitintegrates a vast library of fonts for seamless selection and application, supporting professional typography needs in image editing.
Third-party plugins, developed by independent vendors, extend Photoshop's capabilities in specialized areas like photo enhancement and effects, often installed as .8bf files compatible with the software's plugin architecture. A prominent example is the Nik Collection by DxO, with its 2025 version 8 offering tools for analog film emulation through modules like Color Efex Pro and selective adjustments via Viveza, allowing precise local edits to exposure, color, and structure. Another key plugin is Topaz Labs' Gigapixel AI, which uses machine learning to upscale images beyond Photoshop's native resolution limits, preserving details for high-quality enlargements up to 600% without artifacts. These plugins are typically installed via the Creative Cloud app's Marketplace or by placing .8bf files in Photoshop's Plugins folder, accessible through the Filters menu during editing sessions.
Photoshop has supported third-party plugins through its API and the .8bf format since version 1.0, enabling developers to create custom filters, automation scripts, and import/export modules that integrate deeply with the application's raster-based engine. Additional examples include ON1 Photo RAW, which provides raw file processing and effects integration as a plugin, allowing non-destructive edits and batch operations directly from Photoshop's interface. Fontself serves as a tool for creating custom fonts from vector shapes or images within Photoshop, streamlining font design for graphic artists without external software.
In 2025, trends in Photoshop plugins emphasize AI-driven enhancements, with tools like PixelSquid enabling the insertion and manipulation of 3D objects into 2D compositions, supporting rotation, scaling, and layered PSD exports for realistic product visualizations. Compatibility with Photoshop version 26 (released in 2025) mandates a 64-bit operating system and processor, ensuring optimal performance for these resource-intensive extensions on modern hardware.

Performance Considerations

When using Photoshop for complex photo retouching on older hardware, noticeable delays can occur with large files such as RAW images or PSD files with many layers and high resolutions. Slowdowns are common when applying filters, brushes, masks, or zooming. Overall slowness increases if using a hard disk drive (HDD) instead of a solid-state drive (SSD) for file loading and scratch disk operations. Processor limitations, such as slower speeds or fewer cores, further contribute to these performance issues.

Printing Considerations

The warning "The image is larger than the printable area" appears when printing if the image dimensions exceed the printable area of the selected paper, defined by the printer's non-printable margins. This can cause clipping at the edges. To resolve it, in the Print dialog (File > Print), expand "Position and Size" and select "Scale to Fit Media" to automatically adjust the image to fit the available area without clipping.

Installation on Windows

When installed via Adobe Creative Cloud on Windows, Adobe Photoshop defaults to the installation directory C:\Program Files\Adobe\Adobe Photoshop \, where corresponds to the version's release year (e.g., Adobe Photoshop 2024 or Adobe Photoshop 2025). The main executable file is located at Photoshop.exe within this directory. The Adobe Creative Cloud desktop application itself installs separately to C:\Program Files (x86)\Adobe\Adobe Creative Cloud\. These distinct locations allow for version-specific management of individual applications while centralizing cloud services.

Fixing Blank Dialog Boxes

A blank dialog box when attempting to use commands such as Save As, Open, or similar functions in Adobe Photoshop on Windows can result from corruption of the DialogPreferences file in the application's settings directory.
To resolve this issue, first close Photoshop. Then navigate to the following directory in File Explorer:
%AppData%\Adobe\Adobe Photoshop \Adobe Photoshop Settings
where refers to the major version number of the installed Photoshop (e.g., 2025 for Adobe Photoshop 2025).
Locate the file named DialogPreferences (it may appear with or without an extension, such as DialogPreferences.txt). Rename this file to DialogPreferences.old or a similar distinct name. Restart Photoshop, which will automatically recreate the file using default settings.
Alternatively, deleting the DialogPreferences file (instead of renaming it) will also prompt Photoshop to regenerate a new copy upon restart.
This troubleshooting method applies primarily to Windows installations due to the specific file path structure.

Version History

Pre-Release and Early Versions (1.07.0)

Adobe Photoshop's pre-release and early versions, spanning from 1.0 in 1990 to 7.0 in 2002, laid the groundwork for its evolution into a dominant image editing tool, focusing on core raster editing capabilities, platform expansion, and incremental enhancements in usability and functionality. These releases were primarily standalone products targeted at professional photographers, graphic designers, and desktop publishers, with each iteration addressing limitations in color handling, selection precision, and workflow efficiency while maintaining compatibility with evolving hardware like Macintosh and early Windows systems.
Version 1.0, released on February 19, 1990, exclusively for Macintosh systems running System 6.0.3 or later, introduced fundamental digital image editing tools such as basic selection, cropping, and grayscale adjustments for scanned photographs. It required minimal hardwarea 2 MB RAM and 8 MHz processorbut was limited to grayscale mode without color support, emphasizing simple raster manipulation over advanced compositing. This initial release marked Photoshop's commercial debut, enabling users to enhance and edit images in ways previously confined to darkroom techniques.
Photoshop 2.0, launched in June 1991 for Macintosh, expanded color capabilities to include RGB and CMYK modes, alongside new paths for precise vector-based selections and improved text tools for basic typography integration. Codenamed "Fast Eddy," it featured an upgraded user interface with better EPS rasterization, anti-aliasing, and font support, allowing seamless incorporation of Adobe Illustrator files into raster workflows. Still Mac-only, this version addressed early user feedback on color fidelity, making it viable for print production.
The release of Photoshop 3.0 in September 1994 for Mac OS 7, followed by Windows, IRIX, and Solaris ports in November, represented a pivotal advancement with the introduction of layers for non-destructive editing of image elements and tabbed palettes for organized tool access. Priced at $895 for Windows and $1,000 for Mac, it also refined the Magic Wand tool for more accurate selections based on color tolerance. Codenamed "Tiger Mountain" for Mac, this version broadened accessibility beyond Apple ecosystems, fostering adoption in cross-platform design environments.
Photoshop 4.0, released simultaneously for Windows and Macintosh in November 1996 and codenamed "Big Electric Cat," added adjustment layers for reversible color and tonal corrections, along with Actions for automating repetitive tasks via recordable scripts. It included the Navigator palette for zoomed previews, guides and grids for alignment, the Free Transform tool for non-destructive resizing, and support for multicolor gradients, plus 48 international language versions to expand global reach. These features streamlined professional workflows, particularly for batch processing in advertising and publishing.
In May 1998, Photoshop 5.0 introduced multiple undo via the History palette, the Magnetic Lasso for edge-snapping selections, and a Freeform Pen tool for flexible path creation, while enhancing color management with ICC profiles and making type layers fully editable. It also bundled ImageReady for web graphics optimization, including GIF animation and slicing, marking Photoshop's first foray into digital web preparation. Automation plugins further extended batch processing, solidifying its role in both print and emerging online media.
Photoshop 6.0, released in September 2000, brought vector shape tools for scalable graphics, allowing text to be placed and warped along paths for creative layouts, and added compatibility with Mac OS X for native Aqua interface support. New layer styles like drop shadows and bevels, along with content-aware warping controls, expanded artistic options, while improved web output via ImageReady integration enhanced slice-based optimization. This version emphasized hybrid raster-vector workflows, appealing to interface designers.
Finally, Photoshop 7.0, launched in March 2002 with full Mac OS X support, introduced the Healing Brush for seamless retouching by sampling and blending textures, alongside auto color correction tools for one-click white balance adjustments and a built-in File Browser for thumbnail-based organization and batch renaming. It also added spell-check for text and find/replace functionality, refining usability for photo restoration and cataloging tasks. By this point, these early versions had established Photoshop's market leadership through steady refinements in stability and core editing prowess.

Creative Suite Era (CSCS6)

The Creative Suite era (20032012) transformed Adobe Photoshop into a cornerstone of integrated creative software bundles, emphasizing enhanced collaboration, workflow efficiency, and professional-grade tools tailored for designers, photographers, and artists. With the launch of Adobe Creative Suite 1, Photoshop transitioned from a primarily standalone application to a key component within a cohesive ecosystem that included Illustrator, InDesign, and ImageReady, enabling seamless asset sharing and version management across projects. This bundling strategy addressed growing demands for multi-tool workflows in professional environments, such as advertising and publishing, by incorporating shared technologies like file format compatibility and centralized asset handling.
Photoshop CS (version 8.0), released in October 2003, introduced Version Cue, a collaboration tool that provided file versioning and check-in/check-out functionality to prevent conflicts in team-based editing. It also added non-destructive cropping, allowing users to adjust crop boundaries without permanently altering the original image data. As part of the inaugural Creative Suite bundle, Photoshop CS integrated closely with Adobe Illustrator, supporting vector-based workflows and extensible architecture for third-party plugins.
In April 2005, Photoshop CS2 (version 9.0) advanced retouching and organization capabilities with the Spot Healing Brush, which automatically sampled surrounding pixels for seamless repairs, and Smart Objects, enabling non-destructive scaling, warping, and filtering of embedded content. The suite-wide introduction of Adobe Bridge served as a robust file browser and metadata manager, streamlining asset previewing, batch processing, and integration with other CS2 applications like Illustrator and GoLive. These enhancements prioritized speed and precision for professional photographers handling high-resolution images.
Photoshop CS3 (version 10.0), launched in April 2007, focused on non-destructive editing and selection accuracy, introducing Smart Filters that applied effects like blurs or distortions as adjustable layers, and the Quick Selection tool, which used edge detection for intuitive subject masking. Native integration with Adobe Camera Raw 4.0 brought advanced raw processing features, such as recovery and fill light adjustments, directly into the Photoshop interface, reducing the need for separate conversions. The version also supported multicore processors for faster performance in the bundled Creative Suite 3.
Photoshop CS4 (version 11.0), released in October 2008, marked a technical leap with support for 64-bit architecture on Windows, allowing manipulation of files exceeding 4 GB in RAM-intensive tasks like large panoramas. It incorporated GPU acceleration through OpenGL for real-time previews and panning, alongside 3D editing tools for importing, rotating, and texturing 3D models. The new Adjustments panel centralized tonal and color corrections, while Vibrance adjustments preserved skin tones during enhancements, all within the expanded Creative Suite 4 ecosystem.
Building on these advancements, Photoshop CS5 (version 12.0) in May 2010 debuted Content-Aware Fill, an intelligent algorithm that removed objects by synthesizing surrounding textures, and Puppet Warp, which facilitated precise deformation of image elements like limbs or fabrics. Mini Bridge provided an in-app file browser for rapid asset access, complementing the full Bridge application in Creative Suite 5. These tools emphasized creative flexibility for compositing and retouching in professional pipelines.
Photoshop CS6 (version 13.0), released in May 2012, refined artistic and scalability features with the Blur Gallery, offering customizable bokeh, tilt-shift, and motion blur effects via smart objects, and enhanced vector masks for resolution-independent shapes and paths. As the culmination of the perpetual license model within Creative Suite 6, it represented the final boxed release before Adobe's shift to subscription-based Creative Cloud offerings announced in 2013. CS6 also improved overall performance with Mercury Graphics Engine enhancements for smoother 3D and video workflows.

Creative Cloud Era (CC 20132026)

The Creative Cloud era marked Adobe's transition to a subscription-based model for Photoshop, launching version 14.0 in June 2013 as part of the broader Creative Cloud suite. This shift emphasized cloud integration for seamless file syncing, asset sharing, and collaboration, departing from perpetual licenses and enabling continuous updates without major version overhauls. Priced at $19.99 per month for single-app access like Photoshop, the model initially targeted existing CS6 owners with promotional rates before standardizing.
Photoshop CC 2013 (version 14.0) introduced integration with Typekit for accessing thousands of fonts directly within the application, enhancing typography workflows for designers. Cloud saving features allowed users to sync settings, preferences, and libraries across devices via Creative Cloud storage, facilitating multi-computer workflows. Additional enhancements included a redesigned 3D panel modeled after the Layers panel for easier object manipulation and improved sharing options to Behance for portfolio integration.
In CC 2014 (version 15.0), new tools like Focus Mask enabled automatic selection of in-focus areas in images, refining selections based on depth for portrait and macro editing. Perspective Warp allowed non-destructive adjustments to image perspectives, useful for architectural and product photography corrections. These releases also expanded Creative Cloud Libraries for cross-app asset management and introduced Extract Assets for generating optimized web formats like SVG from layers.
CC 2015 (versions 16.0 and 17.0) brought artboards to the desktop, providing an infinite canvas for UI/UX design with multiple resizable panels mimicking device screens. Export as SVG became available for artboards, layers, and documents, streamlining vector output for web and mobile development. Other updates included enhanced content-aware tools and performance improvements for large files.
From CC 2017 to 2019 (versions 18.0 to 20.0), Photoshop advanced typography and selection capabilities. Variable fonts support arrived in 2017, allowing dynamic adjustments to weight, width, and slant within a single file for flexible text design. The Frame tool, introduced in 2018, simplified image placement by converting shapes into editable frames that automatically mask imported photos. By 2019, the Object Selection tool leveraged AI to detect and isolate subjects like people or vehicles with a single lasso or brush stroke, improving efficiency in compositing.
The 20202022 releases (versions 21.0 to 23.0) deepened AI integration through Neural Filters, debuting in 2020 as a gallery of Sensei-powered effects for tasks like skin smoothing, smart portrait adjustments, and colorization of black-and-white images. Sky Replacement, launched in 2021, used machine learning to detect horizons and swap skies while matching lighting and colors in landscapes. Adaptive presets in 2022 extended to color grading, automatically adjusting tones based on image content for faster photo corrections.
In 20232024 (versions 24.0 to 25.0), Generative Fill entered beta in 2023, powered by Adobe Firefly, enabling users to add, remove, or expand content via text prompts while maintaining realistic integration. The Remove Background tool, enhanced in 2024, provided one-click subject isolation with improved edge detection for e-commerce and social media prep. These AI features prioritized ethical development, aligning with Adobe's post-2023 guidelines emphasizing accountability, transparency, and content authenticity in generative tools.
Photoshop 2025 (version 26.0) expanded Firefly capabilities with Image Edit for precise object modifications, Generative Upscale for artifact-free resolution enhancement up to 4x, and Harmonize for blending elements by matching lighting and shadows. Updates through September 2025 (v26.11) added native AVIF format support for efficient web images and dynamic text reflow for responsive layouts. In October 2025, Photoshop 2026 (version 27.0) introduced performance enhancements including immediate responsiveness for brushes, sliders, transforms, layers, and text edits; faster file operations; a modernized UI with improved accessibility; and Quiet Mode to minimize distractions. Guides remain document-specific and are not automatically inherited or maintained when creating a new document; new documents start without guides, with no changes in releases up to version 27.4 (February 2026). Community-developed patches for Wine have enabled the installation and basic functionality of the Photoshop 2025 installer on Linux, though this represents unofficial compatibility without Adobe support. By 2025, Creative Cloud surpassed 32 million subscribers, reflecting widespread adoption of these cloud-AI innovations. As of March 3, 2026, the current version is Photoshop 2026 with the latest update being version 27.4 from February 2026.

Related Products

Current Applications

Adobe Photoshop serves as the core desktop application within Adobe's ecosystem, with several related products extending its capabilities across consumer, professional, and mobile workflows as of 2025.
Photoshop Elements 2026 is a consumer-oriented version designed for hobbyists and non-professionals, offering simplified tools such as guided edits, an AI-powered Organizer for managing photo libraries, and the AI-powered Restore Photo tool. The Restore Photo tool automatically detects and removes scratches, cracks, noise, fading, and other wear from old or damaged photos, while enhancing contrast and recovering lost details; it is accessed via Enhance > Restore Photo, with adjustments available via sliders for noise reduction and face enhancement (for portraits), and manual refinements using tools like Remove or Crop. It includes generative AI features with 25 monthly credits for actions like content creation and removal, accessible via a 3-year term license without recurring subscription. Priced at $149.99, it supports offline editing for core features, with generative AI requiring an internet connection.
Lightroom, available in Classic and cloud-based CC editions, acts as a companion for raw image processing, cataloging, and non-destructive adjustments, complementing Photoshop's pixel-level editing. Lightroom Classic emphasizes local storage and advanced tethering for studio workflows, while Lightroom CC prioritizes cloud synchronization for cross-device access. Both are bundled in the Creative Cloud Photography plan, which includes Photoshop and 20GB of cloud storage for $9.99 per month on an annual commitment (equivalent), enabling photographers to streamline workflows from capture to final output. In 2025, Lightroom added enhanced AI denoising and masking tools powered by Adobe Firefly.
Photoshop Express provides a free mobile and web application for quick, on-the-go edits such as cropping, filters, and basic retouching, suitable for social media content creation. Premium features, including advanced healing tools and ad removal, require a subscription starting at $4.99 per month or $34.99 annually, integrating seamlessly with other Adobe apps for asset sharing. It is part of the broader Adobe Express ecosystem for quick creative tasks.
Photoshop for iPad, introduced in 2019 as a touch-optimized version, features a contextual user interface with a left toolbar for tools, right panels for layers and adjustments, supporting multitouch gestures such as pinch to zoom and two-finger rotation, as well as Apple Pencil for precision. It provides professional photo editing features optimized for touch interfaces, including opening and editing large PSD files; working with layers, masks, and adjustment layers; brushes for painting and drawing; selections and masks; retouching with the Healing Brush, Dodge, Burn, Sponge, and Content-Aware Fill; AI-powered Generative Fill and Generative Expand; Curves for tonality adjustments; transform, crop, and rotate operations; Type layers; Smart Objects; Camera Raw support; integration with Adobe Fresco and Lightroom; touch gestures and shortcuts; keyboard shortcuts; and cloud auto-save and sync. These capabilities support seamless mobile workflows with cross-device editing, though it is not a full desktop replacement. It delivers core editing tools adapted for stylus input, enabling advanced edits like head swaps using built-in tools such as Select Subject, Object Selection, layer masks, and adjustments, although lacking an official tutorial with iPad-specific interface screenshots. Adapted steps from general Photoshop face swap guidance include: 1. Open both images via import options. 2. In the source image, tap the selection tool and use Select Subject or Object Selection to isolate the head. 3. Copy the selection and paste it as a new layer onto the target image. 4. Use Free Transform (via Edit menu or pinch gestures) to resize, rotate, and align the head, temporarily lowering opacity for alignment. 5. Add a layer mask (Layer > Layer Mask > Reveal All) and use the Brush tool (black to hide, white to reveal) to blend edges. 6. Refine with adjustments like Match Color (Image > Adjustments) for skin tone matching and the Blur tool for seamless integration. With the 2025 update enhancing full layer support, generative fill via text prompts, and contextual task bar for efficient mobile workflows, it supports complex compositions on the go, syncing edits with desktop versions.
All current Photoshop-related applications integrate through Creative Cloud Libraries, allowing users to share colors, shapes, and assets across desktop, mobile, and web platforms for consistent branding. Adobe Projects, a collaborative feature complementing Libraries, enables bulk sharing of multiple assets with teams for enhanced collaboration, such as inviting contributors to organized folders with permission controls.

Discontinued Applications

Adobe has discontinued several applications that were originally developed as spin-offs or companions to Photoshop, primarily to streamline its product portfolio, integrate features into core tools, and shift focus toward cloud-based and unified mobile experiences. These discontinuations reflect Adobe's strategic evolution following key acquisitions and the transition to Creative Cloud, allowing resources to concentrate on high-impact capabilities like Photoshop Express for mobile editing.
Photoshop Fix, launched in 2016 as a mobile app for iOS and Android, specialized in on-the-go retouching tasks such as healing, cloning, liquify, and color adjustments, enabling quick fixes for photos directly from devices. It was designed to complement desktop Photoshop by offering touch-based tools for professional-grade edits without needing a computer. Adobe discontinued Photoshop Fix on June 4, 2024, after removing it from app stores earlier that year, as its core features were merged into the more comprehensive Photoshop Express app to simplify the mobile ecosystem and reduce redundancy.
Similarly, Photoshop Mix, introduced in 2014 initially for iOS and later expanded to Android, focused on layer-based compositing and blending for mobile users, allowing seamless combination of images, adjustments, and light effects to create collages or composites. This app targeted creative professionals needing portable alternatives to Photoshop's layer system for quick projects. Like Fix, it was discontinued on June 4, 2024, with its functionalities integrated into Photoshop Express to unify Adobe's mobile photo editing tools under a single, evolving platform.
ImageReady, first bundled with Photoshop 5.5 in 1998, served as a dedicated tool for web graphics optimization, including GIF animation creation, image slicing for web layouts, and file compression to reduce load times without quality loss. It was essential for early web designers working alongside Photoshop for raster-based web assets. Adobe discontinued ImageReady with the release of Creative Suite 3 in 2007, transferring its key featuressuch as Save for Web & Devices, now available as Save for Web (Legacy) in current Photoshop versions for web optimizationdirectly into Photoshop to eliminate the need for a separate application and streamline workflows in the evolving digital design landscape. In Photoshop on Mac, Save for Web (Legacy) saves only the optimized web version of the image (e.g., JPEG, PNG) and does not save or overwrite the original document (PSD or other format); the document remains open, so users should save it separately using File > Save (Command + S) or File > Save As (Command + Shift + S) if changes were made.
Adobe Fireworks, acquired from Macromedia in 2000, was a vector and bitmap editor optimized for web prototyping, interface design, and interactive mockups, supporting rapid creation of screen graphics, symbols, and export to web formats. It filled a niche between Photoshop's raster focus and Illustrator's vector precision, particularly for web and UI work. Development ceased in May 2013 as Adobe prioritized broader Creative Cloud integrations, redirecting efforts to tools like Edge Animate (later also discontinued) and enhancing Photoshop and Illustrator for similar tasks, in line with post-acquisition portfolio rationalization after the 2005 Macromedia merger.
Adobe Muse, released in 2012, provided a visual website builder for designers without coding expertise, allowing drag-and-drop creation of responsive sites integrated with Photoshop and other Adobe assets for graphics and layouts. It aimed to democratize web design by exporting clean HTML/CSS directly. Support ended on March 26, 2020, with no new features added since 2018, as Adobe folded its site design capabilities into Adobe XD to consolidate prototyping and UI/UX tools within the Creative Cloud suite, emphasizing cloud unification and modern web standards.

Cultural and Economic Impact

Influence on Industries

Adobe Photoshop has profoundly transformed multiple industries since its introduction in the early 1990s, serving as a cornerstone for digital image manipulation and creative workflows. By enabling precise editing capabilities that surpassed traditional analog methods, it accelerated the shift from physical to digital processes across photography, graphic design, advertising, film, and publishing. This influence is evident in its adoption as an industry standard, powering everything from professional retouching to complex visual effects and contributing significantly to economic growth in creative sectors.
In photography, Photoshop revolutionized workflows by introducing a digital darkroom that allowed photographers to scan film, perform manipulations, and output high-quality prints via inkjet printers, effectively replacing many aspects of traditional film processing. This transition streamlined post-production, enabling adjustments like dodging, burning, and color correction that were once labor-intensive darkroom tasks. By the early 2000s, Photoshop had become the dominant tool for professional photo retouching, with over 90% of creative professionals relying on it for image editing tasks.
Photoshop's role in graphic design and advertising is equally pivotal, establishing it as the go-to software for compositing images, layering elements, and creating campaign visuals. Designers use its tools to blend photographs, add text, and apply effects, making it indispensable for producing advertisements, web graphics, and branding materials. In 2025, Adobe holds approximately 70% of the creative software market, and more than 90% of Fortune 500 companies utilize Adobe tools, including Photoshop, for their visual content creation. As of September 2025, 99% of Fortune 100 companies have used AI features in Adobe applications, further driving adoption and revenue growth in AI-powered creative tools.
In film and entertainment, Photoshop facilitated advancements in visual effects, particularly through matte painting and compositing techniques. Co-creator John Knoll, a visual effects supervisor at Industrial Light & Magic, applied Photoshop in groundbreaking work on Star Wars films, such as creating digital environments and integrating elements for scenes in the prequel trilogy. Its seamless integration with Adobe After Effects allows for importing layered PSD files to animate images, enhancing post-production pipelines for motion graphics and VFX in movies and television.
Economically, Photoshop's ecosystem has driven substantial revenue for Adobe, with the company's Digital Media segmentincluding Creative Cloud applications like Photoshopgenerating $15.86 billion in fiscal year 2024, reflecting 12% year-over-year growth. This success has spurred job creation in digital retouching, where thousands of specialized positions exist globally, from photo editors in advertising agencies to VFX artists in entertainment studios. A key example of its early impact was the 1990s adoption in publishing following the desktop publishing revolution, where Photoshop integrated with tools like Adobe Illustrator to enable efficient image preparation for magazines and books, transforming print production.

Controversies and Ethical Issues

Adobe Photoshop has faced significant criticism for enabling extensive image manipulation, leading to high-profile scandals that raised questions about journalistic integrity and authenticity. In 2010, the magazine admitted to publishing a fabricated image of six symmetrical fighter jets submitted by contributor William Lascelles, who confessed to creating it digitally after initial claims of it being a genuine shot, highlighting ongoing ethical lapses in photo verification processes. These incidents underscored Photoshop's role in blurring the line between reality and fabrication, prompting National Geographic to implement stricter guidelines against manipulation.
The software's widespread use in the beauty industry has drawn scrutiny for perpetuating unrealistic body standards through retouching, contributing to negative effects on public perception and mental health. Extensive digital alterations in advertisements and editorials have been linked to increased body dissatisfaction, particularly among young women, as images are slimmed, smoothed, and idealized to an unattainable degree. In response, France enacted a law in 2017 requiring commercial photos of models altered to appear thinner or thicker to carry a "photographie retouche" label, with fines up to 37,500 for non-compliance, aiming to promote transparency and combat harmful stereotypes. This legislation reflects broader calls for regulation, as similar concerns have influenced policies in Israel and the UK.
With the integration of AI features like Generative Fill in Photoshop since 2023, ethical concerns have intensified around data usage, copyright infringement, and the potential for deepfakes. Adobe's Firefly AI models, which power these tools, are trained exclusively on licensed content from Adobe Stock, public domain works, and openly licensed materials, with a post-2023 policy ensuring creator compensation and opt-out options to address intellectual property issues. However, critics argue that Generative Fill raises risks of generating misleading content, including deepfakes that could exacerbate misinformation, while ongoing debates question whether AI outputs infringe on artists' copyrights despite Adobe's safeguards. By 2025, these tools have amplified calls for ethical AI guidelines in creative software.
Legal challenges have targeted Adobe's business practices, particularly its subscription model and market dominance. In 2024, the U.S. Federal Trade Commission sued Adobe and two executives, alleging deceptive tactics in the "Annual, Paid Monthly" plan, including hidden early termination fees and barriers to cancellation that trapped consumers in unwanted subscriptions. Concurrently, the European Commission scrutinized Adobe's attempted $20 billion acquisition of Figma under antitrust laws, citing potential monopolization of digital design tools, which ultimately led to the deal's abandonment in December 2023 amid competition concerns.
To mitigate AI-related misinformation, Adobe introduced Content Credentials in 2024 as part of the Content Authenticity Initiative, embedding tamper-evident metadata into images and videos to track edits, origins, and AI usage, providing a verifiable digital "nutrition label" for content creators and viewers. This feature, integrated into Photoshop and other tools, aims to restore trust by disclosing generative AI involvement and preventing unauthorized alterations.
