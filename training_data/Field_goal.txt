 A field goal in American football is a scoring play worth three points, achieved when the offensive team kicks the ball from the field of play through the vertical plane of the opponent's goalposts above the crossbar and between the uprights, using either a place kick or a drop kick. The kick must originate from on or behind the line of scrimmage or from the spot of a fair catch, and the ball must not touch the ground or any offensive player before passing through the goal.
Field goals are typically attempted on fourth down when a team is positioned close enough to the end zone to make the kick feasible, often within 50 yards, serving as a conservative alternative to going for a first down or turning the ball over on downs. Performed by specialist placekickers who use a tee for placement and receive blocking from teammates known as the "protection unit," successful field goals require precise technique, including a holder to position the ball and a snap from the long snapper to initiate the play. In professional leagues like the NFL, field goals play a crucial role in close games, influencing strategies such as two-minute drills and end-of-half decisions.
The field goal has been a fundamental element of American football since the sport's early days in the late 19th century, initially executed primarily via drop kicks before the place kick became dominant in the early 20th century due to advancements in equipment and technique. Rule changes have shaped its prominence: in 1933, the NFL moved goalposts from the end line to the goal line to facilitate longer kicks and reduce ties, only to relocate them back to the end line in 1974 to discourage excessive field goal attempts and promote touchdowns. Modern innovations, including square-toed kicking shoes phased out in the 1980s and improved synthetic footballs, have extended ranges; the NFL record for the longest field goal is 68 yards, set by Jacksonville Jaguars kicker Cam Little on November 2, 2025. Career leaders in field goals made include Adam Vinatieri with 599, underscoring the position's evolution into a high-precision specialty.

Definition and Rules

Basic Definition

In American and Canadian football, a field goal is a scoring play in which the ball is kicked through the plane of the opponent's goalposts from the field of play during a down from scrimmage. This method of scoring awards three points to the kicking team and differs from a touchdown, which is worth six points and involves advancing the ball into the end zone, an extra point attempt worth one point following a touchdown, and a punt, which is a defensive kick intended to advance the ball without scoring.
The goalposts consist of two vertical uprights connected by a horizontal crossbar and are positioned on the end line, forming the boundary of the end zone. In the NFL, for example, the uprights extend 35 feet above the crossbar, which is elevated 10 feet from the ground, providing a defined target for the kick.
A less common variant of the field goal is the fair catch free kick, which permits the receiving team an uncontested attempt after signaling for a fair catch on an opponent's punt, without requiring a scrimmage; if successful, it counts as a standard three-point field goal.

Scoring and Legal Requirements

A successful field goal is worth three points in American football, awarded when the entire ball passes through the vertical plane of the goaldefined as the area above the crossbar and between the uprights, or if above the uprights, between their outside extensionswithout first touching the ground or any offensive player after the kick.
In the National Football League (NFL), the goalposts feature uprights that are 18 feet 6 inches apart inside measurement, with the crossbar positioned 10 feet above the ground; the uprights extend an additional 35 feet above the crossbar and measure between 3 and 4 inches in diameter, topped by an orange ribbon 4 inches by 42 inches. College football under National Collegiate Athletic Association (NCAA) rules uses identical dimensions for the uprights (18 feet 6 inches apart) and crossbar height (10 feet). High school football, governed by the National Federation of State High School Associations (NFHS), widens the uprights to 23 feet 4 inches apart inside measurement while maintaining the 10-foot crossbar height.
Field goal attempts must originate from behind the line of scrimmage during a scrimmage down, executed as a placekick or drop kick; the sole exception is the fair catch kick, a free kick allowed from the spot of a fair catch without defensive rush, which can score three points if successful. After the kick, the ball must not touch the ground or any offensive player before entering the goal plane to count as a score.
Upon a successful field goal, the game clock stops immediately when the ball crosses the goal plane, with the clock remaining stopped until the subsequent snap in standard situations; in the final two minutes of either half, this stoppage aligns with two-minute clock rules, preventing the clock from restarting until the next play begins, thereby preserving time for the non-scoring team.
Violations of procedural rules during a field goal attempt result in penalties, such as the five-yard loss and replay of the down for illegal participation (e.g., too many men on the field), which occurs if 12 or more players from one team are on the field at the snap. Other illegal procedures, like an invalid snap or offside alignment, also incur a five-yard penalty and loss of down, nullifying the attempt if it affects the play.

Execution

Kicking Technique

The kicking technique for a field goal involves a precise sequence of movements designed to generate power, accuracy, and the necessary trajectory to clear the uprights. The kicker typically begins with a measured approach run, taking three steps backward from the ball's placement point and then two lateral steps to the side, aligning the body for a straight path toward the target. This approach, often a four-step sequence (left-right-left-right for a right-footed kicker), allows the kicker to build momentum while maintaining balance. The plant foot is then positioned firmly next to the ball, about 6 to 8 inches to the side, pointing toward the target to stabilize the body and facilitate hip rotation. Finally, the kicking leg swings through with the foot striking the ball's lower half using the instep or laces, aiming for a contact point approximately 4 inches above the ball's tip to impart upward lift and forward drive.
Two primary styles of field goal kicking have evolved historically: the straight-on technique, which uses the toes or laces directly facing the ball, and the soccer-style technique, which employs the side of the foot (instep) at an angle. The straight-on method, dominant in early American football, involved a head-on approach and toe contact for a more linear strike but offered limited power and a smaller margin for error due to the narrow contact surface. In contrast, the soccer-style kick, introduced to the NFL by Hungarian immigrant Pete Gogolak in 1964, angles the body and uses the instep for a broader contact area, enabling greater hip rotation, foot speed, and angular momentum for enhanced distance and control. This style became dominant by the late 1960s and 1970s, revolutionizing the position as field goal success rates improved from under 60 percent in the early 1960s to around 80 percent today, largely attributed to its biomechanical advantages.
Several factors influence the distance and accuracy of a field goal kick, including ball rotation, environmental conditions, and surface variables. Kickers typically impart an end-over-end rotation to the ball, which minimizes aerodynamic drag compared to a spiral and promotes a more stable, predictable trajectory by reducing wobble and allowing the ball to "tumble" through the air efficiently. Wind direction and speed can deflect the ball laterally or reduce carry, with crosswinds posing the greatest challenge to alignment; temperature and altitude also play roles, as colder air increases density and drag while higher elevations decrease it, potentially extending range. Field conditions, such as grass length, moisture, or turf type, affect ball placement and footing stability, further impacting the launch angle and velocity essential for success.
Training for field goal kicking emphasizes repetition, progression, and physical conditioning to refine technique under varying conditions. Kickers often start with elevated tees (1 to 2 inches high) for shorter distances, gradually lowering the tee height and increasing range to simulate game scenarios, building confidence from 20-yard attempts up to 50 yards or more. Drills incorporate tracking makes and misses to set session goals, typically aiming for 100 to 300 kicks per week across multiple sessions, focusing on consistency in approach and contact. Strength training, including squats and resistance exercises like pushing blocking sleds, develops explosive lower-body power to enhance kick velocity. The holder's role in precisely placing the ball laces away aids alignment during these sessions.
The evolution of footwear has paralleled the shift in kicking styles, optimizing contact and performance. Early straight-on kickers used square-toed cleats or modified shoes to maximize toe surface area for direct strikes, a design common through the mid-20th century that provided stability but limited flexibility. With the rise of soccer-style kicking, modern pointed-toe cleats became standard, offering better instep alignment, pivoting for the angled approach, and overall mobility, contributing to the technique's widespread adoption and improved outcomes.

Team Setup and Roles

In American football, the field goal unit operates as a coordinated special teams group, with each player fulfilling a precise role to facilitate a successful attempt from scrimmage. The unit typically includes the long snapper, holder, kicker, and a protection contingent, aligning in a formation designed to protect the kick while ensuring a smooth snap-to-kick sequence. This setup prioritizes speed and precision, as the entire operation often unfolds in under two seconds to counter defensive pressure.
The long snapper serves as the center of the unit, positioned approximately 7 to 8 yards behind the line of scrimmage for field goal attempts. This player must deliver a firm, accurate snap directly to the holder in a low, spiraling trajectory, typically between the holder's legs, to allow immediate ball placement while minimizing interception risks by the defense. Long snappers are specialized athletes trained for this short-distance hike, distinct from standard centers due to the emphasis on velocity and consistency under duress.
The holder, often the team's punter serving dual roles, positions 7 to 8 yards behind the snapper and catches the incoming ball cleanly. Upon securing the snap, the holder swiftly places it on the ground, with the laces oriented away from the kicker for optimal contact, and may adjust the angle based on the kicker's preferred striking method. The holder also signals the kicker with a verbal or visual cue once positioned, and remains vigilant for time-sensitive situations, such as calling a timeout if the play clock nears expiration.
Forming the frontline defense against blockers, the protection unit consists of 7 to 9 linementypically drawn from offensive line reserves or special teams specialistswho align tightly on the line of scrimmage. These players create a compact wall, often in a traditional "wings" formation with the snapper in the middle and blockers fanning out to cover gaps, using techniques like inside-foot overlaps to seal off rush lanes and prevent defensive penetration. Their primary objective is to absorb and redirect the opposing team's 10 or fewer rushers (limited by rules), buying the 1.2 to 1.5 seconds needed for the kick.
While the standard scrimmage formation remains consistent across scenarios, teams make minor adjustments for indoor versus outdoor venues and surface types. In indoor stadiums, which often feature artificial turf, the protection unit and holder may opt for turf-specific cleats to enhance traction and stability, as synthetic surfaces provide less natural grip than outdoor natural grass fields. Outdoor grass setups occasionally require the holder to account for minor elevation variances from uneven terrain, though the core alignment and snap distance stay uniform. These adaptations ensure reliability without altering the fundamental roles.

Strategy and Decision-Making

Situational Factors

In end-of-half or end-of-game scenarios, teams frequently attempt field goals to secure leads, tie the score, or force overtime when time is limited and a touchdown is unlikely. For instance, coaches prioritize field goal tries in the final seconds to add three points without risking a turnover, as seen in practices emphasizing these high-stakes moments where a successful kick can determine the outcome.
Field position plays a critical role in deciding whether to attempt a field goal, with teams typically considering kicks from within 50 yards of the goal line due to high success rates in that range. In the NFL, field goals under 50 yards succeed at rates around 85-90%, making them a reliable option when the offense advances to the opponent's 33-yard line or closer, whereas longer attempts drop to about 70-75% success.
Adverse weather conditions, such as wind and rain, significantly influence field goal decisions by reducing accuracy and prompting more conservative strategies. Strong winds exceeding 20 mph can lower field goal percentages to approximately 77%, while rain affects ball grip and trajectory, often leading teams to avoid attempts beyond medium range or opt for punts instead.
On fourth down, the choice between attempting a field goal, punting, or going for a first down hinges on distance to the goal line, with analytics favoring field goals when the line of scrimmage is within 40-45 yards of the end zone to maximize expected points. For example, from the opponent's 35-yard line (a 52-yard attempt), punting may be preferred over a longer kick, but closer positions tilt toward the field goal to secure points rather than risk failure on a conversion try.
Historical trends show a marked increase in field goal attempts since the 1990s, driven by advancements in kicker training and equipment that have boosted overall accuracy from about 74% in 1990 to over 85% in recent seasons. This improvement has particularly elevated long-range success, with 50-plus-yard attempts rising from roughly one every three games in the 1990s to a near-weekly occurrence today, encouraging more aggressive usage in various situations.

Risk Assessment

In the National Football League (NFL), field goal success rates vary significantly by distance, influencing the risk calculus for coaches. For attempts from 39 yards or shorter during the 2024 season, kickers converted 98% of tries, reflecting high reliability for shorter kicks. Longer attempts, however, carry greater uncertainty; from 50 yards or beyond, the success rate was nearly 70% in 2024, down from historical averages but still elevated due to improved kicking techniques. Overall, the league-wide field goal make percentage stood at just over 85% for the season, with average attempt distances reaching 40 yards.
Deciding between a field goal attempt and going for it on fourth down involves weighing potential rewards against the risk of losing possession. Analytics models indicate that field goals provide a secure three points with minimal turnover risk, but failure results in no gain and a change of possession at midfield or closer. In contrast, attempting a fourth-down conversion offers the chance to sustain a drive and potentially score more, yet a stop by the defense hands the opponent favorable field position, often leading to their scoring opportunity. Studies show that while field goals are preferable in high-risk, short-yardage situations near the end zone, going for it becomes optimal beyond certain distances, such as fourth-and-5 from the opponent's 40-yard line, where expected value favors aggression.
Coaching philosophies on field goal attempts range from conservative to aggressive, shaped by game context like score margins and time remaining. Conservative approaches prioritize securing points in close games or late scenarios, avoiding the downside of a miss that could tie or lose the lead; for instance, teams trailing by fewer than seven points late in the fourth quarter often opt for field goals over riskier plays. Aggressive coaches, however, push boundaries in high-leverage spots, such as attempting long field goals to extend leads or going for it to seize momentum, particularly when trailing by more than a touchdown. Research indicates that while NFL coaches have grown more aggressive since the early 2010s, they remain more conservative than win-probability models recommend, influenced by factors like opponent strength and weather.
Statistical models, particularly those using expected points added (EPA), guide these decisions by quantifying trade-offs. EPA measures the average change in scoring potential from a given situation; for example, a 40-yard field goal attempt yields an EPA of about 2.5 points if successful, but drops to -1.5 on a miss due to lost field position. Models from sources like ESPN and NFL Next Gen Stats compare this to fourth-down plays, where a successful conversion might add 3-4 EPA by extending drives, but failure subtracts 2-3 EPA from turnover risks. These tools have increased fourth-down attempts league-wide, yet field goals remain the default in 70-80% of eligible situations under 40 yards.
Recent NFL rule changes in 2025, particularly the expanded use of "K-balls"specialized kicking footballs provided in greater quantities (60 per team) for preseason break-inhave boosted long-range confidence. This adjustment, aimed at standardizing equipment, effectively adds 5-7 yards to kick distances by allowing better preparation, reducing the perceived risk of attempts from 50+ yards. As a result, long field goal tries have surged without a corresponding drop in success rates, altering strategic trade-offs in favor of more aggressive kicking.

Outcomes

Successful Field Goals

A successful field goal awards three points to the kicking team when the ball, kicked as a place kick or drop kick, passes above the crossbar and between the goal posts in flight. Following the score, possession changes as the scoring team executes a kickoff from their own 35-yard line, allowing the opposing team to receive the ball and begin their drive from wherever the kickoff return ends.
The game clock stops immediately when the ball crosses the goal line through the uprights, remaining stopped during the brief period for officials to confirm the score and prepare the kickoff. It then restarts on the ensuing kickoff once the ball is legally kicked and either touched in flight by a receiver or hits the ground.
Post-score celebrations by the kicking team are permitted but subject to restrictions under unsportsmanlike conduct rules; excessive, prolonged, or choreographed actionssuch as using the goal posts as props or engaging in tauntingresult in a 15-yard penalty on the kickoff and potential fines up to $14,491 for a first offense. These penalties aim to maintain sportsmanship while allowing brief team acknowledgments.
In overtime periods, field goals carry significant tiebreaker implications, particularly in the sudden-death phase. For regular-season games, after each team has had one possession (unless the first ends in a touchdown), any subsequent scoreincluding a field goalends the game immediately, awarding victory to the scoring team. Postseason overtime follows a similar structure but extends indefinitely until a score occurs.
A rare variant of successful field goals occurs via the fair catch kick, also known as a free kick, which the receiving team may attempt immediately after signaling for and securing a fair catch on a punt within field goal range. If made, it awards three points like a standard field goal, but procedural differences include no snap requiredthe ball is held or placed at the spot of the catchand the defense must line up at least 10 yards away until the kick, with no tee permitted for place kicks (though drop kicks are allowed). The most recent successful fair catch kick was executed by the Los Angeles Chargers on December 19, 2024, from 57 yards, marking the first successful fair catch kick since 1976 and only the fifth in NFL history since 1941. After success, the scoring team proceeds to a standard kickoff, mirroring regular field goal outcomes.

Missed Field Goals

When a field goal attempt fails to pass between the uprights and above the crossbar, no points are awarded to the kicking team. Missed field goals are categorized by their trajectory: wide misses veer to the left or right of the goal posts, while short misses either strike the crossbar and rebound without crossing the plane or fall short of the goal entirely. 
Under NFL rules, a missed field goal is treated similarly to an unsuccessful punt for enforcement purposes, with the ball remaining live until it becomes dead by rule. If the ball is untouched by the defense and goes out of bounds or into the end zone, it results in a touchback, and the defending team gains possession at their 20-yard line. Otherwise, if the ball remains in play untouched, the defending team takes possession at the spot of the kick (the line of scrimmage), unless that spot is inside their 20-yard line, in which case possession begins at the 20. 
The defending team has the opportunity to return a missed field goal if they secure possession of the live ball, potentially advancing it for significant yardage, though such returns are rare and governed by general scrimmage kick rules. Psychologically, a missed field goal can disrupt the kicking team's momentum, fostering frustration and defensive confidence, but empirical studies indicate that the overall impact on subsequent performance is often overstated due to the frequency of such events (occurring in nearly 20% of attempts). Statistically, teams experiencing multiple missed field goals in a season correlate with lower win percentages, as each miss forfeits potential points that could decide close contests. 

Blocked Field Goals

A blocked field goal in American football occurs when a defensive player deflects the ball immediately after the kicker makes contact, preventing it from traveling toward the goal posts. This defensive play requires precise timing, as rushers must navigate the offensive line's protection to reach the kick trajectory. Common methods include interior linemen or edge rushers pushing through gaps in the line or leaping over blockers to swat the ball, often targeting a narrow "block zone" near the kicker's foot.
Key defensive players in these efforts are typically athletic edge rushers, who use speed to loop around the end, and powerful interior linemen, who explode upward from a low stance to disrupt the kick path. The success hinges on the rushers' ability to read the snap and kicker's motion, leaping at the exact moment the ball leaves the foot to avoid illegal contact penalties. Teams often employ specialized block packages, assigning specific gaps to players for coordinated pressure.
Upon a successful block, the ball becomes a live loose ball that either team can recover and advance, similar to a fumbled punt, provided it remains behind the line of scrimmage. If the ball crosses the line untouched before the block, the kicking team cannot advance a recovery. Should the offensive (kicking) team recover the deflected ball in their own end zone and be tackled there, it results in a safety, awarding 2 points to the defense.
In professional leagues like the NFL, blocked field goals occur at a rate of approximately 1-2% of attempts, with higher incidences on longer kicks over 50 yards due to slower ball speed and wider block windows.
Notable examples include the Washington Redskins' block of Miami Dolphins kicker Garo Yepremian's 42-yard attempt in Super Bowl VII; after the block, Yepremian picked up the loose ball and attempted a pass, which slipped from his hands and was intercepted by Washington cornerback Mike Bass, who returned it 49 yards for a touchdown, sparking a 14-point run that shifted momentum in a 14-7 victory. Such plays often prove pivotal, turning potential scores into defensive opportunities and altering game dynamics.

History

Early Development

The field goal emerged in the late 19th century as American football evolved from rugby-influenced rules, where kicking between upright goalposts during play from scrimmage became a key scoring method. The sport's early contests, starting with the first intercollegiate game in 1869 between Rutgers and Princeton, primarily emphasized touchdowns and conversions, but field goals gained prominence by the 1880s as a strategic alternative to pushing for touchdowns in close games. The first recorded field goal in college football occurred on November 30, 1882, during a matchup between Princeton and Yale at the Polo Grounds, when Princeton's J. T. Haxall successfully placekicked a 65-yard effort, setting an early benchmark for distance despite the loss. This kick highlighted the field's open nature and the rounder, rugby-style ball used at the time, which favored drop-kicks over modern placekicks.
Early scoring values for field goals varied under the Intercollegiate Football Association's rules, reflecting debates over balancing kicking with running plays. From 1883 onward, a field goal was worth 5 points, outvaluing a touchdown (2 points) but often compared to a touchdown plus conversion (6 points total), which encouraged teams to prioritize long-range kicks in an era of fields measuring about 110 yards (330 feet) long. This system persisted until 1898, when the touchdown's value rose to 5 points, matching the field goal and beginning to shift emphasis toward advancing the ball; the field goal then dropped to 4 points in 1904 amid efforts to elevate touchdowns, before standardization at 3 points in 1909 to further incentivize advancing the ball. Walter Camp, often called the father of American football, played a pivotal role in these changes; as a Yale player and rules committee member, he advocated for the 1883 point system that formalized field goals' importance, arguing it rewarded precision and strategy over brute force in rugby's chaotic scrums.
Drop-kicking dominated early field goal attempts, as players dropped the prolate spheroid ball to the ground and struck it on the bounce for better control with the era's less aerodynamic equipment. This technique, inherited from rugby, was the norm through the 1890s, with kickers like Haxall occasionally experimenting with placement but facing inconsistent results due to uneven turf and no specialized holders. Straight-on placekicking, where the ball is held upright for a stationary kick, began transitioning in the early 1900s, pioneered by teams like Princeton in 1897, which adopted a holder-assisted method to improve accuracy and distance as the ball's shape became more pointed. By the 1910s, placekicking edged out drop-kicks for reliability, though the latter persisted in professional play into the 1940s.
The field goal's adoption in professional football mirrored college trends, entering the newly formed American Professional Football Association (predecessor to the NFL) in 1920. Field goals were part of professional play from the start; the first in league history was a 25-yard drop kick by Frank Ursella for the Rock Island Independents on September 26, 1920, against the St. Paul Ideals. Jim Thorpe, playing for the Canton Bulldogs, also used drop-kicks effectively that season, underscoring kicking's viability in the pro ranks despite the era's emphasis on running and tackling. Thorpe, a multisport legend and the league's first president, exemplified the versatile athlete-kicker.

Rule Changes and Evolution

The evolution of field goal rules in American football has been shaped by efforts to balance offensive strategy, player safety, and game flow, with several key modifications to goalpost placement and dimensions over the decades. In the late 1920s, following college football's lead, the NFL initially positioned goalposts at the back of the end zone (the end line) starting around 1927, a shift from their prior location on the goal line; this change aimed to reduce collisions during goal-line stands but inadvertently extended field goal distances by 10 yards, making attempts more challenging. However, when the NFL established its own rulebook in 1933, it relocated the goalposts forward to the goal line, which spurred a surge in field goal attempts as the effective kicking distance shortened, contributing to a dramatic increase from 6 field goals league-wide in 1932 to 61 in 1934.
This positioning remained until 1974, when the NFL again moved the goalposts to the end line to discourage excessive reliance on field goals and promote touchdown scoring, a response to the high volume of kicking plays in prior seasons; simultaneously, the uprights were extended from 20 feet above the crossbar (set in 1966) to 30 feet for better visibility from the sidelines and stands. The 1974 adjustment reduced field goal success rates initially, with kickers making about 15% fewer points from kicks that year compared to 1973, though accuracy rebounded as techniques advanced. Later safety enhancements included adding padding to the uprights and stanchions in the 1980s, following incidents of player injuries during celebrations or collisions, to mitigate risks without altering gameplay dimensions.
Hash mark adjustments in the 1970s further influenced field goal angles and setup. Prior to 1972, NFL hash marks were positioned approximately 20 yards from the sidelines, creating uneven kicking angles that favored attempts from one side of the field; the league moved them to about 23.5 yards from the sidelines (18 feet 6 inches apart, aligning with goalpost width) to facilitate balanced offensive plays and more symmetrical field goal approaches, which improved overall kicking consistency.
The introduction of soccer-style kicking marked a pivotal non-rule evolution in 1964, when Pete Gogolak joined the AFL's Buffalo Bills as the first professional to employ the instep technique borrowed from soccer, allowing for greater distance and accuracy compared to the traditional toe-poke method; his success, including a league-leading 102 points that season, prompted widespread adoption and extended practical field goal ranges from around 40 yards to over 50 yards reliably.
More recently, the 2025 season introduced K-ball provisions under new equipment rules, allocating 60 break-in balls per team at the season's start to better condition kicking balls for consistency; this has led to noticeably longer field goal attempts, with kickers making 73.7% of 50-plus yard tries through the first four weeksup from prior yearsand reports of 5-7 additional yards on average due to optimized ball flight. Complementing this, the permanent adoption of the dynamic kickoff formatfirst trialed in 2024has indirectly encouraged aggressive field position strategies, potentially increasing long-range field goal opportunities by altering return dynamics. In 2014, the uprights were further extended to 35 feet to curb end-zone celebrations involving the structure, prioritizing safety while maintaining visibility.

Records

Longest Successful Field Goals

In the National Football League (NFL), the record for the longest successful field goal stands at 68 yards, achieved by Jacksonville Jaguars kicker Cam Little on November 2, 2025, during a game against the Las Vegas Raiders. This surpassed the previous mark of 66 yards set by Baltimore Ravens kicker Justin Tucker in 2021 against the Detroit Lions. Little's kick, made as time expired in the second quarter, cleared the crossbar with significant distance and marked a notable advancement in professional kicking capabilities.
In the Canadian Football League (CFL), the record is 63 yards, first set by Saskatchewan Roughriders kicker Paul McCallum on October 27, 2001, against the Edmonton Eskimos at Taylor Field. This distance was tied by Winnipeg Blue Bombers kicker Sergio Castillo on August 9, 2025, against the Calgary Stampeders at McMahon Stadium, providing a late lead in a closely contested game. The wider field dimensions in the CFL, measuring 110 yards long and 65 yards wide, contribute to the challenges and occasional wind-aided successes in long attempts compared to the NFL.
At the collegiate level in the National Collegiate Athletic Association (NCAA), the longest verified field goal is 69 yards, kicked by Ove Johansson for Abilene Christian University against East Texas State on October 16, 1976. Johansson, a 28-year-old Swedish soccer player transitioning to American football, benefited from ideal conditions in this NAIA game, which predates stricter measurement standards but remains recognized. A notable near-record is 67 yards by Steve Little for the University of Arkansas against the University of Texas in 1977, tying for the second-longest in major college history and highlighting the era's emphasis on powerful kicking amid evolving equipment. Recent NCAA seasons have seen increased attempts beyond 60 yards, with the 2025 season's longest at 63 yards by Iowa State's Kyle Konrardy, reflecting improved training and ball specifications.
In high school football, governed by the National Federation of State High School Associations (NFHS), the longest recorded field goal is 68 yards by Dirk Borgognone of Reno High School (Nevada) against Sparks High School on September 27, 1985. This kick, made under high school rules with shorter uprights and variable field conditions, ended Borgognone's season due to injury but established a benchmark for amateur levels. Other verified long kicks include 67 yards by Russ Cowsert of W.T. White High School (Texas) in 1978, underscoring the physical demands on younger athletes.
In U Sports, Canada's governing body for university athletics, the longest field goal is 59 yards by Calgary Dinos kicker Niko DiFonte on November 11, 2017, securing a playoff victory over the UBC Thunderbirds. This record, made on a 110-yard field similar to the CFL, was a walk-off kick that ended UBC's season. A recent highlight is 57 yards by Evan Astolfo of the Waterloo Warriors on August 30, 2025, tying the game against the Toronto Varsity Blues and setting a new Ontario University Athletics (OUA) mark as the second-longest in U Sports history. An unofficial 78-yard attempt has been mentioned in historical accounts but lacks verification from official U Sports records.
In professional spring leagues like the United Football League (UFL), the longest successful field goal is 64 yards by Michigan Panthers kicker Jake Bates on March 30, 2024, against the St. Louis Battlehawks, clinching a game-winning victory. Bates repeated a similar 64-yard make later in the season, drawing NFL interest and emphasizing the league's role in showcasing emerging talent on standard 100-yard fields.
Across independent and women's professional leagues, such as the former Independent Women's Football League (IWFL), successful field goals tend to be shorter due to variations in field dimensions, equipment, and competitive levels, with reported highs around 55 yards influenced by smaller rosters and training resources. These leagues often play on 100-yard fields but face unique logistical challenges, limiting verified long-distance records compared to major circuits.
General trends show a rise in long field goals, particularly in the NFL, driven by the 2025 K-ball rule changes, which provide teams with 60 dedicated kicking balls per season for better break-in and consistency, reportedly adding 5-7 yards to average kick distance. In 2024, the NFL recorded 26 makes from 55+ yards through the first seven weeksa prior highthat number has increased in 2025, with kickers citing enhanced ball quality and coaching as key factors in pushing boundaries beyond 60 yards more frequently.

Longest Missed Field Goal Returns

In American and Canadian football, a missed or blocked field goal attempt becomes a live ball that the receiving team can return for significant yardage, potentially scoring a touchdown if the returner advances it into the end zone. These plays are rare due to the defensive coverage typically set up to prevent returns, but they can occur when the ball bounces unpredictably or the returner finds a seam in the coverage. The mechanics require the ball to remain live after the miss or block, allowing the returner to field it anywhere on the field and run it back, often aided by blockers creating lanes against pursuing defenders.
In the National Football League (NFL), the record for the longest missed field goal return is 109 yards for a touchdown, a mark first set by San Diego Chargers cornerback Antonio Cromartie on November 4, 2007, against the Minnesota Vikings, where he fielded a missed 57-yard attempt by Ryan Longwell in the end zone and returned it untouched. This record was tied by Jacksonville Jaguars return specialist Jamal Agnew on September 26, 2021, against the Arizona Cardinals, as he fielded a missed 68-yard field goal attempt by Matt Prater in the end zone and sprinted 109 yards for a score, equaling the longest play from scrimmage in NFL history. Another notable near-record return occurred on November 13, 2005, when Chicago Bears cornerback Nathan Vasher took a missed 52-yard field goal by San Francisco's Joe Nedney 108 yards for a touchdown, highlighting the defensive challenges in containing a speedy returner with open-field blocking.
The Canadian Football League (CFL) features longer fields at 110 yards, enabling even more extended returns compared to the NFL's 100-yard dimensions. The league record stands at 131 yards for a touchdown, achieved on August 22, 1958, by Toronto Argonauts players Boyd Carter and Dave Mann against the Montreal Alouettes; Carter advanced the missed field goal 15 yards before lateraling to Mann, who ran the remaining 116 yards. This play remains the longest in CFL history, underscoring the advantages of the wider field and three-down format that can lead to more open returns. More recent examples include Saskatchewan Roughriders' Tristan Jackson returning a missed field goal 129 yards for a touchdown on July 15, 2012, against the BC Lions, demonstrating ongoing potential for game-altering plays despite improved coverage techniques. In 2025, notable examples included a 128-yard return for a touchdown by Winnipeg Blue Bombers' Trey Vaval against the Ottawa REDBLACKS in September, a 120-yard return by Saskatchewan Roughriders' Derek Slywka on June 29, and a 117-yard return by Ottawa REDBLACKS' Kalil Pimpleton against the Winnipeg Blue Bombers on August 14.
In NCAA Division I college football, returns exceeding 100 yards have occurred, with Auburn Tigers cornerback Chris Davis setting a memorable mark by returning a missed 57-yard field goal attempt by Alabama's Adam Griffith 109 yards for a touchdown on the final play of the 2013 Iron Bowl, securing a 34-31 victory in a play known as the "Kick Six." Such returns are infrequent at the college level due to varying talent and the emphasis on special teams coverage, but they often result in touchdowns when the returner exploits disorganized pursuit after a miss. In U Sports, Canada's university football league, which uses 110-yard fields similar to the CFL, long returns of over 100 yards are feasible and have been recorded in games, though specific records mirror the professional level's potential for extended plays owing to the field's dimensions.
