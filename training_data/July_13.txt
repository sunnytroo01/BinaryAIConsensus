 July 13 is the 194th day of the year (195th in leap years) in the Gregorian calendar. There are 171 days remaining until the end of the year.

Events

Pre-1600

Pope John III (d. July 13, 574), born Catelinus, served as pope from 561 until his death in Rome; his pontificate faced Lombard invasions that destroyed records of his tenure, limiting knowledge of specific actions beyond general papal duties during a period of instability in Italy. He was succeeded by Benedict I amid ongoing threats from the Lombards.

Pope Leo VII (d. July 13, 939), a Roman Benedictine monk elevated to the papacy in 936, focused on monastic reform by inviting Abbot Odo of Cluny to mediate Roman factional disputes and promote Cluniac observances; his death marked the end of a brief reform-oriented reign under the influence of Alberic II of Spoleto, who controlled papal elections.

Henry II (d. July 13, 1024), Holy Roman Emperor from 1014 and King of Germany from 1002, died at age 51 in his palace at Grona near Gttingen from a prolonged urinary ailment after returning from a pilgrimage to Bamberg; childless, his death ended the Ottonian dynasty, leading to the election of Conrad II from the Salian family and a shift in imperial power dynamics.

Rashi (Rabbi Shlomo Yitzhaki, d. July 13, 1105), medieval French Jewish scholar born c. 1040 in Troyes, died at age 65; renowned for his foundational commentaries on the Talmud and Torah that clarified texts for broader study, his passing occurred amid rising tensions before the First Crusade's pogroms, but his works endured as essential to Jewish learning without immediate institutional disruption.

Jacobus de Voragine (d. July 13 or 14, 1298), Italian chronicler and Archbishop of Genoa from 1292, authored the influential Legenda Aurea (Golden Legend), a compilation of saints' lives shaping medieval hagiography; his death in Genoa followed ecclesiastical duties, with his writings continuing to influence religious literature and art into the Renaissance.

16011900

Albert VII, Archduke of Austria (15591621), a Habsburg cardinal who served as viceroy of Portugal and co-sovereign of the Spanish Netherlands with his wife Isabella Clara Eugenia from 1598, died on July 13, 1621, in Brussels after a brief illness. His governance focused on defending the Netherlands against Dutch independence efforts during the Eighty Years' War, maintaining Spanish influence through military and diplomatic means.

Edward Braddock (16951755), a British Army general dispatched to North America during the French and Indian War, succumbed to wounds on July 13, 1755, at Great Meadows, Pennsylvania, following an ambush by French and Native American forces at the Battle of the Monongahela. Braddock's expedition aimed to capture Fort Duquesne but failed due to tactical errors, including underestimating guerrilla warfare, resulting in heavy British casualties and his own mortal injuries from multiple gunshot wounds. His defeat highlighted European linear tactics' vulnerabilities against irregular combatants, influencing later colonial strategies.

James Bradley (16931762), English astronomer and third Astronomer Royal from 1742, died on July 13, 1762, in Chalford, Gloucestershire. Bradley discovered the aberration of light in 1728, providing empirical evidence for Earth's orbital motion around the Sun, and later identified stellar nutation in 1748, refining astronomical measurements of star positions. His observations at the Royal Greenwich Observatory advanced celestial mechanics, correcting Ptolemaic and early telescopic errors through precise instrumentation and data analysis.

Jean-Paul Marat (17431793), a French revolutionary leader, physician, and journalist who headed the radical Montagnard faction, was stabbed to death on July 13, 1793, in his Paris bathtub by Charlotte Corday, a Girondin sympathizer acting to halt revolutionary excesses. Through his newspaper L'Ami du peuple, Marat incited violence against moderates, monarchists, and Girondins, demanding purges and executions that contributed to thousands of deaths during the Reign of Terror's early phases. Corday justified her act as a preemptive strike against Marat's role in radicalizing the Revolution, citing his lists of proscription that targeted political opponents. Marat's assassination, rather than curbing extremism, martyred him in Jacobin eyes, accelerating the Terror's escalation under Robespierre. 

John C. Frmont (18131890), U.S. Army officer, explorer, and politician dubbed "The Pathfinder" for leading five expeditions mapping Western territories from 1841 to 1853, died on July 13, 1890, in New York City from peritonitis. Frmont's surveys documented geography, resources, and routes like the Oregon Trail, aiding westward expansion and California's statehood after his role in the Bear Flag Revolt during the Mexican-American War. He commanded Union forces early in the Civil War, issuing controversial emancipation orders in Missouri, and ran as the Republican Party's first presidential candidate in 1856, opposing slavery's extension. Later territorial governorships in Arizona underscored his influence on American frontier policy.

1901present

1951 Arnold Schoenberg, Austrian-born composer and music theorist who pioneered atonal composition and the twelve-tone technique, thereby revolutionizing modern music by abandoning traditional key centers and tonal harmony, died in Los Angeles at age 76 from heart failure. His innovations, including works like Pierrot Lunaire and the String Quartet No. 2, influenced generations despite initial resistance from audiences accustomed to romantic-era conventions. Schoenberg, who fled Nazi persecution in 1933, held a lifelong superstition about the number 13, having been born on September 13 and dying on July 13.

1954 Frida Kahlo, Mexican artist renowned for self-portraits incorporating surrealist elements drawn from her chronic pain and cultural heritage, died at her home in Coyoacn at age 47; the official cause was pulmonary embolism following pneumonia, though no autopsy was performed and suspicions of intentional overdose on painkillers persist due to her documented heavy medication use for lifelong disabilities from polio and a 1925 bus accident. Kahlo's marriage to muralist Diego Rivera involved mutual infidelities, including her affair with exiled communist Leon Trotsky, reflecting her adherence to Trotskyite ideology amid Mexico's post-revolutionary politics. While celebrated for raw depictions of suffering, critics have noted similarities in her folkloric style to lesser-known Mexican painters, fueling unproven plagiarism claims that challenge narratives of unassisted originality.

2013 Cory Monteith, Canadian actor best known for portraying Finn Hudson on the television series Glee, died in a Vancouver hotel room at age 31 from mixed toxicity of intravenously administered heroin and ingested alcohol, ruled accidental by coroner's inquest. Monteith had entered rehabilitation for substance abuse in 2013, having struggled with addiction since adolescence involving marijuana, alcohol, and harder drugs.

2024 Shannen Doherty, American actress recognized for roles in Beverly Hills, 90210 and Charmed, died at age 53 in Malibu after breast cancer metastasized to her brain and bones following diagnosis in 2015, remission, and recurrence in 2020. Doherty underwent chemotherapy, radiation, and mastectomy but documented her battle publicly, emphasizing resilience amid invasive treatments.

2024 Richard Simmons, American fitness instructor and television personality who popularized aerobics through shows like Sweatin' to the Oldies, died at age 76 in Los Angeles; the cause was determined natural, with no foul play, following decades of promoting weight loss via enthusiastic, accessible exercise videos. Simmons, who weighed over 300 pounds in youth, built a career reducing obesity stigma but withdrew publicly after 2014 due to health issues including knee replacements.

2024 Attempted assassination of Donald Trump at a rally in Butler, Pennsylvania, in which 20-year-old Thomas Matthew Crooks fired shots from a nearby rooftop, grazing Trump's ear; the shooter was killed by U.S. Secret Service counter-snipers, one spectator was fatally shot, and two others were injured.

Holidays and observances

Religious observances

In the Roman Catholic liturgical calendar, July 13 commemorates Saint Henry II (9731024), Holy Roman Emperor and King of Germany, who was canonized by Pope Eugene III in 1146 for his devout life, promotion of ecclesiastical reforms, and defense of the Church against secular encroachments. Henry, married to Saint Cunigunde, abdicated the throne in 1024 to pursue monastic life, embodying the ideal of Christian kingship rooted in Carolingian traditions of imperial piety as described in contemporary annals like those of Thietmar of Merseburg. His hagiography emphasizes miracles attributed post-mortem, including healings at his tomb in Bamberg Cathedral, verified through episcopal inquiries during the canonization process, which followed 12th-century papal standards requiring eyewitness testimonies and papal approval.
The day also marks the feast of other figures in Catholic tradition, such as Saint Clelia Barbieri (18561870), founder of the Little Sisters of the Holy Family and Italy's youngest canonized foundress at age 23, whose canonization by Pope Pius XII in 1951 drew on documented visions and charitable works amid 19th-century cholera epidemics. Blessed Carlos Manuel Rodrguez Santiago (19181963), a Puerto Rican lay catechist beatified by Pope John Paul II in 1997, is likewise observed, with his cause advanced by the Archdiocese of San Juan based on writings promoting liturgical renewal and evidence of mystical experiences influencing his evangelization efforts.
In the Bah Faith, July 13 corresponds to the Feast of Kalimt (Words), the communal gathering inaugurating the fifth month of the Bah calendar, as fixed by Bahu'llh in his Kitb-i-Aqdas for periodic devotion and consultation without work suspension, unlike the nine principal holy days. This observance, held at sunset's onset of the Bah day, centers on recitation from the writings of Bahu'llh and Abdu'l-Bahemphasizing the prophetic chain from earlier dispensationsfollowed by administrative discussions among local assemblies, as outlined in Shoghi Effendi's directives for sustaining community unity through scriptural fidelity.

National and cultural holidays

Montenegro observes Statehood Day on July 13, commemorating the Congress of Berlin's recognition of the Principality of Montenegro as an independent state on that date in 1878, following its expansion through wars against the Ottoman Empire. This holiday underscores Montenegro's historical quest for sovereignty amid Balkan geopolitics, where great-power arbitration at Berlin affirmed its borders after Prince Nikola I's military gains, including the annexation of territories like Niki and Podgorica. In contemporary context, celebrations reinforce national identity and commitments to Western integration, including NATO membership since 2017 and ongoing EU accession efforts, which have advanced through chapters opened on judiciary and fundamental rights despite domestic political divisions.
Mongolia's Naadam Festival, a state-recognized national holiday spanning July 11 to 13, centers on the "three manly games" of wrestling, archery, and long-distance horse racing, drawing from nomadic pastoralist traditions that emphasize physical prowess and communal gatherings. Originating in military training exercises traceable to the era of Genghis Khan in the 13th century, the festival evolved into a secular celebration of ethnic unity across Mongolia's 24 aimags (provinces), with the national event in Ulaanbaatar featuring thousands of child jockeys in races up to 30 kilometers and wrestlers competing in open-air bouts without weight classes. UNESCO-listed since 2010 for its intangible cultural heritage value, Naadam perpetuates causal links to steppe warfare and herding economies, fostering social cohesion in a post-communist society where participation rates exceed 1,000 competitors per discipline annually.
Pakistan designates July 13 as Kashmir Martyrs' Day (Youm-e-Shuhada-e-Kashmir), a national observance commemorating the deaths of 21 or 22 Muslim protesters killed by Dogra Kingdom forces outside Srinagar Central Jail in 1931 during the trial of activist Abdul Qadeer for sedition related to anti-maharaja speeches. The incident stemmed from longstanding grievances under Maharaja Hari Singh's Hindu Dogra rule over a Muslim-majority population, including land tenure inequalities and exclusion from civil service, sparking mass demonstrations that escalated when crowds pelted stones amid calls for independence from princely autocracy. Accounts of the day's casualties vary slightly in number but consistently attribute them to gunfire during prayer-time unrest; however, narratives differ on provocation, with Pakistani sources emphasizing martyrdom against oppression while some historical analyses highlight Qadeer's role in inciting communal sedition and question the selective omission of broader 1931-1932 violence that included reprisals affecting non-Muslims, contributing to entrenched sectarian divides culminating in the 1947 partition riots. This observance, actively promoted in Pakistan and Pakistan-administered Kashmir, has fueled irredentist sentiments, with post-1947 commemorations linked to militant groups invoking the event for recruitment amid the ongoing territorial dispute.

Unofficial and awareness days

National Barbershop Music Appreciation Day recognizes the tradition of barbershop harmony, an a cappella vocal style originating in the United States during the late 19th century, characterized by four-part close harmony emphasizing melody and barbershop tags. This observance, promoted annually on July 13 by organizations like the Barbershop Harmony Society and Sweet Adelines International, highlights community singing quartets and choruses that foster social bonds through unaccompanied performances of popular songs arranged in homorhythmic style. Participation includes local chapter events and performances, with roots in verifiable historical practices traceable to African American origins in the 1870s and widespread adoption by white ensembles in the early 20th century, though modern appreciation days largely serve promotional purposes for preserving the genre amid declining membership.
Embrace Your Geekness Day, observed on July 13, encourages individuals to celebrate niche interests in science fiction, technology, gaming, and related subcultures, originating from a 2006 creation by authors Thomas and Ruth Roy via their website wellcat.com as part of a series of whimsical holidays. The day promotes self-acceptance of unconventional hobbies without formal institutional backing, with activities like costume events or fan gatherings drawing from measurable growth in geek culture conventions, such as Comic-Con attendance exceeding 130,000 annually by the 2010s, though its observance remains informal and lacks empirical data on widespread participation beyond online promotion. Critics note such days often function as lighthearted marketing rather than substantive awareness, with limited evidence of causal impact on cultural stigma reduction.
