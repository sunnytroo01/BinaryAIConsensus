Value of information
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The value of information (VOI) is a core concept in decision theory that quantifies the expected benefit gained from acquiring additional information to improve decision-making under uncertainty, representing the difference between the expected value of an optimal decision with and without that information. It provides a framework for determining how much one should invest in information gathering, such as through research, testing, or data collection, to reduce risks and enhance outcomes in uncertain environments.
The concept of VOI emerged in the mid-20th century as part of Bayesian decision theory, pioneered by Howard Raiffa and Robert Schlaifer in their seminal 1961 work Applied Statistical Decision Theory, which formalized methods for evaluating information's role in statistical inference and choice under uncertainty. Building on earlier probabilistic foundations from thinkers like Frank Ramsey and Bruno de Finetti, VOI shifted focus from mere probability assessments to actionable economic valuations of knowledge, influencing fields beyond statistics into economics and operations research.
Central to VOI analysis are metrics like the expected value of perfect information (EVPI), which calculates the maximum potential payoff from obtaining complete certainty about uncertain parameters, often serving as an upper bound for information investments; and the expected value of sample information (EVSI), which estimates the worth of imperfect, partial data from specific sources like experiments or surveys. These measures are computed using decision trees or probabilistic models, comparing pre- and post-information expected utilities to isolate the informational gain.
VOI has broad applications across disciplines, including healthcare for prioritizing clinical trials by estimating research benefits against costs, civil engineering for assessing inspection strategies in infrastructure management, and public policy for evaluating environmental monitoring programs. In business and economics, it guides resource allocation for market research or technology adoption, ensuring decisions align with the marginal returns of information in dynamic, uncertain settings.

Definitions and Terminology

Core Definition

The value of information (VOI) in decision theory refers to the expected improvement in decision outcomes attributable to acquiring additional information, typically measured as the difference between the expected utility of the optimal decision with the new information and the expected utility of the best decision without it. This concept quantifies the potential benefit of reducing uncertainty prior to committing to a course of action, serving as a rational basis for deciding whether to invest resources in data collection or analysis.
At its core, VOI rests on expected utility theory, which posits that rational decision-makers choose actions to maximize their expected utilitya measure that combines the utilities (or payoffs) of possible outcomes with their probabilities under uncertainty. Without additional information, a decision-maker selects the action with the highest prior expected utility based on current beliefs; with new information, updated beliefs (via Bayesian revision) may lead to a different, superior action, thereby increasing the overall expected utility. Qualitatively, VOI captures how much better off the decision-maker is after obtaining the data, often manifesting as an expected increase in payoff, a reduction in risk exposure, or a decrease in opportunity costs from suboptimal choices.
A prominent special case is the expected value of perfect information (EVPI), which represents the VOI when the additional information completely resolves all uncertainty about relevant parameters. This upper bound on VOI helps benchmark the maximum worth of any information-gathering effort in a given decision context.

Related Terms

Several terms are closely associated with the value of information (VOI), serving as synonyms, specific variants, or related concepts in decision analysis and statistics. These include "value of data," which is often used interchangeably in broader information economics to denote the utility derived from data assets, though it lacks the precise decision-theoretic framing of VOI. A specific variant is the expected value of sample information (EVSI), which quantifies the anticipated benefit from imperfect, sample-based data in Bayesian decision making.
A key distinction arises with "information gain," which in machine learning contextsparticularly decision tree algorithmsmeasures the reduction in entropy or uncertainty from splitting data on a feature, based on mutual information or Kullback-Leibler divergence. This contrasts with VOI in decision theory, where the focus is on the expected improvement in utility or payoff from acquiring information, rather than purely informational entropy reduction. Similarly, "marginal value" refers to the incremental VOI gained from additional units of partial or sequential data, highlighting how the benefit diminishes as more information accumulates.
The following glossary outlines 4-5 key related terms, with brief notes on their origins in Bayesian statistics where applicable:

Expected Value of Perfect Information (EVPI): The maximum price a decision maker would pay to eliminate all uncertainty about a parameter, computed as the difference between the expected value with perfect foresight and without it; originates from Bayesian decision theory as formalized in Raiffa and Schlaifer's seminal work on statistical decision making.

Expected Value of Sample Information (EVSI): The expected net gain from acquiring a specific sample of imperfect information, averaging the conditional EVPI over possible sample outcomes; also rooted in the Bayesian framework of Raiffa and Schlaifer (1961), emphasizing preposterior analysis.

Information Gain: A metric in statistical learning for selecting attributes that maximally reduce predictive uncertainty, derived from information theory concepts like entropy; distinct from VOI, it emerged in the context of ID3 algorithm development in the 1980s rather than direct Bayesian utility maximization.

Marginal Value of Information: The derivative or incremental benefit of one more piece of information, often non-concave in sequential settings; builds on Bayesian VOI foundations to address learning paradoxes and optimal sampling.

Value of Perfect Partial Information (EVPPI): A variant focusing on the value of resolving uncertainty in a subset of parameters, useful for prioritizing research; extends EVPI within Bayesian health economics and decision models.

Theoretical Foundations

Historical Development

The concept of the value of information traces its early roots to 18th-century probability theory, particularly the work of Thomas Bayes, who developed a theorem for updating beliefs based on new evidence. Bayes' theorem, formulated in the 1740s and published posthumously in 1763, provided the mathematical basis for quantifying how additional information alters probabilistic assessments, laying groundwork for later evaluations of informational worth in uncertain environments.
This foundation evolved in the early 20th century through contributions to subjective probability and decision-making under uncertainty. Frank Ramsey's 1926 work on truth and probability introduced ideas of personal probability and betting coherence, while Bruno de Finetti's 1937 theorem on the equivalence of personal probability assessments to expected utilities further solidified Bayesian approaches to rational belief updating and choice.
The formal introduction of value of information occurred in the mid-20th century within Bayesian decision analysis. In the 1950s and 1960s, decision theorists Howard Raiffa and Robert Schlaifer developed systematic frameworks for assessing the expected utility gains from acquiring information, as detailed in their seminal 1961 text Applied Statistical Decision Theory. This work integrated subjective probabilities and utilities to compute pre-posterior analyses, marking the transition from abstract probability to practical decision tools.
Key milestones in the 1960s included the application of value of information concepts in operations research, where Bayesian methods informed resource allocation and risk assessment in military and industrial contexts post-World War II. Practical assessments of information value were influenced by World War II efforts in code-breaking and early computing, where operations research teams, such as those at Bletchley Park, used probabilistic techniques akin to Bayesian updating to prioritize intelligence decryptions and strategic decisions. These wartime applications, including the use of early machines like the Bombe for Enigma code analysis, demonstrated the tangible benefits of valuing information in high-stakes scenarios and spurred post-war formalizations in decision theory.
By the 1980s, the field expanded into economics through Roger Myerson's contributions to mechanism design, particularly his 1981 analysis of optimal auctions, which incorporated informational rents and virtual valuations to evaluate asymmetric information's impact on outcomes.

Key Characteristics

The value of information (VOI) possesses several intrinsic properties that underpin its utility in decision analysis. Primarily, VOI is always non-negative, as additional information cannot reduce the expected utility of a decision and may only improve or leave it unchanged. This ensures that rational agents would never pay a positive cost for information that harms their outcomes. Furthermore, VOI follows the law of diminishing returns, where the marginal benefit of successive units of information decreases as uncertainty is progressively resolved. For instance, the first piece of relevant data may substantially refine beliefs, but subsequent pieces yield progressively smaller improvements in expected utility. VOI is also inherently context-dependent, scaling with the stakes or consequences of the underlying decision; higher potential losses or gains amplify the worth of clarifying information.
Key factors influencing VOI include its sensitivity to prior beliefs and levels of uncertainty. When prior probabilities are diffuse or highly uncertain, VOI tends to be elevated because information has greater potential to shift decision-relevant posteriors. Conversely, strong or precise priors diminish VOI by limiting the scope for meaningful updates. Uncertainty levels similarly modulate VOI: greater variability in outcomes or parameters increases the expected benefit of information that reduces it. Decision reversibility further shapes VOI; in scenarios where choices are irreversible, the value of pre-decision information rises to avoid costly commitments under uncertainty.
Qualitatively, VOI equals zero when the information is irrelevant to the decision problem or fully anticipated based on existing knowledge, rendering it redundant. In multi-information settings, VOI can exhibit supermodularity, where the combined value of multiple signals exceeds the sum of their individual values due to synergistic reductions in uncertainty. These traits were evident in early applications within statistical decision theory, such as evaluating sampling strategies in uncertain environments.

Advanced Approaches

Advanced approaches to computing the value of information (VOI) extend beyond analytical solutions by addressing complex scenarios involving sample data, uncertainty in priors, and interdependencies among information sources. These methods are essential for practical applications where exact calculations are infeasible due to model complexity.
The expected value of sample information (EVSI) quantifies the benefit of acquiring data from a proposed experiment or study, often computed using Monte Carlo simulation to approximate the nested expectations involved. In Monte Carlo methods, samples are drawn from the prior predictive distribution to simulate possible data outcomes, and for each outcome, the posterior distribution is updated to evaluate the improved decision value, averaging these to estimate EVSI. This approach reduces computational burden compared to full nested integration, enabling EVSI estimation in seconds rather than days for complex models. Numerical integration techniques, such as quadrature over discretized parameter spaces, provide an alternative for lower-dimensional problems by directly evaluating the multiple integrals defining EVSI, yielding accurate results when simulation variance is a concern.
Bayesian updating forms the core of VOI computations in sequential decision settings, where the value of future information is assessed through posterior distributions. The posterior VOI, which captures the expected improvement after observing data, is given by
PosteriorVOI=VOI(posterior)f(dataprior)ddata,
\text{Posterior VOI} = \int \text{VOI}(\text{posterior}) \, f(\text{data} \mid \text{prior}) \, d\text{data},
PosteriorVOI=VOI(posterior)f(dataprior)ddata,
where VOI(posterior)\text{VOI}(\text{posterior})VOI(posterior) represents the decision value enhancement under the updated beliefs, and f(dataprior)f(\text{data} \mid \text{prior})f(dataprior) is the likelihood of the data given the prior. This integral is typically approximated via sampling methods to handle the intractability of exact evaluation in high-dimensional parameter spaces.
When information sources are correlated, standard VOI calculations must account for redundancies and synergies, often requiring joint modeling of dependencies to avoid overestimation. Approximation methods, such as those in partially observable Markov decision processes (POMDPs), address this by evaluating the marginal contribution of each source while considering covariances. Myopic VOI approximations assume one-step lookahead, selecting information actions that maximize immediate expected utility without considering long-term impacts, which simplifies computation but may undervalue multi-stage benefits. In contrast, lookahead methods extend to deeper horizons in POMDPs, recursively computing the value of potential future observations to better capture non-myopic gains from correlated data.
Computational challenges in advanced VOI estimation arise primarily from the curse of dimensionality, where the exponential growth in state or parameter space volume renders exhaustive integration or simulation prohibitive. Sampling-based techniques, including importance sampling and multilevel Monte Carlo, mitigate this by focusing computations on high-value regions of the space, achieving scalable approximations with controlled error in high-dimensional settings.

Applications

Decision Theory Contexts

In normative decision theory, the value of information (VOI) represents the expected improvement in decision outcomes from acquiring additional data under uncertainty, guiding rational agents to optimize information acquisition by comparing its expected benefits against costs. This framework, rooted in Bayesian decision analysis, quantifies VOI as the difference between the expected utility of decisions with and without the information, assuming risk-neutral expected utility maximization. Seminal work by Raiffa and Schlaifer formalized these concepts, emphasizing pre-posterior analysis to evaluate how information resolves uncertainty prior to action.
VOI plays a central role in risk analysis by informing whether to pursue tests or studies that reduce uncertainty in high-stakes decisions. In medical diagnosis, for instance, VOI assesses the worth of diagnostic tests, such as screening for breast cancer or estimating HIV prevalence, where it helps prioritize research by calculating the expected net benefit of sampling (ENBS) to maximize health outcomes within resource constraints. Similarly, in business contexts like market research, VOI evaluates surveys to refine forecasts, ensuring investments align with potential shifts in strategy. These applications leverage VOI to balance the costs of information gathering against reductions in decision risk, often using expected value of perfect information (EVPI) as a benchmark.
A prominent case illustrating VOI in risk analysis is oil wildcatting, where companies decide whether to drill exploratory wells amid geological uncertainty. In the classic wildcatter problem, a firm faces a 25% prior probability of oil discovery, with net payoffs of $400 million if successful and losses of $40 million if unsuccessful; seismic surveys provide imperfect information, updating probabilities to 75% (if positive) or 4% (if negative), yielding a VOI of $17 million if the survey costs less than this threshold. Here, VOI justifies seismic surveys by demonstrating how they alter drilling decisions, with the expected value of perfect information reaching $30 million, far exceeding typical survey expenses and reducing dry-hole risks. This example underscores VOI's practical utility in resource allocation for exploration.
While VOI thrives in expected utility models, it faces limitations in non-expected utility frameworks like prospect theory, which incorporates loss aversion and probability weighting, potentially distorting VOI calculations by overweighting low-probability extreme outcomes. Adjustments for prospect theory require reframing utilities relative to a reference point, as standard VOI may undervalue information in loss domains where risk-seeking behavior prevails, necessitating behavioral extensions to normative models. Such adaptations highlight the need for context-specific VOI assessments beyond classical assumptions. Computation methods, like Monte Carlo simulations for EVSI, can quantify these applications but must account for theoretical deviations.

Information Economics Examples

In the context of adverse selection, the value of information (VOI) arises from the potential to mitigate information asymmetries that distort market outcomes, as exemplified by Akerlof's model of the "market for lemons." In this framework, sellers possess private knowledge about product qualitysuch as used cars ranging from low-quality "lemons" to high-quality vehicleswhile buyers face uncertainty and offer prices based on the average expected quality. This leads to adverse selection, where high-quality sellers withdraw, flooding the market with lemons and potentially causing trade to collapse entirely. The VOI manifests in mechanisms like warranties or certifications that signal quality, enabling high-quality sellers to participate and restoring market efficiency by increasing trade volume. For instance, partial information revelation by sellers can paradoxically reduce overall trade if it exacerbates the buyer's pessimism about unobserved quality, underscoring the nuanced economic value of targeted signals over complete disclosure.
In auctions and mechanism design, VOI plays a critical role in common-value settings, where bidders' private signals inform a shared asset value, as analyzed in the Milgrom-Weber theorem. This theorem establishes the linkage principle, which posits that auction formats revealing more informationsuch as through open bidding in English auctionslink the winner's payment more closely to their private signal, reducing the winner's curse (overbidding due to overly optimistic estimates) and increasing seller revenue. Bids themselves serve as informative signals, with their VOI lying in updating other bidders' beliefs about the common value; for example, in oil lease auctions, observing rivals' bids provides valuable inferences that refine estimates and enhance bidding strategies. This informational linkage promotes efficiency by encouraging truthful revelation and higher participation, contrasting with sealed-bid formats where less information disclosure can suppress revenues due to heightened uncertainty.
A prominent application appears in insurance markets, where VOI drives underwriting processes to counter adverse selection. Insurers collect data on applicants' risk profilessuch as health records or driving historyto classify policyholders and set premiums accordingly, reducing the pool of high-risk individuals who might otherwise dominate under symmetric pricing. In the Rothschild-Stiglitz equilibrium, without such information, competitive markets may fail to offer full insurance to low-risk types, leading to separating contracts where high-risk individuals receive suboptimal coverage; the VOI of underwriting data thus enables risk-adjusted pricing, expanding coverage and stabilizing premiums. For example, genetic testing or telematics in auto insurance generates positive VOI by allowing precise risk assessment, potentially lowering costs for low-risk customers while deterring adverse selection.
Welfare implications of VOI gaps highlight systemic market failures from underinvestment in information acquisition, often addressed through subsidies. When private incentives undervalue information due to externalitieslike uninternalized benefits to other market participantsadverse selection persists, reducing overall trade and allocative efficiency. Subsidies for information provision, such as government-funded health screenings or premium adjustments in insurance, can bridge these gaps by incentivizing data collection and revelation, thereby restoring competitive equilibria and enhancing social welfare. In health insurance contexts, linear premium subsidies correct adverse selection by encouraging broader participation without excessive coverage distortions, demonstrating how policy interventions leverage VOI to mitigate inefficiencies.

Modern Extensions

VOI in Machine Learning

The value of information (VOI) has been integrated into machine learning paradigms to guide data acquisition and decision-making processes under uncertainty, particularly in scenarios where labeling or exploring data is costly. In active learning, VOI serves as a criterion for selecting the most informative data points to label, thereby maximizing model performance gains with minimal annotation effort. A seminal approach is query-by-committee, where multiple models (a "committee") vote on unlabeled instances, and the VOI is quantified as the expected reduction in predictive uncertainty for points with high disagreement among committee members, as introduced by Seung et al. in their foundational work on active learning strategies. This method has been shown to outperform random sampling in tasks like image classification on benchmark datasets such as MNIST.
In reinforcement learning, VOI plays a crucial role in balancing exploration and exploitation, especially in multi-armed bandit problems where agents must decide whether additional information from pulling an arm outweighs immediate rewards. The VOI here is often computed as the expected utility gain from resolving uncertainty about action values, formalized in Bayesian bandit frameworks where the posterior over reward distributions informs the exploration policy. For instance, the Bayes-UCB algorithm incorporates VOI by selecting arms that maximize the upper confidence bound adjusted for informational value, leading to regret bounds that scale logarithmically with time horizons in theoretical analyses. Empirical studies in simulated environments demonstrate that VOI-guided policies reduce cumulative regret compared to epsilon-greedy methods in high-dimensional bandit settings.
Recent advances in the 2020s have focused on scalable VOI approximations for deep learning, leveraging neural network uncertainty quantification to handle the computational intractability of exact VOI in large-scale models. Bayesian neural networks (BNNs) approximate VOI by estimating epistemic uncertainty through variational inference, where the expected information gain is derived from the mutual information between model parameters and predictions, enabling efficient active learning in neural architectures. A key contribution is the work by Lakshminarayanan et al. on deep ensembles for uncertainty estimation, which approximates BNN posteriors and has been applied to select high-VOI samples in vision tasks, yielding accuracy improvements with less data in datasets like CIFAR-10. Further progress includes amortized VOI estimation using neural amortization networks, as proposed by Foster et al., which precompute VOI surrogates to make real-time decisions feasible in resource-constrained settings like edge AI. Recent 2025 works have further advanced amortized VOI for real-time AI decisions in experimental design.
An illustrative application of VOI in machine learning is in personalized medicine AI, where systems prioritize acquiring patient-specific data to refine treatment recommendations. For example, in Bayesian optimization for drug dosing, VOI guides the selection of diagnostic tests or genomic sequencing for patients, computing the expected utility increase from reducing uncertainty in individual response models; studies in clinical simulations show this approach can improve outcome predictions while minimizing invasive procedures.

Ethical and Practical Considerations

The assessment of the value of information (VOI) often involves significant privacy trade-offs, particularly when acquiring personal data for decision-making processes. In contexts such as online platforms and AI systems, individuals weigh the utility of sharing information against privacy risks, with empirical studies showing that the perceived value of preventing secondary use of personal data ranges from $39.83 to $49.78 per instance. Regulations like the EU's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), implemented in 2018, have amplified these trade-offs by increasing compliance costs for data acquisition, leading to a 20% rise in average processing expenses and reduced data usage by firms. This can diminish the net VOI, as the regulatory burdenssuch as mandatory consent and data minimizationmay exceed the expected benefits from information gains, prompting organizations to limit collection efforts.
Incomplete priors in VOI calculations can introduce biases that propagate into discriminatory outcomes, especially in AI-driven applications. In Bayesian frameworks underlying VOI, reliance on partial or flawed initial assumptions leads to skewed estimates of information utility, exacerbating confirmation bias where analysts prioritize evidence aligning with existing beliefs over disconfirming data. For instance, in intelligence analysis using probabilistic argumentation, incomplete priors modeled at probabilities like 0.65 for "likely" events can undervalue critical evidence, resulting in unbalanced hypotheses that favor certain groups and lead to unfair AI decisions in areas like hiring or lending. Such biases highlight the need for robust prior elicitation to mitigate systemic discrimination in machine learning contexts where VOI guides data selection.
Practical hurdles in VOI implementation frequently arise when the costs of computation and data acquisition surpass the anticipated value, rendering information pursuit uneconomical. VOI analysis serves as a tool to rationalize these expenses by comparing project outcomes with and without new data, but challenges like data fuzzinesswhere imprecise measurements reduce expected gainscan lower VOI below acquisition thresholds. In data-driven decision making, the monetary and effort costs of obtaining information, as formalized in decision theory, often lead to suboptimal choices if not balanced against VOI, with real-world examples in resource exploration showing that fuzzy data halves the potential value compared to crisp alternatives. These barriers underscore the importance of pre-acquisition VOI assessments to avoid overinvestment.
In the 2020s, VOI considerations have extended to sustainable AI, where environmental costs of data centers and computation must be weighed against informational benefits to promote eco-friendly practices. Digital technologies, including AI, contribute approximately 2% of global greenhouse gas emissions through data centerscomparable to aviationand are projected to consume around 2% of worldwide electricity as of 2025, with estimates reaching 3-4% by 2030, raising the effective cost of information acquisition in VOI models. Frameworks for information management in climate action emphasize VOI's role in optimizing data use for net-zero goals, such as AI-driven energy efficiency that could cut emissions by 4% by 2030, while advocating renewable energy integration to balance these impacts. This evolution reflects a broader push, post-COP26 in 2021, to incorporate sustainability metrics into VOI for applications in machine learning and economics.
