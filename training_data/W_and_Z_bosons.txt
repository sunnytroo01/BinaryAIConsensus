W and Z bosons
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The W and Z bosons are massive elementary particles that serve as the mediators of the weak interaction, one of the four fundamental forces in the Standard Model of particle physics. The charged W bosons (W and W) carry electric charges of +1 and 1 elementary charges, respectively, enabling processes that change the flavor of quarks and leptons, such as beta decay, while the neutral Z boson (Z) has zero electric charge and mediates flavor-conserving neutral current interactions. Both are spin-1 vector bosons with short lifetimes on the order of 10 seconds due to their large massesapproximately 80.4 GeV/c for the W bosons and 91.2 GeV/c for the Z bosonacquired through the Higgs mechanism in electroweak symmetry breaking.
Predicted by the electroweak theory developed by Sheldon Glashow, Abdus Salam, and Steven Weinberg in the late 1960s, which unified the electromagnetic and weak forces into a single framework, the W and Z bosons provided crucial experimental confirmation of this unification when discovered in 1983 at CERN's Super Proton Synchrotron collider by the UA1 and UA2 collaborations. Their detection, involving high-energy proton-antiproton collisions producing W e and Z ee decays, earned Carlo Rubbia and Simon van der Meer the 1984 Nobel Prize in Physics for enabling the necessary collider technology. These bosons play essential roles in astrophysical processes, including stellar nucleosynthesis, where W-mediated reactions fuse hydrogen into helium and drive the Sun's energy production, as well as in enabling the formation of heavier elements beyond iron through weak interactions in supernovae.
Subsequent precision measurements at facilities like LEP, the Tevatron, and the LHC have refined their properties, including decay widths (2.20 GeV for W and 2.50 GeV for Z) and branching ratiosW bosons decay roughly 10.8% to an electron (or positron) and neutrino and Z bosons about 3.4% to electron-positron pairstesting the Standard Model's predictions to high accuracy and constraining extensions beyond it, such as supersymmetry or extra dimensions. Recent ATLAS and CMS results, including a 2024 high-precision W mass measurement of 80.360 0.010 GeV/c by CMS that aligns with Standard Model predictions and resolves prior measurement discrepancies, continue to validate electroweak parameters like the weak mixing angle while probing for subtle deviations that could signal new physics. The bosons' interactions also underpin phenomena like neutrino oscillations and parity violation in weak decays, underscoring their foundational importance in particle physics and cosmology.

Charges and Quantum Numbers

The W and Z bosons possess distinct electric charges that distinguish their roles in electroweak interactions: the W+^++ carries a charge of +1+1+1 (in units of the elementary charge eee), the W^- carries 1-11, and the Z0^00 is neutral with charge 000. These charges arise naturally from the structure of the underlying gauge theory and determine whether the bosons mediate charged-current or neutral-current processes.
Under the weak isospin symmetry of the electroweak model, the W+^++, W^-, and Z0^00 form an isotriplet with total weak isospin quantum number I=1I=1I=1. The third component of weak isospin is I3=+1I_3 = +1I3=+1 for W+^++, I3=1I_3 = -1I3=1 for W^-, and I3=0I_3 = 0I3=0 for Z0^00. This triplet assignment stems from the SU(2)L_LL gauge symmetry proposed in the partial unification of weak and electromagnetic interactions.
The W bosons transform as the adjoint (triplet) representation of the SU(2)L_LL gauge group, corresponding to the gauge fields associated with the generators of this group. The neutral Z boson, however, emerges as an orthogonal combination of the neutral SU(2)L_LL gauge field W3W^3_\muW3 and the U(1)Y_YY hypercharge gauge field BB_\muB:

This mixing ensures the correct gauge invariance and mass assignments in the theory.
As gauge bosons, the W and Z particles are vector bosons with spin quantum number S=1S=1S=1. They exhibit negative intrinsic parity P=1P=-1P=1, reflecting the parity-violating nature of weak interactions. The self-conjugate Z0^00 boson has C-parity C=1C=-1C=1. These spin and parity assignments are consistent with their role as mediators of the short-range weak force.

Role in Weak Interactions

Charged Currents via W Bosons

These interactions involve the transition of a down-type quark or charged lepton into its up-type counterpart, accompanied by the emission or absorption of a W or W boson. Neutral Currents via Z Boson

The Z boson mediates neutral current weak interactions, which are flavor-conserving processes that do not involve the exchange of electric charge between participating fermions. These interactions arise from the exchange of the neutral Z boson in the t-channel or s-channel, preserving the identity and charge of the fermions involved, in contrast to charged current processes that change fermion flavor and charge.

Unlike the W boson, which couples exclusively to left-handed chiral currents, the Z boson couples to both left- and right-handed fermions through the vector part, modulated by the weak mixing angle.

The first experimental evidence for neutral currents came from the Gargamelle bubble chamber experiment at CERN's Proton Synchrotron in 1973, which observed events consistent with neutrino-hadron interactions producing only hadrons, without accompanying charged leptons such as muons.
At atomic scales, neutral currents manifest in parity-violating effects due to interference with electromagnetic interactions, as seen in atomic parity violation experiments. For instance, measurements of parity violation in cesium atoms, where the weak neutral interaction slightly mixes atomic states of opposite parity, confirmed the predicted couplings with high precision.

Theoretical Predictions

Electroweak Unification Model

The Glashow-Weinberg-Salam (GWS) model, formulated in the 1960s, provides a unified gauge theory for the electromagnetic and weak interactions, predicting the existence of the charged W bosons and neutral Z boson as mediators of the weak force. In 1961, Sheldon Glashow proposed an initial framework based on the gauge group SU(2) U(1), where the weak interactions are mediated by four vector bosons: an SU(2) triplet (two charged W, W and one neutral W) and a U(1) singlet B, alongside the photon, to accommodate both parity-violating weak processes and parity-conserving electromagnetism. This model addressed limitations in earlier Fermi four-fermion theories but initially lacked a mechanism for boson masses.
In 1967, Steven Weinberg advanced the theory by incorporating spontaneous symmetry breaking through a complex scalar Higgs field, which generates masses for the W and Z bosons via the Higgs mechanism while leaving the photon massless, thus preserving the unbroken U(1) electromagnetic symmetry. The underlying gauge structure is the chiral group SU(2)_L U(1)_Y, where SU(2)_L acts on left-handed fermion doublets and U(1)_Y on hypercharge; the corresponding gauge bosons are the triplet W^1, W^2, W^3 for SU(2)_L and the singlet B for U(1)_Y. After electroweak symmetry breaking, linear combinations yield the physical fields: the charged W^ from W^1 and W^2, the neutral Z from a mixture of W^3 and B, and the massless photon A orthogonal to Z. Abdus Salam independently developed an equivalent formulation in 1968, emphasizing the unification at high energies above the symmetry-breaking scale.
A key feature of the GWS model is its prediction of weak neutral currents mediated by the Z boson, which resolved inconsistencies in the pure vector-axial vector (V-A) description of weak interactions by allowing parity-violating but neutral processes involving neutrinos and electrons. Weinberg's 1967 work specifically derived these currents from the gauge structure, predicting their strength relative to charged currents via the weak mixing angle _W, defined by the coupling constants of SU(2)_L and U(1)_Y. The model relates the boson masses through
mW=mZcosW,
m_W = m_Z \cos \theta_W,
mW=mZcosW,
where _W 28.7 corresponds to sin_W 0.231, a value emerging from the unification parameters and later refined by experiments. For their contributions to this electroweak unification, Glashow, Salam, and Weinberg shared the 1979 Nobel Prize in Physics.

Mass Generation via Higgs Mechanism

In the Standard Model of particle physics, the Higgs mechanism explains how the W and Z bosons acquire mass while preserving the gauge invariance of the electroweak theory. This process occurs through spontaneous symmetry breaking (SSB) of the SU(2)_L U(1)_Y electroweak symmetry, introduced in the seminal formulation of the unified electroweak model.
The Higgs field is modeled as a complex scalar doublet with weak isospin I=1/2 and hypercharge Y=1/2, whose potential allows it to develop a nonzero vacuum expectation value (VEV) in its neutral component. The magnitude of this VEV is v 246 GeV, determined from the Fermi coupling constant G_F via the relation v = (2 G_F)^{-1/2}. After SSB, the Higgs doublet expands around this VEV as = (0, (v + h)/2)^T, where h represents the Higgs boson field.

Substituting the VEV into this term yields quadratic mass contributions for the gauge bosons.
These contributions form a mass matrix in the (W^3_, B_) basis, which is diagonalized by the weak mixing angle _W, defined such that cos _W = g / (g^2 + g'^2) and sin _W = g' / (g^2 + g'^2). During SSB, the three broken generators of the electroweak group produce Goldstone bosons, which are "eaten" by the W^ and Z bosons via the Higgs mechanism, endowing them with longitudinal polarization modes necessary for consistency at high energies. The tree-level mass ratio m_W / m_Z = cos _W has been verified to high precision, confirming the consistency of the Higgs mechanism within the electroweak framework.

Discovery and Early Experiments

Pre-Discovery Searches

The Glashow-Weinberg-Salam (GWS) electroweak model, developed in the late 1960s and early 1970s, predicted the existence of massive W and Z bosons as mediators of the weak interaction, with estimated masses in the range of approximately 50-100 GeV based on the Fermi constant and weak mixing angle measurements from neutrino scattering data. These predictions highlighted the need for particle accelerators capable of achieving center-of-mass energies exceeding twice this mass scale to produce the bosons, motivating the development of high-energy colliders beyond the capabilities of existing facilities in the early 1970s.
A pivotal early experimental hint came from the Gargamelle bubble chamber at CERN, which in 1973 provided the first direct evidence for weak neutral currents through observations of neutrino-induced hadronic showers without associated charged leptons in a muon-neutrino beam from the Proton Synchrotron. This result, analyzed from over 83,000 neutrino and 207,000 antineutrino interactions, confirmed the theoretical prediction of Z boson-mediated neutral currents in the GWS model and ruled out alternative models lacking such processes, such as purely charged-current theories. The observation strengthened the case for the full electroweak unification, including both W and Z bosons, and spurred further searches for their direct production.
In parallel, early proton-antiproton collision experiments at CERN's Intersecting Storage Rings (ISR), operational from 1971 with center-of-mass energies up to 63 GeV, sought signatures of intermediate vector bosons through missing transverse energy events indicative of invisible neutrino decays. These searches in the late 1970s yielded null results that established lower mass limits, underscoring the ISR's limitations for full discovery while validating the need for higher energies.
Complementing these efforts, neutrino beam experiments at SLAC and Fermilab in the 1970s focused on charged-current interactions to probe the W boson. Early bubble chamber runs analyzed deep inelastic scattering events to derive lower bounds on the W mass, reaching around 20 GeV by the late 1970s and eliminating low-mass intermediate vector boson hypotheses that had persisted from earlier models. These results, derived from large numbers of interactions, aligned with GWS expectations and ruled out scalar or low-mass vector alternatives for weak interactions.
The culmination of these pre-discovery efforts was the 1978 approval by CERN's council of the Super Proton Synchrotron (SppS) modification into a proton-antiproton collider, designed for a center-of-mass energy of 540 GeV to access the predicted W and Z production threshold. This decision, driven by the accumulating theoretical and experimental evidence, incorporated stochastic cooling techniques pioneered at the ISR to achieve sufficient luminosity, paving the way for definitive searches at energies sufficient for boson production.

1983 CERN Discoveries

The landmark discoveries of the W and Z bosons occurred in 1983 at CERN's Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS), which had been repurposed as a proton-antiproton collider with a center-of-mass energy of s=540\sqrt{s} = 540s=540 GeV, enabling the production of these heavy particles through quark-antiquark annihilation processes such as ppW+Xp \bar{p} \to W^\pm + XppW+X and ppZ0+Xp \bar{p} \to Z^0 + XppZ0+X. The UA1 collaboration, led by Carlo Rubbia and comprising around 130 physicists from multiple institutions, analyzed data from the 1982 run and announced the observation of the W boson on 20 January 1983 during a seminar at CERN. They reported six candidate events consistent with WeeW \to e \nu_eWee decays, where a high-transverse-momentum (pTp_TpT) electron (pT>11p_T > 11pT>11 GeV) was accompanied by significant missing transverse energy (ET>11E_T > 11ET>11 GeV) from the undetected neutrino, yielding a transverse mass distribution peaking around 80 GeV. This initial mass estimate for the W boson was 81581 \pm 5815 GeV, in agreement with electroweak theory predictions of approximately 80 GeV.
The UA2 collaboration, consisting of about 60 physicists and led by Pierre Darriulat, provided independent confirmation of the W boson shortly thereafter, presenting their results on 21 January 1983 and publishing on 17 March 1983. UA2 observed four events matching the W signature, including both WeeW \to e \nu_eWee and WW \to \mu \nu_\muW decays, again featuring isolated high-pTp_TpT leptons paired with large missing ETE_TET. Their transverse mass analysis similarly indicated a W mass around 81 GeV, with the combined UA1 and UA2 datasets establishing a statistical significance of approximately 5\sigma for the W discovery, corresponding to a background probability of less than 10610^{-6}106. These observations validated the charged-current weak interaction mediated by the charged W bosons, a cornerstone of the Glashow-Weinberg-Salam electroweak model.
In May 1983, during a subsequent collider run, the UA1 experiment announced the discovery of the Z boson, reporting four candidate events from Ze+eZ \to e^+ e^-Ze+e decays characterized by back-to-back high-pTp_TpT electron-positron pairs with an invariant mass clustering around 93 GeV. The initial mass measurement was 93393 \pm 3933 GeV, consistent with theoretical expectations near 90 GeV, and the observation achieved about 6\sigma significance based on the dilepton invariant mass distribution and low background from other processes. The UA2 collaboration independently confirmed the Z boson later in 1983 with similar dilepton events, further solidifying the neutral-current weak interaction via the neutral Z boson.
These discoveries at CERN not only confirmed the existence of the weak force carriers but also provided direct evidence for electroweak unification, as the measured masses aligned closely with Standard Model predictions incorporating the Higgs mechanism. The achievements were recognized with the 1984 Nobel Prize in Physics awarded to Carlo Rubbia, spokesperson for UA1, and Simon van der Meer, whose stochastic cooling technique enabled the high-luminosity proton-antiproton beams essential for the experiments.

Precision Measurements

W Boson Mass and Width

The mass of the W boson, $ m_W $, and its total decay width, $ \Gamma_W $, are fundamental parameters in the Standard Model (SM), constraining electroweak radiative corrections and probing potential new physics. Experimental determinations rely on high-precision reconstructions of W boson production and decay at hadron colliders, primarily through the dominant charged-current process $ pp $ or $ p\bar{p} \to W \to \ell \nu + X $, where $ \ell $ is an electron or muon and $ \nu $ the neutrino. Direct reconstruction involves identifying the charged lepton and inferring the neutrino momentum from missing transverse energy ($ E_T^{\rm miss} $), forming the transverse mass $ m_T = \sqrt{2 p_T^\ell E_T^{\rm miss} (1 - \cos \Delta \phi)} $, where $ p_T^\ell $ is the lepton transverse momentum and $ \Delta \phi $ the azimuthal angle between the lepton and $ E_T^{\rm miss} $. The endpoint of the $ m_T $ distribution, known as the Jacobian peak, encodes the W boson's mass and width, allowing fits to extract these parameters while accounting for detector resolution, QCD radiation, and electroweak effects.
At the Tevatron, the CDF and D0 collaborations utilized proton-antiproton collisions at $ \sqrt{s} = 1.96 $ TeV to achieve early precision measurements via lepton-plus-jet (or missing energy) events. The CDF collaboration's 2022 analysis of 8.8 fb1^{-1}1 of data yielded $ m_W = 80.4335 \pm 0.0094 $ GeV using template fits to the $ m_T $ spectrum in electron and muon channels, incorporating advanced modeling of QCD parton showers and detector efficiencies. This result initially indicated a 4.2 tension with the SM prediction of approximately 80.357 GeV (including higher-order corrections), sparking discussions of beyond-SM contributions to the W mass. At the LHC, ATLAS and CMS experiments with proton-proton collisions at $ \sqrt{s} = 7, 8, $ and 13 TeV employed similar $ m_T $ distribution fits but benefited from higher luminosities and improved tracking. The 2023 ATLAS measurement from 7 TeV data gave $ m_W = 80.360 \pm 0.016 $ GeV, while the 2024 CMS analysis of 13 TeV data from 2016 (36 fb1^{-1}1) reported $ m_W = 80.360 \pm 0.010 $ GeV, both aligning closely with SM expectations. A preliminary 2025 LHC combination, incorporating these and ongoing ATLAS updates, yields $ m_W \approx 80.360 $ GeV with sub-10 MeV uncertainty, consistent with SM predictions and highlighting a ~5 discrepancy with the CDF result, suggesting potential issues in the Tevatron analysis.
The W boson width is extracted indirectly from production cross sections, leveraging the relation $ \sigma(pp \to W) \propto \Gamma(W \to \ell \nu) / m_W^2 $, where the partial leptonic width scales with the total width assuming lepton universality. Measurements of the inclusive $ \sigma(pp \to W) $ at the LHC and Tevatron, normalized to theoretical QCD predictions (NNLO+NNLL accuracy), yield $ \Gamma_W / m_W $ ratios that, combined with direct $ m_W $, determine $ \Gamma_W $. Radiative corrections, including electroweak virtual and real emissions, are incorporated via resummation techniques to achieve percent-level precision. The current world average is $ \Gamma_W = 2.085 \pm 0.042 $ GeV, consistent with SM expectations from the Higgs mechanism and loop-induced decays. In 2024, ATLAS reported the first direct measurement of the W width at the LHC, $ \Gamma_W = 2.05 \pm 0.15 $ GeV from 7 TeV data, consistent with the world average.

Z Boson Mass and Width

The high-precision measurements of the Z boson's mass and total decay width were conducted primarily at the Large Electron-Positron Collider (LEP1) between 1989 and 1995, where the accelerator operated at center-of-mass energies s\sqrt{s}s tuned to the Z pole (smZ\sqrt{s} \approx m_ZsmZ) through energy scans of the resonance line shape, accumulating approximately 17 million Z boson events across the ALEPH, DELPHI, L3, and OPAL experiments. These data enabled rigorous tests of the electroweak sector of the Standard Model by quantifying the Z resonance parameters with unprecedented accuracy.
The Z boson mass is $ m_Z = 91.1876 \pm 0.0021 $ GeV, achieving a relative precision of about 20 parts per million (corresponding to an absolute uncertainty of roughly 1 MeV), while the total decay width is $ \Gamma_Z = 2.4952 \pm 0.0023 $ GeV, determined from the peak height and angular width of the resonance in the measured cross-sections for hadronic and leptonic final states. These values were extracted via parametrized fits to the Breit-Wigner lineshape formula, which models the propagator as $ \frac{s}{(s - m_Z^2)^2 + m_Z^2 \Gamma_Z^2} $ (in the s-dependent width convention), incorporating corrections for initial-state QED radiation, beamstrahlung, and luminosity effects to account for the convoluted experimental response.
The invisible partial width, computed as $ \Gamma_{\rm inv} = \Gamma_Z - \sum \Gamma_{\rm vis} $ (subtracting visible hadronic and leptonic contributions), measures $ \Gamma_{\rm inv} = 499.0 \pm 1.5 $ MeV and implies an effective number of neutrino species $ N_\nu = 2.984 \pm 0.008 $, providing compelling evidence for exactly three light neutrino generations as predicted by the Standard Model minimal structure.
The hadronic partial width, $ \Gamma_{\rm had} = 1.744 \pm 0.002 $ GeV, serves as a key probe of quantum chromodynamics within the electroweak framework and allows extraction of the strong coupling constant $ \alpha_s(m_Z) $ with percent-level precision through QCD radiative corrections to the quark decay modes. Reanalyses of LEP data as of 2024 have yielded only minor refinements to these parameters, preserving their excellent consistency with Standard Model expectations and their role in pre-2012 constraints on the Higgs boson mass through radiative corrections.

Decay Modes

W Boson Decays

The W boson, being charged, mediates charged-current weak interactions and primarily decays into a lepton and its corresponding neutrino or into a quark-antiquark pair of different generations. These decays are two-body processes, with the total decay width dominated by the available phase space and Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa (CKM) matrix elements. The leptonic decay modes are particularly clean experimentally, as they produce isolated high-transverse-momentum leptons and missing energy from the undetectable neutrino.
The dominant leptonic decays are $ W^- \to \ell^- \bar{\nu}_\ell $ for =e,,\ell = e, \mu, \tau=e,,, each with a branching ratio of approximately 10.86%. These modes are nearly universal across lepton flavors due to the absence of strong (QCD) corrections and the V-A structure of the weak interaction, which treats all charged leptons similarly up to small electroweak radiative effects. Z Boson Decays

The Z boson predominantly decays into fermion-antifermion pairs through neutral weak currents, with the primary channels being invisible decays to neutrinos, leptonic decays to charged lepton pairs, and hadronic decays to quark pairs. These decays are governed by the vector and axial-vector couplings of the Z boson to fermions, leading to distinct branching ratios that align closely with Standard Model predictions.
The invisible decay mode, $ Z \to \nu \bar{\nu} $, where the neutrino flavors are electron, muon, and tau, has a branching ratio of approximately 20%. This channel is particularly important for determining the number of light neutrino species, with measurements from the LEP experiments yielding $ N_\nu = 2.984 \pm 0.008 $, consistent with three generations in the Standard Model.
Leptonic decays, $ Z \to l^+ l^- $ for $ l = e, \mu, \tau $, each occur with a branching ratio of about 3.36%. These modes produce clean signatures with two high-energy leptons and minimal hadronic activity, making them ideal for precise scans of the Z boson resonance lineshape and mass determination.
Hadronic decays, $ Z \to q \bar{q} $, dominate with a branching ratio of approximately 69.9% and are enhanced by quantum chromodynamic (QCD) corrections that increase the multiplicity of final-state hadrons. Light quarks (up, down, and strange) contribute the majority of these events, while bottom-quark decays, identifiable through b-tagging techniques, account for approximately 21.6% of the hadronic channel.

(Zff)=GFmZ362Nc(gV2+gA2),

Measurements from LEP confirm Standard Model expectations for these decays, including the ratio $ R_b = \Gamma(Z \to b \bar{b}) / \Gamma_\mathrm{had} \approx 0.2166 $, which provides sensitivity to heavy quark couplings and QCD dynamics.
