Atomic mass
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Atomic mass is the rest mass of an atom in its ground state, typically expressed in unified atomic mass units (u). The unified atomic mass unit is a non-SI unit defined as exactly one twelfth of the mass of a carbon-12 atom in its ground state, equivalent to the atomic mass constant of 1.66053906892 10^{-27} kg (as of 2022 CODATA). This unit facilitates precise measurements of atomic and molecular masses, with the mass of an individual atom primarily determined by the protons and neutrons in its nucleus, as electrons contribute negligibly to the total mass (about 1/1836 that of a proton).
For chemical elements, which often consist of multiple isotopes, the atomic mass commonly refers to the relative atomic mass, a dimensionless quantity representing the ratio of the weighted average mass of the atoms of an element (accounting for isotopic abundances) to the unified atomic mass unit. This value, also known as the standard atomic weight, is listed in the periodic table and varies slightly for elements with unstable or varying isotopic compositions, such as hydrogen (1.0080 u) or uranium (238.02891 u) (as of 2023 IUPAC). Accurate atomic masses are essential for applications in chemistry, including stoichiometry, determining molar masses, and predicting reaction yields, as well as in physics for nuclear calculations and mass spectrometry.
The determination of atomic masses relies on high-precision techniques like mass spectrometry, which separates isotopes based on their mass-to-charge ratio, and international standards set by organizations such as the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) and the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST). These values are periodically updated to reflect new measurements, ensuring consistency in scientific data across disciplines.

Fundamentals

Definition and Scope

Atomic mass refers to the rest mass of an individual atom in its ground state, excluding any contributions from kinetic energy or excitation. This quantity is typically expressed in unified atomic mass units (u or Da), where one u is defined as exactly one-twelfth the mass of a carbon-12 atom in its ground state. The mass arises primarily from the atom's constituent particles: protons and neutrons in the nucleus (collectively known as nucleons), with electrons contributing negligibly due to their much smaller massapproximately 1/1836 that of a proton.
The scope of atomic mass encompasses both isotopic masses, which apply to specific isotopes of an element, and average atomic masses (also called atomic weights) for elements with multiple stable isotopes, weighted by their natural abundances. For instance, the atomic mass of the hydrogen-1 isotope (protium) is approximately 1.007825 u, reflecting the combined mass of its single proton and electron. In contrast, the carbon-12 isotope serves as the international standard, with its atom defined to have an exact mass of 12 u, providing a reference scale for all other atomic masses.
This foundational concept underpins discussions of atomic structure and periodic trends, distinguishing atomic mass from related quantities like molar mass, which scales the value by Avogadro's number for bulk samples.

Units and Scales

The atomic mass unit (u), also known as the unified atomic mass unit, serves as the primary unit for expressing atomic masses. It is defined exactly as one-twelfth the mass of an unbound neutral atom of carbon-12 in its nuclear and electronic ground state. This definition establishes the atomic mass unit as approximately 1.660539 10^{-27} kg, with modern evaluations providing higher precision, such as the CODATA 2022 recommended value of 1.66053906892(52) 10^{-27} kg. The unit is non-SI but accepted for use with the International System of Units (SI), facilitating precise quantification of masses at the atomic scale.
An alternative name for the atomic mass unit is the dalton (Da), which is synonymous and often used in biochemical contexts to denote the same quantity. In relation to SI base units, the atomic mass unit connects directly to the kilogram (kg) through its defined value, while for molar quantities, it relates to grams per mole (g/mol) via Avogadro's constant NAN_ANA. Specifically, the molar mass constant MuM_uMu is exactly 1 g mol^{-1}, such that the mass of one mole of atoms each with a mass of 1 u is 1 g, linking atomic-scale measurements to macroscopic chemical applications. This relationship underscores the unit's role in bridging microscopic and bulk properties without altering the fundamental definition.
Atomic masses can be expressed on either absolute or relative scales. The absolute scale uses the atomic mass unit or kilograms to denote actual physical mass, whereas the relative scale is dimensionless, scaling masses proportionally to one-twelfth the mass of a carbon-12 atom (i.e., relative atomic mass is the mass in u, numerically). This relative scale predominates in tables of atomic weights and isotopic masses for its convenience in comparisons. Historically, prior to 1961, the standard referenced the mass of oxygen-16 (with chemists using natural oxygen and physicists the isotope), leading to discrepancies of about 0.0275% between scales. In 1961, the International Union of Pure and Applied Physics (IUPAP) and the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) adopted the unified atomic mass unit based on carbon-12, resolving these inconsistencies and establishing the current international standard. Modern CODATA evaluations maintain this standard with rigorous uncertainty assessments, ensuring consistency across scientific disciplines.

Isotopic and Average Masses

Relative Isotopic Mass

The relative isotopic mass, denoted $ A_r $ for a specific isotope, is defined as the ratio of the mass of that isotope to one-twelfth the mass of a carbon-12 atom in its ground state. This dimensionless quantity provides a standardized measure relative to the carbon-12 scale, where the relative isotopic mass of $ ^{12}\mathrm{C} $ is exactly 12 by definition. Although the relative isotopic mass is numerically close to the mass number $ A $ (the total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus), it deviates slightly due to differences in nuclear binding energies, which arise from the strong nuclear force holding the nucleus together. For instance, the relative isotopic mass of $ ^{13}\mathrm{C} $ is 13.003 354 837(13), showing a fractional deviation of about 0.025% from 13. These deviations reflect the mass-energy equivalence described by Einstein's equation $ E = mc^2 $, where binding energy reduces the total mass below the sum of individual nucleon masses.
Isobars, which are nuclides with the same mass number $ A $ but different elements (and thus different proton numbers), exhibit relative isotopic masses that are similar yet distinctly different, highlighting the influence of nuclear structure. A representative example is $ ^{40}\mathrm{Ar} $ with $ A_r = 39.962 383 122(23) $ and $ ^{40}\mathrm{Ca} $ with $ A_r = 39.962 590 86(2) $, differing by approximately 0.00021 u due to varying binding energies in their nuclei.
Values for relative isotopic masses are rigorously compiled in resources like the Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME), which integrates experimental data from techniques such as Penning trap mass spectrometry to achieve high precision, often on the order of 10^{-8} or better for stable isotopes. The latest AME2020 evaluation, for example, provides updated masses for over 3,000 nuclides, ensuring consistency with the carbon-12 standard and accounting for measurement uncertainties.

Atomic Weight and Relative Atomic Mass

The relative atomic mass $ A_r $, commonly known as the atomic weight, of an element is the abundance-weighted average of the relative atomic masses of its naturally occurring isotopes, expressed as a dimensionless quantity relative to $ \frac{1}{12} $ the mass of a carbon-12 atom. This value represents the typical mass ratio for atoms of the element in standard terrestrial samples and is essential for stoichiometric calculations in chemistry. The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC), through its Commission on Isotopic Abundances and Atomic Weights (CIAAW), evaluates and publishes standard atomic weights based on peer-reviewed isotopic abundance data.
The calculation follows the formula

where $ f_i $ is the fractional natural abundance of isotope $ i $ (summing to 1), and $ A_{r,i} $ is the relative atomic mass of that isotope. For elements with multiple stable isotopes, this results in a non-integer value, reflecting the isotopic mixture rather than a single nuclide mass. Standard atomic weights are listed in periodic tables and updated biennially to incorporate refined measurements, with uncertainties or intervals indicating precision and natural variability.
Chlorine provides a representative example, with two principal stable isotopes: $ ^{35}\text{Cl} $ (relative atomic mass 34.96885, abundance 0.7576) and $ ^{37}\text{Cl} $ (relative atomic mass 36.96590, abundance 0.2424). Related Physical Concepts

Mass Defect and Binding Energy

The mass defect, denoted as m, represents the difference between the actual atomic mass of a nuclide and the sum of the masses of its individual constituent particles: the protons, neutrons, and electrons. This discrepancy arises because the nucleus is a bound system, where the strong nuclear force converts a portion of the rest mass into binding energy, resulting in a measurable reduction in the total mass. The mass defect is quantified by the equation:
m=matom
\Delta m = \left - m_\text{atom}
m=matom
where ZZZ is the atomic number (number of protons), AAA is the mass number (total number of nucleons), mpm_pmp is the mass of a proton, mnm_nmn is the mass of a neutron, mem_eme is the mass of an electron, and matomm_\text{atom}matom is the measured atomic mass of the nuclide.
The mass defect is directly linked to the nuclear binding energy EbE_bEb through Albert Einstein's mass-energy equivalence principle, E=mc2E = mc^2E=mc2, where ccc is the speed of light in vacuum. Thus, the total binding energy is given by Eb=mc2E_b = \Delta m \cdot c^2Eb=mc2. In practical units, since atomic masses are often expressed in atomic mass units (u), the conversion factor is approximately 931.494 MeV/u, allowing EbE_bEb to be calculated in mega-electronvolts (MeV). This binding energy measures the energy required to disassemble the nucleus into its free constituents, explaining the stability of atomic nuclei: higher binding energies indicate greater resistance to fission or other disruptive processes.
When expressed as binding energy per nucleon (Eb/AE_b / AEb/A), the value reveals trends in nuclear stability across the periodic table. This quantity increases with mass number AAA up to a peak near iron-56 (56Fe^{56}\text{Fe}56Fe), where it reaches about 8.79 MeV per nucleon, making iron one of the most stable nuclides and marking the point beyond which fusion becomes endothermic. For lighter nuclei, such as helium-4 (4He^{4}\text{He}4He), the mass defect is approximately 0.0304 u, corresponding to a total binding energy of 28.3 MeV or about 7.07 MeV per nucleon, illustrating how even simple nuclei exhibit significant binding that stabilizes them against decay.
In exotic nucleithose far from the line of stability with extreme proton-to-neutron ratiosrecent precision mass measurements have uncovered deviations in mass defects that probe subtle nuclear interactions. For instance, data from facilities like TRIUMF in 2024 have revealed reduced neutron pairing effects in neutron-rich tin isotopes, leading to smaller-than-expected mass defects and implications for rapid neutron-capture processes in astrophysics. Similarly, measurements of proton-rich silicon-23 in 2025 have quantified isospin symmetry breaking through mass defect anomalies, enhancing models of shell evolution in unstable nuclei. These insights, derived from Penning trap and storage ring experiments, highlight how mass defects in exotic systems can signal new physics, such as altered magic numbers or deformation effects not prominent in stable isotopes.

Distinctions from Similar Quantities

Atomic mass refers to the measured or calculated rest mass of a single atom, typically expressed in atomic mass units (u), and is distinct from the atomic number, which is the number of protons in the nucleus and determines the element's identity. The atomic number, denoted Z, is an integer that does not directly relate to mass but to charge and periodic table position. In contrast, the nucleon number (or mass number, A) is the total count of protons and neutrons in the nucleus, also an integer approximation used for nuclide notation, whereas atomic mass accounts for the precise, non-integer contributions from subatomic particles and binding effects.
Atomic mass further differs from molar mass, which scales the atomic mass to one mole of atoms and is expressed in grams per mole (g/mol); the molar mass constant, defined as exactly 1 g/mol, links the two by multiplying the relative atomic mass by this constant to yield the molar mass value. For a specific isotope, atomic mass is synonymous with nuclidic mass, defined as the mass of a nuclide (an atom specified by Z and A) in atomic mass units.
The term "atomic weight" historically denoted what is now precisely called relative atomic mass (A_r), a dimensionless ratio of the average atomic mass to 1/12 of carbon-12's mass, often leading to confusion with the absolute atomic mass in u. The IUPAC-preferred term "standard atomic weight" specifically refers to the recommended A_r value for an element, accounting for isotopic abundances in normal terrestrial materials. "Exact atomic mass" typically describes theoretically calculated values based on fundamental constants and binding energies, as opposed to experimentally measured atomic masses that include small discrepancies from nuclear structure.
Post-2022 IUPAC recommendations, building on the 2021 technical report, emphasize terminology for isotopic variations by expressing standard atomic weights as intervals for elements like argon and lead, where natural abundances vary significantly, to better reflect real-world isotopic diversity without implying fixed values. This approach clarifies that atomic mass pertains to individual nuclides, while standard atomic weights address elemental averages amid such variations.

Determination and Evolution

Methods of Measurement

The determination of atomic masses has evolved from early mass spectrometric techniques to highly precise methods utilizing Penning traps. In 1919, Francis Aston developed the parabola method in his mass spectrograph, which deflected ionized atoms using parallel electric and magnetic fields to produce parabolic traces on a photographic plate, allowing the identification and measurement of isotopes such as those of neon with a resolving power sufficient to distinguish mass differences of about 1 in 1000. This approach relied on the mass-to-charge ratio of ions, marking the first practical demonstration of isotopic mass separation and laying the groundwork for modern spectrometry.
Contemporary measurements predominantly employ Penning trap mass spectrometry, where ions are confined in a strong homogeneous magnetic field and a weak quadrupole electric field, enabling the determination of mass through the cyclotron frequency c=qB2m\nu_c = \frac{qB}{2\pi m}c=2mqB, with qqq as the ion charge, BBB the magnetic field strength, and mmm the ion mass. Facilities like ISOLTRAP at CERN's ISOLDE use this technique to achieve relative precisions down to 10810^{-8}108 for short-lived isotopes, by measuring the cyclotron frequency ratio of the ion of interest against a reference ion, often via time-of-flight ion-cyclotron-resonance (TOF-ICR) methods that excite the ion and detect its revolution time after release from the trap. In TOF-ICR, the ion's phase accumulation during excitation is mapped to its flight time, providing high-resolution mass values even for exotic nuclides produced online.
Accelerators such as JYFLTRAP at the University of Jyvskyl extend these capabilities for neutron-rich and refractory elements, employing double Penning trap setups to purify ion beams and measure masses with uncertainties below 1 keV/c, as demonstrated in recent studies of antimony and iodine isotopes. Cyclotron frequency ratio measurements form the core of these experiments, often using phase-imaging ICR (PI-ICR) for non-scanning, higher-throughput determinations that surpass traditional TOF-ICR precision by factors of 10 or more in favorable cases. Uncertainties arise from factors like ion excitation efficiency, magnetic field stability, and relativistic corrections, which are rigorously analyzed through repeated measurements and statistical fitting to yield final error bars typically at the parts-per-billion level.
Since atomic masses are measured for ions, the nuclear mass is derived by subtracting the masses of the Z electrons associated with the atomic number Z, along with small corrections for electron binding energies, which are negligible compared to nuclear scales but essential for consistency in evaluations. Comprehensive compilations, such as the Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME2020), integrate these experimental results from Penning traps and other sources, applying least-squares adjustments to over 4,000 data points while propagating uncertainties and resolving discrepancies through outlier rejection and covariance analysis. As of 2025, AME2020 remains the benchmark, with ongoing updates incorporating new trap measurements to refine masses for over 3,400 nuclides, ensuring atomic mass data supports precision nuclear physics and astrophysics.

Historical Development

In the early 19th century, the concept of atomic mass emerged from efforts to quantify the relative weights of elements based on chemical combinations. Jns Jacob Berzelius, a Swedish chemist, conducted systematic analyses in the 1810s, determining the relative atomic weights of nearly all known elements by 1818, using oxygen as the standard with a value of 100. His tables, revised periodically, provided the first comprehensive set of these values, laying the groundwork for stoichiometric calculations in chemistry.
Shortly thereafter, in 1815, British chemist William Prout proposed his hypothesis that the atomic weights of all elements were integer multiples of hydrogen's weight, suggesting hydrogen as the primordial building block of matter. This idea, though later challenged by precise measurements showing non-integer values, stimulated decades of research into atomic weights and their deviations. Prout's hypothesis highlighted the quest for simplicity in atomic masses, influencing subsequent experimental refinements.
The discovery of isotopes revolutionized atomic mass understanding in the early 20th century. In 1919, Francis William Aston developed the mass spectrograph, a device that separated ions by their mass-to-charge ratio, enabling the first precise measurements of individual atomic masses and revealing isotopesvariants of elements with the same atomic number but different masses. Aston's work demonstrated that non-integer atomic weights arose from isotopic mixtures, confirming isotopes in over 50 elements and earning him the 1922 Nobel Prize in Chemistry.
Standardization of atomic mass scales evolved in the 1920s and 1930s. By the mid-1920s, the international community adopted the 16^{16}16O = 16 scale, where the mass of the oxygen-16 isotope was defined as exactly 16 units, providing a unified reference for relative atomic masses that bridged chemistry and physics. This scale, formalized in reports by the International Commission on Atomic Weights (ICAW) in 1932, accounted for isotopic compositions and improved consistency in tabulated values.
The 1932 discovery of the neutron by James Chadwick provided a physical explanation for mass defectsthe observed deviations from integer sums of proton and neutron massesattributing them to nuclear binding energy. Chadwick's experiments, using beryllium irradiation, estimated the neutron mass as approximately 1.008 Da, slightly greater than the proton's, resolving why atomic masses were not exact integers and advancing nuclear models.
Post-World War II advancements in isotope separation enhanced precision in atomic mass measurements. Calutrons, large-scale mass spectrometers originally developed for uranium enrichment during the Manhattan Project, were repurposed in the late 1940s and 1950s to produce highly enriched isotopic samples, allowing more accurate mass determinations for lighter elements and rare isotopes. These efforts, conducted at facilities like Oak Ridge National Laboratory, contributed to refined atomic mass tables by minimizing isotopic impurities in samples.
A major shift occurred in 1961 when the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) and the International Union of Pure and Applied Physics (IUPAP) adopted the 12^{12}12C = 12 scale, defining the mass of carbon-12 as exactly 12 atomic mass units to unify chemical and physical standards. This replaced the 16^{16}16O scale, reducing discrepancies in cross-disciplinary data and establishing the unified atomic mass unit (u).
In 1969, the Committee on Data for Science and Technology (CODATA) established its Task Group on Fundamental Constants, which began periodic evaluations of atomic masses alongside other physical constants, ensuring self-consistent datasets based on global experimental inputs. These evaluations, updated every four years, integrated mass spectrometry and nuclear reaction data for high precision.
Modern developments include digital and computational evaluations of atomic masses, particularly for unstable isotopes. The Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME) collaboration, through the Atomic Mass Data Center (AMDC), released AME2020 with masses for over 3,400 nuclides, incorporating Penning trap measurements for short-lived species. As of November 2025, AME2020 remains the current evaluation, with the next full update anticipated in mid-2026; these advances, vital for nuclear astrophysics and reactor design, reflect ongoing refinements in handling uncertainties for unstable nuclides beyond stable-element focus.

Extensions and Applications

Relation to Molecular Mass

The molecular mass of a molecule, denoted as the absolute mass, is the sum of the atomic masses of its constituent atoms. The relative molecular mass $ M_r $, also known as the molecular weight, is defined as the ratio of the average mass of a molecule to the unified atomic mass unit $ u $, calculated as the sum of the relative atomic masses $ A_r $ of its atoms weighted by their stoichiometric coefficients:
Mr=niAr,i
M_r = \sum n_i A_{r,i}
Mr=niAr,i
where $ n_i $ is the number of atoms of each element $ i $. This relative scale facilitates comparisons without absolute mass units, building directly on atomic mass values.
The molar mass $ M $ of a molecular substance, expressed in grams per mole, is numerically equal to its relative molecular mass $ M_r $ when $ M_r $ is a pure number, due to the definition of the unified atomic mass unit as $ u = 1 , \mathrm{g \cdot mol^{-1}} / N_A $, where $ N_A $ is the Avogadro constant $ \approx 6.022 \times 10^{23} , \mathrm{mol^{-1}} $. Thus, for a molecule, the molar mass is $ M = M_r \times 1 , \mathrm{g \cdot mol^{-1}} $, enabling the conversion between the mass of a sample and the amount of substance in moles.
For water (HO), using standard relative atomic masses of hydrogen ($ A_r \approx 1.008 )andoxygen() and oxygen ()andoxygen( A_r \approx 15.999 $), the relative molecular mass is approximately 18.015, so its molar mass is 18.015 g/mol. Isotopic variations affect this value; for example, heavy water (DO), where deuterium has $ A_r \approx 2.014 $, yields a relative molecular mass of approximately 20.027 and molar mass of 20.027 g/mol, demonstrating how isotopic composition influences molecular mass in applications like isotopic labeling.
These quantities are essential in chemical applications: in stoichiometry, relative molecular masses determine mole ratios and predict reacting masses from balanced equations, supporting quantitative predictions of chemical reactions. In spectroscopy, particularly mass spectrometry, the molecular mass provides the mass-to-charge ratio for identifying molecular structures and compositions.

Role in Nuclear and Chemical Contexts

In nuclear physics, atomic mass plays a central role in calculating the energy released during fission and fusion reactions through the mass defect, which represents the difference between the mass of the reactants and the products. For instance, in nuclear fusion, such as the deuterium-tritium reaction powering experimental reactors, the atomic masses of the isotopes are used to determine the mass defect, enabling the computation of energy output via Einstein's mass-energy equivalence. Similarly, in fission processes like uranium-235 splitting, the atomic masses of the fission fragments and neutrons allow precise quantification of the energy yield, which is approximately 200 MeV per event, essential for reactor design and safety assessments.
In astrophysics, atomic mass data are indispensable for modeling nucleosynthesis processes that explain elemental abundances in stars and the solar system. Simulations of the rapid neutron-capture (r-process) in neutron star mergers rely on accurate atomic masses to predict isotopic yields, which are compared against observed solar abundances to validate models; for example, machine learning-enhanced mass tables have improved fits to solar r-process patterns for elements up to mass number 200. These masses also inform slow neutron-capture (s-process) contributions in asymptotic giant branch stars, where discrepancies in predicted versus observed solar abundances for barium and strontium isotopes highlight the need for refined mass evaluations.
Atomic mass influences chemical contexts through isotope effects, where differences in isotopic masses lead to fractionation during reactions and diffusion processes. In kinetic isotope effects, lighter isotopes react faster due to lower zero-point energies, as seen in hydrogen-deuterium exchange reactions where the rate ratio can exceed 7 at room temperature, affecting reaction pathways in enzymatic processes. Diffusion fractionation occurs in aqueous solutions, such as magnesium isotopes where lighter ^{24}Mg diffuses up to 1.5% faster than ^{26}Mg over short distances, influencing geochemical signatures in minerals and fluids. In geochemistry, the atomic mass of carbon-14 (14 u) is critical for radiocarbon dating, as accelerator mass spectrometry distinguishes ^{14}C from ^{12}C and ^{13}C by accelerating ions to measure their mass-to-charge ratios, enabling age determinations up to 50,000 years with precisions of 20 years.
Emerging applications leverage precise atomic masses in quantum technologies and medicine. In trapped-ion quantum computing, the atomic masses of ions like ytterbium-171 or calcium-43 determine trap frequencies and motional modes, with mass uncertainties on the order of 10^{-6} u ensuring stable qubit coherence times exceeding 1 second for scalable gate operations. For medical imaging, the mass number of fluorine-18 (18 u) identifies this positron-emitting isotope in positron emission tomography (PET) scans, where its 109.8-minute half-life and precise mass allow synthesis of tracers like ^{18}F-FDG for detecting tumors with spatial resolutions of 4-6 mm.
Atomic mass data underpin simulations like the TALYS code, which integrates evaluated masses from databases such as AME2020 to model reaction cross-sections for energies up to 200 MeV, aiding predictions of neutron-induced reactions in reactor fuels and astrophysical environments. Recent updates to the Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME), including ongoing refinements as of 2025, continue to improve the accuracy of these databases. In 2025, the Superheavy Element (SHE) Factory at JINR reported discoveries of isotopes like livermorium-289 and copernicium-280, contributing to enhanced models of stability in the superheavy region beyond Z=118.
