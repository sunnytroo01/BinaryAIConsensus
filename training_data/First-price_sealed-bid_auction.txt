First-price sealed-bid auction
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 A first-price sealed-bid auction is a procurement or sales mechanism in which multiple bidders independently submit confidential bids for an item or contract, the highest (or lowest, in reverse auctions) bid wins the allocation, and the winner pays exactly the amount of their submitted bid. This format contrasts with open auctions by preventing bidders from observing or reacting to competitors' offers in real time, emphasizing the role of private information and strategic decision-making.
In such auctions, bidders typically possess independent private values (IPV), where each estimates the item's worth based on personal signals drawn from a known distribution, and these values are uncorrelated across participants. Under symmetric IPV assumptionswith risk-neutral bidders, the item awarded to the highest-value participant, and zero expected surplus for the lowest-value bidderthe auction admits a Bayesian Nash equilibrium in which each bidder shades their bid below their true valuation to balance the trade-off between winning probability and payment. Specifically, in the symmetric case with J bidders and common value distribution G(v), the equilibrium bid function is B(v) = / G(v)^{J-1}, which is strictly increasing and differentiable, ensuring a unique bid for almost all valuations. For the common uniform distribution on , this simplifies to B(v) = ((J-1)/J) v, illustrating bid shading by a factor of (J-1)/J.
Notably, the revenue equivalence theorem establishes that, under the IPV conditions, first-price sealed-bid auctions generate the same expected revenue for the seller as second-price sealed-bid, English, or Dutch auctions, equal to the expected value of the second-highest bidder's signal. However, equivalence fails in asymmetric settings or with affiliated values, where first-price formats may yield lower revenue due to more aggressive shading by weaker bidders. In multi-unit scenarios, the format often operates as discriminatory, with each winner paying their own bid, leading to price dispersion among recipients.
First-price sealed-bid auctions are widely applied in real-world settings, including government procurement contracts where the lowest bid secures the deal (effectively reversing the highest-bid rule), U.S. Treasury bill sales prior to the shift to uniform pricing in 1998, and offshore oil lease bidding. In recent years, first-price sealed-bid auctions have also been adopted in digital advertising platforms for programmatic ad buying. These applications leverage the format's simplicity and confidentiality to minimize collusion risks, though they can encourage overbidding in common-value environments like mineral rights auctions due to the winner's curse.

Overview

Definition

A first-price sealed-bid auction is an auction format in which prospective bidders simultaneously submit confidential bids without knowledge of others' offers; the bidder submitting the highest (or lowest, in reverse auctions) bid wins the allocation and pays precisely the amount they bid. This mechanism ensures that bids remain private until the opening, promoting independent decision-making based on each bidder's valuation of the item. This format is commonly used in government procurement (as reverse auctions where the lowest bid wins) and historical sales such as U.S. Treasury bills.
The format was introduced to modern economic theory by William Vickrey in his seminal 1961 paper, where it served as a benchmark for analyzing strategic bidding behavior in contrast to alternative sealed-bid designs. Although Vickrey's work formalized its theoretical properties, practical implementations of first-price sealed-bid auctions predate this analysis, with widespread use in government procurement dating back to at least the nineteenth century in processes like public tendering for contracts. Such auctions have also been used in spectrum allocation, for example in early implementations in New Zealand and Australia in the 1990s, where sealed bidding helped allocate rights efficiently.
Key elements of the first-price sealed-bid auction include the sealed nature of bids, which prohibits any communication or observation among participants during submission, and the payment rule that obligates the winner to cover their own bid amount without adjustment. The design typically assumes a single item for sale, though extensions to multiple items exist in specialized applications. This sealed-bid approach is shared with second-price auctions, differing primarily in the payment determination.

Mechanism and Rules

In a first-price sealed-bid auction, participants submit confidential bids simultaneously without knowledge of others' offers, and the highest (or lowest, in reverse auctions) bidder wins the allocation while paying exactly the amount bid. The auctioneer facilitates the process by announcing the item for sale, the rules, and any reserve price if applicable, ensuring all bidders understand the format before submissions.
The procedural steps are as follows:

The auctioneer announces the auction details, including the item, bidding rules, and submission deadline.

Each bidder independently submits a single, sealed bid representing their offer, typically in a format that conceals the amount from other participants.

The auctioneer collects all bids and opens them privately, without revealing contents to bidders during the process.

The bid with the highest (or lowest, in reverse auctions) value is identified, and that bidder is declared the winner, who then pays their bid amount to acquire the item.

In case of ties among the highest (or lowest) bids, the winner is selected randomly or by a predefined rule, such as the lowest bidder index or alphabetical order.

The primary roles include the auctioneer, who organizes the auction, collects and evaluates bids, and enforces the rules to maintain confidentiality and fairness; and the bidders, who each submit one bid based on their private valuation of the item, with no opportunity for post-submission negotiation or revision.
Practical considerations encompass bid submission methods, such as sealed physical envelopes for traditional settings or secure electronic platforms in modern implementations, both designed to enforce sealing and prevent collusion or premature disclosure of bids. Bids are typically binding and nonnegative, with the auctioneer verifying compliance to ensure the integrity of the sealed process.

Symmetric Nash Equilibrium

This equilibrium strategy ensures that the bidder with the highest value wins the object with probability 1, while shading bids below true values to balance the trade-off between winning probability and payment conditional on winning.

Equilibrium Analysis

Example with Two Bidders

Consider a first-price sealed-bid auction with two risk-neutral bidders whose private valuations are independently drawn from a uniform distribution on . In the symmetric Nash equilibrium, each bidder employs the linear bidding strategy $ b(v) = \frac{v}{2} $, where $ v $ is their valuation, shading their bid below the true value to balance the probability of winning against the profit margin if they win.
To illustrate, suppose bidder 1 draws a valuation of $ v_1 = 0.8 $ and thus submits a bid of $ b_1 = 0.4 $. Bidder 1 wins the auction if bidder 2's bid is less than 0.4. Since bidder 2 follows the equilibrium strategy $ b_2(u) = \frac{u}{2} $ with $ u \sim \text{Uniform} $, this occurs when bidder 2's valuation $ u < 0.8 $, yielding a winning probability of 0.8. Generalization to n Bidders

In the symmetric independent private values model with nnn bidders whose valuations are independently drawn from a uniform distribution on , the symmetric Bayesian Nash equilibrium bidding strategy in a first-price sealed-bid auction is $ b(v) = \frac{n-1}{n} v $, where $ v $ is a bidder's private valuation. This linear strategy arises from each bidder maximizing their expected utility, balancing the trade-off between the probability of winning and the surplus extracted upon winning; the probability of winning for a bidder with valuation $ v $ is $ v^{n-1} $, reflecting the likelihood that all other bidders have lower valuations. This generalizes the two-bidder case, where the equilibrium bid simplifies to $ b(v) = \frac{1}{2} v $.
As the number of bidders $ n $ increases, the bid shading factor $ \frac{n-1}{n} $ approaches 1, meaning bids converge to the true valuation $ v $ and shading diminishes due to heightened competition. Consequently, the seller's expected revenue, given by $ \frac{n-1}{n+1} $, also approaches the expected value of the highest valuation, which is $ \frac{n}{n+1} $, converging to 1 as $ n \to \infty $. This scalability highlights how first-price auctions become more efficient in large markets, with revenue nearing the upper bound set by the valuation distribution.
When bidder types differ, such as having heterogeneous valuation distributions, the equilibrium becomes asymmetric, with no closed-form solution in general and requiring numerical methods for computation. Existence of such equilibria is established under regularity conditions on the distributions, though the symmetric case remains the primary focus for analytical tractability.

Variants

Incentive-Compatible Variant

The incentive-compatible variant of the first-price sealed-bid auction is constructed via the revelation principle as a direct revelation mechanism, in which bidders simultaneously report their private values, the good is allocated to the bidder with the highest reported value, and payments are determined to ensure that truthful reporting constitutes a Bayesian Nash equilibrium equivalent to the strategic equilibrium of the standard first-price auction. This approach transforms the indirect mechanismwhere strategic shading occursinto a Bayesian incentive-compatible (BIC) direct mechanism that achieves the same expected allocation and revenue outcomes.
In the symmetric independent private values model, where values are drawn i.i.d. from a known distribution FFF on with positive density, the mechanism allocates the good efficiently to the highest reported value and sets the winner's payment equal to the equilibrium bid function $ \beta(v_i) $, where $ \beta(v) $ is the symmetric equilibrium bidding strategy of the standard first-price auction. For the common case of uniform distribution on with nnn bidders, the equilibrium bid in the standard first-price auction is $ \beta(v) = \frac{n-1}{n} v $, so the direct mechanism charges the winner $ p = \frac{n-1}{n} v_w $, where $ v_w $ is the winner's reported value; this ensures truthful reporting maximizes expected utility in equilibrium.
This variant preserves the efficiency of the standard first-price auction, as the good always goes to the highest-value bidder, while achieving Bayesian incentive compatibility, meaning no bidder benefits from misreporting their value assuming others report truthfully. By revenue equivalence, it yields the same expected seller revenue as the second-price auction (which is dominant-strategy incentive compatible), but the payment depends solely on the winner's own reported value, retaining the first-price intuition of self-determined pricing without reliance on others' bids.

Common Value Model

In the common value model of first-price sealed-bid auctions, the item's value VVV is identical for all bidders but unknown at the time of bidding, drawn once from some prior distribution. Each bidder iii receives a private signal xix_ixi about VVV, typically modeled as xiN(V,2)x_i \sim N(V, \sigma^2)xiN(V,2) independently given VVV, where the signals are noisy estimates centered around the true value. Bidders form bids based on their conditional expectation of VVV given their signal, but the equilibrium strategy accounts for the strategic implications of winning. This setup contrasts with the independent private values model, where each bidder's value is idiosyncratic and independent of others.
A key feature of this model is the winner's curse, where the winner tends to overvalue the item due to selection bias: winning occurs when a bidder's signal is the highest among competitors, implying that the true VVV is likely lower than suggested by the winner's signal alone. If bidders naively bid their unconditional expectation EEE, they risk negative utility, as high signals increase the chance of winning but also indicate overestimation relative to the actual value. To mitigate this, equilibrium bids adjust downward more aggressively than in private value settings, shading bids to reflect the adverse selection of winning. This curse was first highlighted in the context of high-risk bidding, where overbidding leads to systematic losses for winners.
In the symmetric equilibrium with risk-neutral bidders, the bidding function is b(x)=Eb(x) = Eb(x)=E, where "win" conditions on xxx being the highest signal, ensuring zero expected profit conditional on winning. This adjustment is stricter than simply bidding EEE, as the winning condition incorporates information from rivals' likely lower signals. For illustration, consider mineral rights auctions for oil leases, where VVV represents the recoverable oil value, and signals come from private seismic tests; here, aggressive bidding based on optimistic signals has historically led to the winner's curse, with firms realizing lower-than-expected returns post-auction.

Comparisons

With Second-Price Sealed-Bid Auction

In the second-price sealed-bid auction, bidding one's true valuation constitutes a weakly dominant strategy for each participant, as any deviationbidding higher risks overpaying without increasing the chance of winning, while bidding lower risks losing when one would have profitedcannot improve expected utility. By contrast, the first-price sealed-bid auction lacks such a dominant strategy, compelling bidders to engage in bid shading by submitting offers below their true valuations to optimize the tradeoff between winning probability and profit conditional on winning; in symmetric equilibrium with values drawn from the uniform distribution on , the optimal bid for a bidder with valuation vvv among nnn participants is b(v)=n1nvb(v) = \frac{n-1}{n} vb(v)=nn1v, reflecting this strategic restraint.
Despite these contrasting strategic demands, both auction formats generate identical expected revenue for the seller in the symmetric independent private values (IPV) model assuming risk-neutral bidders and efficient allocation to the highest valuation. This revenue equivalence theorem holds because the expected second-order statistic (the second-highest valuation) determines seller revenue in equilibrium for both mechanisms. A proof sketch via the envelope theorem demonstrates this by showing that each bidder's expected payment equals their valuation minus the integral of their interim allocation probability over the type space; since both auctions yield the same allocation probabilities in symmetric Nash equilibrium (the good goes to the highest type), paymentsand thus revenuescoincide.
From the bidders' perspective, the first-price format incentivizes aggressive shading adjustments based on beliefs about rivals' values, which can advantage those with superior information or lower risk tolerance by allowing less conservative bids, but it also amplifies payoff variance due to the direct link between one's bid and payment upon winning. The second-price format mitigates this risk through truthful bidding, decoupling the winner's payment from their own strategic choice and stabilizing expected utilities across types.

With English Auction

The first-price sealed-bid auction contrasts sharply with the English auction in its procedural format. In the first-price sealed-bid auction, participants submit their bids simultaneously and confidentially, with the highest bidder winning and paying their own bid amount, and no information about competitors' actions is disclosed until the bids are opened. In the English auction, bidding occurs openly and orally in an ascending manner: the auctioneer begins with a low reserve price and incrementally raises it, allowing all participants to observe ongoing bids and the dropout signals of rivals as the price climbs. This open process enables real-time adjustments based on visible competitor behavior, unlike the static, information-isolated nature of the sealed-bid format.
Strategically, the English auction promotes near-truthful bidding through a dominant strategy equilibrium in independent private values settings, where each bidder remains active until the price reaches their true valuation and then drops out, maximizing expected payoff without shading. By contrast, the first-price sealed-bid auction lacks such a dominant strategy, requiring bidders to strategically underbid (shade) their true values to optimize the trade-off between winning probability and surplus, all without access to dropout signals or interim information from others. This shading in first-price auctions introduces greater uncertainty and risk aversion in bidding compared to the iterative, informative progression in the English format.
In terms of outcomes, the English auction's progressive information revelationthrough observable dropoutsenhances efficiency and seller revenue, particularly in environments with affiliated or common values, where it links private signals to reduce the winner's curse and yields higher expected prices via the linkage principle. The first-price sealed-bid auction, while simpler to administer due to its one-shot, non-interactive structure, offers no such dynamic revelation, potentially exacerbating information asymmetries and the winner's curse in common value contexts. Additionally, the sealed-bid mechanism's opacity makes it less prone to collusion in practice than the English auction, as open ascending formats facilitate signaling and tacit coordination among bidders through observable actions.
