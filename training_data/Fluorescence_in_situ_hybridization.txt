Fluorescence _in situ_ hybridization
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) is a cytogenetic laboratory technique that uses fluorescently labeled DNA or RNA probes to detect and localize specific nucleic acid sequences on chromosomes or within cells, allowing visualization of genetic material in its native context through fluorescence microscopy. The process involves denaturing the target DNA to make it single-stranded, hybridizing it with complementary probes tagged with fluorescent dyes, and then observing the bound probes under a microscope to identify chromosomal locations or abnormalities.
The technique originated in the 1960s when Joseph Gall and Mary Lou Pardue developed in situ hybridization using radioactive probes to visualize ribosomal RNA genes in cells, marking the first method to localize specific nucleic acids within intact chromosomes. By the early 1980s, advancements replaced radioactive labels with non-isotopic fluorescent tags, such as biotin or digoxigenin, improving safety, stability, and resolution while enabling multicolor detection for simultaneous analysis of multiple targets. This evolution transformed FISH into a cornerstone of molecular cytogenetics, contributing to projects like the Human Genome Project for precise gene mapping.
FISH has diverse applications in research and clinical settings, including the detection of chromosomal abnormalities such as aneuploidy in prenatal diagnostics (e.g., trisomy 21 in Down syndrome), gene deletions or duplications in hereditary diseases like Charcot-Marie-Tooth disorder, and translocations in cancers such as chronic myelogenous leukemia. Probes can be locus-specific for targeting individual genes, alphoid for centromeric regions to assess chromosome number, or whole-chromosome paints for creating spectral karyotypes that highlight structural rearrangements across the genome. In oncology, it aids prognosis by enumerating cancer-related gene copies, such as HER2 amplification in breast cancer, guiding targeted therapies.
Despite its strengths in providing high-specificity, morphology-preserving visualization of genetic changesoften superior to traditional karyotypingFISH has limitations, including lower resolution for low-copy-number sequences and the need for skilled interpretation to avoid artifacts. Recent innovations, like multicolor FISH, have expanded its throughput, though it complements rather than replaces emerging technologies such as next-generation sequencing in comprehensive genomic analysis.

Introduction

Definition and principles

Fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) is a molecular cytogenetic technique that employs fluorescently labeled nucleic acid probes to detect and visualize specific DNA or RNA sequences directly within cells or on chromosomes, preserving their spatial context. This method allows for the localization of genetic material at the single-cell level, enabling the identification of chromosomal abnormalities, gene mappings, and expression patterns without extracting the nucleic acids from their native environment.
The core principles of FISH rely on the denaturation of target nucleic acids to expose single-stranded regions, followed by the hybridization of complementary probes and subsequent fluorescence-based detection. Denaturation typically involves heat or chemical agents to disrupt the hydrogen bonds in the double helix of target DNA or RNA, rendering it accessible for probe binding. Hybridization occurs when the single-stranded probe anneals to its complementary sequence on the target through specific base pairing (adenine-thymine and guanine-cytosine), ensuring high selectivity for the intended locus. Detection is achieved via fluorescence microscopy, where the emitted light from the bound probes reveals the location and intensity of the target sequences, often after washing away unbound probes to reduce background noise.
Key components of FISH include fluorophores attached to the probes for signal generation, such as fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC) for green emission or rhodamine for red, which excite under specific wavelengths and allow multiplexing with distinct colors. Probes vary in type but commonly include short oligonucleotides, typically 20-50 nucleotides in length, designed for precise targeting of unique sequences. To enhance weak signals, especially in low-abundance targets, amplification techniques like tyramide signal amplification (TSA) are employed, where enzymatic deposition of tyramide-conjugated fluorophores multiplies the detectable signal.
Unlike polymerase chain reaction (PCR)-based methods, which amplify nucleic acids in solution for quantitative analysis but lose spatial information, FISH maintains the in situ (in place) architecture of chromosomes or cellular structures, providing direct visualization of sequence positions.

History and development

The origins of fluorescence *in situ* hybridization (FISH) trace back to the development of non-fluorescent in situ hybridization (ISH) techniques in the 1960s and 1970s, which relied on radioisotope-labeled probes to localize nucleic acids within cells. The pioneering work by Joseph G. Gall and Mary Lou Pardue in 1969 demonstrated the first successful ISH experiment, using tritium-labeled ribosomal RNA (rRNA) to detect complementary DNA sequences in the amplified nucleoli of Xenopus laevis oocytes, establishing the feasibility of hybridizing nucleic acids directly on cellular structures. This radioautographic approach overcame limitations of earlier biochemical methods by preserving spatial context, though it suffered from issues like long exposure times, low resolution, and radiation hazards. Subsequent refinements in the 1980s, including the use of synthetic oligonucleotide probes, improved sensitivity and specificity, laying the groundwork for fluorescent adaptations.
The transition to fluorescence-based detection began in the early 1980s, marking the birth of FISH as a safer, more versatile technique. In 1980, the first direct fluorescent labeling was achieved by hybridizing RNA probes end-labeled with a fluorophore to detect cellular mRNA in tissue sections, enabling visual detection without isotopes. A key innovation came in 1981 with the enzymatic synthesis of biotinylated DNA probes by Pennina R. Langer and colleagues, which allowed indirect detection via fluorescently conjugated avidin, amplifying signals and facilitating multiplexing. This hapten-labeling strategy was pivotal for chromosomal applications, culminating in 1986 when Daniel Pinkel and team reported the first high-sensitivity FISH for cytogenetic analysis, using chromosome-specific DNA libraries to paint entire human chromosomes in metaphase spreads and interphase nuclei. These advancements rapidly expanded FISH's utility in gene mapping and aberration detection.
By the 1990s, FISH evolved through combinatorial labeling schemes that enabled multicolor detection, allowing simultaneous visualization of multiple targets. Early two-color FISH emerged around 1989, but the decade saw the development of spectral karyotyping (SKY) and multiplex FISH (M-FISH) using 5-7 fluorophores in unique combinations to label all 24 human chromosomes distinctly. Commercialization accelerated adoption, with companies like Vysis (acquired by Abbott Molecular in 2001) introducing standardized probe kits and automation tools in the mid-1990s, making FISH accessible for clinical diagnostics. The completion of the Human Genome Project in 2003 further transformed probe design, providing bacterial artificial chromosome (BAC) libraries that yielded locus-specific probes with high sequence fidelity, enhancing resolution for genomic studies.
In the 2010s, FISH advanced toward single-molecule resolution with the refinement of single-molecule FISH (smFISH), which uses densely tiled oligonucleotide probes to detect and quantify individual RNA transcripts in situ. Initially demonstrated in 1998 for mRNA counting, smFISH gained prominence post-2010 through optimized protocols that achieved subcellular localization and absolute quantification, revolutionizing transcriptomics without amplification biases. This shift built on earlier FISH foundations, emphasizing higher throughput and integration with imaging technologies for dynamic cellular insights.

Probes

RNA probes

RNA probes are essential tools in fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) for visualizing specific RNA transcripts within cells or tissues, offering direct detection of gene expression at the subcellular level. These probes are designed to be complementary to target RNA sequences, forming stable hybrids that can be labeled for fluorescent detection. The two primary types of RNA probes used in FISH are riboprobes, which are longer RNA molecules transcribed in vitro from DNA templates, and antisense oligonucleotides, which are shorter synthetic sequences targeted to mRNA. Riboprobes provide robust signal amplification due to their length, while antisense oligonucleotides enable precise targeting of specific transcript regions.
Riboprobes are generated through in vitro transcription of a DNA template cloned into a vector containing a promoter, such as the T7 bacteriophage promoter, which directs synthesis by T7 RNA polymerase to produce single-stranded antisense RNA complementary to the target. The DNA template is typically linearized upstream of the insert to ensure full-length transcription, and labeled nucleotideseither fluorophore-conjugated or hapten-modified (e.g., digoxigenin or biotin)are incorporated directly during the reaction for subsequent detection. These probes generally range from 200 to 1000 nucleotides in length, balancing specificity, hybrid stability, and tissue penetration while minimizing non-specific binding. This preparation method, pioneered in the 1980s for efficient RNA probe synthesis, allows for high-yield production suitable for sensitive RNA detection in FISH.
Antisense oligonucleotides for mRNA targeting in RNA FISH are chemically synthesized as short, single-stranded DNA or RNA sequences (typically 20-50 nucleotides) designed to hybridize specifically to target transcripts, often as a pool of 20-48 probes covering multiple regions for enhanced signal-to-noise ratio. These probes can be directly conjugated to fluorophores at their 5' or 3' ends or modified with amines for post-synthesis labeling, enabling multiplex detection without the need for enzymatic amplification.
A key advantage of RNA probes in FISH is their high specificity for detecting mature RNA transcripts, as they target exon-only sequences in spliced mRNA, avoiding hybridization to intronic regions present in genomic DNA or nascent pre-mRNA. This intronless targeting reduces cross-reactivity compared to DNA probes, which may bind genomic loci, and RNA-RNA hybrids exhibit greater thermal stability due to stronger base-pairing interactions. However, RNA probes are highly susceptible to degradation by ubiquitous RNases, necessitating rigorous RNase-free protocols, including the use of diethyl pyrocarbonate (DEPC)-treated solutions to inactivate contaminating nucleases during preparation and hybridization.
RNA probes have proven particularly valuable for detecting non-coding RNAs, such as long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs), enabling visualization of their nuclear or cytoplasmic localization and interactions in spatial contexts. For instance, RNA FISH with riboprobes or oligonucleotide sets has been used to map lncRNA expression patterns in cancer cells, revealing roles in chromatin regulation. Optimization strategies for these probes, including probe design and hybridization conditions for spatial transcriptomics, are detailed in a comprehensive 2020 technical guide that emphasizes multiplexing and signal enhancement for single-cell resolution.

DNA probes

DNA probes in fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) are double-stranded or denatured nucleic acid sequences derived from genomic DNA, designed to hybridize to specific chromosomal targets such as genes, loci, or repetitive regions. Unlike RNA probes, which are typically generated through in vitro transcription to target single-stranded RNA transcripts, DNA probes are produced from cloned or synthetic DNA fragments to bind complementary double-stranded genomic sequences in fixed cells or tissues. These probes enable precise visualization of chromosomal abnormalities, gene amplifications, or structural variations when labeled with fluorescent or hapten moieties.
Common types of DNA probes include short oligonucleotide probes, which are synthetic single-stranded DNA sequences of 20-50 nucleotides tailored for high specificity to unique genomic loci, and larger-insert clones such as bacterial artificial chromosomes (BACs) and P1-derived artificial chromosomes (PACs) that contain 100-200 kb genomic fragments for broader locus coverage. Fosmids, similar to BACs but with ~40 kb inserts, are particularly useful for mapping repetitive regions like centromeres and telomeres due to their stability in bacterial hosts and ability to span complex heterochromatic sequences. Many BAC and PAC probes originate from libraries constructed during the Human Genome Project, providing a comprehensive resource for human chromosome mapping and FISH applications.
Preparation of DNA probes begins with amplification or isolation of the target sequence, often via polymerase chain reaction (PCR) for oligonucleotides or bacterial cloning in vectors like BACs, PACs, and fosmids for large inserts. Labeling incorporates fluorescent dyes or haptens during or after synthesis; direct methods attach fluorophores chemically to nucleotides or enzymatically via PCR, while indirect methods use modified nucleotides like biotin or digoxigenin, which are later detected with fluorophore-conjugated antibodies or avidin. Common enzymatic labeling techniques include nick translation, which uses DNase I and DNA polymerase I to generate 200-500 bp fragments with incorporated labeled dNTPs, and random priming, which employs random hexamer primers for exponential amplification and labeling of single-stranded templates. These processes yield probes suitable for hybridization, typically producing fragmented DNA at concentrations of 10-50 ng/L for optimal signal intensity.
To enhance specificity and minimize cross-hybridization, especially in genomes rich in repetitive elements, probe sequences are often repeat-masked during design to exclude highly conserved repeats like Alu or LINE elements, ensuring targeting of unique genomic regions. Computational tools such as BLAST are employed to verify probe uniqueness by aligning candidate sequences against the reference genome, identifying potential off-target sites and refining designs for single-locus binding. This approach is critical for locus-specific probes, reducing background noise and improving resolution in FISH assays.

Procedure

Sample preparation

Sample preparation is a critical initial step in fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) that involves fixing and processing biological specimens to preserve structural integrity while allowing access to target nucleic acids for probe binding. Common sample types include fixed cells, tissue sections, metaphase chromosomes, and interphase nuclei, often derived from sources such as peripheral blood lymphocytes, tumor biopsies, or paraffin-embedded tissues. For instance, blood smears from lymphocytes provide metaphase chromosomes or interphase nuclei, while formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded (FFPE) biopsies are frequently used for clinical diagnostics on archival human tissues. These preparations ensure that the cellular architecture remains intact, enabling subsequent visualization of chromosomal abnormalities or gene locations.
Fixation methods are selected based on the need to balance morphological preservation with probe permeability. Paraformaldehyde (PFA), typically at 4% in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), is widely used for fixing cells and tissues to maintain three-dimensional nuclear structure, with incubation times ranging from 10 minutes for cultured cells to overnight for thicker samples like mouse brain sections at 4C. Methanol or acetone fixation, often applied for 10 minutes, enhances membrane permeability in adherent cells, while a 3:1 mixture of methanol and glacial acetic acid serves as a standard fixative for chromosomes to rapidly halt cellular processes. For FFPE tissues, 10% neutral buffered formalin is employed to cross-link proteins, though this requires subsequent deparaffinization with xylene and rehydration through graded ethanol series to restore accessibility. Pretreatments further optimize target exposure; for example, digestion with pepsin (1 mg/mL) or proteinase K (10 g/mL) for 220 minutes at 37C removes proteins obstructing DNA, and brief exposure to 0.2 N HCl (2050 minutes) demasks nucleic acids in fixed samples.
Chromosome spreading is essential for metaphase FISH, particularly in cytogenetic studies of animal and plant cells. In animal cells, such as human lymphocytes, colchicine (0.06 mg/mL) arrests cells in metaphase for 4 hours, followed by hypotonic swelling in 0.075 M KCl for 710 minutes to disperse chromosomes, and fixation with three changes of 3:1 methanol:acetic acid before dropping onto slides at a 45 angle for even spreading. Plant cells, like mitotic root tips, undergo similar arrest but are squashed in 45% acetic acid on coverslips and frozen at -80C for storage, accommodating tougher cell walls. These steps yield well-separated chromosomes suitable for high-resolution analysis. Fixed samples prepared this way are compatible with both DNA and RNA probes, though RNA targets require gentler conditions to avoid degradation.
Unique considerations include enzymatic treatments to minimize interference and proper storage to maintain sample viability. For DNA-targeted FISH, RNase A treatment (100 g/mL at 37C for 1 hour) eliminates endogenous RNA that could cause nonspecific binding, a step unnecessary for RNA FISH protocols. Prepared slides or cells are typically stored in 70% ethanol at -20C for up to 2 years or at -80C for longer periods, preventing degradation while preserving antigenicity and nucleic acid integrity for delayed hybridization.

Hybridization and detection

The hybridization phase of fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) begins with denaturation of the target DNA in the prepared sample, typically achieved by heating slides in a solution of 70% formamide and 2 SSC (saline-sodium citrate) buffer at 6885C for 27 minutes to separate double-stranded DNA into single strands, enabling probe access. This step is critical for exposing complementary sequences without excessively damaging cellular morphology. Following dehydration, the denatured probe (often at 2050 g/mL concentration) is applied in a hybridization buffer containing 50% formamide, 2 SSC, 10% dextran sulfate, and blocking agents like tRNA or salmon sperm DNA to prevent non-specific binding. Annealing then occurs by incubating the sample at 3742C overnight (typically 1216 hours) in a humidified chamber, allowing specific hybridization between the probe and target sequences under controlled stringency. Stringency is primarily regulated by the formamide concentration, temperature, and salt levels in the buffer, which destabilize mismatched hybrids to minimize background noise from non-specific probe attachment.
Post-hybridization, unbound and weakly bound probes are removed through a series of washes to enhance signal specificity. Standard washing involves multiple rinses in 2 SSC at room temperature for 20 minutes each (four times), followed by 4 SSC for 10 minutes (twice), often with the addition of 0.10.5% Tween-20 or Triton X-100 detergent to further reduce non-specific adhesion. These steps are performed at temperatures matching or slightly above the hybridization phase to maintain stringency, with salt concentration reductions progressively stripping off mismatched probes. Counterstaining with DAPI (4',6-diamidino-2-phenylindole) at 1 g/mL is then applied for 510 minutes to visualize cell nuclei, followed by a final rinse in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS). Probe preparation, such as labeling efficiency, can briefly influence hybridization yield by affecting probe stability in the buffer, but optimal conditions ensure high specificity.
Detection relies on the fluorescent labels incorporated into the probes, visualized using epifluorescence or confocal microscopy equipped with appropriate filters for the fluorophores (e.g., FITC, Texas Red, or Cy3/Cy5 for multi-color setups). For indirect labeling, where probes are tagged with haptens like biotin or digoxigenin, detection involves sequential incubation with primary antibodies (e.g., anti-biotin at 1:100 dilution for 5 minutes at 37C) followed by fluorochrome-conjugated secondary antibodies (e.g., FITC-goat anti-rabbit for 3060 minutes at 37C), amplifying the signal through layered binding. For low-abundance targets, tyramide signal amplification (TSA) is employed, where horseradish peroxidase-conjugated probes catalyze tyramide deposition, yielding up to 1000-fold signal enhancement for sensitive detection in fixed tissues. Confocal microscopy is particularly useful for three-dimensional imaging in interphase nuclei, providing optical sectioning to resolve spatial relationships. Slides are mounted in an antifade medium containing propidium iodide or similar to preserve fluorescence during observation.
Quantitatively, FISH signal intensity is generally proportional to the number of target DNA copies, allowing estimation of gene amplification or deletion events through spot counting or intensity measurement under standardized imaging conditions. Resolution is limited by optical diffraction and chromatin compaction, achieving approximately 50 kb in metaphase chromosome spreads for detecting adjacent loci, while fiber FISH extends this to 15 kb by stretching DNA for linear mapping. These limits underscore FISH's utility for structural variant detection without requiring cell culturing.

Variations

Single-molecule and multiplex techniques

Single-molecule RNA fluorescence in situ hybridization (smFISH) enables the visualization and absolute quantification of individual mRNA transcripts within fixed cells by using sets of approximately 48 short, singly labeled oligonucleotide probes that hybridize to specific target sequences, producing bright, diffraction-limited spots corresponding to single molecules. This stochastic labeling approach avoids the need for amplification while achieving high signal-to-noise ratios, allowing researchers to count mRNA copy numbers per cell and map their subcellular localization, such as in the cytoplasm or nucleus. smFISH has become a cornerstone for studying gene expression heterogeneity in single cells, revealing bursty transcription dynamics and cell-to-cell variability that bulk methods obscure.
Multiplexing in FISH extends smFISH to simultaneously detect hundreds or thousands of RNA species, addressing limitations in traditional assays that target only a few genes. One seminal method, multiplexed error-robust FISH (MERFISH), introduced in 2015, employs combinatorial barcoding with error-correcting Hamming distance-1 codes, where each RNA is assigned a unique binary barcode read out through sequential hybridization rounds using 4-16 fluorescent colors, enabling reliable identification of up to 140 genes in early implementations and scaling to thousands with optimizations. This approach minimizes decoding errors from incomplete hybridization or background noise, preserving single-molecule resolution in intact tissues.
Another key multiplex technique, sequential FISH (seqFISH), developed in 2014, uses iterative rounds of hybridization with orthogonal probe sets, each tagged with a distinct fluorophore, to barcode up to 32 mRNAs initially and later expanded to over 1,000 through combinatorial addressing without color overlap. By clearing fluorophores between rounds, seqFISH achieves high multiplexing while maintaining spatial information, facilitating the mapping of gene expression gradients in cell populations. These methods leverage combinatorial probe designs that theoretically support 2^n unique identifiers from n hybridization rounds, integrating seamlessly with spatial transcriptomics to profile RNA distributions across tissue architectures.
To support analysis of smFISH and multiplex data, STARFISH software, released in 2019 by the Chan Zuckerberg Initiative, provides scalable pipelines for image processing, including spot detection, decoding of barcodes, and cell segmentation, automating workflows for high-throughput quantification and addressing challenges in single-cell resolution from large datasets.

Fiber, quantitative, and flow-based methods

Fiber-FISH, or fluorescence in situ hybridization on extended chromatin fibers, enables high-resolution mapping of genomic regions by stretching decondensed DNA onto slides, achieving resolutions of 1-10 kb for ordering bacterial artificial chromosome (BAC) clones over megabase-scale distances. This method involves preparing extended DNA fibers from lysed cells, hybridizing fluorescent probes to specific sequences, and visualizing signals under microscopy to determine their linear order and spacing, which is particularly useful for validating genome assemblies and identifying structural variations.
Quantitative FISH (Q-FISH) employs peptide nucleic acid (PNA) probes to measure telomere length by correlating fluorescence intensity with the number of telomeric repeats, such as (TTAGGG)_n in humans. Developed for metaphase chromosomes or interphase nuclei, Q-FISH uses calibration curves derived from known telomere lengths to convert intensity units to absolute lengths in kilobases, enabling precise assessment of telomere shortening in aging and disease contexts. For example, applications in aging research have shown progressive telomere attrition in somatic cells, linking length dynamics to cellular senescence.
Flow-FISH integrates FISH with flow cytometry to quantify average telomere lengths in uncultured cell populations, utilizing BODIPY- or FITC-labeled PNA probes that hybridize to telomeric repeats in permeabilized cells. This high-throughput approach analyzes thousands of cells per second, providing subpopulation-specific measurements via simultaneous surface marker staining, which has revealed telomere dynamics in hematopoietic lineages during early childhood. Unlike static imaging, Flow-FISH offers statistical robustness for heterogeneous samples, supporting studies on telomere maintenance in clinical settings.

Specialized hybrid and microbial applications

Fusion FISH, also known as dual-fusion FISH, employs two differently colored probes targeting the BCR and ABL1 genes to detect chromosomal translocations such as the t(9;22)(q34;q11) resulting in the BCR-ABL1 fusion characteristic of chronic myeloid leukemia (CML). In this technique, colocalization of the green (BCR) and red (ABL1) signals produces a fused yellow signal on the derivative chromosome 22 (Philadelphia chromosome), while split signals on chromosomes 9 and 22 indicate the translocation breakpoints, enabling precise mapping of fusion points even in variant rearrangements. This method's sensitivity allows detection of fusions in up to 95% of CML cases, outperforming conventional karyotyping for cryptic insertions, and has been validated in interphase nuclei without requiring metaphase spreads. For instance, tri-color dual-fusion FISH incorporates an additional probe for the ASS1 gene on chromosome 9 to distinguish true BCR-ABL1 fusions from variant translocations involving a third chromosome, reducing false positives in complex cases.
Microautoradiography combined with FISH (MAR-FISH) integrates radiolabeled substrate uptake to identify metabolically active microbial cells within environmental samples, addressing limitations of standard FISH that cannot distinguish viable from dormant bacteria. In MAR-FISH, microbial communities are incubated with radioactively labeled nutrients (e.g., H-leucine for protein synthesis or C-acetate for carbon assimilation), followed by emulsion coating to detect silver grain accumulation over active cells; subsequent FISH with 16S rRNA-targeted oligonucleotide probes then assigns phylogenetic identity to these silver grain-positive cells using fluorophores. This ecophysiological approach has revealed, for example, that only a subset of Alphaproteobacteria in wastewater biofilms actively degrade acetate under low-oxygen conditions, providing insights into microbial function in situ. The technique's resolution at the single-cell level has been enhanced in protocols optimizing fixation and hybridization to minimize signal quenching, achieving detection limits for activity as low as 10 mol cell h.
Comparative genomic hybridization (CGH) adapted for FISH enables genome-wide screening of DNA copy number variations (CNVs) by competitively hybridizing differentially labeled test and reference DNA to normal metaphase chromosomes. In this variant, test DNA is labeled with a green fluorophore (e.g., FITC) and reference DNA with red (e.g., Texas Red), and the green-to-red signal ratio along chromosomes quantifies gains (ratio >1.0) or losses (ratio <0.5), with thresholds calibrated against known aneuploidies. Originally developed for solid tumors, FISH-based CGH has identified amplifications in genes like MYC or EGFR in up to 30% of breast cancers, offering higher resolution than metaphase CGH for sub-megabase alterations when combined with locus-specific probes. Its application has been pivotal in prenatal diagnostics for constitutional CNVs, though it requires intact metaphases and has largely been supplanted by array CGH for higher throughput.
Emerging CRISPR-FISH techniques leverage catalytically inactive Cas9 (dCas9) fused to fluorescent proteins and guide RNAs (gRNAs) for programmable, sequence-specific labeling of genomic loci, overcoming FISH's reliance on fixed probes for non-repetitive targets. In CRISPR FISHer, a 2022 innovation, phase separation domains on dCas9 concentrate multiple gRNAs at target sites to amplify signals via recruited fluorescent antibodies, enabling visualization of single-copy genes like PPP1R2 in live mammalian cells with a signal-to-noise ratio exceeding 10:1. Post-2020 advancements, such as aptamer-enhanced gRNAs, have extended this to plant genomes, allowing multiplexed tracking of up to 10 loci simultaneously without denaturation, thus integrating genome editing tools for dynamic chromatin studies. These methods address FISH's limitations in live imaging, with applications in real-time visualization of locus-specific replication timing.

Applications

Medical diagnostics

Fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) plays a pivotal role in prenatal diagnostics by enabling rapid detection of common aneuploidies, such as trisomy 21 (Down syndrome), trisomy 18 (Edwards syndrome), and trisomy 13 (Patau syndrome), using locus-specific probes on uncultured amniocytes or chorionic villi samples. This approach provides results within 24-48 hours, significantly shortening the diagnostic timeline compared to traditional karyotyping, which requires cell culture and can take up to two weeks. In a study of 5,049 consecutive uncultured amniocyte samples, FISH detected all 70 cases of trisomy 21 with 100% sensitivity and no false positives, confirming its reliability for targeted aneuploidy screening. FISH is routinely employed in prenatal laboratories for its high sensitivity (>95%) in detecting numerical chromosomal abnormalities at targeted loci, facilitating timely clinical decisions.
In cancer cytogenetics, FISH is essential for identifying gene amplifications and structural rearrangements that guide targeted therapies. For instance, locus-specific probes detect HER2 (ERBB2) gene amplification in breast cancer tissue, with FDA-approved kits such as HER2 FISH pharmDx, first approved in 2005, to assess eligibility for trastuzumab (Herceptin) treatment. These assays quantify HER2 copy number, where amplification (typically >6 copies per cell) correlates with aggressive disease and improved response to HER2-directed therapies. Spectral karyotyping (SKY), a multiplex FISH variant, visualizes all 24 human chromosomes in distinct colors to unravel complex chromosomal rearrangements in tumor cells, aiding in the classification of leukemias and solid tumors. SKY has proven particularly valuable for detecting marker chromosomes and homogeneously staining regions in cancer karyotypes that are ambiguous by conventional banding.
The integration of FISH with array comparative genomic hybridization (array-CGH) supports virtual karyotyping, a high-resolution method for identifying submicroscopic deletions and duplications not visible by standard cytogenetics. In cases of DiGeorge syndrome (22q11.2 deletion syndrome), array-CGH complements FISH by detecting copy number variations across the genome, revealing deletions in patients with negative FISH results but suggestive phenotypes. This combined approach enhances diagnostic yield for microdeletion syndromes, where FISH targets specific loci but array-CGH provides broader coverage, identifying abnormalities as small as 50-100 kb.
FISH also aids in species and pathogen identification within clinical samples, particularly for infectious diseases. Probes targeting ribosomal RNA sequences enable direct visualization of Mycobacterium species, such as M. tuberculosis, in infected tissue sections, offering a rapid alternative to culture-based methods for diagnosing tuberculosis in formalin-fixed biopsies. These assays achieve high specificity for differentiating tuberculous from nontuberculous mycobacteria, supporting prompt initiation of antimicrobial therapy. Fusion-FISH variants briefly reference gene fusion detection in oncology, such as BCR-ABL in chronic myeloid leukemia, to inform tyrosine kinase inhibitor use.

Research and evolutionary studies

Fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) has been instrumental in elucidating chromosome evolution by mapping conserved synteny blocks across species, allowing researchers to identify large-scale genomic rearrangements such as inversions and translocations that have accumulated over millions of years. For instance, FISH probes targeting Hox gene clusters have revealed highly conserved syntenic blocks surrounding these developmental genes in vertebrates, including humans and mice, demonstrating that despite evolutionary divergence, the genomic context of Hox loci remains largely intact, which underscores the functional constraints on these regions. Such analyses highlight how FISH facilitates the reconstruction of ancestral karyotypes and the timing of chromosomal changes, providing evidence for evolutionary mechanisms like segmental duplications that preserve synteny while permitting localized rearrangements.
In comparative genomics, Zoo-FISHusing whole-chromosome painting probes from one species on anotherhas been pivotal in tracing primate phylogeny and identifying key fusion events. This technique confirmed that human chromosome 2 resulted from the telomeric fusion of two ancestral acrocentric chromosomes present separately in great apes, a discovery supported by FISH mapping of pericentromeric regions that revealed vestigial telomeres and a neocentromere at the fusion site. Zoo-FISH studies across primates have delineated conserved chromosomal homologies and rearrangements, such as Robertsonian translocations, contributing to a detailed understanding of karyotype evolution within the order, with implications for reconstructing the last common ancestor of humans and other apes.
FISH serves as a key research tool in spatial genomics when integrated with single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq), enabling the visualization of gene expression patterns in their chromosomal context to study evolutionary dynamics at the cellular level. Techniques like sequential FISH (seqFISH) combine multiplexed probe hybridization with scRNA-seq data to profile hundreds of genes in situ, revealing spatial organization and co-expression modules that inform how genomic architecture influences evolutionary adaptation. This integration addresses challenges in linking transcriptomic variation to chromosomal evolution.
Early applications of FISH in the 1990s provided foundational evidence for human-chimpanzee divergence by confirming interchromosomal rearrangements through comparative chromosome painting, which identified only a few key differences in karyotype despite high sequence similarity. More recently, post-2020 studies have applied FISH to investigate polyploid plant evolution, using genomic in situ hybridization to distinguish parental genomes in hybrids and track chromosome sorting during diploidization, as seen in analyses of allotetraploid formation in crops like wheat, where it reveals biased gene retention and structural variations driving speciation.
