Heel
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The heel is the rounded prominence at the posterior end of the human foot, formed by the projection of the calcaneus, the largest of the seven tarsal bones, which lies below the ankle and behind the foot's arch. This structure serves as the primary point of contact with the ground during the initial phase of walking or running, known as heel strike.
Anatomically, the heel comprises the calcaneus bone, a thick subcutaneous fat pad for shock absorption, the Achilles tendon (which connects the calf muscles to the posterior calcaneus), and the plantar fascia (a dense fibrous band extending from the heel to the toes). The calcaneus articulates superiorly with the talus bone to form the subtalar joint, enabling inversion and eversion of the foot, while its inferior surface supports the longitudinal arch via ligamentous attachments.
Functionally, the heel bears a significant portion of body weight during standing and facilitates propulsion by acting as a short lever for the gastrocnemius and soleus muscles during push-off. It absorbs and dissipates ground reaction forces to protect the musculoskeletal system, with the fat pad and plantar fascia playing key roles in cushioning and maintaining foot stability. In evolutionary terms, the heel's development supported bipedalism by enhancing balance and energy efficiency in locomotion.
The heel is prone to various pathologies due to its load-bearing role, including plantar fasciitis (inflammation of the plantar fascia causing stabbing heel pain), Achilles tendinitis (overuse injury to the tendon leading to swelling and stiffness), and calcaneal spurs (bony outgrowths often associated with chronic strain). These conditions affect millions annually, often resulting from repetitive stress, poor footwear, or biomechanical imbalances, and may require conservative treatments like rest, orthotics, or physical therapy.

Anatomy

Human Heel Structure

The calcaneus, commonly referred to as the heel bone, is the largest of the seven tarsal bones in the human foot and forms the prominent posterior projection of the heel. It has a roughly rectangular prism shape, with a broader posterior aspect that tapers anteriorly, and is positioned inferior to the talus and posterior to the midfoot bones. In adults, the average anteroposterior length of the calcaneus measures approximately 7.5 cm, though this can vary slightly by sex and population. The bone articulates superiorly with the inferior surface of the talus to form the subtalar joint, which allows for inversion and eversion of the foot, and anteriorly with the cuboid bone via the calcaneocuboid joint. Laterally, it features a prominent peroneal tubercle for tendon attachments, while medially, it has a sustentaculum tali that supports the talus and serves as an attachment for ligaments.
The soft tissue layers overlying the calcaneus provide essential cushioning and structural support. The subcutaneous fat pad, a thick layer of adipose tissue directly beneath the skin, covers the plantar surface of the heel and averages about 1.4 cm in thickness in healthy adults, ranging from 1.0 to 1.7 cm. This fat pad is compartmentalized by fibrous septa, enhancing its shock-absorbing properties. The plantar fascia, a dense band of connective tissue, originates from the medial calcaneal tuberosity on the plantar aspect of the calcaneus, extending distally to support the longitudinal arch of the foot. Posteriorly, the Achilles tendon inserts onto the roughened posterior surface of the calcaneus at the calcaneal tuberosity, anchoring the gastrocnemius and soleus muscles.
Vascular supply to the heel region, including the calcaneus and surrounding soft tissues, primarily arises from branches of the posterior tibial artery, such as the calcaneal branches, which form an anastomotic network around the bone. Innervation of the heel is provided mainly by the tibial nerve, which supplies sensory fibers to the skin and motor branches to the intrinsic foot muscles via its medial and lateral plantar divisions, with additional lateral sensory input from the sural nerve.

Comparative Anatomy in Animals

The heel, anatomically centered on the calcaneus bone, exhibits diverse structures across animal taxa, adapting to varied locomotor demands and environmental pressures. In mammals, stance typesplantigrade, digitigrade, and unguligradeprofoundly influence heel morphology, with the calcaneus serving as a key lever for weight distribution and propulsion.
Digitigrade species, such as dogs, feature an elevated heel where only the digits contact the ground, supported by a large and elongated calcaneus that anchors the common calcaneal tendon. This structure contrasts with the plantigrade human heel, where the calcaneus directly contacts the substrate for stability. In unguligrade mammals like horses, the heel is markedly raised, with the calcaneus forming part of the hock joint and integrated into the hoof mechanism; the equine calcaneus typically measures 12-15 cm in length, roughly double that of the human calcaneus at 7-9 cm.
Evolutionary adaptations further diversify heel anatomy in even-toed ungulates (artiodactyls), where tarsal bones including the calcaneus undergo fusion to form a rigid, elongated structure, as seen in deer, enhancing cursorial efficiency. Some reptiles lack a distinct heel, with the calcaneus reduced or fused into a less prominent tarsal complex, differing from the more defined mammalian form.
In birds, the heel region is characterized by a short tarsus, fused with metatarsals into the tarsometatarsus, which positions the ankle joint proximally for efficient perching and grasping. Cetaceans display extreme hindlimb modifications, with vestigial pelvic elements lacking a functional calcaneus or heel, while their forelimbs evolve into flippers without analogous heel structures.

Physiology and Function

Role in Human Locomotion and Support

The heel plays a pivotal role in the gait cycle, particularly during the heel strike phase, where it serves as the initial point of contact with the ground in typical human walking and running patterns. In long-distance running, such as jogging over extended straight distances, heel strike is often preferable due to its efficiency in minimizing metabolic cost and optimizing energy economy, leveraging the shock absorption and propulsion mechanisms of the heel. This phase marks the beginning of the stance period, lasting approximately 10% of the gait cycle, during which the heel absorbs the initial impact forces generated as the body weight transfers forward. These forces can reach up to 3-5 times body weight, depending on speed and surface, helping to decelerate the body's forward momentum while initiating support for the entire lower limb chain.
Shock absorption during heel strike is facilitated by specialized mechanisms within the heel structure. The subcutaneous fat pad beneath the calcaneus compresses upon impact, dissipating energy through viscoelastic deformation and attenuating up to 50-90% of the initial stress wave before it propagates proximally along the skeletal system. Complementing this, the Achilles tendon undergoes eccentric lengthening in the early stance phase, storing elastic strain energyestimated at 35-60% of the gastrocnemius-soleus muscle-tendon unit's mechanical workwhich is subsequently released during concentric contraction near toe-off to enhance propulsion efficiency. These mechanisms collectively reduce peak loading on joints and tissues, optimizing energy economy in locomotion.
The heel's posterior positioning relative to the body's center of mass contributes significantly to anterior-posterior balance and stability during gait. By defining the rear boundary of the base of supportspanning from the heel to the contralateral toesit allows the center of pressure to shift posteriorly at initial contact, countering forward angular momentum and maintaining equilibrium as the body vaults over the stance foot. This configuration supports controlled progression, with deviations in heel placement potentially increasing fall risk by altering stability margins.
Biomechanically, ground reaction forces (GRFs) at heel contact exhibit distinct vector characteristics that underpin locomotion dynamics. The vertical GRF component peaks abruptly (the "impact transient") shortly after contact, while the anterior-posterior vector initially directs posteriorly as a braking force (negative shear, up to 0.2-0.3 times body weight), resisting forward slip and facilitating weight acceptance before reversing to a propulsive direction in mid-stance. Vector analysis of these forces, often resolved in three dimensions, reveals how heel-ground interaction influences joint moments and overall gait efficiency, with heel strikers experiencing higher initial vertical peaks compared to forefoot patterns in running.

Functions in Non-Human Animals

In ungulates such as horses, the heel, formed by the calcaneus bone in the hock joint, serves as a critical lever arm that enhances propulsion during locomotion by providing mechanical advantage to the gastrocnemius and other hindlimb muscles, allowing for powerful extension of the foot and increased stride length. This elevated heel structure in unguligrade stance reduces ground contact time and optimizes energy transfer, enabling horses to achieve gallop speeds up to 60 km/h during sprints for escape or pursuit.
In birds, the heel region contributes to an energy-efficient perching mechanism through a passive tendon-locking system, where flexor tendons running along the back of the leg automatically engage when the bird bends its ankle upon landing, causing the toes to grip the perch without sustained muscular effort. This arrangement, involving the heel as part of the tendon's pathway, allows perching species like songbirds to rest or sleep securely on branches while conserving metabolic energy, as no active contraction is required to maintain the hold.
Aquatic adaptations in cetaceans, such as whales, have led to a drastic reduction in heel function due to the vestigial nature of their hind limbs, which include rudimentary elements like the tarsus and calcaneus buried within the body, no longer supporting locomotion on land or in water. Instead, propulsion relies on the powerful tail fluke, with the minimized heel and hindlimb structures contributing to a streamlined body form that minimizes drag during swimming, facilitating efficient movement through water at speeds up to 50 km/h in some species.
In certain mammals like the platypus, the heel area features specialized spurs on the hind legs that deliver venom for defensive purposes, with males using these keratinized structures connected to crural glands to inject painful toxins into predators or rivals during encounters. This mechanism deters threats without lethality in larger animals, as evidenced by human envenomations causing intense pain but no fatalities, and supports survival by allowing quick repulsion of attackers like dogs or birds of prey.

Clinical Aspects

Common Heel Conditions

Common heel conditions encompass several non-traumatic disorders that affect the heel, often resulting from everyday factors like environmental exposure, lifestyle, or age-related changes. These include cracked heels, retrocalcaneal bursitis, fat pad atrophy, Achilles tendinitis, and calcaneal spurs, each presenting with pain, discomfort, or functional limitations that can impact mobility if untreated. While these conditions are prevalent across populations, they are particularly common in individuals with certain risk factors, and management typically involves conservative measures to alleviate symptoms and prevent progression.
Cracked heels, also known as heel fissures, occur when the skin on the heel becomes dry and thick, leading to splits that can cause pain and, in severe cases, bleeding. Common causes include dry skin exacerbated by low humidity or harsh soaps, obesity which increases pressure on the heels, and prolonged standing on hard surfaces that promotes skin thickening and cracking. Symptoms often manifest as itching, tenderness, or sharp pain during walking, with deeper fissures potentially inviting infection. Prevention and initial management focus on daily application of thick moisturizers, such as those containing urea or petroleum jelly, to restore skin hydration, alongside wearing supportive closed-toe shoes to reduce friction.
Retrocalcaneal bursitis involves inflammation of the bursa located between the Achilles tendon and the calcaneus, leading to pain at the back of the heel. This condition typically arises from overuse, such as repetitive activities involving ankle flexion or excessive running, which irritates the bursa and causes fluid accumulation. Symptoms include swelling, redness, stiffness in the morning or after inactivity, and pain that worsens with pressure or movement, often limiting heel elevation. General management entails rest, ice application to reduce inflammation, and anti-inflammatory medications, with supportive footwear to offload the area.
Fat pad atrophy refers to the thinning of the subcutaneous fat layer beneath the heel, which normally cushions impact during weight-bearing activities. This age-related degeneration reduces shock absorption, resulting in deep, bruising pain under the heel, particularly upon first steps in the morning or after prolonged standing. It is commonly associated with aging, where the fat pad loses volume and elasticity, and affects older adults, with a US community-based study reporting a prevalence of 4.2% in those aged 65 and older. The heel's anatomical fat pad, composed of fibrous septa enclosing adipose tissue, becomes compromised, heightening vulnerability to mechanical stress. Basic home remedies include using cushioned orthotic inserts or heel pads to redistribute pressure and provide additional support.
Achilles tendinitis is inflammation of the Achilles tendon, often due to overuse from activities like running or jumping, leading to pain, swelling, and stiffness at the back of the heel. It commonly affects athletes and middle-aged individuals engaging in sudden increases in activity. Symptoms include tenderness along the tendon, worsened by walking uphill or climbing stairs, and morning stiffness. Treatment typically involves rest, ice, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), and eccentric strengthening exercises, with most cases resolving in 3-6 months through conservative care.
Calcaneal spurs, or heel spurs, are bony outgrowths on the underside of the calcaneus, frequently associated with chronic strain from plantar fasciitis or other repetitive stress. They are often asymptomatic but can cause sharp heel pain if the surrounding tissues become inflamed. Risk factors include prolonged standing, obesity, and poor footwear. Management emphasizes treating underlying causes with stretching, orthotics, and NSAIDs; surgical removal is uncommon and reserved for persistent cases.
Several risk factors contribute to the development of these conditions, including diabetes which impairs skin healing and circulation, and ill-fitting shoes that cause uneven pressure distribution on the heel. Obesity amplifies mechanical load on the heel, while open-backed footwear exposes the area to drying elements. Overall, maintaining foot hygiene, selecting properly fitted shoes, and incorporating padding or moisturizing routines can mitigate risks and support symptom relief for most individuals.

Injuries and Treatments

Achilles tendon rupture is a common traumatic injury to the heel region, often occurring during sports activities involving sudden acceleration or deceleration, such as tennis or basketball, where the tendon is subjected to excessive force during push-off maneuvers. Patients typically experience a sudden popping sensation at the back of the heel, followed by sharp pain, swelling, and difficulty bearing weight or pushing off the affected foot. Diagnosis is confirmed clinically through the Thompson test, in which squeezing the calf muscle fails to produce plantar flexion of the foot if the tendon is ruptured, often supplemented by imaging such as ultrasound or MRI for verification. Treatment options include nonoperative management with immobilization in a cast or boot for 6-8 weeks followed by rehabilitation, or surgical repair via open or percutaneous techniques to reapproximate the tendon ends, which is preferred for active individuals to restore strength and reduce re-rupture risk. Recovery timelines for surgical tendon repairs generally involve 6-12 weeks of non-weight-bearing immobilization and physical therapy, with full return to activities taking 4-6 months; complication rates include re-rupture, historically 1-2% with surgical approaches compared to 10-40% with conservative treatment, though recent studies indicate similar rates between the two (risk difference 1.6%).
Calcaneal fractures, or breaks in the heel bone, result from high-energy trauma such as falls from height or motor vehicle accidents, compressing the bone due to axial loading forces. These fractures are classified as intra-articular, involving the subtalar joint and potentially leading to arthritis if malaligned, or extra-articular, affecting the bone's outer portions without joint disruption. Diagnosis relies on X-rays and CT scans to assess displacement and joint involvement, with non-displaced extra-articular fractures often managed conservatively via casting and elevation. For displaced or intra-articular fractures, surgical fixation is standard, employing methods like open reduction and internal fixation (ORIF) with plates and screws through a lateral extensile approach, or minimally invasive percutaneous techniques to restore anatomy and minimize soft tissue complications. Recovery typically spans 3-6 months, with weight-bearing restricted for 6-12 weeks post-surgery, and long-term outcomes focusing on pain relief and mobility restoration, though intra-articular cases often develop subtalar arthritis as a long-term complication.
Plantar fasciitis, while often chronic, can manifest as an acute injury from repetitive strain in activities like running on hard surfaces, leading to micro-tears at the plantar fascia's attachment to the calcaneus. Initial management emphasizes conservative therapies, including Achilles and plantar stretching exercises to alleviate tension, custom orthotics to provide arch support and reduce fasciial strain, and activity modification. For persistent cases, extracorporeal shockwave therapy (ESWT) delivers acoustic waves to promote healing and reduce inflammation, showing efficacy in 60-80% of patients after 3-6 sessions. Most individuals recover within 6-12 months with these interventions, though severe cases may require corticosteroid injections or, rarely, surgical release.

Etymology and Cultural References

Historical and Linguistic Origins

The word "heel" in English derives from Old English hla, which traces back to Proto-Germanic *hanhilaz, a diminutive form related to the concept of a bend or curve in the leg, reflecting the anatomical contour of the rear foot. This etymological root connects to Indo-European *kenk-, denoting the heel or bend of the knee, and appears in cognates across Germanic languages, such as Old Norse hll and Dutch hiel. In anatomical contexts, the Latin term calcaneus for the heel bone originates from calx (genitive calcis), meaning "heel," possibly borrowed from a pre-Indo-European substrate language like Etruscan, with the word also evoking the chalky or lime-like quality in related usages.
Ancient medical texts provide early references to heel anatomy and pathology. Around 400 BCE, Hippocrates described conditions affecting the heel, including pain from the calcaneal tendonreferred to as neura megala (great nerves)noting that bruising or severing it could disable the limb, highlighting its critical role in mobility. Archaeological evidence from ancient Egypt reveals heel deformities in mummified remains; for instance, the mummy of Pharaoh Siptah (c. 11901080 BCE) from the 19th Dynasty exhibits talipes equinovarus (clubfoot), a congenital condition distorting the heel inward and elevating it, likely due to poliomyelitis or intrauterine factors.
During the Renaissance, anatomical understanding advanced significantly through direct observation and illustration. In 1543, Andreas Vesalius detailed and depicted the calcaneus in his seminal work De Humani Corporis Fabrica Libri Septem, providing precise drawings of the heel bone's structure and articulations, which corrected earlier inaccuracies from Galen and established a foundation for modern osteology.
The modern eponym "Achilles tendon" for the calcaneal tendon emerged from classical mythology and gained widespread anatomical acceptance in the 18th and 19th centuries. Inspired by Homer's Iliad (c. 8th century BCE), where the hero Achilles is vulnerable only at his heel after being dipped in the River Styx, the term chorda Achillis was first used in 1693 by anatomist Philip Verheyen in his Corporis Humani Anatomie. By the 19th century, as anatomical nomenclature standardized through works like those of the Nomina Anatomica, "Achilles tendon" became the conventional term in English and European medical literature, emphasizing its mythological and structural significance.

Heel in Culture and Symbolism

In Greek mythology, the phrase "Achilles' heel" originates from the legend of the hero Achilles, whose mother Thetis dipped him in the River Styx to render him invulnerable, but held him by his heel, leaving that spot unprotected; this vulnerability was exploited when Paris, guided by Apollo, shot an arrow into his heel during the Trojan War, leading to his death. Although the core narrative of Achilles appears in Homer's Iliad (c. 8th century BCE), the specific heel detail emerges in later ancient sources, such as Statius' Achilleid (1st century CE), and has since symbolized a critical weakness in an otherwise strong entity.
Biblically, the heel features in Genesis 3:15 as part of God's curse on the serpent after the Fall, declaring enmity between the serpent's offspring and the woman's, where the latter "will crush your head, and you will strike his heel," interpreted as a prophecy of temporary human suffering contrasted with ultimate victory over evil. This verse, often called the protoevangelium, uses the heel bruise to evoke a non-fatal wound, foreshadowing messianic triumph despite adversity.
In Chinese folklore, heel-binding, or foot-binding, practiced from the 10th century until the early 20th century, deformed women's feet into a "lotus" shape for aesthetic ideals of beauty and status, severely limiting mobility and symbolizing feminine virtue and social refinement among the elite. This custom, which compressed the foot including the heel to about 3-4 inches, reflected patriarchal control and erotic allure in imperial society, persisting until banned in 1912 amid modernization efforts.
Modern symbolism of the heel often ties to fashion and socioeconomic metaphors; high heels trace to 16th-century Persian cavalry boots, designed with elevated heels for secure footing in stirrups during horseback riding, later adopted in Europe as a status symbol among nobility before becoming gendered feminine attire. Idiomatic expressions like "down at the heels" emerged in the late 17th century to denote poverty or shabby appearance, derived from worn-out shoe heels indicating financial neglect.
Artistically, the heel's vulnerability is depicted in Renaissance works, such as Peter Paul Rubens' The Death of Achilles (c. 1630-1635), where the arrow pierces Achilles' heel amid his fall, emphasizing dramatic pathos and mythological tragedy in Baroque style.
