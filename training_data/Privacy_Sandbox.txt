Privacy Sandbox
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Privacy Sandbox was an initiative by Google, launched in 2019, to create a suite of web platform APIs enabling privacy-focused alternatives to third-party cookies for use cases such as targeted advertising, fraud prevention, and content measurement, with the aim of reducing cross-site tracking while sustaining digital ecosystems.
Key technologies included the Topics API for cohort-based interest grouping via on-device classification, the Protected Audience API for remarketing through privacy-preserving auctions, and Attribution Reporting for aggregated conversion measurement without individual identifiers.
The project underwent extensive trials and faced prolonged regulatory scrutiny, particularly from the UK Competition and Markets Authority over antitrust risks, including potential foreclosure of competitors in ad markets due to Google's dominant position.
Originally tied to plans for phasing out third-party cookies in Chrome, the initiative shifted in 2024 to emphasize user choice mechanisms rather than mandatory deprecation, but was ultimately retired in October 2025 after years of delays, limited industry adoption, and unresolved concerns about its effectiveness in delivering genuine privacy enhancements without consolidating Google's ad tech influence.

Origins and Development

Initial Announcement and Motivations

The Privacy Sandbox initiative was announced by Google on August 22, 2019, as a project to develop open web standards for enhancing online privacy. The announcement positioned it as a response to the limitations of third-party cookies, which facilitate cross-site user tracking by embedding identifiers from external domains into web pages, enabling advertisers to compile detailed behavioral profiles without user awareness or consent.
Google's primary stated motivation was to phase out support for third-party cookies in its Chrome browserinitially targeted for completion by 2022while preserving the functionality of the ad-supported web ecosystem. This shift was driven by escalating privacy risks associated with third-party cookies, including security vulnerabilities from data aggregation and unauthorized sharing across sites, amid increasing regulatory scrutiny such as the European Union's GDPR and California's CCPA. The initiative sought to replace individualized tracking with aggregated, on-device processing methods to limit personal data exposure, allowing advertisers to target audiences and measure campaign effectiveness through anonymized signals rather than persistent identifiers.
Critics, including privacy advocates, have questioned whether the motivations extend beyond user protection to consolidating Google's dominance in digital advertising, given its control over Chrome (with over 65% global browser market share as of 2019) and existing first-party data advantages from services like Search and YouTube. Nonetheless, Google's official rationale emphasized balancing privacy enhancements with economic sustainability for publishers and developers reliant on ad revenue, which third-party cookies had underpinned since their widespread adoption in the late 1990s.

Evolution of Goals and Timeline

Google announced the Privacy Sandbox initiative on August 22, 2019, with the primary goal of developing open web standards to replace third-party cookies, enabling advertisers to target users and measure ad performance without cross-site tracking or revealing individual user data. The initial objectives emphasized balancing user privacy enhancementssuch as limiting data sharingwith the sustainability of an ad-supported web ecosystem, addressing growing concerns over fingerprinting and pervasive tracking. On January 14, 2020, Google specified plans to phase out support for third-party cookies in Chrome by the end of 2022, positioning Privacy Sandbox APIs as direct alternatives for key functions like ad auctions and cohort-based targeting.
Early proposals evolved in response to technical and privacy critiques; for instance, the Federated Learning of Cohorts (FLoC) API, introduced in January 2020 for grouping users into privacy-preserving cohorts, faced backlash for potential fingerprinting vulnerabilities and was trialed briefly before Google announced its discontinuation on July 14, 2021, replacing it with the Topics API on January 25, 2022, which classifies user interests based on browsing history processed on-device. Other APIs, such as TURTLEDOVE (later evolved into Protected Audience API by April 2023), aimed at remarketing without persistent identifiers, reflecting iterative refinements to mitigate risks of re-identification while preserving ad relevance. These changes marked a shift from broad cohort models toward more granular, device-bound mechanisms, driven by feedback from developers and privacy researchers during origin trials.
Timeline milestones were repeatedly adjusted amid industry testing and regulatory scrutiny, including a UK Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) investigation launched January 7, 2021. In June 24, 2021, Google outlined a revised schedule: late 2022 for expanded trials of key APIs, a nine-month migration period starting then, and full third-party cookie phase-out over three months ending late 2023, contingent on CMA approval. Delays followed, pushing the phase-out to 2024 and then early 2025 by April 23, 2024, as adoption lagged and concerns grew over API efficacy in delivering comparable revenue to cookies.
By July 22, 2024, goals pivoted from mandatory cookie deprecation to user-controlled settings in Chrome, allowing opt-in for third-party cookies while continuing Privacy Sandbox API development and enhancements like IP Protection in Incognito mode, acknowledging insufficient ecosystem readiness. This hybrid approach aimed to foster privacy innovations without disrupting ad markets, but further evolution in 2025 led to abandonment of the opt-in model by April, retaining cookies by default. On October 17, 2025, Google announced retirement of most Privacy Sandbox APIsincluding Attribution Reporting, Protected Audience, Topics, and Private Aggregationdue to low adoption rates, while preserving select tools like CHIPS and focusing future efforts on interoperable standards via the W3C Private Advertising Technology Working Group. This marked a de-emphasis on proprietary Sandbox technologies toward broader web platform improvements and fraud reduction, reflecting scaled-back ambitions amid persistent advertiser resistance and regulatory hurdles.

Technical Framework

Core Architecture and Privacy Claims

The Privacy Sandbox's core architecture consists of a set of open web platform APIs integrated into Chromium-based browsers, designed to enable key web functionalities such as ad targeting, auctioning, and conversion measurement without third-party cookies or cross-site identifiers. These APIs emphasize client-side processing on the user's device, where data computation occurs locally to limit server-side exposure of raw browsing signals. Central components include the Topics API, which classifies users into broad interest categories based on on-device analysis of recent site visits (e.g., selecting up to 5 topics per week from a predefined taxonomy); the Protected Audience API, which facilitates in-browser ad auctions for predefined audiences using ephemeral, partitioned storage to avoid persistent tracking; and the Attribution Reporting API, which aggregates event-level data into noisy summaries for cross-site attribution without revealing individual user actions. Supporting elements like Fenced Frames for secure content embedding and Shared Storage for limited cross-site scripting further partition data by top-level origin to enforce site-specific isolation.
Google's privacy claims center on eliminating individualized cross-site tracking by replacing it with cohort-based or aggregated signals that cannot be linked to specific users over time. Key mechanisms include differential privacy through calibrated noise addition to reports (e.g., in attribution summaries, where random perturbations ensure that no single user's data influences outcomes detectably); privacy budgets that cap the frequency of observable signals per user; and double-partitioned storage keyed by both frame and top-level site to prevent data leakage across contexts. The architecture purportedly guarantees that advertisers receive only anonymized, ephemeral insightssuch as topic frequencies or auction outcomeswithout access to browsing histories or IP addresses in unprotected forms, thereby reducing the risk of re-identification compared to cookie-based systems. User controls, including opt-out options for personalized ads, are integrated to allow disabling of these APIs entirely, with fallback to non-targeted experiences.
These claims rely on browser enforcement and on-device enforcement to mitigate fingerprinting and other workarounds, with Google asserting that the system's design inherently limits data utility for surveillance while sustaining ad ecosystem viability. However, the claims presuppose strict adherence to API boundaries and do not address potential aggregation attacks across multiple signals or Google's first-party data advantages, which independent analyses have highlighted as residual risks despite the stated safeguards. As of October 2025, while the foundational architecture remains documented for reference, many core APIs (e.g., Topics, Protected Audience, Attribution Reporting) have entered deprecation phases in Chrome, shifting focus to standards like W3C Attribution and cookie partitioning extensions such as CHIPS.

Key APIs and Proposals

The Privacy Sandbox framework encompasses several interconnected APIs designed to support advertising, measurement, and personalization use cases while limiting cross-site tracking. These include the Topics API, Protected Audience API, Attribution Reporting API, and Private Aggregation API, each addressing specific aspects of third-party cookie replacement through differential privacy techniques, on-device processing, and aggregated reporting. These proposals evolved from earlier iterations, such as the Federated Learning of Cohorts (FLoC) system, which was deprecated in favor of Topics due to concerns over cohort-based grouping risks.
The Topics API enables interest-based ad targeting by classifying user browsing history into broad topical categories, such as "Sports" or "Travel," generated weekly on the user's device without sharing raw history data. Browsers observe site visits over a three-week period to select up to five topics, sharing only one per site visit with the top five domains, subject to user controls for viewing and resetting topics. This API aims to provide relevance signals to advertisers while preventing individual profiling, with taxonomy updates managed by the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF). Trials indicated it could deliver comparable ad relevance to cookies for broad categories but faced criticism for potential re-identification risks in niche topics.
The Protected Audience API, previously known as FLEDGE (Federated Learning of Cohorts for Personalized and Private Auctions with Data Protection via Encrypted Generations of Embeddings), supports remarketing and custom audience targeting by allowing advertisers to upload hashed audience lists to the browser for private matching against user signals. Ad auctions occur within a secure environment where bids and selections remain isolated from the publisher, with no cross-site visibility into user interests or auction details. It incorporates noise addition and buyer/seller constraints to obscure individual behaviors, enabling on-device audience joining without third-party access to raw data. As of 2023, it entered origin trials in Chrome, focusing on privacy-intensive scenarios like retargeting.
The Attribution Reporting API facilitates conversion measurement by attributing ad events (e.g., clicks or views) to outcomes like purchases through privacy-preserving reports that aggregate data across users and add noise to limit granularity. It supports event-level and summary reports with configurable windows (e.g., 1-day or 7-day clicks), filtering to high-value conversions, and browser-generated encryption to prevent source identification. This API replaces cross-site attribution reliant on identifiers, with implementations tested in Chrome since 2021, showing potential for 70-90% accuracy in aggregated metrics but reduced signal for low-volume campaigns.
Complementing these, the Private Aggregation API enables the secure summation of cross-site metrics, such as ad impressions or revenues, by processing encrypted contributions in a trusted aggregator service. It supports use cases like cross-site reach and frequency capping through predefined buckets that distribute data noise to protect against inversion attacks, requiring API keys for access control. Integrated with Attribution Reporting, it was made generally available in Chrome by mid-2023, emphasizing differential privacy budgets to balance utility and anonymity.

Implementation and Trials

Testing Phases and Metrics

The primary testing mechanism for Privacy Sandbox APIs has been Chrome's origin trials, which allow developers to experiment with features in real-world environments while limiting exposure to a subset of users. These trials began with individual APIs in late 2020 and early 2021, transitioning to a unified origin trial for the core relevance and measurement proposalsTopics API, Protected Audience API (formerly FLEDGE), and Attribution Reporting APIin April 2022, initially limited to Chrome Beta users in version 102. Isolated experiments for specific API groups followed in March 2023, with traffic allocations starting at 1% per group, while the unified trial expanded to up to 7% of Chrome Stable traffic for components like the Private Aggregation API by June 6, 2023. Trials progressed from Canary channels to Beta and then Stable releases, requiring developer registration and enrollment for access, with ongoing monitoring through Chrome DevTools.
Quantitative evaluations during these phases included controlled experiments comparing Privacy Sandbox performance against third-party cookie deprecation scenarios. In Google's Q1 2024 tests using Google Ad Manager and AdSense, disabling third-party cookies without Privacy Sandbox APIs resulted in programmatic revenue drops of 34% for Ad Manager publishers and 21% for AdSense publishers relative to baseline controls. Incorporating Privacy Sandbox APIs mitigated these losses to 20% and 18%, respectively, yielding incremental uplifts of 13% for Ad Manager and 3% for AdSense publishers compared to no-API cookie deprecation. These directional estimates excluded guaranteed programmatic and non-programmatic transactions, focusing on auction-based advertising utility.
Additional metrics assessed privacy-utility tradeoffs, such as auction success rates, conversion attribution accuracy under noise addition for differential privacy, and developer-reported data flow performance, though specific figures varied by API and required custom implementations for measurement. The UK Competition and Markets Authority oversaw independent quantitative testing to evaluate competitive impacts, emphasizing metrics like publisher revenue equivalence and cross-site tracking leakage, with progress tracked in quarterly reports up to Q1 2025. Developer feedback from trials highlighted challenges in matching pre-cookie utility levels, informing iterative refinements like summary report optimizations in Attribution Reporting.

Browser Integration and Cross-Platform Efforts

The Privacy Sandbox initiative has been primarily integrated into Google Chrome, where core APIs such as Topics API and Protected Audience API have been progressively enabled since Chrome 96 in late 2021, with ongoing refinements through 2025 to support privacy-preserving ad targeting without third-party cookies. In Chrome, these features operate by default for users who have not opted out, aligning with Google's phased approach to cookie deprecation, though full third-party cookie removal was postponed indefinitely as of April 2025 to prioritize user choice and regulatory compliance. Chromium-based browsers, including Microsoft Edge, inherit much of this functionality due to shared rendering engines, enabling partial compatibility without custom implementation.
Microsoft Edge has shown limited but notable alignment with Privacy Sandbox principles, announcing its own Ad Selection API in March 2024 to facilitate on-device ad auctions similar to Google's proposals, though full adoption remains selective and tied to Microsoft's enterprise-focused privacy controls rather than wholesale endorsement. In contrast, non-Chromium browsers like Mozilla Firefox and Apple Safari have rejected direct integration, opting instead for stricter default blocking of third-party cookiesFirefox since 2019 and Safari via Intelligent Tracking Prevention since 2017citing concerns over centralized control and insufficient privacy guarantees in Google's framework. These browsers prioritize alternative mechanisms, such as Firefox's Enhanced Tracking Protection and Safari's opt-in stateful partitions, which fragment cross-browser interoperability for Sandbox APIs.
Cross-platform efforts have extended Privacy Sandbox beyond web browsers to Android, announced in February 2022 as a multi-year project to replace cross-app identifiers like Advertising ID with anonymized, on-device processing for ad measurement and personalization. On Android, initial betas launched in February 2023 for select apps, with APIs like Topics and Attribution Reporting adapted for mobile environments to support free app models amid restrictions from platforms like Apple's App Tracking Transparency. By 2025, Android implementations focus on developer testing via protected environments that aggregate user data without persistent identifiers, though widespread rollout remains in progress without firm public timelines for general availability. No equivalent efforts target iOS, where Apple's ecosystem enforces device-level privacy controls incompatible with Google's proposals, underscoring the initiative's confinement to Google-dominated platforms.

Reception and Debates

Industry Adoption and Support

Initial support for the Privacy Sandbox emerged from select ad technology providers and Google, with early trials indicating potential for privacy-preserving advertising alternatives to third-party cookies. Companies such as NextRoll conducted testing in 2024, reporting that private auctions via APIs like Protected Audience were feasible despite publisher participation rates below expectations, which limited inventory and performance. Similarly, Index Exchange's 2024 evaluations highlighted a 33% decline in cost-per-mille (CPM) rates for Sandbox-enabled impressions compared to cookie-based ones, attributing issues to latency and incomplete ecosystem integration.
Broader industry participation involved investments from publishers and vendors since the initiative's 2019 announcement, with many acknowledging resource expenditures on development and compliance testing. Criteo, a demand-side platform, noted in mid-2024 trials that overall publisher adoption hovered below 55%, insufficient to meet regulatory benchmarks set by bodies like the UK's Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) for revenue neutrality. Feedback from early adopters in 2025 Q1 underscored challenges from slower uptake by major players, hindering scalable test launches and revealing technical hurdles like auction latency exceeding 100 milliseconds in some cases.
Despite these efforts, adoption metrics consistently underperformed, with independent tests showing publisher revenue drops of approximately 30% and advertiser spend reductions ranging from 42% to 67% across evaluators, far exceeding Google's internal estimates of 14% via its Display & Video 360 platform. Small ad-tech firms faced disproportionate burdens from implementation costs, estimated in the millions for integration, favoring larger entities with deeper resources. While some testers identified "silver linings" in data signals for future refinements, the lack of widespread publisher and advertiser buy-inevidenced by paused integrations and frozen pilots by mid-2025signaled faltering momentum, culminating in the initiative's partial abandonment.

Criticisms from Advertisers and Publishers

Advertisers and publishers have criticized the Privacy Sandbox for potentially causing substantial revenue declines, with testing indicating losses of up to 60% for publishers reliant on Chrome traffic compared to third-party cookie-based systems. Independent evaluations by the UK's Competition and Markets Authority in June 2025 confirmed significant revenue shortfalls in trials, where publisher earnings dropped markedly without adequate mitigation from Sandbox APIs.
Critics, including the Interactive Advertising Bureau Tech Lab, highlighted a lack of transparency in the Sandbox's processes, arguing that it fails to replicate the efficacy of third-party cookies for publishers while introducing opaque mechanisms that disadvantage smaller players. Advertisers reported concerns over signal loss and reduced targeting precision, as APIs like Topics and Protected Audience provided coarser user cohorts than individualized cookie data, leading to lower ad performance and higher costs.
Implementation challenges, such as increased latency in ad auctionssometimes exceeding 100 millisecondswere flagged by industry testers as directly contributing to lost impressions and revenue, with supply-side platforms noting that these delays eroded competitiveness against faster cookie-dependent alternatives. Publisher feedback from trials also emphasized Google's outsized control, where Sandbox integration boosted Google's share of publisher revenues from 23% to 83% in some scenarios, raising fears of market consolidation that favors dominant platforms over diverse ad ecosystems.
Overall, these groups contended that the framework's privacy constraints undermined ad monetization without delivering promised alternatives, prompting many to pivot toward first-party data strategies amid the initiative's eventual phase-out in 2025.

Privacy and Antitrust Concerns

Privacy advocates and researchers have criticized the Privacy Sandbox for failing to adequately protect user data despite Google's assertions of enhanced privacy through on-device processing and limited data retention. For instance, the Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) analyzed the APIs and concluded that they enable persistent user profiling via mechanisms like the Topics API, which classifies users into interest categories based on browsing history, potentially allowing inference of sensitive information and undermining anonymity. Independent audits, including those referenced by regulators, identified risks of cross-site linkage and re-identification, contradicting claims of equivalence to third-party cookie elimination. The UK's Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) echoed these issues in its 2021-2024 investigations, noting that Sandbox proposals like Federated Learning of Cohorts (FLoC, predecessor to Topics) exposed users to cohort-based tracking that could leak private attributes, prompting opt-out defaults in tools like Privacy Badger.
Antitrust regulators expressed concerns that the initiative reinforced Google's dominance in digital advertising rather than fostering competition. The CMA's probe, initiated in 2021, scrutinized whether replacing third-party cookies with Google-controlled APIssuch as Topics and Protected Audiencewould create gatekeeper effects, enabling Google to favor its ad exchange (DoubleClick) and discriminate against rivals by controlling access to user signals. In the EU, the European Commission's 2021 antitrust investigation into Google's ad tech practices highlighted similar risks, arguing that Sandbox implementation could extend Google's 90%+ market share in search ads to broader open-web auctions through proprietary standards. U.S. Department of Justice filings in related ad tech litigation (2023-2025) viewed the Sandbox as a potential workaround to remedies, centralizing auction dynamics under Chrome's influence, which covers over 65% of global browsers, thereby stifling innovation from independent ad tech providers. These regulatory interventions delayed rollout multiple times, with the CMA requiring behavioral commitments from Google in 2024 to mitigate anti-competitive design.

Retirement and Aftermath

Phase-Out Announcement

On October 17, 2025, Google announced the retirement of most technologies developed under the Privacy Sandbox initiative, citing low adoption rates and ecosystem feedback indicating limited value. The decision encompasses the phase-out of ten specific APIs across Chrome and Android platforms, including Attribution Reporting, IP Protection, On-Device Personalization, Private Aggregation (including Shared Storage), Protected Audience, Protected App Signals, Related Website Sets (including requestStorageAccessFor and Related Website Partition), SelectURL, SDK Runtime, and Topics. Google stated, "After evaluating ecosystem feedback about their expected value and in light of their low levels of adoption, we've decided to retire the following Privacy Sandbox technologies."
The announcement followed the April 2024 reversal of plans to deprecate third-party cookies in Chrome, marking a shift away from the broader Privacy Sandbox framework launched in 2019 as a privacy-focused alternative to cookie-based tracking. While phasing out the majority of APIs, Google committed to ongoing support for three technologies showing stronger adoption: CHIPS (Cross-app/site identifiers for privacy-preserving signals), Federated Credential Management (FedCM), and Private State Tokens. A Google spokesperson confirmed to AdWeek, "We will be continuing our work to improve privacy across Chrome, Android and the web, but moving away from the Privacy Sandbox branding."
Implementation of the retirements will adhere to standard Chrome and Android deprecation processes, with status updates tracked on the project's feature overview page. Google also outlined future efforts, including collaboration with the W3C on an interoperable Attribution standard and initiatives to enhance first-party data protection and web experiences. This effectively ends the core Privacy Sandbox project after six years of development, trials, and delays.

Causal Factors in Failure

Google retired most Privacy Sandbox APIs on October 17, 2025, after six years of development, attributing the decision primarily to persistently low adoption rates despite extensive trials and industry feedback. Independent testing revealed that the APIs failed to deliver ad performance comparable to third-party cookies, with publishers facing potential revenue declines of up to 60% in cookie-deprecated scenarios, as measured against benchmarks set by the UK's Competition and Markets Authority (CMA). This shortfall stemmed from core design choices emphasizing privacy through techniques like data aggregation, noise injection, and on-device processing, which degraded signal accuracy for targeting and attribution, rendering the system less viable for revenue-critical applications.
Implementation hurdles further exacerbated low uptake, particularly for smaller ad-tech firms burdened by high engineering costs and resource demands to integrate the APIs. Larger entities reported delays in ecosystem coordination, with early adopters citing challenges in launching tests due to insufficient participation from key partners, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of limited scalability. The APIs' complexity, including requirements for cross-origin coordination and compliance with stringent privacy constraints, contrasted with the relative simplicity of legacy cookie-based systems, deterring widespread experimentation beyond Google's controlled environments.
Technical limitations compounded these issues, as Privacy Sandbox's reliance on browser-level interventions restricted cross-device user profiling and precise audience segmentation, areas where third-party cookies excelled. Feedback from quarterly reports highlighted a privacy-utility imbalance, where protective measures like randomized reporting intervals undermined the granular data advertisers demanded for premium campaigns, leading to suboptimal return on investment. Concurrently, the ad industry's pivot toward first-party data collection and AI-enhanced modeling offered more flexible alternatives, diminishing the perceived necessity of Sandbox technologies amid evolving regulatory landscapes that preserved cookie options.

Implications for Advertising and Privacy Standards

The retirement of Privacy Sandbox on October 17, 2025, due to insufficient industry adoption, has preserved third-party cookie-based targeting in Chrome, allowing advertisers to maintain cross-site behavioral tracking without immediate disruption. As of February 2026, Chrome does not block third-party cookies by default, having abandoned plans to deprecate them in 2025 in favor of a user-choice model where individuals can enable or disable them via existing browser settings without a new consent prompt; cookies remain available unless manually blocked. Consequently, there is no widespread blocking impacting pop-up ads, which are primarily limited by Chrome's built-in popup blocker and can still use third-party cookies for targeting and functionality where users allow them, resulting in minimal change from previous behavior. This outcome averts the revenue losses projected from Sandbox APIs, which limited user identity data and hindered cross-device ad coordination, as evidenced by trials showing reduced auction competition and reporting accuracy. Advertisers and publishers, who achieved only low single-digit adoption rates for key APIs like Topics and Protected Audience, now prioritize first-party data collection, server-side tagging, and AI-enhanced probabilistic modeling to sustain personalization amid ongoing regulatory scrutiny.
The initiative's failure underscores structural challenges in ad tech innovation, where privacy constraints clashed with incentives for precise targeting; Sandbox trials revealed up to 20-30% efficiency drops in ad performance compared to cookies, deterring scalable deployment. This has accelerated diversification away from cookie reliance, with platforms investing in contextual signals and consented data pools, though fragmentation risks higher costs for smaller publishers unable to amass first-party assets.
For privacy standards, Privacy Sandbox's demise exposes the fragility of voluntary, browser-led reforms in curbing tracking; designed to replace cookies with aggregated auctions and topic-based cohorts, it preserved behavioral ad selection within Google's ecosystem, prompting opt-outs from tools like Privacy Badger for failing to eliminate individualized profiling. Regulatory bodies, including the UK's CMA, had conditioned progress on antitrust mitigations, but persistent concerns over Google's auction dominancepotentially entrenching its 90%+ search ad sharecontributed to stalled adoption. The phase-out reinforces reliance on legislative baselines like GDPR and emerging U.S. state laws, while highlighting cross-browser inconsistencies: Safari and Firefox already block cookies by default, fostering a patchwork that undermines uniform standards but pressures Chrome toward stricter defaults without proprietary APIs. Long-term, it signals that enforceable rules, rather than industry self-regulation, may drive verifiable reductions in cross-site data flows, as empirical resistance to diluted targeting reveals causal primacy of revenue over de-identification in ad ecosystems.
