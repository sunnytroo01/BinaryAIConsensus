Fundamentals

Definition

An acoustic wave is a mechanical disturbance that propagates through an elastic medium as a longitudinal pressure wave, in which the displacement of particles occurs parallel to the direction of wave propagation. This results in alternating regions of compression, where particles are closer together, and rarefaction, where they are farther apart, creating oscillations in pressure, density, and particle velocity.
Unlike electromagnetic waves, which can propagate through a vacuum, acoustic waves require a physical mediumsuch as gases, liquids, or solidsfor transmission, as they rely on the interactions between particles in the medium to carry the disturbance.
These waves are generated by vibrating sources that perturb the medium, such as the prongs of a struck tuning fork or the diaphragm of a speaker, which produce successive compressions and rarefactions that travel outward from the source.
The frequency of acoustic waves spans a broad range, with audible sound typically between 20 Hz and 20 kHz for human hearing, while frequencies below 20 Hz constitute infrasound and those above 20 kHz are ultrasound. The amplitude of an acoustic wave determines its intensity, which is proportional to the square of the amplitude, and relates to perceived loudness, where higher amplitudes correspond to greater loudness on a logarithmic scale.

Types

Acoustic waves are classified based on the medium of propagation, the direction of particle motion relative to the wave's direction, and frequency ranges. In fluids, such as air or water, acoustic waves are longitudinal, characterized by particle oscillations parallel to the direction of propagation, resulting in alternating compressions and rarefactions without any shear component. This type dominates in gaseous and liquid media due to the inability of fluids to support shear stresses.
In solids, acoustic waves can be either longitudinal or transverse. Longitudinal waves in solids, often called compressional or P-waves, feature particle motion parallel to propagation, similar to those in fluids but capable of transmitting shear as well. Transverse shear waves, or S-waves, exhibit particle displacement perpendicular to the propagation direction, enabling polarization in two orthogonal planes and allowing propagation only in materials that resist shear, like solids. Surface waves, confined near boundaries, include Rayleigh waves, which combine longitudinal and vertical shear components to produce elliptical particle orbits that decay exponentially with depth from the surface, typically penetrating about one wavelength. Love waves, a type of transverse surface wave, involve horizontal particle motion perpendicular to propagation and are prominent in layered media, such as the Earth's crust.
Acoustic waves are further categorized as bulk or guided. Bulk waves propagate freely through the interior of a homogeneous medium without boundary constraints, encompassing the longitudinal and transverse modes described above. Guided waves, in contrast, are confined within structures like waveguides, rods, or plates, where boundaries reflect and interfere with the waves to form discrete modes that propagate along the guide, often used in nondestructive testing for long-range inspection.
Frequency-based classification delineates infrasonic waves (below 20 Hz), audible waves (20 Hz to 20 kHz), and ultrasonic waves (above 20 kHz). Infrasonic waves, generated by events like earthquakes or volcanic eruptions, are inaudible to humans but detectable by animals such as elephants. Audible waves correspond to the human hearing range, encompassing everyday sounds like speech. Ultrasonic waves find applications in medical imaging, such as echocardiography, where high-frequency pulses enable detailed visualization of tissues.
In anisotropic media, such as crystals, acoustic waves exhibit polarization, where the direction of particle motion is constrained by the medium's directional elastic properties, leading to quasi-longitudinal (quasi-P) and quasi-transverse (quasi-S) waves with velocity and polarization dependent on propagation direction.

Propagation Speed

The propagation speed of acoustic waves, often denoted as the speed of sound $ c $, is a fundamental property that determines how quickly pressure disturbances travel through a medium. In ideal gases, this speed is given by the formula $ c = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P}{\rho}} $, where $ \gamma $ is the adiabatic index (ratio of specific heats), $ P $ is the pressure, and $ \rho $ is the density. This expression arises from the adiabatic compression and rarefaction of gas parcels during wave propagation, linking thermodynamic properties to wave dynamics.
In liquids, which are nearly incompressible fluids, the speed simplifies to $ c = \sqrt{\frac{K}{\rho}} $, with $ K $ representing the bulk modulus that measures the medium's resistance to uniform compression. For example, in water at room temperature, this yields speeds around 1480 m/s, significantly higher than in air due to the greater stiffness of liquids.
Solids support both longitudinal and shear acoustic waves, with distinct speeds derived from the material's elastic constants. The longitudinal wave speed is $ c_L = \sqrt{\frac{\lambda + 2\mu}{\rho}} $, where $ \lambda $ and $ \mu $ are the Lam parameters characterizing interatomic interactions, while the shear wave speed is $ c_S = \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\rho}} $. These formulas reflect the coupling of compressional and shear deformations in solids, leading to typical values like $ c_L \approx 5000 $ m/s and $ c_S \approx 3000 $ m/s in metals such as steel.
Environmental factors influence the speed in gases like air. Temperature has the dominant effect, with an approximate linear relation $ c \approx 331 + 0.6T $ m/s, where $ T $ is in degrees Celsius; this stems from the direct proportionality of $ c $ to the square root of absolute temperature in the ideal gas formula. Pressure has negligible impact at constant temperature, as $ c $ remains independent of $ P $ since density scales proportionally. Humidity slightly increases the speed; at 20C, sound travels about 0.35% faster (approximately 1.2 m/s) in air at 100% relative humidity compared to dry air, due to the lower average molecular mass of moist air.
In complex media such as crystals or composites, acoustic waves exhibit anisotropy, where propagation speed varies with direction relative to the material's symmetry axes, requiring tensorial descriptions of elastic properties beyond isotropic assumptions. Additionally, dispersion occurs in such media, causing the phase speed to depend on frequency owing to frequency-selective scattering or relaxation processes, which complicates wave propagation compared to non-dispersive cases.

Propagation Phenomena

Reflection and Transmission

When an acoustic wave encounters an interface between two different media, part of the wave is reflected back into the incident medium, while the remainder is transmitted into the second medium. This partitioning of energy is governed by the acoustic properties of the media, particularly their densities and sound speeds. The behavior arises from the requirement that certain physical quantities remain continuous across the boundary.
The key boundary conditions at the interface are the continuity of acoustic pressure and the normal component of particle velocity. These conditions ensure that there is no abrupt change in pressure or net flow of mass across the boundary, leading to the generation of reflected and transmitted waves whose amplitudes satisfy these constraints.

This effect, analogous to optics, enables applications like acoustic waveguides. Propagation speed differences between media drive the impedance mismatch underlying these reflections.

This phenomenon arises in inhomogeneous media where gradients in sound speed ccc, often due to variations in temperature, density, or salinity, alter the direction of propagation. In stratified media with continuous speed gradients, such as oceanic layers, refraction can produce acoustic mirages and shadowing zones. Acoustic mirages emerge from strong near-surface temperature gradients, like those above a hot roadway, where sound speed increases rapidly with height, bending rays upward and creating illusory distant sources or excess attenuation of 1020 dB in shadow zones for frequencies of 210 kHz. In the ocean, the sound channel (or SOFAR channel) forms a waveguide due to downward refraction in the thermocline, trapping low-frequency rays and enabling long-range propagation while producing shadow zones beyond the channel where rays are refracted away from receivers.

In graded-index media, where sound speed varies continuously across the propagation direction, refraction leads to beam displacement and focusing effects. Beams experience lateral shifts as rays curve through speed gradients, analogous to optical beam displacement at interfaces but extended to continuous media. Gradient-index acoustic metasurfaces, engineered with subwavelength structures like porous silicone rubber, enable precise focusing of airborne sound at scales below the wavelength, achieving subwavelength resolution for imaging by modulating phase and redirecting rays toward a focal point.
Atmospheric propagation exemplifies these effects through temperature inversions, where sound speed increases with height, refracting rays downward and extending audible range, as in hearing distant sounds across water at night. Conversely, daytime lapse rates bend rays upward, forming shadow zones that limit propagation.

Absorption and Attenuation

Absorption and attenuation refer to the irreversible loss of energy in acoustic waves as they propagate through a medium, converting mechanical energy into heat via dissipative mechanisms. These processes reduce the amplitude of the wave over distance, distinguishing them from reversible phenomena like reflection. Beyond classical mechanisms, relaxation processes contribute significantly to attenuation, particularly in gases and liquids where internal molecular degrees of freedom lag behind the rapid pressure changes of the wave. Wave Interactions

Interference

Acoustic waves, being linear, obey the principle of superposition, whereby the total displacement at any point in the medium is the vector sum of the displacements produced by each individual wave. This principle holds because acoustic waves in fluids and solids typically involve small-amplitude perturbations where nonlinear effects are negligible.
When two acoustic waves of slightly different frequencies, f1f_1f1 and f2f_2f2, superpose, they produce an interference pattern known as beats, characterized by periodic variations in amplitude at a modulation frequency of f1f2|f_1 - f_2|f1f2. The resulting sound intensity fluctuates with a period equal to 1/f1f21/|f_1 - f_2|1/f1f2, creating an audible pulsing effect that is most prominent when the frequency difference is small, such as a few hertz.
An analogy to Young's double-slit experiment in optics can be drawn for sound waves using two coherent point sources, such as speakers, separated by a small distance. Constructive interference occurs at points where the path length difference \delta from the two sources satisfies =m\delta = m\lambda=m, with mmm an integer and \lambda the wavelength, leading to maxima in sound intensity. This setup produces observable fringes of louder and quieter regions, demonstrating spatial interference patterns in air.
For such interference fringes to be clearly observable, the waves must maintain coherence over the relevant path lengths, quantified by the coherence length, which is the distance beyond which phase relationships become random due to factors like source bandwidth or medium fluctuations. In acoustics, coherence lengths on the order of meters are typical for narrowband sources in controlled environments, but they decrease with broader frequency spectra or turbulent propagation.
A practical application of destructive interference is found in noise-canceling headphones, where microphones detect ambient noise and generate an antiphase signal that superposes with the incoming waves to attenuate low-frequency sounds through cancellation. This technique relies on the superposition principle to achieve reductions of up to 20-30 dB in targeted frequency bands, primarily below 1 kHz.

Diffraction

Diffraction refers to the bending and spreading of acoustic waves as they encounter obstacles or pass through apertures, allowing sound to propagate into regions that would otherwise be shadowed in geometric acoustics. This phenomenon arises because acoustic waves, like other waves, do not strictly follow straight-line paths but instead curve around edges due to their wave nature. The extent of diffraction depends on the wavelength relative to the obstacle size; longer wavelengths diffract more readily than shorter ones.
The Huygens-Fresnel principle provides the foundational explanation for acoustic diffraction, positing that every point on a wavefront acts as a source of secondary spherical wavelets that propagate forward and interfere to form the subsequent wavefront. In acoustics, this principle models how plane waves bend around barriers or through slits by treating the wavefront as a superposition of these wavelets, with diffraction patterns emerging from their coherent summation. For instance, in surface acoustic wave propagation near a channel wall, the principle predicts a knife-edge diffraction effect where the wave interacts with the boundary to produce cylindrical wavefronts.

Standing Waves

Applications

In Fluids

Acoustic waves propagate primarily as longitudinal waves in fluids, where particle motion aligns with the direction of wave travel, enabling efficient transmission through gases and liquids.
In underwater acoustics, these waves facilitate sonar ranging systems that emit sound pulses to measure distances to submerged objects by calculating the time for echoes to return, essential for navigation and detection in opaque marine environments. Marine mammals, such as dolphins and whales, employ echolocationa biological form of sonarby generating high-frequency clicks that reflect off prey or obstacles, allowing precise localization in water where visibility is limited. This process relies on the relatively low attenuation of sound in seawater compared to light, enabling ranges up to several kilometers for certain species.
Atmospheric applications harness low-frequency infrasound waves, below 20 Hz, for weather monitoring by detecting pressure perturbations from events like storms or tornadoes, which propagate over long distances with minimal dissipation in air. In aviation, acoustic waves inform noise control strategies, such as designing engine components to disrupt turbulent airflow that generates excessive sound, reducing community exposure near airports.
High-intensity ultrasound in fluids introduces challenges like cavitation, where rapid pressure cycles form and collapse vapor bubbles, potentially causing tissue damage or enhancing processes like emulsification in medical and industrial settings. Acoustic radiation pressure, arising from the nonlinear interaction of intense sound waves with fluid particles, enables levitation and manipulation of small objects or droplets in air or liquids, suspending them against gravity without physical contact for applications in microgravity research. In medicine, ultrasound waves traverse water-based tissues, such as those in the human body, to produce diagnostic images by reflecting off boundaries between soft structures, providing non-invasive visualization of organs and blood flow.

In Solids

Acoustic waves in solids differ from those in fluids due to the ability of solids to support both longitudinal (compressional) and shear (transverse) wave modes, enabling a wider range of propagation behaviors influenced by material elasticity and anisotropy. These waves are generated and detected using piezoelectric transducers, which convert electrical energy into mechanical vibrations via the piezoelectric effect in materials like quartz or lead zirconate titanate (PZT), and vice versa for reception. This bidirectional transduction is fundamental to applications in solid media, where waves propagate at speeds typically ranging from 1 to 6 km/s depending on the material, such as 5.9 km/s for longitudinal waves in steel.
Ultrasonic testing employs high-frequency acoustic waves (above 20 kHz) for non-destructive evaluation of solid structures, particularly welds and materials, using the pulse-echo technique. In this method, a short ultrasonic pulse is transmitted into the solid, and echoes from internal flaws like cracks or voids are received and analyzed to determine defect location and size based on time-of-flight measurements. Developed extensively since the mid-20th century, this technique is standardized in industries such as aerospace and manufacturing, with pulse-echo enabling detection of defects as small as 0.5 mm in metals.
In geophysics, seismic wavesprimary (P-waves, longitudinal) and secondary (S-waves, shear)propagate through Earth's solid crust and mantle, providing critical data for earthquake modeling and subsurface imaging. P-waves travel faster (up to 8 km/s in the crust) and can pass through fluids, while S-waves, limited to solids, move at about 4-5 km/s and reveal shear modulus variations. These waves, generated by natural earthquakes or controlled sources, are modeled using elastic wave equations to simulate rupture dynamics and predict ground motion, as in finite-difference simulations that incorporate attenuation and anisotropy.
Guided acoustic waves, such as Lamb waves in thin plates and rods, are particularly useful for long-range inspection in solids due to their multimodal propagation confined by boundaries. Lamb waves, symmetric and antisymmetric modes, disperse with frequency and thickness, allowing sensitivity to defects at various depths; for instance, the A0 mode is effective for detecting surface cracks in aluminum plates up to several meters away. In rods, torsional and extensional guided waves similarly enable efficient energy transmission over distances, minimizing attenuation compared to bulk waves.
A key application is structural health monitoring in bridges, where piezoelectric transducers are embedded or surface-mounted to continuously generate and detect guided waves for real-time flaw detection. For example, in steel girder bridges, Lamb wave arrays identify corrosion or fatigue cracks by analyzing changes in wave scattering patterns, with systems achieving detection ranges of 10-20 meters and accuracies better than 5% in damage localization. This approach enhances safety by enabling early intervention without disassembly.

Sensing and Imaging

Acoustic waves play a crucial role in sensing and imaging technologies by enabling the detection, measurement, and visualization of physical phenomena through pressure variations in media. Acoustic sensors convert these mechanical waves into electrical signals, facilitating applications from sound recording to underwater exploration. In imaging, acoustic waves allow non-invasive probing of internal structures by exploiting wave propagation, reflection, and scattering properties.
Microphones serve as fundamental acoustic sensors, typically employing a diaphragm that responds to pressure changes from incident sound waves, which deflects to modulate an electrical output via capacitive, piezoelectric, or resistive mechanisms. For instance, condenser microphones use a charged diaphragm and backplate to produce a voltage proportional to acoustic pressure, offering high sensitivity for audio applications. Hydrophones, designed for underwater environments, similarly detect pressure waves but are optimized for low-frequency, high-pressure aquatic propagation, often using piezoelectric ceramics like lead zirconate titanate (PZT) to transduce signals in sonar systems. These sensors are essential for marine mammal monitoring and submarine detection, with sensitivities reaching -200 dB re 1 V/Pa.
Pulse-echo ultrasound represents a primary imaging modality, where short acoustic pulses are transmitted into a medium, and echoes from interfaces or scatterers are received to reconstruct spatial information, forming B-mode scans that display amplitude as grayscale intensity. This technique operates at frequencies from 1 to 20 MHz in medical contexts, achieving resolutions down to 0.1 mm for soft tissue visualization. Doppler ultrasound extends this by measuring frequency shifts in backscattered waves to quantify motion, such as blood flow velocity, calculated as $ v = \frac{(f_r - f_0) c}{2 f_0 \cos \theta} $, where $ f_r $ is the received frequency, $ f_0 $ the transmitted frequency, $ c $ the speed of sound, and $ \theta $ the angle of insonation; this enables real-time vascular assessments with accuracies better than 10% for peak velocities up to 2 m/s. Photoacoustic imaging combines optical excitation with acoustic detection, wherein laser pulses absorbed by tissues generate thermoelastic waves that propagate and are captured to map optical absorption, providing high-contrast images of hemoglobin distribution in oncology at depths up to 5 cm.
Array processing enhances acoustic sensing and imaging through beamforming, where multiple transducers are arranged to steer and focus waves directionally, improving signal-to-noise ratios by 10-20 dB in noisy environments. In phased-array ultrasound probes, time delays are applied to elements to form synthetic apertures, enabling 3D volumetric imaging with frame rates exceeding 50 Hz. Practical examples include fetal ultrasound, which uses 2-5 MHz probes for real-time B-mode and Doppler assessment of cardiac activity and amniotic fluid, detecting anomalies with over 95% sensitivity in routine obstetrics. In industrial settings, acoustic wave imaging via ultrasonic testing identifies flaws like cracks in metals, employing pulse-echo from arrays to achieve defect resolutions of 1 mm in weld inspections, critical for aerospace safety.
