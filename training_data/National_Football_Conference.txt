National Football Conference
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The National Football Conference (NFC) is one of the two principal conferences in the National Football League (NFL), the premier professional American football league in the United States, consisting of 16 teams organized into four geographic divisions of four teams each. It was formally established in 1970, as part of the merger between the original NFL and the rival American Football League (AFL), with the NFC absorbing the bulk of the pre-merger NFL franchises to preserve historical rivalries and competitive balance.
The NFC's structure promotes regional competition and balanced scheduling, with divisions named East, North, South, and West; each team plays a 17-game regular season schedule, including games against divisional opponents, other conference teams, and select inter-conference matchups. The conference's divisions and current teams are as follows:

NFC East: Dallas Cowboys, New York Giants, Philadelphia Eagles, Washington Commanders

NFC North: Chicago Bears, Detroit Lions, Green Bay Packers, Minnesota Vikings

NFC South: Atlanta Falcons, Carolina Panthers, New Orleans Saints, Tampa Bay Buccaneers

NFC West: Arizona Cardinals, Los Angeles Rams, San Francisco 49ers, Seattle Seahawks

At the conclusion of the regular season, the top six NFC teamscomprising the four division winners and two wild-card qualifiersadvance to the playoffs, culminating in the NFC Championship Game, whose victor competes against the American Football Conference (AFC) champion in the Super Bowl for the NFL title. Since its inception, the NFC has been a powerhouse in Super Bowl history, with its teams securing 28 victories as of the 2024 season, highlighting franchises like the San Francisco 49ers (five titles).

Overview and Formation

Establishment in NFL Merger

The merger between the National Football League (NFL) and the American Football League (AFL), which led to the creation of the National Football Conference (NFC), stemmed from negotiations that culminated in an agreement on June 8, 1966. This pact, announced by NFL Commissioner Pete Rozelle and AFL founder Lamar Hunt, aimed to end the costly bidding war for players and stabilize professional football by integrating the two rival leagues into a single entity. Congress provided the necessary antitrust exemption through Public Law 89-800, signed by President Lyndon B. Johnson on October 21, 1966, allowing the merger to proceed without legal challenges under federal competition laws.
The merger was fully implemented in 1970, marking the official unification of the leagues for the upcoming season, with the first combined regular-season schedule commencing that fall. As part of the restructuring, the new NFL was divided into two conferences of equal size to promote balanced competition: the American Football Conference (AFC), comprising all 10 former AFL teams plus three established NFL franchisesthe Cleveland Browns, Baltimore Colts, and Pittsburgh Steelersand the NFC, formed by the remaining 13 original NFL teams. This division preserved the historical identities of the leagues while ensuring parity, as the reassigned teams helped equalize the conferences at 13 teams each.
The primary purposes of establishing the NFC through this merger were to integrate the AFL's innovative elements, such as wider passing rules and faster-paced play, with the NFL's established structure; to eliminate duplicative franchises in the same markets; and to create a streamlined path to a unified championship via the Super Bowl, which had already been introduced experimentally in 1967. By fostering inter-conference rivalry while maintaining intra-conference scheduling, the setup enhanced overall league competitiveness and fan engagement, laying the foundation for the modern NFL's growth into a dominant sports entity.

Organizational Structure and Divisions

The National Football Conference (NFC) is governed by the NFL Commissioner, who serves as the league's chief executive officer and holds ultimate authority over operations for both the NFC and the American Football Conference (AFC). Conference-specific decisions, such as those related to scheduling rotations and playoff seeding, are handled through NFL committees like the Competition Committee, where proposals require approval by at least 75% of the league's 32 team owners, including those from the NFC.
The NFC consists of 16 teams organized into four geographic divisions: East, North, South, and West, with each division containing four teams to foster regional rivalries and balanced competition. This divisional structure was implemented during the 2002 NFL realignment, which accommodated the addition of the 32nd team and reconfigured both conferences into eight divisions of four teams each, replacing the prior uneven setup to enhance scheduling equity and playoff access.
Scheduling within the NFC adheres to the NFL's uniform formula, ensuring a mix of intra-conference and inter-conference matchups over the 17-game regular season. Each NFC team plays its three division rivals twiceonce at home and once on the roadfor a total of six games, emphasizing divisional intensity. Teams then face four opponents from one rotating NFC division (two home, two away) and one game each against two teams from the other NFC divisions, selected based on the prior season's division rankings to alternate home/away advantages. This intra-conference rotation guarantees that every NFC team competes against every other NFC team at least once every three years.
To resolve ties for NFC division winners or wild card berths, the NFL employs a sequential tiebreaker system prioritizing direct competition and performance context. For two teams tied in a division race, the primary criterion is head-to-head winning percentage; if unresolved, it proceeds to division games winning percentage, common opponents' winning percentage, and conference games winning percentage. For wild card contenders from different divisions, the process begins with conference winning percentage, followed by common games (minimum four shared opponents), ensuring ties reflect broader competitive strength without favoring geography. In cases involving three or more tied teams, division-specific tiebreakers are applied first to rank and eliminate clubs before advancing to wild card procedures.

Current Teams

Division Breakdown

The National Football Conference (NFC) is divided into four divisions: East, North, South, and West, each containing four teams that compete for divisional titles and playoff berths.

NFC East

The NFC East features longstanding rivalries among its teams, based in the northeastern and mid-Atlantic regions. The Dallas Cowboys, founded in 1960 and based in Dallas, Texas, play their home games at AT&T Stadium. The New York Giants, established in 1925 and located in East Rutherford, New Jersey, share MetLife Stadium with the New York Jets. The Philadelphia Eagles, founded in 1933 in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, host games at Lincoln Financial Field. The Washington Commanders, originating in 1932 and representing the Washington, D.C., area from their home in Landover, Maryland, play at Commanders Field.

NFC North

Known as one of the NFC's most competitive divisions, the NFC North includes teams from the Midwest with a mix of historic franchises and modern facilities. The Chicago Bears, founded in 1919 in Chicago, Illinois, play at Soldier Field, the oldest stadium in the NFL. The Detroit Lions, established in 1930 in Detroit, Michigan, use Ford Field as their home venue. The Green Bay Packers, also founded in 1919 and based in Green Bay, Wisconsin, are renowned for their community-owned structure and play at Lambeau Field. The Minnesota Vikings, founded in 1961 in Minneapolis, Minnesota, compete at U.S. Bank Stadium.

NFC South

The NFC South encompasses teams from the southeastern United States, characterized by warm-weather venues and rapid growth in fan bases. The Atlanta Falcons, founded in 1966 in Atlanta, Georgia, play at Mercedes-Benz Stadium. The Carolina Panthers, established in 1995 and based in Charlotte, North Carolina, host games at Bank of America Stadium. The New Orleans Saints, founded in 1967 in New Orleans, Louisiana, use Caesars Superdome as their home. The Tampa Bay Buccaneers, originating in 1976 in Tampa, Florida, play at Raymond James Stadium.

NFC West

Spanning the western United States, the NFC West features a blend of the league's oldest team and recent high-tech stadiums, fostering intense regional matchups. The Arizona Cardinals, founded in 1898 and located in Glendale, Arizona, play at State Farm Stadium. The Los Angeles Rams, established in 1936 in Los Angeles, California, share SoFi Stadium with the Los Angeles Chargers. The San Francisco 49ers, founded in 1946 and based in Santa Clara, California, compete at Levi's Stadium. The Seattle Seahawks, founded in 1976 in Seattle, Washington, and realigned to the NFC in 2002, play at Lumen Field.

Team Relocations and Recent Changes

The 2002 NFL realignment significantly altered the composition of the National Football Conference by shifting the Seattle Seahawks from the AFC West to the NFC West, creating a more geographically cohesive division that included the Seahawks alongside the San Francisco 49ers, St. Louis Rams, and Arizona Cardinals. This move was part of the league's broader restructuring to accommodate 32 teams following the addition of the Houston Texans in the AFC, resulting in eight four-team divisions across both conferences. Concurrently, the realignment introduced the NFC South division, comprising the Atlanta Falcons, Carolina Panthers, New Orleans Saints, and Tampa Bay Buccaneers, which better aligned southern-based franchises and enhanced regional rivalries.
The Arizona Cardinals, having relocated from St. Louis to Phoenix in 1988 and rebranded as the Arizona Cardinals in 1994, experienced relative stability in their market post-2000 but were repositioned from the NFC East to the NFC West during the 2002 realignment, placing them in competition with West Coast teams for the first time in decades. Since then, the Cardinals have remained in the Phoenix metropolitan area, with no further relocations, solidifying their presence in the Southwest despite ongoing stadium upgrades, such as the move to State Farm Stadium in Glendale in 2006.
A notable relocation within the NFC occurred in 2016 when the Rams franchise returned to Los Angeles after 21 seasons in St. Louis, approved by NFL owners on January 12 of that year to share a new stadium with the Chargers, thereby reestablishing an NFC West team in the nation's second-largest media market. This move ended the Rams' tenure in St. Louis, where they had won Super Bowl XXXIV in 1999, and prompted a $790 million settlement in 2021 between the NFL, Rams ownership, and St. Louis interests over relocation guidelines.
No new NFC teams have been added through expansion since the Carolina Panthers joined in 1995, maintaining the conference at 16 franchises as of 2025, though league discussions have explored potential growth tied to international markets, including up to 10 overseas games by 2026 and expanded global marketing for existing teams like the Cardinals in Canada.
These changes have notably intensified intra-division rivalries, particularly between the Seahawks and Rams, who have met twice annually since 2002, fostering a competitive dynamic highlighted by the Seahawks' nine NFC West titles in that span compared to the Rams' five.

Historical Development

Early Years and Expansions

Following the 1970 NFL-AFL merger, the National Football Conference (NFC) began operations with 13 teams organized into three divisions: the East (Dallas Cowboys, New York Giants, Philadelphia Eagles, St. Louis Cardinals, and Washington Redskins), the Central (Chicago Bears, Detroit Lions, Green Bay Packers, and Minnesota Vikings), and the West (Atlanta Falcons, Los Angeles Rams, New Orleans Saints, and San Francisco 49ers). This structure aimed to maintain geographical and competitive balance amid the integration of former rival league teams, though challenges arose in scheduling and realignment to ensure even competition across conferences.
The inaugural NFC Championship Game, held on January 3, 1971, for the 1970 season, featured the Dallas Cowboys defeating the San Francisco 49ers 17-10 at Kezar Stadium in San Francisco, marking the conference's first postseason title contest under the new format. This game highlighted early integration hurdles, as the merger required blending playing styles, player personnel from both leagues, and divisional alignments to prevent imbalances, with the NFC drawing primarily from established NFL franchises. Over the initial years, the conference adapted through minor adjustments, but competitive parity remained a focus as former AFL influences permeated the broader league.
In 1976, the NFL expanded to 28 teams by adding the Seattle Seahawks and Tampa Bay Buccaneers. The Seahawks were placed in the NFC West (finishing 2-12), while the Buccaneers competed in the AFC West (posting a 0-14 record). To achieve better balance, the teams swapped conferences prior to the 1977 season, permanently placing the Seahawks in the AFC West and the Buccaneers in the NFC Central, which expanded that division to five teams. This realignment helped distribute talent and geography more evenly, contributing to the NFC's growth while addressing post-merger disparities in division sizesthe East and Central now at five teams each, and the West at four.
The NFC continued its expansion in the 1990s, adding the Carolina Panthers to the NFC West in 1995 as part of the league's growth to 30 teams, while the Jacksonville Jaguars joined the AFC Central. The Panthers, awarded the franchise on October 26, 1993, debuted with a 7-9 record under head coach Dom Capers, quickly establishing competitiveness in their new division alongside established West teams like the Rams, 49ers, Saints, and Falcons. This addition temporarily enlarged the NFC West to five teams, reflecting ongoing efforts to enhance regional representation and fan engagement in the Southeast without disrupting the conference's foundational structure from the merger era.

Realignments and Conference Shifts

Following the 1970 AFL-NFL merger, the National Football Conference was initially structured with three uneven divisions to preserve pre-merger rivalries and regional alignments. The NFC East consisted of five teams: the Dallas Cowboys, New York Giants, Philadelphia Eagles, St. Louis Cardinals, and Washington Redskins. The NFC Central included four teams: the Chicago Bears, Detroit Lions, Green Bay Packers, and Minnesota Vikings. The NFC West had four teams: the Atlanta Falcons, Los Angeles Rams, New Orleans Saints, and San Francisco 49ers.
Adjustments in the late 1970s addressed expansion and conference balance. In 1977, the Tampa Bay Buccaneers, who had joined as an expansion team in the AFC West in 1976, switched conferences and were placed in the NFC Central to even out divisional sizes and incorporate the new franchise into a competitive Midwestern grouping, joining the Bears, Lions, Packers, and Vikings. Other shifts during this period, such as the St. Louis Cardinals' continued placement in the NFC East from their pre-merger roots, aimed to maintain established Eastern rivalries while adapting to the league's growth. These changes from 1977 to 2001 focused on incremental balancing without major overhauls.
The 1995 expansion introduced temporary realignments to accommodate new teams. The Carolina Panthers, entering as an expansion franchise, were assigned to the NFC West alongside the 49ers, Rams, Saints, and Falcons, creating a five-team division to test competitive dynamics in the southeastern and western regions before further restructuring. This placement was provisional, reflecting the league's strategy to integrate expansions without immediately disrupting longstanding divisional identities.
The most significant realignment occurred in 2002, coinciding with the addition of the Houston Texans and expanding the league to 32 teams. The NFC shifted to four equal divisions of four teams each: the NFC North (Bears, Lions, Packers, Vikings), NFC East (Cowboys, Eagles, Giants, Redskins), NFC South (Falcons, Panthers, Saints, Buccaneers), and NFC West (49ers, Rams, Seahawks, Cardinals). The Seattle Seahawks fully transitioned from the AFC West back to the NFC West, their original conference upon joining in 1976, to improve geographic cohesion on the West Coast. Meanwhile, the New Orleans Saints moved from the NFC West to the newly formed NFC South to group southern teams together, enhancing regional matchups. This overhaul was driven by goals of geographic balance, preservation of key rivalriessuch as the Bears-Packers in the Northand competitive equity across divisions.

Season and Competition Format

Regular Season Operations

The National Football Conference (NFC) operates within the NFL's regular season framework, where each of its 16 teams plays a 17-game schedule over an 18-week period, a format adopted starting in 2021 following agreement with the NFL Players Association. This structure includes 272 total regular-season games across both conferences, with the NFC's matchups determined by a standardized formula to ensure competitive balance and rotation of opponents. The schedule is released annually in May, with games typically spanning Thursdays, Sundays, and Mondays, though flex scheduling may adjust primetime slots for viewer interest.
NFC teams' opponents are selected via the NFL's scheduling formula, which breaks down as follows: six games against divisional rivals (home and away versus each of the three other teams in their division); four games against all teams in one other NFC division, rotating every three years to cycle through the East, North, South, and West; four games against all teams in one AFC division, rotating every four years; two games against teams from the remaining two NFC divisions, matched by the prior season's divisional finish (e.g., first-place teams play each other); and one additional inter-conference game against an AFC team based on the prior season's standings, with the home conference alternating yearlyin 2025, AFC teams host this matchup. This rotation ensures each NFC team faces every other NFL team at least once every four years, promoting league-wide parity.
Each NFC team receives one bye week for rest and preparation, scheduled between Weeks 5 and 14 to avoid clustering at the season's end, with no byes in the final four weeks to heighten late-season intensity. In 2025, NFC teams are assigned byes variably, such as the Atlanta Falcons and Chicago Bears in Week 5, and the Minnesota Vikings in Week 6, allowing for recovery amid a demanding slate. Home and away designations alternate to balance travel, with NFC teams playing eight regular-season home games in 2025 due to the AFC hosting the extra inter-conference matchup, reversing the prior year's split. International games add logistical variety; in 2025, NFC participants include the Minnesota Vikings (hosting in London and playing in Dublin), Los Angeles Rams (London), Atlanta Falcons (Berlin), and Washington Commanders (Madrid), fulfilling the league's goal of global expansion with seven overseas contests total.
Standings within the NFC are tracked by win-loss records, with tiebreakers including head-to-head results, division record, and conference record to determine divisional winners and overall seeding, directly influencing postseason positioning.

Playoff System and Qualification

The National Football Conference (NFC) playoff system, aligned with the American Football Conference (AFC), determines qualification and advancement for its seven participating teams through a structured process based on regular-season performance. Since the 2020 season, the NFL expanded the postseason to 14 teams total, with seven from each conference, comprising the four division winners and three wild-card teams selected by the best overall records among non-winners. This format ensures broader competition while prioritizing divisional champions, who earn automatic berths regardless of their win-loss record.
Division winners are seeded 1 through 4 based on their regular-season winning percentage, with the No. 1 seed receiving a first-round bye. The three wild-card spots (seeds 5 through 7) go to the NFC teams with the strongest records outside the division winners, irrespective of divisional affiliation; ties for these positions are resolved using a series of criteria, starting with head-to-head results, followed by conference winning percentage, common-game records (minimum four games), strength of victory, and strength of schedule, among others, until a single team advances. For multiple-team ties involving clubs from different divisions, the process first eliminates lower-ranked teams per division before applying the same sequence to determine wild-card order.
The playoffs follow a single-elimination tournament across three rounds within the conference: the wild-card round, divisional round, and conference championship. In the wild-card round, matchups pit the No. 2 seed against the No. 7 seed, No. 3 against No. 6, and No. 4 against No. 5, with all games hosted by the higher-seeded team to provide home-field advantage. The No. 1 seed advances directly to the divisional round, where winners from the wild-card games face the top seeds, again with higher seeds hosting; subsequent rounds maintain this reseeding by seed number. Postseason games cannot end in ties and proceed to overtime if necessary, where each team is guaranteed at least one possession from the 25-yard line; play continues in 15-minute periods until a score occurs, with 2025 rules aligning postseason overtime fully with the regular season by ensuring equal opportunities unless the defense scores on the initial possession via interception return or safety.

Championships and Achievements

NFC Championship Game

The NFC Championship Game is the annual postseason contest that crowns the champion of the National Football Conference (NFC) and earns the victor a berth in the Super Bowl. Following the 1970 merger between the National Football League (NFL) and the American Football League (AFL), the game was formalized as the culminating event in the NFC playoff structure, with the inaugural edition played on January 3, 1971, when the Dallas Cowboys defeated the San Francisco 49ers 1710 at Kezar Stadium in San Francisco. Since its inception, the game has served as a high-stakes showdown, often featuring intense rivalries and defining moments that shape NFL legacies.
The format involves the winners of the NFC's two divisional playoff games facing off, with the higher-seeded team hosting at its home stadium to provide a competitive advantage based on regular-season performance. This structure ensures the game concludes the NFC playoff path, which begins with wild-card matchups and divisional rounds. Neutral sites have not been used historically for the NFC Championship, though early NFL conference games before the merger occasionally employed them, such as at Tulane Stadium for select finals in the 1960s.
Notable aspects include the San Francisco 49ers holding the record for most appearances with 19 since the merger, while the 49ers and Dallas Cowboys share the mark for most victories with eight each. The first overtime NFC Championship Game occurred in 1998, when the Atlanta Falcons upset the Minnesota Vikings 3027 on a Morten Andersen field goal, marking a dramatic breakthrough for the Falcons franchise. Other overtime thrillers, such as the 2007 New York Giants' 1714 win over the Green Bay Packers and the 2009 Minnesota Vikings' 2831 overtime loss to the New Orleans Saints on a game-winning field goal, highlight the game's potential for sudden-death excitement under NFL postseason overtime rules.
Venue trends have centered on the host team's stadium, fostering passionate home-field atmospheres at iconic sites like Lambeau Field and AT&T Stadium, with no fixed rotation implemented to date. As of 2025, ongoing league discussions propose shifting to neutral sitespotentially rotating among major marketsto boost revenue and test Super Bowl infrastructure, though no changes have been adopted, preserving the traditional higher-seed hosting model.

Super Bowl Success and Rivalries

The National Football Conference (NFC) holds a slight edge in Super Bowl history, with 29 victories compared to the American Football Conference's (AFC) 28 as of the conclusion of Super Bowl LIX. This includes the Philadelphia Eagles' 40-22 triumph over the Kansas City Chiefs in Super Bowl LIX on February 9, 2025, marking the Eagles' second NFC-representing championship and avenging their 2023 loss to the same opponent. Among NFC teams, the San Francisco 49ers lead with five Super Bowl wins (1982, 1985, 1989, 1990, and 1995), tied with the Dallas Cowboys (1972, 1978, 1993, 1994, and 1996) for the conference record. These successes underscore the NFC's competitive depth, with 11 different franchises securing at least one Super Bowl title under the conference banner.
Notable dynasties have defined eras of NFC dominance, particularly the San Francisco 49ers of the 1980s and early 1990s, who captured four Super Bowls (XVI, XIX, XXIII, and XXIV) under head coach Bill Walsh and later George Seifert, powered by quarterback Joe Montana and wide receiver Jerry Rice. Similarly, the Dallas Cowboys' 1990s run yielded three consecutive Super Bowl appearances and victories in XXVII, XXVIII, and XXX, led by quarterback Troy Aikman, running back Emmitt Smith, and wide receiver Michael Irvin in what is often called the "Triplets" era under coach Jimmy Johnson and later Barry Switzer. These periods not only elevated the NFC's profile but also set benchmarks for offensive innovation and sustained excellence within the conference.
Key rivalries have fueled the NFC's intensity, with the Chicago Bears-Green Bay Packers clash standing as the oldest in NFL history, dating to 1921 and featuring 210 meetings, including multiple playoff showdowns that have shaped divisional lore. In the NFC East, the Philadelphia Eagles-Dallas Cowboys matchup embodies regional ferocity, with 133 games since 1960 marked by high-stakes division battles and recent playoff implications. The NFC West's San Francisco 49ers-Seattle Seahawks rivalry intensified in the 2010s, driven by the Seahawks' "Legion of Boom" defense and quarterbacks like Russell Wilson clashing against the 49ers' resurgence, highlighted by NFC Championship appearances in 2013 and 2019.
In terms of legacy, the NFC has produced 55 conference champions since the 1970 AFL-NFL merger, with the Cowboys and 49ers tied for the most titles at eight each, reflecting the conference's tradition of perennial contenders. NFC Championship Games have also been a proving ground for Pro Football Hall of Famers, including icons like Montana (four NFC titles with the 49ers), Rice (three with San Francisco), and Aikman (three with Dallas), whose performances in these contests contributed to 12 Hall of Fame inductees from the 1980s 49ers dynasty alone.

Branding and Broadcasting

Logos and Visual Identity

The National Football Conference (NFC) adopted its first official logo in 1970, coinciding with the NFL-AFL merger that established the conference structure. This original design featured a dark blue angular "N" in a bold, serif block font, accompanied by three white stars positioned along the diagonal bar of the letter. The stars symbolized the three divisions (Eastern, Central, and Western) in place at the time, while the design drew inspiration from the former American Football League's emblem to reflect the league's unified heritage.
The logo remained largely unchanged from 1970 through 2009, emphasizing simplicity and athletic boldness with a brighter blue hue in early iterations that gradually shifted to a deeper tone. In 2010, as part of an NFL-wide branding refresh following the 2002 realignment to four divisions, the NFC updated its logo by adding a fourth white star to represent the expanded structure (North, South, East, and West). The "N" was slightly enlarged and refined with broader proportions and subtler serifs, rendered in a muted dark blue (hex #043b63) against white, maintaining a metallic sheen for modern appeal while preserving historical elements.
This current design has seen no major alterations as of 2025, underscoring the conference's commitment to enduring visual identity. The NFC logo appears prominently on the NFC Championship Trophy, official merchandise, game broadcasts, and playoff materials, serving as a unifying emblem for its 16 teams. Symbolically, the deep blue evokes tradition, stability, and national pride, while the shield-like form of the "N" and stars convey the conference's competitive strength and divisional balance.

Television and Media Coverage

The National Football Conference (NFC) has been a cornerstone of NFL television coverage since the league's early broadcast era, with its games distributed across major networks under multi-billion-dollar rights agreements. As of the 2023-2033 media rights deal, valued at over $110 billion, Fox holds the primary broadcast rights for NFC regular-season games, a package it has maintained since acquiring it from CBS in 1994 for $4.4 billion over four years, marking a significant shift that elevated Fox's status in sports broadcasting.
Fox's NFC package includes the majority of Sunday afternoon games featuring NFC teams, broadcast nationally and regionally, and generates approximately $2.25 billion annually for the league, underscoring the conference's high commercial value. ESPN/ABC complements this with Monday Night Football, which frequently features NFC matchups as part of its $2.7 billion-per-year agreement, delivering primetime exposure to conference games and contributing to the NFL's overall viewership dominance. These arrangements ensure broad accessibility, with NFC games often leading in regional markets due to the conference's competitive rivalries.
In the playoffs, Fox exclusively covers the NFC side through the divisional and wild-card rounds, culminating in the NFC Championship Game, which it has broadcast since 1994. For Super Bowl LIX in February 2025, Fox aired the event featuring the NFC champion, drawing a record average audience of 127.7 million viewers, highlighting the conference's role in peak television moments. Super Bowl broadcasts rotate among networks, with Fox scheduled for additional NFC-representing Super Bowls in 2029 and 2032 under the current deal.
Internationally, NFC games reach global audiences via DAZN's NFL Game Pass, which streams every regular-season matchup, playoffs, and Super Bowl live outside the United States, Canada, and China as part of a multi-year partnership expanded in 2025 to include enhanced features like multiview for up to four simultaneous games. This service has driven growth in regions like Europe and Asia, with 2025 additions such as localized promotions and integration with streaming platforms like Amazon Prime for select Thursday Night Football games involving NFC teams.
NFC broadcasts consistently achieve high ratings, particularly for marquee events like Thanksgiving Day games, which often feature conference rivalries such as the traditional Detroit Lions matchup and have historically topped charts with audiences exceeding 40 million viewers for NFC-heavy slates. The 2025 season's early weeks averaged 22.3 million viewers per game across networks, with NFC contests benefiting from trends like increased scoring and dramatic finishes that boost engagement.
