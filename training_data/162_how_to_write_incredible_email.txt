Here is exactly how to write an incredible email, step by step, from blank screen to send button.

Step one. Before you type a single word, answer three questions in your head. Who am I writing to? What do I want them to do after reading this? What is the single most important thing I need to communicate? If you cannot answer these three questions clearly, you are not ready to write. Think until you can.

Step two. Write the subject line last, not first. Most people agonize over the subject line before they know what the email says. Write the email first. Then summarize it in the subject line. A good subject line is specific and tells the reader exactly what the email is about. Bad: "Quick question." Good: "Can you review the Johnson proposal by Thursday?" Bad: "Update." Good: "Project Atlas is on track for April 15 launch."

Step three. Open with the point. Do not start with "I hope this email finds you well" followed by three paragraphs of context before you get to what you actually want. The reader should know why you are writing within the first two sentences. "I am writing to request your approval on the revised budget for Q3." "I wanted to share the results of last week's customer survey." "Can we move our Wednesday meeting to Thursday at 2 PM?" Lead with the ask. Context comes after.

Step four. Keep paragraphs to three sentences maximum. Long paragraphs are walls of text that people skip. Short paragraphs are easy to scan. Each paragraph should contain one idea. If you have three things to say, use three paragraphs, not one.

Step five. Use bullet points for lists, options, or action items. If you are presenting three options, do not bury them in a paragraph. List them:
Option A: Extend the deadline by two weeks. Cost: zero dollars. Risk: client may be disappointed.
Option B: Add two contractors. Cost: fifteen thousand dollars. Risk: quality may vary.
Option C: Reduce scope by cutting the reporting module. Cost: zero dollars. Risk: reduced functionality.

Step six. Make action items unmistakable. If you need someone to do something, say exactly what, exactly who, and exactly when. "Please send me the updated figures by Friday at noon" is actionable. "It would be great if we could get those numbers at some point" is not.

Step seven. End with a clear next step. "I will send the revised proposal by Wednesday." "Please confirm by end of day whether you can attend." "Let me know if you have any questions; otherwise I will proceed with Option B." The reader should finish your email knowing exactly what happens next.

Step eight. Read it once from the reader's perspective before sending. Pretend you are the recipient opening this email with fifty others in your inbox. Would you understand the point in ten seconds? Would you know what you need to do? Would you feel respected? If the answer to any of these is no, revise.

Step nine. Cut everything that does not serve the purpose. If a sentence does not inform, persuade, or move the reader toward the action you want, delete it. Brevity is respect. Every unnecessary word is a small tax on the reader's time and attention.

Step ten. Proofread. Spelling errors, grammar mistakes, and formatting inconsistencies undermine your credibility. Read the email slowly. Read it aloud if you can. Check names, dates, numbers, and attachments. One typo in an otherwise excellent email can shift the reader's perception from "this person is competent" to "this person is careless."

Here is a complete example of an incredible email:

Subject: Approval Needed: Q3 Marketing Budget (Revised)

Hi Sandra,

I have revised the Q3 marketing budget based on the feedback from Tuesday's meeting. The total is now two hundred and forty thousand dollars, down from the original two hundred and eighty thousand, a fifteen percent reduction.

Key changes:
Reduced paid social spend by twenty thousand dollars based on diminishing returns data from Q2.
Eliminated the print advertising line item (twelve thousand dollars) per your suggestion.
Added eight thousand dollars for the influencer pilot program that Tom proposed.

I need your approval by Friday so we can lock in the media buys before rates increase on April 1.

The full budget spreadsheet is attached. Happy to walk through any line items on a call if that would be helpful.

Thanks,
James

This email works because it leads with the point, provides specific details in a scannable format, makes a clear request with a deadline and a reason, offers additional support, and respects the reader's time. Total reading time: thirty seconds. Total clarity: one hundred percent. That is an incredible email.
