Central Atlantic magmatic province

 The Central Atlantic Magmatic Province (CAMP) is a vast large igneous province characterized by extensive tholeiitic basalt flows, dike swarms, sills, and layered intrusions, emplaced primarily around 201 million years ago during the Late Triassic to Early Jurassic as the supercontinent Pangea began to rift apart, initiating the formation of the central Atlantic Ocean. Spanning more than 10 million square kilometers across eastern North America, northeastern South America, northwestern Africa, and southwestern Europe in their pre-rift configuration, CAMP represents the most extensive continental flood basalt event in Earth's Phanerozoic history, with an estimated total magmatic volume exceeding 3 million cubic kilometers, of which a significant portion remains as subvolcanic intrusions. Its short-lived eruptive pulses, lasting less than 1 million years with peak activity straddling the Triassic-Jurassic boundary, released massive volumes of carbon dioxide and sulfur dioxide, contributing to global warming, ocean acidification, and the end-Triassic mass extinction that eliminated about 76% of terrestrial and marine species. Geochemically, CAMP rocks are predominantly low-titanium basalts derived from a sublithospheric mantle source, with minor high-titanium variants in some regions, reflecting partial melting influenced by the impingement of a mantle plume or edge-driven convection beneath the thinning lithosphere.
CAMP's formation is intrinsically tied to the tectonic evolution of the Atlantic margins, where rifting progressed from localized extension in rift basins to seafloor spreading, with magmatic activity facilitating lithospheric weakening and continental separation. The province's remnants are best preserved in sedimentary basins such as the Newark Basin in the United States and the High Atlas in Morocco, where interleaved volcanic layers provide detailed records of eruption timing and environmental perturbations. Paleomagnetic and radiometric data confirm a rapid, synchronous emplacement across its full extent, underscoring the role of a large-scale mantle anomaly in driving this geologically instantaneous event. Ongoing research highlights CAMP's influence on subsequent ocean basin development and its implications for understanding links between volcanism, climate, and biodiversity crises in deep time.

Overview

Definition and Characteristics

The Central Atlantic Magmatic Province (CAMP) is defined as the largest known continental large igneous province (LIP), encompassing an estimated area of approximately 11 million km across four continents, including eastern North America, northwestern Africa, and portions of Europe and South America. This extensive province represents a major episode of mafic magmatism that emplaced vast volumes of igneous material during the Late Triassic to Early Jurassic, playing a pivotal role in the geological evolution of the supercontinent Pangaea.
The primary rock type of CAMP is tholeiitic basalt, characterized by low titanium content (low-Ti) in most formations, with minor occurrences of intermediate and high-Ti variants. The total erupted and intrusive volume is estimated at 24 million km, predominantly consisting of intrusive bodies rather than extrusive flows. Key characteristics include thick sequences of flood basalts, extensive dyke swarms (some exceeding 500 km in length), widespread sills, and rare layered intrusions, all formed through short-duration magmatic pulses that facilitated rapid emplacement over regional scales. These features reflect derivation from mantle-derived melts undergoing limited fractional crystallization, resulting in relatively uniform geochemical signatures across the province.
CAMP was historically recognized in the 1980s through geochemical and petrological correlations of basaltic rocks preserved in rift basins of eastern North America and northwestern Africa, building on earlier 1970s studies that noted similarities in composition and age. This synthesis established CAMP as a coherent LIP, with subsequent research refining its extent and linking it to broader tectonic processes such as continental rifting.

Age and Timing

The Central Atlantic Magmatic Province (CAMP) was primarily emplaced at approximately 201 Ma, coinciding with the Triassic-Jurassic boundary, as determined by high-precision U-Pb dating of baddeleyite and zircon crystals from intrusive and extrusive rocks across its extent. This timing places CAMP volcanism as a key event in the breakup of the supercontinent Pangaea, with the main phase of activity constrained to a brief interval near 201.5 Ma based on chemical abrasion isotope dilution thermal ionization mass spectrometry (CA-ID-TIMS) analyses.
High-resolution geochronology reveals four distinct eruptive pulses spanning roughly 600,000 years, each characterized by rapid outpouring of basaltic magmas over durations of less than 100 years. For instance, U-Pb dates from the North Mountain Basalt in Canada yield 201.475 0.026 Ma, while samples from the Senador Pompeu dike in northeastern Brazil date to 201.579 0.057 Ma, highlighting the near-synchronous nature of early pulses across continents. These pulses exhibit a pulsed tempo, with sub-centennial-scale events in the initial phase lasting about 46,000 years, as correlated through astrochronology and U-Pb data from the Fundy and Newark basins.
The short duration of CAMP emplacement underscores its role as a transient geological perturbation, with recent modeling confirming that rapid basalt extrusion and subsequent weathering acted as a net CO2 sink, drawing down atmospheric carbon through enhanced silicate weathering and leading to minor global cooling of 12 C over longer timescales. This geochronological framework also temporally links CAMP's onset to the end-Triassic mass extinction, with the first pulse aligning closely with biotic turnover around 201.51 Ma.

Tectonic and Geological Setting

Relation to Continental Rifting

The Central Atlantic Magmatic Province (CAMP) played a pivotal role in the initial rifting of the supercontinent Pangaea during the Late Triassic to Early Jurassic, marking the onset of the Central Atlantic Ocean's opening around 201 Ma. This extensive magmatism, spanning North America, northwest Africa, and parts of South America, coincided with the diachronic breakup of Pangaea, where extensional tectonics transitioned from intracontinental rifting to seafloor spreading. The timing of CAMP's emplacement, constrained by high-precision U-Pb dating to a brief pulse of less than 1 million years, aligns closely with the T-J boundary and the earliest marine transgressions in rift basins, underscoring its influence on continental fragmentation.
CAMP's volcanic and intrusive features exhibit strong spatial correlation with major rift basins along the pre-Atlantic margins of Pangaea. In eastern North America, the Newark Basin preserves thick sequences of CAMP basalts interbedded with synrift sediments, indicating eruption along the basin's border faults during active extension. Similarly, in northwest Africa, the Argana Basin in Morocco contains CAMP flows overlying Triassic red beds, with the volcanics concentrated near reactivated Variscan faults that facilitated rifting. These alignments highlight how CAMP magmatism exploited pre-existing crustal weaknesses, synchronizing with the development of half-graben structures across the conjugate margins.
Evidence from fault patterns and basin sedimentation further demonstrates that CAMP magmatism preceded and facilitated the progression to seafloor spreading. In the northern rift segments, such as the Fundy and Newark basins, fault reactivation and accelerated subsidence rates are recorded in the sedimentary record immediately following the main eruptive phase, with a notable hiatus in deposition attributed to volcanic loading and uplift. Southern basins, like those in the High Atlas of Morocco, show earlier rifting initiation around 230 Ma without significant volcanism, but CAMP's arrival synchronized fault propagation and basin inversion, bridging the transition to oceanic crust formation by approximately 175 Ma. This sequence implies that magmatism weakened the lithosphere, promoting strain localization along rift axes.
The enormous volume of CAMP magma, estimated at over 3 10^6 km across its full extent, profoundly influenced crustal thinning and rifting dynamics by inducing thermal erosion and mechanical weakening of the lower crust. In rift basins, this led to localized underplating and intrusion-driven extension, with crustal thicknesses reduced to 30-35 km beneath areas of peak magmatism, compared to unthinned continental crust. Such effects amplified extensional stresses, accelerating the rift-to-drift transition and contributing to the asymmetric development of the Central Atlantic margins.

Mantle Plume Involvement

The leading hypothesis for the origin of the Central Atlantic Magmatic Province (CAMP) posits that a large mantle plume head impinged on the base of the lithosphere beneath the supercontinent Pangaea around 201 Ma, triggering widespread magmatism and contributing to continental rifting. This model, initially proposed for large igneous provinces (LIPs), suggests that the plume head, approximately 2000 km in diameter and 100200C hotter than ambient mantle, generated extensive decompression melting upon arrival.
Petrological and geochemical evidence supports plume involvement through indications of elevated mantle temperatures and primitive mantle signatures in CAMP magmas. Primary basaltic melts from CAMP exhibit high mantle potential temperatures of 14501550C, exceeding those of mid-ocean ridge basalts by 100150C, consistent with thermal anomalies from a deep plume source. Trace element enrichments, such as high abundances of highly siderophile elements (e.g., elevated Os relative to mid-ocean ridge basalts) and chondritic to slightly suprachondritic Re/Os ratios (initial 187Os/188Os 0.13, Os +3), point to derivation from a minimally depleted, plume-like mantle reservoir rather than recycled or lithospheric sources.
Seismic tomography reveals potential remnants of plume-related structures in the modern upper mantle beneath the central Atlantic, including low-velocity zones aligned with ancient rifting loci and extending to depths of ~1000 km, suggesting lingering thermal or compositional heterogeneity from the CAMP event. Recent 2025 studies on lithosphere-magmatism interactions reaffirm the plume model as dominant, with geochemical similarities between CAMP dikes and distal volcanic lines indicating a shared deep asthenospheric source influenced by plume upwelling. Recent studies as of 2025 suggest possible extensions of CAMP magmatism eastward to the proto-Cameroon Line, with geochemical similarities supporting a shared mantle source.
Alternative explanations, such as edge-driven convection at lithospheric boundaries or delamination-induced upwelling, have been proposed to account for the localized and short-lived nature of CAMP magmatism without invoking a deep plume. These models suggest that thermal insulation beneath Pangaea or edge effects at craton margins could elevate mantle temperatures modestly (~50100C) and trigger melting, but they struggle to explain the volume and synchronicity of CAMP eruptions as effectively as the plume hypothesis.

Magmatic Features

Extrusive Volcanism

The extrusive volcanism of the Central Atlantic Magmatic Province (CAMP) primarily consists of continental flood basalts erupted during the Late Triassic to Early Jurassic, forming extensive lava flow fields across the supercontinent Pangaea. These basalts represent the surface manifestations of massive magmatic activity linked to the initiation of rifting that led to the breakup of Pangaea. The eruptions were predominantly effusive, producing low-viscosity tholeiitic lavas that spread over vast areas in rift basins, with preserved sequences reaching thicknesses of up to 500 meters in some locations. Stacked flow units, often separated by thin paleosols or sedimentary interbeds, document multiple eruptive pulses over a geologically brief period of less than 1 million years.
CAMP extrusives are organized into four main formations corresponding to distinct magmatic pulses, with well-preserved examples in rift basins of eastern North America, northwest Africa, and northern South America. In Morocco's Central High Atlas and Rif regions, these include the Lower Formation (e.g., Talassemtane unit in the Rif, 55173 m thick with 29 flows), Intermediate Formation (up to 130 m thick), Upper Formation (1576 m thick), and Recurrent Formation (550 m thick). Equivalents in eastern North America, such as the Orange Mountain Basalt within the Newark Supergroup (part of the Watchung sequence), exhibit similar stratigraphy and geochemistry, with the Orange Mountain flow representing the initial pulse at approximately 100150 m thick. In northern South America, correlative basalts occur in the Parnaba and Solimes basins, sharing chemical affinities with the Moroccan and North American sequences, though preservation is more fragmentary due to erosion and overprinting by younger events. These formations indicate synchronized eruptions across continents, with magma flows potentially connected over hundreds of kilometers via subaerial pathways. Recent U-Pb geochronology confirms the rapid, synchronous nature of these pulses around 201 Ma.
The eruption styles were dominantly effusive, characterized by compound and simple phoehoe flows formed at low effusion rates (0.110 m/s per fissure segment), with rare rubbly phoehoe or a flows indicating occasional higher-viscosity or faster emplacement conditions. Individual flows typically range from 530 m thick, with inflated toes and lobate margins common in phoehoe types, while pillow lavas appear locally where flows entered shallow water bodies. The Newark Supergroup in eastern North America preserves an estimated 30,000 km of these basaltic flows, fed by underlying dikes and sills, highlighting the scale of regional flood basalt provinces within CAMP. Overall, while intrusive volumes dominate the total CAMP magma budget (exceeding 2 10 km), the extrusives underscore the province's role as one of the largest continental flood basalt events, with original erupted volumes likely substantially greater than preserved remnants due to erosion.

Intrusive Bodies

The intrusive bodies of the Central Atlantic Magmatic Province (CAMP) primarily consist of dykes and sills, which form the subsurface framework of this large igneous province. Dykes, often organized into radial swarms, can extend up to 800 km in length and reach widths of up to 100 meters, as exemplified by prominent swarms in the eastern North American rift basins and along the West African margin. These discordant intrusions served as conduits for magma transport, with some feeding overlying extrusive flows in a limited capacity. Sills, in contrast, are concordant sheet-like bodies intruded parallel to sedimentary layering, achieving thicknesses of hundreds of meters and covering vast areas; notable examples include the voluminous sills in the Amazon and Solimes basins of South America, where individual complexes can exceed 100,000 km in volume.
Layered intrusions represent a smaller but significant subset of CAMP's plutonic features, characterized by cumulate textures resulting from crystal settling in magma chambers. The Freetown Layered Complex in Sierra Leone stands out as a well-preserved example, 65 km long by 14 km wide (910 km) and up to 7 km thick, displaying rhythmic layering of ultramafic and mafic rocks formed through repeated magma replenishment and differentiation. Such bodies, though rare compared to dykes and sills, provide insights into the crystallization processes within shallow crustal reservoirs during CAMP emplacement.
These intrusions played a critical role in altering the surrounding sedimentary basins through thermal effects. The emplacement of hot magma into organic-rich sediments induced contact metamorphism, leading to the generation and release of volatiles such as methane and sulfur compounds from heated kerogen. This process is particularly evident in rift basins like the Newark Basin in North America and the Argana Basin in Morocco, where aureoles of baked sediments extend several kilometers from sill contacts. Overall volume estimates indicate that intrusive bodies account for approximately two-thirds of CAMP's total magma budget, estimated at 34 million km, underscoring their dominance over extrusive components in the province's magmatic architecture.

Geographical Extent

Eastern North America

The Central Atlantic Magmatic Province (CAMP) in eastern North America is primarily manifested within a series of rift basins that formed during the initial stages of Pangea breakup, including the Fundy, Hartford, and Newark basins. These basins contain thick sequences of Late Triassic to Early Jurassic sedimentary and volcanic rocks, with total fills reaching up to approximately 10 km in thickness, comprising interbedded continental sediments and CAMP-related igneous units. The Newark Basin, one of the largest, preserves over 7 km of strata in its deeper sections, while the Fundy Basin in Nova Scotia exhibits similar substantial accumulation.
The initial volcanic activity in these basins is represented by the Orange Mountain Basalt in the Newark Basin and its equivalents, such as the North Mountain Basalt in the Fundy Basin, which mark the onset of extrusive CAMP magmatism around 201 Ma. These basaltic flows, part of the first eruptive pulse, overlie Triassic sediments and are interbedded with early Jurassic strata, indicating rapid emplacement during continental rifting. High-precision U-Pb dating of zircons from these units yields ages of approximately 201.475 0.014 Ma for the North Mountain Basalt, confirming its role as an early CAMP expression.
Intrusive features, including extensive dyke swarms, further characterize CAMP in eastern North America. The Fundy dyke swarm, oriented predominantly NE-SW, radiates from the Fundy Basin and extends across Nova Scotia and adjacent regions, serving as feeders for the associated lava flows and sills. These dykes, composed of tholeiitic basalt, reflect the regional stress field during rifting and are part of broader intrusive networks within the basins.
Recent geochronological studies, including 2023 U-Pb analyses, have refined the timing of CAMP events in eastern North America, demonstrating synchronicity across the Fundy, Hartford, and Newark basins within analytical uncertainties of less than 100 ka. These dates, derived from baddeleyite and zircon in sills and flows, align the North American manifestations with the broader CAMP event at ~201.5 Ma, supporting a coordinated magmatic onset linked to the end-Triassic extinction. Magnetostratigraphic correlations further tie these units to global polarity records.

Northwest Africa

In northwest Africa, the Central Atlantic Magmatic Province (CAMP) is prominently exposed in Morocco, where it manifests primarily through extensive basalt flows, sills, and dykes emplaced into Mesozoic rift basins along the future Atlantic margin. The key depositional basins include the Argana Valley in the western High Atlas and the broader High Atlas chain, which preserve some of the most complete stratigraphic records of CAMP volcanism, with lava sequences reaching thicknesses of up to 300 meters in the Central High Atlas. These basins formed as intracontinental rifts during the Late Triassic, providing sedimentary substrates for CAMP intrusions and extrusions dated to approximately 201 Ma.
The volcanic succession in these regions features compound pahoehoe lava flows interbedded with minor sedimentary layers, particularly in the INTERCAL zone of the High Atlas, where the Talassemtane Formation represents intermediate-composition flows derived from parental basalts with evidence of crustal contamination. Sills, typically 1050 meters thick, intrude the underlying Triassic red-bed sediments of the Argana Group, which consist of fluvial and playa deposits rich in iron oxides. These intrusions induced contact metamorphism, resulting in widespread bleaching of the red beds through reduction of hematite to magnetite via hydrothermal fluids and organic matter interactions, altering the characteristic reddish hues to gray or green in proximity to the sills.
Geochemical analyses of Moroccan CAMP rocks, including those from the 2019 study on platinum-group elements in High Atlas flood basalts, confirm their predominantly low-titanium tholeiitic compositions, with silica contents of 4552 wt% and magnesium numbers around 0.50.6, indicative of 510% partial melting of an enriched peridotite mantle source. These compositions, coupled with elevated incompatible trace elements like Nb/Ta ratios >15, support links to sublithospheric processes, potentially involving a mantle plume, as evidenced by Os isotopic ratios (Os up to +200) suggesting recycled ancient crust in the source.
Palynological records from sections in the High Atlas and Argana basins reveal a distinctive fern spikedominated by trilete spores of Cyathidites and other pteridophyte taxaimmediately overlying the earliest CAMP flows, signaling a post-eruptive ecological collapse and recovery phase marked by opportunistic fern proliferation amid angiosperm decline. Isotopic data unique to these Moroccan profiles, including C excursions of -2 to -4 in organic matter below the flows and Sr-Nd-Pb systematics (e.g., Nd ~ -5 to +2), further highlight localized crustal assimilation and mantle heterogeneity not fully replicated elsewhere in CAMP. Such signatures correlate broadly with tholeiitic trends observed province-wide, underscoring shared petrogenetic origins.

South America

The Central Atlantic Magmatic Province (CAMP) in South America is primarily represented by intrusive features in northeastern Brazil, particularly within the Borborema Province, where mafic dykes and sills intrude Precambrian basement and overlying Paleozoic sediments. These structures form part of the easternmost exposures of CAMP on the continent, spanning northeastern Brazil for approximately 800 km. The Senador Pompeu dike swarm, a prominent example, stretches about 360 km, with roughly 295 km hosted in crystalline basement rocks and 65 km in sedimentary cover, reflecting extensional tectonics associated with the initial rifting of Pangea.
High-precision geochronology confirms the timing of these intrusions, with a CA-ID-TIMS U-Pb zircon age of 201.579 0.057 Ma obtained from the Senador Pompeu dike, aligning closely with global CAMP pulses and the end-Triassic extinction event at approximately 201.51 Ma. Additional dykes and sills occur along the southeastern and northeastern borders of the Parnaba Basin, where sills reach thicknesses up to 500 m and exhibit tholeiitic compositions typical of CAMP magmatism. In the Recife region of Pernambuco state, within the Borborema Province, similar NNW-trending dykes intrude Neoproterozoic to Paleozoic sequences, contributing to the province's ~2000 km inland penetration from the modern Atlantic margin.
Although the Paran-Etendeka large igneous province in southern Brazil shares a Gondwanan structural inheritance with CAMP, its younger age (~132 Ma) distinguishes it as a separate event, with only debated links to potential early precursors in the region. Recent high-precision dating has clarified distinctions between CAMP intrusives and later Cretaceous features now attributed to the Equatorial Atlantic Magmatic Province (EQUAMP), an intrusive LIP tied to ongoing South Atlantic opening. These South American CAMP elements connect briefly to conjugate African margins across the Atlantic, mirroring dyke orientations from rifting. Exposures in the So Francisco Basin remain limited, with minor tholeiitic intrusions potentially influenced by CAMP-related extension, though not as extensively documented as in the northeast.

Other Regions

Remnants of the Central Atlantic Magmatic Province (CAMP) occur in Europe, primarily as intrusive features along the margins of the proto-Atlantic. In the Iberian Peninsula, the Messejana-Plasencia dyke swarm represents one of the most prominent expressions, consisting of tholeiitic dolerite dykes extending over 500 km from Portugal to Spain, with thicknesses up to 100 m and dated to approximately 200 Ma via U-Pb zircon geochronology. These dykes exhibit geochemical affinities to low-Ti basalts typical of CAMP, including high FeO and low CaO contents, indicating derivation from an enriched mantle source. Further north, in Portugal's Algarve Basin, CAMP-related sills and dykes intrude Mesozoic sediments, providing paleomagnetic evidence that aligns with the province's overall magnetic polarity sequence.
Offshore, the Equatorial Atlantic Magmatic Province (EQUAMP), a later magmatic event related to Atlantic opening, comprises extensive sill complexes and dyke swarms beneath the sedimentary cover of the Equatorial Atlantic margins. High-precision U-Pb dating from 2025 establishes EQUAMP intrusions at 133-126 Ma, postdating main CAMP activity but linked to continued rifting of West Gondwana, with volumes estimated at over 100,000 km based on geophysical modeling. This province correlates temporally with the Paran-Etendeka event, suggesting a prolonged magmatic pulse during the South Atlantic opening, as evidenced by seismic reflection profiles revealing layered intrusive bodies up to 5 km thick.
In West Africa, beyond the primary Moroccan and Malian exposures, scattered CAMP sills intrude Precambrian basement and sedimentary basins, notably in Guinea where tholeiitic sills up to 50 m thick yield Pb isotopic ratios (e.g., 206Pb/204Pb 18.5) matching those from coeval North American and African CAMP sites. These intrusions, dated to ~200 Ma, occur as isolated bodies within the Fouta Djallon region, reflecting localized emplacement during the initial Pangea breakup.
Potential connections to the Caribbean plate arise from the tectonic framework of CAMP rifting, where early Jurassic extension along the proto-Caribbean margins may have incorporated distal CAMP melts into the evolving plate boundary, though direct magmatic links remain debated due to subsequent subduction and alteration.
Preservation of CAMP features in these peripheral regions is limited by post-emplacement erosion, with estimates suggesting up to 5 km of section removed in parts of Iberia and West Africa since the Jurassic, obscuring surface expressions. Seismic data reveal hidden intrusive volumes, such as subsurface sills exceeding 10,000 km in the Equatorial Atlantic, inferred from velocity anomalies and reflection patterns that indicate unexposed magmatic underplating. These geophysical signatures support debates on the total erupted versus intruded proportions, with erosion biases potentially underestimating the province's global extent by 20-30%.

Petrology and Geochemistry

Rock Compositions

The rocks of the Central Atlantic Magmatic Province (CAMP) consist predominantly of tholeiitic basalts, subdivided into low-Ti and high-Ti varieties based on TiO content, with low-Ti types comprising over 90% of the preserved volume. Low-Ti basalts exhibit TiO concentrations below 2 wt%, typically ranging from 0.5 to 1.6 wt% across subgroups such as the Prevalent-CAMP (1.01.3 wt%), Tiourjdal (1.31.5 wt%), and Holyoke (0.81.0 wt%) formations, and are widespread in eastern North America and northwest Africa. High-Ti basalts, geographically restricted to northeastern Brazil and parts of West Africa, show TiO values exceeding 2.1 wt%, often 3.04.0 wt% in units like the Mosquito Formation and Cassipor dykes. These compositions reflect derivation from variably enriched mantle sources, with SiO ranging 4855 wt% and MgO 410 wt% in most samples, indicating moderate differentiation.
Mineralogically, CAMP basalts feature phenocrysts of olivine (Fo7085, often altered), plagioclase (An6080, zoned with more sodic rims), and augitic clinopyroxene, embedded in a fine-grained groundmass of the same phases plus pigeonite, orthopyroxene in some sills, magnetite-ilmenite, and accessory apatite. High-MgO variants (up to 12 wt%, Mg# ~70) are rare and represent near-primary melts, while most rocks show evidence of 1050% fractional crystallization of olivine, plagioclase, and clinopyroxene, leading to correlated decreases in MgO and increases in incompatible elements like Zr and La.
Texturally, the basalts display intergranular to subophitic fabrics in sills and dykes, with micro-porphyritic and porphyritic varieties common in flows, reflecting variable cooling rates. Extrusive units often exhibit compound or simple pahoehoe morphologies with segregation vesicles and pipe vesicles in thicker flows (>10 m), indicative of rapid quenching at the surface. Intrusive bodies show chilled margins and layered differentiation, such as olivine-rich horizons in the Palisades sill.
Minor andesites (SiO 5560 wt%) and granophyric rhyolites occur sporadically within sills and as differentiates in flows, arising from advanced fractional crystallization and local crustal assimilation, though these felsic components represent less than 5% of the total volume.

Isotopic Analyses

Isotopic analyses of Central Atlantic magmatic province (CAMP) rocks have provided critical insights into the mantle sources and petrogenetic processes involved in its formation. Strontium-neodymium-lead (Sr-Nd-Pb) isotopic compositions indicate derivation from an enriched mantle type 1 (EM-1) source, characterized by relatively high radiogenic Sr and low Nd isotopic ratios. Specifically, initial 87Sr/86Sr ratios range from approximately 0.704 to 0.707, with Nd values typically between -5 and +1, and Pb isotopic ratios plotting near the Northern Hemisphere Reference Line but shifted toward enriched compositions. These signatures suggest involvement of a heterogeneous mantle plume or recycled crustal material within the source, distinct from depleted mid-ocean ridge basalt (MORB) mantle.
High-precision rhenium-osmium (Re-Os) isotopic data from Moroccan basalts further support minimal crustal contamination during magma ascent and emplacement. For instance, analyses of low-Ti basalts from the Central High Atlas yield initial 187Os/188Os ratios of 0.127 to 0.135, aligning closely with primitive mantle values and indicating limited interaction with ancient continental crust, which would otherwise elevate Os radiogenicity. These results imply rapid transit of magmas from mantle depths to the surface, preserving primary mantle signatures despite the province's emplacement through thick sedimentary sequences.
Recent studies have highlighted the role of volatiles in CAMP petrogenesis through analyses of fluid inclusions. In particular, investigations of methane (CH4)-rich inclusions in shallow basaltic intrusions reveal massive fluxing from magma-sediment interactions, a process unique to the end-Triassic CAMP setting due to its interaction with organic-rich and evaporitic sediments. These findings, based on high-temperature reactive porous flow models, underscore how such volatile release contributed to the province's geochemical distinctiveness and broader environmental impacts.

Environmental Impact and Mass Extinction

Correlation with Triassic-Jurassic Boundary

The Central Atlantic Magmatic Province (CAMP) volcanism is temporally linked to the Triassic-Jurassic (T-J) boundary, dated at approximately 201.4 Ma, through high-precision geochronology. The initial eruptive pulse of CAMP, represented by the oldest basaltic flows in the Newark and Fundy basins, has been dated to 201.59 0.043 Ma using U-Pb zircon geochronology combined with cyclostratigraphic analysis of sedimentary cycles. This timing places the onset of CAMP magmatism within about 0.2 million years of the T-J boundary, supporting a causal connection to the end-Triassic mass extinction event.
Recent paleomagnetic and U-Pb studies reveal that CAMP eruptions occurred in four distinct pulses during the initial phase, each lasting less than 100 years and spanning roughly 40 kyr overall. The first pulse aligns precisely with the main phase of the continental end-Triassic extinction, while subsequent pulses correspond to prolonged environmental perturbations and secondary extinction phases in marine and terrestrial records. High-resolution correlations using geomagnetic secular variation further demonstrate sub-centennial-scale synchroneity between these eruptive events and biotic turnovers.
Earlier debates regarding the precise timingwhether CAMP preceded or lagged the T-J boundaryhave been resolved by advanced U-Pb dating, indicating that the initial eruptions preceded the boundary by less than 100 kyr. For instance, U-Pb ages from key sills and flows, such as 201.566 0.031 Ma for the North Mountain Basalt, confirm this tight temporal overlap without significant lag. These data refute prior uncertainties from less precise radioisotopic methods and affirm the role of CAMP as a trigger for the extinction.
Globally, biostratigraphic evidence ties CAMP timing to ammonite faunal turnovers across the T-J boundary. The boundary is formally defined by the first occurrence of the ammonite Psiloceras planorbis at the Global Stratotype Section and Point (GSSP) in Kuhjoch, Austria, marking a sharp replacement of late Triassic (Rhaetian) ammonite assemblages by early Jurassic (Hettangian) forms. This ammonite-defined horizon correlates with the extinction's marine pulse and the initial CAMP pulse via integrated chemostratigraphy and magnetostratigraphy in sections from Europe, North America, and Morocco.

Mechanisms of Extinction

The massive volcanic eruptions associated with the Central Atlantic Magmatic Province (CAMP) released enormous quantities of carbon dioxide (CO) and sulfur dioxide (SO) into the atmosphere, driving profound environmental perturbations that contributed to the end-Triassic mass extinction. CO emissions, estimated to have increased atmospheric concentrations by several times baseline levels through pulsed injections, triggered rapid global warming, with paleoclimate models indicating a temperature rise of approximately 7.4C over the transition period at rates exceeding 10C per million years. Concurrently, SO emissions formed sulfate aerosols that caused short-term cooling episodes but primarily led to widespread acid rain, which acidified soils, freshwater systems, and oceans, disrupting nutrient cycles and harming terrestrial and aquatic life. These gaseous outputs aligned temporally with the extinction event, exacerbating climatic instability. These pulses released sulfur at rates 500 times greater than modern large eruptions, intensifying acid rain and cooling episodes.
In addition to greenhouse gases, CAMP volcanism emitted halogens such as chlorine and bromine, which catalytically destroyed stratospheric ozone, leading to significant ozone layer depletion and increased ultraviolet (UV) radiation reaching the Earth's surface. Recent 2024 research indicates that volcanic emissions of halogens from CAMP destroyed stratospheric ozone, leading to increased UV radiation and mutagenesis in vegetation, as evidenced by malformed fern spores. This ozone loss, combined with warming, intensified environmental stress, particularly in high-latitude and terrestrial settings where UV penetration was most acute.
Ocean acidification from dissolved CO lowered seawater pH in pulses, promoting marine anoxia by reducing oxygen solubility and expanding oxygen minimum zones, which severely impacted benthic and pelagic communities. This process is linked to the extinction of approximately 80% of marine species, including reefs and ammonoids, as anoxic conditions stifled respiration and food webs collapsed. On land, the combined stressors fueled spikes in wildfire activity, evidenced by charcoal peaks in sedimentary records, which devastated forests and led to widespread ecosystem collapse, favoring opportunistic ferns and early dinosaurs in the aftermath.

Evidence from Sedimentary Records

Sedimentary records from continental basins provide critical proxy evidence linking the Central Atlantic Magmatic Province (CAMP) volcanism to environmental perturbations at the Triassic-Jurassic (T-J) boundary, including biotic turnover and climate shifts associated with the end-Triassic mass extinction. These records, preserved in rift basins of eastern North America (ENA) and northwest Africa, capture signals of rapid volcanic pulses through magnetostratigraphic, palynological, and geochemical indicators. A comprehensive review of these continental archives highlights the synchronicity between CAMP activity and extinction events, drawing on high-resolution dating and proxy data from sections like the Newark and Argana basins.
Magnetostratigraphic studies reveal a short-lived reversed polarity zone (E23r) immediately below the CAMP lava piles in the Newark Basin, correlating with the T-J boundary and indicating brief geomagnetic instability during initial volcanism. In Moroccan sections, such as those in the Central High Atlas, the upper CAMP flows exhibit predominantly normal polarity but include a brief reversed chron within the intermediate unit, allowing precise correlation across ENA and African records to the global T-J chronostratigraphy. These polarity patterns, combined with U-Pb dating of ~201 Ma, confirm that the main CAMP emplacement occurred in pulsed episodes spanning less than 300,000 years, aligning with the extinction horizon.
Palynological analyses from the Newark and Argana basins document a dramatic ecological shift post the first CAMP pulse, marked by a fern spike dominated by Polypodiisporites polymicroforatus spores, reflecting widespread deforestation and opportunistic regrowth amid gymnosperm decline. In the Newark Basin, last occurrences of key gymnosperm taxa like Patinasporites densus and Lunatisporites rhaeticus precede the spike, indicating a ~60% loss in sporomorph diversity tied to the extinction interval. Similar patterns in the Argana Basin, with abrupt increases in fern spores following gymnosperm extinctions, underscore regional vegetation collapse linked to volcanic stressors, as synthesized in continental palynofloral reviews.
Geochemical proxies in these sediments further implicate CAMP as the driver of environmental crisis. Mercury (Hg) spikes, with enrichments up to several hundred ppb normalized to total organic carbon (Hg/TOC), occur in layers just below and within the lowermost CAMP flows in ENA and Moroccan basins, signaling pulsed volcanic emissions and atmospheric Hg loading. Negative carbon isotope excursions (C ~ -2 to -6 in organic matter) coincide with these Hg anomalies and the palynological turnover, attributed to massive CO release from CAMP magmatism perturbing the carbon cycle. Osmium isotope ratios (Os/Os) show decreases during the late Rhaetian, reflecting enhanced continental weathering and volcanic inputs that amplified climate warming at the T-J boundary. In Moroccan sections, Re-Os dating corroborates this timing, with ages of ~201 Ma aligning the isotopic shifts directly to CAMP pulses.
