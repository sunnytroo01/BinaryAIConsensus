International Commission on Stratigraphy
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The International Commission on Stratigraphy (ICS) is the largest and oldest constituent scientific body within the International Union of Geological Sciences (IUGS), serving as the authoritative organization for establishing global standards in stratigraphic classification and chronostratigraphy. Its primary role is to define and ratify the hierarchical unitssuch as eons, eras, periods, epochs, and agesthat form the International Chronostratigraphic Chart, which underpins the Geological Time Scale and enables precise correlation of rock sequences and geological events across the world. Founded to promote long-term international cooperation among expert stratigraphers, the ICS ensures unified nomenclature and methodologies for interpreting Earth's 4.6-billion-year history through rock strata, fossils, and geochronological data.
The ICS operates as a permanent commission under the IUGS, which was established in 1961 to advance geological sciences globally. It coordinates efforts through 17 specialized subcommissions, each focused on a specific interval of geological time (e.g., Quaternary, Cretaceous, Permian), supported by an executive committee comprising a chair, vice-chair, secretary-general, and informatics and graphics officers. These subcommissions, involving hundreds of titular and corresponding members from over 40 countries, conduct rigorous research to propose and vote on boundaries for stratigraphic units, emphasizing integration of biostratigraphy, lithostratigraphy, magnetostratigraphy, and radiometric dating.
A cornerstone of the ICS's work is the designation of Global Boundary Stratotype Sections and Points (GSSPs), which serve as physical reference points in rock sections worldwide to mark the base of each stage in the chronostratigraphic hierarchy. As of the latest updates in the 2024/12 version of the International Chronostratigraphic Chart, the ICS has ratified GSSPs for most Phanerozoic stages, with ongoing efforts to complete remaining boundaries and refine the scale using advanced geochronology. The commission also maintains the International Stratigraphic Guide, a key resource for standardizing principles and practices in the field, and supports educational initiatives to disseminate stratigraphic knowledge. Through these activities, the ICS not only standardizes Earth's geological timeline but also facilitates interdisciplinary research in paleontology, paleoclimatology, and tectonics.

History and Establishment

Founding

The International Commission on Stratigraphy (ICS) emerged from a long history of international efforts to standardize stratigraphic nomenclature, beginning with the inaugural International Geological Congress (IGC) in Paris in 1878, where initial discussions on stratigraphical classification were held. These early attempts continued at the IGC session in Bologna in 1881 but faced significant challenges, including failed initiatives in the late 19th and early 20th centuries due to divergent national geological traditions and terminologies. Progress advanced in 1910 at the Stockholm IGC, which established the Commission on an International Lexicon of Stratigraphy to compile unified terms, followed by a reorganization in 1952 at the Algiers IGC into the Commission on Stratigraphy, incorporating subcommissions on stratigraphic lexicon and nomenclature. These precursor bodies laid the groundwork for global coordination amid post-World War II efforts to revive international geological collaboration.
The post-war era saw heightened emphasis on unifying stratigraphic scales worldwide, culminating in the formation of the International Union of Geological Sciences (IUGS) in 1961 to foster global cooperation in earth sciences. The existing Commission on Stratigraphy was integrated as a subordinate body of the IUGS in 1965, marking its transition into a more formalized international entity. Key figures in these precursor roles included American geologist Raymond C. Moore, who served as inaugural chairman of the American Commission on Stratigraphic Nomenclature from 1946 to 1952, contributing influential codes and principles that shaped subsequent international standardization efforts.
The ICS was formally established in 1973 when its statutes were endorsed by the membership, with ratification by the IUGS in 1974 initiating full operations and positioning it as the largest subordinate body within the union. Early objectives centered on promoting international agreement on stratigraphic classification, terminology, and boundaries to create a cohesive global chronostratigraphic framework, building on the post-war momentum for collaborative geological science.

Key Milestones

In 1976, the International Commission on Stratigraphy (ICS) adopted the first edition of the International Stratigraphic Guide, a foundational document establishing standardized principles for stratigraphic classification, terminology, and procedure to foster global consistency in geological mapping and correlation. This guide, edited by H.D. Hedberg, emphasized the use of type sections and units to resolve ambiguities in stratigraphic nomenclature, marking a pivotal step toward international harmonization.
During the 1980s and 1990s, the ICS ratified the initial Global Stratotype Sections and Points (GSSPs) for numerous Phanerozoic stages, transitioning from vague zonal definitions to precise boundary stratotypes anchored in physical rock sections worldwide. This era saw key approvals, such as the GSSP for the Silurian-Devonian boundary in 1972 and several Ordovician and Carboniferous stages by the early 1990s, enabling more accurate global chronostratigraphic correlations through integrated fossil and lithologic markers. By the late 1990s, over 30 GSSPs had been established, fundamentally shifting stratigraphic practice toward empirical, location-specific references.
The second edition of the International Stratigraphic Guide was published in 1994, updating the original framework to incorporate advancements in stratigraphic methods, including clearer guidelines on lithostratigraphy, biostratigraphy, and chronostratigraphy. Edited by Amos Salvador, this revision addressed evolving needs in seismic stratigraphy and sequence analysis, reinforcing the ICS's role in promoting uniform procedures across international geological surveys.
In the 2010s, the ICS advanced the integration of high-precision radiometric dating with traditional biostratigraphy, refining numerical ages for chronostratigraphic boundaries and enhancing the accuracy of the global time scale. This synthesis, exemplified by recalibrations using U-Pb and Ar-Ar methods alongside fossil datums, reduced uncertainties in Phanerozoic stage boundaries to within 0.1-1% of their ages. Concurrently, in 2016, the ICS formalized the Working Group on the Anthropocene within the Subcommission on Quaternary Stratigraphy, approving its investigation as a potential formal chronostratigraphic unit to capture human-induced geological changes, though debates persist over its status as an epoch versus an event.
The ICS released version 2023/06 of the International Chronostratigraphic Chart in June 2023, incorporating refined boundary definitions and numerical ages based on recent subcommission approvals. A notable update was the formalization of the Chibanian Stage base at 781 ka, ratified as the Global Stratotype Section and Point in the Chiba section, Japan, aligning Middle Pleistocene subdivisions with magnetostratigraphic and tephrochronologic data.
The Ediacaran-Cambrian boundary GSSP at Fortune Head, Newfoundland, was ratified in 1992, defined by the first appearance of the trace fossil Treptichnus pedum. Recent studies as of 2025 continue to integrate new U-Pb zircon dates and chemostratigraphic data to better understand the rapid ecological transitions at this boundary.

Organizational Structure

Governance

The governance of the International Commission on Stratigraphy (ICS) is managed by an Executive Committee composed of six officers: three voting members (Chair, Vice-Chair, and Secretary-General) and three non-voting members (Treasurer, Informatics Officer, and Graphics Officer). These officers are responsible for the overall coordination of ICS activities, including liaison with the International Union of Geological Sciences (IUGS). The Chair and Vice-Chair are elected by the Voting Commissionconsisting of the Executive officers and chairs of the subcommissionsfor four-year terms, renewable once, with elections typically held 12 months before the International Geological Congress; the Secretary-General is appointed by the Chair, while non-voting officers are appointed by the Executive Committee for similar terms.
Major decisions, including the ratification of chronostratigraphic boundaries such as Global Stratotype Sections and Points (GSSPs), are proposed by relevant subcommissions and voted on by the full Voting Commission. Voting occurs via postal or electronic ballot, requiring a quorum of at least 60% participation and approval by a 60% majority of votes cast; in-person ballots are permitted during meetings if a quorum is present. Following ICS approval, proposals are submitted to the IUGS Executive Committee for final ratification.
Funding for ICS operations is primarily provided through annual grants from the IUGS, supplemented by contributions from members, affiliated geological surveys, and revenues from the sale of publications such as the International Chronostratigraphic Chart. Subcommissions and task groups submit budget requests to the Executive Committee for allocation from these sources, with occasional external grants supporting specific projects.
As of November 2025, the current leadership includes Chair Elisabetta Erba (University of Milan, Italy; term 20242028), Vice-Chair Shuzhong Shen (Nanjing University, China), Secretary-General Charles Henderson (University of Calgary, Canada), Treasurer Alessandro Zuccari (Societ Geologica Italiana ETS, Italy), Informatics Officer (Webmaster) Nicholas Car (Kurrawong AI, Australia), and Graphics Officer Kim Cohen (Utrecht University, The Netherlands).

Subcommissions

The International Commission on Stratigraphy (ICS) is organized into 17 subcommissions, each dedicated to a specific geological system or period, such as the Cambrian, Jurassic, or Quaternary. These subcommissions form the primary decentralized units for stratigraphic work, comprising approximately 20 voting members per subcommission, drawn from a global pool of around 350 titular members across all subcommissions, plus additional corresponding members. Each subcommission is led by a chair, secretary, and up to two vice-chairs, with the executive oversight provided by the ICS Executive Committee to ensure alignment with overall commission goals.
The core responsibilities of the subcommissions include proposing and conducting ballots on stage boundaries within their respective periods, as well as establishing task groups to research and evaluate potential Global Stratotype Section and Point (GSSP) sites for chronostratigraphic units. These activities focus on refining the international geological timescale through rigorous scientific assessment, emphasizing stratigraphic correlation and boundary definition without extending to final ratification, which falls under broader ICS processes. Subcommissions also foster specialized research, such as integrating biostratigraphic, chemostratigraphic, and magnetostratigraphic data to support boundary proposals.
A representative example is the Subcommission on Quaternary Stratigraphy (SQS), which manages the Quaternary system encompassing the Pleistocene and Holocene epochs, spanning the last 2.6 million years. The SQS promotes unified chronostratigraphic nomenclature, evaluates new stratigraphic methods, and disseminates knowledge through education and collaboration, operating with its own chaircurrently Adele Bertiniand dedicated task groups for specific initiatives like timescale calibration.
Membership in the subcommissions is open to qualified geologists and stratigraphers worldwide, selected to reflect regional, methodological, and disciplinary diversity. Voting members, limited to up to 20 per subcommission including the management team, are elected by the subcommission officers and confirmed by the ICS Executive Committee, while corresponding members provide advisory input on an ongoing basis. This structure ensures broad international representation and expertise in advancing period-specific stratigraphic standards.

Objectives and Role

Primary Aims

The International Commission on Stratigraphy (ICS) primarily aims to establish a global standard for chronostratigraphy by defining a hierarchy of unitseonothems (eons), erathem (eras), systems (periods), series (epochs), and stages (ages)that represent intervals of geologic time based on the superposition and correlation of rock strata worldwide. These units are delineated using boundary stratotypes, ensuring that they encompass all rocks formed during specific time spans, regardless of their lithology or geographic distribution, to provide a unified framework for interpreting Earth's history. This standardization is achieved through the ongoing development and revision of the International Chronostratigraphic Chart, which serves as the authoritative reference for the Geological Time Scale.
A key objective is to promote uniformity in stratigraphic terminology and correlation on a global scale, unifying regional nomenclature and documenting stratigraphic units in a comprehensive database to facilitate consistent communication among geoscientists. By maintaining this database, the ICS enables precise correlations between disparate rock sequences, bridging local and international datasets to support accurate reconstructions of paleoenvironments and evolutionary events.
The ICS also facilitates the integration of multiple dating methods, including biostratigraphy, magnetostratigraphy, chemostratigraphy, and geochronology, into a multidisciplinary approach for refining chronostratigraphic boundaries and enhancing the precision of the time scale. This integration allows for the cross-validation of data from diverse proxies, such as fossil assemblages, magnetic reversals, isotopic signatures, and radiometric ages, to achieve robust global correlations.
To ensure accessibility, the ICS promotes education in stratigraphic methods and disseminates knowledge through freely available resources, including the International Chronostratigraphic Chart, guides, and glossaries, making essential tools for research and teaching openly accessible to the global scientific community. As a constituent body of the International Union of Geological Sciences (IUGS), these aims align with broader efforts to foster international agreement on stratigraphic classification.

Integration with IUGS

The International Commission on Stratigraphy (ICS) holds a prominent position as the largest and oldest constituent scientific body within the International Union of Geological Sciences (IUGS), having been established in 1961 as a permanent commission dedicated to advancing stratigraphic standards. It reports directly to the IUGS Executive Committee through annual consolidated reports submitted by the ICS Chair, which encompass subcommission activities, budgets, and key decisions such as the ratification of Global Boundary Stratotype Sections and Points (GSSPs). This direct reporting structure ensures alignment with IUGS oversight, including the requirement for IUGS approval of ICS executive elections, stratigraphic unit definitions, and subcommission formations.
In its collaborative framework, the ICS provides essential stratigraphic expertise to affiliated organizations under the IUGS umbrella, notably through close coordination with the International Union for Quaternary Research (INQUA) via the ICS Subcommission on Quaternary Stratigraphy (SQS). This partnership addresses the specific stratigraphic needs of the Quaternary community, bridging INQUA's research focus with IUGS-sanctioned global standards to facilitate unified chronostratigraphic classifications.
Joint initiatives between the ICS and IUGS include co-sponsorship of the International Geological Congress (IGC), the flagship event of IUGS held quadrennially since 1878, where ICS aligns its terms of office and contributes to thematic sessions on stratigraphy. Additionally, shared funding mechanisms support GSSP fieldwork, with IUGS providing annual allocations to ICS subcommissions for essential research and boundary definitions.
The influence of the ICS extends through the widespread adoption of its standards in IUGS publications, including the International Chronostratigraphic Chart featured prominently in the IUGS journal Episodes, which serves as a global reference for geological time scales. These standards underpin IUGS-endorsed initiatives and are integrated into geological surveys worldwide, providing a unified framework for expressing Earth's history in economic, scientific, and educational contexts.

Methodology

Chronostratigraphic Classification

The chronostratigraphic classification system of the International Commission on Stratigraphy (ICS) provides a standardized hierarchy for organizing the Earth's rock record into units that reflect specific intervals of geologic time, enabling global correlation and communication among geoscientists. This framework distinguishes chronostratigraphythe subdivision of rocks by their time of formationfrom geochronology, which deals with abstract time spans, with rock units serving as the tangible basis for defining time relationships.
At the pinnacle of the hierarchy is the eonothem, the rock equivalent of the eon, encompassing the Archean, Proterozoic, and Phanerozoic eonothems, which span billions of years of Earth history. These are subdivided into erathem units, corresponding to eras such as the Paleozoic, Mesozoic, and Cenozoic erathems. The next level comprises system units, aligned with periods like the Carboniferous or Cretaceous systems, which are further divided into series (epochs, e.g., Lower, Middle, Upper subdivisions) and stage units (ages), the fundamental global building blocks defined for precise correlation. Lower tiers include substage (subage) divisions for finer regional resolution and informal chronozone units based on specific events or markers. This descending hierarchyfrom eonothem to stageensures a nested structure where each unit is bounded by synchronous horizons, facilitating hierarchical subdivision without overlaps or gaps.
A core principle of this classification is the definition of units by their bounding chronostratigraphic horizons rather than midpoints, with the lower boundary serving as the primary reference to anchor the unit and promote diachronous-free correlations worldwide. Unlike lithostratigraphy, which relies on local rock properties like composition or texture, chronostratigraphy prioritizes international applicability through globally correlatable criteria, transcending regional variations to establish a unified timescale. This boundary-based approach minimizes ambiguity, as each unit includes all rocks formed between two isochronous surfaces, regardless of their lithological or depositional differences.
To achieve robust definitions, the ICS integrates multiple stratigraphic disciplines, combining lithostratigraphy for physical rock characteristics, biostratigraphy for evolutionary sequences in fossils, magnetostratigraphy for reversals in Earth's magnetic field, and cyclostratigraphy for periodic sedimentary cycles driven by astronomical forcings. This multidisciplinary synthesis enhances the precision of boundary identification and correlation, particularly in diverse depositional environments, ensuring that chronostratigraphic units are verifiable across continents and ocean basins.
The classification undergoes periodic revisions to incorporate advancing scientific evidence, such as refined dating techniques or new fossil discoveries; for example, the 2018 updates to the International Chronostratigraphic Chart modified notations for Precambrian units to better align them with emerging data on early Earth history. These amendments, particularly for pre-Cambrian subdivisions like the Ediacaran systemthe only formally ratified Precambrian chronostratigraphic unitreflect ongoing efforts to extend the hierarchical framework to older, less fossiliferous rocks using isotopic and geochemical proxies.

Defining GSSPs

A Global Stratotype Section and Point (GSSP) serves as the principal reference section and point for the base of a chronostratigraphic stage, providing a precise, globally correlatable marker on the International Chronostratigraphic Chart. It consists of a designated stratigraphic section with a specific point (often marked by a physical spike or line) where biological, geochemical, or other signals define the boundary, ensuring consistency in geological time scale correlations worldwide. The GSSP concept, formalized in the 1970s and refined through ICS guidelines, emphasizes empirical anchors over arbitrary dates to anchor the hierarchy of stratigraphic units.
Selection of a GSSP prioritizes sites with continuous sedimentation to avoid gaps in the record, abundant and well-preserved index fossils for biostratigraphic precision, and multiple independent dating signals such as chemostratigraphy, magnetostratigraphy, or cyclostratigraphy for robust global correlation. The primary markertypically the first appearance or extinction of a cosmopolitan taxonmust offer fine-scale resolution, supplemented by secondary markers like carbon isotope excursions or geomagnetic reversals to enhance reliability across diverse depositional environments. Accessibility for ongoing study and protection from erosion or development are also essential criteria, as outlined in the International Stratigraphic Guide.
The ratification process begins with a proposal developed by a working group within an ICS subcommission, detailing the site's geology, markers, and correlation potential in a structured format of approximately 20,000 words or equivalent figures. The subcommission votes on the proposal, requiring a supermajority of at least 60% approval from its voting members (typically around 20, with terms limited to 12 years). If approved, the ICS Executive Committee reviews and endorses it, followed by final ratification by a majority vote of the International Union of Geological Sciences (IUGS) Executive Committee. Upon ratification, the GSSP is published in the journal Episodes, the International Chronostratigraphic Chart is updated, and a physical marker is installed at the site; auxiliary sections, known as Standard Auxiliary Boundary Stratotypes (SABS), may be designated as backups for enhanced correlation. This multi-stage process ensures scientific consensus and durability.
Early examples include the GSSP for the base of the Frasnian Stage (Upper Devonian), ratified in 1986 at the Col du Puech de la Suque section in Montagne Noire, France, defined by the first occurrence of the conodont Ancyrodella rotundiloba amid continuous carbonate sedimentation and diverse fossils. More recently, in 2023, the ICS ratified an SABS for the Chibanian Stage (Middle Pleistocene Subseries of the Quaternary) at the Ideale section in Montalbano Jonico, southern Italy, complementing the primary GSSP in Japan and providing additional marine-terrestrial correlation signals like oxygen isotopes and pollen records. These updates reflect ongoing refinements to Quaternary boundaries for better integration with paleoclimate data.

Activities and Contributions

Time Scale Development

The International Commission on Stratigraphy (ICS) plays a central role in developing the global geological time scale by integrating chronostratigraphic units with numerical ages, ensuring a standardized framework for correlating Earth's history across disciplines. This process involves compiling hierarchical divisionssuch as eons, eras, periods, epochs, and agesinto the International Chronostratigraphic Chart, which serves as the authoritative reference for geochronology. The scale's construction relies on defining boundaries through Global Stratotype Sections and Points (GSSPs) and calibrating them with absolute dates to provide precise timelines for geological events.
A key aspect of time scale development is the calibration of chronostratigraphic boundaries with numerical ages, primarily using radiometric dating methods like uranium-lead (U-Pb) dating for Precambrian rocks and astronomical tuning for sedimentary cycles in younger strata. Radiometric techniques, such as U-Pb on zircon crystals, offer high-precision ages for ancient boundaries, enabling anchors for the broader scale in the Precambrian eon where fossil records are sparse. Astronomical tuning, which aligns cyclic patterns in sediments (e.g., Milankovitch cycles) with orbital parameters, refines ages in the Phanerozoic, achieving resolutions down to thousands of years for Cenozoic and Mesozoic intervals. These methods are synthesized in comprehensive works like the Geologic Time Scale 2020, which incorporates data from hundreds of studies to assign ages to over 100 units.
The ICS maintains the time scale through regular updates to the International Chronostratigraphic Chart, with annual revisions incorporating new ratifications and age refinements; the 2023 version featured approximately 80 ratified GSSPs for the 102 Phanerozoic stages, while the 2024/12 update (latest as of November 2025) reached 81 ratified GSSPs, or about 79% ratification for Phanerozoic stages overall. These revisions reflect ongoing subcommission work to resolve uncertainties, such as adjusting boundary ages based on improved dating technologies or new stratigraphic correlations. Numerical ages remain provisional for unratified units, marked with approximations to highlight areas needing further research.
Challenges in time scale development include resolving contentious proposals, such as the Anthropocene epoch, which was proposed to begin in 1950 CE based on markers like nuclear fallout but rejected by the ICS in March 2024 due to concerns over its brevity and alignment with existing Quaternary divisions; as of 2025, it remains unratified and is not incorporated into the official chart. Such debates underscore the need for consensus on boundary criteria amid rapid environmental changes, while ensuring the scale's stability for long-term geological correlations.
Outputs of this development extend to digital integration, with the time scale embedded in tools like the GeoBiodiversity Database (GBDB), the official ICS repository established in 2012 for stratigraphic and paleontological data. The GBDB facilitates global access to calibrated sections, enabling researchers to query boundaries, ages, and correlations interactively, thus supporting applications in paleoclimate modeling and biodiversity studies.

International Collaboration

The International Commission on Stratigraphy (ICS) maintains extensive partnerships with international organizations to advance stratigraphic standardization and geological heritage preservation. Through its affiliation with the International Union of Geological Sciences (IUGS), the ICS collaborates with UNESCO's International Geoscience and Geoparks Programme, contributing stratigraphic expertise to the designation and management of UNESCO Global Geoparks, which highlight sites of international geological significance often tied to chronostratigraphic boundaries. Additionally, the ICS works closely with national geological surveys such as the United States Geological Survey (USGS) and the British Geological Survey (BGS), which provide critical data for Global Stratotype Sections and Points (GSSPs) and integrate ICS chronostratigraphic frameworks into national mapping and research efforts. The ICS also partners with the Paleobiology Database (PBDB), an international repository of paleontological data that aligns its temporal classifications with ICS standards to facilitate global analyses of evolutionary patterns and biodiversity through time.
A key aspect of the ICS's collaborative activities involves co-organizing major international conferences to foster dialogue among stratigraphers worldwide. The ICS delegates and oversees the International Congress on Stratigraphy (STRATI), serving as the primary forum for discussing advancements in stratigraphic methods, time scale updates, and interdisciplinary applications; the upcoming STRATI 2026 is scheduled for June 28 to July 3 in Suzhou, China, continuing a tradition of hosting events across continents to promote inclusive participation.
The ICS extends its global outreach through initiatives aimed at broadening access to stratigraphic knowledge, particularly in underrepresented regions. The International Chronostratigraphic Chart is translated into multiple languages, including Spanish, French, Chinese, Russian, and Arabic, to enhance its utility for researchers and educators in diverse linguistic contexts and support international standardization efforts. While specific ICS-led training workshops in developing countries are coordinated through subcommissions and IUGS networks, these efforts often include capacity-building sessions on stratigraphic techniques during international meetings and field excursions. In recent years, the ICS has intensified joint projects with the IUGS on climate-related stratigraphy, notably through the Anthropocene Working Group under the Subcommission on Quaternary Stratigraphy, which examines stratigraphic evidence of human-induced climate change and issued a joint IUGS-ICS statement in March 2024 addressing ongoing debates.

Publications

International Chronostratigraphic Chart

The International Chronostratigraphic Chart serves as the official global standard for visualizing the hierarchy of chronostratigraphic units, including eonothems, erathems, systems, series, and stages, along with their numerical ages in millions of years (Ma) and the locations of Global Stratotype Sections and Points (GSSPs) that define their lower boundaries. This colored chart provides a concise reference for Earth's geological timescale, integrating key elements such as geomagnetic polarity chrons (magnetozones), eustatic sea-level curves, and major bioevents to contextualize stratigraphic correlations. The latest version, released in December 2024 (v2024/12), is available in PDF format and multilingual options, with an interactive digital version accessible online for enhanced exploration.
The chart's development began in the 1970s under the auspices of the International Commission on Stratigraphy (ICS), with initial printed versions establishing a unified framework for international stratigraphic communication. By the 2000s, it transitioned to digital formats, facilitated by software like TimeScale Creator, which enabled dynamic updates and broader dissemination through the ICS website. Subsequent revisions, such as the 2023/06 edition, have incorporated ratified GSSPs and refined age estimates based on ongoing ICS subcommission work, ensuring the chart reflects the most current chronostratigraphic consensus.
As the foundational reference for geological timelines worldwide, the chart underpins research in paleontology, sedimentology, and Earth history reconstruction, allowing scientists to correlate local rock successions with global standards. It is freely downloadable from stratigraphy.org in high-resolution PDF and interactive formats, promoting open access for educational, professional, and public use without requiring specialized software beyond standard web browsers.

Guides and Journals

The International Stratigraphic Guide serves as the foundational document for standardizing stratigraphic classification, terminology, and procedures worldwide. An abridged version released in 2018 outlines rules for defining stratigraphic units, including lithostratigraphic, chronostratigraphic, biostratigraphic, and other types, while detailing procedures for proposing and ratifying boundaries such as Global Boundary Stratotype Sections and Points (GSSPs). Edited by Michael A. Murphy and Amos Salvador, with revisions by Werner E. Piller and Marie-Pierre Aubry, the guide emphasizes principles for international consistency in stratigraphic communication, omitting extensive historical context or glossaries to focus on practical applications.
The ICS also supports the peer-reviewed journal Stratigraphy, launched in 2004 as a dedicated platform for research exploring broader implications of stratigraphic methods, including biostratigraphy, chemostratigraphy, magnetostratigraphy, and sequence stratigraphy. Published by Micropress, the journal features articles that integrate these tools to interpret Earth history and serves as an outlet affiliated with stratigraphic nomenclature bodies, though it is not exclusively ICS-managed. Access to Stratigraphy is available through subscription-based platforms, with individual articles purchasable online.
In addition to its own guide and journal, the ICS contributes to the IUGS quarterly publication Episodes by submitting ratified GSSP proposals for announcement, ensuring global dissemination of new chronostratigraphic boundaries. These announcements appear as formal articles detailing the stratigraphic evidence and significance of each GSSP. The ICS further produces annual reports summarizing activities, such as subcommission progress and time scale updates, with editions available from 2018 through 2024 (as of November 2025) that highlight ongoing standardization efforts.
All ICS guides, including the International Stratigraphic Guide, are freely downloadable from the official website, promoting open access to core stratigraphic standards. Journal articles from affiliated publications like Stratigraphy can be accessed via their respective publisher portals, supporting researchers in applying ICS frameworks.
