Index of coincidence
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The index of coincidence (IC), also known as the coincidence index, is a statistical tool in cryptanalysis that measures the probability that two randomly selected letters from a given text are identical, thereby quantifying the text's redundancy and deviation from uniformity. Developed by American cryptologist William F. Friedman in the early 1920s, it provides a key method for distinguishing monoalphabetic substitution ciphers (where IC approximates the natural language's value) from polyalphabetic ones (where it trends toward randomness), and for estimating key lengths in periodic ciphers like the Vigenre.
The IC is computed using the formula
IC=i=126fi(fi1)N(N1) \mathrm{IC} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{26} f_i (f_i - 1)}{N(N - 1)} IC=N(N1)i=126fi(fi1)
where $ f_i $ represents the frequency of the $ i $-th letter in the 26-letter English alphabet, and $ N $ is the total length of the text; this yields an expected value of approximately 0.066 for plaintext English due to uneven letter frequencies, versus 0.0385 for a uniformly random string. In application, cryptanalysts divide the ciphertext into subsequences offset by a candidate key length $ k $, then calculate the IC for each; a value near 0.066 indicates a likely match to the true period, as each subsequence then behaves like a monoalphabetic encryption of the language.
Beyond core cryptanalytic uses, the index has been adapted for broader linguistic and information-theoretic analysis, such as evaluating text authenticity or comparing frequency distributions across languages, with expected IC values varying by language (e.g., around 0.076 for German). Friedman's original work, detailed in his 1922 publication The Index of Coincidence and Its Applications in Cryptography, revolutionized codebreaking by introducing rigorous statistical rigor to the field, influencing U.S. military cryptology during World War II and beyond.

Background

Definition

The index of coincidence (IOC) is a statistical measure used in cryptanalysis to quantify the probability that two randomly selected letters from a given text are identical. This metric reflects the regularity of letter frequencies in the text, serving as an indicator of whether the text exhibits the uneven distribution characteristic of natural languages or the uniformity expected in random or certain enciphered sequences.
In natural languages, letter frequencies are highly non-uniform, leading to a higher IOC compared to random text. For example, in English, the letter 'E' appears approximately 12.7% of the time, while less common letters like 'Z' occur far less frequently, resulting in an IOC of about 0.067 for typical plaintext or monoalphabetic ciphertexts. This disparity arises because the probability of matching letters increases with such imbalances, allowing the IOC to distinguish language-like text from more evenly distributed alternatives.
For a random text with a uniform distribution over an alphabet of size $ n $, the expected IOC simplifies to $ \frac{1}{n} $, as each letter has an equal probability of $ \frac{1}{n} $. In the case of the 26-letter English alphabet, this yields approximately 0.0385, which is notably lower than the value for natural English text. This derivation assumes independent selections and highlights how uniformity minimizes coincidences.
The index of coincidence was developed by William Friedman in the 1920s specifically for cryptanalytic purposes, providing a foundational tool for analyzing ciphertext properties.

Historical Development

The index of coincidence emerged from foundational work in statistical cryptanalysis during the 19th century, building on frequency analysis methods introduced by Friedrich Kasiski in his 1863 treatise Die Geheimschriften, welche im Umlauf sind und die Entzifferungskunst, which used repeated sequences to detect cipher periods, and by Auguste Kerckhoffs in his 1883 La Cryptographie Militaire, which emphasized the role of language statistics in breaking substitution ciphers. These approaches provided the conceptual groundwork, but lacked a unified statistical measure for quantifying letter coincidences across texts.
The method was formalized and named by William F. Friedman in 1922, during his tenure at Riverbank Laboratories' Department of Ciphers and Codes in Geneva, Illinois, where he developed it as a tool for distinguishing monoalphabetic from polyalphabetic encryptions. Friedman detailed the technique in the military publication The Index of Coincidence and Its Applications in Cryptography, a seminal pamphlet that integrated probability theory with practical cryptanalytic procedures and was distributed through restricted U.S. government channels.
During World War II, the index of coincidence proved instrumental in Allied cryptanalytic efforts, particularly at Bletchley Park, where codebreakers in Hut 8 applied it to analyze naval Enigma machine outputs, aiding in rotor order determination via techniques like Banburismus for naval traffic. Its statistical reliability complemented mechanical aids such as the Bombe, enabling faster identification of polyalphabetic structures in high-volume intercepts.
In the post-war period, the index gained broader academic prominence through David Kahn's 1967 history The Codebreakers: The Story of Secret Writing, which credited it as Friedman's most enduring contribution to cryptology and contextualized its evolution within military intelligence traditions. By the 1980s, as computational resources proliferated, it was implemented in early cryptanalysis software for automated frequency testing, reflecting the shift toward digital tools in the field.
Since the 2000s, the index of coincidence has been integrated into digital forensics standards and open-source platforms, including CrypTool 2 (development started in 2008), where algorithmic optimizations enhance its efficiency for language identification and cipher detection in forensic investigations.

Computation

Monographic Formula

The monographic index of coincidence (IOC) quantifies the probability that two randomly selected letters in a text are identical, serving as a measure of letter frequency unevenness in cryptanalysis. It was introduced by William Friedman in 1922 as a statistical tool for distinguishing cipher types based on frequency distributions.
The standard formula for the monographic IOC is derived from combinatorial counting of matching letter pairs. For a text of length NNN with fif_ifi denoting the frequency of the iii-th letter across an alphabet of size \alpha (typically 26 for English), the IOC is given by:
IOC=i=1fi(fi1)N(N1)
\text{IOC} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{\alpha} f_i (f_i - 1)}{N (N - 1)}
IOC=N(N1)i=1fi(fi1)
This expression counts the total number of ways to choose two identical letters, fi(fi1)\sum f_i (f_i - 1)fi(fi1), divided by the total number of possible letter pairs, N(N1)N (N - 1)N(N1). For sufficiently large NNN, this approximates the probability form pi2\sum p_i^2pi2, where pi=fi/Np_i = f_i / Npi=fi/N is the relative frequency of letter iii. This probability-based derivation links directly to chi-squared statistics for testing uniformity, as pi2\sum p_i^2pi2 relates to the deviation from a uniform distribution via the equation 2/N(pi21/)\chi^2 / N \approx \alpha (\sum p_i^2 - 1/\alpha)2/N(pi21/), where \alpha is the alphabet size.

Calculation Procedure

To compute the index of coincidence (IC) for a given text sample, first prepare the text by converting all characters to uppercase and removing any non-alphabetic characters, such as punctuation, spaces, or numbers, to ensure only letters are considered. This step focuses the analysis on the 26 letters of the English alphabet, yielding a total letter count NNN.
Next, tally the frequency fif_ifi of each letter iii (from A to Z) in the prepared text, where i=1i = 1i=1 to 26. For each letter, calculate fi(fi1)f_i \times (f_i - 1)fi(fi1), which represents the number of ways to choose two occurrences of that letter, and sum these values across all 26 letters to obtain fi(fi1)\sum f_i (f_i - 1)fi(fi1).
Finally, divide the sum by N(N1)N \times (N - 1)N(N1) to yield the IC value:
IC=i=126fi(fi1)N(N1).
\text{IC} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{26} f_i (f_i - 1)}{N (N - 1)}.
IC=N(N1)i=126fi(fi1).
This result approximates the probability that two randomly selected letters from the text are identical.
For reliable results, the text should contain at least 200 letters, as smaller samples (N<100N < 100N<100) introduce significant variability due to random fluctuations in letter counts. Shorter texts may produce IC values ranging widely (e.g., 0.04 to 0.08 for English-like distributions), reducing confidence in the estimate.
The precision of the IC improves with larger NNN, but sample size affects variance; a rough approximation for the standard deviation is 2IC(1IC)/N\sqrt{2 \cdot \text{IC} \cdot (1 - \text{IC}) / N}2IC(1IC)/N, which helps gauge confidence intervals for the estimate.
The following pseudocode implements the procedure in Python, using a loop to compute frequencies:
def index_of_coincidence(text):
 # Step 1: Prepare text
 text = text.upper()
 text = ''.join(c for c in text if c.isalpha())
 N = len(text)
 if N < 2:
 return 0.0 # Edge case: insufficient length
 
 # Step 2: Count frequencies
 freq = * 26
 for c in text:
 freq += 1
 
 # Step 3: Compute sum and IC
 sum_val = sum(f * (f - 1) for f in freq)
 ic = sum_val / (N * (N - 1))
 return ic
This code handles text preparation automatically and returns the IC value, suitable for integration into larger cryptanalytic tools.

Cryptanalytic Applications

Period Detection in Polyalphabetic Ciphers

In polyalphabetic substitution ciphers, such as the Vigenre cipher, the index of coincidence (IC) of the overall ciphertext is typically lower than that of the source language because the key mixes multiple substitution alphabets, flattening letter frequencies toward uniformity. However, if the ciphertext is regrouped by extracting every k-th letter to form k separate subgroupswhere k is hypothesized to be the period (key length)each subgroup corresponds to a single substitution alphabet, restoring letter frequencies closer to those of the natural language. The IC is then recomputed for each subgroup; when k matches the true period, the average IC across subgroups approximates the language's expected value, such as 0.066 for English, distinguishing it from random alignments where the average remains near 0.0385.
This method, developed by cryptologist William Friedman in the early 20th century, provides a statistical basis for period detection beyond manual inspection. To apply it, candidate periods are tested by iterating over possible k values, typically from 2 up to the square root of the ciphertext length N to limit computation, forming the subgroups for each k, calculating their individual ICs using the standard formula, and selecting the k that yields the highest average IC closest to the language norm. For efficiency, the process often starts with a rough overall IC estimate to predict the period range before detailed subgroup analysis.
The IC approach integrates well with the Kasiski examination, a earlier technique from 1863 that identifies repeated n-grams in the ciphertext and computes distances between their occurrences, which are likely multiples of the period. Kasiski provides a shortlist of candidate k values by finding the greatest common divisors of those distances, after which IC testing verifies the most probable period by checking subgroup ICs against language thresholds, reducing false positives from coincidental repeats.
Despite its effectiveness, the method has limitations: it performs best with longer keys and sufficiently long ciphertexts (ideally N > 1000), as short keys introduce overlap noise that blurs subgroup distinctions, and mixed alphabets or non-standard encodings can skew IC values away from expected norms. In such cases, the average subgroup IC may not sharply peak at the true period, requiring cross-validation with other cryptanalytic tools.

Ciphertext Language Identification

The index of coincidence (IOC) serves as a key tool in identifying the natural language of a ciphertext when the plaintext language is unknown, by comparing the computed IOC of the ciphertext to established reference values for various languages. For a monoalphabetic substitution cipher, the IOC of the ciphertext closely approximates that of the underlying plaintext language due to the preservation of letter frequencies under simple substitution. Analysts compute the IOC from the ciphertext and match it against a database of expected IOC values derived from large corpora of plaintext in different languages, enabling language attribution even without decryption.
Reference IOC values vary by language, reflecting differences in letter frequency distributions, with minor variations possible across dialects due to subtle shifts in usage (e.g., British English IOC 0.0665 versus American English 0.0667). In practice, a high IOC matching a known language profile indicates a monoalphabetic cipher enciphering text in that language, as the substitution maintains the original redundancy. Conversely, a low IOC (near 0.038 for a 26-letter alphabet) suggests either a polyalphabetic cipher, which flattens frequencies across multiple substitutions, or random padding, distinguishing it from natural language patterns. This distinction aids in initial cipher classification before applying more advanced techniques, such as period detection for polyalphabetics.
In modern digital forensics, IOC analysis identifies the source language of encrypted files or communications, facilitating targeted decryption efforts by narrowing the frequency models used in attacks.
Reliable identification requires a sufficiently large sample size, typically exceeding 500 characters, to minimize statistical variance and ensure the computed IOC converges to the true value; smaller texts can yield unreliable estimates due to sampling error. Factors like artificial or constructed languages may introduce false positives, as their IOC can deviate from natural patterns, potentially mimicking randomness or unrelated tongues.

Extensions and Variations

Digraphic and Higher-Order Indices

The digraphic index of coincidence extends the monographic version by considering pairs of consecutive letters (digrams) rather than individual letters, providing a measure of redundancy in adjacent symbol pairs. The formula for the digraphic index is given by

\text{IOC}_2 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{26} \sum_{j=1}^{26} f_{ij} (f_{ij} - 1)}{N (N - 1)},
IOC2=N(N1)i=126j=126fij(fij1),
where fijf_{ij}fij is the frequency count of the digram consisting of the iii-th and jjj-th letters of the alphabet, and NNN is the number of digrams in the text (approximately the text length minus one). An approximation, valid for large NNN, replaces the numerator with N2i,jpij2N^2 \sum_{i,j} p_{ij}^2N2i,jpij2, where pijp_{ij}pij is the probability of the digram ijijij.
This approach generalizes to higher-order indices for kkk-grams (k>2k > 2k>2), such as trigrams. The kkk-th order index is
IOCk=mpm2,
\text{IOC}_k = \sum_m p_m^2,

Computation becomes increasingly demanding as kkk grows, with time and space complexity on the order of O(26k)O(26^k)O(26k) due to the exponential number of possible kkk-grams in a 26-letter alphabet.
Higher-order indices offer advantages over the monographic version by capturing dependencies between adjacent symbols, enabling detection of periodicity in shorter texts or more sophisticated ciphers, such as autokey variants where single-letter analysis may be inconclusive. For instance, they can reveal subtle patterns in ciphers with longer periods or superimposed messages. In English text, the digraphic index typically exhibits greater redundancy than random text, reflecting linguistic constraints on common digrams like "th" or "he."
These extensions are particularly useful in refining period estimates for polyalphabetic ciphers when the monographic index yields ambiguous results, such as in cases where text length limits resolution or noise from encipherment masks single-letter patterns. They also aid in analyzing advanced systems like running-key ciphers by highlighting multi-letter repetitions. Adaptations for Non-English Languages

The index of coincidence (IOC) requires adjustments when applied to languages with alphabets differing from the 26 letters of English, as the expected value for random text is approximately $ \frac{1}{n} $, where $ n $ is the alphabet size. For instance, Russian uses the Cyrillic alphabet with 33 letters, yielding a random IOC of about 0.030, lower than English's 0.038 due to the larger symbol set. Similarly, Arabic employs 28 letters in its script, resulting in a random IOC of roughly 0.036. These adjustments ensure the metric accurately distinguishes natural language patterns from randomness in cryptanalysis.
Non-Latin scripts necessitate romanization or transliteration prior to IOC computation to map characters to a compatible set, such as the Latin alphabet, while preserving frequency distributions. In Arabic, for example, digrams are analyzed after transliteration, treating diacritics (like vowel marks) as distinct symbols if included, which can slightly increase $ n $ and affect the random IOC baseline. Hebrew and other Semitic scripts follow similar processes, with transliteration enabling standard IOC formulas. This adaptation maintains the metric's utility in identifying polyalphabetic periods or monoalphabetic substitutions in ciphertext.
Logographic languages like Japanese and Chinese pose unique challenges due to their character-based systems rather than strict alphabets. In Japanese, kanji texts exhibit elevated redundancy due to frequent compound formations and a limited subset of common characters (despite thousands available), compared to hiragana's syllabic structure. For Chinese, pinyin romanization uses the Latin alphabet (26 letters) with additional marks or numbers for tones, resulting in an effective symbol set of approximately 26-50 depending on tone representation, allowing IOC computation on the romanized text to approximate the original logographic frequencies. These adaptations prioritize character-level analysis over phonetic units to capture redundancy in compounds.
For multilingual texts, a weighted IOC can be computed by segmenting the text by language and averaging the individual IOC values proportional to segment lengths, accounting for mixed alphabets or scripts. Modern tools like CrypTool 2 facilitate this through built-in multilingual statistics and automated adaptations, supporting over 15 languages with updated frequency data as of 2020s releases for seamless cryptanalysis.

Practical Examples

Vigenre Cipher Analysis

Consider a sample ciphertext generated by encrypting the repeated English pangram "THEQUICKBROWNFOXJUMPSOVERTHELAZYDOG" (210 letters total) using the Vigenre cipher with the key "KEY" (period 3). The resulting ciphertext is:

This example demonstrates the step-by-step use of the index of coincidence (IOC) to detect the period and recover the key, as originally developed by William F. Friedman for polyalphabetic cipher analysis.
Step 1: Compute the overall IOC.
The IOC for the entire ciphertext is calculated as
IOC=i=126fi(fi1)n(n1),
\mathrm{IOC} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{26} f_i (f_i - 1)}{n (n - 1)},
IOC=n(n1)i=126fi(fi1),
where fif_ifi is the frequency of the iii-th letter and n=210n = 210n=210 is the length. This yields approximately 0.04, which is close to the random value of 1/260.03851/26 \approx 0.03851/260.0385 and significantly lower than the English IOC of about 0.0667, indicating a polyalphabetic cipher rather than a simple substitution.
Step 2: Test possible periods.
Divide the ciphertext into kkk subgroups based on position modulo kkk, for k=2k = 2k=2 to 101010, and compute the IOC for each subgroup (requiring at least 20-30 letters per subgroup for reliability). Values closer to the English IOC suggest the correct period. For k=3k=3k=3, each of the three subgroups has an IOC of approximately 0.036. This is lower than the typical English value of 0.066 due to the artificial nature of the repeated pangram, which results in relatively uniform subgroup frequencies; for genuine long English texts, the IOC would approach 0.066, clearly distinguishing the correct period from random-like values for incorrect kkk. This aligns with the general period detection method using IOC.
Step 3: Frequency analysis on subgroups.
With period 3 confirmed, extract the three cosets, each consisting of 70 letters. Each coset is a Caesar cipher on the corresponding plaintext subsequence. Perform frequency analysis on each: identify high-frequency letters and test shifts to align them with common English letters like 'E' (~12.7% frequency) or 'O' (~7.5%). Due to multiple high-frequency letters in the subgroups, the mapping may require testing candidates. For coset 0, one of the most frequent letters is O (~9%); shifting so O maps to E requires a key shift of 10 (K). For coset 1, the letter I (~9%) shifts to E with key 4 (E). For coset 2, the letter C (~9%) shifts to E with key 24 (Y). Thus, the key is recovered as "KEY".
Decryption verification.
Decrypting the ciphertext using the key "KEY" (subtracting key shifts modulo 26) recovers the original plaintext: THEQUICKBROWNFOXJUMPSOVERTHELAZYDOG repeated six times. The IOC of the decrypted text is ~0.066, confirming it matches natural English and validating the key recovery.

Random vs. Natural Language Comparison

The index of coincidence (IOC) serves as a key distinguisher between random strings and natural language texts, reflecting the underlying structureor lack thereofin letter distributions. For random text, assuming a uniform distribution over 26 letters, the expected IOC is approximately 0.0385, as derived from the probability that two randomly selected letters match under equiprobable conditions. In contrast, natural English text exhibits an IOC of about 0.0667 to 0.0669 due to the uneven letter frequencies inherent in language.
Consider a 100-letter uniform random string, where each letter occurs roughly 3.85 times on average, leading to a flat frequency profile and an IOC of approximately 0.038. This near-uniformity results from the absence of patterns, with no letter dominating the distribution. For comparison, take a 100-letter excerpt from Shakespeare's Hamlet (Act 3, Scene 1): "tobeornottobethatisthequestionwhethertisnoblerinthemindtosuffertheslingsandarrowsofoutrageousfortu" (adjusted to exactly 100 letters, ignoring spaces and punctuation, lowercase). Here, the IOC computes to approximately 0.067, driven by pronounced peaks in common letters such as E (appearing about 12 times, or 12%), T (10 times, 10%), and A (8 times, 8%), consistent with standard English frequencies.
The frequency distributions highlight these differences starkly. In random text, a bar chart would display nearly equal bars at 3.85% height across all letters, underscoring equiprobability. For the Shakespeare excerpt, the bar chart reveals a skewed landscape, with tall bars for E, T, and A contrasting short ones for rarer letters like Z or Q (near 0%).
