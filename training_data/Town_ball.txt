Town ball

1x
 Town ball is a bat-and-ball game played in North America during the 18th and 19th centuries, featuring elements of striking a pitched ball with a bat and running between bases or safe havens while evading fielders, who could retire runners by hitting them directly with the balla practice known as "soaking" or "plugging." As a safe-haven sport, it emphasized local variations rather than standardized rules, distinguishing it from later codified games, and it typically used a softer ball than modern equivalents. This game represented an early form of organized recreation, often played in open fields or urban lots by working-class men and boys, without uniforms, gloves, or fixed team sizes.
The origins of town ball trace back to British games like rounders, which were adapted in colonial America, with the earliest documented references appearing in the mid-18th century in regions such as North Carolina's lowlands. By the early 19th century, it had spread widely across the United States, evolving into distinct regional styles; for instance, Philadelphia town ball emerged in the 1830s as one of the more formalized variants, leading to the establishment of clubs like the Olympic Ball Club in 1831. Historical accounts note its popularity among figures like Abraham Lincoln, who played it during his youth in Illinois in the 1830s, highlighting its role in everyday American social life before the Civil War. The game's prevalence waned after the 1850s as the New York-style rulesemphasizing fair play without soakinggained national prominence through organizations like the National Association of Base Ball Players.
Key features of town ball varied by locale, but common elements included teams of 8 to 15 players, innings structured as "all-out-side-out" (ending only when every player on a side was retired), and fields arranged in a square or circular pattern with stakes marking bases rather than the diamond shape of modern baseball. In the prominent Philadelphia variant, bases formed a 30-foot diameter circle, pitching was underhand and non-aggressive, and a "bound rule" allowed catches on the first bounce to count as outs, while no fixed defensive positions were assigned. Soaking remained a core mechanic in many versions, adding a physical element absent in contemporary baseball, though some areas like Cincinnati developed rules prohibiting it to align closer with emerging standards. These flexible rules fostered community engagement but contributed to the game's eventual supersession by more uniform regulations.
As a foundational influence on baseball's development, town ball's legacy persists in the sport's basic mechanics of batting, fielding, and base-running, with its variants helping bridge folk games to the professional era beginning in the 1860s. While largely extinct by the late 19th century, the term "town ball" has been retrospectively applied to early baseball-like games and, in modern contexts like Minnesota's amateur leagues, evokes a similar spirit of local, non-professional playthough these are direct descendants of codified baseball rather than the original form.

History and Origins

Early Development in North America

Town ball emerged in North America as an adaptation of European bat-and-ball games, particularly stoolball and rounders, which were brought by early colonists in the 17th century. Stoolball, a game involving a batter defending a stool target from a thrown ball using a bat or hands, was played by Pilgrims at Plymouth Colony as early as 1621, when Governor William Bradford reprimanded workers for engaging in it on Christmas Day, viewing the activity as contrary to religious observance. Similarly, in 1609 at Jamestown, Virginia, Polish craftsmen introduced a bat-and-ball game known as pilka palant, involving striking a ball with a bat, which was documented in a 1625 memoir as a recreational diversion among laborers. These early imports reflected a broader cultural transfer of folk games from England and continental Europe, where roundersfeaturing base-running and battinghad been popular since at least the 16th century, though the term "rounders" itself was rarely used in American contexts.
By the mid-18th century, bat-and-ball games had spread across colonial settlements, appearing in diaries, church records, and local ordinances, often under names like "base," "bat ball," or simply "ballplaying." In 1738, New York authorities punished boys for playing bat-and-ball games during divine service, indicating such activities were common enough to disrupt community gatherings. Further evidence comes from the 1750s in North Carolina, where "town-ball" and related games like "cat" were noted as popular among males in frontier areas, suggesting localized adaptations in rural Piedmont regions. A pivotal early reference to "base ball" specifically appears in a 1791 Pittsfield, Massachusetts, town bylaw prohibiting play within 80 yards of the new meetinghouse to protect its windows, alongside other games like cricket and wicket; this ordinance underscores the game's prevalence in New England communities. Immigration from Britain and Europe facilitated this diffusion, carrying traditions to Mid-Atlantic colonies like New York and Pennsylvania, as well as southern frontiers, where games evolved amid diverse settler populations.
Through the early 19th century, these games continued to evolve into informal bat-and-ball variants later collectively known as town ball, with the term emerging in the 1830s, played informally in both urban and rural settings under variants like "base," "round ball," or the "New England game." Diaries from the period, such as those of Dutch settlers in 1755 near Binghamton, New York, describe juvenile "bat and ball" play, while military journals from 1758 at Fort Ticonderoga note adult soldiers engaging in similar pastimes during lulls in the French and Indian War. Town ball served a vital social role in early American communities, fostering camaraderie among children in schoolyards, adults at town meetings, and mixed groups during holidays or fairs, without any formalized rules or organizations until the mid-19th century. Its accessibilityrequiring minimal equipmentmade it a staple of frontier life, promoting physical activity and social bonds in the absence of structured sports.

Connection to Modern Baseball

In the mid-19th century, debates over baseball's origins pitted proponents of British influences against advocates of a purely American invention. British-born sportswriter Henry Chadwick, often called the "father of baseball," argued that the game evolved from the English bat-and-ball sport of rounders, citing shared elements like bases, batting, and running between them. Chadwick's views, expressed in articles and guides from the 1860s onward, emphasized baseball's development from rounders through American adaptations such as town ball, a precursor game played in informal settings across the United States. Opposing this, American nationalists like sporting goods magnate Albert G. Spalding insisted on indigenous origins to bolster baseball's status as a uniquely American pastime, rejecting any foreign roots.
These tensions culminated in the 1905 formation of the Mills Commission, chaired by Abraham G. Mills and initiated by Spalding, to investigate baseball's beginnings. The commission's 1907 report attributed the game's invention to Abner Doubleday in Cooperstown, New York, in 1839, drawing on testimony from Abner Graves who claimed Doubleday refined local town ball rules by introducing diagrams for diamond-shaped fields and fixed teams. This narrative positioned town ball as a direct precedent, with Doubleday's supposed innovationssuch as eliminating the practice of hitting runners with the ball (soaking)bridging informal play to organized baseball, though later scholarship debunked the Doubleday claim due to lack of contemporary evidence and Doubleday's absence from Cooperstown that year.
The transition from town ball to modern baseball accelerated in the 1850s and 1860s as regional variants adopted standardized "New York rules." Town ball clubs, which varied in rules like base paths and player counts, increasingly shifted to the more structured Knickerbocker Rules formalized in 1845 by the Knickerbocker Base Ball Club of New York. These rules refined town ball elements by standardizing nine players per side, establishing foul lines, and requiring underhand pitching, while prohibiting soaking and introducing force outs, thus creating a safer, more consistent game that spread beyond New York. By the late 1850s, this evolution was evident in inter-club matches, with primary sources like the New York Clipper reporting on 1857 conventions where town ball-influenced groups debated adopting New York-style rules to unify play.
Town ball's decline followed the American Civil War, as the push for uniform rules gained momentum amid baseball's professionalization. In the 1870s, the National Association of Professional Base Ball Players enforced standardized regulations derived from New York rules, marginalizing regional town ball variants nationwide. Town ball persisted in isolated rural areas, particularly in the Midwest like Minnesota, into the 1880s, where amateur leagues played hybrid forms before fully transitioning to modern baseball.
Spalding reinforced the American origins mythology in his 1911 book America's National Game, which popularized the Mills Commission's Doubleday story and portrayed town ball as a foundational American evolution, free of British influence. Though debunked by 20th-century historians through archival research revealing baseball's folk-game roots, this narrative underscored town ball's role as a key link in the game's development, cementing its historical significance despite the myth's flaws.

Rules and Gameplay

Core Mechanics

Town ball, as a bat-and-ball game played in 19th-century North America, often utilized a square infield configuration with four bases positioned at the corners, spaced approximately 60 feet apart in some variants or shorter in others. The batter, known as the striker, stood at a designated home base or line, often considered a fifth marker separate from the running bases, allowing for a distinct starting position outside the square. This setup emphasized open play without defined boundaries, enabling the game to adapt to available town lots or fields.
Team composition in town ball was flexible, commonly featuring 10 to 20 players per side rather than a standardized limit like modern baseball's nine, accommodating varying group sizes in informal settings. Games proceeded without fixed innings; instead, an "all-out" rule prevailed, where the batting side continued until every player was retired, or in some variants, a single out ended the turn for the entire side. Matches typically concluded after a predetermined number of runs scored or upon time expiration, such as four hours, rather than a set number of innings. Runners advanced counterclockwise around the bases upon a successful hit, with no foul territory designatedall struck balls were considered fair and in play.
The pitcher delivered the ball underhand from a central position, aiming for a hittable toss to the striker, who used a wooden bateither flat and paddle-like or roundto make contact. Basic equipment consisted of a soft, often homemade ball made from yarn or wool wrapped around a core, weighing around 2 to 2.75 ounces, paired with bats of varying lengths but no stricter dimensional rules. Minimal protective gear was employed, reflecting the game's informal nature. A key out mechanism involved "soaking," where fielders could retire runners by throwing the ball to hit them directly while between bases.

Key Differences from Baseball

One of the most distinctive features of town ball was its method of recording outs, which relied heavily on "soaking"directly hitting a runner with a thrown ball while they were between bases or off a baserather than the tagging or force outs central to modern baseball. Additional outs could occur if a runner crossed paths with a thrown ball or through bound catches (first bounce), and early forms lacked formalized force outs at bases, allowing multiple runners to occupy the same base without penalty. These mechanics emphasized fielding agility and direct confrontation over the more protective base-path rules of today.
Pitching in town ball was always delivered underhand, with the pitcher positioned at a set distancesuch as 35 feet in some variantsand required to throw the ball within the batter's reach on their preferred side, without a defined strike zone. Unlike modern baseball's overhand pitching and called strikes, pitchers aimed primarily to induce weak contact rather than overpower hitters, and while some variants allowed batters multiple pitches until contact, others included rules for outs after missed strikes (such as three), fostering a pace focused on continuous play rather than pitcher-batter duels.
Base running rules further diverged by permitting runners to overrun bases without risk of being tagged out, prohibiting balks or pickoff attempts, and allowing multiple runners on a single base, which encouraged aggressive advancement and reduced the emphasis on precise timing seen in contemporary baseball. This setup promoted fluid, communal movement on the field, contrasting with the stricter path adherence and defensive strategies of the modern game.
Scoring and strategy in town ball highlighted mass participation with teams often exceeding nine players, leading to high-scoring games where totals frequently surpassed 100 runs per side due to the absence of formalized walks, stolen bases, or intentional defensive alignments. In some variants, innings continued until all batters were retired, while in others a single out ended the turn, prioritizing collective scoring over individual tactics like sacrifices or hit-and-runs, which reflected the game's roots in informal community recreation.
The soaking out mechanism often resulted in rough physical play, increasing injury risks and contributing to the eventual adoption of safer tagging rules in evolving baseball variants, while game lengths varied by local normsoften concluding after a fixed time or tally threshold rather than fixed inningsto accommodate social gatherings. This variability in pace and safety underscored town ball's adaptation to amateur, town-based play before standardization.

Regional Variations

Philadelphia Town Ball

The Olympic Ball Club of Philadelphia, established in 1833, stands as the earliest known organized club dedicated to town ball, emerging from the merger of two pre-existing player groupsone from Philadelphia and another from nearby Camden, New Jerseyfollowing their inaugural match on July 4 of that year. This union formalized the club's structure, though games typically featured 11 players per side to align with the all-out nature of play. The club's 1838 constitution, a pioneering document for bat-and-ball clubs, outlined organizational bylaws, membership rules, and basic game governance, including the appointment of a scorekeeper to officiate disputes, reflecting an emphasis on structured amateur recreation among young professionals and students.
Philadelphia town ball, as codified by the Olympics, featured a compact playing field with five stakes arranged in a circle of approximately 30 feet in diameter, promoting fast-paced action within urban constraints. Games consisted of a variable number of innings (often two or more by agreement), each concluding when all 11 players on a side were retired (all-out side-out). The bat was a short, round-handled implement suited for quick swings, and the pitchertermed the "ball giver"delivered underhand tosses from within the base circle at a slow pace, emphasizing placement over speed. Soaking, or directly hitting a runner with the ball for an out, remained a key mechanic, distinguishing it from emerging baseball norms.
A notable event highlighting the game's vitality occurred in September 1857, when the New York Clipper reported a town ball match in Germantown (now part of Philadelphia), involving 20 schoolmates in a nostalgic exhibition that underscored the sport's local roots amid baseball's rise. By 1860, the Olympics voted to adopt the New York-style baseball rules, marking a pivotal shift, though the club continued town ball sporadically into the early 1860s. This version of the game influenced other East Coast clubs through the Olympics' longevity and example, with town ball matches documented as far as Mauch Chunk, Pennsylvania, where a local team challenged Philadelphia squads in 1866 under similar guidelines.
The widespread adoption of standardized baseball rules led to town ball's decline in Philadelphia by 1865, as clubs like the Olympics fully transitioned, though echoes of the game persisted in regional exhibitions and oral histories, preserving its role in the city's sporting heritage.

Midwestern and Western Variants

In the Midwest, particularly in Cincinnati, Ohio, town ball evolved through informal clubs that adapted the game to local preferences during the mid-19th century. The Excelsior Townball Club, formed around 1860 by young schoolteachers, hospital interns, and their friends, played match games against other local teams, such as the Olympic Club, emphasizing community participation over rigid structure. Games typically featured 10 to 15 players per side and lasted four innings, reflecting a casual format suited to shorter play sessions in urban settings.
The Cincinnati Buckeye Townball Club, established more formally in 1863, represented a transitional phase as town ball began incorporating elements of emerging base ball rules amid the Civil War era. This club utilized small, primitive bats and soft balls, which allowed for safer, less competitive play in open lots and emphasized skill over power hitting. Such equipment choices aligned with the game's informal roots, prioritizing accessibility for amateur players in a growing industrial city.
In Indiana, town ball thrived as a pre-Civil War schoolyard activity, particularly in rural and frontier communities where organized sports were scarce. Author Edward Eggleston, drawing from his youth in the 1840s and 1850s, described it as "one of the old games from which the scientific but not half so amusing 'national game' of base-ball has since evolved," played "in a free and happy way, with soft balls, primitive bats, and no nonsense," where "there were no scores, but a good time was had by all." This emphasis on fun over competition fostered social bonds among children and young adults, often without formal umpires or recorded outcomes, distinguishing it from more structured Eastern variants.
Town ball spread westward in the 1850s along expanding frontiers in Illinois and Wisconsin, where reports document its popularity in nascent settlements amid open prairies. In Chicago, the Unions club played town ball as early as 1856 on vacant lots, adapting to the region's vast, uneven landscapes with larger fields that accommodated variable team sizes of up to 20 players. Similarly, in eastern Illinois during the 1820s and 1830s, the game was recalled as "base-ball in a rude state," with flexible boundaries to navigate rough terrain like wooded areas and streams, allowing for improvised rules such as adjustable base distances. Abraham Lincoln, while living in Illinois, participated in town ball during his youth, highlighting its role in frontier recreation.
These Midwestern and Western adaptations featured more lenient rules compared to Eastern organized play, such as permitting all struck balls in play without foul lines and allowing plugging (hitting runners with the ball for outs) only in modified forms to suit irregular fields. Team sizes and innings varied based on available participants, often exceeding nine players per side, which promoted inclusivity in sparse populations.
Post-1860s, town ball persisted in informal exhibitions across frontier outposts, including mining communities in Pennsylvania's anthracite regions, where it served as leisure amid harsh labor conditions until the 1880s. In places like Mauch Chunk, evolving into base ball influences, such games maintained community ties through all-out-side-out formats, even as national rules standardized play elsewhere.

Massachusetts Game

The Massachusetts Game emerged as a codified variant of town ball in New England, formalized on May 13, 1858, when representatives from ten clubs around Boston convened in Dedham, Massachusetts, to establish 21 standardized rules under the newly formed Massachusetts Association of Base Ball Players. These rules specified match games with 10 to 14 players per side, four bases arranged in a square 60 feet apart, and a game concluding after one side reached 100 runs, or "tallies," emphasizing prolonged play and endurance. The first match under these regulations occurred on May 31, 1858, between Boston's Olympic Club and the Winthrop Club of Holliston, marking the debut of organized competition in this form.
Core gameplay featured the striker positioned in a designated space between first and home bases, with the ball delivered underhand by a thrower standing 35 feet away, aimed slowly within the batter's reach to facilitate hitting. Outs could be recorded by catching a fly ball, striking at and missing the ball three times with each miss caught by the fielding side, or "soaking" a runner by hitting them with a thrown ball while off base; tagging was not explicitly required, and there were no foul lines, allowing batted balls in any direction to remain in play unless caught. This setup promoted aggressive fielding and running, with sides alternating after each out to maintain continuous action.
Clubs such as the Dedham Town Ball Club and the Franklin Club of Salem actively participated, contributing to the game's regional spread through annual conventions in the 1860s, where rules were refined and the association briefly reorganized as the New England Association of Base Ball Players in 1860. The Massachusetts Game dominated New England play into the 1870s, influencing local traditions until the adoption of New York-style baseball rules gradually supplanted it. Its high run totals, often exceeding 100 per game, underscored an emphasis on stamina and scoring volume, with the full rules preserved in historical compilations like the Baseball Almanac.

Other Forms

In the southern United States, town ball saw sparse adoption during the early to mid-19th century, with documented play in locations such as North Carolina's lowlands in the 1750s, South Carolina's Willington Academy in 1804, and Ridgeway, South Carolina schools around 1855. These instances often blended elements of English rounders, a predecessor game involving batting and running between bases, reflecting the region's transatlantic cultural ties. However, the American Civil War severely disrupted organized play, as Confederate troops occasionally engaged in town ball in campssuch as reports from the 1861 Charleston Mercurybut post-war reconstruction limited widespread continuation in Virginia and the Carolinas.
Post-1860s exhibitions of "old-fashioned base ball" represented hybrid forms that merged town ball traditions with emerging baseball rules, preserving pre-Knickerbocker elements like larger fields and soaking outs in nostalgic events. A notable example occurred in Mauston, Wisconsin, during the 1888 Old Settlers Jubilee, where locals staged a game combining town ball's informal structure with early baseball mechanics to celebrate pioneer heritage.
Immigrant communities, particularly German and Irish settlers, introduced variations in New York and the Midwest by the 1850s, often expanding team sizes beyond the standard nine players to accommodate larger groups, echoing town ball's flexible fielding of 20 to 50 participants. In New Orleans, for instance, German and Irish immigrants formed early clubs that incorporated these scaled-up elements into base ball play, influencing local adaptations.
Transitional variants like "round ball" emerged in Ohio during the 1840s and 1850s, serving as a bridge between unstructured town ball and the formalized Knickerbocker Rules of 1845, with rural western Ohio boys playing bass ball variants that emphasized running and batting similar to rounders. These games, documented in Cincinnati-area accounts, featured improvised equipment and foul-tolerant rules, facilitating the sport's evolution toward standardized baseball. John Montgomery Ward's 1888 history references such early forms, noting town ball's prevalence over 50 years prior in Philadelphia and its distinction from rounders, underscoring these peripheral variants' role in the game's broader development.

Legacy and Cultural Impact

Notable Players and Figures

Abraham Lincoln engaged in town ball during his formative years in Indiana from the 1820s to the 1830s and continued playing into his early adulthood in Illinois during the 1830s and 1840s, as recalled in accounts from contemporaries like James Gourley and Frank P. Blair. Biographies describe these activities as integral to his youth, drawing from interviews collected in Herndon's Informants, which detail Lincoln's physical prowess in such pastimes alongside wrestling and footraces.
Tyrus Raymond "Ty" Cobb, the future baseball legend known as the Georgia Peach, encountered town ball variants in rural Franklin County, Georgia, during the early 1890s, when local youth blended older games like town ball and "cat" with emerging baseball rules. By age 14 around 1900, Cobb was already competing in town ball for the Royston Reds, experiences that honed his aggressive base-running and competitive edge, traits that defined his professional career with the Detroit Tigers starting in 1905.
During the American Civil War, soldiers in Union and Confederate regiments frequently organized early baseball games in army camps and prisons to maintain morale and foster camaraderie amid the hardships of conflict, with officers endorsing the activity for its physical and mental benefits. These matches, often played between units like the 3rd Wisconsin Infantry and opposing forces, provided a vital diversion and helped spread the game's popularity across regions.
Early town ball clubs, such as the Olympic Ball Club of Philadelphiaactive since 1831relied on dedicated umpires to enforce rules in their matches, a role that evolved as the club transitioned from town ball to modern baseball in 1860.
Town ball's informal structure contributed to skill development for many who later excelled in professional baseball, as evidenced by 19th-century memoirs and oral histories linking frontier play to athletic foundations. Due to sparse contemporary records, much of the documentation on these figures draws from secondary analyses by organizations like the Society for American Baseball Research (SABR), which compile biographical and anecdotal evidence from period sources.

Modern Revivals and Exhibitions

The vintage baseball movement, which gained momentum in the post-1960s era, has played a significant role in reviving town ball through clubs that adopt 19th-century rules incorporating elements like the Massachusetts Game variant. Organizations such as the Vintage Base Ball Association (VBBA) support over 200 teams nationwide that play historic base ball, including town ball-inspired formats emphasizing period customs and equipment. One prominent example is the Leatherstocking Base Ball Club in Cooperstown, New York, which has played the Massachusetts Gamea form of town ballevery Sunday since its founding, using rules that feature 15 players per side, a smaller field, and no foul territory.
Early exhibitions of "old-fashioned" town ball date back to the late 19th century, such as the 1888 Old Settlers Jubilee in Mauston, Wisconsin, where locals staged a game reflecting pre-modern baseball styles as part of community festivities. In the modern era, annual events continue this tradition, including vintage games in Pennsylvania where teams like the Neshanock Base Ball Club recreate Philadelphia town ball with stakes for bases and soaking outs, and in Massachusetts at sites like Spencer-Peirce-Little Farm, where 1860s-style matches draw crowds to historic fields.
Key organizations dedicated to preservation include the Protoball project, a collaborative effort by the 19th Century Base Ball Association that documents and reconstructs town ball rules from primary sources, such as the 1858 Massachusetts Rules featuring 11 bases and soaking (hitting runners with the ball for outs). In the 21st century, Midwest clubs like those affiliated with Twenty-First Century Townball have adopted authentic soaking mechanics to evoke pre-Civil War play, organizing cross-regional series with all-star teams from Minnesota to the West Coast.
Minnesota's town ball stands as a distinct revival, evolving from amateur leagues formed in the 1920s into a enduring community tradition under the Minnesota Baseball Association, which unified independent teams in 1924. By 2023, the league marked its centennial with over 300 teams, attracting 17,700 spectators to state tournaments that emphasize local roots and unaltered rules, separate from professional baseball's evolution; as of 2025, the league continues with 273 teams across classes A, B, and C.
Supporting resources include publications like Town Ball: The Glory Days of Minnesota Amateur Baseball (2006), which chronicles the state's amateur heritage through photographs and oral histories, and the 2022 presentation 21st Century Town Ball by Daniel D. Jones and Grant More, detailing modern reconstructions. YouTube documentaries, such as "Vintage Baseball meets Town Ball in Forest Lake" (2016), showcase Midwest exhibitions blending 1860s rules with contemporary play.

Influence on American Society

Town ball played a pivotal role in 19th-century American society by fostering community bonds and youth development through informal, accessible play in urban and rural settings, where diverse groups gathered in open spaces for recreation and social interaction. As a staple of schoolyard activities, it encouraged physical fitness and teamwork among children, serving as an early precursor to organized youth sports that emphasized cooperation and fair play. During the Civil War, early baseball variants boosted soldier morale by providing diversion, physical conditioning, and camaraderie across ranks, with large-scale matches drawing thousands of spectators and uniting troops from varied backgrounds, including immigrants.
The game's inclusivity extended to women and minorities in some regions, addressing gaps in traditional narratives; the Vassar College Resolutes participated actively from 1866 into the 1870s, offering public spaces for gender mixing and autonomy without chaperones, while emerging women's teams appeared in the late 1860s and 1870s. African American players formed clubs such as the Pythians, who in 1869 played the first documented interracial game against white teams, symbolizing early efforts toward integration amid segregationist pressures. Culturally, town ball embodied American ingenuity in sports origins debates, evolving from folk games into a symbol of national identity and frontier vitality, influencing literature like 1860s dime novels and Ernest Thayer's 1888 poem "Casey at the Bat," which depicted heroic archetypes akin to cowboy lore in art and storytelling.
Educationally, town ball's emphasis on collective play in schools laid groundwork for teaching values like perseverance and unity, shaping modern youth programs. Its decline accelerated in the mid-19th century as the Knickerbocker Rules standardized baseball in 1845, marginalizing regional variants like town ball for a uniform national game that favored urban clubs. Despite this, town ball endures in American folklore and regional identities, particularly in Midwestern traditions, where modern studies highlight traditional games' symbolic value in preserving cultural heritage, community cohesion, and intergenerational ties.
