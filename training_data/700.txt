700

1x
 Year 700 (DCC) was a leap year in the Julian calendar, occurring during the 8th century AD amid the Early Middle Ages in Europe and parallel expansions in Islamic and Asian civilizations. The Umayyad Caliphate dominated much of the Middle East, North Africa, and the Iberian Peninsula's approaches, having consolidated conquests from the preceding decades and preparing further advances into Europe. In East Asia, the Tang Dynasty under the influence of Empress Wu Zetian maintained a period of cultural and economic flourishing, while in Southeast Asia, the Srivijaya Empire established dominance over key maritime trade routes in the Indonesian archipelago. Europe featured fragmented polities, including the Merovingian Frankish kingdom, the Visigothic realm in Hispania, and the Byzantine Empire's defensive struggles against Arab pressures in the eastern Mediterranean. This era reflected broader transitions from late antiquity, with no singular cataclysmic event defining 700 precisely but ongoing shifts in power, trade, and religious influence shaping global contours.

Events by Place

Byzantine Empire

In 700, the Byzantine Empire was ruled by Emperor Tiberius III, who had usurped the throne in 698 following a naval revolt triggered by the disastrous loss of Carthage to Umayyad Arab forces in the preceding year. This event marked the effective end of Byzantine control over North Africa, with the empire now confined primarily to Anatolia, the Balkans, and parts of southern Italy. Tiberius, originally a droungarios of the fleet named Apsimar, focused on stabilizing the core territories amid ongoing existential threats from Arab incursions.
The year occurred during the Twenty Years' Anarchy (695717), a phase of profound internal instability characterized by successive coups and short-lived emperors, which eroded administrative cohesion and military effectiveness. Externally, Umayyad armies continued raiding Anatolia, pressuring the eastern frontiers, while Tiberius organized defensive campaigns led by his brother Heraclius as monostrategos of Asia Minor; these yielded initial successes but ultimately failed to prevent the loss of Armenia to Arab control. To fortify the capital, Tiberius repaired Constantinople's sea walls, recognizing the vulnerability to naval assaults in light of recent defeats.
Administrative reforms emphasized the evolving theme system, whereby provinces were reorganized into military districts (themata) combining civil and martial authority to sustain soldier-farmers capable of defending against invasions without reliance on distant tagmata units. This adaptation, accelerated by territorial losses since the mid-7th century, aimed to distribute land grants (stratiotika ktemata) in exchange for hereditary military service, fostering resilience in Anatolia. Deposed Emperor Justinian II, exiled to Cherson after mutilation in 695, remained at large, his alliances with local forces foreshadowing further upheaval. Overall, 700 exemplified the empire's contraction and militarization, as it grappled with caliphal expansion without major pitched battles recorded that year.

Western Europe

In the Frankish kingdoms, Pepin of Herstal served as mayor of the palace, exercising de facto control over Austrasia, Neustria, and Burgundy since unifying them after his victory at the Battle of Tertry in 687, while nominal Merovingian kings like Childebert III (r. 695711) held symbolic authority. Pepin's administration focused on consolidating power through military campaigns against external threats, including ongoing skirmishes with the Frisians and internal stabilization, laying the groundwork for Carolingian dominance without major recorded battles precisely in 700.
In Hispania, Visigothic King Egica (r. 687702) governed a centralized kingdom encompassing most of the Iberian Peninsula and Septimania, marked by internal policies emphasizing Catholic orthodoxy and royal authority following the legal reforms of his predecessor Ervig. Egica's reign saw heightened persecution of Jews, culminating in the Seventeenth Council of Toledo in 694, where accusations of conspiracy with external forces led to enslavement or forced conversion for non-compliant Jews, reflecting efforts to enforce religious uniformity amid perceived threats from Muslim advances in North Africa. No large-scale military events are documented for 700, but Egica began associating his son Wittiza in governance around this period, ensuring dynastic continuity until his death.
In the Lombard Kingdom of Italy, King Cunipert died after a 12-year reign, succeeded by his young son Liutpert, whose brief eight-month rule ended with deposition by Duke Raginpert of Turin, who seized power amid factional strife between Bavarian and Lombard nobles. This transition highlighted ongoing instability in Italy, where Lombard rulers contended with Byzantine enclaves in the south and Frankish influences to the north, though Raginpert's usurpation was short-lived as he died soon after, leading to further civil conflict.

Britain

In 700 AD, Britain remained fragmented into multiple kingdoms following the withdrawal of Roman authority in the early 5th century, with Anglo-Saxon settlers having established dominance in the east and south. The Anglo-Saxon territories formed the core of what would become England, comprising seven principal kingdoms known as the Heptarchy: Northumbria, Mercia, East Anglia, Wessex, Essex, Sussex, and Kent. Northumbria held preeminence among these, extending from the Humber to the Forth and wielding cultural and military influence, supported by monastic centers like Lindisfarne and Jarrow. 
Celtic Britons had been displaced westward, retaining control in Wales, Cornwall, and parts of Cumbria and Strathclyde, where they maintained distinct languages and traditions amid ongoing raids and border conflicts. In the north, Pictish kingdoms dominated much of modern Scotland, while the Gaelic kingdom of Dl Riata held the western coasts, fostering early interactions that would shape Scotland. By this date, Christianization of the Anglo-Saxon kingdoms was largely complete, following missions from Rome and Ireland, with Roman practices prevailing after the Synod of Whitby in 664, though isolated pagan elements persisted in remote areas.
No major recorded battles or accessions occurred precisely in 700, but the period reflected a balance of power tilting toward Northumbria under King Aldfrith, whose scholarly court patronized learning, evidenced by early coinage and manuscript production. Mercia, under rulers like Cenred, was ascendant in the Midlands, constructing defenses against Welsh incursions that foreshadowed Offa's Dyke. Economic life centered on agriculture, trade via emporia like York and London, and artisanal crafts, with silver sceattas circulating as currency. Archaeological evidence from sites like Sutton Hoo tombs indicates elite wealth from tribute and commerce, though rural settlements predominated for the majority.

Islamic World

In 700 CE, the Umayyad Caliphate under Caliph Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan (r. 685705) focused on consolidating territorial gains and suppressing resistance in peripheral regions. Military campaigns targeted Berber tribes in North Africa, building on the 698 reconquest of Carthage by Hassan ibn al-Nu'man, to extend control westward into the Maghreb amid ongoing revolts led by figures like al-Kahina. These operations involved Umayyad armies comprising Arab troops and allied Berber converts, aiming to secure tax revenues and strategic ports for further Mediterranean expansion.
Administrative centralization advanced concurrently, with directives issued around 700 mandating Arabic as the sole language for government records and correspondence in Syria and beyond, replacing Greek and Persian to foster bureaucratic uniformity and reinforce Arab cultural dominance. This Arabization policy, implemented by governors like al-Hajjaj ibn Yusuf in Iraq, streamlined fiscal diwans and reduced reliance on non-Muslim administrators, enhancing loyalty to the Damascus court.
On the eastern front, preliminary advances occurred in Transoxiana, with Muslim forces occupying Kish amid skirmishes against local Sogdian rulers, setting the stage for deeper incursions under later governors. These efforts reflected the caliphate's strategy of border stabilization post-Second Fitna, prioritizing revenue extraction over immediate mass conversion, as non-Muslims continued paying jizya while mawali converts faced integration challenges. The year's activities underscored Abd al-Malik's pragmatic governance, balancing military pressure with institutional reforms to sustain an empire stretching from the Atlantic approaches to the Amu Darya River.

Asia

In China, the Tang dynasty under Empress Wu Zetian exercised authority over a territory that included much of East Asia and exerted influence into Central Asia via frontier protectorates established after campaigns against the Gktrks. Wu, who had declared the Second Zhou dynasty in 690 while retaining Tang administrative structures, governed from Luoyang in 700, emphasizing a meritocratic civil service examination system and patronage of Buddhism, which saw the construction of major temples like the Longmen Grottoes expansions. Her rule featured ongoing suppression of aristocratic factions and expansionist policies, though internal plots by her sons foreshadowed her 705 abdication.
Across the Korean Peninsula, the Silla kingdom, having allied with Tang forces to conquer Baekje in 660 and Goguryeo in 668, maintained de facto unification by 700 following victories in the Silla-Tang War (670676) that secured independence from Chinese suzerainty. Silla's King Sinmun (r. 681692) and successors centralized power, adopting Chinese-style bureaucracy and Buddhism as state religion, with capital at Gyeongju serving as a hub for continental influences.
In Japan, Emperor Monmu (r. 697707) oversaw the late Asuka period, marked by consolidation of imperial authority through land reforms and the promotion of Shinto-Buddhist syncretism, setting the stage for the Nara period's capital relocation in 710. The court compiled genealogies and myths in preparation for official histories like the Kojiki (completed 712), reflecting efforts to legitimize the Yamato lineage amid emulation of Tang models.
The Indian subcontinent featured fragmented polities, with the Chalukya dynasty controlling the Deccan plateau under Vikramaditya I's successors, while the Pallava kingdom in Tamil Nadu reached cultural heights under Narasimhavarman II (r. c. 700728), who commissioned the Kailasanatha Temple in Kanchipuram exemplifying Dravidian architecture. Rivalries between these powers persisted, alongside northern kingdoms like the Gurjara-Pratiharas emerging in Rajasthan, amid a post-Gupta era of regionalism without centralized empire.
In Southeast Asia, the Srivijaya maritime empire, centered on Sumatra, dominated straits trade routes connecting India and China, fostering Buddhist scholarship at centers like Borobudur's precursors and controlling vassals in Java and the Malay Peninsula by 700. Chenla in Cambodia transitioned toward Khmer consolidation, while emerging Thai polities in the north interacted with Mon kingdoms.
Central Asia saw declining Gktrk khaganates fragmented by Tang interventions and rising Tibetan Empire pressures under King Me Agtsom (r. 704755), with oasis states like those in the Tarim Basin serving as Silk Road conduits under shifting suzerainties. Umayyad Arab forces began probing Transoxiana frontiers around this time, though major conquests followed post-700.

Africa

In North Africa, the Umayyad Caliphate consolidated its control following the conquest of Carthage in 698, marking the effective end of Byzantine remnants and significant Berber resistance in the Maghreb. The Arab invasions, initiated in the mid-7th century, had progressed through military campaigns against local Christian and pagan polities, with key victories such as the Battle of the Masts in 655 securing naval dominance in the Mediterranean. By 700, Islamic governance was establishing administrative structures, including the appointment of governors like Musa ibn Nusayr, who would later expand further west, while gradual conversion to Islam among Berber populations began, facilitated by tribal alliances and the jizya tax system on non-Muslims.
Further south, in the Sahel region of West Africa, the Ghana Empire exercised dominance over trans-Saharan trade routes, taxing gold, salt, and ivory exchanged between North African merchants and sub-Saharan producers. Centered around the upper Niger River, Ghana's rulers, known as ghana or "warrior kings," maintained a prosperous state by 700, leveraging cavalry forces and fortified towns like Koumbi Saleh, which supported a multi-ethnic economy without direct Arab political control.
In Central Africa near Lake Chad, the precursors to the Kanem Empire emerged around 700 under the Kanembu people, forming a confederation that controlled pastoral and trade networks, setting the stage for later expansion into one of Africa's longest-lasting states. Meanwhile, in East Africa, the Kingdom of Aksum persisted amid declining Red Sea trade due to the rise of coastal Islamic principalities, though Bantu-speaking communities continued ironworking and agricultural expansion southward, with early Swahili coastal settlements fostering Indian Ocean commerce. Nubian kingdoms in the Sudan maintained autonomy through the Baqt treaty of 652 with Arab Egypt, paying tribute in slaves to avert invasion, thus preserving Christian institutions into the 8th century.

Mesoamerica

In central Mexico, the once-dominant city of Teotihuacan had undergone substantial decline by 700 CE, following widespread burning of structures around 600 CE and factors including internal conflict and resource strain, marking the transition to the Epiclassic period with emerging regional powers.
The Zapotec center of Monte Albn, a major highland urban complex established around 500 BCE, entered its terminal phase around 700 CE, with archaeological evidence indicating reduced activity and eventual abandonment by circa 750 CE due to socioeconomic shifts and possible external pressures.
In the Maya lowlands, city-states like Tikal experienced population expansion and monumental construction during the late 7th century, exemplified by the reign of Jasaw Chan K'awiil I (682734 CE), who oversaw victories against rivals such as Calakmul in 696 CE and initiated projects like Temple II, reflecting heightened political and ritual activity. This era underscored the Classic Maya's peak in urbanism, with hundreds of settlements supporting intensified agriculture and hieroglyphic recording of elite deeds.

North America

In the American Southwest, Ancestral Puebloan peoples transitioned during the Pueblo I period (circa 700900 CE) from dispersed pit-house settlements to clustered above-ground villages constructed with adobe and stone, reflecting adaptations to aridity and agricultural intensification through maize, beans, and squash cultivation. Great kivaslarge subterranean ceremonial structuresappeared as early as 600700 CE in areas like Mesa Verde, serving communal ritual functions amid environmental shifts.
Across the Midwest and Southeast, late Woodland period societies (extending to circa 1000 CE) exhibited subsistence changes, including increased reliance on maize horticulture introduced from Mesoamerica, which supported gradual population growth and mound-building traditions in regions like the American Bottom. Prior to the Mississippian cultural florescence (emerging around 7001000 CE), settlements remained small and dispersed, with evidence of Levanna-style projectile points indicating continued hunting practices alongside early farming from 700 CE onward.
In the Northeast and Great Lakes, Woodland cultures persisted with ceramic technologies, bow-and-arrow adoption, and seasonal resource exploitation, marking a continuum from earlier Hopewell influences without major disruptions recorded for 700 CE. Archaeological data from sites like Moccasin Bend highlight earthen mounds and village patterns sustained into the post-700 CE era, underscoring regional stability amid climatic variability.

South America

In the Andean highlands of present-day Peru, the Wari (Huari) polity, which had coalesced around 670 CE, advanced into the Cuzco Valley circa 700 AD, marking a key phase of territorial expansion and administrative consolidation across the central Andes. This incursion facilitated the establishment of outposts for resource management and cultural integration, with evidence including the deposition of eight large portrait jars, likely used in elite rituals or record-keeping with quipu-like threads. Wari influence supplanted waning local powers, promoting standardized architecture, textiles, and ceramic styles that unified disparate highland and coastal groups during the Middle Horizon period.
Further south, near Lake Titicaca in present-day Bolivia, the Tiwanaku center sustained growth from earlier phases, with monumental constructions like the Akapana Pyramid completed around 600 AD supporting a population of up to 20,000 and regional sway extending into northern Chile and Peru. Ongoing infrastructure, including enlarged stone-lined canals for agriculture and ritual platforms like the Gateway of the Sun (erected ca. 500 AD), underscored Tiwanaku's role as a ceremonial and economic hub, though its peak influence occurred later around 900 AD.
On Peru's northern coast, the Moche civilization, centered on urban complexes with advanced irrigation huacas, experienced environmental stressors from El Nio floods circa 630 AD, contributing to political fragmentation and the eclipse of centralized authority. Elite continuity persisted, as evidenced by a high-status priestess burial around 750 AD containing sacrificed attendants and sumptuary goods, reflecting adaptive responses amid socio-ecological pressures. Beyond the Andes, evidence of complex societies remains scant, with Amazonian and Patagonian populations primarily organized in small-scale, mobile groups reliant on foraging and rudimentary horticulture.

Events by Topic

Religion

In the Islamic Umayyad Caliphate, Caliph Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan (r. 685705) advanced religious and administrative consolidation by reforming the empire's currency around 696697 CE, issuing the first dinars and dirhams bearing exclusively Arabic inscriptions of Islamic phrases such as the shahada, while removing figural depictions and symbols from Byzantine and Sasanian precedents like crosses and fire altars. This aniconic monetary system symbolized the caliphate's shift toward a distinct Islamic identity, replacing multicultural coinage that had diluted religious uniformity during earlier conquests. Concurrently, Arab forces under Musa ibn Nusayr extended Islamic control in North Africa, capturing Tripoli circa 701 CE following the fall of Carthage in 698 CE, which diminished Christian communities in the region and accelerated conversions or displacements amid ongoing jihad campaigns.
In Christianity, the Byzantine Empire under Emperor Tiberius III (r. 698705) maintained Orthodox dominance amid defensive wars against Umayyad incursions, with religious life centered on Constantinople's patriarchal authority despite territorial losses in Syria and Egypt. In Western Europe, Anglo-Saxon kingdoms saw deepening Roman Christian influence, exemplified by the scholarly output of monasteries like Jarrow, where Bede (c. 673735) began chronicling ecclesiastical history, reflecting a synthesis of Celtic and Roman traditions post-Synod of Whitby (664 CE). North African Christianity faced existential pressure from these conquests, with Berber tribes increasingly adopting Islam, though pockets of resistance persisted in remote areas.
In East Asia, Chinese Empress Wu Zetian (r. 690705) elevated Buddhism as a state ideology, commissioning temples and scriptures to legitimize her rule, including the promotion of Maitreya eschatology that positioned her as a messianic figure, thereby integrating Buddhist cosmology with imperial authority during a period of Tang dynasty expansion. Mahayana Buddhism also gained traction in Indonesia around 700 CE, influencing maritime trade networks and syncretic practices with local animism. In India, Buddhist centers like Nalanda continued scholarly activity under Pala patronage precursors, though Hinduism's resurgence foreshadowed decline, with Madhyamaka philosopher Chandrakirti's works (active c. 600700 CE) refining emptiness doctrines amid royal support.

Art and Architecture

In Western Europe, early medieval architecture under Merovingian influence revived the basilica form by the early eighth century, adapting Roman precedents for Christian worship with wooden-roofed naves and simple apses. In Anglo-Saxon England, sculpted stone high crosses emerged around 700 AD, such as precursors to the eighth-century Bewcastle and Ruthwell crosses, featuring interlaced patterns, biblical scenes, and runic inscriptions that blended pagan and Christian motifs.
In the Byzantine Empire, pre-iconoclastic art around 700 AD continued the mosaic tradition from the sixth century, with gold-ground panels depicting Christ, saints, and imperial figures in churches like those in Ravenna and Constantinople, emphasizing spiritual symbolism over naturalism. This period saw the persistence of ivory carvings and illuminated manuscripts, though imperial edicts against icons issued in 726 AD by Emperor Leo III marked the onset of iconoclasm, disrupting figural representation.
Umayyad architecture advanced Islamic monumental building in the early eighth century, exemplified by the Great Mosque of Damascus, constructed between 706 and 715 AD on the site of a Roman temple of Jupiter, measuring approximately 157 meters by 100 meters with a courtyard, prayer hall, and minarets drawing from Syrian, Byzantine, and Persian elements. The mosque's mihrab niche, introduced around 707709 AD in Medina, represented an early innovation in directing prayer orientation. Similarly, the Umayyad Mosque of Aleppo, begun circa 710 AD, incorporated marble columns and mosaics evoking paradisiacal themes.
In Tang China, a golden age of artistic production flourished around 700 AD, with ceramic tomb figurinessuch as sancai-glazed (three-color lead-glaze) equestrian warriors, court ladies, and attendantsburied in elite graves to serve the deceased, reflecting cosmopolitan influences from Central Asia and realistic proportions up to 41.5 cm in height. These earthenware pieces, often from Henan province and dating to 700750 AD, showcased advancements in glazing techniques and secular themes amid Buddhist sculpture's dominance in temples.

Science and Technology

In China during the Tang dynasty, woodblock printing developed around 700 CE, involving the carving of text and images into wooden blocks inked and pressed onto paper, initially for reproducing Buddhist scriptures and later secular works, which facilitated broader access to knowledge beyond manual copying. This method represented an incremental advance over earlier stamping techniques, though full commercialization occurred later in the dynasty.
In Persia, vertical-axis windmills with reed sails appeared by the 7th century CE, designed to grind grain and pump water in desert environments by rotating around a vertical shaft, providing a reliable mechanical power source independent of water availability. These structures, oriented to catch prevailing winds, demonstrated early engineering adaptations to local geography and climate, influencing subsequent Islamic mechanical designs.
Elsewhere, technological progress remained tied to established traditions, such as ongoing refinements in Chinese metallurgy for cast iron production and the spread of stirrups from Asia to Europe via trade routes, enhancing cavalry effectiveness though not newly invented in 700 CE. No major breakthroughs in optics, mechanics, or empirical science are recorded precisely for this year, reflecting a period of consolidation rather than rapid innovation outside East Asia and the Near East.

Notable Individuals

Births

No notable births for the year 700 are documented in surviving primary sources such as Frankish charters, Byzantine chronicles, or early Islamic histories, which emphasize dynastic successions, conquests, and ecclesiastical appointments over individual nativities. This scarcity stems from the era's administrative focus on adult roles in governance and faith, where birth dates were incidental unless linked to inheritance or prophecy, often reconstructed retrospectively in hagiographies or genealogies centuries later without corroboration. Figures occasionally associated with circa 700, such as regional nobles or ascetics in later traditions, lack precise verification due to the oral and fragmentary nature of 8th-century records.

Deaths

Muhammad ibn al-anafiyyah (c. 633700), son of the fourth caliph Al ibn Ab lib and a key figure in early proto-Shia movements such as Kaysanism, died in Medina in 700, prompting some followers to claim he entered occultation rather than perish. His death marked the effective end of the Kaysaniyya sect's direct leadership, with succession passing to his son Ab Hshim.
Di Renjie (630700), a Tang dynasty statesman and chancellor under Empress Wu Zetian renowned for his role in judicial reforms and political counsel, died in 700 after serving in high office during a period of imperial consolidation.
Saint Werburga (d. c. 700), an Anglo-Saxon abbess and daughter of King Wulfhere of Mercia, died around 700; she founded monasteries including at Ely and Weedon and is venerated as patron of Chester for purported miracles involving wildlife.
Agricolus (c. 630700), bishop of Avignon noted for charitable works and intercession against natural calamities like locust plagues, died circa 700 and is locally invoked for favorable weather and harvests.
