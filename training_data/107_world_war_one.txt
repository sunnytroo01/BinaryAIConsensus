World War One, fought from 1914 to 1918, was the first truly global military conflict and one of the deadliest in human history. It killed approximately seventeen million people, including ten million soldiers and seven million civilians, wounded another twenty million, and destroyed four empires: the German, Austro-Hungarian, Ottoman, and Russian. It redrew the map of Europe and the Middle East, created the conditions that led to the rise of communism in Russia and fascism in Germany and Italy, and set in motion the events that would produce an even more devastating conflict just twenty-one years later. The "war to end all wars," as it was optimistically called, ended nothing. It was the beginning of a century of violence, upheaval, and transformation that continues to shape the world today.

The causes of World War One are among the most debated topics in historical scholarship, but several factors are widely recognized as contributing to the outbreak. The alliance system divided Europe into two hostile blocs: the Triple Alliance of Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy, and the Triple Entente of France, Russia, and Britain. These alliances were designed to deter aggression by ensuring that any attack on one member would trigger a response from all, but they had the opposite effect: they transformed what might have been a localized conflict into a continental war.

Imperialism created competition among European powers for colonies, markets, and resources in Africa, Asia, and the Pacific. Nationalism stoked ethnic tensions within the multi-ethnic empires of Austria-Hungary and the Ottoman Empire, where minority populations increasingly demanded independence or union with their ethnic homelands. Militarism, the glorification of military power and the arms race among European nations, created a culture in which war was seen not as a catastrophe to be avoided but as a noble enterprise to be embraced.

The immediate trigger was the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary by Gavrilo Princip, a Bosnian Serb nationalist, on June 28, 1914, in Sarajevo. Austria-Hungary, backed by Germany, issued an ultimatum to Serbia. Russia mobilized in support of Serbia. Germany, facing a two-front war against France and Russia, implemented the Schlieffen Plan, which called for a rapid invasion of France through neutral Belgium. Britain, which had guaranteed Belgian neutrality, declared war on Germany. Within weeks, most of Europe was at war.

The war that followed was unlike anything the world had ever seen. The romantic nineteenth-century conception of warfare, with cavalry charges, individual heroism, and decisive battles, was obliterated by the industrial reality of machine guns, barbed wire, artillery, poison gas, and trench warfare. On the Western Front, a continuous line of trenches stretched from the English Channel to the Swiss border, approximately four hundred and seventy-five miles of fortified positions in which millions of soldiers lived, fought, and died in conditions of almost unimaginable horror.

The trenches were narrow ditches, typically six to eight feet deep, reinforced with sandbags and timber, and separated from the enemy by a strip of devastated ground called no man's land, which could be as narrow as fifty yards or as wide as several hundred. The ground between the trenches was cratered by artillery, tangled with barbed wire, and littered with the bodies of soldiers killed in previous attacks. Life in the trenches was defined by mud, cold, rats, lice, disease, and the constant threat of death from snipers, artillery shells, and poison gas.

The battles of World War One were characterized by enormous casualty figures and negligible territorial gains. The Battle of the Somme, fought from July to November 1916, resulted in over one million casualties on both sides for a maximum advance of about seven miles. The first day of the Somme, July 1, 1916, remains the bloodiest day in the history of the British Army: nearly twenty thousand British soldiers were killed and another forty thousand wounded in a single day. The Battle of Verdun, fought from February to December 1916, produced approximately seven hundred thousand casualties, French and German combined, for no significant change in the front line.

New technologies transformed warfare but did not break the stalemate. Machine guns made frontal infantry assaults suicidal. Artillery became the dominant killer, responsible for approximately sixty percent of all casualties. Poison gas, first used by Germany in 1915, added a new dimension of horror but proved difficult to control and was eventually countered by gas masks. Tanks, introduced by Britain in 1916, showed promise but were too slow, too unreliable, and too few to achieve a decisive breakthrough. Aircraft evolved from unarmed observation platforms to fighters and bombers over the course of the war, establishing the foundations of air power that would dominate future conflicts.

The war at sea was dominated by the British Royal Navy's blockade of Germany, which sought to starve the Central Powers of food and raw materials, and Germany's submarine campaign, which sought to cut Britain's supply lines. The German decision to resume unrestricted submarine warfare in 1917, sinking any ship, including neutral ones, in waters around Britain, was the primary factor that drew the United States into the war.

The entry of the United States in April 1917 was the decisive factor in the war's outcome. American industrial capacity and two million fresh troops tipped the balance on the Western Front. Germany's Spring Offensive of 1918, a last desperate attempt to win the war before American forces arrived in strength, achieved initial breakthroughs but ultimately exhausted Germany's reserves. The Allied counteroffensive in the summer and autumn of 1918 drove the German army back across France and Belgium, and on November 11, 1918, Germany signed an armistice ending the fighting.

The Treaty of Versailles, signed in June 1919, imposed harsh terms on Germany: loss of territory, forced disarmament, admission of sole responsibility for the war, and massive reparations payments. These terms, widely perceived in Germany as unjust and humiliating, created a reservoir of resentment that Adolf Hitler would exploit in his rise to power. The treaty also created new nations from the wreckage of the defeated empires, including Poland, Czechoslovakia, Yugoslavia, and the Baltic states, drawing borders that often did not correspond to ethnic or cultural boundaries, creating new sources of tension that would persist for decades.

The Russian Revolution of 1917, triggered in part by the war's devastating toll on Russian society, overthrew the Tsarist autocracy and eventually brought the Bolsheviks to power under Vladimir Lenin. The establishment of the Soviet Union, the world's first communist state, created an ideological division that would define global politics for the rest of the twentieth century.

World War One shattered the nineteenth-century faith in progress, reason, and the civilizing power of European culture. The generation that survived the trenches, the "Lost Generation," produced literature, art, and philosophy marked by disillusionment, existential questioning, and a profound skepticism about the institutions, values, and certainties that had led them into the catastrophe. The war did not end war. It created the conditions for a second, even more destructive one. And the questions it raised about nationalism, imperialism, alliance systems, arms races, and the relationship between technology and destruction remain as relevant today as they were a century ago.
