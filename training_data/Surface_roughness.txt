Surface roughness
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Surface roughness refers to the finer spaced irregularities in a surface's texture that typically result from the inherent action of a production process or by material condition. It constitutes one component of overall surface texture, alongside waviness (broader deviations), lay (the direction of the surface pattern), and form (the general shape), and is quantified at a microscopic scale through deviations from an ideal flat surface.
In engineering and manufacturing, surface roughness is a critical quality parameter that directly impacts component performance, including dimensional accuracy, friction, wear, adhesion, heat transfer, corrosion resistance, and lubrication efficiency. For instance, smoother surfaces reduce drag in aerodynamic applications and enhance biocompatibility in medical devices, while controlled roughness can improve grip or oil retention in mechanical assemblies. Excessive roughness may lead to premature failure in high-precision parts, such as those in aerospace or automotive industries, underscoring its role in design, quality control, and functionality.
Common parameters for characterizing surface roughness include the arithmetic average roughness (Ra), which calculates the average absolute deviation of profile heights from the mean line over a sampling length; the root mean square roughness (Rq), which provides a statistical measure of deviations; the total profile height (Rt), the maximum peak-to-valley distance; and the ten-point height (Rz), the average of the five highest peaks and five deepest valleys, both indicating the extent of surface irregularities. These are defined in standards like ASME B46.1, which guides measurement and specification for ensuring consistency across manufacturing processes.
Measurement of surface roughness employs techniques such as stylus profilometry for contact-based profiling, optical interferometry for non-contact analysis, and scanning probe microscopy for nanoscale resolution, each suited to different scales and applications. Factors influencing roughness include machining parameters like cutting speed, feed rate, tool geometry, and material properties, with tighter control achieved through processes like polishing or grinding. Overall, optimizing surface roughness enhances product durability, efficiency, and reliability in diverse fields from optics to structural engineering.

Fundamentals

Definition and Scales

Surface roughness refers to the deviations of a real surface from its ideal geometric form, measured in the direction of the surface normal, and is characterized by the height, wavelength, and directional pattern of these surface irregularities. These deviations arise primarily from manufacturing processes such as machining, grinding, or polishing, where tool geometry, material properties, and process parameters influence the resulting texture.
Surface roughness is categorized into scales based on the wavelength of the irregularities, which reflects the spatial frequency of surface features. Macro-roughness encompasses broader features often visible to the naked eye and resulting from large-scale forming processes like casting or forging that introduce undulations. Micro-roughness involves finer features typically generated by intermediate manufacturing steps such as milling or turning, where tool marks and feed rates create periodic patterns. Nano-roughness includes the smallest features, emerging from advanced finishing techniques like chemical etching or atomic layer deposition, which produce variations at atomic- or molecular-scale. In standards such as ASME B46.1 and ISO 4287, the separation between roughness and waviness scales is defined by cutoff wavelengths (s) selectable in the range of 0.08 mm to 8 mm, depending on the application.
Key concepts in describing surface roughness include lay, wavelength, and amplitude. Lay denotes the predominant direction of the surface pattern, usually aligned with the manufacturing tool pathfor instance, parallel to the cutting direction in lathe operations or perpendicular in grinding. Wavelength, denoted as , represents the horizontal spacing between repeating surface features, such as the distance between consecutive tool marks. Amplitude refers to the vertical height deviations of the surface peaks and valleys from a reference level. To visualize, consider a two-dimensional surface profile resembling a wavy line with irregular peaks and troughs; the lay would indicate the overall orientation of these waves, wavelength the repeat distance along the horizontal axis, and amplitude the peak-to-trough height.
A foundational aspect of roughness analysis is the mean line, a reference line that divides the surface profile such that the area above it equals the area below, providing a baseline for measuring deviations. Profile deviation then describes the vertical distances from this mean line to the actual surface points, capturing both positive (peaks) and negative (valleys) excursions without quantifying them statistically here. These elements establish the terminology for assessing how roughness influences functional performance in engineering contexts.

Historical Context

The understanding of surface roughness originated in the early 20th century amid advancements in precision manufacturing, particularly with the development of stylus-based profilometers in the 1930s. In 1933, E. J. Abbott and F. A. Firestone at the University of Michigan introduced the first profilometer, a mechanical device using a diamond-tipped stylus to trace surface profiles and quantify deviations from flatness, enabling more objective assessment beyond visual or manual inspection. By 1936, Abbott commercialized an improved version through the Profilometer Company, which measured roughness via electrical amplification of stylus movements, marking a shift from qualitative to quantitative evaluation in industries like automotive parts production. These early instruments laid the groundwork for metrology but were limited to basic profiles, often requiring manual operation.
Post-World War II, the rapid expansion of automotive and aerospace sectors drove standardization efforts to ensure component reliability and interchangeability, as inconsistent surface finishes contributed to failures in high-stress applications like aircraft engines. In the late 1940s, the American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME) formalized the arithmetic average roughness parameter, Ra, in its B46.1 standard of 1940 (updated in 1947), defining it as the average absolute deviation of profile heights from the mean line over a sampling length, which became a cornerstone for specifying tolerances in manufacturing drawings. This parameter simplified communication of surface quality requirements, influencing global practices in post-war reconstruction and military-to-civilian technology transfers. By the 1950s, comparator blocks with etched roughness samples emerged as visual aids for calibration, further standardizing inspections in these industries.
The 1970s saw a transition from manual to automated measurement systems, spurred by electronic advancements, with stylus profilometers incorporating digital data acquisition for faster, repeatable profiling of machined surfaces. From the late 1970s, analog systems evolved into digital ones, allowing computational analysis of profiles and reducing operator error in quality control. In the 1980s, the advent of computing introduced fractal analysis to characterize multiscale roughness, as proposed by Mandelbrot in 1984, where the fractal dimension quantifies self-similar irregularities across length scales, providing a scale-invariant descriptor beyond traditional parameters like Ra. This approach revealed limitations in linear metrics for complex surfaces, influencing tribology research.
The 1990s marked the shift toward three-dimensional areal characterization, extending 2D profile methods to volumetric data as optical and stylus technologies advanced, with initial concepts for areal parameters emerging to capture isotropic roughness in modern machining. The ISO 25178 series, developed from late-1990s deliberations by ISO TC 213, was officially numbered in 2005 and published starting in 2010, redefining texture terms for areal metrology and incorporating invariants like segmentation for functional analysis. Recent post-2020 developments integrate artificial intelligence for predictive modeling, particularly in additive manufacturing, where machine learning algorithms forecast surface roughness from process parameters like layer thickness and scan speed, enabling real-time optimization and reducing post-processing needs. These AI-driven methods address gaps in pre-2000 standards, which emphasized 2D parameters and overlooked predictive capabilities for emerging fabrication techniques.

Importance and Applications

Engineering and Manufacturing Impacts

Surface roughness plays a pivotal role in manufacturing processes, directly affecting machinability, coating adhesion, and fatigue life of components. In turning operations, excessive surface roughness generated by high feed rates or improper tool geometry can reduce tool life and increase cutting forces, leading to diminished overall process efficiency. Similarly, in milling, uneven roughness profiles from vibration or spindle speed variations compromise surface integrity, often requiring secondary operations to achieve functional tolerances. Grinding processes, while capable of producing finer finishes, are sensitive to wheel wear, where increased roughness accelerates abrasive degradation and elevates energy consumption during production.
Optimal surface roughness enhances coating adhesion by promoting mechanical interlocking and increasing contact area between substrate and coating layers, thereby improving durability in applications like protective films on machined parts. However, overly rough surfaces can trap contaminants or create stress risers, weakening adhesion strength and leading to delamination under load. Regarding fatigue life, machined surfaces with lower roughness exhibit higher endurance limits by minimizing crack initiation sites from peaks and valleys; for instance, in high-cycle fatigue tests of additive manufactured components, reducing roughness through post-processing can improve fatigue strength by up to 50% under cyclic loading.
In product performance, surface roughness critically influences assembly fit, sealing effectiveness, and aesthetic quality across industries. Controlled roughness ensures precise tolerances in press fits and interference assemblies, preventing misalignment or excessive wear during mating of components. Higher surface roughness reduces dimensional accuracy and precision fit due to surface profile variations that impact measurements and assembly tolerances. Smoother surfaces improve accuracy. For sealing applications, smoother surfaces reduce leakage paths in gaskets and O-rings, enhancing pressure retention in hydraulic systems. Aesthetically, polished low-roughness finishes improve visual appeal in consumer goods, correlating with perceived quality in automotive exteriors and electronic casings. In the automotive sector, piston-cylinder interfaces demand specific roughness levelstypically 0.20.8 mto balance lubrication retention for reduced friction while maintaining sealing against blow-by gases, thereby optimizing engine efficiency and emissions. In electronics manufacturing, PCB pad roughness affects solder joint formation; moderate roughness improves wetting and intermetallic bond strength in lead-free soldering, reducing void formation and enhancing thermal cycling reliability.
Economically, achieving low surface roughness incurs higher production costs due to extended machining times, specialized tooling, and post-processing steps like polishing or chemical treatment, which can double part expenses in high-precision components. These investments yield benefits in longevity, such as extended service life in fatigue-prone parts, often justifying the premium through reduced warranty claims and maintenance. In additive manufacturing, post-2020 research highlights roughness challenges in 3D-printed lightweight structures, where as-printed surfaces with Ra values exceeding 10 m compromise compressive strength and fatigue resistance in lattice designs; hybrid approaches combining printing with CNC finishing have demonstrated up to 70% roughness reduction, enabling viable lightweight components without excessive material use. As of 2025, advancements in AI-optimized hybrid manufacturing have achieved up to 80% roughness reductions in polymer AM processes, improving sustainability. Additionally, higher surface roughness in additively manufactured parts decreases corrosion resistance by increasing exposed surface area, creating crevices for corrosive agents, and disrupting passive films, leading to higher susceptibility to pitting and localized corrosion. Smoother finishes or post-processing (e.g., polishing, electropolishing) improve resistance, though residual stresses can also play a major role.

Effects in Tribology and Surface Interactions

Surface roughness significantly influences tribological performance by promoting asperity-asperity interactions in contacting surfaces, which increases the real area of contact and elevates the coefficient of friction compared to smooth interfaces. In the Greenwood-Williamson model, rough surfaces are characterized by multiple spherical asperities with varying summit heights following a Gaussian distribution; under load, only the tallest asperities deform elastically, leading to discrete contact spots that contribute to higher frictional resistance through shear at these junctions. This effect is pronounced in dry or boundary lubrication regimes, where friction coefficients can rise significantly for roughness values exceeding 0.1 m Ra, as asperity interlocking amplifies tangential forces.
Surface texture also modifies the Stribeck curve, which plots friction coefficient against the lubrication parameter (viscosity speed / load), by shifting the transition from boundary to mixed and hydrodynamic regimes. Increased roughness raises the minimum friction point and broadens the mixed regime, as asperities penetrate the lubricant film, delaying full hydrodynamic separation; for instance, in textured surfaces with dimples or grooves, optimal patterns can reduce friction by up to 30% in the mixed regime by trapping lubricant and minimizing direct asperity contact.
Roughness exacerbates wear mechanisms, including abrasive, adhesive, and fatigue types, by concentrating stresses at peaks and valleys that accelerate material removal. In abrasive wear, sharp asperities act as cutting tools, increasing wear rates proportionally with roughness amplitude; adhesive wear intensifies as valleys trap debris, promoting material transfer between surfaces under high loads. Fatigue wear arises from cyclic loading on asperity junctions, leading to subsurface crack initiation and spalling, particularly in rolling contacts. In bearings, excessive roughness (e.g., >0.2 m Ra) can double fatigue life reduction due to pitting from asperity-induced stress concentrations, while in gears, it heightens scuffing risks by disrupting lubricant films and promoting adhesive seizure under high-speed meshing.
In lubricated conditions, smoother surfaces generally enhance wear resistance by reducing friction and asperity contact; higher roughness often increases wear rates due to greater abrasion and friction. These effects vary with dry versus lubricated conditions and are influenced by specific parameters such as skewness and kurtosis, where negative skewness (indicating more valleys for lubricant retention) and appropriate kurtosis values can improve wear resistance under boundary and mixed lubrication regimes, though an optimal roughness often exists depending on the application.
In lubrication interactions, surface roughness alters oil film thickness and elastohydrodynamic lubrication (EHL) efficacy, often reducing film stability in high-pressure contacts like gears and cams. Roughness protrusions disrupt the pressure buildup in the inlet zone, thinning the central film by 10-20% for composite roughness >0.3 m, and increasing the lambda ratio (film thickness / roughness) sensitivity; in full EHL, smooth surfaces maintain thicker films (>1 m), but roughness transitions contacts to mixed regimes where asperities intermittently touch, elevating wear.
The real contact area fraction , influenced by roughness, relates to load F and hardness H via the approximation $ A = \eta \frac{F}{H} $, where A is the real contact area. This derives from plastic deformation theory: under full yielding, the load is supported at the hardness pressure H (3Y, with Y the yield strength), so for complete contact, $ A_r = \frac{F}{H} $; however, on rough surfaces, only a fraction of the nominal area deforms into contact ( 0.01-0.1 for typical engineering roughness, decreasing with finer texture), yielding the scaled form. To arrive at this, start with Hertzian elastic contact for a single asperity (load $ w \propto \delta^{3/2} $, area $ a \propto \delta $), sum over asperity distribution for rough surfaces (real area $ A_r \propto F $), then for plastic dominance in boundary conditions, cap pressure at H, resulting in linear proportionality modulated by from roughness statistics.
Beyond tribology, surface roughness affects biological interactions, enhancing implant biocompatibility by promoting cell adhesion and osseointegration; moderate roughness (1-2 m Ra) on titanium implants increases osteoblast proliferation by 50-100% via improved protein adsorption, though excessive roughness (>5 m) can induce inflammation. In optics, roughness induces scattering losses proportional to the square of the roughness amplitude over wavelength, with rms roughness > /10 (e.g., 50 nm at 500 nm light) causing >1% total integrated scatter, degrading performance in mirrors and lenses. Recent studies on nano-roughness (10-100 nm scales) highlight its role in self-cleaning surfaces, where hierarchical textures mimic lotus leaves to achieve water contact angles >150, repelling contaminants; post-2020 advancements include laser-textured polymers with nano-protrusions enduring 1000 abrasion cycles while maintaining superhydrophobicity for applications in solar panels and textiles.

Measurement Methods

Contact-Based Techniques

Contact-based techniques for measuring surface roughness involve physical interaction between a probe and the surface, allowing direct tracing of the topography to generate a profile. These methods, primarily used in engineering and manufacturing, provide high-resolution data for assessing surface texture but require careful handling to avoid altering the sample. Stylus profilometry, the most established approach, employs a diamond-tipped stylus that traverses the surface along a straight line, capturing vertical displacements as it follows the contours.
In stylus profilometry, the stylus tiptypically with a radius of 25 mmoves at a constant speed over a sampling length, which is divided into evaluation and traverse segments to isolate the surface profile from form errors. Cutoff filters are applied to separate waviness (longer wavelengths, s) from roughness (shorter wavelengths, c), with c often set between 0.08 mm and 8 mm according to standards like ISO 4287. These instruments achieve lateral resolutions down to 0.1 m and vertical resolutions of about 1 nm, making them suitable for precise profile measurements on metals and polymers.
Beyond mechanical stylus tracing, other contact methods utilize inductive and capacitive sensors to detect surface variations without direct scraping. Inductive sensors measure changes in electromagnetic inductance caused by surface proximity, while capacitive sensors detect alterations in electrical capacitance between the probe and surface. These are often integrated into portable or automated systems for industrial applications. Datum establishment in these setups can be skidded, where a shoe or skid rides on the surface to provide a reference plane, or skidless, which uses optical or mechanical means for more accurate alignment on curved or irregular surfaces, reducing errors from skid averaging.
Contact-based techniques offer advantages in accuracy for one-dimensional profiles, particularly on conductive or rigid materials, and are calibrated using step height artifacts traceable to national standards, ensuring traceability to sub-nanometer levels. However, limitations include potential surface damage from the probe's contact pressure (typically 0.11 mN), which can scratch soft materials, and challenges in measuring steep slopes beyond the stylus tip angle (around 90). To address these, hybrid contact-optical methods have emerged since 2020, combining stylus probing with real-time optical feedback for in-situ monitoring in manufacturing processes like additive fabrication.
While non-contact techniques avoid these risks for delicate surfaces, contact methods remain essential for validating roughness parameters in controlled environments.

Non-Contact Techniques

Non-contact techniques for measuring surface roughness offer advantages over contact-based methods by avoiding surface deformation and enabling rapid assessment of delicate or complex geometries, such as those in additive manufacturing components. These methods primarily utilize optical principles to capture 3D topography without physical probing, achieving high-speed data acquisition suitable for in-line industrial applications.
Optical interferometry stands as a cornerstone of non-contact measurement, leveraging interference patterns from light waves to reconstruct surface profiles. White light interferometry (WLI), also known as coherence scanning interferometry (CSI), employs broadband light sources to scan vertically and detect coherence peaks, providing vertical resolutions below 1 nm for smooth surfaces up to several micrometers in height range. Phase-shifting interferometry (PSI), a variant using monochromatic light, achieves sub-nanometer vertical precision (e.g., /1000 0.6 nm for = 600 nm) by analyzing phase shifts in interference fringes, though it is limited to step heights under /4 (150 nm) without additional scanning. However, both techniques face field-of-view limitations, typically restricted to small areas (e.g., 0.52 mm laterally), necessitating stitching for larger samples.
Confocal microscopy and laser scanning methods extend non-contact capabilities to structured 3D topography mapping, particularly for optically rough surfaces. Confocal laser scanning microscopy uses a pinhole to eliminate out-of-focus light, enabling vertical resolutions around 110 nm and lateral resolutions of 0.20.5 m, ideal for areal measurements over fields up to several millimeters. Laser scanning often incorporates structured light projection, such as fringe patterns, to triangulate surface points and generate full 3D datasets, with vertical resolutions of 1050 nm suitable for complex geometries in additively manufactured parts. Coherence scanning interferometry (CSI), bridging interferometry and confocal approaches, combines vertical scanning with coherence detection to achieve nanoscale height resolution (down to 1 nm) while handling slopes up to 45, making it robust for varied topographies.
Advanced non-contact methods push resolutions to the nanoscale, with atomic force microscopy (AFM) operating in non-contact or tapping (semi-contact) modes to measure roughness without direct tip-surface abrasion. In non-contact mode, the AFM tip oscillates near the sample in the attractive van der Waals regime, yielding sub-nanometer lateral and vertical resolutions (e.g., <1 nm) over scan areas from nanometers to micrometers, though it is slower for large fields. Emerging post-2020 developments integrate AI-enhanced image analysis for real-time roughness evaluation in additive manufacturing, where machine learning models process optical or laser-scan images to predict parameters like Ra with accuracies exceeding 90%, enabling in-process monitoring and reducing post-fabrication inspection needs.
Key comparison metrics among these techniques highlight trade-offs in resolution and data type. Interferometric methods excel in vertical resolution (<1 nm) but offer moderate lateral resolution (0.51 m due to diffraction limits), favoring profile data for smooth surfaces, whereas confocal and laser scanning provide balanced resolutions (lateral 0.21 m, vertical 110 nm) and are better suited for areal 3D datasets on rough or curved geometries. AFM achieves the highest resolutions (sub-nm laterally and vertically) for nano-scale features but is limited to small areas, contrasting the broader fields of optical techniques. Overall, selection depends on surface scale and geometry, with optical methods prioritizing speed for industrial use.

Roughness Parameters

Profile Roughness Parameters

Profile roughness parameters quantify the texture of a surface along a single linear trace, known as the profile, separating it into amplitude (height-related), spacing (lateral), slope, counting, and functional aspects. These parameters are standardized in ISO 21920-2:2021, which specifies terms and calculation methods for surface texture evaluation by profile methods, replacing earlier standards like ISO 4287 to incorporate updated functional parameters such as those related to material ratio for better correlation with performance. Calculations are performed over an evaluation length, typically comprising multiple sampling lengths to average out local variations, ensuring statistical reliability.

Graphically, Ra illustrates the typical "average" roughness height, smoothing over outliers like deep scratches, making it widely used for general quality control due to its simplicity and low sensitivity to extreme features. The root mean square (RMS) deviation, Rq, weights larger deviations more heavily and is given by

This parameter, analogous to standard deviation in statistics, provides a quadratic measure of roughness amplitude, often slightly higher than Ra for non-sinusoidal profiles. For peak-to-valley extents, Rz represents the average maximum height across the profile, computed as the mean of (maximum peak height minus maximum valley depth) over five equal sampling sections within the evaluation length; graphically, it captures the "ten-point" average roughness depth, emphasizing repetitive high-low excursions rather than a single outlier. In contrast, Rt denotes the total profile height, simply the difference between the absolute highest peak and lowest valley over the entire evaluation length, offering a direct visual measure of the full vertical span but sensitive to isolated defects.
Spacing and slope parameters address the horizontal and angular characteristics of the profile. The mean spacing between profile elements, RSm, quantifies the average distance between consecutive local maxima (peaks) or the mean width of repeating motifs, calculated by summing peak-to-peak distances and dividing by the number of intervals; graphically, it reflects the dominant wavelength of surface undulations, useful for identifying periodic machining marks. Counting parameters, such as peak count Pc, enumerate discrete features by counting the number of profile crossings above a specified threshold (typically 10% of Rz) per unit length, providing a density metric for protrusions; graphically, it visualizes the frequency of resolvable peaks, aiding in assessing contact points for wear prediction.
The bearing ratio curve, or Abbott-Firestone curve, derives functional parameters from the cumulative distribution of profile heights, plotting the material ratio (bearing length percentage) against normalized height levels from the highest peak downward. The relative material ratio Rmr(dc) (or Rmr(p,dc)) is the material ratio at the section level cp + dc, where cp corresponds to material ratio p (default 0%) and dc is the relative profile depth (usually <0%), originating from integrating the probability density function of heights, offering insight into load-bearing behavior at specific depths below a reference level. Peak material volume Vmp, a related functional metric, quantifies the volume of material above the core region in the peaks (typically from the highest peak down to a material ratio threshold, e.g., 5%), crucial for assessing initial wear in tribological applications. These parameters, updated in ISO 21920-2 from ISO 4287, emphasize functional relevance over purely geometric description.

Areal Roughness Parameters

Areal roughness parameters, as defined in the ISO 25178-2:2021 standard, extend traditional profile-based metrics to three-dimensional surface topographies, enabling the characterization of complete areal fields rather than linear traverses. These parameters account for the full spatial variation in height, accommodating both isotropic and anisotropic textures prevalent in modern manufacturing processes. The ISO 25178-2:2021 standard specifies over 30 such parameters, categorized into amplitude, spacing, hybrid, functional, and feature-based types, with computations performed on digitized surface data after form and waviness removal. Unlike profile parameters, areal metrics capture interactions across the entire evaluation area, providing more representative assessments for functional performance in applications like sealing and friction.

Standards and Advanced Characterization

International Standards

The primary international standards for surface roughness specification, measurement, and reporting are developed under the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) Geometrical Product Specifications (GPS) framework, which ensures consistent terminology, parameters, and procedures across industries. ISO 4287:1997 defined key profile roughness parameters, such as the arithmetical mean deviation $ R_a $, and established sampling lengths equal to the cut-off wavelength $ \lambda_c $ of the profile filter to separate short-wavelength roughness from longer-wavelength waviness components. These standards specified Gaussian filters as the default for profile separation, with phase-correct variants to minimize distortion in the roughness profile during filtering.
ISO 4288:1996 complemented this by providing rules and procedures for assessing surface texture conformity, including guidelines for stylus instrument measurements, tolerance comparisons, and the application of the 16% rule for parameter evaluation across multiple sampling lengths. Although these standards were withdrawn following the publication of updated equivalents, they remain foundational for legacy applications and continue to influence global practices.
The ISO 21920 series (Parts 13, published 2021) modernizes the profile method, replacing ISO 4287 and 4288 with refined terms, parameters, and graphical symbols for technical drawings, including default cut-off values and enhanced rules for specification to improve interoperability. For areal (3D) surface texture metrology, the ISO 25178 seriesparticularly Part 2 (updated 2021)defines over 40 parameters for characterizing functional surfaces, incorporating segmentation of the surface into peak, core, and valley zones based on the areal material ratio curve to assess material removal or addition effects. These parameters are organized into invariance classes, such as those robust to scale changes (e.g., fractal-based) or rotation, enabling consistent evaluation across measurement scales.
In the United States, ASME B46.1-2019 adapts ISO GPS principles for practical implementation, defining surface texture constituents (roughness, waviness, lay) and parameters while providing US-specific guidance on measurement and control. It includes rules for symbolization on engineering drawings, where the standard roughness symbola check mark with a horizontal linecan be annotated with the $ R_a $ value and tolerance to indicate required surface finish.
Post-2020 developments address advanced applications, with ISO 21920 incorporating provisions for nano-scale textures through finer resolution parameters and cut-offs suitable for micro- and nano-manufacturing. Emerging efforts also integrate surface roughness into digital twin frameworks under ISO 23247 (2021), enabling virtual simulation and prediction of texture evolution in manufacturing processes like additive methods.

Multi-scale analysis extends fractal approaches by addressing the limitations of assuming scale-invariance, particularly for non-stationary surfaces where roughness varies with measurement scale. Wavelet decomposition techniques enable hierarchical separation of macro- and micro-scale features, decomposing the surface profile into approximation and detail coefficients at multiple resolution levels to isolate contributions from different frequency bands. This method is motivated by real-world surfaces, such as those in manufacturing or biology, where long-wavelength undulations (form) coexist with short-wavelength asperities (roughness), requiring scale-specific analysis for accurate functional assessment.
In applications, fractal models facilitate wear prediction by relating DfD_fDf and scaling parameters to contact mechanics and material removal rates, often using Weierstrass-Mandelbrot functions to simulate evolving topographies under load. Similarly, these models support topography simulation for design optimization, generating synthetic surfaces that mimic measured fractal signatures. Recent advancements integrate machine learning for estimating fractal parameters, such as in biomimetic superhydrophobic surfaces inspired by natural textures like lotus leaves, where convolutional neural networks analyze topographic data to predict DfD_fDf and enhance wetting property simulations.
Despite these benefits, fractal and multi-scale analyses remain non-standardized and serve as complements to ISO-defined roughness parameters like RaR_aRa or SaS_aSa, lacking formal metrology protocols for routine industrial use.
