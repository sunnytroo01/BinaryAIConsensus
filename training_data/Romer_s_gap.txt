Romer's gap
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Romer's gap refers to a notable hiatus in the fossil record of early tetrapods, spanning approximately 15 to 20 million years from around 360 to 345 million years ago, during the transition from the Late Devonian to the Early Carboniferous periods. This gap, characterized by a scarcity of skeletal remains of four-limbed vertebrates, obscures key stages in the evolutionary shift from aquatic fish-like ancestors to fully terrestrial animals, despite the presence of pre- and post-gap fossils indicating active diversification of tetrapods.
The phenomenon was first highlighted in the mid-20th century by American paleontologist Alfred Sherwood Romer (18941973), who observed the abrupt disappearance of Devonian tetrapod fossils and their sparse reappearance in the Carboniferous, prompting questions about the completeness of the geological record during this interval. Romer's analysis suggested that the gap might reflect genuine biological rarity rather than mere preservational bias, as tetrapods appear to have originated in the Late Devonian but left few traces until the Tournaisian stage of the Early Carboniferous. Tetrapods from Scottish sites like East Kirkton Quarry, now dated to within the gap at approximately 346 million years ago, exhibit advanced adaptations including strengthened limbs, lung development, and reduced reliance on water for reproduction, marking the onset of amniote-like traits.
Several hypotheses have been proposed to explain the gap's causes, with evidence pointing to environmental factors like low atmospheric oxygen levels (around 1215%) during this time, which may have constrained the survival and terrestrialization of early air-breathing vertebrates and arthropods following the Frasnian-Famennian mass extinction. This oxygen-poor interval, coinciding with the Hangenberg event, likely acted as an evolutionary bottleneck, favoring only resilient lineages and limiting diversification until oxygen levels rebounded in the mid-Carboniferous. However, recent discoveries of diverse fauna suggest that oxygen levels may not have been as restrictive as previously thought. Other factors, such as acidic depositional environments or sampling biases in non-marine sediments, have been considered but are less supported compared to the oxygenation model.
Recent discoveries have begun to illuminate this obscured period, with radiometric dating in 2025 revising the age of Scottish fossils from East Kirkton Quarry including the early tetrapod Westlothiana lizziaeto 346 million years ago, firmly placing them within the gap and revealing a more diverse early vertebrate fauna than previously thought. Additionally, clawed footprints from the Snowy Plains Formation in Australia, dated to approximately 359354 million years ago in the early Tournaisian, provide the earliest evidence of crown-group amniotes during the gap, suggesting that tetrapod cladogenesis and terrestrial adaptations were underway earlier and more rapidly than earlier estimates indicated. These findings, combined with ongoing excavations in sites like Nova Scotia and the Scottish Borders, indicate that Romer's gap may represent a preservational artifact rather than a complete evolutionary stasis, reshaping timelines for the conquest of land by vertebrates.

Discovery and Definition

Historical Recognition

The historical recognition of what would become known as Romer's gap originated with the observations of American paleontologist Alfred Sherwood Romer, who systematically documented the paucity of tetrapod fossils spanning the Devonian-Carboniferous boundary. In his 1956 paper, Romer explicitly proposed the concept of a major temporal hiatus in the tetrapod record, based on the conspicuous absence of vertebrate remains immediately following the Late Devonian, a pattern he identified through comprehensive reviews of global collections up to that time. This proposal marked the first formal acknowledgment of the gap as a distinct feature in vertebrate paleontology, shifting focus from isolated discoveries to broader evolutionary patterns.
Romer's analysis centered on the well-known Late Devonian tetrapods, such as Ichthyostega from Greenland, which he described as primitive, aquatic forms with limb-like appendages suggestive of early terrestrial adaptations, contrasting sharply with more advanced amphibians appearing later in the stratigraphic record. He highlighted the subsequent appearance of robust, labyrinthodont forms in the Carboniferous, and estimated the intervening hiatus at 15-20 million years, during which no transitional tetrapod fossils had been documented despite the relative abundance of fish and invertebrate remains from the same strata. This disparity underscored Romer's view of the gap as a critical void in understanding the transition to land life.
Early 20th-century fossil-hunting efforts, including those by Romer himself, had repeatedly failed to uncover tetrapod material from the problematic interval, even as collections from Devonian and later Carboniferous sites grew. Romer's extensive work on amphibian evolution, beginning in the 1920s with studies of Permian and Carboniferous labyrinthodonts and culminating in major monographs, involved fieldwork across North America and Europe that yielded abundant pre- and post-gap specimens but nothing intermediate; for instance, his 1950s expeditions to Carboniferous exposures in Nova Scotia reinforced the pattern of scarcity without bridging the divide. These endeavors, documented in his prolific output on vertebrate phylogeny, laid the groundwork for recognizing the gap as a persistent challenge in paleontological research.
Romer initially interpreted the gap not solely as a sampling artifact but as evidence of potential real evolutionary stasis, positing that early tetrapods might have experienced a prolonged phase of limited diversification or retreat to marginal aquatic habitats, delaying their radiation and fossil preservation until more favorable conditions in the mid-Carboniferous. This perspective, grounded in his synthesis of morphological and stratigraphic data, influenced subsequent debates on whether the absence reflected biological realities or geological biases alone.

Scope and Characteristics

Romer's gap constitutes a notable hiatus in the tetrapod fossil record, spanning approximately 15 million years from the latest Famennian stage of the Late Devonian (~359 Ma) to the earliest Visean stage of the Early Carboniferous (~345 Ma). This period, first highlighted by Alfred Romer in the mid-20th century, represents a near-absence of skeletal remains of early tetrapods, the four-limbed vertebrates that mark the transition from aquatic to terrestrial life. The gap underscores a critical phase in vertebrate evolution, where the fossil evidence abruptly ceases after the diversification of Devonian forms, creating a perceived discontinuity in the lineage leading to modern amphibians and amniotes.
Key characteristics of Romer's gap include the sudden disappearance of the diverse Late Devonian tetrapod assemblages, which featured primarily aquatic or semi-aquatic taxa adapted to near-shore environments, followed by a prolonged delay in the appearance of more derived Carboniferous lineages such as amphibamids and temnospondyls. These Carboniferous groups exhibit enhanced terrestrial adaptations, including stronger limbs and smaller body sizes more suited to land movement, contrasting sharply with the fin-like limbs and larger aquatic bodies of their Devonian predecessors. The phenomenon is not indicative of a complete extinction but rather a stark reduction in preservable remains, emphasizing the biological shift from predominantly aquatic habitats to increasingly terrestrial ones during this interval.
Recent advancements in radiometric dating techniques, particularly U-Pb zircon geochronology, have refined the temporal boundaries of the gap to more precisely 360345 Ma, incorporating new fossil-bearing strata from sites like East Kirkton Quarry in Scotland, now dated to approximately 346 Ma. These improvements, combined with discoveries of Tournaisian tetrapod faunas, have narrowed the perceived duration and highlighted that the hiatus is likely an artifact of uneven sampling and preservation biases rather than a true evolutionary void. In distinction from the subsequent broader Carboniferous tetrapod radiationcharacterized by explosive diversification and global distributionRomer's gap specifically captures the transitional challenges of this near-shore to terrestrial shift, where environmental and taphonomic factors limited fossilization.

Geological Context

Temporal Extent

Romer's gap commences at approximately 358.9 Ma, coinciding with the conclusion of the Famennian stage in the Late Devonian period and the Devonian-Carboniferous boundary. This temporal boundary is prominently associated with the Hangenberg extinction event, a significant mass extinction that severely impacted marine and terrestrial ecosystems, including early tetrapod lineages. The event's precise timing has been constrained through high-resolution geochronology, placing it between 358.97 0.11 Ma and 358.89 0.20 Ma.
The gap terminates around 345 Ma, marking the transition to the Visean stage of the Early Carboniferous, where the initial unequivocal tetrapod fossils following the interval emerge in the stratigraphic record. This endpoint aligns closely with the latter portion of the Tournaisian stage, the inaugural stage of the Mississippian subsystem within the Carboniferous period. Overall, Romer's gap encompasses a duration of roughly 13 to 15 million years, predominantly occupying the Tournaisian stage (spanning approximately 358.9 Ma to 346.7 Ma).
The chronological limits of Romer's gap have been established using advanced geochronological techniques, including U-Pb dating of zircon crystals from volcanic ash layers interbedded with boundary strata, which provides precise absolute ages for the Hangenberg event and the Devonian-Carboniferous transition. Complementing this, conodont biostratigraphy serves as the primary tool for correlating the boundary globally, defined by the first appearance datum of the conodont species Siphonodella sulcata within its evolutionary lineage from Siphonodella n. sp. A. These methods together ensure robust calibration of the gap's extent against the international geological timescale.

Stratigraphic Position

Romer's gap occupies a stratigraphic position straddling the Devonian-Carboniferous boundary, falling within the earliest stages of the Carboniferous Period, specifically the lower Mississippian subgroup. This interval encompasses the uppermost Famennian of the Devonian and the lowermost Tournaisian of the Carboniferous, characterized by a transition from marine-dominated Devonian deposits to the initial terrestrial-influenced sediments of the Carboniferous. The boundary itself is defined conodont biostratigraphically at the first appearance of Siphonodella sulcata or equivalent markers, marking a pivotal shift in global stratigraphy amid significant biotic turnover.
In European sections, particularly in the Rhenish Massif and Ardennes regions, Romer's gap is closely associated with the Hangenberg black shales, a distinctive lithological unit of organic-rich, anoxic marine deposits that delineate the lower part of the Hangenberg Crisis. These shales overlie the Etroeungt Formation and are succeeded by the Stockum and Hangenberg limestones, forming a characteristic tripartite sequence across the boundary. The gap aligns with Romer's proposed "Strunian" substage, an informal uppermost Famennian chronostratigraphic unit defined by miospore and conodont zonations such as the Vallatisporites vallatus-Rhygasporites lignosus (LL) zone, representing the final marine pulse before the Carboniferous transgression.
Globally, the stratigraphic interval of Romer's gap correlates with the Kinderhookian Series in North American chronostratigraphy, encompassing the lower Tournaisian equivalents in formations such as the Hannibal Shale and Chouteau Limestone in the Midcontinent region. These units reflect similar eustatic sea-level changes and conodont biozonations, linking the gap to a worldwide pattern of post-extinction recovery in shallow marine to marginal environments. In eastern Asia, comparable successions occur in South China, where boundary sections in the Wufeng-Longmaxi transition areas show analogous black shale developments, though specific tetrapod-bearing equivalents remain sparse.
The Kellwasser and Hangenberg anoxic events played a critical role in shaping the pre-gap faunal assemblages, contributing to the ecological backdrop for the gap's inception. The Upper Kellwasser event, occurring in the middle Frasnian, initiated widespread oceanic anoxia that decimated shallow-water marine communities, including early stem-tetrapod relatives among sarcopterygians. This was compounded by the Hangenberg event at the boundary, which involved intensified anoxia during a eustatic transgression, leading to near-total extinction of conodonts, ammonoids, and placoderm fisheskey components of Devonian aquatic ecosystemsand severely curtailing the diversity of pre-gap tetrapodomorphs, thereby setting the stage for the observed fossil scarcity.

Explanations for the Apparent Gap

Preservation and Sampling Biases

The perceived scarcity of tetrapod fossils during Romer's gap is primarily attributed to taphonomic biases arising from a major shift in depositional environments. Late Devonian tetrapod-bearing sites are predominantly marine or marginal-marine, where rapid burial in fine-grained sediments favored the preservation of small-bodied forms, whereas Early Carboniferous (Tournaisian) equivalents transitioned to terrestrial and fluvial settings with coarser clastics and soils that offered poorer preservational conditions for delicate skeletal material.
Sampling biases have further exacerbated this apparent hiatus, as historical paleontological efforts in the late 19th and early 20th centuries prioritized marine invertebrate assemblages over continental vertebrate deposits, resulting in minimal systematic excavation of Tournaisian non-marine strata until the late 20th century. Increased targeted collecting in recent decades, such as at sites in Scotland and Nova Scotia, has yielded diverse tetrapod remains, underscoring how undersampling contributed to the gap's perception.
Lithological factors in gap-equivalent strata compound these issues, including low sedimentation rates that exposed remains to prolonged subaerial weathering and acidic groundwater or soils that promoted bone dissolution through diagenetic processes. For instance, in floodplain and wetland deposits, slow accumulation allowed organic decay to generate acidic conditions, selectively destroying small tetrapod bones while preserving more robust fish or plant material.
Quantitative assessments of the fossil record highlight the scale of these biases; analyses of collection data indicate that Tournaisian tetrapod-bearing formations have been explored at rates far lower than Upper Devonian ones. Low atmospheric oxygen levels may have complemented these physical biases by influencing taphonomic windows, though this remains secondary to preservational artifacts.

Environmental Hypotheses

One prominent environmental hypothesis for the scarcity of tetrapod fossils during Romer's gap posits that low atmospheric oxygen levels following the Hangenberg event severely constrained the terrestrial activities of early vertebrates, although later geochemical models have challenged this view, suggesting oxygen levels were likely higher (above 15%) during the Tournaisian. According to the earlier modeling, atmospheric O concentrations fell to around 1215% (below the ~20% threshold for efficient air-breathing) between approximately 360 and 345 million years ago, insufficient for efficient air-breathing in primitive lungs adapted for amphibious lifestyles. This hypoxia would have limited foraging on land, reduced metabolic capabilities, and increased vulnerability to predation or desiccation, thereby hindering diversification and fossil preservation of early tetrapods.
Climate shifts in the Early Carboniferous also feature in explanations, with evidence indicating a transition to cooler and more arid conditions that diminished suitable habitats for amphibious tetrapods reliant on warm, humid environments. The aftermath of the end-Devonian mass extinction is associated with global cooling and the onset of icehouse conditions, including glaciation in southern Gondwana, which led to fluctuating sea levels and reduced continental humidity. These changes likely contracted wetland and floodplain ecosystems, forcing early tetrapods into more restricted, marginal aquatic niches where survival was challenging and terrestrial exploration was curtailed.
Sedimentological evidence supports these environmental constraints, as widespread coal swamp development in the mid-Carboniferous indicates a later rise in oxygenation and humidity that postdated Romer's gap. The scarcity of terrestrial carbonaceous deposits during the Tournaisian contrasts with the proliferation of peat-forming wetlands by the Visean, reflecting improved atmospheric conditions that enabled vascular plant expansion and fire regimes indicative of O levels exceeding 16%. This shift underscores how initial low-oxygen, arid phases gave way to more favorable environments, facilitating the eventual tetrapod burst.
Recent studies, including updated oxygen reconstructions and increased fossil sampling, suggest that preservational and sampling artifacts play a larger role than previously thought environmental bottlenecks, with the low-oxygen model now considered less likely.

Fossil Record

Pre-Gap Tetrapods

The Late Devonian tetrapod assemblage, dating to the Famennian stage approximately 372 to 359 million years ago, represents the earliest known diversification of limbed vertebrates just prior to Romer's gap. This group consists of stem-tetrapods and closely related elpistostegalian fish, primarily known from fragmentary but informative fossils recovered from Euramerica. These early forms were predominantly aquatic, adapted to shallow freshwater or marginal marine environments, with limbs that facilitated paddling rather than terrestrial locomotion.
Diversity during this interval is estimated at 10 to 17 genera, though most are represented by isolated bones or partial skeletons, limiting comprehensive phylogenetic resolution. Key elpistostegid taxa include Tiktaalik roseae from Ellesmere Island, Canada, which exhibits robust pectoral fins with skeletal elements homologous to tetrapod limb bones, bridging the fin-to-limb transition. More derived stem-tetrapods such as Acanthostega gunnari and Ichthyostega stensioei, both from the Upper Devonian of East Greenland, display fully formed limbs with polydactylyAcanthostega possessing up to eight digits on its forelimbsalong with a mix of fish-like and tetrapod-like features. Other notable genera include Elpistostege watsoni from Canada and Ventastega paradoxides from Latvia, further illustrating the global distribution of these early lineages across Laurasian continents.
Evolutionary innovations in these Famennian fossils highlight adaptations for life in shallow-water habitats, including the elaboration of endoskeletal elements in paired appendages that prefigure tetrapod limbs. For instance, Tiktaalik's fin skeleton includes a clavicle, scapula, and radials that enhance weight-bearing capacity, suggesting improved maneuverability in vegetated shallows. Polydactyly and retention of internal gills, as evidenced by ossified gill bars in Acanthostega, indicate these animals were obligate aquatics reliant on gill respiration supplemented by lungs, rather than fully terrestrial forms. Such traits underscore a gradual fin-to-limb evolution driven by selective pressures in coastal or riverine ecosystems, with no evidence of pentadactyly (five digits) until later periods.
The pre-gap tetrapod record terminates abruptly with the Hangenberg extinction event at the Devonian-Carboniferous boundary around 359 million years ago, which caused long-term losses exceeding 50% of vertebrate diversity, including the extinction of most Devonian tetrapod and prototetrapod lineages. This crisis, linked to anoxia, sea-level fluctuations, and biotic turnover, severely pruned the early tetrapod clade, setting the stage for a radiation of more derived forms in the Early Carboniferous.

Gap-Period Fauna and Discoveries

The limited tetrapod fossils from the Tournaisian stage of Romer's gap, spanning approximately 358.9 to 346.7 million years ago, were initially highlighted by classic discoveries in Scotland that provided the first glimpses into post-Devonian vertebrate diversification. One such find is Pederpes finneyae, a nearly complete skeleton from the Ballagan Formation near Dumbarton, dated to around 348 million years ago, featuring clear digit impressions on the pes indicative of early terrestrial adaptations in a functionally pentadactyl limb structure. Another key specimen is Crassigyrinus scoticus, an amphibian-like aquatic predator from the same formation, known from partial skeletal material including a robust skull and limbs suited for swimming rather than weight-bearing on land, suggesting persistence of fish-like traits in early Carboniferous tetrapods.
The TW:eed Project (Tetrapod World: early evolution and diversification), conducted from 2012 to 2016, significantly expanded the known gap-period fauna through targeted fieldwork in southeastern Scotland's Ballagan Formation, yielding five new tetrapod genera that demonstrate a mix of aquatic and semi-terrestrial morphologies. These include Perittodus apsconditus, characterized by a slender body and elongated limbs; Koilops herma, with a specialized skull for feeding; Ossirarus kierani, featuring robust girdles; Diploradus austiumensis, showing duplicated vertebral elements; and Aytonerpeton microps, a small form with reduced eyes adapted to dimly lit environments. Among these, some specimens exhibit anthracosaur-like features, such as embolomere-grade vertebral centra, pointing to early diversification within basal tetrapod clades during this interval.
Recent 2025 analyses have further refined the timeline and contents of Romer's gap. Radiometric dating of the East Kirkton Quarry in Scotland has redated Westlothiana lizziae, previously thought to be upper Visan (~330 Ma), to 341 3 million years ago, placing it in the lower Visan just after the main gap interval (360345 Ma) and confirming its status as an amniote-like reptile with a lightweight skeleton and scaly skin, contributing to understanding the transition to fully terrestrial vertebrates shortly post-gap. In the same Ballagan Formation, new rhizodontid materiala large predatory sarcopterygian fish close to the tetrapod stemhas been described, including jaw fragments and scales that highlight the morphological continuum between finned fishes and limbed forms during the Tournaisian. Additionally, fossil footprints from the Snowy Plains Formation in southeastern Australia, dated to approximately 359354 million years ago, represent the earliest known tracks attributable to crown-group amniotes, featuring clawed, pentadactyl impressions that indicate fully terrestrial locomotion far earlier than previously recognized.
These cumulative discoveries underscore that Romer's gap is largely an artifact of preservation biases rather than a true diversity crash, with over ten tetrapod and stem-tetrapod taxa now documented from the interval compared to none recognized in 1956 when Alfred Romer first described the paucity. The emerging fauna suggests continuity in tetrapod evolution, with no evidence of a major extinction but rather incremental adaptations to terrestrial habitats amid fluctuating Carboniferous environments.

Major Fossil Localities

Scottish Sites

The Scottish sites, primarily in the Midland Valley and Borders regions, represent key Tournaisian exposures that have yielded crucial evidence addressing Romer's gap through targeted excavations and recent geochronological studies. These localities, embedded in continental deposits of the Lower Carboniferous, have revealed a mix of aquatic and semi-terrestrial vertebrates, challenging earlier notions of a complete fossil paucity during this interval.
East Kirkton Quarry, a disused limestone site near Bathgate in West Lothian, has produced significant assemblages of lobe-finned fish and early tetrapods, including the stem tetrapod Westlothiana lizziae, a lizard-like form initially interpreted as the earliest amniote but later reclassified based on its mosaic features. Excavations here, initiated in the 1980s under NERC funding led by researchers like Jennifer Clack, recovered diverse gap-period specimens from oil shale and limestone layers, highlighting a wetland ecosystem. A 2025 U-Pb zircon dating study refined the maximum depositional age to 346 million years ago, positioning these fossils in the early Tournaisian and the core of Romer's gap, thereby bridging temporal uncertainties in tetrapod evolution.
The Ballagan Formation, encompassing red beds and cyclic mudstones across southern Scotland, has preserved additional gap-era vertebrates through its floodplain and lacustrine deposits. Recent 2025 discoveries include well-preserved rhizodontid materiallarge predatory sarcopterygians with at least two distinct morphogroupsindicating a complex aquatic food web during the Tournaisian. The NERC-funded TWeed project has employed drill cores from this formation to conduct stable isotope analyses (C and O) on calcite nodules and fossils, demonstrating fluctuating environmental conditions in seasonal coastal wetlands that supported early tetrapod diversification.
Other notable sites include Burnmouth on the Berwickshire coast and exposures along Whiteadder Water in the Scottish Borders, where Ballagan Formation outcrops have yielded isolated tetrapod bones, fish scales, and arthropod remains from narrow stratigraphic intervals. These Borders localities parallel the coastal wetland facies of Blue Beach in Nova Scotia, offering comparable insights into Tournaisian ecosystems.
Ongoing research, building on NERC consortium grants since the 1980s, has amassed over 50 verifiable gap-period specimens from these Scottish sites, transforming our understanding of post-Devonian vertebrate transitions through systematic fieldwork and interdisciplinary analysis. The fauna recovered, including stem amphibians and basal amniotes, underscores a gradual rather than abrupt shift to terrestriality during this enigmatic interval.

North American and Other Sites

One of the key North American localities contributing to the understanding of Romer's gap is Blue Beach in Nova Scotia, Canada, situated along the Avon River near Hantsport. This site, part of the Blue Beach Member of the Horton Bluff Formation, dates to the early Tournaisian stage of the Early Carboniferous, approximately 350 million years ago, based on palynomorph biostratigraphy from late Tn2 to early Tn3 stages. The formation consists of alternating thin to moderately bedded grey silty sandstones interbedded with shales, representing high-energy near-shore to intertidal environments such as tidal flats, which favored the preservation of disarticulated bones from larger vertebrates while smaller forms are rarer. Fossils from here include skeletal elements of whatcheeriid tetrapods resembling Pederpes finneyi, embolomere-like humeri, and possible holdovers from Devonian forms such as Tulerpeton-like ulnae, alongside tetrapod trackways indicating small, potentially clawed individuals and fish tracks from rhizodonts and other sarcopterygians. These discoveries reveal a diverse tetrapod fauna at the base of Romer's gap, suggesting evolutionary continuity across the Devonian-Carboniferous boundary rather than a complete hiatus.
Beyond Nova Scotia, preservation challenges in the North American Appalachian region have limited exposures of potential gap-period strata. The Catskill fluvial system, which deposited much of the Early Carboniferous sediments in the northern Appalachians, experienced rapid uplift and erosion during the Late Devonian to Early Carboniferous, resulting in the removal of significant overburden and the scarcity of suitable outcrops for fossil-bearing horizons. Paleosol studies in these areas, using depth to calcic horizons as a proxy for paleoprecipitation, indicate variable environmental conditions but highlight how tectonic activity and subsequent erosion have obscured or destroyed many depositional sites, contributing to the apparent sparsity of tetrapod remains.
In Gondwana, recent Australian discoveries provide evidence of early terrestrialization during the gap interval. A slab of sandstone from the Home Station Sandstone Member of the Mansfield Group (Snowy Plains Formation) in Victoria, Australia, preserves 17 clawed, pentadactyl footprints dated to the early Tournaisian, approximately 355 million years ago. These tracks, attributed to a primitive sauropsid amniote, measure 3-4 cm in length and show digit impressions consistent with early reptile-like locomotion, pushing the origin of crown-group amniotes back by 35-40 million years and indicating their presence by the Devonian-Carboniferous boundary in southern continents. This find underscores Gondwanan contributions to filling Romer's gap, contrasting with the more aquatic-focused Laurasian records and suggesting parallel diversification of tetrapod lineages.
Correlations with Chinese sites reveal sparser records from the gap period. Early Carboniferous formations in northern China have yielded isolated sarcopterygian fish remains but no tetrapod fossils have been reported to date, highlighting regional biases in preservation and sampling. These fish-dominated assemblages point to aquatic environments but underscore the absence of terrestrial tetrapod evidence in East Asian strata during this time.
Potential for further discoveries exists in the Antarctic Beacon Supergroup, which spans Devonian to Triassic ages (approximately 400-250 million years ago) and includes Early Carboniferous intervals in sequences like the Taylor Group. Although vertebrate fossils from the gap period are currently unreported, the supergroup's non-marine sandstones and paleosols in the central Transantarctic Mountains represent promising, under-explored depositional settings analogous to those yielding tetrapods elsewhere, with ongoing surveys targeting these horizons for gap-filling material.
