 Land O'Lakes, Inc. is a farmer- and agricultural retailer-owned cooperative founded in 1921 as the Minnesota Cooperative Creameries Association to enable dairy farmers to market butter collectively. Headquartered in Arden Hills, Minnesota, it adopted the Land O'Lakes brand name in 1924 following a contest to create a distinctive identity for its sweet cream butter, which remains its flagship product.
The cooperative has expanded beyond dairy into animal nutrition feeds, crop inputs, and sustainable agriculture initiatives, operating across all 50 U.S. states and over 60 countries with more than 9,000 employees. In 2023, Land O'Lakes achieved net sales of $16.8 billion, reflecting its scale as one of America's largest cooperatives dedicated to enhancing farmer profitability through supply chain efficiencies and innovation. Key milestones include the 1951 invention of calf milk replacer, which transformed livestock feeding, and mergers such as the 1970 integration with Farmers Regional Cooperative to broaden agribusiness services. While renowned for high-quality dairy products like butter and cheese used in consumer and foodservice markets, the company emphasizes cooperative principles to support member-owners in producing crops, livestock, and dairy amid evolving agricultural challenges.

History

Founding and early development (19211950s)

The Minnesota Cooperative Creameries Association was established on June 7, 1921, when 350 dairy farmers from throughout Minnesota assembled in St. Paul and unanimously voted to create a statewide cooperative dedicated to centralized marketing of butter and other dairy products, addressing fragmented local creamery operations and inconsistent quality. Incorporated on July 8, 1921, the association started with $1,375 in capital, including a $1,000 loan from the American Farm Bureau Federation. John Brandt, a farmer from Clitherall, Minnesota, joined the initial board of directors and assumed the presidency in 1923, steering the organization toward standardized production of high-quality "sweet cream" butter to compete nationally.
In February 1924, a public naming contest with over 100,000 entries selected "Land O'Lakes" for the cooperative's butter brand, awarding $500 each to winners Ida Foss and George Swift; the association soon adopted the name and debuted its distinctive packaging featuring an Indian maiden logo. The Capper-Volstead Act of 1922 bolstered these efforts by exempting agricultural cooperatives from antitrust laws, enabling collective bargaining power. By April 1924, Land O'Lakes secured a U.S. Navy contract for 430,000 pounds of butter, validating its quality standards. In 1926, the entity rebranded as Land O'Lakes Creameries, Inc., reflecting its growing focus on branded dairy exports.
The Great Depression prompted diversification and market interventions; in 1928, the cooperative launched egg and poultry divisions, followed by an Agricultural Services Division in 1929 to provide farmers with supplies and advice. To counter plummeting prices, it purchased 7 million pounds of surplus butter in 1930, stabilizing the market within four months. By 1934, Land O'Lakes oversaw 95 cheese factories via member cooperatives. In 1937, it opened its first milk-drying facility in Luck, Wisconsin, manufacturing skim milk powder, casein, and buttermilk powder, eventually expanding to 22 plants by World War II.
Wartime demand accelerated growth, with dry milk output surging from 22 million pounds in 1941 to 119 million pounds shipped in 1945, positioning Land O'Lakes as the global leader in dried milk production. Postwar, in 1946, it ventured into ice cream mixes and fluid milk processing while pioneering calf milk replacers to optimize dairy herd nutrition. The 1951 invention of commercial calf milk replacer further reduced reliance on whole skim milk for feeding, laying groundwork for expanded feed operations. Brandt's death in 1952 ended his nearly three-decade tenure, during which the cooperative evolved from a butter-marketing alliance into a multifaceted dairy processor serving thousands of Midwest farmers.

Expansion into diversified operations (1960s1990s)

During the 1960s, Land O'Lakes primarily consolidated its dairy operations through acquisitions such as Terrace Park Dairies in Sioux Falls, South Dakota, and H. C. Christians Company in Chicago, alongside a merger with Dairy Maid Products Cooperative in Eau Claire, Wisconsin, which doubled assets to nearly $100 million and boosted sales to $400 million by decade's end. These moves enhanced processing capacity but remained focused on dairy products like cheese following the 1960 acquisition of Dairy Belt Cheese and Butter Company.
Diversification accelerated in 1970 with the merger of Farmers Regional Cooperative (Felco), integrating production of agricultural inputs including fertilizers and insecticides to support member farmers beyond dairy outputs. This expansion prompted a corporate rename to Land O'Lakes, Inc., reflecting a shift toward multifaceted agribusiness services. In 1978, the company ventured into red meat processing by acquiring Spencer Beef, though this division was divested in the late 1980s amid operational challenges.
Further broadening occurred in 1980 through soybean processing at Dawson Mills, marking initial steps into crop inputs and oilseed operations to stabilize revenue amid dairy market volatility. Overexpansion contributed to a $19 million loss in 1982, prompting strategic refinements. A cornerstone diversification came in 1987 via the Cenex/Land O'Lakes Ag Services joint venture with Cenex Cooperative, which marketed animal feed, seeds, farm chemicals, and petroleum products, enhancing supply chain integration for cooperative members.
Into the 1990s, these efforts supported domestic and nascent international growth, including feed mill establishments in Taiwan by 1996, though core diversification emphasized risk mitigation through non-dairy segments representing fertilizers, feeds, and crop protection to counter cyclical dairy demands. This period transformed Land O'Lakes from a butter-centric cooperative into a comprehensive agricultural entity, with diversified operations comprising a significant portion of its portfolio by the decade's close.

Modern restructuring and acquisitions (2000spresent)

In the early 2000s, Land O'Lakes underwent significant restructuring to streamline operations and focus on core competencies in dairy processing, animal nutrition, and agricultural inputs, divesting non-strategic assets amid competitive pressures in the dairy sector. In June 2000, the cooperative sold its fluid dairy operations to Dean Foods, transferring facilities with approximately $310 million in annual sales to enhance Dean's market position while allowing Land O'Lakes to concentrate on branded dairy products like butter and cheese. Later that year, it recorded $9.7 million in restructuring charges for facility consolidations and workforce reductions, particularly at its newly formed Land O'Lakes Farmland Feed joint venture with Farmland Industries, which combined feed operations to achieve $1.6 billion in initial annual sales and expanded production capacity. These moves reflected a broader strategy to exit commoditized segments vulnerable to price volatility, prioritizing higher-margin value-added processing and supply chain integration for farmer-members.
Subsequent divestitures continued this refocus, including the 2001 contribution of its aseptic dairy business to the Advanced Food Ingredients joint venture and asset sales totaling $22.4 million in 2002 to shed underperforming units. By 2014, Land O'Lakes accelerated exits from animal protein ventures, adopting a plan to divest substantially all assets of its Moark egg production operations in the West and Midwest regions, culminating in the sale of its Missouri egg division to Opal Foods for an undisclosed sum as part of winding down a business hampered by disease outbreaks and regulatory costs. This period also saw strategic partnerships, such as the 2001 merger of its seed business with Agriliance's crop protection division to form WinField Solutions, LLC, enhancing agronomic services, and a 2001 brand licensing deal with Nestl Purina Petcare for animal nutrition products.
Acquisitions in the 2010s bolstered growth in specialty dairy and precision agriculture. In 2012, Land O'Lakes acquired Kozy Shack Enterprises, integrating pudding and refrigerated desserts into its dairy portfolio to diversify consumer packaged goods. The company expanded its crop inputs segment through the 2016 merger with United Suppliers, Inc., creating WinField United to provide integrated solutions in seeds, crop protection, and data analytics for farmers. That year, it also purchased Ceres International for $17 million, its largest deal to date, to strengthen global grain marketing capabilities. In 2017, the acquisition of Vermont Creamery added premium goat cheese and cultured dairy production, leveraging the target's award-winning products to target high-end markets. Further, the 2018 purchase of Agren enhanced agribusiness consulting services, while ongoing investments in digital tools and supply chain efficiencies supported member-owned resilience amid fluctuating commodity prices. These actions have positioned Land O'Lakes as a farmer-centric agribusiness giant, with revenues exceeding $14 billion by the mid-2020s through targeted expansion rather than broad diversification.

Ownership and Governance

Cooperative model and member structure

Land O'Lakes operates as a member-owned agricultural cooperative, established in 1921 by dairy farmers to collectively market butter and enhance profitability through shared resources and bargaining power. In this model, membersprimarily farmers and agricultural retailerssupply raw commodities such as milk, crops, or feed inputs to the cooperative, which processes and markets them, returning profits as patronage dividends proportional to each member's business volume or patronage income. This structure incentivizes alignment between member contributions and cooperative success, differing from shareholder-owned corporations by prioritizing long-term rural economic stability over short-term stock gains.
Membership encompasses individual farmers and other cooperatives, totaling over 2,500 entities nationwide. Dairy producers, numbering more than 1,200, form a core group, supplying milk for processing into products like butter and cheese while adhering to cooperative standards for quality and sustainability. Agricultural producers, exceeding 500, contribute crops such as corn and soybeans, integrating into supply chains for feed and inputs. Additionally, over 800 retail owners, often agribusinesses, participate by distributing cooperative products like animal feed and seeds, extending the network to end-users and fostering localized support.
Governance reinforces the cooperative model through democratic representation. The board of directors, comprising 20 members, is elected by voting members at the annual meeting for four-year terms, with nominations allocated by region and scaled to business volume, reviewed quinquennially. Dairy members nominate nine directors, while agricultural members nominate eleven, ensuring balanced input from both sectors; up to three non-voting advisory directors may also serve. This elected board sets policies, oversees finances, and appoints the CEO, directly channeling member priorities into operations.

Leadership and key executives

Beth Ford has served as President and Chief Executive Officer of Land O'Lakes, Inc. since August 1, 2018, succeeding Chris Galen in the role. Under her leadership, the cooperative has emphasized operational efficiency, supply chain resilience, and expansion in agricultural services amid challenges like labor shortages and market volatility. Ford, previously president of the company's dairy foods division, brings experience from roles at Archer Daniels Midland and Pillsbury, focusing on farmer-centric strategies in a member-owned structure.
Key executives reporting to Ford include Brett Bruggeman, Chief Operating Officer for Land O'Lakes and Executive Vice President of Ag Businesses, overseeing crop inputs and animal nutrition segments; Bill Pieper, Senior Vice President and Chief Financial Officer, managing financial strategy and reporting; Heather Malenshek, Senior Vice President and Chief Marketing Officer, directing brand and consumer engagement; and Julie Sexton, Senior Vice President and Chief Human Resources Officer, handling talent and organizational development. Other notable leaders encompass Yone Dewberry as Senior Vice President and Chief Supply Chain Officer, responsible for logistics and procurement, and Sheilah Stewart in strategic operations roles.
As a farmer-owned cooperative, Land O'Lakes' governance features a board of 20 voting directors, with 9 nominated by dairy members and 11 by agricultural members from designated regions based on patronage volume; directors serve four-year terms elected at the annual meeting. The board establishes policies, objectives, and financial controls while appointing the CEO to manage daily operations; up to three non-voting advisory directors may also serve. This structure ensures alignment with member interests, with periodic reviews of regional boundaries every five years to reflect business activity.

Business Operations

Dairy production and processing

Land O'Lakes Dairy Foods division processes raw milk sourced from its member farmers into butter, cheese, spreads, specialty dairy powders, and bulk dairy ingredients. These facilities receive milk from dairy herds primarily located in the Northeast, Upper Midwest, and California, ensuring a steady supply for manufacturing operations.
Processing at Land O'Lakes plants involves initial steps such as pasteurization and quality testing upon milk arrival, followed by separation into cream and skim milk for specialized production lines. Butter manufacturing includes churning separated cream, while cheese production entails coagulation, cutting, cooking, and pressing of curds, often followed by aging in controlled environments. Facilities like the one in Melrose, Minnesota, feature evaporators and dryers for converting liquid milk into non-fat dry milk and other powders, alongside refrigerated storage for handling large volumes.
The division operates multiple dairy processing plants across the United States, contributing significantly to the cooperative's output with dairy product sales exceeding $5 billion in 2024. This segment focuses on both consumer-packaged goods and industrial ingredients, leveraging the cooperative model to aggregate milk volumes and distribute value back to producer-members after processing costs.

Animal nutrition and feed

Land O'Lakes operates its animal nutrition and feed business primarily through its wholly owned subsidiary, Purina Animal Nutrition, which focuses on developing research-backed feeds and supplements tailored to specific nutritional needs for optimal animal health, growth, and productivity. The division serves a range of species, including cattle, swine, sheep, horses, backyard poultry, and rabbits, with specialized products such as Fortiva feeds, Land O'Lakes Young Animal Milk Solutions for neonates, and Nutrablend supplements. It also provides nutrition for exotic animals and zoo species through the Mazuri brand, extending to customers like zoos and exotic pet owners.
The division traces its roots to Land O'Lakes' acquisition of Purina Mills in 2001 for $360 million plus $130 million in assumed debt, integrating the established Purina name and expertise into the cooperative's operations. Purina's legacy dates to 1894, emphasizing innovation in animal feeding, including probiotics, prebiotics, vitamins, and minerals for immunity support in products like Flock Strong poultry feeds and the Microbiome Quotient platform for equine gut health. Research at facilities such as the Purina Animal Nutrition Center drives product development, with recent expansions enhancing capabilities for nutritional studies across species.
Financially, the Animal Nutrition segment reported a pretax loss of $3 million in 2023, attributed to higher feed prices and reduced sales volumes, but rebounded to $34 million in pretax earnings in 2024 amid favorable market conditions and operational improvements. These efforts align with broader commitments to research-proven solutions supported by Ph.D. nutritionists, positioning the division as a key component of Land O'Lakes' diversified agribusiness portfolio.

Crop inputs, seeds, and agricultural services

Land O'Lakes' Crop Inputs segment, primarily managed through its WinField United brand, supplies farmers with a range of products including seeds, crop protection chemicals, fertilizers, and plant nutrition solutions. This division focuses on delivering field-tested inputs backed by research to optimize crop yields and farm profitability. Key seed offerings include the CROPLAN line, developed for specific regional agronomic needs.
Crop protection products encompass herbicides, insecticides, fungicides, seed treatments, and adjuvants such as drift control agents and surfactants, designed to manage weeds, pests, and diseases while enhancing application efficiency. Fertilizer and nutrient options, including macronutrient formulations, are provided through WinField United Crop Nutrients, with emphasis on market intelligence, supply chain management, and customized nutrient plans to address soil deficiencies.
Agronomic services include on-site consultations, field monitoring, yield forecasting, and data analytics to support precision farming decisions from planting to harvest. The Answer Plot program stands out, generating insights from over 5 million data points across nearly 200 demonstration sites annually, enabling comparisons of WinField United products against competitors for evidence-based recommendations. Complementary financing via SECURE loans offers fixed-rate options for purchasing seeds, crop protection, fertilizers, and related inputs, aiding cash flow during volatile markets.
WinField United originated from the 2015 merger of Land O'Lakes' crop inputs business with United Suppliers, Inc., approved in August 2015 and completed later that year, combining seed, protection, and nutrient operations to expand retailer networks and service reach. The division supports ag retailers and direct farmer-customers, leveraging innovation centers for product testing and sustainable practices integration. In 2024, Crop Inputs generated $166 million in pretax earnings, reflecting market challenges like input price fluctuations despite ongoing service expansions.

Products and Brands

Core dairy offerings

Land O'Lakes' core dairy offerings center on butter and cheese, which form the foundation of its consumer and foodservice portfolios, derived from milk supplied by over 1,200 member dairy farms. These products include salted and unsalted butter sticks, whipped butter, butter spreads, and specialty butters, positioning the brand as the leading seller of branded butter in the United States. Cheese offerings encompass deli slices, shredded varieties, chunks, snack portions, and process cheeses like Extra Melt for commercial use, with recent expansions including 12 new SKUs in shredded, chunk, and snack formats launched in partnership with regional retailers.
Supporting these staples are complementary items such as margarine, half-and-half, whipping creams, and eggs for retail consumers, alongside bulk dairy powders and ingredients for industrial applications in snacks, baked goods, and sauces. The company's dairy foods segment emphasizes high-quality, farm-sourced milk processing into versatile formats for cooking, baking, and snacking, with butter marketed under the "Butter is Everything" campaign highlighting its role in everyday applications. Production occurs at facilities like creameries in Minnesota and Wisconsin, ensuring consistency in products that generate significant revenue from both branded retail and private-label channels.

Feed and crop protection products

Land O'Lakes' Animal Nutrition division produces specialized feed products designed for livestock including cattle, swine, sheep, and poultry, focusing on nutritional formulations that promote health, growth, and productivity. Through its subsidiary Purina Animal Nutrition, the company offers a broad portfolio of feeds, supplements, and milk replacers, such as the LAND O LAKES Pasteurized Milk Balancer, which standardizes nutrient levels in waste milk for calf rearing.
In crop protection, Land O'Lakes operates via WinField United, providing farmers with herbicides, adjuvants, fungicides, and insecticides to control weeds, pests, and diseases while optimizing yields. The division's offerings include over 200 products under the AgriSolutions line, acquired from Agriliance in 2007, emphasizing adjuvant technologies that enhance pesticide efficacy.
A 2015 merger with United Suppliers integrated seed and crop protection operations into WinField US, expanding access to customized input solutions backed by agronomic data and field trials. In April 2024, Land O'Lakes received a U.S. patent for multifunctional agricultural adjuvant compositions (MAACs), which incorporate liquid carriers to improve crop protection application and performance.

Licensing and branded merchandise

Land O'Lakes has licensed its brand name to third-party manufacturers for dairy products it does not produce directly, enabling broader market reach through established distribution networks. In July 2002, Dean Foods Company obtained a national licensing agreement to sell fluid milk and cultured dairy items, including yogurt, sour cream, and cottage cheese, under the Land O'Lakes label. This deal expanded the brand's presence in refrigerated dairy aisles beyond Land O'Lakes' core butter and cheese focus. In March 2003, the companies formalized a joint venture for certain dairy operations, incorporating long-term provisions for continued brand licensing to support joint production and sales.
Licensing activities have occasionally led to legal challenges. Following Land O'Lakes' 2019 divestiture of fluid dairy assets to Danone North America, Danone filed suit in November 2020, claiming Land O'Lakes violated the acquisition terms by subsequently licensing the trademarks for ultra-pasteurized cream products to Dairy Farmers of America subsidiaries and Prairie Farms Dairy. Danone sought an injunction to halt the licensing and compel product recalls, arguing the agreements undermined exclusive rights transferred in the sale.
Branded merchandise beyond food products appears limited, with no major ongoing licensing programs for apparel, accessories, or non-dairy consumer goods documented in corporate disclosures or recent business reports. Historical advertising, such as a 1970 promotion pairing butter with clothing items, hints at exploratory merchandising, but contemporary efforts prioritize core dairy extensions over diversified retail goods. Land O'Lakes maintains strict trademark oversight, as evidenced by a 2016 federal appeals court ruling affirming its rights against unauthorized uses of "Land O Lakes" in non-dairy contexts.

Financial Performance

Revenue trends and key metrics

Land O'Lakes, as a farmer-owned cooperative, reports net sales reflecting volatility in agricultural commodity markets, particularly dairy, animal feed, and crop inputs. Net sales grew modestly from $13.8 billion in 2019 to $13.9 billion in 2020 amid pandemic-related disruptions, then accelerated to $16 billion in 2021 driven by elevated demand and pricing for dairy products. Sales peaked at $19 billion in 2022 amid high global feed and fertilizer prices, before contracting 12% to $16.8 billion in 2023 as commodity prices normalized. Preliminary 2024 figures indicate further decline to $16.2 billion, a 3.5% drop year-over-year.
Net earnings, a key metric for member returns, mirrored revenue trends with peaks during high-commodity periods. Earnings reached $295 million in 2021, supported by strong dairy and feed segments, but fell to $258 million in 2023 and $238 million in 2024 amid margin pressures from input costs and market softening. The cooperative returned $175 million to members in 2023 through patronage dividends, prioritizing owner equity over retained profits.
Other operational metrics include approximately 9,000 employees and 2,580 member-owners as of 2024, with total assets at $9.5 billion and stockholder equity at $3.1 billion. EBITDA for the first nine months of 2024 rose 50% year-over-year to $97 million, signaling resilience in core segments like lifestyle feeds despite overall revenue contraction.

Profitability and member distributions

Land O'Lakes has demonstrated consistent profitability as a diversified agricultural cooperative, with net earnings of $258 million on net sales of $16.8 billion in fiscal year 2023, reflecting strength in branded dairy products and foodservice operations despite broader market volatility. In 2024, net earnings declined to $238 million on revenues of approximately $16.2 billion, influenced by downturns in crop inputs and animal nutrition segments, though the dairy foods division achieved $64 million in pre-tax earnings, a 33% increase from the prior year. These results underscore the cooperative's resilience amid commodity price fluctuations and supply chain pressures, supported by operational efficiencies and market share gains in core dairy offerings.
As a member-owned entity, Land O'Lakes operates on a cooperative model that prioritizes returning value to its approximately 1,700 member-owners, primarily dairy farmers and agricultural retailers, through patronage refunds calculated based on individual members' business volume and the cooperative's overall margins. The company targets distributing 60% of the prior year's net earnings in cash patronage refunds, with the balance retained as allocated equity to support long-term capital needs and revolving funds redeemed over time. In 2023, total cash returns to members, encompassing patronage dividends, revolving equity redemptions, and estate settlements, approximated $180 million. For 2024, similar cash distributions were projected at around $170 million, aligning with the targeted payout ratio and reflecting prudent equity retention for reinvestment in processing facilities and supply chain enhancements.
Patronage refunds payable, as reflected in quarterly financials, stood at $159.7 million as of the first quarter of 2024, indicating the scale of forthcoming member allocations. This structure incentivizes member loyalty by directly tying returns to cooperative performance, though actual payouts can vary with board decisions on equity revolutions and fiscal priorities, ensuring sustainability over short-term maximization.

Marketing and Branding

Historical advertising strategies

Land O'Lakes, originally formed as the Minnesota Cooperative Creameries Association in 1921 and rebranded in 1926, initiated its early advertising efforts with a public naming contest in 1924 for its sweet cream butter, offering a $500 gold prize that drew over 100,000 entries and selected "Land O'Lakes" to evoke the purity of Minnesota's lake region dairy lands. This contest strategy fostered consumer engagement and brand ownership while tying the product to regional identity and cooperative farmer origins.
In 1928, the cooperative introduced the "Mia" maiden illustration on butter packaging, created by artist Arthur C. Hanson of Brown & Bigelow and modeled after Muriel Barbaree, daughter of a Minnesota dairy farmer of European descent, to symbolize pastoral abundance and distinguish the premium sweet cream butter in competitive markets. The imagery, depicting a kneeling figure amid lakeside scenery holding a butter carton, served as a core visual strategy for nearly a century, reinforcing brand recognition through consistent packaging rather than frequent redesigns and emphasizing natural, high-quality sourcing from cooperative farms. This approach leveraged symbolic regionalism over direct farmer depictions, aligning with early 20th-century trends in food branding that prioritized evocative icons for shelf appeal.
By the 1930s, Land O'Lakes expanded into national print advertising campaigns promoting sweet cream butter's superiority, which correlated with record sales of 100 million pounds in 1930 amid the Great Depression, while opening sales branches to penetrate smaller retail outlets and stabilize cooperative markets through volume-driven promotion. Ads in magazines highlighted product purity, cooperative efficiencies, and versatility, such as 1931 promotions for sweet cream butter and 1930s features tying butter to seasonal items like turkey, broadening appeal beyond dairy staples. The maiden image was refined in 1939 and the 1950s by Ojibwe artist Patrick DesJarlait, incorporating beadwork and shoreline details to enhance cultural resonance without altering the core strategy of enduring visual consistency.
Into the mid-20th century, advertising incorporated cross-promotions, as seen in a 1970 Seventeen magazine ad linking Land O'Lakes butter to Native American-inspired fashion, aiming to appeal to younger demographics by associating the brand with lifestyle and cultural motifs while maintaining focus on indulgent, everyday use. Overall, historical strategies prioritized long-term brand equity through iconic, low-cost visuals like packaging and targeted print media, supported by cooperative-scale efficiencies, rather than high-volume TV or celebrity endorsements common in later eras.

Butter packaging redesign and debates

!
In February 2020, Land O'Lakes announced a redesign of its butter packaging to commemorate the company's 100th anniversary, emphasizing its farmer-owned cooperative structure by incorporating images of member farms and dairy operations rather than the longstanding Native American maiden illustration. The updated design featured serene landscapes of lakes and pine trees, aligning with the brand's name evoking Minnesota's natural features, and began appearing on products such as butter spreads and deli cheeses. This change effectively removed the maiden figure, known as "Mia," from primary packaging elements, though the company did not explicitly cite cultural sensitivity as the motivation in initial announcements.
The maiden imagery originated in 1928 as a simple profile of a Native American woman and was redesigned in 1939and refined in the 1950sby Ojibwe artist Patrick DesJarlait, who portrayed her kneeling in a field holding a butter carton against a backdrop of lakes and trees to symbolize the cooperative's regional roots. DesJarlait intended the figure as a dignified representation inspired by Ojibwe women in his family, avoiding common stereotypes of the era like feathered headdresses or peace pipes. Prior to the 2020 update, the image had faced intermittent criticism for purportedly objectifying Native women through its kneeling pose and idealized depiction, which some viewed as exoticizing indigenous imagery in a commercial context.
The redesign's removal of Mia gained widespread attention in April 2020, prompting polarized debates amid broader discussions on racial symbolism in branding. Detractors of the original logo, including some activists, welcomed the change as addressing outdated and potentially demeaning tropes, arguing it perpetuated a subservient portrayal of Native women. Conversely, defenders, including Robert DesJarlaitson of the artistcontended that erasing the image dishonored a Native-created artwork that positively evoked cultural pride and regional identity, without evidence of intent to demean. Critics of the redesign, often from conservative outlets, framed it as an unnecessary concession to political correctness, questioning the cooperative's alignment with cultural erasure trends despite the pre-planned anniversary refresh. Reactions among Native communities were divided, with some mourning the loss of a non-stereotypical emblem crafted by an indigenous artist, while others supported modernization. The company maintained that the update prioritized showcasing real farmers over historical icons, with no reversal indicated as of 2024 despite satirical claims suggesting otherwise.

Controversies

Animal welfare allegations at suppliers

In September 2009, People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals (PETA) released footage from a five-month undercover investigation at a Pennsylvania dairy farm supplying milk to Land O'Lakes, documenting instances of workers beating cows with metal rods, dragging injured animals by chains, and leaving downer cowsthose unable to standin filth without veterinary care or euthanasia. The farm's owners, Lloyd Reitz and his son Andy Reitz, faced 21 misdemeanor animal cruelty charges, but these were dismissed in March 2010 after a judge ruled the acts did not constitute cruelty under Pennsylvania's agriculture exemption laws, amid opposition from farming groups arguing the footage represented isolated incidents rather than standard practice.
Subsequent investigations highlighted ongoing concerns. In May 2017, Animal Outlook (formerly Mercy for Animals) exposed abuse at Mason Dixon Farms in Pennsylvania, a Land O'Lakes supplier, where hidden camera footage showed workers kicking, beating, and shocking cows with electric prods, leading to a worker's guilty plea for animal cruelty. That November, another probe at Zonneveld Dairy, a California Land O'Lakes supplier, revealed calves housed in extreme heat, covered in maggots, and denied food and water, with investigators attributing the conditions to neglect violating basic care standards.
In April 2021, Direct Action Everywhere released findings from undercover work across 14 Land O'Lakes supplier dairies in Fresno and Tulare counties, California, documenting over two dozen violations of the company's animal welfare policy and state laws, including routine mutilations without anesthesia, overcrowded conditions causing injuries, and failure to treat lame or sick cows promptly. Land O'Lakes maintains a Supplier Code of Conduct requiring welfare protocols such as prompt veterinary intervention and humane handling, but has not publicly detailed terminations or audits in response to these specific claims; activist sources assert such violations reflect inherent issues in large-scale dairy operations rather than aberrations.

Other operational and ethical critiques

In 2008, the U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) filed a lawsuit against Land O'Lakes, alleging that the company's severance agreement policy unlawfully required terminated employees to waive their right to file charges with the EEOC or other agencies regarding discrimination claims in exchange for benefits, thereby interfering with protected rights under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act. The case highlighted concerns over operational practices that could deter workers from pursuing legitimate grievances, though it was resolved without admission of liability.
Land O'Lakes has also encountered scrutiny over its management of employee retirement plans. A 2020 class-action lawsuit under the Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA) claimed that company fiduciaries breached duties by failing to monitor excessive recordkeeping fees paid to providers and by allowing conflicted fee arrangements between the recordkeeper and investment advice firm, resulting in overcharges to participants. The suit, filed in the U.S. District Court for the District of Minnesota, was settled in 2022 for $1.8 million to compensate affected employees, with no admission of wrongdoing by the cooperative.
Operationally, Land O'Lakes faced antitrust allegations in 1997 when the state of Minnesota, joined by other attorneys general, sued the company for participating in a bid-rigging and market allocation scheme involving milk supply contracts with over 140 school districts and institutions. Prosecutors contended that the practices suppressed competition and inflated costs, reflecting ethical concerns about collusive behavior in procurement processes; the case was resolved through a consent decree requiring compliance reforms.
Critics, including consumer advocacy groups, have raised ethical questions about Land O'Lakes' emphasis on diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) programs, such as mandatory unconscious bias training for leaders and goals for diverse hiring, arguing that these initiatives prioritize ideological commitments over operational efficiency and shareholder interests. Such critiques, drawn from analyses of the company's ESG reports, contend that resource allocation to these efforts diverts focus from core dairy production and farmer support, though the cooperative maintains that inclusive practices enhance workforce performance.

Sustainability Initiatives and Impact

Environmental and farming programs

Land O'Lakes, through its sustainability arm Truterra, operates programs aimed at enhancing soil health, reducing greenhouse gas emissions, and improving resource efficiency on member farms. Truterra's initiatives include carbon market enablement, where farmers adopt conservation practices such as cover cropping and reduced tillage to generate verifiable carbon credits, with the program distributing over $21 million in payments to participants in its first three years of operation ending around 2024. These efforts emphasize regenerative agriculture to maintain farm profitability while addressing environmental concerns like nutrient runoff and soil degradation.
In March 2021, Land O'Lakes announced the Dairy 2025 Commitments, targeting its more than 1,600 member-dairy farms to complete intensive on-farm sustainability assessments aligned with the U.S. Dairy Stewardship Commitment by the end of 2025. These assessments collect over 250 data points per farm on practices including fuel and electricity efficiency, water usage, nutrient application, crop rotation, and manure management, with the goal of universal compliance with the National Milk Producers Federation's Farmers Assuring Responsible Management (FARM) program. The commitments support broader reductions in environmental footprints, such as through increased cover crop adoption in partnerships like one with Bel Brands USA.
Land O'Lakes collaborates with partners to fund and implement farm-level improvements. In August 2023, the company partnered with the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency and The Hershey Company to allocate $2 million toward Land O'Lakes member dairy farms in Pennsylvania, focusing on water quality enhancements and mitigation of dairy production's environmental impacts through technical assistance and best management practices. Additional efforts include work with Mars Incorporated on on-farm conservation to lower emissions and resource use at supplier operations, and targeted methane management strategies to address dairy's contribution to potent greenhouse gases. Company-wide, Land O'Lakes has set long-term targets including net-zero emissions by 2050, integrated into its ESG framework to align farming practices with measurable sustainability outcomes.

Community and farmer support efforts

Land O'Lakes, as a farmer-owned cooperative, provides financial support to its member dairy farmers through programs like SECURE Financing, which offers flexible lines of credit up to $500,000 based on a one-page application and credit rating, enabling farmers to cover operating costs during periods of market uncertainty. The cooperative also operates TruCarbon, a farmer-owned carbon credit marketplace launched to reward regenerative agricultural practices, where participating farmers can earn approximately $20 per ton of carbon sequestered by adopting verified sustainable methods such as cover cropping and reduced tillage.
Through Land O'Lakes Venture37, a nonprofit arm established to strengthen global agricultural economies, the cooperative delivers training, mentorship, and market access programs that have impacted over 814,000 lives in farming communities worldwide by improving productivity and resilience in regions including Africa and Southeast Asia. Domestically, the cooperative advocates for policies benefiting members, such as streamlined U.S. Department of Agriculture programs in the 2023 Farm Bill, which aim to enhance risk management and conservation support for dairy producers.
For broader community efforts, the Land O'Lakes Foundation, funded by an annual 2% allocation of the cooperative's pre-tax profits, distributed $10.5 million in 2024 toward hunger relief, education advancement, and rural vitality initiatives, including product donations and grants to local organizations. The First Run Program, active for 14 years, has donated nearly 7.3 million pounds of dairy products to U.S. food banks, addressing food insecurity in both rural and urban areas. Additional support includes disaster relief partnerships, such as a $100,000 collective donation in 2021 with other cooperatives to aid winter storm victims through the American Red Cross and cattle associations. These efforts emphasize volunteerism and matching grants to amplify local impact in member communities.
