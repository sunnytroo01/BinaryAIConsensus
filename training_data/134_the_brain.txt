The brain is the most complex object in the known universe. Three pounds of soft tissue, roughly the consistency of firm gelatin, containing eighty-six billion neurons connected by approximately one hundred trillion synapses, consuming twenty percent of the body's energy while constituting only two percent of its mass. It is the organ that sees, hears, feels, remembers, imagines, plans, dreams, loves, fears, creates, and understands. It is the organ that is reading these words right now, converting patterns of light into electrical signals, translating those signals into language, and extracting meaning from symbols that would be completely meaningless to any machine or organism that had not learned to decode them.

The brain is organized into distinct regions, each specialized for particular functions, though these regions work together in integrated networks that produce the unified experience of consciousness.

The cerebral cortex, the wrinkled outer layer of the brain, is the seat of higher cognitive functions. It is divided into four lobes. The frontal lobe, located behind the forehead, is responsible for planning, decision-making, working memory, personality, and voluntary movement. It is the most recently evolved part of the brain and is proportionally much larger in humans than in any other animal. The parietal lobe, behind the frontal lobe, processes sensory information including touch, temperature, pain, and spatial awareness. The temporal lobe, on the sides of the brain, processes auditory information, language comprehension, and memory. The occipital lobe, at the back of the brain, processes visual information.

The hippocampus, a seahorse-shaped structure deep within the temporal lobe, is essential for forming new memories. Damage to the hippocampus prevents the formation of new long-term memories while leaving existing memories intact. The famous patient known as H.M., who had his hippocampus surgically removed in 1953 to treat severe epilepsy, could remember events from before the surgery but could not form any new long-term memories for the remaining fifty-five years of his life. He lived in a permanent present tense, meeting his doctors as strangers every day despite having seen them thousands of times.

The amygdala, an almond-shaped structure near the hippocampus, is the brain's alarm system. It processes emotions, particularly fear and anxiety, and triggers the fight-or-flight response when it detects a threat. The amygdala can activate a fear response before the conscious mind has even registered what the threat is, which is why you might jump at a shape in the dark before you realize it is just a coat on a hook. This rapid, unconscious threat detection was essential for survival in an environment where predators and dangers required immediate reaction.

The cerebellum, located at the base of the brain behind the brainstem, coordinates movement, balance, and motor learning. It contains more neurons than the rest of the brain combined, despite being only about ten percent of the brain's total volume. The cerebellum does not initiate movement, but it fine-tunes it, ensuring that your movements are smooth, coordinated, and accurate. Damage to the cerebellum does not cause paralysis but produces clumsy, uncoordinated movement, a condition called ataxia.

Neurons communicate through a combination of electrical and chemical signals. When a neuron is activated, an electrical impulse called an action potential travels along its axon at speeds of up to two hundred and seventy miles per hour. When the impulse reaches the synapse, the junction between two neurons, it triggers the release of chemical neurotransmitters that cross the tiny gap and bind to receptors on the next neuron, either exciting it or inhibiting it. The most important neurotransmitters include dopamine, which is involved in reward, motivation, and movement; serotonin, which is involved in mood, sleep, and appetite; glutamate, which is the primary excitatory neurotransmitter; and GABA, which is the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter.

Neuroplasticity is the brain's ability to reorganize itself by forming new neural connections throughout life. This ability is strongest in childhood, when the brain is developing rapidly, but it persists throughout adulthood. Every time you learn a new skill, form a new memory, or adapt to a new situation, the connections between your neurons are being modified. London taxi drivers, who must memorize the layout of twenty-five thousand streets to pass their licensing exam, have been shown to have enlarged hippocampi compared to non-taxi drivers, and the enlargement is proportional to the number of years they have been driving. This demonstrates that intensive learning physically changes the structure of the brain.

Sleep is essential for brain function. During sleep, the brain consolidates memories, moving information from short-term to long-term storage. It clears metabolic waste products that accumulate during waking hours through a system called the glymphatic system, which is most active during deep sleep. Sleep deprivation impairs attention, decision-making, emotional regulation, and immune function. Chronic sleep deprivation has been linked to increased risk of obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, depression, and neurodegenerative diseases including Alzheimer's.

Consciousness, the subjective experience of being aware, remains the deepest unsolved problem in neuroscience. We know which brain regions are active during conscious experience. We know that damage to certain regions abolishes consciousness. We know that anesthesia, which disrupts communication between brain regions, eliminates consciousness temporarily. But we do not understand how the physical activity of neurons gives rise to the subjective experience of seeing red, hearing music, or feeling love. This is what philosopher David Chalmers calls the "hard problem" of consciousness: not explaining what the brain does, which is the easy problem, but explaining why there is a subjective experience associated with what the brain does.

The brain is the organ that makes us human. It gives us language, art, science, music, mathematics, and morality. It allows us to remember the past, imagine the future, and reflect on our own existence. It enables us to understand the universe, from the subatomic particles that constitute matter to the galaxies that constitute the cosmos. And it enables us to study itself, the most improbable feat of all: a three-pound organ that has figured out how to investigate its own functioning. The brain studying the brain is the universe contemplating itself, and it is the most remarkable achievement of four billion years of evolution.
