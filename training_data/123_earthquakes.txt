An earthquake is a sudden release of energy in the Earth's crust that produces seismic waves, vibrations that radiate outward from the source and shake the ground, sometimes with devastating force. Earthquakes are among the most destructive natural disasters on Earth, capable of toppling buildings, triggering landslides, generating tsunamis, and killing hundreds of thousands of people in a matter of minutes. They strike without warning, cannot be prevented, and in many cases cannot be predicted with useful accuracy. Understanding earthquakes, their causes, their effects, and our ability to prepare for them, is essential for any society that occupies seismically active territory, which includes a significant fraction of the world's population.

The vast majority of earthquakes occur at tectonic plate boundaries, where the stress of plates pushing against, pulling apart from, or sliding past each other builds up over years, decades, or centuries until the rock along the boundary suddenly ruptures and releases the accumulated energy. The point within the Earth where the rupture begins is called the focus or hypocenter. The point on the surface directly above the focus is called the epicenter.

There are three types of fault motion that produce earthquakes. Normal faults occur at divergent boundaries where the crust is being stretched apart. The rock on one side of the fault drops down relative to the other. Reverse faults, also called thrust faults, occur at convergent boundaries where the crust is being compressed. The rock on one side of the fault is pushed up and over the other. Strike-slip faults occur at transform boundaries where the crust is being sheared horizontally. The San Andreas Fault in California and the North Anatolian Fault in Turkey are strike-slip faults.

Earthquake magnitude measures the total energy released by the earthquake. The most commonly used scale today is the moment magnitude scale, which replaced the older Richter scale for large earthquakes. The scale is logarithmic: each whole number increase in magnitude represents a thirty-two-fold increase in energy released. A magnitude 6 earthquake releases thirty-two times more energy than a magnitude 5, and a magnitude 7 releases approximately one thousand times more energy than a magnitude 5.

Earthquakes below magnitude 2 are generally not felt by humans and are detected only by sensitive instruments. Magnitude 3 to 4 earthquakes are often felt but rarely cause damage. Magnitude 5 to 6 earthquakes can cause significant damage to poorly constructed buildings. Magnitude 7 to 8 earthquakes cause severe damage over large areas and can be catastrophic in densely populated regions. Magnitude 9 earthquakes are extremely rare, occurring approximately once every hundred years on average, and represent the most powerful seismic events the planet can produce. The 2004 Indian Ocean earthquake, magnitude 9.1, generated a tsunami that killed approximately two hundred and thirty thousand people across fourteen countries, making it one of the deadliest natural disasters in recorded history.

The 2011 Tohoku earthquake off the coast of Japan, magnitude 9.1, triggered a massive tsunami with waves up to forty meters high that devastated the northeastern coast of Japan, killed approximately twenty thousand people, and caused the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster, the worst nuclear accident since Chernobyl. The earthquake was so powerful that it shifted the Earth's axis by approximately six and a half inches and shortened the length of the day by approximately 1.8 microseconds.

Seismic waves come in several types, each traveling through the Earth at different speeds and causing different types of ground motion. Primary waves, or P-waves, are compressional waves that travel through the Earth like sound waves through air, alternately compressing and expanding the rock. They are the fastest seismic waves and the first to arrive at a seismograph station. Secondary waves, or S-waves, are shear waves that move the rock perpendicular to the direction of travel, like shaking a rope. They are slower than P-waves and cannot travel through liquids, which is how scientists determined that the Earth's outer core is liquid: S-waves are not detected on the opposite side of the Earth from an earthquake because they cannot pass through the liquid outer core. Surface waves travel along the Earth's surface and are the slowest but most destructive seismic waves. They produce the ground motion that topples buildings and ruptures infrastructure.

The Ring of Fire is a horseshoe-shaped zone of intense seismic and volcanic activity that encircles the Pacific Ocean, following the boundaries of the Pacific Plate and several smaller plates. Approximately seventy-five percent of the world's active volcanoes and ninety percent of the world's earthquakes occur along the Ring of Fire. The zone includes the Pacific coasts of South America, Central America, North America, Alaska, Russia, Japan, the Philippines, Indonesia, and New Zealand.

Earthquake prediction remains one of the great unsolved problems in geophysics. Despite decades of research, scientists cannot reliably predict when, where, or how large an earthquake will be with the precision needed for practical warning. Short-term predictions, specifying that a significant earthquake will occur in a specific area within a few days, have never been achieved with scientific credibility. Long-term forecasts, which estimate the probability of a significant earthquake occurring in a specific area over decades, are more useful and have been reasonably successful. For example, seismologists have estimated that there is approximately a sixty percent probability of a magnitude 6.7 or greater earthquake in the San Francisco Bay Area before 2043, based on the historical seismicity of the region and the accumulated stress on the Hayward and San Andreas faults.

Earthquake early warning systems, while not predictions, provide seconds to tens of seconds of warning before strong shaking arrives at a location. These systems work by detecting the fast-moving but less destructive P-waves, which travel ahead of the slower but more destructive S-waves and surface waves. When P-waves are detected by seismometers near the epicenter, an alert is transmitted electronically, which travels at the speed of light, reaching distant locations before the destructive waves arrive. Japan's earthquake early warning system, the most advanced in the world, can provide ten to thirty seconds of warning for locations a few hundred miles from the epicenter. This is enough time to stop trains, open fire station doors, shut down gas pipelines, and allow people to take protective cover.

Earthquake engineering has saved countless lives by developing building codes and construction techniques that allow structures to withstand seismic forces. Base isolation, in which a building is placed on flexible bearings that absorb seismic energy, allows the ground to move beneath the building while the building itself remains relatively stationary. Reinforced concrete and steel frame construction distribute seismic forces through the structure rather than allowing them to concentrate at weak points. Shear walls, cross-bracing, and moment-resisting frames provide additional resistance to lateral forces. In the 2010 Chile earthquake, magnitude 8.8, modern buildings constructed to seismic codes performed remarkably well, with relatively few fatalities despite the enormous magnitude. In the 2010 Haiti earthquake, magnitude 7.0, a much weaker earthquake, approximately two hundred and fifty thousand people died because most buildings were constructed without any seismic provisions.

Tsunamis are one of the most devastating secondary effects of earthquakes. A tsunami is generated when an earthquake displaces a large volume of water, typically through the vertical movement of the sea floor at a subduction zone. The resulting wave is barely detectable in the open ocean, where it may be only a foot or two high spread over a wavelength of hundreds of miles. But as the wave approaches shore and the water depth decreases, the wave compresses, its height increases dramatically, and it can arrive as a wall of water thirty feet or more high, traveling inland at speeds of up to thirty miles per hour, destroying everything in its path.

The deadliest earthquake in recorded history was the 1556 Shaanxi earthquake in China, which killed an estimated eight hundred and thirty thousand people. Many of the victims lived in yaodongs, cave dwellings carved into soft loess cliffs that collapsed during the earthquake. The deadliest earthquake in the modern era was the 2010 Haiti earthquake, which killed approximately two hundred and fifty thousand people in a country with virtually no building codes, no earthquake early warning, and no disaster preparedness infrastructure.

Living with earthquake risk requires a combination of scientific understanding, engineering preparedness, and individual readiness. In earthquake-prone regions, buildings should be constructed or retrofitted to seismic codes. Emergency supplies, including water, food, first aid kits, flashlights, and batteries, should be maintained by every household. Families should have a communication plan and a meeting point in case they are separated. Heavy furniture should be secured to walls. Gas shut-off valves should be accessible. And every person should know the basic protective response: Drop, Cover, and Hold On. Drop to your hands and knees. Take cover under a sturdy desk or table. Hold on to the table and be prepared to move with it. This simple action protects against the falling objects and debris that cause the majority of earthquake injuries.

Earthquakes are a reminder that we live on the surface of a planet that is neither still nor stable. The solid ground we trust to hold our buildings, our roads, and our lives is a thin crust floating on a molten interior, cracked into pieces that are constantly in motion. Most of the time, this motion is imperceptible. Occasionally, violently, it is not. Preparing for that violence is not paranoia. It is wisdom.
