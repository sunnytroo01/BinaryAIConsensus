Edmontosaurus

 Edmontosaurus is a genus of large ornithischian dinosaur in the family Hadrosauridae, characterized by its duck-like bill, flat skull lacking a bony crest, and robust build adapted for both bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion. It encompasses two recognized species: Edmontosaurus regalis and Edmontosaurus annectens, both herbivorous hadrosaurines that used specialized dental batteries of hundreds of teeth to grind tough vegetation. These dinosaurs inhabited coastal plains and river valleys in western North America during the Late Cretaceous epoch.
The genus is known from abundant fossil material, including complete skeletons, trackways, and exceptionally preserved "mummified" specimens that provide insights into soft tissue anatomy, such as pebbly skin textures and possible fleshy combs or crests on the head and neck. Edmontosaurus regalis, the type species, was first described in 1917 by Lawrence Lambe based on specimens from the Horseshoe Canyon Formation in Alberta, Canada, dating to the late Campanian stage around 7371 million years ago. In contrast, Edmontosaurus annectens was originally named in 1892 by Othniel Charles Marsh and is more widespread, with fossils from Maastrichtian formations (approximately 7066 million years ago) across regions including South Dakota, Wyoming, Montana, and Alaska's North Slope.
Reaching lengths of up to 12 meters (39 feet) and weights of about 4 metric tons (4.4 short tons), Edmontosaurus individuals were among the largest hadrosaurids, comparable in size to a school bus, with powerful hind limbs, hoof-like forefoot nails, and a broad, flat muzzle suited for cropping low-lying plants. Bonebeds and track sites suggest they lived in large herds, potentially migrating seasonally, and paleopathological evidence from specimens shows healed injuries likely inflicted by predators such as Tyrannosaurus rex. As one of the most common dinosaurs in its ecosystems, Edmontosaurus played a key role in Late Cretaceous food webs until the end-Cretaceous mass extinction event around 66 million years ago.

Discovery and naming

Early finds and initial descriptions

The initial discoveries of Edmontosaurus fossils occurred during the intense period of paleontological exploration in North America known as the Bone Wars, a rivalry between Edward Drinker Cope and Othniel Charles Marsh that spurred rapid but often hasty classifications of dinosaur remains. In 1872, Cope described a partial skeleton from the Lance Formation in Wyoming as Agathaumas sylvestris, initially interpreting it as a large ceratopsian but later recognized as belonging to a hadrosaurid similar to Edmontosaurus; this material included vertebrae, ribs, and pelvic elements, marking one of the earliest hadrosaur finds in the region. Cope's work on hadrosaur skulls, including those from Montana exposures, emphasized their broad, flattened rostra, which he likened to a duck's bill, influencing early perceptions of these dinosaurs as specialized herbivores.
In response to Cope's efforts, Marsh named Claosaurus annectens in 1892 based on a partial skeleton, including a well-preserved skull (USNM 2414), collected from the Lance Formation near Lusk, Wyoming (though exposures extend into South Dakota). This specimen, measuring about one-third the size of the earlier Hadrosaurus foulkii, featured slender proportions and a crestless skull, which Marsh described in detail as indicative of a transitional ornithopod form bridging earlier duck-billed dinosaurs. The naming reflected the competitive fervor of the era, with Marsh aiming to outpace Cope by establishing new genera amid fragmented remains that were poorly understood at the time.
Canadian discoveries advanced understanding of Edmontosaurus in the early 1900s, particularly along the Red Deer River Valley in Alberta, where exposures of the Horseshoe Canyon Formation (then called the Edmonton Formation) yielded multiple well-preserved skeletons. In 1912, a Sternberg family expedition led by Charles H. Sternberg uncovered significant hadrosaur material near Drumheller, including partial skeletons with skin impressions and ossified tendons, which highlighted the abundance of these dinosaurs in floodplain environments. Building on these finds, Lawrence Lambe formally named Edmontosaurus regalis in 1917 from a nearly complete skeleton (holotype CMN 2288) collected from the same formation at the mouth of Three Hills Creek, describing it as a large, crestless hadrosaur with robust postcranial elements adapted for terrestrial locomotion.
Early interpretations were marred by the Bone Wars' rivalries, leading to misconceptions such as over-splitting genera and erroneous reconstructions of hadrosaur posture and ecology; for instance, both Cope and Marsh depicted these animals as primarily bipedal, underestimating their facultative quadrupedality based on incomplete skeletons. These initial efforts, despite their flaws, laid the groundwork for recognizing Edmontosaurus as a dominant Late Cretaceous herbivore across western North America.

Iconic specimens and mummified remains

The first iconic mummified specimen of Edmontosaurus annectens, cataloged as AMNH 5060, was discovered in 1908 by paleontologist Charles H. Sternberg in the Lance Formation of east-central Wyoming. This nearly complete individual preserved extensive skin impressions over the torso and limbs, along with traces interpreted as possible gut contents, marking it as the earliest known example of a dinosaur "mummy" and providing unprecedented insights into hadrosaur soft anatomy.
In 1912, Henry Fairfield Osborn published a detailed description of AMNH 5060, highlighting the polygonal mosaic of small, non-overlapping skin scales across much of the body and the specimen's characteristic "puffed-up" posture, which he attributed to post-mortem desiccation that inflated the integument before burial. Osborn coined the term "dinosaur mummy" for such preservations, emphasizing the rarity of the skin renderings that suggested a terrestrial lifestyle with scaly, reptilian-like covering. The outstretched forelimbs and twisted neck further indicated the animal's final moments, likely involving rapid exposure followed by entombment.
A second exceptionally preserved specimen, known as the Senckenberg mummy (SMF R 4036), was unearthed in 1910 by Sternberg and his team from the same Lance Formation locality, featuring broad areas of skin impressions including the upper bill and limb regions. This individual displayed folded hindlimbs and a contracted posture consistent with death by sudden sediment entrapment, preserving details of the integument that complemented the first find. It was acquired by the Senckenberg Museum and first noted in scientific literature around 1914, underscoring the localized "mummy zone" in Wyoming's floodplain deposits.
During the 1920s and 1930s, additional mummified and skin-impression-bearing Edmontosaurus specimens emerged from excavations in Alberta's Edmonton Formation and Montana's Hell Creek Formation, including partial individuals with substantial soft-tissue coverage that expanded knowledge of hadrosaur integument variation. These finds, often collected by field teams led by figures like Charles M. Sternberg, revealed consistent patterns of scaly skin and occasional webbing-like structures in the manus. Early paleontologists analyzed their preservation as resulting from rapid burial in fine-grained floodplain sediments, which protected the desiccated carcasses from scavengers and decay, facilitating the formation of detailed impressions in anoxic conditions.

Taxonomic revisions and recent studies

The taxonomic history of Edmontosaurus is complex, involving multiple synonymies and reclassifications. The species E. annectens originated as Claosaurus annectens in 1892, while E. regalis was named in 1917. In 1942, Richard Swann Lull and Wright combined both into the genus Anatosaurus, with A. annectens and A. copei (for regalis). This arrangement persisted until 1979, when John H. Ostrom and Peter Dodson synonymized Anatosaurus with Edmontosaurus, restoring the original genus name. Phylogenetic analyses since the 2000s have consistently placed Edmontosaurus as a derived hadrosaurine within Hadrosauridae, closely related to other North American duck-billed dinosaurs like Saurolophus.
Recent studies as of 2025 have refined species distinctions and expanded the known range. In 2020, remains from Alaska's Prince Creek Formation were assigned to Edmontosaurus sp., supporting a broader Arctic distribution. A 2023 study described new Edmontosaurus material from Alberta's Brazeau Formation, confirming its presence in early Maastrichtian deposits. In 2025, a bonebed in the Horseshoe Canyon Formation yielded multiple specimens, aiding chronostratigraphic correlation, while reanalysis of the Wyoming "mummy zone" revealed hoof-like structures and fleshy features in E. annectens. These findings affirm the validity of the two species and highlight ongoing taxonomic stability amid new discoveries.

Taxonomy and species

Recognized species

The genus Edmontosaurus currently includes two recognized species: the type species E. regalis and E. annectens. Edmontosaurus regalis, described by Lawrence Lambe in 1917, is known primarily from the late Campanian Horseshoe Canyon Formation in Alberta, Canada, approximately 7371 million years ago. It is characterized by a relatively compact postcranial skeleton and cranial features such as a broader frontal bone compared to E. annectens.
Edmontosaurus annectens, named by Othniel Charles Marsh in 1892, is more widespread and abundant, with fossils from Maastrichtian formations (about 7066 million years ago) including the Hell Creek, Lance, and Prince Creek formations across western North America, from Montana to Alaska. It tends to exhibit a more elongate skull in mature individuals and larger overall size.

Synonymy, debates, and phylogenetic position

Over the history of hadrosaurid taxonomy, several names have been proposed for fossils now attributed to Edmontosaurus. The genus Thespesius, erected by Edward Drinker Cope in 1872 for fragmentary remains (T. occidentalis) from the Maastrichtian Lance Formation, was an early designation for what is now recognized as E. annectens, rendering it a junior synonym due to priority rules and subsequent reassignments. Additionally, Thespesius edmontoni (Gilmore, 1924), from the Campanian of Alberta, has been synonymized with E. regalis based on morphometric analyses showing overlap in cranial variation.
Similarly, Anatosaurus was established by Lull and Wright in 1942 to encompass A. annectens (the type species) and A. copei, based on specimens from the Hell Creek and Lance Formations; however, Michael K. Brett-Surman in 1979 synonymized Anatosaurus with Edmontosaurus (except for A. copei), prioritizing the senior name Edmontosaurus from 1917.
The status of Anatotitan copei (established by Lull and Wright in 1942 as a species of Anatosaurus) has been particularly contentious, with some retaining it as a distinct genus due to its larger size and more elongate skull. A 2011 morphometric analysis of nearly all known edmontosaur skulls by Campione et al. demonstrated that A. copei represents the mature growth stage of E. annectens, characterized by allometric changes in cranial proportions, thus supporting its synonymy with Edmontosaurus annectens. This revision reduced the recognized diversity of late Maastrichtian megaherbivores in North America, emphasizing ontogenetic variation over taxonomic separation.
Taxonomic debates regarding Ugrunaaluk kuukpikensis, named in 2015 by Druckenmiller et al. for Alaskan hadrosaurine remains from the Prince Creek Formation, initially distinguished as a separate genus based on subtle cranial differences such as a narrower jugal process. However, a 2017 cladistic reevaluation by Prieto-Mrquez et al. argued that these features fall within the intraspecific variation of Edmontosaurus annectens, particularly shared traits like the lateral exposure width of the maxilla. A 2020 study further supported this, concluding that Ugrunaaluk is a junior synonym of E. annectens based on detailed cranial comparisons and phylogenetic analyses.
Phylogenetically, Edmontosaurus occupies a basal position within Saurolophinae, the flat-headed clade of Hadrosauridae. A comprehensive 2010 analysis by Prieto-Mrquez, incorporating 70 taxa and over 200 characters, recovered Edmontosaurus (with Shantungosaurus) outside the core Saurolophinae clade in parsimony analyses, or in a basal hadrosaurine position in Bayesian methods, supported by plesiomorphic traits such as a robust but unadorned skull roof. This basal placement within saurolophines has been corroborated by subsequent matrix-based cladograms in the 2020s.
Additionally, potential Asian relatives, such as the 2019 Japanese hadrosaurine Kamuysaurus japonicus from the Yezo Group, suggest broader dispersal of edmontosaurins across Beringia, with cladistic results placing Kamuysaurus as a close outgroup to Edmontosaurus based on shared dental battery features and limb robusticity.

Description

Size and general anatomy

Edmontosaurus was one of the largest hadrosaurid dinosaurs, with adults of E. annectens reaching lengths of up to 1213 meters and estimated masses around 4 metric tons. For E. regalis, maximum lengths extended to approximately 13 meters, with potential masses up to 4.5 metric tons derived from femoral scaling relationships. These dimensions reflect a robust, herbivorous ornithopod adapted to Late Cretaceous floodplains, where body size likely aided in predator deterrence and resource competition.
The general build of Edmontosaurus was that of a facultatively bipedal quadruped, capable of shifting between quadrupedal locomotion for foraging and bipedal stances for vigilance or escape. It featured a robust torso supported by a strong axial skeleton, a long tail comprising roughly 50% of total body length for balance and propulsion, and broad feet that distributed weight across soft substrates. This configuration emphasized stability and efficiency in terrestrial movement, with the tail serving as a counterweight during both gaits.
Body proportions highlighted adaptations for versatile locomotion, with hindlimbs approximately 60% longer than forelimbs to facilitate bipedal bursts while maintaining quadrupedal support. Estimated hip heights ranged from 3 to 4 meters in adults, contributing to an overall stature that elevated the animal above much of the vegetation it consumed. These limb disparities underscore Edmontosaurus's evolutionary emphasis on hindlimb power for speed and maneuverability.
Mass estimates for Edmontosaurus have been refined through volumetric methods, including 3D modeling from laser imaging and photogrammetry to reconstruct body volume and density. For instance, Bates et al. (2009) applied LiDAR scanning to specimens, generating sensitivity-tested models that account for soft tissue variation and yield more accurate scaling than traditional skeletal measurements alone. Such approaches reveal how Edmontosaurus's massive frame was distributed, with denser torso regions and lighter tail contributing to overall balance.

Skull and oral structures

The skull of Edmontosaurus featured a distinctive flat, broad rostrum formed by the edentulous premaxillae, which expanded laterally to create a duckbill-like structure for cropping vegetation, and an edentulous predentary bone at the anterior tip of the lower jaw. In adult specimens, the overall skull length reached up to 1.28 meters, with the posterior region of the cranium widening to provide attachment sites for robust jaw adductor muscles, enabling powerful bite forces. Unlike lambeosaurine hadrosaurs, Edmontosaurus lacked any bony nasal or supraorbital crest, resulting in a relatively smooth, triangular cranial profile in lateral view.
The dentition of Edmontosaurus was highly specialized, comprising a complex dental battery with over 1,000 teeth distributed across both upper and lower jaws in adults, organized into approximately 60 tooth families per dentary and maxilla. Each tooth position typically held 27 teeth at various stages of growth, with the functional occlusal surfaces forming diamond-shaped wear facets ideal for shearing fibrous plant matter through a grinding motion. Tooth crowns in the mid-battery measured about 2937 mm in height and 812 mm in width, featuring a prominent median ridge and secondary ridges on the lingual surface in some teeth to enhance food processing efficiency. Replacement occurred rapidly, with new teeth erupting every 50 days to maintain the battery's integrity despite heavy wear.
Additional oral structures supported advanced herbivory, including a secondary bony palate formed by the palatines, pterygoids, and ectopterygoids, which separated the nasal and oral cavities and featured transverse ridges for manipulating food boluses. The quadrate bone, articulating the upper and lower jaws, was dorsoventrally elongate and robust, curving posteriorly to facilitate transverse jaw movement, with heights exceeding 30 cm in large adults to accommodate the expanded temporal fenestrae. The orbit, bordered by the prefrontal, postorbital, and jugal bones, measured approximately 20 cm in diameter in mature specimens, providing space for large eyes suited to its terrestrial habitat.

Postcranial skeleton and limbs

The postcranial skeleton of Edmontosaurus supported its massive, versatile body. The vertebral column included approximately 15 dorsal vertebrae with elongated centra that were longer than tall, featuring anteriorly convex shapes in the anterior dorsals and increasing neural spine height posteriorly for enhanced muscular support. Cervical and dorsal ribs were robust and curved, contributing to a barrel-shaped ribcage that protected vital organs and aided in quadrupedal stability.
The pectoral girdle consisted of long, blade-like scapulae with expanded anterior margins, held roughly parallel to the vertebral column, and coracoids that formed a broad attachment for forelimb musculature. Forelimbs were robust but shorter than hindlimbs, with humeri featuring broad proximal heads for weight-bearing, ulnae and radii providing strong support, and manual phalanges ending in hoof-like unguals suited for quadrupedal stance.
The pelvic girdle was characterized by an elongate ilium with a flattened posterior edge inclined anteriorly, short pubes, and robust ischia forming the posterior margin. Hindlimbs were powerful, with straight-shafted femora, tibiae longer than fibulae, and a pes featuring three main weight-bearing toes capped by keratinous hooves, as revealed in preserved soft tissues; the fourth toe was reduced. Metatarsals IIIV were subequal, supporting efficient terrestrial locomotion.

Skin impressions and soft tissues

Skin impressions from Edmontosaurus fossils reveal a predominantly scaled integument, consisting of a mosaic of small, non-overlapping polygonal scales measuring 1-4 mm in diameter across much of the body, with larger, pebble-like tubercles present on the tail and lower limbs. These patterns are documented in multiple well-preserved specimens, including recent analyses of mummified individuals from the Hell Creek Formation, where the scales exhibit a pebbly texture similar to modern lizards. Adult Edmontosaurus lacked feathers, consistent with the scaled skin typical of advanced ornithischians, though comparisons to juvenile ornithischians suggest the possibility of sparse filamentous structures in early growth stages.
Impressions from mummified remains provide exceptional detail on soft tissue preservation. For instance, the iconic AMNH 5060 specimen preserves extensive skin textures, including outlines of dehydrated muscles along the limbs and impressions on the manus suggesting interdigital webbing, now interpreted as skin folds resulting from rapid desiccation rather than true webbing. Similarly, the Dakota mummy (cf. Edmontosaurus sp.) from North Dakota retains three-dimensional soft-tissue structures, such as layered integument and possible muscle fibers, preserved through mineralization processes. Recent 2025 analyses of two additional mummified specimens reveal a fleshy crest extending along the midline of the neck and trunk, interdigitating rows of spikes along the back, and hoof-like keratin sheaths encasing the tips of the three main hind toes, confirming adaptations for terrestrial life on soft substrates. These features highlight how burial in fine-grained sediments facilitated the retention of external anatomy.
Chemical analyses confirm the presence of original biomolecules in these soft tissues. A 2009 study of the Dakota mummy identified organic molecules, including collagen-derived compounds and associated microstructures, within mineralized skin layers, demonstrating exceptional molecular preservation in hadrosaur integument. More recently, in 2025, tandem mass spectrometry detected hydroxyprolinean amino acid unique to collagenin samples from an Edmontosaurus fossil, verifying endogenous proteins and advancing understanding of soft-tissue durability over 66 million years. Coloration evidence is limited, with no indications of extensive pigments, though scale impressions suggest countershading patterns with darker dorsal and lighter ventral tones for camouflage.
Such impressions are preserved on approximately 20% of known Edmontosaurus specimens, predominantly those of E. annectens from Late Cretaceous deposits in North America, underscoring the taphonomic conditions favoring integumental fossilization in floodplain environments.

Paleobiology

Diet, feeding mechanics, and gut contents

Edmontosaurus was a herbivorous dinosaur that primarily low-browsed on vegetation such as conifers, ferns, and horsetails in its Late Cretaceous floodplain environments. Dental microwear texture analysis of its teeth reveals rougher surface textures compared to earlier ornithopods, indicating a dietary shift toward more abrasive foodstuffs, likely including silica-rich phytoliths from emerging angiosperms and tougher herbaceous plants.
The feeding mechanics of Edmontosaurus involved a complex jaw system enabling efficient processing of fibrous plant material. Its dental battery featured tightly packed, diamond-shaped teeth that sheared vegetation through palinal (rearward) motion of the mandible combined with transverse rotation, allowing for grinding and pulverization similar to modern herbivorous mammals. This mechanism was facilitated by flexible intracranial joints, which permitted independent movement of the palate and dentary during mastication. Estimated bite forces ranged from moderate levels suitable for cropping and shearing tough vegetation, though precise values vary with skull size and modeling assumptions.
Direct evidence of diet comes from preserved gut contents in well-preserved mummified specimens of Edmontosaurus annectens from Maastrichtian formations such as the Hell Creek Formation. Analysis of these contents identified carbonized plant remains including conifer needles, fern fronds, and various seeds, confirming a mixed herbivorous intake focused on soft to moderately tough lowland flora.
Stable isotope studies of tooth enamel provide additional confirmation of a C3 plant-based diet, with C values ranging from -10 to -6, consistent with consumption of non-grass vegetation under a closed-canopy or riparian habitat. These values exhibit intra-tooth variation of up to 4, interpreted as seasonal shifts in diet reflecting changes in plant availability or foraging behavior during wetter and drier periods.

Growth patterns and ontogeny

Edmontosaurus exhibited rapid growth during its juvenile phase, characterized by highly vascularized, woven-fibered bone tissue that supported fast linear growth rates, before decelerating after approximately 10 years of age. Individuals reached sexual maturity between 8 and 10 years, with somatic maturity occurring around 9-15 years, and overall lifespans extending to 20-30 years based on lines of arrested growth (LAGs) in long bones.
Bone histology reveals fibrolamellar bone deposition indicative of fast, continuous growth, interrupted by annuli or LAGs that suggest seasonal pauses in deposition, particularly in humeri and femora from bonebed assemblages. In Edmontosaurus annectens, this pattern enabled individuals to attain approximately 4 tons (reaching 95% of asymptotic body mass of about 5.6 tons) within roughly 15 years, transitioning to slower appositional growth in adulthood marked by secondary osteons.
Ontogenetic changes were pronounced in both cranial and postcranial morphology. Juveniles displayed more slender, mediolaterally narrower snouts suited for selective foraging, while adults developed robust, wide jaws with square snouts for broader browsing. Postcranially, early juveniles were predominantly bipedal with relatively longer hindlimbs, shifting toward a quadrupedal stance in subadults and adults as forelimb proportions strengthened and body mass increased, as evidenced by limb allometry in growth series.
Specimen series from monodominant bonebeds, such as the Ruth Mason Dinosaur Quarry, document age clusters corresponding to late juveniles (around 2 years), subadults (3-7 years), and adults (over 6 years), indicating gregarious growth within age-segregated cohorts that facilitated rapid development in social groups.

Locomotion, posture, and speed

Edmontosaurus was capable of both bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion, with juveniles primarily bipedal and adults more facultatively quadrupedal due to increased body mass and forelimb robustness. Trackways suggest walking speeds of 10-20 km/h (6-12 mph), with potential for faster bursts up to 25-30 km/h (15-19 mph) when fleeing predators, based on limb proportions and biomechanical models.

Brain structure, senses, and pathologies

The brain of Edmontosaurus was relatively small compared to body size, with an endocranial volume estimated at 300-500 cm, indicating moderate cognitive capabilities focused on sensory processing for foraging and social interactions. Sensory adaptations included large olfactory bulbs suggesting a good sense of smell for locating vegetation, and evidence of pathologies such as healed bite marks on tails and limbs, likely from predator attacks by Tyrannosaurus rex.

Social behavior and life history

Bonebeds and track sites indicate Edmontosaurus lived in large herds, potentially numbering in the hundreds, with evidence of seasonal migrations along coastal plains. Age-segregated assemblages suggest ontogenetic segregation, where juveniles formed separate groups from adults, possibly for protection and resource partitioning. Reproductive behavior is inferred from growth data, with sexual maturity at 8-10 years and possible nesting in colonies similar to other hadrosaurs.

Paleoecology

Temporal and spatial distribution

Edmontosaurus ranged across western North America during the Late Cretaceous, from the late Campanian to the Maastrichtian stages, spanning approximately 73 to 66 million years ago. Fossils of the genus have been recovered from over 100 localities within the Western Interior, extending from the Prince Creek Formation in northern Alaska to formations in Wyoming and South Dakota, but notably absent from eastern North America in the Appalachian region separated by the Western Interior Seaway. This broad distribution reflects adaptation to coastal plain habitats along the continent's western margin.
The two recognized species exhibit temporally segregated occurrences. Edmontosaurus regalis is primarily known from the late Campanian, with key finds in the Horseshoe Canyon Formation of the Edmonton Group in Alberta, Canada, dated to around 7271 million years ago. This species shows a northern biogeographic bias, with localities concentrated in southern and central Alberta, suggesting a preference for higher-latitude coastal environments during its time. In contrast, Edmontosaurus annectens dominated the late Maastrichtian, appearing in formations such as the Lance Formation in Wyoming (approximately 6968 million years ago) and the Hell Creek Formation across Montana, North Dakota, and South Dakota (around 6866 million years ago).
Stratigraphically, Edmontosaurus is prominent in several key units of the Western Interior Basin, including the Edmonton Group in Canada, where it contributes to diverse dinosaur assemblages. The genus reached peak abundance in the intervals immediately preceding the Cretaceous-Paleogene extinction boundary, particularly in the uppermost Maastrichtian deposits like the Hell Creek and Lance formations, underscoring its role as a dominant megaherbivore in late Laramidian ecosystems. Recent Alaskan discoveries further extend the known range northward, reinforcing the genus's extensive latitudinal distribution.

Formation environments and ecosystems

The formations yielding Edmontosaurus fossils, including the Hell Creek Formation in the western United States and the upper Edmonton Group in western Canada, represent depositional environments of subtropical coastal plains dominated by meandering fluvial systems, deltas, and associated floodplains. These settings featured anastomosing rivers with channels that migrated laterally, forming oxbow lakes and crevasse splays, alongside overbank areas prone to periodic inundation and sediment aggradation. In the Hell Creek Formation, sediments consist primarily of sandstones and mudstones indicative of low-sinuosity to meandering streams within an alluvial plain, transitioning eastward to deltaic influences near the Western Interior Seaway. The Edmonton Group similarly records fluvial deposition in a foreland basin, with coal-bearing overbank and channel-fill deposits reflecting a coastal plain environment influenced by subsidence and sediment supply from the rising Rocky Mountains.
The paleoclimate of these Maastrichtian environments was warm-temperate, with mean annual temperatures estimated at 2025C based on oxygen isotope (O) analyses of continental vertebrates, indicating a latitudinal gradient that moderated toward subtropical conditions at lower paleolatitudes. Seasonal rainfall patterns prevailed, with humid conditions punctuated by dry seasons, as evidenced by O values in fossil phosphates and carbonates that suggest elevated evaporation in floodplain settings during low-precipitation periods. Palynological assemblages from the Hell Creek Formation further support this, revealing pollen spectra dominated by thermophilic taxa adapted to warm, seasonally variable moisture regimes.
Vegetation in these ecosystems was characterized by angiosperm-dominated forests, comprising approximately 8090% of the floral diversity, with understories of ferns and a canopy including magnoliids and early palms. Conifers accounted for about 20% of the assemblage, primarily as scattered stands in upland or disturbed areas, while palynomorphs indicate a mix of broad-leaved deciduous and evergreen elements suited to the subtropical setting. This floral structure reflects a multi-tiered woodland adapted to the warm-temperate climate, with angiosperms like magnolia relatives providing key structural components.
Taphonomic patterns of Edmontosaurus fossils, particularly in monodominant bonebeds, arise from catastrophic events such as river floods that transported and buried herds in overbank deposits. These assemblages are preserved in fine-grained, organic-rich mudstones formed during crevasse splay or avulsion events on the floodplain, where rapid sedimentation protected disarticulated skeletons from prolonged exposure and scavenging. In the Hell Creek Formation, such bonebeds often show hydraulic equivalence and sorting consistent with flood-generated hyperconcentrated flows, entombing multiple individuals in channel-proximal overbank settings. Similar taphonomic signatures occur in the Edmonton Group, where floodplain inundation preserved herd mortality events in low-energy depositional traps.

Interactions with predators and competitors

Edmontosaurus served as a primary prey item for large tyrannosaurid theropods in its Late Cretaceous ecosystems. In the Maastrichtian Hell Creek Formation, Tyrannosaurus rex is documented as a predator through direct evidence, including a shed tooth crown embedded in the caudal centrum of an Edmontosaurus tail vertebra, surrounded by reactive bone growth indicating the animal survived the initial attack. Bite marks attributable to tyrannosaurids are frequently observed on Edmontosaurus and other hadrosaur bones, with up to 14% of specimens from certain formations showing puncture and drag traces consistent with theropod dentition; many such marks on adult individuals display signs of healing, suggesting non-lethal encounters. Earlier in the Campanian, Daspletosaurus preyed on similar hadrosaurs in the Dinosaur Park Formation, while Albertosaurus preyed on Edmontosaurus regalis in the Horseshoe Canyon Formation, as inferred from co-occurrence in bonebeds and matching theropod bite traces on hadrosaur remains.
Juveniles of Edmontosaurus were particularly vulnerable to smaller carnivores, including dromaeosaurids such as Saurornitholestes, which left diagnostic tooth marks on the bones of young hadrosaurs during scavenging or predation attempts. Adult Edmontosaurus likely mitigated predation risks through social herd dynamics, where coordinated stampedes could overwhelm or evade pursuing tyrannosaurids.
Edmontosaurus coexisted and competed with other hadrosaurids, such as Parasaurolophus in formations like the Dinosaur Park and Hypacrosaurus with E. regalis in the Horseshoe Canyon Formation, for low- to mid-level browse in floodplain environments. Analysis of skull mechanics and body proportions reveals no significant niche partitioning by feeding height among these taxa, with all capable of accessing vegetation up to approximately 5 meters when bipedal, implying overlap in resource use.
Fossils of Edmontosaurus are abundant in Maastrichtian strata immediately below the Cretaceous-Paleogene (K-Pg) boundary, including extensive monodominant bonebeds that reflect high population densities and gregariousness, with no indication of a pre-boundary decline in diversity or abundance. The genus abruptly vanishes above the iridium-enriched K-Pg boundary clay, consistent with the sudden mass extinction triggered by the Chicxulub asteroid impact, though no individual Edmontosaurus specimens preserve direct traces of impact-related effects such as iridium anomalies or ejecta layers in their sediments.
