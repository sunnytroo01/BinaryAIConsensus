 A doughnut, also spelled donut, is a sweet fried pastry typically made from a dough of flour, eggs, sugar, and leavened with yeast or baking powder, deep-fried until golden and often topped with glazes, icings, or sprinkles. It is commonly shaped as a ring with a hole in the center or as solid balls, and may be filled with jams, creams, or custards.
The origins of the doughnut trace back to European culinary traditions, but it gained prominence in America through Dutch settlers in the 17th century who introduced "olykoeks," or "oil cakes," which were balls of sweetened fried dough. The iconic ring shape is credited to American ship captain Hanson Crockett Gregory, who in 1847 reportedly punched out the centers of dough balls using a pepper box to improve even cooking and prevent soggy cores during sea voyages. Commercial production began in the early 20th century, with Adolph Levitt inventing the first automated doughnut machine in 1920 to meet rising demand during World War I, when the Salvation Army popularized doughnuts among soldiers.
Doughnuts are broadly categorized into two main types: yeast-raised doughnuts, which are light and airy due to fermentation, and cake doughnuts, which are denser and leavened with baking powder or soda for a crumbly texture. Popular varieties include the classic glazed yeast doughnut, Boston cream filled with vanilla custard and chocolate icing, jelly-filled options, and crullers made from twisted pte choux dough. Regional and modern innovations, such as cronuts (a croissant-doughnut hybrid), mochi doughnuts with chewy rice flour exteriors, and chocolate fondant donuts featuring molten chocolate centers or ganache fillings that can be replicated at home using baked or fried recipes, reflect the pastry's adaptability and global appeal.
In American culture, doughnuts symbolize comfort and indulgence, with chains like Dunkin' and Krispy Kreme driving mass consumption since the mid-20th century, and National Doughnut Day observed annually on the first Friday of June to honor the Salvation Army's wartime efforts. Today, they remain a staple in bakeries worldwide. Small-batch artisan doughnut shops differentiate their flavor offerings through handcrafted production using premium, natural ingredients, enabling creative experimentation not feasible in mass production. These shops innovate with global inspirations (such as matcha, taro, and hibiscus), twists on classics (such as miso caramel and maple bacon), seasonal and limited-time options (such as pumpkin spice and peppermint bark), and unique pairings (such as lavender honey and salted chocolate caramel), emphasizing novelty, freshness, visual appeal, and dietary accommodations like vegan options. This appeals to consumers seeking indulgent, personalized experiences beyond standard doughnuts, underscoring their evolution from simple fried treats to a versatile dessert.

History

Early British and Colonial Origins

The precursors to the modern doughnut can be traced to medieval British cuisine, where fried dough fritters were common treats prepared by coating fruits, herbs, or simple dough in batter and deep-frying them in oil or fat. These fritters, such as the "cryppys" or "crisps" described in 14th-century English manuscripts like The Forme of Cury, were often sweetened with honey and served as festive desserts, representing an early form of deep-fried dough confections that influenced later European baking traditions.
Dutch settlers introduced a more direct ancestor of the doughnut to the American colonies in the 17th century, bringing olykoeksliterally "oily cakes"to New Amsterdam (present-day New York) around the 1620s as part of their colonization efforts in New Netherland. These were solid balls of yeast-leavened dough made with flour, eggs, milk, sugar, and sometimes fruit or nuts for flavor, fried in pork fat or oil to create a golden exterior while remaining dense inside; a recipe for olykoeks appears in the 1667 Dutch cookbook De Verstandige Kock, emphasizing their portability for sailors and settlers. English immigrants also contributed similar fried dough traditions upon their arrival, blending with Dutch influences to popularize the treat in colonial households. By the late 18th century, these confections were becoming known in American households, blending Dutch and English traditions. The term "doughnut" first appeared in print in 1809, in Washington Irving's A History of New York, referring to "oly koeks" as dough nuts.
A pivotal evolution occurred in 1847 when American sea captain Hanson Crockett Gregory, from the Rockport-Camden area of Maine and then a teenage crew member on a lime-trading ship, reportedly punched holes in the center of olykoek-style dough balls to ensure even cooking and prevent raw centers, transforming them into ring-shaped pastries; he later shared the technique with his mother upon returning home. According to Gregory's own account in a late-19th-century Boston Post interview, he used a tin pepper box or bayonet to create the hole during a stormy voyage, improving digestibility and allowing the treats to be skewered on the ship's wheel for storage; while the legend's details vary and some historians note skepticism due to earlier ring-shaped fried dough in Dutch olykoeks or other cultures, Gregory is credited with popularizing the American ring doughnut for uniform frying, with his claim recognized by a commemorative plaque at his birthplace in Rockport, Maine, and an inscription on his gravestone in Quincy, Massachusetts.

Expansion in the 19th and 20th Centuries

In the 19th century, doughnuts transitioned from homemade treats to a more widespread American staple, particularly in New England and the Midwest, where they were fried in lard and often spiced with nutmeg or cinnamon for breakfast or afternoon snacks. This period saw early commercialization as bakeries and street vendors began selling them alongside coffee, capitalizing on their affordability and portability amid urbanization and immigration waves that introduced variations from Dutch and German influences. By the mid-1800s, the ring-shaped doughnut, credited to sailor Hanson Gregory's innovation of punching out the center to ensure even cooking, had become emblematic of American ingenuity and thrift.
The early 20th century marked a pivotal shift toward industrialization, exemplified by Russian immigrant Adolph Levitt's invention of the first automated doughnut machine in 1920 in New York City. This device, which extruded, cut, fried, and cooled doughnuts at a rate of 60 per minute, enabled mass production and addressed post-World War I demand; Levitt's Doughnut Corporation of America soon supplied bakeries nationwide, generating millions in annual revenue by the 1930s. Concurrently, the Salvation Army's "doughnut lassies"about 250 female volunteersplayed a crucial role during World War I by frying up to 9,000 doughnuts daily near the front lines in France using improvised equipment like shell casings as rolling pins, providing comfort to American soldiers and embedding the treat in military culture, which fueled its postwar surge in popularity.
Commercial chains further propelled doughnut dissemination in the mid-20th century, with Krispy Kreme founded in 1937 by Vernon Rudolph in Winston-Salem, North Carolina, using a secret yeast-raised recipe that emphasized fresh, hot glazing; by the 1950s, the company adopted automated equipment like the Ring King machine, scaling production to hundreds of dozens per hour per store. Dunkin' Donuts followed in 1950, established by William Rosenberg in Quincy, Massachusetts, initially as a coffee-and-doughnut shop that franchised rapidly, emphasizing variety with over 50 flavors. These chains integrated doughnuts into the fast-food landscape, with Krispy Kreme expanding southward and Dunkin' northward into Canada by the 1960s.
Post-World War II suburbanization and the interstate highway system accelerated doughnut growth in the U.S. and Canada, as drive-thru shops proliferated in new commercial strips, making them a convenient family treat amid economic prosperity. In the U.S., annual production reached billions by the late 20th century, while in Canada, chains like Tim Hortons (founded 1964) adapted the model, blending doughnuts with coffee culture to serve millions daily in urban and suburban settings. This era solidified doughnuts' role in everyday consumerism, from school fundraisers to office breaks, reflecting broader trends in convenience foods.

Etymology

Origins of "Doughnut"

The term "doughnut" emerged in early 19th-century American English, derived from the combination of "dough," referring to the kneaded flour mixture, and "nut," alluding to the small, rounded, nut-like shape of the fried treats. This etymological pairing captured the compact, ball-shaped form of the initial versions, which were essentially sweetened dough balls fried in fat, distinguishing them from larger fritters or cakes.
The earliest known printed reference to "doughnuts" appears in Washington Irving's satirical work A History of New York (1809), where he describes a Dutch colonial feast featuring "balls of sweetened dough, fried in hog's fat, the Dutch call them Doughnuts, or Oly Koeks." Irving's account, drawing on New York City's Dutch heritage, portrays these as nut-sized fried dough items enjoyed at gatherings, marking the term's debut in literature.
In Britain, a variant spelling "dow nuts" appears in a handwritten recipe book compiled around 1800 by Baroness Elizabeth Dimsdale of Hertfordshire, providing one of the earliest documented recipes for similar small fried dough items in an English context. By the early 19th century, American cookbooks began standardizing "doughnut" (or "dough nuts") for these fried preparations, as seen in Susannah Carter's The Frugal Housewife (1803 American edition), which includes a recipe for yeast-leavened dough shaped into rounds and fried in butter, yielding light, spongy balls. This usage solidified the term for both yeast-based and cake-style dough balls, reflecting their growing presence in household cooking.
These early references highlight "doughnut" as a descriptive name for modest, nut-resembling fried dough confections, predating the ring-shaped varieties that would later dominate.

Adoption and Popularization of "Donut"

In the 1920s, Russian immigrant Adolph Levitt, seeking to capitalize on the growing demand for doughnuts following World War I, invented the first automated doughnut-making machine and began marketing it under the simplified spelling "donut" to make the term easier for his largely immigrant workforce and customers to spell and pronounce. This innovation, dubbed the "Wonderful Almost Human Automatic Donut Machine," was displayed in a Times Square storefront in New York City, where it produced up to 120 doughnuts per hour, further embedding the abbreviated form in early commercial contexts.
Levitt founded the Doughnut Corporation of America (DCA) in the mid-1920s, which became the dominant force in U.S. doughnut production by the 1930s, controlling up to 80% of automated manufacturing through its machines and standardized mixes. The company actively standardized "donut" as its preferred spelling during this period, primarily for its brevity in signage, packaging, and advertising, which facilitated quicker and more cost-effective promotion amid the expansion of chain bakeries and coffee shops. The spelling "donut" had appeared earlier in print, with the earliest known use in 1870 by humorist Josh Billings and a notable instance in George W. Peck's 1900 novel Peck's Bad Boy and His Pa. By the late 1920s, amid this commercial push, it gained further visibility in mainstream American media, as in a Los Angeles Times article on August 10, 1929, where it appeared in a humorous critique of spelling trends.
The influence of DCA and subsequent brands solidified "donut" in American English by the mid-20th century, with its inclusion as a variant in major dictionaries like Merriam-Webster reflecting the commercial momentum behind the shift. While "doughnut" remains the traditional and preferred spelling in British English and formal U.S. style guides, "donut" gained widespread cultural acceptance in the United States for its phonetic simplicity and association with mass-produced treats.

Ingredients and Preparation

Core Ingredients and Dough Composition

The core ingredients in doughnut dough provide structure, flavor, tenderness, and lift, with variations depending on whether the doughnut is yeast-raised or cake-style. Wheat flour forms the base, offering structural integrity through gluten development during mixing and kneading; in yeast-raised doughnuts, gluten is encouraged for a chewy texture, while in cake doughnuts, mixing is minimized to limit gluten for a denser crumb. Sugar contributes sweetness, aids in browning through the Maillard reaction, and tenderizes the dough by interfering with gluten formation. Eggs act as binders, adding moisture and richness while helping to emulsify fats for a cohesive batter or dough. Milk or water provides hydration to form the dough, with milk adding flavor and tenderness through its proteins and fats. Fats such as butter or shortening enhance tenderness by coating flour proteins, reducing gluten strength, and imparting flavor.
Leavening agents distinguish the two primary doughnut types. Yeast-raised doughnuts employ active dry or instant yeast, which ferments sugars to produce carbon dioxide and alcohol, creating a light, airy structure during proofing; this biological process requires time for rising and results in a pillowy texture. In contrast, cake doughnuts rely on chemical leaveners like baking powder or baking soda, which react with liquids and acids to release carbon dioxide quickly, yielding a tender, cakelike crumb without extended rising.
Standard recipes maintain balanced ratios to achieve consistent results and proper hydration without a sticky or dry dough. Traditional formulations contain common allergens like gluten from wheat flour and dairy from milk or butter, but modern adaptations incorporate gluten-free flour blends (e.g., rice or almond-based) and dairy alternatives such as plant milks or vegan shortenings to accommodate dietary needs.

Frying, Baking, and Shaping Techniques

The frying process for doughnuts involves immersing the shaped dough in hot oil, typically maintained at 350375F (177190C), to achieve the characteristic golden-brown exterior through the Maillard reaction and internal puffing from steam generation. This temperature range ensures the dough sinks briefly before rising to the surface in about 5 seconds, allowing even cooking for 75120 seconds until the doughnuts float and develop a crisp crust without excessive oil absorption. Precise oil temperature control is essential; temperatures below 350F lead to sogginess and greasiness due to prolonged exposure and higher oil uptake, while exceeding 375F risks burning the exterior and undercooking the interior. After frying, doughnuts are drained on wire racks to remove excess oil, preventing a heavy texture.
Baking offers a lower-fat alternative to frying, particularly for cake-style doughnuts made from batters similar to those used in traditional recipes but adapted for oven use. These are prepared by filling greased donut molds and baking at 350400F (177204C) for 715 minutes, until the tops spring back when touched and a toothpick inserted comes out clean. This method reduces oil content significantly compared to deep-frying, yielding a lighter product while still achieving a tender crumb through leavening agents like baking powder.
Shaping techniques vary by dough type, with yeast-raised doughnuts requiring rolling the rested dough to about -inch thickness, then cutting uniform rings using a 34-inch diameter cutter and a smaller 1-inch cutter for the center hole to ensure even frying. Cut pieces are placed on floured surfaces and proofed for 4560 minutes in a warm environment until nearly doubled in size, allowing yeast fermentation to create air pockets for fluffiness. For filled varieties, a piping bag is used post-frying to inject creams or jams through a small slit, while cake doughnuts are often shaped by depositing batter directly into the fryer via a hopper for natural ring formation during cooking.

Types

Ring-Shaped Doughnuts

Ring-shaped doughnuts, also known as toroidal doughnuts, represent the archetypal form of this fried pastry, characterized by a circular shape with a central hole that enhances even cooking by increasing exposure to hot oil. This design originated from practical innovations in the 19th century, transforming solid dough balls into rings to prevent undercooked centers.
The creation of the ring shape is famously attributed to Hanson Crockett Gregory, a 16-year-old sailor from Camden, Maine, in 1847. While serving as a cook's assistant on a lime-trading schooner, Gregory reportedly punched out the centers of dough balls using a tin pepper box to ensure uniform frying, as the solid versions often remained raw in the middle despite crispy exteriors. This legend, passed down through family accounts and local histories, underscores the hole's role in promoting consistent heat distribution during deep-frying.
Yeast ring doughnuts achieve their signature light and airy texture through biological leavening, where active yeast ferments the dough, producing carbon dioxide that creates bubbles during proofing. The process involves mixing flour, yeast, sugar, milk, eggs, and butter into a soft dough, which is then rolled to about 1/2 inch thick, cut into rings using a doughnut cutter, and allowed to proof in a warm environment for 30-60 minutes until doubled in size before frying at 350-375F. This fermentation step results in a tender, fluffy crumb that contrasts with denser alternatives.
In contrast, cake ring doughnuts rely on chemical leaveners like baking powder or baking soda for rise, yielding a denser, more cake-like interior with a crisp, golden exterior formed during frying. The batter, typically combining flour, sugar, eggs, milk, and fat with the leavening agent, is mixed to a drop-batter consistency, piped or spooned into rings, and fried immediately without proofing, often at slightly lower temperatures around 325-350F to prevent over-browning. This method produces a subtle chewiness and robustness suited to bolder flavors.
Standard ring doughnuts measure 3 to 4 inches (7.6 to 10 cm) in outer diameter, with the central hole typically 1 to 1.5 inches (2.5 to 3.8 cm) wide, though commercial examples like Krispy Kreme's Original Glazed are precisely 8 cm across. Variations in dough thickness before fryingranging from 1/4 to 3/4 inchaffect the final chewiness, with thicker cuts yielding a more substantial bite after expansion during cooking.

Filled and Enclosed Varieties

Filled and enclosed doughnuts feature internal fillings that provide a burst of flavor within a sealed or injected exterior, distinguishing them from open-ring varieties. These types typically involve a yeast-based dough that is fried first, then filled post-frying to prevent the filling from leaking or causing the dough to burst during cooking. Injection techniques commonly employ pastry bags fitted with long, narrow tips or specialized machines that pierce the cooled doughnut and dispense filling evenly without compromising the structure.
Common fillings for these doughnuts include fruit jams such as raspberry or apple, which offer a tart contrast to the fried dough, as well as custards and creams that add richness. Bavarian cream, a stabilized custard made by folding whipped cream into a chilled pastry cream base gelatinized with sheets or powder, is particularly favored for its light, airy texture that holds shape inside the doughnut. This filling technique ensures the cream sets firmly after cooling, preventing it from oozing out when the doughnut is handled or eaten. Chocolate fondant fillings, consisting of a molten chocolate mixture or ganache-like chocolate, are also popular for their rich, flowing center upon biting.
Enclosed varieties often take the form of solid yeast balls rather than rings, fully encasing the filling for a uniform bite. Bomboloni, traditional Italian doughnuts originating from Tuscany, are round, yeast-risen spheres fried until golden, then filled with pastry cream, jam, or chocolate hazelnut spread and rolled in granulated sugar. Similarly, sufganiyot became popular as jelly-filled doughnuts for Jewish Hanukkah celebrations in 20th-century Israel, evolving from European jelly doughnuts like German Berliners, adapted with kosher fats; they are deep-fried in oil to symbolize the holiday's miracle of oil, and traditionally injected with strawberry or apricot jam after frying.
Chocolate fondant doughnuts can be replicated at home using recipes for baked or fried donuts filled or topped with chocolate fondant (a molten or ganache-like chocolate mixture). A popular healthier baked version uses oat and almond flour dough, topped with a fondant made from evaporated milk and dark chocolate. Other recipes involve yeast dough, frying, and filling with ganache for a molten center.
Achieving the proper balance of filling-to-dough ratio is essential in these varieties, typically limiting the filling to about one-third of the doughnut's volume to avoid pressure buildup that could cause leakage upon biting, while ensuring the interior remains moist without overwhelming the fried exterior. This careful proportioning, combined with post-frying injection, maintains structural integrity during consumption.

Alternative Shapes and Textures

Beyond the classic ring-shaped doughnuts, alternative forms include twisted varieties made by rolling yeast dough into ropes and braiding or twisting them before frying, resulting in a elongated, spiral structure that offers varied crunch along its length. Bar doughnuts, also known as long johns when unfilled, are formed by cutting the dough into long rectangular strips, typically about 4-5 inches in length, providing a denser, bar-like shape that contrasts with rounded forms. Miniature doughnut holes, produced from the excess dough trimmed during ring doughnut preparation or as standalone small spheres, are often sold separately in bulk for snacking, emphasizing their bite-sized convenience over full-sized pieces.
Old-fashioned doughnuts feature a cracked, irregular surface achieved through a denser cake batter fried at lower temperatures, providing a chewy contrast to smooth-glazed types.
In terms of textures, the cronut represents a hybrid innovation, combining the laminated, buttery layers of croissant doughachieved through repeated folding and rolling to create flaky stratawith the fried exterior of a doughnut, yielding a crisp shell and airy interior. Churros offer an extruded texture, where choux pastry dough is piped through a star-shaped nozzle to form ridged, stick-like tubes before frying, then coated for added crispness, distinguishing them from molded doughnut shapes.
Health-conscious alternatives achieve crunch without deep-frying by baking or air-frying the dough, which circulates hot air to mimic a fried crispness while reducing fat content by up to 70% compared to traditional methods, preserving a light yet textured bite. Novelty shapes like square "dough-squares," cut from rolled dough into uniform squares and fried, provide a geometric variation that simplifies production and yields even, pillow-like puffs upon rising.

Toppings and Flavors

Basic Glazes and Sprinkles

Basic glazes for doughnuts typically consist of a simple mixture of powdered sugar, milk, and vanilla extract, which forms a smooth, pourable coating that adds sweetness and a glossy finish. The powdered sugar provides the bulk of the sweetness and thickness, while the milk thins the mixture to a consistency suitable for coating, and vanilla extract imparts a subtle flavor without overpowering the doughnut's taste. This base recipe can be adjusted by varying the milk quantity to achieve the desired viscosity, ensuring the glaze adheres well to the warm doughnut surface for better absorption.
Application methods for basic glazes include dipping the warm doughnut directly into the mixture or drizzling it over the top to achieve even coverage. Dipping, often done using fingers, a slotted spoon, or chopsticks, allows the glaze to envelop the entire surface and set as it cools, creating a uniform sheen. Drizzling, suitable for thinner glazes, involves spooning or pouring the mixture from a height to let it cascade evenly, minimizing excess pooling and promoting a lighter coating. Applying the glaze while the doughnut is still warm enhances absorption and helps form a stable layer that integrates with the fried or baked exterior.
Sprinkles serve as a common addition to basic glazes, providing textural contrast through small, colorful or flavored particles pressed onto the wet surface before it sets. Chocolate jimmies, which are rod-shaped chocolate sprinkles, offer a rich, crunchy element that complements the glaze's sweetness. Rainbow jimmies, featuring multicolored sugar strands, add visual appeal and a subtle crunch without altering the flavor profile significantly. Chopped nuts, such as walnuts or pecans, can also be used as sprinkles for a nutty texture and flavor enhancement, though they are less common in basic preparations due to potential allergen concerns.
The application of basic glazes impacts doughnut shelf life by sealing in moisture from the interior, which extends freshness but can lead to stickiness over time due to moisture migration between the glaze and doughnut. This migration occurs as water activity differences cause the glaze to absorb ambient or internal humidity, resulting in a tacky surface that reduces appeal after 24-48 hours. Proper storage in low-humidity environments helps mitigate this effect, preserving the glaze's integrity for up to several days.

Nutella, the hazelnut-chocolate spread, became a popular filling in the early 2010s, injected into yeast doughnuts for a creamy, molten center that pairs the spread's nuttiness with fried dough's lightness, as seen in recipes from professional bakers emphasizing its viral appeal in urban bakeries. Chocolate fondant donuts represent a decadent filling innovation, featuring a molten or ganache-like pure chocolate mixture that creates a gooey, flowing center. These can be replicated at home using yeast dough that is fried and filled with chocolate ganache or dipped in chocolate glaze, or through healthier baked versions using oat and almond flour dough topped with a fondant made from evaporated milk and dark chocolate. Regional Variations

North American Styles

In North America, doughnut styles are heavily shaped by commercial chains and regional immigrant influences, transforming the treat into a mass-produced staple with widespread accessibility. Major chains like Krispy Kreme and Tim Hortons have defined everyday consumption, offering yeast-raised varieties that emphasize simplicity and flavor pairings suited to local tastes. These styles often prioritize frying techniques for a light, airy texture, with glazes and dips providing the primary appeal over elaborate fillings.
The Original Glazed doughnut from Krispy Kreme exemplifies a quintessential U.S. style, introduced in 1937 by founder Vernon Rudolph, who acquired a yeast-raised recipe from a French chef in New Orleans. This ring-shaped doughnut is fried to a golden crisp and immediately coated in a sweet glaze that melts upon serving, creating a signature "hot now" experience that has fueled the chain's expansion across the United States. Its commercial success underscores the appeal of minimalist, high-volume production in American doughnut culture.
In Canada, Tim Hortons has popularized the maple dip doughnut, a yeast-raised variety featuring a soft, pillowy texture dipped in a rich maple-flavored fondant, often evoking the country's syrup heritage. This style emerged as a menu staple in the chain's early years, blending traditional doughnut forms with distinctly Canadian sweetness to cater to drive-thru convenience and national pride.
Regional influences add diversity, particularly in the southwestern U.S., where Mexican-inspired churros have integrated into doughnut traditions through border proximity and cultural exchange. These elongated, fried dough pastries, rolled in cinnamon sugar, trace their adoption in areas like Tucson from Mexican street food vendors, offering a crispier alternative to round doughnuts in local bakeries. In New Orleans, beignets reflect Caribbean and French colonial ties, presenting as square, pillow-like fried dough squares generously dusted with powdered sugar, popularized by establishments like Caf du Monde since the 19th century. These square fritters, adapted from French recipes via Caribbean migration routes, emphasize a denser, chewier bite suited to the city's Creole cuisine.
A unique commercial tradition in California involves the use of pink boxes for doughnut packaging, originating in the 1980s among Cambodian immigrant-owned shops in Los Angeles. These affordable, bulk-sale boxeschosen for their low cost and eye-catching huebecame a symbol of the state's independent doughnut industry, enabling small businesses to compete with chains by offering dozens at budget prices.
The North American doughnut market demonstrates immense scale, with over 10 billion units consumed annually in the United States alone, driven by chain dominance and cultural embedding in daily routines. This volume highlights the region's role in globalizing industrialized doughnut production.

European Traditions

In Europe, doughnut-like pastries have deep roots in festive and seasonal traditions, often featuring yeast-leavened doughs fried to a golden crispness and enhanced with fillings or dustings. These treats vary by region, reflecting local ingredients and cultural practices, from Carnival celebrations in Central Europe to New Year's rituals in the Low Countries.
German Krapfen, also known as Berliner in some areas, are iconic filled pastries traditionally prepared during Fasching or Carnival season. Made from a sweet yeast dough, they are deep-fried whole without a central hole, then injected with apricot jam or custard, and generously dusted with powdered sugar for a contrasting sweet crunch. Historical recipes emphasize frying in lard to achieve a rich, tender interior, though modern versions often use vegetable oil for practicality. These pastries symbolize indulgence before Lent, with their round shape evoking completeness and abundance in Germanic folklore.
In France, beignets represent a versatile deep-fried pastry tradition, with varieties in various shapes, such as round or rectangular, dusted heavily with confectioners' sugar. Originating from medieval French techniques, traditional beignets use a light yeast or choux dough, fried until puffed and airy, then coated in sugar to highlight their delicate, pillow-like texture. They are commonly enjoyed during festivals like Mardi Gras, offering a uniform bite that melts on the tongue. While early recipes favored animal fats like lard for frying, contemporary preparations shift to neutral oils to maintain neutrality in flavor.
British dough rings embody a straightforward ring-shaped style, contrasting with the filled balls of neighboring traditions. These yeast-based doughnuts, fried to a crisp exterior and often glazed or sugared, gained popularity in the mid-20th century through influences from American servicemen during World War II, evolving from earlier twisted or ball forms into the familiar holed rings sold at seaside resorts and bakeries. Unlike denser continental varieties, British rings prioritize a light, airy crumb, typically fried in vegetable oil in modern times but historically in lard for enhanced flavor depth.
Dutch oliebollen, or "oil balls," offer a spherical alternative to ring shapes, consisting of batter enriched with raisins, currants, and diced apples, dropped into hot fat to form irregular, golden orbs. A staple of New Year's Eve celebrations since at least the 17th century, they are fried in oilearning their nameand dusted with powdered sugar, symbolizing prosperity and warding off evil spirits in folklore. This batter-based method, rather than rolled dough, yields a chewy interior with fruity bursts, distinguishing them from the structured rings of British tradition; lard appears in some regional variants for a subtler richness, but oil remains dominant for its high smoke point.
Eastern European pczki, particularly in Poland, are plush, yeast-raised doughnuts filled with wild rose petal jam, a hallmark of their delicate floral sweetness. Deep-fried to a burnished hue and topped with a shiny glaze or powdered sugar, they are traditionally consumed on Tusty Czwartek (Fat Thursday), the Thursday before Ash Wednesday, as a pre-Lenten feast to use up rich ingredients like eggs and butter. The rose jam filling, derived from native wild roses, provides a tart contrast to the dough's softness, with historical accounts noting their preparation in lard for optimal crispness, though vegetable oil is now prevalent in home and commercial baking to align with health preferences.
In Russia, " " (homemade ponchiki) or " " refer to homemade donuts that resemble American-style varieties, often prepared in domestic settings as everyday or nostalgic treats. These are made from yeast-leavened dough using simple ingredients such as flour, milk, sugar, eggs, and butter, and can be ring-shaped or filled, fried in oil or occasionally baked. They are commonly finished with glazes such as icing, chocolate, or dusting of powdered sugar, emphasizing accessibility and familial preparation in Russian home cooking.
Across these traditions, the choice of frying medium underscores a shift from lardvalued in pre-industrial recipes for its stability and flavorto vegetable oils in contemporary practice, allowing for cleaner tastes and easier availability while preserving the pastries' festive essence.

Asian Adaptations

In Asia, doughnut adaptations often blend local ingredients like rice flour, coconut, and tropical fruits with frying techniques influenced by street food traditions, resulting in both sweet and savory variants that differ from Western ring-shaped pastries. These treats are typically prepared fresh by vendors and incorporate regional staples such as glutinous rice or ghee to achieve unique textures, from chewy to flaky.
Japanese mochi doughnuts, known as pon de ring, feature a chewy texture derived from glutinous rice flour mixed with tapioca starch, forming connected ring shapes that are deep-fried and often glazed or filled with flavors like matcha or custard. Originating from Mister Donut in Japan in the early 2000s, they draw on Hawaiian poi mochi influences but emphasize the bouncy "mochi-mochi" consistency prized in Japanese sweets. These doughnuts have become a popular fusion item in urban bakeries, highlighting rice-based innovations over wheat dough.
In India, balushahi represents a festive, syrup-soaked adaptation akin to a doughnut, made from a dough of all-purpose flour, ghee, and yogurt, which is deep-fried to a flaky crispness before immersion in cardamom-infused sugar syrup. This North Indian sweet, also called badusha in the south, is denser and more brittle than airy doughnuts, with its layered texture achieved through careful kneading and low-heat frying to prevent oil absorption. Commonly prepared for holidays like Diwali, balushahi showcases ghee's role in creating a rich, melt-in-the-mouth quality that ties into traditional Indian confectionery.
Filipino shakoy, or bicho-bicho, is an elongated, twisted doughnut made from a yeast-leavened wheat dough that's stretched, braided, and deep-fried until golden and pillowy inside with a crisp exterior, then liberally coated in granulated sugar. This street snack, popular in the Visayas region, evolved from Chinese influences but uses local baking soda for extra fluffiness, making it lighter than solid crullers. Shakoy is often enjoyed as a quick breakfast or merienda, emphasizing its affordability and portability in Filipino vendor culture.
Chinese youtiao serves as a savory doughnut adaptation, consisting of long, golden-brown fried dough sticks made from a simple wheat flour, water, and baking powder dough that's proofed, stretched, and deep-fried in pairs to puff up airy and crisp. Typically unsweetened and paired with congee or soy milk for breakfast, youtiao dates back to the Song Dynasty and emphasizes its light, hollow interior achieved through high-heat frying. This staple underscores Asia's savory twists on fried dough, prioritizing dipping accompaniments over toppings.
In Indonesia, cakwe mirrors youtiao as long, fried dough sticks prepared from a flour-based batter with baking agents, deep-fried to a chewy yet crispy texture and often sliced for eating with chili sauce or porridge. Influenced by Chinese immigrants, cakwe is a ubiquitous savory snack in Indonesian cuisine, fried in large batches for its expanding, hollow form during cooking. It highlights the region's adaptation of doughnut frying for everyday meals rather than desserts.
Across Asia, these doughnut variants thrive in street vendor settings, where fresh frying on portable stoves caters to morning crowds and emphasizes quick, communal consumption in bustling markets from Manila to Jakarta. In Muslim-majority countries like Indonesia and Malaysia, halal variations are prevalent, with certifications ensuring no alcohol or pork-derived ingredients in doughs and glazes, as seen in chains like J.CO Donuts that adapt recipes for broader appeal. This focus on halal compliance integrates doughnuts into daily halal diets, boosting their role in urban street economies.

Variations in Other Regions

In South Africa, vetkoek, known as "fat cake" in Afrikaans, represents a fried dough hybrid influenced by Dutch colonial settlers in the 17th century, often filled with savory ingredients like minced meat, cheese, or curry for street food consumption. This deep-fried yeast dough, similar to a doughnut in shape but denser and typically unsweetened, reflects Afrikaner culinary traditions adapted through migration and local resource availability.
In Brazil, bolinho de chuva, or "rain drop doughnut," emerged from Portuguese colonial influences, featuring small, drop-fried batter balls coated in cinnamon and sugar, evoking a lighter beignet-style treat popular at home gatherings. The name derives from the rounded shape formed as batter falls into hot oil, and its simplicity ties to 19th-century immigrant adaptations in South American kitchens.
In the Middle East, Iranian zulbia is a grid-shaped fritter made from fermented batter of yogurt and starch, deep-fried and soaked in rosewater-saffron syrup, prominently featured during Nowruz celebrations as a symbol of renewal. This sweet, crispy confection traces to Persian culinary heritage, with its intricate patterns influenced by ancient frying techniques shared across regional migrations. In Morocco, sfinz (or sfenj) is an airy, yeast-leavened doughnut fried to a golden crisp, often flavored with anise and incorporating semolina for texture, served as street food during holidays like Mimouna. Its unsweetened base allows for sugar dusting or honey dipping, stemming from North African Jewish traditions blended with Andalusian influences via historical expulsions and trade routes.
In Oceania, Australian lamingtons, a non-fried sponge cake dipped in chocolate icing and desiccated coconut, serve as a doughnut-adjacent sweet from British colonial times in the late 19th century, named after Queensland Governor Lord Lamington and adapted for tea-time rituals. Modern fusions like lamington doughnuts combine this coating with fried rings, highlighting ongoing culinary crossovers. New Zealand features similar adaptations, such as cream-filled doughnuts introduced via American military presence during World War II, evolving local bakery staples through Pacific migration patterns.
These variations underscore broader influences of colonialism and migration, where European frying methods merged with indigenous ingredients and festivals in Africa, the Middle East, South America, and Oceania, creating hybrid fried or coated sweets distinct from North American expansions.

Cultural and Commercial Significance

Role in Popular Culture and Media

Doughnuts have become enduring symbols in American television, often representing indulgence and everyday comfort. In the long-running animated series The Simpsons, the character Homer Simpson's obsession with pink-frosted doughnuts with rainbow sprinkles exemplifies this trope, portraying them as an irresistible vice that drives much of his comedic behavior. A notable instance occurs in the Treehouse of Horror episode "Treehouse of Horror IV," where Homer trades his soul to the devil for one such doughnut, highlighting its role as a metaphor for gluttonous temptation. Similarly, the 1982 parody series Police Squad! reinforces the longstanding stereotype of police officers paired with doughnuts and coffee, using it for absurd humor in scenes where detectives like Frank Drebin navigate crime while indulging in the treat, satirizing the image of cops as donut enthusiasts during night shifts.
In literature, doughnuts appear as markers of transient American diner culture, evoking the restlessness of post-war life. Jack Kerouac's seminal novel On the Road (1957) captures this through scenes of roadside stops where characters consume doughnuts alongside coffee, symbolizing quick, unpretentious sustenance amid endless travel and existential searching. These moments, such as the protagonists buying fresh doughnuts in a San Francisco bakery after a night out, underscore the doughnut's place in Beat Generation narratives of freedom and cultural rebellion against 1950s conformity.
Doughnuts have also featured prominently in advertising, amplifying their cultural footprint. Dunkin' Donuts, founded in 1950, launched its first major campaigns in the 1950s to promote fresh, affordable treats, evolving into iconic 1980s slogans like "Time to Make the Donuts," which personified the brand's dedication through the character Fred the Baker. Collaborations with media properties, such as the 2025 Dunkin' Netherlands promotion tying into The Simpsons broadcast on Star Channel, have offered character-themed doughnuts like Homer-inspired varieties, blending nostalgia with modern marketing to engage fans.
Symbolically, doughnuts in media oscillate between emblems of gluttony and nostalgic comfort, particularly in wartime contexts. They often represent excess and moral lapse, as in portrayals of overindulgence critiquing consumer culture, yet they also evoke fond memories of resilience, such as the "doughnut lassies" of World War I who fried treats for soldiers, inspiring stories of morale-boosting simplicity amid hardship. This duality positions the doughnut as a versatile icon in films, TV, and literature, balancing critique of indulgence with celebrations of communal joy.

Industry Production and Economic Impact

The doughnut industry relies on advanced global manufacturing processes to meet consumer demand, with automated production lines enabling high-volume output in facilities operated by major chains and independent producers. Key technologies include continuous sheeting and extrusion systems that form dough into rings or filled shapes, followed by proofing, frying, and glazing stages, often integrated into seamless assembly lines. For instance, modern automated lines can produce up to 120 doughnuts per minute, allowing for efficient scaling in large-scale operations.
Prominent companies dominate the sector, including Krispy Kreme, which reported annual revenue of approximately $1.7 billion in 2023 (with last twelve months revenue at $1.53 billion as of Q3 2025), and Dunkin', a subsidiary of Inspire Brands since its $11.3 billion acquisition in 2020, generating systemwide sales of about $11.3 billion in the same year. These players, alongside regional bakeries, drive innovation in production capacity, with Krispy Kreme emphasizing fresh, hot doughnuts through proprietary glazing tunnels and Dunkin' focusing on diverse menu integration in quick-service formats. The supply chain for doughnuts centers on sourcing core ingredients like vegetable oils for fryingoften palm oil or shorteningsand flavor compounds such as vanilla extracts and fruit purees, which are procured from global agricultural suppliers to ensure consistency and cost control.
Economically, the U.S. doughnut market was valued at approximately $7.4 billion in 2023, contributing to broader growth in the global sector valued at $13.7 billion that year (with the global market at $11.22 billion in 2024 and projected to reach $15.12 billion by 2032). This activity supports significant job creation within the baking sector, where doughnut production and retail employ thousands, including roles in manufacturing, distribution, and storefront operations; for example, the U.S. baking industry as a whole sustains over 635,000 jobs through induced economic impacts.
Sustainability challenges persist, particularly around palm oil sourcing, which accounts for a substantial portion of frying fats but has been linked to deforestation in production regions, prompting commitments from companies like Dunkin' to use certified sustainable palm oil. Additionally, waste from unsold goods poses environmental concerns, with efforts underway to repurpose excess dough and toppings through donation programs and biodegradable packaging to minimize landfill contributions.

Holidays, Festivals, and Traditions

In the United States, National Doughnut Day is observed annually on the first Friday of June to honor the Salvation Army's "Doughnut Lassies," women who provided doughnuts to soldiers during World War I as a morale booster in service huts near the front lines. The day originated in 1938 in Chicago as a fundraiser during the Great Depression, initiated by the Salvation Army to support those in need through doughnut sales, a tradition that continues today with participating bakeries often offering free or discounted doughnuts to raise awareness and funds.
During the Jewish holiday of Hanukkah, sufganiyotround, deep-fried jelly doughnuts dusted with powdered sugarhold a prominent place in celebrations, symbolizing the miracle of oil lasting eight days in the Hanukkah story. This tradition gained widespread popularity in Israel during the 1920s, when the Histadrut labor federation promoted sufganiyot as an official Hanukkah treat to provide employment for Jewish workers during the holiday season, leading to their global adoption in Jewish communities.
In Poland, Fat Thursday (Tusty Czwartek), observed on the Thursday before Ash Wednesday, features the indulgent consumption of pczki, traditional yeast doughnuts filled with plum jam or custard and topped with icing or powdered sugar, as a final feast before the Lenten fast. This custom dates back to medieval times, rooted in pre-Christian Slavic rituals of abundance, and remains a national event where Poles collectively devour millions of pczki, emphasizing communal feasting and the avoidance of bad luck if one is not eaten.
Balushahi, also known as badusha, is a cherished doughnut-like sweet in Indian culture, consisting of deep-fried flour balls soaked in cardamom-scented sugar syrup, commonly prepared during Diwali to symbolize prosperity and shared joy in family gatherings. In Iran, zulbiaa crispy, net-like fritter made from fermented batter and drenched in rosewater-saffron syrupserves as a festive treat during Nowruz, the Persian New Year, representing renewal and sweetness in the springtime celebrations that include the symbolic Haft-Seen table.
Doughnut-themed festivals and competitions worldwide highlight competitive eating and culinary creativity, such as the annual America's Greatest Donuts contest, where fan votes determine top doughnut varieties from U.S. bakeries, fostering regional pride and innovation. International events include Major League Eating's World Donut Hole Eating Championship in Indiana, where competitors consume hundreds of mini doughnuts in timed challenges, and similar fairs in Europe and Asia that incorporate local doughnut variants into eating contests during cultural festivals.
