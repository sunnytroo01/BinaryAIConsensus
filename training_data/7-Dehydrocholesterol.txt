7-Dehydrocholesterol
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 7-Dehydrocholesterol (7-DHC), with the molecular formula CHO and systematic name (3S,9S,10R,13R,14R,17R)-10,13-dimethyl-17--2,3,4,9,11,12,14,15,16,17-decahydro-1H-cyclopentaphenanthren-3-ol, is a zoosterol and cholesterol derivative that functions as a key intermediate in sterol lipid metabolism. It serves as the immediate precursor to cholesterol via the Kandutsch-Russell pathway and as provitamin D, undergoing photochemical conversion to vitamin D (cholecalciferol) in the skin upon exposure to ultraviolet B (UVB) radiation in the 290320 nm range. This dual role positions 7-DHC at the intersection of cholesterol homeostasis and vitamin D synthesis, with the highest concentrations of 7-DHC found in the epidermis where it supports cutaneous vitamin D production.
In biosynthesis, 7-DHC is produced primarily in the endoplasmic reticulum of keratinocytes and other cells through the de novo cholesterol pathway, starting from acetyl-CoA and involving the enzyme sterol -desaturase (SC5DL), which introduces a double bond at the C5C6 position of lathosterol to yield the characteristic 5,7-diene structure. The enzyme 7-dehydrocholesterol reductase (DHCR7) then reduces the double bond to form cholesterol, completing the terminal step in this pathway; dysregulation of DHCR7 activity, such as through cholesterol-mediated proteasomal degradation, can shift the balance toward increased 7-DHC accumulation and enhanced vitamin D precursor availability. 7-DHC is present in various tissues including the adrenal gland, brain, liver, and testis, as well as in mammalian milk, and its levels are tightly regulated to maintain sterol balance.
Medically, elevated 7-DHC levels are a hallmark of SmithLemliOpitz syndrome (SLOS), an autosomal recessive disorder caused by mutations in the DHCR7 gene, leading to impaired cholesterol synthesis, 7-DHC buildup, and multisystem developmental abnormalities with an incidence of 1 in 10,00070,000 births. In SLOS, the deficiency reduces cholesterol availability critical for cell membrane integrity and signaling, while excess 7-DHC may contribute to toxicity, though therapeutic strategies like statins can partially restore DHCR7 function. Emerging research highlights 7-DHC's protective roles beyond metabolism, including as an endogenous suppressor of ferroptosisan iron-dependent form of cell death driven by lipid peroxidationdue to its conjugated diene structure enabling rapid scavenging of peroxyl radicals in phospholipids, thereby conferring resistance in contexts like cancer cells and ischemia-reperfusion injury.

Chemical Properties

Molecular Structure

7-Dehydrocholesterol is a sterol molecule characterized by the chemical formula C27H44OC_{27}H_{44}OC27H44O and a molecular weight of 384.64 g/mol. Its systematic IUPAC name is cholesta-5,7-dien-3-ol, reflecting its unsaturated structure within the cholesterol family.
At its core, 7-dehydrocholesterol features a tetracyclic ring system based on the cholestane backbone, consisting of three six-membered rings (A, B, and C) fused to a five-membered ring (D). A hydroxyl group is attached to carbon 3 in ring A with -stereochemistry, providing polarity to the molecule. Ring B contains two double bonds: one between carbons 5 and 6 (5) and another between carbons 7 and 8 (7), which contribute to its conjugated diene system and UV absorption properties. Additionally, an eight-carbon isooctyl side chain, featuring a branched methyl group, is attached at carbon 17 of ring D, completing the sterol's hydrophobic tail. The stereochemistry at key chiral centers, including C3, C10, C13, C14, C17, and C20, follows the typical configuration of natural sterols, ensuring proper integration into biological membranes.
The stereospecific SMILES notation for 7-dehydrocholesterol is C(CCCC(C)C)1CC21(CC32CC=C43(CC(C4)O)C)C, which encodes the three-dimensional arrangement of atoms. In comparison to cholesterol, which has a single double bond at 5, 7-dehydrocholesterol includes an additional double bond at 7, making it a more unsaturated precursor in sterol biosynthesis.

Physical and Chemical Properties

7-Dehydrocholesterol appears as a white to off-white crystalline powder.
It has a melting point of 148-152 C.
The compound is soluble in ethanol, chloroform, and ethyl acetate, slightly soluble in DMSO, and insoluble in water.
7-Dehydrocholesterol is sensitive to light and air oxidation owing to its conjugated diene system, though it remains stable under inert conditions.
Its spectroscopic properties include UV absorption maxima at 271, 281, and 293 nm, attributable to the 5,7-diene moiety, and characteristic NMR peaks for the ring protons and the side chain.
In terms of reactivity, 7-dehydrocholesterol undergoes photoisomerization upon exposure to UVB light, involving electrocyclic ring opening of the conjugated diene, and serves as a substrate for reductases such as 3-hydroxysterol-7-reductase.

Biosynthesis and Metabolism

Biosynthetic Pathway

7-Dehydrocholesterol is produced as an intermediate in the cholesterol biosynthetic pathway, specifically within the Kandutsch-Russell branch following the formation of lanosterol from squalene 2,3-epoxide in the mevalonate pathway. The mevalonate pathway begins with the condensation of three acetyl-CoA molecules to form HMG-CoA, which is reduced to mevalonate by HMG-CoA reductase, the rate-limiting enzyme; subsequent steps involve phosphorylation, decarboxylation, and isomerization to produce isopentenyl pyrophosphate (IPP), which dimerizes with dimethylallyl pyrophosphate (DMAPP) to form geranyl pyrophosphate, extending to farnesyl pyrophosphate and ultimately squalene via squalene synthase. Squalene is then epoxidized by squalene monooxygenase to squalene 2,3-epoxide, which cyclizes to lanosterol under the action of lanosterol synthase, marking the entry into the post-squalene sterol synthesis phase shared by both the Kandutsch-Russell and Bloch pathways.
The Kandutsch-Russell pathway diverges from the Bloch pathway immediately after lanosterol formation, prioritizing early reduction of the 24 double bond in the sterol side chain rather than later in the sequence. Key intermediates in this branch include 24,25-dihydrolanosterol, formed by reduction of lanosterol's side chain double bond; this is followed by sequential demethylations at C14 and C4, reduction of the 14 double bond, leading to zymosterol (cholesta-8-en-3-ol). Zymosterol then undergoes isomerization of the double bond from the 8 to the 7 position, catalyzed by 8-7 sterol isomerase (EBP), to form lathosterol (cholest-7-en-3-ol), which is converted to 7-dehydrocholesterol (cholesta-5,7-dien-3-ol) by the introduction of a 5 double bond. This sequence was first delineated by Kandutsch and Russell in their 1960 study on sterol metabolism in preputial gland tumors.
The pathway's flux is regulated primarily upstream through feedback inhibition of HMG-CoA reductase by cholesterol, which binds to Insig proteins to promote sterol regulatory element-binding protein (SREBP) retention in the endoplasmic reticulum, thereby limiting mevalonate production and overall sterol synthesis. Isotope labeling studies reveal tissue-specific variations in Kandutsch-Russell pathway usage, with contributions from multiple routes rather than exclusive reliance on this branch, influencing the relative abundance of intermediates like 7-dehydrocholesterol in different organs. A simplified overview from squalene to 7-dehydrocholesterol involves cyclization to lanosterol, side chain reduction, demethylations, double bond adjustments, and culminates in the 5,7-diene structure of 7-dehydrocholesterol, without the final reduction to cholesterol.

Key Enzymes

The biosynthesis and metabolism of 7-dehydrocholesterol involve several key enzymes in the cholesterol pathway, particularly within the Kandutsch-Russell branch. One upstream enzyme is 24-sterol reductase (DHCR24), which catalyzes the reduction of the 24 double bond in early sterol precursors such as lanosterol, forming 24,25-dihydrolanosterol, a key step en route to lathosterol and 7-dehydrocholesterol. This step is essential for progressing through the post-squalene phases of sterol synthesis.
The conversion of lathosterol to 7-dehydrocholesterol is mediated by lathosterol 5-desaturase (SC5D, also known as sterol-C5-desaturase-like protein, encoded by SC5DL), which introduces a 5 double bond to the sterol ring structure, though sometimes referred to in pathway contexts involving 7 configurations. This enzymatic action yields 7-dehydrocholesterol as the immediate precursor to cholesterol.
The terminal enzyme in this sequence is 7-dehydrocholesterol reductase (DHCR7, EC 1.3.1.21), a microsomal membrane protein that reduces the 7 double bond in 7-dehydrocholesterol to produce cholesterol, utilizing NADPH as a cofactor. DHCR7 expression is regulated by the sterol regulatory element-binding protein-2 (SREBP-2) transcription factor, which responds to cellular sterol levels to coordinate cholesterol homeostasis. Additionally, vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) can rapidly suppress DHCR7 activity, potentially forming a feedback loop to balance vitamin D precursor availability and cholesterol synthesis.
The human DHCR7 gene is located on chromosome 11q13 and spans multiple exons, with mutations in this gene leading to the accumulation of 7-dehydrocholesterol due to impaired reductase function. More than 160 distinct mutations have been identified as of 2018, underscoring its critical role in sterol metabolism.

Occurrence and Distribution

In Human Physiology

7-Dehydrocholesterol is primarily located in the epidermis, particularly in the stratum spinosum and stratum basale layers of the skin, where it is a significant provitamin D precursor. These layers contain the highest concentrations of the molecule among skin compartments.
It is also present in other sites, including the liver, a major site of cholesterol synthesis where 7-dehydrocholesterol serves as an intermediate, serum at low levels, the brain, adrenal glands, and intestinal mucosa.
Typical concentrations include approximately 0.2-0.3 mg/g tissue in skin and 0.05-0.2 g/mL in plasma of adults. These levels vary with age, diet, and ultraviolet exposure.
In circulation, 7-dehydrocholesterol is transported bound to lipoproteins, analogous to cholesterol.
Homeostasis of 7-dehydrocholesterol is regulated by the balance between its synthesis and enzymatic conversion to cholesterol, with skin levels declining with advancing age.

In Other Organisms

7-Dehydrocholesterol is ubiquitous in vertebrates, where it serves as a key precursor for vitamin D3 synthesis upon exposure to ultraviolet B radiation. In fish, such as Atlantic salmon and rainbow trout, it is particularly abundant in the skin, facilitating efficient conversion to vitamin D3 for UV protection and metabolic needs. Similarly, birds and reptiles localize 7-dehydrocholesterol in the skin, enabling photochemical production of vitamin D3, with additional provitamin forms like 24-dehydroprovitamin D3 detected in their epidermal tissues. In contrast, 7-dehydrocholesterol is absent or present at low levels in invertebrates, where it may play a minor role in sterol metabolism but not in vitamin D synthesis.
In fungi and plants, 7-dehydrocholesterol is absent, with ergosterol instead functioning as the analogous provitamin D2 precursor that converts to vitamin D2 under UVB exposure. Ergosterol predominates in fungal cell membranes, and while trace amounts may appear in plants due to fungal contamination, plants generally lack 7-dehydrocholesterol and rely on diverse phytosterols.
Through microbial engineering, 7-dehydrocholesterol has been produced in the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae by overexpressing enzymes in the mevalonate (MVA) pathway and introducing heterologous genes like DHCR24 to redirect post-squalene flux, achieving yields up to 109 mg/L in initial shake-flask cultures.
Evolutionarily, 7-dehydrocholesterol biosynthesis emerged with chordates to support cholesterol and vitamin D requirements, as evidenced by the presence of the pathway in amphibians like Xenopus. Homologs of the enzyme 7-dehydrocholesterol reductase (DHCR7) are highly conserved across mammals, underscoring its essential role in sterol homeostasis.
Comparative studies reveal higher 7-dehydrocholesterol levels in rodent skin relative to humans, with normal mouse skin showing ratios of 7-dehydrocholesterol to cholesterol around sevenfold higher in hair and epidermal samples. This elevated baseline facilitates the use of rodent models, particularly DHCR7-knockout mice, in research on Smith-Lemli-Opitz syndrome (SLOS), where accumulated 7-dehydrocholesterol mimics human disease phenotypes for studying sterol-related developmental defects.

Role in Vitamin D Production

Photochemical Conversion

The photochemical conversion of 7-dehydrocholesterol (7-DHC) to vitamin D3 precursors occurs primarily upon exposure to ultraviolet B (UVB) radiation in the wavelength range of 290-320 nm. This process involves the absorption of UVB photons by 7-DHC, a provitamin D3 sterol present in the skin, leading to an electrocyclic ring-opening reaction that cleaves the B-ring between carbons C9 and C10, forming previtamin D3 as the immediate photoproduct. The reaction is a conrotatory 6 electrocyclic process, consistent with the Woodward-Hoffmann rules for photochemical pericyclic reactions.
The conversion proceeds in two main steps: first, the photoisomerization of 7-DHC to previtamin D3, followed by a non-enzymatic thermal isomerization of previtamin D3 to cholecalciferol (vitamin D3) at physiological body temperature (around 37C). This thermal step occurs spontaneously over time, with the half-life of previtamin D3 being approximately 2-3 days in vitro but faster in vivo due to skin conditions. The efficiency of this photochemical process shows optimal performance at wavelengths of 295-300 nm, where absorption by 7-DHC is maximal. However, prolonged or excessive UVB exposure beyond the point of maximal previtamin D3 formation (typically 10-20% of total 7-DHC) leads to further photoproducts, including the biologically inert tachysterol and lumisterol, which do not contribute to vitamin D3 production and instead represent a regulatory mechanism to prevent overproduction.
Several factors influence the efficiency of photoconversion in human skin. Increased skin pigmentation, due to higher melanin content, absorbs and scatters UVB radiation, reducing its penetration and thereby inhibiting the conversion of 7-DHC to previtamin D3 by a factor of approximately 1.3-1.4 compared to lightly pigmented skin. Additionally, UVB penetration is limited to the superficial layers of the epidermis, approximately 0.02-0.1 mm deep, where 7-DHC is concentrated, ensuring that the reaction occurs primarily in the viable epidermal layers without deeper dermal involvement.

Physiological Importance

7-Dehydrocholesterol serves as the primary provitamin for endogenous vitamin D3 synthesis in the skin, where ultraviolet B radiation converts it to previtamin D3, which thermally isomerizes to vitamin D3, thereby supporting calcium absorption and preventing conditions like rickets through maintenance of mineral homeostasis. This process is crucial for regulating serum calcium and phosphate levels, ensuring proper bone mineralization and neuromuscular function.
As a sterol intermediate, 7-dehydrocholesterol incorporates into cell membranes, particularly in the epidermis where historical estimates suggest it constitutes a small percentage of total sterols (early studies reported approximately 4%, though based on methods now considered limited), contributing to membrane fluidity and the formation of lipid rafts due to its unsaturated structure. This integration modulates membrane properties such as electric potential and curvature, facilitating signaling processes like Ret receptor activation in cholesterol-limited conditions.
7-Dehydrocholesterol acts as a regulatory switch in sterol metabolism, balancing cholesterol production and vitamin D synthesis; its accumulation, mediated by the enzyme 7-dehydrocholesterol reductase (DHCR7), signals pathway flux and promotes diversion toward vitamin D when cholesterol levels are sufficient, as cholesterol induces DHCR7 degradation to favor provitamin accumulation. This mechanism maintains cellular sterol homeostasis in keratinocytes, preventing excessive cholesterol buildup while enabling adaptive vitamin D responses.
Adequate 7-dehydrocholesterol levels support immune function and bone health indirectly through sustained vitamin D3 production, which enhances antimicrobial peptide expression in immune cells and promotes osteoblast activity for skeletal integrity. Low synthesis capacity from insufficient 7-dehydrocholesterol can thus impair these outcomes by limiting vitamin D availability.
Evolutionarily, the presence of 7-dehydrocholesterol in the skin represents an adaptation in terrestrial vertebrates, enabling efficient solar-dependent vitamin D3 production to meet calcium demands in environments lacking dietary sources, with increased epidermal concentrations enhancing photoconversion efficiency over ancestral forms.

Clinical Significance

Associated Disorders

Smith-Lemli-Opitz syndrome (SLOS) is an autosomal recessive disorder primarily associated with dysregulation of 7-dehydrocholesterol (7-DHC), resulting from mutations in the DHCR7 gene that encodes 7-dehydrocholesterol reductase. This enzymatic deficiency impairs the conversion of 7-DHC to cholesterol, leading to severe cholesterol deficiency (often less than 50% of normal levels) and massive accumulation of 7-DHC, which can constitute up to 90% of total sterols in affected individuals. Clinical manifestations include congenital anomalies such as microcephaly, dysmorphic facial features (e.g., ptosis, micrognathia), syndactyly of toes 2 and 3, developmental delays, intellectual disability, and failure to thrive. The syndrome's incidence is estimated at 1 in 20,000 to 60,000 live births, with higher rates in populations of European descent.
Severe variants of SLOS can present with holoprosencephaly, a brain malformation where the forebrain fails to divide into two hemispheres, occurring in approximately 5-6% of cases and often linked to profound 7-DHC elevation. Additionally, sterol imbalances in SLOS are associated with a high prevalence of autism spectrum disorders, with studies reporting near-universal autistic features in verbal children with the syndrome, potentially due to disrupted cholesterol-dependent neurodevelopmental signaling.
The accumulation of 7-DHC in SLOS promotes oxidative stress through the formation of toxic oxysterol metabolites, contributing to neurotoxicity and embryotoxicity observed in affected individuals. In animal models, such as Dhcr7-deficient mice, elevated 7-DHC levels induce premature neurogenesis, perturbed neuronal migration, and developmental defects, highlighting the role of these metabolites in pathogenesis.
Diagnosis of SLOS relies on detecting markedly elevated plasma 7-DHC levels, often exceeding 90% of total sterols, typically confirmed via gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS). The syndrome was first described in 1964 by Smith, Lemli, and Opitz based on clinical observations in affected children, with the underlying biochemical defect in cholesterol biosynthesis identified in 1994 and the causative DHCR7 gene pinpointed in 1998.

Therapeutic and Diagnostic Applications

7-Dehydrocholesterol (7-DHC) serves as a key biomarker for diagnosing Smith-Lemli-Opitz syndrome (SLOS), an autosomal recessive disorder caused by mutations in the DHCR7 gene, through elevated levels detected via sterol profiling in plasma or other tissues. Sterol profiling typically employs liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) or gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS) to quantify 7-DHC and its ratio to cholesterol, providing high sensitivity and specificity for SLOS confirmation. Prenatal diagnosis is feasible via amniocentesis, where measurement of the 7-DHC/cholesterol ratio in amniotic fluidabnormal if exceeding 3.1 when multiplied by 1000allows early detection in at-risk pregnancies.
Therapeutic strategies for SLOS primarily target pathway modulation rather than direct 7-DHC intervention, focusing on alleviating its toxic accumulation and cholesterol deficiency. Cholesterol supplementation, often at doses of 100-150 mg/kg/day, elevates plasma cholesterol levels and reduces 7-DHC concentrations in most patients, though clinical benefits vary. Simvastatin, a statin inhibitor of upstream HMG-CoA reductase, has shown efficacy in clinical trials by decreasing 7-DHC and 8-dehydrocholesterol levels while improving the dehydrocholesterol/total sterol ratio and behavioral symptoms like irritability, without significant adverse effects. Experimental DHCR7 gene therapy in SLOS mouse models restores enzyme function, normalizes sterol homeostasis in the central nervous system, and improves biochemical profiles, suggesting potential for future human applications.
Emerging evidence as of 2025 supports the use of cholic acid (CA) supplementation (10-15 mg/kg/day) in combination with cholesterol therapy. In a case series of eight SLOS patients treated for up to 15 months, CA improved cholesterol levels in seven patients, promoted growth toward age-appropriate percentiles, and led to positive behavioral changes including enhanced social interaction and communication, with good tolerability and minimal adverse events. Larger longitudinal studies are needed to confirm these benefits.
In the context of vitamin D metabolism, 7-DHC levels are monitored in deficiency states, particularly in SLOS patients prone to impaired synthesis, to assess precursor availability. Oral 7-DHC administration in murine trials dose-dependently increases non-hydroxylated vitamin D3 in the liver and kidney, but fails to elevate circulating 25-hydroxyvitamin D3, limiting its therapeutic utility due to chemical instability and poor bioavailability.
Beyond clinical use, 7-DHC finds applications in research, including metabolic engineering of Saccharomyces cerevisiae to produce vitamin D3 precursors via optimized biosynthetic pathways, enabling scalable industrial synthesis. Animal models, such as Dhcr7-knockout mice, utilize elevated 7-DHC to study neurodevelopmental deficits in SLOS, revealing mechanisms like premature neurogenesis and oxysterol toxicity.
Current challenges include the absence of direct 7-DHC-targeted therapeutics, with management relying on indirect modulation of the cholesterol synthesis pathway to mitigate accumulation and downstream effects.
