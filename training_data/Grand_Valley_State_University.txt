 Grand Valley State University (GVSU) is a public research university in Allendale, Michigan, with additional campuses in Grand Rapids and regional sites across the state, offering over 350 undergraduate and graduate programs to approximately 22,011 students. Founded in 1960 as Grand Valley State College through Public Act 120 and opening its doors in 1963 with 226 freshmen on a 876-acre campus in rural Allendale, the institution has evolved into a comprehensive university emphasizing hands-on learning, professional programs in fields like business, engineering, and health sciences, and a student-faculty ratio of 15:1.
The university's name underwent changes reflecting its growth: redesignated as Grand Valley State Colleges in 1973 to encompass multiple units, reverting to Grand Valley State College in 1983, and elevated to university status in 1987 by Governor James Blanchard amid expansions including the Grand Rapids campus. Enrollment expanded rapidly, reaching 7,142 by 1979 and 10,914 by 1989, driven by new facilities like the Kirkhof Center (1974) and the Eberhard Center (1988), as well as graduate programs in social work (1980) and nursing (1983). Today, GVSU serves 19,002 undergraduates and 3,009 graduates, including 625 international students, with 96% receiving financial aid and an alumni network of 148,109, predominantly in Michigan.
Academically, GVSU is recognized for affordability and social mobility, earning spots on Washington Monthly's "Best Bang for the Buck" list and The Princeton Review's Green Colleges for 15 years, while featuring 27 LEED-certified buildings. In the 2025-2026 U.S. News & World Report rankings, it placed #242 among National Universities and #136 (tie) among public institutions. Athletically, the Lakers compete in NCAA Division II as part of the Great Lakes Intercollegiate Athletic Conference, founded in 1972, and the university enriches the region through public service, equity initiatives, and partnerships like the Meijer Public Broadcast Center.

History

Founding and early years

In 1958, the Michigan Legislature commissioned a study led by Dr. John Dale Russell to assess higher education needs in western Michigan, which revealed significant population growth and a lack of local four-year college options, recommending the creation of a new state-supported institution to serve the region. This effort was spearheaded by L. William Seidman, who formed the Committee to Establish a Four-Year College (CEFYC) to advocate for legislative support. On April 26, 1960, Governor G. Mennen Williams signed Public Act 120 (also known as House Bill 183), establishing Grand Valley State College as Michigan's tenth state-supported four-year institution, with Seidman chairing the newly formed Board of Control. In April 1961, the Board selected an 876-acre site in Allendale Township, bordered by the Grand River, after extensive evaluation by a planning committee led by Seidman.
Construction began with groundbreaking on August 28, 1962, for the college's initial academic buildings, including those in the Great Lakes Group such as Lake Michigan Hall. The first classes commenced on September 26, 1963, enrolling 226 freshmen students and approximately 15 faculty members, all housed in temporary facilities while permanent structures were completed. Enrollment grew rapidly in the ensuing years, reaching 530 students by fall 1965 and 2,718 by fall 1970, reflecting the institution's appeal amid regional demand for accessible higher education. The college held its first commencement on June 18, 1967, awarding bachelor's degrees to 136 graduates.
A notable early event occurred in 1969, when the administration attempted to censor an issue of the student newspaper The Lanthorn featuring controversial cartoons, sparking a free speech dispute; the matter was resolved in favor of the newspaper's First Amendment protections, affirming student press rights on campus. That same year, construction on the Zumberge Librarynamed for the college's first president, James H. Zumbergebegan, with the facility opening in spring 1969 to support the expanding academic programs. In 1973, the institution restructured into a cluster college model and changed its name to Grand Valley State Colleges to reflect this organization, though it reverted to Grand Valley State College in 1983.

Expansion to university status

In 1973, Grand Valley State College reorganized into a cluster college model comprising multiple specialized units, prompting a name change to Grand Valley State Colleges to better reflect this multi-campus structure. This shift aimed to foster diverse academic environments while maintaining a unified institution. However, by 1983, amid administrative restructuring and a return to a more centralized model, the name reverted to Grand Valley State College, eliminating the plural form.
The push toward university status accelerated in the mid-1980s, culminating in 1987 when the Michigan Legislature passed a bill renaming the institution Grand Valley State University, signed into law by Governor James Blanchard. This legislative action granted comprehensive university designation, enabling expanded degree offerings and research initiatives beyond traditional college boundaries. The change symbolized the institution's maturation into a full-fledged public university serving West Michigan's higher education needs.
Program diversification marked this era, with the introduction of graduate-level offerings in the 1980s to address regional demands for advanced education. Notable additions included the Master of Social Work program in 1980, followed by a graduate nursing program in 1983 and accreditation for the physical therapy graduate program in 1985. These developments, alongside the 1989 establishment of the School of Criminal Justice, broadened the academic portfolio and attracted a more diverse student body.
To extend reach beyond the Allendale campus, Grand Valley initiated satellite operations in the late 1980s, beginning with the Grand Rapids Center. Planning began in 1984 with state funding for architectural designs in downtown Grand Rapids, leading to groundbreaking in 1986 and dedication of the L. V. Eberhard Center in 1988. This facility provided off-site classes and community engagement, laying groundwork for further regional presence.
Enrollment growth underscored the university's expanding influence, surpassing 15,000 students by the 19971998 academic year with a fall headcount of 15,676. This milestone reflected heightened accessibility and program appeal, up from approximately 11,000 in the early 1990s.
Infrastructure enhancements in Allendale supported this expansion, with key constructions including the 1982 dedication of a new fieldhouse after the original's collapse and the 1989 opening of Robert Kleiner Commons. The 1990s saw further builds, such as the 1991 Cook-DeVos Center for campus ministry and the 1996 Seymour and Esther Padnos Hall of Science, bolstering research and instructional capacity. These facilities exemplified the university's commitment to modernizing its physical plant during the transition to full university operations.

Modern developments and growth

In 2010, Grand Valley State University marked its 50th anniversary with a series of events spanning 2010-2011, including receptions, parades, exhibitions, musical performances, poetry readings, and art displays that highlighted the institution's evolution and community ties.
By 2012, the university announced major construction projects to enhance academic infrastructure, including the selection of VIA Design for the renovation and expansion of Zumberge Library and Wolverine Building Group as construction manager for a new science facility featuring advanced biology laboratories, part of a broader $77 million investment in campus facilities set for completion in subsequent years. Following these initiatives, GVSU experienced enrollment growth and recovery post-2010, peaking at 22,406 students in Fall 2021 amid regional expansions. Key developments included the opening of the Mary Idema Pew Library Learning and Information Commons in June 2013 at a cost of $65 million, which introduced 150,300 square feet of modern learning spaces and earned LEED Platinum certification for its sustainable design.
Recent milestones underscore ongoing institutional expansion, with the Steelcase Library renovationadding a testing center and enhanced resourcescompleted in Summer 2025 after a closure from April to August. The Board of Trustees approved a $409 million operating budget for 2025-26, reflecting a 1.8% spending increase over the prior year to support student opportunities and operations. That fall, GVSU welcomed its second-largest incoming class in history, comprising 6,465 new undergraduate and graduate students, contributing to an overall enrollment of 22,035a 1% rise from 2024.
In 2025, the university faced federal investigations by the U.S. Department of Education into alleged racial discrimination related to race-based scholarships and diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) practices, prompted by complaints from civil rights groups. Additionally, a February 2025 controversy arose over student artworks perceived as offensive to some Christian groups, sparking debates on free speech and religious sensitivity on campus. On November 12, 2025, the Board of Trustees approved $17.15 million for three new construction projects on the Allendale Campus.
GVSU's broader post-2010 evolution has amplified its regional role, generating a $3 billion annual economic impact on Michigan in fiscal year 2023-24equivalent to the activity of 10 Super Bowlsthrough alumni earnings, job support for over 17,200 positions, and $369 million in state tax revenue. The university has prioritized sustainability via its 2010-2015 Strategic Plan, achieving LEED Silver for Zumberge Hall in 2014 and fostering partnerships like the Community Sustainability Partnership, which grew to over 180 organizations by 2010 to promote regional environmental and economic resilience.

Campuses

Allendale Campus

The Allendale Campus serves as the primary and historical hub of Grand Valley State University, located in Allendale Charter Township, Michigan, approximately 12 miles west of Grand Rapids. Spanning 1,469 acres, it was established as the university's original site following the institution's chartering in 1960, with site planning and design commencing in 1961. The campus is situated along the banks of the Grand River, adjacent to a system of ravines that contribute to its natural, wooded landscape and proximity to Lake Michigan's shoreline.
As the administrative center, the Allendale Campus hosts the majority of the university's undergraduate programs and central offices. Key facilities include Lubbers Stadium, a multi-purpose venue with a capacity of 10,444 used primarily for football and track & field events; the Kirkhof Center, functioning as the main student union with dining, recreational, and event spaces; and the DeVos Fieldhouse Arena, an indoor facility seating 4,200 for basketball, volleyball, and other sports. Academic infrastructure features over 100 buildings, including 122 classrooms distributed across academic cores, supporting a range of instructional needs.
The Mary Idema Pew Library, opened in 2013, anchors the campus's academic resources with 150,000 volumes in open stacks, alongside automated storage for additional materials, collaborative study spaces, and LEED Platinum-certified sustainable design. Residential options include 29 residence halls offering traditional, suite-style, and honors housing, plus three on-campus apartment complexes such as Grand Valley Apartments and Laker Village, collectively housing approximately 6,000 students (as of 2023), with plans to expand capacity to 7,000 beds by 2033. These facilities foster a suburban campus environment emphasizing community, recreation, and integration with the surrounding natural features like the Grand River valley.

Grand Rapids Campuses

The Grand Rapids campuses of Grand Valley State University consist of the Robert C. Pew Campus and the adjacent Health Campus, providing urban facilities that complement the primary Allendale Campus by emphasizing professional and graduate education in a downtown setting. These sites integrate academic instruction with the vibrant economic and medical environments of Grand Rapids, fostering opportunities for experiential learning through partnerships with local hospitals and businesses.
The Robert C. Pew Campus spans 69 acres in downtown Grand Rapids and features 11 buildings that support a range of programs, including the Cook-DeVos Center for Health Sciences. Established in the 1980s as part of the university's expansion into urban centers, the campus has undergone significant developments, such as the 2003 opening of the Cook-DeVos Center, a 217,000-square-foot facility dedicated to health sciences education. Further growth in the 2010s included enhancements to infrastructure supporting interdisciplinary studies. Among its facilities, the Steelcase Library, located in the DeVos Center, underwent a major renovation and reopened in fall 2025 after temporary closure for construction during spring and summer semesters.
The Health Campus covers 19 acres along the Medical Mile, a corridor renowned for its concentration of healthcare institutions, and focuses on training in health professions such as nursing, physical therapy, and occupational therapy. Key infrastructure includes the Daniel and Pamella DeVos Center for Interprofessional Health, a $70 million facility with groundbreaking in October 2018 and opening in 2021, designed to promote collaborative learning through 15 classrooms and 14 interactive laboratories. This center builds on earlier expansions, enhancing the campus's role in preparing students for interprofessional healthcare environments.
Together, the Grand Rapids campuses serve primarily graduate and professional students, offering proximity to over 20 major hospitals and numerous businesses that enable hands-on clinical rotations, internships, and research collaborations. Housing options include two residence hallsWinter Hall and Secchia Hallaccommodating students in furnished apartments and efficiencies near downtown amenities. These facilities underscore the university's commitment to integrating academic programs with real-world applications in Michigan's second-largest city.

Regional and satellite locations

Grand Valley State University operates several non-residential regional and satellite locations across Michigan to extend educational access beyond its main campuses, offering credit-bearing undergraduate and graduate courses, as well as non-credit professional development and workforce training programs. These centers partner with local community colleges and industries to address regional needs, serving approximately 1,000 to 2,000 students annually through hybrid, online, and in-person formats.
The Muskegon Regional Center, established in 1995 as part of the Stevenson Center for Higher Education in partnership with Muskegon Community College, focuses on community education and workforce development in the lakeshore region. It provides a range of undergraduate and graduate courses, including those in liberal studies and health professions, alongside non-credit offerings through the adjacent Muskegon Innovation Hub, which supports entrepreneurship, small business training, and economic growth initiatives with local partners. The center's facilities, including the 2001-opened Lake Michigan Center housing the Annis Water Resources Institute, emphasize applied learning in environmental and professional fields.
Established in the fall of 1995 at Northwestern Michigan College's University Center, the Traverse City Regional Center delivers undergraduate and graduate programs tailored to northern Michigan residents, with a strong emphasis on health sciences and social services. Key offerings include the Bachelor of Science in Allied Health Sciences for degree completion, Physician Assistant Studies, Social Work, Public Health, and Liberal Studies, often in hybrid formats to accommodate working professionals. The center collaborates with regional healthcare providers and recently relocated to a new Front Street facility in August 2025 to enhance accessibility and support services. Enrollment at the site supports around 60 unique students per term, contributing to broader regional access.
The Holland Meijer Campus, opened in 1998 following a land donation from the Meijer family, serves Ottawa County with liberal arts and professional programs designed for non-traditional students and degree completers. It hosts the Frederik Meijer Honors College, courses in education, nursing, and integrative studies, as well as the Police Academy for law enforcement training. The campus emphasizes flexible scheduling and partnerships with local businesses to support workforce preparation in the region's manufacturing and service sectors.
Launched in 2012 with the purchase and renovation of the historic Barden Towers building in downtown Detroit, the Detroit Center provides urban access for southeast Michigan residents through workshops, seminars, and select graduate programs in areas like business and education. It serves as a hub for professional development, charter school administration, and community outreach, with recent partnerships such as the October 2025 collaboration with Michigan Central to expand youth STEM programming and adult upskilling credentials aligned with local tech and mobility industries. The non-residential site focuses on short-term, high-impact training to bridge educational gaps in the urban economy.

Governance and administration

Leadership and organization

Grand Valley State University is governed by an eight-member Board of Trustees, with members appointed by the Governor of Michigan and confirmed by the state Senate for eight-year terms. The board holds general supervision over the university, including control of expenditures and decision-making on major policies, and the institution submits annual accountability reports to the Michigan Legislature detailing academic and economic performance.
The university's current leadership is headed by President Philomena V. Mantella, who has served since July 2019. Mantella oversees the senior leadership team, which includes Provost and Executive Vice President for Academic Affairs Jennifer A. Drake, appointed to the role in April 2025. Each of the seven collegesBrooks College of Interdisciplinary Studies, College of Computing, College of Education and Community Innovation, Seymour and Esther Padnos College of Engineering, College of Health Professions, College of Liberal Arts and Sciences, and Seidman College of Businessis led by a dean responsible for academic programs and faculty within their units as of 2025. This structure follows a 2024 reorganization that separated the former Padnos College of Engineering and Computing into the College of Computing and Seymour and Esther Padnos College of Engineering.
Organizationally, the university is structured around its colleges, supported by administrative units such as enrollment management, institutional advancement, and student affairs to facilitate operations and strategic initiatives. GVSU employs approximately 3,600 faculty and staff members to support its academic and administrative functions.
Historically, James H. Zumberge served as the university's first president from 1962 to 1969, guiding its initial development as Grand Valley State College during its formative years. Following the institution's elevation to university status in 1987, its leadership model evolved under subsequent presidents, expanding administrative roles and decentralizing authority to the colleges while maintaining centralized oversight by the Board of Trustees and president.

Campus safety and services

The Department of Public Safety at Grand Valley State University operates a sworn police force consisting of MCOLES-licensed officers who provide full law enforcement services across all campuses. These officers conduct 24/7 patrols and incident response on the Allendale and Grand Rapids campuses, ensuring continuous coverage for students, faculty, staff, and visitors. The department complies with the Clery Act by publishing an Annual Security and Fire Safety Report, which details crime statistics for the prior three years and underscores the university's low crime rates compared to national averages and other Michigan state universities.
Key services include emergency management coordination, which encompasses threat assessment, crisis response, and recovery planning through the university's Comprehensive Emergency Management Plan. Transportation safety options feature the Safewalk escort program, offering free accompaniment for individuals feeling unsafe while walking on campus from 6 p.m. to 2 a.m. during fall and winter semesters, as well as complimentary rides on The Rapid bus system using a valid GVSU ID. Support for mental and physical well-being is provided via the University Counseling Center, which delivers free confidential therapy sessions, and the Trinity Health Campus Health Center for medical care. Sustainability efforts integrate into campus infrastructure, with LEED-certified buildings like the Mary Idema Pew Library enhancing environmental safety through energy-efficient designs that reduce hazards from outdated systems.
Facilities supporting these operations include 24/7 dispatch centers at the Allendale Campus (1011 Service Building) and the Grand Rapids Pew Campus (609 Watson St. SW), enabling rapid response across locations. The department maintains partnerships with local agencies, such as Ottawa County Central Dispatch and Grand Rapids Police Department, for coordinated emergency support and resource sharing.
Following 2020, the university enhanced safety protocols amid the COVID-19 pandemic and enrollment growth, introducing the Laker Guardian mobile app for real-time alerts and reporting, alongside updated health guidelines through the Lakers Together initiative. These measures, including ongoing Incident Management Team oversight, have bolstered resilience to public health emergencies while maintaining low overall crime incidence.

Academics

Colleges and programs

Grand Valley State University organizes its academic offerings across eight primary colleges, each focusing on distinct disciplinary and interdisciplinary areas to support a broad range of educational pathways. These include the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences, which encompasses humanities, social sciences, natural sciences, and mathematics; the Brooks College of Interdisciplinary Studies, emphasizing integrated approaches to global and cultural studies; the Frederik Meijer Honors College, dedicated to enriched learning for high-achieving students; the College of Education and Community Innovation, addressing teacher preparation and public service initiatives; the Kirkhof College of Nursing; the Seymour and Esther Padnos College of Engineering and Computing; the College of Health Professions; and the Seidman College of Business.
The university provides 102 undergraduate majors and 45 graduate degrees, alongside numerous minors, certificates, and combined programs, totaling over 350 academic options that integrate theoretical knowledge with practical application. Undergraduate programs span traditional disciplines such as biology, English, and computer science, while graduate offerings include master's degrees in fields like public administration and data science.
Among the most popular undergraduate majors are those in health professions, with nursing pre-admit enrolling 1,192 students in fall 2025, followed by psychology at 1,132 students and education at 697 students, reflecting a strong emphasis on these areas where the majority of undergraduatesparticularly in health and liberal arts fieldsare concentrated. In terms of degrees conferred, health professions awarded 250 bachelor's degrees in 2021, alongside notable numbers in exercise science and biomedical sciences, underscoring their appeal for career-oriented students.
GVSU distinguishes itself through programs that prioritize hands-on learning, with 25% of students engaging in faculty-led research and over 4,900 participating in experiential activities such as internships, co-ops, and study abroad opportunities embedded in the curriculum. Unique graduate offerings include professional doctorates like the Doctor of Physical Therapy (D.P.T.), a three-year program preparing entry-level practitioners, and the Doctor of Occupational Therapy (Dr.O.T.), available as a post-professional option or combined with a master's degree. Additionally, interdisciplinary programs such as sustainability studies allow students to blend environmental science with policy and ethics across colleges. The Seidman College of Business holds AACSB accreditation, and the Kirkhof College of Nursing is accredited by the Commission on Collegiate Nursing Education.

Applications are submitted online through the university's portal or via paper form, accompanied by official high school transcripts sent directly from the school. The acceptance rate stands at 83%, reflecting a moderately selective process that admits a large portion of applicants while prioritizing those with strong academic preparation. Among admitted freshmen who submit test scores, the middle 50% range includes SAT scores of 10101230 and ACT scores of 2127, with GPAs typically between 3.3 and 3.9.
Graduate admissions at Grand Valley State University vary by program but generally require a bachelor's degree from an accredited institution, a minimum undergraduate GPA of 3.0, official transcripts, and program-specific elements such as letters of recommendation, resumes, or standardized tests like the GRE. Applicants submit materials through a centralized online portal, with deadlines differing across departmentsoften earlier for fall entry. Student life

Housing and residential life

Grand Valley State University offers diverse on-campus housing options designed to support student success and community building, with the majority located at the Allendale Campus and limited facilities at the Pew Campus in Grand Rapids. Room and board costs for the 2025-2026 academic year total approximately $10,788 for on-campus undergraduates, covering double-occupancy housing and a standard meal plan, positioning GVSU's rates among the lowest quarter of Michigan's public universities.
The university emphasizes themed living experiences through dedicated learning communities tailored for first-year and honors students, promoting academic integration and personal growth. Examples include wellness-focused options like the Women in Science & Engineering (WISE) Living Center, global perspectives in the International House, and interdisciplinary themes such as engineering or gender justice, all integrated into select residence halls to encourage collaborative programming.
Residential life extends beyond accommodations through structured events and governance, including hall governments that organize community activities, social gatherings, and leadership opportunities to enhance student engagement; over 1,200 such programs reached 6,500 residents in 2023-2024. On-campus living correlates with improved retention, with residents achieving a 92.3% fall-to-winter retention rate in 2023 compared to 89.3% for off-campus students, and specific learning communities reporting up to a 9% retention increase for targeted groups in 2024. Off-campus housing options abound in Allendale, including private apartments and townhomes within walking or short driving distance to campus.
In response to fall 2025 total enrollment reaching 22,035 students, including the second-largest incoming class of 6,465 new students in university historyGVSU has implemented capacity adjustments, including new residence hall constructions adding 600 beds at Allendale and planning for 400 beds at Pew, aiming to expand overall housing to 7,000 beds by 2033 while addressing overflow through denser assignments and off-campus partnerships.

Student organizations and activities

Grand Valley State University features a vibrant array of student organizations and activities that foster leadership, community engagement, and personal development. The Student Senate serves as the primary student government body, comprising an executive board and general assembly with elected representatives from various colleges and campuses. It acts as a liaison between students, faculty, and administration, advocating for student concerns, resolving issues, and allocating funds to support campus initiatives and events.
Fraternity and Sorority Life at the university encompasses four governing councilsInterfraternity Council, National Pan-Hellenic Council, National Panhellenic Conference, and Multicultural Greek Councilwith 31 active chapters as of 2025. These organizations involve over 1,000 members, representing approximately 5% of the undergraduate population, and emphasize academics, leadership, and community service through events like Greek Week. Participation has grown post-2020, with expansions including new chapters such as Sigma Chi in fall 2025, though the community faced challenges like the suspension of Pi Kappa Phi in 2024.
Beyond Greek life, the university supports more than 400 registered student organizations (RSOs), spanning cultural groups like the African Student Association, service-oriented clubs such as Habitat for Humanity, and pre-professional societies including the Accounting Student Association. These RSOs promote interdisciplinary collaboration and hobbies, with resources provided by the Office of Student Life for funding, event planning, and recruitment. The LGBTQ+ Faculty & Staff Association (FSA) complements student efforts through initiatives like a 2024-2025 member survey, where 52 respondents (80% of participants) highlighted needs for greater inclusivity, including addressing deadnaming and pronoun disregard to enhance campus equity.
Key activities include New Student Orientation, which welcomed 6,465 incoming undergraduates and graduates in fall 2025the second-largest class in university historyoffering sessions for academic advising, campus tours, and organization involvement to ease the transition. Homecoming 2025, held from November 3-9, featured spirit days, a selfie station in the Kirkhof Center, alumni tailgates, and a parade, drawing the community together to celebrate Laker traditions. Many organizations integrate with on-campus housing to build community among residents.

Arts and media

Grand Valley State University's student media outlets provide opportunities for hands-on experience in journalism, broadcasting, and digital production. The Grand Valley Lanthorn, established in 1968, serves as the primary student newspaper, published twice weekly and covering campus news, sports, and features both in print and online formats. WGVU Public Media, operated by the university, functions as affiliates of PBS and NPR, delivering local television programming on channels 35 and 52, along with radio broadcasts on 88.5 FM and 95.3 FM to serve west Michigan communities.
Student-run broadcast media further enhance creative expression at GVSU. WCKS, known as Whale Radio, is the university's only student-operated radio station, offering 24/7 programming focused on new and underground music, talk shows, and campus coverage, accessible via online streaming. Similarly, GVTV operates as the sole student-run television network, producing content ranging from news and sports to lifestyle segments, with opportunities open to students across all majors.
In the arts, the Department of Music, Theatre, and Dance fosters innovative performances and education. The New Music Ensemble, an undergraduate group, promotes contemporary music through commissions, tours, recordings, and performances, marking its 20th anniversary in 2025 with a special concert featuring alumni. The trumpet ensemble has achieved national recognition, winning first place at the 2006 National Trumpet Competition. Theatre programs offer Bachelor of Arts and Bachelor of Science degrees, emphasizing practical training in production and performance through staged shows in the Haas Center for Performing Arts. Visual arts initiatives complement these efforts, integrating creative practices across disciplines.
Recent faculty and student projects highlight GVSU's commitment to applied arts. In 2025, art therapy efforts led by professor Kendra Stanley-Mills and students collaborated with pediatric patients at Helen DeVos Children's Hospital to create artwork that reduces fear during medical procedures, demonstrating the therapeutic potential of artistic expression. Annual exhibitions and performances, including those from the theatre and music ensembles, continue to engage the campus and regional audiences.

Sustainability efforts

Grand Valley State University has prioritized sustainable building practices, committing to Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design (LEED) Silver certification or higher for all new constructions, additions, and major renovations since 2009. As of 2025, the university features 27 LEED-certified buildings across its campuses, encompassing a range of certifications from Silver to Platinum and representing approximately 33% of its total building square footage. These structures incorporate energy-efficient designs, such as advanced HVAC systems and sustainable materials, contributing to annual energy cost savings exceeding $2 million through over 250 implemented projects that monitor and reduce consumption.
The university offers an interdisciplinary Environmental and Sustainability Studies minor, requiring 21 credits focused on holistic, place-based education in environmental issues, social perspectives, and solutions-oriented problem-solving. This program integrates sustainability into the curriculum across disciplines, with over 200 undergraduate and graduate courses incorporating sustainability themes. Campus-wide efforts include comprehensive recycling programs and zero-waste initiatives, such as the annual Campus Race to Zero Waste competition and Zero Waste Football Games, which aim to minimize landfill waste through sorting stations for compost, recycling, and landfill at dining locations and events. In recognition of these efforts, Grand Valley received a Gold rating of 74.9 in the 2023 STARS assessment by the Association for the Advancement of Sustainability in Higher Education, reflecting ongoing progress from earlier benchmarks like the 2011 A- grade from the Sustainable Endowments Institute.
Key initiatives include the Michigan Alternative and Renewable Energy Center (MAREC), which serves as a hub for research, education, and commercialization of renewable technologies like solar, wind, and hydrogen fuel cells at the Muskegon Innovation Hub, a LEED Gold facility. The university's Climate Action Plan outlines strategies to achieve carbon neutrality by 2043, emphasizing renewable energy adoption and emissions reductions, supported by facilities like the on-campus Solar Garden. These efforts align with broader goals, including recent state funding pursuits for infrastructure that enhance sustainability, as highlighted in 2025 budget discussions.
Student involvement is central to these sustainability efforts, with organizations like the Student Environmental Coalition and Seidman Sustainability Association leading advocacy, clean-ups, and educational events to foster peer-to-peer engagement. These groups collaborate with the Office of Sustainability Practices to embed sustainability in campus life, from event planning to research projects, ensuring students gain practical experience aligned with curricular goals.

Athletics

Teams and conferences

Grand Valley State University sponsors 19 varsity athletic teams that compete at the NCAA Division II level as members of the Great Lakes Intercollegiate Athletic Conference (GLIAC), a league the institution helped charter in 1972. The Lakers nickname reflects the proximity of the Allendale campus to Lake Michigan, while the official mascot is Louie the Laker, a costumed character introduced in the 1960s to energize crowds at sporting events.
The athletics program features 9 men's teams and 10 women's teams, promoting balanced participation and adhering to federal gender equity requirements under Title IX through equitable funding, facilities access, and recruitment efforts. Approximately 850 student-athletes across these teams balance competition with academic pursuits, supported by dedicated academic advising and resources tailored to their needs.
Men's varsity sports include baseball, basketball, cross country, football, golf, swimming and diving, tennis, (/page/Track and field), and wrestling. Women's varsity sports encompass basketball, cross country, golf, lacrosse, soccer, softball, swimming and diving, tennis, (/page/Track and field), and volleyball.
The program's roots trace to the institution's founding as a college in 1960, with early competition in the National Association of Intercollegiate Athletics (NAIA); however, it fully transitioned to NCAA Division II in 1987 alongside the elevation to university status, enabling broader regional and national competition within the GLIAC framework.

Facilities and achievements

Grand Valley State University's athletic facilities are centered in the Raleigh J. Finkelstein Athletic Village on the Allendale Campus, providing venues for NCAA Division II competition and recreational use. Lubbers Stadium serves as the primary home for Laker football and soccer, featuring a capacity of 10,440 seats and synthetic turf installed in 2010, with recent renovations in 2023-2024 enhancing fan amenities and safety features. The GVSU Fieldhouse Arena accommodates basketball, volleyball, and indoor track events with a seating capacity of 4,200 and three multi-sport courts completed in 1982. The Kelly/Childs Tennis Center, renovated in 2018 and located adjacent to the Kelly Family Sports Center, includes eight outdoor courts for tennis practices and matches.
The Lakers have achieved remarkable success in NCAA Division II athletics, securing 31 national championships across multiple sports as of 2025. Women's track and field has been particularly dominant, claiming the outdoor national title in 2025 for the fifth time overall, while the program has contributed 15 of the university's championships since 2010. Football has won four national titles since 2001, including appearances in six championship games. Other recent victories include the 2025 women's basketball national championship, highlighting the program's depth in 19 varsity sports.
Grand Valley State holds the NCAA Division II record with 17 Learfield Directors' Cup awards, recognizing overall athletic excellence, including the 2024-25 season win with 1,080.5 points across 19 sports. The Lakers captured the GLIAC President's Cup for the 26th consecutive year in 2024-25, reflecting sustained conference dominance in events like cross country (23rd straight men's title and 24th for women in 2025) and swimming and diving. On November 8, 2025, both the men's and women's cross country teams swept the NCAA Division II Midwest Region Championships for the 23rd consecutive year, qualifying for nationals. In club sports, the men's and women's rugby teams have won multiple Great Lakes Rugby Conference championships, with the men's squad claiming four straight from 2022 to 2025.
Laker student-athletes demonstrate strong academic performance, with programs like women's track and field posting a 3.53 cumulative GPA in 2024 and numerous honorees on GLIAC All-Academic Teams (requiring a 3.0-3.49 GPA). The 2025 Homecoming events, highlighted by the football game against Michigan Tech on November 8 at Lubbers Stadium, drew significant alumni participation and underscored the community's engagement with athletic traditions.

Notable people

Notable alumni

Grand Valley State University has produced over 150,000 alumni worldwide, with approximately 98,880 residing and working in Michigan.

Business and Politics

Brian Calley, who earned an MBA from GVSU in 2000, served as the 54th Lieutenant Governor of Michigan from 2011 to 2019, focusing on economic development and education policy. John Beyrle, who earned a bachelor's degree in 1975, became a prominent diplomat, serving as the U.S. Ambassador to Russia from 2008 to 2012 and later as ambassador to Bulgaria. In business, Peter Mayhak, a 1991 B.B.A. graduate, leads n Vision/USA as president and CEO, specializing in optical technologies and earning recognition as a distinguished alumnus.

Sports and Entertainment

Tony Ferguson, who attended GVSU in communication, achieved fame as a professional mixed martial artist in the UFC, holding the interim lightweight championship and compiling a 25-11-0 record (as of November 2025). Matt Thornton, who played baseball at GVSU in the early 2000s, enjoyed a 13-year MLB career with teams including the Seattle Mariners and Chicago White Sox, appearing in 687 games as a reliever. In entertainment, John Keating, a 1979 broadcast journalism alumnus, served as a television host for Detroit sports broadcasts, including pre- and post-game shows for the Tigers and Red Wings, for 45 years before retiring in 2025.

Other Notable Alumni

Alumni from the music program, such as composers Adam Cuthbert, Matt Finch, and Daniel Rhode, have contributed to national new music ensembles, with their works performed in venues across New York, Atlanta, and beyond through the GVSU New Music Ensemble's tours. In 2025, Khadijah Johnson ('13, health professions and Spanish) received the Alumni Service Award for her work in public health equity, while Gloria Masterson Hunter ('17) earned the Young Alumni Award for advancements in community health inclusion.
GVSU alumni drive significant economic contributions to Michigan, generating $7.6 billion in annual earnings, including $1.8 billion in annual incremental earnings, sustaining 116,000 jobs statewide (as of 2025). The alumni network, exceeding 140,000 members, fosters connections through events like the annual Homecoming on November 8, 2025, featuring the Laker Alumni Marching Band, and regional gatherings such as the Phoenix Alumni Network's community outreach.

Notable faculty

Grand Valley State University (GVSU) boasts a distinguished faculty whose contributions span research leadership, innovative teaching, and administrative excellence. In academic administration, Jennifer A. Drake, Ph.D., serves as Provost and Executive Vice President for the Division of Academic Affairs, a role she assumed permanently in April 2025 after serving as acting provost. Drake oversees a division that supports over 350 academic programs, fostering interdisciplinary scholarship and faculty development through initiatives like the annual Faculty Awards Convocation.
In water resources research, Stuart Jones, Ph.D., leads as Executive Director and Professor at the Robert B. Annis Water Resources Institute (AWRI), appointed in January 2025. An aquatic ecologist specializing in limnology and microbial ecology, Jones directs studies on freshwater systems, including nutrient cycling and climate impacts on Great Lakes ecosystems, building on AWRI's legacy of securing multimillion-dollar grants from agencies like the National Science Foundation. Complementing this, Alan D. Steinman, Ph.D., former AWRI Director and Allen and Helen Hunting Research Professor, advanced understanding of lake salinization and harmful algal blooms through over 150 peer-reviewed publications and leadership in collaborative restoration projects. Faculty like Bopaiah A. Biddanda, Ph.D., further contribute as aquatic microbial ecologists, investigating carbon dynamics in Lake Michigan with funding from the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency.
For renewable energy and innovation, Kevin Ricco, Ph.D., has served as Executive Director of the Muskegon Innovation Hub (formerly the Michigan Alternative and Renewable Energy Center, or MAREC) since 2014. Ricco drives interdisciplinary efforts in sustainable technologies, including solar and wind energy applications, supporting business incubators that have generated over $10 million in economic impact through grants and partnerships with entities like the Michigan Economic Development Corporation. His work emphasizes mentorship for student researchers in energy policy and prototyping, aligning with GVSU's broader sustainability goals.
In the arts, Alex Wilson, D.M.A., Associate Professor of Trumpet, has directed the GVSU Trumpet Studio since 2015, guiding ensembles to national acclaim, including second place in the 2005 National Trumpet Competition Ensemble Division and third place in 2009. Wilson's pedagogical approach integrates performance with music education, mentoring students who have advanced to professional orchestras and further graduate studies.
Recognizing teaching excellence, Adrienne Wallace, Ph.D., Associate Professor of Advertising and Public Relations, was named a 2025 Michigan Distinguished Professor of the Year for her innovative use of integrated communications in public affairs and social media curricula. Wallace's interdisciplinary scholarship includes publications on digital marketing equity and mentorship programs that have earned her the GVSU Outstanding University Teacher Award. Similarly, Valerie Peterson, Ph.D., received the 2025 Glenn A. Niemeyer Faculty Mentoring Award for her guidance of underrepresented students in biology and environmental science, facilitating their involvement in grant-funded research projects. These honors underscore GVSU faculty's commitment to high-impact mentorship across disciplines, with collective contributions including over $20 million in annual research grants and extensive publications in top-tier journals.
