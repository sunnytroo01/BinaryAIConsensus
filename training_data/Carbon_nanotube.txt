Carbon nanotube
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Carbon nanotubes (CNTs) are cylindrical nanostructures composed of carbon atoms arranged in a hexagonal lattice, essentially rolled-up sheets of graphene with diameters typically ranging from 0.4 to 100 nanometers and lengths that can extend from micrometers to centimeters, resulting in aspect ratios exceeding 1,000,000. These seamless tubes represent a one-dimensional allotrope of carbon, distinct from other forms like diamond, graphite, and fullerenes. First observed in 1991 by Sumio Iijima using high-resolution transmission electron microscopy during investigations of arc-discharge synthesis of fullerenes, CNTs were initially identified as multi-walled variants consisting of concentric cylindrical graphene layers. Single-walled CNTs, featuring a single graphene cylinder, were subsequently discovered in 1993.
CNTs are classified into two primary types: single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs), which have a diameter of about 1 nanometer and exhibit properties highly dependent on their chiral vector (defining the roll-up direction of the graphene sheet), and multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs), which comprise multiple nested tubes with interlayer spacings of approximately 0.34 nanometers, similar to graphite. The chirality in SWCNTs can render them either metallic (conducting) or semiconducting, while MWCNTs generally display metallic behavior due to the presence of inner tubes with varying electronic properties. Synthesis methods include arc discharge, laser ablation, and chemical vapor deposition (CVD), with CVD being the most scalable for industrial production.
Renowned for their exceptional properties, CNTs possess a Young's modulus of about 1 TPa, comparable to that of diamondand a tensile strength approaching 200 gigapascals (GPa), surpassing steel by over 100 times in specific strength. Their thermal conductivity exceeds 3000 W/mK along the tube axis, rivaling or surpassing that of diamond, enabling efficient heat dissipation. Electrically, metallic CNTs can carry current densities up to 10^9 A/cm without significant heating, far beyond copper. These attributes stem from the sp-hybridized carbon bonds and the quantum confinement in their nanoscale structure.
Due to these superior characteristics, CNTs find applications across diverse fields, including reinforcing polymer composites for enhanced mechanical strength and lightweight materials in aerospace. In electronics, they serve as transistors, interconnects, and sensors owing to their tunable conductivity and high electron mobility. Biomedical uses encompass drug delivery vectors, biosensors, and tissue engineering scaffolds, leveraging their biocompatibility when functionalized and high surface area. Additionally, CNTs are explored in energy storage devices like batteries and supercapacitors for improved charge capacity and rate performance, as well as in environmental remediation for pollutant adsorption. Ongoing research addresses challenges such as scalable purification, alignment, and toxicity to fully realize their potential.

History

Discovery and early research

Early observations of structures resembling multi-walled carbon nanotubes date back to the 1950s, when Soviet scientists L.V. Radushkevich and V.M. Lukyanovich reported hollow graphitic carbon fibers with diameters around 50 nm formed during the decomposition of carbon monoxide on iron catalysts, as imaged via electron microscopy. These filaments were noted for their tubular morphology but were not fully characterized or recognized as distinct nanomaterials at the time.
In the 1970s, further experimental work advanced the understanding of such carbon filaments. Morinobu Endo, during his PhD research, synthesized vapor-grown carbon fibers with central hollow cores, proposing a model based on rolling graphene sheets into cylindrical structures, which laid early theoretical groundwork for nanotube formation. Independently, in 1976, A. Oberlin, M. Endo, and T. Koyama observed filamentous carbon growth through benzene decomposition at high temperatures, using high-resolution electron microscopy to reveal multi-layered tubular architectures with diameters of several nanometers. These findings highlighted the potential for controlled synthesis of graphitic tubules, though they remained contextualized within broader carbon fiber research rather than isolated as nanotubes.
The definitive experimental discovery of carbon nanotubes occurred in 1991, when Sumio Iijima at NEC Laboratories examined soot produced by arc-discharge evaporation of graphite electrodesinitially intended for fullerene synthesisand identified helical, multi-walled nanotubes via high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM). Iijima's seminal paper in Nature described these structures as seamless cylinders of graphene sheets, typically 230 nm in diameter and up to several micrometers long, marking the first clear recognition and naming of carbon nanotubes as a novel nanomaterial class. This work built directly on the prior filament observations but provided unprecedented structural detail and sparked widespread interest. Subsequent efforts in 1993 led to the synthesis of single-walled carbon nanotubes by Iijima and colleagues using metal catalysts in arc discharge.

Key advancements and commercialization

The synthesis of single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs) marked a pivotal advancement in 1993, when Sumio Iijima and Toshinari Ichihashi at NEC Corporation reported their production using an arc-discharge method involving carbon electrodes in a helium atmosphere. Independently, Donald S. Bethune and colleagues at IBM achieved similar results using an arc-discharge method co-vaporizing carbon and cobalt, yielding SWCNTs with diameters around 1.2 nm. These discoveries built on earlier multi-walled nanotube observations and opened pathways for exploring SWCNTs' unique electronic and mechanical properties.
In the 2000s, scalable production methods emerged, notably the high-pressure carbon monoxide (HiPco) process developed by Richard E. Smalley's group at Rice University, which utilized catalytic decomposition of CO at 9001,200C to produce high-purity SWCNTs at rates up to 100 mg/hour. Smalley, who shared the 1996 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for discovering fullerenesa foundation for nanotube researchextended his expertise to CNTs, emphasizing their potential in materials science. By the 2010s, chemical vapor deposition (CVD) techniques advanced to enable aligned nanotube arrays, with milestones including fluidized-bed CVD for mass production of vertically aligned arrays up to millimeters in length and improved density through regrowth processes on substrates.
Commercialization accelerated with early patents filed in the 1990s, including Sumio Iijima's 1991 NEC filings on nanotube synthesis and applications, which laid legal groundwork for industrial exploitation. By 2025, production scaled dramatically via floating catalyst CVD (FCCVD), with LG Chem operating facilities totaling 6,100 tons annually for conductive additives in batteries and electronics, and Jiangsu Cnano Technology achieving over 450 tons per year of high-purity SWCNTs for composites and energy storage. Recent developments from 2023 to 2025 focused on defect-free CNT fibers, such as wet-spun variants with minimal structural imperfections for high-strength polymer composites, enhancing tensile strengths beyond 1 GPa while maintaining electrical conductivity.

Multi-walled carbon nanotubes

Multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs) consist of two or more concentric single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs) arranged in a coaxial fashion, forming nested cylindrical graphene layers. These structures typically feature 2 to 50 walls, with overall diameters ranging from 2 to 100 nm.
The layering in MWCNTs can follow two primary models: the Russian doll model, where multiple discrete graphene cylinders are nested within one another like matryoshka dolls, and the parchment model, in which a single graphene sheet is continuously rolled around itself multiple times. The Russian doll configuration is more commonly observed in experimental samples. Adjacent walls are separated by an interwall spacing of approximately 0.34 nm, closely resembling the interlayer distance in graphite.
The concentric walls in MWCNTs are held together primarily by van der Waals forces, which provide weak but sufficient interlayer cohesion without covalent bonding..pdf) This multi-layered architecture confers greater structural stability and higher defect tolerance compared to SWCNTs, as imperfections in one wall can be compensated by the integrity of the others.
Due to their multi-layered structure, MWCNTs generally exhibit superior mechanical strength and high thermal conductivity compared to SWCNTs in many practical applications, making them especially suitable for reinforcement in composites, catalysis, and filtration.
MWCNTs were first observed in the 1950s through electron microscopy images of carbon filaments produced via catalytic decomposition, but their nanotubular nature was not fully recognized until after Sumio Iijima's 1991 identification of both SWCNTs and MWCNTs using high-resolution transmission electron microscopy.

Types and Variants

Chirality and symmetry

The chirality of a single-walled carbon nanotube (SWCNT) is defined by the integers (n, m) that specify the chiral vector, which rolls the graphene sheet into a seamless cylinder. This vector determines the nanotube's helical arrangement, symmetry, and resulting physical properties. Armchair nanotubes, where n = m, possess a high degree of rotational symmetry and exhibit metallic conductivity. Zigzag nanotubes (m = 0) and general chiral nanotubes (n m, m 0) display varying degrees of helicity, influencing their electronic behavior.
The electronic properties of SWCNTs are directly governed by their chirality through the zone-folding approximation of graphene's band structure. Tight-binding calculations reveal that armchair nanotubes are always metallic, while zigzag and chiral nanotubes are metallic if n - m is a multiple of 3 (i.e., n - m 0 mod 3) and semiconducting otherwise. For semiconducting tubes, the direct bandgap decreases inversely with diameter, approximated as $ E_g \approx \frac{0.8}{d} $ eV, where d is the diameter in nm; this dependence arises from the quantization of graphene's and * bands.
Chiral SWCNTs lack mirror symmetry along their axis, exhibiting a specific handedness (left- or right-handed helix), with the enantiomer corresponding to swapped indices (m, n). Despite this asymmetry, the electronic band structure remains identical for enantiomers due to the underlying graphene lattice equivalence. The overall symmetry includes rotational and translational elements, with the chiral angle (ranging from 0 for zigzag to 30 for armchair) modulating the curvature effects on bonding.
In the electronic structure, the circumferential quantization leads to discrete subbands, where the allowed k-vectors are confined by the chiral vector's periodicity. The density of states (DOS) for both metallic and semiconducting tubes features sharp van Hove singularities at the onset of each subband, reflecting the one-dimensional nature and causing enhanced optical absorption and Raman scattering at specific energies. These singularities are more pronounced in smaller-diameter tubes due to wider subband spacing.
Experimentally, chirality is often identified via resonant Raman spectroscopy, leveraging the radial breathing mode (RBM), a coherent vibration where the nanotube cross-section oscillates uniformly. The RBM frequency scales inversely with diameter as $ \omega_{RBM} = \frac{227}{d} $ cm (d in nm), allowing diameter estimation; combined with the G-band profile and excitation energy matching van Hove transitions (the Kataura plot), full (n, m) assignment is possible for individual tubes.

Junctions, crosslinking, and other morphologies

Carbon nanotube junctions represent structural deviations from linear tubular forms, enabling branched architectures that mimic molecular wiring. Y-junctions, characterized by three arms meeting at a central point with angles near 120, can form through controlled chemical vapor deposition (CVD) using catalysts like iron-molybdenum nanoparticles on silica substrates, where carbon deposition creates topological defects incorporating pentagons and heptagons in the graphene lattice. Similarly, T-junctions arise from the fusion of two nanotubes or via ion beam irradiation introducing defects, resulting in perpendicular branching suitable for three-terminal devices. These junctions hold promise for constructing three-dimensional (3D) networks, as multiple branches can interconnect to form conductive scaffolds, potentially enhancing applications in nanoelectronics such as interconnects.
Crosslinking in carbon nanotubes involves forming covalent bonds between adjacent tubes, often through chemical reactions at defect sites or functionalized sidewalls, which stabilizes bundled structures and improves load transfer. For instance, irradiation-induced defects allow direct sp-hybridized bonds between tubes, creating robust networks without external linkers. Carbon nanotubes typically terminate with fullerene-like caps, hemispherical structures composed of twelve pentagons integrated into the hexagonal lattice to close the tube ends, as observed in high-resolution transmission electron microscopy studies of arc-discharge synthesized tubes. Alternatively, open-ended nanotubes result from oxidative treatments that remove caps, exposing reactive edges for further assembly.
Beyond straight and branched forms, carbon nanotubes exhibit diverse morphologies arising from growth conditions or defects. Bamboo-like nanotubes feature periodic compartments formed by internal graphene partitions, synthesized via CVD with iron catalysts where carbon diffusion through the particle creates stacked caps. Coiled nanotubes, resembling helical springs, emerge from CVD using sulfur-promoted catalysts like Fe-Mo on alumina, introducing pentagon-heptagon pairs that impart regular curvature. Toroidal nanotubes form closed loops by connecting nanotube ends with matching pentagon-heptagon defects, achievable through annealing or templated growth, yielding ring structures with potential for cyclic electronics. Double-walled variants, consisting of two concentric single-walled tubes, display morphologies ranging from straight to slightly twisted, often synthesized by CVD with specific catalysts to control interwall spacing around 0.34 nm.

Physical Limits

Diameter and length constraints

Carbon nanotubes exhibit significant constraints on their diameter and length, influenced by synthesis methods, stability considerations, and fundamental physical limits. The smallest experimentally achieved single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs) have diameters of approximately 0.4 nm, corresponding to the (3,3) armchair structure, synthesized in 2002 using zeolite AlPO4-5 templates that confine carbon growth within narrow channels. These ultrasmall SWCNTs represent a milestone in nanoscale engineering, as the zeolite pores dictate the tube diameter, enabling the formation of structures at the edge of experimental feasibility.
Theoretical models predict an even smaller minimum diameter of about 0.3 nm for the (2,2) armchair SWCNT, though such tubes approach instability due to high curvature strain. In practice, stable SWCNTs typically range from 0.7 to 2 nm in diameter, as smaller sizes induce excessive strain that distorts the graphene lattice and promotes defects, while larger diameters reduce quantum confinement effects critical to their unique properties. Chirality plays a subtle role here, with armchair configurations allowing slightly smaller diameters than zigzag ones for equivalent stability. Quantum confinement in these narrow tubes quantizes electronic states more sharply, widening bandgaps and altering reactivity, but the accompanying radial strain can lead to bond elongation and potential collapse.
Regarding length, the longest reported SWCNTs reached approximately 0.65 m in 2019 through chemical vapor deposition (CVD) techniques optimized for continuous growth on substrates. This record highlights advances in catalyst design and growth conditions that minimize interruptions. However, theoretical limits arise from defect accumulation during prolonged synthesis, where thermal fluctuations and catalyst interactions introduce vacancies or Stone-Wales defects that propagate along the tube axis, compromising structural integrity beyond certain lengths. These constraints underscore the challenge of balancing dimensional control with material perfection in carbon nanotube production.

Properties

Mechanical properties

Carbon nanotubes (CNTs) exhibit exceptional mechanical properties, primarily due to their strong covalent sp carbon-carbon bonds and cylindrical structure, which confer high stiffness and strength. For defect-free single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs), the Young's modulus is approximately 1 TPa, representing one of the highest values among known materials. The tensile strength of these ideal SWCNTs ranges from 50 to 100 GPa, far surpassing that of steel or other conventional engineering materials. These properties arise from the seamless hexagonal lattice, enabling efficient load transfer along the tube axis.
The fundamental stress-strain relationship for CNTs under axial loading follows Hooke's law, expressed as =E\sigma = E \epsilon=E, where \sigma is the stress, EEE is the Young's modulus, and \epsilon is the strain. Experimental determination of EEE often involves measuring vibrational frequencies, such as resonance modes in atomic force microscopy or Raman spectroscopy, which relate directly to the elastic constants via the modulus-frequency relation derived from continuum mechanics models. This approach confirms the near-terapascal stiffness in pristine tubes, with linear elasticity holding up to strains of about 10-15% before fracture.
In bending and buckling scenarios, CNTs demonstrate remarkable flexibility. The persistence length, a measure of stiffness against thermal bending fluctuations, exceeds 100 m for SWCNTs, indicating minimal deflection over micrometer scales. Under radial compression, the modulus ranges from 10 to 80 GPa, depending on diameter, allowing tubes to withstand significant deformation without permanent collapse, as observed in nanoindentation experiments. These characteristics enable applications in flexible composites and sensors.
Real-world CNT samples, however, suffer from defects such as vacancies or Stone-Wales rotations introduced during synthesis, which can reduce tensile strength by up to 50% compared to ideal values.

Electrical properties

Carbon nanotubes exhibit remarkable electrical properties that vary significantly depending on their structure, particularly the chirality, which determines whether they are metallic or semiconducting. Metallic single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs) display ballistic conductance, with a quantum of conductance reaching 4e/h per conducting channel due to two spin-degenerate modes, enabling near-perfect electron transport over micrometer lengths at room temperature. In contrast, semiconducting SWCNTs possess a bandgap typically ranging from 0.1 to 1 eV, inversely proportional to their diameter, which allows for tunable electronic behavior suitable for semiconductor applications. These properties position carbon nanotubes as superior alternatives to traditional materials like copper for conductivity and silicon for semiconductors.
Carrier mobility in semiconducting carbon nanotubes exceeds 10 cm/Vs, surpassing that of silicon and enabling high-speed electron transport with minimal scattering. This high mobility arises from the one-dimensional nature of the nanotubes, where phonons and impurities have limited impact on charge carriers. Electromechanical effects further enhance their utility; under axial strain, piezoresistance occurs due to modulation of the bandgap, with the relative change approximated as E_g / E_g 0.2 (l/l) for semiconducting tubes, where strain alters the band structure and resistance by up to several orders of magnitude. In metallic tubes, sufficient strain can open a bandgap, transitioning them from conductors to semiconductors.
Doping and electrostatic gating are essential for engineering carbon nanotube field-effect transistors (CNTFETs), where chemical or electrostatic doping controls carrier type and density to achieve p-type or n-type conduction. Optimal doping levels, around 10^{-3} dopants per atom, balance ohmic contacts and channel performance, yielding on/off current ratios exceeding 10^5 and subthreshold swings near the Boltzmann limit of 60 mV/decade in short-channel devices. Aligned semiconducting nanotube arrays in CNTFETs demonstrate drive currents over 1 A per nanotube at low voltages, with power efficiency 10 times higher than silicon CMOS equivalents.
Recent advancements in 2025 highlight quantum dot-like behavior in short carbon nanotubes, where gate-tunable spectra exhibit discrete energy levels and long coherence times up to 200 ns in gatemon qubit configurations, limited primarily by charge noise. This behavior emerges in nanotubes shorter than 100 nm, enabling applications in quantum computing through hybridization with superconducting circuits.

Thermal properties

Carbon nanotubes exhibit exceptionally high axial thermal conductivity at room temperature, typically ranging from 3000 to 6000 W/mK for single-walled variants, surpassing that of diamond and making them promising for thermal management applications. This superior heat transport is dominated by phonons, with long mean free paths enabling near-ballistic conduction along the nanotube axis. In contrast, multi-walled carbon nanotubes show slightly lower values, around 20003000 W/mK, due to interlayer interactions scattering phonons.

Aligned nanotube arrays can enhance overall effective conductivity by minimizing cross-tube scattering, though practical values remain below intrinsic axial limits due to junction resistances.

Optical properties

Carbon nanotubes exhibit distinctive optical properties arising from their one-dimensional structure and electronic band structure, particularly in semiconducting variants where excitonic effects dominate light-matter interactions. The primary optical transitions in single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs) are excitonic, occurring in the near-infrared (near-IR) and visible spectral regions due to the strong Coulomb interaction that binds electrons and holes into excitons with binding energies on the order of hundreds of meV. These transitions correspond to van Hove singularities in the density of states, with the lowest-energy excitonic transition (E_{11}) typically falling between 0.8 and 1.5 eV for diameters around 1 nm.
The relationship between a nanotube's chiral indices (n, m) and its optical absorption peaks is mapped by the Kataura plot, an empirical diagram plotting transition energies against nanotube diameter or radial breathing mode frequency. Developed from experimental absorption and Raman data, the Kataura plot enables prediction of absorption wavelengths for specific chiralities, revealing how structural variations lead to distinct spectral signatures; for instance, smaller-diameter tubes exhibit blue-shifted transitions due to increased curvature effects. This tool has been refined over time to account for environmental influences like surfactant wrapping, improving accuracy for assignments in aqueous suspensions.
Semiconducting SWCNTs display photoluminescence (PL) primarily from the radiative decay of E_{11} excitons, emitting in the near-IR range with peaks that mirror their absorption spectra. The PL quantum yield for these tubes is typically low, ranging from approximately 1% to 10% for isolated, defect-free species in solution, limited by nonradiative recombination pathways such as exciton-exciton annihilation and trapping at defects. Chirality-selective excitation enhances PL intensity, allowing identification of specific (n, m) species through excitation-emission maps.
Raman spectroscopy, particularly resonant Raman, combined with photoluminescence, serves as a powerful method for assigning nanotube chirality by correlating radial breathing mode frequencies with excitonic transition energies. Under resonant conditions, where the laser energy matches an optical transition, the Raman signal intensifies, enabling precise mapping to Kataura plot coordinates; for example, the (6,5) chirality shows a characteristic breathing mode at ~310 cm^{-1} resonant with ~1.2 eV excitation. Photoluminescence spectroscopy complements this by providing emission data that confirms semiconducting nature and refines assignments, especially for ensembles with mixed chiralities.
In nonlinear optics, SWCNTs demonstrate saturable absorption, where high-intensity light bleaches the ground-state population, leading to decreased absorption and increased transmission. This property arises from the fast relaxation of excitons (on picosecond timescales) and has been exploited in mode-locking lasers, where CNT-based saturable absorbers generate ultrashort pulses in fiber lasers operating from near-IR to mid-IR wavelengths. Seminal demonstrations include erbium-doped fiber lasers producing femtosecond pulses with repetition rates up to gigahertz, highlighting CNTs' broadband operation and low saturation fluence compared to traditional absorbers.

Biological and chemical properties

Carbon nanotubes (CNTs) exhibit distinct chemical reactivity influenced by their structural integrity. Pristine CNTs feature chemically inert sidewalls due to the stable sp-hybridized carbon lattice, akin to graphite, which resists reactions under ambient conditions and contributes to their hydrophobicity. However, defects such as vacancies, Stone-Wales rotations, or ad-dimers dramatically increase reactivity at those localized sites, enabling selective chemical functionalization, adsorption of molecules, and enhanced interaction with reactive species like oxygen or radicals. This defect-driven reactivity is quantified by energy barriers lowered by 4670 kcal/mol for -bonded adsorbates compared to pristine surfaces.
In biological contexts, CNTs display notable antibacterial properties, primarily through the generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) that induce oxidative stress and direct mechanical disruption of bacterial cell membranes, leading to leakage and cell death. These effects are particularly effective against Gram-negative bacteria like Escherichia coli, where multi-walled CNTs inhibit growth at concentrations of 10100 g/mL. Functionalization strategies can further tune this reactivity to enhance or mitigate biological interactions.
Biocompatibility of CNTs is modulated by key physicochemical factors, including surface charge and length, which govern cellular uptake and interaction with biological systems. Cationic surface charges promote electrostatic attraction to negatively charged cell membranes, facilitating higher uptake rates, whereas neutral or anionic charges reduce this interaction and improve dispersibility in physiological media. Longer CNTs (>1 m) often exhibit lower cellular internalization due to endocytic barriers and increased entanglement, while shorter variants enhance uptake efficiency. Recent 2024 investigations reveal that protein coronas formed in biological fluids can attenuate CNT toxicity by passivating reactive surfaces and altering biodistribution, thereby influencing cellular responses and reducing oxidative damage.

Synthesis

Primary synthesis methods

Recent 2024 reviews confirm that the primary synthesis methods for carbon nanotubes (CNTs) remain arc discharge, laser ablation, and chemical vapor deposition (CVD), with catalytic CVD emphasized as the most promising for large-scale production due to its high yield, low cost, minimal impurities, and superior control over nanotube structure, including distinctions between single-walled (SWCNTs) and multi-walled (MWCNTs).
The primary synthesis methods for carbon nanotubes (CNTs) are arc discharge, laser ablation, and chemical vapor deposition (CVD), each offering distinct advantages in terms of product quality, scalability, and control over nanotube structure. These techniques were developed in the early 1990s and have formed the foundation for CNT production, enabling the growth of both multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs) and single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs). Arc discharge and laser ablation typically produce high-quality but lower-yield materials, while CVD provides greater scalability for industrial applications.
Arc discharge synthesis involves the evaporation of graphite electrodes at high temperatures in an inert atmosphere, such as helium, using a direct current (DC) arc between a graphite anode and cathode. The process generates a plasma at approximately 4000 K, causing carbon atoms to condense and form CNTs primarily in the cathode deposit and surrounding web-like structures, yielding both MWCNTs and SWCNTs when metal catalysts like yttrium or nickel are incorporated. This method, first reported for MWCNTs in 1991 and extended to SWCNTs in 1993, typically results in CNT yields of 30-70% in the raw product, with purity limited by amorphous carbon and metal impurities requiring subsequent purification.
Laser ablation employs a pulsed laser to vaporize a graphite target mixed with metal catalysts (e.g., Ni/Co) in a high-temperature inert gas environment, typically at 1200C, promoting the formation of SWCNTs with narrow diameter distributions. The vaporized carbon condenses as the gas flows over a cooler collector, producing bundles of high-quality SWCNTs with fewer defects compared to other methods. Developed in 1996, this technique achieves CNT yields around 30-70%, often with higher initial purity (up to 70% SWCNTs) due to the controlled condensation, though it is energy-intensive and less scalable.
Chemical vapor deposition (CVD) decomposes hydrocarbon precursors, such as methane or ethylene, on metal catalyst particles (e.g., Fe, Ni, or Co) supported on substrates at temperatures of 500-1000C, allowing catalytic growth of CNTs directly on surfaces for aligned arrays or films. This method, pioneered for SWCNTs in 1996 using CO disproportionation and refined for patterned growth in 1998, enables scalable production and offers yields up to 90% with optimized catalysts and conditions, though early products often contain more structural defects than those from arc or laser methods.

Recent advances in production

Recent advancements in carbon nanotube (CNT) production have focused on enhancing yield, improving chirality control, and enabling industrial scaling, building on established chemical vapor deposition (CVD) techniques as highlighted in recent reviews. Optimizations in floating catalyst CVD (FCCVD) have enabled the continuous production of high-strength CNT fibers through refined reactor designs that promote better catalyst dispersion and gas flow dynamics. For instance, parametric studies of FCCVD processes have demonstrated the synthesis of CNT fibers with aspect ratios exceeding 10^6, allowing for the spinning of continuous aerogels into kilometer-scale lengths under controlled conditions. These improvements have achieved CNT purities greater than 95% by minimizing amorphous carbon formation, as evidenced by elevated Raman I_G/I_D ratios approaching 10.
Plasma-enhanced CVD (PECVD) has emerged as a key method for low-temperature growth, facilitating CNT synthesis on temperature-sensitive substrates at temperatures as low as 135C. This approach leverages plasma activation to decompose carbon precursors efficiently, enabling vertically aligned CNT arrays with reduced thermal stress and improved uniformity. Complementing this, water-assisted CVD has significantly reduced defects by introducing water vapor to etch away amorphous carbon deposits on catalyst particles, resulting in higher crystallinity and Raman I_G/I_D ratios up to 10.23 for double-walled CNTs.
Chirality-selective growth has advanced through the use of solid catalysts engineered for specific nanotube types, such as (6,5) single-walled CNTs. Porous magnesia-supported manganese-rhenium (MnRe/MgO) catalysts, featuring sigma-phase intermetallic nanoparticles, have enabled over 70% selectivity for (6,5) SWCNTs by stabilizing growth kinetics and favoring low-energy chiral structures during CVD. These solid catalysts provide precise control over nucleation sites, outperforming traditional soluble precursors in reproducibility.
On the industrial front, LG Chem expanded its multi-walled CNT (MWCNT) production capacity to 6,100 tons per year in 2025 through the operation of a fourth dedicated plant at its Daesan Complex, driven by demand in electric vehicle batteries and conductive additives. This scaling, alongside broader industry optimizations, has contributed to cost reductions, with MWCNT prices dropping below $100 per kg in bulk markets as of late 2024.

Purification

Techniques for separation

Post-synthesis purification of carbon nanotubes (CNTs) is essential to remove impurities such as amorphous carbon and metal catalyst residues introduced during production methods like chemical vapor deposition or arc discharge.
Chemical techniques primarily target the selective removal of non-tubular carbon and metallic impurities. Oxidation processes exploit the higher thermal stability of CNTs compared to amorphous carbon, which can be oxidized at lower temperatures. Dry oxidation involves heating CNTs in air or oxygen at 300600C to burn off amorphous carbon, while microwave-assisted oxidation accelerates this by generating localized heat. Wet oxidation uses chemical agents like nitric acid or hydrogen peroxide to oxidize and dissolve amorphous carbon, often achieving up to 90% removal of such impurities without significantly damaging the CNT structure.
Acid treatment is commonly employed to eliminate metal catalysts, such as iron or nickel particles encapsulated in carbon shells. Refluxing CNTs in dilute hydrochloric or nitric acid (13 M) for several hours dissolves these metals, with microwave assistance reducing treatment time to minutes while maintaining efficacy; this can remove over 95% of residual metals, though some iron content may persist if encapsulated. These chemical methods are effective for initial purification but can introduce defects or shorten CNT lengths, necessitating combination with physical separation.
Physical techniques enable sorting of CNTs by structural properties like diameter, chirality, or length after initial chemical cleaning. Density gradient ultracentrifugation (DGU) suspends surfactant-wrapped CNTs in a density gradient medium (e.g., iodixanol) and applies high-speed centrifugation (up to 500,000 g), separating tubes based on buoyant density, which correlates with diameter and electronic type (metallic vs. semiconducting); this yields highly enriched fractions with purities exceeding 99% for specific types, though overall recovery is often below 50% due to broad distributions.
Chromatography methods, including gel permeation and density gradient variants, facilitate separation by exploiting differences in tube-surfactant interactions or hydrodynamic size. In gel chromatography, CNTs migrate through a porous matrix under solvent flow, sorting primarily by length and diameter; this has achieved >95% purity for diameter-sorted single-walled CNTs (SWCNTs). Electrophoresis techniques, such as gel electrophoresis or dielectrophoresis, apply electric fields to dispersed CNTs, separating them by length (longer tubes migrate slower) or charge differences related to chirality; for instance, SDS-PAGE has produced length ladders with 1020% polydispersity. Filtration, often via micro- or ultrafiltration membranes, removes larger aggregates and sorts by length through size exclusion, yielding up to 99% impurity-free material but requiring multiple passes for fine control.
Despite high purities, these physical methods face scalability challenges, as they process small quantities (milligrams) and require specialized equipment, limiting industrial application while offering precise control for research.

Benefits of monochiral purification

Monochiral purification isolates single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs) with identical chirality, defined by the (n,m) indices that dictate their electronic band structure, ensuring all tubes in a sample are either metallic or semiconducting. This uniformity eliminates variability inherent in as-synthesized mixtures, where metallic and semiconducting tubes coexist, leading to inconsistent device performance. In electronics, monochiral samples enable precise control over charge carrier type and behavior, facilitating the fabrication of reliable field-effect transistors (FETs) with reduced scattering and higher on/off ratios.
The enhanced performance of monochiral SWCNTs manifests in superior electrical conductivity, particularly in thin films where aligned monochiral arrays exhibit quasi-ballistic transport and current densities exceeding those of polycrystalline samples. For instance, monochiral semiconducting SWCNTs achieve carrier mobilities over 10,000 cm/Vs, supporting high-frequency operation in FETs with cutoff frequencies above 70 GHz, ideal for next-generation integrated circuits. Optically, these purified tubes display narrower absorption peaks due to homogeneous bandgap energies, improving the efficiency of photodetectors and enabling selective wavelength responses in optoelectronic devices.
Gel chromatography has demonstrated outcomes such as yields exceeding 90% purity for (6,5) semiconducting SWCNTs, producing gram-scale quantities suitable for device integration. These high-purity (6,5) tubes enhance sensor sensitivity by providing consistent near-infrared fluorescence, as seen in copper ion detection platforms with detection limits below 1 nM.
In 2025, DNA-wrapped sorting advanced scalability through salt-driven aqueous two-phase extraction, achieving milligram-scale production of enantiomerically pure monochiral SWCNTs with optimized cation ratios for reproducible partitioning. This method supports industrial applications by reducing costs and enabling high-throughput enrichment of specific chiralities for advanced electronics and imaging.

Functionalization

Covalent modifications

Covalent modifications of carbon nanotubes (CNTs) involve the formation of direct chemical bonds with the nanotube surface, typically at sidewall carbons or pre-existing defects, which permanently alter their electronic and chemical properties. One prominent method is sidewall functionalization through the addition of aryl groups using diazonium salts. In this process, aryl diazonium salts are reducedoften electrochemicallyto generate aryl radicals that add to the -conjugated sp-hybridized carbon lattice of the CNT sidewall, forming strong C-C bonds and converting the affected sp carbons to sp hybridization. This disruption localizes structural changes but extends electronic perturbations over distances greater than 1 nm along the nanotube axis.
Defect-site reactions target open ends, Stone-Wales defects, or other structural imperfections on CNTs, where oxidative treatments with strong acids like nitric or sulfuric acid introduce carboxylic acid (-COOH) groups. These reactions preferentially occur at high-curvature sites or defects due to their higher reactivity compared to pristine sidewalls, enhancing the solubility of CNTs in aqueous and organic solvents by providing polar functionalities that promote dispersion and prevent bundling. The degree of carboxylation correlates with the initial defect density, as determined by Raman spectroscopy (via the D-band to G-band intensity ratio, I_D/I_G), allowing controlled functionalization yields that scale with available defect sitestypically expressed as the number of functional groups per unit length, such as 1-10% coverage for moderate oxidation conditions.
Such covalent modifications significantly impact the intrinsic properties of CNTs, particularly their electronic structure. In metallic single-walled CNTs, sidewall additions create localized impurity states near the Fermi level, opening a bandgap and converting the tubes from metallic to semiconducting behavior. This effect arises from the breaking of the continuous -electron delocalization, as modeled by density functional theory, where the reaction yield for defect incorporation follows a linear dependence on initial defect concentration, as quantified by changes in the Raman I_D/I_G ratio.
Representative examples of covalent modifications include the formation of ester or amide linkages from carboxylated CNTs to graft polymers, improving compatibility with matrices like polyesters or polyamides. For instance, reacting -COOH groups with hydroxyl- or amine-terminated polymers via condensation yields covalent ester (-COO-) or amide (-CONH-) bonds, enabling uniform dispersion in composites and enhancing mechanical reinforcement without phase separation. These linkages maintain the CNT's structural integrity while tailoring surface wettability, as demonstrated in applications for reinforced polymers where grafting densities of 1-5 chains per 100 nm achieve up to 20-30% improvements in tensile strength.
As of 2025, advances in non-disruptive covalent functionalization have emerged, such as cycloaddition reactions using azido aryl compounds to form 'open-ring' aziridine bridges on single-walled CNTs (SWCNTs) and double-walled CNTs (DWCNTs). This method allows sidewall modification while minimizing disruption to the electronic properties compared to traditional additions.

Non-covalent modifications

Non-covalent modifications of carbon nanotubes (CNTs) utilize adsorption-based interactions, such as - stacking, hydrophobic effects, and van der Waals forces, to attach molecules to the CNT sidewall without disrupting the sp-hybridized carbon lattice. This approach preserves the intrinsic electronic, optical, and mechanical properties of CNTs, distinguishing it from covalent methods that introduce structural defects.
A prominent technique involves - stacking with aromatic compounds, particularly pyrene derivatives, which exploit the delocalized -electrons of both the aromatic and CNT surfaces for strong, reversible adsorption. For instance, pyrenebutyric acid derivatives have been used to non-covalently coat single-walled CNTs (SWCNTs), facilitating subsequent attachment of functional moieties like succinimidyl esters for biomolecular conjugation. This method enhances CNT processability while maintaining sidewall integrity.
Surfactant wrapping provides another effective strategy for dispersion, where amphiphilic molecules like sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) or sodium cholate form helical or random coils around the CNT surface via hydrophobic interactions between their alkyl tails and the graphitic lattice. The polar head groups then stabilize the wrapped CNTs in aqueous or organic solvents, achieving stable dispersions at concentrations up to several mg/mL without altering the electronic bandgap.
Polymer encapsulation, notably with DNA or RNA oligonucleotides, enables chirality-selective interactions through - stacking of nucleobases with the CNT sidewall, leading to helical wrapping that recognizes specific (n,m) chiralities. Seminal work demonstrated that single-stranded DNA sequences disperse bundled SWCNTs into individual tubes and allow separation of metallic from semiconducting species, or even single-chirality enrichment, via ion-exchange chromatography or aqueous two-phase extraction. This selectivity arises from sequence-dependent binding affinities, with certain motifs favoring narrower or larger-diameter tubes.
These modifications markedly improve CNT solubility in polar and non-polar mediaoften by orders of magnitudefacilitating handling and integration into composites, while preserving the bandgap and thus optoelectronic characteristics essential for applications like transistors or sensors. Furthermore, controlled non-covalent interactions promote self-assembly into aligned bundles or hierarchical structures, such as microspheres, driven by balanced attractive and repulsive forces between functionalized tubes.
Non-covalent functionalization via - stacking or surfactant mediation supports targeted drug delivery, enabling CNT conjugates to transport therapeutics like doxorubicin to tumor sites. As of October 2025, recent advancements include novel functionalization strategies and hybrid nanostructures that enhance specificity and reduce off-target effects in CNT-based drug delivery systems.

Modeling and Metrology

Computational modeling approaches

Computational modeling of carbon nanotubes (CNTs) employs a hierarchy of methods to predict their electronic, mechanical, and thermal properties at scales ranging from atomic to macroscopic. These approaches bridge quantum mechanics for fundamental interactions with classical simulations for larger systems, enabling predictions of behavior under various conditions without direct experimentation. Density functional theory (DFT) and tight-binding models dominate quantum-level simulations, while molecular dynamics (MD) and continuum mechanics handle dynamic and structural analyses, often integrated through multiscale frameworks.
Quantum mechanical methods provide detailed insights into the electronic structure of CNTs. DFT calculations, based on solving the Kohn-Sham equations, accurately determine the band structure, density of states, and optical properties of single-walled CNTs (SWCNTs), revealing metallic or semiconducting behavior dependent on chirality. For instance, DFT has been used to study optical transitions in semiconducting SWCNTs, predicting excitation energies that align with experimental spectra. Tight-binding models, parameterized from ab initio data, offer a computationally efficient alternative for bandgap calculations; they approximate the -electron system using nearest-neighbor interactions, yielding bandgaps that scale inversely with nanotube diameter and vary with chiral indices (n,m). These models have been refined to reproduce DFT results for a wide range of SWCNT configurations, facilitating rapid screening of electronic properties.
At the atomic scale, molecular dynamics simulations capture mechanical and thermal behaviors using empirical force fields. The ReaxFF reactive force field, which accounts for bond breaking and formation, simulates CNT deformation, fracture, and heat transport by modeling covalent interactions in carbon systems. Applications include predicting Young's modulus values around 1 TPa and thermal conductivities exceeding 3000 W/mK for pristine SWCNTs, with variations due to defects or bundling. ReaxFF has proven effective for sp carbon structures like CNTs and graphene, reproducing experimental tensile strengths and phonon contributions to thermal properties.
For larger assemblies such as CNT bundles or composites, continuum models treat nanotubes as elastic shells or beams, incorporating van der Waals interactions between tubes. These models predict effective moduli and buckling under compression, often linking to atomistic data via homogenization techniques. Multiscale approaches bridge quantum and continuum levels; for example, embedding DFT-derived potentials into MD simulations scales up to bundle mechanics, revealing load transfer mechanisms in aligned CNT arrays with effective stiffness up to 100 GPa.
Simulations have elucidated strain effects on CNT chirality, showing that axial tension can induce bandgap oscillations and potential semiconductor-to-metal transitions in chiral tubes, as predicted by tight-binding models under up to 10% strain. Recent advances incorporate AI-accelerated MD, using machine learning potentials trained on quantum data to simulate defect dynamics; these enable long-timescale studies of vacancy migration and healing in SWCNTs, achieving microsecond resolutions with DFT-level accuracy by 2025.

Characterization techniques

Characterization techniques for carbon nanotubes (CNTs) are crucial for elucidating their structural parameters, such as diameter, length, and chirality, as well as electronic and thermal properties that dictate their performance in diverse applications. These methods encompass high-resolution imaging, vibrational spectroscopy, and transport measurements, enabling precise assessment of nanotube quality, purity, and defects without destructive sampling in many cases. Seminal works have established these techniques as standards, with ongoing refinements improving resolution and in-situ capabilities.
Microscopy provides direct visualization of CNT morphology at the nanoscale. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) is widely employed to determine diameter, length, and the number of walls, offering atomic-scale resolution to distinguish single-walled CNTs (SWCNTs) from multi-walled CNTs (MWCNTs) by imaging concentric graphene layers. For instance, high-resolution TEM reveals interlayer spacing around 0.34 nm, confirming graphitic structure, and is often used post-purification to verify uniformity. Atomic force microscopy (AFM) complements TEM by mapping surface topography on substrates, measuring individual nanotube diameters (typically 1-2 nm for SWCNTs) and lengths up to micrometers, with the advantage of operation in ambient conditions. Scanning tunneling microscopy (STM) excels in probing chirality and electronic structure, as it images the helical arrangement of carbon atoms and maps local density of states, allowing differentiation of metallic and semiconducting CNTs based on bandgap features.
Spectroscopic techniques offer rapid, non-destructive insights into composition and electronic behavior. Raman spectroscopy is a primary tool, characterized by the radial breathing mode (RBM) in the 100-300 cm range, whose frequency inversely correlates with diameter via the relation RBM248d\omega_{RBM} \approx \frac{248}{d}RBMd248 (where ddd is in nm), enabling precise sizing of ensembles. The G band near 1580 cm arises from tangential vibrations of sp carbon atoms, indicating high crystallinity, while the D band at ~1350 cm reflects disorder or defects; the intensity ratio IG/ID>10I_G / I_D > 10IG/ID>10 signifies low-defect, high-purity CNTs. Ultraviolet-visible-near-infrared (UV-Vis-NIR) spectroscopy identifies metallic versus semiconducting types through distinct absorption peaks: semiconducting SWCNTs exhibit van Hove singularities between 400-1400 nm, whereas metallic ones show a broad peak around 500-600 nm due to interband transitions.
Electrical and thermal measurements quantify transport properties essential for device integration. The four-probe method assesses longitudinal conductivity, revealing near-ballistic electron transport in defect-free SWCNTs with values exceeding 10510^5105 S/cm at room temperature, far surpassing copper. Thermal conductivity (\kappa) is evaluated using suspended-bridge techniques or scanning thermal microscopy, demonstrating exceptional values up to 3500 W/mK for individual SWCNTs, attributed to phonon transport along the nanotube axis.
Advancements as of 2025 include in-situ metrology during synthesis, such as operando TEM and X-ray diffraction (XRD), which capture real-time dynamics like catalyst evolution and growth kinetics under chemical vapor deposition conditions. For example, in-situ TEM has resolved atomic-scale CNT elongation from iron catalysts at 800C, correlating structure with growth mechanisms. These experimental data often validate computational modeling approaches for predicting CNT properties.

Health and Safety

Toxicity mechanisms

The toxicity of carbon nanotubes (CNTs) is primarily driven by their physicochemical properties, which influence their interactions with biological systems. Key factors include fiber length, aspect ratio, and the presence of metal impurities. CNTs with lengths exceeding 5 m exhibit asbestos-like persistence in tissues, as they surpass the threshold for effective clearance by macrophages, leading to prolonged biopersistence and potential fibrogenic effects. High aspect ratios, typically greater than 3:1, enhance this pathogenicity by promoting fiber-like behavior that resists phagocytosis and exacerbates inflammatory responses. Additionally, metal impurities such as iron, cobalt, and nickel, introduced during synthesis as catalysts, catalyze reactive oxygen species (ROS) generation and amplify oxidative damage.
In 2014, the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) classified one type of multi-walled carbon nanotube (MWCNT-7) as possibly carcinogenic to humans (Group 2B), while other CNTs were classified as not classifiable (Group 3).
At the cellular level, CNT-induced toxicity involves oxidative stress and inflammatory mechanisms. Exposure to CNTs triggers the production of ROS through direct electron transfer or via metal-catalyzed Fenton reactions, overwhelming cellular antioxidant defenses and causing lipid peroxidation, protein damage, and DNA strand breaks. This oxidative stress is compounded by frustrated phagocytosis, where long, rigid CNTs cannot be fully engulfed by alveolar macrophages, leading to incomplete degradation, release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-1, TNF-), and persistent inflammation that can progress to fibrosis. Genotoxicity in lung models, such as micronucleus formation and chromosomal aberrations in epithelial cells, further underscores these pathways, often observed at occupationally relevant doses.
Dose-response relationships in in vitro studies reveal cytotoxicity thresholds, with half-maximal inhibitory concentrations (IC50) for cell viability typically ranging from 10 to 100 g/mL in human lung cell lines, depending on CNT type and dispersion state. Single-walled CNTs (SWCNTs) generally exhibit greater toxicity than multi-walled CNTs (MWCNTs) due to their smaller diameter and higher dispersibility in biological media, which increases cellular uptake and surface interactions.

Exposure routes and mitigation strategies

Carbon nanotubes (CNTs) pose occupational health risks primarily through inhalation, which is the dominant exposure route in manufacturing, handling, and processing environments where airborne particles can be generated during activities such as powder dispersion or machining of CNT-containing materials. Dermal exposure represents a minor risk, mainly involving skin irritation from direct contact rather than significant absorption, while ingestion is considered low probability, typically occurring via hand-to-mouth transfer in poorly controlled settings.
To mitigate these risks, strategies include chemical functionalization of CNTs, which can alter surface properties to decrease their tendency to aerosolize and disperse freely in air, thereby reducing inhalation potential during handling. Encapsulation within polymer composites or matrices further limits exposure by binding CNTs and preventing their release as respirable particles during use or abrasion of the final product. Engineering controls such as local exhaust ventilation and wet processing methods are also recommended to capture airborne CNTs at the source.
Regulatory guidelines emphasize airborne exposure limits to protect workers; as of 2025, the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) aligns with the National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH) recommended exposure limit of approximately 1 g/m for respirable CNTs as an 8-hour time-weighted average. Personal protective equipment (PPE), including NIOSH-approved respirators with high-efficiency particulate air (HEPA) filters, gloves, and protective clothing, is advised when engineering controls are insufficient.
Recent studies demonstrate that modulating CNT dimensions can influence biological retention; for instance, a 2021 investigation showed that shortening CNTs via oxidation significantly reduced their lung biopersistence and associated inflammation compared to longer variants in rodent models. This length-dependent effect underscores the value of size control in mitigation efforts to minimize long-term pulmonary accumulation.

Applications

Current commercial uses

Carbon nanotubes (CNTs), particularly multi-walled CNTs (MWCNTs), have established commercial applications in composites and electronics, leveraging their exceptional mechanical strength and electrical conductivity. Recent reviews highlight that MWCNTs offer superior mechanical strength and thermal conductivity, making them particularly suitable for composites, catalysis, and filtration applications, while single-walled CNTs (SWCNTs) feature chirality-dependent electronic properties (metallic or semiconducting) ideal for advanced sensors and drug delivery. The global CNT market reached USD 12.5 billion in 2025, with significant growth driven by demand for MWCNTs in the automotive sector for enhanced material performance.
In composites, CNTs are incorporated as reinforcements in polymers to improve mechanical properties for demanding industries. For instance, CNT-enhanced rubber formulations are used in tire treads to boost wear resistance, tensile strength, and overall durability, with commercial products like OCSiAl's TUBALL enabling lighter, high-performance tires for electric vehicles (EVs) and motorcycles. In aerospace, CNT-reinforced polymer composites provide superior strength-to-weight ratios, applied in structural components such as aircraft skins and interiors to reduce weight while maintaining rigidity; these materials are integrated in commercial aviation programs to enhance fuel efficiency.
In electronics, CNTs, particularly single-walled CNTs (SWCNTs), serve as conductive additives in lithium-ion batteries, improving electrode conductivity and enabling higher energy density and faster charging in EV applications. LG Chem supplies CNT materials as additives for EV battery cathodes, contributing to enhanced overall battery performance and safety. Additionally, CNT-based conductive inks are commercially utilized in touchscreens and flexible electronics, offering transparent, durable alternatives to traditional materials; for example, CHASM Advanced Materials' MWCNT inks are employed in display manufacturing for their high conductivity and printability.

Biomedical and sensing applications

Carbon nanotubes (CNTs) have emerged as promising nanomaterials in biomedical applications due to their unique mechanical, electrical, and optical properties, which can be tailored through functionalization to enhance biocompatibility and enable targeted interactions with biological systems. In drug delivery, CNTs serve as efficient carriers for therapeutic agents, particularly in cancer therapy, where their high surface area allows for substantial drug loading. For instance, single-walled CNTs functionalized with polyethylene glycol (PEG) have demonstrated loading efficiencies of approximately 50% for chemotherapeutic drugs like doxorubicin, enabling targeted delivery to tumor cells via receptor-mediated endocytosis and reducing systemic toxicity. This approach has been validated in preclinical models, showing enhanced tumor regression compared to free drug administration. Recent reviews emphasize SWCNTs' chirality-dependent electronic properties making them particularly suitable for targeted drug delivery and high-sensitivity sensors.
In biosensing, CNTs are integral to high-sensitivity devices, leveraging their electrical conductivity for real-time detection of biomolecules. Field-effect transistor (FET)-based sensors incorporating CNTs exhibit exceptional sensitivity for glucose monitoring, achieving detection limits as low as 10 nM, which surpasses traditional enzymatic sensors in response time and stability. Recent advancements include wearable CNT patches developed in 2025, which integrate flexible CNT networks with microfluidic channels for non-invasive sweat-based glucose tracking, offering continuous monitoring for diabetes management with accuracy comparable to commercial glucometers. These devices rely on brief functionalization with biocompatible polymers to minimize immune responses and ensure skin adhesion. CNTs also enable high-sensitivity gas sensing, with SWCNTs particularly effective for detecting gases such as NO at ppb levels through changes in electrical resistance upon gas adsorption.
For imaging applications, CNTs functionalized with Raman-active dyes act as contrast agents, providing enhanced visualization of cellular structures and tissues due to their strong near-infrared resonance signals. These agents have been used in photoacoustic imaging to achieve sub-millimeter resolution in deep-tissue tumor detection, with signal amplification up to 100-fold over conventional dyes. In tissue engineering, CNT-incorporated scaffolds promote cell proliferation and differentiation; for example, multi-walled CNT composites in 3D scaffolds have supported neural stem cell growth, improving regeneration in spinal cord injury models by mimicking extracellular matrix stiffness. Such scaffolds exhibit mechanical properties tunable to 1-10 MPa, aligning with native tissues.
Recent developments underscore the translational potential of CNTs in biomedicine. CNTs have shown potential as adjuvants in vaccine platforms for infectious diseases, demonstrating up to 80% higher antibody titers in animal models. Additionally, CNT-polymer coatings on medical implants have shown strong antibacterial effects against pathogens like Staphylococcus aureus, reducing biofilm formation by over 90% through reactive oxygen species generation, without compromising eukaryotic cell viability. As of 2025, most biomedical applications of CNTs remain in preclinical stages, with no major regulatory approvals for clinical use. These applications highlight CNTs' role in advancing precision medicine while emphasizing the need for rigorous safety assessments.

Emerging and future developments

In the field of energy applications, carbon nanotubes (CNTs) are being integrated into proton exchange membrane (PEM) fuel cells to enhance performance through improved catalyst support and proton conductivity. Recent advancements in CNT-reinforced membranes have demonstrated up to 20% higher power density compared to traditional setups, attributed to better platinum dispersion and reduced ohmic losses. For hydrogen storage, CNT-based composites, particularly multi-walled variants doped with metals like iron or phosphorus, have shown storage capacities reaching 7.8 wt% under moderate pressures, leveraging enhanced surface area and spillover effects for reversible adsorption. These developments position CNTs as key enablers for efficient, lightweight hydrogen systems in sustainable energy infrastructures.
CNTs are also explored in water purification, particularly through adsorption and membrane filtration. MWCNTs and functionalized CNTs effectively remove heavy metals (e.g., arsenic up to 94.5% efficiency), dyes (up to 100% removal), organic pollutants, and pathogens, leveraging high surface area and tunable chemistry for enhanced contaminant removal. In catalysis, CNTs serve as supports for improved activity and selectivity, while in filtration, CNT-based membranes reduce fouling and enhance performance in environmental applications.
In quantum technologies, CNTs serve as hosts for spin qubits, offering long coherence times due to their low charge noise and one-dimensional structure. A 2025 demonstration of a carbon nanotube gatemon qubit achieved quantum control with fidelity exceeding 99%, paving the way for scalable quantum processors. Similarly, in spintronics, chiral CNTs enable gate-controlled spin transport, with recent devices exhibiting spin coherence lengths over 10 micrometers, supporting applications in low-power magnetic memory. These properties stem from CNTs' tunable bandgap and ballistic electron transport, briefly highlighting their suitability for quantum information processing.
Market projections indicate robust growth for CNTs, driven by demand in electronics and energy sectors, with the global market expected to reach approximately $43 billion by 2034 at a compound annual growth rate of 16.5%. However, challenges in chirality control persist, particularly for optoelectronics, where separating single-chirality single-walled CNTs remains difficult, limiting bandgap precision and device efficiency. Advances in catalytic synthesis are addressing this, but scalable production of uniform chirality tubes is essential for infrared sensors and flexible displays.
Looking ahead, theoretical assessments of CNTs for space elevators emphasize their intrinsic tensile strength of 100200 GPa, far surpassing steel, though practical defects reduce effective limits to below requirements for geostationary tethers. Ongoing research explores CNT hybrids for advanced biomedical actuators and drug delivery systems. These concepts underscore CNTs' potential in extreme engineering, contingent on overcoming synthesis and alignment hurdles.
