Dialogue is where stories come alive. It is where characters reveal themselves, where conflicts erupt and resolve, where information is exchanged and emotions are expressed, where relationships are built and broken. Good dialogue sounds natural, moves the story forward, and reveals character. Bad dialogue sounds like a textbook, stops the story dead, and makes every character sound the same. The difference between the two is one of the most important skills any writer can develop.

The first rule of dialogue is that people do not speak in complete, grammatically perfect sentences. In real conversation, people interrupt themselves, trail off, change direction mid-thought, use contractions, speak in fragments, and leave things unsaid. Written dialogue should reflect this natural imperfection without becoming unintelligible. The goal is controlled messiness. It should sound real without actually being real, because truly real dialogue, transcribed word for word from an actual conversation, is repetitive, unfocused, and boring.

Let us look at what bad dialogue looks like and why it fails.

Bad dialogue: "Hello, John. I am glad to see you today. I wanted to inform you that I have been thinking about the situation with the company, and I believe that we should consider restructuring the marketing department in order to improve our overall efficiency and bottom line."

This is bad because nobody talks like this. It is a written statement disguised as speech. It contains no personality, no emotion, no rhythm, and no subtext. Compare it to this:

Good dialogue: "John. Sit down. We need to talk about marketing." "What about it?" "It is not working." "What do you mean it is not working? We just had our best quarter in three years." "The numbers look good on paper, sure. But half that growth came from one client, and I just got off the phone with that client. They are leaving."

This version reveals character through how each person speaks. The first speaker is direct, authoritative, and in possession of information the other does not have. The second speaker is defensive, proud of the recent success, and about to be blindsided. The dialogue moves the story forward by introducing a conflict. And it sounds like two real people talking.

The second rule of dialogue is that every character should have a distinct voice. If you covered the dialogue tags, the reader should still be able to tell who is speaking based on word choice, sentence structure, attitude, and speech patterns. A teenager does not speak like a professor. A grandmother does not speak like a soldier. A nervous person does not speak like a confident one.

Here are five characters, each with a distinct voice, responding to the same situation: being told that their flight has been canceled.

The business executive: "Canceled? No. I need to be in Chicago by six. What about the seven fifteen? Can you reroute me through Denver? I do not care about the layover. Just get me there."

The anxious traveler: "Canceled? Oh no. Oh no no no. I knew this would happen. I told myself not to book the early flight. Is there another option? Please tell me there is another option. My daughter's wedding is tomorrow."

The laid-back surfer: "Canceled? Huh. Okay. Well, is there anything going out later today? If not, I guess I am finding a hotel and hitting whatever beach is closest. Honestly, worse things have happened."

The elderly grandmother: "Well, that is a shame. But these things happen, don't they, dear? I remember when your grandfather and I were supposed to fly to Miami in 1987 and the whole airline went on strike. We ended up driving instead and it turned into the best trip of our lives. What other flights do you have?"

The teenager: "Wait, what? Canceled? Are you serious right now? This is literally the worst thing that has ever happened to me. My friends are going to be there without me. Can I at least get my bag back?"

Same situation. Five completely different responses. Each reveals character through speech.

The third rule of dialogue is that the most important things are often left unsaid. This is called subtext. In real life, people rarely say exactly what they mean. They hint, deflect, evade, imply, and use humor to avoid vulnerability. Great dialogue captures this indirectness.

On the surface, these two people are talking about a lamp. Underneath, they are talking about their relationship:

"You moved the lamp."
"I moved it to the other table."
"It has been on that table since we moved in."
"Things can be in different places, Sarah."
"I liked where it was."
"You like where everything was."
"What is that supposed to mean?"
"Nothing. Forget the lamp."
"No. Tell me what you meant."
"I meant that sometimes things need to change. That is all."
"We are not talking about the lamp anymore, are we?"
"I do not know. Are we?"

This exchange is about change, control, and the unspoken tension in a relationship. Not one word of emotion is explicitly stated. The characters are talking around the real issue, using the lamp as a proxy. That is subtext. It is the iceberg principle: the reader sees the ten percent above the surface and feels the ninety percent below.

The fourth rule is that dialogue should be accompanied by action and description. People do not stand perfectly still while they talk. They fidget, pace, look away, cross their arms, tap their fingers, stir their coffee, check their phone, adjust their glasses, lean forward, lean back, and perform a thousand small physical actions that communicate as much as their words.

"I need to tell you something." She picked up the salt shaker and turned it in her hand, watching the crystals shift. "About the house."

"What about the house?"

She set the salt shaker down precisely where it had been, aligning it with the edge of the placemat. "I think we should sell it."

He put down his fork. Not gently. It clattered against the plate. "Sell the house."

"It is too big for two people."

"It is not too big. It is right. It was always right." He pushed his chair back from the table. Not far enough to stand. Just far enough to create distance.

Notice how the physical actions reveal what the words do not. She is anxious, fidgeting, controlling small objects because she cannot control the conversation. He is shocked, and his shock manifests physically: the fork, the chair, the sudden need for space. These actions are more eloquent than any dialogue tag.

The fifth rule is about dialogue tags. A dialogue tag tells the reader who is speaking. "He said" and "she said" are the most common and the most effective. They are virtually invisible to the reader, which is exactly what you want. The reader's eye passes over "said" without stopping, the way it passes over a period at the end of a sentence. The word is so common that it disappears.

Avoid overusing creative dialogue tags. "He exclaimed." "She murmured." "He interjected." "She retorted." "He bellowed." These draw attention to themselves and away from the dialogue. Worse, they often tell the reader something that the dialogue itself should already convey. If the character's words are clearly angry, you do not need to write "he snapped." If they are clearly whispering a secret, you do not need to write "she whispered." Let the dialogue do the work.

The most elegant approach is to replace dialogue tags with action beats. Instead of "I do not believe you," he said angrily, write: "I do not believe you." He stood up so fast the chair tipped backward.

Let us look at an extended dialogue scene that demonstrates all of these principles working together:

The café was nearly empty. Rain streaked the windows in uneven lines that made the street outside look like a watercolor painting left out in the storm.

"I got your message," Claire said. She folded her hands around her coffee cup but did not drink from it.

"Which one?"

"The one at two in the morning."

David exhaled. "I was not sober when I sent that."

"I know."

"So you can ignore it."

"I read it seven times."

He looked at her then, really looked, and something shifted behind his eyes. "Claire."

"You said you missed me. You said that the apartment feels wrong without my things in it. You said that the silence at night is louder than any argument we ever had." She paused. "You said you were an idiot."

"I was an idiot."

"Past tense?"

"That is what I need to talk to you about." He reached across the table, not all the way to her hands, but halfway, leaving his palm open on the scratched wooden surface like an offering. "I have been going to therapy. Three weeks now."

She looked at his hand. "Three weeks."

"Every Wednesday at four o'clock. Her name is Dr. Ashworth and she has a plant in her office that I am pretty sure is fake and a poster of the ocean that I stare at when she asks me questions I do not want to answer."

A small sound escaped Claire's mouth. Not quite a laugh. Not quite anything else.

"I am not asking you to come back," he said. "I am asking you to let me earn the right to ask."

She picked up her coffee. Drank. Set it down. The rain outside had not stopped but the light was changing, the way it does when a cloud thins enough to let the sun remember it exists.

"Wednesday at four?" she said.

"Wednesday at four."

"Maybe I could meet you after. For coffee."

"I would like that."

"This is not me coming back."

"I know."

"This is coffee."

"I know."

She smiled. It was small and cautious and it did not reach her eyes, not yet, but it was there.

This scene works because the dialogue is natural and halting, the way real conversations about painful things actually sound. The subtext is rich: these two people love each other and are afraid to say so directly. The physical details, the rain, the coffee, the open palm, the chair, the light, create an atmosphere that mirrors the emotional state of the characters. And the dialogue tags are minimal, replaced by action beats that reveal character and emotion.

Good dialogue is the closest a writer gets to acting. Each character is a role you are playing, with their own voice, their own fears, their own way of deflecting and revealing and wanting and hiding. Master dialogue and you master the human voice itself. And the human voice, in all its imperfection and beauty, is the most compelling instrument in all of literature.
