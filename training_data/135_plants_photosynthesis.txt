Plants are the foundation of almost every ecosystem on Earth. They capture the energy of sunlight and convert it into chemical energy through photosynthesis, a process so fundamental that virtually all life on Earth depends on it either directly or indirectly. Plants produce the oxygen we breathe, the food we eat, the fibers we wear, the wood we build with, and the medicines we take. They stabilize the soil, filter the water, moderate the climate, and create the habitats in which other organisms live. Without plants, the land surface of the Earth would be a barren wasteland of rock and dust, and complex animal life, including human life, would not exist.

Photosynthesis is the process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. The overall equation is deceptively simple: carbon dioxide plus water plus light energy yields glucose plus oxygen. But the actual process involves two complex sets of reactions occurring in the chloroplasts, the organelles within plant cells that contain the green pigment chlorophyll.

The light-dependent reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplasts. Chlorophyll absorbs light energy, primarily in the red and blue wavelengths, and reflects green wavelengths, which is why plants appear green. The absorbed light energy is used to split water molecules into hydrogen ions, electrons, and oxygen. The oxygen is released as a byproduct, which is the oxygen we breathe. The electrons and hydrogen ions are used to produce ATP and NADPH, energy-carrying molecules that power the next stage of photosynthesis.

The light-independent reactions, also called the Calvin cycle, occur in the stroma of the chloroplasts. They use the ATP and NADPH produced by the light-dependent reactions to convert carbon dioxide from the atmosphere into glucose. The enzyme RuBisCO, which catalyzes the first step of the Calvin cycle, is the most abundant protein on Earth. The glucose produced by photosynthesis is used by the plant as an energy source for growth and metabolism, and it serves as the starting material for building all the other organic molecules the plant needs, including cellulose for cell walls, starch for energy storage, and amino acids for proteins.

The evolution of photosynthesis was one of the most transformative events in the history of life on Earth. The earliest photosynthetic organisms, cyanobacteria, appeared approximately two and a half to three billion years ago. Before photosynthesis, the Earth's atmosphere contained virtually no oxygen. The oxygen produced by cyanobacteria gradually accumulated in the atmosphere over hundreds of millions of years, eventually reaching levels sufficient to support oxygen-breathing organisms. This event, known as the Great Oxidation Event, approximately two point four billion years ago, fundamentally changed the chemistry of the Earth's atmosphere, oceans, and rocks, and paved the way for the evolution of complex multicellular life.

Plants colonized land approximately four hundred and seventy million years ago, transforming the terrestrial environment from bare rock and soil into the green, living landscape we know today. The transition from water to land required a series of evolutionary innovations: roots to anchor the plant and absorb water from the soil, a waxy cuticle to prevent water loss from the leaves, stomata, tiny pores that can open and close to regulate gas exchange, and vascular tissue, internal tubes that transport water upward from the roots and sugars downward from the leaves.

The diversity of plants is extraordinary. There are approximately four hundred thousand known species of plants, ranging from tiny mosses a few millimeters tall to giant sequoia trees that can reach heights of over three hundred feet and live for over three thousand years. The largest living organism on Earth is a grove of quaking aspen trees in Utah called Pando, which is actually a single organism connected by a common root system, covering one hundred and six acres and weighing an estimated thirteen million pounds.

Trees are the most impressive of all plant forms. A large oak tree can transpire over forty thousand gallons of water per year, drawing water from the soil through its roots and releasing it as vapor through its leaves. This transpiration creates a cooling effect equivalent to ten room-sized air conditioners running twenty hours a day. Trees also absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere: a single mature tree can absorb approximately forty-eight pounds of carbon dioxide per year, making forests crucial carbon sinks in the fight against climate change.

Plants have evolved remarkable relationships with other organisms. Flowering plants and their pollinators, including bees, butterflies, hummingbirds, and bats, have co-evolved over millions of years. The flower provides nectar and pollen as food for the pollinator, and in return, the pollinator transfers pollen from one flower to another, enabling sexual reproduction. Some of these relationships are extraordinarily specific: the yucca moth pollinates only yucca plants, and yucca plants can be pollinated only by yucca moths. Neither can survive without the other.

Agriculture, the cultivation of plants for food, fiber, and fuel, is the foundation of human civilization. It began approximately ten thousand years ago in the Fertile Crescent of the Middle East with the domestication of wheat, barley, and other crops. Agriculture allowed humans to settle in permanent communities, develop specialized occupations, build cities, and create the complex societies that eventually produced writing, mathematics, science, and art. Today, agriculture occupies approximately thirty-eight percent of the Earth's ice-free land surface and produces the food that sustains nearly eight billion people.

Plants are not passive organisms. They sense and respond to their environment in ways that are far more sophisticated than most people realize. They grow toward light. They send chemical signals through their roots to communicate with neighboring plants about insect attacks. They release volatile chemicals that attract the predators of the insects that are eating them. Some plants, like the Venus flytrap, can move rapidly to capture prey. Others, like the sensitive plant Mimosa pudica, fold their leaves when touched. These behaviors do not require a nervous system. They are mediated by hormones, electrical signals, and mechanical responses that demonstrate the remarkable adaptability of plants to their environment.

Photosynthesis is the engine that drives life on Earth. It converts the energy of starlight into the chemical energy that powers virtually every living organism on the planet. It produces the oxygen that fills the atmosphere and makes aerobic life possible. It removes carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and helps regulate the climate. And it has been doing all of this, continuously, for nearly three billion years, one photon at a time, in every leaf on every plant on every continent, with an efficiency and a reliability that no human technology has ever matched.
