Splenic artery
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The splenic artery, also known as the lienal artery, is the largest branch of the celiac trunk and serves as the primary vessel supplying oxygenated blood to the spleen, while also providing vascularization to portions of the pancreas and stomach. It originates from the celiac trunk at the level of the lower border of the twelfth thoracic vertebra (T12) and follows a characteristically tortuous course, initially descending inferiorly before traveling horizontally to the left, passing along the superior border of the pancreas, anterior to the left kidney and adrenal gland, and posterior to the stomach, ultimately entering the spleen via the splenorenal ligament at the hilum. This artery measures approximately 1 cm in diameter at its origin and can extend up to 14 cm in length, with its winding path helping to accommodate the mobility of the spleen and pancreas during respiration and digestion.
Along its trajectory, the splenic artery emits several key branches that support adjacent structures, including multiple pancreatic arteriessuch as the dorsal, inferior (or transverse), and great pancreatic arteriesthat nourish the neck, body, and tail of the pancreas, as well as the posterior gastric artery and up to seven short gastric arteries that vascularize the fundus and upper greater curvature of the stomach. Near the splenic hilum, it also gives rise to the left gastroepiploic artery, which contributes to the blood supply of the stomach's greater curvature via anastomoses with the right gastroepiploic artery from the gastroduodenal artery. Upon reaching the spleen, the artery divides into superior and inferior terminal (lobar) branches, each further subdividing into 26 segmental intrasplenic branches that anastomose within the organ to ensure robust perfusion across its segments.
Anatomical variations of the splenic artery occur in a notable proportion of individuals, with approximately 8% originating directly from the abdominal aorta rather than the celiac trunk, and branching patterns differing between a "magistral" type (branches near the hilum) and a "distributing" type (branches along the course). These variations can influence surgical approaches, such as in splenectomy or pancreatic resections, where the artery's tortuosity and proximity to the pancreas increase the risk of intraoperative complications like bleeding or ischemia. The splenic artery supplies the spleen, enabling its roles in immune surveillance and blood filtration, while its branches support digestive processes in the foregut derivatives.

Anatomy

Origin and Course

The splenic artery is the largest branch of the celiac trunk, originating from its superolateral aspect as it arises from the anterior surface of the abdominal aorta at the level of the T12-L1 intervertebral disc, corresponding to the upper border of the duodenum. In adults, it has a mean diameter of approximately 5.9 mm at its origin, with a typical range of 5-7 mm, though this can vary by sex and ethnicity, being larger in males.
From its origin, the splenic artery follows a highly tortuous course, measuring about 12-15 cm in length, directed leftward and posteriorly along the superior border of the pancreas within the splenorenal ligament. Along most of its path, it is accompanied by the splenic vein and associated lymphatics, which parallel its trajectory posterior to the stomach and adjacent to the pancreatic tail.
Near the splenic hilum, the artery divides into superior and inferior terminal branches that enter the spleen to supply its segments.

Branches

The splenic artery gives rise to multiple branches along its course, primarily supplying the pancreas, stomach, and spleen. These branches emerge at various points from its origin at the celiac trunk to its termination at the splenic hilum.
Among the initial branches are those to the pancreas. The dorsal pancreatic artery arises from the proximal third of the splenic artery and courses along the posterior margin of the pancreas, distributing to its body and tail. The greater pancreatic artery represents the largest pancreatic branch; it originates near the two-thirds mark along the splenic artery and extends along the posterior or inferior margin of the pancreas, supplying the body and tail. The inferior (transverse) pancreatic artery is a smaller recurrent branch that arises variably from the splenic artery or the greater pancreatic artery and runs transversely along the inferior border of the pancreas, forming arcades to supply the body.
Nearer the splenic hilum, gastric branches predominate. The short gastric arteries, typically five to seven in number, originate from the distal splenic artery and pass through the gastrosplenic ligament to reach the fundus of the stomach, where they distribute via the gastric wall. The left gastroepiploic artery arises adjacent to the hilum from the splenic artery, courses along the greater curvature of the stomach, and anastomoses with the right gastroepiploic artery while supplying the greater curvature. Additionally, a small posterior gastric artery may emerge from the splenic artery to supply the posterior wall of the stomach, though its presence varies.
At the splenic hilum, the splenic artery terminates by dividing into two to three branches, commonly including superior and inferior segmental arteries, which enter the spleen via its trabeculae.

Relations

The splenic artery courses posterior to the stomach, with the lesser sac (omental bursa) intervening between them, providing a potential space that separates the two structures along much of the artery's tortuous path. Occasionally, the transverse colon or its splenic flexure may lie anterior to the artery near the splenic hilum, particularly in variations of peritoneal attachments.
Posteriorly, the splenic artery relates to the left kidney, running anterior to its upper pole within the splenorenal ligament, and is in close proximity to the left adrenal (suprarenal) gland, which lies superior and medial to the kidney. Additionally, the artery passes near the left crus of the diaphragm, contributing to its posterolateral relations in the upper abdomen.
The splenic artery maintains a superior position relative to the pancreas, coursing along its superior border and often classified as suprapancreatic in approximately 70% of cases, though it may embed within the pancreatic tissue in about 15% of individuals. Inferiorly, it lies superior to the splenic vein, which runs along the posterior surface of the pancreas. The artery enters the spleen at the hilum via the splenorenal ligament (also known as the lienorenal ligament), traveling within its posterior layer alongside the tail of the pancreas and splenic vein.
In particular, the splenic artery exhibits close proximity to the tail of the pancreas, typically separated by 1-2 cm, as it approaches the splenic hilum.

Development and Variations

Embryology

The celiac trunk, from which the splenic artery derives, forms through the fusion of primitive ventral splanchnic arteries arising from the abdominal aorta during weeks 45 of gestation, coinciding with the differentiation of the gastrointestinal tract into foregut, midgut, and hindgut regions. These ventral splanchnic arteries represent segmental branches that supply the developing foregut derivatives, with the 10th segmental artery persisting to form the celiac trunk as the primary arterial supply to this region. This early vascular network is associated with the vitelline arterial system, which contributes to the formation of the celiac axis through longitudinal anastomoses along the primitive gut tube.
By week 6 of gestation (approximately Carnegie stage 14), the splenic artery emerges as a distinct branch of the celiac trunk, accompanying the initial splenic primordiuma mesodermal aggregation within the dorsal mesogastrium overlying the dorsal pancreatic endoderm. At this stage, primitive vessels are detectable in the dorsal mesogastrium, providing the foundational vascular supply to the emerging splenic bulge. The splenic primordium becomes more defined between Carnegie stages 14 and 17, marking the onset of organogenesis for the spleen.
During weeks 78 (Carnegie stages 1923), the splenic artery plays a key role in the rotational migration of the spleen, elongating and developing tortuosity as the organ shifts leftward within the abdomen in coordination with gastric and hepatic rotation via the dorsal mesogastrium. Vascular remodeling progresses with the formation of a splenic hilum after stage 20, where larger vessels enter the primordium, and parallel artery-vein arrangements become evident by stage 23. Initial multiple splenic arteries, arising from the mesenchymal growth of the spleen, undergo fusion into a single main trunk by approximately week 10, consolidating the vascular supply influenced by the expanding splenic mesenchyme. This process ensures efficient perfusion as the spleen transitions toward its mature hematopoietic and immune functions.

Anatomical Variations

The splenic artery most commonly originates from the celiac trunk, accounting for 99.11% of cases across large cadaveric and imaging studies. Less frequent variants include direct origin from the abdominal aorta (0.65%) or superior mesenteric artery (0.08%), with rare instances of hepatosplenic or gastrosplenic trunk formations reported in 3-5% of individuals in systematic reviews. These origin deviations arise during embryological development but are typically asymptomatic unless encountered in surgical contexts.
In terms of course, the splenic artery displays a range of trajectories, classified as straight in 43.03% of cases, sinusoidal (tortuous) in 27.58%, serpentine in 20.91%, and alternating in 8.48%. Relative to the pancreas, a suprapancreatic position predominates at 70-76%, with dorsal or retropancreatic courses in 10-24% and intrapancreatic segments in 4-15%; suprapancreatic variants are more prevalent in females (78.87%) than males (71.79%). Accessory polar arteries, supplying superior or inferior splenic poles, occur frequently, with superior polar branches in 60.27% and inferior in 19.69% of specimens.
Branching patterns vary significantly, with the artery often following a distributed configuration (36.06%) or bifurcation (Y-type, 43.94%), while magistral (single trunk) patterns appear in 20%. Terminal branching at the splenic hilum typically involves 2-4 vessels (82.73% for two, 9.70% for three, 3.03% for four), though early origin of the left gastroepiploic artery from the main trunk is noted in up to 30% of cases in anatomical dissections. In approximately 78.9% of instances, two primary branches enter the hilum, with three in 13.6%; absent short gastric arteries may be compensated by collateral vessels from the left gastric or gastroepiploic systems, though specific prevalence remains under 5% in reported series.
Tortuosity, often sinusoidal or serpentine, increases with age and is observed in nearly 50% of adults overall, with higher rates (up to 70%) in those over 50, potentially influenced by hemodynamic factors. Rare anomalies include complete duplication of the splenic artery (1-2% prevalence in cadaveric studies) or origin from the renal artery (<1%), both of which can complicate vascular imaging and interventions. Overall, anatomical variations in the splenic artery occur in 17-41% of the population based on meta-analytic estimates, underscoring the need for preoperative assessment in abdominal procedures.

Function

Supply to the Spleen

The terminal branches of the splenic artery, typically numbering two to five, enter the spleen at the hilum on the visceral surface, where they immediately divide into segmental arteries that supply distinct regions of the organ, including superior and inferior polar branches. These segmental arteries course through the splenic parenchyma without significant interconnections, giving rise to trabecular arteries that follow the supportive framework of the splenic capsule and trabeculae.
Within the spleen, the trabecular arteries branch into central arterioles, which are surrounded by the white pulpthe lymphoid tissue responsible for adaptive immune responsesand supply this compartment with oxygenated blood. Smaller branches from the central arterioles, known as penicillar arterioles (or ellipsoids), extend into the surrounding red pulp, the sinusoidal region involved in blood filtration, where they release blood directly into the open circulation or connect to venous sinuses. This internal arborization ensures targeted perfusion that supports the spleen's dual roles in immunity and hematopoiesis.
The splenic artery delivers a substantial blood flow of approximately 250 mL/min to the spleen in adults, representing about 5% of total cardiac output and enabling efficient filtration of aged red blood cells and immune cell activation. This high perfusion rate, equivalent to roughly 168 mL/min per 100 g of splenic tissue, is essential for the organ's metabolic demands and its capacity to sequester platelets and pathogens.
The splenic vascular bed exhibits an end-arterial configuration, lacking meaningful anastomoses between its segmental branches, which predisposes the spleen to localized ischemia and infarcts upon arterial occlusion in a specific segment. Additionally, the spleen maintains blood flow stability through autoregulation, wherein adjustments in vascular resistancemediated by myogenic responses and metabolic factorspreserve perfusion during systemic hypotension or pressure fluctuations.

Supply to Pancreas and Stomach

The splenic artery supplies the body and tail of the pancreas primarily through its pancreatic branches, which include the dorsal pancreatic artery, the inferior (or transverse) pancreatic artery, and the greater pancreatic artery. The dorsal pancreatic artery originates from the proximal segment of the splenic artery, shortly after its emergence from the celiac trunk, and descends posteriorly to the pancreatic neck, where it divides into branches that perfuse the body and tail while anastomosing with the superior pancreaticoduodenal artery to form a continuous arcade along the posterior surface. The inferior pancreatic artery arises from the midportion of the splenic artery and supplies the body of the pancreas, contributing to the transverse arcade. This arcade ensures uniform distribution of oxygenated blood to the exocrine acinar cells and endocrine islets throughout these pancreatic regions.
The greater pancreatic artery, often termed the arteria pancreatica magna, arises from the midportion of the splenic artery and travels inferiorly along the anterior surface of the pancreas, parallel to the pancreatic duct. It anastomoses proximally with the dorsal pancreatic artery and distally with the inferior pancreatic artery, completing the transverse pancreatic arcade that provides redundant perfusion to the pancreatic body and tail, thereby supporting both digestive enzyme production and insulin secretion. These branches collectively deliver a substantial portion of the splenic artery's flow to the pancreas, with interconnections to branches of the superior mesenteric artery preventing isolated ischemia.
For the stomach, the splenic artery contributes via the posterior gastric artery, the short gastric arteries, and the left gastroepiploic artery, all originating from its distal segment near the splenic hilum. The posterior gastric artery supplies the posterior surface of the gastric fundus. The short gastric arteries, typically five to seven in number, course through the gastrosplenic ligament to reach the gastric fundus, where they form intramural plexuses that nourish the mucosal and submucosal layers of this region. The left gastroepiploic artery emerges as a terminal branch and extends medially along the greater curvature of the stomach, approximately 2 cm inferior to the gastric wall, supplying the anterior and posterior aspects of the body while anastomosing with the right gastroepiploic artery near the pylorus. Additionally, the left gastroepiploic artery gives off branches to the greater omentum, perfusing its adipose and lymphoid tissues.
Overall, these gastric branches account for a notable share of the splenic artery's output to the stomach, complemented by collateral pathways such as anastomoses between the short gastric arteries and the left gastric artery, as well as connections via the gastroduodenal artery through the right gastroepiploic branch, which maintain perfusion during potential occlusions. This interconnected network underscores the splenic artery's role in sustaining gastric and pancreatic function beyond its primary splenic delivery.

Clinical Significance

Aneurysms

Splenic artery aneurysms (SAAs) represent a significant clinical entity due to their potential for rupture, which can lead to life-threatening hemorrhage. These aneurysms form through degenerative changes in the arterial wall or as a result of external disruption, and they are classified into true aneurysms, which involve all three layers of the vessel wall (intima, media, and adventitia) with thinning of the medial layer, and pseudoaneurysms (also known as false aneurysms), which arise from a tear in the arterial wall and are contained by surrounding hematoma or perivascular tissue rather than the full vessel wall.
The prevalence of SAAs in the general population ranges from 0.04% to 0.1% based on autopsy studies and up to 0.78% on angiography, making them the third most common intra-abdominal aneurysm after those of the aorta and iliac arteries, and the most frequent type of splanchnic artery aneurysm, accounting for 60% to 70% of visceral artery aneurysms. They occur 3 to 9 times more frequently in women than in men.
Key risk factors for SAA formation include multiparity, hypertension, atherosclerosis, and portal hypertension, which contribute to medial degeneration and weakening of the arterial wall. Pregnancy poses a particularly elevated risk due to hormonal influences (such as increased estrogen and progesterone levels) and hemodynamic changes (including increased cardiac output and intra-abdominal pressure), with approximately two-thirds of ruptures occurring in the third trimester and an overall rupture risk during pregnancy estimated at up to 25% in some series. Chronic pancreatitis and trauma are primary precipitants for pseudoaneurysms, while true aneurysms more commonly arise from degenerative processes. The tortuous course of the splenic artery may predispose certain segments to aneurysm development, as noted in anatomical descriptions.
Most SAAs are asymptomatic and discovered incidentally on imaging, but symptomatic cases may present with epigastric or left upper quadrant pain, nausea, or vomiting due to mass effect or local inflammation. Rupture, though rare in the general population (occurring in 2% to 3% of cases), manifests acutely with severe abdominal pain, hypotension, and signs of hypovolemic shock from hemoperitoneum; untreated rupture carries a mortality rate of 10% to 25%, rising to 25% to 75% in pregnant women and up to 95% for the fetus. Pseudoaneurysms have a higher propensity for rupture compared to true aneurysms, often linked to their etiology in pancreatitis or trauma.
Diagnosis relies on imaging modalities, with contrast-enhanced computed tomography (CT) angiography serving as the gold standard for detecting aneurysm location, size, and morphology, while ultrasound can provide initial screening and Doppler assessment of flow. Magnetic resonance angiography (MRA) offers a non-ionizing alternative, and conventional catheter angiography remains useful for both diagnosis and therapeutic planning in complex cases. Anatomical variations, such as aberrant origins, may increase diagnostic challenges and rupture risk in select patients.
Management of SAAs prioritizes rupture prevention, with endovascular coil embolization emerging as the preferred approach for unruptured aneurysms due to its minimally invasive nature and high technical success rate of approximately 90% to 98%, particularly effective for saccular or distal lesions while preserving splenic perfusion. For ruptured cases or when endovascular access is unsuitable, surgical options include arterial ligation, aneurysm resection, or splenectomy, which carry lower success rates but are essential in emergencies. Surveillance with serial imaging is recommended for unruptured true aneurysms smaller than 2 cm that are asymptomatic and stable, especially in patients with comorbidities; however, intervention is indicated for aneurysms exceeding 3 cm, those showing growth, symptomatic lesions, or any size in women of childbearing potential per Society for Vascular Surgery guidelines. Similar recommendations are provided in the 2025 European Society for Vascular Surgery guidelines, suggesting treatment for asymptomatic aneurysms 30 mm. Pseudoaneurysms generally warrant prompt intervention regardless of size due to their instability.

Trauma and Surgical Considerations

The splenic artery is susceptible to injury in blunt abdominal trauma, with vascular injuries, including lacerations, occurring in approximately 20% of cases of blunt splenic trauma. Such lacerations often result from deceleration forces that shear the vessel against fixed points like the pancreatic tail, leading to active extravasation and hemodynamic instability if untreated. Post-injury pseudoaneurysm formation is a recognized complication, reported in 2-27% of non-operatively managed splenic injuries, potentially causing delayed hemorrhage days to weeks after the initial event. In non-operative management of stable patients, transcatheter embolization serves as a key hemostatic intervention, achieving splenic salvage rates exceeding 90% by targeting the bleeding site to control hemorrhage while preserving organ function.
During splenectomy, the splenic artery is typically ligated at the hilum to facilitate spleen removal, though proximal ligation near its origin from the celiac trunk may be employed in emergent cases to rapidly control massive bleeding; this approach preserves collateral flow via the short gastric arteries to the stomach fundus, minimizing gastric ischemia. The artery's intimate proximity to the pancreatic tail heightens the risk of pancreatic injury during dissection, with postoperative pancreatic fistula occurring in up to 20% of cases, often requiring drainage and nutritional support. In distal pancreatectomy for lesions in the pancreatic tail, the splenic artery is divided near the splenic hilum, relying on the Warshaw technique for splenic preservation through short gastric collaterals; reconstruction of the artery is occasionally needed if proximal pancreatic head vascularity must be maintained, such as in combined resections.
Endovascular interventions, including transcatheter embolization, are increasingly utilized for iatrogenic bleeding, such as post-biopsy hemorrhage, where selective coil placement targets the site of extravasation to achieve hemostasis without surgery. Proximal coil embolization reduces overall splenic perfusion pressure for diffuse bleeding, while distal embolization isolates focal lesions, both preserving viable splenic tissue through collaterals but with varying risks of infarction. Postoperatively, patients require close monitoring for rebleeding, which carries a 5% risk following embolization, often necessitating repeat angiography. Partial splenic infarction from embolization can predispose to abscess formation in 3-5% of cases, typically managed percutaneously with drainage to avoid splenectomy. Anatomical variations, such as a high origin or tortuosity (detailed in the Anatomical Variations section), may complicate precise ligation or embolization, potentially increasing procedural time or failure rates.

Other Pathologies

Thromboembolic occlusion of the splenic artery, frequently originating from cardiac sources such as atrial fibrillation, represents a primary cause of splenic infarction, accounting for 29-67% of cases in adults over 40 years old. This embolic event disrupts blood flow, leading to segmental ischemia and potential hypersplenism due to compensatory splenic hyperactivity in partially preserved parenchyma. In patients with infective endocarditis, splenic infarcts occur in up to 19% of cases, often from septic emboli lodging in arterial branches.
Atherosclerotic narrowing of the splenic artery is uncommon, comprising less than 5% of visceral arterial stenoses, and typically manifests in older patients with widespread atherosclerosis. Vasculitis, such as polyarteritis nodosa, can induce segmental stenosis through necrotizing inflammation of medium-sized vessels, including the splenic artery, leading to symptoms like left upper quadrant pain from ischemia. These conditions may progress to infarction if untreated, with pain exacerbated by postprandial states due to increased demand.
Infectious involvement of the splenic artery often arises from bacterial endocarditis, causing endarteritis and mycotic changes that compromise vessel integrity. Additionally, abscesses can extend from adjacent pancreatic inflammation in acute or chronic pancreatitis, eroding into the perivascular space and leading to secondary arterial infection or occlusion.
Compression syndromes, notably median arcuate ligament syndrome, exert extrinsic pressure on the celiac artery origin, indirectly reducing splenic artery inflow and potentially causing flow reversal or ischemia during expiration. This rare condition may precipitate splenic infarcts in susceptible individuals.
Diagnostic evaluation relies on Doppler ultrasound to assess flow velocity, with elevated peak systolic velocities (>200 cm/s) indicating stenosis in the celiac or splenic arteries. CT angiography provides detailed visualization of stenotic segments, offering a sensitivity of approximately 95% for detecting significant visceral artery narrowing compared to digital subtraction angiography.
