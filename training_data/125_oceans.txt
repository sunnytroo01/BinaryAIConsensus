The oceans cover approximately seventy-one percent of the Earth's surface and contain about ninety-seven percent of all the water on the planet. They are the dominant feature of Earth as seen from space, giving our planet its distinctive blue appearance and earning it the nickname "the blue marble." The oceans regulate the climate, produce more than half of the world's oxygen, absorb a quarter of all carbon dioxide emissions, support the most diverse ecosystems on Earth, and provide food and livelihoods for billions of people. They are, in every meaningful sense, the life support system of the planet.

There are five named oceans, though they are all connected and form a single continuous body of salt water. The Pacific Ocean is the largest, covering more area than all the land on Earth combined. It stretches from the Americas in the east to Asia and Australia in the west, and from the Arctic in the north to Antarctica in the south. The Atlantic Ocean is the second largest, separating the Americas from Europe and Africa. The Indian Ocean lies south of Asia, between Africa and Australia. The Southern Ocean encircles Antarctica. The Arctic Ocean covers the North Pole and is the smallest and shallowest of the five.

The average depth of the ocean is approximately twelve thousand one hundred feet, or about two point three miles. The deepest point is the Challenger Deep in the Mariana Trench, in the western Pacific Ocean, which reaches a depth of approximately thirty-six thousand feet, nearly seven miles below the surface. If you placed Mount Everest at the bottom of the Mariana Trench, its peak would still be more than a mile underwater. The pressure at the bottom of the trench is approximately fifteen thousand pounds per square inch, more than a thousand times the atmospheric pressure at sea level.

Ocean currents are rivers of water flowing within the ocean, driven by differences in temperature, salinity, and wind. Surface currents are driven primarily by wind and the rotation of the Earth. They form large circular patterns called gyres: clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and counterclockwise in the Southern Hemisphere. The Gulf Stream, one of the most important ocean currents, carries warm water from the Gulf of Mexico northeastward across the Atlantic, warming the climate of Western Europe. Without the Gulf Stream, London would have a climate similar to Labrador in Canada, despite being at the same latitude.

Deep ocean currents, also called thermohaline circulation, are driven by differences in water density caused by temperature and salinity. Cold, salty water is denser than warm, fresh water and sinks to the ocean floor. In the North Atlantic, cold water sinks near Greenland and flows southward along the ocean floor, eventually reaching the Southern Ocean and spreading into the Indian and Pacific Oceans. This global conveyor belt, as it is sometimes called, takes approximately one thousand years for a parcel of water to complete the circuit. It plays a crucial role in distributing heat around the planet and regulating global climate.

The ocean is home to an astonishing diversity of life, from microscopic phytoplankton that produce oxygen through photosynthesis to the blue whale, the largest animal that has ever lived on Earth, reaching lengths of up to one hundred feet and weights of up to two hundred tons. Coral reefs, sometimes called the rainforests of the sea, cover less than one percent of the ocean floor but support approximately twenty-five percent of all marine species. The Great Barrier Reef off the coast of Australia is the largest living structure on Earth, visible from space, stretching over fourteen hundred miles.

The deep ocean, below the reach of sunlight, was once thought to be a barren wasteland. The discovery of hydrothermal vents on the ocean floor in 1977 shattered that assumption. These vents, found along mid-ocean ridges where tectonic plates are spreading apart, release superheated water rich in dissolved minerals and chemicals. Around these vents, entire ecosystems thrive in total darkness, powered not by sunlight but by chemosynthesis, the process by which bacteria convert chemical energy from hydrogen sulfide and other compounds into organic molecules. Giant tube worms, clams, crabs, and shrimp cluster around the vents in densities that rival the most productive ecosystems on the surface.

The ocean absorbs approximately thirty percent of the carbon dioxide produced by human activities, which has helped to slow the rate of climate change but has come at a cost: ocean acidification. When carbon dioxide dissolves in seawater, it forms carbonic acid, which lowers the pH of the water. The ocean's pH has already decreased by approximately 0.1 units since the beginning of the industrial era, representing a thirty percent increase in acidity. This acidification threatens organisms that build shells or skeletons from calcium carbonate, including corals, mollusks, and some species of plankton that form the base of marine food chains.

Overfishing is one of the most serious threats to ocean ecosystems. Modern industrial fishing fleets, equipped with sonar, GPS, and enormous nets that can capture entire schools of fish in a single haul, have depleted fish populations across the globe. According to the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations, approximately thirty-four percent of the world's fish stocks are overfished, meaning they are being harvested at a rate that exceeds their ability to reproduce. The collapse of the Atlantic cod fishery off the coast of Newfoundland in 1992, which destroyed an industry that had sustained communities for five hundred years, is a cautionary example of what happens when fishing exceeds the ocean's capacity to regenerate.

Plastic pollution is an increasingly visible threat to ocean health. An estimated eight million tons of plastic enter the ocean every year, where it breaks down into smaller and smaller pieces but never fully degrades. These microplastics are now found in every part of the ocean, from the surface to the deepest trenches, and have been detected in the tissues of marine organisms at every level of the food chain, from plankton to whales. The Great Pacific Garbage Patch, a concentration of floating debris in the North Pacific, covers an area roughly twice the size of Texas.

We have explored more of the surface of the Moon than we have of the ocean floor. Despite its proximity and its importance to our survival, the deep ocean remains one of the least explored environments on Earth. More than eighty percent of the ocean has never been mapped, observed, or explored by humans. The technologies needed to explore the deep ocean, including pressure-resistant submersibles, remotely operated vehicles, and autonomous underwater drones, are advancing rapidly, but the scale of the task is enormous. The ocean is deep, dark, cold, and vast, and it guards its secrets with the patience of something that has been keeping them for four billion years.
