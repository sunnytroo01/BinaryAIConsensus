 Jack DeJohnette (August 9, 1942 October 26, 2025) was an American jazz drummer, pianist, and composer, widely regarded as one of the most influential and versatile percussionists in the genre's history. Born in Chicago, Illinois, he began his musical training on piano at age four and later transitioned to drums during high school, drawing early influences from local jazz scenes, Ahmad Jamal, and the blues. Over a six-decade career, DeJohnette shaped modern jazz through innovative rhythms blending post-bop, fusion, and world music elements, earning acclaim for his dynamic sideman roles and leadership in acclaimed ensembles.
DeJohnette's breakthrough came in the 1960s amid Chicago's vibrant jazz community, where he studied at the Chicago Conservatory of Music and performed with emerging talents. He gained prominence as a sideman, joining the Charles Lloyd Quartet in 1966 for the landmark live album Forest Flower and briefly playing with John Coltrane's quintet that same year. His pivotal association with Miles Davis began in 1969, replacing Tony Williams and contributing to the groundbreaking fusion recording Bitches Brew, which propelled jazz into electric territories during Davis's band from 1969 to 1972. These collaborations extended to artists like Herbie Hancock, Ornette Coleman, and Sonny Rollins, showcasing DeJohnette's adaptability across acoustic and electric contexts.
As a bandleader, DeJohnette formed influential groups such as Directions in the 1970s, the avant-garde Special Edition in the 1980s, and the acoustic trio Gateway with John Abercrombie and Dave Holland. He co-led the enduring Keith Jarrett/Gary Peacock/Jack DeJohnette trio starting in 1983, producing dozens of ECM Records albums that emphasized intuitive interplay and standards repertoire. DeJohnette's compositional output included over 20 leader albums, from his debut The DeJohnette Complex (1968) to later works like Made in Chicago (2015), a reunion with Association for the Advancement of Creative Musicians (AACM) peers. His stylecharacterized by polyrhythmic grooves, melodic sensitivity, and incorporations of African and Latin influencesearned him repeated DownBeat magazine "Drummer of the Year" honors.
DeJohnette received numerous accolades, including the National Endowment for the Arts Jazz Masters Award in 2012, the highest U.S. honor for jazz excellence, and two Grammy Awards: Best New Age Album for Peace Time (2009) and Best Jazz Instrumental Album for Skyline (2022). He also held an honorary doctorate from Berklee College of Music (1991) and the French Grand Prix du Disque (1979). DeJohnette passed away on October 26, 2025, in Kingston, New York, from congestive heart failure at age 83, leaving a legacy as a bridge between jazz traditions and innovations that continues to inspire generations of musicians.

Biography

Early life and musical beginnings

Jack DeJohnette was born on August 9, 1942, in Chicago, Illinois, into a family that placed a high priority on music appreciation. His mother, Eva Jeanette Wood DeJohnette, was a songwriter who penned early versions of tunes like "Stormy Monday," while his uncle, Roy I. Wood (also known as Roy L. Wood Sr.), was a prominent jazz disc jockey on Chicago's WVON radio station, exposing young Jack to a rich array of recordings by artists such as Duke Ellington and Count Basie. This environment immersed him in gospel, blues, and jazz from an early age, fostering a deep affinity for diverse musical styles.
DeJohnette began studying classical piano at age four, taking private lessons and later attending the Chicago Conservatory of Music, where he trained formally for about ten years. He also studied at the American Conservatory of Music, developing a strong foundation in classical repertoire that he practiced daily alongside emerging interests in jazz. By age 14, during high school, DeJohnette expanded his instrumental palette by joining the school concert band on drums, an opportunity sparked when a fellow musician left a drum set at his home; he taught himself the instrument while continuing piano. Influenced by pioneering drummers like Max Roach and Art Blakey, whose records he emulated, DeJohnette honed his skills through persistent practice, blending classical precision with jazz's rhythmic vitality.
In the mid-1960s, DeJohnette entered Chicago's vibrant jazz scene, performing as a sideman on both piano and drums in local clubs and ensembles. He collaborated with key figures from the Association for the Advancement of Creative Musicians (AACM), including Muhal Richard Abrams, Roscoe Mitchell, and Joseph Jarman, contributing to groups that explored hard bop, R&B, and avant-garde jazz. These experiences included brief stints with visiting artists like John Coltrane, whose quintet he joined for performances in 1966 alongside Rashied Ali, marking his immersion in Coltrane-inspired improvisational approaches. DeJohnette's early recordings emerged around this time, with his debut as a leader, The DeJohnette Complex, released in 1968 on Milestone Records, capturing the eclectic energy of his Chicago roots.

Breakthrough with Miles Davis

In 1969, Jack DeJohnette joined Miles Davis's Second Great Quintet as drummer, replacing Tony Williams following his acclaimed work with pianist Bill Evans. Davis had been impressed by DeJohnette's live performances and versatility, leading to his recruitment for what would become a transformative phase in the trumpeter's career. This period positioned DeJohnette at the forefront of Davis's exploration into electric instrumentation and fusion, bridging jazz traditions with rock and funk influences.
DeJohnette's contributions were central to several landmark recordings that defined jazz-rock fusion. On the double album Bitches Brew (1970), his layered, polyrhythmic drummingoften alongside Lenny White and othersprovided a pulsating foundation for Davis's experimental soundscapes, earning praise from Davis himself for delivering a "deep groove" that inspired the leader's improvisations. He followed with key roles on A Tribute to Jack Johnson (1971), where his energetic, driving beats supported the band's raw, film-inspired grooves, and On the Corner (1972), emphasizing street-funk rhythms that pushed Davis further into accessible, dance-oriented jazz. These albums not only showcased DeJohnette's technical prowess but also his ability to adapt to Davis's directive, open-ended sessions that incorporated electric bass, keyboards, and percussion.
Live performances during this era highlighted DeJohnette's innovations in blending genres. At the Isle of Wight Festival on August 29, 1970, before a massive rock audience of over 600,000, DeJohnette's powerful, rock-infused drumming on tracks like "Spanish Key" and "It's About That Time" energized the septetfeaturing Chick Corea and Keith Jarrett on electric keyboardsinfusing jazz improvisation with high-volume intensity and rhythmic drive. Similarly, the band's residencies at New York's Fillmore East in March and June 1970 captured DeJohnette propelling extended jams with funky, propulsive patterns that appealed to the venue's rock crowd while preserving improvisational depth, as heard on releases like Miles Davis at Fillmore.
DeJohnette departed Davis's band in early 1972, motivated by a growing desire to lead his own projects and explore independent creative paths. In his autobiography, Davis reflected on DeJohnette's unique style, describing his drumming as multidirectional and versatile, akin to "a blind man falling upstairs," underscoring the drummer's unpredictable yet supportive energy that had fueled the group's evolution toward jazz-rock. DeJohnette's work during these years significantly influenced Davis's shift to electric fusion, introducing rhythmic complexities and textural depth that expanded jazz's boundaries and inspired subsequent generations of musicians.

Solo career and leadership (1970s1980s)

Following his tenure with Miles Davis, Jack DeJohnette established himself as a prominent bandleader in the 1970s, building on his 1968 debut album The DeJohnette Complex, which featured an octet including Bennie Maupin and Eddie Gomez and showcased his compositional range blending post-bop and modal elements. In 1971, he co-formed the jazz-rock fusion cooperative Compost with Bob Moses on drums, Harold Vick on saxophone, Jack Gregg on bass, and Jumma Santos on percussion, releasing the self-titled album Compost that year and Life Is Round in 1973, both emphasizing rhythmic experimentation and electric instrumentation.
DeJohnette launched his Directions band in the mid-1970s, a quintet initially featuring guitarist John Abercrombie, saxophonist Alex Foster, bassist Mike Richmond, and later bassist Peter Warren, which explored fusion and free improvisation through albums like Cosmic Chicken (1975), Untitled (1976), and New Rags (1977). The band's sound drew from DeJohnette's electric jazz experiences, incorporating groove-oriented drumming and textural guitar work. Concurrently, the Gateway triocomprising DeJohnette, Abercrombie, and bassist Dave Hollanddebuted with the 1975 ECM album Gateway, a milestone in collective improvisation that emphasized sparse, interactive textures over dense fusion; the group continued with Gateway II in 1978, solidifying its reputation for subtle, acoustic interplay.
In 1978, DeJohnette formed the New Directions quartet with Abercrombie, trumpeter Lester Bowie, and bassist Eddie Gomez, releasing the ECM album New Directions that reflected his multifaceted career through extended, atmospheric pieces blending post-bop structures with avant-garde flourishes. Transitioning into the 1980s, he established Special Edition, a reed-heavy ensemble featuring tenor saxophonist David Murray and alto saxophonist Arthur Blythe alongside rotating members like bassist Peter Warren, which fused post-bop swing with avant-garde energy on albums including Special Edition (1979), Tin Can Alley (1980), Inflation Blues (1981), and Album Album (1984). This group highlighted DeJohnette's leadership in bridging mainstream jazz accessibility with experimental reed voicings and polyrhythmic propulsion.
DeJohnette's 1980s explorations extended to collaborative formats, such as the 1986 duo album In Our Style with Murray (augmented by bassist Fred Hopkins on select tracks), which delved into looser, conversational improvisation drawing on blues and world music inflections for a more intimate, exploratory dynamic. These endeavors underscored his evolution as a composer and leader, prioritizing ensemble cohesion and stylistic versatility over solo dominance.

Later career (1990s2025)

In the 1990s, DeJohnette solidified his role as a prolific bandleader through diverse ensembles, including the all-star project Parallel Realities (1990), featuring guitarist Pat Metheny, pianist Herbie Hancock, and bassist Dave Holland, which blended jazz fusion with expansive improvisation. He also explored more introspective territory on ECM Records, notably with Dancing with Nature Spirits (1996), a trio recording with pianist Michael Cain and multi-instrumentalist Steve Gorn that incorporated world music influences and ambient textures. These works reflected DeJohnette's maturing vision, bridging his fusion roots with contemplative, nature-inspired compositions.
Entering the 2000s, DeJohnette deepened his longstanding ECM partnership with albums like Invisible Nature (2002), a quartet effort emphasizing rhythmic innovation, and The Ripple Effect: Hybrids (2005), which fused electronics and acoustic elements. He formed successor projects to earlier trios, such as the collaborative Music We Are (2008) with pianist Danilo Prez and bassist John Patitucci, evoking the interactive spirit of classic piano trios while incorporating Latin jazz nuances. In the 2010s, DeJohnette marked significant reunions, including the AACM-inspired live album Made in Chicago (2015), recorded at the Chicago Jazz Festival with pianist Muhal Richard Abrams, bassist Larry Gray, saxophonist Roscoe Mitchell, and saxophonist Henry Threadgill, celebrating his Chicago origins through free improvisation and collective composition. He also led the intergenerational trio on In Movement (2016) with saxophonist Ravi Coltrane and bassist Matthew Garrison, paying homage to John Coltrane's legacy through reinterpreted standards and originals.
DeJohnette's later projects highlighted reflective collaborations, such as the Keith Jarrett Trio's archival live release After the Fall (2018), capturing a 1998 performance that reunited Miles Davis alumni Jarrett, bassist Gary Peacock, and DeJohnette in fluid standards interpretations. He co-founded the Hudson supergroup in 2017, joining bassist Larry Grenadier, keyboardist John Medeski, and guitarist John Scofield for an eponymous album inspired by the Hudson Valley's communal ethos, blending rock-inflected jazz with DeJohnette's signature propulsion. Throughout this period, DeJohnette remained active as an educator and mentor, conducting workshops and residencies at institutions like Berklee College of Music, where he shared insights on improvisation and ensemble dynamics with emerging musicians. Sound Travels (2012), included the poignant "Song for World Forgiveness," a meditative piece underscoring his lifelong commitment to peace-themed music.
DeJohnette passed away on October 26, 2025, at the age of 83 in Kingston, New York, from congestive heart failure. His death prompted widespread tributes from the jazz world, with peers and critics lauding his transformative influence on drumming, composition, and collaboration across six decades.

Musical Style and Technique

Drumming approach

DeJohnette's drumming style is renowned for its melodic and polyrhythmic qualities, heavily influenced by Elvin Jones's multi-limb independence and the textural freedoms of free jazz innovators like Sunny Murray. This approach treats the drum set as an extension of melodic expression, blending harmony, rhythm, and improvisation to elevate the drummer's role beyond mere timekeeping. In his 1981 instructional book co-authored with Charlie Perry, The Art of Modern Jazz Drumming: Multi-Directional Technique, DeJohnette articulates his philosophy of "multi-directional" playing, where each limb operates with autonomous yet interconnected purpose, creating layered polyrhythms that propel ensemble dynamics.
A hallmark of DeJohnette's technique involves versatile implement choices, including brushes for subtle, sweeping textures in ballads and mallets for resonant, orchestral tones on toms and cymbals during introspective passages. In fusion contexts, he incorporated electronics such as drum synthesizers to expand sonic palettes, adding synthetic swells and triggered effects to grooves during his tenure with Miles Davis's band in the late 1960s and early 1970s. These elements are evident in the pulsating, layered rhythms of Davis-era tracks like "Bitches Brew," where DeJohnette's backbeat fuses shuffle patterns with electronic-tinged propulsion. Later, in his Special Edition band during the late 1970s and 1980s, he reverted to acoustic setups for free jazz explorations, using brushes to navigate textural contrasts in improvisations alongside David Murray and Arthur Blythe.
DeJohnette developed signature patterns that define his quartet work, often described as the "DeJohnette swing"a buoyant yet nuanced feel that balances forceful drive with delicate subtlety, allowing space for soloists while maintaining forward momentum. This is showcased in his solos on Bitches Brew, where he layers cross-rhythms over odd-meter ostinatos, creating a hypnotic swing that shifts seamlessly between power and restraint. His tom work is tuned to a G dominant 7 chord for clarity, providing a resonant backdrop that enhances melodic elements.
Over his career, DeJohnette's style evolved from the acoustic post-bop precision of his early sideman rolesrooted in straight-ahead swing and fluid ride patternsto the electronic textures of his 1970s leadership projects. In albums like Directions (19701972) with John Abercrombie, he explored odd-meter feels such as 5/4 and 7/8, integrating synthesizer elements for ambient depth while preserving jazz's improvisational core. This progression reflected broader fusion trends, where DeJohnette's polyrhythms adapted to electric instrumentation without sacrificing melodic intent.
In workshops and interviews, DeJohnette's teaching philosophy stresses limb independence and mindful use of space, encouraging drummers to listen actively and create "breathing room" within rhythms to foster group interplay. He advocates practicing improvisation across meters and dynamics to build versatility, drawing from his own multi-instrumental background to emphasize drums as a narrative voice. This ethos extended into the 2010s, where electronic experiments in the Jack DeJohnette Groupfeaturing processed percussion and fusion electronicscontinued to push boundaries, blending acoustic sensitivity with digital augmentation for mercurial, genre-defying grooves.

Piano and compositional work

DeJohnette began studying classical piano at the age of four, undergoing formal training at the Chicago Conservatory of Music for a decade, which instilled in him a rigorous technical foundation emphasizing precision and expression. This early immersion in classical music profoundly shaped his transition to jazz improvisation, allowing him to blend structured phrasing with spontaneous melodic invention on the keyboard. His piano work on the album New Directions exemplifies this integration, particularly in the emotive, introspective soloing on "Silver Hollow," where his classical roots inform a lyrical, flowing improvisation.
DeJohnette's compositional style characteristically fuses modal jazz frameworksdrawing on sustained harmonies and scalar explorationwith infectious funk grooves and polyrhythmic elements inspired by world music traditions, creating expansive soundscapes that prioritize rhythmic vitality and textural depth. In his 1980s quartets, such as Special Edition, this approach manifests in pieces like "Silver Hollow," a contemplative ballad that layers modal progressions over subtle percussive undercurrents, and "Festival," an upbeat composition incorporating African-derived rhythms to evoke communal celebration. These works highlight his ability to craft multi-sectional forms that evolve organically, balancing introspection with energetic propulsion.
Within the Gateway trio, alongside guitarist John Abercrombie and bassist Dave Holland, DeJohnette occasionally assumed the role of pianist, contributing originals that underscore his melodic sensitivity and harmonic sophistication in a sparse ensemble setting. He further explored experimental territories, expanding the piano's palette beyond conventional jazz expression.
DeJohnette's piano influences prominently include Bill Evans, whose impressionistic voicings and trio interplay informed his own subtle harmonic layering during their 1960s collaboration, and McCoy Tyner, whose modal intensity and quartal harmonies shaped DeJohnette's approach to building rich, resonant chord progressions. In his later works, thematic evolution toward spiritual and nature-inspired motifs becomes evident, as in the expansive suites on Made in Chicago, where improvisational dialogues evoke a sense of rooted transcendence and organic flow, reflecting his deep connection to improvisational heritage.

Collaborations

Key groups and bands

DeJohnette formed the fusion-oriented band Directions in the mid-1970s, featuring core members saxophonist Alex Foster, guitarist John Abercrombie, pianist Warren Bernhardt, and bassist Mike Richmond. The ensemble debuted with the album Untitled in 1976, emphasizing electric instrumentation and improvisational grooves that blended jazz with rock elements. By 1978, DeJohnette formed New Directions as a quartet with trumpeter Lester Bowie from the Association for the Advancement of Creative Musicians, guitarist John Abercrombie, and bassist Eddie Gomez, which shifted its sound toward avant-garde explorations while retaining fusion underpinnings, as heard on the self-titled album New Directions and the live recording In Europe (1980). This configuration disbanded around 1981 after a series of recordings that highlighted DeJohnette's compositional versatility and leadership in navigating stylistic transitions.
In 1979, DeJohnette launched Special Edition, a cooperative band known for its rotating lineup of horn players that infused post-bop structures with avant-garde flair. The debut album Special Edition spotlighted saxophonists David Murray and Arthur Blythe alongside bassist Peter Warren, establishing a dynamic front line that balanced melodic themes with free improvisation. Subsequent personnel changes included tenor saxophonist Chico Freeman and baritone saxophonist John Purcell, contributing to the band's evolution through albums like Tin Can Alley (1980), a post-bop milestone praised for its tight ensemble work and DeJohnette's propulsive drumming that anchored extended solos. Special Edition remained active until 1989, releasing further works such as Inflation Blues (1983) and Album Album (1984) that underscored DeJohnette's commitment to nurturing emerging talents within a flexible, horn-driven format.
DeJohnette co-led the Gateway trio with guitarist John Abercrombie and bassist Dave Holland starting in 1975, creating an enduring outlet for introspective, interactive jazz. The debut album Gateway (1976) featured originals by all three members, emphasizing subtle interplay and textural nuance over virtuosic display. After a hiatus, the trio reconvened in the 1990s, releasing Homecoming (1995), which reflected matured compositions and a deeper collective empathy honed through years of collaboration. Further reunions produced In the Moment (1996) and Page of Illusion (2012), marking the group's final studio effort before Abercrombie's death ended its run, though live performances persisted intermittently until 2012.
During the 1990s and 2000s, DeJohnette explored quartet and trio formats with prominent collaborators, expanding his leadership into broader jazz-rock hybrids. In 1990, he co-led a touring quartet with pianist Herbie Hancock, guitarist Pat Metheny, and bassist Dave Holland, capturing their chemistry on the live album Parallel Realities, which fused acoustic swing with electric textures. Later in the decade and into the 2000s, partnerships with guitarist John Scofield yielded the organ trio Trio Beyondfeaturing organist Larry Goldingswhose debut Saudades (2006) honored the legacy of pianist Larry Young through modal explorations and rhythmic drive. This evolved into the Hudson quartet with Scofield, keyboardist John Medeski, and bassist Larry Grenadier, debuting with the self-titled album in 2017 and blending covers of Joni Mitchell and The Band with originals that celebrated DeJohnette's Woodstock roots and 75th birthday.
In the 2010s, DeJohnette formed a forward-looking trio with saxophonist Ravi Coltrane and bassist Matt Garrison, drawing on historical tiesDeJohnette had played with their fathers, John Coltrane and Jimmy Garrisonin service of contemporary improvisation. The ensemble's ECM debut In Movement (2015) reinterpreted Coltrane repertoire alongside new pieces, showcasing DeJohnette's piano contributions and the group's telepathic rapport. Active through live tours into the late 2010s, including performances in 2019, the trio dissolved following DeJohnette's death in October 2025, leaving a legacy of bridging generational and stylistic divides.

Sideman and guest roles

DeJohnette's early sideman work with saxophonist Charles Lloyd from 1966 to 1968 helped define a transitional era in jazz. Joining Lloyd's quartet, which included pianist Keith Jarrett and bassists Cecil McBee and Ron McClure, DeJohnette provided rhythmic drive on live albums like Forest Flower: Charles Lloyd at Monterey (1966), recorded at the Monterey Jazz Festival. This release captured the group's fusion of cool jazz lyricism with free jazz spontaneity, achieving crossover appeal to rock audiences and earning critical acclaim for its innovative energy.
DeJohnette's collaborations with Keith Jarrett, initially forged in the Lloyd quartet, evolved into a enduring partnership in the Standards Trio with bassist Gary Peacock starting in 1983. Serving as sideman on Jarrett-led recordings, DeJohnette anchored sessions for albums such as Standards, Vol. 1 (1983) and Changes (1983), where his nuanced drumming supported explorations of the Great American Songbook alongside original material. The trio's interplay emphasized DeJohnette's ability to blend swing precision with avant-garde flexibility, influencing subsequent jazz trio aesthetics over decades.
DeJohnette made impactful guest appearances with Herbie Hancock, notably in the acoustic quartet featuring Pat Metheny and Dave Holland during the early 1990s. This ensemble, reuniting elements of Miles Davis's alumni in an all-acoustic format reminiscent of VSOP reunions from the 1970s and 1980s, performed extensively and released DeJohnette, Hancock, Holland, Metheny in Concert (1991), showcasing DeJohnette's supportive role in blending post-bop structures with contemporary improvisation.
In the 1990s and 2000s, DeJohnette contributed to reissues of Bill Evans Trio material from his 1968 stint, including live tracks from Bill Evans at Montreux that highlighted his early empathetic interplay with the pianist and bassist Eddie Gmez. He also guested with the Pat Metheny Group on select recordings and tours, adding textural depth to their fusion-oriented sound. In 1999, DeJohnette performed a series of duo concerts with Irish guitarist Mark O'Leary in Cork, Ireland. More recently, in the 2010s, DeJohnette joined Sonny Rollins for international tours and appeared on Road Shows, Vol. 2 (2011), where his mature, interactive drumming complemented Rollins's thematic tenor explorations.
Among DeJohnette's one-off sessions, he filled in for live performances with Thelonious Monk in the late 1960s, absorbing the pianist's angular rhythms during club dates. He participated as sideman on Ornette Coleman's Song X (1985), a collaborative electric venture with Metheny that expanded harmolodic concepts into rock-influenced jazz. In the 2000s, DeJohnette guested on McCoy Tyner's Guitars (2008), sharing the date with Brad Mehldau on piano for tracks featuring guest guitarists, demonstrating his adaptability in ensemble settings.

Discography

As leader

DeJohnette's debut album as a leader, The DeJohnette Complex (1969, Milestone), featured a sextet including Charles Lloyd and Bennie Maupin, blending post-bop improvisation with emerging fusion elements and marking his emergence as a compositional force beyond sideman roles. His early ECM effort, Pictures (1977, ECM), was a duo with guitarist John Abercrombie exploring atmospheric and textural interplay between drums, piano, and guitar.
In the 1970s, DeJohnette's ECM phase highlighted fusion experimentation, with albums like Sorcery (1974, Prestige) featuring Bennie Maupin on reeds, John Abercrombie on guitar, and Dave Holland on bass, incorporating rock influences to bridge jazz traditions with progressive sounds. This evolved in New Rags (1977, ECM), with Abercrombie and bassist Mike Richmond, delving into lyrical and rhythmic grooves that underscored DeJohnette's melodic sensitivity.
The 1980s saw DeJohnette shift toward the Blue Note label for select projects, launching his Special Edition band with Special Edition (1980, ECM), a quintet including David Murray and Arthur Blythe that fused free jazz energy with structured grooves, earning acclaim for its dynamic ensemble interplay. The compilation Works (1985, ECM) included tracks from DeJohnette's earlier sessions, highlighting his contributions across small-group settings. In the same year, The Jack DeJohnette Piano Album (Landmark) showcased his piano work alongside Eddie Gmez on bass.
Entering the 1990s and 2000s, DeJohnette explored unity and spirituality in leadership, as in Oneness (1997, ECM), a quartet recording with Michael Cain on piano, Jerome Harris on guitar and bass, and Don Alias on percussion, evoking interconnectedness through improvised soundscapes. This thematic thread continued in Music in the Key of Om (2005, Golden Beams/Universal), a solo piano and drum album drawing from meditation and world music, reflecting his interest in healing through sound. Later, Music We Are (2008, Kindred Rhythm) reunited him in trio with Danilo Prez on piano and John Patitucci on bass, integrating Latin rhythms.
In the 2010s and 2020s, DeJohnette's leadership emphasized reunions and family ties, exemplified by After the Fall (2018, ECM), a trio with Ravi Coltrane and Matthew Garrison that paid homage to Elvin Jones through live recordings blending straight-ahead jazz with subtle innovation. Hudson (2021, Mack Avenue) brought together John Scofield, John Medeski, and Bill Frisell for an all-star jam rooted in upstate New York influences, celebrating communal creativity. Skyline (2021, Legacy), co-led with Ron Carter on bass and Gonzalo Rubalcaba on piano, earned a Grammy Award for Best Jazz Instrumental Album in 2022.

As sideman

DeJohnette's work as a sideman in the late 1960s and early 1970s was pivotal in shaping jazz fusion, particularly through his contributions to Miles Davis's groundbreaking albums. On Davis's Bitches Brew (1970), DeJohnette's drumming provided dynamic, layered rhythms that blended jazz improvisation with rock and electric elements, helping to pioneer the fusion genre's expansive sound. His innovative approach, including polyrhythmic patterns and textural depth, supported the album's chaotic yet cohesive ensemble interplay. Similarly, on Jack Johnson (1971), DeJohnette delivered propulsive, groove-oriented percussion that underscored the album's raw, film-score energy, with his steady yet flexible grooves complementing Davis's trumpet lines and the band's electric intensity.
In the mid-1960s, DeJohnette briefly served as a substitute drummer for John Coltrane's quartet, performing live alongside Coltrane, Jimmy Garrison, and Pharoah Sanders during engagements in New York, including at the Half Note club in 1966. These appearances showcased DeJohnette's ability to navigate Coltrane's intense, spiritual improvisations at a young age, though no official studio or posthumous recordings from these sessions have been released.
DeJohnette's association with Keith Jarrett began in the late 1960s and extended into the 1970s through Jarrett's American Quartet, featuring Dewey Redman on saxophone and Charlie Haden on bass. On sessions like Treasure Island (1974) and The Survivor (1977), DeJohnette's versatile drummingmarked by subtle swing, textural shadings, and responsive interplayenhanced the quartet's exploratory post-bop and free jazz explorations. While Jarrett's landmark solo album The Kln Concert (1975) featured no ensemble, DeJohnette's prior collaborations with Jarrett laid the groundwork for their enduring partnership in subsequent trios.
DeJohnette also contributed to Herbie Hancock's projects in the 1970s and 1980s, including live and recorded work that bridged acoustic jazz and fusion. Although not credited on the studio recording of Head Hunters (1973), DeJohnette's influence from shared Miles Davis band experiences informed Hancock's rhythmic innovations during that era's live performances. On the live album An Evening with Herbie Hancock and Chick Corea in Concert (1979), DeJohnette's drumming provided interactive energy in duo and trio settings alongside Hancock.
In the 2010s and early 2020s, DeJohnette continued as a sought-after sideman, participating in tours and recordings that highlighted his enduring adaptability. He joined Sonny Rollins for select performances, including Rollins's 80th birthday concerts in 2010 and collaborative shows in the mid-2010s, where his drumming added buoyant, interactive energy to Rollins's tenor explorations. DeJohnette also reunited with Chick Corea for live dates, such as the 2012 Seattle performance with Stanley Clarke, blending fusion grooves and improvisational flair in a nod to their shared history from the Miles Davis era.

Awards and Honors

Grammy achievements

Jack DeJohnette earned two Grammy Awards during his career, along with six nominations across jazz and new age categories. These accolades recognized his versatility as a leader, collaborator, and composer, spanning traditional jazz ensembles and more experimental works.
His first win came at the 51st Annual Grammy Awards in 2009, for Best New Age Album with Peace Time, a continuous hour-long solo piano composition that explored meditative and ambient soundscapes. The album highlighted DeJohnette's ability to transcend drumming, drawing on his classical piano training to create immersive, boundary-pushing music.
DeJohnette's second Grammy arrived at the 64th Annual Grammy Awards in 2022, winning Best Jazz Instrumental Album for Skyline, a trio recording with bassist Ron Carter and pianist Gonzalo Rubalcaba. The album exemplified his signature approach to rhythmic propulsion and interactive dialogue in small-group settings, earning praise for its elegant fusion of swing and modern improvisation.
Among his six nominations, several underscored his leadership in jazz instrumental projects. In 1999, he received a nomination for Best Jazz Instrumental Performance, Individual or Group. In 2005, Ivey Diveyfeaturing pianist Danilo Prez and interpreting Thelonious Monk's compositionswas nominated for Best Jazz Instrumental Album, Individual or Group. That same year, his ambient release Music in the Key of Om earned a nod for Best New Age Album. Subsequent nominations included The Out-of-Towners with Keith Jarrett and Gary Peacock (2005, Best Jazz Instrumental Album), Saudades with Trio Beyond (2007, Best Jazz Instrumental Album), and In Movement with Ravi Coltrane and Matthew Garrison (2017, Best Jazz Instrumental Album). These honors reflected the Recording Academy's expanding recognition of jazz's diverse subgenres during DeJohnette's active years.

Other recognitions

In 1979, DeJohnette received the French Grand Prix du Disque for his contributions to jazz recording. In 1989, he was awarded the ASCAP Jazz Vanguard Award, recognizing his innovative contributions to jazz composition and performance.
DeJohnette earned numerous victories in the DownBeat Critics' Poll for drums, reflecting his consistent influence on the instrument across decades.
In 1991, Berklee College of Music awarded DeJohnette an honorary doctorate of music for his enduring impact on jazz education and performance. He was inducted into the Modern Drummer Hall of Fame in 2007. He received the Doris Duke Performing Artist Award in 2012, a grant supporting mid-career artists whose work advances the field of jazz.
DeJohnette was inducted into the Percussive Arts Society's Hall of Fame in 2010, acknowledging his mastery of percussion techniques and their application in improvisational contexts. In 2019, he joined the University of Pittsburgh International Academy of Jazz Hall of Fame, celebrating his global stature in the genre.
DeJohnette was honored with the National Endowment for the Arts Jazz Masters Fellowship in 2012, the nation's highest accolade for lifetime achievement in jazz.
Following his death on October 26, 2025, from congestive heart failure, numerous tributes appeared in obituaries worldwide, highlighting his revolutionary drumming style and collaborations with icons like Miles Davis and Keith Jarrett. Posthumously, he was inducted into the DownBeat Hall of Fame in the magazine's December 2025 issue as its 176th member.

Publications

Instructional materials

Jack DeJohnette co-authored the instructional book The Art of Modern Jazz Drumming: Multi-Directional Technique in 1981 with Charlie Perry, published by Drum Center Publications and later distributed by Hal Leonard. The book emphasizes advanced drumming concepts tailored to jazz, including independence exercises that develop limb coordination across the drum set, polyrhythmic applications for phrasing and improvisation, and techniques for meter-within-meter playing to enhance rhythmic complexity. It covers topics such as interaction between drum parts, cymbal rhythms, triplet patterns, and independent performance strategies, providing exercises that encourage drummers to approach the kit as a melodic instrument.
DeJohnette contributed to Hal Leonard's drum method series through this publication, which serves as a guide for rudiments adapted to jazz contexts and ensemble playing dynamics, helping students integrate foundational stick control with improvisational freedom in group settings. The methods promote practical application in jazz ensembles, focusing on how rudiments like paradiddles and flams can support collective improvisation and rhythmic dialogue.
In addition to print resources, DeJohnette produced video instructional materials, including the 1992 DVD Musical Expression on the Drum Set released by Homespun Tapes and Hal Leonard, where he demonstrates techniques for conveying emotion and narrative through drumming. He conducted workshops at institutions like Berklee College of Music, such as a 2016 drum clinic series that explored creative approaches to the instrument, and presented at Percussive Arts Society International Convention (PASIC) events from the 1980s through the 2010s, including a notable 2009 clinic featuring performance demonstrations of improvisational strategies. These sessions highlighted creative improvisation, urging participants to blend technical proficiency with personal expression in jazz drumming.
DeJohnette's instructional works have influenced jazz pedagogy, with excerpts from The Art of Modern Jazz Drumming incorporated into curricula at conservatories for teaching multi-limb independence and polyrhythmic improvisation. His materials, including video content, continue to shape educational practices by prioritizing artistic development over rote technique.

Sheet music collections

Jack DeJohnette's sheet music publications primarily consist of transcribed scores that capture his compositions and improvisations for educational and performance purposes. The most comprehensive collection is The Jack DeJohnette Collection, published by Hal Leonard in 1997, which features transcribed scores for 14 of his contemporary jazz compositions. This volume includes melodies, harmonies, bass lines, vamps, and drum parts for pieces such as "Ahmad the Terrible," "Ebony," and "Herbie's Hand Cocked," allowing musicians to study and perform his multifaceted arrangements in ensemble settings.
Transcriptions of DeJohnette's drum solos and ensemble performances have appeared in jazz periodicals during the 1980s and 1990s, often in dedicated folios focused on his work with groups like Special Edition. For instance, DownBeat magazine has published detailed drum transcriptions of his solos, including a notable example from his recordings in the November 2019 issue, highlighting his innovative phrasing and polyrhythmic approach. These publications served as resources for jazz educators and drummers seeking to analyze his contributions to post-bop and fusion styles.
