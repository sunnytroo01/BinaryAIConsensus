Supersymmetric quantum mechanics
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Supersymmetric quantum mechanics (SUSY QM) is a formulation of quantum mechanics that incorporates supersymmetry, a symmetry relating bosonic (integer-spin) and fermionic (half-integer-spin) degrees of freedom, enabling the construction of exactly solvable Hamiltonians and the analysis of their spectral properties through factorization methods.
Introduced by Edward Witten in 1981 as a toy model in zero spatial dimensions to explore dynamical supersymmetry breaking, it provides a simplified arena for studying phenomena that appear in higher-dimensional supersymmetric field theories, such as the hierarchy problem and instanton effects.
In SUSY QM, the total Hilbert space is the direct sum of a bosonic subspace (spanned by coordinate eigenstates without fermionic excitations) and a fermionic subspace (with one fermionic excitation), with the supersymmetric Hamiltonian $ H $ expressed as $ H = \frac{1}{2} { Q, Q^\dagger } $, where $ Q $ and $ Q^\dagger $ are supercharge operators satisfying $ { Q, Q^\dagger } = 2H $ and $ = = 0 $.
This algebra ensures that non-zero energy eigenstates come in degenerate pairs, one bosonic and one fermionic, while the ground state (if it exists) is uniquely bosonic with zero energy if supersymmetry is unbroken.
The Hamiltonian factorizes into partner forms $ H = \begin{pmatrix} H_- & 0 \ 0 & H_+ \end{pmatrix} $, where $ H_- = A^\dagger A $ and $ H_+ = A A^\dagger $, with the lowering/raising operator $ A = \frac{\hbar}{\sqrt{2m}} \frac{d}{dx} + W(x) $ defined via a superpotential $ W(x) $ that generates partner potentials $ V_\pm (x) = W^2 (x) \pm \frac{\hbar}{\sqrt{2m}} \frac{dW}{dx} $.
SUSY QM connects to classical factorization techniques pioneered by Schrdinger and Dirac in the 1930s and 1940s, but extends them by systematically generating isospectral potentials and unbroken supersymmetric ground states.
Key applications include the systematic derivation of shape-invariant potentials (such as the harmonic oscillator, hydrogen atom, and Morse oscillator), whose spectra are analytically known, and the design of novel solvable models for periodic potentials, nonlinear differential equations, and condensed matter systems like Dirac electrons in graphene.
Furthermore, Witten's 1982 work linked SUSY QM to Morse theory in differential geometry, showing that the Witten index (a topological invariant counting the difference between bosonic and fermionic ground states) equals the Euler characteristic of the manifold, bridging quantum mechanics with topology.
Extensions to higher-order (nonlinear) supersymmetries and multi-dimensional settings have further broadened its scope, influencing areas from quantum optics to integrable systems.

Introduction and History

Overview

Supersymmetric quantum mechanics (SUSY QM) is a theoretical framework in quantum mechanics that incorporates supersymmetry, a symmetry relating bosonic degrees of freedom (integer spin states) to fermionic ones (half-integer spin states), thereby unifying their descriptions within a single Hilbert space. This setup constructs partner Hamiltonians whose spectra are nearly identical, differing at most in the ground state, which facilitates the exact solvability of the Schrdinger equation for certain one-dimensional potentials that are otherwise difficult to solve analytically.
The development of SUSY QM is motivated by the desire to explore supersymmetrya key feature of many proposed extensions to the Standard Model in particle physicsin a simplified, non-relativistic context that avoids the complexities of full quantum field theory. It also builds on historical factorization techniques for the Hamiltonian, originally introduced by Dirac and later refined by Infeld and Hull, to systematically generate solvable quantum systems and reveal deep connections between quantum mechanics and geometry.
Central concepts include the boson-fermion symmetry, realized through supercharges that interchange the two sectors while preserving the Hamiltonian's structure, and the Witten index, a topological invariant defined as the trace of (1)FeH(-1)^F e^{-\beta H}(1)FeH (where FFF is the fermion number operator and 0+\beta \to 0^+0+), which counts the net number of zero-energy states and is robust against perturbations. This index provides a criterion for supersymmetry breaking, as a non-zero value implies unbroken supersymmetry. SUSY QM assumes prior knowledge of standard quantum mechanics, such as the time-independent Schrdinger equation and the equidistant spectrum of the harmonic oscillator. For example, the harmonic oscillator in SUSY QM has a partner potential with the same spectrum above the ground state.

Historical Development

The roots of supersymmetric quantum mechanics (SUSY QM) trace back to the factorization method for solving quantum mechanical differential equations, initially explored by Schrdinger in the 1940s and systematically developed by Infeld and Hull in the early 1950s. This technique expressed the Hamiltonian as a product of first-order differential operators, enabling the algebraic construction of exact eigenvalues and eigenfunctions for specific potentials like the hydrogen atom and oscillator, and it prefigured the intertwined partner Hamiltonians central to SUSY QM.
The modern field of SUSY QM emerged in 1981 with Edward Witten's introduction of a one-dimensional model incorporating bosonic and fermionic degrees of freedom to investigate dynamical supersymmetry breaking in a simplified setting analogous to quantum field theory. In the 1980s, key extensions built on this foundation, including the 1983 concept of shape-invariant potentials by Gendenshtein, which allowed for the generation of infinite families of exactly solvable systems through parameter translations in the superpotential. Researchers such as Cooper, Khare, and Sukhatme further advanced these ideas in the mid-1980s, developing practical algorithms for identifying and solving shape-invariant potentials, thereby expanding the toolkit for algebraic spectral analysis.
In the 1990s, SUSY QM revealed profound connections to integrable systems, where partner potentials were linked to classical soliton equations like the Korteweg-de Vries hierarchy, facilitating the exploration of non-perturbative effects and infinite-dimensional symmetries in solvable models. The framework also gained recognition for enhancing semiclassical methods, such as the WKB approximation; Comtet, Bandrauk, and Campbell demonstrated in the 1980s that a supersymmetry-inspired WKB yields exact energy levels for shape-invariant potentials by resolving standard turning-point ambiguities. Similarly, links to statistical mechanics solidified through mappings of Fokker-Planck equations to SUSY Hamiltonians, enabling exact solutions for diffusion processes and equilibrium distributions in stochastic systems, as explored in works from the late 1980s onward.
By the 2020s, theoretical progress in SUSY QM has been incremental, with emphasis shifting toward numerical implementations for potentials lacking exact solvability, including variational quantum algorithms that leverage SUSY structures for simulating mixed boson-fermion dynamics on quantum hardware.

Examples

Harmonic Oscillator

The harmonic oscillator serves as the simplest and most illustrative example of supersymmetric quantum mechanics (SUSY QM), where the symmetry remains unbroken, leading to exact degeneracy in the energy spectrum for excited states. In the standard formulation, the potential is given by $ V(x) = \frac{1}{2} m \omega^2 x^2 $, with the ground-state wave function $ \psi_0(x) = \left( \frac{m \omega}{\pi \hbar} \right)^{1/4} e^{- m \omega x^2 / 2 \hbar} $. This potential admits a complete analytic solution in ordinary quantum mechanics, but SUSY QM provides a natural factorization that highlights the equidistant energy levels as a consequence of the underlying symmetry.
To construct the SUSY extension, one defines the superpotential $ W(x) = \omega \sqrt{\frac{m}{2}} x $, which generates the partner potentials through the relations $ V_-(x) = W^2(x) - \frac{\hbar}{ \sqrt{2m} } W'(x) $ and $ V_+(x) = W^2(x) + \frac{\hbar}{ \sqrt{2m} } W'(x) $, in the convention where the kinetic term is scaled appropriately. Substituting the superpotential yields partner potentials of identical shape but shifted by constants: $ V_-(x) = \frac{1}{2} m \omega^2 x^2 - \frac{\hbar \omega}{2} $ and $ V_+(x) = \frac{1}{2} m \omega^2 x^2 + \frac{\hbar \omega}{2} $. The corresponding partner Hamiltonians are $ H_- = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2}{dx^2} + V_-(x) $ and $ H_+ = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2}{dx^2} + V_+(x) $, which share the same spectrum except for the ground state of $ H_- $. The operators are $ A = \frac{\hbar}{\sqrt{2m}} \frac{d}{dx} + W(x) $ and $ A^\dagger = -\frac{\hbar}{\sqrt{2m}} \frac{d}{dx} + W(x) $, with $ H_- = A^\dagger A $ and $ H_+ = A A^\dagger $. This construction reveals the SUSY factorization of the harmonic oscillator Hamiltonian, explaining its algebraic solvability in a unified bosonic-fermionic framework.
The energy spectrum of $ H_- $ is $ E_n = n \hbar \omega $ for $ n = 0, 1, 2, \dots $, with ground state at zero energy, while $ H_+ $ has $ E_n = (n+1) \hbar \omega $ for $ n = 0, 1, 2, \dots $, matching $ H_- $ from the first excited state onward. Each level from $ \hbar \omega $ exhibits exact twofold degeneracy due to the pairing of bosonic and fermionic states. The ground state of $ H_- $ (corresponding to the standard $ \frac{1}{2} \hbar \omega $ after shifting) is annihilated by both supercharges $ Q $ and $ Q^\dagger $, confirming unbroken supersymmetry as there is no spontaneous breaking. For excited states, the wave functions involve Hermite polynomials: the $ n $-th state for $ H_+ $ is proportional to the operator $ A \psi_{n-1}^- $, where interleaving solutions between partners explicitly demonstrates the degeneracy $ \psi_n^+ \sim A \psi_{n-1}^- $.
In comparison to the non-supersymmetric treatment, SUSY QM naturally factorizes the Hamiltonian as $ H = { Q, Q^\dagger } / 2 $, revealing the equidistant spectrum as an artifact of the superalgebra rather than an ad hoc assumption, and underscoring the oscillator's role as a trivial case of shape invariance.

Shape-Invariant Potentials

Shape-invariant potentials represent a significant class of solvable models in supersymmetric quantum mechanics, where the partner potentials satisfy a specific transformation property that facilitates algebraic determination of the energy spectrum. Specifically, a potential is shape-invariant if the positive partner potential can be expressed as $ V_+(x; a) = V_-(x; a') + R(a) $, where $ a $ is a set of parameters, $ a' $ denotes a shifted value (e.g., $ a + \delta $), and $ R(a) $ is a remainder function independent of $ x $. This condition, first identified in the context of supersymmetry, ensures that the spectrum of the Hamiltonian can be obtained recursively from the ground-state energy without solving the full Schrdinger equation.
A prominent example is the hydrogen atom, where the radial Coulomb potential is treated as an effective one-dimensional problem. The effective potential takes the form $ V(r) = -\frac{2m e^2}{\hbar^2 (4\pi \epsilon_0) r} + \frac{l(l+1)}{r^2} $, incorporating the centrifugal term for angular momentum quantum number $ l $. In the supersymmetric framework, the superpotential is given by $ W(r) = \frac{l+1}{r} - k $, where $ k $ is a constant related to the binding strength, leading to partner potentials that shift the parameter $ a = l+1 $ to $ a' = a + 1 $, with $ R(a) = 2a $ (in appropriate units). This shape invariance mirrors the degeneracy structure of the hydrogen atom levels.
The energy spectrum for the hydrogen atom is derived via the supersymmetric recursion relation, starting from the ground-state energy and iterating the shape-invariance condition. The energies are $ E_n = -\frac{m e^4}{2 \hbar^2 (4\pi \epsilon_0)^2 (n + l + 1)^2} $, where $ n $ is the principal quantum number, matching the exact Rydberg formula obtained through traditional methods. This algebraic approach highlights how supersymmetry unifies the bound-state solutions across different $ l $ values.
Other notable shape-invariant potentials include the Morse potential, $ V(x) = D_e (1 - e^{-\alpha (x - x_e)})^2 $, which models diatomic molecular vibrations and exhibits shape invariance under shifts in the energy parameter, yielding equally spaced vibrational levels up to dissociation. The Pschl-Teller potential, $ V(x) = \frac{\hbar^2 \alpha^2}{2m} \left( \frac{s(s-1)}{\sin^2 (\alpha x)} + \frac{s(s-1)}{\cos^2 (\alpha x)} \right) $, is relevant for scattering problems and reflectionless potentials. Classically, six families of such potentials were classified via the factorization method, encompassing the harmonic oscillator, Coulomb (hydrogen), three-dimensional oscillator, Morse, Scarf (trigonometric and hyperbolic), and Rosen-Morse potentials, all of which admit exact supersymmetric solutions.
The primary advantage of shape-invariant potentials lies in their ability to generate the complete bound-state spectrum and wave functions iteratively from the ground state, bypassing numerical solution of the Schrdinger equation and providing insight into the algebraic structure underlying exactly solvable systems.

Advanced Topics

Shape Invariance Method

The shape invariance method provides a systematic approach to obtaining exact solutions for the energy spectra and wave functions of certain one-dimensional quantum mechanical systems within the framework of supersymmetric quantum mechanics (SUSY QM). Introduced as an integrability condition, it leverages the structure of partner potentials to generate infinite hierarchies of solvable models from a seed potential. This technique classifies potentials that allow recursive application of SUSY transformations, yielding closed-form expressions for eigenvalues without solving the full Schrdinger equation.
The formal condition for shape invariance specifies that the partner potentials satisfy $ V_+(x; a) = V_-(x + s; a') + R(a) $, where $ V_+ $ and $ V_- $ are the SUSY partner potentials parameterized by $ a $, $ s $ represents a spatial shift, $ a' = a + \varepsilon $ denotes a parameter translation by $ \varepsilon $, and $ R(a) $ is a known function independent of $ x $. This relation, first derived in the SUSY context, ensures that the effective potential form remains unchanged up to the shift and remainder, facilitating iterative SUSY ladder operations.
Applying this condition leads to a recursion for the energy eigenvalues: $ E_{n+1}(a) = E_n(a') + R(a) $. Iterating over $ n $ steps yields the spectrum $ E_n(a) = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} R(a + k \varepsilon) + E_0(a) $, where $ E_0(a) $ is the ground-state energy, often zero in unbroken SUSY. Wave functions are constructed by successive applications of raising operators, starting from the ground state of the final iterated potential.
Normalization in the shape invariance recursion requires careful handling of wave function overlaps to ensure unitarity. In unbroken SUSY, the zero-energy ground state is normalizable, and the iterative process preserves orthogonality through the SUSY algebra; however, in broken SUSY cases, where no zero-energy state exists, adjusted normalizations account for non-vanishing ground-state energy, maintaining proper probability densities.
Extensions of the method include non-standard shape invariance, such as translations in the coordinate $ x $ (via $ s \neq 0 $) or additive parameter shifts beyond simple $ \varepsilon $-steps, which generate broader families of potentials from an initial seed. These allow construction of infinite solvable hierarchies, including radial problems like the hydrogen atom, by embedding partner potentials in effective one-dimensional forms.
While primarily applicable to one-dimensional potentials, the method faces limitations in higher dimensions or non-standard geometries. Recent developments in the 2020s have introduced numerical generalizations for quasi-exactly solvable cases, where only a finite subset of states is exactly known, using SUSY-inspired algorithms to approximate spectra for near-shape-invariant potentials.

Supersymmetry Breaking

In supersymmetric quantum mechanics, supersymmetry is considered broken when the ground state of the Hamiltonian is not annihilated by both the supercharge $ Q $ and its adjoint $ Q^\dagger $, resulting in a positive ground state energy $ E_0 > 0 $ and the absence of zero-energy modes. This spontaneous breaking occurs because the supercharges fail to map the ground state to itself, disrupting the pairing between bosonic and fermionic sectors.
Examples of supersymmetry breaking include the Dirac oscillator, where certain extensions of the coupling lead to broken supersymmetry despite the presence of hidden supersymmetry in soluble cases. Another instance arises in potentials supporting odd wavefunctions, for example, superpotentials with odd leading terms, such as $ W(\phi) = \lambda \phi^3 $, where no normalizable zero-energy state exists. Phase transitions in these models can also induce breaking, analogous to quantum phase transitions where the symmetry is lost due to non-perturbative effects like instantons.
The Witten index provides a diagnostic for breaking, defined as $ \Delta = \mathrm{Tr} $, where $ F $ is the fermion number operator and $ \beta $ is inverse temperature; this trace is independent of $ \beta $ and equals the difference between the number of bosonic and fermionic zero modes. In the broken phase, $ \Delta = 0 $, indicating either an equal number of bosonic and fermionic zero modes or none at all, which contrasts with unbroken supersymmetry where $ |\Delta| \geq 1 $.
Supersymmetry can be restored through fine-tuning of the superpotential to ensure the existence of a normalizable zero-energy ground state, such as adjusting parameters in double-well potentials to cancel instanton contributions from tunneling. In lattice discretizations of supersymmetric quantum mechanics, restoration in the continuum limit avoids excessive fine-tuning by preserving ward identities via specific boundary conditions.
The breaking affects spectral degeneracy, lifting the pairing of excited states and leading to non-degenerate spectra overall, with implications for exact solvability and scattering amplitudes. Post-2010 numerical studies, such as lattice simulations of PT-invariant potentials, detect breaking through observables like the expectation value of the superpotential derivative $ \langle W' \rangle \neq 0 $ and violations of ward identities, revealing phase-like transitions in chaotic-like systems.

Applications and Extensions

Supersymmetric quantum mechanics (SUSY QM) enhances semiclassical approximations, particularly through the supersymmetric WKB (SWKB) method, which provides quasi-exact quantization conditions for a broad class of potentials. Unlike the standard WKB approximation, SWKB incorporates supercharges to yield exact energy eigenvalues for shape-invariant potentials, such as the harmonic oscillator and Coulomb potential, by ensuring the quantization rule aligns precisely with the supersymmetric structure. For non-shape-invariant cases, SWKB offers improved accuracy over traditional WKB, especially for excited states, as demonstrated in higher-order expansions that minimize errors in the quantization condition. This approach has been applied to angular equations in quantum mechanics, combining perturbation theory with SWKB to achieve better convergence for quasi-exactly solvable systems.
In statistical mechanics, SUSY QM facilitates the mapping of Fokker-Planck equations to Schrdinger-like equations, enabling analytical solutions for diffusion processes in complex potentials. This isomorphism reveals ground state dominance in equilibrium distributions, where the supersymmetric ground state wave function corresponds to the stationary probability density, providing insights into thermodynamic free energy landscapes. For instance, in double-well potentials, SUSY techniques solve time-dependent Fokker-Planck equations exactly, highlighting isospectrality between partner Hamiltonians that mirrors strict isospectral evolution in non-stationary distributions. Such mappings extend to Langevin dynamics, connecting classical stochastic processes to quantum supersymmetry and aiding the study of phase transitions and relaxation phenomena.
SUSY QM inspires numerical algorithms for diagonalizing large Hamiltonians by leveraging partner potentials to reduce computational complexity in finding eigenvalues. Exact diagonalization studies of supersymmetric models, for example, use the superalgebra to probe quantum chaos, revealing boundaries between chaotic and integrable regimes through out-of-time-order correlators.
Extensions of SUSY QM to higher dimensions and multi-particle systems generalize the one-dimensional formalism by introducing multiple supercharges compatible with the geometry of the configuration space. For a particle in higher dimensions, the theory incorporates Clifford algebra structures for complex structures, preserving supersymmetry while allowing radial and angular separations. In multi-particle settings, new generalizations define collective superpotentials for indistinguishable particles, enabling exact solvability for interacting systems like Calogero-Moser models. These frameworks link to quantum field theory through supersymmetric sigma models, where SUSY QM serves as the dimensional reduction, describing bosonic and fermionic degrees on curved targets with N=1 or N=2 supersymmetry. Additionally, SUSY QM connects to integrable systems, such as the Korteweg-de Vries (KdV) hierarchy, via supersymmetric extensions that preserve infinite conserved quantities and soliton solutions.
Modern interpretations position SUSY QM within quantum information theory, where supersymmetry generates entangled states across bosonic and fermionic sectors, respecting superselection rules. Ground states of quadratic supersymmetric Hamiltonians exhibit dual entanglement measures, with bosonic and fermionic contributions related by a universal factor, facilitating quantification via concurrence for spin-continuous hybrids. This entanglement arises naturally from supercharges acting on separable states, offering a pathway to construct multipartite entanglement without external gates. In AdS/CFT-inspired models as of 2025, SUSY QM duals describe boundary theories for AdS2 geometries, such as orbifolded AdS2 S7, preserving extended supersymmetry such as N=6 or N=8 and probing microstates of black holes through integrable limits.
