Mathematical Background

Chebyshev Polynomials

Chebyshev polynomials of the first kind, denoted $ T_n(x) $, are a sequence of orthogonal polynomials named after the Russian mathematician Pafnuty Chebyshev, who developed them in the 19th century as part of his foundational work in approximation theory. These polynomials emerged from Chebyshev's investigations into mechanisms and function approximation, providing tools for minimizing errors in polynomial representations of continuous functions.

A key characteristic is the equioscillation property: $ T_n(x) $ attains its maximum absolute value of 1 alternately at $ n+1 $ points in , specifically at the extrema $ x_k = \cos(k\pi/n) $ for $ k = 0, 1, \dots, n $, where it alternates between $ +1 $ and $ -1 $. Zeros and Extrema of Chebyshev Polynomials

The zeros of the Chebyshev polynomial of the first kind $ T_n(x) $ are the values of $ x $ in the interval where $ T_n(x) = 0 $. These arise from the trigonometric representation $ T_n(\cos \theta) = \cos(n \theta) $, where setting $ \cos(n \theta) = 0 $ implies $ n \theta = \frac{(2k-1)\pi}{2} $ for integers $ k = 1, \dots, n $. Solving for $ \theta $ yields $ \theta_k = \frac{(2k-1)\pi}{2n} $, so the zeros are explicitly given by

The extrema of $ T_n(x) $, which are the local maxima and minima including the endpoints, occur where the polynomial reaches values of $ \pm 1 $ and equioscillates $ n+1 $ times across . From the same trigonometric identity, these critical points correspond to the locations where $ \cos(n \theta) = \pm 1 $, achieved when $ n \theta = k \pi $ for integers $ k = 0, \dots, n $. Thus, $ \theta_k = \frac{k \pi}{n} $, leading to the explicit expressions

Both the zeros and extrema exhibit clustering near the endpoints $ x = \pm 1 $, a consequence of the cosine mapping that projects equispaced points in $ \theta $ onto the interval with increasing density toward the boundaries. The asymptotic density of these points follows the arcsine distribution $ \rho(x) \propto (1 - x^2)^{-1/2} $, resulting in inter-node spacing that scales as $ O(1/n^2) $ near $ x = \pm 1 $. Specifically, for the zeros, the point closest to the endpoint 1 satisfies $ x_1 \approx 1 - \frac{\pi^2}{8 n^2} $, enhancing numerical stability in applications like interpolation by mitigating the Runge phenomenon.

Numerical Integration

Clenshaw-Curtis quadrature is a numerical integration method that employs the extrema of the Chebyshev polynomial of the first kind, specifically the points $ x_k = \cos\left( \frac{k \pi}{n} \right) $ for $ k = 0, 1, \dots, n $ on the interval , to approximate the integral $ \int_{-1}^{1} f(x) , dx $. These points, known as Chebyshev-Lobatto points, cluster near the endpoints, providing stability for high-degree approximations. The exact weights wkw_kwk are computed using the discrete cosine transform from the Chebyshev expansion coefficients, given by $ w_k = \frac{2}{n} \sum_{m=0}^{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor} \frac{1 + (-1)^{m(n-k)} \delta_{m,n/2}}{1 - (2m)^2} \cos\left( \frac{2 \pi m k}{n} \right) $ with adjustments for endpoints and parity, ensuring the rule is exact for polynomials of degree at most nnn.
The method derives its efficiency from the connection to Chebyshev series expansions of the integrand. By expressing $ f(x) $ in terms of Chebyshev polynomials $ T_j(x) $, the integral can be approximated by summing the series coefficients, leveraging the unweighted integrals $ \int_{-1}^{1} T_j(x) , dx = 0 $ for odd jjj, 222 for j=0j=0j=0, and $ \frac{2}{1-j^2} $ for even $j \geq 2 $. Aliasing in the discrete cosine transform used to compute coefficients enhances accuracy, often achieving near-optimal performance comparable to Gaussian quadrature despite using non-optimal nodes.
For analytic functions on , Clenshaw-Curtis quadrature exhibits exponential convergence, with error bounds decaying as $ O(\rho^{-n}) $ where $ \rho > 1 $ depends on the distance to the nearest singularity in the complex plane. For example, approximating $ \int_{-1}^{1} e^x , dx $ with $ n = 10 $ (11 points) yields an error less than $ 10^{-6} $, demonstrating rapid convergence for entire functions.
Adaptations of Clenshaw-Curtis quadrature extend to infinite or semi-infinite intervals through variable substitutions that map the domain to , such as $ x = \tan(\theta) $ for $ (-\infty, \infty) $. This contrasts with Gauss-Chebyshev quadrature, which is tailored for the specific weight function $ (1 - x^2)^{-1/2} $ using zeros of the Chebyshev polynomial and requires weight incorporation, whereas Clenshaw-Curtis handles uniform weights more directly post-substitution.

Variants

Modified Even-Order Nodes

The concept of modified even-order Chebyshev nodes is not standardly defined in primary literature on the topic. Standard Chebyshev nodes of the first kind exclude endpoints for any n, while variants like Chebyshev-Lobatto include them regardless of parity. For applications requiring endpoint inclusion with even n, Chebyshev-Lobatto points are typically used instead.
