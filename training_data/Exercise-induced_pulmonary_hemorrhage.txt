Exercise-induced pulmonary hemorrhage

 Exercise-induced pulmonary hemorrhage (EIPH) is a syndrome characterized by the rupture of pulmonary capillaries and subsequent bleeding into the alveoli or airways during or immediately after intense physical exertion. The condition was first noted in the 17th century, with epistaxis in racehorses described by Gervase Markham in 1688; however, the source of bleeding was not confirmed until endoscopy in 1974 identified blood originating from the lungs, and the term EIPH was introduced in 1981. This condition is most prevalent in equine athletes, particularly Thoroughbred and Standardbred racehorses, where it affects the majority of individuals engaged in high-speed exercise, though it also occurs in other species such as Greyhounds and, rarely, in human endurance athletes. EIPH is diagnosed based on the presence of blood in the lungs, often confirmed through post-exercise endoscopy or bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL), and it can lead to impaired respiratory function if recurrent.
In horses, EIPH arises primarily from the extreme pulmonary capillary pressuresreaching up to 100 mmHggenerated by increased cardiac output and negative intrapleural pressures during strenuous exercise, causing stress failure of the alveolar-capillary membrane. Repeated episodes contribute to chronic lung pathology, including hemosiderophage accumulation and fibrosis in the dorsocaudal lung regions, which may subtly reduce athletic performance over time. Prevalence studies indicate that nearly all racing horses exhibit some degree of EIPH when assessed via BAL, with visible epistaxis (nosebleeds) occurring in only 0.1513% of cases, highlighting the condition's subclinical nature in most affected animals. The American College of Veterinary Internal Medicine (ACVIM) classifies EIPH as a disease based on these hemodynamic mechanisms, emphasizing its ubiquity in performance equines worldwide.
While less common in humans, EIPH manifests in case reports among elite athletes, such as marathon runners or triathletes, where intense exertion elevates pulmonary pressures similarly, potentially leading to hemoptysis, dyspnea, and radiographic evidence of alveolar hemorrhage. In these instances, the condition is often self-limiting but can recur with extreme physical demands, as seen in a 45-year-old man who developed symptoms after short-distance runs. Management strategies, primarily validated in horses, include the use of furosemide to attenuate capillary hypertension and reduce hemorrhage severity by 8090%, though its application in humans remains investigational. Ongoing research focuses on preventive measures like nasal strips or nutritional supplements to mitigate the vascular stress underlying EIPH across species.

Introduction

Definition and overview

Exercise-induced pulmonary hemorrhage (EIPH) is defined as the detection of blood in the airways or alveoli of horses originating from the pulmonary vasculature during or immediately after strenuous exercise.
This condition is most commonly observed in performance equines, particularly Thoroughbred racehorses, but also affects Standardbreds, eventers, and other horses in high-intensity disciplines. In most cases, EIPH is subclinical, with no visible external signs such as epistaxis, which occurs in fewer than 5% of instances. Repeated occurrences can result in hemosiderin accumulation within the lungs, leading to potential chronic remodeling over time.
EIPH results from the rupture of pulmonary capillaries subjected to extreme stress during intense physical exertion. Severity is evaluated through standardized endoscopic scoring systems, graded from 0 (no blood visible) to 4 (blood pooling in the trachea).
As a prevalent issue in equine athletics, EIPH raises welfare concerns due to its links with exercise intolerance and diminished performance, with bronchoalveolar lavage detecting it in over 90% of Thoroughbred racehorses.

Historical context

Exercise-induced pulmonary hemorrhage (EIPH) has been observed in horses for centuries, with the earliest documented description appearing in the late 17th century when Gervase Markham noted epistaxis following intense exercise, attributing it to excessive blood accumulation or vein rupture. Systematic veterinary observations emerged in the early 20th century, as Robertson in 1913 linked post-exercise nosebleeds in racehorses to hyperemia of pulmonary vessels. By the 1930s, such epistaxis was increasingly reported in Thoroughbred racehorses, often viewed as a sporadic complication of extreme exertion rather than a widespread syndrome.
The condition was formalized in the 1970s through the advent of flexible fiberoptic endoscopy, which enabled post-exercise visualization of blood in the airways of racehorses, revealing its high prevalenceranging from 50% to 95%far beyond visible epistaxis cases. In 1981, Pascoe et al. coined the term "exercise-induced pulmonary hemorrhage" to describe pulmonary-origin epistaxis after strenuous activity and linked its occurrence directly to exercise intensity via endoscopic evidence in over 40% of examined racehorses. The 1980s marked a key milestone with the introduction of bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL), which identified hemosiderophagesiron-laden macrophages indicating prior alveolar bleedingestablishing EIPH as a chronic syndrome even in asymptomatic horses.
During the 1990s, research emphasized subclinical EIPH, showing through repeated endoscopy and BAL that bleeding occurred in most high-intensity equine athletes without overt signs, shifting perceptions from a rare event to a near-universal response to maximal effort. International conferences, such as the International Conference on Equine Exercise Physiology in the 2000s, facilitated standardization of diagnostic terminology, grading scales for hemorrhage severity, and research protocols, promoting global consistency in EIPH studies.
By the 2010s, EIPH was widely recognized as a prevalent subclinical condition affecting up to 100% of Thoroughbred racehorses after intense exercise, prompting broader investigations into its welfare implications. In the 2020s, attention has turned to genetic factors, with studies identifying variants in genes like ENTPD1 and ENTPD2 associated with increased EIPH risk in athletic horses, suggesting heritable predispositions that may influence breeding and management strategies.

Epidemiology

Prevalence and incidence

Exercise-induced pulmonary hemorrhage (EIPH) affects a substantial proportion of equine athletes, with endoscopic evaluations revealing blood in the airways of up to 90% of Thoroughbred racehorses at some point during their careers. In non-racing equids, such as elite endurance horses, the prevalence is notably lower, ranging from 45% to 50% following long-distance competitions. These figures are derived primarily from post-exercise tracheobronchoscopy, the standard diagnostic method for grading EIPH severity from 0 (no blood) to 4 (blood pooling in the trachea).
Incidence varies by racing discipline, with higher rates observed in flat racing Thoroughbreds (up to 75%) compared to harness racing Standardbreds (approximately 5080%). The occurrence of EIPH also escalates with exercise intensity, showing increased prevalence in association with greater racing speeds and longer distances.
Prevalence rates have remained consistently high since the 1990s, with multiple longitudinal studies reporting no substantial decline despite various management interventions, as documented in reports from the American Association of Equine Practitioners (AAEP). For instance, endoscopic detection rates in Thoroughbreds hovered between 55% after a single race and over 85% across multiple races in early 2000s analyses, mirroring findings in recent investigations.
Demographically, EIPH is particularly prevalent among young racehorses aged 2 to 3 years, coinciding with the onset of intense training and racing. There is no strong sex bias overall, though intact stallions exhibit slightly lower rates (49%) compared to mares (73%) and geldings (74%).

Affected populations and risk factors

Exercise-induced pulmonary hemorrhage (EIPH) primarily affects equine athletes engaged in high-intensity activities, with the highest prevalence observed in Thoroughbred racehorses, where rates can reach 55-95% following strenuous exercise. Standardbred racehorses experience lower rates than Thoroughbreds, with endoscopic prevalence reported around 5080%. Quarter Horses in barrel racing show approximately 60% prevalence. The condition also occurs in other equine disciplines involving intense effort, such as polo ponies and show jumpers. Although rare outside equines, EIPH has been documented in racing Greyhounds and elite human athletes, such as endurance runners and cyclists, typically after maximal exertion.
Non-modifiable risk factors include breed predisposition, with Thoroughbreds (a hot-blooded breed) showing higher susceptibility compared to Standardbreds. Age contributes through cumulative exposure, as older horses with longer racing careers exhibit increased prevalence due to repeated lung stress. Genetic factors play a role, with heritability estimates for EIPH (often assessed via epistaxis) ranging from 0.23 to 0.50 in Thoroughbred populations, based on pedigree analyses indicating sire-line influences.
Modifiable risk factors center on training and environmental conditions that exacerbate pulmonary stress. High-intensity training, such as prolonged high-speed gallops, elevates risk, with each additional year in training associated with 1.5-fold higher odds of EIPH detection. Environmental elements like lower ambient temperatures below 20C and racing in winter or spring seasons increase odds by approximately 2.4 times compared to warmer periods. Track surface type also influences susceptibility, with studies linking higher prevalence and severity to both dirt and turf surfaces relative to other conditions.
These factors often interact cumulatively; for instance, combining extended high-intensity training with cold-weather conditions amplifies EIPH odds beyond individual effects, underscoring the compounded vulnerability in intensively managed racehorses.

Pathophysiology

Hemodynamic mechanisms

Exercise-induced pulmonary hemorrhage (EIPH) in horses is primarily driven by hemodynamic changes in the pulmonary circulation during strenuous exercise, leading to elevated pressures that exceed the structural limits of pulmonary capillaries. At rest, mean pulmonary arterial pressure in horses is typically 25-30 mmHg, but during maximal exercise, systolic pulmonary arterial pressures can exceed 100 mmHg, often reaching 90-120 mmHg, creating conditions for capillary rupture. These pressures generate high transmural forces across the capillary walls, where the difference between intravascular and alveolar pressures promotes stress failure in the thin blood-gas barrier.
The core mechanisms involve a dramatic increase in cardiac output, which can rise up to 10-fold from resting levels of approximately 30-40 L/min to over 400 L/min during intense exercise, overwhelming the relatively fixed pulmonary capillary bed and elevating vascular pressures. Hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction further contributes by increasing vascular resistance in response to regional alveolar hypoxia, thereby amplifying mean pulmonary artery pressure and transmural stress in dependent lung regions. Additionally, left atrial pressure surges due to elevated venous return and pulmonary venous congestion, peaking at up to 70 mmHg, which raises downstream capillary hydrostatic pressure and exacerbates the pressure gradient across capillary walls.

For instance, telemetry data from treadmill trials show that pressures above 100 mmHg during maximal effort are consistently associated with higher hemorrhage scores, underscoring the hemodynamic origin of capillary disruption.

Structural and traumatic factors

Structural and traumatic factors contributing to exercise-induced pulmonary hemorrhage (EIPH) in horses primarily involve anatomical features of the equine lung and mechanical stresses from high-intensity locomotion. The equine pulmonary vasculature is characterized by extensive capillary networks that cover a substantial portion of the alveolar surface area, estimated at around 1700 m for capillary surface alone in athletic horses, facilitating efficient gas exchange but rendering the tissue vulnerable to disruption during strenuous exercise. This dense capillary bed, combined with a thin blood-gas barrieraveraging approximately 1-2 m in thicknessincreases susceptibility to rupture under mechanical or pressure-related strain. The minimal supportive connective tissue surrounding these capillaries further exacerbates fragility, as the structure prioritizes diffusive efficiency over mechanical resilience.
A key pathological adaptation in chronic EIPH is veno-occlusive remodeling of pulmonary veins, where repeated hemorrhage episodes trigger fibrosis and intimal proliferation, leading to luminal narrowing and elevated upstream pressures in the capillary bed. This remodeling is predominantly observed in the caudodorsal lung regions and has been identified in a subset of affected horses through postmortem histopathologic analysis. Such changes, characterized by collagen deposition within venous walls, impair venous drainage and perpetuate capillary stress, contributing directly to recurrent bleeding. Histological studies confirm that this lesion is more severe in horses with higher grades of EIPH, linking it causally to the disease's progression.
Locomotory trauma during galloping has been proposed as a contributing mechanical factor, as the forelimb impacts generate concussive forces transmitted through the thoracic skeleton to the lungs, inducing pressure transients that can exceed 100 kPa and cause alveolar-capillary rupture. These forces, arising from ground strikes at velocities over 15 m/s, particularly affect the dorsocaudal pulmonary regions due to wave reflections within the lung parenchyma, aligning with the typical distribution of EIPH lesions. Experimental models demonstrate that such impact-induced trauma mimics blast injury mechanisms, disrupting the fragile pulmonary microvasculature without requiring extreme intravascular pressures alone.
The interplay between structural remodeling and repeated locomotory trauma forms a vicious cycle in EIPH pathogenesis, wherein initial capillary disruptions from impacts lead to hemorrhage, hemosiderin accumulation, and subsequent fibrotic remodeling that stiffens veins and heightens fragility for future episodes. Histopathologic and imaging evidence, including postmortem examinations, reveals concentrated lesions in high-impact dorsal zones, underscoring how cumulative trauma amplifies veno-occlusive changes over time. This feedback loop is evident in racing horses, where ongoing exercise intensifies both mechanical insults and pathological adaptations, sustaining the condition.
Similar hemodynamic mechanisms contribute to rare cases of EIPH in humans, particularly elite endurance athletes, where pulmonary capillary pressures can exceed 30-40 mmHg at the lung base during intense exercise, leading to stress failure and hemoptysis.

Clinical Presentation

Signs and symptoms

Exercise-induced pulmonary hemorrhage (EIPH) in horses most commonly presents without overt clinical signs, with subclinical cases accounting for the majority of affected animals. Approximately 80-90% of horses experiencing EIPH show no visible manifestations, as the bleeding occurs internally within the lungs and is typically detected only through specialized diagnostic methods.
Visible signs are rare and generally limited to severe episodes. Epistaxis, or nosebleed, occurs in approximately 5% of horses with EIPH, typically indicating the most severe grade (grade 4) immediately after exercise, often appearing as bilateral blood flow from the nostrils that resolves within hours. Coughing or the presence of blood-tinged sputum is uncommon and not reliably associated with EIPH, occurring infrequently even in advanced cases.
Systemic symptoms in affected horses often include fatigue and reduced stamina, particularly noticeable after strenuous activity. These manifestations may subtly impair overall well-being and contribute to diminished athletic performance.
EIPH episodes are typically acute and occur as isolated events following high-intensity exercise, but recurrent bleeding in chronic forms results in iron overload within pulmonary macrophages, known as hemosiderosis. This condition is characterized by the accumulation of hemosiderin-laden macrophages in the lungs, leading to long-term inflammation and fibrotic changes in the caudodorsal lung regions.
In humans, EIPH is rare and typically presents with hemoptysis (coughing up blood), dyspnea, and fatigue following extreme endurance exercise, such as marathons, often self-limiting but potentially recurrent.

Impact on athletic performance

Exercise-induced pulmonary hemorrhage (EIPH) can impair short-term athletic performance in affected horses by disrupting pulmonary gas exchange, leading to arterial hypoxemia and ventilation-perfusion inequalities during intense exercise. This impairment manifests as reduced oxygen uptake and increased blood lactate accumulation, contributing to early fatigue in racehorses with moderate to severe EIPH (grades 2 or higher). In unmedicated Thoroughbreds, horses without EIPH (grade 0) are over twice as likely to win races compared to those with EIPH (odds ratio 2.3, 95% CI 1.4-3.7), and they finish approximately one length ahead of EIPH-positive counterparts. Similarly, horses with EIPH grade 2 are less likely to finish in the top three positions or achieve high earnings percentiles. 
Long-term consequences of EIPH include potential career shortening, particularly in severe cases, due to cumulative lung damage and repeated episodes that exacerbate exercise intolerance. While mild to moderate EIPH (grades 0-3) shows no significant association with reduced career length, starts, or earnings in Thoroughbreds, grade 4 EIPH is linked to shorter careers (hazard ratio 2.06) and 12 fewer starts on average. Horses with grade 2 EIPH earn substantially less over time, with post-diagnosis earnings about one-third those of horses with lower grades. These effects often necessitate extended recovery periods, typically 10 days to two weeks of rest following a bleed to allow capillary healing, which delays training and racing schedules. 
From large-scale analyses, EIPH-positive horses demonstrate inferior competitive outcomes, with studies of over 1,000 Thoroughbred starts indicating reduced win rates and placements for those with detectable hemorrhage. In one cohort of 1,000 races, EIPH was associated with lower likelihood of top finishes and earnings in the highest decile (odds ratio 0.4 for grade 1 vs. grade 0). Welfare concerns arise from this exercise intolerance, as chronic or severe EIPH may lead to early retirement to prevent ongoing performance declines and lung pathology. 

Diagnosis

Endoscopic methods

Endoscopic methods for diagnosing exercise-induced pulmonary hemorrhage (EIPH) primarily utilize flexible tracheobronchoscopy, which allows direct visualization of blood in the upper airways of horses. This procedure is performed on a standing horse under light sedation, typically 30 to 120 minutes following strenuous exercise, to capture fresh blood that has drained into the trachea from the lungs. As the gold standard for confirming EIPH, bronchoscopy involves passing a flexible endoscope through the nasal passage into the trachea and bronchi, enabling real-time assessment without the need for general anesthesia.
Severity is quantified using a standardized 0 to 4 grading scale based on the extent and distribution of blood observed in the trachea. Grade 0 indicates no blood observed in the trachea or mainstem bronchi; grade 1 features one or more flecks of blood or no more than two short, narrow streams of blood visible from the tracheal bifurcation; grade 2 shows one long stream of blood or more than two short streams occupying less than one-third of the tracheal circumference; grade 3 involves multiple, distinct streams of blood covering more than one-third of the tracheal circumference, with no blood pooling at the thoracic inlet; and grade 4 is characterized by multiple, coalescing streams of blood covering more than 90% of the tracheal surface, with blood pooling at the thoracic inlet. This system, originally described in early studies and refined through widespread use, demonstrates high inter-observer reliability, with agreement rates often exceeding 90% and weighted kappa values up to 0.94 across evaluators.
The primary advantages of endoscopic evaluation lie in its specificity for identifying hemorrhage in the tracheobronchial tree and its capacity for immediate, non-invasive (relative to surgical options) diagnosis in clinical settings. It detects tracheal blood in approximately 45% to 75% of racing horses post-exercise, providing a reliable indicator of EIPH while also allowing differentiation from other sources of upper airway bleeding, such as guttural pouch infections. Performed routinely in veterinary practices near racetracks, the method supports ongoing monitoring of affected horses without significant disruption to training schedules.
Despite its efficacy, endoscopic methods have notable limitations, including their invasive nature, which carries risks of mucosal irritation or infection, and their inability to visualize alveolar-level bleeding that does not reach the tracheapotentially underestimating the full extent of EIPH, as revealed by complementary fluid analysis techniques. Optimal timing is essential, as blood visibility peaks shortly after exercise but can diminish as it is coughed up or absorbed, remaining detectable for 1 to 3 days in some cases, though examinations beyond this window may yield false negatives.

Fluid analysis techniques

Bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL) involves the collection of alveolar and small airway fluid through a flexible catheter passed via endoscopy into a dorsocaudal pulmonary lobe, where sterile saline is infused and then aspirated for analysis. This technique samples the terminal respiratory units, providing a more sensitive assessment of alveolar hemorrhage than upper airway methods. Typically, 250500 mL of warmed, buffered saline is used in aliquots, with recovery rates of at least 50% considered adequate for evaluation. Samples should be processed within 24 hours to minimize cell degradation and maintain accuracy in cell counts.
Cytopathological examination of BAL fluid focuses on identifying hemosiderophages, which are alveolar macrophages that have phagocytosed erythrocytes and accumulated hemosiderin from prior hemorrhage. These cells are quantified as a percentage of total macrophages, with levels exceeding 20% indicative of EIPH in athletic horses, while normal values in non-exercising or control horses are typically less than 5%. Prussian blue staining (Perls' Prussian blue) is the standard method to detect intracellular iron deposits, confirming hemosiderin presence and distinguishing hemosiderophages from other macrophages. In acute EIPH episodes, differential cell counts often reveal elevated neutrophils (greater than 1015% of total nucleated cells), reflecting an inflammatory response to fresh blood in the alveoli, though this is less prominent in chronic cases.
Interpretation of BAL results commonly employs the hemosiderin index or total hemosiderin score (THS), a semi-quantitative system grading individual macrophages from 0 (no hemosiderin) to 4 (severe loading) across 200300 cells, yielding a score ranging from 0 to 100% when normalized or up to 1200 in absolute terms. A THS greater than 75 is diagnostic for EIPH, with a sensitivity of approximately 8594% for detecting chronic hemorrhage and specificity around 88%, outperforming endoscopy in identifying subclinical cases. This scoring correlates with hemorrhage severity and persists for weeks to months post-episode, aiding in monitoring disease progression.
Recent advances include flow cytometry for precise quantification of macrophage subpopulations in BAL fluid, enabling automated detection of hemosiderin-laden cells via fluorescence markers for iron or phagocytosis without manual grading biases. This method improves reproducibility and allows simultaneous assessment of inflammatory markers, though it requires specialized equipment and is not yet routine in clinical practice.

Diagnosis in humans

In humans, EIPH is rare and diagnosed primarily through clinical history of hemoptysis or dyspnea immediately following intense endurance exercise, supported by imaging such as high-resolution computed tomography (HRCT) showing bilateral ground-glass opacities or consolidation indicative of alveolar hemorrhage. Bronchoalveolar lavage or bronchoscopy may confirm hemosiderin-laden macrophages or fresh blood but is infrequently performed due to the condition's self-limiting nature and low prevalence. Diagnosis requires exclusion of other causes like cardiac or infectious etiologies.

Treatment and Management

Pharmacological interventions

Furosemide, commonly known as Lasix, is the primary pharmacological intervention for mitigating exercise-induced pulmonary hemorrhage (EIPH) in horses. Administered intravenously at a dose of 0.51 mg/kg body weight approximately 4 hours prior to exercise, it acts as a potent diuretic that reduces plasma volume and pulmonary capillary hydrostatic pressure, thereby decreasing the severity of hemorrhage. Studies indicate that this treatment lowers peak pulmonary arterial pressure by about 7 mmHg during strenuous exercise, contributing to its protective effect against capillary rupture.
Randomized controlled trials have demonstrated furosemide's efficacy, with prerace administration reducing the incidence of EIPH by 311 times compared to placebo and decreasing severity scores in 6768% of affected Thoroughbred racehorses. A meta-analysis of multiple studies confirmed that 68% (95% CI 6176%) of horses experienced reduced EIPH severity following treatment, supporting its widespread use in racing protocols where permitted.
Other pharmacological agents have been explored but show limited promise. Nitroglycerin, a vasodilator, has been tested experimentally to lower pulmonary vascular pressures during exercise; however, intravenous administration does not significantly attenuate EIPH or exercise-induced pulmonary hypertension in Thoroughbreds. Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as flunixin meglumine target inflammation potentially associated with EIPH, but evidence for their efficacy in reducing hemorrhage severity remains inconclusive, with studies primarily focusing on analgesic effects rather than direct EIPH mitigation.
Treatment protocols emphasize weight-based dosing (e.g., 1 mg/kg IV) and timing to maximize benefits while complying with regulatory restrictions. Post-2020 reforms in several U.S. jurisdictions, including bans on race-day furosemide in stakes races and for 2-year-olds, reflect efforts to limit its use amid concerns over performance enhancement.
Common side effects of furosemide include dehydration, with horses losing 1015 liters of urine shortly after administration, and electrolyte imbalances such as hypokalemia and hypocalcemia, which can impair muscle function and recovery. Long-term use has raised questions about masking underlying vascular issues without addressing root causes of EIPH.

Supportive and preventive measures

Supportive measures for exercise-induced pulmonary hemorrhage (EIPH) in horses primarily involve rest and recovery protocols to allow healing of the pulmonary vasculature following an episode. After diagnosis, a period of stall rest lasting approximately three weeks is recommended to facilitate lung recovery, with a gradual reintroduction to exercise over the subsequent weeks to minimize stress on the respiratory system. This approach helps prevent immediate recurrence by permitting repair of damaged capillaries, though evidence indicates that most horses may still experience EIPH upon returning to prior training intensities.
Training adjustments play a key role in prevention by modifying exercise regimens to reduce pulmonary pressures. Interval training, which alternates high-intensity efforts with recovery periods, has been shown to improve fitness with fewer strides compared to continuous incremental training. Additionally, environmental controls are essential to reduce airway irritation and trauma that contribute to EIPH severity. Maintaining dust-free stables through the use of low-dust bedding materials, such as wood pellets or coarse shavings, minimizes inhalation of particulates that inflame the respiratory tract and worsen hemorrhage. Provision of humidified or water vapor-saturated air has demonstrated efficacy in reducing EIPH grades in affected horses by maintaining airway moisture and preventing desiccation of lung tissues during exercise. Softer track surfaces, which absorb impact better than hard dirt, can decrease concussive forces transmitted to the pulmonary system, thereby lowering the risk of capillary rupture.
Ongoing monitoring through routine post-exercise endoscopic screening every 3-6 months allows for early detection of recurrence in at-risk horses, enabling timely adjustments to management plans. Nutritional support focuses on antioxidants to counter oxidative stress associated with repeated hemorrhage and inflammation. Emerging therapies as of 2025, such as shock wave therapy applied to the lungs, show promise in reducing EIPH severity as a drug-free option, though further research is needed.

Research Directions

Current studies and controversies

Ongoing research into exercise-induced pulmonary hemorrhage (EIPH) continues to explore its etiology, with a particular focus on genetic factors contributing to susceptibility. Earlier efforts, including genome-wide association studies, have sought to identify genes related to EIPH predisposition in Thoroughbred horses, such as those involved in cell stiffness and blood flow. A 2024 investigation of variants in the ENTPD1 and ENTPD2 genes found high allele frequencies but no significant associations with EIPH risk, highlighting the need for broader genomic profiling.
A major controversy surrounds the use of furosemide (Lasix) for EIPH management, balancing animal welfare benefits against potential performance enhancement. Proponents argue it reduces hemorrhage severity and improves respiratory function, thereby enhancing welfare, while critics contend it may confer unfair speed advantages by acting as a diuretic, leading to polarized debates within the equine industry. Recent 2023-2024 analyses have questioned the causal direction between EIPH and athletic performance, with evidence suggesting severe EIPH (grade 3) correlates with inferior outcomes like lower speed indices and poorer finishing positions, but not clarifying if hemorrhage impairs performance or if underlying factors predispose both.
Diagnostic methods remain contentious, particularly the comparative validity of bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL) fluid analysis versus post-exercise tracheobronchoscopy (endoscopy) for detecting subclinical EIPH. BAL is considered more sensitive for identifying low-level hemorrhage through red blood cell counts in alveolar samples, potentially detecting cases missed by endoscopy, which primarily visualizes tracheal blood but may underestimate diffuse or mild events. Prevalence studies using BAL indicate near-universal evidence of EIPH in racing horses, underscoring the condition's ubiquity in racing populations.
Regulatory changes in U.S. Thoroughbred racing have intensified scrutiny of EIPH management since 2021, when race-day furosemide was banned in stakes races for 2-year-olds, expanding under Horseracing Integrity and Safety Authority (HISA) oversight by 2023-2025. These restrictions reduced furosemide administration rates from approximately 90% to 37% in affected cohorts, prompting studies on welfare impacts; while direct EIPH incidence data post-ban is emerging, preliminary scoping at select tracks shows variable prevalence (around 74%), with enforcement challenges including inconsistent compliance and calls for alternative preventive strategies.

Emerging diagnostic and therapeutic advances

Recent advancements in diagnosing exercise-induced pulmonary hemorrhage (EIPH) have leveraged artificial intelligence to automate and improve the accuracy of severity grading. A deep learning model using a custom RetinaNet architecture analyzes whole-slide images of bronchoalveolar lavage fluid cytology to detect and classify hemosiderophages, key indicators of EIPH in horses, achieving 85% concordance with expert pathologists and processing gigapixel images in under two minutes. This approach addresses limitations in manual grading, such as subjectivity and time intensity, and demonstrates mean average precision of 0.66 on test sets.
Therapeutic developments include investigational regenerative treatments derived from stem cells. EquiFlow, a nebulized concentrated equine protein serum sourced from donor horse stem cells, has shown preliminary efficacy in reducing EIPH bleeding incidence and aiding recovery from lung inflammation in field studies involving 30 horses in 2024, with a follow-up 36-horse trial underway in 2025 at Three Rivers Arena in Glen Rose, Texas. Led by experts at Washington State University, this therapy aims for FDA veterinary approval and represents a non-pharmacological alternative to traditional interventions like furosemide. Additionally, extracorporeal shock wave therapy is under evaluation for its potential to mitigate EIPH episodes and enhance performance in affected horses, with ongoing studies assessing bleeding reduction and respiratory outcomes in barrel racing contexts.
Genomic research is paving the way for biomarker-driven screening and personalized approaches. Investigations into candidate genes like ENTPD1 and ENTPD2 have explored associations with EIPH susceptibility in Thoroughbred horses, revealing high allele frequencies but no significant links, highlighting the need for broader genomic profiling to identify at-risk individuals. Epigenetic modifications induced by exercise, including alterations in DNA methylation and histone acetylation, are emerging as potential targets for tailoring prevention strategies based on individual athletic responses. Future directions emphasize integrating genomics with equine performance data to enable precision breeding and risk stratification, potentially reducing EIPH prevalence through early interventions.
