Here is how to cook, from absolute beginner to making meals that impress people, explained in plain language with no assumptions about what you already know.

Cooking is the application of heat to food to make it safe, digestible, and delicious. That is all it is. Everything else, the recipes, the techniques, the equipment, the ingredients, is just variation on that simple idea. If you can follow instructions and pay attention, you can cook. The barrier is not talent. It is practice.

Essential equipment. You need a sharp knife, a cutting board, a large skillet or frying pan, a medium saucepan, a large pot for boiling water, a baking sheet, a spatula, a wooden spoon, measuring cups and spoons, and a colander for draining pasta and vegetables. That is it. You do not need a stand mixer, a food processor, an immersion blender, or any of the other gadgets that kitchen stores try to sell you. Professional chefs cook ninety percent of their food with the same basic tools listed above.

The five fundamental techniques that cover ninety percent of all cooking are: sauteing, roasting, boiling, braising, and seasoning.

Sauteing means cooking food quickly in a hot pan with a small amount of oil. Heat the pan over medium-high heat. Add a tablespoon of oil. When the oil shimmers, add the food in a single layer. Do not crowd the pan, as overcrowding causes steaming instead of browning. Let the food sit for two to three minutes without moving it so it develops a golden-brown crust, then flip or stir. Sauteing is how you cook chicken breasts, stir-fries, vegetables, shrimp, and eggs.

Roasting means cooking food in the oven using dry heat. Preheat the oven to four hundred degrees. Cut vegetables or meat into even-sized pieces so they cook at the same rate. Toss with oil, salt, and pepper. Spread on a baking sheet in a single layer. Roast until golden brown and tender, typically twenty to forty minutes depending on the size. Roasting is how you cook broccoli, sweet potatoes, chicken thighs, salmon, and root vegetables. It is the easiest technique because the oven does the work.

Boiling means cooking food in water at two hundred and twelve degrees. Fill a pot with water, add salt generously, and bring to a rolling boil. Add the food and cook until tender. Pasta takes eight to twelve minutes. Rice takes fifteen to twenty minutes with a lid on. Eggs take six minutes for soft-boiled and twelve minutes for hard-boiled. Potatoes take fifteen to twenty minutes. Drain through a colander when done.

Braising means cooking food low and slow in liquid. Brown the meat in a hot pan to develop flavor. Transfer to a pot. Add liquid, broth, wine, or tomatoes, until it comes halfway up the meat. Cover with a lid. Cook at low heat for two to four hours until the meat is falling-apart tender. Braising is how you make pot roast, pulled pork, beef stew, and chili. It is the most forgiving technique because the long cooking time and the moisture make it very difficult to ruin.

Seasoning is the difference between food that tastes like nothing and food that tastes incredible. Salt is the most important seasoning. It does not make food taste salty. It makes food taste more like itself. A tomato without salt tastes flat. A tomato with the right amount of salt tastes intensely tomato-like. Season food at every stage of cooking, not just at the end. Taste as you go and adjust.

Beyond salt, the other essential seasonings are black pepper, garlic, onion, olive oil, butter, acid such as lemon juice or vinegar, and fresh herbs like parsley, cilantro, and basil. With just these ingredients and the five techniques above, you can cook hundreds of different meals.

Here are five meals every beginner should learn to make:

Pasta with garlic and olive oil. Boil pasta according to package directions. While it cooks, heat olive oil in a skillet over medium heat. Add four cloves of minced garlic and cook for one minute until fragrant. Add a pinch of red pepper flakes. Drain the pasta, reserving half a cup of the cooking water. Toss the pasta in the skillet with the garlic oil and a splash of the pasta water. Add salt, pepper, and parmesan cheese. Total time: fifteen minutes.

Roasted chicken thighs with vegetables. Preheat oven to four hundred and twenty-five degrees. Place bone-in chicken thighs on a baking sheet. Surround with chopped potatoes, carrots, and onions. Drizzle everything with olive oil. Season generously with salt, pepper, garlic powder, and paprika. Roast for thirty-five to forty minutes until the chicken skin is crispy and the internal temperature reaches one hundred and sixty-five degrees. Total time: forty-five minutes.

Simple stir-fry. Cut chicken breast or tofu into thin strips. Cut vegetables into bite-sized pieces: bell peppers, broccoli, snap peas, carrots. Heat oil in a skillet or wok over high heat. Cook the protein first until golden, about four minutes. Remove. Cook the vegetables until tender-crisp, about three minutes. Return the protein. Add soy sauce, a splash of rice vinegar, and a teaspoon of sesame oil. Serve over rice. Total time: twenty minutes.

A simple salad that actually tastes good. The secret to a great salad is the dressing. In a jar, combine three tablespoons of olive oil, one tablespoon of vinegar or lemon juice, one teaspoon of Dijon mustard, a pinch of salt, and a pinch of pepper. Shake until emulsified. Toss with mixed greens, cherry tomatoes, cucumber, and whatever other vegetables or proteins you have. The dressing makes it.

Scrambled eggs. Crack three eggs into a bowl. Add a pinch of salt and a splash of milk or cream. Whisk until the yolks and whites are fully combined. Heat a nonstick pan over medium-low heat. Add a tablespoon of butter. When the butter foams, pour in the eggs. Stir slowly with a spatula, pushing the curds gently across the pan. Remove from heat while the eggs are still slightly wet, as they will continue cooking from residual heat. Total time: five minutes.

The most important thing about cooking is to start. Do not wait until you have the perfect kitchen, the perfect recipe collection, or the perfect set of skills. Pick one recipe. Buy the ingredients. Make it. It will probably not be perfect. Make it again next week. It will be better. Keep cooking. Within a month, you will have a handful of meals you can make confidently. Within a year, you will be the person other people ask for recipes.
