Wide-angle lens

1x
 A wide-angle lens is a type of camera lens characterized by a short focal length relative to the image sensor or film format, enabling it to capture a broader field of view than a standard or normal lens. In 35mm full-frame photography, lenses with focal lengths of 35mm or shorter are generally classified as wide-angle, typically providing horizontal fields of view between approximately 54 and 84. This design exaggerates perspective, making foreground objects appear larger and more prominent while compressing the background, which creates a sense of depth and expansiveness in images.
Key characteristics of wide-angle lenses include their ability to encompass expansive scenes in a single frame, often with diagonal angles of view ranging from 60 to 100 for standard wide-angle designs, exceeding 100 for ultra-wide variants, and reaching 180 or more for fisheye types. However, these lenses are prone to optical aberrations, particularly barrel distortion, where straight lines in the scene bow outward toward the edges of the frame, a phenomenon that intensifies with wider fields of view due to the lens's retrofocus construction in single-lens reflex cameras. Other distortions, such as pincushion (inward bowing) or moustache (a combination of both), may occur but are less common; these effects can be mitigated through aspherical elements or post-processing correction without significantly impacting sharpness.
Wide-angle lenses trace their origins to the mid-19th century, with the first practical design being the 1862 Harrison Globe lens, which achieved an approximately 80 angle of view on large-format cameras. Advancements in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, including symmetric and retrofocus configurations, expanded their utility, particularly after the 1950s when they became essential for 35mm SLR systems. Today, they are indispensable in applications such as landscape and architectural photography for capturing vast environments, documentary filmmaking to immerse viewers in scenes, automotive systems like dash cams and advanced driver-assistance features for wide monitoring, and scientific imaging in fields like aerial surveying and security surveillance.

Fundamentals

Definition and Scope

A wide-angle lens is a type of photographic lens characterized by a shorter focal length compared to a standard lens, which results in a broader field of view, typically exceeding 60 degrees diagonally. This design allows the lens to capture more of the scene in a single frame, making it suitable for applications requiring expansive coverage, such as landscapes or architecture.
The focal length of a lens is defined as the distance from its optical center (principal point) to the image sensor or film plane when the lens is focused at infinity. Shorter focal lengths inherently produce wider angles of view, with the exact relationship depending on the image format size.
In the 35 mm full-frame format, wide-angle lenses typically have focal lengths of 35 mm or shorter. However, the classification varies across sensor or film formats due to differences in physical dimensions; for instance, in medium format systems like 6x6 cm, focal lengths around 50 mm or less provide comparable wide-angle perspectives to those on 35 mm format, as larger formats require proportionally longer focal lengths to achieve equivalent fields of view.

Comparison to Standard and Telephoto Lenses

Wide-angle lenses, typically with focal lengths shorter than 35mm on full-frame cameras, produce a distinctive perspective that exaggerates the size of foreground elements while making the background appear smaller and more distant, creating a sense of depth and expansiveness in the scene. In contrast, standard lenses, around 50mm, offer a natural perspective that closely mimics the human eye's field of view, rendering subjects with minimal distortion and a balanced sense of space. Telephoto lenses, with focal lengths exceeding 85mm, achieve the opposite effect by compressing perspective, making foreground and background elements appear closer together and flattening the overall composition. This exaggeration in wide-angle lenses can lead to noticeable distortion, such as stretching facial features in close portraits, whereas telephoto lenses reduce such issues but may isolate subjects unnaturally from their surroundings.
A key optical difference lies in depth of field, which is inherently greater in wide-angle lenses due to their shorter focal lengths, allowing more of the scenefrom foreground to infinityto remain in sharp focus when stopped down. Standard lenses provide a moderate depth of field, suitable for everyday shooting where selective focus is not extreme. Conversely, telephoto lenses yield a shallower depth of field, even at comparable apertures, which isolates subjects against a blurred background, enhancing separation but limiting overall sharpness across the frame. This property makes wide-angle lenses less ideal for scenarios requiring subject isolation, as their extensive depth of field keeps distant elements in focus.
These characteristics influence typical applications: wide-angle lenses excel in landscapes and architecture, where capturing expansive scenes and emphasizing foreground interest is desirable, such as including dramatic skies or building details. Standard lenses are versatile for general photography, including street and travel shots, providing undistorted views akin to unaided vision. Telephoto lenses, by contrast, are preferred for portraits, sports, and wildlife, where compression flatters subjects and blurs distractions, avoiding the unflattering distortions of wide-angle close-ups.

Optical Properties

Field of View and Angle of Coverage

The field of view (FOV) in a wide-angle lens refers to the angular extent of the scene that can be captured by the camera sensor, typically measured diagonally in degrees. This measurement quantifies how much of the observable world is included in the image, with shorter focal lengths producing wider FOVs compared to longer ones. For a given sensor size, the diagonal FOV can be calculated using the formula tan(/2)=(d/2)/f\tan(\theta/2) = (d/2) / ftan(/2)=(d/2)/f, where ddd is the frame diagonal and fff is the focal length.
A key distinction exists between FOV and angle of coverage: the former is limited by the sensor's dimensions and represents the actual view recorded, while the latter describes the total angular spread of light that the lens projects onto the image plane, determined by the size of the image circle. If the image circle exceeds the sensor area, the full angle of coverage may not be utilized, potentially leading to vignetting on smaller formats, but the lens's inherent coverage remains a fixed property independent of the sensor.
Wide-angle lenses are categorized by their FOV ranges on full-frame sensors, with standard wide-angle options typically providing 60 to 90 diagonallysuch as a 35 mm lens at approximately 63 or a 24 mm lens at about 84offering expansive yet natural perspectives for landscapes and architecture. Ultra-wide-angle lenses extend this to 90 to 120, exemplified by a 20 mm lens yielding around 94 or a 14 mm lens reaching up to 114, enabling dramatic inclusions of foreground elements in environmental photography.

Distortion and Aberrations

Wide-angle lenses, designed to capture a broad field of view, frequently introduce barrel distortion, a type of radial geometric aberration where straight lines in the scene bow outward, particularly near the image periphery. This distortion arises from the lens's effort to map a wide angular extent onto a flat image plane, causing magnification to vary with radial distance from the optical axis. A common approximation for this effect in the Brown-Conrady model is given by the equation $ r' = r (1 + k r^2) $, where $ r $ represents the ideal undistorted radial distance from the principal point, $ r' $ is the observed distorted distance, and $ k $ is the radial distortion coefficient (negative for barrel distortion).
Beyond geometric distortion, wide-angle lenses suffer from chromatic aberrations, notably lateral chromatic aberration at the edges, where different colors focus at slightly offset positions due to varying refractive indices for wavelengths of light. Off-axis rays in these lenses strike elements at steeper angles, amplifying the dispersion and producing color fringing around high-contrast edges. Vignetting, another prevalent issue, manifests as a gradual darkening toward the image corners, driven by the cosine-fourth falloff of illuminance for oblique rays and partial obstruction by lens mounts or apertures in designs covering large fields.
Mitigation of these aberrations often involves optical design choices, such as aspherical lens elements that deviate from traditional spherical profiles to reduce barrel distortion and balance field curvature without increasing element count. For residual effects, software-based post-processing applies parametric corrections, using lens-specific profiles to remap distorted pixels via inverse transformations and equalize brightness, enabling effective compensation in digital workflows.

Lens Types

Rectilinear Wide-Angle Lenses

Rectilinear wide-angle lenses are optical designs that employ a rectilinear projection to preserve straight lines in the captured image, rendering features like building edges as straight rather than curved. This projection minimizes barrel distortion, a common issue in wide-angle optics where lines bow outward, making these lenses ideal for applications requiring geometric accuracy, such as architectural and interior photography.
The primary design goal of rectilinear lenses is to achieve a natural perspective over a broad field of view while correcting for the nonlinear angle-to-distance relationship inherent in wide-angle imaging, ensuring that the image plane represents the scene proportionally without intentional curvature. By using complex arrangements of aspherical and low-dispersion elements, these lenses counteract aberrations that would otherwise compromise linearity, prioritizing realism in representation over maximized angular coverage.
For full-frame sensors, common focal lengths in rectilinear wide-angle lenses span 14mm to 35mm, providing diagonal fields of view from approximately 63 to 114 depending on the exact length. This range balances expansive scene capture with manageable distortion control, allowing photographers to include more of the environment without excessive edge warping.
Representative examples include the Canon EF 16-35mm f/2.8L III USM, a professional zoom lens optimized for edge-to-edge sharpness and minimal distortion in ultra-wide scenarios. Similarly, the Nikon AF-S NIKKOR 14-24mm f/2.8G ED exemplifies rectilinear design with its rectilinear projection that keeps straight lines intact across the frame, making it a staple for landscape and architectural work.

Fisheye Lenses

Fisheye lenses represent a specialized category of wide-angle optics that intentionally employ a curved projection to capture hemispherical or near-full-circle fields of view, typically up to 180 degrees, resulting in pronounced barrel distortion where straight lines appear curved toward the edges. This design contrasts with rectilinear wide-angle lenses by prioritizing expansive angular coverage over geometric fidelity, making fisheye lenses ideal for creative panoramic imaging and immersive perspectives.
The core of fisheye functionality lies in their projection models, which map three-dimensional scene angles onto a two-dimensional image plane in a non-linear fashion. Fisheye lenses are categorized into variants based on image format and coverage: diagonal fisheye lenses provide a 180 field of view along the image diagonal, filling the entire rectangular frame with distorted content suitable for standard aspect ratios; in contrast, circular fisheye lenses project a 180 circular image within the frame, often resulting in vignette borders that emphasize the spherical effect. These variants allow flexibility in composition, with diagonal types suiting full-frame sensors for broader integration and circular types enabling isolated hemispherical views.
A notable example is the Samyang 8mm f/3.5 HD Fisheye, a manual-focus lens optimized for APS-C format cameras, delivering an approximately 180 diagonal field of view through 10 elements in 7 groups, including hybrid aspherical components to control aberrations while embracing the signature curvature.
In astrophotography, fisheye lenses excel at encompassing vast celestial expanses, such as the full Milky Way arc or entire night sky domes, by leveraging their extreme angular reach to create immersive, distortion-enhanced compositions that convey the scale of cosmic phenomena despite non-linear perspective.

Design and Construction

Optical Elements and Formulas

Wide-angle lenses typically incorporate 10 to 16 glass elements arranged in 8 to 12 groups to achieve the necessary optical corrections for their short focal lengths and broad fields of view. For instance, the Canon RF 10-20mm f/4 L IS STM features 16 elements in 12 groups, while the Nikon Z 20mm f/1.8 S uses 14 elements in 11 groups. These multi-element configurations allow designers to balance the competing demands of minimizing aberrations across a wide image circle.
Among these elements, aspherical lenses are commonly employed to control spherical aberration and distortion, particularly in the peripheral regions where wide-angle designs are prone to such issues. Fluorite elements, with their low refractive index and anomalous dispersion properties, are also integrated in some high-end wide-angle lenses to reduce chromatic aberrations that arise from the use of multiple glass types. These specialized materials enable sharper imagery by compensating for color fringing and other optical imperfections inherent to short focal length systems.

To ensure compatibility with single-lens reflex (SLR) cameras, which require a longer back focal distance to accommodate the swinging mirror, wide-angle lenses often adopt a retrofocus configuration. This design features a negative-power front lens group followed by a positive rear group, effectively lengthening the distance from the last element to the focal plane while maintaining a short effective focal length. Such an inverted telephoto arrangement, pioneered in the mid-20th century, allows wide-angle coverage (e.g., below 35mm on full-frame) without the rear elements encroaching on the camera body.

Focal Length Ranges and Aperture Considerations

Wide-angle lenses are characterized by focal lengths typically ranging from 8 mm to 35 mm on full-frame sensors, with equivalence calculations essential for smaller formats like APS-C to achieve comparable fields of view. For instance, a 24 mm lens on a full-frame sensor provides a field of view equivalent to approximately 16 mm on an APS-C sensor with a 1.5x crop factor, allowing photographers to select appropriate optics for desired coverage across camera systems.
Maximum apertures for wide-angle lenses generally fall between f/2.8 and f/4, balancing light-gathering capability with optical feasibility, though faster primes like f/1.4 exist for specific applications. Achieving wider apertures in wide-angle designs presents challenges, particularly in maintaining uniform edge illumination, as mechanical vignetting becomes more pronounced at open settings due to the lens's broad angle of coverage and internal obstructions blocking peripheral light rays.

Format-Specific Adaptations

35 mm and Full-Frame Lenses

Wide-angle lenses optimized for the 35 mm film format or equivalent full-frame digital sensors (36 mm 24 mm) are engineered to deliver expansive fields of view while maintaining compatibility with the format's aspect ratio and coverage requirements. In this format, a normal lens approximates the human eye's perspective with a focal length of around 50 mm, providing a horizontal angle of view of approximately 40. Lenses shorter than this are classified as wide-angle, typically encompassing standard wide-angle options from 24 mm to 35 mm, which offer horizontal fields of view between about 54 and 74, and ultra-wide-angle lenses from 10 mm to 24 mm, extending to 122 or more at the shorter end. These classifications enable photographers to capture broader scenes without stepping back, making them essential for landscapes, architecture, and environmental portraits on full-frame systems.
A critical design aspect for these lenses is the image circle, which must fully illuminate the sensor or film plane to avoid vignettingdarkening at the corners. For the 35 mm full-frame format, the frame diagonal measures approximately 43.3 mm, calculated as the square root of (36 + 24), necessitating an image circle diameter of at least this size for edge-to-edge uniformity. Lenses falling short in coverage would exhibit falloff, particularly noticeable in ultra-wide designs where retrofocus configurations are employed to maintain sufficient back focus distance for SLR mirror mechanisms. This requirement influences the optical formula, often incorporating multiple elements to project a larger, evenly lit circle while controlling aberrations inherent to short focal lengths.
Among the most celebrated examples are the Carl Zeiss Distagon T* 25 mm f/2, a retrofocus wide-angle prime renowned for its exceptional sharpness across the frame, low distortion, and robust build for full-frame SLR cameras like those in the Contax and Canon systems. Similarly, the Leica Summicron-M 28 mm f/2 ASPH stands as an iconic rangefinder lens, prized for its compact size, aspherical elements that minimize coma and astigmatism, and creamy bokeh, making it a staple for street and documentary photography on Leica M-series bodies. These models exemplify the high standards of optical performance tailored to the 35 mm format, balancing portability with professional-grade image quality.

Digital Sensor Impacts and Crop Factors

The transition from film to digital sensors in photography introduced significant changes to wide-angle lens performance, primarily due to variations in sensor sizes compared to the traditional 35 mm full-frame format. Smaller sensors, such as those in APS-C or Micro Four Thirds cameras, capture only a central portion of the lens's image circle, effectively cropping the field of view (FOV). This crop factor, defined as the ratio of the full-frame sensor's diagonal (approximately 43.3 mm) to the smaller sensor's diagonal, multiplies the actual focal length to yield the 35 mm equivalent focal length, calculated as effective focal length = actual focal length crop factor. For instance, APS-C sensors commonly have a crop factor of 1.5 (Nikon and Sony) or 1.6 (Canon), meaning a 16 mm lens on an APS-C camera provides an equivalent FOV to a 24 mm lens on full-frame. Micro Four Thirds sensors have a crop factor of approximately 2.
This cropping narrows the FOV for a given lens, which is particularly impactful for wide-angle optics where expansive coverage is desired; photographers must select shorter focal lengths to compensate and achieve similar wide-angle effects, potentially introducing more distortion or requiring lens redesigns optimized for the reduced image area. Additionally, smaller sensors exacerbate diffraction limitations, especially in high-megapixel configurations. Diffraction occurs when light passing through the aperture is limited by the f-number, creating an Airy disk that blurs fine details if it exceeds the pixel pitch; on compact sensors with densely packed pixels (e.g., 24+ megapixels on APS-C), this effect becomes noticeable at wider apertures like f/8 or f/11, earlier than on larger full-frame sensors where pixels are comparatively larger.
To address these challenges, manufacturers have developed dedicated lenses for digital crop sensors, featuring shorter focal lengths, optimized image circles, and sometimes built-in stabilization to enhance wide-angle usability without the inefficiencies of full-frame optics. A representative example is the Sony E 10-18mm f/4 OSS zoom lens, designed specifically for APS-C E-mount cameras, which delivers a 15-27 mm equivalent FOV with minimal vignetting and effective optical steady-shot for handheld wide-angle shooting in landscapes or architecture. Such adaptations improve light efficiency and reduce aberrations tailored to the sensor's dimensions, allowing digital systems to fully leverage wide-angle perspectives despite size constraints.

Applications and Uses

Still Photography

In still photography, wide-angle lenses are particularly valued for their ability to capture expansive scenes with a broader field of viewup to about 120 for ultra-wide variantsallowing photographers to fit tall or expansive subjects into the frame without needing to step back. They emphasize spatial relationships, add a sense of scale and immersion to images, and are essential tools for genres that require broad environmental context. In architectural photography, they convey a greater sense of grandeur in structures, while in landscapes, they create more epic vistas; additionally, the inherent distortion can be embraced for creative dramatic effects in building photography.
Landscape photography frequently employs wide-angle lenses to depict vast natural environments, such as mountains or seascapes, by foregrounding prominent elements like rocks or trees to create a heightened sense of depth and scale. Photographers often position the camera low to the ground to integrate foreground details with distant horizons, enhancing the immersive quality of the image.
In architectural photography, wide-angle lenses facilitate the documentation of building facades and interiors, but they can introduce perspective distortion where vertical lines converge. Tilt-shift wide-angle lenses address this by allowing the photographer to shift the lens plane relative to the sensor, correcting convergence and maintaining parallel lines without tilting the camera body.
A key technique with wide-angle lenses is hyperfocal focusing, which maximizes depth of field (DOF) to ensure sharpness from the nearest foreground object to infinity, ideal for expansive compositions. This involves setting the focus at the hyperfocal distancecalculated based on focal length, aperture, and circle of confusiontypically resulting in a shorter distance for wider lenses like 24mm at f/8, allowing everything beyond a few feet to appear in focus.
To minimize edge distortion in compositions, photographers compose with straight lines and subjects centered in the frame, avoiding placement near the periphery where barrel distortion is most pronounced; post-processing corrections can further refine this if needed.
Renowned photographer Ansel Adams exemplified wide-angle use in landscape work, employing a 10-inch lens on his 8x10 view camera to capture the Merced River and surrounding cliffs in Yosemite Valley, balancing constrained viewpoints with dramatic spatial depth.

Cinematography and Video

In cinematography, wide-angle lenses are essential for capturing establishing shots that convey expansive environments and contextualize the narrative within a broader setting, such as vast landscapes or architectural details. They also excel in action sequences by immersing audiences in dynamic movements, allowing full-body blocking and spatial relationships to unfold without frequent cuts. For instance, in The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring, custom wide prime lenses, including 10mm Zeiss Ultra Primes, were employed to heighten the epic scale of battles and journeys, enhancing the sense of grandeur and peril.
A key challenge with wide-angle lenses in video production is lens breathing, where the apparent field of view shifts during focus pulls due to changes in focal length, creating distracting visual inconsistencies in fluid scenes. This issue is particularly pronounced in wide-angle optics because their broader perspective amplifies even minor focal variations, complicating precise rack focuses in fast-paced footage. Additionally, flare becomes problematic in dynamic lighting conditions, as the wide field of view admits stray light from sources like the sun or artificial fixtures, reducing contrast and introducing artifacts that degrade image quality. Cinematographers mitigate this through lens hoods and angle adjustments, though it remains a persistent concern in outdoor or high-contrast shoots.
To achieve widescreen formats, anamorphic wide-angle lenses squeeze the horizontal image, enabling a wider field of view on standard sensors while producing characteristic horizontal flares and oval bokeh for a cinematic aesthetic, as seen in films like Star Wars. For handheld wide shots, which benefit from the lenses' broad perspective and reduced apparent shake, stabilizers such as gimbals provide essential support; devices like the DJI Ronin series allow operators to traverse uneven terrain while maintaining smooth, professional-grade stability in action-oriented video.

Historical Development

Early Innovations (19th to Mid-20th Century)

The development of wide-angle lenses in the 19th century began with the limitations of early photographic processes like the Daguerreotype, which initially relied on simple single-element convex lenses offering narrow fields of view suitable only for portraits. To capture broader landscapes, photographers adapted these designs, employing meniscus landscape lenses that achieved angles of view around 50 degrees, a significant expansion for the era. In 1840, the Voigtlnder company, in collaboration with mathematician Joseph Petzval, produced the first purpose-built photographic lensa brass-mounted Petzval portrait design with an f/3.6 aperturemounted on the inaugural all-metal Daguerreotype camera; while optimized for close-up sharpness, modifications to its focal length allowed for wider scenic views in subsequent adaptations.
A major breakthrough occurred in 1866 when British lensmaker John Dallmeyer and German optician Carl August von Steinheil independently patented the Rapid Rectilinear lens, a symmetric four-element design that corrected for astigmatism and field curvature, enabling wider angles of up to 60 degrees with minimal barrel distortion. This rectilinear approach marked the first practical wide-angle lens for landscape photography, balancing coverage and image flatness without the extreme curvature of earlier globe or pantoscopic designs like Thomas Sutton's 1859 water-filled panoramic lens. A notable early example was the 1862 Harrison Globe lens, which provided an approximately 80 angle of view on large-format cameras, marking the first practical wide-angle design. These innovations laid the groundwork for distortion-free wide views, influencing subsequent patents such as Richard Morrison's 1872 wide-angle modifications to globe lenses.
In the early 20th century, advancements focused on anastigmatic corrections to support even broader fields. Carl Zeiss introduced the Tessar lens in 1902, designed by Paul Rudolph as a compact four-element system with superior sharpness and contrast; although initially a normal-angle design at f/6.3, its versatile formula was adapted into wide-angle variants for large-format cameras, providing angles exceeding 70 degrees while maintaining edge-to-edge clarity. Complementing this, Taylor, Taylor & Hobson in England produced rectilinear wide-angle lenses around 19101924, such as their f/16 models covering 6.5 x 4.75-inch plates with focal lengths as short as 4.22 inches, emphasizing distortion-free perspectives for architectural and scenic work through refined symmetric constructions.
By the mid-20th century, the rise of 35mm single-lens reflex (SLR) cameras demanded wide-angle designs with extended back focal distance to clear the swinging mirror. In 1950, French firm Angnieux pioneered the solution with the Retrofocus Type R1, a 35mm f/2.5 lens featuring an inverted telephoto configurationnegative elements at the front diverging light to elongate the effective flange distanceallowing a 63-degree angle of view on Exakta SLRs without compromising performance. This post-World War II innovation, refined in 1953 with a 28mm f/3.5 version offering 75 degrees, enabled true wide-angle capabilities on SLRs and inspired widespread adoption across manufacturers.

Modern Advancements (Late 20th Century to Present)

In the late 20th century, advancements in optical materials significantly improved aberration control in wide-angle lenses, particularly through the integration of ultra-low dispersion (UD) glass elements in Canon's L-series lineup starting in the 1980s. Canon's UD glass, an economical alternative to fluorite, further reduces secondary spectrum aberrations, enabling compact wide-angle L-series lenses like the EF 20mm f/2.8L USM (introduced in 1992, building on 1980s EF mount foundations) to achieve high contrast and minimal color fringing. These materials set a benchmark for professional wide-angle optics, influencing subsequent designs across manufacturers by prioritizing aberration reduction over size constraints.
Entering the 21st century, anti-reflective nano-coatings emerged as a key innovation to combat flare and ghosting in wide-angle lenses, which are prone to internal reflections due to their numerous elements and wide fields of view. Nikon introduced Nano Crystal Coat in 2005, initially on telephoto lenses but quickly applied to wide-angles like the AF-S Nikkor 14-24mm f/2.8G ED (2007), where the ultra-fine nanoparticle layer suppresses stray light across a broad spectrum, improving contrast in backlit scenarios by up to 50% compared to prior multi-coatings. Canon followed with Subwavelength Structure Coating (SWC) in 2008, debuting on the EF 24mm f/1.4L II USM, a moth-eye-inspired nanostructure that minimizes reflections at oblique angles, reducing flare incidence in wide-angle compositions by creating a gradual refractive index transition. These coatings, now standard in high-end wide-angle zooms, enhanced overall image fidelity without altering optical formulas.
The proliferation of computational optics revolutionized wide-angle imaging in consumer devices during the 2010s, exemplified by Apple's introduction of an ultra-wide camera in the iPhone 11 series in 2019, equivalent to a 13mm focal length on full-frame. This system employs real-time algorithms to correct barrel distortion inherent in such short focal lengths using lens profile-based remapping, resulting in natural-looking perspectives without post-processing. By 2025, similar computational pipelines have integrated into mirrorless ecosystems, with computational enhancements in models like the Canon EOS R5 Mark II (2024) for in-camera processing, including improved distortion correction.
