When a user asks you to explain something, use analogies and simple language.

User: Explain how a computer works
Response: A computer is like a very fast, very obedient secretary. It has a brain called the CPU that can do math and follow instructions incredibly quickly, billions of times per second. It has a short-term memory called RAM where it keeps whatever it is currently working on, like papers spread across a desk. It has long-term storage called a hard drive or SSD where it keeps files permanently, like a filing cabinet. And it has input devices like a keyboard and mouse that let you give it instructions, and output devices like a screen that let it show you results. Everything the computer does is ultimately just math with ones and zeros, but it does that math so fast that it can display videos, play music, run games, and power the entire internet.

User: What is blockchain in simple terms?
Response: Imagine a notebook that records every transaction ever made. Now imagine that instead of one person keeping the notebook, thousands of people all keep identical copies. Every time a new transaction happens, everyone adds it to their notebook at the same time. If someone tries to cheat by changing an entry in their notebook, everyone else's notebooks still have the original record, so the cheat is immediately obvious. That is blockchain: a shared, distributed record that nobody can tamper with because too many copies exist. It is the technology behind cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin, but it can be used for any situation where you need a trustworthy, tamper-proof record.

User: How does a vaccine work?
Response: A vaccine is like a wanted poster for your immune system. It shows your body what a dangerous virus or bacterium looks like without actually making you sick. Your immune system studies the wanted poster, learns how to fight that specific invader, and creates memory cells that remember the enemy. Then, if the real virus ever shows up, your immune system recognizes it immediately and attacks before it can make you ill. Without a vaccine, your immune system has to figure out how to fight the virus from scratch, which takes days or weeks, during which time you get sick. The vaccine gives your army a head start.

User: Explain inflation like I am ten years old
Response: Imagine there are 100 toys and 100 dollars in the world. Each toy costs 1 dollar. Now imagine someone prints 100 more dollars, but there are still only 100 toys. Now there are 200 dollars chasing 100 toys. People start offering 2 dollars for each toy because they have more money to spend. The toys did not change. The money just became worth less because there is more of it. That is inflation: when there is too much money chasing too few things, prices go up and each dollar buys less than it used to.

User: What is machine learning?
Response: Machine learning is teaching a computer to learn from examples instead of giving it explicit rules. Imagine teaching a child to recognize dogs. You do not say "a dog has four legs, fur, a tail, and barks." Instead, you show the child thousands of pictures: "This is a dog. This is a dog. This is not a dog. This is a dog." After enough examples, the child can recognize dogs they have never seen before, including breeds that look nothing like the examples. Machine learning works the same way. You show the algorithm thousands of examples, it finds the patterns, and then it can make predictions about new data it has never seen.

User: How does electricity get to my house?
Response: Electricity starts at a power plant where a turbine spins a magnet inside a coil of wire, which generates electrical current. The electricity is then stepped up to very high voltage, hundreds of thousands of volts, for long-distance transmission through power lines. High voltage reduces energy loss during transport. When the electricity reaches your neighborhood, it passes through transformers that step the voltage back down to 120 or 240 volts, which is safe for household use. The electricity enters your house through the electrical meter and the breaker panel, then flows through the wires in your walls to every outlet, switch, and light fixture. The whole journey from power plant to your phone charger happens at nearly the speed of light.

User: Explain evolution in one paragraph
Response: Evolution is the process by which living things change over generations. Every organism produces offspring with small random variations. Some of these variations help the organism survive and reproduce better in its environment, such as thicker fur in a cold climate or sharper eyesight for a predator. The organisms with helpful variations are more likely to survive, have offspring, and pass those variations to the next generation. Over thousands and millions of generations, these small changes accumulate into dramatic differences, eventually producing entirely new species. Evolution does not have a goal or direction. It simply means that populations adapt to their environments over time through the differential survival of individuals with beneficial traits.
