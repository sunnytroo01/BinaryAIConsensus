Ruthann Friedman
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Ruthann Friedman (born July 6, 1944) is an American folk singer-songwriter and guitarist best known for composing the 1967 number-one hit "Windy," which was recorded by the sunshine pop band The Association and became one of the era's defining tracks.
Born in the Bronx, New York, as the youngest of three children in a leftist household, Friedman was exposed to folk music through her older sister's bohemian college friends and began playing guitar at age eight. Her family relocated to North Hollywood, California, in 1954 due to her father's health issues, where she attended Grant High School, wrote her first song at age 12, and developed a solitary interest in music amid personal challenges, including her father's death when she was 15. Influenced by beat poets like Jack Kerouac and Allen Ginsberg, as well as jazz, folk, country, and show tunes, Friedman moved in Los Angeles circles with figures such as Janis Joplin, the Grateful Dead, and Jefferson Airplane in the 1960s, even being considered briefly as a lead singer for the latter.
Her breakthrough came with "Windy," penned in about 20 minutes as an ode to an idealized free-spirited partner and featuring distinctive recorder and harmony elements; as one of the few women to write a number-one single in the rock era, it provided financial independence and marked her as a key contributor to 1960s pop. Signed to A&M Records, she released the psychedelic folk single "Little Girl Lost-and-Found" with her short-lived group The Garden Club in 1967, produced by Curt Boettcher and Tommy LiPuma. Her solo debut, the introspective folk album Constant Companion, followed in 1970 on Warner Bros./Reprise, drawing comparisons to Joni Mitchell and Linda Perhacs for its melodic vulnerability and poetic lyrics. She also contributed songs to the 1971 cult film Peace Killers and briefly ventured into entrepreneurship with her Easy Writer Portable Stationery Kit invention.
After her early recordings, Friedman largely retired from music for four decades to raise two daughters and pursue education, earning a B.A. in English from UCLA magna cum laude in the early 2000s. She returned in the 2000s with reissues like A Hurried Life: Lost Recordings 1965-1971 (2006, featuring her original "Windy" demo) and Windy: A Ruthann Friedman Songbook (2013), which revived interest in her "astral folk" style and earned praise from artists like Devendra Banhart. Her comeback album Chinatown (2013 on Wolfgang Records), produced with collaborators including Van Dyke Parks, blended personal narratives, political commentary, and covers like Peggy Seeger's "Springhill Mining Disaster," showcasing her wry humor and enduring voice. Now based in Venice, California, with her husband, Friedman continues songwriting, gardening, and mentoring young musicians, with recent performances as of 2023, maintaining a legacy as an understated yet influential figure in folk and psychedelic music.

Early Life

Childhood and Family Background

Ruthann Friedman was born on July 6, 1944, in the Bronx, New York, during the final years of World War II. As the youngest of three children, she grew up in a leftist household in a six-story walk-up apartment building on Gun Hill Road, where her family emphasized progressive values tied to labor unions and social justice. Her father owned a factory producing knock-off evening gowns and was a member of a garment workers' union, while a relativedescribed as her uncle in one account or her aunt's husband in anotherserved as president of the International Ladies' Garment Workers' Union (ILGWU), reflecting the family's deep involvement in organized labor.
Family dynamics were marked by tension, particularly in her relationship with her combative father, who suffered from a heart condition that influenced major life decisions. To navigate uncomfortable silences during car rides, the family often sang together, fostering a communal but strained atmosphere in their urban New York home filled with classical music, opera, and show tunes played on a Victrola. In 1954, at the age of ten, the family relocated to North Hollywood in the San Fernando Valley, California, primarily for her father's health, transitioning from the dense, walk-up apartments of the Bronx to a more suburban environment that offered space and milder climate. Her father died of a heart attack when she was 15, within five years of the move, adding to the personal challenges of her adolescence.
This urban-to-suburban shift exposed Friedman to new cultural landscapes, though details on her immediate family's adaptation remain limited. Her early non-musical interests included avid reading and an appreciation for literature and poetry, which her liberal upbringing encouraged as tools for understanding social issues and personal expression. These experiences, rooted in a household attuned to workers' rights and progressive ideals, helped shape her formative worldview before deeper engagements with music. Her initial exposure to folk music icons like Woody Guthrie came through her sister's bohemian college friends, further embedding cultural influences in her youth.

Musical Beginnings and Education

Ruthann Friedman developed an early passion for music, beginning at age eight when she picked up the guitar and taught herself to play by emulating records of folk pioneers Pete Seeger, Josh White, and Woody Guthrie, largely influenced by her sister's bohemian friends who introduced her to these artists.
By age twelve, Friedman had composed her first song, "I'll Never Fall in Love," which earned her a performance slot on the local television talent show Rocket to Stardom. This marked her initial public exposure, showcasing her budding songwriting talent.
Friedman attended Ulysses S. Grant High School in North Hollywood, California, after her family's relocation to the area in 1954, where she served as president of the school's Folk Song Club and honed her skills amid a growing interest in the local music scene. During her high school years, she began performing at "Hoot Nights" open-mic events at The Troubadour in West Hollywood, opportunities that connected her with emerging musicians including guitarist Steve Mann, who became a key mentor, and Hoyt Axton.
In adulthood, after a period focused on family and other pursuits, Friedman returned to education and earned a B.A. in English from the University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA) in 2003 at age 59, studying literature and poetry that further enriched her lyrical approach to songwriting.

Career

Emergence in the 1960s Folk Scene

Ruthann Friedman's professional music career began in earnest in 1963, when, at the age of 19, she secured her first paid gig at the Green Spider Coffee House in Denver, Colorado. This performance marked her transition from amateur pursuits to earning a living through music, amid the burgeoning folk revival. Drawing inspiration from high school-era appearances at the Troubadour in Los Angeles, where she participated in hootenanny nights alongside emerging talents like Hoyt Axton, Friedman honed her skills performing covers of Bob Dylan songs and traditional blues numbers.
In the mid-1960s, Friedman relocated to San Francisco as part of the hippie migration westward, initially arriving with friend and fellow musician Steve Mann and later living in the Haight-Ashbury district. There, she immersed herself in the vibrant California folk and psychedelic scenes, frequenting venues like the Fillmore Auditorium and Big Sur Folk Festival while traveling between San Francisco and Los Angeles. She formed close connections with key figures in the counterculture music world, including members of Jefferson Airplanesuch as Jorma Kaukonen and Jack Casady, with whom she briefly shared housingCountry Joe McDonald, Janis Joplin, whom she met backstage at the Fillmore, and Van Dyke Parks, introduced through Mann. These relationships placed her at the heart of the evolving Bay Area sound, blending folk traditions with emerging psychedelic experimentation.
By 1968, Friedman co-formed the band Petrus alongside guitarist Peter Kaukonen, brother of Jefferson Airplane's Jorma Kaukonen, as they toured up and down the California coast. The duo's collaboration reflected the transitional folk-rock ethos of the era, with Friedman contributing vocals and original material. Throughout the decade, she experimented with songwriting, crafting introspective pieces influenced by her surroundings, and delivered live performances in intimate settings such as coffee houses like the Fifth Estate in Hollywood and clubs including Barney's Beanery, where she showcased both covers and her budding compositions to receptive audiences in the folk circuit.

Breakthrough with "Windy" and Recordings

In 1967, while residing in an apartment at David Crosby's house in the Beverly Glen area of Los Angeles, Ruthann Friedman composed her breakthrough song "Windy" in approximately 20 minutes. The track, originally conceived about a boy but revised to feature a female protagonist, captured the era's breezy optimism and was quickly pitched to The Association through her connections in the San Francisco music scene.
Recorded by The Association and released as a single in 1967, "Windy" topped the Billboard Hot 100 chart for four weeks, marking a major commercial success and establishing Friedman as the third female songwriter to pen a solo number-one hit without male co-writersfollowing Carole King and Sharon Sheeley. The song's sunny melody and whimsical lyrics aligned perfectly with the burgeoning sunshine pop genre, blending folk-rock elements with orchestral arrangements that defined late-1960s radio hits.
Friedman's songwriting triumph paved the way for her recording career. In 1967, she released the psychedelic folk single "Little Girl Lost-and-Found" with her short-lived group The Garden Club on A&M Records, produced by Curt Boettcher and Tommy LiPuma. This culminated in the release of her debut album, Constant Companion, on Reprise Records in November 1969. Produced during the height of the sunshine pop and folk-rock movements, the LP featured 12 tracks, including originals like "Topsy-Turvy Moon," "Piper's Call," and "Fairy Prince Rainbow Man," which showcased her intricate, introspective style infused with psychedelic folk influences. The album's warm, acoustic-driven sound reflected the era's cultural shift toward more personal and harmonious expressions in popular music. In 1971, Friedman wrote and sang songs, including "White Dove" and "Rebel", for the cult film The Peace Killers.
Prior to and alongside Constant Companion, Friedman issued limited early singles and maintained a trove of unreleased material from the period, including demos of "Windy" and other compositions like "Hurried Life" recorded between 1965 and 1971. These works, later compiled in archival releases, highlighted her prolific output during the late 1960s, with home recordings and publishing demos capturing raw versions of songs that blended folk introspection with emerging pop sensibilities.

Hiatus and Later Revival

Following the release of her single "Carry On (Glittering Dancer)" in 1970, Friedman entered an extended hiatus from the music industry after a family tragedy involving her sister's suicide. She shifted her focus to raising her children and personal pursuits, effectively pausing her professional career for over three decades.
Friedman's return began in 2006 with the reissue of her 1970 album Constant Companion by Water Records, which sparked renewed interest in her work. That same year, Water also released Hurried Life, a compilation of 15 previously unreleased demos, home recordings, and lost songs from 1965 to 1971 drawn from her personal archive. In 2011, she issued the single "White Dove / Motorcycle Madness," featuring two unreleased studio tracks from 1970 produced by Van Dyke Parks and Kirby Johnson.
The year 2013 marked a significant resurgence with the release of Chinatown, her first album of new material in over 40 years, containing 11 tracks that blended folk elements with mature songwriting, including contributions from Van Dyke Parks on piano and accordion. Also in 2013, Now Sounds issued Windy, A Ruthann Friedman Songbook, an 18-track collection highlighting her compositions beyond her famous hit "Windy," featuring rare recordings and covers.
Friedman resumed local performances in the Los Angeles area, including shows at venues like Healing Force of the Universe in Pasadena in 2024. She appeared on the Vintage Annals Archive Podcast in March 2024, discussing her career and songwriting. She performed at the International Pop Overthrow festival in Los Angeles on August 3, 2025.

Personal Life

Marriage and Family

Ruthann Friedman married Jeffrey Carlisle, an ESPN senior writer, in 1979. The couple settled in California and welcomed two daughters, whom Friedman primarily raised in Venice Beach over four decades. She dedicated significant time to motherhood during this period, viewing it as a fulfilling priority that shaped her personal life.
In the late 1960s, shortly after the release of her debut album Constant Companion, Friedman endured a devastating family tragedy: the suicide of her sister around 1969. This loss profoundly affected her, prompting an emotional retreat as she returned to her family's Los Angeles home and shifted focus away from her public career. The event contributed to her temporary withdrawal from the spotlight, aligning with broader family challenges that influenced her decision to step back from music for several years.
As of 2023, Friedman and Carlisle remained married while living in Venice, California. Her daughters, now adultsone of whom became a doctorhave grown up with their mother's emphasis on family stability and personal growth.

Non-Musical Pursuits

During her extended hiatus from music, which spanned over three decades starting in the mid-1970s, Ruthann Friedman pursued higher education, completing a bachelor's degree in English from the University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA) in 2007, graduating magna cum laude. This academic endeavor allowed her to immerse herself in literature and poetry, enhancing her critical perspective on songwriting and writing in general.
Parallel to her studies, Friedman founded and operated a stationery business in the 1970s, initially named the Easy Writer Stationery Moving Company, which she co-started with a friend using playful graphics inspired by rolling papers. The venture later evolved into the Writing High Stationery Moving Company, sustaining her financially through the 2000s while she balanced entrepreneurial responsibilities with family life and academic commitments.
Her English degree directly informed her literary interests, as she maintained a lifelong habit of journaling that preserved her writing skills during her time away from music. This period of focused non-musical activitiesencompassing education, business management, and personal writingprovided a foundation for her creative revival, culminating in the 2006 reissue of her debut album and subsequent return to performing.

Discography

Studio Albums

Ruthann Friedman's debut studio album, Constant Companion, was released in late 1969 (sometimes dated to 1970) by Reprise Records (RS 6363). Produced by Joe Wissert at Sunwest Studios in Hollywood, California, the album features 11 original tracks by Friedman plus one cover, blending folk-rock with psychedelic elements, showcasing Friedman's fragile vocals and acoustic guitar work alongside lead guitar from Peter Kaukonen.
The album's themes revolve around introspection, whimsy, and emotional fragility, evident in tracks like "Fairy Prince Rainbow Man," which evokes Joni Mitchell-inspired psychedelia, and the title track "Constant Companion," a gentle folk closer reflecting solitude. Other highlights include "Piper's Call," with its haunting melody, and "People," drawing Simon & Garfunkel-like harmonies. The sparse arrangements emphasize Friedman's voice and guitar, though some critics noted they could benefit from fuller orchestration to enhance the material's potential.

The album's style echoes the breezy optimism of Friedman's hit "Windy," adapted here into more personal, psychedelic folk expressions.
Friedman's second studio album, Chinatown, emerged in 2013 on Wolfgang Records, marking her return after a long hiatus with 11 acoustic tracks that highlight her evolved songwriting. Produced by John Muller with contributions from notable musicians like Van Dyke Parks on piano for select songs, the album was recorded emphasizing Friedman's guitar and vocals, blending folk introspection with jazz inflections.
Themes of autobiography, reflection on aging, and subtle social critique dominate, as in the opening "That's What I Remember," which traces her life from childhood rebellion to later serenity, and "iPod," a wry commentary on modern detachment. Tracks like the jazzy title song "Chinatown" and the optimistic "What a Joy" convey a mellow grace, mixing personal stories with wry humor and 1960s idealism. Compilations and Singles

In the mid-2000s, a revival of interest in Ruthann Friedman's music led to the release of archival compilations that unearthed previously unavailable material from her early career. The inaugural such collection, Hurried Life: Lost Recordings 1965-1971, was issued in 2006 by Water Records as a CD featuring 15 tracks of unreleased demos, home recordings, and lost songs recorded between 1965 and 1971. This compilation provides a window into her formative songwriting, with selections like "Hurried Life," "That's All Right," "To Treat A Friend," and a raw demo of "Windy," alongside other originals such as "Sky Is Moving South" and "Morning Becomes You." The album was reissued on limited-edition baby blue vinyl in 2021 by Tompkins Square Records, marking the first vinyl pressing of this material.
Building on this momentum, Windy: A Ruthann Friedman Songbook followed in 2013 from Now Sounds as an 18-track CD compilation of rarities, alternate takes, and unreleased recordings spanning her career. Curated to showcase her diverse stylistic range, it includes tracks such as "Halfway There," "Birdie's Blues," "Faces," and covers like "I Think It's Going to Rain Today," emphasizing her folk and psychedelic influences through non-album gems. These compilations, alongside reissues of her core albums, played a key role in reintroducing her work to new audiences during the 2000s archival boom.
Friedman's singles output was limited but significant, particularly in highlighting standalone or promotional material. In 1967, as part of the short-lived group The Garden Club, she released the psychedelic folk single "Little Girl Lost-and-Found" b/w "I Must Love Her" on A&M Records (848), produced by Curt Boettcher and Tommy LiPuma.
Friedman wrote and performed two songs for the 1971 biker film The Peace Killers: "White Dove" and "Rebel". "White Dove" remained unreleased until later.
In 1970, Reprise Records released the 7" single "Carry On (Glittering Dancer)" backed with "People," drawn from her Constant Companion sessions and arranged by Kirby Johnson to capture her ethereal pop sensibility. Decades later, in 2011, Ether Records issued a limited-edition 7" vinyl single of "White Dove" b/w "Motorcycle Madness," featuring two previously unreleased studio tracks from 1970 produced by Van Dyke Parks and Kirby Johnson. This release, limited to a small pressing, underscored the ongoing archival efforts to preserve her lesser-known recordings.

Legacy

Songwriting Style and Influences

Ruthann Friedman's songwriting style is characterized by a seamless blend of folk traditions, sunshine pop sensibilities, and introspective singer-songwriter elements, often delivered through simple yet impactful lyrics that convey a mellow yet lively emotional resonance. Her work emphasizes personal introspection, drawing on themes of freedom, relationships, and the countercultural vibes of the 1960s and 1970s hippie era, as seen in songs that evoke communal harmony and self-discovery. This approach is exemplified in her composition "Windy," which captures a pop-folk crossover with its breezy, optimistic narrative.
Early influences on Friedman included folk pioneers Woody Guthrie, Pete Seeger, and Josh White, whom she listened to while learning guitar at age eight, shaping her foundational acoustic style rooted in social commentary and narrative storytelling. Over time, her sound evolved to incorporate psychedelic and astral folk elements, blending ethereal, cosmic atmospheres with progressive blues undertones, reflecting the experimental spirit of the late 1960s California scene. Additional inspirations from figures like Bob Dylan, David Crosby, and Timothy Leary further infused her writing with beatnik introspection and psychedelic lyricism.
In terms of technique, Friedman employs emotive fingerpicking on guitar, reminiscent of Josh White and Dave Van Ronk, providing a sparse yet supportive accompaniment that highlights her vocal delivery and lyrical depth. This method allows for an intimate, atmospheric quality in her compositions, prioritizing emotional nuance over ornate arrangements. Compared to contemporaries like Joni Mitchell, Friedman's style shares a feminist edge and confessional tone but achieved less commercial prominence, focusing instead on cult-favorite recordings that underscore her niche within the folk revival.

Recognition and Cultural Impact

Friedman's song "Windy," originally written in 1966, achieved widespread recognition when recorded by The Association, topping the Billboard Hot 100 for four weeks in 1967 and becoming one of the era's signature sunshine pop hits. This success marked Friedman as only the third female songwriter to pen a U.S. number-one single without a male co-writer, a milestone shared with predecessors like Sharon Sheeley and Carole King. The track's enduring popularity led to numerous covers, including instrumental versions by Percy Faith in 1967 and Living Brass in 1968, as well as renditions by artists like Teddy Robin & The Playboys and Sun Dragon, highlighting its versatility across pop, jazz, and folk interpretations. In 1999, "Windy" was honored by Broadcast Music, Inc. (BMI) as the 61st most-performed song of the 20th century, underscoring its lasting radio and cultural footprint.
Despite this breakthrough, Friedman's broader recognition has remained niche, often confined to folk and reissue enthusiasts who celebrate her as an "astral folk goddess" for her ethereal, introspective style. She has been championed by contemporary artists such as Devendra Banhart, and the reissue of her lost recordings Hurried Life: Lost Recordings 1965-1971originally released in 2006 and reissued on vinyl by Tompkins Square Records in 2021drew comparisons to cult figures like Linda Perhacs. This acclaim reflects her underappreciated role in the 1960s California folk scene, where she performed alongside luminaries and influenced the wave of female songwriters emerging in Los Angeles and San Francisco, though mainstream acknowledgment has been limited by her sporadic releases and personal hiatuses.
In recent years, Friedman has experienced a revival through media features and live performances, bridging her legacy to modern folk audiences. She appeared on the Jewish Vintage Annals Archive Podcast in March and August 2024, discussing her career and the creation of "Windy," which introduced her work to new listeners amid a broader interest in overlooked women in 1960s music. By 2025, she continued this momentum with appearances at McCabe's Guitar Shop in June alongside Rachel Goodrich and at UnUrban Coffee House in November with Dylan Kanner, events that emphasized her ongoing contributions to folk revival contexts. These reappraisals highlight persistent gaps in her mainstream visibilitydespite "Windy"'s ubiquitywhile affirming her foundational impact on female-led songwriting and the psychedelic folk aesthetic that resonates in today's indie scenes.
