Wheat Field with Cypresses
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Wheat Field with Cypresses is the title of three similar oil-on-canvas paintings produced by the Dutch post-impressionist artist Vincent van Gogh in 1889, during his voluntary confinement as a patient at the Saint-Paul-de-Mausole psychiatric asylum in Saint-Rmy-de-Provence, France. Each version portrays a sunlit golden wheat field swaying in the wind, dominated by tall, dark cypress trees in the foreground and framed by a swirling, turbulent sky of blues and whites, capturing the vibrant energy of the Provenal countryside through van Gogh's expressive impasto technique and vivid color palette.
The series originated in late June 1889, when van Gogh ventured outside the asylum to paint directly from nature, producing an initial study now held at The Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York (73.2 93.4 cm). He then created two studio renditions in September 1889 while confined indoors due to his health: a large-scale version at the National Gallery in London (72.1 90.9 cm) and a smaller replica originally intended as a gift for his mother and sister, currently in a private collection. A related reed-pen drawing of the composition, executed in the summer of 1889, is housed at the Van Gogh Museum in Amsterdam. Van Gogh regarded the cypress motif with particular affection, likening the trees to "flame-like" symbols of eternity and comparing their form to Egyptian obelisks in letters to his brother Theo, while viewing the overall landscapes as among his finest summery works.
These paintings exemplify van Gogh's fascination with the cypress as a Provenal emblem of death and rebirth, set against the life-affirming cycle of the wheat harvest, and demonstrate his innovative approach to conveying movement and emotion through rhythmic lines and textured brushwork influenced by the region's mistral winds. The series marks a pivotal moment in van Gogh's Saint-Rmy period, bridging his earlier Arles sunflowers with later starry night skies, and has been celebrated for its synthesis of natural observation and symbolic depth.

Historical Context

Van Gogh's Time at Saint-Rmy

In May 1889, following a severe mental breakdown in Arles that included the infamous incident on December 23, 1888, when Vincent van Gogh cut off part of his left ear in a state of acute confusion, he voluntarily admitted himself to the Saint-Paul-de-Mausole asylum in Saint-Rmy-de-Provence. The asylum, housed in a former Augustinian monastery, provided a structured environment amid the rolling hills of Provence, where Van Gogh sought treatment for his episodes of hallucinations, anxiety, and despair after several hospitalizations in Arles proved insufficient. Upon arrival on May 8, he was diagnosed with epilepsy and acute mania, though modern interpretations suggest possible bipolar disorder or temporal lobe epilepsy; for the first month, he was confined strictly to the asylum grounds to monitor his condition.
Van Gogh's daily routines at Saint-Paul-de-Mausole were highly regimented due to his restricted movements, allowing him to paint only within the asylum's walled garden or during supervised excursions beyond the grounds starting in June. He rose early, often working on drawings or paintings in the morning light, and used the asylum's communal meals and quiet evenings to maintain a semblance of normalcy, though he occasionally experienced relapses that left him bedridden. By summer, his mental state showed signs of improvement, enabling more frequent escorted walks to nearby wheat fields and olive groves, where he captured the Provenal landscape despite ongoing challenges like auditory and visual hallucinations. In July 1889, the relentless mistrala fierce, cold northwesterly winddisrupted his outdoor sessions, forcing him to secure his easel with ropes and paint in short, intense bursts as the gusts bent the vegetation and scattered his materials.
Throughout his stay, Van Gogh maintained a steady correspondence with his brother Theo, his primary emotional and financial support, sharing vivid accounts of the asylum's isolation and his resolve to continue creating art. In a letter dated late May 1889, he wrote, "I wanted to tell you that I think I've done well to come here, first, in seeing the reality of the life of the diverse mad or cracked people in this menagerie," reflecting on the communal yet confining atmosphere. By early September, as his condition stabilized further, he described periods of recovery amid suffering: "This inevitability of suffering and despair anyway, here I am again, recovered for a period I'm thankful for it," underscoring his determination to work through hallucinations that sometimes rendered him unable to paint for days. These letters reveal a gradual optimism by mid-1889, with Van Gogh producing over 150 works during his year at the asylum, including his broader series of wheat field paintings that documented the surrounding countryside.

Inspiration from the Local Landscape

The asylum of Saint-Paul-de-Mausole, where Vincent van Gogh resided from May 1889 to May 1890, was situated in the foothills of the Alpilles mountains near Saint-Rmy-de-Provence, providing expansive views of the surrounding Provenal countryside. From the east-facing window of his room, Van Gogh observed enclosed wheat fields, olive groves, and clusters of cypress trees stretching toward the rugged, limestone peaks of the Alpilles. These elements formed a vivid, sun-drenched landscape that captured his attention, with the distant mountains offering a stark, craggy backdrop to the fertile plains below.
In mid-summer 1889, during the height of the wheat harvest, the fields around the asylum glowed with golden hues under the intense Provenal sun, while cypress trees stood as tall, dark silhouettes punctuating the bright expanse. The painting Wheat Field with Cypresses reflects this seasonal vibrancy, executed in July or early September amid the ripening crops and clear summer skies. The cypresses, with their flame-like forms rising dramatically against the luminous fields, became a focal point, evoking the dynamic interplay of light and shadow in the Mediterranean heat.
Van Gogh expressed profound fascination with the Provenal landscape in his correspondence, particularly the cypresses, which he described as "beautiful as regards lines and proportions, like an Egyptian obelisk" and a "splash of black in a sunny landscape." In letters to his brother Theo, he highlighted the "beautiful effects" of sunlight filtering through the wheat and the relentless mistral wind rippling across the fields, creating movement and shifting patterns of light that contrasted sharply with the arid, rocky terrain of the nearby Alpilles. These natural phenomena inspired him to capture the region's intense luminosity and vitality, viewing the cypresses as emblematic of the area's timeless, sculptural beauty.
The vista in Wheat Field with Cypresses draws from Van Gogh's permitted walks around the asylum grounds, where he sketched and painted typical rural scenes of undulating fields uninterrupted by human figures. This perspective allowed him to immerse himself in the unpopulated expanse, emphasizing the harmonious rhythm of nature without the intrusion of daily life.

Creation and Versions

The En Plein Air Version

The en plein air version of Wheat Field with Cypresses was created by Vincent van Gogh in late June 1889, during his voluntary confinement at the Saint-Paul-de-Mausole asylum in Saint-Rmy-de-Provence, France. Painted directly outdoors on site amid the strong gusts of the Provenal mistral wind, Van Gogh employed a portable easel to capture the landscape's immediacy on a large canvas measuring 73 cm 93.4 cm, cataloged as F717 in standard references to his works.
The mistral's forceful conditions influenced the painting's execution, leading to a notably thicker impasto as Van Gogh layered paint heavily to combat the wind and evoke its dynamic effects on the swaying wheat and turbulent sky. This outdoor approach imparts a sense of raw vitality and spontaneity, with more vigorous swirling patterns in the sky than seen in the later studio iterations.
On July 2, 1889, Van Gogh wrote to his brother Theo describing the composition as one featuring cypresses amid ears of wheat, poppies, and a vivid blue sky akin to a Scottish plaid, which he regarded as among his finest summer landscapes. Shortly thereafter, he dispatched the freshly completed canvas to Theo in Paris, underscoring its significance in his prolific output from the asylum.

The Studio Versions

Following the en plein air version painted in late June 1889, Vincent van Gogh created two studio versions of Wheat Field with Cypresses in September 1889 while residing in the asylum at Saint-Rmy-de-Provence, working from memory and preparatory sketches in his indoor studio. These replicas allowed him to revisit the composition under controlled conditions, resulting in more refined executions compared to the impulsive outdoor original.
The larger studio version measures 72.1 cm 90.9 cm and is housed in the National Gallery, London (F615, JH 1755). Intended partly as a potential sale piece, it features a smoother finish and adjusted perspective for greater harmony, including the addition of an olive bush on the left side to balance the composition against the dominant cypresses on the right. The smaller version, 51.5 cm 65 cm and held in a private collection (F743, JH 1790), was created as a gift for Van Gogh's mother and sister, with its format emphasizing the vertical thrust of the central cypress tree through tighter framing and heightened contrast.
In correspondence with his brother Theo, Van Gogh noted that these studio replicas were executed more quickly than the original, enabling him to produce multiple iterationsincluding small-scale copies of this and other worksfor personal exchange and exhibition purposes. This approach reflected his strategy to replicate favored compositions for broader dissemination while refining details like color modulation and spatial depth indoors.

Artistic Description

Composition and Motifs

In Wheat Field with Cypresses, Vincent van Gogh arranges the scene horizontally across the canvas, with a vast wheat field occupying the foreground and extending toward the midground, where it meets clusters of vegetation including a prominent olive bush on the left and two tall cypress trees dominating the right side. The cypresses rise vertically like obelisks, serving as a central, flame-shaped accent that anchors the composition and draws the viewer's eye upward from the undulating field. In the background, rolling hills and the distant Alpilles mountains provide a receding horizon, while the turbulent sky fills the upper third, introducing a sense of expansive depth and atmospheric movement. This layout emphasizes the isolation of the natural landscape, with no human figures present to interrupt the sweeping vista.
The key motifs draw from the Provenal countryside near Saint-Rmy, where van Gogh painted during his time at the asylum. The golden wheat field evokes the abundance of the summer harvest, swaying in implied wind to convey vitality across the flat expanse. The cypresses, with their dark, evergreen forms, act as stark vertical contrasts against the horizontal flow of the field and hills, creating a rhythmic tension in the spatial structure. Olive trees and bushes in the midground add layered texture to the foreground-to-background progression, reinforcing the thematic focus on untamed nature.
Van Gogh employs a low viewpoint, looking upward from the edge of the wheat field, which enhances the sense of immersion and scale, transforming the flat Provenal terrain into a dynamic scene with pronounced depth from the near ground to the far mountains. Swirling cloud formations in the sky contribute to this movement, linking the earthly motifs to the overhead expanse and unifying the composition's vertical and horizontal elements.
Across the three oil versionspainted in late June and September 1889subtle variations appear in the cypress positioning and sky intensity, with the en plein air study (Metropolitan Museum of Art) featuring a more immediate, vibrant execution with emphasis on the trees, while the studio version (National Gallery, London) refines the rhythmic lines of the mountains and field edges for greater harmony. The smaller replica in a private collection maintains the core layout without altering the dominant motifs.

Technique and Style

Van Gogh executed Wheat Field with Cypresses using oil on canvas, a medium that allowed for the layered application of paint central to his Post-Impressionist approach. All three versions share this material base, enabling the buildup of dense pigment layers that contribute to the paintings' tactile quality.
His brushwork is defined by thick impasto, applied especially to the rolling wheat fields and turbulent sky to impart a sculptural texture that captures the landscape's immediacy and energy. Swirling, rhythmic strokes predominate, mimicking the wind's movement across the fields, clouds, and cypress forms, thereby infusing the scene with dynamic motion. These techniques enhance the composition's rhythmic flow, guiding the viewer's eye from the foreground wheat to the dominant trees.
The color palette employs vibrant contrasts to evoke the intense Provenal sunlight: chrome yellow mixed with zinc white dominates the sunlit wheat fields, cobalt blue and zinc white form the expansive sky, viridian and emerald green define the vegetation and cypress foliage, while darker tones, including black accents, render the cypresses as striking vertical silhouettes against the brighter elements.
Drawing from Japanese prints, Van Gogh integrated bold outlines and flat areas of color, which amplify the expressive distortion in the forms and heighten the emotional resonance. This stylistic evolution extends the bold experimentation of his Arles periodmarked by vivid hues and simplified shapesbut intensifies during his Saint-Rmy asylum stay, where personal turmoil manifests in more exaggerated, turbulent expressions of nature.

Provenance and Legacy

Ownership History

The en plein air version of Wheat Field with Cypresses, painted in late June 1889, was initially owned by Vincent van Gogh's brother Theo van Gogh following its creation. Upon Theo's death in 1891, it passed to his widow, Jo van Gogh-Bonger, who managed the artist's estate. It was owned by the Berlin collector Franz von Mendelssohn before 1939, stored by a friend outside Berlin during World War II, inherited by Peter Witt in 1949 after he emigrated to Switzerland, sold to the Swiss industrialist Emil G. Bhrle in 1951, and passed to his son Dieter Bhrle after 1956. The Metropolitan Museum of Art acquired it in 1993 through a purchase supported by a $57 million gift from the Annenberg Foundation.
The larger studio version, executed in September 1889, also entered Theo van Gogh's collection upon completion. After Theo's death, it passed to Jo van Gogh-Bonger. In 1903, she sold it to the British collector Lord Marcus Salting via the dealer Arthur Tooth & Sons. Salting bequeathed the painting to the National Gallery in London upon his death in 1910, though it was formally acquired by the institution in 1923 using the Courtauld Fund and has been in its permanent collection since.
The smaller studio version, completed in September 1889 as a gift from Vincent to his mother and sister, remained in private hands within the van Gogh family until 1970, when it fetched $1.25 million at Sotheby's in New York. The painting was sold again in 1987 at Sotheby's New York for $53.5 million to an anonymous private owner, where it remains today.
Posthumous exhibitions of van Gogh's works, including versions of Wheat Field with Cypresses, in Paris in 1901 at Galerie Bernheim-Jeune and in Amsterdam in 1905 at the Stedelijk Museum, significantly boosted market interest and contributed to the rising values seen in subsequent sales of the paintings.

Current Locations and Cultural Impact

The three oil versions of Wheat Field with Cypresses are currently housed in prominent institutions and a private collection. The en plein air version, painted in JuneJuly 1889, is on view at the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York, located in Gallery 822. The larger studio version, completed in September 1889, resides at the National Gallery in London, displayed in Room 43. The smaller studio version remains in a private collection in the United States and is occasionally loaned for public exhibitions.
These paintings have been featured in notable exhibitions that highlight Van Gogh's Provence landscapes. In 2023, the Metropolitan Museum of Art's "Van Gogh's Cypresses" exhibition reunited the New York and London versions for the first time, drawing over 400,000 visitors and emphasizing the artist's fascination with cypress trees. Digital reproductions of the works have appeared in immersive museum displays worldwide, enhancing public access to Van Gogh's oeuvre.
Beyond exhibitions, the paintings hold significant cultural resonance. They appear in the 2017 animated film Loving Vincent, which recreates Van Gogh's style through hand-painted frames, contributing to renewed interest in his life and mental health struggles during his asylum stay at Saint-Rmy. The works' creation amid Van Gogh's voluntary commitment to the Saint-Paul-de-Mausole asylum has influenced discussions in modern art therapy, where they illustrate the therapeutic potential of landscape painting for emotional expression and recovery.
High-resolution scans of the Metropolitan Museum and National Gallery versions have been available online since 2011 through museum websites and the Google Arts & Culture platform, allowing global audiences to zoom into intricate details like the swirling brushstrokes and vibrant color contrasts. This digital accessibility has democratized engagement with the paintings, supporting educational programs and virtual tours.

Symbolism and Reception

Van Gogh's Intentions

Van Gogh expressed particular satisfaction with Wheat Field with Cypresses, viewing it as a vital achievement during his voluntary confinement at the Saint-Paul-de-Mausole asylum in Saint-Rmy-de-Provence. In a letter to his brother Theo dated 25 June 1889, he described the cypress tree central to the composition as "beautiful of line and proportion as an Egyptian obelisk," emphasizing its distinctive green and its role in adding dynamism to the scene. He further elaborated on 2 July 1889, detailing the painting's elementsa canvas of cypresses with ears of wheat, poppies, and a blue sky reminiscent of "a piece of multicoloured Scotch plaid"and noted its execution in thick impasto, underscoring his pride in its brightness and energy. Reputable analyses of his correspondence confirm that he regarded this work as one of his best summer landscapes, highlighting its vitality as a standout among his Provenal output.
His motivations for creating the painting stemmed from a deep fascination with the cypress, which he sought to render as "a splash of black in a sunny landscape," capturing its intriguing contrast and difficulty as a subject. The surrounding wheat field served as an emblem of life's cyclical renewal, a recurring motif in his writings that reflected his philosophical contemplation of growth, harvest, and regeneration amid personal turmoil. This choice aligned with his broader intent to channel the restorative power of nature, as the cypress motif drew directly from the iconic trees of the local Provenal countryside.
Philosophically, Van Gogh connected the painting to themes of eternity and nature's grandeur, using it to counterbalance his isolation and mental health challenges during recovery. In letters from this period, he linked expansive landscapes like wheat fields to infinite cycles and spiritual depth, finding in them a sense of timeless continuity that affirmed his artistic productivity. The work formed part of a series of canvases produced in the summer of 1889, aimed at demonstrating his ongoing creative output and resilience while under medical supervision at the asylum.

Critical Interpretations

Scholars have interpreted the cypress trees in Wheat Field with Cypresses as potent symbols of death and eternity, drawing on their traditional associations in Mediterranean culture as markers of mourning and the afterlife. Van Gogh himself likened the cypresses to "flame-like" forms, and art historian Ronald Pickvance discussed their significance in the context of Van Gogh's Saint-Rmy period in his 1986 exhibition catalog for the Metropolitan Museum of Art, linking the motif to the artist's preoccupation with life's impermanence. Complementing this, the golden wheat fields evoke transience and renewal, influenced by Van Gogh's admiration for Japanese ukiyo-e prints, which often depicted nature's cyclical beauty and ephemerality through bold, flattened compositions.
Stylistically, the painting exemplifies Van Gogh's Post-Impressionist innovations, where distorted forms and vibrant color express inner emotion rather than mere optical reality. In his 1950 monograph Van Gogh, art historian Meyer Schapiro analyzed such landscapes as breakthroughs in conveying psychological intensity through rhythmic brushwork and heightened contrasts, transforming the Provenal countryside into a vehicle for subjective experience. This approach, as Schapiro noted, marked a shift from Impressionist transience to a more expressive, almost symbolic rendering of nature's vitality and turmoil.
In 20th- and 21st-century scholarship, interpretations have increasingly tied the work to Van Gogh's mental health struggles, with the turbulent sky and looming cypresses reflecting episodes of anxiety and isolation during his asylum period. A 2023 BBC Culture article described the cypresses as Van Gogh's "darkest symbol," representing resilience amid despair, while also evoking the artist's fragile psychological state through their dark, undulating silhouettes. Comparisons to The Starry Night (1889) highlight shared swirling motifs, where cosmic energy in the sky mirrors the emotional vortices in both compositions, underscoring Van Gogh's innovative use of movement to convey turmoil.
The painting's reception evolved from mixed contemporary responses in the 1890s to its current status as an iconic piece in major retrospectives. Following Van Gogh's death in 1890, his works at exhibitions like the Socit des Artistes Indpendants elicited divided critiques, with some reviewers praising the bold vitality while others dismissed the "crude" distortions as signs of madness. By the 2020s, Wheat Field with Cypresses has become central to exhibitions such as the Metropolitan Museum of Art's 2023 show Van Gogh's Cypresses, which reunited multiple versions of the painting to explore Van Gogh's fascination with the motif. Recent analyses, such as Michael Lobel's 2024 book Van Gogh and the End of Nature, further frame the undulating fields as prescient symbols of environmental cycles disrupted by human impact.
