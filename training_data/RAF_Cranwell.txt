 RAF Cranwell is a Royal Air Force station located near Sleaford in Lincolnshire, England, serving as the primary center for initial officer training and aviation education within the RAF. Originally commissioned as the Royal Naval Air Service Central Training Establishment on 1 April 1916 under Commodore Godfrey M. Paine, the site spans approximately 2,500 acres requisitioned from the Earl of Bristol's estate. Following the formation of the Royal Air Force on 1 April 1918, it was redesignated RAF Cranwell and initiated flying training operations that same year.
The station's most defining feature is the Royal Air Force College Cranwell, established as the world's first military air academy on 5 February 1920 by Air Marshal Sir Hugh Trenchard, with the inaugural cadet course commencing that day. The college's foundation stone for College Hall was laid in 1929, and College Hall was opened in 1934 by the Prince of Wales. Over its history, RAF Cranwell has been pivotal in aviation milestones, including the first flight of the Gloster Whittle jet prototype on 16 May 1941 and the maiden flight of the Gloster F9/40 (later Meteor) in 1943; it also trained notable figures such as then-Prince Charles in 1971.
In its contemporary role, RAF Cranwell hosts a range of training institutions, including No 3 Flying Training School, No 6 Flying Training School, the Central Flying School, the Air Warfare School, the Tedder Leadership Academy, and the Robson Academy of Studies. It is also home to the Band of the RAF College, RAF Recruitment, and the RAF Air Cadets headquarters, while supporting ongoing activities such as basic flying instruction with aircraft like the Grob Tutor and Phenom 100.

Historical Development

Origins and Establishment

The Royal Naval Air Service (RNAS) established its Central Training Establishment at Cranwell in Lincolnshire during World War I to address the growing need for trained air personnel. In November 1915, the Admiralty requisitioned approximately 2,500 acres of farmland on the Cranwell Hall estate to create a dedicated training site, selected for its central location that could support emerging RNAS stations along the East Coast. Construction began in December 1915, including a hutted camp for accommodations, aircraft hangars, and balloon sheds to facilitate initial operations. The establishment was officially commissioned on 1 April 1916 under the command of Commodore Godfrey M. Paine, serving as a surrogate central flying school for the RNAS to train officers and ratings independently of existing facilities.
The primary purpose of the Cranwell site was to provide comprehensive training for pilots and observers, focusing on flying aeroplanes, kite balloon observation, and airship operations to bolster coastal defenses and shore-based aviation efforts. Early training utilized light aircraft such as the Avro 504, a versatile two-seat trainer employed from April 1916 for basic flight instruction, while the Sopwith Pup served as an intermediate trainer for advancing students by mid-1918, emphasizing its docile handling characteristics. Infrastructure development prioritized practicality for wartime needs, with grass runways on the northern airfield enabling initial flying activities from 1916, supplemented by the hangars for aircraft maintenance and storage. A Boys' Training Wing was also incorporated to prepare air mechanics and riggers, ensuring a steady supply of ground support personnel.
On 1 April 1918, coinciding with the formation of the independent Royal Air Force through the amalgamation of the RNAS and Royal Flying Corps, the Cranwell establishment transitioned to RAF control and was renamed RAF Cranwell. This shift marked the site's evolution from a naval-focused training hub to a foundational RAF facility, with subsequent expansions into formalized officer training programs in the interwar years.

Interwar Period

Following the formation of the Royal Air Force in 1918, the need for a dedicated institution to train its permanent commissioned officers became evident, leading to the establishment of the RAF Cadet College at Cranwell on 1 November 1919. This marked the creation of the world's first military air academy, designed specifically to produce career officers through a structured program. Under the leadership of its first commandant, Air Commodore Charles Longcroft, appointed on 3 November 1919, the college achieved command status on 5 February 1920, coinciding with the arrival of the initial intake of 52 flight cadets. These cadets, drawn primarily from public schools and universities, underwent a rigorous two-year course that integrated academic subjects such as mathematics, history, and languages with military discipline and practical aeronautical instruction, including flight training on biplanes like the Avro 504. The curriculum emphasized leadership development and the ethical foundations of air power, setting a precedent for RAF officer education that balanced intellectual rigor with operational readiness.
During the 1920s, the college's infrastructure began to evolve from temporary naval huts inherited from World War I to more permanent facilities, reflecting the RAF's commitment to institutional permanence in peacetime. Efforts spearheaded by Air Minister Sir Samuel Hoare secured funding for expansive construction, with architectural plans for the main college buildings finalized in 1929. The Officers' Mess was completed in 1933, followed by the opening of College Hall in 1934 by the Prince of Wales (later Edward VIII), which included grand halls, squadrons blocks, and ceremonial gates that became iconic landmarks. These developments not only accommodated growing cadet numbersreaching around 100 per entry by the mid-1920sbut also symbolized the academy's role in fostering a distinct RAF ethos. Concurrently, flying facilities expanded with the addition of grass runways and aircraft hangars to support the aeronautical component of training, enabling standardized protocols for pilot qualification that influenced RAF-wide practices.
Notable milestones in the interwar era underscored the college's maturation. The first passing-out parade for the inaugural entry occurred in early 1922, marking the graduation of the initial cohort as commissioned pilots and establishing annual traditions of ceremonial reviews. Under Air Marshal Sir John F. A. Higgins, who served as commandant from December 1926 to September 1930, the institution underwent significant administrative refinement, including the simplification of its title to RAF College in 1929 and enhanced emphasis on interdisciplinary training to prepare officers for emerging technologies like wireless communication and aerial reconnaissance. Higgins's tenure, drawing on his experience as a World War I squadron leader, promoted a culture of innovation and discipline that solidified Cranwell's position as the cornerstone of RAF officer development, training approximately 700 graduates by 1939 who would later lead in World War II. By the late 1930s, as rearmament accelerated, the college's protocols for selection and instruction had become the model for auxiliary training units across the service.

World War II

During World War II, RAF Cranwell underwent significant transformation to meet the urgent demands of the conflict, shifting from its pre-war focus on officer cadet training to serving as a primary flying training facility. With the outbreak of war in September 1939, instructional staff and cadets were recalled from leave on 30 August, and regular officer cadet courses were suspended, with the last flight cadets commissioned in March 1940. The site was repurposed as the RAF College Service Flying Training School (SFTS), later redesignated No. 17 SFTS in 1940, forming a key part of No. 21 Group within Flying Training Command, which oversaw pilot instruction from December 1938 until July 1944. This adaptation allowed Cranwell to contribute to the accelerated production of pilots essential for RAF operations, training airmen in ab initio and advanced flying over three-month courses, including personnel from allied nations. The station also hosted significant aviation milestones, including the first flight of the Gloster Whittle jet prototype on 16 May 1941 and the maiden flight of the Gloster F9/40 (later Meteor) in 1943.
The training at Cranwell emphasized practical flight instruction for Bomber Command and Fighter Command personnel, utilizing a diverse fleet of aircraft suited to elementary and service-level training. By the war years, the station hosted 12 types, including the de Havilland Tiger Moth for basic instruction, North American Harvard for advanced aerobatics and gunnery, Airspeed Oxford for multi-engine familiarization, and Miles Master as an intermediate trainer, alongside earlier biplanes like the Avro Tutor and Hawker Hart. Spitfires and Avro Ansons were also employed for specialized advanced courses, enabling trainees to progress from solo flights to operational readiness. These programs hosted advanced flying courses that prepared pilots for frontline duties, with commissioning rates adjusted from 100% pre-war to a selective percentage based on performance upon earning wings.
To accommodate the wartime expansion, Cranwell's infrastructure was adapted for heightened operational tempo and defensive needs, supporting a peak strength of approximately 7,000 servicemen and 3,000 civilians. The airfield featured grass surfaces in the northern sector and a mix of grass and asphalt runways in the south, facilitating increased air traffic without major disruptions. Additional barracks and support facilities were constructed or repurposed to house the influx of trainees, while standard blackout measuresmandatory across UK military siteswere implemented to mitigate risks from Luftwaffe raids during the 1940 Blitz period. Despite threats from aerial attacks, Cranwell demonstrated resilience, maintaining continuous training operations throughout the conflict without significant interruptions from enemy action.

Post-War Evolution

Following the conclusion of World War II, RAF Cranwell experienced a period of demobilization and reorganization as the Royal Air Force scaled back its wartime operations. The station, which had served as No. 17 Service Flying Training School during the conflict, reverted to its peacetime functions, with the Royal Air Force College reopening for cadet training in September 1947. This transition reflected the broader contraction of RAF personnel and facilities amid post-war austerity, though Cranwell retained its central role in officer development.
The Korean War (19501953) reversed this trend, prompting a temporary expansion of RAF flying training to support increased operational needs, including the introduction of jet aircraft for advanced and refresher courses. New training units were established across the service, and Cranwell contributed to this effort by incorporating early jet trainers like the de Havilland Vampire and Gloster Meteor in the early 1950s, aligning with the RAF's shift to all-jet elementary flying programs. These expansions were short-lived, with many units disbanding after the armistice as training demands normalized.
During the Cold War, institutional changes solidified Cranwell's position in the RAF's professionalization. In 1966, the Royal Air Force College merged with the RAF Technical College from Henlow, broadening its mandate to encompass engineering and technical officer training alongside traditional leadership education. This integration enhanced efficiency and supported the service's need for specialized personnel amid escalating nuclear and conventional threats. The station also hosted the temporary headquarters of the Central Flying School from April 1976 to September 1977, facilitating standardized instructor training during a period of doctrinal refinement.
In the late 20th century, Cranwell adapted to technological and organizational shifts, including a growing emphasis on multi-engine pilot training in the 1960s and 1970s as the RAF diversified its aircrew roles beyond single-engine fighters. No. 45 Squadron, reformed as No. 45 (Reserve) Squadron in 1992, exemplified this evolution by delivering advanced fixed-wing instruction at the station using aircraft like the Handley Page Jetstream T1. By the 1990s, Cranwell became the permanent home of Air Cadet Headquarters, supporting youth development programs, while the RAF pursued digitization of personnel and training records to modernize administrative processes. Following the 9/11 attacks in 2001, the station implemented enhanced security protocols as part of wider RAF measures to counter terrorism, including improved perimeter defenses and access controls.

Current Operations and Training

Officer Training Programs

The Military Initial Officer Training Course (MIOTC) at RAF College Cranwell serves as the foundational program for developing RAF officers, emphasizing leadership, military skills, and professional acumen over a 24-week duration (or 18 weeks for those with prior service). This course prepares cadets for commissioning by integrating academics, physical conditioning, and practical exercises to foster decision-making under pressure and alignment with RAF core values. Conducted in multiple intakes throughout the year, it accommodates around 200 cadets annually, drawing from diverse backgrounds to build a cohesive officer corps.
The curriculum is divided into four progressive modules, each building on the previous to transition cadets from basic induction to advanced command responsibilities. Module 1 focuses on military skills, covering weapons handling, counter-chemical, biological, radiological, and nuclear (CBRN) training, drill, first aid, field craft, general service knowledge, and live firing on ranges, culminating in Practical Leadership Exercise 1 (PLE1) to instill discipline and teamwork fundamentals. Module 2 advances self-awareness through Development 1 activities, including academics on air and space power, command and leadership studies, and defence writing; field exercises like Phoenix Edge and Eagle's Edge; a residential leadership course at Crickhowell; and PLE2, which tests resilience in simulated operational scenarios. Module 3 shifts to Development 2, emphasizing line management and command via Exercise Commander's Edge, welfare responsibilities, ethical decision-making, and introductory air command and control training, preparing cadets for junior leadership roles. The final Module 4, Consolidation, integrates all prior learning through summative assessments, a simulated expeditionary air wing headquarters exercise, and preparation for graduation, reinforcing themes such as strengths deployment inventory, conflict understanding, team building, and RAF ethos including service, integrity, and excellence. Physical fitness is embedded throughout, with regular Multi-Stage Fitness Tests (MSFT) requiring level 8.06 for males and 6.07 for females, alongside endurance runs and strength conditioning to ensure operational readiness.
Entry to the MIOTC requires candidates to be at least 17.5 years old (up to 48 for certain branches), hold UK nationality or specific residency qualifications, and meet academic thresholds equivalent to 2 A-levels at grade C/4 or above (or 64 UCAS points from two subjects), alongside GCSEs in English and maths at grade C/4 or higher. Selection involves aptitude testing, medical examinations, interviews assessing leadership potential, and a pre-joining fitness assessment comprising a 2.4 km run in under 13:20 minutes for males or 15:48 minutes for females (ages 1729). A familiarization visit to Cranwell includes a confirmatory MSFT and immersion in the training environment to gauge suitability. Training occurs primarily in the RAF College's historic facilities, such as College Hall for accommodation and messes, and modern classrooms for academic sessions, with outdoor areas for leadership exercises and ranges; simulation-based learning for command scenarios utilizes dedicated spaces within the college grounds.
Upon successful completion, cadets are commissioned as Pilot Officers, ready for specialist branch training while embodying RAF values through instilled habits of ethical leadership and operational excellence. The program's emphasis on holistic development ensures officers can lead in diverse contexts, from air operations to ground support, with a pass rate reflecting rigorous standards that prioritize long-term service effectiveness.

Flying Training Activities

No. 3 Flying Training School, headquartered at RAF Cranwell, is responsible for delivering elementary flying training and multi-engine pilot training to ab initio pilots from the Royal Air Force, Royal Navy, and Army Air Corps. The school's syllabus emphasizes foundational skills such as basic handling, navigation, instrument flying, and formation tactics, progressing from initial solo flights to more advanced maneuvers in preparation for operational conversion units. Elementary training utilizes the Grob Prefect T1, a turboprop aircraft operated by No. 57 Squadron, which supports up to 50 hours of flight time per student in a structured 24-week course. Advanced multi-engine phases employ the Embraer Phenom 100 T1 with No. 45 Squadron, focusing on crew resource management, systems operation, and mission-specific simulations for fixed-wing and ISTAR roles.
The Headquarters of the Central Flying School, also located at RAF Cranwell, oversees the qualification of flying instructors across the RAF, ensuring standardized instructional techniques and safety protocols. Its core program trains Qualified Flying Instructors (QFIs) through a rigorous 16- to 20-week course that includes advanced aerobatics, instructional methodology, and leadership development, drawing on over a century of expertise to maintain RAF-wide flying standards. This training equips instructors to deliver consistent, high-quality education, with emphasis on risk mitigation and adaptability to modern threats.
Operational activities at RAF Cranwell integrate advanced simulator technology to enhance safety and efficiency, including a human centrifuge facility opened in 2019 that simulates up to 9G forces for physiological conditioning without airborne risk. Recent trials of mixed reality software further support immersive pre-flight preparation, reducing reliance on live sorties while maintaining skill proficiency. Post-2010 adaptations under the UK Military Flying Training System have shifted to more fuel-efficient platforms like the Prefect and Phenom, enabling multi-role pilot development for versatile operational demands across fast jet, rotary, and transport streams (as of 2025). Officer cadets receive initial flying exposure here as part of broader leadership development.

Recruitment and Selection

RAF Recruitment and Selection, headquartered at RAF Cranwell, serves as the central hub for processing applications, providing career advising, and conducting initial screenings for all roles within the Royal Air Force, including officers, enlisted personnel, and aircrew. This unit operates alongside Armed Forces Careers Offices across the United Kingdom to identify and attract high-quality candidates capable of meeting the RAF's operational demands, emphasizing an agile and inclusive recruitment strategy to build the force of the future.
The Officer and Aircrew Selection Centre (OASC), located at Adastral Hall within RAF Cranwell, is responsible for rigorously assessing potential officers and aircrew through a multi-stage process typically spanning 45 days, including arrival the day prior. Candidates undergo a series of aptitude tests covering verbal reasoning, numerical reasoning, work rate, spatial reasoning, electrical comprehension, and mechanical comprehension, alongside psychometric evaluations to gauge cognitive and personality fit. Team-based exercises, such as group discussions, planning tasks, and leadership simulations, evaluate interpersonal skills, decision-making, and teamwork under pressure, while medical and fitness assessments ensure physical suitability for service. For aircrew applicants, the process incorporates specialized simulator assessments to test aviation aptitude and operational readiness.
Since 2020, RAF Recruitment has implemented diversity initiatives aimed at broadening participation, particularly by increasing representation of women and ethnic minorities through targeted outreach, inclusive policies, and removal of barriers in the selection process to foster a more representative force. These efforts align with the RAF's commitment to an inclusive organization, supporting goals such as achieving 40% female and 20% ethnic minority personnel by 2030, though challenges in meeting these targets have been noted in official inquiries.
Successful candidates from OASC proceed to specialized training pipelines at RAF Cranwell, such as Initial Officer Training, where their selection outcomes directly influence assignment to roles like pilots or weapon systems operators. Pass rates at OASC remain competitive, generally ranging from 30% to 40%, reflecting the high standards required for RAF service.

Air Cadet Support

RAF Cranwell serves as the location for the Headquarters of the Royal Air Force Air Cadets (HQ RAFAC), which administers the Air Training Corps (ATC) and the Combined Cadet Force (RAF) across the United Kingdom.
This headquarters oversees operations for more than 43,000 cadets aged 12 to 20, supported by approximately 10,000 adult volunteers who manage over 1,000 squadrons and sections nationwide (as of 2025). HQ RAFAC handles policy development, including guidelines for gliding scholarships that enable cadets aged 14 and older to achieve solo flight qualifications, and flying scholarships such as the Air Cadet Pilot Scheme, which provides up to 12 hours of powered flight training for those over 18.
At RAF Cranwell, the station facilities support a range of cadet activities, including annual summer and adventure camps where participants engage in drills, STEM workshops, and visits to operational sections. Leadership courses, such as the week-long Cadet Leadership Courses for 16- to 17-year-olds, are conducted on-site, emphasizing practical skills like team management and decision-making through field exercises. The annual national Air Cadet music camp also takes place at the RAF College Cranwell, allowing cadets to develop musical and leadership abilities while earning recognition badges.
The Air Cadet programs at Cranwell integrate the Duke of Edinburgh's Award scheme, with thousands of cadets annually achieving Bronze, Silver, or Gold levels by combining squadron activities like volunteering, physical expeditions, and skill-building with additional challenges. Following the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, HQ RAFAC expanded digital outreach through online portals, virtual training sessions, and tools like ChromeOS Flex to enable remote engagement and maintain squadron connectivity for cadets unable to attend in-person events.
These initiatives at Cranwell foster pathways to RAF careers, with cadet training and qualifications directly contributing to recruitment success, as many participants progress to officer or aircrew roles. Annual events, including the RAFAC Review showcases and leadership assessments, highlight cadet achievements and reinforce community ties.

Infrastructure and Facilities

Airfield Layout

RAF Cranwell's airfield encompasses the main operational zones for flying training. The layout centers on two primary asphalt runways oriented to accommodate prevailing winds in the Lincolnshire region. Runway 08/26, the longest, measures 2,081 meters in length and 47 meters in width, with asphalt surfacing and a pavement classification number (PCN) of 25 F/B/W/T. Adjacent to it, Runway 01/19 provides a secondary hard surface option at 1,462 meters long and 45 meters wide, also asphalt with a PCN of 25 F/B/W/T. Complementing these is a grass strip (Runway 08R/26L), 761 meters long and 25 meters wide, designated for lighter training aircraft and variable conditions based on maintenance programs.
Supporting infrastructure includes a central control tower at the main Cranwell site, which oversees air traffic during MonThu 08001730 LT, Fri 08001700 LT, SatSun 09001700 LT, with additional night flying TueThu for 8 weeks per year. A network of taxiwayssuch as Alpha, Bravo, Charlie, Delta, and Echolinks the runways to aprons and dispersal areas, with widths of 15 meters and asphalt/concrete surfaces rated at PCN 25 F/B/W/T. These dispersal areas enable safe parking and dispersal of training aircraft, minimizing congestion during active flight periods.
The airfield features instrument landing capabilities, including an ILS/DME system on Runway 26 with a 3 glide path and frequency 108.500 MHz, supplemented by TACAN and NDB aids for precision navigation. It handles over 50 aircraft movements daily during intensive training, reflecting its role as a key hub for RAF pilot instruction.
Maintenance upgrades in the 2010s, including runway resurfacing completed around 2018, enhanced compatibility with modern trainers like the Embraer Phenom 100, ensuring sustained operational reliability.

Future Developments

Planned Relocations

The relocation of the Recruit Training Squadron from RAF Halton to RAF Cranwell, originally scheduled for December 2025, has been delayed until at least 2030 as part of adjustments to the Ministry of Defence's estate management priorities. This move aims to consolidate Phase 1 basic recruit training for other ranks alongside officer training at Cranwell, adding dedicated facilities for initial military instruction, including barracks and instructional spaces. Preparations include site enhancements under a broader expansion plan to increase bed spaces from 680 to 1,849, though specific construction for recruit facilities is now aligned with the revised timeline and may involve temporary operational adjustments at Halton during the interim period.
In parallel, the RAF Centre of Aviation Medicine is set to transfer from RAF Henlow to a new purpose-built facility at RAF Cranwell by mid-2026, following the anticipated closure of Henlow under the Defence Estate Optimisation Programme. The new site will feature advanced aerospace medicine research labs, including hypobaric chambers, hypoxia training rigs, and aircraft cockpit simulators, enabling continued research into aviation physiology and medical support for air operations. Construction is targeted for completion in January 2026, with equipment installation finalized by June 2026, minimizing disruptions through phased relocation of existing assets.
These relocations form part of infrastructure investments exceeding 45 million for the aviation medicine facility alone, integrated into Cranwell's ongoing developments that include new training centers and accommodation blocks. The broader enhancements, encompassing the recruit training expansion, draw from a 300 million contract for advanced training systems, though exact allocations for basic facilities remain tied to the delayed schedule. Temporary disruptions may include phased site works and equipment moves, with no major operational halts anticipated for 20252026 beyond the recruit delay.
The primary rationale for these moves is the centralization of RAF training functions under the Defence Estate Optimisation Programme, aimed at streamlining resources, reducing maintenance costs across multiple sites, and enhancing overall training efficiency in response to recent defence reviews. This approach supports post-Brexit adjustments in defence posture by consolidating capabilities at key hubs like Cranwell, optimizing the estate for modern operational needs without expanding the footprint.

Long-Term Strategic Enhancements

RAF Cranwell's long-term strategic enhancements align with the United Kingdom's broader defence policy framework, particularly the 2021 Integrated Review, which emphasizes agility, digital integration, and enhanced training to address evolving global threats. This review underscores the need for responsive forces capable of incorporating advanced technologies to maintain operational superiority. Complementing this, the 2025 Strategic Defence Review further commits to reinvigorating RAF training through reforms that prioritize synthetic environments and interoperability, aiming to boost capacity and flexibility while reducing reliance on costly live exercises. These policies position Cranwell as a pivotal centre for implementing such transformations in officer and pilot development.
As the primary hub for RAF officer commissioning and initial flying training, Cranwell holds strategic importance in developing next-generation pilots and leaders to meet NATO commitments, including joint intelligence, surveillance, reconnaissance, and high-readiness air forces. The station supports UK-led multinational pilot training programs, such as the NATO Flight Training Europe project, which integrates allies in operating jet fighters, helicopters, and transport aircraft to enhance collective defence capabilities. Amid heightened NATO demands on the eastern flank, Cranwell's role ensures the RAF contributes to agile combat employment concepts tested in large-scale exercises.
The RAF faces significant challenges from persistent pilot shortages, with training pipelines experiencing delays of up to a year for new entrants, exacerbated by inadequate fast jet capabilities and the need to outsource training abroad. Opportunities arise through planned expansions at Cranwell, including eight new buildings to add approximately 1,200 bed spaces, tripling staff and student capacity to support increased throughput and address these gaps. Additionally, potential growth in training scopes could encompass space operations, aligning with RAF investments in recapitalizing infrastructure for air and space power domains. These enhancements aim to build resilience against shortages while fostering expertise in emerging domains.
Integration of artificial intelligence (AI) and virtual reality (VR) into training represents a core advancement, with the RAF targeting 80% synthetic training by 2040 by leveraging systems like the single virtual environment. At Cranwell, this includes trials of VR flight devices under the Military Flying Training System and augmented reality overlays for immersive pilot preparation, enhancing skills in uncrewed systems and drone operations. The 2025 review supports this through a Defence Uncrewed Systems Centre by early 2026 and a protected Defence AI Investment Fund, enabling Cranwell to pioneer drone simulation for autonomous collaborative platforms integral to future RAF doctrine.

Command Structure

Station Commanders

The position of Station Commander at RAF Cranwell is held by senior officers, typically of Wing Commander or Group Captain rank, responsible for the overall leadership, operational efficiency, and strategic direction of the station, including its training and support elements. Following a reorganisation, the title was changed to Officer Commanding, RAF College, Cranwell on 9 April 2020. Appointments have averaged 23 years in duration since the station's establishment under Royal Air Force control in 1918. Early command was often combined with the role of Commandant of the Royal Air Force College, with distinct station-focused appointments emerging in the 1930s.
Prior to formalized station commands, Air Commodore Charles Alexander Holcombe Longcroft served as the first RAF Commandant and acting Air Officer Commanding from 2 November 1919 to 2 November 1929, laying the foundational structures for officer training at the newly established RAF College. Successors in this combined role included Air Vice-Marshal Arthur Murray Longmore (16 December 1929 to 30 January 1933), who directed key infrastructural developments, including the construction of College Hall, which was opened in October 1934.
From the mid-1930s, dedicated station commands were recorded separately, often involving oversight of expanding training and wartime preparations. Post-World War II leaders, such as Air Commodore Thomas Gilbert Pike (appointed 23 August 1945), played pivotal roles in modernizing facilities and reorganizing officer and aircrew training schools amid demobilization and Cold War preparations.
