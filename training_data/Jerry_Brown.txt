 Edmund Gerald "Jerry" Brown Jr. (born April 7, 1938) is an American Democratic politician and attorney who served as the 34th and 39th Governor of California from January 6, 1975, to January 3, 1983, and from January 3, 2011, to January 7, 2019, accumulating 16 years in office and becoming the state's longest-serving governor. The son of former California Governor Pat Brown, he entered politics after studying at the University of Santa Clara and Yale Law School, briefly pursuing Jesuit seminary training before focusing on law and public service.
Brown's career spanned diverse roles, including Secretary of State of California (19711975), Mayor of Oakland (19992007), and Attorney General of California (20072011), during which he prosecuted financial crimes and enforced environmental regulations. He mounted unsuccessful bids for the Democratic presidential nomination in 1976, 1980, and 1992, campaigning on themes of campaign finance reform, nuclear disarmament, and fiscal restraint, earning the nickname "Governor Moonbeam" for his unconventional ideas like satellite-based technology investments.
As governor, Brown's first tenure emphasized fiscal conservatism, achieving budget surpluses through spending cuts and rejecting grandiose projects, while his later terms prioritized climate initiatives like cap-and-trade and renewable energy mandates alongside infrastructure expansions such as high-speed rail, which faced criticism for cost overruns exceeding $100 billion and delays. His administrations balanced budgets amid economic recoveries but drew scrutiny for policies contributing to California's housing shortage through stringent regulations and environmental reviews that inflated development costs, exacerbating affordability crises.

Early life and education

Family background and childhood

Edmund Gerald Brown Jr., commonly known as Jerry Brown, was born on April 7, 1938, in San Francisco, California, to Edmund Gerald "Pat" Brown Sr. and Bernice Layne Brown. His father, born April 21, 1905, in San Francisco to a family of Irish Catholic descent, pursued a legal career after studying at San Francisco Law School and passing the bar in 1927; he entered politics as a Democrat, winning election as San Francisco District Attorney in 1943 and serving from 1944 to 1950. Pat Brown then served as California Attorney General from 1951 to 1959 before being elected governor, holding office from 1959 to 1967. His mother, born in 1908, worked as a schoolteacher prior to marriage and was known for her quiet, bookish demeanor.
Brown grew up as the only son among four children, born third after two older sisters, with a younger sister, Kathleen, arriving in 1945. The family resided in San Francisco during his father's rising political prominence, which included Pat Brown's unsuccessful runs for higher office in the 1930s and early 1940s before his DA victory amid World War II-era dynamics. Described as shy and introverted in his youthtraits likened to those of his mother rather than his outgoing fatherBrown experienced a relatively stable urban childhood in a politically active household, though specific personal anecdotes from this period remain limited in public records.

Jesuit training and early career explorations

Brown attended St. Ignatius College Preparatory, a Jesuit high school in San Francisco, graduating in 1955. He then enrolled at Santa Clara University, a Jesuit institution, for his freshman year before entering the Jesuit seminary.
In August 1956, at age 18, Brown entered the Sacred Heart Novitiate in Los Gatos, California, intending to pursue ordination as a Catholic priest. The novitiate provided rigorous spiritual, intellectual, and moral formation, including daily examinations of conscience and studies in philosophy and theology. He resided there for approximately three and a half years, engaging in meditation, silence, and Jesuit spiritual exercises until departing in January 1960. Brown later reflected that the experience emphasized human contact and disciplined reasoning, influences from his Jesuit schooling that shaped his emphasis on first-hand engagement over abstract theory.
Upon leaving the seminary, Brown abandoned plans for the priesthood and enrolled at the University of California, Berkeley, where he earned a Bachelor of Arts in Classics, focusing on Latin and Greek, in 1961. He then attended Yale Law School, receiving a Juris Doctor in 1964. These academic pursuits marked a pivot from religious vocation to legal training, reflecting explorations in secular intellectual disciplines.
Following law school, Brown served as a law clerk to Associate Justice Mathew Tobriner of the California Supreme Court, gaining practical exposure to judicial processes. He subsequently traveled extensively in Mexico and Latin America for study and immersion, broadening his perspectives on international affairs and development. Returning to Los Angeles, he briefly practiced law at a firm, including work with Tuttle & Taylor, before shifting toward public service roles. These post-law activities represented tentative professional steps amid uncertainty about long-term career direction, preceding his formal entry into electoral politics.

Entry into politics

California Secretary of State tenure

Edmund G. "Jerry" Brown Jr. was elected California Secretary of State on November 3, 1970, defeating Republican candidate E. R. "Ted" Wills with 52.5% of the vote in a contest emphasizing government transparency and administrative reform. He assumed office on January 4, 1971, becoming the first Democrat to hold the position since 1938, and served until resigning on January 6, 1975, to become governor. Brown's campaign platform focused on modernizing the office, which had been criticized for inefficiency, and promoting openness in state operations amid growing public distrust following events like the Watergate scandal.
During his tenure, Brown prioritized legal actions to enforce corporate accountability, including arguing before the California Supreme Court in cases against Standard Oil of California (now Chevron), where he secured victories challenging the company's practices under state antitrust laws. He also championed initiatives to streamline business filings and public records access, laying groundwork for technological upgrades in record-keeping that improved efficiency for corporations and the public.
A signature achievement was Brown's sponsorship of Proposition 9, the California Political Reform Act of 1974, which voters approved on June 4, 1974, with 57.5% support. This measure, enacted in response to national scandals, mandated full disclosure of campaign contributions and expenditures, banned "pay-to-play" practices, established conflict-of-interest rules for public officials, and created the Fair Political Practices Commission (FPPC) to oversee enforcement. As Secretary of State, Brown certified the initiative's qualification for the ballot after reformers gathered over 600,000 signatures, positioning California's law as the nation's first comprehensive post-Watergate political reform package and influencing subsequent federal legislation like the Federal Election Campaign Act amendments. The act's emphasis on empirical transparencyrequiring detailed reporting of funds from sources exceeding $100aimed to curb undue influence through verifiable data rather than relying on unenforced ethical norms.
Brown's oversight extended to election administration, where he enforced fair ballot access and challenged irregularities, contributing to a reputation for rigorous, non-partisan management despite his Democratic affiliation. His tenure ended amid his successful 1974 gubernatorial bid, during which he leveraged the office's reformist image to appeal to voters seeking fiscal prudence and anti-corruption measures. Sources on these efforts, including official state archives and public broadcasting accounts, consistently highlight implementation details over partisan narratives, though contemporary media coverage occasionally reflected establishment skepticism toward Brown's outsider approach to entrenched bureaucracies.

1974 gubernatorial election

Incumbent Republican Governor Ronald Reagan announced in early 1974 that he would not seek a third term, opening the gubernatorial race. Democratic Secretary of State Jerry Brown, aged 36, entered the contest, leveraging his statewide visibility from enacting campaign finance reforms as Secretary of State, including support for Proposition 9, the Political Reform Act, which voters approved in June 1974 with limits on contributions and spending disclosure.
In the Democratic primary on June 4, 1974, Brown secured the nomination with 1,085,752 votes (37.75%), defeating San Francisco Mayor Joseph L. Alioto, who received 544,007 votes (18.91%), and Assembly Speaker Bob Moretti, among others, in a fragmented field. Brown's campaign emphasized political reform amid the Watergate scandal, portraying himself as an outsider committed to curbing government waste and improving education, while distancing from his father Pat Brown's legacy of expansive spending.
The general election pitted Brown against Republican State Controller Houston I. Flournoy, who had won his party's nomination. Brown campaigned on fiscal restraint, pledging no new taxes and an "era of limits," symbolized by his use of a 1974 Plymouth Satellite sedan rather than a limousine. Flournoy criticized Brown's inexperience and family ties but could not overcome Democratic voter registration advantages and post-Watergate sentiment favoring reform candidates.
On November 5, 1974, Brown won with 3,131,648 votes (50.11%) to Flournoy's 2,952,954 (47.25%), a margin of 178,694 votes out of approximately 6.25 million cast, or about 1.86 percentage points. The narrow victory, despite Democrats holding a registration edge of roughly 5-6 percentage points, highlighted Flournoy's competitive challenge and Brown's appeal as a fresh, austere alternative.

First governorship (19751983)

Fiscal austerity and budget reforms

Upon assuming office as California's 36th governor on January 6, 1975, Jerry Brown inherited a state budget surplus from his predecessor, Ronald Reagan, amid national economic pressures including the 1973 oil crisis and ensuing inflation. Brown campaigned on fiscal restraint, pledging to avoid tax increases and embrace an "era of limits" in government spending, reflecting a departure from expansive Democratic norms. He immediately implemented austerity by reducing the governor's office budget by 7 percent and rejecting symbolic extravagances, such as refusing to build a new state mansion or accept a gubernatorial limousine, opting instead for personal vehicles and economy measures.
Brown prioritized balanced budgets through spending controls rather than revenue growth, vetoing numerous legislative appropriations to enforce discipline; for instance, he frequently exercised line-item vetoes to trim excess from bills, maintaining fiscal solvency without broad tax hikes during his early terms despite recessions in 1974-1975 and 1980-1982. His administration reorganized state government operations for efficiency, consolidating agencies and emphasizing cost-saving innovations, which helped sustain surplusesreaching approximately $500 million by 1978while funding initiatives like the California Conservation Corps in 1976 as a low-cost alternative to traditional welfare expansion. These reforms contrasted with federal trends under Presidents Carter and Reagan, where Brown advocated "pay-as-you-go" principles, critiquing deficit spending as unsustainable given California's structural constraints.
The passage of Proposition 13 on June 6, 1978, which capped property tax rates at 1 percent of assessed value and required supermajorities for new local taxes, drastically reduced local revenues by an estimated $4-7 billion annually, prompting a fiscal crisis for municipalities and schools. Initially opposing the measure as potentially destabilizing, Brown adapted by leveraging the state's surplus to provide a "bailout" through supplemental appropriations, shifting funding burdens to Sacramentosuch as increasing state aid to education from 40 percent to over 60 percent of local needswhile imposing stricter controls on local spending to prevent deficits. This response preserved short-term stability but centralized fiscal power, altering California's budget dynamics by making state revenues more pivotal to local services and foreshadowing future tensions between austerity and service demands. Brown's approach earned bipartisan praise for prudence but drew criticism from liberals for constraining progressive programs, underscoring his emphasis on empirical fiscal limits over ideological expansion.

Environmental and energy initiatives

During his first term as governor, Jerry Brown prioritized energy conservation and alternative sources in response to the 1970s oil crises, directing the newly operational California Energy Commissionestablished by the 1974 Warren-Alquist Actto forecast demand, enforce efficiency standards, and oversee power plant siting while reducing reliance on fossil fuels. Under his administration, the legislature enacted substantial tax credits of up to 55 percent for investments in wind, solar, geothermal, and biomass technologies, spurring early development of renewable capacity in California.
Brown also targeted the oil sector early, signing legislation on May 15, 1975, that repealed a depletion allowance tax exemption previously benefiting producers, aiming to curb subsidies amid rising gasoline prices that exceeded $1 per gallon statewide by late 1979. These measures aligned with his advocacy for decentralized energy solutions, including solar applications and cogeneration, though implementation faced challenges from technological immaturity and fluctuating federal policies.
On the environmental front, Brown signed the California Coastal Act on September 20, 1976, codifying permanent protections for the state's 1,100-mile coastline by requiring local plans to prioritize public access, ecological preservation, and limits on incompatible development, building on the temporary Coastal Commission created via Proposition 20 in 1972. This law empowered the California Coastal Commission to review projects impacting sensitive habitats, rejecting or conditioning thousands of proposals in its initial years to mitigate erosion, habitat loss, and urban sprawl. Brown's appointees to the commission reinforced its regulatory role, though tensions arose with developers over permit delays averaging 18 months for major projects.

Agricultural labor relations and social policies

Upon taking office in January 1975, Governor Jerry Brown prioritized agricultural labor reforms amid ongoing campaigns by the United Farm Workers (UFW) led by Cesar Chavez for better conditions and union rights in California's fields. On June 5, 1975, Brown signed the California Agricultural Labor Relations Act (ALRA), the first state law in the United States to grant farmworkers the right to organize unions and conduct secret-ballot elections for collective bargaining representation. The Act established the Agricultural Labor Relations Board (ALRB) to administer elections and resolve disputes, aiming to reduce strikes and violence by providing a structured process for union certification.
The ALRA facilitated over 1,800 union elections in its first year, with the UFW winning certification in about half, though implementation faced resistance from growers who challenged access to workers and alleged intimidation, leading to delays and legal battles. Brown's personal rapport with Chavez, including meetings and UFW endorsement of his presidential bids, underscored his commitment, as Chavez had nominated Brown at the 1976 Democratic National Convention. Despite initial activity, union representation stagnated over time due to factors like seasonal workforce mobility, employer countermeasures, and inter-union rivalries, with UFW contracts covering fewer than 10% of farmworkers by the early 1980s.
In broader social policies, Brown's administration emphasized diversity, with appointments to state positions prioritizing women and minorities to reflect demographic realities and promote equity in governance. He maintained a deterrent approach to crime, expanding prison capacity while avoiding expansive welfare programs, aligning with his fiscal restraint ethos that limited social spending growth amid California's economic pressures. These measures sought causal stability in labor markets and social structures without unchecked expansion of entitlements.

Presidential campaigns and national profile

Brown entered the 1976 Democratic presidential race in February 1976, less than a year into his governorship, promoting a "new philosophy committed to human beings" that emphasized fiscal restraint and limits to growth amid post-Watergate disillusionment. He focused on late primaries, securing victories in Maryland on May 18, 1976, where he defeated Jimmy Carter, as well as in New Jersey, North Carolina, and Oregon, before sweeping his home state of California on June 8. These wins garnered media attention, positioning Brown as a youthful alternative to establishment candidates, though Carter clinched the nomination with superior delegate counts. The campaign earned him the derisive nickname "Governor Moonbeam" from Chicago columnist Mike Royko, who mocked Brown's support for unconventional proposals like a space-based communications satellite as attracting the "moonbeam vote."
In 1980, Brown challenged incumbent President Carter amid party dissatisfaction over inflation and the Iran hostage crisis, entering the race in March 1979 but delaying formal announcement until after Carter's early primary dominance. Employing grass-roots tactics and new-age rhetoric, Brown's effort faltered without primary wins, overshadowed by Senator Ted Kennedy's stronger insurgency; he withdrew on April 1, 1980, following a third-place finish in Wisconsin. The brief bid reinforced perceptions of Brown as an eccentric outsider but yielded minimal delegates and little traction against Carter's renomination.
Brown's 1992 campaign targeted Democratic establishment flaws, pledging to forgo political action committee donations, cap personal campaign spending at $100, and advocate term limits and campaign finance overhaul. He notched an upset win in Connecticut on March 24, 1992, capturing 28% of the vote against frontrunner Bill Clinton's 27%, but faltered in subsequent contests, including Super Tuesday losses, leading to his withdrawal in June. At the Democratic National Convention, Brown spoke but withheld endorsement of Clinton, highlighting intraparty divisions.
These repeated bids, though unsuccessful, cemented Brown's national stature as a Democratic maverick, blending Jesuit-influenced austerity, environmental priorities, and skepticism of big government with critiques of party orthodoxy, drawing both admiration for principled stands and ridicule for perceived impracticality. His campaigns amplified California's influence in national politics and portrayed him as a philosophical counterweight to more conventional liberals, sustaining visibility through media fascination with his unconventional persona.

Political setbacks and resurgence

1982 Senate defeat and interim activities

In 1982, incumbent Governor Brown announced his candidacy for the U.S. Senate seat in California vacated by retiring Republican Senator S. I. Hayakawa, opting against seeking a third gubernatorial term. On June 8, 1982, Brown secured the Democratic primary nomination with a plurality over state Senator Al Alquist and author Gore Vidal, among others.
Brown faced San Diego Mayor Pete Wilson, the Republican nominee, in the general election held on November 2, 1982. Wilson, portraying himself as a moderate and receiving an endorsement from President Ronald Reagan, maintained a polling lead throughout the campaign despite Democratic gains in other 1982 Senate races nationwide. Brown received 3,494,968 votes (44.78%), while Wilson garnered 4,022,565 votes (51.54%), with the remainder going to minor candidates including Theresa F. "Tena" Gortner of the Libertarian Party. The loss ended Brown's consecutive terms in statewide office and marked a setback amid perceptions of his erratic political shifts, including prior presidential explorations.
Brown completed his second gubernatorial term on January 3, 1983, succeeded by Republican George Deukmejian. In the ensuing years, he largely withdrew from frontline politics, forming a political action committee and launching two policy-oriented organizations: the National Commission on Industrial Innovation and the Institute for National Strategy. From 1983 to 1988, Brown traveled internationally, including extended stays in Japan during 19861987 to deepen his Zen Buddhist studies and write, as well as time in India assisting Mother Teresa's humanitarian efforts.
Reentering organized Democratic activities, Brown was elected chairman of the California Democratic Party on February 12, 1989, focusing on party rebuilding and fundraising amid internal divisions. He resigned the post on February 4, 1991, initially to explore a U.S. Senate rematch against Wilson but pivoting toward a presidential bid later that year. This period of reflection and low-profile engagement allowed Brown to cultivate a outsider image, drawing on his Jesuit background and interest in philosophy, though it drew criticism for lacking substantive policy output.

1992 presidential campaign

Former California Governor Edmund G. "Jerry" Brown Jr. announced his candidacy for the 1992 Democratic presidential nomination on October 21, 1991, positioning himself as an outsider challenging entrenched political corruption and special interests. Brown's campaign emphasized systemic reform, including a pledge to accept no individual contributions exceeding $100 and to reject all political action committee (PAC) funds, relying instead on small-donor contributions solicited through a toll-free hotline and federal matching funds. This approach aimed to demonstrate independence from lobbyists and demonstrate a model for reducing money's influence in politics, though it limited his fundraising compared to rivals like Bill Clinton.
Central to Brown's platform was a radical tax overhaul proposing a 13% flat income tax combined with a 13% national value-added tax (VAT) to replace the progressive federal income tax system and eliminate the Internal Revenue Service code, which he argued perpetuated inequality and complexity favoring the wealthy and connected. He contended this would promote fairness by broadening the tax base and simplifying compliance, while also advocating for congressional term limits and stricter campaign finance restrictions to curb elite influence. Critics, including some economists and Democratic opponents, dismissed the flat tax as regressive, disproportionately burdening lower-income households despite Brown's claims of compensatory mechanisms like rebates, and questioned its revenue neutrality amid deficit concerns.
Brown's primary performance began weakly, with low showings in early contests like New Hampshire, but gained traction as a protest candidate after Paul Tsongas's withdrawal, appealing to voters disillusioned with Clinton's personal scandals and perceived establishment ties. He secured upset victories in Nevada on March 9, 1992, marking his second primary win, and in Connecticut on March 24, 1992, where he defeated front-runner Clinton by emphasizing anti-corruption themes amid voter skepticism of Clinton's viability. Narrow losses followed, such as in Wisconsin where Clinton edged him 37% to 35%, but Brown's persistence highlighted fractures in Democratic support for Clinton, culminating in continued campaigning through Super Tuesday and beyond despite Clinton's delegate lead.
Brown refused to endorse Clinton promptly, sustaining a contentious rivalry into the Democratic National Convention in July 1992, where he criticized party leadership but ultimately did not derail Clinton's nomination. His campaign garnered roughly 18% of the national Democratic primary vote, winning a handful of small states but failing to amass sufficient delegates, underscoring the challenges of grassroots funding and outsider appeals against a resilient establishment favorite amid economic recession and Bush's incumbency. The effort reinforced Brown's reputation as a maverick reformer but highlighted the primacy of organizational strength and broader voter coalitions in securing nominations.

Oakland mayoralty (19982006)

Election and urban revitalization efforts

In October 1997, former Governor Jerry Brown announced his candidacy for mayor of Oakland, emphasizing local politics as essential for effective governance amid perceptions of state and federal inefficacy. His campaign highlighted the need to revitalize the city's declining downtown, drawing on his prior executive experience to promise pragmatic urban renewal without relying on traditional political networks.
The 1998 Oakland mayoral election occurred on June 2 as a primary following the resignation of incumbent Ron Dellums, who had been appointed to a congressional seat; Brown faced ten opponents and secured an outright victory with approximately 60% of the vote, avoiding a November runoff. Early returns showed him leading with nearly two-thirds of ballots counted at 19%, reflecting strong citywide support driven by high turnout and grassroots organizing that covered half of Oakland's precincts. In November 1998, voters approved Measure X by landslide margins, expanding the mayor's authority over budgeting, hiring, and policy implementation to facilitate direct leadership in urban projects.
Brown assumed office on July 1, 1998, and promptly launched the 10K Plan, targeting 10,000 new residents in downtown Oakland and the Jack London Square area through incentives for housing development and public-private partnerships. The initiative prioritized converting underused industrial buildings into market-rate lofts and apartments, aiming to reverse decades of population loss and economic stagnation by fostering a denser urban core. By 2005, Oakland had approved sufficient unitsrequiring about 6,000 total for the goalwith officials reporting 85% progress toward housing the targeted residents, including completed projects totaling 3,549 units from approvals during Brown's tenure. These efforts, supported by developer incentives and zoning adjustments, spurred investments exceeding $90 million in select projects and laid groundwork for sustained downtown growth, though critics noted limited affordable housing integration relative to market-rate emphasis.

Crime reduction and development projects

Jerry Brown pursued crime reduction through targeted policing strategies and multiple initiatives, including efforts to fill police department vacancies and implement zero-tolerance enforcement. Overall reported crimes in Oakland fell from 37,182 offenses in 1998, prior to his inauguration, to 32,250 in 2006, marking a decline of more than 13 percent according to FBI Uniform Crime Reports data. This reduction exceeded the national decrease of under 9 percent over the same period, driven by drops in categories such as forcible rapes, felony assaults, larceny, and burglaries. Annual felony counts also averaged approximately 28,000 under Brown, down from about 40,000 per year before 1998. Early in his term, homicides dropped 17 percent in 1999 compared to the prior year.
However, violent crime persisted as a challenge, with murders doubling from 72 in 1998 to 145 in 2006, alongside increases in robberies and auto thefts. Homicides rose to 122 in 2006 from 94 in 2005, amid a surge in gang-related violence that drew criticism for undermining Brown's overall record. Brown appointed Police Chief Richard Word to enforce measures like aggressive questioning of suspects, locating over 750 parolees, serving 1,500 arrest warrants, and pursuing federal prosecution for gun violations, aiming for a 20 percent annual crime reduction. Despite sponsoring nearly two dozen crime-related programs, outcomes were mixed, with persistent high rates in East Oakland housing projects contributing to a murder rate more than double that of San Francisco or New York City.
Brown's development efforts centered on the 10K Plan, launched in 1999 to attract 10,000 new residents to downtown Oakland by promoting "elegant density" through market-rate housing and mixed-use projects. The initiative involved selling underutilized city properties to developers and facilitating conversions of blighted sites, such as transforming an abandoned General Motors assembly plant into loft apartments and constructing a 20-story condominium tower at Lake Merritt. The Uptown district emerged as a flagship project, replacing drug-infested vacant lots with high-rise residential and commercial buildings via public-private partnerships, including a $60 million city subsidy to developer Forest City Enterprises.
These projects spurred investment by capitalizing on Oakland's commercial rents, which were 50 to 65 percent lower than in San Francisco or Berkeley, drawing relocations and revitalizing the downtown core. Brown expanded the city's redevelopment footprint to cover about half of Oakland's land area, enabling tax-increment financing for infrastructure upgrades and new construction around areas like Lake Merritt. By 2005, the plan had achieved dramatic progress in residential growth, though the full 10,000-resident target was not met during his tenure; it laid the foundation for sustained urban renewal, with new high-rises and reduced blight transforming previously declining neighborhoods.

Attorney General of California (20072011)

Key legal actions and enforcement priorities

As California's Attorney General from 2007 to 2011, Jerry Brown emphasized enforcement against mortgage fraud during the subprime crisis, predatory lending practices, environmental violations, and consumer scams targeting vulnerable populations. His office pursued high-profile settlements and lawsuits to recover funds and impose penalties, reflecting a priority on protecting homeowners and enforcing state laws amid economic distress.
A cornerstone action was the October 6, 2008, settlement with Countrywide Financial Corporation, yielding $8.68 billion in relief for borrowers affected by predatory lending, including deceptive loan origination and servicing practices that contributed to widespread foreclosures. This agreement, enforced nationwide but initiated by Brown's office, required loan modifications, principal reductions, and payments to the state, marking the largest predatory lending settlement at the time and prioritizing homeowner relief over mere fines.
Brown's office also targeted foreclosure rescue fraud, filing a July 15, 2009, lawsuit against 21 individuals and 14 companies for exploiting distressed homeowners through false promises of loan modifications and equity stripping schemes. The suit sought $7.5 million in civil penalties, full victim restitution, and permanent injunctions to halt operations, underscoring enforcement against opportunistic scams that exacerbated the crisis. In parallel, Brown launched a statewide initiative in October 2008 to combat loan modification fraud, urging banks to disclose anti-foreclosure plans and coordinating with local prosecutors.
On environmental fronts, Brown secured an August 21, 2007, settlement in a global warming lawsuit against six major automakers, compelling compliance with California's emissions standards and paving the way for stricter fuel economy regulations. Additionally, in November 2009, his office obtained a $19.5 million judgment against Shell Oil Company for over 300 violations of underground storage tank and hazardous waste laws at California gas stations, enforcing cleanup and deterrence against industrial non-compliance.
Consumer protection efforts included a September 3, 2008, $1 million settlement with Hy Cite Corporation for discriminatory sales tactics and false advertising in cookware marketing, which disproportionately targeted low-income and minority consumers. Brown also joined a March 20, 2009, lawsuit against seven private labs for overcharging Medi-Cal by hundreds of millions through inflated diagnostic tests, aiming to reclaim public funds diverted via fraudulent billing. These actions highlighted priorities in fraud recovery and regulatory compliance, though critics later questioned the long-term impact on systemic housing issues.

Stances on death penalty, foreclosure crisis, and same-sex marriage

As California's Attorney General from 2007 to 2011, Jerry Brown upheld the state's death penalty statutes in court despite his personal opposition to capital punishment, fulfilling his duty to defend existing laws and convictions. He oversaw the defense of hundreds of death row cases, including appeals, even as he had previously vetoed death penalty expansion legislation as governor in the 1970s, a veto overridden by the legislature. Brown's enforcement role contrasted with his moral reservations, which he expressed publicly during his campaigns and tenure, though he did not seek to undermine executions through discretionary actions.
During the 2008 financial crisis, Brown prioritized combating foreclosure-related fraud and abuses by initiating lawsuits against predatory operators and negotiating major settlements with lenders. In October 2008, he announced an $8.68 billion multi-state agreement with Countrywide Financial Corporation to address deceptive lending practices that contributed to widespread defaults, providing consumer relief including loan modifications and payments to affected borrowers. Additionally, in response to scams targeting distressed homeowners, his office sued entities like U.S. Loan Auditors and affiliates in October 2010, seeking $60 million in penalties for false promises of foreclosure prevention through unauthorized legal services. These efforts aimed to protect vulnerable Californians amid over 400,000 annual foreclosures at the crisis peak, though critics later noted that settlement funds were sometimes redirected under subsequent administrations.
On same-sex marriage, Brown took a position favoring legalization by urging the California Supreme Court to invalidate Proposition 8, the 2008 voter-approved amendment defining marriage as between a man and a woman, arguing it infringed on constitutional liberties. In legal filings following the proposition's passage, which overturned a prior state court ruling permitting same-sex unions, he contended that Prop 8 exceeded its scope by retroactively invalidating thousands of existing marriages without due process, though the court ultimately upheld its core effect while preserving pre-Prop 8 unions. Brown's stance aligned with his broader support for marriage equality, distinguishing him from proponents of the ban and influencing subsequent federal challenges where California's defense was minimal.

Second governorship (20112019)

Third term: Economic recovery and budget balancing

Upon entering office on January 3, 2011, Brown inherited a $25.4 billion budget shortfall for the 2011-12 fiscal year amid the aftermath of the Great Recession, compounded by structural imbalances from prior spending commitments exceeding revenues. His initial budget proposal outlined $12.5 billion in spending reductions across state agencies, including cuts to education, health services, and welfare programs; $12 billion from temporary revenue extensions and modifications; and $1.9 billion in additional solutions like fund shifts, targeting total General Fund spending of $84.6 billion within a $127.4 billion overall budget. These measures aimed to avoid deeper borrowing while addressing an estimated $26 billion total deficit, though they required legislative approval without Republican support for tax extensions absent voter ratification.
Faced with a June 2011 special election where voters rejected a ballot measure for tax extensions (Proposition 6, which sought to extend certain vehicle fees and sales taxes but was conflated with broader fiscal plans), Brown pivoted to deeper cuts, securing a $15 billion reduction package without new revenues. The legislature approved the FY2012 budget on June 28, 2011, which Brown signed, implementing an 8-10% across-the-board reduction in state operations, deferrals in school funding, and curbs on services for low-income families, the elderly, and disabled individuals to close the gap. This approach balanced the budget on paper but relied heavily on one-time maneuvers and assumed economic rebound, with critics noting it shifted burdens to local governments and vulnerable populations without resolving underlying pension and entitlement growth.
As California's economy recovered from recession lowsdriven by tech sector expansion, housing stabilization, and national upturnstate revenues began surpassing projections by mid-decade, enabling deficit paydowns and modest reserve building. In November 2012, voters approved Proposition 30, Brown's initiative extending higher income tax rates on high earners (up to 13.3% for incomes over $1 million) and raising sales taxes by 0.25% through 2018, generating an estimated $6-8 billion annually for schools and public safety while aiding budget stability. By fiscal year 2014-15, these inflows, combined with spending restraint, produced a balanced $113 billion budget proposal that Brown described as "precariously" so, incorporating boosts to education funding and initial rainy-day reserves amid warnings of cyclical volatility. Overall, accumulated deficits nearing $30 billion were retired through revenue surges rather than permanent reforms, with empirical outcomes crediting Brown's fiscal discipline for short-term equilibrium but highlighting reliance on temporary taxes and boom-time growth over structural fixes like entitlement curbs.

Fourth term: Climate policies and infrastructure pushes

During his fourth term as governor, which began on January 5, 2015, Jerry Brown advanced several climate-related initiatives building on prior legislation. In October 2015, he signed Senate Bill 350, the Clean Energy and Pollution Reduction Act, which mandated that California utilities generate 50 percent of their electricity from renewable sources by 2030 and double the state's energy efficiency savings in buildings. This measure aimed to integrate transportation, electricity, and natural gas sectors into broader decarbonization efforts, though implementation faced challenges from industry groups citing potential reliability issues in the power grid. In 2016, Brown signed Senate Bill 32, which codified and extended the state's greenhouse gas reduction goals under the Global Warming Solutions Act of 2006 (AB 32), requiring a 40 percent cut in emissions below 1990 levels by 2030.
Brown also extended the cap-and-trade program, a market-based mechanism first established in 2012, through legislation passed in 2017, allowing it to continue beyond its initial 2020 expiration and generating revenue estimated at billions for low-carbon investments, though critics argued it increased energy costs for consumers without proportionally reducing emissions due to leakage to uncapped sectors. In September 2018, he issued Executive Order B-55-18, directing state agencies to achieve carbon neutralitybalancing emissions with removalsas soon as possible, and to reach net-zero emissions by 2045 at the latest, positioning California as a leader in subnational climate action amid federal policy shifts. These policies emphasized regulatory mandates over voluntary measures, with Brown's administration allocating cap-and-trade auction proceedstotaling over $5 billion by 2018to programs like transit and affordable housing, though audits later revealed uneven effectiveness in emission reductions.
On infrastructure, Brown prioritized large-scale projects tied to climate resilience and economic development. He continued advocating for the California High-Speed Rail project, approved by voters in 2008, committing over $2.5 billion in state cap-and-trade funds during his term to advance construction of the initial Central Valley segment, despite escalating costs projected to exceed $64 billion total and delays pushing full operations beyond 2030. The project faced bipartisan criticism for mismanagement and lack of private investment, with federal funding cuts under the Trump administration exacerbating shortfalls. For water infrastructure, Brown defended the twin Delta Conveyance Projecttwo 40-foot-diameter tunnels under the Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta to transport water southwardproposing it as a solution to drought vulnerability and seismic risks, though it encountered legal challenges from environmental groups and northern California stakeholders over ecological impacts on fisheries. By the end of his term in January 2019, neither project was complete, leaving their viability to successors amid ongoing debates over cost-benefit analyses showing high taxpayer burdens relative to projected benefits.

Proposition 30 and tax increases

In 2012, facing a projected state budget shortfall of approximately $15.7 billion, Governor Jerry Brown proposed Proposition 30 as a ballot initiative to temporarily raise taxes and avert deep cuts to education and public safety funding. The measure, formally titled "Temporary Taxes to Fund Education. Guaranteed Local Public Safety Funding," sought to increase the state sales tax by 0.25 percentage pointsfrom 7.25% to 7.5%for four years from January 1, 2013, to December 31, 2016, and impose temporary additional income tax rates on high earners for seven years from 2012 to 2018. Specifically, the income tax surcharges ranged from 10.3% on taxable income over $500,000 for joint filers (or $250,000 for singles) up to 13.3% on income exceeding $1 million, targeting the top 1-2% of earners and projected to generate about $6-8 billion annually in combined revenue.) 
Brown framed the proposition as essential to maintaining fiscal stability without broader austerity, arguing that rejection would trigger automatic $6 billion in reductions to K-12 schools and community colleges, alongside potential triggers for public safety cuts. The campaign, backed by Brown personally through rallies and ads emphasizing education's role in economic recovery, secured endorsements from teachers' unions, law enforcement, and business groups wary of deeper recessionary impacts. Opponents, including some fiscal conservatives and taxpayer advocates, contended that the measure perpetuated reliance on volatile high-income taxes, which fluctuate with economic cycles and capital gains, rather than addressing structural spending issues.
On November 6, 2012, Proposition 30 passed with 53.9% of the vote, providing the revenue stream integral to Brown's enacted budget and helping shift California from deficits to surpluses by 2013-2014.) Funds from the income tax portion were directed to the Education Protection Account, boosting K-12 and community college spending by an average of $1,000-1,500 per student annually during its duration and contributing to per-pupil expenditures rising from about $8,800 in 2012 to over $10,000 by 2016. The sales tax hike, while regressive in application, was offset by its temporary nature and dedication to averting broader program eliminations.
Post-passage analyses highlighted mixed outcomes: the influx stabilized short-term finances and supported Brown's narrative of pragmatic fiscal conservatism, yet critics noted that expiring revenues by 2018 necessitated ongoing budget maneuvers, with California's income tax basealready the most progressive in the U.S.exacerbating revenue swings, as evidenced by subsequent shortfalls during economic dips. Brown defended the approach as calibrated to California's demographics, where high earners disproportionately fund state services, but acknowledged in later reflections that permanent reforms, like pension adjustments, were needed alongside such measures to sustain balances. Overall, Proposition 30 exemplified Brown's strategy of using voter-approved, time-limited hikes to bridge gaps amid legislative gridlock, yielding $30-40 billion in total revenue over its lifespan but underscoring ongoing debates over tax policy's role in long-term solvency.

Criminal justice reforms and housing challenges

During his third and fourth terms as governor, Jerry Brown advanced criminal justice reforms aimed at reducing California's prison overcrowding and incarceration rates, which had been ruled unconstitutional by the U.S. Supreme Court in Brown v. Plata (2011). In April 2011, Brown signed Assembly Bill 109 (AB 109), known as Public Safety Realignment, which shifted responsibility for supervising and incarcerating certain non-violent, non-serious offenders from state prisons to county jails and probation systems, saving the state an estimated $1 billion annually in prison costs by fiscal year 20152016. This reform reduced the state prison population by over 40,000 inmates between 2011 and 2018.
In November 2014, California voters approved Proposition 47, the Safe Neighborhoods and Schools Act, which Brown endorsed; it reclassified certain drug possession and theft offenses (e.g., theft under $950) from felonies to misdemeanors, leading to the resentencing or early release of approximately 40,000 individuals and further decreasing the incarceration rate by about 30% statewide since its implementation. Proponents argued these measures prioritized rehabilitation over punishment for low-level crimes, with funds redirected to schools and victim services. However, empirical analyses indicated mixed public safety outcomes: while overall crime rates remained historically low, property crimes such as larceny-theft rose by 9.3% and motor vehicle thefts increased post-2014 compared to pre-reform trends, with some studies attributing these shifts to reduced deterrence from felony downgrades. Independent research, including comparisons to national trends, found Prop 47 correlated with a 79% uptick in certain theft categories in the years immediately following its passage, though violent crime rates showed no significant statewide increase.
Concurrently, Brown confronted California's escalating housing crisis, characterized by median home prices exceeding $500,000 by 2015the highest in the U.S.and a shortage of over 1 million units driven by restrictive local zoning, environmental regulations, and slow permitting processes. In September 2017, he signed Senate Bill 35 (SB 35), which streamlined approvals for multifamily housing projects in cities failing to meet state production targets (at least 5,000 units regionally over five years), bypassing local reviews for qualifying developments that included a minimum percentage of affordable units. This was part of a 15-bill package intended to accelerate construction by reducing entitlement timelines from years to months in high-need areas. By 2023, SB 35 had facilitated 156 approved or pending projects totaling thousands of units, though implementation faced legal challenges and uneven local compliance.
Despite these efforts, housing production lagged behind demand, with only about 80,000 new units permitted annually during Brown's tenure against a need for 180,000, exacerbating affordability issues and contributing to a rise in homelessness from 113,000 in 2013 to over 150,000 by 2018. Brown's earlier proposals, such as 2016 streamlining reforms, stalled in the legislature due to opposition from local governments, underscoring causal barriers like NIMBYism and regulatory entrenchment that his administration's procedural fixes did not fully overcome. Critics, including policy analysts, noted that while Brown's initiatives marked incremental progress, they insufficiently addressed underlying supply constraints, leaving median rents up 24% statewide from 2012 to 2017.

Post-governorship activities

Policy advocacy and international engagements

Following his departure from the California governorship in January 2019, Jerry Brown assumed the role of chair for the California-China Climate Institute (CCCI), a University of California, Berkeley-affiliated think tank launched on September 23, 2019, in partnership with Chinese officials to promote bilateral cooperation on climate mitigation, adaptation, and clean energy technologies. The institute conducts joint policy research, trains scientists and policymakers from both regions, and facilitates dialogues aimed at aligning emissions reduction strategies, with Brown emphasizing the need for subnational actors to drive global progress amid federal uncertainties in the United States.
Brown's advocacy through the CCCI has centered on countering U.S.-China economic decoupling, which he warned in April 2023 could precipitate severe financial disruptions comparable to the 2008 crisis, including banking failures and market meltdowns, while underscoring shared interests in averting climate catastrophe via technological and trade collaboration. He has participated in ongoing "Climate Dialogues" hosted by the institute, including events as recent as March 2025, to foster exchanges on carbon markets, renewable energy deployment, and resilience measures. Additionally, as a council member of the China Council for International Cooperation on Environment and Development (CCICED), Brown contributes to advisory efforts on sustainable development, leveraging his position to advocate for integrated global responses to environmental challenges.
In parallel, Brown serves as executive chair of the Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists, a role he took post-governorship, where he has influenced assessments of existential risks, including climate change intertwined with geopolitical tensions, as reflected in the organization's Doomsday Clock updates signaling proximity to global catastrophe. His international commentary has extended to praising federal climate initiatives, such as the 2022 Inflation Reduction Act, while critiquing barriers to U.S.-China partnerships that he argues are essential for equitable global transitions in developing economies. These efforts position Brown as a proponent of pragmatic, cross-border policy frameworks prioritizing empirical risk reduction over ideological confrontations.

Recent commentary on national issues (2019present)

Following his departure from the California governorship in January 2019, Jerry Brown offered commentary on the 2020 U.S. presidential election through interviews and a dedicated NPR podcast series, "The Political Mind of Jerry Brown," where he analyzed the race dynamics, drawing parallels to his own past campaigns and emphasizing pragmatic governance over ideological purity. In an August 2020 interview, Brown discussed President Donald Trump's handling of the coronavirus pandemic and climate policy, critiquing federal responses while advocating for state-level resilience amid national uncertainties, though he stopped short of a formal endorsement of Joe Biden.
In April 2023, Brown cautioned that Biden's prospective 2024 re-election bid was "not a slam dunk any way you look at it," identifying Biden as the strongest Democratic contender but highlighting vulnerabilities from challenger campaigns by figures like Robert F. Kennedy Jr. and Marianne Williamson, as well as broader voter concerns over ageBiden would be 82 at the start of a second term. Brown reflected on his own experience at 84, stating, "I can handle the job but I dont think the politics can handle my age," and expressed skepticism about Vice President Kamala Harris's readiness, noting, "I dont think vice presidents are ever ready."
After Trump's victory in the November 2024 election, Brown attributed the outcome to Democratic shortcomings, including Biden's decision to prolong his candidacy despite evident declines, which he deemed "obvious" in retrospect. In a November 22, 2024, interview, Brown explained Trump's narrow 1.5-point national win as stemming from voter dissatisfaction with inflation, open borders, and rapid social changes under the Biden-Harris administration, urging Democrats to recalibrate rather than dismiss the results. He emphasized national divisions as persistent, stating that such electoral shifts reflect deeper economic and cultural tensions rather than transient anomalies.
Regarding Trump's second term, Brown anticipated heightened federal-state conflicts over climate policy, predicting Trump would dismantle national environmental regulations, potentially exacerbating emissions and extreme weather events, yet viewed this as a potential "silver lining" through reductio ad absurdum: "Hes going to prove the environment is really important... take something far enough, you demonstrate its absurd and people want the opposite." Brown advocated for increased dialogue among global leaders, including between the U.S. and China, to address shared challenges, while stressing that preserving the American Dream amid economic anxieties would require "real talent and luck," alongside persistent efforts against recurring issues like poverty and crime. He defended state-level immigration measures, such as California's sanctuary policies, as compliant with federal cooperation on violent crimes but called for a controlled national process given global migration pressures.

Political ideology and legacy

Fiscal conservatism amid progressive shifts

During his first tenure as governor from 1975 to 1983, Jerry Brown earned a reputation for fiscal restraint, emphasizing spending cuts and rejecting major tax increases amid economic challenges like stagflation. He reduced state workforce size, vetoed numerous spending bills, and prioritized efficiency, such as refusing to build a new governor's mansion and opting for a modest state-issued Plymouth Satellite as his official vehicle. This approach aligned with his "era of limits" philosophy, rooted in Jesuit-influenced asceticism rather than ideological conservatism, allowing room for progressive initiatives like environmental protections without unchecked deficits.
In his second stint from 2011 to 2019, Brown confronted a $26 billion budget shortfall inherited from prior administrations and the Great Recession, implementing $12.5 billion in cuts to health, welfare, and higher education while securing voter approval for Proposition 30a temporary 2012 measure raising income taxes on high earners and sales taxes to avert deeper reductions. By fiscal year 2015-16, the state achieved a $113 billion balanced budget with $1.2 billion added to reserves, transitioning to multi-billion-dollar surpluses that funded progressive priorities including expanded Medi-Cal coverage and climate programs via cap-and-trade revenue auctions. This fiscal discipline persisted through vetoes of tax breaks and insistence on annual budget scrutiny, preventing the boom-bust cycles seen in earlier decades.
A cornerstone of Brown's later fiscal strategy was establishing and maximizing the Rainy Day Fund under Proposition 2 (2014), which mandated surplus allocations to reserves; by 2018, it reached $13.8 billionthe largest in state historyproviding a buffer against revenue volatility from California's progressive tax structure, which relies heavily on capital gains from high-income earners. Critics, including fiscal watchdogs, argued that underlying liabilities like pension debts exceeding $282 billion in 2017 undermined long-term solvency, yet empirical outcomes showed sustained budget balance without federal bailouts, enabling investments in infrastructure and social programs that aligned with Brown's blend of prudence and progressivism. This framework contrasted with more expansive spending in other blue states, reflecting Brown's meta-preference for causal realism in governance over ideological excess.

Major achievements and empirical outcomes

During his second governorship (20112019), Jerry Brown oversaw California's transition from a projected $26.6 billion budget deficit in 2011 to multi-billion-dollar surpluses by 20142018, attributed to a combination of voter-approved tax increases via Proposition 30, spending restraint, and robust economic expansion driven by the technology sector. Proposition 30, enacted in November 2012, temporarily raised the state sales tax by 0.25% through 2016 and increased marginal income tax rates by up to 3% on incomes over $250,000 (adjusted for inflation) through 2018, generating approximately $68 billion annually in additional revenue primarily allocated to K-12 education and public safety, averting deep program cuts and enabling full funding of the Local Control Funding Formula for schools ahead of schedule. However, surpluses peaked at around $512 billion before reserves were built to $15 billion by 2018, with critics noting structural vulnerabilities exposed by reliance on volatile high-income tax receipts amid a national recovery where California's GDP grew at an average annual rate of about 3.5% from 20112019, outpacing the U.S. average but with unemployment declining from 12.1% to 3.9%still higher than the national drop from 8.5% to 3.7%.
Brown advanced climate initiatives, including the extension and strengthening of the cap-and-trade program under Assembly Bill 398 in 2017, which reduced free allowances by over 40% by 2030 and directed auction revenues toward disadvantaged communities and emissions-reduction projects. Empirical data show statewide greenhouse gas emissions fell 5.3% from 2013 to 2017 and dropped below 1990 levels for the first time in 2017, supporting Senate Bill 32's target of 40% reductions below 1990 by 2030, though attribution to cap-and-trade alone is complicated by concurrent factors like renewable energy mandates and vehicle efficiency standards.
Criminal justice reforms under Brown's tenure, including Proposition 47 (2014) and Public Safety Realignment (2011), downgraded certain non-violent offenses from felonies to misdemeanors and shifted low-level offenders to county jails, reducing the state prison population from a peak of over 160,000 in 2009 to under 96,000 by 2021a decline of more than 45,000 inmates. These measures lowered recidivism for specific resentencing groups, such as felony murder reform releases (3% within one year, 7% within two years), but broader outcomes included a 9.3% rise in larceny thefts in Los Angeles post-Prop 47 and statewide property crime increases of 39% in synthetic control analyses, with no statistically significant violent crime uptick yet debates over causal links to retail theft surges and fentanyl-related deaths.

Criticisms and policy failures

Brown's advocacy for the California High-Speed Rail Authority project, initiated during his third term, faced substantial criticism for escalating costs and minimal progress. Originally estimated at $33 billion in 2008, the project's price tag had risen to approximately $100 billion by 2018, with only preliminary construction in the Central Valley completed despite over $5 billion spent. Critics, including fiscal watchdogs, argued that Brown's insistence on proceeding amid delays and funding shortfalls exemplified poor resource allocation, diverting funds from immediate infrastructure needs like road repairs.
In criminal justice reform, Proposition 47, signed into law by Brown on November 5, 2014, reclassified certain nonviolent offenses such as shoplifting under $950 and drug possession from felonies to misdemeanors, reducing prison populations but correlating with rises in property crimes. Statewide larceny theft rates increased by about 9% from 2014 to 2019, and retail theft incidents surged in major cities like San Francisco and Los Angeles, with prosecutors citing the measure's thresholds as enabling organized "smash-and-grab" operations. While some analyses attributed overall crime declines to broader trends, empirical data from the California Department of Justice showed felony theft arrests dropping 25% post-passage, prompting partial reversals like Proposition 36 in 2024. Brown defended the initiative as reducing recidivism costs, but detractors contended it prioritized leniency over deterrence, contributing to public safety erosion.
Housing affordability deteriorated markedly under Brown's tenure, with median home prices exceeding $500,000 by 2018 and rents in coastal metros averaging over $3,000 monthly, positioning California as the U.S. epicenter of poverty when adjusted for cost of living. Homelessness counts rose from 113,000 in 2010 to 151,000 by 2018, despite Brown's allocation of $7.2 billion in the 2018-19 budget for supportive housing and streamlined approvals. Critics highlighted regulatory barriers like CEQA lawsuits and local zoning resistance, which Brown failed to sufficiently override, exacerbating shortages amid population growth and high taxes.
Fiscal policies drew rebukes for masking structural deficits through temporary revenue booms while unfunded pension liabilities ballooned to $285 billion by 2023 for state and local systems. Although Brown enacted pension reforms in 2013 limiting benefits for new hires, overall obligations grew due to optimistic return assumptions and deferred payments, straining future budgets. His budgets achieved surpluses via Prop 30 tax hikes but omitted comprehensive liability paydowns, leaving successors with volatile finances amid economic cycles.
Energy mandates under Brown, including the 2018 law for 100% clean electricity by 2045 and 50% renewables by 2030, were linked to grid instability and blackouts. The closure of the San Onofre Nuclear Generating Station in 2013, influenced by state policies, reduced baseload capacity, while reliance on intermittent solar and wind contributed to rolling outages during the 2020 heatwave affecting 800,000 customers. Electricity prices in California reached $0.28 per kWh by 2018, double the national average, as renewable integrations strained transmission without adequate storage. Opponents argued these aggressive targets prioritized emissions reductions over reliability, ignoring empirical risks of supply variability.

Electoral history

Gubernatorial elections

Edmund G. "Jerry" Brown Jr. first sought the California governorship in 1974, following the decision of incumbent Ronald Reagan not to seek a third term. As the Democratic nominee after securing the primary on June 4, 1974, Brown faced Republican State Superintendent of Public Instruction Houston I. Flournoy in the general election held on November 5, 1974. Brown defeated Flournoy by approximately 175,000 votes in a contest with roughly 6 million votes cast, marking a narrow victory amid a national Democratic wave post-Watergate.
In his 1978 re-election bid, Brown, the incumbent governor, competed against Republican Attorney General Evelle J. Younger. The November 7, 1978, general election resulted in a decisive win for Brown, who received 3,878,812 votes (56.04%), compared to Younger's 2,526,534 (36.50%), with the remainder going to minor candidates including Libertarian Ed Clark. This outcome reflected Brown's strengthened position despite criticisms of his fiscal austerity measures and unconventional governance style.
Brown did not seek a third consecutive term in 1982, instead pursuing national ambitions including a presidential run in 1980. He returned to statewide office as attorney general from 2007 to 2011 before announcing his candidacy for governor again in 2010, capitalizing on California's term limit provisions that allowed non-consecutive terms.
The 2010 gubernatorial election pitted Brown against Republican Meg Whitman, former CEO of eBay, who invested over $140 million of her personal fortune in the campaign. On November 2, 2010, Brown prevailed with 5,428,458 votes (53.77%), while Whitman garnered 4,127,371 (40.88%), despite her spending dwarfing Brown's $36.5 million total campaign expenditure. Whitman's campaign was hampered by revelations of her prior employment of an undocumented nanny.
Seeking re-election in 2014, Brown faced Republican Neel Kashkari, a former U.S. Treasury official involved in the TARP program. In the November 4, 2014, election, Brown secured 4,388,368 votes (59.97%), decisively outperforming Kashkari's 2,929,213 (40.03%), buoyed by a favorable economic recovery and Democratic voter turnout advantages in the state. This victory marked Brown's fourth term as governor, a record unmatched in California history.

Other races and presidential bids

Brown was elected California Secretary of State in the November 3, 1970, general election, defeating Republican incumbent James L. Flournoy in a close contest and serving from 1971 to 1975. During his tenure, he implemented reforms to modernize election procedures and advocated for environmental protections in state records management.
After leaving the governorship in 1983, Brown ran for mayor of Oakland in 1998, securing victory in the June 2 primary by obtaining over 50% of the vote against ten opponents, thus avoiding a runoff and taking office later that year. He was reelected in 2002 with minimal opposition, serving until 2006, during which he focused on urban revitalization, crime reduction, and downtown development projects like the Fox Theater restoration.
In 2006, Brown campaigned for California Attorney General, winning the Democratic primary on June 6 against Los Angeles City Attorney Rocky Delgadillo and then defeating Republican state Senator Chuck Poochigian in the November general election, assuming office in January 2007. His campaign emphasized public safety, mortgage fraud enforcement, and opposition to the death penalty, aligning with his prior gubernatorial record.
Brown mounted three unsuccessful bids for the Democratic presidential nomination. In 1976, less than a year into his first gubernatorial term, he entered the primaries late, promoting a platform of fiscal austerity, nuclear power skepticism, and space program investment under a "new philosophy" of limited government; he prevailed in the Maryland, Rhode Island, New Jersey, and California primaries but could not close the delegate gap with Jimmy Carter, who clinched the nomination at the July convention where Cesar Chavez formally nominated Brown. In 1980, he challenged incumbent President Carter amid party dissatisfaction with inflation and Iran hostage crisis handling, but garnered minimal support, suspending his campaign after a weak April 1 showing in the Wisconsin primary. His 1992 effort targeted front-runner Bill Clinton with anti-establishment themes, including a 13% flat tax on incomes over $10,000, strict campaign finance limits, and opposition to political action committee influence; victories in Connecticut and Vermont were offset by Super Tuesday defeats, leading to his withdrawal from active contention though he persisted to the convention.

Personal life

Relationships and family

Edmund Gerald "Jerry" Brown Jr. was born on April 7, 1938, in San Francisco as the only son of Edmund G. "Pat" Brown Sr., who served as California's governor from 1959 to 1967, and Bernice Layne Brown; he has three sistersBarbara, Cynthia (who died in 2015), and Kathleen, the latter of whom pursued her own political career including as California's state treasurer from 1991 to 1995.
Brown remained unmarried for much of his life, earning a reputation as a bachelor during his early political career. He had a prominent romantic relationship with singer Linda Ronstadt from approximately 1971 to 1983, during which they made public appearances together and shared interests in music and politics; Ronstadt later described it in her memoir as a period of mutual enjoyment with Brown, whom she found "smart and funny" but ultimately incompatible for marriage due to lifestyle differences.
On June 18, 2005, Brown married Anne Gust, a former Gap Inc. executive and attorney whom he had met in 1990; it was the first marriage for both, and Gust played a significant role in his subsequent campaigns, including for state attorney general in 2006. The couple has no children.

Religious influences and lifestyle

!
Brown was raised in a devout Catholic family and educated in Jesuit institutions, attending St. Ignatius College Preparatory in San Francisco and briefly Santa Clara University. In 1956, he entered the Sacred Heart Novitiate, a Jesuit seminary in Saratoga, California, to pursue priesthood, studying philosophy, Latin, and theology for four years before departing in 1960 to attend Yale Law School. This Jesuit formation instilled in him a preference for rigorous, human-centered education and intellectual discipline, influences he later credited for shaping his approach to governance.
Following his first stint as California governor (19751983), Brown explored Zen Buddhism, traveling to Japan for six months in the 1980s to study under roshi Yamada Koun and Christian-Zen practitioner Hugo Enomiya-Lassalle. He also engaged in meditation practices and worked with Mother Teresa in Calcutta, blending Catholic roots with Eastern contemplative traditions. Brown has described Zen as transcending doctrine, emphasizing attention and meditation over rigid beliefs, while maintaining Catholic ties, such as exchanging Latin texts with priests and meeting Pope Francis, the first Jesuit pontiff.
Brown's lifestyle reflected monastic austerity, particularly during his initial governorship, when he rejected the Sacramento Governor's Mansion, opting instead for a modest $250-per-month apartment where he slept on a mattress on the floor. He drove inexpensive vehicles, including a 1974 Plymouth Satellite, eschewed limousines, and minimized material possessions, embodying anti-materialist principles drawn from his spiritual background. In later years, after marrying Anne Gust in 2005, he adopted more conventional living arrangements, such as a Sacramento loft and eventually the Governor's Mansion, though he retained elements of personal simplicity and daily prayer or meditation.
