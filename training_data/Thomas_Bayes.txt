Thomas Bayes
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Thomas Bayes (c. 17011702 7 April 1761) was an English mathematician and Presbyterian minister best known for his posthumously published essay that introduced Bayes' theorem, a foundational principle in probability theory and statistics that enables the updating of probabilities based on new evidence.
Born likely in Sheffield, Yorkshire, to a prominent Nonconformist family, Bayes was the son of Joshua Bayes, a Presbyterian minister and one of the first six Nonconformists ordained in England after the Act of Uniformity was repealed, and Anne Carpenter. His family had roots in Sheffield, where they amassed wealth from the cutlery trade before relocating to London. Little is known about his early education, though he likely received private tutoring; records indicate he studied logic and divinity at the University of Edinburgh around 17191723.
Bayes pursued a career in the ministry, assisting his father at the Presbyterian chapel in Holborn from 1723 to 1734 before becoming the minister at Mount Sion Chapel in Tunbridge Wells around 1733, a position he held until retiring in 1752 due to health issues and financial independence from family inheritance. He was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1742, recognizing his mathematical interests, though he published little during his lifetime beyond theological and mathematical defenses. His known works include possibly Divine Benevolence, or an Essay on Charity (1731, anonymous), a theological treatise arguing that happiness is the principal end of divine providence, and An Introduction to the Doctrine of Fluxions, and a Defence of the Analyst (1736), a mathematical response supporting Isaac Newton's calculus against critics like Bishop Berkeley.
Bayes' most significant contribution emerged after his death: the manuscript An Essay towards solving a Problem in the Doctrine of Chances, edited by his friend Richard Price and published in 1763 in the Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society. This work addressed inverse probability, proposing a method to infer causes from observed effects using what is now called Bayes' theorem, which mathematically expresses how prior probabilities are revised by likelihoods to yield posterior probabilities. Motivated partly by philosophical debates on induction, such as those in David Hume's writings, Bayes' ideas laid the groundwork for Bayesian inference, though they remained obscure until revived in the 20th century. He died in Tunbridge Wells and was buried in Bunhill Fields, London, with his theorem gaining widespread recognition only posthumously, influencing fields from statistics to machine learning.

Biography

Early Life and Education

Thomas Bayes was born around 1701, likely between July 1701 and April 1702, possibly in Sheffield, London, or Bovingdon in Hertfordshire, England. He was the eldest son of Joshua Bayes (16711746), a prominent Nonconformist Presbyterian minister who served as president of the Independent Fund, a key organization supporting dissenting ministers, and his wife Anne (ne Carpenter), from a London family. The Bayes family originated from Sheffield in northern England, where they had amassed wealth in the cutlery trade before relocating to London, and Thomas was the first of seven childrenfour boys and three girlsraised in this dissenting tradition that valued intellectual rigor amid religious nonconformity.
Bayes likely received his initial formal education in London, possibly under the tutelage of John Ward (1679?1758), a mathematician, antiquary, and dissenting academy instructor who emphasized Latin and Greek classics, preparing students for university. This early training aligned with the family's Presbyterian emphasis on reason, evidence, and moral philosophy as foundations for faith, influences that shaped Bayes' later theological and logical pursuits.
In 1719, Bayes matriculated at the University of Edinburgh, a leading center for Presbyterian education outside English jurisdiction, where he studied for approximately three years until 1722. His curriculum focused on divinity, logic under Colin Drummond (professor of logic and metaphysics), and mathematics with James Gregory (professor of mathematics), alongside metaphysics and theology, fostering a blend of rational inquiry and scriptural analysis central to Scottish Presbyterianism. By 1722, Bayes had delivered probationary homilies on biblical texts, such as Matthew 7:2427 and Matthew 11:2930, earning his license to preach and continuing the family tradition in the ministry.

Professional Career

After completing his studies, Bayes assisted his father at the Leather Lane Presbyterian chapel in Holborn from around 1723 until about 1734. In 1734, Thomas Bayes assumed the role of minister at the Mount Sion Chapel, a Presbyterian independent congregation in Tunbridge Wells, succeeding the late John Archer. He served initially as an assistant before becoming the head minister, a position he held until 1752, during which he contributed to the chapel's theological and community activities in this fashionable spa town. His tenure coincided with Tunbridge Wells' peak as a social and health resort, where he delivered sermons and engaged with a diverse nonconformist community.
Bayes' scientific inclinations led to his election as a Fellow of the Royal Society on November 4, 1742, nominated by prominent figures including Philip Stanhope, the 2nd Earl Stanhope, and John Eames. This recognition highlighted his growing reputation in mathematical circles, facilitated by correspondence with Royal Society members such as John Ward, with whom he exchanged letters on scholarly topics as early as 1720. His fellowship connected him to broader intellectual networks, though he published little during this period.
By 1752, declining health, possibly rheumatism or gout, prompted Bayes to resign from the ministry at Mount Sion Chapel, after which he retired to Tunbridge Wells, supported by inheritance from his family's wealth, originally amassed in the cutlery trade and later in investments. He remained active in local affairs, as indicated by records in the Kent Quarter Sessions, reflecting his involvement in community governance. Additionally, Bayes demonstrated actuarial interests through connections to the Equitable Society for Assuring Lives (later the Equitable Life Assurance Society), where his notebook containing probabilistic calculations was preserved, underscoring his application of mathematics to insurance matters.

Personal Life and Death

Thomas Bayes remained unmarried throughout his life and had no children. He was the eldest son in a family of seven siblings, including his sister Mary, who outlived him until 1780. Little is known about his daily personal routines beyond his professional and intellectual pursuits, as he lived a relatively private existence following his retirement from active ministry in 1752.
In his later years, Bayes suffered from declining health, which prompted his move to Tunbridge Wells, a spa town known for its restorative waters. He died there on April 7, 1761, at the age of 59 or 60. Bayes was buried in the family vault at Bunhill Fields, a nonconformist cemetery in London, where the cost to reopen the vault for his interment was recorded as 14 shillings.
The only purported portrait of Bayes, which appeared in a 1936 publication, has been subject to doubts regarding its authenticity since that time. Historical analysis has highlighted anachronisms in the attire, such as the wig style and clerical collar, which do not align with mid-18th-century nonconformist fashion or portraits of his contemporaries. No verified image of Bayes exists.
Following his death, Bayes' estate and papers were handled by his close friend Richard Price, to whom he bequeathed 100 in his will. Price discovered an unpublished manuscript on probability among Bayes' effects and edited it for publication, presenting it to the Royal Society in 1763; this work introduced what became known as Bayes' theorem.

Published Works

Theological Writings

Thomas Bayes, a Presbyterian minister, published his only known theological treatise, Divine Benevolence, or an Attempt to Prove That the Principal End of the Divine Providence and Government is the Happiness of His Creatures, anonymously in 1731 through London bookseller John Noon. This work served as a direct rebuttal to John Balguy's Divine Rectitude (1731), which emphasized God's justice and moral rectitude as the primary divine attributes. Bayes argued that benevolencedefined as a "kind affection" toward creaturesforms the core of God's nature, positing that divine actions aim to maximize happiness within the constraints of finite capacities, thereby addressing the problem of evil and suffering in the world. He supported this through evidence-based theology, drawing on scriptural references, rational deduction, and observations of natural order to demonstrate that providence promotes creaturely well-being.
The treatise emerged amid 18th-century theological debates, including responses to deism's challenges to divine intervention and providence, though Bayes did not explicitly engage deistic authors. While David Hume's skepticism regarding miracles appeared later in 1748, Bayes' emphasis on probabilistic-like reasoning in assessing divine intent may reflect broader intellectual currents countering such doubts, potentially influencing his later mathematical explorations. This approach prefigures elements of Bayesian epistemology by prioritizing evidential inference in theological claims, though without formal probability.
Bayes' writing style blended rigorous reason with scriptural exegesis and empirical insights, characteristic of Nonconformist discourse that sought to harmonize faith and Enlightenment rationality. The pamphlet's clear, polemical structuredivided into propositions and responsesfacilitated its appeal in dissenting circles, where it garnered "considerable attention" from contemporaries like Philip Doddridge. Circulation remained limited, primarily among English Presbyterians and Nonconformists, with later editions scarce and the work eventually falling into obscurity, as noted by Richard Price in 1765 who lamented its unavailability. Despite this, it reinforced Arian-leaning views within these communities, underscoring benevolence as a counter to stricter Calvinist emphases on justice.
As an active minister at Mount Sion Chapel in Tunbridge Wells, Bayes delivered numerous sermons, but none beyond Divine Benevolence were published during his lifetime, limiting the broader dissemination of his religious thought.

Mathematical Publications

In 1736, Thomas Bayes published anonymously An Introduction to the Doctrine of Fluxions, and a Defence of the Mathematicians Against the Objections of the Author of the Analyst, a work aimed at elucidating the principles of Newtonian calculus while countering the philosophical critiques leveled by Bishop George Berkeley in his 1734 treatise The Analyst. Bayes responded to Berkeley's accusations that fluxionsNewton's method of infinitesimals for handling rates of changerelied on vague notions of infinity and lacked rigorous foundations, potentially undermining both science and religion. The tract systematically defends the mathematical community by arguing that such objections misrepresent the practical utility and logical coherence of fluxional methods, emphasizing their role in advancing natural philosophy over speculative metaphysics.
The core of Bayes' exposition involves a clear explanation of fluxions as infinitesimal quantities representing instantaneous velocities and accelerations, building on Newton's original framework but providing more accessible derivations for key rules and principles. He employs geometric proofs to demonstrate the validity of these concepts, using diagrams and Euclidean-style arguments to resolve ambiguities in handling infinitesimals without invoking higher-order infinities that Berkeley deemed problematic. For instance, Bayes illustrates how fluxions enable the solution of problems in motion and curvature through methodical integration and differentiation, avoiding the "ghosts of departed quantities" that Berkeley derided. These geometric approaches underscore Bayes' commitment to visual and intuitive rigor, making the doctrine more defensible against charges of obscurity.
Philosophically, Bayes challenges Berkeley's elevation of abstract logic and metaphysics as superior arbiters of truth, contending that empirical demonstration in mathematics offers a more reliable path to knowledge than purely speculative reasoning. He critiques Berkeley's portrayal of mathematical debates as divisive to faith and society, asserting instead that fluxions harmonize with theological principles by revealing divine order in nature. This defense not only rehabilitates the Newtonian method but also positions Bayes as a mediator in the broader 18th-century controversy over calculus's foundations.
The publication's reception reflected the intensity of contemporary debates, with Bayes' anonymous intervention contributing to the ongoing discourse that influenced later refinements in calculus pedagogy and philosophy. It demonstrated his deep engagement with 18th-century mathematical thought, showcasing analytical precision that would later inform his probabilistic inquiries. Though not immediately transformative, the work highlighted Bayes' skill in bridging technical innovation with philosophical defense, earning retrospective recognition in historical analyses of early modern mathematics.

Bayes' Theorem

Historical Development

Thomas Bayes composed his most significant contribution to probability theory, the manuscript titled An Essay Towards Solving a Problem in the Doctrine of Chances, sometime during the late 1740s, though the exact date of completion remains uncertain. This work, which served as the centerpiece for exploring inverse probability, was prepared for potential submission to the Royal Society but was never published during Bayes's lifetime, remaining among his unpublished papers until after his death in 1761.
Following Bayes's passing, his close friend and intellectual executor, the Presbyterian minister and philosopher Richard Price, was tasked by Bayes's family with examining his mathematical notes and papers. Price discovered the essay in late 1761 or early 1762 and, impressed by its novelty, undertook substantial editorial revisions to make it suitable for publication. These included clarifications, additional propositions, numerical examples to illustrate the concepts, and an appendix demonstrating one of the essay's key rules; Price's contributions reportedly comprised roughly half of the final published version. He also engaged in correspondence with John Canton, a fellow of the Royal Society and instrument maker, to refine the presentation and secure its communication to the Society.
Price wrote a letter to Canton dated November 10, 1763, submitting the edited essay for communication to the Royal Society, where it was read on December 23, 1763, resulting in its posthumous appearance in volume 53 of the Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society. Price's appendix, providing further elaboration and examples, followed in volume 54 in 1764. This publication occurred within the broader context of mid-18th-century debates on inverse probability, where Bayes's ideas intersected with those of contemporaries such as Thomas Simpson, who had explored related problems of chance and ratio in works like his 1753 treatise on the doctrine of chances.
Scholars have suggested that Bayes's essay may have been influenced by philosophical and mathematical currents of the era, including a potential response to David Hume's empiricist skepticism regarding causation and miracles as outlined in An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding (1748), as well as familiarity with Abraham de Moivre's foundational texts on probability, such as The Doctrine of Chances (1718).

Mathematical Formulation

Bayes' theorem provides a mathematical framework for updating the probability of a hypothesis based on new evidence. In its general probabilistic form, the theorem states that the posterior probability of event AAA given evidence BBB is given by
P(AB)=P(BA)P(A)P(B),
P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A) P(A)}{P(B)},
P(AB)=P(B)P(BA)P(A),
where P(A)P(A)P(A) is the prior probability of AAA, P(BA)P(B \mid A)P(BA) is the likelihood of observing BBB given AAA, and P(B)P(B)P(B) is the marginal probability of BBB, serving as a normalizing constant. This formulation, while expressed here in modern notation, originates from Bayes' propositions on inverse probabilities in his 1763 essay.
Bayes derived the theorem using a geometric approach, modeling probabilities through proportions and limits of binomial events on a continuous space. He considered the problem of determining the probability that an unknown event's true probability \theta lies within specified bounds after observing ppp successes and qqq failures in p+qp + qp+q trials, assuming no prior information. The derivation relies on integrating over possible values of \theta, represented as areas under curves derived from binomial expansions. Specifically, the chance is expressed as the ratio of areas bounded by ordinates of the curve y=Exp(1x)qy = E x^p (1 - x)^qy=Exp(1x)q between limits xxx and XXX, to the total area under the curve, where EEE is a constant ensuring the total probability sums to 1. This leads to the key equation for the posterior density proportional to the likelihood times a uniform prior.
For the continuous case, Bayes employed a billiard table analogy to illustrate the uniform prior assumption. Imagine a square table where a ball is thrown uniformly at random to a position ooo, determining \theta as the proportion of the table to one side of a line from the cue end. Subsequent balls thrown to simulate trials fall above or below this line with probability \theta or 11 - \theta1. The probability that \theta lies between two lines after ppp "successes" (balls above) and qqq "failures" is the ratio of the intercepted area in the geometric figure to the total area, capturing the limiting behavior of binomial proportions.
A special case for inverse probability appears in Bayes' Proposition 5: if an event AAA has prior probability proportional to ppp, and evidence BBB has conditional probability p/bp/bp/b given AAA, then the posterior P(AB)=p/bP(A \mid B) = p / bP(AB)=p/b. This discrete form assumes uniformity across possible causes. Richard Price, in editing the essay, added a concrete discrete example in the appendix using a lottery with an unknown ratio of blanks to prizes to demonstrate the theorem. For instance, after observing 10 blanks and 1 prize, the chance that the true ratio lies between 9:1 and 11:1 is approximately 0.07699 (or odds of about 12.3:1 against), illustrating how evidence updates the probability under a uniform prior over possible ratios.
The original formulation has limitations, notably lacking a general method for specifying non-uniform priors; Bayes focused exclusively on uniform priors, justified by the absence of prior evidence favoring any particular probability degree. This assumption simplifies the marginal P(B)P(B)P(B) to an integral of the likelihood over a uniform distribution but restricts applicability to scenarios without informative priors.

Bayesianism

Philosophical Interpretations

Bayesian probability, in its philosophical interpretation, represents an epistemic approach to probability, viewing it as a measure of rational degree of belief or confidence rather than an objective frequency of events. This contrasts sharply with the frequentist interpretation, which defines probability as the long-run relative frequency of outcomes in repeated trials under fixed conditions. In the Bayesian framework, probabilities quantify an agent's subjective credences, updated rationally in light of new evidence to maintain coherence with the axioms of probability theory. This epistemic view emphasizes inductive reasoning, where prior beliefs are adjusted to form posterior beliefs, providing a normative model for rational belief revision.
Bayes' theological background as a Presbyterian minister profoundly shaped this interpretation, linking probabilistic updating to arguments for divine design and the rationality of religious belief. His work emerged in the context of 18th-century debates, particularly as a response to David Hume's skepticism regarding miracles in "Of Miracles" (1748), where Hume argued that testimony for extraordinary events could never outweigh the uniformity of natural laws. Although Bayes' essay does not explicitly address miracles, it provided a framework that his editor Richard Price used to counter Hume, demonstrating how evidence from testimony could rationally increase confidence in improbable events, such as miracles, through belief updatingthus defending Christian doctrines like the resurrection by showing that divine intervention need not violate probabilistic coherence. This application framed probability as a tool for theological apologetics, allowing priors representing initial skepticism to be overcome by sufficient evidential support.
The early philosophical reception of Bayes' ideas, primarily through Richard Price's 1768 dissemination and elaboration, sparked debates on the subjectivity of prior probabilities in inductive logic. Price applied Bayes' framework to miracles, using uniform priors to argue that reliable testimony could establish high posterior probabilities for divine acts, influencing 18th- and 19th-century discussions on evidence and induction. Critics, including later figures like Pierre-Simon Laplace, contested the arbitrariness of subjective priors, advocating objective principles like indifference to mitigate bias, yet this subjectivity remained central to Bayesianism's appeal in philosophy as a flexible model of rational inquiry. Key concepts such as coherenceensuring beliefs avoid inconsistenciesand Dutch book arguments, which demonstrate that incoherent credences lead to sure losses in betting scenarios, were rooted in these early formulations, though formalized later, underscoring probability's role in enforcing rational belief.

Historical Evolution

Following the posthumous publication of Thomas Bayes's essay in 1763, which introduced a method for updating probabilities based on evidence, the development of Bayesian approaches entered a period of generalization and application by subsequent scholars. Pierre-Simon Laplace independently rediscovered and extended the theorem in his 1774 memoir "Mmoire sur la probabilit des causes par les vnements," formulating it in integral form as a general rule for inverse probability, where the probability of a cause given an event is proportional to the probability of the event given the cause multiplied by the prior probability of the cause. Laplace further applied this framework extensively from 1774 to 1812 in celestial mechanics and astronomy, using it to estimate planetary masses and orbits from observational data, such as in his analysis of Jupiter's satellites and the probability of cometary returns, thereby establishing Bayesian methods as a tool for scientific inference in physical sciences.
In the 19th and early 20th centuries, Bayesian ideas faced both advancement and critique amid growing emphasis on logical and empirical foundations of probability. George Boole contributed to probabilistic logic in his 1854 work An Investigation of the Laws of Thought, where he explored conditions for probable inferences and criticized subjective priors in inverse probability as overly reliant on unverified assumptions, influencing a shift toward more objective interpretations. John Venn advanced the frequentist view in his 1866 book The Logic of Chance, rejecting Bayesian updating for one-off events in favor of long-run frequencies, arguing that probability should reflect empirical limits rather than personal degrees of belief. John Maynard Keynes, in his 1921 A Treatise on Probability, incorporated Bayesian elements by treating probability as a logical relation between evidence and conclusions but critiqued strict Laplacean objectivity, proposing instead a non-numerical "degree of rational belief" to handle uncertainty in economics and induction. These efforts coincided with a temporary decline in Bayesian methods, overshadowed by the rise of frequentist statistics promoted by Ronald A. Fisher through maximum likelihood estimation in the 1920s and the Neyman-Pearson hypothesis-testing framework in the 1930s, which prioritized long-run error rates over prior beliefs and dominated statistical practice in experimental sciences.
The mid-20th century marked a revival of Bayesian statistics through foundational works that addressed philosophical and methodological critiques. Harold Jeffreys's 1939 Theory of Probability defended objective Bayesian inference by advocating non-informative priors and applying the approach to geophysical data, positioning it as a coherent alternative to frequentist methods for hypothesis testing and parameter estimation. Leonard J. Savage's 1954 The Foundations of Statistics formalized subjective Bayesian decision theory, axiomatizing personal probabilities as utilities in behavioral terms and demonstrating their consistency with rational choice under uncertainty. Complementing this, Bruno de Finetti emphasized subjectivism in his 1937 paper "La prvision: ses lois logiques, ses sources subjectives," arguing that all probabilities are inherently personal degrees of belief, exchangeable over sequences, and that objective frequencies emerge as limits of subjective coherence, thus reframing Bayesianism as a normative theory of coherence rather than empirical description.
This revival gained practical momentum during World War II through the code-breaking efforts at Bletchley Park, where I. J. Good and Alan Turing employed Bayesian methods to decipher German Enigma messages. Turing developed the Banburismus technique, using sequential probability ratios to assess wheel settings, while Good refined Bayesian updating for estimating unseen message patterns from partial cribs, enabling efficient allocation of computing resources like the Bombe machines and contributing to the Allies' intelligence advantage.

Modern Applications and Impact

Bayesian methods have found extensive applications in machine learning, where naive Bayes classifiers are employed for tasks such as text classification and spam detection due to their efficiency with high-dimensional data. Bayesian networks further enable probabilistic modeling of complex dependencies, supporting decision-making in areas like predictive analytics. In medicine, these techniques underpin diagnostic testing by computing positive and negative predictive values, allowing clinicians to update disease probabilities based on test results and prior prevalence data. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration has endorsed Bayesian statistics for medical device trials, highlighting their role in incorporating prior evidence to improve trial efficiency. In artificial intelligence, probabilistic programming languages facilitate Bayesian inference for scalable models, enabling applications in uncertainty quantification within deep learning frameworks. Finance leverages Bayesian approaches for risk assessment, such as estimating Value at Risk through posterior distributions that integrate historical data with market priors.
Advancements in computation have propelled Bayesian methods into big data eras since the 1990s, primarily through Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms, which approximate posterior distributions by sampling from complex probability spaces. MCMC's development, tracing back to foundational work in the late 1940s but gaining prominence in the 1990s, revolutionized Bayesian analysis by making it feasible for high-dimensional problems previously intractable. Complementing MCMC, variational inference provides faster approximations by optimizing lower bounds on posterior densities, widely adopted in large-scale machine learning pipelines.
Recent recognitions underscore Bayes' enduring legacy. The University of Edinburgh established the Bayes Centre in 2018 as a hub for data-driven innovation, fostering interdisciplinary research in AI and informatics. In 2021, Cass Business School was renamed Bayes Business School to honor Thomas Bayes' contributions to probability, reflecting a commitment to ethical naming and probabilistic education.
Bayesian frameworks influence evidence-based policy by quantifying uncertainties in decision-making, as seen in public health and regulatory analyses that blend expert priors with empirical data. In climate modeling, they support attribution studies, estimating the probability of human-induced warming through posterior assessments of greenhouse gas impacts. Philosophically, Bayesianism shapes the philosophy of science by formalizing belief updating, though debates persist on prior selection, with critics arguing that subjective choices can bias inferences if not rigorously justified via sensitivity analyses.
