 The multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem is a foundational model in decision theory and machine learning that formalizes the exploration-exploitation dilemma in sequential decision-making under uncertainty. In its basic stochastic formulation, a decision-maker confronts a finite set of KKK actions, termed "arms," each linked to an unknown probability distribution over rewards, typically assumed to be independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) draws from bounded support, such as . At each discrete time step t=1,2,,Tt = 1, 2, \dots, Tt=1,2,,T, the decision-maker selects one arm to "pull," receives a reward sampled from its distribution, and seeks to maximize the expected cumulative reward E\mathbb{E}\leftE over a finite horizon TTT, where rtr_trt is the observed reward. This setup contrasts with full-information settings by providing only partial feedbackthe reward from the chosen armnecessitating a balance between exploiting arms with empirically high rewards and exploring others to uncover potentially superior options.
The problem was first systematically formulated by Herbert Robbins in 1952 as part of broader investigations into sequential experimental design, where it emerged as a paradigm for adaptive allocation in uncertain environments. Early work focused on asymptotic optimality, with Lai and Robbins later establishing lower bounds on regretthe difference between the optimal cumulative reward and that achieved by the algorithmin 1985, highlighting the inherent cost of learning in such problems. The name "multi-armed bandit" draws from the analogy to a row of slot machines, known as one-armed bandits, each offering unknown payout probabilities, evoking the gambler's challenge of choosing which to play.
Multi-armed bandits serve as a cornerstone for more complex frameworks in reinforcement learning, providing tractable models for analyzing finite-sample performance in Markov decision processes without state dependencies. Their significance lies in quantifying the exploration-exploitation trade-off through metrics like regret, which guides algorithm design for real-world optimization under limited feedback. Notable applications span clinical trials for adaptive treatment allocation, online advertising for selecting ad variants to maximize clicks, A/B testing in web services, and resource allocation in communications networks, where bandits enable data-driven decisions that improve efficiency and outcomes.

Historical and Empirical Context

Origins and empirical motivation

The multi-armed bandit problem draws its empirical motivation from the classic analogy of a gambler facing a row of slot machines, each with an unknown probability distribution of payouts, often called "one-armed bandits" in gambling lore. The gambler must repeatedly choose which machine (or "arm") to play over a series of pulls to maximize total reward, but without prior knowledge of the machines' payout rates, they face the core challenge of balancing exploitationsticking with arms that have yielded good rewards so farand explorationsampling under-tried arms to potentially discover better options. This dilemma mirrors everyday decision-making scenarios, such as foraging for food where an animal weighs the safety of known patches against the risk of venturing to unknown ones, highlighting the tension between short-term gains and long-term learning through trial and error.
The problem's roots trace back to early 20th-century psychology, particularly Edward Thorndike's experiments on animal learning, which emphasized trial-and-error processes where behaviors leading to satisfaction are reinforced while others are diminished, laying the groundwork for understanding adaptive choice under uncertainty. In the 1940s, foundational work in game theory by John von Neumann and Oskar Morgenstern explored sequential decision problems under uncertainty, providing conceptual precursors to bandit-like scenarios in economic and strategic contexts. The formal multi-armed bandit framework emerged in the 1950s through statistical sequential analysis, with Herbert Robbins introducing the problem in 1952 as a model for optimal experimentation design, where the goal is to allocate trials across options with unknown success probabilities to minimize error in estimation. Concurrently, Richard Bellman incorporated similar ideas into dynamic programming in 1957, framing the bandit problem as a special case of sequential decision-making to optimize over time.
Interest in the multi-armed bandit problem waned mid-century but revived in the 1980s amid advances in artificial intelligence, machine learning, and reinforcement learning, where it became a foundational model for studying adaptive algorithms in uncertain environments. Seminal work by Tze Leung Lai and Herbert Robbins in 1985 established asymptotic lower bounds on performance and efficient strategies, spurring statistical applications and algorithmic developments that integrated the problem into broader AI frameworks. This resurgence emphasized the band's role in quantifying the exploration-exploitation trade-off, influencing fields from adaptive control to online optimization.

These applications prioritize patient safety through constrained exploration, ensuring that high-risk doses are not over-assigned, though ethical challenges arise in balancing individual welfare against collective learning benefits. For instance, bandit models provide a framework for ethical randomization in trials, unifying utilitarian and individual ethics by minimizing overall harm while adhering to informed consent principles.

More recent developments from 2020 to 2025 have expanded bandit applications, including risk-inclusive contextual bandits for early-phase clinical trials in oncology to better balance efficacy and safety, and quantum-enhanced bandits for decision-making in quantum computing environments. In e-commerce and streaming, advanced bandit variants continue to drive personalization, with reported revenue lifts of 5-15% in deployments as of 2024. Deployment challenges include scalability in high-dimensional settings, where numerous arms and covariates demand efficient algorithms to handle vast action spaces without prohibitive computational costs. In healthcare, ethical considerations such as patient safety in adaptive trials require safeguards like forced exploration limits to prevent harm from suboptimal assignments. Success metrics focus on conversion rates and revenue lift; for example, bandit policies have outperformed fixed strategies by 5-15% in real-world ad and pricing deployments, with Yahoo!'s experiments demonstrating revenue gains through elevated click-through rates that translated to higher advertiser value.

The Standard Multi-Armed Bandit Model

Similarly, achieving exact asymptotic optimality becomes impractical for finite horizons $ T $, where approximation methods are often necessary.

Epsilon-greedy and semi-uniform strategies

The epsilon-greedy strategy addresses the exploration-exploitation trade-off in the standard multi-armed bandit model by selecting, at each time step $ t $, the arm with the highest empirical mean reward $ \hat{a}_t = \arg\max_i \hat{\mu}_i(t) $ with probability $ 1 - \epsilon $, and a uniformly random arm with probability $ \epsilon $, where $ \epsilon > 0 $ is a fixed parameter that determines the exploration frequency. To adapt to accumulating information, $ \epsilon $ is often scheduled to decay over time, such as $ \epsilon_t = 1/t $, which reduces exploration as confidence in the estimates grows.
Semi-uniform strategies form a broader class of methods that blend exploitation of top-performing arms with uniform randomization over all arms to ensure exploration. These include variants like the $ \epsilon $-first approach, in which the initial $ N $ steps are allocated to uniform pulls across the $ K $ arms (roughly $ N/K $ pulls per arm), followed by pure exploitation of the arm showing the highest empirical mean for the remaining horizon. Such strategies explicitly partition or probabilistically mix exploration and exploitation, distinguishing them from fully greedy methods while maintaining simplicity.
For fixed $ \epsilon $, the expected regret of epsilon-greedy is $ O(T \epsilon + K \log T / \epsilon) $, combining linear regret from ongoing random exploration with a term capturing suboptimal arm pulls due to estimation errors. This performance is suboptimal relative to the $ O(\log T) $ regret of optimal strategies, as the uniform exploration does not prioritize uncertain arms.
Epsilon-greedy gained prominence in early reinforcement learning literature, notably in Sutton and Barto's 1998 textbook, where it served as a foundational heuristic for bandit problems. These strategies excel in ease of implementation and low computational cost but falter in sparse-reward environments, where uniform pulls inefficiently sample low-reward arms.

Upper confidence bound strategies

Upper confidence bound (UCB) strategies address the exploration-exploitation trade-off by selecting, at each time step $ t $, the arm $ i $ that maximizes an optimistic estimate of its mean reward: $ \hat{\mu}_i(t) + c \sqrt{\frac{\log t}{n_i(t)}} $, where $ \hat{\mu}_i(t) $ is the empirical mean reward of arm $ i $ after $ n_i(t) $ pulls, $ t $ is the current time step, and $ c > 0 $ is an exploration parameter (often set to $ \sqrt{2} $ for sub-Gaussian rewards). This "optimism in the face of uncertainty" principle favors arms with high empirical performance or high uncertainty (low $ n_i $), promoting targeted exploration of under-sampled options.
The canonical UCB1 algorithm initializes by pulling each arm once, then applies the UCB rule thereafter. For bounded rewards in , UCB1 achieves a regret bound of $ R(T) \leq \left( 8 \sum_{i \neq i^} \frac{\log T}{\Delta_i} \right) + \left( 1 + \frac{\pi^2}{3} \right) \sum_{i \neq i^} \Delta_i $, which is $ O\left( \frac{K \log T}{\Delta} \right) $ in the worst case but instance-dependent and logarithmic overall, matching the Lai-Robbins lower bound up to constants. Variants like UCB-V handle non-stationary or heavy-tailed rewards, while KL-UCB uses the KL divergence for tighter bounds under general distributions.
UCB algorithms are computationally efficient, requiring $ O(K) $ time per step, and have been widely adopted in practice for their strong theoretical guarantees and empirical performance across bandit benchmarks.

Thompson sampling and probability matching

Probability matching is a strategy in multi-armed bandit problems where an arm iii is selected with probability proportional to the posterior probability that it is the best arm given the observed data. This approach aims to balance exploration and exploitation by allocating pulls according to beliefs about relative arm qualities derived from Bayesian inference. A naive form of probability matching simply replicates the empirical frequencies of past rewards for each arm, which can lead to suboptimal performance as it does not fully account for uncertainty in the estimates.

Pricing-based strategies

In pricing-based strategies, the multi-armed bandit model is adapted to dynamic pricing problems, where the arms represent discrete price levels $ p_i $ for $ i = 1, \dots, K $, and the reward at each time step $ t $ for selecting arm $ a_t = i $ is $ r_t = p_i \cdot b_t $. Here, $ b_t $ is a Bernoulli random variable with success probability $ \mu_i = \Pr(b_t = 1 \mid p_i) $, which decreases with $ p_i $ to reflect the underlying demand curve $ D(p) $. The goal is to learn the unknown demand curve while maximizing cumulative revenue, the sum of realized rewards over $ T $ periods, rather than just the sum of unit rewards as in the standard model. This setup accounts for correlated rewards, as the buy probability $ \mu_i $ depends on the price, and the objective shifts to revenue optimization under uncertainty about customer demand.
Key strategies leverage bandit algorithms tailored to profit maximization. The upper confidence bound (UCB) method is applied to estimated profits $ \hat{\nu}_i = p_i \hat{\mu}_i $, where $ \hat{\mu}_i $ is the empirical mean of observed buy indicators for price $ p_i $. At each step, the algorithm selects the arm maximizing $ \hat{\nu}_i + c \sqrt{\frac{\log t}{n_i}} $, with $ n_i $ the number of pulls for arm $ i $, $ t $ the current time, and $ c > 0 $ a exploration parameter; this encourages testing higher prices despite their lower buy probabilities to identify revenue-optimal points. Multiplicative weights updates can also select prices by weighting them proportionally to exp(\eta \hat{\nu}_i), with learning rate $ \eta $, providing a hedge against suboptimal choices while converging to the best price. These approaches differ from standard bandit methods by focusing on revenue as the performance metric and handling the monotonicity of $ D(p) $ to prune low-profit prices early.

Contextual Bandits

Model setup and differences from standard

In the contextual bandit model, the decision-making process incorporates side information, referred to as contexts, to enable more personalized choices. At each time step $ t = 1, 2, \dots, T $, the learner first observes a context $ x_t \in \mathbb{R}^d $, which may represent features such as user demographics or environmental variables. The learner then selects an arm $ a_t $ from a finite set of $ K $ arms and receives a reward $ r_t $ sampled from a distribution that depends on both the context and the chosen arm: $ r_t \sim f(x_t, a_t, \theta^) + \epsilon_t $, where $ \theta^ $ is an unknown parameter governing the reward function and $ \epsilon_t $ is noise, often assumed to be zero-mean and sub-Gaussian. The expected reward for arm $ i $ in context $ x_t $ is denoted $ \mu_i(x_t) = \mathbb{E} $.
This framework differs from the standard multi-armed bandit model, where rewards for each arm are drawn independently from fixed, context-independent distributions, leading to stationary expected rewards across rounds. In contextual bandits, the reward distributions are non-stationary with respect to the observed contexts, allowing arm qualities to vary dynamically per instance; for example, the mean rewards may take the form $ \mu_i(x_t) = x_t^\top \theta_i $ for unknown parameters $ \theta_i $ per arm. The standard model emerges as a special case when contexts are absent or constant, reducing to independent and identically distributed rewards without personalization. Additional structure, such as linear or Lipschitz continuity of the reward functions, is often imposed to facilitate learning. Historically, the core idea originated in advertising applications with Woodroofe's (1979) analysis of a one-armed bandit incorporating covariates to model sequential decisions under uncertainty. The multi-armed contextual bandit was later formalized and popularized in the machine learning literature during the 2000s, building on stochastic approximation techniques.

Adversarial Bandits

Model and adversarial assumptions

In the adversarial multi-armed bandit model, rewards lack the independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) structure assumed in the stochastic setting, where each arm has a fixed unknown mean and rewards are drawn from stationary distributions enabling logarithmic regret. Instead, rewards $ r_{t,i} \in $ for arm $ i $ at time $ t $ are arbitrary and selected by an adversary without distributional assumptions, emphasizing worst-case performance over any environment. This shift focuses analysis on regret scaling with $ \sqrt{T} $, reflecting the absence of exploitable statistical patterns like fixed means.
The model operates over $ T $ rounds with $ K $ arms: at each round $ t $, the learner chooses action $ I_t \in {1, \dots, K} $, then receives and observes only the reward $ r_{t, I_t} $ under bandit feedback (unlike full-information settings where all $ r_{t,i} $ are revealed). The adversary determines the reward vector $ \mathbf{r}t = (r{t,1}, \dots, r_{t,K}) \in ^K $; it is oblivious if the entire sequence $ {\mathbf{r}t}{t=1}^T $ is fixed in advance, independent of the learner's past actions, or adaptive if $ \mathbf{r}t $ depends on $ I_1, \dots, I{t-1} $. While some analyses focus on oblivious adversaries for simplicity, key algorithms achieve optimal bounds against adaptive adversaries as well.

EXP3 algorithm

The EXP3 (Exponential-weight algorithm for Exploration and Exploitation) algorithm addresses the adversarial multi-armed bandit problem by maintaining a set of weights $ w_{t,i} $ for each arm $ i = 1, \dots, K $ at time step $ t $, initialized to $ w_{1,i} = 1 $. Advanced and Specialized Variations

Infinite-armed and continuous bandits

In the infinite-armed bandit problem, the action space consists of an uncountably infinite set of arms, such as a continuous interval A=A = A= or more generally a compact subset of Rd\mathbb{R}^dRd. To make the problem tractable, a common structural assumption is that the mean reward function :A\mu: A \to :A is Lipschitz continuous, satisfying $|\mu(a) - \mu(a')| \leq L |a - a'| $ for some constant L>0L > 0L>0 and Euclidean norm \|\cdot\|. This smoothness ensures that nearby arms have similar expected rewards, allowing algorithms to exploit local information rather than treating each arm independently.
A key challenge in this setting is the absence of a finite number of arms, which precludes direct application of upper confidence bound (UCB) strategies designed for discrete cases. Instead, algorithms rely on adaptive discretization of the action space. The zooming algorithm, for instance, maintains a collection of "zoom cells" that partition the space based on uncertainty estimates, expanding promising regions and contracting uninformative ones over time. This approach achieves sublinear regret by focusing exploration on areas near the optimal arm, with a regret bound of O(T(d+1)/(d+2))O(T^{(d+1)/(d+2)})O(T(d+1)/(d+2)) for ddd-dimensional arms under the Lipschitz assumption, matching known lower bounds up to logarithmic factors. Hierarchical partitioning methods, such as those using kd-trees, similarly refine the discretization dynamically, pulling arms from finer partitions in high-reward regions.
For more general smoothness captured by kernels, the Gaussian Process Upper Confidence Bound (GP-UCB) algorithm models the reward function as a sample from a Gaussian process prior with a kernel (e.g., squared exponential) that encodes the assumed regularity. GP-UCB selects actions by optimizing an upper confidence bound derived from the posterior mean and variance, yielding regret bounds of O(TT)O(\sqrt{T \gamma_T})O(TT) where T\gamma_TT is the maximum information gain, which grows slowly for smooth kernels. In the continuous case, algorithms often sample actions from shrinking confidence sets around the estimated optimum, with lower bounds on regret derived from the metric entropy of the function class, quantifying the complexity of covering the space with balls of radius \epsilon.
These methods address the curse of dimensionality inherent in continuous spaces but remain computationally intensive due to the need for optimization over the action set at each step. Infinite-armed bandits find practical application in hyperparameter tuning, where the continuous space of configurations (e.g., learning rates) is explored under Lipschitz-like smoothness assumptions on the validation loss landscape.

Combinatorial and structured bandits

In combinatorial multi-armed bandits (CMAB), the action space consists of super arms, which are subsets or combinations of K base arms drawn from a predefined combinatorial structure S\mathcal{S}S, such as matroids or knapsacks, where each base arm has an unknown reward distribution. The reward for selecting a super arm stSs_t \in \mathcal{S}stS at time ttt is typically the sum of the rewards from the included base arms, subject to constraints like cardinality limits, and the learner receives semi-bandit feedback by observing the individual rewards of all base arms in sts_tst. This setup extends the standard multi-armed bandit by allowing complex actions that leverage correlations or synergies among base arms, while the objective remains minimizing regret over T rounds compared to the optimal super arm.
Key strategies for CMAB include the Combinatorial Upper Confidence Bound (CUCB) algorithm, which maintains optimistic estimates (UCB indices) for each base arm's mean reward and selects the super arm maximizing the sum of these indices, balancing exploration and exploitation through confidence widening that scales with the inverse square root of observed counts. To address challenges like local optima in constrained settings, variants incorporate perturbations or randomization to escape suboptimal selections, ensuring convergence to global optima under monotonic reward assumptions. These methods achieve a regret bound of O(mTlogK)O(\sqrt{m T \log K})O(mTlogK), where m represents the maximum size of any super arm (e.g., the number of base arms in the largest subset), establishing problem-dependent scaling that improves on naive extensions of standard bandit regret when combinatorial structure is exploited.
Structured bandits build on this framework by imposing additional relational constraints on the base arms, such as graph representations where arms correspond to nodes and rewards are additive over selected paths or subgraphs. In graph bandits, the learner selects paths or traversals, receiving rewards as the cumulative sum along the chosen structure (e.g., node or edge weights), with feedback revealing partial observations aligned to the graph topology; this is particularly useful for network routing or dependency modeling, where regret bounds leverage the graph's sparsity or spectral properties to reduce exploration costs. Semi-bandits, a core feedback model in both combinatorial and structured variants, provide observations for every base arm in the selected super arm, enabling more efficient estimation than full bandit feedback and tightening regret to near-logarithmic terms in some graph-structured cases.
Applications of combinatorial and structured bandits include display advertising, where base arms represent individual ad creatives or items, super arms are bundles or slot combinations (e.g., top-m selections on a page), and rewards aggregate click-through rates or coverage under budget constraints, allowing dynamic optimization of ad placements to maximize engagement while respecting layout structures.

Dueling and collaborative bandits

Dueling bandits extend the multi-armed bandit framework to scenarios where absolute rewards are unavailable, and the learner instead obtains relative feedback through pairwise comparisons between arms. In each round, the algorithm selects two arms to "duel," receiving a binary or ternary outcome: a reward of +1 if the first arm is preferred, 0 if the second is preferred, and 0.5 in case of a tie, with the outcome subject to noise. This setup is particularly suited to preference-based learning tasks, such as optimizing rankings.
Preferences in dueling bandits are commonly modeled using the Bradley-Terry-Luce (BTL) model, where the probability that arm aaa is preferred over arm bbb is given by

with i>0\mu_i > 0i>0 denoting the latent quality (or strength) of arm iii. Under this model, the goal is often to identify a Condorcet winneran arm that beats every other arm in pairwise comparisons with probability greater than 0.5. Dueling bandits gained prominence in the 2000s for applications in information retrieval, such as learning-to-rank in search engines, where user click data provides implicit pairwise preferences between documents.
Prominent algorithms for dueling bandits include Relative Upper Confidence Bound (RUCB), which tracks empirical estimates of pairwise preference probabilities and selects duels to balance exploration and exploitation by favoring pairs involving high-confidence bounds. Another influential approach is Beat-the-Mean (BTM), which minimizes dueling regretthe cumulative suboptimality of selected duels relative to always pitting the best arm against a mean benchmarkby adaptively sampling arms to refine quality estimates. A notable challenge in dueling bandits arises from potential intransitive preferences, where cyclic dominance (e.g., a>b>c>aa > b > c > aa>b>c>a) violates transitivity and may preclude a clear Condorcet winner, increasing the complexity of regret minimization.
Collaborative bandits address multi-agent settings where several learners (agents) interact with the same or related bandit instances, sharing information to accelerate collective regret minimization while potentially preserving privacy. This framework is especially relevant in recommender systems, where agents represent users or devices, and collaboration mitigates data sparsity through aggregated insights without fully exposing individual preferences. Privacy enhancements, such as differential privacy mechanisms, are integrated to bound information leakage during sharing, enabling applications in federated environments.
In collaborative setups, information exchange can be centralized, with a coordinator aggregating pulls and rewards from all agents to compute global estimates, or distributed, relying on peer-to-peer protocols like gossiping to propagate local observations asynchronously. Gossip-based methods, for instance, allow agents to randomly select peers for updates, reducing reliance on a central server while bounding communication overhead. However, these approaches face significant challenges from communication costs, as excessive messaging in large-scale or bandwidth-constrained networks can dominate the regret, necessitating algorithms that minimize rounds of exchange.
