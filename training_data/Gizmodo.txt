 Gizmodo is a technology news website specializing in coverage of gadgets, consumer electronics, science, and digital culture. Founded in 2002 by Peter Rojas as one of the early internet tech blogs, it originally operated under the Gawker Media network established by Nick Denton.
The site has experienced multiple ownership transitions amid the turbulence of digital media, including acquisition by Univision Communications in 2016 as part of Gawker Media's assets post-bankruptcy for $135 million, a sale to private equity firm Great Hill Partners in 2019 forming Gizmodo Media Group, and transfer to European digital publisher Keleops in 2024 to expand tech-focused content.
Gizmodo achieved prominence through investigative reporting on tech leaks, such as the 2010 publication of details on an unreleased iPhone prototype purchased from an anonymous source, which prompted a police raid on editor Jason Chen's home and sparked debates over journalistic protections in the digital age.

History

Founding and Gawker Media Era (20022016)

Gizmodo launched on July 15, 2002, when its founding editor Peter Rojas published the site's inaugural post, establishing it as a weblog dedicated to consumer gadgets and emerging technology under the nascent Gawker Media network led by Nick Denton. Rojas, a technology writer, aimed to cover devices like personal digital assistants and early mobile phones with a focus on hands-on reviews and insider news, differentiating it from traditional tech journalism through a conversational, enthusiast-driven tone. The site quickly gained traction amid the blogosphere's rise, benefiting from Gawker Media's model of independent, ad-supported digital publishing that prioritized traffic over institutional gatekeeping.
Rojas departed in March 2004 to co-found Engadget, leaving Gizmodo to editors including Joel Johnson, who expanded its scope to include more analytical reviews and cultural commentary on tech trends. Under Gawker Media, the site grew into a leading tech outlet, launching international editions such as Gizmodo Australia in April 2007 under license, and amassing millions of monthly pageviews through scoops on product leaks and prototypes. Its stylesnarky, speculative, and occasionally provocativemirrored Gawker's broader ethos of unfiltered reporting, though Gizmodo maintained a narrower emphasis on hardware and software innovations.
A pivotal moment came in April 2010, when Gizmodo staff acquired a lost iPhone 4 prototype for $5,000 from individuals who found it at a California bar and published a detailed teardown revealing its redesigned flat glass body and stainless steel band. The story drew over 3 million views but prompted an investigation by the San Mateo County Sheriff's Office, culminating in a June 2010 raid on editor Jason Chen's home where police seized computers and devices amid allegations of receiving stolen property. No charges were filed against Gizmodo or its staff, but the incident highlighted tensions between aggressive tech journalism and corporate secrecy, with Apple criticizing the purchase as unethical. By 2016, as Gawker Media grappled with bankruptcy following a $140 million defamation judgment against its parent company in Bollea v. Gawker, Univision Communications acquired Gizmodo's assets (excluding Gawker.com) for $135 million, marking the end of its direct Gawker oversight.

Univision Acquisition and Gizmodo Media Group (20162019)

In August 2016, Univision Communications acquired the principal assets of the bankrupt Gawker Media for $135 million in a U.S. Bankruptcy Court auction. The deal, executed through Univision's Fusion digital unit, encompassed key properties including Gizmodo, Deadspin, Jezebel, Lifehacker, and Splinter, while excluding the shuttered Gawker.com domain following Gawker's insolvency triggered by a $140 million defamation judgment in the Hulk Hogan privacy lawsuit. This purchase marked Univision's bid to diversify beyond its core Spanish-language television audience into English-language digital media, targeting millennials via high-traffic blogs with combined monthly uniques exceeding 100 million at the time.
The assets were promptly reorganized and rebranded as Gizmodo Media Group (GMG), integrated into Univision's Fusion Media Group division. On September 21, 2016, Univision named Raju Narisetti, formerly managing editor of The Wall Street Journal's international edition and a senior vice president at News Corp, as GMG's CEO to direct editorial and business strategies across the portfolio. Narisetti's appointment aimed to stabilize operations post-Gawker's chaotic collapse, emphasizing scale in digital publishing amid industry shifts like algorithm-driven traffic declines on platforms such as Facebook.
GMG's period under Univision ownership from late 2016 to early 2019 was marked by persistent challenges, including cultural friction between Univision's legacy broadcast leadership and GMG's independent digital staff, as well as ineffective synergies in ad sales bundling with Univision's TV inventory. The group's English-focused, irreverent content clashed with Univision's Hispanic-market priorities, limiting cross-promotion and revenue uplift; former executives cited mismanagement and ballooning costs as exacerbating factors in an already strained digital ad environment. Financially, GMG posted $8085 million in 2018 revenue but incurred tens of millions in losses, prompting a $32.5 million asset write-down in Q4 2018.
Editorial and staffing turbulence intensified these pressures. Narisetti exited in April 2018 amid Univision-wide executive shakeups, including the departure of Fusion CEO Felipe Holgun and chief content officer Isaac Lee. Company-wide layoffs exceeding 150 roles in April 2018 rippled into GMG and Fusion, followed by voluntary buyout offers to GMG staff in June 2018 as part of broader cost-slashing to address $300 million in targeted reductions. By July 2018, with IPO ambitions abandoned and losses mounting, Univision launched a formal sale process for GMG and its separately acquired Onion properties (purchased for around $70 million in 20162017). The Univision era concluded on April 8, 2019, when GMG was sold to private equity firm Great Hill Partners for an undisclosed sum estimated below $50 million combined with The Onion, reflecting the acquisition's ultimate underperformance.

G/O Media Ownership and Internal Struggles (20192024)

In April 2019, private equity firm Great Hill Partners acquired the Gizmodo Media Groupwhich included Gizmodo, Jezebel, The A.V. Club, Deadspin, and other digital propertiesfrom Univision Communications, along with The Onion, forming G/O Media Inc. as the new parent company. Jim Spanfeller, a former Forbes executive, was appointed CEO and became an investor in the venture, with Great Hill Partners holding predominant ownership. The acquisition aimed to consolidate these assets under a growth-oriented structure, but it quickly led to operational changes including staff reductions of approximately 25 employees shortly after the deal closed.
Tensions escalated in late 2019 with the Deadspin controversy, where G/O Media management issued a directive to site staff to "stick to sports," prompting the firing or resignation of editors and the mass exodus of the entire editorial team of over 20 journalists in October and November. This followed complaints from staff about perceived editorial interference, leading to further leadership instability as editorial director Paul Maidment resigned on November 5, 2019. Staff across G/O properties, including those at Gizmodo, expressed concerns over management decisions such as enforced office hours, dress codes, and pushes toward automated content, which they viewed as undermining journalistic independence. 
Unionization efforts intensified these conflicts, culminating in a strike by the Gizmodo Media Group Unionaffiliated with the Writers Guild of America Eastbeginning March 1, 2022, involving roughly 100 workers from outlets including Gizmodo, Jezebel, The Root, and Jalopnik. The action, the first open-ended strike in U.S. digital media history, protested stalled contract negotiations over wages, job security, and working conditions; it lasted five days before settlement on a new agreement with improved provisions. 
Ongoing internal friction under Spanfeller's leadership contributed to site closures and layoffs through 2023, including the November 2023 shutdown of Jezebel, which resulted in 23 editorial staff redundancies, amid broader critiques of cost-cutting and content strategy shifts. These events reflected persistent clashes between private equity-driven efficiency demands and staff priorities for editorial autonomy, though Spanfeller defended the changes as necessary for financial viability. By late 2023, such struggles had eroded morale and prompted preparations for portfolio divestitures, setting the stage for subsequent sales.

Sale to Keleops Media (2024Present)

In June 2024, G/O Media sold Gizmodo to Keleops Media Group, a Switzerland-headquartered digital publisher focused on technology and consumer electronics news with operations in Europe. The deal, announced on June 4, represented the third ownership change for the site in eight years and was described by G/O Media CEO Jim Spanfeller as fetching a "substantial premium." 
Keleops cited the acquisition as a means to accelerate its U.S. expansion, double its global audience reach, diversify revenue streams, and integrate Gizmodo's journalistic expertise with Keleops' digital publishing capabilities. The company committed to retaining Gizmodo's full editorial team without immediate layoffs or restructuring. Financial terms, including the purchase price, were not publicly disclosed.
On December 5, 2024, Gizmodo introduced its first major leadership updates under Keleops ownership, appointing Rose Pastore as Editor-in-Chief and Mylne Poncet as Managing Director for Gizmodo U.S. Pastore, who joined the site in 2017 as science editor and advanced to executive editor in 2023, was tasked with guiding editorial strategy and content innovation. Poncet, bringing over a decade of experience in international marketing and operationsincluding as marketing director at HONOR Technologies Francefocused on business operations, audience growth, and technological enhancements. 
By mid-2025, Keleops reported substantial audience gains for Gizmodo, with monthly unique visitors rising from 3 million at acquisition to 14 million, driven by heightened content investment and organic growth strategies. In September 2025, the site launched a redesigned website emphasizing improved navigation, mobile optimization, and direct traffic channels to boost user retention and engagement. Poncet attributed these gains to prioritizing loyal direct visitors over reliance on social media algorithms.

Editorial Focus and Style

Technology and Gadget Coverage

Gizmodo's technology and gadget coverage originated with its founding on August 1, 2002, by Peter Rojas, who established it as "The Gadgets Weblog," a platform initially focused on consumer electronics, early mobile devices, and innovative hardware. This emphasis on gadgets formed the core of its editorial output during the Gawker Media era, featuring hands-on reviews, unboxing posts, and commentary on products like digital cameras, PDAs, and nascent smartphones, often prioritizing accessibility for tech enthusiasts over purely technical analysis.
The site's gadget reporting evolved to include structured annual features, such as "The Best Gadgets" leaderboards ranking devices like smartphones, laptops, and wearables based on usability, innovation, and market impact, with updates reflecting real-time testing and user feedback integration. Coverage extends to major trade shows, exemplified by its "Best of CES" awards, which in 2025 highlighted prototypes and available products in categories including foldable phones, large-screen TVs, and AI-integrated hardware, selected by editors for delighting potential and technical promise. Similarly, year-end retrospectives, like the 2023 top gadgets list featuring the iPhone 15 and other flagships, combine sales data, benchmark performance, and subjective editor evaluations to guide consumer decisions.
Dedicated sections for gadgets and tech news aggregate content on hardware advancements, with recurring themes including failed innovations (e.g., historical analyses of products like the Microsoft Kin or Nexus Q), concept devices that never commercialized, and comparative reviews of competing ecosystems such as Android foldables versus Apple counterparts. This approach often incorporates visual timelines and lists to contextualize gadget evolution, as seen in retrospectives on Sony's 53-year product history or decade-defining innovations like the Nest Thermostat and Samsung Galaxy Note. 
Gizmodo's gadget focus maintains a consumer-oriented lens, critiquing market flops through empirical lenses like sales figures and adoption ratessuch as Windows Vista's compatibility issues or the Nintendo 64's cartridge limitationswhile celebrating pivotal devices that reshaped categories, evidenced by polls and editorials debating the "most important gadget ever" from printing presses to modern touchscreens. Despite expansions into broader tech policy and software, hardware remains central, with ongoing coverage of emerging trends like smart glasses and AI hardware, often benchmarking against competitors on metrics including price, battery life, and feature parity.

Expansion into Science, Entertainment, and Politics

Gizmodo's initial focus in 2002 centered on consumer gadgets and emerging technologies, but by the late 2000s, it began incorporating science fiction and broader entertainment topics through affiliated Gawker Media properties. In January 2008, Gawker Media launched io9 as a dedicated blog for science fiction, fantasy, futurism, and related pop culture, edited by Annalee Newitz, which expanded Gizmodo's reach into entertainment analysis, including film, television, and literature critiques often tied to speculative genres. This move diversified content beyond hardware reviews to cultural commentary, with io9 later integrating into Gizmodo's structure during a 2015 reorganization under Univision ownership.
Science coverage evolved alongside, initially embedded in tech articles but gaining prominence through io9's early emphasis on scientific concepts in fiction and Gizmodo's own growing roster of space, physics, and biology stories. By 2017, Gizmodo Media Group introduced Earther as a sub-blog on September 27, dedicated to environmental science, climate impacts, and human adaptation to planetary changes, reflecting heightened editorial interest in earth systems amid global debates. Earther's launch addressed gaps in mainstream tech reporting by focusing on empirical data from fields like meteorology and ecology, though its content often highlighted anthropogenic influences, aligning with Gizmodo's shift toward interdisciplinary science journalism. Gizmodo's science tag now encompasses regular features on archaeology, health, and astrophysics, supported by specialized editors.
Political reporting emerged more assertively in the 2010s, intersecting with technology policy such as data privacy, antitrust actions against tech firms, and election-related tech influences. Following the 2016 U.S. presidential election, Gizmodo expanded into general politics in 2017, covering topics like government transparency and partisan science disputes, often framing them through a tech lenssuch as critiques of regulatory rollbacks or surveillance policies. This diversification drew from Gawker-era sensibilities, prioritizing provocative angles on power structures, but relied on verifiable events like FOIA disputes or legislative proposals; however, sources note occasional alignment with progressive critiques, as in analyses of Republican environmental stances. By the 2020s, politics content under Gizmodo's umbrella included cryptocurrency regulations and AI governance, maintaining a focus on empirical policy outcomes over partisan endorsement.

Sensationalism, Clickbait, and Journalistic Practices

Gizmodo's journalistic practices have frequently drawn criticism for prioritizing traffic metrics over rigorous reporting, a hallmark inherited from its Gawker Media origins. During the Gawker era (20022016), the site emphasized provocative headlines and insider scoops designed to generate high engagement, often blending speculation with fact in gadget coverage to exploit reader curiosity. The Columbia Journalism Review's 2015 analysis of Gawker Media's operations highlighted how real-time analytics tools like Chartbeat incentivized editors to chase pageviews, fostering a culture where sensational framingsuch as hyping unverified prototypes or minor tech quirks as revolutionarysuperseded balanced analysis.
Specific examples underscore this approach. In 2014, Gizmodo published an article headlined "Strap this wind-powered generator to your backpack and charge your gadgets on the go," which critics argued misrepresented a prototype's capabilities by implying near-limitless energy generation akin to perpetual motion, despite the underlying technology's physical constraints and lack of independent verification. The piece exemplified broader complaints that Gizmodo's headlines often overpromised to lure clicks, eroding trust when content failed to deliver substantive evidence. Similarly, the site's handling of leaked devices, including paying $5,000 for an iPhone 4 prototype in 2010, blurred lines between journalism and spectacle, prompting debates over ethical sourcing in tech reporting.
Under G/O Media ownership from 2019 onward, these practices evolved amid cost-cutting pressures, with a pivot toward automated content generation amplifying concerns over quality. In July 2023, Gizmodo published AI-generated articles, including a factually flawed "Chronological List of Star Wars Movies & TV Shows" riddled with errors like misordered releases, despite internal staff protests that such output prioritized volume for ad revenue over accuracy. This incident, coupled with the removal of bylines from AI-translated pieces, reflected a metrics-obsessed model where clickbait-style automation supplanted human editorial oversight, leading to accusations of diluting journalistic integrity in favor of scalable, engagement-baiting filler. Critics, including former contributors, argued this exacerbated longstanding tendencies toward snarky, alarmist tech narratives that favored viralitysuch as exaggerated AI doomsayingover empirical scrutiny.

Ownership, Structure, and Associated Outlets

Key Leadership and Editorial Changes

Gizmodo was founded in 2002 by Pete Rojas, who served as its initial editor until his departure in March 2004 to pursue other projects.
Following the 2019 acquisition by G/O Media, the site experienced multiple editorial leadership transitions amid internal challenges, including staff strikes over automation policies and content directives. In July 2020, John Biggs was appointed editor-in-chief, emphasizing a return to independent tech commentary during his prior brief stint with the site in 2005.
In June 2023, Dan Ackerman, previously executive editor at CNET, became editor-in-chief, aiming to refocus on gadget reviews and tech analysis. Ackerman's tenure ended shortly thereafter, with David M. Ewalt having preceded him as editor-in-chief from 2021 until his exit in 2023 to join The Messenger. In December 2023, Rose Pastore was elevated to executive editor, a role she held through the site's June 2024 sale to Keleops Media.
Under Keleops ownership, the first major leadership updates occurred on December 5, 2024, with Pastore's promotion to editor-in-chiefretaining the full editorial staff from the prior regimeand the appointment of a new managing director to oversee growth strategies. These changes were positioned as efforts to stabilize operations and expand tech coverage post-acquisition.

Sub-Blogs and Sister Sites

Gizmodo's sub-blogs have primarily included io9, a section dedicated to science fiction, fantasy, and related pop culture, which was launched on January 2, 2008, as an extension of the main site to cover futuristic and speculative topics. In November 2015, io9 was fully integrated into Gizmodo.com as a subsection rather than a standalone blog, allowing shared resources while maintaining its specialized focus on genre entertainment, books, comics, and media.
Another key sub-blog, Earther, launched around 2017 under the Gizmodo Media Group, concentrates on environmental science, climate change, and planetary issues, operating as a dedicated channel on Gizmodo.com to provide in-depth reporting on ecological topics. Earther's content emphasizes data-driven analysis of human impacts on the environment, distinct from Gizmodo's core gadget and tech emphasis.
Sister sites associated with Gizmodo through its parent companies have formed a network of vertically integrated digital publications targeting niche audiences. During the Gawker Media and Gizmodo Media Group eras (up to 2019), these included Kotaku for video gaming coverage, Jalopnik for automotive culture and news, Lifehacker for productivity and life hacks, Deadspin for sports commentary, and Jezebel for women's issues and feminism. This structure enabled cross-promotion and shared editorial strategies across tech-adjacent verticals, with Gizmodo serving as the flagship for consumer technology.
Under G/O Media ownership (20192024), the portfolio expanded to include entertainment-focused outlets like The A.V. Club, though core tech and lifestyle sisters such as Kotaku and Jalopnik remained closely linked operationally. Following Gizmodo's 2024 sale to Keleops Media, a European tech publisher, Kotaku joined as a direct sister site in July 2025, consolidating gaming and tech under unified management to streamline content production and audience reach. Other former sisters like Lifehacker were shuttered or sold separately amid broader divestitures.

International Editions and Expansions

Gizmodo's international expansions began in the mid-2000s through licensing agreements that localized content for specific markets. In 2006, Japanese publisher Mediagene launched Gizmodo Japan, focusing on product reviews and tech trends tailored to Japanese audiences; the edition has since expanded its digital footprint, with its YouTube channel surpassing 900,000 subscribers by February 2025.
Allure Media introduced Gizmodo Australia in 2007, adapting U.S. content for Australian readers with local perspectives on gadgets and technology. The site operated for over 15 years but closed in July 2024 as part of broader cutbacks by Allure Media, which cited unsustainable economics in the Australian digital publishing sector. Similarly, Gizmodo Brazil emerged around this period, delivering Portuguese-language coverage of tech news and remains operational via its dedicated domain.
In August 2011, Future Publishing partnered with Gawker Media to debut Gizmodo UK, a fully localized site integrated into Future's UK tech portfolio alongside outlets like TechRadar. For Spanish-speaking markets, Gawker Media rolled out Gizmodo en Espaol in January 2013, marking an early step in broader international outreach with region-specific gadget reviews and science reporting. Under G/O Media ownership, the Spanish edition encountered operational shifts in August 2023, when its editorial team was laid off and replaced with AI-translated articles from the English site, prompting criticism over translation quality and loss of cultural nuance.
The June 2024 acquisition by Keleops Media, a Switzerland- and France-based firm specializing in tech journalism across Europe, signals potential for renewed growth in non-English markets. Keleops has emphasized leveraging Gizmodo's brand to bolster its continental presence, though specific new editions have yet to launch as of late 2025. These efforts reflect Gizmodo's historical reliance on partnerships for adaptation, amid challenges like staffing costs and algorithmic shifts affecting global traffic.

Major Controversies

TV-B-Gone Keychain Incident (2005)

In January 2008, at the Consumer Electronics Show (CES) in Las Vegas, Richard Blakeley, a videographer employed by Gawker Mediathe parent company of Gizmodo at the timecovertly deployed a TV-B-Gone keychain device to disable numerous television displays across expo booths and during live product demonstrations. The TV-B-Gone, invented by Mitch Altman in 2004, is a compact universal remote that sequentially transmits power-off infrared codes compatible with most TV brands, effectively silencing screens from up to 30 feet away without user intervention beyond activation. Blakeley targeted high-profile setups, including a wall of monitors during a Motorola presentation, causing abrupt blackouts that interrupted ongoing demos and frustrated exhibitors who had invested significant resources in their displays.
Gizmodo amplified the disruption by publishing a blog post on January 10, 2008, titled "The Meanest Thing We Did at CES," in which staff admitted to receiving free TV-B-Gone units from Make magazine and using them to "shut off a TV. And then another. And then a wall of TVs." The post framed the actions as playful irreverence, but it provoked sharp backlash from industry participants, who labeled the behavior as sabotage rather than journalism. Exhibitors reported technical confusion and delays, with one anonymous source describing it as "totally unprofessional" and questioning potential liability for disrupted events costing thousands in setup and lost productivity. The Consumer Electronics Association (CEA), CES organizer, banned Blakeley from the 2008 event midway through and extended the prohibition to all future CES attendance, citing interference with business operations.
The episode drew broader scrutiny to Gizmodo's editorial practices, exemplifying a pattern of prioritizing sensational stunts over professional decorum in tech reporting. Critics, including fellow bloggers and exhibitors, argued that the prank undermined trust in gadget media, potentially harming smaller vendors reliant on uninterrupted showcases for sales leads. Gawker Media defended the actions internally as consistent with its irreverent brand, but the incident fueled debates on ethical boundaries, with some outlets like Gear Diary condemning it as "hostility" that risked alienating sources and readers. No legal actions ensued, though it contributed to Gizmodo's reputation for provocative tactics amid its rapid growth in the mid-2000s blogosphere.

iPhone 4 Prototype Acquisition (2010)

In April 2010, Gizmodo acquired a prototype of Apple's then-unannounced iPhone 4 smartphone, which had been lost by Apple engineer Gray Powell at the Gourmet Haus Staudt bar in Redwood City, California, following his birthday celebration on or around March 25. The device was discovered by Brian Hogan, who, along with associate Sage Wallower, contacted multiple tech outlets before selling it to Gizmodo for $5,000 in cash, with an additional $3,000 promised upon verification. Gizmodo staff disassembled the unit, confirming features such as a flat glass back panel, stainless steel antenna band, front-facing camera, and support for 802.11n Wi-Fi, which they detailed in articles published on April 19, 2010, titled "This Is Apple's Next iPhone" and "How Apple Lost the iPhone 4."
Apple quickly identified the leak and demanded the prototype's return, asserting it constituted stolen property under California law, as prototypes are classified as trade secrets. On April 23, 2010, San Mateo County sheriff's deputies executed a search warrant at the home of Gizmodo editor Jason Chen, seizing computers, servers, and other electronics in a raid criticized by press freedom advocates for its scope and potential chilling effect on journalism. Gizmodo defended the acquisition as legitimate journalism, arguing the device was merely lost, not stolen, and that they had attempted to contact Apple beforehand without response; the site continued publishing related coverage, generating significant traffic.
Prosecutors declined to indict Gizmodo or its staff, citing insufficient evidence of criminal intent in purchasing what appeared to be a found item, though the case highlighted tensions between aggressive reporting and intellectual property protections. Hogan and Wallower, however, pleaded no contest to misdemeanor theft charges in October 2011, receiving one year of probation, community service, and fines totaling around $250 each, avoiding jail time. The incident preempted Apple's official iPhone 4 unveiling at WWDC on June 7, 2010, but the device's design closely matched Gizmodo's disclosures, validating the leak while drawing scrutiny to Gizmodo's ethics in sourcing unverified hardware.

Facebook Trending Topics Report (2016)

On May 9, 2016, Gizmodo published an article titled "Former Facebook 'News Curators' Say They Suppressed Conservative News," based on accounts from an anonymous former contract worker in Facebook's trending topics team. The report alleged that human curators, tasked with reviewing algorithmically suggested topics, systematically suppressed stories from conservative outlets by maintaining an internal blacklist of sites including Breitbart, the National Review, and the Daily Caller. Stories originating from these blacklisted sources required corroboration from at least two non-conservative mainstream outlets to qualify for trending status, a standard not uniformly applied to left-leaning topics. Additionally, curators were instructed to manually "inject" selected stories deemed newsworthy into the trending module, such as coverage of the disappearance of Malaysia Airlines Flight MH370 or the death of a transgender rights activist, bypassing algorithmic thresholds.
The article's primary source was a single former curator who spoke on condition of anonymity, citing fear of retaliation; Gizmodo did not provide independent verification of the blacklists or practices beyond this testimony and descriptions from other unnamed ex-employees. While the claims highlighted potential human discretion in a system purportedly driven by algorithms, the reliance on anonymous sourcing limited empirical substantiation, and subsequent internal reviews by Facebook found no evidence of widespread partisan suppression. Gizmodo's reporting, published amid Gawker Media's broader journalistic controversies, drew scrutiny for amplifying unverified insider claims, though it prompted immediate political backlash from conservative figures, including U.S. Senators who demanded investigations into platform neutrality.
Facebook rejected the allegations of intentional bias, with a spokesperson stating that curators followed guidelines to ensure topic diversity and did not artificially suppress or promote political viewpoints, asserting that approval rates for liberal and conservative topics were "virtually identical." CEO Mark Zuckerberg personally addressed the controversy on May 12, 2016, affirming the company's commitment to apolitical operations and announcing an internal audit to examine trending processes. In response, Facebook implemented changes on May 24, 2016, reducing human oversight by prioritizing algorithmic selection and standardizing rules to minimize subjective interventions, effectively phasing out much of the curator role to restore trust in the feature's impartiality.
The incident fueled broader debates on tech platform moderation, with Republican lawmakers citing it as evidence of Silicon Valley's left-leaning institutional biases influencing content visibility ahead of the 2016 U.S. presidential election. Despite Facebook's audit concluding no systemic favoritismbased on logged curator decisions showing balanced handling of partisan contentthe procedural overhaul underscored vulnerabilities in hybrid human-algorithm systems to perceptions of editorializing. Gizmodo maintained the story's accuracy, framing it as exposing undisclosed manual overrides, but critics noted the outlet's sourcing constraints and potential for sensationalism in tech journalism.

Bobby Kotick and Gaming Industry Coverage (2025)

In early 2024, Gizmodo published articles scrutinizing former Activision Blizzard CEO Bobby Kotick's leadership amid lingering fallout from the company's 2021 workplace misconduct scandal, which involved allegations of sexual harassment, discrimination, and a "frat boy" culture leading to employee walkouts, SEC investigations, and a $54 million settlement with California's Department of Fair Employment and Housing in December 2023.
These pieces referenced specific claims against Kotick, including purported involvement in tolerating or inquiring about unconventional managerial practices, which he later alleged were based on recanted or dismissed employee complaints that G/O MediaGizmodo's parentknew to be unsubstantiated.
On March 13, 2025, Kotick filed a defamation suit in Delaware's Court of Chancery against G/O Media, targeting Gizmodo and sister site Kotaku for two 2024 articles that, according to the complaint, deliberately revived withdrawn harassment and discrimination allegations to damage his reputation following his December 2023 departure from Activision Blizzard after its Microsoft acquisition.
Kotick's filing asserts that the outlets' gaming industry coverage, which amplified broader Activision critiques during the Microsoft deal's regulatory reviews, prioritized sensational claims over verified facts, including those retracted after internal probes found no substantiation.
The lawsuit seeks damages and retraction, arguing the reporting exploited post-scandal narratives in tech-gaming journalism, where outlets like Gizmodo have historically emphasized corporate accountability but faced accusations of selective emphasis on unproven executive misconduct to align with anti-industry sentiments.
As of October 2025, the case remains unresolved, highlighting tensions between gaming media's role in exposing verifiable abusessuch as Activision's $18 million SEC fine in 2023 for disclosure failuresand the risks of citing disputed internal anecdotes without contextual retraction updates.

Criticisms of Bias and Integrity

Alleged Left-Leaning Political Slant

Media bias rating organizations have consistently classified Gizmodo as left-leaning. Media Bias/Fact Check rates it as Left Biased, citing editorial positions that almost always favor liberal viewpoints through story selection, loaded emotional language, and failure to separate news from opinion, which can mislead readers on political issues. AllSides assigns a Lean Left rating, based on an independent review noting that Gizmodo's politically oriented articles often employ bias in framing former President Donald Trump and conservative policies negatively. Ground News and Biasly similarly score it as Lean Left or Somewhat Left, aggregating data from multiple evaluators.
Specific examples of this slant appear in Gizmodo's coverage of conservative figures and policies. A 2019 article described the Trump administration's pro-coal policy as "spinning" benefits for minorities, using phrasing that implies deception rather than neutral reporting. Another piece labeled a 2019 White House social media summit as an invitation to a "consortium of conservative internet trolls," denigrating participants with pejorative terms typically reserved for ideological opponents. Such word choices and framing, as analyzed by bias evaluators, prioritize narrative alignment with left-wing critiques over balanced presentation, even when underlying facts are sourced accurately from entities like the EPA or Bloomberg.
Gizmodo's editorial philosophy has reinforced perceptions of inherent subjectivity. In a 2012 piece, an editor declared, "Gizmodo is not objective. It never has been, I don't think. And I hope it never will be," arguing that pursuing objectivity in tech and cultural journalism is "meaningless" and preferring unfiltered perspectives. This stance aligns with broader critiques of tech media outlets, where left-leaning institutional biasesstemming from homogeneous editorial staffs and cultural environments in urban tech hubsinfluence topic selection and tone, often sidelining conservative viewpoints unless framed adversarially. Despite these tendencies, Gizmodo maintains a high factual reporting record, with proper sourcing and no major failed fact checks in recent years, suggesting bias manifests more in emphasis and language than outright fabrication.

Ethical Lapses and Sensationalism

Gizmodo has been criticized for ethical lapses in its handling of sourced material, particularly in cases involving potentially illicit acquisitions that raised questions about journalistic integrity and incentives for wrongdoing. In the 2010 iPhone 4 prototype incident, Gizmodo purchased a lost device from an individual later identified as an Apple employee, publishing detailed photos and analysis that previewed the phone's design months before its official reveal; ethics analysts argued this rewarded theft and blurred lines between reporting and complicity in property crimes.
More recently, under G/O Media ownership, Gizmodo incorporated AI-generated content despite internal objections, leading to verifiable inaccuracies that undermined factual reliability. On July 5, 2023, the site published an AI-drafted article listing Star Wars movies and TV shows in chronological order, which erred by placing "The Mandalorian" (premiered 2019) before "Rogue One" (2016) and missequencing other entries like "Solo" relative to animated series timelines; the piece lacked disclosure of AI use and required post-publication corrections after reader complaints. Staff and union representatives highlighted AI's risks of hallucination and unedited propagation of falsehoods, arguing it prioritized cost-cutting over verification and eroded trust in tech journalism.
Sensationalism in Gizmodo's output often manifests through hyperbolic headlines and teaser-style reporting on unconfirmed tech developments, amplifying rumors to drive traffic at the expense of context. Critics have noted a pattern in gadget coverage, where speculative leaks receive outsized emphasissuch as early Android prototypes framed as paradigm shiftsfostering hype cycles that mislead on product viability without rigorous caveats on sourcing reliability. This approach, inherited from its Gawker Media roots, prioritizes virality over measured analysis, as evidenced by traffic-driven metrics influencing editorial decisions during periods of ownership flux.

Responses from Conservative Perspectives

Conservative commentators and media outlets have frequently accused Gizmodo of demonstrating a pronounced left-leaning bias through selective framing, loaded terminology, and adversarial coverage of right-leaning figures. Breitbart News highlighted Gizmodo's repeated criticism of conservative provocateur MILO Yiannopoulos, noting that in a 2016 compilation of objectionable Breitbart articles, six of the ten targeted pieces were authored by MILO, suggesting disproportionate animus toward conservative voices. Similarly, in October 2016, Gizmodo was criticized for excavating and publicizing Reddit comments from debate standout Ken Bone to undermine his neutral image, a tactic likened by Breitbart to the shaming practices of its predecessor Gawker Media. Daily Wire echoed this, portraying the episode as emblematic of elite media efforts to discredit non-partisan conservatives who gain unexpected visibility.
Such incidents contribute to broader conservative critiques of Gizmodo's editorial slant, including the use of pejorative phrasing like "Trump regime" in a 2018 article on Wikipedia edits regarding immigration facilities, which Breitbart argued exemplified biased language that denigrates conservative policies without equivalent scrutiny of left-leaning administrations. Media bias assessors align with these views: Media Bias/Fact Check rates Gizmodo as left-biased, citing consistent editorial favoritism toward progressive positions, such as framing Trump-era environmental policies as harmful to minorities, and a lack of clear separation between news reporting and opinion. AllSides concurs with a "Lean Left" designation, observing that Gizmodo's politically charged stories often cast President Trump and conservative stances in a negative light.
From a conservative standpoint, these patterns reflect not isolated lapses but systemic ideological capture in tech journalism, where outlets like Gizmodo prioritize narrative alignment over neutral analysis, eroding trust among right-leaning audiences. Critics contend this bias manifests in underreporting conservative concerns about big tech overreachironically, even as Gizmodo occasionally exposes such issues, like Facebook's 2016 trending topics curationwhile amplifying progressive cultural critiques. Conservatives argue that such outlets' high factual accuracy ratings, as per Media Bias/Fact Check, mask opinion-driven selectivity that distorts public discourse on technology's societal impacts.

Impact and Legacy

Influence on Tech Journalism

Gizmodo, launched in August 2002 by Peter Rojas under Gawker Media, emerged as one of the earliest dedicated tech blogs, focusing on gadgets and emerging technologies with a weblog format that emphasized rapid updates, personal commentary, and visual storytelling. This approach contrasted with traditional tech journalism in print outlets like PC Magazine, prioritizing insider scoops, hands-on teardowns, and irreverent analysis over formal reporting, which helped attract millions of monthly pageviews by the mid-2000s and established a model for audience engagement through timely, opinionated content.
The site's influence extended to shaping the competitive landscape of digital tech media, as Rojas co-founded Engadget in 2004, directly transplanting Gizmodo's fast-paced, gadget-centric style to a rival platform that grew alongside it. Gizmodo's emphasis on exclusive leakssuch as the 2010 iPhone 4 prototype story, which garnered widespread attention despite ethical backlashnormalized aggressive sourcing tactics in tech coverage, influencing outlets like The Verge and TechCrunch to pursue viral, high-stakes revelations while blending news with cultural critique. This Gawker-era innovation democratized tech publishing by leveraging blogging's low barriers to entry, fostering a shift toward personality-driven narratives that prioritized reader retention via provocative headlines and multimedia over institutional neutrality. 
Over two decades, Gizmodo's bold voice in challenging tech giants, as seen in critiques of Apple's antenna design flaws during the iPhone 4 launch, contributed to a more adversarial tone in the field, encouraging scrutiny of corporate narratives amid growing consumer tech adoption. However, its legacy includes criticisms of sensationalism, with early reliance on rumors occasionally undermining accuracy, a practice that echoed in broader tech media's evolution toward speed over verification. By 2022, as one of the longest-running tech sites, it had influenced the genre's hybridization of science, design, and fiction, maintaining a template for independent commentary despite ownership shifts and industry pressures like AI integration debates.

Achievements in Breaking News and Analysis

Gizmodo has earned recognition for investigative reporting that exposed flaws in law enforcement's use of predictive policing algorithms. In collaboration with The Markup, the outlet published "Prediction: Bias" in 2021, revealing how software from companies like Palantir and PredPol amplified racial disparities in crime forecasting, leading to skewed arrest patterns in cities such as Los Angeles and Chicago. This work contributed to a 2022 Edward R. Murrow Award for Investigative Reporting from the Radio Television Digital News Association, highlighting systemic errors in algorithmic decision-making tools deployed across U.S. police departments.
Earlier, Gizmodo journalists Kashmir Hill and Surya Mattu received the National Press Foundation's 2018 Technology in Journalism Award for a series of articles probing privacy invasions enabled by emerging technologies. Their reporting detailed how facial recognition systems, powered by vast scraped image databases, were being tested by retailers and law enforcement without adequate safeguards, including experiments where shoppers were tracked via hidden cameras in stores like Walmart. This coverage underscored vulnerabilities in consumer data protection and influenced subsequent regulatory scrutiny of biometric surveillance tools.
In analysis, Gizmodo has provided detailed breakdowns of corporate tech practices, such as a 2022 expos on Amazon's "Last Mile" delivery operations, which documented hazardous working conditions for drivers through firsthand accounts and leaked documents, prompting internal reforms and public debate on gig economy labor standards. Similarly, investigations into Ring's partnerships with over 2,000 police departments revealed automatic video-sharing agreements that bypassed user consent, raising alarms about privatized surveillance networks and contributing to policy changes in data access protocols. These pieces combined empirical evidence from leaks, interviews, and data analysis to critique causal links between tech deployment and societal risks, often prioritizing verifiable impacts over unsubstantiated hype.

Current Status and Future Under Keleops

Keleops Media Group, a Swiss digital media company focused on high-tech publications, acquired Gizmodo from G/O Media on June 4, 2024, as part of a strategy to accelerate organic growth, broaden its global audience, diversify revenue streams, and strengthen its position in the tech media sector. The acquisition marked Gizmodo's third ownership change in eight years and aligned with Keleops' ambition to expand its U.S. footprint, building on its portfolio of over 40 million monthly unique visitors worldwide.
Under Keleops' ownership, Gizmodo implemented its first major leadership transition on December 5, 2024, appointing a new editor-in-chief and managing director to oversee editorial strategy and operations, with an emphasis on fostering sustainable expansion. By October 2025, the site rolled out a comprehensive design refresh, featuring faster load times, an improved mobile interface, enhanced content recommendations, and tools for topic and author following to boost user interaction and revive its origins as a conversational blog. Keleops reported significant traffic gains post-acquisition, with monthly unique visitors rising from 3 million to 14 million, alongside metrics such as 62,000 daily direct return visitors averaging 2.7 pages per session and newsletters achieving 40% open rates.
Looking forward, Keleops has signaled continued investment in Gizmodo through portfolio synergies, exemplified by its July 2, 2025, acquisition of gaming site Kotaku from G/O Media in an undisclosed all-cash deal, which complements Gizmodo's tech focus and targets further U.S. market dominance. Planned enhancements include scaling to 8-10 newsletters, amplifying social media presence on platforms like Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube, launching podcasts and audio content, and integrating AI-driven features such as audio generation, advanced search, and chatbots in subsequent phases. Keleops' CEO has expressed intent for additional U.S. acquisitions to support this "bold expansion plan," positioning Gizmodo within a broader ecosystem of specialized tech and gaming outlets.
