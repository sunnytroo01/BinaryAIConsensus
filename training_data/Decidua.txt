Decidua

1x
 The decidua is the specialized, transient endometrial lining of the uterus that undergoes morphological and functional transformation during pregnancy in humans and other mammals, forming under the influence of progesterone to support embryonic implantation and development. This process, known as decidualization, involves the differentiation of endometrial stromal fibroblasts into decidual stromal cells, accompanied by genetic reprogramming, leukocyte recruitment, and the downregulation of pro-inflammatory pathways to establish uterine receptivity. Unlike in non-primate mammals where decidualization is conceptus-dependent, in humans it occurs cyclically during the luteal phase of each menstrual cycle, independent of fertilization, ensuring preparedness for potential pregnancy.
The decidua is anatomically divided into three distinct regions based on their position relative to the implanted embryo: the decidua basalis, which underlies the chorionic villi and contributes to the maternal component of the placenta; the decidua capsularis, which covers the developing embryo and fuses with the chorion; and the decidua parietalis (or vera), which lines the remainder of the uterine cavity. These regions form through a decidual reaction involving cellular hypertrophy, vascular remodeling, and extracellular matrix changes, creating a primary avascular zone followed by a secondary vascularized zone to facilitate nutrient exchange and structural support.
Functionally, the decidua plays a pivotal role in pregnancy establishment and maintenance by regulating trophoblast invasion through secretion of factors like relaxin, providing nutritional support via glycogen and lipid release from decidual cells, and enabling gas exchange and maternal circulation via remodeling of spiral arterioles into low-resistance vessels. It also produces hormones such as prolactin, which aids fetal homeostasis and lung maturation, and prostaglandins that prepare the cervix for labor. Critically, the decidua maintains immune tolerance at the maternal-fetal interface by recruiting uterine natural killer (uNK) cells (comprising 60-70% of decidual leukocytes), macrophages, and regulatory T cells, which modulate responses to prevent embryo rejection while defending against pathogens through cytokines like IL-10 and angiogenic factors such as VEGF. Impaired decidualization is associated with complications including preeclampsia, recurrent miscarriage, and preterm birth, underscoring its essential role in reproductive health.

Overview

Definition and Characteristics

The decidua is the modified endometrial lining of the uterus in pregnant placental mammals, transformed by progesterone into a specialized maternal tissue that forms the maternal component of the placenta and is shed after parturition or during menstruation if pregnancy does not occur. This transformation, known as decidualization, creates a transient structure that supports embryo implantation and early development by providing a nutrient-rich environment and immunological protection. In humans, decidualization begins cyclically during the luteal phase of each menstrual cycle independent of fertilization, with the pre-decidualized endometrium shed if no implantation occurs; in contrast, most non-primate mammals require conceptus signals for decidualization.
Key characteristics of the decidua include enhanced vascularity with dilated and tortuous blood vessels, glandular modifications involving increased branching and secretion, and hypertrophy of stromal cells, which collectively facilitate nutrient and gas exchange while maintaining structural integrity. This tissue is transient, peaking in thickness and functionality during the first trimester before regressing in later pregnancy stages to accommodate placental growth. The decidua is characteristic of species with hemochorial placentation, where maternal blood directly contacts fetal trophoblast, enabling intimate maternal-fetal interactions essential for viviparous reproduction.
Evolutionarily, the decidua is a defining feature of eutherian mammals, emerging in their stem lineage alongside the development of invasive hemochorial placentation to support extended gestation and fetal nourishment. Formation begins post-ovulation in the mid-secretory phase of the menstrual cycle, requiring 810 days of progesterone stimulation, and fully develops with embryo implantation around week 2 of human pregnancy.

Anatomical Divisions

The decidua is anatomically divided into three distinct regions based on their positions relative to the implanted embryo and the uterine wall: the decidua basalis, decidua capsularis, and decidua parietalis. These divisions form shortly after implantation, with the basalis and capsularis arising directly at the implantation site while the parietalis lines the remainder of the uterine cavity.
The decidua basalis is the region underlying the chorionic villi at the site of implantation, positioned between the chorionic plate and the myometrium, where it serves as the maternal component of the developing placenta. It directly interacts with the trophoblast through anchoring villi that attach the fetal chorionic sac to the uterine wall. This layer forms the maternal placenta plate and grows into the chorionic plate, organizing into cotyledons via stem villi.
In contrast, the decidua capsularis is a thin layer that covers the abembryonic pole of the chorion, enclosing the amniotic cavity and the developing embryo while separating it from the uterine lumen. It overlies the smooth chorion (chorion laeve) and initially surrounds the conceptus on the side facing the uterine cavity. As pregnancy advances, the decidua capsularis expands with the growing amniotic sac and fuses with the decidua parietalis by the mid-second trimester, leading to the obliteration of the uterine cavity. This fusion results in the thinning and eventual atrophy of the capsularis due to compression and reduced vascular supply.
The decidua parietalis, also known as decidua vera, lines the rest of the uterine cavity away from the implantation site, forming a thick layer along the uterine wall that is not directly associated with the chorionic villi. It gradually converts from the endometrial lining and provides a supportive barrier throughout gestation. By the end of pregnancy, after fusion with the capsularis, the parietalis remains as the primary decidual layer and is expelled as part of the afterbirth following delivery.
Spatially, these regions are initially separated post-implantation, with the basalis fixed at the implantation site and the capsularis expanding outward from the embryo, while the parietalis encircles the entire structure. As the fetus grows, the uterine cavity diminishes, causing the capsularis to contact and merge with the parietalis around 12-16 weeks, after which the capsularis atrophies in the second trimester due to the expanding amniotic sac. This dynamic process culminates in the decidua's role in delineating maternal-fetal boundaries until parturition, when the remaining parietalis and basalis are shed.

Histology and Cellular Composition

Decidual Stromal Cells

Decidual stromal cells, also known as decidual cells, originate from endometrial fibroblasts within the uterine lining, which undergo a profound transformation known as decidualization during early pregnancy. This process, primarily driven by progesterone, converts these fibroblastic precursors into the dominant cellular component of the decidua. The resulting cells exhibit a distinctive morphology, appearing as large, polygonal or epithelioid structures with abundant eosinophilic cytoplasm that accumulates glycogen granules and lipid droplets for metabolic support. Their nuclei are characteristically enlarged, often becoming polyploid through endoreduplication, which enhances biosynthetic capacity without cell division. In histological sections, these cells display prominent nucleoli, dilated rough endoplasmic reticulum, and a well-developed Golgi apparatus, reflecting their secretory phenotype.
The decidua, enriched with these stromal cells, is histologically stratified into two main layers that reflect varying degrees of cellular density and function. The stratum compactum forms the superficial layer, characterized by tightly packed decidual cells with minimal extracellular space, providing a protective barrier at the endometrial surface. Beneath it lies the stratum spongiosum, an intermediate zone with looser arrangement of cells interspersed with dilated glands and vascular structures, allowing for tissue expansion during implantation. These layers collectively house the bulk of decidual stromal cells, with the compactum and spongiosum predominating in the functional decidua during gestation.
Decidual stromal cells contribute significantly to the tissue's architecture through production of extracellular matrix components, including laminin and type IV collagen, which form a supportive scaffold around vessels and glands. At the maternal-fetal interface, these cells facilitate the deposition of fibrinoid material, culminating in the formation of Nitabuch's layera hyalinized zone of extracellular matrix and cellular debris that separates the decidua basalis from invading trophoblast. In standard hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining, decidual stromal cells are readily identifiable as enlarged, rounded or polygonal profiles with pale, vacuolated cytoplasm and eccentric, hyperchromatic nuclei, often clustering around the modified spiral arteries to encase their walls. This perivascular arrangement highlights their role in vascular adaptation, visible under light microscopy at magnifications of 20x to 40x.

Immune and Vascular Components

The vascular architecture of the decidua undergoes significant remodeling, particularly in the spiral arteries, to support increased blood flow and nutrient exchange during pregnancy. This process involves the loss of vascular smooth muscle cells and partial replacement or apoptosis of endothelial cells, transforming coiled, high-resistance arteries into dilated, low-resistance conduits with widened lumens. Endothelial changes include increased permeability, facilitated by trophoblast invasion and immune cell interactions, which enhance maternal-fetal exchange without vasomotor control. Additionally, the decidua features large venous sinuses that collect blood from remodeled arteries, alongside modified smooth muscle layers that contribute to vascular cushionsaggregations of extracellular matrix and cells that stabilize vessel walls. These adaptations are critical for accommodating the exponential rise in uteroplacental blood flow, from approximately 50 mL/min pre-pregnancy to over 500 mL/min by term.
The immune infiltrate in the decidua is dominated by uterine natural killer (uNK) cells, which are CD56bright and constitute up to 70-80% of leukocytes in the first trimester, alongside macrophages (20-25%) and T regulatory cells (10-15%). Granulocytes, such as neutrophils and eosinophils, are notably scarce, comprising less than 5% of the leukocyte population throughout gestation, reflecting a specialized anti-inflammatory microenvironment. These immune cells, particularly uNK and macrophages, play a key role in tissue remodeling by promoting endothelial and smooth muscle alterations in spiral arteries, ensuring vascular patency without eliciting rejection.
Histologically, immune cells are predominantly clustered around spiral arteries and uterine glands, with uNK cells often infiltrating vascular walls to facilitate remodeling. This perivascular and periglandular distribution supports coordinated interactions with stromal elements, forming vascular cushions that consist of immune cell aggregates, decidual stromal cells, and fibrinoid material to regulate blood flow and prevent hemorrhage. Leukocyte proportions shift across trimesters, with uNK cells peaking at 70% in the first trimester before declining to about 30% by the third, while macrophages and T regulatory cells increase relatively to maintain remodeling and structural integrity.

Development and Decidualization

Hormonal Initiation

The formation of the decidua is primarily initiated by hormonal signals following ovulation, with progesterone serving as the main driver of decidualization in endometrial stromal cells. Produced by the corpus luteum in the ovary, progesterone levels surge in the luteal phase of the menstrual cycle, typically around days 14-28 in humans, promoting the differentiation of stromal fibroblasts into decidual cells. Estrogen, derived from the growing ovarian follicles during the follicular phase, plays a crucial priming role by preparing the endometrium for progesterone responsiveness, enhancing uterine receptivity to subsequent hormonal cues.
The initial ovarian origin of these hormones shifts as pregnancy progresses; human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG), secreted by the implanting trophoblast cells of the early placenta starting around day 8-10 post-ovulation, sustains the corpus luteum and thereby maintains elevated progesterone production to support ongoing decidualization. Local autocrine factors, such as relaxin primarily from the corpus luteum, further contribute to this initiation by modulating stromal cell responses and vascular adaptations in the endometrium.
At the molecular level, these hormonal signals involve dynamic changes in receptor expression. Progesterone receptors, including the isoforms PR-A and PR-B, are upregulated in the endometrial stroma during the mid-luteal phase, enabling progesterone to activate transcriptional networks essential for decidual onset; PR-A predominates and is critical for stromal differentiation. Estrogen receptors undergo modulation, with downregulation of ER following estrogen priming to prevent overstimulation and allow progesterone dominance. Concurrently, activation of the cAMP signaling pathway in stromal cells, often triggered by factors like prostaglandin E2, synergizes with progesterone to initiate decidual gene expression programs.
While the core hormonal mechanisms are conserved across mammals, species variations exist, particularly in timing and independence from implantation. In humans and other primates, decidualization begins spontaneously in the mid-luteal phase of every cycle due to the extended luteal phase (approximately 14 days), independent of embryo presence. In contrast, rodents exhibit implantation-triggered decidualization with a shorter luteal phase (4-5 days), though the roles of progesterone and estrogen remain fundamentally similar.

Cellular Transformation Process

Decidualization begins during the transition from the proliferative to the secretory phase of the menstrual cycle, where endometrial stromal cells undergo initial morphological changes in response to hormonal cues, preparing the tissue for potential implantation. This process advances with the onset of the secretory phase, marked by the differentiation of fibroblastic stromal cells into larger, rounded decidual cells, though full transformation requires the implantation of the blastocyst to trigger complete decidual development. Following implantation, decidualization intensifies locally at the implantation site within the functionalis layer, leading to the distinct formation of the decidua basalis, capsularis, and parietalis regions.
Key molecular markers characterize this transformation, including the upregulation of prolactin (PRL) and insulin-like growth factor-binding protein-1 (IGFBP-1), which are secreted by differentiating stromal cells and serve as indicators of decidual progression. HOXA10 expression is also induced during decidualization, playing a regulatory role in endometrial receptivity and stromal differentiation through transcriptional control of downstream genes. HOX gene regulation, particularly of HOXA10 and HOXA11, involves complex transcriptional networks that coordinate cell fate decisions, with dysregulation linked to impaired decidualization. Epigenetic modifications, such as DNA methylation, further modulate these processes by altering chromatin accessibility and gene expression patterns in stromal cells, contributing to the stable phenotypic switch during decidualization.
At the cellular level, decidualization involves the arrest of stromal cell proliferation, shifting resources toward differentiation and secretory functions, which halts further mitotic activity in the transforming population. Stromal fibroblasts differentiate into epithelioid decidual cells, characterized by enlarged cytoplasm, prominent nuclei, and accumulation of glycogen and lipid droplets, enhancing their supportive role. Concurrently, extracellular matrix (ECM) remodeling occurs through the deposition of fibrillar collagens, fibronectin, and laminins by decidual cells, restructuring the endometrial architecture to accommodate implantation and trophoblast invasion. Endometrial glands also contribute by increasing secretion of histiotroph, a nutrient-rich fluid containing glycoproteins, lipids, and growth factors that supports early embryonic development prior to placental circulation.
In vitro models of decidualization replicate these changes using primary human endometrial stromal cells cultured with progesterone and cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) analogs, such as medroxyprogesterone acetate and 8-bromo-cAMP, to mimic hormonal induction and study the process in isolation. These models demonstrate robust expression of markers like PRL and IGFBP-1 within 6-14 days of treatment, allowing researchers to dissect molecular pathways without in vivo complexities. Such systems have revealed the synergistic role of progesterone and cAMP in activating protein kinase A pathways, which drive morphological and functional differentiation akin to in vivo decidualization.

Physiological Functions

Nutrient and Gas Exchange

The decidua basalis serves as the primary maternal interface for nutrient and gas exchange between the mother and fetus, where maternal blood in the intervillous space diffuses essential substances across the syncytiotrophoblast layer into fetal capillaries. This diffusion-based mechanism enables the transfer of nutrients such as amino acids, lipids, and glucose, as well as oxygen, while facilitating the removal of fetal waste products like carbon dioxide and urea. In early pregnancy, prior to the establishment of full maternal circulation, histiotrophic nutrition predominates, with glandular secretions from the decidualized endometrium providing histiotrophea nutrient-rich fluid containing proteins, carbohydrates, and growth factorsthat supports embryonic development through phagocytosis by trophoblast cells.
Vascular adaptations in the decidua are critical for optimizing exchange efficiency, particularly through the remodeling of spiral arteries. During the first trimester, these arteries undergo dilation and loss of their muscular and elastic layers, transforming into wide, low-resistance conduits that deliver high-volume maternal blood flowup to 500700 mL/min by termdirectly into the intervillous space, ensuring adequate perfusion without turbulence that could damage delicate villi. This remodeling also involves selective endothelial permeability, allowing controlled leakage of plasma components into the space while maintaining barrier integrity against excessive blood ingress.
Gas exchange dynamics rely on a steep oxygen gradient established across the placental barrier, with maternal arterial blood (PO 100 mmHg) releasing oxygen into the intervillous space and fetal capillaries (PO 2030 mmHg) absorbing it via simple diffusion, achieving transfer rates of approximately 67 mL O/min/kg fetal weight in late gestation. To prevent hypoxia, particularly during rapid fetal growth phases, vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) secreted by decidual cells and trophoblasts promotes angiogenesis, expanding the capillary network in the chorionic villi and enhancing overall oxygen delivery capacity.
In early pregnancy, nutrition shifts from histiotrophic reliance on endometrial secretions to hemotrophic nutrition via maternal blood after approximately 1012 weeks, when trophoblast plugs dissolve and spiral artery flow commences, markedly increasing nutrient flux to support fetal organogenesis. Decidual cells contribute to this transition by upregulating glucose transporter 1 (GLUT1), which facilitates increased glucose uptake and transport during decidualization, ensuring a steady supply of this key energy substrate. This adaptive change aligns with the exponential rise in fetal metabolic demands, maintaining euglycemia across the interface.

Endocrine Secretion

The decidua functions as a major endocrine organ during pregnancy, synthesizing and releasing a variety of hormones that support implantation, placental development, and maternal adaptations. Among these, local prolactin represents the highest extrapituitary source, primarily produced by decidual stromal cells under progesterone influence, where it acts in an autocrine and paracrine manner to promote cell survival and differentiation without significantly entering maternal or fetal circulation. Relaxin, another key hormone secreted by decidual cells, facilitates tissue remodeling and vascular adaptations essential for early pregnancy establishment. Cortisol secretion from decidual tissue contributes to local stress responses and metabolic regulation, often in synergy with placental hormones like human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) and progesterone. Additionally, the decidua modulates hCG levels through inhibitory factors, such as proteins released by decidual cells that suppress trophoblast hCG production by up to 75% in coculture models, thereby fine-tuning embryonic signaling.
Decidual cells also produce critical growth factors and cytokines that regulate angiogenesis, implantation, and maternal metabolism. Insulin-like growth factors I and II (IGF-I/II), along with their binding proteins, are secreted by decidualized stromal cells and play a pivotal role in modulating maternal nutrient partitioning and fetal growth by enhancing insulin sensitivity and glucose uptake in maternal tissues. Vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), particularly VEGF-A, is prominently secreted by progesterone receptor-expressing decidual stromal cells to drive uterine angiogenesis and vascular permeability, ensuring adequate blood supply to the implantation site. Leukemia inhibitory factor (LIF), produced by decidual leukocytes and stromal cells, supports blastocyst implantation by promoting trophoblast adhesion and invasion through epithelial remodeling in the endometrium.
Secretory activity in the decidua operates primarily through paracrine and autocrine loops, where hormones and factors like prolactin and IGFs reinforce local cellular transformations and interactions with trophoblasts. Production peaks during the first trimester, coinciding with intense decidualization and implantation demands, as evidenced by elevated prolactin and VEGF levels that facilitate early vascularization. Toward term, secretory output declines sharply due to progesterone withdrawal, triggering decidual regression and labor initiation as part of the parturition process.
Decidual prolactin serves as a reliable biomarker for assessing endometrial receptivity in in vitro fertilization (IVF) protocols, with its secretion in decidualization assaysoften measured alongside IGF-binding protein-1indicating successful stromal cell differentiation and implantation potential in response to progesterone exposure.

Immunology and Immune Tolerance

Mechanisms of Tolerance

The decidua establishes maternal immune tolerance to the semi-allogeneic fetus through multiple interconnected mechanisms that suppress cytotoxic responses and promote regulatory pathways at the maternal-fetal interface. A primary strategy involves the expression of non-classical human leukocyte antigen G (HLA-G) by extravillous trophoblast cells, which binds to inhibitory receptors such as LILRB1 (on natural killer (NK) cells, T cells, and other immune cells) and LILRB2 (on myeloid cells like macrophages and dendritic cells), thereby inhibiting their activation and cytotoxic activity. Additionally, Fas ligand (FasL) expressed on trophoblast surfaces induces apoptosis in activated maternal lymphocytes via the Fas/FasL pathway, selectively eliminating potentially harmful immune effectors while sparing regulatory cells. Complementing these, decidual cells upregulate indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO), which catalyzes tryptophan depletion in the local microenvironment, starving T cells of this essential amino acid and arresting their proliferation to enforce tolerance.
Physical and biochemical barriers further reinforce this tolerance by limiting direct immune contact and shaping the cytokine environment. Fibrinoid layers, including Nitabuch's layer at the interface between trophoblast and decidua basalis and Rohr's layer beneath anchoring villi, form acellular deposits of extracellular matrix and fibrin that physically separate maternal leukocytes from fetal tissues, reducing opportunities for allorecognition. Concurrently, the decidua maintains a dominant regulatory cytokine milieu, characterized by elevated levels of transforming growth factor- (TGF-) and interleukin-10 (IL-10), which suppress pro-inflammatory Th1 responses and promote differentiation of tolerogenic immune subsets.
Decidual tolerance extends systemically, with signals from the interface modulating peripheral immune responses to sustain pregnancy. Soluble factors such as HLA-G isoforms and IDO metabolites circulate to induce peripheral regulatory T cells and dampen systemic inflammation, contributing to broader immunosuppression. This integration also mitigates autoimmune flares during gestation, as decidual-derived TGF- and IL-10 counteract autoreactive lymphocyte activation, protecting both maternal and fetal health in autoimmune-prone individuals.
Recent advances in single-cell and spatial transcriptomics have illuminated gradients of tolerance factors across the decidua, revealing heterogeneous expression patterns of factors such as HLA-G, IDO, and regulatory cytokines that intensify near the trophoblast interface to create zoned immunosuppression. These studies highlight dynamic transcriptional networks, such as enriched CCR8+ regulatory T cell signatures, that fine-tune tolerance and offer insights into disruptions in pathological pregnancies.

Roles of Decidual Immune Cells

Uterine natural killer (uNK) cells represent the most abundant leukocyte population in the decidua during early pregnancy, constituting approximately 70% of decidual leukocytes in the first trimester. These cells promote angiogenesis by secreting interferon-gamma (IFN-), which induces vascular permeability and remodeling, and XCL1, a chemokine that enhances endothelial cell recruitment and trophoblast motility. Through these mechanisms, uNK cells facilitate the transformation of spiral arteries, ensuring adequate blood supply to the developing placenta.
Decidual macrophages, comprising 2030% of leukocytes in the first trimester, contribute to tissue homeostasis by phagocytosing apoptotic trophoblast debris and extracellular matrix remnants, which prevents excessive inflammation and supports placentation. These cells predominantly adopt an M2-like polarization state, characterized by expression of CD206 and CD163, enabling them to secrete anti-inflammatory mediators and foster a tolerogenic environment that protects the semiallogeneic fetus.
Regulatory T cells (Tregs), particularly FoxP3-expressing CD4+ subsets, actively suppress effector T cell proliferation and cytokine production in the decidua, thereby mitigating maternal immune responses against paternal antigens derived from the fetus. Tregs achieve this through the release of immunosuppressive cytokines, including interleukin-10 (IL-10) and transforming growth factor- (TGF-), which modulate dendritic cell function and promote long-term adaptive tolerance essential for gestation.
The composition of decidual immune cells undergoes dynamic shifts throughout pregnancy, with uNK cells declining from over 70% of leukocytes in the first trimester to approximately 30% by the third trimester, reflecting their specialized role in early implantation. In contrast, macrophages maintain stable or relatively increased proportions into mid-gestation, supporting ongoing remodeling, while single-cell RNA sequencing analyses have identified functional subsets within these populations, such as distinct uNK clades (dNK13) and M1/M2 macrophage variants, highlighting their evolving contributions to maternal-fetal interactions.

Clinical Significance

Normal Variations and Diagnostics

The thickness of the decidua basalis exhibits normal variations with gestational age during the first trimester of pregnancy. In healthy pregnancies, ultrasonographic measurements reveal a mean decidual thickness of 6.8 mm at 6 weeks, decreasing slightly to 5.7 mm at 7 weeks, 5.5 mm at 8 weeks, and 6.1 mm at 9 weeks, with an overall mild negative correlation to advancing gestational age ( = -0.207, P = 0.006). A distinct decidual layer becomes consistently visible sonographically from 5-6 weeks, reaching peak thickness at 6-7 weeks before thinning and becoming inconsistently detectable toward the end of the trimester. These changes reflect the dynamic expansion of the decidua basalis to support implantation and early placentation.
Maternal factors such as parity and age also influence decidual characteristics. Decidual tissue from multigravida women displays a more activated and immunoregulatory immune phenotype compared to that from primigravida women, potentially enhancing tolerance in subsequent pregnancies. In women of advanced maternal age, decidualization exhibits blunted hormonal responsiveness to progesterone and estrogen, resulting in reduced stromal cell proliferation. Population studies highlight ethnic variations in related processes, with placental efficiencyclosely tied to decidual supporthighest in Caucasian women (7.1 1.2) and lowest in Afro-Caribbean women (6.5 0.9), suggesting potential differences in decidualization efficiency across groups.
Non-invasive and invasive diagnostics assess decidual health and receptivity in normal pregnancies. Transvaginal ultrasound evaluates decidual thickness and reaction, providing reference ranges to confirm normal development from early gestation. Endometrial biopsy assesses receptivity by staining for prolactin, a key marker of decidual differentiation, as prolactin production by endometrial explants correlates directly with the extent of decidual transformation in the secretory phase. Serum measurement of placental protein 14 (PP14), a decidual secretory product, serves as a biomarker for endometrial function, with detectable levels indicating adequate decidualization and potential for implantation.
Postpartum, the decidua undergoes normal regression through shedding as lochia, beginning immediately after placental delivery and continuing for the first few days. Full endometrial restoration, including regeneration of the basal layer, typically occurs within about 3 weeks, driven by falling progesterone levels that trigger involution without complications in uncomplicated cases.

Pathological Conditions and Disorders

Pathological conditions affecting the decidua often arise from inflammatory processes, impaired differentiation, or abnormal interactions with trophoblast cells, leading to adverse pregnancy outcomes such as preterm birth, miscarriage, and hemorrhage. Chronic deciduitis, characterized by plasma cell infiltration in the placental basal plate, is a key decidual pathology associated with recurrent miscarriage, preterm labor, and preterm prelabor rupture of membranes. This condition involves maternal immune cell influx, including lymphocytes and plasma cells, which disrupts the decidual-placental interface and correlates with spontaneous preterm birth, particularly when plasma cell counts are elevated in the second trimester. Histologically, brisk plasma cell infiltration distinguishes chronic deciduitis from acute lesions, contributing to fetal growth restriction and long-term neurodevelopmental risks in offspring.
Defects in decidualization, the progesterone-driven transformation of endometrial stromal cells, are implicated in recurrent miscarriage and infertility. In women with recurrent pregnancy loss, endometrial biopsies frequently reveal impaired decidualization, marked by reduced stromal cell responsiveness and pro-senescent gene expression profiles that compromise feto-maternal tolerance. These defects lead to inadequate placentation and increased miscarriage risk, independent of maternal age, as stalled decidual reactions erode the implantation site post-loss. In infertility cases, up to 62.5% of patients show decidualization failure on biopsy, correlating with higher miscarriage rates and poor endometrial receptivity. Insufficient decidualization also underlies broader complications like intrauterine growth restriction and early preterm birth by failing to support trophoblast invasion.
The decidua plays a central role in preeclampsia through defective trophoblast invasion and resultant hypoxia. Shallow cytotrophoblast penetration into the decidua limits spiral artery remodeling, causing placental ischemia and endothelial dysfunction that manifest as maternal hypertension and proteinuria. This decidual resistance to invasion fosters a hypoxic environment, exacerbating systemic inflammation and fetal growth issues. Quantitative analyses confirm reduced trophoblast depth in preeclamptic decidua compared to normal pregnancies, linking the pathology directly to adverse outcomes.
In ectopic pregnancy, where implantation occurs outside the uterus, the decidual reaction in the endometrium becomes exaggerated and asynchronous with trophoblast development, often leading to bleeding from decidual sloughing. This abnormal decidual response mimics intrauterine pregnancy on imaging but contributes to tubal rupture and hemorrhage if undiagnosed.
Molar pregnancies involve excessive trophoblastic proliferation that overwhelms the decidua, resulting in hydropic villi and separation from the endometrial lining, which triggers vaginal bleeding and uterine distension. Histopathologically, complete moles show circumferential trophoblast hyperplasia with minimal decidual involvement, while partial moles exhibit focal decidual changes alongside abnormal villi, increasing risks of persistent gestational trophoblastic disease. Activated decidual T lymphocytes in molar tissue further indicate immune dysregulation at the interface.
Recent single-cell RNA sequencing studies of the decidua have illuminated immune dysregulation in gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM), revealing altered maternal-fetal interface dynamics that promote inflammation and impaired tolerance. These analyses highlight shifts in decidual immune cell subsets, such as increased pro-inflammatory macrophages and T cells, linking hyperglycemia to endothelial dysfunction and adverse outcomes like macrosomia. Transcriptomic profiling in GDM placentas and decidua confirms heterogeneous cellular responses, with immune maladaptation exacerbating metabolic stress. Interventions like progesterone supplementation provide luteal support to enhance decidualization, improving implantation rates and reducing miscarriage in assisted reproduction by stabilizing the endometrial environment. Such therapy during the luteal phase boosts live birth rates in frozen embryo transfers by up to 10-15%, underscoring its role in overcoming decidual defects.
Histopathologically, ectopic decidua in endometriosis arises from progesterone-induced stromal transformation in extrauterine endometrial implants, forming nodular lesions that mimic malignancy but remain benign. These deciduosis foci, often peritoneal, show well-circumscribed decidual cells without glands, potentially causing pain or obstruction during pregnancy. In post-term pregnancies, retained decidual tissue contributes to secondary postpartum hemorrhage by preventing uterine involution and promoting infection or subinvolution. This retention, part of broader products of conception, affects 1-2% of deliveries and necessitates evacuation to avert severe bleeding.

History and Terminology

Etymology

The term "decidua" derives from the Latin adjective deciduus, meaning "falling off" or "shedding," which itself stems from the verb decidere, signifying "to fall down" or "to fall off." This etymology aptly captures the tissue's key characteristic: its postpartum expulsion from the uterus as part of the afterbirth process.
The word entered medical nomenclature in the 18th century, specifically within obstetrics, to denote the specialized, transient uterine lining that develops during pregnancy and is subsequently shed, with the earliest recorded use in 1772 by Charles White. It was further detailed by the Scottish anatomist William Hunter (17181783) in his seminal work Anatomia uteri humani gravidi tabulis illustrata (1774), where he described it as the "shedding membrane" to distinguish its temporary nature from the persistent structure of the non-pregnant uterus.
In terminology, "decidua" contrasts with "endometrium," the latter referring to the uterus's permanent mucosal layer that undergoes cyclic regeneration outside of gestation, whereas the decidua represents its pregnancy-induced, modifiable form destined for elimination after delivery.

Historical Discovery

The concept of the decidua emerged from early anatomical observations of the uterus during pregnancy. In 1672, Regnier de Graaf, in his treatise De Mulierum Organis Generationi Inservientibus, described changes in the uterine lining associated with ovarian cycles and pregnancy, noting increased vascularity and thickening of the endometrium in pregnant animals, which hinted at the tissue's role in gestation. These findings linked ovarian activity to endometrial transformation, though de Graaf did not yet identify the tissue as distinct. Building on this, William Hunter provided a more detailed description in 1774 with his illustrated atlas Anatomia Uteri Humani Gravidi Tabulis Illustrata, where he coined the term "membrana decidua" for the temporary uterine membrane that lines the gravid uterus and is shed postpartum, distinguishing its parietal and capsular portions based on dissections of human specimens.
Advancements in the 19th century refined understanding of implantation and structure through embryological and histological studies. Karl Ernst von Baer, in his seminal 1827 work De Ovi Mammalium et Hominis Genesi, first detailed the mammalian ovum's journey and its implantation into the uterine wall, describing how the blastocyst embeds within the transformed endometrium to initiate pregnancy. Concurrently, microscopic techniques enabled histologists like Theodor Langhans to identify the cellular layers of the decidua in the 1870s, revealing its stratified compositionincluding decidual cells derived from stromal elementsand its proximity to placental structures.
The 20th century brought hormonal and immunological insights into decidualization. In 1929, George W. Corner and Willard M. Allen isolated active extracts from sow corpora lutea, demonstrating their induction of progestational endometrial proliferation in rabbitsa process now recognized as decidualizationthus establishing progesterone's essential role in preparing the uterus for implantation. Beginning in the 1980s, studies by researchers such as Judith N. Bulmer used immunohistochemistry to characterize decidual leukocytes, including natural killer cells and macrophages, highlighting their contributions to immune tolerance at the maternal-fetal interface and preventing rejection of the semi-allogeneic fetus.
In the modern era, high-resolution genomic techniques have unveiled the decidua's dynamic cellular landscape. A 2023 study by Vento-Tormo et al., published in Nature, employed single-cell spatial transcriptomics on first-trimester samples to map interactions between decidual stromal cells, immune populations, and extravillous trophoblasts, revealing spatiotemporal changes that support vascular remodeling and tolerance.
