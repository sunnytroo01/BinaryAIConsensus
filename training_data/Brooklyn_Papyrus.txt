Brooklyn Papyrus
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Brooklyn Papyrus (accession numbers 47.218.48 and 47.218.85), also known as the Brooklyn Medical Papyrus, is an ancient Egyptian medical treatise housed in the Brooklyn Museum that focuses exclusively on the identification and treatment of snakebites, blending empirical remedies with magico-religious rituals as a handbook for specialized priests.
Dating to the Late Period of ancient Egypt (approximately 664332 BCE), the papyrus represents a copy of an earlier composition, likely originating from traditions in the New Kingdom or earlier, and was written in hieratic script on papyrus sheets totaling around 175 cm in length when unrolled. Acquired by Egyptologist Charles Edwin Wilbour in the late 19th century during his expeditions in Egypt, it entered the Brooklyn Museum's collection in 1947 through a bequest from his daughter, Theodora Wilbour, as part of the museum's renowned Wilbour Collection of Egyptian antiquities.
The document is structured in two main parts: the first catalogs approximately 37 species of venomous snakes, describing their physical characteristics, habitats, and the specific symptoms caused by their bites to aid in accurate identificationa practice that parallels modern herpetological and toxonomic approaches. The second part provides detailed treatments for around 140 different bites, prescribing mixtures of natural ingredients such as onions, honey, natron, and animal fats, often applied topically or ingested, alongside incantations invoking deities like Serqet (the scorpion goddess associated with protection from venom) and Thoth (god of wisdom and healing). These remedies reflect the ancient Egyptian worldview, where medicine, magic, and religion were inseparably intertwined, with priests of Serqet serving as the primary healers for venomous injuries in a society where snakes posed a significant threat to daily life.
First fully translated into French by Egyptologist Serge Sauneron in the 1960s and published posthumously in 1989 as Un trait gyptien d'ophiologie, the papyrus offers invaluable insights into ancient Egyptian ophiology (the study of snakes) and pharmacology, demonstrating sophisticated knowledge of toxicology and regional biodiversity that remains relevant to contemporary studies of ethnomedicine. Its significance extends to highlighting the role of specialized guilds in Egyptian society and the cultural emphasis on preventive and curative rituals, though the exact provenance remains unknown due to the antiquities market's practices at the time of acquisition. Not currently on public display, the fragile manuscript is preserved in sections under glass, underscoring its status as a key artifact for understanding the evolution of medical texts in the ancient world.

Manuscript Description

Physical Characteristics

The Brooklyn Papyrus is composed of papyrus material formed into a single scroll, measuring approximately 175 cm in length and 27 cm in width overall. It bears inscriptions in hieratic script primarily on the recto side and consists of 12 sheets joined together. The artifact is divided into two principal fragments due to separation that occurred while it was still rolled: the upper portion under accession number 47.218.48 and the lower under 47.218.85, each measuring about 66.5 cm by 27.5 cm.
The papyrus exists in a fragmentary state, comprising over 100 piecesmostly small fragmentswith significant portions lost to damage, rendering it incomplete. Its condition reflects the broader poor preservation of the Brooklyn Museum's papyrus collection, attributable to advanced age, prior improper conservation treatments, and inadequate historical housing practices that exacerbated deterioration. The scroll was unrolled, conserved, and framed in 1967 (and again in 1969) by Egyptologist Serge Sauneron to stabilize its fragile structure.
Housed permanently in the Brooklyn Museum in New York as part of the Charles Edwin Wilbour Collection (acquired via bequest in 1947), the papyrus is not available for public display, likely due to its delicate state.

Dating and Condition

The Brooklyn Papyrus, accession numbers 47.218.48 and 47.218.85 at the Brooklyn Museum, is paleographically dated to the Late Period of ancient Egypt, specifically the second half of Dynasty 26 (ca. 589525 BCE), based on analysis of its hieratic script. This places the physical manuscript within the broader Late Period (664332 BCE), during which such medical treatises were copied and preserved by temple scribes. However, the linguistic and stylistic features of the text indicate that it represents a later copy of an earlier composition, with characteristics possibly originating as early as Dynasty 6 (ca. 2200 BCE).
Scholars attribute a possible origin to the temple complex at Heliopolis near Cairo, drawing on the script's formal qualities and the papyrus's focus on ophiological knowledge linked to priestly cults in that region. Alternative suggestions point to Thebes as a potential scribal center, given the content's ties to Serqet worship and medical practices in Upper Egypt, though definitive provenance remains elusive.
The papyrus's condition reflects typical challenges for Late Period documents: extensive fragmentation into multiple sections, lacunae affecting readability, and deterioration from natural aging, including ink flaking and papyrus brittleness. After collector Charles Edwin Wilbour's death in 1896, it was stored improperly alongside other artifacts in a biscuit box, exacerbating damage from environmental exposure, mold, insects, and mechanical stress from its rolled state until the bequest in 1947. Conservation efforts commenced in the 1960s under Egyptologist Serge Sauneron, who unrolled and mounted the fragments; subsequent work at the Brooklyn Museum in the 1990s and beyond involved consolidation with traditional adhesives like funori, removal of deleterious tapes and backings, and aqueous treatments to stabilize the material. Today, it is housed in climate-controlled conditions within UV-filtered Plexiglas enclosures with supportive ragboard inlays to minimize further degradation.
Notable gaps persist in the archaeological record, including the exact date and circumstances of discovery, which were not documented when collector Charles Edwin Wilbour acquired the fragments in Egypt prior to 1947. This incompleteness highlights broader challenges in tracing the transmission of ancient Egyptian medical texts from temple libraries to modern collections.

Contents

Ophiological Section

The ophiological section, comprising Part One of the Brooklyn Papyrus, consists of a structured catalog of 38 reptiles, with the first 14 registers lost, leaving 24 extant descriptions (23 snakes and one chameleon). Each entry follows a consistent format, detailing the reptile's physical appearance, behavioral traits, habitat preferences, effects of its bite, and an associated protective deity invoked for ritual purposes. These descriptions emphasize observable characteristics to aid in rapid identification, such as coloration, scale patterns, head shape, and vocalizations, alongside symptomatic outcomes like swelling, paralysis, or rapid fatality.
The catalog's primary purpose is to serve as a diagnostic manual for ancient Egyptian priests and healers, particularly those affiliated with the goddess Serqet, enabling them to distinguish snake species through visual cues and post-bite symptoms for subsequent treatment selection. This systematic approach reflects a blend of empirical observation and ritual knowledge, prioritizing traits like seasonal activity or regional distributionsuch as snakes active in the Nile Delta versus desert regionsto contextualize encounters in everyday or ceremonial settings.
Prominent examples illustrate the catalog's depth and variety. The Great Serpent Aapep, described in register 15 as entirely red with a white belly and four fangs, causes instantaneous death upon biting and is linked to the chaos deity Apophis, embodying cosmic disorder in Egyptian cosmology. In contrast, the gany snake (register 16) appears entirely black with a broad muzzle and similarly fatal bite, associated with the crocodile god Sobek, suggesting a manifestation of divine ferocity in reptilian form. Among the remaining entries, such as the blower viper (register 27), which emits a shrill noise when threatened and inflicts prolonged fever, or the Asiatic viper (register 22) with its reddish hues tied to Horus, unique attributes like iridescent scales, nocturnal habits, or hibernation patterns highlight the Egyptians' nuanced categorization of serpentine threats.
Symbolically, the reptiles are interwoven with Egyptian mythology, portraying them as avatars of gods or demons that demand ritual appeasement. Protective figures like Serqet, invoked against venomous perils, contrast with antagonistic entities such as Apophis, underscoring snakes' dual role as harbingers of danger and conduits for divine power in funerary and healing rites. This mythological framing elevates the catalog beyond mere taxonomy, positioning reptile identification as a sacred act to restore cosmic balance.

Treatment Section

The second part of the Brooklyn Papyrus, known as the treatment section, provides detailed remedial prescriptions primarily for bites from the 24 preserved descriptions in the preceding ophiological register, along with additional remedies for scorpion stings and spider bites. This section encompasses around 140 treatments, including numerous spells, herbal poultices, and basic surgical instructions, structured as a practical handbook for non-specialist healers, such as priests of the goddess Serqet, who administered emergency care in ancient Egyptian communities. The remedies follow a consistent pattern: identification of the venom source or symptoms, listing of ingredients, preparation methods, application instructions, and expected outcomes, reflecting a blend of empirical observation and ritual practice aimed at rapid intervention.
Treatment methods integrate incantations that invoke protective deities, plant-based antidotes, and protective amulets, tailored to counteract venom effects like swelling, paralysis, elevated heart rate, and respiratory distress. Incantations often call upon gods such as Serqet for scorpion stings, where spells direct the practitioner to draw images of Ptah, Isis, and Serqet on papyrus and recite phrases to expel the poison, emphasizing the scorpion's submission to divine authority. For severe snake bites, such as those from the Aapep (a red serpent associated with chaos), rituals involve chants invoking Min and Horus to ritually expel the venom, sometimes accompanied by fumigation or the application of amulets made from materials like green porphyry for ongoing protection. Plant-based antidotes frequently feature onions ground in beer as emetics to induce vomiting and purge the venom, or mixtures of acacia, honey, and oils applied as poultices to reduce inflammation; these are derived from accessible local flora believed to possess inherent neutralizing properties linked to deities like Isis. Surgical elements include the "knife treatment," involving incisions near the bite to draw out poison, combined with topical applications for milder cases without accompanying spells.
The scope of these treatments underscores an emphasis on emergency response, prioritizing immediate symptom reliefsuch as addressing limb paralysis through targeted poultices or systemic poisoning via ingested herbal decoctions like roots of the "wood-of-the-snake" plant in wineto prevent fatality in a context where snakebites posed a common hazard. For less venomous snakes, simpler topical remedies suffice, such as crushed watermelon and onion applied to the forehead, demonstrating a graduated approach based on observed severity rather than uniform ritual intensity. Overall, the prescriptions reveal ancient Egyptian healers' empirical knowledge of venom symptoms, integrated with magico-religious elements to empower non-elite practitioners in rural or domestic settings.

Historical Background

Discovery and Acquisition

The Brooklyn Papyrus, a Late Period Egyptian medical treatise focused on ophiology and treatments for venomous bites, likely functioned as a specialized handbook for the kherep priests of Serqet, the scorpion goddess linked to healing and protection against poisons, within temple or ritual contexts. Following its active use, the document was probably archived, buried, or concealed in a sacred site, consistent with the disposal practices for religious and medical texts in ancient Egypt. However, its precise discovery location and circumstances remain undocumented, with evidence suggesting it surfaced on the late 19th-century Egyptian antiquities market amid widespread unregulated excavations and trade during British colonial influence, which often involved looting from archaeological sites.
Around 1889, during one of his annual winter expeditions up the Nile, American Egyptologist and collector Charles Edwin Wilbour (18331896) acquired the fragmented papyrus from local dealers in Egypt, adding it to his extensive personal assemblage of ancient artifacts. Wilbour, a pioneering figure in American Egyptology, amassed thousands of items through direct purchases in the antiquities trade, reflecting the era's lax export regulations. The papyrus remained in his private collection until after his death, when his daughter, Theodora Wilbour, bequeathed it to the Brooklyn Museum in 1947, where it received the accession numbers 47.218.48 and 47.218.85.
In Wilbour's collection, the papyrus underwent initial handling typical of the time, including basic stabilization efforts to preserve its fragile state, though specific details from this period are sparse. Upon transfer to the museum, it was stored in wooden drawers under uncontrolled environmental conditions and mounted between glass sheets sealed with adhesive tapea common but ultimately harmful 20th-century conservation method that caused further deterioration through acidity and pressure. Museum records document these early interventions, which were later addressed in a comprehensive 1990 review by the paper conservation department, involving removal of tapes, consolidation, and rehousing in climate-controlled, acid-free enclosures.
Provenance gaps persist, with no verified excavation records or pre-acquisition ownership history, underscoring ethical concerns over colonial-era artifact trafficking and the potential displacement of cultural heritage from Egypt without consent. While the Brooklyn Museum has committed to ongoing provenance research, recent discussions in museum ethics highlight the papyrus as emblematic of broader repatriation debates for unprovenanced Egyptian holdings, advocating for transparency and possible return to source countries.

Publication History

The Brooklyn Papyrus (inventory numbers 47.218.48 and 47.218.85) entered the collection of the Brooklyn Museum as part of the Charles Edwin Wilbour bequest, with the papyri formally acquired and cataloged in the late 1940s following earlier provisional access in the 1930s. Initial scholarly examination was limited due to the papyrus's fragmentary condition, consisting of twelve sheets with numerous gaps, which delayed comprehensive study and publication. Photographic documentation began in the mid-20th century to facilitate analysis, though full high-resolution imaging awaited later conservation efforts.
Partial transcriptions and preliminary studies emerged in the 1970s, often focusing on specific sections amid ongoing efforts to reassemble fragments, but a complete edition remained elusive until the late 1980s. The definitive scholarly publication came posthumously in 1989 with Serge Sauneron's Un trait gyptien d'ophiologie: Papyrus du Brooklyn Museum no. 47.218.48 et .85, which provided a full hieratic transcription, French translation, and detailed commentary on the ophiological and therapeutic content. This work, prepared during Sauneron's studies in the 1960s at the Institut franais d'archologie orientale, addressed the papyrus's challenges, including lacunae and variant readings, establishing it as the primary reference for subsequent research.
In the ensuing decades, English-language scholarship expanded access to the text. The first complete English translation appeared in a 2020 PhD thesis by Wendy R. J. Golding, which rendered the full content alongside comparative analysis of ancient snakebite literature. Additional translations and commentaries followed in specialized works, such as a 2023 examination of the papyrus's magico-medical elements by Wendy R. J. Golding and a 2024 study on snake identification by Lidija McGrath, further elucidating its structure. Digital facsimiles and high-resolution images became available through the Brooklyn Museum's online collection portal in the early 2000s, enabling broader scholarly and public access despite persistent fragmentation issues. These resources have supported ongoing conservation and digitization projects, mitigating earlier delays caused by the papyrus's physical deterioration and incomplete state.

Scholarly Analysis

Snake Identification

Contemporary scholars have employed ecological niche modeling and herpetological analyses to match the ancient descriptions in the Brooklyn Papyrus with modern snake species, focusing on habitat suitability, morphological traits, and venom effects during the Late Period (c. 660330 BCE). A seminal 2023 study by McBride, Winder, and Wster utilized maximum entropy modeling to predict paleodistributions for ten proposed identifications, incorporating mid-Holocene environmental data and current occurrence records to assess viability in ancient Egyptian landscapes. This approach revealed that nine of the tested species had suitable ranges encompassing ancient Egypt, highlighting a richer venomous snake biodiversity than today due to a wetter climate. Complementing this, Sanchez et al.'s 2024 interdisciplinary analysis of the papyrus's 24 preserved snake registers integrates Egyptology, linguistics, and biogeography to propose identifications based on descriptive details like color, habits, and bite symptoms.
Key findings link approximately 24 of the papyrus's described snakes to specific taxa, with notable matches including the "gany" snake to the black desert cobra (Walterinnesia aegyptia), characterized by its small head and broad muzzle, and several entries to the Egyptian cobra (Naja haje), whose neurotoxic venom aligns with reported rapid paralysis and respiratory failure. Other identifications encompass the puff adder (Bitis arietans), identified via its quail-like patterning and hissing sound, and now-extinct or regionally absent species like the boomslang (Dispholidus typus), proposed for the rare four-fanged "great snake of Apophis" based on its lethal, hemorrhagic effects. These matches confirm that ancient Egyptians encountered a diverse array of vipers, elapids, and colubrids, with four speciessuch as Bitis arietans and Macrovipera lebetinapredicted to have had suitable habitats overlapping modern Egypt in antiquity, though not all persist there today.
Challenges in identification arise from metaphorical language in the text, such as references to "fiery breath," which may symbolize venom projection rather than literal traits, and from regional extinctions driven by post-Holocene aridification, complicating direct correlations with current distributions. For instance, species like Daboia palaestinae occupied isolated habitat patches in antiquity but are absent today, while lost descriptions for 13 of the 37 listed snakes limit comprehensive mapping. Additionally, gaps in ancient genetic data preclude definitive phylogenetic confirmations, though future paleogenomic studies could address this.
Post-2023 research, including Sanchez et al.'s work, updates earlier incomplete identifications by incorporating recent biodiversity loss models for Egypt, emphasizing how climate shifts reduced venomous snake populations from over 30 viable species in antiquity to fewer than ten today, thus refining our understanding of the papyrus's ophiological catalog.

Medical and Cultural Interpretations

The Brooklyn Papyrus integrates magic, empiricism, and religion in its approach to healing snakebites, reflecting a holistic system where physical remedies are augmented by spiritual interventions to address both bodily affliction and supernatural causation. Treatments typically begin with diagnostic observations of symptoms, followed by the application of herbal, animal, and mineral substances, such as onions for their anti-inflammatory effects and honey for antibacterial properties, demonstrating an empirical foundation in practical pharmacology. Incantations and spells, often recited by specialized priests, invoke divine forces to neutralize venom, underscoring the religious dimension of Egyptian medicine where healing was seen as a restoration of cosmic balance.
This priestly medicine is closely tied to the cult of Serqet, the scorpion goddess associated with protection against venomous creatures, with the papyrus serving as a handbook for her priests who performed rituals blending therapeutic applications and mythological narratives. Deities like Isis, Horus, Ptah, and Thoth frequently appear in invocations, linking treatments to broader religious frameworks where Isis's role as a healerexemplified in myths of protecting Horus from scorpion stingsmirrors the papyrus's emphasis on maternal and protective magic. In a snake-prevalent environment along the Nile, ophiology held critical cultural importance, as venomous bites posed constant threats to daily life, agriculture, and ritual practices, positioning such knowledge as essential for societal survival and religious piety.
The papyrus's content influenced later Greco-Roman ophiological traditions, as seen in parallels with Nicander's Theriaca, where Egyptian-inspired identifications of snakes like the horned viper and Egyptian cobra appear alongside adapted remedies, indicating a transmission of knowledge through Hellenistic channels. Modern scholarship views the text as offering valuable insights into ancient toxicology, revealing how remedies like emetics and poultices provided symptomatic reliefsuch as reducing swellingdespite lacking true antivenom, while the magical elements served psychological and communal functions in crisis response.
Recent studies from the 2020s have deepened understandings of magico-medical syncretism in the papyrus, emphasizing the Serqet priests' role in fusing ritual efficacy with botanical efficacy, as in the symbolic and therapeutic use of plant roots and barks to "expel" poison. These analyses highlight how the document fills gaps in the religious integration of healing, portraying medicine not as isolated empiricism but as a sacred duty intertwined with divine agency.
The papyrus's significance lies in its compilation during the Late Period (c. 664332 BCE) as a copy of an earlier original, likely drawing from New Kingdom (c. 15501070 BCE) or earlier knowledge traditions, thus illustrating the evolution and preservation of ophiological expertise across dynasties amid changing religious and environmental contexts.
