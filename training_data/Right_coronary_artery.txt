Right coronary artery
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The right coronary artery (RCA) is a major coronary vessel that originates from the right aortic sinus of the ascending aorta and supplies oxygenated blood to the right atrium, right ventricle, portions of the left atrium and left ventricle, the posterior third of the interventricular septum, and the atrioventricular (AV) node. It arises superior to the right coronary semilunar cusp of the aortic valve and follows an initial course in the right atrioventricular (coronary) sulcus, passing between the right atrium and pulmonary trunk before curving posteriorly around the heart's acute margin toward the crux of the heart. This artery plays a critical role in myocardial perfusion, ensuring adequate oxygen delivery to support the heart's pumping function, particularly on the right side and inferior aspects.
Key branches of the RCA include the sinoatrial nodal artery, which supplies the sinoatrial node in approximately 60% of individuals; the conus artery, providing blood to the infundibulum of the right ventricle; the right marginal artery, which runs along the right ventricle's acute margin to nourish its anterior and lateral walls; the atrioventricular nodal artery at the crux; the posterior descending artery (in right-dominant circulations); and posterolateral branches that extend to the posterior left ventricle. In about 70-80% of people, the RCA is right-dominant, meaning it gives rise to the posterior descending artery that supplies the inferior interventricular septum and posteromedial papillary muscle, while left-dominant (10%) or co-dominant (20%) variations occur when this branch arises from the left circumflex artery.
Clinically, the RCA is vital for maintaining cardiac conduction and contractility; occlusion or stenosis can lead to inferior or posterior myocardial infarction, right ventricular infarction, AV block, or sinus node dysfunction due to its supply to key nodal tissues. It is frequently involved in coronary artery bypass grafting and is assessed via angiography for diseases like atherosclerosis, which affects its patency and overall heart function.

Anatomy

Origin and Course

The right coronary artery (RCA) originates from the right aortic sinus of Valsalva, positioned just above the right cusp of the aortic valve, as one of the two primary coronary arteries branching from the aortic root. This ostium typically emerges anteriorly from the ascending aorta, allowing the artery to supply oxygenated blood to the right side of the heart.
From its origin, the RCA follows an initial course within the right atrioventricular groove (also known as the coronary sulcus), traveling posteriorly along the epicardial surface between the right atrium and right ventricle. It curves around the heart's right border, passing the acute margin and continuing toward the posterior aspect, ultimately reaching the crux cordisthe junction of the atrioventricular and interventricular grooves on the diaphragmatic surface. In right-dominant hearts, which constitute approximately 85% of individuals, the RCA continues beyond the crux as the posterior descending artery; otherwise, it may terminate near this point.
The RCA is conventionally divided into three segments: the proximal segment, extending from the ostium to the origin of the acute marginal branch; the middle segment, from the acute marginal branch to the posterior descending artery; and the distal segment, encompassing the terminal branches. Its typical length measures approximately 12 to 14 cm, with a proximal diameter of 3 to 4 mm that narrows distally. Throughout its path, the RCA lies adjacent to the right atrium superiorly and the right ventricle inferiorly, embedded in the atrioventricular groove's fatty tissue, which may subject it to potential compression from surrounding myocardial or connective tissues during cardiac motion.

Branches

The right coronary artery (RCA) gives rise to several major branches along its course through the right atrioventricular groove, which collectively supply portions of the right atrium, right ventricle, and parts of the left heart in right-dominant circulations. These branches originate from distinct segments of the RCA: the proximal segment near its aortic origin, the middle segment along the acute margin, and the distal segment at the crux cordis where the artery bends posteriorly.
The conus artery, often the first branch arising from the proximal RCA within millimeters of its origin, ascends anteriorly to supply the infundibulum (outflow tract) of the right ventricle; it emerges in approximately 50-60% of cases directly from the RCA, though it may occasionally arise separately from the right aortic sinus. The sinoatrial nodal artery typically originates from an atrial branch of the proximal RCA and courses superiorly along the right atrial wall toward the superior vena cava, encircling it to reach the sinoatrial node; this branch arises from the RCA in 60-68% of individuals, independent of overall coronary dominance.
From the middle segment of the RCA, the right marginal artery emerges and travels inferiorly along the acute margin of the heart, paralleling the right atrioventricular groove to provide perfusion to the lateral and inferior aspects of the right ventricle. At the distal RCA near the crux, the atrioventricular nodal artery branches off and penetrates the base of the atrial septum, directing blood to the atrioventricular node; it originates from the RCA in 82-90% of cases, predominantly in right-dominant systems.
In right-dominant circulations, which occur in about 85% of the population, the posterior descending artery (PDA) arises from the distal RCA and descends along the posterior interventricular sulcus alongside the middle cardiac vein, supplying the inferior interventricular septum and posteromedial papillary muscle. Accompanying the PDA are one to three posterolateral branches from the distal RCA, which extend leftward across the posterior left ventricular wall to nourish its inferior and posterolateral regions; co-dominance (shared PDA supply from both RCA and left circumflex) affects roughly 10% of individuals, while left dominance (PDA from left circumflex only) is seen in 5%.
Microscopically, the RCA and its branches exhibit the structure of medium-sized muscular arteries, featuring an inner tunica intima lined by endothelium over a subendothelial layer and internal elastic lamina, a tunica media composed of multiple layers of circumferentially arranged smooth muscle cells with interspersed elastic fibers, and an outer tunica adventitia of loose connective tissue containing vasa vasorum.

Anatomical Variations

Anatomical variations in the right coronary artery (RCA) encompass deviations in its dominance, origin, course, and branching patterns, which can influence myocardial perfusion territories. Coronary dominance is determined by the origin of the posterior descending artery (PDA), with right dominance being the most common configuration in approximately 85% of cases, where the PDA arises from the RCA to supply the inferior left ventricle and interventricular septum. Left dominance occurs in about 10% of individuals, with the PDA originating from the left circumflex artery, while co-dominance, involving shared supply from both the RCA and left circumflex, is seen in roughly 5%. These patterns are identified through angiographic studies, with variations in prevalence reported across populations but consistently showing right dominance as predominant.
Origin anomalies of the RCA are infrequent but significant. High takeoff, where the ostium is positioned more than 5 mm above the sinotubular junction, has an incidence of 0.3% to 1% based on coronary angiography and computed tomography (CT) data, with modern CT angiography post-2020 detecting higher rates due to improved resolution. Anomalous origin from the left sinus of Valsalva is rare, occurring in 0.03% to 0.92% of cases, while origin from the pulmonary artery is exceptionally uncommon at less than 0.1%. These variations arise during early embryonic development and can alter the vessel's proximal trajectory.
Course variations often accompany anomalous origins, particularly when the RCA arises from the left sinus. The interarterial course, passing between the aorta and pulmonary artery, represents a malignant subtype prone to compression, whereas retroaortic, prepulmonic, or intraseptal paths are typically benign. In right sinus origins, a posterior or tortuous course, such as the shepherd's crook variant, occurs in about 5% of cases and may complicate catheter engagement during procedures. Branch anomalies include a separate ostium for the conus artery in 30% to 50% of individuals, as observed in angiographic series, potentially compensating for absent right marginal branches. Other rare features encompass duplicated PDAs or ectatic (dilated) segments, with incidences below 1% in large cohorts.
The embryological basis for these variations stems from the formation of coronary buds from the proepicardial organ, located at the dorsal atrioventricular groove near the sinus venosus, during weeks 4 to 8 of gestation. Disruptions in the remodeling of this endothelial network or abnormal ingrowth into the aortic sinuses can lead to ectopic origins or aberrant courses, highlighting the intricate vasculogenic and angiogenic processes involved in RCA development. Recent CT angiography studies have refined incidence estimates for these variants, emphasizing their detection in asymptomatic populations.

Function and Supply

Territories Supplied

The right coronary artery (RCA) primarily perfuses the right atrium and the majority of the right ventricle, including its free wall and outflow tract, as well as the inferior portion of the interventricular septum. In right-dominant hearts, which constitute approximately 70-80% of the population, the RCA also supplies the posterior one-third of the left ventricle via its terminal branches. Additionally, the RCA provides blood to key components of the cardiac conduction system, including the sinoatrial (SA) node in about 60% of individuals and the atrioventricular (AV) node in about 90% of cases.
Specific branches of the RCA contribute to these perfusion territories in defined ways. The conus artery, often the first branch, supplies the infundibulum or conus arteriosus of the right ventricular outflow tract. The right marginal artery provides blood to the anterior and inferior aspects of the right ventricle, particularly its free wall. In right-dominant circulation, the posterior descending artery (PDA), a major terminal branch, perfuses the inferior third of the interventricular septum, the apex, and the AV node, while the posterolateral branches supply the posterior wall of the left ventricle.
Overall, the RCA supplies approximately 25-35% of the total myocardial mass in right-dominant hearts, encompassing the bulk of the right ventricular mass (about 25% of the heart) plus portions of the left ventricle and septum. In left-dominant hearts (about 10% of cases), this contribution is reduced, as the PDA arises from the left circumflex artery, limiting RCA perfusion to primarily the right-sided structures. Collateral networks exist, including potential anastomoses between RCA septal perforators and those from the left anterior descending artery, which can interconnect right and left coronary territories.

Physiological Role

The right coronary artery (RCA) primarily functions to deliver oxygenated blood under pulsatile flow from the aorta to the right atrium, right ventricle, and portions of the conduction system, ensuring adequate myocardial perfusion for these structures. This delivery is characterized by phasic flow that predominantly peaks during diastole, facilitated by the lower intramural pressures in the right ventricle compared to the left, which minimizes systolic compression of the intramural vessels. Unlike the left coronary system, where systolic compression is more pronounced, the RCA's flow pattern supports efficient nutrient exchange during the heart's relaxation phase.
At rest, the RCA exhibits an average blood flow rate of approximately 60-80 mL/min, reflecting the lower metabolic demands of the right heart, which accounts for about 20% of the total myocardial oxygen consumption. During exercise, this flow increases 3- to 5-fold, up to 200-400 mL/min, primarily through vasodilation of the coronary microvasculature to match heightened oxygen needs. Flow regulation occurs via autoregulation mechanisms involving local metabolites such as adenosine, which promotes vasodilation during increased demand, and nitric oxide, which maintains basal tone and responds to shear stress. Sympathetic innervation further modulates flow by enhancing -adrenergic-mediated dilation, contributing up to 25% of the exercise-induced increase, while -adrenergic effects can induce constriction under certain conditions.
The RCA plays a critical role in cardiac electrophysiology by supplying the sinoatrial (SA) node in about 60% of individuals and the atrioventricular (AV) node in over 90%, enabling rhythm generation and impulse propagation essential for coordinated atrial and ventricular contraction. This supply ensures the nodes receive sufficient oxygenation to maintain pacemaker activity and conduction integrity. The RCA's contributions interdepend with the left coronary system to achieve balanced global myocardial perfusion, with the right system's lower demand allowing for adaptive redistribution during stress, supported by potential collateral pathways.

Clinical Significance

Pathology and Disease

The primary pathology affecting the right coronary artery (RCA) is atherosclerosis, characterized by the accumulation of lipid-rich plaques within the arterial wall, leading to luminal stenosis or complete occlusion. This process underlies coronary artery disease (CAD), with RCA commonly involved in affected patients. RCA occlusion, often triggered by plaque rupture and superimposed thrombosis, is the cause of approximately 80-90% of inferior myocardial infarctions, disrupting blood supply to the inferior wall of the left ventricle and portions of the right ventricle.
In acute scenarios, thrombosis within the RCA during ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI) frequently results in right ventricular infarction, particularly when the occlusion is proximal. This leads to reduced right ventricular contractility and characteristic hemodynamic instability, including bradycardia from sinoatrial node ischemia and hypotension due to diminished preload to the left ventricle. Such events exacerbate morbidity in up to 50% of inferior MIs involving the RCA.
Chronic RCA pathology manifests as stable angina, typically arising from fixed stenoses exceeding 70% of the luminal diameter, which impairs myocardial oxygen delivery during increased demand. Anomalous RCA origins, such as from the left sinus of Valsalva, heighten arrhythmia risk through mechanisms like interarterial compression during exertion, potentially triggering ventricular tachyarrhythmias. Risk factors specific to RCA disease align with broader CAD drivers, including hypertension, diabetes mellitus, and cigarette smoking, which promote endothelial dysfunction and plaque progression.
Pathological consequences of RCA occlusion include impaired right ventricular systolic function, atrioventricular conduction block from involvement of the atrioventricular nodal artery, and progression to cardiogenic shock in approximately 3-5% of inferior MIs, driven by biventricular failure and systemic hypoperfusion. Rare conditions affecting the RCA encompass spontaneous dissection, which may occur postpartum due to hormonal influences on vascular integrity or following trauma, coronary embolism from proximal sources like atrial fibrillation, and vasculitis such as Kawasaki disease, leading to coronary aneurysms or accelerated stenosis in pediatric populations.

Diagnosis and Imaging

Coronary angiography remains the gold standard for diagnosing right coronary artery (RCA) disease, providing detailed visualization of luminal narrowing and irregularities through catheter-based injection of iodinated contrast dye, typically accessed via the femoral or radial artery. This invasive procedure allows for real-time assessment of the RCA's patency and is essential for confirming obstructive lesions prior to interventions.
Non-invasive imaging options include computed tomography coronary angiography (CTCA), which utilizes multidetector CT scanners (64-slice or higher) to detect stenoses greater than 50% in the RCA with high diagnostic accuracy, often exceeding 90% sensitivity and specificity in per-vessel analysis. Cardiac magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) complements this by evaluating myocardial perfusion and viability in RCA-supplied territories, particularly during stress protocols to identify inducible ischemia without ionizing radiation.
Echocardiography offers an indirect diagnostic approach by detecting regional wall motion abnormalities in the inferior and posterior walls, which correspond to RCA territory ischemia, with studies showing correlation in up to 73% of cases with proximal RCA stenoses. Functional assessments, such as stress electrocardiography (ECG) or nuclear perfusion imaging, further aid diagnosis by revealing RCA-specific ischemia, often manifested as ST-segment changes in the inferior leads (II, III, aVF) during exercise or pharmacologic stress. Nuclear scintigraphy can quantify perfusion defects in the inferior wall, enhancing specificity for RCA involvement.
In multivessel disease, the SYNTAX score integrates RCA lesion characteristicssuch as location, severity, and complexityto quantify overall coronary anatomy and guide revascularization decisions, with higher scores indicating greater procedural risk.
Recent advancements incorporate artificial intelligence (AI) in CTCA for enhanced plaque characterization in the RCA, where 2024 studies demonstrate improved detection of high-risk features like low-attenuation plaques and positive remodeling, achieving superior accuracy over traditional methods in identifying vulnerable lesions.

Treatment and Interventions

For patients with stable stenosis of the right coronary artery (RCA), medical management focuses on risk factor modification and symptom control using antiplatelet agents, lipid-lowering therapy, and antianginal medications. Aspirin is recommended as lifelong therapy to reduce the risk of cardiovascular events, with clopidogrel added as dual antiplatelet therapy for up to 6-12 months following any revascularization procedure in stable ischemic heart disease. High-intensity statin therapy, such as atorvastatin 40-80 mg daily, is indicated to achieve LDL cholesterol levels below 70 mg/dL, thereby stabilizing plaques and reducing progression of RCA atherosclerosis. Beta-blockers like metoprolol are first-line for angina relief in stable RCA disease, as they reduce myocardial oxygen demand and improve exercise tolerance when tolerated.
Percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) is a cornerstone for symptomatic RCA stenosis exceeding 70% luminal narrowing, typically involving balloon angioplasty followed by implantation of drug-eluting stents (DES) to minimize neointimal hyperplasia. DES, such as those coated with everolimus or zotarolimus, are preferred over bare-metal stents for lesions greater than 70% due to lower rates of target lesion revascularization, with procedural success rates exceeding 95% in the RCA owing to its favorable anatomy and larger vessel diameter. Balloon angioplasty alone may be used adjunctively or in cases unsuitable for stenting, though it carries higher restenosis risk without drug elution. In acute settings like ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI) with RCA occlusion, thrombolysis with alteplase is employed if PCI is delayed beyond 120 minutes from first medical contact, achieving reperfusion in approximately 50-60% of cases and reducing mortality when administered within 12 hours of symptom onset.
For complex RCA lesions, such as multivessel disease or those unsuitable for PCI (e.g., long diffuse stenoses or calcified segments), coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) provides durable revascularization using saphenous vein grafts (SVG) or the right internal mammary artery (RIMA) anastomosed to the RCA or its posterior descending branch. SVG to the RCA offers good early patency but declines over time, while RIMA use enhances long-term outcomes in suitable anatomies, particularly when combined with left internal mammary artery grafting. The 2021 ACC/AHA/SCAI guidelines recommend PCI as class I for single-vessel RCA disease to improve symptoms and quality of life, with CABG preferred (class I) for complex multivessel involvement including the RCA based on the SYNTAX score. RCA-specific PCI outcomes show lower restenosis rates (approximately 5-10% at 1 year) compared to the left anterior descending artery due to the RCA's larger caliber (average 3.5-4 mm), with 1-year stent patency exceeding 90% using contemporary DES. The ISCHEMIA trial (2020) provided RCA-relevant data, demonstrating that invasive strategies like PCI yield similar hard cardiovascular outcomes to optimal medical therapy in stable disease but superior angina relief. Trials of bioresorbable scaffolds like ABSORB (2023 follow-up) reported 5-year target lesion failure rates around 11-12%, higher than with drug-eluting stents, and are not currently guideline-recommended.
