Neurochemical
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 A neurochemical is a small organic molecule, peptide, or other compound generated by or acting within the nervous system to facilitate neural communication, modulation, and signaling between neurons, glands, or muscle cells. These substances encompass a broad category, including neurotransmitters that transmit signals across synapses, neuromodulators that alter neuronal responsiveness, and neuropeptides that influence longer-term brain functions. Neurochemicals play critical roles in regulating essential physiological processes such as heartbeat, blood pressure, memory formation, learning, stress responses, hormone secretion, digestion, hunger, thirst, sleep patterns, wound healing, and aging.
Key examples of neurochemicals include dopamine, which governs reward pathways, motivation, pleasure, motor control, and executive functions; serotonin, which modulates mood, anxiety, appetite, sleep, and neuropsychological processes; norepinephrine, which activates the fight-or-flight response, enhances alertness, and influences attention and inflammation; GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid), the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter that reduces neuronal excitability and anxiety; and oxytocin, which promotes social bonding, recognition, and physiological responses like labor contractions. Imbalances in these neurochemicalsdue to factors such as insufficient release, rapid reabsorption, or altered receptor sensitivitycan contribute to mental health disorders including depression, anxiety, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, ADHD, and addiction. In neuroscience, the study of neurochemicals, known as neurochemistry, examines their identities, structures, synthesis, release, and functions to understand nervous system dynamics and develop treatments for related conditions.

Definition and Scope

Definition

A neurochemical is defined as a small organic molecule, peptide, or other compound that participates in neural activity, serving as a key mediator within the nervous system. These substances encompass a range of compounds that facilitate communication between neurons, including classical neurotransmitters such as glutamate, which acts primarily as an excitatory signal, and unconventional ones like nitric oxide, a gaseous molecule involved in retrograde signaling. Their roles extend to neurotransmission, where they transmit signals across synapses; neuromodulation, altering the strength or efficacy of synaptic connections; and neural development, influencing processes like synaptogenesis and plasticity.
In contrast to broader biochemicals that operate throughout the body, neurochemicals are distinguished by their specific localization and functional impact on the nervous system, where they selectively regulate neural processes. For instance, they can directly modulate ion channels to alter membrane excitability or activate intracellular second messenger pathways, such as those involving cyclic AMP, to amplify or inhibit signaling cascades. This specificity ensures precise control over brain functions, differentiating neurochemicals from general metabolites or hormones that lack such targeted neural effects.
The scope of neurochemicals includes both amino acid-derived small molecules and larger peptide structures, such as neuropeptides, highlighting their diversity in supporting adaptive neural responses. The field of neurochemistry systematically investigates these substances and their interactions.

Relation to Neurochemistry

Neurochemistry is defined as the study of chemical processes and substances within the nervous system, encompassing the synthesis, release, and effects of neurochemicals such as neurotransmitters and neuromodulators. Neurochemicals serve as the central subjects of this discipline, as their molecular structures, interactions, and functions underpin neural communication and brain activity.
A key application of neurochemistry lies in drug development for neurological disorders, where insights into neurochemical imbalances guide the design of targeted therapies to restore neural function. For instance, understanding neurochemical pathways has facilitated the creation of medications that modulate neurotransmitter activity to treat conditions like schizophrenia and depression. Additionally, imaging techniques such as positron emission tomography (PET) scans enable non-invasive mapping of neurochemical distributions in the brain, aiding in the diagnosis and monitoring of disorders by visualizing receptor densities and metabolic changes.
Neurochemistry integrates closely with pharmacology, particularly through the development of drugs that target specific neurochemical systems to influence synaptic transmission and behavioral outcomes. In biochemistry, it draws on principles like enzyme kinetics to analyze the rates of neurochemical synthesis and degradation along neural pathways, providing a mechanistic foundation for understanding metabolic regulation in the brain. These interdisciplinary connections enhance the field's ability to translate basic research into clinical advancements.

History of Neurochemical Research

Early Discoveries

The foundations of neurochemical research in the 19th century emerged from physiological studies on nerve impulses and glandular secretions, with early speculations about chemical mediators influencing neural function, as explored in European laboratories during that era. These ideas built upon observations of autonomic responses, setting the stage for experimental validation in the early 20th century.
A pivotal breakthrough occurred in 1921 when Otto Loewi conducted experiments on isolated frog hearts, demonstrating chemical synaptic transmission. Loewi stimulated the vagus nerve of one heart, causing it to slow, and transferred the perfusate to a second heart, which also slowed, indicating a diffusible substancelater termed "Vagusstoff"was responsible for the effect. This work provided the first direct evidence against purely electrical transmission, establishing that nerves release chemical agents to communicate with target organs.
Henry Dale's subsequent research confirmed the identity of Vagusstoff as acetylcholine, a compound he had isolated and studied earlier for its effects on smooth muscle and parasympathetic responses. In collaborative efforts, Dale and his team showed acetylcholine's role in synaptic transmission across various systems, solidifying its status as the first identified neurotransmitter. For their combined discoveries on chemical nerve transmission, Loewi and Dale shared the 1936 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine.
These findings resolved longstanding debates from the late 19th and early 20th centuries between proponents of electrical and chemical theories of synaptic transmission, with Loewi and Dale's evidence tipping the balance toward chemical mediators as the primary mechanism in the peripheral nervous system.

Key Milestones in the 20th and 21st Centuries

In the 1950s and 1960s, neurochemical research advanced significantly with the identification of key monoamines as neurotransmitters and breakthroughs in visualizing synaptic structures. Swedish pharmacologist Arvid Carlsson demonstrated in 1957 that dopamine functions as an independent neurotransmitter in the mammalian brain, distinct from its role as a precursor to norepinephrine, through experiments showing its depletion by reserpine and restoration by L-DOPA, which alleviated parkinsonian symptoms in animal models. Similarly, in 1953, pharmacologists Betty Twarog and Irvine Page identified serotonin (5-hydroxytryptamine) in the central nervous system of mammals, confirming its presence and role in neural signaling beyond peripheral vasoconstriction. Concurrently, the development of electron microscopy revealed synaptic vesicles as discrete organelles within presynaptic terminals; Eduardo De Robertis and Henry Bennett's 1954 observations in frog neuromuscular junctions showed these vesicles as electron-lucent structures storing neurotransmitters, providing ultrastructural evidence for quantal release mechanisms.
The 1970s through 1990s saw the expansion of neurochemical diversity with the discovery of neuropeptides and endocannabinoids, alongside molecular tools for studying receptors. In 1975, Hans Kosterlitz and John Hughes isolated enkephalins from porcine brain, identifying them as endogenous opioid peptides that bind to mu and delta receptors to modulate pain and reward; this work, building on earlier opioid receptor findings, led to the broader class of endorphins, including beta-endorphin isolated by Choh Hao Li. The endocannabinoid system emerged in 1992 when Raphael Mechoulam's team isolated anandamide from porcine brain, characterizing it as an endogenous ligand for cannabinoid receptors (previously identified in the 1980s), which regulates synaptic plasticity and neuroprotection. Receptor cloning techniques revolutionized the field starting in the mid-1980s; the nicotinic acetylcholine receptor alpha subunit was first cloned in 1982 by Eric Barnard's group from Torpedo electric organ, enabling detailed studies of ligand-gated ion channel structure and function across neurotransmitter systems.
From the 2000s onward, innovative genetic and optical tools enabled precise manipulation and analysis of neurochemical pathways. Optogenetics, pioneered in 2005 by Karl Deisseroth and colleagues, involved expressing channelrhodopsin-2 (a light-gated cation channel from algae) in neurons to control neurotransmitter release with millisecond precision using blue light, transforming studies of dopamine signaling in reward circuits and serotonin in mood regulation. In the 2010s, CRISPR-Cas9 genome editing, adapted from bacterial defense systems and refined for mammalian use by Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier in 2012, facilitated targeted modifications of neurochemical genes; for instance, early applications knocked out tyrosine hydroxylase (essential for dopamine synthesis) in rodent models to dissect biosynthesis pathways and Parkinson's disease mechanisms. These advances have since integrated with imaging and electrophysiology to elucidate dynamic neurochemical interactions in vivo.

Classification of Neurochemicals

Small-Molecule Neurotransmitters

Small-molecule neurotransmitters are low-molecular-weight organic compounds, typically less than 500 daltons, that serve as primary chemical messengers facilitating rapid communication between neurons. These include simple derivatives such as individual amino acids and biogenic amines, which are packaged into synaptic vesicles and released upon presynaptic depolarization to act swiftly at postsynaptic sites. In contrast to larger peptide neuromodulators, their compact structure enables quick diffusion across the synaptic cleft and efficient clearance, supporting precise temporal control in neural signaling.
The major categories of small-molecule neurotransmitters encompass amino acid-based types, biogenic amines, and acetylcholine. Amino acid neurotransmitters consist primarily of glutamate, the main excitatory agent in the vertebrate brain, and inhibitory counterparts such as -aminobutyric acid (GABA) and glycine, which predominate in inhibitory circuits throughout the central nervous system. Biogenic amines include catecholamines like dopamine and norepinephrine, along with indolamines such as serotonin and other compounds like histamine, derived from amino acid precursors and involved in diverse modulatory yet rapid signaling roles. Acetylcholine, synthesized from choline and acetyl-CoA, is essential for autonomic functions and neuromuscular transmission in the peripheral nervous system.
These neurotransmitters enable fast synaptic transmission by binding to ionotropic receptors, which are ligand-gated ion channels that directly modulate ion flow and postsynaptic membrane potential upon activation. They are ubiquitous across the central nervous system, where amino acids account for the majority of excitatory and inhibitory synapses, and the peripheral nervous system, exemplified by acetylcholine at neuromuscular junctions. This widespread prevalence underscores their essential role in coordinating sensory integration, motor reflexes, and autonomic functions through high-speed, point-to-point neural interactions.

Peptides and Neuromodulators

Peptide neurochemicals, also known as neuropeptides, consist of short chains of 3 to 100 amino acids that function as signaling molecules in the nervous system. These peptides exhibit structural diversity, ranging from simple sequences like the 11-amino-acid substance P to more complex forms, and are synthesized via ribosomal translation in the neuronal cell body before being cleaved and processed into active forms. Unlike classical small-molecule neurotransmitters, which mediate rapid synaptic transmission, peptide neurochemicals such as substance P play key roles in sensory processing, including the transmission of pain signals from primary afferent neurons to the spinal cord. Following synthesis, these peptides are packaged into large dense-core vesicles in the Golgi apparatus and transported along axons for release.
Neuromodulators represent a broader class of neurochemicals that exert non-traditional transmitter functions, often influencing neuronal excitability and circuit dynamics over extended timescales and distances. Prominent examples include endocannabinoids, such as anandamide, which is an endogenous lipid derived from arachidonic acid and acts as a retrograde messenger to regulate synaptic strength. Other neuromodulators, like certain eicosanoids, contribute to diffuse signaling by spreading beyond individual synapses to modulate multiple neurons simultaneously, thereby fine-tuning network activity. This mode of action contrasts with the localized, point-to-point communication of small-molecule neurotransmitters.
A key distinction of peptides and neuromodulators lies in their slower, modulatory effects, primarily mediated through metabotropic receptors that activate intracellular signaling cascades rather than directly opening ion channels. These effects enable prolonged changes in neuronal responsiveness, contributing to synaptic plasticitythe basis for learning and memoryand the regulation of mood states via alterations in emotional processing circuits. For instance, neuropeptides like substance P can enhance plasticity in pain pathways, while endocannabinoids such as anandamide influence mood by modulating reward and stress responses.

Biosynthesis and Metabolism

Synthesis and Storage

Neurochemicals, encompassing small-molecule neurotransmitters and peptide neuromodulators, are synthesized through distinct biochemical pathways tailored to their chemical nature. Small-molecule neurochemicals, such as monoamines (e.g., dopamine, serotonin) and amino acid derivatives (e.g., glutamate, GABA), are primarily produced in the neuronal cytoplasm, often within presynaptic terminals where the necessary enzymes are localized. For instance, dopamine synthesis begins with the conversion of tyrosine to L-DOPA by the rate-limiting enzyme tyrosine hydroxylase, followed by decarboxylation to dopamine. Similarly, serotonin is synthesized from tryptophan via tryptophan hydroxylase and aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase (AADC). These hydroxylases and decarboxylases, which require cofactors like tetrahydrobiopterin and pyridoxal phosphate, enable rapid, on-demand production in response to neuronal activity.
In contrast, peptide neurochemicals, such as substance P and neuropeptide Y, are synthesized as inactive precursors (prohormones) on ribosomes bound to the rough endoplasmic reticulum in the neuronal cell body. These precursors undergo initial folding and glycosylation in the endoplasmic reticulum before transport to the Golgi apparatus for further processing. Key enzymes in this pathway include prohormone convertases (PCs), such as PC1/3 and PC2, which cleave the prohormones at dibasic amino acid sites (e.g., Lys-Arg) to generate active peptides; this processing occurs primarily in the trans-Golgi network and immature secretory vesicles. The resulting peptides are then packaged for axonal transport to synaptic terminals.
Once synthesized, neurochemicals are stored in synaptic vesicles to protect them from cytoplasmic degradation and regulate their availability for release. Monoamine small-molecule neurochemicals are actively transported into small synaptic vesicles (40-50 nm diameter) via vesicular monoamine transporters (VMATs), such as VMAT2, which use a proton electrochemical gradient generated by vacuolar H+-ATPase to exchange cytoplasmic monoamines for vesicular protons. This packaging concentrates the neurochemicals up to millimolar levels, ensuring efficient storage and preventing non-specific leakage. Amino acid small-molecule neurotransmitters, such as glutamate and GABA, are similarly transported into small synaptic vesicles using distinct vesicular transporters that also exploit the proton gradient: vesicular glutamate transporters (VGLUT13) for glutamate and the vesicular GABA transporter (VGAT) for GABA (and glycine). Peptide neurochemicals, being larger, are stored in large dense-core vesicles (80-200 nm diameter), which form from the trans-Golgi network and contain an electron-dense core visible under electron microscopy; these vesicles also transport peptides along microtubules to distant release sites. Vesicle filling and maturation for both types can be modulated by intracellular calcium levels, where localized calcium influx influences proton pump activity and vesicle acidification, thereby optimizing storage capacity.

Inactivation Mechanisms

After synaptic release, neurochemicals must be rapidly inactivated to terminate signaling and prevent overstimulation of receptors, ensuring precise temporal control of neural transmission. The primary mechanisms include reuptake, enzymatic degradation, and diffusion, often working in concert to clear the synaptic cleft and extrasynaptic space.
Reuptake is a key process mediated by specific transporter proteins that recycle neurochemicals back into presynaptic neurons or glial cells, driven by electrochemical gradients. For monoamines, the dopamine transporter (DAT) facilitates the uptake of dopamine into dopaminergic terminals by co-transporting one dopamine molecule with two sodium ions (Na) and one chloride ion (Cl), powered by the sodium gradient established by the Na/K-ATPase pump. Similarly, the serotonin transporter (SERT) reuptakes serotonin (5-HT) with one Na, one Cl, and counter-transport of one potassium ion (K), also relying on this energy-dependent sodium gradient for efficient clearance. These transporters belong to the solute carrier 6 (SLC6) family and exhibit an alternating access mechanism, transitioning between outward- and inward-facing conformations to move substrates across the membrane.
Enzymatic degradation provides another route for inactivation, particularly for monoamines and peptides. Monoamine oxidase (MAO), a flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD)-dependent enzyme anchored to the outer mitochondrial membrane, catalyzes the oxidative deamination of monoamines such as dopamine, serotonin, and norepinephrine, producing aldehydes, hydrogen peroxide, and ammonia as byproducts; these are further metabolized by aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH) into acids like 3,4-dihydroxyphenylacetic acid (DOPAC) for dopamine. Complementing MAO, catechol-O-methyltransferase (COMT), a cytosolic magnesium-dependent enzyme, methylates catecholamines like dopamine and norepinephrine using S-adenosylmethionine as a methyl donor, yielding inactive metabolites such as 3-methoxytyramine. For neuropeptides, extracellular peptidases like neprilysin and aminopeptidase N hydrolyze peptide bonds to generate inactive fragments, preventing prolonged receptor activation. MAO-A predominates in catecholaminergic neurons, while MAO-B is more abundant in serotonergic neurons and astrocytes.
Diffusion away from the synapse, combined with glial uptake, contributes to the clearance of extrasynaptic neurochemicals, modulating spillover signaling. Astrocytes play a crucial role by expressing transporters such as SERT and the norepinephrine transporter (NET), which uptake monoamines from the extracellular space, complementing neuronal reuptake and maintaining homeostasis. These cells also facilitate the diffusion of neurochemicals through volume transmission, where unbound molecules spread to distant sites, and convert taken-up substrates (e.g., glutamate to glutamine via glutamine synthetase) for recycling. This glial clearance is vital for preventing excitotoxicity and influencing network activity; for instance, selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) like fluoxetine block SERT on both neurons and astrocytes, elevating extracellular serotonin levels and enhancing therapeutic effects in mood disorders by prolonging signaling.

Mechanisms of Neural Signaling

Release and Synaptic Transmission

The release of neurochemicals occurs through a highly regulated process of exocytosis at the presynaptic terminal, where synaptic vesicles containing these signaling molecules fuse with the plasma membrane to discharge their contents into the synaptic cleft. This process is initiated when an action potential propagates to the axon terminal, causing membrane depolarization that activates voltage-gated calcium channels, allowing an influx of Ca ions into the presynaptic neuron. The elevated intracellular Ca concentration then binds to sensor proteins like synaptotagmin, which trigger the formation of a SNARE complex involving v-SNARE (VAMP) on the vesicle and t-SNAREs (syntaxin and SNAP-25) on the target membrane, driving the rapid fusion of the vesicle with the plasma membrane.
Neurochemical release is inherently quantal, meaning it occurs in discrete packets corresponding to the contents of individual synaptic vesicles, a concept established through studies at the neuromuscular junction. Spontaneous release generates miniature end-plate potentials (MEPPs), small depolarizations reflecting the discharge of a single quantum without an action potential, with their frequency providing insight into the readily releasable pool of vesicles. Upon arrival of an action potential, evoked release involves the synchronous fusion of multiple vesicles, where the number of quanta releasedtermed quantal contentis modulated by the release probability (Pr), which can vary from low (e.g., 0.10.3 in some central synapses) to high (near 1 in others) depending on factors like Ca dynamics and presynaptic modulation, thereby fine-tuning synaptic strength.
Once released, neurochemicals traverse the synaptic clefta narrow extracellular space measuring 2040 nm in widthprimarily via passive diffusion, establishing transient concentration gradients that determine the speed and spatial extent of signaling. For small-molecule neurochemicals, this diffusion is rapid, peaking at millimolar concentrations within microseconds and decaying within milliseconds to ensure precise, point-to-point transmission. In contrast, larger or slower-diffusing molecules produce more prolonged gradients, supporting slower modulatory effects over broader areas, though the core dynamics remain governed by the cleft's geometry and diffusion barriers.
This release process relies on prior biosynthesis and storage of neurochemicals in vesicular pools, ensuring availability for rapid deployment during signaling.

Receptor Binding and Signal Transduction

Neurochemicals exert their effects primarily through binding to specific receptors on the postsynaptic membrane or within cells, initiating a cascade of intracellular events that propagate neural signals. This interaction is fundamental to synaptic communication, where the neurochemical acts as a ligand that recognizes and binds to receptor proteins with high specificity. The binding process follows principles of molecular recognition, governed by complementary shapes and chemical properties between the ligand and receptor binding site, as established in foundational studies of receptor-ligand interactions.
Receptors for neurochemicals are broadly classified into two main types: ionotropic and metabotropic. Ionotropic receptors, also known as ligand-gated ion channels, form a central pore that opens upon ligand binding, allowing rapid influx or efflux of ions such as Na, K, Ca, or Cl, which directly alters the membrane potential to mediate fast synaptic transmission. A classic example is the AMPA receptor, an ionotropic glutamate receptor that facilitates excitatory postsynaptic potentials by permitting Na and K flow. In contrast, metabotropic receptors are G-protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) that, upon ligand binding, activate associated G-proteins to modulate intracellular signaling without directly altering ion conductance, leading to slower, modulatory effects. For instance, serotonin receptors like the 5-HT1 family are metabotropic and couple to inhibitory G-proteins to decrease neuronal excitability. This dichotomy allows neurochemicals to elicit both phasic (rapid) and tonic (sustained) responses in neural circuits.
The kinetics of receptor binding are characterized by affinity, typically quantified by the dissociation constant (K_d), which indicates the ligand concentration required for half-maximal receptor occupancy; lower K_d values reflect higher affinity. Agonists mimic the endogenous neurochemical to activate the receptor, while antagonists competitively block binding without activation, thereby inhibiting signal propagation. Allosteric modulators bind to sites distinct from the orthosteric ligand-binding pocket, enhancing or inhibiting the receptor's response to the primary ligandfor example, benzodiazepines potentiate GABA_A receptor function at an allosteric site to amplify inhibitory signaling. These kinetic properties ensure precise temporal control of signaling, with binding and unbinding rates determining the duration and strength of the response.
Signal transduction following receptor binding varies by receptor type and leads to diverse cellular outcomes. In ionotropic receptors, ligand-induced conformational changes open the ion channel, resulting in excitatory (e.g., depolarization via cation influx) or inhibitory (e.g., hyperpolarization via Cl influx) effects that can trigger action potentials within milliseconds. Metabotropic receptors, however, initiate slower pathways through second messengers; for example, activation of adenylyl cyclase by G_s-coupled receptors increases cyclic AMP (cAMP) levels, which activates protein kinase A to phosphorylate targets and modulate ion channels or gene expression. Long-term effects, such as synaptic plasticity, arise from sustained signaling that influences transcription factors, leading to changes in protein synthesis and neuronal connectivity, as seen in cAMP-response element-binding protein (CREB) activation. These pathways integrate multiple neurochemical inputs to fine-tune neural function.

Prominent Neurochemicals

Amino Acid-Based Neurochemicals

Amino acid-based neurochemicals, classified as small-molecule neurotransmitters, primarily mediate rapid excitatory and inhibitory signaling in the central nervous system through ionotropic receptors. These compounds, derived directly from amino acids, play essential roles in maintaining neural balance, with glutamate driving excitation, while GABA and glycine promote inhibition. Their actions are critical for processes ranging from sensory processing to motor control, and dysregulation can lead to neurological disorders.
Glutamate serves as the principal excitatory neurotransmitter in the mammalian brain, accounting for the majority of excitatory synaptic transmission across diverse neural circuits. It binds to ionotropic receptors such as AMPA and NMDA types, facilitating rapid depolarization and calcium influx that underpin synaptic plasticity. A key example of its role in learning is its involvement in long-term potentiation (LTP), a persistent strengthening of synapses observed in the hippocampus, where glutamate activation of NMDA receptors triggers signaling cascades essential for memory formation. However, excessive glutamate release can cause excitotoxicity, a pathological process where overactivation of receptors leads to neuronal damage via calcium overload and oxidative stress; this mechanism contributes significantly to cell death in ischemic stroke, where energy failure impairs glutamate clearance.
Gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) functions as the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter in the brain, counterbalancing excitatory inputs to prevent hyperexcitability and support network stability. It is synthesized from glutamate by the enzyme glutamic acid decarboxylase (GAD), which catalyzes the decarboxylation reaction in GABAergic neurons, ensuring localized production for vesicular storage and release. GABA exerts its effects mainly through GABA_A receptors, ligand-gated chloride channels that hyperpolarize postsynaptic neurons upon activation. Benzodiazepines, a class of anxiolytic drugs, enhance GABAergic inhibition by positively modulating GABA_A receptors at a distinct allosteric site, increasing channel opening frequency and chloride conductance to amplify inhibitory signaling.
Glycine acts predominantly as an inhibitory neurotransmitter in the spinal cord and brainstem, where it modulates motor reflexes and sensory integration via strychnine-sensitive glycine receptors that permit chloride influx for hyperpolarization. These receptors are pentameric chloride channels composed of alpha and beta subunits, enabling fast inhibitory postsynaptic potentials in interneurons and motoneurons. Beyond inhibition, glycine serves as an obligatory co-agonist at NMDA receptors, binding to a specific site on the GluN1 subunit to relieve magnesium block and facilitate calcium entry in response to glutamate, thus contributing to excitatory processes like synaptic plasticity in higher brain regions. Disruption of glycinergic signaling, such as through strychnine poisoning, blocks glycine receptors competitively, leading to disinhibition, muscle spasms, and potentially fatal convulsions due to unchecked motor neuron excitation.

Monoamine Neurochemicals

Monoamine neurochemicals, including dopamine, serotonin, and norepinephrine, are biogenic amines that function primarily as neuromodulators in the central and peripheral nervous systems, influencing mood, motivation, and arousal through slower, longer-lasting effects compared to the rapid signaling of amino acid neurotransmitters.
Dopamine is synthesized from the amino acid tyrosine in a two-step enzymatic process: tyrosine is first converted to L-3,4-dihydroxyphenylalanine (L-DOPA) by the rate-limiting enzyme tyrosine hydroxylase, which requires tetrahydrobiopterin and molecular oxygen as cofactors, followed by decarboxylation of L-DOPA to dopamine by aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase. Dopamine plays a central role in the brain's reward system via the mesolimbic pathway, where neurons originating in the ventral tegmental area project to the nucleus accumbens, releasing dopamine to reinforce behaviors associated with pleasure and motivation. A deficit in dopamine production and nigrostriatal pathway function underlies Parkinson's disease, characterized by the progressive loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra, leading to motor symptoms such as tremors and bradykinesia.
Serotonin, also known as 5-hydroxytryptamine (5-HT), is synthesized from the essential amino acid L-tryptophan, which is transported across the blood-brain barrier and hydroxylated to 5-hydroxytryptophan by tryptophan hydroxylase before decarboxylation to serotonin by aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase. Serotonergic neurons primarily originate in the raphe nuclei of the brainstem, from which they project diffusely to modulate various brain regions involved in emotion and cognition. Dysregulation of serotonin signaling is implicated in major depressive disorder, where reduced serotonergic transmission contributes to symptoms of low mood and anhedonia; selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), such as fluoxetine, treat this by blocking the serotonin transporter to increase synaptic serotonin availability and enhance postsynaptic receptor activation over time.
Norepinephrine is derived from dopamine through hydroxylation at the beta position, catalyzed by the copper-dependent enzyme dopamine beta-hydroxylase (DBH), which is localized in synaptic vesicles and requires ascorbate as a cofactor. As a key mediator of the sympathetic nervous system, norepinephrine promotes arousal and the "fight-or-flight" response by activating adrenergic receptors in target tissues, increasing heart rate, blood pressure, and vigilance during stress. In attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), impaired norepinephrine signaling contributes to symptoms of inattention and impulsivity; atomoxetine, a selective norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor, addresses this by blocking the norepinephrine transporter to elevate extracellular norepinephrine levels in the prefrontal cortex, improving executive function without stimulant effects.
