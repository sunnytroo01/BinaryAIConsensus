 February 24 is the 55th day of the year in the Gregorian calendar, leaving 310 days (311 in leap years) until December 31.
On this date in 1582, Pope Gregory XIII issued the papal bull Inter gravissimas, promulgating the Gregorian calendar reform to correct the drift in the Julian calendar by skipping ten days in October and adjusting leap year rules.
In 1920, the German Workers' Party, reorganized as the National Socialist German Workers' Party (NSDAP), publicly announced its 25-point program under Adolf Hitler's influence, marking a key step in the formation of what became the Nazi Party.
The ground offensive of Operation Desert Storm, the U.S.-led coalition's campaign to expel Iraqi forces from Kuwait, began on February 24, 1991, following weeks of aerial bombardment.
Most notably in recent history, Russian forces launched a full-scale invasion of Ukraine on February 24, 2022, escalating the ongoing conflict that originated in 2014 with the annexation of Crimea and fighting in Donbas; this operation, framed by Russia as a "special military operation" to "denazify" and demilitarize Ukraine, prompted widespread international sanctions and military aid to Ukraine.

Pre-1600

On February 24, 484, King Huneric of the Vandals, adhering to Arian Christianity, deposed Nicene (Catholic) bishops across his North African kingdom during a council convened in Carthage earlier that month; he banished approximately 466 bishops, priests, and deacons to Corsica and other remote areas, while executing some resisters, as part of efforts to enforce Arian dominance over Nicene clergy.
The Battle of Pavia took place on February 24, 1525, pitting French forces led by King Francis I against an Imperial-Spanish army under the viceroys Charles de Lannoy and Frank of Bourbon during the Italian War of 15211526; the Imperial troops, bolstered by German Landsknechts and Spanish tercios, decisively defeated the French, killing or capturing much of their armyincluding Francis himselfand marking a turning point that weakened French ambitions in Italy.
On February 24, 1530, Pope Clement VII crowned Charles V as Holy Roman Emperor in a ceremony at Bologna's Basilica of San Petronio, the last time a pope performed such an imperial coronation outside Rome; this event, following Charles's election in 1519 and German coronation in 1520, symbolized papal acknowledgment of his imperial authority amid ongoing conflicts with France and the Ottoman Empire.

1607 Claudio Monteverdi's L'Orfeo premiered on February 24 in Mantua, Italy, marking the first work widely recognized as an opera, blending Renaissance polyphony with dramatic narrative based on the Orpheus myth.
1711 George Frideric Handel's opera Rinaldo, his first for a London audience, debuted on February 24 at the Queen's Theatre in Haymarket, achieving immediate commercial success with 15 performances and establishing Italian opera's popularity in England.
1803 The U.S. Supreme Court, in Marbury v. Madison, ruled on February 24 that Section 13 of the Judiciary Act of 1781 was unconstitutional, thereby establishing the principle of judicial review, empowering the Court to invalidate laws conflicting with the Constitution.
1868 The U.S. House of Representatives impeached President Andrew Johnson on February 24 by a vote of 126 to 47 for high crimes and misdemeanors, primarily over his violation of the Tenure of Office Act through the dismissal of Secretary of War Edwin Stanton, reflecting post-Civil War tensions with Radical Republicans.

19012000

1908 In Muller v. Oregon, the U.S. Supreme Court unanimously upheld a state law limiting women's workday to ten hours, marking the first time the Court intervened to protect female workers from excessive labor hours based on arguments emphasizing women's physical differences from men.
1917 The U.S. ambassador to the United Kingdom, Walter Hines Page, forwarded the Zimmermann Telegram to President Woodrow Wilson, revealing Germany's proposal for a military alliance with Mexico should the United States enter World War I, which included offers of territory in Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona.
1918 The Estonian National Council (Maapev) declared independence from the Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic amid the chaos of the Russian Revolution and World War I, establishing the Republic of Estonia, though full recognition came later after conflicts with Bolshevik and German forces.
1920 The German Workers' Party was renamed the National Socialist German Workers' Party (NSDAP, or Nazi Party) in Munich, with Adolf Hitler presenting its 25-point program, which combined nationalist, antisemitic, and anti-capitalist elements, attracting early membership that grew rapidly in the following years.
1942 The "Battle of Los Angeles" occurred when U.S. Army anti-aircraft batteries fired over 1,400 shells at unidentified aircraft over the city in response to wartime alerts, resulting in no confirmed enemy contact but five civilian deaths from falling debris and panic; official investigations attributed it to weather balloons and nerves amid fears of Japanese attack.
1946 Juan Pern was elected president of Argentina with 52% of the vote in the nation's first general election under universal suffrage, campaigning on labor rights and nationalism, supported by trade unions and his wife Eva's mobilization efforts.
1949 Egypt and Israel signed a formal armistice agreement in Rhodes, Greece, establishing the EgyptIsrael Mixed Armistice Commission to supervise the truce line along the former Palestine Mandate border, ending active hostilities from the 1948 Arab-Israeli War but leaving territorial disputes unresolved.
1955 The Baghdad Pact was signed by Iraq, Turkey, Iran, Pakistan, and the United Kingdom in Baghdad, forming a defensive alliance against Soviet expansion during the Cold War, later joined by the United States as a partner without full membership. Holidays and Observances

Religious Observances

In the Roman Catholic Church, February 24 is the feast day of Saint Matthias, one of the Twelve Apostles selected by lot to replace Judas Iscariot after his betrayal and death, as described in Acts 1:15-26 of the New Testament. Matthias, traditionally believed to have been born in Bethlehem and to have preached in Judea, Cappadocia, and Ethiopia before his martyrdom by stoning or beheading, is invoked as a patron for those suffering from small-pox and for carpenters; his relics are enshrined in Rome's Santa Maria degli Angeli.
In the Eastern Orthodox Church, the date marks the First and Second Finding of the Head of Saint John the Baptist, commemorating the recovery of the prophet's headsevered by Herod Antipasfirst by his disciple Innozentius during the fourth century and rediscovered later amid persecutions, with the relic historically venerated in Emesa (modern Homs, Syria) before its transfer to Constantinople. Additional commemorations include martyrs such as Montanus, Lucius, Julian, Victoricus, Flavian, and companions, killed in Carthage around 259 AD during Valerian's persecutions, emphasizing themes of baptismal perseverance and clerical martyrdom.
Other Christian traditions observe minor saints' days on this date, including Saint Ethelbert of Kent (died circa 616), an early Anglo-Saxon king credited with converting his kingdom to Christianity under Augustine of Canterbury's influence, though his feast is less universally fixed. No major fixed observances in non-Christian religions align with February 24 in the Gregorian calendar, as holidays in Judaism, Islam, Hinduism, and Buddhism typically follow lunar or lunisolar cycles.

National and Independence Days

Estonia observes Independence Day (Iseseisvuspev) on February 24, marking the proclamation of independence from Soviet Russia by the Estonian Provincial Assembly in Tallinn on February 24, 1918, during the dissolution of the Russian Empire amid World War I and the Bolshevik Revolution. This declaration established the first Republic of Estonia, though it faced immediate invasion by the Red Army in November 1918, sparking the Estonian War of Independence that concluded with a peace treaty in 1920 recognizing sovereignty. The holiday is Estonia's most significant national observance and a public holiday, featuring flag-raising ceremonies at Pikk Hermann Tower, presidential addresses, wreath-laying at war memorials, military parades in Tallinn, and cultural events such as folk singing and fireworks.
Mexico commemorates Flag Day (Da de la Bandera) on February 24, a civic national holiday dedicated to honoring the tricolor flag adopted in its current form in 1968, with roots tracing to the 1821 Trigarante flag signaling the end of the War of Independence from Spain. Established by decree in 1937 and formalized in 1940, the observance promotes patriotism through mandatory flag-raising rituals in public buildings, schools, and homes, accompanied by the playing of the "Toque de Bandera" hymn and educational programs on the flag's symbolismgreen for hope, white for purity, and red for the blood of national heroes. While not a federal day off with closures, it involves widespread ceremonies and is reinforced by laws requiring proper flag handling to foster national unity.

Professional and Secular Observances

World Bartender Day is observed annually on February 24 to recognize the bartending profession, highlighting bartenders' expertise in crafting cocktails, providing hospitality, and fostering social environments in bars worldwide. The day celebrates bartending as one of the oldest trades, with origins tracing back to informal acknowledgments but officially designated in 2018 to distinguish it from national variants like the U.S. National Bartender Day in May.
DiscoverE Girl Day, also called Introduce a Girl to Engineering Day, falls on February 24 and promotes girls' participation in engineering and STEM fields through hands-on activities, mentorship, and outreach events led by professionals and educators. Sponsored by the DiscoverE organization, it seeks to address gender disparities in engineering enrollment and careers, where women comprise less than 15% of the global engineering workforce according to industry reports, by inspiring K-12 students via role models and demonstrations.
Dragobete is a traditional Romanian holiday observed on February 24, celebrating love, nature, and the onset of spring, often compared to Valentine's Day, with customs including gathering medicinal herbs and playful pursuits between young men and women in the forests.
World Spay Day, held annually on the last Tuesday in Februarywhich can coincide with February 24promotes the spaying and neutering of companion animals to reduce overpopulation and euthanasia rates, coordinated by the Humane Society International and partners worldwide through clinics, events, and advocacy.
In the United States, February 24 marks several informal national days, including National Tortilla Chip Day, honoring the corn-based snack enjoyed with dips; National Trading Card Day, recognizing the hobby of collecting and trading cards featuring sports figures, characters, and more; and Twin Peaks Day, a fan commemoration tied to the cult television series.
Thailand's National Artist Day, observed on February 24, honors the country's artists and commemorates the birth of King Rama II, known for his literary and artistic contributions, with ceremonies recognizing recipients of the National Artist award.)
Sweden Finns' Day (Sverigefinnarnas dag), celebrated on February 24, highlights the history, language, and culture of the Sweden Finns, a minority group of Finnish descent, through events and awareness initiatives.
In the Philippines, Cebu City Charter Day on February 24 commemorates the city's establishment as a chartered city in 1937, observed as a special non-working holiday with local ceremonies and programs.
