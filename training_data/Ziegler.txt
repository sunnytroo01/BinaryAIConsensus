 Jean Ziegler (born Hans Ziegler, 19 April 1934) is a Swiss sociologist, author, and former United Nations official renowned for his advocacy on global hunger and critiques of capitalist institutions. A professor emeritus at the University of Geneva and former member of the Swiss National Council for the Social Democratic Party from 1963 to 1983, Ziegler gained international prominence as the UN Special Rapporteur on the right to food from 2000 to 2008, during which he emphasized food as a fundamental human right and investigated policies contributing to malnutrition in regions like Africa and Latin America. His work, including reports on biofuels exacerbating food shortages and condemnations of corporate exploitation, has influenced debates on economic justice, though it has faced scrutiny for selective focus amid documented inconsistencies, such as his receipt of the 2002 Gaddafi International Prize for Human Rights from the Libyan regime he elsewhere critiqued minimally. Ziegler has also drawn controversy for equating Israel's Gaza policies to a "concentration camp" and praising leaders like Fidel Castro and Hugo Chvez, associations that critics, including UN Watch, attribute to ideological partiality in UN human rights mechanisms often marked by institutional imbalances favoring certain political narratives over empirical accountability. Despite such debates, his exposs on Swiss banking practices tied to dormant Holocaust-era accounts contributed to reparations discussions in the 1990s.

Surname

Etymology and meaning

The surname Ziegler is a German occupational name denoting a brickmaker, bricklayer, or tiler. It functions as an agent noun derived from Middle High German ziegel, signifying "brick" or "roof tile," with the suffix indicating "one who works with" the material. This etymology traces to Old High German ziagal, reflecting medieval building trades involving clay-fired tiles for roofing or bricks for construction in German-speaking regions.
Among Ashkenazi Jewish populations, Ziegler similarly adopted the occupational connotation from the same German linguistic root, rather than originating from Hebrew or Yiddish phrases, as surnames were often germanized based on professions during the 18th-19th century naming mandates in Central Europe. Variants such as Zeigler represent phonetic adaptations, particularly in anglicized contexts, while forms like Cigler appear in Slovenian-influenced areas but retain the core tile- or brick-working association tied to the German progenitor.

Historical distribution and notable families

The Ziegler surname is most prevalent in Germany, where it ranks as the 88th most common surname with approximately 65,155 bearers. Its historical concentration lies in southern and southwestern regions, particularly Bavaria, reflecting occupational origins tied to brickmaking and tiling in medieval German-speaking areas. Genetic ancestry analyses of bearers indicate over 40% alignment with French and German heritage, underscoring roots in Central European populations.
Migration patterns trace to 18th-century emigration from German territories to North America, with early settlers arriving in Pennsylvania amid broader Palatine German influxes. Michael Ziegler, originating from Germany around 1680, established in Perkiomen Township by 1717, exemplifying initial family implantation in colonial settlements like Skippack. By the mid-18th century, nearly 100 Ziegler immigrants had entered via Philadelphia, contributing to Pennsylvania German communities.
Notable early lineages include the Bavarian Ziegler zu Meisenhausen und Rosenberg branch, elevated to baronial status in 1843, with roots in medieval establishments and holdings in areas like Prgen and Schonstett. Ziegler families also featured among Volga Germans, with groups departing from Hessian and Danish ports in the 1760s to settle Russian colonies such as Galka, preserving the name through 1767 censuses. These distributions highlight verifiable patterns of occupational dispersal and targeted colonial invitations rather than unverified noble ubiquity.

Visual artists

Eustace Paul Ziegler (18811969) was an American painter renowned for his landscapes, figure studies, and frontier genre scenes, particularly those capturing Alaskan wilderness and indigenous life. Born in Detroit, Michigan, he relocated to Alaska in 1908, where he became a pivotal figure in the region's art scene, serving as the first president of the Puget Sound Group of Northwest Painters in 1920 and exhibiting extensively in Seattle galleries. His oil paintings, such as "50 Winters" and works depicting miners and native subjects, fetched significant auction prices, with one canvas selling for $65,450 in a recent sale; Ziegler's style emphasized dramatic lighting and rugged naturalism drawn from direct observation during expeditions.
Adolf Ziegler (18921959) was a German painter associated with academic realism and mythological nudes, later appointed by the Nazi regime as president of the Reich Chamber of the Visual Arts in 1936, overseeing the sale of confiscated "degenerate" artworks to fund German rearmament. Initially trained in Munich under Max Doerner, he produced early watercolors of Bavarian streetscapes and landscapes before shifting to idealized female figures in works like The Four Elements (19371943), which embodied regime-approved heroic classicism. His output, while technically proficient, reflected state-sanctioned aesthetics prioritizing racial purity over modernist innovation, with post-war denazification limiting his legacy.
Archibald Ziegler (19031971), a British painter and draughtsman, specialized in portraits, landscapes, and biblical scenes after studying at the Central School of Arts and Crafts and the Royal College of Art under William Rothenstein from 1927 to 1930. Teaching at various London institutions, he exhibited with the Royal Academy and produced works held in UK public collections, including symbolic compositions blending traditional technique with subtle modernism; his draughtsmanship emphasized precise linework and tonal depth.
Richard Ziegler (18911992) was a self-taught British-German painter who began creating after World War I, experimenting with diverse techniques including oils, watercolors, and abstracts influenced by Cubism and Surrealism. Lacking formal training, he developed a versatile style evident in solo exhibitions into his nineties, with holdings in public collections like those documented by Art UK; his longevity allowed evolution from representational landscapes to more experimental forms.
Toby Ziegler (born 1972) is a contemporary British painter whose works synthesize digital imagery, historical art references, and algorithmic processes to explore perception and representation, often layering motifs from Old Master still-lifes and photographs onto canvases. Represented by Galerie Max Hetzler, his pieces, such as those deriving from Freud family portraits, employ glazing and fragmentation to critique image mediation in the digital age.

Authors and writers

Jean Ziegler (born 1937) is a Swiss sociologist and author whose publications focus on critiques of globalization, capitalism, and international economic policies. His 2006 book L'Empire de la honte (translated as The Empire of Shame) analyzes the exploitation of developing nations by multinational corporations and Western governments, drawing on case studies from Africa and Latin America. Other notable works include Betting on Famine: Why the World Deliberately Starves (2011), which argues that food crises result from speculative financial practices rather than scarcity, supported by data on commodity markets from 20072010.
Philip Ziegler (24 December 1929 22 February 2023) was a British historian and biographer specializing in 19th- and 20th-century British figures and events. His 1969 book The Black Death provides a detailed account of the 13481350 plague's demographic and social impacts in Europe, estimating 2550 million deaths based on contemporary chronicles and modern epidemiological analysis. Ziegler authored official biographies such as Mountbatten (1985), covering Admiral Lord Mountbatten's naval career and political roles through World War II, and King Edward VIII: The Official Biography (1990), which examines the abdication crisis using declassified documents from 1936.

Advisors and spokespersons

Ronald Louis Ziegler (19392003) served as White House Press Secretary under President Richard Nixon from January 1969 to August 1974. Appointed at age 29, he was the youngest person to hold the position at the time. Ziegler, a former advertising executive and Nixon campaign aide, handled daily press briefings and communications strategy, often navigating tensions between the administration and a skeptical media corps.
During the Watergate scandal, Ziegler played a central role in defending the Nixon administration publicly. Following the June 17, 1972, break-in at the Democratic National Committee headquarters in the Watergate complex, he described the incident as a "third-rate burglary" in a June 19 press briefing, downplaying its significance amid emerging evidence of White House involvement. He conducted over 1,000 briefings during his tenure, frequently issuing statements that later proved inconsistent with revelations from investigations, such as his April 17, 1973, declaration that prior White House comments on the scandal were "inoperative" after Nixon's national address admitted knowledge of a cover-up. These communications contributed to perceptions of obfuscation, as congressional probes and the Watergate tapes exposed deeper administration efforts to conceal involvement.
Ziegler resigned effective August 9, 1974, immediately after Nixon announced his own resignation amid the scandal's culmination, including the Supreme Court's July 24 ruling mandating release of subpoenaed tapes. His loyalty to Nixon persisted post-tenure; in later reflections, he acknowledged the administration's errors but maintained that the press secretary role required balancing access with executive protection, a stance echoed in archival records of his briefings. No other individuals surnamed Ziegler have held prominent advisory or spokesperson positions in U.S. federal government comparable to Ziegler's role.

Science, technology, and academia

Chemists and engineers

Karl Ziegler (18981973) was a German chemist whose research on organometallic compounds led to the development of catalysts enabling the stereospecific polymerization of olefins into high-molecular-weight, linear polymers. In the early 1950s, Ziegler discovered that triethylaluminum, when combined with titanium compounds, acted as an effective catalyst for polymerizing ethylene at low pressures and temperatures, producing high-density polyethylene (HDPE) with superior strength and density compared to earlier low-density variants. This breakthrough, patented in 1953 by Ziegler's team at the Max Planck Institute for Coal Research, facilitated industrial-scale production of HDPE starting in 1954 by companies like Hoechst and later DuPont.
Ziegler's catalyst system, later known as Ziegler-Natta after Italian chemist Giulio Natta's extensions to propylene polymerization, revolutionized polyolefin manufacturing by allowing control over polymer tacticity and microstructure, essential for thermoplastics used in packaging, pipes, and bottles. For these discoveries in high polymer chemistry and technology, Ziegler shared the 1963 Nobel Prize in Chemistry with Natta, recognizing the catalysts' role in enabling commercial stereospecific polymerization processes that began yielding isotactic polypropylene by 1957. Ziegler's innovations stemmed from systematic studies of aluminum alkyls' reactivity with transition metals, avoiding high-pressure free-radical methods and yielding polymers with precise chain lengths and minimal branching.

In 2025, she published Personhood: The New Civil War over Reproduction, which traces the historical and legal debates over fetal personhood and its implications for equality beyond abortion contexts. Ziegler has held visiting positions, including at Harvard Law School in spring 2022, and her work draws on archival sources to challenge conventional narratives in reproductive law historiography.

Ziegler's academic contributions emphasize the sociology of developing societies, with research grounded in fieldwork on global inequality and resource distribution, though his interpretations often reflect a critical stance toward Western economic policies, as evidenced in his university-established programs focused on non-Western social structures.
Tamar Ziegler is an Israeli mathematician renowned for advancing higher-order Fourier analysis and applying dynamical systems methods to arithmetic combinatorics and ergodic theory. She holds positions at institutions like the Hebrew University of Jerusalem and the Weizmann Institute, contributing to peer-reviewed publications on quantitative equidistribution and host-guest problems in ergodic theory.
Gnter M. Ziegler is a German mathematician and professor at Freie Universitt Berlin, specializing in discrete geometry, convexity, and combinatorial optimization. Since joining the university in 1992, his research has produced foundational results on polytopes, including the proof of the Hirsch conjecture's disproof in higher dimensions, and he has authored over 200 papers with applications to computational geometry. Ziegler's textbook Lectures on Polytopes (1995, updated editions) remains a standard reference, emphasizing algebraic and topological methods in discrete mathematics.

Other uses

Scientific processes and catalysts

The ZieglerNatta catalyst consists of a transition metal compound, typically titanium tetrachloride, combined with an organoaluminum cocatalyst such as triethylaluminum, facilitating the stereospecific polymerization of -olefins like ethylene and propylene into linear, high-molecular-weight polymers. This heterogeneous system operates via coordination-insertion mechanisms, where the metal center activates the monomer and propagates chain growth with high regioselectivity and tacticity control, yielding products such as isotactic polypropylene and syndiotactic variants. Discovered in 1953 for ethylene polymerization, it enabled the low-pressure synthesis of high-density polyethylene (HDPE), with industrial-scale production commencing by 1955 at facilities like those of Hoechst AG.
Refinements in the 1960s introduced high-activity variants, enhancing catalyst efficiency and reducing production costs for polyolefins, including linear low-density polyethylene (LLDPE) via copolymerization of ethylene with -olefins like 1-butene or 1-hexene. ZieglerNatta systems remain dominant for HDPE and LLDPE manufacturing, accounting for over 80% of linear low-density polyethylene output due to their ability to produce copolymers with tailored molecular weight distributions and branching.
The Ziegler process, distinct from the catalyst, involves the organoaluminum-mediated oligomerization of ethylene to form aluminum alkyl chains, followed by oxidation and hydrolysis to yield linear primary fatty alcohols ranging from C6 to C20. This chain-growth method, initiated with triethylaluminum under moderate pressures (around 50100 bar) and temperatures (100150C), produces unbranched alcohols suitable for detergents and surfactants, contrasting with branched products from oxo processes. Industrially adopted in the late 1950s, it provided an alternative route to natural-derived fatty alcohols, with key implementations by companies like Conoco (now Phillips) for C12C14 fractions.
