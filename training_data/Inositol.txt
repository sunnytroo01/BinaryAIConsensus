Inositol

1x
 Inositol is a group of nine stereoisomers of hexahydroxycyclohexane, a carbocyclic sugar alcohol with the general molecular formula CHO, where myo-inositol is the most abundant isomer in mammalian tissues and the primary biologically active form, while D-chiro-inositol is another key isomer utilized in therapeutic supplementation. Myo-inositol features a cyclohexane ring with hydroxyl groups on each carbon, adopting a chair conformation that facilitates its incorporation into cellular structures and signaling molecules. As a vitamin-like nutrient, it functions primarily as a precursor to phosphatidylinositol, a key component of cell membranes, and inositol phosphates, which serve as second messengers in pathways regulating insulin action, calcium mobilization, and gene expression.
In living organisms, inositol is ubiquitous, derived biosynthetically from glucose and widely distributed in plants and animals, where it supports osmoregulation, growth, and metabolic homeostasis. Humans obtain inositol through dietary intake from sources such as whole grains, beans, nuts (including almonds and walnuts), fruits like cantaloupe and citruses, and vegetables, with typical daily consumption ranging from 0.5 to 1 gram in Western diets. Endogenous production occurs in the kidneys and brain via the enzyme myo-inositol-3-phosphate synthase, converting glucose-6-phosphate, ensuring physiological needs are met even under low dietary conditions.
Beyond its structural roles, inositol exhibits diverse physiological effects, including enhancement of insulin sensitivity and reduction of hyperandrogenism in polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS). Supplementation with myo-inositol (typically 4 g/day) improves insulin sensitivity, menstrual regularity, ovulation, and egg quality in women with PCOS, serving as an alternative to metformin with better tolerability. Combination with D-chiro-inositol in a 40:1 myo-inositol to D-chiro-inositol ratio (e.g., 4 g myo-inositol + 100 mg D-chiro-inositol) is often preferred, as clinical evidence and guidelines indicate it more effectively restores ovarian balance, hormone levels, and metabolic parameters than either isomer alone. While myo-inositol primarily supports insulin signaling and ovarian function, D-chiro-inositol alone or in high doses may impair ovarian function and egg quality. It also contributes to lipid metabolism as a lipotropic agent, preventing fat accumulation in the liver, with clinical studies demonstrating benefits in non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) through reduced liver fat accumulation, lowered serum ALT and AST levels, and improved cardiometabolic factors such as insulin resistance and lipid profiles. It shows promise in managing metabolic syndrome through improvements in glucose tolerance and inflammatory markers. Research highlights its safety as a nutraceutical, with supplementation doses up to 18 grams per day tolerated without significant adverse effects in clinical studies; however, mild side effects including nausea, gas, and difficulty sleeping have been reported at doses of 12 g/day or higher. There is no established best time to take inositol supplements (including combinations with choline), which can generally be taken at any time of day, often recommended with meals to minimize gastrointestinal side effects; for those concerned about potential sleep disturbances at high doses, daytime intake may be preferable.

Chemical Properties

Structure and Isomers

Inositol is a cyclohexanehexol with the molecular formula CHO, characterized by a six-membered carbon ring where each carbon atom bears a hydroxyl group. This structure allows for multiple stereoisomeric forms due to the presence of six chiral centers on the ring.
The possible stereoisomers of inositol total nine, arising from the various configurations of the hydroxyl groups around the ring; seven of these are meso compounds, which are achiral because of an internal plane of symmetry, while the remaining two constitute a pair of enantiomers that exhibit optical activity. Among these, myo-inositol is the predominant and most biologically significant isomer, accounting for approximately 90% of the total inositol in human tissues. In its stable chair conformation, myo-inositol features five hydroxyl groups in equatorial positions and one axial hydroxyl group at the C2 position, conferring a plane of symmetry that renders it meso.
Key examples include D-chiro-inositol, one of the chiral enantiomers lacking a plane of symmetry, which displays optical activity and has hydroxyl group orientations that differ from the meso forms, specifically with axial hydroxyls at positions C1 and C2 in the standard numbering (or equivalent in chair flip). Scyllo-inositol, a meso isomer, adopts a highly stable chair conformation with all six hydroxyl groups in equatorial positions, maximizing symmetry and minimizing steric interactions. The non-meso forms, such as the chiro-inositols, are optically active due to their enantiomeric nature, rotating plane-polarized light in opposite directions for the D and L variants, whereas all meso isomers, including myo- and scyllo-inositol, are optically inactive.

Physical and Chemical Characteristics

Inositol, particularly its predominant myo-isomer, presents as an odorless white crystalline solid at room temperature. Its density is 1.75 g/cm, reflecting a compact molecular packing typical of polyols. The compound melts at 225227 C, above which it decomposes without boiling under standard conditions.
Myo-inositol exhibits high solubility in water, reaching up to 140 g/L at 25 C, which facilitates its role in aqueous biological environments. In contrast, it shows low solubility in most organic solvents, such as ethanol (approximately 2.5 g/L at 25 C) and negligible solubility in non-polar solvents like chloroform or ether.
Chemically, inositol functions as a non-reducing sugar alcohol due to the absence of a free carbonyl group, rendering it stable under conditions that degrade reducing sugars. It demonstrates good thermal and oxidative stability, remaining resistant to mild oxidation but reacting with strong oxidizing agents; it is combustible but non-flammable under normal handling. The six hydroxyl groups have pKa values around 12.6, indicating weak acidity and limited deprotonation at physiological pH, though these groups readily undergo phosphorylation to form inositol phosphates.
Spectroscopic methods are essential for identification. In infrared (IR) spectroscopy, myo-inositol shows a broad O-H stretching band at approximately 3350 cm and C-O stretching vibrations between 1050 and 1150 cm, confirming its polyol structure. Proton nuclear magnetic resonance (H NMR) in DO reveals characteristic multiplets for the methine protons between 3.10 and 3.60 ppm, with a distinct triplet at 3.28 ppm for the axial proton at C-2.
Among isomers, myo-inositol displays greater aqueous solubility compared to scyllo-inositol, whose molar solubility is roughly 8.6 times lower, attributed to differences in hydrogen bonding efficiency. Scyllo-inositol also exhibits enhanced conformational stability in polar solvents due to its higher symmetry, minimizing steric interactions.

History

Discovery and Isolation

In 1850, German chemist and physician Johann Joseph Scherer isolated a novel compound from extracts of muscle tissue, naming it inositol after the Greek word "inos," referring to muscle or sinew, due to its sweet taste reminiscent of a sugar. This marked the first identification of inositol as a distinct substance, initially termed "muscle sugar" for its organ-specific occurrence and organoleptic properties.
Subsequent purification efforts advanced the understanding of inositol's chemical identity. In 1887, French chemist Lon Maquenne obtained crystalline inositol from plant leaves and established its empirical formula as CHO, confirming its structural relation to carbohydrates while distinguishing it from glucose. Maquenne later refined the isolation using boiled horse urine as a source, though this method drew local complaints owing to the odor.
The 1940s brought confirmation of inositol's stereochemical diversity through the systematic studies of Swiss biochemist Th. Posternak in 1942, who elucidated the configurations of its nine possible isomers and identified myo-inositol as the predominant form in biological systems.
Early extraction methods from plant materials focused on abundant sources like corn steep liquor and rice bran, where inositol exists primarily as phytic acid (inositol hexaphosphate). These involved acid hydrolysis of phytate to liberate free inositol, followed by precipitation, filtration, and recrystallization, enabling scalable isolation for research by the mid-20th century.

Recognition as a Biochemical Compound

In the 1920s and 1930s, inositol began to gain attention as a potential essential nutrient due to its role as a growth factor in microorganisms and animals. In 1940, D.W. Woolley demonstrated that inositol was required for optimal growth and prevention of alopecia in mice fed a purified diet, prompting its classification as a member of the vitamin B complex, specifically vitamin B8. However, experiments in the 1940s revealed that mammals, including mice and rats, could synthesize inositol endogenously from glucose, leading to its retraction as an essential vitamin by the mid-20th century.
By the 1950s, research shifted toward inositol's integration into cellular structures, particularly its presence in phospholipids. In 1953, Lowell E. Hokin and Mabel R. Hokin reported the "Hokin effect," observing that acetylcholine stimulation in pigeon pancreas slices rapidly increased the incorporation of radioactive phosphate into phosphatidylinositol, highlighting its dynamic role in cellular responses. This discovery laid foundational insights into inositol lipids' involvement in signal transduction, later contributing to Nobel Prize-recognized advancements in understanding receptor-mediated signaling pathways.
The nomenclature of inositol evolved alongside its biochemical characterization. Coined in 1850 by Johann Joseph Scherer from the Greek word "inos" (meaning muscle fiber or sinew), reflecting its initial isolation from muscle tissue, the term "inositol" was standardized to specify stereoisomers. By the mid-20th century, myo-inositol emerged as the predominant biologically active form, distinguished from other isomers like scyllo- and chiro-inositol through structural analyses.
A pivotal milestone occurred in the 1970s with Robert H. Michell's elucidation of inositol phosphates' functions. In his 1975 review, Michell proposed that receptor activation at the cell surface triggered the hydrolysis of phosphatidylinositol, generating inositol phosphates as second messengers essential for calcium mobilization and cellular signaling. This framework integrated prior observations into a cohesive model of inositol's biochemical significance, influencing decades of subsequent research.

Biosynthesis and Production

Biosynthetic Pathways

In living organisms, myo-inositol is synthesized de novo through a conserved two-step enzymatic pathway beginning with glucose-6-phosphate. The rate-limiting first step is catalyzed by myo-inositol-3-phosphate synthase (MIPS), which isomerizes and cyclizes glucose-6-phosphate to myo-inositol-3-phosphate in an NAD-dependent reaction. The second step involves hydrolysis of myo-inositol-3-phosphate to free myo-inositol by the enzyme inositol monophosphatase (IMPase).

MIPS activity is encoded by the INO1 gene in yeast, where it is essential for viability in the absence of exogenous inositol, and by the ISYNA1 gene in humans, which performs the analogous function.
The biosynthesis is tightly regulated to maintain cellular homeostasis, primarily through feedback mechanisms involving inositol phosphates that inhibit MIPS and IMPase activities, preventing overaccumulation. In yeast, INO1 expression is transcriptionally repressed by high intracellular inositol levels via the Ino2/Ino4/Opi1 regulatory circuit. In mammals, regulation occurs largely at the post-transcriptional level, including phosphorylation of MIPS to modulate its activity in response to metabolic demands. Variations across organisms include transcriptional induction of the iol operon in bacteria under osmotic stress or nutrient limitation, contrasting with the more constitutive expression in mammalian cells.

Industrial Production Methods

The industrial production of myo-inositol, the predominant stereoisomer used commercially, relies mainly on the chemical hydrolysis of phytic acid (myo-inositol hexaphosphate) extracted from agricultural byproducts such as corn steep liquor or rice bran. Phytic acid is isolated through solvent extraction or precipitation, then subjected to acid hydrolysis using concentrated hydrochloric or sulfuric acid under high temperature and pressure conditions, which cleaves the phosphate esters to liberate free myo-inositol along with inorganic phosphates. This process typically yields approximately 3.5% myo-inositol relative to the dry weight of the starting material, though actual recovery varies with source quality and process efficiency.
Purification of the hydrolysate involves multiple steps to achieve pharmaceutical-grade purity exceeding 99%. The mixture is first neutralized and filtered to remove insoluble residues, followed by ion-exchange chromatography using cation and anion resins to separate myo-inositol from salts, sugars, and residual phosphates. The eluate is then concentrated under vacuum and cooled for recrystallization, often from ethanol-water solvents, producing white crystalline myo-inositol. Post-1980s advancements, including automated chromatography systems and optimized crystallization conditions, have improved overall yields to 80-90% from the crude hydrolysate while reducing waste.
Alternative synthetic routes are employed infrequently due to higher costs but include chemical reduction of inositol phosphates using reducing agents like lithium aluminum hydride or catalytic hydrogenation, though these are limited by stereoselectivity challenges. Microbial fermentation represents a promising biotech alternative, where engineered or native strains such as Klebsiella terrigena overexpress phytase enzymes to hydrolyze phytic acid substrates into myo-inositol, often achieving titers up to 100 g/L in fed-batch processes with glucose or starch feedstocks. These methods enhance sustainability by operating under milder conditions and utilizing renewable feedstocks.
As of the 2020s, global myo-inositol production totals approximately 15,000 metric tons annually, with over 80% originating from China, leveraging its vast corn and rice processing industries for low-cost raw materials. Production costs, ranging from $5-10 per kg, are driven by acid consumption, energy for hydrolysis, and purification expenses, while sustainability issues like acid waste generation and high water use have prompted shifts toward enzymatic processes to lower environmental footprints and improve resource efficiency.

Biological Functions

Role in Cell Membranes

Inositol serves as a critical structural component of phosphatidylinositol (PI), a phospholipid integral to eukaryotic cell membranes. Phosphoinositides derived from PI, particularly its phosphorylated form phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PIP), are minor phospholipids in plasma membranes, with PIP comprising approximately 12 mol% of total phospholipids in the cytoplasmic leaflet. PI itself is more abundant in intracellular membranes such as the endoplasmic reticulum. This composition helps maintain membrane integrity and asymmetry, with PI acting as a precursor for more complex phosphoinositides.
One key function of PI and PIP is to regulate membrane fluidity by influencing lipid packing and phase behavior. These lipids create packing defects in phospholipid bilayers, promoting a fluid phase that facilitates protein mobility and membrane curvature essential for cellular processes. Additionally, the inositol headgroup in PI forms the core of glycosylphosphatidylinositol (GPI) anchors, which covalently attach proteins to the outer leaflet of the plasma membrane, enabling their stable surface presentation without transmembrane domains.
PI exhibits enriched distribution in specialized membranes, such as neural myelin sheaths and retinal cells, where it supports insulation and photoreceptor function, respectively. In these tissues, higher PI levels correlate with the demands of rapid signal transmission and structural stability.
The membrane pool of PI undergoes rapid turnover, with a half-life on the order of seconds to minutes for its derivatives like PIP, facilitated by recycling through de novo synthesis pathways that replenish the lipid from inositol and diacylglycerol precursors. This dynamic recycling ensures sustained availability for structural maintenance amid ongoing cellular demands.

Signaling via Inositol Phosphates

Inositol phosphates play a central role in intracellular signaling, particularly through the phosphoinositide signaling pathway that modulates calcium homeostasis and other cellular responses. The process begins with the hydrolysis of phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PIP2), a membrane phospholipid, by phospholipase C (PLC). This enzyme cleaves PIP2 into inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate (IP3) and diacylglycerol (DAG), both of which act as second messengers. IP3 diffuses into the cytosol and binds to IP3 receptors (IP3R) on the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), triggering the release of stored calcium ions (Ca2+) into the cytoplasm. This calcium mobilization initiates a cascade of downstream events, including activation of calmodulin-dependent kinases and other effectors that regulate processes such as muscle contraction, secretion, and gene expression.
The signaling cascade is typically initiated by G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) on the cell surface, which, upon ligand binding, activate heterotrimeric G proteins. The Gq subunit then stimulates PLC isoforms (primarily PLC), leading to PIP2 hydrolysis and the generation of IP3 and DAG. DAG remains membrane-bound and activates protein kinase C (PKC), amplifying the signal, while IP3-mediated Ca2+ release sustains the response. Prior to hydrolysis, PIP2 is synthesized through sequential phosphorylation of phosphatidylinositol (PI), which is localized in cell membranes. This involves type III PI 4-kinases (PI4K) phosphorylating PI at the 4-position to form PI(4)P, followed by type I PI 4-phosphate 5-kinases (PIP5K) adding a phosphate at the 5-position to yield PIP2. The reaction can be represented as:

These kinase activities ensure a dynamic pool of PIP2 available for signaling.
Beyond IP3, higher-order inositol phosphates such as inositol hexakisphosphate (IP6, also known as phytic acid) contribute to signaling networks involved in nuclear processes. IP6 stabilizes protein complexes in non-homologous end-joining (NHEJ) DNA repair pathways and interacts with chromatin-remodeling factors to influence gene expression and epigenetic regulation. For instance, IP6 binds to the Ku70/Ku80 heterodimer, facilitating recruitment of DNA-PKcs for double-strand break repair.
The inositol signaling system is also modulated by therapeutic agents like lithium, which supports the inositol depletion hypothesis. Lithium inhibits inositol monophosphatase (IMPase), the enzyme that dephosphorylates IP1 to free inositol, thereby reducing intracellular inositol levels and attenuating the resynthesis of PIP2. This depletion disrupts the phosphoinositide cycle, particularly in overactive signaling contexts, without directly affecting IP3 production but limiting pathway replenishment.

Physiological Roles

In Metabolism and Insulin Action

Inositol isomers, particularly myo-inositol and D-chiro-inositol, function as second messengers in insulin signaling pathways, mimicking insulin's effects to enhance cellular responses to the hormone. These compounds are generated from the hydrolysis of glycosylphosphatidylinositol anchors upon insulin receptor activation, facilitating downstream metabolic processes. Specifically, D-chiro-inositol-containing inositol phosphoglycan INS-2 activates pyruvate dehydrogenase phosphatase, which dephosphorylates and thereby stimulates pyruvate dehydrogenase activity, promoting the conversion of pyruvate to acetyl-CoA for energy production. This mechanism underscores inositol's role in linking insulin stimulation to mitochondrial glucose oxidation.
Inositol contributes to glycogen synthesis by supporting insulin-mediated glucose uptake and storage in muscle and liver tissues, while also influencing lipogenesis through regulation of lipid anabolic pathways. Deficiency in these inositol forms, often observed in insulin-resistant states, impairs these processes, leading to elevated blood glucose and reduced cellular energy storage efficiency. For instance, altered myo-inositol to D-chiro-inositol ratios in tissues have been associated with diminished insulin sensitivity and metabolic dysfunction.
Supplementation with myo-inositol (MI) and D-chiro-inositol (DCI) has been studied to restore physiological ratios and enhance insulin action. Clinical evidence from randomized trials and meta-analyses supports the use of a 40:1 MI:DCI combination (typically 4 g/day MI with 100 mg/day DCI), which mimics the plasma ratio in healthy individuals and improves insulin sensitivity, reduces markers of insulin resistance such as HOMA-IR, and ameliorates metabolic parameters more effectively than either isomer alone in conditions including insulin resistance and polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS). This combination provides benefits comparable to metformin but with better tolerability, including improvements in glucose control, lipid profiles, and overall metabolic health.
Improved insulin sensitivity from inositol supplementation supports better nutrient partitioning, directing glucose and other nutrients toward muscle tissue and energy storage rather than fat accumulation, and aids in muscle preservation, particularly in states of insulin resistance. Myo-inositol is safe and non-hormonal, with therapeutic doses up to 18 g/day generally well-tolerated without significant adverse effects.
Regarding lipid metabolism, inositol supplementation improves overall profiles by significantly reducing triglycerides (standardized mean difference -1.24), total cholesterol (-1.09), and low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (-1.31) in individuals with metabolic diseases, though effects on high-density lipoprotein cholesterol are not significant. These changes help mitigate dyslipidemia linked to insulin resistance, potentially by decreasing hepatic lipid accumulation and enhancing fatty acid oxidation. In the context of non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), myo-inositol supplementation contributes to reducing hepatic fat accumulation and improving liver function, effects that align with its enhancement of insulin signaling and reduction in hepatic lipid storage. A double-blind, placebo-controlled randomized trial demonstrated that 4 g/day of myo-inositol for 8 weeks significantly lowered alanine aminotransferase (ALT) levels (p=0.027), improved the AST/ALT ratio (p=0.006), and reduced NAFLD severity (33.3% absolute risk reduction for one-grade improvement) in obese patients, with no reported hepatotoxicity and instead evidence of improved liver enzymes.
Animal studies demonstrate that oral administration of myo-inositol or related compounds like D-pinitol enhances glucose uptake in skeletal muscle by stimulating the translocation of glucose transporter 4 (GLUT4) to the plasma membrane, thereby lowering postprandial glucose and insulin levels. In C57BL/6 mice, doses of 1 g/kg body weight increased GLUT4 translocation following glucose challenge, highlighting inositol's potential to bolster insulin-dependent glucose transport.

In Reproduction and Neural Processes

Inositol, particularly the myo-inositol isomer, plays a critical role in reproductive processes, especially in oocyte maturation. It influences meiotic progression in oocytes by enhancing intracellular calcium oscillations, which are essential for germinal vesicle breakdown and maturation. Studies in animal models, such as mice, have shown that myo-inositol supplementation at concentrations like 25 mM significantly boosts oocyte maturation rates while reducing oxidative stress markers, such as oxygen radicals. In human assisted reproduction, myo-inositol improves oocyte quality during in vitro fertilization (IVF) by promoting better follicular development and increasing the number of mature metaphase II oocytes retrieved after ovarian stimulation. This enhancement also leads to higher fertilization rates and superior embryo quality, including improved cleavage rates and reduced gonadotropin requirements. Seminal fluid contains notably high concentrations of myo-inositol, ranging from 2 to 5 g/L in mammalian species, supporting sperm motility and function as it moves through the reproductive tract.
In neural processes, inositol serves as a key precursor in the synthesis of inositol phosphates, which are integral to the signal transduction pathways of neurotransmitters like serotonin, dopamine, and GABA. This role facilitates neurotransmitter release and receptor modulation, contributing to overall brain signaling efficiency. Myo-inositol specifically modulates serotonin 5-HT2 receptors, reducing their induced neuronal responses in neocortical tissue, which helps regulate excitatory signaling. Additionally, inositol is involved in myelination, where it activates phosphoinositide pathways in oligodendrocytes to promote myelin sheath formation during neural development. It is a structural component of myelin sheets and cell membranes in the central nervous system, supporting the integrity and turnover of myelin lipids.
During fetal development, inositol is vital for brain growth and preventing congenital defects. It acts as an osmolyte and marker for neuroglial cells, with its levels increasing in the fetal brain to support astrocytic proliferation and overall neurodevelopment. Myo-inositol and its transporter are abundantly expressed in the developing brain, aiding in membrane phospholipid synthesis essential for neuronal expansion. Supplemental inositol has been shown to prevent neural tube defects (NTDs) in genetic models resistant to folic acid, such as the curly tail mouse, by reducing spinal and cranial NTD incidence through mechanisms involving improved neural tube closure. This protective effect highlights inositol's complementary role to folic acid in mitigating folate-nonresponsive NTD risks during embryogenesis.

Clinical Applications

Metabolic and Reproductive Disorders

In polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), myo-inositol (MI) supplementation at doses of 24 g/day has been shown to improve insulin sensitivity, menstrual regularity, ovulation, and egg quality, as well as reduce hyperandrogenism by modulating insulin-dependent pathways in ovarian theca cells. MI often serves as an alternative to metformin with better tolerability. D-chiro-inositol (DCI) supports glycogen synthesis and may aid some metabolic parameters, but alone or in high doses may impair ovarian function and egg quality. A 2024 meta-analysis to inform the 2023 international evidence-based PCOS guidelines found limited and inconclusive evidence supporting the use of inositol for PCOS management, with potential benefits for ovulation from D-chiro-inositol but no clear effects on androgen levels or menstrual regularity. The 2023 guidelines recommend against routine use of inositol due to limited clinical benefits. More recent reviews and position statements as of 2025-2026 indicate potential benefits particularly for MI or MI:DCI combinations in improving metabolic and reproductive parameters, though evidence quality remains low to moderate. These effects are attributed to myo-inositol's role in second messenger signaling, which corrects the impaired inositol phosphoglycan-mediated insulin action prevalent in PCOS.
For gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM), prophylactic supplementation with myo-inositol, typically starting in the first trimester for high-risk women, has demonstrated a substantial reduction in incidence by 5065% in clinical trials and meta-analyses conducted between 2021 and 2025. This preventive benefit is linked to improved glycemic control, with mixed effects on insulin requirements, without adverse effects on maternal or fetal outcomes.
In thyroid disorders and metabolic syndrome, myo-inositol supplementation, often combined with selenium, improves thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) levels and lipid profiles by enhancing thyroid hormone synthesis and reducing oxidative stress in subclinical hypothyroidism. Additionally, D-chiro-inositol serves as an adjunct in type 2 diabetes management, promoting insulin sensitization and glycemic control when used alongside standard therapies.
In non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), particularly among obese patients, myo-inositol supplementation has shown benefits in a double-blind, placebo-controlled randomized trial. Administration of 4 g/day for 8 weeks significantly reduced serum alanine aminotransferase (ALT) levels (p = 0.027) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST) levels (p = 0.066), improved the AST/ALT ratio (p = 0.006), and achieved a 33.3% absolute risk reduction for a one-grade improvement in steatosis severity (number needed to treat = 3). It also improved insulin resistance (reduced HOMA-IR, p = 0.046), lipid profiles (reductions in total cholesterol, triglycerides, and LDL-cholesterol; increase in HDL-cholesterol), and cardiometabolic factors, with no reported adverse effects. These results indicate that myo-inositol can reduce liver fat accumulation and enhance liver function in NAFLD without evidence of hepatotoxicity; instead, it often improves liver enzyme levels in this context.
The primary mechanisms underlying these benefits involve inositols' enhancement of insulin signaling through activation of glycogen synthase and other downstream effectors, thereby alleviating insulin resistance. In PCOS specifically, the combination of myo-inositol and D-chiro-inositol in a 40:1 molar ratio optimizes physiological plasma levels, restoring ovulation and metabolic balance more effectively than either isomer alone, with modest improvements in insulin resistance, lipids, glucose, and blood pressure based on clinical evidence as of 2025-2026. An example is the supplement Cursol, which contains myo-inositol and d-chiro-inositol in a 40:1 ratio (typically 2000 mg myo-inositol and 50 mg d-chiro-inositol per serving) and is commonly used for PCOS and insulin sensitivity support; other regimens use 4 g myo-inositol + 100 mg d-chiro-inositol daily.
Myo-inositol and D-chiro-inositol are frequently combined with omega-3 fatty acids (from fish oil) and vitamin D in supplement regimens and commercial products targeting PCOS or metabolic benefits, with no significant interactions reported in reliable sources and some studies demonstrating improved outcomes from such combinations. Mild gastrointestinal side effects such as nausea or gas may occur with inositol supplementation, and there is no evidence that inositol causes elevated liver enzymes or hepatotoxicity; conversely, clinical evidence shows improvements in these markers among patients with NAFLD. Individuals should consult a healthcare provider before starting any supplement regimen.

Mental Health Conditions

Myo-inositol has been studied as an adjunctive treatment for depression, with early clinical trials demonstrating potential benefits at doses of 12 g per day. In a double-blind, placebo-controlled trial involving 28 patients with major depression, 12 g daily of myo-inositol for 4 weeks led to significant improvements in Hamilton Depression Rating Scale scores compared to placebo, with response rates indicating clinical efficacy similar to selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) in subsequent comparisons. A 2013 meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials, including studies from the 1990s, confirmed marginal superiority of inositol over placebo for depressive symptoms, particularly in premenstrual dysphoric disorder subsets, though larger trials are needed for broader validation.
For bipolar disorder, myo-inositol at 1218 g per day has shown mixed results as an add-on therapy. A pilot open-label study of 24 adults with bipolar depression on stable mood stabilizers found that 12 g daily for 6 weeks resulted in at least 50% reduction in Montgomery-sberg Depression Rating Scale scores in 8 of 12 completers, suggesting potential adjunctive value without significant adverse effects. Magnetic resonance spectroscopy studies from the early 2000s further support myo-inositol's role in bipolar pathophysiology, with levels altered in manic states and normalized by lithium, aligning with its mechanism of inositol phosphate depletion.
In obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD), myo-inositol at 18 g per day has reduced symptoms in initial trials, potentially through modulation of serotonin signaling similar to SSRIs. A double-blind, placebo-controlled study of 13 patients showed significant decreases in Yale-Brown Obsessive Compulsive Scale scores after 6 weeks of 18 g daily treatment, comparable to SSRI effects via modulation of second messenger pathways. However, a 2023 narrative review of trials from the 1990s to 2020s noted inconsistent replication, with augmentation studies showing no added benefit over SSRIs alone, though a 2013 meta-analysis highlighted trends toward efficacy for impulse-control aspects, including OCD-like compulsions and related behavioral disorders.
For trichotillomania, doses of 18 g per day have demonstrated symptom reduction in small studies. An open-label trial in 5 adults reported marked improvements in hair-pulling behaviors after 612 weeks, with Yale-Brown scores decreasing by over 50% in responders, potentially through enhanced serotonin signaling. Limited evidence from case series supports its use for related impulse-control disorders, consistent with OCD findings.
Myo-inositol at 1218 g per day attenuates anxiety and panic symptoms in controlled trials. A double-blind crossover study of 21 patients with panic disorder found 18 g daily equivalent to fluvoxamine (150 mg) in reducing panic attack frequency and severity over 6 weeks, with fewer side effects. Acute dosing of 12 g has shown rapid attenuation of induced panic in challenge models, supporting its role in acute management. For post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), an early open-label trial of 13 combat veterans using 12 g daily for 4 weeks reported no significant changes in intrusion or avoidance symptoms on the Impact of Event Scale. Emerging biomarker studies from 20222024 link elevated myo-inositol levels in the anterior cingulate cortex to PTSD severity in veterans, suggesting potential therapeutic targets but lacking confirmatory treatment trials.
Evidence for myo-inositol in autism spectrum disorder remains limited and mixed. A 1997 double-blind crossover trial in 9 children using approximately 12 g daily (200 mg/kg) for 12 weeks found no improvements in behavioral symptoms or global functioning. Recent pilot efforts, such as a 2018ongoing open-label trial combining myo-inositol with omega-3 fatty acids and N-acetylcysteine in adolescents with autism and mood issues, suggest possible enhancements in social behaviors, though results are preliminary and not specific to inositol alone.
Scyllo-inositol, a stereoisomer, shows preclinical promise for amyloid clearance in Alzheimer's disease models relevant to cognitive decline in mental health contexts. In transgenic mouse models, scyllo-inositol binds soluble amyloid-beta oligomers, promoting microglial clearance and reducing plaque burden without toxicity. A phase 2 randomized trial in 312 mild-to-moderate Alzheimer's patients using 2,000 mg twice daily for 78 weeks found no overall cognitive benefit but identified responders with slower brain atrophy and stable cerebrospinal fluid biomarkers, supporting further isoform-specific research.
Myo-inositol supplementation has been investigated for potential effects on sleep and insomnia. In a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial involving pregnant women, 2000 mg daily of myo-inositol improved global sleep quality, subjective sleep quality, sleep duration, and sleep efficiency as measured by the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index. Inositol has been used for insomnia but lacks strong scientific evidence for this purpose; WebMD notes no good scientific evidence for insomnia. Evidence for treating general insomnia or providing broad sleep benefits in non-pregnant adults is limited and lacks strong scientific support. There is no established best time to take inositol supplements specifically, and they can generally be taken at any time of day, often with meals to minimize gastrointestinal side effects. High doses of inositol (e.g., 12 g+), as used in some mental health studies, may cause difficulty sleeping in some individuals, suggesting daytime intake might be preferable for those concerned about sleep. Proposed mechanisms may involve modulation of neurotransmitter signaling (e.g., serotonin pathways) as a secondary messenger in the brain, potentially influencing relaxation and sleep regulation, but these are not well-established or confirmed by robust studies.

Industrial and Miscellaneous Uses

Chemical and Pharmaceutical Applications

Inositol is utilized in pharmaceutical formulations as an excipient, particularly in chewable tablets, where its physical properties such as solubility and compressibility make it suitable as a base material comparable to mannitol. It also serves as a precursor for inositol niacinate (also known as inositol hexanicotinate), a compound employed as a lipid-lowering agent that reduces plasma triglycerides and atherogenic apolipoprotein B levels while minimizing some side effects associated with other niacin derivatives. Additionally, myo-inositol is a critical component in cell culture media for both mammalian and plant cells, acting as a supplement that supports proliferation, metabolism, and growth by facilitating phospholipid synthesis and signaling pathways.
In the food industry, inositol is incorporated into fortified products, notably infant formulas, to provide essential nutritional support mimicking levels found in breast milk and meeting regulatory requirements for early development. It functions as a fermentation aid in processes involving sugar matrices, where supplementation enhances the yield and efficiency of myo-inositol production during microbial fermentation, benefiting applications in functional foods.
Historically, inositol underwent nitration to produce inositol hexanitrate, a high explosive similar to pentaerythritol tetranitrate (PETN) in sensitivity and power, which was explored for use in dynamite and blasting formulations during the World War II era but did not gain widespread adoption postwar due to superior alternatives. In contemporary nutraceutical applications, inositol is standardized in dietary supplements at doses of 24 g per day, leveraging its role in metabolic and reproductive health support. Additionally, myo-inositol has been investigated as a biobased additive in polymer formulations to improve thermo-oxidative stability and crystallization kinetics, as reported in studies from 2025.

Environmental and Illicit Uses

Inositol and its derivatives, particularly phytic acid (inositol hexaphosphate), have been incorporated into road de-icing formulations as anti-corrosion agents to reduce the environmental and infrastructural damage from traditional chloride salts like sodium chloride (NaCl), with testing in the 2010s showing enhanced ice melting and reduced corrosion. Corn-derived de-icing mixtures including inositol with other polyols like erythritol and chloride salts have demonstrated effective performance in lowering freezing points while addressing the persistence of chloride salts. Additionally, inositol-based products are utilized in corrosion-free de-icing of aircraft frames, providing eco-friendly options for aviation maintenance.
Phytic acid serves as a natural chelating agent for heavy metal removal in wastewater treatment, forming stable complexes with ions such as aluminum, iron, and lead to facilitate their precipitation from acidic leachates. This application exploits phytic acid's six phosphate groups to bind metals selectively, aiding in the remediation of industrial effluents with minimal secondary pollution.
Recent research since the 2020s has investigated myo-inositol supplementation to boost microalgal biomass and lipid production for biofuel applications, such as in Chlorella vulgaris cultures where it enhances biodiesel yields under nutrient-limited conditions like molasses wastewater. For instance, optimal inositol concentrations significantly promote lipid accumulation while supporting overall growth, positioning it as a sustainable additive in algal biofuel processes.
In illicit contexts, inositol has been reported as a common cutting agent in MDMA (ecstasy) since the 1990s, valued for mimicking the drug's crystalline texture and bitter taste without altering psychoactive effects; such adulteration persists into the 2020s in festival and street samples. Detection typically involves nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy to identify inositol alongside MDMA in seized powders.
Overall, inositol exhibits low environmental toxicity, with an oral LD50 exceeding 10,000 mg/kg in mice, and is classified as readily biodegradable under OECD Guideline 301B, degrading over 94% within 28 days in standard tests.
