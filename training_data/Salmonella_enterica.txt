 Salmonella enterica is a species of Gram-negative, facultatively anaerobic, rod-shaped bacterium belonging to the family Enterobacteriaceae, typically measuring 25 m in length and 0.51.5 m in width, and characterized by motility via peritrichous flagella as well as O, H, and sometimes Vi antigens. This ubiquitous pathogen comprises over 2,600 distinct serovars, making it one of the most diverse bacterial species and a leading cause of bacterial foodborne illnesses globally.
The taxonomy of S. enterica recognizes two main species within the genus SalmonellaS. enterica and S. bongoriwith S. enterica further subdivided into six subspecies, of which subspecies I (also called S. enterica subsp. enterica) accounts for nearly all infections in humans and warm-blooded animals. Nontyphoidal serovars, such as Enteritidis and Typhimurium, primarily cause self-limiting gastroenteritis known as salmonellosis, while typhoidal serovars like Typhi and Paratyphi lead to systemic infections including typhoid and paratyphoid fevers. Transmission occurs mainly through the fecal-oral route via contaminated food and water, with common sources including poultry, eggs, meat, produce, and reptiles.
Epidemiologically, nontyphoidal S. enterica infections result in an estimated 73.9 million cases of diarrheal gastroenteritis and approximately 62,000 deaths from diarrheal forms annually worldwide (as of 2019), plus around 500,000 invasive cases and 70,000 additional deaths, ranking it among the top four causes of diarrheal diseases. In the United States, Salmonella causes approximately 1.35 million infections each year (including non-foodborne), with domestically acquired foodborne cases estimated at 1.28 million infections, 12,500 hospitalizations, and 238 deaths (as of 2019), predominantly from sources like chicken and pork. Typhoidal strains affect approximately 9.3 million people globally per year (as of 2021), with higher burdens in regions with poor sanitation, such as South Asia. Antibiotic resistance is an emerging concern, particularly in multidrug-resistant strains, complicating treatment and underscoring the need for enhanced surveillance and prevention strategies.

Taxonomy and Nomenclature

Classification

Salmonella enterica belongs to the domain Bacteria, phylum Pseudomonadota, class Gammaproteobacteria, order Enterobacterales, family Enterobacteriaceae, genus Salmonella, and species enterica. The genus Salmonella includes only two species: S. enterica and S. bongori, with S. enterica comprising over 99% of all Salmonella isolates.
Historically, the nomenclature of Salmonella underwent significant revision in 2005, when the International Committee on Systematics of Prokaryotes approved the unification of the genus under S. enterica for the majority of serovars that had previously been classified as distinct species. For instance, what was formerly known as S. typhimurium became S. enterica serovar Typhimurium. This change aimed to standardize taxonomy while adhering to the rules of the International Code of Nomenclature of Prokaryotes.
S. enterica is further divided into six subspecies: enterica (I), salamae (II), arizonae (IIIa), diarizonae (IIIb), houtenae (IV), and indica (VI). Subspecies enterica (I) includes the vast majority of strains pathogenic to humans and warm-blooded animals. Over 2,600 serovars have been identified within S. enterica through variation in O, H, and sometimes Vi antigens.

Serovars and Subspecies

Salmonella enterica exhibits significant intraspecies diversity, primarily classified into serovars using the Kauffmann-White-Le Minor scheme, which identifies strains based on their somatic (O) antigens from the lipopolysaccharide layer and flagellar (H) antigens from the flagella. This serological classification, maintained by the World Health Organization Collaborating Centre for Reference and Research on Salmonella, encompasses over 2,600 recognized serovars across the species. The H antigens display phase variation, allowing bacteria to switch between phase 1 (primary) and phase 2 (secondary) forms, which is denoted in antigenic formulas when applicable.
Nomenclature for serovars follows specific conventions: well-established serovars, particularly those in subspecies enterica, receive trivial names such as S. enterica subsp. enterica serovar Typhi, while less common ones are designated by their full antigenic formulas, including O group, O factors in brackets or commas, H phase 1, and H phase 2 if present. For example, S. enterica subsp. enterica serovar Typhimurium has the antigenic formula 1,4,,12:i:1,2, indicating O antigens 1, 4, and 5 (with 5 variable), H phase 1 antigen i, and H phase 2 antigens 1,2. This system ensures precise identification without italicizing serovar names and capitalizing them as proper nouns.
Among the serovars, several are prominent in human infections, including Typhimurium (associated with nontyphoidal gastroenteritis), Typhi (causing typhoid fever), Paratyphi A, B, and C (linked to paratyphoid fever), and Enteritidis (involved in foodborne outbreaks). These primarily belong to serogroups B, C, and D.
The six subspecies of S. enterica show uneven distribution of serovars, with subsp. enterica (I) hosting nearly 60% of all serovars (nearly 1,500 out of over 2,600 as of 2024), including most major human pathogens and being prevalent in warm-blooded animals. In contrast, subsp. salamae (II) comprises several hundred serovars mainly from cold-blooded hosts and the environment, while subsp. arizonae (IIIa), diarizonae (IIIb), and houtenae (IV) are more commonly associated with reptiles and other cold-blooded animals, each with dozens to hundreds of serovars; subsp. indica (VI) has a small number of serovars. This distribution reflects ecological adaptations within the species. The total number and distribution continue to evolve as new serovars are identified and classified by the WHO Collaborating Centre.

Morphology and Physiology

Physical Characteristics

Salmonella enterica is a Gram-negative, rod-shaped bacterium, classified as a bacillus with typical dimensions of 0.71.5 m in width and 25 m in length, often observed as single rods or in pairs under microscopic examination.
The cells are motile due to peritrichous flagella distributed over the surface, enabling movement in liquid environments, and they are non-spore-forming, lacking the ability to produce endospores for dormancy.
As a Gram-negative organism, S. enterica possesses a thin peptidoglycan layer in its cell wall, overlaid by an outer membrane containing lipopolysaccharide (LPS), which serves as an endotoxin responsible for inflammatory responses during infection.
Surface structures include fimbriae or pili that facilitate adhesion to host cells, though most strains lack a true polysaccharide capsule; an exception is the Vi antigen, a capsular polysaccharide present in S. enterica serovar Typhi, which aids in immune evasion.

Growth and Metabolism

Salmonella enterica is a facultative anaerobe that preferentially utilizes aerobic respiration when oxygen is available, enabling efficient energy production through the electron transport chain. Under anaerobic conditions, such as within host tissues or low-oxygen environments, it shifts to mixed-acid fermentation, primarily via the Embden-Meyerhof-Parnas pathway, yielding products including lactate, acetate, succinate, and formate. This metabolic flexibility allows S. enterica to adapt to varying redox states, supporting growth in diverse niches like the inflamed gut where it exploits host-derived substrates such as lactate for respiration.
Optimal growth occurs at temperatures between 35C and 37C, aligning with mammalian body temperature, and at a pH range of 6.5 to 7.5, where cellular processes like enzyme activity are maximized. The bacterium can survive refrigeration at 4C for extended periods, facilitating persistence in chilled food products, but it is effectively inactivated by pasteurization at 71.7C for 15 seconds, a standard thermal treatment in food processing. These tolerances underscore S. enterica's resilience in fluctuating environmental conditions while highlighting vulnerabilities to targeted interventions.
Nutritionally, S. enterica thrives on minimal media supplemented with glucose as the sole carbon source, demonstrating its auxotrophic simplicity and ability to synthesize essential biomolecules from basic substrates. It ferments glucose, mannitol, and maltose, producing acid and gas, but does not ferment lactose or sucrose, aiding in its differentiation from other enteric bacteria. Additionally, it produces hydrogen sulfide (HS) on selective media and is indole-negative, reflecting specific enzymatic pathways in sulfur metabolism and tryptophan degradation.
Biofilm formation enhances S. enterica's persistence by allowing attachment to abiotic surfaces such as stainless steel or plastic in food processing equipment, where extracellular matrices shield cells from sanitizers and desiccation. This structured community growth promotes survival for months to years, contributing to recurrent contamination in industries like poultry and produce handling.

Habitat and Ecology

Natural Reservoirs

Salmonella enterica is a zoonotic pathogen that primarily persists through asymptomatic carriage in the gastrointestinal tracts of various animals, serving as its natural reservoirs. These include livestock such as poultry, cattle, and pigs; reptiles like turtles, tortoises, and lizards; and wild animals including birds and rodents. Among reptiles, Salmonella bacteria are commonly found in species including turtles and tortoises, even in healthy-looking animals, although not all individuals carry the bacteria. Many reptiles harbor the bacteria in their digestive tracts and can shed it intermittently in feces, making them a frequent source of zoonotic infection. Infected animals often exhibit no clinical signs but shed the bacteria in feces, facilitating transmission to humans via the fecal-oral route.
Poultry represents a major reservoir for S. enterica, particularly in chickens and turkeys, where serovars such as Enteritidis and Typhimurium can colonize the reproductive organs, including ovaries, leading to internal contamination of eggs. This vertical transmission allows the pathogen to persist in flocks without overt disease, contributing significantly to zoonotic spread.
In contrast, certain serovars like Typhi have adapted specifically to humans and do not establish reservoirs in animals, relying instead on human-to-human transmission cycles. Domestic pets, such as turtles, also act as reservoirs; their role in outbreaks prompted the U.S. Food and Drug Administration to ban the sale of small turtles with shells less than 4 inches in length in 1975.

Environmental Survival

Salmonella enterica exhibits remarkable resilience to various abiotic stresses encountered outside host environments, enabling its persistence and transmission through contaminated food, water, and soil. A key adaptation is the acid tolerance response (ATR), which is triggered by exposure to mildly acidic conditions (pH around 4.5) and upregulates approximately 50 acid shock proteins to enhance survival at extreme gastric pH levels of 2 to 4. This response primarily relies on the glutamate decarboxylase system, which consumes protons by decarboxylating exogenous glutamate to produce gamma-aminobutyric acid, thereby maintaining intracellular pH homeostasis and conferring protection against lethal acidity. The ATR is adaptive, as pre-exposure to sublethal acid stress significantly boosts viability during subsequent challenges, facilitating oral transmission from contaminated sources.
In environments with low water availability, such as dried foods and surfaces, S. enterica withstands desiccation and osmotic stress, surviving water activities (a_w) as low as 0.94 or below for extended periods. This tolerance involves the accumulation of compatible solutes like betaine, which stabilize cellular proteins and membranes against dehydration-induced damage, particularly during prolonged exposure in hyperosmotic conditions. Such adaptations allow the bacterium to persist in low-moisture foods like peanut butter or spices, where it can remain viable for months, posing risks during rehydration or consumption.
Thermal stresses are also managed effectively; for heat, decimal reduction times (D-values, the time required for a 90% population reduction) at 60C typically range from 1 to 5 minutes, varying with food matrix and strain, though desiccation-adapted cells show increased resistance. Conversely, S. enterica demonstrates cold tolerance, surviving for months in refrigerated foods, soil, and water at 4C, with populations declining slowly over time due to metabolic slowdown rather than rapid inactivation. These traits underscore its environmental hardiness beyond primary animal intestinal reservoirs.
Bile resistance further aids survival in the gallbladder during transient colonization, mediated by multidrug efflux pumps such as AcrAB-TolC, which actively expel toxic bile salts from the cytoplasm to prevent membrane disruption and oxidative damage. This pump system is crucial for withstanding bile concentrations encountered in the biliary tract, supporting persistence that can contribute to fecal shedding and environmental contamination.

Genetics and Molecular Biology

Genome Organization

The genome of Salmonella enterica consists of a single circular chromosome that typically ranges in size from 4.5 to 4.9 million base pairs (Mb), encoding approximately 4,300 to 4,600 genes, with a G+C content of 51-52%. This chromosomal structure is conserved across serovars, providing the foundational genetic framework for essential cellular functions such as metabolism, replication, and basic physiology. The chromosome is organized into functional regions, including the origin of replication (oriC) and ribosomal RNA operons, which support efficient gene expression and cellular division.
Accessory genetic elements significantly contribute to the genomic plasticity of S. enterica, including plasmids, prophages, and genomic islands. Plasmids, such as the ~94 kb pSLT virulence plasmid found in serovar Typhimurium, carry genes that enhance survival and host interaction, though they are absent or varied in other serovars. Prophages, integrated bacteriophage genomes, can comprise up to 5% of the total genome content and facilitate the acquisition of novel traits through lysogenic conversion. Genomic islands, notably the Salmonella pathogenicity islands (SPIs) such as SPI-1 through SPI-5, are clustered regions of ~10-150 kb that encode specialized functions, often acquired via horizontal gene transfer and distinguished by atypical G+C content.
Serovar-specific genomic variations arise primarily from horizontal gene transfer mediated by phages and other mobile elements, driving antigenic diversity through the exchange of surface antigen loci like those for O- and H-antigens. The S. enterica pan-genome, encompassing genes from multiple strains, features a core genome of approximately 2,800 genes shared across all isolates, representing conserved housekeeping functions, while the accessory genome includes thousands of variable genes that account for serovar-specific adaptations and pathogenicity. This open pan-genome structure allows for ongoing expansion through gene acquisition, contributing to the species' adaptability.
Chromosome replication in S. enterica initiates bidirectionally from the oriC locus and proceeds to the terminus, completing in about 40-60 minutes under optimal laboratory conditions, with a generation time of roughly 20-30 minutes in nutrient-rich media at 37C. This rapid replication supports the bacterium's fast growth and proliferation in host environments.

DNA Repair Systems

Salmonella enterica employs several DNA repair systems to maintain genomic integrity, particularly under oxidative and genotoxic stresses encountered during infection and environmental persistence. These systems include base excision repair (BER), recombinational repair, mismatch repair (MMR), and the SOS response, each addressing specific types of DNA damage to ensure bacterial survival and adaptation.
Base excision repair (BER) in S. enterica primarily counters oxidative and alkylative damage by removing modified bases through DNA glycosylases, such as Ung, which excises uracil from cytosine deamination, and Fpg, which targets oxidized guanines like 8-oxoG. These glycosylases create abasic (AP) sites, which are then cleaved by AP endonucleases like XthA or Nfo, followed by gap filling via DNA polymerase I and ligation by DNA ligase. BER is crucial for repairing damage from host-derived reactive nitrogen species, such as nitric oxide during macrophage residence, and bile salts in the gastrointestinal tract, where mutants lacking key BER components exhibit heightened sensitivity and reduced virulence.
Recombinational repair in S. enterica relies on the RecBCD pathway to process double-strand breaks (DSBs), which arise from replication fork collapse or environmental stressors. The RecBCD enzyme complex, comprising RecB helicase/nuclease, RecC, and RecD, binds DSB ends, unwinds and resects DNA to generate single-stranded tails, and loads RecA to facilitate homologous recombination with an intact template. This pathway is essential for bacterial survival within macrophages, where DSBs impair replication, and contributes to tolerance of bile exposure by enabling repair of genotoxic lesions. Mutants defective in recBCD show severe attenuation in systemic infection models, underscoring its role in pathogenesis.
Mismatch repair (MMR) corrects base mismatches and small insertion/deletion loops arising from DNA replication errors in S. enterica. The system initiates with MutS recognizing the mismatch, followed by recruitment of MutL, which activates MutH endonuclease to nick the unmethylated strand, enabling excision and resynthesis. Defects in mutS or mutL, common in hypermutator strains, elevate mutation rates by 10^2- to 10^3-fold, accelerating the evolution of antibiotic resistance across multiple classes without relying on secondary mutations. Such MMR-deficient isolates pose heightened public health risks due to enhanced adaptability.
The SOS response in S. enterica is a global regulon activated by single-stranded DNA accumulation from damage, where RecA binds and promotes autocleavage of LexA repressor, derepressing over 40 genes. This induces error-prone DNA polymerases, including Pol V (UmuDC), which performs translesion synthesis across damaged templates to resume replication, albeit with increased mutagenesis. SOS activation enhances survival under genotoxic conditions like antibiotic exposure or host defenses but drives resistance evolution by elevating mutation rates. In prophage-bearing strains, SOS also triggers lytic cycles, influencing bacterial fitness.

Small Noncoding RNAs

In Salmonella enterica, small noncoding RNAs (sRNAs) serve as key post-transcriptional regulators of gene expression, enabling rapid adaptation to environmental stresses and host interactions. Deep sequencing of the S. enterica serovar Typhi transcriptome has identified 97 novel non-protein-coding RNA candidates (npcRNAs), expanding the known sRNA repertoire beyond previously annotated examples. These sRNAs, typically 50500 nucleotides in length, are often encoded in intergenic regions or within pathogenicity islands and exhibit condition-specific expression, such as during stationary phase or stress exposure. Recent studies as of 2025 have identified additional novel sRNAs, such as SaaS, that regulate virulence during infection.
Representative sRNAs illustrate their diverse regulatory functions. RyhB homologs (RyhB-1 and RyhB-2) modulate iron homeostasis by repressing genes involved in iron acquisition and storage under iron-limiting conditions, thereby conserving cellular iron resources during infection. DsrA enhances acid resistance by stabilizing the mRNA of the RpoS sigma factor, which activates acid tolerance pathways essential for survival in the host gastrointestinal tract. Similarly, IsrA inhibits biofilm formation by downregulating genes associated with surface adhesion and matrix production, potentially limiting persistent colonization in non-host environments.
Mechanistically, sRNAs in S. enterica primarily function through antisense base-pairing with target mRNAs, which either blocks ribosome binding to inhibit translation or recruits ribonucleases to promote mRNA degradation. This process is frequently chaperoned by the Sm-like protein Hfq, which stabilizes sRNA-mRNA duplexes and facilitates interactions; Hfq mutants exhibit widespread dysregulation of sRNA targets. Collectively, these sRNAs influence the expression of approximately 1020% of the bacterial genome, affecting hundreds of transcripts involved in metabolism, stress responses, and virulence. For instance, SgrS counters glucose-phosphate stress during intracellular replication by repressing phosphotransferase system components and promoting sugar efflux, thereby preventing toxic accumulation in the host cytosol. MgrR, induced under envelope stress, represses outer membrane porin synthesis to maintain membrane integrity against antimicrobial peptides.
Pathogenic serovars of S. enterica, such as Typhimurium and Enteritidis, display enhanced sRNA diversity compared to non-pathogenic strains, with additional loci often located in Salmonella pathogenicity islands (SPIs). This expanded repertoire enables fine-tuned virulence adaptation, such as coordinating invasion effectors or modulating host immune evasion, underscoring the evolutionary role of sRNAs in pathogenicity.

Pathogenesis

Virulence Factors

Salmonella enterica possesses a repertoire of virulence factors that enable it to colonize hosts and evade immune responses. These factors include secretion systems, adhesins, toxins, effector proteins, and surface polysaccharides, many of which are encoded on pathogenicity islands within the bacterial genome.
The primary virulence determinants are encoded on Salmonella pathogenicity islands (SPIs), which are genomic regions acquired through horizontal gene transfer. SPI-1, located at centisome 63 of the chromosome, encodes a type III secretion system (T3SS-1) that facilitates bacterial invasion of host epithelial cells by injecting effector proteins. Key effectors from SPI-1 include SipA, which promotes actin polymerization and stabilizes bacterial attachment during entry, and SopE, a guanine nucleotide exchange factor that activates Cdc42 and Rac1 to induce membrane ruffling.
In contrast, SPI-2 encodes another T3SS (T3SS-2) essential for intracellular survival and systemic dissemination within host cells, particularly macrophages. This system translocates effectors that modify the Salmonella-containing vacuole (SCV) to prevent lysosomal fusion and promote nutrient acquisition. A critical SPI-2 effector is SifA, which interacts with host proteins like SKIP and kinesin to maintain SCV integrity and positioning near the perinuclear region.
Adhesins such as type 1 fimbriae, encoded by the fim operon, mediate initial attachment to host mucosal surfaces and contribute to biofilm formation on abiotic surfaces. Toxins include hemolysin E (HlyE), a pore-forming cytolysin that disrupts host cell membranes and aids in tissue damage during systemic infection. The lipopolysaccharide (LPS) O-antigen polysaccharide confers resistance to serum complement and antimicrobial peptides, enhancing bacterial persistence in the host bloodstream.
Approximately 30-40 effector proteins are translocated by the SPI-1 and SPI-2 T3SS into host cells to manipulate cytoskeletal dynamics, signal transduction, and immune responses. For instance, AvrA, an acetyltransferase effector secreted by both T3SS, inhibits NF-B activation and reduces inflammatory cytokine production, thereby dampening host innate immunity. These effectors collectively reprogram host cell functions to favor bacterial replication and dissemination.
In certain serovars like S. enterica serovar Typhi, the Vi capsular polysaccharide serves as a major virulence factor by shielding the bacterium from opsonization and phagocytosis. The Vi antigen, a linear -1,4-N-acetylgalactosaminuronic acid polymer, inhibits complement deposition and antibody binding, promoting survival in the bloodstream during typhoid fever. Expression of Vi is regulated by the tviBCDE locus on a pathogenicity island distinct from SPIs. Additionally, the typhoid toxin, an AB-type genotoxin composed of CdtB (the catalytic subunit) and PltA/PltB (binding subunits), is secreted via a specialized type III secretion system from within SCVs. It induces DNA double-strand breaks, leading to cell cycle arrest and apoptosis in host cells, contributing to the systemic manifestations of typhoid fever.

Mechanisms of Infection

Salmonella enterica primarily infects the host through the gastrointestinal tract, where it attaches to and invades the intestinal mucosa. The bacterium targets microfold (M) cells overlying Peyer's patches in the small intestine, using long polar fimbriae (Lpf) encoded by the lpf operon to facilitate adherence. Once attached, S. enterica employs the Salmonella pathogenicity island 1 (SPI-1)-encoded type III secretion system (T3SS-1) to inject effector proteins, such as SopE, SopE2, SopB, SipA, and SipC, directly into host cells. These effectors activate Rho GTPases, leading to actin cytoskeleton rearrangement and membrane ruffling, which promotes bacterial uptake via macropinocytosis without causing significant cell death.
Following invasion, S. enterica resides within a specialized membrane-bound compartment known as the Salmonella-containing vacuole (SCV). Formation of the SCV is initiated by T3SS-1 effectors like SopB and SptP, which modify host vesicular trafficking to prevent immediate degradation. To ensure long-term intracellular survival, the bacterium activates the SPI-2-encoded T3SS-2, which secretes effectors such as SifA and SopD2 that maintain SCV integrity and actively avoid fusion with lysosomes by altering interactions with late endosomes. This protective niche allows S. enterica to replicate within macrophages, typically beginning 46 hours post-invasion and expanding the bacterial population intracellularly.
In typhoidal serovars such as S. Typhi and S. Paratyphi, the infection progresses to systemic dissemination, with bacteria entering the bloodstream and lymphatic system to colonize the reticuloendothelial organs like the liver, spleen, and bone marrow. In contrast, nontyphoidal serovars like S. Typhimurium generally cause localized gastroenteritis confined to the gut, with systemic spread occurring in less than 5% of cases, primarily in immunocompromised individuals. To support persistence, S. enterica modulates the host environment by inducing inflammation, which generates electron acceptors such as tetrathionate from the oxidation of thiosulfate by host cells; the bacterium then utilizes tetrathionate as a terminal electron acceptor in anaerobic respiration for enhanced growth in the inflamed gut. Additionally, effectors including SopB and AvrA inhibit host cell apoptosis by blocking pathways like JNK and TNF--induced NF-B signaling, thereby prolonging the survival of infected cells and bacterial replication.

Epidemiology

Global Burden

Salmonella enterica imposes a substantial global health burden, causing an estimated 93.8 million cases of gastroenteritis and 155,000 deaths annually worldwide as of 2018. Nontyphoidal serovars account for the majority, with approximately 80.3 million foodborne cases, while typhoidal serovars contribute approximately 9 million cases, predominantly in endemic regions. These infections primarily occur through contaminated food, underscoring the pathogen's role in foodborne disease.
Vulnerable populations face heightened risks from S. enterica infections. Children under 5 years, adults over 65 years, and immunocompromised individuals experience more severe outcomes, including invasive disease with higher hospitalization and mortality rates. Incidence is substantially elevated in low- and middle-income countries compared to high-income settings due to factors like limited sanitation and healthcare access.
Serovar distribution varies geographically, reflecting differences in transmission dynamics and host factors. In developed countries, S. Enteritidis and S. Typhimurium dominate nontyphoidal infections, often linked to poultry and eggs. In contrast, S. Typhi prevails in Asia and sub-Saharan Africa for typhoidal fever, while emerging monophasic variants of S. Typhimurium (such as 4,,12:i:-) are increasingly reported worldwide, contributing to multidrug-resistant outbreaks.
The economic toll of S. enterica is considerable, particularly in high-income nations with robust surveillance. In the United States, annual costs from outbreaks, medical care, and productivity losses are estimated at approximately $17 billion as of 2023. These figures highlight the need for targeted interventions to mitigate both health and financial impacts.

Transmission Routes

Salmonella enterica primarily spreads to humans through the fecal-oral route, with the majority of infections attributed to foodborne transmission. Approximately 66% of salmonellosis cases result from direct consumption of contaminated food, including poultry such as chicken and eggs, pork products, and produce like tomatoes irrigated with contaminated water. Common contamination occurs during animal slaughter, processing, or inadequate cooking, leading to outbreaks; for instance, a 2024 multistate outbreak in the United States linked to imported cucumbers sickened over 68 people and prompted a nationwide recall by SunFed Produce LLC.
Waterborne transmission accounts for a smaller proportion of cases, primarily in developing regions where fecal contamination of drinking water sources is prevalent due to poor sanitation infrastructure. In areas with treated municipal water supplies, such incidents are rare, though recreational exposure to contaminated surface waters can occasionally lead to infection.
Direct or indirect contact with animals serves as another key transmission pathway, particularly involving pet reptiles, including tortoises and turtles, and amphibians. While not all pet tortoises or other reptiles carry Salmonella, the bacteria are commonly found in their gastrointestinal tracts, even in healthy-appearing animals that show no symptoms. Reported carriage rates are high, up to 90% in some studies, and shedding occurs intermittently in feces without clinical signs in the animals. This makes handling pet reptiles a frequent source of zoonotic infection. Health authorities recommend treating all reptiles as potential carriers and practicing strict hygiene measures, such as thorough handwashing with soap after contact with the animals, their enclosures, or related items. Person-to-person spread also occurs, notably in settings like childcare facilities where poor hand hygiene facilitates transmission among young children.
Travel-related cases contribute to the spread, often involving infections acquired in endemic areas with higher prevalence and imported back to low-incidence countries; additionally, vehicle-to-vehicle transmission can occur within global food supply chains through contaminated transport equipment or cross-contamination during international shipping.

Clinical Aspects

Diseases Caused

Salmonella enterica serovars cause a range of diseases in humans, primarily differentiated by whether they are nontyphoidal or typhoidal. Nontyphoidal serovars, such as S. Enteritidis and S. Typhimurium, typically lead to acute gastroenteritis known as nontyphoidal salmonellosis. Symptoms appear 12 to 72 hours after ingestion of contaminated food or water, manifesting as diarrhea, fever, and abdominal cramps. The illness generally lasts 4 to 7 days and resolves without treatment in healthy individuals, though approximately 5% of cases may progress to bacteremia, particularly in vulnerable populations such as the young, elderly, or immunocompromised.
Typhoidal serovars, including S. Typhi and S. Paratyphi, cause systemic infections like typhoid fever and paratyphoid fever. These diseases have an incubation period of 7 to 14 days, followed by sustained high fever, headache, malaise, and relative bradycardia. Additional signs may include rose-colored spots on the trunk, hepatosplenomegaly, and abdominal discomfort. Without intervention, complications such as intestinal perforation, gastrointestinal bleeding, and encephalopathy can arise in 10% to 15% of untreated cases.
Invasive nontyphoidal salmonellosis (iNTS) represents a severe form of infection by nontyphoidal serovars, often resulting in bacteremia without prominent gastrointestinal symptoms. This condition is particularly prevalent among individuals with HIV/AIDS or malnutrition in sub-Saharan Africa, where it carries a high mortality rate of 20% to 25%.
Asymptomatic carriage occurs in 1% to 5% of individuals following S. enterica infection, serving as a reservoir for ongoing transmission. In typhoid fever cases, chronic carriage is frequently associated with gallbladder colonization, especially in the presence of gallstones, allowing persistent bacterial shedding in feces.

Diagnosis and Treatment

Diagnosis of Salmonella enterica infections primarily relies on laboratory confirmation, as clinical symptoms such as diarrhea, fever, and abdominal pain can overlap with other enteric pathogens. The gold standard method is microbiological culture, which involves isolating the bacterium from clinical specimens like stool, blood, urine, or other relevant sites depending on the infection type; for nontyphoidal salmonellosis, stool culture is most common, while blood culture is preferred for invasive or typhoid cases. Culture typically requires 48-72 hours for results, allowing for serotyping and antimicrobial susceptibility testing to guide therapy and track outbreaks.
Molecular methods, such as polymerase chain reaction (PCR), offer faster detection, often within hours, by targeting specific genes like invA, which encodes an invasion protein essential for S. enterica pathogenicity; real-time PCR assays are increasingly used for rapid screening in clinical and food safety settings, though positive results require confirmatory culture for isolate characterization. Serological tests, including the Widal test for typhoid fever, detect antibodies against S. Typhi O and H antigens but have limited specificity (50-70%) due to cross-reactivity with other infections and prior vaccinations, making them unreliable as standalone diagnostics in endemic areas.
Treatment for most S. enterica infections, particularly nontyphoidal gastroenteritis, is supportive, as the illness is self-limiting in healthy individuals within 4-7 days; key measures include oral rehydration to prevent dehydration, electrolyte replacement (e.g., using oral rehydration salts), and antipyretics for fever, while anti-diarrheal agents like loperamide should be avoided in cases of bloody diarrhea or high fever to prevent toxin retention. Antibiotics are reserved for severe, invasive, or systemic infections, high-risk patients (e.g., infants, elderly, immunocompromised), or bacteremia; for nontyphoidal invasive disease, options include fluoroquinolones like ciprofloxacin (500 mg orally twice daily for 7-10 days) or third-generation cephalosporins like ceftriaxone (2 g IV daily for 10-14 days), while azithromycin (1 g orally on day 1, then 500 mg daily for 6 days) is an alternative for uncomplicated cases. For typhoid fever caused by S. Typhi, azithromycin or ceftriaxone are first-line due to rising resistance, with treatment durations of 7-14 days depending on severity.
Antimicrobial resistance complicates therapy, with multidrug-resistant (MDR) strains resistant to ampicillin, chloramphenicol, and trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole prevalent globally, and extensively drug-resistant (XDR) S. Typhi emerging since 2016 in Pakistan, showing additional resistance to fluoroquinolones and ceftriaxone. Similar ceftriaxone-resistant strains have since been reported in Bangladesh and India, with outbreaks noted in 2024. Fluoroquinolone resistance in S. Typhi has risen sharply in Asia, exceeding 70% in countries like India and Bangladesh by 2024, prompting shifts to alternative agents and susceptibility testing for all isolates. The World Health Organization's Global Antimicrobial Resistance Surveillance System (GLASS) monitors these trends to inform treatment guidelines and public health responses.
For chronic carriers, who persist in shedding S. enterica (especially S. Typhi) for over a year, primarily in the gallbladder, management includes prolonged antibiotics like ciprofloxacin (28 days) or amoxicillin, but cholecystectomy is recommended for those with gallstones, achieving cure rates of 70-80% by removing the bacterial reservoir.

Prevention and Control

Public Health Measures

Public health measures to control Salmonella enterica transmission emphasize multifaceted strategies targeting food production, processing, consumer practices, and surveillance systems. In food processing, the Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points (HACCP) system is widely implemented to identify and mitigate contamination risks at key stages, such as during slaughter and packaging of meat and poultry, thereby reducing the prevalence of S. enterica in final products. Pasteurization is a critical intervention for eggs and dairy, heating products to temperatures that eliminate S. enterica without significantly altering quality, as evidenced by its routine application in commercial egg processing to prevent outbreaks linked to undercooked eggs. Similarly, food irradiation using gamma rays or electron beams has been approved for poultry and eggs in several countries, effectively reducing S. enterica loads by up to 5 log cycles while preserving nutritional value, though adoption varies due to consumer perceptions. Consumer-level food safety guidelines recommend cooking poultry, ground meats, and eggs to an internal temperature of at least 74C (165F) to ensure pathogen inactivation, a standard supported by thermal death time studies showing rapid lethality at this threshold.
Hygiene practices form the cornerstone of preventing S. enterica spread in households and food service settings. Thorough handwashing with soap and water for at least 20 seconds before and after handling raw animal products is essential to interrupt fecal-oral transmission, as hands can transfer contaminants to ready-to-eat foods. Similar precautions apply to handling pet reptiles. Reptiles, including pet tortoises and turtles, do not always carry Salmonella enterica, but the bacteria are commonly found in their digestive tracts even in healthy-looking animals, leading to recommendations from health authorities such as the CDC and FDA to treat all reptiles as potential carriers and to practice strict hygiene, including thorough handwashing after handling these animals or their habitats, to prevent zoonotic transmission. Using separate cutting boards and utensils for raw meats and produce minimizes cross-contamination, with guidelines specifying that boards used for raw poultry should not contact uncooked vegetables without sanitization in between. Regulatory measures, such as the U.S. FDA requirement for refrigeration of shell eggs at or below 7.2C (45F) during storage and transport, curb bacterial growth post-laying, significantly lowering the risk of S. enterica serovar Enteritidis proliferation in eggs.
Surveillance systems enable rapid detection and response to S. enterica outbreaks. In the United States, PulseNet, a molecular subtyping network operated by the CDC, uses whole-genome sequencing to compare bacterial isolates from infected individuals, facilitating the identification of multistate clusters and linking cases to contaminated food sources like produce or poultry. S. enterica infections are designated as nationally notifiable diseases in the U.S. and reportable in most countries worldwide, allowing public health agencies to track incidence and implement targeted interventions, such as recalls, through integrated laboratory and epidemiological data.
Animal health initiatives at the farm level target pre-harvest reduction of S. enterica colonization. Biosecurity protocols in poultry and livestock operations include restricted access to farms, rodent control, and disinfection of equipment to limit introduction and spread of the pathogen from environmental reservoirs. Competitive exclusion using probioticsmixtures of beneficial gut bacteria administered to day-old chicksestablishes a protective microbiota that outcompetes S. enterica for attachment sites in the intestine, reducing cecal colonization by 2-3 log units and subsequent carcass contamination during processing. These measures, often combined in integrated farm-to-fork programs, have contributed to declining S. enterica prevalence in poultry flocks in regulated regions.

Vaccination and Interventions

Vaccination efforts against Salmonella enterica primarily target serovars causing typhoid fever in humans, such as S. enterica serovar Typhi, through licensed vaccines that induce humoral and cellular immunity. The live attenuated oral vaccine Ty21a, administered in three doses, demonstrates 50-80% efficacy against typhoid fever, with protection lasting up to three years and higher effectiveness in children aged 5-9 years (53% vaccine efficacy). The injectable Vi capsular polysaccharide vaccine provides 50-70% protective efficacy against blood culture-confirmed typhoid for approximately two years, primarily through anti-Vi antibody responses that block bacterial invasion. Newer typhoid conjugate vaccines (TCVs), such as Vi-tetanus toxoid conjugates, were prequalified by the World Health Organization in 2018 and show 79-85% efficacy in children aged 9 months to 16 years after a single dose, offering longer-lasting immunity via T-cell dependent responses suitable for routine immunization in endemic areas.
In veterinary applications, live attenuated vaccines against nontyphoidal serovars like S. enterica serovar Enteritidis are widely used in poultry to curb zoonotic transmission, reducing cecal colonization and fecal shedding by 50-90% through induction of mucosal and systemic immunity. Bivalent formulations targeting multiple serovars, such as Enteritidis and Typhimurium, have demonstrated safety and efficacy in commercial broiler and layer flocks, limiting environmental contamination and human foodborne outbreaks.
Emerging interventions include bacteriophage therapy, with trials from 2023-2025 showing promise against multidrug-resistant (MDR) S. enterica strains by achieving up to a 2-log reduction in bacterial load in infected tissues and the gut microbiome of animal models. These lytic phages specifically target Salmonella without disrupting beneficial microbiota, offering a targeted alternative for treating persistent infections in livestock. CRISPR-Cas-based tools are advancing diagnostics for rapid S. enterica detection, with recombinase polymerase amplification (RPA)-CRISPR/Cas12b assays identifying serovars like Typhimurium and Indiana at sensitivities of 10^2-10^4 CFU/mL in under an hour, aiding outbreak response. Exploratory prevention strategies leverage CRISPR to edit bacterial virulence factors or enhance host immunity, though clinical translation remains in early stages.
Key challenges in S. enterica vaccination include the absence of a universal vaccine for nontyphoidal serovars, which cause diverse invasive and gastrointestinal diseases without cross-protection from typhoid vaccines. Herd immunity is further limited by asymptomatic carriers in both humans and animals, who shed bacteria subclinically and perpetuate transmission cycles. However, as of 2025, phase 1 clinical trials for vaccines targeting invasive nontyphoidal Salmonella, including combination typhoid-iNTS formulations, have shown promising safety and immunogenicity profiles.
