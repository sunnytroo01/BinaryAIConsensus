Diethylene glycol

1x
 Diethylene glycol (DEG) is an organic compound with the chemical formula (HOCHCH)O, appearing as a colorless, practically odorless, hygroscopic liquid with a sweet taste and density greater than water. It is miscible with water and many organic solvents, exhibiting moderate viscosity and boiling point around 245 C. Industrially produced via acid-catalyzed hydration of ethylene oxide, primarily as a byproduct during ethylene glycol synthesis, DEG serves as a solvent, humectant in inks and polyesters, and component in brake fluids and antifreeze mixtures, though its applications are constrained by inherent toxicity.
Despite its utility, DEG poses significant health risks, with oral LD50 values in rodents ranging from 2 to 25 g/kg, indicating moderate acute toxicity that manifests as metabolic acidosis, renal failure, and neurological damage upon ingestion due to metabolites like 2-hydroxyethoxyacetic acid. Human exposure, particularly through contaminated pharmaceuticals where DEG substitutes for safer glycols like glycerin, has caused over a dozen documented mass poisonings since the early 1900s, resulting in hundreds of fatalities across multiple countries. These incidents underscore causal pathways of toxicity involving delayed onset of symptomsinitial inebriation followed by organ failurenecessitating empirical detection methods and regulatory vigilance to prevent adulteration in excipients for liquid medicines. Long-term survivors often suffer persistent renal and neurologic impairments, highlighting DEG's cumulative bioaccumulation risks absent in less toxic analogs.

Properties

Chemical Structure and Nomenclature

Diethylene glycol possesses the molecular formula CHO and features a linear chain structure composed of two ethylene units bridged by an ether linkage, with hydroxyl groups at each terminus: HOCHCHOCHCHOH. This configuration includes two primary alcohol functional groups and one ether functional group, distinguishing it from ethylene glycol by the additional oxyethylene segment.
The preferred IUPAC name for diethylene glycol is 2-(2-hydroxyethoxy)ethanol, reflecting its systematic nomenclature as a substituted ethanol derivative. Alternative systematic names include 2,2'-oxydi(ethan-1-ol) and 2,2'-oxybis(ethanol). The common name "diethylene glycol" derives from its formation through the etherification of two ethylene glycol molecules, losing one water molecule, and it is widely used in chemical literature and industry under this designation. Other synonyms encompass bis(2-hydroxyethyl) ether and 2,2'-dihydroxydiethyl ether, emphasizing the ether and alcohol moieties.

Physical and Thermodynamic Properties

Diethylene glycol is a colorless, low-volatility, hygroscopic liquid with low viscosity under ambient conditions. It exhibits a density greater than that of water, rendering it sink in aqueous environments. The compound is practically odorless and stable, with no perceptible odor at typical concentrations.
Key physical properties include a melting point of -6.5 C and a normal boiling point of 244.9 C at 1013.25 hPa. Its density measures 1.1182 g/cm at 20/20 C, while dynamic viscosity is 35.7 mPas at 20 C. The refractive index is 1.4472 at 20 C, and it is fully miscible with water (100 wt% solubility at 20 C). The flash point is 138 C (closed cup).

Chemical Reactivity and Stability

Diethylene glycol exhibits high chemical stability under ambient conditions of temperature and pressure, remaining largely unreactive with water or common materials during storage and transport. It is hygroscopic, readily absorbing moisture from the atmosphere, which necessitates sealed storage to prevent dilution or contamination. Thermal stability persists up to recommended use temperatures, typically below 200250 C, beyond which decomposition may occur, releasing carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide, and other toxic vapors. No hazardous polymerization takes place under normal handling.
In terms of reactivity, diethylene glycol is incompatible with strong oxidizing agents, such as permanganates or chromates, which can cause vigorous oxidation reactions potentially leading to fire or explosion. It reacts exothermically with strong acids like sulfuric acid, increasing the risk of fire or explosive decomposition if temperatures rise uncontrollably. Similarly, exposure to strong bases under heating conditions may trigger violent reactions. As a diol, it participates in typical alcohol reactions, including esterification with carboxylic acids or dehydration to form ethers, but these are controlled processes rather than spontaneous hazards.
Conditions to avoid include elevated temperatures exceeding its flash point of approximately 143 C (open cup), contact with ignition sources, or confinement that could promote pressure buildup from decomposition. Prolonged exposure to air does not lead to peroxide formation, unlike some ethers, due to its stable ether linkage flanked by hydroxyl groups. Inert atmospheres are recommended for long-term storage to enhance color stability and minimize oxidative side reactions.

Production

Industrial Synthesis

Diethylene glycol is primarily produced industrially as a co-product during the thermal or catalytic hydration of ethylene oxide with excess water, a process optimized mainly for monoethylene glycol but yielding diethylene glycol through sequential ring-opening reactions. The initial reaction involves nucleophilic attack by water on the ethylene oxide epoxide ring, forming monoethylene glycol (HOCH2CH2OH), which then reacts further with unreacted ethylene oxide to produce diethylene glycol (HOCH2CH2OCH2CH2OH). This oligomerization pathway is inherent to the uncatalyzed thermal process conducted at 150250 C and 1030 bar pressure in a tubular reactor, with water-to-ethylene oxide ratios of 7:1 to 20:1 minimizing higher glycols while generating diethylene glycol at 810% yield relative to ethylene oxide input.
The reactor effluent, comprising water, monoethylene glycol (90% yield), diethylene glycol, and minor triethylene glycol, undergoes multistage fractional distillation under vacuum to isolate components based on boiling points: 197 C for monoethylene glycol, 245 C for diethylene glycol, and 285290 C for triethylene glycol. Ethylene oxide feedstock is itself manufactured via silver-catalyzed partial oxidation of ethylene with oxygen at 220280 C, achieving 8090% selectivity to ethylene oxide, which feeds directly into the hydration step. Process variations, such as sulfuric acid catalysis or lower water ratios, can enhance diethylene glycol selectivity to 1520% but are less common due to the economic priority on monoethylene glycol.
Alternative routes, including ethylene oxide reaction with monoethylene glycol or transesterification of ethylene carbonate with methanol, exist but represent minor fractions of global production, limited by cost and scalability compared to the dominant ethylene oxide hydration method. Global diethylene glycol output, exceeding 1 million metric tons annually as of 2020, derives predominantly from integrated ethylene oxide/ethylene glycol plants operated by major producers like Shell and Dow.

Commercial Manufacturing Processes

Diethylene glycol (DEG) is commercially manufactured as a co-product during the acid- or base-catalyzed, or more commonly thermal (non-catalytic), hydration of ethylene oxide (EO) with water, integrated within large-scale ethylene glycol production facilities. The primary reaction involves the ring-opening of EO by water to form monoethylene glycol (MEG), with subsequent nucleophilic attack by MEG on additional EO molecules yielding DEG (HO-CH-CH-O-CH-CH-OH) and higher oligomers like triethylene glycol (TEG). This sequential oligomerization is governed by the water-to-EO molar ratio, typically maintained at 10:1 to 20:1 for MEG-dominant output but lowered (e.g., 4:1 to 8:1) in processes optimized for higher glycol yields including DEG.
The hydration occurs in a continuous liquid-phase reactor at temperatures of 150250C and pressures of 1030 bar to ensure EO solubility and reaction kinetics, often without added catalysts in thermal processes licensed by major providers like Shell. Reaction conditions are precisely controlled to minimize unwanted side products like aldehydes or dioxanes, with residence times on the order of minutes to achieve conversions exceeding 90% for EO. The effluent mixture, containing 8090% MEG, 812% DEG, and 13% TEG by weight depending on process tuning, undergoes multi-stage fractional distillation under vacuum to separate the glycols based on boiling points (MEG: 197.3C, DEG: 245.0C at 1 atm).
Purification of DEG involves further distillation or ion-exchange to remove water, acids, and impurities to meet commercial specifications (e.g., 99.5% purity, low aldehyde content <10 ppm). Integrated facilities, such as those operated by MEGlobal, leverage proximity to EO production from ethylene oxidation, enabling efficient feedstock utilization and economies of scale, with global capacity exceeding 1.4 million tonnes annually as of recent estimates. Alternative routes, like direct synthesis from ethylene glycol and EO, are not commercially dominant due to lower selectivity and higher costs compared to co-production.

Uses

Solvent and Humectant Applications

Diethylene glycol (DEG) functions as a solvent for numerous organic compounds, including nitrocellulose, resins, dyes, oils, and other relatively insoluble substances, owing to its polar nature and moderate solvency power. In industrial applications, it facilitates the dissolution and dispersion of pigments and dyes in printing inks, paints, and coatings, enhancing product consistency and application properties. DEG also serves as a solvent coupler and stabilizer in soluble oil dispersions, aiding in the separation of aromatic and paraffinic hydrocarbons during petroleum refining processes. Additionally, it is employed in the extraction of solvents from petroleum products and in the formulation of wallpaper strippers and fabric treatments.
As a humectant, DEG leverages its strong hygroscopic properties to retain moisture in various materials, preventing desiccation in processing and storage. In the tobacco industry, it is applied to maintain optimal humidity levels during production and packaging, improving handling and combustion characteristics. Similar moisture-regulating roles extend to cork products, casein-based materials, synthetic sponges, glues, and printing inks, where it inhibits brittleness and supports flexibility. In textile finishing and leather dressing, DEG acts as a lubricating and softening agent, contributing to smoother textures and durability. Limited use occurs in personal care formulations like lotions and shampoos for texture enhancement and moisture retention, though regulatory restrictions cap concentrations due to toxicity concerns, such as the U.S. FDA limit of 0.2% in ingested products.

Antifreeze and Heat Transfer Uses

Diethylene glycol (DEG) functions as a freezing point depressant in antifreeze and coolant mixtures, primarily for automotive engines, industrial equipment, and de-icing operations. When blended with water, typically at concentrations of 30-50% by volume, DEG reduces the freezing point to as low as -50C or below, preventing crystallization that could damage pipes, radiators, or storage tanks during subzero conditions. Its hygroscopic nature also inhibits corrosion in metal components by maintaining moisture balance and forming protective films. 
In de-icing applications, DEG is incorporated into fluids for runways, sidewalks, and aircraft lavatories, where it melts ice effectively while offering lower volatility than shorter-chain alcohols, reducing evaporation losses in open environments. Automotive antifreeze formulations may include DEG as a co-solvent with ethylene glycol to enhance low-temperature performance and boiling point elevation, with mixtures boiling above 106C under pressure to support engine cooling in high-heat scenarios. 
For heat transfer uses, DEG serves as a key ingredient in closed-loop systems, such as those in chemical processing plants, HVAC units, and solar thermal collectors, due to its low viscosity (approximately 35 cP at 20C), high boiling point (245C), and thermal stability up to 150-200C without significant degradation. These properties enable efficient heat dissipation while minimizing pump energy requirements and vapor lock risks, though DEG-based fluids exhibit 10-20% lower specific heat capacity than pure water (around 2.3 J/gC for DEG-water mixes), necessitating optimized circulation rates for maximal efficiency. In industrial settings like gas dehydration or oilfield operations, DEG facilitates heat exchange in non-freezing environments, often outperforming propylene glycol in cost and fluidity but requiring careful handling due to its toxicity profile compared to less hazardous alternatives. 

Other Industrial and Miscellaneous Roles

Diethylene glycol functions as a dehydrating agent in natural gas processing, leveraging its strong hygroscopic properties to absorb water vapor and prevent the formation of gas hydrates, which could obstruct pipelines and equipment. This application is critical in upstream oil and gas operations, where DEG is circulated in absorption towers to achieve low dew points, typically below -10C, ensuring dry gas output for transportation and storage.
In polymer manufacturing, DEG serves as a key intermediate in the synthesis of unsaturated polyester resins (UPR), where it reacts with dicarboxylic acids such as phthalic anhydride and maleic anhydride to form flexible, crosslinkable polymers used in fiber-reinforced composites for marine, automotive, and construction applications. Typical formulations incorporate 10-30% DEG by weight to balance viscosity, reactivity, and mechanical properties like tensile strength exceeding 50 MPa in cured laminates. Additionally, DEG contributes to polyurethane production as a component in polyester polyols for thermoplastic polyurethanes (TPUs) and as a diol chain extender, influencing phase separation, elasticity, and self-healing characteristics in coatings and elastomers; for instance, its use enhances hydrogen bonding and microphase mixing in hexamethylene diisocyanate-based systems.
Other roles include acting as a plasticizer intermediate for nitrocellulose lacquers, enamels, adhesives, and coatings, where it improves flexibility and adhesion without significant migration; as a grinding aid in glass and cement processing to reduce energy consumption by 5-10%; and in niche applications such as mold release agents, emulsifiers, foundry sand binders, and drywall joint compounds, capitalizing on its low volatility and compatibility with inorganic fillers. DEG also appears in lubricant formulations and printing inks as a compatibilizer for dyes and pigments, aiding dispersion and stability.

Toxicology

Toxicokinetics

Diethylene glycol (DEG) is rapidly and nearly completely absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract following oral administration, with invasion constants of approximately 34 h observed in rat studies at doses of 15 ml/kg. In humans, gastrointestinal absorption is similarly rapid, though transcutaneous absorption through intact skin is minimal.01251-7/fulltext)
Following absorption, DEG distributes quickly from the blood into tissues and organs, with highest concentrations in the kidneys, followed by brain, spleen, liver, muscle, and fat in rats; the apparent volume of distribution is approximately 298 ml. Blood kinetics in rats show biphasic elimination, with 6491% of radiolabeled activity cleared in 1216 hours (initial half-life of 3.4 hours) and the remainder over 3949 hours.
DEG undergoes hepatic metabolism primarily via alcohol dehydrogenase to the intermediate 2-hydroxyethoxyacetaldehyde, which is further oxidized to 2-hydroxyethoxyacetic acid (2-HEAA), the major metabolite; a minor pathway produces diglycolic acid (DGA), which is responsible for nephrotoxicity by inducing ATP depletion and necrosis in proximal tubule cells. Metabolism saturates at high doses, as evidenced by prolonged half-lives (up to 10 hours at 10 ml/kg in rats).
Excretion occurs predominantly via the kidneys, with 7396% of the dose recovered in urine as metabolites and unmetabolized DEG, and 0.72.2% in feces; renal clearance of unmetabolized DEG is approximately 66 ml/h, indicating partial tubular reabsorption (clearance ratio to inulin of 0.64). Urinary excretion half-lives range from 6 hours at lower doses to 10 hours at higher doses in rats.

Mechanisms of Action

Diethylene glycol (DEG) is primarily metabolized in the liver via alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) to 2-hydroxyethoxyacetaldehyde, which is then oxidized by aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH) to 2-hydroxyethoxyacetic acid (HEAA); an alternative pathway yields diglycolic acid (DGA), the principal nephrotoxic metabolite. Unlike ethylene glycol, DEG metabolism does not generate glycolic acid or oxalic acid, avoiding calcium oxalate crystalluria but still inducing severe metabolic acidosis through accumulation of acidic metabolites like HEAA and DGA.
The renal toxicity of DEG stems mainly from DGA, which accumulates in proximal tubular cells and disrupts mitochondrial respiration by chelating magnesium ions essential for Krebs cycle enzymes, such as -ketoglutarate dehydrogenase, leading to energy depletion, cellular vacuolization, edema, and necrosis. This proximal tubule injury obstructs urine flow, precipitates acute kidney injury with anuria and uremia, and correlates with elevated blood urea nitrogen and creatinine levels observed in animal models and human cases. DGA uptake into renal cells is facilitated by the sodium-dicarboxylate cotransporter NaDC-1, with inhibition of this transporter reducing toxicity in vitro.
Neurotoxicity mechanisms remain less defined but involve HEAA or other metabolites causing peripheral neuropathy and central effects, potentially through direct neuronal damage or secondary to acidosis and electrolyte imbalances; animal studies show dose-dependent nervous system involvement without the pronounced osmolar effects of ethylene glycol. Hepatic effects are milder, attributed to metabolite-induced oxidative stress rather than primary hepatocyte targeting. Overall, toxicity thresholds exhibit interspecies variability, with rats showing marked sensitivity linked to efficient metabolism and DGA accumulation. Inhibition of ADH with agents like fomepizole prevents metabolite formation and mitigates organ damage in experimental models.

Acute and Chronic Effects

Acute poisoning from diethylene glycol (DEG) ingestion typically progresses in phases, beginning with gastrointestinal symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea within hours of exposure. These initial effects are followed by metabolic acidosis, renal dysfunction manifesting as oliguria, anuria, elevated creatinine and blood urea nitrogen levels, and acute kidney injury (AKI), often accompanied by anion gap metabolic acidosis. Hepatic involvement may occur, evidenced by elevated alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST) levels, while neurological symptoms including ataxia, disorientation, convulsions, stupor, and delayed neuropathies can emerge, sometimes leading to coma or death despite interventions like hemodialysis. The primary nephrotoxic metabolite, diglycolic acid, induces mitochondrial dysfunction and proximal tubular injury in the kidneys, driving much of the organ failure. 
Chronic or repeated low-level exposure to DEG, though less documented in humans due to its rarity outside acute contamination events, is associated with potential kidney damage, as observed in animal studies and safety data sheets classifying it as a specific target organ toxicant for kidneys via prolonged or repeated exposure. Symptoms from subacute repetitive dosing may include persistent weakness, drowsiness, irregular pupils (anisocoria), hypersomnolence, and neurological sequelae such as reduced reflexes or limb weakness, mirroring patterns seen in glycol ether exposures. Hepatic and renal toxicity, including lethality, have been noted in repetitive oral ingestion scenarios, underscoring cumulative organ stress without the rapid onset of acute phases. Human data remain limited, primarily inferred from mass poisoning aftermaths rather than controlled occupational studies, with no widespread evidence of carcinogenicity or reproductive toxicity at chronic thresholds below acute toxic doses.

Diagnosis and Treatment

Diagnosis of diethylene glycol (DEG) poisoning is presumptive, based on a history of exposure to contaminated products, such as adulterated medications, combined with clinical features and laboratory abnormalities, given the limited availability of rapid confirmatory assays. Initial symptoms include nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea, progressing to acute kidney injury (AKI) with oliguria or anuria within 1-3 days, often accompanied by anion gap metabolic acidosis. Laboratory evaluation reveals elevated serum creatinine and blood urea nitrogen indicative of renal failure, with possible hepatotoxicity, pancreatitis, or cardiac arrhythmias; an osmolar gap may be present early but is less pronounced than in ethylene glycol poisoning. Delayed neurologic effects, such as peripheral neuropathies or cranial nerve palsies, emerge 1-2 weeks post-exposure and support the diagnosis in outbreak settings. Definitive confirmation requires gas chromatography-mass spectrometry detection of DEG or metabolites like 2-hydroxyethoxyacetic acid (HEAA) or diglycolic acid in serum or urine, though results may take days to weeks.
Treatment focuses on supportive care, metabolic correction, and enhanced elimination, as no specific antidote targets DEG's toxic metabolites. Airway management, intravenous fluids for volume resuscitation, and electrolyte correction are initial priorities, with sodium bicarbonate administered for severe acidosis (pH <7.3). Alcohol dehydrogenase inhibition using fomepizole (15 mg/kg IV loading dose, followed by 10 mg/kg every 12 hours, increased to every 4 hours during hemodialysis) or ethanol (target serum level 100-150 mg/dL) is recommended empirically to reduce formation of nephrotoxic metabolites like diglycolic acid, despite DEG's lower affinity for the enzyme compared to ethylene glycol; evidence derives from case reports showing resolution of acidosis and prevention of progression. Hemodialysis is indicated for severe AKI, refractory acidosis, uremia, elevated DEG levels (>50 mg/dL), or neurologic compromise, as it rapidly removes DEG (reducing half-life from ~27 hours to 3-5 hours) and corrects acid-base disturbances. Gastrointestinal decontamination (e.g., activated charcoal) is ineffective beyond 1-2 hours post-ingestion due to rapid absorption. No interventions reliably prevent delayed neurotoxicity, which requires long-term supportive care; mortality remains high (up to 50-100% in outbreaks with anuria) despite aggressive management.

Epidemiology of Poisonings

1937 United States Incident

In September 1937, the S.E. Massengill Company of Bristol, Tennessee, manufactured and distributed Elixir Sulfanilamide, a liquid formulation of the newly introduced antibiotic sulfanilamide intended for patients unable to swallow tablets, particularly children. The company dissolved 100 grains of sulfanilamide per fluid ounce in diethylene glycol (DEG), selected as the solvent for its low cost, viscosity approximating glycerin, and sweet taste, without conducting toxicity testing on animals or humans, as premarket safety proof was not required under the 1906 Pure Food and Drug Act. Approximately 99% of the elixir's volume consisted of DEG, a industrial chemical known for its antifreeze properties but unrecognized at the time for its lethal nephrotoxicity in pharmaceutical contexts.
The product was shipped nationwide starting September 18, 1937, with over 1,000 pints distributed to physicians and pharmacies before reports of adverse effects emerged in late September. Initial cases in Tulsa, Oklahoma, involved pediatric patients prescribed the elixir for infections, who developed acute symptoms including nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, oliguria, and anuria, progressing to renal shutdown and death within days. By early October, similar fatalities were confirmed across 15 states, with autopsies revealing visceral congestion, degenerated kidney tubules, and glycol metabolites in tissues, directly attributing deaths to DEG poisoning rather than sulfanilamide itself, which had proven safe in solid forms. At least 105 individuals died, predominantly children under 12 years old, though exact figures varied due to underreporting and varying diagnostic capabilities at the time.
The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA), lacking authority to mandate recalls, coordinated with Massengill to halt production on October 14, 1937, after laboratory confirmation of DEG's toxicity via dog tests showing fatal doses equivalent to human therapeutic amounts. Federal investigations recovered most shipped elixir, but residual circulation prolonged risks; no effective antidote existed, with treatments limited to supportive care like fluids and stimulants, which failed against DEG's metabolic acidosis and oxalate crystal-induced renal damage. The incident exposed regulatory gaps, as DEG's prior use in unguents and emollients had not prompted scrutiny for oral safety, and Massengill's chief chemist later testified that toxicity was unforeseen despite known animal data on related glycols.
This event, often termed the "Elixir Sulfanilamide disaster," catalyzed the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act of 1938, mandating premarket safety demonstrations for new drugs, though enforcement relied on post-market evidence until later amendments. Massengill faced no criminal charges, as intent was absent, but paid civil settlements; the company's president publicly expressed regret while denying manufacturing fault, highlighting era-specific deference to industry self-regulation.

19691990s Incidents in Developing Nations

In 1969, an outbreak in Cape Town, South Africa, resulted in the deaths of seven children from acute renal failure following ingestion of a sedative elixir in which diethylene glycol (DEG) had been substituted for propylene glycol as the solvent. The children presented with vomiting, diarrhea, dehydration, oliguria, and progressive deterioration, highlighting DEG's nephrotoxic effects even in small quantities. This incident underscored early risks of unregulated solvent substitution in pharmaceutical formulations in resource-limited settings.
In Nigeria, between June and September 1990, at least 47 children aged 1 month to 5 years died at Jos University Teaching Hospital from acute renal failure after consuming paracetamol syrup adulterated with DEG, used as a cheap diluent instead of glycerol. Clinical features included abdominal pain, vomiting, fever, and rapid progression to anuria and coma, with autopsy confirming tubular necrosis consistent with DEG toxicity. Broader estimates suggest over 200 pediatric deaths nationwide linked to similar contaminated syrups, reflecting systemic failures in quality control and import oversight.
Bangladesh experienced a major epidemic from late 1990 to 1992, where approximately 339 children developed fatal renal failure after ingesting paracetamol elixir containing DEG as the primary solvent, sourced from local manufacturers prioritizing cost over safety. Affected children exhibited nonspecific symptoms like fever and abdominal pain initially, escalating to oliguria, seizures, and death within days; laboratory analysis confirmed DEG concentrations up to 20% in implicated brands from companies like Adflame Pharmaceuticals. The government banned liquid paracetamol formulations in December 1992 following whistleblower identification of the contaminant, though underreporting likely inflated the true toll.
In Haiti, from November 1995 to June 1996, DEG-contaminated glycerin used in acetaminophen syrup manufacturing caused an epidemic of acute kidney injury, killing at least 85 children under age 13, with over 100 cases reported. The adulterated glycerin, imported and containing 18%22% DEG, led to symptoms of nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and anuria; median DEG ingestion was estimated at 1,340 mg/kg, far exceeding toxic thresholds. Traceback revealed formulation errors by a local company, exacerbated by inadequate testing of excipients from unregulated suppliers.
These incidents shared a pattern of DEG adulteration in pediatric oral medicationsoften paracetamol or sedativesdue to its lower cost compared to pharmaceutical-grade glycerol or propylene glycol, compounded by weak regulatory enforcement, counterfeit supply chains, and limited analytical capabilities in developing nations. Mortality rates approached 90% in untreated cases, primarily from renal tubular necrosis, with survivors facing chronic sequelae like hypertension. No effective antidotes existed at the time, emphasizing reliance on early detection and hemodialysis where available.

20062008 Global Outbreaks

In 2006, Panama experienced the largest recorded mass poisoning from diethylene glycol (DEG)-contaminated pharmaceutical products, affecting primarily adults who consumed tainted cough syrup. The outbreak originated from 46 barrels of industrial DEG mislabeled and sold as pharmaceutical-grade glycerin by a Chinese supplier, Longsheng Chemical Industrial Co., to a Spanish trading firm, which then exported it to a Panamanian customer. This adulterated glycerin was incorporated into approximately 60,000 bottles of liquid cough syrup manufactured by Laboratorios Lasmon in Panama, distributed nationwide through public health programs and pharmacies. Consumption led to acute kidney injury, with symptoms including abdominal pain, vomiting, and renal failure; official investigations confirmed at least 174 cases of poisoning, resulting in 115 deaths.
The Panamanian Ministry of Health identified the contamination through toxicological analysis after an initial cluster of renal failure cases in May 2006, prompting a nationwide recall and alerting international bodies like the World Health Organization (WHO) and U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA). Epidemiological studies linked exposure to the syrup via case-control methods, showing odds ratios exceeding 50 for consumption among cases compared to controls. The incident exposed vulnerabilities in global supply chains for inactive pharmaceutical ingredients, as the DEG substitution was driven by cost-cutting, with the Chinese firm falsifying documentation to evade quality checks.
Following the Panama crisis, heightened scrutiny revealed DEG in glycerin shipments to multiple countries, though without widespread poisonings until 2008 in Nigeria. There, from late 2008 into 2009, at least 84 young children died from acute renal failure after ingesting DEG-contaminated teething syrup (acetaminophen-based) manufactured locally using imported, adulterated raw materials traced to similar supply issues. The Nigerian outbreak involved over 100 suspected cases, primarily infants under age 3, with post-mortem analyses confirming DEG metabolites and renal tubular necrosis consistent with DEG toxicity.
The 20062008 period also saw non-fatal DEG detections in consumer products, notably toothpaste exported from China containing up to 4% DEG as a sweetener substitute, leading to U.S. FDA import bans and recalls in 20072008 across Panama, the U.S., and other nations. No confirmed deaths resulted from toothpaste exposure, but the incidents amplified global regulatory responses, including FDA guidance for glycerin testing. These events underscored recurring failures in verifying excipient purity, particularly from low-cost suppliers, despite prior historical precedents.

20192022 Syrup Contaminations

In 2022, a series of diethylene glycol (DEG) contaminations in paediatric cough syrups and liquid medicines affected multiple countries, resulting in over 300 child deaths across at least seven nations, primarily due to acute kidney injury from toxic excipients substituting for safe solvents like propylene glycol or glycerin. These incidents underscored vulnerabilities in pharmaceutical supply chains, where cost-saving adulteration introduced DEG into formulations intended for children, often in paracetamol or promethazine-based products. The World Health Organization (WHO) issued multiple alerts, confirming laboratory analyses of unacceptable DEG levels exceeding safe limits by orders of magnitude.
In The Gambia, at least 66 children died between mid-2022 and early 2023 after ingesting four Indian-manufactured cough syrupsPromethazine Oral Solution, Kofexmalin Baby Cough Syrup, Imodium drops, and Magicol Infant Dropscontaminated with high DEG concentrations, as verified by WHO and Gambian laboratory testing. The outbreak involved over 1,000 cases of acute kidney injury among children under five, with symptoms including vomiting, lethargy, and renal failure; epidemiological links traced exposures to these imported products distributed without adequate pre-market testing. WHO's October 5, 2022, alert prompted global recalls, revealing lapses in exporter quality controls and importer verification.
Similar contaminations struck Uzbekistan, where 18 children in Samarkand died in 2022 from DEG-laced cough syrups, and Indonesia, reporting 178 fatalities from ethylene glycol (EG) and DEG in locally produced syrups, predominantly paracetamol variants affecting over 200 cases of renal toxicity. In Indonesia, nearly all 103 implicated syrups contained DEG/EG as adulterants in sweetening agents, confirmed by national health authorities and WHO analysis, with deaths peaking from acute renal failure in infants and toddlers. These events, concentrated in 2022 with precursors possibly in 2021 supply issues, prompted enhanced WHO calls for solvent authentication testing but highlighted persistent enforcement gaps in low-resource settings.

2025 India Cough Syrup Crisis

In September 2025, clusters of acute kidney injury and fatalities emerged among children in Madhya Pradesh, India, primarily in Chhindwara district, linked to oral cough syrups contaminated with diethylene glycol (DEG). At least 22 children under age five died after consuming these products, with symptoms including abdominal pain, vomiting, and renal failure.
The World Health Organization issued a medical product alert on October 13, 2025, identifying three substandard syrups: COLDRIF (manufactured by Sresan Pharmaceutical), Respifresh TR (Rednex Pharmaceuticals), and ReLife (Shape Pharma), all testing positive for DEG contamination beyond safe limits. Laboratory analysis of COLDRIF revealed DEG concentrations approaching 45%, or nearly 500 times the permissible threshold of 0.1%, confirming substitution of industrial-grade DEG for pharmaceutical solvents like propylene glycol to reduce production costs. This adulteration, described by experts as deliberate cost-cutting rather than accidental, exploited weak domestic testing requirements, as DEG screening was mandated only for exports prior to the incident.
India's Central Drugs Standard Control Organisation (CDSCO) responded by suspending production licenses, initiating recalls of affected batches, and sealing facilities, including Sresan Pharmaceutical's plant in Tamil Nadu; the company's owner was arrested. The WHO highlighted persistent regulatory gaps in India's oversight of locally marketed syrups, despite export testing improvements, urging enhanced surveillance and accountability to prevent recurrence. No exports of these batches reached international markets, including the United States.

Regulatory Responses

Historical Bans and Restrictions

The 1937 Elixir Sulfanilamide disaster in the United States, where diethylene glycol (DEG) was used as a solvent in an antibiotic formulation, resulted in 107 deaths primarily from acute kidney failure, highlighting the acute oral toxicity of DEG at doses as low as 0.14 g/kg body weight. This incident exposed the absence of premarket safety requirements for drugs under the 1906 Pure Food and Drugs Act, prompting swift regulatory action. On June 25, 1938, the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FD&C Act) was signed into law, mandating that manufacturers prove the safety of new drugs, including excipients like solvents, before interstate marketing. This effectively restricted DEG's use in pharmaceuticals by requiring toxicological evidence of safety, which DEG could not satisfy for oral applications due to its proven nephrotoxic and metabolic effects via hydrolysis to toxic aldehydes and acids.
The FD&C Act's implementation led to the withdrawal of DEG-containing products and influenced pharmacopeial standards, such as those in the United States Pharmacopeia, which began emphasizing solvent purity and toxicity testing. By the 1940s, DEG was explicitly prohibited in food additives and direct ingestion products under FDA interpretations of the Act, as its lethal dose in humans was established at approximately 1-2 g/kg, far below levels tolerable in ethylene glycol analogs. Internationally, similar restrictions followed; for example, European regulatory bodies, informed by the U.S. incident and early toxicological data, incorporated DEG limitations into early pharmaceutical monographs, barring its use as a vehicle in liquid medicines without rigorous safety justification.
Further historical restrictions targeted specific consumer products. In the mid-20th century, DEG was banned from brake fluids and antifreeze formulations intended for potential incidental ingestion in some jurisdictions due to chronic exposure risks, though industrial uses persisted under handling controls. By the 1970s, amid recurring contamination reports, agencies like the FDA codified limits such as no more than 0.2% DEG impurity in polyethylene glycol food additives under 21 CFR, reflecting cumulative evidence of bioaccumulation and organ damage from repeated low-dose exposure. These measures prioritized causal links between DEG's glycol ether metabolites and renal tubular necrosis over prior assumptions of solvent inertness.

Modern Testing Protocols and Standards

In response to recurrent diethylene glycol (DEG) contaminations in pharmaceutical excipients, particularly following the 2006 Panama incident and the 2022 global outbreaks in pediatric syrups, regulatory bodies have established standardized testing protocols emphasizing chromatographic analysis. The United States Pharmacopeia-National Formulary (USP-NF) monographs for high-risk components such as glycerin mandate a limit test for DEG and ethylene glycol (EG) not exceeding 0.10%, conducted via gas chromatography (GC) with flame ionization detection. This involves preparing a standard solution of 2.0 mg/mL USP Glycerin RS spiked with DEG and EG, injecting samples into a suitable GC column (e.g., polyethylene glycol stationary phase), and quantifying peaks against reference standards to ensure compliance.
The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) reinforced these requirements in its May 2023 guidance, "Testing of High-Risk Drug Components for Diethylene Glycol and Ethylene Glycol," which applies current good manufacturing practices (cGMP) to excipients including glycerin, propylene glycol, sorbitol, and certain polyethylene glycols. Manufacturers must test every incoming lotor all containers for small volumesusing USP-NF methods, including identity verification via infrared spectroscopy or GC-mass spectrometry, followed by DEG/EG limit testing; non-conforming materials trigger rejection and supplier requalification. For components lacking specific USP-NF DEG/EG tests, orthogonal methods with acceptance criteria of 0.10% are required, with documentation retained for FDA inspections.
The World Health Organization (WHO) complements these with a tiered approach outlined in its July 2024 technical document on DEG/EG tests for liquid oral dosage forms, prioritizing resource-limited settings. Initial screening employs thin-layer chromatography (TLC) on silica gel plates with a mobile phase of chloroform-methanol (9:1), visualizing spots under UV light or iodine vapor to detect DEG/EG at 0.1% levels; positive screens proceed to confirmatory GC with limits of detection around 0.01-0.05%. WHO recommends this for pediatric medicines, urging pre-market testing of excipients and finished products, especially in high-risk supply chains from Asia. USP announced in February 2025 plans to publish a GC-based method for DEG/EG in finished dosage forms on its Emerging Standards webpage by May 2025, enabling direct analysis without excipient isolation.
These protocols align with European Pharmacopoeia (Ph. Eur.) standards, which similarly cap DEG in glycerin at 0.10% via GC, though enforcement varies globally. Validation emphasizes linearity (R >0.99), precision (RSD <2%), and recovery (95-105%) across 0.01-1.0% concentrations, with certified reference materials from suppliers like NIST ensuring traceability.

Challenges in Enforcement and Prevention

Enforcement of regulations against diethylene glycol (DEG) contamination faces significant hurdles in resource-limited settings, where national regulatory authorities often lack the capacity for routine inspections and supply chain monitoring. In developing nations, such as India, weak oversight has allowed substandard manufacturers to substitute DEG for glycerol in liquid medicines due to its lower cost, leading to repeated outbreaks despite established licensing systems like India's Online National Drug Licensing portal introduced in 2021. For instance, in October 2025, at least 22 children died in India from cough syrup containing nearly 45% DEG, highlighting persistent failures in pre-market testing and post-market surveillance.
Global supply chains exacerbate these issues, as excipients like glycerin are sourced from multiple suppliers across borders, complicating traceability and quality assurance. Contaminations frequently originate in low-regulation environments and propagate through exports, as seen in 2022 when Indian cough syrups tainted with DEG caused 70 child deaths in Gambia and 18 in Uzbekistan. Regulatory fragmentation hinders coordinated action, with international bodies like the WHO recommending standardized testing such as thin-layer chromatography (TLC) for national labs, yet implementation lags due to inadequate funding and technical expertise.
Detection methods, while improved through FDA and USP guidance mandating gas chromatography (GC) limits of no more than 0.10% DEG in high-risk components, suffer from limitations like false negatives in screening tests with higher limits of detection (e.g., 0.5% for some TLC variants). Economic incentives for adulteration persist, as DEG's affordability drives illicit substitution, and enforcement is undermined by corruption or insufficient penalties, allowing repeat offenders to evade accountability even after warnings. A WHO-UNODC report emphasizes the need for enhanced excipient oversight and criminal investigations, but causal factors like profit motives in unregulated markets continue to outpace preventive measures.
