The Sermon on the Mount, recorded in chapters five through seven of the Gospel of Matthew, is the most famous and most comprehensive collection of Jesus's teachings. It begins with the Beatitudes, a series of blessings that invert the values of the world:

Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven. Blessed are those who mourn, for they will be comforted. Blessed are the meek, for they will inherit the earth. Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they will be filled. Blessed are the merciful, for they will be shown mercy. Blessed are the pure in heart, for they will see God. Blessed are the peacemakers, for they will be called children of God. Blessed are those who are persecuted because of righteousness, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven.

These teachings were revolutionary. In a world that valued power, wealth, strength, and status, Jesus declared that the kingdom of God belongs to the poor, the mourning, the meek, the hungry, the merciful, and the persecuted. He turned the social hierarchy upside down and proclaimed that the last shall be first and the first shall be last.

In the Sermon on the Mount, Jesus also taught about anger, calling his followers to reconcile with their enemies rather than harboring resentment. He taught about love for enemies: "You have heard that it was said, 'Love your neighbor and hate your enemy.' But I tell you, love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you, that you may be children of your Father in heaven." This command to love one's enemies is perhaps the most radical ethical teaching in all of human history.

Jesus taught about prayer, giving his disciples the model prayer known as the Lord's Prayer: "Our Father in heaven, hallowed be your name. Your kingdom come, your will be done, on earth as it is in heaven. Give us today our daily bread. And forgive us our debts, as we also have forgiven our debtors. And lead us not into temptation, but deliver us from evil."

He taught about worry and trust in God's provision: "Therefore I tell you, do not worry about your life, what you will eat or drink; or about your body, what you will wear. Is not life more than food, and the body more than clothes? Look at the birds of the air; they do not sow or reap or store away in barns, and yet your heavenly Father feeds them. Are you not much more valuable than they?"

He taught about judgment: "Do not judge, or you too will be judged. For in the same way you judge others, you will be judged, and with the measure you use, it will be measured to you. Why do you look at the speck of sawdust in your brother's eye and pay no attention to the plank in your own eye?"

The parables of Jesus are among the most memorable and powerful teaching stories ever told. The Parable of the Good Samaritan answers the question "Who is my neighbor?" by telling the story of a man who was robbed and beaten on the road from Jerusalem to Jericho. A priest passed by without helping. A Levite passed by without helping. But a Samaritan, a member of a group despised by the Jewish community, stopped, tended the man's wounds, took him to an inn, and paid for his care. Jesus asked, "Which of these three do you think was a neighbor to the man who fell into the hands of robbers?" The answer was obvious: the one who showed mercy. "Go and do likewise," Jesus said.

The Parable of the Prodigal Son tells the story of a young man who demands his inheritance early, leaves home, squanders everything in reckless living, and finds himself destitute, feeding pigs and envying their food. He decides to return home and beg his father's forgiveness, expecting to be treated as a hired servant. But when the father sees his son approaching from a distance, he runs to him, embraces him, and throws a lavish celebration, saying, "This son of mine was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found." The parable illustrates God's unconditional love and willingness to forgive anyone who returns to him, regardless of what they have done.

The Parable of the Talents tells of a master who entrusts three servants with different amounts of money before going on a journey. The first two servants invest their money and double it. The third servant, out of fear, buries his money in the ground and returns it unchanged. The master praises the first two servants for their faithfulness and condemns the third for his failure to use what he was given. The parable teaches that God expects us to use our gifts, abilities, and opportunities to create value, not to hide them out of fear.

The crucifixion of Jesus is the defining event of the Christian faith. Jesus was arrested in the Garden of Gethsemane, tried before the Jewish council and the Roman governor Pontius Pilate, beaten, mocked, and sentenced to death by crucifixion, the most painful and humiliating form of execution in the Roman world. He was nailed to a wooden cross and hung between two criminals. His final words from the cross, recorded across the four Gospels, include: "Father, forgive them, for they do not know what they are doing." "Today you will be with me in paradise." "It is finished."

Christians believe that Jesus's death was not a tragedy but a sacrifice: that he willingly gave his life to pay the penalty for human sin, reconciling humanity to God. The resurrection, his rising from the dead on the third day after his crucifixion, is the cornerstone of the Christian faith. The apostle Paul wrote, "If Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile." The resurrection is understood as God's vindication of Jesus, the defeat of death, and the promise of eternal life for all who believe.

The influence of Jesus on human history transcends religion. His teachings on love, forgiveness, human dignity, care for the poor, and the value of every individual life have shaped the development of human rights, the abolition of slavery, the founding of hospitals and universities, and the moral frameworks of societies around the world. Whether one regards him as the Son of God, a great moral teacher, or a historical figure of extraordinary influence, the impact of Jesus of Nazareth on human civilization is without parallel.

His simplest teaching remains his most powerful: love one another.
