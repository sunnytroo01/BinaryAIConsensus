High pressure
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 High pressure refers to the application of forces significantly exceeding standard atmospheric pressure (approximately 0.1 GPa), often reaching gigapascals (GPa) or terapascals (TPa), which induces dramatic transformations in the elastic, electronic, magnetic, structural, and chemical properties of solids, liquids, and gases. These conditions push materials beyond conventional phase boundaries, enabling phenomena such as metallization of insulators, novel superconductivity, and the formation of previously unknown compounds. In scientific research, high pressure is typically generated using devices like piston-cylinder apparatuses for moderate ranges (up to several GPa) or diamond anvil cells for extreme static pressures exceeding 400 GPa, allowing in situ probing with synchrotron radiation to observe real-time changes.
The field of high-pressure physics and chemistry originated in the early 20th century through the pioneering work of Percy Williams Bridgman, who developed leak-proof packing techniques and achieved pressures up to 10 GPa using hydraulic presses, earning the 1946 Nobel Prize in Physics for his inventions that opened experimental access to this regime. Bridgman's efforts revealed pressure-induced phase transitions, such as the polymorphism of ice, and laid the foundation for understanding matter under Earth's interior conditions. Subsequent advancements, including the invention of the diamond anvil cell in the 1950s, extended reachable pressures to over 1 TPa in modern facilities like those at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory, where dynamic compression techniques simulate planetary cores and fusion environments. These tools have facilitated studies of high-energy-density physics, involving densities 1001,000 times greater than solids and temperatures up to millions of degrees Celsius.
High-pressure research has profound applications across disciplines, including the industrial synthesis of superhard materials like diamond via high-pressure high-temperature (HPHT) processes exceeding 5 GPa and 1,500C, which revolutionized abrasives and cutting tools. In geophysics, it elucidates the behavior of mantle minerals, such as olivine deformation at 910 GPa, informing models of Earth's tectonic activity and seismic wave propagation. Emerging discoveries include the potential metallization of hydrogen at around 500 GPa, with recent dynamic compression experiments at the National Ignition Facility reaching pressures up to 800 GPa as of 2024, relevant to astrophysics and quantum materials, as well as pressure-tuned superconductivity in two-dimensional materials like MoS, achieving critical temperatures up to 11.5 K. Overall, high pressure serves as a versatile control parameter for designing novel materials with enhanced properties, from high-Tc superconductors to polyamorphic phases, bridging fundamental science with technological innovation.

Fundamentals

Definition and scope

In physics and materials science, pressure is fundamentally defined as the force per unit area, expressed by the equation $ P = \frac{F}{A} $, where $ F $ is the applied force and $ A $ is the area over which it acts. High pressure refers to conditions exceeding approximately 1 GPa (equivalent to 10,000 atmospheres or 10 kbar), at which atomic and molecular structures of materials undergo significant alterations, such as enhanced compressibility and shifts in bonding characteristics, in stark contrast to low-pressure regimes near ambient conditions (around 0.1 MPa) where such effects are negligible.
The scope of high pressure studies encompasses both static pressures, which are sustained over extended durations to allow equilibrium measurements, and dynamic pressures, generated transiently through shock waves to probe rapid responses. This field is central to condensed matter physics for exploring interatomic interactions, to geophysics for modeling planetary interiors, and to engineering for developing advanced materials under extreme conditions.
Initial compressibility effects, such as measurable volume reductions in solids and liquids, emerge in the 0.11 GPa range, while pressures exceeding 100 GPa enable exotic behaviors like the metallization of insulators. A key distinction in high-pressure environments is between hydrostatic conditions, where pressure is uniformly applied via a fluid medium to minimize shear stresses, and non-hydrostatic conditions, which predominate at higher pressures due to the solidification of transmitting media, introducing deviatoric stresses that influence material deformation.

Units and scales

In high-pressure physics, the standard unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa), defined as one newton per square meter, with the gigapascal (GPa = 10 Pa) serving as the primary multiple for expressing pressures in the gigapascal range and beyond. Historical units, such as the atmosphere (atm 101,325 Pa), bar (10 Pa), and kilobar (10 Pa), were widely used in early experiments but have largely been supplanted by SI units in modern research.
Key conversion factors include 1 GPa 9,869 atm and 145,038 pounds per square inch (psi), facilitating comparisons across engineering and scientific contexts. For ultra-high pressures exceeding 100 GPa, the megabar scale is often employed, where 1 megabar = 100 GPa = 10 Pa.
These scales provide essential context for natural and experimental extremes; for instance, pressures at Earth's core reach up to approximately 360 GPa, while laboratory dynamic methods have attained up to approximately 1 TPa. In scientific literature, pressures are typically notated in MPa (10 Pa) or GPa, with the straightforward relation given by $ P $ (GPa) = $ P $ (Pa) / 10. As a baseline reference, standard atmospheric pressure is approximately 0.1 MPa.

Historical development

Pioneering experiments

The origins of high-pressure research trace back to the late 19th century, when French physicist mile-Hilaire Amagat conducted pioneering experiments on gas compressibility using a piston-based manometer apparatus. Between 1887 and 1893, Amagat developed instruments sealed with viscous liquids such as molasses and castor oil, enabling measurements up to 3,000 atmospheres (0.3 GPa). His work focused on isotherms for gases like oxygen, hydrogen, nitrogen, air, carbon dioxide, and ethylene across temperatures from 0C to 258C, revealing deviations from ideal gas behavior at elevated pressures and establishing foundational data on compressibility coefficients.
In the early 20th century, American physicist Percy Williams Bridgman advanced the field through innovative apparatus designs, beginning systematic studies in 1908. By the 1910s, Bridgman had redesigned his setup with a hydraulic ram and self-tightening packing using rubber or soft metal gaskets, achieving routine pressures of 12,000 kg/cm (approximately 1.2 GPa) and short-term peaks up to 20,000 kg/cm (2 GPa). Over the 1910s to 1930s, he conducted extensive investigations into the compressibility, polymorphic transitions, melting points, and electrical properties of more than 70 elements, alloys, and compounds, often under hydrostatic conditions achieved with transmitting fluids like water or oils to ensure uniform pressure distribution.
These early experiments faced significant challenges, primarily from the limitations of material strength; for instance, steel pistons and vessels frequently deformed or exploded under load, with Bridgman reporting multiple failures during initial trials in 1910. To mitigate this, he incorporated stronger alloys like chrome-vanadium steel and later tungsten carbides, while relying on precise calibration via manganin resistance gauges and fixed transition points such as bismuth's at 25,000 kg/cm. These efforts laid the groundwork for later developments, such as the diamond anvil cell in the mid-20th century.

Key advancements and Nobel contributions

In the mid-20th century, Percy Williams Bridgman's pioneering work in high-pressure physics earned him the 1946 Nobel Prize in Physics for inventing apparatus capable of generating pressures up to several gigapascals, fundamentally expanding experimental capabilities in the field. His innovations, including self-sealing packings and piston-cylinder devices, provided a robust foundation for subsequent research, influencing the design of later high-pressure systems and enabling systematic studies of material behavior under compression. Bridgman's emphasis on operational definitions and precise measurement techniques continued to shape theoretical understanding, as seen in posthumous extensions of his methods into the 1950s and beyond.
A major milestone occurred in 1954 when H. Tracy Hall at General Electric achieved the first reproducible synthesis of diamond from graphite under high-pressure high-temperature conditions of approximately 56 GPa and 1500C, using a belt-type apparatus. The 1950s and 1960s marked significant technological breakthroughs that pushed pressure ranges toward 10 GPa and facilitated key syntheses. In 1958, H. Tracy Hall developed the tetrahedral multi-anvil press, a device using four anvils to achieve uniform pressures of up to 10 GPa at elevated temperatures, revolutionizing large-volume high-pressure experiments. Concurrently, in 1957, Robert H. Wentorf Jr. at General Electric Laboratories synthesized cubic boron nitride (c-BN) under high-pressure high-temperature conditions of approximately 5-6 GPa and 1500-2000C, using alkali metal catalysts to transform hexagonal BN into its superhard cubic phase, a material second only to diamond in hardness. This achievement, patented as Borazon, demonstrated the potential for industrial-scale production of synthetic superhard materials via compression.
International efforts further advanced the field during this era. In the Soviet Union, Leonid F. Vereshchagin established the Institute of High Pressure Physics in 1958, where his team achieved diamond and c-BN synthesis by the early 1960s and developed toroid-type apparatuses reaching pressures of 20 GPa, enabling studies of phase transitions in metals and semiconductors. Japanese researchers contributed to megabar-scale pursuits through refinements in multi-anvil and diamond anvil cell designs starting in the 1960s, with early experiments at institutions like Osaka University exploring pressures beyond 100 GPa for geophysical simulations. The diamond anvil cell, independently invented in 1958 by teams at the National Bureau of Standards and the University of Chicago, extended static pressures to the megabar regime by 1975, allowing in situ optical and X-ray observations of compressed samples.
A notable milestone came in 1996 with the first experimental claim of metallic hydrogen, reported by S. T. Weir, A. C. Mitchell, and W. J. Nellis at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory using dynamic shock compression to 140 GPa (1.4 Mbar), where fluid hydrogen exhibited metallic conductivity, though the observation remains debated due to challenges in confirming the phase. This work built on theoretical predictions and prior high-pressure studies, highlighting the interplay between compression and electronic structure changes.

Techniques for generation and measurement

Static high-pressure methods

Static high-pressure methods enable the generation and maintenance of elevated pressures in laboratory environments under equilibrium conditions, allowing for extended-duration experiments on material behavior. These techniques typically involve mechanical compression using solid or hydraulic components to achieve quasi-hydrostatic conditions, facilitating in-situ measurements such as spectroscopy and diffraction. Unlike dynamic approaches, they prioritize low strain rates to minimize non-equilibrium effects, supporting studies of phase stability and structural changes over hours or days.
The piston-cylinder apparatus represents one of the earliest and most accessible static high-pressure tools, employing a hydraulic press to drive a piston into a cylindrical sample chamber sealed with soft metal gaskets, such as sodium chloride or pyrophyllite, to contain the pressure medium and prevent leakage. This setup routinely achieves pressures of 35 GPa at temperatures up to 2000C, making it suitable for simulating crustal conditions in petrological experiments. The gaskets deform plastically to accommodate volume reduction, ensuring uniform pressure distribution around the sample, though frictional losses along the piston walls limit higher pressures.
Multi-anvil presses extend the accessible pressure range by employing multiple solid anvilstypically eight cubic tungsten carbide cubes arranged to compress an octahedral pressure medium containing the sampleto distribute forces more evenly and access larger sample volumes than uniaxial devices. These systems, often configured in Kawai-type geometry, generate pressures of 1025 GPa with sample volumes on the order of 10100 mm, enabling high-temperature synthesis up to 2500C for geophysical simulations. The applied force $ F $ relates to the target pressure $ P $ via $ F = P \times A $, where $ A $ is the effective anvil face area, though geometric adaptations account for anvil truncation and medium compressibility to optimize hydrostaticity.
The diamond anvil cell (DAC) stands as the cornerstone of static high-pressure research since its development in the late 1950s, utilizing two opposed gem-quality diamonds with flat or beveled culets to compress a sample chamber typically 50200 m in diameter, achieving hydrostatic or near-hydrostatic conditions through a pressure-transmitting medium like helium or silicone oil. Pressures exceeding 300 GPa have been realized, far surpassing other static methods, with the diamond's exceptional hardness and transparency enabling optical access for probes like X-ray diffraction and Raman spectroscopy. Laser heating, often via a doubled Nd:YAG source, allows simultaneous temperatures up to 4000 K, facilitating exploration of extreme interior conditions. The technique originated from early efforts at the National Bureau of Standards to adapt diamond optics for compression.
A key advancement across these methods, particularly the DAC, is in-situ pressure calibration using ruby fluorescence, where tiny chips of ruby (Cr-doped AlO) are embedded in the sample chamber. Excitation at 532 nm produces sharp R-line emissions, with the prominent R line shifting linearly with pressure by approximately 0.365 nm/GPa at room temperature under hydrostatic conditions, allowing non-invasive determination of pressure from the wavelength shift via spectroscopy. This standard, refined over decades, provides accuracy to within 0.1 GPa up to 30 GPa and remains reliable to higher pressures with equation-of-state corrections.

Dynamic high-pressure techniques

Dynamic high-pressure techniques involve the generation of transient shock waves to achieve ultra-high pressures in materials, typically ranging from 100 GPa to several TPa, over durations of microseconds or less. These methods probe non-equilibrium states where rapid compression induces high entropy and allows investigation of dynamic material responses, such as wave propagation and strength, that differ from quasi-static conditions. Unlike sustained compression, shock waves create planar fronts that propagate through samples, enabling the measurement of equation-of-state data under extreme conditions.
Shock waves are generated using high-velocity drivers, including gas guns, chemical explosives, and high-power lasers, which accelerate projectiles or ablate surfaces to launch compression fronts. Gas guns propel impactors to speeds of several kilometers per second, while explosives provide rapid energy release for similar velocities, and lasers deliver intense pulses that vaporize targets, generating plasma-driven shocks. These approaches achieve pressures in the 100 GPa to TPa regime by converting kinetic or photonic energy into compressive work on short timescales, often nanoseconds to microseconds. For instance, two-stage light-gas guns can reach impact velocities up to 8 km/s, producing shocks that reveal material behavior at terapascal levels.
A primary implementation is the plate impact experiment, where a flyer plate is launched at 510 km/s to collide with a target sample, generating a well-defined shock wave. The resulting pressure $ P $ on the principal Hugoniot curve, which describes the locus of shock states, is given by $ P = \rho_0 U_s u_p $, where $ \rho_0 $ is the initial density, $ U_s $ the shock speed, and $ u_p $ the particle velocity behind the shock. Measurements of arrival times and velocities via interferometry or velocity gauges yield these parameters, allowing derivation of sound speeds and yield strength under dynamic loading. Such experiments have mapped Hugoniot states up to 950 GPa in silicates, highlighting deviations from hydrostatic behavior.
Advanced variants include laser-driven compression for inertial confinement, where focused pulses exceed 1 TPa by ablating and imploding targets, creating spherical or cylindrical shocks for fusion-relevant studies. The Z-machine at Sandia National Laboratories employs magnetic compression via pulsed power, imploding liners to terapascal pressures while incorporating laser preheating for enhanced confinement. These techniques measure dynamic properties like sound speed and shear strength, providing insights into material failure and phase changes under non-equilibrium conditions.
The origins of these methods trace to the 1940s during the Manhattan Project, where shock compression was developed for nuclear weapons design, with adaptation to broader materials science in the 1960s through declassified research on equations of state. Early plate impact setups in the 1950s established the field, evolving to include laser and magnetic drivers by the late 20th century. Static methods, such as ruby fluorescence in diamond anvil cells, occasionally calibrate dynamic results at lower pressures for continuity.

Physical and chemical effects

Changes in material properties

High pressure significantly alters the mechanical and thermodynamic properties of materials by reducing their volume and modifying interatomic interactions. Compressibility, a key measure of this response, is quantified by the bulk modulus $ K = -V \left( \frac{\partial P}{\partial V} \right)_T $, which represents the material's resistance to uniform compression. As pressure increases, the bulk modulus typically rises for most solids, liquids, and gases, indicating that materials become progressively harder to compress further due to closer atomic packing and stronger repulsive forces. For example, water, with an initial bulk modulus of approximately 2.2 GPa at ambient conditions, experiences about a 4% volume reduction at 0.1 GPa under isothermal compression, highlighting its relatively high initial compressibility compared to metals.
In solids and liquids, density changes arise directly from this compression, with volume reductions leading to substantial increases in mass per unit volume. For metals, the extent of densification varies with atomic structure and bonding strength; softer alkali metals like sodium can undergo up to fourfold volume compression (corresponding to a density increase of about 4 times) at around 100 GPa, transitioning toward more complex electronic behaviors. In contrast, transition metals like iron show more modest changes, with densities rising by roughly 30-40% at 100 GPa due to their higher initial bulk moduli around 170 GPa. Fluids exhibit similar trends, but with additional rheological shifts: viscosity in liquids generally increases under high pressure as molecular free volume decreases, impeding flow; for instance, certain lubricating oils can see viscosity rise by up to tenfold at pressures exceeding 1 GPa, affecting their performance in high-load applications.
Thermodynamic properties, such as melting points, also shift under pressure according to the Clausius-Clapeyron relation, where increased pressure favors the denser phase and elevates the temperature required for melting in most materials. For iron, the melting temperature rises from 1811 K at ambient pressure to approximately 3090 K at 103 GPa, reflecting strengthened metallic bonding and reduced entropy change upon melting at high densities. These thermal effects can trigger phase transitions in extreme cases, but the continuous modifications in properties like density and modulus provide foundational insights into material stability.
High pressure can further influence electronic properties, enhancing superconductivity in select materials by optimizing phonon-mediated electron pairing. In magnesium diboride (MgB), which has a baseline critical temperature $ T_c $ of about 39 K at ambient pressure, hydrostatic pressure up to 1 GPa initially causes a slight increase in $ T_c $ (by up to 1-2 K in high-quality samples) before a decline, attributed to pressure-induced adjustments in the phonon spectrum. Such enhancements underscore how pressure tunes quantum properties without inducing discrete structural changes.

Phase transitions and new states of matter

High pressure induces discrete phase transitions in solids, where atomic or molecular arrangements undergo abrupt changes to more compact structures, often driven by the minimization of Gibbs free energy. A classic example is the solid-solid transition in silica (SiO), where quartz transforms to the denser polymorph coesite at pressures of 23 GPa under ambient temperatures, followed by a further transition to stishovite at approximately 10 GPa. These transitions are reconstructive, involving significant bond breaking and reformation, and are pivotal in understanding shock-induced changes in crustal rocks.
The thermodynamics of such pressure-induced transitions is governed by the Gibbs free energy difference between phases, where the stable phase is the one with the lowest G at given temperature T and pressure P. For two phases in equilibrium, the change in Gibbs free energy is zero (G = 0), approximated as:
G=HTS+PV
\Delta G = \Delta H - T \Delta S + P \Delta V
G=HTS+PV
Here, H is the enthalpy change, S the entropy change, and V the volume change (typically negative for high-pressure phases, favoring denser structures at elevated P). This PV term shifts the transition pressure upward from ambient conditions, enabling the stabilization of high-density polymorphs.
Beyond structural rearrangements, high pressure can trigger electronic phase transitions, such as from insulating to metallic states, by altering orbital overlaps and band structures. Solid oxygen, for instance, undergoes an insulator-to-metal transition at 96 GPa, accompanied by a structural change within its -phase without molecular dissociation, leading to increased electrical conductivity. Similarly, sodium at around 200 GPa forms an electride phase, where valence electrons become interstitial and delocalized, exhibiting topological semimetal properties with Dirac nodal surfaces.
Extreme pressures also yield novel states of matter inaccessible at ambient conditions. Water, under compression exceeding 50 GPa and temperatures near 2,000 K, enters a superionic phase (ice XVIII), where oxygen ions form a fixed lattice while protons diffuse freely, resembling a solid-liquid hybrid with high ionic conductivity. Metallic hydrogen, a long-predicted state, is theoretically expected above 500 GPa, where molecular bonds dissociate into an atomic lattice with superconducting potential at low temperatures.
A landmark discovery in the 1980s confirmed that silicate perovskites, synthesized at mantle-relevant pressures above 24 GPa, dominate the lower mantle's mineralogy, constituting approximately 50% of Earth's total volume and influencing its seismic profile and heat transport.

Applications and implications

Industrial and technological uses

One of the most prominent industrial applications of high-pressure technology is the synthesis of diamonds using the high-pressure high-temperature (HPHT) method, first achieved by General Electric in 1955 through the use of a belt press apparatus. This process replicates natural diamond formation by subjecting a carbon source, such as graphite, along with a metal catalyst, to pressures of 56 GPa and temperatures around 1,400C, enabling the direct conversion from graphite to diamond within the stable diamond region of the carbon phase diagram. Today, HPHT accounts for over 90% of industrial diamond production, with global output of approximately 15.4 billion carats annually as of 2023, primarily for abrasives, cutting tools, and drilling applications, generating billions in economic value for industries like mining and manufacturing.
The growth rate of diamonds in the HPHT process is fundamentally influenced by pressure (P) and temperature (T), as determined by their position relative to the graphite-diamond equilibrium line in the carbon phase diagram; for instance, optimal growth occurs at P > 5 GPa and T 1,4001,600C, where the supersaturation of carbon in the metallic catalyst drives the rate of diamond nucleation and layer-by-layer addition, typically yielding crystals at rates of several micrometers per hour under controlled conditions.
High-pressure synthesis also enables the production of superhard materials like polycrystalline diamond (PCD) and cubic boron nitride (cBN), which are formed by sintering fine particles of diamond or hexagonal boron nitride with metal binders under extreme pressures exceeding 5 GPa and temperatures above 1,400C. These materials exhibit hardness approaching that of single-crystal diamond, making them essential for cutting tools in machining ferrous and non-ferrous metals, where they extend tool life by up to 100 times compared to traditional carbide inserts and reduce manufacturing costs in automotive and aerospace sectors. PCD, in particular, dominates non-ferrous applications, while cBN is preferred for high-speed steel machining due to its thermal stability, contributing to an annual market value exceeding $1 billion for superabrasives.
In food processing, high-pressure processing (HPP) utilizes isostatic pressures of 400600 MPa at ambient temperatures to achieve pasteurization, inactivating pathogens like Listeria and Salmonella by disrupting microbial cell membranes without applying heat, thereby preserving nutritional value, flavor, and texture in products such as juices, ready meals, and seafood. This non-thermal method has revolutionized the industry by extending shelf life to 23 times that of conventional pasteurization while minimizing energy use and waste, with global HPP capacity exceeding 600 units as of 2025 and enabling premium pricing for high-quality preserved foods, valued at hundreds of millions annually.

Geophysical and planetary science insights

High-pressure research has profoundly illuminated the composition and dynamics of Earth's interior. In the 1990s, diamond anvil cell (DAC) experiments synthesized and characterized MgSiO perovskite, now known as bridgmanite, confirming it as the most abundant mineral in the planet, constituting approximately 38% of Earth's volume in the lower mantle. This phase dominates from depths of about 660 km to 2,700 km, where pressures exceed 24 GPa, providing a structural framework that aligns with geophysical models of mantle convection and heat transfer.
Deeper in the mantle, near the core-mantle boundary (CMB) at around 135 GPa and 2,500 K, MgSiO undergoes a phase transition to post-perovskite, a denser structure observed in the D layer spanning the lowermost 200300 km of the mantle. This transition, first identified through in situ X-ray diffraction in laser-heated DACs, explains seismic discontinuities such as shear-wave velocity reductions by up to 3% across the D layer, influencing mantle plume dynamics and slab subduction. At the core, the melting curve of iron, extrapolated and measured to 300 GPa, indicates a melting temperature of approximately 6,0006,500 K at inner core boundary conditions (around 330 GPa), with recent 2024 studies refining this to about 6,230 K, constraining the thermal state and solidification processes that generate Earth's magnetic field.
Seismic wave speeds in the deep Earthcompressional (Vp) and shear (Vs) velocitiescorrelate closely with high-pressure elastic properties of these phases. For instance, ab initio calculations and Brillouin scattering measurements of MgSiO perovskite and post-perovskite under mantle pressures (up to 150 GPa) yield Vp values of 1013 km/s and Vs of 68 km/s, matching preliminary reference Earth model (PREM) profiles and resolving lateral heterogeneities in the lower mantle. Iron-bearing variants further refine these matches, with iron content reducing Vs by 12% per 10 mol%, aiding interpretations of tomographic images that reveal subducted slabs and hotspots.
Beyond Earth, high-pressure insights extend to planetary science, particularly exoplanets. In super-Earths with masses 510 times Earth's, mantle models predict MgSiO post-perovskite persists up to 1 TPa before dissociating into oxides, influencing radiative heat transport and magnetic dynamo activity in these worlds. For icy giants like Uranus and Neptune, where pressures reach 100200 GPa in water-rich mantles, phases such as Ice VIIa body-centered cubic structureform under 260 GPa and temperatures up to 1,000 K, contributing to layered convection and the planets' tilted magnetic fields through ionic conductivity.
