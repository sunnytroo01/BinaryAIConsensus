Bob Waterfield

 Bob Waterfield (July 26, 1920 March 25, 1983) was an American professional football player who excelled as a quarterback in the National Football League (NFL), most notably leading the Cleveland Rams to an NFL championship in his rookie season of 1945 and the Los Angeles Rams to another title in 1951. Born in Elmira, New York, and raised in California, Waterfield first gained prominence at UCLA, where he quarterbacked the Bruins to a Rose Bowl victory in 1943 before entering the professional ranks.
Over an eight-year NFL career spent entirely with the Rams franchisefirst in Cleveland (1945) and then in Los Angeles (19461952)Waterfield appeared in 91 games, amassing 11,849 passing yards, 97 touchdown passes, and a total of 573 points, while also contributing as a punter and placekicker. He earned NFL Most Valuable Player honors as a rookie in 1945, was selected to five All-NFL teams (either first- or second-team), and led the league in passing touchdowns twice (1945 and 1946). Waterfield's innovative play-calling and leadership helped revolutionize the quarterback position during the league's early postwar era, and he was inducted into the Pro Football Hall of Fame in 1965.
Beyond the field, Waterfield's personal life intersected with Hollywood glamour through his marriage to actress Jane Russell, whom he met as a high school sweetheart at Van Nuys High School in Los Angeles. The couple wed on April 24, 1943, in Las Vegas and remained together for over 25 years until their divorce in 1968; during this time, they co-founded the Russ-Field Corporation, a film production company that produced several movies. Waterfield, who passed away at age 62 from a heart attack, is remembered not only for his athletic prowess but also for bridging the worlds of professional sports and entertainment.

Early life and education

Childhood and family background

Robert Stanton Waterfield was born on July 26, 1920, in Elmira, New York, to parents Staton "Jack" Waterfield and Frances Gallagher Waterfield.
Shortly after his birth, the family relocated to Van Nuys in the San Fernando Valley area of Los Angeles, California, seeking better job opportunities for his father.
In March 1930, when Waterfield was nine years old, his father Jack died suddenly at age 38 from acute indigestion while at a doctor's office, leaving Frances to raise Waterfield alone.
Growing up in Van Nuys, Waterfield developed an early passion for sports, often playing baseball and football with friends in local vacant lots and schoolyards, which laid the groundwork for his future athletic pursuits.
During his childhood in the neighborhood, Waterfield became acquainted with fellow Van Nuys resident Ernestine Jane Geraldine Russell, a connection that would later evolve into a significant personal relationship.

High school athletic career

Bob Waterfield attended Van Nuys High School in Van Nuys, California, beginning in 1934 after his family's relocation to the Los Angeles area.
At Van Nuys, Waterfield emerged as an all-around athlete, participating in football and gymnastics, where he demonstrated versatility and skill that foreshadowed his future success. He earned recognition as one of the greatest athletes in Los Angeles City Section history during his time there.
In football, Waterfield played as a quarterback and did not become the starting signal-caller until his senior year in 1937, leading the team in that role through graduation in 1938. His performances drew early local attention as a promising talent, attracting interest from colleges.
Waterfield also developed a personal relationship during high school with Jane Russell, a fellow Van Nuys High School student who became his sweetheart; the two would later marry in 1943.

College career and military service

UCLA Bruins performance

Bob Waterfield enrolled at the University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA) in 1940 as a physical education major after working in an aircraft factory following his high school graduation. He did not play football during his freshman (1940) year, effectively redshirting that season before making his debut with the Bruins in 1941.
As a versatile triple-threat player, Waterfield excelled at quarterback, punter, and safety for the UCLA Bruins, contributing on both offense and defense while balancing his academic commitments during the early years of World War II, when eligibility rules for student-athletes were influenced by wartime demands on higher education. In the 1941 season, his first year of varsity play, he completed 49 of 130 passes for 828 yards and three touchdowns.
Waterfield's breakout came in 1942, when he guided the Bruins to their first Pacific Coast Conference (PCC) championship and a berth in the Rose Bowl, marking UCLA's inaugural appearance in the game; he also earned selection as the quarterback on the All-Pacific Coast team. That season, he threw for 1,095 yards on 57 completions out of 140 attempts (40.7% completion rate) with 12 touchdown passes, while leading the nation in punting average. His leadership helped secure UCLA's first victory over rival USC, solidifying his status as a campus standout amid the disruptions of wartime rationing and enrollment shifts.
In the 1943 Rose Bowl against top-ranked Georgia, Waterfield quarterbacked the Bruins in a defensive struggle that remained scoreless through three quarters before Georgia scored a safety on a blocked punt by Waterfield deep in UCLA's end zone and added a touchdown for a 9-0 victory. Despite the loss before over 90,000 fans in Pasadena, Waterfield's poise under pressure highlighted his defensive contributions as a safety and his overall command of the game.
Waterfield returned to UCLA for the 1944 season after a military interruption, completing 55 of 136 passes for 901 yards and nine touchdowns while ranking first in the PCC in punting; the Bruins finished with a 4-5 record, but he earned second-team All-Pacific Coast honors as quarterback. His ability to manage rigorous training, games, and coursework as a physical education student exemplified the resilience required of athletes navigating World War II's impact on college sports eligibility and campus life.

U.S. Army enlistment and duties

In early 1943, at the age of 22, Bob Waterfield left the University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA), shortly after leading the Bruins in the Rose Bowl, to enlist in the U.S. Army during World War II. Motivated by the call to serve amid the ongoing global conflict, he sought to become an officer and was sent to Fort Benning, Georgia, for Officer Candidate School.
At Fort Benning, Waterfield was commissioned as a second lieutenant and assigned to the 176th Infantry Regiment of the 45th Infantry Division. During his brief active duty, he continued his athletic pursuits by playing quarterback for the regiment's football team, known as the 176th Infantry Spirits, which competed against other military units to maintain morale and physical fitness among service members.
Waterfield's military service was cut short when he sustained a knee injury during a football game with the 176th Infantry team, exacerbating a prior condition from his college play. This led to his honorable medical discharge in June 1944, after only a few months of service, allowing him to return to UCLA for the 1944 football season.

Professional football career

NFL debut and 1945 championship season

Waterfield was selected by the Cleveland Rams in the fifth round, 42nd overall, of the 1944 NFL Draft while still serving in the U.S. Army. Upon his discharge from military service, he joined the Rams for the 1945 season without any reported trade, making his NFL debut as a rookie that year. His background at UCLA, where he had excelled as a multi-positional player, facilitated a smooth transition to professional football.
In 1945, Waterfield quickly established himself as a versatile contributor, serving primarily as the quarterback but also handling punting, placekicking, and defensive back duties for the Rams. He shared quarterback responsibilities early in the season with halfback Dante Magnani and others, starting only four of the team's ten regular-season games amid an adaptation to the pro level's physical demands. Despite this, Waterfield completed 89 of 171 passes for 1,609 yards and 14 touchdowns, leading the Rams to a dominant 91 regular-season record in the NFL's Western Division. His kicking prowess was evident as well, making 1 of 3 field goal attempts and 31 of 34 extra points, a performance that underscored his all-around value.
The Rams advanced to the NFL Championship Game on December 16, 1945, at Cleveland Municipal Stadium, where Waterfield orchestrated a narrow 1514 victory over the Washington Redskins. In the title contest, he threw two touchdown passesa 37-yarder to Jim Benton and a 44-yarder to Jim Gillettefor 192 yards, while also contributing on defense with an interception. The win marked the Rams' first NFL championship and highlighted Waterfield's rookie dominance. For his outstanding performance, he was awarded the Joe F. Carr Trophy as the league's most valuable player, becoming only the second rookie to earn the honor.

Mid-career achievements (19461950)

Following the Rams' relocation to Los Angeles for the 1946 season, where they began playing home games at the Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum, Bob Waterfield solidified his role as the team's primary quarterback and offensive leader. In that year, he led the NFL in pass completions with 127 and pass attempts with 251, while throwing for 1,747 yards and 17 touchdowns across 11 games. Waterfield earned first-team All-Pro honors and contributed defensively with five interceptions, helping the Rams achieve a 6-4-1 record and a second-place finish in the NFL Western Division. His versatility as a passer, punter, and defender exemplified the two-way play common in the era, setting the foundation for the franchise's West Coast identity.
The 1947 and 1948 seasons saw Waterfield continue to anchor the Rams' offense amid the transition to their new market, though the team posted middling results of 6-6 in 1947 and 6-5-1 in 1948. In 1948, he threw for 1,354 yards and 14 touchdowns in eight starts, while intercepting four passes on defense, contributing to a unit that kept games competitive. Waterfield's leadership helped evolve the Rams' T-formation attack into one of the league's more dynamic passing games, emphasizing deep throws to receivers like Tom Fears and Elroy Hirsch, though he did not receive All-Pro recognition that year.
Waterfield's performance peaked in 1949, when he started 11 games and threw for a career-high 2,168 yards with 17 touchdowns, earning first-team All-Pro honors for the third time in five seasons. His efforts propelled the Rams to an 8-2-2 record and the Western Division title, though they fell short in the NFL Championship Game. Defensively, Waterfield added to his career total of 20 interceptions, showcasing his value in an era without specialized positions.
In 1950, injuries limited Waterfield to six starts, where he completed 57.3% of his passes for 1,540 yards and 11 touchdowns, leading the NFL in completion percentage and earning a Pro Bowl selection along with second-team All-Pro honors. Despite sharing quarterback duties with Norm Van Brocklin, his presence stabilized the offense as the Rams finished 8-4 and reached the divisional playoffs. Waterfield's mid-career span highlighted his role in pioneering the Rams' high-powered aerial attack, which averaged over 25 points per game in 1946 and influenced modern passing strategies.

Final seasons and retirement (19511952)

In the 1951 season, Bob Waterfield shared quarterback responsibilities with Norm Van Brocklin amid recurring injuries, starting 10 of the Los Angeles Rams' 12 regular-season games. He completed 88 of 176 passes for 1,566 yards and 13 touchdowns, leading the NFL in passer rating (81.8) and yards per attempt (8.9). The Rams compiled an 84 record and advanced to the NFL Championship Game, where Waterfield's team defeated the Cleveland Browns 2417 to claim the league title. For his performance, he earned second-team All-Pro honors from the Associated Press, New York Daily News, and United Press International, as well as a Pro Bowl selection.
Waterfield's participation diminished further in 1952 due to a broken hand and other injuries, as he split starting duties evenly with Van Brocklin, appearing in all 12 games but starting only six. He went 51 of 109 passing for 655 yards and three touchdowns during the regular season. The Rams posted a 93 mark and reached the divisional playoff round, falling 3121 to the Detroit Lions in Waterfield's final game.
On December 1, 1952, the 32-year-old Waterfield announced his retirement effective at the season's end, attributing the decision to the cumulative physical toll of his injuries over eight professional seasons. The Rams marked the occasion with "Bob Waterfield Day" during their December 14 home finale against the (/page/Green Bay Packers), and the franchise retired his No. 7 jersey that yearthe first such tribute in team history. Looking ahead, Waterfield expressed intentions to pursue coaching opportunities with the Rams while expanding his entertainment pursuits, including film production alongside his wife, actress Jane Russell.

Overall statistics and positional versatility

Bob Waterfield's NFL career showcased his proficiency as a quarterback, with 814 completions on 1,617 attempts for 11,849 passing yards, 97 touchdowns, and 128 interceptions, resulting in a 50.3% completion rate and a 61.6 passer rating. His passing prowess was highlighted by a career-high 2,168 yards in 1949, during an era when forward passing was still evolving as a primary offensive strategy.
Beyond quarterbacking, Waterfield contributed significantly in multiple roles, amassing 13 rushing touchdowns on 75 carries for 21 yards, while serving as the team's punter with 315 punts averaging 42.4 yards for a total of 13,367 yards. As the placekicker, he connected on 60 of 110 field goal attempts (54.5%) and 315 of 336 extra points (93.8%), accounting for 573 total points scored across eight seasons and 91 games. Defensively, he played safety during his first four seasons, intercepting 20 passes for 228 return yards.

Waterfield's versatility distinguished him as one of the most multi-faceted players in early NFL history, uniquely excelling as a punter, kicker, and defensive back while serving as quarterback for the Los Angeles Rams. This all-around capability was integral to two NFL championships (1945, 1951), where his contributions extended beyond offense to special teams and secondary play, setting a standard for player adaptability in the single-platoon era.

Post-football endeavors

Film production and acting involvement

Following his retirement from professional football in 1952, Bob Waterfield transitioned into the entertainment industry, leveraging his marriage to actress Jane Russell to explore opportunities in film production and acting.
In 1954, Waterfield and Russell co-founded Russ-Field Productions, an independent film company aimed at producing motion pictures outside the major studio system. The venture was influenced by Russell's established Hollywood career and Waterfield's growing interest in the business, allowing them to collaborate on projects that capitalized on her star power while giving him hands-on experience in production.
Russ-Field's key productions included Gentlemen Marry Brunettes (1955), a musical comedy starring Russell and Jeanne Crain; Run for the Sun (1956), an adventure thriller featuring Richard Widmark and Jane Greer; The King and Four Queens (1956), a Western with Clark Gable and Eleanor Parker; and The Fuzzy Pink Nightgown (1957), a romantic comedy led by Russell and Ralph Meeker. These films represented Waterfield's role as executive producer, where he oversaw aspects of financing and development, drawing on his football fame to secure industry connections and talent.
Waterfield had some acting experience during his playing career, including portraying football player Bob Miller in the adventure film Jungle Manhunt (1951), where he appeared alongside Johnny Weissmuller. Following retirement, he appeared as himself in Crazylegs (1953), a biographical film about his Rams teammate Elroy Hirsch. He had uncredited or minor parts in other productions, using his celebrity from sports to gain entry into Hollywood circles.
Despite initial promise, Russ-Field faced significant challenges, including financial losses from underperforming films like The King and Four Queens, which failed to achieve commercial success. These setbacks, compounded by the competitive independent production landscape, led to the company's dissolution in 1957 after the release of The Fuzzy Pink Nightgown.

Coaching positions with the Rams

After retiring as a player, Waterfield returned to the Los Angeles Rams organization in coaching capacities during the 1950s. He served as the team's kicking coach in 1954 under head coach Hamp Pool and continued in that role in 1955 under Sid Gillman. In 1958, Waterfield rejoined the Rams' staff as an assistant coach focused on quarterback development.
Following Gillman's resignation after a 210 season in 1959, Waterfield was appointed head coach in January 1960, signing a five-year contract. In his first year, the Rams compiled a 471 record, finishing sixth in the NFL Western Conference. The 1961 season yielded a similar 410 mark, again placing the team sixth. Waterfield's tenure ended midway through 1962; after an 17 start, he resigned on November 6, citing the team's poor performance, with defensive coordinator Harland Svare named as his successor for the remainder of the season. His overall head coaching record with the Rams was 9241.
Following his resignation, Waterfield remained affiliated with the Rams in a scouting capacity through the 1960s and into the 1970s, contributing to personnel evaluations during the franchise's transitional periods.

Personal life and legacy

Marriages, family, and adoptions

Bob Waterfield met Jane Russell at Van Nuys High School in Los Angeles, where they became sweethearts during their teenage years. On April 24, 1943, the couple married in Las Vegas, Nevada, shortly before Waterfield entered military service. Their union blended Waterfield's football career with Russell's rising Hollywood stardom, leading them to co-found Russ-Field Productions in 1953 to develop films together. The marriage lasted 25 years until their divorce was finalized on July 30, 1968, amid strains from conflicting professional demands and mutual infidelities.
Unable to have biological children due to Russell's earlier health issues, Waterfield and Russell adopted three children during the 1950s. In February 1952, they adopted their daughter Tracy from an orphanage. Later that year, in December, they adopted son Thomas (often called Tommy), a 15-month-old boy whose adoption from Londonwith a mother of Irish origin from Derrydrew international media attention and scrutiny, sparking a scandal over alleged impropriety and influencing international adoption policies. Their third child, son Robert "Buck" Waterfield, joined the family in 1956. The family resided in a home in the Encino area of Los Angeles, where they navigated the challenges of public life under Russell's fame, including intense media interest in their adoptions and personal affairs.
Waterfield and Russell shared family hobbies such as hunting and fishing, activities that provided respite from their high-profile careers; upon his 1952 retirement from playing, Waterfield received hunting gear as a gift from teammates. The couple's divorce proceedings emphasized amicable arrangements for child custody and property division, reflecting their commitment to their family's stability despite the union's end.
Following the divorce, Waterfield remarried Janet Ann Green in 1970; the couple had no additional children and remained together until Waterfield's death. Their adopted children pursued private lives away from the spotlight: Tracy married and became Tracy Foundas, Thomas adopted the initial K. as part of his name, and Buck maintained a low profile while honoring his parents' legacy through family tributes.

Health issues, death, and posthumous recognitions

In the years following his coaching stint with the Los Angeles Rams in the early 1960s, Waterfield experienced a decline in health due to a prolonged respiratory illness that increasingly limited his activities. This condition, which began manifesting in his later middle age, required extended medical care and contributed to his overall reduced public presence after retiring from professional football endeavors. His second wife, Janet Ann Green, provided support during this period, remaining by his side as his health deteriorated.
Waterfield was hospitalized at St. Joseph Medical Center in Burbank, California, on March 11, 1983, and passed away there on March 25 from respiratory failure at the age of 62. His funeral was attended by notable NFL figures, including longtime Rams teammate and rival Norm Van Brocklin, who delivered a toast in Waterfield's honor at the subsequent wake. Following the service, Waterfield was cremated, and his ashes were scattered beneath pine trees at his favorite hunting spot near Caliente, California.
Posthumously, Waterfield's personal legacy has been preserved through family tributes and reflections on his role in early adoption advocacy during his marriage to Jane Russell, which influenced the establishment of the WAIF (World Adoption International Fund) to support international adoptions. In 2019, his son Thomas K. Waterfield shared memories of family outings in Santa Maria, California, highlighting Waterfield's approachable fatherhood amid his celebrity life. No major new personal awards or honors have emerged between 2020 and 2025, but his enduring family narrative underscores a commitment to philanthropy in adoption causes originally championed during his lifetime.

Honors and hall of fame induction

Major NFL awards

Bob Waterfield earned the NFL's Most Valuable Player award in 1945, receiving the Joe F. Carr Trophy as a rookie quarterback for the Cleveland Rams after leading the team to a 9-1 regular-season record and throwing for 1,609 yards and 14 touchdowns. He narrowly edged out Philadelphia Eagles running back Steve Van Buren for the honor, becoming the first rookie to win the league MVP.
Waterfield received three First-Team All-Pro selections at quarterback from the Associated Press in 1945, 1946, and 1949, recognizing his dual-threat capabilities as a passer and defensive back. He earned two Second-Team All-Pro honors in 1950 and 1951, during which he shared quarterback duties with Norm Van Brocklin while maintaining strong performance in passing and kicking.
He was selected to the Pro Bowl twice, representing the Los Angeles Rams in 1950 and 1951 as one of the league's top quarterbacks. These appearances highlighted his versatility, as he contributed significantly on offense, defense, and special teams.
As a player, Waterfield helped secure two NFL championships: the 1945 title with the Cleveland Rams, where he threw two touchdown passes in a 15-14 victory over the Washington Redskins in the championship game, and the 1951 title with the Los Angeles Rams, defeating the Cleveland Browns 24-17 in a high-scoring affair.
Waterfield led the NFL in key passing categories multiple times, tied for the lead in touchdown passes in 1945 (14) and 1946 (17), as well as passer rating in 1951 (81.8); he also tied for the lead in touchdown passes in those years but did not lead in passing yards. His multifaceted skills extended to special teams, where he led the NFL in field goals made in 1949 (9) and 1951 (13), earning recognition for his kicking prowess alongside his quarterback duties. Additionally, his punting average of 42.4 yards per punt over his career was among the best in the league, contributing to All-NFL honors for his special teams excellence in multiple seasons.

Inductions and franchise tributes

Bob Waterfield was inducted into the Pro Football Hall of Fame as part of the Class of 1965, recognizing his pivotal role as a quarterback, defensive back, kicker, and punter who led the Rams to two NFL championships and earned two MVP awards during his eight-season career. His enshrinement highlighted his versatility and leadership, with career passing totals of 11,849 yards and 97 touchdowns, as well as his contributions to the evolution of the quarterback position in the post-World War II era.
Posthumously, Waterfield was honored as a charter member of the UCLA Athletic Hall of Fame in 1984, one year after his death, celebrating his standout college career where he quarterbacked the Bruins to a 1943 Rose Bowl victory and earned All-America recognition in 1942. This induction underscored his impact on UCLA football, including his record-setting performances as a passer and punter during an era interrupted by military service.
The Rams franchise paid early tribute to Waterfield by retiring his No. 7 jersey in 1952 upon his retirement, marking the first such honor in team history and symbolizing his foundational contributions as the club's inaugural superstar. Later, as part of the St. Louis Rams' Ring of Fame established in 1999, Waterfield was inducted alongside other legends, affirming his enduring legacy within the organization during its Midwest tenure. His broader influence extended to the quarterback role, pioneering accurate passing and multi-positional play that shaped modern NFL strategies, though he remains eligible for the College Football Hall of Fame based on his UCLA accomplishments without formal induction to date.
