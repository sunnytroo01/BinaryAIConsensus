Vladimir Drinfeld

1x
 Vladimir Gershonovich Drinfeld (born 1954) is a Ukrainian-born American mathematician renowned for his foundational contributions to algebraic geometry, representation theory, and mathematical physics, particularly through the development of quantum groups and advancements in the Langlands program.
Born in Kharkiv (then Kharkov, USSR), Drinfeld grew up in a family of academics, with his father a professor of mathematics and his mother a professor of Latin. He demonstrated early talent in mathematics, earning a gold medal at the International Mathematical Olympiad in 1969. Drinfeld pursued his undergraduate and graduate studies at Moscow State University from 1969 to 1977, completing a bachelor's degree in 1974 and a PhD in 1978 under the supervision of Yuri Manin.
Early in his career, Drinfeld worked as an assistant professor at Bashkir State University in Ufa from 1978 to 1980 and then at the Institute for Low Temperature Physics and Engineering in Kharkiv from 1981 to 1998. In 1999, he joined the University of Chicago as a professor, where he holds the Harry Pratt Judson Distinguished Service Professor position and continues his research.
Drinfeld's most influential work includes the introduction of Drinfeld modules in the 1970s, which provided new analogies between elliptic curves over number fields and function fields, enabling a proof of the global Langlands conjecture for GL(2) over function fields using the concept of shtukas. In the 1980s, he co-developed the theory of quantum groupsdeformations of universal enveloping algebrasas Hopf algebras, bridging algebra, geometry, and physics, including applications in quantum field theory and integrable systems. Collaborating with Alexander Beilinson, Drinfeld launched the geometric Langlands program in the 1990s, reformulating the classical Langlands conjectures in geometric terms and connecting number theory to aspects of quantum field theory, as highlighted by Edward Witten. More recently, his work has advanced p-adic Hodge theory and prismatic cohomology, including a 2012 proof of the independence of -adic local systems for prime .
His groundbreaking achievements have been recognized with the Fields Medal in 1990 "for his decisive breakthrough and prompting a wealth of research in Langlands program and quantum groups," the Wolf Prize in Mathematics in 2018 (shared with Beilinson) for "groundbreaking work in algebraic geometry, representation theory and mathematical physics," and the Shaw Prize in Mathematical Sciences in 2023 (shared with Shing-Tung Yau) for "contributions related to mathematical physics, to arithmetic geometry, to differential geometry and to Khler geometry." Drinfeld was elected to the National Academy of Sciences in 2016 and has held visiting positions, including at the Institute for Advanced Study.

Early Life and Education

Family Background and Childhood

Vladimir Drinfeld was born on February 14, 1954, in Kharkiv, Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic (now Ukraine), into a Jewish family of academics. His father, Gershon Ikhelevich Drinfeld (19082000), was a prominent mathematician who served as head of the Mathematics Department at Kharkiv University from 1944 to 1962 and specialized in differential geometry. His mother, Frida Iosifovna Lutskaya-Litvak (19212011), was a classical philologist and professor of Latin.
The Drinfeld family home in Kharkiv provided an intellectually stimulating environment that nurtured curiosity from an early age, with both parents emphasizing scholarly pursuits amid the challenges of Soviet life. Kharkiv, a major industrial and educational hub in post-World War II Ukraine, had a significant Jewish population that had endured severe losses during the Holocaust, yet retained a vibrant tradition of intellectual achievement in sciences and humanities despite official Soviet policies of atheism and cultural suppression. Jewish families like the Drinfelds often thrived in academia, contributing to the city's reputation as a center for Jewish scholars, including several Nobel laureates in physics and other fields.
Drinfeld's initial exposure to mathematics came through his father's engaging guidance during his childhood, fostering an early interest in the subject within the family's scholarly atmosphere. This familial influence laid the groundwork for his later pursuits, as he grew up in a Soviet-era setting where intellectual development was valued amid broader societal constraints on Jewish cultural expression.

Early Achievements and Schooling

Vladimir Drinfeld attended School No. 27 in Kharkiv from 1965 to 1969, a specialized physico-mathematical institution founded by the renowned mathematician Naum Il'ich Akhiezer to nurture talented students in mathematics and physics. This environment allowed Drinfeld, who had shown prodigious mathematical ability since early childhood under the guidance of his father, Gershon Drinfeld, a professor of mathematics at Kharkov University, to deepen his engagement with advanced topics. By his teenage years, Drinfeld's interests centered on algebra and geometry, areas where he began exploring complex problems independently.
During his school years, Drinfeld participated in various Soviet mathematical competitions, including regional and national olympiads that served as qualifiers for international events. These experiences honed his problem-solving skills and marked his rapid ascent among the country's young mathematical talents in the late 1960s. His standout performance came in 1969, when, at the age of 15, he represented the Soviet Union at the International Mathematical Olympiad (IMO) in Bucharest, Romania. There, Drinfeld scored 40 out of 42 points, securing the gold medal and becoming the youngest gold medalist at the time.
That same year, Drinfeld demonstrated his research potential by authoring his first mathematical paper, "A Cyclic Inequality," which addressed a challenging problem in the style of G.H. Hardy and was later published in Matematicheskie Zametki in 1971. This early publication underscored his precocious talent and foreshadowed his future contributions to mathematics.

University Studies and Early Research

Drinfeld enrolled at the Mechanics and Mathematics Department of Moscow State University in 1969, following his success in the International Mathematical Olympiad, and graduated in 1974 with a specialization in mathematics. During his studies, he began research under the supervision of Yuri Manin starting in 1971, focusing on algebraic geometry and number theory topics influenced by Manin's seminars. He continued as a graduate student at the same institution until 1977, completing his Candidate of Sciences degreethe Soviet equivalent of a PhDin 1978 at Moscow State University, with a dissertation on aspects of the Langlands program over function fields. In 1988, he earned his Doctor of Sciences degree from the Steklov Mathematical Institute in Moscow, a higher qualification akin to a habilitation, based on advanced work in related areas.
Drinfeld's early research centered on developing function field analogues of elliptic curves, introducing the concept of elliptic modules (now known as Drinfeld modules) in his seminal 1974 publication. This work, published in Matematicheskii Sbornik under the title "Elliptic modules," explored modules over rings of functions that mimic the behavior of elliptic curves in number fields, providing tools to address conjectures in the Langlands program for GL(2) over global function fields. By age 24, he had produced around 13 papers, establishing foundational results on these analogues and their applications to class field theory in positive characteristic.
In the 1970s Soviet academic environment, young mathematicians like Drinfeld encountered significant barriers, including travel restrictions that limited international collaborations and conferences, often justified by ideological controls on information exchange. Anti-Semitism and administrative hurdles further constrained opportunities for Jewish scholars, such as difficulties in securing positions in major centers like Moscow. These challenges fostered informal networks and domestic seminars as key avenues for progress among emerging researchers.

Academic Career

Positions in the Soviet Union

Following the defense of his Candidate of Sciences dissertation in 1978 under Yuri Manin at Moscow State University, Drinfeld accepted an appointment as Assistant Professor at Bashkir State University in Ufa, a position he held from 1978 to 1980. This role marked his entry into Soviet academia outside the major centers of Moscow and Leningrad, where he taught mathematics amid the constraints of a regional institution.
In 1980, Drinfeld returned to Kharkiv, his birthplace, and served as Lecturer at Kharkov State University, delivering courses and seminars that contributed to the local mathematical community, including topics in algebraic geometry. This brief teaching position facilitated his integration into Kharkiv's academic environment before transitioning to research-focused work.
From 1981 to 1998, Drinfeld held a research fellowship at the B. Verkin Institute for Low Temperature Physics and Engineering in Kharkiv, part of the Ukrainian Academy of Sciences, where he conducted advanced mathematical investigations despite the institute's primary orientation toward experimental physics. The institution provided a conducive setting for theoretical work, allowing Drinfeld to pursue algebraic and geometric problems in relative isolation from international influences. In 1988, he defended his Doctor of Sciences thesis at the Steklov Institute of Mathematics in Moscow, securing the higher academic degree and affirming his rising prominence within Soviet mathematics.
Drinfeld's career in the Soviet Union was influenced by systemic challenges, including severe restrictions on international travel and access to global conferences until the reforms of perestroika in the mid-to-late 1980s, which gradually eased barriers for Soviet scholars. Based in Kharkiva peripheral hub compared to MoscowDrinfeld relied on domestic networks for collaboration, maintaining ties with mentors like Yuri Manin and engaging with physicists such as Evgeny Sklyanin on topics intersecting algebra and integrable systems. These interactions, often through correspondence or limited visits, sustained his research momentum within the USSR's insular academic landscape.

Transition to the United States

In the years leading up to his permanent relocation, Drinfeld held several short-term visiting positions in the United States, including memberships at the Institute for Advanced Study (IAS) in Princeton, New Jersey, during 1990, 19961997, and January to May 1998. These visits provided opportunities for collaboration with leading mathematicians and exposure to Western academic environments, building on his established reputation following his work in the Soviet Union at the Institute for Low Temperature Physics and Engineering in Kharkiv.
Drinfeld's transition to the United States culminated in his invitation to join the University of Chicago as a full professor in late 1998, arriving on campus in December of that year alongside other prominent recruits to the mathematics department. This move, completed in 1999, was facilitated by the post-Soviet opening of academic opportunities in the West after the USSR's dissolution in 1991, which enabled greater international mobility for scholars like Drinfeld, and was influenced by family considerations, as he relocated with his wife and nine-year-old son to provide a stable environment for their future.
Upon arriving at the University of Chicago, Drinfeld quickly integrated into the department, leveraging long-standing collaborations, particularly with Alexander Beilinson, whom he had known for over two decades and who had joined the faculty in 1993. In his initial years, he began advising graduate students, marking the start of his mentorship role at the institution. This period also saw a shift in his research focus toward deepening explorations in the geometric Langlands program, facilitated by the collaborative resources at Chicago, though he maintained continuity with his earlier algebraic geometry interests.

Major Awards and Honors

Vladimir Drinfeld received the Fields Medal in 1990 from the International Mathematical Union, recognizing his groundbreaking contributions to quantum groups and number theory, particularly in the context of the Langlands program, during his early career at the Institute for Low Temperature Physics in Kharkiv.
In 1992, Drinfeld was elected a corresponding member of the National Academy of Sciences of Ukraine, honoring his emerging international stature in algebraic geometry while still based in his home country. Later, after his move to the University of Chicago in 1999, he was elected to the American Academy of Arts and Sciences in 2008, acknowledging his sustained influence on mathematical physics and representation theory. In 2016, he became a member of the National Academy of Sciences of the United States, reflecting his profound impact on arithmetic geometry and related fields over the subsequent decades.
Drinfeld shared the 2018 Wolf Prize in Mathematics with Alexander Beilinson, awarded by the Wolf Foundation for their pioneering advancements in the geometric Langlands program, including the development of key geometric frameworks that bridge number theory and quantum field theory, at a stage when Drinfeld had established himself as a leading figure in the field.
In 2023, Drinfeld was jointly awarded the Shaw Prize in Mathematical Sciences with Shing-Tung Yau by the Shaw Prize Foundation, cited for their transformative contributions to arithmetic geometry and mathematical physics, notably Drinfeld's invention of shtukas as a foundational tool in p-adic geometry, marking a capstone to his career-long explorations.

Quantum Groups

Quantum groups, also known as quantum enveloping algebras, were independently introduced by Vladimir Drinfeld and Michio Jimbo in the mid-1980s as deformations of the universal enveloping algebras $ U(\mathfrak{g}) $ of semisimple Lie algebras $ \mathfrak{g} $. Jimbo defined $ U_q(\mathfrak{g}) $ in 1985 as a $ q $-deformation incorporating a parameter $ q $ (typically on the unit circle or a root of unity), motivated by solutions to the Yang-Baxter equation in statistical mechanics. Drinfeld's parallel work, presented at the 1986 International Congress of Mathematicians, framed these structures within the broader context of Hopf algebras arising from the quantum inverse scattering method.
As Hopf algebras, quantum groups $ U_q(\mathfrak{g}) $ possess a coproduct, antipode, and counit, enabling them to act on tensor products in a way that mimics classical Lie group actions but with non-cocommutative features. In the classical limit $ q \to 1 $, the coproduct on a generator $ X $ is primitive:

\Delta_q(X) = X \otimes q^h + q^{-h} \otimes X,

The quasi-triangular structure is encoded by a universal $ R $-matrix $ \mathcal{R} \in U_q(\mathfrak{g}) \otimes U_q(\mathfrak{g}) $, satisfying the Yang-Baxter equation $ \mathcal{R}{12} \mathcal{R}{13} \mathcal{R}{23} = \mathcal{R}{23} \mathcal{R}{13} \mathcal{R}{12} $, which provides a braiding on representations and ensures compatibility with the coproduct via $ \Delta^{\mathrm{op}}(a) = \mathcal{R} \Delta(a) \mathcal{R}^{-1} $. Representations of $ U_q(\mathfrak{g}) $ include highest weight modules, analogues of classical Verma modules, generated by a highest weight vector annihilated by raising operators and with weights scaled by $ q $-powers along the weight lattice.
Drinfeld made foundational contributions to the realization and generalization of these structures, notably through the quantum double construction, which builds a quasi-triangular Hopf algebra from any Hopf algebra by taking the double $ D(H) = H^* \bowtie H $ (with opposite comultiplication on the dual), endowing it with a canonical $ R $-matrix. This framework unifies various quantum group realizations and extends to quasi-Hopf algebras. Additionally, Drinfeld introduced twistscocycle elements in $ U_q(\mathfrak{g}) \otimes U_q(\mathfrak{g}) $to deform existing Hopf algebras into new ones while preserving the module category up to equivalence, providing a systematic quantization procedure for Lie bialgebras.
These quantum groups have profound applications in integrable systems, where the $ R $-matrix facilitates solutions to the Yang-Baxter equation, underpinning the quantum inverse scattering method for exactly solvable models like the quantum nonlinear Schrdinger equation. In knot theory, representations of $ U_q(\mathfrak{g}) $ yield quantum invariants, such as colored Jones polynomials, via links colored by highest weight modules and braided by the $ R $-matrix.

Geometric Langlands Program

Shtukas and Related Developments

In the late 1980s, Vladimir Drinfeld introduced shtukas as a key tool in proving the Langlands correspondence for the general linear group over function fields, defining them as pairs (E,)(E, \phi)(E,) where EEE is a vector bundle on a curve over a finite field and :EE(q)\phi: E \to E^{(q)}:EE(q) is a Frobenius morphism relative to the qqq-Frobenius twist E(q)E^{(q)}E(q). This concept was soon generalized in the 1990s to the p-adic setting, where shtukas are moduli stacks parametrizing vector bundles (or more generally, torsors under reductive groups) over schemes over p-adic fields equipped with Frobenius-level structures, often formalized as pairs (X,)(X, \phi)(X,) with :XX(p)\phi: X \to X^{(p)}:XX(p) a morphism compatible with the p-Frobenius twist, extending the notion of Drinfeld modules from characteristic p to mixed characteristic. These p-adic shtukas provide a geometric framework for studying deformations of Galois representations, generalizing elliptic modules to higher rank and incorporating Frobenius actions that capture local Galois data.
Shtukas play a central role in the p-adic Langlands correspondence, bridging automorphic representations of p-adic groups with continuous Galois representations of local fields, by associating to cuspidal automorphic forms geometric objects whose cohomology realizes the desired Galois side. In arithmetic geometry, they facilitate local-global principles, such as those appearing in the study of special values of L-functions and class field theory analogs over p-adic fields, where the moduli stacks of shtukas encode compatibility conditions between local and global data. For instance, rank-1 shtukas over p-adic fields relate directly to Lubin-Tate formal groups, enabling explicit computations of local class field theory via their connected components.
The connections between shtukas and p-adic Hodge theory are profound, with Rapoport-Zink spaces serving as formal moduli spaces of p-adic shtukasspecifically, they classify p-divisible groups (or tale local shtukas) with additional quasi-isogeny data relative to a fixed Frobenius structure, providing integral models for Shimura varieties at primes of good reduction. These spaces, inspired by Drinfeld's original construction, allow the computation of cohomology groups via nearby cycles functors, which relate the generic fiber (tale cohomology) to the special fiber (crystalline cohomology), thus linking de Rham and crystalline structures in the p-adic setting. Such computations are essential for verifying Hodge-Newton decompositions and establishing isomorphisms between cohomology of shtuka stacks and that of associated period domains.
In 2012, Drinfeld proved a conjecture of Deligne stating that for any smooth variety XXX over a finite field, the set of isomorphism classes of irreducible \ell-adic local systems on XXX with finite-order determinant is independent of the prime \ell. This extended previous results for curves, obtained by Laurent Lafforgue from his proof of the Langlands conjecture, to higher-dimensional varieties and has implications for the motivic fundamental group and Tannakian reconstruction in the function field setting.
Recent developments have further integrated shtukas with Peter Scholze's perfectoid spaces, where perfectoid techniques provide uniformizations of shtuka moduli stacks over rigid analytic spaces, enabling the resolution of local Langlands for GL_2(Q_p) and advancing the global p-adic Langlands program through geometric Eisenstein series and Hecke actions. Since 2018, Drinfeld has contributed to prismatic cohomology, a unified p-adic cohomology theory for formal schemes introduced by Bhatt and Scholze, through works on prismatization functors and stacky approaches to prismatic crystals, which relate to shtuka moduli and provide new tools for p-adic Hodge theory. Drinfeld's foundational contributions to shtukas were recognized in the 2023 Shaw Prize in Mathematical Sciences, awarded for their pivotal role in p-adic Hodge theory and the Langlands program, highlighting their expected centrality in ongoing advances toward a full geometric realization of the Langlands correspondence over number fields.
