 Kokomo is a city in central Indiana, United States, and the county seat of Howard County.
As of the U.S. Census Bureau's July 1, 2024, estimate, its population stands at 60,093, reflecting modest growth from the 2020 census figure of 59,603.
The city has long been defined by its manufacturing economy, with automotive production as a cornerstone; major employers include Stellantis, BorgWarner, and Haynes International, contributing to its reputation as a hub for advanced manufacturing and high export activity relative to GDP.
Historically, Kokomo earned the moniker "City of Firsts" through innovations like Elwood Haynes's development of one of America's earliest gasoline-powered automobiles in 1894 and the subsequent founding of the Haynes-Apperson Company in 1898, which produced vehicles until 1924.
Established in 1844 amid Miami Indian lands, the settlement grew rapidly with railroad expansion and natural resource extraction, including natural gas, fueling its industrial ascent in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

History

Founding and Early Settlement

The area now known as Kokomo was originally inhabited by the Miami tribe, with villages documented near Wildcat Creek as early as 1839, consisting of approximately ten houses and around 200 Miami individuals remaining in the vicinity by 1840. Following U.S. treaties with the Miami that ceded lands in northern Indiana, white settlement accelerated after the construction of the Michigan Road in the 1830s, which improved access from Indianapolis northward. Howard County itself was established as a "paper" county by the Indiana legislature in 1835 but remained largely unorganized until the early 1840s.
David Foster, a frontier trader often regarded as the founder of Kokomo, arrived in the area around 1842 and established a trading post, acquiring substantial land holdings through purchases at roughly $2 per acre. On May 15, 1844, commissioners designated the site as the Howard County seat, with Foster donating 40 acres to facilitate development; the town was formally platted by Foster on December 26, 1844. Early arrivals included families such as the Harrisons in 1841, marking the onset of permanent white settlement amid ongoing Miami presence until their forced removal began in 18461847.
The name "Kokomo" originates from a Miami leader known as Kokomo (variously spelled Ma-Ko-Ko-Mo or Co-come-waw), a "head man" who signed the 1834 Treaty of Tippecanoe and was associated with prominent Miami families like those of Meshingomesia and Richardville; it was officially adopted by county commissioners on August 17, 1844, reflecting the site's proximity to Native villages rather than unsubstantiated local legends. A log courthouse was promptly built north of Wildcat Creek to serve the nascent community, underscoring the rapid transition from frontier outpost to county hub.

Railroads and Initial Industrial Growth

The arrival of the Peru and Indianapolis Railroad in Kokomo in January 1853 introduced the first wood-burning locomotive to the community, establishing a vital transportation link that connected the town to larger markets. This line, built by investors in the late 1840s to supplant wagon transport, extended tracks to Kokomo and facilitated the movement of agricultural products and raw materials out of Howard County while enabling imports of supplies essential for local development. The railroad's completion spurred immediate economic activity, including the construction of grain elevators and related infrastructure near the tracks, which supported the processing and shipment of regional commodities like corn and livestock.
Subsequent rail expansions further entrenched Kokomo's position as a transportation hub. By the 1860s, connections to Logansport and Indianapolis integrated the city into broader networks, including lines that would evolve into the Lake Erie and Western Railway and the Pittsburgh, Cincinnati, Chicago and St. Louis Railroad (later part of the Pennsylvania Railroad). These routes enhanced freight efficiency, reducing reliance on cumbersome overland methods and attracting settlers and capital to the area. The cumulative effect was a transition from subsistence farming to commercial agriculture, with railroads enabling the export of surplus goods and fostering the establishment of support industries such as packing plantsexemplified by Rufus Dolman's facility on the east side of town in the early 1860sand brick yards to meet construction demands.
This rail infrastructure laid the foundation for Kokomo's initial industrial base, primarily in agro-processing and basic manufacturing, by providing reliable access to markets and labor. Prior to the natural gas discoveries of the 1880s, the railroads drove sustained population and economic expansion, positioning the city as a manufacturing precursor through improved logistics that lowered costs and expanded trade opportunities. Small-scale operations in lumber milling, grain handling, and construction materials emerged, capitalizing on the influx of workers and materials, though the full manufacturing boom awaited later energy resources.

Natural Gas Boom and Expansion

The natural gas boom in Kokomo commenced in 1886 with the drilling of the area's first significant well on Wildcat Creek, approximately a quarter mile northeast of the intersection of Park and McCann streets in Howard County. This discovery, at a depth of around 900 feet, tapped into vast reserves that initially appeared inexhaustible, providing a cheap and abundant fuel source previously unavailable in the region. The event marked the onset of Indiana's broader Trenton Field exploitation, which shifted local economies toward industrialization by powering energy-intensive manufacturing processes.
Rapid expansion followed as additional wells were drilled; by 1888, the Kokomo Board of Trade reported ten operational wells yielding a combined 46 million cubic feet of gas per day. To capitalize on this resource, city leaders offered incentives such as free natural gas and land grants to prospective manufacturers, drawing enterprises that required high-volume, low-cost energy. Glass production, in particular, proliferated due to the gas's suitability for high-temperature furnaces, revolutionizing operations akin to earlier booms in Pennsylvania. Entrepreneurs like Monroe Seiberling relocated to Kokomo in 1887, establishing early industrial ventures that leveraged the fuel for rubber and related products.
The influx spurred demographic and economic transformation, with Kokomo's population more than doubling during the late 1880s as workers and businesses migrated to exploit the boom. Agricultural dominance gave way to a burgeoning industrial base, fostering infrastructure development and laying groundwork for Kokomo's emergence as a manufacturing hub in east-central Indiana. This period of expansion, however, sowed seeds for later depletion challenges, as unchecked consumption accelerated reserve drawdown without regard for long-term sustainability.

"City of Firsts" Innovations

Kokomo earned its nickname "City of Firsts" primarily through the innovations of inventor Elwood Haynes, who tested the Pioneer, recognized as one of the earliest gasoline-powered automobiles in the United States, on July 4, 1894. The single-cylinder, one-horsepower vehicle achieved speeds of 6 to 7 miles per hour along Pumpkinvine Pike during Kokomo's Independence Day festivities, marking a pivotal moment in local automotive history. This test run predated several national claims, though the Duryea brothers received a patent for a similar vehicle in 1895; Haynes' design featured a vertical two-cycle engine and emphasized practical roadworthiness over prior experimental models.
Building on this foundation, Haynes partnered with brothers Edgar and Elmer Apperson to establish the Haynes-Apperson Automobile Company in 1898, which became one of America's earliest commercial automakers, producing vehicles until the mid-1920s. Haynes further advanced automotive engineering by pioneering the use of aluminum alloys in engine components, enabling lighter and more efficient castings that improved performance in early cars. In 1903, he patented the tilt steering wheel, a safety feature allowing adjustable driver positioning to reduce fatigue and enhance control.
Beyond automobiles, Haynes developed Stellite, a hard cobalt-chromium alloy introduced in the early 1900s, valued for its wear resistance and later applied in cutting tools, aerospace components, and medical implants. His work on corrosion-resistant metals laid groundwork for modern stainless steels, with alloys tested for durability in harsh environments. These metallurgical breakthroughs, refined in Kokomo laboratories, supported industrial applications during World War I and beyond.
Kokomo's legacy extended into electrical systems through Delco Electronics, a General Motors division established in the city, which introduced the first push-button car radio in 1938, simplifying tuning for drivers. By 1956, Delco engineers produced the first transistorized signal-seeking car radio, blending vacuum tubes and transistors for improved reception and compactness. In 1977, the facility installed SIGHT-I, the inaugural industrial computer vision system on a U.S. automotive assembly line, enhancing quality control through automated inspection. These developments solidified Kokomo's role in advancing vehicle electronics and manufacturing precision.

1913 Flood and Reconstruction

The Great Flood of 1913 reached Kokomo, Indiana, on March 25, following five days of intense rainfall totaling up to 11 inches across central Indiana, causing Wildcat Creek to breach its levees and flood the city's southern districts. Water levels surged to eight feet in streets, effectively bisecting the city and rendering bridges impassable due to structural instability from the torrent. Communication disruptions were widespread, with thousands of telephone poles toppled, and the municipal power plant submerged, halting electricity supply and complicating rescue operations.
Property losses were severe, with more than five buildings completely destroyed and over 150 others sustaining major structural damage; neighborhoods adjacent to Park Road and Washington Street experienced total inundation, while Foster Park was similarly overwhelmed by the creek's expansion to nearly a mile wide. No fatalities were recorded in Kokomo itself, though the event mirrored statewide devastation that destroyed over 180 bridges and crippled rail transport, the era's primary artery for goods and aid.
Reconstruction commenced swiftly in the ensuing months, prioritizing the repair and fortification of bridges, roads, and levees along Wildcat Creek to mitigate future overflows. These efforts, funded partly through local and state resources amid Indiana's estimated $25 million in total flood damages (equivalent to hundreds of millions today), incorporated raised embankments and debris clearance, reflecting a regional shift toward engineered flood controls like reinforced dams and walls in vulnerable Midwestern communities. By mid-1914, key infrastructure had been restored, enabling Kokomo's industrial base to rebound, though the flood underscored the limitations of pre-existing levees and prompted ongoing vigilance against creek flooding.

Ku Klux Klan Dominance in the 1920s

In the early 1920s, the Ku Klux Klan rapidly expanded in Indiana, attracting native-born white Protestants concerned with immigration, Catholicism, and perceived moral decay, with statewide membership peaking at approximately 250,000 by the mid-decade. In Kokomo, Klan recruitment mirrored this trend, yielding an estimated 3,998 to 5,000 local members by 1925 from a city population of around 30,000, enabling significant influence over community institutions and elections. The organization's appeal stemmed from its promotion of "100% Americanism," aligning with Prohibition-era values and opposition to urban Catholic political machines, though it also enforced social conformity through intimidation tactics against non-conformists.
The pinnacle of Klan visibility in Kokomo occurred on July 4, 1923, when the city hosted the largest Klan gathering in U.S. history at Malfalfa Park, drawing 50,000 to 200,000 attendees for the inauguration of D.C. Stephenson as Grand Dragon of Indiana and seven neighboring states by national leader Hiram Wesley Evans. Stephenson's address emphasized constitutional fidelity and Protestant dominance, with the event featuring parades, speeches, and cross burnings that showcased organizational discipline and logistical prowess, including Klan-provided concessions and traffic management. This "Konklave in Kokomo"selected partly for the city's name evoking the Klan's "KKK" motif and its ownership of the parksymbolized the group's temporary command of public spaces and underscored Kokomo's role as a regional hub for Klan activities.
Politically, the Klan leveraged its membership to sway local governance, endorsing candidates who pledged support for its platform of excluding Catholics from public office and enforcing moral ordinances. In 1925, Klan-backed Silcott Spurgeon was elected mayor of Kokomo, reflecting the faction's ability to mobilize voters and infiltrate city hall alongside school boards and law enforcement. This control facilitated policies favoring Protestant interests, such as anti-Catholic hiring biases and vice suppression, though overt violence remained limited compared to southern chapters, relying more on economic boycotts and electoral pressure. However, dominance eroded swiftly following Stephenson's 1925 conviction for rape and murder, which exposed internal corruption and prompted mass resignations; by 1928, Kokomo's Klan rolls had dwindled to 4,000, fracturing its political grip as public disillusionment grew.

Mid-20th Century Manufacturing Ascendancy

The postwar economic expansion in the United States fueled rapid growth in Kokomo's manufacturing base, particularly through the automotive supply chain. General Motors' Delco Electronics division, which had established initial operations in Kokomo in 1936 with 428 employees, expanded significantly to meet surging demand for vehicle radios, ignition systems, and other electrical components as civilian automobile production resumed and boomed after 1945. By the 1950s, Delco had become the city's largest employer, anchoring a cluster of related factories that produced starters, generators, and wiring harnesses, contributing to Kokomo's reputation as a key node in Indiana's automotive ecosystem outside Detroit.
This manufacturing surge drove substantial population increases, reflecting influxes of workers attracted by stable, unionized jobs. The city's population rose from 33,795 in 1940 to 38,672 in 1950a 14.4% gainand further to 47,197 by 1960, a 22.0% jump, as families relocated for opportunities in Delco and supporting industries like Continental Steel, which had modernized its operations for automotive-grade products. These figures outpaced state averages, underscoring Kokomo's role in Indiana's broader postwar industrial ascent, where automotive parts manufacturing leveraged proximity to Midwest assembly plants and a skilled labor pool honed by earlier gas-era innovations.
Delco's growth exemplified causal links between national auto demand and local prosperity: U.S. vehicle output climbed from under 2 million units in 1946 to over 8 million by 1955, necessitating scaled-up component production that Delco facilities in Kokomo fulfilled through wartime-honed efficiencies and investments in assembly lines. Employment at Delco Remy division-wide, with major Kokomo plants, expanded from around 10,000 in 1940 to over 14,000 by 1950, sustaining high wages that bolstered middle-class expansion despite occasional strikes over automation. Complementary firms, such as Haynes International's superalloy foundry established in 1912 but thriving on jet and auto engine needs, added specialized manufacturing depth, though automotive electronics remained dominant. This era peaked around 1960, before foreign competition and recessions exerted pressures.

Deindustrialization Pressures from the 1980s Onward

Beginning in the 1980s, Kokomo's economy, anchored by automotive manufacturing giants like General Motors' Delco Electronics division, encountered mounting deindustrialization pressures from global competition, particularly Japanese imports eroding U.S. market share, and cyclical recessions that exposed structural inefficiencies such as high labor costs and overcapacity. The early 1980s auto slump prompted nationwide GM layoffs exceeding 89,000 workers in 1980 alone, with ripple effects in supplier hubs like Kokomo where Delco employed over 10,000 at its peak facilities. Tensions over job security and concessions boiled over in a November 1986 strike at Delco's Kokomo plants, involving 7,500 unionized workers demanding better terms amid automation threats and parts shortages that idled 16,700 GM employees across assembly lines. These events underscored causal factors including legacy wage structures and resistance to productivity-enhancing changes, contributing to a gradual erosion of employment stability despite temporary resolutions.
The 1990s brought further strain as Delco Electronics was spun off from GM into the independent Delphi Corporation in 1999, inheriting burdensome pension liabilities and uncompetitive cost structures amid rising offshoring to low-wage countries like Mexico. Delphi's October 2005 bankruptcy filing, one of the largest in U.S. history, proposed slashing 24,000 of its 28,000 U.S. hourly jobsequivalent to nearly 86%and closing or selling 21 of 29 domestic plants to address $400 million annual costs from idled "jobs bank" workers receiving full pay without production. Although Kokomo's key Delphi sites were spared outright closure through negotiations, the fallout included forced UAW concessions on wages (proposed cuts to $10/hour for production roles), buyout incentives, and tiered labor systems that reduced the local workforce by thousands via attrition and early retirements, reflecting broader sector shifts toward automation and global supply chains.
The 2008 global financial crisis intensified these dynamics, triggering Chrysler's December 2008 idling of its Kokomo transmission and casting plantsmajor employers of nearly 5,000initially for 60 days amid plummeting demand and inventory gluts, with extensions tied to the firm's subsequent bankruptcy. Compounded by Delphi's lingering restructuring and GM's parallel woes, Kokomo's unemployment rate rocketed from under 6% in 2007 to a peak of 19.1% in June 2009, surpassing national averages and signaling acute distress in a metro area where manufacturing comprised over 40% of jobs. Between 2000 and 2010, the Kokomo region shed roughly 25% of total employment, with manufacturing bearing the brunt due to persistent cost disadvantages against foreign competitors and insufficient diversification. These pressures, rooted in causal realities like rigid labor contracts and delayed adaptation to technological shifts, prompted federal bailouts for GM and Chrysler in 2009, which preserved some capacity but at the expense of diluted equity for taxpayers and long-term vulnerabilities to further consolidations.

Ryan White Case and Public Health Debates

Ryan White, born on December 6, 1971, in Kokomo, Indiana, was diagnosed with hemophilia A shortly after birth and contracted HIV through contaminated clotting factor treatments used to manage his condition. He received his AIDS diagnosis on December 17, 1984, at age 13, with physicians estimating he had only six months to live. After treatment-related absences, White sought to return to Western Middle School in nearby Russiaville for the 1985-1986 school year, but the school board barred his attendance on August 1, 1985, citing unsubstantiated risks of transmission to other students despite expert testimony affirming no evidence of casual spread. Petitions supporting the exclusion gathered signatures from 117 parents and 50 teachers, reflecting widespread community apprehension.
White's mother, Jeanne, filed a lawsuit in federal court on August 21, 1985, challenging the expulsion under civil rights laws, leading to temporary phone-based class participation starting August 26, 1985. Full readmission was granted in April 1986 following hearings with medical experts, but persistent harassmentincluding death threats, vandalism to the family home, and false rumors of biting incidentsprompted their relocation to Cicero, Indiana, in May 1987. There, Hamilton Heights High School admitted him after implementing a proactive AIDS education program, contrasting sharply with Kokomo's response and fostering a supportive environment. The Kokomo community remained deeply divided, with some residents viewing the disease through a moral lens as divine retribution, while others grappled with ignorance-fueled fears; oral histories decades later reveal lingering shame and reluctance to revisit the episode publicly.
The case ignited national debates on public health policy, pitting scientific consensus against societal panic. By 1985, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) had established that HIV transmitted exclusively via specific bodily fluidsblood, semen, vaginal fluids, and breast milkand not through casual contact like sharing utensils or airborne means, with no documented school transmissions. Yet public perception lagged, exacerbated by the epidemic's early uncertainties and media portrayals associating AIDS primarily with high-risk behaviors, leading to demands for isolation despite low viral shedding in non-hemophiliacs like White. Educators and officials reported prevalent misconceptions, such as fears of transmission via Band-Aids, underscoring the need for mandatory health education to counter stigma without endorsing unfounded caution.
White's advocacy amplified these discussions, as he testified before Congress and met with figures like President Reagan, humanizing the epidemic beyond stereotypes and advocating for blood supply reforms already underway post-1985 screening implementations. His experience directly influenced the Ryan White Comprehensive AIDS Resources Emergency (CARE) Act, enacted in August 1990 shortly after his death on April 8, 1990, providing federal funding for HIV care, testing, and education to underserved populations while addressing barriers like discrimination. In Indiana, the case spurred localized policy shifts toward inclusive schooling and awareness campaigns, though it exposed tensions between empirical risk assessmentzero casual transmission risk for hemophiliacs with precautionsand visceral community responses rooted in incomplete knowledge. The legacy underscores causal realities: effective public health hinges on disseminating verified transmission data over fear, preventing discriminatory overreactions that hinder integration and care access.

Geography

Physical Setting and Topography

Kokomo occupies a position in north-central Indiana within Howard County, centered at approximately 4029N 8605W. The city spans the watershed of Wildcat Creek, a tributary of the Wabash River, which bisects the surrounding landscape and influences local drainage patterns. This setting places Kokomo amid the broader Central Till Plain physiographic region, shaped by Pleistocene glacial advances that deposited thick layers of unconsolidated till, resulting in a predominantly agricultural expanse with scattered urban development.
The topography of Kokomo features level to nearly flat terrain, with surface slopes typically ranging from 0 to 2 percent, characteristic of the glacial till plains in this part of Indiana. Elevations across the city vary modestly between about 770 feet and 850 feet above mean sea level, reflecting the subtle undulations from glacial deposition rather than significant erosional features or tectonic activity. Predominant soils, such as the Kokomo seriessilty clay loams formed in tillpredominate in low-lying depressions, supporting fertile but poorly drained conditions that necessitate artificial drainage for urban and farming uses.
Hydrologically, Wildcat Creek forms a key natural boundary and corridor through Kokomo, with its meandering channel prone to seasonal flooding due to the flat gradient and upstream agricultural runoff. Smaller tributaries like Kokomo Creek contribute to the local network, feeding into reservoirs such as the nearby Kokomo Reservoir, which aids in water management amid the otherwise uniform plain. This configuration underscores the area's reliance on engineered infrastructure to mitigate the inherent flatness-induced flood risks while leveraging the stable glacial substrate for development.

The warm season spans from late May to mid-September, with July featuring the highest average temperature at 76F, including daily highs reaching 85F and lows of 66F. Winters, from mid-November to early March, bring freezing conditions, with January averages of 27F, highs around 35F, and lows near 20F. Neighborhood Layouts

Kokomo's neighborhoods are organized around a central downtown core, with older districts featuring rectilinear grid patterns and newer suburbs employing curvilinear streets, reflecting the city's evolution from 19th-century industrial growth to mid-20th-century expansion. Approximately one-third of the housing stock dates to before 1940, concentrated in the urban core and adjacent historic areas, while post-1950 developments dominate the periphery, linked by sidewalks, trails, and roadways to schools, parks, and commercial nodes. Land use zoning designates varying residential densitieslow, medium, and highto guide infill on vacant lots and prevent sprawl, prioritizing redevelopment over peripheral annexation.
The Old Silk Stocking Neighborhood exemplifies the historic core, situated immediately west of downtown and bounded by South Phillips Street to the west, North Webster Street to the east, West Taylor Street to the north, and Wildcat Creek to the south. Emerging during the 1890s natural gas boom, which attracted industry and wealth, this district housed affluent professionals in Queen Anne-style homes, including the 1891 Seiberling Mansion built at a cost of $50,000; as of 2008, 350 structures within it were listed on the National Register of Historic Places, though preservation varies. Its layout underscores early class-based spatial segregation, contrasting with contemporaneous working-class housing near factories.
Downtown Kokomo integrates a modest residential footprint amid commercial buildings, supporting mixed-use vitality without extensive suburban-style separation. Peripheral neighborhoods, such as Bent Creek, Cedar Crest, Bon Air, and Boulders West, form post-World War II and later subdivisions on the city's edges, often with wooded lots, ponds, and proximity to highways like U.S. 31 for commuter access; for instance, Fredrick Farms lies on the west side near Dixon Road, emphasizing single-family homes. These areas cater to families and retirees, with recent high-density proposals targeting corridors like East Walnut Street to accommodate growth amid deindustrialization pressures.
The city fosters neighborhood associations to address local maintenance and organization, covering diverse areas from inner-city grids to outer subdivisions, with ongoing infill programs constructing market-rate single-family homes on underutilized lots to counteract vacancy rates exceeding 10% in some older zones. This structure balances preservation of pre-1940 heritage with adaptive reuse, though challenges persist from aging infrastructure and economic shifts reducing manufacturing-driven population densities.

Major Natural Disasters

The city has been impacted by recurrent flooding along Wildcat Creek, with major events including the Great Flood of 1913, when heavy rains from March 23 to 26 caused the creek to overflow its levees, inundating southern Kokomo with up to eight feet of water and necessitating widespread evacuations and infrastructure repairs. Subsequent floods, such as those in July following 13 inches of rainfall, have repeatedly flooded residential and commercial areas, though levee improvements post-1913 mitigated some severity.
Tornadoes represent another primary hazard, with 137 events of magnitude F2 or higher recorded in or near Kokomo since reliable tracking began. The most destructive occurred on April 11, 1965, during the Palm Sunday outbreak, when an F4 tornado tore through Kokomo and Russiaville, destroying homes, businesses, and vehicles across Howard County with winds exceeding 207 mph. More recently, on August 24, 2016, an EF3 tornado with peak winds of 150 mph tracked 8.78 miles through the city for 14 minutes at widths up to 300 yards, injuring 20 people, demolishing structures like a Starbucks at Kokomo Mall, and causing millions in damage without fatalities. These events underscore Indiana's position in a region conducive to supercell thunderstorms, though no major earthquakes or hurricanes have struck due to the area's inland Midwestern geography.

Post-1960, the city saw stagnation and intermittent declines, with population dipping to 44,042 in 1970 before recovering modestly to 47,808 in 1980, reflecting initial impacts of national economic shifts and local plant fluctuations. Further erosion occurred through the 1990s and 2000s, dropping to 45,468 by 2010, attributable to manufacturing job losses from automation, offshoring, and closures such as those at Delphi Corporation facilities amid the 2008 recession, which positioned Kokomo among U.S. cities with acute economic distress.

Economy

Core Industrial Base

Kokomo's core industrial base emerged during the Indiana natural gas boom of the 1880s, when vast underground reserves provided inexpensive fuel that propelled rapid manufacturing growth. Local incentives, including free gas and land grants, drew factories for glassworks, metal fabrication, and early heavy industry, transforming Kokomo from an agricultural outpost into an industrial hub by the 1890s. This energy-driven expansion laid the foundation for Kokomo's reputation as the "City of Firsts," fostering innovations like the mechanical corn picker in 1920 and the first mass-production tire-building machine in 1923.
The automotive sector became a cornerstone of Kokomo's industry starting with Elwood Haynes's development of one of America's first gasoline-powered automobiles in 1894, followed by the Haynes-Apperson company's production of vehicles until 1920. Metalworking and steel production also solidified as key pillars, exemplified by the Continental Steel Corporation, formed in 1927 from local mills and becoming a major employer producing nails, wire, and fencing from scrap until its decline post-World War II. By the mid-20th century, automotive manufacturing dominated, with facilities like the Chrysler Indiana Transmission Plantestablished in 1965 for die-cast partsemploying thousands and anchoring the local economy.
Today, advanced manufacturing persists as the industrial core, with major employers including Stellantis (formerly Chrysler), which operates multiple plants supporting over 6,000 jobs in transmission and casting production, alongside BorgWarner for automotive components and Haynes International for high-performance alloys derived from early stainless steel innovations. These sectors account for more than 70% of regional employment, underscoring manufacturing's enduring role despite national deindustrialization trends.

Automotive Manufacturing Dominance

Kokomo's automotive manufacturing heritage originated in 1894 when local inventor Elwood Haynes achieved the first successful road test of a gasoline-powered automobile on the city's streets, predating many national milestones in motorized transport. Haynes subsequently partnered with brothers Edgar and Elmer Apperson to establish the Haynes-Apperson Motor Company in 1902, which produced high-quality automobiles until the early 1920s, contributing to Indiana's early-20th-century status as a hub with over 170 vehicle and parts manufacturers across more than 30 communities by 1919. This early activity earned Kokomo a reputation as "Little Detroit" due to its concentration of automotive innovation and production.
By the mid-20th century, Kokomo solidified its dominance through large-scale operations tied to the "Big Three" automakers, particularly in transmissions and components. General Motors established facilities in Kokomo in 1936 for producing automotive electronic components, which continue to support multiple vehicle platforms. Chrysler, now Stellantis, developed a major transmission complex, including the Kokomo Transmission Plant spanning 3.1 million square feet on a 110-acre site and the Indiana Transmission Plant covering 1.2 million square feet on 233 acres, focusing on propulsion systems for models like minivans. These plants positioned Kokomo as the world's largest community for automotive transmission production, with Stellantis employing 6,614 workers as of 2021, making it the top employer in Howard County.
The sector's preeminence is evident in employment data, where automotive firms like Stellantis, alongside suppliers such as Aptiv (formerly Delphi), dominate local payrolls, historically accounting for a significant share of the workforce amid Indiana's broader manufacturing legacy. This concentration has shaped Kokomo's economy, driving mid-century growth through specialized, high-volume output essential to national vehicle assembly lines, though reliant on Detroit's demand cycles.

Factors Contributing to Mid-Century Boom

The post-World War II economic expansion in the United States, characterized by surging consumer demand for automobiles and household appliances, significantly boosted Kokomo's manufacturing sector. Kokomo's population grew from 30,057 in the 1940 census to 35,706 in 1950 and 44,121 in 1960, driven largely by job opportunities in local factories. This growth mirrored Indiana's broader industrial resurgence, where manufacturing employment doubled from pre-war levels amid national prosperity from 1945 to 1973. Key to Kokomo's boom was the Delco-Remy Division of General Motors, which ramped up production of electrical components like starters, generators, and ignition systems for the booming automotive market; by 1950, Delco-Remy's total employment exceeded 14,600 workers across its Indiana facilities, with Kokomo hosting major plants contributing to high-wage jobs.
Chrysler Corporation's transmission plants in Kokomo, established prior to the war but expanded significantly afterward, further fueled growth by supplying automatic transmissions for domestic vehicle production, capitalizing on the shift toward higher-volume car manufacturing. These facilities benefited from Kokomo's central geographic position, providing logistical advantages for raw material imports and finished goods distribution via emerging interstate highways like U.S. Route 31. The availability of a skilled, semi-rural labor pool, drawn from surrounding Howard County and incentivized by union-negotiated wages often exceeding national averages, supported rapid workforce scaling without acute shortages.
Federal policies, including the GI Bill's facilitation of veteran reintegration into industrial jobs and infrastructure investments under the Interstate Highway Act of 1956, indirectly amplified these dynamics by enhancing mobility and market access. Kokomo's established industrial base from earlier natural gas and automotive innovations provided a foundation for diversification into defense-related subcontracting during the Korean War era, sustaining momentum into the 1960s. However, this reliance on heavy manufacturing also sowed seeds for later vulnerabilities to cyclical downturns.

Causes and Consequences of Manufacturing Decline

The decline in Kokomo's manufacturing sector, particularly automotive production, stemmed primarily from the city's heavy reliance on Chrysler Corporation (later Stellantis) facilities, which employed a significant portion of the local workforce and made the economy vulnerable to national automotive industry downturns. The 1979-1980 recession exacerbated these vulnerabilities, as high fuel prices following the oil crises reduced demand for large American vehicles, while increased competition from fuel-efficient Japanese imports eroded U.S. automaker market share; Chrysler's near-bankruptcy led to widespread layoffs and production cuts, including at Kokomo's transmission plants. Further contributing factors included rising labor costs amid union negotiations, slower adoption of automation compared to competitors, and broader deindustrialization trends such as offshoring of component production, which reduced domestic manufacturing jobs across the Midwest by over 20% from 1980 to 2005 in affected metros.
The 2008-2009 financial crisis intensified the downturn, with Chrysler's bankruptcy filing in April 2009 triggering plant idlings and mass layoffs in Kokomo; unemployment in Howard County surged to over 15%, far exceeding the national average, as the company's transmission and casting operationskey to local employmentfaced temporary shutdowns and reduced shifts. Manufacturing employment in the Kokomo MSA, which peaked around the late 1970s, had fallen by approximately 25% by 2010, reflecting both cyclical recessions and structural shifts away from labor-intensive assembly.
Consequences included persistent population stagnation and outmigration, with Howard County's population peaking at 86,896 in 1980 before declining to 82,363 by 2017, as younger workers sought opportunities elsewhere amid job scarcity. Poverty rates rose sharply, reaching 18.5% in the Kokomo MSA by 2010, correlating with reduced household incomes and increased reliance on social services, while the local tax base eroded from plant underutilization and foregone property payments by Chrysler in 2009. These effects compounded socioeconomic challenges, including higher rates of family instability and skill mismatches in the workforce, though some diversification into logistics and advanced manufacturing mitigated total collapse.

Recent Revitalization Efforts and Investments

In response to manufacturing declines, Kokomo has pursued targeted revitalization initiatives emphasizing housing development, infrastructure improvements, and attraction of advanced manufacturing investments since the early 2020s. The city's Infill Program, administered by the Kokomo Community Development Corporation, constructs market-rate single-family homes on vacant and underutilized lots, with preparations underway for its fifth round of construction as of 2024 and a revamped version set to reopen in late 2025. This effort aims to add over 100 affordable homes, addressing housing shortages while enhancing neighborhood stability.
Significant private and public investments have bolstered the manufacturing sector, particularly in automotive and emerging technologies. Stellantis announced a $100 million investment in October 2025 to upgrade facilities for producing GMET4 EVO engines starting in 2025, creating 100 new jobs alongside a prior $230 million commitment to retool three plants, retain 660 positions, and develop new transmissions. Haynes International pledged $173 million in June 2025 for facility expansions, supported by city tax abatements, to add 111 jobs in specialty metals production. Additionally, the Kokomo Gigafactory reached a construction milestone in March 2025 for electric-vehicle battery manufacturing, while Korean firm JWA selected Kokomo for its first U.S. site to recycle chemical waste for EV battery supply chains.
Downtown redevelopment has focused on mixed-use projects to foster vibrancy and economic activity. Construction began in 2025 on a $110 million "Hub Downtown" development incorporating residential, commercial, and recreational spaces. A 55,000-square-foot hotel and conference center, planned for over a decade, advanced with site work in March 2025 to host events for up to 1,000 guests. Complementing these, a $6 million private investment yielded 58 apartments across new builds and historic restorations, while the city approved $26 million in bonds in July 2025 for the Pleasant Brook industrial project to spur further growth. These initiatives, backed by local resolutions and infrastructure outlays like $33.6 million for the Kokomo Industrial Park, signal a strategic pivot toward diversified, high-tech employment amid national manufacturing resurgence trends.

Government and Politics

Municipal Structure and Administration

The government of Kokomo operates under a mayor-council system as authorized by Indiana statutes for second-class cities, dividing authority into executive and legislative branches. The executive branch is headed by the mayor, who is elected to a four-year term commencing at noon on the first Monday in January following the election. As chief executive, the mayor enforces city ordinances, supervises administrative departments, prepares the annual budget for council approval, and possesses veto power over council legislation, which can be overridden by a two-thirds vote.
The legislative branch consists of the Kokomo Common Council, composed of nine members: six elected from single-member districts apportioned by population and three elected at-large across the city. Council members serve staggered four-year terms, with meetings held to adopt ordinances, resolutions, and the city budget, as well as to confirm mayoral appointments to boards and commissions. The council president, selected from among its members, presides over sessions and performs ceremonial duties.
Administrative operations fall under eight executive departments directed by the mayor, including law, finance, human resources, development, engineering, aviation, public works and safety, and economic development, with additional support from the city clerk (handling elections and records) and controller (managing fiscal affairs). Autonomous entities such as the board of public works, plan commission, and zoning board of appeals handle specialized functions like infrastructure approvals and land-use planning, often with council oversight. Indiana's Dillon Rule limits municipal powers to those expressly granted by state law or necessarily implied, ensuring state-level constraints on local authority.

Political Landscape and Party Control

The City of Kokomo employs a mayor-council form of government, with the mayor elected citywide and the nine-member Common Council consisting of six district representatives and three at-large members. Republicans have controlled both the mayoralty and the council majority since the 2019 municipal elections, when the GOP flipped the council from a prior 7-2 Democratic majority to full Republican dominance.
Tyler O. Moore, a Republican, has served as mayor since January 2020, following his 2019 election victory with 69% of the vote over Democrat Abbie Smith. Moore was reelected in November 2023, securing a second term amid Republican retention of council control, though Democrats captured the Second District seat held by Bob Stephenson, resulting in an 8-1 Republican majority. 
Howard County, encompassing Kokomo, exhibits a consistent Republican voting pattern in federal elections, supporting the GOP presidential nominee in every cycle since 2000. In the 2024 presidential election, Republican Donald Trump received 25,148 votes countywide, comprising the majority against the Democratic ticket. This alignment extends to local races, with Republicans sweeping all county offices in 2024. The shift to sustained GOP control at the municipal level since 2019 mirrors broader conservative tendencies in the region's working-class electorate.

Key Policy Debates and Local Governance

The City of Kokomo's local governance features a mayor-council system with executive authority vested in the mayor and legislative powers held by the nine-member Common Council, consisting of six district-elected representatives and three at-large members. Republicans have maintained control of both the mayoralty and council since the 2019 elections, when the party flipped the council from a 7-2 Democratic majority to full GOP dominance, reflecting Howard County's strongly conservative political leanings where only 32.7% of voters supported Democrats in recent presidential contests. This structure has shaped policy implementation amid fiscal pressures, with the mayor proposing budgets and the council approving ordinances, often navigating public input through hearings.
A central debate in recent years has focused on municipal budgeting and revenue generation, exacerbated by state-level changes to business personal property tax credits that reduced local income from commercial properties. In September 2025, the Common Council addressed these shortfalls during hearings on the 2026 budget, highlighting tensions between maintaining services and avoiding tax hikes under Indiana's residential-favoring property tax caps, which incentivize shifting priorities toward business attraction for revenue stability. Critics argued that such reforms disproportionately burden cities like Kokomo, reliant on industrial tax bases, forcing debates on whether to prioritize economic development incentives or infrastructure spending.
The proposed $10 monthly trash collection fee emerged as a flashpoint, symbolizing broader fiscal conservatism versus service funding needs. Initially rejected by the council in November 2024 after public pushback, the fee was reintroduced by Mayor Tyler Moore's administration in 2025 to cover rising refuse costs, leading to overflow crowds at September meetings where residents voiced opposition to what they termed an unfunded mandate. Despite testimonials highlighting financial strain on households, the council approved the measure on September 29, 2025, by a 7-1 vote as part of Ordinance 72-08, underscoring divisions between cost-recovery advocates and those prioritizing low taxes in a post-manufacturing economy.
Public safety and infrastructure funding have also fueled contention, including a 1.2% local option income tax increase earmarked for a new jail, debated as essential for addressing crime but criticized for expanding government spending without corresponding revenue growth. Governance responses to homelessness have drawn scrutiny, with 2024 editorials faulting the Moore administration and council for inadequate enforcement of ordinances and reliance on temporary measures over structural solutions like shelter expansions or zoning reforms. These debates reflect causal pressures from deindustrializationevident in Kokomo's historical manufacturing reliancepushing leaders to balance revitalization incentives, such as business abatements approved in July 2025, against resident demands for fiscal restraint.

Education

K-12 Public School System

The Kokomo School Corporation (KSC) administers public K-12 education for approximately 5,238 students across 12 schools in Kokomo, Indiana, including preschools, elementary, middle, and high schools. The district serves a student body where 40% are minorities and 51% qualify as economically disadvantaged, with a student-teacher ratio of 15:1. KSC operates five preschool locations, eight elementary schools (covering pre-K through grade 5 or 6), three middle schools (grades 6-8), Kokomo High School (grades 9-12 with 1,472 enrollees), the Kokomo Virtual Academy for online learning, and McKinley Alternative School for specialized needs.
Academic performance in KSC lags behind state averages, with only 27% of students proficient in math and reading on Indiana state assessments. ISTEP+ pass rates for English language arts and math have shown declines or stagnation in recent years, prompting state-approved restructuring plans for underperforming schools such as Pettit Park Elementary, Bon Air Elementary, and Bon Air Middle under a Transformation Zone initiative to address chronic low achievement. Kokomo High School reports a 53.4% free/reduced lunch rate among its students, correlating with broader district challenges in college and career readiness metrics.
Funding constraints have exacerbated operational pressures, including teacher retention, academic interventions, and transportation. In November 2024, voters rejected a proposed operating referendum that would have raised $6.16 million annually through a $0.26 property tax rate increase for eight years, aimed at salaries, curriculum enhancements, and bus replacements. KSC relies on standard state tuition support, local property taxes, and federal grants, but the failed referendum highlighted fiscal limitations amid rising costs and enrollment stability around 5,000-5,400 students since 2006.

Public Library System

The Kokomo-Howard County Public Library (KHCPL) traces its origins to the Kokomo Free Library, organized in 1885, which evolved into a Carnegie-funded institution by 1905 when a new building was constructed largely through a grant from philanthropist Andrew Carnegie. The current system operates three branches serving Howard County residents, providing access to physical and digital collections, public computing resources, and community programs designed to promote literacy and lifelong learning. In line with broader trends in public librarianship, KHCPL eliminated overdue fines for borrowed materials effective January 1, 2024, to enhance accessibility and reduce barriers to usage.
The Main branch, located at 220 North Union Street in downtown Kokomo, serves as the system's headquarters and houses the Genealogy and Local History Department, which maintains specialized resources for family research, including local records, photographs, and guidance from dedicated staff. Constructed in 1966 and fully renovated in 2010, this facility anchors the network with extended hours, including evenings and weekends. The South branch at 1755 East Center Road caters to southern Kokomo residents with similar core services, while the Russiaville branch extends coverage to rural areas in the county's northwest, operating from a smaller footprint focused on community needs. All locations offer free Wi-Fi, public computers, and inter-branch material delivery to facilitate countywide access.
KHCPL's services encompass a diverse collection of books, audiobooks, e-books, and multimedia, supported by programs such as author fairs, reading challenges, and educational workshops on topics ranging from genealogy to digital literacy. In fiscal year 2020, the system reported holdings exceeding 240,000 physical items and facilitated substantial circulation, reflecting its role as a vital community hub amid Indiana's public library network. These offerings align with the library's mission to foster community development through equitable resource provision, with ongoing initiatives like the Howard County Reads List engaging residents in shared literary experiences.

Culture and Society

Arts, Historic Sites, and Festivals

The Seiberling Mansion, constructed in 18891891, serves as the centerpiece of the Howard County Historical Society's campus and houses the Howard County Museum, featuring exhibits on local history including automotive innovations tied to Kokomo's industrial past. Adjacent to it, the Elliott House, a restored 1886 Victorian structure, provides additional historical insights into 19th-century domestic life in the region. Other notable historic sites include the Elwood Haynes Museum, dedicated to inventor Elwood Haynes who developed early automobiles in Kokomo around 1894, and landmarks such as the Vermont Covered Bridge, relocated to Highland Park in 1946, the Chief Kokomo statue commemorating the Potawatomi leader, and Old Ben, the preserved remains of the world's largest steer exhibited from 1932.
Kokomo's arts scene centers on its downtown cultural district, which features public murals, large-scale sculptures, and transformed art alleys promoting local creativity. The Kokomo Art Association operates galleries and studios at 210 N. Main Street, offering classes, workshops, and exhibits by regional artists since its establishment as a nonprofit. Additional venues include the Indiana University Kokomo Art Gallery, hosting thought-provoking exhibitions for students and the community, and the Kokomo Community Arts Commission, which supports local projects through grants and events.
Annual festivals highlight Kokomo's heritage and community spirit, with the Haynes Apperson Festival held each July since 1977, drawing crowds for parades, car shows, live music, carnival rides, and fireworks celebrating the city's automotive pioneers Elwood Haynes and the Apperson brothers. The Kokomo Strawberry Festival, typically in June, features shortcake, live entertainment, food vendors, and artisan crafts, attracting thousands to Memorial Park. Other events include the Howard County 4-H Fair in July with agricultural exhibits and rides, First Fridays arts open houses on the first Friday monthly, the Craft Fest in June showcasing handmade goods, and holiday traditions like Christmas at the Seiberling Mansion with tours and decorations starting in November.

Infrastructure

Transportation Networks

U.S. Route 31 serves as the primary north-south artery through Kokomo, linking the city to Indianapolis approximately 50 miles south and South Bend to the north, with recent upgrades converting segments to a limited-access freeway featuring interchanges rather than at-grade intersections. The US 31 Kokomo Freeway project, advanced by the Indiana Department of Transportation, eliminates traffic signals and rail crossings along the corridor, achieving full freeway standards by the end of 2026 to enhance safety and capacity for the 25,000 daily vehicles. East-west connectivity relies on State Road 26, which bisects the city and supports regional commerce, while State Road 931 designates the previous US 31 alignment through urban areas.
Freight rail operations in Kokomo are handled by the Central Railroad of Indianapolis (CERA), a Class III shortline carrier managing approximately 58 miles of track across north-central Indiana, including lines through the city for handling commodities such as agricultural products, chemicals, and scrap metal. CERA interchanges with the Toledo, Peoria and Western Railway locally and Norfolk Southern farther north in Marion, supporting industrial shipments to facilities like those tied to the area's manufacturing base. No passenger rail service operates in Kokomo.
The Kokomo Municipal Airport (FAA: OKK), located five miles northeast of downtown, functions as a general aviation facility owned by the city's Board of Aviation Commissioners, with a primary runway measuring 6,001 feet and an elevation of 832 feet. It accommodates private, corporate, and instructional flights but offers no scheduled commercial service, relying on nearby Indianapolis International Airport for regional air travel.
Local public transit consists of the City Line Trolley, a fare-free fixed-route system with five linesBlue, Red, Green, Yellow, and Orangeoriginating from a central transit hub and covering over 250 stops focused on educational, retail, medical, and residential zones. Complementing this is the Spirit of Kokomo paratransit service, providing curb-to-curb rides for individuals aged 60 and older or those with qualifying disabilities under the Americans with Disabilities Act. Intercity bus connections are available through Greyhound, with stops at a Marathon station along State Road 931 for routes to Indianapolis and beyond.

Utilities, Contamination, and Environmental Concerns

Kokomo's electricity is supplied by Duke Energy, which operates a local plant and has invested in transmission infrastructure to support industrial growth in Howard County. Natural gas service is provided by Northern Indiana Public Service Company (NIPSCO), serving residential, commercial, and industrial customers across the region. Water and wastewater services are managed by Indiana American Water for the Kokomo district, with billing handled through the City of Kokomo's utility department; the system draws from municipal wells and treats for compliance with federal standards, though raw groundwater sources have shown natural minerals like iron and manganese.
The most significant contamination issue is the Kokomo Contaminated Groundwater Plume, a Superfund site designated by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) in 2015, where vinyl chloridea known carcinogen used in plastics productionand arsenic have contaminated groundwater, impacting several city municipal wells. These contaminants were first detected in well samples around 2007, with low levels occasionally appearing in untreated raw water but not exceeding treatment thresholds in finished drinking water; the plume's source remains unidentified despite proximity to facilities handling chlorinated solvents. In response, the EPA installed six groundwater monitoring wells in 2020 to delineate the plume's extent, with ongoing assessments as of that year. Another notable site, the former Continental Steel Corporation facility, underwent EPA-led cleanup of slag processing areas contaminated by heavy metals and other industrial residues; remediation concluded prior to 2010, enabling site redevelopment into solar and recreational uses.
Additional groundwater risks stem from leaking underground storage tanks at gas stations, contributing to localized solvent plumes as documented in community investigations through 2023. Environmental analyses, including those by the Environmental Working Group, have flagged elevated arsenic in tap water samplesup to 313 times above health guidelines based on federal datathough the EPA maintains that post-treatment levels meet safe drinking water standards.
Broader concerns include nonpoint source pollution in waterways like Kokomo Creek and upper Wildcat Creek, driven by agricultural runoff, manure application, and urban stormwater carrying sediments, nutrients, and chemicals; the Indiana Department of Environmental Management has established total maximum daily loads for pollutants such as E. coli and phosphorus in these systems. Air quality poses a moderate risk, with projections of increased poor air days due to industrial emissions and regional factors, though Kokomo lacks facilities ranked among Indiana's highest toxic air polluters. These issues trace to Kokomo's manufacturing legacy, including automotive and metalworking, which historically released solvents and metals into soil and water without modern containment.

The area supports over 100 shopping listings ranging from local artisans to national brands, with a focus on unique, locally made items.
For entertainment, the AMC CLASSIC Kokomo 12 cinema, located within Kokomo Town Center, provides 12 screens for movie screenings, including family-oriented showings and premium formats. Downtown venues contribute to nightlife with live music events, such as monthly performances at Black Wax Cafe, and eclectic options including pubs and breweries like Cook McDoogal's Irish Pub and Half Moon Restaurant and Brewery. Festivals and seasonal events in the district enhance entertainment, drawing on historic settings for community gatherings.

Notable Individuals

Ryan White (December 6, 1971 April 8, 1990), a resident of Kokomo, became a prominent HIV/AIDS awareness advocate after contracting the virus through contaminated blood treatment for hemophilia in 1984; his legal battle to attend Western Middle School amid local opposition highlighted discrimination against those with AIDS and influenced the passage of the Ryan White CARE Act in 1990.
Opha May Johnson (May 4, 1878 August 11, 1955), born in Kokomo, enlisted in the United States Marine Corps on August 13, 1918, becoming the first woman known to join the branch during World War I; she served as a clerk in the Marine Corps Recruiting Publicity Bureau in New York until her honorable discharge in 1919.
Elwood Haynes (October 14, 1857 April 13, 1925), who relocated to Kokomo in 1891 to manage a natural gas plant, pioneered automotive innovations there including the first American automobile with a metal clutch and gears in 1893 and stainless-steel alloys; he co-founded the Haynes-Apperson company, producing vehicles until 1905, and his Kokomo residence now houses the Elwood Haynes Museum.
Norman Bridwell (February 15, 1928 December 12, 2014), born and raised in Kokomo where he graduated from Kokomo High School, created the Clifford the Big Red Dog children's book series, publishing over 100 titles starting in 1963 that have sold more than 126 million copies worldwide.
Steve Kroft (born August 22, 1945), born in Kokomo and a Kokomo High School graduate, served as a longtime correspondent for CBS's 60 Minutes from 1986 to 2019, earning nine Emmy Awards and three Peabody Awards for investigative reporting on topics including nuclear smuggling and government scandals.
Strother Martin (March 26, 1919 August 1, 1980), born in Kokomo, appeared in over 180 films as a character actor, notably delivering the line "What we've got here is failure to communicate" in Cool Hand Luke (1967), and also directed water polo teams to national championships while competing as a swimmer.

Representations in Popular Culture

The Beach Boys' 1988 song "Kokomo," featured in the film Cocktail, shares its name with the Indiana city but portrays a fictional tropical destination off the Florida Keys with no relation to the Midwestern locale; songwriter Mike Love selected the title for its ironic contrast, noting the real Kokomo's inland, non-Caribbean character.
Kokomo has appeared as a setting in various films, often highlighting its Midwestern identity. The 1939 Warner Bros. comedy The Kid from Kokomo centers on an orphan boxer from the city searching for his family, with much of the story unfolding there. In The Blues Brothers (1980), the protagonists perform blues at the fictional Bob's Country Bunker, a rural honky-tonk bar explicitly located in Kokomo. The 1987 thriller Assassination includes a driving sequence through the city, though inaccurately depicting it with mountainous terrain. The low-budget 1988 action film Terror Squad, starring Chuck Connors, is set in Kokomo where Libyan terrorists seize a nuclear plant and take school hostages, with principal filming at local sites including Kokomo High School (as a fictional high school), Central Middle School, and downtown areas around the Howard County Courthouse; production in 1986 provided economic boost amid high local unemployment, drawing 9001,000 residents to auditions despite critical panning as "moronic, low-grade trash."
Television representations include the 1989 TV movie The Ryan White Story, which dramatizes the Kokomo youth's fight against school exclusion due to his AIDS diagnosis, filmed mostly in Indiana and portraying local community tensions. The HBO miniseries Band of Brothers (2001) references Kokomo in episodes 1 and 3. In the ABC series 666 Park Avenue (20122013), two lead characters hail from the city, mentioned in the first two episodes. Animated film Cats Don't Dance (1997) features a protagonist cat originating from Kokomo in his backstory.
Earlier depictions include the 1947 musical Mother Wore Tights, which features the song "Kokomo, Indiana" in a vaudeville family narrative, and the 1953 film Sweethearts on Parade, set in Kokomo amid a traveling medicine show. A 1917 silent film, A Romance of Kokomo, was both set and filmed locally, premiering at the Isis Theater.
