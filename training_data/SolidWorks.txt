SolidWorks

 SolidWorks is a proprietary computer-aided design (CAD) and computer-aided engineering (CAE) software application developed for creating three-dimensional models of solid objects, widely used in mechanical engineering, product design, and manufacturing industries. It enables users to build parametric models of parts and assemblies, generate engineering drawings, perform simulations, and facilitate collaboration in product development workflows, running on Microsoft Windows operating systems, where it is officially supported only on native installations; the software is not officially supported on macOS or in virtualized environments such as Parallels Desktop, which may result in compatibility and stability issues.
SolidWorks competes with other 3D CAD tools such as Autodesk Fusion 360, with each platform offering distinct advantages depending on factors like cost, platform support, and project scale.
Founded in 1993 by Jon Hirschtick in Concord, Massachusetts, SolidWorks Corporation aimed to deliver intuitive 3D CAD tools accessible on standard personal computers, departing from the high-end workstations required by earlier software. The company released its first version in 1995, which rapidly gained popularity for its user-friendly interface and affordability, leading to its acquisition by Dassault Systmes in 1997 for approximately $310 million in stock. Under Dassault Systmes ownership, SolidWorks has evolved into a comprehensive suite, integrating advanced features like finite element analysis, motion simulation, and cloud-based collaboration tools, while maintaining its core focus on parametric solid modeling.
As of 2025, SolidWorks serves over 8 million users worldwide across diverse sectors including aerospace, automotive, consumer goods, and medical devices, supported by a robust ecosystem of add-ins, certifications, and educational resources. Its annual releases, such as SOLIDWORKS 2026, continue to enhance productivity with improvements in performance, usability, and integration with the 3DEXPERIENCE platform for extended enterprise collaboration.

History

Founding and Early Development

SolidWorks Corporation was established on December 30, 1993, in Concord, Massachusetts, by Jon Hirschtick, an MIT alumnus with prior experience in computer-aided design (CAD) software development. Hirschtick assembled an initial team of engineers, including Scott Harris, Bob Zuffante, Constantine Delcos, Mike Payne, and Tom Li, many of whom were also MIT graduates who had worked on earlier CAD projects at companies like Applicon and Computervision. The company's inception was driven by the goal of developing affordable and intuitive 3D CAD software targeted at mid-market users, such as small to medium-sized engineering firms, in contrast to expensive, complex high-end systems like CATIA that dominated the industry and required specialized workstations.
Initial funding came from $1 million in seed capital raised by Hirschtick from his earnings as a member of the MIT Blackjack Team, supplemented by investments from angel investors including Atlas Venture. Development began immediately, leveraging the emerging Windows NT platform to ensure broad accessibility on standard PCs, a departure from the Unix-based systems prevalent in professional CAD at the time. The team operated from founders' homes initially before securing office space at Hanscom Air Force Base in Concord in 1994. A prototype was demonstrated to industry analysts and media in late 1994, marking the first public beta testing phase.
The official release of SolidWorks 95 occurred in November 1995, priced at $4,000significantly lower than competitors costing up to $18,000positioning it as an accessible tool for everyday engineering tasks. Key emphases included an intuitive Windows-based interface with features like fully shaded visualizations, parametric modeling via the FeatureManager design tree, and seamless integration for parts, assemblies, and drawings, which streamlined workflows and reduced the learning curve for non-experts. This launch established SolidWorks as a disruptive force in 3D CAD, eventually leading to its acquisition by Dassault Systmes in 1997.

Acquisitions and Milestones

In 1997, Dassault Systmes acquired SolidWorks Corporation for approximately $310 million in stock, marking a significant expansion of its portfolio in computer-aided design software. This transaction, announced on June 24, involved issuing 4.85 million shares to SolidWorks' shareholders and was completed in the third quarter of that year, subject to regulatory approvals. The acquisition positioned SolidWorks as a complementary offering to Dassault's established CATIA product, targeting mainstream design-centric markets while allowing SolidWorks to operate as an independent subsidiary focused on accessible 3D modeling tools.
Key product milestones followed the acquisition, enhancing SolidWorks' capabilities in visualization and collaboration. The 1999 release of SolidWorks 99 introduced PhotoWorks, an early rendering tool that enabled users to create realistic images from 3D models, laying the groundwork for advanced photorealistic rendering in subsequent versions. In 2000, SolidWorks launched eDrawings, a free viewer and collaboration tool that allowed secure sharing of 3D models and 2D drawings without requiring a full SolidWorks license, facilitating real-time feedback across design teams.
Further growth came through strategic partnerships and integrations in simulation and automation. In 2001, Dassault Systmes acquired Structural Research & Analysis Corporation (SRAC) for $22 million, bringing the COSMOS analysis software suite under its umbrella; this technology was integrated into SolidWorks as COSMOSWorks, and by 2008, it was rebranded and expanded as SolidWorks Simulation to provide embedded finite element analysis for structural validation directly within the CAD environment. That same year, SolidWorks incorporated DriveWorksXpressa design automation add-in from partner DriveWorksinto every license of SolidWorks 2008, enabling rule-based customization of parts, assemblies, and drawings to streamline repetitive engineering tasks.
In the 2010s, SolidWorks advanced toward cloud-enabled ecosystems with the announcement in 2012 of its integration into Dassault Systmes' 3DEXPERIENCE platform, debuting fully in SolidWorks 2013 to support collaborative, web-based workflows for design review, data management, and simulation sharing across global teams. This shift enabled seamless connectivity between desktop SolidWorks and cloud services, reducing IT dependencies and enhancing real-time collaboration. Entering the 2020s, SolidWorks emphasized AI-driven innovations, particularly through xDesigna browser-based, cloud-native extension of the platform launched around 2021 and evolving with AI features by 2025, such as intelligent selection helpers and generative drawing views that automate routine tasks and suggest design intents based on user patterns.

Technical Foundations

Parametric Modeling

Parametric modeling in SolidWorks represents a history-based design paradigm that captures engineering intent through a sequence of editable features, such as extrusions, revolves, and sweeps, interconnected by dimensions and geometric relations. These features are built upon 2D sketches, forming the foundational elements of 3D geometry, and are organized in a feature tree that records the model's creation history for subsequent modifications. This approach allows users to define and control complex shapes parametrically, ensuring that the model's geometry remains driven by user-specified parameters rather than static forms.
The core process begins with creating a 2D sketch on a plane, where users apply dimensions and relations to constrain the geometry precisely. Features are then added sequentiallyfor instance, extruding a sketch to form a base solid, followed by revolving another sketch around an axis for cylindrical components, or sweeping a profile along a path to generate tubing-like structures. Changes to an early element, such as adjusting the diameter of a base sketch, automatically propagate through the feature tree, updating all dependent features and rebuilding the model without manual intervention; for example, altering a dimension in the initial sketch of an extruded block will resize the entire part accordingly. This sequential dependency maintains the integrity of the design history, enabling targeted edits at any stage.
One key advantage of this parametric workflow is its support for rapid iterations, as users can experiment with design variations by simply modifying parameters, avoiding the need to reconstruct models from scratch. It also facilitates design reuse through configurations, where multiple variants of a single modelsuch as different sizes of a componentcan be managed within the same file, promoting efficiency in engineering projects. Furthermore, by embedding relations and dimensions directly into the features, parametric modeling preserves the original engineering intent, making it easier to adapt designs to new requirements while ensuring consistency.
SolidWorks enhances parametric control with specialized tools like equation-driven dimensions, which allow users to define mathematical relationships between featuresfor instance, setting one dimension as twice another to enforce proportional scaling. Global variables further extend this capability by assigning named parameters (e.g., "Length = 100") that can drive multiple dimensions across the model, enabling centralized updates that affect the entire design simultaneously. These tools integrate seamlessly with the feature-based system, supporting advanced parametric behaviors without altering the underlying history tree. The parametric modeling features are powered by the Parasolid geometry kernel for robust computational handling.

Geometry Kernel and Data Management

SolidWorks employs the Parasolid geometric modeling kernel, licensed from Siemens Digital Industries Software, as its core engine for handling 3D geometry. This kernel utilizes boundary representation (B-rep) modeling to define solid objects through their surfaces, edges, and vertices, enabling precise representations of complex geometries. Parasolid supports non-uniform rational B-splines (NURBS) for smooth surface modeling and facilitates both solid and sheet modeling operations, ensuring high-fidelity data for design and analysis.
Data management in SolidWorks revolves around native file formats that encapsulate the parametric design history. Part files use the .SLDPRT extension, assembly files use .SLDASM, and drawing files use .SLDDRW, each storing the complete model data including features, mates, and annotations. The FeatureManager design tree serves as the primary interface for this data, organizing the sequence of features in a hierarchical structure that preserves the chronological order of design operations, allowing users to navigate and modify the model's history.
Interoperability is achieved through support for standard exchange formats, enabling seamless data transfer with other CAD systems and manufacturing processes. SolidWorks can import and export files in STEP (ISO 10303) for comprehensive assembly and product manufacturing information, IGES for surface and wireframe data, and STL for stereolithography in 3D printing applications. STL files are typically imported into part documents as graphics bodies by default, which are intended for visualization and reference only and lack parametric geometry as well as selectable entities such as faces, edges, or vertices, thereby preventing direct application of mates or relationships in assemblies. Direct insertion of STL files into assemblies as graphics bodies without first importing into a part document, saving the part, and then inserting it as a component is not supported. With the ScanTo3D add-in enabled (included in Professional and Premium editions), STL files can be imported as mesh bodies, which serve as reference geometry, enable editing in hybrid modeling contexts combining solid, surface, and mesh elements, support visualization and analysis tasks, and in newer versions allow limited mating capabilities. Mesh bodies do not automatically convert to fully editable parametric solid bodies via a built-in one-click tool in versions up to 2026. Conversion to parametric features typically requires the ScanTo3D add-in, which allows users to generate surfaces from mesh data, followed by knitting, trimming, and thickening operations to create solid bodies. Workarounds for mating requirements with graphics bodies include using reference geometry such as planes. Enhancements in SolidWorks 2025 primarily focused on mesh preparation improvements for 3D printing workflows and performance optimizations for handling mesh data, rather than introducing direct mesh-to-parametric solid conversion capabilities. Alternatively, users can import STL files using specific import options to create more functional body types suitable for generating 2D drawings. To create a 2D drawing from an STL file in SolidWorks: 1. Open SolidWorks and go to File > Open. 2. Select your STL file, then click "Options" in the dialog box. 3. In the import options, set "Import as" to Solid Body (or Surface Body for better results in some cases). 4. Click OK and open the file. The STL imports as a mesh body in a new part document. 5. With the part open, go to File > Make Drawing from Part/Assembly. 6. In the drawing, insert standard views (e.g., front, top, isometric) of the imported model. Note: STL files are triangular meshes, so imported models are non-parametric mesh bodies. Resulting 2D drawings may show faceted edges, limited hidden line accuracy, and challenges with precise dimensioning or annotations. For cleaner results, use the ScanTo3D add-in (available in Professional/Premium versions) to convert the mesh to editable surfaces or solids before creating the drawing.
The rollback bar enhances non-destructive editing by allowing temporary reversion to earlier points in the feature history without altering subsequent operations, supporting iterative design refinements while maintaining data integrity.
For handling large assemblies, SolidWorks implements lightweight modes to optimize memory usage and loading times. In lightweight mode, components load with only essential graphics and mate data, deferring full resolution until necessary, which significantly improves performance for assemblies exceeding thousands of parts. Virtual components further aid efficiency by embedding part or subassembly data directly within the .SLDASM file, avoiding external references and reducing file management overhead in complex top-level designs.

Core Features

Part and Assembly Design

SolidWorks part design relies on parametric modeling to create 3D models from 2D sketches, where geometric relations such as coincidentaligning entities at shared pointsand parallelensuring lines or planes maintain equal anglesconstrain sketch elements for precise control and design intent preservation. These relations, applied during sketching, allow automatic inference of constraints like collinear or perpendicular, enabling fully defined profiles that adapt to modifications without losing relationships. Sketches form the foundation for extruded, revolved, or more complex 3D features, supporting iterative design changes through parameter-driven updates.
This parametric framework enables efficient editing of existing features. For example, to reposition a Boss/Base Extrude feature, right-click the feature in the FeatureManager design tree and select Edit Sketch. In sketch mode, select the desired elements and relocate them using the Move tool (Sketch Tools > Move) or by holding Ctrl while dragging. Exiting the sketch causes the feature to regenerate with the updated position. To move the entire resulting solid body without modifying the original sketch, use Insert > Features > Move/Copy, select the body, and specify the translation or rotation. Avoid reordering features in the FeatureManager tree if such changes would disrupt dependencies between features.
For complex geometry, SolidWorks offers advanced 3D features like loft, which generates surfaces or solids by transitioning between two or more profiles, ideal for shapes requiring smooth curvature such as aircraft fuselages or ergonomic handles. The sweep feature moves a profile along a path to create tubes, wires, or helical structures, while the boundary boss/base extends this by spanning multiple profiles in one or two directions, producing organic forms with tangent or curvature continuity for high-fidelity surfacing. Surfacing tools complement these by enabling non-solid geometry creation, such as boundary surfaces that maintain continuity across edges, facilitating the development of Class-A surfaces in automotive or consumer product design before thickening into solids.
One valuable surfacing tool is Extend Surface, which extends existing surfaces beyond their current boundaries along selected edges or faces. This is useful in various workflows, including lengthening parts. To use Extend Surface: Click Extend Surface on the Surfaces toolbar or select Insert > Surface > Extend. In the PropertyManager, under Edges/Faces to Extend, select one or more edges or faces. The Propagate option can be used to extend to tangent surfaces automatically. Choose Extension Type: Same Surface (follows the original surface geometry) or Linear (creates a tangent extension). Set End Condition: Distance (specify a numerical value), Up to Point, or Up to Surface. Click the green checkmark to apply.
To lengthen a solid part using a surfacing workflow, first delete the end faces with the Delete Face tool to open the body into surfaces, extend the relevant surfaces using Extend Surface, and then knit the surfaces together if multiple pieces are involved or apply the Thicken tool to reform the solid body with the added length.
SolidWorks Mold Tools provide capabilities for injection mold design, enabling the splitting of 3D models along parting surfacesincluding curved surfaces, which may require knitting for completenessto automatically generate core and cavity components. These tools support the insertion of mold blocks for visualization and allow for surface extensions to ensure full enclosure of the model, aiding in the preparation of designs for manufacturing processes like injection molding.
Assembly design in SolidWorks involves inserting parts into a virtual environment and defining their interactions via standard mates, including coincident for face-to-face or point-to-plane alignment, concentric for cylindrical alignments around axes, and distance for specifying offsets between entities. These mates, combinable with parallel or tangent relations, ensure degrees of freedom are constrained appropriately, simulating real-world kinematics without over-constraining the model. Exploded views enhance visualization by offsetting components along axes while preserving relative positions, aiding in assembly instructions or bill-of-materials reviews. Interference detection scans for overlaps or gaps between components, highlighting issues with volume calculations and isolation options for targeted resolution. Motion studies further extend this by animating assemblies under mate-driven movements, incorporating motors or gravity for basic kinematic validation.
Advanced capabilities address large-scale assemblies, where SpeedPak configurations optimize performance by creating lightweight subsets of components, faces, and references, suppressing unnecessary details to reduce load times and memory usage without altering the full assembly. Top-down design leverages in-context references, allowing features in individual parts to link directly to assembly geometrylike a layout sketch or adjacent component edgesfor synchronized updates across the model, promoting collaborative workflows in complex product development. Building briefly on parametric principles, these tools ensure changes propagate predictably, maintaining design integrity in multi-part environments.
A representative example is designing a spur gear, where the involute tooth profile is generated parametrically using equation-driven curves in a 2D sketch to define the precise curvature based on module and pressure angle parameters, enabling scalable gear families.
Alternatively, SolidWorks Toolbox offers pre-configured standard spur gears as an efficient alternative to fully custom parametric modeling for many applications. To insert a spur gear, open the Design Library task pane, navigate to Toolbox > > Power Transmission > Gears, and drag a spur gear into an active part or assembly. In the Configure Component PropertyManager, set parameters including Module (metric pitch), Number of Teeth, Pressure Angle (typically 20), Face Width (gear thickness), along with options such as bore diameter and hub style. Confirm the configuration by clicking OK to insert the gear.

Simulation and Analysis Tools

SolidWorks Simulation is a fully integrated suite of computer-aided engineering (CAE) tools within the SolidWorks environment, enabling users to perform virtual testing and validation of designs to predict performance under various physical conditions. It leverages finite element analysis (FEA) to model and solve engineering problems, allowing for the evaluation of stress, strain, deformation, and other responses in parts and assemblies without physical prototyping. This capability supports iterative design improvements by identifying potential failures early, reducing development time and costs.
The core of SolidWorks Simulation involves linear and nonlinear static analyses, which assess structural integrity under applied loads, including forces, pressures, and temperatures. Thermal analyses simulate heat transfer and temperature distributions, while frequency and buckling studies evaluate natural vibration modes and stability under compressive loads, respectively. These analyses employ the finite element method (FEM) to discretize models into a mesh of elements, typically using tetrahedral 3D solid elements for volumetric parts, which provide flexibility in handling complex geometries. Mesh generation is automated but customizable, with options for curvature-based refinement to ensure accuracy in curved or detailed regions. Fatigue analysis extends these capabilities to predict component lifespan under cyclic loading, and optimization tools allow automated design variations to meet performance criteria. All studies support both parts and assemblies, incorporating connections like bolts and welds for realistic load paths.
Motion simulation in SolidWorks complements structural analysis by enabling kinematic and dynamic studies of assemblies, simulating real-world motion driven by motors, springs, dampers, and gravity. Users define event-based controls, such as time schedules or sensor triggers, to model interactions including contact forces between components. The solver computes velocities, accelerations, and reaction forces, outputting results as animations, graphs of motion parameters, and exported data for further review. This tool verifies mechanism functionality and identifies interference or excessive wear before manufacturing.
SolidWorks Flow Simulation provides computational fluid dynamics (CFD) capabilities for analyzing fluid flow, heat transfer, and related phenomena in internal and external domains. It supports simulations of gases and liquids, including compressible and incompressible flows, with features for conjugate heat transfer between solids and fluids. The module uses a finite volume method integrated with the SolidWorks geometry, automatically generating computational meshes and solving for parameters like velocity, pressure, and temperature fields. Results are visualized through cut plots, streamlines, and isosurfaces, aiding in optimizing designs for cooling, drag reduction, or mixing efficiency.
The Sustainability module performs life cycle assessments (LCA) to quantify environmental impacts such as carbon footprint, energy consumption, air acidification, and water eutrophication across a product's full lifecyclefrom raw material extraction to end-of-life disposal. It evaluates assembly processes, transportation, and manufacturing energy use, providing real-time feedback in a dashboard to guide eco-friendly design choices, like material substitutions or process optimizations. This tool complies with standards like ISO 14040 for LCA, helping users minimize ecological footprints without compromising performance.
Advanced analysis options derive from the integration of legacy COSMOS technologies, originally developed as COSMOSWorks for seamless FEA within SolidWorks. This foundation enables nonlinear analyses for scenarios involving large deformations, material plasticity, or geometric nonlinearity, where linear assumptions fail. TolAnalyst, an add-on tool, conducts tolerance stack-up analysis in assemblies by calculating worst-case, statistical (RSS), and sensitivity contributions of dimensional tolerances to gap or alignment variations between features, ensuring manufacturability and fit. These enhancements extend SolidWorks' simulation portfolio for complex, multiphysics problems.

Applications and Ecosystem

Industry Uses

SolidWorks is extensively applied in manufacturing and mechanical engineering, serving as a core tool for product design, prototyping, and iterative development of mechanical components. In the automotive sector, Ford Motor Company integrates SolidWorks with tolerance analysis software to evaluate assembly builds and ensure quality standards for vehicle parts before production release. For consumer goods, Procter & Gamble employs SolidWorks through integrated mold design tools to create precise components and packaging, streamlining the transition from concept to manufacturable products. SolidWorks' Mold Tools facilitate this by allowing users to define parting surfaces, including curved ones that may require knitting for completeness, and to split 3D models into core and cavity components using features like Tooling Split or Core/Cavity, which supports injection molding preparation and preview with mold blocks.
In aerospace and defense, SolidWorks supports the engineering of intricate assemblies that demand high-precision surfacing for structural integrity and performance. Boeing's subsidiary, Continental DataGraphics, utilizes the software to convert legacy 2D drawings into 3D models, accelerating the design of aircraft components and enabling faster responses to manufacturing needs.
The medical devices industry relies on SolidWorks for developing custom implants and prosthetics tailored to individual patients, combining parametric modeling with simulation tools to assess fit, durability, and biocompatibility under real-world conditions. This approach allows engineers to virtually test device interactions with biological tissues, reducing the need for physical prototypes and expediting regulatory approvals.
Globally, SolidWorks has over 8 million users as of 2025, with particularly strong adoption among small and medium-sized businesses owing to its cost-effective licensing and intuitive interface that lowers barriers to advanced CAD capabilities.

Integration and Extensions

SolidWorks provides extensive integration capabilities through its Application Programming Interface (API), enabling users to automate tasks and develop custom plugins. The API supports multiple programming languages, including Visual Basic for Applications (VBA), C#, Visual Basic .NET (VB.NET), and C++, allowing developers to create macros that record and replay user interface actions for repetitive processes such as feature creation or file exports. Macros, often written in VBA or VSTA, can be edited, tested, and deployed to customize workflows, with the API offering hundreds of functions to interact with SolidWorks objects like parts, assemblies, and drawings. This extensibility supports the creation of add-ins that extend core functionality, such as automated reporting or integration with external databases, fostering tailored solutions for engineering teams. SolidWorks also includes built-in add-ins, such as the SOLIDWORKS XPS Driver, which enables exporting SOLIDWORKS documents (parts, assemblies, and drawings) to XPS (XML Paper Specification) format using specific file extensions: *.eprtx for parts, *.easmx for assemblies, and *.edrwx for drawings. This add-in can be disabled via the Tools > Add-Ins dialog box by unchecking the SOLIDWORKS XPS Driver option, which removes the XPS export options from the Save As dialog box.
For data management, SolidWorks integrates seamlessly with SOLIDWORKS PDM (Product Data Management), a robust system that centralizes file storage, revision control, and collaborative access for design teams. PDM acts as a secure vault, enabling version tracking, automated workflows for approvals, and real-time notifications to prevent conflicts during multi-user edits. It supports branch and merge operations for parallel development, ensuring data integrity across distributed teams while integrating directly with SolidWorks CAD files to maintain associativity between models and related documents like drawings or simulations. This integration facilitates efficient collaboration in large-scale projects by providing role-based permissions and audit trails for compliance in regulated industries.
Cloud-based extensions enhance SolidWorks' accessibility through the 3DEXPERIENCE WORKS platform, which connects desktop applications to a unified cloud environment for remote collaboration and data sharing. This platform allows users to access SolidWorks files from anywhere via secure cloud storage that preserves CAD relationships, enabling real-time co-editing and version synchronization without local installations. Complementing this, SOLIDWORKS xDesign offers browser-based parametric modeling, running entirely in web browsers on the 3DEXPERIENCE platform to support lightweight design tasks, assemblies, and simulations without downloading software. These extensions promote flexible workflows, such as mobile reviews or hybrid desktop-cloud operations, while maintaining compatibility with traditional SolidWorks files for seamless transitions.
SolidWorks supports a wide array of third-party tools through native add-ins and APIs, expanding its utility in manufacturing and visualization pipelines. For computer-aided manufacturing (CAM), integrations like Mastercam provide direct access to SolidWorks models for generating toolpaths in 2- to 5-axis milling, turning, and wire EDM operations, streamlining the transition from design to production. In rendering, KeyShot's add-in enables live-linking for real-time updates between SolidWorks assemblies and high-fidelity visualizations, supporting motion studies and material assignments directly within the CAD environment. Additional third-party add-ins, such as those for sheet metal unfolding (e.g., AMFG Studio) or electrical design (e.g., Zuken E3.series), embed specialized tools into the SolidWorks interface, allowing users to perform tasks like flat pattern generation or schematic routing without exporting data. These integrations reduce workflow disruptions and enhance productivity by leveraging SolidWorks' open architecture for ecosystem compatibility.

Comparison with Fusion 360

In 2025, neither SolidWorks nor Fusion 360 is universally "better"it depends on user needs. Fusion 360 is often preferred for most users due to its affordability (free for hobbyists/students/startups, $545/year commercial), intuitive interface, cloud-based collaboration, integrated CAD/CAM/CAE tools, and accessibility on Mac/Windows. It excels for beginners, freelancers, product design, and small teams. SolidWorks is superior for professional engineering in industries like aerospace/automotive, offering advanced simulations, large assembly handling, parametric design, and industry compliance, but it is more expensive ($2,800/year), has a steeper learning curve, and is Windows-only. Many 2025 reviews recommend Fusion 360 for value and versatility in most scenarios.

Versions and Support

Release History

SolidWorks has followed an annual release cycle since its initial launch in November 1995 with version 95, the first Windows-native 3D CAD system, delivering major updates typically in the fall to introduce new features and enhancements. This cadence allows users to access innovations progressively, with each version building on parametric modeling foundations while expanding capabilities in design, simulation, and collaboration.
Key milestones include the 2010 release, which introduced Instant3D, enabling direct editing of features by dragging geometry and dimensions without entering edit mode, streamlining modifications for faster iteration. In 2015, SolidWorks added Visualize, a standalone tool for photorealistic rendering and animation from CAD models, enhancing visualization workflows beyond basic PhotoView 360. The 2020 version enhanced cloud collaboration through integration with the 3DEXPERIENCE platform, allowing seamless data sharing and real-time teamwork in a connected ecosystem. The 2022 release introduced the Design Assistant, an AI-powered tool using machine learning to suggest selections and automate tasks like sketch completions based on user patterns. By 2023, further AI enhancements included the Sketch Helper in 3D Creator for predictive sketching. The 2024 release added features for supplier collaboration with older versions and simplified large assemblies. SOLIDWORKS 2025, released in fall 2024, improved design productivity with AI-driven generative drawings (beta), enhanced 3DEXPERIENCE integration for real-time notifications, and mesh preparation for 3D printing and performance improvements. No major new direct mesh-to-solid conversion feature was introduced in 2025; mesh bodies are supported for reference, editing, and hybrid modeling, but conversion to editable solid features typically requires the ScanTo3D add-in (available in Professional/Premium editions) to create surfaces from meshes, then knit/trim/thicken them into solids.
SolidWorks 2026, released in fall 2025, has not introduced a major new direct mesh-to-solid conversion feature as of early 2026, with enhancements continuing to focus on areas such as performance and existing mesh handling capabilities rather than fundamental changes to mesh conversion workflows.
Complementing major releases, SolidWorks issues service packs multiple times per yeartypically four to fivefor bug fixes, performance improvements, and minor feature additions, ensuring ongoing stability without requiring full upgrades. Each major version receives support for approximately three years from release, after which no new service packs are provided; for instance, the 2023 version, released in October 2022, is supported until approximately October 2025. Regarding file compatibility, newer versions can open and edit files from previous releases, but older versions cannot open files saved in newer formats, preserving data integrity while encouraging upgrades. Recent versions include options to save as previous versions for backward compatibility.

Licensing and Community Resources

SolidWorks provides flexible licensing options, including term-based subscriptions and perpetual licenses with required subscription services. Term licenses, offered under the 3DEXPERIENCE SOLIDWORKS platform, allow users to access the software on an annual or quarterly basis without long-term commitment, with pricing starting at $2,820 USD per year for the Standard package, $3,456 USD for Professional, and $4,716 USD for Premium; these bundles include core CAD tools, with Professional adding data management features and Premium incorporating advanced simulation and analysis capabilities. Perpetual licenses grant lifetime access to a specific version but require an initial two-year subscription for new purchases, providing ongoing updates and support, though exact pricing is determined through authorized resellers and typically involves a one-time fee plus annual maintenance costs around 20% of the license value.
Support for SolidWorks users is facilitated through the Subscription Services program, which delivers immediate access to software upgrades, service packs, and live technical assistance from certified value-added resellers (VARs) during business hours. This network of over 200 global resellers also offers customized training programs, implementation services, and ongoing consultation to optimize software usage across industries. Additionally, online resources such as the SOLIDWORKS Resource Center provide self-service tools, knowledge bases, and tutorials for troubleshooting and best practices.
The SolidWorks community fosters user engagement through dedicated platforms and events, including the MySolidWorks online forums where users discuss tips, share models, and seek peer advice on design challenges. Local and virtual user groups under the SOLIDWORKS User Group Network (SWUGN) connect professionals for networking, knowledge exchange, and hands-on workshops, with over 300 chapters worldwide since 1996. The annual 3DEXPERIENCE World conference, held in Houston, Texas, from February 1-4, 2026, serves as the premier event for product announcements, technical sessions, and community building, attracting thousands of users, partners, and experts. Certification programs, such as the entry-level Certified SOLIDWORKS Associate (CSWA) and the Certified SOLIDWORKS Professional (CSWP), validate expertise in SOLIDWORKS modeling through proctored exams, enhancing career opportunities for certified individuals. The official CSWA sample exam PDF (CSWASampleExam.pdf) contains 8 questions. It is a shorter practice test designed to be completed in 90 minutes or less, with a recommendation to score at least 6 correct to be ready for the full CSWA exam, which has 14 questions.
The CSWA exam includes a Drafting Competencies section that tests knowledge of drawing view types and their creation in SOLIDWORKS. Key drawing view types relevant to the exam are:

Standard 3 View: Basic set of orthogonal views (front, top, right) derived from the 3D model.

Model View: A drawing view directly from a part or assembly model in a specified orientation.

Projected View: Orthogonally projected from an existing view (e.g., side view from front).

Auxiliary View: Orthographic view normal to a selected reference edge, for angled surfaces.

Section View: Cuts through a parent view with a section line (straight, offset, or concentric) to reveal internal features.

Aligned Section View: Section with angled lines for rotational symmetry or non-straight cuts.

Detail View: Enlarged portion of an existing view, defined by a closed profile (e.g., circle).

Broken-out Section View: Removes material from a closed profile in an existing view to expose internal details to a specified depth.

Break View: Shortens long parts with break lines for better sheet fit while maintaining scale.

Crop View: Removes portions outside a closed profile in a view.

Alternate Position View: Superimposes an alternate position (e.g., range of motion) using phantom lines on an existing view.

These views are tested on identification, creation procedures, and interpretation. The exam focuses on understanding these types in the context of SOLIDWORKS drafting tools.
For educational purposes, SolidWorks offers the Student Edition at $60 USD per year, providing full-featured desktop software identical to commercial versions for personal use by verified students, including access to over 600 training videos and cloud collaboration tools. Universities and accredited institutions can obtain the SOLIDWORKS Education Edition through discounted academic licensing, which supports classroom deployment for degree-seeking programs and includes the Student Access Initiative allowing off-campus use on personal devices to extend learning beyond the curriculum. These options aim to prepare students for professional environments while maintaining institutional affordability.

Compatibility and Known Issues

SOLIDWORKS officially supports specific virtualized environments, including designated versions of Parallels Desktop for Mac (e.g., version 20.2.0 for SOLIDWORKS 2025), allowing the software to run in a Windows virtual machine on Mac hardware. A GPU is required for optimal performance in virtualized setups. However, users have reported compatibility issues in virtualized environments, including the "sldim error" in SOLIDWORKS 2025 SP05 on Parallels Desktop for Mac. This error is likely caused by compatibility problems related to licensing or installation manager components, graphics acceleration, module loading, or general stability in virtualized configurations. No specific official fix for this exact error is documented. Common user recommendations include updating to the supported version of Parallels, enabling 3D acceleration, and allocating sufficient system resources, though these may not resolve the issue in all cases. For full support, reliability, and to avoid potential compatibility problems, use a native Windows machine.
