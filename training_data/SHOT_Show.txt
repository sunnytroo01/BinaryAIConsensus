 The SHOT Show, an acronym for Shooting, Hunting, and Outdoor Trade Show, is the largest annual trade event dedicated to professionals in the shooting sports, hunting, law enforcement, outdoor, and related industries. Organized and owned by the National Shooting Sports Foundation (NSSF), the firearm industry trade association, it serves as a platform for exhibitors to unveil innovative products, conduct business transactions, and participate in educational programming. Restricted to verified trade attendees, government entities, and mediaexcluding the general publicthe event emphasizes B2B networking over consumer sales.
Held each January in Las Vegas at the Venetian Expo and Caesars Forum, the SHOT Show has expanded significantly since its inception, with the 2025 edition marking the 47th occurrence and featuring record-breaking exhibit space exceeding 815,000 square feet, a historic number of over 2,700 exhibitors, and robust attendance driving industry momentum. This growth underscores its role as the world's premier gathering for the sector, where manufacturers, distributors, retailers, and innovators debut advancements in firearms, ammunition, optics, apparel, and tactical gear.
The event's significance lies in its facilitation of market trends, product launches, and policy discussions tailored to industry stakeholders, contributing to economic impact through billions in generated orders and reinforcing supply chain resilience amid fluctuating demand. While occasionally critiqued by gun control organizations for spotlighting the firearms trade, the SHOT Show remains a cornerstone of legitimate commerce and innovation, unburdened by public access to prioritize professional discourse.

History

Founding and Early Development

The Shooting, Hunting, and Outdoor Trade Show (SHOT Show) was established by the National Shooting Sports Foundation (NSSF) in 1979 to serve as a centralized platform for the firearms, ammunition, hunting, and outdoor industries to conduct business, showcase innovations, and generate revenue for NSSF's advocacy and promotional programs. The inaugural event took place from January 1922, 1979, at the St. Louis Convention Center in St. Louis, Missouri, featuring 290 exhibitors occupying 52,153 net square feet of space and drawing 5,600 attendees. It was officially opened by St. Louis Mayor John Conway, with former Texas Governor John Connally delivering the keynote address, and was managed by the Charles Snitow Organization under an NSSF committee.
In its early years, the SHOT Show demonstrated rapid growth amid rotating venues to accommodate expanding participation. The 1980 edition shifted to the Moscone Center in San Francisco, California, attracting 8,500 attendees. By 1981, held in New Orleans, Louisiana, attendance surged to 17,800. The fifth show in 1983, back in Dallas, Texas, expanded to 962 exhibitors, 162,000 net square feet, and nearly 20,000 attendees, solidifying its role as a key funding mechanism for NSSF initiatives by the mid-1980s. This progression reflected the industry's need for a unified, professional trade venue, distinct from fragmented regional events, while NSSF ownership ensured alignment with trade association goals over commercial interests.

Expansion and Milestones

The SHOT Show underwent substantial growth in scale following its early iterations, transitioning from a modest gathering to the world's largest trade event for the shooting, hunting, and outdoor industries. Initially rotating among cities such as Las Vegas, Orlando, New Orleans, and Dallas from 1979 to 2009, the event attracted increasing numbers of exhibitors and attendees as industry participation expanded. By the late 2000s, annual attendance consistently exceeded tens of thousands, reflecting rising demand for business-to-business networking in firearms, ammunition, and related sectors.
A pivotal milestone occurred in 2010, when the National Shooting Sports Foundation established the SHOT Show as a permanent annual event in Las Vegas at the Sands Expo Center, ending the rotation to capitalize on the city's infrastructure and accessibility for international participants. This decision facilitated consistent logistics and vendor planning, contributing to further expansion; by 2014, attendance soared to a new record, surpassing prior highs and drawing professionals from over 100 countries. Subsequent years saw incremental records, including nearly 65,000 attendees in 2017, underscoring the show's maturation into a cornerstone of industry commerce.
The 2018 edition marked the 40th anniversary, with over 60,000 industry professionals navigating 12.5 miles of aisles across expanded floors, highlighting decades of compounded growth from the inaugural 1979 show's 5,600 attendees and 290 exhibitors. Venue enhancements accelerated in the 2020s; after cancellation in 2020 and a virtual format in 2021 due to the COVID-19 pandemic, the 2022 return achieved the largest attendance to dateover 70,000while incorporating Caesars Forum for additional exhibit space totaling more than 800,000 square feet. This expansion addressed surging post-pandemic industry momentum, with background checks for firearm sales reaching 40 million from 2020 to 2021.
Continued milestones include record exhibitor participation in specialized areas, such as the Suppliers Showcase in 2019, and overall scale achievements in 2024, positioning the SHOT Show as the third-largest trade event in Las Vegas by footprint. These developments have solidified its role in driving innovation, with features like product previews and educational programming adapting to exhibitor needs amid evolving market dynamics.

Adaptations to Industry Changes

In response to the COVID-19 pandemic, the National Shooting Sports Foundation canceled the 2021 SHOT Show on October 30, 2020, due to surging infection rates, venue restrictions, and health risks for the indoor gathering of over 50,000 attendees. Organizers had explored hybrid formats but ultimately prioritized safety, deferring product launches and networking to 2022, when the event resumed with capacity limits, masking requirements, and testing protocols amid Omicron concerns from major exhibitors. This marked the first cancellation in the show's history, reflecting adaptations to public health crises that disrupted supply chains and accelerated industry shifts toward direct-to-consumer sales.
Facing intensified regulatory pressures, including ATF rule changes and state-level restrictions, SHOT Show incorporated dedicated policy forums to foster dialogue between industry leaders and officials. The Governors' Forum, launched around 2022 and in its fourth year by January 2025, featured pro-Second Amendment governors emphasizing the firearms sector's economic role and opposing restrictive measures. Similarly, the Attorneys General Forum, introduced in 2024, addressed federal and state regulatory challenges, such as Zero Tolerance Policy enforcement and emerging compliance "off-ramps" for manufacturers. These sessions adapted the event from a product-focused trade show to a platform for advocacy, countering perceived overreach amid stalled reforms like suppressor deregulation.
Market evolution toward digital and technological integration prompted expansions in programming and exhibitor categories. Post-2020, seminars on e-commerce trendssuch as "buy online, pickup in-store" models and payment diversificationhelped retailers navigate online sales growth, which surged during pandemic-driven demand spikes. The show increasingly showcased innovations like modular firearms, advanced optics, and eco-friendly ammunition, mirroring consumer shifts toward precision, sustainability, and concealability amid ammunition shortages and environmental scrutiny. By 2025, exhibit space records underscored this diversification, incorporating law enforcement tools and virtual training systems to align with broadening industry applications beyond traditional hunting.

Organization and Governance

Role of the National Shooting Sports Foundation

The National Shooting Sports Foundation (NSSF), established in 1961 as the trade association for the firearms, ammunition, hunting, and shooting sports industries, owns and sponsors the SHOT Show, the world's largest trade event for these sectors. Through this ownership, NSSF leverages the annual gatheringfirst held in 1979to advance its core mission of promoting, protecting, and preserving hunting and recreational shooting activities, while fostering business development among manufacturers, distributors, retailers, and related professionals.
NSSF's governance role extends to setting exhibitor eligibility criteria, enforcing industry standards such as compliance with federal regulations, and curating programming that includes product launches, educational seminars, and networking opportunities tailored to law enforcement, military, and outdoor enthusiasts. The foundation collaborates with event management firm ConvExx for operational execution, including venue logistics and attendee registration, but retains strategic oversight to align the show with broader advocacy goals, such as countering restrictive legislation and supporting hunter recruitment initiatives.
Revenues from the SHOT Show, generated through exhibitor fees, sponsorships, and attendance since its inception, have served as a primary funding source for NSSF's programs, enabling investments in public policy lobbying, safety education, and range development grants that total millions annually. This financial model underscores NSSF's use of the event not merely as a trade exposition but as a cornerstone for sustaining industry-wide efforts to maintain access to shooting sports amid evolving regulatory landscapes.

Event Format and Programming

The SHOT Show is structured as a four-day business-to-business trade event, restricted to qualified industry professionals including manufacturers, distributors, retailers, buyers, and media, with no public attendance allowed. Held annually in mid-January, the event features a central exhibit hall where over 2,500 exhibitors display firearms, ammunition, optics, apparel, and related outdoor products across categories like hunting, shooting sports, and law enforcement equipment. The show floor operates daily from approximately 9:00 a.m. to 5:00 p.m., facilitating product demonstrations, order placements, and direct business negotiations between exhibitors and buyers.
Preceding the main trade show days is Industry Day at the Range, a dedicated Monday event sponsored by the National Shooting Sports Foundation (NSSF), where invited media, buyers, and influencers access a private shooting range to test new products under controlled conditions. This hands-on programming extends the overall "SHOT Week" footprint, incorporating setup, teardown, and ancillary gatherings that span five to six days total.
Educational and professional development programming runs concurrently with the exhibit hall, emphasizing skill-building and regulatory compliance. SHOT University provides a la carte seminars and workshops on retail management, range operations, and business strategies, with sessions priced at $40 for NSSF members and $75 for non-members, typically offering a dozen classes per event. Export Training Sessions address evolving international regulations for firearms and ammunition exports, targeting manufacturers and exporters to ensure compliance. The Law Enforcement Education Program (LEEP) delivers specialized training for officers and agency representatives, including tactical product overviews and sessions on emerging technologies, scheduled throughout the week.
Networking forms a core component, with organized receptions, luncheons, and informal meetups designed to foster industry connections among attendees from the shooting, hunting, and tactical sectors. These events, often held in adjacent venues or during off-hours, complement the transactional focus of the trade floor by promoting collaborations and information exchange. A Supplier Showcase on upper levels of the venue highlights ancillary services like logistics and booth materials, open during move-in periods to support exhibitor preparation.

Historical Locations

From 1980 through 2009, the event followed a rotational schedule across multiple U.S. cities, selected for their convention center capacities, logistical accessibility, and appeal to the shooting sports industry. This approach facilitated expansion amid rising demand, as exhibit space and attendance grew from modest beginnings to tens of thousands by the late 2000s, though specific venue constraints sometimes limited scalability compared to later fixed locations.
Key host cities during this period included Dallas and New Orleans, which hosted multiple editions, alongside single or fewer instances in other metropolitan areas. The rotation reflected practical considerations like facility size and regional industry presence, but by 2009, the need for larger, consistent infrastructure prompted a shift away from annual changes.

Las Vegas Era and Future Contracts

The SHOT Show established its ongoing presence in Las Vegas in 2010, marking a shift from prior rotations among cities such as Orlando and New Orleans to a fixed location capable of handling the event's expanding footprint of over 600,000 square feet of exhibit space. This era has seen consistent growth in scale, with the Sands Expo and Convention Center as the anchor venue through its initial years, chosen for its centralized infrastructure, accessibility, and capacity to support industry-only access without public disruption. By 2018, the show celebrated its 20th Las Vegas hosting at Sands, reflecting cumulative attendance exceeding 60,000 professionals amid rising exhibitor numbers.
Venue expansions addressed surging demand, as the core Sands facility proved insufficient for record-breaking layouts topping 815,000 square feet by 2023. Starting in 2020, exhibit space extended to the MGM Grand Conference Center, followed by integration of Caesars Forum in 2021 for specialized sections like archery and optics; this multi-site model persisted after Sands Expo's 2021 rebranding to Venetian Expo under new ownership, preserving operational continuity. The 2025 edition, held January 2124, utilized Venetian Expo and Caesars Forum to host over 2,500 exhibitors and 55,000 attendees, underscoring Las Vegas's role in enabling segmented programming across 2.6 million gross square feet.
Contracts with Las Vegas partners, including Venetian Expo operators and Caesars Entertainment, secure the venue through 2027, with provisions for cost containment amid industry expansion. In April 2023, the National Shooting Sports Foundation initiated talks to extend agreements through 2030, prioritizing low booth ratesaveraging $43 per square footand venue investments tailored to shooting sports logistics, such as secure firearms handling and proximity to ranges for product testing. As of 2025, the 2026 event remains scheduled for January 2023 at Venetian Expo and Caesars Forum, with no announced shifts, affirming Las Vegas's entrenched status despite occasional critiques of urban density challenges.

Scale and Economic Impact

Attendance Trends

The SHOT Show's attendance has expanded dramatically since its 1979 debut, reflecting the firearms and outdoor industry's maturation from a niche sector to a major economic force. Early iterations in the 1980s and 1990s drew crowds in the low tens of thousands, with figures reaching 35,102 by 1997 as the event gained prominence among manufacturers, retailers, and media. Steady increases through the 2000s, averaging around 30,000 annually, culminated in mid-2010s peaks driven by surging demand for shooting sports and accessories, topping 67,000 in 2014.
Pre-pandemic highs persisted, with nearly 65,000 in 2017 and over 60,000 in 2020, underscoring the show's status as a premier trade venue amid rising U.S. firearm ownership rates. The COVID-19 pandemic disrupted this trajectory: the 2021 edition shifted entirely virtual, forgoing in-person attendance, while the 2022 return registered 43,200 participants amid health protocols and supply chain issues.
Post-2022 recovery has stabilized attendance near 55,000, exceeding the immediate pandemic low but falling short of mid-2010s records, as evidenced by 52,305 in 2023, over 55,400 in 2024, and more than 54,000 in 2025. This trend aligns with NSSF reports of robust buyer turnout from all 50 states and over 100 countries, though international participationhistorically 10-15% of totalshas varied with travel restrictions and economic factors.

Contributions to Economy and Industry

The SHOT Show generates more than $88 million in non-gaming revenue annually for the Las Vegas economy through attendee spending on lodging, dining, transportation, and related services. This impact stems from attracting over 55,000 industry professionals, including buyers, exhibitors, and media from all 50 U.S. states and more than 119 countries, who occupy over 830,000 net square feet of exhibit space across venues like The Venetian Expo and Caesars Forum. As one of the largest trade shows in Las Vegas, the event ranks among the top contributors to local tourism and hospitality sectors, with its scale equivalent to hosting thousands of temporary visitors during a concentrated January period.
Beyond the host city, the SHOT Show bolsters the national economy by serving as the central hub for the $8.9 billion firearms and ammunition manufacturing sector, which generates a total economic multiplier effect exceeding $90.1 billion and sustains over 384,000 jobs across manufacturing, retail, distribution, and support industries. The event facilitates billions in business transactions, including orders for firearms, optics, ammunition, and accessories, directly contributing to industry revenue that funds excise taxes exceeding $1 billion yearly to the Wildlife Restoration Trust Fund for conservation efforts. Proceeds from the show also support the National Shooting Sports Foundation's (NSSF) operations, enabling advocacy, research, and compliance programs that enhance sector stability and growth.
Within the firearms, hunting, and outdoor industries, the SHOT Show drives innovation and market expansion by providing a platform for over 2,800 exhibitors to debut products, forge partnerships, and conduct educational sessions on safety, technology, and regulatory compliance. This annual convergence accelerates product development cycles, as demonstrated by historical launches of influential technologies like advanced optics and suppressors, which subsequently increase retail sales and manufacturer output nationwide. By concentrating buyer-seller interactions, the event reduces transaction costs and expands market reach, particularly for small and mid-sized firms, fostering a competitive ecosystem that has seen the industry's economic footprint grow 379% since 2008.

Exhibitors and Innovations

Exhibitor Categories

The SHOT Show organizes exhibitors into specialized product categories that reflect the diverse segments of the shooting, hunting, and outdoor trade industries, enabling targeted networking and product discovery for attendees. These categories, maintained by the National Shooting Sports Foundation (NSSF), cover core areas like firearms manufacturing and accessories, while extending to supportive sectors such as law enforcement equipment and outdoor apparel. The classification system prioritizes relevance to commercial buyers, with eligibility restricted to verified industry participants offering products or services aligned with shooting sports, hunting, and related pursuits.
Major categories include firearms and components, where exhibitors showcase handguns, rifles, shotguns, barrels, grips, stocks, and maintenance tools; ammunition and reloading supplies, encompassing cartridges, magazines, and reloading equipment; and optics such as scopes, sights, telescopes, and related accessories. Additional groupings address hunting and outdoor gear, including crossbows, treestands, feeders, camping items, bags, packs, and survival kits; apparel and personal equipment like men's and women's clothing, footwear, eyewear, hearing protection, and leathergoods; and cutlery, knives, sharpeners, and holsters. Law enforcement-focused exhibitors highlight training aids, range equipment, vehicles, communications devices, lighting, and protective gear, often tailored for tactical and professional applications.
The Supplier Showcase provides a distinct venue for manufacturing inputs and services, featuring exhibitors in tools, machinery, plastics, metal extrusions, fabrics, and custom fabrication, which support upstream production in the firearms and outdoor sectors. International exhibitors are integrated across categories, with dedicated recognition for global participants contributing to the event's scope. Wholesalers, distributors, retailer services, media publications, and gunsmithing tools round out the ecosystem, facilitating supply chain connections and industry support.

Key Product Developments and Launches

The SHOT Show annually serves as a primary platform for unveiling new firearms, ammunition, optics, suppressors, and accessories, with the New Product Center displaying thousands of entries eligible for NSSF innovation awards. Exhibitors often time major releases to coincide with the event to capitalize on industry attendance exceeding 50,000 professionals, enabling immediate feedback and pre-orders.
Historically, the show has hosted pivotal introductions that influenced market trends, such as Smith & Wesson's debut of the M&P15 semi-automatic rifle in 2006, which expanded AR-15 style platforms into mainstream polymer-framed designs for civilian and law enforcement use. Subsequent years saw advancements in modular systems and materials; for instance, the adoption of lightweight alloys and composites became prominent in the 2010s, reflecting demands for reduced weight in hunting and tactical gear without sacrificing durability.
In recent iterations, product launches emphasize enhanced ergonomics, suppressor integration, and caliber versatility. At the 2025 show, KelTec introduced the PR57, a compact striker-fired pistol chambered in 5.7x28mm with a 13-round capacity and optics-ready slide, targeting concealed carry users seeking high-velocity performance. KGM Technologies launched the FDP suppressor, featuring flow-through design for reduced backpressure and compatibility with 5.56mm and 7.62mm rifles, addressing gas blowback issues in semi-automatic hosts. KRISS USA revealed the Vector Gen 3, incorporating delayed blowback improvements and modular rails for better customization in submachine gun derivatives.
Ammunition developments have also accelerated, with 2025 debuts including lead-free shotshells from Federal and CCI for hunting compliance in restricted areas, alongside precision rifle loads like Hornady's expanded 6.5 Creedmoor variants optimized for long-range accuracy. Optics innovations featured thermal imaging monoculars from Pulsar with extended battery life up to 8 hours, enhancing low-light hunting capabilities. These launches underscore a shift toward technology integration, such as electronic sighting and material science, driven by consumer demands for reliability in diverse environments.

Controversies

Opposition from Gun Control Advocates

Gun control organizations, including Everytown for Gun Safety and its affiliate Students Demand Action, have criticized the SHOT Show as a venue where firearms manufacturers promote products amid ongoing gun violence, organizing protests and public campaigns to demand industry accountability. These groups argue that the event, organized by the National Shooting Sports Foundation (NSSF), facilitates the introduction of new firearms and accessories that they link to mass shootings and everyday violence, calling for reforms such as enhanced tracing of crime guns and restrictions on certain product sales.
In January 2018, shortly after the October 2017 Las Vegas mass shooting that killed 58 people near the show's typical venue, gun control advocates staged protests outside the SHOT Show in Las Vegas, highlighting the event's location and urging stricter regulations on gun sales to prevent similar incidents. Everytown and allied activists renewed calls for measures like universal background checks, framing the trade show as insensitive and emblematic of industry priorities over public safety.
Protests continued in subsequent years, with Students Demand Action leading demonstrations on January 17, 2023, outside the Las Vegas Convention Center during the SHOT Show; participants, including survivors of school shootings, demanded that manufacturers cease production of assault-style weapons and improve supply chain oversight to reduce guns diverted to criminals. Everytown supported these actions with targeted digital advertising in Las Vegas, emphasizing data on guns recovered in crimes that originated from licensed dealers exhibiting at such events.
Advocacy persisted into 2024 and 2025, with Everytown issuing reports and press releases ahead of the annual show, portraying it as a gathering where industry executives prioritize profitssuch as through new product launcheswhile gun violence claims over 40,000 lives annually in the United States, according to their cited figures from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. These efforts, including in-person confrontations with executives, aim to pressure the NSSF and exhibitors to adopt voluntary safety measures, though critics of the groups note their funding from sources like Michael Bloomberg and a focus on policy advocacy that overlooks data on defensive gun uses or criminal misuse patterns not directly tied to trade shows.

Industry Responses and Legal Defenses

The National Shooting Sports Foundation (NSSF), the trade association organizing the SHOT Show, has responded to opposition from gun control advocates by emphasizing the event's focus on lawful commerce and industry self-regulation. Following the 2012 Sandy Hook Elementary School shooting, NSSF President Steve Sanetti addressed attendees at the 2013 SHOT Show, rejecting accusations of industry culpability and stating that "we are not the evildoers," while attributing responsibility to criminals and failures in mental health systems rather than legal firearm sales. Similarly, after the 2017 Las Vegas mass shooting, NSSF and exhibitors proceeded with the event despite proximity to the site, with industry representatives discussing voluntary measures like phasing out bump stocks to mitigate public relations fallout without awaiting federal mandates.
In countering protests by groups such as Everytown for Gun Safety and Students Demand Actionwhich have targeted the SHOT Show annually in Las Vegas with demonstrations against perceived profiteering from violenceNSSF has portrayed such actions as politically motivated theatrics that ignore industry efforts on safety. For example, NSSF has promoted programs like Project ChildSafe, which distributes free firearm safety kits, and Real Solutions for Safer Communities, arguing these address root causes like unauthorized access more effectively than restrictive legislation. NSSF critiques gun control narratives as prioritizing advocacy over evidence, noting that lawful owners and manufacturers comply with federal background checks and tracing requirements under laws like the Gun Control Act of 1968.
Legally, NSSF has mounted defenses against broader industry challenges often highlighted at SHOT Show forums, framing them as "lawfare" aimed at circumventing protections like the Protection of Lawful Commerce in Arms Act (PLCAA) of 2005, which shields manufacturers from liability for third-party criminal acts. Annual State Attorneys General Forums at the event, such as the January 2025 session with five pro-Second Amendment officials, coordinate strategies to counter state-level suits alleging public nuisance or negligent marketing. NSSF filed an amicus brief supporting defendants in a PLCAA-related case and challenged New York's 2021 public nuisance law allowing suits against firearm sellers, though a 2025 Second Circuit ruling upheld the statute against facial invalidity claims. These efforts underscore NSSF's position that legal attacks undermine constitutional rights without reducing crime, as evidenced by FBI data showing most firearm violence involves illegally obtained guns.

Significance

Role in Firearms and Outdoor Industries

The SHOT Show functions as the premier annual trade event for professionals in the firearms, shooting sports, hunting, law enforcement, and outdoor industries, serving as a critical platform for business-to-business interactions and product exposure. Organized by the National Shooting Sports Foundation (NSSF), it connects manufacturers, distributors, retailers, ranges, and government agencies through extensive exhibits of firearms, ammunition, optics, apparel, and related gear, enabling direct negotiations, demonstrations, and order placements that drive retail inventories and supply chains. Restricted to verified industry participants from all 50 U.S. states and over 100 countries, the event excludes public access to prioritize professional dealings and maintain focus on commercial viability.
Central to its role is the facilitation of innovation and trend-setting, where exhibitors debut new technologies and designs that influence manufacturing standards and market directions across the sectors. Annual product launchesoften numbering in the thousandsallow companies to gauge buyer interest, secure partnerships, and accelerate adoption of advancements in areas like precision optics, modular firearms, and outdoor equipment durability. The 2025 edition exemplified this with over 2,850 exhibitors occupying record exhibit space, showcasing breakthroughs amid sessions on technological integration and supply chain resilience.
Additionally, the SHOT Show bolsters industry sustainability through educational programming, networking forums, and advocacy resources that equip participants with insights into regulatory compliance, export strategies, and operational efficiencies. These elements cultivate long-term growth by addressing challenges like material innovations and market expansion, reinforcing the event's status as a barometer of sector momentum and a catalyst for collective advancement in firearms production, hunting recreation, and outdoor pursuits.

Broader Cultural and Policy Influence

The SHOT Show, organized by the National Shooting Sports Foundation (NSSF), functions as a key platform for firearms industry leaders, policymakers, and advocates to discuss and advance pro-Second Amendment legislation. Annual events such as the Governors' Forum bring together state executives to highlight support for expanding outdoor shooting opportunities and enacting laws that shield the industry from financial discrimination by banks and insurers. For instance, in January 2025, governors praised legislative efforts in states like Wyoming to broaden recreational shooting access and counter legal challenges termed "lawfare" by industry representatives.
On the federal level, the NSSF's advocacybolstered by SHOT Show networkinghas positioned it as a leading gun lobby, outspending other groups on issues like opposing restrictions on suppressors and short-barreled rifles. The event also facilitates international policy influence through the U.S. Department of Commerce's International Buyer Program, which since 2013 has hosted global attendees to promote American firearms exports, contributing to over $1.7 billion in annual sales to Latin America alone by 2023.
Culturally, SHOT Show reinforces a narrative of responsible firearm ownership and hunting heritage, countering media portrayals through initiatives like the NSSF's "Faces of Firearms Safety" campaign launched in 2025, which highlights diverse gun owners to challenge stereotypes. It has notably driven growth in women's engagement with shooting sports, with exhibitors adapting products and marketing since the mid-2010s to reflect increasing female participation, evidenced by dedicated women's sections and sessions at the show. Events like the Congressional Sportsmen's Foundation gatherings at SHOT Show further embed firearms in conservation and outdoor traditions, featuring high-profile speakers to promote these values amid broader societal debates on gun culture.
