The Big Bang is the prevailing cosmological model explaining the origin and evolution of the universe. According to this theory, the universe began approximately 13.8 billion years ago from an incredibly hot, dense state and has been expanding and cooling ever since. This is not merely a hypothesis or a speculative idea. It is supported by multiple independent lines of evidence so compelling that it is accepted by virtually every working cosmologist and physicist in the world.

The story of the universe begins at a point that challenges the limits of human comprehension. At the very beginning, everything that exists today, every galaxy, every star, every planet, every atom of every element, every photon of light, every human being and everything they have ever built, was compressed into a region smaller than an atom. The density and temperature were so extreme that the laws of physics as we understand them break down. We cannot describe the conditions at time zero with our current understanding of physics, which is why the moment of the Big Bang itself remains one of the deepest unsolved problems in science.

What we can describe is what happened immediately after. Within the first fraction of a second, the universe underwent a period of extraordinarily rapid expansion called cosmic inflation, during which it grew from subatomic size to many times larger than the observable universe today. Inflation explains several puzzling features of the cosmos: why the universe appears to be the same temperature in every direction, why it is geometrically flat rather than curved, and why no magnetic monopoles have ever been detected despite theoretical predictions that they should exist.

After inflation ended, the universe continued to expand and cool. In the first three minutes, the temperatures were hot enough for nuclear fusion to occur, and protons and neutrons combined to form the nuclei of the simplest elements: hydrogen, helium, and trace amounts of lithium and beryllium. This process, called Big Bang nucleosynthesis, produced approximately seventy-five percent hydrogen and twenty-five percent helium by mass, a ratio that is still observed in the oldest stars and in regions of the universe that have not been significantly processed by stellar nucleosynthesis. The precise agreement between the predicted and observed abundances of these light elements is one of the strongest confirmations of the Big Bang theory.

For the first three hundred and eighty thousand years, the universe was so hot and dense that photons could not travel freely. They were constantly being absorbed and re-emitted by the plasma of charged particles that filled space. The universe was opaque, like the interior of a star. Then, as the universe cooled to approximately three thousand Kelvin, the electrons and protons combined to form neutral hydrogen atoms in a process called recombination. With no more free electrons to scatter photons, light was suddenly free to travel. The universe became transparent. The photons that were released at that moment have been traveling through space ever since, and we can still detect them today. They form the Cosmic Microwave Background, or CMB, a faint glow of radiation that fills the entire sky and corresponds to a temperature of approximately 2.7 Kelvin, about minus four hundred and fifty-four degrees Fahrenheit. The discovery of the CMB in 1965 by Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson, who initially thought the signal was noise from bird droppings on their antenna, was one of the most important observations in the history of science. It provided direct evidence for the Big Bang and earned them the Nobel Prize in Physics.

The tiny variations in the temperature of the CMB, measured with exquisite precision by satellites including COBE, WMAP, and Planck, reveal the seeds of all the structure in the universe. Regions that were slightly denser than average attracted more matter through gravity over hundreds of millions of years, eventually forming the first stars, galaxies, and galaxy clusters. Regions that were slightly less dense became the vast voids that separate galaxy clusters today. The entire large-scale structure of the cosmos, the cosmic web of galaxies connected by filaments of dark matter and separated by enormous voids, grew from these microscopic variations in the density of the early universe.

The first stars formed approximately one hundred to two hundred million years after the Big Bang. These were monsters: hundreds of times more massive than our Sun, burning through their nuclear fuel in a few million years and then exploding as supernovae. These explosions were crucial because they produced heavy elements, everything beyond hydrogen and helium, and scattered them into the surrounding gas, enriching it with the raw materials needed to form rocky planets, liquid water, and eventually, life. Every atom of carbon in your body, every atom of oxygen you breathe, every atom of iron in your blood, was forged inside a star that exploded billions of years ago. We are, quite literally, made of star stuff.

The universe continues to expand today, and the expansion is accelerating. This was discovered in 1998 by two independent teams studying distant supernovae, and it was so unexpected that it won the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2011. The acceleration is attributed to dark energy, a mysterious form of energy that permeates all of space and exerts a repulsive gravitational effect. Dark energy constitutes approximately sixty-eight percent of the total energy content of the universe. Dark matter, another mysterious substance that interacts with ordinary matter only through gravity, constitutes approximately twenty-seven percent. Ordinary matter, the protons, neutrons, and electrons that make up everything we can see, touch, and measure, constitutes only about five percent of the universe. We live in a cosmos in which ninety-five percent of the content is something we do not understand.

The evidence for the Big Bang is overwhelming and comes from multiple independent sources. The expansion of the universe, discovered by Edwin Hubble in 1929, which shows that galaxies are moving away from each other at speeds proportional to their distance. The Cosmic Microwave Background, which is exactly the radiation pattern predicted by the Big Bang theory. The abundance of light elements, which matches the predictions of Big Bang nucleosynthesis to remarkable precision. The observed evolution of galaxies over cosmic time: distant galaxies, which we see as they were billions of years ago because their light has taken billions of years to reach us, look different from nearby galaxies, confirming that the universe has changed over time. The age of the universe, determined independently from the expansion rate, the CMB, and the ages of the oldest known stars, consistently yields a value of approximately 13.8 billion years.

The Big Bang theory does not claim to explain why the universe exists or what caused the initial conditions. It does not claim to describe what happened before the Big Bang, or whether "before" even has meaning in a context where time itself may have begun with the Big Bang. It does not explain what dark matter or dark energy are, or why the universe has the specific properties it has. These are open questions at the frontier of physics, and answering them is the work of the next generation of scientists.

What the Big Bang theory does provide is a coherent, evidence-based, mathematically precise account of how the universe evolved from a hot, dense initial state to the vast, cool, structured cosmos we observe today. It is one of the most successful scientific theories ever developed, tested against data from particle accelerators, space telescopes, microwave detectors, and gravitational wave observatories. It tells us where we came from, in the deepest physical sense: from a point of infinite density, through billions of years of expansion and cooling, through the births and deaths of countless stars, to a small blue planet orbiting an ordinary star in an unremarkable galaxy, where a species of curious primates looked up at the night sky and figured out how it all began.
