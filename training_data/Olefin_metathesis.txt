Olefin metathesis

1x
 Olefin metathesis is a transition metal-catalyzed reaction in organic chemistry that reorganizes the carbon-carbon double bonds of two olefins, resulting in the exchange of alkylidene groups to form new olefin products, often under mild conditions and with high selectivity. This process, first observed in the 1950s during the polymerization of norbornene, enables the efficient construction of complex carbon skeletons from simple alkenes and has become a cornerstone of modern synthetic chemistry.
The mechanism of olefin metathesis, proposed by Yves Chauvin in 1971, proceeds through the formation of a metal-carbene species that undergoes cycloaddition with an olefin to generate a metallacyclobutane intermediate, followed by cycloreversion to yield new carbene and olefin species. This carbene-exchange pathway allows for reversible redistribution of double bonds and is driven by thermodynamic factors, such as the release of ethylene in certain variants. Early catalysts were ill-defined mixtures, but the development of well-characterized complexes revolutionized the field: Richard R. Schrock introduced high-oxidation-state molybdenum and tungsten alkylidene catalysts in the 1990s, known for their exceptional reactivity but sensitivity to functional groups, while Robert H. Grubbs pioneered robust ruthenium-based catalysts, such as the first-generation Grubbs catalyst ((PCy)ClRu=CHPh), which tolerate air, moisture, and a wide array of functional groups. These advancements earned Chauvin, Grubbs, and Schrock the 2005 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for their contributions to the metathesis method in organic synthesis.
Olefin metathesis encompasses several key variants, including ring-closing metathesis (RCM), which forms cyclic alkenes by intramolecular bond exchange and is widely used in natural product synthesis; ring-opening metathesis polymerization (ROMP), which generates polymers from strained cyclic olefins like norbornene; and cross-metathesis (CM), enabling intermolecular exchanges between terminal olefins. These reactions have profound applications in pharmaceuticals (e.g., synthesis of anticancer agents like epothilone), materials science (e.g., production of specialty polymers), and industrial processes (e.g., conversion of seed oils to biofuels), promoting greener chemistry by minimizing waste and avoiding harsh reagents. Ongoing developments, including aqueous and stereoselective variants, continue to expand its utility in bioorthogonal and sustainable synthesis; as of 2025, advances such as de novo designed artificial metathase enzymes have further enhanced catalyst efficiency and selectivity.

Fundamentals

Definition and Principles

Olefin metathesis is a catalytic organic reaction that involves the redistribution of carbon-carbon double bond fragments between two alkenes (olefins), resulting in the formation of new alkenes. This process enables the exchange of alkylidene groups across the double bonds, providing a versatile method for constructing carbon-carbon bonds in synthetic chemistry. At its core, the reaction proceeds through a cycloaddition-cycloreversion sequence involving a metal carbene species, which facilitates the cleavage and reformation of the alkene bonds without net consumption of the catalyst.
The general reaction scheme for olefin metathesis can be represented as the interchange of two different alkenes, often depicted in its equilibrium form to emphasize the reversible nature:
R1CH=CHR2+R3CH=CHR4R1CH=CHR3+R2CH=CHR4
\mathrm{R_1CH=CHR_2 + R_3CH=CHR_4 \rightleftharpoons R_1CH=CHR_3 + R_2CH=CHR_4}
R1CH=CHR2+R3CH=CHR4R1CH=CHR3+R2CH=CHR4
This equation illustrates the symmetric redistribution, where the products are new combinations of the original alkylidene units (R, R, R, R). Alkenes, the requisite substrates, are hydrocarbons containing at least one carbon-carbon double bond, which serves as the reactive site for this bond reorganization. The reaction is typically mediated by transition metal catalysts, which activate the alkenes toward this exchange, though the specific catalytic species are not detailed here.
A key aspect of olefin metathesis is the stereochemistry of the resulting alkenes, which can produce mixtures of E and Z isomers depending on the substituents and reaction conditions. The E isomer is often favored due to thermodynamic control, driven by lower steric interactions in the trans configuration, particularly when bulky groups are present. This selectivity arises from the equilibrium nature of the reaction, allowing isomerization to the more stable product over time.

Scope and Terminology

Olefin metathesis encompasses a broad range of reactions involving the interchange of alkylidene groups between alkenes, applicable to both acyclic and cyclic substrates. This includes unstrained acyclic alkenes, such as terminal or internal olefins used in cross-metathesis, as well as cyclic alkenes ranging from small strained rings (e.g., cyclopropene or cyclobutene) to larger unstrained systems (e.g., cyclohexene derivatives). Strained cyclic alkenes are particularly reactive in processes like ring-opening due to relief of ring strain, while unstrained systems require careful control to favor desired outcomes over polymerization. The scope is limited to carbon-carbon double bonds in alkenes and excludes related transformations like alkyne metathesis, which involves triple bonds and distinct catalytic mechanisms.
Key terminology in olefin metathesis ensures precise description of reaction pathways and products. Self-metathesis refers to the exchange between two identical alkene molecules, often yielding symmetrical dimers and ethylene as a byproduct in the case of terminal alkenes. Productive metathesis describes the selective formation of unsymmetrical cross-products from dissimilar alkenes, minimizing self-metathesis side reactions. A carbene, in this context, denotes a reactive metal-alkylidene intermediate (M=CR) that initiates and propagates the reaction via cycloadditions. Ethylene frequently appears as a volatile byproduct or substrate, driving equilibrium in many transformations. According to IUPAC recommendations, metathesis products are named using standard substitutive nomenclature for alkenes, with the "-ene" suffix indicating the double bond position and E/Z descriptors for stereochemistry where applicable; organometallic carbenes are designated with the "-ylidene" suffix in additive nomenclature.
Olefin metathesis reactions are classified based on catalytic mode and reaction environment. Homogeneous catalysis employs soluble, well-defined metal complexes (e.g., ruthenium or molybdenum alkylidenes) for precise control in laboratory synthesis, offering broad substrate scope and stereoselectivity. In contrast, heterogeneous catalysis uses supported metal oxides (e.g., rhenium or tungsten on alumina) for industrial-scale processes, enabling catalyst recovery but often with narrower scope due to lower activity for complex substrates. Reactions can also occur in solution for bulk processing or on solid phases, such as polymer resins, facilitating combinatorial synthesis or sequential transformations without intermediate isolation. Common abbreviations include CM (cross-metathesis) for intermolecular exchanges, RCM (ring-closing metathesis) for intramolecular cyclizations, and ROMP (ring-opening metathesis polymerization) for chain-growth polymerizations of cyclic monomers.

Types

Cross-Metathesis

Cross-metathesis (CM) is an intermolecular variant of olefin metathesis involving the exchange of alkylidene groups between two distinct alkenes, typically catalyzed by transition metal complexes such as ruthenium carbenes. This reaction enables the formation of new carbon-carbon double bonds and is particularly valuable for constructing functionalized alkenes in organic synthesis. The general reaction between a terminal alkene and an internal alkene can be represented as:
RCH=CHX2+RXCH=CHRXRCH=CHRX+CHX2=CHRX
\ce{RCH=CH2 + R'CH=CHR'' -> RCH=CHR' + CH2=CHR''}
RCH=CHX2+RXCH=CHRXRCH=CHRX+CHX2=CHRX
where the cross-product is the desired disubstituted alkene, and ethylene or other byproducts may form depending on the substrates.
A primary challenge in CM is achieving high selectivity amid the statistical distribution of products, which arises from competing self-metathesis reactions of each alkene, potentially yielding up to three distinct products (two homodimers and one cross-product) in a 1:1 mixture. To address this, alkenes are classified by reactivity with ruthenium catalysts into Type I (highly reactive, e.g., terminal alkenes that rapidly homodimerize and undergo secondary metathesis), Type II (moderately reactive, e.g., allyl silanes or protected alcohols with slower homodimerization), and Type III (low reactivity, e.g., sterically hindered or electron-deficient alkenes like styrenes that resist homodimerization). Optimal selectivity is achieved by pairing a Type I alkene with a Type II or III partner, leveraging reactivity differences to favor the cross-product while minimizing homodimers; for instance, using excess of the more reactive alkene can shift the equilibrium toward the desired outcome.
Typical CM conditions employ ruthenium-based catalysts at temperatures ranging from 25 to 100C, often in aprotic solvents such as dichloromethane or toluene, to promote efficient exchange without catalyst decomposition. Excess ethylene is frequently introduced (e.g., at 1 atm) to drive the equilibrium forward by removing homodimer byproducts, particularly when terminal alkenes are involved, enhancing yields up to 90% in compatible systems. A representative synthetic application is the formation of disubstituted alkenes from allyl compounds, such as the cross-metathesis of allyl acetate with 1-hexene, yielding (E)-oct-2-enyl acetate in good yield and E-selectivity under mild conditions with second-generation Grubbs catalysts.

Ring-Closing and Ring-Opening Metathesis

Ring-closing metathesis (RCM) is an intramolecular variant of olefin metathesis that converts dienes into cyclic alkenes by forming a new carbon-carbon double bond within the molecule while releasing ethylene as a byproduct. The general reaction can be represented as:
CH2=CH(CH2)nCH=CH2cyclo(CH2)nCH=CH+CH2=CH2
\mathrm{CH_2=CH-(CH_2)_n-CH=CH_2 \rightarrow cyclo-(-CH_2)_n-CH=CH- + CH_2=CH_2}
CH2=CH(CH2)nCH=CH2cyclo(CH2)nCH=CH+CH2=CH2
where $ n $ determines the ring size formed. This process is particularly effective for forming five- to seven-membered rings, with six-membered rings being the most favorable due to their minimal ring strain energy of approximately 0 kcal/mol, compared to 6.5 kcal/mol for five-membered rings and 6.3 kcal/mol for seven-membered rings. Larger rings (eight- or nine-membered) are more challenging and often require additional conformational constraints to favor cyclization over competing intermolecular reactions.
The thermodynamic driving force for RCM primarily arises from the entropy gain associated with the release of gaseous ethylene, which shifts the equilibrium toward the cyclic product. However, challenges include side reactions such as oligomerization, which can be mitigated by conducting the reaction at low substrate concentrations to promote intramolecular closure. Additionally, for larger rings incorporating heteroatoms like nitrogen or oxygen, chelation effects can deactivate the metal catalyst, reducing efficiency in macrocycle formation. Cross-metathesis serves as a competing intermolecular pathway that must be minimized to achieve high selectivity in RCM.
Ring-opening metathesis (ROM) involves the catalytic cleavage of a cyclic alkene to generate reactive olefin species, often driven by the relief of ring strain. Strained cycloalkenes such as norbornene or cyclooctene are particularly reactive substrates; for example, norbornene undergoes ring opening to form a linear diene intermediate. Under controlled conditions, this process yields oligomers or linear polymers, with the reaction's feasibility enhanced by the substantial strain energy in these ringsnorbornene has a strain energy of about 18 kcal/mol, while cyclooctene possesses around 13.8 kcal/mol.
Even more strained systems, like cyclopropenes, exhibit exceptional reactivity in ROM due to their high ring strain energy of approximately 55 kcal/mol, which provides a strong thermodynamic incentive for ring cleavage. The general equation for ROM of a strained cycloalkene, such as cyclopropene, illustrates the transformation to an open-chain diene:
(CH2)mC=CH2(cyclic)CH2=CH(CH2)mCH=CH2
\mathrm{(CH_2)_mC=CH_2 \ (cyclic) \rightarrow CH_2=CH-(CH_2)_m-CH=CH_2}
(CH2)mC=CH2(cyclic)CH2=CH(CH2)mCH=CH2
where $ m $ reflects the ring's substitution pattern. This strain relief dominates the reaction energetics, enabling ROM even under mild conditions.

Olefin Metathesis Polymerization

Olefin metathesis polymerization refers to chain-growth processes that leverage olefin metathesis reactions to assemble macromolecules from monomers containing one or more alkene functionalities. Two key variants are ring-opening metathesis polymerization (ROMP) and acyclic diene metathesis (ADMET) polymerization, which enable the synthesis of polymers with precisely controlled architectures and functionalities.
Ring-opening metathesis polymerization (ROMP) involves the conversion of strained cyclic alkenes into linear or branched polymers known as polyalkenamers through repeated insertion of the monomer into a metal-carbene chain end. The process is driven by the relief of ring strain in monomers such as norbornene, where the metal carbene catalyst initiates by coordinating to the double bond, followed by cycloaddition and subsequent cycloreversion to form a new carbene at the chain terminus. This propagation step repeats, incorporating multiple monomer units while preserving the double bond in the backbone.
For norbornene as a representative example, the polymerization yields polynorbornene, a polyalkenamer with the repeating unit derived from the opened ring. The general process can be represented as:

Acyclic diene metathesis (ADMET) polymerization, in contrast, proceeds via the self-metathesis of ,-dienes under equilibrium conditions, producing unsaturated polymers and evolving ethylene as a byproduct. This step-growth-like process requires removal of ethylene to drive the reaction forward, typically under vacuum or inert gas flow. A typical equation for an ,-diene monomer such as 1,9-decadiene is:
nCHX2=CH(CHX2)X6CH=CHX2Xn+(n1)CHX2=CHX2

yielding a polymer with internal trans double bonds. Early developments using tungsten-based catalysts demonstrated this for various dienes.
In both ROMP and ADMET, polymer properties such as molecular weight are controlled by the monomer-to-catalyst ratio; for instance, in living ROMP systems, higher ratios yield higher molecular weights with narrow polydispersity indices (PDI < 1.1), enabling precise tuning from oligomers to high polymers. Tacticity, influenced by catalyst selectionsuch as molybdenum initiators for stereoregular insertionaffects material strength, with isotactic configurations enhancing crystallinity and mechanical properties in polynorbornenes.
ROMP exhibits living polymerization characteristics when using well-defined initiators like those developed by Schrock and Grubbs, featuring fast initiation, minimal termination or transfer, and linear growth kinetics, which allow for block copolymer synthesis and end-group functionalization. Ruthenium-based catalysts in ROMP provide exceptional tolerance to polar functional groups, such as esters, amides, and alcohols, facilitating the incorporation of bioactive moieties without catalyst deactivation.

Mechanism

Carbene Mechanism Overview

The Chauvin mechanism describes olefin metathesis as a catalytic process involving metal carbene intermediates, where a transition metal alkylidene (M=CR) reacts with an alkene substrate. The key step is a cycloaddition between the metal carbene and the coordinated alkene, forming a four-membered metallacyclobutane intermediate. This intermediate then undergoes cycloreversion, breaking in an alternative manner to generate a new metal carbene and a new alkene product.
A simplified representation of the catalytic cycle is shown below for the self-metathesis of 2-butene, where the initial carbene M=CH reacts with RCH=CHR (R = CH):
M=CHX2+RCH=CHRcycloreversionM=CHR+CHX2=CHR
\begin{align*}
&\ce{M=CH2 + RCH=CHR -> cycloaddition ] } \\
&\ce{ -> M=CHR + CH2=CHR}
\end{align*}
M=CHX2+RCH=CHRcycloreversionM=CHR+CHX2=CHR
This cycle regenerates the active metal carbene species, allowing the reaction to proceed catalytically, and the overall process is reversible due to the equilibrium between reactants and products.
Experimental evidence supporting the involvement of carbene intermediates includes isotope labeling studies, such as those using stereospecifically labeled 2-butene, which demonstrated non-pairwise exchange of alkylidene groups consistent with the metallacyclobutane pathway rather than a direct olefin dimerization. Additionally, spectroscopic characterization, particularly H and C NMR, has confirmed the presence of metal carbene species, with the carbene carbon resonances typically appearing in the 200300 ppm range for high-oxidation-state alkylidenes.

Initiation and Propagation Steps

The initiation step in olefin metathesis begins with the activation of the precatalyst to generate the active metal-carbene species. In ruthenium-based systems, this typically occurs through dissociative substitution, where a phosphine ligand (L) dissociates from the precatalyst, forming a 14-electron active species that coordinates to the alkene substrate. For example, the process can be represented as:
M(L)2(=CHR)M(L)(=CHR)+L
\text{M(L)}_2(=\text{CHR}) \rightarrow \text{M(L)}(=\text{CHR}) + \text{L}
M(L)2(=CHR)M(L)(=CHR)+L
This dissociation is rate-determining for first-generation Grubbs catalysts, with rates around 9.6 s at 353 K, while second-generation catalysts with N-heterocyclic carbene (NHC) ligands exhibit slower initiation due to stronger binding (e.g., 0.13 s under similar conditions). In Hoveyda-type catalysts, initiation proceeds via ligand exchange rather than simple dissociation, involving reversible coordination of the alkene to the precatalyst.
Propagation involves the repeated cycloaddition of the metal-carbene with an alkene to form a metallacyclobutane intermediate, followed by cycloreversion to exchange carbene substituents and regenerate the active species. The metallacyclobutane adopts a kite-shaped, bottom-bound geometry, with the alkene binding trans to the ancillary ligand, facilitating selective E/Z product formation in some cases. Beta-hydride elimination is largely avoided in well-designed ruthenium systems, particularly those stabilized by NHC ligands, which prevent hydride formation from the metallacyclobutane and maintain catalytic activity. In non-living polymerizations, chain transfer occurs through secondary metathesis of the growing chain with monomer or internal alkenes, leading to broader molecular weight distributions.
The rate of propagation depends on alkene substitution, with electron-poor alkenes such as acrylates accelerating the reaction due to enhanced coordination to the electrophilic metal center. Typical turnover frequencies for ruthenium catalysts range from 10 to 10 h, reflecting efficient cycling under optimal conditions. Termination is uncommon but can arise from bimolecular coupling of carbene species, forming inactive dinuclear ruthenium complexes, or carbene dimerization, particularly with methylidene intermediates.

Catalysts

Early and Molybdenum-Based Catalysts

The early development of olefin metathesis catalysts in the 1960s relied on heterogeneous systems, primarily supported metal oxides that enabled the disproportionation of olefins under industrial conditions. One of the first reported catalysts was molybdenum oxide on silica-alumina (MoO3/SiO2-Al2O3), which facilitated the metathesis of propylene at elevated temperatures around 300C, converting two molecules of propylene into ethylene and 2-butene. This reaction, observed by Banks and Bailey at Phillips Petroleum, marked a significant breakthrough in understanding olefin redistribution, though the catalyst required high temperatures and was limited to non-polar substrates.
Shortly thereafter, rhenium-based heterogeneous catalysts, such as Re2O7 supported on alumina (Re2O7/Al2O3), emerged as more active systems for propylene metathesis at lower temperatures (around 25100C), offering improved efficiency for the same transformation:
2CHX3CH=CHX2CHX2=CHX2+CHX3CH=CHCHX3
2 \ \ce{CH3-CH=CH2 CH2=CH2 + CH3-CH=CH-CH3}
2CHX3CH=CHX2CHX2=CHX2+CHX3CH=CHCHX3

These catalysts, developed by Calderon and coworkers at Goodyear, demonstrated the potential for metathesis in petrochemical processes but suffered from low active site densities and sensitivity to poisons, restricting their scope to simple alkenes.
Transitioning to homogeneous catalysis in the 1980s and early 1990s, molybdenum-based systems, particularly Schrock-type imido-alkylidene complexes of the general formula Mo(=CHR)(NAr)(OR')2 (where Ar is typically 2,6-diisopropylphenyl and R' is an alkoxy group like OCMe(CF3)2), provided well-defined initiators for olefin metathesis. These high-oxidation-state Mo(VI) complexes, first synthesized by Schrock and coworkers, exhibited exceptional activity for ring-opening metathesis polymerization (ROMP) of strained cyclic olefins like norbornene, achieving turnover numbers on the order of 10^4 to 10^5.
These molybdenum catalysts preferentially formed E-alkenes in cross-metathesis reactions and were highly effective for polymerizing electron-rich or strained substrates, fitting the carbene mechanism through cycloadditions. However, their Lewis acidity led to intolerance of polar functional groups, such as carbonyls, which coordinate to the metal and deactivate the catalyst.
A major limitation of these early and molybdenum-based catalysts was their extreme sensitivity to air and moisture, necessitating rigorous anaerobic and anhydrous conditions for handling and use, which constrained their practical applicability compared to more robust later developments. Additionally, the narrow substrate scopefavoring non-functionalized or strained olefinshighlighted the need for advancements in catalyst stability and tolerance.

Ruthenium-Based Catalysts

Ruthenium-based catalysts represent a major advancement in olefin metathesis due to their enhanced stability and tolerance toward functional groups compared to earlier systems. The first-generation Grubbs catalyst, with the structure $ \ce{RuCl2(PCy3)2(=CHPh)} $, was introduced in 1992 and marked a significant step forward in catalyst design. This complex initiates metathesis through the dissociation of one tricyclohexylphosphine (PCy) ligand, generating the active 14-electron species:
(PCyX3)X2ClX2Ru=CHPh(PCyX3)ClX2Ru=CHPh+PCyX3
\ce{(PCy3)2Cl2Ru=CHPh (PCy3)Cl2Ru=CHPh + PCy3}
(PCyX3)X2ClX2Ru=CHPh(PCyX3)ClX2Ru=CHPh+PCyX3
This process allows the catalyst to engage with olefins effectively, enabling applications in ring-closing metathesis and other transformations under mild conditions. The catalyst's air stability and ease of synthesis facilitated its commercial adoption.
The second-generation Grubbs catalysts, developed in 1999, incorporated N-heterocyclic carbene (NHC) ligands such as 1,3-bis(2,4,6-trimethylphenyl)-4,5-dihydroimidazol-2-ylidene (SIMes) to replace one PCy ligand. The general structure is $ \ce{RuCl2(SIMes)(PCy3)(=CHPh)} $. These modifications increase electron donation and steric bulk, accelerating initiation and propagation rates by up to 10-fold relative to the first generation while improving thermal stability and substrate tolerance. The NHC ligands enhance the catalyst's performance in challenging environments, such as those with polar functional groups.
Hoveyda-Grubbs catalysts, introduced in 1999, feature a chelating benzylidene ligand, typically with an o-isopropoxy group (e.g., $ \ce{RuCl2(SIMes)(=CH-o-OiPrC6H4)} $ for the second-generation variant). This design promotes recyclability by allowing the catalyst to be recovered via precipitation or chromatography after reaction, with yields often exceeding 90% in multiple cycles. The chelate stabilizes the complex, reducing phosphine dissociation issues and enabling use in biphasic or heterogeneous systems. These catalysts maintain high activity for cross-metathesis and ring-closing reactions while offering improved handling.
Post-2010 developments have focused on enhancing stereoselectivity, particularly for Z-selective olefin metathesis. Catalysts incorporating stereogenic or bulky ligands, such as those developed by the Grela group, achieve high Z-selectivity (often >90%) through restricted rotation and steric control in the transition state. For instance, a 2013 ruthenium complex with a modified NHC and chelating benzylidene demonstrated efficient Z-selective cross-metathesis of terminal olefins. These advances stem from ligand tuning to favor the syn-addition pathway, broadening applications in natural product synthesis.

Recent Catalyst Developments

Since 2015, developments in olefin metathesis catalysts have emphasized enhanced stereocontrol, recyclability, and sustainability, building on ruthenium foundations to address limitations in selectivity and environmental impact. Key innovations include modifications for Z-selectivity, immobilization strategies for catalyst recovery, and exploration of earth-abundant metals or light-activated systems to reduce reliance on precious metals. These advances have enabled applications in complex molecule synthesis and polymer production with improved efficiency and lower waste.
Z-selective catalysts have seen significant progress through ruthenium complexes featuring bulky N-heterocyclic carbene (NHC) ligands, such as those with cycloadamantyl substituents, which sterically hinder E-isomer formation to achieve greater than 98% Z-selectivity in cross-metathesis (CM) and ring-closing metathesis (RCM). Cyclometalated ruthenium variants, incorporating ligands like catechothiolates or thiophenolates, further enhance Z-control by modulating electronic and steric effects, demonstrating high fidelity in CM of terminal olefins and RCM of dienes to form medium-sized rings with Z-purities exceeding 95%. Dithiolate-based ruthenium catalysts, often with bidentate sulfur ligands, provide robust Z-stereoretention in challenging substrates, including enol ethers, with selectivities above 98% and turnover numbers up to 500 in aqueous media. These systems outperform earlier generations in stereospecificity for natural product synthesis.
Immobilization techniques have advanced to facilitate catalyst reuse and reduce ruthenium contamination in products. Polymer-supported ruthenium catalysts, tethered via NHC or Hoveyda-type ligands to polystyrene resins, enable up to five recycling cycles in RCM reactions with minimal activity loss, maintaining yields above 80%. Fluorous-tagged variants, incorporating perfluoroalkyl chains on the benzylidene ligand, allow phase separation in fluorous solvents for efficient recovery, achieving over 90% catalyst retrieval in CM processes conducted in the 2020s. Metal-organic framework (MOF)-encapsulated ruthenium complexes, such as those within UiO-66 frameworks, prevent bimolecular decomposition while supporting stereoselective metathesis, with recyclability exceeding ten cycles and turnover numbers around 200. Ionic liquid-immobilized systems, using noncovalent interactions with imidazolium supports, have shown promise for continuous-flow applications, preserving activity over multiple runs in ROMP.
Sustainable alternatives have emerged to minimize precious metal use, including experimental iron-based carbenes that exhibit low but measurable activity in olefin metathesis. Three-coordinate iron(II) complexes with bis(imino)pyridine ligands catalyze ring-opening metathesis polymerization (ROMP) of norbornene derivatives, achieving molecular weight control and yields up to 70% under mild conditions, though turnover numbers remain below 100 due to competing side reactions. Osmium carbenes, while historically limited, have seen renewed interest in hybrid forms but display low activity compared to ruthenium analogs. Photocatalyst-initiated systems, such as ruthenium complexes activated by deep red to near-infrared light (600800 nm), enable spatiotemporal control in metathesis, with initiation rates improved by factors of 10 and applications in light-mediated CM without thermal activation.
Biocatalytic hybrids represent a growing trend for aqueous metathesis, combining ruthenium catalysts with protein scaffolds for enhanced stability and selectivity. De novo designed -barrel proteins conjugated to Grubbs-Hoveyda catalysts form artificial metalloenzymes that catalyze RCM in water with turnover numbers exceeding 1,000 and Z-selectivities up to 90%, outperforming free catalysts in biocompatibility. These biohybrids, evolved via directed mutation, achieve conversions over 95% in CM of unprotected peptides, minimizing organic solvent use.
In the 2020s, artificial intelligence has accelerated ligand design for metathesis catalysts through machine learning-guided de novo evolution of NHC structures. Computational models predict optimal steric and electronic parameters, yielding ruthenium variants with improved initiation rates and turnover numbers up to 640 in challenging CM reactions. This approach has identified novel ligands enhancing Z-selectivity beyond 98% while reducing synthesis steps.

History

Initial Discoveries

Olefin metathesis was first observed in the early 1950s during industrial research at Phillips Petroleum Company, where chemists investigating olefin polymerization unexpectedly discovered the disproportionation of propylene into ethylene and 2-butene using a heterogeneous molybdenum oxide on silica-alumina catalyst. This serendipitous finding, stemming from experiments aimed at polymer production, marked the initial empirical recognition of the reaction, with related patents filed in the early 1960s based on work from the late 1950s. This led to the Phillips Triolefin Process, operational from 1966 to 1972, which commercially converted propylene to ethylene and 2-butene.
In the 1960s, academic interest grew as researchers explored the reaction's scope with heterogeneous catalysts. Robert L. Banks and Grant C. Bailey at Phillips Petroleum published a seminal paper in 1964 describing the disproportionation of linear olefins, such as propene and butene, into homologs using molybdenum-based catalysts, and they proposed a mechanism involving a cyclobutane intermediate. Independently, Nissim Calderon and colleagues at Goodyear Tire & Rubber conducted detailed studies on the interconversion of olefins over tungsten-based heterogeneous catalysts, coining the term "olefin metathesis" in 1967 to describe the skeletal reorganization of unsaturated hydrocarbons.
By the 1970s, efforts to elucidate the reaction mechanism advanced significantly. In 1971, Yves Chauvin and Jean-Louis Hrisson at the French Petroleum Institute proposed a chain-growth mechanism involving metal carbene (alkylidene) intermediates, drawing an analogy to Ziegler-Natta polymerization, where the carbene initiates olefin exchange through metallacyclobutane formation. This proposal shifted understanding from pairwise exchanges to a catalytic cycle, though early studies remained limited by the absence of well-defined homogeneous catalysts, confining research primarily to simple terminal olefins like propene and butene under heterogeneous conditions.

Key Milestones and Nobel Recognition

In the 1980s, the development of well-defined transition metal catalysts marked a pivotal advancement in olefin metathesis, transitioning from ill-defined heterogeneous systems to precise homogeneous ones. In 1990, Richard R. Schrock synthesized the first molybdenum imido-alkylidene complexes, such as Mo(NAr)(=CHCMe2_22Ph)(OTf)2_22(dme), which demonstrated high activity in ring-opening metathesis polymerization (ROMP) and cross-metathesis reactions, enabling better control over reaction outcomes and broader substrate scope. These catalysts laid the foundation for modern metathesis by confirming the carbene mechanism proposed earlier and facilitating academic and industrial exploration of the reaction.
The 1990s ushered in the Grubbs era, with ruthenium-based carbenes offering superior air stability and tolerance to polar functional groups compared to molybdenum systems. In 1992, Robert H. Grubbs reported the first well-defined ruthenium alkylidene complex, RuCl2_22(=CHPh)(PPh3_33)3_33, prepared from RuCl2_22(PPh3_33)4_44 and diphenylcyclopropene, which initiated ROMP of strained cycloolefins like norbornene. Well-defined catalysts like these enabled expanded commercialization and broader applications; for instance, initial ROMP-based products such as Norsorex, a high-molecular-weight polynorbornene introduced in 1976 using early catalysts and scaled up in the 1980s for applications in adhesives and impact modifiers, benefited from improved control, while polyoctenamer (Vestenamer), produced via ROMP of cyclooctene, entered commercial production in the early 1970s and gained traction in tire compounding during the 1980s due to its crystalline properties.
The transformative impact of these innovations was recognized in 2005, when the Nobel Prize in Chemistry was awarded jointly to Yves Chauvin, Robert H. Grubbs, and Richard R. Schrock "for the development of the metathesis method in organic synthesis." Chauvin's foundational carbene mechanism (1971), combined with Schrock's high-oxidation-state catalysts and Grubbs's robust ruthenium variants, revolutionized synthetic efficiency, enabling greener processes that minimize steps, waste, and hazardous reagents in pharmaceutical and materials production.
Post-2005, olefin metathesis expanded into challenging environments, with the 2010s witnessing developments in aqueous-compatible catalysts, such as PEG-tagged ruthenium complexes, allowing metathesis in water for bioconjugation and polymer synthesis without organic solvents. Stereoselective variants emerged, including Hoveyda-Grubbs type II catalysts modified with cyclic alkyl amino carbenes to achieve >95% Z-selectivity in cross-metathesis, facilitating access to bioactive natural products. In the 2020s, sustainability has driven innovations like ethenolysis of renewable fatty acids using recyclable ruthenium catalysts, converting plant oils into platform chemicals with high atom economy and reduced energy input.

Applications

Synthetic Organic Chemistry

Olefin metathesis has become a cornerstone in synthetic organic chemistry for constructing complex carbon skeletons, particularly in the assembly of bioactive molecules, due to its ability to form carbon-carbon double bonds under mild conditions that accommodate a wide array of functional groups. This functional group tolerance allows for selective transformations even in the presence of sensitive moieties like alcohols, ethers, and heterocycles, enabling efficient late-stage modifications in multistep syntheses. The reaction's versatility stems from well-defined catalysts that operate at ambient temperatures, minimizing side reactions and preserving stereochemistry in target-oriented routes.
In natural product synthesis, ring-closing metathesis (RCM) has proven invaluable for forming macrocyclic rings, as exemplified in the 1997 total syntheses of epothilones A and B by Nicolaou and coworkers, where RCM efficiently closed the 16-membered lactone core from a linear precursor, providing access to these microtubule-stabilizing antitumor agents in high yield. Similarly, cross-metathesis (CM) facilitates the installation of stereodefined alkenes in taxol analogs; for instance, in the 2006 synthesis of simplified paclitaxel derivatives by Georg and colleagues, Grubbs-catalyzed RCM formed bridged analogs with moderate cytotoxicity.
Pharmaceutical applications highlight olefin metathesis's efficiency in drug candidate preparation, notably in the synthesis of HIV-1 protease inhibitors. In a 2005 structure-based design effort by Ghosh and team, RCM using Grubbs' catalyst formed macrocyclic scaffolds for nonpeptidic inhibitors while maintaining potent inhibitory activity (Ki < 10 nM). This approach not only shortened routes but also improved scalability for lead optimization in antiviral therapies.
More recent case studies demonstrate olefin metathesis in enhancing peptide therapeutics through stapling, where RCM constrains secondary structures to boost proteolytic stability and cellular uptake. For example, in 2016 work by Vantourout et al., orthogonal RCM and alkyne metathesis on solid phase generated bicyclic peptides targeting protein-protein interactions for potential anticancer applications. Such modifications have since been applied in 2023 to -hairpin antimicrobial peptides, where hydrocarbon stapling via metathesis improved resistance to enzymatic degradation by over 10-fold, enabling effective treatment of Gram-negative infections.

Polymer Synthesis

Ring-opening metathesis polymerization (ROMP) of strained cyclic olefins, such as norbornene, enables the synthesis of well-defined polymers with tailored properties for advanced materials. Polynorbornene, produced via ROMP, exhibits high thermal stability, extraordinary optical transparency, and a high refractive index, making it suitable for optical applications including lenses. Commercialized in the 1990s, polynorbornene-based materials have been employed in high-performance optical components due to these attributes. Additionally, certain ROMP-derived polynorbornenes demonstrate glass transition temperatures exceeding 100C, such as 124C in variants synthesized with nickel(II) catalysts, contributing to their robustness in demanding environments.
Acyclic diene metathesis (ADMET) polymerization provides an alternative route to linear polymers with precise control over unsaturation levels, particularly useful for elastomer production. For instance, ADMET of 1,9-decadiene yields linear polyoctenamers featuring trans double bonds in the backbone, which can be tuned for enhanced elasticity and mechanical properties. These materials mimic traditional polyoctenamers but offer superior control over microstructure, enabling applications in flexible, durable elastomers with defined cross-linking potential via the retained unsaturations.
Living ROMP facilitates the preparation of block copolymers that self-assemble into nanostructures, a development prominent in the 2000s. Sequential addition of norbornene-based monomers in living ROMP produces amphiphilic block copolymers, such as those combining poly(norbornene) with polystyrene or fullerene-functionalized segments, which form ordered morphologies like micelles or lamellae for photonic and sensing applications. These self-assembling structures, with domain spacings tunable from tens to hundreds of nanometers, enable advanced materials like biosensors and switchable photonic crystals.
In recent years, functionalized norbornene monomers have been utilized in ROMP to create polymers for biomedical applications, including drug delivery systems. For example, ROMP of norbornene-derived block copolymers incorporating carboxylate or related functional groups yields responsive nanoparticles that encapsulate and release therapeutics like doxorubicin in a stimuli-sensitive manner, such as under reactive oxygen species. These 2020s advancements highlight the versatility of ROMP in producing biocompatible, high-molecular-weight polyolefins with pendant functionalities for targeted delivery and reduced toxicity.

Industrial and Commercial Uses

Olefin metathesis plays a pivotal role in the production of bulk chemicals through processes like the Shell Higher Olefin Process (SHOP), which converts ethylene into linear alpha-olefins (LAOs) essential for detergents, lubricants, and plasticizers. In SHOP, ethylene is oligomerized to form internal olefins, which undergo isomerization and subsequent cross-metathesis with ethylene to yield desired LAOs such as 1-hexene, 1-octene, and 1-decene. This integrated process, operational since the 1970s, achieves high selectivity and efficiency using nickel-based oligomerization catalysts combined with heterogeneous metathesis catalysts like supported tungsten oxides. Shell's Geismar facility alone produces over 1.3 million tonnes of alpha-olefins annually (as of 2019), contributing significantly to the global LAO supply exceeding 3 million tonnes per year.
In polymer synthesis for industrial applications, metathesis enables the commercial production of specialty rubbers like Norsorex (trans-polynorbornene) via ring-opening metathesis polymerization (ROMP) of norbornene. Norsorex, developed by CdF Chimie and commercialized since the 1970s, offers exceptional vibration damping and grip properties, making it ideal for tire treads, anti-vibration mounts in automotive and railway systems, and acoustic insulation. Its high molecular weight and trans double-bond configuration provide superior mechanical resilience compared to traditional elastomers. Similarly, Vestenamer (polyoctenamer), produced by Evonik (formerly Hls) through ROMP of cyclooctene, serves as a versatile additive in rubber blends, enhancing processability, compatibility between polar and non-polar elastomers, and recyclability of waste rubber for molded goods and conveyor belts. Vestenamer's linear and cyclic structure imparts flexibility and improves extrusion efficiency in tire manufacturing and other rubber products.
Recent advancements in the 2020s have expanded metathesis into bio-based applications, particularly cross-metathesis of fatty acids and vegetable oils for oleochemicals. This involves ethenolysis or self-metathesis of unsaturated fatty acid esters like methyl oleate to produce terminal olefins and diesters, which serve as precursors for bio-surfactants, lubricants, and polymers from renewable feedstocks. For instance, ruthenium-catalyzed cross-metathesis of oleic acid derivatives yields 1-decene and other alpha-olefins for surfactant production, offering a sustainable alternative to petrochemical routes with up to 90% selectivity under mild conditions. These developments align with growing demand for green chemistry, with pilot-scale processes demonstrating viability for industrial surfactants in personal care and detergents.
Economically, olefin metathesis has driven a burgeoning market, with the global metathesis catalyst sector projected to reach approximately USD 950 million by 2033 (as of 2024 estimates), fueled by broader applications in petrochemicals and renewables. Advancements in ruthenium-based catalysts since the early 2000s, including second-generation Grubbs and Hoveyda-Grubbs variants, have enhanced stability and activity, reducing operational costs and enabling scalable processes that support a multi-billion-dollar olefins industry. The economic impact is evident in SHOP's contribution to LAO markets valued at over USD 5 billion annually, underscoring metathesis's role in efficient, high-volume chemical manufacturing.
