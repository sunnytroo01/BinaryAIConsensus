 Follies is a musical with music and lyrics by Stephen Sondheim and book by James Goldman, which premiered on Broadway on April 4, 1971, at the Winter Garden Theatre. Set in 1971 at the decaying Weismann Theatre on the eve of its demolition, the story centers on a reunion of former showgirls from the fictional "Weismann's Follies," a chorus line revue inspired by the real-life Ziegfeld Follies, as they confront past regrets and unfulfilled dreams through the lens of two middle-aged couples: Sally and Buddy, and Phyllis and Ben.
Originally conceived as The Girls Upstairs, the production evolved into Follies under the direction of Harold Prince and Michael Bennett, featuring a score that blends original songs with pastiche numbers evoking 1920s and 1930s show tunes. Notable songs include "I'm Still Here," a resilient anthem performed by the character Carlotta; "Losing My Mind," a poignant lament by Sally; "Broadway Baby," showcasing ambition; and "Could I Leave You?," a sharp marital confrontation. The narrative culminates in a surreal "Loveland" sequence where characters don elaborate costumes to act out their idealized fantasies, highlighting themes of nostalgia, disillusionment, and the passage of time.
The original production starred Alexis Smith as Phyllis, Dorothy Collins as Sally, John McMartin as Ben, and Gene Nelson as Buddy, running for 522 performances despite mixed critical reception that praised its opulence and score but critiqued its emotional tone. At the 1972 Tony Awards, Follies received 11 nominations and won seven, including Best Original Score for Sondheim, Best Actress in a Musical for Smith, Best Direction for Prince and Bennett, Best Choreography for Bennett, Best Scenic Design for Boris Aronson, Best Costume Design for Florence Klotz, and Best Lighting Design for Tharon Musser, though it lost Best Musical to Two Gentlemen of Verona. It also earned the New York Drama Critics' Circle Award for Best Musical.
Despite its initial financial loss, Follies has achieved enduring popularity, inspiring acclaimed revivals such as the 1985 concert version at Lincoln Center, the 2011 Broadway production directed by Eric Schaeffer, and the 2017 staging at London's National Theatre. The musical is celebrated for its innovative structure, sophisticated score, and exploration of midlife crisis in the context of show business, cementing its status as a cornerstone of the American musical theater canon.

Development and Background

Conception and Creation

James Goldman first conceived the idea for Follies in 1965 as a musical centered on a backstage reunion of former showgirls gathering at their old theater prior to its demolition.
By 1970, Goldman partnered with Stephen Sondheim, who would provide the music and lyrics, and Harold Prince, serving as director and producer, to develop the project further. The creative team held initial workshops that year, during which they refined the script through multiple revisions, including the key decision to frame the reunion around a fictionalized version of the Ziegfeld Follies to capture the era's extravagant theatrical legacy. A first reading of the evolving script occurred in 1970, marking a pivotal step in solidifying the narrative structure.
The production encountered substantial budget hurdles, with initial estimates of $700,000 ballooning to nearly $800,000 due to the elaborate sets, costumes, and large cast required to evoke the show's opulent pastiche styleone of the highest capitalization figures for a Broadway musical at the time. Despite these challenges, funding was assembled primarily under Prince's leadership, enabling the project to proceed. Rehearsals commenced in early 1971, building toward the Broadway premiere.

Composition and Influences

Stephen Sondheim's score for Follies masterfully integrates pastiche songs evoking the 1920s and 1930s Broadway era with contemporary character-driven pieces, creating a layered musical landscape that contrasts nostalgia with present-day disillusionment. The pastiche elements draw directly from composers such as George Gershwin, Jerome Kern, Irving Berlin, and Cole Porter, incorporating their rhythmic syncopations, melodic contours, and harmonic progressions to homage the Ziegfeld Follies style. For instance, songs like "Broadway Baby" emulate Kern's lilting waltzes and Berlin's upbeat tempos, while "I'm Still Here" nods to Harold Arlen's torch song sensibilities with its bluesy resilience and dramatic swells.
This stylistic fusion supports the show's innovative hybrid structure, blending the narrative depth of a traditional book musical with the episodic, performative flair of a revue. Unlike pure revues that prioritize disconnected numbers, Follies uses pastiche to mirror characters' youthful illusions and faded dreams, transitioning seamlessly into modern solos that expose their emotional fracturessuch as the ghostly younger selves performing alongside their aged counterparts in sequences like "Beautiful Girls" and "Who's That Woman?" This "concept musical" approach, pioneered by Sondheim and director Harold Prince, emphasizes thematic cohesion over linear plot, with the revue format amplifying the collective regret of the reunion.
Sondheim's composition process involved iterative revisions to heighten emotional resonance, particularly in response to out-of-town tryouts. The score was largely completed by late 1970, allowing rehearsals to begin in early 1971, but adjustments continued through the Boston tryout from February 20 to March 20, 1971. Notably, "I'm Still Here" was added during this phase to replace "Can That Boy Foxtrot," tailored to Yvonne De Carlo's dramatic delivery and providing a defiant counterpoint to Carlotta's Hollywood survivor persona. Further tweaks refined the pastiche-modern interplay, ensuring the music underscored the characters' psychological turmoil without overwhelming the narrative.
External influences from film noir and psychological drama shaped the score's tonal ambiguity, infusing it with shadowy introspection akin to 1940s cinema's exploration of regret and moral decay. Book writer James Goldman's input was pivotal, guiding Sondheim to align musical arcs with the couples' unraveling relationshipsemphasizing dissonant harmonies in pieces like "Too Many Mornings" to evoke Ben and Sally's fractured illusions. This collaboration, rooted in Goldman's vision of midlife entropy, resulted in a score finalized with minor adjustments following the tryouts, before the Broadway opening on April 4, 1971.

Synopsis

Plot Summary

Follies is set in 1971 at the decaying Weismann Theatre in New York City, which is about to be demolished to make way for a parking lot. Broadway impresario Dimitri Weismann hosts a reunion for the former performers of his Ziegfeld-inspired revue, the Weismann Follies, which ran from the 1910s through the 1940s. The evening brings together aging showgirls, chorines, and supporting cast members, many in their 50s to 80s, who arrive amid a surreal atmosphere where ghostly apparitions of their younger selves silently reenact past performances.
Among the guests are two central couples whose intertwined histories drive the drama: Sally Durant Plummer and her husband Buddy, a traveling salesman; and Phyllis Rogers Stone, a former showgirl turned socialite, and her husband Ben, a failed political candidate. Sally, who once dreamed of a life with Benher backstage admirer and brief lover before he chose Phyllisarrives hoping to rekindle their romance, while Buddy masks his dissatisfaction with a mistress and unfulfilled ambitions. Phyllis and Ben, meanwhile, navigate a marriage strained by Ben's regrets over abandoning his ideals and Sally, and Phyllis's bitterness at being sidelined for his career. As the reunion unfolds in Act I, the guests mingle, perform nostalgic pastiche numbers evoking the era's glamour (such as "Broadway Baby" and "I'm Still Here"), and share awkward conversations that peel back layers of pretense, revealing midlife disillusionments and unresolved tensions.
Tensions escalate through intimate confrontations: Sally and Ben duet on "Too Many Mornings," lamenting lost possibilities; Phyllis lashes out at Ben in "Could I Leave You?" questioning their union; and Buddy confesses his wandering heart in "The Right Girl." The act builds to chaotic revelations, including Ben's drunken advances toward Sally (witnessed by Buddy) and Buddy's confession of his infidelity to Sally, shattering the evening's fragile camaraderie.
In Act II, the production transitions into the surreal "Loveland" sequence, a dreamlike Follies-style revue where the principal characters don opulent costumes and interact with their idealized younger selves to explore their inner fantasies and regrets. Sally imagines a blissful life with Ben in "Losing My Mind"; Buddy envisions endless youthful pursuits in "The God-Why-Don't-You-Love-Me Blues"; Phyllis asserts her resilience through "The Story of Lucy and Jessie"; and Ben attempts a grand, hollow paean to optimism in "Live, Laugh, Love," which collapses into a raw admission of failure. These numbers blend seamlessly with the present, forcing each character to confront the gap between illusion and reality.
As dawn breaks, the illusions fade, and the couples emerge from the theater transformed yet resigned. Sally and Ben acknowledge their paths have diverged irreparably; Buddy and Phyllis find tentative common ground in shared endurance. The group disperses into the morning light, the ghosts vanishing as the theater's demolition looms, symbolizing the end of an era and a bittersweet acceptance of life's compromises.

Structure and Style

Follies employs a hybrid revue-book format that interweaves contemporary dramatic scenes among the aging performers with spectral reenactments of past follies numbers, creating a concept musical where music, lyrics, dance, and dialogue form an integrated thematic tapestry of nostalgia and disillusionment. This structure blends the episodic, spectacle-driven style of a revue with the character-driven narrative of a book musical, allowing the show to evoke the grandeur of bygone Broadway revues while advancing personal stories of regret and regret.
The production innovates through double-casting, with older actors portraying the present-day characters and younger performers appearing as their youthful "ghosts" or shadows, visually juxtaposing the passage of time and enabling fluid interactions between past and present selves. This technique heightens the emotional duality, as the ensemble's shadow-selves engage in two-way exchanges that underscore themes of memory and loss.
Staging elements further enhance this temporal interplay, particularly Boris Aronson's original set design, which depicts a crumbling, cobweb-draped theater with sliding platforms to evoke decay and faded opulence, symbolizing the ruins of show business glamour. Central to the style are the "mirror" sequences, where reflections and apparitions blur reality and reminiscence, culminating in surreal tableaux that reflect the characters' inner turmoil.
The pacing builds methodically: Act I accumulates layers of nostalgia through ensemble interactions and pastiche numbers reminiscent of 1920s and 1930s revues, establishing a wistful atmosphere without intermission in the original production. Act II shifts to disintegration in the "Loveland" dream ballet sequence, a non-linear fantasia where characters enact exaggerated, grotesque versions of their fantasies amid tinsel and mirrors, fracturing the nostalgic illusion into chaos.
These elements represent stylistic innovations in Sondheim's oeuvre, particularly the use of music to manipulate non-linear timepastiche scores evoke eras while underscoring emotional fragmentationinfluencing subsequent works like A Little Night Music and Sweeney Todd by prioritizing psychological depth over linear storytelling. For instance, the pastiche in numbers like "Broadway Baby" exemplifies how stylistic mimicry propels the dramatic form forward.

Song Analysis

In Stephen Sondheim's Follies, the songs serve as intricate vehicles for character revelation and thematic depth, blending pastiche of early 20th-century musical styles with innovative lyrical and harmonic structures to explore personal disillusionment. Key numbers like "I'm Still Here," "Losing My Mind," and "The Story of Lucy and Jessie" exemplify this approach, using musical forms reminiscent of the Ziegfeld Follies erasuch as torch songs and vaudeville routineswhile delving into the psychological complexities of aging performers confronting regret and fractured identities.
"I'm Still Here," performed by Carlotta Campion, functions as a survival anthem that encapsulates resilience amid Hollywood's fickle fortunes. Its jazz-blues style, characterized by driving rhythms and blues-inflected phrasing, evokes the gritty showbiz underbelly of the 1920s1940s, with lyrics cataloging cultural fads like ukuleles, the WPA, and the Windsor affair to map a century of personal triumphs and setbacks. The ironic tone in lines such as "Good times and bum times, Ive seen them all... But Im here" underscores Carlotta's defiant endurance, masking vulnerability with bravado, and the song's structure builds to an electrifying climax through escalating repetition and harmonic tension.
In contrast, "Losing My Mind," Sally Durant Plummer's torch song, mimics the romantic ballads of the Follies era but infuses them with modern unease through atonal elements and repetitive motifs that mirror her delusional obsession with Ben. The simple, cyclical structurereiterating "I think about you" across verses depicting a monotonous dayhighlights Sally's emotional paralysis, as the melody's subtle dissonances and unresolved phrases reflect her unraveling psyche and denial of a failed marriage. This blend of traditional torch song simplicity with harmonic ambiguity conveys her internal delusion, where past promises haunt the present without resolution.
"The Story of Lucy and Jessie," Buddy's fantasy number reimagined through Phyllis's perspective, employs a vaudeville pastiche with high-energy jazz rhythms and rapid-fire patter to depict his escapist tendencies. The lyrics contrast Lucy (the naive, thrill-seeking younger self) and Jessie (the cynical adult), using Cole Porter-esque internal rhymes and multi-syllabic schemes to propel the narrative of divided loyalties and unfulfilled desires. This structure advances Buddy's psychological fragmentation, portraying his flight from responsibility as a manic duality rooted in era-specific showmanship.
Sondheim's compositional techniques in these songs further enhance their emotional impact, featuring complex rhyme schemes that layer internal and cross-line patterns for rhythmic propulsion, as seen in the triplet-motivic repetitions of "Could I Leave You?" which echo the verbal dexterity of Follies pastiches. Harmonically, he employs dissonance through "effect harmonies" like the "Nostalgia chord" (a dissonant cluster of 0-2-4-7-9), introduced in ensemble numbers and carried into solos to evoke memory's unease, with diagonal dissonances between treble and bass lines creating tension at emotional peaks. These progressions often delay resolution via chromatic pedal points and second-inversion tonics, mirroring unresolved inner conflicts.
Collectively, the songs mirror the Follies era's glamorous facadesdrawing on vaudeville, torch, and revue styleswhile advancing modern psychological insights by exposing the gap between performative personas and authentic selves, as in the characters' confrontations with memory's distortions. This duality promotes vulnerability as a path to growth, transforming nostalgic pastiche into a meditation on regret and self-deception.

Themes and Analysis

Central Themes

Follies delves into the core human experiences of regret, illusion, and aging, using the reunion of former Ziegfeld-style showgirls as a backdrop to examine how the passage of time reshapes personal histories. The musical portrays regret as an inescapable companion to reflection, with characters confronting the consequences of choices made decades earlier in pursuit of glamour and stability. This theme emerges vividly in the interactions among the ensemble, where past aspirations clash with present disillusionment, revealing lives marked by compromise and loss.
A stark contrast between nostalgia and reality permeates the narrative, as the characters are haunted by their youthful ideals embodied by spectral dancers who mirror their former selves on stage. These ghostly figures symbolize the idealized memories that distort the harsh truths of their current existences, warning against the seductive pull of romanticized pasts. The crumbling theater setting reinforces this dichotomy, serving as a physical manifestation of faded glory where nostalgia offers temporary solace but ultimately exacerbates the pain of unfulfilled promises.
The midlife crises of the four central protagonistsSally Durant Plummer, Buddy Plummer, Phyllis Rogers Stone, and Ben Stoneanchor the exploration of failing marriages and unfulfilled dreams, illustrating how accumulated regrets fracture long-term relationships. Sally's desperate clinging to a youthful romance and Buddy's escapist infidelity highlight the emotional turmoil of middle age, while Phyllis and Ben's power struggles expose the erosion of ambition into bitterness. These personal breakdowns are woven into the broader ensemble dynamics, emphasizing how individual disappointments ripple through shared histories.
Gender roles in show business are scrutinized through the lens of the showgirls' exploitation and endurance, depicting women as both celebrated icons and vulnerable commodities in a male-dominated industry. The former performers, many now widows or unemployed, recount lives defined by objectification during their heyday, enduring physical and emotional tolls for fleeting applause. This theme underscores the gendered inequities of the era, where women's value was tied to youth and beauty, leading to post-career marginalization.
Illusion functions as a primary coping mechanism, culminating in the "Loveland" sequencea surreal fantasy realm where characters don elaborate costumes to reenact their idealized selves in a pastiche of 1940s musical numbers. This extended dreamlike interlude allows the protagonists to confront suppressed desires, but its chaotic breakdown reveals the fragility of such escapism, as illusions shatter under the weight of reality. The sequence's structure, blending vaudeville tropes with psychological unraveling, amplifies the theme's tension between fantasy and truth.
Stephen Sondheim's perspective on time and memory, shaped by his immersion in New York theater culture and observations of the Ziegfeld Follies era, infuses the work with a poignant meditation on how recollection warps experience. Drawing from the optimistic yet ephemeral shows of the interwar period, he illustrates memory as a selective force that both preserves and distorts, influenced by his own encounters with songwriters and performers from that time. This philosophical undercurrent ties the themes together, portraying time not as linear but as a haunting loop of what was and what might have been.

Critical Interpretations

Early critical interpretations of Follies in the 1970s highlighted its emotional rawness and unflinching portrayal of personal and cultural decline. Frank Rich, reviewing the pre-Broadway tryout for The Harvard Crimson, described the musical as a poignant elegy linking the characters' relational failures to the demise of the Ziegfeld-era revue tradition, emphasizing its raw depiction of disillusionment and loss. Later queer theory analyses have examined Follies through the lens of gender performance, interpreting the show's fractured marriages and nostalgic pastiches as critiques of heteronormative roles and the performative nature of identity in mid-20th-century American theater.
Biographical studies have uncovered autobiographical elements in Follies, particularly in its exploration of marital discord and regret, which echo Stephen Sondheim's own family dynamics. This reading aligns with broader scholarly views of Sondheim's oeuvre as drawing from his isolated childhood to probe themes of disconnection.
Sondheim's works often share motifs of regret and the inescapability of past choices. Critics have noted similarities in how Follies and other musicals, such as A Little Night Music (1973), dissect the illusions of love and the weight of time, with Follies' chaotic reunion contrasting more whimsical farces while exploring romantic disillusionment.
In modern interpretations, particularly following the 2001 off-Broadway revival, Follies has been reframed through feminist lenses emphasizing female empowerment amid aging and societal obsolescence. The song "I'm Still Here," performed by Carlotta Campion, is frequently analyzed as a defiant anthem of survival and resilience, celebrating women's endurance against patriarchal expectations and personal setbacks in post-war America.
Following Sondheim's death in November 2021, recent analyses (as of 2025) have deepened reflections on Follies' themes of memory and regret, viewing the musical as a prescient meditation on artistic legacy and the trickster nature of recollection amid personal and cultural decline. For instance, post-2021 scholarship highlights how the show's ambiguous endings exemplify Sondheim's ambivalence toward optimism, blending cynicism with acceptance of human frailty.
Scholarly debates persist over the musical's ambiguous ending, which juxtaposes the characters' shattered illusions against the ghosts of their youthful optimism. Some interpreters view it as fundamentally cynical, a stark rejection of romantic redemption in favor of enduring bitterness, while others argue it offers a tempered optimism through acceptance of imperfection, reflecting core themes of compromise and human frailty.

Productions

Original Broadway Production (1971)

The original Broadway production of Follies premiered at the Winter Garden Theatre in New York City, directed by Harold Prince and co-directed by Michael Bennett, with choreography by Bennett and scenic design by Boris Aronson. The show began out-of-town tryouts at Boston's Colonial Theatre from February 20 to March 20, 1971, where revisions were made, including the addition of the song "I'm Still Here" to replace an earlier number during rehearsals. Following the tryouts, it transferred to New York for 12 previews starting March 24, 1971, before officially opening on April 4, 1971.
The production featured a notable ensemble of Broadway and Hollywood veterans, including Alexis Smith as Phyllis Rogers Stone, John McMartin as Benjamin Stone, Dorothy Collins as Sally Durant Plummer, and Gene Nelson as Buddy Plummer, alongside Yvonne De Carlo as Carlotta Campion and others portraying the aging showgirls and their spouses. Upon opening, Follies received mixed critical reception, with reviewers praising its innovative structure and Sondheim's score while critiquing its length and occasional uneven pacing, prompting minor adjustments such as cuts to streamline the show.
The production ran for 522 performances, concluding on July 1, 1972, after more than a year on Broadway. Despite earning several Tony Awards and maintaining strong attendance, it closed primarily due to escalating production costs that exceeded its initial $800,000 budget, resulting in a financial loss of about $750,000.

Early Revivals and Tours (1970s1980s)

Following the original Broadway production's closure in 1972, a national touring company of Follies was launched, featuring principal cast members from the New York run, including Dorothy Collins as Sally Durant Plummer, John McMartin as Benjamin Stone, Gene Nelson as Buddy Plummer, and Alexis Smith as Phyllis Rogers Stone. This road company performed at various U.S. venues from mid-1972 into early 1973, marking the show's initial post-Broadway outreach but lasting only a limited season due to the production's logistical demands and escalating operational expenses.
A subsequent Los Angeles engagement opened on July 22, 1972, at the Shubert Theatre in Century City, following a one-week tryout at The Muny in St. Louis earlier that month. Directed by Harold Prince and Michael Bennett, this West Coast production retained much of the Broadway staging and featured a cast highlighted by Alexis Smith as Phyllis, Gene Nelson as Buddy, and replacements such as Janet Blair as Sally and Edward Winter as Ben, alongside Yvonne De Carlo as Carlotta Campion, adding a layer of Hollywood glamour. The run, which concluded on October 1, 1972, after approximately 11 weeks, was shorter than anticipated, reflecting the challenges of mounting the show's elaborate sets, large ensemble of over 40 performers, and full 28-piece orchestra outside New York, where costs often exceeded box-office returns.
The musical's first Australian staging premiered on September 7, 1979, at the Camberwell Civic Theatre in Melbourne, produced under the auspices of the Melbourne International Festival of the Arts. This fully staged production, directed by Peter Farago, starred local performers including Fay Brown as Sally, Bev McKern as Phyllis, Clive Hearne as Ben, and Chris Langham as Buddy, emphasizing the show's themes through an all-Australian lens while adapting to a more intimate venue. Though critically praised for its energy, the limited run underscored ongoing financial hurdles, as the production's scaleretaining a substantial chorus and orchestrastrained resources in a market untested for such ambitious Sondheim works.
By the mid-1980s, efforts to revive Follies increasingly turned to scaled-down formats to mitigate its notorious production costs, which stemmed from the need for a large aging ensemble, intricate period costumes, and expansive orchestrations. In the U.S., a high-profile concert adaptation was presented by the New York Philharmonic at Avery Fisher Hall in Lincoln Center on September 67, 1985, directed by Jonathan Tunick and featuring a star-studded lineup including Carol Burnett, Elaine Stritch, Barbara Cook, and George Hearn. This two-performance event, later broadcast on PBS's Great Performances and released as a recording, preserved the score's grandeur with minimal staging, proving an effective model for accessibility amid budgetary constraints. Concurrently, the European premiere occurred as a full regional production at the Forum Theatre in Wythenshawe, Manchester, England, from April 30 to June 1, 1985, directed by Howard Lloyd-Lewis with a British cast led by Josephine Blake and Kevin Colson. These adaptations highlighted how Follies' demands often necessitated compromises, such as reduced scenery or concert formats, to enable stagings beyond major commercial centers.
The decade's most successful early revival arrived in London with the West End debut on July 21, 1987, at the Shaftesbury Theatre, produced by Cameron Mackintosh and directed by Mike Ockrent. Starring Julia McKenzie as Sally, Diana Rigg as Phyllis, David Healy as Buddy, and Daniel Massey as Bensupported by veterans like Dolores Gray as Carlottathis production ran for 644 performances until February 4, 1989, benefiting from a slightly streamlined orchestration while retaining the show's opulent visual style designed by Maria Bjrnson. Despite the financial risks posed by the large cast and orchestra, the London run demonstrated Follies' enduring appeal, though it still required careful budgeting to avoid the losses that had plagued earlier efforts.

Major International Productions (1990s2000s)

The 1990s marked the beginning of Follies' broader international presence through a series of high-profile concert and semi-staged productions that highlighted the musical's emotional depth and Sondheim's score. In May 1996, a concert version directed by Michael Scott was presented at Dublin's National Concert Hall, featuring Lorna Luft as Sally Durant Plummer, Millicent Martin as Phyllis Rogers Stone, and Dave Willetts as Ben Stone, drawing acclaim for its intimate exploration of the show's themes of regret and nostalgia. Later that year, on December 8, 1996, another concert took place at London's Theatre Royal Drury Lane, starring Barbara Cook as Sally, Maria Friedman as Phyllis, Daniel Massey as Ben, and David McCallum as Buddy, under the direction of Paul Kerry; the event was broadcast on BBC2 in February 1997 and praised for Cook's poignant rendition of "Losing My Mind."
Building on this momentum, Follies reached Australia for the first time in February 1998 with a semi-staged concert at the Sydney Opera House, accompanied by the Sydney Symphony Orchestra and directed by David King, featuring Toni Lamond as Sally, Terence Donovan as Ben, and Jill Perryman as Carlotta Campion. The production, which served as a highlight of the Sydney Gay and Lesbian Mardi Gras, received enthusiastic reviews for its lavish orchestration and the performers' evocation of the Ziegfeld-era glamour central to the show. These concerts helped sustain interest in the musical amid limited full stagings, emphasizing its conceptual blend of pastiche and psychological drama without the logistical demands of a complete production.
The early 2000s saw more ambitious revivals, starting with the Roundabout Theatre Company's 2001 Broadway production at the Belasco Theatre, directed by Matthew Warchus with choreography by Bob Avian, which ran from April 5 to July 14, 2001. Starring Blythe Danner as Phyllis, Gregory Harrison as Ben, and Treat Williams as Buddy, the staging adopted a darker, more introspective tone than the original, incorporating minimalist sets to underscore the characters' disillusionment; critics noted its innovative approach but debated its emotional resonance compared to earlier versions.
Internationally, a significant full production followed in London at the Royal Festival Hall from August 6 to 31, 2002, directed by Sean Mathias and produced in association with Raymond Gubbay, restoring several songs cut from the 1971 premiere, including "Ah, Paree!" and expanded ensemble numbers. The cast included Maria Friedman as Sally, Louise Gold as Phyllis, and Simon Russell Beale as Ben, with the large-scale staging lauded for its visual spectacle and the restored material's enhancement of the show's satirical edge on American innocence, though some reviews critiqued the pacing in the intimate venue.
In the United States, regional efforts continued to expand access to the work, exemplified by a semi-professional concert mounting at Ann Arbor's Michigan Theatre on January 45, 2003, presented by the University Musical Society and directed by Roger Webb. Featuring Debbie Reynolds as Carlotta Campion, Donna McKechnie as Sally, and Linda Lavin as Phyllis, the production reunited original Broadway cast members and was celebrated for its nostalgic authenticity and the performers' lived connection to the material. These productions from the 1990s and 2000s demonstrated Follies' growing global appeal, bridging concert formats with fuller stagings to introduce new audiences to its intricate tapestry of memory and loss.

Recent Revivals and Concerts (2010s2025)

A revival of Follies opened at the John F. Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts in Washington, D.C., on May 12, 2011, directed by Eric Schaeffer and featuring Bernadette Peters as Sally Durant Plummer, Jan Maxwell as Phyllis Rogers Stone, Elaine Paige as Carlotta Campion, and Donna Murphy as Carlotta Campion (alternating with Paige in the initial run). The production, which included a 28-piece orchestra and restored elements of the original score, transferred to Broadway's Marquis Theatre on August 23, 2011, for a limited engagement that concluded on January 22, 2012, after 156 performances.
Following its Broadway run, the same Schaeffer-directed production moved to the Ahmanson Theatre in Los Angeles for a six-week engagement from May 3 to June 9, 2012, retaining much of the original cast including Peters, Maxwell, Paige, and Murphy, alongside local performers. The Los Angeles mounting emphasized the musical's lavish production values, with elaborate sets evoking the crumbling Weissmann theater and a full orchestra underscoring Sondheim's score.
In March 2013, Follies received its French premiere as a fully staged production at the Opra de Toulon, directed by Olivier Bnzech and conducted by David Charles Abell, featuring an international cast that included Charlotte Page as Sally Durant Plummer, Liz Robertson as Phyllis Rogers Stone, Graham Bickley as Benjamin Stone, and Jrme Pradon as Buddy Plummer. Performed in English over three nights from March 8 to 10, the production was filmed live and later released on DVD, highlighting the work's themes of nostalgia through a blend of English and French performers.
A concert version of Follies was presented in Australia in May 2016 at the Melbourne Recital Centre, directed by Tyran Parke and featuring a cast of veteran Australian musical theater artists including Toni Lamond, Jill Perryman, Judi Connelli, and Terence Donovan, with a focus on the score's emotional depth and pastiche numbers. The two-night event, limited to Melbourne, showcased a streamlined adaptation that prioritized vocal performances and orchestral accompaniment over full staging.
The National Theatre in London mounted a major revival directed by Dominic Cooke, opening in the Olivier Theatre on September 7, 2017, with Imelda Staunton as Sally Durant Plummer, Janie Dee as Phyllis Rogers Stone, Philip Quast as Ben Stone, and Peter Forbes as Buddy Plummer, supported by a 37-member cast and a 21-piece orchestra. The production, which ran until January 3, 2018, followed by a return engagement from February 12 to May 11, 2019, incorporated restored musical elements and innovative staging to blend the show's ghostly pastiches with its present-day drama.
On June 20, 2024, Transport Group presented a one-night-only concert of Follies at Carnegie Hall's Stern Auditorium / Perelman Stage, hosted by Kurt Peterson and Ted Chapin, featuring a star-studded cast of 50 performers including Jennifer Holliday as Carlotta Campion, Norbert Leo Butz as Ben Stone, Donna Murphy as Sally Durant Plummer, and Beth Leavel as Phyllis Rogers Stone, accompanied by a 30-piece orchestra. The Broadway-style event celebrated the musical's legacy through semi-staged performances of key numbers, drawing on the full score to evoke its themes of regret and showbiz glamour.
In December 2024, Northern Ireland Opera announced a fully staged revival of Follies for the Grand Opera House in Belfast, running from September 13 to 20, 2025, marking the musical's Northern Irish premiere with a 32-member cast of local and Irish talent, directed by Oliver Mears and featuring a full orchestra. The production, which opened as scheduled, emphasized the score's orchestration and the show's exploration of aging performers reuniting amid personal reckonings.
Victorian Opera presented the first fully staged Australian production of Follies from February 1 to 6, 2025, at the Palais Theatre in St Kilda, directed by Stuart Maunder with a cast of Australian theater veterans including Marina Prior as Sally Durant Plummer, Lisa McCune as Phyllis Rogers Stone, and Anthony Warlow as Ben Stone, accompanied by a full orchestra to highlight the musical's grandeur and themes of nostalgia.
A planned concert production of Follies at the Pasadena Playhouse, scheduled for January 31 to February 1, 2025, was canceled on January 10, 2025, due to the ongoing Eaton and Palisades wildfires threatening the Los Angeles area, alongside a concurrent cancellation of Anything Goes in concert.
Throughout the 2010s and into the 2020s, revivals of Follies have increasingly favored concert formats over full stagings, driven by the high costs of mounting the show's large cast, elaborate sets, and orchestra, while often restoring Jonathan Tunick's original orchestrations to highlight Sondheim's complex score. This trend has made the musical more accessible for regional and one-off presentations, preserving its emotional and musical integrity amid budgetary constraints.

Characters and Casting

Principal Characters

The principal characters in Follies form a quartet of middle-aged individuals grappling with personal histories, each embodying distinct archetypes that drive the narrative's interpersonal dynamics. Sally Durant Plummer serves as the idealistic former showgirl, characterized by her delusions about a past romance that prevent her from confronting her present dissatisfaction. Her dramatic function centers on embodying unfulfilled longing, creating emotional tension within her marriage and relationships. Buddy Plummer, her husband, represents the frustrated everymana boisterous traveling salesman who escapes his marital woes through fleeting fantasies and infidelities. His role highlights themes of personal stagnation and the search for vitality amid routine discontent.
Phyllis Rogers Stone, the cynical socialite and wife of Ben, masks her underlying vulnerability with a veneer of sophistication and biting wit, serving as a counterpoint to more openly emotional figures. She functions to expose the compromises of ambition and social climbing, challenging the stability of her partnership. Ben Stone, an ambitious politician haunted by regrets over choices made in youth, embodies the archetype of the charismatic yet inwardly tormented leader whose public success conceals private failures. His dramatic role propels conflicts arising from indecision and unresolved affections, linking the central couples' intertwined pasts.
The ensemble of former showgirls, such as Carlotta Campion and Stella Deems, represents the collective memory of the troupe's glamorous era, with Carlotta as the resilient survivor who asserts enduring strength and Stella as the bold, nostalgic performer evoking shared history. These figures function to amplify the group's reminiscences and provide individual vignettes that mirror the principals' struggles. Supporting the main roles are the young counterpartsghostly, idealized versions of the characters from their chorus daysthat underscore the duality between youthful promise and aged disillusionment, appearing as spectral presences to highlight contrasts in aspiration and reality.

Original and Notable Casts

The original Broadway production of Follies in 1971 featured a cast of veteran performers drawn from stage and screen, emphasizing the show's theme of faded glory. Dorothy Collins portrayed Sally Durant Plummer, John McMartin played Benjamin Stone, Gene Nelson was Buddy Plummer, and Alexis Smith embodied Phyllis Rogers Stone. Supporting roles included Yvonne De Carlo as the resilient Carlotta Campion, whose signature number "I'm Still Here" highlighted the diva archetype. Fifi D'Orsay appeared as Solange LaFitte, bringing a touch of French flair to the ensemble of former showgirls.
The 2001 Broadway revival at the Belasco Theatre cast established actors in the principal roles to evoke mid-century elegance and regret. Judith Ivey starred as Sally Durant Plummer, Treat Williams as Buddy Plummer, Gregory Harrison as Benjamin Stone, and Blythe Danner as Phyllis Rogers Stone. Notable supporting performers included Polly Bergen as Carlotta Campion and Betty Garrett as Hattie Walker, both leveraging their extensive Broadway experience for the demanding ensemble numbers.
In the 2011 Broadway revival directed by Eric Schaeffer, the cast blended contemporary stars with musical theater legends, focusing on emotional depth for the aging characters. Bernadette Peters led as Sally Durant Plummer, Jan Maxwell as Phyllis Rogers Stone, Ron Raines as Benjamin Stone, and Danny Burstein as Buddy Plummer. Elaine Paige originated Carlotta Campion, delivering a powerhouse rendition of "I'm Still Here," while later replacements included Terri White as Dee Dee West and Mary Beth Peil as Heidi Schiller, enhancing the production's run through mid-2012. Jayne Houdyshell joined as Hattie Walker, noted for her comedic timing in the role's vaudeville sequence.
The 2017 London production at the National Theatre, directed by Dominic Cooke, assembled a British-heavy ensemble to capture the show's introspective melancholy. Imelda Staunton starred as Sally Durant Plummer, Janie Dee as Phyllis Rogers Stone, Peter Forbes as Buddy Plummer, and Philip Quast as Benjamin Stone. Josephine Barstow portrayed Heidi Schiller with operatic precision, and Tracie Bennett took on Carlotta Campion, emphasizing the character's survivor spirit.
Casting across major productions has consistently prioritized performers of appropriate age to reflect the characters' middle-aged disillusionment, often attracting Broadway and West End divas for roles like Carlotta and Phyllis that demand strong belting and dramatic presence. These parts require vocal ranges spanning mezzo-soprano to alto with belt capabilities, alongside acting chops for conveying layered regret and resilience in Sondheim's intricate score.

Reception

Critical Response

Upon its 1971 Broadway premiere, Follies received widespread acclaim for Stephen Sondheim's score, which critics hailed as one of his most innovative and emotionally resonant works to date, featuring intricate pastiche numbers and introspective ballads that captured the show's themes of regret and illusion. However, reviewers like Clive Barnes of The New York Times found the production uneven overall, praising its stylish staging and musical sophistication while critiquing the sentimental excess that occasionally overwhelmed the narrative. Similarly, Walter Kerr noted the score's brilliance but questioned the coherence of James Goldman's book, which some saw as structurally ambitious yet pacing-challenged in blending reunion drama with showbiz homage.
The 2001 Roundabout Theatre Company revival, directed by Matthew Warchus, elicited mixed responses, with Ben Brantley of The New York Times appreciating how the smaller-scale production restored a sense of intimate grit to the faded glamour of the original, allowing Sondheim's songs to resonate more personally amid the characters' marital discord. Yet, Brantley and others faulted the staging for occasionally feeling pedestrian, underscoring persistent debates over Goldman's book, which was viewed as intellectually provocative but uneven in sustaining dramatic momentum.
In the 2011 Broadway revival at the Marquis Theatre, directed by Eric Schaeffer, Ben Brantley lauded the emotional depth achieved through the ensemble's raw portrayals, emphasizing how the production illuminated the score's haunting exploration of lost dreams and relational fractures. Charles Isherwood echoed this sentiment in broader commentary on the season, highlighting the revival's success in deepening the work's psychological layers without over-relying on spectacle.
The 2017 National Theatre production in London, under Dominic Cooke, garnered strong praise from UK critics for its innovative staging, which employed shadow figures to intertwine past and present selves, creating a visually poetic framework that amplified the score's melancholy elegance. Michael Billington in The Guardian described it as a "breathtaking" revival that fused splendor with sadness, while Variety's review celebrated the lavish yet languid approach that showcased Sondheim's compositions anew. The Stage called it "transcendent," particularly for how the direction revitalized Goldman's narrative through seamless emotional transitions.
More recent productions have continued to receive acclaim. The 2021 concert at the Kennedy Center in Washington, D.C., was praised for its starry cast and faithful rendering of the score. In 2025, Northern Ireland Opera's staging at the Grand Opera House in Belfast earned positive reviews for its operatic treatment and emotional depth, with The Times noting its "suave" revival and The Stage describing it as "high-octane musical theatre." Similarly, the Volksoper Wien production in May 2025 was lauded for its musical quality.
Across productions, a consistent theme in critical reception has been unequivocal praise for Sondheim's score as a pinnacle of musical theater sophistication, blending Broadway nostalgia with avant-garde lyricism, while Goldman's book has drawn mixed assessments for its ambitious structure, often critiqued for pacing issues that dilute the interpersonal drama. Over time, later reviews have evolved to emphasize feminist undertones in the women's arcs of regret and agency, interpreting their follies as critiques of gendered expectations in mid-20th-century America. Scholarly views, as explored elsewhere, further contextualize these layers in thematic analyses.

Commercial Performance

The original 1971 Broadway production of Follies had a budget of $800,000 and ran for 522 performances at the Winter Garden Theatre, but it ultimately failed to recoup its investment, resulting in a total financial loss of nearly $800,000 despite strong attendance averaging 85-90% capacity in its later months.
Subsequent revivals have shown varied financial outcomes, often hampered by the show's high production demands. The 2001 Broadway revival at the Belasco Theatre completed 117 performances and generated a total gross of $7,080,718, with average weekly attendance at 88% capacity, but it closed prematurely due to escalating weekly operating costs exceeding revenues. In contrast, the 2011 Broadway revival at the Marquis Theatre ran for 152 performances, achieving a total gross of $19,566,692making it one of the highest-grossing Sondheim revivals at the timeyet it still did not recoup its investment, with average weekly grosses of about $815,000 falling short of running costs estimated at about $600,000 per week.
Key commercial challenges for Follies stem from its large-scale requirements, including a full 28-piece orchestra in major productions, which significantly inflates operating expenses compared to more streamlined Sondheim works, while its niche thematic focus on aging, regret, and midlife disillusionment limits broad mainstream appeal. Concert versions have proven more financially viable, as seen in the 1985 Lincoln Center staging and subsequent adaptations, which reduce staging costs while highlighting the score's strengths and attracting dedicated fans without the full logistical burden of a book musical.
Internationally, the 1987 West End production at the Shaftesbury Theatre achieved solid success with 644 performances over 18 months, though it faced initial hurdles from high expectations and adaptation tweaks that slightly altered the tone for UK audiences. The 2017 revival at London's National Theatre, directed by Dominic Cooke, sold out its entire run of 117 performances almost immediately, bolstered by star casting including Imelda Staunton and strong pre-opening buzz, demonstrating renewed commercial potential in limited-engagement formats.
Audience demographics for Follies have historically skewed toward older theatergoers, particularly devoted Sondheim enthusiasts in their 50s and above drawn to the show's introspective exploration of later-life themes, though recent revivals like the 2017 London production and 2021 Kennedy Center concert have attracted a modestly younger cohortaveraging closer to the broader Broadway median of 40through heightened cultural interest in Sondheim following biographical films and streaming accessibility.

Recordings and Adaptations

Cast Recordings

The original Broadway cast recording of Follies was released by Capitol Records in 1971, featuring 17 tracks from the production starring Alexis Smith, John McMartin, Dorothy Collins, and Gene Nelson. Produced by Dick Jones and engineered by Bob Arnold, the album was abbreviated due to commercial constraints, condensing what was planned as a two-disc set into a single LP; this resulted in heavy abridgments of several numbers and the omission of songs such as "Can That Boy Foxtrot!" and the full "Buddy's Eyes." Despite these cuts, the recording captured key performances, including Alexis Smith's rendition of "I'm Still Here."
The 1987 London production at the Shaftesbury Theatre yielded the first complete cast recording of the score, released on vinyl by First Night Records and later on CD by TER/Jay Productions. Produced by Chris Walker and engineered by David Hunt at CTS Studios, the two-disc album featured the cast led by Diana Rigg as Phyllis Rogers Stone, Julia McKenzie as Sally Durant Plummer, Daniel Massey as Ben Stone, and David Healy as Buddy Plummer, along with supporting performers like Pearl Carr and Teddy Johnson. Running approximately 84 minutes, it preserved the full orchestration by Jonathan Tunick and included all musical numbers without the abridgments of the original Broadway version, marking a significant archival achievement for the musical.
The 2011 Broadway revival, directed by Eric Schaeffer and produced by the Roundabout Theatre Company at the Belasco Theatre, received a comprehensive two-disc cast album on PS Classics, released on November 29, 2011. Produced by Tommy Krasker and Philip Chaffin, the recording restored the complete score, including previously omitted material like "Can That Boy Foxtrot!" and an appendix of eight cut songs, totaling 33 tracks and over 106 minutes. Featuring Bernadette Peters as Sally Durant Plummer, Jan Maxwell as Phyllis Rogers Stone, Danny Burstein as Buddy Plummer, Ron Raines as Ben Stone, and Elaine Paige as Carlotta Campion, it was recorded live with a 28-piece orchestra and nominated for the Grammy Award for Best Musical Theater Album at the 55th Annual Grammy Awards. The package included a 52-page booklet with production photos, lyrics, and essays.
The 20172019 National Theatre revival in London, directed by Dominic Cooke, resulted in a digital cast album released in early 2019 by Warner Music Group's Arts Music division, with a limited CD edition following via Dress Circle Records on May 17, 2019. Capturing the cast from the production's run at the National Theatre Olivier, the recording starred Imelda Staunton as Sally Durant Plummer, Janie Dee as Phyllis Rogers Stone, Tracie Bennett as Carlotta Campion, and Philip Quast as Ben Stone, among others including Josephine Barstow as Heidi Schiller. Spanning 26 tracks and about 80 minutes, it presented the full score as performed, emphasizing the ensemble's dramatic interpretations and the 44-piece orchestra conducted by Paul McCullough.

Film and Other Adaptations

Efforts to adapt Follies into a feature film began shortly after its 1971 Broadway premiere. In 1972, director and producer Hal Prince proposed a screen version to Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer (MGM), envisioning a narrative centered on faded Hollywood stars gathering on a backlot facing demolition, interwoven with musical numbers filmed separately to capture a cinema vrit style, in collaboration with documentary filmmaker D. A. Pennebaker. The project aimed to incorporate archival footage and benefit the Motion Picture & Television Fund through appearance fees for veteran performers. Potential casting included icons such as Bette Davis, Gene Kelly, Debbie Reynolds, and Gloria Swanson, with preferences for retaining elements of the original Broadway cast like Dorothy Collins and Gene Nelson. However, the plans were abandoned due to MGM's concerns over the material's melancholic tone and lack of commercial appeal, leading to a quarrel between Prince and the studio. Prince then shifted the project to 20th Century Fox in 1973, involving writers Jean-Claude van Itallie and later Hugh Wheeler, but it was ultimately shelved in favor of another musical production.
The closest Follies has come to a screen adaptation remains non-feature formats, particularly concert presentations and filmed stage productions captured for television and cinema. In 1985, the New York Philharmonic staged a semi-staged concert version at Lincoln Center's Avery Fisher Hall, directed by Harold Prince and featuring a star-studded cast including Barbara Cook as Sally Durant Plummer, George Hearn as Ben Stone, Lee Remick as Phyllis Rogers Stone, Mandy Patinkin as Buddy Plummer, and guest performers like Carol Burnett, Elaine Stritch, and Betty Comden and Adolph Green. This event was documented in the 1986 PBS broadcast Follies in Concert as part of the Great Performances series, blending performance footage with backstage insights into rehearsals over four days in New York; a companion documentary, Sondheim: A Celebration at Carnegie Hall, also highlighted excerpts. The production aired internationally, including on BBC Two in the UK as Sondheim on Broadway: Follies - Four Days in New York, emphasizing the musical's emotional depth through its all-American ensemble rather than a British cast.
The 2017 National Theatre revival was filmed for National Theatre Live and broadcast in cinemas worldwide on November 16, 2017, directed by Dominic Cooke and starring Imelda Staunton as Sally, Janie Dee as Phyllis, Tracie Bennett as Carlotta, and Philip Quast as Ben. The production was later released for streaming and home video, providing a full staged adaptation captured for screen audiences in over 2,000 venues across 60 countries.
Other adaptations have appeared in video recordings of concerts and tribute events, often excerpting key songs. In 1998, London's Lyceum Theatre hosted Hey, Mr. Producer!, a gala celebrating producer Cameron Mackintosh that included Follies numbers such as "Broadway Baby" performed by Ute Lemper and "I'm Still Here" by Ann Miller, with footage later released on video and DVD. Selections from Follies have also featured in broader Sondheim tribute shows, including the 1992 Sondheim: A Musical Tribute at the Library of Congress and various PBS specials, preserving the score's iconic moments like "Losing My Mind" and "The Road You Didn't Take" through live performances.
A concert production of Follies was staged at Carnegie Hall on June 20, 2024, directed by Jack Cummings III and featuring stars such as Donna Murphy, Christine Ebersole, Norbert Leo Butz, and Norm Lewis, with music direction by Joey Chancey and a 30-piece orchestra. Presented by Transport Group as a one-night-only event, it sold out and highlighted the full score.
Despite these efforts, no full feature film adaptation of Follies has been realized to date, with historical challenges attributed to the musical's complex structure, ensemble demands, and rights complexities involving Sondheim's estate and Goldmans book. Interest revived in 2019 when Heyday Films and BBC Films acquired the rights for a cinematic version directed by Dominic Cooke, known for On Chesil Beach, though production details remain undeveloped as of 2025.

The original 1971 Broadway production of Follies earned 11 nominations at the 26th Tony Awards in 1972, securing seven victories, a record at the time for a musical. These included awards for Best Original Score (Stephen Sondheim), Best Performance by a Leading Actress in a Musical (Alexis Smith), Best Scenic Design (Boris Aronson), Best Costume Design (Florence Klotz), Best Lighting Design (Tharon Musser), Best Choreography (Michael Bennett), and Best Direction of a Musical (co-directed by Harold Prince and Michael Bennett).

Cultural Impact

Follies marked a pivotal advancement in musical theater by exemplifying the "concept musical," a form where the show's central ideahere, the interplay of past and present amid aging and regretdrives the structure rather than a linear plot, influencing subsequent works in Stephen Sondheim's catalog such as Sunday in the Park with George (1984) and Into the Woods (1987), which similarly prioritized thematic exploration over conventional narrative. This innovation, developed in collaboration with director Harold Prince, shifted Broadway toward more introspective, presentational formats that commented on societal and personal themes, redefining the genre's possibilities in the post-Rodgers and Hammerstein era.
The musical has permeated popular culture, particularly through the song "I'm Still Here," a resilient torch number embodying survival amid fame's vicissitudes, which has been covered by prominent artists including Barbra Streisand on her 1994 live album The Concert and Elaine Stritch in various concerts, amplifying its status as an anthem of endurance. The song's lyrics, rich with references to 20th-century American fads like ukuleles, Amos 'n' Andy, and the Dionne Quintuplets, serve as a cultural time capsule, reflecting the Ziegfeld Follies era's glamour and transience.
Follies revived fascination with the original Ziegfeld Follies revue style of the early 20th century, blending pastiche of period songs with modern introspection to honor and critique Broadway's golden age, thereby sparking renewed appreciation for theatrical nostalgia in contemporary productions. Academic analyses have highlighted its portrayal of aging performers, positioning it as a key text for examining midlife disillusionment and memory in the performing arts, as explored in scholarly works on Sondheim's treatment of middle age. Following Sondheim's death in November 2021, the musical experienced a surge in stagings worldwide, including high-profile revivals that underscored his enduring legacy and prompted broader tributes to his oeuvre, with ongoing productions as of 2025 such as those by Northern Ireland Opera and Victorian Opera.
Internationally, Follies has seen adaptations and productions in countries like Germany, with notable stagings at the Staatsoperette Dresden in 2019 and Staatstheater Wiesbaden in 2023, adapting its themes to local contexts while preserving Sondheim's intricate score. As part of Sondheim's body of workcreated by the openly gay composerthe musical contributes to LGBTQ+ theater history by featuring show business narratives that resonate with queer performers' experiences of reinvention and marginalization, influencing discussions on identity and resilience in the arts.
