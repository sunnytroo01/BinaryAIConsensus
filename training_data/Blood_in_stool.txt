 Blood in stool, medically termed rectal bleeding or hematochezia when bright red and melena when black and tarry, is the presence of blood in feces originating from the gastrointestinal (GI) tract, often signaling an underlying disorder in the digestive system. This symptom can manifest as visible bright red blood coating the stool or toilet paper, maroon or dark red clots mixed with feces, or black, sticky stools due to digested blood from upper GI sources. It may occur acutely with heavy bleeding or chronically with small, occult amounts detectable only through tests like the fecal occult blood test (FOBT). While often benign, such as from hemorrhoids or anal fissures, it can indicate serious conditions including colorectal cancer, inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), or peptic ulcers, necessitating prompt medical evaluation.
Gastrointestinal bleeding, of which blood in stool is a primary manifestation, arises from the upper GI tract (esophagus, stomach, duodenum), small intestine, or lower GI tract (colon, rectum, anus), with the appearance of the blood providing clues to the sourcebright red typically from lower sources and black from upper ones due to digestion. Common causes include hemorrhoids (swollen rectal veins causing painless bright red bleeding), anal fissures (tears in the anal lining from constipation, leading to painful bleeding), and diverticular disease (pouches in the colon that bleed or become inflamed). More serious etiologies encompass colorectal polyps or cancer (growths that bleed intermittently), IBD like ulcerative colitis or Crohn's disease (causing chronic inflammation and bloody diarrhea), and upper GI issues such as peptic ulcers (sores from H. pylori infection or NSAID use). Less common triggers involve infections, intussusception (particularly in children), vascular abnormalities like angiodysplasia, or ischemia (reduced blood flow to the intestines).
Associated symptoms may include abdominal pain, cramps, diarrhea, constipation, dizziness, or fatigue from anemia due to chronic blood loss, with severe cases risking hypovolemic shock characterized by rapid heartbeat, low blood pressure, and confusion. Diagnosis typically involves a medical history, physical exam including digital rectal examination, stool tests for occult blood, and endoscopic procedures like colonoscopy or upper endoscopy to visualize and biopsy the bleeding site. Treatment varies by cause and severity: minor cases may resolve with dietary changes, fiber supplements, or topical ointments, while serious bleeding requires medications (e.g., proton pump inhibitors for ulcers), endoscopic interventions to stop bleeding, or surgery in refractory instances. Individuals should seek immediate medical attention for large amounts of blood, persistent bleeding, or accompanying symptoms like lightheadedness, as early intervention can prevent complications.

Definition and Presentation

Types of Rectal Bleeding

Blood in stool, also known as rectal bleeding, refers to the passage of blood from the gastrointestinal tract, which can be visible (gross) or occult (hidden).
Hematochezia is characterized by the passage of bright red or maroon blood from the rectum, typically indicating bleeding from a lower gastrointestinal source due to rapid transit through the colon. This presentation can vary in volume, ranging from small streaks of blood on the stool surface or toilet paper in mild cases to profuse bleeding that mixes with stool or fills the toilet bowl in more significant episodes.
In contrast, melena presents as black, tarry, and foul-smelling stools resulting from the digestion of blood, usually signaling an upper gastrointestinal source where the blood has had sufficient time to be altered by digestive processes. The hemoglobin in the blood undergoes breakdown in the acidic environment of the stomach and upper intestine, converting to acid hematin, which imparts the characteristic dark color and sticky consistency.
Occult blood in stool is not visible to the naked eye and requires microscopic detection, often through tests such as the fecal occult blood test (FOBT), with no apparent changes in stool appearance.
The appearance of blood in stool is influenced by factors such as the volume of blood lost and the transit time through the gut; for instance, melena typically requires at least 50 to 100 mL of intraluminal blood to develop, while faster transit in the lower gut preserves the red color of hematochezia.

Clinical Significance

Blood in stool, also known as rectal bleeding, is a common symptom in adults over 40, with a one-year prevalence of approximately 10% in primary care settings, though many cases go unreported. While about 90% of cases stem from benign etiologies such as hemorrhoids, 10-15% may indicate serious underlying conditions, including colorectal cancer or advanced adenomas, underscoring the need for prompt evaluation to rule out malignancy. The presence of visible blood, particularly hematochezia (bright red blood), often signals lower gastrointestinal involvement and warrants attention due to its potential implications.
Significant clinical concern arises from the risk of life-threatening hemorrhage, especially in cases of massive lower gastrointestinal bleeding, which can lead to hemodynamic instability manifested by signs such as tachycardia, hypotension, and shock, necessitating immediate emergency intervention. Additionally, chronic low-volume bleeding can result in iron-deficiency anemia, characterized by symptoms including fatigue, pallor, and weakness, as ongoing blood loss depletes iron stores over time. Red flags that heighten suspicion for malignancy include unexplained weight loss, a persistent change in bowel habits (such as diarrhea or constipation), and a family history of colorectal cancer, which collectively increase the urgency for diagnostic assessment.
Beyond physical health risks, rectal bleeding can have a notable psychological impact, often inducing anxiety and fear due to its alarming visibility and association with serious disease, which may paradoxically lead to delayed medical seeking as individuals avoid confronting the symptom. This emotional distress highlights the importance of patient education to encourage timely care and alleviate unnecessary worry while addressing potential underlying issues.

Causes

Upper Gastrointestinal Sources

Upper gastrointestinal sources of blood in stool originate from bleeding in the esophagus, stomach, or duodenum, typically presenting as melena due to the digestion of blood during its transit through the gastrointestinal tract. These sources account for the majority of cases where stool appears black and tarry, distinguishing them from brighter red bleeding from lower sites.
The pathophysiology involves rapid transit of blood from the upper tract, where it mixes with gastric acid and digestive enzymes, leading to partial breakdown of hemoglobin into hematin, which produces the characteristic black, sticky melena upon reaching the colon. This process usually requires at least 50-100 mL of blood loss to manifest as visible melena, though smaller amounts may cause occult blood detection.
Risk factors for upper gastrointestinal bleeding include advanced age over 50 years, which correlates with higher incidence due to cumulative mucosal damage; excessive alcohol consumption, which erodes mucosal barriers; and use of anticoagulants or nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), which impair platelet function and prostaglandin-mediated mucosal protection. Additional contributors include Helicobacter pylori infection, promoting chronic inflammation, and conditions like portal hypertension that predispose to variceal formation.
Peptic ulcers represent the most common upper gastrointestinal source, accounting for 32-36% of cases, arising from erosions in the stomach or duodenal lining that penetrate underlying vessels. Gastric ulcers often result from H. pylori-induced mucosal inflammation or NSAID use, which inhibits cyclooxygenase-1 (COX-1) and reduces protective prostaglandin synthesis, while duodenal ulcers more frequently stem from excess acid secretion exacerbated by H. pylori. Bleeding typically occurs when ulcers erode into submucosal arteries, leading to significant hemorrhage that digests into melena.
Esophageal varices, dilated submucosal veins in the lower esophagus, cause 10-20% of upper bleeding episodes and arise in the context of portal hypertension, often secondary to liver cirrhosis from chronic alcohol abuse or viral hepatitis. Elevated portal pressure exceeding 12 mm Hg promotes portosystemic collateral formation, making varices prone to rupture and massive, life-threatening bleeding that manifests as melena after digestion.
Mallory-Weiss tears, comprising 5-15% of upper gastrointestinal bleeds, are longitudinal mucosal lacerations at the gastroesophageal junction resulting from forceful retching or vomiting, commonly associated with excessive alcohol intake or bulimia. These superficial tears bleed intermittently but can produce substantial melena if the laceration involves larger vessels.
Erosive gastritis and esophagitis contribute to 18-24% of cases, involving diffuse inflammation and ulceration of the stomach or esophageal mucosa from irritants such as alcohol, NSAIDs, or gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD). Gastritis features multiple superficial erosions that ooze blood, while esophagitis leads to friable mucosa prone to bleeding upon acid exposure, both resulting in digested blood appearing as melena.

Lower Gastrointestinal Sources

Lower gastrointestinal (GI) bleeding arises from sources distal to the ligament of Treitz, encompassing the small intestine, colon, rectum, and anus, and typically manifests as hematocheziabright red or maroon blood in the stooldue to the relatively rapid transit time that preserves the red color of extravasated blood through slower digestion in the lower tract. This contrasts with upper GI bleeding, where blood is often partially digested, appearing as melena. Common etiologies include vascular, inflammatory, neoplastic, and infectious conditions, with prevalence varying by age and risk factors; for instance, in adults over 40, diverticular disease and neoplasms account for a significant proportion of cases.
Hemorrhoids, swollen veins in the rectum or anus, are a common cause of lower GI bleeding, particularly in younger adults (<50 years), with symptomatic hemorrhoids affecting 4-39% of the population depending on the study, often triggered by chronic straining during defecation, constipation, or pregnancy. Internal hemorrhoids, located above the dentate line, may bleed painlessly with bright red blood coating the stool or appearing on toilet paper, while external hemorrhoids can cause thrombosis and more acute pain with bleeding. Diagnosis is typically clinical, with conservative management effective in most cases, though persistent bleeding warrants anoscopy.
Anal fissures, linear tears in the anal canal mucosa, commonly result from the passage of hard stools or trauma, leading to sharp pain during defecation and minimal bright red bleeding streaked on the stool or paper. These are particularly prevalent in young adults and can become chronic if underlying constipation is unaddressed, with sphincter spasm exacerbating the tear. Treatment focuses on stool softening and topical agents to promote healing, with surgery reserved for refractory cases.
Diverticulosis, the presence of outpouchings in the colonic wall, particularly in the sigmoid colon, is a leading cause of acute lower GI bleeding in older adults, with inflammation or erosion of a diverticular vessel causing painless, intermittent hematochezia that can be brisk and voluminous. Risk factors include low-fiber diets and age over 50, with bleeding episodes self-resolving in about 80% of cases but recurring in up to 25%. Colonoscopy is key for identification once stabilized.
Colorectal polyps and cancer contribute to chronic or occult lower GI bleeding, with adenomatous polyps serving as precancerous lesions that can ulcerate and bleed, especially in the left colon where lesions are more likely to cause visible hematochezia due to narrower lumen and faster transit. Right-sided colonic cancers, conversely, often present with iron deficiency anemia from slower, occult blood loss rather than overt bleeding. Screening via colonoscopy detects these in up to 25% of average-risk individuals over 50, underscoring their role in preventive management. Chronic bleeding from these sources may lead to anemia, necessitating iron supplementation alongside etiology-specific therapy.
Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), including Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis, causes lower GI bleeding through mucosal ulceration and friability, often presenting as chronic, low-volume hematochezia mixed with stool, particularly in ulcerative colitis which is confined to the colon. Crohn's disease can affect any segment, including the small intestine, leading to more variable bleeding patterns influenced by transmural inflammation and fistulas. Medical therapy with anti-inflammatories or biologics controls bleeding in most patients, though surgery may be required for complications.
Infectious colitis from bacterial pathogens such as enterohemorrhagic Escherichia coli (EHEC), Shigella spp., Salmonella, or Clostridioides difficilethe latter surging post-2020 due to increased antibiotic use during the COVID-19 pandemicinduces bloody diarrhea through toxin-mediated mucosal damage, often with abdominal cramps and fever. Viral agents like cytomegalovirus in immunocompromised hosts and parasitic infections such as Entamoeba histolytica can similarly cause colitis with bleeding, though less commonly. Stool studies confirm the diagnosis, with supportive care and targeted antimicrobials resolving most cases.
Intussusception, the telescoping of one segment of the intestine into another, is a significant cause of lower GI bleeding in young children, most commonly between 6 months and 3 years of age. It is the most common cause of intestinal obstruction in children under 3 years and often presents with intermittent colicky abdominal pain, vomiting, and stool mixed with blood and mucus, classically described as "currant jelly" stools. Most cases in children are idiopathic, though a lead point (such as a Meckel's diverticulum or polyp) may be present; it is rare in adults. Diagnosis is typically confirmed by ultrasound, and treatment usually involves non-surgical reduction using air or contrast enema, with surgery reserved for cases involving perforation or failure of reduction.
Ischemic colitis, resulting from reduced blood flow to the colon often in elderly patients with atherosclerosis or during hypotensive states, leads to segmental mucosal ischemia and bloody diarrhea, typically affecting the left colon's watershed areas. It accounts for 5-10% of lower GI bleeds, with prompt revascularization or supportive measures improving outcomes in mild cases. CT angiography aids in acute evaluation.

Non-Gastrointestinal Causes

Non-gastrointestinal causes of blood in stool encompass systemic conditions, medications, and rare extrinsic factors that impair hemostasis, damage vascular integrity, or mimic bleeding without originating from primary gastrointestinal pathology. These etiologies often amplify minor GI insults or present independently, leading to hematochezia or melena through mechanisms like coagulopathy or vascular fragility.
Coagulopathies represent a key category, where defects in the clotting cascade result in excessive bleeding from even trivial mucosal disruptions. Hemophilia, an inherited X-linked disorder characterized by deficiencies in factors VIII or IX, can manifest as gastrointestinal hemorrhage, including rectal bleeding, due to impaired thrombin generation and fibrin clot formation. Thrombocytopenia, whether from bone marrow failure, immune-mediated destruction, or drug effects, reduces platelet counts below critical thresholds (typically <50,000/L), prolonging bleeding time and promoting oozing from vascular sites in the rectum or colon. Vitamin K deficiency, often secondary to malnutrition, malabsorption, or broad-spectrum antibiotic use, inhibits synthesis of clotting factors II, VII, IX, and X, leading to prolonged prothrombin time and heightened risk of spontaneous mucosal bleeding. These conditions frequently coexist with underlying GI lesions but originate from systemic hemostatic failure.
Medication-induced bleeding arises from agents that either directly injure mucosa or interfere with coagulation. Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), such as ibuprofen and aspirin, inhibit cyclooxygenase enzymes, reducing prostaglandin-mediated mucosal protection and causing erosions or ulcers throughout the gastrointestinal tract, including the lower segments, with rectal bleeding reported in up to 10-20% of chronic users experiencing lower GI events. Anticoagulants like warfarin, which antagonizes vitamin K-dependent factors, or direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) such as rivaroxaban, which target thrombin or factor Xa, exacerbate bleeding by extending international normalized ratio (INR) or directly impairing clot stability, often precipitating hematochezia in patients with concurrent minor trauma or polyps. The combination of NSAIDs with anticoagulants synergistically elevates risk, with studies showing odds ratios exceeding 4 for major gastrointestinal hemorrhage.
Vascular disorders contribute through systemic abnormalities affecting gut vasculature. Angiodysplasia, though often localized to the GI tract, can occur in the context of systemic vascular fragility, particularly in the elderly, where chronic hypoxia or pressure changes dilate submucosal vessels, leading to recurrent painless hematochezia; it accounts for 5-10% of lower GI bleeds in patients over 70. Hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia (HHT), an autosomal dominant disorder due to mutations in ENG or ACVRL1 genes, causes widespread arteriovenous malformations, including telangiectasias in the gastrointestinal mucosa, resulting in chronic or acute bleeding; up to 25% of HHT patients experience GI hemorrhage, often presenting as iron-deficiency anemia alongside rectal blood.
Factitious causes involve intentional simulation of bleeding, typically in the context of factitious disorder (formerly Munchausen syndrome), where individuals surreptitiously ingest blood, add red dyes to stool, or self-inflict minor trauma to produce apparent hematochezia. These presentations often lack objective evidence of internal bleeding on imaging or endoscopy and are motivated by a desire for medical attention; case reports describe patients fabricating bright red blood per rectum through external contamination, which can complicate the diagnosis of unexplained GI bleeding.
Rare systemic conditions, such as amyloidosis or connective tissue diseases, can indirectly cause rectal bleeding by infiltrating or compromising vascular walls. In systemic amyloidosis, particularly AL type from light chain deposition, amyloid proteins can accumulate in gastrointestinal vessels, leading to fragility and hemorrhage; overt bleeding occurs in some cases of GI amyloid involvement. Connective tissue disorders like systemic sclerosis promote vascular ectasias or ischemia through fibrosis and autoimmunity, associating with angiodysplasia-like lesions and lower GI bleeding in affected patients.
These non-gastrointestinal factors often overlap with intrinsic GI pathologies but are distinguished by their extrinsic origins, such as laboratory evidence of coagulopathy or negative endoscopic findings for structural lesions.

Diagnosis

History and Risk Assessment

The initial evaluation of a patient presenting with blood in stool begins with a detailed history to characterize the bleeding and identify potential etiologies. Key elements include the onset (acute versus chronic), amount and color of the blood (bright red suggesting distal sources versus maroon or black indicating proximal), presence of clots, and frequency of episodes. Associated symptoms such as abdominal pain, changes in bowel habits (diarrhea or constipation), weight loss, fever, or recent travel (which may point to infectious causes) should be elicited, along with dietary history to rule out food-related mimics.
Risk factors are systematically assessed to guide differential diagnosis and urgency. Age over 50 years increases suspicion for colorectal cancer, while family history of inflammatory bowel disease or malignancy warrants further scrutiny. Medication use, particularly nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, anticoagulants, or antiplatelet agents, heightens bleeding risk, as do comorbidities like cirrhosis (predisposing to varices) or cardiovascular disease. Prior gastrointestinal bleeding, surgeries, or radiation therapy are also probed.
The physical examination focuses on hemodynamic stability and local findings. Vital signs, including orthostatic measurements, detect hypovolemia from significant blood loss (e.g., tachycardia or hypotension). Abdominal palpation identifies tenderness, masses, or distension, while a digital rectal examination assesses for anal fissures, hemorrhoids, or intraluminal masses and confirms the presence of blood.30071-X/fulltext)
Age-specific considerations refine the approach. In pediatrics, painless rectal bleeding may suggest Meckel's diverticulum or juvenile polyps, with history emphasizing recent infections, allergies, or growth patterns; anal fissures are common with constipation. In the elderly, diverticular disease and ischemic colitis predominate, compounded by polypharmacy and frailty, necessitating evaluation for comorbidities like atherosclerosis.
For suspected upper gastrointestinal sources presenting as melena, brief risk stratification using scores like the Rockall or Blatchford helps predict rebleeding or need for intervention, incorporating factors such as age, comorbidities, and vital signs without requiring endoscopy.

Laboratory and Imaging Tests

Laboratory evaluation begins with a complete blood count (CBC) to assess hemoglobin levels for anemia, which indicates the extent of blood loss, and platelet count to screen for coagulopathy that may contribute to bleeding. Coagulation studies, including prothrombin time (PT)/international normalized ratio (INR) and activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT), are routinely performed to detect underlying bleeding disorders such as coagulopathy or anticoagulant effects.
Blood chemistry analysis, particularly the blood urea nitrogen (BUN) to creatinine ratio, aids in distinguishing upper from lower gastrointestinal bleeding; ratios greater than 30 suggest an upper source due to absorption and digestion of blood proteins in the upper tract.
For detecting occult blood in stool, the fecal immunochemical test (FIT) is the preferred method over the guaiac-based fecal occult blood test (gFOBT), offering higher sensitivity (79-92% for colorectal cancer) and specificity (90-96%), along with no need for dietary restrictions, making it more patient-friendly for screening. According to 2025 updates from colorectal cancer screening guidelines, FIT is emphasized as the superior stool-based test for early detection of colorectal neoplasia.
Stool studies, including culture and polymerase chain reaction (PCR) assays, are recommended when infectious colitis is suspected, such as in cases with recent travel or bloody diarrhea, to identify bacterial pathogens like Campylobacter, Salmonella, or Shigella.
Imaging modalities provide non-invasive localization of bleeding sources. Computed tomography (CT) angiography is effective for identifying active extravasation in hemodynamically unstable patients, demonstrating sensitivity exceeding 90% for bleeding rates above 0.3 mL/min. Capsule endoscopy is the first-line test for obscure small bowel bleeding after negative upper and lower endoscopy, with a diagnostic yield of about 60% for identifying vascular lesions or ulcers.

Endoscopic Evaluation

Endoscopic evaluation plays a crucial role in directly visualizing the gastrointestinal tract to identify and characterize sources of bleeding in patients with blood in stool, allowing for biopsy and potential immediate intervention. These procedures are typically pursued after initial stabilization and non-invasive assessments, guided by clinical presentation such as the color and volume of blood, which may suggest upper or lower gastrointestinal origins.
Upper endoscopy, or esophagogastroduodenoscopy (EGD), is the procedure of choice for suspected upper gastrointestinal sources of bleeding, such as peptic ulcers or esophageal varices, enabling direct visualization of the esophagus, stomach, and duodenum. It facilitates biopsy of suspicious lesions and assessment of high-risk features like active bleeding or visible vessels. According to the 2021 American College of Gastroenterology (ACG) guidelines, EGD should be performed within 24 hours of presentation in most patients with upper GI bleeding to optimize outcomes.
For lower gastrointestinal bleeding, colonoscopy serves as the gold standard diagnostic tool, providing comprehensive visualization of the colon and terminal ileum to detect common etiologies including diverticula, polyps, vascular malformations, and colorectal cancer. It offers therapeutic capabilities, such as polypectomy for bleeding polyps or hemostasis for active sites. The 2023 ACG guidelines endorse colonoscopy as the preferred initial endoscopic approach for hemodynamically stable patients with lower GI bleeding, noting its high diagnostic utility.
In urgent scenarios or when full bowel preparation is not feasible, flexible sigmoidoscopy may be employed to evaluate the distal colon and rectum, particularly for bright red rectal bleeding suggestive of anorectal or sigmoid sources like hemorrhoids or ischemic colitis. This limited-scope procedure allows rapid assessment in unstable patients but misses proximal lesions.
When bleeding is suspected in the small bowel, such as angiodysplasia or Meckel's diverticulum after negative upper and lower endoscopy, capsule endoscopy or device-assisted enteroscopy (e.g., balloon-assisted) is utilized for deeper evaluation. Capsule endoscopy, a non-invasive wireless camera pill, offers a diagnostic yield of approximately 60% for obscure small bowel bleeding, while enteroscopy enables both diagnosis and therapy like clip placement. The American Society for Gastrointestinal Endoscopy (ASGE) recommends capsule endoscopy as the first-line test for suspected small bowel sources in stable patients.
Timing of endoscopy is critical: for hemodynamically unstable patients with significant lower GI bleeding, urgent colonoscopy within 24 hours is recommended per the 2023 ACG guidelines to facilitate early source identification and intervention. In upper GI bleeding, early endoscopy (within 12-24 hours) is similarly advised for those with signs of hemodynamic compromise.
Complications of endoscopic procedures are generally low but include perforation, with risks ranging from 0.03% to 0.2% for diagnostic colonoscopy and up to 0.5% in therapeutic cases, particularly in elderly patients or those with diverticulosis. Sedation-related issues, such as respiratory depression or aspiration, occur in about 0.5-1% of procedures, necessitating monitoring by trained personnel.
The diagnostic yield of colonoscopy in lower GI bleeding varies but commonly exceeds 80% when performed after adequate preparation, successfully identifying the bleeding source in the majority of cases and guiding subsequent management. For upper endoscopy in overt upper GI bleeding, the yield approaches 90-95% for lesion detection.

Management

Supportive Care

Supportive care for blood in stool, typically manifesting as hematochezia from lower gastrointestinal bleeding, focuses on immediate stabilization to prevent complications such as hypovolemic shock and organ failure, while preparing for diagnostic evaluation. Initial hemodynamic resuscitation is critical for patients showing signs of instability, such as tachycardia, hypotension, or altered mental status; this involves administering intravenous crystalloid fluids, such as normal saline or lactated Ringer's solution, through at least two large-bore peripheral intravenous lines (18-gauge or larger) to restore intravascular volume and normalize vital signs prior to further interventions.
Blood transfusion is indicated for significant anemia, with packed red blood cells transfused to maintain a hemoglobin level above 7 g/dL in most hemodynamically stable patients, or higher (around 9 g/dL) in cases of massive bleeding, severe comorbidities like cardiovascular disease, or when endoscopy is delayed. For ongoing or severe bleeding, monitoring in an intensive care unit is recommended, including continuous vital sign assessment and serial hemoglobin measurements every 6-12 hours to detect rebleeding early.
In patients on anticoagulants or antiplatelet agents, reversal of coagulopathy is essential to minimize bleeding risk; for warfarin, prefer prothrombin complex concentrates (PCC) over fresh frozen plasma, with vitamin K as adjunct; for direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs), use idarucizumab for dabigatran or andexanet alfa for factor Xa inhibitors in life-threatening bleeding if last dose within 24 hours; platelet transfusions if counts fall below 50,000/L. For chronic blood loss leading to iron-deficiency anemia, oral iron supplementation is the first-line therapy, with intravenous iron (e.g., ferric carboxymaltose) preferred in cases of poor oral tolerance, nonadherence, or ongoing bleeding to rapidly replete stores and avoid transfusions. Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents like erythropoietin may be considered in select cases of refractory anemia, particularly perioperative settings or with comorbidities limiting transfusion.
To facilitate endoscopic evaluation, bowel preparation with 4-6 liters of polyethylene glycol-based solutions over 3-4 hours is advised once hemodynamic stability is achieved, aiming for clear rectal effluent; a nasogastric tube can assist if the patient cannot tolerate oral intake. In chronic cases associated with malabsorptionsuch as from inflammatory bowel diseasenutritional support involves enteral feeding with protein-enriched formulas (1.2-1.5 g/kg/day) and micronutrient supplementation (e.g., vitamins B12, D, and iron) to address deficiencies and promote recovery. Patients are kept nil per os initially during acute phases to reduce aspiration risk, transitioning to supportive nutrition as tolerated.

Cause-Specific Interventions

Treatments for blood in stool are tailored to the underlying etiology identified through diagnostic evaluation. For upper gastrointestinal sources, such as peptic ulcers, pre-endoscopic management includes consideration of high-dose proton pump inhibitors (PPIs), though routine use is not strongly recommended; post-endoscopy, high-dose PPIs like pantoprazole or omeprazole (80 mg bolus followed by 8 mg/hour infusion) are standard to reduce gastric acid, stabilize clots, and promote healing, decreasing rebleeding rates by up to 50% in high-risk cases. For actively bleeding ulcers or those with high-risk stigmata (e.g., visible vessel, adherent clot), endoscopic hemostasis using mechanical (clips) or thermal (heater probe, bipolar electrocoagulation) methods is recommended, achieving initial hemostasis in 85-95% of cases; epinephrine injection should be combined with another modality. All patients with peptic ulcer bleeding should be tested for Helicobacter pylori (e.g., urea breath test after PPI cessation) and receive eradication therapy if positive, using regimens like bismuth quadruple therapy to prevent recurrence. For esophageal varices, endoscopic variceal ligation (EVL) using rubber band banding achieves immediate hemostasis in over 90% of cases and is repeated every 2-4 weeks until obliteration, often combined with vasoactive agents like terlipressin or octreotide. In refractory cases unresponsive to endoscopic therapy, surgical interventions such as oversewing of bleeding sites or portosystemic shunting may be required, particularly when hemodynamic instability persists.
For lower gastrointestinal sources, initial conservative measures for common benign causes like hemorrhoids include high-fiber diet (25-35 g/day), stool softeners, sitz baths, and topical agents (e.g., hydrocortisone or witch hazel) to relieve symptoms and promote healing; these are first-line for grade I-III internal hemorrhoids. For persistent or severe hemorrhoidal bleeding, rubber band ligation effectively controls acute episodes in 70-90% of patients by interrupting blood supply, or sclerotherapy involving injection of a sclerosant like polidocanol to shrink vascular cushions. Diverticular bleeding, a frequent cause of acute lower GI hemorrhage, is initially managed supportively with resuscitation and bowel prep; colonoscopy within 24-48 hours can identify stigmata of recent hemorrhage, treated endoscopically with clips or band ligation (rebleeding reduction to 8-9% early vs. 19% untreated), while massive bleeding may require CT angiography followed by embolization (technical success 98%). Colonic polyps causing bleeding undergo polypectomy during colonoscopy, with snare resection achieving complete removal and hemostasis in most sessile or pedunculated lesions, reducing recurrence risk. Infectious colitis, such as that due to Clostridium difficile, is treated with targeted antibiotics; oral vancomycin (125 mg four times daily for 10 days) is first-line for non-severe cases, eradicating the pathogen and resolving bloody diarrhea in approximately 85% of patients.
Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) manifesting as rectal bleeding requires etiology-specific pharmacotherapy; 5-aminosalicylic acid (5-ASA) compounds, such as mesalamine (2.4-4.8 g daily orally or rectally), serve as first-line anti-inflammatories for mild-to-moderate ulcerative colitis, inducing remission in 40-80% of cases by reducing mucosal inflammation. For moderate-to-severe Crohn's disease or refractory ulcerative colitis, biologic agents targeting tumor necrosis factor-alpha (anti-TNF), including infliximab (5 mg/kg infusion at weeks 0, 2, and 6) or adalimumab (160 mg initial dose), achieve clinical response in 60-70% of patients within 12 weeks, often combined with immunomodulators. Surgical options, such as colectomy for fulminant ulcerative colitis or segmental resection for localized Crohn's complications, are reserved for medical failures, with restorative proctocolectomy providing definitive control in up to 90% of selected cases.
Colorectal cancer presenting with rectal bleeding follows staging-guided multimodal therapy; surgical resection via low anterior resection or abdominoperineal resection removes the tumor and affected margins, achieving 5-year survival rates of 65-90% for stages I-II. Adjuvant chemotherapy with regimens like FOLFOX (folinic acid, fluorouracil, oxaliplatin) is standard for stage III disease, reducing recurrence by 30%, while neoadjuvant chemoradiation (45-50 Gy radiation with 5-fluorouracil) is preferred for locally advanced rectal cancer to downstage tumors and control bleeding symptoms prior to surgery.
Vascular etiologies, including angiodysplasia, are addressed endoscopically with argon plasma coagulation when feasible, or with angiographic embolization using coils or particles to occlude feeding vessels, achieving hemostasis in 80-95% of acute lower GI bleeds refractory to endoscopy. For variceal bleeding, octreotide (50 mcg IV bolus followed by 50 mcg/hour infusion) reduces portal pressure and transfusion requirements by 40-50%, serving as adjunctive therapy to endoscopic banding.
As of 2025, advancements in endoscopic hemostasis include over-the-scope clips (OTSC), such as the Ovesco system, which provide full-thickness closure for refractory non-variceal bleeds, demonstrating technical success rates of 95% and clinical success in 93% of cases, outperforming standard clips in high-risk lesions up to 20 mm. For IBD, targeted therapies have evolved with next-generation biologics like vedolizumab (anti-integrin, 300 mg IV at weeks 0, 2, 6) and Janus kinase inhibitors such as tofacitinib (10 mg twice daily), offering remission induction in 40-60% of anti-TNF failures through gut-selective mechanisms.
Post-treatment follow-up includes surveillance colonoscopy, recommended 3-6 months after hemostatic intervention for high-risk adenomas or incomplete exams to detect residual lesions, with intervals extending to 3 years for low-risk findings per U.S. Multi-Society Task Force guidelines.
