 New Hampshire is a state in the New England region of the northeastern United States, one of the thirteen original colonies, bordered by Vermont to the west across the Connecticut River, Massachusetts to the south, Maine and the Atlantic Ocean to the east with only 13 miles of coastline, and the Canadian province of Quebec to the north. Its area spans 9,351 square miles, making it the ninth-smallest state by land area, while its population stood at 1,409,032 as of July 1, 2024. The capital is Concord, and Manchester is the largest city with over 115,000 residents. Known as the Granite State for its abundant granite formations and quarries, New Hampshire adopted the motto "Live Free or Die" in 1945, originating from a Revolutionary War toast by General John Stark, reflecting its emphasis on individual liberty.
Geographically diverse, the state features the rugged White Mountains in the north, including Mount Washington as the highest peak in the Northeast at 6,288 feet, expansive lakes such as Lake Winnipesaukee, and forested terrain covering about 85% of its land, supporting tourism and outdoor recreation as key economic drivers. New Hampshire holds historical significance as the site of the first presidential primary in the nation, a tradition dating to 1920 that gives its voters outsized influence in selecting major party nominees, though the state law mandates it occur at least seven days before any other similar contest.
The state's economy benefits from no broad-based state income tax or sales tax, attracting businesses in manufacturing, high-technology sectors, and services, with a low overall tax burden contributing to robust growth, including a 4.5% GDP expansion rate in the third quarter of 2023. This fiscal policy aligns with New Hampshire's reputation for limited government intervention, fostering diverse industries while maintaining high per capita income relative to national averages.

Geography

Terrain and landforms

New Hampshire's terrain is characterized by a narrow coastal plain along its 18-mile Atlantic shoreline, giving way inland to rolling hills of the Piedmont province, fertile river valleys, and the rugged peaks of the White Mountains in the north. The state's total land area measures 9,349 square miles, with elevations ranging from sea level to 6,288 feet at Mount Washington. This diversity arises from Appalachian geological formations, including metamorphosed sedimentary and volcanic rocks shaped by ancient tectonic forces and subsequent glaciation, which carved valleys, lakes, and U-shaped troughs across the landscape.
The southeastern coastal lowlands feature flat to gently undulating terrain with sandy beaches, dunes, and tidal marshes, transitioning abruptly to the Eastern New England Uplands, a piedmont-like region of moderate hills and plateaus. Prominent in this area is the Monadnock region, named after Mount Monadnock, an isolated granite monadnock rising 3,165 feet, which exemplifies erosional remnants resistant to weathering. Further west, the Merrimack River valley forms a broad lowland conducive to agriculture, while the Connecticut River delineates the western border, creating a narrow alluvial plain flanked by steeper slopes.
Dominating the northern third, the White Mountainspart of the Northern Appalachian rangeencompass dense forests, steep ravines, cirques, and over 1,500 named summits, with approximately 50 peaks exceeding 4,000 feet. The Presidential Range, including Mount Washington in the White Mountain National Forest, hosts the highest concentrations of elevation, with subsidiary ridges like the Franconia and Twin ranges featuring narrow cols and glacial features such as tarns and hanging valleys. Glacial activity during the Pleistocene left widespread drumlins, eskers, and kettle lakes, notably in the Lakes Region around Lake Winnipesaukee, New Hampshire's largest lake at 71 square miles. Major rivers, including the Merrimack (110 miles long) and Connecticut (407 miles, forming the border), have incised valleys that enhance the state's hydrological landforms.

Climate and environment

New Hampshire experiences a humid continental climate, characterized by cold, snowy winters and warm, humid summers, with significant variation due to elevation and proximity to the Atlantic Ocean. Statewide average annual temperatures have increased by approximately 3F since the early 20th century, with greater warming observed in winter months. Annual precipitation averages about 43 inches, including roughly 70 inches of snowfall in southern areas and over 100 inches in northern mountainous regions, with recent decades showing a 7-20% increase since 1970. In Concord, the state capital, January highs average 32F and lows 13F, while July highs reach 83F and lows 59F.
The state's climate is moderated by the Gulf Stream to the southeast, which brings milder conditions to coastal areas, while the White Mountains in the north amplify colder temperatures and heavier snowfall through orographic lift. Summers are influenced by continental air masses, leading to occasional heat waves, though moderated by afternoon thunderstorms. Winters feature frequent nor'eastersintense low-pressure systems originating off the Atlanticthat deliver heavy snow, gale-force winds, and coastal flooding; these storms have caused significant impacts, such as widespread power outages and infrastructure damage in events like the 2025 weekend nor'easter affecting the Seacoast region. Remnants of tropical hurricanes occasionally bring extreme rainfall, exacerbating inland flooding, which has emerged as the primary climate-related hazard in recent years due to more intense precipitation events.
Environmentally, New Hampshire is dominated by temperate forests covering about 85% of its land area, supporting diverse ecosystems including northern hardwoods and spruce-fir stands in higher elevations. The state features over 800 lakes and ponds, such as Lake Winnipesaukee, and approximately 19,000 miles of rivers and streams, which provide critical habitats and recreational resources but are vulnerable to seasonal fluctuations. Wetlands play a key role in flood mitigation and carbon storage, though development pressures threaten their resilience amid rising precipitation.
Water quality challenges stem primarily from nonpoint source pollution, with over 90% of issues linked to stormwater runoff carrying sediments, nutrients, road salts, and contaminants like PFAS into surface waters. Cyanobacteria blooms in lakes have increased due to warmer temperatures and nutrient loading, prompting fish consumption advisories in affected areas such as southern New Hampshire lakes with elevated PFOS levels. Observed shifts include declining inorganic nitrogen deposition but rising organic forms in precipitation, reflecting changes in emissions and land use. Conservation efforts focus on maintaining forest cover and addressing runoff, as these ecosystems buffer against observed increases in extreme precipitation and associated erosion.

Flora, fauna, and natural resources

New Hampshire's forests cover approximately 84% of the state's land area, ranking it as the second most forested state in the United States after Maine. These forests encompass about 4.7 million acres, with timberland capable of sustained production spanning 4.6 million acres. Dominant forest types include northern hardwoods (such as sugar maple, beech, and yellow birch), eastern white pine, spruce-fir, eastern hemlock, red oak, and aspen-birch associations. The eastern white pine serves as the state tree, historically significant for shipbuilding masts during the colonial era.
The state's flora supports diverse natural communities, ranging from widespread upland forests to rarer wetland and coastal types, though specific plant species inventories highlight common understory elements like ferns, mosses, and wildflowers adapted to acidic soils in coniferous stands.
Wildlife in New Hampshire includes prominent mammals such as the white-tailed deer (official state animal since 1983), eastern moose (estimated population around 7,000 as of recent surveys), American black bear, bobcat, fisher, coyote, red fox, and gray fox. Avian species abound, with common sightings of wild turkey, ruffed grouse, and various warblers; reptiles and amphibians include timber rattlesnake (threatened), eastern milk snake, blue-spotted salamander, and slimy salamander. Aquatic fauna features brook trout and other freshwater fish in the state's rivers and lakes. Extirpated mammals include gray wolf, cougar, wolverine, and caribou, reflecting historical habitat pressures from settlement and hunting.
Forestry represents a key natural resource, with the industry generating $1.4 billion annually and employing over 7,700 workers, rooted in practices dating to 1634. Mineral extraction focuses on nonfuel materials, including crushed stone (primarily granite and traprock), construction sand and gravel, dimension stone (chiefly granite), and natural gemstones. Granite quarrying, concentrated in areas like the Conway region, supplies building materials domestically. While hydroelectric power was historically prominent, current resource emphasis lies in sustainable timber harvesting and aggregate production rather than large-scale energy generation.

Settlements and metropolitan areas

New Hampshire exhibits a settlement pattern characterized by dispersed small towns and villages, with urban development concentrated in the southern and southwestern regions proximate to the Massachusetts border. This distribution stems from historical industrialization along rivers and rail lines, as well as modern commuting to the Boston metropolitan economy, resulting in higher population densities in Hillsborough and Rockingham counties. Approximately 37% of the state's residents live in rural areas, while 84% of the land area qualifies as rural, underscoring the predominance of unincorporated and low-density townships in the north and east.
The state's largest municipalities include Manchester, with an estimated 2024 population of 116,386, functioning as the primary industrial and commercial hub; Nashua, at 91,851, known for technology and manufacturing sectors; and the capital Concord, with 44,674 inhabitants, serving administrative roles. Dover (33,909) and Rochester (33,598) represent coastal and mid-sized urban centers, while towns like Derry (34,062) contribute to suburban growth. New Hampshire distinguishes between 13 cities and 221 towns, the latter governed by annual town meetings, fostering localized decision-making over expansive urban bureaucracies.

History

Indigenous peoples and early settlement

The territory comprising modern New Hampshire was occupied by Indigenous peoples for millennia prior to European arrival, with archaeological evidence of human presence extending back at least 11,000 years. These groups, primarily Algonquian-language speakers, included the Pennacook (also known as Penacook) who inhabited southern regions along the Merrimack River and extending into southeast New Hampshire and adjacent areas of Maine and Massachusetts, as well as the Western Abenaki who occupied northern and western parts of the state. The Pennacook formed a confederacy of subtribes, while the Abenaki were part of the broader Wabanaki alliance, with both peoples maintaining seasonal villages and engaging in trade networks across the Northeast. 
Pre-contact subsistence relied on hunting large game like deer and moose, fishing in rivers and coastal waters, gathering wild edibles, and small-scale agriculture of crops including maize, beans, and squash in cleared riverine plots. Communities were semi-sedentary, with winter longhouses and summer wigwams, and social organization centered on kinship bands led by sachems; women handled farming and gathering, while men focused on hunting and warfare. European contact, beginning with intermittent visits by fishermen and explorers such as Martin Pring in 1603, introduced devastating epidemicsprimarily smallpox, measles, and influenzafor which Indigenous populations lacked immunity, causing mortality rates estimated at 50-90% across New England by the mid-17th century and severely disrupting societies before widespread settlement. 
The first permanent European settlement occurred in 1623, when Scottish trader David Thomson, acting under a land grant from Captain John Mason, established a fishing outpost at Odiorne's Point in present-day Rye with a small group of English colonists. This was followed by additional outposts, including the Piscataqua Plantation (later Portsmouth) founded in 1630 by settlers from the Massachusetts Bay Colony, and Exeter in 1638 by Reverend John Wheelwright's group of religious dissenters. These early ventures focused on fishing, timber, and fur trade, often involving alliances with local Indigenous bands for resources, but escalating land pressures and cultural clashes led to sporadic conflicts, such as raids during the broader Anglo-Powhatan and later Beaver Wars, contributing to Pennacook dispersal northward by the late 1600s. By the close of the 17th century, European numbers had grown to several hundred, while Indigenous populations in the region had plummeted due to disease and displacement, setting the stage for colonial expansion.

Colonial era and American Revolution

The first permanent English settlements in the region that became New Hampshire were established in 1623. Brothers Edward and William Hilton founded a fishing and trading outpost at Hilton Point (present-day Dover Neck in Dover), commissioned by London merchants, marking the earliest sustained European presence. Concurrently, Scottish settler David Thomson established Pannaway Manor at Odiorne's Point in Rye, though it proved short-lived. These outposts focused on fishing, fur trading with Indigenous peoples, and exploiting the abundant timber resources of the Piscataqua River area.
Initially administered as part of the Massachusetts Bay Colony under proprietary grants to John Mason and Sir Ferdinando Gorges, the territory was formally named New Hampshire in 1629 after Mason's ancestral county in England. Conflicts over governance led to its separation; on September 18, 1679, King Charles II issued a royal charter establishing the Province of New Hampshire as a distinct royal colony, appointing John Cutt as its first president. The provincial economy expanded through fishing (notably cod and herring), lumber production for masts and barrels, shipbuilding at Portsmouth, and small-scale agriculture yielding corn, wheat, dairy, and maple products, supplemented by beaver pelt exports. 
As imperial tensions escalated, New Hampshirites resisted British policies, exemplified by the December 1415, 1774, raid on Fort William and Mary in New Castle, where patriots under Paul Revere's warning seized 100 barrels of gunpowder and arms, which were later used at the Battle of Bunker Hill. Following the April 19, 1775, Battles of Lexington and Concord, New Hampshire militia rapidly mobilized, contributing regiments to the Siege of Boston and subsequent campaigns. On January 5, 1776, the New Hampshire Provincial Congress adopted a resolution declaring independence from Britainthe first colony to do soand enacted the Continental Association's non-importation measures into law, effectively establishing a new state government.
New Hampshire delegates Josiah Bartlett, William Whipple, and Matthew Thornton signed the July 4, 1776, Declaration of Independence, reflecting broad provincial support for separation. Although no major battles occurred within its borders, over 2,000 New Hampshire troops served in Continental Army units, participating in key engagements like the Battle of Saratoga (1777) and General John Stark's victory at Bennington (August 16, 1777), which bolstered American morale and supply lines. The state's commitment extended to ratifying the U.S. Constitution on June 21, 1788, as the ninth state, securing its adoption.

19th century industrialization

New Hampshire's industrialization accelerated in the early 19th century, transitioning the state from a predominantly agrarian economy reliant on farming and small-scale milling to one centered on factory-based manufacturing, particularly textiles powered by abundant water resources from rivers like the Merrimack and Connecticut. This shift was facilitated by the availability of water power sites, which dotted the landscape and enabled the construction of mills for processing cotton, wool, and other fibers to serve growing domestic and export markets. By the 1820s, textile production had become the state's dominant industry, with facilities ranging from small, localized operations to expansive complexes that employed wage labor drawn from rural areas and later immigrants.
Pioneering textile mills emerged as early as the 1800s in locations such as New Ipswich, where the first mechanized operations in the state processed wool and cotton using water-driven machinery imported or adapted from British designs. The Amoskeag Manufacturing Company exemplified this growth, originating from a modest cotton mill constructed by Benjamin Prichard at Amoskeag Falls in 1804 and formalized as a joint-stock venture in 1831, which by mid-century had developed into a vast millyard in Manchester producing yarns, fabrics, and finished goods on an unprecedented scale. At its height in the late 19th century, the Amoskeag complex spanned multiple buildings along the Merrimack River, incorporating innovations in machinery and vertical integrationfrom spinning to weaving and dyeingthat made it one of the largest cotton textile producers globally, with operations peaking before economic pressures mounted in the 20th century. Similar mills proliferated in towns like Dover, Nashua, and Concord, where proximity to railroads from the 1840s onward enhanced raw material imports and product distribution, further entrenching manufacturing as the economic core.
Railroad expansion complemented this manufacturing surge, with lines like the Boston & Maine connecting rural mills to urban markets and ports by the 1850s, reducing transport costs and enabling bulk shipments of textiles and machinery components. By the century's end, railroads had become New Hampshire's largest employer, supporting ancillary industries such as iron foundries and machine shops that supplied equipment to textile operations, though overbuilding led to consolidations and financial strains. This infrastructure boom drew labor inflows, including French-Canadian migrants to Manchester's mills, sustaining output amid agricultural decline as rocky soils and soil exhaustion pushed farmers toward factory work. Environmental impacts included river channel alterations for dams and canals, which boosted power generation but degraded habitats and water quality through waste discharge from dye houses and scouring processes. Despite these transformations, the state's industrialization remained regionally concentrated, with textiles accounting for over half of manufacturing value by 1900, underscoring New Hampshire's integration into broader New England patterns of water-powered, export-oriented production.

20th century developments

The textile and manufacturing industries that had driven New Hampshire's economy in the 19th century began a prolonged decline in the early 20th century, as southern states offered lower labor costs and newer facilities, leading to mill closures and job losses particularly in Manchester and Nashua by the 1920s. Labor unrest emerged during this period, with strikes reflecting worker frustrations amid falling employment in traditional sectors. The Great Depression exacerbated these challenges, with widespread farm abandonment and urban unemployment, though federal relief programs and the development of winter tourismfacilitated by improved roads and ski infrastructureprovided some economic diversification starting in the 1930s.
During World War II, remaining mills received federal contracts for wartime production, sustaining employment and averting deeper collapse, while New Hampshire residents contributed through military service and civilian efforts. In July 1944, the Bretton Woods Conference convened at the Mount Washington Hotel, where delegates from 44 nations established the International Monetary Fund and World Bank, laying foundations for the post-war global financial system pegged to the U.S. dollar and gold standard. Post-war recovery accelerated with population growth from 491,524 in 1940 to 737,681 by 1970, driven by suburban expansion and influxes from neighboring states, alongside a shift toward electronics, machinery, and defense-related manufacturing that replaced textiles.
New Hampshire's political landscape evolved with its 1916 law formalizing the nation's first binding presidential primary, which gained national prominence by mid-century as candidates increasingly prioritized the state for early voter feedback. By the late 20th century, the economy further diversified into services, high-technology sectors like semiconductors, and outdoor recreation, with tourism drawing visitors to the White Mountains and Lakes Region; manufacturing employment peaked then stabilized, reflecting adaptation to global competition rather than outright revival of old industries. These changes supported steady per capita income growth, though rural areas lagged behind urban centers like the Seacoast Region in job opportunities.

Recent history (post-2000)

New Hampshire experienced steady population growth in the post-2000 era, increasing from 1,235,786 residents in 2000 to 1,409,032 by 2024, a rise of approximately 14 percent driven primarily by net domestic migration attracted by the state's absence of income and sales taxes. This influx, particularly from high-tax states like Massachusetts and New York, contributed to housing pressures and economic expansion but also sparked debates over infrastructure capacity.
Politically, the state alternated between Democratic and Republican governors: Democrat Jeanne Shaheen (19972003), Republican Craig Benson (20032005), Democrat John Lynch (20052013), Democrat Maggie Hassan (20132017), and Republican Chris Sununu (20172025). In the 2024 election, Republican Kelly Ayotte succeeded Sununu, defeating Democrat Joyce Craig to become governor on January 9, 2025. New Hampshire retained its first-in-the-nation presidential primary status, shaping national campaigns; notable examples include Barack Obama's 2008 victory aiding his nomination and Donald Trump's 2016 win boosting his momentum. The legislature shifted Republican in 2010 and 2016, reflecting fiscal conservatism, though Sununu vetoed over 100 bills during his tenure to moderate policy extremes. In the 2024 presidential race, Kamala Harris carried the state with a narrow margin over Donald Trump, continuing New Hampshire's trend of Democratic presidential votes since 1992 despite Republican gains elsewhere.
Economically, New Hampshire's GDP reached $84 billion in 2022, ranking 44th nationally, with diversification into services, manufacturing, and tourism offsetting manufacturing declines. Unemployment remained low, averaging below the national rate, bolstered by high-tech sectors in Nashua and Portsmouth. The state legalized same-sex marriage in 2010 via legislation, one of the first to do so without court mandate.
Natural disasters included the 2006 Mother's Day flood causing $200 million in damage, Tropical Storm Irene in 2011 leading to widespread evacuations, and Hurricane Sandy in 2012. During the COVID-19 pandemic, Governor Sununu implemented targeted restrictions, achieving one of the lowest per-capita death rates in the Northeast while prioritizing economic reopening, drawing both praise for pragmatism and criticism from public health advocates. By 2025, the opioid crisis persisted, with overdose deaths peaking at 500 annually in 2017 before declining due to expanded treatment access.

Demographics

Population trends and distribution

As of the 2020 United States Census, New Hampshire had a population of 1,377,529 residents. U.S. Census Bureau estimates indicate the state's population grew to 1,409,032 by July 1, 2024, reflecting a 2.3% increase from the 2020 base. This growth equates to approximately 31,500 additional residents over four years, primarily driven by net domestic in-migration exceeding natural increase, as births have lagged behind deaths in recent years. From 2010 to 2020, the population rose by 4.6%, from 1,316,470 to 1,377,529, continuing a pattern of modest expansion fueled by inflows from higher-tax neighboring states like Massachusetts and New York.
Historically, New Hampshire's population growth has accelerated since the mid-20th century, shifting from stagnation in the early 1900s to annual rates averaging around 0.5% in the 2010s and 2020s. The state experienced a 6.5% increase from 2010 to 2024, reaching about 1.4 million, with nine of ten counties posting gains between 2020 and 2024, though urban centers like Manchester saw relative stagnation or minor declines amid suburban and rural expansion. Growth has been uneven, with lakes region and mountain areas leading due to remote work trends post-2020, while traditional industrial cities faced outflows.
New Hampshire maintains a population density of about 159 persons per square mile as of 2020, ranking moderately among states despite its small size and rural character. Over two-thirds of residents live in urban areas, concentrated in the southwestern corridor along the Massachusetts border, including the Manchester-Nashua metropolitan area (population approximately 420,000 in 2020) and the Portsmouth-Rochester area. The northern and eastern regions remain sparsely populated, with densities below 50 per square mile in Coos County, the least populous at around 30,000 residents. This distribution underscores a divide between densely settled southern suburbs tied to Boston's commuter belt and the more isolated, slower-growing North Country.

Ethnic composition and immigration

According to the 2020 United States Census, New Hampshire's population of 1,377,529 was composed of 87.2% non-Hispanic White residents, 4.3% Hispanic or Latino of any race, 2.6% Asian, 1.4% Black or African American, 0.6% American Indian and Alaska Native, 0.1% Native Hawaiian and Other Pacific Islander, and 3.8% identifying as two or more races. This places New Hampshire among the states with the highest proportions of non-Hispanic White residents, ranking fourth nationally at 87.2%. Recent estimates indicate modest shifts, with non-Hispanic Whites at approximately 88.5% in 2023, Hispanics at 4.8%, Asians at around 3%, and Blacks at 1.6%, reflecting gradual diversification primarily among younger cohorts where 20.2% of those under 18 identified as racial or ethnic minorities in 2020.
European ancestries dominate self-reported heritage, with significant portions tracing roots to English (18%), Irish (19.4%), French (including French Canadian at 14.6%), French Canadian specifically, German, Italian, and Scottish origins, reflecting waves of 19th- and early 20th-century immigration from Britain, Ireland, Quebec, and continental Europe. These patterns underscore New Hampshire's historical settlement by English Puritans and later industrial-era migrants drawn to textile mills and manufacturing in cities like Manchester and Nashua.
The state's foreign-born population remains low relative to national averages, comprising about 5.9% to 7.1% of residents or roughly 82,000 to 94,000 individuals as of recent data through 2023-2024, with primary origins including Canada, India, China, and Bhutanese refugees. Immigration has contributed to recent population gains, particularly post-2020, helping offset native outflows amid housing constraints and economic pressures, though undocumented estimates are minimal at around 15,000. Unlike more diverse states, New Hampshire's immigration is characterized by skilled workers in tech and healthcare sectors rather than large-scale low-wage labor inflows, aligning with its emphasis on self-reliance and limited welfare entitlements.

Religion and cultural affiliations

New Hampshire exhibits one of the lowest levels of religious adherence in the United States, with 48% of adults identifying as religiously unaffiliated. This figure exceeds the national average and reflects broader secularization trends in New England, where only 15% of adults report being highly religious, defined by frequent prayer, church attendance, and strong belief in God. Among the religiously affiliated, Christians constitute 45% of adults, predominantly Protestants and Catholics. Evangelical Protestants account for about 10%, mainline Protestants another 10%, while Catholics represent roughly 20% of Christians (approximately 9% of adults), though church adherent data from 2020 indicates around 220,000 Catholics, or 16% of the state's population. Non-Christian faiths, including Judaism (<1%), Islam (<1%), Buddhism (<1%), and Hinduism (<1%), comprise about 5% combined, with smaller groups like Unitarians also present.
Cultural affiliations in New Hampshire emphasize individualism, self-reliance, and a libertarian ethos encapsulated in the state motto "Live Free or Die," adopted in 1945, which fosters skepticism toward institutional authority, including organized religion. This aligns with the state's historical Yankee Protestant rootsoriginally Congregationalist and Baptist influences from early settlersbut has evolved amid 19th- and 20th-century immigration waves of Irish, French-Canadian, and Italian Catholics, who established parishes and festivals preserving ethnic traditions like Franco-American Acadian heritage in northern counties. Occupational and folk traditions, such as maple sugaring, quilting, and maritime crafts among coastal communities, reinforce community bonds outside formal religious structures, often documented through state folklife programs. Political culture leans toward fiscal conservatism and limited government, correlating with lower religiosity; for instance, PRRI data shows 52% white Christians but high unaffiliated rates among younger demographics, indicating ongoing shifts away from traditional affiliations.

Economy

Economic overview and GDP

New Hampshire's economy is characterized by a mix of manufacturing, services, and tourism, bolstered by the state's absence of a broad-based personal income tax and general sales tax, which has historically supported business relocation and retention. In 2023, the state's nominal gross domestic product (GDP) totaled $114.4 billion, reflecting a 7.1% increase from 2022, while real GDP in chained 2017 dollars stood at $93.6 billion. Preliminary data for 2024 indicate real GDP grew to $94.9 billion, a 1.4% annual increase, driven by expansions in professional services and manufacturing amid national economic recovery from prior disruptions.
Per capita real GDP in New Hampshire reached $68,488 in 2024, surpassing the national average and ranking among the higher figures for U.S. states, attributable to a relatively small population of approximately 1.4 million and concentrations of high-value industries. This metric underscores the state's productivity, with per capita personal income at $82,878 for the same year, placing New Hampshire eighth nationally. Economic growth from June 2019 to June 2024 added 28,300 net private sector jobs, a 4.7% expansion, outpacing some neighboring states due to strengths in advanced manufacturing and technology clusters.
The finance, insurance, real estate, rental, and leasing sector contributed the largest share to GDP in 2023, followed by manufacturing and professional/scientific services, reflecting a transition from traditional industries like textiles to high-tech and knowledge-based activities. Tourism and outdoor recreation added $3.9 billion, or 3.4% of GDP, leveraging natural assets such as the White Mountains and Lake Winnipesaukee, though services overall dominate employment at around 80% of the workforce. These dynamics position New Hampshire as economically resilient, with low unemployment around 2.5% in mid-2024, though vulnerabilities to national slowdowns in construction and retail persist.

Major industries and employment

New Hampshire's economy relies on a mix of traditional and advanced sectors, with manufacturing, healthcare and social assistance, tourism, and high-technology industries forming the core. Advanced manufacturing, including electronics, machinery, and composites, benefits from the state's business-friendly policies, such as the absence of a broad-based sales or income tax, fostering competitiveness against higher-tax neighbors. The sector's growth has outpaced regional and national trends, with its share of state jobs 60 percent higher than the U.S. average. Tourism, leveraging the state's natural attractions like the White Mountains and Lake Winnipesaukee, ranks as the second-largest industry, supporting seasonal employment in hospitality and recreation. Healthcare and social assistance has emerged as the dominant employer, driven by an aging population and expanding services, while high-tech clusters in software, life sciences, and research contribute to professional and business services.
Nonfarm employment averaged 704,300 jobs in 2024, reflecting a modest 0.8 percent increase from 2023 amid labor force constraints. Healthcare and social assistance overtook retail trade to become the largest sector by employment in 2024, with projections indicating over 9,100 new jobs through 2032, a nearly 10 percent rise. Manufacturing employed an average of 70,311 workers in 2023, securing its position as the third-largest sector and accounting for roughly 10 percent of total nonfarm jobs. Leisure and hospitality, tied to tourism, sustains significant employment, particularly in rural and northern regions, while professional and business servicesencompassing high-techemploy a substantial portion of the workforce, contributing to low unemployment rates around 2.5 percent in late 2024.

Taxation and fiscal conservatism

New Hampshire maintains one of the most restrictive state tax regimes in the United States, characterized by the absence of a broad-based personal income tax and a general sales tax, policies rooted in a longstanding commitment to fiscal conservatism that prioritizes limited government intervention, individual economic liberty, and libertarian principles embodied in the state motto "Live Free or Die." The state imposes no sales tax on most goods or services, including professional services, with limited exceptions such as taxes on meals and rentals, communications services, and utilities. As of January 1, 2025, the state fully repealed its interest and dividends tax, which had previously applied at a rate of 5 percent (reduced to 3 percent for periods ending after December 31, 2024), leaving New Hampshireas with Alaskathe only state without any form of individual income tax. This structure, sustained through constitutional amendments and voter referenda rejecting broader taxes since the mid-20th century, has historically deterred proposals for income or sales taxes, with political opposition framing such measures as threats to economic migration from neighboring high-tax states like Massachusetts. These policies foster a pro-business environment that has contributed to gross state product growth outpacing that of Vermont, Connecticut, and Rhode Island from 2015 to 2024.
Fiscal conservatism manifests in deliberate revenue constraints and tax reductions, exemplified by phased cuts to business levies under Republican-led administrations. The Business Profits Tax (BPT), imposed at 7.5 percent on net business income for taxable periods ending after December 31, 2023, and the Business Enterprise Tax (BET) at 0.55 percent on the enterprise value tax base, generate significant state revenue but have seen iterative loweringfrom BPT rates of 8.5 percent in 2015 to current levelsreflecting a policy emphasis on enhancing competitiveness. In October 2025, amid revenue shortfalls, Republican legislators advanced proposals to further reduce the BET to 0.5 percent, underscoring ongoing efforts to curb business taxation despite fiscal pressures. These measures align with New Hampshire's sixth-place ranking in the 2025 State Tax Competitiveness Index, which credits the absence of income and sales taxes for fostering business attraction and resident inflows, though critics from left-leaning policy groups argue such cuts exacerbate reliance on regressive alternatives.
The state's revenue model compensates for forgone income and sales taxes through elevated property taxes, which fund local services and the statewide education property tax. New Hampshire's effective property tax rate stands at 1.61 percent of owner-occupied housing value, ranking fourth-highest nationally, with median annual payments of $6,372 as of recent assessments; local rates vary widely, from $11.18 per $1,000 of assessed value in Portsmouth to over $27 in rural towns like Acworth. This dependence on property leviesconstituting about 60 percent of state and local revenuestems from constitutional mandates tying education funding to property wealth, reinforcing fiscal discipline by devolving spending decisions to municipalities while limiting state-level expansion. Governors such as Chris Sununu have championed these policies, signing legislation in 2021 to accelerate the interest and dividends tax phase-out and business tax reductions, earning high marks from conservative analysts for restraining expenditures relative to population growth.

Recent economic challenges (2020s)

New Hampshire's economy experienced a rapid recovery from the initial COVID-19 downturn, with nonfarm employment reaching 709,000 jobs by June 2024, reflecting a 1.3 percent increase from the prior year, though job growth remained constrained by structural labor market limitations. The tourism sector, which lost nearly 17,000 jobs in 2020 due to pandemic restrictions, rebounded but highlighted vulnerabilities in seasonal industries reliant on out-of-state visitors. By 2025, national headwinds such as rising prices and stalled growth compounded local issues, pushing the unemployment rate above 3 percent for the first time in nearly a decade, excluding the brief pandemic spike.
A primary challenge has been a severe housing shortage, exacerbating affordability pressures and hindering workforce expansion. Home purchase costs in the state surged over 275 percent in the quarter-century leading to 2025, outpacing wage growth and creating barriers for families and potential migrants attracted by the absence of state income or sales taxes. Median home prices rose faster than incomes, with low inventory persisting despite modest supply increases, driven by restrictive zoning, environmental regulations, and local opposition to new development. This crisis not only strained household budgets but also deterred businesses from relocating or expanding, as employees struggled to find attainable residences.
Workforce shortages persisted as a core impediment, with only 49 workers available per 100 job openings in 2024, leaving approximately 20,000 positions unfilled compared to pre-pandemic norms. Labor force participation remained below historical levels, influenced by demographic aging, retirements accelerated by the pandemic, and barriers like inadequate child care access, which disproportionately affected female participation. Sectors such as health care, management, and manufacturing faced acute gaps, with 34 occupations projected to have significant shortfalls, limiting economic dynamism despite low overall unemployment.
Additional pressures included elevated energy and child care costs, which eroded disposable income and further constrained household formation and business operations. Inflationary trends, combined with these localized factors, slowed net job gains in 2025, even as the state's fiscal conservatismmanifest in balanced budgets and no broad-based taxesprovided resilience against deeper downturns. These challenges underscored the tension between New Hampshire's policy of limited government intervention and the need for targeted reforms in housing supply and workforce development to sustain long-term growth.

Government and Law

State constitution and branches

The Constitution of New Hampshire, established on October 31, 1783, and effective from June 2, 1784, forms the foundational legal framework of the state. Divided into Part First (Bill of Rights) and Part Second (Form of Government), it outlines individual rights and governmental structure, with subsequent amendments addressing evolving needs such as judicial reforms in 1966 that elevated the Supreme and Superior Courts to constitutional status.
The legislative branch, termed the General Court, exercises supreme legislative authority through a bicameral assembly consisting of the Senate, with 24 members elected from single-member districts, and the House of Representatives, comprising 400 members apportioned by population across towns and wards. Members serve two-year terms without term limits, and the body convenes annually in a part-time capacity, emphasizing citizen participation; the House's size, the largest of any state lower chamber, stems from constitutional provisions amended in 1942 to cap it between 375 and 400 seats.
The executive branch centers on the Governor, elected biennially, who holds powers including commander-in-chief duties and nomination authority, checked by the five-member Executive Council elected from geographic districts. The Council reviews and approves gubernatorial nominations for key positions, state contracts over $10,000, land acquisitions, pardons, and expenditures, functioning as a distinctive institutional restraint on executive discretion not replicated in other states. Unlike most governors, New Hampshire's lacks a general veto over legislation, with bills becoming law upon General Court passage unless reconsidered internally.
The judicial branch operates under a unified system administered by the Supreme Court, which includes a Chief Justice and four Associate Justices nominated by the Governor, confirmed by the Executive Council, and serving until age 70. This court holds appellate jurisdiction over lower courts, including the trial-level Superior Court and Circuit Court divisions handling criminal, civil, family, and probate cases; constitutional mandates ensure separation of powers, with the judiciary independent from legislative and executive influence.

Local governance

New Hampshire's local governance is characterized by the New England town model, emphasizing direct participation through town meetings and decentralized authority. The state comprises 10 counties, 221 towns, and 13 cities, totaling 234 municipalities that handle most public services such as roads, police, fire protection, and planning. Counties play a limited administrative role, primarily overseeing jails, courts, and some welfare functions, with commissioners elected to three-member boards but lacking broad taxing or service-delivery powers; towns and cities assume the majority of local responsibilities.
In towns, which form the bulk of municipalities, the traditional form of government features an open town meeting where registered voters convene annuallytypically in Marchto deliberate and vote on warrant articles including budgets, bylaws, and elected positions. This direct democratic process, rooted in colonial practices, allows residents to amend proposals, question officials, and decide fiscal matters by majority vote, with no binding outcome unless specified in the warrant. A board of selectmen (often termed selectboard), usually comprising three or five elected members serving staggered three-year terms, serves as the executive body, managing day-to-day operations, appointing a town administrator in some cases, and implementing meeting decisions. Variations include the Official Ballot Referendum (SB2) system, adopted by over half of towns, which separates deliberation into a non-binding session from secret-ballot voting on key issues like budgets to accommodate scheduling constraints while retaining town meeting elements.
Cities operate under special charters granted by the state legislature, typically employing mayor-council, mayor-alderman, or council-manager structures rather than town meetings. Mayors, elected directly or by council, oversee executives, while councils or aldermen handle legislative duties; for instance, Manchester, the largest city, uses a mayor-alderman system with a nine-member board. School districts function independently from municipal governments, governed by elected school boards that set policies on curriculum, hiring, and budgets within state mandates, often spanning multiple towns in cooperative arrangements. Recent trends include towns withdrawing from cooperative districts to form independent ones, driven by desires for localized control, with at least a dozen separations approved since 2020 amid debates over efficiency and costs.
Special districts, numbering around 128, address specific needs like water, fire, or village services, governed by elected commissioners and funded via targeted assessments. This fragmented structure fosters fiscal accountability, as voters directly influence expenditures, contributing to New Hampshire's reputation for limited government intervention at the local level.

Unique legal policies

New Hampshire maintains a distinctive fiscal structure by imposing neither a broad-based personal income tax nor a general sales tax on retail transactions, making it the only U.S. state without either levy. This policy, rooted in longstanding resistance to such taxes, relies instead on property taxes, business profits taxes, meals and rooms taxes, and excise taxes for revenue, preserving individual economic liberty amid fiscal conservatism.
In firearms regulation, New Hampshire adopted constitutional carry in 2017 through Senate Bill 12, effective February 22, permitting any person aged 18 or older who is not federally prohibited from possessing a firearm to carry it concealed without a license or permit. This extends to open carry without restrictions in most public places, subject to federal disqualifiers, reflecting a permissive stance absent requirements for licensing, waiting periods, or bans on assault weapons and standard-capacity magazines.
The state uniquely lacks a mandatory seat belt requirement for adults aged 18 and older, positioning it as the sole U.S. jurisdiction without such a primary enforcement law, thereby prioritizing personal responsibility over compelled safety measures. Similarly, motorcycle helmet laws apply only to riders under 18, with no mandate for adults.
New Hampshire's Right-to-Know Law (RSA Chapter 91-A), enacted to ensure "openness in the conduct of public business," mandates broad public access to government records and requires advance notice and minutes for most public meetings, with exemptions narrowly construed to favor transparency over secrecy. This framework, applicable to all branches of state and local government, underscores a commitment to public scrutiny uncommon in its stringency compared to federal FOIA equivalents.
In 2024, New Hampshire enacted Senate Bill 255, establishing comprehensive consumer data privacy protections effective January 1, 2025, granting residents rights to access, delete, and opt out of data sales by controllers meeting revenue or processing thresholds, without a private right of action but with enforcement by the Attorney General.

Judicial system and recent rulings

The New Hampshire judiciary operates as a unified court system comprising the Supreme Court, the Superior Court, and the Circuit Court, with jurisdiction distributed according to the state constitution and enabling statutes. The Supreme Court, the state's sole appellate court, exercises mandatory jurisdiction over appeals from final judgments in capital cases and discretionary review in other matters, lacking an intermediate appellate tier. It consists of a chief justice and four associate justices, who are nominated by the governor and confirmed by a majority vote of the five-member Executive Council, serving terms until mandatory retirement at age 70.
The Superior Court functions as the primary trial court of general jurisdiction, handling felony prosecutions, high-value civil disputes exceeding $1,500, and certain equitable matters across 11 county-based locations with 22 full-time justices. Established in 1901, it conducts jury trials and issues interlocutory appeals to the Supreme Court. Complementing this, the Circuit Court, consolidated in 2012 from prior district, family, and probate divisions, manages misdemeanors, small claims up to $10,000, domestic relations, guardianships, and probate across 32 divisions statewide with 48 full-time judges. This structure emphasizes efficiency, with cases originating in the Circuit Court escalating to Superior or directly to the Supreme Court as needed.
Recent Supreme Court rulings have centered on education finance, interpreting Part II, Article 83 of the state constitution's mandate for an "adequate" public education funded partly by property taxes. On June 10, 2025, in Rand v. New Hampshire, the court reversed a trial decision, holding that the statewide education property tax (SWEPT), imposed at a uniform rate of $3.00 per $1,000 of equalized valuation since 1999, complies with constitutional uniformity requirements; it permits municipalities retaining excess local revenues above the state share, rejecting claims of disparate impact on lower-wealth districts.
In a July 1, 2025, decision affirming a 2023 Superior Court finding in the ConVal Regional School District litigation, the Supreme Court declared the state's base adequacy aid formula unconstitutional for providing inadequate per-pupil fundingapproximately $3,958 as of fiscal year 2023falling short of demonstrably sufficient amounts for core educational opportunities like proficient instruction and facilities, as evidenced by comparative data from higher-performing districts. The ruling remanded for legislative remedy, including potential per-pupil increases beyond the $4,100 threshold set in 2019, while upholding a trial court's conservative remedial order avoiding judicial overreach into appropriations.
Other 2024-2025 decisions include Appeal of Hoekstra (May 14, 2024), which clarified municipal authority under permissive zoning statutes to regulate short-term rentals as accessory uses rather than prohibiting them outright, impacting local land-use controls in tourist areas. In October 2025, Appeal of Pittsfield School District addressed ongoing adequacy disputes, though specifics remain tied to evidentiary reviews of funding efficacy. These cases underscore the court's role in enforcing fiscal constitutional limits amid New Hampshire's decentralized governance, with education rulings prompting legislative sessions to allocate over $200 million in additional aid by October 2025.

Politics

Political culture and libertarian leanings

New Hampshire's political culture emphasizes individual liberty and skepticism toward centralized authority, as symbolized by the state motto "Live Free or Die," derived from a 1809 toast by Revolutionary War General John Stark to his comrades and formally adopted for state documents in 1945. This ethos has historically manifested in resistance to expansive taxation and regulation, with the state maintaining no broad-based sales or income tax since its founding, a policy sustained through 16 voter-rejected attempts to introduce an income tax between 1967 and 2002. The culture prioritizes self-reliance and minimal government interference, distinguishing New Hampshire from neighboring New England states that have adopted more progressive fiscal frameworks. This libertarian-leaning, pro-business environment has attracted net in-migration, particularly from high-tax neighbors like Massachusetts, and supported population growth rates exceeding those of most other New England states.
Voter behavior underscores this independence, with undeclared (independent) voters forming the plurality of the electorate at 39.69% of registered voters as of the most recent comprehensive data, compared to 32.04% Republicans and 28.27% Democrats among approximately 1.1 million total registrants. The state's open primary system permits undeclared voterswho outnumber partisans in either partyto cross over and participate in Democratic or Republican primaries without affiliation, encouraging pragmatic rather than ideological voting and contributing to frequent shifts in legislative control since 2006. This structure aligns with a broader aversion to partisan lockstep, where candidates from both major parties must appeal to libertarian-leaning independents on issues like low taxes and regulatory restraint.
Libertarian principles permeate policy preferences, blending fiscal conservatism with tolerance for personal autonomy. New Hampshire's regulatory environment features light-touch oversight in business and land use, exemplified by the 2021 expansion of "constitutional carry" permitting concealed carry without permits for law-abiding adults, alongside early legalization of same-sex marriage via judicial ruling in 2009 and subsequent legislative affirmation. The Cato Institute has ranked the state as America's freest based on metrics of taxation, government spending, and individual rights, attributing this to a unique fusion of market-oriented economics and resistance to both social and economic mandates. Public opposition to broad-based taxes remains fierce, with proposals routinely defeated even under divided government, reflecting a causal link between the state's granite quarrying heritagesymbolizing enduranceand a cultural norm favoring limited state burdens on personal enterprise.

Electoral history and primaries

New Hampshire has conducted the first presidential primary election in the nation since 1920, a tradition established after the state legislature set its primary date ahead of others to assert priority in the nomination process. State law mandates that the primary occur on the Tuesday seven days prior to any competing contest elsewhere, ensuring its lead position despite challenges from other states and national parties. Undeclared voters, comprising approximately 40% of the electorate as of 2024, may participate in either the Democratic or Republican primary, contributing to high turnout and influence from independents. 
The primary's outcomes have frequently shaped national candidacies, with notable upsets including Eugene McCarthy's strong 1968 Democratic showing against incumbent Lyndon B. Johnson, prompting Johnson's withdrawal, and John McCain's 2000 Republican victory over George W. Bush, boosting McCain's viability. In recent cycles, the 2016 Republican primary saw Donald Trump place second to McCain's endorsed successor, yet propelled his momentum, while the 2020 Democratic contest featured Pete Buttigieg's surprise second-place finish behind Bernie Sanders amid a crowded field.
In general presidential elections, New Hampshire's four electoral votes have aligned with the national winner in 44 of 59 contests through 2020, reflecting its swing-state status. The state supported Republicans consistently from 1952 to 1984 before shifting Democratic in every election from 1988 to 2024, with Kamala Harris defeating Donald Trump in the latter by a margin of about 2.5 percentage points. At the state level, gubernatorial elections have favored Republicans in recent decades, with Chris Sununu securing four terms from 2017 to 2025 before Kelly Ayotte's 2024 victory extended GOP control. Legislative control has fluctuated, with Democrats gaining the House in 2020 for the first time since 2012, only for Republicans to reclaim majorities in both chambers by 2022 and retain them in 2024. 

Federal representation

New Hampshire elects two United States senators to six-year terms and two members of the United States House of Representatives to two-year terms, with congressional district boundaries redrawn after each decennial census; following the 2020 census, the state retained its two House seats based on a population of 1,377,529. The current congressional delegation, as of October 2025, consists entirely of Democrats, reflecting outcomes of the 2024 federal elections despite the state's history of competitive races.
The senior senator is Jeanne Shaheen (D), who was first elected in 2008 and has been reelected in 2014 and 2020; her term expires on January 3, 2027, after which she plans to retire, prompting a 2026 open-seat contest. The junior senator is Maggie Hassan (D), elected in 2016 and reelected in 2022, with her current term ending January 3, 2029.
In the House, New Hampshire's 1st congressional district, encompassing the southeastern portion including Manchester and Nashua, is represented by Chris Pappas (D), who won a 2018 special election to succeed Carol Shea-Porter and has held the seat since January 3, 2019, through subsequent reelections. The 2nd congressional district, covering the western and northern areas including Concord and Lebanon, is held by Maggie Goodlander (D), who was elected in November 2024 following Ann McLane Kuster's retirement and assumed office on January 3, 2025. Both House members won their most recent races with margins under 10 percentage points, underscoring the districts' status as battlegrounds.
New Hampshire's senators and representatives participate in key committees relevant to the state's economy, including agriculture, commerce, and environment; for instance, Shaheen serves on the Senate Foreign Relations and Small Business Committees, while Pappas sits on the House Agriculture and Armed Services Committees. The delegation has collaborated on issues like federal funding for substance use treatment, as evidenced by a July 2025 letter urging restoration of $80 million in grants.

Free State Project and policy influences

The Free State Project (FSP) is a political migration initiative founded in 2001 by Jason Sorens, a political science doctoral student at Yale University, who proposed concentrating 20,000 libertarian-leaning individuals in a single low-population state to advocate for reduced government intervention, emphasizing individual liberty, free markets, and limited taxation. New Hampshire was selected in a 2003 vote among participants due to its existing absence of a state income or sales tax, strong tradition of local control, part-time legislature, and state motto "Live Free or Die," which aligned with the project's goals of fostering policies prioritizing personal autonomy over centralized authority.
By 2022, over 6,000 individuals had relocated to New Hampshire as verified "movers" under FSP criteria, with pledges exceeding 20,000 and ongoing annual influxes reported into 2025, including events like monthly newcomer gatherings and the Porcupine Freedom Festival attracting thousands. FSP participants, often self-identified as "porcupines" for their defensive posture against overreach, have integrated into local communities, forming businesses, advocacy groups like the Liberty Alliance, and running for office, which has amplified libertarian voices in a state already resistant to expansive welfare programs or regulatory growth.
The FSP has influenced New Hampshire's policy landscape by mobilizing voters and legislators toward deregulation, including support for the 2017 right-to-work law prohibiting mandatory union dues, expansions of school choice via education freedom accounts enacted in 2021, and resistance to proposed taxes on interest and dividends, which were repealed in 2021 after earlier imposition. On gun rights, FSP-backed candidates have reinforced the state's constitutional carry framework, dating to 2017 but rooted in prior reforms, while opposing COVID-19 mandates in 2020, leading to legislative overrides of gubernatorial emergency powers and bans on vaccine passports. Crypto-friendly measures, such as recognizing blockchain signatures for contracts in 2020, reflect FSP advocacy for technological liberty. By 2025, FSP affiliates held significant sway in the Republican-dominated legislature, with critics attributing a shift toward prioritizing deregulation over traditional fiscal conservatism, though empirical data from indices like the Cato Institute's Freedom in the 50 States consistently rank New Hampshire highest, crediting both native culture and migrant activism.
Opposition from established residents has focused on perceived cultural disruptions, such as challenges to local zoning or public funding in areas like Gunstock Recreation Area in 2022, where FSP-linked commissioners voted to dismiss management, prompting backlash and recalls. Despite this, the project's causal impact is evident in electoral gains, with libertarian-leaning Republicans forming a plurality in the House by the mid-2020s, blocking progressive initiatives on housing mandates and environmental regulations while advancing reforms like occupational licensing reductions.

Infrastructure and Transportation

Roadways and highways

New Hampshire's roadway network totals approximately 17,000 miles, encompassing state-managed highways, turnpikes, interstates, and municipal roads, with the New Hampshire Department of Transportation (NHDOT) overseeing about 3,460 miles as of 2021. The system supports high vehicle miles traveled, with 10.9 billion miles driven annually as reported in federal data. Interstate highways form the backbone, comprising three primary routes and two auxiliaries, while the tolled NH Turnpike System adds 167 miles of limited-access roadways, 71 of which overlap with the Interstate system and utilize E-ZPass for collection.
Interstate 93, the state's primary north-south corridor, extends 81 miles from the Massachusetts border near Salem to Vermont near Littleton, passing through Manchester and Concord and serving as the Blue Star Turnpike in its southern segment with tolls. Interstate 89 runs 60 miles northwest from I-93 near Concord to the Vermont border, providing access to Lebanon and the Upper Valley region. Interstate 95 briefly traverses 17 miles along the southeastern coast from Massachusetts to the Maine border near Portsmouth, facilitating coastal connectivity. Auxiliary routes include Interstate 293, a 4-mile connector in Manchester linking I-93 to the Everett Turnpike, and Interstate 381, a short 1-mile spur in Portsmouth.
U.S. Highways and state routes supplement the interstates, with key examples including U.S. Route 3 (the Everett Turnpike, tolled from Massachusetts to Nashua), U.S. Route 4 (Spaulding Turnpike, tolled eastward to Dover), and U.S. Route 1 along the coast. State routes, numbered under the NH system, total over 1,100 miles and include NH Route 16, which parallels I-93 northward through the White Mountains. Approximately 34% of major roads remain in poor or mediocre condition, contributing to annual driver costs of $622 million in repairs and delays. Maintenance efforts prioritize federal-aid eligible roads, which constitute 75% of NHDOT's inventory.

Aviation and rail

Manchester-Boston Regional Airport (MHT), located in Manchester, serves as New Hampshire's primary commercial aviation hub, handling domestic flights to major U.S. cities via airlines such as Southwest, Delta, and United, with over 1 million enplanements annually in recent years. The airport, situated less than 50 miles north of Boston, processes significant cargo volumes, exceeding 150 million pounds in cumulative operations, supporting regional logistics without reliance on larger neighboring hubs like Logan International. Portsmouth International Airport at Pease (PSM) complements MHT by providing service to the Seacoast region, offering domestic and limited international flights through carriers like Allegiant Air and Breeze Airways, with convenient access via Interstate 95.
New Hampshire maintains 25 public-use airports, predominantly for general aviation, including facilities like Concord Municipal Airport (CON) and Lebanon Municipal Airport, which support private, corporate, and recreational flying. In calendar year 2023, the state registered 1,614 aircraft, generating approximately $482,600 in revenue for aviation bureaus through fees and registrations, underscoring the sector's role in economic activity beyond commercial passenger service. These airports facilitate emergency medical transport, business travel, and tourism, with federal funding covering about 90% of improvements via FAA grants.
Passenger rail service in New Hampshire remains limited, with no intra-state commuter or intercity options operational as of 2025; the Amtrak Downeaster provides the sole regular service, extending from Boston to Brunswick, Maine, and stopping at Exeter, Durham, and Dover in the Seacoast area. Proposals for expansion, such as the Capitol Corridor to connect Nashua and Manchester from Lowell, Massachusetts, have advanced to environmental assessments but face legislative hurdles, including a 2025 bill prohibiting state funding for new passenger rail projects.
Freight rail dominates the state's rail network, with 10 railroads operating 301 miles of track and employing 145 workers, transporting goods like aggregates, lumber, and manufacturing outputs essential to northern and central economies. The state owns over 200 miles of active lines within a total of approximately 443 miles, managed by carriers including the New England Central Railroad and New Hampshire Northcoast, which provide daily service for commodities such as sand to regional markets. Recent developments, like the 2024 resumption of service to Whitefield via Vermont Rail System, highlight ongoing efforts to revitalize underutilized lines for industrial transloading.

Public transit and ports

New Hampshire operates 12 local public bus systems, primarily serving urban and regional areas, with additional intercity and specialized services for seniors and individuals with disabilities. These systems include the Manchester Transit Authority, which provides fixed-route bus services within Manchester and commuter routes to Concord, Nashua, and Salem, all equipped with wheelchair accessibility. The Nashua Transit System offers local routes connecting Greater Nashua, emphasizing safe and reliable operations. In the Seacoast region, the Cooperative Alliance for Seacoast Transportation (COAST) delivers customer-focused bus services across southeastern New Hampshire and adjacent areas, including real-time tracking and route planning tools.
Regional providers extend coverage to rural and inter-community needs; Advance Transit operates fare-free services in the Upper Valley, spanning six communities to promote accessibility and environmental sustainability. Concord Area Transit serves the capital region with local fixed routes, while CART in southern New Hampshire provides on-demand response transportation and a free fixed-route shuttle in Salem. Intercity options like Concord Coach Lines and C&J Trailways connect New Hampshire to Boston Logan Airport, New York City, and other destinations, offering amenities such as Wi-Fi and charging outlets, though overall public transit remains limited outside population centers due to the state's rural character and emphasis on personal vehicles.
The state's ports center on Portsmouth Harbor, New Hampshire's only deep-draft facility on the Piscataqua River, which shares navigation with Maine and handles approximately 3.5 million tons of cargo annually, supporting commercial, industrial, and recreational maritime activities. Managed by the New Hampshire Port Authority, operations include the Market Street Marine Terminal in Portsmouth, which facilitates general cargo handling, public access, and moorings amid the state's tidal waters and harbors. Historically one of the nation's busiest ports, Portsmouth continues active shipping alongside fisheries and pleasure boating, bolstered by U.S. Army Corps of Engineers maintenance for channel depths accommodating larger vessels. No other significant seaports exist in New Hampshire, as the state's 18-mile coastline limits broader deep-water access.

Education

K-12 system and school choice

New Hampshire's K-12 public education system is administered through 177 school districts governed by locally elected school boards, with oversight from the state Department of Education. In the 2021-2022 school year, the state enrolled 168,909 students in 502 public schools, supported by 14,731 full-time equivalent teachers, yielding a pupil-teacher ratio of approximately 11.5:1. The system emphasizes competency-based education, requiring students to demonstrate mastery of standards rather than seat time, a policy shift formalized in the early 2010s to align with performance outcomes over traditional inputs.
Student performance on the National Assessment of Educational Progress (NAEP) places New Hampshire above national averages, reflecting effective resource allocation despite structural constraints. In 2024, fourth-grade mathematics scores averaged 242 points statewide, exceeding the national average of 237, while eighth-grade reading scores reached 264, compared to 257 nationally; these results rank the state in the top quartile for both subjects. Fourth-grade reading scores stood at 221, slightly below the 2022 level but above the national 214. Total per-pupil spending averaged $22,252 in the 2023-2024 school year, among the highest nationally, but the state's contribution remains the lowest in the U.S. at around $4,800 per pupil, with local property taxes funding the majority and federal aid supplementing post-2020 at $2,179 per student in 2024.
School choice options in New Hampshire have expanded significantly, driven by legislative reforms emphasizing parental control and competition. Public charter schools, authorized by the State Board of Education, numbered 37 as of 2025, serving 6,020 studentsa 44% increase from 2019representing about 3.5% of public enrollment; these tuition-free schools must meet performance standards but offer curricular flexibility. Inter-district and intra-district open enrollment policies enable transfers to non-resident public schools, subject to district capacity.
Homeschooling has grown rapidly, particularly post-2019, with enrollment surging amid dissatisfaction with remote learning and curriculum concerns; by 2023, non-public options including homeschool drew thousands, supported by notifications to local superintendents rather than state approval. The cornerstone of choice is the Education Freedom Accounts (EFAs) program, enacted in 2021 and expanded via Senate Bill 295 in June 2025 to universal eligibility for all K-12 students regardless of income, capped at 10,000 participants for the 2025-2026 year with priority for prior recipients and escalators for demand. EFAs allocate a portion of the state's per-pupil adequacy aidaveraging $4,100 to $4,800for approved expenses like private tuition, homeschool materials, tutoring, or therapies, costing $27.7 million for 5,321 participants in 2024-2025 and representing about 23% of total public per-pupil costs to minimize fiscal strain on districts. Proponents argue EFAs enhance outcomes by redirecting funds to individualized needs, while critics question accountability, though empirical data from similar programs elsewhere shows neutral to positive effects on public school performance via competition.

Higher education institutions

New Hampshire's higher education landscape features a mix of public institutions governed by the University System of New Hampshire (USNH) and independent private colleges, with approximately 28 colleges and universities serving around 235,000 students statewide, predominantly undergraduates. The USNH includes the flagship University of New Hampshire (UNH) in Durham, founded in 1866 as a land-grant institution focused on agriculture and mechanical arts, now a comprehensive research university with an undergraduate enrollment of 13,626. Other USNH campuses include Keene State College, established in 1909 as a teachers college with 2,735 undergraduates emphasizing liberal arts, and Plymouth State University, enrolling 3,839 students in programs blending education, business, and environmental sciences.
Among private institutions, Dartmouth College in Hanover stands out as an Ivy League university chartered in 1769 by Eleazar Wheelock to educate Native Americans and English youth, evolving into a selective liberal arts and research institution with 6,746 total students, including 4,447 undergraduates, and an acceptance rate of 5.4%. Southern New Hampshire University (SNHU), founded in 1932 in Manchester as a secretarial school, has expanded into a nonprofit emphasizing online education, boasting over 184,000 total enrollments though traditional campus undergraduates number about 2,615.
Smaller private colleges contribute to the state's offerings, such as Saint Anselm College in Manchester with around 2,000 students focused on Catholic liberal arts education, and Franklin Pierce University in Rindge, known for health sciences and athletics. State funding for public higher education remains among the lowest nationally, at $179 million in fiscal year 2024, prompting reliance on tuition and influencing enrollment trends amid demographic shifts.

Recent reforms and controversies

In June 2025, Governor Kelly Ayotte signed Senate Bill 295 into law, expanding New Hampshire's Education Freedom Account (EFA) program to universal eligibility by eliminating the previous household income cap of 350% of the federal poverty level, allowing any K-12 student to access state funds for private school tuition, homeschooling expenses, or other approved educational services up to approximately $4,000 per student annually, subject to a statewide enrollment cap of 10,000 students starting in the 2025-26 school year with provisions for automatic increases based on demand. Proponents, including education choice advocates, argued the reform empowers parental decision-making and improves outcomes by introducing competition, citing data from similar programs showing gains in student performance metrics. Critics, such as the New Hampshire chapter of the National Education Association, contended it diverts taxpayer funds from under-resourced public schools without sufficient accountability for private recipients, potentially exacerbating inequities amid ongoing enrollment declines in the public system, which served about 178,000 students in 2024-25.
Public school funding has sparked repeated legal controversies, with the New Hampshire Supreme Court ruling on July 30, 2025, that the state's adequacy grants under the 2021-23 biennial budget violated the constitutional mandate for a "thorough and efficient" education by providing insufficient per-pupil aid, particularly in districts like ConVal Regional School District where local property taxes cannot bridge the gap without straining taxpayers. A Merrimack County Superior Court judge reaffirmed this on August 20, 2025, declaring the funding model unconstitutional for failing to cover essential costs like special education and facilities, echoing prior decisions and prompting Republican lawmakers to propose remedies such as targeted property tax hikes or revised formulas while Democrats advocated broader revenue increases. These rulings stem from empirical shortfalls: state aid covered only about 20-30% of total K-12 costs in recent years, forcing reliance on volatile local revenues amid property value disparities.
Curriculum and instructional policies have fueled debates over the 2021 "divisive concepts" law (HB 2), which prohibits public schools from teaching that one race or sex is inherently superior or that individuals bear responsibility for historical actions of their group, leading to lawsuits claiming it censors discussions of systemic racism or gender dynamics and chills educator speech. In January 2025, public school educators and their union challenged the law in federal court, arguing it violates First Amendment rights by vaguely targeting concepts like implicit bias, with a U.S. District judge previously blocking enforcement of related provisions in 2022. Education Commissioner Frank Edelblut has defended the statute as protecting viewpoint diversity, accusing some districts of promoting ideologically driven materials despite pandemic-related learning losses that dropped statewide proficiency rates by 10-15 percentage points in reading and math from 2019 to 2022. Separate controversies include parent lawsuits against districts over transgender inclusion policies, such as restroom access, highlighting tensions between privacy rights and anti-discrimination mandates.
In higher education, state budget cuts have drawn scrutiny, with the University of New Hampshire eliminating nearly 70 positions and raising tuition by 3-5% for 2025-26 in response to reduced appropriations totaling about $15 million less than prior years, potentially impacting workforce development as enrollment stagnates around 13,000 undergraduates amid national trends of declining college-going rates. Advocates for public institutions warn that chronic underfundingstate support per student fell 20% in real terms since 2008could hinder economic competitiveness, though fiscal conservatives attribute pressures to rising operational costs and prioritize K-12 adequacy.

Culture and Society

State symbols and mottos

The state motto of New Hampshire is "Live Free or Die," adopted by the legislature in 1945. The phrase originates from a 1809 toast by Revolutionary War General John Stark, who wrote in a letter declining an invitation to a veterans' reunion: "Live free or die: Death is not the worst of evils." It appears on state highway signs, license plates since 1970, and the state seal.
The state flag, adopted in 1909, features the state seal centered on a blue field, with the seal's laurel wreath encircled by nine stars representing New Hampshire as the ninth state to ratify the U.S. Constitution. The Great Seal of the State, established in its current form in 1931 following revisions from an 1784 design, depicts a broadside view of the frigate USS Raleigh under construction on stocks amid a granite boulder, with a rising sun above the horizon, symbolizing shipbuilding heritage in Portsmouth; the border bears the inscription "Seal of the State of New Hampshire 1776" surrounded by laurel leaves and stars.
The state emblem, adopted in 1945, is a profile illustration of the Old Man of the Mountain, a granite formation in Franconia Notch that resembled a human face and symbolized rugged independence; the formation collapsed on May 3, 2003, due to natural erosion, but the stylized profile remains in official use on items such as the state quarter and highway signs.

Arts, literature, and media

New Hampshire has produced or attracted several notable literary figures, many drawing inspiration from the state's rural landscapes and small-town ethos. Robert Frost, one of America's most celebrated poets, resided in New Hampshire for extended periods, including at his Derry farm from 1900 to 1909, where he composed early works like "Mending Wall" and "The Road Not Taken," reflecting themes of nature and individualism. J.D. Salinger, author of The Catcher in the Rye, lived reclusively in Cornish from 1953 until his death in 2010, avoiding public life amid the state's relative seclusion. Other prominent writers include John Irving, born in Exeter in 1942 and known for novels like The World According to Garp, and Dan Brown, born in Exeter in 1964, whose The Da Vinci Code sold over 80 million copies worldwide by 2009. The New Hampshire Literary Hall of Fame, established in 2015, inducted Frost, Irving, poet Donald Hall, and novelist Grace Metaliousauthor of the 1956 bestseller Peyton Place, which exposed small-town hypocrisies and sold 12 million copies by 1961as inaugural honorees.
Visual arts in New Hampshire center on institutions like the Currier Museum of Art in Manchester, founded in 1929 with a collection exceeding 12,000 works spanning European and American art from the Renaissance to contemporary periods, including pieces by Monet and Picasso. The Hood Museum of Art at Dartmouth College in Hanover, established in 1772 as part of the college's founding, holds over 65,000 objects with strengths in Native American art, African sculpture, and modern works, hosting exhibitions that connect global cultures. Community-driven venues, such as the Rochester Museum of Fine Arts, emphasize accessible contemporary exhibits through volunteer efforts since 1994.
Performing arts thrive in regional theaters and music halls. The Music Hall in Portsmouth, built in 1878 and restored in 1999, presents over 200 events annually, including concerts, comedy, and literary readings, accommodating 900 patrons in its historic space. The Palace Theatre in Manchester, operational since 1927, features Broadway-style musicals, rock performances, and community theater in a 1,300-seat venue. The Capitol Center for the Arts in Concord, renovated in 1995, hosts national tours and local productions, drawing 100,000 visitors yearly across its 1,300-seat theater and smaller spaces.
Media outlets in New Hampshire include daily newspapers like the Foster's Daily Democrat in Dover, circulating over 20,000 copies as of 2020, covering regional news with a focus on southern counties. WMUR-TV, the state's ABC affiliate based in Manchester since 1954, reaches 96% of households and provides local news, weather, and sports coverage. New Hampshire Public Radio (NHPR), launched in 1981, operates as a nonprofit with five stations broadcasting NPR programming and original reporting to 300,000 weekly listeners, emphasizing independent journalism funded by memberships. Film production remains modest, supported by the New Hampshire Film and Television Office since 1996, which has facilitated shoots for commercials and features leveraging the state's varied terrain, though no major studios dominate.

Cuisine and traditions

New Hampshire's cuisine centers on seasonal, farm-fresh ingredients from its agricultural lands and coastal fisheries, with maple syrup as a cornerstone product derived from sugar maple sap collected in late winter. In 2023, the state yielded 167,000 gallons of syrup, supporting local economies through tapping operations that yield about 0.286 gallons per tap. Sugaring traditions culminate in community "sugaring-off" gatherings featuring syrup-drenched pancakes, doughnuts, and beans, practices rooted in colonial-era methods adapted to the region's climate.
Apple production spans over 1,300 acres of orchards, fostering fall harvest customs like pick-your-own events and the widespread consumption of apple cider doughnuts, fried treats coated in cinnamon sugar and cider glaze. Hearty inland dishes include boiled dinner, comprising corned beef, cabbage, potatoes, and root vegetables simmered for hours, a staple reflecting Yankee frugality and farm surplus. Coastal fare emphasizes seafood such as New England clam chowder a creamy broth of clams, potatoes, and onionsand fried clams, harvested from tidal flats, though lobster rolls gain prominence in summer despite regional overlap with Maine.
Regional specialties bear immigrant imprints, notably gorton (or cretons), a spiced pork spread simmered with onions and cloves, tracing to French-Canadian settlers in mill towns; hermit cookies, chewy molasses-spiced bars durable for farm labor; and seacoast beach pizza, distinguished by its focaccia-like crust and provolone cheese. These items underscore practical adaptations to local resources and labor histories, with poutinea fries, gravy, and cheese curd dishappearing in adapted forms at diners, influenced by Quebec proximity.
Cultural traditions in New Hampshire preserve folklife through intergenerational transmission of skills in storytelling, crafts like woodworking and quilting, and occupational practices tied to logging, fishing, and farming. Folk music and dance, including fiddle tunes and contra dancing, thrive in rural halls and festivals, drawing from English, Scottish, and Yankee roots documented in state archives. Seasonal observances, such as maple sugaring parties and apple harvest fairs, blend culinary rites with community bonding, while events like Old Home Dayoriginating in 1899 to reunite emigrantsreinforce ties to ancestral towns through parades and suppers. These customs emphasize self-reliance and localism, contrasting with more centralized holiday commercialism elsewhere.

Sports and recreation

New Hampshire does not host major professional sports franchises but supports minor league baseball via the New Hampshire Fisher Cats, a Double-A affiliate of the Toronto Blue Jays based in Manchester, which plays at Delta Dental Stadium and draws crowds for its season from April to September. The state also features summer collegiate teams such as the Nashua Silver Knights and Keene Swamp Bats in the Futures Collegiate Baseball League, emphasizing affordable, community-oriented games. Motorsports stand out at New Hampshire Motor Speedway in Loudon, a 1.058-mile oval track that hosts NASCAR Cup Series races, including the annual event on August 23, 2026, as the penultimate regular-season race, alongside NASCAR Craftsman Truck Series and ARCA Menards Series competitions.
Collegiate athletics play a significant role, with the University of New Hampshire Wildcats competing in NCAA Division I as part of America East Conference for most sports, including prominent men's ice hockey in Hockey East, where the team has reached the NCAA tournament multiple times, and football in the Colonial Athletic Association. Dartmouth College's Big Green fields Division I teams in the Ivy League, excelling in winter sports like men's and women's ice hockey, with the men's program claiming seven national championships historically, though recent performance varies.
Recreation in New Hampshire centers on outdoor pursuits leveraging its terrain and climate, with hiking prominent in areas like Franconia Notch State Park and Mount Monadnock State Park, the latter attracting over 125,000 visitors annually for its accessible 3,165-foot summit via maintained trails. Winter sports thrive across more than 20 ski areas, including resorts like Gunstock Mountain Resort with 49 trails on 227 skiable acres and Cannon Mountain offering terrain parks and glades, supporting skiing, snowboarding, snowshoeing, and over 7,000 miles of snowmobile trails managed by state clubs. 
Summer activities emphasize water-based recreation on Lake Winnipesaukee, New Hampshire's largest lake at 71 square miles, where boating and fishing target species including landlocked salmon, lake trout, rainbow trout, smallmouth bass, and largemouth bass under a two-tier management system prioritizing coldwater and warmwater fisheries. State parks facilitate camping, swimming, biking, and ATVing on designated trails, with additional options like whitewater rafting on the Saco River and moose-watching tours in the White Mountains. These pursuits draw from the state's 85 percent forested landscape, promoting self-reliant, nature-focused engagement over urban entertainment.

Notable Residents

Franklin Pierce (November 23, 1804 October 8, 1869), born in Hillsborough, served as the 14th President of the United States from 1853 to 1857, having previously been a U.S. Senator and the youngest president elected at the time.
Daniel Webster (January 18, 1782 October 24, 1852), born in Salisbury, was a leading American statesman, lawyer, and orator who represented New Hampshire and Massachusetts in Congress, advocating for national unity amid sectional tensions.
Alan Shepard (May 18, 1923 July 21, 1998), born in Derry, achieved historic milestones in spaceflight as the first American astronaut to enter space on May 5, 1961, via the Mercury-Redstone 3 mission, and later commanded Apollo 14, becoming the fifth person to walk on the Moon in February 1971.
Josiah Bartlett (November 21, 1729 May 19, 1795), a physician and statesman who resided in Kingston, signed the Declaration of Independence on behalf of New Hampshire and later served as governor from 1790 to 1794.
Mary Baker Eddy (July 16, 1821 December 3, 1910), born in Bow, founded the Church of Christ, Scientist, in 1879 and established The Christian Science Monitor newspaper in 1908.
Sarah Josepha Hale (October 24, 1788 April 30, 1879), born in Newport, was an influential editor and author who advocated for the national observance of Thanksgiving, authoring the poem "Mary Had a Little Lamb" and editing Godey's Lady's Book for over 40 years.
In literature, John Irving, born in Exeter on March 2, 1942, is known for novels such as The World According to Garp (1978) and The Cider House Rules (1985), both adapted into Academy Award-winning films.
Dan Brown, born in Exeter on June 22, 1964, authored the bestselling thriller The Da Vinci Code (2003), which sold over 80 million copies worldwide and spawned a film franchise.
Robert Frost (March 26, 1874 January 29, 1963), who moved to New Hampshire as a child and resided in places like Derry and Franconia, won four Pulitzer Prizes for poetry depicting rural New England life, including works like "The Road Not Taken."
J.D. Salinger (January 1, 1919 January 27, 2010), who lived reclusively in Cornish from 1953 until his death, wrote the iconic novel The Catcher in the Rye (1951), a seminal work in 20th-century American literature.
