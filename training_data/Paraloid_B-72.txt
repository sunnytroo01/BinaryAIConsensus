Paraloid B-72
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Paraloid B-72 is a thermoplastic acrylic resin copolymer composed of approximately 70% ethyl methacrylate and 30% methyl acrylate, manufactured by Rohm and Haas (now part of Dow Inc.) as a general-purpose material originally intended for surface coatings and flexographic inks. Widely adopted in cultural heritage conservation since the early 1980s, it serves as a versatile consolidant, adhesive, and protective varnish for fragile artifacts, including paintings, sculptures, ceramics, fossils, stained glass, and wooden supports, owing to its low glass transition temperature of 40C, high ethanol tolerance, chemical stability, and reversibility in solvents like acetone and ethanol.
The resin is supplied as transparent beads or pellets, which are dissolved in organic solvents to form solutions typically ranging from 5% to 20% concentration, depending on the application; its flexibility and non-yellowing nature make it particularly suitable for long-term preservation without altering the appearance of treated surfaces. In conservation practice, Paraloid B-72 excels as a barrier layer to enhance the reversibility of other adhesives and as a consolidant for friable materials, such as degraded lime wood or archaeological ceramics, where its colorless and resistant properties ensure minimal visual impact while providing structural support.
Beyond traditional uses, recent innovations include its modification for superhydrophobic coatings on heritage objects to repel water and contaminants, and its application in fossil preparation as a protective varnish or gap-filling adhesive, highlighting its adaptability across interdisciplinary fields like paleontology and materials science. Despite its advantages, careful solvent selection is critical, as certain mixtures can affect film formation and long-term performance, underscoring the need for standardized protocols in professional conservation.

Chemical Composition and Properties

Polymer Structure

Paraloid B-72 is a thermoplastic acrylic resin defined as a copolymer of ethyl methacrylate and methyl acrylate. This composition forms the basis of its utility in conservation applications, where the acrylic backbone provides durability and reversibility.
The specific monomer ratio in standard formulations is approximately 70% ethyl methacrylate and 30% methyl acrylate by weight, though earlier versions prior to 1975 used a 68:32 ratio. GC-MS analysis indicates the presence of approximately 2% butyl methacrylate in the copolymer. These proportions contribute to the balanced flexibility and hardness of the resulting polymer.
As a random copolymer, Paraloid B-72 exhibits a linear chain structure, with the monomers linking to form long, unbranched polymer chains characteristic of thermoplastic acrylics. This linear configuration enables its thermoplastic behavior, allowing the material to soften upon heating or dissolve in appropriate solvents, thereby facilitating reversibility in applications such as coatings and adhesives. The general chemical backbone can be represented as ester-substituted acrylate units, without cross-linking, which underscores its solubility and reworkability.

Physical Characteristics

Paraloid B-72 is supplied as a granular solid in the form of clear, irregular beads or pellets. This physical form facilitates easy handling and dissolution for preparation of solutions, while the solid state ensures long-term stability during storage. The material exhibits a bulk density of approximately 0.66 g/cm and a specific density of 1.15 g/cm, influencing its packing efficiency and volumetric measurements in practical applications. Post-1976 formulations are odorless, unlike earlier versions with a slight acrylic odor.
The glass transition temperature (Tg) of Paraloid B-72 is approximately 40C, a property that allows it to form soft, flexible films at ambient room temperatures, providing a balance between pliability and structural integrity. This Tg value, lower than that of harder acrylics like Paraloid B-66, contributes to its suitability for applications requiring minimal brittleness. Additionally, the softening point occurs around 7075C, at which point the resin begins to melt and flow, enabling thermoplastic reshaping without degradation; full flowing is achieved near 145150C. These thermal characteristics ensure the material remains stable under typical environmental conditions but can be manipulated with moderate heat.
The molecular weight of Paraloid B-72 has a weight-average (Mw) of approximately 65,000 g/mol (with number-average around 11,00020,000 g/mol), which supports adequate viscosity in solutions and sufficient film strength for protective coatings. As an ethyl methacrylate-methyl acrylate copolymer, this molecular architecture underpins its observed flexibility and thermal behavior. Furthermore, Paraloid B-72 demonstrates excellent durability, remaining non-yellowing and resistant to degradation under prolonged UV exposure and artificial aging tests, making it a reliable choice for long-term material protection.

Solubility and Compatibility

Paraloid B-72 exhibits excellent solubility in a range of organic solvents, making it versatile for preparation in conservation and coating applications. It is fully soluble in acetone, ethanol (with notably high tolerance compared to other acrylic resins), toluene, xylene, and various mixtures thereof, allowing for clear solutions that dry to form durable films. It shows partial solubility in certain aliphatic hydrocarbons, such as Shellsol A, but is insoluble in others like white spirit, which influences solvent selection for specific uses.
Solvent compatibility can be predicted using Hansen solubility parameters, which quantify the dispersion (_d = 19.2 MPa^{1/2}), polar (_p = 11.2 MPa^{1/2}), and hydrogen-bonding (_h = 1.8 MPa^{1/2}) interactions of the resin, with a radius of interaction (R_0) of 11.0 MPa^{1/2}. These parameters enable the identification of suitable solvents or blends that match the resin's solubility sphere, ensuring effective dissolution without phase separation.
The resin demonstrates strong compatibility with pigments, fillers, and other materials, exhibiting low reactivity with sensitive phosphorescent and luminescent pigments to yield stable, non-yellowing coatings. It integrates well with resins such as vinyls, cellulosics, chlorinated rubbers, and silicones, facilitating blended formulations. Its solubility in polar solvents like acetone and ethanol ensures reversibility, allowing removal or adjustment of applied films through re-dissolution if needed.
Paraloid B-72 solutions maintain pH neutrality and high chemical stability, remaining non-reactive and shelf-stable indefinitely under ambient conditions, which supports its long-term reliability in sensitive applications.

History and Development

Invention and Early Use

Paraloid B-72 was developed by the chemical company Rohm and Haas in the mid-20th century as a thermoplastic acrylic resin. Early references to its use appear in the company's publications as early as 1950, when it was applied as a protective coating for metal artifacts in exhibitions traveling from Vienna to U.S. museums. By 1961, it was commercialized in products such as the Krylon Crystal Clear No. 41301 spray coating, highlighting its initial availability for industrial applications.
The resin was originally designed as a general-purpose material for surface coatings and as a vehicle in flexographic printing inks. It served as a binder in pigment dispersions for inks and provided clear finishes over wood, metals, and plastics in industrial settings. These early formulations emphasized its role in creating durable, transparent layers for packaging and printing industries.
Key properties targeted during its development included high flexibility to form softer films compared to similar resins like Paraloid B-66, exceptional tolerance to ethanol for compatibility with milder solvents, and resistance to yellowing for long-term clarity in coatings. As an ethyl methacrylate-methyl acrylate copolymer, it was engineered to produce stable, non-reactive films suitable for sensitive applications. These attributes made it ideal for industrial coatings requiring durability without compromising aesthetics.
Following Rohm and Haas's acquisition by The Dow Chemical Company on April 1, 2009, production and distribution of Paraloid B-72 transitioned to Dow, which continues to market it under the same brand for various applications.

Adoption in Conservation

Although initial uses in conservation date back to at least 1950 for metal artifacts, Paraloid B-72's more systematic adoption in the field began in the early 1970s, when it was first used in Italy as a consolidant and protective treatment for stone and wall paintings, valued for its ability to penetrate porous surfaces without altering appearance. This marked a shift from earlier, less stable resins, as conservators sought materials that could stabilize fragile heritage without causing further degradation. Around 1978, the manufacturer reformulated the resin from white irregular lumps to transparent beads with a slight adjustment in composition (from approximately 68% to 70% ethyl methacrylate), prompting initial concerns in the conservation community about potential changes in performance, though subsequent studies confirmed its continued stability. By the late 1970s and into the 1980s, its use expanded to ceramics and glass consolidation, with early applications focusing on archaeological artifacts where mechanical strength and minimal residue were essential.
Key milestones in the 1980s included its adoption for stained glass conservation, where it was applied as a surface consolidant to prevent flaking and corrosion on historic painted panels, often in combination with other resins for enhanced durability. During this period, initial research in journals like the Journal of the American Institute for Conservation (JAIC) and Studies in Conservation documented its efficacy, with studies emphasizing its role in artifact reassembly and surface protection. By the 1990s, Paraloid B-72 achieved widespread use in museum labeling and general artifact stabilization, particularly for applying accession numbers on ceramics and metals, as standardized by institutions like the National Park Service.
The resin's adoption was driven by its superior reversibility in common solvents like acetone, allowing future interventions without damage; its low toxicity profile compared to older resins such as nitrocellulose, which posed flammability and degradation risks; and its versatility across materials, from porous stone to delicate glass. These properties, confirmed through aging tests showing stability over decades, positioned it as a preferred alternative in conservation protocols.

Manufacturing and Commercial Aspects

Production Process

Paraloid B-72 is synthesized via free radical solution polymerization of ethyl methacrylate and methyl acrylate monomers in a 70:30 weight ratio, a method standard for producing thermoplastic acrylic copolymers as solid resins. This process, originally developed by Rohm and Haas, involves dissolving the monomers in an organic solvent that favors monomer solubility but leads to polymer precipitation during reaction.
The polymerization begins with initiation using organic peroxides, such as benzoyl peroxide, which decompose to generate free radicals that add to the monomer double bonds, propagating chain growth through repeated addition steps until termination occurs via combination or disproportionation. Reaction conditions, including temperature (typically 70100C) and initiator concentration, control the molecular weight, aiming for a weight-average value around 50,000100,000 g/mol to achieve the resin's desired flexibility and solubility. Following polymerization, the copolymer is isolated by pouring the solution into a non-solvent (e.g., methanol or water) to induce precipitation, followed by filtration, washing to remove residual monomers and solvent, and drying to yield transparent solid beads.
Quality control throughout production emphasizes precise monomer feeding to maintain the 70:30 ratio, verified by techniques like NMR spectroscopy, and molecular weight consistency via gel permeation chromatography, ensuring batch-to-batch reproducibility in properties such as glass transition temperature (approximately 40C) and solubility. Deviations in these parameters can alter film formation and adhesion, critical for applications in conservation.
Under Dow Inc., which acquired Rohm and Haas in 2009, modern manufacturing aligns with broader environmental and sustainability goals, including efforts to reduce volatile organic compound emissions in coatings production and lower the overall carbon footprint.

Available Forms

Paraloid B-72 is primarily distributed as a 100% solid thermoplastic acrylic resin in the form of clear beads or pellets, which allows users to dissolve it in solvents as needed for custom applications. These beads are commonly packaged in 500 g or 1 kg containers to suit both small-scale conservation work and larger projects.
For convenience in conservation settings, pre-mixed solutions of Paraloid B-72 at concentrations ranging from 12% to 25% in solvents like acetone or ethanol are available from specialized suppliers such as TALAS and Conservation Resources. These solutions facilitate immediate use without on-site preparation, particularly for delicate tasks requiring precise application.
Ready-to-use formats include barrier coats and lacquers designed for labeling museum objects, often as a 25% solution in acetone provided in small jars with built-in brushes for easy application; examples include clear and opaque variants from Gaylord Archival. These products serve as protective layers to prevent ink from bleeding into artifacts.
Dow Inc. supplies bulk quantities of the raw resin for industrial and large-scale users, while conservation-grade versions are distributed through vendors like MuseuM Services, ensuring high purity and consistency for heritage applications.

Applications

In Cultural Heritage Conservation

Paraloid B-72 serves as a consolidant in cultural heritage conservation, particularly for stabilizing friable materials such as stone, ceramics, glass, and wall paintings, where low-concentration solutions (typically 1-5% w/v in solvents like acetone) are applied to bind loose particles and enhance structural integrity without altering surface appearance. For instance, a 3% solution has been used to impregnate and reattach fragments in wall paintings, providing reversible penetration into porous substrates. Similarly, 5% solutions in acetone have proven effective for consolidating carbonate stones, reducing permeability while maintaining compatibility with the material.
As an adhesive, Paraloid B-72 is employed at higher concentrations (10-20% w/v in acetone) to reattach fragmented artifacts, including ceramics and wood, forming strong yet reversible bonds that allow for future disassembly using the same solvent. This approach has been particularly valuable in reassembling archaeological ceramics, where its optical clarity and moderate strength enable precise alignment of pieces, as demonstrated in the reconstruction of fragile Roman glass from Troia, Portugal. For wood, such as degraded lime supports in heritage objects, it provides stable adhesion without introducing discoloration.
In its role as a protective varnish, thin films of Paraloid B-72 (5-10% solutions) are applied to paintings and metals to shield against abrasion and environmental damage while preserving visual qualities, owing to its non-yellowing and low-reactivity properties. On paintings, it acts as a medium-gloss isolation layer that saturates colors without altering optics, commonly used in overall consolidation of flaking paint layers. For metals in heritage contexts, it forms durable coatings that prevent handling-induced wear, as seen in applications for archaeological artifacts.
Specific examples highlight its long-term efficacy in stabilization efforts, including the consolidation of archaeological finds like ceramics and glass since the 1980s, where it has facilitated the preservation of over 100 fragments in complex reconstructions. Notably, since the early 1980s, Paraloid B-72 has been a standard for consolidating stained glass, applied to museum-held panels to mitigate crizzling and surface deterioration while ensuring removability.

In Industrial and Other Settings

Paraloid B-72 was originally developed as a vehicle for flexographic printing inks, where it provides flexibility to the ink film and facilitates effective pigment dispersion, particularly for fluorescent pigments. This application leverages its thermoplastic properties to produce stable, non-yellowing prints suitable for high-speed industrial printing processes.
In surface coatings, Paraloid B-72 is incorporated into paints and lacquers to enhance durability on substrates such as metals and plastics, offering excellent exterior durability, corrosion resistance, and compatibility with solvent-based systems. It is particularly valued in nitrocellulose-modified coatings for general product finishing and lacquers applied to plastic surfaces, where it contributes to improved paint stability and adhesion without yellowing over time.
Beyond printing and coatings, Paraloid B-72 serves as a consolidant for fossils in paleontology, where custom solutions in various solvents stabilize fragile specimens during preparation, outperforming many other resins due to its reversibility and compatibility. As of 2024, innovations include its use as a surface adhesive method for conserving bones and fossils, demonstrating enhanced penetration and reversibility. It is also used as an additive in adhesives for bonding materials like glass, metal, plastic, and wood, providing structural strength. Additionally, it acts as a barrier layer in museum artifact labeling applications to prevent ink migration.
On a modern industrial scale, Paraloid B-72 is integrated into thermoplastic films for its ability to form softer, flexible films compared to similar resins like B-66, and it functions as a softener in resin formulations to improve overall flexibility and processability in manufacturing.

Preparation and Application Methods

Solvent Selection

The selection of solvents for dissolving Paraloid B-72 is guided by the specific requirements of the application, the sensitivity of the substrate, and practical factors such as evaporation rate and safety. Acetone is commonly recommended for applications requiring fast evaporation, such as protective coatings on stable inorganic materials like ceramics or stone, where rapid drying minimizes handling time and reduces the risk of uneven application. In contrast, ethanol is preferred for sensitive organic substrates, such as paper, wood, or paintings, as it is less aggressive and causes minimal swelling or discoloration compared to more polar or volatile options like acetone. Toluene, an aromatic solvent, is selected for scenarios demanding high-strength adhesives, such as bonding archaeological fragments, due to its ability to produce durable bonds with slower evaporation that allows deeper penetration.
Solvent mixtures are often employed to optimize performance, balancing properties like drying speed and substrate interaction. For instance, mixtures of acetone and ethanol, such as 85:15 to 50:50 ratios, provide balanced drying times and improved penetration on moderately porous surfaces, reducing issues like bubbling or stringiness associated with pure acetone solutions. Such blends should be prepared based on the general solubility profile of Paraloid B-72, which exhibits good dissolution in a range of polar and non-polar organic solvents.
Key considerations in solvent choice include volatility, toxicity, and substrate compatibility to ensure safe and effective use. High-volatility solvents like acetone evaporate quickly but may limit penetration into porous materials, while lower-volatility options like toluene offer better flow but prolong working time. Toxicity varies significantly; toluene poses higher inhalation and skin absorption risks, necessitating use in well-ventilated areas or fume hoods, whereas ethanol is generally milder but still requires caution in concentrated forms. Compatibility is criticalaromatic solvents such as toluene should be avoided on plastics or certain synthetic materials, as they can cause cracking or dissolution.
For custom blends or unique substrates, solubility testing protocols are essential to verify effectiveness and prevent damage. These involve preparing small-scale solutions at varying concentrations (e.g., 5-20% w/v) and applying them to inconspicuous areas of the substrate, observing for dissolution, cloudiness, or adverse reactions over 24-48 hours under controlled conditions. This empirical approach ensures the solvent-resin system aligns with the desired outcomes while maintaining reversibility, a hallmark of Paraloid B-72 in conservation practice.

Mixing and Application Techniques

Paraloid B-72 is typically prepared by dissolving its solid beads or pellets in an appropriate organic solvent at room temperature, often with gentle stirring or passive suspension to achieve a clear solution without heat, which could alter the polymer's properties. The dissolution process generally takes 1 to 24 hours, depending on the solvent, concentration, and method; for instance, acetone facilitates faster dissolution (4-12 hours) compared to ethanol. A common technique is the cheesecloth method, where beads are wrapped in cheesecloth and suspended in the solvent-filled container, allowing passive diffusion without constant agitation, which minimizes residue and ensures even mixing.
Concentrations are prepared by weight/volume (w/v) measurements for precision, as volume can vary with solvent evaporation; low concentrations of 2-5% w/v (e.g., 2-5 g resin in 100 ml solvent) are used for surface consolidation to allow deep penetration without excessive buildup, while 10-15% w/v suits coatings and varnishes. For adhesives, higher concentrations up to 50% w/v (e.g., 50 g resin in 100 ml solvent) are common, often with additives like fumed silica for improved rheology, though solutions above 10% w/v may require caution in humid environments to avoid bubbling, and above 25% can become viscous during stirring. For accuracy, resins are weighed directly into solvent, and solutions are often made fresh to avoid degradation, with magnetic stirring recommended for 2-4 hours in lab settings.
Application methods include brushing for targeted areas, spraying for uniform coverage on irregular surfaces, and immersion for overall treatment of small objects, with brushes or atomizers cleaned immediately in fresh solvent to prevent clogging. To ensure even coverage, solutions are applied in thin layers, allowing 10-30 minutes drying time between coats depending on humidity and solvent volatility, and multiple layers (2-4) may be built up for durability while monitoring for over-saturation. Tips for optimal results include pre-wetting the substrate with pure solvent to enhance penetration and using vapor enclosures to control evaporation rates, reducing gloss or migration issues.
Cleanup involves soaking tools and containers in water before the solvent fully evaporates, allowing residue to peel off easily, while applied films can be removed or reversed using the same solvents (e.g., acetone or ethanol) applied with swabs or immersion, leveraging the resin's solubility for non-destructive conservation.

Advantages and Limitations

Key Benefits

Paraloid B-72 exhibits remarkable versatility, serving effectively as a consolidant, adhesive, and protective coating within a single formulation, which streamlines conservation workflows by reducing the need for multiple materials. This multifunctionality stems from its thermoplastic properties, allowing it to be adjusted in viscosity and application method to suit diverse substrates such as stone, wood, and glass artifacts.
A key advantage is its reversibility, as the resin dissolves readily in common solvents like acetone and ethanol, enabling non-destructive removal during future treatments and aligning with ethical conservation principles that prioritize minimal intervention. This solubility ensures that interventions can be undone without damaging underlying materials, a critical feature for preserving cultural heritage.
The material demonstrates exceptional long-term stability under typical low-light museum conditions, remaining non-yellowing and resistant to aging, with 1970s photochemical tests projecting no significant discoloration or solubility changes for up to 200 years of exposure, though later research indicates potential degradation under high UV exposure. Additionally, its high tolerance for ethanol minimizes the risks associated with stronger solvents, allowing safer application on sensitive surfaces while maintaining durability and flexibility.
Paraloid B-72 also offers a favorable safety profile, with the pure resin exhibiting very low toxicity through ingestion, inhalation, or skin contact under normal use conditions, and it leaves minimal residues upon removal due to its clean solubility characteristics. This low hazard level, combined with inertness toward sensitive pigments, enhances its suitability for handling in conservation environments.

Potential Drawbacks

Despite its widespread use, Paraloid B-72's reliance on volatile and flammable solvents such as acetone, toluene, or ethyl acetate for dissolution and application introduces handling risks, including fire hazards and potential skin irritation during preparation and use.
Excessive application of Paraloid B-72 can lead to over-consolidation, particularly on flexible materials like leather, where it forms stiff bonds that compromise the material's natural pliability.
Long-term studies indicate that while Paraloid B-72 exhibits good overall stability under indoor conditions, exposure to environmental factors such as UV light and oxidationparticularly in outdoor settingscan result in minor embrittlement, yellowing, and increased brittleness over decades, potentially leading to fissures and detachments on treated surfaces.
Additionally, conservation-grade Paraloid B-72 is more expensive per unit weight for small quantities, typically $15$50 per pound from specialized suppliers, compared to under $10 per pound for large-scale industrial volumesdue to packaging, purity standards, and distribution for heritage applications.
