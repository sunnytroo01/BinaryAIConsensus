 A microstrip is a planar transmission line consisting of a flat conducting strip placed on one side of a dielectric substrate, with a ground plane on the opposite side, used to transmit microwave and radio-frequency signals. This open structure allows electromagnetic fields to extend into both the dielectric substrate and the surrounding air, resulting in a quasi-transverse electromagnetic (quasi-TEM) mode of propagation that supports frequencies from DC up to several gigahertz, though with frequency-dependent effective dielectric constants. Developed in the early 1950s as a compact alternative to coaxial and waveguide lines, microstrip enables the integration of passive and active components in microwave integrated circuits (MICs) and monolithic microwave integrated circuits (MMICs). Its characteristic impedance typically ranges from 20 to 120 ohms, determined by the strip width, substrate thickness, and dielectric constant (usually 2 to 10 times that of free space), making it suitable for broadband applications despite losses from radiation, conductors, and dielectrics. Key advantages include ease of fabrication using printed circuit board techniques, small size, low weight, and compatibility with semiconductor devices for hybrid and monolithic integration, though challenges like unwanted higher-order modes limit upper-frequency operation to below the point where substrate thickness exceeds one-quarter wavelength. Microstrips are widely employed in antennas, filters, couplers, and RF front-ends for wireless communications, radar systems, and consumer electronics.

Historical Development

The origins of microstrip technology trace back to the early 1950s, when researchers at ITT Laboratories sought alternatives to bulky coaxial cables for high-frequency transmission. In 1952, D. D. Grieg and H. F. Engelmann introduced microstrip as a planar transmission line consisting of a strip conductor on a dielectric substrate backed by a ground plane, demonstrating its potential for kilomegacycle (microwave) frequencies in a seminal paper published in the Proceedings of the IRE. This innovation, patented and developed at ITT, addressed the need for compact, low-loss waveguides suitable for emerging radar and communication systems, marking the first practical realization of what would become a cornerstone of microwave engineering.
During the 1960s and 1970s, microstrip evolved rapidly as a key element in microwave integrated circuits (MICs), enabling the integration of passive and active components on a single substrate. Harold A. Wheeler advanced the theoretical foundation in 1965 with his analysis of transmission-line properties for parallel strips separated by a dielectric sheet, providing formulas for characteristic impedance that facilitated precise design and widespread adoption. Concurrently, M. V. Schneider's 1969 work detailed microstrip lines specifically for MIC applications, emphasizing fabrication techniques using thin- and thick-film deposition on substrates like alumina, which supported hybrid circuits operating up to millimeter waves and spurred commercial MIC production for telecommunications and defense. These contributions, building on early MIC concepts from the mid-1960s, transformed microstrip from a novel line into the backbone of planar microwave circuitry by the late 1970s.
The 1980s saw microstrip's transition to monolithic microwave integrated circuits (MMICs), particularly with gallium arsenide (GaAs) substrates that offered superior high-frequency performance over silicon. Early GaAs MMIC efforts, starting around 1972 but accelerating in the early 1980s, integrated microstrip interconnects with MESFET transistors, enabling compact amplifiers, mixers, and phase shifters for radar and satellite systems. This shift reduced assembly costs and improved reliability, with microstrip's planar geometry proving ideal for GaAs processing, leading to the first commercial MMIC products by mid-decade.
In the 1990s, microstrip expanded beyond pure microwave analog applications into high-speed digital circuits, where its low dispersion and ease of integration with printed circuit boards (PCBs) addressed signal integrity challenges in emerging gigabit data rates. Researchers developed coupled microstrip models for mixed-signal environments, supporting applications in GaAs-based digital logic and interconnects for computers and networks. Wheeler's impedance formulations remained influential, adapted for broadband digital transmission lines.
Recent milestones from the 2000s to 2025 have integrated microstrip with advanced structures like substrate-integrated waveguides (SIW), metamaterials, and 5G/6G systems. SIW, pioneered in the early 2000s by D. Deslandes and K. Wu, embeds microstrip-like vias in substrates to mimic rectangular waveguides, enhancing low-loss performance for millimeter-wave circuits. By the 2010s, microstrip antennas incorporating metamaterials improved gain and bandwidth for 5G base stations, with ongoing developments in reconfigurable designs for 6G terahertz bands. As of 2025, hybrid microstrip-metamaterial patches enable compact, high-efficiency arrays for IoT and beyond-5G networks, reflecting microstrip's enduring adaptability.

Electromagnetic Characteristics

Inhomogeneity and Quasi-TEM Mode

Microstrip transmission lines exhibit inherent inhomogeneity arising from the dielectric interface, where the region above the conducting strip is filled with air (permittivity = 1) and the region below consists of a substrate with relative permittivity _r > 1. This configuration leads to hybrid electromagnetic fields that are not confined solely to either medium, with electric field lines partially embedded in the dielectric and partially fringing into the air.
Consequently, microstrip cannot support a pure transverse electromagnetic (TEM) mode, as the differing wave velocities in air and the dielectric prevent uniform transverse field propagation; instead, the dominant propagation mode is quasi-TEM, featuring small but non-zero longitudinal components of both electric (E_z) and magnetic (H_z) fields. The quasi-TEM approximation holds well for narrow strips, where the width-to-substrate-height ratio (w/h) is low, and at low frequencies, typically f < 1020 GHz, provided the operating wavelength significantly exceeds the cross-sectional dimensions.
In the quasi-TEM mode, the field distribution is predominantly transverse, with concentrated electric fields between the strip and ground plane in the dielectric, but substantial fringing fields extend laterally and upward into the air, blending characteristics of both TEM and higher-order TE/TM modes without fully aligning with either. This fringing effect arises from the sharp discontinuity at the dielectric-air boundary, distorting the field lines and contributing to the hybrid nature of the mode.
Beyond the quasi-TEM regime, higher-order modes such as the lowest-order TM mode emerge when the frequency surpasses their cutoff values, which are determined by the strip geometry and substrate parameters, potentially leading to multimode propagation and signal distortion. These cutoffs, often in the range of several GHz for typical substrates, define the practical upper frequency limit for reliable quasi-TEM operation in microstrip designs.
The structural inhomogeneity further manifests in dispersion, where the phase velocity of the quasi-TEM mode decreases with frequency, transitioning from a higher value at low frequencies (with fields more extended into air) toward the slower velocity in the dielectric at higher frequencies, thereby altering the effective propagation characteristics.

Influence of Enclosure

The presence of a metallic enclosure around a microstrip line significantly alters its electromagnetic behavior by confining the fringing fields that normally extend into the air region above the substrate. This confinement increases the proportion of the electric field lines within the dielectric substrate, thereby raising the effective dielectric constant _eff compared to an open structure. Consequently, the characteristic impedance Z_0 decreases for the same strip width and substrate thickness, as Z_0 is inversely related to the square root of _eff.
In partial enclosures, such as those with only a top metallic cover (covered microstrip), the primary impact is on the upper fringing fields, leading to a moderate increase in _eff and reduction in Z_0, particularly when the cover height is comparable to the substrate thickness. Full enclosures, or boxed microstrip, incorporate side walls in addition to the top cover, further restricting lateral fringing fields and providing more uniform field confinement. These shielding types suppress higher-order modes by defining boundary conditions that prevent spurious waveguide-like propagation, which is beneficial for maintaining quasi-TEM operation up to higher frequencies. However, if the enclosure dimensions are not sufficiently large, cavity resonances can arise, introducing frequency-dependent variations in Z_0 and potential unwanted coupling between circuit elements.
The trade-offs of enclosing a microstrip line include reduced radiation losses and improved electromagnetic interference (EMI) shielding, which enhance signal integrity in dense integrated circuits, but at the cost of potentially higher ohmic and dielectric losses at elevated frequencies due to increased current crowding near the metal walls. Experimental measurements on shielded structures, using techniques like ring resonators for dispersion characterization, have validated theoretical models, showing deviations in predicted Z_0 of less than 5% when hybrid-mode analysis is employed.
Design guidelines for enclosed microstrip emphasize dimensioning the enclosure to minimize perturbations while achieving shielding benefits: the height from the ground plane to the top cover should exceed 5 to 8 times the substrate thickness h, and the lateral distance from the strip edges to side walls should be at least 4 to 5 times the strip width w to render enclosure effects negligible on Z_0. For tighter enclosures where corrections are needed, empirical adjustments to open-line Z_0 formulas are applied, often incorporating geometric ratios like cover height to substrate thickness; full-wave electromagnetic simulations are recommended for precise optimization in high-frequency applications.

Variations

Suspended Microstrip

Suspended microstrip is a variant of the microstrip transmission line in which the conducting strip is positioned above a dielectric substrate with an intervening air gap, often supported by low-_r foam, dielectric bridges, or support posts. This configuration places the substrate directly on the ground plane, but the air gap between the strip and substrate reduces the portion of the electromagnetic field confined within the dielectric.
The primary benefits of suspended microstrip over standard microstrip include a lower effective dielectric constant, which enables higher characteristic impedances, reduced propagation and dielectric losses, wider bandwidth, and minimized dispersion effects. These improvements arise from the increased field presence in the low-loss air region, leading to higher Q-factors and better performance in high-frequency circuits. For instance, suspended structures exhibit significantly lower transmission loss at millimeter-wave frequencies, such as 60 GHz.
Characteristic impedance calculations for suspended microstrip adapt standard microstrip formulas by incorporating a modified effective dielectric constant that accounts for the air gap height $ d $. Specifically, $ \varepsilon_\text{eff} $ is approximated as $ \varepsilon_\text{eff} = 1 + \frac{h}{h + d} (\varepsilon_r - 1) $, where $ h $ is the substrate thickness and $ \varepsilon_r $ is the substrate dielectric constant, reflecting the reduced dielectric filling factor. The impedance $ Z_0 $ then follows the conventional microstrip expression using this $ \varepsilon_\text{eff} $.
Fabrication of suspended microstrip presents challenges, including the precise creation and maintenance of the air gap using support posts or bridges to avoid mechanical instability or additional losses from support materials. These structures require careful alignment and may involve multi-layer processing for integration in complex circuits.
Suspended microstrip finds applications in high-power microwave components, such as filters, directional couplers, and transitions, particularly up to mm-wave frequencies, where its low-loss characteristics and high power-handling capabilityenhanced by better heat dissipation through the air gapare critical.

Inverted Microstrip

The inverted microstrip consists of a conducting strip positioned directly beneath a dielectric substrate, with the ground plane located on the top surface of the substrate, thereby placing the dielectric material primarily between the strip and the ground plane, unlike the standard microstrip where the strip resides atop the substrate. This configuration results in stronger field confinement within the dielectric, leading to an effective dielectric constant eff\epsilon_\text{eff}eff that approaches the substrate's relative permittivity r\epsilon_rr, particularly for wider strips where fringing fields into the air below the strip are minimized.
Key advantages of the inverted microstrip include enhanced shielding against radiation losses due to the ground plane's position above the dielectric, which reduces spurious emissions compared to the open structure of conventional microstrip lines. It also exhibits lower dispersion because of the more homogeneous dielectric environment for the propagating fields, making it suitable for broadband applications, and supports integration in multilayer printed circuit boards (PCBs) where the structure can be embedded between layers for improved isolation.
The characteristic impedance Z0Z_0Z0 of an inverted microstrip can be approximated using the parallel-plate transmission line model, given by Z0377rhwZ_0 \approx \frac{377}{\sqrt{\epsilon_r}} \cdot \frac{h}{w}Z0r377wh for wide strips (whw \gg hwh), where hhh is the substrate thickness and www is the strip width; this reflects the near-full immersion of fields in the dielectric. More precise calculations often require numerical methods, as closed-form expressions account for fringing effects but vary with geometry.
Despite these benefits, the inverted microstrip presents drawbacks such as increased fabrication complexity, particularly in multilayer environments where precise alignment of the embedded strip is needed, and potential for higher conductor losses due to challenges in achieving uniform contact between the strip and substrate. Additionally, it may support higher-order modes more readily in embedded forms, complicating single-mode operation in integrated circuits.
In integrated circuits, the inverted microstrip is employed for its superior isolation properties, enabling compact routing in silicon-based RFICs or hybrid modules where minimizing crosstalk between adjacent lines is critical.

Discontinuities

Bends

Bends in microstrip transmission lines are essential for routing signals in compact microwave circuits, but they introduce discontinuities that disrupt the characteristic impedance and generate unwanted radiation. These effects arise primarily from abrupt changes in the current path, leading to excess electric and magnetic fields at the bend region. Sharp right-angle bends, whether unmitered or mitered, and smooth curved arcs represent the main types, each with distinct modeling approaches and performance characteristics.
Right-angle bends exhibit discontinuity effects modeled by an equivalent circuit consisting of a shunt capacitance due to charge accumulation at the inner corner and a series inductance from current crowding at the outer edge. The shunt capacitance is given by $ C_b \approx 0.02 \left( \frac{w}{h} \right) $ pF for substrates with relative permittivity $ \varepsilon_r \leq 9.6 $, where $ w $ is the strip width and $ h $ is the substrate thickness. The associated series inductance is approximately $ L_b \approx 0.23 \left( \frac{w}{h} \right)^{-0.5} $ nH, with accuracy within 3% for $ 0.1 \leq w/h \leq 2.0 $. These lumped elements cause impedance mismatches, resulting in reflections that increase with frequency; for unmitered bends, return loss can exceed -10 dB above several GHz depending on the line width.
Radiation losses in bends stem from the excitation of transverse electromagnetic fields, with power loss increasing as the bend angle sharpens due to enhanced fringing. For 90-degree right-angle bends on typical substrates, radiated power can account for up to 11% of the incident energy, compared to 6-7% for straight sections, as measured using TEM cell techniques and numerical simulations. Curved arc bends mitigate some radiation by distributing the turn over a radius, but small radii still induce losses proportional to the curvature; equivalent circuit models for radial bends incorporate distributed capacitance and inductance along the arc to predict these effects.

Junctions

Junctions in microstrip transmission lines represent abrupt changes in structure, such as variations in strip width or branching configurations, which introduce discontinuities that disrupt the quasi-TEM mode propagation. These elements are essential in microwave circuits for power division, filtering, and matching, but they generate parasitic reactances leading to reflections and insertion losses if not properly modeled. Common types include step junctions, where the strip width changes suddenly; T-junctions, featuring a main line with a perpendicular branch; and cross-junctions, involving two intersecting perpendicular lines.
Step junctions occur at transitions between microstrip sections of differing widths, primarily inducing a shunt capacitance due to fringing fields at the wider strip's edge. This capacitance arises from excess charge accumulation and can be approximated using fringing field models, such as $ C_p = \frac{\varepsilon_0 (\varepsilon_{eff} + 1)}{2} (w_2 - w_1) \ln\left(1 + \frac{2h}{w_1}\right) $ for small steps (where $ w_2 > w_1 $), or more accurate empirical expressions accounting for width ratio and substrate permittivity. A series inductance also arises from the magnetic field constriction at the narrower strip, modeled empirically as $ L_s \approx \mu_0 h \left \left( \frac{Z_{01}}{Z_{02}} - 1 \right) $, where $ Z_{01} $ and $ Z_{02} $ are the characteristic impedances of the narrower and wider lines, respectively. Equivalent circuit models, typically pi- or T-networks, represent these as lumped elements: a shunt capacitor across the junction flanked by series inductors for the step.
T-junctions and cross-junctions exhibit more complex behavior due to their three- or four-port nature, exciting higher-order modes and orthogonal fields that amplify parasitic reactance. In T-junctions, the branch arm introduces a shunt capacitance and series inductances in a T-network configuration, with reactance values depending on arm widths and substrate parameters, leading to frequency-dependent scattering parameters. Cross-junctions similarly model as pi-networks with central shunt capacitance and arm inductances, where parasitic elements are determined by junction geometry and effective dielectric constant using planar patch analysis or spectral domain methods, causing mode conversion and radiation losses. These effects are analyzed using full-wave methods like spectral domain techniques for accurate multi-mode characterization up to millimeter-wave frequencies.
To mitigate reflections from these junctions, tapered transitions are employed, such as linear ramps or exponential curves that gradually vary the strip width over a length of several wavelengths, achieving broadband impedance matching with return loss below -20 dB across octave bands. For complex or high-frequency designs, full-wave electromagnetic simulations using tools like finite element or method-of-moments solvers are recommended to capture dispersive effects and enclosure influences not accounted for in quasi-static models.

Coupled Microstrips

Even and Odd Mode Analysis

Coupled microstrip lines consist of two parallel conducting strips separated by a small distance $ s $ on a dielectric substrate above a ground plane, forming a structure that supports two primary quasi-TEM propagation modes: the even mode, characterized by symmetric field distributions, and the odd mode, characterized by antisymmetric field distributions.
In the even mode, the voltages along the two strips are in phase and equal in magnitude, resulting in a magnetic wall at the plane of symmetry between the strips, where the tangential magnetic field is maximum and no transverse current flows across this plane. For the odd mode, the voltages are equal in magnitude but 180 degrees out of phase, establishing an electric wall at the symmetry plane, where the potential is zero and the tangential electric field is maximum. These boundary conditions simplify the analysis by allowing the coupled system to be modeled as two independent single microstrip lines with modified geometries.
The inhomogeneous dielectric environment of microstrip lines leads to distinct effective permittivities for each mode, causing differences in phase velocities. The even mode has a higher effective dielectric constant ($ \epsilon_{\text{eff,even}} $) because more electric field lines penetrate the substrate to the ground plane, whereas the odd mode exhibits a lower $ \epsilon_{\text{eff,odd}} $ as fields concentrate primarily in the air region between the strips, reducing substrate influence. This velocity disparity is quantified by the velocity coupling coefficient $ k_v = \frac{v_{\text{odd}} - v_{\text{even}}}{v_{\text{odd}} + v_{\text{even}}} $, which measures the modal mismatch and impacts device performance in applications like directional couplers.
The even- and odd-mode analysis framework for coupled transmission lines emerged from the foundational developments in coupled mode theory during the 1950s, pioneered for microwave applications by researchers such as Pierce and Miller.

As an illustrative example, consider symmetric coupled microstrips with strip width $ w = 1 $ mm, gap $ s = 0.5 $ mm, substrate height $ h = 0.635 $ mm, and $ \varepsilon_r = 4.5 $. The single-line $ Z_{0,\text{single}} \approx 48.5 $ ; using Chen's formulas, $ Z_{0e} \approx 62.3 $ and $ Z_{0o} \approx 36.8 $ , while full-wave simulation (e.g., via method of moments) yields 63.1 and 36.2 , respectively, demonstrating formula accuracy within 1.3%.

The resulting modes, often termed c-mode and -mode, exhibit mixed field distributions that account for the unequal line geometries.
Analysis of these structures typically employs numerical methods, such as the spectral domain approach, which transforms the problem into the Fourier domain to solve for mixed-mode propagation constants and impedances via Galerkin's method or integral equations. This method efficiently handles the hybrid nature of microstrip modes (quasi-TEM with higher-order effects) and provides accurate results for dispersion and coupling coefficients, validated against measurements with errors below 2% in effective permittivity for lines with w1/h=0.5w_1/h = 0.5w1/h=0.5, w2/h=1.5w_2/h = 1.5w2/h=1.5, and s/h=0.2s/h = 0.2s/h=0.2.

These supermodes represent collective excitations, enabling efficient simulation of crosstalk and signal integrity in dense interconnects. Such configurations are commonly applied in power dividers, where multiple coupled sections achieve compact designs with isolation resistors, as in -type structures.

Losses and Dispersion

Conductor and Dielectric Losses

In microstrip transmission lines, conductor losses stem from the ohmic resistance of the metallic strip and ground plane, dominated by the skin effect at microwave frequencies. The skin effect confines currents to a thin layer near the conductor surface, yielding a surface resistance $ R_s = \sqrt{\pi f \mu / \sigma} $, where $ f $ is the operating frequency, $ \mu $ is the magnetic permeability, and $ \sigma $ is the conductor conductivity (typically for copper, $ \sigma \approx 5.8 \times 10^7 $ S/m). This resistance contributes to the attenuation constant $ \alpha_c \approx R_s / (Z_0 w) $ for the dominant strip contribution, where $ Z_0 $ is the characteristic impedance and $ w $ is the strip width; contributions from the ground plane are smaller but can be included via an effective width adjustment.
Dielectric losses arise from energy dissipation in the substrate material, characterized by its loss tangent $ \tan \delta $. The attenuation due to these losses is approximated as $ \alpha_d = \frac{k_0}{2} \sqrt{\epsilon_\mathrm{eff}} \tan \delta \frac{\epsilon_\mathrm{eff} - 1}{\epsilon_r - 1} $, where $ k_0 = 2\pi f / c $ is the free-space wavenumber, $ \epsilon_\mathrm{eff} $ is the effective permittivity, $ \epsilon_r $ is the relative permittivity of the substrate, and $ c $ is the speed of light. This expression accounts for the quasi-TEM mode where fields partially occupy air, reducing the effective loss compared to fully embedded lines via the filling factor $ \frac{\epsilon_\mathrm{eff} - 1}{\epsilon_r - 1} $, which represents the fraction of electric energy in the dielectric.
The total attenuation $ \alpha = \alpha_c + \alpha_d $ exhibits frequency dependence: $ \alpha_c $ scales with $ \sqrt{f} $ due to the skin depth decreasing as $ 1/\sqrt{f} $, while $ \alpha_d $ increases linearly with $ f $ as dissipation grows proportionally to the electric field energy stored in the dielectric. To separate these components experimentally, the Wheeler incremental inductance method measures the change in quality factor or resonant frequency of a microstrip resonator upon small dimensional perturbations, isolating conductor effects from dielectric ones. In coupled microstrips, the odd mode suffers slightly higher attenuation than the even mode owing to enhanced electric fields in the coupling region, amplifying both conductor and dielectric contributions.

Radiation and Surface Wave Losses

In microstrip transmission lines, radiation losses manifest as power leakage into free space, primarily triggered by discontinuities such as abrupt open ends, bends, and junctions, where asymmetric fringing fields disrupt guided wave propagation. These effects are exacerbated in regions of high curvature, like sharp corners, leading to unintended electromagnetic radiation that reduces signal integrity and efficiency. For instance, an abruptly open-circuited microstrip line can radiate significantly due to the sudden termination, with the radiated fields behaving similarly to those of a slot antenna.
Radiation losses increase with operating frequency, as higher frequencies enhance field fringing and wavelength scaling, and with strip width, which broadens the radiating aperture. Mitigation strategies include the use of guard rings around discontinuities to confine fields and metallic shielding enclosures to suppress outward propagation, thereby reducing spurious emissions.
Surface wave losses occur when guided mode energy couples to substrate-bound waves, particularly the dominant TM0_00 mode, which lacks a cutoff frequency and propagates at all frequencies within the dielectric. Excitation of this mode is prominent in structures with thick substrates (large height hhh) or wide strips (www), as these dimensions increase the overlap between the microstrip fields and the substrate interface, promoting power transfer to non-radiating surface waves that attenuate the signal. Attenuation models for surface waves typically involve solving for the mode's propagation constant and power distribution, showing that losses rise with frequency due to enhanced mode coupling and with strip width via greater evanescent field extent into the substrate.
These radiation and surface wave mechanisms contribute to dispersion in microstrip lines, where the effective permittivity eff\varepsilon_{\text{eff}}eff varies with frequency, resulting in group velocity fluctuations that distort pulse propagation. Dispersion primarily arises from the frequency dependence of the effective dielectric constant due to the hybrid nature of modes and coupling to higher-order modes, modeled using standard analytical approaches such as the Schneider model. The inherent inhomogeneity of the microstripspanning air and dielectric regionsfacilitates this coupling to both radiative space waves and substrate-bound modes.
Liquid crystal polymers (LCP) offer low dielectric loss tangent (tan <0.002\delta < 0.002<0.002) and dimensional stability up to millimeter waves, enabling microstrip designs with insertion losses as low as 0.11 dB/mm at 40 GHz (as measured around 2007), with ongoing applications in 5G circuits as of 2020. Hybrid LCP formulations further reduce transmission losses compared to traditional polyimides, supporting high-frequency applications while minimizing surface wave excitation.

Applications

Microwave Circuits and PCBs

Microstrip transmission lines play a pivotal role in monolithic microwave integrated circuits (MMICs) and microwave integrated circuits (MICs), enabling the design of compact components such as filters, low-noise amplifiers, and oscillators that operate across frequencies from 1 to 100 GHz. In MMICs, microstrip structures facilitate high-integration levels for applications like X-band amplifiers, where they support low insertion loss and precise impedance matching essential for signal amplification in radar and communication systems. Similarly, microstrip-based oscillators and filters leverage the planar geometry to achieve tunable performance in microwave subsystems, minimizing parasitic effects at these frequencies.
In printed circuit boards (PCBs), microstrip lines are employed as impedance-controlled traces to ensure signal integrity in high-speed digital interfaces, including USB 3.0 and PCIe 5.0, supporting data rates up to 32 GT/s per lane. For USB implementations, microstrip traces maintain a differential impedance of 90 5 , reducing reflections and crosstalk in environments with bit rates exceeding 5 Gbps. Characteristic impedance control in these traces is critical for preserving waveform quality over extended lengths. In PCIe designs, microstrip routing on outer layers helps mitigate losses while adhering to 100 differential standards, enabling reliable operation in server and networking hardware.
Key design considerations for microstrip in PCBs include via transitions and multilayer stacking, where microstrip is typically placed on outer layers to interface with embedded striplines. Via transitions, such as microstrip-to-stripline conversions, require careful impedance matching to minimize parasitic inductance, often using blind or buried vias with diameters of 0.20.4 mm and spacing at least /20 for RF isolation. In multilayer configurations, stitching vias provide continuous ground paths across layers, enhancing shielding and reducing radiation in high-frequency signals.
Recent advancements in 5G mm-wave PCBs incorporate microstrip lines with low-loss dielectrics, such as those with dielectric constants (Dk) below 3.5 and loss tangents (Df) under 0.003, to support frequencies from 24 to 53 GHz while meeting 2025 3GPP standards for sub-6 GHz and mm-wave bands. These materials, like Rogers RO3000 series, enable hybrid integration of mm-wave modules with minimal insertion loss in smartphone and base station PCBs.
A notable case study involves microstrip directional couplers in Doppler motion detection sensors, such as the MURENA system operating at 1011 GHz, where the coupler achieves a coupling factor of -6.1 dB, directivity over 28 dB, and 84% efficiency for coherent signal mixing in radar-like security applications. This integration demonstrates microstrip's utility in compact, low-cost radar subsystems by cascading coupled lines on standard PCBs with _r = 3.5.

Antennas and Filters

Microstrip antennas, commonly implemented as patch antennas, consist of a metallic patchtypically rectangular or circularetched on one side of a dielectric substrate with a ground plane on the opposite side. Rectangular patches resonate in the fundamental TM10 mode when the patch length equals approximately half the guided wavelength (g/2), determined by the substrate's effective dielectric constant and thickness. Circular patches operate similarly, with resonance occurring when the patch radius is approximately 0.293 _g (or 0.586 _g / 2) for the dominant TM11 mode, offering circular polarization potential. These configurations provide broadside radiation patterns with gains around 6-7 dBi, though inherent bandwidths are narrow, typically 1-5% for return loss below -10 dB, limited by the substrate's low Q-factor and thin profile.
In filter applications, microstrip structures enable compact frequency-selective devices, such as edge-coupled bandpass filters formed by parallel coupled-line sections that act as /2 resonators. These filters leverage coupled-line theory for even- and odd-mode propagation to achieve bandpass responses, with design parameters like coupling gaps and line widths controlling the center frequency and bandwidth. Stub-loaded filters use open- or short-circuited stubstypically /4 long at the center frequencyconnected in shunt or series to create bandpass or bandstop characteristics, offering simplicity for narrowband selectivity. Interdigital filters employ interleaved finger-like resonators in a comb structure, providing higher-order responses and sharper roll-offs in a compact layout, suitable for integration in RF front-ends.
The primary advantages of microstrip antennas and filters stem from their planar, low-profile nature, facilitating easy fabrication on printed circuit boards and seamless integration with feed networks like microstrip lines or corporate feeds for array configurations. This conformability supports applications in aerospace and mobile systems where space and weight constraints are critical.
Post-2010 advances have focused on bandwidth enhancement through metamaterial loading, where structures like split-ring resonators or complementary split-ring resonators are incorporated as superstrates or defected grounds to introduce negative permittivity or permeability, effectively broadening the impedance match. For instance, metamaterial-loaded patches can increase bandwidth by 2-3 times compared to conventional designs by exciting additional resonances and improving radiation efficiency.
A representative example is a 2.4 GHz rectangular microstrip patch antenna for WiFi applications, designed on an FR4 substrate (r = 4.4, h = 1.6 mm) with patch dimensions of length 28.61 mm and width 37.93 mm, fed via an inset microstrip line of width 2.52 mm and depth 10.69 mm. This configuration achieves resonance at 2.393 GHz with a bandwidth of 58 MHz (2.4%), return loss of -38.86 dB, and VSWR of 1.02, demonstrating efficient performance for S-band communications.
