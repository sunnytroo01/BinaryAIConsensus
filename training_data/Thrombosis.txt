Thrombosis
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Thrombosis is the formation of a blood clot, known as a thrombus, within a blood vessel that partially or completely obstructs the normal flow of blood. This pathological process differs from hemostasis, the body's normal mechanism to stop bleeding after injury, as thrombosis occurs abnormally and can disrupt circulation in arteries, veins, or capillaries.
Thrombosis is broadly classified into venous and arterial types, with venous thrombosis primarily affecting the deep veins of the legs in a condition called deep vein thrombosis (DVT), while arterial thrombosis often arises from atherosclerotic plaques. Venous thromboembolism (VTE), which encompasses DVT and its potential complication of pulmonary embolism (PE), represents a significant subset of venous events. The underlying mechanisms are described by Virchow's triad, involving blood stasis, vascular endothelial injury, and hypercoagulability of the blood.
Key risk factors for thrombosis include advanced age, immobility, surgery, trauma, pregnancy, obesity, and certain medical conditions such as cancer or inherited thrombophilias. Arterial thrombosis shares some risk factors with venous types, including hypertension, diabetes, and hyperlipidemia, highlighting potential overlaps in pathophysiology.
Complications of thrombosis are severe and life-threatening; venous clots can dislodge to cause PE, leading to respiratory failure, while arterial blockages may result in myocardial infarction, stroke, or limb ischemia. Globally, thrombotic disorders contribute substantially to morbidity and mortality, with acute arterial and venous events being leading causes of death.

Definition and Classification

Definition

Thrombosis is the formation of a blood clot, termed a thrombus, within a blood vessel that impedes normal blood flow. This pathological process contrasts with physiological hemostasis, the body's natural mechanism to arrest bleeding at sites of vascular injury by forming temporary clots that dissolve once healing occurs. While hemostasis maintains vascular integrity, uncontrolled thrombosis can disrupt circulation and lead to tissue damage.
The term "thrombosis" originates from the Ancient Greek word thrombos, meaning "clot" or "lump," reflecting its description of blood coagulation. It was first systematically described in 1856 by German pathologist Rudolf Virchow, whose observations on postmortem examinations linked thrombi to vascular pathology.
A thrombus is primarily composed of aggregated platelets, cross-linked fibrin strands, and entrapped red blood cells, forming a semi-solid mass that adheres to the vessel wall. This structure can cause local ischemia by occluding blood supply or result in embolism if fragments detach and travel downstream. Unlike an embolus, which is a mobile particle such as a dislodged thrombus or other debris circulating in the bloodstream, a thrombus develops in situ at the site of initiation.

Types

Thrombosis is primarily classified into arterial and venous types based on the vascular bed affected, with further subdivisions by location and etiology.
Arterial thrombosis involves clot formation within arteries, typically resulting in white thrombi that are platelet-rich due to the high shear stress environment. These thrombi often develop on atherosclerotic plaques and can lead to downstream ischemia. Subtypes include coronary artery thrombosis, which affects the heart's blood supply; cerebral artery thrombosis, impacting brain vasculature; and peripheral artery thrombosis, commonly occurring in the limbs and causing acute limb ischemia.
Venous thrombosis, in contrast, forms in veins and produces red thrombi that are predominantly composed of fibrin and entrapped red blood cells, arising in regions of stasis or low flow. Key subtypes encompass deep vein thrombosis (DVT), which occurs in deep veins such as those in the legs or arms; superficial vein thrombosis, involving veins closer to the skin surface; and splanchnic vein thrombosis, affecting abdominal veins like the portal or hepatic systems.
Special types of thrombosis occur in unusual sites or through distinct processes. Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis involves clot formation in the brain's dural sinuses, a rare venous variant that impedes cerebral drainage. Retinal vein thrombosis affects the veins draining the retina, potentially leading to ocular vascular compromise. Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) represents a consumptive form, characterized by widespread microvascular thrombosis triggered by systemic activation of coagulation, often in critical illnesses.
Etiologically, thrombosis is distinguished as an in situ process of clot formation at the site of vascular injury or stasis, whereas embolic events involve clots that originate elsewhere and travel to occlude vessels, though the focus remains on primary in situ development in most classifications.

Epidemiology

Incidence and Prevalence

Thrombosis, encompassing both venous thromboembolism (VTE) and arterial thrombotic events, represents a significant global health burden with varying incidence rates. The annual incidence of VTE is estimated at 1-2 cases per 1,000 adults worldwide, primarily affecting those in high-income regions where diagnostic capabilities are advanced. For arterial thrombosis, acute myocardial infarction (MI) accounts for approximately 7 million new cases annually worldwide, as part of broader ischemic heart disease with over 30 million incident cases.
Prevalence escalates markedly with age, with individuals over 65 years facing a 5-10 times higher risk compared to younger adults, driven by cumulative physiological changes and comorbidities. Overall VTE incidence is similar between sexes, but elevated in women during reproductive years due to pregnancy-related hypercoagulability, which increases risk 4-5 fold compared to non-pregnant peers. In contrast, arterial thrombosis, including MI and stroke, shows higher rates in men across most age groups.
Geographically, VTE incidence is higher in Western countries, with around 900,000 cases reported annually in the United States alone, reflecting differences in lifestyle, healthcare access, and population demographics. Trends indicate a rising burden globally, attributed to aging populations and increased immobility from sedentary lifestyles. As of 2025, the global burden continues to rise, with cardiovascular disease disability-adjusted life years (DALYs) reaching 437 million in 2023, driven by aging and post-pandemic effects. Recent data highlight a post-COVID-19 surge, with thrombotic events increasing 2-3 fold among hospitalized patients due to the virus's prothrombotic effects. Overall, thrombotic diseases contribute to approximately 15 million deaths annually worldwide (1 in 4 global deaths), underscoring their role as a leading cause of mortality.

Risk Factors

Risk factors for thrombosis encompass a range of non-modifiable, modifiable, acquired, and inherited elements that contribute to the development of both arterial and venous forms, often interacting to amplify predisposition. These factors align with components of Virchow's triad, such as blood stasis from immobility or hypercoagulability from genetic mutations.

Non-Modifiable Risk Factors

Non-modifiable risk factors include advancing age and genetic predispositions that cannot be altered. Age greater than 40 years approximately doubles the risk of thrombosis compared to younger individuals, with the incidence rising progressively thereafter due to cumulative vascular changes. Genetic factors, such as the Factor V Leiden mutation, which affects the factor V protein and impairs inactivation by protein C, occur in about 5% of Caucasians and confer a 3- to 8-fold increased risk of venous thromboembolism (VTE). Similarly, the prothrombin G20210A mutation, present in 1-2% of the general population, leads to elevated prothrombin levels and a 2- to 3-fold higher risk of VTE.

Modifiable Risk Factors

Modifiable risk factors involve lifestyle and behavioral choices that can be addressed to mitigate thrombosis risk. Smoking significantly elevates the risk of arterial thrombosis by 2- to 4-fold through endothelial damage and pro-thrombotic effects on blood components. Obesity, defined as a body mass index (BMI) greater than 30 kg/m, increases VTE risk by 2- to 3-fold by promoting inflammation, venous stasis, and altered coagulation. Immobility, such as prolonged bed rest after surgery or during long-haul flights exceeding 4 hours, heightens risk by causing venous stasis, with post-surgical immobility associated with a 10- to 100-fold transient increase depending on the procedure.

Acquired Risk Factors

Acquired risk factors arise from medical conditions, treatments, or physiological states. Cancer substantially raises thrombosis risk by 4- to 7-fold, particularly with pancreatic, lung, or colorectal malignancies, due to tumor-induced hypercoagulability and inflammation. Pregnancy increases VTE risk approximately 5-fold, especially in the third trimester and postpartum period, owing to venous compression, hormonal changes, and reduced fibrinolysis. Use of oral contraceptives, particularly those containing estrogen, elevates VTE risk by 3- to 6-fold, with higher risks for third- and fourth-generation progestins.

Disease-Related Risk Factors

Certain underlying diseases predispose individuals to specific types of thrombosis. Atherosclerosis, characterized by plaque buildup in arteries, serves as a primary risk factor for arterial thrombosis by providing a nidus for platelet activation and clot formation. Heart failure increases venous thrombosis risk through venous stasis from reduced cardiac output and systemic inflammation. Emerging data highlight COVID-19 as a significant risk factor for both arterial and venous thrombosis, driven by endothelial damage, hypercoagulability, and inflammatory cytokine storms, with hospitalized patients facing up to a 20-fold higher risk.

Inherited Thrombophilias

Inherited thrombophilias represent rare genetic deficiencies that markedly elevate baseline thrombosis risk. Protein C deficiency, affecting 0.2-0.5% of the population, impairs anticoagulation and increases VTE risk by 7- to 10-fold. Protein S deficiency, with a prevalence of 0.03-0.13%, similarly disrupts the protein C pathway, conferring a 2- to 11-fold risk elevation. Antithrombin deficiency, occurring in 0.02-0.2% of individuals, inhibits thrombin and other coagulation factors, resulting in a 5- to 25-fold increased VTE risk.

Pathophysiology

Virchow's Triad

Virchow's triad, a foundational concept in the pathophysiology of thrombosis, was first proposed by German pathologist Rudolf Virchow in 1856 to explain the development of thrombi and emboli. This framework identifies three primary factors that predispose to thrombus formation: disturbances in blood flow (stasis), injury to the vascular endothelium, and hypercoagulability of the blood. Despite its age, the triad remains a central model for understanding thrombosis risk across arterial and venous systems.
The three components interact dynamically to promote clot formation. Stasis refers to slowed or turbulent blood flow, which allows coagulation factors to accumulate and reduces the clearance of activated clotting elements; a classic example is deep vein thrombosis (DVT) induced by prolonged bedrest, where immobility leads to venous pooling in the lower extremities. Endothelial injury involves damage to the inner lining of blood vessels, exposing subendothelial collagen and tissue factor that trigger platelet adhesion and the coagulation cascade; this is evident in atherosclerosis, where plaque rupture disrupts the endothelium and initiates arterial thrombosis. Hypercoagulability encompasses acquired or inherited states that shift the balance toward clot formation, such as elevated levels of procoagulant factors; in cancer patients, tumor cells release tissue factor and induce a prothrombotic state, significantly increasing VTE risk.
In clinical scenarios, these factors often coexist and amplify one another, as seen in postoperative settings where surgical trauma causes endothelial injury while immobilization promotes stasis. For instance, smoking, a key risk factor for endothelial injury, can compound other elements of the triad by promoting oxidative stress and inflammation in vessel walls.
Contemporary perspectives have expanded Virchow's triad to incorporate inflammation as a unifying mechanism that interconnects its components. Inflammatory processes, mediated by cytokines such as interleukin-6 (IL-6), can induce endothelial dysfunction, exacerbate stasis through vasomotor changes, and directly enhance hypercoagulability by upregulating procoagulant proteins like tissue factor. This inflammatory overlay helps explain recurrent or multifactorial thrombosis, positioning it as an overarching driver in conditions like venous thromboembolism (VTE).

Molecular Mechanisms

Thrombosis initiation involves platelet activation at sites of vascular injury. Under high shear conditions, circulating platelets adhere to the subendothelial extracellular matrix primarily through the glycoprotein Ib-IX-V receptor complex binding to immobilized von Willebrand factor (vWF), which tethers to exposed collagen via the glycoprotein VI (GPVI) receptor. This adhesion triggers intracellular signaling cascades, including calcium mobilization and protein kinase C activation, leading to platelet shape change from discoid to spherical with pseudopod extension. Subsequent aggregation is amplified by the release of soluble agonists from platelet dense granules, such as adenosine diphosphate (ADP), which binds to P2Y1 and P2Y12 receptors to propagate G-protein-coupled signaling, and thromboxane A2 (TXA2), synthesized from arachidonic acid via cyclooxygenase-1 and acting on TP receptors to enhance calcium influx and integrin IIb3 activation for fibrinogen-mediated bridging. These processes form a platelet plug that temporarily seals the breach but requires stabilization by coagulation for persistent thrombus formation.
The coagulation cascade reinforces platelet aggregation through enzymatic amplification, converging on thrombin generation. The extrinsic pathway is rapidly initiated when tissue factor (TF), a transmembrane glycoprotein exposed on damaged endothelium or expressed de novo on activated cells, forms a complex with circulating factor VIIa to activate factor X to Xa and factor IX to IXa. Parallelly, the intrinsic pathway, triggered by contact activation of factor XII on anionic surfaces, sequentially activates factor XI and IX, with IXa combining with cofactor VIIIa on phospholipid membranes to further generate Xa. Factor Xa assembles with cofactor Va and calcium on anionic phospholipid surfacespredominantly provided by activated plateletsto form the prothrombinase complex, which proteolytically cleaves prothrombin (factor II) at Arg271 and Arg320 to yield active thrombin (factor IIa). Thrombin, in turn, cleaves fibrinogen into fibrin monomers that spontaneously polymerize into protofibrils, stabilized by activated factor XIIIa cross-linking, creating a fibrin mesh that entraps erythrocytes and leukocytes to consolidate the thrombus.
Pathological thrombosis arises from dysregulated regulatory mechanisms and inflammatory inputs that tip the hemostatic balance toward clot persistence. Reduced fibrinolysis, often due to plasminogen activator inhibitor-1 (PAI-1) overexpression in response to inflammatory cytokines or metabolic stress, inhibits tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) and urokinase plasminogen activator (uPA), thereby suppressing plasmin formation and impairing fibrin degradation, which sustains occlusive thrombi. Similarly, endothelial dysfunction characterized by diminished netrin-1 expression fails to suppress leukocyte chemotaxis and adhesion via UNC5B receptor signaling, promoting inflammatory cell recruitment that exacerbates prothrombotic states. Inflammation further propagates thrombosis as activated leukocytes, particularly monocytes and neutrophils, upregulate TF expression to initiate the extrinsic pathway. Neutrophils contribute through the release of neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs), web-like structures of DNA, histones, and antimicrobial proteins that provide a scaffold for platelet adhesion, aggregation, and activation of coagulation factors, enhancing thrombin generation and fibrin formation independent of cellular contact. These NETs, along with TF-bearing microparticles derived from leukocyte plasma membranes, amplify localized and systemic coagulation.
Arterial and venous thrombi exhibit distinct molecular emphases reflecting hemodynamic differences. In arteries, high shear stress (above 1000 s) prioritizes rapid platelet adhesion and TXA2/ADP-driven aggregation for white, platelet-dense thrombi, with coagulation providing secondary reinforcement. Conversely, venous thrombosis under low-flow, stagnant conditions (below 100 s) favors TF-mediated coagulation and thrombin-fibrin formation, resulting in red thrombi rich in erythrocytes enmeshed in a loose fibrin network, with platelets playing a lesser role.

Natural Course

The natural course of thrombosis involves a series of stages following initial clot formation, beginning with initiation where a temporary platelet plug forms at the site of vascular injury, rapidly adhering to the damaged endothelium to halt bleeding. This is followed by the propagation stage, during which the thrombus grows along the vessel wall through the activation of the coagulation cascade on the surface of activated platelets, extending the clot to occlude more of the vessel lumen. Stabilization then occurs as the fibrin network cross-links and organizes, incorporating inflammatory cells and fibroblasts that transform the thrombus into a more structured, scar-like tissue over time, reducing its fragility.
Resolution of the thrombus primarily relies on fibrinolysis, a process mediated by plasmin, which is generated when tissue-type plasminogen activator (tPA) converts plasminogen to its active form, leading to the enzymatic degradation of the fibrin meshwork. In many cases, this results in partial or complete lysis, particularly in smaller thrombi, but resolution is often incomplete in chronic thrombosis, promoting recanalization where channels reopen within the organized clot to restore some blood flow. The timeline of these events varies: the acute phase spans hours to days, characterized by an unstable, loosely adherent thrombus prone to dislodgement; the subacute phase lasts weeks with partial lysis and early organization; and the chronic phase extends over months, marked by fibrosis and permanent vascular remodeling.
The progression and outcome of thrombosis are influenced by the dynamic balance between pro-thrombotic factors, such as thrombin that amplifies coagulation, and anti-thrombotic mechanisms, including antithrombin and endogenous heparin-like glycosaminoglycans that inhibit thrombin activity and promote clot breakdown. A shift toward pro-thrombotic dominance can accelerate propagation and delay resolution, while enhanced anti-thrombotic activity favors earlier lysis.
Throughout propagation, there is a notable risk of thrombus fragmentation, particularly in venous thrombi, where portions of the growing clot may break off and embolize distally, potentially leading to pulmonary embolism if originating from deep veins. This embolization risk peaks during the unstable acute phase when the thrombus is least organized.

Clinical Presentation

Arterial Thrombosis

Arterial thrombosis leads to acute ischemia in the affected vascular territory due to rapid occlusion of high-flow vessels, resulting in tissue hypoxia and characteristic symptoms. The classic presentation of acute arterial occlusion, particularly in the extremities, is remembered by the "6 P's": pain (sudden and severe, often the first symptom), pallor (pale skin due to reduced perfusion), pulselessness (absent or diminished pulses distal to the occlusion), paresthesia (numbness or tingling from nerve ischemia), paralysis (motor weakness or loss), and poikilothermia (coolness of the limb compared to the contralateral side). These signs reflect the downstream effects of blocked arterial flow and can progress to irreversible tissue damage if not addressed promptly.
Common sites of arterial thrombosis include the coronary, cerebral, and peripheral arteries, each producing distinct ischemic manifestations. In the coronary arteries, thrombosis often underlies acute myocardial infarction (MI), presenting as sudden chest pain or pressure that may radiate to the neck, jaw, shoulder, or arm, accompanied by shortness of breath, diaphoresis, or nausea; MIs are classified as ST-elevation MI (STEMI) or non-ST-elevation MI (NSTEMI), with a heightened risk of arrhythmias such as ventricular fibrillation. Cerebral arterial thrombosis causes ischemic stroke in approximately 85% of all stroke cases, manifesting as sudden hemiparesis (weakness on one side of the body), aphasia (impaired speech), sensory deficits, or altered consciousness; transient ischemic attack (TIA) represents a temporary form with symptoms resolving within 24 hours. Peripheral arterial thrombosis may present as intermittent claudication (cramping pain in the legs during exertion, relieved by rest) in chronic cases or acute limb ischemia with the 6 P's in sudden occlusions.
Other sites include the mesenteric and retinal arteries. Mesenteric arterial thrombosis typically causes sudden, severe abdominal pain out of proportion to physical findings, often accompanied by nausea (44%), vomiting (35%), diarrhea (35%), or rectal bleeding (16%). Retinal arterial thrombosis leads to abrupt, painless monocular vision loss or blurring, potentially progressing to complete blindness if the central retinal artery is involved.
Unlike venous thrombosis, arterial thrombosis has a rapid onset driven by high shear stress in the vessel, frequently triggered by rupture or erosion of an atherosclerotic plaque, leading to platelet-rich thrombus formation on injured endothelium.

Venous Thrombosis

Venous thrombosis typically manifests through symptoms related to local stasis and impaired venous return, often presenting with swelling, warmth, redness, and pain in the affected area. In the lower extremities, deep vein thrombosis (DVT) commonly causes unilateral leg swelling, calf tenderness, and a sensation of heaviness or cramping that worsens with standing or walking. These signs arise from obstruction of venous flow, leading to fluid accumulation and inflammation in the surrounding tissues.
Upper extremity venous thrombosis, particularly in the axillary-subclavian veins, is less common but often effort-induced, as seen in Paget-Schroetter syndrome among young, active individuals. Symptoms include sudden-onset arm pain, swelling, a sensation of heaviness, and sometimes cyanosis or prominent superficial veins over the chest and shoulder (Urschel sign). In the head and neck, cerebral venous thrombosis frequently presents with severe headache, seizures, and signs of increased intracranial pressure such as papilledema or diplopia. Splanchnic venous thrombosis affects abdominal veins; portal vein involvement leads to abdominal pain and ascites due to portal hypertension, while hepatic vein occlusion in Budd-Chiari syndrome causes hepatomegaly, right upper quadrant pain, and ascites. Renal vein thrombosis typically produces flank pain, gross hematuria, and decreased urine output, reflecting acute renal congestion. Jugular vein thrombosis may result in neck swelling, tenderness, and a palpable cord-like structure, whereas cavernous sinus thrombosis is characterized by orbital edema, proptosis, ocular pain, and headache.
Unlike arterial thrombosis, which causes rapid ischemic symptoms from high-flow occlusion, venous thrombosis has a slower onset due to the lower-pressure venous system, emphasizing stasis-related congestion over acute tissue necrosis. It carries a higher risk of embolization, where fragments dislodge and travel to distant sites, potentially causing sudden respiratory distress like dyspnea or hemoptysis in cases originating from lower extremity DVT. Long-term, venous thrombosis predisposes to post-thrombotic syndrome, a chronic condition involving persistent limb swelling, pain, skin changes, and venous ulcers due to valvular damage and recurrent inflammation. This syndrome affects up to 50% of patients within two years of DVT, highlighting the importance of early intervention to mitigate ongoing venous insufficiency.

Diagnosis

Laboratory Investigations

Laboratory investigations play a crucial role in the diagnosis of thrombosis by assessing coagulation status, identifying predisposing factors such as thrombophilia, and evaluating associated inflammatory responses. These tests primarily involve blood-based assays to detect biomarkers of clot formation, degradation, or hypercoagulability, often used in conjunction with clinical assessment to rule out or confirm venous thromboembolism (VTE).
Coagulation tests are fundamental for evaluating thrombotic risk and activity. The D-dimer test measures fibrin degradation products released during clot breakdown, with levels above 500 ng/mL (or 0.5 mg/L fibrinogen equivalent units) indicating possible thrombosis; a negative result below this threshold has a sensitivity of approximately 95% for excluding VTE in low- to moderate-risk patients. Prothrombin time (PT) and activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT) assess the extrinsic and intrinsic coagulation pathways, respectively, and may be normal or prolonged in thrombotic conditions, helping to identify deficiencies or inhibitors.
Thrombophilia screening targets inherited or acquired defects promoting clot formation. Assays for antithrombin levels, protein C activity, and protein S activity detect deficiencies that impair natural anticoagulation, with antithrombin deficiency conferring the highest thrombosis risk among these. Genetic testing, such as polymerase chain reaction (PCR) for Factor V Leiden mutation, identifies the most common inherited thrombophilia, present in about 5% of Caucasians and associated with a 3- to 8-fold increased VTE risk.
Platelet function evaluation is relevant in arterial thrombosis or conditions like heparin-induced thrombocytopenia. A complete blood count (CBC) screens for thrombocytopenia, where platelet counts below 150,000/L may signal consumption in disseminated intravascular coagulation or immune-mediated destruction linked to thrombosis. Specific platelet function assays, such as light transmission aggregometry, assess resistance to antiplatelet agents like clopidogrel, which can contribute to recurrent thrombotic events in high-risk patients.
Inflammatory markers provide context for thrombosis as an inflammatory process. C-reactive protein (CRP) and fibrinogen levels rise as acute-phase reactants during acute thrombosis, with elevated CRP (>3 mg/L) correlating with increased VTE risk and fibrinogen (>400 mg/dL) reflecting ongoing coagulation activation.
Specialized tests address secondary causes, such as lupus anticoagulant detection for antiphospholipid syndrome (APS), a condition doubling the thrombosis risk. Lupus anticoagulant is identified through phospholipid-dependent coagulation assays like dilute Russell viper venom time, confirming APS when persistent and associated with clinical events.
Despite their utility, these tests have limitations; for instance, D-dimer is non-specific and often elevated in infections, malignancy, or surgery, reducing its positive predictive value to below 50% in high-prevalence settings. Interpretation requires integration with pretest probability to avoid overdiagnosis.

Imaging Modalities

Ultrasound is the first-line imaging modality for evaluating suspected deep vein thrombosis (DVT) in the extremities, particularly the lower limbs, where symptoms such as unilateral leg swelling may prompt its use. Compression ultrasonography assesses vein compressibility, with non-compressible veins indicating thrombus presence, while Doppler ultrasound evaluates blood flow to detect occlusions. For proximal DVT, this combined approach achieves a sensitivity of approximately 95% and high specificity, making it noninvasive and cost-effective for initial diagnosis. In superficial thrombophlebitis, compression alone suffices to identify localized clots without flow abnormalities.
Computed tomography (CT) and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) provide detailed vascular visualization for thrombosis in central locations. CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA) is the standard for diagnosing pulmonary embolism (PE), revealing intraluminal filling defects in pulmonary arteries that may appear central or eccentric, forming acute angles with vessel walls; it offers rapid turnaround and high accuracy as the preferred initial test for suspected PE. For cerebral venous thrombosis (CVT), magnetic resonance venography (MRV), often combined with conventional MRI, detects absent flow signals or abnormal thrombus signals in venous sinuses, with excellent diagnostic performance exceeding 90% sensitivity and specificity. These modalities excel in assessing thrombus extent and associated parenchymal changes without the invasiveness of traditional angiography.
Conventional angiography serves as the gold standard for arterial thrombosis, particularly in coronary vessels, where it directly visualizes filling defects, reduced contrast density, and staining indicative of thrombus during acute events like myocardial infarction. In complex venous cases, contrast venography outlines non-filling defects in deep veins, though it is reserved for situations where ultrasound is inconclusive due to its invasive nature and use of iodinated contrast. These catheter-based techniques provide real-time dynamic imaging but carry risks of vascular injury.
Echocardiography, especially transesophageal echocardiography (TEE), is essential for detecting cardiac thrombi, such as in the left atrial appendage (LAA) during atrial fibrillation, where it identifies echodense masses with near-100% sensitivity and specificity. Transthoracic echocardiography may suffice for larger ventricular thrombi post-myocardial infarction, but TEE offers superior resolution for the LAA, guiding anticoagulation decisions.
As of 2025, emerging technologies enhance thrombosis imaging efficiency. AI-enhanced CT algorithms improve PE detection on routine scans by automating filling defect identification, reducing diagnostic time and increasing sensitivity for incidental findings in up to 90% of cases. Functional MRI, including perfusion-weighted sequences, assesses tissue viability and venous perfusion deficits in CVT or PE, aiding prognosis by quantifying hypoperfused areas without contrast agents.
The choice of imaging modality depends on thrombosis site and clinical context: ultrasound is first-line for limb DVT due to its accessibility, while CT is preferred for chest and abdominal involvement like PE or mesenteric thrombosis, and MRI/MRV for intracranial veins. This site-specific approach balances diagnostic yield with patient safety and resource availability.

Prevention

Primary Prevention

Primary prevention of thrombosis focuses on strategies to avert the initial formation of thrombi in individuals at elevated risk, without a history of prior thrombotic events. These approaches target modifiable risk factors and high-risk scenarios, drawing from established guidelines and clinical evidence to reduce both arterial and venous thrombosis incidence. Key interventions encompass lifestyle modifications, pharmacological agents, mechanical methods, and public health measures tailored to vulnerable populations such as the elderly, pregnant individuals, and postoperative patients.
Lifestyle interventions play a foundational role in mitigating thrombosis risk by addressing factors like stasis, endothelial injury, and hypercoagulability. Regular physical activity, such as moderate aerobic exercise, promotes venous return and reduces blood stasis, thereby lowering the incidence of venous thromboembolism (VTE) by approximately 13% according to a meta-analysis of prospective cohort studies. Weight management is equally critical; maintaining a normal body mass index (BMI) through diet and exercise can decrease VTE risk by 40-60% in genetically susceptible individuals, as healthier lifestyles counteract prothrombotic states associated with obesity. Smoking cessation is particularly impactful for arterial thrombosis prevention; quitting reduces the risk of major cardiovascular events, including myocardial infarction and stroke due to atherothrombosis, by approximately 30-40% within 5-10 years compared to continued smoking, primarily by improving endothelial function and reducing platelet aggregation.
Pharmacological prophylaxis is recommended for specific high-risk groups to inhibit clot formation proactively. Low-dose aspirin (75-100 mg daily) is used for primary prevention of arterial thrombosis in select individuals at elevated cardiovascular risk, such as those with multiple risk factors but no prior events, reducing cardiovascular events by about 12-14% while balancing bleeding risks. In hospitalized medical patients at risk for VTE, low-molecular-weight heparin (e.g., enoxaparin 40 mg subcutaneously daily) or unfractionated heparin provides effective prophylaxis, decreasing VTE incidence by 50-60% compared to no intervention. These agents are particularly beneficial in acutely ill elderly patients or those with immobility.
Mechanical methods offer non-pharmacological options, especially when bleeding risks preclude drug therapy. Graduated compression stockings (15-30 mmHg pressure) are advised for long-haul travelers to prevent deep vein thrombosis by enhancing venous flow; randomized trials show they reduce asymptomatic DVT by 50-90% during flights exceeding 4 hours. Inferior vena cava (IVC) filters are reserved for highly select high-risk cases, such as trauma patients with contraindications to anticoagulation, to mechanically trap potential emboli, though routine primary use is not recommended due to increased DVT risk post-insertion.
Public health initiatives emphasize guideline-driven prevention, particularly in surgical and at-risk settings. The American College of Chest Physicians (ACCP) guidelines advocate risk-stratified VTE prophylaxis for nonorthopedic surgical patients, recommending low-molecular-weight heparin or fondaparinux for those with moderate-to-high risk (e.g., Caprini score 3), which can halve postoperative VTE rates (as per 2012 guidelines; see CHEST 2021 for updates). Vaccination against prothrombotic infections, such as COVID-19, indirectly prevents thrombosis by averting severe illness associated with high VTE risk due to inflammatory hypercoagulability, particularly in older adults and pregnant individuals. Targeted efforts for elderly, pregnant, and postoperative groups include early ambulation post-surgery and routine screening for risk factors like immobility.

Secondary Prevention

Secondary prevention of thrombosis aims to reduce the risk of recurrent events in patients who have experienced venous thromboembolism (VTE) or arterial thrombosis. For patients with VTE, such as deep vein thrombosis (DVT) or pulmonary embolism (PE), extended anticoagulation is recommended following initial treatment. In cases of provoked VTE, where a transient risk factor like surgery or hormone therapy is identified, anticoagulation with direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) such as rivaroxaban or warfarin is typically continued for 3 to 6 months to allow resolution of the acute event and mitigate short-term recurrence risk. For unprovoked VTE, where no clear provoking factor exists, indefinite anticoagulation is advised due to the higher risk of recurrence, estimated at 20-30% over 5 years without ongoing therapy.
In patients with cancer-associated VTE, guidelines recommend indefinite anticoagulation, preferably with low-molecular-weight heparin (LMWH) or a DOAC, as the ongoing malignancy sustains a high thrombotic risk, with recurrence rates exceeding 20% annually if therapy is discontinued prematurely. For extended therapy beyond the initial phase, reduced-dose DOACs (e.g., rivaroxaban 10 mg daily or apixaban 2.5 mg twice daily) may be considered to balance efficacy against bleeding risk, supported by trials showing approximately 80% reduction in recurrent VTE compared to placebo.
For arterial thrombosis, such as that occurring after coronary stent placement, dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) with aspirin and a P2Y12 inhibitor like clopidogrel is standard to prevent stent thrombosis and recurrent ischemic events. ESC guidelines recommend DAPT for at least 6 to 12 months post-percutaneous coronary intervention, depending on bleeding risk (as per 2023 ESC guidelines for ACS), with evidence from trials demonstrating a 20-30% relative reduction in major adverse cardiovascular events. In patients with a history of ischemic stroke due to arterial thrombosis, long-term single antiplatelet therapy (e.g., aspirin or clopidogrel) is preferred for secondary prevention, as DAPT is typically limited to the acute phase.
Lifestyle modifications play a supportive role in secondary prevention by addressing modifiable risk factors. Patients are advised to cease hormone therapy, such as oral contraceptives or hormone replacement therapy, if it contributed to the initial event, as continuation increases recurrence risk by 2- to 4-fold; transdermal alternatives may be considered in select cases under specialist guidance. Ongoing monitoring includes weight management, smoking cessation, and regular physical activity to reduce overall thrombotic burden; adherence to these measures can lower recurrence risk in at-risk populations.
Major guidelines from the European Society of Cardiology (ESC) and American Society of Hematology (ASH) emphasize individualized duration based on provoked versus unprovoked etiology and patient-specific factors like bleeding risk. For provoked events with major transient factors, 3 months of therapy is sufficient per ESC recommendations, while ASH suggests 3-6 months; for unprovoked or persistent risks (e.g., cancer), both endorse indefinite treatment (as per latest updates as of 2020).
To guide decisions on discontinuing anticoagulation in low-risk patients after initial therapy, periodic D-dimer testing is recommended, with a negative result (adjusted for age) supporting cessation and reducing unnecessary prolonged exposure; this approach, per ASH guidelines, has low certainty evidence but aids in stratifying recurrence risk to below 5% annually.

Management

Anticoagulation

Anticoagulation therapy is a cornerstone in the management of active thrombosis, aimed at preventing clot propagation and embolization by inhibiting key components of the coagulation cascade. Parenteral anticoagulants, such as unfractionated heparin (UFH) and low-molecular-weight heparin (LMWH), are typically used for initial treatment in acute settings due to their rapid onset of action. UFH is administered intravenously for immediate effect in hospitalized patients with acute venous thromboembolism (VTE) or acute coronary syndrome (ACS), with dosing initiated as a bolus of 80 units/kg followed by an infusion of 18 units/kg per hour, adjusted to maintain an activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT) 1.5 to 2.5 times control. LMWH, exemplified by enoxaparin, is preferred for subcutaneous administration in most non-critically ill patients with acute VTE, dosed at 1 mg/kg twice daily, offering predictable pharmacokinetics without routine monitoring.
Oral anticoagulants include vitamin K antagonists (VKAs) like warfarin and direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs). Warfarin, a VKA, is dosed to achieve an international normalized ratio (INR) target of 2.0 to 3.0 for VTE treatment, often requiring bridging with parenteral agents during initiation due to its delayed onset. DOACs, such as the direct thrombin inhibitor dabigatran (150 mg twice daily after initial parenteral therapy) and factor Xa inhibitors like apixaban (10 mg twice daily for 7 days, then 5 mg twice daily) or rivaroxaban (15 mg twice daily for 21 days, then 20 mg daily), provide fixed dosing with half-lives around 12 hours for apixaban, enabling once- or twice-daily regimens without routine coagulation monitoring. These agents are first-line for long-term VTE management in eligible patients and are also indicated in ACS to reduce recurrent ischemic events, particularly when combined with antiplatelet therapy. Bridging with short-acting anticoagulants like LMWH is recommended during interruptions for procedures to maintain therapeutic levels.60127-5/pdf)
Anticoagulants exert their effects by targeting the coagulation cascade: UFH and LMWH enhance antithrombin-mediated inhibition of thrombin (factor IIa) and factor Xa, while DOACs directly inhibit these enzymesdabigatran binds thrombin's active site, and apixaban or rivaroxaban block factor Xa. VKAs like warfarin indirectly inhibit factors II, VII, IX, and X by depleting vitamin K-dependent gamma-carboxylation. Importantly, these agents do not affect platelet function or aggregation, distinguishing them from antiplatelet therapies.
Emerging investigational agents, such as factor XI inhibitors (e.g., asundexian), have shown promise in phase 2 trials for preventing thrombosis with potentially reduced bleeding risk compared to current anticoagulants, though they are not yet approved for clinical use as of November 2025.
The primary side effect of anticoagulation is bleeding, with major hemorrhage rates varying by agent but generally lower for DOACs compared to VKAs in VTE trials. Heparin use carries a risk of heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT) in 1-5% of patients receiving UFH, necessitating platelet monitoring, though LMWH has a lower incidence. Reversal strategies are critical for bleeding management: protamine sulfate fully neutralizes UFH and partially reverses LMWH; idarucizumab rapidly reverses dabigatran; and andexanet alfa inactivates factor Xa inhibitors like apixaban and rivaroxaban. As of 2025, these specific reversal agents for DOACs are widely available in clinical practice, improving safety in emergency settings.

Thrombolysis

Thrombolytic therapy, or fibrinolysis, employs pharmacological agents to actively dissolve thrombi by enhancing the body's natural fibrinolytic process, which converts plasminogen to plasmin for rapid clot degradation. This approach is distinct from anticoagulation, as it targets existing clots for immediate reperfusion rather than merely preventing new thrombus formation. Key agents include recombinant tissue plasminogen activator (tPA, such as alteplase), streptokinase, and urokinase, all of which bind to fibrin in the thrombus and catalyze the conversion of plasminogen to plasmin, the enzyme that hydrolyzes fibrin cross-links to lyse the clot.
These agents can be delivered systemically through intravenous infusion to achieve widespread thrombolysis or via catheter-directed administration for localized delivery directly to the thrombus site, minimizing systemic exposure and potentially reducing bleeding risks. Systemic thrombolysis is preferred for rapid intervention in life-threatening scenarios, while catheter-directed methods are used in select cases for peripheral or pulmonary thrombi to enhance efficacy at lower doses. The choice depends on the thrombotic location, patient stability, and contraindication profile.
Indications for thrombolysis are limited to conditions where rapid reperfusion is critical to prevent irreversible organ damage or death, such as massive pulmonary embolism (PE) with sustained hypotension (systolic blood pressure <90 mmHg), acute ischemic stroke within 4.5 hours of symptom onset, and ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI) when primary percutaneous coronary intervention is not immediately available. In massive PE, thrombolysis is recommended for hemodynamically unstable patients to restore right ventricular function and improve hemodynamics. For acute ischemic stroke, intravenous alteplase is indicated for eligible patients to salvage brain tissue. In STEMI, it serves as an alternative to reperfusion therapy when delays exceed 120 minutes.
Dosing regimens vary by agent and indication; for instance, alteplase for STEMI is typically administered as 100 mg intravenously over 2 hours, with 15 mg as a bolus followed by 50 mg over 30 minutes and 35 mg over 60 minutes, adjusted for body weight under 67 kg. Streptokinase for PE is given as 250,000 IU over 30 minutes followed by 100,000 IU/hour for up to 24 hours, while urokinase involves an initial 4,400 IU/kg bolus over 10 minutes then 4,400 IU/kg/hour infusion. Contraindications are absolute or relative and include active internal bleeding, recent (within 3 months) intracranial or intraspinal hemorrhage or surgery, recent major trauma or surgery (within 2-4 weeks), uncontrolled hypertension (>180/110 mmHg), and known bleeding diathesis.
The primary risks of thrombolysis stem from its fibrinolytic action, which can cause systemic hypofibrinolysis leading to major bleeding in 6-10% of patients and intracranial hemorrhage in approximately 1%, with higher rates (up to 2%) in PE and varying by agentstreptokinase carries greater immunogenicity and bleeding risk than alteplase. Additional complications include reperfusion injury, manifesting as arrhythmias or hypotension upon clot dissolution, and allergic reactions with streptokinase due to its bacterial origin. Patient selection is crucial to balance benefits against these hemorrhagic risks.
Clinical evidence supports thrombolysis in select high-risk cases; meta-analyses of randomized trials in PE demonstrate a significant mortality reduction (odds ratio 0.57, approximately 43% relative risk reduction) compared to heparin alone, from 3.9% to 2.3%, though with a threefold increase in major extracranial bleeding and ninefold in intracranial hemorrhage. Similar benefits are seen in STEMI, where early thrombolysis reduces 30-day mortality by 25-30% in the GUSTO trial with alteplase versus streptokinase. In acute stroke, the NINDS trial showed improved outcomes with alteplase, establishing its role despite a 6.4% symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage rate. These findings underscore thrombolysis as a high-impact intervention when guidelines are followed.

Surgical and Endovascular Interventions

Surgical thrombectomy is an open procedure involving direct removal of thrombi from blood vessels, often indicated for acute limb ischemia when medical therapies fail or are contraindicated, such as in cases of recent onset severe deep vein thrombosis (DVT) symptoms lasting fewer than 7 days or embolic occlusions threatening limb viability. For instance, surgical embolectomy is recommended as primary treatment for acute arterial embolism in lower limbs to restore perfusion rapidly, particularly in patients with contraindications to thrombolysis like recent surgery or bleeding risks. Risks associated with this approach include excessive bleeding potentially leading to death, infection, and damage to the blood vessel at the incision site, with overall morbidity influenced by patient comorbidities. In chronic arterial thrombosis, bypass grafting serves as a reconstructive option, using autologous veins or synthetic grafts to reroute blood flow around occluded segments, thereby improving long-term patency in peripheral arterial disease.
Endovascular interventions offer minimally invasive alternatives, including catheter-directed thrombolysis (CDT), which delivers low-dose thrombolytics like tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) directly to the thrombus via a catheter to enhance dissolution while minimizing systemic effects, in line with the 2025 European Society of Vascular Medicine (ESVM) guidelines recommending involvement of vascular experts for catheter-based therapies in acute VTE. This is particularly suited for massive iliofemoral DVT unresponsive to anticoagulation, achieving technical success rates of approximately 90% in restoring venous patency. Mechanical thrombectomy complements or replaces pure pharmacological approaches, employing devices such as aspiration catheters (e.g., Penumbra system) for direct clot suction in DVT or acute aortic occlusion, and stent retrievers (e.g., Solitaire or Trevo) for large-vessel occlusions in ischemic stroke to achieve rapid recanalization. Specific techniques include AngioJet rheolytic thrombectomy, which uses high-velocity saline jets for maceration and aspiration of thrombi in peripheral vessels, demonstrating procedural success but carrying risks of hemolysis and acute kidney injury due to red blood cell destruction. Another advancement is the EKOS system, an ultrasound-enhanced CDT method that employs acoustic pulses to disrupt fibrin strands and improve thrombolytic penetration, yielding safe and effective outcomes in acute limb ischemia with reduced infusion times.
Common risks across endovascular procedures encompass vessel perforation (reported in up to 2% of cases), distal embolization of clot fragments, and access-site complications, though major bleeding rates remain lower than with systemic therapies. Outcomes vary by thrombus age and location, with CDT and mechanical methods achieving 88-97% primary patency at 24 months in iliofemoral DVT cohorts.30312-9/fulltext) As of 2025, robotic-assisted platforms are emerging for enhanced precision in endovascular thrombectomy, with first-in-human trials demonstrating feasibility in stroke cases by enabling remote catheter navigation and reducing operator radiation exposure. These interventions are typically reserved for patients with high-risk features, such as phlegmasia cerulea dolens or acute occlusions not resolving with initial anticoagulation.

Complications and Prognosis

Major Complications

Thrombosis can lead to severe downstream effects through embolization, where thrombi dislodge and travel to distant sites, causing acute life-threatening events or chronic impairments. In venous thrombosis, such as deep vein thrombosis (DVT) in the lower extremities, emboli often migrate to the pulmonary arteries, resulting in pulmonary embolism (PE). PE can impose significant strain on the right ventricle, leading to right heart strain and, in severe cases, cor pulmonalea form of right-sided heart failure characterized by pulmonary hypertension and ventricular dilation. Arterial thrombosis, particularly from cardiac sources like mural thrombi in the left atrium or ventricle, enables systemic embolization, with thrombi lodging in cerebral, mesenteric, or peripheral arteries. A common manifestation is ischemic stroke, where cerebral infarction occurs due to occluded vessels, potentially causing permanent neurological deficits.
Ischemic damage from thrombosis arises when blood flow is obstructed, depriving tissues of oxygen and nutrients, which culminates in infarction or necrosis. In the coronary arteries, thrombosis overlying atherosclerotic plaques triggers myocardial infarction (MI), where myocardial tissue undergoes necrosis, impairing cardiac contractility and risking arrhythmias or heart failure. Similarly, cerebral arterial thrombosis can produce ischemic stroke through infarction of brain parenchyma, leading to motor, sensory, or cognitive impairments depending on the affected region. In peripheral arteries, such as those in the limbs, prolonged ischemia may progress to gangrene, a form of tissue death that often necessitates amputation if revascularization is not promptly achieved.
Post-thrombotic syndrome (PTS) represents a chronic sequela primarily following proximal DVT, affecting 20-50% of patients and manifesting as chronic venous insufficiency. This syndrome involves valvular incompetence and venous obstruction, leading to persistent leg pain, swelling, hyperpigmentation, and in severe cases, venous leg ulcers that impair quality of life and mobility. Without appropriate prophylaxis, recurrence of venous thrombosis occurs in 20-30% of cases within 5 years, often due to unresolved hypercoagulability or inadequate anticoagulation, perpetuating a cycle of embolization and tissue damage.
Rare complications include paradoxical embolism, where a venous thrombus traverses a patent foramen ovale (PFO) or other right-to-left shunt, entering the arterial circulation and causing systemic events like stroke despite originating from a venous source. In disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) associated with severe thrombosis, initial widespread microvascular clotting depletes coagulation factors and progresses to hemorrhagic diathesis, resulting in uncontrolled bleeding from multiple sites. Long-term, cardiac thrombiparticularly those involving valvular structurescan cause endothelial damage and fibrosis, leading to valvular dysfunction such as stenosis or regurgitation, which compromises cardiac output over time.

Prognostic Factors

Prognostic factors in thrombosis significantly influence patient outcomes, with mortality rates varying by type and underlying conditions. In venous thromboembolism (VTE), the first-year all-cause mortality is approximately 15-20%, often driven by the underlying disease or recurrent events. For arterial thrombosis, such as myocardial infarction (MI), the 30-day mortality ranges from 5-10%, reflecting advances in reperfusion therapies but persistent risks from comorbidities. Ischemic stroke associated with arterial thrombosis carries a 1-year mortality of 10-20%, with higher rates linked to initial severity and age.
Favorable prognostic indicators include early intervention, which improves survival by mitigating clot propagation and complications, and provoked VTE events, where identifiable triggers like surgery lead to lower recurrence risks compared to unprovoked cases. Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) further enhance prognosis by reducing VTE recurrence by approximately 80% relative to no anticoagulation, offering a safer profile than traditional vitamin K antagonists for extended therapy.
Adverse factors worsen outcomes, particularly unprovoked VTE, which has a 30% recurrence rate over 5-10 years after anticoagulation cessation, elevating long-term morbidity. Cancer-associated thrombosis is associated with a 12-month mortality of about 40%, largely due to the interplay of malignancy and thrombotic burden.00158-8/fulltext) Advanced age over 75 years independently heightens mortality risk, with odds ratios up to 18-fold higher in elderly cohorts due to frailty and comorbidities.
Clinical scoring systems aid in stratifying prognosis. The Pulmonary Embolism Severity Index (PESI) predicts 30-day mortality in pulmonary embolism (PE), classifying patients into low- (0-9% risk) to high-risk (25-65% risk) categories based on age, comorbidities, and vital signs. For atrial fibrillation-related thrombosis, the CHADS2 score assesses stroke risk and guides anticoagulation, with scores 2 indicating annual risks of 4-18% without therapy.
Long-term prognosis involves quality-of-life impacts, such as post-DVT chronic pain affecting about 25% of patients through post-thrombotic syndrome (PTS), leading to persistent leg discomfort and reduced mobility.13322-2/fulltext)
As of 2025, genetic profiling emerges as a promising tool for personalized prognosis, with whole-genome sequencing identifying variants in genes like ABO and F8 that predict VTE recurrence risk up to 180% higher, enabling tailored anticoagulation strategies.
