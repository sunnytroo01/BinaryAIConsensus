Tee

 A tee is a stand or device used in various sports to support and elevate a stationary ball prior to being struck with a foot, club, or bat. It is most commonly associated with golf, where it is a small peg, typically made of wood or plastic, used to elevate the ball slightly above the ground at the beginning of each hole, facilitating a more effective initial drive with the club. Alternatively, in golf it can refer to the teeing ground itself, a designated rectangular area from which play on a hole commences, often marked by markers or boundaries. Tees are also used in tee-ball (a form of baseball and softball for young players), as well as kicking tees in American football, rugby, and Gaelic football. This device is essential in these sports, enabling players to start shots or kicks without the ball resting directly on the turf or ground, which would otherwise hinder contact.
The origins of the tee trace back to 18th-century Scotland, where early golfers formed natural mounds from wet sand or earth to prop up the ball, a practice documented in the first codified rules of golf established by the Company of Gentlemen Golfers at Leith in 1744, which specified that "Your Tee must be upon the Ground." The term "tee" likely derives from the Scottish Gaelic word "tigh," meaning a small house or heap (linked to curling terminology), or possibly the Dutch "tuitje," referring to a small conical pile, and by the 19th century, it had become standardized terminology for both the starting position and the supporting structure. Mechanical innovations emerged in the late 1800s, with the first patented tees appearing in 1889 by Scottish inventors William Bloxsom and Arthur Douglas, who designed a rubber slab with prongs that lay flat on the ground to hold the ball.
The modern wooden golf tee was invented in 1899 by Boston dentist Dr. George F. Grant, who patented a shank of wood topped with gutta-percha to replace the cumbersome sand method, though it gained limited adoption until the early (/page/20th century). By the 1920s, mass-produced wooden tees became commonplace, evolving into the inexpensive, disposable items familiar today, often 2 to 3 inches (5 to 7.6 cm) in length with a concave top to cradle the ball. Contemporary variations include biodegradable options made from materials like bamboo or compressed pegs, as well as longer tees for drivers, reflecting ongoing adaptations to player preferences and environmental concerns. The tee's role extends beyond utility in its respective sports, influencing strategic decisions such as positioning within the starting area.

Etymology and Overview

Etymology

The term "tee" in the context of sports equipment originates from the Old Scots word "teaz," first attested in 1673 as a reference to a small heap of sand or earth from which the golf ball was struck. This Scottish term, of uncertain etymology, was likely a substantive and verb form describing the act of preparing such a mound, possibly influenced by earlier linguistic elements though direct Scandinavian roots, such as Old Norse "te" for a small piece of wood, remain unconfirmed and debated among linguists. The etymology is further complicated by alternative theories, including derivation from Scottish Gaelic "tigh" meaning "house" (analogous to the target area in curling) or Dutch "tuitje" referring to a small conical shape. By the early 18th century, "teaz" was reanalyzed as a plural, leading to the back-formation of the singular "tee," which entered golf usage around 1721.
The word's evolution reflects the transition from natural improvised supports to more defined starting points in play. In the earliest codified rules of golf, the 1744 "Articles and Laws in Playing at Golf" issued by the Gentlemen Golfers of Leith, "tee" appears explicitly: "You must Tee your Ball within a Club's length of the Hole" and "Your Tee must be upon the Ground," standardizing it as the designated area or mound ahead of the previous hole. Literary references soon followed, with Thomas Mathison's 1743 poem "The Goff" employing the term in lines such as "'Twill set you on the 'Tee,' boys," marking one of the first printed uses in golf-themed literature.
Over time, as golf equipment advanced from sand heaps to manufactured pegs in the late 19th century, "tee" retained its association with the starting support, rooted in the original mound's terminology. This linguistic persistence underscores the term's specialization within sports, distinct from broader applications of "T"-shaped objects.

Definition and General Use

A tee is a low stand or peg, typically T-shaped, used to elevate and support a stationary ball for striking with a foot, club, or bat in various sports. The term derives from the Old Scots word teaz, possibly of Scandinavian origin. This design allows the ball to remain stationary at a consistent height above the ground, facilitating cleaner contact during the initial strike and reducing interference from the playing surface. The T-shape provides inherent stability, with the crossbar serving as a cradle for the ball while the vertical stem anchors the device securely.
Tees are primarily employed to support balls during starting shots, such as tee shots, or in training drills to promote repetitive practice with uniform ball positioning. They are constructed from durable materials like wood, plastic, or rubber to withstand impacts while ensuring reliable ball contact without excessive friction or breakage. Wooden variants, often made from cedar or bamboo, offer a natural grip, while plastic and rubber provide enhanced longevity for repeated use.
In response to environmental concerns in outdoor sports, there has been a notable shift toward biodegradable and eco-friendly tees, such as those made from bamboo or compostable composites, to minimize litter and long-term waste accumulation on courses and fields. Plastic tees, which can persist for centuries in the environment, have prompted this transition to sustainable alternatives that decompose naturally without harming wildlife or soil.

Golf Tee

History

The origins of the golf tee trace back to 15th-century Scotland, where the game emerged on the eastern coastal links, and players elevated their ballsinitially pebbles or early featherie golf ballsusing small mounds formed from wet sand or earth to facilitate cleaner strikes off the uneven terrain. This practice, known as forming a "tee" from natural materials, was essential in the rudimentary conditions of early golf courses, where the ball was placed on these improvised elevations before striking it toward distant holes marked by flags.
A pivotal shift occurred in 1744 with the first codified rules of golf, established by the Gentlemen Golfers of Leith near Edinburgh, which stipulated that "Your Tee must be upon the Ground," requiring that the sand-based tee be constructed directly on the ground to allow a small mound for slight ball elevation while promoting fairness and consistency in starting positions. This rule, later echoed in the 1754 Articles and Laws in Playing at Golf from St. Andrews, compelled golfers to refine sand-based tees or seek artificial alternatives, setting the stage for innovation amid the sport's growing formalization. By the late 19th century, these constraints inspired the first patented designs, including the 1889 British patent by William Bloxsom and Arthur Douglas for a rubber platform tee that rested flat on the ground with prongs to hold the ball. This was followed in 1899 by U.S. dentist George F. Grant's wooden peg tee, featuring a concave rubber cup atop a tapered shank for secure ball placement, marking the first American patent for such a device.
The early 20th century saw mass production transform the tee into an accessible tool, beginning with dentist William Lowell's 1921 introduction of the "Reddy Tee," a wooden peg initially topped with a gutta-percha head for durability, which he began manufacturing and selling widely by 1922, painting the tops red for visibility in grass. Lowell's design, patented in 1925, quickly gained traction, with sales reaching $100,000 annually by the mid-1920s and becoming the standard for professional play, including its first prominent use in major tournaments like the U.S. Open shortly thereafter. By the 1920s, artificial tees supplanted sand mounds entirely in competitive golf, enhancing consistency and efficiency for players. Plastic variants, such as celluloid tees patented in 1924 and more durable iterations emerging in the mid-20th century, further evolved the design for longevity and reduced breakage.
In the late 20th century, governing bodies like the USGA and R&A formalized tee specifications within the Rules of Golf, emphasizing simplicity while allowing material flexibility to accommodate innovation. The 21st century brought sustainability-focused developments, including biodegradable bamboo tees, which became commercially available in the late 2000s and offer comparable strength to wood but decompose faster, aligning with environmental concerns on golf courses. These eco-friendly options, stronger than traditional wood and naturally sourced from rapidly renewable bamboo, represent the latest milestone in the tee's evolution from humble sand origins to a refined, planet-conscious accessory.

Design and Materials

Golf tees are characterized by a T-shaped design, featuring a vertical stem that is inserted into the ground and a horizontal head that supports the ball. The head typically incorporates a shallow cup or multiple prongs to cradle the golf ball securely while minimizing surface contact to reduce friction and spin during impact.
Tee lengths vary based on the club used, with shorter options around 1.5 inches suitable for irons and hybrids, and longer ones from 2.75 to 3.25 inches preferred for drivers to elevate the ball optimally. Prong-style heads, often with 3 to 5 prongs, provide less resistance than traditional cup designs by further limiting ball-tee interaction, promoting longer and more accurate drives.
Traditional golf tees are crafted from wood, commonly bamboo or maple, which offer natural flexibility and a classic feel that absorbs impact without splintering easily. Bamboo, in particular, provides superior strength compared to standard wood due to its dense fiber structure, while maple ensures durability in hardwood form. Plastic tees, on the other hand, are valued for their high durability and reusability, resisting breakage over multiple rounds and maintaining shape under repeated use.
Modern variants include performance tees with multi-prong configurations, such as those from Zero Friction, designed to achieve near-zero contact and friction for enhanced distance and control. These innovations build on traditional designs by incorporating offset or directional elements in some models to help reduce unwanted spin and promote straighter ball flight.
Since the 2010s, biodegradable tees made from corn-based plastics have emerged as an eco-friendly innovation, decomposing naturally without leaving persistent waste on courses. These plant-derived materials maintain the strength of conventional plastics while breaking down via microbial action over time. As of 2025, professional golfers predominantly favor 2.75- to 3.25-inch wooden tees for their balance of elevation and natural performance in competitive play.
Manufacturing processes differ by material: bamboo tees begin with selecting and pruning elongated rods, followed by trimming and shaping via lathe or CNC machinery to form the precise T-profile. Plastic tees are produced through injection molding, where molten polymer pellets are injected into custom molds to create uniform, high-volume batches with integrated prong or cup features.

Rules and Specifications

The Rules of Golf, jointly governed by the United States Golf Association (USGA) and The Royal and Ancient Golf Club of St. Andrews (R&A), specify that a golf tee must not exceed 4 inches (101.6 mm) in length, with no minimum length required. Tees are classified as foreign material but are permitted exclusively within the teeing area for starting a hole, where they may be used to elevate the ball off the ground.
To ensure fair play, tees must not be designed or manufactured to indicate the line of play, unduly influence the ball's movement, or assist in stroke-making, such as through springs, fans, brushes, or angled features that could alter ball flight. For instance, the 2019 rules modernization reinforced these prohibitions by clarifying that any device aiding ball launch beyond simple elevation violates conformity standards.
In usage, a player must make the stroke from within the teeing area, defined as a rectangle extending two club-lengths behind the forward points of the tee markers, using one conforming tee or the ground for each starting stroke. The tee may be placed anywhere in this area, but the ball must lie within its boundaries when addressed, and players may stand outside it to execute the stroke.
Non-conforming tees incur the general penalty: loss of hole in match play or two strokes in stroke play for the first breach, with the player required to correct by using a conforming tee; continued use or failure to correct results in disqualification. Official conformity testing by the USGA and R&A measures tee length precisely to enforce these standards, ensuring no undue advantage.

Training Tees in Baseball and Softball

Tee-Ball Tee

The tee-ball tee is a key piece of equipment in tee-ball, a simplified variant of baseball designed for children aged 4 to 7 to build foundational skills in a low-pressure setting. It features an adjustable-height rubber stand positioned directly on home plate, typically ranging from 24 to 36 inches in height to accommodate young players, with a flexible arm or cup that secures softballs or baseballs at approximately waist level for optimal striking. This design allows batters to focus on form without the complexity of live pitching, enhancing hand-eye coordination and swing confidence.
In tee-ball gameplay, the ball remains on the tee until struck by the batter, after which fielders retrieve it to advance runners around the bases in a non-competitive format that prioritizes participation over scoring. The sport originated in 1956 when Jerry Sacharski developed it in Albion, Michigan, as a safe introductory game to teach baseball basics like hitting, throwing, catching, and base running to young children, eliminating risks associated with pitched balls. Leagues such as Little League emphasize this approach, with no formal pitching or strict competitive rules to foster enjoyment and skill development.
Tee-ball tees are typically made from durable, heavy rubber for the base to provide stability on dirt or grass surfaces, preventing tipping during swings, while the upright stem is often steel or reinforced plastic for longevity. Portable, collapsible versions with soft, rounded edges support travel teams and underscore safety by reducing injury potential from impacts. These materials ensure the tee withstands repeated use in outdoor youth settings without compromising accessibility.
In the 2020s, modern adaptations have integrated technology into tee-ball training, such as electronic swing analyzers with attachable sensors that pair with tees to deliver real-time data on metrics like bat speed and swing path, aiding coaches in refining young players' techniques during practice sessions. Devices like the Blast Motion sensor, compatible with youth bats, provide app-based feedback to promote precise, data-informed improvements while maintaining the tee's core role in stationary hitting drills.

Batting Practice Tee

The batting practice tee is a portable training device used in baseball and softball to develop hitting mechanics through controlled, repetitive swings without the variability of live pitching. Unlike fixed tees for youth games, these are designed for versatility across skill levels, allowing players to focus on bat path, timing, and contact consistency in indoor cages or outdoor fields.
Traditional post-style batting tees feature a vertical pole, typically 24 to 42 inches adjustable in height to simulate various strike zones, topped with a rubber cup or flexible bristle holder to secure the ball securely while minimizing resistance during impact. Hybrid models incorporate an arm-extended arm from the base to the ball holder, mimicking a pitcher's throwing motion and encouraging proper swing plane alignment for strike-zone simulation. Most designs include portable weighted bases, often filled with sand or water, to provide stability on different surfaces without permanent installation.
These tees support repetitive swing drills targeting specific pitch locations, such as inside, middle, or outside, to build muscle memory and refine hand-eye coordination for players of all ages. Adjustable heights accommodate youth to professional levels, with common setups in MLB batting cages dating back to the 1970s for daily pre-game routines and skill maintenance. Professional players, including former MLB outfielder Shawn Green, have credited extended tee sessions with career-long improvements in power and consistency.
Materials emphasize durability for high-volume use, with poles constructed from aluminum or fiberglass for lightweight portability and resistance to bending under repeated hits, while bases often use high-density polyethylene or rubber for anti-tip stability. Variations include specialized inside-out tees, which position the ball holder offset toward the batter's inside to train contact points on inner-half pitches, promoting an inside-out swing path for better line-drive production. Some youth variants adapt these designs with shorter poles for tee-ball transitions, but adult models prioritize robustness for intense training.
In the 2020s, technological advancements have integrated smart features into batting tees, such as app-connected systems that use smartphone cameras or embedded sensors to track bat speed, launch angle, and exit velocity during drills. The Attack Tee app, for instance, provides real-time feedback and personalized hitting programs based on tee work data, enhancing practice efficiency for both amateur and elite athletes. Similarly, the Blast Vision app analyzes tee-stationary hits to measure swing metrics, allowing users to quantify progress without additional hardware.

Kicking Tees in Team Sports

American Football Kicking Tee

The American football kicking tee is a low-profile device designed to elevate the pointed end of the football slightly above the ground, facilitating stable placement for kickoffs. Unlike taller tees in other sports, it features a short peg or cradle measuring 1 inch in height to minimize interference with the kicker's approach while providing sufficient lift for the ball's prolate spheroid shape. This design ensures the ball remains balanced on its tip, allowing for consistent contact during the swing. For standard kickoffs, the tee is positioned at the kicking team's 35-yard line, as mandated by NFL rules, promoting fair starting field position for the receiving team. As of 2025, the NFL's dynamic kickoff rules maintain the 1-inch tee limit while altering player alignments for safety.
Specialized variants exist for onside kicks, where the tee incorporates angled supports or a flat platform to position the ball horizontally or at a low angle, enabling it to travel a shorter distance and stay in bounds for recovery attempts. These onside tees measure 1 inch in height with a textured surface for grip, and their compact footprinttypically 6 inches long by 6 inches wideallows quick setup during time-sensitive plays. The inline configuration orients the ball perpendicular to the goal line for straight-line kickoffs, while cross or angled setups adjust the ball's tilt to enhance directional accuracy, particularly in variable conditions. Such adaptations have become standard in professional play, with the Ground Zero 1-inch tee, featuring built-in onside capabilities, used in every NFL game since the 2000-01 season.
Kicking tees support kickers by holding the ball steady without a human holder, a practice permitted in the NFL since the reintroduction of artificial tees in 1948 after a post-World War I ban on elevated devices lasted approximately 30 years. Prior to this, teammates manually held the ball, but the shift to tees streamlined kickoffs and reduced variability. NFL regulations now limit tee height to 1 inch maximum for kickoffs, standardized in 1994 following the abolition of natural dirt mounds in 1983, ensuring uniformity across games. This equipment is essential for professional kickers, who rely on it for every opening kickoff, and it has been used in all Super Bowls since the 2000-2001 season via the innovative Ground Zero model.
Constructed primarily from durable rubber or reinforced plastic, kicking tees prioritize weather resistance to withstand rain, wind, and repeated impacts without degrading. The rubber composition, often black and heavy-duty, provides a non-slip textured top to secure the ball's leather surface, while integrated spikes or prongs anchor the base into natural grass or artificial turf for stability during high-velocity kicks. Portability is a key feature, with lightweight designs allowing easy transport and rapid field placement. Post-2000 innovations include adjustable-height models limited to 1 inch for NFL compliance, aiding kickers in adapting to field conditions like soft turf or gusty winds, though fixed 1-inch tees remain dominant in the NFL for their proven consistency.

Rugby and Gaelic Football Kicking Tee

In rugby union and Gaelic football, kicking tees are devices used to elevate and stabilize the ball for place kicks, facilitating more accurate and powerful strikes during specific game situations. In rugby union, they are primarily employed for conversion kicks following tries and penalty kicks, allowing the kicker to position the ball at an optimal angle without assistance from a teammate. In Gaelic football, kicking tees are mainly utilized by goalkeepers for kick-outsrestarts after scores or widesto protect the pitch surface and enhance kick distance and consistency. These tees have evolved from rudimentary supports to specialized tools, reflecting adaptations in gameplay and equipment regulations in both sports.
The history of kicking tees in rugby union traces back to informal methods before formal adoption. Prior to the mid-20th century, players relied on a teammate to hold the ball in place for kicks, with charging by opponents banned for penalty kicks in 1925 to allow unhindered placement. By 1958, rules permitted solo kicks without a holder for conversions, prompting the use of sand or mud mounds in regions like South Africa during the 1970s for elevation. The first manufactured kicking tee in rugby emerged in 1988, invented by Canadian rugby enthusiast Don Burgess, who adapted designs from other sports. World Rugby officially permitted "sand, sawdust, or approved kicking tees" in 1992, marking widespread acceptance; this followed trials at the 1987 Rugby World Cup where sand was allowed. A notable innovation was the Supertee, developed in 1993 by New Zealand's Peter Williams and rugby player Daryl Halligan to meet a mandate for tees in conversions; after prototypes using fabric pouches and malleable plastics, the final hard plastic version launched in 1996, offering adjustable height and stability.
In Gaelic football, kicking tees were introduced later to address pitch wear and goalkeeper technique. The Gaelic Athletic Association (GAA) trialed tees around 2005 as part of rule changes aimed at reducing physical strain on kickers and preserving grass surfaces during restarts. Initially compulsory in some competitions, they became optional by the early 2010s, with formal approval for "an artificial tee of a standard approved by the Central Council" codified in the GAA Official Guide. This adaptation aligned with evolving goalkeeper roles, where long, precise kick-outs became central to strategy, influenced by players like Monaghan's Rory Beggan who popularized powerful, tee-assisted deliveries. Unlike rugby's focus on angled place kicks, Gaelic tees emphasize straight, high-distance restarts from the goal line.
Designs for kicking tees in both sports prioritize stability, minimal interference with ball flight, and player-specific adjustments, typically made from durable, lightweight materials like hard rubber or molded plastic to withstand impacts. Rugby tees often feature a single prong or cradle to hold the ball's pointed end at varying heightslow for straight-on kicks or higher for curved trajectoriesallowing customization for kickers like New Zealand's Dan Carter, who endorsed the Supertee for its wind resistance and exposure of the ball's "sweet spot." Weights are kept under 100 grams to avoid affecting distance, with softer rubbers introduced in the 2000s for better follow-through without deflection. In Gaelic football, tees like the Puntee or Murphy's models use bristled or flexible plastic bases for ground-level support, available in low (20mm for precision), medium (35mm for balance), or high (45mm for power) variants to suit short-range accuracy or long-range punts. These designs reduce injury risk by minimizing awkward ball placement and are GAA-approved for safety and performance.
Rules governing kicking tees emphasize fairness and safety without prescriptive design limits. In rugby union, World Rugby defines a kicking tee as "any device approved by the match organiser to support the ball when taking a place-kick," permitted for conversions and penalties but not drop kicks or restarts. No height or material restrictions exist due to negligible safety risks, though tees must not alter the ball's natural shape or provide unfair advantage; once placed, the kick intent is binding, locking in the goal attempt. In Gaelic football, GAA Rule 4.6 allows an optional artificial tee solely for kick-outs, requiring Central Council approval to ensure uniformity and pitch protection; it cannot be used for frees or other kicks, and the kicker may strike the ball multiple times before handover but not handle it. Violations, such as unauthorized use, result in free kicks to the opposition. Both codes prioritize match organiser discretion to maintain equity.
