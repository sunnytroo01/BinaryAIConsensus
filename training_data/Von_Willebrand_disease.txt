 Von Willebrand disease (VWD) is the most common inherited bleeding disorder, affecting up to 1% of the general population and occurring with equal frequency in males and females. It results from quantitative deficiencies or qualitative defects in von Willebrand factor (VWF), a multimeric glycoprotein synthesized primarily in endothelial cells and megakaryocytes that mediates platelet adhesion to subendothelial collagen and stabilizes factor VIII in plasma. This impairment disrupts primary hemostasis and secondary coagulation, leading to prolonged mucocutaneous bleeding and excessive hemorrhage after trauma, surgery, or dental procedures.
First described in 1926 by Finnish physician Erik Adolf von Willebrand, who identified the condition in a large family on the land Islands characterized by severe bleeding tendencies, VWD was initially termed "hereditary pseudohemophilia" before the role of VWF was elucidated in the mid-20th century. The disorder is genetically heterogeneous, arising from mutations in the VWF gene on chromosome 12, and is classified into three primary types: type 1 (partial quantitative VWF deficiency, autosomal dominant inheritance, comprising 70-80% of cases and often mild); type 2 (qualitative VWF defects with four subtypes2A, 2B, 2M, and 2Ntypically autosomal dominant, accounting for about 15-20% of cases and variable in severity); and type 3 (virtually complete VWF absence, autosomal recessive, rare but severe). Acquired forms, though less common (1-5% of cases), can occur secondary to conditions like lymphoproliferative disorders, cardiovascular diseases, or hypothyroidism.
Clinical manifestations vary by type and severity but commonly include epistaxis lasting longer than 10 minutes, easy bruising, menorrhagia (heavy menstrual bleeding) in women, and postpartum or postsurgical hemorrhage. Many individuals with mild type 1 VWD remain undiagnosed until a bleeding event prompts testing, while severe cases may present in infancy with significant challenges. Diagnosis involves a combination of clinical history, family pedigree, and laboratory assays measuring VWF antigen, activity (ristocetin cofactor), and factor VIII levels, with genetic testing for confirmation in ambiguous cases. Management focuses on preventing or treating bleeding episodes through desmopressin, VWF concentrates, antifibrinolytics, or hormonal therapies, as well as recent advancements like expanded approvals for recombinant VWF products in pediatrics (as of September 2025), tailored to the patient's type and needs.

Overview

Definition and Role of von Willebrand Factor

Von Willebrand disease (VWD) is the most common inherited bleeding disorder, affecting approximately 1% of the population, and results from a quantitative deficiency or qualitative dysfunction of von Willebrand factor (VWF). VWF is essential for normal hemostasis, and its impairment leads to prolonged bleeding tendencies due to disrupted primary hemostasis.
VWF is a large multimeric glycoprotein synthesized primarily in endothelial cells and megakaryocytes as a single-chain precursor, pro-VWF, with a molecular weight of about 340 kDa. Following synthesis in the endoplasmic reticulum, pro-VWF undergoes N-linked glycosylation and dimerization through disulfide bonds at the C-terminus in the Golgi apparatus; subsequent multimerization occurs via N-terminal disulfide linkages, forming ultra-large multimers that can exceed 20,000 kDa and are stored in specialized organellesWeibel-Palade bodies in endothelial cells and alpha-granules in platelets. These ultra-large multimers are the most hemostatically active forms, released upon endothelial stimulation to support rapid thrombus formation.
The primary roles of VWF in hemostasis include mediating platelet adhesion to the subendothelium at sites of vascular injury and serving as a carrier for coagulation factor VIII (FVIII). Under high shear stress conditions, such as those in arteries or small vessels (typically >1,000 s), VWF unfolds and binds to exposed collagen via its A3 domain, while its A1 domain interacts with platelet glycoprotein Ib-IX-V, enabling platelet tethering and rolling to form the initial platelet plug. As a carrier protein, VWF binds FVIII with high affinity through its D' and D3 domains, shielding it from proteolytic degradation and extending its plasma half-life from about 2-3 hours when unbound to 8-12 hours in the VWF-FVIII complex, thereby supporting the intrinsic coagulation pathway.
In the normal hemostatic process, VWF facilitates primary hemostasis by promoting platelet aggregation at injury sites under high shear, where traditional platelet-collagen interactions are insufficient; this VWF-dependent adhesion is critical for arresting circulating platelets and initiating plug formation before fibrin reinforcement. VWF's structure and functions are highly conserved across mammalian species, with molecular coevolution alongside FVIII to maintain balanced hemostasis throughout evolutionary diversification.

Epidemiology

Von Willebrand disease (VWD) is the most common inherited bleeding disorder, with genetic variants affecting approximately 1% of the global population, though clinically significant disease manifests in about 1 in 1,000 individuals. Among diagnosed cases, type 1 VWD, characterized by mild quantitative deficiency of von Willebrand factor (VWF), accounts for 70-80% of cases; type 2, involving qualitative defects, comprises 15-20%; and type 3, the severe form with near-total VWF absence, represents about 5%. These estimates derive from population-based studies and registries, highlighting type 1 as the predominant form due to its partial penetrance and milder phenotype.
Demographic patterns show equal genetic occurrence in males and females, but diagnosis rates are higher among females, primarily due to heavy menstrual bleeding prompting earlier medical evaluation. Ethnic variations influence VWF levels and disease expression; for instance, lower baseline VWF in individuals of African or South Asian descent can complicate diagnosis, while certain European populations exhibit higher type 3 prevalence linked to founder mutations like the 2435delC variant. Risk factors for severe disease, particularly homozygous type 3, include consanguinity in isolated communities, which increases the likelihood of inheriting two deleterious VWF alleles.
Underdiagnosis remains a significant issue, especially for mild type 1 cases, as many individuals with low VWF levels (30-50 IU/dL) experience subtle bleeding without seeking care; recent post-2020 screening studies in unselected populations estimate true prevalence up to 1-1.3%, far exceeding reported rates of 0.01-0.1%. Improved screening protocols, including targeted testing for at-risk groups like those with heavy menstrual bleeding or postpartum hemorrhage, have driven rising diagnosis rates; for example, U.S. delivery hospitalization data show VWD prevalence increasing from 2.1 to 5.1 per 10,000 between 2010 and 2020, a trend continuing into 2025 with enhanced guidelines and awareness campaigns.

Pathophysiology and Genetics

Genetics

The von Willebrand factor gene (VWF) is located on the short arm of chromosome 12 at position 12p13.2 and spans approximately 178 kilobases of genomic DNA, comprising 52 exons that range in size from 40 to 1,379 base pairs. The gene encodes a pre-pro-protein of 2,813 amino acids, which undergoes processing to form the mature von Willebrand factor (VWF) protein. This structure includes a partial pseudogene on chromosome 22, which complicates some genetic analyses but does not produce functional protein.
Inherited von Willebrand disease (VWD) follows autosomal dominant inheritance patterns in most cases of Type 1 and Type 2, where a single pathogenic variant in one VWF allele is sufficient to cause the disorder, though penetrance can vary. Type 3 VWD, characterized by severe deficiency, is inherited in an autosomal recessive manner, requiring biallelic pathogenic variants. Variable penetrance in Type 1 VWD is influenced by modifier genes, such as those in the ABO blood group system, where non-O blood types are associated with higher VWF levels and may mitigate mild variants.
Common mutation types in VWD include missense mutations, which predominate in Type 2 and often disrupt specific protein functions like multimerization or domain interactions. In Type 3, null alleles, frameshift mutations, nonsense mutations, or large deletions lead to absent or undetectable VWF protein. A notable example in Type 1 is the Vicenza mutation (p.R1205H) in exon 28, which causes increased VWF clearance from plasma despite normal synthesis. For Type 2A, missense mutations in the A2 domain, such as p.S850P or those affecting codons 742-874, impair collagen binding and lead to loss of high-molecular-weight multimers.
Advancements in genetic testing, particularly next-generation sequencing (NGS), enable comprehensive identification of VWF variants across all exons and intronic regions, improving diagnostic accuracy for ambiguous cases and revealing polygenic modifiers like ABO. NGS panels can also assess copy number variations and distinguish VWD from related disorders.
Carrier detection through targeted sequencing is recommended for relatives of individuals with Type 3 VWD or severe Type 2 cases, facilitating risk assessment. Prenatal counseling and testing, including chorionic villus sampling or amniocentesis for VWF variant detection, are offered in families with severe autosomal recessive forms to inform family planning and perinatal management.

Pathophysiology

Von Willebrand disease (VWD) arises from defects in von Willebrand factor (VWF), a multimeric glycoprotein essential for hemostasis, resulting in impaired primary and secondary hemostatic processes. In primary hemostasis, VWF facilitates platelet adhesion to exposed subendothelial collagen under high shear stress by tethering platelets via glycoprotein Ib (GPIb) receptors, while in secondary hemostasis, VWF acts as a carrier protein that stabilizes factor VIII (FVIII), protecting it from proteolysis and enabling thrombin generation in the coagulation cascade. Disruptions in these functions lead to prolonged bleeding due to inadequate platelet plug formation and reduced fibrin clot stability.
Quantitative defects, characteristic of type 1 and type 3 VWD, involve reduced plasma VWF levels, leading to insufficient VWF multimers for effective platelet adhesion at injury sites and accelerated FVIII degradation due to lack of stabilization. In type 1 VWD, partial deficiency results in mildly to moderately low VWF concentrations, often compounded by increased VWF clearance from circulation via mechanisms such as enhanced hepatic uptake or proteolysis, which further diminishes available VWF half-life. Type 3 VWD represents a virtual absence of VWF, severely compromising both hemostatic arms and often resulting in profound bleeding tendencies.
Qualitative defects, predominant in type 2 VWD, feature dysfunctional VWF despite normal or near-normal antigen levels, with abnormalities in multimerization, binding affinity, or stability. For instance, in type 2A VWD, loss of high-molecular-weight multimers (HMWM) impairs shear-dependent platelet tethering and aggregation, as these larger multimers are critical for unfolding and binding under vascular flow conditions. In type 2B VWD, gain-of-function mutations in the VWF A1 domain cause enhanced binding to platelet GPIb, leading to dominant-negative effects where mutant VWF multimers sequester platelets and promote their clearance, resulting in thrombocytopenia that worsens under stress.
Age-related physiological increases in VWF levels, driven by cumulative endothelial activation and inflammation, can mask mild type 1 VWD by elevating concentrations into the normal range (>50 IU/dL), potentially altering disease expression and complicating diagnosis in older individuals. This age-dependent rise may normalize low VWF in some cases, reducing bleeding risk over time, though it does not apply uniformly to severe forms like type 3.
Additionally, external factors such as inflammation or stress modulate VWF dynamics by triggering exocytosis from endothelial Weibel-Palade bodies (WPBs), where stored VWF is released in response to stimuli like histones or histamine, acutely elevating circulating VWF multimers to support hemostasis but potentially exacerbating imbalances in VWD. This regulated release enhances VWF availability during acute events but highlights how inflammatory states can variably influence disease severity.

Clinical Presentation

Signs and Symptoms

Von Willebrand disease (VWD) primarily manifests through mucocutaneous bleeding, including epistaxis, gingival bleeding, and easy bruising, as well as prolonged bleeding following surgery or trauma. These symptoms arise due to impaired primary hemostasis from deficient or dysfunctional von Willebrand factor (VWF), leading to excessive bleeding from superficial vessels.
In type 1 VWD, the most common form, symptoms are typically mild and include mucocutaneous bleeding such as epistaxis and easy bruising, with menorrhagia affecting 74-93% of women. Type 3 VWD presents more severely, with spontaneous joint and muscle bleeds resembling those in hemophilia, alongside frequent mucocutaneous and postoperative bleeding. Type 2B VWD is associated with mild thrombocytopenia, which can exacerbate bleeding tendencies.
Easy bruising often appears in childhood for individuals with type 1 VWD, while mild cases may remain undiagnosed until adulthood, particularly until events like childbirth or surgery provoke significant bleeding. Some patients with VWD also experience gastrointestinal bleeding due to angiodysplasia.
Symptom severity is quantified using bleeding assessment tools such as the International Society on Thrombosis and Haemostasis Bleeding Assessment Tool (ISTH-BAT), which evaluates bleeding history across multiple sites to aid in clinical characterization.
Complications include iron deficiency anemia from chronic blood loss, particularly in women with recurrent menorrhagia, and rare pseudotumor formation in type 3 VWD due to recurrent deep hemorrhages.

Oral Manifestations

Patients with von Willebrand disease (VWD) commonly present with mucocutaneous bleeding in the oral cavity, including prolonged bleeding following tooth extractions and spontaneous gingival hemorrhage, often triggered by routine activities such as brushing or flossing. These symptoms arise due to the impaired platelet adhesion and stabilization of factor VIII mediated by deficient or dysfunctional von Willebrand factor (VWF).
Periodontal involvement is notable, with increased gingival bleeding observed even in mild cases of VWD; however, severe periodontitis is uncommon but can occur secondary to recurrent infections resulting from delayed wound healing. Patients with VWD commonly experience gingival bleeding, but systematic reviews indicate no increased prevalence or severity of periodontitis compared to the general population, though careful periodontal screening is recommended due to potential false positives in bleeding indices.
Post-procedural risks are significant, particularly after invasive dental interventions such as scaling, root planing, or implant placement, where hemorrhage can persist and is more frequent and severe in Type 3 VWD due to near-complete VWF deficiency. In cohorts including VWD patients, post-extraction bleeding occurs in approximately 15% of cases.
Associated oral findings may include petechiae or ecchymoses on the buccal mucosa and soft palate, reflecting the underlying platelet dysfunction. Epistaxis in VWD can occasionally extend posteriorly into the nasopharynx, leading to oral blood drainage.
The impact on quality of life is substantial, as fear of bleeding complications often leads to avoidance of dental care, resulting in untreated dental caries, advanced periodontal disease, and overall oral health deterioration. Surveys reveal that 18% of VWD patients have been refused dental treatment upon disclosing their diagnosis.
Recent cohort studies report that oral bleeding, including gingival hemorrhage, manifests as an initial symptom in 23-34% of VWD patients, with higher rates (up to 74%) of excessive gum bleeding among affected women.

Diagnosis and Classification

Diagnostic Approach

The diagnosis of von Willebrand disease (VWD) begins with a thorough clinical evaluation, focusing on a detailed personal and family history of bleeding. A comprehensive bleeding history is essential, incorporating standardized bleeding assessment tools such as the ISTH-BAT (International Society on Thrombosis and Haemostasis Bleeding Assessment Tool) to quantify mucocutaneous bleeding symptoms like epistaxis, menorrhagia, or postoperative hemorrhage, which helps identify patients warranting further testing. Family history assessment is equally critical, as VWD is often inherited, and patterns of bleeding in relatives can support suspicion of the disorder, particularly in cases of autosomal dominant transmission.
Laboratory testing forms the cornerstone of confirming VWD, typically initiated in patients with suggestive histories. Initial assays measure von Willebrand factor (VWF) quantity via VWF antigen (VWF:Ag) levels and VWF function through VWF ristocetin cofactor activity (VWF:RCo), alongside factor VIII (FVIII) coagulant activity, as reduced FVIII can occur secondary to VWF deficiency. According to the 2021 ASH/ISTH/NHF/WFH guidelines, a VWF level below 0.30 IU/mL (referring to VWF:Ag and/or VWF activity) diagnoses VWD regardless of bleeding history, while levels of 0.30-0.50 IU/mL require evidence of abnormal bleeding for diagnosis; these thresholds account for assay variability and are measured in IU/dL or IU/mL. For VWF levels between 0.30 and 0.50 IU/mL, a diagnosis of VWD requires evidence of abnormal bleeding. Testing should be performed at least twice to confirm persistence of low levels, ideally under baseline conditions without recent stressors.
For cases with discrepant VWF:Ag and VWF:RCo results or suspected qualitative defects, additional specialized assays are employed. Multimer analysis, typically via low-resolution agarose gel electrophoresis followed by immunoblotting, evaluates VWF multimer distribution to detect loss or abnormalities in high-molecular-weight multimers, aiding in identifying functional variants. Ristocetin-induced platelet aggregation (RIPA) is particularly useful for assessing enhanced VWF-platelet interactions, such as in certain qualitative subtypes, where aggregation occurs at low ristocetin concentrations (e.g., <0.8 mg/mL). These tests are not routine but recommended when initial screening suggests dysfunction.
Genetic testing plays a confirmatory role following phenotypic diagnosis, especially in severe cases or atypical presentations. Sequencing of the VWF gene is indicated for type 3 VWD, where biallelic null variants lead to virtual absence of VWF, or in ambiguous cases to differentiate VWD from mimicking conditions like mild hemophilia A. It is not first-line due to the genetic heterogeneity and incomplete penetrance but supports family counseling and prenatal diagnosis when pathogenic variants are identified.
Diagnostic challenges arise from physiological and pathological variability in VWF levels, necessitating cautious interpretation. VWF concentrations fluctuate with ABO blood type (lowest in type O, approximately 25-50% lower than type AB), pregnancy (which can normalize low levels), hormonal influences, and acute-phase responses like inflammation or stress, potentially masking or exaggerating deficiencies. Repeat testing, spaced 4-6 weeks apart under non-influencing conditions, is advised to establish diagnostic stability, as single measurements may lead to misclassification.
The 2021 ASH/ISTH/NHF/WFH guidelines advocate a multidisciplinary approach involving hematologists, laboratorians, and sometimes gynecologists or dentists for comprehensive evaluation, emphasizing evidence-based thresholds and avoidance of over-testing in low-risk individuals. A 2025 monitoring report by ASH/ISTH/NBDF/WFH reaffirmed the clinical relevance of these recommendations, with no major revisions needed based on post-2021 literature, though ongoing advances in assays like automated VWF activity tests continue to refine precision.

Types of von Willebrand Disease

Von Willebrand disease (VWD) is classified into three main inherited types based on the quantity and quality of von Willebrand factor (VWF), with type 1 representing a partial quantitative deficiency, type 2 involving qualitative defects, and type 3 indicating a complete quantitative deficiency. This classification, established by the International Society on Thrombosis and Haemostasis (ISTH), distinguishes variants by laboratory measurements such as VWF antigen (VWF:Ag), VWF ristocetin cofactor activity (VWF:RCo), and factor VIII (FVIII) levels, alongside multimer analysis. Inherited forms are primarily due to mutations in the VWF gene on chromosome 12, while acquired VWD arises secondarily from other conditions.
Type 1 VWD, the most common form accounting for approximately 70-80% of cases, features a partial quantitative deficiency of VWF with levels typically ranging from 30% to 50% of normal (though diagnostic thresholds are often <30 IU/dL for confirmation). Symptoms are generally mild, including easy bruising and mucosal bleeding, and inheritance is autosomal dominant with variable penetrance. The VWF:RCo/VWF:Ag ratio is usually >0.7, and multimer patterns on gel electrophoresis are normal, reflecting proportionally reduced but functionally intact VWF.
Type 3 VWD represents a severe, complete quantitative deficiency with VWF:Ag and VWF:RCo levels <10 IU/dL, often approaching undetectable, leading to profound bleeding tendencies such as spontaneous joint and muscle hemorrhages akin to hemophilia A. It is inherited in an autosomal recessive manner, typically requiring compound heterozygous or homozygous mutations, and FVIII levels are markedly reduced (<10 IU/dL) due to VWF's stabilizing role. Multimers are absent or severely reduced.
Type 2 VWD encompasses qualitative defects with normal or reduced VWF:Ag but dysfunctional VWF, subdivided into four subtypes based on specific functional impairments. Type 2A, the most common subtype (about 10-15% of VWD cases), involves decreased platelet-dependent adhesion due to loss of high-molecular-weight (HMW) multimers, resulting in a low VWF:RCo/VWF:Ag ratio (<0.6) and moderate bleeding symptoms. Type 2B features increased binding affinity of VWF to platelet glycoprotein Ib, causing transient thrombocytopenia and consumption of HMW multimers, with a low VWF:RCo/VWF:Ag ratio and variable bleeding severity exacerbated by stress. Type 2M is characterized by defective platelet or collagen binding despite normal multimer distribution, yielding a low VWF:RCo/VWF:Ag ratio (<0.6) and mild to moderate mucocutaneous bleeding. Type 2N, often mimicking mild hemophilia A, results from impaired FVIII binding to VWF, leading to low FVIII levels (<10-40 IU/dL) with relatively normal VWF:Ag and VWF:RCo, and inheritance that can appear recessive when co-inherited with hemophilia mutations.
Platelet-type VWD (also known as pseudo-VWD) is a rare inherited variant distinct from VWF gene mutations, caused by gain-of-function mutations in the GP1BA gene encoding platelet glycoprotein Ib, enhancing VWF-platelet interactions under shear stress. This leads to thrombocytopenia, aggregation of platelets, and loss of HMW VWF multimers, phenotypically resembling type 2B but confirmed by genetic testing of GP1BA rather than VWF. Clinical features include mild to moderate bleeding, particularly mucocutaneous.
Acquired VWD (AVWD), not inherited, develops secondary to underlying disorders and is rare, with prevalence varying by the associated condition (e.g., 15-20% in myeloproliferative neoplasms), mimicking inherited types through mechanisms like autoantibodies against VWF, adsorption to tumor or malignant cells, increased shear-induced proteolysis (e.g., in ventricular assist devices), or enhanced clearance. Common associations include myeloproliferative neoplasms (e.g., essential thrombocythemia), hypothyroidism, Wilms tumor, and aortic stenosis, with symptoms varying by severity but often presenting as new-onset bleeding in adulthood. Laboratory findings typically show reduced HMW multimers and low VWF:RCo, resolving with treatment of the primary condition.

Treatment and Management

Treatment Options

The primary treatment for von Willebrand disease (VWD) aims to correct the dual deficiency of von Willebrand factor (VWF) and factor VIII (FVIII) activity to prevent or manage bleeding episodes, with options tailored to disease type and severity. Desmopressin (DDAVP) is recommended as first-line therapy for type 1 VWD and select type 2 subtypes responsive to it, as it stimulates the release of endogenous VWF and FVIII from endothelial cells, typically increasing VWF activity levels by 2- to 6-fold within 30-60 minutes. The standard dose is 0.3 mcg/kg administered intravenously or subcutaneously over 30 minutes, with intranasal administration (150-300 mcg) as an alternative for minor bleeds in responsive patients. However, tachyphylaxisa progressive decrease in responselimits repeated use, typically to every 24-48 hours, and it is contraindicated in type 2B and type 3 VWD due to lack of efficacy or risk of thrombocytopenia.
For patients unresponsive to desmopressin or with type 3 VWD, VWF/FVIII concentrates are the cornerstone of therapy, providing exogenous VWF to restore hemostasis. Plasma-derived concentrates, such as Humate-P or Alphanate, contain both VWF and FVIII and are dosed to achieve VWF ristocetin cofactor (VWF:RCo) activity >50 IU/dL for minor surgery or bleeding control. Recombinant VWF concentrates, like Vonvendi, offer a virus-inactivated option without FVIII and were expanded in September 2025 by the FDA for routine prophylaxis in adults across all VWD types, dosed at 40-60 IU/kg twice weekly to maintain trough levels >50 IU/dL. In perioperative settings, targets are elevated to VWF:RCo and FVIII >100 IU/dL for major procedures, with levels monitored every 12-24 hours to sustain hemostasis for 7-14 days post-surgery.
Adjunctive therapies complement primary treatments for specific bleeding sites. Antifibrinolytics, such as tranexamic acid (10-15 mg/kg orally or intravenously every 6-8 hours), are used for mucosal bleeds like epistaxis or menorrhagia by inhibiting fibrinolysis. Hormonal therapies, including combined oral contraceptives, are effective for heavy menstrual bleeding in women with type 1 VWD by stabilizing the endometrium and increasing endogenous VWF. In emergencies for type 3 VWD, such as refractory bleeding, platelet transfusions may be employed as VWF is stored in platelet alpha-granules, though this is reserved for cases where concentrates are unavailable.
Prophylaxis with VWF concentrates is recommended for severe type 3 VWD or type 2B with recurrent joint or muscle bleeds, administered at 40-60 IU/kg VWF:RCo 2-3 times weekly to prevent spontaneous hemorrhages. As of 2025, expanded approvals like Vonvendi's support broader adult prophylaxis across types, reducing annualized bleeding rates by up to 80% in clinical studies.
Treatment response is monitored through pre- and post-infusion assays of VWF antigen, VWF:RCo, and FVIII activity, with individual desmopressin trials required to confirm at least a twofold rise and sustained levels >50 IU/dL for 4-6 hours. Emerging investigational approaches, such as gene therapy to restore VWF expression, show promise in early trials but are not yet standard care.

Dental Considerations

Patients with von Willebrand disease (VWD) require careful preoperative assessment prior to dental procedures to minimize bleeding risks. This involves consultation with a hematologist to evaluate the patient's specific type of VWD and bleeding history, along with laboratory testing of von Willebrand factor (VWF) and factor VIII (FVIII) levels approximately 1-2 weeks before the procedure to guide prophylactic therapy.
Prophylactic measures are tailored to the VWD subtype and procedure invasiveness. For responsive type 1 patients, desmopressin (DDAVP) is administered 30-60 minutes pre-procedure to transiently increase VWF and FVIII levels, while type 2 and 3 patients typically receive VWF concentrates 1 hour prior to achieve target hemostatic levels above 0.50 IU/mL. Local hemostatic adjuncts, such as fibrin sealants or antifibrinolytic mouthwashes (e.g., tranexamic acid), are routinely used to enhance clot stability during and after procedures.
Bleeding risks vary by procedure and disease severity; extractions and periodontal surgeries carry high risk due to potential for significant tissue trauma, whereas routine cleanings pose low risk in mild cases without prophylaxis. Postoperative care includes close monitoring for 24-48 hours to detect delayed bleeding, with routine use of sutures and absorbable hemostatic agents like gelatin sponges to promote wound closure.
A multidisciplinary approach involving hematologists and dentists is essential for coordinated care, including patient education on home oral hygiene, recognition of bleeding signs, and adherence to follow-up.

History and Other Aspects

History

Von Willebrand disease (VWD) was first identified in 1926 by Finnish physician Erik Adolf von Willebrand, who described a hereditary bleeding disorder in a large family from the isolated land Islands in the Baltic Sea, initially terming it "hereditary pseudohemophilia" to distinguish it from classical hemophilia. He observed severe mucosal bleeding, particularly in a young girl named Hjrdis, who ultimately succumbed to complications during a menstrual period, and noted the condition's autosomal inheritance affecting both sexes equally, unlike hemophilia's X-linked pattern. This seminal description laid the foundation for recognizing VWD as a distinct entity involving prolonged bleeding time without the joint hemorrhages typical of hemophilia.
In the 1950s, researchers established that the bleeding defect in VWD stemmed from a deficiency in a plasma factor separate from factor VIII, with Swedish studies in 1957 identifying von Willebrand factor (VWF) as the key mediator of platelet adhesion responsible for the prolonged bleeding time. By the late 1970s, Theodore E. Zimmerman advanced understanding through the development of multimer analysis, revealing VWF's structure as a series of multimers ranging from dimers to ultra-large polymers essential for hemostasis. Concurrently, in 1977, Pier Mannuccio Mannucci and colleagues conducted the first successful trials of desmopressin (DDAVP), demonstrating its ability to release endogenous VWF stores from endothelial cells, providing a non-transfusional treatment option for milder forms of the disease.
The 1980s brought molecular insights with the cloning of the VWF gene in 1985 by four independent research groups, localizing it to chromosome 12 and enabling genetic studies of VWD variants. In 1994, the International Society on Thrombosis and Haemostasis (ISTH) subcommittee proposed a revised phenotypic classification, dividing VWD into quantitative (types 1 and 3) and qualitative (type 2 subtypes) defects based on VWF levels, activity, and multimer patterns, which standardized diagnosis and research. Mannucci's ongoing contributions during this era included pivotal studies on DDAVP protocols and plasma-derived concentrates, shaping evidence-based management strategies.
The 2000s marked progress in therapeutic innovation, including the development of recombinant VWF to address viral safety concerns with plasma-derived products. Recent advancements include the 2021 joint guidelines from the American Society of Hematology (ASH), ISTH, National Hemophilia Foundation (NHF), and World Federation of Hemophilia (WFH), which refined diagnostic thresholds (e.g., VWF levels <0.30 IU/mL for diagnosis) and emphasized bleeding assessment tools to improve accuracy and reduce overdiagnosis. In September 2025, the FDA expanded approval of Vonvendi (recombinant VWF) for routine prophylaxis in adults with all types of VWD, reducing bleeding frequency, alongside recognition in children for on-demand use. Parallel to these developments, there has been increasing awareness of acquired VWD forms, often linked to underlying conditions like malignancies or cardiovascular devices, prompting integrated diagnostic approaches.

Occurrence in Other Animals

Von Willebrand disease (VWD) is recognized in various animal species, with dogs being the most commonly affected, where it manifests as an inherited bleeding disorder due to deficiencies or dysfunctions in von Willebrand factor (VWF). In canine populations, VWD is prevalent in over 50 breeds, with Type 1 being the most common form, accounting for more than 95% of cases and often presenting mildly, similar to human Type 1 VWD. Doberman Pinschers exhibit the highest incidence, with carrier rates exceeding 70% in screened populations, while Scottish Terriers are notably predisposed to the severe Type 3 variant, characterized by near-total absence of functional VWF.
The disease is rarer in other species; in cats, it has been documented in only a few individuals across various breeds, and in horses, cases are sporadic, primarily in Quarter Horses, with limited clinical reports suggesting low overall prevalence. Pathological features in affected animals mirror human presentations, including prolonged bleeding after trauma or surgery, epistaxis, and in severe cases like Type 3, hemarthrosis or gastrointestinal hemorrhage. Canine models, particularly Type 1 in breeds such as Doberman Pinschers, have proven valuable for research, closely recapitulating human mild VWD and serving as platforms for gene therapy trials, including adeno-associated virus-based approaches to restore VWF expression.
Diagnosis in veterinary practice parallels human methods, relying on assays for VWF antigen (VWF:Ag) and ristocetin cofactor activity (VWF:RCo) to quantify and assess VWF levels and function, respectively, alongside genetic testing for carrier identification in breeding programs. Treatment strategies emphasize supportive care, with desmopressin (DDAVP) showing variable efficacy in mild Type 1 canine cases by transiently boosting VWF release, though it is less reliable than in humans; severe bleeding episodes are managed with cryoprecipitate or fresh frozen plasma transfusions to provide exogenous VWF, or off-label use of human VWF concentrates. Animal models continue to advance human therapeutics, exemplified by the creation of vWF-knockout porcine models using CRISPR-Cas9 in 2014, which demonstrate potential for correcting VWF mutations and informing translational therapies.
