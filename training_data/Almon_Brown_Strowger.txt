Almon Brown Strowger
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Almon Brown Strowger (February 11, 1839 May 26, 1902) was an American inventor and undertaker best known for patenting the first practical automatic telephone exchange, U.S. Patent No. 447,918, issued on March 10, 1891 (filed March 12, 1889), a device that automated call routing and eliminated reliance on human switchboard operators. Born in Penfield, New York, Strowger served in the Union cavalry during the Civil War before becoming a schoolteacher and eventually an undertaker in Kansas City, Missouri, where he settled in the late 1880s.
Strowger's invention stemmed from frustration with the manual telephone system of the era, where operatorsoften biasedroutinely misdirected calls from his undertaking business to a competitor, whose wife worked as a central exchange operator. His electromechanical stepping switch used a rotary arm and electromagnets activated by push buttons (later refined to include a rotary dial patented as U.S. No. 486,909) to connect callers directly via a 10-by-10 contact grid supporting up to 99 lines. With financial backing from family and associates, including engineers A. E. Keith and the Erickson brothers, Strowger founded the Strowger Automatic Telephone Exchange Company in 1891; the system's first commercial installation occurred in La Porte, Indiana, in November 1892, serving 75 subscribers.
The Strowger switch proved scalable and reliable, addressing privacy concerns and inefficiencies of operator-mediated calls, and it became the global standard for automatic telephony until the late 1970s, when touch-tone and electronic systems supplanted it. By 1898, Strowger sold his patent rights and company shares, retiring to St. Petersburg, Florida, where he died in 1902 from an aneurysm (after suffering from anemia); his innovation laid the groundwork for modern telecommunications networks, earning him posthumous induction into the National Inventors Hall of Fame in 2006.

Early Life

Childhood and Family

Almon Brown Strowger was born on October 19, 1839, in Penfield, New York, into a large farming family rooted in the rural agrarian communities of upstate New York. His parents were Samuel Wesley Strowger (17971867) and Jane Clark Strowger (c. 18021880), who raised their children on a modest farm where daily life revolved around agricultural labor and self-sufficiency. As one of several brothers, Strowger grew up in an environment that emphasized practical skills and ingenuity, with limited opportunities for formal education beyond basic schooling in the local area.
Strowger's early childhood was marked by the challenges and routines of 19th-century rural life, including farm chores and community involvement in Penfield, a town near Rochester known for its milling and farming heritage. No records indicate any formal higher education for him during this period, reflecting the typical circumstances of working-class families in the region. By his early twenties, Strowger began transitioning toward broader opportunities, eventually enlisting in military service as a young adult.
On February 1, 1865, shortly after the Civil War, Strowger married Rosaltha Melissa Clarke (18381876) in Ohio, where the couple settled initially. They had two daughters: Harriet Malvina "Hattie" Strowger (18701903) and Mary Rosaltha Strowger (18761952). The family relocated within Ohio to Rochester in Lorain County by 1870, before moving westward to Dragoon Township in Osage County, Kansas, around 1880, seeking new prospects in the growing Midwest.

Civil War Service

Almon Brown Strowger enlisted in the Union Army at the outset of the American Civil War, reflecting the widespread mobilization from his home region in upstate New York. On October 19, 1861, at age 22, he enrolled at Penfield to serve three years in the 8th New York Cavalry Regiment (Veteran), also known as the Rochester Regiment, and was mustered in as a bugler in Company A on October 28, 1861. As a musician, his initial role involved signaling duties on the battlefield, supporting the regiment's operations in the defenses of Washington, D.C., and subsequent campaigns in Virginia.
Strowger advanced through the ranks during his service, demonstrating leadership amid intense combat. He was promoted to chief trumpeter on February 29, 1864, and later to sergeant, before being commissioned as second lieutenant in Company B on August 22, 1864 (with rank from July 16), and mustered in that role on November 1, 1864. The 8th New York Cavalry participated in major engagements, including the Peninsula Campaign, Fredericksburg, Chancellorsville, Gettysburg, and the 1864 Rapidan Campaign, before shifting to the Shenandoah Valley under Major General Philip Sheridan. Strowger returned to Company A briefly on July 1, 1864, contributing to these efforts as the regiment pursued Confederate forces.
Strowger's service ended abruptly due to combat injury during the Shenandoah Valley Campaign. On September 19, 1864, he was wounded in action at the Third Battle of Winchester (also known as the Battle of Opequon), a pivotal Union victory where Sheridan's forces routed Confederate Lieutenant General Jubal Early's army, inflicting heavy casualties and securing control of the valley. Less than three months later, on December 8, 1864, he was mustered out at Rochester, New York, ahead of the regiment's full term, likely in connection with his recovery from the wound.

Career as an Undertaker

After the American Civil War, Almon Brown Strowger returned to the Rochester, New York, area, where he worked as a school principal in Penfield before transitioning into the undertaking profession. In 1882, he relocated to Topeka, Kansas, establishing himself as an undertaker and initially serving as the city's sole practitioner in the field. This move allowed him to build a successful practice, capitalizing on the growing demand for professional funeral services in a burgeoning frontier town.
By 1886, Strowger had moved again, this time to Kansas City, Missouri, where he set up a prominent funeral home. The city, with its expanding population and urban development, presented increased competition among undertakers, but Strowger's establishment gained recognition for its reliability and comprehensive services. His wife, Alice, occasionally assisted in the business operations during these relocations, supporting the family's involvement in the venture.
In the late 19th century, Strowger's daily work as an undertaker encompassed preparing bodies for burial, a role that evolved significantly post-Civil War with the widespread adoption of embalming techniques using arsenic-based fluids to preserve remains for viewing and transport. Undertakers like Strowger also supplied coffins, arranged transportation to cemeteries, and coordinated community rituals, positioning themselves as essential figures in providing dignified farewells and emotional support to grieving families. This profession demanded a blend of practical skills and sensitivity, as undertakers often doubled as furniture dealers or carpenters to meet the era's needs for custom preparations.

Invention of the Automatic Telephone Exchange

Motivation and Initial Concept

In the late 1880s, Almon Brown Strowger, an undertaker operating in Kansas City, Missouri, noticed a sharp decline in his funeral business. He suspected that a local telephone operatorthe wife of a rival undertakerwas deliberately rerouting incoming calls for his services to her husband's establishment, thereby diverting potential clients during a time when telephone inquiries were increasingly common for such arrangements.
This personal setback underscored wider frustrations with the prevailing manual telephone switchboards, where human operators manually connected calls using plugs and cords, often leading to connection delays, errors, and opportunities for bias or interference. Strowger's complaints to the telephone company yielded no resolution, amplifying his determination to address the system's inherent vulnerabilities.
Lacking formal engineering training but motivated by professional necessity, Strowger resolved in 1888 to invent an automatic telephone exchange that would bypass operators entirely. He started with rudimentary sketches and prototypes, such as a device using collar box pins and later electromagnets, gradually conceptualizing an electromechanical switching system capable of routing calls directly based on user input. This realization marked the genesis of a technology that would transform telephony by removing human mediation.

Development and Design

Strowger, lacking formal engineering training, drew on his mechanical aptitude as an undertaker to conceptualize and build an automatic telephone switching system beginning in 1889, driven by a brief suspicion that local operators were intercepting his business calls. His self-taught approach involved iterative experimentation to eliminate human intervention in call routing, focusing on electromechanical principles for reliable connections.
Strowger constructed the initial prototype himself using readily available materials, fashioning the frame from a round collar box, employing straight pins as contacts, and incorporating electromagnets to drive the stepping action, creating a rudimentary yet functional model that demonstrated direct call selection. Later, in late 1893, he enlisted the help of electrician A. E. Keith and mechanics John and Charles J. Erickson to refine the design and develop improved versions.
The core of the design was a two-dimensional selector mechanism capable of 100 positions, arranged in 10 rows of 10 contacts each, where electromagnets enabled stepwise vertical lifting for the first digit and rotary horizontal motion for the second, allowing precise routing based on electrical signals from the caller. The initial design used a set of push-button keys to generate electrical signals for the digits. Strowger later introduced a finger-hole diala precursor to the modern rotary dialpatented in 1892, which generated electrical pulses as the user rotated and released it. Early testing was conducted on small-scale models to verify connection integrity. This selector formed the basis for scalable exchanges, prioritizing simplicity and reliability over complex wiring.

Patenting and First Installations

Strowger filed an application for U.S. Patent No. 447,918 on March 12, 1889, which was granted on March 10, 1891, covering the "Automatic Telephone Exchange" that formed the basis of his electromechanical switching system. This patent described a mechanism allowing subscribers to initiate calls without operator intervention, using a series of stepping switches to route connections automatically.
The first public demonstration and commercial installation of the Strowger system took place in La Porte, Indiana, on November 3, 1892, where it served about 75 subscribers with a total capacity of 99 lines. This small-scale exchange marked the transition from experimental prototypes to practical use, operating reliably in a rural setting and proving the viability of automatic switching for limited networks.
Strowger followed with additional patents to refine the system, including U.S. Patent No. 486,909, granted on November 29, 1892, which detailed enhancements to the automatic exchange, notably the finger-wheel dial mechanism for precise pulse generation to control switch selection.
In the years after the La Porte installation, the Strowger switch expanded to other small independent telephone exchanges across the United States, with installations in places like Fall River, Massachusetts, by 1901 serving around 400 subscribers. By 1900, improved versions were deployed in small and medium-sized systems, representing about 4% of independent lines by 1904. However, scaling to larger networks proved challenging, as the initial design supported only up to 100 lines through a single 10x10 bank, necessitating multilevel selector innovations to achieve 6,000 to 10,000-line capacities, though direct subscriber control limited efficiency in complex urban environments with interoffice trunking.

Commercialization and Finances

Company Formations

In 1891, Almon B. Strowger, along with associates M. A. Meyer and Joseph Harris, founded the Strowger Automatic Telephone Exchange Company in Chicago to commercialize his patented automatic telephone switching system. Meyer served as president, Strowger as vice president, and Harris as secretary, with the group raising initial capital to produce prototype switches.
The following year, engineer A. E. Keith joined the company after leaving the Brush Electric Company, taking on a key role in refining the technology and overseeing early installations. In late 1893, the Erickson brothersJohn and Charles J.and inventor Frank Lundquist integrated their competing automatic system designs into the Strowger operation, bolstering engineering and operational capabilities. These associates helped organize production and secure capital for expansion, focusing on practical deployment of the electromechanical step-by-step switches.
The company pursued early contracts to install automatic exchanges in small U.S. towns, beginning with the world's first commercial system in La Porte, Indiana, on November 3, 1892, serving about 75 subscribers. Subsequent installations included such as Albuquerque, New Mexico, by 1896, demonstrating the system's viability for independent telephone operations outside Bell's manual networks.
To streamline manufacturing and sales, the Strowger Automatic Telephone Exchange Company reorganized in 1901, forming the Automatic Electric Company in Chicago. Under new leadership including C. D. Simpson as president and Joseph Harris as vice president and general manager, the entity exclusively licensed Strowger's patents and handled all production, with A. E. Keith as general superintendent. This structure supported broader adoption, leading to numerous automatic exchanges installed across the United States within the first decade of operations.

Patent Sales and Royalties

In 1896, Strowger sold his initial patents for the automatic telephone exchange to his associates for $1,800, a sum that provided short-term financial relief amid his personal debts. Two years later, in 1898, he sold his shares in the Automatic Electric Company for $10,000, effectively divesting his direct involvement in the commercialization of his invention.
These transactions yielded Strowger a total of $11,800 from his intellectual property, a modest return that failed to reflect the transformative potential of the Strowger switch.
The true value of Strowger's patents emerged posthumously; in 1916, after his death in 1902, the remaining rights were acquired by the Bell System for $2.5 million, highlighting the profound undervaluation of the technology during his lifetime. This sale marked a pivotal economic milestone for the invention, enabling widespread adoption while underscoring the financial challenges Strowger faced as its originator.

Business Challenges

Strowger encountered significant resistance from telephone operators and established companies like the Bell System during the commercialization of his automatic exchange. Operators, primarily women employed in manual switchboards, feared widespread job losses as the technology would automate call routing, eliminating the need for human intervention in local connections. The Bell System, which dominated the industry, opposed adoption due to concerns over reduced control, potential service quality declines, and the "not invented here" syndrome, as the invention originated outside their organization; they refused to purchase the system, rejected interconnection with independent automatic exchanges, and even dismantled Strowger equipment after acquiring competing companies.
Patent disputes further hindered progress in the 1890s, with the Bell System threatening lawsuits against early Strowger installations, alleging infringement on their own patents related to switch designs; these threats delayed sales and widespread implementation, though Strowger's core patent was never successfully challenged in court and ultimately withstood scrutiny.
Early models of the Strowger exchange also faced technical limitations, particularly in reliability and scalability for larger installations. Initial systems required constant technical maintenance and support, rendering them less dependable and more expensive than manual alternatives, with issues arising as exchanges grew beyond small-scale setups of around 100 lines. In bigger urban exchanges, the mechanical step-by-step switching proved slower and more prone to wear, complicating efficient operation without significant refinements.
These business and technical hurdles imposed severe personal financial strains on Strowger, an undertaker by trade with limited resources for prolonged development and marketing. Facing near-bankruptcy for his company by 1897 amid slow adoption and legal pressures, he sold his patents to associates at modest prices to alleviate debts, effectively ceding control of his invention's commercialization.

Later Life and Death

Relocation to Florida

Following the sale of his patents for $1,800 and shares in the Strowger Automatic Telephone Exchange Company for $10,000 in 1896 and 1898, respectively, which provided him financial security, Almon Brown Strowger decided to retire from active business involvement and relocate to St. Petersburg, Florida, in the late 1890s. The move was motivated by the region's warmer climate, appealing for a more relaxed lifestyle in retirement and relief from health issues. Accompanied by his second wife, Susan, whom he married in 1897 after the death of his first wife Rosaltha in 1876, Strowger settled in the burgeoning resort town, which was attracting northern visitors through its beaches and mild weather during the early 1900s.
Upon arrival, the couple purchased a house on Second Avenue N, near what would later become the site of the Florida International Museum. According to the 1900 United States Census, Strowger listed his occupation as a real estate agent in St. Petersburg, indicating his involvement in the local property development scene as the city expanded with tourism and infrastructure growth.
After retirement, Strowger took no further active role in telephony developments. His relocation marked a shift toward a quieter life focused on local opportunities rather than his earlier inventive pursuits.

Final Years and Health

In the late 1890s, after leaving the Strowger Automatic Telephone Exchange Company in the fall of 1896 due to health concerns, Almon Brown Strowger retired to St. Petersburg, Florida. There, he and his wife Susan purchased a home on Second Avenue N., where he spent his remaining years in relative seclusion, removed from the active development and commercialization of his automatic telephone switch. His health continued to worsen, culminating in severe anemia that confined him further as he aged.
Strowger's daily life in retirement involved minimal engagement with inventive pursuits; leisure activities were limited by his physical condition and the humid Florida climate, which exacerbated his age-related ailments. His family life centered on his wife, with his daughters from his first marriagegrown and independent adults by the time of his relocation (Harriet b. 1870, Mary b. 1876)pursuing their own paths elsewhere.
Though detached from the business, Strowger remained cognizant of his invention's expanding adoption, as automatic exchanges based on his design proliferated in cities across the United States following early installations in the 1890s; however, he played no direct role in these advancements after selling his interests.

Death and Burial

Almon Brown Strowger died on May 26, 1902, at the age of 63 in St. Petersburg, Florida, from a ruptured aneurysm, following a period of declining health marked by anemia.
His funeral arrangements were managed by local undertakers in St. Petersburg, an ironic turn given his former profession as an undertaker in Kansas City.
Strowger was buried the following day in Greenwood Cemetery in St. Petersburg, where his grave was initially marked by a simple stone.
His family handled the settlement of his modest estate, which included no significant bequests tied to his earlier patent sales, as he had divested his interests in the automatic telephone technology years prior.

Legacy

Technological Impact

The Strowger switch fundamentally transformed telephony by automating the routing of local calls through electromechanical stepping selectors, thereby eliminating the reliance on human operators for connecting subscribers and enabling direct dialing via rotary pulse mechanisms. This automation addressed key limitations of manual switchboards, such as capacity constraints and potential human error or bias, allowing systems to handle connections more efficiently without intervention. By 1914, over 400,000 dial telephones were in operation across the United States, comprising about 14% of the nation's total phone lines and demonstrating early scaling to urban and rural networks.
The technology's impact extended globally, with Strowger-based step-by-step exchanges adopted widely beyond the U.S. starting in the early 20th century. The first public automatic exchange in the United Kingdom opened in Epsom in 1912, marking the beginning of European deployment, while exports and local manufacturing spread the system to Canada, South America, Australia, Asia, and other regions. By the 1920s, Strowger derivatives accounted for approximately 80% of the world's dial-operated automatic telephone equipment, powering thousands of exchanges that supported expanding networks until the introduction of digital switches in the 1970s and 1980s. While largely supplanted during this period, some Strowger exchanges remained in service into the 1990s in rural and remote areas.
Economically, the shift to automated switching reduced operational costs for telephone providers by minimizing labor requirements, but it came at the expense of widespread job displacement among switchboard operators, a workforce predominantly composed of women. In the United States, operator employment peaked at around 342,000 in 1950 amid partial automation, then plummeted to 40,000 by 1984 as Strowger and successor systems dominated, resulting in the loss of over 300,000 positions in that country alone by the late 20th century. Globally, the transition similarly eliminated hundreds of thousands of such roles, reshaping labor markets in telecommunications while enabling lower call rates and broader access to service.
By establishing pulse dialing as an enduring standard and proving the viability of electromechanical automation, the Strowger switch paved the way for modern telecommunications infrastructure. Its principles influenced the development of more advanced crossbar and electronic systems, serving as a critical bridge from manual to fully automated and eventually digital networks that underpin contemporary voice and data services.

Honors and Recognition

In 1945, telephone industry officials placed a bronze plaque on Strowger's grave in Greenwood Cemetery, St. Petersburg, Florida, to honor his pivotal invention of the automatic telephone exchange.
Strowger received posthumous induction into the Hall of Fame of the United States Independent Telephone Association (later known as the United States Telecom Association, or USTA) in 1965, acknowledging his groundbreaking contributions to telephony innovation.
In 2006, he was inducted into the National Inventors Hall of Fame for developing the automatic telephone-dialing system (U.S. Patent No. 447,918), which enabled direct control of telephone networks without human operators.
The Kansas Historical Society highlighted Strowger's Topeka rootswhere he operated as an undertaker from 1882 to 1886 before his inventive workin a 2024 issue of their magazine Zephyr, underscoring his early career in the state as foundational to his legacy.
