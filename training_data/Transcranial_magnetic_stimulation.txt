Transcranial magnetic stimulation
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) is a noninvasive neurophysiological technique that uses rapidly changing magnetic fields to induce electric currents in specific regions of the brain, thereby stimulating neurons without requiring surgery or direct electrical application. Developed in the mid-1980s, it operates on the principle of electromagnetic induction, where a coil placed against the scalp generates a magnetic pulse that passes through the skull to depolarize superficial cortical neurons, typically up to 2 cm deep.
TMS encompasses several variants, including single-pulse and paired-pulse forms used primarily for diagnostic purposes, such as mapping motor cortex excitability or assessing cortical connectivity, and repetitive TMS (rTMS), which delivers trains of pulses to modulate neural activity over time. High-frequency rTMS (typically >5 Hz) tends to increase cortical excitability, while low-frequency rTMS (1 Hz) decreases it, enabling targeted neuromodulation for therapeutic effects. Advanced forms, such as deep TMS (dTMS), employ specialized coils to reach deeper brain structures like the prefrontal cortex or subcortical regions.
Clinically, TMS has been approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) since 2008 for treating major depressive disorder (MDD) in adults unresponsive to medications, with subsequent expansions to obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) in 2018, migraine with aura in 2013, and as a first-line adjunct for adolescent depression beginning in 2024, with additional clearances for other systems in 2025. It is also applied off-label or investigatively for conditions including anxiety disorders, post-traumatic stress disorder, chronic pain, stroke rehabilitation, Parkinson's disease, and schizophrenia, often showing efficacy in reducing symptoms through sustained changes in neural circuits. In research, TMS serves as a tool to probe brain function, connectivity, and plasticity, contributing to insights into cognition, motor control, and neuropsychiatric mechanisms.
TMS is generally considered safe, with mild side effects such as scalp discomfort or headache occurring in a minority of sessions, and rare serious risks like seizures limited to specific protocols or patient vulnerabilities. Ongoing advancements, including accelerated protocols cleared by the FDA in 2025 for major depressive disorder and anxious depression, and personalized targeting via neuroimaging integration, promise broader applications and improved outcomes in neuromodulation therapy.

Fundamentals

Definition and principles

Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) is a non-invasive technique that uses rapidly changing magnetic fields to induce electrical currents in targeted regions of the brain, thereby depolarizing neurons without requiring surgical intervention. This method relies on electromagnetic induction to stimulate superficial cortical areas, typically up to 2 cm deep, allowing researchers and clinicians to probe or modulate brain activity in vivo. At its core, TMS exploits the biophysical property of neurons to generate action potentials when their membrane potential reaches a depolarization threshold, fundamentally involving the influx of ions through voltage-gated channels that propagate electrical signals across neural networks.
The primary principle underlying TMS is Faraday's law of electromagnetic induction, which states that a time-varying magnetic field induces an electric field in a conductor, such as neural tissue. In practice, a high-intensity magnetic pulse (typically 1-2 Tesla) generated by an electromagnetic coil placed on the scalp penetrates the skull and soft tissues with minimal attenuation, as magnetic fields are not significantly resisted by biological barriers. This induced electric field, oriented tangentially to the cortical surface, can reach strengths sufficient to depolarize neuronal membranes if it exceeds the activation threshold, estimated at approximately 50-100 V/m for cortical pyramidal cells. Direct activation primarily affects axons in the superficial layers of the cortex, leading to synchronous firing of neuronal ensembles.
TMS can be delivered in various forms to achieve different effects on brain function. Single-pulse TMS delivers isolated stimuli to map cortical representations, such as identifying motor areas by eliciting muscle twitches. Paired-pulse TMS involves two closely timed pulses to assess intracortical excitability and inhibition, providing insights into neural circuit dynamics. Repetitive TMS (rTMS), consisting of trains of pulses at specific frequencies, induces longer-lasting changes in cortical excitability, often analogous to synaptic plasticity mechanisms like long-term potentiation (LTP) for high-frequency stimulation or long-term depression (LTD) for low-frequency patterns. These effects arise from Hebbian-like strengthening or weakening of synaptic connections, enabling modulation of neural circuits beyond the immediate stimulation period.

Physics of magnetic stimulation

Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) relies on the generation of rapidly changing magnetic fields using specialized coils placed on the scalp. These coils, typically figure-8 or circular designs, produce pulsed magnetic fields with peak strengths of 1-2 Tesla at the coil surface. The magnetic field decays rapidly with distance from the coil, approximating an inverse cube law (B 1/r) due to its dipole-like nature, which limits effective stimulation to superficial brain regions.
The core biophysical mechanism of TMS is electromagnetic induction, governed by Faraday's law of induction. A time-varying magnetic field (B) induces an electric field (E) in conductive tissues according to the equation:

This induced electric field drives neuronal depolarization without direct electrical contact. For suprathreshold stimulation of cortical neurons, the induced E-field typically reaches 100-200 V/m at the cortical surface, depending on coil orientation and pulse parameters.
The penetration depth of TMS is shallow, generally 1-2 cm into the brain for standard coils, as the induced E-field attenuates exponentially with depth. Focality varies by coil type; figure-8 coils achieve a more localized stimulation volume of approximately 1-2 cm (or a half-maximum area of about 5 cm), concentrating the field under the coil's junction, whereas circular coils produce broader, less focal patterns.
In brain tissue, the induced electric field generates eddy currents due to the conductivity of neural structures (approximately 0.3 S/m in gray matter). These looping currents depolarize cell membranes but do not cause direct heating or tissue damage under standard protocols, with estimated temperature rises below 0.1C.
Safety thresholds for TMS are guided by the International Commission on Non-Ionizing Radiation Protection (ICNIRP) guidelines, which limit induced current densities to prevent nerve stimulation or thermal effects. For pulsed fields like those in TMS (frequencies <10 Hz), the basic restriction is an induced current density of 10 mA/m (occupational exposure) averaged over a 1 cm cross-section of CNS tissue.

Procedure and techniques

Stimulation protocols

Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) employs various protocols tailored to diagnostic or modulatory goals, each defined by specific pulse patterns and parameters to target cortical excitability. Single-pulse TMS delivers isolated magnetic pulses, typically at intensities around 100-150% of the resting motor threshold (RMT), to evoke measurable responses such as motor evoked potentials (MEPs) in peripheral muscles, facilitating brain mapping and assessment of corticospinal pathway integrity.
Paired-pulse TMS extends this by applying two successive pulses separated by a short interstimulus interval (ISI) of 1-20 milliseconds, enabling evaluation of intracortical inhibitory and facilitatory circuits involving interneurons; for instance, ISIs of 1-5 ms often reveal short-interval intracortical inhibition (SICI), while 10-20 ms intervals assess intracortical facilitation (ICF).
Repetitive TMS (rTMS) involves trains of pulses to induce neuroplastic changes, with high-frequency protocols (5-20 Hz) promoting excitation and low-frequency ones (1 Hz) fostering inhibition; common parameters include frequencies of 1-20 Hz, pulse durations of 100-300 s, train lengths of 4-8 seconds, and inter-train intervals of 20-30 seconds to maintain safety and coil temperature control.
Central to protocol implementation is the determination of RMT, calculated as the lowest stimulator output that elicits an MEP amplitude exceeding 50 V in at least 5 of 10 consecutive trials from the relaxed target muscle, usually the abductor pollicis brevis, using electromyography for recording.
A standard session begins with securing the patient in a stable position, either seated or supine, followed by identifying the stimulation sitesuch as the motor cortex hotspot via MEP optimizationand setting intensity to 80-120% of RMT based on the protocol's demands.
Theta-burst stimulation (TBS) represents an efficient variation of rTMS, comprising bursts of three 50 Hz pulses repeated at 5 Hz to emulate hippocampal theta rhythms and accelerate synaptic plasticity; intermittent TBS applies 2-second bursts every 10 seconds, whereas continuous TBS delivers uninterrupted 40-second sequences.
Therapeutic regimens commonly involve 20-30 sessions of rTMS or TBS, scheduled daily or near-daily across 4-6 weeks, with parameters adjusted per individual RMT to optimize efficacy.

Coil designs and targeting

Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) relies on specialized coils to generate magnetic fields that induce electric currents in targeted brain regions. Coil designs vary in shape, size, and configuration to balance focality, depth of penetration, and stimulated volume. Early designs, such as circular coils, produce a broad, relatively diffuse magnetic field suitable for deeper but less precise stimulation, often penetrating up to several centimeters with a wider spread of activation.
Figure-of-eight (or butterfly) coils represent a widely adopted focal design, consisting of two adjacent circular loops that create a concentrated electric field at their intersection, typically achieving depths of 1-2 cm with high spatial resolution of about 1 cm in diameter. This configuration is particularly effective for superficial cortical targets, such as in motor cortex mapping, due to its ability to minimize unwanted stimulation in adjacent areas. For deeper brain structures, H-coils and double-cone coils have been developed to extend penetration to 3-6 cm while maintaining reasonable focality. H-coils, with their elongated, helmet-like windings, generate fields that reach subcortical regions like the anterior cingulate cortex, offering broader coverage compared to figure-of-eight coils but with reduced superficial intensity. Double-cone coils, featuring two large loops angled at 90-120 degrees, provide efficient energy transfer for deeper targets, such as the leg motor area, balancing stimulated volume and field strength for clinical applications like treating obsessive-compulsive disorder.
Targeting in TMS involves precise coil placement to align the induced electric field with specific neuroanatomical sites. Traditional methods use anatomical landmarks, such as positioning the coil 5 cm anterior to the motor hotspot (identified via motor evoked potentials) to target the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) in depression treatment. This beam's eye view approach relies on scalp measurements but can vary by up to 2-3 cm across individuals due to anatomical differences.
Advanced neuronavigation systems enhance accuracy by integrating structural MRI or functional MRI (fMRI) data to guide coil positioning in real-time, achieving sub-millimeter precision and reducing inter-session variability to less than 1 cm. These systems employ optical tracking or electromagnetic sensors to register the patient's head to a 3D brain model, allowing individualized targeting based on gyral landmarks or functional connectivity maps. Frameless stereotaxy and robotic arms further improve reproducibility; for instance, robotic positioning compensates for patient head movements by dynamically adjusting the coil orientation, maintaining alignment throughout sessions lasting up to 40 minutes. Integration with EEG or MEG provides real-time feedback on cortical responses, enabling adaptive adjustments to optimize stimulation efficacy.
Recent innovations include double-cone variants optimized for specific deep structures and multi-coil arrays that enable simultaneous stimulation of multiple sites, potentially improving therapeutic outcomes in network-based disorders. Wearable TMS devices with lightweight, battery-powered coils address portability, reducing setup time while incorporating cooling mechanisms to sustain prolonged use.
Challenges in coil design and targeting include managing thermal buildup from repetitive pulses, which can limit session duration and require active cooling like pre-chilled heat sinks or optimized winding materials to prevent overheating above 40-50C. Patient movement compensation remains critical, as even small shifts (1-2 mm) can displace the field by up to 5 mm, underscoring the need for robust tracking in clinical settings.

Clinical applications

Diagnostic uses

Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) serves as a non-invasive tool for mapping the motor cortex by delivering single-pulse stimuli to evoke motor evoked potentials (MEPs) in peripheral muscles, thereby assessing the integrity of the corticospinal tract. This technique is particularly valuable in evaluating motor pathway dysfunction following stroke, where TMS-induced MEPs help quantify the extent of corticospinal damage and predict recovery potential by measuring latency and amplitude variations across affected and unaffected hemispheres. In amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), single-pulse TMS reveals early corticospinal tract involvement through prolonged central motor conduction times and reduced MEP amplitudes, aiding in the confirmation of upper motor neuron degeneration. Threshold-tracking TMS further enhances diagnostic precision in ALS by reliably detecting cortical hyperexcitability as an early biomarker, correlating with disease progression.
Cortical excitability testing employs paired-pulse TMS protocols to probe intracortical inhibitory and facilitatory circuits, providing insights into pathophysiological imbalances in neurological disorders. Short-interval intracortical inhibition (SICI), measured at interstimulus intervals of 1-5 ms, reflects GABAergic inhibition and is often reduced in early Parkinson's disease, indicating impaired intracortical inhibition that contributes to motor symptoms like tremor. Similarly, intracortical facilitation (ICF) is diminished in Parkinson's, further highlighting altered excitatory-inhibitory balance detectable via these protocols. In epilepsy, paired-pulse TMS demonstrates cortical hyperexcitability through reduced inhibition and enhanced facilitation, offering a sensitive measure for identifying seizure-prone networks, particularly in temporal lobe epilepsy where bilateral tonic-clonic seizures correlate with excitability changes.
Combining TMS with electroencephalography (TMS-EEG) enables the assessment of effective connectivity within brain networks by analyzing perturbation-evoked responses. This approach probes causal interactions in the default mode network (DMN), revealing interregional connectivity patterns through integrated TMS-EEG and MRI data, which can highlight disruptions in resting-state networks associated with cognitive and neurological impairments.
Preoperative TMS applications, particularly navigated repetitive TMS (nrTMS), facilitate the identification of eloquent cortical areas to guide tumor resection and minimize postoperative deficits. For language mapping, nrTMS induces speech arrest by targeting perisylvian regions, reliably delineating speech-eloquent sites in glioma patients, with high concordance to intraoperative direct electrical stimulation even in cases involving anatomical distortions. This method supports precise localization of areas critical for speech production, enhancing surgical planning for lesions near language centers.

Therapeutic uses

Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS), particularly repetitive TMS (rTMS), serves as a non-invasive therapeutic modality primarily for modulating cortical excitability in psychiatric and neurological disorders, with established applications in treatment-resistant cases where conventional pharmacotherapy has failed. High-frequency rTMS protocols aim to enhance activity in underactive regions, while low-frequency approaches inhibit overactive areas, leading to symptom alleviation through neuroplastic changes.
In major depressive disorder (MDD), especially treatment-resistant depression (TRD), the standard protocol involves high-frequency rTMS (10 Hz) applied to the left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) at 120% of the motor threshold, delivering 3000 pulses per session over 4-6 weeks. This typically entails 2030 sessions administered 5 days per week. Many patients begin to notice symptom improvement after 24 weeks (approximately 1020 sessions), with fuller effects often seen by the end of the course; individual responses vary, and some may require more sessions. This targets hypoactive prefrontal circuits implicated in mood regulation, with remission rates of 30-50% in TRD patients who have failed at least two adequate antidepressant trials of different classes, defined as less than 50% symptom reduction after sufficient dose and duration (typically 4-8 weeks). Meta-analyses confirm superiority over sham stimulation, with moderate effect sizes (standardized mean difference 0.5-0.8) for response and remission, based on Hamilton Depression Rating Scale improvements. Patient selection emphasizes baseline assessments, including symptom severity via standardized scales and confirmation of TRD criteria, often incorporating diagnostic mapping to optimize DLPFC targeting for stratification. Antidepressant durability post-rTMS induction averages 6-12 months, with sustained responder rates of approximately 50% at one year, particularly when maintenance sessions are included. As of 2024-2025, FDA clearances have expanded indications to adolescents aged 15 and older as a first-line adjunct for MDD. Internationally, Asan Medical Center in Seoul, South Korea, officially introduces repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) as a non-invasive treatment option for depression on its health information page, describing it as using magnetic fields to stimulate brain neurons to produce excitatory or inhibitory effects. The hospital has conducted related research in departments such as rehabilitation medicine and includes it in their depression disease encyclopedia, illustrating broader international recognition and use beyond FDA-approved contexts.
Accelerated rTMS protocols, delivering multiple sessions per day over 5-10 days, have received FDA clearance as of 2025 for MDD treatment, offering rapid symptom relief with response rates of 60-80% and remission rates up to 50% in TRD, comparable to or exceeding standard protocols while reducing overall treatment time.
For migraine with aura, single-pulse transcranial magnetic stimulation (sTMS) is FDA-approved since 2013 for acute pain relief, with devices like the Cerena TMS applied at aura onset to abort headaches; preventive use is also cleared for ages 12 and older, reducing monthly migraine days by 50% in clinical trials.
For obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD), low-frequency rTMS (1 Hz) targeting the supplementary motor area (SMA) inhibits hyperactivity in cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical circuits, typically administered over 20-30 sessions at 100-110% motor threshold with 1000-2000 pulses per session. Response rates, defined as at least 25-35% reduction in Yale-Brown Obsessive Compulsive Scale scores, reach approximately 30% in medication-refractory cases, with meta-analyses indicating modest overall efficacy (Hedge's g = 0.79) superior to sham, especially for low-frequency protocols over SMA compared to other targets like the orbitofrontal cortex.
Beyond psychiatric indications, rTMS shows evidence for neurological applications. In chronic neuropathic pain, high-frequency stimulation (5-20 Hz, 3000 pulses/session) of the contralateral primary motor cortex (M1) yields definite efficacy (Level A evidence), reducing pain intensity by 30-50% in conditions like fibromyalgia or post-stroke pain, through modulation of thalamocortical pain networks. For tinnitus, low-frequency rTMS (1 Hz, 2000 pulses/session) over the temporal cortex, guided by auditory mapping, demonstrates efficacy in meta-analyses, with significant reductions in Tinnitus Handicap Inventory scores (effect size ~0.4-0.6) and response in 20-40% of chronic cases, though durability varies. In post-stroke motor recovery, high-frequency rTMS (10 Hz) to the ipsilateral M1 enhances excitability in the affected hemisphere, improving upper extremity function (Fugl-Meyer Assessment gains of ~5-10 points) particularly in subacute phases (<6 months) and severe impairments, as supported by low-bias randomized trials.

Safety and side effects

Adverse effects

The most common adverse effects of transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) are mild and transient, including scalp discomfort or pain, which occurs in approximately 30-50% of patients, often due to the mechanical tapping of the stimulation coil on the scalp.01862-4/fulltext) Headaches are reported in 20-35% of individuals, typically resolving shortly after sessions and decreasing with continued treatment.01862-4/fulltext) Transient facial twitching or muscle spasms, resulting from indirect stimulation of nearby nerves or muscles, affect about 5-20% of patients, particularly during high-frequency protocols targeting the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex.
In the context of treating depression, these side effects are minor, such as headache and scalp discomfort, and TMS offers the advantage of avoiding systemic side effects associated with antidepressant medications while being fully non-invasive.
Rare but serious adverse effects include seizures, with an incidence of approximately 0.07% per patient (or 0.71 per 1,000 patients) and 0.31 per 10,000 sessions when using standard protocols, though the risk is elevated in those with a history of epilepsy. Recent meta-analyses as of 2025 confirm that TMS remains safe across diverse populations, with no increased adverse event rates in conditions like schizophrenia or substance use disorders. Hearing impairment can occur if ears are not protected during treatment, as the coil generates loud impulsive noise exceeding 100 dB, potentially causing temporary threshold shifts; permanent loss is rare with proper earplugs.
Adverse effects are often dose-dependent, with higher stimulation intensity or frequency increasing the likelihood and severity of scalp discomfort and headaches, while cumulative sessions over multiple days may lead to mild fatigue in some patients. To mitigate risks, pre-treatment monitoring includes audiometry to assess baseline hearing and screening for seizure history or factors like medication use that could lower the seizure threshold.
Long-term studies, including MRI assessments, show no evidence of structural brain changes or cognitive decline associated with repeated TMS sessions. Risks may be heightened in patients with contraindications such as metal implants near the stimulation site.

Contraindications and limitations

Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) has specific absolute contraindications due to the risk of device malfunction or tissue damage from the strong magnetic fields generated. These include the presence of ferromagnetic implants or devices in or near the head, such as pacemakers, implantable cardioverter defibrillators, cochlear implants, aneurysm clips, or other metallic hardware within 30 cm of the stimulation site. A history of seizures or epilepsy is also considered an absolute contraindication in many guidelines, as TMS can lower the seizure threshold and potentially induce convulsions.
Relative contraindications involve conditions where TMS may be used with caution and close monitoring, but require careful risk-benefit assessment. These encompass medications that lower the seizure threshold, such as bupropion, tricyclic antidepressants, or antipsychotics; and skull defects or previous craniotomies, which may alter the distribution of induced electric fields and increase the risk of unintended stimulation. Pregnancy, while previously considered a relative contraindication due to limited data, is now supported as safe by recent studies as of 2025, with no adverse fetal effects reported and its use recommended for perinatal depression under monitoring. Patients with a family history of seizures or other neurological conditions that could predispose to adverse events also fall into this category, necessitating thorough pre-treatment screening.
Practical limitations of TMS stem from its biophysical properties and biological variability. The magnetic fields penetrate only to a depth of approximately 2-3 cm into the cortex, making it ineffective for stimulating deeper subcortical structures without excessive superficial stimulation that exceeds safety limits. Additionally, there is significant inter-individual variability in response to TMS, influenced by factors such as cortical anatomy, genetics, and baseline neurophysiology, which can affect treatment efficacy and require personalized dosing or targeting adjustments.
Access to TMS is hindered by logistical and economic barriers. Each session typically lasts 20-40 minutes, with a full course requiring 20-36 sessions over 4-6 weeks, demanding substantial time commitment from patients. The need for specialized equipment, such as magnetic coils and navigation systems, confines TMS to clinical settings, and costs range from $300-500 per session, potentially totaling $6,000-15,000 for a complete treatment, though insurance coverage varies.
Ethical considerations in TMS emphasize robust informed consent, particularly for off-label applications beyond approved indications like major depressive disorder. Patients must be fully apprised of potential risks, including rare seizures or common transient side effects like headaches, and the uncertain efficacy in unapproved uses to ensure autonomous decision-making.

History and development

Invention and early research

The conceptual foundations of transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) trace back to mid-20th-century explorations of magnetic fields for nerve stimulation, building on earlier electromagnetic principles. In the 1950s and 1960s, researchers demonstrated that time-varying magnetic fields could induce electrical currents in biological tissues to stimulate peripheral nerves without direct contact or pain. For instance, in 1959, Alexander Kolin and colleagues showed that pulsed magnetic fields could elicit nerve activation and muscle contraction in frog sciatic nerves, laying groundwork for non-invasive applications. By 1965, Bickford and Fremming extended this to humans, using damped sinusoidal magnetic pulses (500 Hz) to stimulate peripheral nerves and induce muscular responses in intact subjects, confirming the feasibility of painless nerve excitation across species.
The invention of TMS for central nervous system stimulation occurred in 1985, when Anthony T. Barker and colleagues at the University of Sheffield developed the first prototype device capable of non-invasively stimulating the human brain. Motivated by the limitations of invasive electrical stimulation, which caused discomfort due to scalp electrode impedance, Barker's team adapted principles of electromagnetic induction to generate focal magnetic fields that penetrate the skull and induce currents in cortical neurons. The device employed a circular coil powered by a high-voltage capacitor discharge, producing magnetic pulses up to 1.5 Tesla to target superficial brain regions without skin contact.
Early experiments from 1985 to 1990 focused on single-pulse TMS to evoke motor evoked potentials (MEPs) in peripheral muscles, validating non-invasive access to cortical motor areas. In their seminal demonstration, Barker et al. applied pulses over the hand motor cortex, reliably eliciting contralateral thumb movements and MEPs in abductor pollicis brevis muscles with latencies consistent with direct corticospinal activation. These studies confirmed that TMS could depolarize superficial cortical neurons at thresholds around 50-70% of maximum stimulator output, providing a painless alternative to transcranial electrical stimulation for mapping motor pathways. Initial findings highlighted TMS's safety, with no reported pain or discomfort during stimulation of the hand area, in contrast to the scalp irritation from electrical methods.
The foundational publication, Barker et al.'s 1985 report in The Lancet, detailed the first human TMS experiments and established the technique's viability for clinical neurophysiology. Titled "Non-invasive magnetic stimulation of human motor cortex," it described the device's construction and the elicitation of MEPs, sparking widespread adoption for motor cortex research. Subsequent papers by the Sheffield group in the late 1980s refined single-pulse protocols, demonstrating applications in assessing corticospinal excitability in healthy volunteers and patients with neurological disorders.

Key milestones

In the 1990s, repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) emerged as a key advancement, with Alvaro Pascual-Leone and colleagues introducing the technique in 1991 to study cortical plasticity through rapid-rate pulses that induced speech arrest and counting errors in normal volunteers, demonstrating its potential to modulate brain excitability safely. This built on single-pulse TMS by enabling patterned stimulation to investigate neuroplasticity. Later that decade, Mark S. George and team conducted the first clinical trials of rTMS for depression in 1995, showing mood improvements in patients after daily left prefrontal sessions, marking the shift toward therapeutic applications.
The 2000s saw technological refinements and regulatory progress, including the standardization of figure-8 coils, which improved focal stimulation depth and precision over circular designs, becoming the default for clinical rTMS by mid-decade due to their ability to target cortical regions up to 2 cm deep. In 2005, Ying-Zu Huang developed theta burst stimulation (TBS), a high-frequency protocol mimicking natural brain rhythms to induce rapid, long-lasting changes in cortical excitability with fewer pulses than traditional rTMS, facilitating shorter sessions. A pivotal clinical milestone occurred in 2008 when the FDA cleared the NeuroStar TMS system for treating major depressive disorder in adults unresponsive to antidepressants, based on sham-controlled trials showing significant symptom reduction.
During the 2010s, TMS expanded to additional indications and advanced hardware. In 2013, the FDA cleared the Cerena TMS device for acute relief of migraine pain with aura, validating its role in pain management through single-pulse stimulation. Deep TMS coils, developed by BrainsWay using H-coil designs patented in 2002, gained FDA clearance in 2013 for depression, enabling stimulation of deeper brain structures like the anterior cingulate with fields up to 3 cm, enhancing efficacy for treatment-resistant cases. Integration with neuroimaging, such as functional MRI-guided targeting, advanced in the mid-2010s, allowing personalized coil placement based on individual connectivity patterns to optimize outcomes. In 2018, the FDA cleared BrainsWay's Deep TMS for obsessive-compulsive disorder, supported by randomized trials demonstrating symptom remission in 38% of patients.
Pre-2023 evidence solidified TMS's role through meta-analyses confirming its efficacy for depression, with a 2022 review of 81 trials reporting a moderate effect size (Hedges' g = 0.55) for response rates compared to sham. Global guidelines, such as the 2016 Canadian Network for Mood and Anxiety Treatments (CANMAT) recommendations, positioned rTMS as a second-line option for moderate-to-severe depression, emphasizing its tolerability and antidepressant augmentation potential.
Up to 2023, accelerated protocols represented a recent breakthrough, exemplified by the Stanford Accelerated Intelligent Neuromodulation Therapy (SAINT) developed by Nolan Williams and colleagues, with key publications in 2020, which delivered high-dose intermittent TBS over five days with fMRI-guided targeting, achieving 79% remission in treatment-resistant depression. The FDA granted breakthrough device designation in 2021 for the SAINT system and provided full clearance in September 2022, accelerating its development for rapid symptom relief.
Subsequent years saw further regulatory expansions. In 2024, the FDA cleared NeuroStar TMS as a first-line adjunct for major depressive disorder in adolescents. As of November 2025, additional clearances included BrainsWay's Deep TMS for adolescents aged 15-21 and neurocare group's Apollo TMS for major depression in this age group.

Current status and future directions

Regulatory approvals

In the United States, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) first cleared transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) for therapeutic use in 2008, approving the NeuroStar TMS Therapy system for the treatment of major depressive disorder (MDD) in adult patients who have failed to achieve satisfactory improvement from one prior antidepressant medication at or above the minimal effective dose and duration. Subsequent approvals expanded indications: in 2013, the Cerena TMS device was cleared for the acute treatment of pain associated with migraine headaches with aura in patients aged 18 years and older. In 2018, the BrainsWay Deep TMS system received clearance as an adjunct for treating obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) in adults with treatment-resistant symptoms. Additionally, in 2020, the BrainsWay Deep TMS system was cleared as an aid for short-term smoking cessation in adults aged 22 years and older who have smoked at least 10 cigarettes per day for at least one year and failed at least one prior cessation attempt. In March 2024, the FDA cleared the NeuroStar Advanced Therapy system as a first-line adjunct for treating MDD in adolescents aged 15 to 21 years. In November 2025, the FDA cleared the BrainsWay Deep TMS system for adjunctive treatment of MDD in adolescents aged 15 to 21 years, expanding from its prior adult indication.
In the European Union, TMS devices have obtained CE marking under the Medical Device Regulation, certifying conformity with health, safety, and environmental protection standards. CE marks have been granted for repetitive TMS (rTMS) treatment of depression since at least 2011, with protocols similar to FDA approvals for MDD, and as a co-adjuvant for OCD using comparable stimulation parameters. The certification is broader for diagnostic applications, including motor evoked potential mapping for neurological assessments, as several systems like MagVenture's are approved for such uses across EU member states.
Health Canada has issued medical device licenses mirroring many FDA indications, including TMS for treatment-resistant depression since 2008 and OCD in 2018, with the BrainsWay Deep TMS system licensed in 2022 specifically for smoking cessation as an adjunctive aid. Approvals in Asia remain more limited; for instance, China's National Medical Products Administration (NMPA) approved certain TMS devices for depression treatment around 2013, though broader indications like OCD or pain are not uniformly authorized.
Insurance coverage for TMS varies by region and indication. In the US, Medicare and Medicaid cover TMS for treatment-resistant depression (TRD) following the 2008 FDA clearance, typically requiring failure of at least four antidepressant trials and applying to outpatient sessions up to 36 treatments over the initial course, with maintenance possible under certain conditions. In the UK, the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) recommended rTMS for severe depression in 2015, enabling coverage through the National Health Service (NHS) for patients unresponsive to pharmacological and psychological therapies. Private insurers in the US and elsewhere show significant variability, with many covering FDA-approved indications like depression and OCD after prior authorization and documentation of medical necessity, though denials are common for off-label uses due to differing policy criteria.
Many TMS applications, such as for anxiety disorders, remain off-label and require case-by-case approval from insurers or regulatory bodies, often necessitating demonstration of inadequate response to standard treatments.

Ongoing research and emerging applications

Recent advancements in transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) have focused on technological innovations to enhance treatment precision and efficiency. AI-guided personalization, leveraging machine learning algorithms to optimize stimulation targets based on individual neuroimaging data, has shown promise in improving outcomes for various neurological conditions. For instance, deep neural network models integrating functional MRI (fMRI), diffusion tensor imaging (DTI), and electroencephalography (EEG) data can predict patient-specific responses to TMS, enabling tailored protocols that boost clinical efficacy. Accelerated TMS protocols, such as the Stanford Accelerated Intelligent Neuromodulation Therapy (SAINT), deliver intensive sessions over days rather than weeks, achieving remission rates of up to 79% in treatment-resistant depression within one week, compared to 4-6 weeks for standard TMS. These approaches, refined in 2024-2025 studies, reduce treatment duration while maintaining or exceeding traditional response rates of 45-55% in depression.
Emerging applications are expanding TMS to new indications beyond established uses. In stroke rehabilitation, high-frequency repetitive TMS (rTMS) targeting the ipsilesional primary motor cortex (M1) has demonstrated significant motor function gains, as evidenced by 2025 meta-analyses showing improved upper limb performance in post-stroke patients. Bibliometric analyses from 2025 highlight a surge in research on TMS for stroke, with hotspots in neuroplasticity and recovery protocols, underscoring its growing role in motor rehabilitation. For neurodevelopmental disorders, ongoing 2025 clinical trials are investigating rTMS for autism spectrum disorder, applying stimulation to the motor cortex to enhance motor skills and social functioning. Similar trials for attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) explore rTMS to modulate prefrontal activity, aiming to improve attention and executive function. Additionally, following the 2020 FDA clearance of deep TMS for smoking cessation, studies report reduced craving and higher quit rates when combined with nicotine replacement therapy.
Integration of TMS with advanced modalities is driving precision neuromodulation. Closed-loop TMS-fMRI systems enable real-time adjustment of stimulation based on brain activity feedback, particularly in post-stroke recovery, where 2025 innovations allow for dynamic targeting of affected networks to promote neuroplasticity. Neuroimaging-AI hybrids further refine this by fusing multimodal data for individualized dosing, as outlined in a 2025 Frontiers review emphasizing enhanced accuracy and reduced variability in treatment responses.
Challenges in TMS research include developing long-term maintenance protocols for major depressive disorder (MDD), where 2025 randomized trials demonstrate that rTMS maintenance therapy prevents relapse in treatment-resistant cases with efficacy comparable to lithium, sustaining remission over 12 months. Trends toward equity in access are emerging through portable TMS devices, which aim to lower costs and enable home-based use, though high initial expenses remain a barrier in low-resource settings as of 2025.
