Extreme weather

 Extreme weather consists of rare meteorological phenomena that deviate markedly from typical conditions at a specific location and season, generally defined as events falling in the uppermost or lowermost decile of historical probability distributions for variables such as temperature, precipitation, or wind speed. These include heatwaves, cold snaps, heavy precipitation leading to floods, droughts, tornadoes, and tropical cyclones, each characterized by intensities that can overwhelm natural and built environments.
Such events arise from interactions among atmospheric dynamics, ocean currents, and land surface processes, with natural variabilitysuch as El Nio-Southern Oscillation cyclesplaying a primary role in their occurrence, alongside potential influences from elevated greenhouse gas concentrations that physical principles suggest could amplify certain extremes like heat but not uniformly others. Empirical observations reveal heterogeneous trends: frequencies of hot extremes have risen in many land areas since the mid-20th century, correlating with global mean temperature increases, while data for tropical cyclone intensity and drought extent show no clear upward trajectory globally when accounting for observational completeness and regional variability.
Extreme weather inflicts substantial human and economic tolls, with annual global fatalities numbering in the thousands and insured losses exceeding tens of billions of dollars, though normalized cost series adjusted for population growth, wealth accumulation, and exposure indicate that per capita impacts have not escalated proportionally with event counts. Controversies persist over attribution, as institutional analyses often emphasize anthropogenic drivers despite mixed empirical signals for non-thermal extremes, underscoring the need for rigorous statistical baselines over narrative-driven projections. Adaptation measures, including resilient infrastructure and early warning systems, mitigate risks more effectively than debated mitigation efforts whose causal links to event reduction remain unproven at scale.

Definition and Measurement

Defining Extreme Weather

Extreme weather consists of meteorological phenomena that are rare at a specific location and time of year, deviating significantly from established climatological norms and often exhibiting high intensity or duration. These events include, but are not limited to, heatwaves, cold outbreaks, intense precipitation, droughts, and severe storms, distinguished from routine variability by their position in the tails of historical probability distributions.
Definitions emphasize statistical rarity, typically quantified via return periods or percentile thresholds relative to a baseline climatology, such as 30-year normals. For instance, the World Meteorological Organization characterizes extremes as events with annual probabilities of 10% or less (e.g., 1-in-10-year occurrences), though rarer thresholds like 1% (1-in-100-year events) are used for high-impact analyses. Methodologies often employ relative thresholds, such as exceeding the 90th, 95th, or 99th percentile of daily maximum temperature or precipitation totals from historical records, allowing adaptation to local conditions; absolute thresholds, like daily rainfall over 50 mm or temperatures above fixed values (e.g., 35C), may supplement these for impact-oriented assessments.
This statistical framing underpins indices like the Warm Spell Duration Index for heat events (days above the 90th percentile) or Rx1day for precipitation (annual maximum daily amount), enabling consistent characterization across regions while accounting for non-stationarity in distributions due to factors like urbanization or long-term trends. Such approaches prioritize empirical distributions over subjective judgments, though baseline selection (e.g., 19611990) influences thresholds and requires transparency for comparability. Distinctions from "severe weather"which may include frequent hazards like thunderstorms without rarity criteriahighlight extreme weather's focus on outlier probabilities rather than mere intensity.

Indices and Statistical Thresholds

Extreme weather is quantified through indices and statistical thresholds that identify deviations from climatological norms, often focusing on the upper or lower tails of probability distributions. Relative thresholds, such as the 90th or 95th percentile of daily maximum temperature or precipitation totals derived from historical data, define events like hot days or heavy rainfall episodes. Absolute thresholds, for example, a daily maximum temperature exceeding 35C or minimum below -10C, are applied in contexts where fixed values indicate heightened risk regardless of local climate. These approaches allow for standardized comparisons across regions, though they may not capture non-stationary trends in a changing climate.
Return periods estimate the recurrence interval of events surpassing a specified magnitude, calculated as the inverse of the exceedance probability; a 1-in-100-year flood implies a 1% annual chance. Such metrics, rooted in extreme value theory, inform risk assessment but assume statistical stationarity, which empirical evidence challenges amid observed shifts in event frequencies. For instance, NOAA analyses use 95th percentile thresholds for extreme heat or cold verification, corresponding roughly to 1-in-20-year events under normal distributions.
Composite indices integrate multiple variables to reflect human or ecological impacts. The heat index combines air temperature and relative humidity to approximate the perceived temperature on human skin, with values above 40C signaling dangerous conditions per National Weather Service criteria. Wind chill, factoring temperature and wind speed, similarly gauges effective cold exposure, where equivalents below -27C prompt frostbite warnings. Standardized indices from the Expert Team on Climate Change Detection and Indices (ETCCDI), such as TX90p (warm days exceeding the 90th percentile) or RX5day (consecutive 5-day precipitation exceeding the 95th percentile), facilitate global monitoring of temperature and precipitation extremes.
While these tools enable rigorous analysis, definitions vary by application; statistically non-extreme events can yield disproportionate impacts due to societal vulnerability, as noted in IPCC assessments. Caution is advised in interpreting trends, as autocorrelation and model assumptions can inflate perceived changes in extremes.

Historical Perspective

Pre-Industrial and Early Modern Events

The Little Ice Age, a period of regional cooling from roughly 1300 to 1850, featured amplified variability in weather patterns across the Northern Hemisphere, including more frequent and intense cold snaps, storms, and hydrological extremes that strained pre-industrial societies reliant on subsistence agriculture. Proxy records such as tree rings, ice cores, and historical chronicles indicate cooler average temperatures, with phases of intensified cooling around 1300 and again from 1560 onward, leading to shorter growing seasons and increased vulnerability to crop failures. These conditions exacerbated events like river freeze-ups and storm surges, as documented in European annals, though quantitative data remains limited to qualitative accounts due to the absence of widespread instrumental measurements.
A prominent example of atmospheric extremes was the Great Storm of 1703, which battered southern England and Wales from November 26 to 27 (Julian calendar), generating winds equivalent to a Category 2 hurricane with gusts exceeding 95 mph (153 km/h). The tempest demolished over 2,000 chimney stacks and 4,000 homes in London alone, uprooted 4,000 oak trees in the New Forest, and sank or damaged hundreds of Royal Navy vessels anchored at the Downs, resulting in approximately 8,000 to 15,000 fatalities, the majority among sailors caught at sea. Contemporary eyewitness reports, including those from Daniel Defoe, described roofs torn off churches and steeples collapsing, underscoring the storm's unprecedented ferocity in British records.
Temperature extremes peaked during the Great Frost of 1709, the coldest European winter in at least 500 years, with mean temperatures plunging up to 7C below 20th-century norms across the continent from late 1708 into early 1709. Rivers like the Thames froze solid for weeks, enabling foot travel and markets on the ice, while vineyards in France burst and wine froze in bottles; the ensuing thaw triggered catastrophic flooding that destroyed bridges and mills. This event, linked to volcanic influences and atmospheric blocking, caused widespread livestock deaths, harvest shortfalls, and human mortality estimated in the tens of thousands, particularly in France and northern Italy, where famine followed due to frozen soils preventing spring planting.
Hydrological disruptions were also recurrent, as seen in 16th-century Transylvania, where archival letters detail sudden floods and erratic temperature swings during the Little Ice Age, leading to drowned villages, failed crops, and population displacements amid cooler, unstable conditions. Droughts periodically compounded these issues, such as the severe 1652 dry spell in Scotland that scorched pastures and reduced water sources, impacting livestock and human sustenance based on parish records. These pre-industrial events highlight how natural climatic oscillations, without modern infrastructure, amplified local devastation through direct causal chains from weather anomalies to societal strain.

Industrial Era Shifts and Records

Since the onset of the Industrial Revolution around 1760, instrumental records of weather extremes have become available primarily from the mid-19th century onward, with global coverage improving significantly after 1950. Global surface temperatures have risen by approximately 1.1C from 1850 to 2020, with the rate accelerating to about 0.2C per decade since 1980, leading to shifts in temperature extremes. The frequency of cold waves has declined since the early 1900s, while heat waves have increased since the mid-1960s, particularly in land regions. These changes align with overall warming, though data homogeneity issues, such as urban heat island effects and sparse early records, complicate direct attributions.
Records indicate that the hottest years on record have clustered in recent decades; for instance, 2024 marked the warmest year globally since records began in 1850, with temperatures 1.29C above the 20th-century average. In the contiguous United States, the number of days exceeding 90F has increased since 1960, while record highs are now more common than record lows by a ratio exceeding 2:1 in many locations. Globally, the percentage of weather stations reporting new daily high temperature records has risen, particularly since 2000, though regional variations persist, with some areas like the Arctic showing amplified extremes due to polar amplification. Cold extremes, conversely, have diminished; for example, the frequency of days below freezing has decreased in mid-latitudes. These trends are supported by reanalysis datasets like ERA5, but pre-1950 data exhibit greater uncertainty due to fewer observation sites.
Hydrological extremes show mixed signals. Heavy precipitation events, defined as the top percentile of daily rainfall, have increased in frequency and intensity over land areas since the mid-20th century, contributing to more intense regional flooding in parts of North America and Europe. Droughts, however, lack a clear global trend; while agricultural and hydrological droughts have intensified in regions like the Mediterranean and southern Africa, other areas such as the Sahel have seen reductions. Flood records tied to extreme precipitation have risen in some river basins, but confounding factors like land-use changes and dam construction obscure climatic signals.
Atmospheric storms exhibit no robust global increases. Tropical cyclone frequency has remained stable or slightly declined since reliable satellite monitoring began in the 1970s, with limited evidence for intensification beyond natural variability; major hurricane landfalls in the Atlantic show no long-term upward trend through 2020. Extratropical storms and tornadoes lack sufficient long-term global data for definitive shifts, though U.S. tornado counts have not increased when normalized for improved detection. Billion-dollar disaster costs have escalated since 1980, but this reflects population growth, economic development, and better reporting rather than inherent increases in event frequency or severity.

Types of Extreme Weather Events

Temperature Extremes

Temperature extremes refer to meteorological events characterized by air temperatures that deviate markedly from regional climatological norms, often persisting for multiple days and exceeding defined statistical thresholds such as the 95th percentile for maximum temperatures (heat) or 5th percentile for minimum temperatures (cold). These events occur across various spatial scales, from local to continental, and temporal durations, including daily maxima/minima or multi-week periods, with impacts on human health, agriculture, and infrastructure driven by physiological stress and energy demands.
The absolute extremes underscore the planet's thermal variability: the highest reliably recorded surface air temperature is 54.4C (129.9F) at Furnace Creek Ranch, Death Valley, California, USA, on 10 July 1913, verified by the World Meteorological Organization (WMO), while the lowest is -89.2C (-128.6F) at Vostok Station, Antarctica, on 21 July 1983. Recent decades have seen regional records challenged, such as 48.8C in Sicily, Italy, in August 2021, amid broader patterns of elevated global mean temperatures.
Observational data from 1950 onward reveal asymmetric trends: globally, the frequency, intensity, and duration of hot extremes have risen, with heat events increasingly dominating over cold ones, as average temperatures climb approximately 0.06C per decade since 1850. Cold extremes, measured by metrics like cold spell duration, have declined in magnitude and occurrence at rates exceeding those of heat increases in many datasets, though regional variability persistse.g., fewer prolonged cold waves in mid-latitudes but persistent polar lows. In North America, empirical analyses confirm reduced likelihood of extreme cold events recurring by 2100 under moderate emissions scenarios, based on reanalysis and model hindcasts.
These shifts align with thermodynamic principles where a warmer baseline expands the upper tail of the temperature distribution, amplifying hot outliers while compressing cold ones, though natural variability from oscillations like El Nio modulates event timing. Attribution studies link intensified heat extremes to anthropogenic forcing, but cold snaps remain capable of severe impacts, as seen in the 2021 Texas event driven by stratospheric polar vortex disruption. Data from sources like NOAA's global records emphasize empirical verification over modeled projections for historical trends.

Heat Waves

A heat wave consists of a prolonged period of abnormally hot weather, typically lasting at least two days, with temperatures exceeding regional historical averages. These events may occur with or without high humidity, though humidity exacerbates human discomfort and health risks by impairing sweat evaporation. Definitions vary by location; for instance, the U.S. National Weather Service often identifies heat waves as periods where daytime highs are at least 10F (5.6C) above normal for multiple consecutive days.
Meteorologically, heat waves arise from persistent high-pressure systems in the upper atmosphere that subside air masses, trapping heat near the surface and inhibiting cloud formation and precipitation. This subsidence warms air through compression (adiabatic heating), while stagnant weather patternssuch as blocked jet streamsprolong the event, preventing cooler air advection. Urban heat islands amplify local intensities, with concrete and asphalt retaining heat, raising nighttime temperatures and reducing recovery periods.
Notable historical heat waves include the 1936 North American event, which caused over 5,000 deaths in the U.S. amid Dust Bowl conditions, with temperatures reaching 121F (49.4C) in North Dakota. Europe's 2003 heat wave killed approximately 70,000 people, driven by anticyclonic blocking over the continent, with France recording highs up to 114F (45.6C). Russia's 2010 heat wave resulted in over 55,000 excess deaths and widespread wildfires, fueled by a similar high-pressure dome.
In the contiguous United States, heat wave frequency and duration increased from the 1960s to recent decades in many cities, with the season lengthening by about two weeks on average. However, the 1930s featured the most severe events by intensity and extent in longer records, with metrics like consecutive 100F days surpassing modern peaks in some regions. Globally, empirical analyses indicate heat wave risk has risen fivefold over the past century, particularly in drylands, though attribution to specific drivers like greenhouse gases remains model-dependent amid natural variability.
Heat waves pose significant health risks, primarily through heatstroke, dehydration, and cardiovascular strain, disproportionately affecting the elderly, infants, and those with pre-existing conditions; U.S. heat-related deaths average 1,000-2,000 annually but spike during events. Agriculturally, they reduce crop yields via physiological stress on plants, such as pollen sterility in grains and accelerated evapotranspiration, with corn and wheat losses exceeding 10% in severe cases. Infrastructure failures, like rail buckling and power grid overloads, compound economic damages, estimated at billions per major event in developed nations.

Cold Snaps and Waves

Cold snaps are brief, abrupt episodes of sharply lowered temperatures, typically lasting a few days and characterized by rapid cooling rates, often exceeding 1020C within 24 hours in affected regions. Cold waves, in contrast, denote prolonged periodsusually several days to weeksof sustained abnormally low temperatures, defined by agencies such as FEMA as a rapid temperature drop within 24 hours followed by extreme lows relative to local norms, posing risks to human health, infrastructure, and ecosystems. These events primarily arise from the advection of Arctic air masses southward, facilitated by disruptions in the polar vortex or blocking high-pressure systems that stall cold air incursions.
Historically, some of the most severe cold waves have occurred in North America, including the February 1899 outbreak, which established all-time low temperature records across dozens of U.S. cities, such as Atlanta's -9C and Grand Rapids' -24C, driven by an exceptional Arctic air plunge. The 1936 North American cold wave similarly set enduring records in the Midwest and Plains, with meteorological winter averages marking the coldest on record for states like Iowa and North Dakota, exacerbating the Dust Bowl era's hardships through frozen soils and livestock losses. In Europe and Asia, events like the 20092010 Eurasian cold wave caused thousands of deaths from hypothermia and strained energy grids, underscoring vulnerabilities in densely populated areas.
Observational data reveal a marked decline in the frequency, duration, and intensity of cold waves over the past several decades, particularly in the northern midlatitudes, where severity has steadily decreased since the mid-20th century. For instance, U.S. EPA analyses show fewer days with widespread low temperatures, with the 20th century's cold spikessuch as in the late 1970sbecoming rarer amid rising baseline temperatures. Recent extreme events, including the 2021 Texas cold snap (with temperatures dropping to -18C in Dallas, causing over 200 deaths and $195 billion in damages primarily from power failures) and similar outbreaks in western Europe (2018), China (2023), and Brazil (2024), have been attributed to natural variability like negative Pacific-North American patterns, but modeling indicates their recurrence probability has fallen to 1050% by 2100 under moderate emissions scenarios due to anthropogenic warming elevating mean temperatures. The IPCC assesses decreases in cold spell frequency and intensity as virtually certain over most land areas, driven by reduced meridional temperature gradients that weaken cold air outbreaks.
Impacts of cold snaps and waves include elevated mortality from exposureestimated at thousands annually in vulnerable populationsdisrupted transportation, frozen pipes leading to water shortages, and agricultural losses from frost damage, as seen in the 2021 U.S. event's $23 billion crop hit. While short-term snaps may intensify local whiplash effects in a warming world by contrasting with preceding mild spells, long-term trends point to milder overall cold extremes, challenging narratives that equate all weather volatility with increasing severity across event types. Projections under continued greenhouse gas emissions forecast further diminishment, with cold waves' median return periods lengthening substantially by mid-century.

Hydrological Extremes

Hydrological extremes involve significant disruptions in the water cycle, manifesting as floods from excessive precipitation or droughts characterized by prolonged deficits. These events arise from natural variability in atmospheric moisture transport and precipitation patterns, compounded by land surface conditions such as soil saturation and urbanization. Floods typically result from intense, short-duration rainfall overwhelming river channels or from prolonged rain saturating basins, while droughts stem from extended dry spells reducing soil moisture and streamflow. Empirical observations indicate that while regional variations exist, global trends in their frequency are not uniformly increasing, with confounding factors like land-use changes and data limitations complicating attribution.
Floods from heavy precipitation, including pluvial and riverine types, have caused substantial historical impacts. The Great Flood of 1889 in Johnstown, Pennsylvania, triggered by 6-8 inches of rain over 24 hours breaching the South Fork Dam, resulted in over 2,200 deaths and the destruction of entire communities. In the 20th century, U.S. floods averaged annual damages exceeding $6 billion, driven primarily by excessive rainfall rather than uniform trends in event frequency. Globally, paleoflood records reveal megafloods from glacial outbursts or monsoon intensifications, such as those in the Channeled Scablands from Pleistocene ice dam failures, with discharges up to 1 million cubic meters per second. Observed trends show increasing flashiness in U.S. streams under high-emissions scenarios, projected at 7.9% by century's end, but global river flood magnitudes exhibit no consistent upward trajectory, influenced more by antecedent soil moisture and infrastructure than precipitation alone.
Droughts, classified as meteorological (precipitation shortfall), agricultural (soil moisture deficit), or hydrological (streamflow reduction), have persisted across eras without a clear global escalation in frequency. The U.S. Dust Bowl of the 1930s, spanning 1930-1940 with the longest episode from July 1928 to May 1942, arose from persistent low rainfall and poor land management, displacing millions and eroding 100 million acres of topsoil. In global records, meteorological droughts show no substantial changes over the past 120 years, though regional agricultural droughts have intensified in areas like the Sahel due to land degradation. Recent analyses attribute some severity increases to higher evaporative demand from warming, amplifying impacts by 40% in dry regions, yet land-use and land-cover changes have independently heightened drought frequency over half of global land since pre-industrial times. Multiyear droughts, such as those in East Africa from 2011 or the Amazon in recent decades, underscore vulnerabilities in rain-fed agriculture, but empirical data emphasize natural oscillations like ENSO over monotonic anthropogenic trends.

Floods from Heavy Precipitation

Floods from heavy precipitation, often termed pluvial or flash floods, arise when rainfall intensity surpasses the infiltration capacity of soil and the conveyance limits of drainage systems, resulting in rapid surface runoff and inundation. These events differ from riverine floods by not requiring overflow from established watercourses; instead, water accumulates directly from precipitation, frequently in short bursts exceeding 2-6 inches per hour over small watersheds. Slow-moving thunderstorms, atmospheric rivers, or stalled weather fronts are primary meteorological drivers, with antecedent conditions like saturated soils or frozen ground exacerbating runoff. Urban areas amplify risks through impervious surfaces such as concrete, which reduce absorption and accelerate flow velocities up to 10 times faster than in natural landscapes.
Historical instances illustrate the destructive potential: the 1903 Heppner flood in Oregon, triggered by 1.5-2 inches of rain per hour over Willow Creek's headwaters, unleashed a 20-foot wall of water that killed 247 people and destroyed the town. Similarly, the 1927 Black Hills flood in South Dakota followed 15 inches of rain in under six hours, causing 238 fatalities and peak discharges estimated at 100,000 cubic feet per second on Rapid Creek. More recently, the July 2022 eastern Kentucky floods stemmed from 8-10 inches of rain in 24 hours across steep terrain, resulting in 45 deaths and over $1 billion in damages, as documented in NOAA's Storm Events Database. Such events underscore localized vulnerabilities, with fatalities often exceeding those from slower-onset floods due to minimal warning times of 30 minutes or less.
In the United States, the proportion of total annual precipitation from extreme single-day events (exceeding the 99th percentile) has risen from 9% in the 1960s to 12% in recent decades, per NOAA analyses of gauge data, correlating with increased flash flood reports. Globally, peer-reviewed assessments of streamflow records indicate no uniform increase in peak flood magnitudes over the 20th century, attributing variability more to land-use changes and natural oscillations than to atmospheric moisture trends alone. While billion-dollar flooding disasters, including those from heavy rain, numbered 45 from 1980-2024 per NOAA's normalized economic data, confounding factors like population growth in floodplains and infrastructure expansion explain much of the escalation in impacts, rather than isolated shifts in precipitation extremes.

Droughts and Water Scarcity

Droughts occur when precipitation falls significantly below normal levels over an extended period, typically a season or longer, resulting in water shortages that adversely impact ecosystems, agriculture, and human activities. They are classified into types including meteorological drought, defined by deficits in rainfall; agricultural drought, characterized by insufficient soil moisture affecting crops; and hydrological drought, marked by reduced streamflow, reservoir levels, and groundwater. Hydrological droughts often lag meteorological ones due to the time required for surface and subsurface water systems to respond to precipitation shortfalls.
Major historical droughts illustrate their recurrent nature and severe consequences. In the United States, the Dust Bowl era from 1930 to 1940 featured prolonged dry conditions across the Great Plains, exacerbating soil erosion and displacing over 2.5 million people. The 1950s drought, spanning July 1949 to September 1957, affected broad regions of the U.S., leading to widespread crop failures. Globally, the North China Drought of 18761878 contributed to famines killing millions, while the Ethiopian famine-linked drought from 1983 to 1985 resulted in approximately 400,000 deaths.
Natural variability drives most droughts through climatic oscillations such as El Nio-Southern Oscillation (ENSO), which can suppress rainfall in regions like the southwestern U.S. or southeastern Australia during positive phases. Volcanic eruptions and solar cycles also influence precipitation patterns, though their effects are episodic. Anthropogenic factors, including greenhouse gas emissions, have increased atmospheric evaporative demand via higher temperatures, intensifying drought impacts in some areas even without precipitation deficits; for instance, since 2000, heat rather than reduced rainfall has dominated western U.S. droughts. However, global meteorological drought frequency shows no clear long-term increase attributable solely to human influence, as natural decadal variations often rival forced changes on regional scales.
Water scarcity extends beyond drought, encompassing situations where demand exceeds available supply due to factors like population growth and inefficient management rather than absolute shortages. By 2022, population pressures had heightened water demand in arid regions, outpacing infrastructure development and leading to overuse of aquifers and rivers. Mismanagement, such as subsidized overuse in agriculturewhich consumes about 70% of global freshwaterwith minimal efficiency gains exacerbates scarcity, as seen in California's reservoirs dropping to historic lows during the 20122016 drought amid high consumption. Urbanization and poor governance further strain supplies, with over 2 billion people lacking safe drinking water as of 2020, often due to allocation failures rather than climate alone. Effective scarcity mitigation requires demand-side reforms, as supply augmentation via desalination remains costly and energy-intensive.

Atmospheric Storms

Atmospheric storms refer to intense disturbances in the Earth's atmosphere featuring strong winds, heavy rainfall, and often convective activity, including tropical cyclones, extratropical cyclones, tornadoes, and hailstorms. These events derive energy primarily from atmospheric instability, temperature gradients, or latent heat release, leading to destructive impacts such as wind damage, flooding, and structural failure. Globally, they contribute significantly to weather-related fatalities and economic losses, with the United States experiencing over 1,000 tornadoes annually on average since 1950.

Tropical Cyclones

Tropical cyclones, known as hurricanes in the Atlantic and typhoons in the northwest Pacific, form over warm ocean waters above 26.5C (80F) with low wind shear, sustaining winds exceeding 119 km/h (74 mph) for hurricanes. They consist of a low-pressure center surrounded by thunderstorms spiraling inward, capable of rapid intensification; for instance, Hurricane Wilma in 2005 reached Category 5 status with winds of 295 km/h (183 mph) within 24 hours. Historical records show the deadliest Atlantic hurricane was the Galveston storm of 1900, killing between 6,000 and 12,000 people.
Observational data indicate no significant long-term trends in global tropical cyclone frequency, with basin-specific variations; the Atlantic averaged 14 named storms per season from 19912020. Accumulated cyclone energy (ACE), a measure of intensity and duration, has shown an upward trend in the Atlantic over the past 30 years, with eight of the ten highest ACE years since 1950 occurring after 1995, potentially linked to warmer sea surface temperatures. However, century-scale analyses reveal no robust evidence of anthropogenic influence on frequency or overall activity, though rapid intensification events have increased.

Extratropical Storms, Tornadoes, and Hail

Extratropical cyclones, prevalent in mid-latitudes, arise from baroclinic instability along frontal boundaries, producing gale-force winds and precipitation across large areas; trends show a decrease in mid-latitude frequency but increases in high latitudes since the mid-20th century. These storms can undergo extratropical transition from decaying tropical cyclones, enhancing their destructive potential through increased wind speeds and storm size, with projections indicating rising destructiveness under warming scenarios.
Tornadoes, violently rotating columns of air extending from thunderstorms, predominantly occur in the U.S. Great Plains, with an annual average of about 1,200 reports since 2010, though strong (EF3+) tornadoes exhibit no clear upward trend over decades despite improved detection. The 2025 U.S. tornado season through May recorded 1,040 events, 172% above average, driven by favorable atmospheric conditions rather than long-term shifts.
Hailstorms, involving supercooled water droplets freezing into ice pellets within strong updrafts, have shown increasing severity in some regions, with larger hailstones more frequent amid climate change due to heightened atmospheric moisture and instability; U.S. hail damages in 2024 exceeded those from hurricanes and floods combined. European projections suggest a 3040% rise in severe hailstorms by century's end even in moderate warming scenarios, though global frequency trends remain uncertain.

Tropical Cyclones

Tropical cyclones are large-scale atmospheric disturbances characterized by a low-pressure center, strong winds exceeding 17 meters per second (39 miles per hour), and organized convection, forming over warm tropical or subtropical ocean waters. They require sea surface temperatures of at least 26.5C (79.7F) to depth of about 50 meters, low vertical wind shear, sufficient moisture, and Coriolis force away from the equator for development. Globally, 80 to 100 such systems occur annually, distributed across the Atlantic, eastern and western Pacific, and Indian Ocean basins, with about half intensifying into hurricanes or typhoons.
These storms derive energy from latent heat release in rising moist air, leading to spiral bands of thunderstorms, heavy rainfall, and potential for rapid intensification. Intensity is classified using scales like the Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale in the Atlantic, categorizing storms from 1 to 5 based on maximum sustained wind speeds, with Category 3 or higher denoting major hurricanes capable of winds over 50 m/s (111 mph). Accumulated Cyclone Energy (ACE), a measure combining frequency, duration, and intensity, quantifies overall activity; global ACE has shown no upward trend since reliable satellite records began in the 1970s, with a decline noted from 1990 to 2021. 
In the North Atlantic, historical data from 1851 indicate an average of 12 named storms, 6 hurricanes, and 3 major hurricanes per year, with no long-term increase in major hurricane frequency after accounting for observational improvements. Periods of elevated activity, such as 19952020, correlate with natural oscillations like the Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation rather than monotonic trends. Globally, peer-reviewed analyses confirm decreasing trends in both hurricane counts and ACE over recent decades, contrasting with projections of potential intensity increases under warming scenarios.

Extratropical Storms, Tornadoes, and Hail

Extratropical storms, also known as mid-latitude cyclones, form along frontal boundaries in the temperate zones and are characterized by large-scale low-pressure systems that generate strong winds, heavy precipitation, and severe weather including blizzards and flooding. These storms typically span hundreds to thousands of kilometers and can persist for days, impacting vast regions across North America, Europe, and the North Pacific. Notable examples include nor'easters along the U.S. East Coast, such as the 1993 Storm of the Century, which produced record snowfall exceeding 50 inches in parts of the Appalachians and caused over $5 billion in damages.
Observational data indicate mixed trends in extratropical cyclone frequency and intensity. In the Northern Hemisphere, some studies report no significant change in overall cyclone counts from 1948 to recent decades, while others note increases in the intensity of the strongest storms, such as nor'easters with maximum wind speeds showing a significant upward trend since the mid-20th century. Regional variations persist, with potential poleward shifts in storm tracks and enhanced precipitation due to greater atmospheric moisture, though attribution to anthropogenic factors remains uncertain amid natural variability like the North Atlantic Oscillation.
Tornadoes are rapidly rotating columns of air extending from thunderstorm cumulonimbus clouds to the ground, most frequently occurring in the central United States within the "Tornado Alley" region. The U.S. experiences over 1,200 tornadoes annually, with intensities rated on the Enhanced Fujita (EF) scale from EF0 (winds 65-85 mph) to EF5 (over 200 mph). Historical records from NOAA show annual counts fluctuating between 800 and 1,800 since 1950, with no long-term national increase in frequency; instead, rises in weaker (EF0-EF1) reports likely stem from improved detection via radar and population growth in vulnerable areas. 
Strong and violent tornadoes (EF3 and above) have shown a declining trend over decades, with the U.S. recording record lows in recent years, such as only two EF4+ events in 2023. Spatial analyses reveal increasing trends in tornado days in parts of the Southeast and Midwest, but decreases elsewhere, underscoring regional variability driven by shear and instability patterns rather than a uniform global warming signal. 
Hail consists of solid ice particles formed in strong thunderstorm updrafts, where supercooled water freezes around nuclei and grows through accretion. Hailstorms are most common in continental interiors during spring and summer, producing damage from crop losses to structural impacts; for instance, U.S. insured hail losses exceeded $10 billion in 2023 alone. Trends in hail frequency show regional differences: increases in Europe and Australia linked to convective vigor, but decreases projected in East Asia and North America under warmer conditions that may suppress hail formation aloft. In the U.S., larger hail (2+ inches) occurred in 12% of storms in 2024, up from prior years, potentially tied to stronger updrafts, though long-term data emphasize natural convective cycles over monotonic trends. 

Natural Drivers

Climatic Oscillations and Cycles

Climatic oscillations encompass quasi-periodic fluctuations in ocean-atmosphere interactions that drive variability in extreme weather events over interannual to multidecadal timescales, independent of long-term trends. These modes, such as the El Nio-Southern Oscillation (ENSO), Pacific Decadal Oscillation (PDO), and Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation (AMO), redistribute heat and moisture globally, altering storm tracks, precipitation patterns, and temperature anomalies that manifest as intensified heat waves, droughts, floods, and tropical cyclone activity. Empirical analyses of instrumental records since the late 19th century reveal that positive or negative phases of these oscillations correlate with clustered occurrences of extremes, often overriding regional mean climate states for years or decades. For instance, ENSO's 27 year cycle accounts for up to 3050% of variance in seasonal precipitation extremes in tropical and subtropical regions, as quantified through global risk pattern assessments using reanalysis data.
ENSO, characterized by anomalies in equatorial Pacific sea surface temperatures, exemplifies how oscillatory phases trigger opposing extreme outcomes. During El Nio (warm phase), enhanced convection shifts toward the central Pacific, weakening trade winds and increasing flood risks in southeastern South America and southern United States while exacerbating droughts in Indonesia, Australia, and northeastern Brazil; conversely, La Nia (cool phase) intensifies easterlies, promoting drier conditions in the southern U.S. and wetter anomalies in the Pacific Northwest. Observational data from 19502020 show El Nio events associated with 2040% higher probabilities of extreme rainfall in Peru and elevated wildfire risks in Southeast Asia due to reduced moisture, while La Nia correlates with intensified cold snaps and blizzards in North America via strengthened polar vortex influences. These teleconnections arise from Rossby wave propagation, linking tropical sea surface temperature gradients to extratropical circulation, as evidenced by composite analyses of 50+ events.
The PDO and AMO operate on longer scales, modulating basin-wide extremes through persistent sea surface temperature patterns. The PDO, with 2030 year phases, influences North Pacific storminess and U.S. drought propensity; negative phases enhance precipitation variability, contributing to floods in the Pacific Northwest, while positive phases align with arid conditions in the southwestern U.S. Similarly, the AMO's 6080 year cycle drives Atlantic sector variability: positive (warm) phases elevate sea surface temperatures by 0.51C, fostering vertical wind shear reductions that boost major hurricane formation by 50100% in the North Atlantic, as seen in elevated activity from 19952020 compared to the prior cool phase. Positive AMO also correlates with 2030% higher drought frequencies across the central U.S. via altered moisture convergence, with severe episodes peaking when coinciding with negative PDO, explaining multidecadal clusters like the 1930s Dust Bowl and 1950s droughts.
Interactions among these oscillations amplify regional extremes beyond individual effects. For example, concurrent positive AMO and negative PDO phases since the 1990s have sustained elevated drought risks in the U.S. Southwest and Texas, with soil moisture deficits persisting 2050% longer than under neutral conditions, as reconstructed from tree-ring and instrumental proxies spanning 18502000. These natural drivers underscore that much observed variability in extreme event frequency stems from phase alignments rather than unidirectional trends, with paleoclimate records indicating recurrent high-impact episodes every few decades to centuries. Attribution studies confirm these modes explain significant portions of event anomalies, such as 40% of North American precipitation extremes tied to PDO-ENSO covariance in 20th-century data.

Solar, Volcanic, and Geological Influences

Solar activity modulates Earth's climate primarily through fluctuations in total solar irradiance (TSI), which varies by about 0.1% over the 11-year sunspot cycle, equivalent to roughly 1 W/m change and a global temperature response of approximately 0.1C. These variations influence atmospheric dynamics, particularly via ultraviolet radiation changes that alter stratospheric ozone concentrations and, in turn, planetary wave patterns, potentially contributing to the positioning of jet streams and the frequency of blocking highs associated with regional extremes like heat waves or cold outbreaks. Periods of reduced solar activity, such as grand minima (e.g., the Maunder Minimum from 16451715), have correlated with cooler Northern Hemisphere temperatures and increased winter storminess, though the causal strength is debated and estimated to account for less than 0.1C of multidecadal warming in recent centuries.
Explosive volcanic eruptions inject sulfur dioxide into the stratosphere, where it forms reflective sulfate aerosols that reduce incoming solar radiation, causing temporary global cooling of 0.10.5C lasting 13 years depending on eruption scale. The 1991 Mount Pinatubo eruption, for example, released about 20 million metric tons of SO, lowering global temperatures by 0.5C, suppressing Indian summer monsoon rainfall by up to 20% through altered atmospheric circulation, and increasing drought risk in affected regions. Similarly, the 1815 Tambora eruption triggered the 1816 "Year Without a Summer," with Northern Hemisphere summer temperatures dropping 0.40.7C, leading to unseasonal frosts, heavy rains, and floods in North America and Europe that exacerbated crop failures. Post-eruption precipitation patterns often shift, with studies showing reduced global land rainfall, especially in monsoon zones, due to stabilized atmospheric layers inhibiting convection. While volcanic CO emissions contribute to long-term greenhouse forcing, their annual output (around 0.3 gigatons) is dwarfed by anthropogenic sources and insufficient to drive warming extremes on short timescales.
Geological processes, including tectonic activity and associated seismicity, exert primarily indirect and long-term influences on climate and weather extremes through alterations in topography, ocean basin configurations, and silicate weathering rates that regulate atmospheric CO over millions of years. On shorter scales relevant to extreme events, large earthquakes can mobilize dust or aerosols into the atmosphere, potentially perturbing local cloud formation or radiation balance, but such effects are transient, localized, and negligible compared to meteorological drivers, with no robust evidence linking them to widespread storm intensification or temperature anomalies. Volcanic arcs tied to subduction zones amplify eruption risks in tectonically active regions, indirectly heightening susceptibility to aerosol-induced weather disruptions, though these are captured under volcanic forcing. Overall, contemporary extreme weather attribution rarely invokes geological mechanisms, as their causal role diminishes beyond paleoclimate reconstructions.

Anthropogenic Factors

Greenhouse Gas Contributions

Anthropogenic emissions of greenhouse gases, primarily carbon dioxide (CO) from fossil fuel combustion and methane (CH) from agriculture and energy production, have elevated atmospheric CO concentrations to approximately 425 ppm as of October 2025, compared to pre-industrial levels around 280 ppm. This accumulation has produced an effective radiative forcing (ERF) of about 2.16 W/m from well-mixed greenhouse gases since 1750, with the Annual Greenhouse Gas Index indicating a 51% increase in total forcing relative to 1990 levels as of 2023. Such forcing contributes to global mean surface temperature rise, estimated at 1.11.2C above pre-industrial baselines, which in turn modulates the tails of weather distributions, increasing the likelihood of certain extremes through thermodynamic and dynamic mechanisms.
For heatwaves, empirical analyses attribute roughly half of the observed intensification since pre-industrial times to emissions from major carbon producers, with global warming shifting temperature distributions such that events exceeding historical norms become more frequent. Peer-reviewed studies confirm detectable human influence on heatwave magnitude and duration, particularly in mid-latitudes, where anthropogenic forcing has amplified extremes by 0.51C in attribution frameworks. 
In heavy precipitation events, greenhouse gas-induced warming enhances atmospheric moisture-holding capacity by approximately 7% per degree Celsius via the Clausius-Clapeyron relation, enabling more intense rainfall during storms. Observations show a detectable shift toward heavier precipitation globally, with medium confidence in human-induced contributions to increased event frequency since the mid-20th century, though regional variability persists due to natural oscillations. 
Contributions to tropical cyclone intensity remain less conclusive; while rainfall rates within storms are projected to rise with warming, global trends in overall frequency or peak wind speeds show no robust increase attributable solely to greenhouse gases, with some metrics indicating a modest uptick in major cyclone proportions (510% per decade since 1979) amid observational uncertainties. For droughts, anthropogenic warming exacerbates evaporative demand, reducing soil moisture and prolonging events in arid regions like the western United States, where high evaporative demand has overtaken precipitation deficits as the dominant driver since 2000, increasing global drought severity by an average 40% since 1981. However, greenhouse gas effects on drought can be regionally offsetting, such as alleviating conditions in parts of Central Asia through altered circulation patterns.
! Attribution efforts, often reliant on climate models, highlight greenhouse gases as a primary driver of thermodynamic changes but underscore limitations in capturing dynamic feedbacks, with natural variability confounding signals in shorter records. Empirical detection requires multi-decadal data, and while peer-reviewed consensus supports enhanced extremes in heat and precipitation, claims of uniform intensification across all event types warrant caution given heterogeneous trends. 

Land Use, Urbanization, and Development Effects

Urban development contributes to the urban heat island (UHI) effect, where built environments absorb and retain more solar radiation than natural landscapes, elevating local temperatures and amplifying extreme heat events. Impervious surfaces such as asphalt and concrete, combined with reduced vegetation and anthropogenic heat from buildings and vehicles, can increase urban temperatures by 1.8 to 5.4F (1 to 3C) relative to surrounding rural areas, thereby intensifying the duration and intensity of heat waves. Sprawling urban forms exacerbate this, with studies showing such cities experience over twice the number of very hot summer days compared to compact urban designs, based on analysis of U.S. metropolitan areas from 1960 to 2003.
Urbanization heightens flood risks primarily through the expansion of impervious surfaces, which impede rainfall infiltration and accelerate surface runoff, resulting in higher peak streamflows and more frequent flooding. In the United States, urban development has been documented to increase flood magnitudes by reducing hydrologic response times and altering drainage patterns, with effects observed in streamflow data from urbanizing watersheds. For example, redevelopment in urban areas can magnify flood peaks beyond design capacities by elevating effective imperviousness, as evidenced in hydrological modeling of Chinese cities where impervious cover rose from 20% to 40% between 2000 and 2015. Coastal urbanization compounds this by channeling more runoff into tidal zones, increasing nuisance flooding frequency; in U.S. East Coast cities, a 10% rise in impervious area correlates with 20-50% higher tidal flood days annually.
Land use changes, including deforestation and agricultural conversion, modify local moisture cycles and temperature regimes, influencing the occurrence of precipitation extremes and droughts. Deforestation in tropical regions reduces evapotranspiration, leading to drier local conditions and annual temperature increases of about 1C (1.8F), which heightens heat stress during extremes and can suppress convective rainfall. In contrast, certain urban land perturbations enhance convective activity, boosting mean and heavy rainfall over affected domains by 10-20% in simulations of European cities during heatwaves. These localized alterations, while secondary to broader atmospheric drivers, demonstrate how development disrupts surface-atmosphere feedbacks, with peer-reviewed syntheses indicating variable impacts on extremes depending on vegetation loss scale and regional hydrology. Empirical data from satellite observations confirm that cleared lands often exhibit altered cloudiness and rainfall patterns compared to intact forests, underscoring the causal role of vegetation cover in modulating local extremes.

Attribution Science and Debates

Methodologies for Event Attribution

Event attribution science seeks to quantify the influence of anthropogenic factors, particularly greenhouse gas emissions, on the probability or intensity of specific extreme weather events by comparing simulated climates with and without human-induced forcings. The primary methodology is probabilistic event attribution (PEA), which employs large ensembles of climate model simulations to estimate changes in event likelihood. In PEA, researchers define the event using observational data, then run models under factual conditions (including observed greenhouse gas levels) and counterfactual scenarios (pre-industrial or natural forcings only) to compute metrics such as the risk ratiothe ratio of event probability in the factual versus counterfactual worldor the fraction of attributable risk (FAR), which indicates the proportion of the event's occurrence attributable to human influence. For instance, if the risk ratio exceeds 1, climate change increases the event's odds; FAR values near 1 suggest near-certain attribution to anthropogenic causes, though values below 0.5 often preclude robust claims due to uncertainties.
Key steps in PEA include precise event definition to isolate meteorological features, evaluation of model performance against historical data to minimize biases, and generation of thousands of simulations to sample internal variability. Climate models, often from ensembles like CMIP6, simulate atmospheric dynamics, but resolutions limit accuracy for sub-grid phenomena like convection-driven extremes. Process-based attribution complements PEA by dissecting physical pathways, such as enhanced atmospheric moisture from warming leading to heavier precipitation, using targeted diagnostics rather than full ensembles. The storyline approach, an emerging method, constructs narratives of event evolution under altered climates, emphasizing causal chains over statistical probabilities to highlight vulnerabilities in specific cases like heatwaves.
Despite advances, methodologies face significant limitations, including model biases that underestimate extremes, such as precipitation tails, rendering some simulations unfit for attribution of rare events. Natural variability can mask anthropogenic signals, particularly for short-duration or regionally confined events like tornadoes, where attribution remains infeasible due to inadequate model resolution and observational gaps. Rapid attribution efforts, aiming for results within weeks, rely on pre-computed ensembles but sacrifice depth for speed, increasing uncertainty; real-time claims post-2010s have grown, yet many studies caution against overinterpreting single-event results as definitive causation, as PEA quantifies altered odds, not deterministic origins. Critiques highlight that selective event framing and model selection can bias outcomes, with peer-reviewed assessments noting that while large-scale trends (e.g., heat extremes) show robust signals, attribution for compound or cold events often yields inconclusive results due to forcing uncertainties. Overall, these methods inform policy by linking emissions to risk escalation but require transparent uncertainty quantification to avoid conflating probabilistic shifts with direct blame.

Evidence on Trends in Frequency and Intensity

Empirical data indicate that trends in the frequency and intensity of extreme weather events vary significantly by event type and geographic region, with some categories showing increases attributable to observed global warming, while others exhibit stability or declines when normalized for detection biases and exposure changes. For instance, cold extremes have decreased in frequency globally since the mid-20th century, consistent with rising average temperatures. In contrast, heat-related extremes have increased, though attribution to anthropogenic factors requires accounting for urban heat islands and land-use changes that amplify local measurements.
!
Heatwaves, defined as prolonged periods of anomalously high temperatures, have risen in frequency and duration across much of the globe. In the United States, the average annual number of heatwavesperiods of two or more consecutive days with temperatures exceeding the 95th percentilegrew from two per year in the 1960s to six per year in the 2010s and 2020s, with similar patterns in Europe and Asia. Globally, the IPCC assesses high confidence in the human influence on observed increases in hot extremes since 1950, particularly in mid-latitudes, though pre-1950 records show comparable events like the 1930s Dust Bowl heatwaves in the U.S.. Intensity metrics, such as days above historical thresholds, have also escalated, with 2023 marking a record for global hottest days.
Heavy precipitation events, often linked to flooding, have increased in intensity in many regions. In the contiguous U.S., the portion of precipitation from events exceeding the 99th percentile rose by 4-10% since 1950, with nine of the ten wettest years occurring after 1995. Globally, the IPCC reports medium confidence in upward trends for extreme daily precipitation over land areas with sufficient data, particularly in Asia and North America, though total annual precipitation trends are modest at +0.03 inches per decade. These shifts align with thermodynamic expectations from warmer atmospheres holding more moisture, but regional decreases occur in subtropical dry zones.
Drought trends are heterogeneous, with no robust global increase in frequency or severity when using precipitation-based indices like the Standardized Precipitation Index (SPI). Agricultural and hydrological droughts have varied regionally: expansions in the Mediterranean, southern Africa, and parts of Australia since the 1970s, but reductions in central North America and eastern Asia. The Palmer Drought Severity Index shows U.S. averages fluctuating without a long-term upward trajectory, influenced more by evapotranspiration rises than precipitation deficits. Recent snapshots, such as 48% of global land under extreme drought in at least one 2023 month, reflect episodic severity rather than a monotonic trend.
Tropical cyclone frequency has remained stable globally since reliable records began around 1970, with 80-90 systems annually and no significant upward or downward trend. Intensity metrics are mixed: the Accumulated Cyclone Energy (ACE) index shows a modest rise in the North Atlantic since 1990, driven by more Category 4-5 storms, but global power dissipation has not increased uniformly, with recent decades exhibiting lower activity in the Pacific. The IPCC assigns low confidence to long-term trends in TC frequency but medium confidence in a higher proportion reaching major hurricane status.
For extratropical storms, tornadoes, and hail, U.S. datamost comprehensive globallyreveal no increase in normalized frequency of strong (EF3+) tornadoes since 1950, despite raw reports rising from better radar and reporting (e.g., from ~900/year in the 1990s to over 1,200 in recent years). Regional shifts include declines in the Great Plains and upticks in the Southeast, tied to atmospheric patterns rather than monotonic intensification. Hail events show similar stability when adjusted for exposure.
U.S. billion-dollar disasters have climbed to 28 events in 2023, totaling 403 since 1980, but this reflects greater population vulnerability, asset values, and event categorization more than inherent geophysical trends. NOAA attributes part of the cost escalation to societal factors, cautioning against conflating economic losses with climatic shifts. Overall, while warming drives detectable changes in thermodynamic extremes like heat and rain, dynamical events like storms lack clear intensification signals beyond natural variability.

Case Studies of Attributed vs. Unattributed Events

The 2021 Pacific Northwest heat dome, occurring from June 25 to July 1, exemplifies an extreme weather event where attribution studies have quantified a substantial human influence. Temperatures reached record highs, including 49.6C (121.1F) in Lytton, British Columbia, and 46.7C (116.2F) in Portland, Oregon, leading to over 1,400 excess deaths across the region. Multiple rapid attribution analyses, using climate models and statistical methods, concluded that anthropogenic warming made the event at least 150 times more likely and intensified its magnitude by about 2C. These findings rely on comparing observed data against counterfactual scenarios without greenhouse gas forcing, though critics note potential model biases in simulating regional blocking highs and soil moisture feedbacks that amplified the event independently.
In contrast, U.S. tornado activity provides cases where empirical trends show no detectable anthropogenic signal amid natural variability. The 2011 Super Outbreak, from April 2528, produced 360 tornadoes, including four EF5s, causing 324 fatalities and $11 billion in damages, yet long-term records indicate no upward trend in tornado frequency or intensity linked to climate change. NOAA analyses of data since 1950 reveal stable annual counts of F/EF15 tornadoes, with a decline in days featuring multiple strong tornadoes and no evidence of greenhouse gas-driven increases. Spatial variations exist, such as slight upticks in weaker tornado reports in the Southeast due to improved detection, but stronger (EF3+) events show no century-scale rise, attributable instead to oscillatory patterns like El Nio-Southern Oscillation.
Atlantic hurricane trends further illustrate unattributed extremes, as observed records lack robust evidence of human-induced intensification. Category 45 hurricanes made 18 U.S. landfalls from 19802023, with no statistically significant increase over historical baselines; for instance, the 2005 season's 28 named storms, including Katrina, aligns with multidecadal cycles rather than a warming fingerprint. NOAA and IPCC assessments confirm no detected century-scale uptick in global tropical cyclone frequency or major hurricane counts, despite projections of potential future intensity rises from warmer seas; current data emphasize natural modes like the Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation. Attribution efforts for specific storms, such as increased rainfall in Harvey (2017), detect thermodynamic enhancements but fail to link overall genesis or rapid intensification to anthropogenic forcing amid data limitations pre-satellite era.
These cases highlight attribution science's asymmetries: heat events often yield high-confidence human signals due to thermodynamic scaling, while dynamic phenomena like tornadoes and cyclones resist detection owing to sparse data, model uncertainties, and dominant internal variability. Peer-reviewed syntheses underscore that while greenhouse gases thermodynamically favor extremes, causal chains for individual events require disentangling from natural drivers, with over-attribution risks in media narratives exceeding evidential support.

Societal Impacts

Human Health and Mortality

Extreme weather events contribute to human mortality primarily through temperature extremes, with cold-related deaths vastly outnumbering heat-related ones globally, at approximately 4.6 million versus 489,000 annually based on data from 20002019. Overall, non-optimal temperatures account for about 5 million premature deaths per year, representing 9.4% of total global mortality, though cold dominates due to moderate rather than extreme conditions. Despite claims of increasing risks from warming, weather-related death rates have declined significantly over decades when adjusted for population growth, attributable to improved forecasting, infrastructure, and adaptation measures rather than reduced event frequency.
Heatwaves pose acute risks via heatstroke, cardiovascular strain, and exacerbation of chronic conditions, particularly among the elderly and vulnerable populations. Between 2000 and 2019, an estimated 489,000 heat-related deaths occurred annually worldwide, with 45% in Asia and 36% in Europe, and mortality for those over 65 rising 85% from 20002004 to 20172021. The 2003 European heatwave exemplifies high-impact events, causing over 70,000 excess deaths across the continent due to unpreparedness and factors like urban heat islands and air conditioning scarcity at the time. More recent events, such as the 2023 global heatwave, were linked to 178,000 excess deaths, or 23 per million, concentrated in southern and eastern Europe, though per capita rates remain low in regions with robust cooling infrastructure.
In contrast, cold extremes and moderate cold periods drive the majority of temperature-related mortality, with ratios of cold to heat deaths around 9:1 or higher in various studies. Projections under moderate warming scenarios suggest potential net reductions in total temperature-related deaths if cold mortality decreases outweigh heat increases, though this depends on adaptation; without it, heat gains could dominate in warmer regions. In the United States, for instance, cold-related winter death rates exceed heat by factors of 812% seasonally, underscoring that warming may mitigate some cold risks while amplifying heat ones unevenly.
Storms, floods, and droughts contribute fewer direct deaths compared to temperature extremes but inflict trauma, drowning, and indirect effects like disease outbreaks. Globally, weather disasters kill 40,00050,000 annually on average, with storms causing 72% of such losses over the past 50 years, yet improved early warning systems have reduced fatalities despite rising event frequency. In vulnerable regions, death rates from extreme weather are 15 times higher than in resilient ones, highlighting socioeconomic factors over climatic trends alone. U.S. data from 19802024 show tropical cyclones leading in fatalities among billion-dollar events at 7,211 deaths, followed by drought/heatwaves at 4,658, but overall per capita risks have not escalated proportionally with event costs.

Adaptation and Resilience

Forecasting Advances and Warning Systems

Advances in numerical weather prediction (NWP) models have enhanced the resolution and accuracy of extreme weather forecasts. The European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) implemented its Artificial Intelligence Forecasting System (AIFS) operationally in May 2025, marking the first fully AI-based ensemble model, which improves deterministic forecasts for tropical cyclone tracks, particularly beyond day 3. Similarly, NOAA's Hurricane Analysis and Forecast System (HAFS), based on the FV3 dynamical core, achieved outstanding performance in track and intensity predictions during the 2024 Atlantic hurricane season, enabling accurate anticipation of rapid intensification events.
Machine learning (ML) integration has accelerated forecasting efficiency and skill for rare extremes. ML-based weather prediction models, such as those reviewed in probabilistic frameworks, demonstrate greater accuracy and lower computational demands than traditional NWP for medium-range forecasts, including extremes like heatwaves and floods. NOAA's integration of data-driven ML models into operational systems has shown enhanced predictive skill for life-threatening events, including hurricanes and winter storms, by processing vast observational datasets more effectively. In June 2025, NSF-supported NCAR initiated experimental global forecasts at 3-kilometer resolution, providing unprecedented detail for sub-seasonal extreme event tracking.
Warning systems have benefited from these modeling gains, extending lead times and reducing false alarms. NOAA's Warn-on-Forecast System, developed at the National Severe Storms Laboratory, supports probabilistic severe weather warnings up to hours in advance, yielding 15-20% improvements in intensity forecasts for thunderstorms and tornadoes. Early warning systems globally, when delivering 24-hour notices for hazards like floods and storms, can mitigate up to 30% of potential damage, as endorsed by the World Meteorological Organization in 2025. AI-assisted impact predictions, such as for droughts and heavy rainfall, further refine these systems by simulating cascading effects, with operational adoption accelerating post-2020 through expanded radar and satellite data. Observations from ESA's Arctic Weather satellite, integrated into ECMWF models by October 2025, have robustly improved polar extreme forecasts, addressing previous data gaps in high-latitude events.

Engineering, Policy, and Behavioral Responses

Engineering responses to extreme weather emphasize fortifying infrastructure against floods, storms, and heat through measures such as coastal defenses, elevated buildings, and improved drainage systems, which have demonstrated potential to reduce damages in vulnerability assessments. For instance, post-Hurricane Katrina reconstructions in the United States incorporated stronger levees and flood barriers by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, though empirical evaluations indicate variable long-term effectiveness due to ongoing subsidence and storm intensification. In the Netherlands, the Delta Works program, initiated after the 1953 North Sea flood that killed over 1,800 people, features storm surge barriers and dikes designed to withstand 1-in-10,000-year events, contributing to zero flood-related fatalities from such events since completion in 1997. Nature-based solutions, like mangrove restoration for coastal protection, have shown cost-benefit ratios exceeding 5:1 in reducing erosion and wave impacts in tropical regions.
Policy frameworks for resilience include federal and state initiatives that integrate climate risk into planning, such as the U.S. Department of State's 2024-2027 Climate Adaptation Plan, which mandates incorporating extreme weather considerations into foreign policy and infrastructure investments. At least twelve U.S. states have appointed Chief Resilience Officers by 2025 to coordinate adaptation efforts, focusing on wildfires, heat, and flooding through updated building codes and land-use regulations. However, empirical studies reveal that extreme weather events do not consistently trigger substantive policy reforms, with many local governments exhibiting limited adaptation due to institutional inertia and short-term fiscal priorities. The European Union's Floods Directive, implemented in 2007, requires member states to map risks and develop management plans, resulting in reduced flood exposures in compliant regions like Germany, where investments averted an estimated 1.5 billion in damages during the 2021 floods.
Behavioral adaptations by individuals and communities often involve immediate actions like evacuation during hurricanes or using air conditioning during heatwaves, but studies indicate these responses can be maladaptive, such as underestimating risks leading to delayed evacuations that increased fatalities in events like Hurricane Maria in 2017. In heat-prone areas, common adjustments include increased hydration and lighter clothing, though access disparities exacerbate vulnerabilities, with low-income households showing 20-30% lower adoption rates of cooling measures. Community-level responses, such as voluntary home elevations in flood zones, have mitigated losses by up to 70% in U.S. coastal areas post-2005, per insurance data, yet overall behavioral shifts remain inconsistent, with extreme weather sometimes inhibiting pro-environmental actions due to psychological fatigue. Impact-based warnings, which specify localized threats rather than general alerts, have improved compliance rates by 15-25% in European trials, enhancing evacuation efficacy during storms.

Controversies in Interpretation

Natural Variability vs. Human Signal Disputes

!
The core dispute in interpreting extreme weather trends revolves around apportioning observed events between natural climate variabilityencompassing phenomena like the El Nio-Southern Oscillation (ENSO), Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation (AMO), and Pacific Decadal Oscillation (PDO)and an anthropogenic signal from greenhouse gas emissions. Attribution science employs probabilistic methods, simulating event likelihoods in "factual" (with human forcings) versus "counterfactual" (pre-industrial) worlds via climate models, and storyline approaches emphasizing physical pathways. Critics contend these methods often overstate human contributions due to model deficiencies in capturing natural variability, dynamical processes, and uncertainties in circulation patterns, potentially leading to inflated risk assessments.
For tropical cyclones, global observational data from 1970 onward reveal no significant long-term increase in frequency or accumulated cyclone energy, with fluctuations aligning closely with multidecadal oscillations such as the AMO rather than a monotonic anthropogenic trend. NOAA records indicate no trend in U.S. landfalling hurricane counts or intensities since the late 19th century, despite model projections of intensification; recent decades' activity remains within historical variability bounds. Similarly, normalized economic losses from weather-related disasters, adjusted for socioeconomic factors like population growth, wealth accumulation, and exposure, show no detectable climate-driven trend across 62 reviewed studies from 1998 to 2023. This normalization processscaling past losses to present-day economic conditionsreveals that apparent rises in raw damages stem primarily from increased vulnerability rather than heightened event frequency or severity.
In domains like droughts and floods, empirical trends exhibit strong regional and decadal variability attributable to natural forcings, with global drought indices showing no uniform increase when accounting for land-use changes and observational biases; some analyses even indicate declining drought sensitivity in vegetation productivity. Heat extremes display a more discernible warming signal in bulk statistics, yet extreme attribution remains contested due to urban heat island effects, data adjustments, and incomplete model physics, where natural variability modulates event probabilities on shorter timescales. While IPCC assessments assert medium confidence in human influence for certain extremes like heavy precipitation, dissenting analyses highlight mismatches between modeled signals and observations, suggesting over-reliance on ensembles that underweight natural cycles. These disputes underscore methodological challenges in detection and attribution, with some researchers attributing interpretive biases to institutional pressures favoring anthropogenic narratives over parsimonious variability explanations.

Narratives on Alarmism and Media Influence

Critics contend that media portrayals of extreme weather events often amplify alarmist narratives by framing them as unequivocal harbingers of anthropogenic climate change, sidelining natural variability and long-term data trends that show no commensurate increase in many hazard frequencies or intensities. For example, coverage frequently links individual disasters to global warming without rigorous probabilistic attribution, a practice Roger Pielke Jr. has termed "climate porn," arguing it misleads by conflating rising nominal damagesdriven by socioeconomic factors like population growth and asset exposurewith climate-driven escalation. This approach, evident in headlines during events like Hurricane Sandy in 2012, prioritizes sensationalism over normalized loss metrics, which reveal weather-related disasters as a stable or declining proportion of global GDP since the mid-20th century. 
Such narratives have roots in earlier predictions that failed to materialize, including forecasts from the 1970s and 1980s of surging tropical cyclone activity, famines, and unreliable harvests due to climatic shifts, none of which aligned with observed records. More recent claims of escalating hurricanes, droughts, and floods attributable to warming have similarly been refuted by data; global tropical cyclone frequency has reached historic lows, and normalized U.S. disaster costs have not trended upward with temperature rises. Media emphasis on these unfulfilled projections contributes to public skepticism, with surveys indicating that many view crisis rhetoric as exaggerated and agenda-driven rather than empirically grounded.
English-language news exhibits biases in hazard selection and climate linkages, disproportionately covering floods and heatwaves while underreporting others, and more readily attributing reported events to human influence than neutral analyses warrant. This selective framing, compounded by institutional tendencies toward consensus-enforcing narratives in academia and journalism, shapes policy debates toward mitigation over adaptation, despite evidence that vulnerability reductions have curbed mortality from weather extremes by over 90% since 1920. Alarmism's persistence, critics argue, erodes trust when discrepancies emerge, as seen in post-event analyses revealing overstated climate signals in events like European heatwaves or U.S. wildfires.
