Giving feedback is one of the most important and most avoided responsibilities in professional life. Most managers dread it. Most colleagues avoid it. Most people would rather let poor performance continue indefinitely than have a fifteen-minute conversation about it. This avoidance is understandable because giving feedback feels risky: you might damage the relationship, trigger defensiveness, make the person cry, or discover that the problem is more complex than you thought. But avoiding feedback is far more costly than giving it badly. Silence in the face of poor performance is not kindness. It is neglect. It allows problems to compound, resentment to build, and people to continue making mistakes that nobody told them about.

The goal of feedback is not to make someone feel good or bad. It is to change behavior. If the behavior does not change after the feedback, the feedback did not work, regardless of how diplomatically it was delivered. Effective feedback produces three outcomes: the person understands specifically what they did, they understand the impact of what they did, and they know specifically what to do differently going forward.

The SBI model, developed by the Center for Creative Leadership, is one of the most effective frameworks for delivering feedback. SBI stands for Situation, Behavior, Impact.

Situation: describe when and where the behavior occurred. This anchors the feedback in a specific context rather than making it feel like a generalized character attack. "During yesterday's team meeting..."

Behavior: describe what the person did, in observable, factual terms. Not your interpretation. Not your feelings about it. What actually happened that a video camera would have captured. "...you interrupted Sarah three times while she was presenting her analysis..."

Impact: describe the effect of the behavior on you, the team, the project, or the organization. "...which made it difficult for the team to fully understand her findings, and I noticed that she became visibly frustrated and stopped elaborating on her points. As a result, we made a decision without hearing her complete analysis, which may not have been the best informed decision."

This formula works because it separates the person from the behavior. You are not saying "You are rude." You are saying "In this specific situation, this specific behavior had this specific impact." The person can receive this feedback without feeling attacked because it is about what they did, not who they are.

Positive feedback follows the same structure and is just as important as corrective feedback. Most managers give positive feedback vaguely, if at all. "Good job on the presentation." This tells the person nothing useful. They do not know what specifically was good, so they cannot replicate it. Better: "During the board presentation on Tuesday, the way you handled the CEO's challenging question about market share was exceptional. You stayed calm, acknowledged the validity of the concern, and then walked him through the data point by point until his objection was fully addressed. The board approved the proposal unanimously, and I believe your handling of that moment was the turning point. I want to see more of that in future presentations."

This positive feedback is specific enough that the person knows exactly what to do more of. It reinforces the specific behaviors that led to the positive outcome, making it more likely those behaviors will be repeated.

The feedback sandwich, the technique of sandwiching negative feedback between two pieces of positive feedback, is widely taught and widely ineffective. The problem is that both the giver and the receiver know the formula. The positive feedback at the beginning feels like a setup. The person tenses up waiting for the "but." When the negative feedback arrives, they discount the positive feedback that preceded it as insincere. And the positive feedback at the end feels like a consolation prize. The entire exchange feels manipulative rather than genuine.

A better approach is to separate positive and negative feedback into different conversations entirely. When you see something good, praise it immediately and specifically. When you see something that needs to change, address it directly and specifically. Do not combine them into a single awkward sandwich.

Delivering difficult feedback. Some feedback conversations are genuinely hard because the stakes are high, the behavior is serious, or the relationship is important. Here is how to navigate these conversations with courage and compassion.

Prepare your talking points. Write down the specific behavior, the specific impact, and the specific change you want to see. Do not wing difficult feedback conversations. The emotional intensity can cause you to lose your train of thought, say something you regret, or soften the message so much that the person does not understand the severity of the issue.

Choose the right time and place. Never give critical feedback in public. Never give it when either party is emotionally escalated. Never give it in passing or on the way to another meeting. Schedule a private conversation with enough time for discussion. Signal that the conversation is important: "I would like to set aside thirty minutes to talk about something that is important to me and to our working relationship."

Be direct. Do not circle around the issue. Do not use euphemisms. Do not hope they get the hint. State the issue clearly and specifically. "I need to talk to you about the accuracy of your work. In the last three reports you submitted, I found a total of fourteen factual errors, seven of which were significant enough that they would have misinformed the client if they had not been caught in review. This is a pattern that I am concerned about and I want to understand what is happening so we can fix it together."

Listen after delivering. After stating the feedback, stop talking and let the person respond. They may have context you are not aware of. They may be going through a personal crisis that is affecting their performance. They may have a legitimate explanation. Or they may acknowledge the problem and be grateful that someone finally told them. Whatever their response, listen fully before continuing the conversation.

Co-create the solution. The best feedback conversations end with a collaboratively developed plan for improvement. "What do you think would help you catch these errors before submission? Would a checklist help? Would a peer review process help? Would more time between drafting and submitting help? Let us find a solution that works for both of us."

Follow up. Feedback without follow-up is incomplete. Check in after a week, after two weeks, after a month. Acknowledge improvement when you see it. Address continued issues promptly. The follow-up communicates that the feedback was not a one-time event but the beginning of a sustained commitment to growth.

Receiving feedback. The ability to receive feedback gracefully is equally important as the ability to give it. When someone gives you feedback, your instinct is to defend yourself. Resist that instinct. Instead: listen without interrupting. Ask clarifying questions: "Can you give me a specific example?" Thank the person for the feedback, even if it is uncomfortable: "I appreciate you telling me this. It takes courage and I value your honesty." Take time to process before responding: "I want to think about what you have said before I respond. Can I come back to you tomorrow with my thoughts?" And then actually change. The most powerful response to feedback is not words. It is changed behavior that proves you heard, you understood, and you cared enough to do something about it.

Feedback is a gift, even when it does not feel like one. It is the information you need to grow, delivered by someone who cares enough about you or about the work to take the risk of telling you the truth. In a world where most people take the easier path of silence, the person who gives you honest, specific, well-delivered feedback is someone you should treasure.
