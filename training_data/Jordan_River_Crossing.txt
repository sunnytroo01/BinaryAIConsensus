 The Jordan River Crossing is the northernmost international land border crossing between Israel and Jordan, established on November 10, 1994, as part of the Israel-Jordan peace treaty, and located approximately 10 kilometers east of Beit She'an in Israel, near the historic Sheikh Hussein Bridge that spans the Jordan River to connect with Irbid in Jordan. Operated by the Israel Airports Authority, it facilitates the passage of tourists, Israeli passengers, and commercial commodities, including vehicles and pedestrians, serving as a vital link for trade and travel between the two nations and providing access to Jordan's industrial hub in Irbid and proximity to Amman. Since its inauguration, the crossing has undergone expansions, including new bridges in 1996, a cargo terminal handling thousands of trucks annually, and infrastructure improvements in 2001, 2006, and 2018-2019, enhancing its capacity amid the ongoing normalization of relations post-peace agreement.

History

Establishment and Early Operations (Post-1948 to 1967)

The Allenby Bridge, originally constructed in 1918 by British forces during World War I to facilitate crossings between the East and West Banks of the Jordan River, was destroyed on June 16, 1946, as part of the Haganah's "Night of the Bridges" operation aimed at disrupting British Mandate infrastructure. Following its destruction, a temporary Bailey bridgea portable, pre-fabricated steel structure commonly used for military and rapid reconstructionwas erected in its place, restoring basic connectivity across the river near Jericho. This Bailey bridge remained the primary span until its destruction during the 1967 Six-Day War.
After the 1948 Arab-Israeli War, Jordan asserted control over the West Bank, including the western approach to the bridge, following its conquest of the territory during the conflict. On April 24, 1950, Jordan formally annexed the West Bank, designating it as Cisjordan to distinguish it from the East Bank (Transjordan), thereby transforming the Allenby Bridge into an internal Jordanian crossing point linking the unified kingdom's two banks. Under Jordanian administration, the bridge served as a vital artery for the movement of civilians, goods, and administrative traffic between the West Bank cities like Jericho and the capital Amman, handling routine overland travel despite the rudimentary Bailey structure's limitations in capacity and durability.
Operations during this era were marked by stringent security measures, reflecting ongoing regional hostilities and border skirmishes with Israel along the Jordan River line established by the 1949 armistice agreements. The crossing facilitated Jordanian citizens' and West Bank residents' access to eastern economic hubs but prohibited Israeli passage, consistent with the state of war between the two nations. Incidents of sabotage and military tensions occasionally disrupted functionality, though the bridge endured as a key logistical node until Jordanian forces retreating in June 1967 demolished it to hinder Israeli advances. No formal passenger terminals or advanced customs infrastructure existed, with oversight relying on Jordanian military and police checkpoints to manage flows estimated in the thousands annually for essential travel.

Post-Six-Day War Opening and Development (1967-1990s)

Following the Six-Day War in June 1967, during which Israeli forces captured the West Bank from Jordanian control, the original Allenby Bridgespanning the Jordan River near Jerichowas destroyed amid the conflict. Israel subsequently established military administration over the western bank, including the crossing point, transforming it into the primary controlled land route for West Bank residents to access Jordan.
In 1968, a temporary truss-type bridge was constructed to replace the destroyed structure, enabling limited vehicular and pedestrian traffic under strict Israeli oversight. The western terminal fell under the Israeli Civil Administration's predecessor military government, which implemented a permit system regulating crossings primarily for Palestinian residents of the West Bank seeking family reunifications, medical treatment, or economic activities in Jordan. Jordan maintained control of the eastern terminal, but coordination was minimal, leading to bottlenecks and delays. Initially, in September 1967, Israel temporarily closed the Jordan River bridges to manage an influx of refugees attempting to return to the West Bank, prioritizing security and demographic stability.
Throughout the 1970s and 1980s, the crossing operated daily with Israeli security checks focusing on preventing infiltration and arms smuggling, while facilitating essential outbound travel for hundreds of thousands of Palestinians annually, though exact figures varied with political tensions. Infrastructure remained rudimentary, relying on the temporary bridge and basic terminal facilities, with Israeli authorities enhancing fencing and inspection points to address threats from fedayeen groups operating across the border. Trade was limited to small-scale exports like agricultural goods, subject to Israeli customs, underscoring the crossing's role as a lifeline amid restricted access to other international outlets.
The First Intifada, erupting in December 1987, prompted periodic closures and heightened restrictions, reducing traffic volumes as Israel curtailed permits to suppress unrest coordination with Jordan-based networks. By the early 1990s, amid emerging peace negotiations, minor upgrades to processing areas were introduced on the Israeli side to handle growing demand, though the truss bridge persisted until post-1994 reconstruction. This era solidified the Allenby crossingknown to Jordanians as the King Hussein Bridgeas a symbol of partitioned mobility, with Israeli control ensuring oversight but often criticized for inefficiencies and selective access.

Peace Era Expansions and Interruptions (1994-Present)

Following the Israel-Jordan peace treaty signed on October 26, 1994, the Allenby Bridgealso known as the King Hussein Bridgewas upgraded with the construction of a new modern paved terminal adjacent to the existing structure, facilitating civilian crossings for the first time since its military origins in World War I. This development, managed by Israel's Airports Authority under the treaty's provisions, enabled passenger and limited commercial traffic primarily serving Palestinians from the West Bank traveling to Jordan, marking a shift from prior military-only use.
Infrastructure expansions continued in subsequent years to address growing demand. In 2013, Israel installed a new X-ray scanning system at the terminal, projected to boost Palestinian exports through the crossing by 30 percent, accompanied by an extension of operating hours by two additional hours daily. By 2017, plans were announced for short- and long-term upgrades, including enhancements to passenger halls and construction of a new terminal estimated at 200 million NIS (approximately $55 million USD at the time), aimed at enabling around-the-clock operations. Further improvements materialized in 2021 with a dedicated container scanning facility that doubled daily inspection capacity from 100 to 200 units, streamlining cargo processing. In 2022, following U.S. diplomatic pressure, a pilot program launched for 24-hour passenger operations on weekdays, expanding to nearly continuous service by April to reduce bottlenecks and support economic flows.
Operations faced significant interruptions due to security concerns and health crises. During the Second Intifada (20002005), crossings were severely restricted, with Palestinian police and immigration presence at the Allenby terminal revoked in 2002 amid heightened violence, limiting traffic to essential Palestinian movements and contributing to economic disruptions in the West Bank. The COVID-19 pandemic prompted Jordan to curtail access starting March 9, 2020, restricting the bridge to Palestinians and diplomatic personnel only to curb virus transmission. More recently, security incidents have led to repeated closures; for instance, in September 2025, Israel indefinitely shut the crossing following a deadly attack that killed three Israelis, reopening it partially to passenger traffic only on September 26 after international concerns over stranded West Bank residents. These interruptions underscore the crossing's vulnerability to asymmetric threats, with operations resuming under tightened protocols each time.

Geography and Infrastructure

Location and Physical Features

The Jordan River Crossing, also known as the Allenby Bridge or King Hussein Bridge, spans the Jordan River in the Jordan Valley rift, connecting the West Bank near Jericho to Jordan near Al-Karameh. It is situated approximately 5 kilometers east of Jericho at coordinates 3152 N, 3532 E, making it the southernmost land border crossing between Jordan and Israel or the Palestinian territories. The site lies within the Dead Sea Transform fault system, at an elevation of 273 meters below sea level, the lowest terrestrial point accessible by this route.
The physical structure consists of a four-lane concrete bridge completed in 2001, replacing earlier iterations including a World War I-era wooden bridge and a 1946 Bailey bridge that was damaged during the 1967 Six-Day War. The Jordan River at this crossing is narrow and shallow, typically 10-30 meters wide with depths varying seasonally due to upstream diversions for agriculture and water supply, resulting in reduced flow compared to historical levels. The surrounding terrain features arid desert landscapes interspersed with agricultural fields, notably extensive banana plantations on the West Bank side within a 32-kilometer-wide valley floor.
On the Jordanian side, the terminal is positioned in the southern Jordan Valley, roughly 57 kilometers from Amman, facilitating access via Highway 90 and local roads amid similar rift valley topography characterized by low-lying floodplains and escarpments rising to higher plateaus eastward. The crossing includes separate terminals on each bank, connected by a neutral zone traversed by shuttle buses, with infrastructure designed for vehicular, pedestrian, and freight traffic amid the region's seismic activity and flash flood risks.

Bridge and Terminal Design

The Sheikh Hussein Bridge, spanning the Jordan River at the Jordan River Crossing, features a design incorporating pre-cast prestressed concrete girders supported by cast-in-situ elements, with an earth platform constructed over the riverbed to facilitate building and equipment staging. In 1996, two additional bridges were erected between the Israeli and Jordanian terminals to enhance transit efficiency for vehicles and goods.
The Israeli terminal, operated by the Israel Airports Authority on a 180-dunam site, includes a cargo facility designed to handle approximately 3,122 trucks per year carrying 33.6 tons of goods on average, alongside a Duty Free Shop opened in 1998 and a Departures Hall constructed in 2005. Accessibility provisions encompass wheelchair ramps and audio guidance systems for hearing- and visually-impaired users, while a 2019 addition provides a spill pallet compound for managing hazardous materials spills.
The Jordanian terminal, located near Irbid, mirrors this functional layout with dedicated zones for immigration, customs inspection, and baggage handling, primarily accommodating pedestrian and shuttle-based crossings for passengers and limited commercial traffic. Overall, both terminals prioritize security screening, including bag checks and X-ray scanners, within modular structures optimized for high-volume border processing rather than aesthetic or monumental architecture.

Crossing Procedures and Hours

Travelers from Jordan to Israel or the West Bank begin at the Jordanian terminal, where they pay a departure tax of 10 Jordanian dinars (approximately $14 USD) unless covered by a Jordan Pass, undergo passport control for an exit stamp, and then purchase a shuttle ticket (typically 2-3 JOD per person) to cross the approximately 1 km no-man's land over the Jordan River. In the reverse direction, from Israel or the West Bank to Jordan, procedures start with Israeli exit formalities at the Allenby terminal, including security checks and passport stamping, after which travelers board the shuttle to the Jordanian side. At the Jordanian terminal, entrants apply for a visa on arrival (40 JOD for single-entry, or waived with a Jordan Pass covering at least one nights accommodation), pay any applicable fees in local currency, and complete customs declarations; Jordanian visas are not issued directly at this crossing for those without prior arrangements. Entry Requirements

Visas for Jordan

Travelers entering Jordan through the Jordan River Crossing, also known as the King Hussein or Allenby Bridge, must obtain a visa in advance, as no visas are issued upon arrival at this border point. This requirement applies to most nationalities, including U.S. citizens and Israeli passport holders, differing from other Jordanian entry points like Queen Alia International Airport or the Sheikh Hussein Bridge, where visa on arrival is available for eligible visitors. 
An electronic visa (E-visa) can be applied for online via the Jordanian Ministry of Interior's portal at https://eservices.moi.gov.jo, or obtained from a Jordanian embassy or consulate in the traveler's country of residence. Single-entry tourist visas typically cost 40 Jordanian dinars (approximately $56 USD) and are valid for one month from the date of issue, with possible extensions available at Jordanian police stations. Passports must remain valid for at least six months beyond the planned entry date into Jordan. 
Israeli citizens require a visa for entry into Jordan regardless of the crossing point, and must ensure compliance with these pre-arrival procedures when using the Jordan River Crossing from Israeli or West Bank territory. Exceptions may apply for organized tour groups or diplomatic personnel, but individual travelers should verify requirements directly with Jordanian authorities to avoid denial at the border.

Incidents and Responses

On September 8, 2024, a Jordanian truck driver exited his vehicle at the Allenby Bridge terminal and opened fire on Israeli security personnel, killing three Israeli civilians before being shot dead by Israeli forces. The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) identified the attack as terrorism and temporarily closed the crossing for investigation and heightened security checks. Jordanian authorities condemned the violence, with the foreign ministry stating it rejected "all illegal actions that harm security and stability."
A similar attack occurred on September 18, 2025, when a Jordanian driver transporting humanitarian aid intended for Gaza opened fire and carried out a stabbing at the crossing, killing two IDF personnelreservist Lieutenant Colonel Yitzhak Harosh, aged 68, and Sergeant Oran Hershko, aged 20before being neutralized by security forces. In response, Israel immediately closed the Allenby Bridge indefinitely, suspended all aid transfers from Jordan to Gaza via the route, and enhanced security protocols at adjacent crossings, including the northern Jordan River terminal. Jordan reopened the crossing on its side three days later, but Israel maintained the shutdown, citing ongoing threats. The Jordanian government condemned the incident as an act of violence while reaffirming its opposition to instability in the region.
These incidents, occurring despite the 1994 Israel-Jordan peace treaty, prompted Israeli officials to highlight vulnerabilities in cross-border aid inspections and potential infiltration risks from Jordan. Prior to 2024, major attacks at the site were rare, with the 2024 event described by analysts as the first significant violence in approximately 30 years, underscoring the crossing's role as a controlled but tense interface. Responses have consistently involved rapid neutralization of threats by Israeli security, followed by closures to prevent escalation, though such measures have drawn criticism from Palestinian and Jordanian sources for restricting civilian and aid access.
