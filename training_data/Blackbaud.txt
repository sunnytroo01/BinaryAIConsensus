 Blackbaud, Inc. (NASDAQ: BLKB) is an American cloud software company founded in 1981 and headquartered in Charleston, South Carolina, that develops and provides specialized solutions powering social impact for nonprofit organizations, educational institutions, foundations, corporations, and healthcare providers.
The company's core offerings include constituent relationship management (CRM), fundraising tools such as Blackbaud Enterprise Fundraising CRM and Raiser's Edge, scholarship and award management software such as Blackbaud Award Management, financial management software, and analytics platforms designed to streamline operations, donor engagement, and revenue growth in the social good sector.
Blackbaud connects and empowers over a million users spanning more than 100 countries, positioning it as a market leader in its niche with annual revenues exceeding $1 billion, sustained investment in research and development, and recognitions including Fast Company's 2025 Best Workplaces for Innovators and TIME's America's Best Mid-Size Companies.
A defining controversy arose from a 2020 ransomware attack that compromised sensitive customer data across thousands of organizations, prompting criticism for inadequate security measures, delayed breach notifications, and ransom payment, which culminated in regulatory actions including a $49.5 million multistate settlement requiring enhanced data protection practices and a separate Federal Trade Commission consent order mandating deletion of unnecessary consumer data.

History

Founding and Early Years

Blackbaud was founded in 1981 by Anthony Bakker, a British native who had studied physics in college and self-taught computer programming. While working as a banker at Manufacturers Hanover Trust in New York City, Bakker developed the company's inaugural producta computerized student billing systemin response to needs at the Nightingale-Bamford School, an independent institution in Manhattan. This system addressed accounts receivable for private K-12 schools, marking Blackbaud's entry into administrative software for educational nonprofits.
The company was formally incorporated in 1982 in Long Island, New York, with its name coined as a pun blending "blackboard"evoking educationand "baud," the measure of data transmission rates. Early growth relied on word-of-mouth referrals from initial clients, prompting expansion beyond billing to complementary tools like accounts payable management and donor fundraising software, which coalesced into the foundational Raiser's Edge platform. By focusing exclusively on the nonprofit sector from inception, Blackbaud differentiated itself in a nascent market for specialized computing solutions, predating widespread personal computer adoption.
In 1989, Blackbaud relocated its headquarters from Long Island to Mount Pleasant, South Carolina, seeking reduced operational costs, a business-friendly environment, and milder weather compared to the Northeast. This move supported scaling operations while maintaining a client base concentrated in U.S. independent schools and emerging charitable organizations, setting the stage for product evolution amid the 1990s shift to graphical user interfaces like Windows.

Growth Through Acquisitions and Product Expansion

Blackbaud pursued aggressive growth via acquisitions starting in the mid-2000s, targeting complementary technologies in fundraising, CRM, and nonprofit management to broaden its market reach and product capabilities. By 2025, the company had completed at least 17 acquisitions, with peaks in 2016, 2017, and 2022, enabling integration of specialized tools into its core platform and expansion beyond traditional on-premise software into cloud-based and international offerings.
Early acquisitions focused on enhancing online and scalable fundraising solutions. In August 2007, Blackbaud acquired eTapestry for $24.8 million plus up to $1.5 million in contingent payments, incorporating a best-of-breed on-demand fundraising platform that served smaller nonprofits and complemented its enterprise-focused Raiser's Edge product. This move facilitated entry into the growing SaaS market for donor management. In May 2008, the company announced the acquisition of Kintera, a competitor in online fundraising, with closure around July 2008, which added peer-to-peer and event management capabilities, though it later involved sunsetting some overlapping products.
Subsequent deals expanded into analytics, corporate giving, and global markets. The January 2012 acquisition of Convio, funded partly by cash and credit expansion, integrated advanced online marketing and digital engagement tools, strengthening Blackbaud's digital fundraising suite for mid-sized organizations. In 2017, Blackbaud completed a 95 million purchase of JustGiving, a leading UK-based online donation platform, marking a key step in international expansion and peer-to-peer fundraising for European nonprofits. Domestic growth continued with the April 2018 acquisition of Reeher LLC, adding higher education analytics for prospecting and stewardship.
Later acquisitions emphasized corporate social responsibility (CSR) and engagement innovations. In January 2019, Blackbaud bought YourCause for approximately $157 million, integrating a platform for employee giving and CSR programs used by Fortune 500 companies. The January 2022 acquisition of EVERFI introduced SaaS tools for ESG training and social impact initiatives, reaching millions of learners annually and diversifying into corporate philanthropy. Later that year, in August 2022, Kilter was acquired to bolster activity-based peer-to-peer fundraising, supporting health, wellness, and community events.
These acquisitions drove product expansion by merging acquired technologies into unified ecosystems, such as enhancing Blackbaud's CRM with cloud analytics, digital payment processing, and CSR modules, while reducing silos through integrations like those post-Convio and Kintera. This strategy shifted Blackbaud from a primarily U.S.-centric fundraising vendor to a global provider of end-to-end nonprofit software, though it occasionally involved discontinuing redundant acquired products to streamline offerings.

Products and Services

Core Fundraising and CRM Solutions

Blackbaud's primary fundraising and customer relationship management (CRM) solutions center on two flagship products tailored for nonprofit organizations: Raiser's Edge NXT and Blackbaud Enterprise Fundraising CRM. These tools facilitate donor management, campaign execution, and relationship building, with Raiser's Edge NXT serving mid-sized nonprofits through cloud-based donor database functionality, while Blackbaud Enterprise Fundraising CRM targets larger enterprises with scalable, customizable features.
Raiser's Edge NXT provides comprehensive donor data management, enabling organizations to build constituent profiles, track interactions, add notes and tasks, and segment audiences for targeted outreach. It supports end-to-end gift processing, including flexible entry, prospect research integration, and AI-powered analytics for suggested solicitation amounts, alongside portfolio management and automated workflows to enhance fundraising efficiency. As of October 2025, enhancements include UK-specific features for Gift Aid processing, GDPR compliance, and donor engagement tools, reflecting adaptations for regional regulatory needs. The platform integrates with external services like prospect screening providers to enrich donor insights and streamline moves management.
However, Raiser's Edge NXT does not include native peer-to-peer (P2P) fundraising features. Blackbaud provides separate P2P solutions such as TeamRaiser and JustGiving, which integrate with Raiser's Edge NXT to sync fundraisers, donors, donations, and campaign data into the CRM for unified tracking. TeamRaiser is praised for handling P2P events (e.g., walks/runs, DIY fundraisers), customization, milestones through gamification and leaderboards, and revenue generation, with strong integration to Raiser's Edge NXT for data consistency and donor management.
User feedback for Raiser's Edge NXT on independent review platforms shows mixed to positive reception, with praise for its robust features balanced against notable limitations, particularly for smaller organizations. On GetApp, it holds a 4.2/5 rating from 415 verified reviews, with users praising its comprehensive donor management, robust reporting, cloud access, and versatility, though many criticize its steep learning curve, complexity requiring significant training and technical expertise, high costs, and inconsistent support. Reviewers across platforms frequently note that while reporting features are powerful and customizable, they involve complex and time-consuming query/report building, often requiring technical expertise, and can feature confusing interfaces for routine data pulls. Many users indicate the system is better suited for larger organizations with dedicated staff, as smaller to medium nonprofits often find the reporting overwhelming and less user-friendly due to these complexities and high costs that may not suit limited budgets. On Gartner Peer Insights, it scores 3.9/5 from 19 ratings and is described as a solid high-end donor CRM. Product pages with user reviews and comparisons are available on G2 and Capterra, while no dedicated Trustpilot page or reviews were found.
Blackbaud Enterprise Fundraising CRM, designed for enterprise-scale nonprofits, higher education institutions, and healthcare entities, combines advanced fundraising modules with relationship management, predictive analytics, and multichannel marketing capabilities. It features robust direct marketing segmentation tools that enable the creation of segments based on constituent characteristics such as giving history, wealth, demographics, or location. These segments can be added to marketing efforts, including mailings, to target specific audiences. List segments, often for imported or vendor-managed lists, can be assigned usages such as test, retest, continuation, or reuse, and linked to packages, ask ladders, and source codes for tracking and performance analysis. Segment groups facilitate bulk addition of segments to efforts. Additionally, Blackbaud analytics applies cluster segmentation to group constituents based on capacity, giving behavior, sociodemographics, and attitudes toward giving, creating donor personas that enable personalized messaging across channels. It allows customization for complex operations, such as capturing consent data, recording interactions, and deploying integrated campaigns across digital channels. The solution supports large databases and high-volume processing, with built-in functionality for analytics-driven decision-making and service integrations to underpin consent and engagement processes.
Both solutions emphasize integration within Blackbaud's ecosystem, enabling data synchronization for holistic constituent views and automation of routine tasks via low-code tools. They prioritize scalability, with Raiser's Edge NXT handling standardized workflows for growing organizations and Enterprise CRM accommodating bespoke requirements for major philanthropies. Usage data indicates these platforms contribute to facilitating over $100 billion in annual fundraising across Blackbaud's customer base.

Financial Management and Operational Tools

Blackbaud's financial management tools center on Blackbaud Financial Edge NXT, a cloud-based accounting platform engineered for nonprofits and government entities to manage complex fund accounting, including restricted funds, grants, and donor agreements. This software facilitates budgeting per fund or grant, automated reconciliation of bank and credit card transactions, and compliance through document storage and intelligence features, enabling organizations to maintain a single source of truth for fiscal accountability. It integrates with other Blackbaud systems, such as Raiser's Edge NXT for donor data synchronization, and third-party tools via the Blackbaud Marketplace for payroll, HR, and advanced reporting.
Key functionalities include expense management with patented submission processes, automated routing based on rules, budget checks, and audit trails to control spending and provide holistic visibility into cash flow and liquidity. Recent enhancements, introduced in September 2024, incorporate an action hub in the general ledger, close management tools, and AI-powered insights to streamline monthly closes and reduce manual checklists. The platform supports accounts payable automation, integrating with ERP systems to minimize errors and accelerate processing.
Complementing core accounting, Blackbaud offers specialized financial tools like Blackbaud Financial Aid Management, targeted at K-12 private schools for handling tuition discounts, scholarships, and need-based awards through customizable applications, AI-driven document review, and real-time disbursement tracking. Blackbaud Grantmaking streamlines grant lifecycle operations, from application tracking to reporting and compliance, aiding foundations and nonprofits in efficient fund distribution. Blackbaud Award Management is a cloud-based scholarship and award management software designed for higher education institutions. It improves student access to awards and scholarship funds by automating matching of candidates to eligible scholarships, providing personalized recommendations, and including application tracking. The product is listed on G2.com with user reviews (including 24 filtered reviews), features listings, and comparisons with competitors such as SurveyMonkey Apply, AwardSpring, and Submittable.
Operational tools include Blackbaud Integrated Payments, which provides AI-enhanced payment processing with fraud protection, digital wallet support, and PCI compliance, integrating seamlessly to boost transaction efficiency and revenue capture for nonprofits. Blackbaud Tuition Management further supports educational operations by automating billing, payments, and financial aid workflows, including family portals for document submission and reminders. These tools collectively enhance day-to-day fiscal and administrative processes, prioritizing nonprofit-specific compliance and scalability over general-purpose enterprise software.

2020 Cybersecurity Breach

Breach Mechanics and Initial Detection

In early 2020, unauthorized actors gained initial access to a Blackbaud-hosted customer database, exploiting the company's inadequate security controls, including unpatched vulnerabilities and insufficient network segmentation. The intruders then leveraged administrator credentials to laterally move across Blackbaud's cloud and on-premises environments, systematically exfiltrating unencrypted donor data from multiple customer instances over several months. This included personally identifiable information such as names, contact details, Social Security numbers, and bank account data for millions of individuals associated with over 13,000 nonprofit customers. The breach mechanics involved persistent reconnaissance and data harvesting without immediate disruption, enabling the theft of vast datasets before escalating to ransomware deployment.
The intrusion commenced on February 7, 2020, and continued intermittently until May 20, 2020, remaining undetected for roughly three months due to Blackbaud's failure to implement timely monitoring, encryption of sensitive data at rest, or effective anomaly detection tools. Initial detection occurred on May 14, 2020, when Blackbaud's security team identified indicators of compromise, including anomalous network activity and the onset of ransomware encryption attempts targeting systems. By May 20, the company had isolated the affected systems and paid approximately 24 Bitcoin (equivalent to about $250,000 at the time) to the attackers, who claimed to delete the stolen data in exchangethough Blackbaud did not independently verify this destruction. This ransomware variant locked files to coerce payment, but the prior exfiltration had already compromised the data's integrity.

Company Response and Data Handling

Blackbaud detected the ransomware attack on May 14, 2020, and promptly engaged cybersecurity experts to expel the intruders from its network. The company paid the attackers approximately $235,000 in Bitcoin as ransom, securing a promise from the cybercriminals to delete the exfiltrated data, which consisted of a subset of donor records including names, addresses, email addresses, phone numbers, dates of birth, Social Security numbers, and partial financial information for select individuals. Independent third-party forensic investigators and law enforcement corroborated the attackers' assurances that the stolen data had been destroyed, with no evidence of subsequent misuse or resale emerging at the time.
On July 16, 2020, nearly two months after detection, Blackbaud issued its first public disclosure of the incident via a press release and began notifying affected customers, asserting that sensitive payment details such as bank account numbers and credit card information remained uncompromised. The company emphasized in these communications that the exfiltrated data was limited and had been irretrievably deleted following the ransom payment, though subsequent regulatory scrutiny revealed these statements omitted the full scope of data theft known internally. Blackbaud's SEC filings reflected this approach: an August 4, 2020, Form 10-Q acknowledged the attack's occurrence without detailing exfiltration, while a September 29, 2020, Form 8-K provided further incident details.
In handling the compromised data, Blackbaud relied on post-ransom verification rather than proactive recovery or public disclosure of all potentially affected records, leading to criticisms from regulators that the firm prioritized operational continuity over transparent breach assessment. The company did not implement immediate widespread credit monitoring offers to donors but advised customers to evaluate risks independently, contributing to downstream notifications by nonprofits to their constituents. This response delayed broader awareness, as many organizations only learned of the exposure through Blackbaud's July notifications.

Immediate Impacts on Customers

Following Blackbaud's public disclosure of the ransomware attack on July 16, 2020, the company notified over 13,000 affected customers, primarily nonprofits relying on its fundraising and CRM software such as Raiser's Edge. These customers, spanning sectors like education, healthcare, and philanthropy, then bore the responsibility of assessing the scope of compromised data within their own instances and notifying impacted donors, a process that began immediately but was complicated by Blackbaud's initial assurances that no sensitive financial or Social Security number data had been accessedclaims later contradicted by evidence of such exposure. 
The exfiltrated data included donor names, addresses, phone numbers, email addresses, donation histories, and, in some cases, bank account details and Social Security numbers from over one million files, heightening immediate risks of targeted scams, phishing, and social engineering attacks exploiting known philanthropic patterns. Nonprofits faced urgent operational burdens, including the rapid deployment of credit monitoring services for donors, public communications to mitigate panic, and internal reviews of data backups, which strained limited resources and contributed to a reported spike in breach notifications across healthcare and education entities dependent on Blackbaud's systems. 
Misleading early disclosures from Blackbaud delayed full transparency, forcing customers to revise notifications in late September 2020 upon confirmation of broader data theft, which eroded trust in the vendor and prompted some organizations to question ongoing reliance on its platforms amid fears of residual vulnerabilities. While no widespread service outages were reported, the incident disrupted routine CRM operations as customers implemented ad-hoc security measures and monitored for fraud, with donors advised to scrutinize statements for unauthorized activity and remain wary of fraudulent donation solicitations leveraging stolen histories.

Legal and Regulatory Consequences

SEC and FTC Actions

In March 2023, the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) charged Blackbaud with violations of Sections 17(a)(2) and 17(a)(3) of the Securities Act of 1933 and Section 13(a) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, stemming from misleading disclosures about a ransomware attack that occurred between February and May 2020. The SEC alleged that Blackbaud's July 16, 2020, public announcement falsely stated the attacker had not accessed customer bank account or credit card information, despite the company's internal awarenessbased on forensic analysis completed by June 25, 2020that such data had been compromised and downloaded. Additionally, the SEC found Blackbaud maintained inadequate disclosure controls and procedures, including insufficient processes for evaluating and escalating cybersecurity incident details to senior management and the board. Blackbaud settled the matter without admitting or denying the findings, consenting to a cease-and-desist order and paying a $3 million civil penalty.
In February 2024, the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) proposed a consent agreement with Blackbaud, finalized on May 20, 2024, under Section 5 of the FTC Act, alleging unfair security practices enabled the 2020 breach, which exposed sensitive consumer data such as names, addresses, Social Security numbers, and financial information from millions of individuals via Blackbaud's nonprofit and educational clients. The FTC complaint highlighted deficiencies including failure to enforce multifactor authentication on remote access tools, inadequate encryption of sensitive data at rest and in transit, lack of network segmentation, and retention of unnecessary personal information without a formal policy, which facilitated the intruder's initial access via compromised credentials and subsequent exfiltration of over 10 terabytes of data. The settlement imposed no monetary payment but required Blackbaud to implement a comprehensive data retention schedule, delete all unnecessary retained personal data within 120 days (with ongoing deletion obligations), conduct biennial data inventories, enhance access controls and encryption, undergo third-party security audits for 20 years, and refrain from misrepresenting its data security practices. Blackbaud neither admitted nor denied the allegations.

Multistate Settlements and Lawsuits

In October 2023, Blackbaud reached a $49.5 million multistate settlement with attorneys general from 49 states and the District of Columbia to resolve an investigation into its handling of the 2020 ransomware attack. The agreement stemmed from allegations that Blackbaud violated state consumer protection laws and breach-notification statutes by maintaining deficient data security practices, paying a ransom to attackers without verifying data deletion, delaying notifications to affected customers for months, and making misleading public statements that minimized the breach's scope and impact. Under the terms, Blackbaud committed to implementing comprehensive reforms, including enhanced encryption, multi-factor authentication, regular security audits, and timely breach reporting protocols, without admitting liability. The funds were distributed to participating states based on factors such as the number of affected residents and Blackbaud's revenue from those states; for example, Tennessee received $882,312, while Nebraska's share supported consumer protection efforts.
Parallel to the multistate resolution, Blackbaud faced numerous class-action lawsuits filed by affected individuals and nonprofits, alleging negligence in data protection and breach response. These were consolidated into multidistrict litigation (MDL No. 2972) in the U.S. District Court for the District of South Carolina, where plaintiffs claimed Blackbaud failed to employ reasonable safeguards for sensitive donor information, including names, contact details, and financial data from millions of records across thousands of client organizations. In May 2024, the court denied class certification in a key consumer subclass action, ruling that plaintiffs failed to demonstrate commonality of injury or typicality, as harms varied widely and no uniform damages were ascertainable from the breach alone. Individual claims and potential settlements continue in the MDL, with ongoing discovery into Blackbaud's security lapses and post-breach representations.
Additionally, California pursued a separate enforcement action, securing a $6.75 million judgment in June 2024 against Blackbaud for similar violations under state data protection laws, requiring court approval and further compliance measures like data minimization and vendor oversight enhancements. This action highlighted Blackbaud's inadequate response to identified vulnerabilities prior to the breach and its initial underreporting of compromised data volume.

Ongoing Litigation and Security Reforms

Following the denial of class certification in May 2024 for the consolidated class action lawsuit stemming from the 2020 breach, where U.S. District Judge Bruce Howe Hendricks ruled that plaintiffs failed to demonstrate commonality of harm across proposed classes, the litigation persists in individual capacities. Blackbaud's 2024 Annual Report on Form 10-K, filed in February 2025, confirms that related proceedings remain active after the Fourth Circuit Court of Appeals denied plaintiffs' petition for permission to appeal the certification denial. Separate claims by insurers alleging coverage disputes over breach-related losses were dismissed by a court in April 2025.
In response to regulatory scrutiny, Blackbaud has implemented security reforms mandated by settlements, including the FTC's finalized order on May 20, 2024, which requires deletion of previously collected consumer data no longer necessary for business purposes and establishment of a comprehensive information security program with biennial third-party assessments for 20 years. The order addresses prior deficiencies such as inadequate encryption, multifactor authentication, and network segmentation, which the FTC alleged enabled the breach. Similarly, the June 2024 California settlement imposes ongoing obligations for enhanced data security measures, breach notification protocols, and annual reporting to the state attorney general for three years, alongside a $6.75 million penalty.
The October 2023 multistate settlement with 49 states and the District of Columbia, totaling $49.5 million, further enforces compliance with data minimization principles and security best practices, though it lacks the detailed prescriptive reforms of the FTC agreement. These reforms collectively aim to mitigate risks from the breach, where attackers accessed unencrypted data via compromised customer credentials undetected for months, but Blackbaud has maintained that its core platform security was not fundamentally altered beyond settlement-driven changes.

Criticisms and Broader Controversies

Vendor Practices and Customer Dependencies

Blackbaud maintains a dominant position in the nonprofit software market, holding an 11.9% share as of 2024 and serving over 22,000 customers worldwide, which fosters significant customer dependencies due to the specialized nature of its fundraising, CRM, and financial management tools like Raiser's Edge. Nonprofits often integrate Blackbaud's ecosystem deeply into operations, handling donor data, gift processing, and reporting, which creates high switching costs including data migration complexities, staff retraining, and potential disruptions to fundraising cycles. This lock-in is exacerbated by the sector's limited alternatives tailored to nonprofit compliance and workflows, leaving organizations vulnerable to vendor pricing changes and service issues without easy exit options.
Customer complaints highlight restrictive contract practices, including auto-renewal clauses that complicate cancellations and require negotiation through customer success managers rather than unilateral termination. Better Business Bureau records document multiple instances where Blackbaud refused contract cancellations, denied refunds for disputed charges, and insisted on payments despite alleged failures to deliver agreed services, such as maintenance support. Users have reported annual renewal increases of up to 23% over recent periods, attributed by Blackbaud representatives to across-the-board adjustments affecting all clients, further straining dependent nonprofits with fixed budgets.
These practices amplify risks for customers, as evidenced by post-2020 breach attempts to exit contracts, where organizations argued Blackbaud breached terms by suspending updates or support, yet faced resistance in termination. While Blackbaud's terms emphasize internal business use and indemnity limitations, the combination of market entrenchment and enforcement rigidity has drawn criticism for prioritizing retention over flexibility, potentially hindering nonprofits' ability to adapt to evolving needs or mitigate vendor-specific vulnerabilities.

Ethical Concerns in Philanthropy Software

Blackbaud's philanthropy software, including platforms like Raiser's Edge and Financial Edge NXT, centralizes vast amounts of sensitive donor data such as names, contact information, donation histories, and philanthropic inclinations, prompting ethical scrutiny over privacy protections and data minimization. Critics argue that the software's design encourages prolonged retention of personal data beyond active fundraising needs, heightening vulnerability to unauthorized access and misuse, as evidenced by regulatory findings that Blackbaud maintained unnecessary records without adequate justification or safeguards. This practice raises causal concerns about whether such aggregation inherently prioritizes operational efficiency over individual donor autonomy, potentially eroding trust in philanthropic institutions reliant on the software.
The company's service to politically divisive clients, notably the National Rifle Association (NRA), has fueled debates on moral neutrality in philanthropy technology provision. Between 2017 and 2021, Blackbaud received over $10 million in fees from the NRA for software supporting fundraising and donor management, despite Blackbaud's self-positioning as a "social good" provider focused on mission-driven organizations. This association prompted calls from advocacy groups for nonprofits to scrutinize vendor partnerships, arguing that enabling data tools for entities involved in gun rights advocacypost-mass shootingsimplicates providers in facilitating potentially "tainted" philanthropy flows. In response, entities like the Chartered Institute of Fundraising paused affiliations with Blackbaud-linked sponsors in June 2022, highlighting tensions between commercial impartiality and ethical alignment in software ecosystems.
Wealth screening features integrated into Blackbaud's tools, which analyze public and proprietary data to identify high-capacity donors, have drawn industry-wide ethical questions about consent and intrusiveness, though Blackbaud-specific incidents remain tied to broader data handling lapses. These capabilities, while effective for resource allocation, can perpetuate inequities by favoring wealthy prospects based on algorithmic inferences, potentially sidelining grassroots giving and raising fairness issues in donor solicitation. Regulatory settlements, including a 2024 FTC order mandating data deletion protocols and enhanced governance, underscore the ethical imperative for philanthropy software to embed privacy-by-design principles, yet implementation challenges persist amid customer dependencies on legacy systems.

Philanthropy and Industry Contributions

Blackbaud Institute Research

The Blackbaud Institute, established as Blackbaud's dedicated research lab, analyzes data from the company's extensive client baseencompassing billions in annual transactions across nonprofits, foundations, and educational institutionsto produce empirical insights into philanthropy and social impact operations. Its studies leverage proprietary datasets to track trends in donor behavior, fundraising efficacy, and sector challenges, often benchmarking performance by organization size, subsector, and giving channel. While grounded in real-time transaction data, the Institute's findings primarily reflect organizations using Blackbaud's software, potentially underrepresenting smaller or non-client entities.
Annual Charitable Giving Reports form a cornerstone of the Institute's output, documenting year-over-year shifts with granular metrics. For instance, the 2024 analysis revealed a 1.9% increase in overall U.S. charitable giving, approaching pre-pandemic peaks, driven partly by a 2.2% rise in online donations and an 11.3% surge in healthcare subsector contributions; the mean gift size reached a record $937 across subsectors. Earlier reports highlighted patterns such as 34% of 2024 giving concentrated in the final quarter, consistent with decade-long seasonality tied to holidays and elections. These reports also segment data by donor demographics, noting higher retention among event-based gifts (up to 60% in some years) versus one-off online donations.
The Institute extends beyond giving volumes to operational strategies, as in the 2025 Status of Fundraising Report, which surveyed over 550 professionals and linked technology adoptionparticularly AI integrationto revenue outcomes; organizations prioritizing digital tools saw up to 5% higher growth, while laggards faced declines amid stagnant manual processes. Complementary studies address risks, with the 2025 Risk Readiness Report identifying 13 top threats like cybersecurity breaches and regulatory changes, recommending data-driven mitigation based on high-performing peers' practices.
Generational and collaborative research further diversifies the portfolio. Reports on donor cohorts, such as "Charitable Support Across Generations," detail preferences: Gen Z favors digital micro-gifts and cause-aligned activism, while Boomers prioritize legacy giving via planned gifts. A 2025 partnership with GivingTuesday analyzed donor retention, finding 65% of event participants donated again the following year versus 52% for non-participants, underscoring sustained engagement value. Forward-looking work, like "Giving in 2035," projects trends such as AI-personalized appeals boosting mid-level giving by 20-30%, informed by historical data extrapolations. These outputs equip practitioners with actionable benchmarks, though critics note the Institute's vendor affiliation may emphasize tech-centric solutions.

Corporate Social Responsibility Efforts

Blackbaud structures its corporate social responsibility (CSR) efforts around four priority areas: people and culture, fueling social impact, environmental sustainability, and governance. These initiatives emphasize employee engagement, philanthropy facilitation through proprietary software, decarbonization, and ethical oversight, as detailed in annual Impact Reports.
In fueling social impact, Blackbaud's platforms, including YourCause and Raiser's Edge NXT, have supported 166,400 charities through employee giving programs, tracked 8.1 million hours of employee volunteering, and facilitated $1.27 billion in corporate grants. JustGiving, another Blackbaud tool, processed 20 million donations and supported 23,100 nonprofits with over 1.5 million fundraising pages created. Nonprofits using Raiser's Edge NXT reported a 48% average increase in donations within five years of adoption, alongside $9.95 million saved in 2024 through fee offset programs like Complete Cover. Overall, Blackbaud's software ecosystem raised, granted, or managed over $100 billion annually as of 2024.
Employee volunteering forms a core component, with over 70% of Blackbaud's workforce participating in 2024three times the global medianand a 65% increase in volunteer hours powered by YourCause observed in 2023. The company organized over 125 employer-led volunteer opportunities in 2024, including its annual Month of Caring. In January 2024, Blackbaud established an Inclusion and Sustainability Council, chaired by CEO Mike Gianoni, to integrate ESG strategies across operations.
On environmental sustainability, Blackbaud achieved carbon neutrality for the fourth consecutive year in 2024, following a 92% reduction in Scope 1 and 2 emissions since 2019 and a 23% decrease in energy use at its global headquarters over the same period. Decarbonization efforts include ongoing climate risk assessments by executive and ESG teams. Governance measures prioritize cybersecurity investments and ethical standards, though ESG reports undergo internal audits without external assurance.
Blackbaud hosts the annual Corporate Social Impact Summit, with the 2025 event held April 7-9 in Dallas focusing on employee engagement and philanthropy innovation, and the 2026 edition announced for North America. The company received the TrustRadius Tech Cares Award for the fourth consecutive year in August 2025, recognizing its CSR framework.
