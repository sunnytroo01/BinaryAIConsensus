Maleic acid
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Maleic acid, systematically named (2Z)-but-2-enedioic acid, is an organic compound classified as a dicarboxylic acid with the molecular formula CHO. It features a four-carbon chain with a cis-configured double bond between the central carbons, each flanked by a carboxylic acid group, distinguishing it as the cis isomer of fumaric acid. This structure enables maleic acid to form a five-membered cyclic anhydride, maleic anhydride, which is a key intermediate in its industrial applications.
As a white to colorless crystalline solid with a faint odor, maleic acid has a melting point of 130.5132.5 C and decomposes before boiling, while exhibiting high solubility in water (approximately 788 g/L at 25 C) and moderate solubility in alcohols and acetone. Its density is 1.59 g/cm at 20 C, and it is combustible, producing irritating fumes upon heating. Chemically, it is acidic with pKa values of 1.94 and 6.22, reflecting the dissociation of its two carboxyl groups, and it undergoes reactions such as hydration to form malic acid or ozonolysis to yield glyoxylic acid.
Maleic acid is primarily produced industrially through the hydrolysis of maleic anhydride, which itself is synthesized via the catalytic oxidation of n-butane or benzene using vanadium pentoxide as a catalyst. Alternative methods include the catalytic isomerization of fumaric acid. Recent advances include bio-based synthesis from renewable feedstocks such as furfural. U.S. production volumes have historically ranged from 1,000,000 to 10,000,000 pounds per year as of 2019.
In industry, maleic acid serves as a versatile building block for synthesizing resins, plasticizers, and adhesives, as well as in water treatment, food acidulation (with a tolerable daily intake of 0.5 mg/kg body weight), and other applications including pharmaceuticals and polymers. Environmentally, it is biodegradable with an atmospheric half-life of about 2 days and shows low bioaccumulation potential, though it can be toxic to aquatic organisms.

Nomenclature and History

Etymology

The name "maleic acid" is derived from the French term acide malique, which was coined in 1834 by the French chemist Thophile-Jules Pelouze (18071867) upon isolating the compound through the dry distillation of malic acid. This nomenclature represents a slight alteration of acide malique, the established name for malic acid, which traces its roots to the Latin mlum ("apple"), reflecting the fruit from which malic acid was first extracted. The connection underscores the historical linkage between maleic acid and its precursor, emphasizing the apple-derived origins in early organic chemistry naming.
In distinction, the isomer fumaric acid derives its name from the genus Fumaria (fumitory plants), specifically Fumaria officinalis, the source from which it was isolated in 1832 by German chemist Friedrich Ludwig Winckler (18041840), who termed it Fumarsure. This botanical basis contrasts with the fruit-inspired etymology of maleic acid, highlighting diverse natural origins in the nomenclature of these dicarboxylic acids.
During the 19th century, naming conventions for dicarboxylic acids in organic chemistry predominantly followed trivial systems, where compounds were designated based on their isolation sources, precursors, or perceived properties, often drawing from Latin or Greek terms to evoke natural associationssuch as oxalic from the sorrel plant (Oxalis) or succinic from amber (succinum). These practices, prevalent before systematic IUPAC rules, facilitated recognition among chemists while embedding etymological clues to chemical heritage.

Discovery and development

In the early 19th century, organic chemistry advanced through the isolation and transformation of compounds from natural products, with malic acidderived from applesserving as a prominent example. First isolated from apple juice by Carl Wilhelm Scheele in 1785, malic acid became a foundational material for exploring oxidation reactions and dicarboxylic acid derivatives.
Maleic acid was discovered in 1834 by Justus von Liebig and Friedrich Whler, who obtained it through the oxidation of malic acid using potassium dichromate and sulfuric acid. This synthesis marked an early triumph in deriving unsaturated dicarboxylic acids from hydroxy acids, establishing maleic acid as the cis isomer of butenedioic acid. The compound's name derives from malic acid, underscoring its natural origins.
Key developments in the late 19th century included the 1887 confirmation of maleic acid's cis configuration by Johannes Wislicenus, who distinguished it from the trans isomer fumaric acid and coined the term "geometric isomerism" to explain their structural differences. This insight, based on physical properties and reactivity, laid groundwork for stereochemistry. Milestones such as the first synthesis of maleic acid from fumaric acid in 1894 further illuminated interconversion pathways between the isomers.
The 20th century saw a shift from laboratory-scale production reliant on natural precursors like malic acid to large-scale synthetic routes, with commercial manufacture beginning in the 1930s via catalytic oxidation of benzene or hydrocarbons. This transition enabled broader applications and reduced dependence on botanical sources.

Structure and Properties

Molecular structure

Maleic acid possesses the molecular formula C4H4O4 and the systematic IUPAC name (2Z)-but-2-enedioic acid.
The molecule consists of a four-carbon chain with a central carbon-carbon double bond between positions 2 and 3, exhibiting a cis (Z) configuration that positions the two terminal carboxylic acid groups (-COOH) on the same side of the double bond. This stereochemistry arises from the restricted rotation around the sp2-hybridized double bond, distinguishing it as the geometric isomer of butenedioic acid.
The core structure adopts a planar geometry due to the sp2 hybridization of the alkene carbons (C2 and C3) and the carbonyl carbons in the carboxylic groups, which enforces trigonal planar arrangements around these atoms with bond angles approaching 120. This planarity enables intramolecular hydrogen bonding, where the hydroxyl hydrogen of one -COOH group interacts with the carbonyl oxygen of the adjacent -COOH group, forming a five-membered ring-like motif that stabilizes the monomer in both gas and solution phases.
In the crystalline state, maleic acid forms monoclinic prisms belonging to the space group P21/c, as refined through X-ray crystallographic analysis in 1974, revealing a structure where molecules are linked via intermolecular hydrogen bonds into dimers and chains. The unit cell parameters confirm the centrosymmetric packing, with the planar molecules aligned to accommodate the intramolecular bonds while minimizing steric clashes.
In comparison to its trans isomer, fumaric acid ((2E)-but-2-enedioic acid), the cis geometry of maleic acid introduces greater steric repulsion between the proximate carboxylic groups, rendering it less thermodynamically stable overall, though the intramolecular hydrogen bonding partially offsets this by lowering the energy of the cis form relative to a non-bonded conformation. Fumaric acid, with its extended trans arrangement, favors intermolecular hydrogen bonding in the solid state, contributing to its higher melting point and greater lattice stability.

Physical properties

Maleic acid is a white crystalline solid with a faint acidic odor. Its planar molecular structure, arising from conjugation and intramolecular hydrogen bonding, contributes to the formation of well-defined crystals. The solid exhibits a density of 1.590 g/cm at 25 C.
The compound melts in the range of 130135 C, after which it decomposes to maleic anhydride rather than boiling, as heating leads to dehydration without vaporization under standard pressure. This behavior is influenced by intramolecular hydrogen bonding between the carboxylic groups, which stabilizes the molecule and reduces intermolecular associations, enhancing relative volatility compared to its trans isomer, fumaric acid. The heat of combustion for the solid is -1,355 kJ/mol, reflecting the energy released upon complete oxidation to CO and HO.
Maleic acid demonstrates high solubility in polar solvents, dissolving at approximately 780 g/L in water at 25 C, and is freely soluble in ethanol and acetone. In contrast, it shows low solubility in nonpolar solvents such as benzene, where it is practically insoluble. This selectivity stems from the polar carboxylic groups facilitating interactions with protic and polar aprotic media.
Key thermodynamic properties under standard conditions (298 K, 1 bar) include the standard enthalpy of formation (H_f) of -789.5 kJ/mol for the solid phase, standard molar entropy (S) of 160.8 J/molK, and standard Gibbs free energy of formation (G_f) of -679.4 kJ/mol. These values indicate the compound's thermodynamic stability relative to its elements in standard states.

The cis configuration of maleic acid introduces steric strain between the adjacent carboxylic acid groups, rendering it less thermodynamically stable than its trans isomer, fumaric acid. Maleic acid exhibits redox behavior, particularly in electrochemical contexts, where it undergoes anodic oxidation in acidic media with near-complete faradaic efficiency (~97%). Its reduction potential at a lead electrode is approximately 0.1 V lower than that of fumaric acid, indicating greater ease of reduction and potential utility in electron transfer processes.

Synthesis and Production

Laboratory methods

One common laboratory method for synthesizing maleic acid involves the hydrolysis of maleic anhydride with water, often under mild acid catalysis to accelerate the reaction. The balanced equation for this process is:
CX4HX2OX3+HX2OCX4HX4OX4
\ce{C4H2O3 + H2O -> C4H4O4}
CX4HX2OX3+HX2OCX4HX4OX4
The reaction is typically carried out by adding maleic anhydride to water and heating gently until dissolution, followed by cooling to precipitate the product. Yields are generally high, exceeding 90%, with careful temperature control to avoid side reactions like polymerization. Acid catalysis, such as with dilute sulfuric acid, can shorten the reaction time but is optional for small-scale preparations.
Another approach is the oxidation of malic acid, historically significant and still used in educational settings to demonstrate selective dehydrogenation. Malic acid is oxidized using nitric acid or potassium permanganate in acidic conditions. These methods require fume hood use due to toxic fumes.
Purification of crude maleic acid is achieved via recrystallization from water, exploiting its temperature-dependent solubility (approximately 78 g/100 mL at 25C versus higher solubility at boiling temperatures). The crude product is dissolved in the minimum volume of boiling distilled water, filtered hot to remove insoluble impurities, and allowed to cool slowly to yield white crystals (mp 130131C). Typical recovery is 8090%, with multiple recrystallizations if needed for analytical purity. Safety precautions include wearing gloves, goggles, and lab coats, as maleic acid causes skin and eye irritation; avoid inhalation of dust and handle in a well-ventilated area. Yields from hydrolysis often exceed 95% after purification, far superior to older methods.
In the 19th century, maleic acid was first synthesized in the lab by distilling malic acid at 180200C, a dehydration process yielding a mixture of maleic acid and anhydride, as described by Thophile-Jules Pelouze in 1834; this method produced low yields (3050%) and required subsequent hydrolysis of the anhydride fraction. Modern variants, such as the hydrolysis and oxidation routes, offer higher efficiency, safer conditions, and purer products compared to these early thermal decompositions, which risked charring and low selectivity.

Industrial production

Maleic acid is primarily produced industrially through the hydration of maleic anhydride, which serves as the key intermediate obtained via the catalytic partial oxidation of n-butane. In this process, n-butane is oxidized in the vapor phase with air over vanadium-phosphorus oxide (VPO) catalysts, typically in fixed-bed or fluidized-bed reactors operating at temperatures of 350450C and pressures near atmospheric. The reaction proceeds in two stages: initial dehydrogenation to butadiene and subsequent oxidation to maleic anhydride, with overall yields from n-butane to maleic anhydride ranging from 55% to 65%, leading to an effective yield of 6070% for maleic acid after hydration.
Historically, maleic anhydride production relied on benzene oxidation, but a shift to n-butane feedstock began in the 1970s due to economic advantages, including lower raw material costs (n-butane being cheaper and more abundant) and improved energy efficiency, as the butane route avoids the energy-intensive steps associated with benzene purification and reduces byproduct formation. This transition enhanced process scalability and reduced operational costs by approximately 2030% in mature plants.
Global production of maleic acid is closely tied to maleic anhydride output, with anhydride capacity exceeding 5 million tons per year as of 2024, primarily in China (over 70% of capacity) and the United States. Leading producers include facilities operated by companies such as Huntsman Corporation in the US and various enterprises in China. As of 2024, China added nearly 1.5 million tons of new maleic anhydride capacity.
Recent advancements since the 2010s have explored bio-based routes to maleic acid from renewable feedstocks like furfural derived from lignocellulosic biomass, using heterogeneous catalysts such as vanadium-based systems or graphitic carbon nitride in oxidative processes with hydrogen peroxide, achieving yields up to 70% under mild conditions. These methods aim to reduce reliance on petrochemicals and improve sustainability, though they remain at pilot scale compared to conventional processes.

Reactions

Isomerization to fumaric acid

Maleic acid, the cis isomer of butenedioic acid, undergoes isomerization to its more stable trans counterpart, fumaric acid, via a reversible cis-trans reaction:
(HOX2C)CH=CH(COX2H)Xcis(HOX2C)CH=CH(COX2H)Xtrans
\ce{(HO2C)CH=CH(CO2H)_{cis} (HO2C)CH=CH(CO2H)_{trans}}
(HOX2C)CH=CH(COX2H)Xcis(HOX2C)CH=CH(COX2H)Xtrans
This process is acid-catalyzed, with the equilibrium strongly favoring fumaric acid due to reduced steric hindrance in the trans configuration. The reaction enthalpy (H) for the isomerization is approximately -4.9 kcal/mol, reflecting the thermodynamic preference for the trans form, while the activation energy is around 15.8 kcal/mol. Rate constants for the forward and reverse reactions vary with conditions, such as temperature and catalyst concentration; for instance, in aqueous solution under hydrothermal conditions at elevated temperatures (e.g., 190 C), the forward rate constant can reach values on the order of 10^{-3} s^{-1} under optimized heating.
Various catalysts facilitate this isomerization. Traditional options include thiourea, which promotes the reaction in aqueous media at moderate temperatures, and halogens such as bromine or bromate ions, which accelerate the process through oxidative mechanisms. A more recent advancement involves thiyl radical catalysis using cysteine or its analogs, enabling efficient isomerization under mild aqueous conditions without harsh acids, as reported by Kosaka et al. in 2025. This method leverages radical addition-elimination pathways for high selectivity at low temperatures (around 4060C).
In educational settings, the isomerization is commonly demonstrated by heating maleic acid in dilute hydrochloric acid (e.g., 12 M HCl at 100C for 3060 minutes), yielding a precipitate of fumaric acid due to its lower solubility. Enzymatic variants employ maleate isomerase, isolated from bacteria like Pseudomonas species, which catalyzes the stereospecific conversion at physiological temperatures and pH, offering a biocatalytic model for the reaction.
Industrially, this isomerization is key for producing high-purity fumaric acid used as a food additive (E297) in beverages and confections, where its tart flavor and solubility properties are valued. Under optimized conditions, such as thiourea catalysis at 2060 C or hydrothermal processes, yields exceed 90%, enabling efficient conversion from maleic acid derived from petrochemical sources.

Other reactions

Maleic acid undergoes dehydration to form maleic anhydride, a key intermediate in organic synthesis, typically achieved by heating the acid or using dehydrating agents such as acetic anhydride. The reaction proceeds as follows:

This transformation is industrially relevant for recovering anhydride from aqueous maleic acid solutions, often involving azeotropic distillation with solvents like xylene to facilitate water removal.
As an electron-deficient alkene, maleic acid serves as a dienophile in Diels-Alder cycloaddition reactions with conjugated dienes, yielding cyclohexene derivatives with vicinal carboxylic groups. For instance, its reaction with 1,3-butadiene produces 4-cyclohexene-1,2-dicarboxylic acid, a useful building block for polymers and pharmaceuticals; the reaction is thermally driven and stereospecific, retaining the cis configuration of the acid. This cycloaddition highlights maleic acid's synthetic utility in constructing six-membered rings.
Hydrogenation of maleic acid reduces the carbon-carbon double bond to produce succinic acid, commonly catalyzed by palladium on carbon (Pd/C) under mild conditions in aqueous media. The stoichiometry is:

This process achieves high selectivity (>98%) and conversion, with water promoting the reaction by stabilizing intermediates and lowering activation barriers; it is a sustainable route for bio-based succinic acid production.
Addition reactions across the double bond include hydration to form malic acid and halogenation to yield dihalo derivatives. Hydration, often acid- or base-catalyzed, adds water in a cis manner to give DL-malic acid (2-hydroxybutanedioic acid), with yields up to 90% under hydrothermal conditions without added catalysts. Halogenation, such as bromination, proceeds via electrophilic addition, predominantly trans in acidic media, forming 2,3-dibromosuccinic acid, which demonstrates the stereochemical preferences influenced by the cis geometry of maleic acid.
Maleic acid participates in free-radical polymerization, particularly as a comonomer in the synthesis of unsaturated polyester resins, where its double bond copolymerizes with styrene via initiation by peroxides. These resins, formed from maleic anhydride (derived from the acid) and glycols like propylene glycol, exhibit enhanced mechanical strength and thermal stability due to the rigid structure imparted by maleic units, finding widespread use in composites and coatings.

Derivatives and Applications

Maleic anhydride

Maleic anhydride is the cyclic dicarboxylic anhydride derived from maleic acid through intramolecular dehydration, with the molecular formula CHO. This five-membered ring structure incorporates a double bond between two carbon atoms, each attached to a carbonyl group, distinguishing it from the open-chain form of maleic acid (CHO). The formation reaction is represented as:
C4H4O4C4H2O3+H2O
\mathrm{C_4H_4O_4 \rightarrow C_4H_2O_3 + H_2O}
C4H4O4C4H2O3+H2O
This dehydration is endothermic, with a standard enthalpy change of approximately +35 kJ/mol, requiring elevated temperatures typically above 100 C to proceed efficiently.
The physical properties of maleic anhydride include a melting point of 52.8 C and a boiling point of 202 C at standard pressure, appearing as colorless crystalline needles or flakes with an acrid odor. Its density is about 1.48 g/cm at 20 C. The compound exhibits high reactivity primarily due to the strain in its five-membered ring, which facilitates ring-opening reactions and enhances its electrophilicity at the carbonyl carbons.
Industrially, maleic anhydride is produced mainly through the partial oxidation of hydrocarbons like benzene or n-butane using vanadium-based catalysts, though it can also be synthesized via dehydration of maleic acid in processes involving fractionation columns to separate the anhydride from water. Global production volume reached approximately 2.6 million metric tons in 2024, driven largely by demand in polymer and resin manufacturing.
In terms of reactivity, maleic anhydride readily undergoes hydrolysis in the presence of water to revert to maleic acid, a reversible process that occurs under mild conditions. It serves as an effective acylating agent, reacting with nucleophiles such as alcohols to form esters or amines to produce amides, owing to the electrophilic nature of its anhydride functionality.

Maleates

Maleates are the salts and esters derived from maleic acid, a dicarboxylic acid that readily undergoes reactions to form these compounds due to its acidity. The salts, known as maleates, are produced through neutralization of maleic acid with bases, resulting in either divalent maleate salts (fully deprotonated) or monovalent hydrogen maleate salts (partially deprotonated). For instance, treatment with sodium hydroxide yields disodium maleate, a common water-soluble salt used in various applications.
Esters of maleic acid, also referred to as maleates, are synthesized via esterification reactions, typically the Fischer esterification method involving the acid and an alcohol in the presence of an acid catalyst. A representative example is the preparation of dimethyl maleate from maleic acid and methanol, as shown in the following equation:

This equilibrium reaction is driven forward by removal of water and is widely employed for producing these esters on laboratory and industrial scales.
The salts of maleic acid generally exhibit enhanced water solubility compared to the parent acid; for example, disodium maleate is freely soluble in water. Additionally, maleate salts can function as chelating agents, forming stable complexes with metal ions through their carboxylate groups, which is useful in applications requiring metal ion sequestration. In biochemical contexts, the maleate ion acts as an inhibitor of transaminase enzymes, interfering with amino group transfer reactions and thereby serving as a tool in enzymatic studies.
Common examples of maleates include disodium maleate, which is employed in buffer solutions and as a metabolite in biological systems; calcium maleate, a less soluble salt used in some nutritional formulations; dimethyl maleate, a volatile liquid ester applied in organic synthesis; and diethyl maleate, another ester noted for its role in Diels-Alder reactions and as a glutathione depleting agent in research.

Industrial and pharmaceutical uses

Maleic acid is widely utilized in the industrial production of resins and polymers, where it serves as a key building block for unsaturated polyester resins, which account for a significant portion of its consumption and are essential in composites for construction, automotive, and marine applications. It is also incorporated as a copolymer component in resin formulations, such as those combined with styrene to create adhesives and coatings with enhanced adhesion and durability. Additionally, maleic acid acts as an intermediate in the synthesis of glyoxylic acid through ozonolysis processes, yielding high selectivity for this compound used in further chemical and personal care applications, and in the formulation of lubricant additives to improve viscosity and stability.
In the pharmaceutical sector, maleic acid is employed to form maleate salts of active pharmaceutical ingredients, enhancing their solubility and chemical stability for better formulation and bioavailability; a prominent example is chlorpheniramine maleate, an antihistamine used to treat allergic reactions, hay fever, rhinitis, urticaria, and asthma. These salts allow for improved drug delivery in solid dosage forms by adjusting microenvironment pH and preventing degradation.
Beyond these core applications, maleic acid is incorporated into hair spray formulations for its conditioning and smoothing effects on damaged hair by strengthening internal bonds and sealing the cuticle. It contributes to the production of artificial resins for various surface coatings and acts as a preservative in food, beverages, and personal care products to inhibit microbial growth and extend shelf life.
The global demand for maleic acid has exhibited steady growth since 2020, with a compound annual growth rate of approximately 5.8%, largely driven by expanding applications in polymers and resins amid rising industrial needs in automotive and construction sectors. In synthetic chemistry, maleic acid participates in Diels-Alder reactions as a dienophile to produce bicyclic intermediates, which are valuable for creating pharmaceuticals, agrochemicals, and advanced materials through efficient cycloaddition processes.

Biological and Environmental Aspects

Biological occurrence and role

Maleic acid occurs naturally in trace amounts in various plants, including ginseng, tobacco leaves, sour cherry fruits, cacao plants, and corn, where it is not considered a major metabolite but rather a minor component potentially arising from photooxidation processes such as the degradation of linolenic acid. These low concentrations highlight its limited role in primary plant metabolism compared to structurally similar compounds like fumaric acid.
In biological systems, maleic acid serves as a substrate for specific enzymes in certain bacteria, notably maleate isomerase, which catalyzes the cis-trans isomerization of maleic acid to fumaric acid as part of aerobic degradation pathways, such as those involved in nicotinate metabolism in species like Pseudomonas putida and Pseudomonas fluorescens. This enzymatic conversion allows bacteria to utilize maleic acid as a carbon source, integrating it into broader metabolic routes like the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle after isomerization. Studies of this enzyme have contributed to understanding bacterial adaptation to environmental pollutants and enzyme kinetics in isomerization reactions.
Biochemically, maleic acid exhibits inhibitory effects on key enzymes, including aconitase in the citric acid cycle, where it disrupts the conversion of citrate to isocitrate, leading to metabolic perturbations in vivo at elevated concentrations. This inhibition has been leveraged in research to probe enzyme mechanisms and respiratory pathways, revealing maleic acid's role as a tool for studying thiol-dependent reactions and energy homeostasis in cellular models. In animal studies, high doses of maleic acid disrupt overall metabolism, inducing Fanconi-like syndromes through interference with renal energy production, with oral LD50 values reported as 708 mg/kg in rats and 2,400 mg/kg in mice.
Recent research in the 2020s has focused on microbial degradation pathways, demonstrating that bacteria such as Pseudomonas species can biodegrade maleic acid under varying environmental conditions, including atmospheric simulations where pH and light influence enzymatic activity and survival rates. These studies underscore maleic acid's biodegradability in microbial communities, with implications for environmental remediation of related pollutants like maleic anhydride derivatives.

Safety and toxicity

Maleic acid is classified as harmful if swallowed, with an oral LD50 in rats of 708 mg/kg, indicating moderate acute toxicity via ingestion. It causes severe irritation to the eyes and skin upon contact, potentially leading to burns or allergic reactions with repeated exposure. Inhalation of dust or vapors irritates the respiratory tract, causing coughing, shortness of breath, and mucosal inflammation.
Chronic exposure to maleic acid may result in renal toxicity, including kidney damage observed in animal studies at repeated doses. It is not classified as carcinogenic by the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC), with no evidence of mutagenic or carcinogenic effects in available data.
Safe handling requires personal protective equipment (PPE), including chemical-resistant gloves, protective clothing, safety goggles, and a face shield to prevent skin and eye contact. Respiratory protection, such as a NIOSH-approved dust mask or respirator, is recommended in dusty environments or if ventilation is inadequate. Minimize dust generation during transfer and use local exhaust ventilation.
Under EU REACH regulations, maleic acid is registered without specific authorization or restriction requirements for its uses, though it is subject to standard risk management measures. The Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) has not established a specific permissible exposure limit (PEL) for maleic acid, but general controls for irritant dusts apply, recommending exposure below levels causing irritation.
For first aid, if ingested, rinse the mouth and do not induce vomiting; seek immediate medical attention. In case of skin contact, remove contaminated clothing and wash affected areas with plenty of water for at least 15 minutes; seek medical advice if irritation persists. For eye exposure, flush eyes with water for 15 minutes while holding eyelids open and consult a physician. If inhaled, move to fresh air and provide oxygen if breathing is difficult; medical evaluation is advised for persistent symptoms.
Storage guidelines include keeping maleic acid in a cool, dry, well-ventilated area away from incompatible materials like bases, oxidizing agents, and metals. Containers should be tightly sealed to prevent moisture absorption and dust release, and stored locked to restrict access.

Environmental impact

The production of maleic acid, primarily through the hydrolysis of maleic anhydride derived from n-butane oxidation, generates emissions including volatile organic compounds (VOCs) such as unreacted hydrocarbons and carbon dioxide (CO2) as a major by-product from the partial oxidation process. In n-butane-based processes, approximately 60% of the reacted butane yields maleic anhydride, with the remainder forming CO2, carbon monoxide, and water vapor, contributing to greenhouse gas emissions estimated at around 972 kg CO2 per metric ton of product in related oxidation routes. The hydrolysis step to form maleic acid from maleic anhydride involves water addition, with relatively low additional environmental burden due to minimal wastewater generation compared to the upstream oxidation, though overall water usage in integrated processes supports sustainability assessments.
Maleic acid exhibits high biodegradability under aerobic conditions, achieving over 97% degradation within 28 days in standardized tests, qualifying it as readily biodegradable per OECD Guideline 301B criteria (which require at least 60% removal in 28 days). Its persistence in the environment is low, as it hydrolyzes rapidly in aqueous systems and does not accumulate in soil or water.
Bioaccumulation potential is negligible, with an octanol-water partition coefficient (log Kow) ranging from -1.3 to -0.48, indicating high hydrophilicity and poor partitioning into lipids. Aquatic toxicity is low to moderate, with LC50 values for fish ranging from 5 mg/L (fathead minnow) to >300 mg/L in acute exposure tests.
Waste from maleic acid production and use is managed through recycling in the polymer industry, where residues are recovered for reuse in resin manufacturing to minimize disposal. Discharges are regulated under U.S. EPA guidelines for industrial effluents, including limits on chemical oxygen demand and pH in wastewater under the Clean Water Act, with maleic acid exempt from certain pesticide tolerances when used as a stabilizer but subject to general hazardous waste rules if characteristics like corrosivity are present.
Sustainability efforts in the 2020s include a shift toward bio-based feedstocks, such as biomass-derived furfural and levulinic acid, enabling catalytic production routes that reduce fossil fuel dependence and CO2 emissions by up to 50% compared to traditional petrochemical methods. These advancements leverage renewable resources like agricultural residues, aligning with broader trends in green chemistry for C4 dicarboxylic acids.
