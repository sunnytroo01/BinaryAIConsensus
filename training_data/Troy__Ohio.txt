 Troy is a city in Miami County, Ohio, United States, serving as the county seat of the county. As of the 2020 United States census, the city had a population of 26,305. Located along the Great Miami River approximately 20 miles north of Dayton, Troy occupies a strategic position near the intersection of Interstate 75 and U.S. Route 40, facilitating its role in regional logistics and manufacturing.
Historically, Troy has been a center for industrial innovation, exemplified by the WACO Aircraft Company, which manufactured biplanes contributed to World War II efforts before ceasing operations in 1947. The city's economy continues to emphasize advanced manufacturing and aerospace, with key employers including UTC Aerospace Systems and F&P America, underscoring its evolution from early 19th-century settlementnamed evocatively after the ancient city of Troyto a modern hub integrated into the Dayton metropolitan area.

History

Founding and early settlement (18071850)

!
Miami County was organized by the Ohio General Assembly in 1807, and in the summer of that year, commissioners Jesse Newport, Daniel Wilson, and Joseph Lamb selected a site on the west bank of the Great Miami River as the county seat, purchasing land from Aaron Tullis, William Barbee, Alexander McCullough, and W. H. Gahagan. The town was platted by surveyor Andrew Wallace, who filed the initial plat on December 2, 1807, laying out 87 lots beginning at Water and Clay Streets. Named Troy after the ancient city in Asia Minor, the settlement reflected the classical naming conventions common among early American pioneers.
The first permanent structure in Troy was a log tavern built by Benjamin Overfield, a son of a Revolutionary War veteran from Pennsylvania, completed by fall 1808 on Lot 2 at the corner of Water and Mulberry Streets; it opened for business on September 13, 1808, and also served initially as the county courthouse on its upper floor. Early settlers included William Barbee, who established the first blacksmith shop, and Joseph Culbertson, who began wool hat manufacturing in 1808 with apprentices. Other arrivals by 1811 included physician Dr. Asa Coleman, while saddler Squire Brown contributed to local crafts and later served as justice of the peace. Many early residents were Revolutionary War veterans or their descendants seeking economic opportunities in the Northwest Territory.
Troy was formally incorporated as a village in 1814, with a post office established by 1824. Community institutions emerged quickly: Methodists constructed the first log church near Main and Clay Streets in 1812, and the inaugural school opened in 1813 at Market and Water Streets, with John G. Clarke teaching by 1816. The completion of the Miami and Erie Canal to Troy in 1837 facilitated trade and migration, spurring modest population growth and small-scale industries like foundries and wagon works by the 1840s, though the arrival of the first railroad from Dayton in 1850 began shifting transportation dynamics. By mid-century, Troy had evolved from a frontier outpost into a functional county seat with basic civic infrastructure.

Canal and railroad expansion (18501900)

The Miami and Erie Canal continued to support commerce in Troy during the early 1850s, with its local infrastructureincluding a culvert constructed in 1837 and Lock 12, opened the same year under the attendance of General William Henry Harrisonenabling the shipment of agricultural products and manufactured goods northward toward Lake Erie. However, canal traffic began declining as railroads offered superior speed and capacity; by the mid-1850s, rail lines had largely supplanted water transport for bulk freight in the region, rendering the canal's role in Troy marginal for commercial purposes despite its prior contributions to population growth and milling operations.
Railroad development accelerated after the arrival of the first train from Dayton on approximately March 28, 1851, which connected Troy to southern markets via an early line extending from the city limits. This initial north-south route, later incorporated into the Cincinnati, Hamilton and Dayton Railroad system, facilitated passenger and freight movement, with extensions northward to Piqua by the mid-1850s under the Dayton and Michigan Railroad, leased to the CH&D in 1863. The infrastructure spurred local industry, as evidenced by increased butter production shipments, positioning Troy as an intermediate hub between Dayton and northern Ohio lines.
Further expansion occurred in the 1880s with the chartering of the Piqua and Troy Branch Railroad in 1881, which connected to the Baltimore and Ohio system and added east-west linkages, enhancing Troy's rail network to approximately 10 miles of trackage by 1900. These developments, totaling over 8,900 miles of track statewide by century's end, prioritized freight efficiency over canals, with Troy's stationssuch as the Dayton and Michigan depot at South Clay and East Main Streetsserving until the 1890s before repurposing. By 1900, railroads had fully eclipsed canal transport, driving economic shifts without the seasonal limitations of water routes.

Industrial growth and 20th-century developments (19002000)

The Hobart Electrical Manufacturing Company, established in 1897 by Charles C. Hobart in Troy, drove early 20th-century industrial expansion through innovations in electric motors and commercial kitchen equipment. Initially focused on engines and generators, the firm shifted toward food processing machinery, introducing advancements that mechanized manual tasks in baking and meat grinding. By the 1910s, Hobart had become a major employer, with its production of mixers and slicers supporting national foodservice growth.
Hobart Brothers Company, incorporated in 1917 by C.C. Hobart alongside his wife Lou Ella, specialized in welding electrodes and equipment, capitalizing on wartime demands during World War I and further expanding in the interwar period. The Hobart Cabinet Company, another Hobart family venture, produced metal cabinets and furniture, diversifying local output. These firms, clustered in Troy, benefited from proximity to rail lines established in the prior century, facilitating raw material imports and product distribution. World War II spurred additional growth, as Ohio's manufacturing sector, including Troy's contributions to food processing and welding for military logistics, saw heightened production under federal contracts.
Postwar economic booms in the 1950s and 1960s reinforced Troy's manufacturing orientation, with Hobart designating its Troy facility as world headquarters in 1968 amid expansions in global exports. The Troy Sunshade Company, evolving from carriage toppers to automotive and furniture components, exemplified secondary industrial maturation. Other niche producers, such as Lorimer Manufacturing's phonograph motors from 1919 and Skinner Irrigation systems, added to the employment base, though Hobart entities dominated output value. By century's end, these developments had transformed Troy from agrarian roots into a hub for durable goods, with manufacturing comprising a core economic pillar despite national shifts toward services.

Recent historical events (2000present)

Troy's population grew steadily in the early 21st century, rising from 21,999 in the 2000 U.S. Census to 25,262 in 2010, reflecting suburban expansion and economic stability in Miami County. This upward trend persisted, with the population reaching 26,305 by the 2020 Census, driven by job opportunities from major employers like Hobart Corporation and regional manufacturing.
Severe weather events disrupted the community multiple times. On February 28, 2011, heavy rainfall combined with rapid snowmelt from prior storms caused widespread flooding along the Great Miami River, prompting flood warnings and road closures in Troy as water levels exceeded minor flood stage. The Great Miami River gauge at Troy recorded levels approaching 16 feet, flooding low-lying areas near Route 25A and the county fairgrounds, though levees mitigated worse damage within the city core. Similar flash flooding occurred on May 20, 2014, when intense thunderstorms dumped several inches of rain, inundating downtown streets, stranding vehicles, and requiring emergency responses to clear debris and rescue motorists.
Economic development efforts intensified amid broader Ohio manufacturing challenges, where the state lost over 3,500 factories between 2001 and 2011. The Troy Development Council, a public-private partnership, supported job retention and expansion, contributing to nearly 7,000 new positions since its inception, including incentives like enterprise zone tax credits for capital investments. By 2023, employment in Troy stood at approximately 13,100, with manufacturing remaining a key sector despite national declines. The city also maintained loan programs since 1990 to bolster local businesses, adapting to post-2008 recession pressures through diversification into logistics and advanced manufacturing.

Geography

Location and physical features

Troy is located in central Miami County, in the western region of the U.S. state of Ohio, serving as the county seat. The city lies along the Great Miami River, approximately 19 miles (31 km) north of Dayton, and forms part of the Dayton metropolitan statistical area. Its geographic coordinates are approximately 40.0395N 84.2033W.
The terrain surrounding Troy consists primarily of the flat to gently rolling landscape characteristic of the Great Miami River valley, shaped by glacial deposits and alluvial soils that support agriculture in the broader Miami Valley region. The city itself occupies a total area of 11.98 square miles (31.03 km), with 11.76 square miles (30.46 km) comprising land and 0.22 square miles (0.57 km) covered by water, much of which relates to the river and associated waterways.
Elevation in Troy averages 837 feet (255 meters) above sea level, with variations due to the river's floodplain. The Great Miami River bisects the city, with the central business district and main urban areas positioned south and west of the waterway; flood protection levees have been constructed along the river to mitigate historical inundation risks.

Recent economic initiatives and challenges

The Troy Development Council, a public-private partnership established in 1984, has driven economic growth by attracting international companies such as Honda and Collins Aerospace, facilitating nearly 7,000 new jobs and over $1 billion in investments primarily from business expansions. The council promotes Troy's strategic location at the I-75/I-70 interchange, emphasizing workforce development, clean energy initiatives, and business competitiveness to support ongoing job retention and diversification. Complementing these efforts, the city's Economic Development Revolving Loan Fund provides gap financing to businesses using federal funds, with program updates announced in October 2025 to sustain local business support since its inception in 1990.
In 2024, the Economic Development Department revised the Residential Exterior Loan program to offer funding for repairs on owner-occupied homes, aiming to enhance housing stock and community appeal. For 2025, capital projects include the $12.3 million Great Miami River Recreation Connectivity initiative, largely grant-funded at $11.1 million from sources like the Ohio Strategic Fund and H2Ohio, involving low-head dam removal, new trails, and Herrlinger Park upgrades to boost tourism and recreational economic activity. Infrastructure demands from steady growth, such as the multi-year West Main Street reconstruction and upgrades to century-old downtown buildings, strain development timelines and resources. Despite robust home construction and business interest, balancing expansion with housing availability remains a persistent issue amid national trends.

Government and Politics

Municipal government structure

Troy, Ohio, operates under a statutory council-mayor form of government pursuant to the Ohio Revised Code, with the mayor serving as the chief executive and the city council as the legislative body. The mayor is elected citywide to a four-year term, enforces municipal ordinances, supervises administrative departments, prepares the annual budget for council approval, and may veto legislation subject to council override. 
The city council consists of nine memberssix elected from single-member wards and three elected at-largeeach serving two-year terms, with elections held biennially. A president of council, elected separately, presides over meetings, appoints council committees, and assigns legislative matters to them for review and recommendation in the form of resolutions or ordinances. Council committees address issues such as finance, public safety, and planning before full council votes, ensuring legislative proposals align with city priorities.
Additional independently elected officials include the city auditor, who oversees financial records and audits; the treasurer, responsible for revenue collection and fiscal management; and the law director, who provides legal counsel and represents the city in litigationall serving four-year terms. This structure emphasizes separation of powers, with council holding authority over taxation, zoning, and appropriations, while the mayor directs day-to-day operations without a professional city manager.

Culture and Recreation

Historic sites and preservation

Troy preserves numerous structures from its founding in 1807, with downtown featuring intact 19th-century commercial and public buildings that illustrate the city's role as a Miami County seat and early Ohio frontier hub. Key sites include the Overfield Tavern, constructed in 1808 by Benjamin Overfield as a two-story hewn-log building along the Great Miami River, which functioned as a social and civic center, serving as the county's first courthouse from 1808 to 1811 and operating as a tavern until 1824. The tavern, now the Overfield Tavern Museum, houses artifacts depicting pre-1850 frontier life and was added to the National Register of Historic Places in 1976; recent excavations following a 2025 fire uncovered 19th-century coins, pig bones, and an arrowhead beneath the structure.
The Miami County Courthouse, built in 1841, stands as a prominent Greek Revival edifice central to Troy's public square and has faced deterioration challenges, earning designation as one of Ohio's most endangered historic sites in 2023 by Preservation Ohio due to structural issues despite not being a certified historic site at the time. In response, the Troy Historic Preservation Alliance raised over $1 million in donations for repairs by August 2025, transferring ownership to Glass City Holdings in January of that year to facilitate rehabilitation and adaptive reuse. Other notable preserved properties include the Asa Mayo House at 11 North Walnut Street, erected in 1841 and utilized as an Underground Railroad station during the Civil War.
Troy maintains a designated Historic District bounded by the Miami River to the north, Clay Street to the east, Canal Street to the south, and Oxford Street to the west, encompassing sites of architectural and historical significance protected under city zoning ordinances. The city code establishes Historic Preservation Overlay Districts to safeguard structures with historical, architectural, or cultural value, requiring review for alterations to prevent demolition or incompatible modifications.
The nonprofit Troy Historic Preservation Alliance, focused on restoration and repurposing, advocates for these assets through public campaigns, research on property histories, and collaboration with developers, complementing state incentives like the Ohio Historic Preservation Tax Credit program for rehabilitating significant buildings. Additional efforts include the Museum of Troy History in a mid-19th-century brick home at 125 East Water Street, which documents local heritage through exhibits and records.

Troy High School's Trojans athletics program competes in the Miami Valley League across sports including football, basketball, soccer, track, cross country, and ice hockey, with home games at Troy Memorial Stadium. Golf enthusiasts access the Troy Country Club, an 18-hole course established for public and member play in the Upper Miami Valley.
Outdoor pursuits center on the city's 26 parks spanning 365 acres, supplemented by over 30 miles of trails for hiking, biking, and birdwatching. Key sites include Duke Park with multi-use loops and mountain biking trails, and the Brukner Nature Center offering 6 miles of woodland, wetland, and prairie paths for wildlife observation. The Great Miami River Recreational Trail provides 32 miles of paved path along the river for running, cycling, and fishing, connecting to broader Miami County networks. Kayaking and canoeing occur on the Great Miami River, while the Miami County Park District preserves areas like Twin Arch Reserve for passive recreation and nature exploration.
