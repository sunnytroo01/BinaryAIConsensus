Black Elk
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Heka Spa (c. December 1863 August 19, 1950), known as Black Elk, was an Oglala Lakota holy man and medicine man who experienced a profound vision during childhood illness around age nine, guiding his role as a spiritual leader amid the Lakota's conflicts with the United States in the late 19th century. As a youth, he participated in the Battle of the Little Bighorn in 1876 and later, as an adult, was wounded during the Wounded Knee Massacre in 1890 while involved in Ghost Dance rituals. In 1904, following the death of his first wife, Black Elk converted to Roman Catholicism, receiving the baptismal name Nicholas, and subsequently served as a catechist on the Pine Ridge Reservation, actively promoting Christian teachings while retaining knowledge of traditional Lakota practices. His oral accounts formed the basis of Black Elk Speaks (1932), mediated by poet John G. Neihardt, which popularized Lakota cosmology globally but drew criticism for Neihardt's interpretive additions that amplified mystical elements and minimized Black Elk's Catholic commitments, reflecting the author's poetic agenda over verbatim transcription. Despite such mediations, Black Elk's documented visions and healings contributed to preserving Lakota spiritual traditions amid cultural erosion, influencing later Native American revitalization efforts.

Early Life

Birth and Family

Black Elk, whose Lakota name was Heka Spa, was born in December 1863 along the Little Powder River in what is now Wyoming territory, into an Oglala Lakota family noted for its paternal lineage of medicine men and healers. His father, also named Black Elk, was a warrior whose cousin was the father of the renowned Lakota leader Crazy Horse, though the family held no chiefly titles prior to that era. Black Elk identified himself as the fourth individual in his paternal line to bear the name, with his paternal grandfather having been a prominent medicine man killed in a skirmish with Pawnee warriors.
His mother was White Cow Sees, daughter of Refuse-to-Go and Plenty Eagle Feathers, whom Black Elk recalled from his early childhood as part of the extended family network that emphasized Lakota spiritual and warrior traditions. The family's nomadic lifestyle followed the seasonal movements of the Oglala band, integrating hunting, warfare, and ceremonial practices amid the pre-reservation era of Lakota autonomy on the Northern Plains. No verifiable records detail additional siblings, though Black Elk's accounts highlight the clan's emphasis on male roles in vision quests and combat from a young age.

Childhood and Early Visions

Black Elk was born in December 1863 along the Little Powder River in present-day Wyoming to an Oglala Lakota family with a longstanding tradition of medicine men and healers. He was the fourth individual in his paternal line to bear the name Black Elk and was a second cousin to the Oglala leader Crazy Horse. His family included one brother and five sisters, and they followed the nomadic Lakota lifestyle of seasonal migrations and buffalo hunting, which was increasingly disrupted by U.S. military incursions and settler expansion during his early years.
From approximately age five, Black Elk began experiencing minor visions amid the cultural and territorial upheavals affecting his people. These early spiritual encounters foreshadowed a more profound event at age nine in the summer of 1872, when he fell gravely ill and lapsed into a trance-like state lasting several days.
In what became known as his Great Vision, Black Elk described being carried by thunder beings to a council with the Six Grandfathersspiritual entities embodying the four cardinal directions, the earth, and the skywho conferred upon him sacred gifts symbolizing powers for healing, renewal, and protection of his people. Key among these were a pipestone pipe, a flowering stick representing life, and herbs for medicinal purposes, which he understood as a mandate to restore harmony to the Lakota world. He concealed the full details of this vision for years, sharing them only later in adolescence during traditional rites, as premature revelation was believed to invite danger. The account originates from Black Elk's oral recollections, later documented by poet John G. Neihardt, whose interpretive rendering in Black Elk Speaks (1932) emphasizes mythic elements but draws directly from the holy man's words.

Participation in Lakota Resistance

Black Elk, born in December 1863 to the Oglala Lakota, participated in the Great Sioux War of 18761877 as a young boy amid resistance to United States military campaigns enforcing the Treaty of Fort Laramie violations and gold prospectors' incursions into the Black Hills. At approximately 12 years old, he joined his band's warriors under his second cousin Crazy Horse in engagements against U.S. forces, reflecting the broader Lakota effort to defend sacred lands and hunting grounds against federal expansion.
On June 17, 1876, Black Elk was present at the Battle of the Rosebud in present-day Montana, where Crazy Horse's Oglala and Cheyenne allies clashed with General George Crook's column of over 1,000 troops and scouts, delaying Crook's advance and contributing to the subsequent concentration of Lakota forces. Though young, he accompanied the fighters, as recounted in his later narratives linking the Rosebud skirmishdescribed as Crazy Horse "whipping" Crookto the momentum building toward the decisive victory at Little Bighorn.
Eight days later, on June 2526, 1876, during the Battle of the Little Bighorn (known to the Lakota as the Battle of the Greasy Grass), Black Elk, then 12, actively engaged despite his youth. Initially tasked with tending horses, he delivered a rifle to his older brother amid the assault on Major Marcus Reno's detachment, then joined warriors in the timber along the river, where he witnessed intense close-quarters fighting. Under an elder's direction, he used a captured six-shooter to scalp a wounded U.S. soldier, claiming his first scalp, which he later presented to his mother as a mark of transition toward warrior status. The Lakota and Cheyenne forces overwhelmed Lieutenant Colonel George Custer's battalion, annihilating it in a significant, though temporary, triumph that heightened U.S. resolve for retaliation.
Following these battles, Black Elk remained with Crazy Horse's non-treaty band through the harsh winter of 18761877, evading U.S. pursuit amid dwindling resources and internal divisions. On May 6, 1877, he and his family surrendered at the Red Cloud Agency in Nebraska as part of Crazy Horse's group, documented in the official ledger listing Black Elk alongside relatives and warriors, ending organized armed resistance for his band. This capitulation, driven by starvation and military pressure, confined the Lakota to reservations and marked the close of Black Elk's early combat involvement, though his health had begun declining from visionary illnesses around age 9.

Mid-Life Challenges and Travels

Tour with Buffalo Bill's Wild West

In 1886, Black Elk, then approximately 23 years old, departed from the Pine Ridge Reservation to join Buffalo Bill's Wild West show as a performer. The troupe featured Lakota Sioux, including Black Elk, in demonstrations of horsemanship, traditional dances, and reenactments of frontier battles, performing in major U.S. cities such as Omaha and Chicago. These spectacles drew large audiences, showcasing Native American skills alongside cowboys and marksmanship by William F. Cody himself.
The show embarked on a European tour in 1887, where Black Elk participated in performances across England, including a command appearance before Queen Victoria during her Golden Jubilee celebrations in London. Additional stops included Manchester, exposing him to urban environments and diverse spectators. Following the conclusion of Buffalo Bill's engagements in late 1887, Black Elk and three companions were inadvertently left behind in England without funds or means of return, stranding them abroad.
Unable to communicate effectively in English, the group eventually connected with Mexican Joe's rival Wild West exhibition in London and integrated into its performances. This alternative troupe toured continental Europe, visiting Germany, France, and Italy, where Black Elk continued exhibiting Lakota customs and equestrian feats through 1888. During this period, he formed a romantic connection with a French woman and endured illnesses, including a severe sickness that prompted reflections on his visions. Black Elk rejoined Buffalo Bill's company in early 1889 and returned to the United States that year, concluding his approximately three-year involvement with Wild West exhibitions.

Ghost Dance Participation and Wounded Knee Massacre

Black Elk returned to the Pine Ridge Reservation in 1889 following his European tour, where he encountered reports of the Ghost Dance movement originating from Paiute prophet Wovoka (Jack Wilson) in Nevada. The dance, which promised spiritual renewal, the return of the buffalo, and the expulsion of white settlers through ritual performance rather than violence, rapidly spread among the Lakota Sioux by late 1889, attracting approximately 25,000 practitioners amid widespread famine and reservation hardships. Black Elk initially resisted but soon participated, becoming one of its leaders on Pine Ridge, viewing it as aligned with his earlier visions of renewal.
In early 1890, Black Elk engaged in Ghost Dance ceremonies, during which he experienced a significant vision while dancing; he collapsed into a trance, perceiving himself flying over a verdant "Promised Land" with an eagle-bonneted figure interpreted as a divine messenger, reinforcing his sense of spiritual power and linking the rite to his childhood theophany. These dances involved circular formations, hand-holding, and songs invoking ancestors and the Messiah, often lasting days without food or water, with participants wearing "ghost shirts" believed to repel bullets. U.S. Indian agents, alarmed by the movement's scale and reports of armed gatheringsexacerbated by the killing of Sitting Bull on December 15, 1890requested military intervention, deploying the 7th Cavalry to Pine Ridge. Black Elk interpreted the federal response as confirmation of prophetic warnings against white encroachment.
Black Elk was present near the Wounded Knee Creek camp on December 29, 1890, where about 350 Miniconjou and Hunkpapa Lakota under Chief Spotted Elk (Big Foot), fleeing after Sitting Bull's death, surrendered to the 7th Cavalry under Colonel James W. Forsyth. During the attempted disarmament of roughly 120 able-bodied men, a shotpossibly accidental from a deaf warrior's rifletriggered a massacre; soldiers fired Hotchkiss guns and rifles into the camp, killing an estimated 250-300 Lakota, including over 100 women and children, while 25 soldiers died, some from crossfire. Black Elk, observing from a distance with other Oglala, recounted hearing continuous gunfire for hours, seeing frozen bodies strewn across the snow-covered field, and noting the failure of ghost shirts to protect against bullets, which shattered his expectations of supernatural intervention. He later described the event as a profound tragedy marking the effective end of armed Lakota resistance, with survivors' accounts, including his, emphasizing the chaos and disproportionate force used against largely unarmed people.

Spiritual Transformation

Conversion to Catholicism

In December 1903, Black Elk's wife, Katie War Bonneta Catholic convert whom he had married in 1892died, leaving him with three children who had already been baptized as Catholics. This personal loss occurred amid broader pressures on the Oglala Lakota, including reservation life and missionary influences on Pine Ridge, where Jesuit priests had established missions.
On December 6, 1904the feast day of St. NicholasBlack Elk, then approximately 41 years old, was baptized into the Catholic Church at St. Agnes Mission on the Pine Ridge Reservation, receiving the Christian name Nicholas Black Elk. Historical records from diocesan and Jesuit sources confirm the event, noting his deliberate choice amid a community where traditional Lakota practices coexisted uneasily with Christianity.
Black Elk reportedly perceived compatibility between Catholic teachings and Lakota concepts of Wakan Tanka (the Great Spirit or sacred mystery), viewing the Christian God as aligning with his visionary experiences of a unified sacred reality, though he continued elements of Lakota ritual in parallel. Diocesan investigations for his potential canonization, drawing on eyewitness accounts and mission logs, portray the conversion as a sincere integration rather than mere expediency, despite scholarly debates over the depth of his doctrinal adherence.

Ministry as Catechist and Healer

Following his baptism as Nicholas Black Elk on December 6, 1904the feast of St. Nicholashe began serving as a catechist on the Pine Ridge Reservation in South Dakota, instructing Lakota people in Catholic doctrine amid a scarcity of priests. He collaborated closely with Jesuit missionaries, leading Sunday services that included hymns, prayers, Scripture readings, and preaching, while training others in evangelization. Black Elk employed pictorial aids like the Two Roads catechism, a visual depiction of salvation history adapted for Native audiences, to teach children and adults; a photograph from circa 1928 shows him using such materials with Lakota youth. Despite chronic health issues stemming from childhood illnesses and wounds, he traveled long distances across the reservation on foot or horseback to baptize converts and conduct services, reportedly facilitating around 400 baptisms over decades. 
Parallel to his catechetical duties, Black Elk continued practicing traditional Lakota healing, responding to calls from community members for aid in illness or misfortune. He performed ceremonies involving drumming, incantations, and herbal remedies, as in a documented case where he treated a gravely ill child with such rites before a priest arrived to administer last rites. These efforts persisted post-conversion, sometimes in semi-secrecy, reflecting an ongoing integration of his prior role as a wiha wakn (holy man or medicine man) with Catholic commitments, though accounts from Lakota contemporaries emphasize his deepening sacramental devotion, including self-taught reading of the Lakota Bible and reception of Communion. He viewed these healing acts as compatible with his faith, drawing on visions and prayers oriented toward the "Great Spirit" understood through Christian lenses.
Black Elk sustained this ministry for over 45 years, undeterred by personal losses or reservation hardships, until his death on August 19, 1950, at age 86. Interviews with Lakota elders conducted in the late 20th century, as documented by researcher Michael F. Steltenkamp, affirm his reputation as both a faithful catechist and respected healer who bridged spiritual traditions without fully abandoning Lakota elements. 

Encounters with Western Interpreters

Collaboration with John G. Neihardt

In 1930, John G. Neihardt, Nebraska's poet laureate, visited the Pine Ridge Indian Reservation in South Dakota to gather material from Lakota elders for his epic poem Cycle of the West, during which he first encountered Nicholas Black Elk, then in his late 60s and known as a piwkea (holy man) among the Oglala Lakota. The collaboration deepened with a series of interviews in spring 1931, primarily May 1719 at Manderson on the reservation, where Black Elk shared details of his great vision, life experiences, and Lakota spiritual traditions.
Black Elk spoke in Lakota, with his son Ben Black Elk serving as translator into English; Neihardt's daughter Enid recorded the sessions in shorthand, forming the raw material that Neihardt later edited into narrative form. This process involved multiple layers of mediationoral transmission, sequential translation, and transcriptionwhich scholars note introduced opportunities for interpretive shaping, as the shorthand notes reflect collaborative input between Ben Black Elk and Neihardt during clarification.
Neihardt, driven by literary aims to evoke the epic quality of Lakota history rather than strict anthropological documentation, restructured and embellished elements for dramatic effect in Black Elk Speaks: Being the Life Story of a Holy Man of the Oglala Sioux, published February 19, 1932. Subsequent analysis of the full 1931 transcripts, published in The Sixth Grandfather: Black Elk's Teachings Given to John G. Neihardt (1984), reveals Neihardt's additions, such as amplified mythic language and thematic emphases aligning with his poetic vision, prompting critiques that the book prioritizes Neihardt's artistry over verbatim fidelity to Black Elk's account. Black Elk reportedly endorsed sharing his story to preserve Lakota knowledge for future generations, viewing Neihardt as a sympathetic conduit despite the cultural and linguistic gaps.

Interactions with Joseph Epes Brown

In 1947, Joseph Epes Brown, a scholar of comparative religion and ethnologist then affiliated with the University of Montana, sought out Black Elk on the Pine Ridge Indian Reservation in South Dakota, having been directed there by poet John G. Neihardt, whose earlier collaboration with Black Elk had piqued Brown's interest in Lakota spiritual traditions. Brown, who had prior experience studying Native American practices among Plains tribes, established a close rapport with the 83-year-old Black Elk, living with him for roughly one year to document oral transmissions of Lakota cosmology and ritual.
During this period, Black Elk, as one of the last surviving wicasa wakan (holy men) versed in the pre-reservation Oglala Sioux priesthood, shared the esoteric details of the seven sacred ritesknown collectively as the "religion of the pipe"which encompassed ceremonies such as the Keeping of the Soul, the Rite of Purification (sweat lodge), the Sun Dance, and others revealed through visions to Lakota leaders in the late 18th and 19th centuries. Black Elk emphasized their origins in prophetic visions, including those attributed to figures like the White Buffalo Calf Woman, and stressed their role in maintaining harmony with Wakan Tanka (the Great Mystery), viewing the rites as essential for Lakota renewal amid cultural erosion from assimilation policies and missionization. To symbolize trust and authority, Black Elk presented Brown with his personal pipe bag, a sacred artifact containing the calumet used in these rites, affirming Brown's role as a conduit for preservation rather than initiation.
Brown's approach differed from Neihardt's poetic framing by prioritizing verbatim transcription through interpreters, including Black Elk's son Ben, to capture doctrinal precision over narrative drama, though some scholars note Brown's Perennialist lensdrawing from universalist religious studiesinfluenced selections without fabricating content. This interaction, spanning 1947 to 1948, reflected Black Elk's dual commitment to Catholicism (adopted in 1904) and ancestral traditions, as he sought to safeguard the latter's integrity for future generations before his death on December 2, 1950. The resulting volume, The Sacred Pipe: Black Elk's Account of the Seven Rites of the Oglala Sioux, published in 1953 by the University of Oklahoma Press, remains a primary source for Lakota ritual exegesis, with Brown's preface underscoring Black Elk's insistence on authenticity amid declining practitioners.

Legacy and Debates

Publication of Black Elk Speaks

Black Elk Speaks, subtitled Being the Life Story of a Holy Man of the Oglala Sioux, was first published in 1932 by William Morrow & Company in New York as a hardcover edition illustrated with drawings by the Oglala Lakota artist Standing Bear. The content derived from interviews John G. Neihardt conducted with Black Elk primarily in late 1930 and early 1931 on the Pine Ridge Indian Reservation in South Dakota, where Black Elk's son, Ben Black Elk, served as translator. Neihardt, appointed Poet Laureate of Nebraska in 1921, framed the narrative poetically to convey Black Elk's visions and life experiences within a broader epic of Lakota history.
The 1932 edition appeared amid the Great Depression, garnering modest initial attention and readership among a limited audience interested in Native American oral traditions. Subsequent reprints included a 1947 edition, but widespread popularity emerged with the 1961 University of Nebraska Press edition, which featured a new preface by Neihardt and an introduction by Vine Deloria Jr., aligning it with the era's growing interest in indigenous spirituality. This edition sold millions of copies over decades, establishing the book as a cornerstone text in Native American literature. Later versions, such as the 2008 annotated edition by Raymond J. DeMallie and the 2014 "Complete Edition" incorporating previously omitted material from Neihardt's notes, further refined scholarly access to the original transcripts.

Authenticity Controversies

Critics have questioned the fidelity of Black Elk Speaks (1932) to Black Elk's original accounts, arguing that John G. Neihardt's editorial interventionsconducted during interviews on the Pine Ridge Reservation in December 1930, January 1931, and September 1931imposed a poetic structure and dramatic embellishments that prioritized literary appeal over verbatim accuracy. Neihardt, working through interpreters and his daughter Enid's transcriptions of Lakota-to-English relays, admitted to reconstructing the narrative in his own voice to convey an epic quality, omitting details like Black Elk's extensive Catholic catechist work after 1904 and selectively amplifying pre-reservation spiritual elements.
Scholarly analyses, particularly from the 1980s onward, contend that these adaptations distorted Lakota worldview by essentializing it into a timeless, romanticized archetype consumable by white readers, while downplaying historical contingencies such as colonial disruptions and Black Elk's syncretic faith blending Oglala traditions with Christianity. For instance, comparisons with Joseph Epes Brown's 1947 interviews, published as The Sacred Pipe (1953), reveal variances in ritual descriptionsNeihardt's version heightens visionary mysticism, whereas Brown's ethnographic approach yields more literal, less homogenized depictions of pipe ceremonies and cosmology.
Lakota intellectuals and some descendants have amplified these concerns, labeling the text as a collaborative fabrication that privileges Neihardt's messianic framing over Black Elk's lived pragmatism as a healer who integrated sacraments like baptism into his practice post-1890s. Critics like Julian Rice, in examinations of stenographic notes, alleged inventions such as exaggerated battle scenes, though defenders counter that core visions align with Lakota oral corroborations from the era. These disputes underscore broader issues in transcribing indigenous oral histories through non-native mediators, where linguistic filters and cultural presumptions can yield hybridized outputs unverifiable against originals lost to time.

Canonization Process and Religious Synthesis

The cause for the canonization of Nicholas Black Elk, baptized as Nicholas in 1904, was formally petitioned in the Diocese of Rapid City on March 14, 2016, when over 1,600 signatures were presented to Bishop Robert Gruss advocating for recognition of his heroic virtue as a catechist. On November 14, 2017, the United States Conference of Catholic Bishops approved the canonical consultation for his sainthood cause by voice vote during their fall general assembly in Baltimore. The diocesan investigation phase opened on October 21, 2021, marked by a Mass celebrated by Bishop Gruss at Holy Rosary Church on the Pine Ridge Reservation, where Black Elk had served.
By late 2023, the process had revealed archival evidence of Black Elk's lifelong Catholic devotion, including his role as a catechist who instructed over 400 people in the faith and administered sacraments despite limited formal education. The cause advanced to the Dicastery for the Causes of Saints (formerly Congregation) in Rome, requiring proof of two miracles attributed to his intercession for beatification and canonization. Observers estimate the full process could span over a decade, amid some opposition from Lakota traditionalists who question the compatibility of his Catholic commitment with indigenous practices. 
Black Elk's religious outlook integrated Lakota cultural heritage with orthodox Catholicism, prioritizing the latter as the fulfillment of his spiritual vocation after conversion. Baptized amid the hardships of reservation life, he taught himself to read the Lakota-translated Bible and served as a missionary catechist for nearly 50 years, baptizing and catechizing hundreds while invoking Christ in healings that echoed traditional wicasa wakan roles. He discerned parallels between Lakota cosmologysuch as the sacred hoop representing life's interconnectednessand Christian doctrines of creation and redemption, yet subordinated indigenous elements to sacramental faith, rejecting syncretism that would dilute Catholic orthodoxy. This approach, evident in his prayers blending petitions to the "Great Spirit" with explicit Trinitarian references, reflected a personal synthesis rooted in evangelization rather than cultural relativism, as affirmed by diocesan inquiries into his writings and testimonies. Primary accounts from his lifetime, including letters and mission records, indicate he viewed Catholicism as transformative, not merely additive, to his Lakota identity, countering scholarly narratives that overemphasize pre-conversion visions at the expense of his documented piety.

Broader Cultural Impact

Black Elk Speaks, the 1932 account of Black Elk's life and visions as narrated to John G. Neihardt, has shaped global perceptions of Lakota spirituality and 19th-century Plains Indian cultures, introducing concepts of harmony with nature and the sacred hoop of the world to non-Indigenous audiences. The text's emphasis on interconnectedness and ecological balance has influenced environmental discourse, highlighting Indigenous critiques of resource exploitation and advocating for sustainable land relationships rooted in traditional practices. Scholars note its role in illuminating Lakota worldview's integration with natural cycles, such as timing rituals by seasonal events, which contrasts with industrialized disconnection from the environment.
In Native American activism, particularly during the 1960s and 1970s, the book served as a vital source for reclaiming traditional power visions, influencing figures like Vine Deloria Jr. in efforts to revitalize Indigenous identity and resist assimilation. Black Elk's collaborations extended Lakota oral traditions into world literature, fostering appreciation for Sioux wisdom beyond tribal boundaries and contributing to cultural preservation amid historical disruptions. This dissemination has supported broader Indigenous movements for rights and sovereignty, positioning Black Elk as a symbol of resilience against colonial erasure.
The narrative's literary form as a narrated spiritual autobiography established precedents in Native American literature, dominating scholarly examinations of Plains religions and inspiring ongoing dialogues on cultural synthesis and authenticity. Its enduring appeal lies in bridging Indigenous epistemologies with Western intellectual traditions, though interpretations vary in fidelity to original Lakota contexts.
