American College of Sports Medicine
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) is a professional organization dedicated to advancing sports medicine, exercise science, and health promotion, founded in 1954 by a group of physical educators and physicians who sought to address health issues linked to lifestyle factors such as physical inactivity and smoking.
With nearly 50,000 members and certified professionals from 90 countries, ACSM serves as the world's largest society in its field, encompassing physicians, researchers, educators, and fitness experts committed to evidence-based practices. Its mission is to educate and empower professionals to advance the science and practice of health and human performance.
ACSM's core activities include offering globally recognized certifications, such as for personal trainers and clinical exercise physiologists, to ensure high standards in fitness and rehabilitation. It publishes six peer-reviewed journals, including the flagship Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, along with over 50 books and more than 300 online continuing education courses to disseminate cutting-edge research.
The organization hosts an annual scientific meeting that attracts thousands of attendees for presentations on topics like exercise physiology and injury prevention, while also engaging in advocacy efforts to influence public policy on physical activity.
Notable initiatives include Exercise is Medicine, a global program launched in 2007 in partnership with the American Medical Association to integrate physical activity assessment into clinical care, and the American Fitness Index, which annually ranks U.S. cities on community health metrics. Through the ACSM Foundation, it distributes over $100,000 yearly in grants for research, education, and professional development.

History

Founding

The American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) was formed on April 22, 1954, in New York City as the American Federation of Sports Medicine. This initiative brought together a small group of physical educators and physicians who recognized the critical links between lifestyle choices, physical exercise, and rising health concerns, particularly cardiovascular disease exacerbated by sedentary behaviors and poor habits like smoking. Influenced by earlier international efforts, such as the 1928 establishment of the International Federation of Sports Medicine, the founders aimed to foster collaboration between exercise science and medical fields to advance understanding of physical activity's role in health.
The organization was officially incorporated in 1955 as a nonprofit under its current name, the American College of Sports Medicine. Its 11 founders included eight physical educatorsClifford Brownell, Leonard Larson, Grover Mueller, Neils Neilson, Peter Karpovich, Ernst Jokl, Josephine Rathbone, and Arthur Steinhaus (with Josephine Rathbone as the sole woman)and three cardiologists: Joseph B. Wolffe, Albert Hyman, and Louis Bishop. This interdisciplinary core group, blending expertise from education, physiology, and cardiology, established the organization's foundational structure with three initial divisions: physiology, physical education, and cardiology.
Early ACSM objectives centered on integrating sports medicine and exercise science to combat public health issues stemming from physical inactivity, emphasizing evidence-based strategies to promote active lifestyles and prevent chronic diseases. By prioritizing the physiological impacts of exercise, the organization sought to educate practitioners and inform policy, setting the stage for its expansion into a global leader with nearly 50,000 members today.

Evolution and Milestones

Following its incorporation in 1954, the American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) experienced steady growth in the 1960s, marked by the launch of its official journal, Medicine and Science in Sports, in 1969, which provided a dedicated platform for disseminating research in exercise physiology and sports medicine. This publication, later renamed Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, became a cornerstone for advancing scientific discourse and remains one of the field's most influential journals.
The 1970s represented a pivotal era of professionalization, with ACSM establishing its first certification programs in 1975, initially targeting exercise test technologists and program directors to standardize competencies in clinical exercise testing and prescription. Concurrently, the organization released the inaugural edition of ACSM's Guidelines for Exercise Testing and Prescription in 1975, a seminal resource that has undergone multiple revisions and shaped global standards for safe and effective exercise programming. These initiatives addressed the rising need for evidence-based practices amid growing awareness of physical inactivity's health impacts.
In 1984, ACSM relocated its national headquarters from Madison, Wisconsin, to Indianapolis, Indiana, to centralize operations, facilitate collaboration with institutions like Indiana University-Purdue University Indianapolis, and accommodate expanding administrative and educational activities. This move supported organizational scalability as membership surpassed 13,000 by the early 1990s. By the 2020s, ACSM had grown to nearly 50,000 members and certified professionals worldwide, reflecting its broadened influence in sports medicine and exercise science.
ACSM has actively responded to public health challenges, notably launching the Exercise is Medicine (EIM) initiative in 2007 in partnership with the American Medical Association to integrate physical activity assessment and promotion into routine clinical care, directly targeting the obesity epidemic and sedentary lifestyles. This program emphasizes exercise as a vital sign, with tools for healthcare providers to prescribe activity tailored to patients' needs. In parallel, ACSM has embraced technological advancements through its annual Worldwide Survey of Fitness Trends, initiated in 2006, which highlights innovations like wearable technology and mobile exercise apps to enhance adherence and outcomes in preventing chronic diseases such as obesity. For instance, the organization's 2025 survey positioned wearable technology as the top trend, underscoring its role in real-time monitoring and personalized interventions.

Organizational Structure

Membership Categories

The American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) offers four primary membership categories designed to support individuals at various stages of their careers in sports medicine and exercise science: Alliance of Health Fitness Professionals, Professional, Professional-in-Training, and Students. These categories provide tailored eligibility criteria and benefits to foster professional growth, resource access, and networking within the field.
The Alliance of Health Fitness Professionals category is intended for allies in the fitness industry, such as trainers and facility managers, who may not hold advanced degrees but contribute to exercise promotion. Eligibility requires active involvement in fitness-related roles, without a specific educational threshold. Benefits include access to the Health & Fitness Journal, educational webinars, certification exam discounts, and participation in special interest groups for networking.
Professional membership targets certified experts, including physicians, exercise physiologists, and researchers with relevant credentials. Eligibility typically involves a bachelor's degree or higher in sports medicine, exercise science, or a related discipline, along with professional certification or clinical practice. Members gain exclusive access to peer-reviewed journals like Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, leadership opportunities on committees, mentorship programs, and discounted registration for ACSM conferences and publications.
The Professional-in-Training category supports emerging professionals, such as postdoctoral fellows and residents in medical or clinical programs. Eligibility is limited to those enrolled in accredited training programs, emphasizing career development during transitional phases. Benefits encompass career resources, journal subscriptions, special interest group involvement, and reduced fees for educational events to aid networking and skill-building.
Student membership caters to academic trainees pursuing degrees in exercise science or allied health fields. Eligibility requires full-time enrollment in an accredited college program, verified by credit hours or academic status. Advantages include career webinars, travel grants for conferences, book and certification discounts, and mentorship connections to prepare for professional entry.
As of 2025, ACSM's membership exceeds 50,000 individuals and certified professionals across international, national, and regional levels, enabling a global community dedicated to advancing sports medicine and exercise science.

Regional Chapters

The American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) maintains 11 regional chapters across the United States, each designed to facilitate localized engagement among members in sports medicine and exercise science. These chapters serve as grassroots extensions of the national organization, promoting collaboration, professional development, and the application of ACSM's resources within specific geographic areas. By organizing activities closer to members' locations, the chapters enhance accessibility and foster community-level impact on research and practice.
The chapters are divided by state and territorial groupings to ensure comprehensive coverage of the U.S. states, territories, and the District of Columbia. For example, the Central States Chapter encompasses Arkansas, Kansas, Missouri, and Oklahoma, while the Southeast Chapter covers a broader area including Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Kentucky, Louisiana, Mississippi, North Carolina, Puerto Rico, South Carolina, Tennessee, and Virginia. As of 2024, regional chapters engage about 7,900 members (29% of ACSM's total), with annual meetings attracting over 5,000 attendees and distributing around $87,000 in awards, grants, and scholarships.
Each chapter plays a vital role in hosting local events, such as annual meetings that blend research presentations, clinical discussions, and educational sessions tailored to regional interests. These gatherings, which collectively attract thousands of attendees annuallyfor instance, over 5,000 across all chapters in recent yearsprovide discounted registration to encourage broad participation, including for students at $15 and professionals at $35. Networking opportunities are central, enabling members to connect with peers, collaborate on projects, and assume leadership roles within the chapter. Additionally, chapters disseminate ACSM's national resources, such as position statements and guidelines, at the community level through workshops, awards programs, and grants totaling over $87,000 in recent distributions to support local initiatives.
The establishment of regional chapters began in the early 1970s as ACSM sought to decentralize its efforts and build stronger ties with members beyond the national level. The Midwest Chapter was the first, receiving a provisional charter in 1972 to serve as a model for localized organization. This was followed by rapid expansion: the Southeast Chapter formed in 1973 with a founding meeting in Gatlinburg, Tennessee, attended by representatives from seven states, and gained its permanent charter in 1976. The Central States Chapter, evolving from the original Midwest framework, secured its permanent status in 1975 and has since emphasized grassroots involvement for students and professionals in its four-state region. Other chapters emerged throughout the decade, with the Southwest Chapter achieving permanent recognition in 1985 after provisional efforts dating to 1977, incorporating states like California and Arizona to promote regional research collaboration. This evolution reflects ACSM's strategic growth to 11 chapters by the 1980s, enabling sustained promotion of sports medicine research and practice through decentralized, community-focused structures that now engage approximately 29% of the organization's total membership.

Governance and Leadership

The American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) is governed by a Board of Trustees, which holds ultimate authority over the organization's policies, strategic direction, and operations, as outlined in the ACSM Constitution. The Board consists of approximately 30 trustees, including officers and elected members serving staggered three-year terms, with elections conducted annually among eligible members to ensure continuity and diverse representation from fields like exercise physiology, sports medicine, and public health. These elections, managed through a structured nomination and voting process facilitated by the Nominating Committee, select trustees and key officers to align leadership with ACSM's mission of advancing health through scientific discovery and evidence-based practice.
At the helm of leadership are the President, President-Elect, and Immediate Past-President, who form the core executive officers, alongside the Chief Executive Officer (CEO). The President, currently Carrie Jaworski, MD, FACSM, serves a one-year term, providing visionary guidance and presiding over Board meetings. The President-Elect, Karin Allor Pfeiffer, PhD, FACSM, FNAK, is elected to prepare for the presidency and contributes to ongoing initiatives, while the Immediate Past-President, Stella L. Volpe, PhD, RDN, FACSM, ACSM-CEP, offers continuity through advisory roles. The CEO, Katie Feltman, CAE, who assumed the position in February 2025, manages day-to-day operations, including staff oversight and implementation of Board directives, reporting directly to the trustees. These roles are filled through member elections, with eligibility typically requiring active professional membership and significant contributions to the field.
Supporting the Board and officers are specialized standing committees that handle operational and developmental functions. The Committee on Certification and Registry Boards (CCRB) oversees the development and maintenance of professional certifications, ensuring rigorous standards for credentials like the Certified Exercise Physiologist. The Publications Committee manages content creation and quality for journals and educational materials, while the Health and Science Policy Committee drives advocacy efforts by formulating evidence-based recommendations on public health issues. Additional governance committees, such as the Constitution, Bylaws and Operating Codes Committee and the Strategic Planning Committee, refine organizational policies and long-term objectives. These volunteer-led groups, comprising experts from ACSM's membership, report to the Board and facilitate collaborative decision-making.
The governance framework emphasizes oversight of strategic planning, financial management, and policy development to sustain ACSM's impact. The Board approves the multi-year Strategic Plansuch as the 2024-2027 iteration, which includes five bold goals focused on education, research, and inclusivityand monitors progress through regular updates and metrics. Financial stewardship involves long-term budgeting and resource allocation, often in coordination with the ACSM Foundation, to support programs without compromising fiscal health. Policy development is guided by committees to produce position statements and guidelines that influence national health initiatives, ensuring all actions align with ACSM's vision of enriching lives through movement.

Publications

Journals

The American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) publishes six peer-reviewed journals that serve as key platforms for advancing research and practice in sports medicine, exercise science, and related fields. The flagship journal, Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise (MSSE), launched in 1969, features original investigations, clinical studies, and comprehensive reviews on topics spanning exercise physiology, biomechanics, sports medicine, and public health implications of physical activity. As a monthly publication, MSSE emphasizes multidisciplinary research that informs evidence-based guidelines and clinical applications, with submissions undergoing rigorous peer review through an online portal managed by the publisher, Wolters Kluwer.
Complementing MSSE, ACSM's other journals address specialized areas to bridge research with practical and translational applications. ACSM's Health & Fitness Journal, a bimonthly publication since 1997, targets fitness professionals with practical articles on exercise programming, nutrition, behavior change, and certification-related topics, fostering the application of science in community and clinical settings. Current Sports Medicine Reports, launched in 2002 as a monthly electronic journal, focuses exclusively on clinical aspects of sports medicine, providing concise reviews and updates on emerging topics like injury prevention and athlete care for physicians and clinicians. Exercise and Sport Sciences Reviews (ESSR), originating in 1972 and transitioning to quarterly format in 2000, offers invited forward-thinking reviews and hypotheses on transformative topics in exercise physiology and sports sciences, shaping future research directions through expert synthesis. The Translational Journal of the American College of Sports Medicine (TJACSM), established in 2016, publishes original research, clinical trials, and meta-analyses that translate scientific findings into actionable interventions for physical activity promotion and health outcomes. Most recently, Exercise, Sport, and Movement (ESM), an open-access journal launched in 2022, covers a broad scope including scientific, clinical, public health, and policy aspects of exercise and movement across the lifespan, with features like graphical reviews to enhance accessibility.
These journals collectively disseminate high-impact, evidence-based research, with submission processes typically involving online platforms for peer-reviewed evaluation to ensure quality and relevance. Publication metrics highlight their influence: as of 2023, MSSE holds an impact factor of 4.1, ranking in the top 15 among sports sciences journals; ESSR at 4.9, ranking 7th; TJACSM at 2.2; ACSM's Health & Fitness Journal at 1.5; and CSMR at 1.4. By prioritizing seminal studies and clinical translations, ACSM's journals contribute significantly to the field's conceptual advancements, such as integrating biomechanics with public health strategies, while avoiding exhaustive listings of all metrics to emphasize their role in guiding professional practice.

Books and Guidelines

The flagship publication of the American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) in the realm of books is ACSM's Guidelines for Exercise Testing and Prescription, first published in 1975 as a foundational resource synthesizing evidence-based protocols for assessing fitness levels and designing safe exercise programs. This manual has been updated regularly to incorporate advancements in exercise physiology and clinical research, with the 12th edition released in 2021 emphasizing updates on exercise dosing, risk stratification, and special populations such as those with chronic conditions. Targeted at healthcare professionals, exercise physiologists, and clinicians, it prioritizes practical application of scientific evidence to promote physical activity for health benefits while minimizing risks.
Another key title, ACSM's Resources for the Personal Trainer, first published in 2004, provides comprehensive guidance for fitness professionals on client assessment, program design, and behavioral coaching, now in its sixth edition as of 2022. Aimed at aspiring and practicing personal trainers, the book integrates foundational science with real-world strategies, including sections on exercise leadership and business aspects of training. Its development draws from ACSM's expertise in translating research into actionable tools for non-clinical settings.
In the area of nutrition, ACSM's Nutrition for Exercise Science, first published in 2018 with a second edition in 2024, offers an evidence-based overview of nutrient timing, hydration, and supplementation tailored to athletic performance and recovery, authored by leading experts. Designed for students, coaches, and sports dietitians, it emphasizes practical dietary strategies supported by physiological principles, such as optimizing energy availability during training.
ACSM books, including these titles, are developed through collaborative processes involving multidisciplinary experts, such as exercise scientists and clinicians, who review content against the organization's rigorous evidence-based standards to ensure accuracy and relevance. This approach aligns publications with ACSM's mission to advance sports medicine and exercise science, fostering widespread adoption in professional practice and education.

Professional Development

Certifications

The American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) offers a suite of core certification programs designed to standardize professional competencies in exercise science, fitness training, and clinical applications, ensuring certified individuals possess the knowledge and skills to deliver safe, evidence-based services across various settings. These certifications, accredited by the National Commission for Certifying Agencies (NCCA), emphasize practical application of scientific principles and contribute to elevating industry standards by requiring rigorous education, experience, and examination.
ACSM's primary certifications include the Certified Personal Trainer (CPT), which targets fitness coaching in non-clinical environments and requires candidates to be at least 18 years old with a high school diploma or equivalent, plus current adult CPR/AED certification (first aid mandatory starting 2027); the Certified Exercise Physiologist (EP-C), focused on community-based exercise programming, necessitating a bachelor's degree in exercise science, exercise physiology, or kinesiology, along with CPR/AED and first aid from 2027; the Clinical Exercise Physiologist (CEP), oriented toward patient care in clinical settings, demanding either a master's degree in clinical exercise physiology with 600 hours of hands-on clinical experience or a bachelor's degree in a related field with 1,200 hours, plus basic life support or professional rescuer CPR (first aid mandatory starting 2027); and the Group Exercise Instructor (GEI), aimed at leading group fitness sessions, with prerequisites mirroring the CPT's age, education, and CPR/AED requirements (first aid from 2027). Each certification's exam content draws from foundational texts like ACSM's Guidelines for Exercise Testing and Prescription, which candidates often use for preparation.
The certification process begins with an online application through the ACSM website, followed by verification of prerequisites and payment of fees (ranging from $310$460 for initial exams, with discounts for members). Exams are computer-based, featuring multiple-choice and scenario-based questions delivered at Pearson VUE testing centers or via online proctoring, and cover domains such as exercise assessment, program design, and client safety; starting July 10, 2025, all exams align with the 12th edition of the Guidelines for Exercise Testing and Prescription to reflect evolving professional competencies. Recertification occurs every three years to maintain currency, requiring 45 continuing education credits (CECs) for CPT and GEI holders or 60 CECs for EP-C and CEP, alongside proof of current CPR/AED (up to 5 CECs credited) and a fee of $60$70, with options for earning credits through ACSM-approved courses, volunteering, or publications.
ACSM certifications enjoy global recognition and portability, with exams available at over 5,000 international Pearson VUE centers and online proctoring in most countries (excluding select nations like China and Iran), enabling professionals worldwide to obtain credentials that facilitate cross-border practice. While specific telehealth integrations were not explicitly incorporated into core certification updates post-2020, the organization's broader guidelines have increasingly emphasized virtual delivery methods in response to industry shifts, ensuring certified professionals are equipped for hybrid service models.

Conferences and Events

The American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) hosts its flagship event, the Annual Meeting, as the premier gathering for professionals in sports medicine, exercise science, and allied health fields. This multidisciplinary conference features over 1,500 scientific abstracts and clinical cases, more than 200 hours of educational content, and interactive formats including oral presentations, poster sessions, and exhibits from industry leaders. Topics span foundational and emerging areas, such as exercise physiology, biomechanics, and innovations in exercise technology, fostering knowledge exchange through evidence-based sessions and collaborative discussions. The 2025 Annual Meeting occurred from May 27 to 30 in Atlanta, Georgia, at the Georgia World Congress Center and Signia by Hilton Atlanta, while the 2026 event is scheduled for May 26 to 29 in Salt Lake City, Utah, at the Salt Palace Convention Center. Abstract submissions for the 2026 meeting closed on November 10, 2025, at noon PST, with notifications sent to submitters thereafter; session proposals had a deadline of July 10, 2025. Attendance provides substantial networking opportunities, including exhibit hall interactions and receptions, with ACSM members eligible for discounted registration fees.
Beyond the Annual Meeting, ACSM organizes regional chapter meetings to facilitate localized professional development and collaboration across its 11 U.S. chapters. These events typically include research presentations, workshops, and panel discussions tailored to regional interests in sports medicine and exercise science, promoting knowledge exchange among clinicians, researchers, and educators. For instance, the Midwest Chapter Annual Meeting took place October 1517, 2025, in Grand Rapids, Michigan; the Greater New York Chapter Fall Meeting occurred on November 8, 2025, in New York City; and the Central States Chapter Annual Meeting is set for February 2627, 2026, at the University of Oklahoma-Tulsa. These gatherings emphasize practical applications and networking in smaller, accessible formats compared to national events.
ACSM also offers webinars and virtual sessions as flexible options for ongoing knowledge exchange, often featuring expert-led discussions on current research methodologies and applications in exercise science. These online events support professional networking through Q&A interactions and are designed for busy practitioners seeking targeted insights without travel.
In addition, ACSM conducts specialized symposia and roundtables, often integrated into larger meetings or as standalone events, addressing emerging issues such as sports concussion management and youth athletics participation. These focused gatherings include expert panels and case studies on topics like concussion recovery protocols and the risks of early sport specialization in youth, contributing to evidence-based advancements in clinical practice.

Research and Advocacy

Position Statements and Guidelines

The American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) issues position statements and guidelines as official, evidence-based pronouncements addressing critical topics in exercise science and sports medicine, serving as authoritative resources for professionals and policymakers. These documents synthesize current research to provide recommendations on safe and effective physical activity practices, distinct from broader educational publications by focusing on concise policy-oriented declarations.
ACSM position statements are developed through a rigorous, evidence-based protocol overseen by expert committees, beginning with the identification of topics by the Pronouncements Committee and Board of Trustees, often incorporating member nominations. The process involves formulating PICOT (Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome, Time) questions, conducting systematic literature reviews, evaluating for bias, and grading evidence strength, followed by drafting by appointed authors, external review by 5-7 content experts, and two rounds of peer review by the Pronouncements Committee. Final approval by the Board of Trustees ensures alignment with the latest science, with periodic revisions to reflect emerging research; for instance, statements are targeted for completion within 18 months from approval to publication.
Key examples include the 2011 position stand on the quantity and quality of exercise for developing and maintaining cardiorespiratory, musculoskeletal, and neuromotor fitness in apparently healthy adults, which recommends at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week alongside resistance training twice weekly to promote health outcomes. Another is the 2016 systematic review position on physical activity, fitness, cognitive function, and academic achievement in children, emphasizing the role of regular exercise in enhancing youth cognitive and scholastic performance. Regarding exercise preparticipation screening, ACSM's 2022 collaborative call to action with the American Medical Society for Sports Medicine adapted cardiovascular screening protocols to address COVID-19 risks, recommending enhanced symptom and exposure assessments prior to activity resumption. On youth sports participation, ACSM supports evidence-based approaches through related statements, such as integrating physical activity to mitigate injury risks and foster long-term engagement in sports. For safe and sustainable fat loss, including for beginners, ACSM recommends a rate of no more than 2 pounds (0.9 kg) per week, often 1-2 pounds, to minimize muscle loss and health risks when combined with exercise and moderate calorie restriction, emphasizing gradual progress and initial goals of 5-10% body weight loss over 3-6 months.
These position statements exert significant influence on public policy, such as informing national physical activity guidelines, and shape clinical practice by standardizing exercise prescriptions in healthcare settings worldwide, while contributing to global standards through free public access and promotion via ACSM channels. Their research-driven revisions ensure ongoing relevance, impacting fields from preventive medicine to youth development programs.

Advocacy Initiatives

The American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) engages in advocacy by producing issue briefs and leveraging position statements to influence policymakers on critical areas such as enhancing physical education in schools, securing funding for exercise research, and improving access to sports facilities. These documents, developed by ACSM's Health and Science Policy Committee, provide evidence-based recommendations tailored for elected officials, administrators, and community leaders to promote physical activity as a public health priority. For instance, ACSM's advocacy for school-based physical education emphasizes evidence-based curricula to foster lifelong activity habits among youth, while efforts to increase research funding highlight the need for grants supporting collaborations across academia, industry, and healthcare.
ACSM collaborates with government agencies like the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) to advance public health campaigns, notably through the seminal 1995 recommendationco-authored with the CDCfor adults to accumulate at least 30 minutes of moderate-intensity physical activity on most days of the week, equivalent to 150 minutes weekly. This guideline, which ACSM continues to promote, aligns with global standards from the World Health Organization (WHO) and underpins broader initiatives to reduce inactivity-related costs, estimated at billions annually in the U.S. Additionally, ACSM supports legislative measures like the Personal Health Investment Today (PHIT) Act, reintroduced in 2023, which would expand flexible spending accounts to cover up to $1,000 annually in fitness expenses, thereby enhancing access to facilities and programs.
As of 2025, ACSM has launched the Congress Plus advocacy platform, enabling members to track legislation, receive action alerts, and contact representatives via email, phone, or social media to lobby on issues like the FY 2026 federal budget and physical activity integration in healthcare. In alignment with the "Make Our Children Healthy Again" (MAHA) strategy, ACSM advocates for designating physical activity as a vital sign in child health, targeting 150 minutes of weekly moderate-to-vigorous activity, reinstating school fitness assessments, and incorporating wearable technology for post-pandemic activity monitoring and research on inactivity's role in chronic diseases. These efforts emphasize technology-integrated approaches, with wearables ranking as the top fitness trend for 2025 to support sustained engagement in physical activity.

ACSM Foundation

Establishment

The ACSM Foundation was established in 1984 as a 501(c)(3) nonprofit organization dedicated to supporting the educational and scientific missions of the American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) through targeted fundraising initiatives.
From its inception, the Foundation's primary objectives centered on managing endowments and issuing grants to advance ACSM's core activities in sports medicine and exercise science, while maintaining a clear separation of philanthropic operations from the parent organization's day-to-day functions. This independent structure allowed the Foundation to focus exclusively on resource development, ensuring that donations and endowments directly bolstered research, education, and outreach without overlapping with ACSM's membership or programmatic efforts.
Incorporated in 1984 in Indianapolis, Indiana, the Foundation achieved federal tax-exempt status under section 501(c)(3) of the Internal Revenue Code in June of that year, aligning closely with ACSM's relocation of its national headquarters to the same city. This strategic timing facilitated integrated operations while preserving the Foundation's distinct legal and financial autonomy as a charitable entity.

Programs and Funding

The ACSM Foundation supports the advancement of sports medicine through a variety of grant and scholarship programs targeted at students and early-career professionals in exercise science. Annually, the Foundation awards more than $100,000 in research grants, scholarships, and fellowships to foster innovative research and professional development in areas such as physical activity epidemiology, clinical sports medicine, and exercise physiology.
Key initiatives include the Doctoral Student Research Grant, which provides up to $5,000 for one year to support experimental research involving subjects, supplies, and equipment for doctoral candidates exploring exercise science topics. Another prominent program is the Breaking Barriers Research Grant, developed in collaboration with the American Medical Society for Sports Medicine Foundation, which funds projects aimed at improving health outcomes for historically underrepresented communities in sports medicine, such as through enhanced care access and representation in athletic training and physician roles. Additional offerings, like the Research Endowment and Paffenbarger Blair Fund Grant, target early-career researchers with funding for hypothesis-driven studies on exercise mechanisms and physical activity's health impacts.
The Foundation sustains these programs through diverse fundraising efforts, including direct donations via the Annual Fund, which allows flexible support for emerging needs; endowments established in honor of notable figures, such as the Lawrence Golding, Ph.D., FACSM Fund for student certification scholarships; and strategic partnerships that amplify resources for specific initiatives. Funded projects have demonstrated measurable impacts, including the publication of peer-reviewed studies on topics like resistance training's effects on musculoskeletal outcomes in older adults and the establishment of new research labs advancing exercise metabolism knowledge, thereby contributing to broader evidence-based practices in sports medicine.
