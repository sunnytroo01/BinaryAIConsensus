 The Short Film Palme d'Or is the highest accolade bestowed upon the best short film in the official competition at the Cannes Film Festival, an annual event celebrating cinematic excellence. This prize recognizes innovative works no longer than 15 minutes in duration, including credits, selected from global submissions to highlight emerging storytelling in the short format. Awarded during the festival's closing ceremony, it underscores the competition's role in discovering new voices in cinema since its inception.
The international Short Film Competition was established in 1952 as part of the Cannes Film Festival, which itself began in 1946 to promote artistic films amid post-World War II recovery. From the outset, a Palme d'Or has been presented annually to the top short film, evolving into the distinctly named Short Film Palme d'Or to distinguish it from the feature film category's top honor. Typically, around 10 films are chosen for the competition each year, screened in venues like the Buuel and Debussy theaters, with selections emphasizing originality, technical prowess, and narrative depth. A dedicated jury, often chaired by an acclaimed director such as Maren Ade in 2025 or Ildik Enyedi in 2023, evaluates the entries and selects the winner alongside any special mentions.
The Short Film Palme d'Or has a storied legacy of propelling filmmakers toward international acclaim, serving as a vital springboard from shorts to features. Pioneering director Agns Varda received the award in 1958 for saisons, chteaux, marking an early triumph that complemented her feature debut La Pointe courte three years prior. In 1986, Jane Campion became the first woman to win for Peel, a milestone that foreshadowed her 1993 Palme d'Or for the feature The Piano, making her the only director to claim both short and feature prizes. Other luminaries include Jim Jarmusch, honored in 1993 for Coffee and Cigarettes, which exemplified his signature minimalist style. Recent recipients, such as Neboja Slijepevi for The Man Who Could Not Remain Silent in 2024 and Tawfeek Barhom for I'm Glad You're Dead Now in 2025, continue this tradition of spotlighting bold, socially resonant works.
Beyond individual achievements, the award reinforces Cannes' dedication to the short film as a concise yet powerful medium, often addressing themes of identity, society, and human experience with limited runtime. Winners frequently gain visibility for further projects, with many shorts advancing to Oscar contention or inspiring feature adaptations, thus bridging experimental cinema and mainstream success. The competition's rigorous selectiondrawing from thousands of submissions annuallyensures it remains a cornerstone of the festival, fostering diversity and innovation in global filmmaking.

History

Inception and Early Years (19461954)

The Cannes Film Festival was established in 1946 as a counterpoint to the Venice Film Festival, which had been criticized for its political biases under fascist influence during World War II. Conceived in 1938 by French officials including Philippe Erlanger to promote an apolitical celebration of cinema, the inaugural event was postponed due to the war and finally launched from September 20 to October 5, 1946, in the immediate aftermath of Europe's post-war reconstruction. This founding aimed to foster international collaboration through film, with 21 countries participating and an international jury awarding Grand Prix prizes across categories, including early recognition for short films in documentary and narrative forms.
Short film awards emerged prominently from the festival's outset, reflecting the medium's role in accessible storytelling amid resource constraints. In 1946, the jury honored shorts in specialized categories, such as the Grand Prix International du film a scnario - court mtrage awarded to Vanocni Sen directed by Karel Zeman from Czechoslovakia, and the Grand Prix International du film scientifique - court mtrage to La Cit des Abeilles by Andrev Winnitski from the Soviet Union. By 1947, awards continued for short documentaries, exemplified by the Grand Prix for Inondations en Pologne directed by Jerzy Bossak and Waclaw Kazwierski from Poland, highlighting themes of human resilience in war-torn regions. These early prizes, often tied to technical or thematic excellence, faced challenges from post-war logistical issues, including limited film stock and travel restrictions that reduced submissions primarily to European nations.
The formalization of short film recognition advanced with the introduction of the Grand Prix du Court Mtrage in 1952, establishing a dedicated top honor for the category and serving as the direct precursor to the modern Palme d'Or for shorts. That year, the award went to Het Schots Is Te Boord by Dutch director Herman van der Horst, a documentary capturing maritime life. In 1953, French filmmaker Albert Lamorisse received the Grand Prix for his poetic narrative Crin Blanc, le Cheval Sauvage, emphasizing the award's growing prestige for innovative fiction shorts. The 1954 edition shifted to specialized prix rather than a singular Grand Prix, with honors like the Prix du film de ralit to Stare Miasto by Jerzy Bossak from Poland and the Prix du film de fantaisie potique to The Pleasure Garden by James Broughton from the United States, amid ongoing recovery efforts that still limited global participation but encouraged experimental works.

Evolution of the Award (1955present)

The Short Film Palme d'Or was introduced in 1955 as the highest honor for short films at the Cannes Film Festival, replacing the previous Grand Prix du Court Mtrage that had been awarded since the competition's inception in 1952. This change aligned the short film category with the festival's newly established Palme d'Or for features, symbolizing a unified prestige for the competition's top prizes and emphasizing artistic excellence in concise storytelling. The first recipient was Norman McLaren's experimental animated film Blinkity Blank, marking a milestone for innovative short-form cinema.
From 1964 to 1974, amid evolving festival policies, the award reverted to the Grand Prix International du Court Mtrage, reflecting a temporary return to earlier naming conventions influenced by broader organizational shifts at Cannes. This period included significant disruptions, such as the complete cancellation of the 1968 festival due to nationwide protests during the May 1968 events in France, resulting in no short film awards that year. In 1971, while the main festival proceeded, no top short film prize was conferred, highlighting occasional gaps in the competition's structure during this era. The Palme d'Or designation was permanently reinstated in 1975 for short films, solidifying its status as the enduring emblem of short film achievement at Cannes.
In 1998, the award's administration evolved with the launch of the Cinfondation section, dedicated to supporting emerging filmmakers from film schools worldwide; this integration created a unified jury responsible for selecting both Cinfondation entries and short film competitors, fostering greater emphasis on youthful innovation. The shared jury process has since enhanced cross-pollination between student works and professional shorts, with prizes awarded annually to the top three in Cinfondation alongside the Short Film Palme d'Or. External challenges continued to test the award's resilience, notably in 2020 when the COVID-19 pandemic forced the cancellation of the physical festival; however, a special three-day event in October still enabled the short film competition, awarding the Palme d'Or to Sameh Alaa's I'm Afraid to Forget Your Face. This adaptation ensured continuity, with 3,810 submissions from 137 countries reviewed that year, underscoring the award's global reach.
Post-2000, the Short Film Palme d'Or has reflected increasing international diversity, with winners hailing from over 20 countries, including non-European nations like Lebanon (2015's Waves '98), China (2021's All the Crows in the World and 2022's The Water Murmurs), and Hungary (2023's 27). Such trends have elevated the award's role in promoting underrepresented voices in short-form cinema, continuing with the 2025 winner I'm Glad You're Dead Now by Tawfeek Barhom (Palestine/France/Greece co-production).

Award Process

Eligibility and Criteria

To qualify for the Short Film Palme d'Or in the Cannes Film Festival's Official Competition, films must adhere to strict eligibility rules designed to ensure premieres of recent, high-caliber works. The runtime is limited to 15 minutes, including credits, allowing for concise storytelling while excluding longer formats that fall outside the short film category. Additionally, films must have been produced within the 12 months preceding the festival and must not have been screened outside their country of origin, presented at any other international festival (in competitive or non-competitive sections), or made available online prior to the Cannes premiere. These requirements guarantee a world or international premiere at Cannes, preserving the festival's status as a launchpad for new talent.
The competition primarily features live-action fiction, animation, and experimental films, emphasizing narrative-driven cinematic expression, with recent selections excluding documentaries, which are recognized through separate awards like the L'il d'Or for best documentary overall. This focus aligns with the competition's aim to highlight innovative storytelling in short formats, drawing from global submissions while prioritizing professional productions over student or educational films (the latter directed to the distinct La Cinf section). Historically, eligibility criteria have evolved; early winners included films longer than 15 minutes and documentaries, but the modern standard limits runtimes to 15 minutes and focuses on fiction, animation, and experimental works.
Submissions are open to international filmmakers and must be entered through the festival's official online portal, "My Account," with no entry fee required. The process involves completing a submission form by early March (for the 2025 edition, March 3) and uploading the film by March 7 at midnight, enabling broad accessibility for emerging directors worldwide. Selected films are notified before the mid-April press conference, after which producers commit to screening obligations and non-commercial exploitation restrictions during the festival period.
Once selected, films are judged by a dedicated juryshared with the Cinfondation section since 1998based on criteria including originality, artistic merit, technical innovation, and narrative impact, as outlined in festival guidelines emphasizing exceptional cinematic quality. The final decision rests at the sole discretion of the Association Franaise du Festival International du Film, ensuring subjective evaluation of creative excellence over rigid metrics.
Over time, core eligibility has remained consistent to maintain the award's prestige, with the 15-minute runtime limit established as standard in the modern era to accommodate evolving short-form techniques. Prior to 1998, the competition emphasized professional shorts, including both fiction and occasional documentaries, but the introduction of the Cinfondation section that year broadened the overall short film ecosystem at Cannes by integrating student works under a common jury, indirectly allowing for more diverse short forms in the festival's programming without altering the Palme d'Or's professional focus.

Jury and Selection Procedure

The Short Film Palme d'Or is awarded by the Short Films and La Cinef Jury, a body established in 1998 alongside the creation of the Cinfondation section to support emerging filmmakers. This shared jury, typically comprising 4 to 6 members including a president, evaluates both the short films in official competition and student works in La Cinef, selecting the Palme d'Or winner from the former while distributing three prizes among the latter. Prior to 1998, a dedicated short film jury operated independently to adjudicate the competition.
Jurors are appointed annually by the Festival de Cannes's general delegate and board, drawing from a mix of international filmmakers, actors, producers, and industry figures to provide balanced perspectives. For instance, the 2025 jury, presided over by director Maren Ade, included Reinaldo Marcus Green (director and producer), Camlia Jordana (actress and songwriter), Jos Mara Prado Garca (photographer and former film archive director), and Neboja Slijepevi (director), representing Germany, the United States, France, Spain, and Croatia. Influential past presidents have included Martin Scorsese in 2004 and Jane Campion in 2013, both acclaimed directors whose involvement highlighted the jury's role in championing innovative short-form cinema. Since around 2010, juror selections have increasingly prioritized diversity in gender, nationality, and professional background to reflect broader representation in global filmmaking.
The selection procedure commences with the festival's selection committee, composed of programmers and industry experts, reviewing thousands of submissionsover 4,700 in 2025 aloneto narrow the field to 7 to 11 finalists for the official competition. These shorts are then screened publicly during the festival, with the jury conducting private viewings and deliberations to assess artistic merit, originality, and technical execution.
Decisions are made through secret ballot voting among jury members, requiring an absolute majority in the first two rounds and a relative majority thereafter to resolve ties or select the winner. There is no formal runner-up award, though the jury has occasionally issued special mentions for notable entries. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the 2020 festival edition was canceled, but a special market event included short film showcases without a competitive jury; the 2021 edition proceeded with a standard in-person jury under enhanced health protocols.

Other Short Film Awards

Primary Additional Awards

The Prix du Jury, introduced in the 1950s alongside the establishment of the short film competition at Cannes, serves as the primary secondary honor for outstanding artistic and narrative achievement in short films. Awarded by the short films jury to one or more entries from the Palme d'Or finalists, it recognizes works that exhibit exceptional creativity, emotional depth, or innovative filmmaking without claiming the top prize for overall mastery. This award has been a consistent feature since the category's formalization in 1952, allowing the jury to highlight a runner-up that pushes boundaries in short-form storytelling.
Historically, the Prix du Jury was often categorized by genre, such as fiction or animation, to better reflect diverse short film styles. For instance, in 1979, it went to Boom by Bretislav Pojar in the animation category for its inventive visual narrative and to a fiction short by Lluis Racionero Grau for its bold thematic exploration. Similarly, in 1986, the fiction Prix du Jury was bestowed on Les Petites Magiciennes by Yves Robert and Vincent Mercier, praised for its whimsical homage to traditional filmmaking techniques. These selections underscore the award's focus on complementary excellence to the Palme d'Or, drawing from the same pool of 7-10 competing shorts each year.
In contemporary iterations, the Prix du Jury's role has evolved into the Special Mention of the Jury, a streamlined recognition for technical prowess or special distinction among Palme contenders, awarded since the 1990s to honor films with remarkable craft in areas like cinematography, sound design, or experimental form. Criteria emphasize innovation that enhances the short's impact, often spotlighting underrepresented voices or bold aesthetics. Examples from the 1970s onward include the 2019 Special Distinction to Monstruo Dios by Agustina San Martn for its haunting visual style, though the exact "Grand Prix du Court Mtrage" nomenclature appears limited to early top-prize variants rather than ongoing additional honors. In the 2020s, this award has continued as a key complement, with 2025's Special Mention to Ali by Adnan Al Rajeev for its poignant cultural insight, 2024's to Bad for a Moment by Daniel Soares for its tense pacing, and 2023's to Fr (Intrusion) by Gunnur Martinsdttir Schlter for its atmospheric tension.
Honorable mentions, frequently manifested as additional Special Mentions or jury distinctions, are granted at the short films jury's discretion to acknowledge multiple exemplary shorts beyond the primary prizes, promoting broader recognition in a competitive field limited to fewer than 10 entries annually. Introduced in the 1950s to foster emerging talent and diverse perspectives, these mentions occur one to two times per festival, focusing on films that excel in originality or social relevance without meeting the Palme's holistic criteria. Their role is to amplify visibility for shorts that might otherwise go unnoticed, often leading to further festival circuits or distribution opportunities; for example, the 2019 Special Distinction extended this tradition by citing Monstruo Dios alongside the Prix structure. In the 2020s, they have maintained this function, as seen in the 2025 mention for Ali, which highlighted its debut international impact for Bangladeshi cinema.
These awardsPrix du Jury, its modern Special Mention equivalent, and honorable distinctionsdistinguish themselves from the Palme d'Or by targeting specific strengths like technical innovation or secondary narrative power, ensuring the competition celebrates a spectrum of short film excellence rather than a singular victor. Unlike the Palme's emphasis on comprehensive impact, they provide nuanced acclaim, often to films from Palme shortlists, fostering a supportive ecosystem for global short filmmakers.

Special and Historical Awards

In the inaugural Cannes Film Festival of 1946, short films were recognized through several category-specific Grand Prix awards rather than a single top honor. These included the Grand Prix International du film d'actualit for Molodost Nasej Strany directed by Sergue Youtkvitch, the Grand Prix International du film scientifique and Grand Prix International du film pdagogique both for La Cit des Abeilles by Andrev Winnitski, and the Grand Prix International du film scnario for Vnon sen by Karel Zeman. Additionally, special prizes highlighted innovative shorts, such as the Prix du Comit International pour la diffusion des Arts et des Lettres awarded to paves by Jacques-Yves Cousteau, and the Grand Prix International du dessin anim shared by Zvrtka a Petrovst by Ji Trnka and Make Mine Music by Joshua Meador et al.
By 1947, the festival continued this approach with targeted accolades for short formats, notably the Grand Prix Documentaires given to Inondations en Pologne directed by Jerzy Bossak and Wacaw Kamierczak, which underscored the emphasis on documentary shorts depicting post-war realities. The Grand Prix Dessin Anim was awarded to Dumbo, llphant Volant by Ben Sharpsteen, recognizing excellence in animation. These awards reflected the era's focus on diverse short film genres, including avant-garde works like Maya Deren's Meshes of the Afternoon, which received the Best Avant-garde Film prize.
In 1949, following the cancellation of the 1948 edition due to geopolitical tensions, Cannes introduced specialized short film prizes emphasizing technical and artistic achievements, such as the Prix pour la couleur court mtrage for Images Mdivales by William Novik, the Prix pour le sujet court mtrage for Palle Alene i Verden by Astrid Henning-Jensen, and the Prix pour le montage court mtrage for Pacific 231 by Jean Mitry. No overarching Grand Prix for shorts existed that year, but these distinctions filled gaps in recognizing overlooked formats like reportages and photography in shorts, exemplified by Seal Island by James Algar and Biay Redyk by Stanislas Mozdenski.
The 1950 and 1951 festivals maintained similar special mentions for shorts, often tied to narrative, documentary, or innovative elements, though records indicate a shift toward consolidating categories amid growing international submissions. This evolution from fragmented, genre-specific honors in the late 1940s culminated in the introduction of a unified Grand Prix du court mtrage in 1952, awarded to 't Schot is te boord! by Herman van der Horst, alongside special jury prizes like the one for Indisk By by Arne Sucksdorff. Such one-off distinctions, including brief experiments with animation-focused awards in the early years, paved the way for the modern short film competition by standardizing recognition and transitioning to the Palme d'Or in 1955.
During the Grand Prix era for the main competition (19641974), short films occasionally received supplementary one-time prizes for experimental works, such as jury distinctions for avant-garde entries exploring surrealism and abstraction, though these were not recurring categories. This period's special mentions, like those for innovative techniques in shorts, highlighted Cannes' historical adaptability before the Palme d'Or became the sole pinnacle for short films.
