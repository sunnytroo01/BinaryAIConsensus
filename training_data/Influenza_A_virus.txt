Influenza A virus
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Influenza A virus is an enveloped, single-stranded, negative-sense RNA virus belonging to the family Orthomyxoviridae, characterized by a helical nucleocapsid and eight segmented RNA genome. It represents one of four main types of influenza viruses (A, B, C, and D) that cause respiratory infections, but it is the only type capable of infecting a broad range of animal species, including birds, pigs, horses, and humans, and is responsible for all known influenza pandemics. Seasonal epidemics are primarily driven by influenza A (subtypes like H1N1 and H3N2) and B viruses, while type C causes milder illness and type D mainly affects cattle. Influenza A viruses are classified into 18 hemagglutinin (H) subtypes and 11 neuraminidase (N) subtypes based on the two major surface glycoproteins that determine host specificity, antigenicity, and virulence, with over 130 possible H and N combinations identified in nature.
These viruses primarily cause seasonal epidemics of influenza, known as flu season, through antigenic driftsmall genetic mutations in the H and N genes that allow evasion of population immunityand can lead to pandemics via antigenic shift, a major reassortment of genome segments when two different subtypes co-infect a host, resulting in novel strains to which humans lack immunity. Transmission occurs mainly through respiratory droplets from coughing or sneezing, as well as contact with contaminated surfaces, with an incubation period of 14 days and high contagiousness in crowded settings. In humans, infection typically presents with sudden onset of fever, chills, headache, muscle aches, cough, and sore throat, lasting about one week, though complications like pneumonia can arise, particularly in vulnerable groups such as young children, the elderly, pregnant individuals, and those with chronic conditions.
Globally, influenza A viruses, especially subtypes like A(H1N1)pdm09 and A(H3N2), contribute to approximately 1 billion cases annually, with 35 million severe illnesses and 290,000650,000 respiratory deaths, disproportionately affecting developing countries where 99% of influenza-related deaths in children under 5 years occur. Historically, four major pandemics have been caused by influenza A: the 1918 H1N1 "Spanish flu" (estimated 50 million deaths worldwide), 1957 H2N2 "Asian flu," 1968 H3N2 "Hong Kong flu," and the 2009 H1N1 pandemic (over 60 million U.S. cases alone). In animals, influenza A circulates as a natural reservoir in wild birds, with zoonotic spillovers posing ongoing risks, such as highly pathogenic avian influenza strains like H5N1. As of November 2025, more than 70 human cases of H5N1 have been reported in the United States since 2024, with additional cases globally, emphasizing persistent spillover risks.
Prevention relies heavily on annual vaccination, which targets circulating strains and provides 40%60% efficacy against infection, alongside antiviral medications like oseltamivir for treatment and prophylaxis in high-risk cases. Surveillance systems monitor genetic evolution and emergence of novel subtypes to guide vaccine updates and public health responses, emphasizing the virus's capacity for rapid global spread due to air travel and dense populations.

Virology

Taxonomy and classification

The Influenza A virus is classified within the family Orthomyxoviridae and the genus Alphainfluenzavirus, characterized as an enveloped, single-stranded, negative-sense RNA virus with a segmented genome. This taxonomic placement reflects its membership in a family that includes other influenza genera, distinguished by the enveloped virions and helical nucleocapsids typical of orthomyxoviruses.
Subtyping of Influenza A viruses is based on antigenic variations in two major surface glycoproteins: hemagglutinin (HA), which facilitates viral attachment and entry, and neuraminidase (NA), which aids in viral release from host cells. Currently, 18 HA subtypes (H1 through H18) and 11 NA subtypes (N1 through N11) have been identified, with the majority circulating in wild aquatic birds as the primary reservoir. These subtypes combine in various ways to form distinct strains, such as H1N1 or H5N1, contributing to the virus's diversity and zoonotic potential.
The standard nomenclature for Influenza A virus isolates follows the format A//// (), omitting the host if human; for example, A/California/07/2009 (H1N1) denotes a human isolate from California in 2009, strain number 07, of the H1N1 subtype. This system, established by the World Health Organization, ensures precise identification for surveillance and research purposes.
Influenza A viruses are differentiated from Influenza B, C, and D viruses primarily by their eight RNA genome segments and broad host range, infecting birds, humans, pigs, horses, and other mammals, whereas Influenza B viruses also have eight segments but are almost exclusively human pathogens, Influenza C viruses possess seven segments and primarily affect humans and swine causing milder, sporadic infections, and Influenza D viruses have seven segments and primarily infect cattle. Seasonal epidemics are primarily driven by influenza A (subtypes like H1N1 and H3N2) and B viruses. This segmentation allows for reassortment in Influenza A, enabling antigenic shifts that generate novel subtypes, unlike the more stable Influenza B, C, and D.
Recent expansions to the subtype repertoire include the discovery of H17N10 in fruit bats from Central America in 2012 and H18N11 in bats from Peru in 2013, marking the first identification of influenza A-like viruses in chiropteran hosts and highlighting bats as potential reservoirs beyond avian species. These findings were based on metagenomic sequencing of bat samples, revealing genetic divergence from classical subtypes while retaining orthomyxoviral features.

Structure and genome

The Influenza A virus (IAV) is an enveloped virus with a pleomorphic morphology, typically appearing as spherical particles approximately 80120 nm in diameter or as filamentous forms exceeding 300 nm in length. The virion consists of a host-derived lipid bilayer envelope surrounding a helical ribonucleoprotein (RNP) core, which protects the viral genome and facilitates its transport. The envelope is studded with glycoprotein spikes, while the internal matrix layer provides structural rigidity.
The surface of the IAV virion features two major glycoproteins: hemagglutinin (HA) and neuraminidase (NA). HA, present in higher abundance (approximately four times that of NA), mediates viral attachment to host sialic acid receptors and facilitates membrane fusion during entry into the host cell endosome. NA, in contrast, cleaves sialic acid residues to promote the release of newly formed virions from infected cells, preventing aggregation. Embedded within the envelope are M2 ion channels, which regulate internal pH during uncoating. Internally, the matrix protein M1 lines the envelope, interacting with the RNP core composed of nucleoprotein (NP) and the viral RNA-dependent RNA polymerase subunits (PB1, PB2, and PA). These components collectively enable the virus to maintain structural integrity and initiate infection.
The genome of IAV comprises eight distinct segments of single-stranded, negative-sense RNA, totaling approximately 13.5 kilobases. Each segment is encapsidated by NP and associated with the polymerase complex to form viral RNPs (vRNPs), which adopt a double-helical structure critical for packaging. The segments vary in length from about 890 to 2,341 nucleotides and encode up to 11 proteins through alternative splicing and reading frames; for instance, segment 7 produces the M1 matrix protein and the M2 ion channel via splicing, while segment 8 yields non-structural protein 1 (NS1) and nuclear export protein (NEP). Other key encodings include PB2 (segment 1), PB1 (segment 2), PA (segment 3), HA (segment 4), NP (segment 5), and NA (segment 6). Subtype classification of IAV strains is based on antigenic variations in HA and NA.
IAV replication occurs primarily in the nucleus of infected host cells, distinguishing it from most RNA viruses. Upon nuclear import of vRNPs via importin-mediated transport, the viral polymerase initiates transcription by "cap-snatching," wherein the PB2 subunit binds the 5' cap of host pre-mRNAs transcribed by RNA polymerase II, and the PA subunit cleaves 1013 nucleotides downstream to generate a primer for viral mRNA synthesis. This process produces positive-sense mRNAs that are exported to the cytoplasm for translation into viral proteins. Subsequent replication generates full-length complementary RNA (cRNA) intermediates, which serve as templates for new negative-sense vRNA synthesis, enabling amplification of the genome.
The segmented nature of the IAV genome confers a high potential for genetic reassortment during co-infection of a host cell by multiple strains, allowing the exchange of entire RNA segments and rapid generation of novel viral variants. This mechanism, facilitated by specific inter-segment RNA interactions within vRNPs, promotes efficient packaging of the full set of eight segments into progeny virions.

Epidemiology

Evolution and history

The Influenza A virus originated in wild aquatic birds, particularly species in the orders Anseriformes (such as ducks and geese) and Charadriiformes (such as gulls and shorebirds), which serve as its primary natural reservoir. In this reservoir, the virus typically causes asymptomatic infections and maintains genetic diversity through ongoing circulation. Phylogenetic analyses indicate that Influenza A diverged from other orthomyxoviruses, such as those in the Influenzavirus B and C genera, approximately 1,000 to 2,000 years ago, based on estimates of hemagglutinin (HA) gene evolution.
The primary evolutionary drivers of Influenza A are antigenic drift and antigenic shift. Antigenic drift involves gradual accumulation of point mutations in the viral genome, particularly in the HA and neuraminidase (NA) genes, leading to minor changes in surface proteins that enable immune evasion and seasonal epidemics. Antigenic shift, in contrast, occurs through genetic reassortment when two different Influenza A strains co-infect a host, such as a pig acting as a mixing vessel, resulting in a novel subtype with HA and/or NA from one parent virus and other genes from the other; this mechanism is responsible for major antigenic changes and pandemics.
Historical pandemics illustrate these evolutionary dynamics. The 1918 H1N1 pandemic, also known as the Spanish flu, emerged from an avian-origin virus and caused an estimated 50 million deaths worldwide, with about 675,000 in the United States alone. The 1957 H2N2 pandemic (Asian flu) arose via reassortment between human H1N1 and avian strains, leading to approximately 1.1 million global deaths. This was followed by the 1968 H3N2 pandemic (Hong Kong flu), another reassortant incorporating avian HA into the prior H2N2 backbone, resulting in about 1 million deaths worldwide. The 2009 H1N1 pandemic stemmed from reassortment in swine, combining genes from North American triple-reassortant swine viruses (avian, human, and classical swine origins) with Eurasian avian-like swine lineages.
Post-pandemic, circulating strains often undergo replacement due to immunological pressure and competition. For instance, the 1918 H1N1 subtype dominated human influenza circulation for nearly 40 years until its displacement by the 1957 H2N2 strain, after which H2N2 prevailed until the 1968 shift to H3N2. In recent decades, evolutionary trends in swine populations have included the emergence of triple-reassortant viruses around 1998, which incorporate avian, human, and classical swine genes and have become enzootic in North American pig herds, contributing to ongoing genetic diversity and potential for further reassortment events.

Zoonotic infections and transmission

The primary reservoir for influenza A virus is wild aquatic birds, particularly species such as ducks and geese, which harbor low-pathogenic avian influenza (LPAI) strains that circulate asymptomatically in their gastrointestinal tracts. These birds serve as the natural host from which the virus can spill over to domestic poultry and other species, maintaining a diverse gene pool of all known hemagglutinin (HA) and neuraminidase (NA) subtypes.
Key intermediate hosts facilitate interspecies transmission and genetic reassortment. Pigs act as "mixing vessels" due to their respiratory epithelium expressing both avian-type (2,3-linked) and human-type (2,6-linked) sialic acid receptors, allowing co-infection with avian, swine, and human influenza A viruses and enabling the emergence of novel reassortants. Emerging reservoirs include horses, which sustain equine influenza A(H3N8) strains; dogs, hosting canine influenza A(H3N2); and bats, which carry unusual subtypes like H17N10 and H18N11 that may contribute to viral diversity.
Transmission routes vary by host. In wild aquatic birds, the virus spreads primarily via the fecal-oral route, with infected birds shedding high viral loads in feces that contaminate water bodies and shared environments. In mammals, including pigs and humans, respiratory transmission predominates through direct contact with infected secretions, aerosols from coughing or sneezing, and fomites such as contaminated surfaces or equipment in farm settings. Zoonotic spillover to humans often occurs through direct handling of infected poultry or exposure to contaminated environments like live bird markets.
Notable zoonotic events highlight the risks of interspecies transmission. The 1997 H5N1 outbreak in Hong Kong involved direct transmission from infected poultry to humans, resulting in 18 confirmed cases with 6 fatalities, prompting the culling of over 1.5 million birds to halt spread. Similarly, the 2013 emergence of H7N9 in China, originating from avian sources in live poultry markets, led to 1,568 human infections as of June 2025, predominantly through environmental exposure rather than sustained human-to-human transmission.
A major barrier to efficient zoonotic transmission is host receptor specificity. Avian influenza A viruses preferentially bind to 2,3-linked sialic acids abundant in bird respiratory and intestinal tracts, while human-adapted strains favor 2,6-linked sialic acids in the upper human respiratory tract; mutations enabling dual binding can enhance cross-species potential but are rare.

Surveillance and pandemic potential

The World Health Organization's Global Influenza Surveillance and Response System (GISRS), established in 1952, comprises 144 National Influenza Centres, six WHO Collaborating Centres, four Essential Regulatory Laboratories, and reference laboratories that collectively monitor influenza viruses worldwide through virological and epidemiological surveillance. These centres isolate influenza strains from clinical and environmental samples, perform genetic sequencing to track antigenic drift and shift, and contribute data to annual vaccine strain recommendations, enabling early detection of emerging variants with pandemic potential. GISRS facilitates global data sharing via platforms like the FluNet database, supporting risk assessments for zoonotic spillovers and seasonal epidemics.
Pandemic preparedness for influenza A relies on frameworks updated after the 2009 H1N1 outbreak, including the WHO's four-phase systeminterpandemic (routine surveillance), alert (animal infections with human risk), pandemic (sustained human-to-human transmission), and post-pandemic (virus integration into seasonal circulation)which replaced the earlier six-phase model to streamline global coordination. Key elements include national stockpiles of antivirals like oseltamivir and neuraminidase inhibitors, as well as adjuvanted vaccine platforms for rapid production against novel strains, coordinated through international agreements to ensure equitable access during alerts. These measures aim to mitigate delays observed in 2009, emphasizing pre-positioned resources and simulation exercises for response activation.
High-risk strains, particularly avian influenza A(H5N1) clade 2.3.4.4b circulating since 2020, pose significant pandemic threats due to their high pathogenicity in birds and mammals, with 991 confirmed human cases and 476 deaths reported globally as of October 2025, primarily from poultry exposure in Asia. This clade has shown mammalian adaptations, including spillovers into U.S. dairy cattle starting in March 2024, where the virus caused outbreaks across multiple states, leading to reduced milk production and 70 human cases among farm workers, mostly mild but highlighting evolutionary risks. Surveillance detects these shifts through genomic surveillance, revealing reassortment potential with seasonal human influenza viruses.
Factors amplifying pandemic potential include global air travel facilitating rapid dissemination of novel strains, live poultry markets serving as amplification sites for zoonotic transmission, and climate change altering wild bird migration patterns to expand viral reservoirs into new regions. These drivers underscore the need for enhanced one-health surveillance integrating veterinary and human data to preempt cross-species jumps.
During the 2024-2025 Northern Hemisphere season, influenza activity peaked in January 2025, with A(H1N1)pdm09 and A(H3N2) dominating circulating strains and contributing to high hospitalization rates across age groups. Concurrently, H5N1 detections in wildlife surged, with over 10,000 cases in more than 160 North American bird species reported by mid-2025, reflecting ongoing clade 2.3.4.4b persistence in migratory populations and elevating spillover risks. This season highlighted GISRS's role in integrating seasonal and zoonotic monitoring to inform timely public health responses.

Clinical aspects

Pathogenesis

The pathogenesis of Influenza A virus begins with viral entry into host cells, primarily facilitated by the hemagglutinin (HA) glycoprotein. The HA receptor-binding domain attaches to sialic acid-containing receptors on the surface of susceptible cells, initiating receptor-mediated endocytosis of the virion into an endosome. Within the endosome, the M2 ion channel allows influx of protons, acidifying the virion interior and preventing premature HA activation while promoting uncoating; the low pH then induces conformational changes in HA, enabling fusion of the viral envelope with the endosomal membrane and release of viral ribonucleoproteins (vRNPs) into the cytoplasm.
Following entry, the vRNPs, consisting of viral genomic RNA segments complexed with nucleoprotein (NP) and the viral RNA polymerase (comprising PB1, PB2, and PA subunits), are transported into the host cell nucleus via nuclear localization signals on NP. In the nucleus, the viral polymerase initiates primary transcription of viral mRNAs through cap-snatching, where the PB2 subunit binds to the 5' cap of nascent host pre-mRNAs, cleaving them to prime viral transcription and produce positive-sense viral mRNAs. These viral mRNAs are exported to the cytoplasm, where they are translated by host ribosomes into viral proteins, including additional polymerase components and NP to support subsequent rounds of replication; later, full-length complementary RNA is synthesized to serve as templates for new genomic RNA segments.
Influenza A virus employs several mechanisms to evade the host immune response, notably through the non-structural protein 1 (NS1), which antagonizes the interferon (IFN) pathway by binding to double-stranded RNA and interacting with host factors like RIG-I and IRF3 to inhibit type I IFN production and signaling. In severe infections, such as those during the 1918 pandemic, the virus can trigger a dysregulated immune response characterized by hypercytokinemia, or cytokine storm, involving excessive release of proinflammatory cytokines like TNF- and IL-6, which amplifies tissue damage despite NS1-mediated suppression.
The virus exhibits primary tissue tropism for the respiratory epithelium, where sialic acid receptors are abundantly expressed, allowing efficient attachment and infection of ciliated and non-ciliated cells in the upper and lower airways. In high-pathogenic strains, such as certain H5N1 subtypes, the virus can disseminate systemically to extrapulmonary sites including the brain, demonstrating neurotropism facilitated by adaptations that enhance endothelial crossing and neuronal receptor binding. Key virulence factors include the PB2 E627K mutation, which improves polymerase activity and replication efficiency in mammalian cells at lower temperatures (e.g., 3337C), promoting host adaptation from avian origins. Additionally, neuraminidase (NA) activity cleaves sialic acid residues to release newly assembled virions from infected cells, balancing HA-mediated attachment and preventing viral aggregation to facilitate spread.

Signs and symptoms in humans

Influenza A virus infections in humans typically have an incubation period of 14 days, with symptoms appearing abruptly after exposure. Common signs and symptoms include high fever ranging from 3840C, chills, muscle aches (myalgia), headache, fatigue, and a non-productive cough, often accompanied by sore throat, runny or stuffy nose, and malaise. These symptoms reflect the virus's impact on the respiratory tract and systemic inflammation, leading to an acute illness that resolves in most cases without intervention.
The severity of illness varies widely depending on the host's age and health status. In healthy adults, uncomplicated influenza A typically lasts 37 days, with symptoms usually peaking and starting to improve after a few rough days, and full recovery typically taking a week or two, though cough and fatigue may persist longer. In children with influenza A, fever typically lasts 3 to 5 days, with the acute phase including fever usually resolving within 3 to 7 days in uncomplicated cases; the worst symptoms peak around days 2 to 4, after which improvement begins. High-risk groups, including the elderly, young children, pregnant individuals, and those who are immunocompromised, face greater risks of severe outcomes, with hospitalization rates substantially elevatedoften several times higher than in healthy populationsand approximately 2344% of cases in elderly patients with comorbidities requiring hospitalization within 30 days during peak seasons (based on 20142019 data).
Complications can arise, particularly in severe cases, including primary viral pneumonia due to direct lung damage by the virus and secondary bacterial infections such as pneumonia caused by pathogens like Streptococcus pneumoniae. Additionally, in children treated with aspirin during influenza illness, Reye's syndromea rare but serious condition involving liver and brain damagehas been associated with salicylate use. These complications contribute to higher morbidity in at-risk populations.
Mortality patterns differ between pandemic and seasonal influenza A outbreaks. Seasonal epidemics show a U-shaped mortality curve, with the highest death rates among the elderly due to comorbidities and weakened immunity. In contrast, pandemics like the 1918 H1N1 outbreak exhibited a distinctive W-shaped curve, with elevated mortality in young adults alongside infants and the elderly, possibly linked to robust but dysregulated immune responses.
Diagnosis of influenza A relies on clinical presentation combined with laboratory findings, such as leukopenia or relative lymphopenia on complete blood count, elevated C-reactive protein levels indicating inflammation, and confirmation via reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) testing of respiratory specimens, which is the gold standard for accurate detection.

Signs and symptoms in animals

In birds, the primary natural reservoir for influenza A viruses, clinical manifestations vary markedly by strain pathogenicity and host species. Low-pathogenic avian influenza (LPAI) viruses generally cause mild or subclinical infections in wild aquatic birds, but in domestic poultry such as chickens and turkeys, they can induce respiratory signs including ruffled feathers, nasal discharge, and coughing, along with a substantial drop in egg productionoften up to 40-50% in laying henswithout high mortality. In contrast, highly pathogenic avian influenza (HPAI) strains, particularly subtypes H5 and H7, lead to severe systemic disease in gallinaceous birds, characterized by sudden onset of edema, cyanosis of the comb and wattles, respiratory distress, diarrhea, and widespread hemorrhages in multiple organs, culminating in mortality rates of 90-100% in unvaccinated flocks.
In pigs, influenza A viruses cause a respiratory illness resembling human flu, with common signs including fever, depression, barking cough, nasal and ocular discharge, sneezing, labored breathing, and eye inflammation, often leading to reduced feed intake and growth rates in affected herds. Many infections are mild or asymptomatic, particularly in weaned pigs, facilitating widespread subclinical carriage and shedding that sustains viral circulation within swine populations.
Equine influenza, primarily caused by the H3N8 subtype (A/equine/H3N8), manifests as an acute respiratory disease in horses, with initial signs of high fever (39-41C), dry cough, nasal discharge, lethargy, and myalgia, progressing to wheezing and depression in severe cases; outbreaks typically affect unvaccinated or young horses in dense populations, resolving in 2-3 weeks but causing significant morbidity.
In dogs, influenza A infections from H3N8 (originally equine-derived) or H3N2 (avian-origin) subtypes present as a contagious respiratory syndrome akin to kennel cough, featuring persistent honking cough, serous nasal discharge, fever (up to 104-105F), lethargy, ocular discharge, and anorexia; while most cases are mild and self-limiting, secondary bacterial infections can escalate to severe pneumonia, especially in puppies or immunocompromised animals.
Bats serve as reservoirs for certain influenza A virus subtypes, such as H17N10 and H18N11, where infections are typically asymptomatic or induce only mild, transient respiratory signs, allowing persistent viral shedding without overt disease due to bats' unique immune tolerance mechanisms that dampen inflammation.
From 2020 to 2025, highly pathogenic H5N1 clade 2.3.4.4b viruses spilled over from birds to various mammals, causing notable clinical syndromes. In cats, infections often begin with fever, anorexia, and lethargy, rapidly progressing to severe respiratory distress (nasal discharge, dyspnea) and neurological abnormalities (ataxia, tremors, seizures, blindness), with fatality rates exceeding 50% in domestic cases. In marine mammals like seals and sea lions, H5N1 leads to acute neurological disease, including disorientation, lethargy, fever, and circling, followed by rapid progression to paralysis and death, contributing to mass mortality events along coastlines. In dairy cattle, symptoms are generally milder and include decreased milk production (with colostrum-like milk), fever, reduced appetite, and transient respiratory or gastrointestinal signs such as rhinitis and loose stools, with most animals recovering without intervention but potential for herd-level economic impact. As of 2025, this clade has infected more than 1,000 dairy herds in the United States, resulting in substantial economic impacts from reduced milk production despite predominantly mild cases.

Prevention and control

Vaccination

Vaccination against the influenza A virus primarily relies on annual seasonal vaccines designed to elicit immune responses targeting the hemagglutinin (HA) and neuraminidase (NA) surface proteins of circulating strains. These vaccines are formulated to protect against the two major influenza A subtypes, H1N1 and H3N2, which predominate in human populations, as well as influenza B lineages in trivalent formulations. The World Health Organization (WHO) coordinates global surveillance to recommend vaccine strains twice yearly, ensuring formulations address prevalent antigenic variants based on antigenic cartography and genetic sequencing data from the Global Influenza Surveillance and Response System (GISRS).
Three main types of influenza vaccines are available: inactivated influenza vaccines (IIVs), live-attenuated influenza vaccines (LAIVs), and recombinant HA vaccines. IIVs, the most widely used, contain killed virus particles and are administered intramuscularly; they include standard-dose, high-dose, and adjuvanted options suitable for various age groups, including adults over 65. LAIVs use temperature-sensitive, replication-incompetent viruses delivered intranasally, primarily recommended for healthy children and young adults aged 2 to 49 years due to their ability to induce mucosal immunity. Recombinant vaccines express HA protein produced in insect cells via baculovirus vectors, avoiding whole-virus cultivation and suitable for individuals with egg allergies. Trivalent formulations incorporate two influenza A strains (H1N1pdm09 and H3N2) alongside one influenza B lineage (Victoria); the B/Yamagata lineage has not been detected since March 2020 and is considered extinct, prompting WHO to recommend trivalent vaccines excluding it since the 20242025 season, while some quadrivalent vaccines may still include it unnecessarily.
Vaccine production employs three primary platforms: egg-based, cell-culture-based, and recombinant. Egg-based manufacturing, used for most IIVs and LAIVs, involves propagating viruses in embryonated chicken eggs, a method established since the 1940s but prone to supply constraints during high-demand periods like pandemics. Cell-culture platforms, such as those using Madin-Darby canine kidney (MDCK) cells, offer faster production and avoid egg-related issues, exemplified by vaccines like Flucelvax. Recombinant methods produce HA proteins without live virus propagation, enhancing safety and scalability, as seen in Flublok. WHO strain recommendations guide manufacturers to update antigens annually; for the 2025-2026 northern hemisphere season, egg-based trivalent vaccines include A/Victoria/4897/2022 (H1N1)pdm09-like and A/Croatia/10136RV/2023 (H3N2)-like viruses, while cell-culture or recombinant versions use A/Wisconsin/67/2022 (H1N1)pdm09-like and A/District of Columbia/27/2023 (H3N2)-like viruses, alongside B/Austria/1359417/2021 (Victoria)-like strains.
Vaccine efficacy typically ranges from 40% to 60% in preventing influenza-like illness when well-matched to circulating strains, with higher protection (up to 70%) against hospitalization and severe outcomes in vaccinated individuals. Efficacy diminishes against antigenically drifted variants, often falling below 20% for mismatched H3N2 strains, underscoring the need for annual updates. High-risk groups, such as older adults and those with comorbidities, benefit from boosters or enhanced formulations like high-dose IIVs, which elicit stronger antibody responses and reduce severe disease risk by 24% compared to standard doses.
Key challenges include egg-adaptation mutations during production, particularly in H3N2 viruses, where amino acid changes in HA (e.g., at position 156 or 225) alter antigenicity and reduce effectiveness by up to 30% against cell-passaged reference strains. Efforts toward universal vaccines focus on conserved epitopes, such as the HA stalk domain, which exhibits over 90% sequence conservation across subtypes and induces broadly neutralizing antibodies capable of cross-protection against diverse influenza A strains. Recent surveillance of clade 2.3.4.4b H5N1 threats has prompted evaluations showing partial heterologous protection from seasonal vaccines, informing potential pre-pandemic updates.

Treatment and antivirals

Treatment of Influenza A virus infections primarily involves supportive care to alleviate symptoms and prevent complications, alongside antiviral medications when indicated. Supportive measures include ensuring adequate hydration through oral fluids or, in severe cases, intravenous rehydration to maintain electrolyte balance and prevent dehydration from fever and reduced intake. Antipyretics such as acetaminophen are recommended to manage fever and discomfort, while aspirin should be avoided in children and adolescents due to the risk of Reye's syndrome. For patients with pneumonia or respiratory distress, supplemental oxygen therapy is essential to support oxygenation.
Antiviral drugs are the cornerstone of specific therapy for Influenza A, with neuraminidase inhibitors being the first-line options. Oseltamivir, administered orally twice daily for five days, and zanamivir, given via inhalation twice daily for five days, reduce the duration of illness by approximately one to two days when initiated within 48 hours of symptom onset, particularly in high-risk individuals. Intravenous peramivir is reserved for hospitalized patients with severe disease, providing a single-dose option for those unable to tolerate oral or inhaled routes. These agents inhibit viral neuraminidase, preventing the release of progeny viruses from infected cells and thereby limiting replication.
Resistance to oseltamivir has been observed in certain Influenza A strains, notably the 2009 H1N1 pandemic variant due to the H275Y mutation in the neuraminidase gene, which can reduce susceptibility and complicate treatment. As an alternative, baloxavir marboxil, a single-dose oral endonuclease inhibitor, targets a different viral enzyme and is approved for acute uncomplicated influenza in patients aged 5 years and older when started within 48 hours of symptoms. It demonstrates comparable efficacy to oseltamivir in reducing symptom duration but with faster viral clearance in some studies.
The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) and World Health Organization (WHO) recommend antiviral therapy for all hospitalized patients with confirmed or suspected Influenza A, as well as for outpatients at high risk of complications, such as those with chronic conditions or immunosuppression, regardless of symptom duration. Hospitalization criteria include severe respiratory distress, hypoxia, or signs of bacterial superinfection, with prompt initiation of antivirals to improve outcomes.
Adjunctive therapies address secondary complications, including empirical antibiotics for suspected bacterial co-infections like pneumonia, guided by clinical response and cultures. Monoclonal antibodies are under investigation in clinical trials for severe cases, such as H5N1 infections, showing potential to neutralize the virus and reduce mortality when combined with antivirals.

Research

Vaccine development

Vaccine development for Influenza A virus has focused on creating next-generation candidates that offer broader and more durable protection than traditional seasonal vaccines, targeting conserved viral components to address antigenic drift and pandemic threats. Universal influenza vaccines aim to elicit immune responses against invariant regions, such as the hemagglutinin (HA) stalk or the extracellular domain of matrix protein 2 (M2e), which are less prone to mutation across subtypes. For instance, HA stalk-directed approaches induce antibodies that neutralize diverse influenza A strains by focusing on the conserved stem domain rather than the variable head. M2e-based vaccines, often presented as tetramers or fused to carriers, have shown promise in preclinical models for cross-protection against multiple subtypes, with phase II trials of the M-001 vaccine demonstrating safety and immunogenicity in adults.
Advanced platforms are accelerating these efforts. Messenger RNA (mRNA) vaccines, leveraging lipid nanoparticle delivery, enable rapid adaptation to emerging strains; Moderna's mRNA-1018 candidate for pandemic H5N1 influenza elicited robust immune responses in phase I/II trials, with 97.8% of participants achieving protective antibodies three weeks post-vaccination. Viral vector platforms, such as adenovirus type 5 (Ad5) expressing HA (Ad5-HA), provide strong T-cell and mucosal immunity; intranasal Ad5-HA vaccination protected against homologous and heterologous challenges in animal models, offering complete protection at low doses. Nanoparticle-based vaccines, including ferritin nanoparticles displaying stabilized HA stems, enhance immunogenicity through multivalent antigen presentation; NIAID's H2HA-Ferritin vaccine was safe and induced stalk-specific antibodies in a phase I trial of healthy adults, advancing toward broader group 2 HA coverage.
To mitigate zoonotic risks from avian strains, vaccines target H5 and H7 subtypes in poultry and humans. Recombinant H5/H7 vaccines in poultry reduced H7N9 positivity by 92% and human cases by 98% in Guangdong Province, China, from 20172018, demonstrating their role in breaking transmission chains. For humans, the U.S. maintains stockpiles of H5N1 vaccines from multiple manufacturers, produced in eggs or cell cultures, sufficient for rapid deployment in pandemics, though not yet licensed for routine use.
Key challenges include achieving broad protection across HA phylogenetic groups 1 (e.g., H1, H5) and 2 (e.g., H3, H7), as current candidates often favor one group, and ensuring efficacy in the face of pre-existing immunity. Adjuvants like AS03 address dose-sparing needs for pandemics by boosting antibody titers and breadth, as seen in enhanced responses to chimeric HA vaccines, though historical reactogenicity concerns persist.
Recent progress in 2025 includes accelerated mRNA vaccine trials for H5N1 following spillovers into U.S. dairy cattle; although HHS awarded funding in January 2025 to support Moderna's efforts to match circulating clades, this was terminated in May 2025. Preclinical mRNA-lipid nanoparticle vaccines protected calves against severe disease, eliciting strong neutralizing antibodies.

Antiviral and therapeutic research

Research into next-generation antivirals for Influenza A virus focuses on targeting viral replication machinery to overcome limitations of existing neuraminidase inhibitors, such as emerging resistance. Favipiravir, a pyrazine carboxamide derivative that acts as an RNA-dependent RNA polymerase inhibitor, has demonstrated broad-spectrum activity against influenza A subtypes by inducing viral mutagenesis and halting genome replication. Clinical trials, including phase III evaluations, have shown its efficacy in reducing symptom duration and viral load in patients with uncomplicated influenza A infections, particularly when administered early, though its use is tempered by potential teratogenic effects. Similarly, pimodivir (also known as VX-787), a small-molecule inhibitor of the PB2 cap-binding domain in the viral polymerase complex, has advanced to phase III trials for severe influenza cases, exhibiting potent inhibition of influenza A replication in hospitalized patients and synergizing with standard-of-care treatments to shorten recovery time.
Host-directed therapies represent a promising shift toward modulating human cellular factors to impede influenza A infection, potentially reducing the risk of viral resistance. Strategies targeting interferon (IFN) pathways, such as agonists like poly(I:C) that enhance innate immune responses, have shown preclinical efficacy in boosting antiviral cytokine production to limit viral spread in respiratory epithelial cells. Inhibition of the TMPRSS2 protease, which facilitates viral entry by cleaving the hemagglutinin protein, has been explored using compounds like MI-432, demonstrating blockade of influenza A entry in cell models and offering a pan-influenza approach applicable to multiple subtypes. Additionally, small interfering RNA (siRNA) therapeutics, including 5-triphosphate-modified siRNAs, provide dual-action by silencing viral genes (e.g., M1/M2) while activating RIG-I pathways for enhanced innate immunity.
Advancements in diagnostics are enhancing rapid detection and surveillance of influenza A subtypes, enabling timely therapeutic interventions. CRISPR-based assays, leveraging Cas12a or Cas13a nucleases combined with recombinase polymerase amplification, allow point-of-need subtyping of avian and human influenza A strains (e.g., H5N1, H9N2) with high sensitivity and specificity within 30-60 minutes, facilitating outbreak response in resource-limited settings. Complementing these, AI-driven genomic surveillance tools analyze viral sequences to predict antiviral resistance, such as oseltamivir susceptibility in circulating strains; frameworks like FluRisk integrate machine learning on over 1,000 molecular markers to forecast mutation impacts on drug efficacy, aiding proactive adjustments in treatment protocols.
Immunotherapeutic approaches harness antibody-mediated neutralization to combat severe influenza A infections. Broadly neutralizing monoclonal antibodies like VIS410, which bind conserved epitopes on the hemagglutinin stem of group 1 influenza A viruses (e.g., H1N1, H5N1), have progressed to phase II trials, showing reduced viral replication and symptom alleviation in uncomplicated cases without significant adverse events. Convalescent plasma therapy, rich in polyclonal antibodies from recovered patients, has proven viable for H5N1 infections, with early administration leading to rapid viral clearance and improved survival in case series of severe avian influenza.
As of 2025, trials are evaluating dual-action drugs to address resistance in H3N2 strains dominant in the 2024-2025 season, where neuraminidase inhibitor resistance remains below 1% (around 0.1-1%) in some clades but with emerging mutations in subclade K (J.2.4.1) contributing to a potentially severe season. Investigational agents like CD388, a zanamivir-Fc conjugate with dual neuraminidase inhibition and immune effector functions, demonstrate enhanced potency against resistant H3N2 variants in preclinical models, while PROTACs targeting viral-host protein interactions offer broad efficacy against oseltamivir-resistant isolates. These efforts underscore a move toward combination regimens to mitigate the high resistance rates observed in recent surveillance data.
