Fishlake National Forest
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Fishlake National Forest is a United States National Forest in south-central Utah, covering approximately 1.8 million acres across nine counties: Beaver, Garfield, Iron, Juab, Millard, Piute, Sanpete, Sevier, and Wayne. Named for Fish Lake, Utah's largest natural freshwater mountain lake at an elevation of over 8,800 feet, the forest encompasses diverse landscapes from sagebrush-covered valleys and aspen groves to alpine meadows and coniferous forests. Elevations range from about 4,740 feet in the Canyon Range to 12,169 feet at Delano Peak, the highest point in the Tushar Mountains. It supports rich wildlife habitats for species including mule deer, elk, black bears, cougars, wild turkeys, and mountain goats, while serving as a critical watershed for nearby agricultural valleys and communities.
Established on February 10, 1899, as the Fish Lake Forest Reserve by President William McKinley to safeguard the Fish Lake and Fremont River watersheds, the area initially spanned 67,840 acres. In 1907, it was redesignated as Fishlake National Forest under the broader national forest system, with subsequent expansions through mergers: the Glenwood Forest Reserve joined in 1908, and the Fillmore National Forest was incorporated in 1923, bringing the total administered acreage to 1,764,072. The forest's headquarters, originally in Salina, Utah, relocated to Richfield in 1959 to better manage its growing scope. Historical ties extend to ancient indigenous peoples, with nearby Fremont Indian State Park preserving rock art and artifacts from a Fremont community that thrived here around 1,000 years ago.
The forest offers abundant recreational opportunities across its expansive terrain, including over 1,000 miles of trails for hiking, mountain biking, and horseback riding, as well as hunting, fishing in lakes and streams stocked with trout, and winter activities like snowmobiling. Key attractions include 29 developed campgrounds, dispersed camping sites, and rentable cabins such as the Aquarius Guard Station, alongside scenic byways like the Fishlake Scenic Byway at 8,850 feet elevation. Notable natural wonders encompass the Pando aspen clonebelieved to be the world's largest and oldest living organismand pristine areas designated under the 1964 Wilderness Act, emphasizing multiple-use management for recreation, timber, and conservation as outlined in the 1960 Multiple-Use Sustained-Yield Act. Managed by the U.S. Forest Service's Intermountain Region, the forest remains a vital ecological and economic resource, with ongoing projects addressing wildfire risk, habitat restoration, and visitor access.

Overview

Location and Boundaries

Fishlake National Forest is situated in south-central Utah, encompassing diverse landscapes across nine counties: Beaver, Garfield, Iron, Juab, Millard, Piute, Sanpete, Sevier, and Wayne. The forest's administrative headquarters are located in Richfield, Utah, the nearest major city, facilitating access for visitors and management operations. Centered approximately at 384230N 1115733W, it provides a central point of reference for its expansive terrain.
The forest's boundaries adjoin several other protected areas, including Dixie National Forest to the south, where shared lands support interconnected ecosystems and recreational corridors. To the north, it connects with Uinta-Wasatch-Cache National Forest, and to the east, it borders Manti-La Sal National Forest, creating a network of federal lands that enhance regional biodiversity and trail systems. These adjacencies reflect the forest's position along key physiographic transitions, incorporating portions of the Colorado Plateau, Basin and Range Province, and Middle Rocky Mountains, which contribute to its varied geological and ecological character.
Elevations within the forest range from a low of about 4,200 feet to a high of 12,169 feet at Delano Peak in the Tushar Mountains, offering a broad spectrum of habitats from lower valleys to alpine summits. The Tushar Mountains, rising over 12,000 feet, form a prominent backbone of the forest and serve as a critical watershed source. This elevational diversity underscores the forest's role in spanning multiple ecological zones across its south-central Utah footprint.

Size and Administration

Fishlake National Forest encompasses approximately 1.8 million acres (7,300 km) of public land in central Utah.
The forest is administered by the U.S. Forest Service, an agency within the United States Department of Agriculture (USDA), which oversees its protection, recreation, and resource management. Local operations are coordinated through the supervisor's office in Richfield, Utah, with public contact available via district offices for permits, information, and reporting. The forest is divided into four ranger districtsBeaver, Fillmore, Fremont River (based in Loa), and Richfieldeach responsible for specific geographic areas and activities such as trail maintenance and fire prevention.
The forest attracts significant annual visitation, with historical estimates around 450,000 visitors based on early 2000s data; recent figures are not publicly available but reflect growing interest in outdoor recreation. The forest's name derives from Fish Lake, Utah's largest natural freshwater mountain lake, which lies within its boundaries and serves as a key recreational and ecological feature.

History

Indigenous and Early Use

The region encompassing what is now Fishlake National Forest has been occupied by humans for approximately 12,000 years, beginning with Paleoindian peoples who hunted large Ice Age mammals such as mammoths and bison in the warming post-glacial landscape. Over subsequent millennia, Archaic and later cultures, including the Fremont culture from approximately 250 to 1350 CE, who left behind rock art, pithouses, and granaries, as well as ancestors of the Ute and Paiute peoples, sustained themselves through foraging, hunting small game, and gathering wild plants across the high plateaus and valleys. These early inhabitants relied on the area's diverse ecosystems for seasonal resources, with evidence of campsites and tools indicating long-term adaptation to the montane environment.
The Ute people were among the primary indigenous users of the Fish Lake Basin prior to the arrival of the Paiute, employing it as a vital summer retreat for hunting, fishing, and spiritual ceremonies to connect with the Great Spirit. The Ute and Paiute later coexisted peacefully in the area, sharing its cool elevationsreaching nearly 9,000 feetfor seasonal migrations to escape lowland heat, where they fished abundant trout, hunted rock chucks and other game, and performed sacred rituals like sun dances and burials. Paiute oral histories and archaeological findings confirm this basin as a crossroads of cultures, with continuous use for these activities spanning centuries until European encroachment disrupted traditional patterns.
European exploration reached the Fishlake area in the early 19th century via the Fish Lake Cutoff of the Old Spanish Trail, a rugged trade route used by Spanish and Mexican traders to transport goods and, regrettably, to raid Native communities for captives destined for enslavement. Mid-century Mormon pioneers, arriving in Utah's Sevier Valley from the 1850s onward, further integrated the region into Euro-American networks, establishing settlements that capitalized on its waters for sustenance and expansion.
A defining moment for indigenous water access came on March 11, 1889, when the Paiute Tribe conveyed all rights to Fish Lake's waters to the Fremont Irrigation Company in exchange for nominal goodsnine horses, 500 pounds of flour, one beef steer, and one suit of clotheswhile retaining perpetual fishing privileges at the lake's outlet. This transaction curtailed traditional Paiute reliance on the lake for fishing and migration, paving the way for settler-dominated resource extraction. In the late 1800s, Mormon settlers developed extensive irrigation canals and initiated large-scale ranching, diverting streams and introducing livestock grazing that modified watersheds, reduced riparian habitats, and shifted vegetation patterns across the plateau.

Establishment and Expansion

The Fish Lake Forest Reserve was established on February 10, 1899, by President William McKinley, encompassing 67,840 acres in central Utah primarily to safeguard the watersheds of Fish Lake and the Fremont River. This initial designation marked one of the early efforts under the Forest Reserve Act of 1891 to protect timber and water resources in the American West. Subsequent proclamations expanded the reserve's boundaries, including an enlargement in 1904, to enhance watershed protection and forest integrity.
In 1906, President Theodore Roosevelt created the Fillmore Forest Reserve on May 2 (399,600 acres), and in 1907, the Glenwood Forest Reserve on February 6 (173,896 acres), both adjacent to the existing Fish Lake Reserve, further consolidating federal oversight of regional water sources. On March 4, 1907, the Fish Lake Forest Reserve was renamed the Fishlake National Forest, reflecting the broader transition of forest reserves to national forests under the U.S. Forest Service. The formal establishment as a national forest occurred on July 1, 1908, when Executive Order 908 merged the Fishlake and Glenwood units, creating a unified administration focused on multiple resource uses including timber, grazing, and recreation. A further proclamation in March 1911 added lands to the forest.
Further expansion came on September 24, 1923, via Executive Order 3908, which integrated the Fillmore National Forest into the Fishlake National Forest, significantly increasing its acreage to over 1.5 million acres. Throughout the 20th century, additional boundary adjustments occurred, including land additions from the Manti National Forest in 1913 and 1917, and further incorporations via executive orders and public land orders in 1930, 1948, 1956, and 1967, all aimed at bolstering watershed protection and ecological connectivity. These changes solidified the forest's role in sustaining Utah's water supply and natural resources.

Geography and Geology

Terrain and Landforms

Fishlake National Forest encompasses a diverse topography shaped by tectonic activity, volcanism, and erosion, spanning elevations from 4,740 feet (1,445 m) in the Canyon Range to 12,169 feet (3,709 m) at Delano Peak in its highest peaks. The forest's landscape includes rugged mountain ranges, high plateaus, fault-block basins, and transitional zones between physiographic provinces, creating a mosaic of alpine ridges, steep canyons, and expansive meadows. This varied terrain results from the interplay of Basin and Range extension, volcanic episodes, and Pleistocene glaciation, which carved deep valleys and deposited moraines across the region.
The forest's major mountain ranges dominate its western and central areas. The Tushar Mountains, Utah's third-highest range, form the core of the forest with peaks reaching 12,169 feet (3,709 m) at Delano Peak; these mountains consist of layered volcanic rocks from the Bullion Canyon Volcanics (2235 million years ago) and Mount Belknap Volcanics (about 21 million years ago), including the Joe Lott Tuff Member, with a prominent caldera collapse around 19 million years ago. To the south, the Monroe Mountains arose from explosive volcanic eruptions approximately 25 million years ago, forming the expansive Monroe Peak Caldera (14 by 11 miles), marked by flood deposits and massive landslides such as the Thompson Creek Landslide. The Pahvant Range, along the eastern boundary, exhibits a "split personality" with Paleozoic marine sedimentary rocks (over 240 million years old) on its western slopes creating steep cliffs, while the eastern front features younger Tertiary conglomerates, sandstones, and shales; ongoing uplift along active faults continues to elevate this range. High plateaus like Thousand Lake Mountain, capped by Miocene volcanic rocks and Pliocene lava flows, add to the forest's elevated landforms, sculpted by landslides into colorful monoliths and red rock outcrops.
Geologically, the forest lies at the transition between the Colorado Plateau to the east and the Basin and Range Province to the west, with much of its terrain originating from the Miocene Marysvale volcanic field (3222 million years ago), which produced ash flows, lava domes, and calderas across the area. The Fish Lake Basin, a key lowland feature, formed through extensional faulting where a central block dropped between parallel faults over a 10-mile span, later filled with basalt flows from fissures and modified by glacial erosion that incised canyons like Pelican Canyon and left moraine deposits. A notable landform is the sandstone cliffs near Chalk Creek, where Miocene-age sedimentary layers host ancient petroglyphs, illustrating the exposure of older stratigraphic units amid the volcanic-dominated landscape. Overall, fluvial, eolian, and glacial processes have rounded volcanic slopes and exposed diverse rock types, contributing to the forest's alpine zones and canyon systems.

Hydrology and Water Resources

Fishlake National Forest serves as a critical hydrological hub in central Utah, encompassing the headwaters of major rivers including the Fremont River, Otter Creek, and Sevier River, which originate from high-elevation plateaus and volcanic highlands within the forest. These waterways, along with numerous alpine lakes and streams, form the core of the Sevier River Basin, one of Utah's largest entirely internal drainage systems. The forest's watersheds protect vital water sources for downstream communities, channeling precipitation and snowmelt into streams that support regional ecosystems and human needs.
Prominent among the forest's water bodies is Fish Lake, Utah's largest natural mountain lake, spanning approximately 6 miles (9.7 km) in length and covering 2,500 acres (10 km) at an elevation exceeding 8,800 feet (2,680 m). This reservoir, fed by highland streams, exemplifies the forest's alpine hydrology, with its waters contributing to the broader Sevier system. The Fremont River emerges from springs and snow-fed tributaries in the Boulder Mountain area, while Otter Creek and the upper Sevier River draw from the Tushar and Paunsaugunt plateaus, sustaining perennial and intermittent streams across the landscape. Collectively, these features yield an average of 823,000 acre-feet annually to the Sevier River Basin.
Historically, water from the forest has driven irrigation development in central Utah, with approximately 903,460 acre-feet diverted yearly to irrigate 354,320 acres of farmland, primarily for alfalfa and pasture. Today, it continues to supply downstream agriculture and municipalities, including towns like Richfield and Torrey, while supporting groundwater recharge estimated at millions of acre-feet in adjacent valleys. The U.S. Forest Service manages these resources through watershed restoration and water rights programs to maintain quality and quantity for over 180 million people nationwide who rely on national forest-derived water.
Hydrological challenges arise from seasonal variations, with peak flows dominated by snowmelt from April to June, followed by diminished summer base flows reliant on springs and monsoon rains in July and August. Early diversions, numbering in the hundreds across the forest, have altered regimes by reducing peak and base flows, dewatering stream reaches, and limiting downstream delivery to just 4% of the Sevier's total volume reaching terminal lakes. Climate influences exacerbate these issues, with variable precipitation and reduced snowpack threatening consistent supply, prompting ongoing monitoring of groundwater levels and riparian health.

Ecology

Flora and Vegetation

The Fishlake National Forest encompasses a variety of vegetation zones that transition from desert shrub communities in the lower elevations to coniferous forests and alpine tundra at higher altitudes, reflecting the diverse topography and climate gradients across its 1.8 million acres. Lower elevations feature pinyon-juniper woodlands and mountain brush, including curl-leaf mountain mahogany and Gambel oak, while mid-elevations are dominated by open mountain meadows lush with forbs and grasses interspersed among aspen stands. Higher elevations support mixed conifer forests of Engelmann spruce, subalpine fir, ponderosa pine, and Douglas-fir. The forest is approximately 50-60% forested overall.
Majestic aspen groves (Populus tremuloides) are a hallmark of the forest's vegetation, particularly in the mid-elevation zones where they provide critical ecological functions such as soil stabilization and habitat connectivity. A prominent example is the Pando clonal colony, located about one mile southwest of Fish Lake along Utah Route 25, which spans 106 acres and consists of over 47,000 genetically identical stems connected by a single root system. Estimated to be between 16,000 and 80,000 years old and weighing approximately 13 million pounds, Pando represents one of the oldest and largest known organisms on Earth. These aspen ecosystems support a diverse array of understory plants and contribute to overall biodiversity by facilitating nutrient cycling and erosion control.
The forest hosts high vegetation diversity, with numerous plant community types including vascular plants, shrubs, forbs, and grasses, as mapped in ecological assessments using USGS data. Aspen stands, in particular, enhance habitat diversity for various species, though they face ongoing decline due to prolonged drought stress and increased vulnerability to insect infestations such as those from forest tent caterpillars and leaf miners. Additionally, excessive browsing by ungulates like mule deer and elk has severely impacted regeneration, particularly in Pando, where young suckers are heavily grazed. Climate-induced drought exacerbates these threats, leading to widespread die-off in aspen populations across the Intermountain West, including in the Fishlake.

Fauna and Wildlife

Fishlake National Forest supports a diverse array of wildlife, owing to its varied elevations ranging from 5,000 to over 12,000 feet, which create multiple habitats for mammals, birds, fish, reptiles, and amphibians. The forest's ecosystems, including montane forests, alpine meadows, and riparian zones, foster high biodiversity, with species adapted to seasonal changes and migratory patterns.
Among mammals, large herbivores such as Rocky Mountain elk (Cervus canadensis) and mule deer (Odocoileus hemionus) are prominent, utilizing the forest's migratory corridors for summer foraging in high meadows and winter ranges in lower valleys. Black bears (Ursus americanus) roam widely, foraging on berries, roots, and occasionally scavenging, while cougars (Puma concolor) prey on ungulates like deer and elk across the rugged terrain. Moose (Alces alces) inhabit wetland areas near lakes and streams, and introduced mountain goats (Oreamnos americanus) thrive on steep, rocky slopes. Smaller mammals including red foxes (Vulpes vulpes), bobcats (Lynx rufus), and beavers (Castor canadensis) contribute to the ecosystem by controlling rodent populations and engineering wetland habitats. These populations are actively managed through cooperative efforts between the U.S. Forest Service and the Utah Division of Wildlife Resources to maintain healthy herd sizes.
The forest is a notable birdwatching destination, particularly around Fish Lake, where over 150 species have been observed. Raptors such as bald eagles (Haliaeetus leucocephalus) nest near water bodies, while great horned owls (Bubo virginianus) and various woodpeckers, including the northern flicker (Colaptes auratus), inhabit forested areas. Wild turkeys (Meleagris gallopavo) are common in open meadows and aspen groves, which provide foraging and roosting sites for browsing birds.
Aquatic fauna thrive in the forest's lakes, reservoirs, and streams, with Fish Lake renowned for trophy trout fishing. Rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss), cutthroat trout (including native Bonneville cutthroat, Oncorhynchus clarkii utah), and lake trout (mackinaw, Salvelinus namaycush) dominate, supported by cold, oxygen-rich waters. Brook trout (Salvelinus fontinalis) and brown trout (Salmo trutta) are also present in tributaries, contributing to a robust fishery managed for sustainability.
Reptiles and amphibians occupy lower-elevation habitats, with species like the Great Basin rattlesnake (Crotalus oreganus lutosus) and other non-venomous snakes found in arid canyons and rocky outcrops. Amphibians, including the tiger salamander (Ambystoma tigrinum), breed in temporary ponds and streams, playing key roles in insect control and nutrient cycling. The forest's elevation gradient enhances overall wildlife diversity by providing refugia for species sensitive to climate variations.

Management and Conservation

Resource Management Practices

Fishlake National Forest operates under the multiple-use mandate established by the Multiple-Use Sustained-Yield Act of 1960, which directs the U.S. Forest Service to balance commodity production, recreation, and conservation on national forest lands. This framework guides resource management practices, emphasizing sustained yield of timber, forage, and minerals while protecting soil, water, and wildlife habitats. The 1986 Land and Resource Management Plan (LRMP), amended periodically, provides specific standards for these activities, ensuring long-term productivity across the forest's 1.8 million acres.
Livestock grazing is a primary use, authorized through a permit system for local ranchers who manage cattle and sheep on approximately 1.3 million acres of allotments, with utilization around 137,053 animal unit months (AUMs) as of 1986. Permittees are responsible for maintaining fences, water developments, and other improvements to support sustainable practices that prevent resource degradation and promote rangeland health. For example, recent reauthorizations on allotments like Koosharem and Rock Springs emphasize livestock distribution to avoid overgrazing, aligning with forest goals to conserve vegetation and watersheds. In February 2025, local ranchers filed a lawsuit against the U.S. Forest Service, alleging arbitrary and science-flawed grazing restrictions on Fishlake allotments.
Sustainable timber harvesting targets coniferous stands of Engelmann spruce, ponderosa pine, Douglas-fir, and subalpine fir, as well as aspen communities that have degraded due to fire suppression and conifer encroachment. Annual harvest levels are maintained at 2.5 to 3.0 million board feet (MMBF), well below the maximum sustained yield of 8.3 MMBF (as per the 1986 Forest Plan), to enhance forest resilience, diversity, and regeneration through methods like selective cutting and prescribed fire. These practices restore aspen by promoting root sprouting on about 240,000 acres, supporting ecosystem health without compromising timber supply for local industries.
Mineral and energy resource management allows exploration and limited extraction on 99% of the forest, with restrictions varying by sensitivity (6% high, 38% moderate, 56% low). Key activities include coal production from the Sufco Mine (approximately 4.9 million short tons annually as of 2025), geothermal leasing at Sulphurdale for power generation up to 20 megawatts, and seismic surveys for oil and gas, alongside locatable minerals like uranium and gold panning. All operations undergo National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) reviews to minimize surface impacts and balance extraction with conservation objectives.
Ongoing monitoring occurs through annual Schedule of Proposed Actions (SOPA) reports and environmental assessments, tracking harvest volumes, grazing utilization, and allotment conditions to ensure compliance with LRMP standards. For instance, the 2024 SOPA details evaluations of multiple grazing allotments and range improvements, confirming sustainable levels that support both economic uses and ecological integrity.

Conservation Challenges and Efforts

Fishlake National Forest faces significant conservation challenges from wildfires, exacerbated by prolonged drought conditions and changing climate patterns. In 2025, the Monroe Canyon Fire, ignited in July, rapidly expanded to 73,721 acres across Sevier and Piute Counties, becoming Utah's largest wildfire of the year before reaching full containment in September. Later that year, the Wildcat Fire burned approximately 10 acres in Beaver County in October, challenging firefighters due to inaccessible terrain. These events highlight the forest's vulnerability to intense fire seasons, intensified by historical fire suppression that has allowed conifer encroachment, reducing fire-resilient aspen stands. Following the Monroe Canyon Fire, the Forest Service conducted Burned Area Emergency Response (BAER) assessments, with restoration costs estimated at $6.7 million to address soil stabilization, watershed protection, and habitat rehabilitation.
Drought and invasive species further compound these threats, with long-term dry conditions stressing ecosystems and facilitating the spread of non-native plants across Utah's forests, including Fishlake. Climate change poses particular risks to aspen groves, hindering tree maturity through altered precipitation and temperature regimes, which disrupt regeneration and increase susceptibility to pests and drought.
To address these issues, the U.S. Forest Service employs comprehensive fire management strategies, including prescribed burns to reduce fuel loads and restore fire-adapted ecosystems, alongside active suppression during uncontrolled blazes. For the iconic Pando aspen clone, protection efforts since the early 2000s have included installing fencing to exclude grazing by deer and elk, which previously inhibited regeneration, along with mechanical treatments and ongoing monitoring to promote clonal health. Watershed restoration initiatives focus on repairing historical diversions that altered natural flows, enhancing riparian habitats and water quality through revegetation and erosion control projects.
Partnerships play a vital role in these conservation efforts, with the U.S. Forest Service collaborating with the nonprofit Friends of Pandoestablished in 2021 and formalized as a partner in 2022to support on-the-ground projects like fence maintenance and research aimed at preserving biodiversity. Broader Forest Service ecosystem programs emphasize strategies to protect and restore habitat diversity, integrating fire, invasive species control, and climate adaptation measures.
In April 2025, the Forest Service launched a new web platform to improve public engagement on conservation, providing updated resources on fire prevention, habitat protection, and volunteer opportunities to foster community involvement in sustainability.

Recreation and Human Use

Visitor Activities

Fishlake National Forest provides a diverse array of outdoor recreation opportunities, attracting visitors seeking immersive experiences in its alpine landscapes and water bodies. Popular activities include fishing, particularly for trophy trout in lakes such as Fish Lake, the largest natural mountain lake in Utah; hiking along trails that access unique features like the Pando aspen clone and high-elevation alpine zones in the Tushar Mountains; camping in designated areas; hunting during designated big game seasons for species like deer, elk, and turkey; birdwatching, especially around wetland areas; and off-highway vehicle (OHV) use on approximately 2,700 miles of trails and roads.
Activities vary by season to capitalize on the forest's changing conditions. In summer, fishing and boating dominate, with motorized boating permitted on select lakes and non-motorized options like kayaking available on others; fall draws visitors for aspen viewing amid vibrant foliage, particularly around Pando. Winter offers snowmobiling on groomed trails and cross-country skiing in snowy meadows, while spring transitions bring opportunities for early-season hikes as snow melts.
Permits are required for many pursuits to ensure sustainable use and compliance with regulations. Fishing and hunting necessitate Utah Division of Wildlife Resources licenses, with specific seasons and limits for big game; OHV use requires an off-road vehicle sticker or permit for non-street-legal vehicles. Guided tours to sensitive sites like Pando are available through permitted commercial outfitters to minimize environmental impact.
Visitors should prioritize safety given the forest's high-elevation terrain, where weather can shift rapidly from sunny to stormy, even in summer, and elevations exceed 8,800 feet at key sites like Fish Lake. Encounters with wildlife, including black bears and large game animals, require precautions such as storing food properly and carrying bear spray; avalanche risks in winter necessitate checking conditions before snow-based activities. Always stay on designated trails to avoid hazards like unstable slopes and respect fire restrictions to prevent wildfires.

Facilities and Access

Fishlake National Forest provides a range of developed and dispersed camping options to accommodate visitors. The forest maintains 29 developed campgrounds, such as those at Fish Lake and Doctor Creek, equipped with amenities including restrooms, picnic tables, and fire rings. Reservations for these sites are available online through Recreation.gov or by calling 1-877-444-6777, with some operating on a first-come, first-served basis during off-peak periods. Dispersed camping is permitted across much of the forest outside restricted areas, allowing for primitive experiences without designated facilities, though popular spots may include basic toilets.
Historic cabins, including the Aquarius Guard Station, are available for rental and provide rustic lodging options tied to the forest's heritage. Camping stays are limited to 16 consecutive days, after which visitors must relocate at least five road miles away. Fees for developed sites vary by location and season, typically ranging from $10 to $25 per night for single-family units, with group sites up to $110; day-use fees, such as $10 per vehicle at picnic areas, apply in certain zones and can be covered by an Interagency Annual Pass.
Access to the forest is primarily via Utah State Routes 25, 72, and 153, which connect major entry points from towns like Richfield and Beaver. The trail system spans over 1,000 miles, encompassing hiking paths, the Paiute ATV Trail (with more than 275 miles of main route and extensive side trails), and groomed snowmobile routes totaling about 100 miles. Many backcountry roads and trails require four-wheel-drive vehicles or off-highway vehicles (OHVs) limited to 50 inches in width for safe navigation, particularly in rugged terrain like the Great Western Trail segments.
Ranger stations serve as key visitor centers, offering maps, permits, and informational resources. The Richfield Ranger District office, located at 115 East 900 North in Richfield, Utah, operates Monday through Friday from 8 a.m. to 4:30 p.m. Similarly, the Beaver Ranger District office at 575 South Main Street in Beaver, Utah, is open weekdays from 8 a.m. to 5 p.m., providing guidance on forest conditions and access. Additional districts in Fillmore and Loa offer comparable services.
Accessibility is influenced by seasonal factors, with closures possible due to severe weather, such as heavy snow in winter, or fire risks, as seen in ongoing 2025 restrictions and prescribed burns in areas like Merchant Valley, as well as the Monroe Canyon Fire closure order effective September 12, 2025. Road conditions can change abruptly from weather or maintenance, requiring visitors to check current alerts. In 2025, the forest transitioned to an updated online platform in April for improved access to reservations and information, enhancing logistics for planning visits.
