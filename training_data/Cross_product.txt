Definition

In Euclidean Three-Space

In three-dimensional Euclidean space R3\mathbb{R}^3R3, the cross product is a binary operation on two vectors u\mathbf{u}u and v\mathbf{v}v that yields a third vector uv\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}uv perpendicular to both u\mathbf{u}u and v\mathbf{v}v, lying normal to the plane they span. This resulting vector is unique up to sign, as any vector orthogonal to both inputs belongs to the one-dimensional subspace orthogonal to their plane, and the operation is defined specifically in this dimension due to the structure of oriented Euclidean space.
The cross product assumes familiarity with vector addition and scalar multiplication as prerequisites, positioning it as the third essential operation in vector algebra, complementing the dot productwhich produces a scalar measuring projectionand the scalar triple product u(vw)\mathbf{u} \cdot (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{w})u(vw), which computes signed volumes. In mathematics and physics, the standard orientation for the cross product follows the right-hand rule: curling the fingers of the right hand from u\mathbf{u}u to v\mathbf{v}v causes the thumb to point in the direction of uv\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}uv.
For intuition, consider two vectors in a two-dimensional plane, where an analogous operation yields a scalar equal to the signed area of the parallelogram they form; extending this to three dimensions, the cross product produces a vector normal to that plane, encoding both the area magnitude and the perpendicular direction. The magnitude of uv\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}uv relates to the sine of the angle between u\mathbf{u}u and v\mathbf{v}v.

Magnitude and Direction

The magnitude of the cross product ab\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}ab is given by the formula ab=absin\|\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}\| = \|\mathbf{a}\| \|\mathbf{b}\| \sin \thetaab=absin, where \theta is the angle between the vectors a\mathbf{a}a and b\mathbf{b}b. This quantity represents the area of the parallelogram spanned by a\mathbf{a}a and b\mathbf{b}b as its adjacent sides.
The direction of ab\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}ab is determined by the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of the right hand from a\mathbf{a}a to b\mathbf{b}b, with the thumb pointing in the direction of the cross product vector.
Special cases arise depending on the angle \theta: when a\mathbf{a}a and b\mathbf{b}b are parallel (=0\theta = 0^\circ=0 or 180180^\circ180), sin=0\sin \theta = 0sin=0, so ab=0\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{0}ab=0, the zero vector with zero magnitude; the magnitude is maximized at ab\|\mathbf{a}\| \|\mathbf{b}\|ab when the vectors are perpendicular (=90\theta = 90^\circ=90); it is also zero if either vector is the zero vector.
The cross product is antisymmetric, satisfying ba=(ab)\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{a} = -(\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b})ba=(ab), which reflects the reversal of direction under vector interchange while preserving the magnitude.

Geometric Interpretation

Parallelogram Area

The cross product ab\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}ab of two vectors in three-dimensional space serves as an area vector for the parallelogram they define, with its magnitude equal to the area of that parallelogram and its direction perpendicular to the plane containing a\mathbf{a}a and b\mathbf{b}b, determined by the right-hand rule. Geometrically, if a\mathbf{a}a is taken as the base of length a\|\mathbf{a}\|a, the height is the perpendicular distance from the tip of b\mathbf{b}b to the line of a\mathbf{a}a, given by bsin\|\mathbf{b}\| \sin \thetabsin where \theta is the angle between them; thus, the area is absin\|\mathbf{a}\| \|\mathbf{b}\| \sin \thetaabsin, matching the magnitude ab\|\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}\|ab. This interpretation holds for the signed area, where the vector ab\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}ab encodes both the scalar area and the orientation of the plane.
To visualize, consider a\mathbf{a}a and b\mathbf{b}b emanating from a common origin, their tips connected to form the parallelogram's opposite vertex; the fourth vertex completes the shape. The cross product vector emerges orthogonal to this plane, pointing in the direction where curling fingers from a\mathbf{a}a to b\mathbf{b}b aligns the thumb with the result. This normal direction distinguishes the "front" and "back" sides of the parallelogram, providing a signed sense crucial for oriented surfaces in geometry.
For more complex shapes like polygons lying in a plane, the total area vector can be computed by partitioning the polygon into triangles and summing the cross products for each, scaled by one-half for the triangular areas; this yields the overall signed area vector normal to the polygon's plane. This approach extends the parallelogram interpretation, analogous to the 2D shoelace formula but using vector cross products to capture both magnitude and orientation in 3D.
A concrete example is a triangle with sides formed by vectors u\mathbf{u}u and v\mathbf{v}v, where the area is half the parallelogram area: 12uv\frac{1}{2} \|\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}\|21uv, with the full cross product uv\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}uv giving the signed area vector.

A similar construction extends to the octonions O\mathbb{O}O, an eight-dimensional non-associative division algebra over R\mathbb{R}R, where the imaginary octonions (seven-dimensional subspace orthogonal to the real axis) admit a cross product defined analogously via the imaginary part of the commutator, yielding a binary operation that preserves the norm but loses associativity. Unlike quaternions, octonion multiplication is neither associative nor fully alternative, though it remains alternative for subalgebras generated by two elements, and the cross product in this setting calibrates the exceptional Lie group G2G_2G2. This generalization underscores the cross product's role in higher-dimensional division algebras, where dimensionality constraints limit stable cross products to dimensions 3 and 7 beyond the trivial cases.
An illustrative application appears in quaternion-based rotations, where the Rodrigues rotation formula, derivable from quaternion multiplication, expresses the rotated vector v\mathbf{v}'v around a unit axis u\mathbf{u}u by angle \theta as
v=vcos+(uv)sin+u(uv)(1cos).
\mathbf{v}' = \mathbf{v} \cos \theta + (\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}) \sin \theta + \mathbf{u} (\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}) (1 - \cos \theta).

This formula arises from conjugating v\mathbf{v}v (as a pure quaternion) by the unit quaternion q=cos(/2)+sin(/2)uq = \cos(\theta/2) + \sin(\theta/2) \mathbf{u}q=cos(/2)+sin(/2)u, yielding $ \mathbf{v}' = q \mathbf{v} q^{-1} $, and expanding the product using the cross and dot products.

Multilinear Extensions

In multilinear algebra, the cross product of two vectors in three-dimensional Euclidean space can be understood as a special instance of the wedge product within the exterior algebra framework. This duality ensures the cross product's properties, such as orthogonality and the magnitude-area relation, emerge naturally from the exterior structure.

Historical Development

Early Concepts

The roots of cross product concepts trace back to ancient Greek geometry, where mathematicians like Euclid explored areas of parallelograms and other figures as products of linear dimensions, without employing vector representations. In Euclid's Elements (circa 300 BCE), areas were treated as magnitudes derived from geometric constructions, such as the area of a parallelogram formed by two line segments, which intuitively parallels the magnitude aspect of a modern cross product but remained purely scalar and geometric. This approach emphasized proofs based on axioms and common notions, focusing on spatial relationships rather than directed quantities.
An early algebraic precursor appeared in 1773 when Joseph-Louis Lagrange introduced the component form of both the dot and cross products to study the volume of a tetrahedron in three dimensions. In calculating the tetrahedron's volume using coordinates, Lagrange effectively used expressions equivalent to the scalar triple product, involving the cross product components.
In the 18th century, Leonhard Euler and Joseph-Louis Lagrange further developed intuitive notions of cross product-like operations through mechanics, particularly in analyzing rotational effects via moment arms, which served as precursors to torque. Euler, in works like Theoria motus corporum solidorum (1765), examined the moment of a force about an axis by considering the perpendicular distance from the line of action to the axis, effectively capturing the sine of the angle between force and lever arm in equilibrium and motion problems. Lagrange, building on this in Mcanique Analytique (1788), integrated moment arms into the principle of virtual work, where infinitesimal displacements multiplied by forces yield zero for equilibrium, implicitly involving angular dependencies akin to cross product components. These treatments advanced the physical understanding of perpendicular interactions without formal vector notation.
Physical intuition for cross product ideas also emerged in electromagnetism through Andr-Marie Ampre's force law (1820), which described interactions between current-carrying elements with terms implying dependence on the relative orientations of current directions and the connecting line, resembling a cross product structure. Ampre's formulation, motivated by rsted's discovery of magnetic effects from currents, quantified forces as varying with the sine of angles between elements, providing an early empirical basis for vectorial perpendicularity in nature.
August Ferdinand Mbius contributed to early vector-like methods in 1837 with Lehrbuch der Statik, applying his barycentric calculus to statics by representing forces and positions as weighted combinations of points, enabling calculations of resultant effects that anticipated vector addition in three dimensions. This geometric framework treated directed segments in a manner that facilitated resolving forces into components perpendicular to lines of action, bridging intuitive mechanics toward algebraic vector methods.

Formalization and Notation

The formalization of the cross product emerged in the mid-19th century through William Rowan Hamilton's development of quaternions, where the vector part of the product of two pure vector quaternions corresponds to the modern cross product. In 1843, Hamilton introduced quaternions as $ q = a + bi + cj + dk $, with the multiplication rule yielding a vector component that matches the cross product of the imaginary parts, such as the term $ i(yz' - zy') + j(zx' - xz') + k(xy' - yx') $ for vectors (x,y,z)(x, y, z)(x,y,z) and (x,y,z)(x', y', z')(x,y,z). This cross-like operation captured the perpendicularity essential for three-dimensional rotations, laying the algebraic foundation for vector products without separating scalar and vector aspects initially. Hamilton's 1846 paper formalized terms like "scalar" and "vector," and his 1853 Lectures on Quaternions expanded these into a comprehensive system, influencing subsequent vector analysis.
By the 1880s, Josiah Willard Gibbs and Oliver Heaviside independently advanced a scalar-vector notation that decoupled the cross product from quaternions, emphasizing its geometric utility. Gibbs's unpublished pamphlet Elements of Vector Analysis (18811884), printed privately for Yale students, introduced the symbol for the cross product, denoted as \mathbf{\alpha} \times \mathbf{\beta}, and provided its component formula in Cartesian coordinates:

\end{vmatrix} = (a_2 b_3 - a_3 b_2) \mathbf{i} + (a_3 b_1 - a_1 b_3) \mathbf{j} + (a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1) \mathbf{k},

Heaviside, in his 1885 paper on electrical theory and 1893 book Electromagnetic Theory, adopted a nearly identical notation, promoting the cross product as a tool for physical applications like electromagnetism. Gibbs's pamphlet, though not widely circulated initially, exerted significant influence through its adoption in teaching materials, sparking debates in scientific journals from 1890 to 1894 that resolved in favor of this system by the early 1900s.
In the 1890s, Giuseppe Peano provided the first fully axiomatic treatment of vector spaces and operations, including the cross product, within a rigorous geometric framework. In his 1888 Calcolo geometrico secondo lAusdehnungslehre di Hermann Grassmann, Peano defined vectors as elements of linear systems with axioms for addition, scalar multiplication, and products, extending Grassmann's exterior algebra to axiomatize the cross product as an antisymmetric bilinear operation yielding a perpendicular vector. His 1893 Lezioni di analisi infinitesimale and subsequent Formulario mathematico (18951908) further formalized these axioms, emphasizing logical foundations and applications in differential equations, which solidified the cross product's place in abstract mathematics.
The Gibbs-Heaviside notation achieved standardization in textbooks by 1900, notably through Edwin Bidwell Wilson's 1901 Vector Analysis, which popularized the symbol and component formula globally. Gibbs's pamphlet played a pivotal role in this adoption, despite its limited distribution, by influencing educators and resolving quaternion-versus-vector debates in favor of the simpler system. In modern mathematical software, this notation persists through functional calls like cross(a, b) in MATLAB or Cross in Mathematica, reflecting the enduring legacy of 19th-century formalizations. The component formula's determinant mnemonic, while mnemonic rather than derivational, aids computation in these contexts.
