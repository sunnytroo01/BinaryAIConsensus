Amyloid-related imaging abnormalities
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA) are a spectrum of magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) findings observed in patients undergoing anti-amyloid- monoclonal antibody therapies for Alzheimer's disease, primarily comprising parenchymal edema or sulcal effusions (ARIA-E) and cerebral microhemorrhages or superficial siderosis (ARIA-H). These abnormalities arise from treatment-induced disruption of vascular integrity due to rapid amyloid clearance, leading to fluid or blood extravasation in brain tissue.
ARIA were first systematically described in clinical trials of amyloid-targeting immunotherapies, such as lecanemab and donanemab, where they represent the most common adverse events, occurring in up to 36% of treated patients depending on the agent and dosage. The pathophysiology involves an inflammatory response triggered by antibody-mediated amyloid plaque removal, which impairs perivascular clearance pathways and exacerbates underlying cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA), particularly in individuals with pre-existing vascular vulnerabilities. On MRI, ARIA-E manifests as hyperintense lesions on T2-weighted fluid-attenuated inversion recovery (FLAIR) sequences indicating vasogenic edema, while ARIA-H appears as hypointense foci on gradient-echo or susceptibility-weighted imaging due to hemosiderin deposition. These changes typically emerge early in treatment, often within 3-6 months during dose escalation, and resolve spontaneously in most cases over 3-4 months, though ARIA-E and ARIA-H frequently co-occur in about 49% of instances.
Risk factors for developing ARIA include carriage of the APOE 4 allele (with homozygous individuals at 4-5 times higher risk), higher drug doses, pretreatment microhemorrhages, and greater baseline amyloid burden as measured by cerebrospinal fluid A42 levels or white matter hyperintensity scores. Clinically, ARIA are asymptomatic in approximately 74% of cases, but symptomatic episodespredominantly with ARIA-Ecan present with headache, confusion, dizziness, or seizures in 6-39% of affected patients, rarely leading to serious neurological sequelae. Management involves regular MRI monitoring per standardized protocols, including baseline and periodic scans during the initial treatment phase (with recent updates recommending additional early scans for certain therapies as of August 2025), dose pausing or discontinuation for moderate-to-severe cases, and supportive care like corticosteroids for symptomatic inflammation; ongoing research emphasizes personalized risk assessment to balance therapeutic benefits against these complications.

Definition and Background

Definition

Amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA) refer to a spectrum of magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)-detected brain changes, primarily consisting of vasogenic edema or sulcal effusions (ARIA-E) and microhemorrhages or superficial siderosis (ARIA-H), that occur in patients treated with anti-amyloid- monoclonal antibodies for Alzheimer's disease. These abnormalities arise due to increased vascular permeability associated with the removal of amyloid plaques by the therapeutic agents.
ARIA emerged as a key adverse event during clinical trials of amyloid-targeting therapies, including aducanumab, lecanemab, and donanemab, which are designed to slow Alzheimer's disease progression by clearing amyloid- deposits from the brain. Unlike spontaneous imaging findings in untreated Alzheimer's disease or cerebral amyloid angiopathy, ARIA is specifically tied to the pharmacological mechanism of amyloid clearance induced by these monoclonal antibodies, rather than the natural progression of amyloid pathology.
The prevalence of ARIA varies by drug, dose, and patient factors such as apolipoprotein E 4 carrier status, occurring in approximately 10-40% of treated patients across phase 3 trials. For instance, higher rates are reported with aducanumab (up to 35% for ARIA-E) compared to lecanemab (around 13% for ARIA-E), while most cases are asymptomatic and resolve over time.

Historical Context

Amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA) were first reported in 2010 during Phase 1 clinical trials of bapineuzumab, a monoclonal antibody targeting amyloid-beta in Alzheimer's disease patients.70015-7/fulltext) These initial observations involved microhemorrhages and vasogenic edema detected on MRI scans in a subset of treated participants, marking the earliest recognition of ARIA as a potential adverse effect of anti-amyloid immunotherapy.
Early trials, including those for bapineuzumab, underestimated the incidence and severity of ARIA, prompting retrospective analyses that revealed higher rates than initially anticipated and leading to protocol adjustments such as dose reductions and enhanced MRI monitoring.70015-7/fulltext) This underestimation contributed to challenges in subsequent studies, exemplified by the 2012 Phase 3 trials of solanezumab, where ARIA events, though less frequent, highlighted the need for improved safety protocols without halting the trials outright. Post-2020, awareness evolved significantly with a strong emphasis on APOE 4 genotyping to identify high-risk carriers, as this genotype was linked to substantially increased ARIA susceptibility in trials of emerging therapies.
Key regulatory milestones accelerated in the 2020s amid growing ARIA concerns. In June 2021, the FDA granted accelerated approval to aducanumab despite debates over its efficacy and ARIA risks, requiring post-approval studies to monitor adverse events. This was followed by the FDA's full approval of lecanemab in July 2023, incorporating mandatory MRI screening and APOE genotyping to mitigate ARIA. Donanemab received FDA approval in July 2024 with similar ARIA-related caveats, including dosing adjustments for at-risk patients. By 2023, both FDA and EMA guidelines standardized ARIA management, mandating baseline and periodic MRIs for all patients on anti-amyloid monoclonal antibodies.
As of 2025, ongoing post-marketing surveillance has refined ARIA incidence estimates, revealing real-world rates often lower than in pivotal trialssuch as 18.6% for lecanemab in early adopterswhile underscoring the importance of continued genotyping and imaging vigilance. These developments reflect ARIA's central role in shaping the safe deployment of anti-amyloid therapies.

Clinical Types

ARIA-E

Amyloid-related imaging abnormalities with edema or effusion (ARIA-E) represent a subtype of ARIA characterized by sulcal or parenchymal edema, predominantly involving the white matter, due to vascular leakage triggered by the clearance of amyloid-beta plaques during anti-amyloid immunotherapy. This vasogenic edema arises from increased permeability of cerebral blood vessels, leading to interstitial fluid accumulation without evidence of infarction. On magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), ARIA-E manifests as hyperintensities on fluid-attenuated inversion recovery (FLAIR) and T2-weighted sequences, typically without restricted diffusion on diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI), distinguishing it from cytotoxic edema or acute ischemia.
The incidence of ARIA-E is dose-dependent and notably higher in clinical trials of monoclonal antibodies targeting amyloid-beta, such as aducanumab (approximately 35% overall in phase 3 studies), lecanemab (12.6% in phase 3), and donanemab (24% in phase 3), with rates of approximately 35% at higher doses (10 mg/kg aducanumab), 26% at lower doses (3-6 mg/kg aducanumab), and less than 5% with placebo. These events are generally transient, with most cases resolving within 3-4 months of onset or treatment interruption, though complete resolution can extend up to 6 months in some instances. ARIA-E lesions predominantly occur in regions of high amyloid plaque burden, including periventricular areas, subcortical white matter, and cortical regions, with a predilection for parieto-occipital lobes.
Risk factors for ARIA-E include genetic predisposition, particularly homozygosity for the apolipoprotein E 4 (APOE 4) allele, which is associated with a 4-7-fold higher risk compared to non-carriers, likely due to greater amyloid deposition in vessel walls. While most ARIA-E cases are asymptomatic, severe presentationsoccurring in less than 1% of treated patientsmay involve symptoms such as headache, confusion, or seizures, often resolving with conservative management or temporary treatment suspension. ARIA-E can occasionally overlap with ARIA-H in mixed presentations, but its edematous features remain distinct from hemorrhagic components.

ARIA-H

Amyloid-related imaging abnormalities with hemosiderin deposition (ARIA-H) encompass a spectrum of hemorrhagic features observed in patients receiving anti-amyloid therapies for Alzheimer's disease, including cerebral microhemorrhages (typically <10 mm in diameter), superficial siderosis (characterized by linear cortical hypointensities due to hemosiderin deposition), and less commonly, macrohemorrhages (10 mm). These abnormalities reflect disrupted vascular integrity, often linked to underlying cerebral amyloid angiopathy, and are primarily detected through iron-sensitive MRI sequences such as gradient-recalled echo (GRE) T2*-weighted imaging or susceptibility-weighted imaging (SWI), which reveal hypointense lesions indicative of blood breakdown products.
In clinical trials of anti-amyloid monoclonal antibodies, the incidence of ARIA-H ranges from 15% to 30%, varying by agent and dose; for example, rates were approximately 20% with aducanumab, 31.4% with donanemab (phase 3), and 17.3% with lecanemab (phase 3, 2022). Unlike ARIA-E, ARIA-H is generally less reversible, with lesions often persisting or stabilizing rather than resolving, and carries a potential for progression, particularly in patients with preexisting microbleeds. ARIA-H frequently co-occurs with ARIA-E in approximately 40% of affected individuals, as seen in major trials like aducanumab and gantenerumab.
ARIA-H lesions predominantly localize to cortical and subcortical regions, often in lobar or peripheral distributions at the gray matter-white matter junction, aligning with sites of amyloid plaque and vascular deposition in cerebral amyloid angiopathy. The presence of baseline microbleeds increases the risk of new or worsening ARIA-H, with higher rates observed in APOE 4 carriers.
On imaging, ARIA-H manifests as focal hypointensities on GRE or SWI sequences, with superficial siderosis appearing as curvilinear signals along the brain surface and microhemorrhages as punctate or rounded foci. Severity is graded based on lesion count and type, such as mild (1-4 microhemorrhages or trace siderosis), moderate (5-9 microhemorrhages or mild siderosis), or severe (10 microhemorrhages, macrohemorrhage, or extensive siderosis), guiding therapeutic decisions like dose adjustment or discontinuation.
Clinically, ARIA-H is asymptomatic in the majority of cases (over 80%), but severe instances have been associated with focal neurological deficits, encephalopathy, headaches, or confusion, particularly when extensive or compounded by co-occurring ARIA-E. Long-term consequences are typically limited at the group level, with no significant impact on cognition or function reported in trials, though individual cases may require vigilant monitoring to prevent rare events like hemorrhagic stroke.

Pathophysiology

Underlying Mechanisms

The primary mechanism underlying amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA) involves the rapid clearance of amyloid-beta (A) from the walls of cerebral blood vessels affected by cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA), which compromises vascular structural integrity and leads to leakage. This process is triggered by anti-A monoclonal antibodies that bind to deposited A in vessel walls, promoting its removal and temporarily increasing vascular fragility, particularly in patients with pre-existing CAA.
The pathogenic sequence begins with antibody binding to vascular A, which facilitates phagocytosis by perivascular macrophages, microglia, and pericytes, resulting in the mobilization and clearance of A deposits. This phagocytic activity induces local inflammation through the release of cytokines such as interleukin-6 and tumor necrosis factor-alpha, which further stress endothelial cells and disrupt the blood-brain barrier (BBB). BBB disruption allows for vasogenic edema formation (ARIA-E) or vessel rupture leading to microhemorrhages (ARIA-H), as the sudden removal of supportive A weakens the extracellular matrix in vessel walls.
Pre-existing CAA in Alzheimer's disease patients exacerbates these effects by providing a substrate of A-laden vessels that are already prone to endothelial stress and reduced perivascular clearance capacity. Endothelial cell activation and apoptosis under this stress contribute to increased permeability, culminating in edema or hemorrhagic events, with the severity often correlating to the extent of baseline CAA. This association with the APOE 4 genotype may amplify vascular vulnerability through enhanced A deposition in CAA.
Evidence from animal models supports these mechanisms, with studies in transgenic mice exhibiting CAA, such as Tg2576 and TgCRND8 strains, demonstrating dose-dependent increases in microhemorrhages and edema-like changes following anti-A antibody infusions. For instance, passive immunization in these models led to A clearance accompanied by vascular leakage and inflammatory infiltrates, mirroring human ARIA-H pathology in a treatment-dose manner.
An inflammatory component is evident from elevated cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) levels of glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP) and pro-inflammatory cytokines during ARIA episodes, reflecting astrocyte activation and microglial response to vascular disruption. These biomarkers peak concurrently with imaging abnormalities, indicating acute neuroinflammatory processes driven by A clearance.

Risk Factors

Amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA) are associated with several genetic risk factors, most prominently the carriage of the APOE 4 allele. Patients homozygous for APOE 4 face a substantially elevated risk of ARIA, with incidence rates of 32.6% for ARIA-E in lecanemab trials compared to 5.4% in non-carriers, representing approximately 6-fold higher risk, while heterozygotes experience intermediate rates around 15.8%. APOE 4 homozygotes also show 3-6 times higher overall ARIA risk across anti-amyloid therapies, depending on the specific ARIA type and agent, prompting recommendations for pre-treatment genotyping to assess and communicate this predisposition. Similar patterns are observed with donanemab, where ARIA-E incidence reaches up to 40.6% in homozygotes.
Demographic factors further modulate ARIA susceptibility, including advanced age and markers of baseline cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA), such as prior microbleeds on MRI, which correlate with increased vascular fragility. Older age (>65 years, typical of trial populations) elevates ARIA-H hazard by approximately 1.06 per year increment. Female sex has been noted in some analyses as potentially contributing to higher ARIA vulnerability, possibly due to sex-specific vascular differences, though trial data show inconsistent associations and no significant univariate difference in gantenerumab studies.
Therapeutic variables significantly influence ARIA occurrence, with higher antibody doses and agents exhibiting stronger effector functions posing greater risks. For instance, aducanumab at 10 mg/kg yields ARIA-E rates of 35.2%, versus 12.6% for lecanemab at 10 mg/kg, attributable to differences in Fc receptor binding and amyloid clearance potency. Rapid infusion rates and escalating doses without gradual titration also heighten ARIA-E incidence in a dose-dependent manner, as observed in bapineuzumab trials (19.9% at 2 mg/kg versus 5.6% at 0.5 mg/kg).
Comorbidities that impair vascular integrity amplify hemorrhage-related ARIA risks. Anticoagulant or antithrombotic use increases ARIA-H hazard by 2.20-fold, while hypertension shows mixed evidence but up to 30% ARIA-E incidence in affected patients versus 11.9% in normotensives during gantenerumab therapy. A history of stroke, often exclusionary in trials, further elevates risk through preexisting cerebrovascular compromise.
Predictive tools, particularly baseline MRI assessment, aid in risk stratification; the presence of more than 4 cerebral microbleeds often contraindicates therapy initiation per guidelines for aducanumab and lecanemab, with odds ratios for ARIA-E exceeding 13 in those with microhemorrhages. These factors collectively underscore ARIA's ties to underlying CAA pathology, where amyloid-laden vessels are prone to disruption during immunotherapy.

Diagnosis and Classification

Imaging Characteristics

The primary imaging modality for detecting amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA) is magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), which utilizes fluid-attenuated inversion recovery (FLAIR) sequences to identify edema and susceptibility-weighted imaging (SWI) or gradient-recalled echo (GRE) sequences to detect hemorrhage. Routine MRI monitoring protocols are drug-specific; for example, for lecanemab, obtain baseline MRI (within 1 year prior to treatment initiation) and scans prior to the 5th, 7th, and 14th infusions, with additional scans as clinically indicated; similar enhanced monitoring during early treatment is recommended for other agents like aducanumab. These protocols allow for early identification of parenchymal changes associated with amyloid clearance.
ARIA-E, or amyloid-related imaging abnormalities with edema or effusion, manifests as hyperintense signals on T2-weighted and FLAIR sequences, typically appearing as sulcal effusions, parenchymal interstitial vasogenic edema, or gyral swelling predominantly in the occipital and parietal lobes. These lesions do not show restricted diffusion on diffusion-weighted imaging and generally lack gadolinium enhancement on post-contrast T1-weighted images unless complicated by secondary factors such as infection. The hyperintensities reflect localized fluid accumulation due to vascular permeability alterations.
ARIA-H, or amyloid-related imaging abnormalities with hemorrhage or hemosiderin deposition, is characterized by hypointense blooming artifacts on SWI or GRE sequences, representing microhemorrhages (typically 10 mm in diameter) or larger parenchymal hemorrhages. Superficial siderosis appears as linear or curvilinear hypointensities along the cortical surface, indicating hemosiderin leakage from superficial cortical vessels. These findings arise from hemosiderin deposits in the context of cerebral amyloid angiopathy.
Computed tomography (CT) serves as an adjunct modality for confirming acute hemorrhage in cases where MRI is contraindicated or to assess for mass effect, though it is less sensitive for microhemorrhages compared to MRI. Amyloid positron emission tomography (PET) imaging, using tracers such as florbetapir, correlates ARIA occurrence with regions of amyloid plaque reduction, providing context for the spatial distribution of abnormalities.
The temporal evolution of ARIA-E typically shows onset during early treatment cycles, peaking between 4 and 8 weeks post-infusion, with resolution occurring in most cases by 12 to 16 weeks, often following dose adjustment or treatment pause. In contrast, ARIA-H findings, particularly hemosiderin deposits, tend to persist beyond the resolution of ARIA-E, remaining detectable on follow-up imaging for months to years. These patterns inform monitoring strategies, with ARIA-E classified into severity grades based on extent and location for risk assessment.

MRI Classification Criteria

The standardized classification of amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA) on MRI was initially established by the 2011 Alzheimer's Association Research Roundtable workgroup to guide monitoring in anti-amyloid therapeutic trials. These criteria were adopted in the 2021 U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approval of aducanumab and used in the 2023 FDA approval of lecanemab. In 2024, the Alzheimer's Association convened an ARIA Workgroup to review and update recommendations for clinical practice, focusing on implementation and research needs.
ARIA-E is graded based on the extent and location of FLAIR hyperintensities indicative of vasogenic edema or sulcal effusions, with severity influencing treatment decisions. Mild ARIA-E is characterized by sulcal hyperintensity confined to a single region measuring less than 5 cm, typically without parenchymal involvement. Moderate ARIA-E involves FLAIR hyperintensity 5-10 cm or in multiple sites each less than 10 cm. Severe ARIA-E features extensive involvement greater than 10 cm, often with sulcal effacement, gyral swelling, or spread to deep structures like the corpus callosum, and is frequently associated with symptoms such as headache or confusion.
ARIA-H grading focuses on the number and distribution of microhemorrhages or superficial siderosis detected on susceptibility-weighted imaging (SWI) or gradient-echo (GRE) sequences. Mild ARIA-H includes 0-4 new microhemorrhages or a single focal area of superficial siderosis. Moderate ARIA-H encompasses 5-9 new microhemorrhages or two focal areas of superficial siderosis. Severe ARIA-H is defined by 10 or more new microhemorrhages, more than two focal areas of superficial siderosis, or any new intracerebral hemorrhage greater than 1 cm.
Clinical decision rules integrate ARIA severity with symptom presence to balance therapeutic benefits and risks. For asymptomatic mild ARIA-E or ARIA-H, treatment continuation is generally recommended with enhanced MRI monitoring every 6-12 weeks. Moderate asymptomatic ARIA-E or ARIA-H typically requires temporary dose suspension until resolution or stabilization, confirmed by follow-up MRI in 4-8 weeks, after which dosing may resume at a reduced rate if appropriate. Severe ARIA-E or ARIA-H, particularly if symptomatic or recurrent, mandates dose hold and clinical evaluation, often leading to permanent discontinuation to prevent complications like cerebral hemorrhage. In mixed ARIA cases, the highest severity grade across ARIA-E and ARIA-H dictates the response, with prioritization of hemorrhage management if present.
Despite these guidelines, limitations persist, including inter-rater variability in MRI interpretation, with intraclass correlation coefficients ranging from 0.83 to 0.89 for ARIA-E components, potentially affecting consistency in multicenter settings. As of 2025, emerging proposals advocate for AI-assisted quantification tools, such as FDA-cleared software like icobrain aria, to automate ARIA detection, volume measurement, and grading, reducing subjectivity and improving efficiency in clinical practice.

Clinical Management

Symptoms and Presentation

The majority of amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA) cases, approximately 70-90%, are asymptomatic and detected incidentally through scheduled magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) monitoring in clinical trials of anti-amyloid therapies. In such instances, patients exhibit no clinical manifestations, with ARIA identified solely by radiographic changes like edema or hemorrhage.
Mild symptoms occur in a subset of symptomatic cases, typically manifesting as headache, fatigue, or transient confusion that resolve spontaneously without intervention. These symptoms affect 6-39% of ARIA events depending on the therapy and dose, often correlating with lower severity grades on MRI classification scales.
Severe presentations are uncommon, occurring in about 1-2% of cases and potentially leading to hospitalization, with symptoms including focal neurological deficits such as aphasia due to temporal lobe involvement, seizures, or accelerated cognitive decline. For example, in trials of gantenerumab, serious symptomatic ARIA involved confusion, aphasia, and seizures in 1% of participants, all requiring inpatient care.
Symptoms generally emerge 1-4 weeks following infusion, aligning temporally with the onset of imaging abnormalities.
Differential diagnosis involves distinguishing ARIA from Alzheimer's disease progression or acute stroke, primarily through acute MRI features; unlike ischemic events, ARIA lacks restricted diffusion on diffusion-weighted imaging.

Monitoring and Treatment Strategies

Monitoring protocols for amyloid-related imaging abnormalities (ARIA) in patients receiving anti-amyloid therapies such as lecanemab and donanemab emphasize regular magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) surveillance combined with clinical assessments to detect early changes. A baseline brain MRI is required prior to initiating treatment to establish a reference for subsequent comparisons. For lecanemab, recommended MRI scans occur prior to the 3rd, 5th, 7th, and 14th infusions, with an additional scan between the 2nd and 3rd infusions as per 2025 FDA guidance; enhanced clinical vigilance, including symptom checklists at each visit, is advised during the first 14 weeks. For donanemab, surveillance MRIs are scheduled prior to the 2nd, 3rd, 4th, and 7th infusions, with an additional scan before the 12th dose for higher-risk individuals, followed by imaging every 6-12 months thereafter. These protocols integrate with established MRI classification criteria to grade ARIA severity and guide adjustments.
Management strategies for ARIA focus on dose modification and supportive interventions tailored to severity. For mild ARIA, treatment can continue with close monitoring via monthly MRIs and clinical evaluations. Moderate ARIA, whether asymptomatic or symptomatic, warrants temporary dose interruption until resolution, with resumption considered only if symptoms resolve and MRI findings improve to mild or absent. Severe or serious ARIA requires permanent discontinuation of therapy, regardless of symptoms, and prompt initiation of supportive care, including high-dose glucocorticoids in approximately 10% of cases to manage associated inflammation. Antithrombotic agents, such as anticoagulants, should be avoided or used cautiously in patients with ARIA-H due to heightened hemorrhage risk, with recommendations from appropriate use guidance advising against starting therapy in those requiring anticoagulation until further safety data emerge.
Prevention of ARIA incorporates risk stratification through APOE genotyping prior to therapy initiation, as APOE 4 homozygotes face substantially higher risksup to 4-5 times greater for ARIA-E compared to non-carriers. Patients with multiple risk factors, such as more than 10 cerebral microbleeds on baseline MRI or concurrent use of antithrombotics, are generally advised against therapy or require careful consideration. Slower dose titration schedules for lecanemab and donanemab have been shown to reduce ARIA incidence without compromising amyloid clearance efficacy; for donanemab, the 2025 FDA-approved updated regimen (350 mg first infusion, 700 mg second, 1050 mg third, then 1400 mg monthly) decreased ARIA-E rates compared to the original schedule. Modified protocols decreasing ARIA-E rates by up to 40% in high-risk groups.
Long-term outcomes indicate that approximately 80% of ARIA cases, including symptomatic ones, resolve with temporary therapy withholding and supportive measures, typically within weeks to months. As of 2025, extended follow-up data from phase 3 trials and real-world use indicate rare but reported cases of serious outcomes, including fatalities attributable to ARIA (e.g., 6 cases with lecanemab), though no overall increase in mortality rates, with vigilant monitoring essential to mitigate such events. Adherence to these strategies has supported the safe integration of anti-amyloid therapies into clinical practice for early Alzheimer's disease.
