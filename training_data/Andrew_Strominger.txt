Andrew Strominger

1x
 Andrew Strominger (born July 30, 1955) is an American theoretical physicist renowned for his pioneering work in string theory, quantum gravity, and black hole physics. He earned his AB in physics from Harvard College in 1977, his MA in physics from the University of California, Berkeley in 1979, and his PhD from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology in 1982, with a dissertation on the large symmetry approximation in quantum chromodynamics. Currently, Strominger serves as the Gwill E. York Professor of Physics at Harvard University, director of the Center for the Fundamental Laws of Nature, and a founding member of the Black Hole Initiative.
Strominger's research has significantly advanced the understanding of fundamental physics through innovative applications of string theory. He co-discovered D-brane solutions, which revealed profound duality symmetries in the theory and provided a framework for connecting string theory to particle physics and quantum field theory. In a landmark 1996 collaboration with Cumrun Vafa, he derived a microscopic explanation for the Bekenstein-Hawking entropy of certain supersymmetric black holes using D-branes, resolving key paradoxes in quantum gravity and demonstrating how string theory accounts for black hole information storage. Earlier contributions include seminal work on Calabi-Yau compactifications and topology changes in string theory, elucidating how extra dimensions shape the universe's low-energy physics.
More recently, Strominger has pioneered celestial holography, a novel approach that reformulates quantum gravity and scattering amplitudes on the celestial sphere, aiming to bridge asymptotic symmetries with observable phenomena like photon rings around black holes. His efforts emphasize testable predictions, connecting abstract theoretical constructs to real-world observations in general relativity and cosmology.
Strominger's groundbreaking contributions have earned him prestigious accolades, including the 2017 Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics for transformative advances in quantum field theory, string theory, and quantum gravity, shared with collaborators. He also received the 2014 Oskar Klein Medal for elucidating string theory's links to elementary particle physics and the 2020 Guggenheim Fellowship for pathbreaking work in classical and quantum gravity.

Early life and education

Early life

Andrew Eben Strominger was born on July 30, 1955, in Cambridge, England. He is the son of Jack L. Strominger, a renowned immunologist and Harvard professor of biochemistry, and his wife Ann Strominger.
Strominger grew up in a scientifically oriented family, with his father's groundbreaking work on immune system molecules and bacterial cell walls shaping his early fascination with science. Jack Strominger's relentless curiosity and dedication to discovery served as a profound influence, fostering Andrew's interest in pursuing a career in physics. The family, which included Strominger and his two brothers, Ethan and Paul, relocated to Cambridge, Massachusetts, in the late 1960s when Jack joined the Harvard faculty in the Department of Biochemistry and Molecular Biology.
This move immersed the young Strominger in Harvard's vibrant academic community, where exposure to his father's laboratory environment and discussions about scientific breakthroughs further nurtured his intellectual development. He later transitioned to undergraduate studies at Harvard College, building on these formative experiences.

Education

Strominger earned his Bachelor of Arts (AB) degree in physics from Harvard College in 1977. He then pursued graduate studies at the University of California, Berkeley, where he obtained his Master of Arts (MA) in physics in 1979.
Strominger completed his PhD in physics at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) in 1981. His doctoral thesis, titled "The large symmetry approximation in quantum field theory," was supervised by Roman Jackiw. During his time at MIT, Strominger gained early exposure to advanced topics in quantum field theory, which shaped his initial research interests in symmetries and approximations within the field. This foundational training under Jackiw, a prominent figure in theoretical physics, laid the groundwork for Strominger's subsequent contributions to high-energy physics.

Academic career

Early positions

Following his PhD in physics from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology in 1982, under the supervision of Roman Jackiw, Andrew Strominger began his postdoctoral career as a long-term member in the School of Natural Sciences at the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton, New Jersey, from 1982 to 1987. This position provided an immersive environment for early theoretical physics research, including foundational work in quantum field theory and emerging ideas in string theory during the mid-1980s.
In 1986, Strominger transitioned to a faculty appointment in the Department of Physics at the University of California, Santa Barbara (UCSB), where he served as a professor until 1997. At UCSB, he joined a dynamic research community centered around the newly established Kavli Institute for Theoretical Physics (KITP), founded in 1979 as the Institute for Theoretical Physics, which became a leading hub for string theory and high-energy physics in the 1980s and 1990s. This environment, bolstered by collaborators like David Gross, fostered rapid advancements in theoretical models and attracted international talent, enabling Strominger to build his expertise in quantum gravity and unified theories.
During the late 1980s and early 1990s, Strominger's career at UCSB marked key transitions amid the "superstring revolutions," including the 1984-1985 heterotic string developments and the 1990s dualities era, which solidified UCSB's role as a pivotal center for string theory innovation without direct experimental constraints. These years positioned him for subsequent leadership in the field before his move to Harvard in 1997.

Harvard faculty roles

In 1997, Andrew Strominger joined the Harvard University faculty as a professor of physics, following a faculty position at the University of California, Santa Barbara. He currently holds the title of Gwill E. York Professor of Physics.
Strominger serves as Director of Harvard's Center for the Fundamental Laws of Nature, a role he has held since 1997, overseeing research in high-energy theoretical physics and related fields. He is also a Senior Fellow of the Harvard Society of Fellows, appointed in 2000, contributing to the selection and support of interdisciplinary junior fellows.
Additionally, Strominger has been involved in administrative leadership through the Black Hole Initiative at Harvard, where he is a founding member and faculty affiliate focused on interdisciplinary black hole research.
Throughout his tenure at Harvard, Strominger has mentored numerous graduate students and postdoctoral researchers, advising over 20 PhD theses in theoretical physics as of 2025.

Research contributions

Foundations in string theory

Andrew Strominger's foundational contributions to string theory in the 1980s and 1990s established key frameworks for understanding quantum gravity, compactifications, and dualities within superstring models. His early work focused on constructing consistent vacua and exploring perturbative aspects of superstrings, laying groundwork for later advances in unifying different string theories. These efforts emphasized the role of extra dimensions and background fields in achieving supersymmetric configurations, influencing the development of heterotic string theory as a viable candidate for grand unification.
In the mid-1980s, Strominger investigated Calabi-Yau compactifications as a mechanism to preserve supersymmetry in heterotic string theory, demonstrating how these Ricci-flat Khler manifolds could yield four-dimensional effective theories with the standard model's gauge groups. His 1985 paper on vacuum configurations for superstrings analyzed the conditions under which compactification on Calabi-Yau threefolds produces chiral matter spectra and moduli spaces, addressing challenges in embedding the heterotic E8 E8 gauge structure while stabilizing extra dimensions. This work highlighted the geometric origins of Yukawa couplings and highlighted the need for mirror symmetry to resolve discrepancies in Hodge numbers between seemingly dual compactifications.
Building on these ideas, Strominger explored superstring perturbations in backgrounds with torsion, particularly in the heterotic sector. In his 1986 collaboration, he derived the conditions for spacetime supersymmetry in the presence of non-zero H-flux (torsion), showing how the Green-Schwarz anomaly cancellation mechanism allows consistent propagation of heterotic strings on curved manifolds beyond simple toroidal compactifications. This perturbative analysis revealed how torsion modifies the beta functions for the metric and dilaton, ensuring conformal invariance at one-loop order and providing a pathway to non-Khler geometries in later compactification schemes. These results were crucial for understanding how heterotic strings could incorporate realistic particle physics without introducing tachyons or ghosts.
A pivotal achievement came in 1992 with Strominger's co-development of the Callan-Giddings-Harvey-Strominger (CGHS) model, a exactly solvable two-dimensional theory of dilaton gravity coupled to N massless scalar fields representing conformal matter. The model, introduced to capture essential features of higher-dimensional stringy black holes, features a linear dilaton potential and admits black hole solutions analogous to the Schwarzschild metric, but with quantum backreaction effects computable via conformal field theory techniques. Classically solvable, the CGHS framework allows exact calculation of Hawking radiation and information loss paradoxes in a simplified setting, demonstrating how stringy effects dilute the singularity and modify the evaporation endpoint without introducing inconsistencies. This toy model provided the first rigorous testbed for quantum gravity in string theory, influencing subsequent numerical and analytical studies of evaporating horizons.
Strominger's exploration of string theory dualities in the late 1980s and early 1990s addressed the apparent non-perturbative equivalences between different superstring formulations. His 1988 work on quantum gravity coupling constants examined how dual descriptions resolve ultraviolet divergences in perturbative expansions, showing that strong-weak coupling duality interchanges the roles of electric and magnetic charges in heterotic and type IIB theories. These insights prefigured the second superstring revolution, where dualities unify the five consistent superstring theories under M-theory, with Strominger contributing analyses of how perturbative spectra match across dual frames.
Culminating these efforts, Strominger proposed the Strominger-Yau-Zaslow (SYZ) conjecture in 1996, positing that mirror symmetry between Calabi-Yau threefolds arises from T-duality along special Lagrangian tori fibrations. The conjecture asserts that for a pair of mirror manifolds X and Y, both admit a fibration over a common base with toroidal fibers, where the dualities exchange complex structure moduli of X with Khler moduli of Y, resolving the apparent mismatch in their cohomology. This geometric realization, devoid of worldsheet instantons in the large volume limit, provides a non-perturbative understanding of mirror symmetry and has driven subsequent constructions of explicit fibrations and stability conditions for supersymmetric cycles. The SYZ framework bridges type IIA and type IIB string theories on Calabi-Yau spaces, with implications for counting BPS states and verifying duality predictions.

Black hole physics

Strominger's most influential work in black hole physics came from his 1996 collaboration with Cumrun Vafa, where they provided the first exact microscopic explanation for the Bekenstein-Hawking entropy of certain supersymmetric black holes in string theory. Using D-branes as the fundamental building blocks, they modeled five-dimensional extremal black holes in type II string theory compactified on K3 S, demonstrating that the entropy arises from the quantum degeneracy of BPS-saturated bound states of these branes. This approach resolved a long-standing puzzle by showing how the macroscopic area law could emerge from a precise count of microscopic string configurations at weak coupling, where perturbative methods are reliable.
The key insight was to construct the black hole as a bound state of D1-branes (wrapping the S), D5-branes (wrapping K3 S), and units of Kaluza-Klein momentum along S, preserving a fraction of supersymmetry. The microscopic entropy is computed by counting the number of ways to distribute the momentum among open string excitations stretching between the D-branes, governed by a two-dimensional superconformal field theory on the worldvolume. For large charges, this degeneracy yields the statistical entropy formula that precisely matches the Bekenstein-Hawking value:
S=2Q1Q5n
S = 2\pi \sqrt{Q_1 Q_5 n}
S=2Q1Q5n
where Q1Q_1Q1 is the number of D1-branes, Q5Q_5Q5 the number of D5-branes, and nnn the momentum charge. This agreement holds because the effective central charge of the dual CFT is c=6Q1Q5c = 6 Q_1 Q_5c=6Q1Q5, and the Cardy formula for the entropy of BPS states in such a theory gives exactly the above expression. The macroscopic derivation follows from the supergravity solution, where the horizon area A=8G5Q1Q5nA = 8\pi G_5 \sqrt{Q_1 Q_5 n}A=8G5Q1Q5n (with G5G_5G5 the five-dimensional Newton constant) leads to SBH=A/(4G5)S_{\mathrm{BH}} = A/(4 G_5)SBH=A/(4G5), confirming the match without adjustable parameters.
Strominger and Vafa extended this framework to extremal black holes in heterotic string theory compactified on T, where the microstates are counted from excitations in a four-dimensional N=4 super-Yang-Mills theory arising from multiple D0-branes (or instantons) carrying electric charges. In this case, the entropy for supersymmetric configurations with three electric charges QiQ_iQi (i=1,2,3) and momentum QHQ_HQH approximates S2QHQ1Q2Q3/2S \approx 2\pi \sqrt{Q_H Q_1 Q_2 Q_3 / 2}S2QHQ1Q2Q3/2 for large values, again aligning with the area law. These results apply specifically to extremal, BPS-preserving black holes, where the zero-temperature entropy reflects ground-state degeneracy protected by supersymmetry, providing a robust test of string theory's consistency with semiclassical gravity. The D-brane method has since become a cornerstone for understanding black hole thermodynamics in supersymmetric settings.

Celestial holography and recent advances

In the 2000s, Strominger proposed the dS/CFT correspondence, a holographic duality that relates quantum gravity in (d+1)-dimensional de Sitter space to a Euclidean conformal field theory (CFT) on the d-dimensional boundary sphere, providing a framework for understanding de Sitter black holes and cosmology through boundary dynamics. This idea extended earlier anti-de Sitter holography to positive curvature spacetimes, suggesting that de Sitter entropy arises from CFT degrees of freedom on the spatial boundary.
Building on this, in the 2010s, Strominger co-authored the Kerr/CFT correspondence, which posits that near-horizon quantum gravity for extremal Kerr black holes is dual to a two-dimensional chiral CFT with central charge $ c = 12 J $, where $ J $ is the black hole angular momentum. This duality reproduces the macroscopic Bekenstein-Hawking entropy via Cardy formula and has been realized microscopically in string theory embeddings, offering insights into rotating black hole microstates without relying on supersymmetry.
Strominger pioneered celestial holography in the late 2010s, proposing a duality that maps four-dimensional asymptotically flat scattering amplitudes to correlation functions in a two-dimensional CFT on the celestial sphere, where particle boosts become conformal transformations. Key post-2017 developments include the celestial dictionary, which transforms momentum-space amplitudes into position-space operators on the null boundary, enabling the study of infrared structures like soft factors as CFT primaries.
A central result in this framework is the equivalence between soft theorems, asymptotic symmetries, and Ward identities in asymptotically flat spacetimes, known as the infrared triangle. Soft theorems, which express the low-energy limit of scattering amplitudes (e.g., the leading Weinberg soft graviton theorem), are precisely the Ward identities for large gauge transformations, such as BMS supertranslations and superrotations at null infinity. This equivalence implies that infrared divergences are regulated by infinite-dimensional symmetries, with subleading soft theorems corresponding to superrotations and memory effects arising from symmetry-induced transitions between vacua, fundamentally linking S-matrix elements to boundary conformal structures.
In 2024, Strominger and collaborators extended these ideas to leaf amplitudes in twistor space, demonstrating that maximally helicity-violating (MHV) tree-level amplitudes transform under the same infinite-dimensional soft S-algebra as celestial amplitudes, bridging momentum and twistor representations for holographic consistency. This reveals universal algebraic structures governing both bulk and boundary descriptions in gauge and gravity theories.
Recent 2025 work by Strominger includes analyses of quantum fields on time-periodic AdS3/Z_3/\mathbb{Z}3/Z orbifolds, exploring free massive scalar theories on spacetimes with closed timelike curves to probe causality, unitarity, and holographic duals in non-standard geometries. Additionally, investigations into chiral soft algebras for N=2\mathcal{N}=2N=2 gauge theories have uncovered infinite-dimensional symmetries acting on scattering data, further enriching the celestial framework for non-gravitational sectors.

Recognition and impact

Major awards

Andrew Strominger has received numerous prestigious awards recognizing his contributions to theoretical physics, particularly in string theory and quantum gravity. In 2014, he received the Physics Frontiers Prize in Fundamental Physics from the Breakthrough Prize Foundation for numerous deep and groundbreaking contributions to quantum field theory, quantum gravity, string theory, and geometry. In 2017, he was awarded the Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics, shared with Cumrun Vafa and Joseph Polchinski, for transformative advances in understanding black hole entropy through string theory and quantum field theory frameworks.
In 2014, Strominger received the Dirac Medal from the Abdus Salam International Centre for Theoretical Physics, shared with Ashoke Sen and Gabriele Veneziano, honoring their crucial developments in string theory that have shaped modern understandings of quantum gravity. That same year, he was awarded the Oskar Klein Medal by the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences for his key contributions to string theory and its applications in fundamental physics.
Strominger shared the 2016 Dannie Heineman Prize for Mathematical Physics from the American Physical Society with Cumrun Vafa, recognizing their profound insights into the microscopic structure of black holes and the entropy of extremal black holes in string theory.
In 2025, Strominger and Alexander Zhiboedov received the Frontiers of Science Award from the International Congress of Basic Science for their paper on gravitational memory, BMS supertranslations, and soft theorems, which advanced connections between asymptotic symmetries and quantum gravity.
Additionally, in 2020, Strominger was named a Guggenheim Fellow by the John Simon Guggenheim Memorial Foundation, supporting his ongoing research in theoretical physics.

Influence on physics

Andrew Strominger's research has profoundly shaped the landscape of theoretical physics, particularly in quantum gravity, where his contributions to string theory and black hole entropy have inspired decades of subsequent work. As of 2025, his publications have accumulated over 72,000 citations, underscoring his enduring influence on efforts to unify quantum mechanics with general relativity. This body of work has guided researchers toward holographic principles and asymptotic symmetries, fostering advancements in understanding the fundamental structure of spacetime.
Strominger has forged pivotal collaborations that have advanced string theory and holography, including long-term partnerships with Cumrun Vafa on duality symmetries and microscopic interpretations of black hole properties, as well as with Joseph Polchinski on effective descriptions of string dynamics. These efforts, recognized through shared honors like the 2017 Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics, have extended to broader initiatives, such as his directorship of the Simons Collaboration on Celestial Holography, which explores holographic dualities in flat spacetimes with a team of over 30 researchers.
At Harvard University, where Strominger serves as the Gwill E. York Professor of Physics, he has played a key role in mentoring the next generation of theorists through PhD supervision and course instruction. Notable students under his guidance include Sabrina Gonzalez Pasterski, who founded the Celestial Holography Initiative, and Aaron Simons, contributing to a legacy of innovative quantum gravity research. He periodically teaches advanced seminars on black holes and string theory, such as Physics 211BR: Introduction to Classical and Quantum Black Holes, influencing undergraduates and graduates alike.
Strominger's interdisciplinary impact extends to the Black Hole Initiative at Harvard, where he is a founding member and principal investigator, bridging physics, astronomy, mathematics, and philosophy to address black hole puzzles like information preservation. Complementing this, his public outreach efforts have demystified complex topics for broader audiences through interviews and podcasts, including appearances on the Lex Fridman Podcast in 2023 discussing quantum gravity and the Sean Carroll podcast exploring string theory's real-world connections.
