 GAIL (India) Limited is India's largest state-owned natural gas processing, transmission, and distribution company, functioning as a Maharatna Central Public Sector Enterprise (CPSE) under the Ministry of Petroleum and Natural Gas, Government of India. Incorporated on August 16, 1984, and headquartered at GAIL Bhawan, 16 Bhikaji Cama Place, R.K. Puram, New Delhi, the company oversees an integrated natural gas value chain that encompasses exploration, production, processing, transportation, marketing, and distribution.
With a vast infrastructure including approximately 16,421 kilometers of natural gas pipelines spanning over 20 states, GAIL handles a capacity of around 204 million standard cubic meters per day (MMSCMD) and supplies natural gas to industrial, commercial, and residential sectors through its city gas distribution (CGD) networks. The company also operates six gas processing plants that produce liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) and other liquid hydrocarbons, imports and regasifies liquefied natural gas (LNG), and produces petrochemicals such as linear low-density polyethylene (LLDPE) and high-density polyethylene (HDPE) at its Pata facility. Additionally, GAIL has ventured into renewable energy, boasting an installed capacity of 145 megawatts (MW) from wind and solar projects, underscoring its commitment to sustainable development.
GAIL employs about 5,010 permanent staff and maintains a diversified portfolio through subsidiaries like GAIL Gas Limited for CGD operations and joint ventures in LNG terminals and petrochemical projects. As a key player in India's energy transition, it contributes significantly to the nation's goal of increasing natural gas share in the energy mix to 15% by 2030, while reporting revenue of 1,37,288 crore (US$16 billion) in FY 202425.

History

Formation and early development

GAIL (India) Limited was incorporated on August 16, 1984, as a public sector undertaking under the Ministry of Petroleum and Natural Gas, with the initial name Gas Authority of India Limited. The company's primary mandate was to establish a comprehensive nationwide natural gas transmission and distribution infrastructure to support India's growing energy needs, particularly for fertilizer production and power generation. This initiative aimed to harness domestic natural gas resources efficiently and reduce reliance on imported fuels.
The foundational project under this mandate was the Hazira-Vijaipur-Jagdishpur (HVJ) pipeline, commissioned in phases starting in 1987, which spanned approximately 1,750 kilometers and marked India's first cross-country natural gas transmission system. The pipeline was designed to transport natural gas from the offshore Hazira terminal, connected to upstream fields such as Bombay High, to inland consumption centers in northern India, including fertilizer plants in Uttar Pradesh. Integration with these upstream sources required coordinated efforts to ensure steady gas supply from offshore platforms operated by entities like the Oil and Natural Gas Corporation (ONGC).
Early development faced significant challenges, as the HVJ project was India's pioneering effort in large-scale pipeline infrastructure, involving stiff timelines, complex terrain navigation, and limited domestic expertise. To overcome technical gaps, GAIL relied on imported technologies and foreign collaborations for pipeline construction, welding, and compression systems, which were essential for handling high-pressure gas flow over long distances. These hurdles were addressed through phased commissioning between 1987 and 1991, laying the groundwork for expanded gas utilization despite initial delays and resource constraints.
In 1997, GAIL was granted Navratna status by the Government of India, recognizing its strong performance and enabling greater operational and financial autonomy as a public sector undertaking. This designation empowered the company to pursue joint ventures and capital investments independently, facilitating its evolution from a focused transmission entity to a more versatile gas major.

Key expansions and milestones

GAIL entered the petrochemical sector in 1999 with the commissioning of its first gas-based petrochemical complex at Pata in Uttar Pradesh, which produced linear low-density polyethylene (LLDPE) and had an initial capacity of 310,000 tonnes per annum (TPA). This marked a significant diversification from its core natural gas transmission business, leveraging its gas resources to manufacture value-added products. During the early 2000s, GAIL expanded its polymer production at the Pata facility, increasing capacity to 410,000 TPA for LLDPE and high-density polyethylene (HDPE) by 2002, enhancing its position in the domestic plastics market.
In parallel, GAIL deepened its involvement in city gas distribution (CGD) by acquiring stakes in key entities, including a 26% holding in Indraprastha Gas Limited (IGL) established in 1998 for Delhi and a similar stake in Mahanagar Gas Limited (MGL) for Mumbai, with further expansions into joint ventures for CGD networks in cities like Agra and Lucknow by the mid-2000s. This move supported the growth of compressed natural gas (CNG) infrastructure for vehicles and piped natural gas for households and industries. Concurrently, in 2004, GAIL entered the liquefied natural gas (LNG) import space through its 12.5% stake in Petronet LNG Limited, enabling regasified LNG supplies from the Dahej terminal, India's first such facility, which began operations that year with an initial capacity of 5 million tonnes per annum.
GAIL initially held a near-monopoly on domestic natural gas transmission, controlling over 70% of India's pipeline network and volumes into the 2000s, before regulatory changes and diversification into trading, petrochemicals, and LNG reduced its exclusive dominance while broadening its revenue streams. Following its initial public offering in 1997, GAIL experienced robust market capitalization growth in the 2010s, rising from approximately 18,500 crore in 2010 to about 55,000 crore by 2019, driven by expanded infrastructure and rising gas demand. A pivotal event in 2018 involved discussions on strategic consolidation in the sector, including potential integration of gas assets with Indian Oil Corporation, though it primarily advanced through long-term supply agreements rather than a full merger. By 2020, GAIL's average natural gas transmission volume had reached about 108 million standard cubic meters per day (MMSCMD), supported by over 12,000 km of pipelines across 13 states.

Recent leadership and strategic shifts

In October 2022, Sandeep Kumar Gupta, a seasoned finance professional with over 37 years in the oil and gas sector, assumed the role of Chairman and Managing Director (CMD) of GAIL (India) Limited, succeeding Manoj Jain. Gupta, previously Director (Finance) at Indian Oil Corporation Limited, focused on strengthening GAIL's financial strategies and operational efficiencies during his tenure.
On November 7, 2025, Deepak Gupta was recommended by the Public Enterprises Selection Board as the next CMD of GAIL, bringing 35 years of expertise in the energy sector, including his prior role as Director (Projects) at the company. This leadership transition aims to guide GAIL through evolving market dynamics, succeeding Sandeep Kumar Gupta upon his retirement in February 2026.
Amid global LNG price volatility, GAIL intensified its focus on LNG imports, reviving plans for U.S. LNG acquisitions and securing long-term supplies from Qatar Energy under a five-year deal commencing in April 2025. The company expressed optimism about LNG's role in meeting India's projected natural gas demand growth to 103 billion cubic meters (BCM) annually by 2030, nearly 60% higher than 2023 levels, driven by expanding city gas distribution and industrial usage. To address allocation efficiencies, GAIL implemented a reduction in natural gas allocation for LPG shrinkage from 0.32 million standard cubic meters per day (MMSCMD) to 0.2 MMSCMD, effective October 1, 2025, optimizing resource use amid domestic supply constraints.
In 2025, GAIL expanded its startup investment fund under the 'PANKH' initiative from 100 crore to 500 crore for FY25, targeting innovations in clean energy, renewables, and energy storage to bolster long-term sustainability. Responding to market challenges, including LNG price fluctuations and pipeline commissioning delayssuch as the Mumbai-Nagpur-Jharsuguda pipeline now slated for December 2025GAIL pursued LNG swap tenders to optimize trade routes and mitigate risks through diversified sourcing strategies.

Corporate Governance

Ownership and board structure

GAIL (India) Limited is a public sector undertaking (PSU) with majority ownership held by the Government of India through the Ministry of Petroleum and Natural Gas, which holds approximately 52% of the company's equity shares as of September 2025. The remaining shares are publicly traded on the National Stock Exchange (NSE) and Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE), enabling broad investor participation while maintaining governmental oversight. This ownership structure underscores GAIL's status as a key instrument of national energy policy, balancing public accountability with market-driven efficiency.
Conferred with Maharatna status on February 1, 2013, GAIL enjoys enhanced operational autonomy as one of India's premier PSUs, permitting investments up to 5,000 crore or 15% of its net worthwhichever is lowerwithout prior government approval for domestic and international projects. This designation, granted by the Department of Public Enterprises, recognizes GAIL's strong financial performance, global stature, and contribution to the economy, positioning it among an elite group of 14 Maharatna companies as of November 2025.
The board of directors comprises 12 members, including the Chairman and Managing Director, functional directors responsible for areas such as finance, operations, marketing, and human resources, government-nominated directors representing the Ministry of Petroleum and Natural Gas, and independent directors to ensure diverse expertise and impartiality. This composition aligns with the governance norms for Maharatna PSUs, fostering strategic decision-making across the company's natural gas and diversified operations.
GAIL adheres to robust governance frameworks, including full compliance with the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) Listing Obligations and Disclosure Requirements Regulations, 2015, which mandate transparent reporting and shareholder protections. The company conducts annual board evaluations to assess director performance and effectiveness, while specialized committees such as the audit committee oversee financial integrity, risk management, and regulatory adherence, ensuring accountability in its role as a listed entity.

Core Operations

Natural gas transmission

GAIL (India) Limited operates one of India's largest natural gas transmission networks, spanning approximately 16,421 kilometers as of mid-2025 and capable of transporting over 200 million standard cubic meters per day (MMSCMD) of natural gas across multiple states. This infrastructure forms the backbone of the country's gas supply, facilitating efficient movement from production and import points to consumption centers.
The network comprises key transmission corridors, including the Hazira-Vijaipur-Jagdishpur (HVJ) pipeline, India's pioneering cross-country system stretching over 3,400 kilometers including expansions, and the Dadri-Bawana-Nangal pipeline, covering 862 kilometers to connect northern regions. Additional major routes, such as the Dahej-Uran-Panvel-Dabhol network (930 kilometers), support high-volume flows from western import terminals. Operations rely on compressor stations positioned along these pipelines to sustain gas pressure, enabling reliable long-distance transport. Real-time monitoring is achieved through a centralized Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition (SCADA) system, utilizing Yokogawa's FAST/TOOLS platform to oversee flow, pressure, and equipment status across the entire grid. Tariffs for transmission are determined and regulated by the Petroleum and Natural Gas Regulatory Board (PNGRB) under the PNGRB (Determining the Pipeline Transportation Charges for Natural Gas Pipelines) Regulations, ensuring fair pricing and consumer protection.
Safety and maintenance protocols emphasize pipeline integrity, with annual assessments conducted via inline inspections, direct assessments, and external audits to detect and mitigate risks such as corrosion or third-party damage. These measures comply with industry standards to protect public safety and the environment. Looking ahead, GAIL is expanding its capabilities to incorporate renewable gases, including pilot projects for hydrogen blending into transmission streams, as demonstrated in collaborations like the one with Cummins for hydrogen infrastructure development. This integration aims to support India's transition to lower-carbon energy while leveraging the existing pipeline network.

Gas marketing and distribution

GAIL (India) Limited plays a pivotal role in the marketing of natural gas, serving as the largest marketer in the country and accounting for more than 50% of total natural gas sales in India. The company caters to a diverse customer base, including major sectors such as fertilizers, power generation, and industries, thereby supporting approximately 70% of India's gas-consuming entities through long-term supply arrangements. In particular, GAIL allocates around 40% of its marketed gas to the fertilizer sector, which relies on natural gas as a feedstock for urea production, 25% to the power sector for electricity generation, and the remainder to various industrial applications like refineries and steel manufacturing.
The pricing of natural gas marketed by GAIL follows a dual framework to balance domestic priorities and global market dynamics. Domestic gas production is priced under the Administered Price Mechanism (APM), set by the government to ensure affordability for priority sectors like fertilizers and power, while imported liquefied natural gas (LNG) is priced on a market-linked basis, often tied to international benchmarks such as Henry Hub or Brent crude oil prices. This approach allows GAIL to manage volatility in global energy prices while fulfilling contractual obligations. In fiscal year 2025, GAIL marketed over 100 MMSCMD of natural gas, marking a 3% increase from 98.45 MMSCMD in the previous year, driven by rising demand from industrial and power customers. Key long-term contracts include sales and purchase agreements (SPAs) with major clients such as the National Thermal Power Corporation (NTPC) for regasified LNG supplies from Qatar and Australia, ensuring stable volume commitments.
To enhance customer service and operational efficiency, GAIL has implemented digital initiatives tailored to its gas marketing operations as of 2025. These include online portals for gas nominations, allowing customers to book and schedule supplies in real-time, and the Anjani portal for e-billing and measurement book management, which streamlines invoice processing and payment tracking. Such platforms reduce paperwork, improve transparency, and facilitate quicker dispute resolution, supporting GAIL's extensive network of over 70% of India's gas consumers across industrial, power, and fertilizer segments.

LNG sourcing and trading

GAIL (India) Limited plays a pivotal role in securing liquefied natural gas (LNG) supplies to address India's growing energy needs, primarily through a mix of long-term contracts, spot purchases, and strategic trading mechanisms. The company manages an LNG portfolio of approximately 16.56 million tonnes per annum (MMTPA) as of mid-2025, sourced from key global suppliers including Qatar, the United States, and Australia. This portfolio supports domestic regasification and distribution, integrating with GAIL's broader natural gas infrastructure.
Key long-term contracts form the backbone of GAIL's LNG sourcing. A significant agreement with QatarEnergy, renewed in 2024, secures 4.5 MMTPA of LNG on a delivered ex-ship (DES) basis, extending supplies from 2028 to 2048; this represents GAIL's offtake share (60%) from Petronet LNG's 7.5 MMTPA deal with QatarEnergy. Additionally, GAIL holds a 20-year sales and purchase agreement with Cheniere Energy for 3.5 MMTPA from the Sabine Pass terminal in the United States, signed in 2011 and commencing deliveries in 2016. To supplement these, GAIL engages in spot trades, procuring cargoes through tenders; for instance, in December 2024, it awarded a five-year contract to QatarEnergy Trading for 12 cargoes annually (equivalent to about 0.72 MMTPA) starting April 2025.
Regasification of imported LNG occurs primarily at terminals where GAIL has booked capacity. At the Dahej LNG terminal in Gujarat, operated by Petronet LNG (in which GAIL holds a 12.5% stake), the company has secured 2.5 MMTPA of regasification capacity as part of its overall 5 MMTPA booked across Dahej and Kochi terminals. Expansions are underway to enhance this infrastructure; GAIL plans to increase its regasification footprint, including potential tie-ups to reach up to 7.5 MMTPA at Dahej by leveraging Petronet's brownfield project, which aims to boost the terminal's total capacity from 17.5 MMTPA to 22.5 MMTPA by March 2026.
In LNG trading, GAIL employs sophisticated portfolio management strategies to optimize costs and mitigate risks. The company actively participates in global swaps, such as a July 2025 tender offering 24 U.S.-sourced cargoes from Sabine Pass in exchange for deliveries to its Dhamra terminal, reducing shipping costs amid Red Sea disruptions. Hedging against price volatility is achieved through financial instruments linked to the Japan Korea Marker (JKM) index, the benchmark for Asian spot LNG prices, allowing GAIL to lock in favorable rates during market fluctuations. These activities enable flexible optimization of the global LNG portfolio, balancing long-term commitments with opportunistic spot market engagements.
As of 2025, GAIL remains bullish on LNG's role in India's energy transition, projecting it to constitute up to 25% of the country's natural gas mix by 2030 amid rising demand from power, fertilizers, and city gas sectors. New deals, including a 10-year agreement with Vitol Asia for 1 MMTPA starting 2026 and explorations for U.S. LNG stakes influenced by geopolitical shifts like eased export policies under the Trump administration, underscore GAIL's proactive diversification strategy.

Diversified Businesses

Petrochemicals and polymers

GAIL's petrochemicals and polymers operations focus on converting natural gas feedstocks into high-value thermoplastic resins, leveraging the company's integrated gas supply chain for efficient production. The primary facility is the Pata petrochemical complex in Auraiya, Uttar Pradesh, which features a gas-based naphtha cracker and downstream polymerization units with a total polymer capacity of 810,000 tonnes per annum (KTA). This includes a 200,000 KTA HDPE plant, two 200,000 KTA LLDPE/HDPE swing units, and a 210,000 KTA linear low-density polyethylene (LLDPE) unit, enabling flexible production to meet varying market demands.
Complementing Pata is the Brahmaputra Cracker and Polymer Limited (BCPL) facility in Lepetkata, Assam, where GAIL holds a significant stake and contributes to a polymer capacity of 280,000 KTA, focused on HDPE and LLDPE production using propane dehydrogenation technology. Together, these facilities deliver an annual polymer output exceeding 1 million tonnes, primarily consisting of HDPE, LLDPE, and polypropylene (PP) resins used in packaging, agriculture, and infrastructure applications.
In India's polymer market, GAIL commands approximately 15% share by capacity, positioning it as a key domestic supplier amid growing demand driven by urbanization and manufacturing expansion. A portion of its output is exported to international markets, supporting regional supply chains in Asia. These operations draw ethylene and propylene feedstocks from GAIL's natural gas transmission network, ensuring cost-effective integration with upstream activities.
To enhance competitiveness, GAIL has implemented technological upgrades, including metallocene catalysis in its UNIPOL PE process at Pata, which improves resin properties like clarity and strength while optimizing energy use and yield. Further advancements involve Clariant catalysts for propane dehydrogenation at BCPL, boosting conversion efficiency and reducing operational costs. Looking ahead, GAIL aims to add around 1.81 million tonnes per annum (MMTPA) in petrochemical capacity by 2027 through brownfield expansions and new projects, representing substantial growth beyond the current base to align with India's projected polymer demand increase of 8% annually.

Liquid hydrocarbons and LPG

GAIL's primary facility for LPG production is located at its Vijaipur gas processing plant in Madhya Pradesh, where it extracts liquefied petroleum gas from natural gas streams with an installed capacity of approximately 0.538 million metric tonnes per annum (MMTPA). This plant plays a central role in recovering LPG alongside other components, contributing to GAIL's overall liquid hydrocarbons output as part of its integrated natural gas processing operations. The production process involves cryogenic separation techniques to isolate LPG from raw natural gas feedstocks sourced from upstream suppliers.
In addition to LPG, GAIL's gas processing units, including Vijaipur, extract liquid hydrocarbons such as naphtha and propane, which are vital feedstocks for downstream industries. Propane, often separated for specific applications, complements LPG production, while naphtha serves as a key output for petrochemical uses. These extractions occur across GAIL's five processing plants, with a combined capacity for LPG and liquid hydrocarbons reaching 1.4 MMTPA, enabling efficient valorization of natural gas liquids.
GAIL transmits its LPG output through a dedicated pipeline network spanning 2,038 kilometers, comprising the Jamnagar-Loni Pipeline (JLPL) in the north and the Vizag-Secunderabad Pipeline (VSPL) in the south, with a current transport capacity of up to 3.83 MMTPA. This infrastructure ensures reliable delivery from processing sites to consumption centers, minimizing reliance on road or rail transport and reducing logistical costs. In September 2025, GAIL approved a Rs 5,350 crore expansion of the JLPL to double its capacity to 6.5 MMTPA, alongside a 114 km spur line from Vijaipur, to meet rising demand.
A significant efficiency improvement came in 2025 with the reduction in natural gas allocation for LPG shrinkagethe volume of gas consumed during processingfrom 0.32 million standard cubic meters per day (MMSCMD) to 0.2 MMSCMD, effective October 1, effective for wellhead gas used in extraction. This change, mandated by government policy, lowers operational gas consumption and enhances the net yield of LPG and liquids, supporting cost optimization without impacting output volumes.
GAIL distributes its LPG and extracted liquid hydrocarbons primarily to bottling plants for domestic cylinder filling and to industrial users for heating and feedstock needs, securing approximately 15% of India's LPG market share by capacity. This segment integrates with GAIL's broader natural gas operations by utilizing processed liquids to diversify revenue streams and serve residential, commercial, and manufacturing sectors across the country.

City gas distribution

GAIL's city gas distribution operations deliver piped natural gas (PNG) to domestic, commercial, and industrial consumers, alongside compressed natural gas (CNG) for vehicular use, primarily through urban networks managed by its subsidiaries and joint ventures. These entities operate in more than 10 geographical areas, with GAIL holding authorizations for up to 72 areas out of the country's total 307, enabling widespread urban coverage.
By the end of FY25, GAIL and its group companies had established over 9.6 million PNG connections across more than 300 districts, supported by a robust infrastructure of over 3,100 CNG stations for automotive refueling and PNG pipelines serving households and industries. In that year alone, the group commissioned approximately 1.23 million new PNG connections and 507 CNG stations, achieving a 54% market share in CNG and domestic PNG sales volume of 15.4 million standard cubic meters per day. Gas for these networks is sourced from GAIL's broader marketing and distribution channels to ensure reliable supply.
Expansion efforts under PNGRB authorizations target a 20% compound annual growth rate in connections, aiming to reach 10 million by 2030 through aggressive infrastructure development, including plans to add 260,000 domestic PNG connections and 85 CNG stations in the subsequent two years. This growth aligns with national objectives to achieve 126.3 million PNG connections overall by 2034, enhancing urban energy access.
Key drivers of adoption include the shift from liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) to PNG, which offers continuous supply, reduced logistical hassles, and lower long-term costs for consumers, alongside government incentives promoting CNG as a cleaner alternative for vehicles to support environmental goals and integrate with sustainable mobility initiatives like electric vehicle adoption.

Exploration and production

GAIL (India) Limited engages in upstream exploration and production (E&P) activities to secure domestic and international natural gas resources, complementing its downstream operations. The company holds participating interests in 14 E&P blocks as of 2025, including 11 in India across key sedimentary basins such as Mahanadi, Mumbai Offshore, Cambay, Assam-Arakan, and Tripura Fold Belt (including a new onland block RJ-ONHP-2021/1 awarded in August 2025 under OALP-VII in Rajasthan's Barmer district), as well as two offshore blocks in Myanmar and one shale gas joint venture in the Eagle Ford Basin, Texas, USA. These blocks were primarily acquired through competitive bidding rounds like the New Exploration Licensing Policy (NELP) and Discovered Small Fields (DSF) regimes, enabling GAIL to participate as either operator or non-operator partner.
In the Cambay Basin, GAIL serves as operator for two blocks under NELP-IX, with exploratory drilling activities underscoring its focus on this prolific onshore region in Gujarat. A notable example is block CB-ONN-2010/1, awarded in 2013, where GAIL holds a 25% participating interest alongside partners Bharat PetroResources Ltd (40%), Engineers India Ltd (10%), and Monnet Ispat & Energy Ltd (25%). Initial exploration in this block involved spudding eight wells as part of the minimum work program under the production sharing contract, targeting potential hydrocarbon accumulations in Tertiary formations. GAIL's strategy emphasizes appraisal and development in such assets to unlock unconventional resources, including shale gas, amid India's push for self-reliance in energy supplies.
GAIL's production from E&P joint ventures remains modest, equivalent to 1-2 million metric standard cubic meters per day (MMSCMD), largely derived from international partnerships. In Myanmar's blocks A-1 and A-3, where GAIL maintains a 10% stake, estimated in-place gas reserves range from 5.7 to 10 trillion cubic feet across three fields, with field-wide potential output of up to 16 MMSCMD over 20-25 yearsyielding GAIL's attributable share of approximately 1.6 MMSCMD once fully developed. The shale gas joint venture in the US further supports GAIL's exposure to unconventional resources, though domestic Indian blocks are predominantly in the exploration phase with limited commercial output to date. This production helps offset import dependencies, providing a hedge against supply volatility in GAIL's broader gas portfolio.
To advance its E&P portfolio, GAIL has committed significant capital, allocating approximately 1,400 crore (13% of total capex) to exploration and production in fiscal year 2025-26 as part of a 10,700 crore overall capex plan, marking a strategic increase from prior years' emphasis on midstream infrastructure. Since the 2010s, cumulative investments in E&P have exceeded 500 crore, funding seismic surveys, drilling programs, and appraisal activities, including ongoing efforts in 2024-2025 to evaluate hydrocarbon potential in mature basins like Cambay. These expenditures prioritize high-impact opportunities in unconventional gas, aligning with national goals to enhance domestic output and reduce reliance on liquefied natural gas imports.

Infrastructure and Assets

Pipeline network

GAIL (India) Limited maintains an extensive natural gas pipeline infrastructure, totaling 16,421 kilometers in length, which spans across 20 states including Andhra Pradesh, Assam, Bihar, Delhi, Gujarat, Haryana, Jharkhand, Karnataka, Kerala, Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra, Odisha, Punjab, Rajasthan, Tamil Nadu, Telangana, Uttar Pradesh, Uttarakhand, West Bengal, and Tripura. This network forms a critical backbone for gas transmission in India, connecting major production basins to consumption centers. Key pipelines within this system include the Hazira-Vijaipur-Jagdishpur (HVJ) pipeline, spanning 1,750 kilometers from Hazira in Gujarat to Jagdishpur in Uttar Pradesh, and the Mallavaram-Bhilwara pipeline, approximately 1,200 kilometers long, forming part of the broader Mallavaram-Bhopal-Bhilwara-Vijaipur system operated through a joint venture.
In 2025, a significant development in GAIL's pipeline assets is the nearing completion of the Mumbai-Nagpur-Jharsuguda Pipeline (MNJPL), a 1,707-kilometer project connecting western and eastern India. The pipeline is scheduled for full commissioning by December 31, 2025, following delays attributed to land acquisition and regulatory approvals in multiple states. The project has experienced a cost overrun of 411.12 crore, elevating the total capital expenditure to 8,255.37 crore from the original estimate. This addition will enhance connectivity to industrial hubs in Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, and Odisha.
GAIL's pipeline capacity has undergone substantial expansion over the past decade, growing from approximately 150 million standard cubic meters per day (MMSCMD) in 2015 to a current operational capacity of 204 MMSCMD, supporting increased gas volumes transported across the network. The company targets further enhancements to reach around 250 MMSCMD by 2027, driven by ongoing compressor station upgrades and integration of new pipelines like the MNJPL. These expansions aim to meet rising demand from power, fertilizer, and city gas distribution sectors while optimizing existing infrastructure.
Looking ahead, GAIL is advancing future projects to extend its network, including enhancements to the Northeast gas grid through its joint venture Indradhanush Gas Grid Limited (IGGL), which has achieved full operationalization of a 1,676-kilometer intra-state grid connecting key cities in Assam, Arunachal Pradesh, Meghalaya, Mizoram, Nagaland, and Tripura, commencing natural gas supply to Numaligarh Refinery on November 11, 2025. Additionally, GAIL is conducting hydrogen blending pilots in select pipeline segments, building on successful 5% volumetric blending trials in city gas networks to assess compatibility for broader natural gas infrastructure integration, aligning with India's green energy transition goals.

Terminals, storage, and processing facilities

GAIL (India) Limited operates several key LNG terminals to facilitate the importation and regasification of liquefied natural gas, supporting its natural gas transmission and distribution network. The Dahej LNG terminal in Gujarat, in which GAIL holds a 12.5% stake through Petronet LNG Limited, has a current regasification capacity of 17.5 MMTPA (with expansions), making it India's oldest and largest such facility. This terminal primarily imports LNG from sources like Qatar's Ras Laffan and serves as a critical entry point for natural gas into GAIL's pipeline system. Additionally, GAIL maintains a partial stake in the Dabhol LNG terminal in Maharashtra, with a current regasification capacity of 5 MMTPA, enabling monsoon-season operations and planned expansions to 6.3 MMTPA by mid-2027 to enhance supply reliability. The upcoming Dhamra LNG terminal in Odisha, developed by Adani Total Gas with GAIL securing a long-term regasification agreement and potential equity participation alongside Indian Oil Corporation, is slated for an initial capacity of 5 MMTPA, expandable to 10 MMTPA, to bolster eastern India's gas infrastructure.
For storage capabilities, GAIL utilizes underground facilities at Vaghodia in Gujarat to manage natural gas inventories and mitigate supply fluctuations. These facilities are complemented by strategic reserves initiatives, including joint efforts with Oil and Natural Gas Corporation (ONGC) and Oil India Limited to develop underground storage in depleted hydrocarbon wells, aiming for up to 1 billion standard cubic meters (BSM3) to enhance national energy security against global price volatility.
GAIL's gas processing plants play a vital role in extracting valuable products from raw natural gas. The Vijaipur plant in Madhya Pradesh, one of GAIL's primary gas processing units, recovers liquefied petroleum gas (LPG), propane, pentane, and naphtha from natural gas streams, with a throughput exceeding 15 MMSCMD for LPG production alone. Similarly, the Pata petrochemical complex in Uttar Pradesh processes ethane and propane feedstocks to manufacture polymers such as high-density polyethylene (HDPE) and linear low-density polyethylene (LLDPE), supporting India's plastics industry with an integrated capacity of around 810,000 tonnes per annum.

Subsidiaries and Joint Ventures

Major subsidiaries

GAIL Gas Limited is a wholly-owned subsidiary of GAIL (India) Limited, specializing in city gas distribution (CGD) across India. It manages operations in 16 geographical areas, supplying piped natural gas (PNG) to domestic, industrial, commercial, and compressed natural gas (CNG) to vehicular customers, with a focus on expanding clean energy access in urban and semi-urban regions. As of FY24, the company served a significant portion of GAIL's nearly 9.57 million domestic PNG consumers group-wide and generated revenue of approximately 10,944 crore, reflecting steady growth in its distribution network and customer base.
Brahmaputra Cracker and Polymer Limited (BCPL), in which GAIL holds a 70% stake, operates one of India's key petrochemical complexes in Lepetkata, Dibrugarh, Assam. The facility, with an overall capacity of 2.2 million metric tonnes per annum (MMTPA), includes an ethylene cracker unit producing 220,000 tonnes per annum (TPA) of ethylene and associated downstream polymer plants for high-density polyethylene (HDPE), linear low-density polyethylene (LLDPE), and other products. Commissioned in January 2016, BCPL supports regional industrial development by processing natural gas feedstock into value-added petrochemicals, achieving full operational capacity by 2018 and contributing to GAIL's diversification into polymers.
GAIL Global (Singapore) Pte Ltd, a wholly-owned subsidiary incorporated in 2004, serves as GAIL's international arm for liquefied natural gas (LNG) trading and marketing, primarily in the Asian region. Operationalized for LNG activities in 2012, it facilitates spot and long-term LNG procurement, risk management, and trade optimization to support GAIL's global energy supply chain. The entity plays a critical role in securing diverse LNG sources amid fluctuating market dynamics.
GAIL Global (USA) Inc., another wholly-owned subsidiary, is headquartered in Houston, Texas, and focuses on LNG procurement from the United States market. Established to strengthen GAIL's access to North American supplies, it manages key contracts, including a 20-year agreement with Cheniere Energy's Sabine Pass Liquefaction for 3.5 MMTPA of LNG, indexed to the Henry Hub price. This entity supports GAIL's overall LNG portfolio of around 15.5 MMTPA, enhancing supply security and cost efficiency through direct engagement with U.S. exporters.

Key joint ventures and partnerships

GAIL (India) Limited has formed several strategic joint ventures to expand its presence in city gas distribution, LNG infrastructure, and exploration activities, sharing equity to mitigate risks and leverage partner expertise. One prominent example is Indraprastha Gas Limited (IGL), where GAIL holds a 22.5% stake alongside Bharat Petroleum Corporation Limited (BPCL) at an equal share and the Government of NCT of Delhi at 5%; IGL leads natural gas distribution in the Delhi-NCR region, serving over 3.07 million domestic connections as of March 2025 and a vast CNG vehicle fleet.
Another key partnership is Mahanagar Gas Limited (MGL), in which GAIL maintains a 32.5% stake as the primary promoter, focusing on piped natural gas and CNG supply in Mumbai and surrounding areas, supporting nearly 2.83 million domestic consumers as of March 2025 and over 5,100 industrial units.
In the LNG sector, GAIL participates in Petronet LNG Limited with a 12.5% equity stake, shared equally among GAIL, Indian Oil Corporation Limited, Oil and Natural Gas Corporation Limited, and BPCL; this venture operates major import terminals at Dahej and Kochi, with a total capacity of 22.5 million metric tonnes per annum to bolster India's LNG supply chain.
For exploration and production (E&P), GAIL has collaborated through a 2025 memorandum of understanding with ONGC Videsh Limited to jointly evaluate and pursue overseas upstream opportunities, building on earlier ties with Hindustan Petroleum Corporation Limited (HPCL) for bidding on domestic and international E&P blocks under frameworks like the New Exploration Licensing Policy.
In recent developments, GAIL advanced its sustainability efforts via a March 2025 partnership with Accelera by Cummins to develop green hydrogen production, transportation, and storage projects, targeting applications in transport, power, and steel sectors to support India's net-zero goals. Additionally, GAIL secured a multi-year LNG supply agreement with Vitol in July 2025 for approximately 1 million metric tonnes per annum starting in 2026, enhancing long-term access to global LNG resources.

Global Presence

International subsidiaries and offices

GAIL (India) Limited maintains a strategic international presence through wholly owned subsidiaries focused on LNG trading, procurement, and overseas investments to support its global energy sourcing needs. These entities enable the company to engage directly in international markets, negotiate long-term contracts, and manage risks associated with volatile global gas prices.
In addition to its subsidiaries, GAIL operates representative offices in Cairo, Egypt; Yangon, Myanmar; and Moscow, Russia (as Bharat Energy Office) to pursue business opportunities in those regions.
In Singapore, GAIL operates GAIL Global (Singapore) Pte Ltd, established in 2004 as a wholly owned subsidiary serving as the regional headquarters for Asia-Pacific trading activities. This entity primarily handles LNG trading, shipping, and risk management, contributing to GAIL's overall LNG sourcing portfolio of approximately 15.5 million metric tonnes per annum (MMTPA). It facilitates spot and long-term LNG purchases from key suppliers in the region, enhancing GAIL's supply security and market access.
GAIL's operations in the United States are managed through GAIL Global (USA) Inc. (GGUI), a wholly owned subsidiary based in Houston, Texas, which oversees LNG procurement and related deals. GGUI's subsidiary, GAIL Global (USA) LNG LLC (GGULL), formed in 2013, specifically handles U.S.-based import contracts, including a significant 2.3 MMTPA capacity booking in the Cove Point LNG liquefaction project. This arrangement accounts for a substantial portion of GAIL's LNG imports, supporting diversification of supply sources amid India's growing natural gas demand. The Houston office focuses on negotiating with American suppliers and monitoring North American energy markets.

Overseas projects and investments

GAIL (India) Limited has pursued overseas investments primarily to secure long-term liquefied natural gas (LNG) supplies and diversify its energy portfolio beyond domestic sources. In 2025, the company actively sought equity stakes in U.S.-based LNG projects as part of its strategy to enhance import security and reduce reliance on spot market purchases. Specifically, GAIL issued a tender for up to a 26% equity stake in an American LNG facility, bundled with a 15-year gas supply agreement, receiving bids from five U.S. companies as of May 2025. This move aligns with India's broader goal to increase natural gas's share in its energy mix from 6% to 15% by 2030.
Complementing these efforts, GAIL entered initial talks for a long-term LNG purchase agreement with the proposed Alaska LNG project, which could provide additional volumes to support the company's sourcing needs. The project involves developing a pipeline and export terminal in Alaska, potentially offering competitive pricing amid global LNG market dynamics. GAIL's total LNG sourcing portfolio stands at approximately 15.5 million metric tons per annum (MMTPA), with ongoing explorations aimed at expanding equity participation in international assets.
In Australia, GAIL has secured supply commitments from facilities like the Gladstone LNG project, though direct equity holdings remain limited to trading and sourcing arrangements through its subsidiary.

Sustainability and CSR

Environmental and renewable initiatives

GAIL (India) Limited has advanced its net-zero emissions target to achieve 100% reduction in Scope 1 and Scope 2 greenhouse gas emissions by 2035, with a 35% reduction in Scope 3 emissions by 2040, as part of its broader decarbonization strategy. This commitment includes a target of 1 GW of renewable energy capacity by 2025, though as of 2025, the installed capacity is 145 MW comprising 118 MW from wind power and 27 MW from solar installations, primarily through subsidiaries and joint ventures; the company also targets 3 GW by 2030. GAIL is scaling up further, including a memorandum of understanding with AM Green to jointly develop up to 2.5 GW of renewable energy capacity, focusing on solar and wind integration. Additionally, GAIL has piloted green hydrogen production at its Vijaipur facility in Madhya Pradesh, where a 10 MW proton exchange membrane electrolyzer plant was commissioned in 2024 to blend hydrogen with natural gas, marking a key step toward low-carbon fuels. Recent initiatives include commissioning compressed biogas (CBG) plants in Ranchi and Bengaluru.
To control emissions, GAIL has implemented flare gas recovery units (FGRUs) at multiple sites, such as the C2-C3 flare at its plants, enabling the recycling of approximately 1,000 standard cubic meters per hour of flare gas and avoiding around 18,000 tonnes of CO2 emissions annually. For methane management, the company employs Leak Detection and Repair (LDAR) programs across operations and has installed Leak Detection Systems (LDS) on pipelines like the Kailaras-Malanpur line to identify and mitigate fugitive emissions.
GAIL maintains ISO 14001:2015 certification for its environmental management systems, ensuring compliance with international standards for pollution prevention and continual improvement at its facilities. The company also incorporates biodiversity offsets along pipeline routes that traverse forest areas, implementing afforestation and habitat restoration initiatives to compensate for ecological impacts.

Community engagement and social programs

GAIL (India) Limited fulfills its corporate social responsibility (CSR) obligations by allocating at least 2% of its average net profits over the preceding three years to community development initiatives, as required under Section 135 of the Companies Act, 2013. In FY25, the company expended approximately 150 crore on CSR activities, focusing primarily on education, health, and rural upliftment to foster sustainable social progress.
A flagship initiative, GAIL Utkarsh, launched in 2009, provides specialized residential coaching and skill training to economically disadvantaged youth for competitive exams in engineering, medicine, and other professional fields. The program operates across multiple centers, such as those in Kanpur, Varanasi, and Haldwani, offering free boarding, lodging, and academic support; it has enabled over 1,250 students to secure admissions in premier institutions cumulatively since inception.
In the health domain, GAIL conducts regular medical camps and mobile health units in rural areas, serving underserved populations with check-ups, diagnostics, and awareness sessions on preventive care. These efforts have covered more than 200 villages near company operations, including specialized camps under projects like Saheli for women's and adolescent health, benefiting thousands annually by improving access to basic healthcare services.
GAIL emphasizes women empowerment through initiatives like Sashakt, which supports self-help groups (SHGs) with training in vocational skills, financial literacy, and micro-enterprise development to promote economic independence. In the Northeast region, the company contributes to rural development by facilitating electrification and energy access projects, enhancing infrastructure in remote villages to support livelihoods and community resilience.
In 2025, GAIL expanded its social programs with digital literacy drives targeting rural youth and women, equipping participants with basic computing and online skills to bridge the digital divide, alongside disaster relief efforts providing aid and rehabilitation support in affected areas. These activities align closely with United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), particularly SDG 4 (Quality Education), SDG 3 (Good Health and Well-being), SDG 5 (Gender Equality), and SDG 11 (Sustainable Cities and Communities).

Financial Performance and Investments

Revenue streams and profitability

GAIL's primary revenue streams derive from natural gas transmission and marketing, which form the core of its operations, alongside contributions from petrochemicals, liquefied natural gas (LNG) trading, and other segments such as liquid hydrocarbons. In FY2025, the company's total revenue reached 1.42 lakh crore, with gas marketing accounting for approximately 81% (1,11,666 crore), reflecting its dominant role in India's natural gas infrastructure and wholesale distribution. Petrochemicals accounted for about 6% (8,045 crore), driven by production and sales of polymers and chemicals at facilities like the Pata and Panipat complexes, while LNG trading formed a subset of the gas marketing segment.
Profitability for GAIL has remained resilient amid fluctuating global energy markets, with EBITDA margins typically ranging between 15% and 20% over recent years, supported by regulated tariffs in transmission and cost efficiencies in marketing. In Q2 FY2026, the company reported a profit after tax (PAT) decline of approximately 17% year-over-year to 2,217 crore, impacted by LNG price volatility and compressed petrochemical margins, underscoring challenges despite its diversified portfolio and long-term contracts. Key financial metrics highlight GAIL's strong balance sheet, including a return on equity (ROE) of approximately 16.8%, a low debt-to-equity ratio of 0.25 indicating prudent leverage, and a dividend payout ratio of 30-40% that balances shareholder returns with reinvestment needs.
Cost factors significantly influence GAIL's profitability, particularly volatile feedstock prices for petrochemical operations, which are tied to international benchmarks like Henry Hub natural gas rates, and regulatory tariffs that govern transmission revenues under oversight from the Petroleum and Natural Gas Regulatory Board (PNGRB). Rising feedstock costs can compress margins in downstream segments, while tariff revisionssuch as proposed increases of up to 35% in pipeline ratesoffer potential offsets by enhancing pre-tax earnings, though approvals remain subject to regulatory scrutiny. These elements collectively shape GAIL's financial structure, enabling consistent performance despite external pressures.

Major investments and future expansions

GAIL (India) Limited sustains an annual capital expenditure surpassing 10,000 crore to support its infrastructure growth, with FY25 recording 10,512 crore allocated across pipeline development, city gas distribution, renewables, and operational enhancements. In Q2 FY2026, capex spend reached approximately 1,885 crore, focused on pipelines and petrochemicals. In 2025, the company's capex priorities emphasize the commissioning of the 1,707 km Mumbai-Nagpur-Jharsuguda Pipeline (MNJPL), a critical natural gas transmission project connecting western and eastern India, now targeted for full operationalization by December 31 despite earlier delays and a 411 crore cost overrun. Complementing this, GAIL is advancing approximately 2,000 km of new pipeline infrastructure, including the 114 km Vijaipur-Bina segment authorized by the Petroleum and Natural Gas Regulatory Board (PNGRB) in September 2025 to integrate with BPCL's Bina refinery, enhancing regional gas supply networks (estimated capex 450 crore).
The company is pursuing significant expansions in its downstream operations, particularly in liquefied petroleum gas (LPG), with the ongoing Jamnagar-Loni LPG pipeline project, approved in September 2025, which will double throughput from 3.25 MMTPA to 6.5 MMTPA at a cost of 5,350 crore, supporting petrochemical feedstock integration and domestic supply chains. For LNG infrastructure, GAIL is incorporating additions at the Chhara terminal, commissioned in January 2025 by HPCL, via long-term regasification agreements to bolster import volumes and mitigate supply disruptions, aligning with broader network expansions like the Dabhol terminal's upgrade to 6.3 MMTPA.
Looking ahead, GAIL's strategic roadmap for 2025-2030 envisions diversification into sustainable energy, targeting renewables to comprise a larger share of its portfolio through capacity additions in solar, wind, and hybrid projects. This plan also allocates resources to exploration and production (E&P) activities, aiming to secure upstream gas reserves amid rising domestic demand. However, these ambitions face risks such as project delays, exemplified by the MNJPL timeline extensions, and foreign exchange fluctuations impacting overseas procurement and investments, potentially elevating costs in a volatile global energy market.
