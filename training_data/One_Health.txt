 One Health is an integrated, collaborative framework that recognizes the interdependence of human, animal, and environmental health, emphasizing prevention and control of diseases at their shared interfaces, particularly zoonoses which account for the majority of emerging infectious threats. Originating from early observations of disease transmission across species, such as Rudolf Virchow's 19th-century coinage of "zoonosis" linking human and animal pathology, the modern concept evolved through Calvin Schwabe's "One Medicine" in the 1960s and gained prominence with the 2004 Manhattan Principles following SARS, formalizing multisectoral action. Key organizations including the CDC, WHO, FAO, and WOAH promote its implementation for threats like avian influenza and antimicrobial resistance, asserting that over 75% of new or emerging human diseases stem from animal sources, necessitating ecosystem-wide surveillance and intervention.
Despite conceptual alignment with causal pathways of pathogen spilloverevidenced by historical outbreaks like Ebola and COVID-19empirical demonstrations of broad effectiveness remain context-specific, with successes in rabies elimination programs averting disability-adjusted life years through joint veterinary-public health efforts, yet challenges persist in scaling due to governance silos and resource disparities. Critics highlight theoretical shortcomings, such as insufficient integration of environmental dynamics beyond human-animal foci or risks of anthropocentric bias that instrumentalize wildlife for human-centric goals, potentially overlooking intrinsic ecosystem values or exacerbating global inequities in implementation. National plans, like the U.S. One Health Framework released in 2025, aim to address these by coordinating federal agencies for zoonotic threat mitigation, underscoring the approach's role in resilience against pandemics while calling for rigorous, data-driven evaluation to substantiate claims of systemic health optimization.

Definition and Core Principles

Definition

One Health (Portuguese: "Uma S Sade" or "Sade nica") is an integrated, unifying approach that aims to sustainably balance and optimize the health of people, animals, and ecosystems by recognizing their interconnections and the risks at their interfaces. This framework promotes collaborative, multisectoral, and transdisciplinary efforts across local, regional, national, and global levels to achieve optimal health outcomes, particularly in preventing and responding to zoonotic diseases, antimicrobial resistance, and environmental threats. The approach underscores that human health cannot be isolated from animal and environmental health, as evidenced by the fact that around 75% of newly emerging or re-emerging infectious diseases in humans are zoonotic in origin. Its three main pillarshuman health, animal health, and environmental healthform the base of the concept, though some sources expand to additional components such as integrated surveillance, food safety, and climate adaptation.
Central to One Health is the emphasis on surveillance, research, and policy integration involving sectors such as public health, veterinary medicine, ecology, and environmental science. It advocates for proactive measures at the human-animal-environment nexus to mitigate threats like pandemics, which have demonstrated the cascading effects of disruptions in one domain on others, such as the COVID-19 outbreak originating from animal reservoirs. While primarily focused on health optimization, the concept extends to food security, biodiversity conservation, and ecosystem services, acknowledging plants and wildlife as integral components in some formulations.
The One Health paradigm shifts from siloed disciplines to holistic strategies, fostering communication and coordination among stakeholders to address complex challenges that transcend traditional boundaries. This definition, endorsed by international bodies including the World Health Organization, Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, and World Organisation for Animal Health, reflects a consensus on the empirical reality of interdependent health systems rather than ideological constructs.

Foundational Principles

The foundational principles of One Health center on the recognition that human health is inextricably linked to the health of animals and ecosystems, necessitating an integrated approach to disease prevention and health optimization. Formulated in 2004 through the Manhattan Principles during a symposium hosted by the Wildlife Conservation Society and involving experts from organizations such as the World Health Organization (WHO), Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO), and Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), these principles advocate for holistic strategies to address emerging infectious diseases by bridging silos in human, veterinary, and environmental sciences. The principles underscore causal interconnections, such as zoonotic transmissions driven by habitat disruption and wildlife trade, prioritizing prevention through multisectoral collaboration over reactive measures.
Key tenets include acknowledging the impacts of land and water use on health outcomes, integrating wildlife health into global surveillance systems, and promoting collaborative governance across governments, communities, and disciplines to mitigate threats like antimicrobial resistance and biodiversity loss. The 12 Manhattan Principles specifically recommend: (1) recognizing essential links between human, domestic animal, and wildlife health; (2) noting how land and water decisions affect ecosystems; (3) incorporating wildlife science in prevention; (4) leveraging human health programs for conservation; (5) devising holistic disease control; (6) balancing biodiversity and human needs; (7) regulating wildlife trade; (8) limiting mass culling; (9) boosting health infrastructure investments; (10) fostering cross-sector partnerships; (11) establishing surveillance networks; and (12) enhancing education for awareness. These principles emphasize empirical evidence from outbreaks like severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS) in 2002-2003, where wildlife reservoirs were identified as sources, highlighting the need for proactive, evidence-based interventions grounded in ecological realities rather than isolated sectoral responses.
Subsequent refinements, such as those from the WHO-FAO-OIE tripartite alliance, reinforce these foundations by defining One Health as an approach to sustainably balance health across sectors through shared surveillance and response mechanisms, applicable from local to global scales. Empirical support derives from zoonotic disease patterns, where approximately 75% of emerging pathogens originate in animals, underscoring the causal realism of integrated monitoring to avert pandemics. While later frameworks introduce elements like equity and transdisciplinarity, the core remains rooted in the interdependence principle, avoiding anthropocentric biases by treating ecosystem integrity as a prerequisite for sustained human well-being.

Historical Development

Pre-20th Century Origins

The recognition of interconnections between human, animal, and environmental health dates to ancient civilizations, where empirical observations linked diseases across species and surroundings. Hippocrates (c. 460377 BCE), often regarded as the father of Western medicine, emphasized in his treatise On Airs, Waters, and Places that human health is influenced by environmental factors such as air, water, and locality, which also affect animal well-being and disease transmission. Similarly, Aristotle (384322 BCE) documented parallels in anatomy, physiology, and disease susceptibility between humans and animals, laying early groundwork for comparative approaches to health. These ideas reflected practical awareness of zoonotic risks, as seen in ancient Egyptian and Indian texts describing shared plagues among livestock, wildlife, and people tied to ecological conditions.
In the 18th century, advancements in veterinary science formalized comparative medicine, bridging human and animal health. Claude Bourgelat established the world's first veterinary college in Lyon, France, in 1761, promoting the study of diseases across species through shared pathological principles. Edward Jenner (17491823) applied animal-derived insights to human vaccination, using cowpox material in 1796 to protect against smallpox, demonstrating causal links between animal infections and human immunity. Flix Vicq dAzyr (17481794) further advocated integrating human and veterinary education around 1790, arguing for unified medical training to address transmissible diseases.
The 19th century saw explicit articulation of unified health principles amid rising industrialization and urbanization, which amplified zoonotic threats. Rudolf Virchow (18211902), a German pathologist from a farming background, coined the term "zoonosis" in the 1850s while investigating Trichinella spiralis infections in swine that affected humans, asserting, "Between animal and human medicine there are no dividing linesnor should there be." His work integrated environmental factors, such as sanitation in animal husbandry, into disease causation. William Osler (18491919), influenced by Virchow during training in Germany, reinforced these linkages through lectures on veterinary pathology's relevance to human medicine, emphasizing shared microbial etiologies. These developments, grounded in post-mortem examinations and epidemiological tracing, prefigured modern One Health by prioritizing causal mechanisms over siloed disciplines.

20th Century Formalization

The formalization of the One Health concept in the 20th century built on earlier recognition of zoonotic interconnections, emphasizing unified human-veterinary collaboration. In 1947, James H. Steele, DVM, MPH, established the Veterinary Public Health Division at the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), which focused on epidemiological surveillance and control of zoonoses such as rabies and brucellosis through joint human and animal health efforts. This initiative marked an institutional push toward interdisciplinary zoonotic disease management, with Steele advocating for "One Medicine" in communications and collaborations throughout the latter half of the century.
A pivotal advancement occurred in 1964 when Calvin W. Schwabe, DVM, ScD, MPHa veterinary epidemiologistcoined the term "One Medicine" in the first edition of his textbook Veterinary Medicine and Human Health. Schwabe argued for a single, comparative medical paradigm bridging human and veterinary disciplines, rejecting artificial silos to better combat shared diseases, particularly zoonoses originating from animal reservoirs. In 1966, he founded the Department of Epidemiology and Preventive Medicine at the University of California, Davis School of Veterinary Medicine, which institutionalized training and research in unified health approaches, training professionals in zoonotic epidemiology and preventive strategies.
Schwabe's framework evolved through subsequent works, including the third edition of his textbook in 1984, which elaborated on systemic interactions between humans, animals, and their environments in disease causation and control. Steele and Schwabe's combined efforts, spanning public health administration and academic reform, crystallized "One Medicine" as a formal doctrine by the late 20th century, influencing global zoonotic policy despite persistent disciplinary divides. This period laid empirical groundwork via case studies of outbreaks like those of echinococcosis, underscoring causal links across species without invoking unsubstantiated environmental determinism.

21st Century Expansion and Milestones

The modern expansion of One Health in the 21st century was catalyzed by high-profile zoonotic outbreaks, including the 2003 severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS) epidemic and recurring H5N1 avian influenza events, which underscored the need for integrated surveillance across human, animal, and environmental sectors. This period saw the formalization of collaborative frameworks, establishment of dedicated offices, and international declarations that propelled One Health from conceptual advocacy to operational policy.
A pivotal milestone occurred on September 29, 2004, when the Wildlife Conservation Society hosted a symposium in New York City, resulting in the publication of the 12 Manhattan Principles. These principles advocated for interdisciplinary collaboration to prevent epidemic and epizootic diseases, emphasizing recognition of the interconnected health of humans, animals, and ecosystems as a foundational strategy. 
In June 2007, the American Medical Association adopted a resolution endorsing collaboration between human and veterinary medicine to address zoonotic diseases, marking early institutional buy-in within professional bodies. This was followed by the December 2007 International Ministerial Conference on Avian and Pandemic Influenza in New Delhi, India, which recommended a One Health approach for global pandemic preparedness.
The October 2008 International Ministerial Conference on Animal and Pandemic Influenza in Sharm el-Sheikh, Egypt, released a strategic framework integrating One Health into national and international policies for reducing risks from emerging diseases. In 2009, the U.S. Agency for International Development launched the Emerging Pandemic Threats Program to predict and prevent zoonotic spillovers, while the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) established its One Health Office to coordinate domestic efforts.
The April 2010 Hanoi Declaration, adopted at a conference in Vietnam, called for widespread One Health implementation to combat antimicrobial resistance and zoonoses. Later that year, the Stone Mountain Meeting in Georgia, USA, outlined actionable steps for advancing One Health, including enhanced data sharing and training. The Tripartite alliancecomprising the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO), World Organisation for Animal Health (WOAH, formerly OIE), and World Health Organization (WHO)convened its first meeting in November 2011 in Mexico City, focusing on rabies, influenza, and antimicrobial resistance.
Subsequent events included the February 2012 One Health Summit in Davos, Switzerland, which approved the Davos One Health Action Plan for coordinated global responses, and the first International One Health Congress in February 2011 in Melbourne, Australia, attended by over 650 participants. The One Health Commission, a U.S.-based nonprofit, was founded in 2009 to promote transdisciplinary initiatives.
Expansion accelerated in the 2020s amid the COVID-19 pandemic, which exemplified zoonotic transmission dynamics. In 2022, the Tripartite evolved into the Quadripartite alliance by incorporating the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP), launching the One Health Joint Plan of Action (2022-2026) to foster sustainable health solutions across sectors. On January 10, 2025, the U.S. government released its first National One Health Framework to Address Zoonotic Diseases and Advance Public Health Preparedness (2025-2029), outlining goals for federal coordination on surveillance, prevention, and response to shared threats. These developments reflect One Health's shift toward institutionalized, multisectoral implementation, with over 70 countries reporting adoption of national plans by 2023.

Scientific Foundations

Interdependence of Human, Animal, and Environmental Health

The One Health framework asserts that human health is inextricably linked to animal and environmental health, as pathogens, ecosystems, and behaviors form a dynamic system where changes in one component propagate effects across others. Disruptions such as habitat alteration or biodiversity loss in animal populations can facilitate pathogen spillover to humans, while poor human practices like intensive agriculture amplify risks to animal welfare and ecosystem stability. This interdependence stems from shared evolutionary histories, where most human pathogens originate from animal reservoirs, necessitating integrated surveillance to mitigate cascading health threats.
Empirical data underscore zoonotic transmission as a primary vector of this linkage, with approximately 60% of emerging infectious diseases arising from animal sources and up to 75% classified as zoonotic in broader reviews of outbreaks. For instance, viruses like SARS-CoV-2 and Ebola demonstrate how wildlife reservoirs, stressed by environmental pressures, enable cross-species jumps, often at human-animal interfaces altered by deforestation or urbanization. These events are not random but causally tied to increased human encroachment on wildlife habitats, elevating contact rates and pathogen exposure.
Environmental factors further entwine these health domains, as land-use changes in tropical forested regions correlate with heightened zoonotic emergence risks due to biodiversity hotspots and altered ecological barriers. Climate-driven shifts, including warming temperatures, expand vector habitatssuch as mosquitoes for arbovirusesand disrupt migration patterns of reservoir species, thereby increasing spillover probabilities. Anthropogenic activities like agricultural expansion exemplify bidirectional impacts: they degrade ecosystems, fostering disease reservoirs in livestock, while antimicrobial use in animals contributes to resistance strains affecting human populations. This causal chain highlights the necessity of holistic interventions, as isolated human-centric measures fail to address root environmental and animal drivers.

Empirical Evidence from Zoonotic Diseases

More than 60% of known infectious diseases in humans are zoonotic, capable of transmission from animals to people, while approximately 75% of emerging infectious diseases reported over the past three decades have zoonotic origins, primarily from wildlife reservoirs. These statistics derive from systematic reviews of pathogen data, illustrating how disruptions at human-animal-environmental interfacessuch as habitat encroachment, wildlife trade, and livestock intensificationfacilitate spillovers, as evidenced by genomic tracing of viruses like those causing Ebola and severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS).
Rabies exemplifies empirical success in zoonotic control through One Health integration. Transmitted mainly by domestic dogs, which account for 99% of human cases globally, rabies has been nearly eliminated in humans via mass dog vaccination campaigns targeting 70% population coverage, as modeled and achieved in programs yielding zero indigenous human deaths in Goa, India, from 2015 onward after sustained efforts combining animal immunization, human post-exposure treatment, and joint surveillance by veterinary and public health sectors. Similar outcomes in Bhutan and other regions demonstrate that addressing the reservoir host prevents human morbidity, with cost-benefit analyses showing vaccination averts thousands of deaths annually at lower expense than reactive human treatments.
SARS-CoV-1, emerging in 20022003, provides evidence of multi-host zoonotic chains, with genomic and epidemiological data linking the virus from human patients to civets and ultimately horseshoe bats in southern China, where live animal markets amplified transmission. One Health responses, including closure of wildlife markets and enhanced cross-species surveillance, curtailed the outbreak, which infected over 8,000 people across 29 countries, highlighting how unified animal trade regulations and reservoir monitoring mitigate amplification risks.
Ebola virus disease (EVD) outbreaks, such as those since 1976, trace to zoonotic spillovers from fruit bats and apes, with spatial modeling of 51 wildlife infection sites and human cases revealing hotspots at forest edges where bushmeat hunting and habitat loss converge. Serological surveys in endemic African regions detect antibodies in humans without clinical disease, indicating repeated low-level exposures, while One Health frameworks have enabled predictive mapping and early warning systems that reduced the 20142016 West Africa epidemic's spread through integrated wildlife, veterinary, and human health data. These cases affirm causal pathways from environmental pressures to pathogen emergence, supporting proactive interventions over siloed responses.

Role in Antimicrobial Resistance and Emerging Threats

The One Health framework addresses antimicrobial resistance (AMR) by emphasizing surveillance and stewardship across human medicine, veterinary practice, and environmental compartments, where resistant bacteria and genes circulate bidirectionally. For instance, livestock production contributes significantly to environmental AMR dissemination via manure, wastewater, and soil contamination, with studies detecting clinically relevant resistance genes in surface waters linked to agricultural runoff. The World Health Organization classifies AMR as a top global health threat, estimating 700,000 annual human deaths from resistant infections as of 2016, with projections reaching 10 million by 2050 absent coordinated interventions. Empirical models quantify One Health interventions' potential, such as reducing veterinary antibiotic use by 20-30% in high-income settings to curb human AMR transmission, though cross-sectoral data gaps persist in low-resource areas.
One Health mitigates AMR through integrated policies like joint antimicrobial stewardship programs, which align dosing guidelines and usage monitoring between sectors to prevent selection pressure in animal reservoirs that rebounds to human pathogens. In Southeast Asia, regional reviews highlight how fragmented surveillance overlooks environmental hotspots, advocating One Health platforms for genomic tracking of shared resistomes, as evidenced by methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) strains identical across swine farms, abattoirs, and hospitals. Wildlife interfaces amplify risks, with studies documenting AMR plasmid transfer from domesticated animals to wild species, underscoring the need for ecosystem-wide monitoring to interrupt evolutionary drivers like overuse in aquaculture, where up to 80% of antibiotics may be applied prophylactically.
Beyond AMR, One Health counters emerging threats from zoonotic pathogens and environmental shifts, integrating predictive modeling at human-animal-ecosystems interfaces to forecast spillovers responsible for 60-75% of novel infectious diseases. The Global Early Warning System (GLEWS+), operational since 2010 under FAO-OIE-WHO collaboration, has facilitated early alerts for threats like highly pathogenic avian influenza variants, enabling preemptive containment across borders. Climate-driven habitat changes exacerbate these risks, as warming expands vector ranges and antibiotic-polluted waters foster resistant opportunists, with One Health approaches linking hydrological data to outbreak patterns for proactive vaccination and habitat management. Case analyses from 2000-2020 outbreaks demonstrate that siloed responses delay detection by months, whereas integrated systems reduced avian influenza transmission chains by 40% in collaborative surveillance zones.

Organizational Involvement

International Bodies and Collaborations

The primary international collaboration advancing One Health is the Quadripartite alliance, established among the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO), the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP), the World Health Organization (WHO), and the World Organisation for Animal Health (WOAH, formerly known as OIE). Initially formed as a Tripartite agreement in 2010 between FAO, WHO, and WOAH to address zoonotic diseases and food safety, it expanded to include UNEP in 2022, recognizing the critical role of environmental factors in health interdependencies. This partnership facilitates coordinated global responses to health threats at the human-animal-environment interface, including antimicrobial resistance and emerging pathogens, through shared technical guidance and capacity-building for member states, such as the WHO's OpenWHO.org course "Response preparedness for zoonotic disease outbreaks using a One Health approach," which focuses on integrating human, animal, and environmental health perspectives and includes modules on Introduction to One Health, the human health perspective to One Health, and the animal health perspective on One Health.
The Quadripartite operates via a joint secretariat hosted by WHO, which supports inter-agency communication and implementation of the One Health Joint Plan of Action (20222026). This plan outlines 146 activities across five pillarsgovernance, sectoral integration, evidence and knowledge, financing, and capacity developmentaimed at preventing, predicting, detecting, and responding to health risks while promoting sustainable ecosystems. In December 2023, the alliance released a practical guide for countries, detailing three implementation pathways (governance, sectoral integration, and evidence generation) and five progressive steps to operationalize One Health at national levels. Complementing this, the One Health High-Level Expert Panel (OHHLEP), convened by the Quadripartite in 2019 and expanded with UNEP involvement, provides independent scientific assessments on disease emergence and ecosystem health, with proposed panel members announced in March 2024.
Beyond the Quadripartite, the Global One Health Community platform fosters broader stakeholder engagement, including governments, NGOs, and academia, under Quadripartite auspices to accelerate collaborative research and policy alignment. These efforts emphasize empirical surveillance data from zoonotic outbreaks, such as those analyzed post-2020, to prioritize interventions, though challenges persist in equitable resource distribution across low-income regions.

National and Regional Entities

In the United States, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) established its One Health Office in 2010 to coordinate domestic and global efforts integrating human, animal, and environmental health surveillance, particularly for zoonotic diseases and antimicrobial resistance. This office, directed by Barton Behravesh as of 2025, collaborates with federal partners including the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) and Department of the Interior (DOI) to implement surveillance programs and outbreak responses. On January 10, 2025, the U.S. government released the first National One Health Framework for 2025-2029, uniting representatives from 24 federal agencies to enhance coordination against zoonotic threats through goals in prevention, preparedness, and response.
In the European Union, regional One Health coordination occurs through the European Commission's initiatives, supported by the One Health High Level Expert Panel (OHHLEP), which advises on integrated health policies balancing human, animal, and ecosystem outcomes. Five EU agenciesEuropean Centre for Disease Prevention and Control (ECDC), European Chemicals Agency (ECHA), European Environment Agency (EEA), European Food Safety Authority (EFSA), and European Medicines Agency (EMA)collaborate on cross-cutting issues like antimicrobial resistance and emerging infectious diseases via joint scientific opinions and data-sharing protocols established since 2015. The One Health European Joint Programme (OHEJP), funded from 2018 to 2022, involved 43 institutions across 19 countries to advance research on foodborne zoonoses, antimicrobial resistance, and emerging threats through harmonized surveillance and modeling. In the WHO European Region, which spans 53 countries, One Health implementation advanced via a 2024 joint action plan emphasizing vulnerability assessments at human-animal-environment interfaces.
Nationally in Europe, examples include the United Kingdom's One Health framework under the Animal and Plant Health Agency (APHA) and Environment Agency, focusing on post-Brexit surveillance for diseases like avian influenza, though integration remains challenged by siloed sectors. In Africa, the African Union's Africa CDC supports regional One Health hubs, such as the Afghan One Health Hub model adapted for zoonotic risk mapping, but national programs vary widely due to resource constraints. These entities demonstrate varying degrees of operational success, with U.S. and EU programs benefiting from robust funding and inter-agency mandates, while others rely on international aid for scalability.

Practical Applications

Disease Surveillance and Response Case Studies

The PREDICT project, implemented by USAID from 2009 to 2019, exemplified One Health integration in zoonotic disease surveillance by training wildlife professionals, establishing field labs, and fostering cross-sectoral data sharing across 30 countries to detect potential spillover pathogens before human emergence. In a 2014 Ebola virus disease outbreak in the Democratic Republic of Congo, PREDICT-supported laboratory testing at the Institut National de Recherche Biomdicale identified the virus within one day, confirming a distinct Zaire ebolavirus strain; this enabled swift deployment of contact tracing, mobile laboratories, and quarantine measures, limiting the outbreak to 66 cases over two months.
Similarly, PREDICT's emphasis on wildlife monitoring prevented human transmission in a 2012 yellow fever outbreak in Bolivia, where trained sanctuary staff reported six dead howler monkeys (Alouatta spp.), prompting laboratory confirmation of yellow fever virus; authorities responded within one week with targeted vaccinations, public education on mosquito avoidance, and vector control, resulting in no reported human cases.
Hospital-based and ecological surveillance for Nipah virus in Bangladesh, initiated after the 2001 outbreak and refined through One Health collaborations involving human clinicians, veterinarians, and bat ecologists, identified Indian flying foxes (Pteropus giganteus) as the reservoir and raw date palm sap consumption as a primary spillover pathway during winter months. From April 2001 to December 2021, this system documented 322 human cases (241 confirmed, 81 probable), with annual outbreaks typically clustered in 10-15 districts; interventions such as bamboo skirts on sap collection trees and public awareness campaigns reduced transmission risks, though case fatality remained high at around 75% due to limited therapeutics. Cluster-based enhancements, integrating syndromic alerts from rural hospitals with bat sampling, improved early detection of stage III (widespread community transmission) outbreaks, enabling targeted quarantines and contact tracing.

Food Safety and Ecosystem Management Examples

In food safety, One Health frameworks have been instrumental in controlling brucellosis transmission through unpasteurized dairy and undercooked meat from infected livestock, with integrated veterinary vaccination campaigns, herd testing, and pasteurization mandates leading to virtual elimination of the disease in humans across many countries since the mid-20th century. For instance, in regions where Brucella persists, such as parts of the Middle East and Africa, coordinated efforts tracking prevalence in cattle, sheep, and goats alongside human case reporting have reduced incidence by targeting animal reservoirs, demonstrating causal links between animal health interventions and decreased foodborne human infections.
Another key application involves non-typhoidal Salmonella in poultry production, where One Health surveillance spans farm environments, animal flocks, processing, and human outbreaks to address vertical transmission from hens to eggs. In the European Union, post-1990s interventions including flock vaccination, biosecurity enhancements, and genomic tracing of strains like Salmonella Enteritidis have cut human cases by over 50% from peak levels, with empirical data showing reduced egg contamination rates from 10-20% in infected flocks to near zero through these multi-sectoral measures.
In ecosystem management, One Health strategies mitigate zoonotic risks at wildlife-livestock-human interfaces, such as highly pathogenic avian influenza (HPAI) H5N1, where wild migratory waterfowl serve as reservoirs and ecosystem disruptions like wetland loss amplify spillover to poultry. Surveillance integrating environmental monitoring of bird migration patterns with veterinary controls has informed risk-based culling and habitat zoning; for example, Hong Kong's 1997 response combined market closures, poultry vaccination, and wild bird tracking to avert sustained human-to-human transmission, influencing global protocols that have contained subsequent waves despite ongoing wildlife circulation.
For Nipah virus in South Asia, ecosystem-focused One Health interventions target bat-human contact in deforested areas, where fruit bats roost near agricultural sites and contaminate food sources like date palm sap. In Bangladesh, community-level practices such as installing protective bamboo barriers over sap collection potsguided by ecological studies of bat foraginghave reduced human exposures, with serosurveillance in bats, pigs, and humans since the early 2000s enabling early detection and limiting outbreaks to seasonal clusters rather than epidemics. These examples underscore how habitat preservation and cross-sectoral data sharing causally interrupt transmission chains, though challenges persist in scaling amid climate-driven habitat shifts.

Achievements and Empirical Outcomes

Documented Successes

One Health initiatives have achieved notable reductions in rabies incidence through integrated vaccination, surveillance, and intersectoral collaboration. In Tanzania, a project initiated in 2008 vaccinated approximately 400,000 dogs across 23 districts, attaining 70% coverage and resulting in a 90% decrease in rabies cases. Similarly, Sri Lanka's sustained mass dog vaccination and animal birth control programs, implemented over decades, lowered human rabies deaths to fewer than 50 in 2012. In Bhutan, coordinated efforts between 2006 and 2016 limited confirmed rabies cases to 17, supported by surveillance and population management. Bangladesh reported a decline in human fatalities from 1,500 in 2012 to 200 in 2015 via analogous strategies emphasizing dog vaccination and human post-exposure prophylaxis.
One Health rabies training programs under concept notes have integrated training for veterinarians, physicians, and other professionals to address rabies through intersectoral collaboration, focusing on diagnosis, surveillance, vaccination, and elimination efforts. Examples include the WOAH-WHO Regional Training Workshop on Rabies Diagnosis held in October 2024 in Bengaluru, India, which targeted laboratory professionals from human and animal sectors to strengthen capacity. FAO workshops on rabies elimination in SAARC regions have involved veterinarians and physicians in multisectoral planning and capacity building.
Avian influenza responses have also benefited from One Health frameworks. Nigeria's handling of the 2006 H5N1 outbreak established a National Technical Committee on Avian Influenza, fostering communication between health and agriculture ministries and leveraging $50 million in World Bank funding to contain spread and strengthen preparedness. In Ecuador, a 2024 collaborative investigation into the first human H5N1 case involved multisectoral partners, leading to the patient's recovery without secondary transmissions.
Other zoonoses illustrate comparable outcomes. Uganda's Coordinating Office for the Control of Trypanosomiasis, formed in 1992, curtailed human African trypanosomiasis epidemics through permanent interministerial coordination under the Ministry of Agriculture. For Hendra virus in Australia, a government-led surveillance system and horse vaccine development confined human infections to seven cases from 1995 to 2023, despite near-total horse mortality in outbreaks. Middle East respiratory syndrome coronavirus (MERS-CoV) surveillance in affected Arab countries, bolstered by a 2015 FAO-WHO-OIE workshop, yielded zero reported cases as of June 2023. These cases underscore the causal role of unified human-animal-environmental interventions in mitigating zoonotic threats, with empirical metrics tied to cross-sectoral execution rather than isolated sectoral actions.

Quantifiable Impacts and Metrics

These benefits often stemmed from cross-sectoral collaboration reducing duplication in diagnostics and response efforts, though methodological diversity across studies limits direct comparability.
The World Bank has estimated that proactive One Health investments in surveillance and prevention could yield global annual savings of up to $6 billion by averting outbreaks of zoonotic diseases like avian influenza and Ebola. Similarly, analyses suggest a return of approximately $5 for every $1 invested in One Health strategies, with a proposed global investment of $25 billion potentially preventing economic losses from pandemics and endemic threats.
In rabies control, One Health approaches integrating animal vaccination, human post-exposure prophylaxis, and surveillance have demonstrated measurable reductions in human mortality. For instance, in Goa, India, a data-driven program from 2013 to 2019 achieved zero human rabies deaths by 2019 through coordinated dog vaccination (covering over 70% of the dog population) and enhanced reporting, eliminating the disease in humans while reducing animal cases by 95%. Challenges and Criticisms

Implementation Barriers

Implementation of the One Health approach encounters significant structural and operational hurdles, primarily stemming from entrenched sectoral silos that hinder inter-agency collaboration across human health, veterinary, and environmental domains. These silos arise from differing professional priorities, jurisdictional conflicts, and top-down funding mechanisms that discourage transdisciplinary integration, as evidenced in analyses of global health governance where human medicine and animal health sectors operate with minimal overlap. In low- and middle-income countries, such barriers are exacerbated by policy enforcement gaps and limited farmer education, impeding practical adoption in zoonotic disease hotspots.
Resource constraints represent a core impediment, with chronic shortages of financial, human, and logistical support undermining coordinated surveillance and response efforts. Studies highlight that inadequate funding restricts infrastructure development, such as data-sharing platforms, while insufficient trained personnel hampers capacity building; for instance, a 2023 scoping review of African zoonoses control identified weak governance and resource deficits as primary blockers to strategy execution. Bureaucratic inertia further delays progress, as agencies prioritize siloed mandates over unified One Health platforms, with top-down funding frameworks reinforcing these divisions rather than fostering joint initiatives.
Data management and sharing pose additional technical barriers, including unreliable surveillance systems and insensitive detection mechanisms that limit early zoonotic threat identification. Cross-sectoral data silos, often perpetuated by isolated agency databases, prevent comprehensive analysis, as seen in U.S. state-level assessments where fragmented information flows obstruct integrated health-housing responses analogous to One Health needs. Moreover, the absence of standardized metrics for evaluating One Health outcomes complicates accountability, with governance structures lacking clear roles and goals, leading to uneven implementation across regions.
Political and institutional challenges, including insufficient leadership and policy coherence, further stall advancement, particularly in resource-limited settings where national coordination mechanisms are underdeveloped. A 2024 review noted that without sustained cross-sectoral collaboration independent of short-term donor funding, One Health initiatives risk perceptions of being donor-driven rather than embedded in national priorities. These barriers collectively demand targeted reforms in governance and resource allocation to realize the approach's potential, though empirical evidence of scalable solutions remains limited.

Scientific and Evidentiary Shortcomings

Critics have noted a scarcity of rigorous, standardized evaluations demonstrating the added value of One Health approaches over traditional siloed methods. A systematic review of 1839 unique publications found that only seven reported quantitative outcomes from implemented interventions, with most claimed benefits relying on modeled projections rather than empirical data from real-world applications. This paucity of standardized evaluation frameworks limits the ability to build a robust evidence base for decision-making, as assessments often lack shared methodologies and focus on intermediate rather than final health outcomes.
Theoretical ambiguities further undermine the scientific coherence of One Health. The paradigm fails to resolve "whose health" is prioritized, as humans and animals operate at individual or population levels while ecosystems function at broader scales, leading to conceptual inconsistencies that complicate evidence integration across domains. Similarly, no unified concept of healthsuch as well-being, natural functioning, or resilienceapplies uniformly to humans, animals, and environments, resulting in inconsistent definitions that weaken the validity of cross-disciplinary studies and policy recommendations.
Evidentiary gaps extend to specific interventions, such as zoonotic disease control measures like animal culling, where policies often proceed without sufficient data on cost-effectiveness, proportionality, or long-term impacts. The absence of normative ethical frameworks exacerbates this, as value conflicts (e.g., balancing human safety against animal welfare) are not systematically addressed, hindering reproducible and unbiased evidence generation. Overall, these shortcomings highlight how One Health's holistic aspirations outpace its empirical substantiation, with effectiveness frequently assumed subjectively rather than verified through controlled, longitudinal studies.

Political and Economic Critiques

Critics of the One Health approach argue that its global governance structures exacerbate power imbalances between dominant stakeholders in human medicine, veterinary science, and environmental sectors, often favoring international organizations over national priorities. For instance, recent high-level political attention has intensified tensions, with human health entities seeking primacy amid sectoral silos and institutional inefficiencies. In 2024, 68 civil society organizations urged rejection of One Health provisions in the proposed WHO pandemic agreement, contending that they would impose supranational standards on domestic animal health, agriculture, and environmental policies without adequate national consent, potentially undermining sovereignty.
Implementation faces political hurdles including insufficient political will and weak governance, particularly in resource-constrained settings where coordination across ministries proves elusive. Some analyses highlight how One Health replicates global power dynamics, with wealthier nations and institutions dictating agendas that overlook local contexts in developing countries, such as India's sub-national challenges in operationalizing the framework. Furthermore, the approach is faulted for sidelining structural drivers like commercial influences on health crises, treating human activities as external rather than integral to ecological systems.
Economically, One Health initiatives demand substantial upfront investments in surveillance, capacity-building, and cross-sectoral coordination, yet funding remains precarious without robust demonstrations of return on investment, especially at national levels. While cost-benefit analyses of specific interventions, such as rabies control, often yield positive outcomese.g., mass dog vaccination combined with bite management proving cost-effective in averting deathsbroader applications reveal gaps in scalable economic modeling, with reliance on mixed methods or indices that may undervalue opportunity costs. Critics note that the prevailing socio-economic model, driven by industrial agriculture and globalization, conflicts with One Health goals, as it prioritizes short-term gains over preventive ecosystem stewardship, potentially diverting resources from immediate human health needs. 
Proponents call for radical shifts in budgetary allocations and innovative financing, but skeptics question the feasibility amid fiscal constraints, arguing that unproven long-term benefits risk inefficient resource allocation in low-income regions where basic silos in health systems persist. Economic evaluations underscore the need for context-specific assessments, as generalized claims of value overlook transaction costs of interdisciplinary collaboration.

Surveillance Systems

Global Frameworks

The Quadripartite collaboration, comprising the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO), World Health Organization (WHO), United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP), and World Organisation for Animal Health (WOAH), forms the cornerstone of global One Health frameworks, coordinating efforts to integrate surveillance across human, animal, plant, and environmental sectors. This alliance evolved from the 2010 Tripartite agreement among WHO, FAO, and WOAH (formerly OIE), which focused on zoonotic diseases and food safety, and expanded in 2021 to include UNEP for enhanced environmental integration.
In October 2022, the Quadripartite launched the One Health Joint Plan of Action (OH JPA) for 20222026, a strategic document committing these organizations to advocate for and support multisectoral One Health implementation worldwide. The OH JPA emphasizes six interdependent action tracks to address health threats, including enhancing One Health capacities in health systems, reducing risks from priority zoonoses, antimicrobial resistance (AMR), and vector-borne diseases, and promoting integrated surveillance and response mechanisms. Specifically, it prioritizes the development of unified data platforms and early warning systems to detect threats at the human-animal-environment interface, with deliverables such as technical guidelines for joint surveillance protocols by 2024.
The framework supports global surveillance through tools like the Quadripartite's One Health Knowledge Nexus, an interactive platform for data sharing and co-learning launched in 2023, and aligns with broader instruments such as the International Health Regulations (2005), which mandate rapid reporting of public health events with potential international spread, incorporating One Health for zoonotic risk assessment. The One Health High-Level Expert Panel (OHHLEP), established by the Quadripartite in 2022 and with proposed members announced in March 2024, provides independent scientific advice on surveillance gaps, prioritizing evidence from field data over modeled projections.
Implementation under these frameworks has involved capacity-building initiatives, such as WHO-led training for over 50 countries on integrated zoonotic surveillance by 2023, though challenges persist in data harmonization across sectors due to varying national priorities. The OH JPA's focus on measurable outcomes, like reducing AMR spread through joint monitoring, underscores its causal emphasis on preventing spillovers via ecosystem monitoring rather than reactive measures alone.

National and Localized Approaches

In the United States, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) coordinates national One Health surveillance through its One Health Office, facilitating collaboration among federal agencies like the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) and Department of the Interior to monitor zoonotic diseases at the human-animal-environment interface. On January 10, 2025, the U.S. government issued the first National One Health Framework for Zoonotic Diseases (2025-2029), which emphasizes enhanced surveillance integration, including real-time data sharing across sectors to detect and respond to threats like avian influenza and antimicrobial resistance.
At the localized level in the U.S., state health departments implement tailored surveillance, such as Michigan's Emerging Infectious Diseases program, which in 2019 detected a human case of swine variant influenza (H1N1v) through routine integration of animal exposure data and human testing. Similarly, the CDC's One Health Harmful Algal Bloom System, launched in 2016, aggregates state-level reports on cyanotoxin exposures affecting humans, animals, and water bodies, with participation expanding to provide national estimates of localized risks.
In Europe, national approaches align with supranational frameworks led by the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) and European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control (ECDC), which produce annual One Health Zoonoses Reports compiling surveillance data from EU member states on pathogens like Salmonella and Campylobacter. These reports, such as the 2023 edition released December 10, 2024, integrate whole-genome sequencing (WGS) from the EFSA-ECDC One Health WGS System to track antimicrobial resistance across food, animal, and human samples. Member states adapt this for national use, with coordinated protocols for zoonotic surveillance established in 2023 to identify emerging risks.
Localized European efforts include country-specific monitoring, such as Germany's integration of veterinary and public health data for bat-borne virus surveillance, contributing to broader EU networks. In non-Western contexts, Vietnam's national One Health platform conducts localized field surveillance, identifying zoonotic viruses like influenza A in bats, pigs, and humans through 2023 sampling in high-risk provinces. These approaches highlight adaptive, ground-level integration but often face gaps in resource-limited settings, as noted in global reviews advocating for standardized local protocols.

Future Directions

Identified Gaps and Research Needs

Despite substantial progress in conceptualizing One Health, significant gaps persist in empirical validation of integrated interventions, particularly in quantifying cross-sectoral causal pathways linking human, animal, and environmental health outcomes. A bibliometric analysis of One Health publications from 2000 to 2023 revealed uneven geographic coverage, with over 70% of research originating from high-income countries in North America and Europe, leaving understudied regions like sub-Saharan Africa and Southeast Asia vulnerable to unaddressed zoonotic risks.
Key research needs include developing standardized metrics for evaluating One Health efficacy, as current frameworks often rely on descriptive case studies rather than longitudinal, controlled data on prevented disease burdens or economic returns. For instance, while zoonotic disease surveillance systems exist, there is a dearth of integrated models assessing how biodiversity lossdriven by habitat fragmentationaffects pathogen spillover rates, with only preliminary evidence from ecosystem modeling studies.
In antimicrobial resistance (AMR), gaps center on environmental reservoirs; climate-induced changes, such as flooding, exacerbate AMR dissemination through wastewater and soil, yet few studies employ One Health approaches to track multi-host transmission dynamics under projected warming scenarios up to 2C by 2050. Operational research is urgently needed on scalable interventions in low-resource settings, where governance silos and funding shortfalls hinder multi-sectoral coordination, as evidenced by implementation reviews in Africa showing persistent barriers in resource mobilization despite policy frameworks. 

Workforce and capacity building: Systematic training programs for interdisciplinary teams are lacking, with calls for global mapping of existing One Health competencies to address shortages in veterinary-public health interfaces.

Data integration technologies: Advanced tools for real-time, cross-domain data sharingsuch as AI-driven platformsare underdeveloped, limiting predictive modeling for emerging threats like vector-borne diseases amplified by deforestation.

Economic and policy evaluations: Prospective studies on cost-benefit analyses of One Health investments versus siloed approaches are sparse, particularly in quantifying avoided pandemics or ecosystem service values.

Addressing these requires prioritizing funding for hypothesis-driven trials over advocacy-driven initiatives, ensuring research causality through randomized designs where feasible.

Potential Risks and Skeptical Perspectives

Critics argue that One Health approaches often instrumentalize nonhuman animals by prioritizing human health protection through measures like mass culling of healthy livestock and wildlife, as seen in Norway's policy of eradicating entire pig herds on farms testing positive for livestock-associated methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (LA-MRSA) to prevent human transmission, regardless of animal welfare considerations. Similar actions occurred during the SARS-CoV-2 pandemic, where millions of mink were culled in Denmark and the Netherlands, and tens of millions of birds in the US from 2022 to 2023 due to avian influenza, framing animals primarily as disease vectors rather than entities with intrinsic moral claims to health. This anthropocentric focus, lacking an explicit ethical framework for balancing human, animal, and environmental interests, perpetuates exploitation in practices like industrial farming, which One Health policies may safeguard instead of reforming.
Surveillance systems integral to One Health raise concerns over data privacy and potential misuse, as integrated monitoring of human, animal, and environmental health data encounters barriers from fears of commercial exploitation, blame attribution, and unauthorized access. For instance, sharing veterinary and public health data across sectors risks cyber threats or discriminatory applications, with studies highlighting reluctance among stakeholders due to perceived vulnerabilities in data governance. Critics note that without robust protections, such systems could enable overreach, echoing broader digital epidemiology issues where privacy-by-design falls short amid rapid implementation pressures.
Economically, One Health initiatives impose burdens on agriculture through regulatory compliance and outbreak responses, such as productivity losses from antimicrobial resistance controls and culling mandates that disrupt farming operations without always yielding proportional benefits. Estimates suggest annual investments of up to $3 billion globally for disease surveillance and control, potentially straining low-resource sectors like smallholder farming in zoonotic hotspots. Skeptics question the approach's universality, arguing it overlooks structural constraints in resource-limited settings, such as sub-national coordination failures in biodiversity-rich areas like India's Himalayan regions, where intersectoral silos persist despite advocacy.
From a skeptical standpoint, One Health's ambiguous definitions function as a "boundary object" that facilitates collaboration but conceals normative conflicts, allowing human-centric interpretations to dominate without resolving moral trade-offs in zoonotic control. Operational critiques emphasize that while conceptually sound for zoonoses, rigid application falters against non-zoonotic risks or local contexts, potentially diverting resources from targeted interventions. Proponents' emphasis on integration may understate these persistent dilemmas, risking overhyped expectations that undermine evidence-based skepticism toward unproven scalability.
