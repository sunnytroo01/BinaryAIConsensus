Annexin
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Annexins are a superfamily of structurally related, calcium-dependent phospholipid-binding proteins that are ubiquitously expressed across eukaryotic organisms, from protists and plants to animals and fungi, but absent in prokaryotes and yeasts. These proteins play pivotal roles in membrane-associated processes, including organization, trafficking, repair, and signaling, as well as in extracellular functions such as inflammation modulation and fibrinolysis. Comprising over 30,000 members divided into subfamilies (A through E based on organism type), annexins are evolutionarily ancient, originating approximately 1,000 million years ago through gene duplications, and are implicated in diverse physiological and pathological contexts, including cancer, autoimmune diseases, and pathogen interactions.
Structurally, annexins adopt a compact, disc-like core domain formed by four (or eight in Annexin A6) conserved repeats, each approximately 70 amino acids long and consisting of five -helices arranged in a right-handed superhelix. This core harbors type II calcium-binding sites (characterized by GxGT...D/E motifs) that enable reversible, calcium-mediated attachment to acidic phospholipids like phosphatidylserine on cellular membranes, with binding affinity influenced by membrane composition factors such as cholesterol. A variable N-terminal domain, unique to each annexin, often interacts with cytosolic partners like actin, S100 proteins, or RNAs, allowing functional diversification while the C-terminal core ensures phospholipid specificity.
Functionally, annexins orchestrate intracellular membrane dynamics, including exocytosis, endocytosis, and vesicle fusion, by bridging membranes and cytoskeletal elements. They contribute to plasma membrane repair by facilitating calcium influx and lipid resealing, as seen with Annexins A1, A2, A5, and A6. Extracellularly, Annexin A1 suppresses inflammation by inhibiting phospholipase A2, while Annexin A2 promotes fibrinolysis through plasminogen activation. Emerging roles include tethering RNA granules to lysosomes (Annexin A11) and aiding plant stress responses, underscoring their versatility in cellular homeostasis and adaptation. Dysregulation of annexins is linked to annexinopathies, including amyotrophic lateral sclerosis via Annexin A11 mutations, highlighting their therapeutic potential in diseases like fibrosis and neurodegeneration.

Biological Overview

Definition and Properties

Annexins are a multigene family of soluble, hydrophilic proteins that bind reversibly to negatively charged phospholipids, such as phosphatidylserine, in the presence of calcium ions (Ca). This calcium-dependent membrane association positions annexins as key regulators of cellular membrane dynamics, allowing them to transduce Ca signals into targeted biochemical responses. Biochemically, they exhibit high specificity for acidic phospholipids in the inner leaflet of plasma membranes and organelle membranes, with binding triggered by physiological elevations in cytosolic Ca concentrations.
The annexin family includes over 1,000 unique members distributed across numerous eukaryotic species, from protists and fungi to plants and vertebrates, reflecting an ancient evolutionary origin. These proteins share conserved sequence motifs, particularly in their core regions, that enable Ca coordination and phospholipid recognition, ensuring functional conservation despite sequence divergence. This widespread presence underscores their fundamental role in eukaryotic cellular physiology.
In broad biological contexts, annexins participate in membrane-related processes such as vesicle aggregation, fusion, and repair, often serving as Ca sensors that bridge soluble and membrane-bound components of the cell. Their Ca-dependent binding affinity typically features dissociation constants in the micromolar range, facilitating sensitive and reversible activation in response to transient Ca fluxes. The structural basis for this property involves coordinated Ca sites that position protein loops to engage phospholipid headgroups.

Discovery and Nomenclature

The annexins were first identified in the late 1970s through studies on cellular responses to calcium and steroids. In 1979, Flower and Blackwell isolated a protein from steroid-treated rat peritoneal leukocytes, including neutrophils, that inhibited phospholipase A2 activity and prevented arachidonic acid release for prostaglandin synthesis; this protein, initially called lipomodulin and later renamed lipocortin, was recognized as a major glucocorticoid-inducible anti-inflammatory mediator and is now known as Annexin A1. Earlier that decade, in 1978, Creutz and colleagues described synexin, a calcium-dependent protein from bovine adrenal chromaffin cells that promoted the aggregation and fusion of secretory vesicles; this was the first purified annexin, later classified as Annexin A7.
During the 1980s, additional annexin family members were discovered and characterized under various names reflecting their biochemical properties, leading to a proliferation of terminology. For instance, Annexin A1 (lipocortin) was confirmed as a glucocorticoid-inducible protein comprising up to 4% of cytosolic protein in human neutrophils, while others like calpactins (e.g., Annexin A2) were identified as substrates for tyrosine kinases involved in actin binding, and endonexins (e.g., Annexin A6) were noted for intracellular membrane associations. By the early 1990s, molecular cloning and sequencing efforts had expanded the known family to at least 10 human forms, revealing their evolutionary conservation and shared calcium-dependent phospholipid-binding motifs.
The nomenclature evolved to address the confusion from disparate names such as lipocortins, calpactins, endonexins, chromobindins, and calcimedins. In 1990, an international subcommittee, led by Crumpton and Dedman, proposed the unified term "annexin" (derived from the Latin "annexare," meaning to bind or attach), assigning Roman numeral suffixes based on sequence homology and phylogenetic analysis (e.g., Annexin A1 for the former lipocortin I). This system grouped vertebrate annexins into the A subfamily, with further subfamilies (BE) for non-vertebrate members identified later. By 2002, over 160 annexin proteins had been cataloged across more than 65 species through genomic and proteomic surveys. Since then, while additional orthologs have been annotated in emerging genomes, no major new subfamilies have been established post-2020, with research emphasizing refined classifications and functional refinements within the existing framework.

Molecular Structure

Core Domain Architecture

The core domain of annexins constitutes a highly conserved structural module approximately 310 amino acids in length, forming the foundation for their calcium-dependent interactions with cellular membranes. This domain is characterized by four homologous repeats, each comprising about 70 residues, which fold into a compact, slightly curved, disc-like architecture. Each repeat consists of five alpha-helices (labeled A to E), arranged in a characteristic pattern that packs together to create the overall tertiary structure.
The repeats assemble into a right-handed superhelix, with the alpha-helices forming the backbone of this fold and exposing concave and convex surfaces. The convex surface bears the calcium-binding sites, while the concave side features a central hydrophilic pore; upon calcium binding, hydrophobic edges on the repeat peripheries become exposed, facilitating insertion into phospholipid bilayers. This structural arrangement enables the core's role in membrane association, as the calcium-induced conformational changes position the protein optimally for phospholipid interactions.
Central to the core's function are the type II calcium-binding sites, numbering five to eight per molecule depending on the annexin isoform, located primarily in the AB loops of repeats I, II, and IV (with additional sites in some cases). These sites exhibit a novel coordination geometry, where each Ca ion is typically hepta-coordinated (coordination number 7-8) by oxygen atoms from three main-chain carbonyl groups, a bidentate carboxylate from an aspartate or glutamate side chain, and up to three water molecules, forming a pentagonal bipyramidal arrangement. This binding motif, unique to annexins, allows for high-affinity calcium coordination at micromolar concentrations, triggering the membrane-binding capability.
Sequence conservation within the core domain is striking across the annexin family, with greater than 50% identity among vertebrate orthologs, particularly in the repeat motifs and calcium-coordinating residues that preserve the structural fold and functional sites. This high degree of conservation underscores the core's evolutionary stability and its essential role in the protein family's shared biochemical properties.

Variable N-Terminal Region

The variable N-terminal region of annexins, also known as the head domain, exhibits significant length variation across family members, typically ranging from 14 to 198 amino acid residues, with examples including 16 residues in annexin A3 and approximately 200 in annexin A11. This domain is often unstructured or adopts flexible conformations, such as an amphipathic -helix in the calcium-free state of annexin A1, and extends along the concave surface of the conserved core domain, facilitating regulatory interactions without directly participating in phospholipid binding.
Functionally, the N-terminal region imparts diversity to annexin activities by serving as a regulatory tail that modulates protein localization, stability, and interactions in response to cellular signals. It contains multiple phosphorylation sites targeted by kinases such as protein kinase C (PKC) and epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR), which alter annexin function; for instance, PKC phosphorylates serine 27 in annexin A1 to promote its membrane association, while EGFR phosphorylates tyrosine 21 in the same protein to induce proteolytic cleavage and regulate phospholipase A2 activity. In annexin A1, this domain is particularly critical for anti-inflammatory signaling, where N-terminal peptides like Ac2-26 bind formyl peptide receptors (FPRs) on immune cells, activating JAK/STAT3/SOCS3 pathways to suppress pro-inflammatory cytokine production such as IL-6 and TNF-.
The sequence and length of the N-terminal tail determine specificity in protein-protein interactions, enabling subfamily-specific adaptations; for example, it mediates binding to S100 proteins, such as S100A11 for annexin A1 and S100A10 for annexin A2, which form heterotetramers to enhance membrane aggregation, and to cytoskeletal elements like F-actin in annexin A1 to support vesicle trafficking. Evolutionarily, this hypervariable region has diverged to allow functional specialization among annexin subfamilies, contrasting the conserved core and contributing to their roles in diverse physiological processes across vertebrates.

Cellular Localization

Membrane Association

Annexins primarily associate with cellular membranes in a calcium-dependent manner, exhibiting a strong preference for anionic phospholipids such as phosphatidylserine (PS) and phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PI(4,5)P) enriched on the inner leaflet of the plasma membrane. This specificity arises from the convex side of the annexin core domain, which coordinates Ca ions to insert into phospholipid headgroups, enabling high-affinity binding at micromolar Ca concentrations. Upon membrane contact, annexins frequently undergo trimeric or oligomeric assembly, which amplifies their membrane adhesion and promotes the formation of two-dimensional lattices that organize lipid domains.
The membrane association of annexins is characterized by rapid and reversible dynamics tightly coupled to intracellular Ca fluctuations. Binding occurs swiftly upon Ca elevation, while dissociation follows promptly upon Ca removal, depending on the annexin isoform and lipid composition. This reversibility allows annexins to act as sensors of transient Ca signals, translocating from the cytosol to membranes and back without permanent sequestration.
In physiological contexts, annexins target the plasma membrane during wounding or Ca-mediated signaling, where they contribute to membrane repair by aggregating at injury sites to seal breaches and restore integrity. For instance, upon mechanical disruption, Ca influx triggers annexin recruitment to form a repair cap that bridges the lesion through oligomeric scaffolding and induced membrane curvature. Fluorescence microscopy studies have visualized this process, showing annexin translocation to the plasma membrane wound within 1-3 seconds of Ca elevation, highlighting the speed essential for effective repair.

Nuclear and Cytosolic Distribution

Annexins exhibit a dynamic distribution between the nucleus and cytosol, with specific family members demonstrating distinct localization patterns. Annexin A1 (ANXA1) and Annexin A11 (ANXA11) are notable for their presence in the nucleus, where they contribute to various cellular processes. ANXA1 localizes to the nucleus through a non-classical nuclear localization signal (NLS) within its repeat domain III, facilitating its translocation under specific conditions. Similarly, ANXA11 relies on its long N-terminal tail domain, which functions as an NLS to direct the protein to the nucleus, independent of typical classical signals.
In resting cells, the majority of annexins exist as a soluble pool in the cytosol, serving as a reservoir for rapid recruitment to membranes or other cellular compartments upon stimulation. This cytosolic fraction maintains annexins in a non-bound state at low calcium levels, allowing for quick responses to cellular signals. ANXA11, in particular, participates in nuclear RNA processing and granule formation, linking it to events such as mitosis and stress responses within the nucleus.
Nuclear import of annexins occurs via calcium-independent mechanisms involving importins, contrasting with their calcium-dependent membrane binding. For instance, ANXA1's nuclear translocation is mediated by the importin / pathway, as inhibition of this system blocks its accumulation in the nucleus. Under stress conditions, such as oxidative stress or ischemia, annexins like ANXA1 and ANXA2 show enhanced nuclear accumulation; ANXA1 translocates in response to heat, oxidative insults, or oxygen-glucose deprivation, potentially aiding in DNA repair and anti-apoptotic functions. While primarily acting as reservoirs, the nuclear presence of annexins hints at roles in gene regulation, such as ANXA1's interaction with p53 to modulate transcriptional activity, though these functions require further elucidation.

Extracellular and Tissue-Specific Sites

Annexins are released into the extracellular space through non-classical secretion pathways, including leaderless secretion mediated by ABC transporters such as ABCA1 and ABCC1, which facilitate their translocation across the plasma membrane without a signal peptide. In particular, Annexin A1 (ANXA1) is externalized via these transporters in response to stimuli like glucocorticoids, enabling its anti-inflammatory actions outside the cell. ANXA1 is also incorporated into extracellular vesicles (EVs), such as microvesicles and exosomes, derived from various cell types including intestinal epithelial cells and neutrophils, where it promotes epithelial wound repair and modulates inflammation.
In bone tissue, Annexin A5 (ANXA5) and Annexin A6 (ANXA6) are enriched in matrix vesicles, small extracellular membrane-bound structures that initiate mineralization during endochondral ossification. ANXA5 enhances matrix vesicle adhesion to the extracellular matrix via interactions with collagen type I, thereby facilitating hydroxyapatite deposition and protecting against osteoporotic bone loss, as demonstrated in murine models where ANXA5 overexpression increased bone mineral density. ANXA6 similarly contributes to this process by regulating calcium influx in hypertrophic chondrocytes, though depletion of both ANXA5 and ANXA6 does not grossly impair overall mineralization in growth plates. Annexins are also present in cartilage, where ANXA5 acts as a collagen receptor on chondrocytes, supporting matrix organization and mineralization in the hypertrophic zone.
In atherosclerotic plaques, annexins localize to specific vascular sites; for instance, ANXA1 is expressed in macrophages and endothelial cells within human coronary and carotid plaques, potentially stabilizing lesions in asymptomatic patients. ANXA5 accumulates at sites of apoptosis in plaques, aiding in the detection of phosphatidylserine exposure, while ANXA6 is associated with calcification foci.
A prominent example of extracellular annexin localization involves ANXA5 binding to phosphatidylserine on the outer leaflet of apoptotic cell membranes, including in blebs formed during early apoptosis, which enables its use as a probe for detecting cell death via flow cytometry.
In pathological contexts like arthritis, annexins such as ANXA1 are elevated in synovial fluid, often within neutrophil-derived microvesicles that protect cartilage by interacting with formyl peptide receptor 2 on chondrocytes to reduce pro-inflammatory mediators.

Physiological Functions

Vesicle Trafficking Mechanisms

Annexins play critical roles in vesicle trafficking, particularly in the processes of exocytosis and endocytosis, where they facilitate vesicle fusion and sorting in a calcium-dependent manner. In exocytosis, annexin A2 promotes the fusion of chromaffin granules in adrenal chromaffin cells, a model system for regulated secretion. Studies using isolated chromaffin granule membranes have demonstrated that phosphorylated annexin A2, activated by protein kinase C, induces granule aggregation followed by membrane fusion, as observed through electron microscopy after 12-15 minutes of incubation, leading to 80-90% fusion efficiency under optimal conditions. Similarly, annexin A7 contributes to regulated secretion by interacting with SNARE proteins, such as SNAP23, in alveolar type II cells, where these interactions are modulated by calcium and protein phosphorylation to coordinate surfactant release during lung secretion.
In endocytosis, annexin A1 is essential for phagosome formation in macrophages and other phagocytic cells, acting as a linker between actin filaments and phagosomal membranes to drive particle engulfment. Downregulation of annexin A1 impairs actin accumulation at phagocytic cups and reduces phagocytosis efficiency, highlighting its role in coordinating cytoskeletal dynamics with membrane invagination during uptake. Annexin A2 further supports endocytic processes by organizing clathrin-coated pits, where it associates with clathrin and actin to facilitate vesicle budding from the plasma membrane. This involvement is evident in neuroendocrine cells, where annexin A2 localizes to clathrin-coated vesicles, aiding in the internalization of membrane components post-exocytosis.
The mechanisms underlying these roles involve annexins bridging vesicles to target membranes and serving as calcium sensors to time trafficking events. Annexins bind to negatively charged phospholipids on vesicle and plasma membranes in a calcium-dependent fashion, promoting aggregation and fusion by stabilizing close membrane appositions. In vitro assays with isolated vesicles and liposomes have shown that annexins enhance fusion rates by 2- to 5-fold compared to controls, underscoring their catalytic role in overcoming energy barriers for membrane merger. This calcium sensitivity ensures precise temporal control, as rising intracellular calcium levels trigger annexin recruitment and activation during stimulated secretion or engulfment.

Membrane Scaffolding and Organization

Annexins contribute to membrane scaffolding by forming ordered two-dimensional (2D) lattices on phospholipid surfaces, particularly in the presence of calcium ions. For instance, Annexin A5 oligomerizes into trimers and higher-order hexameric structures that assemble into crystalline arrays on phosphatidylserine-containing membranes, providing structural support that resists mechanical perturbations such as membrane defects during injury repair. These lattices enhance membrane stability by promoting lipid phase separation and gel-like ordering, thereby counteracting local disruptions and maintaining overall membrane integrity under stress.
In membrane organization, specific annexins facilitate the formation and stabilization of specialized lipid domains. Annexin A2, often in complex with S100A10, promotes the assembly of cholesterol-rich lipid rafts by binding to phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate and linking these domains to the actin cytoskeleton, which is essential for organizing membrane microenvironments involved in cellular processes like exocytosis.45228-7/fulltext) Similarly, Annexin A6 associates with caveolae and membrane rafts, where it influences cholesterol distribution and strengthens membrane-cytoskeleton attachments, thereby stabilizing these invaginated structures against dynamic remodeling.
Annexins also sense and respond to membrane curvature, aiding in the structural adaptation during processes like vesicle fusion and fission. Annexin A11, for example, localizes to curved regions such as the midbody during cytokinesis, where it binds preferentially to bent membranes and supports the completion of cell division by stabilizing high-curvature sites. Biophysical studies in model lipid systems demonstrate that annexin binding, particularly by A5, significantly increases membrane rigidityoften by 20-50% through lattice formationaltering bending modulus and reducing fluidity to provide a scaffold for dynamic membrane events.

Signal Transduction Roles

Annexins play crucial roles in modulating cellular signal transduction pathways, particularly through their calcium-dependent interactions with membranes and regulatory proteins. Annexin A1 (ANXA1) acts as a key anti-inflammatory mediator by mimicking the effects of glucocorticoids, which induce its expression to suppress pro-inflammatory signaling. Specifically, ANXA1 binds directly to the p65 subunit of NF-B, inhibiting its nuclear translocation and transcriptional activity, thereby reducing the expression of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-1 and TNF-. Additionally, ANXA1 inhibits phospholipase A2 (PLA2) activity through specific protein-protein interactions, preventing the release of arachidonic acid and subsequent production of pro-inflammatory eicosanoids like prostaglandins and leukotrienes. This dual mechanism positions ANXA1 as a central regulator in resolving acute inflammation by integrating glucocorticoid signaling with downstream inhibitory effects on key inflammatory cascades.
In growth signaling pathways, Annexin A2 (ANXA2) functions as a co-receptor for the epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR), facilitating ligand-induced receptor dimerization and activation at the plasma membrane. Upon EGF binding, ANXA2 enhances EGFR signaling by promoting its endocytosis and sustaining downstream phosphorylation cascades, including activation of MAPK/ERK and PI3K/AKT pathways that drive cell proliferation and migration. This role is amplified by tyrosine phosphorylation of ANXA2 at residue Tyr23, primarily mediated by Src family kinases in response to EGFR activation, which stabilizes the ANXA2-EGFR complex and propagates mitogenic signals. The N-terminal domain of ANXA2, containing these phosphorylation sites, is critical for its integration into these cascades, allowing dynamic regulation of growth factor responses.
Beyond these specific examples, other annexins contribute to signal transduction by regulating ion fluxes and kinase interactions. Annexin A6 (ANXA6) modulates calcium channel activity, forming voltage-dependent Ca channels in phospholipid bilayers and influencing store-operated Ca entry to fine-tune intracellular Ca homeostasis essential for signaling events like PKC activation. ANXA6 also links Ca signaling to cholesterol transport, indirectly affecting receptor tyrosine kinase pathways such as EGFR by altering membrane composition. Furthermore, multiple annexins, including ANXA2, interact with Src kinases; for instance, Src phosphorylates ANXA2 to promote its translocation and enhance signaling from integrins and growth factor receptors, thereby amplifying tyrosine kinase-dependent pathways involved in cytoskeletal remodeling and cell motility.
Emerging research highlights annexins' involvement in mechanotransduction, where they sense and respond to membrane tension changes to transduce mechanical cues into biochemical signals. ANXA6 inhibits rapidly adapting mechanosensitive currents in sensory neurons, potentially by associating with Piezo2 channels to modulate somatosensory responses to mechanical stimuli. Recent studies (post-2020) further implicate ANXA2 in caveolae-mediated mechanotransduction, where it helps buffer plasma membrane tension during mechanical stress, preventing excessive signaling activation and maintaining cellular integrity under shear forces or stretch. These roles underscore annexins' versatility in integrating physical and chemical signals for adaptive cellular responses.

Classification

Subfamilies and Evolutionary Aspects

Annexins are classified into multiple subfamilies based on phylogenetic relationships, sequence homology, and organismal distribution. The subfamily A encompasses vertebrate annexins, including the 12 human members (ANXA1ANXA13), and is the most intensively studied due to its prominence in mammalian systems. Subfamily B is characteristic of invertebrates, such as insects and nematodes, while subfamily C occurs in fungi and select unicellular eukaryotes like Neurospora crassa. Plants feature a distinct subfamily D, and subfamily E is restricted to protozoans, with additional provisional subfamilies identified in diverse eukaryotic lineages, totaling over 40 potential groups.
The evolutionary history of annexins reveals an ancient origin, with the family emerging more than 1.2 billion years ago in early eukaryotic lineages, as evidenced by homologs in protists like Giardia lamblia. Gene duplication events have driven subfamily expansion, particularly in vertebrates, where tandem duplications and whole-genome events during chordate radiation approximately 500600 million years ago gave rise to the diverse A subfamily members from ancestral forms like ANXA13. Plant annexins (subfamily D) likely diverged around 1 billion years ago during the evolution of green algae into land plants.
Sequence conservation is a hallmark of annexins, with the core domaincomprising four approximately 70-amino-acid repeatsexhibiting over 40% identity across eukaryotic species, from slime molds like Dictyostelium discoideum to vertebrates, underscoring its essential role in calcium-dependent phospholipid binding. In contrast, the variable N-terminal domains show marked divergence, facilitating lineage-specific adaptations such as tissue targeting or regulatory interactions. This pattern of conserved core and variable tails reflects evolutionary pressures for functional versatility while maintaining structural integrity.
Genomic and metagenomic analyses have not introduced new formal subfamilies since 2020. Earlier studies have uncovered annexin-like proteins in prokaryotic genomes across bacterial phyla such as Bacteroidetes and Proteobacteria, featuring conserved calcium-binding motifs and up to five domains per protein. These findings suggest a prokaryotic ancestry predating eukaryotic diversification, potentially tracing back to the last universal common ancestor, though horizontal gene transfer remains a debated mechanism for their distribution.

Clinical Significance

Inflammation and Apoptosis

Annexins play critical roles in modulating inflammation, particularly through Annexin A1 (ANXA1), which is externalized on the surface of activated or apoptotic cells to inhibit excessive immune responses. ANXA1 externalization during inflammation limits neutrophil adhesion and migration across endothelial barriers by interacting with formyl peptide receptors (FPRs) on neutrophils, thereby promoting resolution of acute inflammatory events. This interaction desensitizes neutrophils to chemoattractants, reducing transendothelial migration without fully activating downstream signaling pathways like MAP kinase, as demonstrated in studies using N-terminal peptides of ANXA1. Through FPR engagement, particularly FPR2/ALX, ANXA1 released from apoptotic cells dampens monocyte activation by upregulating suppressor of cytokine signaling 3 (SOCS3) via JAK/STAT pathways, which inhibits pro-inflammatory cytokine production such as TNF- in response to endotoxins.
A key mechanism of ANXA1's anti-inflammatory action involves mimicking glucocorticoid effects to suppress pro-inflammatory cytokines. ANXA1 binds to the NF-B p65 subunit, inhibiting its transcriptional activity and thereby reducing the synthesis of cytokines like IL-1 and TNF- in macrophages during inflammatory conditions such as radiation-induced lung injury. This glucocorticoid-like mimicry enhances neutrophil apoptosis and limits cytokine storms, facilitating inflammation resolution. Post-2020 studies have highlighted ANXA1's relevance in viral infections, including COVID-19, where lower serum levels (e.g., 14.8 ng/ml in severe cases versus 25.5 ng/ml in controls) correlate with disease severity and poor resolution of lung inflammation, suggesting its potential as a prognostic biomarker and target for adjunct therapies.
In apoptosis, Annexin A5 (ANXA5) binds with high affinity to phosphatidylserine (PS) exposed on the outer membrane of apoptotic cells in a calcium-dependent manner, serving as a key regulator to prevent secondary inflammatory complications. This binding inhibits the procoagulant and pro-inflammatory activities of dying cells, reducing the risk of secondary necrosis where uncleared apoptotic cells release damaging contents and exacerbate tissue inflammation. ANXA5's role is leveraged in the widely adopted Annexin V assay, a flow cytometric method that uses fluorescein-labeled Annexin V to detect early apoptosis by identifying PS externalization before membrane integrity loss, often combined with propidium iodide to distinguish viable, apoptotic, and necrotic cells.

Coagulation and Fibrinolysis

Annexins play critical roles in regulating coagulation and fibrinolysis, primarily through their interactions with phospholipid surfaces exposed during hemostatic processes. Annexin A5 (ANXA5), also known as annexin V, acts as a potent anticoagulant by binding to phosphatidylserine (PS) on activated platelets and endothelial cells, thereby inhibiting the assembly of the prothrombinase complex. This binding prevents the activation of prothrombin to thrombin, a key step in the coagulation cascade, and reduces thrombus formation under both venous and arterial flow conditions. The core domain of ANXA5, consisting of four homologous repeats that form a concave structure for calcium-dependent phospholipid binding, contains the primary sites that compete with prothrombin for access to PS-rich membranes, effectively shielding procoagulant surfaces. This mechanism not only limits excessive clotting but also maintains vascular homeostasis without significantly increasing bleeding risk.
In fibrinolysis, annexin A2 (ANXA2) facilitates the breakdown of fibrin clots by enhancing tissue plasminogen activator (tPA)-mediated plasminogen activation on the endothelial surface. ANXA2, often in complex with its ligand S100A10, serves as a cell surface receptor that co-localizes tPA and plasminogen, increasing the catalytic efficiency of plasmin generation by up to 60-fold. This profibrinolytic activity promotes clot dissolution and prevents thrombosis in the vascular endothelium, contributing to efficient thrombolysis during physiological remodeling or pathological occlusion. Studies have demonstrated that soluble ANXA2 can amplify tPA activity in vitro, suggesting potential therapeutic applications in combination with low-dose tPA for ischemic conditions.
Annexins exhibit a balanced duality in thrombosis regulation, preventing clot formation under normal conditions while potentially promoting it under hemodynamic stress such as shear forces. While ANXA5 predominantly inhibits thrombosis by blocking procoagulant phospholipid exposure, ANXA2 displays context-dependent effects: its fibrinolysis-enhancing role typically opposes thrombosis, but under disturbed shear stressas in atherosclerosis-prone regionsANXA2 interacts with integrin 5 to drive endothelial inflammation and prothrombotic signaling, exacerbating plaque formation and arterial occlusion. This shear-induced shift highlights annexins' adaptive roles in vascular mechanobiology, where laminar flow maintains antithrombotic protection, but oscillatory or turbulent flow tilts toward pro-thrombotic outcomes.
Clinically, genetic variations in annexins influence thrombotic risks and related disorders. Polymorphisms in the ANXA5 gene, particularly the M2 haplotype involving promoter and 5'-untranslated region variants, are associated with reduced ANXA5 expression and increased risk of recurrent miscarriage, likely due to impaired anticoagulant shielding at the maternal-fetal interface during trophoblast remodeling. Similarly, single nucleotide polymorphisms in the ANXA2 gene have been linked to elevated stroke risk, especially in populations with underlying vascular vulnerabilities like sickle cell disease, where diminished fibrinolysis contributes to ischemic events. These associations underscore annexins' therapeutic potential, such as ANXA5-based anticoagulants for pregnancy-related thrombosis or ANXA2 modulation for stroke prevention.

Roles in Cancer and Neurodegeneration

Annexins play significant roles in cancer pathogenesis, particularly through their involvement in tumor suppression and promotion of metastatic processes. Annexin A1 (ANXA1) functions as a tumor suppressor gene, where its transcriptional silencing via microRNA-196a-mediated downregulation contributes to the progression of leukemias, such as acute myeloid leukemia, by enhancing cell proliferation and survival. In contrast, Annexin A2 (ANXA2) promotes cancer metastasis by facilitating tumor cell invasion; elevated ANXA2 expression enhances extracellular matrix degradation through plasmin activation, enabling migratory behavior in various solid tumors including breast and gastric cancers. This dual functionality underscores the context-dependent effects of annexins in oncology, where ANXA1 loss correlates with poor prognosis in hematologic malignancies, while ANXA2 overexpression is associated with advanced disease stages and therapeutic resistance.
In neurodegeneration, annexins influence pathological protein aggregation and neuronal survival. Annexin A3 (ANXA3) has been implicated in Alzheimer's disease (AD) through altered expression levels that correlate with amyloid-beta (A) pathology, potentially contributing to microglial activation and exacerbated A aggregation in affected brain regions. Suppression of Annexin A5 (ANXA5) has been shown to exert neuroprotective effects in Parkinson's disease (PD) models by stimulating the ERK signaling pathway to reduce oxidative stress and apoptosis in dopaminergic neurons exposed to 6-hydroxydopamine toxicity. These mechanisms highlight annexins' involvement in calcium-dependent processes that mitigate or aggravate neurodegenerative cascades.
Annexins also contribute to membrane repair in stressed neurons, a critical process in neurodegeneration where calcium influx triggers annexin recruitment to reseal plasma membrane lesions caused by A or alpha-synuclein aggregates. For instance, Annexin A6 facilitates repair of amyloid-induced dystrophic neurites in AD mouse models, preventing tau hyperphosphorylation and neuronal loss. In clinical contexts, ANXA2 serves as a diagnostic biomarker for gliomas, with high expression levels indicating aggressive tumor phenotypes and correlating with immune infiltration patterns for prognostic assessment.
Recent research from 2021 to 2025 has explored therapeutic applications, including ANXA1 mimetic peptides like IF7, which bind to ANXA1 on glioblastoma vasculature to enhance drug delivery across the blood-brain barrier, improving targeted therapy efficacy in preclinical models. Additionally, mutations in Annexin A11 (ANXA11) link to amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) via calcium homeostasis dysregulation, impairing stress granule dynamics and exacerbating motor neuron degeneration through disrupted membrane tethering. These findings position annexins as promising targets for disease-modifying interventions in both cancer and neurodegeneration.
