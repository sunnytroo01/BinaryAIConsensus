Ventromedial prefrontal cortex
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) is a region of the brain located on the ventral aspect of the medial prefrontal cortex, occupying the inferior frontal lobe and encompassing Brodmann areas 10 (medial portions), 11, 12, 13, 14, 25, and 32. This area integrates cognitive, emotional, and autonomic signals to support core functions such as value-based decision-making, emotion regulation, and social cognition. The vmPFC's subregions exhibit distinct cytoarchitectonic and connectivity profiles, with posterior portions (e.g., Brodmann area 25) associated with negative affect and fear processing, while more anterior perigenual areas (e.g., ventral Brodmann area 32) link to positive affect and reward valuation.
The vmPFC maintains extensive reciprocal connections with subcortical structures, including the amygdala for emotional salience, the ventral striatum for reward processing, the hypothalamus for autonomic regulation, and the hippocampus for memory integration. These pathways enable the vmPFC to modulate fear extinction by inhibiting amygdala activity, as demonstrated in rodent models where infralimbic cortex (homologous to human Brodmann area 25) projections suppress conditioned fear responses. In decision-making, the vmPFC encodes subjective values of options, facilitating choices that balance immediate rewards against long-term consequences; human neuroimaging studies show its activation during tasks requiring economic trade-offs.
Lesion studies in humans reveal the vmPFC's necessity for adaptive social and emotional behavior, with damage often resulting in preserved intellect but profound deficits in empathy, moral reasoning, and real-world decision-making, such as poor performance on the Iowa Gambling Task and increased risk-taking. For instance, patients with vmPFC lesions exhibit blunted physiological responses to emotional stimuli and altered utilitarian judgments in moral dilemmas. Dysfunctions in the vmPFC are implicated in psychiatric conditions like depression, anxiety, and addiction, where aberrant connectivity disrupts emotion processing and reward sensitivity.

Anatomy

Location and boundaries

The ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) constitutes the medial portion of the prefrontal cortex situated on the ventral surface of the frontal lobe. It encompasses several Brodmann areas, including 10 (medial frontal), 11 (orbitofrontal), 12 (orbitofrontal), 13 (orbito-insular), 14 (orbitofrontal), and 25 (subgenual). This region lies below the anterior cingulate cortex and forms a key part of the medial prefrontal cortex.
The vmPFC is delimited anteriorly by the frontal pole and posteriorly by the anterior cingulate cortex. Laterally, it borders the orbitofrontal cortex, while its inferior extent is marked by the olfactory sulcus, which separates it from the gyrus rectus. Superiorly, the vmPFC extends into the medial frontal gyrus, bounded by the cingulate sulcus.
In terms of sulcal relations, the vmPFC is primarily enclosed by the medial and lateral orbital sulci, which define its ventral and lateral margins on the orbital surface. These sulci, along with the olfactory sulcus, contribute to its demarcation from adjacent orbital regions.
Historically, the vmPFC has been referred to with variations such as the subcallosal prefrontal cortex, medial orbitofrontal cortex, and components of the broader medial prefrontal cortex, reflecting differences in anatomical delineations across studies. These naming conventions highlight its transitional position between orbital and medial frontal structures, with boundaries often adjusted based on cytoarchitectonic or imaging criteria.

Subdivisions

The ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) is characterized by distinct cytoarchitectonic subdivisions that reflect its internal structural organization. Major subregions include the anterior vmPFC, encompassing Brodmann areas 10 (frontopolar) and 11 (medial orbital), which occupy the rostral portion; the posterior vmPFC, comprising Brodmann areas 13 and 14 (polar agranular and medial orbital), located more caudally; the subgenual cingulate, corresponding to Brodmann area 25; and the perigenual anterior cingulate, corresponding to ventral Brodmann area 32. These divisions are delineated based on variations in laminar structure and cellular density observed in histological analyses of human postmortem tissue.
Cytoarchitectonically, the vmPFC consists primarily of agranular and dysgranular cortices, where layer IVthe granular input layeris notably reduced or absent, distinguishing it from more granular prefrontal regions. Agranular features predominate in anterior subregions like areas 10 and 11, with sparse cellular packing and prominent layer V pyramidal cells, while dysgranular characteristics appear in posterior areas 13 and 14, showing a rudimentary layer IV with increased interstitial neurons. Anterior portions, particularly within the perigenual cingulate, exhibit a high density of spindle-shaped von Economo neurons in layer V, which are large, projection-type cells adapted for rapid signaling in social and affective contexts. These features contribute to the region's overall limbic-like architecture, as mapped in seminal comparative studies across primates.
Histological examinations reveal functional heterogeneity along an anterior-posterior gradient, with anterior subregions supporting abstract representational processing due to their agranular composition and sparser lamination, and posterior subregions enabling concrete sensory integration through dysgranular layering and denser connectivity interfaces. This structural dichotomy is evident in Nissl-stained sections, where anterior areas show broader, less differentiated layers suited to higher-order integration, contrasting with the more defined, sensory-oriented posterior zones.
Recent investigations since 2023 have highlighted subregional variations in cortical complexitymeasured via metrics like fractal dimension and gyrification indexwithin the vmPFC, particularly linking anterior subregions to reward valuation. For example, lower cortical complexity in the left vmPFC, extending to orbitofrontal and frontopolar areas, is associated with greater preferences for immediate over delayed rewards. These findings, derived from high-resolution MRI, underscore evolving understandings of subregional specialization without altering core cytoarchitectonic boundaries.

Connectivity

The ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) receives prominent afferent inputs from several limbic and subcortical structures, facilitating the integration of emotional, mnemonic, and reward-related signals. Key inputs originate from the amygdala, providing reciprocal connections that convey emotional and fear-related information via the uncinate fasciculus and stria terminalis pathways. The hippocampus projects directly to the vmPFC through the uncinate fasciculus and fornix, supporting memory-related inputs. Additionally, the nucleus accumbens sends dopaminergic signals to the vmPFC, with projections from its shell targeting the infralimbic subregion and from its core targeting the prelimbic area. Thalamic afferents, particularly from the mediodorsal nucleus, provide sensory relay and cognitive modulation to the vmPFC via reciprocal pathways.
Efferent outputs from the vmPFC extend to autonomic, motor, and interoceptive regions, enabling top-down influence over subcortical processes. Projections to the hypothalamus, primarily from the infralimbic cortex, target the posterior hypothalamus to regulate autonomic responses. The vmPFC sends outputs to the striatum, with the prelimbic region connecting to the dorsal striatum for motor planning integration. Connections to the insula occur through limbic-striatal-pallidal circuits, supporting interoceptive awareness.
Major pathways involving the vmPFC include orbitofrontal-thalamic loops, which link the medial orbitofrontal cortex to the mediodorsal thalamus and back, forming closed circuits for sensory-affective processing. The vmPFC-amygdala projections, often bidirectional, enable top-down modulation via the uncinate fasciculus and cingulum bundle.
Neuroimaging studies, including diffusion tensor imaging (DTI), reveal dense reciprocal connections between the vmPFC and the limbic system, such as the amygdala and hippocampus, with probabilistic tractography showing graded structural patterns along the vmPFC's dorsomedial-ventrolateral axis. For instance, DTI analyses have identified reduced fractional anisotropy in the uncinate fasciculus linking the vmPFC and amygdala in clinical populations, underscoring the pathway's structural integrity. These findings highlight the vmPFC's role in interconnecting emotion and cognition through robust subcortical ties. Subregional differences, such as stronger limbic ties in the medial orbitofrontal versus cingulate portions, further delineate connectivity gradients.

Functions

Value representation and decision-making

The ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) is critically involved in encoding expected value to guide value-based decisions. Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) studies demonstrate that vmPFC activity parametrically scales with the subjective reward magnitude of choice options, integrating factors such as probability, effort, and outcome desirability. For instance, during tasks requiring pairwise comparisons of rewards, vmPFC neurons and BOLD signals reflect the net expected value, facilitating the comparison and selection of higher-value alternatives. This representation supports reinforcement learning mechanisms, where vmPFC tracks value updates based on experienced outcomes.
In risk-related decisions, vmPFC activity modulates responses to potential losses, often showing deactivation during loss anticipation that correlates with risk aversion. This deactivation pattern helps weigh uncertain outcomes against gains, as seen in gambling tasks where reduced vmPFC engagement predicts conservative choices. For intertemporal choices, vmPFC contributes to delay discounting, the tendency to devalue future rewards relative to immediate ones; lesions or disruptions in this region lead to steeper discounting rates, promoting impulsivity in favor of short-term gains.
The vmPFC interacts closely with the orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) to form a distributed network for value integration, with vmPFC specializing in abstract, long-term value representations and the OFC focusing on immediate, sensory-driven rewards. This functional dissociation allows vmPFC to incorporate contextual and delayed outcomes, such as future utilities, while OFC processes primary reinforcers like taste or texture. Neuroimaging evidence highlights their complementary roles, where vmPFC-OFC connectivity strengthens during complex choices requiring temporal abstraction.
Recent research from 2023 to 2025 using noninvasive techniques like transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) has shown that upregulating vmPFC excitability enhances rational decision-making by reducing cognitive biases, such as framing effects in economic tasks. Excitatory tDCS over vmPFC increases adaptive feedback processing and promotes choices aligned with objective values, with effects persisting across sessions. These findings underscore vmPFC's causal role in modulating decision rationality, offering potential therapeutic avenues for bias-related impairments.

Emotion processing and regulation

The ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) plays a central role in top-down emotion regulation by modulating affective responses through inhibitory projections to subcortical structures, particularly the amygdala. During cognitive reappraisal tasks, where individuals reinterpret the meaning of emotional stimuli to reduce negative affect, vmPFC activation correlates with decreased amygdala reactivity, facilitating the downregulation of emotional intensity. This inhibitory mechanism is evident in neuroimaging studies showing that successful reappraisal engages vmPFC-amygdala connectivity to attenuate fear and anxiety responses, promoting adaptive emotional control.
In fear extinction, the posterior vmPFC is essential for the retrieval of context-dependent safety memories that suppress conditioned fear responses. Functional MRI evidence indicates that posterior vmPFC engagement during extinction recall strengthens the inhibition of amygdala-driven fear, allowing for the flexible updating of threat associations based on environmental cues. This subregion's activity supports the consolidation of extinction learning, where safety signals override prior aversive conditioning, as demonstrated in paradigms involving contextual manipulations.
The vmPFC also contributes to implicit emotion processing, particularly through subgenual activation that detects and regulates unconscious emotional biases without deliberate awareness. Studies using multichannel transcranial direct current stimulation targeted at the vmPFC reveal enhanced implicit regulation in anxiety-prone individuals, with subgenual regions modulating automatic affective responses to subliminal stimuli. This process involves vmPFC integration of subtle threat cues, reducing biased emotional reactivity at an unconscious level.
Recent research (20232025) highlights how vmPFC subregions differentially integrate threat and safety signals to enable adaptive emotion regulation. Posterior vmPFC predominantly processes threat omission and basic safety learning, while anterior portions handle more complex protective associations, supporting meta-representations of emotional contexts. These findings, drawn from meta-analyses and functional imaging, underscore the vmPFC's role in balancing aversive and appetitive signals for resilient emotional homeostasis.

Social cognition and prosocial behavior

The ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) plays a key role in social cognition, particularly in affective theory of mind processes that enable individuals to infer others' emotional mental states. Functional neuroimaging studies have shown that the anterior vmPFC activates during tasks involving understanding affective mental states, such as empathy or simulating emotions of similar others. For instance, in a review of neuroimaging literature, mentalizing paradigms engaging affective components consistently activated the medial prefrontal cortex, with anterior regions of the vmPFC implicated in representing mental states of similar individuals. This activation is thought to facilitate intention representation by simulating others' perspectives, with greater vmPFC involvement when reasoning about similar others compared to dissimilar ones.
The vmPFC also contributes to prosocial motivation, underpinning empathy-driven behaviors such as helping others. Lesion studies demonstrate that vmPFC damage impairs the willingness to exert effort for others' benefit, even when it incurs no personal cost, indicating its necessity for prosocial actions. In a 2024 investigation, patients with vmPFC lesions earned fewer rewards for anonymous others in effort-based tasks compared to healthy and lesion-control groups, with voxel-based lesion-symptom mapping implicating the medial vmPFC (Brodmann area 14 and subgenual anterior cingulate cortex) in reduced prosociality. This deficit is particularly evident in empathy-driven helping toward those perceived as similar, as vmPFC patients exhibit diminished responses to others' suffering, relying on emotional cues to guide cooperative actions.
Gender-specific differences emerge in vmPFC processing of social signals, influencing how interpersonal cues are evaluated. Functional MRI research reveals that the vmPFC parametrically encodes subjective value of faces, but males show heightened sensitivity to physical attributes like youthfulness and averagenesskey social signals of attractivenesswhile females do not exhibit this pattern. This asymmetry suggests the vmPFC modulates gender-typical responses to visual social cues, potentially shaping mate selection and social bonding preferences.
Recent findings from 2025 highlight how vmPFC damage disrupts implicit social learning and fairness judgments in economic games. In a reinforcement learning paradigm simulating prosocial exchanges, patients with vmPFC lesions displayed reduced accuracy in learning to select options that reward others, with impaired positive prediction error updating for prosocial outcomes compared to self-reward conditions. Subregions like the subgenual anterior cingulate and anterior cingulate gyrus were specifically implicated in these deficits. Additionally, such damage leads to atypical fairness perceptions, as vmPFC patients donate less in dictator games and show diminished guilt responses, altering equitable decision-making in interactive economic scenarios.

Somatic marker hypothesis

The somatic marker hypothesis, proposed by Antonio Damasio in 1994, posits that the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) plays a central role in generating somatic markersbodily signals associated with emotional experiencesthat guide intuitive decision-making by biasing choices toward advantageous outcomes and away from disadvantageous ones. These markers arise from the integration of past emotional experiences with current stimuli, providing a rapid, non-conscious mechanism to evaluate options in complex, uncertain situations where deliberate reasoning alone may be insufficient. The hypothesis emphasizes that without these somatic signals, individuals struggle to make adaptive decisions, even when cognitive knowledge of risks and rewards is intact.
At its core, the mechanism involves the vmPFC as a convergence zone for visceral afferentsneural signals from the body's internal states, such as heart rate changes or gut feelingsallowing these somatic representations to influence higher-level prefrontal processing. When encountering a decision-relevant cue, the vmPFC reactivates associated somatic states from prior experiences, either covertly biasing attention and response selection or overtly through conscious feelings that qualify options as good or bad. This "marking" process enhances efficiency in value-based choices by incorporating emotional valence into cognitive deliberation, effectively simulating future outcomes through bodily feedback loops.
Empirical support for the hypothesis comes primarily from studies of patients with vmPFC lesions, who exhibit impaired autonomic responses during decision tasks despite normal intellect. In the Iowa Gambling Task, a card-selection paradigm designed to mimic real-world risk-reward trade-offs, healthy participants develop anticipatory skin conductance responses (a measure of somatic arousal) to high-risk decks before consciously recognizing their disadvantage, leading to advantageous choices; in contrast, vmPFC-damaged patients fail to generate these markers, resulting in persistent selection of poor options and financial losses. This dissociation highlights the vmPFC's necessity for translating emotional signals into adaptive behavior.
Criticisms of the somatic marker hypothesis have focused on ambiguities in its formulation, such as the precise role of conscious versus non-conscious markers and potential confounds in task interpretations like the Iowa Gambling Task, where cognitive strategies may partially explain performance deficits. Post-2023 updates have integrated the framework with Bayesian models of value computation, viewing somatic markers as priors that update beliefs about option values under uncertainty, enhancing predictive processing in decision-making. Neuroimaging studies have further confirmed these signals, showing vmPFC activation correlated with autonomic responses during value-guided choices in healthy individuals, using techniques like fMRI to reveal marker-like patterns in real-time emotional biasing.

Clinical significance

Effects of lesions

One of the earliest documented cases of ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) damage is that of Phineas Gage, a 25-year-old railroad foreman who suffered a traumatic injury in 1848 when an iron rod passed through his frontal lobes, severely damaging the vmPFC bilaterally. This accident resulted in profound personality changes, transforming Gage from a responsible and socially adept individual into someone prone to impulsivity, irritability, and poor planning, with diminished foresight and inability to adhere to long-term goals. Modern neuroimaging reconstructions confirm that the rod's trajectory primarily affected the vmPFC while sparing much of the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, linking these behavioral shifts directly to vmPFC disruption.
Contemporary lesion studies, particularly those involving patients like EVR, reveal selective impairments in real-world decision-making among individuals with vmPFC damage, despite preserved intellectual abilities and normal performance on standard IQ tests. These patients exhibit a striking inability to learn from emotional outcomes, often choosing high-risk options with negative long-term consequences, as demonstrated in the Iowa Gambling Task where they fail to develop advantageous strategies. This deficit is attributed to the absence of somatic markersphysiological signals that normally guide value-based choiceshighlighting how vmPFC lesions disrupt the integration of emotional and rational processes without affecting basic cognition.
Neuropsychological assessments of vmPFC lesion patients consistently show a profile marked by perseveration, emotional blunting, and insensitivity to risk. Perseveration manifests as rigid adherence to previously rewarded but now maladaptive behaviors, such as in reversal learning tasks where patients fail to adapt to changing contingencies. Emotional blunting is evident in reduced affective expression and diminished physiological responses to emotional stimuli, leading to flattened affect and impaired social reciprocity. Risk insensitivity appears as a disregard for potential negative outcomes, with patients overvaluing immediate rewards and underestimating hazards in both personal and economic decisions.
Recent advances in voxel-based lesion-symptom mapping (VLSM) have pinpointed subregion-specific effects within the vmPFC. For instance, a 2025 VLSM study of patients with vmPFC lesions found that damage to subregions like the subgenual anterior cingulate cortex (sgACC) and anterior cingulate cortex gyrus (ACCg) disrupts prosocial reinforcement learning, leading to reduced accuracy in learning to reward others.

Role in psychiatric disorders

The ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC), particularly its subgenual portion, exhibits hyperactivity in major depressive disorder (MDD), which correlates with core symptoms such as rumination and anhedonia. This elevated activity in the subgenual cingulate cortex, a key vmPFC subregion, is observed at rest and during tasks involving self-referential processing, contributing to persistent negative thought patterns in MDD patients. Functional connectivity analyses further reveal that vmPFC alterations dissociate anhedonia from general distress, with reduced vmPFC-striatal coupling linked to diminished reward responsiveness.
In posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD) and anxiety disorders, vmPFC dysfunction manifests as impaired coupling with the amygdala, resulting in inadequate top-down regulation of fear responses and persistent threat sensitivity. Neuroimaging studies demonstrate reduced vmPFC-amygdala functional connectivity during fear extinction tasks in PTSD, allowing exaggerated amygdala activation to sustain conditioned fear even after trauma cues are neutralized. This decoupling is also evident in generalized anxiety, where vmPFC hypoactivity fails to suppress amygdala-driven hypervigilance, perpetuating chronic worry and avoidance behaviors.
Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) involves altered vmPFC activation during error monitoring, characterized by reduced deactivation of the vmPFC in response to mistakes, which may amplify perceived errors and compulsive checking. This pattern contrasts with typical task-related suppression in healthy individuals and correlates with heightened error-related negativity signals, potentially driving obsessive doubt and ritualistic behaviors.
Therapeutic interventions targeting the vmPFC, such as deep brain stimulation (DBS) of the subcallosal cingulate, show promise for treatment-resistant depression (TRD), with response rates reaching 90% at 24 weeks and 70% remission in controlled trials. Long-term follow-up data indicate sustained efficacy, with average Montgomery-sberg Depression Rating Scale reductions of 53% at 24 months and response rates of 55-64% at 3-6 years post-implantation. These outcomes highlight vmPFC modulation as a viable strategy for alleviating refractory depressive symptoms, paralleling symptom profiles observed in vmPFC lesion cases but without inducing focal damage.

Implications for addiction

The ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) plays a critical role in reward dysregulation and cue reactivity in substance use disorders, where its dysfunction contributes to compulsive drug-seeking behaviors. In addiction, the vmPFC is involved in integrating reward signals and updating value representations, but chronic drug exposure disrupts this process, leading to heightened sensitivity to immediate drug rewards over long-term consequences. This dysregulation often manifests as impaired inhibitory control and persistent craving, perpetuating the cycle of relapse.
In cocaine abuse specifically, vmPFC hyperactivity has been observed during cue-induced craving, which is linked to deficits in updating the subjective value of rewards. Functional neuroimaging studies of individuals with cocaine use disorder reveal increased vmPFC activation when exposed to drug cues, correlating with heightened craving intensity and diminished ability to reappraise or devalue addictive stimuli, thereby sustaining motivational drive toward drug use. This altered activity may stem from repeated cocaine exposure altering dopamine signaling in vmPFC circuits, impairing the flexible adjustment of reward expectations essential for abstinence.
A broader model of addiction implicates disrupted vmPFC-striatum circuits in promoting impulsive choices, particularly the preference for immediate over delayed rewards. The vmPFC normally modulates ventral striatum activity to weigh future outcomes against immediate gratification, but in addiction, weakened connectivity in this pathway heightens delay discounting, where addicts undervalue larger, postponed rewards in favor of drug-related immediacy. This circuit-level impairment is evident across substances like alcohol and opioids, contributing to poor self-control and heightened relapse risk during decision-making under temptation.
Gender differences further modulate vmPFC involvement in addiction, with females showing greater sensitivity to social cues as relapse triggers. Neuroimaging research indicates that women with substance use disorders exhibit stronger vmPFC responses to social stress or interpersonal drug cues compared to males, potentially amplifying emotional reactivity and vulnerability to relapse in social contexts. This heightened vmPFC engagement in females may reflect sex-specific interactions between ovarian hormones and prefrontal reward processing, leading to more pronounced cue-induced craving and reduced regulatory control.
Recent neuromodulation interventions targeting the vmPFC, particularly from 2023 to 2025, have shown promise in addressing these addiction-related deficits. Transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) applied to the vmPFC has been found to reduce overgeneralized fear responses and risky decision-making biases, enhancing resistance to maladaptive reward seeking in individuals with substance use disorders. For instance, excitatory vmPFC tDCS decreases susceptibility to framing effects and impulsive choices in reward-based tasks, potentially by bolstering value updating and inhibitory signaling. Additionally, repeated sessions of prefrontal tDCS, including vmPFC-focused protocols, improve emotion regulation and reduce craving intensity in abstinent patients, fostering greater treatment adherence and mitigating relapse triggers like fear generalization. These findings suggest tDCS as a non-invasive tool to restore vmPFC function and promote adaptive decision-making in addiction recovery.
