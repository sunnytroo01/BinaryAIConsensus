 IEC 62304: Medical device software Software life cycle processes is an international standard developed by the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) that defines the life cycle requirements for software used in medical devices, whether as standalone software or embedded within hardware. It provides a harmonized framework of processes, activities, and tasks to facilitate the safe design, development, and maintenance of such software, excluding the final validation and release of the overall medical device.
First published on May 9, 2006, as Edition 1.0, the standard was amended in 2015 to incorporate updates on software of unknown provenance (SOUP) and legacy software handling, resulting in the consolidated version IEC 62304:2006+AMD1:2015. This version remains current as of 2025, with the FDA recognizing it as a consensus standard for demonstrating compliance in premarket submissions. A second edition is under development by IEC Technical Committee 62, Subcommittee 62A, with a forecasted publication date of April 2027; draft changes include simplifying safety classifications to two rigor levels, extending scope to non-medical health software and AI applications, and removing direct ties to quality and risk management standards like ISO 13485 and ISO 14971.
Central to IEC 62304 is its risk-based approach to software safety classification, dividing medical device software into three classes (A, B, and C) according to the potential for the software item or system to contribute to a hazardous situation that could result in harm: Class A (no possible injury or damage to health, requiring minimal processes like basic planning and maintenance); Class B (non-serious injury possible, necessitating additional requirements such as software requirements analysis, design, and testing); and Class C (death or serious injury possible, demanding the full set of processes including architectural and detailed design, unit and integration verification, and problem resolution). Classifications can be reduced by one level if effective hardware risk controls mitigate the software's contribution to harm, and the overall software system takes the highest class of any item. The standard mandates integration with risk management per ISO 14971 to inform classification and process tailoring.
The core processes outlined in IEC 62304 encompass software development planning (establishing objectives, resources, and schedules), requirements analysis (defining functional and performance needs), architectural and detailed design, implementation and unit verification, integration and system testing, configuration identification and management, and maintenance planning to handle post-release changes and problem resolution. These processes scale with the safety class, ensuring traceability, verification, and validation while addressing legacy software, SOUP evaluation, and security considerations. By aligning with broader medical device regulations like the EU Medical Device Regulation (MDR) and FDA guidelines, IEC 62304 supports global harmonization for software in devices ranging from diagnostic apps to life-sustaining systems.

Introduction

Purpose and Scope

IEC 62304 is an international standard published by the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) that specifies life cycle processes for medical device software. The standard establishes a framework of processes, activities, and tasks to ensure the safe design, development, maintenance, and risk management of such software, with the primary purpose of mitigating potential hazards associated with software failures in medical applications.
The scope of IEC 62304 applies to the development and maintenance of medical device software in cases where the software itself constitutes a medical device (standalone software, or SaMD), is incorporated into a medical device (software in a medical device, or SiMD), or is intended to transfer, store, convert, or display medical device data. It covers embedded and standalone software components but explicitly excludes the validation of the overall medical device system, even when the device consists entirely of software. The standard classifies software into safety classes (A, B, and C) based on potential risk to patients or operators, tailoring process rigor accordingly, though detailed classification criteria are addressed elsewhere.
Originally published in 2006, IEC 62304 has become integral to regulatory compliance for medical device software worldwide, including recognition by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) as a consensus standard for demonstrating safety and effectiveness, and application under the European Union Medical Device Regulation (EU MDR) for conformity assessment. This role supports manufacturers in meeting global requirements for software that impacts patient health outcomes.

Key Definitions

Medical device software encompasses any software intended for use, alone or in combination with hardware, in the diagnosis, prevention, monitoring, treatment, or alleviation of disease, including standalone software as a medical device (SaMD) or software embedded within a medical device.
A key term in the standard is Software of Unknown Provenance (SOUP), defined as a software item that is already developed and generally available but has not been created specifically for incorporation into the medical device in questionalso known as off-the-shelf softwareor software previously developed for which adequate records of the development processes are unavailable. This category includes legacy software from prior projects within the same organization and third-party software components, such as open-source libraries or commercial tools, where the full development history and controls cannot be verified, necessitating special risk assessment and validation.
The standard further delineates software item as any identifiable part of a computer program, such as a module, component, or subsystem, that can be managed separately during development, verification, or validation. In contrast, a software unit represents the smallest identifiable portion of a software item that can be subjected to independent verification or testing, often requiring decomposition of larger items for detailed analysis.
Legacy software refers to medical device software that was previously developed and legally placed on the market prior to the adoption of the current standard requirements, such as software introduced before the 2006 edition of IEC 62304, and which may lack complete documentation or compliance with modern lifecycle processes. If incorporated into new devices, it must undergo gap analysis to align with the standard's provisions, often treated as SOUP unless fully redeveloped.
Off-the-shelf software, synonymous with commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) or OTS software in regulatory contexts, denotes pre-existing, generally available software products or components, like databases or operating systems, acquired from external vendors for integration into medical devices without custom development for that purpose. Such software falls under SOUP if its provenance is unknown and requires evaluation of known risks, supplier controls, and potential updates.
The software safety class in IEC 62304 is a classification (A, B, or C) assigned specifically to the software system or individual items based on the potential severity of harm resulting from erroneous software output or failure, after considering external risk controls, to dictate the rigor of development processes. This differs from the overall device risk classification, which evaluates the entire medical deviceincluding hardware, software, and user interactionsunder broader standards like ISO 14971, focusing on systemic hazards rather than software-specific contributions.

Historical Development

2006 Edition

The first edition of IEC 62304, titled Medical device software Software life cycle processes, was published in May 2006 by the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC). This edition established a foundational framework for the safe design, development, and maintenance of software used in medical devices. It was subsequently adopted and published by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) under the same designation.
The standard's structure comprises nine main clauses, along with informative annexes. Clauses 1 through 3 provide introductory elements, including the scope (Clause 1), normative references (Clause 2), and terms and definitions (Clause 3). Clause 4 outlines general requirements, such as the classification of medical device software based on safety considerations. Clauses 5 through 9 detail the core processes: software development (Clause 5), software maintenance (Clause 6), software risk management (Clause 7), software configuration management (Clause 8), and software problem resolution (Clause 9). This organization ensures a systematic approach to software lifecycle activities tailored to medical applications.
A key innovation in the 2006 edition was the introduction of three software safety classes (A, B, and C) to classify medical device software according to its potential to contribute to unsafe conditions and resultant harm. Class A applies to software with no potential for causing injury or harm; Class B to software where failure could lead to non-serious injury; and Class C to software where failure might result in death or serious injury. These classes determine the rigor of applicable processes and documentation, with higher classes requiring more stringent controls.
The 2006 edition primarily focused on embedded software integral to medical devices, such as control systems in diagnostic equipment, while including basic provisions for standalone software that functions as a medical device on its own. Key processes like development planning, verification, and validation are outlined to integrate with broader quality management systems, emphasizing traceability and risk-based decision-making throughout the lifecycle.

Amendments and Edition 2 (2027)

Since its initial publication in 2006, IEC 62304 has undergone minor amendments, primarily through Amendment 1 released in 2015, which introduced requirements for legacy software to address pre-existing systems not originally developed under the standard's lifecycle processes. This amendment extended the scope to better accommodate software as a medical device (SaMD) by clarifying integration with risk management practices, including specifications for software of unknown provenance (SOUP) and enhanced testing for legacy components. No further substantive amendments or corrigenda have been issued beyond these clarifications on SOUP evaluation and verification testing.
An earlier attempt to develop the second edition reached the committee draft for vote (CDV) stage in 2021, proposing expansion of the scope to health software beyond medical devices, but was cancelled in May 2021 due to challenges with the broadened applicability. 
As of November 2025, the second edition of IEC 62304 remains in draft form, with the international draft for comments (CDV) released in February 2025 for public review by national committees. Comment resolution is anticipated to start in August 2026, leading to final approval starting October 2026 and publication expected in May 2027. This timeline reflects ongoing harmonization efforts within IEC Subcommittee 62A to incorporate advancements in software technologies.
The second edition introduces several key changes to modernize the standard. It expands the scope to explicitly include SaMD, artificial intelligence/machine learning (AI/ML) software, and health management applications, ensuring coverage of standalone and connected systems beyond embedded medical device software. A major structural shift replaces the original three software safety classes (A, B, C) with two "Software Process Rigor Levels": Level I (low rigor, equivalent to former Class A for no direct harm potential) and Level II (high rigor, combining former Classes B and C for software contributing to harm). The edition emphasizes "harm" as a broader concept in risk assessment, moving away from solely patient-specific injury to encompass general safety impacts from software failures. Additionally, it adds enhanced requirements for AI/ML validation, including lifecycle management for adaptive algorithms and documentation of training data integrity.
These updates aim to align IEC 62304 with evolving global regulations, such as the EU AI Act's risk-based framework for high-risk AI systems in healthcare and the FDA's guidance on SaMD lifecycle processes, promoting consistent compliance for international market access.

Applicability and Classification

Types of Software Covered

IEC 62304 applies to the life cycle processes for medical device software, encompassing both software as a medical device (SaMD) and software in a medical device (SiMD). SaMD refers to standalone software intended for medical purposes, such as diagnosis, prevention, treatment, or alleviation of disease, without being part of a hardware medical device. Examples include diagnostic applications on mobile devices, cloud-based health monitoring tools, and mobile health applications that analyze patient data to provide clinical insights.
SiMD includes embedded software integrated into hardware medical devices to control their functions or provide specific features. For instance, software controlling an infusion pump represents embedded SiMD, as it directly influences the device's therapeutic delivery. In contrast, a telemedicine platform operating independently as a standalone application exemplifies SaMD. The standard also covers software updates and maintenance processes to ensure ongoing safety and efficacy throughout the product's life cycle.
Additionally, IEC 62304 addresses legacy software and software of unknown provenance (SOUP), which are existing software components incorporated into medical devices but not originally developed under the standard's processes. Legacy systems, if unmodified, can be treated as SOUP, requiring risk analysis to evaluate potential hazards, while updates to such software must follow the standard's lifecycle requirements.
The standard excludes general information technology (IT) software not integral to medical devices, as well as administrative software lacking diagnostic or treatment functions, such as appointment scheduling or billing systems. Post-market surveillance activities beyond software maintenance, like general device monitoring, fall outside its scope, though maintenance processes for software changes are included. These boundaries ensure focus on software directly impacting patient safety.

Software Safety Classes

IEC 62304 defines three software safety classesA, B, and Cto categorize medical device software based on the potential severity of harm resulting from a software malfunction, thereby determining the applicable level of development rigor. These classes ensure that software contributing to higher risks undergoes more stringent processes to mitigate hazards.
Class A represents the lowest risk level, where no injury or damage to health is possible from software failure; this applies to software that cannot contribute to a hazardous situation or where any associated risk is deemed acceptable after external risk control measures. Examples include administrative tools for scheduling or record-keeping that do not interface with patient care functions.
Class B indicates a moderate risk, where non-serious injury is possible but no death or serious deterioration of health can occur; this class covers software that may contribute to a hazardous situation, though the resulting harm remains non-severe even after considering risk controls. For instance, software used for monitoring vital signs in non-critical settings falls into this category, as a failure might lead to temporary discomfort but not life-threatening outcomes.
Class C denotes the highest risk, where death or serious injury is possible due to software malfunction; this applies to software that can directly lead to such severe harm despite implemented risk controls. Life-support systems, such as software controlling infusion pumps or ventilators, exemplify this class, given the catastrophic potential of failures.
The determination of a software safety class relies on the severity of possible harm from failure, irrespective of its probability, and is traced back to outputs from the risk management process outlined in ISO 14971. Classification occurs at the level of the software system or individual components, with the overall system assigned the highest class of any contributing element.
These classes directly influence the rigor of the software development lifecycle processes: Class A requires minimal activities, such as basic planning, requirements analysis, and release documentation, often skipping detailed design reviews; Class B introduces moderate requirements, including software architecture planning and unit/integration testing; while Class C mandates the full suite of processes, encompassing detailed design, comprehensive verification at all levels, and problem resolution to ensure maximum safety.

Risk Management

Integration with ISO 14971

IEC 62304 specifies in Clause 7 that the software risk management process must be embedded within the overall device risk management framework established by ISO 14971, ensuring that software-specific risks are addressed in alignment with established principles for medical devices. This integration extends to production and post-production phases, where ongoing monitoring and evaluation of software-related hazards are required to maintain safety throughout the device's lifecycle.
The standard leverages ISO 14971 as the foundational framework for risk identification, evaluation, and control but adapts it to software-centric hazards, such as data integrity failures or interface errors that could lead to unintended clinical outcomes. This mapping ensures that software risks are systematically traced back to potential harms, with controls implemented at appropriate lifecycle stages to mitigate them effectively.
A key mandatory input for IEC 62304 processes is the risk management file developed under ISO 14971, which informs the determination of the software safety class and guides development planning by prioritizing activities based on assessed risk levels. This file provides critical data on hazard sequences and risk acceptability criteria, enabling manufacturers to tailor software development rigor accordingly.
While ISO 14971 addresses comprehensive risk management for entire medical devices, IEC 62304 differentiates by emphasizing integration into the software development lifecycle, focusing exclusively on software contributions to risk rather than hardware or systemic device elements. This targeted approach ensures that software engineering practices directly support risk reduction without duplicating broader device-level analyses.

Hazard Analysis and Risk Controls

Clause 7 of IEC 62304 specifies the software risk management process, which integrates with the overall risk management framework to address software-specific contributions to hazardous situations in medical devices. This process requires manufacturers to identify, evaluate, and mitigate risks arising from software throughout its lifecycle, ensuring that software safety is maintained by linking risk activities directly to development tasks. The activities are tailored to the software safety class (A, B, or C), with higher classes demanding more rigorous analysis and controls.
The identification of software hazards involves a systematic analysis of potential failure modes, such as algorithmic errors that could lead to incorrect dosing in infusion pumps or cybersecurity vulnerabilities that might allow unauthorized access to patient data. This bottom-up approach, often using techniques like Failure Modes and Effects Analysis (FMEA), assumes a 100% probability of occurrence for software failures to conservatively assess impacts. Risks are then evaluated based on their severity and potential to cause harm, determining if they influence the software classification; for instance, a hazard leading to death or serious injury elevates the software to Class C.
Implementation of risk controls follows hazard evaluation and includes measures like redundancy in critical algorithms to prevent single-point failures, robust error handling to detect and recover from anomalies, and segregation of software entities to isolate high-risk functions. These controls must be prioritized, with software mitigations applied only after considering design or procedural options, and documented to show how they reduce identified risks to acceptable levels. Traceability is a core requirement, linking each software hazard and its causes to corresponding requirements, design elements, and verification activities; this matrix is updated iteratively across planning, development, and maintenance phases to ensure ongoing relevance.
Verification of risk controls entails testing the effectiveness of mitigations, such as through unit, integration, and system-level tests that simulate hazardous scenarios to confirm reductions in risk. For Software of Unknown Provenance (SOUP), including off-the-shelf components like operating systems or libraries, risks must be assessed similarly, with controls verified via evaluation of known issues, wrapper functions for monitoring, or replacement if vulnerabilities persist. Post-release monitoring integrates with the software maintenance process to detect emerging risks, such as new cybersecurity threats, through field surveillance and periodic re-analysis, ensuring that any residual or unforeseen hazards are addressed promptly.

Software Development Lifecycle

Planning and Requirements Analysis

The planning and requirements analysis phases in IEC 62304 establish the foundation for the software development lifecycle by defining structured processes for organizing development activities and eliciting precise software needs. These phases, outlined in Clauses 5.1 and 5.2, ensure that medical device software is developed systematically, with activities scaled according to the software's safety classification to manage risks effectively.
Clause 5.1 mandates the creation of a Software Development Plan (SDP) that outlines the lifecycle modelsuch as waterfall or agilealong with allocated resources, schedules, tasks, responsibilities, and interrelations among activities. The SDP must reference the overall system design and development plan, while also addressing configuration management and problem resolution processes to maintain control over the project. For all software safety classes (A, B, and C), the SDP requires basic planning elements like updating the plan as needed and documenting the sequence of activities; however, tailoring increases with risk level, where Class A permits more informal approaches, whereas Classes B and C demand detailed planning for standards, methods, tools, integration testing, verification, risk management, documentation, and defect avoidance.
In Clause 5.2, the software requirements analysis process involves gathering and documenting requirements derived from system-level needs, regulatory inputs, user expectations, and cybersecurity considerations, partitioned into functional, performance, and interface specifications. The resulting Software Requirements Specification (SRS) must be verifiable, complete, unambiguous, and traceable to user needs, system requirements, and identified risks, including explicit incorporation of risk control measures for Classes B and C. Requirements analysis also requires re-evaluating the medical device risk analysis per ISO 14971 integration and verifying the SRS for consistency and feasibility across all classes, ensuring bidirectional traceability to subsequent design and testing phases.
The outputs of these phasesprimarily the SDP and SRSserve as foundational documents that guide the entire development process, enabling risk-based tailoring where Class A software allows minimal formality, Class B adds structured reviews for moderate risks, and Class C enforces rigorous documentation and analysis to mitigate high-risk hazards. This approach promotes efficiency while upholding safety, with the SDP and SRS updated iteratively to reflect changes in scope or risks.

Design and Implementation

The design and implementation phase of IEC 62304 encompasses the software architectural design, detailed design, and unit implementation processes outlined in clauses 5.3, 5.4, and 5.5, respectively, which translate established software requirements into a structured, verifiable software product while applying risk-based rigor according to the software safety class.
In the software architectural design (clause 5.3), applicable to Class B and Class C software, the manufacturer must transform the software requirements into a high-level architecture that defines the major structural components, modules, and interfaces of the software system. This includes specifying the interactions between software items, hardware, and external systems, as well as identifying any segregation of components to support risk controls, such as isolating critical functions to mitigate potential hazards. For software of unknown provenance (SOUP), the architecture must incorporate functional and performance requirements, along with evaluations of hardware compatibility and risks associated with its use. The resulting architecture is documented in a Software Architectural Design document and verified through technical evaluation to ensure it meets system needs and safety objectives.
The software detailed design (clause 5.4), required with full rigor for Class C and optionally for selected units in Class B, refines the architectural design by subdividing the software into discrete software units and specifying low-level details such as algorithms, data structures, control flows, and interfaces for each unit. This phase ensures that the design provides sufficient detail for implementation while maintaining traceability to higher-level requirements and incorporating any necessary risk mitigations. The detailed design is documented in a Software Detailed Design document and verified to confirm its completeness and alignment with the architecture. For Class A software, this clause is not applicable, allowing simpler implementation approaches without formal detailed documentation.
Software unit implementation and verification (clause 5.5), applicable to Class B and Class C with basic procedures for Class A where development occurs, involves coding each software unit in accordance with the detailed design specifications and conducting initial verification to confirm that the implementation meets the unit's design, requirements, and acceptance criteria. The manufacturer establishes strategies, methods, and procedures for this verification, which may include static analysis, code reviews, or dynamic testing focused on aspects like data flows, memory management, and error handling. For Class C, additional rigor such as independent peer reviews of the implementation is mandated to address high-risk scenarios. When using SOUP in implementation, a risk-based evaluation determines the extent of verification needed, without requiring full development activities. The primary outputs of this phase are the implemented code and verification records, ensuring the software units are ready for subsequent integration.

Verification, Testing, and Release

In IEC 62304, verification encompasses a range of methods to confirm that the software design outputs meet the specified software requirements, including reviews, static analysis, dynamic analysis, and testing, with traceability maintained through matrices linking requirements to verification activities. These methods are applied progressively across the development lifecycle, ensuring that integration and system-level outcomes align with documented criteria before release. For software classified under safety Class A, verification is limited to basic documentation and release checks, while Class B requires additional integration verification, and Class C mandates comprehensive analysis including full code coverage.
Integration testing, as outlined in Clause 5.6, involves assembling verified software units into larger software items and ultimately the complete system, followed by tests to validate interfaces, data flows, and interactions under normal and boundary conditions. This process includes black-box testing to assess external behavior against requirements and white-box testing to examine internal structures, with test plans specifying procedures, environments, and pass/fail criteria. For Class C software, integration testing must include rigorous methods to verify interfaces and mitigate risks, such as dynamic testing under various conditions. Traceability matrices are essential here, documenting how each integrated component fulfills its design inputs and mitigates identified hazards.
System testing under Clause 5.7 evaluates the fully integrated software system in its intended operational environment to verify compliance with system-level requirements, including performance under stress, regression after changes, and responses to error conditions. Test plans must define objectives, methods (such as equivalence partitioning or boundary value analysis), and acceptance criteria, with results recorded in detailed reports. Tailoring by safety class is critical: Class A software requires only basic functional testing, Class B adds interface and error-handling tests, and Class C demands comprehensive regression and stress testing to confirm risk controls are effective, with verification methods scaled to the highest rigor. These activities ensure the software performs reliably without introducing unintended hazards.
Software release, per Clause 5.8, occurs only after objective evidence demonstrates completion of all development activities, verification results meet acceptance criteria, and residual risks are verified as acceptable through final reviews. The manufacturer must generate release records including version labeling (e.g., unique identifiers like semantic versioning), archiving of all artifacts for reproducibility, and release notes summarizing changes, known anomalies, and verification summaries. For higher classes, release includes confirmation of full traceability and risk management integration, ensuring the deployed software aligns with ISO 14971 principles without unresolved safety issues. This step finalizes the development process, providing auditable proof of compliance before utilization in medical devices.

Supporting Processes

Software Maintenance Process

The software maintenance process in IEC 62304, outlined in Clause 6, establishes requirements for managing post-release modifications to medical device software to ensure ongoing safety and compliance. This process applies to all software safety classes (A, B, and C) and integrates with the initial software development plan (SDP) by updating it to address change management throughout the product's lifecycle. Manufacturers must define procedures for handling software issues and enhancements after initial release, focusing on maintaining the software's intended functionality without introducing new hazards.
Maintenance planning requires the creation of a dedicated software maintenance plan that details roles, responsibilities, and procedures for problem reporting, evaluation, tracking, resolution, and implementation of modifications. This plan must specify how changes will be controlled, including criteria for classifying issues and mechanisms for re-verification and re-release of updated software. The plan also ensures traceability from identified problems to implemented changes, with updates to the SDP reflecting any evolving maintenance needs. The plan must incorporate risk management activities per ISO 14971 to assess potential impacts on safety for all classes.
Key activities in the maintenance process include problem identification, where user feedback, complaints, or monitoring data are reviewed to detect issues such as defects or performance gaps, leading to the creation of problem reports with descriptions, severity levels, and initial investigations. Following identification, an impact analysis evaluates each potential change for effects on safety risks, software architecture, and classification; this involves applying risk management per ISO 14971 to identify any new or modified hazards, and may result in re-classification of the software if the changes significantly alter its safety profile. Approved changes are then implemented using established development processes, followed by verification through testingsuch as unit, integration, or regression teststo confirm resolution without introducing new problems; for Class C software, comprehensive regression testing is mandatory. Finally, verified modifications are re-released with appropriate version controls and user notifications if safety aspects are affected.
The scope of maintenance encompasses corrective actions to fix defects, adaptive changes to accommodate environmental or regulatory shifts, and perfective updates to improve performance or usability, all while prioritizing patient safety. If modifications could elevate risks, the software safety class may need reassessment, potentially triggering more rigorous processes akin to initial development. This ensures that post-release evolution of the software aligns with the standard's emphasis on lifecycle safety.
Documentation is a core requirement, with a maintenance report compiling the history of all changes, including problem reports, impact analyses, implementation details, verification results, and rationales for decisions. This report must maintain traceability and be updated for each release, serving as evidence of compliance during audits; user documentation and safety instructions are also revised as needed to reflect changes.

Software Configuration Management Process

The Software Configuration Management Process, specified in Clause 8 of IEC 62304, is a supporting process applicable to all software safety classes (A, B, and C) for medical device software, ensuring the identification, control, and traceability of software items to maintain integrity throughout the development and maintenance phases. This process establishes baselines and manages changes to prevent unintended alterations that could affect safety, with consistent requirements across all classes. It integrates with the overall software lifecycle to support verification and release activities, applying equally to custom-developed software and software of unknown provenance (SOUP).
Identification of configuration items forms the foundation of the process, requiring the manufacturer to define and uniquely label all relevant entities, such as source code, executable files, design documents, test records, and development tools, using version numbers or other distinct identifiers. For SOUP, this includes recording the component's name, version, supplier, and any known limitations to enable traceability. These items are documented in a configuration item list or software bill of materials, establishing a reference point for subsequent controls and ensuring that only approved versions are used in builds or releases.
Change control procedures govern modifications to identified items, mandating a formal mechanism for submitting change requests, evaluating their impact on safety and functionality, and obtaining approvals before implementation. Baselinesstable sets of configuration items representing approved states, such as a release versionare established at key milestones to serve as references for verification and future changes. Implemented changes must be verified against the original specifications, with traceability maintained to link them back to the initiating request, ensuring no unapproved alterations compromise the software.
Configuration status accounting involves maintaining comprehensive records of the status of all items, including current versions, change histories, approval details, and audit outcomes, to provide an auditable trail. This accounting supports periodic audits to confirm compliance with the process and detect discrepancies, such as unauthorized changes. While the standard does not mandate specific tools, version control systems like Git are widely adopted to automate identification, track changes, and generate status reports, facilitating application across development, testing, and maintenance activities.

Software Problem Resolution Process

The Software Problem Resolution Process, as defined in Clause 9 of IEC 62304, establishes a systematic approach for identifying, documenting, analyzing, and resolving software problems to ensure the safety and reliability of medical device software throughout its lifecycle. This process applies to all software safety classes (A, B, and C). It mandates that manufacturers implement procedures to capture issues from various sources, such as development testing, user feedback, or post-market surveillance, thereby preventing recurrence and supporting continuous improvement.
Key activities in the process begin with problem reporting, where any identified software defect, anomaly, or potential issue is formally logged in a centralized system, including details like the software version, description, reproduction steps, and initial severity assessment. Following reporting, categorization occurs to classify the problem based on its potential impact, such as severity levels (e.g., high for unacceptable risks, medium for acceptable risks with mitigations, or low for non-safety issues), which triggers escalation to relevant parties such as developers, quality assurance, or regulatory experts for higher-severity cases. Investigation then involves root cause analysis to determine the underlying factors, such as code errors or configuration issues, with thorough documentation of findings, particularly for Class B and C software where safety implications are greater.
Disposition follows, where the team decides on the resolution strategy, such as implementing a fix, accepting the issue with justification, or deferring it, ensuring alignment with overall software objectives. If a fix is chosen, implementation proceeds through controlled changes, often integrated with configuration management processes to maintain version traceability. Verification concludes the activities by confirming the resolution's effectiveness via appropriate testing, including regression tests for Class C software to ensure no new issues arise, before closing the problem report.
Documentation is a core requirement, maintained in a dedicated problem resolution file or log that records all steps, including reports, analyses, root causes, impact assessments, corrective actions, and verification results, providing an auditable trail for compliance. This integration with risk management processes, per ISO 14971, ensures that any safety-related impacts from problems are evaluated and controlled, while links to configuration management handle change control for implemented resolutions. The process emphasizes post-market issues, such as those from customer reports, to address real-world usage problems promptly and inform future updates.
To support process improvement, metrics are tracked, including resolution times, problem recurrence rates, and trend analyses of recurring issues, which help identify systemic weaknesses and refine development practices. Overall, this process operates across the entire software lifecycle but gains particular importance in maintenance phases, where post-release problems can affect device performance and patient safety.

Documentation and Compliance

Classification-Dependent Requirements

IEC 62304 establishes classification-dependent requirements for medical device software, tailoring the extent of documentation and process rigor to the assigned software safety class, which is determined by the potential for harm as defined in clause 4.3. This approach ensures that development efforts are proportionate to the risk level, with Class A imposing the least demands and Class C requiring comprehensive controls to mitigate severe hazards.
For Class A software, where no injury or damage to health is possible, requirements are minimal to promote efficiency in low-risk scenarios. Manufacturers must document the software development plan (clause 5.1) and establish a problem resolution process (clause 9), but architectural design (clause 5.3), detailed design (clause 5.4), and unit verification (clause 5.5) are not required. Basic elements like software requirements analysis (clause 5.2), integration and system testing (clauses 5.6 and 5.7), and release documentation (clause 5.8) suffice, alongside supporting processes such as configuration management (clause 8) and maintenance planning (clause 6).
Class B software, which may result in non-serious injury, builds on Class A by adding moderate rigor. This includes documented software architectural design (clause 5.3), along with verification activities for units, integration, and system levels (clauses 5.55.7). Requirements analysis remains essential (clause 5.2), and traceability between requirements and verification results is recommended to demonstrate coverage, though not as extensively as in higher classes. Supporting processes like risk management (clause 7) must integrate with these elements to address potential harms.
Class C software, associated with the possibility of death or serious injury, demands the full suite of requirements for maximum safety assurance. In addition to all Class B elements, it mandates detailed design specifications for each software unit (clause 5.4), comprehensive verification and validation plans (clauses 5.55.7), and traceability matrices linking requirements to design, code, and test results throughout the lifecycle. Configuration management plans (clause 8) and problem resolution documentation (clause 9) must be thorough, with explicit risk controls integrated into every phase to prevent hazardous situations.

Auditing and Certification

Compliance with IEC 62304 is not achieved through a standalone formal certification process issued by the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC); instead, adherence is demonstrated and verified as part of broader quality management system (QMS) audits, typically under ISO 13485, which integrates software lifecycle requirements for medical devices. Notified bodies, such as TV SD or BSI, may issue supplementary certificates confirming IEC 62304 compliance only after verifying an existing ISO 13485 certification, emphasizing the standard's role within a certified QMS framework.
Audits conducted by notified bodies or under programs like the Medical Device Single Audit Program (MDSAP) focus on reviewing key artifacts to confirm the application of IEC 62304 processes, including software development plans, traceability matrices linking requirements to verification activities, test evidence from unit/integration/system levels, and risk management files aligned with ISO 14971. These reviews assess whether processes mitigate software-related hazards effectively, often sampling documentation for plausibility rather than exhaustive technical audits, as full compliance cannot be solely demonstrated through system audits alone. For software of unknown provenance (SOUP), auditors examine declarations evaluating known issues, residual risks, and integration controls to ensure they do not compromise device safety.
To prepare for audits, organizations employ strategies such as conducting gap analyses against IEC 62304 clauses to identify deficiencies in existing processes, implementing tailored software development lifecycles with automated traceability tools, and preparing comprehensive documentation that demonstrates ongoing compliance. Training teams on standard requirements and integrating risk-based planning further supports evidence generation, particularly for classification-dependent documentation needs.
Challenges in auditing include adapting agile development methods, which require maintaining rigorous traceability and verification records to align with the standard's prescriptive elements, as outlined in guidance like AAMI TIR45. Legacy software transitions pose additional hurdles, often necessitating gap analyses to retroactively document processes or treat unchanged components as SOUP with justified risk controls, avoiding full redevelopment where possible. For preparation toward the anticipated second edition of IEC 62304, expected to expand scope to standalone health software and AI/ML components, organizations should proactively document AI model training data, validation, and post-market surveillance to address enhanced rigor levels.
Regulatory ties reinforce auditing practices: in the US, IEC 62304 compliance evidence supports FDA 510(k) submissions by verifying software validation under 21 CFR Part 820, while in the EU, it aligns with Medical Device Regulation (MDR) Annex IX audits by notified bodies, presuming conformity with essential requirements when harmonized.

Relation to Other Standards

Quality Management Systems

IEC 62304, the international standard for medical device software lifecycle processes, is designed to integrate seamlessly with ISO 13485, the quality management system (QMS) standard for medical devices, to ensure comprehensive oversight of software development and maintenance. This harmonization embeds IEC 62304's software-specific processes into key ISO 13485 clauses, particularly Clause 7.3 on design and development controls, which covers planning, input, output, review, verification, validation, and transfer activities. Additionally, IEC 62304 aligns with ISO 13485's risk management requirements under Clause 7.1 and post-market surveillance provisions in Clause 8.2, creating a unified framework that addresses software risks throughout the product lifecycle.
A QMS compliant with ISO 13485 is recommended to incorporate software-specific procedures derived from IEC 62304, such as those for software development planning (Clause 5.1), requirements analysis (Clause 5.2), and verification (Clause 5.7), particularly for medical devices containing software, to meet regulatory expectations. Audits for certification or compliance often verify both standards concurrently, evaluating how IEC 62304 processes support ISO 13485's documentation and traceability requirements, including design history files and change controls. This integration ensures that software activities are auditable within the broader QMS structure, with outsourced processes (ISO 13485 Clause 4.1.5) and internal audits (Clause 8.2.4) extending to software elements. The current edition of IEC 62304 maintains direct ties to ISO 13485, though the upcoming second edition, forecasted for April 2027, plans to remove such explicit links.
The benefits of this harmonization include full coverage of the software lifecyclefrom initial planning to ongoing maintenancewithin a certified QMS, thereby enhancing patient safety, regulatory compliance, and operational efficiency for medical device manufacturers. For instance, design and development planning under ISO 13485 Clause 7.3 directly references the Software Development Plan (SDP) outlined in IEC 62304 Clause 5.1, ensuring that software objectives, resources, and interfaces are aligned with overall device quality goals. This approach also facilitates overlap with risk management standards like ISO 14971, where software-specific risks identified in IEC 62304 are incorporated into the QMS.

Device-Specific Standards

IEC 62304 integrates closely with IEC 60601-1, the collateral standard for the basic safety and essential performance of medical electrical equipment, by serving as the normative reference for software lifecycle processes within such devices. Specifically, Clause 14 of IEC 60601-1 Edition 3.1 mandates compliance with IEC 62304 for programmable electrical medical systems, ensuring that software development aligns with overall device safety requirements.
Beyond electromedical equipment, IEC 62304 applies to other device-specific standards, such as IEC 61010-1 for safety in electrical equipment for measurement, control, and laboratory use, including in vitro diagnostic devices. In this context, Annex C of IEC 62304 provides guidance on applying its processes to IEC 61010-1, particularly for software validation and testing in laboratory medical devices. Similarly, the ISO 80601 series, which addresses medical electrical equipment for specific therapies like respiratory support, references IEC 62304 for defining software items and units in device development. These integrations emphasize software validation and testing per IEC 62304 to meet therapy-specific performance criteria.
Device-specific standards like IEC 60601-1 and IEC 61010-1 primarily focus on hardware safety, electrical performance, and overall system risks, whereas IEC 62304 concentrates on dedicated software development processes, including planning, design, and maintenance. This complementary scope ensures that software contributions to device safety are addressed through harmonized lifecycle management without overlapping hardware-centric requirements.
The upcoming second edition of IEC 62304, forecasted for April 2027, will enhance alignment with device standards like IEC 60601 by introducing specific clauses on AI design and development planning, addressing the growing integration of artificial intelligence in medical devices. This update aims to extend the standard's scope to AI-enabled software while maintaining compatibility with existing collateral requirements for enhanced regulatory compliance.
