RIG-I-like receptor

 RIG-I-like receptors (RLRs) are a family of cytoplasmic pattern recognition receptors that function as key sensors of viral RNA in the innate immune system, detecting pathogen-associated molecular patterns to initiate antiviral defenses and type I interferon production.
The RLR family comprises three primary members: retinoic acid-inducible gene I (RIG-I, encoded by DDX58), melanoma differentiation-associated protein 5 (MDA5, encoded by IFIH1), and laboratory of genetics and physiology 2 (LGP2, encoded by DHX58). RIG-I and MDA5 possess N-terminal caspase activation and recruitment domains (CARDs) that enable signaling, a central DExD/H-box RNA helicase domain for RNA binding and unwinding, and a C-terminal repressor domain (RD) that regulates ligand specificity, whereas LGP2 lacks CARDs and primarily serves a regulatory role. These receptors recognize distinct viral RNA features, such as 5'-triphosphate ends or double-stranded RNA for RIG-I, and long double-stranded RNA for MDA5, while LGP2 modulates the activity of the other two.
Upon RNA binding, RLRs undergo conformational changes that expose their CARDs, leading to oligomerization and interaction with the adaptor protein mitochondrial antiviral-signaling protein (MAVS), which activates downstream pathways including the transcription factors IRF3 and NF-B to induce type I interferons (IFN-/) and proinflammatory cytokines. This signaling cascade is tightly regulated by post-translational modifications, such as ubiquitination and phosphorylation, as well as regulatory proteins and subcellular localization to prevent excessive inflammation.
RLRs are essential for controlling RNA virus infections, including those by influenza, hepatitis C, and SARS-CoV-2, and cooperate with other immune sensors like Toll-like receptors to shape both innate and adaptive immunity. Dysregulation of RLR signaling is implicated in autoimmune diseases, such as Aicardi-Goutires syndrome and Singleton-Merten syndrome, due to aberrant type I IFN production, and has emerging roles in cancer immunotherapy by enhancing antitumor immune responses.

Overview and Discovery

Definition and Function

RIG-I-like receptors (RLRs), also known as retinoic acid-inducible gene I-like receptors, constitute a family of cytosolic pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) within the RIG-I-like helicase (RLH) subclass of the DExD/H-box RNA helicase superfamily. These receptors primarily function as sensors for detecting viral double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) and other non-self RNA motifs in the cytoplasm of host cells, distinguishing them from extracellular or endosomal RNA sensors like Toll-like receptors (TLRs).
The core function of RLRs is to initiate innate immune responses upon viral RNA recognition, leading to the activation of signaling cascades that culminate in the production of type I interferons (IFNs), such as IFN- and IFN-, as well as proinflammatory cytokines including IL-6 and TNF-. This antiviral signaling establishes an intracellular antiviral state, inhibits viral replication, and coordinates broader immune activation through the recruitment of adaptive immunity components. RLR activation typically occurs via interaction with the adaptor protein mitochondrial antiviral-signaling protein (MAVS), which propagates signals to downstream kinases and transcription factors.
Evolutionarily, RLRs belong to the ancient superfamily of DExD/H-box helicases, with orthologs conserved across vertebrates from teleost fish to mammals, underscoring their fundamental role in antiviral defense. In mammals, the RLR family comprises three principal members: retinoic acid-inducible gene I (RIG-I), melanoma differentiation-associated gene 5 (MDA5), and laboratory of genetics and physiology 2 (LGP2). These receptors collectively ensure robust detection of diverse viral infections, though their specific contributions vary.

Historical Discovery

The discovery of RIG-I (retinoic acid-inducible gene I) originated from studies identifying retinoic acid-inducible genes in human acute promyelocytic leukemia cells, where it was cloned in 1997. Its identification as a DExD/H-box RNA helicase and essential role in sensing double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) to trigger antiviral IFN responses were revealed in 2004 by Yoneyama et al., marking it as a key cytoplasmic sensor. This work established RIG-I as distinct from endosomal Toll-like receptors (TLRs), highlighting its role in intracellular viral detection.
MDA5 (melanoma differentiation-associated protein 5) was identified in 20022003 through microarray screens for IFN-inducible antiviral genes, initially noted as a CARD-containing helicase upregulated in response to viral infection. By 20042005, studies confirmed MDA5's function in recognizing long dsRNA and cooperating with RIG-I in IFN induction, further delineating the RIG-I-like receptor (RLR) family. LGP2 (laboratory of genetics and physiology 2) was recognized in 2005 as a CARD-lacking RLR member that binds RNA but lacks independent signaling capacity, instead acting as a regulator of RIG-I and MDA5 activity.
Between 2004 and 2006, pivotal research by Kawai, Akira, and Takeuchi solidified RLRs as primary cytoplasmic dsRNA sensors, independent of TLR pathways, through identification of the adaptor IPS-1 (now MAVS) that links RLR activation to IFN production. These studies used gene knockdown and overexpression in cell lines to demonstrate RLR specificity for viral RNA motifs, contrasting with TLR3's endosomal localization.
Key milestones included the 2008 crystal structure of RIG-I's C-terminal domain by Cui et al., revealing its RNA-binding mechanism and 5'-triphosphate specificity for viral ligands. In the 2010s, RLR knockout mouse models expanded understanding beyond viruses, showing roles in autoinflammation and antitumor immunity; for instance, RIG-I-deficient mice exhibited altered responses to non-viral stressors like DNA damage. Post-2020 developments featured cryo-EM structures resolving RIG-I activation states, such as distinct conformations for viral versus host RNA binding in 2022. Concurrently, COVID-19 research up to 2025 highlighted SARS-CoV-2 evasion of RLRs via proteins like ORF6 and NSP1, which disrupt MAVS signaling to delay IFN responses, informing therapeutic strategies.

Family Members

RIG-I

RIG-I, encoded by the DDX58 gene located on chromosome 9p21.1, is a cytosolic protein comprising approximately 925 amino acids with a molecular mass of about 106 kDa. It exhibits ubiquitous expression across human tissues, with notably higher levels in immune cells such as macrophages and dendritic cells, where it contributes to innate immune surveillance.
Structurally, RIG-I possesses two N-terminal caspase activation and recruitment domains (CARDs) that mediate downstream signaling, a central DExD/H-box helicase domain responsible for ATPase-dependent RNA unwinding, and a C-terminal regulatory domain that facilitates specific RNA binding. In its inactive state, RIG-I maintains an autoinhibitory conformation, wherein the helicase domains sequester the CARDs to prevent aberrant activation and conserve cellular resources. This closed structure ensures that signaling is triggered only upon precise ligand engagement.
RIG-I serves as the prototypical sensor for short viral RNAs, preferentially recognizing 5'-triphosphate single-stranded or double-stranded RNA (5'ppp-RNA) and blunt-ended double-stranded RNA molecules shorter than 300 base pairs. These ligands are hallmarks of replication intermediates from diverse RNA viruses, including influenza A virus and hepatitis C virus, enabling RIG-I to detect infections with broad tropism. In contrast to MDA5, which favors longer double-stranded RNAs, RIG-I's affinity for compact, phosphorylated motifs allows it to initiate responses to a wider array of acute viral threats.
Upon activation, RIG-I oligomerizes and recruits the adaptor protein MAVS to rapidly induce type I interferon production, particularly IFN-, thereby orchestrating early antiviral defenses against RNA viruses. This pathway is crucial for restricting viral spread in infected cells and priming adaptive immunity. Insights from RIG-I knockout mice reveal severe impairments in controlling paramyxovirus infections, such as those by Sendai virus, highlighting its indispensable role in innate antiviral immunity.

MDA5

MDA5, also known as melanoma differentiation-associated protein 5, is encoded by the IFIH1 gene on human chromosome 2 and consists of approximately 1025 amino acids, resulting in a molecular weight of about 116 kDa. Its expression is inducible by type I interferons (IFNs) and is particularly prominent in epithelial cells and fibroblasts, where it contributes to the innate immune response against RNA viruses.
MDA5 specifically recognizes long double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) molecules exceeding 1 kb in length, as well as synthetic analogs like high-molecular-weight polyinosinic:polycytidylic acid (poly(I:C)), which mimic viral replication intermediates. This ligand preference enables MDA5 to detect certain positive-sense RNA viruses, such as encephalomyocarditis virus (EMCV) and Theiler's virus, that generate extended dsRNA during replication. Structurally, MDA5 features two N-terminal caspase activation and recruitment domains (CARDs), a central DExD/H-box helicase domain composed of Hel1 and Hel2 subdomains, and a C-terminal regulatory domain (CTD) that facilitates dsRNA binding. Upon ligand engagement, MDA5 undergoes ATP-dependent cooperative filament formation along the dsRNA, amplifying signal transduction through CARD-MAVS interactions on mitochondrial membranes, which contrasts with the monomeric activation seen in related receptors.
In its primary function, MDA5 drives sustained type I IFN production, which bridges innate and adaptive immunity by promoting T cell responses and long-term antiviral defense.30179-3) Gain-of-function mutations in IFIH1 underlie Aicardi-Goutires syndrome (AGS), a type I interferonopathy characterized by excessive IFN signaling and neurological inflammation due to aberrant MDA5 activation by endogenous RNAs. Additionally, polymorphisms in IFIH1, such as rs1990760 (A946T), are associated with increased risk of type 1 diabetes through altered MDA5-mediated IFN responses to viral triggers like coxsackievirus. Similar variants link MDA5 dysregulation to systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), where heightened IFN signatures exacerbate autoimmunity. Like other RIG-I-like receptors, MDA5 converges on shared IFN pathways but uniquely sustains responses for chronic threats.

LGP2

LGP2, also known as laboratory of genetics and physiology 2, is encoded by the DHX58 gene located on human chromosome 17, spanning 11,329 base pairs with 14 exons and 13 introns. The protein consists of approximately 678 amino acids, with a molecular mass of about 75 kDa. Like other RLR family members, LGP2 expression is inducible by type I interferons (IFN) and viral infections, with basal levels present in various cell types and elevated expression observed in immune tissues such as spleen and thymus, mirroring patterns seen in RIG-I.
LGP2 binds double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) and single-stranded RNA (ssRNA), including those with 5'-triphosphate ends, though its recognition is less specific for the 5' terminus compared to RIG-I. Unlike RIG-I and MDA5, LGP2 lacks N-terminal CARD domains essential for direct signaling, rendering it incapable of recruiting the adaptor protein MAVS and thus without independent signaling capacity. Instead, it functions primarily as an accessory regulator, modulating the sensitivity of RIG-I and MDA5 to ligands through its central DExH-box helicase domain and C-terminal domain (CTD), which facilitate RNA binding and protein oligomerization. This allows LGP2 to form complexes with viral RNAs, such as 2:1 stoichiometries with short dsRNA segments, influencing downstream antiviral responses without initiating them.
LGP2 fine-tunes antiviral immunity by exerting context-dependent effects on IFN production, acting as a dominant-negative inhibitor or enhancer of RIG-I and MDA5 activities. In infections like West Nile virus, LGP2 inhibits excessive type I IFN responses, promoting CD8+ T cell survival and controlling immunopathology to enhance host protection. Conversely, in enterovirus infections such as encephalomyocarditis virus (EMCV), LGP2 enhances MDA5-mediated signaling to boost IFN induction, making its absence detrimental to antiviral defense. These dual roles highlight LGP2's contribution to balancing innate responses, preventing both insufficient immunity and harmful hyperinflammation.
Studies in LGP2-deficient (Dhx58^{-/-}) mice reveal its regulatory complexity, with phenotypes varying by pathogen. Knockouts exhibit enhanced IFN production and resistance to certain viruses like vesicular stomatitis virus (VSV) and influenza A (H3N2), consistent with LGP2's inhibitory effects on RIG-I signaling in these contexts, potentially linking to autoinflammatory-like hyperactivation of IFN pathways. However, the same mice show impaired IFN responses and increased susceptibility to EMCV and Listeria monocytogenes, underscoring LGP2's essential positive modulation of MDA5 for specific antiviral defenses.

Structural Features

Domain Architecture

RIG-I-like receptors (RLRs) share a conserved domain architecture that enables RNA sensing and signal transduction, consisting of a central DExD/H-box helicase domain responsible for RNA binding and ATPase activity, and a C-terminal repressor domain (also known as CTD) that confers ligand specificity through recognition of RNA features such as 5'-triphosphate ends and maintains autoinhibition in the resting state. The helicase domain, belonging to the superfamily 2 (SF2) RNA helicases, is subdivided into Hel1, Hel2, and an insertion subdomain (Hel2i), facilitating ATP-dependent RNA unwinding and translocation along the nucleic acid backbone.
In RIG-I, the architecture includes two N-terminal caspase activation and recruitment domains (CARDs) that mediate interaction with the adaptor protein MAVS upon activation, positioned upstream of the helicase core and connected by a flexible linker. MDA5 features a distinctive Hel2i insertion domain within the helicase region, which promotes cooperative binding to long double-stranded RNA and enables the formation of filamentous oligomers essential for signal amplification. LGP2, in contrast, lacks the N-terminal CARDs, rendering it incapable of direct signaling, but possesses an expanded RD that enhances regulatory interactions with RNA and modulates the activity of other RLRs through competitive binding.
Upon ligand engagement, RLRs undergo ATP-dependent conformational changes, where the helicase and CTD domains clamp onto RNA, stabilizing the complex and relieving RD-mediated autoinhibition to expose the CARDs in RIG-I and MDA5. This dynamic repositioning, driven by ATP hydrolysis, transitions the receptor from a closed, inactive conformation to an open state primed for downstream interactions, with LGP2 exhibiting similar clamping but without CARD release due to their absence.

RNA-Binding Mechanisms

The RNA-binding mechanisms of RIG-I-like receptors (RLRs) involve specialized domains that enable selective recognition of viral RNA structures. The C-terminal domain (CTD) primarily initiates binding by recognizing features such as 5' triphosphate (5'ppp) ends or blunt duplex termini on double-stranded RNA (dsRNA), while the central DExD/H-box helicase domains utilize ATPase activity to unwind RNA helices and translocate along the ligand, facilitating signal amplification without direct sequence specificity.
In RIG-I, the CTD exhibits high-affinity binding to short dsRNAs bearing 5'ppp or 5' diphosphate ends, mediated by a basic cleft where residues like Phe853 and His830 form hydrogen bonds and stack with the terminal base pair, discriminating against host capped RNAs. The helicase domains, in the absence of ATP, adopt a low-affinity, open conformation for initial RNA engagement; ATP binding and hydrolysis then drive a transition to a high-affinity, closed U-shaped structure, where the helicase grips the RNA with a 9-10 base pair footprint and translocates in a 3' to 5' direction. Conserved motifs in the helicase, such as motif II (DEAD box) for ATP coordination and motif VI (HRIGRTGR) for RNA clamping, are essential for this dynamic process; mutations in these regions, including those associated with familial pancreatitis (e.g., in the ATPase domain), disrupt hydrolysis and prolong RNA binding, leading to dysregulated activity.
MDA5 employs a distinct cooperative mechanism, where individual molecules bind dsRNA as dimers with a 16-18 base pair footprint, but filament formation on longer dsRNAs (>1 kb) amplifies avidity through head-to-tail polymerization into polar helical assemblies spanning the RNA length. The CTD is crucial for this cooperativity by promoting nucleation and interdimer contacts, though it does not directly contact RNA; ATP hydrolysis powers translocation and filament disassembly, with longer filaments exhibiting slower dissociation rates (e.g., half-life of 1.3 minutes for 2 kb RNA versus 0.2 minutes for 0.5 kb). Key helicase residues, including those in motifs I-IV, coordinate ATP and RNA, enabling length-dependent discrimination without requiring end-specific features.
LGP2, lacking CARD domains, binds dsRNA via its CTD and helicase regions in a manner that modulates other RLRs rather than initiating independent signaling. It competitively inhibits RIG-I at high concentrations by sequestering RNA ligands but allosterically enhances MDA5 filament stability and assembly at lower levels, increasing the rate of MDA5-RNA interactions and yielding shorter, more numerous filaments for efficient long RNA coverage. Mutations disrupting LGP2's RNA-binding motifs impair this regulatory balance, underscoring its role in fine-tuning RLR dynamics.

Ligands and Recognition

Types of RLR Ligands

RIG-I-like receptors (RLRs) recognize a variety of RNA ligands characterized by specific structural and chemical features that distinguish viral or aberrant host RNAs from typical cellular transcripts. These ligands primarily include double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) motifs, uncapped 5' ends such as triphosphate (5'ppp) or diphosphate (5'dp), and sequence-specific elements like polyuridine tracts. Viral ligands often arise from replication intermediates, while endogenous ligands stem from cellular stress or damage, and synthetic analogs mimic these to activate RLRs for therapeutic purposes. Ligand recognition thresholds typically require minimal structural elements, such as duplex lengths exceeding 20 base pairs for effective signaling.
Viral RNA ligands for RLRs encompass dsRNA generated during genome replication and transcription of many RNA viruses. For instance, replication intermediates of positive-sense RNA viruses like hepatitis C virus (HCV) produce polyuridine/cytosine (poly-U/UC) motifs in the 3' untranslated region, which serve as potent RIG-I agonists due to their 5'ppp ends and uridine-rich composition that enhances binding affinity. Similarly, negative-sense RNA viruses such as influenza A generate panhandle structuresshort, blunt-ended dsRNAs with 5'ppp terminiformed by base-pairing of the viral genome's 5' and 3' ends, enabling RIG-I detection during early infection stages. Double-stranded RNA from reovirus replication activates MDA5, illustrating how RLRs exploit diverse viral RNA features for broad antiviral surveillance.
Endogenous ligands arise from host RNAs that mimic viral patterns under stress conditions, contributing to sterile inflammation without pathogen involvement. Mitochondrial double-stranded RNA (mt-dsRNA), released into the cytosol upon mitochondrial dysfunction or depletion of polynucleotide phosphorylase, activates MDA5 and drives type I interferon responses, as observed in models of cellular stress. Self-RNAs processed in stress granules, such as RNase L-cleaved transcripts during viral mimicry or infection, can engage RIG-I and MDA5, promoting inflammatory signaling that links to autoimmune pathologies like systemic lupus erythematosus. These endogenous triggers highlight RLRs' role in detecting cellular damage signals.
Synthetic RNA analogs have been engineered to replicate natural ligand features for vaccine adjuvants and immunotherapies. Polyinosinic:polycytidylic acid (poly(I:C)), a long dsRNA mimic, potently stimulates MDA5 to induce robust interferon production, commonly used in preclinical antiviral models. Short 5'ppp-RNA duplexes (3pRNA), designed with blunt ends and triphosphate caps, selectively activate RIG-I and have been incorporated into nanoparticle-based vaccines to enhance antitumor immunity without excessive inflammation.
Certain RNA modifications allow evasion of RLR detection, underscoring the receptors' specificity for uncapped or unstructured motifs. Host capping enzymes add 5' monophosphate caps and 2'-O-methylation to cellular mRNAs, preventing RIG-I recognition, whereas many viruses lack these modifications on their RNAs. Phosphorylation of RNA ends or base modifications like N6-methyladenosine can further attenuate ligand potency, as seen in circular RNAs that reduce RIG-I binding during viral infections.
RLR activation requires ligands to meet detection thresholds, including minimum duplex lengths and end structures to overcome autoinhibitory states. RIG-I favors short dsRNAs (19300 bp) with 5'ppp or 5'dp and blunt or 5'-overhanging ends, while MDA5 requires longer dsRNAs (>1 kb) for cooperative filament formation, ensuring discriminatory sensing of viral versus host RNAs. Family members exhibit distinct preferences for these features, as detailed in subsequent sections on ligand specificity.

Ligand Specificity Across Family Members

The RIG-I-like receptors (RLRs) exhibit distinct ligand specificities that enable non-redundant sensing of viral RNAs, with RIG-I primarily recognizing short double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) molecules bearing 5'-triphosphate (5'-ppp) or 5'-diphosphate ends, typically less than 300 base pairs in length and a minimum of 8-13 base pairs. This preference allows RIG-I to detect replication intermediates from negative-sense RNA viruses such as Sendai virus, vesicular stomatitis virus (VSV), influenza A, and respiratory syncytial virus (RSV). Experimental evidence from structural studies, including X-ray crystallography of RIG-I bound to 5'-ppp RNA, confirms that these termini are critical for high-affinity binding and subsequent interferon- (IFN-) signaling activation.
In contrast, MDA5 specializes in longer dsRNAs exceeding 300 base pairs, such as replicative intermediates or synthetic analogs like polyinosinic:polycytidylic acid (poly(I:C)), without requiring specific 5' modifications like triphosphate groups. This specificity targets positive-sense RNA viruses, including picornaviruses like encephalomyocarditis virus (EMCV) and coxsackievirus, as well as arteriviruses and Dengue virus. Filament formation assays demonstrate MDA5's cooperative binding to long dsRNA, enabling signal amplification, while digestion of poly(I:C) with RNase III converts it from an MDA5 ligand to a RIG-I agonist, underscoring the length-dependent discrimination.
LGP2 displays broader, lower-affinity binding to blunt-ended dsRNA termini without strict length preferences, positioning it as a modulator rather than a direct sensor. It enhances MDA5 signaling for ligands like poly(I:C) by facilitating RNA recruitment and filament assembly, as shown in ATPase domain-dependent assays where LGP2 overexpression potentiates IFN induction in MDA5-expressing cells. Conversely, LGP2 inhibits RIG-I responses to short 5'-ppp RNAs through competitive sequestration, evidenced by reduced IFN- production in co-transfection experiments with VSV RNA.
Despite these distinctions, overlaps exist, enabling cooperative detection during mixed infections; for instance, Dengue virus and reoviruses engage both RIG-I and MDA5, with individual siRNA knockdowns yielding partial defects but double knockdowns abolishing IFN responses entirely. Similarly, poly(I:C) variants can activate multiple RLRs depending on processing, highlighting redundancies that ensure robust antiviral sensing.

Activation and Signaling

Activation Process

In the resting state, RIG-I and MDA5 exist in an autoinhibited conformation within the cytoplasm, where interactions between the helicase domain and the C-terminal repressor domain (RD) mask the N-terminal caspase activation and recruitment domains (CARDs), preventing spurious signaling. This closed structure is further stabilized by phosphorylation on specific residues in the CARDs and RD, maintaining latency until pathogen detection. LGP2, lacking CARDs, adopts a similar autoinhibited pose but serves primarily as a modulator rather than a direct activator.
Upon ligand binding, viral RNAsuch as 5'-triphosphate or short double-stranded RNA for RIG-I, and longer double-stranded RNA for MDA5is recognized by the RD and helicase domain, inducing a conformational shift that disrupts the autoinhibitory interface and exposes the CARDs. This RNA engagement, coupled with ATP binding and hydrolysis by the helicase motifs, further remodels the protein structure, promoting CARD accessibility and initiating downstream events. For LGP2, ligand binding similarly relieves autoinhibition but does not lead to CARD exposure due to its absence, instead influencing RIG-I or MDA5 activity.
Activated RLRs then undergo oligomerization to amplify signaling: RIG-I assembles into filaments upon ligand and ATP engagement, while MDA5 assembles into extended filaments along long dsRNA scaffolds, enhancing cooperative activation. These oligomeric complexes serve as platforms for CARD exposure. To ensure a signaling threshold, K63-linked ubiquitination acts as a critical gate; for instance, TRIM25 ubiquitinates lysine 63 on RIG-I's first CARD, stabilizing the oligomer and facilitating recruitment. The exposed, ubiquitinated CARDs of RIG-I or MDA5 then dock onto mitochondrial antiviral-signaling protein (MAVS) via CARD-CARD homotypic interactions at mitochondrial membranes, priming signal initiation without propagating the full cascade.

Downstream Signaling Pathways

Upon activation by viral RNA ligands, RIG-I-like receptors (RLRs) such as RIG-I and MDA5 recruit the central adaptor protein mitochondrial antiviral-signaling protein (MAVS), also independently identified as IPS-1, VISA, and CARDIF in seminal studies from 2005. MAVS, localized to the outer mitochondrial membrane, serves as a scaffold forming prion-like aggregates that propagate the signal through interactions with TRAF family adaptor proteins. This recruitment initiates the canonical downstream signaling cascades, distinct from upstream ligand recognition.
In the canonical interferon (IFN) pathway, MAVS engages TRAF3 to activate the kinase complex TBK1/IKK, which phosphorylates interferon regulatory factors IRF3 and IRF7. Phosphorylated IRF3 and IRF7 dimerize, translocate to the nucleus, and drive transcription of type I IFNs (IFN- and IFN-) and type III IFNs. Concurrently, the NF-B arm branches from MAVS via TRAF6 recruitment, leading to activation of IKK, which phosphorylates IB and releases NF-B for nuclear translocation. This results in transcription of proinflammatory cytokines such as IL-6 and TNF-, amplifying the immune response.
Non-canonical pathways include crosstalk with the NLRP3 inflammasome, where MAVS-mediated mitochondrial damage and reactive oxygen species production promote NLRP3 assembly and IL-1 secretion. Additionally, RIG-I can directly induce apoptosis in certain viral infections through CARD domain interactions, independent of IFN signaling. These pathways provide alternative outputs beyond type I IFN induction.
The primary outputs of RLR signaling are the induction of interferon-stimulated genes (ISGs), such as protein kinase R (PKR) and 2'-5'-oligoadenylate synthetase (OAS), which establish an antiviral state by inhibiting viral replication and modulating host metabolism. The amplitude and duration of these responses are finely tuned through MAVS aggregation dynamics and downstream feedback, ensuring balanced immunity.

Regulation of RLR Activity

Positive Regulators

Positive regulators of RIG-I-like receptors (RLRs) encompass a variety of host proteins and modifications that enhance RLR sensing of viral RNA, promote oligomerization, and amplify downstream signaling to bolster antiviral immunity. These mechanisms ensure robust activation of pathways leading to type I interferon production without delving into the detailed downstream cascades. Key among them are E3 ubiquitin ligases that facilitate K63-linked ubiquitination, a non-degradative modification critical for RLR conformational changes and interactions with adaptor proteins like MAVS.
TRIM25, an E3 ubiquitin ligase, positively regulates RIG-I by catalyzing K63-linked ubiquitination at lysine 172 within the first caspase activation and recruitment domain (CARD), which stabilizes RIG-I tetramers and enables their recruitment to MAVS on mitochondrial membranes. This modification is essential for RIG-I-mediated interferon induction during infections by viruses such as influenza A and Sendai virus. Similarly, TRIM25 supports MDA5 activation, though to a lesser extent, by promoting its CARD ubiquitination. RNF135, also known as Riplet, further enhances RIG-I signaling through K63-linked ubiquitination of CARD and C-terminal domains, stabilizing filaments and amplifying antiviral responses against diverse RNA viruses like paramyxoviruses.
Chaperone proteins and post-translational modifications provide additional layers of positive regulation. The 14-3-3 family of chaperones binds RIG-I and MDA5, facilitating their oligomerization, mitochondrial translocation, and membrane association to enhance MAVS engagement and interferon-beta production. SUMOylation, mediated by the E3 ligase TRIM38, modifies RIG-I at lysines 43 and 865, and MDA5 at lysines 96 and 888, protecting these sensors from proteasomal degradation and temporally fine-tuning their activity for effective viral clearance. Phospholipid interactions at organelles, such as sphingomyelin- and cholesterol-enriched microdomains on mitochondria and peroxisomes, further promote RLR-MAVS aggregation, stabilizing signaling complexes during RLR activation.
Transcriptional enhancers like IRF1 and IRF3 amplify RLR activity through positive feedback loops induced by initial interferon signaling, upregulating RLR gene expression to prime cells for enhanced RNA sensing in subsequent viral encounters. For instance, IRF3-driven type I interferons induce IRF1 expression, which in turn boosts RIG-I and MDA5 transcription as interferon-stimulated genes.

Negative Regulators and Feedback Mechanisms

Deubiquitinating enzymes (DUBs) play a critical role in negatively regulating RLR signaling by removing K63-linked polyubiquitin chains essential for receptor activation and downstream signal propagation. For instance, the deubiquitinase DUBA (also known as OTUD5) targets TRAF3, a key adaptor in the RLR pathway, by cleaving K63-linked ubiquitins, thereby inhibiting type I IFN production and preventing excessive antiviral responses. Similarly, CYLD deubiquitinates RIG-I at lysine 181, disrupting its interaction with MAVS and suppressing IRF3 activation and IFN- expression during viral infection. Other DUBs, such as USP21, further contribute by directly deubiquitinating RIG-I's CARD domain, reducing its ability to oligomerize and signal through MAVS.
Phosphatases provide an additional layer of negative control by reversing phosphorylation events that sustain signaling. Protein phosphatase 2A (PP2A), recruited by the adaptor RACK1, dephosphorylates activated IRF3 at key serine residues (e.g., Ser396), promoting its inactivation and nuclear export, which terminates type I IFN transcription and restores homeostasis in the RLR pathway. This mechanism ensures timely resolution of the antiviral response, preventing chronic inflammation.
Feedback loops involving IFN-inducible factors maintain balance by suppressing RLR activity post-activation. Type I IFNs induce expression of suppressors of cytokine signaling (SOCS1 and SOCS3), which inhibit JAK-STAT signaling upstream of RLRs, reducing RIG-I and MDA5 transcription and dampening further IFN amplification in a negative feedback manner. LGP2, another RLR family member lacking signaling domains, acts as an endogenous inhibitor by competing for viral RNA ligands with RIG-I and MDA5, thereby attenuating RIG-I-mediated IFN responses while sometimes enhancing MDA5 signaling at low expression levels.
Organelle-specific regulators localize to mitochondria to block MAVS function. NLRX1, a mitochondrial NOD-like receptor, binds directly to MAVS's CARD domain, preventing its association with RIG-I and promoting MAVS degradation via autophagy, which suppresses NF-B and IRF3 activation during viral infection. Autophagy itself degrades activated MAVS aggregates through selective mitophagy, involving Atg5-Atg12 conjugates that intercalate between RIG-I and MAVS, thereby inhibiting signalosome formation and resolving RLR signaling.
In pathogen-free steady states, nitric oxide (NO)-mediated S-nitrosylation inhibits RIG-I to avoid basal activation. NO covalently modifies cysteine 31 in RIG-I's CARD domain via S-nitrosylation, blocking its ubiquitination and interaction with MAVS, thus maintaining low-level IFN production and immune quiescence.

Physiological Roles

Antiviral Immunity

RIG-I-like receptors (RLRs), including RIG-I, MDA5, and LGP2, serve as primary cytosolic sensors for detecting viral RNA in non-immune cells such as epithelial cells and fibroblasts, enabling rapid initiation of innate antiviral responses. Upon recognition of pathogen-associated molecular patterns like 5'-triphosphate RNA or double-stranded RNA, RLRs undergo conformational changes that activate mitochondrial antiviral-signaling protein (MAVS), leading to the production of type I interferons (IFNs) and proinflammatory cytokines. This IFN response not only establishes an antiviral state in infected and neighboring cells but also bridges innate and adaptive immunity by promoting dendritic cell maturation and T cell priming.
Different RLR family members exhibit preferences for specific viral ligands, contributing to tailored antiviral defenses against diverse RNA viruses. RIG-I predominantly senses short, blunt-ended double-stranded RNAs with 5'-triphosphate ends from negative-sense RNA viruses, such as orthomyxoviruses including influenza A virus, where it detects the panhandle structures of viral genomic RNA to trigger IFN production and limit viral replication. In contrast, MDA5 recognizes long, double-stranded RNAs from positive-sense RNA viruses, playing a key role in controlling caliciviruses like murine norovirus by inducing IFN responses that restrict viral spread in intestinal epithelial cells. For coronaviruses, both RIG-I and MDA5 contribute to detection, with studies showing RLR activation against SARS-CoV-2; however, variants like Delta potently suppress the RLR pathway and evade IFN responses.
Activation of RLR signaling in infected cells culminates in cellular outcomes that curtail viral propagation, including the induction of apoptosis to eliminate virus reservoirs and the enhancement of natural killer (NK) cell activity through IFN-dependent mechanisms. Type I IFNs produced downstream of RLRs upregulate proapoptotic genes like TRAIL and Noxa, promoting caspase-mediated cell death specifically in virally infected cells while sparing uninfected ones. Additionally, these IFNs activate NK cells by increasing their cytotoxic granule content and IFN- secretion, enabling targeted lysis of infected cells and amplification of the antiviral response.
In vivo evidence underscores the critical role of RLRs in human antiviral immunity, as loss-of-function mutations in MDA5 (encoded by IFIH1) lead to severe immunodeficiency and increased susceptibility to RNA viruses like rhinoviruses and enteroviruses. For instance, rare human MDA5 deficiencies have been linked to recurrent viral infections and impaired IFN responses, highlighting the receptor's essential function in host defense. Single nucleotide polymorphisms in RIG-I have been associated with altered immune responses to certain viruses, such as enterovirus 71. Emerging research suggests RIG-I may also contribute to control of some DNA viruses like Kaposi's sarcoma-associated herpesvirus (KSHV), though this requires further confirmation. Furthermore, synthetic RLR agonists, mimicking viral RNAs like 5'-triphosphate oligonucleotides, have been developed as vaccine adjuvants to boost IFN production and enhance antibody and T cell responses against influenza and other viruses in clinical trials. Evolutionarily, RLRs have adapted for broad RNA virus control in lower vertebrates; in fish, expanded RLR repertoires, including multiple RIG-I and MDA5 paralogs, provide robust IFN induction against diverse aquatic RNA viruses, reflecting an ancient arms race between hosts and pathogens.

Roles in Inflammation and Autoimmunity

RIG-I-like receptors (RLRs) can be activated by endogenous nucleic acids, such as mitochondrial DNA and RNA, which serve as danger signals in the absence of infection. In Aicardi-Goutires syndrome (AGS), a monogenic interferonopathy, mutations in genes like TREX1 or ADAR1 lead to accumulation of self-nucleic acids that aberrantly stimulate RLRs, particularly MDA5, triggering excessive type I interferon (IFN) production and neuroinflammation. This endogenous activation mimics viral infection, resulting in constitutive RLR signaling that drives interferonopathies characterized by elevated IFN signatures in cerebrospinal fluid and peripheral blood.
Aberrant RLR activation is implicated in several autoimmune diseases through gain-of-function mutations or dysregulated signaling. Gain-of-function mutations in the IFIH1 gene encoding MDA5 cause Singleton-Merten syndrome (SMS), an autoinflammatory disorder featuring aortic calcification, dental dysplasia, and glaucoma due to enhanced MDA5 sensitivity to self-RNA, leading to overproduction of type I IFNs and proinflammatory cytokines. Similarly, in Sjgren's syndrome, upregulated RIG-I expression in plasmacytoid dendritic cells correlates with disease activity and autoantibody production, contributing to glandular inflammation via sustained IFN responses. These genetic and expression alterations highlight RLRs' role in breaking immune tolerance to self-antigens.
RLRs contribute to non-infectious inflammation through activation of NF-B pathways and crosstalk with other sensors like STING, potentiating type I IFN responses to self-nucleic acids and exacerbating chronic inflammatory states in autoimmune tissues.
In chronic autoimmune conditions like rheumatoid arthritis (RA), sustained MDA5 signaling in synovial fibroblasts perpetuates inflammation by inducing chemokine expression, such as CXCL10, which recruits immune cells and maintains synovial hyperplasia. This persistent activation, often triggered by endogenous dsRNA from cellular stress, leads to upregulated IFN-inducible genes and matrix-degrading enzymes, contributing to joint destruction without viral involvement.
Therapeutic strategies targeting RLRs hold promise for mitigating autoimmune flares, with preclinical development of small-molecule inhibitors like those blocking MDA5-ATP binding showing efficacy in reducing IFN production in SMS and AGS models. JAK inhibitors, which indirectly dampen downstream IFN signaling, are approved for interferonopathies and RA. Direct RLR antagonists remain in preclinical or early investigational stages as of November 2025. Negative regulators, such as those detailed in feedback mechanisms, further inform these inhibitor designs to prevent overactivation.

Pathological Implications

Viral Evasion Strategies

Viruses have evolved diverse mechanisms to evade detection and signaling by RIG-I-like receptors (RLRs), thereby suppressing type I interferon (IFN) production and facilitating viral replication within host cells. These strategies target various components of the RLR pathway, including the sensors RIG-I and MDA5, their adaptor MAVS, and the viral RNA ligands themselves, reflecting an ongoing evolutionary arms race between pathogens and host immunity.
Direct inhibition of RLRs represents a common evasion tactic, where viral proteins bind to RLR domains to prevent activation. For instance, the NS1 protein of influenza A virus binds to the C-terminal domain (CTD) of RIG-I, inhibiting its interaction with viral RNA and subsequent ubiquitination by TRIM25, thus blocking downstream signaling. Similarly, the 2A protease of coxsackievirus B3 cleaves MDA5, disrupting its ability to form filaments and sense double-stranded RNA, which impairs IFN induction during infection. Other examples include the V protein of paramyxoviruses, which inhibits MDA5 ATPase activity and filament assembly to evade detection.
Viruses also target MAVS to disrupt signal transduction from RLRs to IRF3 activation. The NS3/4A protease of hepatitis C virus (HCV) cleaves MAVS at a specific site on the mitochondrial outer membrane, preventing its aggregation and oligomerization essential for signaling. These disruptions highlight MAVS as a vulnerable nexus in the pathway.
Ligand masking strategies allow viruses to hide their RNA from RLR recognition. Coronaviruses, such as SARS-CoV-2, cap their viral RNAs at the 5' end with a 7-methylguanosine structure, mimicking host mRNA and evading RIG-I's detection of 5' triphosphate (5'ppp) ends. Flaviviruses employ subgenomic flaviviral RNAs (sfRNAs) as decoys that bind and sequester RIG-I or MDA5, while also promoting 2'-O-methylation of viral RNA to mask pathogen-associated molecular patterns. Additionally, the N protein of SARS-CoV-2 masks viral RNA ligands and inhibits MAVS polyubiquitination, further dampening RLR activation.
Recent studies on emerging viruses illustrate sophisticated evasion mechanisms. In SARS-CoV-2, the ORF9b protein targets TOM70, a mitochondrial import receptor, to induce mitophagy and degrade MAVS, effectively blocking RLR signaling; this was demonstrated in multiple investigations from 2020 onward, underscoring its role in immune suppression during COVID-19. The SARS-CoV-2 Nsp5 protease employs dual mechanisms, cleaving RIG-I and promoting MAVS ubiquitination and degradation, as shown in structural and functional assays. For Ebola virus, the VP35 protein binds double-stranded RNA to prevent RIG-I activation and directly inhibits IRF3 phosphorylation by sequestering TBK1, allowing unchecked viral propagation.
This viral-host antagonism exemplifies an evolutionary arms race, where viruses encode proteins that mimic host regulators or act as decoys to subvert RLR pathways. For example, some viruses produce RLR-like pseudo-enzymes that deamidate key residues to inhibit signaling, while others deploy RNA decoys to titrate RLRs away from genuine viral genomes, adaptations honed over millennia of co-evolution.

Dysregulation in Disease

Dysregulation of RIG-I-like receptors (RLRs), including RIG-I and MDA5, contributes to various non-viral diseases by altering innate immune responses, leading to either insufficient activation or pathological hyperactivation. In cancer, RLR signaling can be suppressed to evade immunosurveillance, while agonists targeting these pathways show promise as antitumor agents. For instance, small-molecule RIG-I agonists like MK-4621 have demonstrated modest antitumor activity in phase I trials for advanced solid tumors when used alone or combined with pembrolizumab, inducing type I interferon responses and immune cell infiltration. Similarly, in lung cancer, redox-mediated suppression of MDA5 expression promotes a T cell-exclusion microenvironment, enhancing resistance to immunotherapy by dampening interferon signaling and innate immune detection of tumor-derived RNA.
Genetic defects impairing RLR function increase susceptibility to severe infections, as seen in herpes simplex encephalitis (HSE). Compound heterozygous loss-of-function mutations in GTF3A disrupt the biogenesis of the host-derived RIG-I ligand RNA5SP141, resulting in diminished RIG-I activation upon HSV-1 infection and heightened HSE risk in affected individuals. This leads to abrogated antiviral responses in neural cells, highlighting RLRs' critical role in central nervous system immunity.
In autoinflammatory and metabolic disorders, RLR hyperactivation by self-RNA drives excessive inflammation. In psoriasis, reduced A-to-I RNA editing by ADAR1 causes accumulation of double-stranded self-RNA, which activates MDA5 and triggers type I interferon production in keratinocytes, perpetuating lesional inflammation. Likewise, elevated RIG-I and MDA5 expression in psoriatic skin correlates with synthetic ligand-induced IL-23 production, exacerbating Th17-driven pathology. In inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), RLR-NLR crosstalk amplifies gut inflammation; for example, NLRX1 negatively regulates RIG-I/MAVS interactions to limit excessive responses, and its dysregulation may contribute to chronic colitis by failing to curb RLR-mediated interferon signaling in intestinal epithelia.
Therapeutic modulation of RLRs offers potential for treating these conditions. RIG-I activators, such as RGT100 (also known as MK-4621), were evaluated in phase I/II clinical trials (NCT03065023, completed in 2019) for advanced solid tumors including melanoma, demonstrating enhancement of antitumor immunity with tolerable safety but modest efficacy. Research as of 2025 continues to explore novel RIG-I agonists, including combinations with checkpoint inhibitors, to overcome RLR suppression in resistant cancers.
Aging further dysregulates RLR function, contributing to increased viral susceptibility in the elderly through immunosenescence. Monocytes from older individuals show impaired RIG-I and MDA5 signaling, resulting in reduced type I interferon secretion and diminished antiviral defenses, which correlates with higher infection rates. Emerging evidence as of 2025 links chronic RLR dysregulation to long COVID pathogenesis, including persistent neuroinflammation, and potential contributions to neurodegenerative diseases via aberrant type I IFN signaling.
