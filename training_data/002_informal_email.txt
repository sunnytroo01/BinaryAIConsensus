Writing an informal email is about connection. It is the digital equivalent of sitting down with someone you care about, having a coffee, and catching up on life. Unlike formal emails, which follow rigid structural rules and prioritize professionalism above all else, informal emails prioritize warmth, personality, and genuine human connection. They are the emails you send to friends, family members, close colleagues, former classmates, and anyone else with whom you share a comfortable, established relationship.

The purpose of an informal email can be anything. You might want to share exciting news. You might want to make plans for the weekend. You might want to recommend a book, a restaurant, or a television show. You might want to check in on someone who has been going through a difficult time. You might want to tell a funny story about something that happened to you. You might want to ask for advice on a personal matter. You might simply want to say hello because you have been thinking about someone and want them to know they are on your mind. Whatever the reason, the goal is always the same: to strengthen the bond between you and the other person through honest, natural, heartfelt communication.

The greeting in an informal email should feel like the way you would actually greet the person in real life. If you always say "Hey" when you see your friend, start with "Hey." If you call your sister by a nickname, use the nickname. If you are writing to an old college roommate you have not spoken to in years, you might start with something warmer and more nostalgic. Here are many examples of informal greetings that work well in different situations:

Hey Sarah, what is going on? Hi Tom, long time no talk! Hello beautiful, I have been thinking about you. Hey buddy, guess what happened to me today. Hi Mom, just a quick note to say I love you. Hey stranger, where have you been hiding? Hi there, hope your week is going well so far. Hey team, quick update from my end. Yo Marcus, you are not going to believe this. Hi love, missing you already. Hey neighbor, got a question for you. Hi old friend, it has been way too long.

Notice how each of these greetings immediately establishes the nature of the relationship and the tone of the conversation. They feel natural. They feel human. That is exactly what you want.

The opening of an informal email should pull the reader in immediately. Think of it as the first few seconds of a phone call. You want the other person to be glad they picked up. The best informal openings do one of three things: they share something exciting, they ask a genuine question, or they reference a shared experience. Here are examples of each approach:

Sharing something exciting: I just got the most incredible news and you are literally the first person I am telling. You are never going to guess what showed up in my mailbox this morning. So remember that job I applied for three months ago and completely forgot about? They called me yesterday. I just booked flights to Tokyo for October and I am absolutely losing my mind with excitement.

Asking a genuine question: How are you really doing? I know things have been tough lately and I want you to know I am here. Did you ever end up going on that camping trip you were planning? Have you seen the new season of that show we were watching together? What is the name of that restaurant you took me to last summer, the one with the amazing pasta?

Referencing a shared experience: Remember when we got lost in Barcelona and ended up at that tiny bar where the old man played guitar all night? I was thinking about that today and it made me smile so hard. I drove past our old school today and the weirdest wave of nostalgia hit me. I was cooking dinner last night and I accidentally made that terrible soup we used to survive on in college. It is still terrible.

The body of an informal email is where you really get to be yourself. This is not a business document. This is a conversation. Write the way you talk. Use contractions freely. Let your sentences flow naturally. Tell stories. Share details. Be specific. The difference between a boring informal email and one that makes the reader smile and want to respond immediately is specificity. Compare these two versions of the same message:

Boring version: I went on vacation last week. It was nice. The weather was good and the food was great. I had a relaxing time and came back feeling refreshed.

Engaging version: So I finally took that trip to the coast I have been talking about for months. We drove down on Thursday morning and the highway was completely empty, which never happens, so we made it there in under three hours. The cabin was this tiny wooden thing right on the cliff overlooking the ocean. I am talking you could hear the waves from the bed. The first morning I woke up at six without an alarm, which is unheard of for me, and I just sat on the porch with a cup of coffee watching the fog burn off the water. We did absolutely nothing productive the entire weekend. We cooked simple meals, read books, went on long walks along the beach, and watched the sunset paint the sky in these ridiculous shades of pink and orange every single evening. I came back feeling like a completely different person. I think my soul needed that trip more than I realized.

See the difference? The second version puts the reader right there with you. They can see the cabin, hear the waves, feel the morning air, taste the coffee. That is good informal writing. It is generous with detail and it makes the reader feel included in your experience.

One of the most important elements of informal email writing is asking questions. A one-sided email, no matter how entertaining, feels like a monologue. Questions show that you care about the other person and want to hear from them. They invite a response. They keep the conversation alive. And they should be genuine questions, not throwaway lines. Instead of "How are you?" try something more specific. "How is the new apartment treating you? Did you ever get that leaky faucet fixed?" Instead of "What is new?" try "What have you been reading lately? I need a good book recommendation." Instead of "Everything good?" try "How did your mom's surgery go? I have been thinking about her all week."

Making plans through informal email is another common and important skill. The key is to be specific enough to be useful but casual enough to not sound like you are issuing a formal invitation. Here are examples that strike the right balance:

A few of us are getting together at Jake's place on Saturday afternoon for a barbecue. Nothing fancy, just burgers and good company. You should totally come. Bring whoever you want. We are planning to be there from around two o'clock onward.

I have been craving Thai food all week. Are you free for dinner on Wednesday or Thursday? There is that new place on Fifth Street that everyone keeps talking about and I have been dying to try it. My treat if you pick the wine.

Want to go for a hike this weekend? I found this trail about forty minutes north of the city that is supposed to have incredible views of the valley. It is about five miles round trip, moderate difficulty. I was thinking we could head out early Saturday morning, do the hike, and then grab brunch somewhere on the way back.

I am hosting a movie night next Friday. I have a projector now so we can watch on the wall in the living room. I am thinking we do a double feature of something classic. Maybe The Princess Bride and Jurassic Park. I will make popcorn and those brownies you like. Let me know if you can make it.

Sharing recommendations is a cornerstone of close relationships, and informal emails are a perfect vehicle for them. When you recommend something, do not just name it. Explain why you love it and why you think the other person specifically would enjoy it. Here are examples:

You absolutely have to read this book called The Midnight Library by Matt Haig. It is about a woman who gets to visit alternate versions of her life, like what if she had made different choices at every major crossroads. I know that sounds like science fiction but it is really more of a meditation on regret and possibility and what makes a life worth living. I thought of you the entire time I was reading it because you are always talking about the road not taken.

I discovered this podcast called Hardcore History and I think it might change your life. The episodes are like four hours long each, which sounds insane, but the host tells history like it is a thriller novel. He did this series on the Mongol Empire that had me sitting in my car in the driveway for thirty minutes because I could not turn it off. You know how much you love history documentaries. This is that times a thousand.

Okay I know you think you do not like Thai food but I need you to trust me on this. Go to that new place called Lemongrass on Maple Avenue and order the massaman curry with chicken. It is not spicy at all. It is creamy and rich and a little sweet and it has potatoes and peanuts in it. It tastes like a warm hug. If you do not like it I will personally apologize and buy you a pizza.

The closing of an informal email should feel like the end of a good conversation. It should leave the reader feeling warm, appreciated, and eager to respond. Here are many examples of natural informal closings:

Miss you tons. Let us catch up soon. Talk to you later, and give the kids a hug from me. Can not wait to hear what you think. Write back when you get a chance, no rush. Love you, and I mean it. Sending you the biggest hug. Keep being amazing. Take care of yourself, and I mean really take care of yourself. Until next time, my friend. Looking forward to seeing you soon. Let me know what you decide and we will make it happen.

The best informal emails leave the reader with a smile. They feel like gifts. They remind people that someone out there is thinking of them, cares about their life, and wants to stay connected. In a world of endless notifications and shallow digital interactions, a thoughtful informal email stands out like a handwritten letter in a mailbox full of junk mail. It says: you matter to me, and I want you to know it.
