Silene latifolia
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Silene latifolia, commonly known as white campion or bladder campion, is a dioecious, short-lived perennial or biennial herb in the Caryophyllaceae family, characterized by erect, hairy stems up to 1 meter tall, opposite lanceolate leaves 410 cm long, and showy white flowers that bloom nocturnally in open, glandular cymes.
Native to Europe, S. latifolia has been widely introduced and naturalized across North America, where it thrives in disturbed ruderal habitats such as roadsides, fields, and waste areas, often behaving as an invasive weed that reproduces primarily by seed, producing up to 24,000 seeds per plant.
The species exhibits sexual dimorphism, with male and female flowers on separate plants, and its flowers release volatile scents predominantly at night to attract moth pollinators.
Silene latifolia serves as a key model organism in evolutionary biology due to its heteromorphic sex chromosomes of relatively recent origin (1020 million years ago), featuring a giant Y chromosome approximately 550 megabases in size that has undergone significant structural evolution, including accumulation of transposable elements and gene loss.

Taxonomy and description

Taxonomy

Silene latifolia is classified in the kingdom Plantae, clade Tracheophytes, clade Angiosperms, clade Eudicots, order Caryophyllales, family Caryophyllaceae, genus Silene, and species S. latifolia Poir. (1789).
The species is known by several synonyms, including Lychnis alba Mill., Melandrium album (Mill.) Garcke, Lychnis vespertina Sibth., and Melandrium album (Mill.) Garcke.
Recognized subspecies include S. latifolia subsp. alba (Mill.) Greuter & Burdet, distinguished by short, obtuse calyx teeth and erect capsule teeth, and the nominotypical S. latifolia subsp. latifolia, with acuminate calyx teeth and patent to recurved capsule teeth. Many North American plants are intermediate, and some authorities do not recognize the subspecies.
The genus Silene encompasses over 700 species worldwide, with S. latifolia closely related to S. dioica, the two forming natural hybrids in areas of sympatry. Dioecy serves as a key taxonomic feature within the genus.

Morphological description

Silene latifolia is a biennial or short-lived perennial herb in the Caryophyllaceae family, characterized by swollen nodes typical of the pink family. It grows 30100 cm tall from a thick woody taproot, with stems that are erect to ascending, branched, and covered in fine hairs (hirsute) along with glandular pubescence.
The leaves are opposite, lanceolate to oval in shape, measuring 410 cm long and 13 cm wide, arranged in basal rosettes or along cauline pairs with ciliate margins.
As a dioecious species, S. latifolia produces separate male and female flowers, which are typically white but occasionally pink in hybrids. The flowers measure 2.53 cm in diameter, featuring five petals with notched tips arising from an inflated, bladder-like calyx 1.52 cm long; male flowers form loose cymes, while female flowers appear in smaller clusters. They bloom from late spring to early autumn and emit a mild evening fragrance.
The fruit is a capsule that dehisces into 10 teeth, containing numerous reniform seeds up to 24,000 per plant; the seeds are 11.5 mm long, dark brown, and tuberculate. The plant exhibits dense hairiness overall and demonstrates cold tolerance down to -20C when dormant.

Distribution and habitat

Geographic distribution

Silene latifolia is native to most of Europe, extending from the Baltic States and Russia in the north to the Mediterranean region in the south, as well as western Asia up to Iran and northern Africa from Morocco to Tunisia. In its native range, the species is commonly found at elevations up to 400 meters in some regions, though it can occur higher in certain areas.
The species has been introduced and naturalized in several regions outside its native range, primarily through human activities such as ship ballast in the early 19th century. In North America, it is widespread across north-central and northeastern United States, all Canadian provinces including Alaska, and extends to elevations from sea level to 2800 meters; it is also reported in parts of South America, Australia, New Zealand, and East Asia including China and Japan.
In introduced areas, S. latifolia exhibits expansion patterns particularly in disturbed habitats such as roadsides, fields, and waste places, where it readily establishes and spreads. In its native ecoregions, such as temperate Eurasian forests, populations remain relatively stable.
Globally, S. latifolia is not considered endangered and holds a NatureServe global rank of GNR (not ranked), with no protections under frameworks like the U.S. Endangered Species Act or Canada's COSEWIC. However, it is regarded as invasive or a weed in some introduced regions, such as U.S. hayfields and disturbed agricultural lands.

Habitat preferences

Silene latifolia thrives in well-drained soils ranging from sandy to clayey, with a preference for neutral to alkaline conditions and a pH range of 6.1 to 8.2. It tolerates mildly acidic soils but performs best in moderately fertile substrates that avoid waterlogging, resenting winter wet.
The plant favors full sun exposure, though it can endure partial shade, and it does not grow in deep shade. It is commonly associated with open, disturbed sites such as roadsides, arable fields, hedges, wasteland, and ruderal habitats, while avoiding dense forests and persistently wet soils.
Adapted to temperate climates, S. latifolia is hardy in USDA zones 3 to 8, demonstrating tolerance to a range of temperatures from cold winters to moderate summers. Seeds exhibit dormancy and can persist in soil for up to 20 years, retaining low viability (1-2%) after that duration. The species is prevalent in anthropogenic landscapes and, in overlap zones, interbreeds with S. dioica.

Reproduction and genetics

Flowering and pollination

Silene latifolia exhibits a flowering phenology from late spring through early autumn, typically blooming between May and September in its introduced North American range, with flowers opening nocturnally to align with moth activity. The nocturnal anthesis is accompanied by an evening fragrance dominated by lilac aldehydes, which serve as key attractants for pollinating moths.
As a dioecious species with separate male and female plants, S. latifolia relies on cross-pollination and is not self-fertile, promoting outcrossing through its sexual system. Primary pollination is mediated by nocturnal moths, particularly species in the genus Hadena such as H. bicruris, which visit flowers for nectar and inadvertently transfer pollen between plants; both male and female moths contribute to pollination efficiency, with female moths fertilizing an average of 154 ovules per visit. Occasional diurnal pollinators, including bees and other insects, provide supplementary service, though nocturnal moths account for the majority of reproductive success.
Female plants produce capsules containing numerous small, tuberculate seeds, with a single plant capable of yielding up to 24,000 seeds under favorable conditions. Seed dispersal occurs primarily via wind due to their lightweight nature, though the rough tuberculate surface may facilitate attachment to animals for secondary zoochory; this high fecundity contributes to the species' proliferation in disturbed habitats. Where ranges overlap with the congener S. dioica, S. latifolia forms fertile hybrids exhibiting intermediate pink flowers, resulting from occasional interspecific pollen transfer.

Sex determination and chromosomes

Silene latifolia is a dioecious plant species characterized by an XY sex-determination system, where females possess two X chromosomes (XX) and males have one X and one Y chromosome (XY), making males the heterogametic sex. This system evolved relatively recently from a pair of autosomes, with the divergence between the X and Y chromosomes estimated at approximately 10 million years ago.
The species has a diploid chromosome number of 2n=24, consisting of 11 pairs of autosomes and one pair of sex chromosomes. The Y chromosome is notably enlarged, measuring about 550 megabases (Mb) in size, which is substantially larger than the X chromosome at approximately 400 Mb, contributing to a total haploid genome size of around 2.7 gigabases (Gb). This size disparity arises from the Y chromosome's accumulation of repetitive sequences and transposable elements following the cessation of recombination in non-homologous regions.
Recombination between the X and Y chromosomes is confined to a pseudoautosomal region (PAR) at their ends, while it is suppressed along the differentiated portions, leading to the evolution of distinct chromosomal regions. Genetic analyses have identified three evolutionary strata on the X and Y chromosomes, reflecting successive episodes of recombination suppression and progressive degeneration of Y-linked genes, with the oldest stratum showing the highest sequence divergence. Dosage compensation on the X chromosome is incomplete, as evidenced by transcriptomic studies showing only partial upregulation of X-linked gene expression in males to balance autosomal levels.
Recent genomic advancements have provided deeper insights into this system's evolution. In February 2025, the full sequencing of the S. latifolia Y chromosome revealed its rapid expansion driven primarily by the proliferation of transposable elements, which constitute over 80% of its length and contribute to structural instability. A August 2025 study using super-resolution microscopy demonstrated dynamic meiotic pairing configurations between the X and Y chromosomes, including multivalent associations that ensure proper segregation despite their heteromorphy. Additionally, a 2025 analysis outlined stratified trajectories of sex chromosome heteromorphy, highlighting how stepwise recombination suppression has shaped gene content and function across strata. This XY system promotes outcrossing, thereby aiding inbreeding avoidance.

Inbreeding avoidance

In Silene latifolia, inbreeding depression manifests as reduced progeny fitness from selfed or closely related crosses, primarily impacting female growth, seed set, and survival rates. Studies have quantified this through controlled crosses, revealing inbreeding coefficients () of approximately 14% in females and 22% in males, driven largely by lower germination success and delayed flowering. For instance, inbred offspring exhibit slower early growth, with fewer leaves and shorter stature compared to outbred counterparts by the third week post-germination. Additionally, inbred females produce fewer viable seeds, contributing to overall fitness declines of 20-35% across life stages in experimental settings.
Outcrossing confers substantial benefits, with outbred males siring roughly 1.6 times more viable offspring than inbred males in competitive paternity analyses under natural pollination. This disparity arises from post-pollination mechanisms that selectively discard inbred pollen tubes, favoring unrelated donors; in mixed-pollen experiments, unrelated males sired 57% of progeny on average, with bias increasing alongside genetic dissimilarity to the maternal plant. The dioecious mating system further enforces outcrossing by separating male and female functions, minimizing selfing opportunities and thereby reducing inbreeding risk. Nuclear-cytoplasmic interactions also enhance hybrid vigor, particularly in admixed populations where diverse cytoplasmic backgrounds from multiple sources boost biomass and reproductive output, countering inbreeding effects.
Experimental evidence underscores these patterns, with inbred lines showing 20-50% fitness losses relative to outbred controls in greenhouse and common garden trials, including reduced flower production (up to 35% fewer in females) and heightened vulnerability to environmental stressors. In fragmented habitats, such as isolated colonies, inbreeding depression threatens persistence by elevating extinction risk in small populations, but pollen-mediated gene flow from nearby patches can rescue fitness by introducing outcrossed alleles and sustaining genetic diversity. These dynamics highlight the adaptive value of outcrossing in maintaining population viability amid habitat fragmentation.

Ecology and interactions

Pathogen susceptibility

Silene latifolia is highly susceptible to the anther smut fungus Microbotryum lychnidis-dioicae (formerly Microbotryum violaceum or Ustilago violacea), a basidiomycete pathogen that causes a sterilizing disease transmitted primarily by insect pollinators. Infected plants exhibit systemic infection, where the fungus overwinters in host meristems and spreads to all flowers in subsequent seasons. The primary symptoms include the replacement of pollen with diploid teliospores in male anthers and spore production in female ovaries, leading to parasitic castration and floral sterilization. This alteration prevents seed and pollen production, with diseased plants often showing increased flower numbers to enhance fungal spore dispersal, though overall host reproduction is severely compromised.
The effects of M. lychnidis-dioicae infection significantly reduce host fitness through complete reproductive sterilization, with limited direct mortality but persistent systemic impact across the plant's lifespan. Infection prevalence is higher in dense populations due to increased pollinator-mediated contact, and the pathogen exploits the host's dioecious mating system, often showing female-biased infection rates. Transmission occurs mainly via nocturnal pollinators such as moths, which inadvertently vector teliospores from diseased to healthy flowers, mimicking sexually transmitted diseases in animals; passive dispersal by wind or rain is possible but minor and short-range. Wild populations of S. latifolia exhibit no effective resistance to the pathogen, with infection success varying by fungal strain but generally high (around 60-92% in artificial inoculations).
This host-pathogen interaction serves as a key model for studying coevolution, local adaptation, and disease dynamics in natural populations, highlighting maladaptation in allopatric pairings where fungal strains perform worse on non-local hosts. While M. lychnidis-dioicae dominates as the primary pathogen, S. latifolia shows minor susceptibility to other fungal diseases, such as rusts caused by Puccinia species, though these are less prevalent and impactful compared to anther smut. No significant viral infections have been widely reported in this species.

Other biotic interactions

Silene latifolia primarily relies on nocturnal pollinators, particularly moths from the family Noctuidae, such as Hadena bicruris, which are attracted to the plant's floral scent compounds, including lilac aldehydes, emitted at night. These moths not only transfer pollen but also serve as nursery pollinators, laying eggs in flowers where larvae consume developing seeds, creating a mutualistic yet antagonistic relationship. Diurnal pollinators, including bees, flies, and wasps, provide secondary pollination services, though they are less effective compared to moths in natural populations. In disturbed habitats, S. latifolia's pollination dynamics support local insect biodiversity by offering nectar and scent cues in otherwise resource-poor environments.
Flowers occasionally face predation from insects, including the larvae of pollinating moths like Hadena bicruris, which target developing ovaries.
As a ruderal species, S. latifolia competes with grasses and other field crops, such as timothy and small grains, in agricultural settings, where it can reduce yields by occupying space and resources. It forms associations with arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi, which enhance nutrient uptake, particularly phosphorus, aiding establishment in nutrient-poor soils. Additionally, S. latifolia hybridizes with the closely related Silene dioica in overlapping ranges, leading to introgression that alters local genetic diversity and potentially adaptive traits.
In ecosystems, S. latifolia acts as a weed in agricultural fields, where it invades disturbed areas but also attracts wildlife, including pollinators and seed dispersers, enhancing trophic interactions.

Uses and significance

Traditional and medicinal uses

Silene latifolia, commonly known as white campion, has limited documented traditional uses among indigenous groups, primarily centered on its medicinal properties by Native American communities. The Ojibwe (also known as Ojibwa or Anishinaabe) people prepare an infusion from the roots of S. latifolia subsp. alba as a cathartic agent to relieve constipation or promote bowel movements. This ethnobotanical application is recorded in early 20th-century surveys of Great Lakes indigenous plant knowledge.
In European folk practices, the plant's roots have been employed as a rudimentary soap substitute, particularly for laundering clothes. The roots are mashed and simmered in hot water to extract saponins, which produce a soapy lather suitable for cleaning fabrics. This use stems from the plant's natural abundance in disturbed habitats, making it accessible for rural households.
Despite these applications, caution is advised with S. latifolia due to its saponin content, which can cause gastrointestinal irritation or toxicity if ingested in large quantities. Saponins, while useful for their foaming properties, are poorly absorbed by the body but may lead to nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea in excessive doses. Topical use in soap preparations is generally considered safe, though sensitive individuals should test for skin irritation.

Ornamental and research significance

Silene latifolia, commonly known as white campion, is occasionally cultivated in gardens for its attractive white flowers, which open at night and emit a pleasant fragrance. It is grown as a biennial border plant in moderately fertile, well-drained soils, preferring full sun or partial shade, though it dislikes wet winter conditions. Hybrids with S. dioica (red campion) produce pale to mid-pink flowers that are valued in ornamental settings for their color variation and vigor.
In scientific research, S. latifolia serves as a key model organism for studying the evolution of plant sex chromosomes, including Y chromosome degeneration and the mechanisms of dioecy. Its heteromorphic X and Y chromosomes, first described in 1923, have facilitated cytogenetic studies on recombination suppression and gene dosage compensation. The 2025 high-quality genome assembly, particularly of the ~550-megabase giant Y chromosome, reveals massive accumulation of transposable elements and stepwise recombination suppression events, enabling detailed investigations into meiotic pairing, gene rearrangements, and sex-determining loci.
Beyond horticulture and genetics, S. latifolia attracts moths to wildlife gardens due to its nocturnal fragrance and nectar-rich blooms, supporting pollinator diversity. It shows potential for use in ecological restoration of disturbed sites, such as grasslands, through seed addition to enhance plant diversity, although its weedy nature requires careful management.
