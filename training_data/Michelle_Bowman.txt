Michelle Bowman
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Michelle W. Bowman is an American lawyer and central banker serving as Vice Chair for Supervision of the Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System since June 9, 2025. Appointed to the Board by President Donald Trump, she took office as a Governor on November 26, 2018, to fill an unexpired term ending January 31, 2020, and was reappointed on January 30, 2020, for a full 14-year term expiring January 31, 2034.
Prior to her roles at the Federal Reserve, Bowman served as Kansas State Bank Commissioner from January 2017 to November 2018, regulating state-chartered financial institutions, and as vice president and senior compliance officer at Farmers & Drovers Bank in Council Grove, Kansas, from 2010 to 2016. She holds a Bachelor of Science in advertising and journalism from the University of Kansas and a Juris Doctor from Washburn University School of Law. Bowman is a member of the New York State Bar Association.

Early life and education

Childhood and family background

Michelle Bowman was born on May 25, 1971, in Honolulu, Hawaii, to a military family that relocated frequently due to her father's service in the U.S. Air Force. Her father, Hank Bowman, served as an Air Force officer, including during the Vietnam War, before retiring; he also represented the fourth generation in a family banking lineage tied to Kansas community institutions. Her mother, Jan Bowman, provided foundational influence during these formative years marked by transience, including a period living in Germany.
The family's enduring connection to rural Kansas stemmed from ancestral involvement in local finance, as her forebears, alongside community members, chartered the Farmers and Drovers Bank in 1882 in Council Grove, embedding a practical orientation toward Midwestern banking traditions. This heritage, devoid of coastal elite affiliations, underscored a trajectory rooted in regional self-reliance rather than institutional pedigrees from distant urban centers.

Academic pursuits and qualifications

Bowman earned a Bachelor of Science degree in advertising and journalism from the University of Kansas. She subsequently obtained a Juris Doctor from Washburn University School of Law.
Bowman is a member of the New York State Bar Association. Her legal education forms the foundational qualification for expertise in regulatory matters, including banking law.

Professional career prior to Federal Reserve

Initial roles in government and private sector

Following her graduation from Washburn University School of Law, Bowman began her professional career in Washington, D.C., serving on the staff of U.S. Senator Bob Dole of Kansas from 1995 to 1996. In this role, she gained initial exposure to federal legislative processes and policy formulation. Subsequently, from 1997 to 2002, she worked as counsel to the U.S. House Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure and the Committee on Government Reform and Oversight, where she contributed to oversight activities, including reviews of government operations and regulatory compliance mechanisms.
In 2002, Bowman joined the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) as Director of Congressional and Intergovernmental Affairs, managing interactions between the agency, Congress, and state governments on disaster response and preparedness policies. She advanced to Deputy Assistant Secretary and policy advisor to Homeland Security Secretary Tom Ridge from 2003 to 2004, advising on national security strategies, interagency coordination, and regulatory frameworks for homeland protection following the department's establishment. These positions provided hands-on experience in federal regulatory enforcement, risk assessment, and crisis management protocols, foundational to later supervisory roles.
After her Washington tenure, Bowman relocated to London in 2004 due to her husband's employment, where she founded and led the Bowman Group, a government and public affairs consultancy, until returning to the United States in 2010. The firm focused on advising clients on international regulatory environments, policy advocacy, and compliance strategies across jurisdictions, offering her exposure to global banking and financial oversight practices without direct operational involvement in U.S. institutions. This period honed her skills in cross-border risk evaluation and stakeholder engagement in regulated sectors.

Kansas state banking positions

In January 2017, Kansas Governor Sam Brownback appointed Michelle Bowman as the state's Banking Commissioner, making her the first woman to be confirmed by the Kansas Senate for the position. She served in this role until November 2018, overseeing the regulation and supervision of approximately 267 state-chartered commercial banks and savings institutions, as well as trust companies and other financial entities under the Kansas Office of the State Bank Commissioner. Her responsibilities included conducting examinations, enforcing compliance with state banking laws, and promoting the safety and soundness of these institutions, with a particular emphasis on community banks that form the backbone of local lending in rural and regional areas.
During Bowman's tenure, Kansas state-chartered banks demonstrated robust health, with no failures recorded amid a national landscape of eight FDIC-insured bank closures in 2017 and five in 2018. This stability reflected ongoing post-financial crisis recoveries, where she prioritized tailored supervision based on empirical risk assessments rather than one-size-fits-all standards that could burden smaller institutions. Bowman advocated for state-level flexibility against perceived federal regulatory impositions, arguing that uniform rules from Dodd-Frank-era reforms often overlooked the distinct risk profiles of community banks compared to large national entities. Her approach emphasized evidence-driven oversight to support viable banks' access to capital and lending capacity without compromising prudential standards.

Federal Reserve tenure

Appointment and initial board service

Michelle W. Bowman was nominated by President Donald Trump on July 10, 2018, to serve as a member of the Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System, confirmed by the U.S. Senate on November 15, 2018, and sworn in on November 26, 2018, by Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell to fill an unexpired term ending January 31, 2020.
In her initial service, Bowman emphasized support for community banks, leveraging her prior experience as Kansas Bank Commissioner and in state banking regulation to advocate for tailored oversight that distinguishes smaller institutions from larger ones. She committed to engaging directly with chief executives of Federal Reserve-supervised community banks to understand their operational challenges and regulatory needs.
Bowman contributed to the Board's supervisory efforts, including involvement in committees addressing bank regulation and supervision, where her background informed discussions on maintaining financial stability without overburdening regional and community institutions. Following the March 2023 collapse of Silicon Valley Bank, she supported measures to protect all depositors at SVB and Signature Bank through federal intervention and called for an independent external review of the Federal Reserve's supervisory practices leading to the failures.

Elevation to Vice Chair for Supervision

On March 24, 2025, President Donald Trump nominated Federal Reserve Governor Michelle Bowman to serve as Vice Chair for Supervision, succeeding Michael Barr whose term had emphasized stricter post-2023 banking crisis regulations. The United States Senate confirmed her nomination on June 4, 2025, in a 48-46 party-line vote. Bowman was sworn into the position on June 9, 2025, by Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell, assuming a four-year term ending in 2029. In this role, she oversees the Federal Reserve's supervision and regulation of depository institutions, with primary responsibility for the largest and most complex banking organizations, including rulemaking and enforcement activities.
Bowman's elevation marked a shift toward regulatory tailoring and pragmatic oversight, contrasting with the prior administration's approach of uniform stringent standards across bank sizes. In early speeches following her confirmation, she outlined priorities including a reemphasis on tailoring regulations to bank risk profiles, enhancing supervisory transparency, and reviewing capital and liquidity requirements to reduce undue burdens while maintaining safety and soundness. She committed to evaluating potential reversals of Biden-era expansions in prudential standards, focusing on empirical assessments of their effectiveness in preventing systemic risks.
Among her initial actions, Bowman advanced plans to modify stress testing methodologies, building on prior announcements for greater transparency such as disclosing model assumptions and averaging results over two years to mitigate annual volatility. She also signaled forthcoming proposals for recalibrating capital rules, aiming to tailor requirements more closely to actual risks faced by supervised institutions rather than applying one-size-fits-all hikes. These efforts reflect a commitment to evidence-based supervision that supports banking sector resilience without stifling credit provision, particularly for community and regional banks.

Key supervisory initiatives and decisions

As Vice Chair for Supervision, Bowman has prioritized regulatory tailoring to align oversight with banks' size, complexity, and risk profiles, arguing that a one-size-fits-all approach imposes undue burdens without enhancing safety and soundness. In a February 5, 2025, speech, she advocated for problem-focused supervision that differentiates requirements, noting that post-2018 Economic Growth, Regulatory Relief, and Consumer Protection Act tailoring exempted smaller institutions from enhanced prudential standards, reducing compliance costs for banks with assets under $100 billion by an estimated 20-30% in reporting and testing obligations. This framework, she stated, preserves resilience for systemically important firms while fostering lending capacity among community banks, which hold about 15% of U.S. deposits but serve rural and underserved areas disproportionately.
Bowman has critiqued practices contributing to de-banking, particularly supervisory pressures that discourage banks from serving certain industries like fossil fuels or firearms without evidence of heightened risk. In February 2025 addresses, including remarks at the American Bankers Association conference, she called for supervision to avoid implicit policy goals unrelated to financial stability, emphasizing that such actions erode competition and access to credit, with data showing a 10-15% drop in banking relationships for de-banked sectors since 2020. She advocated policies supporting innovation, such as streamlined approvals for fintech partnerships, to counteract these trends and maintain a diverse banking sector.
A key decision under her influence was the October 16, 2025, interagency rescission of the 2023 Principles for Climate-Related Financial Risk Management, which had directed large banks to integrate climate scenario analysis into stress testing and governance. Bowman stated the principles diverted resources from core risks without empirical demonstration of material financial impacts, citing studies showing negligible near-term effects on U.S. bank portfolios from physical or transition risks. The move refocuses supervision on quantifiable threats like liquidity and credit, aligning with statutory mandates and reducing annual compliance hours estimated at over 100,000 for affected institutions.
On February 12, 2026, at 10:15 a.m. ET, Bowman was scheduled to discuss "Supervision and Regulation" at the Keefe, Bruyette & Woods 33rd Annual Winter Financial Services Conference (virtual), as part of her ongoing supervisory role.

Monetary policy positions

Dissents on interest rate decisions

Michelle Bowman has occasionally dissented from Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) decisions on interest rates, emphasizing data-dependent assessments of inflation, employment, and economic risks over consensus-driven adjustments. Her votes reflect a cautious approach grounded in incoming economic indicators, diverging from the majority in instances where she viewed policy shifts as premature or insufficiently warranted by evidence. Such Board-level dissents on monetary policy are uncommon, with governors typically aligning more closely than regional presidents.
On September 18, 2024, Bowman dissented from the FOMC's unanimous decision to reduce the target range for the federal funds rate by 50 basis points to 4.755.00 percent, preferring a more modest 25 basis point cut. She argued that recent inflation data, while cooling, remained above the Federal Reserve's 2 percent target, with upside risks persisting due to factors like housing costs and potential supply disruptions; a larger cut risked embedding higher inflation expectations without clear evidence of sustained progress toward price stability. This marked the first monetary policy dissent by a Fed governor since 2005, underscoring the rarity of such public divergence at the Board level.
In the July 30, 2025, FOMC meetingfollowed by her statement on August 1Bowman again dissented, this time alongside Governor Christopher Waller, against the Committee's decision to hold the federal funds rate steady at 4.254.50 percent. She advocated for a 25 basis point reduction, citing weakening labor market signals, including rising unemployment and softening job growth, alongside diminished inflation risks that suggested current policy had become overly restrictive and threatened economic stability. This joint governor dissent was the first in over three decades, highlighting internal debates amid shifting data on employment fragility versus inflation containment.

Broader economic outlook and inflation concerns

In remarks delivered on January 9, 2025, at the Independent Community Bankers of America Government Relations Summit, Bowman expressed optimism regarding the future of the U.S. banking sector, stating that regulators could bolster the system by pursuing tailored policies amid a period of measured monetary restrictiveness to ensure sustained economic stability. She projected that banking conditions would improve with prudent oversight, emphasizing resilience in the face of higher interest rates and deposit outflows observed in prior years.
Bowman has consistently underscored the Federal Reserve's commitment to its dual mandate of maximum employment and price stability. In an August 27, 2025, speech at the Bank for International Settlements, she noted that inflation had advanced substantially toward the 2 percent targetexcluding one-time tariff impactswhile the labor market held steady near full employment, signaling progress without undue risks to either objective. This assessment reflected her view that core inflationary drivers, such as wage pressures, had eased, though vigilance remained essential against potential reacceleration from supply disruptions or fiscal expansions.
On the topic of institutional independence, Bowman affirmed in a July 22, 2025, CNBC interview that preserving the Fed's autonomy in monetary policy decisions is critical to shielding it from fiscal or political encroachments, which could undermine credibility and long-term economic outcomes. She argued that such independence enables data-driven responses to inflation dynamics, rather than reactive adjustments to short-term budgetary demands, thereby fostering durable price stability and growth. In subsequent commentary, including a September 23, 2025, address, Bowman highlighted that recent wage moderation had neutralized labor costs as an inflationary force, allowing focus on broader risks like goods price volatility while anticipating a soft landing.

Banking regulation perspectives

Advocacy for regulatory tailoring

Bowman has consistently advocated for regulatory tailoring since her 2018 appointment to the Federal Reserve Board, emphasizing adjustments to supervisory and regulatory frameworks based on a bank's size, complexity, business model, and risk profile to promote a safe and sound banking system. This principle, codified in the Economic Growth, Regulatory Relief, and Consumer Protection Act of 2018, raised thresholds for enhanced standards and enabled proportionality, which she argues avoids inefficient resource allocation toward low-risk activities at smaller institutions.
Drawing from her tenure as Kansas Bank Commissioner from 2017 to 2018, where she oversaw community banks integral to local economies, Bowman contends that size-based flexibility causally enhances systemic resilience by preserving a diverse banking sector capable of withstanding localized shocks through focused lending rather than compliance burdens. Uniform, one-size-fits-all rules, by contrast, impose disproportionate costs on community bankssuch as those with assets under $10 billionpotentially driving mergers, service reductions, or exits from underserved areas, thereby eroding credit availability and increasing vulnerability to concentrated failures. She highlights how tailored approaches allow these institutions to prioritize core functions, fostering competition and stability absent in overly prescriptive regimes that shift activities to less-regulated nonbanks.
In 2025, following her elevation to Vice Chair for Supervision, Bowman prioritized restoring and extending tailoring to supervision itself, announcing initiatives to calibrate oversight more precisely to institutional differences and unveiling a series of regulatory adjustments in October to address model transparency and related burdens. These efforts aim to mitigate cliff effects at asset thresholds, such as $100 billion, where abrupt rule changes have historically pressured institutions without commensurate risk increases.

Stance on mergers, capital requirements, and innovation

Bowman has criticized excessive rigor in bank merger reviews, advocating for streamlined processes that prioritize efficiency and data-driven assessments over blanket scrutiny. In April 2025 testimony during her Vice Chair nomination, she supported accelerating merger approvals as part of pragmatic tailoring, drawing on post-crisis evidence that prolonged reviews hinder community bank consolidations without proportionally enhancing stability. Her position aligns with pre-2008 deregulation outcomes, where lighter merger oversight facilitated competition and lending growth, contrasted against data showing over 1,700 U.S. bank closures since the crisis due to regulatory pressures.
On capital requirements, Bowman opposes hikes that exceed empirical justifications from post-crisis resilience data, favoring recalibrations to avoid stifling intermediation. In October 2025, under her leadership, the Federal Reserve proposed revisions to the enhanced supplementary leverage ratio (eSLR) to restore it as a backstop rather than a binding constraint, citing Treasury market strains from overly stringent levels. She has endorsed a 27% reduction in tier 1 capital demands for global systemically important banks' subsidiaries, arguing that such tailoringrooted in stress test outcomes showing ample buffersprevents unnecessary credit contraction observed in heavier post-Dodd-Frank regimes.
Bowman promotes banking innovation by opposing de-banking of legal sectors like crypto, while addressing fraud through technological solutions rather than prohibitive rules. In an August 2025 speech, she urged regulators to "not penalize or prohibit a bank from banking a customer engaged in legal activity," referencing debanking incidents driven by regulatory uncertainty that echoed pre-crisis innovation stifling. Balancing fraud risksevident in crypto custody growthshe advocates leveraging AI and blockchain for detection, stating that "if fraud can be addressed using new technology, we should make sure that the regulatory framework does not stand in the way," informed by post-crisis data on tech's role in enhancing safety without broad restrictions. This approach echoes pre-crisis deregulation's facilitation of fintech precursors, yielding efficiency gains now quantifiable in reduced transaction costs via tokenization.

Controversies and public reception

Criticisms from progressive advocates

Progressive advocates, particularly organizations like Better Markets, have accused Michelle Bowman of prioritizing Wall Street interests over financial stability by championing deregulation reminiscent of pre-2008 laxity. In an April 8, 2025, fact sheet, Better Markets listed ten reasons against her elevation to Vice Chair for Supervision, including her support for the 2018 Economic Growth, Regulatory Relief, and Consumer Protection Act's tailoring provisions, which exempted 26 of the 41 largest U.S. banks from enhanced prudential standards and stress testing. Critics contend this rollback weakened safeguards, contributing to vulnerabilities exposed in the 2023 regional bank failures such as Silicon Valley Bank, where inadequate interest rate risk management amid low capital buffers played a role, though empirical analyses indicate compliance costs under Dodd-Frank had ballooned to over $200 billion annually industry-wide by 2019 without averting such idiosyncratic mismanagement.
Further objections center on Bowman's opposition to stricter capital requirements and merger scrutiny, viewed as leniency enabling consolidation and risk-taking. Better Markets highlighted her July 2023 dissent against the Basel III Endgame proposal, which sought a 19% capital hike for global systemically important banks to bolster resilience, arguing her stance ignores how undercapitalization amplified losses in 2023 failures despite tailoring's intent to calibrate rules to bank size and complexityevidenced by data showing smaller institutions faced disproportionate burdens, with community bank lending contracting post-2010 reforms. Advocates also decry her resistance to rigorous merger reviews, such as in the proposed Capital One-Discover deal, claiming it risks anticompetitive outcomes harming consumers, and her skepticism toward climate risk integration in supervision, dismissing it as non-core despite NGFS scenarios projecting potential $2.5 trillion in U.S. financial impacts from unmitigated physical risks by 2050.
Additional criticisms portray Bowman's positions as politicized, tied to Trump-era policies and insufficient defense of Fed independence. A June 3, 2025, Better Markets analysis faulted her April 10, 2025, Senate testimony for evading questions on countering political attacks on monetary policy autonomy, amid concerns over potential tariff-induced stresses unaddressed in stress tests. Senate Democrats echoed these worries during her June 2025 confirmation, voting 48-46 against along party lines, citing her hiring of Wall Street lobbyists and Goldman Sachs alumni as signaling capture, though counter-evidence from Fed data shows tailored regulation preserved credit availabilitye.g., mortgage originations rose 15% post-2018 reliefwithout systemic crises, underscoring debates on regulation's trade-offs between stability and economic growth.

Support from industry and conservative perspectives

Banking industry representatives have lauded Michelle Bowman for her pragmatic approach to supervision, emphasizing that her skepticism toward expansive regulatory proposals fosters greater stability by concentrating oversight on genuine risks rather than blanket mandates. In a November 2023 analysis, American Banker described Bowman as the Federal Reserve's leading skeptic on the regulatory agenda, crediting her dissents with promoting calibrated rules that enhance resilience without imposing undue burdens on institutions.
The Independent Community Bankers of America praised Bowman's June 2025 remarks on "rightsizing" regulations, highlighting her advocacy for tailored supervision that supports community banks' operational viability and reduces compliance costs that could otherwise precipitate failures. Similarly, the American Bankers Association endorsed her March 2025 nomination to Vice Chair for Supervision, arguing her background as a community banker provides essential real-world insight into policies that balance safety with lending capacity. Industry observers, including those cited in Bloomberg reporting, have welcomed her efforts to scale back capital proposals like the Basel III endgame, contending such restraint prevents lending contractions that historically correlate with heightened failure risks.
Conservative lawmakers have aligned with Bowman's emphasis on deregulation to spur economic growth and preserve Federal Reserve independence from politicized overreach. Senate Banking Committee Ranking Member Tim Scott applauded her June 4, 2025, confirmation, framing it as a victory for supervision that prioritizes innovation and efficiency over uniform restrictions. This support underscores perceptions that her tailored frameworks, implemented post-2018 Economic Growth, Regulatory Relief, and Consumer Protection Act, empirically correlate with subdued failure ratesaveraging under 5 annually through 2022by enabling banks to allocate capital toward risk management rather than rote compliance.

Personal life

Family and residences

Bowman is married to Wes Bowman, with whom she has two children.
Raised in the Midwest, Bowman maintained a long-term residence in Kansas, where she worked in community banking and served as state bank commissioner from 2017 to 2018. Following her appointment to the Federal Reserve Board of Governors in November 2018, she relocated to Washington, D.C., to fulfill her role. This shift from her Kansas base underscores her transition from regional regulatory experience to national monetary policy responsibilities.

Public engagements and affiliations

Bowman has participated in numerous public speaking engagements at industry conferences, emphasizing her background in community banking and broader financial topics. On August 19, 2025, she delivered remarks titled "Embracing Innovation" at the Wyoming Blockchain Symposium in Teton Village, Wyoming, discussing technological advancements in finance. Earlier, on July 23, 2025, she hosted a fireside chat with OpenAI CEO Sam Altman at a Federal Reserve-sponsored event focused on artificial intelligence applications in banking and financial services. These appearances highlight her role in bridging regulatory perspectives with emerging industry innovations outside core monetary policy discussions.
She has also delivered welcoming remarks at international and domestic forums, such as the Financial Inclusion Conference in Washington, D.C., on July 15, 2025, via pre-recorded video, underscoring access to financial services. In October 2025, Bowman provided opening remarks at the Community Bank Conference in Washington, D.C., on October 9, engaging with sector leaders on forward-looking community banking dynamics.
Bowman's affiliations center on community banking networks, reflecting her pre-Fed experience as a fifth-generation Kansas banker and former state bank commissioner. The Independent Community Bankers of America (ICBA) features her biography and has consistently endorsed her nominations, including her June 2025 confirmation as Vice Chair for Supervision, citing her advocacy for tailored regulations suited to smaller institutions. She has been profiled in events like the Santander International Banking Conference, where her expertise in supervision is highlighted for global audiences. Throughout her tenure, Bowman has avoided partisan political activism, maintaining the Federal Reserve's tradition of independence from electoral influences, as affirmed in congressional testimonies.
