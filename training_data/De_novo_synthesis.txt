_De novo_ synthesis
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 De novo synthesis, a term derived from Latin meaning "from new" or "anew", refers to the biochemical processes by which living organisms construct complex biomolecules from simple, basic precursors such as carbon dioxide, ammonia, and organic acids, rather than relying on pre-existing similar molecules through recycling or salvage pathways. This fundamental metabolic strategy is essential for cellular growth, division, and adaptation, occurring in nearly all organisms and involving highly regulated multi-step enzymatic pathways.
In nucleotide metabolism, de novo synthesis produces purine and pyrimidine nucleotides from simple precursors, contrasting with salvage pathways that recycle bases and nucleosides. These pathways are crucial for DNA and RNA production and are targets for anticancer drugs such as methotrexate and 5-fluorouracil. Detailed descriptions of the purine and pyrimidine pathways are provided in later sections.
De novo synthesis also plays a key role in lipid metabolism, where lipogenesis converts excess nutrients into fatty acids and other lipids, primarily in the liver and adipose tissue. This process is upregulated in conditions like obesity and metabolic syndrome, contributing to diseases such as hepatic steatosis. Further details on fatty acid and cholesterol synthesis are covered in the lipid biosynthesis section.
In amino acid metabolism, de novo synthesis enables the production of non-essential amino acids from glycolytic or tricarboxylic acid cycle intermediates through processes like transamination, ensuring nitrogen assimilation and carbon utilization. Essential amino acids must be obtained from the diet in animals. Overall, de novo synthesis underscores the autonomy of cellular biochemistry, with dysregulation linked to diseases including cancer, diabetes, and metabolic disorders.

Overview

Definition and etymology

De novo is a Latin phrase meaning "from new" or "anew," which in biochemistry denotes the creation of complex molecules from simple, non-recycled precursors such as amino acids, sugars, or inorganic ions. This contrasts with processes that reuse or recycle existing molecular components.
In biological and chemical contexts, de novo synthesis refers to the biosynthetic pathways that assemble essential biomolecules, including nucleotides and lipids, starting from basic building blocks rather than pre-formed units. These pathways are distinct from catabolic breakdown or salvage routes, which repurpose degraded or exogenous materials, much like constructing a new house from raw lumber and bricks instead of renovating an existing structure.
Building on foundational discoveries such as Hans Krebs' elucidation of the urea cycle and citric acid cycle in the 1930s, which highlighted cyclic yet synthetic aspects of intermediary metabolism, the term gained specific prominence in the late 1940s through studies on nucleotide biosynthesis, notably John M. Buchanan's experiments demonstrating the incorporation of simple precursors like glycine and formate into purine rings. Key applications include the de novo pathways for nucleotides and lipids, essential for cellular growth and function.

Biochemical significance

De novo synthesis plays a critical role in cellular growth and repair by enabling the production of essential biomolecules, such as nucleotides and lipids, from basic precursors without dependence on external supplies. This pathway is particularly vital for rapidly proliferating cells, including those in embryonic development and tumors, where demand for building blocks like deoxyribonucleotides for DNA replication surges. For instance, de novo deoxyribonucleotide biosynthesis directly supports cancer cell proliferation by providing nucleotides necessary for genomic expansion. Similarly, de novo fatty acid synthesis fuels membrane biogenesis in proliferating tumor cells, allowing adaptation to nutrient-variable environments. In contrast, salvage pathways recycle pre-existing nucleotides, serving as a complementary, energy-efficient mechanism in non-proliferating cells.
The high energy demands of de novo synthesis underscore its strategic use in nutrient-abundant conditions, balancing resource allocation across cellular needs. Synthesizing a purine nucleotide de novo requires six ATP molecules, far exceeding the minimal energy input of salvage pathways, which justifies its predominance in metabolically active, proliferating tissues over differentiated ones that prioritize efficiency. This ATP-intensive process reflects an evolutionary trade-off, favoring autonomy in environments with ample carbon and nitrogen sources while conserving resources in stable, low-growth states.
From an evolutionary perspective, de novo synthesis confers metabolic independence, particularly in autotrophs like plants and bacteria that synthesize all amino acids and other biomolecules from inorganic precursors, enabling survival in diverse ecosystems. In heterotrophs such as humans, this capability is restricted to non-essential amino acids and lipids, with essential amino acids requiring dietary intake to avoid limitations in growth and reproduction. This divergence highlights adaptations to trophic levels, where de novo pathways in microbes and plants support full biosynthetic autonomy, whereas animals rely on ecological niches for key nutrients.
Dysregulation of de novo synthesis has significant pathological implications, linking overactivity to diseases like gout, where excessive purine production elevates uric acid levels and triggers inflammation, and atherosclerosis, driven by surplus cholesterol accumulation in vascular tissues. Therapeutically, these pathways are targeted by antifolates such as methotrexate, which inhibit folate-dependent steps in de novo nucleotide biosynthesis, selectively disrupting proliferation in cancer cells while sparing salvage-reliant normal tissues.

Nucleotide biosynthesis

Purine pathway

The de novo purine biosynthesis pathway constructs the purine ring atom by atom directly onto a ribose phosphate backbone, contrasting with the pyrimidine pathway where the base is assembled separately before attachment to ribose. This process begins with 5-phosphoribosyl-1-pyrophosphate (PRPP), derived from ribose-5-phosphate, and incorporates atoms from glutamine (two nitrogen atoms), glycine (two carbon atoms and one nitrogen atom), aspartate (one nitrogen), formate (via N-formyltetrahydrofolate, two carbons), and CO (one carbon), ultimately yielding inosine 5'-monophosphate (IMP) as the first complete purine nucleotide. The pathway is energy-intensive, consuming six ATP molecules per IMP produced, and is essential for providing purine nucleotides (AMP and GMP) in proliferating cells.
The 10-step pathway occurs in the cytosol and assembles the imidazole and pyrimidine rings of the purine base sequentially on the ribose moiety. The committed and rate-limiting first step, catalyzed by phosphoribosyl pyrophosphate amidotransferase (PPAT), replaces the pyrophosphate of PRPP with an amino group from glutamine to form 5-phosphoribosylamine (PRA):

IMP then branches: to AMP via adenylosuccinate synthetase (ADSS) and ADSL, incorporating aspartate and releasing fumarate; or to GMP via IMP dehydrogenase (IMPDH) oxidation to xanthosine monophosphate (XMP) followed by amination by GMP synthetase (GMPS) using glutamine.

Regulation occurs primarily at the PPAT step through allosteric feedback inhibition by AMP, GMP, and IMP, preventing overproduction when purine levels are high; IMP synergistically enhances inhibition by AMP and GMP binding to separate sites. Upstream, PRPP availability is controlled by the isoforms PRPS1 and PRPS2 (phosphoribosyl pyrophosphate synthetases), whose gene expression is modulated by transcription factors like c-Myc to match cellular demand during proliferation. The pathway enzymes often form purinosome complexes in the cytosol to channel intermediates and improve efficiency.
In humans, de novo purine synthesis is most active in the liver and to a lesser extent in the kidney, where it supplies the majority of purine nucleotides for export to other tissues; non-hepatic tissues rely more on salvage pathways, making de novo essential but not the sole source overall.

Pyrimidine pathway

The de novo synthesis of pyrimidine nucleotides begins with the formation of the pyrimidine ring as a free base, which is subsequently attached to ribose-5-phosphate, distinguishing it from purine biosynthesis where the base is assembled directly on the ribose moiety. This pathway produces uridine monophosphate (UMP) as the central intermediate, from which cytidine triphosphate (CTP) and deoxythymidine monophosphate (dTMP) are derived to meet cellular demands for RNA, DNA, and other processes. The process is essential in rapidly dividing cells and is tightly regulated to balance nucleotide pools.
The pathway utilizes simple precursors: glutamine (as the nitrogen source), carbon dioxide (CO), and aspartate, along with ATP and phosphoribosyl pyrophosphate (PRPP). It consists of six enzymatic steps, primarily occurring in the cytosol of mammalian cells, with the first three steps catalyzed by the multifunctional enzyme CAD (carbamoyl-phosphate synthetase II, aspartate transcarbamoylase, and dihydroorotase). CAD is a large hexameric complex (approximately 1.5 MDa) that integrates these activities to efficiently channel intermediates and minimize side reactions.
The initial and rate-limiting step is catalyzed by the CPSII domain of CAD, which condenses glutamine, CO, and two molecules of ATP to form carbamoyl phosphate:

Regulation occurs primarily at the CPSII step within CAD, which is allosterically activated by PRPP to promote synthesis when ribose availability is high and inhibited by UTP through concerted feedback on the CAD complex to prevent overaccumulation of pyrimidines. Additional control involves phosphorylation: MAPK/ERK phosphorylates CAD at Thr456, reducing UTP inhibition and enhancing PRPP sensitivity, while mTORC1-S6K1 phosphorylates at Ser1859/Ser1900 to boost activity during growth signaling. This pathway is most active in the liver, where CAD expression is high to support systemic nucleotide production. An alternative salvage pathway recycles free pyrimidines like uracil via uracil phosphoribosyltransferase, but de novo synthesis predominates during high demand.

Lipid biosynthesis

Fatty acid synthesis

De novo fatty acid synthesis, also known as de novo lipogenesis, is a cytosolic anabolic pathway that constructs long-chain saturated fatty acids, primarily palmitate (C16:0), from acetyl-CoA precursors. This process is essential for producing lipids used in membrane formation, energy storage, and signaling molecules, particularly in nutrient-rich conditions. Acetyl-CoA, sourced from mitochondrial oxidation of glucose or other carbohydrates and transported to the cytosol via the citrate shuttle, serves as the primary building block. The pathway begins with the carboxylation of acetyl-CoA to malonyl-CoA, followed by iterative condensation and reduction reactions catalyzed by the multifunctional fatty acid synthase (FAS) complex, requiring seven cycles to yield palmitate.
The committed and rate-limiting step is catalyzed by acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACC), a biotin-dependent enzyme that adds CO to acetyl-CoA using ATP and bicarbonate to form malonyl-CoA. ACC exists as two isoforms: ACC1 (cytosolic, 265 kDa) primarily for fatty acid synthesis and ACC2 (mitochondrial-associated) for regulating -oxidation via malonyl-CoA inhibition of carnitine palmitoyltransferase I. ACC activity is allosterically activated by citrate, which promotes its polymerization into active filaments, and inhibited by long-chain acyl-CoA products. In mammals, FAS I is a large homodimeric multienzyme complex (~540 kDa) with flexibly tethered domains including -ketoacyl synthase (KS), malonyl/acetyl transferase (MAT), acyl carrier protein (ACP), -ketoacyl reductase (KR), -hydroxyacyl dehydratase (DH), enoyl reductase (ER), and thioesterase (TE). This architecture allows sequential processing within the complex, enhancing efficiency compared to bacterial type II systems.
Each elongation cycle in FAS starts with loading: the MAT domain transfers acetyl-CoA (primer) to the KS cysteine and malonyl-CoA to the ACP phosphopantetheine thiol. Condensation follows, where KS catalyzes decarboxylative Claisen condensation between the acetyl group and malonyl-ACP, forming -ketoacyl-ACP and releasing CO, which drives the thermodynamically unfavorable reaction forward. The -keto group is then reduced by KR using NADPH to -hydroxyacyl-ACP, dehydrated by DH to trans-enoyl-ACP, and finally reduced by ER using another NADPH to yield the elongated saturated acyl-ACP. After seven cycles, the TE domain hydrolyzes palmitoyl-ACP to free palmitate and ACP. This iterative mechanism extends the chain by two carbons per cycle, with NADPH provided mainly by the pentose phosphate pathway and malic enzyme.

Regulation occurs at multiple levels to align synthesis with nutritional status. Insulin upregulates the pathway by promoting ACC dephosphorylation via protein phosphatase activation (e.g., through PI3K/AKT signaling), relieving inhibition, and inducing SREBP-1c transcription, which activates FAS and ACC genes. Conversely, glucagon and epinephrine inhibit via cAMP-dependent protein kinase A, which phosphorylates and inactivates ACC. AMPK, activated during energy depletion (high AMP/ATP), phosphorylates ACC at Ser79, inhibiting it and favoring catabolism. ACC is also biotin-dependent for its carboxylase activity. In the fed state, this orchestration ensures efficient conversion of excess carbohydrates to storage lipids.
The pathway is localized to the cytosol of lipogenic tissues such as liver, adipose, and lactating mammary glands, where palmitate is the main product and is subsequently elongated (e.g., to stearate, C18:0) in the endoplasmic reticulum or desaturated (e.g., to palmitoleate, 16:1n-7) by stearoyl-CoA desaturase. In healthy humans, de novo lipogenesis contributes a minor fraction (typically <5%) to hepatic triglyceride synthesis, supporting very-low-density lipoprotein export or adipocyte storage.

Cholesterol synthesis

De novo cholesterol synthesis, also known as the mevalonate pathway, is a multi-step biosynthetic process that converts acetyl-CoA into cholesterol, essential for cell membrane structure, hormone production, and bile acid formation. This pathway comprises more than 30 enzymatic reactions, beginning with the condensation of three molecules of acetyl-CoA to form 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-CoA (HMG-CoA) and culminating in the cyclization and modification of squalene to cholesterol. Key intermediates include mevalonate, isopentenyl pyrophosphate (IPP), farnesyl pyrophosphate (FPP), and squalene, which serve as building blocks for the sterol ring structure.
The pathway initiates in the cytosol with the reversible condensation of two acetyl-CoA molecules to acetoacetyl-CoA, catalyzed by acetoacetyl-CoA thiolase, followed by the addition of a third acetyl-CoA to form HMG-CoA via HMG-CoA synthase. The committed and rate-limiting step occurs at the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) membrane, where HMG-CoA reductase (HMGCR) reduces HMG-CoA to mevalonate, consuming two molecules of NADPH; this enzyme is the primary target of statins, which inhibit cholesterol production to treat hypercholesterolemia. Mevalonate is then sequentially phosphorylated by mevalonate kinase and phosphomevalonate kinase, followed by decarboxylation to IPP via mevalonate diphosphate decarboxylase. IPP isomerizes to dimethylallyl pyrophosphate (DMAPP), which condenses stepwise with additional IPP units through prenyl synthases (e.g., farnesyl diphosphate synthase) to form geranyl pyrophosphate and ultimately FPP. Two FPP molecules are head-to-head condensed by squalene synthase to produce squalene, which is epoxidized by squalene epoxidase and cyclized by lanosterol synthase to lanosterol. The final 19 steps involve multiple demethylations and reductions, including 14-demethylation and 24-reduction, to yield cholesterol.

Regulation of the pathway primarily occurs at the transcriptional level through sterol regulatory element-binding protein-2 (SREBP-2), which activates HMGCR and other genes like squalene epoxidase when cellular cholesterol levels are low, with processing and nuclear translocation of SREBP-2 inhibited by excess sterols in the ER. Cholesterol exerts feedback inhibition by accelerating HMGCR degradation via ubiquitin-mediated proteasomal pathways and repressing SREBP-2 maturation. Additionally, bile acids repress synthesis by activating the farnesoid X receptor (FXR), which induces small heterodimer partner (SHP) to inhibit SREBP-2 and CYP7A1 (in bile acid synthesis), thereby coordinating cholesterol homeostasis. Post-translational control includes phosphorylation of HMGCR by AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK), reducing its activity during energy stress.
Cholesterol synthesis predominantly occurs in the ER, with some initial steps (e.g., thiolase activity) localized to peroxisomes, and is most active in the liver, which accounts for the majority of production. In humans, the pathway generates approximately 1 g of cholesterol per day, with pathway intermediates also serving as precursors for non-sterol products such as vitamin D, steroid hormones, and ubiquinone. This process shares its initial acetyl-CoA precursor with fatty acid synthesis but diverges into branched isoprenoid formation.

Nucleic acid synthesis

DNA synthesis

DNA synthesis in biological systems primarily occurs during semi-conservative replication, where each parental DNA strand serves as a template for the synthesis of a new complementary strand, ensuring faithful duplication of the genome. In bacteria, DNA polymerase III holoenzyme catalyzes the bulk of this process, incorporating deoxyribonucleoside triphosphates (dNTPs) such as dATP, dGTP, dCTP, and dTTP from the cellular pool. In eukaryotes, DNA polymerases and perform analogous roles, with polymerase typically synthesizing the leading strand and polymerase the lagging strand, supported by accessory proteins for processivity. The dNTP precursors are derived from de novo nucleotide biosynthesis pathways, where ribonucleotide reductase converts ribonucleoside diphosphates to deoxyribonucleoside diphosphates, which are then phosphorylated to dNTPs.
Replication begins with priming by primase, which synthesizes short RNA primers (approximately 10-12 nucleotides) annealed to the template DNA, providing a 3'-OH group for polymerase initiation. Elongation proceeds exclusively in the 5' to 3' direction, with the leading strand formed continuously from a single primer, while the lagging strand is synthesized discontinuously as Okazaki fragments (100200 nucleotides in eukaryotes, 1,0002,000 nucleotides in prokaryotes) due to the antiparallel nature of DNA strands. Each Okazaki fragment requires a new RNA primer, and upon completion, DNA polymerase I (in bacteria) or flap endonuclease 1 (in eukaryotes) removes the primers, replacing them with DNA via nick translation. Proofreading enhances accuracy through the intrinsic 3' to 5' exonuclease activity of replicative polymerases, which excises mismatched nucleotides immediately after incorporation. The core polymerization reaction involves nucleophilic attack by the primer's 3'-OH on the dNTP's -phosphate, releasing pyrophosphate (PPi):

DNA synthesis is tightly regulated during the S phase of the cell cycle, where origins of replication are licensed and fired in a coordinated manner to prevent over-replication. Checkpoints, such as the intra-S-phase checkpoint, monitor replication fork progression and halt advancement upon detection of DNA damage, including UV-induced pyrimidine dimers that stall polymerases and trigger mutations if unrepaired. Unresolved errors can lead to double-strand breaks or persistent mutations, contributing to genomic instability. In synthetic biology, de novo DNA synthesis diverges from cellular mechanisms, employing chemical or enzymatic approaches to construct custom sequences. The phosphoramidite method, a solid-phase chemical synthesis, builds oligonucleotides from 3' to 5' by sequential deprotection, coupling, capping, and oxidation cycles, typically yielding strands up to 100-200 nucleotides with 98-99% efficiency per base. Enzymatic alternatives use terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase (TdT), a template-independent polymerase that adds dNTPs to the 3' end, enabling longer extensions; engineered TdT variants achieve stepwise yields of 98.7%. For large constructs, Gibson assembly isothermally joins overlapping fragments (up to several hundred kilobases) via 5' exonuclease chew-back, polymerase fill-in, and ligase sealing, facilitating gene synthesis exceeding 100 kb, as demonstrated in the assembly of synthetic bacterial genomes.
Recent advances in the 2020s integrate TdT-based enzymatic synthesis with CRISPR-Cas systems for precise genome editing and de novo construction of custom DNA sequences, enabling applications like designer pathways and data storage. Oligo array technologies, combining microarray printing with enzymatic extension, now support yields up to 750 nucleotides per oligonucleotide as of 2025, surpassing traditional chemical limits and accelerating synthetic genomics. These methods emphasize scalability and reduced error rates, with TdT achieving extensions beyond 1,000 bp in optimized systems.

RNA synthesis

RNA synthesis occurs through transcription, a process in which RNA polymerase enzymes assemble ribonucleic acid polymers from nucleoside triphosphate (NTP) monomers using a DNA template. In prokaryotes, a single RNA polymerase (RNAP) handles all transcription, while eukaryotes employ three distinct nuclear RNA polymerases: RNA polymerase I (Pol I) for ribosomal RNA (rRNA), RNA polymerase II (Pol II) for messenger RNA (mRNA) and most non-coding RNAs (ncRNAs), and RNA polymerase III (Pol III) for transfer RNA (tRNA) and certain small RNAs. This synthesis begins anew for each transcript without requiring a primer, distinguishing it from DNA replication, and utilizes NTPs (ATP, GTP, UTP, CTP) derived from either de novo nucleotide biosynthesis pathways or salvage mechanisms.
The transcription mechanism unfolds in three main stages: initiation, elongation, and termination. Initiation starts with RNAP binding to promoter regions on DNA; in eukaryotes, Pol II recognizes the TATA box via the general transcription factor TFIID, which recruits additional factors (TFIIA, TFIIB, TFIIE, TFIIF, TFIIH) to form the pre-initiation complex, melting ~14-17 base pairs of DNA to expose the template strand. In prokaryotes, the sigma () subunit of RNAP binds promoters like the -10 and -35 boxes in bacteria. Elongation involves processive addition of nucleotides complementary to the DNA template, with the chemical reaction NTP + (RNA)n (RNA){n+1} (as RNA-NMP) + PPi, where pyrophosphate (PPi) is released; bacterial RNAP achieves ~50 nucleotides per second. Termination differs by organism: prokaryotes often use rho-independent mechanisms involving GC-rich RNA hairpins followed by U-rich sequences that destabilize the RNAP-DNA-RNA complex, while eukaryotic Pol II termination is coupled to polyadenylation signals, where cleavage and poly(A) addition ~10-30 nucleotides downstream trigger RNAP release via the torpedo or allosteric models.
Transcription produces diverse RNA types essential for cellular function. mRNA serves as templates for protein synthesis, encoding genetic information from exons after processing; rRNA forms the structural core of ribosomes, comprising ~80% of cellular RNA; tRNA acts as adaptors in translation, recognizing codons via anticodons; and ncRNAs, including microRNAs (miRNAs) and long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs), regulate gene expression, chromatin modification, and other processes. In eukaryotes, primary transcripts (pre-mRNAs for Pol II products) undergo co- and post-transcriptional processing: 5' capping adds a 7-methylguanosine cap shortly after initiation (~20-30 nucleotides) to protect against degradation and aid export; splicing removes introns via the spliceosome, joining exons; and 3' polyadenylation appends a poly(A) tail (50-250 adenines) to enhance stability and translation efficiency. Prokaryotic transcripts generally lack these modifications, enabling rapid coupling of transcription and translation.
Regulation of RNA synthesis ensures precise gene expression. Enhancers and silencers, distal DNA elements, modulate promoter activity by looping to interact with transcription factors; for instance, enhancers recruit co-activators to boost Pol II recruitment. General and specific transcription factors, such as TFIID in eukaryotes, fine-tune initiation. Epigenetic modifications, including histone acetylation (e.g., H3K27ac by HATs like p300), loosen chromatin structure to facilitate RNAP access, while deacetylation by HDACs represses transcription. Errors occur at ~10^{-4} to 10^{-5} per nucleotide, primarily from mismatched NTP incorporation, and are largely corrected post-transcriptionally via RNA surveillance pathways like nonsense-mediated decay, rather than direct proofreading during synthesis.
In synthetic biology, de novo RNA synthesis employs enzymatic methods to create custom sequences without templates, paralleling DNA approaches. Template-independent enzymes, such as engineered variants of terminal uridylyl transferase or poly(A) polymerase, add NTPs sequentially using reversibly blocked nucleotides. As of 2025, these techniques achieve oligonucleotides up to 100-200 nucleotides, with applications in RNA therapeutics, aptamers, and synthetic regulatory elements, though challenges in fidelity and length persist compared to DNA methods.
