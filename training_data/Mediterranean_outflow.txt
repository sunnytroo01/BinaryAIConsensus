Mediterranean outflow
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Mediterranean outflow refers to the dense, saline water mass that flows westward from the Mediterranean Sea into the Atlantic Ocean through the Strait of Gibraltar, driven by density differences and forming a high-velocity bottom current in the Gulf of Cadiz. This outflow originates primarily from the deep waters of the western Mediterranean, particularly the Western Mediterranean Deep Water (WMDW) formed primarily in the Gulf of Lions, which flows along the Moroccan continental slope before exiting the strait. Upon entering the Atlantic, the outflow descends the continental slope, undergoing intense mixing and entrainment of North Atlantic Central Water, which increases its volume from approximately 0.5 Sverdrups (Sv) of pure Mediterranean water to 1.52 Sv. It eventually spreads as a warm, salty tongue in the North Atlantic thermocline at depths around 10001100 meters near Cape St. Vincent, influencing regional salinity and thermohaline circulation.
A key feature of the Mediterranean outflow is its generation of meddies (Mediterranean Water eddies), subsurface vortices that efficiently transport and disperse the outflow water far into the North Atlantic, contributing to the basin-wide salinity distribution and nutrient cycling. These eddies form during the descent and spreading phase in the Gulf of Cadiz, where instabilities in the outflow current lead to anticyclonic rotations that can persist for years, with studies tracking their trajectories across the Canary Basin and beyond using RAFOS floats. The outflow's high salinity, typically 1 ppt above surrounding Atlantic waters, creates a distinct signal observable in hydrographic surveys, underscoring its role in the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) by adding salt and heat to intermediate depths.
Recent observations indicate variability in the outflow's properties, including a notable warming trend of 0.339 0.008C per decade in its deepest layers since 2013, driven by increased contributions from warmer Levantine Intermediate Water (LIW) and reduced WMDW formation due to altered buoyancy fluxes in the Mediterranean basin. As of 20232024, warming and salinification trends continue, with sea surface temperatures increasing at 0.45C per decade since 2004, enhancing dense water formation despite reduced WMDW contributions. Accompanying this is a slight weakening of the mean outflow transport, estimated at -0.847 0.129 Sv with a decreasing trend of +0.017 0.003 Sv per decade, potentially linked to submaximal exchange dynamics at the Strait of Gibraltar. These changes have implications for ocean acidification, with a pH decline of -0.0462 0.0006 per decade, and broader effects on North Atlantic circulation patterns amid ongoing climate variability. Overall, the Mediterranean outflow remains a critical component of global ocean dynamics, bridging Mediterranean evaporation-driven density gradients with Atlantic deep circulation.

Overview and Formation

Definition and significance

The Mediterranean outflow consists of a dense, saline water mass that flows westward from the Mediterranean Sea into the North Atlantic Ocean through the Strait of Gibraltar, forming the Mediterranean Outflow Water (MOW), which is distinguished by its salinity exceeding that of the surrounding Atlantic waters. This outflow represents a critical exchange between the semi-enclosed Mediterranean basin and the open Atlantic, occurring via the Strait of Gibraltar, a narrow passage approximately 13 km wide at its narrowest point with a sill depth of about 284 m at the Camarinal Sill.
Early observations of the Mediterranean outflow date to the late 19th and early 20th centuries through pioneering hydrographic surveys, with systematic studies beginning around 1912 under oceanographers like Nielsen, who conducted the first comprehensive surveys of Mediterranean circulation patterns. Modern understanding advanced significantly in the mid-20th century, particularly through the work of Wst in 1961, who applied the core method to trace water masses and elucidate the thermohaline cell, building on data from International Geophysical Year expeditions in the 1950s.
The Mediterranean outflow plays a pivotal role in the global thermohaline circulation by injecting warm, salty water into the North Atlantic, thereby influencing regional and broader ocean heat and salt budgets. It contributes an average volume transport of approximately 1 Sverdrup (Sv; 1 Sv = 10^6 m s), representing a net westward flux that sustains the meridional overturning circulation. This transport equates to roughly 30,000 km of water annually, underscoring its scale in maintaining oceanic density gradients and climate patterns.

Formation mechanisms

The Mediterranean Sea operates as a concentration basin, characterized by a net evaporation exceeding precipitation and river runoff by approximately 0.8 m/year, which progressively increases the salinity and density of its surface and intermediate waters, driving the formation of the dense outflow. This evaporative dominance transforms incoming Atlantic waters, elevating their salinity as they circulate through the basin, ultimately contributing to the high-density characteristics of the Mediterranean Outflow Water (MOW).
Thermohaline processes further amplify this density contrast, particularly through winter cooling in the eastern Mediterranean, where intense air-sea heat loss promotes deep convection and the formation of dense water masses such as Levantine Intermediate Water (LIW). Originating in regions like the Levantine and Aegean Seas, this LIW spreads westward via intermediate-depth circulation, filling the eastern and western basins while maintaining elevated salinity and density that sustain the outflow. These cascading waters integrate with other dense components, like Western Mediterranean Deep Water formed in the Gulf of Lions, enhancing the overall thermohaline forcing. Recent observations as of 2024 indicate variability in formation, with reduced Western Mediterranean Deep Water (WMDW) contributions due to milder winters, altered air-sea buoyancy fluxes, and increased influence from warmer Levantine Intermediate Water, potentially weakening the density-driven outflow.
At the Strait of Gibraltar, the outflow is regulated by hydraulic control at the Camarinal Sill, manifesting as a two-layer exchange where dense Mediterranean waters flow westward below approximately 200 m depth, countering the eastward Atlantic inflow. This regime reflects a density-driven maximal exchange influenced by rotation and sill topography.
Seasonal variations modulate this outflow, with maximum transports in spring (April) associated with variations in dense water properties and basin cooling effects that sharpen the interface between Atlantic and Mediterranean layers. Over longer timescales, paleoceanographic evidence from sediment cores in the Gulf of Cadiz reveals outflow intensification during glacial periods, linked to amplified aridity, reduced sea levels, and greater density contrasts that boosted exchange fluxes.

Physical Properties

Water composition

The Mediterranean outflow water exhibits a distinct chemical composition that sets it apart from the overlying Atlantic waters, primarily due to the intense evaporation and limited freshwater inputs within the Mediterranean basin. Its core salinity ranges from 38.4 to 38.5 practical salinity units (PSU), significantly higher than the ~35.5 PSU of the North Atlantic Central Water, reflecting the basin's net evaporative regime. The temperature of the outflow at the Strait of Gibraltar is approximately 1213C, which is warmer than the deep North Atlantic waters (typically 24C) but compensated by the high salinity to yield overall density.
Dissolved oxygen levels in the outflow are relatively low, around 200250 mol/kg, attributable to the stagnation and limited ventilation of deep Mediterranean waters, in contrast to the more oxygenated Atlantic deep waters (~280 mol/kg). Nutrient concentrations, such as phosphate, are elevated (e.g., ~0.30.6 mol/kg in deep layers) compared to open Atlantic values, largely from anthropogenic and natural coastal inputs along Mediterranean margins that accumulate in the poorly ventilated interior.
Tracer signatures further characterize the outflow's origin and age. Concentrations of chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) are moderately high, indicating a ventilation age of approximately 1020 years for the source waters, reflecting recent mixing in the Mediterranean's intermediate layers. Stable oxygen isotopes (O) in the outflow water are enriched (~1.41.7 relative to Vienna Standard Mean Ocean Water), confirming its evaporative formation and minimal dilution by precipitation or riverine inputs.
The outflow also carries suspended particulate matter, including fine sediments eroded from the seafloor of the Strait of Gibraltar due to high-velocity flow and turbulence, which contributes to its turbidity and aids in tracing the plume's path through nepheloid layers.

These properties are primarily measured using conductivity-temperature-depth (CTD) profilers deployed from research vessels, which provide high-resolution vertical casts, supplemented by autonomous gliders for sustained monitoring of plume evolution.

Flow Dynamics

Passage through the Strait of Gibraltar

The Mediterranean outflow navigates the Strait of Gibraltar as part of a vigorous two-layer exchange, where dense Mediterranean waters flow westward along the bottom beneath an eastward surface inflow of lighter Atlantic water, with net transports of approximately 0.68 Sv for the outflow and 0.72 Sv for the inflow. Over the Camarinal Sill, the primary topographic constriction in the eastern strait, the outflow accelerates into a supercritical regime, characterized by core velocities up to 1.5 m/s and a Froude number exceeding 1, reflecting the balance between flow inertia and gravitational restoration forces. This supercritical state persists westward, particularly at the Espartel Sill, the final constriction, where the flow maintains high momentum despite ongoing hydraulic adjustments.
The strait's channel morphology, narrowing to about 14 km at the Tarifa Narrows in the eastern section, intensifies shear at the interface between the opposing layers, fostering Kelvin-Helmholtz instabilities and enhanced vertical mixing. These instabilities contribute to the initial transformation of the outflow, as the narrow geometry constrains the dense layer to a thickness of roughly 150-200 m while promoting lateral friction against the steep sidewalls. Observational evidence from moored acoustic Doppler current profilers (ADCPs) and conductivity-temperature (CT) probes deployed across the Camarinal Sill channels confirms this bidirectional structure, with mean westward flows of 118-132 m/s per unit width in the northern and southern channels, respectively, exhibiting strong variability tied to the Western Alboran Gyre. Recent Argo float deployments in the 2020s, such as those in the Gulf of Cadiz during the PROTEVS Gibraltar campaign, have begun tracking the early plume evolution by profiling temperature and salinity anomalies immediately post-strait.
Initial entrainment of overlying North Atlantic Central Water begins prominently in the Tangier Basin west of the Camarinal Sill, increasing the outflow volume by approximately 4% from 0.74 Sv to 0.77 Sv while diluting core salinity by about 0.09 PSU (from 38.49 to 38.40) and warming temperatures by 0.1C. This mixing, driven by shear-induced turbulence, effectively dilutes the density anomaly of the pure Mediterranean water (S > 38.4 PSU), with the entrainment rate estimated at 0.03 Sv of Atlantic water. A post-sill hydraulic jump, particularly evident during ebb tides at the lee side of the Camarinal Sill, marks a key site of energy dissipation, where supercritical flow abruptly decelerates, converting kinetic energy into turbulent mixing and internal wave generation, with dissipation rates reaching up to 10^{-3} W/kg in the bottom boundary layer. The density contrast, primarily from elevated salinity in the Mediterranean source waters, provides the gravitational potential driving this entire transit. Beyond the Espartel Sill, the outflow initiates its descent along the continental slope.

Descent and spreading patterns

Upon exiting the Strait of Gibraltar, the Mediterranean outflow undergoes geostrophic adjustment as a dense gravity current, initiating a downslope flow along the Moroccan continental slope. This initial plunge is driven by the release of potential energy, with the water descending rapidly to depths of approximately 10001500 m over a distance of 100200 km, while experiencing significant entrainment of overlying North Atlantic Central Water.
The outflow then forms a main westward plume centered around 35N, propagating along the continental slope in the Gulf of Cdiz before bifurcating into northern and southern branches. The northern branch veers toward the Iberian margin, contributing to the upper core that follows the Portuguese slope, while the southern arm extends more offshore into the Atlantic interior, maintaining volume conservation through the continuity equation u = 0. This spreading pattern reflects topographic steering and Coriolis effects, with the lower core descending further and the overall flow adhering to isopycnal surfaces near = 27.7.
As the plume propagates, core velocities decay from initial values exceeding 1 m/s to 0.51 m/s due to bottom friction, mixing, and entrainment, accompanied by Ekman veering to the right in the Northern Hemisphere. The plume widens to 50100 km, broadening further by a factor of about 2 beyond major bathymetric features like the Cadiz Diapiric Ridge.
High-resolution numerical simulations, such as those using the Princeton Ocean Model, illustrate the double-core structure and chaotic aspects of the descent, where internal waves and topographic interactions contribute to intermittent spreading and instability. More recent efforts with the NEMO model in regional reanalyses highlight the influence of bathymetry on these pathways, confirming the bifurcation and downslope dynamics observed in field data.

Mesoscale Features and Interactions

Meddies: Formation and characteristics

Meddies are anticyclonic, lens-shaped eddies composed of relatively undiluted Mediterranean Outflow Water (MOW), typically exhibiting diameters of 50100 km and lifespans of 13 years. These mesoscale features form as isolated vortices that detach from the main MOW plume, preserving the high-salinity signature of the source water amid the surrounding North Atlantic waters.
The formation of meddies occurs primarily through baroclinic instability of the descending MOW plume as it flows westward along the Iberian continental slope. This instability arises during the plume's descent, where perturbations grow due to the release of potential energy, leading to the shedding of anticyclonic lenses; conditions such as a Richardson number (Ri) less than 0.25 promote intense vertical mixing and vortex detachment from the boundary current. Direct observations near Cape St. Vincent, Portugal, have confirmed this process, with meddies detaching from the undercurrent over periods of days to weeks. The descending plume serves as the primary source, generating meddies at rates estimated at 1520 per year in key formation regions like the Portimo Canyon and Estremadura Promontory.
In their cores, meddies maintain preserved salinity levels exceeding 36.5 PSU, with typical vertical thicknesses of 5001000 m centered at depths of 8001200 m. These cores exhibit anticyclonic rotation with periods of approximately 510 days, driven by azimuthal velocities up to 50 cm/s, which help isolate the lens from ambient mixing. The overall structure features a steep periphery that confines the high-salinity water, minimizing dilution over their lifespan.
Meddies were first identified in the 1980s through tracking with SOFAR floats, which revealed their persistent motion and longevity in the Canary Basin. Subsequent surveys, including RAFOS float deployments, have documented approximately 2030 meddies active at any given time, primarily in the Iberian and Canary Basins.
Each meddy encloses a volume of approximately 10^{12} m of MOW, transporting a significant fraction of the salt associated with the outflowequivalent to about 50% of the annual Mediterranean water salt flux.

Interactions with tides and bathymetry

The principal semidiurnal M2 tide in the Strait of Gibraltar, with a barotropic amplitude of approximately 1 m, propagates as an internal wave that modulates the Mediterranean outflow velocity by up to 35% through partial blocking over the Camarinal Sill. These semidiurnal fluctuations enhance vertical mixing within the outflow layer, increasing turbulent kinetic energy dissipation and altering the descent rate of dense water parcels.
Seafloor topography significantly steers the Mediterranean outflow after its exit from the strait, with features such as the Horseshoe Seamount chain and submarine canyons in the Gulf of Cadiz causing flow deflection, recirculation, and localized upwelling of nutrient-rich waters. The outflow often exhibits supercritical conditions over these bathymetric highs, characterized by a Froude number $ Fr = \frac{U}{\sqrt{g' h}} > 1 $, where $ U $ is the flow speed, $ g' $ the reduced gravity, and $ h $ the layer thickness, leading to hydraulic jumps and intensified entrainment.
Interactions between meddiessubsurface anticyclonic eddies formed from the outflowand topography can result in vortex grounding on continental slopes, promoting erosion of the meddy core and subsequent release of entrained salt into surrounding waters. Acoustic tracking using RAFOS floats has revealed that meddies may become trapped in topographic depressions, such as basins near seamounts, prolonging their lifespan but limiting lateral propagation.
Observational evidence from glider deployments in the 2010s highlights tidal pumping mechanisms that drive periodic variations in salt flux through the Gulf of Cadiz, with enhanced transport during ebb phases. Recent studies from 2023 further demonstrate tidal rectification effects in the region, where nonlinear interactions convert tidal energy into mean flows that sustain the outflow's westward spreading.
Spring-neap tidal cycles introduce variability in meddy generation, with reduced mixing during neap tides allowing for more coherent instability development and amplified eddy formation along the Iberian margin.

Broader Impacts

Influence on North Atlantic circulation

The Mediterranean Outflow Water (MOW) integrates into the North Atlantic as a distinct water mass, forming a prominent salinity maximum at intermediate depths between approximately 700 and 1500 m, where it occupies the layer between North Atlantic Central Water above and Labrador Sea Water below. This high-salinity signature, typically exceeding 36.5 practical salinity units, persists as MOW spreads westward and northward, mixing with surrounding waters while maintaining its identity through the eastern basin. By introducing warmer, saltier, and oxygen-rich water, MOW ventilates the intermediate layers of the Eastern North Atlantic, enhancing oxygen concentrations and reducing nutrient levels in this depth range.
The influx of MOW influences North Atlantic gyre dynamics primarily through salt advection, which increases density gradients and strengthens the subtropical gyre circulation by promoting southward return flows at intermediate depths. This salt transport contributes to the preconditioning of North Atlantic Deep Water (NADW) formation sites in the Nordic Seas, where the elevated salinity along key isopycnals enhances the density of waters destined for deep convection. MOW exerts a positive feedback on the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) by augmenting meridional density gradients at intermediate levels, thereby bolstering the overturning strength. Regionally, the descent and spreading of dense MOW modulate the Azores Front by inducing zonal flows through water mass transformation and entrainment in the Gulf of Cadiz, which in turn affects the downstream separation latitude of the Gulf Stream. Additionally, the cascading of dense MOW intrusions along the continental slope generates intensified near-bottom currents, triggering benthic storms that resuspend sediments and enhance particle fluxes across the eastern North Atlantic seafloor.

Global ocean and climate implications

The Mediterranean Outflow Water (MOW) serves as a critical component of the global thermohaline circulation (THC), functioning as a "conveyor belt" by exporting high-salinity water into the North Atlantic, which sustains the density-driven overturning of the ocean. This salt export promotes the formation of North Atlantic Deep Water (NADW), a key driver of the THC that transports heat and nutrients globally. Indirectly, the enhanced salinity from MOW influences Antarctic Bottom Water (AABW) formation through the THC's connectivity in the Southern Ocean, where NADW upwelling facilitates AABW production and deep ocean ventilation.
Variability in the MOW outflow exerts feedbacks on atmospheric circulation, notably influencing the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) through alterations in North Atlantic salinity and THC strength. During periods of intensified outflow, increased salt advection can amplify NAO-related pressure patterns, affecting mid-latitude weather. Recent warming since 2015 has been accompanied by a slight weakening of the outflow transport, linked to reduced Western Mediterranean Deep Water formation despite increased evaporation. This stems from surface warming and altered buoyancy fluxes, as observed in basin-wide salinity trends. A 2025 analysis of data from 20032017 indicates no statistically significant trend in volume transport but a weakening in salt export, driven by surface evaporation and Gibraltar exchange dynamics.
Paleoclimate records from deep-sea cores reveal that MOW outflow experienced shutdowns or significant reductions during Heinrich events, periods of abrupt ice-rafting in the North Atlantic that disrupted THC. These proxies, including sediment grain size and isotopic signatures, indicate weakened MOW ventilation, which contributed to reduced global CO2 sequestration by limiting deep ocean carbon storage and promoting atmospheric CO2 outgassing via the Atlantic-Pacific seesaw.
Addressing monitoring gaps requires sustained observatories such as EMSO-Azores, which provide real-time data on MOW pathways and variability along the Iberian Margin. Recent 2024 studies underscore the underestimation of meddy-induced salt export in global ocean models, where eddy parameterizations fail to capture the full lateral and vertical transport of Mediterranean salinity, leading to biases in THC simulations.
