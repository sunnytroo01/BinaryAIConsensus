ReConStruction
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Reconstruction era (18651877) was a transformative and contentious period in United States history immediately following the American Civil War, during which the federal government worked to rebuild the war-torn Southern states, secure the rights of approximately four million newly emancipated African Americans, and reintegrate the former Confederacy into the Union while addressing the profound legacies of slavery. This era marked an ambitious experiment in interracial democracy, driven by competing visions of national healing, racial equality, and political restoration, ultimately reshaping the constitutional framework of citizenship and federal authority.
Reconstruction unfolded in two primary phases: Presidential Reconstruction under Presidents Abraham Lincoln and Andrew Johnson, and Congressional (or Radical) Reconstruction led by Republican lawmakers. Lincoln's initial Ten Percent Plan, announced in December 1863, aimed for a swift restoration by allowing Southern states to form new governments once 10 percent of their voters pledged loyalty to the Union and supported emancipation, though it faced resistance in Congress for not sufficiently protecting freedpeople's rights. After Lincoln's assassination in April 1865, Johnson implemented a lenient approach, pardoning most former Confederates and enabling Southern states to establish governments that abolished slavery but enacted restrictive Black Codes to limit African Americans' mobility, labor choices, and civil liberties, often relegating them to exploitative sharecropping systems. In response, Congress overrode Johnson's vetoes of key legislation, including the Civil Rights Act of 1866which defined U.S.-born persons as citizens entitled to equal protectionand the Freedmen's Bureau extensions to aid the transition from slavery. The Reconstruction Acts of 1867 divided the South into five military districts, required new state constitutions granting manhood suffrage regardless of race, and mandated ratification of the Fourteenth Amendment (1868), which established birthright citizenship, due process, and equal protection under the law. This phase culminated in the Fifteenth Amendment (1870), prohibiting racial discrimination in voting, and the Enforcement Acts (18701871), which empowered federal intervention against voter suppression and violence.
Key achievements included unprecedented political empowerment for African Americans, who formed the backbone of Southern Republican coalitions and elected over 600 to state legislatures, 16 to Congress (including Senators Hiram Revels and Blanche K. Bruce), and numerous local officials, shattering racial hierarchies without imposing "black supremacy." Socially, freedpeople reunited families, established independent Black churches as community hubs, and prioritized education, leading to the creation of the South's first statewide public school systems under Republican governments. Economically, these administrations pursued infrastructure like railroads and equitable taxation to foster a "New South," though corruption and overambitious projects strained support. Federal efforts also dismantled the Ku Klux Klan through prosecutions and military action by 1871, temporarily curbing organized terror.
Despite these gains, Reconstruction faced fierce opposition and ultimately faltered due to pervasive racial violence, waning Northern commitment, and institutional setbacks. White supremacist groups like the Ku Klux Klan orchestrated at least 2,000 lynchings and dozens of mass killings between 1865 and 1877 to intimidate Black voters, educators, and leaders, with perpetrators rarely facing justice. Events such as the 1873 Colfax Massacre in Louisiana, where dozens of Black militiamen were slaughtered, exemplified the brutal resistance that eroded Republican control state by state. Supreme Court rulings, including the 1873 Slaughterhouse Cases, limited the scope of federal protections, while economic depression in the North after 1873 shifted public focus away from Southern reforms. The era ended with the Compromise of 1877, a political deal resolving the disputed presidential election by installing Rutherford B. Hayes in exchange for withdrawing federal troops from the South, allowing Democrats to "redeem" the region and impose segregation, disenfranchisement, and debt peonage that persisted into the 20th century.
Reconstruction's legacy endures as an "unfinished revolution," embedding enduring constitutional safeguards against inequalityrevived during the 1960s Civil Rights Movementwhile highlighting the fragility of democratic progress amid entrenched racism and economic disparity.

Overview

Background and Purpose

ReConStruction was the tenth North American Science Fiction Convention (NASFiC), an occasional event organized under the auspices of the World Science Fiction Society (WSFS) to provide a major science fiction and fantasy convention in North America during years when the annual World Science Fiction Convention (Worldcon) is held outside the continent. NASFiC serves as a continental counterpart to Worldcon, focusing on the celebration of speculative fiction genres through panels, author readings, art shows, and fan activities, while adhering to WSFS traditions such as site selection voting by Worldcon members.
Held in 2010, ReConStruction followed the ninth NASFiC, Tuckercon/Archon 31 in Collinsville, Illinois, in 2007, and preceded the eleventh, Detcon1 in Detroit, Michigan, in 2014. The event's necessity arose from the selection of Aussiecon 4 as the 68th Worldcon, scheduled for Melbourne, Australia, making international travel prohibitive for many North American fans and necessitating a domestic alternative to maintain the continuity of large-scale genre conventions. This placement in the NASFiC sequence underscored its role in bridging gaps in the WSFS calendar, ensuring accessibility to the North American science fiction community.
ReConStruction was founded and hosted by the Southern Alliance of Fandom Enthusiasts, Inc. (SAFE), a 501(c)(3) nonprofit organization dedicated to promoting science fiction and fantasy fandom in the southeastern United States. The convention emphasized community engagement within these genres, featuring programming that highlighted literary, artistic, and cultural aspects of speculative fiction without overlapping with Worldcon's global scope. Guests of honor included writer Eric Flint, fan Juanita Coulson, artist Brad Foster, and editor Toni Weisskopf.

Dates, Location, and Attendance

ReConStruction, the tenth North American Science Fiction Convention (NASFiC), took place from August 5 to 8, 2010.
The event was held in downtown Raleigh, North Carolina, United States, selected as the site following the 2008 World Science Fiction Convention's selection of Melbourne, Australia, for 2010. Primary venues included the Raleigh Convention Center for main programming and exhibits, the Raleigh Marriott City Center for additional functions and guest areas, and the Sheraton Raleigh Hotel for overflow accommodations and parties, all chosen for their central location and accessibility via public transit and nearby highways.
Attendance at ReConStruction reflected a modest scale typical of NASFiCs compared to Worldcons, with strong local support from the Southern Fandom Confederation contributing to the turnout.

Bidding and Site Selection

NASFiC Selection Process

The North American Science Fiction Convention (NASFiC) was established through an amendment to the World Science Fiction Society (WSFS) Constitution, ratified at the 1976 Worldcon in Kansas City, Missouri (MidAmeriCon). This provision, now codified in Article 4, Section 4.8 of the Constitution, mandates a NASFiC in North America during any year when the World Science Fiction Convention (Worldcon) is scheduled outside the continent, ensuring ongoing continental representation in major science fiction events. The first NASFiC was held in 1975 in Los Angeles, California, with subsequent events held irregularly based on Worldcon locations.
For the 2010 NASFiC, the process was triggered automatically by the selection of Aussiecon 4 as the 2010 Worldcon site during the WSFS Business Meeting at Denvention 3, the 2008 Worldcon in Denver, Colorado. Under Article 4 of the WSFS Constitution, site selection for a NASFiC follows the same procedures as for Worldcons, requiring bids to be submitted in advance and the winner determined by written ballot at the administering conventionin this case, Anticipation, the 2009 Worldcon in Montreal, Quebec, Canada. Bidders were required to certify their sites' availability and compliance with WSFS standards prior to voting.
Voting for the 2010 NASFiC site took place at Anticipation from August 6 to August 8, 2009, open to all WSFS members via mail-in or in-person ballots. A total of 276 valid votes were cast, with the Raleigh, North Carolina, bid emerging as the winner after meeting certification requirements. This outcome was announced during the WSFS Business Meeting on August 9, 2009, formalizing ReConStruction as the tenth NASFiC.

Raleigh Bid and Competition

The Raleigh bid for the 2010 NASFiC, organized by the Southern Alliance of Fandom Enthusiasts (SAFE), was co-chaired by Warren Buff and Michael D. Pederson. The bid proposed hosting the convention from August 5 to 8 at the Raleigh Convention Center, capitalizing on the city's recent infrastructure improvements, including a newly expanded convention facility completed in 2008. Local support was evident through promotional efforts documented in city records, which highlighted Raleigh's growing status as a hub for conventions and tourism.
Although the Raleigh bid was the only one formally filed by the deadline at Anticipation, the 2009 Worldcon in Montreal, it faced scattered write-in opposition during the site selection vote. Raleigh secured victory with 241 votes out of 276 cast, alongside 3 invalid ballots, demonstrating strong support from Worldcon members. Competing write-ins included Pasadena, California, which received 19 votes; Durham, North Carolina (1 vote); and Ridgecrest, California (1 vote). Humorous or joke write-ins garnered minor attention, such as "Peggy Rae's House" (referencing fan Peggy Rae Pavlat's home) and "Xerps," a fictional planet from science fiction lore.
The bidding process underscored the relatively low-key competition for NASFiCs compared to Worldcons, with Raleigh's proactive promotion at prior conventions like Denvention 3 (2008 Worldcon) helping build momentum. This win marked SAFE's efforts to bring major fandom events to the region.

Organization and Facilities

Committee Structure

The organizing committee for ReConStruction was established shortly after the Raleigh bid secured the NASFiC site selection at the 2009 World Science Fiction Convention in Montreal, enabling a structured buildup to the 2010 event.
Warren Buff served as chair, providing leadership drawn from his experience in southern fandom and prior bidding committees to coordinate all aspects of the convention. Sydnie Krause held the position of treasurer, managing the budget, membership fees, and financial reporting for the nonprofit operation. Christopher Hensley acted as secretary, responsible for official documentation, meeting minutes, and internal communications. The committee's composition reflected a collaborative team from the regional fandom community, with additional key roles assigned to ensure smooth execution across various functions.
Mike Willmoth oversaw both Events and Programming, focusing on scheduling and activity coordination. Dina Krause managed Facilities, handling site arrangements and infrastructure needs. Russ Miller directed Member Services, addressing registration, information desks, and attendee support. Chris Ross led Operations, supervising on-site logistics and volunteer coordination. Tim Miller handled Publications, producing convention materials like the program book and newsletters. Stacey Helton McConnell managed Publicity, promoting the event through media outreach and marketing efforts. Overall, the committee operated under the oversight of the Southern Alliance of Fandom Enthusiasts, Inc. (SAFE), a 501(c)(3) nonprofit organization supporting southern science fiction conventions.

Venues and Logistics

ReConStruction utilized a combination of venues in downtown Raleigh to accommodate its programming and attendee needs. The primary events, including panels, the dealers' room, art show, and masquerade, were hosted at the Raleigh Convention Center, a modern facility that opened in 2008 and provided ample exhibit and meeting space for the convention's scale. Auxiliary functions, such as room parties reframed as "meet and greet" events and some programming, took place at the adjacent Raleigh Marriott City Center and the Sheraton Raleigh Hotel, which offered connected indoor access via skybridges and a parking deck to facilitate movement between sites, particularly during inclement weather.
Logistical planning emphasized efficient attendee flow and resource allocation for an expected capacity of around 1,500 to 2,000 participants, though actual hotel room pickups fell short at approximately 350, contributing to lower-than-expected overall attendance of around 900 participants. Room blocks were secured at the Marriott (at $129 per night plus tax, with a cutoff of July 5, 2010) and Sheraton ($119 per night plus tax, extended cutoff July 23, 2010), totaling an initial commitment of 802 room nights that was successfully renegotiated downward to 529 just two months prior to avoid significant attrition penalties. Registration operated via online preregistration until July 15, 2010 (at rates up to $120), transitioning to at-the-door sales from July 26 onward, with day rates available; the process was managed through PayPal and on-site desks, ensuring program book inclusion for early registrants. Transportation logistics included guidance for airport shuttles from Raleigh-Durham International Airport (about 15 miles away), though no dedicated convention service was provided, relying instead on taxis, rideshares, and public options.
Operations and safety were overseen by division head Chris Ross, who coordinated door guards, rovers, and general logistics using a team of college students recruited via complimentary badges, ensuring smooth execution despite last-minute assembly. Facilities coordination fell to Dina Krause, whose renegotiation of the Sheraton contract saved approximately $49,000 by adjusting room commitments and attrition clauses from 80% to 60%, preventing a potential budget shortfall. Safety measures included bans on laser pointers and vuvuzelas in convention areas to mitigate risks, along with provisions for disability access such as mobility scooter rentals delivered to the sites; child care was available through the Sheraton at additional cost. Downtown accessibility proved a success, with the venues' proximity and covered pathways minimizing weather disruptions, as demonstrated by the banquet's rain-free execution via the parking deckthough challenges arose from oversized function spaces like the exhibit hall, which felt underutilized given lower-than-expected turnout.

Program and Participants

Guests of Honor

ReConStruction featured prominent figures in science fiction literature, art, and fandom as its Guests of Honor, selected to reflect the convention's emphasis on North American speculative fiction traditions.
Eric Flint served as the Guest of Honor, recognized for his contributions to alternate history science fiction. A former labor historian, Flint gained prominence with his 1632 series, which imagines a 17th-century Europe altered by the arrival of a modern West Virginia town, beginning with the novel 1632 in 2000. He also edited the collaborative Grantville Gazette anthology series for Baen Books, fostering community-driven storytelling in the shared universe.
Brad W. Foster was the Artist Guest of Honor, celebrated for his extensive work as an illustrator and cartoonist within science fiction fandom. Since the 1970s, Foster has produced thousands of pieces for fanzines, convention program books, and publications like File 770 and Challenger, often blending whimsical cartoons with genre themes; his art has earned multiple Hugo Award nominations for Best Fan Artist. At ReConStruction, his portfolio was showcased in the art show, where he also received second place for science fiction art.
Juanita Coulson acted as the Fan Guest of Honor, honoring her decades-long impact on fannish culture. A pioneer in the field, Coulson edited the influential fanzine Yandro from 1953 to 1986, which won the Hugo Award for Best Fanzine in 1965 (and received multiple nominations); she is also a foundational filk singer, performing and popularizing science fiction-themed folk music at conventions, and co-organized early events like Midwestcon. Her selection highlighted her status as the first woman named Fan Guest of Honor at a Worldcon, in 1972.
Toni Weisskopf functioned as Toastmaster, bringing her expertise as publisher of Baen Books, where she has championed accessible science fiction since succeeding founder Jim Baen in 2006. Weisskopf's career includes editorial roles that supported authors like Flint, emphasizing innovative distribution models such as ebooks in the early 2000s.
These invitees engaged attendees through panels on writing and publishing, autograph sessions, and keynote speeches that tied into NASFiC's focus on speculative genres, enhancing the convention's celebratory atmosphere.

Programming and Events

The programming at ReConStruction, the 2010 North American Science Fiction Convention (NASFiC), was coordinated by Program Director Mike Willmoth, who employed a team-based approach to manage the schedule, drawing on participant questionnaires, database tools, and input from specialists in various areas to create a balanced lineup of events. This process prioritized fixed events such as the opening ceremony, masquerade, and dances, while incorporating flexible program items like panels and workshops, ensuring participants were scheduled for a minimum number of appearances to support comp memberships and refunds.
Programming tracks encompassed a range of science fiction and fantasy activities, including author readings, artist showcases, fan panels, workshops, and filk sessions. Fan panels addressed diverse topics such as health in fandomexploring ways to integrate exercise with media consumption like watching Doctor Who on a treadmilland crossing genres, where writers discussed blending elements from space opera, horror, sword-and-sorcery, and Westerns in post-pulp narratives. Filk sessions featured original science fiction-themed music, with performers like Tom Erlich delivering a cappella ballads and James Fulkerson contributing humorous adaptations of pop songs to convention life, reflecting filk's roots in 1950s-1960s fandom and its evolution into a distinct genre with influences from folk, parody, and Tolkien-inspired works. Workshops and artist showcases highlighted creative skills, including steampunk costuming demonstrations, where attendees displayed elaborate Victorian-inspired outfits with brass goggles, loupes, and sextants evoking Jules Verne adventures.
Key events formed the convention's core social and interactive experiences, such as the art show displaying speculative artwork, the dealers' room stocked with books, T-shirts, and niche items like genealogy kits for handling historical documents, and dedicated gaming areas for role-playing and strategy sessions. The masquerade encouraged elaborate costumes blending fantasy and science fiction themes, while filk and dance events provided evening entertainment. As a NASFiC held concurrently with the overseas Worldcon in Australia, the programming included unique discussions on international fandom, bridging North American and global speculative communities through panels on cross-cultural influences in genre fiction. Over 1,000 participants engaged in these activities, fostering a vibrant atmosphere of shared enthusiasm for SF/fantasy topics.

Awards and Legacy

Awards Presented

ReConStruction, as the 10th North American Science Fiction Convention (NASFiC), served as the venue for presenting three notable awards in the science fiction and fantasy genres: the Golden Duck Awards, Chesley Awards, and Sidewise Awards. These ceremonies were integrated into the convention's programming schedule, typically held in dedicated sessions to highlight contributions to literature, art, and alternate history.
The Golden Duck Awards recognize excellence in science fiction literature for children, aiming to promote quality works in the genre for young readers. Presented annually since 1992 at Worldcons or NASFiCs, the 2010 edition honored books from the previous year. Winners included The Swamps of Sleethe: Poems from Beyond the Solar System by Jack Prelutsky (illustrated by Mari Takabayashi) in the Picture Book category, Z. Rex by Steve Cole in the Eleanor Cameron Award for Middle Grades, and Catching Fire by Suzanne Collins in the Hal Clement Award for Young Adult. These selections underscored the awards' focus on imaginative storytelling accessible to youth, with Catching Fire notably part of the Hunger Games series that gained widespread acclaim.
The Chesley Awards, administered by the Association of Science Fiction and Fantasy Artists (ASFA), honor outstanding artistic achievements in the fields of science fiction and fantasy. The 2010 ceremony at ReConStruction announced winners across multiple categories, such as Best Cover Illustration (Hardcover) to Raoul Vitale for "Unrequited," Best Magazine Illustration to Eric Fortune for "Allure" in Realms of Fantasy, and Best Gaming-Related Illustration to Rebecca Guay for "Gwenevere." Other notable recipients included Patrick Jones for Best Unpublished Work with "Death of Diana" and John Picacio for Best Art Director. These awards highlighted the vital role of visual art in SF/F media, recognizing both published and conceptual pieces.
The Sidewise Awards, established in 1995 to celebrate alternate history fiction, were also presented during the convention for works published in 2009. In the Long Form category, Robert Conroy received the honor for his novel 1942, an exploration of a hypothetical Japanese invasion of the continental United States. The Short Form award went to Alastair Reynolds for "The Fixation," a story published in The Solaris Book of New Science Fiction, Vol. 3. These selections emphasized innovative narratives diverging from historical events, with Conroy's work exemplifying detailed speculative military history.
The awards ceremonies fostered community engagement at ReConStruction, drawing attendees to celebrate genre achievements and often featuring speeches from winners or presenters.

Impact and Aftermath

ReConStruction drew approximately 1,200 attendees to Raleigh, North Carolina, over its four-day run from August 5 to 8, 2010. The convention received positive feedback in prominent fanzines, with reviewers praising its programming and community atmosphere in publications like File 770 and reports from Locus Online.
Media coverage highlighted the event's role in bringing science fiction enthusiasts to the city, including a feature on News 14 Carolina describing the convention's activities and guest appearances. Additional mentions appeared in SF Scope, noting the successful execution of awards ceremonies.
In the long term, ReConStruction bolstered the Southern science fiction community by fostering regional connections and demonstrating effective convention hosting in the Southeast, influencing subsequent bids like Detcon1, the 2014 NASFiC in Detroit. The event provided valuable lessons on logistics and programming for future North American conventions, contributing to the stability of the NASFiC series.
Archival materials from ReConStruction are preserved through the official website at reconstructionsf.org (now accessible via the Internet Archive) and the central NASFiC.org repository, ensuring ongoing access to programs, photos, and reports. Notably, the convention proceeded without major controversies, maintaining a positive reputation within fandom.
