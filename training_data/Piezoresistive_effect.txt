Piezoresistive effect
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The piezoresistive effect describes the change in electrical resistivity of a semiconductor or metal material when subjected to mechanical strain, distinct from mere geometric alterations in dimensions. This phenomenon arises primarily in semiconductors like silicon and germanium, where the effect can be orders of magnitude larger than in metals due to modifications in carrier mobility and density.
Discovered in 1954 by Charles S. Smith at Bell Telephone Laboratories, the piezoresistive effect revolutionized strain measurement by enabling semiconductor-based gauges with sensitivity far exceeding traditional metallic ones. In semiconductors, mechanical stress alters the band structure, shifting energy levels and affecting the scattering of charge carriers, which in turn modifies resistivity without generating voltageunlike the related piezoelectric effect. The relative change in resistance is quantified by the gauge factor $ K = \frac{\Delta R / R}{\varepsilon} $, where $ \varepsilon $ is strain; for doped silicon, $ K $ can reach 100200, compared to about 2 for metals. Piezoresistive coefficients, such as $ \pi_{44} = 138.1 \times 10^{-11} $ m/N for p-type silicon, describe this anisotropy, with maximum sensitivity often along the direction on (100) wafers.
This effect underpins numerous applications in microelectromechanical systems (MEMS), including pressure sensors for biomedical monitoring (e.g., blood pressure) and automotive fuel levels, as well as accelerometers for vibration and shock detection in safety systems. Configurations like Wheatstone bridges amplify small resistance changes for precise readout, making piezoresistive sensors integral to modern electronics despite challenges like temperature sensitivity. Emerging nanomaterials, such as zinc oxide nanowires with gauge factors up to 1250, extend its utility to high-sensitivity strain detection.

Fundamentals

Definition and Principles

The piezoresistive effect refers to the change in electrical resistivity of a material, such as semiconductors or metals, when subjected to mechanical stress or strain. This phenomenon is distinct from mere geometric alterations in resistance, as it involves a fundamental modification in the material's electrical properties rather than solely dimensional changes like elongation or cross-sectional reduction. In practical terms, it enables the conversion of mechanical deformation into measurable electrical signals, forming the basis for strain sensors.
At its core, the piezoresistive effect comprises intrinsic and extrinsic components. The intrinsic effect arises from stress-induced alterations in the material's band structure or carrier mobility, directly changing the resistivity without geometric influence. In contrast, the extrinsic effect stems from dimensional changes under strain, such as length increase or area decrease, which indirectly affect resistance through geometry alonea principle akin to traditional foil strain gauges but amplified in piezoresistive materials. This distinction highlights why piezoresistivity offers enhanced sensitivity beyond simple geometric responses.
The sensitivity of the piezoresistive effect is quantified by the gauge factor (GF), defined as the ratio of the relative change in resistance to the applied strain:
GF=R/R,
\text{GF} = \frac{\Delta R / R}{\varepsilon},
GF=R/R,
where R\Delta RR is the change in resistance, RRR is the original resistance, and \varepsilon is the mechanical strain. For metals, typical GF values range from 2 to 4, primarily driven by geometric effects. In semiconductors, however, GF can reach 50 to 200 due to the dominant intrinsic contributions, enabling higher precision in sensing applications.
Understanding the effect requires considering stress orientations relative to the current flow. Longitudinal stress acts parallel to the strain direction, maximizing alignment with resistance changes, while transverse stress applies perpendicularly, often yielding opposing or reduced effects depending on material symmetry.

Historical Development

Early Observations

The piezoresistive effect was first observed in the mid-19th century through experiments on metallic wires subjected to mechanical tension. In 1856, William Thomson, known as Lord Kelvin, reported changes in the electrical conductivity of iron and copper wires when elongated using a modified Wheatstone bridge setup, noting that the resistance increased under tension and linking these observations to broader electrokinetic phenomena in metals. These findings demonstrated that mechanical stress could alter electrical properties beyond simple dimensional changes, though the effect was subtle and primarily attributed to geometric factors at the time. Subsequent confirmations, such as those by Tomlinson, further measured direction- and temperature-dependent conductivity variations in strained metals, reinforcing the initial observations.
In the early 20th century, systematic studies on metals under high pressure and tension established piezoresistivity as a distinct phenomenon separate from purely geometric resistance changes. Percy W. Bridgman conducted pioneering experiments in the 1920s, including measurements of resistance variations in various metals under hydrostatic pressures up to several thousand atmospheres and uniaxial tension, revealing anisotropic responses in single crystals. His 1932 tensor formulation described resistance changes in homogeneous stressed crystals, providing a mathematical framework that accounted for stress-induced alterations in conductivity independent of length and cross-sectional area variations. These works highlighted the intrinsic material response to deformation, though the magnitude remained small compared to later semiconductor discoveries.
Early theoretical models in the pre-1950 era attributed piezoresistive changes in metals to lattice compression affecting electron density and increased electron scattering due to strained atomic arrangements. In 1935, J.W. Cookson proposed a model emphasizing conductivity shifts from stress-induced modifications in electron paths within the metallic lattice, distinguishing piezoresistance from overall resistance variations. These explanations focused on classical electron transport in metals, without invoking band structure complexities seen in semiconductors, and aligned with observed small intrinsic effects.
Despite these advances, pre-1950 piezoresistivity in metals exhibited low sensitivity, with gauge factors typically ranging from 1 to 4largely comprising a geometric contribution of about 2 plus a minor intrinsic componentlimiting practical applications to basic scientific measurements rather than engineering uses. The modest effect sizes and challenges in precise strain control hindered broader adoption until explorations in semiconductors revealed much larger responses.

Key Milestones in Semiconductors

In 1954, Charles S. Smith at Bell Laboratories discovered the giant piezoresistive effect in semiconductors, observing resistivity changes in silicon and germanium under strain with gauge factors reaching up to 100200, far exceeding those in metals and prompting a shift toward semiconductor-based sensing. This breakthrough, detailed in his seminal paper, highlighted the material's potential for high-sensitivity transducers due to the piezoresistive coefficients' dependence on carrier mobility and concentration in doped crystals.
During the 1960s, piezoresistive semiconductors advanced toward practical applications, with the integration of doped silicon into strain gauges enabling more reliable and compact designs compared to metallic foils. Kulite Semiconductor Products, founded in 1959, produced the first commercially available silicon-based piezoresistive strain gauges in the early 1960s, incorporating optimized doping levels to enhance sensitivity and temperature stability for aerospace and industrial uses. These developments laid the groundwork for early microelectromechanical systems (MEMS), where piezoresistors were diffused into silicon substrates to measure strain in diaphragms and cantilevers.
The 1980s and 1990s marked the widespread commercialization of piezoresistive pressure sensors, driven by advances in silicon micromachining that allowed batch fabrication of integrated devices. Kulite's ongoing innovations, including ruggedized transducers for harsh environments, contributed to their adoption in automotive and aviation sectors, with sensors achieving pressures up to 1000 bar and response times under 1 ms. By the 1990s, full-scale MEMS production enabled cost-effective piezoresistive sensors for consumer electronics, exemplified by the first integrated silicon pressure sensors reaching market maturity around 1990. Initial explorations of nanostructures during this era hinted at amplified effects, setting the stage for subsequent giant piezoresistance observations.
From the 2000s to the 2020s, research emphasized doping optimizations and hybrid structures to boost performance metrics like gauge factors beyond 100 while addressing limitations in linearity and thermal drift. Studies on silicon nanowires demonstrated gauge factors exceeding 1000 through surface and quantum confinement effects, with doping profiles tailored via ion implantation to maximize carrier scattering under strain. Hybrid approaches, combining silicon with nanomaterials like graphene or carbon nanotubes, yielded flexible sensors with sensitivities up to 232 kPa^{-1} in low-pressure ranges. Recent works, including Gaussian doping models for temperature compensation, have improved accuracy in predicting temperature coefficients. These innovations continue to expand piezoresistive semiconductors into high-temperature and multifunctional sensing platforms.

Physical Mechanisms

Piezoresistivity in Metals

In metals, the piezoresistive effect primarily stems from alterations in the electron mean free path induced by stress-induced lattice distortions, which subtly modify electron scattering rates while causing negligible changes to the overall band structure. This mechanism contrasts with more pronounced electronic rearrangements in other materials, as metallic conduction relies on a sea of delocalized free electrons whose paths are only marginally affected by deformation. Seminal studies under hydrostatic pressure, such as those by Bridgman, established that these changes lead to small resistivity variations, on the order of fractional shifts tied directly to the applied stress tensor.
Quantitatively, piezoresistive coefficients in metals are notably low, typically ranging from less than 1011Pa110^{-11} \, \mathrm{Pa}^{-1}1011Pa1 in transition metals like copper to higher values around 701011Pa170 \times 10^{-11} \, \mathrm{Pa}^{-1}701011Pa1 in alkali metals. For copper, 1011Pa1\pi \approx 10^{-11} \, \mathrm{Pa}^{-1}1011Pa1, reflecting the weak intrinsic response. The overall gauge factor (GF), defined as (R/R)/(\Delta R / R) / \epsilon(R/R)/ where \epsilon is strain, is thus dominated by geometric contributions such as dimensional changes (L/L\Delta L / LL/L), yielding GF values near 2 for typical metals rather than intrinsic material sensitivity. In polycrystalline metals, this response appears isotropic due to orientational averaging, simplifying practical measurements but limiting directional tunability.
An illustrative case is observed in tungsten filaments under compressive stress, where resistance exhibits a slight increase attributable to the combined geometric and piezoresistive effects, though the intrinsic component remains minor. Metals' lower sensitivity compared to semiconductors arises from the absence of carrier-type dependencelacking distinct electron and hole contributionsand the free-electron model's resilience to stress, which minimally impacts mobility or density of states without bandgap involvement. This renders metals suitable for basic strain gauging but inferior for high-precision sensing applications.

Piezoresistive Effect in Bulk Semiconductors

In bulk semiconductors such as silicon (Si) and germanium (Ge), the piezoresistive effect primarily stems from strain-induced modifications to the electronic band structure and carrier transport properties. Applied mechanical strain alters the lattice, leading to changes in the energy levels of conduction and valence bands. In n-type Si, which features six degenerate conduction band minima (valleys) along the 100 directions, uniaxial strain lifts this degeneracy by splitting the valleys into groups with different energies, typically separating two longitudinal valleys from four transverse ones. This redistribution shifts electrons toward lower-energy valleys with higher effective masses, reducing overall carrier mobility and increasing resistivity. In Ge, a similar multi-valley structure exists, but with eight valleys, resulting in comparable but slightly weaker effects due to its indirect bandgap. For p-type materials, strain primarily affects the valence band, causing splitting and warping of the degenerate light- and heavy-hole bands at the -point, which modifies hole effective masses and scattering rates more dramatically than in n-type counterparts.
The doping dependence of piezoresistance is pronounced, with p-type Si and Ge exhibiting significantly larger coefficients than n-type due to the greater sensitivity of hole mobility to strain. In lightly doped p-type Si (e.g., boron concentration ~10^{15} cm^{-3}), the shear piezoresistive coefficient _{44} 138 10^{-11} Pa^{-1}, driven by valence band deformation that enhances anisotropic hole transport (decreasing to around 70 10^{-11} Pa^{-1} at higher doping levels >10^{18} cm^{-3}). In contrast, n-type Si shows smaller longitudinal coefficients (_{11} -100 10^{-11} Pa^{-1} along 100), as the effect relies more on valley repopulation than band warping. Higher doping levels (e.g., >10^{18} cm^{-3}) suppress the effect in both types by increasing ionized impurity scattering, which dominates over strain-induced changes and reduces coefficients by up to 50% or more. This dependence arises because heavy doping screens deformation potentials and alters carrier lifetimes.
A key physical framework for understanding these phenomena in Si is the resistor network model, based on the multi-valley semiconductor theory, which treats the conduction band valleys as parallel resistors in an anisotropic network. Under strain, the conductivities of individual valleys change due to shifts in their energy ellipsoids, with the total resistivity modeled as the inverse sum of valley contributions: ^{-1} = (n_i _i / m_i^), where n_i, _i, and m_i^ are the carrier density, mobility, and effective mass for valley i. This framework explains the strong anisotropy, with compressive strain along 100 increasing resistance by up to 10% per 0.1% strain in n-type Si.
A representative example is the longitudinal piezoresistance in p-doped Si along the 100 direction, where strain parallel to current flow yields high sensitivity due to enhanced hole scattering anisotropy. For moderately doped p-type Si (resistivity ~0.01-0.1 cm), the longitudinal gauge factor (GF = (R/R)/, where is strain) typically ranges from 50 to 150, far exceeding the geometric contribution (~2) from dimensional changes. Giant Piezoresistance in Hybrid Structures

Giant piezoresistance in hybrid structures refers to the dramatically enhanced change in electrical resistance under mechanical strain observed in composite systems combining metals and semiconductors, such as aluminum-silicon hybrids. These structures exploit the intrinsic piezoresistive properties of the semiconductor while using the highly conductive metal as a shunt to amplify the overall effect through current redistribution. Unlike the intrinsic piezoresistivity in bulk semiconductors, which arises from strain-induced changes in carrier mobility and density, the giant response in hybrids stems primarily from geometrical factors that leverage the anisotropic resistivity changes in the semiconductor under stress.
The mechanism involves a stress-induced anisotropy in the semiconductor's conductivity tensor, which redirects current away from the low-resistance metal shunt toward the more resistive semiconductor paths under uniaxial strain. In silicon-aluminum hybrids, for instance, tensile strain along the direction alters the silicon resistivity components (e.g., increasing longitudinal resistivity while decreasing transverse), causing the current to preferentially flow through strained silicon regions. This geometrical amplification, quantified by a factor $ S_G = \frac{L - L_V}{2W} $ for high-aspect-ratio devices (where $ L $ is length, $ W $ is width, and $ L_V $ is voltage probe separation), results in a much larger relative resistance change than in pure silicon. The effect is independent of specific interface properties like Schottky barriers, relying instead on the overall device geometry and the piezoresistive coefficients of silicon ($ \pi_{11} = 6.6 \times 10^{-11} $ Pa$^{-1} $, $ \pi_{12} = -1.1 \times 10^{-11} $ Pa$^{-1} $, $ \pi_{44} = 138.1 \times 10^{-11} $ Pa$^{-1} $).
Representative examples include planar aluminum-silicon hybrid resistors fabricated on silicon-on-insulator substrates, where a narrow silicon bridge (width $ b \approx 5 $ m) shunted by aluminum achieves peak gauge factors (GF) exceeding 800 at room temperature. In these devices, the maximum GF of 843 was measured under low strain levels, far surpassing the ~100 GF typical of bulk p-type silicon. Similar amplification has been demonstrated in microstructured hybrids with varying metal shunt widths, yielding GFs up to several hundred while maintaining operation at ambient conditions. The higher sensitivity compared to bulk materials arises from the confinement of current paths at the hybrid interfaces, which enhances the impact of local strain gradients on overall resistance.
These hybrid structures offer advantages such as quasi-linear strain response over a broader range than pure semiconductors and potentially better temperature stability due to their reliance on established silicon piezoresistive physics rather than nanoscale quantum effects. The geometrical design ensures consistent performance without the hysteresis or drift issues sometimes seen in pure nanostructured silicon, making them suitable for reliable sensing applications.

Piezoresistive Effects in Nanostructures

In nanoscale silicon structures such as nanowires and thin films, the piezoresistive effect is significantly amplified compared to bulk materials due to quantum confinement and a high surface-to-volume ratio. Quantum confinement modifies the electronic band structure, making carrier transport more sensitive to applied strain, while the dominant surface contribution enhances scattering and charge depletion effects under stress. This leads to larger changes in resistivity for small strains, with mechanisms including strain-induced shifts in the bandgap and piezopotential generation that alters carrier concentration and mobility.
Giant piezoresistive effects have been observed in silicon nanowires, particularly p-type ones, where gauge factors (GF) can reach up to 10,000, far exceeding the bulk silicon value of around 100-200. Seminal work from UC Berkeley in 2006 demonstrated piezoresistive coefficients up to 3,550 10^{-11} Pa^{-1} in nanowires with diameters below 100 nm, representing a 38-fold increase over bulk silicon, attributed to strain-enhanced surface states and defect scattering that modulate carrier mobility. These effects enable high sensitivity in nanoelectromechanical systems, though variability arises from nanowire orientation and doping.
The piezoresistive sensitivity in silicon nanostructures exhibits strong size dependence, with reduction in diameter or thickness enhancing the effect through intensified quantum confinement and surface dominance. For instance, as nanowire diameter decreases from 200 nm to 50 nm, the piezoresistive coefficient can increase by orders of magnitude due to greater strain-induced band warping and surface charge modulation. In silicon nanobeams, theoretical models show that cross-sectional dimensions below 50 nm yield gauge factors exceeding 1,000, driven by temperature-independent quantum effects that amplify longitudinal piezoresistance along preferred crystallographic directions like <110>.
Recent advances from 2023 to 2025 have focused on integrating silicon nanowires with 2D materials such as graphene to achieve ultra-high gauge factors beyond 30,000 in hybrid nanostructures, leveraging synergistic quantum enhancements and improved charge transfer for superior strain detection. These developments, including AZO/SiCN hybrids as proxies for silicon-2D integrations, address limitations in standalone nanowires by boosting stability and sensitivity for applications in flexible nanoelectronics.

Devices and Applications

Silicon-Based Piezoresistors

Silicon-based piezoresistors are typically fabricated from doped monocrystalline or polycrystalline silicon, where resistors are ion-implanted and strategically placed on flexible structures such as diaphragms or cantilever beams to detect mechanical deformation. These resistors are oriented along specific crystallographic directions, such as <110> for p-type doping, to maximize the longitudinal piezoresistive coefficient _l, which enhances sensitivity to axial stress. A common design integrates four resistors into a full Wheatstone bridge configuration, with two resistors aligned longitudinally to experience tensile stress and the other two transversely under compressive stress, ensuring differential resistance changes that amplify the output signal. This setup is particularly suited for microelectromechanical systems (MEMS), where the piezoresistors are positioned at high-stress locations on the diaphragm edges or beam surfaces.
The operational principle relies on the piezoresistive effect in silicon, where applied strain alters the material's resistivity, leading to a measurable resistance change. The relative resistance variation is given by the equation:
RR=GF
\frac{\Delta R}{R} = \mathrm{GF} \times \varepsilon
RR=GF
where R/R\Delta R / RR/R is the fractional change in resistance, GF is the gauge factor (typically 50200 for silicon, depending on doping and orientation), and \varepsilon is the applied strain. In MEMS applications, silicon piezoresistors exhibit high sensitivity to both longitudinal and transverse stresses; for instance, longitudinal placement maximizes response to tensile strain along the current flow direction, while transverse effects can oppose or augment the signal based on the Poisson ratio. The Wheatstone bridge converts this resistance imbalance into a voltage output proportional to the stress, enabling precise transduction in dynamic environments.
Performance characteristics of silicon piezoresistors include excellent linearity, with output deviations typically less than 1% of full-scale output (FSO) over a wide pressure range, making them reliable for quantitative measurements. Hysteresis is minimal, often below 0.1%, due to the elastic recovery of silicon without permanent deformation, and fatigue resistance is high, enduring millions of cycles without significant degradation owing to the material's mechanical robustness. These attributes support key applications in pressure and strain sensing, such as automotive engine monitoring, biomedical implants, and inertial navigation systems.
Compared to metallic piezoresistors, silicon-based variants offer a substantially higher gauge factoroften 50100 times greater (GF 100 vs. 2 for metals)resulting in superior sensitivity for low-strain detection. Additionally, their compatibility with complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) processes allows seamless integration with readout circuitry on the same chip, reducing parasitics and enabling compact, cost-effective sensor arrays.

Fabrication Techniques

Bulk micromachining is a foundational technique for fabricating piezoresistive devices, particularly those based on silicon substrates, where structures like thin diaphragms are created through selective removal of material from the wafer bulk. Anisotropic wet etching using potassium hydroxide (KOH) solutions exploits the crystal orientation dependence of etch rates in silicon, preferentially removing material along specific planes such as (100) to form well-defined cavities and membranes with thicknesses as low as 10-50 m. This process typically involves masking the silicon surface with a protective layer like silicon nitride, followed by immersion in heated KOH (around 80C), achieving etch rates of approximately 1 m/min for (100) planes while undercutting (111) planes at rates over 400 times slower. Piezoresistors are integrated via ion implantation, where dopants such as boron or phosphorus are accelerated into the silicon at energies of 50-200 keV to create p-type or n-type regions with controlled resistivity, typically followed by patterning to define Wheatstone bridge configurations.
Surface micromachining offers an alternative for creating suspended structures without deep substrate etching, enabling compatibility with integrated circuits on the same wafer. The process begins with the deposition of a sacrificial layer, often silicon dioxide (SiO) grown thermally or via chemical vapor deposition to a thickness of 1-2 m, which defines the gap under the sensing element. A structural layer of low-pressure chemical vapor deposition (LPCVD) polysilicon, doped in situ or post-deposition for piezoresistive properties, is then deposited atop the sacrificial layer at temperatures around 600C, forming diaphragms or beams 0.5-5 m thick. Release etching with hydrofluoric acid (HF) removes the sacrificial SiO, freeing the polysilicon structure while preserving its integrity through critical point drying to avoid stiction. This method has been widely adopted for piezoresistive pressure sensors.
Advanced fabrication techniques extend piezoresistive capabilities to nanoscale dimensions and hybrid materials. Focused ion beam (FIB) milling and deposition, using gallium ions at currents of 1-50 pA, enable precise sculpting of silicon nanowires or modification of piezoresistive elements with resolutions below 10 nm, enhancing gauge factors in atomic force microscopy probes. Recent developments (2023-2025) include laser engraving for hybrid prototypes, such as those using laser-induced 3C-SiC and carbon allotropes on poly(dimethylsiloxane) (PDMS) substrates, where commercial laser-engraving devices pattern conductive paths to achieve piezoresistive responses suitable for strain sensing without traditional lithography.
Fabrication of piezoresistive devices faces challenges related to precision and material integrity. Accurate alignment during lithography is critical to orient piezoresistors along silicon crystal axes that maximize the piezoresistive coefficient, as misalignment by even 5 can reduce sensitivity by 20-30%. Post-processing annealing, typically at 900-1100C for 30-60 minutes in inert atmospheres, is essential after ion implantation to activate dopants and anneal out lattice defects, restoring carrier mobility and minimizing noise in the final sensor.

Other Traditional Devices

Piezoresistive pressure sensors commonly employ a silicon diaphragm that deforms under applied pressure, with integrated piezoresistors forming a Wheatstone bridge to detect resistance changes from the resulting strain. These devices operate in absolute configurations, where pressure is measured relative to a vacuum reference, or differential modes, comparing pressures across two points, enabling applications from low to high-pressure ranges. A prominent commercial example is the manifold absolute pressure (MAP) sensor in automotive engines, which uses piezoresistive elements on a silicon diaphragm to monitor intake manifold pressure for optimizing fuel injection and ignition timing.
Piezoresistive accelerometers utilize cantilever or bridge structures where inertial forces cause beam deflection, inducing stress that alters the resistance of embedded piezoresistors arranged in a Wheatstone bridge for signal amplification. Similarly, piezoresistive strain gauges in bridge configurations measure tensile stress by detecting resistance variations in semiconductor elements bonded to a substrate, providing precise quantification of mechanical deformation in load-bearing components.
Tactile sensors based on piezoresistive arrays enable force mapping in robotics by arranging multiple resistor elements to capture distributed pressure profiles during object manipulation, as demonstrated in early designs for grasp control. These pre-2020 implementations focused on rigid silicon arrays integrated into robotic grippers to provide spatial resolution for contact force distribution without requiring complex scanning.
Traditional piezoresistive devices suffer from temperature sensitivity, where thermal variations cause resistance drifts that mimic pressure-induced changes, necessitating compensation circuits for stable operation. Additionally, inherent nonlinearity in the resistance-strain relationship can lead to output inaccuracies at higher stress levels, limiting precision in dynamic environments without calibration.

Emerging Flexible Sensors

Emerging flexible piezoresistive sensors primarily utilize carbon-based materials such as graphene, carbon nanotubes (CNTs), and MXenes, often combined with porous polymers like polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) or polyurethane (PU) to enhance stretchability and mechanical compliance. These materials facilitate piezoresistive transduction through mechanisms including microcrack formation in brittle conductive layers, which disrupts pathways under strain, and quantum tunneling between conductive fillers, where inter-particle distances modulate resistance at low pressures. Such designs achieve ultrahigh sensitivities, with some CNT- or MXene-infused composites reporting gauge factors (GF) exceeding 1000, far surpassing traditional rigid sensors.
Wearable patches represent a key design paradigm, integrating these materials into thin, skin-conformable forms for real-time health monitoring, such as pulse waveform detection and joint strain sensing during motion. For example, CNT/PVA composites enable broad detection ranges up to 100 kPa with response times under 50 ms, supporting applications in cardiovascular tracking. In robotic skins, multi-array configurations provide high spatial resolution and sensitivity (GF > 1000 in crack-engineered variants), allowing for tactile feedback in soft grippers and prosthetics. Nanostructure enhancements, such as aligned CNT networks, further amplify these effects by optimizing percolation thresholds.
Advances between 2023 and 2025 have focused on scalable fabrication, including laser-engraved graphene (LEG) sensors on polyimide substrates diffused into PDMS, which deliver sensitivities of 61 mV/V/kPa for ultra-low pressures below 3 Pa, ideal for acoustic and physiological signals. Composite tribo-piezoresistive hybrids, merging piezoresistive resistance changes with triboelectric charge generation, enable multi-modal detection of strain and stress, with outputs distinguishing subtle mechanical inputs in wearable prototypes. MXene-based electrospun nanocomposites have also emerged, offering tunable microstructures for pressures from 0.1 Pa to 1 MPa while maintaining over 10,000 cycles of stability.
These sensors find primary applications in electronic skin (e-skin) for immersive human-machine interfaces, such as gesture-controlled devices, and in advanced wearables for continuous monitoring of vital signs like respiration and gait. Porous polymer variants, prepared via phase separation or 3D printing, excel in breathable patches for long-term adhesion during activities. Despite progress, challenges include ensuring durability against fatigue over thousands of cycles and improving biocompatibility to minimize skin irritation in prolonged use.
