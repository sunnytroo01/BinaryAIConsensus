Here is how to build habits that stick, based on the science of behavior change.

Every habit follows a four-step loop: cue, craving, response, reward. The cue triggers the habit. You see your running shoes by the door. The craving motivates the habit. You want the feeling of energy and accomplishment that comes after a run. The response is the habit itself. You put on the shoes and go for a run. The reward satisfies the craving. You feel energized, proud, and calm. Understanding this loop is the key to building new habits and breaking old ones.

To build a new habit, make it obvious, attractive, easy, and satisfying. These are the four laws of behavior change described by James Clear in Atomic Habits.

Make it obvious. Design your environment so the cues for your desired habit are visible and unavoidable. If you want to read more, put a book on your pillow so you see it when you go to bed. If you want to drink more water, fill a water bottle and put it on your desk. If you want to exercise in the morning, lay out your workout clothes the night before. The more visible the cue, the more likely you are to act on it.

Make it attractive. Pair the habit you need to do with something you want to do. This is called temptation bundling. Only listen to your favorite podcast while exercising. Only watch your favorite show while folding laundry. Only eat at your favorite restaurant after completing a week of workouts. The attractive activity provides motivation to do the necessary one.

Make it easy. Reduce the friction between you and the habit. If you want to go to the gym, choose a gym on your commute route, not one across town. If you want to eat healthier, prep meals on Sunday so healthy food is always available. If you want to write daily, open your document the night before so it is waiting for you in the morning. The two-minute rule says to scale any new habit down to something that takes two minutes or less to start. Do not commit to running five miles. Commit to putting on your running shoes. The rest will follow.

Make it satisfying. Give yourself an immediate reward after completing the habit. Mark an X on a calendar for every day you complete the habit. The visual chain of Xs becomes a powerful motivator because you do not want to break the streak. Celebrate small wins. Tell yourself "I am someone who exercises every day" after each workout. The identity reinforcement is the deepest form of satisfaction.

To break a bad habit, invert the four laws. Make the cue invisible: remove the cookies from the counter, delete social media apps from your phone. Make it unattractive: remind yourself of the consequences every time you feel the craving. Make it difficult: add friction between you and the bad habit. If you want to stop watching television in the evening, unplug it after each use and put the remote in another room. Make it unsatisfying: create an accountability partner who you have to report to if you break the habit.

The most important insight about habits is that you do not rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your systems. Everyone has the same goals. The person who wants to lose weight has the same goal as the person who actually loses weight. The difference is not the goal but the daily system of habits that either moves you toward the goal or away from it. Build better systems and the goals take care of themselves.

Start with one habit at a time. Do not try to overhaul your entire life in a single week. Pick the one habit that would have the biggest positive impact on your life and focus on that for thirty days. Once it is automatic, add the next one. Small changes compounded over time produce extraordinary results. You just have to be patient enough to let them compound.
