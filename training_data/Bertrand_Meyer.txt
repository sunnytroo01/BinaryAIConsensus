Bertrand Meyer
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Bertrand Meyer is a French computer scientist, academic, and software engineer renowned for developing the Eiffel programming language and pioneering Design by Contract, a methodology for ensuring software reliability through formal specifications integrated into object-oriented programming.
Born in France, Meyer earned engineering degrees from cole Polytechnique and Tlcom Paris in the early 1970s, followed by advanced studies including an MS in computer science from Stanford University in 1974 and a doctorate from the University of Nancy in 1985. His early career included nearly a decade at lectricit de France (EDF), where he advanced from research engineer to head of the software support division, focusing on large-scale software development challenges. In 1985, he founded Eiffel Software, serving as president and CEO until 2001 while developing Eiffel as an ISO-standard language emphasizing abstraction, modularity, and verifiable correctness.
From 2001 to 2016, Meyer held the chair of software engineering at ETH Zurich, where he led research in areas such as concurrent object-oriented programming via the SCOOP model, requirements engineering, and agile methods enhanced with formal techniques. He has since become professor emeritus at ETH Zurich and Provost and Professor of Software Engineering at Constructor Institute of Technology, while continuing as chief architect at Eiffel Software in Santa Barbara, California. Meyer's contributions extend to over 300 refereed articles and 13 books, including influential works like Object-Oriented Software Construction and Touch of Class: Learning to Program Well with Objects and Contracts.
His work has earned major recognitions, including the 2006 ACM Software System Award for Eiffel's impact on software quality, the 2010 IEEE Harlan Mills Award for advancing software engineering principles, and election as an ACM Fellow in 2008 for contributions to software engineering and programming languages. Meyer is also a member of Academia Europaea and has received an ERC Advanced Investigator Grant in 2011 for research on automated error handling.

Early life and education

Early life

Bertrand Meyer was born on 21 November 1950 in Paris, France.
Meyer grew up in a post-World War II France, with his mother, Madeleine Meyer (ne Kahn, born 1914), having been involved in the French Resistance in networks such as Garel from 1941 until the Liberation. His sister Simone was deported to Auschwitz and did not return.
He transitioned to higher education at the cole Polytechnique.

Formal education

Bertrand Meyer earned an Engineer's degree, equivalent to a master's in engineering, from the cole Polytechnique in Paris in 1972, focusing on mathematics and physics. He subsequently obtained a Master of Science degree in computer science from Stanford University in 1974. During this period, he also completed a Diplme d'tudes Approfondies (DEA) in computer science from Universit Paris VI in 1972, an MA in Russian Language and Literature from Sorbonne (University of Paris IV) in 1973, and an Engineer's degree from cole Nationale Suprieure des Tlcommunications in Paris in 1974.
Meyer pursued advanced research in France, culminating in a Doctorat s Sciences from Universit Henri Poincar in Nancy in 1985. This doctoral qualification was the highest academic degree in the French system at the time. No specific academic honors from his student years are documented in available records.

Professional career

Industry roles

Bertrand Meyer began his professional career in industry at lectricit de France (EDF), the French national power company, where he spent nine years from 1974 to 1983 in research and development roles focused on software engineering. Starting as a research engineer, he advanced to head of the software support division, overseeing technical teams responsible for developing and maintaining software systems critical to EDF's operations in electrical engineering and numerical computations.
During the 1970s and early 1980s, Meyer's work at EDF involved programming and systems engineering projects that emphasized systematic software development methodologies. He contributed to efforts in data structure description, specification languages, and numerical algorithm programming, often tailored to industrial applications such as simulations for power systems. For instance, his publications from this period include explorations of modern programming concepts using Simula 67 and rational methods for numerical algorithms, reflecting hands-on involvement in building reliable software tools for EDF's R&D needs. These responsibilities highlighted his expertise in applying formal techniques to practical software challenges in a large-scale industrial environment.
In 1985, while concluding his visiting position at the University of California, Santa Barbara, Meyer founded Eiffel Software in Santa Barbara, California, where he served as president and CEO until 2001. During this period, he also led Socit des Outils du Logiciel, the European arm of Eiffel development, as president and CTO from 1986 to 1990. He has continued as chief architect at Eiffel Software since 2001.

Academic positions

Bertrand Meyer began his academic career with a faculty appointment at the University of California, Santa Barbara, where he served from 1983 to 1985.
From 2001 to 2016, Meyer held the position of Professor of Software Engineering at ETH Zurich, the Swiss Federal Institute of Technology, including a term as Chair of the Department of Computer Science from 2004 to 2006. He retains emeritus status at ETH Zurich following his retirement from the full professorship.
Since 2018, Meyer has been Professor of Software Engineering and Provost at Constructor Institute of Technology in Schaffhausen, Switzerland (formerly known as Schaffhausen Institute of Technology).
In addition to his primary appointments, Meyer has held several adjunct and visiting professorships. These include an adjunct professorship at Monash University in Melbourne, Australia, from 1998 to 2003; at ITMO University in Saint Petersburg, Russia, from 2011 to 2014; and at Innopolis University in Kazan, Russia, from 2014 to 2022 (resigned in March 2022). He also served as adjunct professor at Politecnico di Milano in Italy from 2016 to 2020 and as Chair of Excellence at the University of Toulouse in France from 2016 to 2017. Furthermore, Meyer has held visiting positions at the University of Technology Sydney and Monash University in Australia.

Leadership and administrative roles

Bertrand Meyer has held several prominent leadership positions in academic and professional organizations, building on his foundational roles as a professor in software engineering. From 2004 to 2006, he served as Chair of the Department of Computer Science at ETH Zurich, where he oversaw departmental operations, strategic planning, and faculty coordination during a period of significant growth in computer science research and education.
In 2018, Meyer was appointed Provost at the Constructor Institute of Technology in Schaffhausen, Switzerland (formerly the Schaffhausen Institute of Technology), a role he continues to hold as of 2025. As Provost, he provides academic leadership, guiding the institute's programs in technology and engineering, fostering interdisciplinary initiatives, and ensuring alignment with global standards in software engineering education and research.
Meyer played a pivotal role in advancing European computer science through his tenure as founding President of Informatics Europe from 2006 to 2011. In this capacity, he led the association of European computer science departments and research centers, spearheading initiatives such as reports on computer science education in schools, researcher mobility, and the development of pan-European data portals for informatics higher education to promote collaboration and policy advocacy across the continent.
Additionally, Meyer has been a member of the French Academy of Technologies (Acadmie des Technologies) since 1991, contributing to advisory efforts on technological applications and policy in France and Europe.

Contributions to software engineering

Development of Eiffel programming language

Bertrand Meyer conceived the Eiffel programming language in 1985 as a method and notation for software construction aimed at producing high-quality, reliable software. Designed to address the challenges of commercial software development, Eiffel emphasized object-oriented principles from its inception, with Meyer founding Interactive Software Engineering (later Eiffel Software) in the same year to drive its implementation and evolution. The language's development drew on Meyer's prior experience in industry and academia, focusing on modularity, reusability, and verifiability to mitigate common software defects.
Eiffel's core technical features include full support for object-oriented programming through classes as abstract data types, enabling inheritance, polymorphism, and encapsulation. It incorporates multiple inheritance, allowing classes to inherit from several parents while resolving potential name conflicts via mechanisms like rename clauses and inheritance consistency checks. Automatic garbage collection is provided by the runtime system to manage memory safely without manual intervention. Additionally, the language integrates design by contract as a foundational element to enforce reliability, though its methodological aspects extend beyond the language syntax. These features collectively promote type safety and void safety, with static typing and attached/detachable type qualifiers ensuring robust program behavior.
The first Eiffel implementation was released in 1986 by Eiffel Software, marking the language's practical debut and enabling early experimentation in software projects. Subsequent versions refined its syntax and semantics, incorporating feedback from users and extending support for concurrent programming and genericity. Standardization efforts began in the early 2000s under Ecma International's TC39-TG4 committee, culminating in the first edition of ECMA-367 in June 2005 and the second edition in June 2006, which was also adopted as the ISO/IEC 25436 international standard in November 2006. This standardization affirmed Eiffel's maturity and facilitated broader interoperability and tool development.
Eiffel's impact on software quality stems from its emphasis on provable correctness and reusability, reducing defects in complex systems through built-in mechanisms for specification and verification. In industry, it has been adopted for mission-critical applications in sectors including finance, defense, aerospace, healthcare, and telecommunications, where reliability is paramount; for instance, Eiffel tools have supported large-scale projects requiring high assurance. In academia, the language has gained traction for teaching object-oriented concepts and software engineering principles, with growing use in introductory and advanced programming courses due to its clear syntax and focus on quality practices. Overall, Eiffel's design has influenced subsequent languages and methodologies, promoting a shift toward more rigorous development processes.

Design by contract and programming methodology

Bertrand Meyer introduced Design by Contract in the late 1980s as a formal approach to software verification, utilizing preconditions to specify required input conditions for routines, postconditions to define expected outcomes upon completion, and class invariants to maintain consistent states across all operations of a class. This methodology treats software components as parties to a contract, where suppliers (implementers) guarantee postconditions and invariants if callers satisfy preconditions, thereby clarifying responsibilities and enabling systematic error detection during development and runtime.
Meyer's integration of Design by Contract with object-oriented principles emphasizes reliable software construction by embedding these assertions directly into class definitions, fostering modular designs where objects collaborate under precisely defined obligations and benefits. In this framework, inheritance hierarchies respect contract rulessubclasses may weaken preconditions and strengthen postconditions or invariantsto ensure substitutability and prevent cascading failures. This approach shifts verification from ad-hoc testing to a proactive, contract-enforced process that supports both correctness proofs and practical debugging.
Over time, Design by Contract evolved into supporting tools such as AutoTest, developed by Meyer and collaborators in the early 2000s, which automates test generation and execution by leveraging existing contracts as oracles to explore program behaviors and uncover violations without manual test case design. AutoTest has been applied in educational settings, notably in Meyer's introductory programming courses at ETH Zurich, where it integrates with the textbook Touch of Class to teach students contract-based reasoning through hands-on exercises that generate thousands of test scenarios per session. In industry, the methodology has been adopted for building robust systems, as demonstrated in case studies where contract enforcement reduced defect rates by facilitating early detection of interface mismatches in large-scale object-oriented projects.
The influence of Design by Contract extends to formal standards, particularly through its incorporation into the ISO/IEC 25436:2006 specification for the Eiffel language, which mandates support for assertions as a core feature for verifiable software development. Beyond Eiffelwhere it is implemented as a native language mechanismthis methodology has shaped broader software engineering practices, inspiring contract-like features in languages such as Ada (via the 2012 ISO amendment) and influencing quality assurance guidelines in object-oriented design across industries.

Other innovations in object-oriented programming

Bertrand Meyer advanced the theoretical foundations of object-oriented programming (OOP) by formalizing abstract data types (ADTs) as mathematical models in 1976, emphasizing their role in defining operations and properties independently of implementation details. He further developed this in 1981 with a three-level description approachabstract, implementation, and usageproviding a structured basis for reusable software components in OOP.
In addressing genericity, Meyer argued in 1986 that it enables flexible parameterization of types for reusability, as seen in unconstrained (no restrictions) and constrained (operation-specific) forms, but inheritance offers greater power for extending types without code duplication. He integrated both mechanisms in Eiffel starting in 1985, allowing generic classes to inherit from other generics while maintaining static type safety, thus enhancing OOP's extendibility beyond pure inheritance hierarchies. This combination has influenced language designs by prioritizing compatibility and polymorphism in parameterized contexts.
Meyer's work on exception handling in OOP emphasizes discipline through integration with Design by Contract principles, defining exceptions as violations of routine contracts (e.g., precondition failures) that trigger either organized failure or corrective resumption. In his 1987 framework, routines include rescue clauses for handling such events, ensuring propagation of failures to callers unless explicitly resolved, which contrasts with ad-hoc mechanisms in languages like Ada by aligning exceptions with contractual semantics for reliability.
For concurrency in OOP, Meyer introduced the Simple Concurrent Object-Oriented Programming (SCOOP) model in 1993, extending sequential OOP with processors as execution units that manage object access via selective locking to prevent data races at both low and high levels. SCOOP uses type tags (e.g., separate > for remote objects) and contract clauses (controlled for client-side checks, uncontrolled for server-side) to enable race-free reasoning, generalizing inheritance and polymorphism to concurrent settings while minimizing new syntax. This approach supports asynchronous features and covariant redeclaration, promoting safe parallelism in object-oriented systems.
In requirements engineering, Meyer contributed the PEGS framework in his 2022 Handbook of Requirements and Business Analysis, structuring requirements around four pillars: Project (management aspects), Environment (operational context), Goals (stakeholder objectives), and System (functional and non-functional specs). This model integrates business analysis with OOP design by emphasizing traceable, formalizable elements that inform object modeling and verification, replacing outdated standards like IEEE 830-1998 with a holistic, iterative process.
Regarding recent innovations, Meyer critiqued agile methods in his 2014 book Agile! The Good, the Hype and the Ugly, praising practices like the "closed-window rule" for iteration focus while condemning hype (e.g., overemphasized sprints) and ugly aspects (e.g., devaluing upfront requirements and tools), advocating a balanced integration with rigorous OOP methodologies. In verification techniques up to 2025, he co-authored works on formal loop unrolling for enhanced testing coverage (2025 preprint), contradiction seeding to generate comprehensive test suites (2025 paper), and using large language models for verified bug fixes via automatic program repair (2025 preprint), all leveraging OOP contracts for scalable, proof-based assurance in software development.

Awards and honors

Professional awards

In 2005, Meyer received the AITO Dahl-Nygaard Senior Prize, the inaugural senior award recognizing his foundational contributions to object-oriented programming, particularly through the design of the Eiffel language.
The following year, in 2006, he was awarded the ACM Software System Award for the Eiffel programming language and environment, honoring their lasting impact on software quality and development practices.
In 2008, Meyer was elected an ACM Fellow for his contributions to software engineering and programming languages.
He received the IEEE Computer Society's Harlan D. Mills Award in 2010 for practical and fundamental advancements in object-oriented software engineering, software reuse, and integrating formal techniques into development practices.
In 2011, Meyer received an ERC Advanced Investigator Grant for research on automated error handling.
In 2017, Meyer was honored with the ACM SIGSOFT Influential Educator Award for his role in advancing software engineering research and practice through education.
In 2019, he became an IFIP Fellow as part of the organization's first cohort, recognizing his sustained leadership in information processing and software engineering.

Honorary degrees and fellowships

Bertrand Meyer has received honorary doctorates and fellowships recognizing his foundational work in software engineering, object-oriented programming, and reliable software development.
In 2006, ITMO University in Saint Petersburg, Russia, awarded him an honorary doctorate in acknowledgment of his advancements in programming languages and software methodologies. He returned this degree in March 2022.
The University of York granted Meyer an honorary doctorate in 2014, honoring his creation of the Eiffel programming language, development of Design by Contract, and influential role in promoting object-oriented principles for software reliability.
In 2014, Meyer received the IFIP Silver Core Award.
Meyer was elected to membership in the French Academy of Technologies in 1991, where he contributes to discussions on technological applications and innovations in computing.
He became a Fellow of the Association for Computing Machinery (ACM) in 2008, cited for his contributions to software engineering and programming languages, including the design of Eiffel and methodologies for verifiable software construction.
In 2011, Meyer was elected to Academia Europaea.
In 2019, Meyer was inducted as an IFIP Fellow in the inaugural cohort by the International Federation for Information Processing, recognizing his leadership in software engineering research, including object technology, concurrent programming, and quality assurance practices that have shaped international standards.

Other notable events

Wikipedia hoax incident

In December 2005, an anonymous editor altered the German Wikipedia entry on Bertrand Meyer, a French computer science professor and creator of the Eiffel programming language, to falsely claim that he had died on December 24, 2005, in Zurich. The hoax entry referenced unverified exam results published the previous day as a supposed cause, but no such connection existed. This fabrication persisted undetected for several days until January 3, 2006, when it was noticed by Christian Kirsch, a journalist at Heise Online, who alerted Wikipedia administrators.
Meyer, upon learning of the incident through media reports, confirmed his well-being via his secretary at ETH Zurich, leading to an immediate correction by Wikipedia's press team, which removed the hoax from the page history within minutes. In response, Meyer published a detailed treatise titled "Defense and Illustration of Wikipedia" on his ETH Zurich webpage, where he described the event as a likely student prank and praised the platform's transparency and rapid self-correction mechanisms. He also collaborated informally with Wikipedia editors, such as contributing to improvements in the entry on Levenshtein distance, demonstrating a constructive approach to engaging with the community despite the vulnerability exposed.
The incident drew widespread coverage in German-speaking media, including Der Spiegel and Tages-Anzeiger, highlighting concerns over Wikipedia's susceptibility to vandalism and its implications for the reliability of information on notable academics. Meyer used the event to argue that while such hoaxes underscore the need for expert validation in technical entries, Wikipedia's editable nature allows for quicker fixes than static web slander, potentially enhancing rather than undermining academic notability when handled transparently. As a lasting effect, the hoax boosted Meyer's online visibility, with Google searches for his name yielding over 170 results related to the story by early January 2006, amplifying awareness of his contributions in software engineering.
