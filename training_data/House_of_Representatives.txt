United States House of Representatives
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The United States House of Representatives is the lower chamber of the bicameral United States Congress, serving as one of two coequal houses in the federal legislature alongside the Senate. Composed of 435 voting members apportioned among the states according to population, with each state guaranteed at least one representative, the House elects its members from single-member congressional districts every two years. Members must be at least 25 years old, U.S. citizens for seven years, and inhabitants of the state they represent, reflecting the framers' intent for the House to embody direct popular representation in contrast to the Senate's state-equal structure.
The House holds exclusive constitutional powers, including the initiation of all revenue-raising bills and the sole authority to impeach federal officers, such as the President, which underscores its role in fiscal oversight and accountability mechanisms. In practice, it originates most legislation through committees, debates bills on the floor under rules set by the majority party, and requires a simple majority for passage, though reconciliation procedures allow bypassing Senate filibusters for budget-related measures. Defining characteristics include its responsiveness to short election cycles, enabling rapid shifts in partisan controlsuch as the Republican majority secured in the 2024 electionsand its historical role in landmark enactments like civil rights laws and economic reforms, tempered by frequent partisan gridlock that has delayed appropriations and debt ceiling resolutions. Controversies often center on internal rules favoring the majority, gerrymandering's influence on districting, and the fixed 435-member cap since 1911, which has diluted per-capita representation amid population growth from 92 million to over 340 million.

United States House of Representatives

Constitutional Origins and Early History

The United States House of Representatives originated in Article I of the U.S. Constitution, which vests all legislative powers in a bicameral Congress consisting of a Senate and a House of Representatives. Article I, Section 2 specifies that the House shall be composed of members chosen every second year by the people of the several states, with electors possessing the qualifications requisite for the most numerous branch of the state legislature; representation is apportioned among the states according to their respective numbers, counting the whole number of free persons (including those bound to service for a term of years and excluding untaxed Indians) plus three-fifths of other persons, with each state guaranteed at least one representative and no more than one for every thirty thousand persons until reapportioned by law. This structure reflected a deliberate design to balance popular sovereignty with state equality, ensuring the House served as the more directly representative chamber responsive to population size.
The House's framework emerged from debates at the Constitutional Convention in Philadelphia from May to September 1787, where delegates grappled with representation under the proposed replacement for the unicameral Congress of the Confederation. The Virginia Plan, introduced by Edmund Randolph on May 29, advocated a bicameral legislature with both houses apportioned by population or proportional contribution, favoring larger states like Virginia. In opposition, the New Jersey Plan, presented by William Paterson on June 15, called for equal state representation in a unicameral body to protect smaller states. The resulting Connecticut Compromise, or Great Compromise, adopted on July 16, 1787, resolved the impasse by establishing proportional representation in the House based on population (incorporating the three-fifths compromise for apportioning representation and direct taxes regarding enslaved persons) and equal representation of two senators per state, thus creating a lower house reflective of demographic weight and an upper house safeguarding state sovereignty.
Following ratification of the Constitution by the ninth state on June 21, 1788, the first House elections occurred in late 1788 and early 1789, yielding 65 members apportioned temporarily under the Constitution's initial formula. The First Congress convened on March 4, 1789, at Federal Hall in New York City, though the House did not achieve a quorum until April 1, when it elected Frederick Muhlenberg of Pennsylvania as its first Speaker. Early sessions focused on organizing the new government, including establishing executive departments, admitting North Carolina and Rhode Island, and preparing for the 1790 census to enable permanent reapportionment; the House's role in originating revenue bills and impeaching federal officers was exercised from the outset, as seen in its approval of the first federal budget and tariff acts. By 1791, the House had relocated to Philadelphia, with membership fixed at 105 after the 1790 census under the Apportionment Act of April 14, 1792, which increased seats based on enumerated population while adhering to constitutional limits. This period solidified the House as the people's chamber, though initial operations were hampered by incomplete state ratifications and logistical challenges in a decentralized republic.

Evolution of Composition and Apportionment

The apportionment of seats in the United States House of Representatives is governed by Article I, Section 2 of the Constitution, which requires seats to be allocated among the states according to their respective populations, determined by an enumeration conducted every ten years, initially counting free persons (including indentured servants and excluding untaxed Indians) and three-fifths of "other Persons" (slaves), with each state guaranteed at least one representative. This framework aimed to ensure proportional representation reflective of population shifts, though Congress retained authority to determine the total number of seats and the precise method of division.
The First Congress (17891791) operated with 65 seats apportioned temporarily based on 1787 population estimates, yielding an average constituency of about 30,000 persons per representative. Following the 1790 census, which enumerated 3,929,214 persons, the Apportionment Act of April 14, 1792, expanded the House to 105 seats using a fixed ratio of one representative per 33,000 persons, marking the first statutory implementation of constitutional apportionment. Subsequent reapportionments responded to population growth and westward expansion: the Act of January 14, 1802, set 142 seats after the 1800 census; the Act of 1811 raised it to 181 after 1810; the Act of 1822 to 213 after 1820; and the Act of 1836 to 240 after 1830.
Mid-19th-century adjustments reflected political debates over House size and districting uniformity. The Apportionment Act of 1842 reduced seats to 223 following the 1840 census (population ratio one per 70,680), introduced the requirement for contiguous single-member districts to curb at-large elections and multi-member delegations prevalent in states like New York and Pennsylvania, and mandated equal population districts to the extent practicable. Later acts incrementally increased membership amid Civil War-era growth and Reconstruction: 234 seats in 1850, 241 in 1862, 292 in 1872 (after the 1870 census, under the post-14th Amendment full-person counting excluding those denied voting rights without cause), and 325 in 1882. The 14th Amendment, Section 2 (ratified 1868), eliminated the three-fifths clause, basing apportionment on total population while penalizing states for abridging adult male suffrage, though enforcement was limited.
By the early 20th century, delays in reapportionmentsuch as none after the 1890 or 1900 censusesled to oversized constituencies and partisan gridlock, with the House reaching 391 members by 1910. The Apportionment Act of 1911 established 435 seats (433 states plus two for territories), adopting the method of major fractions. The Reapportionment Act of June 18, 1929 (Permanent Apportionment Act), codified 435 as the fixed total, directing future allocations via decennial census without increasing size, to manage chamber overcrowding and administrative burdens. In 1941, Congress shifted to the Huntington-Hill method of equal proportions for its mathematical precision in minimizing relative representation disparities, prioritizing rounding for priority values closest to integers.
Since 1929, apportionment has reallocated the 435 seats after each census without altering the total, with minimum one seat per state (the "Wyoming Rule" effect). Notable shifts include gains for growing Sun Belt states and losses for Northeastern ones: after 1990, California reached 52 seats; post-2010, Texas gained four to 36; and following the 2020 census (population 331,449,281), Texas added two (to 38), Florida and North Carolina one each, while California, Illinois, Michigan, New York, Ohio, Pennsylvania, and West Virginia each lost one, effective for the 118th Congress in 2023. This fixed-size system has preserved relative equality (average district ~761,000 persons as of 2020) but amplified small-state advantages and sparked debates over representational dilution in high-growth areas. Compositionally, while representative qualifications (age 25, seven-year citizenship, state residency) have remained static since 1789, the body's demographic makeup has diversified with expanded suffragevia the 15th (1870), 19th (1920), 24th (1964), and 26th (1971) Amendmentsbut apportionment itself directly influences state delegation sizes rather than individual elector demographics.

Election Processes and Districting

Members of the United States House of Representatives are elected every two years in even-numbered years, with all 435 voting seats contested simultaneously. Candidates must be at least 25 years old, have been a U.S. citizen for at least seven years, and be an inhabitant of the state they seek to represent at the time of election, as stipulated in Article I, Section 2 of the Constitution. Elections occur via a two-stage process in most states: primary elections to select party nominees, followed by general elections where voters choose among major-party nominees and independents using a first-past-the-post system, meaning the candidate with the most votes in a district wins, regardless of majority. Primary formats vary by state, including closed primaries limited to party members, open primaries allowing crossover voting, or nonpartisan "jungle" primaries where the top two advance.
The House's composition is determined by apportionment and districting. Apportionment allocates the fixed 435 seats among the 50 states based on decennial census population data, using the method of equal proportionsa priority-based algorithm that minimizes relative differences in district populations across statesfollowing the Census Bureau's delivery of figures to the President by December 31 of the census year. Each state receives at least one seat, with the total capped at 435 since the Reapportionment Act of 1929; for example, after the 2020 Census, states like Texas gained two seats while New York lost one, reflecting population shifts. Redistricting, the subsequent process of drawing district boundaries within states, must ensure roughly equal population per districtapproximately 761,000 people as of the 2020 apportionmentper the "one person, one vote" principle established by Wesberry v. Sanders (1964).
State legislatures typically handle redistricting, often along partisan lines after gaining control in post-census elections, though 10 states use independent commissions to reduce bias, and court intervention occurs in cases of legal challenges. This process enables gerrymandering, where boundaries are manipulated to concentrate or dilute voter groups for partisan advantage, such as packing opponents into few districts or cracking their support across many; both Democrats and Republicans have employed it when in power, with effects partially offsetting nationally but influencing battleground states. The Supreme Court in Rucho v. Common Cause (2019) ruled federal courts cannot remedy partisan gerrymanders, deeming them non-justiciable political questions, leaving remedies to state courts or legislatures under state constitutions. Despite Voting Rights Act requirements to avoid diluting minority voting power, racial gerrymanders have faced scrutiny, as in Alabama v. Milligan (2023), which mandated additional majority-minority districts where dilution occurs.

Powers, Procedures, and Legislative Role

The United States House of Representatives possesses distinct powers under Article I of the U.S. Constitution, including the exclusive authority to originate all bills for raising revenue, though the Senate may propose or concur with amendments. It also holds the sole power of impeachment for federal civil officers, including the President, with articles of impeachment requiring a majority vote to pass. In electoral contingencies, such as no presidential candidate achieving an Electoral College majority, the House selects the President from the top three candidates, voting by state delegations where each state casts one vote, needing a majority of states for election. These powers reflect the framers' intent to align fiscal and accountability mechanisms with popular representation, given the House's shorter terms and larger, district-based membership.
House procedures emphasize efficiency and majority control, as outlined in the chamber's standing rules adopted at the start of each two-year Congress. Legislation begins with introduction by a representative, assigned a number (e.g., H.R. for bills), and referral to one or more committees by the Speaker, often based on subject matter. Committees, numbering 20 standing panels as of the 118th Congress, conduct hearings for expert testimony, deliberate via markup sessions to amend text, and vote to report bills favorably, unfavorably, or with recommendations; only reported bills advance to the floor unless discharged by majority petition, a rare occurrence requiring 218 signatures. The Committee on Rules then typically crafts a special ruleapproved by majority votegoverning floor consideration, including time limits for debate (often one hour total per bill) and permissible amendments, preventing unlimited germaneness challenges unlike Senate practices.
Floor action proceeds in the Committee of the Whole, requiring a quorum of 218 members, where amendments are offered under the rule's constraints, followed by a motion to rise and report back to the full House for final debate, third reading, and passage by simple majority (218 of 435, assuming full attendance). Electronic voting occurs via recorded teller votes or voice/division, with the Speaker preserving order and recognizing members; unlike the Senate, the House prohibits filibusters, enforces strict germaneness for amendments, and limits individual speaking time to maintain expedition. Procedural motions, such as the previous question to end debate or appeals from the chair's rulings, further streamline operations, drawing on precedents from the House's manual since 1789.
In its legislative role, the House collaborates bicamerally to enact federal statutes, where passed bills must match Senate versions exactly or reconcile via conference committees appointed by leadership, with conferees negotiating differences and submitting a report for up-or-down votes without further amendment. This process underscores the House's "power of the purse," initiating appropriations and tax measures to fund government operations, a prerogative exercised since the First Congress in 1789 when it passed the initial revenue acts. Enacted laws require presidential signature or override of veto by two-thirds in both chambers, positioning the House as the chamber most responsive to constituent fiscal priorities amid divided government dynamics observed in 20 of the last 30 Congresses through 2023.

Organizational Structure and Leadership

The United States House of Representatives is organized primarily along partisan lines, with the two major political partiesthe Democratic Party and the Republican Partymaintaining formal caucuses or conferences that coordinate strategy, policy, and member assignments. The majority party, determined by the outcome of House elections, controls leadership positions, committee chairs, and the legislative agenda, while the minority party holds corresponding shadow roles and influences through procedural opposition. This structure ensures that the party with the most seatsrequiring a simple majority of 218 votes assuming full attendanceelects the presiding officer and shapes floor proceedings under House rules adopted at the start of each two-year Congress.
At the apex of leadership is the Speaker of the House, elected by a majority vote of the full membership at the convening of each new Congress, typically the leader of the majority party who presides over sessions, sets the legislative calendar, and represents the institution in inter-branch relations. The Speaker appoints members to committees, influences bill referrals, and wields significant procedural authority, including the power to name special committees or task forces. Assisting the Speaker are the Majority Leader, who serves as the chief strategist for floor debates and bill prioritization, and the Majority Whip, responsible for counting votes, enforcing party discipline, and mobilizing members on key issues. The minority party mirrors this hierarchy with a Minority Leaderwho acts as the party's spokesperson and develops alternative agendasand a Minority Whip, focusing on unity and potential bipartisan leverage.
The House maintains a committee system as the core of its organizational framework, comprising standing committees (permanent panels with defined jurisdictions), select committees (temporary for specific investigations), and joint committees (shared with the Senate). There are approximately 20 standing committees in the House, each tasked with legislative drafting, oversight of executive agencies, and budget authorization within policy areas such as agriculture, appropriations, judiciary, and ways and means; chairs and a majority of members are allocated to the majority party proportional to its House share. Committee assignments are recommended by party steering committees and approved by the full caucus, with House rules limiting members to service on no more than two standing committees and four subcommittees to promote specialization.
Nonpartisan administrative officers support operations, including the Clerk of the House, elected by the membership to manage records, legislative documents, and payroll; the Sergeant at Arms, who enforces order, provides security, and oversees facilities; and the Chief Administrative Officer, handling budgeting and logistics. These roles, along with the Chaplain for ceremonial duties, ensure continuity across party shifts, though their effectiveness can vary with leadership priorities. Party caucuses further refine internal governance through subcommittees on policy, campaigns, and member services, fostering cohesion amid the House's decentralized nature.

Oversight, Impeachment, and Checks on Other Branches

The House of Representatives conducts oversight of the executive branch primarily through standing committees, such as the Committee on Oversight and Accountability, which reviews federal agency operations, investigates waste, fraud, and abuse, and holds hearings to monitor policy implementation. This function, rooted in Congress's legislative and appropriations powers, enables the House to assess executive compliance with statutes, evaluate program effectiveness, and recommend reforms, thereby informing future legislation. Oversight activities include subpoenaing witnesses and documents, as upheld by courts when tied to legislative purposes, ensuring accountability without encroaching on executive privilege except in cases of demonstrated need.
Under Article I, Section 2, Clause 5 of the Constitution, the House holds the sole power to impeach federal officials, including the President, Vice President, and civil officers of the United States, for "Treason, Bribery, or other high Crimes and Misdemeanors" as specified in Article II, Section 4. The process begins with an inquiry, often led by the House Committee on the Judiciary, culminating in the drafting and majority approval of articles of impeachment as a resolution; no Senate involvement occurs until transmission for trial. Historically, the House has impeached 21 individuals, including eight federal judges, one President (Andrew Johnson in 1868), and two more recently (Bill Clinton in 1998 and Donald Trump in 2019 and 2021), though convictions require a two-thirds Senate vote.
These mechanisms form key checks on other branches within the constitutional separation of powers. Against the executive, House oversight and appropriations controlexclusive to revenue bills under Article I, Section 7can withhold funding for unauthorized actions, while impeachment threatens removal, deterring overreach. For the judiciary, the House's impeachment authority applies to Article III judges and justices, with 15 judicial impeachments to date, eight resulting in Senate convictions and removals, thus maintaining balance without undermining judicial independence. This framework, designed by the Framers to prevent any branch's dominance, relies on political processes rather than judicial enforcement for resolution.

Major Achievements and Legislative Impacts

The U.S. House of Representatives has exercised its constitutional prerogative to originate all bills for raising revenue, profoundly shaping federal fiscal policy since 1789. Early tariff acts, such as the Tariff of 1789 passed by the House on April 8, 1789, provided the primary funding mechanism for the nascent federal government, enabling debt assumption and administrative operations under the Hamilton plan. By the Civil War era, House-originated revenue measures like the Morrill Tariff of 1861 and the Revenue Act of 1862 imposed the first federal income tax, generating over $55 million annually by 1865 to finance Union military expenditures exceeding $3 billion. These fiscal innovations sustained wartime efforts and laid precedents for progressive taxation, with the 16th Amendment's ratification in 1913proposed by Congress including House approvalformalizing income taxes that funded 20th-century expansions in government.
Through its exclusive control over appropriations, the House has directed trillions in federal spending, influencing infrastructure, defense, and social programs. Post-Civil War consolidations under the House Appropriations Committee in 1865 centralized funding for Reconstruction-era initiatives, including railroad subsidies via the Pacific Railway Act of 1862, which facilitated over 50,000 miles of track by 1900 and accelerated westward settlement. In the 20th century, House-passed New Deal legislation from 1933 to 1938, including the Emergency Banking Act and Social Security Act, authorized $10 billion in relief spending by 1936, stabilizing banks and establishing unemployment insurance that covered 26 million workers by 1940. The Federal-Aid Highway Act of 1956, originating in the House, allocated $25 billion over 13 years for the Interstate System, constructing 41,000 miles of roads that boosted GDP growth by an estimated 1% annually through enhanced commerce.
Civil rights advancements represent another cornerstone, with the House passing the Civil Rights Act of 1964 on February 10, 1964, by a 290-130 vote, prohibiting discrimination in public accommodations and employment, which led to a 20% rise in Black employment in affected sectors by 1970. The Voting Rights Act of 1965, cleared by the House on July 9, 1965, enforced suffrage protections, increasing Southern Black voter registration from 29% in 1964 to 61% by 1969. In modern eras, the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, introduced as H.R. 1 and passed by the House on November 16, 2017, reduced corporate rates from 35% to 21%, correlating with unemployment dropping to 3.5% by 2019 and wage growth accelerating to 3.1% for low-income workers. These measures underscore the House's causal role in policy outcomes, though impacts vary by economic context and implementation fidelity.

Criticisms, Controversies, and Reform Debates

The practice of partisan gerrymandering, whereby state legislatures draw congressional districts to maximize seats for their party, has been widely criticized for entrenching one-party dominance and undermining electoral competition in the House. In the 2020 redistricting cycle following the census, both Democrats and Republicans manipulated district boundaries to secure disproportionate representation relative to statewide vote shares, with Harvard analysis identifying such tactics as pervasive across states. Courts have struck down extreme examples, such as North Carolina's maps in 2019 for diluting Black voters' influence and Maryland's for favoring Democrats, yet the practice persists due to state-level control over redistricting. Critics argue this distorts democratic representation by prioritizing partisan advantage over voter preferences, though some studies question its direct causation of broader polarization, attributing safer seats more to residential sorting.
Incumbent House members benefit from a pronounced reelection advantage, with success rates consistently above 90% and reaching 95% in the 2024 elections, fostering low turnover and perceptions of institutional entrenchment. This stems from factors including superior fundraisingincumbents raised an average of $2.5 million per cycle in recent yearsname recognition, franking privileges, and gerrymandered districts that insulate them from competitive challenges. Data from the Federal Election Commission and analyses show that only about 5% of seats typically change parties, compared to higher volatility in earlier decades, leading reformers to decry a system where accountability is diluted by career protections rather than voter demand.
Increasing partisan polarization has exacerbated legislative gridlock in the House, with productivity metrics showing fewer bills enacted per Congress amid slim majorities and intra-party fractures. From 2011 to 2023, the House passed an average of under 100 public laws annually, down from historical norms, as evidenced by Brookings tracking of stalled agendas on issues like infrastructure and debt limits. Procedural norms like the informal Hastert Rulerequiring majority party support for bills to reach the floorhave intensified this, blocking measures with overall House majorities but lacking "majority of the majority" backing, as seen in repeated failures on bipartisan immigration reforms. Critics, including procedural scholars, contend this elevates party loyalty over legislative efficacy, though defenders view it as preserving caucus discipline against minority vetoes.
The House's fixed membership of 435, codified by the 1929 Reapportionment Act amid post-census disputes, faces scrutiny for failing to scale with population growth, leaving each representative serving roughly 761,000 constituents as of 2020over nine times the 1910 ratio. This cap, never revisited despite constitutional silence on size, has prompted debates on diluted representation, with Wyoming's single district spanning vast rural areas while urban states fragment influence. Proposals to expand to 500600 seats, as floated in bills like the 2021 Fair Representation Act, aim for smaller districts to enhance local responsiveness and curb big-state dominance in apportionment, though inertia from added costs and logistics stalls action.
Reform debates center on term limits, campaign finance curbs, and ethics enforcement to address careerism and influence peddling. Constitutional amendments limiting House service to three terms, reintroduced in H.J.Res. 12 of the 119th Congress, seek to disrupt incumbency by promoting fresh perspectives, building on state-level precedents where 15 states imposed congressional limits by 1995 before federal court invalidation. Campaign finance reforms, such as Rep. Ramirez's 2025 package targeting dark money post-Citizens United, aim to reduce donor sway, given that PAC contributions averaged $500,000 per winning incumbent in 2022 cycles. Ethics controversies, including 2020 allegations of members trading stocks on nonpublic pandemic informationprompting the STOCK Act's uneven enforcementunderscore calls for stricter insider trading bans and independent oversight, with the Office of Congressional Conduct investigating dozens of cases since 2020.

Other Legislatures Named "House of Representatives"

National Examples

Australia's House of Representatives is the lower house of the federal Parliament, consisting of members directly elected from single-member electoral divisions. It currently comprises 151 members, with elections held at least every three years, serving as the primary forum for representing population proportions and initiating most legislation, including money bills. The house plays a key role in government formation, as the party or coalition commanding a majority typically forms the executive.
Japan's House of Representatives (Shgiin) functions as the lower chamber of the bicameral National Diet, holding primacy over the upper House of Councillors in legislative matters, including the ability to override vetoes with a two-thirds majority. It consists of 465 members elected for up to four-year terms through a mixed system of single-member districts and proportional representation. The house elects the prime minister and approves the cabinet, exerting significant influence over budget and treaty ratification.
The Philippines' House of Representatives forms the lower house of the bicameral Congress, with 316 members as of the 19th Congress, including district representatives and up to 20% allocated to party-list groups representing marginalized sectors. Members serve three-year terms, with the house initiating revenue, tariff, and appropriation bills while sharing general legislative powers with the Senate. It also holds exclusive authority to impeach high officials, subject to Senate trial.
Nigeria's House of Representatives, known as the Green Chamber, is the lower house of the bicameral National Assembly, comprising 360 members elected from federal constituencies for four-year terms. Established under the 1999 Constitution, it handles legislation on matters not exclusively reserved to the Senate, such as appropriation bills, and conducts oversight of executive actions through committees. The house reflects federal character, with representation apportioned based on population equality across states.
In Malaysia, the Dewan Rakyat, or House of Representatives, serves as the lower house of Parliament, with 222 elected members representing constituencies for five-year terms. It holds superior powers in financial legislation and can override Senate vetoes, forming the basis for prime ministerial selection from its majority. The house debates and passes bills critical to national policy, including those on citizenship and elections.
The Netherlands' House of Representatives (Tweede Kamer) is the directly elected lower chamber of the bicameral States General, with 150 members serving four-year terms via proportional representation. It initiates, amends, and approves legislation, approves the budget, and holds the government accountable through questioning and no-confidence votes. Unlike the indirectly elected Senate, it represents the populace and drives policy debates.

Subnational and State-Level Examples

In the United States, the lower chambers of most bicameral state legislatures are named the House of Representatives, mirroring the federal model's emphasis on popular representation. These bodies typically consist of members elected from districts apportioned by population, serving terms ranging from one to four years, with variations in size and session lengths reflecting state-specific constitutional provisions.
The New Hampshire House of Representatives stands out for its scale, with 400 members elected to two-year terms from 204 districts, making it the largest state lower house and the third-largest legislative body worldwide after the U.S. House and the UK House of Commons. This structure, unchanged since a 1942 constitutional amendment, enables fine-grained districting in a small state, where each representative serves an average of about 3,500 constituents.
In Texas, the House of Representatives includes 150 members elected every two years from single-member districts of roughly equal population, with no term limits. The chamber meets in regular session for no more than 140 days biennially, focusing on appropriations, redistricting, and policy on issues like energy and education, as mandated by the state constitution.
The Massachusetts House of Representatives, originating as the House of Deputies in 1644 within the colonial General Court, represents one of the oldest continuously operating legislative bodies in the Western Hemisphere. Comprising 160 members elected to two-year terms, it derives authority from the 1780 state constitutionthe world's oldest written constitution still in effectand contributed to revolutionary committees that shaped American independence.

Defunct or Historical Examples

The Congress of the Republic of Texas operated a bicameral legislature from 1836 to 1845, including a House of Representatives with 24 to 40 members initially, elected annually for one-year terms by qualified voters in single-member districts. Each county received at least one representative, with eligibility requiring candidates to be at least 25 years old, citizens of the republic, and residents of their district for six months prior to election. The House first convened as part of the initial congress on October 3, 1836, at Columbia (now West Columbia), and participated in nine successive one-year sessions before dissolving upon Texas's annexation by the United States on December 29, 1845.
The Confederate States of America, formed by eleven seceded Southern states, adopted a constitution on March 11, 1861, that vested legislative powers in a Congress comprising a Senate and a House of Representatives, modeled closely on the U.S. structure but with representation apportioned by enslaved population counts. House members served two-year terms, elected by voters meeting state-specific qualifications, with initial apportionment granting seats such as six to South Carolina and ten to Georgia pending a census. The permanent House held sessions in the First Congress (18621864) and Second Congress (18641865), with proceedings recorded in official journals covering both open and secret deliberations, until the body's dissolution in April 1865 amid military defeat.
The Kingdom of Hawaii established a House of Representatives in 1840 under King Kamehameha III's constitution, as the lower chamber of a tricameral legislature also including the monarch and an appointed House of Nobles, with representatives elected by male taxpayers from geographic districts to represent commoner interests. The body reviewed revenue and expenditure bills originating there, with sessions held biennially after 1852, continuing through constitutional revisions in 1852 and 1864 that adjusted noble appointments but retained popular election for the House. It operated until the 1893 coup against Queen Liliuokalani, after which the Republic of Hawaii (18941898) maintained a similar elected House until formal U.S. annexation on August 12, 1898, rendering the institution defunct.
