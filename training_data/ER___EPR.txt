ER = EPR
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 ER = EPR is a conjecture in theoretical physics proposing that quantum entanglement, as described in the EinsteinPodolskyRosen (EPR) paradox, is fundamentally equivalent to the existence of EinsteinRosen (ER) bridges, or wormholes, in spacetime geometry. Formulated by Juan Maldacena and Leonard Susskind in 2013, the idea suggests that pairs of entangled particles are physically connected by non-traversable wormholes, providing a geometric interpretation of quantum correlations.
The conjecture draws from general relativity and quantum field theory, where ER bridges originally described theoretical connections between distant regions of spacetime, such as the interiors of black holes, in a 1935 paper by Albert Einstein and Nathan Rosen. Similarly, the EPR paradox, introduced in another 1935 paper by Einstein, Boris Podolsky, and Rosen, highlighted apparent "spooky action at a distance" in quantum mechanics through entangled states that violate classical locality. By equating these concepts (ER = EPR), Maldacena and Susskind aimed to reconcile quantum entanglement with gravitational physics, particularly in the context of the AdS/CFT correspondence, a duality between anti-de Sitter space gravity and conformal field theory.
A primary motivation for ER = EPR is resolving paradoxes in black hole physics, such as the black hole firewall paradox proposed by Almheiri, Marolf, Polchinski, and Sully (AMPS), which questions the consistency of quantum mechanics and general relativity at black hole horizons. The conjecture posits that entanglement between black holes or between a black hole and its Hawking radiation manifests as ER bridges, potentially preserving unitarity and avoiding information loss without firewalls. This framework has inspired extensions to non-black-hole systems, including proposals for traversable wormholes via quantum teleportation protocols and connections to quantum error correction in holographic theories.
Since its introduction, ER = EPR has influenced research in quantum gravity, with tests explored through toy models like entangled spins linked by wormholes and implications for spacetime emergence from entanglement. However, it remains unproven, serving as a heuristic guide rather than a formal theorem, and continues to spark debate on the nature of spacetime at quantum scales.

Background Concepts

EPR Paradox

In 1935, Albert Einstein, Boris Podolsky, and Nathan Rosen published a seminal paper questioning the completeness of quantum mechanics, arguing that its probabilistic predictions implied "spooky action at a distance" for entangled systems, which they deemed incompatible with a complete physical theory. They proposed a thought experiment involving two particles prepared in an entangled state, suggesting that quantum mechanics must be supplemented by hidden variables to restore locality and realism.
The EPR paradox centers on pairs of particles, known as EPR pairs, which are entangled such that the quantum state of one cannot be described independently of the other. In the classic example using spin-1/2 particles in a spin-singlet state, a measurement of the spin of one particle along any axis instantly determines the spin of the distant partner to be opposite, regardless of the separation distance, without any classical signal transmission. This correlation arises because the joint wave function collapses upon measurement, yielding perfect anti-correlation for the singlet state.
A representative form of this entangled state, often expressed in the computational basis for qubits, is the Bell state:

|\Psi^-\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left( |01\rangle - |10\rangle \right)

where the subscripts denote the states of the two particles. This state implies non-locality in quantum predictions, as the outcome for one particle correlates instantaneously with the other, challenging classical intuitions of locality while preserving no-signaling causality.
The paradox was resolved in 1964 by John Bell's theorem, which demonstrated that no local hidden-variable theory could reproduce all quantum mechanical predictions for such entangled systems, deriving inequalities that quantum mechanics violates. Subsequent experiments, notably those by Alain Aspect in the early 1980s, confirmed these violations by more than five standard deviations using photon pairs, ruling out local realism and affirming quantum mechanics' non-local correlations. Later loophole-free tests in 2015 further solidified these results with high statistical significance.

Einstein-Rosen Bridge

The Einstein-Rosen bridge originates from a 1935 paper by Albert Einstein and Nathan Rosen, who derived a solution to the vacuum Einstein field equations describing a topological connection between two asymptotically flat regions of spacetime based on the Schwarzschild metric. This construction reinterprets the Schwarzschild geometry to eliminate point-like singularities by envisioning a particle as a narrow "bridge" linking two otherwise identical sheets of space, thereby modeling elementary particles within a purely gravitational framework without additional fields. Initially proposed as a unified description of matter and gravity, the solution was later understood as an artifact of general relativity's description of wormhole-like structures, with the term "wormhole" coined by John Archibald Wheeler in 1957 to characterize such spacetime tunnels.

The Conjecture

Historical Context and Proposal

The ER=EPR conjecture emerged from foundational concepts in general relativity and quantum mechanics, including the Einstein-Rosen (ER) bridge proposed in 1935 as a theoretical construct linking distant regions of spacetime, and the Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen (EPR) paradox from the same year, which highlighted apparent non-local correlations in quantum entanglement.
Its historical development accelerated with the AdS/CFT correspondence, introduced by Juan Maldacena in 1997, which demonstrated a duality between gravitational theories in anti-de Sitter space and conformal field theories without gravity, providing a framework for exploring quantum gravity through quantum field theory.
During the 2000s, research on black hole entanglement deepened these connections, particularly through the holographic entanglement entropy proposal by Shinsei Ryu and Tadashi Takayanagi in 2006, which geometrically interpreted quantum entanglement in boundary theories via minimal surfaces in the bulk gravitational dual.
In June 2013, Juan Maldacena and Leonard Susskind formalized the ER=EPR conjecture in their paper "Cool horizons for entangled black holes," asserting that Einstein-Rosen bridges represent a geometric manifestation of quantum entanglement, such that any two entangled systemsranging from particles to black holesare connected by such a structure in quantum gravity.
As described in the paper's abstract, general relativity admits solutions where two distant black holes are linked via an Einstein-Rosen bridge, interpretable as maximally entangled EPR pairs, with the conjecture extending this to suggest that entanglement geometrically realizes wormholes for more general states.
The proposal gained prominence when presented by Susskind and Maldacena at the KITP Rapid Response Workshop "Black Holes: Complementarity, Fuzz, or Fire?" held August 1930, 2013, at the Kavli Institute for Theoretical Physics, where it was positioned as a potential resolution to longstanding issues in quantum gravity, including information paradoxes.

Motivation from Black Hole Paradoxes

The black hole information paradox arises from Stephen Hawking's calculation that black holes emit thermal radiation due to quantum effects near the event horizon, leading to gradual evaporation. This radiation appears completely random and thermal, implying that the quantum information about matter falling into the black hole is lost forever once the black hole fully evaporates. Such information loss conflicts with the fundamental principle of quantum mechanics that evolution must be unitary, preserving the purity of quantum states and all encoded information.
Efforts to resolve this paradox intensified with the proposal of the firewall hypothesis by Almheiri, Marolf, Polchinski, and Sully (AMPS) in 2012, which highlighted a deeper tension involving entanglement. For an old black hole that has emitted significant Hawking radiation while maintaining unitarity, the outgoing radiation must become highly entangled with the early radiation to purify the state and preserve information. However, quantum field theory in curved spacetime predicts that each outgoing Hawking mode is also entangled with a partner mode falling inward across the horizon, forming an Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen (EPR) pair essential for the vacuum structure near the horizon.
This setup invokes the monogamy of entanglement, a quantum principle stating that a given quantum system cannot be maximally entangled with two independent systems simultaneously. The late-time outgoing mode cannot be maximally entangled both with the early radiation (to ensure unitarity) and with its interior partner (to maintain a smooth horizon), creating an irresolvable conflict. To uphold unitarity without violating monogamy, the entanglement across the horizon must break, implying that the horizon is not smooth but instead features a high-energy "firewall" of particles that would incinerate any infalling observer, directly contradicting the equivalence principle of general relativity, which demands no dramatic effects at the horizon for free-falling observers.
Classical Einstein-Rosen (ER) bridges, which describe wormhole-like connections between two asymptotically flat regions in the maximally extended Schwarzschild geometry, fail to alleviate these quantum inconsistencies. These bridges dynamically pinch off almost immediately after formation, rendering them non-traversable and incapable of transmitting signals or quantum information between the exterior and interior regions in a way that could preserve distant entanglement or resolve unitarity violations. Without incorporating quantum effects into the geometry itself, classical ER bridges cannot reconcile the required long-range EPR correlations across horizons with the monogamy constraint, leaving the paradoxes intact.

Theoretical Formulation

Core Ideas and Equivalence

The ER = EPR conjecture proposes that quantum entanglement, exemplified by Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen (EPR) pairs, is fundamentally equivalent to Einstein-Rosen (ER) bridges, which are non-traversable wormholes in spacetime geometry. This equivalence suggests that every EPR-entangled pair of systems is connected by an ER bridge, providing a geometric interpretation of quantum correlations as spacetime connectivity.
A key aspect of this conjecture is that the ER bridges are non-traversable, meaning they do not permit the transmission of signals faster than light, which aligns with the no-signaling principle of quantum mechanics inherent in EPR entanglement. These wormholes thus encode the statistical correlations between entangled particles without enabling causal influence across the bridge, paralleling the non-local yet acausal nature of quantum entanglement where measurements on one particle instantaneously correlate with the other but convey no usable information.
The conjecture asserts that the entanglement entropy $ S $ between two subsystems corresponds to the geometry of the connecting ER bridge, specifically through the area of the minimal surface at the wormhole throat. This relation extends the Ryu-Takayanagi formula, which equates entanglement entropy to the area of a minimal extremal surface in the dual geometry:
S=A4G,
S = \frac{A}{4G},
S=4GA,
where $ A $ is the area of the minimal surface and $ G $ is Newton's gravitational constant. In the ER = EPR framework, this formula interprets the minimal geodesic lengths or surface areas in the spacetime geometry as direct manifestations of the underlying entanglement structure.
In conceptual toy models, maximally entangled statessuch as those approaching a Bell stategenerate Einstein-Rosen bridges that link the entangled subsystems, with the bridge's geometry reflecting the maximal degree of entanglement. For instance, in simplified scenarios involving highly entangled degrees of freedom, the ER bridge emerges as the shortest geometric path encoding the full quantum correlations, demonstrating how entanglement "stitches" spacetime together at a fundamental level.

Connection to Holographic Duality

The AdS/CFT correspondence posits a duality between quantum gravity in anti-de Sitter (AdS) space and a conformal field theory (CFT) on its boundary, where the bulk geometry emerges from the entanglement structure of the boundary quantum state. In this framework, spacetime connectivity in the bulk is tied to quantum entanglement on the boundary, with the Ryu-Takayanagi formula relating the entanglement entropy of a boundary region to the area of a minimal surface in the bulk. This holographic principle provides a controlled setting to explore gravitational phenomena through quantum field theory computations, particularly for entangled states that might correspond to non-trivial bulk topologies.
Within holography, the ER=EPR conjecture manifests as wormholes serving as geometric duals to entangled CFT states, where an Einstein-Rosen bridge connects asymptotically AdS boundaries associated with maximally entangled pairs. For instance, the eternal AdS black hole, dual to a thermofield double state in two entangled CFTs, features an ER bridge linking the horizons, encoding the boundary entanglement geometrically. A concrete realization appears in the holographic dual of an EPR pair in maximally supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory, modeled by a non-traversable wormhole on the worldsheet of a quark-antiquark flux tube, confirming that entanglement generates such bulk connections. Replica wormholes, arising in the path integral over multi-sheeted Riemann surfaces, contribute to computing the entanglement entropy by summing over connected topologies that capture the shared information between replicas.
Key developments in 2013-2014, including extensions by Maldacena and collaborators, demonstrated how ER bridges resolve holographic paradoxes, such as those involving black hole interiors and information recovery, by interpreting entanglement as the underlying glue of spacetime. These works showed that for entangled boundary regions A and B, the mutual information $ I(A:B) = S_A + S_B - S_{A \cup B} $ governs phase transitions in the bulk: a positive value corresponds to a connected wormhole phase where the minimal surface for $ A \cup B $ traverses the bridge, enabling entanglement wedge reconstructionthe bulk region dual to the CFT subalgebra on A (or B) extends across the wormhole. This equivalence highlights how ER=EPR provides a geometric resolution to entanglement-driven bulk reconstruction in AdS/CFT.

Implications

For Quantum Gravity and Spacetime

The ER=EPR conjecture proposes that the fabric of spacetime emerges from underlying quantum entanglement, positing that geometric structures, such as wormholes, arise as effective descriptions of entangled quantum states. In this framework, spacetime geometry is viewed as a coarse-grained manifestation of entanglement networks, where the connectivity between distant regions is dictated by the pattern and strength of quantum correlations rather than fundamental fields or particles. This idea aligns with the "it from qubit" paradigm, which suggests that the physical universe"it"originates from quantum bits of information and their entanglements, providing a pathway to reconcile quantum mechanics with gravitational phenomena.
A key illustration of this emergence comes through tensor network representations, where quantum states are efficiently encoded using networks of interconnected tensors that capture entanglement structure. The multi-scale entanglement renormalization ansatz (MERA), a specific tensor network, demonstrates how hierarchical layers of entanglement can approximate the geometry of anti-de Sitter (AdS) spacetime, with links in the network corresponding to Einstein-Rosen (ER) bridges that encode maximal entanglement akin to EPR pairs. In MERA, the radial direction of the network mirrors the holographic radial coordinate in AdS, allowing local operations on the boundary to propagate through entanglement layers, thereby generating bulk geometric features. This connection underscores how ER=EPR extends to non-black-hole systems, suggesting that spacetime curvature emerges from the renormalization of entanglement across scales.
On a broader scale, ER=EPR bridges the domains of quantum information theory and general relativity, offering a potential foundation for a full theory of quantum gravity by treating entanglement as the primitive element from which gravitational dynamics derive. Rather than quantizing spacetime directly, this approach inverts the problem: quantum entanglement generates effective spacetime geometry, resolving tensions in semiclassical approximations and providing tools like entanglement entropy to probe gravitational degrees of freedom. Particularly in the context of holographic duality, ER=EPR serves as a testing ground for these ideas, where boundary entanglement reconstructs bulk geometry.
Fundamentally, ER=EPR induces a conceptual shift in quantum gravity, elevating entanglement to a primary building block of reality, with wormholes representing its geometric dual rather than exotic topologies. This perspective implies that the smoothness and locality of spacetime are illusions arising from highly entangled microstates, challenging traditional notions of space as a fixed arena and instead portraying it as a dynamic emergent property of quantum correlations.

Applications to Black Holes

One prominent application of the ER=EPR conjecture is its proposed resolution to the black hole firewall paradox, which arises from the apparent violation of quantum monogamy in the entanglement between Hawking radiation and the black hole interior. According to ER=EPR, the Einstein-Rosen (ER) bridges formed by maximal entanglement connect the interiors of entangled black holes, allowing the entanglement across the horizon to be maintained without requiring a high-energy "firewall" at the event horizon that would destroy infalling observers. This geometric interpretation preserves the no-drama principle of general relativity while upholding quantum entanglement principles.
The conjecture also addresses the black hole information paradox by suggesting that quantum effects can render certain ER bridges traversable, enabling information from the black hole interior to escape via Hawking radiation during evaporation. In models using anti-de Sitter/conformal field theory (AdS/CFT) duality, a double-trace deformation in the boundary theory induces negative energy flux that backreacts on the bulk geometry, opening the wormhole throat and permitting causal signal propagation from one side to the other. This mechanism ensures unitarity in the evaporation process, as information encoded in the entangled interior can be teleported to the outgoing radiation without violating causality.
A key computational tool in this framework is the entanglement island formula, which incorporates ER bridges to derive the Page curve for black hole entropy during evaporation. Entanglement islands are regions behind the horizon that are maximally entangled with the radiation; under ER=EPR, these islands are connected to the exterior via microscopic ER bridges, allowing the generalized entropycomprising the area term and the von Neumann entropy of the island plus radiationto follow the unitary Page curve, where entropy rises to a peak at the Page time and then decreases to zero. This resolves the paradox by demonstrating that late-time radiation purifies the early radiation, preserving all information. In Jackiw-Teitelboim gravity models with gravitating baths, the transition to connected wormhole geometries via entanglement explicitly realizes ER=EPR, confirming the island rule's validity.
An illustrative example is the case of two maximally entangled black holes, where the ER=EPR conjecture posits that their quantum entanglement manifests as a traversable wormhole connecting their interiors. As Hawking radiation is emitted, the entanglement structure preserves correlations between the two black holes, with the ER bridge ensuring that information from one interior remains accessible to the other despite the radiation process; this setup avoids firewall formation and supports unitary evolution, as the wormhole dynamics maintain the overall purity of the entangled state.

Developments

Theoretical Advances

Following the proposal of the ER = EPR conjecture, significant theoretical progress occurred between 2014 and 2018, particularly in constructing explicit models that realize traversable wormholes through quantum field theory deformations. In 2016, Gao, Jafferis, and Wall demonstrated that non-traversable Einstein-Rosen bridges in anti-de Sitter (AdS) spacetime can be rendered traversable by introducing a double-trace deformation in the dual conformal field theory (CFT). This deformation, which couples the CFTs on either side of the boundary, effectively allows signals to propagate through the wormhole while preserving unitarity and consistency with the ER = EPR idea that entanglement underpins geometric connectivity. Their construction relies on the holographic duality, where the deformation corresponds to a bulk interaction that shortens the wormhole throat, enabling classical information transfer without violating causality in the field theory.
Tensor network models emerged as powerful tools to simulate and test ER = EPR principles during this period, providing discrete analogs of holographic geometries where entanglement directly maps to spacetime structure. The HaPPY (hyperbolic and planar perfect) code, introduced by Pastawski, Yoshida, Harlow, and Preskill in 2015, constructs a family of quantum error-correcting codes using perfect tensors arranged in hyperbolic tilings, mimicking the AdS/CFT bulk-boundary correspondence. In this framework, bulk operators are reconstructed from boundary degrees of freedom only within entanglement wedges, illustrating how maximal entanglement across boundaries generates wormhole-like connectivity in the emergent geometry, consistent with ER = EPR. Similarly, extensions to the Sachdev-Ye-Kitaev (SYK) model, a solvable large-N quantum mechanical system with random interactions, incorporated random tensor networks to model low-dimensional gravitational duals. By 2018, these SYK-inspired tensor networks captured chaotic dynamics and entanglement scrambling that produce effective ER bridges, where pairwise entanglement in the SYK chain simulates traversable wormhole geometries in the dual description.
Theoretical consistency checks further solidified ER = EPR by showing that generic entangled states in holographic theories yield wormhole-like bulk geometries. Using the Ryu-Takayanagi formula for entanglement entropy, studies in holographic duality have indicated that highly entangled CFT states with causally disconnected boundaries can induce connected bulk spacetimes featuring Einstein-Rosen bridges when the entanglement entropy exceeds a threshold corresponding to the minimal surface area. This result extends to generic pure states, where the mutual information between subsystems dictates the emergence of wormhole topologies, ensuring no violations of quantum monogamy or firewall paradoxes. Susskind's 2016 analysis reinforced this by demonstrating that GHZ-like entangled states maintain consistency with ER = EPR only when interpreted through wormhole geometries that resolve measurement inconsistencies in quantum gravity. These proofs highlight the conjecture's robustness within holography, where entanglement entropy gradients directly prescribe spacetime connectivity.
A pivotal advance in 2019 came from Almheiri et al., who incorporated entanglement islands and replica wormholes to resolve the black hole information paradox while aligning with ER = EPR. In their framework, the fine-grained entropy of Hawking radiation follows the Page curve through the inclusion of "islands"regions behind the horizon entangled with the radiationwhose boundaries are quantum extremal surfaces. Replica wormholes, interpreted as ER bridges in the gravitational path integral, connect the black hole interior to the exterior radiation, ensuring unitary evaporation and purity restoration. This construction quantifies how ER = EPR bridges mediate the entanglement transfer, with the island contribution to the generalized entropy scaling as the area of the ER throat, thus providing a semiclassical realization of the conjecture in evaporating black hole spacetimes.

Recent Proposals and Tests

In 2023 and 2024, several theoretical proposals extended the ER=EPR conjecture by integrating it with models of spacetime featuring fractal granularity and multi-fold universes. These works suggest that entanglement not only generates wormhole-like connections but also contributes to a granular, self-similar structure of spacetime at microscopic scales, potentially resolving issues in holographic duality. For instance, analyses within multi-fold gravity frameworks demonstrate how EPR pairs induce emergent geometries with multiple folding dimensions, aligning ER bridges with non-local entanglement propagation.
Advancing into 2025, key proposals provided more explicit realizations of bulk spacetime from boundary entanglement and computable derivations of ER bridges. In May 2025, a study examined the finite complexity of the ER=EPR state in de Sitter space, showing how boundary entanglement entropy directly yields a finite-area bulk geometry, supporting the conjecture's role in cosmological spacetimes with positive curvature. By October 2025, researchers derived explicit Einstein-Rosen bridge metrics from EPR-entangled states in a thermal conformal field theory, offering a verifiable pathway to recover gravitational dynamics from quantum correlations without ad hoc assumptions. These developments emphasize operational formulations, recasting ER=EPR as a theorem derivable from standard quantum field theory principles.
Experimental tests of ER=EPR have gained traction through quantum simulations and astrophysical probes. Laboratory efforts propose using quantum systems, such as trapped ions and optical lattices, to mimic wormhole teleportation protocols, where entangled qubits simulate ER bridge traversability by measuring information transfer rates across synthetic horizons. A notable 2022 experiment on Google's Sycamore quantum processor simulated traversable wormhole dynamics, providing evidence for ER=EPR-inspired quantum teleportation with measurable fidelity in a small-scale system. On the astrophysical front, gravitational wave observatories like LIGO have been proposed as targets for signatures from entangled binary systems, with predictions of modulated waveforms from EPR-correlated black hole mergers that deviate from general relativity by entanglement-induced phase shifts. These proposals leverage the conjecture's prediction of non-local correlations imprinting on wave strain data.
Extensions of early quantitative frameworks have evolved into 2025 metrics for empirical validation, mapping entanglement measures like negativity or squashed entropy to geometric distances in dual spacetimes. Such metrics provide scalable benchmarks for theoretical consistency in quantum circuits.
