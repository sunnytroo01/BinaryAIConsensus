Major League Baseball

 Major League Baseball (MLB) is the oldest continuously operating professional sports league in the United States, consisting of 30 member clubs located across the US and Canada.
The league is structured into two coequal entities, the American League (AL) and National League (NL), each containing 15 teams divided into three geographic divisions: East, Central, and West.
Teams play a 162-game regular season schedule from late March or early April to late September, followed by postseason playoffs that determine the World Series champion between the AL and NL pennant winners.
The NL originated in 1876 as the first major professional baseball league, while the AL was established in 1901; the modern MLB framework emerged in 1903 via the National Agreement that resolved conflicts and inaugurated the World Series.
MLB achieved a landmark in civil rights by integrating in 1947 when Jackie Robinson debuted for the Brooklyn Dodgers, ending decades of racial segregation in the major leagues.
The league has faced significant controversies, including the 1919 Black Sox gambling scandal that prompted the creation of the commissioner position to safeguard integrity, and the late-1990s to mid-2000s steroid era, where performance-enhancing drugs contributed to record home run totals and led to stricter drug-testing policies after public and congressional scrutiny.

History

Origins and Early Leagues (19th Century)

The modern form of baseball originated in the United States during the mid-19th century, with the Knickerbocker Base Ball Club of New York formalizing a set of rules on September 23, 1845, under the leadership of Alexander Cartwright. These Knickerbocker Rules established foundational elements of the game, including a diamond-shaped infield with bases 90 feet apart, three outs per inning, and the prohibition of throwing the ball at runners to retire them. The rules shifted the game away from earlier variants like town ball and rounders toward a more structured contest emphasizing skill over physical contact.
By 1857, the National Association of Base Ball Players (NABBP) was established as the first organized governing body for baseball, initially restricting participation to amateur clubs from the New York area. The NABBP standardized rules across member teams, which grew to over 90 clubs by 1865, and promoted interstate matches that popularized the sport. However, covert payments to players eroded amateur ideals, leading the association to permit open professionalism in 1869.
The Cincinnati Red Stockings became the first openly professional team that year, hiring Harry Wright as player-manager and paying salaries totaling around $9,300 for the season. Under Wright, the team compiled a 570 record in competitive games, embarking on extensive tours that demonstrated the viability of salaried play and spread baseball's appeal nationwide. This success accelerated the shift to professionalism, culminating in the formation of the National Association of Professional Base Ball Players (NA) in 1871 as the sport's inaugural professional league with nine teams.
The NA operated through 1875 but suffered from lax scheduling, gambling scandals, and contract jumping, prompting Chicago White Stockings owner William Hulbert to organize the National League on February 2, 1876, with eight charter clubs committed to territorial rights, a fixed schedule, and player contracts enforceable by reserve clauses. Hulbert's league imposed stricter discipline, expelling teams for misconduct and prioritizing financial stability over short-term gains. In response to the National League's conservative policies, including bans on Sunday games and alcohol sales, the American Association launched in 1882 as a rival major league, attracting crowds with more permissive rules and lower admission prices. The AA's success forced a 1883 agreement recognizing both as major leagues, establishing a period of competition that expanded baseball's reach before their eventual merger into the National League by 1892.

Dead-Ball Era and Structural Reforms (1900-1919)

The Dead-Ball Era in Major League Baseball, spanning approximately 1900 to 1919, was defined by low-scoring games, with league-wide batting averages hovering around .250 to .270 and home runs exceedingly rare, often totaling fewer than 500 across both leagues in a season. Pitchers dominated through strategies like the spitball, scuffed deliveries, and high-volume complete games, exemplified by Cy Young, who amassed 511 career wins, including 30 in 1901 alone, while maintaining a career ERA of 2.63 over 7,356 innings. Offense relied on "small ball" tactics such as bunting, base stealing, and hit-and-run plays, as baseballs were softer, discolored from repeated use without frequent replacement, and fields featured expansive outfields in early steel-and-concrete ballparks.
A pivotal rule change contributing to the era's defensive tilt was the adoption of the foul-strike rule, implemented by the National League in 1901 and the American League in 1903, which counted most foul balls as strikes after the first, curbing batters' ability to foul off pitches indefinitely and reducing walks while still favoring pitchers. Prior to this, unlimited fouls allowed hitters to wait for favorable pitches, inflating on-base opportunities; post-rule, scoring remained suppressed, with typical games ending 2-1 or 3-2, and stolen bases peakingsuch as 347 league-wide in the AL in 1910. Standout performers included pitchers Walter Johnson, who led the AL in strikeouts seven times, and hitters like Ty Cobb, whose aggressive style yielded a .367 career average through relentless singles and steals.
Structurally, the era saw the American League, reorganized from the minor-league Western League, declare major-league status in 1901 under Ban Johnson, challenging the National League's monopoly by placing teams in key cities like Boston, Chicago, and New York, sparking player raids and the "Baseball War." The conflict resolved with the 1903 National Agreement, establishing the National Commissioncomprising AL president Johnson, NL president Thomas Lynch, and arbitrator August Herrmannto oversee disputes, player contracts via the reserve clause, and interstate commerce protections, while formalizing the World Series as a best-of-nine exhibition between league champions. The inaugural World Series in 1903 pitted the NL's Pittsburgh Pirates against the AL's Boston Americans (later Red Sox), with Boston prevailing 5-3; however, no series occurred in 1904 as NL champion New York Giants owner John T. Brush refused to recognize the AL.
The period also faced external challenges, including the Federal League's entry as a third major circuit in 1914, which lured stars like Hal Chase with higher salaries and prompted antitrust lawsuits, but it folded after 1915, reinforcing MLB's duopoly. Labor tensions simmered under the reserve clause, binding players to teams indefinitely, fostering grievances over low payaveraging $2,000-$4,000 annually amid rising ticket revenues. The era culminated in the 1919 Black Sox Scandal, where eight Chicago White Sox players, including stars Shoeless Joe Jackson and Eddie Cicotte, conspired with gamblers to fix the World Series against the Cincinnati Reds, losing 5-3 amid suspicious play like intentional errors and weak pitching, eroding public trust and prompting Commissioner Kenesaw Mountain Landis's appointment in 1920 to enforce integrity.

Live-Ball Era and Popularity Surge (1920-1940s)

The Live-Ball Era commenced in 1920, marked by significant rule changes that dramatically increased offensive production in Major League Baseball. Umpires were instructed to replace scuffed or discolored balls more frequently, ensuring cleaner and more visible baseballs in play, while the spitball and other doctored pitches were largely banned, with exceptions only for a limited number of grandfathered pitchers. These alterations, combined with a possibly tighter-wound ball, ended the low-scoring Dead-Ball Era and led to a surge in home runs and runs scored league-wide.
Babe Ruth epitomized this offensive revolution after his trade to the New York Yankees in January 1920. Previously setting the single-season home run record with 29 in 1919 for the Boston Red Sox, Ruth shattered it with 54 homers in 1920 and 59 in 1921, feats that redefined hitting strategy and drew massive crowds. His power-hitting prowess not only propelled the Yankees to their first American League pennant in 1921 but also popularized home runs as the game's premier spectacle, shifting focus from small-ball tactics to slugging.
Baseball's popularity exploded during the 1920s, fueled by Ruth's charisma and the era's exciting play. Average attendance climbed steadily, reaching a peak of over 8,200 per game by 1930, reflecting the sport's status as America's pastime amid post-World War I prosperity. The Yankees established dominance, winning three consecutive American League pennants from 1921 to 1923 and securing World Series titles in 1923 and 1927 with the famed "Murderers' Row" lineup featuring Ruth and Lou Gehrig.
The Great Depression tempered growth in the 1930s, with attendance dropping about 40 percent between 1930 and 1933 due to economic hardship, yet the sport retained strong appeal through enduring stars and rivalries. Gehrig's consecutive games streak and Joe DiMaggio's debut in 1936 sustained fan interest, including DiMaggio's unbroken 56-game hitting streak in 1941, while pitching feats like Johnny Vander Meer's consecutive no-hitters in 1938 highlighted continued mound excellence; the inaugural All-Star Game in 1933 at Comiskey Park drew over 49,000 spectators, underscoring baseball's cultural resilience. The Yankees continued their supremacy, capturing four straight World Series from 1936 to 1939, solidifying their dynasty amid the era's challenges.

World War II, Integration, and Postwar Growth (1940s-1960s)

World War II severely depleted Major League Baseball rosters, as over 500 major league players enlisted or were drafted into military service between 1941 and 1945. Prominent stars including Bob Feller, who enlisted in the Navy on December 8, 1941, Ted Williams, and Joe DiMaggio served overseas or in training roles, prompting teams to recruit from minor leagues, semi-pro circuits, and even industrial leagues. The league maintained operations with government approval for public morale, implementing measures like curfews and travel restrictions, while attendance fell to a low of 7,637,817 in 1943 before recovering to 11,923,099 in 1945 amid returning veterans and war's end.
Integration of African American players began in 1947 under Brooklyn Dodgers general manager Branch Rickey, who signed Jackie Robinson to a minor league contract on October 23, 1945, after scouting Negro League talent to bolster competitiveness. Robinson debuted in the majors on April 15, 1947, against the Boston Braves at Ebbets Field, posting a .297 batting average and leading the National League in stolen bases that season despite facing racial hostility from opponents, fans, and some teammates. The Cleveland Indians followed in the American League by promoting Larry Doby on July 5, 1947; additional pioneers included Hank Thompson and Willard Brown with the St. Louis Browns later that year. Integration advanced unevenly, with nine former Negro Leaguers earning MVP awards in the first decade post-1947, but southern teams and holdouts like the Boston Red Sox delayed until Pumpsie Green's debut in 1959.
Postwar economic prosperity and demographic shifts fueled MLB's expansion westward and growth in popularity. Attendance climbed from 13,751,169 in 1946 to peaks like Cleveland's single-team record of 2,620,627 in 1948, reflecting baby boom enthusiasm and suburban migration. Television broadcasts proliferated in the 1950s, with the first coast-to-coast game airing on October 3, 1951, between the New York Giants and Brooklyn Dodgers, enhancing national reach despite initial concerns over minor league declines. Relocations capitalized on untapped markets: the Boston Braves moved to Milwaukee in 1953, drawing 1.8 million fans in their debut season; the St. Louis Browns to Baltimore (1954) and Philadelphia Athletics to Kansas City (1955) followed, before the Dodgers shifted from Brooklyn to Los Angeles and Giants from New York to San Francisco in 1958, introducing West Coast play and spurring further infrastructure like the Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum for temporary use. Unusual events included Houston Colt .45s pitcher Ken Johnson throwing a complete-game no-hitter on April 23, 1964, against the Cincinnati Reds but losing 1-0 due to defensive errors. These changes, alongside returning talent and rule tweaks like expanded night games, positioned MLB for sustained attendance above 20 million annually by the late 1950s.

Expansion, Relocation, and Labor Tensions (1960s-1980s)

The American League initiated MLB's modern expansion era on December 14, 1960, by conducting an expansion draft that stocked two new franchises: the Los Angeles Angels and a replacement Washington Senators team. Both debuted in 1961, with the new Senators replacing the original franchisewhich had relocated to Minneapolis-St. Paul, Minnesota, and become the Minnesota Twinswhile also capitalizing on untapped West Coast and capital markets amid threats from the proposed Continental League. The National League followed suit in 1962, adding the New York Mets and Houston Colt .45's (renamed Houston Astros in 1965) through a similar draft process, restoring a National League presence in New York after the Giants' departure and accessing growing Sun Belt populations. By 1969, further expansion added the Kansas City Royals and Seattle Pilots to the AL alongside the Montreal Expos and San Diego Padres in the NL, increasing total teams to 24 and prompting the introduction of divisional play to manage scheduling; this wave addressed fan demand in mid-sized markets while diluting talent pools initially, as evidenced by the expansion teams' combined .415 winning percentage that season. The AL expanded once more in 1977 with the Seattle Mariners and Toronto Blue Jays, reaching 26 teams and marking MLB's first Canadian entry, driven by television revenue potential and international outreach.
Relocations during this period often stemmed from chronically low attendance, inadequate facilities, and owner quests for more lucrative venues, exacerbating regional imbalances until expansions could stabilize. The Milwaukee Braves relocated to Atlanta in 1966 after drawing just 550,000 fans in 1965 amid competition from the White Sox and Packers, seeking the Southeast's burgeoning economy and securing a 25-year lease at Atlanta-Fulton County Stadium. The Kansas City Athletics moved to Oakland in 1968 following years of sub-400,000 attendance under owner Charlie Finley, who cited financial losses exceeding $500,000 annually and leveraged Oakland's municipal funding for a coliseum upgrade. The short-lived Seattle Pilots franchise, one of the 1969 expansion teams, relocated to Milwaukee as the Brewers after just one season in 1970 due to poor gate receipts (under 700,000 fans) and stadium disputes with the city, reflecting hasty expansion risks without stable infrastructure. Finally, the expansion-era Washington Senators departed for Arlington, Texas, as the Rangers in 1972 after averaging below 800,000 attendees and a fan riot on the final home game that injured players, underscoring D.C.'s challenges in retaining teams post-1950s losses.
Labor tensions escalated as the Major League Baseball Players Association (MLBPA), under executive director Marvin Miller from 1966, challenged the owners' reserve clausea perpetual contract mechanism binding players indefinitelyand pursued collective bargaining to redistribute revenue from booming TV deals and attendance. The first CBA emerged in 1968, elevating the minimum salary from $6,000 (unchanged since the 1940s) to $10,000 and establishing grievance procedures, though it preserved the reserve system amid owners' antitrust exemption. Players struck on April 15, 1972the first work stoppage in MLB historyover stalled pension contributions amid 20% inflation, halting play for 13 days and canceling 86 games until a settlement added $400,000 to retirement funding and introduced salary arbitration in 1973, empowering agents and eroding owner monopsony power. The 1975 Messersmith-McNally arbitration ruling nullified the reserve clause, ushering in free agency by 1976 and spiking average salaries from $45,000 in 1975 to over $200,000 by decade's end, but provoking owner resistance. A 1980 spring lockout lasted 23 days over compensation rules, while the 1981 strike, triggered by owners' demands for mandatory free-agent drafts, idled players from July 12 to August 3 and September 21 to October 12, erasing 713 games, awarding half-seasons' divisions, and imposing a split All-Star format; it resolved with moderated compensation but highlighted causal frictions from asymmetric bargaining, as owners sought to curb salary escalation exceeding 200% since free agency. These conflicts, rooted in players' leverage from talent scarcity and media growth, disrupted schedules but entrenched union gains, with CBAs by 1985 incorporating revenue sharing precursors amid attendance dips from 26 million games lost in strikes.

Steroid Era, Strikes, and Contraction Threats (1990s-2000s)

The 1994 Major League Baseball strike commenced on August 12, 1994, initiated by the Major League Baseball Players Association (MLBPA) in response to club owners' proposals for a salary cap and increased revenue sharing to address rising player salaries and perceived financial disparities among teams. The work stoppage halted play after 113 games, marking the first cancellation of the World Series since 1904, and persisted for 232 days until April 2, 1995, when a federal judge's intervention and a new collective bargaining agreement (CBA) resolved the impasse without a salary cap but with luxury taxes and revenue sharing mechanisms. The strike resulted in lost revenues exceeding $1 billion and a shortened 1995 season of 144 games, contributing to fan disillusionment and an initial attendance decline of about 20% in 1995 compared to 1993 levels.
In the aftermath, MLB faced ongoing labor tensions and financial pressures, culminating in contraction threats in the early 2000s as owners sought to eliminate two franchises to improve economic viability amid unprofitable markets and outdated stadiums. On November 6, 2001, MLB owners voted 28-2 to contract the Minnesota Twins and Montreal Expos by the end of the 2001 season, citing chronic low attendance and revenue for both teamsthe Twins drew under 1.5 million fans annually in the Hubert H. Humphrey Metrodome, while the Expos averaged below 1 million in Olympic Stadium. Legal challenges, including a lawsuit by Hennepin County, Minnesota, enforcing the Twins' lease through 2005, and opposition from the MLBPA under antitrust laws, blocked the plan; the Twins were compelled to play the 2002 and 2003 seasons, eventually securing public funding for Target Field in 2010, while the Expos relocated to Washington, D.C., as the Nationals in 2005.
Parallel to these labor and structural crises, the "Steroid Era" emerged prominently in the late 1990s and early 2000s, characterized by widespread use of anabolic steroids and other performance-enhancing drugs (PEDs) that inflated offensive statistics, particularly home runs, amid lax testing policies until 2003. League-wide home runs per game rose from an average of 0.84 in 1993 to 1.22 in 1999, correlating with empirical evidence of PED prevalence, as anonymous surveys in 2003 revealed over 5% positive tests, prompting mandatory random testing under the Joint Drug Prevention and Treatment Program. Rare offensive achievements persisted, such as Fernando Tatis hitting two grand slams in the same inning on April 23, 1999. The era's visibility peaked with the 1998 home run chase, where Mark McGwire hit 70 home runs to surpass Roger Maris's 1961 record of 61 on September 8, 1998, with Sammy Sosa finishing at 66; this rivalry drew record attendance and television ratings, credited with revitalizing interest post-strike, as MLB attendance surged 22% in 1998 to over 70 million. Durability feats like Cal Ripken Jr.'s record 2,632 consecutive games played from 1982 to 1998, breaking Lou Gehrig's mark in 1995, contrasted with the era's power emphasis.
Subsequent scandals underscored the era's scope, including the 2003 BALCO investigation implicating players like Barry Bonds and Gary Sheffield in using designer steroids, and the December 13, 2007, Mitchell Report, a 409-page independent inquiry commissioned by Commissioner Bud Selig, which identified 89 current and former players linked to PEDs through supplier records, including McGwire, Rafael Palmeiro, Roger Clemens, and Alex Rodriguez. The report highlighted systemic failures in oversight, noting clubhouse trainers and strength coaches as key distributors, and recommended enhanced testing, including human growth hormone protocols implemented in 2011; Palmeiro, for instance, tested positive in 2005 after denying use under oath to Congress, resulting in a 10-game suspension. While PEDs demonstrably enhanced muscle mass and recovery, enabling sustained power outputevidenced by McGwire's admitted use contributing to his 583 career home runstheir health risks, including liver damage and hormonal imbalances, and erosion of competitive integrity tainted records, with no player from the era inducted into the Hall of Fame on first ballot as of 2025 due to voter scrutiny of statistical authenticity.

Modern Developments and Rule Innovations (2010s-Present)

In the 2010s, Major League Baseball deepened its integration of advanced analytics and tracking technology, with the introduction of Statcast in 2015 providing real-time data on player movements, pitch velocities, and batted ball trajectories across all 30 ballparks. This system, powered by high-speed cameras and radar, enabled teams to quantify defensive positioning, exit velocities, and launch angles, shifting strategies toward data-driven decisions that prioritized metrics like outs above average and expected batting average over traditional scouting. The adoption accelerated player evaluation, contributing to higher strikeout rates and home run totals as teams optimized lineups for power hitting, though it also highlighted inefficiencies in traditional play styles.
Facing concerns over lengthening game timesaveraging over three hours by the late 2010sand declining viewership among younger audiences, MLB implemented targeted rule innovations starting in the early 2020s to enhance pace of play and action. The 2023 collective bargaining agreement introduced a pitch clock limiting pitchers to 15 seconds with bases empty or 20 seconds with runners on, alongside restrictions on defensive shifts requiring at least two infielders on each side of second base and all on the dirt, larger bases (from 15 to 18 inches square), and a cap of two pitcher disengagements per plate appearance for pickoff attempts. These changes, tested in the minors since 2021, reduced average game length to 2 hours and 40 minutes in 2023, a drop of about 30 minutes from prior seasons, while increasing stolen bases by 41% and modestly boosting batting averages on balls in play for left-handed hitters by nine points.
Additional innovations addressed player safety and equipment use, including stricter enforcement of foreign substance bans on pitchers in 2021, which curbed spin rates on pitches, and the universal designated hitter rule in 2022, eliminating the American League-National League split to streamline rosters and reduce pitcher injuries from batting. The 2020 season, shortened to 60 games due to the COVID-19 pandemic, featured an expanded playoff format with 16 teams, a structure retained afterward to heighten postseason stakes amid revenue pressures. By 2025, minor adjustments closed shift loopholes, increasing penalties for violationssuch as awarding first base to the batter if a shifted fielder first touches a ground ballaiming to further deter overshifting without overhauling core mechanics.
These reforms, while boosting attendance and engagement metrics in initial years, sparked debates on unintended effects like elevated pitcher workloads under the pitch clock, though empirical analyses indicated no significant injury uptick and even potential decreases in certain ulnar collateral ligament cases. Overall, the era marked a departure from baseball's century-long stasis, prioritizing empirical adjustments to sustain competitiveness against faster-paced entertainment, with Statcast data informing ongoing tweaks to balance offense, defense, and viewer retention.

Gameplay and Rules

Core Objective and Field Dimensions

The core objective of a Major League Baseball game is for one of two competing teams, each consisting of nine players, to score more runs than the opponent over nine innings to secure victory. A run scores when an offensive player starts at home plate, advances counterclockwise around the basesfirst, second, and thirdand returns to home plate without being put out, typically after hitting a pitched ball into fair territory. Each half-inning allows the offensive team up to three outs before teams switch roles, with the home team batting last in the ninth if tied; extra innings extend play until a winner emerges.
The playing field centers on a 90-foot square infield diamond, with bases at each corner: home plate at one vertex and first, second, and third bases at the others, all 90 feet apart along the base paths. Home plate is a pentagonal rubber slab measuring 17 inches across the back edge, 8.5 inches along the sides, and 12 inches on the front point, embedded flush with the ground. The first and third bases are 15-by-15-inch canvas bags placed flush with the ground, while second base is similar but whitened for visibility; all bases sit 3 to 5 inches above the soil.
The pitcher's mound rises 10 inches above home plate level, forming an 18-foot-diameter circle with gradual slopes; at its center, the pitcher's rubbera white slab 24 inches long by 6 inches wideis positioned exactly 60 feet, 6 inches from the rear point of home plate. This distance, standardized since 1893, balances pitcher velocity against batter reaction time based on empirical testing of the era's throwing capabilities.
Outlining the infield, foul lines extend from home plate at 90-degree angles, defining fair territory; the outfield fence lies beyond, with MLB requiring minimum distances of 325 feet from home along the foul lines to the poles and 400 feet to dead center, though park-specific variationssuch as shorter porches or irregular wallspersist to accommodate urban sites and historical designs. The overall field surface combines clay for the infield (with a baseline 3 feet wide) and grass for the outfield, ensuring consistent play under official specifications enforced across all 30 MLB ballparks.

Equipment and Player Positions

The baseball used in Major League Baseball (MLB) consists of a cork, rubber, or similar core wound with yarn and covered by two strips of white horsehide or cowhide stitched together with red thread, measuring between 9 and 9.25 inches in circumference and weighing 5 to 5.25 ounces. Prior to use, each baseball is rubbed with Lena Blackburne Baseball Rubbing Mud sourced from a secret location along the Delaware River in New Jersey to dull the shine and improve grip. A typical MLB baseball lasts only 6-7 pitches before being replaced due to scuffs, dirt, or other factors. The bat is a smooth, round wooden stick no more than 42 inches long and 2.61 inches in diameter at its thickest point, typically made from ash, maple, or birch, with no metal or laminate allowed in professional play. 
Fielding gloves and mitts are regulated by position: the catcher's mitt may not exceed 36 inches in circumference or 15.5 inches from top to bottom; the first baseman's glove or mitt is limited to 12 inches in length and 8 inches in width across the palm; infielders' and outfielders' gloves cannot exceed 12 inches in length or 8 inches in width. Bases are 15-inch square white canvas bags filled with soft material, placed 90 feet apart, with the pitcher's plate a 17-by-8-inch slab of white rubber embedded in the mound. Uniforms include matching jerseys and pants with numbers on the back (except pitchers and catchers), and protective equipment such as batting helmetsmandatory for batters and base runners since 1983and catcher's gear including masks, chest protectors, and shin guards are required for safety. 
MLB teams field nine defensive players across specialized positions, each with defined responsibilities centered on preventing runs by fielding batted balls, throwing out runners, and executing force plays. The pitcher (position 1) delivers the ball from the mound to the catcher, aiming to induce swings or strikes while fielding bunts or come-backers and covering first base on grounders. The catcher (position 2) squats behind home plate to receive pitches, block wild pitches or passed balls, throw out base stealerstypically targeting second baseand call defensive strategies based on batter tendencies.
Infield positions include the first baseman (position 3), who stretches for throws to record outs on grounders and holds runners close to the bag; the second baseman (position 4), who turns double plays with the shortstop, covers second on steals, and fields balls to the right side; the shortstop (position 5), often the defensive captain with the widest range, handling balls up the middle, starting double plays, and backing up third base; and the third baseman (position 6), who charges bunts or slow rollers and fields hard-hit balls down the line. Outfielders comprise the left fielder (position 7), who covers the largest area and throws to third or cutoff men; the center fielder (position 8), the fastest with commanding range over the deepest territory, directing shifts and backing infielders; and the right fielder (position 9), who possesses the strongest arm for long throws to third or home while fielding fly balls to that side. Since the adoption of the universal designated hitter (DH) rule in 2022, the pitcher no longer bats in regular season games, with the DH filling that offensive role exclusively.

Key Rules and Evolving Mechanics

A standard Major League Baseball game consists of nine innings, with each inning divided into two halves: the top half for the visiting team at bat and the bottom half for the home team. The team on offense continues batting until three outs occur, after which the sides switch; the home team wins if leading after the top of the ninth, or the game extends to extra innings if tied. Runs score when a batter or runner advances and touches all four bases in order, with home plate crossing constituting a run; ties are resolved by the team with more total runs at game's end.
The pitcher delivers the ball to the catcher, aiming to induce swings or misses within the strike zonea rectangular area over home plate from the midpoint between shoulders and belt to the hollow beneath the kneecap, as defined for the batter. A pitch is a strike if not swung at and in the zone, swung at and missed, or foul-tipped into the catcher's glove; three strikes result in an out via strikeout, except a foul ball with two strikes does not count as the third unless bunted or foul-tipped. Four balls outside the zone force a walk, advancing the batter to first base; hits occur on fair balls not caught, allowing the batter to reach base safely based on the hit's distance and fielding. Outs also arise from caught fly balls, force plays where runners must advance but are thrown out at the next base, or tags on runners off base by a fielder with the ball.
Umpires enforce rules, with a crew chief and base umpires calling balls, strikes, safe/out, and fair/foul; disputes over certain calls trigger reviews under the replay system. Interference, obstruction, or balks (illegal pitcher motions with runners on base) penalize the defense, advancing runners or nullifying plays.
Major League Baseball has periodically modified rules to adapt to strategic evolutions, officiating accuracy, and pace of play. The designated hitter (DH) rule, allowing a non-fielding batter to hit for the pitcher, was adopted by the American League on January 11, 1973, to boost offense and protect pitchers from injury, while the National League retained pitcher batting until universal adoption in the 2022 collective bargaining agreement. Limited instant replay for home run calls began August 28, 2008, expanding January 16, 2014, to 13 play types including force outs, tags, and catch/no-catch via manager challenges (one per game, plus one if successful, unlimited after sixth inning if correct).
In response to lengthening game times and declining action, 2023 introduced a pitch timer mandating delivery within 15 seconds (bases empty) or 20 seconds (runners on), with violations counting as balls or strikes; defensive shift restrictions requiring two infielders on each side of second base with feet in dirt; 18-inch square bases (up from 15 inches); and pickoff attempt limits tied to disengagements (two per plate appearance, three with successful returns). These changes, approved by the Joint Competition Committee, reduced average nine-inning game length by 24 minutes to 2 hours 40 minutes in 2023, increased stolen bases by 41% to 2,964, and raised batting average on balls in play by .017. For 2024, the timer shortened to 18 seconds with runners on, further trimming games by 3 minutes on average. The automated ball-strike (ABS) challenge system, tested in minors, was approved for full MLB implementation in 2026, allowing one team challenge per game reviewed centrally.

Organizational Structure

Governance and Commissioner's Office

Major League Baseball (MLB) functions as an unincorporated joint venture among its 30 member clubs, with ultimate governance authority vested in the club owners, who collectively make key decisions on league policies, expansion, and revenue sharing. This structure, distinct from public regulation, relies on private agreements among owners to maintain operational uniformity across the National League (NL) and American League (AL), which merged administrative functions in 2000 while preserving separate competitive identities. MLB's antitrust exemption, originating from the U.S. Supreme Court's 1922 ruling in Federal Baseball Club v. National Leaguewhich deemed professional baseball exhibitions not interstate commerce subject to the Sherman Antitrust Actand reaffirmed in 1953's Toolson v. New York Yankees, enables this owner-driven model by shielding league practices like territorial restrictions and the reserve system from federal antitrust scrutiny, fostering a de facto monopoly on major professional baseball in the U.S. and Canada.
The Commissioner's Office, established on January 21, 1921, emerged as the league's central executive authority following the 1919 Black Sox Scandal, where eight Chicago White Sox players conspired to fix the World Series, eroding public trust and necessitating an independent overseer to enforce integrity. Kenesaw Mountain Landis, a federal judge, was appointed as the inaugural commissioner with sweeping powers under a new Major League Agreement, including the ability to investigate, discipline players and clubs, and act unilaterally "in the best interests of baseball," which he exercised by banning the implicated players for life despite their acquittal in court. This office replaced the prior National Commission, a tripartite body of league presidents and a club owner that had proven ineffective amid AL-NL rivalries. Over subsequent decades, commissioners like Happy Chandler (19451951), who approved Jackie Robinson's integration, and Ford Frick (19511965) expanded the role to include labor negotiations and broadcasting deals, though powers gradually shifted toward owner consensus, particularly after the 1970s rise of player unions challenged unilateral authority.
The commissioner's core responsibilities encompass administering on-field rules, resolving disputes between clubs or with the Major League Baseball Players Association (MLBPA), negotiating collective bargaining agreements, securing media rights (which generated $2.6 billion annually as of recent deals), and representing MLB in legal and congressional matters. Elected by a three-quarters vote of owners for a renewable term, the commissioner reports to the Executive Council of top club executives but retains influence over discipline, such as suspensions for performance-enhancing drugs or rule violations. Rob Manfred, the tenth commissioner, was elected on August 14, 2014, by the 30 clubs and assumed office on January 25, 2015, succeeding Bud Selig; his tenure has focused on digital media expansion, international growth, and rule changes like the pitch clock, amid ongoing tensions with the MLBPA over luxury taxes and revenue distribution. The office's deputy commissioners handle specialized areas, including baseball operations (e.g., umpiring and scheduling) and chief legal officer duties, supporting the commissioner's oversight of minor leagues and global initiatives.

League and Division Organization

Major League Baseball operates as a joint organizational entity comprising two separate leagues: the National League (NL), established in 1876 as the oldest professional baseball league, and the American League (AL), founded in 1901 following a period of rivalry and consolidation into the major leagues by 1903. The two leagues maintained distinct identities, including rule differences such as the AL's adoption of the designated hitter (DH) in 1973, while sharing a commissioner and centralized governance under MLB since 1920. This dual-league structure persisted until formal integration under MLB's umbrella, with both leagues retaining competitive separation for regular-season play until the expansion of interleague games.
Each league is divided into three geographic divisionsEast, Central, and Westcomprising five teams per division for a total of 15 teams per league and 30 overall, a configuration established in 1998 after the addition of the Arizona Diamondbacks (NL West) and Tampa Bay Devil Rays (AL East). Divisions were initially introduced in 1969 to manage scheduling and playoffs amid expansion to 12 teams per league, with subsequent realignments in 1994 (adding Central divisions) and 2013 (realigning the Houston Astros from NL Central to AL West to balance the leagues at 15 teams each). This divisional setup determines primary scheduling, with teams playing the majority of games (approximately 52 of 162 in a season) against division rivals, fostering regional rivalries and competitive balance through concentrated matchups.

Labor Relations and Player Association

The Major League Baseball Players Association (MLBPA) originated from earlier, unsuccessful attempts at player organization, such as the Brotherhood of Professional Base Ball Players formed in 1885 and the Baseball Players Fraternity established in 1912, which dissolved by 1918. A more formal MLBPA was founded on December 11, 1956, amid concerns over inadequate pensions, but it remained weak until the hiring of economist Marvin Miller as its first full-time executive director on March 5, 1966. Under Miller's leadership from 1966 to 1982, the union professionalized operations, secured the first collective bargaining agreement (CBA) in 1968which raised the minimum salary to $10,000 and improved pensionsand challenged the reserve clause that had bound players to single teams indefinitely. 
A pivotal victory came on December 23, 1975, when arbitrator Peter Seitz ruled in the Messersmith-McNally case that pitchers Andy Messersmith and Dave McNally, who had played 1975 without signing a contract after their prior deals expired, were free agents, effectively dismantling the perpetual reserve system. This decision established the framework for modern free agency, granting players with six years of service time the right to negotiate with any team starting in 1976, which dramatically increased salariesaverage player pay rose from about $45,000 in 1975 to over $180,000 by 1985. Miller's tenure also navigated multiple work stoppages, including the players' first strike from April 1 to 13, 1972 (13 days, over pension funding), a 17-day owners' lockout in 1973, and a brief 1976 lockout, establishing the union's resolve against owner-imposed restrictions.
Subsequent leaders like Donald Fehr (19832009) oversaw further disputes, including a 50-day strike in 1981 that split the season and awarded extra playoff spots, a 32-day lockout in 1990, and the protracted 199495 strike lasting 232 days from August 12, 1994, to April 2, 1995, which canceled 948 regular-season games and the World Series amid owner demands for a salary cap, ultimately rejected by federal mediation. The most recent major stoppage was the 202122 lockout from December 2, 2021, to March 10, 2022 (99 days), resolving with a new CBA that expanded playoffs, raised minimum salaries to $740,000 in 2022, introduced a 75th service day for arbitration eligibility, and maintained the competitive balance tax over a hard salary cap. 
The current CBA, effective from March 10, 2022, to December 1, 2026, governs terms like revenue sharing (31% of local revenues to small-market clubs), international amateur signing bonuses capped by pool, and player benefits including medical care and licensing revenues, without a salary floor or cap, prioritizing competitive balance through luxury taxes on high-spending teams. Led since 2013 by executive director Tony Clark, the MLBPA represents over 1,200 current players and retirees, focusing on issues like service time manipulationwhere teams delay promotions to control arbitration costsand draft lottery reforms to deter tanking, while preparing for 2026 negotiations where owners may push for a salary cap and international draft amid rising revenues exceeding $11 billion annually. These dynamics reflect a balance where player mobility and earnings have grownaverage salaries topped $4.5 million in 2023but tensions persist over revenue distribution and roster control, with the union's decentralized structure empowering player reps in grievances.

Teams and Franchises

Current Teams and Rosters

Major League Baseball comprises 30 franchises, evenly split between the American League (AL) and National League (NL), with each league organized into three divisions: East, Central, and West. This structure, established in 1994 with the addition of divisions and realignment, facilitates balanced competition and playoff qualification, where division winners and wild cards advance. The teams represent 16 U.S. states, the District of Columbia, and one Canadian province, with no expansions or contractions since 1998.
The AL East includes the Baltimore Orioles, Boston Red Sox, New York Yankees, Tampa Bay Rays, and Toronto Blue Jays. The AL Central consists of the Chicago White Sox, Cleveland Guardians, Detroit Tigers, Kansas City Royals, and Minnesota Twins. The AL West features the Houston Astros, Los Angeles Angels, Oakland Athletics, Seattle Mariners, and Texas Rangers.
In the NL East are the Atlanta Braves, Miami Marlins, New York Mets, Philadelphia Phillies, and Washington Nationals. The NL Central encompasses the Chicago Cubs, Cincinnati Reds, Milwaukee Brewers, Pittsburgh Pirates, and St. Louis Cardinals. The NL West includes the Arizona Diamondbacks, Colorado Rockies, Los Angeles Dodgers, San Diego Padres, and San Francisco Giants.
Each franchise maintains a 40-man roster, which protects players from the Rule 5 Draft and serves as the pool for the active roster. The active roster stands at 26 players from Opening Day through August 31, requiring a minimum of 25, with every player designated as either a pitcher or position player; pitcher limits cap at 13 from Opening Day to September 14 (14 thereafter until September 30). This expands to 28 players starting September 1 through the regular season's end, allowing greater flexibility for call-ups. Postseason rosters revert to 26, with eligibility restricted to players on the 40-man or 60-day injured list by September 1 at noon ET.
Typical active roster composition includes 12-13 pitchers (5 starters, 7-8 relievers) and 13-14 position players (2 catchers, 6-7 infielders, 4-5 outfielders, and a designated hitter in AL or universal DH rules). Teams also utilize taxi squads or injured lists for depth, with the 10-day injured list for most players and 15-day for pitchers, enabling strategic management amid the 162-game schedule.

Franchise Histories and Relocations

The franchise histories of Major League Baseball teams trace back to the establishment of the National League in 1876 and the American League in 1901, with many current clubs maintaining continuity from those eras through mergers, absorptions, and relocations rather than outright dissolutions. Of the 30 active franchises as of 2025, seven originated in the National League before 1900 (Atlanta Braves from 1871, Chicago Cubs from 1876, Cincinnati Reds from 1882, Philadelphia Phillies from 1883, Pittsburgh Pirates from 1882, St. Louis Cardinals from 1882, and Los Angeles Dodgers from 1884), while most American League teams claim 1901 origins, often with predecessor minor-league or earlier iterations absorbed for historical lineage. These histories reflect baseball's adaptation to demographic shifts, economic pressures, and infrastructure demands, with franchises relocating to secure financial viability amid declining attendance in older eastern markets and rising opportunities in growing Sun Belt and West Coast cities post-World War II.
From 1903, following the National Agreement that stabilized the major leagues with 16 teams, no franchises relocated geographically for 50 years, as teams were anchored in established multi-franchise markets like New York, Boston, Chicago, Philadelphia, and St. Louis. This period ended in 1953 when the Boston Braves, hampered by competition from the Red Sox and poor attendance, moved to Milwaukee, drawing over 1.8 million fans in their debut season and validating the shift to a single-team market with a new stadium. The 1950s marked the onset of westward expansion, driven by television revenue potential and population migration; the St. Louis Browns relocated to Baltimore as the Orioles in 1954 after decades of financial losses, while the Philadelphia Athletics moved to Kansas City in 1955 before further shifting to Oakland in 1968 amid ongoing ownership instability under Charles Finley. 
The late 1950s accelerated relocations with the departure of New York's junior circuit teams: the Brooklyn Dodgers transferred to Los Angeles in 1958, playing initially at the Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum before Dodger Stadium opened in 1962, and the New York Giants followed suit to San Francisco that year, both moves approved by MLB owners on May 28, 1957, to tap California's burgeoning population and media markets despite fan backlash in the Northeast. 
Subsequent moves included the Milwaukee Braves to Atlanta in 1966, seeking escape from sagging attendance and a new publicly funded stadium; the Seattle Pilots, an expansion team that lasted one season in 1969, relocated to Milwaukee as the Brewers in 1970 after bankruptcy; and the second Washington Senators franchise (expanded in 1961 after the original moved to Minnesota as the Twins in 1961) shifted to Arlington, Texas, as the Rangers in 1972 following riots over announced departure and poor performance. The most recent relocation occurred in 2005 when the Montreal Expos, struggling with low attendance since their 1969 founding as the NL's first Canadian team, were effectively transferred to Washington, D.C., and rebranded as the Nationals after a period of league-controlled operation and contraction threats.

Season Structure

Preseason and Spring Training

Spring training constitutes the preseason phase of Major League Baseball, during which the 30 teams conduct workouts, intrasquad scrimmages, and exhibition games to ready players for the regular season. This period enables established athletes to regain competitive form after offseason conditioning, permits non-roster invitees and prospects to audition for major league spots, and facilitates managerial assessments of lineup configurations and pitching rotations.
The schedule commences with pitchers and catchers reporting to spring facilities in early to mid-February, followed by position players' arrival approximately one week later, leading to full-squad workouts. For the 2025 season, reporting dates varied by team, such as February 9 for certain Cactus League squads, with initial full-team practices by mid-February. Exhibition games, numbering around 30 to 35 per team against fellow MLB clubsand occasionally minor league or collegiate opponentsspan late February to late March, typically limited to nine innings without extra play, resulting in ties if unresolved. These contests prioritize development over outcomes, with starting pitchers often requiring five to six appearances to build arm strength for the 162-game slate.
Teams convene in two primary circuits: the Grapefruit League in Florida, hosting 15 franchises across venues from Port St. Lucie to Dunedin, and the Cactus League in Arizona, accommodating the other 15 in a compact area around Phoenix and Mesa. Florida's tradition traces to 1910, marking over 130 years of major league presence at 35 distinct sites, while Arizona's began in 1947 when the Cleveland Indians trained in Tucson, expanding post-World War II as an alternative to Florida's humidity. The league monikers derive from regional floragrapefruit for Florida's citrus heritage and cacti for Arizona's desert landscapereflecting geographic specialization that balances weather advantages and logistical efficiencies.
Since 2023, MLB has incorporated the Spring Breakout series, featuring 16 exhibition matchups of top prospects from each organization, scheduled in mid-March to spotlight emerging talent ahead of minor league assignments. Facilities like Sloan Park in Mesa or George M. Steinbrenner Field in Tampa offer modern amenities, including dedicated minor league complexes, underscoring the period's role in talent pipeline management.

Regular Season Scheduling

The Major League Baseball regular season features each of the 30 teams playing 162 games, resulting in a total of 2,430 contests across both leagues. This structure has been standard since 1961, when the schedule expanded from 154 games to accommodate the addition of new franchises and balance competitive demands. The season typically commences in late March or early April with Opening Day and concludes in late September, allowing for a six-month campaign that includes approximately 25-28 series per team, most consisting of three or four consecutive games against the same opponent. For the 2025 season, play began on March 27 and ended on September 28.
Since the adoption of a balanced schedule in 2023, each team competes in 52 intradivision games13 against each of the four other teams in their division, split across four series with seven home games and six away (or vice versa). An additional 64 games occur against intraleague, non-divisional opponents, with teams facing six or seven contests against each of the 10 other clubs in their league to ensure even distribution. Interleague play accounts for the remaining 46 games, with three matchups against each of the 15 teams from the opposing league, promoting broader competition while reducing geographic clustering from prior unbalanced formats that favored regional rivalries. Home and away games are roughly equal, with each team hosting 81 contests and visiting 81 others, though minor adjustments occur for international series or weather-related rescheduling.
Schedules are generated by MLB's central operations team using proprietary software algorithms that input constraints such as stadium availability, travel logistics, broadcast agreements, and competitive balance rules. Key optimizations minimize cross-country flightsprioritizing West Coast teams' sequencesand incorporate off-days (typically Mondays and Thursdays) to reduce fatigue, with doubleheaders scheduled only for rainouts or unavoidable conflicts. The full slate is released in advance, often by August of the prior year, enabling ticket sales and media planning, though adjustments for events like the All-Star break or international openers (e.g., Tokyo series) are integrated early. This computerized approach, refined over decades, ensures logistical feasibility across 29 U.S. and one Canadian venue while adhering to collective bargaining agreements on rest and travel.

Midseason Events and All-Star Game

The Major League Baseball All-Star Game, an annual exhibition matchup between top players from the American League (AL) and National League (NL), anchors the league's midseason festivities and provides a pause in the regular season schedule. Typically occurring in mid-July, the eventoften dubbed the "Midsummer Classic"draws millions of viewers and highlights individual talent amid the grind of 162 games per team. Rosters consist of 34 players per league, including starters selected primarily through fan balloting and reserves chosen by players, managers, and the commissioner's office.
Fan voting for starting position players (excluding pitchers) unfolds in two phases: an initial period from early June to late June, where supporters select up to five times daily across positions, advancing top vote-getters to a runoff finale with unlimited ballots; outfielders receive three slots per league, while other positions yield one starter each. The top overall vote recipient in each league earns the starting nod regardless of phase outcomes. Player balloting then fills nine additional reserves per league, with the commissioner's office adding five pitchers and up to eight injury replacements or previously snubbed standouts. Managers from the prior World Series teams helm the squads, enforcing a universal designated hitter rule and limiting most pitchers to one inning.
Unlike from 2003 to 2016, when the winning league gained World Series home-field advantage, the All-Star outcome carries no postseason weight today; regular-season records determine playoff seeding and Series hosting under the balanced schedule adopted in 2023. The Ted Williams Most Valuable Player Award, established in 2002 and named for the Hall of Famer, honors the game's standout performer, with winners often hailing from the host league.
All-Star Week encompasses supporting events to amplify fan engagement and prospect exposure. The T-Mobile Home Run Derby, contested the evening before the game in a bracketed format among eight power hitters across three timed rounds, has evolved since its 1985 inception into a high-profile spectacle emphasizing distance and volume. The All-Star Futures Game pits top minor-league prospects from AL and NL affiliates the day prior, shifting to this intraleague format in 2019 after years of U.S. versus international competition; the NL holds a 3-1-1 edge since the change. Additional showcases include the HBCU Swingman Classic for historically Black college players and celebrity softball exhibitions, all hosted at the All-Star venue to foster broader baseball interest.

Postseason Playoffs and World Series

The Major League Baseball postseason determines the annual champion through an elimination tournament featuring 12 teamssix from the American League (AL) and six from the National League (NL)expanded from the prior 10-team format in 2022 to increase competitive opportunities. Qualification includes the three division winners per league plus three wild card teams, selected as the non-division-winning clubs with the best regular-season records. The top two division winners in each league receive byes into the Division Series, while the third division winner hosts the Wild Card Series against the lowest-seeded wild card team in a best-of-three matchup; the higher-seeded wild cards host the other two best-of-three series. Tiebreakers for seeding prioritize head-to-head records, intradivision winning percentage, and interdivision records if necessary.
Advancing teams proceed to the Division Series (ALDS or NLDS), a best-of-five contest where higher seeds host the first two games and, if needed, Games 6 and 7. League Championship Series (ALCS and NLCS) follow as best-of-seven series, with the same home-field structure favoring the higher seed for Games 1, 2, 6, and 7. Postseason rosters are limited to 26 players, drawn from those on the 40-man roster or 60-day injured list by September 1, with no further additions allowed. This bracket-style format, locked in through the current collective bargaining agreement, emphasizes early-round intensity via shorter series while preserving extended play in later stages.
The World Series pits the AL and NL champions in a best-of-seven series, establishing the MLB world champion since its inception in 1903 as a contest between the league pennant winners. The format has used a 2-3-2 home-field arrangement since 1922, with the league of the team holding the better regular-season record (or All-Star Game winner pre-2017) hosting Games 1, 2, 6, and 7. Absent in 1904 due to the AL champion's refusal to participate and canceled in 1994 amid a players' strike, the series has otherwise occurred annually, awarding the Commissioner's Trophy to the victor since 1967. Notable expansions in playoff access, such as the 1969 introduction of divisional play and the 1995 wild card, have influenced World Series paths, enabling non-division winners like the 2023 Arizona Diamondbacks to reach the finals.

Economics and Business Model

Revenue Streams and Financial Health

Major League Baseball generated a record $12.1 billion in gross revenues during the 2024 season, marking a 4.3% increase from the $11.6 billion reported for 2023. This figure encompasses contributions from all 30 teams, with average revenue per team reaching approximately $403 million.
Primary revenue streams include gate receipts, which accounted for about 31% of total league income through ticket sales, concessions, and parking; central revenue distributions, comprising roughly 26% from national broadcasting deals, licensing, and revenue sharing; and local media rights, which vary significantly by market but remain a key source despite challenges in the regional sports network (RSN) model. Sponsorships, merchandise sales (often shared centrally), and ballpark naming rights further bolster incomes, with national media contracts from networks like ESPN, Fox, and TBS providing stable, multi-billion-dollar inflows over multi-year terms. Larger-market teams, such as the Los Angeles Dodgers and New York Yankees, derive disproportionate shares from high-value local TV deals and premium ticketing, while smaller-market franchises rely more heavily on revenue sharing, which redistributes approximately 48% of certain local revenues league-wide.
Financially, MLB exhibits robust health, with average team valuations climbing to $2.6 billion in 2025, an 8% rise from the prior year, driven by escalating media rights and attendance recoveries post-pandemic. Operating incomes remain positive for most franchises, though disparities persist: high-revenue teams like the Boston Red Sox reported $574 million in 2024 revenue alongside substantial profits, while lower-revenue clubs benefit from sharing mechanisms to sustain competitiveness. Uncertainty in RSN viability poses risks, as declining linear TV viewership could pressure local media streams, yet overall league profitability and franchise appreciation indicate resilience absent major disruptions.

Player Salaries, Contracts, and Luxury Tax

Player contracts in Major League Baseball are governed by the collective bargaining agreement (CBA) between MLB and the Major League Baseball Players Association (MLBPA), which outlines service time requirements for salary negotiation rights. Players with fewer than three years of Major League service time are typically paid the league minimum salary and have limited bargaining power, as they are subject to team control without arbitration or free agency eligibility. The 2022-2026 CBA sets the minimum salary at $760,000 for 2025, up from $740,000 in 2024, with annual increases to promote competitive balance while rewarding longevity.
Players with three to five years of service become eligible for salary arbitration, where they and their team exchange salary figures, and an arbitrator selects one without compromise, based on comparable player performances and market data. This process often results in one-year contracts, with salaries rising significantly for strong performers; for instance, arbitration-eligible players can see raises of 40-80% depending on metrics like Wins Above Replacement (WAR). After six years of service, players reach unrestricted free agency, allowing them to negotiate multi-year contracts with any team, often yielding the largest deals driven by bidding wars among high-revenue clubs. Notable 2025 contracts include Juan Soto's 15-year, $765 million extension with the New York Mets, featuring a $51 million average annual value (AAV), and Shohei Ohtani's 10-year, $700 million deal with the Los Angeles Dodgers, deferred to minimize present-day payroll impact.
The average MLB player salary reached $5,160,245 in 2025, a 3.6% increase from 2024, reflecting overall revenue growth from media rights and ticket sales, though median salaries dipped slightly to around $1.35 million due to the skew from superstar contracts. High earners dominate payrolls, with the top 10 players accounting for over $576 million combined; Soto led in cash earnings at $128.9 million, bolstered by signing bonuses, while Ohtani's AAV topped $70 million despite deferrals.
In lieu of a hard salary cap, MLB imposes the Competitive Balance Tax (CBT), commonly called the luxury tax, on teams exceeding an annual threshold to deter excessive spending and redistribute funds to lower-revenue clubs. The 2025 threshold was $241 million in total player obligations, including AAVs, benefits, and minor league contracts, with penalties escalating by offense level: 20% for first-time exceeders, 30% for second consecutive years, and up to 50% or more for repeat offenders, plus surcharges for amounts over $20 million or $40 million above the threshold. Funds collected$311 million league-wide in 2024, with three teams (Dodgers, Mets, Yankees) paying 84%are shared 50% to players via bonuses, 25% to low-revenue teams, and 25% to qualifying clubs for draft or international signing aid.
Team payrolls in 2025 highlighted disparities, with the Dodgers leading at $350 million, followed by the Mets ($342 million) and Yankees ($300 million), while small-market teams like the Oakland Athletics operated under $100 million. The system, unchanged fundamentally since the 1997 introduction and refined in the 2022 CBA, aims to maintain parity without stifling big-market investments, though critics argue it fails to curb spending by revenue-rich franchises, as evidenced by nine teams taxed in 2024.

Revenue Sharing, Disparities, and Antitrust Exemption

Major League Baseball operates a revenue sharing system established through its collective bargaining agreements (CBAs) with the Major League Baseball Players Association (MLBPA), under which each of the 30 teams contributes 48% of its net local revenuesprimarily from ticket sales, concessions, local broadcasting, and sponsorships, excluding stadium debt service and certain other expensesinto a central pool that is then distributed equally among all clubs. This mechanism, formalized in the 1990 CBA and expanded in subsequent agreements including the 2022-2026 CBA, supplements equal sharing of national revenues such as central league broadcasting deals, which totaled approximately $2.8 billion annually from ESPN, Fox, and Turner contracts as of 2023. The system aims to mitigate financial imbalances by transferring funds from high-revenue clubs in large markets, like the New York Yankees and Los Angeles Dodgers, to lower-revenue teams in smaller markets, such as the Oakland Athletics or Tampa Bay Rays, with distributions often exceeding $200 million per small-market team in recent years.
Despite this redistribution, significant economic disparities persist, driven by inherent differences in local market sizes, stadium capacities, and regional advertising potential, which enable big-market teams to retain substantial net revenues after sharing obligations. For instance, in the 2023 season, the top six highest-payroll teams averaged 91 wins over the prior five years, compared to lower figures for bottom-spending clubs, with payroll gaps reaching historic levels: the New York Mets' 2023 payroll exceeded $350 million while small-market teams like the Cleveland Guardians operated below $100 million. Luxury tax penalties, which redirect overages from high spenders to a pool partly funding revenue sharing, impose costssuch as the Dodgers' estimated $150.7 million tax bill entering 2025but do not fully equalize competitive spending power, as small-market owners cite revenue constraints in justifying restrained payrolls despite inflows. Critics argue the model entrenches inequality, as high-revenue teams like the Dodgers and Mets leverage private equity and local media deals to sustain dominance, while small-market clubs face structural barriers to consistent contention, evidenced by only occasional World Series success from teams like the 2023 Texas Rangers amid broader trends favoring metropolitan franchises. 
MLB's antitrust exemption, originating from the U.S. Supreme Court's 1922 ruling in Federal Baseball Club v. National League that professional baseball exhibitions did not constitute interstate commerce under the Sherman Antitrust Act, has enabled the league to implement revenue sharing and territorial restrictions without federal challenge, distinguishing it from other sports leagues subject to antitrust scrutiny. The decision was reaffirmed in Toolson v. New York Yankees (1953), where the Court deferred to congressional inaction, and in Flood v. Kuhn (1972), which upheld the exemption as an anomaly while acknowledging its role in preserving baseball's structure, including the reserve clause and minor league controls. This judicial shield allows the commissioner broad authority to enforce uniform economic policies, such as mandatory revenue pooling and franchise relocation approvals, preventing rival leagues or unchecked expansion that could dilute shared revenues, though it has drawn criticism for insulating MLB from competition and enabling owner collusion on player contracts pre-free agency era. The exemption's scope narrowed post-1972 via the Curt Flood Act of 1998, which extended some protections to players for employment terms but preserved league-wide antitrust immunity for core operations like broadcasting and minor league affiliations.

Controversies and Scandals

Gambling and Betting Violations

Major League Baseball has enforced strict prohibitions against gambling since its early years to preserve game integrity, with Rule 21 of the league's official rules stipulating permanent ineligibility for any player, umpire, or official who bets on a baseball game in which they have a direct or indirect interest, or who knowingly associates with gamblers influencing outcomes. Violations carry severe penalties, including lifetime bans, reflecting the league's historical recognition that betting undermines competitive fairness.
The most infamous incident occurred during the 1919 World Series, when eight Chicago White Sox players, including "Shoeless" Joe Jackson, conspired with gamblers to intentionally lose games against the Cincinnati Reds, motivated by low salaries and dissatisfaction with owner Charles Comiskey. Despite acquittal in a 1921 trial due to lack of evidence and witness intimidation, newly appointed Commissioner Kenesaw Mountain Landis banned all eight players for life on August 3, 1921, to deter future corruption and restore public trust eroded by the scandal. This event prompted MLB's formal adoption of anti-gambling rules and lifetime bans as standard punishment.
In the modern era, Cincinnati Reds player-manager Pete Rose faced investigation in 1989 after evidence emerged of his extensive gambling, including bets on 52 Reds games during the 1987 season totaling tens of thousands of dollars, often through bookmaker Ron Peters. On August 23, 1989, Rose accepted a lifetime ban from Commissioner A. Bartlett Giamatti, denying wrongdoing but agreeing to the penalty to avoid further scrutiny, which barred him from Hall of Fame eligibility until a policy change in 2020 allowed ineligible players' plaques without altering bans.
Following the 2018 U.S. Supreme Court decision legalizing sports betting in many states, MLB partnered with betting operators like DraftKings and FanDuel while tightening enforcement, permitting players to bet on non-baseball sports via legal channels but prohibiting all baseball wagers, including minors or international games. In June 2024, the league issued its first lifetime ban since Rose to San Diego Padres infielder Tucupita Marcano, who placed 387 bets totaling over $150,000 on MLB games from October 2022 to October 2023, including 25 on Pittsburgh Pirates games while on their injured list; four othersOakland Athletics pitcher Michael Kelly and minor leaguers Jay Groome, Jos Rodrguez, and Andrew Saalfrankreceived one-year suspensions for similar violations involving hundreds of baseball bets. MLB also disciplined umpire Pat Hoberg for undisclosed gambling activity impeding an investigation, though details remained confidential pending appeal.
On May 13, 2025, MLB reinstated Pete Rose and the seven other Black Sox players, including Joe Jackson, allowing Hall of Fame consideration but not reversing historical bans or implying innocence, a decision Commissioner Rob Manfred described as addressing long-standing fan debates without condoning the acts. These cases underscore MLB's ongoing vigilance, with the league monitoring legal betting's expansion amid concerns over accessibility via mobile apps increasing violation risks for participants.

Performance-Enhancing Drugs and Testing

The use of performance-enhancing drugs (PEDs), particularly anabolic steroids, proliferated in Major League Baseball during the 1990s and early 2000s, coinciding with a surge in offensive statistics such as home runs and batting averages. This period, often termed the "steroids era," saw single-season home run records broken, including Mark McGwire's 70 in 1998 and Barry Bonds' 73 in 2001, amid widespread allegations of PED involvement among players. Empirical data from the era indicate that league-wide home run rates peaked at 1.17 per game in 2000, compared to historical averages below 1.0, correlating with self-reported PED use in player surveys and later admissions.
In response to growing concerns, MLB and the Major League Baseball Players Association (MLBPA) implemented anonymous survey testing in 2003 under the Collective Bargaining Agreement (CBA), revealing positive tests for steroids in 5-7% of samples, which triggered mandatory random testing starting in 2004 with first-time penalties of 10-15 days suspension. Following congressional hearings in 2005 prompted by Jos Canseco's book Juiced, the policy was strengthened to include year-round unannounced urine testing for steroids, amphetamines, and other substances, with suspensions escalating to 50 games for a first offense. The first suspension under the new regimen occurred on April 3, 2005, when outfielder Alex Sanchez received a 10-day ban.
The 2007 Mitchell Report, commissioned by Commissioner Bud Selig and authored by former Senator George Mitchell, documented PED use by 89 current and former players, implicating trainers, executives, and clubs in distribution networks, and recommended enhanced testing protocols including blood tests for human growth hormone (HGH). HGH blood testing was introduced in 2011, following negotiations with the MLBPA, as prior urine tests proved ineffective against the substance. Notable suspensions followed, including Rafael Palmeiro's 10-day ban on August 1, 2005, after a positive test despite his congressional testimony denying use; Manny Ramirez's 50-game suspension in 2009 and 100-game in 2011; and Alex Rodriguez's 211-game penalty (later reduced to 162) in 2013 tied to the Biogenesis clinic scandal, which ensnared over a dozen players in a Miami-based PED supply operation.
Under the current Joint Drug Prevention and Treatment Program, established in the 2011 CBA and updated periodically, players face random urine and blood testingup to three unannounced urine collections per player annually, plus HGH blood testswith penalties of 80 games for a first violation, 162 for a second, and lifetime ban for a third. In 2023, MLB conducted a record 11,783 tests with only one positive result leading to discipline, while 2024 results from 9,455 urine samples and additional blood tests showed no specified positives for PEDs, indicating a marked decline in detected use. Despite these low rates, critics argue that advanced masking agents and undetectable PEDs may evade detection, though statistical normalization of power hitting post-2000home runs per game falling to 1.1 by 2023supports reduced prevalence driven by deterrence. The policy's rigor, including chain-of-custody protocols and independent arbitration, has been credited with restoring integrity, though ongoing vigilance is required given historical circumvention attempts.

Cheating Incidents and Rule Infractions

Cheating incidents in Major League Baseball have primarily involved violations such as sign stealing, doctoring baseballs with foreign substances, and using altered equipment like corked bats, distinct from gambling or performance-enhancing drugs. These infractions undermine the game's integrity by providing unfair advantages through technology, substances, or modifications that alter pitch movement, grip, or ball exit velocity. MLB rules, including Rule 6.03 prohibiting doctored balls and Rule 3.01 banning electronic sign decoding, have evolved to address such tactics, though enforcement has varied historically.
One of the most prominent modern scandals occurred with the Houston Astros in 2017 and 2018, where the team illegally decoded opponents' signs using a center-field camera feed piped to a clubhouse monitor near the dugout. Players then relayed stolen pitches via banging trash cansloud bangs signaling fastballs, silence for off-speed pitchesgiving batters advance knowledge during the regular season and playoffs, including the Astros' 2017 World Series victory over the Los Angeles Dodgers. MLB's investigation, prompted by whistleblower complaints, confirmed the scheme violated replay rules against real-time video decoding; it resulted in one-year suspensions for manager A.J. Hinch and general manager Jeff Luhnow (both fired shortly after), a $5 million fine, and loss of 2020 draft picks, but no player penalties or vacated titles due to commissioner Rob Manfred's deference to the commissioner's office precedent.
Doctoring baseballs with foreign substances to enhance grip and movement has a long history, predating the 1920 ban on the spitball following Ray Chapman's fatal 1920 beaning, though 17 grandfathered pitchers continued using it. Hall of Famer Gaylord Perry epitomized this tactic across a 22-year career (19621983), employing saliva, Vaseline, or petroleum jelly on the ball or glove to create unpredictable movement, winning Cy Young Awards in both leagues (1972 AL, 1978 NL) despite frequent suspicions; he was ejected only once, on August 23, 1982, at age 43 with the Seattle Mariners, after umpires found residue during a game against the Boston Red Sox. Perry's autobiography detailed psyching out batters by feigning the pitch, contributing to 314 wins, though critics argue lax enforcement enabled such longevity.
In 2021, MLB cracked down on "sticky stuff"suntan lotion, rosin mixtures, or Spider Tack applied by pitchers to increase spin rates amid rising velocities and strikeoutsissuing a June 15 memo enhancing Rule 6.02(c) enforcement with random checks and 10-day paid suspensions for violations. Umpires ejected high-profile pitchers like Trevor Bauer (June 15 vs. Angels) and Max Scherzer (August 6 vs. Tigers), who admitted using rosin excessively; the policy reduced league-wide spin rates by about 50 RPM initially but faced pushback from pitchers claiming safety risks on slick balls. Enforcement has since relaxed, with fewer ejections, though the rule remains.
Altered bats, such as those filled with cork, rubber, or superballs to boost distance (up to 20 feet per physics models), violate Rule 6.03(a)(5) but are rarely detected unless shattering occurs, with only about six confirmed cases since 1920. Notable incidents include Sammy Sosa's June 3, 2003, ejection with the Chicago Cubs after his bat splintered revealing cork against the Tampa Bay Devil Rays; Sosa claimed it was a batting practice mistake, receiving an eight-game suspension despite no prior tainted bats among his 76 checked. In 1994, Cleveland Indians' Albert Belle's bat was swapped post-game with a corked one belonging to teammate Paul Sorrento, discovered by umpires and leading to Belle's three-game suspension. Chris Sabo was ejected July 29, 1996, with the Cincinnati Reds after his bat broke, exposing cork.
The 1983 "Pine Tar Incident" highlighted equipment rule disputes when Kansas City Royals' George Brett's July 24 home run off Goose Gossage was nullified for excessive pine tar (33 inches vs. 18-inch limit under Rule 1.10(c)), aiding grip but deemed excessive; Brett's furious dugout charge became iconic, but AL president Lee MacPhail upheld the Royals' protest on July 28, reinstating the homer and resuming the game August 18 (Royals won 5-4). The rule was later relaxed to focus on performance alteration rather than mere excess.

Labor Disputes, Strikes, and Ownership Conflicts

The Major League Baseball Players Association (MLBPA), formed in 1966 under executive director Marvin Miller, marked the beginning of organized labor resistance against longstanding owner control mechanisms, including the reserve clause that indefinitely bound players to their teams. In 1968, the MLBPA negotiated the first collective bargaining agreement (CBA) in professional sports, raising the minimum salary from $6,000 to $10,000 and establishing basic grievance procedures. Challenges to the reserve clause intensified with Curt Flood's 1970 lawsuit, which, though unsuccessful, highlighted its antitrust issues; the clause was ultimately nullified in December 1975 by arbitrator Peter Seitz's ruling in the Messersmith-McNally case, granting players free agency after six years of service and dramatically increasing bargaining power. 
The first MLB players' strike occurred from April 1 to April 13, 1972, lasting 13 days and canceling 86 games over disputes regarding owner contributions to the pension fund and salary arbitration rights; players won modest increases in benefits upon resumption. A more disruptive 50-day strike from June 12 to July 31, 1981, canceled 713 regular-season games and split the season into two halves with a mini-playoff, stemming from owner resistance to expanded free agency and arbitration; the settlement introduced compensation drafts for free-agent signings but preserved core player gains. The 199495 strike, the longest work stoppage in MLB history at 232 days from August 12, 1994, to April 25, 1995 (with a federal injunction briefly resuming play), canceled 948 games including the entire postseason and World Series; triggered by owner demands for a salary cap and reduced benefits amid rising revenues, it ended with players rejecting a cap but accepting revenue sharing and luxury tax provisions, though owners unilaterally imposed changes leading to further litigation.
Ownership conflicts have frequently intersected with labor tensions, as MLB's antitrust exemption grants commissioners broad authority over franchise matters, enabling tactics like collusion and contraction threats to curb player salaries. In the mid-1980s, owners were found guilty in three collusion grievances (covering 19851987 offseasons) for coordinating to suppress free-agent bidding, violating the CBA; arbitrators awarded the MLBPA over $280 million in damages by 1990, including back pay and lost benefits, underscoring owners' attempts to reverse free-agency economics. In November 2001, MLB owners voted 282 to contract two teamsthe Minnesota Twins and Montreal Exposthe first such reduction since 1899, citing financial losses but facing union grievances and court injunctions under the CBA; the plan failed, but it precipitated the Expos' relocation to Washington, D.C., as the Nationals in 2005 after protracted ownership disputes. More recently, the 202122 lockout, initiated by owners on December 2, 2021, lasted 99 days until March 10, 2022, delaying spring training and canceling over a week of games amid negotiations on minimum salaries, luxury tax thresholds, and CBA extensions; the resolution favored players on competitive balance but highlighted ongoing revenue-disparity tensions. These episodes reflect a pattern where owners leverage structural advantages, such as the antitrust shield, against the MLBPA's collective leverage, resulting in periodic impasses resolved through arbitration or federal intervention rather than outright player concessions.

Cultural and International Impact

Role in American Culture and Society

Major League Baseball has embodied America's national pastime since the mid-19th century, evolving from a popular urban game in the 1840s to a cultural institution that mirrored societal shifts during the Civil War, industrialization, and westward expansion. By the 1850s, newspapers described it as a widespread mania, with soldiers on both sides playing it during the Civil War, embedding the sport in national identity as a symbol of leisure and competition. This enduring status stems from its rhythmic pace, strategic depth, and accessibility, fostering intergenerational bonds and community rituals across diverse demographics.
The integration of MLB in 1947, when Jackie Robinson debuted for the Brooklyn Dodgers on April 15, marked a pivotal moment in American civil rights, predating broader desegregation efforts like President Truman's 1948 military order and foreshadowing the 1954 Brown v. Board of Education ruling. Robinson's breakthrough challenged entrenched racial segregation in organized baseball, which had persisted since the late 19th century, and highlighted the sport's role in advancing social equality amid post-World War II pressures for inclusion. While initial resistance from some teams and players reflected societal divisions, MLB's gradual adoption of Black talent influenced public attitudes, though it also contributed to the Negro Leagues' decline by drawing away key players.
Presidential involvement underscores baseball's societal prominence, with William Howard Taft initiating the ceremonial first pitch tradition on April 14, 1910, at the Washington Senators' Opening Day gamea practice continued by nearly every subsequent U.S. president, symbolizing national unity and executive endorsement. Events like the World Series have further cemented this, serving as cultural touchstones that reflect regional rivalries and collective triumphs, often interrupting national routines and shaping family traditions. In 2023, approximately 16.7 million Americans participated in baseball, with the sport leading participation rates among boys aged 6-12, sustaining its grassroots influence despite competition from faster-paced sports. Idioms such as "home run" and "three strikes" permeate everyday language, illustrating baseball's deep linguistic and communal imprint on American life.

Global Expansion and International Play

Major League Baseball has increasingly incorporated international talent, with 265 players born outside the United States comprising 27.8% of Opening Day rosters in 2024, marking the lowest such percentage since 2016 but still reflecting sustained global recruitment. The Dominican Republic leads as the primary source, followed by Venezuela, Cuba, Japan, and Mexico, with over 20 countries represented in the 2024 season. This influx stems from scouting and signing amateur players via international free agency, bypassing the domestic draft to tap regions with established baseball infrastructure, particularly in Latin America where academies have proliferated since the 1980s.
The World Baseball Classic, co-organized by MLB since its inception in 2006, serves as a flagship event for international competition, allowing MLB-affiliated players to represent national teams in a tournament modeled after soccer's FIFA World Cup. Held quadrennially, the event has grown to include 20 teams in 2023, with venues across the Americas, Asia, and Europe, and has boosted global viewership, exemplified by Japan's 2023 championship win drawing millions in Asia. MLB's involvement includes roster approvals and scheduling adjustments to minimize injury risks, though critics note the tournament's irregular format can disrupt regular-season preparation.
Regular-season games abroad, under the MLB World Tour banner, aim to cultivate fanbases and test market viability, with over 200 such contests played in 11 countries since 1996. The first series occurred in Monterrey, Mexico, in 1996 between the San Diego Padres and New York Mets; Tokyo hosted the initial Japan-based games in 2000 and 2004, while recent expansions include the 2019 and 2023 London Series (New York Mets vs. Philadelphia Phillies and Atlanta Braves vs. New York Mets, respectively), 2024 Seoul Series (Los Angeles Dodgers vs. San Diego Padres), and Mexico City games. These efforts target Asia's established fanbasebolstered by stars like Shohei Ohtaniand emerging European interest, though logistical challenges like travel fatigue and variable attendance persist.
MLB supports grassroots development through international academies and training programs, particularly in Latin America, where 30 teams operate facilities in the Dominican Republic to scout and train prospects as young as 16 under signing rules established in 2012. These academies emphasize skill-building alongside basic education, though early reports highlighted exploitative conditions prompting regulatory reforms. In Europe and Asia, MLB funds development centers for coaching and youth leagues, aiming to expand beyond talent export to foster domestic leagues, as seen in partnerships with Japan's Nippon Professional Baseball and Europe's Baseball Federation. Overall, these initiatives have diversified rosters and revenue streams via international broadcasting deals, yet MLB lacks formal expansion franchises abroad, prioritizing talent pipelines over relocation.

Media and Broadcasting

Television and Streaming Coverage

Major League Baseball's television coverage began on August 26, 1939, with the first nationally televised game between the Cincinnati Reds and Brooklyn Dodgers broadcast experimentally on W2XBS in New York. Coverage expanded significantly in the 1950s amid a surge in television set ownership, enabling millions of Americans to view games for the first time and contributing to the sport's national reach. The first coast-to-coast broadcast occurred on October 3, 1951, marking a milestone in nationwide accessibility.
National broadcasting rights evolved into structured deals starting in the 1960s, with ABC securing the first league-wide regular-season package in 1965 for weekend afternoon games. By the late 20th century, networks like ABC, NBC, CBS, and later Fox, ESPN, and Turner Sports dominated, alternating World Series and playoff coverage. As of 2025, MLB's primary national partners include Fox (regular season and select playoffs), ESPN (Sunday Night Baseball and Wild Card games), and TBS (Division Series and League Championship Series games), generating billions in rights fees that fund revenue sharing among teams. These agreements are set to transition post-2025, with ESPN's contract concluding after the season and new pacts nearing completion for 20262028 involving ESPN, NBCUniversal, and Netflix to expand linear and streaming distribution. 
Streaming coverage has grown since MLB.TV launched in 2003 as the first league-operated out-of-market service, offering live games via subscription and establishing a benchmark for sports digital rights. Platforms like Peacock stream select regular-season and postseason games, while Apple TV+ held exclusive Friday Night Baseball rights through 2025 but faces potential discontinuation in favor of broader deals. MLB Network provides complementary studio analysis and out-of-market showcases, averaging 261,000 viewers for live games in 2025, a 13% increase from 2024.
Blackout restrictions limit streaming access to protect regional sports networks (RSNs), preventing MLB.TV subscribers within a team's designated markettypically encompassing the home metropolitan area and surrounding regionsfrom viewing local games live, requiring instead local RSN or over-the-air broadcasts. These rules, rooted in exclusive territorial rights granted to teams and RSNs, apply to both national and international viewers, with Canadian blackouts similarly enforcing local priorities. Critics argue the policy, unchanged amid cord-cutting and streaming shifts, fragments audiences and suppresses overall viewership by forcing fragmented subscriptions, though MLB maintains it safeguards local revenue streams essential for competitive balance. 
Viewership metrics for 2025 reflected gains across national broadcasts, with Fox regular-season games averaging 2.04 million viewers (up 9% year-over-year), FS1 at 324,000 (up 10%), and postseason averages reaching 4.48 million through the League Championship Series, the highest in 15 years for early rounds. These upticks contrast with longer-term declines attributed to factors like extended game times and competition from other media, though recent data indicate stabilization or reversal amid rule changes and high-profile performances. 

Radio, Internet, and Digital Platforms

Radio broadcasting of Major League Baseball games originated on August 5, 1921, when KDKA in Pittsburgh aired the first commercial MLB game, featuring the Pittsburgh Pirates against the Philadelphia Phillies, with announcer Harold Arlin providing play-by-play from Forbes Field. This marked the beginning of a medium that allowed fans beyond stadium capacities to follow games in real time, relying on announcers' descriptive narratives due to the absence of visual elements. By the 1930s, local team broadcasts expanded, often sponsored by sponsors like Wheaties, while national coverage emerged through networks such as the Mutual Broadcasting System, which carried the All-Star Game and World Series starting in 1935.
Today, MLB maintains radio rights with SiriusXM holding national exclusivity for out-of-market games since 2017, offering play-by-play for all 2,430 regular-season contests across its MLB Radio channel. Local stations continue to broadcast home and away games for each of the 30 teams, with flagship partnerships like the New York Yankees' deal with WFAN since 2002 ensuring comprehensive coverage. These broadcasts emphasize announcer expertise in conveying strategy, player movements, and crowd atmosphere, preserving radio's role amid visual media dominance.
MLB pioneered internet streaming on August 26, 2002, with the first live regular-season game broadcast online, predating similar efforts by other leagues and establishing MLB Advanced Media (MLBAM) as a leader in digital distribution. MLB.TV, launched shortly thereafter, grew to over 100,000 subscribers by 2003 at $79.95 per season, offering out-of-market video streams and evolving to include features like multi-game viewing and DVR controls by the 2020s. In 2025, MLB.TV reported record viewership, with partnerships enabling authenticated access via pay-TV providers for postseason games, though blackouts persist for local markets to protect regional rights.
Digital platforms extend MLB's reach through the official MLB App, available on iOS and Android since 2012, which delivers live audio, highlights, scores, and personalized news for over 250 million annual sessions. The app integrates Statcast data for advanced metrics like exit velocity and spin rates, enhancing fan analysis, while MLB.com provides free stats, schedules, and video archives dating back decades. Social media engagement surged in 2024, with MLB platforms generating 37.1 million interactions during the World Seriesa 195% increase year-over-yearand video views reaching 487.6 million, driven by Instagram (up 28%) and X (up 69%) growth. These channels facilitate direct player-fan interactions, such as live Q&As and highlight reels, though MLB enforces content guidelines to curb misinformation on performance-enhancing substances and rule interpretations.

Broadcast Policies and Restrictions

Major League Baseball enforces broadcast restrictions primarily through blackout policies designed to protect regional sports network (RSN) agreements and encourage stadium attendance. These rules limit live streaming access on platforms like MLB.TV within designated home team territories, defined by postal zip codes, applying to both home and away games if the contest is televised locally. Blackouts extend to international viewers for U.S.-based clubs when territories overlap beyond national borders.
Nationally televised games, broadcast on networks such as ESPN, Fox, TBS, or MLB Network, face universal blackouts on streaming services across all markets to uphold exclusive national rights deals. This ensures revenue distribution to teams via centralized agreements, which generated approximately $1.5 billion annually from national media partners as of 2023. Local restrictions prioritize RSN exclusivity, where individual teams negotiate territorial rights; for instance, fans in a team's market must subscribe to the local provider or attend games in person.
Radio broadcasts on MLB.TV remain unrestricted by blackouts, allowing access to any team's audio feed regardless of location. However, flagship radio stations are prohibited from streaming live MLB games over the internet outside their licensed areas under league agreements. These policies stem from MLB's collective bargaining with broadcasters, balancing digital expansion against traditional revenue streams, though they have drawn criticism for reducing overall viewership amid cord-cutting trends. As of 2025, Commissioner Rob Manfred has indicated plans to centralize local rights control, potentially easing restrictions by 2028 when many RSN deals expire.
