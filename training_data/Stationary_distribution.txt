Stationary distribution
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 In probability theory, a stationary distribution, also known as an equilibrium distribution, is a probability distribution over the states of a stochastic process, such as a Markov chain, that remains invariant under the process's transition dynamics, meaning the distribution of the state at any future time equals the initial distribution if starting from it. For a discrete-time Markov chain with finite state space and transition matrix PPP, this distribution \pi satisfies the equation P=\pi P = \piP= while summing to 1, representing the long-run proportion of time the chain spends in each state.
Stationary distributions exist under specific conditions on the Markov chain, such as irreducibility (every state is reachable from every other) and positive recurrence (expected return times to states are finite), ensuring a unique such distribution for ergodic chains that are both irreducible and aperiodic. In continuous-time Markov chains, the stationary distribution \pi similarly satisfies P(t)=\pi P(t) = \piP(t)= for the transition matrix P(t)P(t)P(t) at any time ttt, and it often coincides with the limiting distribution as tt \to \inftyt for well-behaved chains without periodicity issues. Computationally, for finite-state discrete chains, \pi can be found by solving the system (IP)=0\pi (I - P) = 0(IP)=0 subject to normalization, leveraging linear algebra techniques like the Perron-Frobenius theorem for positive matrices. In irreducible positive recurrent chains, the stationary probability i\pi_ii for state iii equals the reciprocal of the mean return time to iii, i=1/i\pi_i = 1 / \mu_ii=1/i. These distributions are fundamental in applications like queueing theory, random walks on graphs, and statistical mechanics, where they describe steady-state behaviors.

General Concept

Definition

In probability theory, a stationary distribution is a probability vector \pi that remains invariant under the application of a stochastic transition matrix PPP, satisfying the equation =P\pi = \pi P=P. This condition implies that if the system starts with distribution \pi, the distribution at any future time is also \pi, representing a fixed point of the Markov operator associated with PPP. More generally, a stationary distribution is unchanged by the action of any Markov operator, preserving the probabilistic structure over iterations.
The components of \pi are non-negative and satisfy the normalization condition ii=1\sum_i \pi_i = 1ii=1, ensuring it qualifies as a proper probability distribution. This normalization distinguishes it from an invariant measure, which satisfies a similar invariance equation but may not sum to 1 and thus need not be a probability measure; in such cases, scaling an invariant measure to sum to 1 yields a stationary distribution if finite.
The concept of stationary distributions has roots in the late 19th century with Ludwig Boltzmann's work on equilibrium distributions in statistical mechanics, where the Boltzmann distribution emerges as the stationary state for systems in thermal equilibrium. The concept was developed in the early 20th century within ergodic theory by Andrey Markov, who in 19061908 established foundational results on chains and their limiting behaviors, including invariant probabilities for finite-state systems. The term 'stationary distribution' was introduced later by S. N. Bernstein in 1926.

Basic Properties

A stationary distribution \pi of a Markov chain possesses the conservation property, meaning that if the chain starts with distribution \pi, the probability assigned to any event AAA in the state space remains (A)\pi(A)(A) after one transition step. This follows directly from the defining equation =P\pi = \pi P=P, where PPP denotes the transition operator, ensuring that the distribution is invariant under the chain's dynamics.
The collection of all stationary distributions for a given Markov chain forms a convex set. Specifically, if 1\pi_11 and 2\pi_22 are stationary distributions, then any convex combination 1+(1)2\lambda \pi_1 + (1 - \lambda) \pi_21+(1)2 for \lambda \in is also stationary, as the linearity of the transition operator preserves the invariance condition.
In the discrete-time, finite-state case, the stationarity condition =P\pi = \pi P=P can be rewritten as (IP)=0\pi (I - P) = 0(IP)=0, where III is the identity matrix. This equation identifies \pi as a left eigenvector of the transition matrix PPP corresponding to the eigenvalue 1, with the normalization ii=1\sum_i \pi_i = 1ii=1 ensuring it is a probability distribution.
For an ergodic Markov chain, where irreducibility and aperiodicity hold, the unique stationary distribution \pi represents the long-run proportion of time the chain spends in each state, as established by the ergodic theorem. Existence and Uniqueness

In discrete-time Markov chains (DTMCs) defined on a countable state space, irreducibility ensures that the chain can transition from any state to any other state with positive probability in finite steps. Specifically, the chain is irreducible if, for every pair of states iii and jjj, there exists some positive integer kkk such that the kkk-step transition probability Pijk>0P^k_{ij} > 0Pijk>0. This property prevents the state space from decomposing into disjoint communicating classes.
Positive recurrence further ensures long-term stability by requiring that the expected return time to any state is finite. A state iii is positive recurrent if the expected value Ei<E_i < \inftyEi<, where i\tau_ii is the first return time to iii after leaving it; in an irreducible chain, this applies to all states. The key theorem states that for an irreducible and positive recurrent DTMC, there exists a unique stationary probability distribution \pi, satisfying P=\pi P = \piP=, and i=1/i\pi_i = 1 / \mu_ii=1/i, where i=Ei\mu_i = E_ii=Ei is the mean return time to iii. If the chain is also aperiodic (gcd of return times is 1), then the limiting distribution exists: PijkjP^k_{ij} \to \pi_jPijkj as kk \to \inftyk for all i,ji, ji,j. This result holds for finite or countably infinite state spaces under these conditions.
In DTMCs with countably infinite state spaces, transience or null recurrence can prevent a stationary probability distribution. Transient chains drift to infinity without returning, yielding no invariant probability measure. Null recurrent chains have finite expected return times that are infinite in expectation, admitting an invariant measure but not normalizable to a probability distribution, resulting in limiting probabilities of zero. Reducibility allows multiple stationary distributions, one per recurrent class, but uniqueness requires full irreducibility and positive recurrence. Periodicity, even in irreducible positive recurrent chains, prevents limiting distributions from existing, though the stationary distribution remains unique.

Existence and Uniqueness

In continuous-time Markov chains (CTMCs) defined on a countable state space, irreducibility ensures that the chain can transition from any state to any other state with positive probability in finite time. Specifically, the chain is irreducible if, for every pair of states iii and jjj, there exists some t>0t > 0t>0 such that the transition probability Pij(t)>0P_{ij}(t) > 0Pij(t)>0. This property, analogous to the discrete-time case but adapted to holding times governed by exponential distributions, prevents the state space from decomposing into disjoint communicating classes.
Positive recurrence further refines the long-term behavior by requiring that the expected return time to any state is finite. A state iii is positive recurrent if the expected value Ei<E_i < \inftyEi<, where i\tau_ii denotes the first return time to iii after leaving it; in an irreducible chain, this classification applies uniformly to all states. In CTMCs with countably infinite state spaces, explosion poses a risk where the process undergoes infinitely many jumps in finite time, rendering it undefined beyond the explosion time and preventing the existence of a stationary distribution. Explosion occurs if the expected number of jumps up to time ttt diverges, often in pure birth processes with rapidly increasing rates (e.g., rates growing exponentially); conditions for non-explosion include bounded jump rates or finite state spaces. To ensure existence, the chain must satisfy regularity conditions, such as those guaranteeing a minimal non-explosive process.
For irreducible CTMCs that are transient or null recurrent, no unique stationary probability distribution exists. In transient cases, the chain drifts to infinity without returning, yielding no invariant probability measure. In null recurrent cases, while an invariant measure exists, it has infinite total mass and cannot be normalized to a probability distribution, leading to limiting probabilities of zero for all states. Reducibility can introduce multiple stationary distributions, one per recurrent class, but uniqueness requires the full irreducibility and positive recurrence.

Computation Methods

Balance Equations

The balance equations form a system of linear equations that the stationary distribution must satisfy, enabling direct computation for finite-state Markov chains.
For discrete-time Markov chains, the stationary distribution \pi satisfies the equation (PI)=0\pi (P - I) = 0(PI)=0, where PPP is the transition probability matrix and III is the identity matrix, subject to the normalization condition ii=1\sum_i \pi_i = 1ii=1. For continuous-time Markov chains, the stationary distribution \pi satisfies Q=0\pi Q = 0Q=0, where QQQ is the infinitesimal generator (rate) matrix, again with the normalization ii=1\sum_i \pi_i = 1ii=1.
For finite-state spaces, Gaussian elimination provides a practical method to solve these systems. Since the full system is singular (due to the rows of PIP - IPI or QQQ summing to zero), one equation is dropped and replaced by the normalization condition, yielding a nonsingular system that can be solved via standard linear algebra techniques.
An alternative is the power iteration method, an iterative approach that approximates the stationary distribution by starting with a uniform initial probability vector and repeatedly multiplying it by the transition matrix PPP (for discrete time) until convergence; under ergodicity, this converges to \pi.

Advanced Techniques

For irreducible Markov chains, the Perron-Frobenius theorem provides a foundational tool to establish the existence and uniqueness of the stationary distribution. Applied to the transition matrix PPP, which is irreducible and stochastic (nonnegative with rows summing to 1), the theorem guarantees that 1 is a simple eigenvalue with a unique positive left eigenvector \pi (normalized to sum to 1), corresponding precisely to the stationary distribution. This eigenvector is strictly positive, ensuring i>0\pi_i > 0i>0 for all states iii.
In reversible Markov chains, the detailed balance condition offers a powerful characterization of the stationary distribution, stating that iPij=jPji\pi_i P_{ij} = \pi_j P_{ji}iPij=jPji for all states i,ji, ji,j. This condition implies reversibility, where the chain's behavior is symmetric under time reversal in stationarity, and it strengthens the global balance equations by enforcing zero net flux between any pair of states. For chains modeling undirected graph flows, such as random walks on undirected graphs, detailed balance holds with i\pi_ii proportional to the degree of node iii, simplifying computations.
The Markov chain tree theorem extends this for reversible chains by linking stationary probabilities to combinatorial structures in the state graph. Specifically, for a finite, irreducible, reversible continuous-time Markov chain, the stationary probability j\pi_jj is proportional to the total "focus" at state jjj, defined as the sum over all spanning trees rooted at jjj of the product of transition rates along the tree's arcs. This reduces finding \pi to enumerating weighted spanning trees, leveraging Kirchhoff's matrix tree theorem on the Laplacian derived from the transition rates.
Spectral decomposition of the transition matrix PPP further aids in verifying and analyzing the stationary distribution, particularly its uniqueness. For an irreducible, aperiodic chain, PPP admits a decomposition P=UU1P = U \Lambda U^{-1}P=UU1, where \Lambda is diagonal with eigenvalues k1|\lambda_k| \leq 1k1, and 1=1\lambda_1 = 11=1 is simple (multiplicity 1) with left eigenvector \pi. The remaining eigenvalues satisfy 2<1|\lambda_2| < 12<1, confirming uniqueness and providing the spectral gap 121 - |\lambda_2|12 for convergence rates to stationarity. This approach is especially useful for chains with symmetric structure, where eigenvalues are real.
For Markov chains with large state spaces, exact computation of stationary distributions becomes intractable, prompting approximation techniques like lumpability and mean-field methods, developed prominently in the 1970s and 1980s. Lumpability aggregates states into macro-states while preserving the Markov property; strong lumpability requires identical transition probabilities from all micro-states in a lump to any other lump, yielding an exact reduced chain with the same stationary marginals, as shown for finite chains. Weak lumpability relaxes this for specific initial distributions or moments, enabling approximations for irreducible chains without full aggregation. Meanwhile, mean-field approximations treat interactions in large populations as independent, replacing the full chain with a deterministic differential equation whose equilibrium approximates the stationary distribution; for density-dependent chains, Kurtz's propagation of chaos theorem ensures convergence as the population size grows, with error bounds scaling as O(1/N)O(1/\sqrt{N})O(1/N). These methods are crucial for systems like queueing networks or interacting particle models.

Applications and Examples

Markov Chain Monte Carlo

Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods leverage stationary distributions to approximate complex target probability distributions, such as posterior densities in Bayesian inference, by constructing ergodic Markov chains that converge to the desired stationary measure \pi. The foundational Metropolis algorithm, introduced in 1953, generates samples from a Boltzmann distribution for physical simulations by proposing moves and accepting or rejecting them based on energy differences, ensuring the chain's stationary distribution matches \pi. This was generalized by Hastings in 1970 to the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm, which allows asymmetric proposal distributions while preserving \pi as the stationary distribution through an acceptance probability that satisfies detailed balance. These techniques, initially developed in the mid-20th century for statistical physics, gained prominence in Bayesian statistics during the 1980s and early 1990s, particularly through applications to marginal density estimation. Gelfand and Smith (1990) demonstrated their utility for computing posterior marginals by simulating chains that target the joint posterior, marking a key advancement in statistical computing.
A critical aspect of MCMC implementation involves addressing the initial transients before the chain reaches stationarity, known as the burn-in period, during which samples are discarded to mitigate starting value bias. Mixing time quantifies the duration required for the chain to explore the state space adequately and approach \pi, influenced by factors like chain dimensionality and proposal efficiency; poor mixing leads to autocorrelation and inefficient sampling. Post-1990s diagnostics, such as trace plots visualizing sample paths over iterations, help assess convergence by revealing trends, stationarity, and potential sticking in modes. The ergodic theorem underpins the validity of MCMC estimates: for an irreducible, aperiodic chain with stationary distribution \pi, the time average of a function fff along the chain converges almost surely to its expectation E\mathbb{E}_\piE, enabling consistent estimation of posterior quantities like means and variances.
Gibbs sampling exemplifies MCMC application in Bayesian inference, iteratively sampling from full conditional distributions to target the joint posterior as the stationary distribution \pi, which proves effective for high-dimensional models. Introduced by Geman and Geman in 1984, it was applied to Bayesian image restoration, where \pi represents the posterior over pixel labels given noisy observations, facilitating denoising via simulated realizations from the Gibbs measure. Despite these strengths, MCMC faces challenges in high dimensions, where ensuring irreducibilityrequiring the chain to reach any state from any other with positive probabilitybecomes difficult due to multimodal targets that may trap the chain in local modes. Reversible chains, which satisfy detailed balance and thus have \pi as invariant, often exhibit faster convergence rates compared to irreversible alternatives, though achieving reversibility without compromising exploration remains a key design consideration.

Queueing Theory

In queueing theory, the stationary distribution captures the long-run probabilities of system states in steady-state operation, essential for analyzing performance metrics such as queue lengths and waiting times in service systems. For Markovian queues modeled as continuous-time Markov chains, the stationary distribution exists under stability conditions like traffic intensity less than unity, enabling predictions of average behavior without simulating transients. This application is pivotal in operations research for designing efficient networks in telecommunications, manufacturing, and computing.

This mean quantifies congestion: for approaching 1, L grows unbounded, highlighting the need for capacity exceeding demand.
Jackson networks extend this to open queueing networks of M/M/1-like queues with exogenous arrivals and probabilistic routing. Jackson's theorem establishes that the joint stationary distribution of queue lengths across nodes factors into a product of marginal geometric distributions, each resembling an isolated M/M/1 queue with effective arrival rates solved via traffic equations, provided the network is open and subcritical (_i < 1 for each node i). This product-form solution simplifies analysis of multi-stage systems, such as job shops, where independence holds despite interactions.

These approximations, often via renewal theory, provide bounds or diffusion limits for mean queue lengths when < 1, bridging to more realistic models in heavy-traffic regimes.
