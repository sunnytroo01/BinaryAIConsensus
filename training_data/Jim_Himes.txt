 James Andrew Himes (born July 5, 1966) is an American politician and former investment banker serving as the U.S. representative for Connecticut's 4th congressional district since 2009. A Democrat, he represents a district encompassing parts of Fairfield County, including Stamford and Bridgeport, and has focused on financial services, intelligence oversight, and housing policy.
Himes was born in Lima, Peru, to American parents working abroad and grew up in New Jersey, graduating from Hopewell Valley Central High School. He earned a bachelor's degree in social studies from Harvard University in 1988 and, as a Rhodes Scholar, a Master of Philosophy in Latin American studies from Oxford University. Early in his career, he worked as a credit analyst at Goldman Sachs and later directed nonprofit finance at Fannie Mae, before serving as CEO of the Connecticut Housing Finance Authority from 2003 to 2006, where his team financed thousands of affordable housing units in the New York metropolitan region using innovative funding mechanisms.
Elected in 2008 by defeating incumbent Republican Chris Shays, Himes has secured reelection in subsequent cycles and holds senior positions, including on the House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligencewhere he has chaired subcommittees on technical and tactical mattersand the Financial Services Committee. His legislative record includes bipartisan votes on defense appropriations and financial regulations, reflecting a pragmatic approach amid partisan divides, though he has faced criticism from progressive factions for positions on foreign policy and intelligence matters such as Russia election interference assessments.

Early Career

Entry into Politics

Initial Political Involvement

Himes' entry into politics was motivated by his professional experience in affordable housing at Enterprise Community Partners, where he addressed urban poverty challenges, prompting him to apply that expertise locally after relocating to Greenwich, Connecticut, in 2003. He cited dissatisfaction with national policies under President George W. Bush's administration following the 2004 re-election as a key factor, describing them as "some pretty awful policies" that drove his Democratic activism.
In 2003, Himes was appointed to the Greenwich Housing Authority as a commissioner and subsequently elected chairman, a position he held until 2006. During his tenure, he restructured the agency by dismissing underperforming consultants, appointing a new executive director, and overseeing the completion of four affordable housing units, thereby enhancing operational efficiency and project delivery. This role marked his initial foray into public service, leveraging his nonprofit background to tackle local housing shortages in the affluent suburb.
Himes expanded his involvement by winning election to the Greenwich Board of Estimate and Taxation in 2006, serving until 2007, where he influenced municipal tax and budget policies as part of this fiscal oversight body. Concurrently, he chaired the Greenwich Democratic Town Committee, organizing grassroots efforts and community engagement to bolster local Democratic activities ahead of broader campaigns. These positions solidified his profile in Greenwich politics, transitioning from housing advocacy to elected fiscal responsibilities and party leadership.

2008 Congressional Campaign

Himes entered the race for Connecticut's 4th congressional district in April 2007, registering his campaign committee with the Federal Election Commission on April 18. The district, encompassing Fairfield County and leaning Democratic despite incumbent Republican Christopher Shays holding the seat since 1987, presented an opportunity amid national anti-Republican sentiment following the Iraq War and economic concerns.
In the Democratic primary on August 12, 2008, Himes secured nomination with 87.0% of the vote against substitute teacher Diane Farrington, reflecting strong party support in a low-turnout contest. His campaign emphasized his background in finance and housing advocacy, positioning him as a pragmatic alternative to Shays, while securing endorsements from groups like the Connecticut AFL-CIO.
Facing Shays in the general election on November 4, 2008, Himes benefited from a Democratic wave driven by Barack Obama's presidential candidacy and voter dissatisfaction with President George W. Bush, as highlighted in Himes' advertising strategy. Himes invested over $500,000 of his own funds and raised competitively, overcoming Shays' initial fundraising lead of nearly $1.8 million in cash on hand. He defeated the 10-term incumbent with 51.3% of the vote to Shays' 48.7%, flipping the district and eliminating New England's last Republican House member.

Policy Positions

National Security and Intelligence Oversight

Jim Himes has served on the House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence (HPSCI) since 2013, where he contributes to oversight of the 17 agencies comprising the U.S. Intelligence Community, including the CIA, NSA, and FBI, focusing on their activities, budgets, and compliance with legal standards. As Ranking Member in the 118th Congress and reappointed in January 2025, Himes leads Democratic efforts to balance intelligence capabilities with accountability, emphasizing bipartisan scrutiny of threats from adversaries like China, Russia, and Iran.
In oversight hearings, Himes has pressed intelligence officials on operational transparency, such as questioning Trump-era officials in March 2025 about leaked Signal messages related to Yemen strikes, highlighting potential lapses in interagency communication and legal adherence. He supported the 2023 bipartisan framework for reauthorizing Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA), which enables warrantless surveillance of non-U.S. persons abroad for foreign intelligence, while advocating reforms to curb incidental collection on Americans and enhance querying safeguards. However, Himes voted against the Intelligence Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2016, citing excessive spending and insufficient protections for civil liberties amid bulk data collection concerns.
Himes has co-led with Republican Chairman Mike Turner in joint statements urging robust funding for intelligence amid rising terror threats, as in December 2023, arguing that tools like Section 702 are essential for counterterrorism without viable alternatives. His positions reflect a pragmatic defense of intelligence efficacyprioritizing empirical assessments of threats over expansive privacy mandates that could impair real-time operationswhile critiquing instances of overreach, though critics from privacy advocates argue his support for FISA extensions perpetuates insufficient reforms against incidental domestic surveillance.

Foreign Policy and Defense

As ranking member of the House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence since 2023, Himes oversees the 18 U.S. intelligence agencies, focusing on threats from adversaries including Russia, China, Iran, and non-state actors like Hamas. In this capacity, he has emphasized robust intelligence sharing with allies, such as resuming support to Ukraine amid its conflict with Russia, while criticizing pauses in such assistance as detrimental to U.S. interests. Himes has also conducted hearings on emerging technologies' role in national security, authoring reports on U.S. advantages over China in innovation despite Beijing's investments.
On Russia and Ukraine, Himes has consistently advocated for strong U.S. support, describing President Volodymyr Zelenskyy as a "courageous leader" of a nation defending its existence against Russia's "illegal and brutal invasion" launched on February 24, 2022. Marking the invasion's third anniversary on February 24, 2025, he called for an "honorable peace" guaranteeing Ukraine's security to deter future aggression, while faulting both Trump and Biden administrations for insufficient pressure on Moscow. He has opposed narratives of U.S. pullback in aid, asserting no "dramatic change" occurred and defending intelligence resumption to Ukraine as essential, despite Trump's past "skeptical" rhetoric toward Kyiv and affinity for Vladimir Putin.
Regarding China, Himes has urged a balanced approach beyond zero-sum rivalry, arguing in a 2023 op-ed for engagement on shared issues like fentanyl trafficking and climate change while addressing threats such as potential Taiwan invasion, South China Sea militarization, and intellectual property theft. In December 2024, he dismissed predictions of a Chinese assault on Taiwan by 2027 as overhyped and strategically "really dumb" for Beijing, given U.S. military preparations and alliances. He has highlighted China's inability to match U.S. innovation in key areas, advocating sustained investment in domestic strengths over alarmist decoupling.
In the Middle East, Himes condemned Hamas's October 7, 2023, attacks on Israel as a "horrific massacre" that killed thousands and shattered families, supporting Israel's right to defend itself and co-sponsoring H.Res. 771 affirming U.S. solidarity against the group. He welcomed the January 15, 2025, ceasefire between Israel and Hamas as a relief after 15 months of war, though he acknowledged Palestinian "legitimate claims" amid historical injustices, while viewing unqualified backing of Israel as politically straightforward in Congress. In oversight roles, Himes pressed intelligence officials on Yemen-related leaks and expressed rare praise for Trump administration diplomacy reducing Russian influence in regional talks.
Himes supports sustained defense funding, voting for the National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) for Fiscal Year 2024, which allocated $886 billion for discretionary military spending, and the December 2024 package benefiting Connecticut's defense sector, including submarine production. He has backed incremental innovations in defense procurement and opposed cuts to programs like UNRWA aid only when tied to Hamas ties, prioritizing U.S. strategic interests over isolationism.

Economic and Financial Policies

Jim Himes has served on the House Committee on Financial Services since entering Congress in 2009, where he has influenced legislation on banking regulation, housing finance, and consumer protection. He voted for the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act in 2010, which established stricter oversight of financial institutions to prevent future crises, including the creation of the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau. However, Himes has advocated for targeted modifications to Dodd-Frank, arguing that certain provisions imposed undue burdens on community banks and smaller lenders without proportionally enhancing systemic stability; in 2018, he was the only Connecticut Democrat to support the Economic Growth, Regulatory Relief, and Consumer Protection Act, which raised the asset threshold for enhanced prudential standards from $50 billion to $250 billion, exempting regional banks from some stress tests and liquidity requirements. He criticized executive efforts to broadly dismantle Dodd-Frank provisions, stating in 2017 that such rollbacks would undermine protections gained post-2008 without evidence of overregulation stifling lending.
On broader economic policy, Himes has prioritized job creation and innovation, particularly in Connecticut's 4th district, which hosts diverse businesses from Fortune 100 firms to startups; he has championed measures to support small business lending and infrastructure investments for long-term growth. In response to recessions, he backed the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009, which included $288 billion in tax cuts and $507 billion in spending on infrastructure and aid, projecting it would create or save 3.5 million jobs and boost GDP by 3.7% by 2010. Similarly, he supported extensions of unemployment insurance during the 2010 downturn, citing Labor Department data that each $1 in benefits generated $2 in economic activity through consumer spending.
Regarding fiscal policy and taxes, Himes has opposed Republican budget proposals that reduce social spending to offset tax cuts for high earners, as seen in his 2025 vote against a resolution he described as prioritizing "tax breaks for billionaires" over vulnerable populations, potentially adding trillions to the deficit. He endorsed the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022, which incorporated $300 billion in deficit reduction through corporate minimum taxes and drug price negotiations, while funding clean energy incentives projected to lower household energy costs. Himes has consistently scored low on conservative indices for economic votes, reflecting alignment with Democratic priorities on spending multipliers and regulatory baselines over austerity measures.

Environmental and Energy Policies

Immigration and Border Security

Jim Himes has consistently supported comprehensive immigration reform emphasizing both border security enhancements and humane pathways for undocumented immigrants to legal status. In 2013, he co-sponsored the House version of a bipartisan bill akin to the Senate's Border Security, Economic Opportunity, and Immigration Modernization Act, which passed the Senate 68-32 and included provisions for increased border security funding, a nationwide E-Verify workplace verification system, an entry-exit tracking system, asylum process improvements, and a 13-year pathway to citizenship for eligible undocumented individuals meeting requirements such as background checks, taxes, and English proficiency.
Himes opposed the Secure the Border Act of 2023 (H.R. 2), voting no on its passage on May 11, 2023, which cleared the House 219-213; the bill sought to resume border wall construction, limit asylum claims, end catch-and-release policies, and mandate congressional approval for certain DHS actions. He has endorsed bipartisan alternatives, such as the 2024 Senate Border Act (S. 4361), negotiated by Senators Chris Murphy (D-CT) and James Lankford (R-OK), which proposed expedited removals for certain migrants, 1,500 additional Customs and Border Protection personnel, 4,300 asylum officers, more immigration judges to reduce backlogs, restrictions on parole authority, and increased border wall funding alongside expanded legal immigration pathways; the measure advanced from committee but stalled amid partisan opposition influenced by former President Trump's directive against deals.
In public statements, Himes has acknowledged a border crisis, criticizing the Biden administration for permitting excessive illegal crossingsover 2.4 million encounters in fiscal year 2023 alonewhile faulting Trump-era policies like family separations at the border, which affected thousands of children and drew widespread condemnation. During an October 30, 2024, debate, he advocated for more border patrol agents and immigration judges to adjudicate claims efficiently, arguing that Republicans preferred politicizing the issue over solutions like the Murphy-Lankford bill. Himes also condemned the Trump administration's 2017 termination of Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals (DACA) and Deferred Action for Parents of Americans (DAPA), which would have protected hundreds of thousands of "Dreamers" and parents from deportation.
More recently, in August 2025, Himes issued a statement opposing intensified Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) raids in Connecticut, emphasizing due process rights and criticizing aggressive enforcement tactics under the incoming administration as undermining legal protections without addressing root causes. His positions align with Democratic priorities for balancing security with humanitarian considerations, though critics from conservative outlets argue such stances contribute to lax enforcement amid record migrant surges, with U.S. Customs and Border Protection reporting over 7 million southwest border encounters since fiscal year 2021. Himes maintains that effective policy requires bipartisan investment in technology, personnel, and legal reforms rather than unilateral restrictions.

Controversies and Criticisms

UAP Disclosure and Intelligence Committee Actions

As Ranking Member of the House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence (HPSCI), Jim Himes has overseen aspects of U.S. government investigations into unidentified anomalous phenomena (UAP), emphasizing national security implications over speculative extraterrestrial hypotheses. During the May 17, 2022, congressional hearingthe first public UFO/UAP session in over 50 yearsHimes questioned Pentagon officials, including Under Secretary of Defense for Intelligence and Security Ronald Moultrie and Deputy Director of Naval Intelligence Scott Bray, on destigmatizing pilot reporting to improve data collection and reduce conspiracy theories. He stressed the need for empirical evidence, noting that unverified claims fuel public misinformation, and advocated for systematic analysis of UAP incidents rather than endorsing unproven narratives of advanced non-human technology.
Himes has consistently expressed skepticism regarding demands for broader UAP disclosure, arguing that most reported phenomena resolve to mundane explanations like drones, balloons, or sensor artifacts upon investigation. In February 2023, following U.S. military shootdowns of unidentified objects over North American airspace, he described the incidents as likely benignsuch as hobbyist balloons or commercial debriswhile cautioning against overreaction without intelligence confirmation, and highlighted the intelligence community's focus on potential foreign adversary threats like Chinese surveillance. Regarding the Office of the Director of National Intelligence's June 2021 preliminary UAP report, which assessed 144 incidents with no conclusive extraterrestrial evidence, Himes noted it would "disappoint a lot of people" expecting revelations of alien craft, reinforcing that available data points to prosaic origins or gaps in understanding rather than paradigm-shifting discoveries.
Under Himes' tenure on HPSCI, the committee has supported establishment and funding for the All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO) within the Department of Defense, tasked with centralized UAP data resolution since 2022, but has not prioritized legislative mandates for declassifying classified programs amid whistleblower claims of hidden non-human biologics or craft. In public statements, such as a March 2023 appearance, Himes clarified that "most of the UFOs we see are really just garbage," aligning with AARO's findings of no verifiable evidence for extraterrestrial involvement, and urged against politicizing UAP inquiries that could distract from genuine counterintelligence priorities. Critics from UAP advocacy circles have accused Himes of downplaying transparency efforts, citing his July 2024 remarks dismissing aggressive disclosure pushes as misguided absent empirical proof of extraordinary claims, though HPSCI briefings continue to address resolved cases and ongoing sensor data analysis without endorsing unverified allegations. Through 2025, Himes has maintained that intelligence oversight prioritizes verifiable threatssuch as potential adversarial hypersonic or stealth technologies mimicking UAPover speculative disclosure, consistent with bipartisan intelligence authorization acts enhancing AARO's reporting mechanisms.

Political Campaigns and Electoral History

2008 Election Victory

In the Democratic primary for Connecticut's 4th congressional district on August 12, 2008, Jim Himes secured the nomination with 87.0% of the vote against challenger Chris Murgo. Himes, a former Goldman Sachs executive and chairman of the Greenwich Democratic Town Committee, had entered politics after a career in investment banking and financing low-income housing projects.
The general election pitted Himes against incumbent Republican Christopher Shays, a moderate who had represented the district since 1987 and was the last Republican congressman in New England. The campaign occurred amid a national Democratic wave driven by dissatisfaction with the Iraq War, the financial crisis, and President George W. Bush's approval ratings, which boosted Barack Obama's presidential bid in Connecticut by a 22.4% margin. Himes raised approximately $2.9 million, competitive with Shays's $3.1 million, including over $500,000 of his own funds, and emphasized themes of change, economic reform, and independence from Wall Street excesses despite his background. 
On November 4, 2008, Himes defeated Shays with 158,475 votes (51.3%) to Shays's 146,854 (47.6%), a margin of 11,621 votes out of 308,776 total cast, marking the first Democratic hold of the district since 1970. Shays conceded that evening, attributing the loss to national anti-Republican sentiment rather than personal factors, while Himes credited voter desire for accountability on the economy and foreign policy. The victory flipped the district, contributing to Democrats' net gain of 21 House seats nationwide.

Re-Election Campaigns and Challenges (20102024)

In the 2010 midterm elections, Himes secured re-election in Connecticut's 4th congressional district against Republican Dan Debicella, a former state senator, capturing 53.1% of the vote to Debicella's 46.9% amid a national Republican surge driven by opposition to the Affordable Care Act and economic concerns. The race was competitive due to the district's history of Republican representation under Chris Shays until Himes's 2008 victory, but Himes benefited from strong turnout in urban areas like Bridgeport and Stamford.
Himes won a third term in 2012 with 59.5% of the vote against Republican State Representative John Shaban, who emphasized fiscal conservatism and criticized Himes's support for Obama administration policies. His fundraising advantage, raising over $1.7 million compared to Shaban's under $500,000, allowed for extensive advertising in the suburban-heavy district.
The 2014 cycle presented a rematch with Debicella, who positioned himself as a moderate alternative amid dissatisfaction with Democratic control of Congress; Himes prevailed with 53.8% to Debicella's 46.2%, a narrower margin reflecting midterm headwinds for incumbents but bolstered by Himes's incumbency and local economic focus.
In 2016, Himes defeated Republican Harry Arora, a physician and businessman, with 59.9% of the vote, as Arora's campaign highlighted Himes's votes on trade deals like the Trans-Pacific Partnership; the victory aligned with Democratic resilience in Connecticut despite national Republican gains under Trump.
Himes's 2018 re-election against Arora again saw him win decisively at approximately 61% to 39%, with Arora criticizing Himes's alignment with national Democratic leadership on issues like taxes and regulation; Himes's emphasis on bipartisanship and district-specific infrastructure funding contributed to the margin in a year of Democratic House gains.
During the 2020 election, Himes overcame Republican Jonathan Riddle, a financial executive, securing victory with 60.6% amid pandemic-related voting and his advocacy for federal relief measures; Riddle focused on economic recovery critiques but trailed due to the district's partisan lean.
In 2022, facing Republican Jayme Stevenson, a former state representative, Himes won with 59.4% by highlighting his work on housing affordability and energy costs, while Stevenson attacked inflation under Biden; the race underscored Himes's entrenched position in a district rated as safely Democratic by non-partisan analysts.
Himes's 2024 re-election pitted him against Republican Michael Goldstein, an attorney, and Independent Benjamin Wesley, with Himes prevailing on November 5; challengers debated issues like inflation and border security, but Himes's long tenure and fundraising edgeover $2 million raisedensured a comfortable win in the general election following unopposed primaries. Throughout these campaigns, Himes faced no serious primary challenges, advancing unopposed as the Democratic nominee each cycle, and consistently outperformed opponents through superior resources and alignment with the district's affluent, educated suburban demographics.

Personal Life

Family and Personal Relationships

James Andrew Himes was born on July 5, 1966, in Lima, Peru, to American parents whose work in international development placed the family abroad during his early years. His father served in nonprofit roles with organizations including the Ford Foundation and UNICEF, leading to residences in Lima and later Bogot, Colombia, where Himes spent his childhood until age ten.
Following his parents' divorce around 1976, Himes relocated to Pennington, New Jersey, with his mother, Judy Himes, and his two sisters. His mother worked as an administrator in nonprofit sectors, including as Director of Board Activities for the New Jersey Board of Higher Education. This transition shaped his upbringing in a single-parent household in a small American town, an experience he has referenced in discussions of economic opportunity and family stability.
On October 15, 1994, Himes married Mary Lynley Scott, a designer and daughter of Janet and Michael Scott of Toronto, Canada, in a ceremony held in Toronto. The couple has resided in the Cos Cob section of Greenwich, Connecticut, for nearly three decades, raising their two daughters, Emma and Linley. Himes has described prioritizing family time amid his congressional duties, such as coordinating schedules to accommodate school events and holidays despite frequent travel. As of recent public statements, the family continues to live together in Greenwich, with the daughters now adults making occasional visits. No public records indicate separation or divorce from Mary Scott.

Religious Beliefs and Community Engagement

James Andrew Himes identifies as Presbyterian, a affiliation consistently reported across multiple congressional terms. He serves as an elder in his local Presbyterian church, reflecting active involvement in congregational leadership.
In June 2016, Himes publicly criticized the U.S. House of Representatives' practice of observing moments of silence following mass shootings, describing them as "an abomination" and advocating instead for open prayer as a more substantive response grounded in faith. This stance underscores his view of prayer's role in public discourse on tragedy, aligning with Presbyterian traditions emphasizing communal supplication.
Beyond religious leadership, Himes has engaged in community service focused on housing and education. Prior to Congress, he chaired the Greenwich Housing Authority board, overseeing affordable housing initiatives in Connecticut, and directed the New York City operations of Enterprise Community Partners, a nonprofit that financed thousands of low-income units using innovative green building techniques. In office, he has secured federal funding for district priorities, including a $12 million Head Start grant awarded in July 2025 to the Alliance for Community Engagement in Bridgeport, expanding early childhood education for low-income families. These efforts prioritize economic opportunity and family support, though they lack explicit ties to faith-based organizations in available records.
