Shahid Kapoor (born 25 February 1981) is an Indian actor who primarily works in Hindi-language films. The son of actors Pankaj Kapur and Neelima Azeem, he started his career as a background dancer in films before transitioning to acting with his debut in the romantic comedy Ishq Vishk (2003). Initially known for light-hearted romantic roles in movies like Vivah (2006) and Jab We Met (2007), Kapoor later received critical praise for intense dramatic performances, including the lead in Vishal Bhardwaj's Haider (2014), an adaptation of Shakespeare's Hamlet, for which he won the Filmfare Award for Best Actor. He further demonstrated range as a drug-addicted musician in Udta Punjab (2016), earning the Filmfare Critics Award for Best Performance, and as a volatile surgeon in the commercially successful but divisive Kabir Singh (2019). Over his two-decade career, Kapoor has accumulated multiple accolades, including three Filmfare Awards, though his filmography features a pattern of commercial hits interspersed with underperformers.

Early life

Family background and childhood

Shahid Kapoor was born on February 25, 1981, in New Delhi to actors Pankaj Kapur and Neelima Azeem. His parents divorced when he was three years old, after which he was primarily raised by his mother in Delhi, experiencing limited contact with his father, who had remarried actress Supriya Pathak in 1988. From his father's second marriage, Kapoor has two paternal half-siblings, Sanah Kapur and Ruhaan Kapoor; on his mother's side, he has a maternal half-brother, Ishaan Khatter, born from Neelima Azeem's subsequent marriage to actor Rajesh Khattar.
The divorce and subsequent family separations contributed to a challenging upbringing marked by emotional and financial difficulties. Kapoor has recounted being bullied in school due to his father's absence from the household, which fostered a sense of isolation despite his parents' industry connections. His mother, who initially worked as a classical dancer and actress, faced hardships after prioritizing childcare, including pauses in her career following Ishaan's birth, leading the family to reside in rented accommodations in Delhi. Kapoor has described his reluctance to seek financial assistance from his father, attributing this to pride and a desire for self-reliance, which shaped his early independence amid these post-divorce economic strains.
Though exposed to the film industry through his parents' professions, Kapoor's childhood emphasized practical self-sufficiency over inherited privilege, with his mother's single-parent efforts providing a foundation of resilience rather than stability. This dynamic, including infrequent early interactions with his father due to the latter's career and new family, instilled a pragmatic outlook unromanticized by familial fame.
Despite these limited interactions, Kapoor has shared humorous anecdotes from his childhood and early life involving his father in interviews. One such memory relates to his father's iconic role in the 1980s television series Karamchand. During a rare outing at Nirulas restaurant in Delhi when Kapoor was a child, fans of the show mobbed them, offering carrots (gajar) to Pankaj Kapur in reference to his character's habit of eating carrots, leading to a chaotic situation that forced them to flee. Kapoor humorously recalled deciding afterward not to go out publicly with his father again.
In another anecdote, Kapoor recounted his father teasing him about potential genetic hair loss, saying: "Abhi toh tu hero ban gaya hai. Baad mein kya hoga tera when you wont have any hair? Jab baal nahi rahenge toh acting karni padegi" (Now you're a hero, but what will happen later when you have no hair? You'll have to do acting then). Kapoor jokingly suggested that this teasing may have motivated him to take acting seriously.

Education and initial aspirations

Kapoor completed his primary education at Gyan Bharati School in Delhi until the fourth standard before transferring to Rajhans Vidyalaya in Mumbai for further schooling. He subsequently enrolled at Mithibai College in Mumbai, where he studied for three years prior to dropping out in order to pursue professional training in dance and acting.
His early aspirations were rooted in dance rather than acting, with a strong admiration for Michael Jackson shaping his ambitions; Kapoor reportedly sacrificed birthday gifts for five years to attend one of the singer's concerts, reflecting a personal commitment to the art form over material incentives. Influenced by his mother Neelima Azeem, a Kathak dancer, he began training in Western contemporary styles under choreographer Shiamak Davar, initially performing as a background dancer in music videos and films.
Despite his father Pankaj Kapur's established acting career, Kapoor emphasized self-reliance, undergoing rigorous dance preparation and enduring around 100 audition rejections before breaking into films, which underscored his focus on skill-building independent of industry nepotism.

Career beginnings

Dance and modeling work

Shahid Kapoor began his professional career as a dancer, training under choreographer Shiamak Davar and performing as a background dancer in Bollywood films during the late 1990s. He made uncredited appearances in the 1997 film Dil To Pagal Hai, including the song "Le Gayi," where he danced alongside Karisma Kapoor and later recounted being nervous, leading to multiple retakes. Kapoor continued with background roles in Taal (1999), contributing to ensemble dance sequences that highlighted his early skills in contemporary and film choreography.
In parallel, Kapoor pursued modeling assignments and commercial work to build visibility in the entertainment industry. He featured in several music videos for Indipop artists during the early 2000s, leveraging his dance training to secure roles that emphasized performance over established connections. These included appearances in advertisements, such as a Pepsi spot with Shah Rukh Khan, which provided initial exposure through television and print media. By around 2002, having accumulated experience from over 250 auditions, Kapoor shifted focus toward lead acting opportunities, marking the end of his primary reliance on dance and modeling gigs. This progression underscored his entry via demonstrated talent in supportive performing arts roles rather than familial leverage alone.

Film debut and breakthrough roles (20032006)

Kapoor made his acting debut as the lead in the romantic comedy Ishq Vishk, released on 9 May 2003, directed by Ken Ghosh, where he portrayed Rajiv Mathur, a college student navigating young love. The film earned moderate box office success, collecting 7.75 crore nett in India and 12.25 crore worldwide against a budget that positioned it as an average performer. His performance garnered recognition, winning the Filmfare Award for Best Male Debut at the 49th ceremony, highlighting his initial appeal rooted in youthful charm and dance sequences.
Following his debut, Kapoor starred in Fida (20 August 2004), a romantic thriller directed by Ken Ghosh, playing Jai Kumar, caught in a love triangle involving crime. The film underperformed commercially, grossing 9.71 crore nett in India and 16.21 crore worldwide, receiving mixed reviews that praised action elements but critiqued narrative inconsistencies. Later that year, in Dil Maange More!!! (31 December 2004), directed by Ananth Mahadevan, he enacted Nikhil, a young man torn between three women; it proved a box office disappointment, earning 5.44 crore nett in India amid failure status, though it boosted co-star Ayesha Takia's visibility. Early critiques noted Kapoor's reliance on physical attractiveness and limited role depth, with industry perceptions questioning his "masculine" lead viability despite debut success, restricting offers to lighter romantic fare suited to his then 20-something image.
Kapoor achieved breakthrough with Vivah (10 November 2006), a family-oriented romantic drama by Sooraj R. Barjatya, portraying Prem, an idealistic suitor in an arranged marriage narrative emphasizing traditional values. The film marked a commercial triumph, grossing 31.56 crore nett in India (43.83 crore gross) and 48.28 crore worldwide, solidifying his image as a romantic hero appealing to family audiences through sincere portrayals of devotion and restraint. This success contrasted prior variability, demonstrating verifiable draw in wholesome roles while underscoring audience preference for his earnestness over edgier versatility at the time.

Film career

Rise with romantic and action films (20072013)

Kapoor's portrayal of the disillusioned Aditya Kashyap in the romantic comedy Jab We Met (2007), directed by Imtiaz Ali and co-starring Kareena Kapoor, marked a turning point, earning critical praise for its engaging narrative and the lead performances while achieving commercial success as a hit with 30.85 crore nett in India. His earlier 2007 release, the ensemble action-comedy Fool n' Final, however, flopped at the box office.
In 2009, Kapoor ventured into action territory with Kaminey, directed by Vishal Bhardwaj, where he essayed dual roles as stuttering twin brothers entangled in crime and politics; the film received acclaim for its gritty storytelling and Kapoor's intense acting but registered average box office returns of 41.3 crore nett in India. This period saw mixed results from other projects, including the romantic drama Kismat Konnection (2008), which performed averagely.
The year 2010 brought further variability: Chance Pe Dance and Paathshaala both flopped, while Badmaash Company, a crime comedy about opportunistic youth in 1990s Mumbai, achieved semi-hit status with 34.98 crore nett collections in India, buoyed by its novel premise despite script inconsistencies noted by reviewers. Kapoor's directorial debut involvement in script selection for these films highlighted a preference for character-driven stories over guaranteed commercial formulas, contributing to the era's box office inconsistencies rather than relying solely on his rising stardom.
By 2011, Mausam, a period romance directed by Pankaj Kapur and co-starring Sonam Kapoor, disappointed commercially as a flop with 29.53 crore nett in India, criticized for its protracted narrative despite ambitious visuals. The 2012 anthology Teri Meri Kahaani, featuring Kapoor in three eras-spanning roles opposite Priyanka Chopra, also underperformed. Kapoor rebounded in 2013 with the action-romance R... Rajkumar, directed by Prabhu Deva, which succeeded commercially through its high-energy stunts and mass appeal, underscoring selective hits amid the period's 3-4 moderate successes like Jab We Met, Kaminey, Badmaash Company, and this film, against several failures attributable to uneven project choices.

Critical recognition amid commercial variability (20142019)

Kapoor's performance in Haider (2014), directed by Vishal Bhardwaj as an adaptation of Shakespeare's Hamlet set in Kashmir, marked a shift toward intense, character-driven roles, earning widespread critical acclaim for his portrayal of a tormented protagonist grappling with loss and revenge. The film received multiple Filmfare Awards, including recognition for Kapoor's acting, though commercial performance was moderate with an India net collection of approximately 41 crore against a budget exceeding 37 crore. This role demonstrated Kapoor's willingness to prioritize artistic depth over mass appeal, contrasting with his earlier romantic leads, but highlighted dependency on strong directorial vision for success.
In Udta Punjab (2016), Kapoor essayed a raw, drug-addled rockstar, a role that involved significant physical transformation and drew praise for its unflinching depiction of addiction amid Punjab's substance crisis. The film's release faced intense censorship battles, with the Central Board of Film Certification demanding 89 cuts, including removal of "Punjab" references, overruled by the Bombay High Court on June 13, 2016, allowing minimal edits. Critically lauded for ensemble performances, including Kapoor's, it achieved average box office returns of around 60 crore net in India, underscoring acclaim without blockbuster earnings.
Subsequent projects revealed commercial inconsistency; Rangoon (2017) underperformed with under 50 crore net, criticized for narrative flaws despite Kapoor's efforts in a period drama. His supporting turn as Maharawal Ratan Singh in Padmaavat (2018) contributed to the film's massive 302 crore net haul in India, but as a cameo, it did little to stabilize his lead-vehicle trajectory. Batti Gul Meter Chalu (2018) flopped commercially at 40.88 crore net, hampered by weak scripting.
The period peaked commercially with Kabir Singh (2019), a remake of Arjun Reddy, where Kapoor's intense portrayal of a volatile surgeon grossed 278 crore net in India and 379 crore worldwide, his highest earner yet. While peers like trade analysts noted its box office dominance, the role sparked debates on glorifying toxicity, with mixed reviews questioning directorial choices over actor execution. Overall, from 2014 to 2019, Kapoor's films yielded a success ratio of about 33%, lower than implied pre-2014 consistency in hits like Jab We Met, reflecting over-reliance on auteur collaborations amid flops.

Shift to OTT and recent theatrical ventures (2020present)

Following the commercial disappointment of Jersey (2022), which grossed approximately 27.9 crore worldwide despite critical praise for Kapoor's performance as a struggling cricketer attempting a comeback, he pivoted toward over-the-top (OTT) platforms amid the lingering effects of the COVID-19 pandemic and uncertain theatrical recoveries. This shift allowed access to broader audiences without box-office dependencies, as Kapoor noted in interviews emphasizing content-driven experiences over release formats.
Kapoor's OTT debut came with the crime thriller series Farzi (2023) on Amazon Prime Video, where he portrayed a counterfeiter navigating moral ambiguities, achieving 37.1 million viewers and ranking as India's most-watched Hindi web series that year. The success prompted a second season, for which Kapoor reportedly earned 40-45 crore, his highest per-project fee, underscoring OTT's viability for sustained engagement amid theatrical volatility. Later that year, he starred in the action film Bloody Daddy on JioCinema, a Hindi remake of the French comedy Raid Dingue, playing a corrupt NCB officer in a high-stakes family rescue; it garnered 7 Filmfare OTT nominations, including for Kapoor's lead role, though reviews highlighted its formulaic intensity over innovation. This dual OTT output marked a strategic diversification, capitalizing on streaming's lower financial risks compared to theaters, where post-pandemic footfalls had declined due to audience preferences for home viewing.
His theatrical return arrived with the romantic science-fiction comedy Teri Baaton Mein Aisa Uljha Jiya (2024), co-starring Kriti Sanon as a humanoid robot, which earned 133-144 crore worldwide, qualifying as a moderate success by recovering costs through a mix of novelty and star appeal but falling short of blockbuster thresholds.
In 2025, Kapoor led the action thriller Deva, released on January 31, portraying a memory-impaired cop reinvestigating a murder in a remake of the Malayalam film Mumbai Police; while praised by some for his intense physicality evoking Kabir Singh-era vigor, it underperformed commercially and drew criticism for narrative inconsistencies and dated tropes. Projects like Shootout at Byculla, a crime drama based on 1990s Mumbai gang wars directed by Sanjay Gupta, remain in development, signaling continued blending of OTT experimentation with selective big-screen risks to mitigate flops' impacts. This era reflects a pragmatic response to industry dynamics, where OTT provides creative freedom and data-driven validation, contrasting theaters' high-stakes dependence on mass appeal.

Controversies

Cinematic disputes and censorship issues

Udta Punjab (2016), starring Shahid Kapoor as a rockstar entangled in Punjab's drug trade, encountered substantial censorship hurdles from the Central Board of Film Certification (CBFC). The board initially denied certification on June 9, 2016, demanding 89 cutsincluding excision of all references to "Punjab" (appearing 178 times), expletives, drug consumption scenes, and political allusionsto prevent alleged glorification of substance abuse and harm to the state's reputation. Producers, including Anurag Kashyap's Phantom Films, contested this in the Bombay High Court, asserting the demands undermined the film's evidentiary basis in Punjab's opioid epidemic, where surveys indicated approximately 15% of the population aged 1160 exhibited current substance dependence.
Kapoor publicly supported the legal challenge, posting the eventual 'A' certificate online with the caption "#win" after the court intervened. On June 13, 2016, the court overruled the CBFC, approving release within 48 hours with merely one cut (a profanity) and deeming the board's stipulations excessive and unsupported by certification guidelines prioritizing artistic expression on social issues. The ruling balanced regulatory aims to curb drug portrayal against filmmakers' claims of causal realism in depicting addiction's societal toll, without disclaimers or location alterations.
The dispute amplified pre-release buzz but coincided with piracy setbacks, as a full version leaked online two days before the June 17, 2016, debut, potentially eroding box-office returns amid prolonged certification delays. No comparable certification battles marked Kapoor's other projects, though he later reflected on escalating regulatory caution stifling politically sensitive narratives akin to his earlier Haider (2014).

Portrayals of masculinity and public backlash

Shahid Kapoor's portrayal of the titular character in Kabir Singh (2019), a Hindi remake of the Telugu film Arjun Reddy, centered on a brilliant but self-destructive surgeon whose obsessive love leads to substance abuse, aggression, and controlling behavior toward his partner. The film faced widespread backlash from critics who accused it of glorifying toxic masculinity, misogyny, and abusive relationships, with claims that it normalized stalking, violence, and patriarchal entitlement as romantic ideals. Feminist commentators argued that the narrative reinforced harmful stereotypes by depicting the female lead's eventual reconciliation with the protagonist without sufficient condemnation of his actions, potentially influencing impressionable audiences to view such dynamics as acceptable.
Despite the criticism, Kabir Singh resonated strongly with audiences, achieving nett collections of 278.24 crore in India and 379.02 crore worldwide, marking Kapoor's highest-grossing film and demonstrating broad empirical appeal beyond elite critique. This success mirrored the original Arjun Reddy's performance, suggesting the portrayal captured culturally familiar traits of intense, flawed masculinity in South Asian romantic contexts rather than fabricating them for sensationalism. Kapoor defended the role by emphasizing character realism, noting in December 2024 that "people like Kabir Singh exist and girls fall for them," positioning the film as an unjudgmental depiction of human dysfunction rather than endorsement, with the story ultimately showing the protagonist's personal consequences.
In October 2025, Kapoor's father, veteran actor Pankaj Kapur, reinforced this view amid renewed debate, stating that "toxic masculinity exists" in society and that the film reflected observable realities without promoting them, praising his son's performance for authentically embodying such complexities. Kapur argued the portrayal served artistic truth over moralizing, countering accusations of glamorization by highlighting the character's ultimate redemption through self-reckoning. This defense aligned with audience reception data, where word-of-mouth drove sustained box office runs despite polarized reviews, indicating selective outrage from progressive circles overlooked the film's cautionary elements and widespread identification with its raw emotional intensity.
Kapoor's public comments on masculinity extended to advocating vulnerability in men during a December 2024 interview, where he shared instances of crying on set due to heartbreak and described emotional openness as "more attractive than aggression," aiming to challenge stigmas around male expressiveness. These remarks drew online backlash, with netizens trolling him as performative or inconsistent given his prior intense roles, framing the advocacy as opportunistic amid post-Kabir Singh scrutiny. Yet, the trolling appeared disproportionate to the film's proven commercial validation, underscoring a pattern where portrayals of unfiltered male agency provoke ideological critique while empirical audience embraceevidenced by remakes' cross-regional successreveals deeper cultural realism in depicting possessive passion and relational volatility over sanitized ideals.

Personal life

Pre-marital relationships

Shahid Kapoor's most prominent pre-marital relationship was with actress Kareena Kapoor, which began around 2004 during the filming of Fida and lasted approximately four to five years. The couple publicly acknowledged their romance, appearing together at events and in media, including promotions for films like 36 China Town in 2006.
Their relationship ended in 2007 amid reports of infidelity and growing closeness between Kareena Kapoor and her Tashan co-star Saif Ali Khan, drawing significant media attention and speculation. The breakup occurred shortly after completing Jab We Met, a film that captured their on-screen chemistry but highlighted off-screen tensions.
Following the high-profile split, Kapoor was linked to co-stars such as Vidya Balan during the 2008 production of Kismat Konnection, though both parties described their interactions as platonic and professional, dismissing romance rumors as unsubstantiated. Similar unconfirmed speculations arose with actresses like Priyanka Chopra and Sonakshi Sinha, often tied to on-set collaborations rather than verified personal involvement. 
Post-2007, Kapoor adopted a more reserved approach to his personal life, prioritizing privacy amid the intrusions of fame and focusing on career stability, which marked a shift toward less publicized romantic associations.

Marriage, family, and lifestyle

Shahid Kapoor married Mira Rajput in an arranged ceremony on July 7, 2015, facilitated through their shared affiliation with the Radha Soami Satsang Beas community. At the time, Kapoor was 34 years old and Rajput was 21, resulting in a 13-year age difference that drew public commentary and led Kapoor to express semi-embarrassment over the gap in a prior interview. The couple has maintained a low-profile family life since, prioritizing privacy amid Kapoor's public career.
The Kapoors have two children: daughter Misha, born in August 2016, and son Zain, born on September 5, 2018. In 2025 reflections, Mira Rajput described the early years of marriage and motherhood as isolating, noting she observed friends pursuing independent ambitions while adjusting to family roles, though she later integrated into the household dynamic. Kapoor's stepmother, Supriya Pathak, praised Rajput in October 2025 for fostering family unity, describing her as akin to a daughter who bridged generational ties.
Their lifestyle emphasizes fitness, disciplined routines, and Rajput's Delhi origins, with the couple endorsing athleisure ventures aligned with wellness practices like vegetarianism and dance. Marking their 10-year anniversary on July 7, 2025, the Kapoors highlighted an enduring partnership resilient to external age-gap scrutiny.

Artistry and public perception

Evolution of acting style

Shahid Kapoor initiated his acting career with refined portrayals of romantic leads in the early 2000s, leveraging his dance-honed physicality to convey youthful charm and emotional accessibility. His training under choreographer Shiamak Davar from age 15 emphasized expressive movement, which informed early roles emphasizing grace and vitality over gritty realism. 
By the mid-2010s, Kapoor shifted toward raw, intense characterizations requiring method-like immersion, as evidenced by his transformative performance in Haider (2014), earning him the National Film Award for Best Actor at the 62nd National Film Awards for embodying psychological turmoil through physical and vocal distortion. This progression continued in roles demanding unfiltered aggression, such as in Kabir Singh (2019), where critics noted his willingness to prioritize character authenticity over polished appeal.
Kapoor has demonstrated versatility across more than ten genres, from romance to action-thrillers, often undergoing meticulous preparation to avoid typecasting, as he articulated in a 2017 interview favoring risk over repetition. However, some reviewers have critiqued inconsistencies, arguing that his stylistic shifts occasionally yield uneven emotional depth amid commercial pressures. In a 2024 discussion, Kapoor himself highlighted actors' tendencies to preserve self-image at the expense of role-driven changes, underscoring his preference for metamorphic commitments.
As of 2025, consensus among observers credits Kapoor's evolution with broadening his range from "chocolate boy" archetypes to method-infused intensity, though debates persist on whether selective role choices constrain further exploration of controversial psychological terrains. This trajectory reflects a deliberate pivot toward substantive transformation, validated by awards and peer acknowledgment, yet tempered by calls for sustained depth beyond high-stakes validations.

Media image, endorsements, and cultural impact

Shahid Kapoor has established a prominent media image as a style icon in Bollywood, recognized for his fashion evolution from early career struggles to trendsetting appearances at events like GQ Fashion Nights. In June 2025, he received the Most Stylish People's Star award at the Bollywood Hungama Style Icons Awards, highlighting his influence in minimalist and effortless elegance that appeals to male audiences. This persona extends to red-carpet looks and casual street style, positioning him as a benchmark for men's fashion in India.
Kapoor's endorsement portfolio reflects his mass appeal, with associations spanning consumer goods, apparel, and lifestyle brands such as Colgate, Dulux Paints, Brut, Tommy Hilfiger, Reebok, Levis, Fanta, and Aramusk deodorants. More recent deals include PokerBaazi.com and Furo Sports Shoes, with reports estimating each brand endorsement at approximately 3 crore as of 2025. These partnerships underscore his versatility in promoting products from hygiene to gaming, leveraging his romantic hero public perception to drive consumer engagement.
His cultural footprint is amplified by social media virality and fan loyalty, with over 47.7 million Instagram followers as of October 2025, enabling direct influence on trends. Dance videos, such as his 2024 bhangra to "Jee Karda" and wedding performances, frequently go viral, reinforcing his reputation as a skilled performer beyond films. Kapoor's humorous public persona further enhances his appeal, as seen in several light-hearted interviews. In a 2018 episode of BFFs with Vogue featuring his wife Mira Rajput, the couple displayed candid shading and sassy moments that highlighted their playful dynamic. Promotional interviews for his 2024 film Teri Baaton Mein Aisa Uljha Jiya with co-star Kriti Sanon also showcased entertaining banter. This sustained online engagement and charismatic media presence sustain his relevance amid career shifts to OTT platforms, where fan dedication persists despite variable box-office outcomes, as measured by consistent social metrics and endorsement value.

Awards and achievements

Major film awards

Shahid Kapoor earned his first major accolade with the Filmfare Award for Best Male Debut for his role as Rajiv Mathur in the romantic comedy Ishq Vishk (2003), presented at the 49th Filmfare Awards on 20 February 2004. This recognition highlighted his transition from a background dancer to a leading actor, selected by Filmfare's jury for his fresh portrayal of youthful romance.
In 2015, Kapoor won the Filmfare Award for Best Actor for his intense depiction of the tormented Haider Meer in Vishal Bhardwaj's adaptation Haider (2014), awarded at the 60th ceremony on 3 January 2015. The jury praised his nuanced performance drawing from Shakespeare's Hamlet, amid the film's five National Film Awards for technical and artistic elements, though Kapoor himself did not secure a personal National honor.
Kapoor received the Filmfare Critics Award for Best Actor in 2017 for playing the multifaceted Tommy Singh in the social drama Udta Punjab (2016), honored at the 62nd Filmfare Awards on 14 January 2017. This critics-voted prize underscored his shift to gritty, socially relevant roles, with the performance noted for its raw depiction of addiction and rebellion.
By 2025, these three Filmfare wins represent Kapoor's primary major film honors, alongside nominations including Best Actor for Kabir Singh (2019) at the 65th Filmfare Awards, where jury decisions favored other performances despite commercial success. His awards reflect jury evaluations of acting merit over popularity, with selections from Filmfare's panel of critics and industry experts.

Box office and commercial milestones

Shahid Kapoor's films have achieved a cumulative worldwide gross exceeding 2,000 crore across more than 25 releases since 2003, though over 60% have resulted in commercial flops or losses, underscoring inconsistent box office traction despite periodic blockbusters. His early hits like Vivah (2006), which earned 31.56 crore nett in India on a modest budget, and Jab We Met (2007), collecting 31.06 crore nett (equivalent to approximately 12 crore in unadjusted early-2000s terms before inflation), established him as a romantic lead capable of driving mid-budget successes.
Later breakthroughs included Kabir Singh (2019), his first solo-lead film to surpass 100 crore nett in India within five days, ultimately grossing 379 crore worldwide and marking a rare all-time blockbuster amid a string of underperformers like Mausam (2011, 31.56 crore nett, flop) and Shaandaar (2015, flop). Padmaavat (2018) contributed 302.15 crore nett in India as his highest-grossing ensemble role, but supporting parts in multi-starrers have amplified totals without guaranteeing lead-driven consistency.
