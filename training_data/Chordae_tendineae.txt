Chordae tendineae
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The chordae tendineae, also known as heart strings, are thin, fibrous cords composed primarily of dense regular connective tissue with collagen and elastic fibers that connect the cusps of the atrioventricular valvesspecifically the mitral valve in the left ventricle and the tricuspid valve in the right ventricleto the papillary muscles protruding from the ventricular walls. These structures originate as extensions of the ventricularis layer of the valve leaflets and insert into the free edges, rough zones, or ventricular aspects of the cusps, forming a branching network covered by endocardium to prevent the valves from prolapsing or inverting into the atria during ventricular systole. By tautening under tension as the papillary muscles contract, the chordae tendineae maintain proper valve coaptation and ensure unidirectional blood flow from the atria to the ventricles, while also contributing to the overall geometry and contraction efficiency of the ventricles.
Anatomically, chordae tendineae exhibit variability in number, thickness, and attachment patterns across individuals, with the left ventricle typically featuring 2 to 20 cords per papillary musclearising from the anterolateral and posteromedial musclesand the right ventricle having attachments to anterior, posterior, and septal muscles. Their composition includes crimped collagen fibers for extensibility and elastin for recoil, allowing them to withstand the high pressures of cardiac contraction without rupture under normal conditions. No two mitral valve apparatuses are identical in chordal arrangement, reflecting individual anatomical variations that influence surgical interventions.
Chordae tendineae are classified into multiple categories based on their morphological and functional characteristics, aiding in both anatomical description and clinical assessment. By origin, they are divided into apical (from the apex of papillary muscles) and basal (from the base); by insertion site, into true (to cusp leaflets), false (to ventricular walls or between muscles), cusp, cleft, and commissural types; by order of attachment, into first-order (marginal, thin cords to leaflet edges preventing prolapse) and second-order (thicker, to rough zones for tension distribution); by shape, including straight, fan-shaped, spiral, and irregular-web forms; and by constitution, as tendinous, muscular, or membranous. Marginal chordae, for instance, are notably numerous (mean of 22.63 per cusp) and critical for leaflet stability, while strut chordae may insert aponeurotically or muscularly.
In terms of function, these cords not only anchor the valves to avert regurgitation but also preserve the ventricular geometry during contraction, optimizing ejection fraction and left ventricular performance. Dysfunction, such as elongation, rupture, or congenital anomalies (e.g., in parachute mitral valve), can lead to valvular prolapse, mitral or tricuspid regurgitation, and heart failure, often requiring repair or replacement surgeries that prioritize chordal preservation to maintain ventricular function.

Anatomy

Gross Anatomy

The chordae tendineae are tendinous fibrous cords with limited elasticity that connect the papillary muscles of the ventricles to the leaflets of the atrioventricular valves, specifically the mitral and tricuspid valves, preventing leaflet eversion during ventricular contraction. These structures originate from the apex and sides of the papillary muscles and insert onto the ventricular surfaces of the valve leaflets, forming a supportive apparatus essential for valve competence. There is significant inter-individual variability in the number, thickness, and attachment patterns of chordae tendineae.
Chordae tendineae are classified based on their insertion sites and morphology into marginal chordae, which attach to the free edges of the leaflets; intermediate chordae, which insert into the rough zone or belly of the leaflets; basal chordae, which connect to the ventricular aspect near the annulus; and strut chordae, which are the thickest and most prominent, providing primary support to the anterior leaflet in the mitral valve. Marginal chordae are typically thin and numerous, ensuring precise alignment of leaflet edges, while strut chordae are robust and fewer in number, acting as key load-bearing elements.
In the mitral valve, chordae tendineae arise primarily from the anterolateral and posteromedial papillary muscles and insert into the anterior and posterior leaflets, with a fan-like arrangement that distributes tension to prevent prolapse. For the tricuspid valve, origins are from the anterior, posterior, and septal papillary muscles, with insertions onto the septal, anterior, and posterior leaflets, exhibiting a more complex, multi-directional fanning pattern. Typically, the mitral valve features 20-25 chordae tendineae, arranged in a balanced distribution across both leaflets, whereas the tricuspid valve has more, around 30-50 chordae tendineae, reflecting its three leaflets. The mitral chordae are generally more robust compared to those of the tricuspid valve, adapted to the higher left ventricular pressure gradients.

Histology

The chordae tendineae are composed primarily of type I collagen fibers, which form approximately 80% of the tissue's area and provide its primary structural integrity and tensile strength. These collagen fibers are densely packed and exhibit a crimped ultrastructure that allows for extensibility under load. Elastin fibers constitute a smaller proportion, roughly 5-10%, mainly in the outer sheath, contributing to recoil and flexibility, while proteoglycans and glycosaminoglycans (GAGs) make up the remainder, aiding in hydration and lubrication of the matrix.
At the cellular level, the chordae tendineae contain fibroblasts and tenocytes, specialized cells aligned parallel to the fiber axis, responsible for matrix maintenance and remodeling. These cells are sparse in the adult tissue, which is avascular, relying on nutrient diffusion from the surrounding endocardium for sustenance.
The ultrastructure features a layered organization, with a central core of parallel, tightly bundled collagen fibrils surrounded by a thinner sheath of looser connective tissue rich in elastin and interspersed collagen. This arrangement optimizes load distribution and prevents localized failure.
The histological properties confer high tensile strength, with a modulus of elasticity ranging from 0.5 to 2 MPa and ultimate tensile stress of approximately 10-20 MPa, enabling the chordae to resist peak systolic pressures up to 120 mmHg without rupture.
With aging, the chordae tendineae undergo progressive stiffening due to increased collagen cross-linking and loss of crimp in the fibrils, accompanied by accumulation of excess fibrous layers around the core, which reduces extensibility.

Function

Role in Valve Dynamics

The chordae tendineae play a critical role in the dynamic function of the atrioventricular valves during the cardiac cycle by anchoring the valve leaflets to the papillary muscles and ventricular wall, ensuring proper opening and closure to maintain unidirectional blood flow. In the mitral and tricuspid valves, these tendinous cords transmit forces from the contracting papillary muscles to the leaflets, preventing excessive motion that could lead to regurgitation. Their primary function is to stabilize the valves against the pressure gradients generated by ventricular contraction, allowing the leaflets to coapt effectively without prolapsing into the atria.
During ventricular systole, the chordae tendineae become taut as the papillary muscles contract at the onset of systole, pulling on the leaflets to keep them in a closed position and preventing prolapse, which would otherwise allow blood to regurgitate back into the atria. This tension ensures the leaflets are stabilized and aligned for coaptation before the pressure rise in the ventricle. Marginal chordae, attached to the free edges of the leaflets, primarily seal the commissures and edges during closure, while basal chordae support the leaflet surfaces against the developing ventricular pressure, collectively contributing to efficient valve sealing. In the mitral valve, this mechanism endures higher left ventricular pressures (up to 120 mmHg), whereas in the tricuspid valve, it operates under lower right ventricular pressures (up to 25 mmHg), yet both systems rely on similar tethering to avoid regurgitation.
In diastole, the chordae tendineae slacken as atrial pressures exceed early diastolic ventricular pressures (e.g., left atrium mean ~8 mmHg vs. left ventricle early diastolic ~0 mmHg; right atrium ~3-5 mmHg vs. right ventricle ~0-2 mmHg), with end-diastolic equilibration (left ventricular end-diastolic pressure ~5-12 mmHg, right ventricular end-diastolic pressure ~1-7 mmHg), permitting the leaflets to open fully and allow blood inflow from the atria to the ventricles. This cyclic tensioning and relaxation integrates the chordae with the overall valve apparatus, including brief attachments to the ventricular myocardium as described in gross anatomy, to support the heart's pumping efficiency across both mitral and tricuspid valves despite their pressure differences.

Biomechanical Properties

The chordae tendineae exhibit viscoelastic behavior, allowing them to accommodate low strain during diastole (typically less than 5% extension) while providing high stiffness during systole to resist peak forces of approximately 0.1-1 N per chorda. This time-dependent response is characterized by decreased extensibility at higher strain rates, enabling efficient energy dissipation and preventing excessive deformation under cyclic loading.
The stress-strain relationship of chordae tendineae is nonlinear, featuring an initial toe region of high compliance due to collagen fiber uncrimping, followed by a linear stiffening phase where the material behaves more elastically. Their fatigue resistance enables the chordae to endure approximately 3 billion loading cycles over a human lifetime, facilitated by the crimped structure of collagen fibers with wavelengths of 10-50 m that allow progressive fiber recruitment without premature failure.
Chordae tendineae display marked anisotropy, with significantly higher tensile strength along the longitudinal axis (failure loads of 1-20 N) compared to the transverse direction, reflecting the aligned orientation of collagen bundles that prioritize load-bearing in the primary direction of tension.
Mitral valve chordae tendineae are generally stiffer than those of the tricuspid valve, attributable to the higher transvalvular pressure gradients in the left heart, which necessitate greater mechanical robustness.

Development

Embryonic Formation

The chordae tendineae originate from the endocardial cushions within the atrioventricular canal during early embryogenesis, where endocardial cells undergo epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) to form mesenchymal populations around weeks 4-5 of gestation. These cushions serve as precursors for both the atrioventricular valve leaflets and the supporting apparatus, including the chordae tendineae, through subsequent remodeling processes driven by signaling pathways such as BMP and FGF. Additional pathways, including Notch and Wnt, contribute to cushion remodeling and valve stratification.
Tbx genes, particularly Tbx2, play critical regulatory roles in this remodeling, with Tbx2 induced by BMP2 to specify the valve-forming regions of the cushions and promote EMT via a feed-forward loop with Tbx3. Tbx5 contributes to left-right asymmetry in overall cardiac development. These transcription factors orchestrate the transformation of cushion mesenchyme into leaflet and chordae precursors, ensuring proper stratification and extracellular matrix deposition essential for tendon-like structures.
The differentiation sequence begins with the formation of papillary muscle buds from ventricular myocardial ridges around week 5, followed by chordal elongation from the ridges of the endocardial cushions in weeks 6-7. Both the mitral and tricuspid valves, including their chordae, develop from the fusion and remodeling of superior and inferior endocardial cushions, facilitating the establishment of attachments to the valve leaflets.
Primitive chordae tendineae begin forming as mesenchymal connections to the developing leaflets around weeks 10-13, marking the transition toward a functional subvalvular apparatus.

Postnatal Maturation

Following birth, the chordae tendineae of the heart valves undergo rapid adaptations to support the transition to postnatal hemodynamics and accommodate cardiac growth. In the neonatal period, these structures initially display hyperelasticity due to immature, loosely organized collagen fibers, allowing greater extensibility under load as evidenced in biomechanical studies of young human and ovine mitral chordae, which exhibit higher failure strains (0.3380.367) compared to aged counterparts. This hyperelasticity diminishes by approximately age 1 year through collagen maturation, including increased fibril alignment and extracellular matrix (ECM) remodeling, such as the deposition of organized type I collagen on the ventricular side and glycosaminoglycans on the atrial side, as observed in murine models where ECM stratification stabilizes by 1 week postpartum. Concurrently, chordae length increases substantiallyaligning with overall heart growth during infancyto maintain proper valve coaptation, with studies in animal models showing up to an 8-fold elongation in leaflet-to-papillary attachments by early postnatal weeks.
During puberty, the chordae tendineae experience further remodeling characterized by thickening and progressive stiffening, driven by hormonal influences that enhance collagen synthesis and deposition to match the surge in cardiac output and somatic growth. This period aligns with adolescent heart expansion, where valve components adapt to increased mechanical demands, potentially manifesting as transient leaflet redundancy in conditions like mitral valve prolapse linked to growth spurts. Hormonal factors, including sex steroids, modulate ECM composition; for instance, estrogen promotes collagen turnover and finer fibril organization, contributing to subtle structural refinements.
Age-related alterations in chordae tendineae involve progressive elongation and enhanced collagen cross-linking, leading to reduced extensibility and elevated rupture risk in the elderly. In human mitral chordae, aging is associated with diminished collagen crimp patterns and excess fibrous tissue accumulation, resulting in significantly higher stiffness (tangent modulus: 310405 MPa) and lower failure strains (0.1920.210), which compromise load-bearing capacity under chronic stress. These changes reflect cumulative mechanical remodeling and increase vulnerability to degeneration.
Sexual dimorphism is evident in chordae tendineae morphology, with females typically showing slightly finer, more unbranched chordae and differences in commissural spreading (e.g., anterolateral chordae extending to the middle third of the leaflet in 23.1% of cases versus 0% in males), potentially attributable to estrogen-mediated effects on collagen density and elasticity. Additionally, chordae tendineae exhibit biomechanical adaptations to applied loads through proteoglycan rearrangements that alter interfibrillar spacing and skewness angles, optimizing collagen organization and tensile strength.

Clinical Significance

Congenital Anomalies

Congenital anomalies of the chordae tendineae represent structural defects in the supporting apparatus of the mitral and tricuspid valves present at birth, often contributing to valvular dysfunction such as stenosis or regurgitation. These anomalies arise from disruptions in embryonic valve development, where failures in the formation of endocardial cushions and mesenchymal remodeling lead to abnormal chordal insertion or development. They are typically identified through echocardiography and can occur in isolation or alongside other cardiac malformations.
One prominent anomaly is the parachute mitral valve, characterized by a single papillary muscle receiving all chordae tendineae from both leaflets, resulting in fused and shortened chordae that restrict leaflet motion and cause mitral stenosis. Congenital mitral stenosis, of which the parachute type is a subtype, accounts for fewer than 0.5% of cases among pediatric patients with congenital heart disease.
The hammock mitral valve, also known as anomalous mitral arcade, features short, thick, and fused chordae tendineae that insert directly onto the ventricular wall or elongated papillary muscles, severely limiting valve opening and leading to regurgitation or stenosis. This rare malformation is frequently associated with ventricular septal defects and other left-sided obstructive lesions.
Absent or hypoplastic chordae tendineae result in inadequate support for the valve leaflets, predisposing to prolapse and significant regurgitation due to excessive leaflet excursion. These defects have been linked to genetic mutations, including those in NOTCH1 and GATA4, which play critical roles in cardiac valvulogenesis and are associated with various congenital valve disorders.
In the double-orifice mitral valve, the valve is divided into two separate orifices by a fibrous bridge, with duplicated chordal attachments supporting each orifice independently, potentially leading to regurgitation if the chordae are dysplastic. This anomaly often coexists with atrioventricular septal defects or other congenital heart diseases.
Congenital chordae tendineae anomalies occur in approximately 0.5% of cases of congenital heart disease, though isolated forms are rarer, and they are more prevalent in patients with trisomy 21, where atrioventricular septal defects frequently involve abnormal chordal structures. Prenatal diagnosis is achievable through fetal echocardiography, enabling early detection of these defects.

Acquired Pathologies

Acquired pathologies of the chordae tendineae encompass a range of non-congenital conditions that compromise their structural integrity and function, often leading to valvular dysfunction such as mitral regurgitation. These disorders arise from degenerative, infectious, or ischemic processes, resulting in elongation, rupture, or stiffening of the chordae, which disrupts the normal closure of the atrioventricular valves.
Rupture of the chordae tendineae represents an acute and severe acquired pathology, frequently triggered by trauma, infective endocarditis, or myocardial ischemia. In cases of blunt chest trauma, the mechanical stress can directly sever the chordae, while infective endocarditis promotes bacterial adhesion to the chordal surfaces, forming vegetations that erode the tissue and precipitate rupture. Ischemic events, particularly infarction of the papillary muscles, indirectly affect the chordae by stretching or avulsing them due to loss of muscular support. Marginal chordae, which attach to the leaflet edges, are particularly vulnerable to rupture owing to their thinner structure and higher stress concentration at the leaflet insertion site. This vulnerability often results in flail leaflet, where the unsupported leaflet segment inverts into the atrium during systole, causing acute and severe mitral regurgitation.
Chronic elongation of the chordae tendineae, leading to redundancy and prolapse, is predominantly associated with myxomatous degeneration, as seen in Barlow's disease. This degenerative process involves proteoglycan accumulation and disorganized collagen fibril architecture within the chordae, resulting in weakening, thinning, and excessive lengthening. The prevalence of myxomatous mitral valve disease, which affects the chordae, is estimated at 2-3% in the adult population, with Barlow's disease representing a more severe form characterized by diffuse myxoid infiltration. Collagen breakdown, mediated by elevated matrix metalloproteinase activity, further contributes to the loss of tensile strength and elastic recoil, allowing chordal redundancy that permits leaflet billowing into the atrium.
Calcification of the chordae tendineae is an age-related degenerative change that stiffens the structures, impairing their flexibility and contributing to restricted valve motion. This pathology is more prevalent in elderly individuals, particularly those over 70 years, where dystrophic calcification deposits along the chordae, often extending from the mitral annulus in cases of rheumatic heart disease. In rheumatic contexts, chronic inflammation leads to fibrosis and secondary calcification, though fusion of chordae is more common than isolated calcification. The stiffening reduces chordal compliance, exacerbating mitral regurgitation by preventing proper leaflet coaptation. Prevalence of such degenerative calcific changes in the mitral apparatus increases significantly with advanced age, aligning with broader valvular sclerosis patterns.
In infective endocarditis, the chordae tendineae serve as a nidus for bacterial colonization due to their endothelialized surfaces, leading to vegetation formation and subsequent tissue destruction. Vegetations, composed of fibrin, platelets, and microbes, adhere preferentially to damaged or turbulent flow areas on the chordae, causing localized inflammation and enzymatic degradation. This process frequently culminates in secondary rupture, with chordal involvement reported in up to 2.5% of endocarditis cases complicated by valvular perforation or insufficiency. The resulting regurgitation can be profound, necessitating urgent intervention.
Ischemic damage to the chordae tendineae often stems from papillary muscle infarction during acute myocardial infarction, which disrupts the anchorage and elongates the attached chordae. Posteromedial papillary muscle infarction is more common due to its single blood supply from the right coronary artery, affecting chordae function in 19-32% of post-infarction cases based on autopsy and imaging studies. This dysfunction manifests as dynamic mitral regurgitation, where ischemic stretching of the chordae impairs leaflet apposition during systole, occurring in approximately 10-20% of myocardial infarctions with significant papillary involvement.

Diagnosis and Treatment

Diagnosis of chordae tendineae disorders primarily relies on imaging modalities that assess valvular function and structural integrity. Transthoracic echocardiography (TTE) serves as the initial diagnostic tool, offering a sensitivity of approximately 65-97% for detecting ruptured or elongated chordae tendineae through visualization of mitral regurgitation jets and leaflet abnormalities, while transesophageal echocardiography (TEE) provides superior resolution with near 100% sensitivity and specificity compared to surgical findings. Clinical presentation often includes a holosystolic murmur at the apex radiating to the axilla due to mitral regurgitation, accompanied by symptoms of heart failure such as dyspnea, fatigue, and orthopnea in acute cases. Electrocardiography (ECG) may reveal signs of ischemia if the rupture is secondary to myocardial infarction, though it lacks specificity for direct chordal assessment. Cardiac magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) complements echocardiography by enabling detailed tissue characterization of the chordae tendineae, papillary muscles, and leaflets, particularly in cases of prolapse or degeneration where fibrosis or thickening is suspected.
Treatment strategies for chordae tendineae rupture emphasize mitral valve repair over replacement to preserve native anatomy and function. Surgical repair techniques include chordal plication for elongated chordae, where redundant tissue is folded and sutured, or artificial chordae replacement using expanded polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) sutures to restore leaflet support, achieving high success rates in experienced centers. For mitral valve prolapse involving chordal elongation, mild cases are managed with watchful waiting and medical therapy to control symptoms and prevent progression, while severe prolapse warrants surgical intervention such as mitral annuloplasty to reshape the annulus or the edge-to-edge Alfieri stitch to approximate leaflets and reduce regurgitation. In patients at risk for infective endocarditis complicating chordal pathology, prophylactic pharmacotherapy with antibiotics is recommended prior to dental or invasive procedures.
Emerging therapies focus on minimally invasive options to expand treatment eligibility. Transcatheter chordal replacement systems, such as the ChordArt device, are under investigation in clinical trials as of 2025, delivering artificial chordae via catheter to address prolapse or flail leaflets without open surgery, showing promising feasibility in early studies. Prognosis following repair is favorable, with success rates exceeding 95% in non-elderly patients and freedom from moderate-to-severe recurrent mitral regurgitation around 93% at 10 years, though complications like reoperation occur in 5-10% of cases due to suture failure or progressive degeneration.
