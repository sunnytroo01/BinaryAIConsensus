1838

1x
 1838 (MDCCCXXXVIII) was a common year in the 19th century marked by pivotal advancements in communication and transportation, alongside major political upheavals and human tragedies. In the United States, Samuel Morse conducted the first public demonstration of his electric telegraph on January 6 at Speedwell Ironworks in New Jersey, transmitting messages over two miles of wire and foreshadowing the transformation of long-distance communication. In Britain, Queen Victoria was crowned on June 28 at Westminster Abbey, ushering in the Victorian era characterized by imperial expansion and social reform, though the ceremony itself was marred by logistical mishaps. Concurrently, the forced removal of the Cherokee Nation intensified with federal troops beginning roundups in May, initiating the Cherokee segment of the Trail of Tears that resulted in thousands of deaths from disease, exposure, and starvation during relocation to Indian Territory. These events, amid ongoing conflicts like the Mormon War in Missouri and the presentation of the People's Charter advocating democratic reforms in Britain, underscored a year of technological promise juxtaposed against ethnic cleansing and nascent labor movements.

Events

JanuaryMarch

On January 6, Samuel F. B. Morse conducted the first public demonstration of his electric telegraph system at the Speedwell Iron Works in Morristown, New Jersey, transmitting signals over a distance of about 1,700 feet using electromagnetic principles. This event marked a foundational step in the development of long-distance communication technology, though commercial implementation occurred later.
On January 10, a major fire ravaged London's financial district, destroying Lloyd's Coffee Housea key center for marine insuranceand the Royal Exchange, causing extensive property damage estimated in the hundreds of thousands of pounds.
On January 12, Joseph Smith, founder of the Latter Day Saint movement, and Sidney Rigdon fled Kirtland, Ohio, for Missouri amid internal church conflicts and apostasy, relocating the church headquarters westward.
On January 27, Abraham Lincoln, then 28 years old and an Illinois state legislator, delivered his Lyceum Address to the Young Men's Lyceum in Springfield, Illinois, cautioning against the dangers of mob violence and emphasizing the need for strict adherence to the rule of law to preserve democratic institutions.
In February, the Weenen Massacre occurred on February 17, when Zulu forces under King Dingane killed approximately 532 Voortrekkers (Dutch settlers), along with Khoikhoi and Basuto individuals, near the Tugela River in present-day South Africa, escalating tensions in the Great Trek.
On February 16, the Kentucky legislature enacted a law allowing women to attend public schools under certain conditions, marking an early legislative step toward female education in the United States.
During the ongoing Upper Canada Rebelliona series of uprisings against British colonial rule that began in 1837rebels and American sympathizers launched incursions into Canadian territory. On March 3, fighters raided Pelee Island in Lake Erie, engaging British forces in a brief battle that resulted in three British deaths and seven injuries before the attackers retreated.
On March 8, the United States Mint in New Orleans officially commenced operations, striking its initial batch of 30 silver dimes as part of efforts to address coinage shortages in the growing southern economy.
On March 6, Austrian playwright Franz Grillparzer's tragedy Weh dem, der lgt! (Woe to Him Who Lies!) premiered at the Burgtheater in Vienna, receiving acclaim for its exploration of truth and deception.

AprilJune

On April 4, 1838, the British paddle steamer SS Sirius departed Cork, Ireland, for New York, completing the first transatlantic crossing primarily under steam power in 18 days and 10 hours, arriving on April 22 despite exhausting coal supplies and resorting to sails for part of the journey. Four days later, on April 8, Isambard Kingdom Brunel's larger steamship SS Great Western embarked from Bristol, England, on its maiden transatlantic voyage, reaching New York on April 23 after 15 days, demonstrating the viability of regular steam-powered ocean travel. These crossings, occurring amid a informal race between the vessels, reduced transatlantic travel time significantly compared to sailing ships and foreshadowed the decline of sail-dominated maritime commerce.
The National Gallery in London, housed in a neoclassical building designed by William Wilkins at Trafalgar Square, opened to the public on April 9, displaying 38 paintings purchased by the British government as a national collection to promote public access to art. On April 16, France began the Pastry Waralso known as the First Franco-Mexican Warby deploying a naval squadron under Admiral Charles Baudin to blockade Mexican Gulf Coast ports, demanding compensation for damages to French citizens' property, including a Paris ptissier named Boulanger whose shop in Mexico City had been looted during 1828 unrest, alongside broader indemnities totaling 3 million pesos that Mexico had defaulted on since independence. The blockade escalated into bombardment of Veracruz in November, but initial actions in April strained Mexico's economy and highlighted European powers' use of gunboat diplomacy to enforce claims against weaker states.
A devastating fire broke out in Charleston, South Carolina, on April 27, destroying over 300 buildingsincluding much of the commercial districtand rendering hundreds homeless, with losses estimated at $7 million in an era when fire suppression relied on manual bucket brigades and limited water supplies. On April 30, Nicaragua formally declared independence from the Federal Republic of Central America, dissolving the union established in 1823 and reflecting regional fragmentation amid political instability and economic disputes.
In May, U.S. Army General Winfield Scott issued Order No. 25 on May 17, initiating the forced roundup of approximately 16,000 Cherokee from their ancestral lands in Georgia, Tennessee, Alabama, and North Carolina into temporary stockades, commencing the Trail of Tears relocation to Indian Territory (present-day Oklahoma) under the 1830 Indian Removal Act. The operation, involving over 7,000 troops, aimed to enforce the controversial Treaty of New Echota (1835), signed by a minority Cherokee faction without tribal consensus, leading to immediate deaths from disease and exposure in camps, with total Cherokee mortality reaching about 4,000 by 1839 due to harsh conditions, inadequate provisions, and winter marches.
On June 10, eleven European stockmen led by John Fleming massacred at least 28 Wirrayaraay Aboriginal people, mostly women, children, and elderly, at Myall Creek station in New South Wales, Australia, in retaliation for prior conflicts over land and livestock; the subsequent trial and conviction of seven perpetrators marked the first time British colonial authorities executed white settlers for killing Indigenous Australians, though it did little to curb frontier violence. The coronation of Queen Victoria occurred on June 28 at Westminster Abbey, where the 19-year-old monarch was anointed and crowned in a ceremony lasting five hours, attended by nobility and featuring traditional rites, though marred by an incident where the elderly Archbishop of Canterbury mistakenly placed the ring on the wrong finger; Victoria documented the day in detail in her personal journal. This event solidified the young queen's position after her accession the previous year and symbolized continuity amid Britain's expanding industrial and imperial power.

JulySeptember

On July 3, the Iowa Territory was formally organized from the Iowa District of the Michigan Territory, encompassing present-day Iowa, most of Minnesota, and parts of the Dakotas, with its capital at Burlington. The Huskar Pit disaster occurred on July 4 in Silkstone, England, when a colliery flood killed 26 child workers aged 716 attempting to escape, highlighting hazardous child labor conditions in British coal mines. On July 7, the Central American Federation, established in 1823, effectively dissolved amid internal conflicts, with Honduras declaring independence, marking the end of the union of Guatemala, El Salvador, Honduras, Nicaragua, and Costa Rica.
In August, the British Empire abolished the apprenticeship system on August 1, granting full emancipation to over 800,000 former slaves across most colonies, including the Bahamas, fulfilling the Slavery Abolition Act of 1833 after a transitional period of forced labor. The 1838 Mormon War began on August 6 in Missouri with violent clashes during elections in Gallatin, escalating into militia conflicts between Latter-day Saints and state forces over settlement disputes and political influence, leading to thousands displaced.
September 3 saw abolitionist Frederick Douglass escape slavery in Maryland by boarding a train to New York disguised as a free Black sailor, using borrowed identification papers, and adopting the surname "Douglass" upon arrival. The Anti-Corn Law League was founded on September 18 in Manchester, England, by Richard Cobden and John Bright to campaign against protective tariffs on imported grain, advocating free trade to lower food prices amid industrial unrest. The London and Birmingham Railway, a pioneering 112-mile steam line, fully opened on September 17, connecting the cities and accelerating Britain's railway expansion with speeds up to 30 mph.

OctoberDecember

On October 1, during the First Carlist War, Carlist forces under Ramn Cabrera defeated a Liberal army led by General Isidro Pardias near the town of Maella in Aragon, Spain, resulting in the death of Pardias and a significant setback for the Liberal cause.
In Texas, the Killough Massacre occurred on October 5, when a group of Native Americans, likely including Cherokees and others, attacked the Killough settlement near present-day Jacksonville, killing at least 18 Anglo-American settlers and marking one of the last major Native American raids on white communities in East Texas.
The 1838 Mormon War in Missouri escalated with Missouri Governor Lilburn Boggs issuing an extermination order on October 27, authorizing the expulsion or elimination of Mormons from the state, followed by the Haun's Mill Massacre on October 30, where a militia from Livingston County attacked a Mormon camp, killing 17 men and boys and wounding 13 others.
The Pastry War between France and Mexico intensified in late 1838; after a blockade begun in April, French forces bombarded the fortress of San Juan de Ula near Veracruz on November 27, capturing it and pressuring Mexico to pay indemnities for damages to French citizens, including a pastry chef's claims.
In Pennsylvania, the Buckshot War erupted over disputed legislative election returns from Philadelphia; on December 4, rival factions clashed at the state capitol in Harrisburg amid riots, with Whig and Democratic supporters arming themselves, leading to a tense standoff resolved by compromise but highlighting partisan divisions.
On December 16, in what is now KwaZulu-Natal, South Africa, a Voortrekker commando of about 464 Boers under Andries Pretorius decisively defeated an estimated 10,000 Zulu warriors led by King Dingane at the Battle of Blood River, suffering no fatalities while inflicting around 3,000 Zulu casualties, a pivotal event in the Great Trek securing Boer expansion inland.
In Central America, Honduras formally declared independence from the Federal Republic of Central America on November 5, contributing to the federation's dissolution amid regional instability.

Date unknown

Swedish chemist Jns Jakob Berzelius coined the term "protein" in 1838, deriving it from the Greek proteios meaning "primary" or "of the first rank," to denote nitrogen-rich organic substances identified by Dutch chemist Gerardus Johannes Mulder as fundamental to living organisms. This nomenclature emphasized their essential role, based on empirical analyses showing consistent elemental compositions across animal and plant materials like blood, egg whites, and silk.
In the United States, the precursor to Duke University was established in Randolph County, North Carolina, initially as Brown's Schoolhouse by educator Brantley York under Methodist auspices, evolving into Union Institute to provide education amid frontier conditions. This institution laid groundwork for higher learning in the region, reflecting broader 19th-century efforts to expand access despite limited resources.

Deaths

JanuaryJune

On January 6, Samuel F. B. Morse and Alfred Vail conducted the first public demonstration of the electric telegraph at the Speedwell Iron Works in Morristown, New Jersey, transmitting messages over a distance of about 1,700 feet using electromagnets and a code of dots and dashes. This event marked a pivotal advancement in long-distance communication technology, building on Morse's earlier private tests.
January 26 saw Tennessee enact the first state-level prohibition law in the United States, classifying the sale of spirituous liquors in taverns and stores as a misdemeanor punishable by fine, reflecting growing temperance sentiments amid concerns over alcohol's social impacts.
On January 27, Abraham Lincoln addressed the Young Men's Lyceum in Springfield, Illinois, in a speech titled "The Perpetuation of Our Political Institutions," cautioning against the dangers of mob rule and vigilantism eroding reverence for law, at a time of rising lynchings and social unrest in the frontier states.
In May, the London Working Men's Association published the People's Charter, outlining six demands for parliamentary reform including universal male suffrage, secret ballots, and equal electoral districts, igniting the Chartist movement among British working classes dissatisfied with the limited franchise under the 1832 Reform Act.
Beginning May 26, U.S. federal troops and Georgia state militia forcibly rounded up approximately 15,000 Cherokee into stockades near present-day Chattanooga, Tennessee, initiating the coerced relocation process under President Martin Van Buren's enforcement of the 1835 Treaty of New Echota, despite opposition from Cherokee leaders like John Ross; this phase preceded the overland marches that autumn, during which thousands perished from disease, exposure, and hardship.
June 28 brought the coronation of Queen Victoria at Westminster Abbey, over a year after her accession upon the death of William IV; the 19-year-old monarch was anointed and crowned in a ceremony lasting five hours, symbolizing the transition to the Victorian era amid pomp and some logistical mishaps due to inadequate rehearsals.

JulyDecember

On July 4, 1838, the Iowa Territory was formally organized by an act of the U.S. Congress, separating it from the Wisconsin Territory and establishing its government with Robert Lucas as the first territorial governor; the territory then spanned from the Mississippi River westward to the Missouri River and northward to the Canadian border. That same day, a severe thunderstorm caused flooding at the Huskar Pit coal mine near Silkstone, Yorkshire, England, where water poured into the workings through a ventilation shaft; 26 child laborers, aged 7 to 12 and employed as hurriers and trappers, drowned while fleeing underground.
August 1, 1838, marked the end of the four-to-six-year apprenticeship period mandated by the Slavery Abolition Act of 1833, resulting in the full emancipation of roughly 800,000 formerly enslaved individuals across British colonies in the Caribbean, Cape Colony, and elsewhere, though many former slaves faced ongoing economic exploitation on plantations.
On September 3, Frederick Douglass (then Frederick Bailey), a 20-year-old enslaved man in Baltimore, Maryland, escaped bondage by disguising himself as a free Black sailor, using forged identification papers and free Black accomplices to travel by train and ferry to Wilmington, Delaware, and then New York City, where he joined the abolitionist movement.
During the First Carlist War, Carlist insurgents under General Ramn Cabrera ambushed and routed a Liberal column led by General Javier de Pardias near Maella, Aragon, on October 1, killing Pardias and inflicting heavy casualties that disrupted government supply lines in eastern Spain.
October 5 saw the Killough Massacre in northwestern Cherokee County, Republic of Texas, where a war party of approximately 60-100 Cherokee, Kiowa, and Delaware raiders attacked the extended Killough family settlement, slaughtering 18 white settlers (including women and children) and taking one captive amid escalating frontier tensions over land and alliances.
The 1838 Mormon-Missouri conflict culminated in the Haun's Mill Massacre on October 27, when about 240 Missouri state militiamen from Daviess and Livingston counties assaulted the Mormon mill settlement on Shoal Creek, Caldwell County, killing 17 Latter-day Saint men and boys (including several children) and wounding at least 13 others in a rapid attack that lasted roughly 15 minutes and prompted widespread Mormon exodus from the state.
Federal enforcement of the 1835 Treaty of New Echota accelerated in October 1838, as U.S. Army General Winfield Scott oversaw the roundup of remaining Cherokee in the southeast into stockades earlier in the summer, followed by the departure of 11 paid detachment marches westward beginning that month; these journeys, part of the Trail of Tears, exposed 13,000-15,000 Cherokee to lethal conditions including dysentery, pneumonia, and hypothermia, with mortality estimates reaching 4,000-6,000 by spring 1839.
France initiated the Pastry War (also known as the First French Intervention in Mexico) in late November 1838, when Admiral Charles Baudin's squadron blockaded Veracruz and bombarded the fortress of San Juan de Ula after Mexico refused to pay 600,000 pesos in reparations for claims by French nationals, including damages to a Parisian pastry chef's Mexico City shop during 1828 riots; the conflict stemmed from broader Mexican debt defaults post-independence and unresolved diplomatic grievances.
On December 16, Voortrekker forces numbering 464 Boers, led by Andries Pretorius, repelled an assault by 10,000-15,000 Zulu warriors under King Dingane at the Ncome River (later called Blood River) in Natal, using a defensive wagon laager and accurate rifle fire to inflict 3,000 Zulu deaths with only three minor Boer injuries, thereby avenging earlier Zulu massacres of Boer parties and enabling Afrikaner settlement north of the Cape Colony.

Science, Technology, and Exploration

Key Inventions and Expeditions

In 1838, Samuel F. B. Morse conducted a public demonstration of his electric telegraph on January 6 in New York City, transmitting signals using a code of dots and dashes he developed to represent letters and numbers, marking a foundational step toward long-distance instantaneous communication. This invention built on earlier electromagnetic experiments but introduced practical electromechanical recording via an electromagnet and paper tape, enabling reliable message relay over wires. Morse's system addressed limitations of prior non-recording telegraphs, though commercial deployment followed in subsequent years after congressional funding in 1843.
Russian inventor Moritz von Jacobi developed electrotyping, a process using electrodeposition to create metal copies of relief surfaces like printing plates, which improved the efficiency of reproducing illustrations and text for books and maps. Jacobi's method involved depositing copper onto a wax mold via electrolysis, yielding durable duplicates that reduced manual engraving labor and enabled mass production of detailed images. This technique gained adoption in printing industries across Europe by the mid-19th century.
The United States Exploring Expedition, also known as the Wilkes Expedition, commenced on August 18, 1838, from Hampton Roads, Virginia, under Lieutenant Charles Wilkes, comprising six vessels to survey uncharted Pacific regions, protect American whaling interests, and collect scientific specimens. The fleet traversed South America, Antarcticawhere Wilkes claimed discovery of the continentand the Pacific Northwest, yielding extensive hydrographic charts, natural history collections, and ethnographic data that informed U.S. territorial claims and scientific institutions like the Smithsonian. Returning in 1842, the expedition documented over 200 new species and produced 19 volumes of reports, advancing oceanography and geography despite logistical challenges and crew hardships.
Joseph Nicollet led a U.S. Army-backed expedition in 1838 into the upper Mississippi River region, mapping the watershed between the Mississippi and Missouri rivers with precise astronomical observations and topographic surveys. Supported by the Corps of Topographical Engineers, Nicollet's team employed barometric measurements and celestial fixes to delineate boundaries, contributing foundational data for future western expansion and federal land management.

Conflicts and Controversies

Major Disputes and Resolutions

The Aroostook War, occurring from 1838 to 1839, involved a non-violent border confrontation between the United States, primarily the state of Maine, and the United Kingdom over the ambiguous northeastern boundary established by the 1783 Treaty of Paris. Tensions escalated in late 1838 when Maine authorities arrested British lumbermen for operating in disputed territory along the Aroostook River, prompting militia mobilizations on both sides, with approximately 10,000 American and several thousand British troops deployed. No shots were fired in major engagements, but the standoff highlighted unresolved ambiguities in the St. Croix River delineation; a temporary armistice was arranged in March 1839 through diplomatic intervention by General Winfield Scott and Lieutenant Governor John Harvey, averting escalation. The dispute's core issues persisted until the Webster-Ashburton Treaty of 1842 delineated the border, ceding significant territory to Maine while securing British navigation rights on the St. John River.
In Mexico, the Pastry War erupted in 1838 as France demanded reparations for damages to its citizens' property during Mexico's internal upheavals, including a claim by Parisian pastry chef Boulanger for 60,000 pesos in losses from 1828 riots. After Mexico's default on a prior 1832 indemnity agreement, France blockaded Veracruz in July 1838 and bombarded coastal fortifications, capturing the fortress of (/page/San Juan de Ula) on November 27. Mexican President Anastasio Bustamante mobilized defenses but faced naval inferiority; the conflict concluded in March 1839 with the Treaty of Veracruz, under which Mexico agreed to pay 3 million pesos plus interest, funded by loans from British bankers, effectively resolving French claims without territorial concessions.
The 1838 Mormon War in Missouri stemmed from escalating frictions between Latter-day Saints settlers in Caldwell and Daviess Counties and non-Mormon residents, fueled by the Mormons' rapid population growth, bloc voting practices, and perceived theocratic governance under Joseph Smith. Violence intensified after October elections in Daviess County, where Mormon participation led to brawls and subsequent raids on Mormon settlements; Missouri Governor Lilburn Boggs issued Executive Order 44 on October 27, authorizing militia use to expel or exterminate Mormons as necessary for public safety. The Haun's Mill massacre on November 30 resulted in 17 Mormon deaths by state militia, prompting surrender negotiations; the conflict resolved in late 1838 with the Saints' forced evacuation to Illinois under a non-aggression agreement, though legal challenges to Boggs' order persisted without reversal.
The forced Cherokee removal, culminating in the Trail of Tears starting May 1838, represented the U.S. government's resolution of land disputes in Georgia through the Indian Removal Act of 1830, disregarding the 1832 Supreme Court ruling in Worcester v. Georgia affirming Cherokee sovereignty. General Winfield Scott oversaw the roundup of approximately 16,000 Cherokees into camps, followed by marches westward; an estimated 4,000 to 6,000 perished from disease, exposure, and starvation en route to Indian Territory. This coercive relocation effectively ended legal resistance to white settlement on Cherokee lands, with the Treaty of New Echota (1835) providing nominal compensation of $5 million, though repudiated by the Cherokee National Council.
