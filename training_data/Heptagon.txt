 A heptagon is a polygon with seven sides and seven vertices in the Euclidean plane. The term derives from the Greek words hepta meaning "seven" and gonia meaning "angle." It is also occasionally referred to as a septagon, combining the Latin prefix sept- with the Greek suffix -gon.
The sum of the interior angles of any heptagon is 900 degrees, calculated using the general polygon formula (n2)180(n-2) \times 180^\circ(n2)180 where n=7n=7n=7. Heptagons are classified into regular and irregular types: a regular heptagon has all sides of equal length and all interior angles measuring approximately 128.57 degrees, while an irregular heptagon has sides and angles of varying measures. Unlike regular polygons with 3, 4, 5, 6, or 15 sides, a regular heptagon cannot be constructed using only a compass and unmarked straightedge due to the non-constructibility of the angle 2/72\pi/72/7 radians in classical geometry. Heptagons feature in mathematical studies of symmetry, tessellations, and Diophantine approximations, and they appear in designs such as certain Islamic geometric patterns and coin shapes.

Definition and Properties

Definition

A heptagon is a polygon with seven sides, seven angles, and seven vertices. The term derives from the Greek words "hepta," meaning seven, and "gnia," meaning angle. It first appeared in English around 1570, in Sir Henry Billingsley's translation of Euclid's Elements.
As a fundamental geometric figure, a heptagon qualifies as an odd-sided polygon, distinguishing it from even-sided polygons like quadrilaterals or hexagons. Heptagons are not inherently regularmeaning their sides and angles are not necessarily equalunless explicitly specified as such.
Heptagons are classified as simple polygons, which may be either convex (with all interior angles less than 180 degrees) or concave (with at least one interior angle greater than 180 degrees), in contrast to self-intersecting forms such as star heptagons. In a simple heptagon, the sides do not cross each other.

Basic Properties

A heptagon, as a simple polygon, consists of seven sides, seven vertices, and seven edges, forming a closed plane figure. The sum of its interior angles is always 900, derived from the general formula for polygons (n2)180(n-2) \times 180^\circ(n2)180 where n=7n=7n=7. Similarly, the sum of the exterior angles of any simple heptagon equals 360, regardless of regularity.
A convex heptagon features all interior angles measuring less than 180 and all diagonals lying entirely within the figure; it can be cyclic, inscribed in a circumscribed circle, if the vertices satisfy the necessary angular conditions. In contrast, a concave heptagon includes at least one reflex interior angle exceeding 180, with some diagonals potentially extending outside the polygon.
For irregular heptagons, side lengths must obey the polygon inequality, ensuring each side is shorter than the sum of the remaining six to form a closed shape without degeneracy. The interior angles vary in measure but collectively total 900, subject to convexity constraints where no angle surpasses 180 in convex cases. In the regular heptagon, all sides and angles are equal, providing a symmetric benchmark.

Regular Heptagon

Construction Methods

A regular heptagon cannot be constructed exactly using only a straightedge and compass, as proven by Pierre Wantzel in 1837 through the demonstration that the cosine of 2/72\pi/72/7 is a root of an irreducible cubic polynomial over the rationals, requiring field extensions of degree 3, which exceed the quadratic extensions permitted by such tools.
Exact constructions are possible using augmented tools that allow solving cubic equations. One such method employs a marked ruler in a neusis construction, where a ruler with two fixed marks is slid and rotated to align specific points, enabling the trisection of angles or direct location of heptagonal vertices; this approach, dating back to ancient Greek techniques and refined in the Renaissance, produces the precise side lengths and angles of a regular heptagon. Another exact method uses origami, leveraging paper folding to solve cubics via simultaneous creases that intersect at points satisfying higher-degree equations; a specific sequence of folds, as detailed by Thomas Hull, constructs a regular heptagon by iteratively bisecting angles and aligning edges to approximate the roots of the equation for cos(2/7)\cos(2\pi/7)cos(2/7), achieving exactness through the geometry of fold intersections.
Approximate constructions with straightedge and compass alone have been developed for practical drawing. Albrecht Drer's method from 1525 provides a simple approximation by starting with a circle, inscribing a regular hexagon, and using derived lengths to divide the circle into seven nearly equal arcs; the steps are as follows:

Draw a circle with center O and radius r; inscribe a regular hexagon with vertices labeled 1 through 6.

Connect every other vertex (1-3, 3-5, 5-1) to form an equilateral triangle.

Draw a radius from O to vertex 2, intersecting side 1-3 at point b.

Use the length from vertex 1 to b (approximately 0.866r) as the side length to mark seven points around the circle.

Connect these points to form the approximate heptagon.

This yields a heptagon with sides of length approximately 0.866r (six sides) and a base of 0.878r, with an angular error of about 0.2, suitable for artistic and architectural applications. Modern variants refine this by incorporating additional bisections for higher accuracy, though still inexact.

The perimeter $ P $ of a regular heptagon with side length $ s $ is given by $ P = 7s $.
The area $ A $ of a regular heptagon can be derived by dividing the figure into 7 congruent isosceles triangles, each sharing the center as a vertex and subtending a central angle of $ 2\pi/7 $ radians. The area of one such triangle with two sides equal to the circumradius $ r $ is $ \frac{1}{2} r^2 \sin(2\pi/7) $, yielding a total area of

In terms of the side length $ s $, the area is
A=74s2cot(7).

Geometric Approximations

Historical approximations for the dimensions of a regular heptagon have been essential for practical applications, given the challenges in exact construction. Heron of Alexandria approximated the side length $ s $ of a regular heptagon inscribed in a circle of radius $ r $ as $ s \approx \frac{7}{8} r \approx 0.875 r $, which introduces a relative error of about 0.84% compared to the exact value $ s = 2r \sin(\pi/7) \approx 0.8678 r $. This simple ratio was used in Heron's Metrica for area calculations and drawing purposes.
Ptolemy's Almagest offers a more precise approximation via his table of chords in a circle with diameter divided into 120 parts. For the central angle of approximately 5126', interpolation in the table yields a chord length of roughly 51;59 parts (or about 103.98 parts for the full chord), corresponding to $ s/r \approx 0.8665 $, with an error of less than 0.15%. This method facilitated astronomical and geometric computations by dividing the circle into seven nearly equal arcs.
Modern approximations leverage algebraic solutions to the heptagon's characteristic equations. The value $ \cos(2\pi/7) \approx 0.6234898019 $ is derived from solving the triple-angle formula $ \cos 3\theta = 4\cos^3 \theta - 3\cos \theta $ with $ \theta = 2\pi/7 $, leading to the cubic equation $ 8x^3 + 4x^2 - 4x - 1 = 0 $; numerical methods or series expansions provide high precision for $ s/r = 2 \sin(\pi/7) $. Richards' geometric method achieves an error under 0.1% through iterative compass adjustments approximating the required angle. Other approaches, such as Conway's neusis-based techniques adapted for approximation or analogs to the golden ratio using continued fractions for $ \cos(2\pi/7) $, enable accurate manual constructions with errors below 0.05%. For instance, a radical-based estimate simplifies to ratios like 1 : 0.8678 for side-to-radius, balancing simplicity and utility in design and engineering.

Symmetry Group

The symmetry group of a regular heptagon is the dihedral group D7D_7D7, which consists of 14 elements comprising all isometries that map the heptagon onto itself. This group is generated by a rotation rrr of order 7 and a reflection sss, satisfying the relations r7=s2=er^7 = s^2 = er7=s2=e and srs1=r1srs^{-1} = r^{-1}srs1=r1.
The rotational symmetries form a subgroup isomorphic to the cyclic group C7C_7C7, consisting of the identity and six non-trivial rotations by angles k3607k \times \frac{360^\circ}{7}k7360 for k=1,2,,6k = 1, 2, \dots, 6k=1,2,,6. Since 7 is prime, the rotational symmetry has order 7 with no proper subgroups other than the trivial one, meaning the heptagon lacks smaller repeating rotational units.
The seven reflections in D7D_7D7 are flips over axes of symmetry that pass through each vertex and the midpoint of the opposite side, as is characteristic of regular polygons with an odd number of sides. These reflections, when composed with rotations, generate the full dihedral group from the rotational subgroup C7C_7C7.
In orbifold notation, the symmetry group of the regular heptagon is represented as *77, indicating a sphere with two cone points of order 7, which captures the dihedral action including both rotations and reflections. The fundamental domain for this group action is a sector of $ \frac{360^\circ}{14} = \frac{180^\circ}{7} $, bounded by two adjacent reflection axes.

Diagonals and Related Figures

A regular heptagon possesses 14 diagonals, determined by the formula $ \frac{n(n-3)}{2} $ for an $ n $-gon with $ n = 7 $. These diagonals connect non-adjacent vertices and exhibit two distinct lengths due to the symmetry of the figure. The shorter diagonals span two sides (connecting vertices separated by two steps along the perimeter), while the longer diagonals span three sides (separated by three steps); the latter approach the diameter of the circumscribed circle but are identical in length to those spanning four steps, as the central angles are supplementary.
Assuming a circumradius $ r $, the length of the shorter diagonal is $ 2r \sin\left(\frac{2\pi}{7}\right) $, and the longer diagonal is $ 2r \sin\left(\frac{3\pi}{7}\right) $. Exact algebraic expressions for these lengths derive from solving the minimal polynomial $ x^3 + x^2 - 2x - 1 = 0 $, whose real root $ \eta \approx 1.24698 $ equals $ 2 \cos\left(\frac{2\pi}{7}\right) $; the other roots correspond to $ 2 \cos\left(\frac{4\pi}{7}\right) $ and $ 2 \cos\left(\frac{6\pi}{7}\right) $, enabling precise computation of the sine values via trigonometric identities. For instance, $ \sin\left(\frac{2\pi}{7}\right) = \sqrt{\frac{7 - \eta - \sqrt{(7 - 3\eta)(\eta + 1)}}{8}} $, though such forms emphasize the algebraic nature of heptagonal geometry over numerical approximation.
A notable figure related to these diagonals is the heptagonal triangle, formed by selecting vertices 1, 2, and 4 of a labeled regular heptagon (or any cyclic permutation thereof). Its sides consist of one heptagon side of length $ 2r \sin\left(\frac{\pi}{7}\right) $, one shorter diagonal, and one longer diagonal, yielding a scalene triangle with angles $ \frac{\pi}{7} $, $ \frac{2\pi}{7} $, and $ \frac{4\pi}{7} $ (approximately 25.71, 51.43, and 102.86). This obtuse triangle exhibits unique properties, such as being the only non-isosceles triangle with orthic similarity (where the orthic triangle is similar to the original) and serving as an extremal case in certain geometric inequalities, like the Dixmier-Kahane-Nicolas condition for triangle centers.
Pascal's theorem finds application in analyzing heptagon diagonals by considering hexagons inscribed in the circumcircle using six of the seven vertices; the theorem guarantees collinearity of intersection points of opposite sides, which, when applied successively around the heptagon, yields configurations relating diagonal intersections to conic properties and aids in deriving concurrency or collinearity results among diagonal segments. This connects to broader projective geometric constructions, such as transforming inscribed heptagons to circumscribed ones via Pascal lines.
The rotational and reflection symmetries of the heptagon dictate the arrangement and intersection patterns of these diagonals, generating 35 intersection points interior to the figure.

Star Heptagons

Schlfli Symbols and Forms

Star heptagons, or heptagrams, are classified using Schlfli symbols of the form {7/k}, where 7 denotes the number of vertices and k the step size in connecting them, with 1 < k < 7/2 and gcd(7,k)=1 to ensure regularity. The case k=1 yields the degenerate regular heptagon {7/1}, while the distinct star forms are {7/2} and {7/3}; the symbol {7/4} is equivalent to {7/3} via reciprocity, as {p/q} coincides with {p/(p-q)} up to orientation reversal.
The density of a star polygon {p/q} equals q, quantifying the winding number of its boundary around the center and the number of edge layers at interior intersections. Thus, {7/2} has density 2, with two edges crossing at each internal vertex figure, and {7/3} has density 3, featuring three edges per intersection point.
In uniform polytope notation, the vertex figure of a regular star heptagon is a dyada line segment of length 2cos(k/7)2 \cos(k \pi / 7)2cos(k/7) for unit edge lengthreflecting the local geometry at each vertex. Each form is self-dual up to orientation reversal under the dihedral group D7D_7D7.
Compound forms arise from combining these symbols on shared vertices, such as the heptagram {7/2} inscribed within the regular heptagon {7/1} via specific diagonals, or the star compound {7/2} + {7/3}, which overlays both stars to form a 14-edged figure exhausting all non-adjacent connections among the seven points.
Terminology varies, but both {7/2} and {7/3} are commonly termed heptagrams, with {7/3} sometimes called the great heptagram or septagram in certain contexts.

Construction and Properties

Star heptagons are constructed by placing seven equally spaced vertices on a circle and connecting every k-th vertex, where k=2 or k=3, producing the distinct forms denoted by the Schlfli symbols {7/2} and {7/3}. For the {7/2} form, this involves skipping one vertex at each step around the circle.
The resulting figure is a self-intersecting 7-pointed star in which the seven extended line segments cross at seven interior intersection points, forming a star outline composed of 14 distinct line segments visible due to the intersections.
When inscribed in a circle of radius $ r $, the side length $ s $ of the {7/2} heptagram is given by the formula
s=2rsin(27). s = 2 r \sin\left( \frac{2\pi}{7} \right). s=2rsin(72).
Exact expressions for this length derive from the minimal polynomial of the 7th cyclotomic field, specifically involving the roots of $ 8x^3 + 4x^2 - 4x - 1 = 0 $ related to $ \cos(2\pi/7) $.
Star heptagons exhibit non-convex geometry with self-intersections and possess isogonal symmetry, where the dihedral group $ D_7 $ acts transitively on the vertices, though they lack isohedral symmetry as the edges are not equivalently positioned relative to the intersections. The {7/3} form is the reciprocal of {7/2} in the sense of distinct density forms.
The interior vertex angle of a regular star polygon {n/k} is given by (n2k)180n\frac{(n - 2k) \times 180^\circ}{n}n(n2k)180. For {7/2}, this is approximately $ 77.14^\circ $. For {7/3}, it is approximately $ 25.71^\circ $.

Heptagons in Higher Geometry

Tiling and Packing Arrangements

Regular heptagons cannot form a monohedral tiling of the Euclidean plane because their interior angle of approximately 128.57 does not divide 360 evenly at vertices; specifically, 360 / 128.57 2.8, which is not an integer, preventing an integer number of heptagons from meeting without gaps or overlaps.
In the hyperbolic plane, regular heptagons tile periodically in the heptagonal tiling denoted by the Schlfli symbol {7,3}, where three heptagons meet at each vertex. A related uniform hyperbolic tiling is the triheptagonal tiling (3.7.3.7), which alternates regular triangles and regular heptagons around each vertex, forming a semiregular arrangement.
Star heptagons, such as the {7/3} heptagram, appear in hyperbolic tilings like the great heptagrammic tiling {7/3,3}, where three star heptagons meet at each vertex. In Euclidean contexts, {7/3} stars serve as motifs in Islamic geometric patterns, often placed within heptagonal grids to create intricate designs without fully tiling the plane.
For packing arrangements in the Euclidean plane, regular heptagons can be arranged in a quasi-hexagonal lattice, but gaps occur due to the angle mismatch, achieving a packing density lower than that of hexagons; projections of the hyperbolic heptagonal honeycomb {7,3,3} onto the Euclidean plane approximate dense coverings with distortions near the boundary.
Aperiodic tilings involving heptagons include sets inspired by Penrose tilings, where irregular heptagons combine with other polygons to force non-periodic coverage of the plane.

Role in Polyhedra

The regular heptagon does not appear as a face in any Platonic solid. This absence stems from the angle deficit at vertices: the interior angle of a regular heptagon is (5180)/7128.571(5 \times 180^\circ)/7 \approx 128.571^\circ(5180)/7128.571, so three such angles sum to approximately 385.714385.714^\circ385.714, exceeding 360360^\circ360 and yielding a negative deficit, which prevents convex closure in Euclidean space. Higher numbers of faces at a vertex would exacerbate this issue, while fewer than three cannot form a polyhedron.
In uniform polyhedra, regular heptagons feature primarily as bases in prisms and antiprisms, which possess 7-fold rotational symmetry. The heptagonal prism consists of two parallel regular heptagonal faces joined by seven square faces, with 14 vertices where three faces meet (two squares and one heptagon). The heptagonal antiprism, in contrast, connects two parallel regular heptagonal faces via 14 equilateral triangular faces, resulting in 14 vertices where four triangles and one heptagon alternate. These are the only uniform polyhedra with regular heptagonal faces, as higher symmetries like icosahedral do not accommodate 7-fold rotations.
Among non-uniform convex polyhedra with regular faces, the heptagonal pyramid (Johnson solid J14) incorporates a single regular heptagonal base capped by seven equilateral triangular faces, all edges equal in length. This structure has 8 vertices (7 on the base and 1 apex) and satisfies Euler's formula with 8 faces and 14 edges. No Johnson solids feature multiple regular heptagonal faces, as the geometry constraints limit such configurations to prismatic or pyramidal forms.
Star heptagons, such as the {7/2} or {7/3} heptagrams, do not appear in the KeplerPoinsot polyhedra, which rely on pentagrammic faces under icosahedral symmetry. Uniform star polyhedra with heptagonal faces are rare and typically abstract or non-convex compounds, lacking the self-intersecting density of icosahedral stars; examples remain limited to theoretical extensions beyond the 75 classical uniform polyhedra.

These coordinates ensure equal edge lengths and angles, facilitating embedding in 3D space for prisms, antiprisms, or pyramids while preserving regularity.

Applications and Examples

Empirical and Natural Occurrences

In architecture, heptagonal forms appear in certain Gothic cathedrals, particularly in the design of apses where seven radial chapels are arranged around a central circumference, necessitating geometric constructions to achieve the shape without direct measurement of the center. For instance, the apse of the Cathedral of Tortosa in Spain incorporates a heptagonal layout derived from medieval geometric principles, blending arithmetical and musical proportions such as 9/8 for its dimensions. This approach addressed the challenges of non-Euclidean figures like the heptagon, which lack straightforward construction methods in classical geometry.
Heptagonal shapes are also employed in modern structures for aesthetic and functional purposes, such as the Heptagon House in the United States, where chamfered floor plates and roofs create four heptagonal facades to optimize window placement and entry access. In numismatics, heptagonal coins provide tactile and visual distinction from round denominations; the British 50 pence coin, introduced in 1969, was the world's first seven-sided currency, designed as an equilateral-curve heptagon for easy identification by sight and touch while maintaining constant width for vending machines. Other examples include the Tanzanian 50 shilling coin, which adopts a similar heptagonal profile for anti-counterfeiting and accessibility features.
In crystallography, true seven-fold rotational symmetry is incompatible with periodic lattices due to the crystallographic restriction theorem, which limits symmetries to two-, three-, four-, and six-fold rotations in natural crystals. However, quasicrystalsaperiodic structures discovered in the 1980scan theoretically exhibit forbidden symmetries like seven-fold, though no stable examples have been observed experimentally as of 2025, with proposals focusing on boron-based materials featuring seven-membered rings. Rare defects or local approximations in quasicrystalline alloys may mimic heptagonal patterns, but these do not form extended 7-fold lattices.
Astronomical observations occasionally reveal heptagonal patterns through asterisms, such as the Winter Heptagon, formed when the bright planet Mars aligns with the Winter Hexagon asterismcomprising stars from Orion, Taurus, Auriga, Gemini, Cancer, and Canis Majorcreating a seven-sided figure visible in the northern winter sky during certain orbital positions. Planetary orbits, being elliptical, do not inherently produce heptagonal geometries, though Keplerian approximations or orbital resonances can yield polygonal-like configurations in multi-body simulations. In engineering, heptagonal lug nuts are utilized in automotive applications for secure wheel fastening, offering seven contact faces that distribute torque evenly and deter theft by requiring specialized sockets incompatible with standard hexagonal tools. These nuts, often forged from high-strength steel like SCM435, are common in aftermarket racing and performance vehicles to enhance grip and prevent loosening under vibration.

Symbolic and Cultural Uses

The number seven has long been regarded as sacred across various religions and philosophical traditions, symbolizing completeness, divinity, and cosmic order. In Islam, for instance, the Quran describes seven heavens, representing layers of spiritual realms that underscore the faith's cosmological framework. This reverence for seven extends to geometric forms like the heptagon and its star variant, the heptagram, which embody these qualities in visual symbolism.
In alchemy, heptagonal diagrams and the heptagram frequently appear to represent the seven classical planets and their corresponding metals, illustrating processes of transformation and the interconnected laws of the astral plane. Alchemists arranged the seven metalsgold, silver, copper, iron, tin, lead, and mercuryin heptagonal patterns to reflect planetary affinities and the stages of alchemical work, such as the seven steps toward enlightenment or transmutation into gold. The heptagram specifically evokes the positive aspects of these seven double laws, serving as a meditative tool for balancing spiritual and material elements.
The heptagram also carries protective connotations in religious and occult contexts. In Catholic tradition, it emerged as a symbol for warding off evil, linked to the seven days of creation and divine perfection. In heraldry, though less common than five- or six-pointed stars, the heptagram has been incorporated into emblems denoting spiritual safeguarding and harmony. In modern paganism and Wicca, it is known as the Faery Star or Elven Star, representing protection, elemental balance, and a connection to faerie realms or nature's mysteries; it is often invoked in rituals for its seven points aligning with directions, qualities, or chakras.
In the history of mathematics, the regular heptagon's constructibility with compass and straightedge was a significant challenge in classical geometry, evoking the limits of human reason and the divine irregularity of seven. Carl Friedrich Gauss's groundbreaking 1796 work on constructible polygons, developed at age 19, established the necessary conditions involving products of powers of two and distinct Fermat primes (such as 3, 5, 17, 257, and 65537), revealing why the heptagon, with its prime side count of 7 (not a Fermat prime), cannot be so constructeda theorem later rigorously proven by Pierre Wantzel in 1837. This theoretical boundary highlighted the heptagon's role in advancing algebraic number theory and cyclotomic fields, blending mathematical rigor with philosophical contemplation of perfection.
In art, the heptagon's intricate symmetry has inspired explorations of pattern and illusion, symbolizing infinite progression through star heptagons in various designs.
In contemporary culture, the heptagon appears in branding and gaming. Heptagon Capital, a London-based investment firm founded in 2005 managing approximately $15 billion in assets as of 2025, incorporates a heptagonal motif in its logo to signify stability and multifaceted growth. In role-playing games (RPGs), seven-sided dice (D7) are used for specialized mechanics requiring outcomes from 1 to 7, such as determining random events or attributes in systems like Dungeons & Dragons variants, despite their rarity compared to standard polyhedral sets; manufacturers like GameScience produce fair D7s via prismatic designs to ensure balanced rolls.
