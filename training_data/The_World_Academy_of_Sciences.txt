The World Academy of Sciences
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The World Academy of Sciences (TWAS) is a merit-based global science academy headquartered in Trieste, Italy, dedicated to advancing scientific capacity and excellence for sustainable development primarily in developing countries. Founded in 1983 by a group of eminent scientists from the global South under the leadership of Pakistani physicist Abdus Salam, a Nobel laureate, TWAS operates as an autonomous international organization and serves as a programme unit of UNESCO with support from the Italian government. Its core mission emphasizes strengthening research, education, policy influence, and diplomatic efforts to foster science and engineering for prosperity in the developing world, with over 1,300 elected fellows from more than 100 countries.
TWAS has established itself through targeted programmes, including postgraduate and postdoctoral fellowships for researchers from low-income nations, research grants, and prestigious awards such as the TWAS-Lenovo Prize for groundbreaking contributions in fields like geosciences and the TWAS Medals for sustained excellence in scientific disciplines. These initiatives prioritize empirical scientific advancement over geopolitical agendas, enabling collaborations that address challenges like food security, climate adaptation, and technological innovation in underrepresented regions. While TWAS maintains a focus on merit-driven selection, its emphasis on the global South reflects a causal recognition of disparities in scientific infrastructure, without evident major controversies undermining its operations or credibility.

History

Founding by Abdus Salam

The World Academy of Sciences (TWAS) was founded in 1983 in Trieste, Italy, under the leadership of Abdus Salam, the Pakistani theoretical physicist and 1979 Nobel Laureate in Physics, who served as its inaugural president. Salam, motivated by persistent barriers to scientific advancement in developing countriessuch as limited access to resources, training, and global networkssought to create an academy that would prioritize capacity building and self-reliance among scientists from the global South. This initiative built directly on his earlier establishment of the International Centre for Theoretical Physics (ICTP) in 1964, also in Trieste, which had already demonstrated the potential for international collaboration to bridge North-South scientific divides by hosting researchers from underrepresented regions.
The founding was driven by a core group of 42 eminent scientists, predominantly from developing nations, who elected themselves as initial fellows to form an autonomous organization focused on elevating science in the Third World without reliance on Western-dominated institutions. Among these, nine were Nobel laureates, reflecting the high caliber of early membership and Salam's ability to rally global expertise toward equitable scientific progress. The emphasis on independence stemmed from recognition that existing international academies often marginalized Southern perspectives, prompting the need for a dedicated voice to advocate for policy, research, and education tailored to developing contexts.
Headquarters in Trieste were selected for practical reasons, utilizing ICTP's established facilities for administration and operations, while symbolically reinforcing Salam's vision of a neutral hub for cross-continental scientific exchange amid Cold War-era geopolitical tensions. This location facilitated immediate functionality without the delays of building new infrastructure, aligning with Salam's pragmatic approach to accelerating impact in underserved regions.

Early Development and Institutionalization

Following its founding in 1983 by a group of scientists from developing countries under the leadership of Nobel laureate Abdus Salam, TWAS transitioned from an informal network into a structured academy, officially inaugurated in Trieste, Italy, in 1985 with 42 elected fellows, nine of whom were Nobel laureates. This initial cohort was selected strictly on the basis of scientific and engineering excellence, prioritizing merit over geographic quotas to establish credibility among global scientific communities. The academy's headquarters were established on the campus of the International Centre for Theoretical Physics in Trieste, leveraging the site's existing infrastructure for international scientific collaboration.
Early operations faced hurdles such as limited initial funding and the broader challenges of intellectual isolation, inadequate research resources, and brain drain affecting scientists in developing nations during the 1980s. These were mitigated through sustained support from the Italian government, which covered operational expenses from inception and facilitated the Trieste base amid geopolitical instabilities in parts of the developing world that hindered cross-border scientific exchanges. Italian backing ensured autonomy while enabling institutionalization, avoiding reliance on unstable donor funding from developing regions.
A pivotal advancement occurred in 1991 with an agreement designating TWAS as a UNESCO programme unit, whereby UNESCO administered its funds and personnel without infringing on the academy's independent governance. This affiliation enhanced TWAS's legitimacy and access to international resources, facilitating a shift toward formalized operations while maintaining meritocratic principles in fellowship elections. Membership expanded from the founding group of 42 to hundreds by the 1990s, reflecting growing recognition and the academy's focus on building a robust cadre of high-caliber scientists from the Global South.

Key Milestones and Expansion

In 2003, TWAS established five regional offices to strengthen its presence and activities across developing regions, covering sub-Saharan Africa, the Arab region, Central and South Asia, East and Southeast Asia and the Pacific, and Latin America and the Caribbean; these entities were redesignated as TWAS Regional Partners in 2018 to reflect their collaborative role in program delivery and South-South cooperation. In 2004, the organization formally changed its name from the Third World Academy of Sciences to The Academy of Sciences for the Developing World, underscoring its commitment to fostering scientific capacity beyond geopolitical labels of the era.
During the presidency of Brazilian mathematician Jacob Palis (20052010), TWAS pursued aggressive expansion, including enhanced fellowship recruitment and program scaling, which contributed to a membership surpassing 1,000 scientists from over 70 countries by the early 2010s; Palis's tenure also positioned TWAS as a key affiliate within the InterAcademy Partnership (IAP), a global network of academies formed to address science-policy interfaces. In 2012, another name evolution occurred, adopting The World Academy of Sciences to signal broader international outreach while retaining its core focus on developing-world science advancement.
Post-2020, TWAS intensified alignment with United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), particularly in health, climate, and capacity building, amid responses to global crises like the COVID-19 pandemic, which prompted targeted fellowships and virtual collaborations; the organization's 2022 annual report documented cumulative support through over 2,700 research grants since inception, aiding scientists in low-resource settings. By the mid-2020s, fellowship numbers exceeded 1,200, with ongoing elections expanding representation from underrepresented nations such as Bolivia, Libya, and Zambia.

Mission and Objectives

Core Mandate for Developing Countries

The World Academy of Sciences (TWAS) was established in 1983 with the foundational mandate to promote scientific capacity and excellence for sustainable development in developing countries, emphasizing the role of endogenous scientific advancement in addressing challenges such as hunger, disease, and poverty. This purpose centers on building self-reliant knowledge economies in the global South, where strengthened science and engineering capabilities enable nations to generate solutions tailored to local contexts rather than relying indefinitely on external aid or imported expertise. By prioritizing investments in homegrown research and talent retention, TWAS counters the emigration of skilled scientistsoften termed brain drainthrough mechanisms that support excellence-driven progress without ideological impositions like competence-undermining quotas.
Central to this mandate is the conviction that scientific capacity acts as a causal driver of prosperity, fostering innovation from within developing regions via South-South and South-North collaborations that exchange verifiable knowledge and best practices. TWAS underscores meritocratic selection of scientists based on demonstrated achievements, ensuring that resources amplify high-impact work conducted in situ to yield empirical benefits like improved agricultural yields or health outcomes, as evidenced by targeted capacity-building efforts since inception. This approach aligns with causal realism by treating science as an impartial instrument for material advancement, detached from narratives of inherent victimhood that might perpetuate dependency cycles.
In practice, the mandate manifests in a commitment to policy influence that integrates rigorous evidence into national strategies, promoting institutional reforms for research environments conducive to sustained output over short-term interventions. Developing countries' most effective path to equitable growth, per TWAS's framework, lies in expanding opportunities for local scientists to harness neutral scientific methods for real-world problem-solving, thereby reducing reliance on exogenous models that overlook regional causal dynamics. This focus has persisted as TWAS's guiding principle, adapting to global shifts while maintaining an unwavering emphasis on verifiable, excellence-oriented capacity enhancement.

Strategic Focus Areas

TWAS prioritizes the advancement of basic sciences as foundational to scientific capacity-building in developing countries, emphasizing disciplines such as mathematics, physics, chemistry, and biology to underpin applied innovations and evidence-based policymaking. This focus stems from the recognition that robust foundational research enables long-term technological self-reliance, rather than reliance on imported solutions, as articulated in the academy's advocacy for basic sciences to address sustainable development challenges in the global South. Engineering and interdisciplinary approaches, including those targeting climate adaptation through empirical modeling and resource management, receive targeted support to foster practical outcomes in under-resourced settings, though TWAS maintains emphasis on verifiable empirical validation over speculative trends lacking rigorous data.
To tailor initiatives to regional contexts, TWAS operates through five regional partner academieslocated in Pretoria (Sub-Saharan Africa), Alexandria (Arab region), Bangalore (Central and South Asia), Beijing (East and Southeast Asia-Pacific), and Rio de Janeiro (Latin America and the Caribbean)which customize programs for local institutional weaknesses, such as limited infrastructure in least developed countries. These partners facilitate capacity enhancement in under-resourced universities and research centers by prioritizing scalable interventions aligned with regional scientific ecosystems, thereby mitigating one-size-fits-all approaches that often falter in diverse governance environments.
Following the adoption of the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals in 2015, TWAS has integrated SDG-related objectives into its Sixth Strategic Plan (2021-2025), which outlines 10 priorities including recognition of excellence, nurturing emerging scientists, and linking science to policy for sustainable prosperity. This shift underscores science, technology, and innovation's role in goals like poverty reduction and environmental resilience, yet highlights constraints in contexts of institutional instability or corruption, where top-down interventions yield limited causal impact without local ownership and empirical monitoring. The plan advocates for diplomacy and evidence-based strategies to overcome such barriers, prioritizing investments that demonstrably build enduring scientific institutions over short-term aid dependencies.

Organizational Structure

Governance and Leadership

The governance of The World Academy of Sciences (TWAS) centers on a Council, nominated and elected by its Fellows to ensure leadership selected on scientific merit rather than political or ideological criteria. The Council, comprising distinguished Fellows, oversees strategic policy, program approval, and organizational direction, with terms typically lasting four years; the current Council assumed office in January 2023 and will serve until the end of 2026. This merit-driven process, rooted in Fellows' expertise, minimizes external capture and prioritizes evidence-based decision-making drawn from empirical scientific contributions. The president, elected from or by the Council, leads executive functions, succeeding Abdus Salam's foundational tenure (19831996) through successive merit-selected leaders focused on advancing science in developing regions.
Operational management falls to the TWAS secretariat in Trieste, Italy, which handles administrative, financial, and programmatic execution under Council guidance, enabling efficient implementation without diluting strategic autonomy. This division separates policy formulation from routine operations, allowing the Council to concentrate on high-level oversight while the secretariat supports global initiatives. Trieste's location, established since TWAS's inception, facilitates proximity to affiliated institutions like the International Centre for Theoretical Physics, enhancing logistical independence.
TWAS maintains autonomy as an independent international body, with UNESCO administering staff and funds since 1991 under an agreement that provides infrastructural support without granting veto authority over scientific or governance decisionsa key distinction from intergovernmental organizations subject to member-state consensus. This status, formalized as a UNESCO programme unit, safeguards TWAS from bureaucratic overreach while leveraging UNESCO's resources for sustainability. Council composition emphasizes representation from developing countries, with selection criteria centered on verifiable scientific output and impact, rather than quotas or non-merit factors, to align leadership with causal drivers of scientific progress in underrepresented regions.

Membership Categories and Selection

TWAS membership is divided into two primary categories: Fellows and Young Affiliates, with the former discontinued Associate Fellow category since 2014. Fellows are elected scientists recognized for significant contributions to science at the highest international standards, serving for life and limited to up to 50 new elections annually to preserve selectivity. Eligibility requires nominees to be under 70 years of age at the time of nomination, demonstrate verifiable excellence through peer-reviewed publications and impactful research, and preferably hold membership in a recognized national science academy; those residing in developed countries must actively promote scientific advancement in developing nations.
The selection process for Fellows emphasizes peer-driven rigor, beginning with nominations by at least two existing TWAS Fellows, submitted by March 31 each year via a formal certificate. Nominations undergo scrutiny by specialized Membership Advisory Committees, followed by review from the TWAS Council, with final approval determined by a majority vote of attending Fellows at the annual General Meeting. This multi-stage evaluation prioritizes empirical evidence of contributions, such as publication records and influence on science in developing contexts, ensuring no dilution from non-scientific criteria like political affiliations.
Young Affiliates target early-career scientists under 40 years old from developing countries, selected to inject fresh perspectives into TWAS activities for a renewable six-year term, after which they transition to alumni status. Up to 25 are chosen annuallyfive per each of TWAS's five Regional Partners (in Alexandria, Bangalore, Beijing, Pretoria, and Rio)based on outstanding accomplishments, including at least 10 peer-reviewed publications and a minimum of three years' experience in a developing country. Selection by these regional bodies focuses on potential for South-South collaboration and verifiable research impact, maintaining standards through documented evidence rather than subjective endorsements.

Programs and Initiatives

Awards and Prizes

The TWAS Awards, previously designated as TWAS Prizes, honor individual scientists from developing countries for exceptional contributions to scientific knowledge or its application in sustainable development across nine fields: agricultural and nutrition sciences, biological sciences, chemical sciences, earth sciences and environmental studies, engineering sciences, mathematical sciences, medical and health sciences, physics, and social sciences (excluding economics). These awards, initiated in the 1980s and formalized since 1985, require recipients to have resided and conducted research in a developing country for a minimum of ten years immediately prior to nomination, thereby incentivizing long-term scientific commitment and talent retention in regions often facing brain drain.
Each of the nine awards includes a cash prize of USD 10,000, with selections occurring biennially to ensure rigorous evaluation of empirical impacts such as advancements in local agriculture, health interventions, or environmental management. For instance, South African epidemiologist Quarraisha Abdool Karim received the award for developing microbicide technologies that reduced HIV transmission risks among women in sub-Saharan Africa, yielding measurable public health outcomes through clinical trials demonstrating efficacy in preventing heterosexual transmission. Similarly, Chinese materials scientist Zhao Dongyuan was recognized for innovations in mesoporous materials that enhance drug delivery and catalysis, with applications improving industrial processes in resource-limited settings. Nominations emphasize documented research outputs, peer-reviewed publications, and tangible benefits to developing economies, prioritizing causal evidence of progress over advocacy or non-scientific criteria prevalent in some international recognitions.
This structure underscores TWAS's focus on merit-based validation of sustained, on-the-ground scientific endeavors, countering tendencies in certain global awards to favor narrative alignment rather than verifiable results from controlled studies or applied innovations. Over decades, the awards have spotlighted researchers addressing region-specific challenges, such as climate-resilient crops or infectious disease modeling, fostering endogenous capacity without reliance on expatriation.

Research Grants and Fellowships

The TWAS Research Grants Programme supports high-level basic research projects in developing countries through competitive funding allocated to individual scientists and research groups. Grants for individuals, awarded in fields such as biology, chemistry, mathematics, and physics, provide up to USD 30,000 over 24 months, with allocations limited to USD 20,000 for equipment and consumables; funds may also cover specialized literature but exclude salaries, travel, or computing devices. Eligibility restricts awards to nationals of 66 science and technology-lagging countries who hold PhDs, are under 45 years old, and are affiliated with institutions in those nations, prioritizing early-career researchers from least developed countries and women. Similar grants for research groups extend up to USD 54,000, focusing on unit-level projects in the same disciplines to enhance laboratory capabilities without supporting personnel costs. As of 2024, TWAS has disbursed over 3,000 such grants, emphasizing outcome-oriented basic research to bolster scientific infrastructure in targeted regions.
TWAS fellowships facilitate advanced training and knowledge exchange for scholars from the global South, structured to encourage temporary mobility rather than permanent relocation. The TWAS-UNESCO Associateship Scheme appoints researchers for three years, enabling two visits of two to three months each to designated advanced centres in developing countries, covering travel and a USD 300 monthly stipend while host institutions provide subsistence; this promotes short-term collaboration and skill transfer back to home institutions. PhD fellowships, numbering approximately 160 annually through partnerships with eight institutions in six countries, support early-career researchers from developing nations in pursuing doctoral studies at host universities, requiring preliminary acceptance and alignment with South-South cooperation goals. Postdoctoral fellowships, around 100 per year via nine partners in five countries, offer similar opportunities for recent PhD holders to conduct research abroad, with programme designs that incentivize return to originating countries to apply acquired expertise locally. Since inception, over 1,000 PhD fellows have graduated through these initiatives, contributing to more than 2,000 peer-reviewed publications, underscoring a focus on repatriation and domestic capacity enhancement.

Capacity Building and Training

TWAS supports long-term capacity building in developing countries through postgraduate fellowships that enable scientists to conduct empirical research at host institutions in the Global South, such as those in Brazil, China, Egypt, India, Malaysia, Mexico, Pakistan, and Thailand. These programs award 100 to 160 PhD fellowships annually, prioritizing advanced training in natural sciences to foster independent inquiry and data-driven analysis over memorization-based education systems common in some regions. Participants from least developed countries receive full funding for up to four years, including stipends and research allowances, to build skills in hypothesis testing and experimental validation.
The TWAS Visiting Expert Programme pairs senior scientists with institutions in developing nations to deliver extended teaching and mentorship, aiming to establish enduring research networks rather than isolated events. Experts commit to periods of one to two weeks, focusing on curriculum development and hands-on laboratory guidance to enhance local faculties' ability to train future researchers in rigorous methodologies. This initiative has facilitated over 1,400 exchange visits since inception, contributing to sustained institutional improvements in scientific pedagogy.
Additional efforts include skill-building workshops and support for regional conferences, such as the 2025 workshop in Nairobi, Kenya, which target early-career researchers with practical training in data analysis and problem-solving. These activities address gaps in critical thinking by emphasizing evidence-based approaches, countering prevalent rote learning inherited from colonial-era systems in parts of Africa and Asia. TWAS has also trained over 750 individuals in science-related skills, including diplomacy components integrated into broader professional development. Partnerships with entities like the Chinese Academy of Sciences and South Africa's National Research Foundation extend these opportunities through joint MSc and PhD schemes tailored for scholars from low-income nations.

Science Diplomacy and Policy Engagement

TWAS promotes science diplomacy through targeted activities that connect scientific expertise with policymaking, emphasizing the role of evidence in addressing global challenges. A flagship initiative is the annual AAAS-TWAS Course on Science Diplomacy, launched in 2014, which trains early- to mid-career professionals from developing and transitional countries in applying scientific knowledge to diplomatic and policy contexts. By 2025, the program had produced over 388 alumni actively influencing national and international policies, with innovations like scientist-decisionmaker pairing models enhancing direct engagement between experts and officials.
In collaboration with UNESCO, under whose auspices TWAS operates, the academy contributes to evidence-based policy frameworks at the United Nations level, including inputs on sustainable development goals and open science initiatives that prioritize data-driven strategies over unsubstantiated advocacy. TWAS also partners with the African Union to advance science and technology policies aimed at regional development, fostering dialogues that link research investments to measurable economic outcomes through workshops and advisory exchanges. These efforts underscore a focus on causal mechanisms, such as institutional prerequisites for translating scientific advice into growth, rather than generic promotion of investment without regard for enabling conditions.
However, the impact of such diplomacy remains limited in non-meritocratic systems prevalent in many developing countries, where governance failuressuch as weak rule of law and corruptionimpede the implementation of policy recommendations, as empirical analyses of aid and technical assistance consistently demonstrate. Evaluations of international science-policy interfaces, including those involving TWAS, highlight that without addressing these institutional constraints, efforts to bridge science and decision-making often yield marginal results, prioritizing verifiable institutional reforms alongside advisory roles to ensure causal effectiveness.

Partnerships and Funding

Institutional Collaborations

TWAS maintains a close operational partnership with the Abdus Salam International Centre for Theoretical Physics (ICTP), both founded by physicist Abdus Salam in 1983 to advance science in developing countries. The organizations share facilities on the ICTP campus in Trieste, Italy, where TWAS headquarters are located, enabling joint training programs such as specialized ICTP-TWAS courses in physics, mathematics, and related fields for scientists from the Global South. This synergy facilitates shared resources for research workshops, postdoctoral training, and capacity-building initiatives targeted at developing world researchers.
To extend its reach across the developing world, TWAS operates through five regional partners that handle nominations for membership categories like Young Affiliates and support localized science promotion. These include TWAS-SAREP in Pretoria, South Africa (hosted by the Academy of Science of South Africa); TWAS-AREP in Alexandria, Egypt (Bibliotheca Alexandrina); TWAS-CASAREP in Bangalore, India (Jawaharlal Nehru Centre for Advanced Scientific Research); TWAS-SAPREP in Beijing, China (Chinese Academy of Sciences); and TWAS-LACREP in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil (Brazilian Academy of Sciences). Each partner screens and nominates up to five early-career scientists annually for TWAS Young Affiliate status, organizes regional conferences and workshops on topics like sustainable development, and disseminates opportunities for collaborative research networks.
TWAS holds membership in the InterAcademy Partnership (IAP), a global network comprising over 140 national, regional, and global academies representing more than 30,000 scientists. IAP's secretariat is co-hosted by TWAS on the ICTP campus, fostering joint efforts in science diplomacy, policy advice, and evidence-based solutions to global challenges such as climate change and health crises. This affiliation enables TWAS to coordinate with peer academies on cross-border initiatives, including shared advocacy for science funding in developing regions and collaborative statements on international scientific priorities.

Financial Sources and Sustainability

The World Academy of Sciences (TWAS) receives its core operational funding primarily from the Italian government, which has supported the organization since its founding in 1983 and hosts its headquarters in Trieste. This includes an initial establishment grant in the 1980s and ongoing contributions formalized by a 2004 Italian law guaranteeing continuous financial support for TWAS activities. In recent program periods, Italy has accounted for a substantial portion of total funding, often comprising around 75% alongside Swedish contributions from the Swedish International Development Cooperation Agency (Sida).
Additional funding comes from UNESCO, under whose administrative umbrella TWAS has operated since a 1991 agreement that transferred responsibility for funds and personnel management. UNESCO facilitates contributions from various multilateral donors, including the OPEC Fund for International Development, the Islamic Development Bank, and national agencies such as Germany's Federal Ministry of Education and Research. These donor agencies provide targeted grants for specific initiatives, supplementing but not replacing core government support.
TWAS generates minimal self-sustaining revenue through endowments or internal mechanisms, relying instead on external grants and partnerships that expose it to fluctuations in donor priorities and geopolitical shifts. For instance, reliance on bilateral donors like Sida introduces risks from changes in foreign aid policies, while Italy's stable commitmentbolstered by lawmitigates some volatility but ties long-term viability to a single host nation's fiscal and political continuity. Without broader diversification into merit-based, unrestricted endowments, TWAS faces potential constraints on independence, as funding dependencies could influence program emphases amid evolving global development agendas.

Impact and Assessment

Measurable Achievements

TWAS has supported the training of over 1,400 PhD-level scientists from developing countries through its fellowship programs, enabling advanced research and capacity building in the global South. Graduates of these programs have advanced to leadership positions in scientific institutions, including presidencies of national societies such as the Brazilian Society of Pharmacology and Experimental Therapeutics.
The academy has awarded more than 2,700 research grants to scientists in developing countries, primarily for high-level projects in biology, chemistry, mathematics, and physics, which equip laboratories and foster outputs like peer-reviewed publications and patents relevant to Sustainable Development Goals such as health, clean energy, and sustainable agriculture. These grants, often up to $15,000 per project, target nationals of developing countries at early- to mid-career stages holding PhDs.
In sub-Saharan Africa, TWAS-funded initiatives have enhanced institutional research capabilities; for example, grants to Senegalese institutions have supported solutions in energy efficiency, public health diagnostics, and agricultural sustainability, demonstrating targeted impacts on regional scientific productivity.
TWAS has also provided over 2,300 postdoctoral fellowships to developing-world researchers, allowing 3- to 12-month advanced training abroad and strengthening long-term research ecosystems.

Effectiveness Evaluations and Criticisms

Evaluations of TWAS programs, such as those funded by Sida from 2017 to 2021, indicate modest effectiveness in enhancing individual researcher capacities in developing countries, with 147 individual and 81 group research grants awarded, leading to over 135 publications and improved application quality over time. However, impacts remain primarily at the individual level, with limited evidence of broader systemic changes in scientific ecosystems, as 36.69% of grantees reported employment or promotions but only 41.21% noted ripple effects beyond their personal work. Conferences and training events, including 31 Regional Conferences for Young Scientists engaging 657 participants, achieved high participant satisfaction (55% fully met expectations) and motivation (87% inspired), yet follow-up on networks was inadequate, hindering sustained collaboration77.51% of grantees did not co-author with peers from other TWAS projects.
Critics have highlighted TWAS's limited success in reversing brain drain, with 38% of Sida-supported grantees planning to seek opportunities abroad, potentially exacerbating talent outflows due to enhanced mobility without corresponding institutional anchors in home countries. Grants and fellowships face underutilization risks from local infrastructure deficits and bureaucratic delays, including equipment sustainability issues tied to maintenance costs and procurement hurdles, which undermine long-term value. Mobility funds were underused, and broader North-South cooperation has yielded little tangible progress despite decades of efforts, reflecting persistent divides in resources and priorities.
Concerns include potential elite capture in contexts of weak governance, where benefits accrue to connected researchers rather than broadly building capacity, compounded by insufficient female role models and hierarchical event structures. Scalability is constrained by high administrative costs for regional partners, variable local contexts, and failure to address root institutional weaknesses, such as poor facilities and isolation, limiting transformative effects compared to endogenous or market-driven scientific advancements. While cost-effective through partnerships, return on investment questions arise when weighed against domestic investments in donor nations, as systemic barriers prevent widespread replication or enduring institutional reforms.
