 Cytokine release syndrome (CRS) is a systemic inflammatory condition triggered by the excessive and rapid release of cytokinessmall proteins that mediate immune responsesfrom activated immune cells, resulting in widespread inflammation that can range from mild to life-threatening organ dysfunction. This syndrome, sometimes referred to as a form of cytokine storm, typically manifests within hours to days following an inciting event and is characterized by elevated levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as interleukin-6 (IL-6) and interferon-gamma (IFN-).
CRS most frequently occurs as an adverse effect of advanced immunotherapies, particularly chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapies used in treating hematologic malignancies like B-cell lymphomas and leukemias, where engineered T cells rapidly proliferate and release cytokines upon targeting tumor cells. It can also be induced by other immune-activating agents, such as bispecific T-cell engagers (e.g., blinatumomab), monoclonal antibodies (e.g., rituximab), severe infections including COVID-19, or autoimmune disorders that overwhelm the immune system. Pathophysiologically, the cascade involves T-cell and macrophage activation, leading to endothelial damage, vascular leakage, and potential progression to hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH) in severe cases.
Clinically, CRS presents with a spectrum of symptoms including high fever, chills, fatigue, nausea, hypotension, tachycardia, and respiratory distress, with neurologic complications like confusion or encephalopathy occurring in approximately 20-30% of CAR T-cell therapy cases, often termed immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS). Diagnosis relies on clinical assessment post-exposure to a trigger, supported by laboratory findings such as elevated C-reactive protein, ferritin, and cytokine levels, alongside exclusion of other causes like infection. Severity is graded using the American Society for Transplantation and Cellular Therapy (ASTCT) consensus criteria (2019), ranging from grade 1 (fever only) to grade 4 (life-threatening organ failure requiring intensive care).
Management focuses on early recognition and supportive care, with pharmacologic interventions tailored to severity: mild cases (grades 1-2) often resolve with antipyretics and fluids, while severe cases (grades 3-4) require interleukin-6 blockade using tocilizumab, an FDA-approved monoclonal antibody (2017), often combined with corticosteroids for refractory symptoms or neurotoxicity. Prophylactic strategies, such as step-up dosing in therapies or preemptive tocilizumab, have improved outcomes as of recent protocols (2023-2025), reducing severe CRS incidence from 15-25% in early trials to under 10%, with overall CRS-related mortality remaining low at under 5%. Despite these advances, CRS remains a significant challenge in expanding immunotherapy applications to solid tumors and autoimmune diseases.

Fundamentals

Definition

Cytokines are small proteins that act as key signaling molecules in the immune system, facilitating communication between cells to orchestrate responses to pathogens and tissue damage.
Cytokine release syndrome (CRS) is a form of systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) characterized by the excessive and rapid production of cytokines from activated immune cells, resulting in a hyperinflammatory state that can lead to capillary leak, endothelial dysfunction, and organ hypoperfusion.
CRS is distinct from cytokine storm, a term often reserved for more severe, life-threatening hyperinflammation involving multi-organ failure and higher mortality, whereas CRS encompasses a spectrum from mild to severe reactions. It also differs from macrophage activation syndrome (MAS), which involves hemophagocytosis by activated macrophages and is typically linked to underlying autoimmune or rheumatic disorders, rather than the broader immune activation seen in CRS.
As a clinical syndrome rather than a primary disease, CRS manifests secondarily to precipitating factors, highlighting its role as an exaggerated immune response rather than an independent pathological entity.

Pathophysiology

Cytokine release syndrome (CRS) arises from the hyperactivation of immune cells, primarily T cells and macrophages, which initiate a rapid and amplified release of proinflammatory cytokines. Upon activation, often through antigen recognition or therapeutic engagement, T cells secrete interferon-gamma (IFN-), which in turn stimulates monocytes and macrophages to produce additional cytokines such as interleukin-6 (IL-6), tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-), and interleukin-1 (IL-1). This creates a positive feedback loop where the released cytokines further recruit and activate immune cells, escalating systemic inflammation in a self-perpetuating manner.
Among the key cytokines, IL-6 plays a central role by promoting fever through hypothalamic prostaglandin synthesis and increasing vascular permeability via induction of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) and monocyte chemoattractant protein-1 (MCP-1), leading to endothelial dysfunction. IL-1 and TNF- contribute to endothelial damage and capillary leak by disrupting tight junctions and promoting leukocyte adhesion, while IFN- amplifies macrophage activation and granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF) supports the proliferation of myeloid cells involved in the inflammatory response.
The inflammatory cascade drives several downstream mechanisms, including endothelial activation marked by elevated angiopoietin-2 (Ang-2) and von Willebrand factor (vWF), which impair vascular integrity and cause capillary leak syndrome with resultant hypotension and tissue edema. This is compounded by coagulopathy, where cytokines like IL-6 activate the coagulation cascade, potentially progressing to disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) through widespread microvascular thrombosis or hemorrhage.
At the molecular level, nuclear factor-kappa B (NF-B) signaling, triggered by IL-1 and TNF-, upregulates proinflammatory gene expression, while the Janus kinase-signal transducer and activator of transcription (JAK-STAT) pathway, particularly STAT3 activation by IL-6, amplifies cytokine production and sustains the inflammatory loop. In mild CRS, the response is typically limited to fever and constitutional symptoms without significant organ involvement, whereas severe CRS involves profound hypoxia due to acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), endothelial injury leading to multi-organ dysfunction, and higher peak levels of circulating cytokines, often necessitating intensive care.

Causes

Infectious causes

Infections represent a primary trigger for cytokine release syndrome (CRS), where pathogens or their components provoke an excessive immune response leading to widespread cytokine dysregulation. This overactivation of the innate and adaptive immune systems can result in a hyperinflammatory state, often mimicking or complicating other conditions. Bacterial, viral, fungal, and parasitic infections are well-documented etiologies, particularly in vulnerable populations such as the immunocompromised.
Bacterial infections frequently initiate CRS through sepsis, with Gram-negative bacteria like Escherichia coli playing a prominent role via lipopolysaccharide (LPS) endotoxins. LPS binds to Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) on immune cells, triggering a cascade of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-) and interleukin-6 (IL-6), which amplify systemic inflammation. Streptococcal infections, especially group A Streptococcus pyogenes, cause streptococcal toxic shock syndrome (STSS), where superantigenic toxins like streptococcal pyrogenic exotoxins directly activate T-cells, bypassing normal antigen presentation and leading to massive cytokine release, including IL-2 and interferon-gamma (IFN-). These bacterial mechanisms highlight how pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) drive innate immune overresponse in CRS.
Viral infections also provoke CRS by dysregulating cytokine production, often through direct viral effects on immune signaling pathways. Severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2), the cause of COVID-19, induces CRS via its spike (S) and nucleocapsid (N) proteins activating nuclear factor-kappa B (NF-B), elevating IL-6 and TNF- levels; elevated IL-6 has been detected in 76% of severe cases compared to 30% in mild ones, correlating with disease progression and acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS). Influenza viruses, including highly pathogenic strains like H5N1, trigger similar cytokine storms by delaying type I interferon responses while hyperactivating pro-inflammatory pathways. Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) contributes to CRS in contexts like hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH), where viral persistence leads to uncontrolled T-cell and macrophage activation, releasing excess IFN- and other cytokines.
Fungal and parasitic infections less commonly but significantly trigger CRS, particularly in immunocompromised hosts such as those undergoing stem cell transplantation. Candida species, for instance, can induce CRS through -glucan components activating TLRs and dectin-1 receptors, prompting IL-1 and IL-6 surges in severe disseminated infections. Parasitic infections, including Plasmodium falciparum in malaria and Leishmania in visceral leishmaniasis, elicit CRS-like responses via repeated antigen stimulation and macrophage activation, leading to elevated TNF- and IL-10 imbalances that exacerbate tissue damage. These pathogens often manifest in patients with underlying immune deficiencies, where opportunistic invasion amplifies cytokine-mediated pathology.
A hallmark mechanism unique to infectious CRS involves LPS from Gram-negative bacteria or other PAMPs engaging TLRs on macrophages and dendritic cells, initiating a feedback loop of cytokine amplification that distinguishes it from non-pathogen-driven triggers. This innate immune hyperresponse can escalate to adaptive immunity involvement, as seen in superantigen-mediated T-cell activation during streptococcal or staphylococcal infections. Historically, the 1918 influenza pandemic exemplifies infection-induced CRS, where the H1N1 virus provoked lethal cytokine storms in young adults, contributing to over 50 million deaths worldwide through excessive lung inflammation and secondary bacterial superinfections.

Non-infectious causes

Non-infectious causes of cytokine release syndrome (CRS) primarily arise from therapeutic interventions and underlying disease processes that trigger excessive immune activation without pathogen involvement. Immunotherapies, particularly chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapies, are a leading trigger due to the rapid expansion and activation of engineered T cells, leading to massive cytokine production. For instance, axicabtagene ciloleucel, an anti-CD19 CAR T-cell therapy approved for relapsed or refractory large B-cell lymphoma, induces CRS in approximately 93% of patients, with grade 3 or higher severity in 13%, often linked to high tumor burden and rapid T-cell proliferation as key risk factors. Similarly, other CAR T-cell products exhibit CRS rates of 70-90% in lymphoma patients, where elevated pretreatment tumor burden correlates with more severe manifestations.
Bispecific antibodies and certain monoclonal antibodies also provoke CRS by bridging immune effector cells to target antigens, amplifying cytokine release. Blinatumomab, a CD19/CD3 bispecific T-cell engager used in acute lymphoblastic leukemia, causes CRS in 5-16% of cases depending on the study population, with higher risks in patients with high disease burden or during initial dosing; most events are low-grade and resolve with supportive care. Rituximab, an anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody for B-cell malignancies, is associated with infusion-related reactions, which may include CRS, in up to 77% of first infusions, though severe CRS remains rare (<1%) and is predominantly seen in patients with lymphocyte counts exceeding 50 10^9/L, reflecting rapid B-cell lysis.
Other iatrogenic triggers include agents historically used for immunosuppression and transplant-related complications. Muromonab-CD3 (OKT3), an anti-CD3 monoclonal antibody once employed for acute graft rejection, classically induces CRS in nearly all patients upon first infusion due to widespread T-cell activation, with symptoms onset within minutes to hours; its use has declined due to this toxicity. Post-hematopoietic stem cell transplantation, graft-versus-host disease (GVHD) can manifest with CRS-like features from donor T-cell reactivity against host tissues, occurring in 40-80% of haploidentical transplant recipients early after infusion, exacerbated by mismatched histocompatibility and high T-cell doses.
Disease-related non-infectious causes encompass hyperinflammatory states in autoimmune disorders, malignancies, and transplant rejection. Hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH), often secondary to autoimmune conditions like systemic juvenile idiopathic arthritis or malignancies such as lymphoma, drives CRS through unchecked macrophage and T-cell activation, releasing cytokines like IFN- and IL-6; incidence post-allogeneic transplant is about 1%, with high tumor burden in malignancy-associated cases increasing severity. In malignancies, particularly hematologic cancers, CRS arises from tumor-induced immune dysregulation or high disease burden promoting cytokine amplification, as seen in up to 50% of HLH-overlapping cases. Acute graft rejection, independent of OKT3, involves host-versus-graft immune responses post-solid organ or stem cell transplant, triggering CRS via endothelial activation and cytokine surges, with risks heightened by prior sensitization or HLA mismatch.
As of 2025, emerging non-infectious triggers include newer bispecific antibodies like teclistamab for multiple myeloma, which induce CRS in approximately 72% of patients, mostly low-grade.

Clinical Manifestations

Signs and symptoms

Cytokine release syndrome (CRS) manifests as a range of clinical features that can vary from mild, self-limited symptoms to severe, life-threatening involvement of multiple organ systems. The syndrome is characterized by an acute inflammatory response triggered by immune activation, leading to observable signs and patient-reported complaints that reflect systemic cytokine-mediated effects.
Constitutional symptoms form the cornerstone of CRS presentation, with fever greater than 38C serving as the defining and most consistent feature, often accompanied by chills. Fatigue, myalgias, nausea, and headache are also frequently reported, contributing to a flu-like illness in milder cases. These symptoms correlate with surges in circulating pro-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-6 and IFN-.
Cardiovascular signs include tachycardia and hypotension, which may arise from increased vascular permeability and capillary leak syndrome, resulting in generalized edema and fluid shifts. In more severe instances, these can progress to hemodynamic instability requiring supportive measures.
Respiratory involvement typically presents as hypoxemia and dyspnea, reflecting pulmonary inflammation and potential fluid accumulation in the lungs. Severe cases may escalate to acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), marked by profound respiratory failure.
Neurologic symptoms encompass confusion, seizures, and encephalopathy, which can impair cognitive function and consciousness, often overlapping with immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity.
Additional features include rash and arthralgias, which may occur in milder presentations, alongside occasional mild hepatic involvement.
The temporal progression of CRS symptoms generally begins within hours to days after the inciting trigger, such as immunotherapy infusion, with peak intensity occurring around 2-3 days.

Complications

Severe cytokine release syndrome (CRS) can progress to multi-organ failure, affecting multiple systems due to the systemic inflammatory response. Renal complications include acute kidney injury, often resulting from hypoperfusion and direct cytokine-mediated damage to glomerular structures. Hepatic dysfunction manifests as elevated transaminases and, in extreme cases, acute liver failure, driven by inflammatory infiltration and microvascular injury. Cardiac involvement may lead to myocarditis or cardiomyopathy, characterized by reduced ejection fraction and arrhythmias, secondary to cytokine-induced myocardial inflammation.
Hematologic complications are common in severe CRS, including thrombocytopenia and anemia, which arise from bone marrow suppression and increased consumption of blood elements. Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) poses significant bleeding risks, triggered by endothelial activation and procoagulant cytokine effects like those of IL-6.
Neurologic sequelae encompass cerebral edema, which can cause increased intracranial pressure and herniation, as well as stroke-like events such as aphasia and hemiparesis due to vascular inflammation and endothelial dysfunction. These are often linked to immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS) overlapping with CRS.
Immune dysregulation in CRS heightens susceptibility to infectious superinfections, with up to 30% of affected patients developing bacterial or fungal infections within weeks, exacerbated by cytokine-induced immunosuppression and macrophage activation.
Mortality risks escalate in grade 3-4 CRS, with rates of 5-22% reported in CAR-T therapy cohorts, primarily from multi-organ failure, neurotoxicity, or secondary infections; overall CRS-attributable mortality remains low at under 2% with modern management.
Long-term effects following resolution of severe CRS may include persistent neurocognitive deficits and chronic immune dysregulation, though these are less well-characterized and depend on the extent of initial damage. A 2025 qualitative investigation of patients undergoing CAR-T therapy revealed significant impairments in multiple quality-of-life domains following treatment, potentially linked to resolved CRS events.

Diagnosis and Assessment

Diagnostic methods

Diagnosis of cytokine release syndrome (CRS) relies on a combination of clinical evaluation, laboratory testing, imaging studies, and exclusion of alternative conditions, guided by consensus criteria that emphasize fever as a core feature in the context of recent immune therapy exposure. The American Society for Transplantation and Cellular Therapy (ASTCT) consensus defines CRS as a systemic inflammatory response involving fever (38.0C) not attributable to any other cause, occurring within a reasonable timeframe after immune effector cell therapy, often accompanied by hypotension, hypoxia, or end-organ dysfunction.
Clinical evaluation begins with a detailed patient history to identify potential triggers, such as recent administration of chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy, monoclonal antibodies, or other immunomodulatory treatments, typically within 1 to 14 days prior to symptom onset. Assessment focuses on symptoms including high fever, tachycardia, and signs of organ involvement, with fever serving as the initial hallmark prompting further investigation; symptoms often mimic sepsis and necessitate prompt evaluation in a monitored setting.
Laboratory tests play a central role in supporting the diagnosis by demonstrating systemic inflammation. Common findings include elevated C-reactive protein (CRP), often rising rapidly to levels exceeding 100 mg/L as a surrogate for interleukin-6 (IL-6) activity, markedly increased ferritin (frequently >10,000 ng/mL), and elevated cytokines such as IL-6 (typically >100 pg/mL in severe cases), interferon-gamma, and tumor necrosis factor-alpha. Additional abnormalities may involve cytopenias (e.g., thrombocytopenia or neutropenia), elevated lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), and transaminitis, though these are nonspecific and must be interpreted in context. Cytokine levels, while confirmatory, are not routinely required due to variability and limited availability, with CRP and ferritin preferred for monitoring.
Imaging is employed to evaluate organ-specific involvement and rule out complications. Chest X-ray or computed tomography (CT) scans are used to detect pulmonary infiltrates, edema, or acute respiratory distress syndrome-like features in patients with hypoxia. Echocardiography assesses cardiac function for myocarditis or cardiomyopathy, which can manifest as reduced ejection fraction or wall motion abnormalities in severe CRS. These modalities are indicated based on clinical symptoms rather than routinely.
Diagnosis requires exclusion of alternative etiologies, such as infection, tumor lysis syndrome, or disease progression, through comprehensive workup including blood cultures, urine analysis, viral PCR panels, and assessment of electrolytes, uric acid, and phosphate levels. Empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics are often initiated pending culture results in febrile patients post-lymphodepletion to differentiate CRS from sepsis. The Lee criteria from 2014, which emphasize clinical and laboratory correlation post-T-cell therapy, remain influential alongside the ASTCT framework for confirming CRS in therapeutic contexts.

Grading and classification

Grading and classification of cytokine release syndrome (CRS) rely on standardized systems to assess severity, primarily based on clinical parameters such as fever, hypotension, and hypoxia, which guide therapeutic interventions. The American Society for Transplantation and Cellular Therapy (ASTCT) consensus grading, established in 2019, serves as the current widely adopted framework for CRS associated with immune effector cell therapies, categorizing severity into grades 0 through 4. This system emphasizes systemic symptoms while excluding organ toxicities and neurologic events from the core CRS score, allowing separate evaluation of immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS).
The ASTCT grading defines grade 0 as the absence of CRS symptoms. Grade 1 involves fever (temperature 38C) without hypotension or hypoxia. Grade 2 includes fever with either hypotension not requiring vasopressors or hypoxia necessitating low-flow nasal cannula oxygen (6 L/min). Grade 3 features fever with hypotension requiring a single vasopressor or hypoxia requiring high-flow nasal cannula oxygen (>6 L/min), face mask, or non-invasive ventilation. Grade 4 represents life-threatening conditions, such as fever with hypotension needing multiple vasopressors (excluding vasopressors required solely for vascular leak syndrome) or hypoxia demanding positive pressure ventilation or intubation/mechanical ventilation. Organ toxicities, if present, are graded separately using Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (CTCAE) v5.0 but do not alter the CRS grade.
Prior to the ASTCT consensus, legacy systems included the CTCAE v5.0, released in 2017 by the National Cancer Institute, which grades CRS from 1 to 5 and incorporates broader adverse event criteria. Under CTCAE v5.0, grade 1 is fever with or without constitutional symptoms; grade 2 involves hypotension responsive to intravenous fluids or hypoxia requiring supplemental oxygen with FiO <40%; grade 3 includes hypotension managed with one vasopressor or hypoxia needing FiO 40%; grade 4 denotes life-threatening consequences requiring urgent intervention; and grade 5 indicates death related to the event. The Lee criteria, proposed in 2014, also use a 1-5 scale but place greater emphasis on neurologic symptoms alongside hypotension and hypoxia, with grade 1 limited to mild symptoms without organ dysfunction; grade 2 requiring moderate intervention like low-dose vasopressors or oxygen; grade 3 involving aggressive support such as high-dose vasopressors or significant organ toxicity with possible encephalopathy; grade 4 as life-threatening with potential ventilator dependence; and grade 5 as fatal.
A key distinction of the ASTCT system from legacy approaches is its deliberate separation of CRS from ICANS, recognizing that neurologic toxicities often co-occur but warrant independent grading to avoid conflation and improve management precision. Severity grading can be influenced by patient-specific factors, such as baseline health status (e.g., comorbidities like obesity or hypertension exacerbating inflammation), and the underlying trigger, with CAR T-cell therapies more frequently associated with higher-grade CRS (grades 3-4 in up to 46% of cases in acute lymphoblastic leukemia) compared to infectious causes, where CRS often manifests as part of sepsis with variable but typically less predictable severity.
These grading systems facilitate escalation of care by stratifying risk; for instance, grades 1 and 2 are generally manageable in outpatient or step-down settings with supportive measures, while grades 3 and 4 necessitate hospitalization and potential pharmacologic intervention.

Treatment

Supportive therapy

Supportive therapy forms the cornerstone of management for cytokine release syndrome (CRS), focusing on stabilizing patients through non-pharmacologic interventions to address hemodynamic instability, respiratory compromise, and organ dysfunction while preventing progression to severe grades. Early recognition and aggressive supportive measures are essential, as recommended by consensus guidelines, to mitigate life-threatening complications such as hypotension and hypoxia.
Fluid resuscitation is a primary intervention for hypotension associated with CRS, particularly in grades 2 and higher, where intravenous (IV) fluids such as normal saline boluses are administered to restore volume and maintain perfusion. However, excessive fluid administration should be avoided to prevent pulmonary edema, with guidelines emphasizing judicious use and early transition to vasopressors if hypotension persists. For refractory shock unresponsive to fluids, vasopressors like norepinephrine are initiated, typically starting at low doses (e.g., 0.050.1 mcg/kg/min) and titrated based on response, as seen in grade 3 CRS requiring one vasopressor or grade 4 needing multiple agents.
Respiratory support is tailored to the degree of hypoxia, beginning with supplemental oxygen via low-flow nasal cannula (6 L/min) for grade 2 CRS to maintain oxygen saturation above 92%. In more severe cases, such as grade 3 or 4 CRS with FiO2 requirements 40% or acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), escalation to high-flow nasal cannula, non-invasive ventilation (e.g., CPAP or BiPAP), or mechanical intubation is necessary to ensure adequate oxygenation and ventilation.
Close monitoring is critical for all patients with CRS, involving frequent assessment of vital signs, including blood pressure, heart rate, temperature, and oxygen saturation, often with continuous telemetry to detect hemodynamic changes promptly. Admission to an intensive care unit (ICU) is recommended for grade 3 or higher CRS to facilitate advanced monitoring and rapid intervention, while grade 12 cases may be managed on a general ward with heightened surveillance.
Nutritional support is provided as part of overall care, particularly for patients with prolonged ICU stays or gastrointestinal involvement, using enteral feeding when possible to maintain caloric needs and prevent catabolism, or parenteral nutrition if enteral routes are not feasible. Pain management involves symptomatic relief with non-opioid analgesics such as acetaminophen for mild discomfort or opioids for severe pain, integrated into the broader supportive framework to improve patient comfort without exacerbating respiratory depression.
These supportive measures align with guidelines from organizations like the American Society for Transplantation and Cellular Therapy (ASTCT) and the National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN; Version 2.2024), emphasizing multidisciplinary care and early intervention to stabilize patients before considering adjunctive targeted therapies.

Targeted therapies

Targeted therapies for cytokine release syndrome (CRS) primarily focus on pharmacologic agents that interrupt key cytokine signaling pathways, particularly interleukin-6 (IL-6), to mitigate the hyperinflammatory response. These interventions are typically initiated for grade 2 or higher CRS, often in conjunction with supportive measures to manage symptoms.
IL-6 inhibitors represent the cornerstone of targeted therapy for CRS, with tocilizumab, a monoclonal antibody against the IL-6 receptor, being the first-line agent. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved tocilizumab in 2017 specifically for the treatment of severe or life-threatening CRS induced by chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy, based on data from a pivotal study showing a 69% response rate, defined as resolution of CRS within 14 days without additional tocilizumab doses beyond two or further corticosteroid use; a biosimilar (tocilizumab-anoh) was approved in August 2025 for the same indication in patients aged 2 years and older. Administered intravenously at a dose of 8 mg/kg (maximum 800 mg per infusion), with subsequent doses spaced at least 8 hours apart if needed (up to four doses), tocilizumab rapidly blocks IL-6 signaling, leading to fever resolution in approximately 86% of patients within 24 hours and overall CRS improvement in the majority within 48 hours. Siltuximab, another IL-6 inhibitor that directly neutralizes free IL-6 rather than the receptor, has shown promise as an alternative or first-line option, particularly for early CRS management post-CAR T-cell therapy, with complete response rates of up to 83% and median resolution times as short as 1 hour in small cohorts. It is dosed at 11 mg/kg IV every 2-3 weeks, though its use in CRS remains off-label pending further validation.
Corticosteroids are employed as second-line targeted therapy for CRS refractory to IL-6 blockade or in cases with prominent neurologic involvement, exerting broad immunosuppressive effects by inhibiting multiple cytokine pathways. Dexamethasone, at doses of 10-20 mg IV every 6 hours for 1-3 days followed by taper, is commonly used for persistent grade 2 or higher CRS, while high-dose methylprednisolone (1-2 mg/kg IV daily) is preferred for severe or refractory cases, particularly those complicated by immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS). These agents achieve rapid symptom control in most patients, though prolonged use requires careful monitoring for secondary infections.
Other targeted agents address alternative cytokines in refractory CRS. Anakinra, an IL-1 receptor antagonist, is utilized for cases unresponsive to IL-6 inhibitors, dosed at up to 12 mg/kg/day IV, and has demonstrated safety and efficacy with overall response rates of 77% post-CAR T-cell therapy, including faster resolution of symptoms at higher doses. Emapalumab, a monoclonal antibody inhibiting interferon-gamma (IFN-), is applied in refractory settings, particularly where IFN- drives persistent inflammation, and has led to hemodynamic stabilization and cytokine reduction in case series without compromising antitumor activity; it received FDA approval in June 2025 for macrophage activation syndrome (MAS) in Still's disease, a complication overlapping with severe CRS.
Emerging targeted therapies include Janus kinase (JAK) inhibitors such as ruxolitinib, which broadly suppress cytokine signaling via the JAK-STAT pathway and have shown efficacy in refractory CRS by reducing serum cytokines and achieving control without impairing CAR T-cell expansion, typically dosed at 5-10 mg orally twice daily. Additionally, granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF) blockers like lenzilumab, a neutralizing monoclonal antibody, are under investigation in phase 3 trials (e.g., SHIELD) to mitigate CRS incidence and severity in CAR T-cell recipients, with preliminary data indicating reduced neurotoxicity rates alongside preserved antitumor efficacy. These agents hold promise for personalized CRS management but await broader regulatory approval.

Prevention

Strategies in therapy

Strategies to prevent cytokine release syndrome (CRS) during therapies prone to triggering it, such as chimeric antigen receptor T-cell (CAR-T) therapy and bispecific T-cell engagers, primarily involve proactive dose optimization to minimize rapid immune activation. Step-up dosing regimens, where lower initial doses are administered followed by escalation to the full therapeutic dose, allow gradual T-cell engagement and have been shown to reduce the incidence and severity of CRS in T-cell engaging therapies. For CAR-T cells, while a single infusion is standard, fractionated or split dosing approaches in select protocols further mitigate risks by controlling peak cytokine levels. Similarly, for monoclonal antibodies like rituximab or obinutuzumab, slower infusion ratesoften starting at 50 mg/hour and increasing graduallyprevent infusion-related reactions that can manifest as CRS-like symptoms by limiting acute cytokine surges.
Pre-medication protocols are a cornerstone of CRS prevention, administered 30-60 minutes prior to infusion to blunt inflammatory responses. Common regimens include antihistamines such as diphenhydramine (25-50 mg intravenously), antipyretics like acetaminophen (650 mg orally), and corticosteroids such as dexamethasone (10-20 mg intravenously) or methylprednisolone, which inhibit early cytokine production and histamine release. These measures are routinely recommended for CAR-T infusions and bispecific antibodies to reduce fever and mild CRS symptoms, though they do not eliminate risk entirely. In practice, higher corticosteroid doses may be used for patients at elevated risk, balancing efficacy against potential impacts on antitumor activity.
Patient selection plays a critical role in risk reduction, emphasizing the identification and management of factors that exacerbate CRS susceptibility. Bridging chemotherapy or radiation is often employed between leukapheresis and CAR-T infusion to decrease tumor burden, particularly in patients with high disease volume, as elevated tumor load correlates with more severe CRS due to increased antigen stimulation. For instance, bridging regimens can achieve disease control in select cases, lowering pretreatment tumor burden and associated cytokine peaks. Additionally, high-risk patientssuch as the elderly or those with significant comorbidities (e.g., cardiovascular disease or renal impairment)may require careful evaluation or exclusion if benefits do not outweigh toxicity risks, with bridging therapies sometimes deferred in low-burden cases to avoid unnecessary cytopenias.
The American Society for Transplantation and Cellular Therapy (ASTCT) provides key guidelines for CRS risk stratification in cellular therapies, recommending assessment of baseline factors like tumor burden, prior therapies, and comorbidities to tailor preventive approaches. Their 2019 consensus emphasizes integrating these into management plans, advocating for enhanced monitoring and adjusted premedication in high-risk strata (e.g., patients with diffuse large B-cell lymphoma and bulky disease). These recommendations facilitate standardized risk categorization, enabling proactive interventions like intensified bridging or modified dosing to optimize safety across institutions.
Clinical evidence underscores the efficacy of these strategies, particularly in bispecific T-cell engagers, where prophylaxis has substantially lowered CRS rates. Without step-up dosing or premedication, CRS incidence can approach 100% in early-phase trials, but implementation of these measures reduces it to 50-70% overall, with severe (grade 3+) cases dropping below 5%. For example, prophylactic tocilizumab alongside standard premedication in teclistamab therapy decreased CRS from 72% to 10%, preserving antitumor responses while enhancing tolerability. These findings highlight how combined preventive tactics shift CRS from a near-universal event to a manageable one, informing broader adoption in high-risk immunotherapies. Emerging strategies include the use of Janus kinase (JAK) inhibitors, such as ruxolitinib, to mitigate CRS by reducing cytokine signaling; early trials as of 2024 show reduced CRS severity without impairing CAR-T efficacy.

Monitoring and early intervention

Monitoring and early intervention in cytokine release syndrome (CRS), particularly following chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy, involve structured surveillance to identify signs of progression promptly and initiate targeted responses. Post-infusion monitoring typically includes daily assessments of vital signs such as temperature, blood pressure, and oxygen saturation, alongside laboratory evaluations of inflammatory markers like C-reactive protein (CRP) and ferritin levels, conducted for 7-14 days or longer in high-risk patients. These protocols aim to detect feverthe hallmark initial symptom of CRSearly, as it often precedes hemodynamic instability. Continuous or frequent cardiovascular monitoring is essential, given the risk of hypotension and capillary leak syndrome.
Biomarkers play a key role in early detection, with rising levels of interleukin-6 (IL-6) serving as a sensitive indicator of impending severe CRS; for instance, a combination of fever 38.9C, IL-6 concentrations 16 pg/mL, and monocyte chemoattractant protein-1 (MCP-1) 1343.5 pg/mL within 36 hours post-infusion predicts grade 4 neurotoxicity with 100% sensitivity and 94% specificity. Similarly, elevations in soluble glycoprotein 130 (sgp130), a natural antagonist of IL-6 signaling, correlate with severe CRS, and early rises in sgp130 during the first few days post-infusion have shown predictive value in distinguishing high-grade from low-grade events. CRP and ferritin, while not as prognostically specific, are routinely tracked as they peak significantly in severe casesmedian ferritin levels exceeding 100,000 ng/mL in grade 4-5 CRSand help gauge inflammatory burden when combined with clinical symptoms.
Surveillance protocols incorporate hospitalization criteria based on CRS grading, such as the American Society for Transplantation and Cellular Therapy (ASTCT) system, where grade 1 CRS may allow outpatient or ward management, but grade 2 or higher necessitates inpatient admission due to risks of hypoxia or organ dysfunction. For outpatient cellular therapy, remote monitoring toolsincluding wearable devices for vital signs and telemedicine appsenable twice-daily virtual check-ins and early detection of fever or symptoms, facilitating safe administration without immediate hospitalization in select low-risk cases. Early intervention thresholds emphasize prompt administration of tocilizumab, an IL-6 receptor antagonist, for grade 2 CRS symptoms such as persistent fever (39C) or mild hypotension unresponsive to fluids, as recommended by ASCO guidelines to prevent escalation.
Studies demonstrate that such vigilant monitoring and timely interventions significantly improve outcomes; for example, preemptive or early tocilizumab use in at-risk patients has been associated with reductions in severe CRS incidence and ICU admissions by up to 50% in high tumor burden cases, without compromising antitumor efficacy. This approach minimizes complications like prolonged hospitalization and enhances overall survival in CAR-T recipients.

Epidemiology and Prognosis

Epidemiology

Cytokine release syndrome (CRS) is a rare condition in the general population, with an incidence of less than 1% outside of specific therapeutic or infectious contexts. In contrast, it occurs frequently in patients receiving chimeric antigen receptor T-cell (CAR-T) therapy, affecting 40-100% of recipients depending on the product and underlying malignancy. Similarly, CRS-like hyperinflammatory responses have been observed in severe cases of infections such as COVID-19.
Demographic patterns reveal variations in CRS incidence and severity. Adults experience higher rates and more severe manifestations compared to pediatric patients, with studies reporting CRS in up to 77% of children treated with CAR-T for B-cell malignancies, though severe grades are less common in younger populations. Racial and ethnic associations also influence outcomes; for instance, Hispanic patients are more likely to develop severe CRS than White non-Hispanic patients. Limited data suggest potential disparities in severity among Black patients, though further research is needed to confirm these trends.
Geographically, reports of CRS have surged since 2020, driven by the global COVID-19 pandemic and the expanded use of immunotherapies like CAR-T cells. North America accounts for the majority of cases due to higher adoption of these therapies and advanced reporting systems. This increase reflects broader access to CAR-T approvals and heightened awareness of cytokine storms in viral infections.
Key risk factors for CRS include advanced age greater than 65 years, which correlates with increased severity in immunotherapy recipients. High disease burden at baseline, such as elevated bone marrow tumor load, significantly elevates the risk, as does prior allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (allo-HSCT).
Global estimates up to 2025, derived from FDA Adverse Event Reporting System (FAERS) data, indicate thousands of CRS-related events linked to CAR-T therapies. Adverse event reporting for CRS has remained stable, supporting ongoing safety monitoring without escalation in frequency. Recent pharmacovigilance analyses as of 2025 continue to track long-term safety signals, including secondary malignancies.

Prognosis and outcomes

The prognosis of cytokine release syndrome (CRS) is generally favorable with prompt management, though severity influences outcomes significantly. Overall mortality associated with CRS in CAR-T cell therapy contexts remains low, at approximately 3.7-5.4% across large cohorts. However, in grade 4 cases involving multi-organ failure, mortality rates escalate to 20-38%, often due to complications like refractory hypotension and respiratory distress.
Recovery timelines vary by CRS grade. For most patients with grade 1-2 CRS, symptoms resolve within 3-7 days, with fever lasting a median of 2.5 days and minimal supportive care required. Severe cases (grade 3-4) may take weeks to resolve, with organ toxicity persisting a median of 24 days post-fever resolution and hospitalization extending to 18 days or more. Prognostic factors favoring better outcomes include early initiation of treatment, low baseline interleukin-6 (IL-6) levels (below fourfold elevation), and absence of concurrent immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS).
Long-term effects of CRS can include neurocognitive deficits in 10-20% of CAR-T recipients, manifesting as impairments in attention, executive function, and memory, alongside potential chronic immune dysregulation. Data from patient registries and meta-analyses indicate that approximately 90% of CRS cases achieve resolution with tocilizumab intervention, underscoring its role in mitigating prolonged sequelae by 2025 analyses. Complications such as persistent cytopenias may indirectly worsen prognosis but are managed supportively.

Research and Future Directions

Historical developments

The recognition of cytokine release syndrome (CRS) emerged in the late 1980s during early clinical trials of the monoclonal antibody muromonab-CD3 (OKT3), used as an immunosuppressant for organ transplantation, where it induced acute systemic inflammatory reactions often termed "cytokine flu" due to fever, chills, and hypotension linked to rapid cytokine release such as tumor necrosis factor and interferon-gamma. These first-dose reactions were systematically associated with T-cell activation and cytokine elevation in studies from 1989 and 1990, marking CRS as a notable toxicity of T-cell targeting therapies. By the early 1990s, the term "cytokine release syndrome" was coined to describe this non-antigen-specific immune hyperactivation following OKT3 infusion.
In the 1990s, parallels were drawn between CRS and the hyperinflammatory state in sepsis, where excessive cytokine productionsuch as interleukin-6 and tumor necrosis factorcontributed to multi-organ dysfunction, prompting investigations into shared mechanisms like endothelial activation and capillary leak. This linkage expanded CRS understanding beyond iatrogenic causes to infectious triggers, with early sepsis models highlighting cytokine storms as drivers of septic shock mortality. These observations laid groundwork for recognizing CRS as a broader syndrome involving dysregulated immune responses.
The 2010s brought heightened attention to CRS through chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapies, exemplified by a 2014 University of Pennsylvania trial of CD19-targeted CAR T cells in relapsed/refractory B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia, where severe CRS occurred in multiple patients, including three fatalities among six at higher doses, underscoring the risks of potent immune activation. That same year, the Lee criteria formalized CRS diagnosis and grading, emphasizing fever with progressive organ toxicities like hypotension and hypoxia to stratify severity from grade 1 (mild, symptomatic) to grade 5 (death), facilitating standardized management across trials.
Regulatory responses intensified post-2017 with FDA approvals of CAR T therapies like tisagenlecleucel, which included black box warnings for CRS and neurologic toxicities due to fatal cases in pivotal trials, alongside approval of tocilizumab as the first targeted CRS treatment. Prior to 2020, CRS was predominantly viewed as therapy-associated, but the COVID-19 pandemic shifted this paradigm, as severe cases revealed CRS-like cytokine stormsmarked by elevated interleukin-6 and poor prognosisdriven by viral immune dysregulation rather than exogenous agents.

Ongoing research

Recent studies have identified novel biomarkers for predicting cytokine release syndrome (CRS) severity, particularly through cytokine panels incorporating interleukin-18 (IL-18). A 2024 analysis of cytokine profiles in CRS patients undergoing CAR-T cell therapy revealed that IL-18 levels were significantly elevated in cases associated with macrophage activation syndrome-like features, suggesting its utility as a prognostic marker for high-grade events. Additionally, 2024-2025 investigations have integrated machine learning models with these panels to enhance prediction accuracy; for instance, deep learning approaches like PrCRS, utilizing transfer learning on limited CAR-T datasets, achieved high sensitivity in forecasting severe CRS onset post-infusion. These models analyze pre-treatment cytokine data alongside clinical variables to stratify risk, enabling earlier interventions.
Advances in pathophysiology have been driven by single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) analyses, uncovering heterogeneous immune responses in CRS. A 2025 study using scRNA-seq on peripheral blood from CAR-T patients with breakthrough toxicities post-anakinra treatment highlighted distinct macrophage subsets, including pro-inflammatory populations contributing to persistent cytokine elevation despite initial suppression. These findings reveal macrophage-driven amplification loops in CRS pathogenesis, with specific subsets expressing elevated IFN- and IL-6 pathways, offering targets for subset-specific modulation.
Emerging therapies focus on mitigating CRS through innovative designs, including gene-edited CAR-T cells. Phase 3 trials of allogeneic CAR-T products, such as those using CRISPR editing to knock out endogenous TCR and HLA, have demonstrated reduced CRS incidence by minimizing off-target immune activation; for example, the CTX130 trial reported CRS in 67% of patients, predominantly low-grade, compared to higher rates in autologous counterparts. Additionally, broad cytokine blockade agents like emapalumab, an IFN- monoclonal antibody, are under evaluation in phase 2/3 settings for refractory CRS, showing promise in solid tumor CAR-T contexts where traditional IL-6 inhibitors fail.
Prevention strategies are advancing with AI-based risk models and prophylactic agents. Machine learning frameworks, building on 2025 predictive tools, integrate multi-omics data to generate personalized risk scores for CRS, facilitating preemptive dosing adjustments in CAR-T protocols. Prophylactic JAK inhibitors, such as itacitinib, have shown efficacy in ongoing trials; a 2025 phase 2 study reported significant reduction in grade 2 CRS and ICANS rates when administered preemptively in CD19-directed immune effector cell therapies.
Ongoing research addresses key gaps, including long-term outcomes in pediatric cohorts and CRS in solid tumors. In pediatric B-ALL patients post-CAR-T, 2024-2025 analyses indicate durable remissions with 4-year overall survival exceeding 60%, though late neurocognitive effects warrant further monitoring. For solid tumors, where CRS incidence remains low (under 20%) but severity high due to tumor microenvironment factors, 2025 studies emphasize tailored management, highlighting increased hypoxia and multi-organ involvement.
