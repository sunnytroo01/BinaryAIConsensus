570

1x
 Year 570 CE is traditionally identified as the birth year of Muhammad ibn Abdullah, the prophet of Islam, who was born in Mecca to the Banu Hashim clan of the Quraysh tribe during the so-called Year of the Elephant, named for a failed Abyssinian expedition against the Kaaba. This dating, derived from early Islamic biographical sources (srah) compiled over a century after his death, aligns with scholarly estimates placing his birth around 570, though precise confirmation remains elusive due to the absence of contemporary non-Islamic records. Muhammad's early life unfolded in a polytheistic Arabian trading hub marked by tribal alliances and economic reliance on pilgrimage routes, setting the stage for his later revelations that birthed Islam amid regional tensions between Byzantine and Sasanian empires. Elsewhere, the Northern Zhou dynasty in China moved to curb Buddhism's spread, reflecting imperial efforts to consolidate Confucian orthodoxy against foreign religious influences.

Events

Europe

In Italy, the Lombard forces under King Alboin advanced further following their invasion from Pannonia in 568, capturing Milan and much of the northern Po Valley by 570, thereby weakening Byzantine control over the exarchate of Ravenna. Alboin proclaimed himself ruler of Italy in Milan that year, though effective Lombard dominance remained limited to fragmented duchies amid ongoing resistance and Byzantine enclaves. This consolidation marked a pivotal shift, as the Lombards, numbering around 150,000200,000 including warriors and families, displaced Roman populations and established semi-independent duchies like Spoleto in central Italy around 570573.
In the Frankish realms of Gaul, the Merovingian kingdomsdivided among brothers Chilperic I in Neustria, Sigibert I in Austrasia, and Guntram in Burgundyexperienced ongoing fraternal rivalries, with Sigibert launching campaigns against Chilperic in the mid-570s that foreshadowed broader civil strife. These conflicts, rooted in inheritance disputes following Clotaire I's death in 561, involved raids on cities like Poitiers and Tours, exacerbating regional instability without a unified royal authority.
On the Iberian Peninsula, Visigothic King Liuvigild (r. 568586) pursued territorial expansion against Byzantine coastal holdings and the Suebic kingdom in the northwest, recapturing Crdoba in 569 and laying groundwork for further unification efforts by 570, including administrative reforms to integrate Hispano-Roman elites. His campaigns, estimated to involve armies of 10,00020,000, aimed at centralizing power in Toledo amid Arian-Catholic tensions.
In Britain, British Celtic kingdoms such as those in Strathclyde and Rheged maintained resistance against expanding Anglo-Saxon settlers, with alliances forming around 570 to counter Northumbrian incursions, though precise battles like that at Gwen Ystrad remain approximately dated to this period based on later poetic accounts. The island's fragmentation persisted post-Roman withdrawal, with Germanic kingdoms like Bernicia gaining ground eastward.

Persia and Near East

In 570, the Sasanian Empire under King Khosrow I (r. 531579) intervened militarily in Yemen to oust Aksumite (Ethiopian) forces that had controlled the region since their invasion around 525, following the defeat of the Himyarite king Dhu Nuwas. This action responded to appeals from local Himyarite elites opposed to Aksumite rule, marking a strategic expansion of Persian influence into southern Arabia to secure trade routes, including access to the Red Sea and Indian Ocean commerce in spices, incense, and textiles. The campaign, led by the Sasanian general Wahriz, culminated in the decisive defeat of Aksumite viceroy Abraha's successors, ending Ethiopian dominance by that year and installing a Persian governor, thereby integrating Yemen as a Sasanian province until the mid-7th century Arab conquests. 
This conquest reflected Khosrow I's broader foreign policy of consolidating power in peripheral territories amid relative peace with the Byzantine Empire, following the 562 truce that had temporarily halted frontier wars after the Iberian conflict. Persian control over Yemen facilitated administrative reforms, including the settlement of Iranian garrisons and officials, which introduced Zoroastrian elements alongside local Jewish and Christian communities, though religious tensions persisted due to the multi-confessional nature of the populace. The move also countered Byzantine-aligned Aksumite ambitions, as Ethiopia had served as a Roman proxy in the Horn of Africa and Arabian trade spheres, underscoring the interconnected geopolitical rivalries of late antiquity in the Near East.
Domestically in the Sasanian core territories of Mesopotamia and Iran proper, 570 saw no recorded major upheavals, with Khosrow I continuing his administrative and fiscal reforms initiated earlier in his reign, such as land surveys and tax equalization, which bolstered the empire's military and economic resilience. These policies, drawing on centralized bureaucracy inherited from predecessors like Kavad I, emphasized merit-based appointments over hereditary nobility, fostering stability in the Near Eastern provinces amid preparations for future Byzantine tensions that erupted in 572.

Arabia

In circa 570 CE, Abraha, the Aksumite viceroy ruling the Himyarite Kingdom in Yemen, launched a major military expedition northward into the Arabian interior, employing war elephants that gave the campaign its later designation in Islamic tradition as the Year of the Elephant. This incursion targeted tribal strongholds along trade routes, including those controlled by the Quraysh tribe in Mecca, amid efforts to supplant the Kaaba's pilgrimage economy with Abraha's rival church in Sana'a, which had been desecrated by Arab pilgrims. South Arabian inscriptions, such as the Ryckmans 541 (CIH 541), corroborate Abraha's earlier campaigns against Arab tribes around 552 CE, providing epigraphic evidence of his expansionist activities, though they do not specify a direct assault on Mecca.
Islamic sources, including early sira literature like Ibn Ishaq's accounts, describe the army reaching the outskirts of Mecca, where it suffered catastrophic losses attributed to divine interventionflocks of birds (ababil) pelting troops with stone-like projectiles, as referenced in Quran 105 (Surah al-Fil)leading to Abraha's retreat and death en route to Yemen. These narratives, while central to later Muslim historiography, lack independent non-Islamic corroboration for the miraculous elements or precise Meccan targeting, with scholars noting potential theological embellishments in oral traditions preserved by the Quraysh. Historical analysis favors a naturalistic explanation: the expedition likely collapsed due to a smallpox epidemic that decimated the unacclimated Aksumite forces, consistent with regional disease patterns and logistical strains of desert warfare.
The failure reinforced the Quraysh's custodianship of the Kaaba, elevating Mecca's status as a neutral sanctuary amid tribal rivalries in the Hijaz, where polytheistic cults coexisted with pockets of Christianity and Judaism. No other major recorded conflicts or political shifts mark Arabia in 570 CE, reflecting the decentralized tribal structure of pre-Islamic society, dominated by Bedouin nomads, caravan traders, and oasis settlements reliant on incense and spice routes.

Asia

In northern China, the Northern Zhou dynasty under Emperor Wu (Yuwen Yong) initiated a military offensive against the rival Northern Qi dynasty, besieging the city of Yiyang (in modern Luoyang, Henan) around the new year 570, with Prince Yuwen Xian leading the forces. General Wei Xiaokuan cautioned against the campaign due to potential vulnerabilities, but it proceeded amid ongoing border skirmishes between the two states. Northern Qi general Hul Guang countered by shifting tactics northward, encroaching on Zhou territory along the Fen River, though the siege did not yield decisive gains for either side.
Further west in Central Asia, the Gktrk Khaganate, ruled by Muqan Qaghan, expanded southward through campaigns against Sasanian Persia and remaining Hephthalite principalities south of the Oxus River (Amu Darya) in 569570, consolidating control over key trade routes and nomadic territories. These operations marked a peak in Gktrk influence, leveraging cavalry superiority to challenge Sasanian holdings in the region without full-scale invasion of core Persian lands.
No major recorded upheavals occurred in South or East Asia proper during the year; the Chalukya dynasty consolidated power in the Deccan Plateau of India under Pulakeshin I (r. circa 543566), focusing on internal consolidation rather than expansion, while Japan's Yamato court under Emperor Kinmei (r. 539571) maintained stability amid Buddhist introductions and clan rivalries.

Religion

According to Islamic tradition, Muhammad ibn Abdullah, the founder of Islam, was born in Mecca around 570 CE to Amina bint Wahb of the Banu Zuhrah clan and Abdullah ibn Abd al-Muttalib of the Banu Hashim clan of the Quraysh tribe. His birth is associated with the Year of the Elephant (m al-Fl), named after the failed expedition of the Aksumite viceroy Abraha against the Kaaba in Mecca circa 570 CE, which Islamic sources attribute to divine intervention protecting the sanctuary. The precise date is recorded in later biographical works as the 12th of Rabi' al-Awwal, though scholarly calculations based on lunar-solar correspondences suggest equivalents ranging from 569 to 571 CE, reflecting the absence of contemporary non-Islamic records.
Muhammad's early life occurred amid the polytheistic religious practices of pre-Islamic Arabia, including pilgrimage to the Kaaba housing idols alongside recognition of Allah as a high god, alongside Hanif monotheism and influences from Judaism and Christianity in the region. Orphaned younghis father before birth and mother by age sixhe was raised by his grandfather Abd al-Muttalib and later uncle Abu Talib, immersing him in Quraysh merchant and custodial roles over the Kaaba. These circumstances positioned him within the socio-religious fabric that would later frame his prophetic mission beginning in 610 CE.
Elsewhere, religious developments were incremental: in China, a limestone statue of a Bodhisattva was crafted in Henan province around this period, exemplifying ongoing Mahayana Buddhist artistic traditions under the Northern Zhou dynasty before its brief suppression of Buddhism in 574 CE. In Europe, the anonymous Pilgrim of Piacenza documented travels to Christian holy sites in Syria, Palestine, and Sinai, contributing to pilgrimage literature amid the Byzantine-Sassanian tensions. No major doctrinal schisms or foundational events in Christianity or other faiths are recorded precisely for 570 CE, with the period marked by consolidation under Justinian I's ecclesiastical policies in the East.

Notable individuals

Births

Muhammad ibn Abdullah, founder of Islam, was born circa 570 CE in Mecca, in the Arabian Peninsula's Hijaz region. Traditional accounts, drawn from 8th-century biographies like Ibn Ishaq's Sirat Rasul Allah, place his birth in the "Year of the Elephant" (m al-fl), corresponding to approximately 570 based on the timing of Abraha's failed invasion of Mecca with war elephants. These sources, compiled over a century after his death, rely on oral traditions and provide the primary evidence for his early life, though modern historians note uncertainties in precise dating due to the absence of contemporary records.
Childebert II, a Merovingian Frankish king, was born in 570 to Sigebert I of Austrasia and Brunhilda. Ascending the throne of Austrasia at age five in 575 following his father's assassination, he ruled under regency until adulthood and later acquired Burgundy in 593, expanding Frankish influence amid familial civil strife. His reign, marked by alliances with the Lombards and conflicts with Byzantium, is documented in contemporary chronicles like those of Gregory of Tours, offering relatively direct evidence from the 6th century.

Deaths

Gildas (c. 500570), a Romano-British monk and historian, died in 570 according to the Annales Cambriae, a medieval Welsh chronicle compiling earlier annals. He is best known for authoring De Excidio et Conquestu Britanniae, a Latin work lamenting the moral decline and Saxon invasions of post-Roman Britain, providing one of the earliest written accounts from a native British perspective.
Abdullah ibn Abd al-Muttalib (c. 546570), father of the Prophet Muhammad, died in late 570 during a trading caravan return from Syria to Mecca, succumbing to illness in Yathrib (later Medina), where he was buried. Traditional Islamic biographies, such as those derived from Ibn Ishaq's Sirat Rasul Allah, date his death shortly before or around Muhammad's birth, emphasizing his role as a merchant from the Quraysh tribe's Banu Hashim clan.
Agnellus (c. 487570), Bishop of Ravenna, died on August 1, 570, after serving from 557 amid efforts to reclaim Arian churches following Justinian I's Gothic War victories and papal interventions. Ravenna's Liber Pontificalis Ecclesiae Ravennatis records his tenure's reconciliations of schismatic communities to orthodoxy.
te of Killeedy (c. 475570), an Irish saint and abbess, died on January 15, 570. Known as the "foster mother of the saints of Ireland" for her monastic community in Limerick, early hagiographies attribute miracles and patronage of infants to her.

Historiography and significance

Primary sources and evidence

The primary sources documenting events of 570 AD are limited, often retrospective, and regionally fragmented, reflecting the era's reliance on oral transmission, ecclesiastical records, and sporadic inscriptions rather than systematic annals. In Western Europe, Gregory of Tours' Decem Libri Historiarum (composed c. 573594 AD) serves as a key near-contemporary account for Merovingian Gaul, detailing Frankish royal disputes and ecclesiastical matters from the 570s onward, such as the activities of King Chilperic I. These narratives, drawn from eyewitness reports and royal archives, prioritize moral and providential interpretations but provide verifiable details on political chronology.
In the Byzantine sphere and Near East, coverage draws from Syriac and Greek ecclesiastical historians like John of Ephesus (c. 507586 AD), whose Ecclesiastical History (preserved in fragments) records events up to the 580s, including plagues, persecutions, and Persian-Byzantine tensions under Emperor Justin II and Sassanid Shah Khosrow I. Earlier chronicles, such as Marcellinus Comes' (up to 548 AD), offer contextual continuity for Gothic wars in Italy, but 570-specific details emerge in later continuations like those of Menander Protector (late 6th century). Sassanid Persia lacks direct narrative texts from 570; evidence includes administrative papyri from Egypt (reflecting frontier interactions) and rock reliefs from prior reigns, with later Syriac sources like John of Ephesus noting diplomatic exchanges and military campaigns.
For Arabia, no contemporaneous documents or inscriptions confirm key traditions, such as the birth of Muhammad in Mecca (dated to c. 570 AD via the "Year of the Elephant," linked to Abraha's expedition). Earliest accounts stem from 8th-century compilations like Ibn Ishaq's Kitab al-Maghazi (d. 767 AD), which chains oral reports (asnad) from alleged transmitters, but these postdate the events by over a century and exhibit hagiographic tendencies favoring prophetic legitimacy. Non-Arab sources, including Byzantine and Syriac texts from the 570s, omit any reference, underscoring reliance on post-event Islamic tradition rather than independent verification. Archaeological traces, such as South Arabian inscriptions alluding to Aksumite-Sassanid conflicts c. 569570 AD, indirectly align with the "Elephant" narrative but yield no personal or Meccan specifics.
In Asia, Chinese dynastic records like the Liang Shu (compiled c. 636 AD but drawing from 6th-century logs) note tributary exchanges with southern kingdoms, providing tangential evidence for regional stability absent major upheavals in 570. Overall, these sources demand cross-verification due to confessional biasesChristian texts emphasizing orthodoxy, Persian materials filtered through later Islamic lensesand the prevalence of oral-to-written transmission, which risks chronological compression or legendary accretion.

Scholarly debates and dating uncertainties

The dating of key events associated with the year 570, particularly the birth of Muhammad and the so-called Year of the Elephant, remains contested among historians due to the reliance on late Islamic biographical traditions lacking contemporary corroboration. Traditional Muslim sources, such as the sra literature compiled by Ibn Isq in the 8th century, anchor Muhammad's birth to the Year of the Elephant (m al-fl), an expedition purportedly led by the Aksumite viceroy Abraha against Mecca, thwarted by divine intervention as referenced in Quran 105 (Srat al-Fl). These accounts, transmitted orally before written fixation, date the event to circa 570 CE, aligning Muhammad's nativity with a pivotal pre-Islamic milestone roughly 4053 years before the Hijra in 622 CE. However, such narratives exhibit hagiographic tendencies, potentially inflating symbolic significance over chronological precision, as primary evidence consists of retrospective chains of transmission (isnd) rather than inscriptions or eyewitness records from the era.
Epigraphic and external historical data introduce uncertainty, suggesting the Abraha expedition may predate 570 CE. South Arabian inscriptions, including those from the Himyarite kingdom under Abraha's rule (ca. 525570 CE), document military campaigns against Arab tribes but omit explicit reference to Mecca or elephants in a 570 context; scholars like M.J. Kister and Christian Robin interpret regnal year evidence (e.g., Ry 661 in the Himyarite era) as placing aggressive northern incursions around 552 CE or shortly after 558 CE, based on cross-referencing with Byzantine chronicler Procopius (ca. 550 CE) and Aksumite records. Irfan Shahid, analyzing pre-Islamic Arab-Byzantine interactions, similarly posits the failed Mecca campaign in the mid-6th century, potentially 552 CE, arguing that Abraha's death (ca. 565570 CE) and successor Sumyafa Ashwa's instability preclude a late-570 timing. This earlier framework implies Muhammad's birth could shift to 552560 CE, disrupting traditional timelines derived from death-age estimates (e.g., 63 lunar years from birth to 632 CE) and highlighting how Islamic historiography calibrated pre-Hijra chronology relative to prophetic biography rather than independent anchors.
Further debates center on the historicity of the elephant narrative itself, with minimal non-Islamic attestation beyond vague Procopius allusions to Ethiopian-Arab conflicts; no archaeological traces of elephants or the battle exist at Mecca, raising questions of legendary embellishment in oral lore to underscore divine protection of the Kaaba. Revisionist analyses, such as those questioning Mecca's 6th-century prominence in trade routes, amplify doubts, though mainstream scholarship accepts a kernel of truth in Abraha's raid while cautioning against conflating Quranic poetry with verbatim history. For Muhammad's birth specificsclaimed as Monday, 12 Rab al-Awwalno direct evidence survives beyond these traditions, and variant reports (e.g., 2nd or 8th of the month) underscore transmission variances; contemporary 7th-century sources like the Doctrina Jacobi (ca. 634 CE) allude to a "prophet among the Saracens" but provide no natal details. These uncertainties reflect broader challenges in early Islamic historiography, where event dating often prioritizes theological coherence over empirical synchronization with Byzantine, Persian, or South Arabian chronologies.

Long-term historical impacts

The birth of Muhammad ibn Abdullah around 570 CE in Mecca to the Quraysh tribe initiated a sequence of events culminating in the establishment of Islam as a major world religion. Muhammad's reported revelations beginning in 610 CE formed the Quran, which after his death in 632 CE inspired the unification of Arabian tribes through the Ridda Wars (632633 CE) under the Rashidun Caliphs. This consolidation enabled rapid military expansions, defeating Byzantine forces at the Battle of Yarmouk in 636 CE and Sassanid Persians at al-Qadisiyyah in the same year, leading to the conquest of Syria, Egypt, Mesopotamia, and Persia by 651 CE. 
These conquests reshaped the geopolitical landscape of the Near East and beyond, dismantling the exhausted Byzantine and Sassanid empires and establishing the Umayyad Caliphate (661750 CE), which extended Islamic rule to the Iberian Peninsula by 711 CE and the Indus Valley by 712 CE. Under subsequent Abbasid rule, the Islamic world fostered the Translation Movement in Baghdad (8th10th centuries CE), preserving and advancing Greek, Persian, and Indian knowledge in mathematics, astronomy, and medicine, exemplified by al-Khwarizmi's development of algebra around 820 CE and Ibn Sina's Canon of Medicine in 1025 CE. Such intellectual pursuits contributed to global scientific progress, indirectly influencing the European Renaissance through transmissions via Al-Andalus and Sicily.
The enduring legacy includes Islam's adherence by approximately 1.9 billion people as of 2023, shaping legal systems via Sharia in numerous states and influencing cultural, architectural, and linguistic elements across Eurasia and Africa. Persistent sectarian divisions originating in early caliphal successions, such as Sunni-Shia splits post-661 CE, continue to affect regional stability, while the faith's emphasis on monotheism and community fostered resilient empires that mediated trade routes and intercultural exchanges for over a millennium. However, the expansion's reliance on warfare and subsequent dhimmi taxation on non-Muslims under Islamic governance imposed long-term demographic and social pressures on conquered populations.
