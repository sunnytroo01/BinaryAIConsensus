ICAM-1

1x
 Intercellular adhesion molecule 1 (ICAM-1), also known as CD54, is a transmembrane glycoprotein encoded by the ICAM1 gene located on human chromosome 19p13.2, belonging to the immunoglobulin superfamily of adhesion molecules. ICAM-1 was first identified in 1988 as a ligand for the leukocyte function-associated antigen 1 (LFA-1). It primarily functions to mediate cell-cell interactions by binding 2-integrins such as LFA-1 (CD11a/CD18) and Mac-1 (CD11b/CD18) on leukocytes, thereby facilitating their adhesion to endothelial cells and transmigration during inflammatory responses.
ICAM-1's structure features five extracellular immunoglobulin-like domains (D1D5), a single transmembrane helix, and a short cytoplasmic tail of approximately 28 amino acids, resulting in a mature protein of 532 residues with a molecular weight ranging from 60 to 114 kDa due to N- and O-linked glycosylation; alternative splicing generates multiple isoforms, including a soluble form (sICAM-1) lacking the transmembrane and cytoplasmic domains. It is constitutively expressed at low levels on endothelial cells, epithelial cells, fibroblasts, and immune cells such as lymphocytes and monocytes, with highest expression in lung and bone marrow tissues, and can be rapidly upregulated on various cell types in response to proinflammatory stimuli.
Beyond its canonical role in leukocyte-endothelial adhesion and immune cell trafficking, ICAM-1 serves as a costimulatory molecule in immune synapse formation, a receptor for pathogens including rhinoviruses and Plasmodium falciparum, and a regulator of intracellular signaling pathways involving Rho GTPases, Src kinases, and actin cytoskeleton reorganization, which influence processes like barrier function, efferocytosis, and wound healing. Its expression is tightly regulated by cytokines such as TNF-, IL-1, and IFN- through transcription factors like NF-B, AP-1, and STAT1, as well as post-transcriptional mechanisms including microRNAs (e.g., miR-141, miR-223) and proteolytic shedding by ADAM proteases to produce sICAM-1, which acts as a decoy receptor or biomarker in circulation.
ICAM-1 plays pivotal roles in pathology, contributing to chronic inflammation in conditions like atherosclerosis, rheumatoid arthritis, and asthma via excessive leukocyte recruitment; it promotes tumorigenesis and metastasis in cancers such as melanoma, breast, and lung carcinoma by enhancing tumor cell adhesion and survival signaling; and it is implicated in susceptibility to infections like malaria and in injury resolution processes, with elevated sICAM-1 levels serving as a prognostic indicator in sepsis, cardiovascular disease, and malignancy.

Introduction and Discovery

Definition and Basic Properties

ICAM-1 (Intercellular Adhesion Molecule 1), also known as CD54, is a transmembrane glycoprotein belonging to the immunoglobulin superfamily. It is encoded by the ICAM1 gene in humans, located on chromosome 19p13.2. This cell surface protein is typically expressed on endothelial cells and immune system cells, playing a foundational role in cellular adhesion processes.
The core structure of ICAM-1 includes five extracellular immunoglobulin-like domains (D1D5) exposed to the cell surface, a single transmembrane helix that anchors it in the membrane, and a short cytoplasmic tail of approximately 28 amino acids involved in intracellular interactions. Alternative splicing generates isoforms, including a soluble form (sICAM-1) lacking the transmembrane and cytoplasmic domains. Due to N- and O-linked glycosylation, the mature protein exhibits a molecular weight of approximately 60-114 kDa, with variations depending on the degree of glycosylation across its immunoglobulin-like domains.
ICAM-1 functions primarily as a ligand for leukocyte 2-integrins, such as lymphocyte function-associated antigen 1 (LFA-1; L2; CD11a/CD18) and Mac-1 (M2; CD11b/CD18), thereby mediating essential cell-cell interactions during immune responses. Its expression is upregulated by inflammatory cytokines, enhancing its availability in response to immune stimuli.

Historical Discovery

ICAM-1 was first described in 1986 as a novel intercellular adhesion molecule expressed on the surface of endothelial cells and other cell types, playing a key role in mediating leukocyte adhesion during inflammatory responses. Researchers at the time identified it using monoclonal antibodies that inhibited phorbol ester-stimulated homotypic aggregation of leukocytes, distinguishing it from the integrin LFA-1 while noting its inducible expression by inflammatory stimuli such as interleukin-1 (IL-1) and interferon-gamma (IFN-). This initial characterization highlighted ICAM-1's presence on cytokine-activated endothelial cells, where it facilitates the recruitment of immune cells to sites of inflammation.
Subsequent studies in 1987 confirmed ICAM-1 as a direct ligand for LFA-1, with purification and reconstitution experiments demonstrating specific binding that supported cell adhesion under physiological conditions. The molecular cloning and sequencing of ICAM-1 were achieved in 1988, revealing its primary structure as a glycoprotein belonging to the immunoglobulin superfamily, with five extracellular Ig-like domains, a transmembrane region, and a short cytoplasmic tail; this work also underscored its homology to neural cell adhesion molecule (NCAM) and its interaction with the integrin family. These findings established ICAM-1's structural basis for adhesive functions in immune interactions.
In 1989, during the Fourth International Workshop on Leukocyte Differentiation Antigens in Vienna, ICAM-1 was officially designated as the cluster of differentiation antigen CD54, standardizing its nomenclature within the leukocyte surface antigen system. This assignment reflected its recognition as a critical marker on various hematopoietic and non-hematopoietic cells involved in immune responses.
Early investigations in the late 1980s and 1990s further linked ICAM-1 to microbial interactions, with confirmatory evidence in 1989 showing it serves as the primary cellular receptor for the major group of human rhinoviruses, enabling viral attachment and entry into host cells such as epithelial cells in the respiratory tract. This discovery, based on binding assays with purified ICAM-1 and monoclonal antibody inhibition, expanded ICAM-1's role beyond immune adhesion to viral pathogenesis.

Gene and Expression

Genomic Organization

The ICAM1 gene, which encodes intercellular adhesion molecule 1, is located on the short arm of human chromosome 19 at position 19p13.2. It spans approximately 15.8 kilobases (kb) of genomic DNA and consists of seven exons separated by six introns. Exon 1 encodes the signal peptide, exons 2 through 6 correspond to the five immunoglobulin-like extracellular domains, and exon 7 encodes the transmembrane and cytoplasmic domains.
The promoter region of ICAM1, situated upstream of exon 1, is a CpG island-rich area that includes multiple transcription factor binding sites critical for inducible expression. Notably, it contains consensus sequences for NF-B and AP-1, which facilitate rapid transcriptional activation in response to inflammatory stimuli. These elements are conserved and play a key role in coordinating gene expression under cytokine influence.
Alternative splicing of the ICAM1 pre-mRNA generates multiple isoforms, including a soluble form known as sICAM-1, which lacks the transmembrane domain encoded by exon 7 and is secreted into the extracellular space. This isoform arises from exon skipping or alternative polyadenylation, resulting in truncated proteins with varying numbers of extracellular domains (typically two to five). Such splicing events contribute to the diversity of ICAM-1 functions in circulation.
Several single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) within the ICAM1 gene influence its expression and protein function. The rs5498 polymorphism (also denoted as K469E), located in exon 6, results in a lysine-to-glutamic acid substitution in the fifth immunoglobulin-like domain and is associated with elevated soluble ICAM-1 levels and altered endothelial expression. This variant has been linked to increased transcriptional activity and higher serum concentrations of sICAM-1 in various populations.

Regulation and Tissue Distribution

ICAM-1 is constitutively expressed at low basal levels on endothelial cells, fibroblasts, and select leukocytes, including lymphocytes and monocytes, under normal physiological conditions, with highest expression observed in lung and bone marrow tissues. This baseline expression supports minimal cell adhesion in non-inflammatory states.
Expression of ICAM-1 is markedly upregulated in response to pro-inflammatory stimuli, particularly cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-), interleukin-1 beta (IL-1), and interferon-gamma (IFN-), which activate the nuclear factor kappa B (NF-B) transcription factor pathway and STAT1 to drive transcriptional induction. This inducible mechanism enhances ICAM-1 surface levels on endothelial and epithelial cells within hours of cytokine exposure. Post-transcriptional regulation also plays a role, including suppression by microRNAs such as miR-141 and miR-223, and generation of sICAM-1 through proteolytic shedding by ADAM proteases. Conversely, anti-inflammatory agents downregulate ICAM-1; glucocorticoids, such as dexamethasone, suppress its expression by inhibiting NF-B activation and translocation in endothelial cells. Similarly, interleukin-10 (IL-10) reduces ICAM-1 levels on IL-1-activated endothelial cells, thereby limiting leukocyte adhesion.
A soluble isoform of ICAM-1 (sICAM-1), generated through proteolytic shedding from the cell surface, circulates in plasma and acts as a biomarker for systemic inflammation, with elevated levels correlating to disease activity in conditions like rheumatoid arthritis and sepsis. Regarding tissue distribution, ICAM-1 exhibits elevated expression in inflamed sites, such as the synovium of rheumatoid arthritis joints where it is prominently displayed on endothelial and synovial lining cells. In contrast, healthy brain endothelium maintains low ICAM-1 levels, contributing to the blood-brain barrier's restrictive nature under basal conditions.

Molecular Structure

Domain Architecture

ICAM-1 is a type I transmembrane protein characterized by an extracellular region composed of five immunoglobulin-like (Ig-like) domains (D1D5), a single hydrophobic transmembrane helix, and a short cytoplasmic tail of approximately 28 amino acids. The Ig-like domains belong to the immunoglobulin superfamily (IgSF), with D1 and D3 classified in the I-set, D2 and D4 in the C2-set (also known as I2-set), and D5 featuring a distorted C2-set topology. The extracellular domains form an elongated, rod-like structure that projects from the cell surface, enabling interactions with extracellular ligands, while the single-span transmembrane domain (spanning residues approximately 480502) anchors the protein in the lipid bilayer.
Each Ig-like domain adopts a beta-sandwich fold consisting of two antiparallel beta-sheets packed against each other, a hallmark of IgSF proteins that confers stability and flexibility to the overall architecture. The N-terminal D1 domain, located at the distal tip, is structurally primed for ligand engagement through exposed loops, though its primary role in binding is supported by the domain's conserved beta-sheet framework. Crystal structures determined in the late 1990s, such as the 2.2 resolution model of D1D2 (PDB: 1IAM), reveal a compact, horseshoe-like arrangement of beta-strands in these domains, with D1 featuring an A/B/E/G-sheet and a C/C'/F-sheet connected by a disulfide bond. Similarly, the structure of D3D5 (PDB: 1P53) highlights the continuity of this beta-rich motif across the extracellular region, underscoring the modular nature of ICAM-1's domain organization.
The cytoplasmic tail lacks intrinsic enzymatic activity but contains motifs that facilitate intracellular signaling and cytoskeletal associations upon extracellular stimulation. Additionally, the protein exhibits potential for dimerization within the membrane, primarily mediated by domain D4, where edge beta-strands from opposing monomers fuse to form a super beta-sandwich, burying approximately 980 of surface area per subunit and stabilized by 12 hydrogen bonds. This dimerization propensity, observed in crystal structures and supported by the transmembrane region's hydrophobic interactions, may enhance avidity in cellular contexts.

Post-Translational Modifications

ICAM-1 undergoes extensive N-linked glycosylation at eight asparagine residues primarily located within its extracellular immunoglobulin-like domains D2 through D4. These modifications add complex and high-mannose oligosaccharides, capped in many cases with sialic acid, which collectively account for approximately 30-50% of the protein's mature molecular weight, elevating it from a core polypeptide of about 60 kDa to 76-114 kDa. The glycosylation enhances protein stability, proper folding, and cell surface localization, while also modulating ligand binding affinity; for instance, high-mannose glycoforms promote stronger monocyte adhesion compared to fully processed complex forms.
In addition to N-linked modifications, ICAM-1 features limited O-linked glycosylation at two serine or threonine residues, primarily in the extracellular region, though these contribute less to overall mass and function than N-glycans. Phosphorylation occurs predominantly in the short cytoplasmic tail, with key sites including tyrosine 485, which is targeted by Src and c-Met kinases upon ligand-induced clustering. This phosphorylation regulates intracellular signaling, cytoskeletal rearrangements, and protein trafficking, thereby influencing ICAM-1's role in cell adhesion dynamics without altering its core structure.
Proteolytic cleavage of the membrane-bound form generates a soluble variant (sICAM-1) through ectodomain shedding mediated by ADAM10, ADAM17, and matrix metalloproteinase-9 (MMP-9) at sites proximal to the transmembrane domain. This process, often triggered by inflammatory stimuli, releases a circulating form that retains partial ligand-binding capacity and can modulate systemic immune responses by competing with membrane ICAM-1. Phosphorylation at Tyr485 facilitates cleavage by MT1-MMP, linking these modifications to enhanced leukocyte transmigration.
The profile of these modifications varies by cell type, with endothelial cells typically exhibiting higher sialylation and complex N-glycan capping on ICAM-1, which fine-tunes adhesion and reduces non-specific interactions, compared to immune cells where high-mannose forms predominate under inflammatory conditions to boost rapid leukocyte recruitment. Such differences arise from cell-specific glycosyltransferase expression and contribute to tissue-specific regulation of ICAM-1 activity.

Biological Functions

Cell Adhesion and Migration

ICAM-1 serves as a key adhesion molecule on endothelial cells, mediating the firm attachment of leukocytes to the vascular wall primarily through interactions with 2-integrins, such as LFA-1 (CD11a/CD18) and Mac-1 (CD11b/CD18). These integrin bindings enable leukocytes to transition from loose rolling to stable arrest under physiological shear stress conditions in the bloodstream, facilitating their recruitment to sites of inflammation.
This adhesion is integral to the multi-step process of leukocyte extravasation, where ICAM-1 contributes to the stabilization of rolling initiated by selectins, promotes firm adhesion, and supports subsequent diapedesis through the endothelial barrier. During inflammation, upregulated ICAM-1 expression on endothelium enhances these interactions, allowing leukocytes to crawl and transmigrate efficiently into tissues. Studies using knockout models and blocking antibodies have shown that disruption of ICAM-1/2-integrin binding severely impairs this extravasation, underscoring its essential role.
Beyond leukocytes, ICAM-1 binds soluble ligands like fibrinogen, which bridges ICAM-1 on endothelium to activated platelets or leukocytes, promoting platelet-leukocyte aggregates and aiding in hemostasis and early wound repair. ICAM-1 also interacts with hyaluronan, a glycosaminoglycan in the extracellular matrix, which supports leukocyte motility and matrix remodeling during tissue healing processes. These additional bindings expand ICAM-1's role in coordinating cellular responses at injury sites.90367-Y)
In vitro assays, such as the modified Stamper-Woodruff binding assay on frozen endothelial sections, have demonstrated the shear-resistant nature of ICAM-1-mediated leukocyte adhesion, where rotating samples simulate blood flow and reveal robust, integrin-dependent attachments that withstand detachment forces. Parallel flow chamber experiments further confirm that ICAM-1/LFA-1 interactions maintain adhesion under dynamic shear stresses up to 2 dyn/cm, mimicking venular conditions. These assays highlight the mechanical strength of ICAM-1 in supporting migration without relying on static binding.

Immune Signaling and Other Roles

Upon ligation by integrins such as LFA-1, the cytoplasmic tail of ICAM-1 recruits Src family kinases, including Lyn, leading to rapid tyrosine phosphorylation and activation of downstream signaling cascades. This activation initiates the MAPK/ERK pathway and PI3K/Akt signaling, which promote cytoskeletal rearrangements essential for cellular responses like endothelial permeability changes during inflammation. In endothelial cells, ICAM-1 engagement further involves SHP-2-mediated Src activation, enhancing PI3K signaling and eNOS phosphorylation to facilitate leukocyte transmigration without direct mention of adhesion mechanics.
ICAM-1 also serves a costimulatory function in adaptive immunity, particularly in T-cell activation and antigen presentation by antigen-presenting cells (APCs). On APCs, ICAM-1 expression lowers the antigen threshold required for nave T-cell activation, amplifying signaling through LFA-1 engagement to enhance proliferation and cytokine production. In T cells themselves, ICAM-1 acts as an alternative costimulatory receptor, synergizing with CD28/B7 pathways via PI3K to promote survival and effector differentiation, distinct from primary adhesion roles. This costimulation is particularly evident in low-antigen scenarios, where ICAM-1/B7 co-expression on APCs markedly boosts IL-2 secretion and T-cell expansion.
Beyond immune signaling, ICAM-1 functions as a cellular receptor for certain viruses, facilitating entry into host cells. It serves as the primary receptor for the major group of human rhinoviruses, binding via the N-terminal domain to enable viral attachment and uncoating in respiratory epithelial cells.90688-0) Similarly, ICAM-1 mediates entry of Coxsackievirus A21, with the binding site overlapping rhinovirus recognition regions in the viral canyon, highlighting its role in non-enveloped virus infections. In reproductive biology, ICAM-1 regulates tight junction dynamics in the blood-testis barrier during spermatogenesis; its expression in Sertoli cells co-localizes with junctional proteins like ZO-1, supporting germ cell transit while maintaining barrier integrity.
The soluble form of ICAM-1 (sICAM-1), generated by proteolytic shedding or alternative splicing, modulates immune responses by acting as a decoy receptor that competes with membrane-bound ICAM-1 for integrin binding, such as LFA-1 on leukocytes. This competition reduces leukocyte adhesion and transmigration at inflammatory sites, exerting anti-inflammatory effects at elevated levels, as observed in conditions with high sICAM-1 circulation. In T-cell contexts, sICAM-1 inhibits costimulatory signaling by blocking LFA-1 interactions, thereby dampening excessive activation and cytokine release.

Protein Interactions

Key Binding Partners

ICAM-1 primarily interacts with 2-integrins on leukocytes, serving as a key ligand for facilitating cell adhesion. The most prominent binding partner is lymphocyte function-associated antigen-1 (LFA-1, CD11a/CD18), which binds to the N-terminal domains (D1 and D2) of ICAM-1 with an affinity in the range of 10-100 nM under physiological conditions. Macrophage-1 antigen (Mac-1, CD11b/CD18) also binds ICAM-1, primarily through its interaction with the third immunoglobulin-like domain (D3), enabling adhesion of monocytes and neutrophils. Additionally, complement receptor 4 (CD11c/CD18) engages ICAM-1 via a site in the fourth domain (D4), supporting interactions with dendritic cells and other antigen-presenting cells.
ICAM-1 functions as a receptor for certain viruses, particularly those in the Picornaviridae family. Major group rhinoviruses bind to the D1 domain of ICAM-1, utilizing this interaction for cellular entry during respiratory infections. Similarly, Coxsackievirus A21 attaches to a conserved site within the D1 domain, overlapping with the rhinovirus binding region and facilitating viral uncoating.
ICAM-1 also serves as a receptor for Plasmodium falciparum-infected erythrocytes via the parasite's erythrocyte membrane protein 1 (PfEMP1), binding primarily to the D1 domain of ICAM-1 in a site that overlaps but is distinct from the LFA-1 binding region; this interaction promotes cytoadherence in malaria pathology.
Other notable partners include fibrinogen, which binds to the first immunoglobulin domain of ICAM-1, potentially bridging leukocytes to endothelial surfaces. ICAM-1 also associates with ezrin, a cytoskeletal linker protein that connects the adhesion molecule to the actin cytoskeleton, aiding in membrane organization. Furthermore, ICAM-1 can bind hyaluronic acid, an extracellular matrix component, through sites that may influence cell-matrix interactions.
Soluble forms of ICAM-1, generated by proteolytic shedding, retain the ability to interact with the same 2-integrins (LFA-1, Mac-1, and CD11c/CD18) in circulation, modulating systemic immune responses.

Interaction Pathways

The interaction between ICAM-1 and integrins, such as LFA-1 (L2), initiates inside-out signaling pathways that enhance leukocyte adhesion. Upon chemokine stimulation, Rap1 GTPase is activated, recruiting talin and kindlin to the integrin cytoplasmic tails, which stabilizes the integrin in a high-affinity conformation for ICAM-1 binding, thereby promoting firm adhesion. This Rap1-mediated mechanism is essential for modulating integrin avidity without relying on other pathways like PI3K or PKC.
ICAM-1 participates in inflammatory cascades through a feedback loop involving NF-B transcription factor and pro-inflammatory cytokines. Cytokines like TNF- and IL-1 induce NF-B activation, which transcriptionally upregulates ICAM-1 expression on endothelial cells, facilitating leukocyte recruitment. In turn, ICAM-1 engagement amplifies cytokine production and sustains NF-B signaling, perpetuating the inflammatory response.
ICAM-1 integrates into the multi-step adhesion model via crosstalk with selectins and chemokine signaling. Initial leukocyte rolling mediated by selectins (e.g., P-selectin or E-selectin) exposes cells to endothelial chemokines, which trigger G-protein-coupled receptor signaling to activate Rap1 and subsequent integrin-ICAM-1 binding for firm arrest. This sequential interplay ensures efficient leukocyte extravasation at inflammatory sites.
In viral infection pathways, ICAM-1 serves as a receptor for major-group human rhinoviruses (HRV), where the ICAM-1-HRV complex undergoes clathrin-mediated endocytosis for viral entry. Binding induces receptor clustering and recruitment of clathrin adaptors, leading to internalization into early endosomes where low pH triggers viral uncoating and genome release.

Clinical and Pathological Significance

Role in Inflammatory and Autoimmune Diseases

ICAM-1 plays a central role in the pathogenesis of inflammatory and autoimmune diseases by facilitating leukocyte adhesion and transmigration across endothelial barriers, thereby promoting tissue infiltration and chronic inflammation. In these conditions, upregulated ICAM-1 expression on endothelial cells and leukocytes enhances the recruitment of inflammatory cells, such as monocytes and eosinophils, exacerbating disease progression. Soluble forms of ICAM-1 (sICAM-1), shed from cell surfaces, often circulate at elevated levels and serve as biomarkers correlating with disease severity.
In atherosclerosis, ICAM-1 is elevated on endothelial cells in vessel walls, where it promotes monocyte recruitment and adhesion, contributing to plaque formation and vascular inflammation. Studies in animal models demonstrate that aldosterone-induced atherosclerosis relies on ICAM-1-mediated mechanisms, as ICAM-1 knockout mice exhibit reduced plaque size, lipid accumulation, and inflammatory cell infiltration compared to wild-type counterparts. This adhesion molecule's expression is particularly prominent in early lesion-prone sites, independent of cholesterol levels, underscoring its role in initiating monocyte extravasation.
Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) involves ICAM-1 upregulation in synovial tissues, facilitating leukocyte infiltration into joints and perpetuating synovitis. Circulating sICAM-1 levels are significantly higher in RA patients' sera compared to healthy controls or osteoarthritis patients, with modest correlations to disease activity markers like joint scores and erythrocyte sedimentation rate. In multiple sclerosis (MS), ICAM-1 supports the transmigration of autoreactive T cells across the blood-brain barrier, enabling central nervous system (CNS) inflammation. During MS relapses, sICAM-1 concentrations increase in cerebrospinal fluid, while cell-surface ICAM-1 on CD3+ T cells decreases, indicating active shedding and serving as a marker of disease activity.
ICAM-1 also contributes to asthma and allergic responses by enhancing eosinophil adhesion to airway epithelium. In inflamed bronchial endothelium, ICAM-1 expression is upregulated, partially mediating eosinophil-endothelial interactions that drive airway eosinophilia and hyperresponsiveness, as evidenced in primate models where ICAM-1 blockade reduced these features. Post-2020 research highlights ICAM-1's involvement in COVID-19-related pathology, where elevated endothelial ICAM-1 expression in lung tissues correlates with severe inflammation, leukocyte recruitment, and cytokine storms involving IL-6 and TNF-, exacerbating endothelial dysfunction and thrombosis.

Therapeutic Targeting and Applications

Therapeutic strategies targeting ICAM-1 primarily aim to inhibit its expression or function to reduce leukocyte adhesion and inflammation in various diseases. Monoclonal antibodies against ICAM-1, such as enlimomab, a murine anti-human ICAM-1 antibody, were investigated in the 1990s and early 2000s for preventing ischemia-reperfusion injury in conditions like acute ischemic stroke and renal transplantation. In the Enlimomab Acute Stroke Trial (EAST), involving 625 patients, enlimomab failed to improve neurological outcomes and was associated with worsened disability, likely due to immunogenicity and proinflammatory effects from the murine origin, leading to its withdrawal from further development. Similarly, in renal transplant trials, enlimomab did not reduce the rate of acute rejection or delayed graft function and showed no significant difference in adverse events, including post-transplant infections, compared to placebo, contributing to its discontinuation.
Small molecules and antisense oligonucleotides represent alternative approaches to modulate ICAM-1. Natriuretic peptides, including atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP) and C-type natriuretic peptide (CNP), have been shown to suppress TNF--induced ICAM-1 expression on endothelial cells, thereby inhibiting leukocyte recruitment and vascular inflammation in preclinical models of ischemia-reperfusion and sepsis. Cannabinoid receptor 2 (CB2) agonists, such as JWH-133 and HU-308, reduce ICAM-1 and VCAM-1 expression in activated endothelial cells by blocking NF-B signaling, demonstrating anti-atherosclerotic effects in animal models through decreased monocyte adhesion. Alicaforsen, an antisense oligonucleotide targeting ICAM-1 mRNA, has undergone phase II and III trials for inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), including Crohn's disease and chronic pouchitis; while phase II rectal administration showed remission in some steroid-dependent patients with pouchitis and was well-tolerated, the phase III trial for chronic refractory pouchitis did not meet its primary endpoints, though it demonstrated safety and some clinical signals; intravenous dosing in Crohn's disease showed limited efficacy in broader cohorts.
Emerging therapies leverage gene editing and targeted delivery systems to downregulate or exploit ICAM-1. CRISPR-based gene knockout of ICAM-1 in hypoimmune cells has been explored to reduce immune cell adhesion in autoimmunity models, enhancing graft survival by limiting T-cell and innate immune responses without broad immunosuppression. ICAM-1-targeted nanoparticles, such as those conjugated with anti-ICAM-1 antibodies or peptides and loaded with drugs like paclitaxel, facilitate site-specific delivery to inflamed endothelium or tumor cells overexpressing ICAM-1, improving anti-cancer efficacy in breast cancer metastasis models and reducing monocyte adhesion in inflammatory sites. These approaches address challenges like off-target effects by focusing on upregulated ICAM-1 in pathology.
Post-2020 research has highlighted ICAM-1's potential in antiviral therapies, particularly by blocking viral entry. Anti-ICAM-1 antibodies inhibit rhinovirus binding and replication in airway epithelial models, as ICAM-1 serves as the primary receptor for major-group rhinoviruses responsible for ~60% of common colds; domain-specific antibodies reduced viral load in vivo, suggesting applications for acute respiratory infections. In COVID-19, elevated soluble ICAM-1 correlates with endothelial dysfunction and disease severity, prompting investigations into ICAM-1 modulation as an adjunct to mitigate coagulopathy and inflammation, though clinical trials remain preclinical or exploratory as of 2025. As of 2025, no ICAM-1-targeted therapies have received regulatory approval for clinical use, though research into ICAM-1 modulation for long COVID and other inflammatory conditions continues in preclinical stages.
