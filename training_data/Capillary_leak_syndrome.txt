Capillary leak syndrome
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Capillary leak syndrome (CLS), also known as systemic capillary leak syndrome (SCLS) or Clarkson disease when idiopathic, is a rare and potentially life-threatening disorder characterized by sudden and transient episodes of increased vascular permeability, resulting in the leakage of plasma and proteins from capillaries into surrounding tissues, leading to hypovolemic shock, generalized edema, hemoconcentration, and hypoalbuminemia. This condition manifests in recurrent attacks that can last from hours to days, often progressing through three phases: a prodromal phase with nonspecific symptoms like fatigue and nausea, an acute leak phase with severe hypotension and swelling, and a recovery phase marked by diuresis and normalization of fluid balance.
CLS is distinguished into primary (idiopathic, also known as SCLS or Clarkson disease) and secondary forms, with the primary type affecting fewer than 1 in 1,000,000 individuals worldwide and approximately 250-300 documented cases since its first description in 1960, predominantly in adults with a mean onset age of 45 years and a slight male predominance. Secondary CLS, which is more common, arises as a complication of infections (such as sepsis or viral illnesses), malignancies (particularly hematologic cancers), autoimmune diseases, or certain medications and immunotherapies like interleukin-2 or gemcitabine. The idiopathic form is often associated with monoclonal gammopathy of undetermined significance (MGUS) in up to 80-90% of cases, though its causal role remains unclear.
The pathophysiology involves endothelial dysfunction, possibly triggered by an aberrant immune response releasing cytokines that disrupt capillary integrity, leading to massive fluid shifts and organ hypoperfusion if untreated. Diagnosis relies on clinical presentation supported by laboratory findings such as elevated hematocrit (>60%), low serum albumin (<2 g/dL), and exclusion of other causes like sepsis or anaphylaxis, with no single definitive test available. Management focuses on supportive care during acute episodes, including cautious intravenous fluid resuscitation to avoid overload, vasopressors for hemodynamic support, and prophylactic therapies like intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) or theophylline-terbutaline combinations to reduce attack frequency and severity, though no curative treatment exists and mortality can reach 30-40% over 10 years in severe cases.

Clinical features

Symptoms

Systemic capillary leak syndrome (SCLS) is characterized by recurrent episodes of sudden and severe symptoms resulting from increased vascular permeability, leading to fluid shifts from the intravascular to the interstitial space. Patients typically experience a prodromal phase lasting hours to days before the acute leak, marked by flu-like malaise, generalized weakness, fatigue, and myalgias. Additional prodromal complaints often include headache, nausea, abdominal pain, vomiting, diarrhea, cough, rhinorrhea, and low-grade fever, which may mimic a viral infection. Lightheadedness, dizziness, and a faint feeling due to early orthostatic hypotension are also common during this period.
The acute leak phase manifests with rapid onset of generalized edema, particularly affecting the extremities, face, trunk, and abdomen, as fluid leaks into tissues. This is often accompanied by sudden weight gain of 2 to 10 kg or more within hours, reflecting massive third-spacing of fluids, along with increased thirst (polydipsia). Severe hypotension ensues, causing symptoms such as profound dizziness, lightheadedness, syncope, and extreme fatigue, potentially progressing to hypovolemic shock while consciousness is typically preserved. Oliguria develops as a result of hypovolemia and reduced renal perfusion. In severe cases, respiratory distress arises from noncardiogenic pulmonary edema or pleural effusions, leading to shortness of breath and hypoxemia. Gastrointestinal symptoms, including persistent abdominal pain and vomiting, may intensify during the attack.

Disease phases

Capillary leak syndrome, particularly in its idiopathic form known as idiopathic systemic capillary leak syndrome (ISCLS), typically follows a characteristic triphasic pattern during acute episodes, consisting of a prodromal phase, a leak phase, and a recruitment phase. This progression reflects transient endothelial dysfunction leading to reversible vascular permeability changes.
The prodromal phase lasts from hours to several days and is marked by nonspecific symptoms resembling a viral illness, such as fatigue, myalgias, or low-grade fever, which may trigger initial endothelial activation through immune dysregulation. Transition to the leak phase is often abrupt, potentially driven by surges in proinflammatory cytokines like interleukin-6 (IL-6) and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-), which exacerbate endothelial barrier disruption.
During the leak phase, which endures 1 to 3 days, massive plasma extravasation occurs due to increased capillary permeability, resulting in acute hypovolemic shock, hemoconcentration (with hematocrit often exceeding 60%), and hypoalbuminemia, alongside risks of multiorgan failure from hypoperfusion. This phase involves up to 70% of plasma volume shifting to the interstitial space, manifesting as generalized edema.
The subsequent recruitment phase spans 1 to 7 days, characterized by spontaneous restoration of endothelial integrity and rapid reabsorption of extravasated fluid into the vascular compartment, leading to polyuria, hypertension, and potential complications such as pulmonary edema or compartment syndrome from swift intravascular volume expansion. Phase resolution occurs as cytokine levels normalize and endothelial function recovers, though the exact triggers remain incompletely understood.
In contrast, secondary forms of capillary leak syndrome, often linked to infections, drugs, or malignancies, typically present as monophasic events without this distinct triphasic sequence, lacking the recurrent and phased nature seen in ISCLS.

Etiology and pathophysiology

Idiopathic form

Idiopathic systemic capillary leak syndrome (ISCLS), also known as Clarkson's disease, is a rare and potentially life-threatening disorder characterized by recurrent, spontaneous episodes of profound hypotension, hemoconcentration, and generalized edema resulting from transient increases in vascular permeability, without an identifiable underlying cause. First described in 1960 by Clarkson et al. in a case of cyclical edema and shock due to capillary hyperpermeability, the condition manifests as episodic attacks that can lead to hypovolemic shock and multiorgan failure if untreated. The idiopathic nature is confirmed by the absence of precipitating factors, distinguishing it from secondary forms, and diagnosis requires exclusion of infections, medications, malignancies, or other triggers through comprehensive clinical evaluation.
A striking feature of ISCLS is its strong association with monoclonal gammopathy of undetermined significance (MGUS), observed in approximately 80% of cases, most commonly involving IgG-kappa paraproteins. While the precise role of these monoclonal proteins remains unclear, they may contribute to endothelial dysfunction by promoting vascular hyperpermeability, potentially through interactions with inflammatory pathways, though direct causation has not been definitively established. The idiopathic form typically presents in middle-aged adults, with a median age of onset around 44 years and no evident familial inheritance pattern, underscoring its sporadic occurrence.
Rare pediatric cases have been reported, often presenting with similar episodic features but potentially differing in associated immunological profiles, such as IgA deficiency rather than MGUS. Limited genetic studies suggest a possible role for variants in endothelial cell receptors, including polymorphisms in genes like PDGFRB and EDG2, which may influence vascular integrity and susceptibility to permeability; however, these findings are preliminary and require further validation in larger cohorts. More recent studies have identified potential variants in genes such as TLN1 (associated with familial cases) and MYOF (in chronic forms), though these remain investigational as of 2025. Pathophysiological mechanisms in ISCLS may involve cytokine-mediated endothelial barrier disruption, leading to plasma extravasation during acute flares.

Secondary forms

Secondary forms of capillary leak syndrome (CLS) are acquired conditions triggered by identifiable external factors, in contrast to the idiopathic form, which occurs spontaneously without a clear precipitant. These secondary cases often present with a monophasic course, resolving upon removal or treatment of the underlying trigger, and are characterized by endothelial barrier disruption leading to plasma extravasation.
Common triggers include sepsis, which is the most frequent cause of secondary CLS, resulting from systemic inflammation and endothelial damage during severe infections. Viral infections, such as COVID-19, have been linked to CLS through cytokine-mediated vascular permeability, with reports of acute presentations in severe cases. Autoimmune diseases, including systemic lupus erythematosus, can precipitate secondary CLS via immune complex deposition and inflammation affecting capillary integrity. Post-surgical states, such as ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome, induce CLS in approximately 2% of severe cases due to vascular endothelial growth factor release and fluid shifts.
Drug-induced secondary CLS is well-documented, particularly with cytokine modulators like interleukin-2 (IL-2), which causes severe fluid retention in up to 64% of treated patients through cytokine release syndrome. Interferon-alpha (IFN-alpha) has been associated with CLS in case reports involving endothelial dysfunction. Chemotherapeutics such as gemcitabine are implicated in the majority of reported cases (83 instances), often presenting as acute or chronic leaks with high mortality risk. Recent immunotherapy-related cases, including pembrolizumab in gynecologic malignancies and relatlimab/nivolumab combinations, have emerged between 2023 and 2025, highlighting immune checkpoint inhibitors as emerging triggers.
Malignancy-associated secondary CLS includes engraftment syndrome following stem cell transplantation, occurring in 7-55% of autologous hematopoietic stem cell transplant recipients due to inflammatory cytokine surges during neutrophil recovery. Hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH), often secondary to infections, autoimmune conditions, or malignancies, features CLS as a component of its hyperinflammatory state. Differentiation syndrome in acute promyelocytic leukemia therapy affects approximately 25% of patients, driven by all-trans retinoic acid-induced myeloid differentiation and capillary leak.

Pathophysiological mechanisms

Capillary leak syndrome (CLS) is characterized by a profound increase in vascular permeability, primarily driven by disruption of endothelial cell adherens junctions (AJs). Cytokines such as vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), interleukin-6 (IL-6), and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-) play central roles in this process by targeting vascular endothelial cadherin, a key component of AJs, leading to endothelial barrier dysfunction and paracellular gap formation. This cytokine-mediated disruption allows protein-rich fluid to extravasate from the intravascular space into the interstitium, resulting in intravascular hypovolemia, generalized edema, and hemoconcentration, with losses of up to 30%-50% of plasma albumin occurring within hours of onset.
Hypercytokinemia underlies much of the systemic inflammatory response in CLS, resembling aspects of sepsis through the release of proinflammatory mediators that amplify endothelial permeability across various tissues. Degradation of the endothelial glycocalyx, a protective carbohydrate layer on the vascular surface, further exacerbates this permeability; elevated levels of shed glycocalyx components like soluble syndecan-1 and hyaluronic acid during acute episodes indicate enzymatic breakdown by matrix metalloproteinases and ADAMTS, promoting fluid leakage and local inflammation. Mast cell degranulation may contribute in some cases by releasing vasoactive mediators that indirectly enhance endothelial permeability, though its role remains under investigation and is not universally observed.
In the idiopathic form of CLS, potential involvement of autoantibodies or paraproteins, such as monoclonal IgG, may impair vascular integrity by interfering with endothelial protective mechanisms, although direct causality has not been established. These paraproteins are detected in a majority of idiopathic cases but their precise contribution to permeability remains unclear.

Epidemiology

Prevalence and incidence

Capillary leak syndrome (CLS) is an exceedingly rare condition, with the idiopathic form (idiopathic systemic capillary leak syndrome, or ISCLS) estimated to have an annual incidence of less than 1 per 1,000,000 individuals worldwide. As of 2025, approximately 250-500 cases of ISCLS have been reported in the medical literature since its initial description in 1960, reflecting its extreme rarity and likely underdiagnosis. The global prevalence is similarly low, with estimates suggesting fewer than 250 active cases at any given time. Underreporting is attributed to diagnostic challenges, including the episodic nature of the disease and its clinical overlap with conditions such as sepsis, which can mimic or complicate recognition.
Secondary forms of CLS, triggered by underlying conditions or therapies, exhibit higher recognition and incidence rates within specific high-risk populations. For instance, severe capillary leak occurs in approximately 2% of cases of ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome (OHSS), a complication of fertility treatments. In patients undergoing chemotherapy, particularly for hematologic malignancies, the incidence of drug-induced CLS varies widely by regimen, ranging from 5.3% to 100% in affected cohorts. Similarly, engraftment syndrome following hematopoietic stem cell transplantation is associated with CLS in 7% to 55% of cases, depending on diagnostic criteria.
Geographically, ISCLS cases are predominantly reported from Europe and North America, where most documented instances originate, possibly due to better diagnostic infrastructure and awareness in these regions. Specialized registries, such as the European multicenter SCLS registry and the National Institutes of Health (NIH) cohort, have facilitated improved tracking and characterization of the disease. Recent trends indicate an uptick in secondary CLS cases since 2020, linked to COVID-19 infections and vaccinations, as well as immunotherapies like checkpoint inhibitors, with multiple case reports and series highlighting these associations in the literature up to 2025.

Demographics and risk factors

Capillary leak syndrome (CLS) primarily affects adults, with a median age at onset ranging from 44 to 48 years, though cases have been reported from newborns to individuals over 85 years old. The condition is rare in children, with only isolated pediatric cases documented in the literature. There is a slight male predominance, with a male-to-female ratio of approximately 1.4:1. While most reported cases occur in middle-aged white adults, CLS has no strong racial or ethnic predilection, with instances noted across Asian, African American, Hispanic, and Middle Eastern populations.
For the idiopathic form of CLS, also known as idiopathic systemic capillary leak syndrome (ISCLS), a key risk factor is the presence of monoclonal gammopathy of undetermined significance (MGUS), observed in 80-90% of adult patients. This association is particularly strong in adults, though MGUS is absent in pediatric cases. Secondary forms of CLS are linked to various predisposing factors, including immunocompromised states such as those following hematopoietic stem cell transplantation, recent infections (particularly upper respiratory tract infections), and exposure to certain high-risk drugs like immune checkpoint inhibitors (e.g., pembrolizumab or nivolumab).
Although typically sporadic without family history, rare familial clusters have been reported, including in Jewish families. Recent studies have identified genetic links, such as Talin1 gene dysfunction, in some cases of ISCLS (as of 2024).

Diagnosis

Clinical presentation

Capillary leak syndrome (CLS) typically presents with an acute onset of a shock-like state, characterized by a rapid and profound drop in systemic blood pressure due to plasma extravasation from the intravascular space into the interstitium. This hypovolemic shock mimics distributive shock or anaphylaxis, often leading to initial misdiagnoses such as sepsis or cardiogenic shock.
In the idiopathic form, patients frequently report a history of recurrent episodes, occurring from once a year to multiple times annually, whereas secondary CLS is more commonly associated with a single acute event triggered by underlying conditions like infections or medications. A prodromal phase, lasting 6 to 24 hours, may precede the leak and includes nonspecific symptoms such as malaise, fatigue, myalgias, headache, or flu-like illness, underscoring the importance of recognizing this phase for early intervention to mitigate severity.
Physical examination reveals generalized anasarca with marked edema in the extremities, face, and sometimes visceral involvement, accompanied by tachycardia, cool and mottled extremities indicative of poor perfusion, and oliguria from reduced renal blood flow. These findings, combined with the rapid development of hemoconcentration, help differentiate CLS from conditions like sepsis or heart failure, where hemoconcentration is not a prominent feature.

Laboratory and imaging findings

Laboratory findings in capillary leak syndrome (CLS) are characterized by evidence of plasma extravasation and hemoconcentration during the acute leak phase. Hallmark abnormalities include hypoalbuminemia, typically with serum albumin levels below 2 g/dL due to protein leakage into the interstitium, and elevated hematocrit often exceeding 60%, reflecting hemoconcentration from intravascular volume depletion. These changes are transient and resolve during the post-leak recovery phase as fluid and proteins return to the vascular compartment.
In the idiopathic form of systemic CLS, serum protein electrophoresis often reveals a monoclonal gammopathy in approximately 80% of cases, usually an IgG kappa paraprotein at low levels (median 0.6 g/dL). Renal involvement is common, manifesting as acute kidney injury secondary to hypoperfusion, with elevated serum creatinine levels (e.g., >2 mg/dL in reported cases).
Imaging studies play a supportive role in identifying complications and excluding alternative diagnoses. Echocardiography is frequently employed to detect pericardial effusions, which may contribute to hemodynamic instability. Computed tomography (CT) scans of the chest, abdomen, or extremities can reveal organ edema, pleural or peritoneal effusions, and, less commonly, compartment syndrome from tissue swelling.
Diagnosis requires exclusion of mimicking conditions through targeted testing. Blood and urine cultures are typically negative, ruling out sepsis as a cause of hypotension and edema. Cardiac biomarkers such as troponins are usually normal, helping differentiate CLS from myocardial infarction or other primary cardiac pathologies.

Management

Acute phase treatment

The acute phase of capillary leak syndrome (CLS) involves immediate supportive interventions to address hypovolemia, hypotension, and organ hypoperfusion during active episodes of vascular leakage, typically managed in an intensive care unit (ICU) setting.
Fluid resuscitation is a cornerstone of acute management, employing cautious administration of intravenous crystalloids or colloids, such as albumin, to restore intravascular volume and maintain organ perfusion while minimizing the risk of exacerbating interstitial edema or pulmonary overload. Guidelines emphasize limiting fluid volumesoften to less than 1-2 liters initiallyand frequent reassessment to avoid over-resuscitation, which can precipitate complications during the subsequent recruitment phase when fluids re-enter the vasculature.
For refractory hypotension despite fluid therapy, vasopressors such as norepinephrine or epinephrine are initiated early to support blood pressure and tissue perfusion, with careful titration under hemodynamic monitoring to prevent further capillary stress. Patients are typically monitored in the ICU using central venous or arterial lines to guide fluid and vasopressor adjustments based on parameters like central venous pressure and cardiac output.
Supportive care addresses organ-specific threats during the acute attack, including diuretics (e.g., furosemide) in the recovery phase to manage fluid overload and prevent pulmonary edema, hemodialysis for acute renal failure if indicated, and mechanical ventilation for severe respiratory compromise. In secondary forms of CLS triggered by inflammation, such as drug-induced cases, glucocorticoids like methylprednisolone may be administered to mitigate the underlying inflammatory response, though their efficacy in idiopathic CLS remains uncertain.

Prophylactic and long-term therapy

Prophylactic therapy for idiopathic systemic capillary leak syndrome (SCLS) primarily involves intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) administered at a dose of 2 g/kg monthly, which has been shown to reduce the frequency of acute attacks by approximately 85-90% through its anti-inflammatory effects and stabilization of vascular permeability. In a cohort of 18 patients, IVIG prophylaxis led to a median annual attack rate dropping from 2.6 episodes per patient to zero, with 15 patients remaining attack-free for up to three years. This approach is particularly effective in cases associated with monoclonal gammopathy of undetermined significance (MGUS), where IVIG improves long-term survival by preventing recurrent flares.
An alternative or adjunctive combination prophylaxis regimen utilizes theophylline and terbutaline, which work synergistically to stabilize the endothelium and reduce vascular permeability by increasing intracellular cyclic adenosine monophosphate levels. This oral therapy has demonstrated efficacy in decreasing attack frequency and severity by up to 30-fold in treated patients, with sustained benefits observed over a median follow-up of nine years in a series of eight cases. While effective, this regimen requires monitoring for sympathomimetic side effects and maintenance of therapeutic theophylline levels to prevent recurrences.
Long-term management includes regular monitoring of serum albumin levels to detect early signs of permeability shifts, alongside patient-maintained attack diaries to track episode patterns and inform therapy adjustments. In cases of sustained remission, such as prolonged absence of attacks, prophylactic doses may be tapered gradually, with some patients successfully reducing IVIG to 0.5-1 g/kg every two weeks while maintaining stability.
Lifestyle measures emphasize avoiding known triggers, particularly infections, which can precipitate episodes; patients are advised to practice rigorous hygiene and infection prevention strategies. Vaccination is recommended but with cautiondeferral during acute illness is standard, and certain vaccines may be contraindicated in those with a history of SCLS due to rare reports of flare induction.
For the approximately 80-90% of SCLS cases associated with MGUS, initial management focuses on observation with periodic monitoring of monoclonal protein levels, complete blood counts, and free light chain ratios to detect progression. Targeted therapy, such as chemotherapy or stem cell transplantation, is reserved for instances of progression to multiple myeloma, which may also resolve associated SCLS flares.

Emerging and investigational approaches

Recent research has explored anti-vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) agents, such as bevacizumab, for managing refractory capillary leak syndrome (CLS), particularly in late-onset cases following allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (allo-HSCT). In a 2025 case report, a 47-year-old woman with acute myeloid leukemia developed severe late-onset CLS 10 months post-allo-HSCT, characterized by oliguria, bilateral lower limb edema, and pleural effusion, unresponsive to initial therapies including methylprednisolone and tocilizumab. Administration of bevacizumab at 5 mg/kg intravenously biweekly for four cycles led to rapid clinical improvement, including increased urine output, resolution of edema, and normalization of plasma albumin levels to 27.5 g/L within one week, with complete resolution of pleural effusion. This success is attributed to bevacizumab's inhibition of VEGF, which reduces endothelial permeability, as evidenced by the patient's pretreatment VEGF level of 7.9 pg/ml.
Cytokine-targeted therapies, including tocilizumab, an interleukin-6 (IL-6) receptor inhibitor, have shown promise in treating CLS driven by hyperinflammatory responses, especially in cases induced by immunotherapy. A 2024 case involved a 62-year-old woman with lung adenocarcinoma who developed severe cytokine release syndrome (CRS) with capillary leak featuresmanifesting as hypotension, tachycardia, and elevated IL-6 levels of 108,878 pg/ml14 days after ipilimumab plus nivolumab. Treatment with tocilizumab (8 mg/kg intravenously) alongside high-dose methylprednisolone and CytoSorb hemadsorption resulted in hemodynamic stabilization, reduced vasopressor requirements, and full recovery of kidney function, allowing ICU discharge after four days. Such interventions target IL-6-mediated endothelial damage in immunotherapy-related CLS, where CRS often overlaps with systemic leakage.
In the context of stem cell transplantation, protocols aimed at modulating engraftment syndrome (ES)a condition featuring capillary leakhave focused on reducing incidence through adjusted conditioning regimens and prophylactic measures. High-dose granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF) use during mobilization has been linked to increased ES risk in pediatric allogeneic HSCT, with incidence rates up to 28% in cohorts receiving it, prompting protocols to limit or avoid G-CSF in high-risk patients to mitigate cytokine-driven endothelial permeability. Additionally, reduced-intensity conditioning and prophylactic low-dose corticosteroids have been employed to lower ES occurrence, as rapid engraftment post-autologous HSCT correlates with higher permeability events. These approaches emphasize endothelial stabilization to prevent CLS-like features during neutrophil recovery.
The surge in secondary CLS cases from 2020 to 2023, particularly those triggered by SARS-CoV-2 infection, has prompted investigation into antivirals and steroids for infection-associated endothelial dysfunction. During the COVID-19 pandemic, SARS-CoV-2 was implicated in 26 severe CLS episodes among 84 patients with monoclonal gammopathy-associated CLS, with a 42% mortality rate compared to 17% in non-COVID cases, highlighting increased incidence of infection-triggered flares. Although corticosteroids were administered in multiple instances, they proved ineffective for severe episodes, while supportive care with intravenous fluids predominated; antivirals like remdesivir were not specifically evaluated for CLS mitigation in these contexts but formed part of broader COVID management. Post-vaccination CLS cases also rose, with steroids and intravenous immunoglobulin used in recovery, though outcomes varied.
Ongoing clinical investigations as of 2025 include optimizations of intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) dosing and endothelial protectants to address unmet needs in CLS management. Natural history studies, such as NCT00936325, continue to evaluate IVIG's role in reducing flare frequency, with protocols exploring tapering from high-dose (2 g/kg) to maintenance (1 g/kg monthly) regimens, achieving attack-free periods of up to 12 years in some patients without increased relapse. For endothelial protection, agents like FX06a synthetic peptide derived from fibrin are under investigation for restoring barrier function in idiopathic systemic CLS crises, showing potential in preclinical models to counteract hyperpermeability. No large-scale phase III trials for CLS-specific endothelial protectants were active in 2025, but these approaches build on VEGF and cytokine modulation strategies.

Prognosis and complications

Survival and outcomes

Capillary leak syndrome (CLS) exhibits variable survival rates depending on the form and management approach. In idiopathic CLS, modern prophylactic treatments have improved outcomes, with reported 5-year survival rates ranging from 73% to 94%. Untreated acute episodes carry a high mortality risk, up to 30% in severe cases.
Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) prophylaxis significantly enhances prognosis by reducing the frequency of attacks. Studies show a median attack rate decreasing from 2.25 episodes per year to zero with IVIG, compared to traditional theophylline-based regimens, effectively limiting episodes to fewer than 0.5 per year in most patients.
Prognosis is influenced by several factors, including early diagnosis, which allows timely intervention and reduces fatal complications, and the absence of comorbidities, which correlates with better overall survival. Idiopathic CLS generally has a more favorable outlook than secondary forms, particularly those associated with malignancy, where outcomes are notably worse due to underlying disease progression.
Long-term outcomes vary, though some patients experience remission with management, while others have persistent edema requiring ongoing care. Recent data from 2023-2025 indicate improved survival in drug-induced CLS cases when the offending agent is promptly discontinued, often leading to resolution without recurrence.

Associated complications

During acute episodes of idiopathic systemic capillary leak syndrome (ISCLS), patients are at risk for several severe complications arising from hypovolemia and tissue hypoperfusion. Rhabdomyolysis occurs due to ischemia-induced muscle breakdown, potentially exacerbating renal injury through myoglobin release. Compartment syndrome develops from massive edema and fluid sequestration in muscle compartments, leading to ischemia and necrosis, sometimes necessitating fasciotomy; this complication carries risks of infection or hyperkalemia-induced cardiac arrest during surgical intervention. Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) can emerge during the recovery phase from rapid fluid remobilization, causing flash pulmonary edema and impaired gas exchange. Multi-organ failure frequently results from prolonged hypoperfusion, affecting vital systems such as the heart, lungs, and brain, and represents a leading cause of mortality during attacks.
Renal complications in ISCLS often stem from recurrent episodes of hypoperfusion and ischemic injury. Acute kidney injury is common during flares, with hypoalbuminemia and hemoconcentration further compromising renal perfusion.
Cardiovascular sequelae include fluid-related overload and electrical disturbances during the post-leak recruitment phase. Pericardial effusion can occur due to capillary leakage into the pericardial space, potentially causing tamponade in severe cases, though it is less frequent than pleural effusions. Arrhythmias, such as ventricular tachycardia, may arise from electrolyte imbalances (e.g., hypokalemia or hyperkalemia from rhabdomyolysis) or myocardial ischemia during hypotensive episodes.
Thromboembolic events pose a significant risk due to hemoconcentration and venous stasis during the oliguric phase of attacks, increasing blood viscosity and promoting clot formation. Prophylactic anticoagulation is often recommended between episodes to mitigate deep vein thrombosis or pulmonary embolism.
Complications secondary to therapeutic interventions include adverse effects from commonly used agents. Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) therapy, employed for prophylaxis, can trigger infusion reactions such as fever, chills, or anaphylaxis in susceptible patients, necessitating careful monitoring. Theophylline, used in some prophylactic regimens to stabilize endothelial function, may cause side effects including arrhythmias, tremor, anxiety, and insomnia, requiring therapeutic drug level monitoring to avoid toxicity.

History and background

Discovery and initial descriptions

Capillary leak syndrome, also known as systemic capillary leak syndrome (SCLS), was first described in 1960 by Bayard Clarkson and colleagues in a report detailing two adult patients who experienced recurrent episodes of severe edema and hypovolemic shock. The primary case involved a 34-year-old woman with episodic attacks characterized by sudden weight gain from fluid retention, hypotension, and generalized anasarca, while the second patient, a 53-year-old man, presented with similar recurrent shocks leading to death. Clarkson et al. demonstrated through infusion experiments that plasma obtained during acute episodes induced similar permeability changes in animal models, establishing the condition's hallmark of transient vascular hyperpermeability without identifiable infection or allergy.
The term "systemic capillary leak syndrome" emerged from autopsy findings in the fatal case, which revealed extensive interstitial edema in organs such as the lungs and heart, low intravascular protein levels, and no evidence of thrombosis or structural vascular damage, pointing to widespread capillary leakage as the underlying mechanism. These observations differentiated SCLS from conditions like anaphylaxis, as attacks lacked urticaria, bronchospasm, or response to antihistamines, and occurred without clear triggers. Early recognition was hampered by diagnostic challenges, with initial presentations often mistaken for idiopathic cyclical edema due to the periodic nature or sepsis given the shock and hemoconcentration.
The condition remained extremely rare in its early years, primarily from Europe and the United States. The condition is also referred to as Clarkson's disease, named in honor of Bayard D. Clarkson, the lead author of the seminal report.

Key developments and research milestones

In the 1980s, research on systemic capillary leak syndrome (SCLS) increasingly highlighted its frequent association with monoclonal gammopathy of undetermined significance (MGUS), with case reports and analyses identifying monoclonal proteins in up to 80% of affected patients, laying the groundwork for recognizing monoclonal gammopathy-associated SCLS as a distinct subtype.
During the 1990s and 2000s, efforts to better characterize the disease led to the establishment of international patient registries, including the European SCLS registry initiated in 1997, which facilitated multicenter data collection on over 28 cases by 2011 and improved understanding of clinical patterns and outcomes. Concurrently, prophylactic intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) therapy emerged as a key advancement, with initial reports in the early 2000s demonstrating its efficacy in reducing attack frequency and severity; a 2008 study by Lambert et al. described successful use in acute episodes and long-term prevention.
The 2010s brought deeper insights into SCLS pathophysiology through cytokine profiling studies, revealing elevated levels of proinflammatory mediators such as IL-6, TNF-, IL-8, CXCL10, and CCL2 during acute episodes, suggesting inflammation and potential infectious triggers contribute to endothelial hyperpermeability. Additionally, the theophylline-terbutaline regimen, introduced in 1999, became more standardized during this decade as clinical reviews and guidelines endorsed its use for prophylaxis, with sustained therapeutic levels reducing episode frequency by up to 30-fold in case series.
In the 2020s, secondary SCLS cases surged due to COVID-19 infections and immunotherapies, with multiple reports documenting severe exacerbations or de novo presentations linked to SARS-CoV-2 and checkpoint inhibitors like nivolumab, prompting heightened awareness of iatrogenic and viral triggers. Bevacizumab case reports in 2025 showed promise in refractory cases by targeting vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF)-mediated permeability, leading to rapid symptom resolution in post-hematopoietic stem cell transplant patients. A comprehensive review published in November 2024 summarized advances in epidemiology, pathophysiology, and management. Cohort studies further confirmed improved survival with IVIG prophylaxis, reporting 5-year rates of 91% in treated patients compared to 47% in untreated ones.
Despite these advances, significant research gaps persist, including the absence of randomized controlled trials due to the disease's rarity, and ongoing investigations into endothelial biomarkers like VEGF and syndecan-1 to enable earlier diagnosis and targeted therapies.
