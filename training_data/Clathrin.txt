Clathrin
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Clathrin is a cytosolic protein complex that self-assembles into a polyhedral lattice to coat vesicles, playing a central role in clathrin-mediated endocytosis (CME) and intracellular membrane trafficking in eukaryotic cells. It consists of triskelion-shaped units, each formed by three heavy chains (approximately 190 kDa) and three associated light chains (25-40 kDa), which polymerize to create a scaffold of hexagons and pentagons that drives membrane invagination and cargo uptake at the plasma membrane. This lattice structure facilitates the selective internalization of receptors, nutrients, and signaling molecules, such as low-density lipoprotein (LDL) and transferrin, essential for cellular homeostasis and signaling regulation.
Beyond endocytosis, clathrin contributes to protein sorting in the trans-Golgi network (TGN) and endosomal compartments, directing cargo to lysosomes or secretory pathways, and has been implicated in diverse processes including mitotic spindle organization and pathogen entry. The assembly of clathrin coats is initiated by adaptor proteins like AP-2, which bind to phosphoinositides and recruit clathrin triskelions, followed by accessory factors such as dynamin for vesicle scission and auxilin/Hsc70 for uncoating after fission. Discovered in the 1970s, clathrin's functions highlight its versatility as a multifunctional scaffold, with disruptions linked to diseases like neurodegeneration and cancer.

History and Discovery

Initial Isolation

Clathrin was first identified and isolated by Barbara Pearse in 1976 from coated vesicles purified from pig brain tissue. The purification process involved homogenizing pig brain in a buffer, followed by differential centrifugation to obtain a crude microsomal fraction, and then sucrose density gradient centrifugation to separate the coated vesicles, which banded at a density of approximately 1.17 g/cm. These vesicles were characterized biochemically, revealing clathrin as the predominant protein component with an apparent molecular weight of 180,000 Da, comprising over 70% of the total coat protein mass.
Electron microscopy of the isolated coated vesicles demonstrated that clathrin forms a distinctive polyhedral lattice structure on the vesicle coats, consisting of closed networks of hexagons and pentagons with a lattice spacing of about 18 nm. This lattice was observed in negatively stained preparations, showing variability in vesicle sizes (around 70-120 nm in diameter) and confirming the geometric arrangement essential for enclosing membrane during intracellular transport.
Early biochemical studies in the late 1970s confirmed clathrin's ability to self-assemble in vitro into cage-like structures under specific ionic conditions, such as low pH (around 6.0) and reduced salt concentrations (e.g., 100-200 mM MES buffer). These assays involved dissociating clathrin coats with Tris buffer at neutral pH and neutral salt, followed by reassembly upon adjusting to acidic conditions, yielding empty polyhedral cages morphologically similar to those on native vesicles.

Key Research Milestones

In the 1980s, significant advances in molecular biology enabled the cloning and sequencing of the clathrin heavy chain gene, providing the first complete primary structure of the protein. Researchers deduced the 1675-amino acid sequence of the rat clathrin heavy chain from cDNA clones, predicting a molecular weight of 191,569 Da and revealing its structural domains. Concurrently, the identification of clathrin light chain isoforms LCa and LCb highlighted their polymorphic nature and shared repeated heptad motifs, which are crucial for heavy chain interactions. These findings established the genetic basis for clathrin's triskelion subunit composition and laid the groundwork for studying its assembly.
During the 1990s and 2000s, genetic screens in yeast and mammals elucidated the roles of adaptor proteins in clathrin-mediated trafficking, identifying key components that link clathrin to cargo and membranes. In yeast, screens for mutants defective in endocytosis uncovered adaptors like Pan1p, a multivalent protein homologous to mammalian Eps15, which coordinates clathrin assembly with actin dynamics. Mammalian studies, including knockout models, confirmed the essential functions of AP-1 and AP-2 complexes in sorting at the trans-Golgi network and plasma membrane, respectively. Additionally, dynamin was discovered and characterized as a GTPase essential for the scission of clathrin-coated vesicles, with early work linking its GTP hydrolysis to vesicle release.
From the 2010s to 2025, structural biology breakthroughs, particularly cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM), resolved clathrin lattices at high resolution, transforming understanding of coat architecture. Structural biology breakthroughs, particularly cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM), resolved clathrin lattices at high resolutions in the 2010s, such as ~9 in 2019, detailing triskelion interactions and lattice curvature mechanisms essential for vesicle budding. More recent 2025 research introduced next-generation small molecule inhibitors targeting the clathrin terminal domain, acutely disrupting clathrin-mediated endocytosis without off-target effects on other pathways. Parallel genomic studies identified de novo mutations in the clathrin heavy chain gene (CLTC) associated with neurodevelopmental disorders, such as intellectual disability, impairing synaptic architecture and learning in model organisms. These milestones underscore clathrin's dynamic role in cellular trafficking and its therapeutic potential.

Molecular Structure

Heavy and Light Chains

Clathrin is composed of heavy chains (CHC) and light chains (CLC) that form the basic structural units of its coats. The clathrin heavy chain, approximately 190 kDa in molecular weight, is encoded by the CLTC gene in humans. This polypeptide features an N-terminal seven-bladed -propeller domain that mediates interactions with adaptor proteins, while the C-terminal region contains the trimerization domain essential for heavy chain oligomerization. The calculated molecular weight of the heavy chain is 192 kDa, though it often migrates at around 180 kDa on SDS-PAGE due to post-translational modifications.
Clathrin light chains exist as two isoforms in vertebrates: CLCa, encoded by the CLTA gene, and CLCb, encoded by the CLTB gene, with molecular weights of approximately 25 kDa and 25 kDa, respectively. These light chains non-covalently associate with the proximal domain near the C-terminus of the heavy chain, stabilizing the structure and modulating clathrin assembly dynamics by influencing polymerization rates and lattice stability. The isoforms share about 60% sequence identity but exhibit distinct regulatory roles, with CLCa and CLCb showing tissue-specific expression patterns.
Clathrin heavy and light chains are highly conserved across eukaryotes, reflecting their fundamental role in membrane trafficking. In vertebrates, the heavy chain orthologs display over 90% sequence similarity, underscoring evolutionary pressures to maintain core structural and functional features. Light chain conservation is similarly robust within vertebrates, though isoform diversification occurred via gene duplication during chordate evolution.

Triskelion and Lattice Formation

Clathrin exists in solution primarily as a triskelion, the basic oligomeric unit composed of three heavy chains (each approximately 190 kDa) and three associated light chains (2535 kDa each), forming a symmetrical three-legged structure with a central vertex and radially extending legs.00164-3) Each leg features a proximal domain near the vertex, a flexible "knee" region, and a distal terminal domain that adopts a seven-bladed -propeller fold, enabling specific interactions during assembly.00164-3) The overall triskelion spans about 20 nm in diameter, with each leg measuring roughly 4550 nm in contour length, allowing it to bridge multiple lattice edges.00164-3)
During lattice formation, triskelions self-polymerize through interactions between their terminal domains and proximal leg segments, generating a polyhedral network where each triskelion occupies a lattice vertex. The lattice comprises primarily hexagonal facets formed by six triskelions arranged around a central vertex, with occasional pentagonal facets introduced to induce curvature.00164-3) This arrangement creates a geodesic dome-like structure, analogous to a fullerene or soccer ball, where the 12 pentagons required for closure provide the geometric defects that drive membrane invagination without requiring additional proteins for the core scaffold.00164-3) The flexible knee regions and -helical segments in the legs accommodate the bending necessary for curved lattices, with leg orientations spiraling inward to stabilize the coat.
In vitro, purified triskelions assemble into empty cage structures mimicking coated vesicles under mildly acidic conditions (pH 6.06.5) and low ionic strength, without the need for nucleotides or membranes, as demonstrated in early reconstitution experiments. At physiological pH (around 7.0), assembly is inefficient for free triskelions but can be facilitated indirectly through accessory factors that modulate polymerization, though the core lattice geometry remains driven by triskelion-triskelion contacts. Light chains contribute to this process by conferring pH sensitivity and stabilizing the triskelion conformation for lattice incorporation.

Cellular Functions

Clathrin-Mediated Endocytosis

Clathrin-mediated endocytosis (CME) is the primary mechanism by which eukaryotic cells internalize plasma membrane components, extracellular ligands, and receptors into small vesicles coated by a polyhedral lattice of clathrin molecules. This process enables selective uptake of cargo while recycling membrane and facilitating signal transduction. Additionally, various pathogens, including viruses like influenza and some bacteria, exploit CME for cellular entry by hijacking the same cargo recognition and internalization machinery. CME begins at the plasma membrane and proceeds through a series of coordinated steps, culminating in the release of a free vesicle within approximately 1 minute.
The process initiates with nucleation, where the heterotetrameric adaptor protein complex AP-2 binds to phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PI(4,5)P) in the plasma membrane, adopting an open conformation that exposes binding sites for cargo receptors and clathrin triskelions. This recruitment typically starts with one or two AP-2 molecules and a single clathrin triskelion, occurring within the first 5 seconds of pit formation. AP-2 serves as the primary adaptor for CME at the plasma membrane, linking the membrane and cargo to the assembling clathrin lattice.
Following nucleation, clathrin triskelions polymerize into a curved, hexagonal lattice that drives membrane invagination, forming a clathrin-coated pit (CCP) with a diameter of 30-120 nm. Pit maturation involves progressive recruitment of additional clathrin and accessory proteins, with productive pits completing invagination in 20 seconds to over 120 seconds, averaging around 1 minute. The lattice curvature, derived from the triskelion's three-legged structure, generates the mechanical force for membrane bending without requiring external energy input at this stage.
Vesicle scission occurs at the deeply invaginated pit neck, mediated by the GTPase dynamin, which assembles into helical oligomers around the membrane tubule. GTP hydrolysis by dynamin provides the energy for conformational changes that constrict and sever the neck, releasing the clathrin-coated vesicle. This step is rate-limiting and ensures efficient separation from the plasma membrane.
Cargo selectivity in CME is achieved through specific motifs on transmembrane receptors that interact with AP-2 or other adaptors, concentrating cargo into forming pits. Canonical motifs include the tyrosine-based Yxx sequence (where is a bulky hydrophobic residue), recognized by the 2 subunit of AP-2; for example, the low-density lipoprotein receptor (LDLR) and transferrin receptor (TfnR) utilize this motif for internalization. Ligand binding, such as transferrin to TfnR, enhances motif exposure and accelerates pit maturation by increasing productive CCP efficiency from about 40% to over 70%. Ubiquitylation of cargo receptors, often on lysine residues, serves as an additional signal for recruitment via ubiquitin-binding domains in adaptors, promoting rapid internalization of proteins like the epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR). These mechanisms ensure high-fidelity sorting, with cargo influencing the transition from abortive to productive pits.

Other Intracellular Trafficking Roles

Clathrin plays a crucial role in intracellular trafficking beyond plasma membrane endocytosis, particularly in the sorting and transport of proteins between the trans-Golgi network (TGN) and endosomes. At the TGN, clathrin coats form on vesicles that mediate the delivery of lysosomal enzymes to endosomes via the adaptor protein complex AP-1. AP-1 recognizes tyrosine-based sorting signals on mannose-6-phosphate receptors (M6PRs), such as the Y974RAL motif in the cation-independent M6PR, facilitating their incorporation into clathrin-coated vesicles for transport to early endosomes. This process ensures that M6PRs bind mannose-6-phosphate-tagged lysosomal hydrolases in the TGN, delivering them to endosomes where the low pH causes dissociation, allowing enzymes to proceed to lysosomes. The cytoplasmic domains of M6PRs are essential for recruiting AP-1 to TGN membranes, and their expression levels directly correlate with AP-1 binding and the number of clathrin-coated vesicles formed, as demonstrated in M6PR-deficient fibroblasts where vesicle production is reduced threefold upon M6PR re-expression.
In endosomal compartments, clathrin contributes to recycling and degradation pathways by coordinating with the endosomal sorting complex required for transport (ESCRT) machinery during multivesicular body (MVB) formation. Clathrin is recruited to endosomal microdomains by ESCRT-0 (via the HRS subunit), where it stabilizes sorting platforms and regulates the timing of ESCRT complex dynamics. This recruitment facilitates the concentration of ubiquitylated cargo, such as receptors destined for degradation, and promotes inward budding of the endosomal limiting membrane to form intraluminal vesicles (ILVs) within MVBs. Depletion of clathrin prolongs ESCRT-0 and ESCRT-I dwell times, impairs ILV scission by ESCRT-III, and results in enlarged ILVs (up to 100 nm) and reduced cargo degradation efficiency, highlighting clathrin's role in coordinating sequential ESCRT waves that occur over approximately 195 seconds per ILV. Through this mechanism, clathrin ensures precise membrane remodeling and cargo sorting for lysosomal degradation, independent of its canonical endocytic functions.
Beyond vesicular trafficking, clathrin participates in non-endocytic processes, including mitotic spindle organization during cell division. In early mitosis, clathrin localizes to kinetochore fibers (K-fibers) of the spindle, where it interacts with the TACC3/ch-TOG complex to stabilize microtubules. TACC3 recruits clathrin and the microtubule polymerase ch-TOG (also known as CKAP5) to spindle microtubules, enabling clathrin to form short inter-microtubule bridges (~15-20 nm) that crosslink adjacent protofilaments and reduce microtubule catastrophe rates. This bridging is essential for maintaining K-fiber integrity, as clathrin depletion reduces microtubule density in K-fibers (from ~28 to ~11 microtubules per fiber) and disrupts chromosome alignment, though it does not directly affect centrosome integrity or cytokinesis onset.
Clathrin also functions in spermiogenesis, contributing to acrosome formation in developing spermatids. During the Golgi and cap phases of spermiogenesis, clathrin-coated vesicles bud from the trans-Golgi apparatus to transport lysosomal-like enzymes and membrane components to the nascent acrosome. Immuno-electron microscopy reveals clathrin coats specifically on vesicles in the Golgi-acrosome transition zone of rat spermatids, supporting anterograde delivery of proacrosomal granules that fuse to form the acrosomal vesicle. This trafficking is coordinated with other coat proteins like -COP for retrograde pathways, ensuring proper acrosome biogenesis and sperm head shaping, with disruptions leading to malformed acrosomes observed in clathrin-related mutants.

Interacting Proteins

Adaptor Protein Complexes

Adaptor protein (AP) complexes are heterotetrameric assemblies that serve as crucial intermediaries between clathrin coats and cellular membranes, facilitating the recruitment of specific cargo proteins into clathrin-coated vesicles. These complexes recognize sorting signals on transmembrane cargo and interact directly with clathrin triskelions, thereby linking the coat lattice to the membrane during vesicle formation. In clathrin-mediated endocytosis, AP complexes ensure selective packaging of receptors and ligands, playing essential roles in intracellular trafficking pathways.
The AP-2 complex is the primary adaptor at the plasma membrane, composed of , 2, 2, and 2 subunits. It exhibits specificity for the plasma membrane through binding to phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PI(4,5)P), which promotes its recruitment and activation. The 2 subunit recognizes tyrosine-based sorting motifs, such as Yxx (where Y is tyrosine, x is any amino acid, and is a bulky hydrophobic residue), on cargo proteins like transferrin receptor. Additionally, AP-2 interacts with -arrestin, which binds phosphorylated G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) and recruits AP-2 to facilitate their sorting and endocytosis via a xxFxxxxxR motif in -arrestin's C-terminal tail.
In contrast, AP-1 and AP-3 complexes function primarily at the trans-Golgi network (TGN) and endosomes, with subunit compositions of -1-1-1 for AP-1 and -3-3-3 for AP-3. AP-1 mediates sorting from the TGN to endosomes or the plasma membrane, binding dileucine-based motifs like (D/E)xxxL(L/I) at the -1 interface. AP-3 contributes to lysosomal biogenesis by directing cargo, such as tyrosinase, to lysosomes, with its 3 subunit also recognizing Yxx motifs; mutations in AP-3 subunits underlie Hermansky-Pudlak syndrome, highlighting its role in lysosome-related organelle formation.
Direct interactions between AP complexes and clathrin occur via clathrin box motifs, such as LLDL, located on the hinge regions or appendages of the and (or ) subunits. These motifs bind to the terminal domain of clathrin heavy chains at the triskelion's distal legs, stabilizing coat assembly and promoting lattice polymerization.

Accessory Proteins

Accessory proteins play crucial roles in modulating the formation, stability, and disassembly of clathrin coats during endocytosis and intracellular trafficking. These proteins interact with clathrin lattices, membranes, and other components to facilitate key steps such as membrane curvature induction, vesicle scission, and coat removal, ensuring efficient vesicle budding and recycling.
Dynamin, a large GTPase, is essential for the scission of clathrin-coated vesicles from the plasma membrane. It assembles into helical collars around the neck of deeply invaginated clathrin-coated pits, and GTP hydrolysis powers a conformational change that constricts the collar, leading to membrane fission and release of the vesicle. This process is conserved across eukaryotic cells and is critical for completing clathrin-mediated endocytosis.
Epsin and CALM (clathrin assembly lymphoid myeloid leukemia protein) are accessory proteins that promote membrane curvature and clathrin recruitment through their ENTH (epsin N-terminal homology) domains. These domains bind specifically to phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PIP2) in the plasma membrane, triggering the insertion of an amphipathic helix that deforms the lipid bilayer to facilitate pit invagination. Additionally, both proteins contain clathrin-binding motifs that stabilize the clathrin lattice and enhance coat assembly, working in concert with adaptor protein complexes to link cargo selection and membrane deformation. Epsin is particularly enriched at sites of high curvature, while CALM influences vesicle size and maturation by regulating clathrin polymerization dynamics.
Auxilin and Hsc70 (heat shock cognate 70) mediate the uncoating of clathrin coats after vesicle fission, allowing the lattice to disassemble and clathrin to be recycled. Auxilin, a J-domain-containing protein, binds directly to clathrin triskelions via its PTEN-type and clathrin-binding domains, recruiting the ATPase Hsc70 to the coat lattice. Hsc70 then uses ATP hydrolysis to drive clathrin disassembly, releasing the coat from the vesicle membrane in an energy-dependent manner shortly after scission. This uncoating step is vital for vesicle fusion with endosomes and preventing unproductive accumulation of coated intermediates.

Regulation and Dynamics

Assembly Mechanisms

The assembly of clathrin coats initiates with nucleation at the plasma membrane, where adaptor protein complex 2 (AP2) clusters on regions enriched in phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PIP2). This clustering is promoted by accessory factors such as FCHO proteins, which induce a conformational opening in AP2, enabling its high-affinity binding to PIP2 and subsequent recruitment of clathrin triskelions via interactions with the clathrin heavy chain terminal domains. AP2 also binds cargo molecules through motifs in its subunits, which further stabilizes the open conformation of AP2 and enhances triskelion recruitment, ensuring selective incorporation of transmembrane proteins into the forming coat. This cargo-dependent stabilization is critical for distinguishing productive assembly sites from transient, non-specific clusters.
Clathrin polymerization proceeds cooperatively, with triskelions adding sequentially to the growing lattice through leg-to-segment interactions between heavy chains, forming a curved polyhedral structure.00790-1) Live-cell imaging reveals an assembly rate of approximately 1-2 triskelions per second during the early stages of coated pit formation, allowing a nascent pit to accumulate 10-20 triskelions within 6-10 seconds and achieve sufficient stability for further growth.00790-1) This kinetic profile reflects a balance between rapid on-rates driven by high local concentrations of adaptors and off-rates that abort unproductive assemblies, thereby optimizing efficiency in cargo uptake.00785-4)
Regulatory phosphorylation of clathrin light chains (LCs) by casein kinase II (CK2) modulates assembly dynamics by altering LC conformation, which influences heavy chain interactions and lattice stability.00486-9) Specifically, CK2-mediated phosphorylation on LCs promotes a switch that facilitates triskelion incorporation into the coat, enhancing overall polymerization rates under cellular conditions.00587-8)
Environmental factors such as calcium ions (Ca2+) and pH finely tune assembly; at physiological levels (~100 nM Ca2+ and neutral pH), LCs inhibit hub domain interactions to prevent premature polymerization, while elevated Ca2+ or mildly acidic pH relieves this inhibition.81611-6) In vitro reconstitution of clathrin cage assembly typically employs buffers mimicking these conditions, such as 100 mM MES (pH 6.4), 0.5 mM MgCl2, 1 mM EGTA, and 2 mM CaCl2, which promote triskelion aggregation into empty cages without membrane templates.

Disassembly and Recycling

Following vesicle scission mediated by dynamin GTPase activity, the clathrin coat undergoes rapid uncoating to release the newly formed vesicle for subsequent trafficking. This process is initiated by the recruitment of auxilin, a J-domain-containing protein, to the clathrin lattice on the vesicle surface. Auxilin binds directly to clathrin heavy chains and serves as a co-chaperone to recruit the constitutive heat shock protein Hsc70 (also known as HSC70).
Hsc70, in its ATP-bound state, interacts with auxilin via the J-domain, which stimulates Hsc70's ATPase activity. ATP hydrolysis by Hsc70 induces conformational changes in the clathrin triskelions, leading to the sequential disassembly of the lattice starting from the edges and progressing inward. This uncoating occurs rapidly, typically within seconds after vesicle release, ensuring efficient recycling of coat components.
Upon disassembly, free clathrin triskelionseach consisting of three heavy chains associated with three light chainsare released into the cytosol. Clathrin light chains (CLCs) play a critical role in recycling by acting as molecular chaperones that maintain the proper folding of heavy chains and prevent aggregation or misfolding during storage and reuse. Defects in this chaperone function, such as mutations in CLCs, result in the accumulation of disassembled coats and impaired triskelion availability for new assembly cycles.
The temporal coordination of disassembly is regulated by reversible phosphorylation of coat components, including adaptor proteins. During assembly, kinases phosphorylate sites on adaptors like AP-2 to promote coat stability; post-vesiculation, protein phosphatase 2A (PP2A) counteracts this by dephosphorylating key residues, such as the 2 subunit of AP-2, to facilitate Hsc70-mediated uncoating and ensure timely recycling.

Physiological and Pathological Implications

Essential Physiological Roles

Clathrin plays a pivotal role in nutrient uptake by facilitating the endocytosis of low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol through the LDL receptor (LDLR) in hepatocytes. In this process, LDL particles bind to LDLR on the hepatocyte surface, clustering in clathrin-coated pits that invaginate to form coated vesicles, which deliver LDL to endosomes for lysosomal degradation and cholesterol release. This mechanism clears approximately 70% of circulating LDL from the plasma, maintaining cholesterol homeostasis and preventing excessive accumulation that could lead to cardiovascular issues.
In the nervous system, clathrin is essential for synaptic transmission through the recycling of neurotransmitter receptors and synaptic vesicles in neurons. Clathrin-mediated endocytosis retrieves internalized membrane components, including receptors such as AMPA receptors, enabling their reuse in synaptic signaling and plasticity. Disruption of clathrin heavy chain function inhibits this recycling, leading to impaired vesicle reformation and progressive decline in neurotransmission during sustained activity. Global knockout of the clathrin heavy chain gene in mice results in embryonic lethality, underscoring its indispensable role in neuronal development and function.
Clathrin contributes to the immune response by supporting MHC class II trafficking for antigen presentation, which is crucial for T cell activation. MHC class II molecules, complexed with the invariant chain, are sorted into clathrin-coated vesicles at the plasma membrane via AP-2 adaptors, directing them to endosomal compartments where antigenic peptides are loaded. This peptide-loaded MHC class II is then presented on antigen-presenting cells to activate CD4+ T cells, initiating adaptive immunity. Inhibition of clathrin function disrupts this trafficking, reducing surface expression of peptide-MHC complexes and impairing T cell responses.

Associations with Diseases

Dysfunction in clathrin-mediated endocytosis (CME) has been implicated in the pathogenesis of Alzheimer's disease (AD), primarily through impaired clearance of amyloid- (A) peptides. In neurons and glial cells, CME facilitates the internalization and degradation of A, but genetic variants in CME regulators such as PICALM and BIN1 reduce endocytic efficiency, leading to extracellular A accumulation and plaque formation. For instance, APOE4 carriers exhibit disrupted CME in astrocytes, hindering A uptake, while PICALM upregulation can partially rescue this defect. Microglial CME inhibition reduces A clearance by up to 80%, exacerbating neuroinflammation and neurodegeneration.
In Parkinson's disease (PD), -synuclein aggregation interferes with CME, blocking synaptic vesicle recycling and contributing to dopaminergic neuron loss. Overexpression of wild-type or mutant (A53T) -synuclein decreases synaptojanin 1 expression, impairing clathrin-coated vesicle fission and uncoating, which results in fewer synaptic vesicles and enlarged endocytic intermediates. This disruption alters phosphatidylinositol-4,5-bisphosphate homeostasis and synaptic function, with restoration of synaptojanin 1 alleviating these deficits in transgenic models.
Clathrin dysfunction also contributes to lysosomal storage disorders, such as Niemann-Pick disease type A/B (NPD A/B), where acid sphingomyelinase deficiency leads to impaired CME and lysosomal trafficking. Fibroblasts from NPD A/B patients show reduced transferrin uptake (less than 50% of wild-type levels), fewer and smaller clathrin-coated pits, and perinuclear clathrin redistribution, indicating defective endocytic sorting to lysosomes. Although AP-3 adaptor complex defects primarily affect lysosomal cargo sorting in related disorders like Hermansky-Pudlak syndrome, NPD A/B cells exhibit broader CME impairment that accumulates undegraded substrates, promoting cellular toxicity. Targeted enzyme replacement via ICAM-1-mediated nanocarriers restores CME function and reduces sphingomyelin accumulation by approximately 94%.
Clathrin dysregulation has been linked to cancer progression. For example, increased clathrin expression in prostate cancer promotes epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) trafficking and signaling, enhancing cell proliferation and invasion. Inactivation of the AP-2 complex, a key clathrin adaptor, acts as a tumor suppressor; its loss reduces cell growth in vitro but paradoxically promotes tumorigenesis in vivo by altering endocytic recycling and immune evasion. These findings suggest therapeutic potential in targeting clathrin-mediated pathways to inhibit cancer cell migration and metastasis.
Recent 2025 studies have identified de novo mutations in the CLTC gene, encoding the clathrin heavy chain, as causes of neurodevelopmental disorders including intellectual disability (IDD56), epilepsy, and developmental delay. These heterozygous variants disrupt clathrin assembly and synaptic architecture, leading to impaired learning and motor function in model organisms like Drosophila. For example, pathogenic CLTC mutations alter intracellular transport, resulting in variable phenotypes from mild to severe intellectual disability often accompanied by microcephaly and corpus callosum hypoplasia. Emerging research suggests potential therapeutic strategies involving CME modulators, such as inhibitors like chlorpromazine or hydroxychloroquine, to mitigate downstream effects of CLTC dysfunction, though clinical applications remain exploratory.
