Alessandro Scarlatti
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Alessandro Scarlatti (2 May 1660 22 October 1725) was an Italian Baroque composer renowned for his extensive output of operas, chamber cantatas, and sacred music, establishing himself as a pivotal figure in the development of the Neapolitan school of opera. Born Pietro Alessandro Gaspare Scarlatti in Palermo, Sicily, he received his early training in Rome, where he married Antonia Maria Vittoria Anzaloni in 1678 and began his professional career as maestro di cappella at the church of San Giacomo degli Incurabili. Over his lifetime, Scarlatti composed approximately 115 operas, more than 600 cantatas, and numerous sacred vocal works, innovating operatic forms through the introduction of the three-movement sinfonia and richer orchestral textures that influenced subsequent European composers.
Scarlatti's career spanned major Italian musical centers, including a significant tenure from 1684 to 1702 and again from 1709 until his death as maestro di cappella at the royal court in Naples, interspersed with periods in Rome and Florence under patrons such as Queen Christina of Sweden and Ferdinando de' Medici. His operas, such as Gli equivoci nel sembiante (1679) and La Griselda (1721), exemplified the evolution from Roman to Neapolitan styles, emphasizing dramatic expression and melodic elegance while adapting to the demands of virtuoso singers. Beyond the stage, his chamber cantatas became a cornerstone of the genre, circulating widely across Europe and showcasing his mastery of affective text setting and da capo form.
As the father of the celebrated keyboard composer Domenico Scarlatti (16851757), Alessandro's legacy extended through his family and the broader Neapolitan tradition, mentoring figures like Johann Adolph Hasse and inspiring later composers such as Leonardo Leo and Niccol Jommelli. Despite achieving fame during his lifetime, Scarlatti died in relative poverty in Naples, leaving behind a profound impact on Baroque music through his prolific creativity and structural innovations that bridged late seventeenth-century opera with emerging classical forms.

Life and Career

Early Life and Education

Alessandro Scarlatti was born on May 2, 1660, in Palermo, Sicily, into a family immersed in the local musical scene. His father, Pietro Scarlata (later anglicized as Scarlatti), served as a tenor, while his mother was Eleonora d'Amato; the family's involvement in Palermo's vibrant cultural life provided young Alessandro with early exposure to music, though specific details of his initial training there remain sparse.
Around the age of 12, in 1672, Scarlatti relocated to Rome with his family, likely driven by economic hardships such as famine in Sicily and the pursuit of greater artistic opportunities in the papal city. In Rome, he received formative education, traditionally attributed to the renowned composer Giacomo Carissimi at the Collegio Germanico, where the curriculum emphasized counterpoint and vocal composition techniques central to sacred and dramatic music of the era. Although direct evidence of this apprenticeship is lackingCarissimi died in 1674 when Scarlatti was just 14contemporary accounts suggest Scarlatti absorbed Carissimi's stylistic principles through exposure at the institution and broader Roman musical circles.
Scarlatti's early development was shaped by the innovative Roman composers of the time, including Alessandro Stradella and Giovanni Legrenzi, whose dramatic vocal works and harmonic experiments influenced his budding style; Arcangelo Corelli's emerging instrumental idioms also left an imprint, particularly in Scarlatti's later explorations of form. By 1678, at age 18, he had married Antonia Maria Vittoria Anzaloni, and the following year marked his compositional debut with the opera Gli equivoci nel sembiante, a pastoral comedy of mistaken identities premiered privately in Rome, which quickly gained patronage from figures like Queen Christina of Sweden and propelled his entry into professional circles.

Roman Period

In 1678, Alessandro Scarlatti married Antonia Anzaloni in Rome, with whom he would have ten children, including the future composers Domenico Scarlatti (born 1685) and Pietro Filippo Scarlatti (born 1679). This personal milestone coincided with his professional establishment in the city, where he had arrived as a young musician trained under Giacomo Carissimi. By 1680, Scarlatti secured a pivotal appointment as maestro di cappella to the exiled Queen Christina of Sweden, whose Roman court became a hub for artistic patronage. In this role, he composed sacred works and cantatas tailored for her circle, including pieces performed during private academies and religious observances, which helped solidify his reputation among Roman elites.
This early Roman period culminated in significant operas, such as Il Pompeo in 1683, premiered at the Teatro Tordinona and exemplifying his emerging mastery of opera seria with its structured arias and recitatives. Scarlatti also received commissions from other figures, like Cardinal Benedetto Pamphili, for whom he wrote oratorios and dramatic works, further embedding him in Rome's vibrant musical scene. However, the era was marred by political instability, particularly under Pope Innocent XI (16761689), whose austere policies restricted theatrical performances and strained artistic funding. These challenges prompted Scarlatti's departure from Rome in 1684 for Naples, where he would spend the next 18 years.
After his long tenure in Naples (detailed below), Scarlatti returned to Rome briefly from 1707 to 1709. During this later Roman stay, he was appointed maestro di cappella to Cardinal Pietro Ottoboni and elected to the Arcadian Academy. He produced operas like Il Mitridate Eupatore (1707) and cultivated ties with Roman nobility amid economic pressures. Prior to this, around 17021704, he had served in Florence under Prince Ferdinando de' Medici, composing operas such as Arminio, which reinforced his connections to northern Italian courts. These interludes bridged his Neapolitan periods but were marked by itinerant commissions and financial instability, leading to his final return to Naples in 1709.

Neapolitan Period

First Neapolitan Period (16841702)

In 1684, Scarlatti moved to Naples and was appointed maestro di cappella to the Viceroy, a position he held until 1702. This period marked the height of his operatic output, with over 40 operas composed, including Gli equivoci in amore (1690) and Laodicea e Bernice (1701). He innovated the Neapolitan style by introducing the three-movement sinfonia and richer orchestral textures, establishing the foundations of the Neapolitan school of opera. His works emphasized dramatic expression and adapted to virtuoso singers, influencing the genre's evolution. Sacred music for the royal chapel and chamber cantatas also flourished, circulating widely in Europe. Despite successes, political changes and patronage disputes contributed to his departure in 1702.

Second Neapolitan Period (17091725)

After leaving Rome amid financial uncertainties, Scarlatti returned to Naples in late 1708, facilitated by the intervention of Cardinal Vincenzo Grimaldi following the Austrian occupation of the city earlier that year. He was promptly reinstated as maestro di cappella at the royal chapel on January 9, 1709, with a monthly stipend of 42 ducats, later augmented by an additional 5 ducats for a servant in 1713, allowing him to resume leadership of the court's musical establishment. In this role, he composed numerous sacred works tailored for the viceregal court, including motets and masses that reflected the evolving liturgical needs under Austrian Habsburg rule.
In 1709, Scarlatti also assumed the position of maestro at the Conservatorio dei Poveri di Ges Cristo, one of Naples's premier institutions for music education, where he trained a new generation of composers and performers. His tenure there emphasized rigorous vocal training and compositional discipline, contributing to reforms in pedagogical methods that emphasized counterpoint and dramatic expression, influencing pupils such as Leonardo Leo. Although his directorial duties were demanding, they provided stability after years of itinerant patronage-seeking, enabling him to balance administrative responsibilities with creative output.
Scarlatti's late compositional activity in Naples included significant operas such as Il trionfo dell'onore (1718), a commedia per musica that achieved local success, and Il Cambise, re di Persia (1719), premiered at the Teatro San Bartolomeo. He continued producing sacred music for the royal chapel, including settings of psalms and antiphons, while occasionally traveling for commissions, such as the opera Griselda (1721) written for Rome. These works demonstrated his enduring adaptability to Neapolitan theatrical traditions amid shifting political patronage. His final opera was La Griselda in 1721.
In his final years, Scarlatti grappled with worsening financial hardships and health decline, exacerbated by irregular court payments and the burdens of supporting his family, including his composer sons Domenico and Pietro. Just ten days before his death, he petitioned the viceroy for four months' withheld salary, underscoring his desperate need for patronage. Scarlatti died on October 22, 1725, in Naples, and was buried in the church of Santa Maria di Montesanto.

Musical Style and Innovations

Developments in Opera

Alessandro Scarlatti composed over 100 operas, the majority of which were in the opera seria genre, establishing him as a foundational figure in the Neapolitan school of composition. These works integrated dramatic narrative with musical expression, particularly through the innovative use of recitativo accompagnato, which employed orchestral accompaniment to heighten emotional intensity and advance the plot beyond the limitations of secco recitative. This technique, first notably applied in operas like Olimpia vendicata (1685), allowed for a more fluid blend of speech-like declamation and instrumental color, marking a shift toward greater musical-dramatic cohesion in Italian opera.
A key innovation was Scarlatti's standardization of the da capo aria in ABA form, which became the dominant vehicle for character development and vocal virtuosity in opera seria. This structure permitted singers to showcase expressive ornamentation in the repeated A section while contrasting it with a contrasting B section, emphasizing emotional depth over mere spectacle. Complementing this, Scarlatti developed the Italian overture, or sinfonia, into a standardized three-movement form (fast-slow-fast), often featuring lively fugal openings, lyrical central movements, and energetic conclusions; this format, evident from his operas starting around 1695, served as an instrumental prelude that influenced the later symphony.
Scarlatti's operas also reflected a broader reform in libretto design, moving away from the elaborate, spectacle-heavy Venetian style toward more intimate, psychologically driven plots focused on fewer characters and moral themes. Influenced by librettists such as Apostolo Zeno, who advocated for Aristotelian unity and emotional verisimilitude, Scarlatti contributed to the Arcadian reform movement by prioritizing dramatic integrity over extraneous elements. A prime example is Mitridate Eupatore (1707), where he reduced the number of solo arias to streamline the action and incorporated more ensemble numbers to enhance interpersonal dynamics and collective expression, thereby advancing the character-centered ethos of opera seria.

Vocal and Instrumental Techniques

Alessandro Scarlatti employed modern modulation techniques in his arias and recitatives to heighten emotional depth, often shifting keys unexpectedly to reflect dramatic tension or affective nuance. For instance, in the cantata "Sarei troppo felice," he modulates to the supertonic, creating a sense of unresolved longing that enhances the text's expression of unfulfilled desire. Similarly, his recitatives frequently incorporate chromatic modulations and circle-of-fifths progressions, as seen in works like "Mha divisio" and "O sol deglocchi," where only a minority remain in a single key, fostering harmonic instability that mirrors emotional turmoil. Scarlatti also varied phrase lengths extensively, using irregular structures in early cantatas to mimic natural speech rhythms and build intensity, such as the broken phrases in "Se cerca, se dice" from LOlimpiade, which contrast short, syllabic lines with extended melismas for greater expressive range. These techniques extended beyond opera into chamber cantatas, where modulation and phrasing served to underscore poetic symmetry and emotional contrast.
In orchestration, Scarlatti advanced idiomatic writing for strings, winds, and continuo across his cantatas and oratorios, integrating them to support vocal lines while adding color and structural definition. His cantatas often feature five-part string ensembles, as in LHonesta negli Amori, where violins and violas provide sustained chords and ritornellos that echo vocal motifs, creating a balanced tonal palette. Winds, including trumpets and oboes, were employed dramatically for heightening tension, such as the four trumpets in the aria "Trombe, che dogni intorno" from Oratorio per la Passione (1708), which evoke majesty and conflict through fanfare-like figures integrated with the strings. Continuo realization, typically with harpsichord, theorbo, and bassoon, underpins these textures, as in Il dolore di Maria Vergine, where it supports chromatic string lines in Mary's lament arias and allows for sparse omissions to emphasize vulnerability. In oratorios like Passio secundum Joannem, a four-part string ensemble (two violins, viola, continuo) accompanies solemn recitatives with chromaticism, while winds like flutes add pastoral imagery in ensemble sections, demonstrating Scarlatti's skill in blending liturgical restraint with theatrical vividness.
Scarlatti contributed to the development of the four-part sonata (sonata a quattro) for chamber ensembles, blending the established trio sonata format with emerging soloistic elements to create more dynamic interplay among instruments. His Quattro sonate a quattro (published 1725), scored for two violins, viola, and cello without harpsichord, feature fugal openings in minor keys that expand the trio sonata's contrapuntal core with individualized viola lines, foreshadowing solo concerto textures. This hybrid form, shared with Neapolitan contemporaries, allowed for greater rhythmic flexibility and thematic contrast within church-sonata structures, as evident in the energetic fugues and chromatic slow movements that prioritize ensemble dialogue over strict polyphony.
Concerti grossi attributed to Scarlatti drew clear influence from Arcangelo Corelli's concertino style, adapting the small solo group's prominence while infusing the works with distinctive rhythmic vitality, though their authorship remains a subject of scholarly debate due to a mysterious genesis. In pieces like the Six Concerti Grossi (c. 1715), they echo Corelli's balanced alternation between concertino (two violins and cello) and ripieno strings but introduce lively hemiola rhythms and syncopations, as heard in the pastoral sequences of the F minor concerto, to drive forward momentum and emotional arc. This rhythmic energy, rooted in his operatic and cantata practices, extends Corelli's formal clarity into more animated expressions, evident in serenatas such as Endimione e Cintia.
Harmonically, Scarlatti innovated through chromaticism in his sacred motets, often departing from strict counterpoint to achieve expressive intensity suited to devotional texts. In motets like Vexilla Regis and Infirinata vulnerata, he incorporates chromatic lines and diminished chords that evoke pathos, loosening palestrina-style polyphony for more homophonic textures that prioritize affective word-painting over linear interdependence. Works such as O magnum mysterium further demonstrate this shift, with chromatic descents in the bass and melismatic solos that blend trio-sonata influences with harmonic tension, marking a transition toward the galant style in sacred music.

Major Works

Operas

Alessandro Scarlatti composed more than 114 operas over his career, spanning from 1679 to 1721, establishing him as a cornerstone of the Neapolitan school of opera. These works, primarily drammi per musica, were crafted for prestigious venues across Italy, including the Teatro San Bartolomeo in Naples, the Teatro Capranica in Rome, and theaters in Florence supported by the Medici court. Many premiered under royal or noble patronage, reflecting the era's blend of courtly spectacle and public entertainment, though a significant portion of his outputover halfhas been lost to time, with only about 64 surviving in whole or partial form.
His earliest notable opera, Lhonest negli amori (1680), premiered at the Palazzo della Regina Cristina in Rome and marked his breakthrough under the patronage of Queen Christina of Sweden, introducing themes of romantic intrigue and fidelity central to his dramatic narratives. By the mid-career period, Scarlatti's operas gained wider acclaim, as seen in La Rosaura (also known as Gli equivoci in amore, 1690), staged at the Palazzo della Cancelleria in Rome, which explored mistaken identities and amorous deceptions amid heroic backdrops. Another highlight from this phase, Tigrane (1715), debuted at the Teatro San Bartolomeo in Naples and delved into tales of love, betrayal, and martial heroism, drawing on historical and mythological sources to heighten emotional stakes. These mid-period works often featured elaborate ensembles and instrumental accompaniments, tailored for the vibrant operatic scenes of Rome and Naples.
In his later years, Scarlatti produced some of his most dramatically intense operas, including Telemaco, ossia L'isola di Circe (1718), premiered at the Teatro Capranica in Rome, which wove themes of adventure, temptation, and redemption through Odysseus's son, demanding virtuosic vocal displays from its cast. His final opera, Griselda (1721), also staged at the Teatro Capranica, centered on endurance in the face of spousal betrayal and tyrannical tests, showcasing heightened dramatic tension and exacting soprano roles that pushed singers to their limits. These late masterpieces, produced amid Scarlatti's return to Naples and intermittent Roman commissions, underscore his evolving focus on psychological depth within operatic storytelling. While full scores of only around 20 operas remain extant, many others are preserved through individual arias extracted for anthologies and recitals, allowing partial reconstruction of their thematic richness.

Cantatas and Sacred Music

Alessandro Scarlatti composed over 600 chamber cantatas, primarily secular works for solo voice and basso continuo, which form a cornerstone of his non-operatic output. These pieces, often exploring pastoral and mythological themes involving archetypal figures such as Clori, Dorinda, and Fileno, were designed for intimate performance settings like private academies or noble salons. Representative examples include Lasciami sospirar, io voglio piangere (for Dorindo and Fileno), a dialogue cantata that intertwines amorous intrigue with lyrical expression. Many of these cantatas remain unpublished, with manuscripts preserved in major collections such as the British Library and the Biblioteca Apostolica Vaticana.
In his sacred vocal music, Scarlatti produced a significant body of oratorios and liturgical compositions that integrated dramatic narrative with devotional elements, often featuring choruses and orchestral accompaniment. Notable oratorios include Santa Maria Maddalena de' Pazzi (1705, libretto by Benedetto Pamphilj), which dramatizes the saint's mystical visions through alternating solos and ensembles, and Il trionfo della grazia, overo La Maddalena pentita (c. 1703), blending penitential themes with vivid characterizations of Mary Magdalene, the devil, and divine grace. These works, typically performed during Lent in Roman palaces or oratories, number around 34 in total and reflect Scarlatti's adaptation of operatic techniques to sacred contexts.
Scarlatti's liturgical output encompasses masses, motets, and psalms tailored for Roman and Neapolitan chapels, emphasizing polyphonic richness and expressive solo lines. The Messa di Santa Cecilia (17201721), composed for the Basilica di Santa Cecilia in Rome, features five soloists (SSATB), chorus, and orchestra, showcasing grand choral sections in the Gloria and intricate counterpoint in the Credo. Other examples include motets like Salve Regina (c. 1707, for the Sistine Chapel) and psalm settings such as Dixit Dominus from the Santa Cecilia cycle, which employ da capo arias and recitatives for meditative intimacy. These pieces, often commissioned by patrons like Cardinal Pietro Ottoboni, survive in manuscripts across Vatican archives and Neapolitan conservatories. Stylistically, Scarlatti's cantatas and sacred works favor a structure of alternating recitatives and da capo arias, prioritizing melodic elegance and rhetorical clarity suited to both chamber and ecclesiastical venues.

Instrumental Compositions

Alessandro Scarlatti's instrumental output, though smaller than his vocal repertoire, demonstrates his versatility in chamber and orchestral forms, often drawing on contemporary Italian traditions while incorporating innovative structural elements. His chamber sonatas include approximately twelve works for violin and continuo, characterized by lyrical melodies and idiomatic writing for the solo instrument, as well as several trio sonatas for two violins and continuo that reflect the influence of Arcangelo Corelli's opulent style, with structured movements alternating slow and fast tempos to emphasize contrapuntal interplay. These sonatas, composed primarily in the later Neapolitan period, showcase Scarlatti's skill in balancing soloistic expression with ensemble dialogue, though they remain less elaborate than Corelli's in terms of virtuosic demands.
In the realm of orchestral music, Scarlatti contributed to the emerging concerto grosso genre through works like the twelve Sinfonie di concerto grosso (R. 533), begun in 1715, which feature a concertino group of solo instrumentsoften including flute or trumpetcontrasted against a ripieno string ensemble, creating dynamic textural contrasts typical of the form. A representative example is the Breve concerto in D major (also known as Sinfonia No. 2), structured in five movements including spiritoso, adagio, allegro, and presto sections, where the interplay between soloists and full orchestra highlights Scarlatti's theatrical sensibility adapted to purely instrumental contexts. These concertos, scored for strings with winds and continuo, mark a shift toward more formalized orchestral writing in his oeuvre, bridging operatic overtures and independent concert pieces.
Scarlatti's keyboard compositions, likely intended for harpsichord, include early toccatas and fugues that reveal his contrapuntal prowess, with pieces such as the Toccata in G minor employing dense chromatic harmonies, arpeggiated passages, and imitative entries to evoke a sense of grandeur reminiscent of Frescobaldi's influence. A set of six preludes and fugues further demonstrates this skill, featuring rigorous fugal subjects and episodic development that prioritize intellectual rigor over ornamental display. These works, among his earliest instrumental efforts from the Roman period, underscore his foundational training in polyphony. Additionally, Scarlatti composed orchestral sinfonias, many derived as standalone adaptations of operatic overtures, such as the three-movement sinfonia from Griselda (1721), which employs strings, winds, and horns for dramatic effect and was repurposed for concert performance, illustrating his practice of repurposing theatrical music for instrumental ensembles.
The dissemination of Scarlatti's instrumental works benefited from publications in Amsterdam during the 1710s, where printer tienne Roger issued editions that facilitated their spread across Europe, including the Sinfonie di concerto grosso around 1716, which aided in establishing Scarlatti's reputation beyond Italy. Manuscripts and prints from this period, often preserved in libraries like the British Museum, highlight the works' role in the broader Baroque instrumental canon, though authenticity concerns persist for some attributed pieces due to copyist errors in contemporary sources.

Legacy and Influence

Impact on Baroque Music

Alessandro Scarlatti's arrival in Naples in 1684 marked a watershed in the city's musical history, laying the groundwork for the Neapolitan school of composition that would profoundly shape European music throughout the 18th century. His integration of Roman influences with local traditions fostered a distinctive style characterized by melodic richness and structural innovation, which directly impacted pupils such as Leonardo Leo and Giovanni Battista Pergolesi. These composers extended Scarlatti's emphasis on expressive vocal writing and dramatic pacing, establishing the Neapolitan approach as a dominant force in opera and sacred music across Italy and beyond.
Scarlatti's opera seria exemplified the Italian style's adaptability, facilitating its export to northern Europe and influencing key figures in London and Germany. His model of structured arias interspersed with recitatives informed George Frideric Handel's Rinaldo (1711), the first Italian opera written for the London stage, where Scarlatti's dramatic intensity and vocal virtuosity resonated with English audiences. In France, Jean-Philippe Rameau incorporated Italian elements, such as da capo arias, to infuse his tragdies en musique with greater expressiveness, bridging national operatic traditions.
Scarlatti advanced the oratorio genre by merging operatic drama with sacred texts, creating works that emphasized narrative tension and affective contrast to engage listeners spiritually. This synthesis contributed to the evolution of the genre, refining elements like the blending of recitative, aria, and chorus to heighten emotional impact and transitioning the oratorio from a static devotional form to a dynamic counterpart of secular opera.
Central to Scarlatti's legacy were his contributions to formal standardization, particularly the da capo aria (ABA structure), which he elevated into a vehicle for singer display and emotional elaboration, becoming ubiquitous in Baroque vocal music. This form's ternary design anticipated the Classical sonata by providing a framework for thematic return and development. Likewise, his sinfonia overturestypically fast-slow-fastcrystallized the Italian overture model, evolving directly into the multi-movement symphony and influencing orchestral genres.
The 20th century saw renewed interest in Scarlatti's oeuvre through scholarly editions, stage revivals, and recordings that underscored his dramatic vitality and innovative orchestration. Performances of operas like Griselda and cantatas in major venues, alongside pioneering discs by ensembles such as the Academy of Ancient Music, revealed his flair for theatrical tension, securing his place in the modern Baroque repertoire.

The Scarlatti Family

Alessandro Scarlatti (16601725) married Antonia Anzalone, with whom he had ten children, several of whom pursued musical careers, though only two achieved notable prominence as composers. His eldest son, Pietro Filippo Scarlatti (16791750), born in Rome, became an organist, choirmaster, and composer, initially serving as a violinist in the Naples royal chapel from 1684 and later contributing to sacred music and operas in Rome and Italy, reflecting his father's Neapolitan foundations in a more localized Italian context.
Domenico Scarlatti (16851757), the sixth child and another composer son, received early training directly from his father in Naples, where Alessandro guided his development in keyboard and vocal composition before Domenico's travels. Domenico's career took him internationally, serving in Portugal from 1719 and Spain from 1729, where he composed over 555 keyboard sonatas incorporating Spanish folk rhythms, guitar techniques, and dance forms like the jota and fandango, marking a departure from Alessandro's operatic focus while building on familial stylistic elements such as rhythmic phrasing. This training fostered a complex father-son dynamic, with Domenico's early cantatas (c. 17011703) echoing Alessandro's innovations in structure and expression, yet evolving into independent genius amid professional support, including a 1705 musical tour arranged by his father.
The Scarlatti family's musical legacy was complicated by early misattributions of works, stemming from shared names and the similarity of their styles, leading to confusions in catalogs where pieces by Alessandro were sometimes ascribed to Domenico or vice versa, such as certain arias and sonatas in 18th-century manuscripts. Other children, including daughters and sons, engaged in music to lesser degrees, with some serving in chapels or families, but none rivaled the prominence of Pietro Filippo and Domenico; descendants occasionally appeared in musical roles, though their contributions remained minor compared to the international reach of the sons' careers, which echoed Alessandro's Neapolitan innovations across Europe.
