Chemically known as 3--4-yl)-3-hydroxy-1-phenylpropyl]-4-hydroxy-2H-1-benzopyran-2-one, it has the molecular formula CHBrO and a molecular weight of 527.4 g/mol, appearing as a yellowish to off-white powder with a melting point of 198.3199.8 C. It exhibits low volatility (vapor pressure of 1.6 10 mm Hg at 25 C) and moderate water solubility (approximately 12.5 mg/L at 20 C), with low to immobile behavior in soil (Koc range 1,56341,600).
Developed by the French company Lipha in the 1960s and patented in 1967, bromadiolone was introduced as an effective agent against warfarin-resistant rodents, with its rodenticidal properties first reported in scientific literature around 1976. It functions by inhibiting vitamin K epoxide reductase (VKOR), disrupting the blood clotting process and causing internal hemorrhaging, shock, and death in target species such as rats, mice, and voles after a single feeding. Primarily used in bait formulations for indoor and outdoor pest control in buildings, vehicles, sewers, and agricultural settings, it is applied at concentrations typically ranging from 0.005% to 0.01%.
Bromadiolone is highly toxic to mammals, with acute oral LD values of 0.561.75 mg/kg in rats and mice, respectively, leading to symptoms like epistaxis, hematuria, and prolonged coagulopathy that can persist for weeks. The antidote is vitamin K, administered promptly to counteract its effects, which can be life-threatening in cases of accidental human or non-target animal exposure. Due to its persistence in the environment (half-life in soil up to 62 days) and potential for secondary poisoning in wildlife, its use is regulated by agencies like the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) under pesticide reregistration programs to minimize ecological risks.

Introduction and History

Description

Bromadiolone is a potent anticoagulant rodenticide classified as a second-generation 4-hydroxycoumarin derivative and a vitamin K antagonist, commonly referred to as a "super-warfarin" due to its enhanced potency compared to earlier compounds. It functions by disrupting blood clotting processes in target animals, making it effective for pest management.
The primary purpose of bromadiolone is to control rodent populations, particularly species resistant to first-generation anticoagulants such as warfarin, by requiring fewer feedings for lethal effects. It is widely used in agricultural, urban, and residential settings to mitigate infestations of rats and mice.
In its pure form, bromadiolone appears as a white to off-white or beige powder that is odorless, with low solubility in water (less than 20 mg/L at 20C) but good solubility in organic solvents like acetone and dichloromethane. Its molecular formula is CHBrO, and it has a molar mass of 527.4 g/mol.
Commercial formulations of bromadiolone are typically a mixture of four stereoisomers, arising from two chiral centers, which contribute to its overall efficacy in rodenticidal baits.

Development and Introduction

Bromadiolone was developed in the 1960s by the French pharmaceutical company Lipha S.A. and patented in 1967 in response to the growing problem of warfarin resistance in rodent populations, which had rendered first-generation anticoagulants less effective for pest control. Its rodenticidal properties were first reported in scientific literature in 1976. As a second-generation anticoagulant, it was designed to provide higher potency and single-feed lethality against resistant strains of rats and mice, addressing the limitations observed in earlier rodenticides.
The compound was first commercialized in France toward the late 1970s, with initial market entry reported around 1978, and quickly gained traction in Europe due to its efficacy. In the United Kingdom, bromadiolone was introduced in 1980 under trade names such as Contrac, marking a significant advancement in urban and agricultural pest management where warfarin-resistant infestations were prevalent.
Early laboratory studies conducted in 1980 demonstrated bromadiolone's effectiveness, achieving complete mortality in warfarin-resistant Norway rats (Rattus norvegicus) within four days and in resistant house mice (Mus musculus) within twelve days at a 0.005% concentration in bait. Subsequent field trials in the UK during the early 1980s confirmed these results, with baiting using 0.005% bromadiolone in oatmeal or wheat fully eradicating resistant rat populations on farms after seven days of exposure.
By the early 1980s, bromadiolone expanded to global markets, including registration by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency in 1980, facilitating its adoption in agricultural settings and urban rodent control programs worldwide. This rapid dissemination highlighted its role as a key tool in integrated pest management, particularly in regions facing resistance challenges.

Chemical Properties

Structure and Properties

Bromadiolone is a second-generation 4-hydroxycoumarin derivative characterized by a coumarin core (2H-1-benzopyran-2-one) with a hydroxy group at the 4-position and a complex alkyl side chain attached at the 3-position. This side chain consists of a 3-hydroxy-1-phenylpropyl group further substituted at the 3-position with a 4-(4-bromophenyl)phenyl moiety, creating a branched structure reminiscent of indandione anticoagulants but featuring a brominated biphenyl system. The bromine atom is positioned at the 4' carbon of the terminal phenyl ring, enhancing the molecule's lipophilic character. The molecular formula is CHBrO, and its IUPAC name is 3--4-yl)-3-hydroxy-1-phenylpropyl]-4-hydroxy-2H-chromen-2-one.
The presence of two chiral centersone at the carbon bearing the hydroxy group and the other at the adjacent carbon linking to the phenylresults in four possible stereoisomers: two pairs of enantiomers forming the (1R,3R)/(1S,3S) and (1R,3S)/(1S,3R) diastereomers. Commercial bromadiolone is produced as a racemic mixture containing all four stereoisomers, with the trans diastereomers ((1R,3S) and (1S,3R)) comprising more than 70% of the total, while the cis forms are present in smaller proportions. All stereoisomers exhibit anticoagulant activity, though subtle differences in potency may exist due to stereoselective metabolism.
Physicochemical properties of bromadiolone reflect its design for persistence in biological systems. It has a melting point of 200210 C, indicating thermal stability below this range, and it decomposes rather than boils at higher temperatures. The octanol-water partition coefficient (logP) is approximately 5.2 at pH 45, signifying high lipophilicity that promotes bioaccumulation in lipid-rich tissues. Vapor pressure is negligible at less than 10 mmHg (20 C), resulting in low volatility and minimal atmospheric dissipation. Under standard conditions, bromadiolone remains stable to hydrolysis across pH 59 but is sensitive to light, undergoing rapid photodegradation with a half-life of 12 hours upon UV exposure.
Identification relies on spectroscopic techniques, with UV-Vis absorption showing characteristic maxima at 260 nm and 310 nm in ethanol, attributable to the conjugated coumarin system. Infrared (IR) spectroscopy provides confirmatory evidence through absorption bands typical of its functional groups, including O-H stretching around 3400 cm, C=O stretching in the 16501700 cm region for the lactone and enol forms, and aromatic C-H stretches near 3000 cm; full FTIR spectra in nujol mull are standard for analytical verification. Compared to the first-generation anticoagulant warfarin, bromadiolone's extended side chain with the brominated biphenyl substitution increases lipophilicity and metabolic resistance, leading to greater potency and prolonged activity.

Synthesis

Bromadiolone is synthesized through a multi-step organic process involving the construction of its characteristic 4-hydroxycoumarin core with a substituted propyl side chain. The primary route, as detailed in the original patent by Lipha, begins with the condensation of 4-hydroxycoumarin and benzylidene-4-(4'-bromobiphenyl)acetophenone in the presence of piperidine as a catalyst in dioxane solvent under reflux conditions for approximately 7 hours. This Knoevenagel-type condensation yields the intermediate ketone, 3--4-yl)-1-phenyl-3-oxopropyl]-4-hydroxy-2H-chromen-2-one, which is isolated by recrystallization from a mixture of alcohol and dioxane, achieving a yield of about 62%.
The subsequent key step involves the stereoselective reduction of this ketone intermediate to form the target alcohol. Using aluminum isopropoxide in isopropanol under reflux for 1 hour, followed by acidification with hydrochloric acid, the reduction proceeds to produce bromadiolone as a mixture of diastereoisomers. An alternative industrial reduction employs potassium borohydride in a 60:40 ethanol-water mixture at 50C for 7 hours, which enhances selectivity and results in a diastereoisomer ratio of 0.30, meeting regulatory standards. This step yields 7890% of the product, depending on scale and conditions. The intermediate formation of 4-hydroxy-3-(1-phenylpropyl)coumarin-like structures occurs implicitly during side-chain assembly, though direct cyclization is not emphasized; instead, the process relies on the coumarin's inherent reactivity.
Industrial production, patented by Lipha Pharmaceuticals (now part of Lipha SA), optimizes this multi-step synthesis for scalability, incorporating continuous distillation and dehydration via spray heating or membrane evaporation to handle the wet product efficiently. Overall yields for the process range from 7080% when using refined conditions to minimize losses, with the final product stabilized by complexing agents like maneb and zinc salts to prevent decomposition.
Purification typically involves precipitation from a 5% sodium bicarbonate solution, followed by recrystallization from aqueous ethanol to achieve high purity (>98%). For isolating specific stereoisomers, which influence potency, column chromatography on silica gel is employed, particularly in analytical or specialized batches, ensuring separation based on diastereomeric differences.
Safety considerations in synthesis focus on the handling of flammable solvents like dioxane and isopropanol, as well as reducing agents such as aluminum isopropoxide or potassium borohydride, which require inert atmospheres to prevent ignition. The pre-installed bromine in the biphenyl moiety eliminates late-stage bromination, but control of stereoisomer ratios is critical to avoid variable toxicity profiles; deviations can lead to reduced efficacy or increased environmental risks. Personal protective equipment, including gloves and respirators, is mandatory due to the compound's high acute toxicity (LD50 <1 mg/kg in rodents), and operations are conducted in ventilated facilities to mitigate dust and vapor exposure.

Mechanism of Action

Biochemical Effects

Bromadiolone acts as a potent inhibitor of vitamin K epoxide reductase (VKOR), the enzyme responsible for reducing vitamin K epoxide back to its active hydroquinone form, thereby disrupting the vitamin K cycle essential for blood coagulation. This inhibition prevents the regeneration of active vitamin K, leading to a progressive depletion of functional vitamin K-dependent clotting factors, particularly prothrombin (factor II), proconvertin (factor VII), Christmas factor (factor IX), and Stuart-Prower factor (factor X), which are synthesized in the liver.
The depletion of these clotting factors initiates a cascade of anticoagulant effects, manifesting as prolonged prothrombin time due to impaired activation of the extrinsic coagulation pathway, ultimately resulting in hypoprothrombinemia and widespread internal hemorrhaging. In rodents, a lethal dose of bromadiolone typically induces initial symptoms of bleeding, such as pallor and weakness, within 2448 hours as factor VII levels drop first, with death occurring via fatal hemorrhage 47 days post-ingestion.
Compared to warfarin, bromadiolone exhibits higher potency owing to its stronger binding affinity to VKORapproximately 100 times greater on a molar basisand greater resistance to hepatic metabolism in rodents, which contributes to its extended biological half-life of several days versus warfarin's approximately 40 hours (range 20-60 hours). This enhanced persistence allows bromadiolone to more effectively overcome warfarin resistance in rodent populations while amplifying its anticoagulant disruption.

Pharmacokinetics

Bromadiolone is rapidly absorbed through the gastrointestinal tract following oral ingestion, with bioavailability exceeding 70% in laboratory rats based on excretion measurements. Absorption via dermal contact or inhalation is possible but occurs at a slower rate compared to oral exposure. Peak plasma concentrations are typically reached within 6-9 hours after ingestion in mammals.
Due to its high lipophilicity (log Kow >5 at acidic pH), bromadiolone distributes widely in the body and accumulates preferentially in lipophilic tissues such as the liver and adipose fat. High concentrations have been observed in rat liver tissues post-administration, with detectable residues persisting in pig liver, skin, fat, and plasma for up to 9 weeks. The compound can cross the placenta in mammals, potentially leading to fetal exposure, though transfer efficiency may vary by species and dose.
Metabolism of bromadiolone occurs primarily in the liver via cytochrome P450-mediated hydroxylation, producing at least 10 minor metabolites and one major hydroxylated derivative at the benzylic carbon, which is subsequently conjugated with glucuronic acid. This process is relatively slow in target rodent species, contributing to the compound's prolonged anticoagulant effects.
Excretion is predominantly fecal, with approximately 53% of the dose eliminated via feces and less than 1% via urine within 48 hours in rats, primarily through biliary secretion (about 30% in bile within 8 hours). In rats, 89% of the dose is excreted within 4 days, though a slower elimination phase follows. The plasma half-life in rats ranges from 26-57 hours initially, with liver residues persisting longer (up to 170 days in the terminal phase).
Species differences in pharmacokinetics are notable, with faster clearance observed in birds compared to mammals; for instance, bromadiolone exhibits lower toxicity and potentially quicker elimination in avian species relative to rodents and carnivores. In non-target mammals like dogs, the terminal half-life is extended, estimated at 21-30 days or longer, increasing the risk of secondary poisoning. In sheep, the plasma half-life is approximately 49.5 hours following intravenous or intraruminal administration.

Applications

Rodent Control

Bromadiolone has been widely employed in integrated pest management programs where permitted to control rodent infestations, particularly where resistance to earlier anticoagulants has emerged. Its use is subject to regional regulations, with bans on second-generation anticoagulant rodenticides like bromadiolone in place in California since January 1, 2025, and restrictions on outdoor applications in the UK since July 2024.
The compound demonstrates high efficacy as a single-feed rodenticide, achieving lethal dosing after one feeding event for warfarin-resistant strains of rats and mice, with death typically occurring within 46 days due to internal hemorrhaging as vitamin K reserves deplete. Primary target species include the Norway rat (Rattus norvegicus), roof rat (Rattus rattus), and house mouse (Mus musculus), against which it provides complete population control in laboratory and field settings when bait is consumed.
Application involves strategic placement of tamper-resistant bait stations containing 0.005% bromadiolone formulations, positioned along rodent runways, near harborages, and at entry points in urban, agricultural, and residential areas for both indoor and outdoor use. Bait stations are spaced 1530 feet apart for rats and 812 feet for mice to ensure coverage, with regular inspection to monitor consumption and replace depleted bait.
Compared to first-generation anticoagulants like warfarin, bromadiolone offers advantages including reduced bait-shy behaviorwhere rodents avoid treated bait after surviving initial exposureand greater potency, requiring minimal intake for lethality rather than multiple feedings over several days. This enhances control efficiency in resistant populations, with field trials showing over 90% reduction in warfarin-resistant rat numbers within a week.
To manage resistance, pest control programs should monitor bait take; persistent low consumption despite accessible placements indicates potential resistance, prompting genetic testing of captured rodents for VKORC1 mutations via PCR or sequencing. Guidelines from the Rodenticide Resistance Action Committee recommend rotating to alternative actives, such as other second-generation anticoagulants (e.g., brodifacoum) or non-anticoagulants (e.g., bromethalin), if resistance is suspected following treatment failure with bromadiolone.

Formulations

Bromadiolone is commonly formulated as a ready-to-use rodenticide bait containing 0.005% active ingredient (50 ppm), with variations up to 0.05% depending on the target species and bait type. These preparations typically include grain-based baits, wax blocks, or soft pasta-like baits designed for high palatability and sustained release. Lower concentrations, such as 0.001% (10 ppm), are used in liquid formulations for specific applications.
Commercial products are marketed under various trade names, including Contrac, Maki, Tomcat, and Super-Caid, reflecting availability in global markets like the United States and Europe. Other examples include Deadline and Lanirat, often tailored to regional pest pressures.
Delivery systems encompass ready-to-use baits in pellet, block, or meal forms; concentrates for on-site mixing into custom baits; and tracking powders for dusting applications. Many block and pellet formulations incorporate paraffin or wax to enhance weather resistance, making them suitable for outdoor deployment in damp environments.
Additives in these formulations include grain attractants such as wheat, oats, or rice to increase acceptance by rodents, along with bittering agents like denatonium benzoate (Bitrex) at approximately 10 mg/kg to discourage accidental ingestion by non-target animals. Indicator dyes are also incorporated for easy identification of treated baits, aiding in safe handling and application.
Storage and handling require sealed, original containers in a cool, dry, well-ventilated area to prevent degradation, with a typical shelf life of 23 years under normal conditions due to the compound's stability and low moisture sensitivity. Precautions include avoiding exposure to excessive humidity, which could affect bait integrity, and using protective equipment during handling to minimize skin or inhalation risks.

Toxicology

Acute Toxicity

Bromadiolone exhibits high acute toxicity to rodents, with oral LD50 values indicating lethality from single exposures. In rats, the oral LD50 is 1.125 mg/kg body weight, while in mice it is 1.75 mg/kg body weight. For rabbits, the oral LD50 is 1 mg/kg body weight, whereas non-rodent mammals show greater tolerance, with a maximum tolerated oral dose exceeding 10 mg/kg in dogs and 25 mg/kg in cats.
Acute exposure primarily manifests through anticoagulant effects leading to hemorrhage, with symptoms including lethargy, pallor, epistaxis (nosebleeds), hematuria (blood in urine), and fatal internal bleeding occurring within 1 to several days. These effects stem from disruption of vitamin K-dependent clotting factors, resulting in uncontrolled bleeding in various tissues.
In humans, accidental exposure to bromadiolone, often via ingestion of baits or occupational contact, can induce coagulopathy with symptoms such as epistaxis, gingival bleeding, hematuria, and severe hemorrhage; case reports document pet poisonings in households leading to secondary human exposure risks, though direct human lethality is rare due to prompt medical intervention. Primary exposure occurs orally through contaminated baits, with secondary routes including dermal absorption or inhalation of dusts, though the latter is less common.
Subacute studies in mammals indicate adverse effects at low dose levels, with no-observed-adverse-effect levels (NOAELs) reported around 0.035 mg/kg/day in developmental rat studies.

Chronic Effects

Subchronic exposure to bromadiolone in rodents and dogs is associated with hemorrhagic changes, including in the liver, at doses around 15 g/kg/day. In a 90-day feeding study with dogs, doses of 15 g/kg/day led to hemorrhagic changes in the liver.
Regarding reproductive and developmental toxicity, bromadiolone exhibits no clear impairment of fertility or teratogenic effects in standard tests with rats and rabbits. No embryotoxicity or teratogenicity was evident in rats up to 70 g/kg/day or in rabbits at tested doses, though maternal toxicity (e.g., hemorrhages) occurred at higher levels.
Bromadiolone is classified as non-carcinogenic by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, with no evidence of tumor induction in long-term animal studies. Bromadiolone is non-mutagenic and non-genotoxic based on standard assays such as the Ames test and chromosome aberration studies.
Neurological effects from chronic bromadiolone exposure are rare and typically secondary to anemia resulting from prolonged coagulopathy in poisoning cases. Reports indicate that severe anemia can lead to symptoms such as weakness and dizziness, but direct neurotoxicity is not a primary concern in subchronic or chronic scenarios.
Human epidemiological data on chronic bromadiolone exposure remain limited, primarily derived from occupational settings among handlers. Studies suggest that repeated low-level exposure may result in mild coagulopathy, characterized by prolonged prothrombin times, though severe outcomes are uncommon with proper protective measures.

Environmental Impact

Persistence and Bioaccumulation

Bromadiolone exhibits moderate persistence in soil, with a half-life ranging from 2 to 60 days under aerobic conditions, primarily due to microbial degradation processes that break it down into carbon dioxide and brominated intermediates. Degradation is slower in anaerobic conditions, limiting rapid dissipation in waterlogged environments. Photodegradation plays a minimal role in soil, as the compound's strong adsorption to soil particles reduces exposure to sunlight, though rapid photolysis (half-life of 12 hours or less) can occur in aqueous solutions under direct irradiation.
The compound has low water solubility, approximately 20 mg/L at 20C, which reduces its mobility in aquatic systems but poses a risk of runoff from bait formulations during precipitation events. Bromadiolone strongly adsorbs to soil organic matter and clay, with a soil organic carbon-water partition coefficient (Koc) of around 10,000 to 50,000 L/kg, indicating very low leaching potential and confinement to surface layers. This binding enhances its persistence in terrestrial environments while minimizing groundwater contamination.
Bromadiolone's high lipophilicity, characterized by a log Kow greater than 5 at low pH (approximately 4.3 at neutral pH), facilitates significant bioaccumulation, with bioconcentration factors (BCF) exceeding 100 in fish species such as bluegill sunfish, reaching values up to 460 or higher in whole-body tissues. This property promotes biomagnification through food chains, particularly in aquatic and terrestrial predators that consume contaminated prey. Its lipophilic nature, referenced in structural analyses, contributes to prolonged retention in lipid-rich tissues across trophic levels.
Atmospheric transport of bromadiolone is negligible due to its low volatility, with a vapor pressure of 2 10 Pa at 25 C, preventing significant airborne dispersal or deposition.

Effects on Non-Target Species

Bromadiolone poses significant risks to non-target species through secondary poisoning, where predators and scavengers consume contaminated rodents, leading to the accumulation of residues in their livers that can cause sublethal effects such as reduced clotting ability and internal hemorrhaging. Raptors like great horned owls and red-tailed hawks are particularly vulnerable, with studies showing detection rates of bromadiolone in liver tissues up to 62% in owls and 80% in sparrowhawks from global surveys of wildlife. Scavengers such as domestic dogs and cats also face high exposure risks, with liver residues often exceeding 100200 g/kg, the threshold for potentially lethal effects in birds and mammals. For instance, owls fed rats killed with bromadiolone have died from fatal hemorrhaging, while sublethal exposure in predators can impair reproduction and survival without immediate death. As of 2024, studies continue to document high exposure rates in wildlife, including top predators in regions like the Canary Islands, prompting ongoing EPA mitigation efforts.
Avian species exhibit moderate acute toxicity to bromadiolone, with an oral LD50 of approximately 138 mg/kg in bobwhite quail, indicating that direct ingestion of bait poses a greater threat than secondary exposure in many cases. Despite this relatively high LD50 compared to rodents, wild birds like barn owls experience elevated mortality from consuming poisoned prey in field conditions, where multiple low-dose exposures accumulate over time. Direct bait consumption by ground-foraging birds further amplifies risks in areas with scattered rodenticides.
Non-target mammals, including livestock, demonstrate heightened sensitivity to bromadiolone, resulting in hemorrhagic disorders such as bleeding from orifices, bruising, and bloody stools upon exposure. In cattle, even sublethal doses can lead to prolonged coagulation defects due to the compound's interference with vitamin K-dependent clotting factors, though specific LD50 values remain less documented than for smaller mammals like dogs (LD50 >10 mg/kg). Domestic animals like cats (LD50 >25 mg/kg) are also affected through scavenging, with secondary poisoning causing weakness, hypothermia, and death if untreated.
The widespread use of bromadiolone contributes to ecosystem disruption by causing declines in rodent predators, which in turn affects biodiversity through altered food webs. In urban areas, intensified rodent control has led to high anticoagulant exposure in species like barn owls, with over 70% of necropsied individuals showing residues that correlate with population-level declines and reduced predatory control of pest species. Case studies from cities like those in California highlight how such poisoning cascades into broader ecological imbalances, including increased rodent outbreaks due to fewer natural predators.
To mitigate these impacts, the use of tamper-resistant bait stations is recommended and often required, as they limit access by non-target wildlife and pets while allowing targeted rodent control. These stations enclose baits in secure enclosures, reducing direct ingestion by birds and mammals, and EPA regulations mandate their use in residential and agricultural settings to minimize secondary exposure risks.

Treatment

Antidote Administration

The primary antidote for bromadiolone poisoning is vitamin K1 (phytonadione). In humans, an initial dose of 1050 mg is administered intravenously or orally, depending on severity, to counteract the coagulopathy induced by the rodenticide. In animals, the initial dose is 25 mg/kg subcutaneously or orally.
The treatment regimen consists of daily vitamin K1 administration for 3 weeks to several months, tailored to the severity and extent of exposure as determined by serial monitoring of prothrombin time (PT). PT levels guide dose adjustments and therapy cessation, ensuring clotting function normalizes without recurrence of coagulopathy. For long-acting second-generation anticoagulants like bromadiolone, extended therapy is often necessary due to the compound's prolonged tissue persistence.
Vitamin K1 reverses bromadiolone's toxic effects by restoring activity to vitamin K epoxide reductase (VKOR), thereby enabling the carboxylation and synthesis of essential clotting factors II, VII, IX, and X. This mechanism directly counters the rodenticide's antagonism of the vitamin K cycle, halting further depletion of functional coagulation proteins.
In acute poisoning scenarios, the injectable form of vitamin K1 is preferred for rapid absorption and onset, while oral formulations are suitable for long-term maintenance once the patient stabilizes. Proper preparation involves slow intravenous infusion if needed to minimize adverse reactions, with storage in a cool, dark environment to preserve potency.
Efficacy of vitamin K1 therapy is high when initiated prior to severe hemorrhage, often fully reversing coagulopathy and preventing life-threatening complications in responsive cases. However, the plasma half-life of vitamin K1 is approximately 30 minutes for its initial distribution phase, requiring frequent dosing to maintain therapeutic levels.

Clinical Management

Diagnosis of bromadiolone intoxication begins with a thorough patient history to identify potential exposure, such as accidental ingestion of rodenticides or occupational contact. Clinical suspicion is confirmed through blood tests revealing prolonged prothrombin time (PT) and international normalized ratio (INR), which indicate coagulopathy due to vitamin K antagonism. Additionally, plasma bromadiolone levels can be quantified using high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) or liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS) to verify exposure and monitor elimination, with detectable concentrations aiding in assessing severity.
Decontamination measures are most effective if initiated promptly after ingestion. For recent exposures within 1-2 hours, administration of activated charcoal is recommended to adsorb the toxin and prevent further gastrointestinal absorption, typically at a dose of 1 g/kg. Induced emesis may be considered under poison control guidance but is generally avoided in cases of altered mental status or delayed presentation to minimize aspiration risk. Gastric lavage is rarely indicated due to the low volume typically ingested and potential complications.
Supportive therapies form the cornerstone of management for bromadiolone poisoning, focusing on correcting coagulopathy and preventing hemorrhagic complications. In patients with severe anemia from blood loss, red blood cell transfusions are administered to restore oxygen-carrying capacity, while fresh frozen plasma (FFP) or prothrombin complex concentrates provide immediate clotting factors for active bleeding or critically elevated INR. Hospitalization is essential for close monitoring of vital signs, serial coagulation profiles, and signs of internal hemorrhage, with intravenous fluids and vasopressors used as needed for hemodynamic stability.
In special populations, such as pregnant patients, management requires heightened vigilance due to risks to both mother and fetus, with treatment protocols emphasizing aggressive correction of coagulopathy while monitoring fetal well-being through ultrasonography. Veterinary protocols for pets, such as dogs and cats, closely mirror human care, involving decontamination, supportive transfusions, and hospitalization for monitoring, adapted to species-specific dosing and physiology.
Prognosis for bromadiolone intoxication is generally excellent with early intervention, including decontamination and supportive care, leading to full recovery in most cases without long-term sequelae. Delayed diagnosis or treatment, however, can result in severe complications such as organ failure from unchecked hemorrhage, particularly affecting the kidneys or central nervous system, with mortality possible up to two weeks post-ingestion if bleeding is extensive.

Regulatory Status

Approvals and Restrictions

Bromadiolone was first registered by the United States Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) in 1980 as a restricted-use pesticide (RUP), necessitating application by certified applicators or under their direct supervision for specified uses to mitigate risks from its high potency. This classification stems from its status as a second-generation anticoagulant rodenticide, which poses significant hazards to humans, pets, and wildlife if mishandled.
In the European Union, bromadiolone is authorized under the Biocidal Products Regulation (BPR, Regulation (EU) No 528/2012) for product-type 14 (rodenticides), with approval renewed in 2017 for use subject to strict risk mitigation measures, including the mandatory deployment of tamper-resistant bait stations to contain baits and reduce exposure to non-target species. These measures address potential secondary poisoning risks identified in environmental assessments. In May 2025, the European Chemicals Agency's Biocidal Products Committee (BPC) adopted an opinion supporting the renewal of bromadiolone for product-type 14, recommending approval for 9 out of 11 assessed uses, while not supporting use against mice by non-professionals and permanent baiting by professionals due to availability of safer alternatives; the final decision is pending from the European Commission and EU Member States.
Product labeling for bromadiolone rodenticides features the signal word "Danger" to indicate acute toxicity, along with requirements for child-resistant packaging to prevent accidental ingestion by children or pets. Such packaging must meet EPA standards for difficult access by young children while remaining operable by adults.
Key restrictions prohibit aerial applications of bromadiolone baits, limiting use to ground-based methods near structures, and impose quantity limitssuch as no more than 8 ounces of bait per station and a maximum of 3 pounds total per property for residential sitesto curb secondary poisoning in wildlife. As part of the ongoing registration review, the EPA's 2022 Proposed Interim Decision enhanced buffer zones by restricting applications to within 50 feet of buildings, particularly near water bodies, to further protect aquatic ecosystems; the final biological evaluation was released on November 22, 2024, with final interim decisions expected later in 2025 or in 2026.

International Variations

In the United Kingdom, following Brexit, the regulation of bromadiolone aligns with the EU Biocidal Products Regulation (BPR) through the establishment of the Great Britain BPR (GB BPR), which governs its approval as an essential active substance for rodent control despite its classification as a persistent, bioaccumulative, and toxic (PBT) substance. Under this framework, bromadiolone remains approved for professional use in biocidal products, but recent initiatives focus on reducing reliance on second-generation anticoagulant rodenticides (SGARs) like bromadiolone through comparative assessments of safer alternatives. Specifically, authorisation for its use in open areas and waste dumps has been withdrawn, with sales ceasing on 4 July 2024 and use of existing stock permitted until 31 December 2024, after which it is prohibited, as part of broader stewardship efforts to mitigate environmental risks.
In Canada, Health Canada's Pest Management Regulatory Agency (PMRA) imposes use restrictions on bromadiolone to protect non-target species, limiting commercial products primarily to indoor applications and outdoor use within 15 to 100 meters of buildings, depending on the setting. These measures vary by province; for instance, in British Columbia, outdoor applications are further confined to within 15 meters of structures to minimize wildlife exposure. Additionally, the PMRA mandates incident reporting for registrants under the Pest Control Products Incident Reporting Regulations, requiring notification of any pesticide-related wildlife incidents in Canada or the United States to inform ongoing risk assessments.
Australia's Australian Pesticides and Veterinary Medicines Authority (APVMA) permits bromadiolone for non-agricultural uses, such as domestic and commercial pest control, where it is available over the counter for indoor applications in residential settings. However, its use is prohibited in agricultural areas due to significant risks to native wildlife, including secondary poisoning of predators like owls and quolls, as evidenced by documented cases of exposure in non-target species. This distinction reflects heightened protections for biodiversity, with emergency permit applications for broader use, such as during mouse plagues, routinely rejected on environmental grounds. The APVMA is conducting an ongoing review of anticoagulant rodenticides, with a proposed regulatory decision anticipated in 2025.
In developing countries across Asia and Africa, bromadiolone sees widespread application in urban and agricultural rodent control with comparatively fewer regulatory restrictions, often due to limited enforcement capacity and high pest pressures in densely populated areas. The World Health Organization (WHO) classifies bromadiolone formulations as moderately hazardous (Class II), allowing its use in integrated pest management where alternatives are unavailable, though this has raised concerns over non-target impacts in regions with diverse wildlife.
Global harmonization efforts, guided by the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) and the Codex Alimentarius Commission, influence standards for maximum residue limits (MRLs) of rodenticides like bromadiolone in food commodities, promoting consistent international trade practices while addressing residue safety in animal-derived products. Although no specific Codex maximum residue limits (CXLs) exist for bromadiolone, these frameworks encourage alignment of national MRLs to prevent discrepancies that could affect food safety and export compliance.
