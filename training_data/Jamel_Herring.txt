 Jamel William Herring (born October 30, 1985), known by the ring name "Semper Fi," is an American professional boxer who fights as a southpaw in the super featherweight division. A veteran of the United States Marine Corps who served two tours in Iraq after enlisting in 2003, Herring represented the United States at the 2012 Summer Olympics in London as captain of the national boxing team, competing in the light welterweight category without advancing past the first round.
Turning professional in December 2012 following his Olympic appearance, Herring built a record of 24 wins and 5 losses, with 12 knockouts, competing primarily at lightweight and super featherweight. His career highlight came in May 2019, when he defeated Japan's Masayuki Ito by unanimous decision to capture the vacant WBO super featherweight title in his first world championship bout, a victory achieved on what would have been his late daughter's 10th birthday. Herring successfully defended the belt three times, including against Lamont Roach Jr. in November 2019 and Carl Frampton in April 2021, before losing it via 10th-round technical knockout to undefeated prospect Shakur Stevenson in October 2021.
Born in Rockville Centre, New York, and raised in nearby Coram on Long Island, Herring overcame a challenging childhood in foster care before finding discipline and purpose in the Marines, where he began boxing competitively. Now based in Cincinnati, Ohio, and promoted by Top Rank, Herring's resilience as a former Olympian and decorated veteran has defined his professional tenure, marked by high-profile bouts against fighters like Carl Frampton and Jonathan Oquendo, even as he navigated setbacks including two early career losses and a 2024 split decision defeat to Jackson Jon England. In July 2025, he signed with Bare Knuckle Fighting Championship (BKFC) for a professional debut.

Background

Early Life

Jamel Herring was born on October 30, 1985, in Rockville Centre, New York, to Michael Mitchell and Jeanine Herring, part of a working-class family facing significant hardships. As an infant, Herring and his brother Jarod were removed from their mother's custody by Child Protective Services due to her involvement with drugs, leading to time in foster care that profoundly shaped his early years. His mother later regained custody, allowing the family to reunite in Coram, a small community on Long Island, where Herring grew up amid ongoing challenges including bullying at school and exposure to a potentially wayward street life.
These formative experiences instilled resilience in Herring, bolstered by key influences like his mother Jeanine's determination to rebuild their family and the guidance of childhood mentor Stephen Brown, whom he met at age six and who introduced him to boxing as a positive outlet. Growing up in Gordon Heights near Coram, Herring showed an early interest in sports, initially drawn to activities like football before discovering boxing during his high school years, which provided structure amid the instability.
Herring attended high school in Coram, New York, graduating in 2003 without pursuing further formal education immediately, instead taking initial steps toward independence before enlisting in the military later that year as a pivotal shift from his challenging youth. The support from his mother and brother during this period emphasized family bonds and discipline, helping him navigate the transition to adulthood.

Military Service

Jamel Herring enlisted in the United States Marine Corps in October 2003 at the age of 18, shortly after graduating high school, motivated by personal hardships, including the loss of his mentor and best friend Stephen Brown to cancer the following year. He completed recruit training at Marine Corps Recruit Depot Parris Island, South Carolina, where he earned the rank of private first class before advancing to roles within engineering support units.
As a field electrician attached to the 8th Engineer Support Battalion, 2nd Marine Logistics Group, Herring deployed on his first tour to Fallujah, Iraq, in 2005, where he conducted repairs on machinery and equipment amid ongoing combat operations, including convoy security and patrols. His duties involved working in high-risk environments, often under fire, as part of efforts to maintain infrastructure in the volatile region. In 2007, he returned for a second tour to Al Taqaddum Air Base in Anbar Province, focusing on similar engineering tasks while serving as a gunner on security convoys, during which he lost count of fellow service members killed in action.
These deployments exposed Herring to intense combat stress, including witnessing numerous casualties and the death of a bomb disposal expert during his second tour, which left him haunted and contributed to initial mental health challenges such as post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). The experiences instilled a profound sense of discipline but also survivor's guilt, as he grappled with the psychological toll of war while his first wife was pregnant back home. To cope, Herring turned to boxing programs offered within the Marine Corps as a physical and emotional outlet upon returning from his second deployment in late 2007, beginning informal training that helped channel his stress and built on his earlier high school interest in the sport.

Amateur Boxing Career

Marine Corps Achievements

Jamel Herring joined the All-Marine Boxing Team in 2006 while serving as a basic electrician with the 8th Engineer Support Battalion at Camp Lejeune, North Carolina, but temporarily left the team in 2007 for a deployment to Iraq. Upon returning late that year, he rejoined the team in 2008 and quickly established himself as a standout in the lightweight division, winning the All-Marine Championship that year after intra-unit competitions. His success continued into 2009, when he captured the All-Marine title again, demonstrating marked improvement in technical skills such as footwork and defensive counterpunching honed through rigorous daily training sessions under coaches including Gunnery Sgt. Narcisco Aleman and head coach Jesse Ravelo.
These victories propelled Herring to represent the Marines in the Armed Forces Boxing Championships, where he earned silver medals in both 2008 and 2009, competing against top boxers from the Army, Navy, and Air Force in bouts that tested his endurance and strategic adaptability. Notable among these was his 2009 final against an Army opponent, where Herring's southpaw stance and combination punching nearly secured gold but fell short in a close decision, further fueling his drive during subsequent deployments that briefly interrupted training but reinforced his mental resilience. By 2011, his consistent performance made him the first active-duty Marine to qualify for the U.S. national boxing team since 1992, earning a spot in the Olympic trials through excellence in national qualifiers while still on active duty.
Herring's training regimen with the All-Marine team emphasized six-day weeks of sparring, strength conditioning, and tactical drills at Camp Lejeune, often simulating service member rivalries to build competitive edge; this approach contributed to his overall amateur record of 81 wins and 15 losses, with many victories over fellow military personnel in internal tournaments.

2012 Olympic Participation

Jamel Herring qualified for the 2012 Summer Olympics in the light welterweight division (64 kg) through a rigorous national selection process. He first earned a spot by winning gold at the 2011 U.S. Olympic Boxing Trials held in Mobile, Alabama, defeating multiple national champions en route to the title. Following that, to secure his Olympic berth amid the competitive qualification pathway, Herring captured the 2012 USA Boxing National Championships in the light welterweight category, described as the deepest field in the tournament. He then advanced to the AIBA Americas Olympic Qualifying Tournament in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, in May 2012, where he won three bouts over four days, including a 12-10 decision in the quarterfinals against Venezuela's Yoelvis Hernandez, to clinch the qualification spot for Team USA. This success built on his prior achievements in Marine Corps boxing competitions, marking him as a standout amateur contender.
As an active-duty U.S. Marine Corps sergeant stationed at Camp Lejeune, North Carolina, Herring faced significant preparation challenges leading up to the London Games. Balancing military duties with elite-level training required careful coordination, including obtaining leave to relocate temporarily to the U.S. Olympic Training Center in Colorado Springs for focused sessions on technique, conditioning, and sparring. His schedule incorporated bag work, agility drills, and strength exercises at facilities like the Veterans Memorial Gym, all while maintaining his service commitments, which he credited with instilling the discipline needed for the Olympic grind. Herring also served as co-captain of the U.S. boxing team alongside Rau'shee Warren, adding leadership responsibilities to his regimen.
At the 2012 London Olympics, Herring's campaign ended in the first round on July 31, 2012, when he faced Kazakhstan's Daniyar Yeleussinov at the ExCeL Arena. Despite a competitive effort marked by aggressive pressure and solid defense in spurts, Herring lost by unanimous decision with a score of 19-9 after three rounds, as Yeleussinov's superior footwork and counterpunching proved decisive. Herring expressed pride in his performance, noting the bout's intensity and his growth as a fighter, even in defeat.
Herring's participation held historical significance as the first active-duty U.S. Marine to qualify for the Olympic boxing team since 1992, when Montell Griffin competed, and the only Marine across all sports at the 2012 Games. His achievement highlighted the intersection of military service and athletic excellence, inspiring fellow service members and underscoring the Marines' legacy in combat sports.

Professional Boxing Career

Early Professional Years (20122018)

Jamel Herring made his professional boxing debut on December 8, 2012, defeating Jose M. Valderrama by unanimous decision after four rounds at the Business Expo Center in Anaheim, California. Drawing on his strong amateur foundation as a 2012 U.S. Olympian and Marine Corps standout, Herring quickly adapted to the pro ranks, showcasing technical skill and resilience in his early bouts.
Over the next three years, Herring built an undefeated record of 15-0, securing victories against a mix of journeymen and prospects, including stoppages against durable opponents such as Yakubu Amidu in October 2015 by unanimous decision over eight rounds at the U.S. Bank Arena in Cincinnati, Ohio. Notable wins included a third-round RTD over Justin Robbins in 2013. His approach emphasized volume punching and southpaw angles, establishing him as a promising lightweight contender by the end of 2015.
Herring's momentum faced its first setback on July 2, 2016, when he suffered his professional debut lossa tenth-round technical knockoutto Denis Shafikov in a lightweight clash at the Santander Arena in Reading, Pennsylvania, ending his unbeaten streak in a grueling battle against the experienced Russian southpaw. He rebounded with a unanimous decision victory over Art Hovhannisyan on February 25, 2017, but encountered another defeat on August 5, 2017, losing a unanimous decision to Ladarius Miller after ten rounds at Sam's Town Hotel & Gambling Hall in Las Vegas, dropping his record to 16-2.
In 2018, Herring signed a promotional deal with Top Rank on January 15, aligning with a major platform to elevate his career trajectory. He parted ways with longtime trainer Mike Stafford and began training under Brian "Bomac" McIntyre in Las Vegas, refining his style ahead of key opportunities. That September, Herring captured the vacant IBF-USBA junior lightweight title with a unanimous decision over 10 rounds against John Vincent Moralde at the Save Mart Arena in Fresno, California, marking a significant step toward world contention. He closed the year with another stoppage, a second-round TKO against Adeilson Dos Santos in December at the American Bank Center in Corpus Christi, Texas, bringing his record to 20-2.

Title Acquisition and Defenses (20192021)

On May 25, 2019, Jamel Herring captured the vacant WBO junior lightweight title by defeating Masayuki Ito via unanimous decision over 12 rounds in Kissimmee, Florida, with scores of 118-110, 118-110, and 116-112. At 33 years old, Herring, a southpaw with a height of 5 feet 10 inches and a 72-inch reach, utilized his physical advantages to outwork the shorter Japanese champion, marking a significant upset as Herring entered as a +400 underdog. This victory elevated Herring to 21-2 in his professional record and established him as a full-time champion after years of building resilience in earlier bouts.
Herring made his first title defense on November 9, 2019, against Lamont Roach Jr. in Fresno, California, securing a unanimous decision victory with scores of 117-110, 116-111, and 116-111 after 12 rounds. In his second defense on September 26, 2020, in Las Vegas, Nevada, Herring retained the belt when Jonathan Oquendo was disqualified in the third round for repeated intentional headbutts that caused a cut above Herring's eye. Herring's third defense came on April 3, 2021, against Carl Frampton in Dubai, United Arab Emirates, where he won by sixth-round TKO after two knockdowns, prompting Frampton's retirement. These defenses showcased Herring's evolution into a pressure-oriented fighter, leveraging his height and reach to close distances aggressively while maintaining a high volume of punches, though critics noted occasional defensive lapses.
Herring's reign ended in a high-profile bout on October 23, 2021, against undefeated contender Shakur Stevenson in Atlanta, Georgia, where he suffered a tenth-round technical knockout loss after being stopped on cuts and swelling. Entering as the favorite at 23-2, Herring's pressure style was neutralized by Stevenson's superior footwork and counterpunching, leading to the title changing hands and Herring vacating the championship. This period from 2019 to 2021 represented Herring's peak, with three successful defenses highlighting his growth into a dominant force at 130 pounds through relentless forward pressure and physical imposition.

Later Fights and Current Status (20222025)

Following the loss of his WBO super featherweight title to Shakur Stevenson in October 2021, Jamel Herring moved up to lightweight for his next bout, facing undefeated contender Jamaine Ortiz on May 21, 2022, at Resorts World Las Vegas in Nevada. Herring dropped a unanimous decision over 10 rounds (98-92, 97-93 twice), marking his first defeat at 135 pounds and prompting a brief retirement announcement shortly thereafter.
Herring returned to the ring over a year later, on November 7, 2023, against unbeaten prospect Nicholas Molina at the Edison Ballroom in New York City, dropping back to super featherweight for the scheduled eight-rounder. He secured a first-round TKO victory at 2:49, stopping Molina with a barrage of punches and showcasing his experience against a younger opponent. This win improved his record to 24-4 (12 KOs) and signaled a potential resurgence, though Herring expressed interest in title contention at 130 pounds moving forward.
His comeback was short-lived, as Herring suffered a split decision loss to Jackson Jon England on April 3, 2024, in a 10-round super featherweight bout at the Adelaide Entertainment Centre in Adelaide, Australia. England outboxed the 38-year-old Herring over the distance (scores of 96-94 twice for England and 97-93 for Herring), handing him his fifth professional defeat and highlighting challenges associated with Herring's advancing age.
As of November 2025, Herring remains inactive in professional boxing since the England defeat, with no scheduled fights announced, leading to speculation about a possible full retirement after a career spanning over a decade. His professional record stands at 24-5-0 (12 KOs), reflecting a post-championship phase marked by sporadic activity and a shift between weight classes amid the physical demands of nearing 40.

Personal Life

Family and Relationships

Jamel Herring has been in a long-term relationship with his wife, Jennifer Dickerson Herring, whom he met as a child when they lived across the street from each other in New York after his mother regained custody of him. The couple, childhood sweethearts, began dating during their teenage years and have been together for nearly two decades, formalizing their union with a marriage on September 26, 2015.
Herring is a father to five surviving childrendaughters Kamren and Jazmyne, and sons Stephen, Jamel Jr., and Justicewith his daughter Ariyanah tragically passing away from sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS) in July 2009 at two months old. His military deployments to Iraq created significant absences during key family moments, such as missing the births of his first two children, Kamren in 2004 and Stephen in 2007, which Herring has described as emotionally challenging, particularly as Stephen was named after a close friend who died during service. These separations strained family dynamics, with Herring later reflecting on the helplessness felt during Ariyanah's illness and death, which occurred shortly after his discharge from the Marines, deepening his commitment to providing stability for his family through his post-military pursuits.
As the primary provider post-military, Herring relocated his family from Long Island, New York, and Camp Lejeune, North Carolina, to Cincinnati, Ohio, around 2012 to train under coach Mike Stafford, seeking a more stable environment for his boxing career and family life away from the uncertainties of service. This move allowed him to focus on professional boxing as a means to support his growing family, turning his athletic talents into a sustainable livelihood. Jennifer has played a pivotal role in his career decisions, offering emotional support despite the stress of his absences for training camps and fights, which Herring credits as a key factor in his resilience during tough periods, including title defenses and personal setbacks.
Throughout his career, Herring has drawn motivation from his family, dedicating victories to them and particularly to Ariyanah's memory, which has influenced his determination to continue boxing even after challenging losses. His familial bonds have remained a cornerstone of personal stability.

Acting and Other Interests

In addition to his boxing career, Jamel Herring has pursued acting opportunities that draw on his physical prowess and life experiences. In 2019, he secured a role as a boxer in Steven Spielberg's remake of West Side Story (2021), responding to an open casting call specifically seeking boxers for a scene set in a 1950s gym. During filming, Herring impressed Spielberg with his authentic performance, leveraging his status as a world champion to bring realism to the part, which involved wearing period-appropriate leather boots, gloves, and protective gear.
Herring's entry into acting began around 2019, transitioning from his rising fame in professional boxing to Hollywood tryouts, where his athletic background provided a natural fit for physically demanding roles. He has since appeared in smaller projects, including the short film The Eye (2018) as Jamel, the series Ayanna Shon's Blood or Not (2020), Differences (2023), and the upcoming The Incident (2025). His military service as a U.S. Marine added authenticity to these portrayals, particularly in scenes requiring discipline and intensity.
Beyond acting, Herring engages in various non-boxing pursuits that reflect his commitment to service and personal growth. He participates in Marine Corps veteran events, such as headlining the 2023 "Broadway Boxing: Heroes On The Hudson" card to honor military personnel and raise support for those who served. Herring also delivers motivational speaking engagements, sharing stories of resilience drawn from his boxing and military background in interviews and social media content to inspire others facing adversity. Additionally, he contributes to fitness coaching by posting training routines and workouts on platforms like YouTube, offering insights into his rigorous preparation that benefit aspiring athletes.
These interests have provided Herring with a creative and communal outlet during periods of career transition in boxing, allowing him to channel his energy into mentorship and performance while maintaining balance in his professional life.

Mental Health and Advocacy

Experiences from Military Service

Jamel Herring's experiences with post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) originated from his two deployments to Iraq between 2005 and 2007, where he served as a U.S. Marine in combat zones including urban warfare in Fallujah during his first tour at age 19. As a field electrician on the initial deployment and later on security details with a machine gun during his second tour, Herring faced intense exposures such as mortar attacks, sniper threats, roadside bombs, and the loss of a sergeant in an explosion, which triggered the onset of PTSD symptoms upon his return. These included persistent flashbacks of wartime events, heightened anxiety from constant fear, and severe insomnia that disrupted his ability to rest.
The daily impacts of these symptoms profoundly affected Herring's reintegration into civilian life after his discharge, manifesting as hypervigilance akin to paranoiasuch as feeling like a "sitting duck"and prolonged periods of isolation where he avoided family interactions for weeks while grappling with a sense of purposelessness. Sleep disturbances exacerbated his emotional exhaustion, contributing to clinical depression and a deep internal struggle that left him in what he described as a "real deep, dark place." These challenges persisted as he transitioned from military service, hindering his adjustment to everyday routines and social connections.
Prior to his formal PTSD diagnosis, Herring turned to boxing as an initial coping mechanism, using intense training sessions to redirect his intrusive thoughts and channel the aggression stemming from his trauma. He has noted that "training helped take my thoughts away," providing a therapeutic outlet that offered structure and purpose amid his psychological turmoil. In the 2010s, following his entry into professional boxing in 2012, Herring sought formal support through the Department of Veterans Affairs (VA), undergoing months of therapy with doctors and counselors to address his service-related trauma.
Herring has briefly referenced these personal struggles in public interviews, emphasizing the lasting effects of his combat experiences.

Public Discussions and Support Efforts

In a May 7, 2025, interview, Jamel Herring openly discussed the severe psychological toll of his military service, revealing that he experienced "grim thoughts" including suicidal ideation stemming from war trauma. He described these struggles as part of ongoing battles with PTSD, emphasizing how flashbacks and emotional isolation intensified after his deployments. Herring credited seeking professional help and public disclosure as key to managing these symptoms, noting that core PTSD manifestations like hypervigilance and depressive episodes persisted but became more controllable through therapy.
Since 2020, Herring has actively engaged in advocacy for veterans' mental health, partnering with organizations like the PTSD Foundation of America to support recovery programs for service members. He has spoken at PTSD awareness events, including visits to facilities such as Camp Hope, where he shares his experiences to encourage others to seek assistance and reduce stigma around mental health in the military community. These efforts align with his broader commitment to Marine Corps-related charities, using his platform as a former champion to highlight resources for trauma survivors.
Herring has frequently addressed the intersection of his military service and boxing in media appearances, linking the discipline gained from both to building resilience against mental health challenges. In ESPN discussions, he joined fellow veterans to explore living with PTSD, underscoring how the sport provides structure amid emotional turmoil.
Addressing his mental health has profoundly shaped Herring's approach to his career, influencing fight preparation by incorporating therapy and mindfulness to maintain focus and avoid burnout.

Legal Issues

January 2025 Arrest

On January 9, 2025, former WBO super featherweight champion Jamel Herring was arrested in Mason, Ohio, by local police on misdemeanor charges of domestic violence and assault.
The incident reportedly involved an altercation with his wife, Jennifer Herring, who alleged that he returned home extremely intoxicated around 2 a.m. and began bullying her over relationship issues before physically attacking her, including punching her multiple times across her body and pulling her hair. Herring was booked into Warren County Jail shortly after 6:30 p.m. and held briefly before being released on bond, with bail set at $50,000 for the assault charge and $75,000 for domestic violence.
His initial court appearance was scheduled for January 21, 2025, in Warren County Municipal Court. The charges were dismissed on February 6, 2025. In the immediate aftermath, Herring denied the allegations of violence in statements to media outlets, attributing tensions to ongoing personal stresses within his family life. Coverage in boxing and sports media highlighted the incident amid Herring's history of military service and family challenges, sparking discussions on the pressures faced by retired athletes.

March 2025 Arrest

On March 18, 2025, former junior lightweight boxing champion Jamel Herring was arrested in Warren County, Ohio, and booked into the Warren County Jail at 4:01 p.m. local time on charges of violating a protective order and violating probation.
The charges arose from Herring's alleged breach of a permanent restraining order issued in January 2025, which prohibited any contact with his wife following a prior domestic incident. The probation violation related to conditions from a prior 2018 domestic violence conviction. This no-contact order was part of protective measures established after the earlier case, and the violation reportedly involved unauthorized communication with his wife.
Herring was initially held without bail pending arraignment. His next court appearance was scheduled for March 20, 2025. Herring's attorney, Clyde Bennett, responded to the arrest by asserting that there was insufficient evidence to support the allegations and describing the claim as false.
