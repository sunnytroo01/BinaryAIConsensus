Histone acetyltransferase
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Histone acetyltransferases (HATs), also known as lysine acetyltransferases (KATs), are a superfamily of enzymes that catalyze the transfer of an acetyl group from acetyl-coenzyme A (acetyl-CoA) to the -amino group of lysine residues on histone proteins. This post-translational modification neutralizes the positive charge of the lysine side chain, reducing the affinity between histones and negatively charged DNA, which typically results in a more relaxed chromatin structure that facilitates access by transcriptional machinery. HATs play a central role in epigenetic regulation, as the acetylation marks they deposit serve as binding sites for reader proteins containing bromodomains, thereby influencing gene expression patterns across diverse cellular processes.
HATs are classified into distinct families based on their structural and catalytic properties, broadly divided into Type A (nuclear) and Type B (cytoplasmic) enzymes. Type A HATs, which include major subfamilies such as GNAT (e.g., GCN5/PCAF/KAT2), MYST (e.g., TIP60, MOF/KAT5), p300/CBP (KAT3), SRC (e.g., SRC1/KAT13A), and TAFII250 (e.g., TAF1/KAT8)primarily acetylate nucleosomal histones in the nucleus to modulate transcription. In contrast, Type B HATs, such as HAT1 (KAT1), act in the cytoplasm to acetylate newly synthesized free histones, aiding in their deposition onto DNA without directly impacting transcription. Structurally, HATs feature a conserved catalytic core for acetyl-CoA binding and substrate recognition, flanked by variable regions that confer specificity for particular lysine residues on histones H2A, H2B, H3, or H4.
Beyond histones, HATs acetylate a wide array of non-histone proteins, including transcription factors (e.g., p53, c-MYC) and coactivators, thereby extending their regulatory influence to processes such as DNA repair, cell cycle progression, and signal transduction. In epigenetics, HAT activity opposes that of histone deacetylases (HDACs), establishing dynamic acetylation-deacetylation cycles that dictate chromatin accessibility and cellular identity. Recent studies as of 2025 have highlighted HATs' roles in metabolism-driven chromatin dynamics and potential as targets for novel inhibitors in disease therapy. Dysregulation of HATs is implicated in various diseases, including cancers where fusions like MOZ-TIF2 drive leukemogenesis through aberrant acetylation, and neurodevelopmental disorders such as Rubinstein-Taybi syndrome linked to CBP mutations. Notable examples include p300/CBP, versatile transcriptional coactivators essential for hematopoietic stem cell function and super-enhancer formation, and GCN5/PCAF, which promote gene activation in response to environmental cues.

Classification and families

Type A (nuclear) HATs

Type A histone acetyltransferases (HATs) are nuclear enzymes primarily responsible for acetylating lysine residues on histones within the context of assembled chromatin, thereby modulating chromatin structure and facilitating gene expression regulation. This acetylation neutralizes the positive charge of lysine, reducing the affinity between histones and negatively charged DNA, which promotes a more open chromatin conformation conducive to transcription. These enzymes play crucial roles in eukaryotic gene activation, development, and cellular responses to environmental cues.
The major families of Type A HATs, classified based on sequence homology and structural motifs, include the GNAT (Gcn5-related N-acetyltransferase) family, the MYST family, and the p300/CBP family. The GNAT family encompasses nuclear members such as Gcn5 (KAT2A) and PCAF (KAT2B), which form multi-subunit complexes like SAGA and are highly conserved from yeast to humans, ensuring essential transcriptional functions across eukaryotes. The MYST family, named after its founding members (MOZ, Ybf2/Sas3, Sas2, and Tip60), includes enzymes like MOF (KAT8) and Tip60 (KAT5), which are also broadly conserved in eukaryotes and involved in dosage compensation and DNA repair processes. The p300/CBP family comprises p300 (KAT3B) and CBP (KAT3A), transcriptional co-activators with HAT domains that exhibit conservation in metazoans and integrate signals from multiple transcription factors.
In contrast to Type B HATs, which primarily acetylate newly synthesized free histones in the cytoplasm to prepare them for nucleosome assembly, Type A HATs specifically target histones in pre-existing chromatin within the nucleus to dynamically control transcription. The landmark discovery of Gcn5 as the first identified HAT in the mid-1990s, through studies in Tetrahymena and yeast, established the direct link between histone acetylation and transcriptional activation, revolutionizing understanding of epigenetic regulation.

Type B (cytoplasmic) HATs

Type B histone acetyltransferases (HATs) are a class of enzymes primarily localized in the cytoplasm, where they acetylate newly synthesized free histones prior to their incorporation into nucleosomes during chromatin assembly. Unlike type A HATs, which act on chromatin-bound histones in the nucleus, type B HATs modify soluble histones to facilitate their maturation, nuclear import, and subsequent deposition onto DNA, playing a crucial role in maintaining chromatin structure during cell division.
The prototypical type B HAT is Hat1, which specifically acetylates newly synthesized histone H4 at lysine residues 5 and 12 (H4K5 and H4K12). Hat1 functions as part of a heteromeric complex with the histone chaperone Hat2 (also known as RbAp46 in mammals), which stabilizes the enzyme and enhances its catalytic efficiency by up to 10-fold through conformational changes that improve substrate binding. This acetylation pattern on free H4 is conserved and essential for proper histone chaperone interactions, such as with NASP, enabling efficient nuclear transport of the histone H3-H4 dimer. In yeast and mammals, the Hat1-Hat2 complex exemplifies the coordinated action of type B HATs in histone processing.
Another key example is Rtt109, a fungal-specific type B HAT that acetylates histone H3 at lysine 56 (H3K56), primarily on nascent histones in the cytoplasm. Rtt109 requires histone chaperones such as Asf1 or Vps75 for activation, with Vps75 stimulating H3K56 acetylation by approximately 30-fold; this modification promotes the binding of H3-H4 dimers to DNA during nucleosome assembly. Although Rtt109 is absent in higher eukaryotes, its function in facilitating replication-coupled chromatin deposition is conserved through orthologous mechanisms involving enzymes like p300/CBP, which perform H3K56 acetylation in metazoans.
Biologically, type B HATs like Hat1 and Rtt109 are integral to DNA replication and repair processes, as their acetylation events prepare histones for timely nucleosome formation at replication forks and damage sites, thereby ensuring genomic stability. For instance, Hat1 contributes to the reassembly of chromatin following double-strand break repair by interacting with repair factors such as RAD51. These enzymes are highly conserved across eukaryotes, from yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) to humans, underscoring their fundamental role in cell cycle progression and histone homeostasis.

Structural features

Conserved catalytic core

The conserved catalytic core of histone acetyltransferases (HATs) exhibits a characteristic alpha/beta fold, consisting of a central beta-sheet typically comprising four to five antiparallel strands surrounded by alpha-helices, which forms the foundational architecture for acetyl group transfer. This core domain varies in size across HAT families, ranging from approximately 110 to 500 residues, with smaller variants like those in the GNAT family encompassing about 160 residues and larger ones in families such as p300/CBP extending to around 400 residues. The structure creates a V-shaped cleft or concave surface that accommodates the acetyl-CoA substrate, burying a significant surface area (e.g., ~1100 in Hat1) through a combination of hydrogen bonds, salt bridges, and van der Waals interactions.
Key motifs within this core include a highly conserved glutamate residue, such as Glu173 in yeast Gcn5, which functions as a general base to deprotonate the epsilon-amino group of the target lysine, facilitating nucleophilic attack on the acetyl carbonyl of acetyl-CoA. The acetyl-CoA binding pocket features conserved motifs like motif A (e.g., Q/RxxGxG in the GNAT superfamily), which interacts with the pyrophosphate moiety, while hydrophobic residuessuch as Ile217, Val254, Pro257, Phe261 in Hat1form a pocket that stabilizes the acetyl methyl group through van der Waals contacts. These elements ensure precise positioning of the cofactor for catalysis across diverse HATs.
Evolutionary conservation of the core domain is evident from yeast to humans, with structural homology in proteins like yeast Gcn5 and human PCAF or p300/CBP, preserving the capacity for acetyl group transfer from acetyl-CoA to lysine residues. This homology underscores a fundamental role in epigenetic regulation, maintained through sequence and structural similarities in the GNAT superfamily and beyond. Biophysical analyses reveal ordered water molecules in the active site, such as bridging waters between residues like Glu162 and Asn258 in Hat1 or catalytic waters in Gcn5, which stabilize the active site conformation and aid in substrate orientation without participating directly in the chemical transfer step.

Family-specific domains and variations

The GNAT family histone acetyltransferases feature a compact catalytic core of approximately 160 residues, characterized by a conserved acetyl-CoA binding motif but lacking extensive accessory domains that confer broad regulatory functions. Unlike other families, GNAT members such as Gcn5 exhibit variable N- and C-terminal extensions that facilitate integration into multisubunit complexes like SAGA, where additional motifs (e.g., potential PCI-like elements in associated subunits) enhance substrate specificity without large appendages. Structural analyses, such as the crystal structure of Gcn5 bound to a histone H3 peptide (PDB: 1QSN), reveal a deep binding cleft for substrate recognition, underscoring the family's reliance on minimalistic architecture for nuclear histone acetylation.
In contrast, the MYST family possesses a larger catalytic core of about 250 residues, incorporating a distinctive C2HC-type zinc finger motif integral to the MYST domain, which coordinates zinc ions and aids in nucleosome targeting. Many MYST acetyltransferases, including MOF and Tip60, further include chromodomains that bind methylated histones, promoting chromatin association and specificity for sites like H4K16. The crystal structure of Esa1 (a yeast MYST homolog) with acetyl-CoA (PDB: 1FY7) highlights how the zinc finger positions the substrate for efficient acetylation, distinguishing MYST enzymes in DNA repair and dosage compensation pathways.
The p300/CBP family stands out with an expansive catalytic core spanning roughly 500 residues, augmented by multiple reader and interaction domains that enable multifaceted regulation. Key appendages include the bromodomain, which recognizes acetyl-lysine residues on histones to reinforce acetylation patterns; the ZZ-type zinc finger, which modulates substrate selectivity for histone H3; and the TAZ domain (also known as TAZ2), which facilitates binding to transcription factors and other proteins. Structural determination of the p300 HAT domain with acetyl-CoA (PDB: 3BIY) illustrates an electronegative groove flanked by these domains, allowing promiscuous acetylation of both histones and non-histone targets.
Rtt109, a fungal histone acetyltransferase structurally homologous to p300/CBP despite low sequence similarity, features a conserved core with unique adaptations for chaperone dependence, lacking the extensive appendages of metazoan counterparts. Its activity critically requires the histone chaperone Vps75 to present substrates like H3K56. The crystal structure of Rtt109 in complex with acetyl-CoA and Vps75 (PDB: 3Q35) demonstrates an asymmetric assembly where Vps75's dimerization stabilizes the catalytic site, enabling replication-coupled acetylation distinct from other families.

Catalytic mechanisms

GNAT family mechanism

The GNAT (GCN5-related N-acetyltransferase) family of histone acetyltransferases, including prototypical members Gcn5 and PCAF, employs an ordered sequential bi-bi catalytic mechanism to transfer the acetyl group from acetyl-CoA to the -amino group of target lysine residues on histone substrates. In this mechanism, acetyl-CoA binds first to the enzyme, inducing a conformational change that creates the binding site for the histone peptide, thereby forming a ternary complex comprising the enzyme, acetyl-CoA, and the histone substrate. This ordered binding ensures efficient catalysis, with the histone substrate associating only after acetyl-CoA occupancy, as confirmed by steady-state kinetic analyses showing intersecting double-reciprocal plots consistent with ternary complex formation.
Central to the reaction is the deprotonation of the substrate lysine's -amino group by a conserved glutamate residue acting as a general baseGlu173 in yeast Gcn5 (yGcn5) or the equivalent Glu122 in Tetrahymena Gcn5 (tGcn5)which enhances the nucleophilicity of the lysine for subsequent attack on the carbonyl carbon of acetyl-CoA. The deprotonated lysine then performs a direct nucleophilic attack, generating a tetrahedral oxyanion intermediate that is stabilized by an oxyanion hole formed by backbone amides in the enzyme's 4 strand, facilitating the collapse of the intermediate and transfer of the acetyl group to the lysine. Following acetylation, the modified histone is released first, with coenzyme A (CoA) departing last, completing the ordered product release phase of the bi-bi mechanism. Mutational studies, such as E173Q in yGcn5, demonstrate a >300-fold reduction in k_cat, underscoring the glutamate's essential role in proton abstraction.
Kinetic characterization reveals a high affinity for acetyl-CoA, with a K_m of approximately 2.5 M for yGcn5 using an H3 peptide substrate, reflecting efficient binding under physiological conditions where acetyl-CoA concentrations range from 1-10 M in cells. In vivo, GNAT family enzymes preferentially acetylate lysine 14 on histone H3 (H3K14), a modification critical for transcriptional activation, though broader specificity emerges in multi-subunit complexes. Structural evidence from high-resolution crystal structures of ternary complexes, such as tGcn5 bound to CoA and an H3 peptide (PDB: 1QSN), illustrates the precise positioning of the lysine side chain near the acetyl-CoA sulfhydryl thioester, with the glutamate poised for deprotonation and the oxyanion hole ready to stabilize the transition state, thereby validating the mechanistic model.

MYST family mechanism

The MYST family of histone acetyltransferases catalyzes lysine acetylation through a ping-pong bi-bi mechanism in members such as MOF (also known as KAT8) and Tip60 (KAT5), in which acetyl-CoA binds first to form an acetyl-enzyme intermediate, followed by coenzyme A (CoA) release and subsequent binding of the histone substrate for acetyl group transfer. This ordered process contrasts with the sequential mechanisms of other HAT families and ensures efficient substrate modification, though some homologs like yeast Esa1 may utilize a sequential mechanism. Kinetic analyses reveal that, in the absence of histone substrate, the enzyme sequentially releases CoA followed by acetate after hydrolysis of the intermediate, highlighting the stability of the acetyl-enzyme state.
Central to this mechanism is the formation of the acetyl-enzyme intermediate via autoacetylation at a conserved active site lysine residue, which primes the enzyme for catalysis by facilitating substrate lysine binding and enhancing overall activity. For instance, in human MOF, lysine 274 undergoes cis-autoacetylation, reorienting to position the acetyl group near the catalytic glutamate (Glu350), which acts as a general base to deprotonate the incoming substrate lysine. Similarly, in Tip60, autoacetylation at the equivalent lysine supports the enzyme's role in acetylating histone H4 and non-histone targets like p53. A conserved cysteine residue, such as Cys316 in MOF, may also contribute to transient acetyl transfer in some MYST variants, though the lysine autoacetylation is rate-limiting and essential for turnover.
Structural studies underscore the role of key residues in stabilizing the intermediate and promoting specificity, particularly for histone H4 lysine 16 (H4K16), a preferred target for MOF that drives chromatin decompaction. In yeast Esa1, the conserved glutamate aids in deprotonating the substrate, while zinc ions coordinated by a C2HC zinc finger motif in the MYST domain stabilize the catalytic core and position substrates for high-fidelity acetylation. Crystal structures of the MOF MYST domain bound to acetyl-CoA analogs confirm this architecture, with the zinc finger enhancing intermediate stability without direct involvement in hydrolysis.
Recent evidence from mass spectrometry of MOF in complex with nucleosome arrays demonstrates processive modifications following H4K16 acetylation, enabling acetylation on adjacent nucleosomes. This high specificity for H4K16, with kinetic preference over other lysines, arises from electrostatic and hydrophobic interactions in the active site, ensuring targeted epigenetic regulation.

Substrate recognition and specificity

Histone lysine targets

Histone acetyltransferases (HATs) exhibit site-specific preferences for lysine residues primarily located in the N-terminal tails of core histones H2A, H2B, H3, and H4, with some targeting sites in globular domains. On histone H3, lysines at positions 9, 14, 18, and 27 serve as major acetylation sites; for instance, GCN5/PCAF family members preferentially acetylate H3K9 and H3K14, while p300/CBP targets H3K18 and H3K27. Histone H4 features a conserved acetylation motif encompassing lysines 5, 8, 12, and 16, where HAT1 specifically modifies K5 and K12 on free H4 tails, and MOF (KAT8) acetylates K16 to influence chromatin structure. For H2A and H2B, acetylation occurs at sites such as H2A K5 and H2B K5, K12, K15, and K20, often mediated by GNAT or MYST family HATs in a context-dependent manner.
The specificity of HATs for these lysine residues is governed by multiple factors, including electrostatic interactions between the positively charged lysine -amino group and negatively charged regions in the HAT active site, as well as the local primary sequence surrounding the target lysine. Histone tail accessibility within the nucleosome core particle plays a critical role, as nucleosomal packaging can restrict HAT access to buried lysines, favoring those in more flexible, exposed tails like the N-termini of H3 and H4. Additionally, HATs often function in multi-subunit complexes that enhance selectivity; for example, association with adaptor proteins can redirect enzymes like HBO1 from H3 to H4 tails.
Representative examples highlight these preferences: PCAF efficiently acetylates H3K14 in nucleosomal contexts, promoting localized chromatin opening, whereas MOF's targeting of H4K16 is essential for dosage compensation in flies and mammals. In vivo studies using ChIP-seq have mapped combinatorial acetylation patterns, revealing that p300-mediated H3K27ac is enriched at active enhancers and promoters, often co-occurring with other marks like H3K18ac to define regulatory landscapes. These patterns underscore how HAT specificity contributes to diverse chromatin states without exhaustive modification of all possible sites.

Non-histone protein acetylation

Histone acetyltransferases (HATs) extend their catalytic activity beyond chromatin to acetylate a diverse array of non-histone proteins, thereby modulating cellular processes such as transcription, metabolism, and cytoskeletal dynamics. Prominent substrates include transcription factors like p53, which is acetylated by p300/CBP at lysine residues such as K382, enhancing its sequence-specific DNA binding and transcriptional activation of pro-apoptotic genes. Similarly, the RelA subunit of NF-B undergoes acetylation by PCAF at sites including K310, which promotes its transcriptional activity and nuclear retention while preventing inhibitory interactions with IB. These modifications exemplify how HATs target conserved lysine motifs in non-histone proteins, often analogous to those in histone tails, to alter charge and conformational properties.
Metabolic enzymes represent another critical class of non-histone targets, with ATP-citrate lyase (ACLY) undergoing acetylation by PCAF at lysines 540, 546, and 554, which stabilizes the enzyme by inhibiting ubiquitination and degradation, thereby boosting acetyl-CoA production for lipid biosynthesis. Cytoskeletal proteins, such as -tubulin, are acetylated by the GNAT family member TAT1 at lysine 40, a modification that enhances microtubule stability and facilitates intracellular trafficking without altering polymerization dynamics. Functional consequences of these acetylations frequently involve enhanced protein stability and altered subcellular localization; for instance, p53 acetylation by p300 promotes its nuclear accumulation and recruitment of coactivators to target promoters, amplifying tumor suppressor functions.
Substrate specificity for non-histone proteins is achieved through lysine motifs flanked by contextual residues that engage HAT active sites, supplemented by interactions with multisubunit complexes that tether enzymes to cellular compartments. For example, p300/CBP often operates within large coactivator complexes to selectively acetylate transcription factors in response to signaling cues. In vitro studies using recombinant HATs demonstrate a broad substrate range, with assays showing efficient acetylation of synthetic peptides mimicking p53 or RelA sequences under optimal conditions. However, in vivo acetylation is more restricted, governed by HAT localizationsuch as nuclear confinement of p300 limiting access to cytoplasmic targetsand dynamic complex formation, ensuring physiological relevance over indiscriminate modification.
Recent investigations highlight the role of HATs in immune cell differentiation, where p300-mediated acetylation regulates T-cell subset specification by modifying transcription factors like Foxp3 in regulatory T cells, influencing epigenetic landscapes for sustained effector functions. This functional diversity underscores non-histone acetylation as a versatile regulatory layer, distinct from chromatin remodeling, with implications for cellular adaptation and response to environmental signals.

Regulation of activity

Intrinsic regulatory mechanisms

Histone acetyltransferases (HATs) exhibit intrinsic regulatory mechanisms that fine-tune their catalytic activity through self-modification and conformational dynamics, ensuring precise control over acetylation events in chromatin. One prominent mechanism is autoacetylation, where HATs acetylate their own lysine residues to modulate enzymatic function. In the p300/CBP family, autoacetylation occurs on a lysine-rich regulatory loop, including sites such as Lys1499, which enhances catalytic efficiency by neutralizing inhibitory electrostatic interactions and promoting substrate access to the active site. Similarly, the fungal HAT Rtt109 undergoes autoacetylation at Lys290, which is essential for initiating acetylation of histone H3 at Lys56, as the unmodified enzyme remains catalytically inactive.
Allosteric regulation further contributes to intrinsic control, involving substrate-induced conformational changes that optimize the catalytic core. In the GNAT family, binding of acetyl-CoA or substrates can trigger structural rearrangements that enhance acetyl transfer efficiency without external cofactors. For the MYST family, inhibitory intramolecular interactions between the unacetylated active site lysine (e.g., Lys274 in hMOF) and nearby acidic residues maintain a repressed state; autoacetylation disrupts these contacts, relieving autoinhibition and activating the enzyme.
Post-translational modifications like phosphorylation and ubiquitination provide additional layers of intrinsic regulation by altering HAT conformation or stability.
Biophysical studies reveal that these mechanisms significantly boost enzymatic performance; for example, autoacetylation in Rtt109 increases the maximum velocity (Vmax) of acetylation by 10- to 100-fold through improved substrate binding and reduced Km values, as quantified in kinetic assays of purified enzymes.

Extrinsic factors and complex formation

Histone acetyltransferases (HATs) often function within large multisubunit complexes that enhance their specificity, processivity, and integration into broader chromatin regulatory networks. The SAGA complex, containing the Gcn5 HAT, is a conserved multi-subunit assembly that includes modules for histone acetylation, deubiquitination, and TBP interaction, thereby promoting targeted acetylation at gene promoters and enhancers to facilitate transcription initiation. Similarly, the NuA4 complex, centered on the Tip60 (KAT5) HAT, acetylates histone H4 and H2A variants, contributing to DNA repair and chromatin remodeling through cooperative subunit interactions that stabilize the catalytic core and direct substrate access. The TFTC/STAGA complex, which incorporates PCAF (KAT2B), shares subunits with SAGA and exhibits histone acetyltransferase activity toward H3 and H2B, enabling efficient modification of nucleosomes in a context-specific manner during transcriptional activation.
Beyond these core complexes, HATs interact with diverse binding partners to achieve recruitment and modulation. Transcription factors such as CREB recruit p300 (KAT3B) to enhancer regions via protein-protein interactions, positioning the HAT for localized histone acetylation that supports gene expression in response to signaling cues. In yeast, the histone chaperone Vps75 associates with Rtt109 (KAT11), stimulating its catalytic activity on H3K56 and promoting chromatin assembly during DNA replication by facilitating histone handover.
Cellular conditions, including nutrient availability and cell cycle progression, further regulate HAT complex formation and localization. Fluctuations in acetyl-CoA levels, influenced by nutrient status, directly impact HAT activity as the essential cosubstrate, with elevated acetyl-CoA promoting histone acetylation and growth-related gene expression through complexes like SAGA. Cell cycle phases modulate assembly; for instance, HAT1 integrates into nuclear modules during S phase to acetylate newly synthesized H4, linking replication to chromatin maturation in a nutrient-sensitive manner.
Recent studies have revealed that HAT-containing complexes participate in biomolecular phase separation to enable localized acetylation. In 2023, investigations showed that the SGF29 subunit of the SAGA complex forms nuclear condensates that reinforce age-related gene regulation by concentrating HAT activity within phase-separated domains, highlighting a mechanism for spatial control of epigenetic modifications.

Interactions with histone deacetylases

Dynamic acetylation-deacetylation balance

Histone acetyltransferases (HATs) and histone deacetylases (HDACs) exhibit opposing enzymatic activities that dynamically regulate chromatin structure and gene expression. HATs catalyze the addition of acetyl groups to lysine residues on histone tails, neutralizing their positive charge and promoting an open chromatin conformation that facilitates access by transcriptional machinery. In contrast, HDACs remove these acetyl groups, restoring the positive charge on lysines and leading to chromatin condensation that represses transcription. This antagonism ensures precise control over epigenetic marks, with HATs such as p300/CBP driving activation and HDACs such as HDAC1 mediating repression.
The acetylation-deacetylation cycle operates with rapid temporal dynamics, particularly at gene promoters during transcriptional activation. Acetylation turnover at active sites, including those on H3K9, H3K14, and H3K18, exhibits half-lives ranging from minutes to hours, enabling quick responses to cellular signals. For instance, fast-turnover pools of acetylated histones at promoters show half-lives of 0.82.3 hours, aligning with transcription bursts where HAT activity surges to acetylate histones at sites on H3K4me3-marked nucleosomes within 1030 minutes, followed by HDAC-mediated deacetylation to reset the state. This high turnover rate, observed across eukaryotes, supports oscillatory gene expression patterns essential for processes like immediate-early gene induction.
At the global level, the acetylation-deacetylation balance is influenced by metabolic cues such as the Acetyl-CoA to acetate ratio, which modulates the availability of substrates for HATs and the overall acetylome homeostasis. Disruptions in this equilibrium, such as HDAC inhibition leading to hyperacetylation or reduced HAT activity causing hypoacetylation, alter chromatin accessibility and can precipitate cellular dysfunction. These shifts highlight the system's sensitivity to environmental and metabolic inputs, maintaining net acetylation levels critical for cellular adaptation.
The co-evolution of HATs and HDACs underscores their integral role in fine-tuned epigenetic regulation, with conserved mechanisms evident from yeast to mammals. Type A HATs, which target nuclear histones, have evolved alongside Class I HDACs (e.g., HDAC13), forming an antagonistic network that balances acetylation for transcriptional control across species. This pairing, preserved through phylogenetic divergence, enables precise epigenomic responses to developmental and stress signals.

Formation of co-regulatory complexes

Histone acetyltransferases (HATs) and histone deacetylases (HDACs) often participate in multi-protein co-regulatory complexes that facilitate switchable control of gene expression by enabling the coexistence or alternation of acetylation and deacetylation activities at specific genomic loci. One prominent example is the Sin3/HDAC complex, which is recruited to repressed genes through interactions with transcription repressors such as Ume6 in yeast, where it promotes histone deacetylation to maintain chromatin condensation and transcriptional silencing. This complex anchors HDACs like Rpd3 to target promoters, counteracting HAT-mediated activation and ensuring stable repression, as demonstrated in studies of gene-specific recruitment mechanisms.
In mammalian systems, the Mediator complex integrates HATs such as p300 with HDACs at poised enhancers, allowing these regulatory elements to remain in a transcriptionally competent but inactive state until stimulated. Poised enhancers marked by H3K4me1 are co-occupied by HATs and HDACs, where p300 catalyzes acetylation to prime chromatin accessibility, while HDAC3 maintains hypoacetylation to prevent premature activation, as observed in hormone-responsive gene regulation. This balanced occupancy within the Mediator scaffold enables rapid transitions between poised and active states upon signaling cues.
Sequential actions of HATs and HDACs within co-regulatory assemblies are exemplified in the circadian rhythm machinery, where the CLOCK/BMAL1 heterodimer recruits HAT activityintrinsic to CLOCK and enhanced by p300to acetylate histones at clock-controlled genes for rhythmic activation, followed by HDACs like SIRT1 for deacetylation and repression to sustain oscillatory expression. This temporal alternation, driven by the PER2-mediated recruitment of SIRT1 to deacetylate BMAL1 and histones, ensures precise 24-hour cycles in transcription.
Scaffold proteins such as cohesin and CTCF further enable co-regulatory complexes by mediating chromatin looping that juxtaposes HATs and HDACs at the same loci, facilitating their coordinated action on distant enhancers and promoters. CTCF-bound loops stabilized by cohesin bring acetylated regions into proximity with deacetylation machinery, dynamically rewiring interactions to modulate gene activity during development or stress responses.
Recent structural studies have illuminated the architecture of HDAC-containing Rpd3 complexes in yeast. The 2023 cryo-EM structure of the Rpd3L complex reveals its organization, with scaffolding subunits like Sin3 positioning HDACs for nucleosome deacetylation. The 2024 cryo-EM structure of the Rpd3S complex bound to nucleosomes further highlights its assembly for deacetylation activity. SAGA and Rpd3 complexes dynamically regulate gene expression, such as in heat shock response, to balance acetylation states.

Biological functions

Chromatin remodeling and accessibility

Histone acetyltransferases (HATs) catalyze the addition of acetyl groups to lysine residues on histone tails, primarily neutralizing their positive charge and thereby weakening the electrostatic interactions between histones and negatively charged DNA. This charge neutralization disrupts the compact nucleosome structure, increasing nucleosome mobility and facilitating the unwinding of higher-order chromatin fibers. As a result, chromatin adopts a more open conformation, enhancing overall accessibility to DNA-binding proteins.
The acetylation-mediated loosening of chromatin structure promotes the recruitment and binding of ATP-dependent chromatin remodelers, such as the SWI/SNF complex, which actively reposition nucleosomes to expose DNA sequences. HAT complexes, including SAGA and NuA4, stabilize SWI/SNF association with promoter nucleosomes through acetyl-CoA-dependent histone modifications, ensuring sustained remodeling activity even after initial activator dissociation. Additionally, acetylated lysines serve as docking sites for reader proteins containing bromodomains, like those in BRD2 or Swi2/Snf2, which further propagate open chromatin states by recruiting additional regulatory factors.
Specific acetylation sites play distinct roles in modulating chromatin accessibility. For instance, acetylation at H4 K16 (H4K16ac) acts as a key regulator by weakening inter-nucleosome interactions, particularly the binding of the H4 tail to the acidic patch on adjacent nucleosomes, thereby creating barrier-free chromatin domains and increasing the diversity of nucleosome conformations. Similarly, H3 K56 acetylation (H3K56ac), introduced by HATs like Rtt109 on newly synthesized H3 during DNA replication, enhances nucleosome sliding by remodelers such as ISW1 and Chd1, resolving closely packed nucleosomes in nascent chromatin and aiding replication fork progression.
Experimental evidence from recent studies underscores these mechanisms. Micrococcal nuclease sequencing (MNase-seq) analyses in yeast mutants defective in H3K56ac deacetylation reveal disrupted nucleosome spacing and increased di-nucleosome formation post-replication, with H3K56ac promoting a 2- to 3-fold faster sliding rate by ISW1 remodelers to restore even nucleosome arrays. Furthermore, activation of HATs correlates with heightened DNase hypersensitivity, indicating broader chromatin opening, as observed in genome-wide profiling of acetylated regions.

Transcriptional regulation

Histone acetyltransferases (HATs) facilitate transcriptional activation by acetylating lysine residues on histone tails, creating binding sites for bromodomain proteins that recruit and stabilize the transcriptional machinery. Specifically, acetylated lysines, such as H3K27ac and H4K16ac, interact with the bromodomains of BRD4, which anchors the pre-initiation complex (PIC) at promoters and promotes RNA polymerase II (Pol II) recruitment and release from promoter-proximal pausing. This mechanism enhances the efficiency of transcription initiation, as BRD4 bridges acetylated chromatin with components like Mediator and TFIID to drive gene expression.
At enhancers, HATs such as p300/CBP deposit H3K27ac marks that signal active regulatory elements, enabling long-range interactions with promoters to control developmental gene programs. These marks are enriched at super-enhancers, clusters of enhancers that amplify expression of key lineage-specifying genes, as seen in processes like adipogenesis where p300 recruits BRD4 to sustain enhancer activity. H3K27ac thus predicts enhancer potency and correlates with cell-type-specific transcription during differentiation.
In a contrasting repressive context, certain HATs paradoxically maintain heterochromatin boundaries to prevent ectopic silencing. For instance, the MYST family acetyltransferase Sas2p acetylates H4K16 in euchromatin adjacent to telomeres, counteracting Sir2p-mediated deacetylation and limiting the spread of repressive Sir3p complexes, thereby insulating active genes from heterochromatin invasion. This boundary function ensures compartmentalized chromatin organization essential for proper gene regulation.
Advances in single-cell epigenomics have revealed HATs' dynamic roles in cell fate transitions, particularly during neurogenesis, where CBP orchestrates acetylation-dependent neuronal differentiation and identity maintenance. For example, CBP targets specific histone sites to modulate chromatin accessibility and gene expression in differentiating neurons, influencing transitions from neural progenitors to mature cell types.

Clinical significance

Roles in cancer and epigenetics

Dysregulation of histone acetyltransferases (HATs), particularly through genetic aberrations, plays a pivotal role in oncogenesis by altering epigenetic landscapes that promote tumor progression. In the TCGA Pan-Cancer Atlas, EP300 (encoding p300) mutations occur in approximately 10% of cancer samples across 10,967 cases, with point mutations accounting for 37% of alterations and missense/truncating mutations comprising the majority of somatic changes. These mutations are notably enriched in melanomas (23.2% frequency) and genitourinary cancers, and p300 aberrations in colorectal cancer correlate with advanced histological grade and lymph node involvement, contributing to impaired tumor suppressor function. Similarly, amplification of MYST2 (also known as HBO1 or KAT7) is observed in breast cancers, particularly in HER2-amplified subtypes, where it drives anchorage-independent growth and oncogenic signaling. Such amplifications enhance HBO1's role in histone H4 acetylation, facilitating DNA replication and cell proliferation in tumor cells.
Mechanistically, HAT dysregulation leads to aberrant acetylation patterns that drive oncogene expression or gene silencing, thereby fueling epigenetic reprogramming in cancer. Hyperacetylation mediated by HATs like PCAF promotes the stability and transcriptional activity of the MYC oncoprotein; PCAF acetylates MYC at key lysine residues, preventing its degradation and enhancing its ability to activate downstream target genes involved in proliferation. Conversely, loss-of-function mutations in p300/CBP reduce acetylation of tumor suppressors and histones, resulting in chromatin condensation and silencing of antiproliferative genes, as seen in CBP-deficient hematopoietic malignancies where p300 compensation is critical for survival. This imbalance disrupts the dynamic acetylation-deacetylation equilibrium, linking HAT aberrations directly to oncogenic transcription, a process that extends their established roles in chromatin accessibility.
Therapeutic targeting of HATs has emerged as a promising strategy in epigenetic cancers, with inhibitors designed to restore balanced acetylation. C646, a selective p300 HAT inhibitor, demonstrates preclinical efficacy by suppressing tumor growth in p300-dependent models, such as CBP-mutated lung and hematopoietic cancers, through reduced histone acetylation and oncogene expression. More advanced, CCS1477a p300/CBP bromodomain inhibitorhas entered phase I/IIa clinical trials for advanced solid tumors, including MYC-overexpressing small cell lung cancer, showing preliminary antitumor activity by blocking HAT recruitment to enhancers. As of November 2025, CCS1477 continues in Phase 2 trials for relapsed/refractory multiple myeloma and other hematological malignancies, with recent dose optimization data indicating promising antitumor responses. Derivatives of garcinol, a natural HAT inhibitor targeting p300 and PCAF, have undergone post-2020 optimizations, with studies in 2023 confirming enhanced potency in reducing invasive phenotypes and inducing apoptosis in rhabdomyosarcoma models via inhibited histone acetylation.
Recent advances from 20212024 CRISPR screens have identified HATs as synthetic lethal targets in hematologic malignancies, informing precision therapies. Genome-wide CRISPR-Cas9 screens in diffuse large B-cell lymphoma revealed a synthetic lethal interaction between CREBBP and EP300, where dual inactivation of these paralogous HATs selectively kills tumor cells reliant on compensatory acetylation pathways. This vulnerability extends to leukemia contexts, with similar screens highlighting HAT dependencies in AML models resistant to single HAT inhibition, such as KAT6A, underscoring potential for combination strategies in epigenetic cancers.

Implications in neurological disorders

Mutations in the CREB-binding protein (CBP), a key histone acetyltransferase (HAT), are a primary cause of Rubinstein-Taybi syndrome (RSTS), a neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by intellectual disability, distinctive facial features, and broad thumbs and toes. These mutations often impair CBP's HAT activity, leading to reduced histone acetylation and disrupted chromatin remodeling essential for neuronal differentiation and brain development. In RSTS patients, haploinsufficiency of CBP results in global hypoacetylation of histones, particularly affecting genes involved in synaptic function and cognitive processes.
Dysregulation of p300, another HAT closely related to CBP, contributes to Alzheimer's disease (AD) pathology through hyperacetylation of tau protein. In AD brains, elevated p300 activity promotes aberrant tau acetylation at lysine residues, inhibiting tau degradation and enhancing its aggregation into neurofibrillary tangles, which impair neuronal function and synaptic integrity. This hyperacetylation disrupts microtubule stability and exacerbates neurodegeneration in regions like the hippocampus and cortex.
HATs play critical roles in memory consolidation and synaptic plasticity, where reduced histone acetylation, such as in CBP-deficient models, leads to impaired long-term memory formation. For instance, CBP haploinsufficient mice exhibit decreased histone H3 and H4 acetylation in the hippocampus, resulting in deficits in contextual fear memory consolidation due to altered gene expression for plasticity-related proteins. Similarly, the HAT Gcn5 (KAT2A) is essential for long-term potentiation (LTP) at hippocampal synapses; its knockout in mice causes reduced H3 acetylation, impaired LTP induction, and long-term memory deficits by disrupting neuroactive ligand-receptor pathways.
In Huntington's disease (HD), inhibition of HAT activity exacerbates disease progression by further disrupting the acetylation balance already compromised by mutant huntingtin. Models with CBP or HAT deficiencies show worsened transcriptional dysregulation, increased neuronal toxicity, and accelerated striatal degeneration, highlighting the protective role of HATs against polyglutamine toxicity. Conversely, HAT activators offer therapeutic promise; for example, pharmacological activation of CBP/p300 in tauopathy mouse models restores histone acetylation, reinstates synaptic plasticity, and rescues memory impairments in preclinical dementia studies.
Recent studies from 2022 to 2025 have elucidated HAT involvement in trauma-induced epigenetic changes, particularly through p300-mediated acetylation in glial cells. In models of central nervous system injury, p300-dependent histone acetylation in astrocytes establishes an epigenetic memory that promotes persistent pro-inflammatory responses, potentially analogous to mechanisms in post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) where trauma alters fear-related gene expression. These findings suggest HAT modulation could mitigate long-term epigenetic sequelae of trauma in neurological contexts.
