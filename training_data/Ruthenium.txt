Ruthenium

 Ruthenium is a chemical element with the symbol Ru and atomic number 44. It is a rare transition metal belonging to the platinum group of the periodic table. Like the other metals of the platinum group, ruthenium is inert to most other chemicals.
Ruthenium is a hard, brittle, silvery-white metal with a bluish tinge. It has a Mohs hardness of about 6.2, making it one of the hardest pure metals. Its melting point is 2334 C (4233 F) and boiling point is 4147 C (7505 F). The density is 12.45 g/cm at room temperature.
It was discovered in 1844 by Karl Ernst Claus, a Russian chemist, who named it after Ruthenia, the Latin name for Russia. Ruthenium occurs naturally in minerals with other platinum group metals, primarily in South Africa, Russia, and North America. It is one of the rarest elements in Earth's crust, with an abundance of approximately 0.00005 parts per million (0.05 ppb).
Most ruthenium is used in electrical contacts, resistors, and as a catalyst in chemical reactions such as ammonia synthesis. It is also alloyed with platinum and palladium to improve hardness and is used in jewelry and medical applications.

Properties

Physical properties

Ruthenium is a hard, silvery-white metal characterized by its lustrous appearance and close-packed hexagonal crystal structure, which contributes to its high density and mechanical strength.
The element exhibits a density of 12.45 g/cm at room temperature, reflecting its compact atomic packing. Its melting point is 2334 C, and the boiling point is 4150 C, indicating exceptional thermal stability typical of platinum-group metals. Ruthenium shows no allotropic forms under standard conditions, maintaining a stable hexagonal close-packed phase.
The atomic radius of ruthenium is 134 pm, while the ionic radius for the Ru ion is 62 pm, influencing its incorporation into alloys and materials. Mechanically, ruthenium has a Mohs hardness of 6.5 and demonstrates high tensile strength with significant resistance to deformation, making it suitable for hardening applications.
Ruthenium possesses moderate thermal conductivity of 117 W/(mK) and electrical resistivity of 7.1 cm at 20 C, with a specific heat capacity of 24.4 J/(molK). These properties support its use in electrical contacts and thermal management. Vapor pressure data indicate low volatility, with equilibrium pressures remaining negligible below 2000 C.

Chemical properties

Ruthenium occupies group 8 and period 5 in the periodic table as a d-block transition metal, possessing an electron configuration of 4d 5s. This configuration contributes to its versatile chemical behavior, enabling participation in a variety of oxidation states ranging from +2 to +8, with +2, +3, and +4 being the most prevalent; the +8 state is exemplified in the highly volatile ruthenium tetroxide (RuO).
The metal exhibits notable inertness toward water and most acids, including aqua regia, owing to its stable electronic structure, but it readily dissolves in fused alkalis such as sodium hydroxide or potassium hydroxide to yield ruthenates (RuO). This resistance to corrosion underscores its utility in harsh environments, while its ability to form stable coordination complexes arises from the involvement of partially filled d-orbitals in bonding with ligands. Ruthenium typically favors octahedral coordination geometry in its complexes, a preference driven by its d electron count in common low oxidation states, though higher oxidation states like +6 and +8 allow for expansion of the coordination sphere to accommodate additional ligands or stabilize oxo species.
Electrochemical studies reveal a standard reduction potential of +0.25 V for the Ru/Ru couple in aqueous solution, indicating moderate stability of the +3 state relative to +2 under standard conditions. Key reactions highlight its redox versatility: strong oxidants, such as sodium periodate or cerium(IV), convert ruthenium compounds to the +8 state as RuO, a powerful yet hazardous oxidant. Conversely, ruthenium can be reduced to the elemental metal using hydrogen gas at elevated temperatures around 500C.

Certain ruthenium isotopes serve as fission products in nuclear reactors, notably Ru and Ru, which arise from the fission of uranium and contribute to the isotopic composition of spent nuclear fuel. Stable ruthenium isotopes exhibit neutron capture cross-sections that vary by nuclide; for instance, the thermal neutron capture cross-section for Ru is 0.28 barns, influencing their behavior in reactor environments.
Isotopic enrichment of ruthenium, particularly for research applications, can be achieved through methods such as electromagnetic separation, which leverages differences in isotopic mass to isolate specific nuclides like Ru with enrichments exceeding 92%.
The radioactive isotope Ru finds application in medical brachytherapy, where plaques containing this nuclide are used to treat ocular tumors such as uveal melanoma, delivering targeted beta radiation while minimizing exposure to surrounding tissues.

Natural occurrence and abundance

Ruthenium is one of the rarest elements in the Earth's crust, with an estimated abundance of 1 ppb by weight, ranking it among the least common chemical elements. Its concentration in seawater is significantly lower, at approximately 0.0007 ppb by weight, reflecting its low solubility and geochemical affinity for solid phases. In the broader cosmic context, ruthenium exhibits a solar system abundance of about 1.86 atoms per 10^6 silicon atoms, consistent with patterns observed in chondritic meteorites.
The element primarily occurs as a byproduct alongside other platinum group metals (PGMs) in ultramafic igneous rocks, where it is closely associated with nickel-copper sulfide minerals, such as pentlandite, in magmatic sulfide deposits. Key geological deposits include the Bushveld Complex in South Africa, which hosts the world's largest reserves of PGMs; the Norilsk-Talnakh ore district in Russia, a major source of nickel-copper-PGM sulfides; and the Sudbury Igneous Complex in Canada, known for its impact-related sulfide ores. Ruthenium is also recovered from alluvial platinum sands in regions like the Ural Mountains and Colombia, where it accumulates through erosion of primary deposits.
In its natural forms, ruthenium appears as microscopic grains of native metal or as alloys, notably rutheniridosmine ((Ir,Os,Ru)), a ternary alloy found in placer deposits and ophiolite complexes. These occurrences are typically associated with osmium and iridium in refractory alloys resistant to weathering. Minor amounts of ruthenium are present in iron meteorites and chondrites, where it partitions into metal phases during planetary formation. Geochemically, ruthenium behaves as a highly siderophile element, preferentially concentrating in the metallic core during Earth's differentiation, though late-stage accretion and mantle recycling via volcanism have replenished its presence in the crust and mantle.

Production

Sources and mining

Ruthenium is obtained almost exclusively as a byproduct during the mining of platinum group metals (PGMs), where it is co-extracted alongside platinum, palladium, and rhodium from sulfide-rich ores.
South Africa is the dominant producer, accounting for approximately 80% of global ruthenium supply through operations such as Anglo American Platinum in the Bushveld Complex, while Russia contributes around 20% primarily via Norilsk Nickel in the Norilsk-Talnakh district; smaller shares come from Zimbabwe and Canada. Global annual production reached about 35 tonnes in 2023 and approximately 30 tonnes in 2024, though this figure highlights supply chain vulnerabilities due to heavy reliance on just two countries for over 90% of output.
PGM ores containing ruthenium, such as those in the Merensky Reef of South Africa's Bushveld Complex, are mined using a combination of open-pit and underground methods, including conventional breast mining with haulages developed in the footwall. Ruthenium typically yields 10-20% of the total PGMs recovered from these deposits.
The byproduct nature of ruthenium extraction amplifies environmental challenges in PGM mining, including acid mine drainage that releases heavy metals and sulfuric acid into waterways, posing risks to ecosystems and water quality.
World recoverable reserves of ruthenium are estimated at approximately 5,000 tonnes, concentrated mainly in South Africa and Russia. Recycling from spent catalysts supplements primary supply by 10-20%, helping to mitigate shortages amid rising demand.

Extraction and refining

Ruthenium is typically extracted from platinum group metal (PGM) concentrates derived from nickel-copper sulfide ores, where initial processing involves smelting the ore to produce a matte, followed by acid leaching to dissolve and concentrate the PGMs.
Separation of ruthenium from other PGMs relies on its unique chemical behavior in chloride solutions. One common method is fractional precipitation by adding ammonium chloride to the acidic PGM solution, forming the insoluble ammonium hexachlororuthenate, (NH)RuCl, which selectively isolates ruthenium while leaving platinum, palladium, and rhodium in solution. Alternatively, ruthenium can be separated by oxidation to volatile ruthenium tetroxide (RuO) and distillation from the solution, often using sodium bromate or hypochlorite as oxidants, followed by absorption in hydrochloric acid to form ruthenium chloride. Solvent extraction using organic phases, such as tributyl phosphate, further refines the separation by selectively partitioning ruthenium chloro-complexes.
The precipitated or extracted ruthenium compounds are then reduced to metallic form. Ammonium hexachlororuthenate is thermally decomposed at high temperatures to yield ruthenium dioxide (RuO), which is subsequently reduced with hydrogen gas at 8001000C to produce sponge ruthenium metal. From ruthenium trichloride (RuCl), direct hydrogen reduction or thermal decomposition in a hydrogen atmosphere achieves similar results, yielding ruthenium powder. For ultra-high purity (>99.99%), the crude metal undergoes electrolytic refining in a molten salt electrolyte, such as ammonium chloride-based baths, where ruthenium is anodically dissolved and cathodically deposited.
Typical recovery yields for ruthenium in these processes exceed 95%, though challenges arise in separating it from rhodium due to similar chloro-complex stabilities, often requiring multiple precipitation or extraction cycles.
Modern advancements enhance efficiency and sustainability, including ion exchange resins that selectively adsorb ruthenium anions from PGM solutions for cleaner separation, and supercritical fluid extraction using CO with chelating agents to achieve high selectivity under mild conditions.

History

Discovery

Impurities in platinum ores were first noted in the 18th century, particularly in the native platinum deposits known as "platina" from South America and later from the Ural Mountains in Russia. These residues, left after dissolving the ore in aqua regia, were analyzed by early chemists who suspected the presence of additional metals associated with platinum.
In 1827, Swedish chemist Jns Jacob Berzelius and Russian chemist Gottfried Wilhelm Osann examined such residues from Ural platinum ores. Osann announced the detection of three new metalsnaming one ruthenium after Russiabut Berzelius failed to confirm these findings, concluding that no new elements were present. Subsequent analysis revealed Osann's ruthenium sample to be an impure mixture containing traces of other platinum-group metals.
The element was successfully isolated in 1844 by Karl Ernst Klaus, a professor of chemistry at Kazan University in Russia. Working with crude platinum residues from Siberian mines, Klaus employed a meticulous process of repeated precipitation of double salts to separate and purify the metal, starting from about 18 pounds (8 kg) of material to yield just 6 grams (0.21 oz) of pure ruthenium. This labor-intensive method confirmed ruthenium as a distinct element with unique chemical properties.
Klaus named the element ruthenium (from Ruthenia, the Latin term for Russia). Early samples of ruthenium compounds exhibited characteristic pink coloration in their salts, distinguishing them from other platinum-group elements.

Early research and naming

Following its isolation by Karl Karlovich Klaus in 1844 from residues of Russian platinum ores, the new element was named ruthenium, derived from Ruthenia, the Latin term for Russia, to recognize the origin of the samples from the Ural Mountains.
Initial studies emphasized the synthesis and characterization of ruthenium compounds. In the 1850s, Klaus prepared ruthenium(III) chloride (RuCl) by reacting the metal with chlorine, marking one of the earliest deliberate syntheses of a ruthenium compound, while ruthenium dioxide (RuO) was identified as a black powder resulting from the oxidation of the element in air.
Significant progress came in the mid-19th century when chemists like Ernst Lenssen contributed to determining atomic weights of platinum-group metals, placing ruthenium around 104 relative to other transition metals like osmium and rhodium. Ruthenium tetroxide (RuO), first prepared in the mid-19th century, was noted for its high volatility with a melting point near 25C and ability to sublime readily, which underscored ruthenium's potential for forming unstable higher oxides.
Early investigations encountered difficulties from the close chemical and physical similarities between ruthenium and osmium, often leading to impure samples and misidentification in platinum residues; Klaus resolved much of this confusion through meticulous fractional precipitation and dissolution techniques. By the late 19th century, spectroscopy provided further distinction by revealing unique emission lines for each element in flame tests.
These efforts were intertwined with the burgeoning Russian platinum industry, where Ural Mountain deposits fueled both economic expansionproducing over 450 tonnes of platinum between 1824 and 1900and scientific inquiry into associated minor metals like ruthenium.

Compounds

Oxides and chalcogenides

Ruthenium dioxide (RuO) is the most stable and commonly studied oxide of ruthenium, adopting a tetragonal rutile-type structure with space group P4/mnm, where each Ru ion is octahedrally coordinated to six O ions. It exhibits metallic conductivity due to partially filled d-bands, making it suitable for applications as a resistor material in thick-film resistors, and possesses high thermal stability. RuO can be synthesized by thermal decomposition or calcination of ruthenium(III) chloride (RuCl) at elevated temperatures, often yielding nanocrystalline forms with particle sizes around 50 nm.
Ruthenium tetroxide (RuO), in contrast, is a volatile, yellow, tetrahedral molecular compound that exists as a liquid at room temperature and serves as a potent oxidizing agent capable of cleaving carbon-carbon bonds in hydrocarbons. It is typically prepared by oxidation of ruthenium salts, such as with periodate or chlorine on alkaline ruthenate, though handling requires caution due to its toxicity and explosiveness above 100C. Thermal decomposition of RuO proceeds via the reaction 2RuO 2RuO + O, regenerating the dioxide and releasing oxygen gas.
Lower ruthenium oxides, such as ruthenium monoxide (RuO) and ruthenium sesquioxide (RuO), are less stable and tend to disproportionate or oxidize to higher oxides under ambient conditions. RuO, for instance, is unstable and difficult to isolate in pure form, while RuO can appear in thin films as a corundum-like structure before converting to rutile RuO upon heating. Mixed-valence ruthenium oxides, featuring intermediate oxidation states between +3 and +8, are prevalent in non-stoichiometric forms and contribute to unique electronic properties, such as enhanced electrocatalytic activity in oxygen evolution reactions.
Ruthenium chalcogenides, including sulfides, selenides, and tellurides, form compounds with diverse structures and semiconducting properties. Ruthenium disulfide (RuS) crystallizes in the cubic pyrite structure (space group Pa-3), where Ru is octahedrally coordinated to six S ions, and acts as a semiconductor with an indirect band gap of approximately 1.3 eV. Ruthenium diselenide (RuSe) adopts a pyrite-type structure and exhibits semiconducting behavior with catalytic potential for hydrogen evolution, while ruthenium ditelluride (RuTe) occurs in both pyrite and marcasite polymorphs, both displaying layered architectures conducive to electrocatalytic applications such as oxygen evolution in acidic media. These chalcogenides generally feature band gaps in the range of 1-2 eV, enabling their use in energy conversion devices.

Halides and oxyhalides

Ruthenium halides encompass compounds in various oxidation states, primarily +2 to +6, with chloride, bromide, fluoride, and iodide derivatives exhibiting distinct synthetic routes and properties. The most studied is ruthenium(III) chloride, RuCl, which exists in hydrated and anhydrous forms. The hydrated form, RuClxHO (where x 3), appears as a brownish solid and is commonly prepared by dissolving metallic ruthenium in hydrochloric acid or aqua regia, followed by evaporation. The anhydrous RuCl adopts a polymeric structure with octahedral ruthenium centers bridged by chloride ligands, forming layered -RuCl (black crystals) or -RuCl (dark-brown powder); the -form is insoluble in water and alcohol, while the -form shows solubility in these solvents. Anhydrous RuCl can be synthesized by direct halogenation of ruthenium metal with chlorine gas at elevated temperatures (500700 C): Ru + 3Cl RuCl.
Other ruthenium halides include ruthenium tribromide (RuBr) and ruthenium diiodide (RuI), prepared similarly via high-temperature reactions of ruthenium with bromine or iodine vapors. RuBr features a layered structure analogous to -RuCl, contributing to its stability and potential magnetic properties. RuI represents a lower oxidation state (+2) and is less stable, often requiring inert conditions for isolation. In the +6 oxidation state, ruthenium hexafluoride (RuF) is a notable volatile compound, appearing as dark brown crystals with a melting point of 54 C and significant vapor pressure, enabling its use in gas-phase applications; it is synthesized by fluorination of ruthenium or lower fluorides at high temperatures.
Ruthenium halides generally exhibit hydrolysis tendencies in aqueous media, where chloride or other ligands are substituted by water molecules to form aquo complexes, such as from RuCl, facilitating further reactivity in solution. Fluorides like RuF are particularly volatile and hydrolyze violently, releasing HF. Oxyhalides, combining oxygen and halogen ligands, include the ruthenium(IV) complex , a chloro-oxo species stable in concentrated HCl solutions and prepared by oxidation of Ru(III) in chloride media. Ruthenium tetroxide (RuO), while primarily an oxide, displays oxyhalide-like behavior in halogenated environments due to its high reactivity and tendency to form mixed species under oxidative conditions. These compounds' synthesis often involves direct halogenation or dissolution in halogen acids, with properties influenced by the halide's electronegativity affecting bond strength and volatility.

Coordination and organometallic complexes

Ruthenium displays versatile coordination chemistry, predominantly in the +2 and +3 oxidation states, where it forms low-spin octahedral complexes with a d electron configuration. Common ligands include cyanide (CN), ammonia (NH), and phosphines, which stabilize these states through -donation and -acceptance. For instance, the hexaammine complex exemplifies a simple octahedral Ru(III) species, prepared via oxidation of the Ru(II) analog. Similarly, the hexacyanoruthenate(II) ion features strong-field cyanide ligands that promote back-donation from the metal to the ligand * orbitals, resulting in a stable low-spin configuration. A representative phosphine complex is RuCl(PPh), which serves as a versatile precursor for further substitutions due to the lability of chloride ligands.
In organometallic chemistry, ruthenium forms notable cluster compounds and -bonded derivatives. The trinuclear cluster Ru(CO) consists of a triangular Ru core with terminal carbonyl ligands and Ru-Ru bonds, illustrating metal-metal bonding to achieve electron satisfaction. This compound adheres to the 18-electron rule, with each ruthenium atom attaining an 18-electron count through contributions from metal-metal bonds and CO back-bonding, where the metal d-orbitals donate electron density to the ligand's antibonding orbitals. Another class includes half-sandwich arene complexes, such as the dimeric , which adopts a piano-stool geometry upon dissociation, featuring -coordination of the arene ligand to provide six electrons to the metal center.
Prominent ruthenium complexes with catalytic relevance include Grubbs' catalysts for olefin metathesis. The first-generation Grubbs catalyst, (PCy)(Cl)Ru=CHPh, incorporates a ruthenium-alkylidene unit stabilized by phosphine and chloride ligands, enabling carbene-mediated transformations with high functional group tolerance. Second-generation variants replace one phosphine with an N-heterocyclic carbene (NHC) ligand, enhancing stability and activity through stronger -donation from the NHC. The Shvo catalyst, a diruthenium hydride complex , facilitates transfer hydrogenation of carbonyls using isopropanol as a hydrogen donor, operating via reversible dehydrogenation of the ligand to generate active monomeric species.
Many ruthenium complexes are synthesized from ruthenium(III) chloride (RuCl) hydrate through ligand substitution under reducing or solvothermal conditions. For example, treatment of RuCl with excess phosphines or ammonia in the presence of reductants yields the corresponding substituted products, often proceeding stepwise due to the lability of aquo or chloro ligands. These air-stable complexes, such as arene derivatives, are typically isolated as dimers or monomers depending on the ligand steric demands.

Applications

Catalysis

Ruthenium-based catalysts serve as effective alternatives to traditional iron catalysts in ammonia synthesis, particularly in the Kellogg Advanced Ammonia Process (KAAP). These catalysts, often consisting of ruthenium supported on activated carbon with promoters such as potassium, barium, and cesium, enable higher ammonia yields at lower operating pressures (around 100 bar) compared to the conventional Haber-Bosch process, which requires pressures exceeding 200 bar. The enhanced activity stems from ruthenium's ability to facilitate nitrogen dissociation more readily under milder conditions, reducing energy consumption and improving process efficiency.
In hydrogenation reactions, ruthenium complexes like RuCl(PPh) act as analogs to Wilkinson's catalyst (RhCl(PPh)), enabling the selective reduction of alkenes to alkanes under mild conditions, often with base additives to accelerate the reaction rate. This complex operates via a mechanism involving oxidative addition of hydrogen and subsequent migratory insertion, achieving efficient turnover for unfunctionalized alkenes. Enantioselective variants, incorporating chiral phosphine ligands, have been developed to produce chiral products from prochiral alkenes, with high enantiomeric excesses reported in asymmetric hydrogenation processes.
Ruthenium carbene complexes, notably the Grubbs and Hoveyda-Grubbs catalysts, are cornerstone reagents in olefin metathesis, facilitating carbon-carbon bond rearrangements with high precision. The Grubbs first-generation catalyst features a benzylidene-ruthenium moiety coordinated to tricyclohexylphosphine ligands, while the second-generation incorporates an N-heterocyclic carbene for improved stability and activity; the Hoveyda-Grubbs variant uses a chelating isopropoxybenzylidene ligand for easier handling and recyclability. A prominent application is ring-closing metathesis (RCM), which forms cyclic alkenes from dienes by expelling ethylene, as illustrated by the general equation:

Additional catalytic roles of ruthenium include the dehydrogenation of alcohols using the Shvo catalyst, a dimeric ruthenium hydride complex that dissociates into active 16- and 18-electron species to transfer hydrogen from secondary alcohols to carbonyls without external acceptors, proceeding via a bifunctional mechanism involving metal-ligand cooperation. In Fischer-Tropsch synthesis, ruthenium acts as a promoter for cobalt-based catalysts, enhancing reducibility, dispersion, and selectivity toward C hydrocarbons while suppressing methane formation, leading to improved chain growth probabilities. Bimetallic Ru-Pt systems further exemplify ruthenium's versatility, exhibiting high turnover frequencies (up to 400 times that of pure Pt at low temperatures) in CO oxidation due to synergistic electronic effects that facilitate CO desorption and oxygen activation. Electronics

Ruthenium's exceptional electrical conductivity, combined with its resistance to corrosion and oxidation, positions it as a vital material in electronic components where reliability under demanding conditions is essential. These properties enable ruthenium to enhance performance in devices requiring stable electrical contacts and thin-film structures, contributing to the advancement of data storage and semiconductor technologies.
In hard disk drives (HDDs), ruthenium thin films are incorporated into granular media for perpendicular magnetic recording, serving as a segregation layer that isolates magnetic grains and improves the signal-to-noise ratio by reducing intergranular exchange coupling. This application has been instrumental in achieving higher areal densities, with ruthenium enabling bit densities exceeding 1 Tb/in in modern drives.
Ruthenium alloys, particularly those with platinum, are widely used for electrical contacts in relays and fuses due to their superior corrosion resistance and wear durability in high-current environments. These alloys maintain low contact resistance even after prolonged use, making them ideal for telecommunications and automotive electronics where arcing and oxidation could otherwise degrade performance.
Ruthenium dioxide (RuO)-based thick-film pastes form the basis of precision resistors, providing a temperature coefficient of resistance around 100 ppm/C, which ensures minimal variation in resistance with temperature changes for applications in hybrid circuits and sensors. In semiconductor devices, ruthenium electrodes in dynamic random-access memory (DRAM) capacitors offer high work function and thermal stability, supporting scaled-down structures with capacitances above 30 fF/cell. Additionally, ruthenium acts as an effective diffusion barrier in copper interconnects, preventing copper migration into dielectrics and maintaining interconnect integrity at feature sizes below 10 nm.
As of 2023, electronics accounted for approximately 10% of global ruthenium production, driven by demand in data storage and microelectronics.

Other applications

Ruthenium serves as a hardening agent in platinum alloys for jewelry, where small concentrations, typically 5%, enhance durability and resistance to wear. These alloys are also employed in luxury items such as fountain pen nibs, providing corrosion resistance and longevity.
In medicine, ruthenium red acts as a polycationic stain for visualizing acid polysaccharides on cell surfaces in electron microscopy. The ruthenium(III) complex NAMI-A (trans-), an antimetastatic agent, has completed Phase I clinical trials and demonstrated selective activity against metastases in preclinical models. Additionally, ruthenium-106 (Ru) plaques are used in beta brachytherapy for treating uveal melanomas, achieving high local tumor control rates with low toxicity for tumors up to 6 mm in thickness.
Emerging applications include ruthenium dyes in dye-sensitized solar cells, such as the N719 complex, which enables power conversion efficiencies exceeding 11%. Ruthenium-based nanomaterials, like nanoplate ruthenium-nickel oxide supported on nickel foam, have shown promise in electrocatalysis for the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER), particularly for hydrogen production from alkaline seawater. In organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs), ruthenium(II) complexes serve as phosphorescent dopants, contributing to efficient luminescence and device stability. Ruthenium plating is applied to jewelry for decorative and protective finishes, leveraging its tarnish resistance as a precious metal surface treatment. Recent trends highlight increased interest in ruthenium for green hydrogen production, with ongoing developments in electrocatalytic nanomaterials addressing sustainability challenges.

Health and safety

Biological role

Ruthenium has no known biological role in humans, animals, plants, or microorganisms and is not classified as an essential trace element. Unlike essential metals such as iron or zinc, which are required for enzymatic functions and metabolic processes, ruthenium does not participate in any vital biochemical pathways across known organisms.
Bioaccumulation of ruthenium in biological systems is generally low, with limited uptake from environmental sources such as soil and water. Studies indicate that ruthenium exhibits low bioavailability and accumulation potential in exposed organisms, primarily due to its geochemical scarcity and poor solubility in biological media. In some bacteria, ruthenium can bind to proteins through coordination with sulfur-containing residues, such as cysteine thiols, though this interaction is not indicative of a functional role but rather incidental exposure.
Microbial interactions with ruthenium are primarily disruptive rather than symbiotic. Ruthenium complexes can inhibit key bacterial enzymes, including analogs of nitrogenase involved in nitrogen fixation, by mimicking or blocking active sites and thereby impairing microbial metabolism. Additionally, certain bacteria, such as those in the genus Corynebacterium, demonstrate potential for biosorption of ruthenium from industrial effluents, suggesting a role in bioremediation by accumulating and recovering the metal from contaminated environments without broader ecological integration.
Dietary intake of ruthenium in humans is negligible, primarily from trace environmental contamination in food and water, with no associated deficiency syndromes reported due to its non-essential status. This low exposure reflects ruthenium's rarity in the biosphere, stemming from its scarcity in the Earth's crust at approximately 0.001 ppm, which limits its natural incorporation into food chains and evolutionary adaptation by organisms.

Toxicity and precautions

Ruthenium metal exhibits low acute toxicity, with an oral LD50 greater than 2000 mg/kg in rats, indicating it is relatively inert under normal conditions. In contrast, ruthenium tetroxide (RuO), a volatile and reactive compound, is highly toxic by inhalation and can cause severe irritation to the respiratory tract, leading to pulmonary edema upon exposure.
Primary exposure routes for ruthenium include inhalation of dusts or fumes during processing, which may irritate the respiratory system, and skin contact with soluble ruthenium salts, potentially causing dermatitis or corrosion.
Chronic exposure to ruthenium compounds may lead to bioaccumulation in organs such as the liver, spleen, kidneys, and bones, with potential for oxidative stress and inflammation, particularly from nanoparticle forms. Ruthenium is not classified as a carcinogen by the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC).
Occupational exposure limits for ruthenium are not specifically established by OSHA, but it is treated as an inert or nuisance dust with a permissible exposure limit (PEL) of 5 mg/m for total dust. Under EU REACH, certain ruthenium compounds, such as chlorides, are classified as skin irritants but not as sensitizers based on available data.
Handling precautions include using fume hoods and personal protective equipment (PPE) such as gloves, goggles, and respirators, especially for volatile compounds like RuO; spills of RuO should be neutralized with sodium bisulfite solutions. Workers in platinum group metal (PGM) refining or processing should undergo regular medical monitoring for respiratory and dermal effects.
Recent studies from 20232024 on ruthenium nanoparticles highlight their potential to induce oxidative stress in liver and spleen tissues upon accumulation, underscoring the need for caution in nanomaterial applications.
