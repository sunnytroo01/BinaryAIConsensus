 Colin Trevorrow (born September 13, 1976) is an American film director, screenwriter, and producer renowned for helming the science fiction comedy Safety Not Guaranteed (2012) and blockbuster entries in the Jurassic World franchise, including Jurassic World (2015), which he co-wrote and directed, and Jurassic World Dominion (2022). His work on Jurassic World marked a rapid ascent from independent cinema to major studio successes, with the 2015 film becoming one of the highest-grossing movies of all time at $1.671 billion worldwide. Trevorrow also co-wrote and executive produced Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom (2018), and he was originally announced as director for Star Wars: Episode IX The Rise of Skywalker in 2015 before departing in 2017 due to creative differences.
Born in San Francisco, California, and raised in Oakland, Trevorrow developed an early interest in filmmaking, creating short films starting at age 12. He studied film at New York University's Tisch School of the Arts, graduating in 1999, where he met future collaborator Derek Connolly. After early efforts in short films and documentaries, including the 2002 comedy Home Base, Trevorrow directed his feature debut Safety Not Guaranteed, which premiered at the Sundance Film Festival to critical praise and received two Independent Spirit Award nominations, winning for Best First Screenplay. Selected by Steven Spielberg to direct Jurassic World, Trevorrow revitalized the Jurassic Park series, blending spectacle with themes of corporate ethics and human hubris.
Following The Book of Henry (2017), a dramatic family thriller that received mixed reviews, Trevorrow focused on producing through his company, Metronome Film Co., founded to nurture emerging talent, including the 2025 crime comedy Deep Cover. As of 2025, he is directing a live-action adaptation of The Jetsons for Warner Bros. with Jim Carrey in talks to star, and an untitled Area 51 conspiracy thriller for Paramount Pictures.

Early life and education

Early life

Colin Trevorrow was born on September 13, 1976, in San Francisco, California. He was raised in Oakland, California, following his family's relocation from the city shortly after his birth.
Trevorrow grew up in a family immersed in the arts, with his father working as a musician in a country rock band and his mother serving as a photographer who also operated a day-care center. This environment provided early exposure to creative pursuits, fostering his interest in artistic expression from a young age. As a boy, Trevorrow sang in the chorus of the San Francisco Opera.
His passion for filmmaking began at age 12, when he started writing and shooting his own short films. During high school, he continued this pursuit, creating additional short films and earning awards at local film festivals while attending Piedmont High School in Piedmont, California.

Education

Trevorrow graduated from Piedmont High School in Piedmont, California, in 1995. During his high school years, he conducted early film experiments by producing short films, which allowed him to explore directing and narrative techniques in a structured educational environment.
In 1995, he enrolled at New York University's Tisch School of the Arts, where he pursued a Bachelor of Fine Arts degree with a focus on film and television production. Trevorrow graduated in 1999, having engaged in key academic experiences such as collaborations with peers including Derek Connolly, a fellow Tisch alumnus from the class of 1998. These interactions, along with involvement in student films and projects, sharpened his skills in directing and screenwriting.
His time at NYU marked a pivotal shift, bridging his amateur high school endeavors to professional filmmaking by providing rigorous training in production techniques and creative collaboration.

Career

Early career

After graduating from New York University, Trevorrow began his professional career in the early 2000s with low-budget short films and documentaries focused on comedic and satirical themes. His debut short, Home Base (2002), a revenge comedy about an ex-boyfriend pranking his former partner's mother, gained significant online traction, amassing over 20 million views by 2012 and establishing his early reputation in independent circles.
Trevorrow expanded into documentaries with Reality Show (2004), which chronicled the chaotic failure of a self-funded reality TV production in Mexico aimed at finding "America's craziest party girl," highlighting the absurdities of aspiring celebrity culture. He co-directed the mockumentary-style TV film Gary: Under Crisis (2005) with Daniel Klein, satirizing 24-hour news coverage through the imagined crisis in a young cartoonist's life. These early works, produced on shoestring budgets, showcased Trevorrow's interest in blending humor with social commentary while navigating the constraints of independent production.
Throughout the late 2000s, Trevorrow contributed to various low-budget projects, submitting works to film festivals such as Sundance to build visibility in the indie scene, though many remained limited in distribution due to funding shortages and lack of major backing. These efforts underscored the hurdles of independent filmmaking, including securing financing through personal networks and grants, as well as challenges in achieving wide theatrical release without studio support.
Trevorrow's breakthrough in features came with Safety Not Guaranteed (2012), his directorial debut, which he developed in collaboration with writer Derek Connolly after Connolly discovered a quirky 1997 classified ad seeking a time-travel companion. Trevorrow directed the sci-fi romantic comedy, which follows magazine journalists investigating the ad's author, blending humor, romance, and subtle emotional depth on a modest $750,000 budget. The film premiered at the 2012 Sundance Film Festival in the U.S. Dramatic Competition, where it received critical acclaim for its witty script and performances, earning Connolly the Waldo Salt Screenwriting Award.
Despite its success at Sundance, where it was acquired by Big Beach Films for distribution, Safety Not Guaranteed faced typical indie challenges, including a tight 24-day shooting schedule and post-production adjustments to align with the limited budget, which initially mismatched Trevorrow's broader visual ambitions. The film ultimately grossed over $4 million worldwide, a strong return that affirmed its commercial viability and critical resonance, with praise for its heartfelt portrayal of regret and connection.

Breakthrough and Jurassic World franchise

Trevorrow's breakthrough came in 2013 when he was selected to direct Jurassic World (2015), the fourth installment in the Jurassic Park franchise, after impressing Universal Pictures executives and producer Steven Spielberg with his debut feature Safety Not Guaranteed (2012), which demonstrated his skill in blending character-driven storytelling with speculative elements. Hired in March 2013 without having read the existing script, Trevorrow found the initial draft lacking focus and depth, prompting him to undertake a major rewrite. He co-wrote the screenplay with Derek Connolly, his collaborator from Safety Not Guaranteed, transforming the story into a tale of corporate overreach in a dinosaur theme park gone awry, emphasizing themes of wonder and peril inherited from the original films.
Released on June 12, 2015, Jurassic World became a massive commercial success, grossing over $1.67 billion worldwide against a $150 million budget, marking the highest-grossing film of 2015 and revitalizing the franchise after a 14-year hiatus. Critically, it received mixed-to-positive reviews, praised for its spectacle and nostalgic nods but critiqued for formulaic plotting, earning a 72% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 357 reviews. The film's achievement elevated Trevorrow from indie filmmaker to blockbuster director, opening doors to major studio projects.
In August 2015, shortly after Jurassic World's release, Lucasfilm announced that Trevorrow would direct Star Wars: Episode IX (later titled The Rise of Skywalker), positioning him to conclude the Skywalker saga with a script he co-wrote alongside Connolly. However, in September 2017, he departed the project due to creative differences with studio executives over the storyline's direction, a split described as mutual but stemming from irreconcilable visions for the narrative. This high-profile exit drew media scrutiny and temporarily stalled Trevorrow's momentum in the franchise space, though it allowed him to refocus on the Jurassic World series, ultimately shaping his career toward more grounded, effects-heavy storytelling rather than expansive sci-fi epics.
Between these milestones, Trevorrow directed The Book of Henry (2017), a dramatic thriller about a precocious 12-year-old boy, Henry (Jaeden Martell), who devises an elaborate plan from his notebook to rescue his neighbor from an abusive stepfather, only for his single mother, Susan (Naomi Watts), to execute it after his death from brain cancer. Production faced challenges in balancing its tonal shifts from family drama to vigilante thriller, with Trevorrow navigating a script that blended genres awkwardly and required sensitive handling of child illness and abuse themes. Released on June 16, 2017, by Focus Features, the film underperformed at the box office, earning just $4.2 million domestically against a $10 million budget, and garnered mixed-to-negative reviews for its uneven pacing and implausible plot twists, holding a 21% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes from 146 critics.
Trevorrow returned to the Jurassic World franchise to direct Jurassic World Dominion (2022), co-writing the screenplay with Emily Carmichael to conclude the trilogy by uniting the original Jurassic Park castAlan Grant (Sam Neill), Ellie Sattler (Laura Dern), and Ian Malcolm (Jeff Goldblum)with the newer leads Chris Pratt and Bryce Dallas Howard in a global story of dinosaur-human coexistence threatened by bioengineered locusts. The film emphasized environmentalism, portraying humanity's hubris in genetic manipulation as a metaphor for real-world ecological crises, with Sattler's arc highlighting corporate exploitation of nature. Released on June 10, 2022, it grossed $1.002 billion worldwide, becoming the 33rd-highest-grossing film ever despite mixed critical reception focused on overcrowded plotting.
Throughout his blockbuster work, Trevorrow's directing style in the Jurassic World films balances high-octane spectaclesuch as dinosaur chases and animatronic integrationswith character development, drawing from Spielberg's influence to ground emotional arcs amid chaos, while incorporating fan service like legacy callbacks to foster nostalgia without overshadowing narrative progression. This approach, evident in his attention to interpersonal dynamics amid visual effects, has defined his transition to tentpole cinema, prioritizing thematic resonance over pure action excess.

Production work and later projects

In 2019, Colin Trevorrow founded Metronome Film Co., a production company based in London and Los Angeles, with the primary purpose of fostering opportunities for emerging filmmakers by developing dynamic stories across film and television. The company aims to replicate the mentorship Trevorrow received from Steven Spielberg on Jurassic World (2015), emphasizing support for up-and-coming talent through grants and project development, including sponsorship of the Scout Film Festival's Emerging Filmmakers Grants. Key partnerships include representation by WME since 2022 and collaborations with studios like Paramount Pictures.
Trevorrow's producing work through Metronome includes executive producing the mockumentary Taste the Revolution (2024), a project originally filmed in 2001 that premiered at the New Orleans Film Festival on October 19, 2024, after receiving approval from star Mahershala Ali. He also served as producer and screenwriter on the action-comedy Deep Cover (2025), directed by Tom Kingsley and starring Bryce Dallas Howard, Orlando Bloom, and Nick Mohammed as improv actors recruited for an undercover police operation in London's underworld. Released on Prime Video in June 2025, the film received strong critical reception, earning a 90% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes and praise for its clever humor and ensemble performances, though it lacked a theatrical box office due to its streaming debut.
Among Trevorrow's upcoming projects are directing a live-action adaptation of The Jetsons (TBA) for Warner Bros., with Jim Carrey in talks to star as of October 2025, and an untitled Area 51 conspiracy thriller (TBA) set in the late 1980s for Paramount Pictures, which he is developing with Ryan Reynolds' Maximum Effort and announced in May 2025.
In 2025 interviews, Trevorrow reflected on his 2017 departure from directing Star Wars: Episode IX, expressing detachment from the franchise after nearly eight years and a desire to re-engage solely as an audience member, while emphasizing lessons from the experience in his producing philosophy. He voiced pride in the Jurassic World trilogy's legacy, particularly its role in launching his career and inspiring Metronome's focus on mentorship amid Hollywood's challenges. Looking ahead, Trevorrow highlighted his commitment to nurturing new voices through Metronome, viewing it as a way to sustain his future in the industry beyond directing blockbusters.

Personal life

Family

Colin Trevorrow is married to Isabelle Trevorrow. The couple has two children, including a daughter. The family maintains a private domestic life, with Trevorrow occasionally sharing how his role as a parent shapes his perspective on filmmaking. For instance, his 2017 film The Book of Henry, which centers on family bonds and a child's ingenuity, drew inspiration from his own experiences as a father navigating modern challenges.
Following the blockbuster success of Jurassic World in 2015, Trevorrow and his family relocated from Burlington, Vermontwhere they had lived since 2008to the United Kingdom in 2017 to be closer to the film industry while prioritizing family stability, and they continue to reside there as of 2025. Trevorrow has described major career decisions, such as directing Jurassic World, as motivated in part by opportunities to provide for his family amid demanding schedules. He emphasizes maintaining work-life equilibrium, noting the emotional toll of high-profile projects but the grounding influence of home life.

Interests and activism

Trevorrow has expressed a deep affinity for science fiction and comedy genres, which shaped his early filmmaking style. His directorial debut, Safety Not Guaranteed (2012), blended sci-fi elements with comedic storytelling, reflecting his passion for these forms as a means to explore human emotions and absurdity. In a 2025 interview, he reiterated his love for comedy, stating, "I love stand-up. I love improv. I love comedy in general," emphasizing its role in his creative influences beyond directing blockbusters.
His interest in music stems from his upbringing, as his father was a musician in the country rock band Hearts on Fire, which opened for artists like Willie Nelson and Waylon Jennings. This familial connection provided early exposure to the performing arts, influencing Trevorrow's appreciation for narrative-driven entertainment. Additionally, Trevorrow has incorporated environmental themes into his work, viewing films as a platform to address ecological concerns. In discussions about the Jurassic World franchise, he highlighted how the series explores "the consequences of our environmental choices," portraying dinosaurs as metaphors for endangered species facing abuse, trafficking, and habitat loss. He has described the trilogy's progression as tackling climate change and animal rights, with Jurassic World: Dominion (2022) emphasizing coexistence between humans and nature amid global anxieties.
On activism, Trevorrow has commented on diversity in Hollywood, particularly gender representation behind the camera. In 2015, he addressed the lack of female directors in blockbusters, noting the industry's challenges but suggesting some top women prioritize artistic control over studio projectsa view that drew criticism for overlooking systemic barriers. He supported feminist critiques within his own films, backing Joss Whedon's callout of sexist marketing for Jurassic World and praising Bryce Dallas Howard's character as the "true star," underscoring his belief in strong female leads. Tied to environmental causes, Trevorrow has advocated through the Jurassic franchise for awareness of planetary stewardship, framing the dinosaurs' plight as a cautionary tale on human impact, including corporate greed and biodiversity loss.
In philanthropy, Trevorrow has supported film education for youth by sponsoring the Scout Film Festival's Emerging Filmmakers Grants since at least 2018. Through a partnership with Visura.co, he funds two $5,000 awards annuallyone for filmmakers 18 and under, and one for ages 19 to 24to aid short films or works in progress, continuing into the 2020s as the program's fourth year in 2022. He has served on judging panels and stated, "Im proud to partner with the Scout Film Festival to support our next generation of storytellers." In 2019, Trevorrow donated all residuals earned from his contributions to Star Wars: Episode IX The Rise of Skywalker to the Alexander Devine Children's Hospice in the United Kingdom. While his production company, Metronome Film Co., founded in 2019, focuses on bold storytelling collaborations, no specific philanthropic initiatives from the company in the 2020s have been publicly detailed.
Trevorrow's public persona includes candid reflections on the personal toll of filmmaking, particularly work-life balance and mental health. In a June 2025 interview, he discussed how "heartbreaking" setbacks, including high-profile project losses, have informed his approach to producing at Metronome Film Co., emphasizing resilience amid industry pressures. He has described directing major films as "scary" and fear-inducing, even with confidence, highlighting the emotional demands of balancing creative risks with professional demands.

As producer

Trevorrow's producing career encompasses a range of feature films and television projects, frequently in collaboration with his production company, Metronome Film Co., established in 2019 to support emerging filmmakers and his own initiatives. His credits highlight involvement in high-profile franchises as well as independent and genre-driven works, often alongside partners like Derek Connolly and Universal Pictures executives.
In 2015, Trevorrow produced Jurassic World, a blockbuster revival of the Jurassic Park series directed by himself, which grossed over $1.6 billion worldwide and marked a significant commercial success for Universal Pictures. He continued his association with the franchise as executive producer on Jurassic World: Fallen Kingdom (2018), directed by J.A. Bayona, where he collaborated with returning cast members like Chris Pratt and Bryce Dallas Howard to expand the dinosaur-themed narrative. This role extended to Jurassic World Dominion (2022), again as executive producer, overseeing the trilogy's conclusion with director himself at the helm and integrating legacy characters from the original films.
Shifting toward non-directorial producing, Trevorrow served as executive producer on the delayed-release mockumentary Taste the Revolution (2024), a political satire originally shot in 2001 and co-written by him during his early career; the project reunited him with early collaborator Mahershala Ali, who starred and co-executive produced the revival. In 2025, he produced the action-comedy Deep Cover through Metronome Film Co., directed by Tom Kingsley and co-written by Trevorrow with Derek Connolly, Ben Ashenden, and Alexander Owen; the film features Bryce Dallas Howard and Orlando Bloom as undercover improv actors infiltrating a criminal syndicate, and was released by Amazon MGM Studios on June 12.
On television, Trevorrow has held executive producer credits since 2020, including the animated series Jurassic World: Camp Cretaceous (20202022), a Netflix spin-off targeting younger audiences with dinosaur adventures, developed in tandem with the live-action films. He extended this role to Jurassic World: Chaos Theory (2024present), another Netflix animated entry exploring post-Dominion storylines with original voice cast.
Among upcoming productions, Trevorrow is attached as producer (and potential director) to The War Magician (TBA), a World War II drama starring Benedict Cumberbatch as illusionist Jasper Maskelyne, developed with StudioCanal and his Metronome banner alongside Cumberbatch's SunnyMarch. He is also producing Atlantis (TBA) at Skydance via Metronome Film Co., which he is set to direct, written by Charmaine DeGrat and centering on a modern thriller inspired by the mythical lost city. Other projects under his producing umbrella include The Ambassador (TBA), a long-gestating sci-fi thriller co-written with Derek Connolly. As of November 2025, these remain in various stages of pre-production.
