Gitanjali
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Gitanjali (Bengali: , lit. 'Song offerings') is a renowned collection of 157 devotional poems and songs composed in Bengali by the Indian poet, philosopher, and polymath Rabindranath Tagore, first published in August 1910. The work draws deeply from Vaishnava and Baul spiritual traditions, expressing themes of divine love, human longing for the infinite, and harmony with nature through intimate, lyrical offerings to the divine. Tagore personally translated a selection of 103 poems from Gitanjali and other collections into English prose poems, published as Gitanjali: Song Offerings in November 1912 by the India Society of London. This English edition propelled Tagore to international acclaim, earning him the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1913the first awarded to a non-European writerfor its "profoundly sensitive, fresh and beautiful verse, by which, with consummate skill, he has made his poetic thought, expressed in his own English words, a part of the literature of the West."
The poems in Gitanjali blend personal devotion with universal spiritual insights, portraying God as an intimate companion and the world as a sacred space for surrender and joy. Influenced by Tagore's own life experiences, including personal losses and his immersion in rural Bengal, the collection reflects a pantheistic worldview where the divine permeates everyday existence, from birdsong to human suffering. Many of the Bengali originals have been set to music as Rabindra Sangeet, enduring as cultural staples in Bengali tradition.
Gitanjali's significance lies in its role as a bridge between Eastern mysticism and Western literary sensibilities, introducing Tagore's humanistic spirituality to a global audience and inspiring figures like W.B. Yeats, who wrote an introduction to the English edition praising its otherworldly purity. The work's translation and Nobel recognition marked a pivotal moment in world literature, highlighting South Asian voices and contributing to the Bengal Renaissance's broader cultural impact. Today, Gitanjali remains a cornerstone of modern Indian literature, studied for its philosophical depth and poetic innovation, with ongoing adaptations in music, theater, and scholarship.

Background and Composition

Original Bengali Composition

The original Bengali Gitanjali was primarily composed between approximately 1904 and 1910, many during Rabindranath Tagore's earlier residence at the family estate in Shilaidaha, a riverside location in what is now Bangladesh. This timeframe followed significant personal tragedies, including the death of Tagore's wife, Mrinalini Devi, in 1902 and his father, Debendranath Tagore, in 1905, which deepened his introspective mood and spiritual reflections. At Shilaidaha, Tagore often sought solitude in the surrounding rural landscape, where his daily boat rides along the Padma River provided a profound source of inspiration, allowing him to immerse himself in the rhythms of nature and compose verses that blended personal devotion with universal longing. Many of these poems were written during Tagore's management of family estates in rural Bengal in the late 1890s and early 1900s.
The poems of Gitanjali were not entirely new creations but a curated selection drawn from Tagore's earlier devotional works, reflecting a process of refinement and thematic cohesion. The collection of 157 poems was assembled from Tagore's devotional lyrics composed over the preceding decade, including selections from earlier works like Naivedya (published in 1901) and Kheya (published in 1906), along with new pieces. These lyrics emerged from Tagore's contemplative isolation, often set against the backdrop of the river's ebb and flow, which symbolized transience and divine connection in his poetry. The full Bengali Gitanjali, comprising 157 songs and poems, was published on August 14, 1910, by the Indian Press in Allahabad, marking the culmination of this selective assembly.
Tagore envisioned Gitanjali as "song offerings," with "git" denoting song and "anjali" signifying offering, positioning the work within the Vaishnava bhakti tradition of devotional lyrics that express humble surrender to the divine. This intent infused the poems with a musical quality, many intended for singing, and emphasized themes of spiritual intimacy over formal ritual.

Personal and Literary Influences

Rabindranath Tagore's composition of Gitanjali was profoundly shaped by personal tragedies during his mid-life, particularly the deaths of close family members that prompted a period of deep introspection and spiritual seeking. In 1902, his wife, Mrinalini Devi, succumbed to illness after 19 years of marriage, an event that plunged Tagore into profound grief and marked the beginning of a transformative phase in his inner life. This loss was compounded by the death of his daughter, Renuka, in 1903 at the age of 12, and the passing of his father, Debendranath Tagore, in 1905, who had been a pivotal spiritual influence as a leader in the Brahmo Samaj. These successive bereavements fostered Tagore's turn toward contemplative spirituality, viewing death not as an end but as part of a cosmic cycle, which infused the devotional tone of Gitanjali's poems.
Literarily, Gitanjali draws from medieval Bengali Vaishnava traditions, echoing the ecstatic devotion found in the works of poets like Chandidas and Jayadeva, whose verses on divine love and union with Krishna provided a model for Tagore's lyrical expressions of longing for the divine. The collection also reflects Upanishadic philosophy, emphasizing the unity of the self with the universal spirit, a worldview inherited from Tagore's family immersion in Brahmo Samaj teachings that reinterpreted ancient Hindu texts. Sufi mysticism further permeated his sensibility, with parallels to Persian poets' themes of annihilation in the divine beloved, as seen in Tagore's syncretic blending of bhakti and mystical surrender during his encounters with Islamic traditions. Earlier compositions, such as his 1890 poetry volume Manasi, laid thematic foundations by exploring human emotions intertwined with spiritual quests, evolving into the more refined offerings of Gitanjali.
The cultural milieu of the Bengal Renaissance, in which Tagore's family played a central role, enriched Gitanjali with a synthesis of Hindu devotionalism and reformist ideals, promoting personal communion with the divine over ritualistic practices. His experiences managing family estates in rural Bengal during the 1890s immersed him in the region's lush landscapes, where nature's rhythms inspired motifs of harmony between the human soul and the cosmos, reflecting Hindu traditions that celebrate the divine in everyday surroundings. Central to this is the concept of bhakti yoga, the path of devotion expressed through song, which Tagore contrasted with formal religion by advocating intimate, musical surrender to the divine, a practice rooted in Bengal's Vaishnava heritage.

Publication History

Bengali Edition

The full Bengali edition of Gitanjali was published in August 1910, comprising 157 poems that Tagore had composed over several years. The collection was released by the Indian Press in Allahabad, reflecting Tagore's preference for independence from major colonial publishing houses. Priced affordably to ensure accessibility to a broader Bengali readership, the edition featured musical notations for select poems, underscoring Tagore's vision of the pieces as songs meant to be performed and sung.
The poems explore devotion, nature, and human longing, which resonated deeply within Bengali literary circles. Early reception in India was positive, with contemporaries such as philosopher Brajendranath Seal praising its lyrical quality and devotional depth, helping to solidify Tagore's reputation as a leading figure in Bengali literature prior to his international recognition. Although the initial print run was modest, the work gained traction through Tagore's recitations at his school in Shantiniketan, where students and visitors encountered the poems in live performances that emphasized their musicality and emotional power. This domestic popularity laid the groundwork for Gitanjali's enduring influence in Indian literary tradition.

English Edition and International Release

During his voyage to England in 1912, Rabindranath Tagore personally translated and adapted 103 poems from Gitanjali and other Bengali collections into English prose poems, reworking the originals to achieve a rhythmic flow suitable for English readers rather than providing a literal translation. This process allowed Tagore to infuse the translations with a lyrical quality that captured the devotional essence of the originals while adapting to Western poetic sensibilities.
The English edition, titled Gitanjali: Song Offerings, was first published in November 1912 by the India Society in London as a limited edition of 750 copies, with only 250 available for sale. In 1913, Macmillan & Co. reissued it in a trade edition, featuring an introduction by W.B. Yeats that significantly elevated its profile. Yeats's preface praised the work as a profound revelation of spiritual depth, describing it as a discovery of the soul's surrender to the divine and emphasizing its courteous and wise tone.
The English Gitanjali was introduced to Western audiences through Tagore's readings during his 1912 visit to London, where he shared the manuscript with figures like painter William Rothenstein, sparking immediate interest among literary circles. The book experienced rapid sales in the United Kingdom following the Macmillan edition, leading to an American edition also published by Macmillan in 1913, which further disseminated the work across the Atlantic and marked its entry into global literary discourse.

Content and Structure

Poetic Form and Organization

The English edition of Gitanjali consists of 103 untitled poems presented as prose poems, selected and translated by Tagore himself from various Bengali works. These poems are numbered sequentially from 1 to 103 in the 1913 Macmillan edition, with no table of contents, allowing for a fluid, uninterrupted reading experience that mirrors the collection's devotional flow.
In form, the poems employ free verse structured as prose, blending the rhythmic cadence of songs with the expansiveness of prose to create a musicality suited to English. This approach incorporates repetition for emphasis, vivid metaphors drawn from nature and daily life, and simple, direct language that evokes emotional depth without ornate complexity. Many of the originals were metered Bengali songs (rabindrasangeet), which Tagore adapted to preserve their lyrical essence while prioritizing spiritual resonance over strict metrical constraints.
The organization follows a loose thematic progression, starting with intimate personal prayers and evolving toward broader cosmic visions of universal praise and divine unity, without rigid sections or divisions. This structure encourages a meditative journey, reflecting the soul's expansion from individual devotion to encompassing all creation. For instance, Poem 1 serves as an opening invocation, addressing the divine with lines like "Thou hast made me endless, such is thy pleasure," establishing the tone of humble surrender and eternal renewal.

Key Poems and Motifs

Gitanjali features several representative poems that exemplify its devotional and philosophical essence. Poem 1 serves as an opening plea for the divine presence, portraying the speaker as an endless vessel repeatedly filled with fresh life by the divine will: "Thou hast made me endless, such is thy pleasure. This frail vessel thou emptiest again and again, and fillest it ever with fresh life." This poem establishes a tone of humble surrender and infinite renewal through God's grace. Similarly, Poem 35 envisions a state of spiritual and intellectual freedom, where the mind operates without fear and reason flows unhindered: "Where the mind is without fear and the head is held high; Where knowledge is free; Where the world has not been broken up into fragments by narrow domestic walls." It calls for enlightenment and unity, awakening the country to a heaven of perfection guided by the divine. Poem 103 concludes the collection with a unified salutation to God, evoking eternal renewal through sensory immersion in the world: "In one salutation to thee, my God, let all my senses spread out and touch this world at thy feet. Like a rain-cloud of July hung low with its burden of unshed showers let all my mind bend down at thy door." These selections highlight the work's focus on personal transformation and cosmic harmony.
Recurring motifs in Gitanjali symbolize the soul's journey toward liberation. River and boat imagery frequently represents life's transient voyage toward the divine, with the boat signifying the human soul navigating earthly existence and the river embodying the flow of time and destiny: the other bank often alludes to the afterlife or spiritual fulfillment. The prison/freedom dichotomy underscores spiritual emancipation, as seen in depictions of the soul imprisoned by worldly attachments yet aspiring to boundless liberty, contrasting confinement with the expansive freedom of divine union. Additionally, the child-parent relationship portrays the divine as a nurturing father to the devoted child, emphasizing innocence, dependence, and intimate communion, as in references to awakening under divine guidance.
Unique to Gitanjali is its seamless integration of everyday Bengali life with abstract spirituality, such as references to monsoon rains in Poem 103's July cloud or harvest cycles evoking renewal and abundance, grounding transcendent themes in familiar rural rhythms. The collection eschews narrative plot in favor of lyrical snapshotsdiscrete, meditative vignettes that capture momentary revelations rather than sequential storytelling. For the English edition, Tagore selected 53 poems from the original Bengali Gitanjali compilation of 157, along with 50 from other Bengali collections, prioritizing those with universal appeal and omitting more regionally specific ones to convey broader spiritual insights.

Themes and Interpretation

Spiritual Devotion and Divinity

In Gitanjali, Rabindranath Tagore presents a devotional core rooted in bhakti, portraying God not as a remote sovereign but as an intimate companion who invites personal surrender and joyful communion. The poems emphasize sharanagati, or complete self-surrender to the divine, as seen in lines where the speaker offers songs and dances as acts of loving response to God's perceived loneliness, fostering a reciprocal relationship that transcends formal worship. This intimacy reflects the bhakti tradition's focus on emotional union over hierarchical distance, drawing from Vaishnava influences where devotion is an outpouring of the heart rather than obligatory rite.
Key concepts in the collection highlight the union of the human soul with the infinite, critiquing empty ritualism in favor of authentic, heartfelt prayer. For instance, in one poem, the speaker declares, "Thou hast made me endless, such is thy pleasure. This frail vessel thou emptiest again and again, and fillest it ever with fresh life," illustrating the soul's boundless expansion through divine grace and the joy found in worship as a natural extension of existence. Tagore rejects mechanical religious practices, urging instead a direct encounter with the divine through inner purity, as evidenced in verses that decry secluded temple rituals and call for recognition of God in everyday labor and humility. This shift underscores bhakti's egalitarian ethos, where devotion arises spontaneously from personal experience.
Philosophically, Gitanjali draws depth from Advaita Vedanta's non-dualistic view of reality while personalizing it through a Vaishnava lens, envisioning divinity as ever-present in mundane life rather than confined to abstract metaphysics. The infinite is not an impersonal force but a loving presence that permeates the ordinary, blending theistic devotion with a quest for universal oneness, as in poems where the soul merges with the divine amid daily rhythms. This synthesis allows Tagore to infuse bhakti with elements of Upanishadic unity, emphasizing that true worship reveals the soul's inherent connection to the eternal without needing intermediaries.
Tagore's unique interpretation in Gitanjali weaves theistic devotion into a humanistic framework, where spiritual surrender promotes universal ethics and social harmony, transforming personal bhakti into a broader call for empathetic living. By portraying God as accessible to all through love and service, the poems advocate a devotion that unites individual spirituality with collective human welfare, echoing Vaishnava ideals but extending them beyond sectarian bounds to foster global understanding. This blend elevates Gitanjali as a testament to devotion's role in ethical realization, where joy in the divine inspires compassion for humanity.

Nature, Love, and Human Experience

In Gitanjali, Rabindranath Tagore integrates the natural world as a profound metaphor for divine beauty and the ephemeral quality of existence, drawing on elements like flora, rivers, and seasons to evoke spiritual insights. Flowers and butterflies symbolize momentary joys that transcend temporal constraints, highlighting nature's ability to capture eternal truths within fleeting instances. Rivers and seasonal cycles further represent the flow of life and renewal, portraying the universe as a living tapestry where human perception aligns with cosmic rhythms. Scholarly analysis notes that Tagore's nature imagery personifies the environment, emphasizing its spiritual significance and the intimate kinship between humanity and the natural realm.
Tagore explores love through sensual and erotic dimensions intertwined with divine yearning, employing the bridegroom God motif to depict the soul's passionate pursuit of spiritual union without descending into explicit romantic narratives. In this framework, the human soul appears as a bride adorned for her eternal lover, as in poem 91: "The garland is ready for the bridegroom. After the wedding the bride shall leave her home and meet her lord alone in the solitude of night." Such imagery infuses divine love with erotic intensity, transforming abstract devotion into a tangible, relational experience that awakens the spirit. Literary examinations underscore how this motif elevates human affection as a conduit for transcendence, blending physical longing with metaphysical fulfillment.
The collection delves into human experience through motifs of profound longing, humility, and unadorned joy, often critiquing materialism by contrasting worldly pursuits with the richness of simple, impoverished lives. Poems evoke the ache of separation from the divine alongside the quiet ecstasy found in everyday humility, as in poem 11, where the speaker urges abandoning ritualistic worship for active service amid toil: "Put off thy holy mantle and even like him come down on the dusty soil!" Vignettes of poverty reveal fulfillment in modest existence, portraying material excess as a barrier to true liberation. Analyses highlight these themes as affirmations of human-spiritual harmony, where simplicity fosters inner wealth over external accumulation.
This distinctive interplay positions nature and love as essential bridges to the divine, anchoring ethereal spirituality in the sensory and relational aspects of human reality. By weaving these earthly elements into devotional expressions, Tagore grounds abstract mysticism in accessible experiences, allowing readers to perceive the sacred within the ordinary.

Translations and Adaptations

Tagore's English Version

Rabindranath Tagore's English version of Gitanjali, published as Song Offerings in 1912, comprises 103 prose poems that he personally translated and adapted from his Bengali originals. Rather than adhering to a word-for-word approach, Tagore employed a free rendering in English, rewriting the songs to capture their spiritual essence while transforming them into prose poems for broader accessibility to non-Bengali readers. This method allowed him to prioritize the devotional mood over strict fidelity to the source text's structure and rhyme.
Of these, 53 poems were drawn directly from the 1910 Bengali Gitanjali, with the remainder selected from other collections, including 15 from Naivedya (1905), 17 from Gitimalya, 11 from Kheya, 1 from Utsarga, and smaller numbers from others such as Sishu and Achalayatan. Stylistically, Tagore infused the translations with an archaic, biblical toneemploying second-person forms like "Thou" to personify the divineto evoke a sense of reverence and timeless universality, aligning the work with Western literary and religious sensibilities. He also simplified intricate Bengali idioms and modulated culturally specific references, such as Krishna-centric imagery rooted in Vaishnava traditions, to emphasize a more inclusive human-divine relationship rather than localized Hindu devotion.
In adapting the originals, Tagore faced challenges in conveying the inherent musicality of the Bengali songs, which were composed for recitation or melody; the English prose form necessarily omitted rhythmic patterns, alliterations, and tuneful elements to focus on thematic clarity and emotional resonance. This shift aimed at universality but sometimes diluted the performative intimacy of the source material. In a prefatory note to the English edition, Tagore described the poems as "revelations of my true self," underscoring their personal, introspective origin beyond mere literary exercise.

Translations in Other Languages

Following the Nobel Prize award in 1913, Gitanjali saw rapid dissemination through translations into several European languages, beginning with German. The first German edition, Gitanjali: Sangesopfer, was translated by Marie Luise Gothein and published in Leipzig by Kurt Wolff Verlag in 1914, capturing the devotional essence through prose renderings that emphasized the lyrical flow of Tagore's original Bengali songs. In France, Andr Gide produced a notable translation titled L'Offrande lyrique in 1919, which adapted all 103 poems and highlighted the mystical intimacy of the text, influencing French literary circles with its poetic prose style. The Spanish version, Ofrenda lrica, translated by Zenobia Camprub de Jimnezthe only translator authorized by Tagore for Spanishappeared in 1918, preserving the rhythmic devotion while incorporating a prologue by W.B. Yeats to underscore its universal appeal.
In Asia, translations emerged concurrently, reflecting Tagore's growing influence. The initial Chinese efforts included Chen Duxiu publishing translations of four poems from Gitanjali in 1915, with multiple versions proliferating in the 1920sseven distinct editions between 1920 and 1925 alonesuch as those by Bing Xin, which introduced modernist poetic sensibilities to Chinese readers and shaped literary exchanges across Asia. In India, adaptations into regional languages like Hindi emphasized the musicality of the originals; early Hindi translations in the 1920s retained devotional rhythms through verse forms suitable for recitation, while later ones like Baijnath Prasad Shukla's 2001 edition aimed for fidelity to the full 157-poem Bengali collection.
Translators have faced significant challenges in conveying Gitanjali's devotional nuances, rhythmic structures, and cultural specificity, often requiring adaptations to maintain poetic brevity and emotional depth across linguistic boundaries. Unlike Tagore's selective English edition of 103 poems, many non-English versions incorporate the complete 157 songs from the original Bengali Gitanjali, allowing for a more comprehensive exploration of its motifs, as seen in bilingual editions that restore song meters. By the 2020s, Gitanjali had been translated into over 50 languages worldwide, including Arabic, Japanese, Russian, and Tamil, with digital editions on platforms like Project Gutenberg enhancing global accessibility and enabling further adaptations. Recent adaptations include Prasenjit Gupta's modern English translation published in 2024 and Santhosh Kana's audio series A Musical Pilgrimage featuring eight selected poems, released in November 2024.

Reception and Legacy

Initial Critical Response

Upon its publication in English in November 1912, Gitanjali elicited widespread acclaim from Western critics, who viewed it as a profound infusion of Eastern spirituality into Western literary traditions. W.B. Yeats, in his preface dated September 1912, praised the collection effusively, describing the poems as embodying "a tradition where poetry and religion are the same thing" and revealing "a world I have dreamed of all my life long," positioning it as essential Eastern wisdom that resonated universally rather than through mere exoticism. Ezra Pound, in his March 1913 review in The Fortnightly Review, echoed this enthusiasm, recounting his visit to Yeats's rooms where the Irish poet was "much excited over the advent of a great poet, someone greater than any of us," and lauding Tagore's work for offering "the pledge of a calm which we need ever more in this age of steel and mechanics." The Times Literary Supplement, in its November 7, 1912, review, highlighted the collection's accessibility, noting that Tagore was "so simple that anyone can understand him; yet this does not mean that he is easy to understand," commending its spiritual depth and lyrical purity.
The book's commercial success underscored this critical enthusiasm, with the Macmillan trade edition of 1913 experiencing rapid demand and multiple printings, reflecting its broad appeal in bridging Eastern mysticism with Western sensibilities. However, responses were not uniformly adulatory; some critics debated the balance between the poems' exotic allure and their timeless universality, with a few questioning whether the mystical elements risked overshadowing the human elements. Despite such nuances, the general consensus celebrated Gitanjali as a landmark in cross-cultural literary exchange.
In India, the English edition's reception post-1912 further solidified Tagore's status as a national icon, amplifying his existing renown from the Bengali original and aligning with the Swadeshi movement's cultural aspirations. Contemporary Indian commentators largely embraced it as a testament to indigenous poetic genius, though a minority critiqued its sentimental tone as occasionally overwrought, preferring the restraint of Tagore's earlier works. Overall, the publication was lauded for elevating Indian literature on the global stage while reinforcing domestic pride in Tagore's devotional themes.

Nobel Prize and Global Recognition

In 1913, Rabindranath Tagore was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature for his collection Gitanjali, becoming the first non-European and first Asian recipient of the honor. The Swedish Academy's citation specifically praised his "profoundly sensitive, fresh and beautiful verse," highlighting the spiritual depth and universal appeal of the poems in their English translation.
Unable to attend the award ceremony in Stockholm due to his location in India, Tagore sent a telegram that was read at the Nobel Banquet on December 10, 1913, expressing gratitude for the recognition that bridged cultural distances and fostered brotherhood among nations. He later visited Sweden in 1926, where he delivered lectures drawing inspiration from Gitanjali's themes of unity and humanism during a period when international travel restrictions from earlier global conflicts had eased.
The Nobel Prize dramatically elevated Gitanjali's profile, propelling it to international acclaim and resulting in widespread publication and readership across Europe and beyond. This surge in popularity facilitated Tagore's extensive world tours from 1916 to 1930, during which he frequently recited selections from the book to audiences in the United States, Japan, Europe, and other regions, amplifying its devotional and philosophical messages.
Among subsequent honors, the prize's prestige contributed to Tagore's broader recognition, including his articulation of internationalist ideals that prefigured the humanistic principles underlying the League of Nations, emphasizing global harmony over narrow nationalism.

Enduring Cultural Influence

Gitanjali has profoundly shaped modern literature, particularly by inspiring Latin American poets such as Octavio Paz and Pablo Neruda, who drew from its mystical depth to infuse their works with universal spiritual themes. Paz, in particular, highlighted Tagore's portrayal of the human-nature relationship as a bridge between Eastern and Western sensibilities, influencing his own explorations of existential harmony. This blending of Eastern mysticism with modernist forms in Gitanjali has also impacted postcolonial literature, where it serves as a model for negotiating cultural hybridity and resisting colonial narratives through poetic universality.
In the realm of music, numerous poems from Gitanjali have been adapted into compositions, forming a core part of Rabindra Sangeet, Tagore's genre of devotional and philosophical songs performed in Indian classical and folk styles. Tagore himself provided musical notations for many of these, ensuring their enduring role in live performances and recordings that evoke spiritual introspection.
The spiritual and social legacy of Gitanjali extends to its influence on figures like Mahatma Gandhi, whose philosophy of non-violent devotion echoed the collection's emphasis on inner surrender and ethical living as paths to truth. Its verses, promoting universal divinity beyond sectarian boundaries, have been recited in interfaith gatherings to foster dialogue and shared humanity. In contemporary contexts, Gitanjali informs mindfulness practices through its meditative reflections on presence and gratitude, while its eco-spiritual motifscelebrating nature as divine manifestationresonate in modern environmental ethics and sustainability movements.
Gitanjali maintains contemporary relevance through accessible digital editions of the English translation on platforms like Project Gutenberg, and scanned copies of the original Bengali text on the Internet Archive, enabling global readership and scholarly analysis. A 2014 documentary, The Story of Gitanjali, traces its creation and global journey, highlighting its role in cross-cultural exchange. Annual festivals in India, such as those organized by Tagore societies during Rabindra Jayanti, feature recitations and performances of Gitanjali poems to celebrate its themes. In postcolonial studies, however, the collection faces critiques for potential Orientalist interpretations in its English version, where Western receptions sometimes exoticized its mysticism to fit imperial gazes.
