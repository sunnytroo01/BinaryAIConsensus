 STS-124 was the 123rd NASA Space Shuttle mission and the 35th orbital flight for Space Shuttle Discovery, serving as the 26th shuttle assembly mission to the International Space Station (ISS). Launched on May 31, 2008, at 5:02 p.m. EDT from Launch Pad 39A at NASA's Kennedy Space Center in Florida, the mission delivered and installed the Pressurized Module (PM)the core component of Japan's Kibo laboratoryand the Japanese Experiment Module Remote Manipulator System (JEMRMS), a 33-foot robotic arm, to the ISS. The 13-day flight, which concluded with a landing at Kennedy Space Center on June 14, 2008, at 11:15 a.m. EDT after traveling more than 5.7 million miles, marked a key step in completing the Kibo facility, enhancing the ISS's scientific research capabilities.
The crew consisted of seven astronauts: Commander Mark E. Kelly, Pilot Kenneth T. Ham, and Mission Specialists Karen L. Nyberg, Ronald J. Garan Jr., Michael E. Fossum, Akihiko Hoshide from the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA), and Gregory E. Chamitoff, who joined Expedition 17 upon arrival while Expedition 16 member Garrett Reisman returned with the shuttle. Hoshide, the second Japanese astronaut to fly on a shuttle mission, played a central role in Kibo-related operations, including robotic arm activities and spacewalks. Discovery docked with the ISS's Harmony node on June 2, 2008, at 1:03 p.m. CDT, enabling the transfer of the 35,100-pound Kibo PM using the shuttle's robotic arm before its relocation to the ISS's Zenith port.
Primary objectives focused on the assembly and activation of Kibo's pressurized elements, including the relocation of the earlier-launched Experiment Logistics Module-Pressurized Section (ELM-PS) from Harmony to Kibo's zenith side and the outfitting of internal racks for experiments in space medicine, materials science, and Earth observation. Three extravehicular activities (EVAs), or spacewalks, were conducted by Fossum and Garan to install the JEMRMS, connect power and data cables, and prepare Kibo for operations, totaling over 20 hours outside the station. The mission also included the transfer of over 7,000 pounds of supplies and scientific payloads, such as the Integrated Strategic Payload that tested shuttle thermal protection systems.
Undocking occurred on June 11, 2008, at 6:42 a.m. CDT, followed by a flyaround of the ISS to document the newly installed Kibo module. The mission's success paved the way for Kibo's full operational status, which began later in 2008 with the addition of exposed facility components, ultimately making it the largest single laboratory module on the ISS and a cornerstone of international collaboration in microgravity research. No major anomalies were reported, underscoring the reliability of the Space Shuttle program in its later assembly phase.

Mission Overview

Background

STS-124 marked the 123rd flight in NASA's Space Shuttle program and the 35th mission for the orbiter Discovery, which lifted off from Launch Pad 39A at the Kennedy Space Center in Florida on May 31, 2008, at 5:02 p.m. EDT. This mission was the 26th dedicated to the assembly and outfitting of the International Space Station (ISS), continuing the program's role in constructing the orbiting laboratory through a series of shuttle deliveries.
The primary focus of STS-124 was to complete the delivery and installation of the Japanese Experiment Module, known as Kibo, by transporting its main Pressurized Module (JPM) and the associated Japanese Remote Manipulator System (J-RMS) robotic arm to the ISS. This followed the partial delivery of Kibo components during the preceding STS-123 mission in March 2008, which had brought the Experiment Logistics Module-Pressurized Section for temporary storage and initial setup. With the JPM's attachment to the ISS's Harmony node, Kibo became the largest single module on the station, enabling expanded research capabilities in microgravity for fields such as space medicine, materials science, and Earth observation.
STS-124 exemplified NASA's international commitments under the 1998 Intergovernmental Agreement (IGA) establishing the cooperative framework for the ISS, which included contributions from partner agencies like the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA) for the development and operation of Kibo. This partnership allowed JAXA to manage real-time control of the module from its Tsukuba Space Center in Japan once activated, fostering shared utilization of the station for global scientific advancement. The mission's timeline had shifted from earlier planning targets in 2007 due to cascading delays from previous shuttle flights, including repairs and scheduling adjustments, as well as weather-related postponements in the lead-up to launch.

Objectives and Payloads

The primary objective of the STS-124 mission was to deliver and install the Kibo Pressurized Module (PM), the main habitable component of Japan's Kibo laboratory, along with the Japanese Experiment Module Remote Manipulator System (JEMRMS), to the International Space Station (ISS). This delivery marked the second of three flights dedicated to assembling the Kibo facility, enabling expanded microgravity research capabilities for the international partnership.
The Kibo PM measures 11.2 meters in length and 4.4 meters in diameter, with a mass of 14,800 kilograms when fully assembled, and is designed to accommodate up to 23 experiment racks for scientific investigations. The JEMRMS consists of a primary 10-meter-long robotic arm capable of handling payloads up to 7,000 kilograms, supporting external operations and assembly tasks for Kibo.
Secondary objectives encompassed the transfer of logistical supplies and experiment racks to the ISS, facilitation of crew rotation, and initial activation of the Kibo PM to prepare it for microgravity research. Eight racks were transferred from the Japanese Logistics Module to the Kibo PM, including equipment for short-duration U.S. experiments such as the Immune Function study on astronaut immune responses, the Middeck Active Vibration Isolation and Suppression experiment (MAUI) for vibration control in microgravity, the Nanoparticle Linear Production-Vaccine-1B for vaccine development, and Sleep-Short for analyzing sleep patterns in space. Activation of Kibo systems commenced on flight day 5 with the powering of Channel B, achieving full operational readiness by flight day 6 to support ongoing research.
For logistical support of the ISS crew rotation, Gregory E. Chamitoff replaced Garrett E. Reisman as the Expedition 17 flight engineer, with the exchange of Soyuz seat liners occurring on flight day 3 to maintain assignment continuity for emergency return capabilities. This rotation ensured seamless personnel transitions while the shuttle crew handled payload integration.

Crew

Composition and Roles

The STS-124 crew consisted of seven members: NASA astronauts Mark E. Kelly as commander, Kenneth T. Ham as pilot, Karen L. Nyberg, Ronald J. Garan Jr., Michael E. Fossum, and Gregory E. Chamitoff as mission specialists, along with Akihiko Hoshide from the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA) as a mission specialist. This multinational team was responsible for delivering and installing the Kibo Pressurized Module and its robotic arm to the International Space Station (ISS), conducting extravehicular activities (EVAs), and facilitating a crew exchange.
Mark E. Kelly, a U.S. Navy commander and NASA astronaut selected in 1996, served as mission commander, overseeing all flight operations, including orbiter systems management, docking, and the rendezvous pitch maneuver. This was Kelly's third spaceflight, following STS-108 in 2001 and STS-121 in 2006, during which he had accumulated over 25 days in space.
Kenneth T. Ham, also a U.S. Navy commander and NASA astronaut selected in 1998, acted as pilot, handling ascent and entry phases, orbiter systems operations, and shuttle robotic arm activities, including undocking from the ISS. Ham, a veteran test pilot with over 3,700 flight hours, was on his first spaceflight and had prior experience as a capsule communicator (CAPCOM) at NASA's Mission Control.
Karen L. Nyberg, a NASA astronaut selected in 2000 with a PhD in mechanical engineering, operated the shuttle robotic arm, ISS Canadarm2, and Kibo's Japanese Experiment Module Remote Manipulator System (JEMRMS) to support Kibo module assembly and transfer. This was Nyberg's first spaceflight; she had previously participated in the NEEMO 10 undersea analog mission as an aquanaut.
Ronald J. Garan Jr., a U.S. Air Force colonel and NASA astronaut selected in 2000, was assigned as the primary EVA crewmate for all three planned spacewalks, focusing on Kibo outfitting, Orbital Boom Sensor System (OBSS) transfer, and other station maintenance tasks. On his first spaceflight, Garan brought experience from the NEEMO 9 undersea mission and a background in Air Force operations.
Michael E. Fossum, a U.S. Air Force Reserve colonel and NASA astronaut selected in 1998, served as lead spacewalker (EV1) for the EVAs, also operating the shuttle arm and assisting with Solar Alpha Rotary Joint (SARJ) maintenance and nitrogen tank assembly swaps. This marked Fossum's second spaceflight after STS-121, where he had logged 306 hours in space and completed three EVAs.
Akihiko Hoshide, a JAXA astronaut selected in 1999, led Kibo module assembly, activation, and integration efforts, including operations of the JEMRMS and support for station arm tasks during installation. Hoshide, who trained at NASA's Johnson Space Center starting in 2004, was on his first spaceflight and contributed expertise in Japanese hardware support, including the H-II Transfer Vehicle (HTV).
Gregory E. Chamitoff, a NASA astronaut selected in 1998 with a PhD in aeronautics and astronautics, joined the ISS as the new flight engineer for Expedition 17, replacing Garrett E. Reisman and assisting with station arm operations and Kibo setup. This was Chamitoff's first spaceflight; he had prior analog mission experience from NEEMO 3 and focused on microgravity research during his ISS tenure.

Training and Notes

The crew for STS-124 was assigned by NASA on March 22, 2007, with selections emphasizing expertise in the assembly and integration of the Japanese Experiment Module (Kibo) laboratory to the International Space Station (ISS). This mission-specific focus guided the composition, drawing on astronauts experienced in robotics, extravehicular activities (EVAs), and station operations to ensure successful delivery and installation of Kibo's pressurized module and robotic arm.
Training for the STS-124 crew commenced in early 2006 and extended over approximately 2.5 years, incorporating ISS systems familiarization alongside mission-specific preparations at NASA's Johnson Space Center. The regimen included intensive simulations using virtual reality labs and full-scale mockups of the Space Shuttle and ISS to replicate flight procedures, rendezvous, and docking scenarios. Robotics practice covered operations of the shuttle's remote manipulator system, the ISS's Canadarm2, and Kibo's dedicated Japanese Remote Manipulator System (JEM RMS), ensuring seamless coordination for module berthing and outfitting.
EVA training occurred primarily at the Neutral Buoyancy Laboratory (NBL), where crew members practiced the three planned spacewalks in a weightless simulation to install Kibo components, relocate spare parts, and conduct station maintenance tasks. This underwater facility allowed for repeated rehearsals of suited mobility, tool handling, and contingency procedures critical to the mission's success.
The training process highlighted international collaboration, particularly with the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA), as astronaut Akihiko Hoshide was selected in March 2007 to serve as a mission specialist focused on Kibo operations. Hoshide, certified as a JAXA astronaut since 2001, participated in joint NASA-JAXA sessions covering general shuttle and ISS protocols alongside specialized Kibo assembly training. Additionally, Gregory E. Chamitoff launched on STS-124 to replace Garrett Reisman as a flight engineer for Expedition 17, joining the station crew shortly after the April 2008 arrival of Soyuz TMA-12.
Commander Mark E. Kelly led pre-mission discussions on crew dynamics, stressing the vital role of teamwork and coordination in executing the complex Kibo installation amid tight timelines and interdependent tasks. This emphasis on collective preparation fostered a cohesive team environment, enabling the crew to adapt effectively during training and operations.

Preparation

Shuttle Processing

Following the successful completion of STS-120, Space Shuttle Discovery landed at Kennedy Space Center on November 7, 2007, and was immediately towed to the Orbiter Processing Facility (OPF) Bay 3 for post-flight safing, maintenance, and preparations for its next mission. Over the ensuing six months, technicians conducted extensive refurbishment activities, including the removal and reinstallation of the three reusable solid rocket motors from the previous flight, detailed inspections and repairs to the thermal protection system tiles to ensure integrity against reentry heating, and the installation of the space shuttle main engines (SSMEs) after off-site testing at Stennis Space Center. System-level functional tests verified the orbiter's avionics, propulsion, and life support subsystems, with the thermal control system checkout completed without anomalies by January 10, 2008.
Payload integration for STS-124 began in early 2008 at Kennedy Space Center's Space Station Processing Facility, where the Kibo Pressurized Module (JEM-PM)a 37-foot-long, 32,000-pound cylindrical laboratory developed by the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agencywas outfitted with internal racks and the Japanese Remote Manipulator System before transfer for mating to the Integrated Cargo Carrier in the OPF. On April 26, 2008, Discovery was rolled over from the OPF to the Vehicle Assembly Building (VAB) High Bay 3, a distance of approximately 1.5 miles, where it underwent final closeout preparations prior to stacking. In the VAB, the orbiter was hoisted and mated to External Tank ET-128the first production unit incorporating post-Return to Flight modifications such as redesigned liquid hydrogen ice/frost ramps and liquid oxygen feedline bracketsand the stacked Solid Rocket Boosters BI-134, which had been assembled earlier from four segments each to balance thrust. Ground fueling tests on the external tank confirmed propellant loading procedures and leak-free performance, minimizing risks of debris shedding during launch.
Several minor issues arose during processing but were resolved without significant schedule impacts. A bent radiator hose was identified on January 30, 2008, prompting engineering assessments and subsequent replacement to maintain cooling efficiency. Additionally, leak checks on Main Engine No. 1's liquid oxygen transducer were performed on January 10, 2008, addressing potential seal anomalies, while a multiplexer-demultiplexer unit was replaced in mid-May to ensure data routing reliability. Delays in external tank delivery due to fuel sensor repairs at Michoud Assembly Facilitystemming from prior missions STS-122 and STS-123pushed ET-128's arrival to March 26, 2008, but overall preparations remained on track, with the fully stacked vehicle rolled out to Launch Pad 39A on May 3, 2008.

Launch Countdown

The terminal countdown for STS-124 began at T-43 hours on May 30, 2008, at approximately 10:02 a.m. EDT, initiating the final preparations for Space Shuttle Discovery's launch from Kennedy Space Center's Pad 39A. This phase encompassed loading hypergolic propellants into the Orbital Maneuvering System and Reaction Control System tanks, powering up the shuttle's onboard systems, and conducting final checks on the solid rocket boosters and external tank.
As the countdown progressed, weather conditions at the Kennedy Space Center and transatlantic abort landing sites were monitored closely, with forecasts indicating favorable conditions including partly cloudy skies and winds within acceptable limits, resolving any minor concerns from earlier in the week. No unplanned holds or scrubs occurred during the terminal countdown, which proceeded smoothly without interruptions beyond standard built-in pauses. At T-3 hours, following a planned hold, the seven-member crewCommander Mark E. Kelly, Pilot Kenneth T. Ham, and Mission Specialists Karen L. Nyberg, Ronald J. Garan Jr., Michael E. Fossum, Akihiko Hoshide, and Gregory E. Chamitoffingressed into Discovery, donning pressure suits and boarding the orbiter for strap-in.
The final 10 minutes featured go/no-go polls among NASA test directors, flight directors, and support teams, all of whom cleared the mission for launch, confirming system readiness and weather acceptability for the opening of the 10-minute launch window at 5:02 p.m. EDT on May 31. This seamless progression underscored the meticulous pre-launch rehearsals, including the Terminal Countdown Demonstration Test completed earlier in May, ensuring all personnel and procedures were aligned.

Launch and Ascent

Liftoff

The Space Shuttle Discovery lifted off from Launch Complex 39A at NASA's Kennedy Space Center on May 31, 2008, at 5:02:12 p.m. EDT (21:02:12 UTC), marking the beginning of the STS-124 mission. The countdown concluded without delays, and the three Space Shuttle Main Engines (SSMEs) ignited at T-6.6 seconds, reaching full thrust by T-0, followed immediately by ignition of the two solid rocket boosters (SRBs) at nominal performance levels.
The ascent profile commenced with a roll program at approximately T+10 seconds to align the stack with the planned launch azimuth, transitioning into a pitch-over maneuver at T+12 seconds to initiate the gravity turn toward orbit. Maximum dynamic pressure (Max-Q) was encountered at T+1:10, prompting a throttle-down of the SSMEs to 65% to minimize aerodynamic stress on the vehicle; the engines subsequently throttled back up post-Max-Q. The SRBs performed nominally and separated at T+2:06, jettisoning into the Atlantic Ocean for recovery. No anomalies were reported during these initial ascent phases.
The SSMEs continued firing until main engine cutoff (MECO) at T+8:35, placing the vehicle on a suborbital trajectory with an apogee of approximately 220 km; the external tank (ET-128) then separated nominally at T+8:50, reentering over the Indian Ocean. Post-launch imagery and analysis confirmed a clean ascent with only minor foam shedding from the ET, consisting of two small losses near the LO2 feedline support fitting; unlike the catastrophic foam impact on STS-107 (Columbia), this event caused no damage to the orbiter or the launch pad infrastructure.

Orbital Insertion

Following the separation of the solid rocket boosters earlier in ascent, the Space Shuttle Discovery's main engines reached cutoff (MECO) at T+8 minutes and 35 seconds, concluding the high-thrust phase of launch and propelling the vehicle toward a suborbital trajectory at approximately 17,000 mph. Immediately after MECO, the External Tank (ET-128) was jettisoned at T+8 minutes and 50 seconds, allowing the orbiter to transition to independent orbital maneuvering while the tank reentered the atmosphere over the Indian Ocean. These events marked the completion of the initial ascent burn, with Discovery now relying on its onboard propulsion for orbit insertion.
At approximately T+10 minutes, the Orbital Maneuvering System (OMS) engines ignited for the OMS-1 burn, lasting about 86 seconds and establishing an initial elliptical parking orbit with a perigee of approximately 105 km (57 nautical miles) and apogee of 220 km, inclined at 51.6 degrees to support rendezvous with the International Space Station. Approximately 44 minutes after liftoff, the OMS-2 burn, lasting about 75 seconds, circularized the orbit at roughly 220 km altitude, achieving a stable low Earth orbit configuration for the duration of the 13-day flight.
At approximately T+1 hour 33 minutes, the payload bay doors were fully opened, enabling active thermal control via radiators and exposing the Kibo module components for deployment preparations. Initial on-orbit verification activities commenced promptly, including a detailed survey of the orbiter's thermal protection system using onboard television cameras and the Orbiter Boom Sensor System (OBSS). The inspection identified no significant debris strikes or damage to the reinforced carbon-carbon panels or tiles, affirming the vehicle's readiness for docking and extravehicular activities.

In-Orbit Operations

Rendezvous and Docking

The rendezvous phase of STS-124 began on flight day 2, approximately 24 hours after launch on May 31, 2008, as Space Shuttle Discovery executed a series of orbital maneuvers to close the distance to the International Space Station (ISS). The mission's rendezvous profile followed standard procedures for ISS-bound shuttle flights, involving ground-targeted burns to adjust Discovery's trajectory from its initial orbit toward the station's path. By the end of flight day 2 on June 1, Discovery had completed initial corrections, positioning it for the final approach on flight day 3.
On flight day 2, the crew conducted a late inspection of Discovery's thermal protection system (TPS), delayed from the previous day due to the large size of the Kibo Pressurized Module in the payload bay, which limited access during the early survey. Mission Specialists Karen Nyberg and Ronald Garan operated the Orbiter Boom Sensor System (OBSS), extending the 50-foot boom equipped with digital cameras and a laser imaging system to scan the orbiter's nose cap and wing leading edges for any debris impact damage. The inspection, lasting several hours, focused on high-risk areas and transmitted high-resolution imagery to ground teams at NASA's Mission Control Center for analysis, confirming no significant TPS concerns.
Key trajectory adjustments included the NC1 (Non-Propulsive Correction 1) burn, performed approximately 24 hours after liftoff using Discovery's reaction control system thrusters to fine-tune the orbit and establish proper phasing for rendezvous. Additional small correction burns on flight day 2 and early flight day 3 further aligned the shuttle's path, positioning it about 31 miles behind the ISS by the start of the terminal phase. These maneuvers, monitored by the shuttle's rendezvous radar and ground navigation, ensured Discovery approached from below along the R-Bar (radial) vector relative to the station.
The terminal phase commenced on flight day 3 with the Ti (Terminal Initiation) burn about 2.5 hours before docking on June 2, firing Discovery's orbital maneuvering system engines for about 12 minutes to accelerate the shuttle toward the ISS at roughly 0.1 feet per second. Approximately 30 minutes later, at about 600 feet below the station, Commander Mark E. Kelly executed the Rendezvous Pitch Maneuver (RPM), a 360-degree backflip lasting nine minutes that allowed the ISS crewusing 400 mm and 800 mm lenses from station windowsto photograph the shuttle's belly, nose, and wings for additional TPS verification. The RPM imagery provided complementary data to the OBSS scans, with resolution down to 0.5 inches per pixel in key areas.
Discovery arrived at the V-Bar (velocity vector) alignment about 1:11 p.m. EDT, initiating the final approach to the Pressurized Mating Adapter-2 (PMA-2) on the Harmony module. Kelly manually piloted the shuttle using handheld translation controls, pausing briefly at 30 feet for station-keeping before contact at 2:03 p.m. EDT, nine minutes behind schedule due to minor alignment adjustments. The docking mechanism engaged softly at a closing rate of 0.1 feet per second, with a three-inch alignment tolerance; preliminary latches captured immediately, followed by full hook engagement by 2:10 p.m. EDT, structurally linking the two vehicles without incident. Post-capture, the ISS entered a 27-minute free-drift period to allow structural dampening before reorienting to protect against micrometeoroids.

Kibo Module Installation

The installation of the Kibo Pressurized Module (PM) to the International Space Station (ISS) began on flight day 5 of the STS-124 mission, June 4, 2008, when the module was grappled by the shuttle's Remote Manipulator System (RMS), operated by mission specialist Karen Nyberg and pilot Kenneth Ham. The module, measuring approximately 11.2 meters in length and weighing 15.9 metric tons, was lifted from Space Shuttle Discovery's payload bay. It was then maneuvered to a position where it was handed off to the ISS's Space Station Remote Manipulator System (SSRMS), or Canadarm2, controlled by Japanese Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA) astronaut Akihiko Hoshide and station commander Sergei Volkov. This coordinated transfer, lasting about 90 minutes, positioned the module for final approach to its berth site without incident.
Berthing operations commenced on June 5, 2008, when the SSRMS aligned the Kibo PM with the nadir port of the Harmony node (Node 2). The Active Common Berthing Mechanism (CBM) on the Harmony node extended and captured the passive CBM on the Kibo PM at approximately 7:34 p.m. EDT, followed by the retraction and hard mate sequence. Ground teams at NASA's Mission Control Center in Houston and JAXA's Tsukuba Space Center in Japan monitored the process, confirming 16 latches engaged successfully to secure the connection. Subsequently, utility umbilical cables were hooked up, providing electrical power, command and data handling, and thermal control services through the CBM, enabling initial pressurization checks and leak verification. These steps ensured the module's structural integrity and environmental readiness for crew access.
Activation and internal outfitting proceeded on flight days 6 and 7, June 6 and 7, 2008, after the crew ingress confirmed a safe internal atmosphere. STS-124 astronauts, including Hoshide and Ronald Garan, transferred eight equipment racks from the temporarily berthed Japanese Experiment Logistics Module (JEM ELM-PS) to the Kibo PM, configuring workstations and storage for future experiments. Power-up occurred in two phases: Channel B was activated on June 4 to support initial ingress, while Channel A was energized on June 5 by JAXA ground controllers, fully powering the module's environmental control and life support systems. As part of the outfitting, the Japanese Experiment Module Remote Manipulator System (JEMRMS) control station was installed inside the Kibo PM, with initial checkout and activation directed by ground teams to verify functionality prior to its external deployment. This phase marked the operational integration of Kibo as the ISS's largest laboratory module, expanding pressurized volume by about 50 percent.

Spacewalks and EVAs

EVA-1

The first extravehicular activity (EVA-1) of the STS-124 mission was conducted by lead spacewalker Michael E. Fossum (EV1) and Ronald J. Garan Jr. (EV2) on June 3, 2008, during flight day 4. The spacewalk lasted 6 hours and 48 minutes, beginning at 16:22 UTC and concluding at 23:10 UTC, with both astronauts egressing from the Quest airlock. Fossum, wearing the suit with red stripes, served as the primary operator for most tasks, while Garan provided support and handled tooling.
The primary objectives focused on preparing the Japanese Experiment Module Pressurized Module (Kibo PM) for its subsequent robotic unberthing and installation on the International Space Station (ISS), as well as supporting ongoing station maintenance. Fossum and Garan disconnected several electrical umbilicals and removed protective thermal covers and insulation from the Kibo PM's common berthing mechanism while it remained in Discovery's payload bay, ensuring readiness for transfer operations the following day. They also relocated the second Crew Equipment Translation Aid (CETA) cart from its position on the P1 truss to the nearby S1 truss, enhancing mobility for future EVAs along the starboard side of the station's truss structure. Additional tasks included retrieving the Materials International Space Station Experiment-6 (MISSE-6) passive materials exposure package from the exterior of the Quest airlock and stowing it in Discovery's payload bay for return to Earth, where it would undergo post-flight analysis of space environment effects on various materials.
A key maintenance objective involved inspecting the starboard Solar Alpha Rotary Joint (SARJ), which rotates the solar arrays including the P6 array to track the Sun for optimal power generation. The crew assessed visible damage on the SARJ race ring, including metallic debris and wear, and tested cleaning techniques using a specialized lint roller tool and wipes to mitigate potential interference with the joint's operation. This inspection indirectly supported the ammonia-based cooling system for the SARJ and adjacent trusses, as the joint's functionality relies on ammonia flow for thermal regulation, though no direct tank inspection or leaks were reported during EVA-1. All primary objectives were fully accomplished, with no significant technical issues encountered; the Extravehicular Mobility Units (EMUs) performed nominally throughout, maintaining stable thermal and power systems. The spacewalk advanced Kibo integration preparations and provided valuable data on SARJ maintenance techniques that informed future ISS repairs.

EVA-2

The second extravehicular activity (EVA-2) of the STS-124 mission was performed by Michael E. Fossum (EV1) in the red-striped suit and Ronald J. Garan Jr. (EV2) in the unmarked white suit on June 5, 2008, during flight day 6. The spacewalk began at 15:04 UTC and ended at 22:15 UTC, lasting 7 hours and 11 minutes.
Primary objectives included outfitting the exterior of the newly installed Kibo Pressurized Module (JEM PM) and preparing for subsequent maintenance tasks. Fossum and Garan installed two external television cameras on the JEM PM to support operations monitoring, removed thermal covers from the Japanese Experiment Module Remote Manipulator System (JEM RMS), and loosened bolts on the starboard Nitrogen Tank Assembly (NTA) to prepare for its replacement. They also retrieved a failing external camera from the P1 truss for return and analysis, installed an S-band antenna on the Kibo module, and transferred orbital replacement units to the External Stowage Platform 2. Additional work involved installing foot restraints and preparing the common berthing mechanism for the relocation of the Experiment Logistics Module-Pressurized Section (ELM-PS). All tasks were completed successfully, advancing the activation of Kibo and station infrastructure upgrades with no major issues reported.

EVA-3

The third extravehicular activity (EVA-3) of the STS-124 mission was performed by mission specialists Michael E. Fossum, serving as extravehicular crewmember 1 (EV1) in the red-striped suit, and Ronald J. Garan Jr., as EV2 in the unmarked white suit. The spacewalk commenced on June 8, 2008 (UTC), during flight day 9, at 13:55 UTC and concluded at 20:28 UTC, lasting 6 hours and 33 minutes. Building on the OBSS boom stowage achieved during EVA-1, this EVA focused on finalizing hardware integrations for the newly installed Kibo laboratory module while addressing station maintenance needs.
Primary objectives centered on replacing a depleted nitrogen tank assembly (NTA) on the starboard truss (S1) with a spare from the port truss (P1), completing outfitting of the Kibo Japanese Experiment Module (JEM), and reinstalling a repaired external television camera on the port truss (P1). Fossum and Garan began by coordinating with the ISS crew to maneuver the spare NTA using the station's Mobile Base System and Special Purpose Dexterous Manipulator (SPDM, or Dextre) for fluid line connections and secure attachment to the S1 truss, ensuring continued functionality of the station's thermal control system. Additional tasks involved removing thermal insulation covers and launch locks from the Kibo Remote Manipulator System (RMS) cameras and aft viewing window to enable operational checks, deploying micrometeoroid orbital debris shields (MODS) at Kibo's nadir and zenith berthing ports, and collecting particulate debris samples from the port Solar Alpha Rotary Joint (SARJ) using adhesive tape for ground analysis to assess potential wear issues. The crew also tightened a loose bolt on the repaired TV camera assembly, reinstalled the unit, and added a thermal cover to the Harmony module's Common Berthing Mechanism (CBM).
A notable event was a demonstration of the Simplified Aid for EVA Rescue (SAFER) jetpack system, where Fossum translated freely using the nitrogen-propelled thrusters to simulate a rescue scenario, validating its reliability for future untethered operations. All objectives were achieved without incident, with the EVA starting 30 minutes ahead of schedule and concluding efficiently. This spacewalk contributed to the mission's cumulative EVA total of 20 hours and 32 minutes across three outings, supporting the full activation of Kibo and enhancing ISS infrastructure longevity.

Undocking and Return

Undocking

The undocking of Space Shuttle Discovery from the International Space Station (ISS) occurred on June 11, 2008, during flight day 12 of the STS-124 mission, at 7:42 a.m. EDT (11:42 UTC). The process began with the opening of the docking hooks and latches via the Orbiter Docking System, followed by the activation of separation springs that provided an initial separation of approximately 0.6 meters (2 feet) to clear the docking mechanisms, after which Pilot Kenneth T. Ham maneuvered the shuttle backward using the reaction control system jets, deactivating them briefly during the closest approach to avoid plume impingement on the station before reactivating them once clear, increasing the distance to approximately 50 meters.
Following the initial separation, Ham performed a manual flyaround of the ISS, completing about 1.5 revolutions over approximately 45 minutes to allow the crew to capture high-resolution photographs and video of the expanded station configuration, including the newly installed Kibo Pressurized Module. The flyaround concluded without incident, and no re-docking was required as all mission objectives at the station had been met. Subsequent departure maneuvers included three Orbital Maneuvering System (OMS) burns designated SOI-1, SOI-2, and SOI-3, with the first burn executed at 9:20 a.m. EDT to increase separation to about 18.6 kilometers; the later burns further widened the distance to ensure safe independent orbits for both vehicles.
Crew exchanges had been completed earlier in the mission, prior to undocking preparations. Gregory E. Chamitoff, who arrived aboard Discovery, remained on the ISS as a flight engineer for Expedition 17, replacing Garrett E. Reisman, who transferred to the shuttle for his return to Earth. This handover ensured continuity for station operations following the delivery of the Kibo components.

Reentry and Landing

The reentry and landing of Space Shuttle Discovery during STS-124 commenced on June 14, 2008, marking flight day 15 of the mission. The deorbit burn was initiated at approximately 9:47 a.m. EDT from an orbital altitude of 220 kilometers (137 miles), utilizing the Orbital Maneuvering System engines for about three minutes to reduce velocity and drop the perigee into the atmosphere, setting the stage for controlled descent.
Entry interface occurred roughly 58 minutes after the deorbit burn, at an altitude of 400,000 feet (122 km) and a velocity of about 17,500 mph (28,200 km/h), where aerodynamic forces became significant and the orbiter's thermal protection system began absorbing intense heat exceeding 3,000F (1,650C). As Discovery plunged through the upper atmosphere at a 40-degree angle of attack, a plasma sheath formed around the vehicle due to ionized air, causing a communications blackout lasting approximately 15 minutes. The crew executed several roll reversals during this phase to modulate lift, drag, and heat distribution while maintaining the ground track toward the landing site.
The descent continued with the orbiter slowing to Mach 2.5 and reaching the Terminal Area Energy Management (TAEM) interface at around 85,000 feet (26 km), about 20 minutes prior to touchdown, where the angle of attack was reduced to 14 degrees and S-turns were performed to dissipate excess energy and align with the runway heading. Discovery then transitioned into the final approach, gliding unpowered over Florida as dual sonic booms announced its arrival. Main gear touchdown occurred at 11:15 a.m. EDT on Runway 15 of the Kennedy Space Center's Shuttle Landing Facility, followed by nose gear deployment and a rollout of approximately 9,300 feet (2,835 m), bringing the vehicle to a halt at 213 mph (343 km/h).
Following wheels stop, ground crews immediately initiated safing procedures, powering down systems, venting residual propellants, and securing hazardous materials to render the orbiter safe for close approach. The seven-member crew egressed via the crew access arm, underwent standard medical checks and a post-flight debrief to review reentry performance, and the vehicle was towed to the Orbiter Processing Facility for inspection and turnaround preparations. The total mission duration was 13 days, 18 hours, 13 minutes, and 7 seconds, encompassing 5.7 million statute miles (9.2 million km) over 219 orbits.

Supplementary Details

Wake-up Calls

The wake-up calls during STS-124 were part of NASA's longstanding tradition of playing music to awaken astronauts in space, a practice that originated with the Gemini 6 mission in 1965 when a parody of "Hello, Dolly!" was broadcast to the crew. This custom, continued through the Space Shuttle era, aimed to boost crew morale, foster a sense of connection to Earth, and often incorporated personal dedications from family, friends, or colleagues to personalize the daily routine. For STS-124, which launched on May 31, 2008, and lasted until June 14, the calls were typically initiated by Mission Control at the Johnson Space Center, featuring songs selected to reflect the crew members' backgrounds, interests, or cultural heritage, thereby enhancing team spirit during the demanding installation of the Kibo laboratory module.
The selections for STS-124 included a diverse array of genres, from rock and folk to theme music and military marches, with many tied to individual astronauts such as Commander Mark E. Kelly, Pilot Kenneth T. Ham, and Mission Specialists Michael E. Fossum, Ronald J. Garan Jr., Karen L. Nyberg, Gregory E. Chamitoff, Akihiko Hoshide (JAXA), and Garrett E. Reisman. These 14 calls, spanning Flight Days 1 through 14, often accompanied brief messages from loved ones, underscoring the emotional support provided to the crew while they conducted complex orbital operations. Notable examples highlighted cultural ties, such as Japanese tracks for Hoshide and an alma mater anthem for Fossum, illustrating how the tradition served not only to energize the team but also to celebrate their diverse identities amid the isolation of space.

Contingency Mission

The STS-124 mission employed a series of predefined abort modes during ascent to mitigate potential failures and ensure the safe recovery of the crew and orbiter. The Return to Launch Site (RTLS) mode was available for early ascent anomalies occurring shortly after solid rocket booster separation, involving the orbiter's main engines burning to empty the external tank, followed by a glide back to Kennedy Space Center Runway 15. Transoceanic Abort Landing (TAL) options targeted pre-designated sites in Europe and Africa, such as the primary site at Zaragoza Air Base in Spain or alternates at Morn Air Base in Spain and Istres-Le Tub Air Base in France, for mid-ascent issues after achieving sufficient velocity for cross-oceanic glide. Abort Once Around (AOA) provided a contingency for partial orbital insertion failures, allowing the orbiter to complete one full orbit before deorbiting to a landing site, typically Edwards Air Force Base or Kennedy Space Center. These intact abort profiles were designed to overlap in capability windows, with flight rules prioritizing the safest option based on real-time performance data.
For contingencies involving the International Space Station (ISS), the mission plan included provisions for the shuttle crew to seek safe haven aboard the station if reentry was deemed unfeasible due to thermal protection system (TPS) damage or other issues preventing a safe return. Post-undocking, Discovery maintained a distance of approximately 46 miles (74 km) from the ISS to enable focused heat shield inspections using onboard cameras and sensors, allowing ground teams to assess any damage before committing to reentry. The ISS was configured to support a stranded shuttle crew for at least 86 days through resource allocation, including power-down procedures for non-essential systems and logistics manifests coordinated by the ISS Program Office. In the event of prolonged stranding, rescue operations could leverage the subsequent STS-127 mission, designated as a potential Launch on Need (LON) flight to retrieve the crew.
Following the Columbia disaster, contingency planning for STS-124 incorporated enhanced TPS inspection protocols to proactively identify and address potential debris impacts. These updates, stemming from Columbia Accident Investigation Board recommendations, mandated on-orbit inspections on Flight Day 2 using a 50-foot extension boom equipped with cameras and laser sensors to scan the wing leading edges and nose cap for reinforced carbon-carbon (RCC) panel integrity. Additional measures included ascent imagery from cameras on the external tank and solid rocket boosters to monitor TPS underside exposure, integrated into the mission timeline alongside extravehicular activity (EVA) repair capabilities using silicone-based ablators for tile repairs. Non-destructive inspection techniques, such as thermography and ultrasound for RCC components, were prioritized in pre-launch evaluations, with real-time monitoring during rendezvous via ISS cameras to support damage assessment decisions. These protocols were fully embedded in STS-124 operations to align with return-to-flight safety standards, emphasizing prevention over reaction.
