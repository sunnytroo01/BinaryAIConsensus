 Salmonella is a genus of Gram-negative, rod-shaped, flagellated, facultatively anaerobic bacilli in the family Enterobacteriaceae, consisting of two speciesS. enterica and S. bongorimany serovars of which cause salmonellosis, a prevalent zoonotic infection transmitted primarily via the fecal-oral route through contaminated food or water.
S. enterica, subdivided into six subspecies with over 2,500 identified serovars, accounts for the majority of human pathogens, including non-typhoidal strains like S. Typhimurium and S. Enteritidis that typically induce self-limiting gastroenteritis, as well as typhoidal serovars such as S. Typhi responsible for systemic enteric fever.
In humans, infection manifests with acute symptoms including fever, abdominal cramps, diarrhea, nausea, and vomiting, onset occurring 6 to 72 hours post-exposure, though severe cases can lead to bacteremia, particularly in immunocompromised individuals or via invasive non-typhoidal strains.
As a leading cause of foodborne illness globally, Salmonella imposes substantial public health and economic burdens, with an estimated 93 million cases and 155,000 deaths annually, often linked to poultry, eggs, and reptiles as reservoirs.
The bacteria's resilience to environmental stresses, such as acidity and desiccation, facilitates survival in food chains, while emerging antimicrobial resistance complicates treatment, underscoring the need for vigilant surveillance and hygiene interventions.

Taxonomy and Nomenclature

Classification and Etymology

The genus Salmonella belongs to the domain Bacteria, phylum Pseudomonadota, class Gammaproteobacteria, order Enterobacterales, and family Enterobacteriaceae. This taxonomic placement reflects its Gram-negative, rod-shaped morphology and facultative anaerobic metabolism, characteristics shared with other enterobacteria.
The genus encompasses two species: Salmonella enterica and Salmonella bongori. S. enterica, which predominates in infections of warm-blooded hosts including humans and livestock, is subdivided into six subspecies: enterica, salamae, arizonae, diarizonae, houtenae, and indica. In contrast, S. bongori is primarily associated with cold-blooded reptiles and rarely causes disease in mammals. This species-level distinction arose from DNA hybridization studies in the 1980s, which revealed genetic divergence exceeding 5% between the two groups.
The etymology of Salmonella honors Daniel Elmer Salmon (18501914), a U.S. veterinary pathologist who led the Department of Agriculture's Bureau of Animal Industry and advanced understanding of bacterial diseases in livestock. The genus name was formally proposed in 1900 by Argentine bacteriologist Joseph Lignires. Notably, the first isolation of a Salmonella strain occurred in 1884 by Salmon's colleague Theobald Smith from the intestine of a pig afflicted with swine fever, though initial attribution favored Salmon. This naming reflects early 20th-century conventions prioritizing institutional leaders over primary discoverers in bacteriological taxonomy.

Serovars and Serotyping

Salmonella species are classified into serovars based on antigenic variation in their lipopolysaccharide O antigens and flagellar H antigens, as defined by the Kauffmann-White-Le Minor (KWL) scheme maintained by the World Health Organization's International Salmonella Reference Laboratories. This phenotypic serotyping identifies over 2,500 recognized serovars, with Salmonella enterica subspecies enterica accounting for approximately 99% of those pathogenic to humans and animals, while subspecies like arizonae and diarizonae are less common in clinical cases.
Serotyping relies on the detection of 67 known O antigens, which form the basis for serogroups (e.g., A through E and others), combined with phase-variable H antigens: phase 1 (specific) and phase 2 (group-specific or non-specific). Strains are designated by formulas such as O group:H1,H2 (e.g., S. enterica serovar Typhimurium as 4,5,12:i:1,2), with monophasic variants lacking phase 2 expression increasingly reported in outbreaks. Vi antigen, present in serovar Typhi and some Paratyphi, provides additional typing but is not universal. The scheme homologates serovars through agreement among reference centers like the Pasteur Institute, ensuring standardized nomenclature despite antigenic cross-reactions requiring absorption techniques for accuracy.

Emerging whole-genome sequencing (WGS)-based prediction of serovars from in silico antigen gene profiles offers higher resolution and objectivity, correlating >95% with traditional results and enabling outbreak tracing without culture dependency. In public health labs, WGS is increasingly integrated for the top serovars driving ~80% of human infections, such as Enteritidis, Newport, and Typhimurium.

Historical Discovery

Early Identification

The bacillus responsible for typhoid fever, now classified as Salmonella enterica serovar Typhi, was first observed by German pathologist Karl Joseph Eberth in 1880, who identified rod-shaped bacteria in the mesenteric lymph nodes and spleen of deceased typhoid patients during microscopic examinations. Eberth's findings, published in 1880, provided the initial histological evidence linking these bacilli to the disease, though cultivation was not achieved at that time. In 1884, Georg Gaffky, a colleague of Robert Koch, successfully isolated and cultured the organism from the spleen and bone marrow of typhoid victims, confirming its role as the etiologic agent through animal inoculation experiments that reproduced the disease in monkeys and rabbits.
Nontyphoidal Salmonella strains were identified shortly thereafter in veterinary contexts. In 1885, American bacteriologist Theobald Smith isolated a bacterium from the intestines of pigs afflicted with hog cholera (now recognized as Salmonella enterica serovar Choleraesuis), marking the first recovery of a Salmonella species from an animal host, though initially misattributed to the viral cause of the disease. This isolation, conducted under the supervision of Daniel E. Salmon at the U.S. Bureau of Animal Industry, highlighted the organism's association with porcine infections but did not immediately distinguish it from other enteric pathogens. The genus Salmonella was formally established in 1900 by Lignires to encompass these and related bacilli, honoring Salmon's contributions despite Smith's primary role in the 1885 isolation. These early identifications relied on morphological observations, culture techniques, and limited pathogenicity tests, laying the groundwork for serological differentiation developed later by White in 1926.

Key Milestones in Research

The Kauffmann-White scheme, introduced in 1934, established the foundational serological classification system for Salmonella serovars, initially cataloging 44 types based on lipopolysaccharide (O) and flagellar (H) antigens to differentiate strains and support epidemiological investigations. This antigen-based framework, refined over subsequent decades, expanded to encompass over 2,500 serovars by 2007, enabling precise identification of pathogenic variants despite challenges from antigenic variability and polyphyletic origins in some serovars.
In the 1990s, the identification of Salmonella pathogenicity islands (SPIs)genomic regions acquired via horizontal gene transferrevealed discrete loci encoding critical virulence machinery, including the type III secretion system (T3SS) in SPI-1 for epithelial cell invasion and SPI-2 for intracellular replication and immune evasion within host macrophages. These discoveries, building on earlier observations of non-homologous genomic insertions, underscored the role of mobile genetic elements in Salmonella's adaptation from environmental niches to systemic infection, with SPIs present across diverse serovars but varying in content and arrangement.
The early 2000s brought transformative genomic insights, with the first complete genome sequences of Salmonella strainssuch as serovar Typhi in 2001exposing the bacterium's ~4.85.2 Mb chromosome, ~50% G+C content, and accessory elements like plasmids contributing to virulence and metabolic versatility. Comparative analyses of these sequences illuminated core genomic conservation alongside serovar-specific adaptations, including pseudogene accumulation in host-restricted lineages like S. Typhi, which correlates with reduced metabolic flexibility but enhanced invasiveness.
By the 2010s, whole-genome sequencing scaled dramatically, amassing over 340,000 Salmonella genomes in public databases by 2021, which facilitated real-time outbreak tracing, source attribution via multilocus sequence typing, and detection of recombination hotspots driving serovar diversification. Concurrently, ancient DNA recovery from human remains yielded eight Salmonella genomes up to 6,500 years old by 2020, evidencing early zoonotic jumps from livestock and prehistoric host shifts that predate modern pandemics, thus reframing Salmonella's evolutionary timeline beyond 19th-century isolations.
Antibiotic resistance research paralleled these advances, tracing plasmid-mediated multidrug phenotypes to the 1960s amid widespread subtherapeutic use in agriculture, with genomic surveillance now linking resistance cassettes in integrons and transposons to clonal expansions like monophasic S. Typhimurium variants. These milestones collectively shifted Salmonella studies from phenotypic description to mechanistic and predictive models, emphasizing causal genomic drivers over correlative epidemiology.

Biological Characteristics

Morphology, Physiology, and Growth

Salmonella species consist of straight, rod-shaped bacilli that are Gram-negative, typically measuring 25 m in length and 0.51.5 m in width. They exhibit motility through peritrichous flagella and lack the ability to form endospores. 
Physiologically, Salmonella are facultative anaerobes capable of aerobic respiration in oxygen-rich environments via oxidative phosphorylation for ATP production, while shifting to fermentation or anaerobic respiration in low-oxygen conditions. They are mesophilic organisms with optimal growth at 3537C, though viable proliferation occurs across 546C, enabling persistence in diverse ecological niches. Tolerance to pH ranges from 3.89.5 supports survival in acidic food matrices and host environments, with peak activity at neutral pH of 77.5.
Growth demands include simple carbon and nitrogen sources, such as glucose fermentation producing acid and gas, alongside minerals like phosphorus and sulfur; nutrient limitation restricts replication but does not preclude long-term viability. Under favorable conditions, doubling times approximate 2030 minutes, facilitating rapid colonization during infection. These traits underpin Salmonella's adaptability as an enteric pathogen, thriving in both extracellular and intracellular host settings despite immune pressures.

Genetics and Genomic Features

Salmonella species possess a single circular chromosome typically ranging from 4.3 to 5.0 megabases (Mb) in size, with an average of approximately 5 Mb across strains. The genome encodes around 4,000 to 5,000 genes, including a core genome of about 3,635 genes shared among diverse S. enterica isolates, while the pangenome expands to over 13,000 genes due to accessory elements acquired via horizontal gene transfer. The G+C content is approximately 52%, reflecting adaptation to enteric environments.
Key genomic features include multiple Salmonella pathogenicity islands (SPIs), which are chromosomally integrated clusters of virulence genes often acquired through horizontal transfer. SPI-1, spanning about 40 kilobases (kb), encodes a type III secretion system (T3SS-1) essential for host cell invasion, comprising 39 genes for structural components, effectors, and regulators. SPI-2, similarly HGT-derived, supports intracellular survival and systemic spread by facilitating vacuole modification and nutrient acquisition within host cells. Additional SPIs (e.g., SPI-3 to SPI-7) vary by serovar; for instance, SPI-7 in certain pathogenic strains functions as an integrative conjugative element carrying toxin-antitoxin systems and adhesins. These islands, ranging from 10 to 200 kb, contribute to serovar-specific pathogenicity, with typhoidal serovars like S. Typhi retaining distinct SPIs absent in nontyphoidal ones. 
Plasmids and prophages further enhance genomic plasticity and virulence. Many strains harbor plasmids such as pSLT in S. Typhimurium, which carry genes for antimicrobial resistance and metabolic functions. Phage-plasmids, hybrid elements with both lytic and replicative capacities, are prevalent in serovars like S. Typhimurium ST19 and ST34, detected in up to 226 of 47,784 isolates, facilitating gene mobilization including resistance determinants. Temperate phages integrate as prophages, altering host fitness and contributing to diversity; for example, P1-like phage-plasmids enable lysogenic conversion and horizontal spread of traits like biofilm formation. Regions of genomic plasticity (RGPs) organize flexible genes, driving evolution across lineages and enabling adaptation to hosts or antibiotics.
Genetic variation underlies serovar diversity, with over 2,600 S. enterica serotypes distinguished by phase-variable loci for O- and H-antigens encoded in the rfb and fli operons, respectively. Whole-genome sequencing reveals clade-specific signatures, such as pseudogene accumulation in host-adapted serovars like S. Pullorum, reducing genome size slightly while enhancing niche specialization. Antimicrobial resistance genes, often on plasmids or SPIs, cluster in up to 29 per genome in multidrug-resistant isolates, underscoring the role of mobile elements in clinical persistence.

Pathogenicity Mechanisms

Virulence Factors and Infection Entry

Salmonella species possess an array of virulence factors that enable host cell invasion, primarily orchestrated by the type III secretion system 1 (T3SS-1) encoded within Salmonella Pathogenicity Island 1 (SPI-1). This needle-like apparatus injects effector proteins, such as SopE, SopE2, SipA, and SipC, directly into host epithelial cells to manipulate actin cytoskeleton dynamics, inducing membrane ruffling characteristic of the trigger invasion mechanism. SopE and SopE2 function as guanine nucleotide exchange factors (GEFs) for host Rho GTPases Cdc42 and Rac1, promoting rapid bacterial uptake into Salmonella-containing vacuoles (SCVs), while SipA and SipC stabilize and bundle actin filaments to facilitate entry. Adhesins like the SPI-4-encoded giant protein SiiE and fimbrial structures (e.g., type 1 fimbriae via FimH) enhance initial attachment to the apical surface of polarized intestinal epithelial cells, priming T3SS-1-dependent invasion.
Infection typically initiates via oral ingestion of contaminated food or water, with bacteria enduring gastric acidity through acid tolerance responses regulated by systems like PhoP/PhoQ. Upon reaching the small intestine, Salmonella preferentially targets microfold (M) cells overlying Peyer's patches for transcytosis, exploiting these antigen-sampling cells for barrier crossing without overt epithelial invasion; SopB effector modulates NF-B and Wnt signaling to induce M-cell differentiation in follicle-associated epithelium. Alternatively, direct invasion of enterocytes occurs via the trigger mechanism, where T3SS-1 effectors trigger extensive cytoskeletal rearrangements and inflammation, or less commonly via a zipper mechanism involving outer membrane proteins like Rck, which bind epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) to induce localized actin polymerization.
For nontyphoidal serovars like S. Typhimurium, these factors drive localized gastroenteritis by eliciting neutrophil influx and fluid secretion, whereas typhoidal serovars (S. Typhi, S. Paratyphi) incorporate additional elements like Vi capsular polysaccharide to dampen initial immune detection during systemic dissemination from the gut. Translocation often involves "Trojan horse" uptake by CD18-expressing dendritic cells or macrophages in the lamina propria, which ferry bacteria to draining lymph nodes and beyond, evading extracellular defenses. Mutants lacking SPI-1 or key effectors exhibit markedly reduced invasion efficiency in cell culture and animal models, underscoring their causal role in pathogenesis.

Resistance to Host Immune Responses

Salmonella species resist host immune responses through a multifaceted arsenal of virulence factors, predominantly type III secretion system (T3SS) effectors encoded on Salmonella pathogenicity islands (SPIs), which enable intracellular persistence and subversion of both innate and adaptive immunity. These mechanisms allow the bacterium to survive phagocytosis by macrophages, modulate inflammatory signaling to limit excessive host responses that could lead to clearance, and impair antigen presentation to evade T-cell detection. 
A primary strategy involves intracellular survival within professional phagocytes, where Salmonella modifies the Salmonella-containing vacuole (SCV) to avoid fusion with lysosomes and oxidative killing. Upon uptake, SPI-2 T3SS effectors such as SifA stabilize the SCV by anchoring it to microtubules via interaction with kinesin, while SseF and SseG recruit Rab GTPases to position the vacuole near the Golgi apparatus, facilitating nutrient acquisition and replication. Additional effectors like SseJ disrupt lipid homeostasis to exclude host NADPH oxidase components, thereby evading reactive oxygen species, and PipB2 promotes Salmonella-induced filament (Sif) formation for vacuole expansion. In S. Typhi, the Vi capsular polysaccharide further shields the bacterium from complement-mediated opsonization and Toll-like receptor (TLR) recognition during systemic dissemination. 
Salmonella also actively dampens innate inflammatory responses to prevent pathogen clearance while promoting its own dissemination. Effectors translocated by both SPI-1 and SPI-2 T3SS interfere with NF-B and MAPK pathways; for example, AvrA functions as a deubiquitinase to stabilize IB, inhibiting NF-B nuclear translocation and pro-inflammatory cytokine production like IL-8, while SpvC dephosphorylates MAPKs to suppress IL-1 release. SspH2 ubiquitinates TRAF2 to block NF-B activation, and SseL deubiquitinates host proteins to inhibit autophagy-mediated degradation. These actions collectively reduce pyroptosis and inflammasome activation (e.g., via NLRP3 and NLRC4 inhibition by SpvC), allowing controlled inflammation that recruits additional host cells for infection without triggering lethal host responses. 
To counter adaptive immunity, Salmonella impairs antigen processing and presentation. The SPI-2 effector SteD promotes ubiquitination of MHC class II molecules via recruitment of the host E3 ligase MARCH8, leading to their lysosomal degradation and reduced CD4+ T-cell priming by dendritic cells. SifA disrupts SCV integrity in a manner that limits cytosolic antigen exposure, while SseI inhibits dendritic cell migration to lymph nodes, hindering T-cell activation. In chronic infections, these strategies, combined with induction of host IL-10 and nitric oxide, foster immune tolerance and persistence. 

Clinical Manifestations

Nontyphoidal Salmonellosis

Nontyphoidal salmonellosis typically presents as acute gastroenteritis, characterized by sudden onset of diarrhea, abdominal cramps, and fever. Symptoms usually begin 12 to 72 hours after ingestion of contaminated food or water, though the incubation period can range from 6 hours to 6 days. Diarrhea is often watery but may be bloody or mucoid in some cases, accompanied by nausea, vomiting, and headache, though these occur less frequently than the primary triad of fever, cramps, and loose stools. Fever is nearly always present and can reach 3839C (100.4102.2F).
In immunocompetent individuals, the illness is generally self-limited, lasting 4 to 7 days without specific antimicrobial therapy. Dehydration from prolonged diarrhea represents the primary risk during this period, particularly in children and the elderly, and may necessitate supportive rehydration. Stool cultures or antigen tests confirm the diagnosis, revealing nontyphoidal serotypes such as Salmonella Enteritidis or Salmonella Typhimurium, which predominate in human infections.
Complications arise in approximately 5% of cases, primarily involving bacteremia or focal extraintestinal infections, with higher incidence among infants under 1 year, adults over 70, and those with immunosuppression such as HIV or sickle cell disease. Invasive disease can manifest as septicemia, with secondary seeding to sites like bones (osteomyelitis), joints (septic arthritis), meninges (meningitis), or endothelium (endocarditis or mycotic aneurysms). In severe bacteremic cases, mortality rates can exceed 20% in vulnerable populations without prompt intervention, though overall case-fatality for uncomplicated gastroenteritis remains under 1%. Reactive arthritis may develop post-infection in genetically susceptible individuals, presenting as sterile joint inflammation weeks after resolution of acute symptoms.

Typhoidal Salmonellosis

Typhoidal salmonellosis encompasses systemic infections caused by Salmonella enterica serovar Typhi (S. Typhi) or serovars Paratyphi A, B, or C, manifesting as typhoid fever or paratyphoid fever, respectively. Unlike nontyphoidal salmonellosis, which primarily involves localized gastrointestinal symptoms, typhoidal forms invade the bloodstream, leading to a disseminated infection that targets the reticuloendothelial system, including the liver, spleen, and bone marrow. The incubation period typically ranges from 6 to 30 days, during which bacteria multiply in the intestinal tract before entering the blood via Peyer's patches in the ileum.
Initial symptoms often begin insidiously with a stepwise increase in fever reaching 3940C (103104F), accompanied by headache, malaise, anorexia, and myalgias. Gastrointestinal features include constipation (more common early on, especially in adults) or less frequently diarrhea, alongside relative bradycardia (pulse-temperature dissociation), coated tongue, and abdominal tenderness. A characteristic rash of rose-colored maculopapular spots (rose spots) may appear on the trunk in 1020% of cases during the second week, fading on pressure. Hepatomegaly and splenomegaly occur in about 50% of patients, reflecting systemic spread. Paratyphoid infections tend to be milder, with lower fever peaks and fewer complications compared to typhoid.
The untreated disease progresses in stages over 34 weeks: the first week features rising fever and toxemia; the second involves peak symptoms with potential delirium; the third risks complications such as intestinal perforation (due to hyperplasia and necrosis of Peyer's patches), hemorrhage, or encephalopathy; and the fourth may show defervescence but with prolonged convalescence. Severe cases can include myocarditis, pneumonia, or osteomyelitis. Without antibiotics, mortality reaches 1020%, primarily from perforation or shock, though fatality drops below 1% with prompt treatment. Carriage states persist in 14% of cases, particularly gallbladder colonization in chronic carriers, enabling ongoing transmission.

Epidemiology and Transmission

Global Burden and Risk Factors

Nontyphoidal Salmonella (NTS) infections, primarily manifesting as gastroenteritis, contribute substantially to the global burden of foodborne illness, with estimates indicating approximately 93.8 million cases and 155,000 deaths annually, predominantly in low- and middle-income countries where invasive disease is more prevalent. Invasive NTS (iNTS) accounts for around 535,000 illnesses and 77,500 deaths yearly, with over 78% of cases and nearly all deaths occurring in children under five in sub-Saharan Africa and other high-burden regions, driven by factors such as malnutrition, malaria co-infection, and HIV prevalence. In high-income settings like the United States, NTS causes about 1.35 million infections annually, mostly foodborne, resulting in roughly 26,500 hospitalizations and 420 deaths.
Typhoidal Salmonella (enteric fever, caused by S. Typhi and S. Paratyphi), transmitted mainly through contaminated water and food in areas with poor sanitation, imposes a distinct burden estimated at 9.3 million cases and 107,500 deaths globally in 2021, with the highest incidence in South Asia, Southeast Asia, and sub-Saharan Africa.00462-0/fulltext) Mortality from typhoidal strains disproportionately affects children and young adults in endemic regions, where access to diagnostics and antibiotics is limited, contrasting with lower incidence in industrialized nations due to improved water treatment and sanitation.
Key risk factors for Salmonella infection include consumption of contaminated foods such as undercooked poultry, eggs, meat, unpasteurized dairy, and fresh produce, which serve as primary vehicles due to fecal-oral transmission from animal reservoirs. Vulnerable populations encompass young children under five, elderly individuals over 65, pregnant people, and those with weakened immune systems (e.g., due to HIV, chemotherapy, or chronic conditions), who face higher rates of severe outcomes like bacteremia and hospitalization. Additional risks arise from direct contact with carrier animals such as reptiles (turtles, lizards), amphibians, poultry, or pets shedding the bacteria, particularly hazardous for infants; international travel to endemic areas; and inadequate hygiene practices in food preparation or water treatment.)

Sources, Outbreaks, and Recent Incidents

Salmonella bacteria are primarily spread through contaminated foodespecially undercooked poultry, eggs, meat, and unpasteurized milkor direct contact with infected animals, occurring via fecal-oral transmission from animal reservoirs, with poultry and eggs serving as leading vehicles due to widespread shedding in avian intestines. For instance, about 1 in every 25 packages of chicken at the grocery store is contaminated with Salmonella. Other common sources include undercooked beef, pork, unpasteurized milk, and contaminated fresh produce like tomatoes, sprouts, and onions, where irrigation with untreated water or manure fertilization introduces the pathogen. Non-food sources encompass direct contact with infected reptiles, amphibians, backyard poultry, or their feces, as well as contaminated pet food and environmental exposures like soil or recreational water. 
Major historical outbreaks underscore these pathways. Between 1973 and 2011, beef implicated in 96 U.S. outbreaks caused 3,684 illnesses, often linked to ground beef processing cross-contamination. The 1985 Illinois milk outbreak, stemming from post-pasteurization contamination at a dairy plant, infected over 200,000 people nationwide, marking the largest single-source Salmonella event in U.S. history. From 1985 to 1991, S. Enteritidis outbreaks tied to shell eggs totaled 380 incidents, sickening 13,056 and killing 50, driven by infected laying hens and inadequate cooking. The 20082009 peanut butter outbreak, originating from a single manufacturer's facility with poor sanitation, affected 714 confirmed cases across 46 states, resulting in 8 deaths and prompting facility shutdowns. Produce-related events, such as the 1990 multi-state tomato outbreak, highlighted risks from field contamination, infecting hundreds. 
Recent incidents reflect persistent challenges in supply chains and animal contact. In 2023, a Salmonella outbreak linked to diced onions sickened dozens across multiple states, traced to processing facilities. By August 2025, an egg outbreak from Country Eggs, LLC distribution infected 95 people in 14 states, with illnesses onset from early July, leading to recalls and emphasizing shell egg risks despite pasteurization efforts. In September 2025, ready-to-eat home delivery meals from Metabolic Meals caused 16 illnesses in 10 states, with onset dates from late July, under investigation for specific ingredients like cheese tortellini. Ongoing FDA probes as of October 2025 include Salmonella Richmond in 1,345 cases and S. Lomalinda in another cluster, often tied to imported produce or processed foods. Backyard poultry and reptile exposures continue driving sporadic cases, with CDC reporting persistent clusters in 2025.

Diagnosis and Detection

Laboratory and Molecular Methods

Laboratory diagnosis of Salmonella infections primarily relies on culture-based methods from clinical specimens such as stool for nontyphoidal salmonellosis and blood or bone marrow for typhoidal forms, as these yield viable isolates for confirmation and antimicrobial susceptibility testing. Fecal specimens are preferred for diarrheal cases, with pre-enrichment in non-selective broth followed by selective enrichment in media like selenite or tetrathionate broth to enhance recovery. Isolation occurs on differential agars such as xylose-lysine-deoxycholate (XLD) or Hektoen enteric (HE) agar, where Salmonella typically forms colorless or red colonies with black centers due to hydrogen sulfide production. 
Confirmation involves biochemical tests, including triple sugar iron agar reactions (acid butt, alkaline slant, H2S positive) and motility, alongside serological typing using the Kauffmann-White scheme for O (somatic) and H (flagellar) antigens. Public health laboratories often submit isolates to reference centers like the CDC for pulsed-field gel electrophoresis (PFGE) or whole-genome sequencing (WGS) subtyping to link cases in outbreaks, achieving serotyping for up to 90% of prevalent U.S. strains. Culture remains the gold standard due to its ability to assess viability and resistance, though it requires 48-72 hours.
Molecular methods, including real-time PCR assays targeting genes like invA or ttrRSBCA, enable rapid detection within 18-24 hours from enriched samples, offering sensitivities of 95-100% and specificities of 97-100% in clinical settings. Loop-mediated isothermal amplification (LAMP) provides an alternative for resource-limited environments, amplifying DNA without thermal cycling for field-deployable detection. Culture-independent diagnostic tests (CIDTs) are increasingly used in surveillance, but positive results typically require reflex culturing for isolate viability and epidemiological typing, as molecular signals may persist post-treatment. Whole-genome sequencing has emerged for precise serovar prediction and virulence profiling, supporting outbreak investigations with higher resolution than traditional methods.

Treatment Approaches

Antibiotic Therapy and Supportive Care

Most cases of nontyphoidal Salmonella gastroenteritis resolve without antibiotics, as antimicrobial therapy does not shorten the duration of diarrhea in healthy adults and may prolong fecal shedding of the organism. Supportive care focuses on preventing dehydration through oral rehydration solutions containing electrolytes and glucose, with intravenous fluids reserved for severe cases exhibiting significant volume depletion or inability to tolerate oral intake. Anti-diarrheal agents like loperamide are generally discouraged, as they can prolong illness by delaying clearance of the pathogen.
Antibiotics are indicated for nontyphoidal Salmonella infections in patients with severe symptoms, such as high fever, bloody diarrhea, or prolonged illness exceeding 7 days; those with bacteremia or focal extraintestinal infections; and high-risk groups including infants under 3 months, individuals over 50 years, or immunocompromised hosts. Empiric therapy typically involves fluoroquinolones such as ciprofloxacin (500 mg orally twice daily for 5-7 days), azithromycin (1 g orally on day 1 followed by 500 mg daily for 6 days), or third-generation cephalosporins like ceftriaxone (2 g intravenously daily for 7-10 days in hospitalized patients). Treatment should be guided by susceptibility testing, as resistance to older agents like ampicillin and trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole is common.
For typhoidal Salmonella (Salmonella Typhi or Paratyphi), antibiotic therapy is essential to reduce mortality and complications, with prompt initiation shortening the clinical course from weeks to days. Recommended regimens include oral azithromycin (1 g daily for 5-7 days) or ciprofloxacin (500 mg twice daily for 7-10 days) for susceptible strains; ceftriaxone (2 g intravenously every 12-24 hours for 10-14 days) is preferred for severe or hospitalized cases. In regions with extensively drug-resistant (XDR) strains, resistant to fluoroquinolones, third-generation cephalosporins, and ampicillin, alternatives such as azithromycin or carbapenems like meropenem (1 g intravenously every 8 hours) are used, though options remain limited. Supportive measures mirror those for nontyphoidal disease, emphasizing fluid and electrolyte replacement, nutritional support, and monitoring for complications like intestinal perforation. Chronic carriers may require prolonged therapy (4-6 weeks) with ciprofloxacin, but eradication success rates vary and cholecystectomy is considered for persistent cases.

Antimicrobial Resistance

Mechanisms and Emergence

Antimicrobial resistance in Salmonella primarily arises through acquired mechanisms rather than intrinsic ones, enabling the bacterium to withstand antibiotics via genetic adaptations that either prevent drug entry, expel agents from the cell, modify targets, or degrade compounds. Enzymatic inactivation represents a dominant strategy, where Salmonella produces hydrolases such as beta-lactamases that cleave the beta-lactam ring in penicillins and cephalosporins, or aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes that phosphorylate, acetylate, or adenylate aminoglycosides like gentamicin and kanamycin, rendering them inactive. Kanamycin resistance is frequently plasmid-mediated, including through ColE1-like plasmids carrying resistance genes, commonly observed in food animal isolates of serovars such as Typhimurium and Newport. Efflux pumps, particularly from the resistance-nodulation-division (RND) family like AcrAB-TolC, actively export multiple antibioticsincluding tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones, and chloramphenicolout of the cytoplasm, reducing intracellular concentrations below lethal thresholds. 
Target modification further contributes to resistance, as seen in ribosomal alterations via methylation or protection proteins that shield rRNA from macrolides and lincosamides, or mutations in DNA gyrase and topoisomerase IV that confer fluoroquinolone tolerance by decreasing drug binding affinity. Reduced outer membrane permeability, achieved through downregulation or mutation of porins like OmpD and OmpF, limits entry of hydrophilic antibiotics such as beta-lactams. These mechanisms often cluster on mobile genetic elements, including plasmids, transposons, and integrons, facilitating rapid dissemination across Salmonella serovars and even to other Enterobacteriaceae. For instance, the plasmid-mediated qnr genes and aac(6')-Ib-cr enzyme enable low-level fluoroquinolone resistance, which can escalate under selective pressure.
Emergence of resistance in Salmonella stems from evolutionary pressures exerted by widespread antibiotic use, particularly in agriculture, where subtherapeutic doses in livestock select for resistant strains that enter human food chains via contaminated meat, eggs, and produce. Horizontal gene transfer accelerates this process, with conjugative plasmids transferring resistance cassettes between bacteria in the gut microbiome or environments like wastewater and soil; for example, IncHI2 plasmids carrying extended-spectrum beta-lactamase (ESBL) genes have proliferated in nontyphoidal Salmonella isolates since the 1990s. Chromosomal mutations, such as those in gyrA for quinolone resistance, arise spontaneously at rates of 10^{-8} to 10^{-10} per cell division but fix in populations under antibiotic exposure, often co-selected with fitness-cost-minimizing compensatory mutations.
Temporal trends underscore this emergence: fluoroquinolone resistance in food-derived Salmonella surged from 0.18% in the early 1990s to 12.11% by the 2020s, driven by veterinary overuse, while multidrug resistance in nontyphoidal strains approached 10% for ciprofloxacin by 2017 in human cases. In Salmonella Typhimurium and Enteritidis, resistance to critically important drugs like third-generation cephalosporins has risen due to blaCMY-2 genes on plasmids, often linked to poultry production. These dynamics highlight how anthropogenic antibiotic pressures, rather than natural host adaptation alone, propel resistance evolution, with global surveillance revealing post-2000 increases across most classes except polymyxins.

Drivers and Public Health Challenges

The primary drivers of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) in Salmonella species stem from selective pressure exerted by widespread antibiotic use in agriculture, particularly in poultry and livestock production, where these pathogens are endemic. Intensive farming practices, including prophylactic and growth-promoting antibiotic administration, favor the emergence and dissemination of resistant strains through horizontal gene transfer via plasmids and integrons. Human antibiotic misuse, such as non-prescription access and overuse for viral or non-bacterial illnesses, further amplifies resistance in nontyphoidal Salmonella (NTS), though agricultural sources predominate in zoonotic transmission chains. Additional contributors include socioeconomic factors, urbanization, climate variability, and inadequate sanitation, which facilitate environmental persistence and cross-species spread.
Post-2000 trends show elevated resistance prevalence in food-associated Salmonella isolates across antimicrobial classes, excluding polymyxins and lincosamides, driven by global increases in antibiotic consumption volumes exceeding 100,000 tons annually in animal agriculture. The CDC's National Antimicrobial Resistance Monitoring System (NARMS) tests Salmonella isolates for susceptibility to kanamycin and other antimicrobials, revealing variable susceptibility with documented resistance in some strains, particularly in serovars like Infantis, Newport, and Typhimurium. Resistance is often plasmid-mediated and more common in animal- and food-related isolates, though rates can be low (e.g., 0.2% in some backyard poultry outbreaks). Regulatory gaps, such as incomplete bans on non-therapeutic use in regions like the European Union (fully implemented by 2022) versus ongoing practices in the United States and developing countries, exacerbate clonal expansion of multidrug-resistant (MDR) serovars like S. Typhimurium and S. Infantis. 
Public health challenges arise from escalating MDR infections, which complicate treatment and elevate mortality risks, particularly in vulnerable groups like children under five and immunocompromised individuals, where NTS bacteremia fatality rates can reach 20-25% in low-resource settings. Between 2018 and 2023, AMR rose in over 40% of monitored pathogen-antibiotic combinations globally, with Salmonella classified as a high-priority pathogen by the World Health Organization due to its zoonotic burden in low- and middle-income countries. 
Surveillance limitations hinder timely detection, as asymptomatic animal carriers and contaminated food imports propagate resistant strains via international trade, evidenced by outbreaks of pESI-plasmid-harboring MDR S. Muenchen in poultry products from 2020-2023. Treatment failures due to resistance to first-line agents like fluoroquinolones (prevalence up to 60% in some poultry isolates) necessitate broader-spectrum alternatives, increasing healthcare costs and secondary resistance risks. Environmental contamination from agricultural runoff compounds these issues, underscoring the need for integrated One Health interventions despite uneven global implementation.

Prevention Strategies

Food Safety and Hygiene Practices

Preventing Salmonella infections through food safety and hygiene practices primarily involves disrupting bacterial transmission in the kitchen and during food preparation, as the bacterium survives on surfaces and multiplies in the temperature danger zone between 40F and 140F. The U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) outlines four core steps: clean, separate, cook, and chill, which empirical data from outbreak investigations link to reduced incidence when followed. 
Hand hygiene is foundational, with frequent washing using soap and water for at least 20 seconds before handling food, after using the restroom, and post-contact with raw meats or poultry, as unwashed hands can transfer fecal-oral pathogens directly. Cleaning surfaces, cutting boards, and utensils with hot soapy water or sanitizers after raw food contact prevents cross-contamination, supported by studies showing Salmonella persistence on untreated surfaces for days. 
Separating raw animal products like poultry, eggs, and meats from ready-to-eat foods avoids transfer of viable bacteria, as raw poultry harbors Salmonella in up to 30% of samples per FDA surveys. Never reuse plates or utensils for cooked food without washing.
Cooking to internal temperatures that kill Salmonella165F for poultry, 160F for ground meats, and firm yolks for eggsensures lethality, as heat above 160F rapidly inactivates the pathogen per thermal death time data. Use a food thermometer for verification, avoiding reliance on color alone.
Chilling perishable foods promptly below 40F inhibits growth, with refrigeration preventing multiplication during storage; never thaw at room temperature. Additional measures include avoiding unpasteurized dairy and raw sprouts, and washing produce under running water, though these reduce but do not eliminate surface bacteria. These practices, when combined, have demonstrably lowered Salmonella cases in controlled studies and public health campaigns.

Vaccination and Control in Agriculture

Vaccination programs targeting Salmonella in livestock aim to reduce bacterial colonization, shedding, and transmission to humans via food products, with poultry representing the primary focus due to its role as a major reservoir. Live attenuated vaccines, such as those based on S. Enteritidis or S. Typhimurium, have demonstrated efficacy in decreasing intestinal loads and clinical disease in broilers and layers when administered via spray, drinking water, or in-ovo methods. In breeder flocks, vaccination has lowered Salmonella prevalence in progeny broilers from 33.5% to 18.1%, highlighting maternal antibody transfer as a mechanism for herd-level protection.
In swine production, bivalent vaccines against S. Choleraesuis and S. Typhimuriumadministered orally or intramuscularlyreduce pneumonia, diarrhea, septicemia, and mortality, with single-dose protocols showing significant attenuation of post-challenge symptoms like fever and fecal shedding in weaned pigs. For cattle, siderophore receptor and porin (SRP) vaccines target S. Newport by inducing antibodies that limit bacterial iron acquisition, applicable to healthy animals six months or older to prevent disease in high-risk environments like dairies. These veterinary vaccines generally prioritize safety and broad serovar coverage, though efficacy varies with strain attenuation and host age, necessitating integration with monitoring for optimal outcomes.
Beyond vaccination, control in agriculture emphasizes biosecurity to block introduction and amplification: strict isolation of new animals for at least two weeks, perimeter fencing, and restricted access can reduce facility contamination by up to 80% in poultry operations. Feed management includes pelleting at temperatures exceeding 80C or chemical treatments like organic acids to eliminate Salmonella, preventing mill cross-contamination through segregated "clean" and "dirty" zones. In cattle and swine herds, hygiene protocolssuch as color-coded equipment to avoid cross-contamination and routine disinfectioncomplement vaccination by minimizing environmental persistence, though persistent challenges arise from wildlife vectors and asymptomatic carriers. Overall, integrated approaches combining vaccines with these measures have lowered pre-harvest prevalence, but gaps in universal adoption and emerging resistance underscore the need for ongoing surveillance.

Host Adaptation and Evolution

Adaptation to Human and Animal Hosts

Salmonella enterica exhibits a spectrum of host adaptation, ranging from broad-host-range serovars that infect diverse species to host-restricted variants specialized for humans or specific animals. Subspecies I, predominant in causing infections in warm-blooded hosts, evolved from ancestors adapted to cold-blooded vertebrates, enabling expansion into mammals and birds through mechanisms like enhanced intracellular survival and immune evasion. Host specificity correlates with genomic features: broad-range serovars such as Typhimurium maintain larger genomes with fewer pseudogenes, supporting versatility across hosts, while restricted serovars accumulate pseudogenes and undergo chromosomal rearrangements that streamline metabolism and virulence for particular niches.
In humans, serovars like Typhi and Paratyphi A, B, and C demonstrate strict host adaptation, causing systemic typhoid fever without significant animal reservoirs. S. Typhi acquired unique virulence factors, including the typhoid toxin, which targets human-specific pathways such as cytoskeletal rearrangement and polarized epithelial responses, facilitating systemic dissemination via macrophages. Genomic analyses reveal extensive pseudogene formation in Typhiover 200 compared to fewer in broad-range ancestorsreducing metabolic flexibility but enhancing human-specific colonization, with chromosomal inversions further altering gene expression for intracellular persistence. Ancient genomes indicate Typhi's human adaptation emerged around 6,500 years ago amid pastoralist societies, linked to pseudogenization of non-essential genes and acquisition of human-restricted effectors.
Animal-adapted serovars illustrate parallel evolutionary paths, with S. Gallinarum restricted to poultry via loss of motility genes and enhanced biofilm formation for avian gut persistence, and S. Dublin specialized for cattle through upregulated carbohydrate metabolism and lipopolysaccharide modifications that evade bovine immunity. These adaptations often involve in-host evolution, such as point mutations in regulatory genes during chronic infections, reducing invasiveness in alternative hosts. Broad-range serovars like Typhimurium exploit conserved host pathways across species, relying on type III secretion systems for broad epithelial invasion, but pathovariants can narrow range via mutations favoring specific immune modulation, as seen in avian isolates. Overall, adaptation hinges on balancing virulence trade-offs: host restriction enhances transmission efficiency in reservoirs but limits spillover, driven by selective pressures from host immunity and ecology.

Genomic and Ancient Evidence

Genomic sequencing of Salmonella enterica subspecies has elucidated patterns of reductive evolution underpinning host adaptation, particularly in lineages restricted to specific hosts like humans or livestock. Host-specialized serovars, such as S. Typhi and S. Paratyphi, accumulate pseudogenes and loss-of-function mutations, leading to genome streamlining that eliminates redundant metabolic pathways while preserving virulence determinants for intracellular survival within macrophages. This reductive trajectory, observed across comparative analyses of over 2,500 serovars, contrasts with broader-host-range strains like S. Typhimurium, which retain more versatile genetic repertoires for environmental persistence and zoonotic transmission. 
Phylogenomic reconstructions from core genomes highlight recurrent gene acquisitions, such as Salmonella pathogenicity islands (SPIs), that drive niche specialization; for example, SPI-2 facilitates systemic infection in warm-blooded hosts, with variations in SPI content correlating to serovar-specific host tropisms. 00038-1) Within-host evolution further generates microdiversity, as evidenced by single-nucleotide polymorphisms and plasmid rearrangements in longitudinal isolates from infected individuals or animals, enabling evasion of immune responses and adaptation to niche pressures like antibiotic exposure. 
Ancient DNA (aDNA) recoveries confirm S. enterica's deep association with human populations, with a basal lineage detected in Bronze Age human remains from Xinjiang, China, dated to circa 1000 BCE, indicating early divergence predating widespread agriculture in Eurasia. Human-adapted strains like S. Paratyphi C emerged around 5,000 years ago during the Neolithic transition to farming and animal husbandry, as inferred from genomic dating and detections in Eurasian and Mexican ancient samples spanning 6,000 years. 
Medieval evidence includes S. enterica genomes from 15th-century Latvian burials, co-occurring with Yersinia pestis, suggesting endemic circulation amid historical plagues, while a 1652 CE isolate from a Spanish soldier in Barcelona reveals a strain closely related to modern typhoidal serovars, underscoring persistent human host fidelity. 00989-5) These aDNA findings, often from skeletal lesions indicative of osteomyelitis, support causal links between Salmonella infections and historical morbidity, with genomic continuity to contemporary strains implying ongoing evolutionary pressures from host immunity and ecology rather than recent origins.

Current Research Directions

Advances in Vaccines and Therapies

In recent years, development of vaccines targeting non-typhoidal Salmonella (NTS) has focused primarily on conjugate and generalized modules for membrane antigens (GMMA) platforms to address invasive NTS (iNTS) disease, particularly in high-burden regions like sub-Saharan Africa. A phase 1 clinical trial completed in October 2025 demonstrated that a bivalent vaccine against typhoid (S. Typhi) and iNTS elicited strong immune responses in participants, with some showing pre-existing antibodies from prior foodborne exposure potentially enhancing efficacy. This trial, involving dose escalation, reported no serious adverse events and highlighted the vaccine's potential to reduce foodborne illness, which affects over 90 million cases annually worldwide.
Ongoing clinical trials include a randomized, placebo-controlled dose-escalation study (NCT03981952) evaluating Salmonella enterica subsp. enterica serovar Enteritidis conjugates (CVD 1000) for safety and immunogenicity, initiated to target prevalent serovars in human infections. Another trial (NCT05525546) assesses a trivalent conjugate vaccine against invasive Salmonella disease, measuring immune responses in healthy adults. For veterinary applications, live attenuated and subunit vaccines have advanced in poultry production, with genetic engineering improving efficacy against colonization; a 2023 review noted promising reductions in shedding and transmission to humans via contaminated meat. No NTS vaccines are yet licensed for widespread human use, reflecting challenges in achieving broad serovar coverage and mucosal immunity.
Therapeutic advances emphasize alternatives to antibiotics amid rising multidrug resistance in NTS strains. Phage therapy has shown efficacy in preclinical models, with a 2025 study demonstrating a phage cocktail eradicating Shigella and Salmonella in vitro within 24 hours, persisting for 72 hours and suggesting viability as an antibiotic substitute. In murine colitis models, phage FPSP6 reduced Salmonella-induced inflammation by modulating the gut microbiome and immune responses, lowering pathogen loads without disrupting commensal bacteria. A March 2025 characterization of novel phages isolated for Salmonella control in primary production highlighted their lytic potential and biosafety, positioning them for pre-harvest interventions in food safety.
Combining phages with antibiotics addresses resistance trade-offs, as a 2025 study found that phage-resistant Salmonella mutants exhibited heightened antibiotic sensitivity, enabling synergistic clearance in resistant populations. Broader emerging therapies for Gram-negative infections, including Salmonella, incorporate anti-virulence agents and antimicrobial peptides, though human trials remain limited; phage applications in the U.S. are expanding via biotech and academic efforts, with compassionate use cases reported for refractory infections. These approaches prioritize targeted specificity to minimize ecological disruption, contrasting with broad-spectrum antibiotics that exacerbate resistance.

Studies on Resistance and Pathogenesis

Genomic studies have identified widespread antimicrobial resistance genes (ARGs) in Salmonella enterica, often disseminated via plasmids and mobile genetic elements. A 2025 analysis of nearly 8,000 Chinese isolates established a comprehensive genome database revealing spatiotemporal dynamics of ARGs, including high prevalence of genes conferring resistance to quinolones, beta-lactams, and aminoglycosides, driven by agricultural antibiotic use. Similarly, a global atlas published in 2025 documented a sharp rise in plasmid-mediated fluoroquinolone resistance among food-derived Salmonella, increasing from 0.18% to 12.11% over recent decades, alongside elevated resistance in poultry sources linked to selective pressures from veterinary antibiotics. Clinical surveillance in 2025 reported near-universal resistance (99.4%) to azithromycin in human-derived strains, with co-resistance to sulfonamides, tetracyclines, and streptomycin exceeding 80%, underscoring the limitations of empirical therapy. These findings highlight horizontal gene transfer as a primary mechanism, with integrons and transposons facilitating multi-drug resistance profiles in serovars like Typhimurium and Enteritidis.
Pathogenesis research emphasizes Salmonella's sophisticated invasion and survival strategies within host cells. A 2025 study elucidated how Salmonella exploits -alanine, derived from both host and bacterial metabolism, to fuel replication inside macrophages, enhancing intracellular persistence and systemic dissemination. Small RNA (sRNA)-mediated regulation has emerged as a key virulence control mechanism; recent advances identified novel sRNAs like SaaS that modulate invasion genes and stress responses, enabling adaptation to host environments such as the gut mucosa and phagocytes. Type III secretion systems (T3SS), particularly T3SS-1, drive non-phagocytic cell invasion via a trigger mechanism involving effector proteins that remodel the actin cytoskeleton, as detailed in infection biology models. Multi-layered host-pathogen interactions, including Salmonella's disruption of colonization resistance through microbiota modulation and efflux of antimicrobial vesicles, further illustrate evolutionary adaptations favoring pathogenesis over host defenses. Virulence gene profiling across databases confirms pathogenicity islands (SPIs) as hotspots for these traits, with genomic erosion in some lineages correlating to host specialization.
