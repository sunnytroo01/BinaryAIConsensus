Per ardua ad astra

 Per ardua ad astra is a Latin phrase meaning "through adversity to the stars" or "through difficulties to the stars," which serves as the official motto of the Royal Air Force (RAF). The phrase encapsulates the spirit of perseverance and aspiration central to aviation and aerospace endeavors, symbolizing the challenges overcome to achieve great heights.
The motto originated in 1912 with the Royal Flying Corps (RFC), the predecessor to the RAF, when Major Frederick Sykes, the Corps' chief staff officer, solicited suggestions for an inspiring slogan to build esprit de corps among its members. Lieutenant J. S. Yule proposed "Per ardua ad astra," and it was approved by King George V on 15 March 1913 and promulgated in Army Orders the following month, becoming the RFC's emblematic phrase during World War I. Upon the formation of the RAF on 1 April 1918 through the merger of the RFC and the Royal Naval Air Service, the motto was retained and officially incorporated into the service's identity, appearing on badges, crests, and documents.
Beyond the RAF, "Per ardua ad astra" has been adopted by other Commonwealth air forces, including the Royal Australian Air Force (RAAF) since 1921 and the Royal Canadian Air Force (RCAF) from 1924 until the 1968 unification (when it was replaced by "Sic itur ad astra"); the current RCAF motto remains "Sic itur ad astra" as of 2025. The phrase's enduring appeal extends to education, science, and culture, inspiring institutions and appearing in literature and space exploration contexts to denote resilience in pursuit of discovery. Its formal tone and motivational essence continue to resonate in military and civilian spheres, underscoring themes of triumph over hardship.

Etymology and Meaning

Literal Translation

The Latin phrase Per ardua ad astra consists of two prepositional phrases constructed in classical Latin grammar. The preposition per governs the accusative case and denotes passage or means, translating literally as "through" or "by means of." This is followed by ardua, the accusative plural neuter form of the adjective arduus, which conveys ideas of steepness, height, or difficulty, often rendered as "difficulties," "hardships," or "heights" in English. The structure per ardua thus indicates progression via challenges or obstacles.
The second part, ad astra, employs the preposition ad, which also takes the accusative and signifies direction or aim, meaning "to" or "toward." Astra is the accusative plural of astrum, referring to celestial bodies or "stars." Grammatically, the phrase consists of two prepositional phrases governed by accusative case: per ardua (through difficulties) and ad astra (to the stars), expressing progression through challenges toward celestial or aspirational goals. The primary literal translation is "through difficulties to the stars" or "through hardships to the stars."

Interpretations and Symbolism

The phrase per ardua ad astra, literally translating to "through difficulties to the stars," serves as a profound symbol of perseverance and human aspiration, encapsulating the idea that enduring hardships is essential to attaining elevated goals or enlightenment. In this metaphorical framework, "ardua" evokes the steep, challenging paths of earthly trials, while "astra" represents not merely celestial bodies but lofty ideals, such as personal growth, artistic mastery, or collective progress. This duality underscores a philosophical commitment to resilience, where obstacles are not endpoints but necessary catalysts for transcendence, reflecting a universal human endeavor to rise above adversity.
In motivational contexts, the motto inspires a narrative of transformation, portraying the journey from mundane struggles to cosmic or existential fulfillment as an archetypal path of self-realization. It encourages individuals and communities to view difficulties as integral to achievement, fostering a mindset where aspiration propels one beyond limitations toward innovation and discovery. For instance, in artistic expressions like Beethoven's Symphony No. 9, the phrase symbolizes the progression from dissonant turmoil to harmonious resolution, embodying the triumph of human spirit over chaos through persistent effort. This interpretation aligns with broader motivational uses, where the stars signify ultimate potential unlocked only via rigorous trials.
The phrase's influence extends to themes of resilience in both classical and modern thought, reinforcing the notion that adversity builds character and leads to higher wisdom. In classical inspirations, it echoes sentiments of heroic quests against formidable odds, promoting endurance as a virtue central to ethical and intellectual pursuits. Modern applications, particularly in literary and cultural analyses, frame it as a beacon of hope amid trauma, illustrating how societies adapt and evolve through collective perseverance toward a brighter horizon.

Historical Origin

Ancient and Literary Roots

The phrase "per ardua ad astra," meaning "through hardships to the stars," draws potential inspiration from classical Latin literature, particularly works emphasizing the arduous path to celestial or divine achievement. In Virgil's Aeneid (Book 9, line 641), the god Apollo praises the young warrior Ascanius with the words "sic itur ad astra" ("thus one journeys to the stars"), part of a larger exhortation: "Macte nova virtute, puer: sic itur ad astra, / dis genite et geniture deos" ("Be blest, my boy, with new-found valor: thus the path is opened to the stars, O son of gods and father of gods to be"). This line underscores the valor required to attain starry heights, echoing themes of struggle and aspiration central to the epic's heroic journey. Similarly, Seneca the Younger, in his tragedy Hercules Furens (line 437), articulates the difficulty of transcendence through Megara's lament: "non est ad astra mollis e terris via" ("there is no easy way from the earth to the stars"). Spoken amid Hercules' trials, this sentiment highlights adversity as an inevitable barrier to the divine realm, influencing later motifs of perseverance toward the heavens.
By the 19th century, the exact phrase "per ardua ad astra" appears in English literature, predating its institutional adoption. In H. Rider Haggard's 1894 adventure novel The People of the Mist, the motto is inscribed above the gates of the protagonist's family estate, symbolizing a noble heritage forged through challenges: "Per ardua ad astra." Haggard uses it to evoke resilience in the face of peril, aligning with the novel's themes of exploration and endurance in uncharted African territories. This literary instance marks one of the earliest documented uses of the full phrase in modern prose, bridging classical ideals with Victorian-era narratives of ambition.
Debates persist regarding the precise origins of "per ardua ad astra" prior to the 20th century, with unverified claims linking it to both classical adaptations and familial traditions. While direct ancient precedents are absentthe phrase synthesizes elements from Virgil and Senecasome trace it to pre-1900 Irish heraldry, specifically as the motto of the Mulvany family, an Anglo-Irish lineage that reportedly employed it for centuries to signify ascent through trials. These attributions remain speculative, as no surviving classical texts contain the verbatim expression, and heraldic records for Mulvany lack comprehensive pre-19th-century verification, fueling scholarly discussion on its evolution from inspirational fragments to a cohesive maxim.

Adoption by the Royal Flying Corps

The adoption of "Per ardua ad astra" as the motto of the Royal Flying Corps (RFC) occurred during the organization's formative years in 1912, when Colonel Frederick Sykes, the first commanding officer of the RFC Military Wing, solicited suggestions from his officers for an appropriate Latin phrase to represent the emerging air service. Lieutenant J. S. Yule, a young officer from the Royal Engineers attached to the RFC, proposed the phrase, drawing inspiration from its appearance in H. Rider Haggard's 1894 novel The People of the Mist, where it symbolized overcoming great trials to achieve lofty goals.
Sykes deemed the suggestion fitting and forwarded it through official channels for approval. The RFC submitted the motto to the Army Council, which concurred, and it was then presented to King George V, who granted royal assent. The Admiralty, overseeing the RFC's naval elements, was consulted and offered no objections, leading to its formal promulgation in Army Orders dated April 1913.
In the context of early aviation, the motto encapsulated the perilous nature of flight at the time, with "ardua" evoking both the literal heights pilots had to attain and the formidable difficulties and risks involved, such as unreliable aircraft and frequent accidents that claimed lives even in training. This symbolism resonated deeply with the RFC's pioneers, underscoring their determination to advance aerial capabilities despite adversity.

Military Usage

Royal Air Force

Upon the formation of the Royal Air Force on 1 April 1918 through the amalgamation of the Royal Flying Corps and the Royal Naval Air Service, the motto Per ardua ad astraadopted by the RFC in 1912was retained as the official emblematic phrase, prominently displayed on badges, crests, and official insignia to symbolize continuity and aspiration in aerial service. This retention underscored the new service's inheritance of the RFC's pioneering spirit amid the challenges of World War I, with the motto appearing on the RAF's warrant and heraldic elements from its inception.
During World War II, Per ardua ad astra embodied the RAF's ethos of perseverance, particularly resonating with pilots and crews during the intense aerial defense of the Battle of Britain in 1940, where it served as a motivational emblem amid high casualties and relentless combat. The phrase similarly inspired resilience in the strategic bomber campaigns, such as those conducted by RAF Bomber Command from 1942 onward, highlighting the arduous path to victory over Nazi-occupied Europe through night raids and heavy losses.
In the contemporary Royal Air Force, Per ardua ad astra remains integral to organizational identity, adorning aircraft roundels, squadron patches, and training materials to foster a culture of overcoming adversity in operations and personnel development. It featured prominently in the 2018 centenary commemorations, including special coins, flypasts, and a message from Queen Elizabeth II invoking the motto to honor a century of service. As of 2025, the phrase continues to appear in memorials, such as the Battle of Britain plaque presented to the US Air Force, and in new compositions like the RAF's dedicated hymn premiered in 2022, reinforcing its role in training and ceremonial events.

Other Military and Air Forces

The Royal Canadian Air Force (RCAF) adopted "Per ardua ad astra" as its official motto in 1924, shortly after its establishment, drawing inspiration from the Royal Air Force. This motto symbolized the challenges faced in aviation and remained in use until 1968, when the RCAF merged with other branches to form the Canadian Armed Forces and adopted "Sic itur ad astra" ("Thus one journeys to the stars").
Following World War II, several Commonwealth air forces incorporated the phrase into their traditions, reflecting shared heritage with the RAF. The Royal Australian Air Force (RAAF), formed in 1921, officially uses "Per ardua ad astra" as its motto, translated as "Through struggle to the stars," emphasizing resilience in aerial service. Similarly, the Royal New Zealand Air Force (RNZAF), established in 1937, adopted the same Latin phrase, underscoring its commitment to overcoming adversity in defense operations. Other Commonwealth forces, such as those in historical contexts like the Royal Indian Air Force during the war, echoed this usage post-1945 as they transitioned to independence, though many later developed unique mottos. Beyond Commonwealth nations, the phrase has appeared in unofficial capacities within non-aligned militaries, particularly in U.S. Air Force units with aviation histories. For instance, the 86th Maintenance Operations Squadron incorporates "Per ardua ad astra" into its emblem, signifying perseverance in technical and operational challenges. Such adoptions highlight the motto's broader inspirational appeal in military aviation circles, even without formal endorsement at the service level.

Usage in Space Exploration

Early Aerospace Adoption

Following World War II, the phrase "Per ardua ad astra" transitioned from its established role in military aviation to symbolize the broader challenges of aerospace engineering and rocketry during the emerging space race. As the Royal Air Force (RAF) shifted focus from conventional flight to high-speed and high-altitude technologies, the motto encapsulated the perseverance required to push beyond atmospheric boundaries, drawing directly from its origins in overcoming aerial hardships.
In the late 1950s and 1960s, Britain's early rocketry efforts included experimental programs such as the Blue Streak intermediate-range ballistic missile, developed as a potential nuclear deterrent amid Cold War tensions. The Spadeadam Rocket Establishment in Cumbria, established in the late 1950s, served as the primary test site for Blue Streak's full-scale firings and simulations, embodying the motto's ethos of striving through adversity toward stellar ambitions. Despite the program's cancellation in 1960 due to technological and strategic shifts, it marked a pivotal step in linking aviation expertise to space propulsion, with test facilities like Spadeadam later supporting satellite and missile simulations.
The phrase also influenced early space enthusiasts and private aviation circles in the 1950s, inspiring the pioneering spirit in non-military pursuits of high-altitude and orbital flight. For instance, organizations like the British Interplanetary Society, active since the 1930s but gaining momentum post-war, pursued interstellar goals in a manner reflecting the motto's broader resonance.
During the Cold War, "Per ardua ad astra" underscored advancements in high-altitude aviation, where "ardua" evoked the steep challenges of extreme altitudes and supersonic speeds. RAF pilots and engineers, building on wartime innovations, conducted record-breaking flightssuch as those with the English Electric Canberra reaching over 70,000 feet in the early 1950spaving the way for space-edge technologies and reinforcing the motto's symbolic push toward the stars.

Modern Space Agencies and Missions

In the context of modern space exploration, the phrase "Per ardua ad astra" has been invoked informally within NASA's astronaut training programs and motivational contexts, particularly drawing from its aviation heritage during the Apollo era of the 1960s and 1970s. Astronauts, many of whom had military aviation backgrounds, referenced the motto in personal reflections and training narratives to emphasize perseverance amid technical challenges and high-stakes simulations. For instance, in biographical accounts of Apollo 17 command module pilot Ronald E. Evans, the phrase underscores the rigorous preparation and adversity faced in NASA's early lunar missions, symbolizing the transition from earthly trials to stellar achievements. Similarly, broader Apollo-era documentation highlights its use in speeches and writings to inspire resilience, aligning with the program's goal of overcoming engineering hurdles to reach the Moon.
The UK Space Agency, established in 2010, has seen the motto featured in strategic essays on national orbital capabilities, such as plans for domestic launches and satellite constellations by 2025. A 2025 policy analysis titled "Per Ardua Ad Astra: The UK's Orbital Imperative" argues for enhanced space infrastructure to counter global threats, using the phrase to frame Britain's push toward independent orbital access through partnerships like Skyrora and Orbex projects.
In the private sector, SpaceX has indirectly referenced the motto through missions emphasizing endurance in reusable rocketry from the 2010s to 2025. The 2021 Inspiration4 all-civilian orbital flight, launched aboard a SpaceX Falcon 9, incorporated "Ad Astra"the concluding element of "per ardua ad astra"as a hidden message in official crew photographs, symbolizing the journey through risks to reach space tourism milestones. This aligns with SpaceX's iterative testing of Starship prototypes, where early failures in 2020s flights exemplified the "ardua" (adversities) leading to successful reusability demonstrations, as noted in mission overviews. Blue Origin officially uses "Gradatim Ferociter" as its creed, meaning "step by step, ferociously."

Cultural Impact

In Literature and Media

The phrase "Per ardua ad astra" has appeared in various science fiction novels, often evoking themes of perseverance in the face of cosmic challenges. In Stephen Baxter's 2013 novel Proxima, it is directly quoted as a motivational motto amid the struggles of interstellar colonization, highlighting the adversity inherent in humanity's push toward the stars.
In film and television, the motto features prominently in productions centered on aviation and space exploration. The 1976 film The Man Who Fell to Earth, directed by Nicolas Roeg, references "per ardua ad astra" as the Royal Air Force motto during a dialogue on human achievement and extraterrestrial perspectives. More recently, James Gray's 2019 science fiction film Ad Astra derives its title from the phrase, framing Brad Pitt's character's perilous quest across the solar system as a literal embodiment of striving through hardship to reach the stars. On television, the 19791981 series Buck Rogers in the 25th Century inscribes "per ardua ad astra" on the hull of the starship Searcher, symbolizing the crew's defiant exploration of the galaxy.
Recent media, including video games and documentaries, continue to invoke the phrase to capture the spirit of spacefaring endeavor up to 2025. In the expansive space simulation game Elite Dangerous (released 2014, with ongoing expansions through 2025), "Per Ardua Ad Astra" titles a key lore episode in community-driven tours, representing pilots' trials in charting uncharted stars. Documentaries on space exploration frequently reference the motto in narratives of human spaceflight perseverance.

In Education and Organizations

The Latin phrase Per ardua ad astra, meaning "through adversity to the stars," has been embraced by various educational institutions as a motto to inspire perseverance and aspiration in learning. Garden Suburb Junior & Infant School in London, UK, adopted it to reflect the school's commitment to guiding students through challenges toward excellence, emphasizing resilience in a community-focused environment. Similarly, Bundaberg State High School in Queensland, Australia, incorporates the motto to motivate students in their pursuit of knowledge and personal growth, aligning with the school's emphasis on holistic development and future readiness. In the Caribbean, The Alexandra School in Barbados has used Per ardua ad astra since 1929, underscoring the value of effort in overcoming obstacles to achieve success, as established during the school's founding on the former Merton property.
Beyond formal schooling, the phrase appears in STEM-oriented educational contexts, where it symbolizes the rigorous path to scientific and technological innovation. For example, Young High School in New South Wales, Australia, features it on student badges issued since the 1960s, promoting a mindset of determination in subjects like science and mathematics. These adoptions highlight the motto's role in fostering a culture of endurance among young learners, particularly in programs that encourage exploration of complex fields.
In organizational settings outside education, Per ardua ad astra serves as a guiding principle for groups focused on personal and collective achievement. Several Freemasonry lodges have integrated it into their identity to represent the journey through trials toward enlightenment and brotherhood. Ad Astra Lodge No. 130 in British Columbia, Canada, explicitly uses the phrase in its name and ethos, drawing on its themes of striving amid difficulties since its establishment under the Grand Lodge of British Columbia and Yukon. Likewise, Astrea Lodge No. 3073 in South Africa North incorporates the motto into its crest alongside an eagle and star, symbolizing ascent to higher ideals within the fraternal tradition. These uses, dating back to the early 20th century in some Masonic circles, emphasize moral and intellectual progress through effort, aligning with the organization's emphasis on self-improvement.

Variants and Related Phrases

Per aspera ad astra

"Per aspera ad astra" translates literally as "through hardships to the stars," with "aspera" serving as the neuter accusative plural form of the adjective asper, which denotes something rough, harsh, severe, or bitter. This variant underscores the rugged, unpleasant, and often bitter quality of the obstacles encountered on the path to achievement, evoking a sense of enduring severe adversities. In comparison, the more widely known "per ardua ad astra" employs "ardua," the neuter accusative plural of arduus, meaning high, steep, elevated, or laborious, thereby emphasizing the challenging ascent or the demanding effort required to reach lofty aims. The substitution of "aspera" for "ardua" thus shifts the nuance from a focus on height and exertion to one of raw harshness and discomfort.
This form of the phrase developed an independent history distinct from its later military associations, notably predating the Royal Air Force's adoption of the "ardua" variant. It first appeared prominently as the official motto of the U.S. state of Kansas in 1861, rendered in inverted order as Ad astra per aspera on the Great Seal of the state to symbolize the profound difficulties surmounted during the territory's violent path to statehood, including the "Bleeding Kansas" conflicts over slavery and the broader Civil War context. Adopted upon Kansas's entry into the Union as the 34th state, the motto encapsulated the resilience of settlers amid political turmoil and frontier hardships. This usage occurred over five decades before the Royal Flying Corps (predecessor to the RAF) selected and had its motto approved by King George V on March 15, 1913.
Such independent applications demonstrate how "per aspera ad astra" evolved as a standalone emblem of endurance, separate from the RAF's aviation-specific interpretation.

Other Linguistic Variations

A notable linguistic variation of the phrase is "Ad astra per aspera," which reverses the original word order while retaining the core meaning of reaching the stars through challenges; this form translates to "To the stars through difficulties" and appears in U.S. Air Force commentaries on space exploration from 2020.
Non-Latin translations of "Per ardua ad astra" include the English equivalent "Through adversity to the stars," frequently invoked in motivational literature to symbolize overcoming obstacles for higher achievements. In French, it is rendered as " travers les embches jusqu'aux toiles," highlighting perseverance amid trials in bilingual discussions of the motto.
