Tangerine
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The tangerine is a small to medium-sized citrus fruit classified as a cultivar group within the mandarin orange species Citrus reticulata, characterized by its deep reddish-orange rind, easy-to-peel skin, and juicy, segmented flesh that typically contains few to no seeds. These fruits measure 58 cm in diameter, feature 1017 segments, and offer a sweet-tart flavor with tender, aromatic pulp.
Tangerines originated from wild Citrus reticulata in Southeast Asia, particularly regions of southern China, where domestication began several thousand years ago through selective breeding and admixture with pummelo (Citrus maxima), leading to diverse hybrids. The name "tangerine" derives from Tangier, Morocco, a key port through which mandarins were imported to Europe from Asia in the 18th and 19th centuries. Today, tangerines encompass various cultivars such as Ponkan, Clementine, Dancy, and Satsuma, distinguished by traits like seedlessness, size, and ripening time, with many being interspecific hybrids valued for their adaptability.
Cultivated primarily in subtropical and tropical climates, tangerines thrive in regions with cool winters and hot summers, such as China (the largest producer), Spain, Brazil, and the United States (notably Florida and California), where they are grown on small, erect trees that yield fruit from fall to winter. Nutritionally, a medium tangerine (109 g) provides approximately 50 calories, 13 g of carbohydrates (including 2 g of fiber), 1 g of protein, and significant amounts of vitamin C (45% DV), vitamin A (6% DV), and potassium (160 mg, 5% DV), along with bioactive compounds like flavanones that contribute to antioxidant properties.

Origins and History

Etymology

The word "tangerine" originated as an English adjective denoting something "of or pertaining to Tangier," the Moroccan seaport on the Strait of Gibraltar, with its earliest recorded use appearing in 1710 in the works of writer Joseph Addison. This linguistic root reflects the city's role as a key export hub for small, loose-skinned citrus fruits arriving in Europe during the 18th and 19th centuries, leading to the term's application to the fruit itself by 1842.
In botanical nomenclature, "tangerina" serves as a descriptor in Latin for varieties of the mandarin orange (Citrus reticulata), often used in species epithets like Citrus reticulata var. tangerina to specify types linked to Tangier exports. The adjective form "tangerinus" further denotes the characteristic tangerine-orange color in plant descriptions.
While "tangerine" specifically highlights the Tangier connection, it is often used interchangeably with "mandarin" in some regions, though the latter term derives from the bright orange robes of Chinese imperial officials and was first applied to the fruit in 1771 due to color resemblance. In contrast, "satsuma" refers to a distinct cold-hardy mandarin cultivar originating from Japan's Satsuma province (now Kagoshima Prefecture), where it was cultivated for centuries before global spread. These naming practices underscore regional variations in identifying subgroups within the Citrus reticulata species.

Historical Development

The mandarin orange, from which modern tangerines derive, was domesticated in southern China approximately 4,000 years ago, with cultivation practices emerging around 2000 BCE as one of the earliest centers of citrus horticulture in the region. This early human involvement transformed wild varieties into cultivated forms prized for their flavor and ease of peeling, marking the beginning of a long history of selective breeding and regional adaptation.
Mandarin oranges reached Europe in the early 19th century, introduced through British diplomatic channels when horticulturist Abraham Hume imported trees from Guangzhou, China, in 1805; from England, the fruit spread to Mediterranean countries by the mid-1800s. The term "tangerine" emerged around this time, referencing Tangier, Morocco, as a key export port for the fruit to European markets. Although Portuguese traders had encountered similar citrus in Asia during the 16th century, widespread cultivation in Europe awaited these later introductions.
In the United States, tangerines arrived in the mid-19th century, with the first documented imports occurring around 1840 when Italian immigrants planted trees in New Orleans, leading to their rapid dissemination to southern states. Commercialization accelerated in Florida and California during this period, where the fruit integrated into expanding citrus industries; Florida saw initial plantings in the 1840s, including the development of the Dancy variety from a Moragne seedling in 1867, establishing the state as a hub for tangerine production. Similarly, California's first commercial citrus groves, planted in the 1840s by William Wolfskill, provided the foundation for later tangerine cultivation amid the Gold Rush-era agricultural boom.
Key milestones in tangerine history include the establishment of dedicated groves across the U.S. in the 1840s, which solidified domestic supply chains, and post-World War II expansion in Mediterranean regions, where improved irrigation and market demands transformed citrus into a major cash crop during the 20th century's economic recovery.

Botanical Aspects

Taxonomy

The tangerine belongs to the genus Citrus L. in the family Rutaceae Juss., a group of flowering plants in the order Sapindales. It is primarily classified as Citrus reticulata Blanco, a species native to regions of southern China, encompassing various mandarins and their close relatives. Alternative nomenclature treats tangerines as Citrus tangerina Tanaka or as varieties within C. reticulata, reflecting historical efforts to distinguish loose-skinned citrus types from other oranges.
Taxonomic classification of tangerines remains debated within Citrus systematics, largely due to extensive hybridization and morphological variability among species. The Swingle system, proposed in 1943, recognizes only 16 basic Citrus species and classifies most cultivated forms, including tangerines, as hybrids or varieties of a few primary taxa like C. reticulata. In contrast, the Tanaka system from 1954 delineates over 150 species, positioning tangerines as a distinct subgroup within the broader mandarin complex (C. reticulata sensu lato), with C. tangerina as a separate entity. These approaches highlight ongoing challenges in resolving polyploidy and ancient admixtures that blur species boundaries.
Genetic analyses reveal that many tangerine forms incorporate contributions from pomelo (Citrus maxima (Burm.) Merr.), particularly through interspecific hybridization that has shaped modern cultivars. Genome sequencing shows admixture events where C. reticulata (mandarin) ancestry mixes with C. maxima (pomelo), leading to traits like larger fruit size in derived hybrids.
According to the World Flora Online consortium, the current accepted taxonomic status aligns Citrus reticulata as the primary name for the species representing tangerines and mandarins, with synonyms including Citrus nobilis Lour. and Citrus deliciosa Ten. This reflects a consensus toward simplifying Citrus nomenclature based on phylogenetic evidence while acknowledging historical synonyms.

Description

The tangerine tree (Citrus reticulata), a member of the mandarin orange group, is an evergreen shrub or small tree typically reaching a height of 3 to 6 meters, with a rounded canopy formed by slender, often thorny branches. The leaves are dark green, glossy, and lanceolate to ovate in shape, measuring up to 4-10 cm in length, providing dense foliage year-round.
The fruit is a hesperidium, a type of modified berry, characterized by an oblate to slightly oblong shape and a diameter of 5 to 8 cm. It features a thin, leathery rind that adheres loosely to the flesh, allowing easy peeling, and contains 10 to 17 juicy segments filled with orange pulp, varying by cultivar. Some types are seedless, while others contain a few seeds. The peel turns deep orange when ripe and emits a characteristic tangy aroma primarily due to essential oils such as limonene.
Tangerine trees produce clusters of small, fragrant white flowers in spring, which develop into fruit over several months, with harvest typically occurring in autumn to winter depending on the region.

Varieties

Pure Varieties

Pure varieties of tangerines, derived directly from mandarin ancestors without intentional hybridization, include several traditional types distinguished by their unique origins, fruit morphology, and environmental adaptations. These varieties typically exhibit the classic loose-skinned, easy-to-peel characteristics of mandarins, with variations in seed content, color, and ripening periods that suit specific growing regions.
The Ponkan mandarin, also known as Chinese honey orange, is a traditional pure variety originating from China, where it has been cultivated for centuries. It produces large, oblate fruits about 78 cm in diameter with a thick, reddish-orange rind that is easy to peel, and sweet, juicy, low-acid flesh typically containing 012 seeds. Ponkan ripens early to mid-season (October to December) and is highly productive, making it one of the most widely grown mandarin varieties globally, particularly in China, India, and Brazil. The tree is vigorous but susceptible to certain diseases and requires subtropical climates.
The Dancy tangerine, one of the oldest recognized pure varieties, originated in 1867 as a seedling from the Moragne tangerine in Colonel G. L. Dancy's grove in Orange Mills, Florida. It produces medium-sized fruit, approximately 2 to 2 inches in diameter, with a deep reddish-orange rind and rich-flavored, deep orange flesh containing 6 to 20 seeds. The fruit ripens midseason from December to January in Florida, though it has a tendency for alternate bearing and does not hold well on the tree once mature. Adapted to subtropical climates, the Dancy tree is vigorous and upright-growing but moderately cold-hardy and highly susceptible to Alternaria brown spot fungus, which causes significant defoliation and crop loss, limiting its commercial viability today.
Satsuma mandarins, botanically classified as Citrus unshiu, represent another key pure variety, with origins tracing to ancient cultivation in China and Japan. They are highly parthenocarpic, producing typically seedless fruit (0 to 6 seeds rarely) even without pollination, and feature medium to small, flattened spherical fruits with brilliant reddish-orange, tender flesh and a rich subacid flavor. Ripening early from September to December, Satsumas are the most cold-hardy of commercial citrus varieties, with mature trees enduring temperatures as low as 14F to 18F without severe damage. This trait enables major production in cooler regions such as southern Japan and the U.S. Southeast, including northern Florida and Gulf Coast states like Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, and Texas.
The Clementine, classified as Citrus clementina, emerged as a chance seedling in the late 19th century in the gardens of an orphanage in Misserghin, Algeria, and was selected and named after Brother Clment (or Clementine) by agronomist Louis Trabut. It is parthenocarpic, yielding seedless or very low-seeded fruit (0 to a few seeds) when not cross-pollinated, with medium-small to medium-sized fruits weighing 30 to 120 grams, deep orange to reddish-orange rind, and juicy, mildly acidic flesh. Clementines ripen early from October to December in Mediterranean climates, requiring low heat units for maturity and benefiting from cross-pollination for consistent bearing. Well-suited to mild-winter regions like the Mediterranean basin and Morocco, they exhibit early maturation in higher-heat areas such as California, though production demands careful management of irrigation and fertilization to avoid irregular cropping.

Hybrids and Cultivars

Tangerine hybrids, commonly classified as tangelos when crossed with grapefruit (Citrus paradisi) or tangors with sweet orange (Citrus sinensis), represent intentional crosses that expand the genetic diversity beyond pure mandarin lines within the Citrus genus. These hybrids emerged prominently in the early 20th century through systematic breeding programs aimed at combining desirable traits like flavor intensity and fruit size.
Breeding techniques for tangerine hybrids evolved significantly during the 20th century, with early efforts relying on controlled hybridization and selection to achieve seedlessness via parthenocarpy or triploidy, where diploid and tetraploid parents produce sterile triploid offspring incapable of seeding. Programs also incorporated screening for disease resistance, such as against citrus tristeza virus, by evaluating hybrid progeny for tolerance while preserving fruit quality. By mid-century, embryo rescue and mutation induction further accelerated the development of resilient, low-seed cultivars suitable for commercial production.
The Murcott, also called Honey Tangerine, exemplifies an early tangor hybrid, likely arising from an undocumented tangerine-sweet orange cross in a United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) planting in Florida around 1916. First propagated in a nursery in Pinellas County by 1922, it gained recognition for its medium-sized, reddish-orange fruits with smooth peel and a distinctive sweet-tart flavor rich enough for premium fresh markets, though typically containing 12 to 24 seeds per fruit.
Another key hybrid is the Minneola Tangelo, resulting from a deliberate cross between Dancy tangerine and Duncan grapefruit, released by the USDA Horticultural Research Station in Orlando, Florida, in 1931. Its bell-shaped, pear-like form with a pronounced neck end sets it apart visually, while the juicy, easy-to-peel fruits offer an excellent balance of sweetness and tartness, bolstered by high vitamin C levels that enhance its nutritional appeal.
Among modern cultivars, Sumoscientifically known as Shiranuistands out as a seedless hybrid developed in Japan in 1972 through a cross between Ponkan tangerine and Kiyomi tangor (itself a sweet orange-Satsuma hybrid). Characterized by its large size, bumpy peel with a prominent stem-end protrusion, and exceptionally sweet, low-acid flesh, Shiranui reflects advanced breeding for consumer-friendly traits like ease of peeling and minimal seeds, achieved through careful pollination control.

Cultivation and Production

Growing Requirements

Tangerines thrive in subtropical climates with optimal daytime temperatures ranging from 15C to 30C (59F to 86F), where warm growing seasons support fruit development and cooler nights enhance coloration. They are frost-sensitive, with young trees suffering damage below -2C (28F) and mature trees tolerating brief exposures down to -7C to -10C (18F to 14F) depending on variety and acclimation, necessitating protective measures like micro-sprinkler irrigation during cold snaps.
Well-drained soils, such as sandy loams, are essential to prevent root rot, with an ideal pH range of 6.0 to 7.5; soils below pH 5.5 may require liming to mitigate aluminum toxicity, while alkaline conditions above 8.0 can limit nutrient availability. Annual irrigation requirements typically fall between 800 and 1200 mm, distributed evenly to maintain soil moisture without waterlogging, with young trees needing more frequent deep watering (e.g., 5-10 gallons twice weekly initially) and mature trees benefiting from drip or microjet systems to match evapotranspiration rates.
Propagation of tangerines is primarily achieved through budding or grafting onto disease-resistant rootstocks like trifoliate orange (Poncirus trifoliata) or 'Swingle' citrumelo, which improve tolerance to phytophthora root rot, nematodes, and cold stress while ensuring true-to-type fruit production over seedling variability.
Pruning is generally minimal to preserve natural tree shape and fruiting wood, focusing on removing dead, damaged, or low-hanging branches post-harvest, along with suckers below the graft union to direct energy toward canopy development and improve air circulation.
Fertilization emphasizes nitrogen for vegetative growth, typically using balanced formulations like 8-8-8 or 10-10-10 N-P-K with micronutrients, applied in split doses (e.g., every 6-8 weeks from February to August) at rates increasing with tree agestarting at 0.5 cups for young trees and reaching 1.1-1.4 pounds of nitrogen per mature tree annuallyspread beyond the drip line to avoid burn.
Basic pest management involves regular scouting for common threats like aphids, scales, mites, and citrus leafminers, with integrated controls such as horticultural oils, insecticidal soaps, or biological agents like Bacillus thuringiensis; cultural practices like sanitation and proper irrigation reduce disease incidence from fungi like Elsinoe fawcettii (scab).

Global Production and Trade

Global production of tangerines, mandarins, and clementines reached 41.95 million metric tons in 2021, according to data from the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAOSTAT). China dominated output with approximately 25.0 million metric tons, representing about 60% of the global total. Other leading producers included Spain at 2.0 million metric tons, Turkey at 1.8 million metric tons, Morocco at 1.2 million metric tons, and Brazil at 1.1 million metric tons. These figures highlight the concentration of production in Asia and the Mediterranean region, where favorable climates support large-scale cultivation. Production increased to 44.2 million metric tons in 2022.

Post-2020, tangerine production has exhibited upward trends, driven by expanded acreage and favorable weather in key areas. Rising demand in Asia, particularly within China where domestic consumption absorbs nearly all production, has fueled this growth as urban populations seek convenient, nutrient-rich fruits. However, climate change poses significant risks, with elevated temperatures and erratic precipitation patterns threatening yields; research indicates potential reductions of up to 25% in citrus production globally due to these factors.
In terms of trade, Spain leads as the primary exporter, shipping the majority of its output to the European Union to meet seasonal demand for fresh citrus. China, while a top producer, exports a smaller share internationally, focusing instead on domestic markets, whereas the United States emphasizes internal consumption with limited export activity. Other key exporters include Turkey, Morocco, and South Africa, which supply Europe, Russia, and North America. Global tangerine trade volume stood at 6.0 million metric tons in 2021, with an estimated value of USD 6.75 billion, reflecting robust international demand despite logistical hurdles.
Trade faces challenges such as overproduction in major hubs like China, which can lead to domestic price suppression and reduced export incentives, alongside import tariffs that complicate cross-border flowsparticularly in the context of U.S.-China trade tensions affecting citrus tariffs. These factors contribute to market volatility, prompting producers to explore diversification and sustainable practices to stabilize supply chains.

Nutrition and Health

Nutritional Profile

Tangerines are a nutrient-dense fruit, with a typical serving of 100 grams providing approximately 53 kilocalories of energy, primarily from carbohydrates. This serving consists of about 85% water, contributing to its hydrating properties, along with 13.3 grams of total carbohydrates, of which 10.6 grams are sugars and 1.8 grams are dietary fiber. Protein content is modest at 0.8 grams, and fat is negligible at 0.3 grams per 100 grams.
In terms of vitamins, tangerines are particularly rich in vitamin C, supplying 26.7 milligrams per 100 grams, which meets about 30% of the daily value for adults. They also provide vitamin A in the form of beta-carotene, equivalent to 34 micrograms of retinol activity equivalents, and folate at 16 micrograms per 100 grams.
Key minerals include potassium at 166 milligrams per 100 grams, supporting electrolyte balance, while calcium and magnesium are present in smaller amounts at 37 milligrams and 12 milligrams, respectively.
Beyond essential nutrients, tangerines contain bioactive compounds such as flavonoids, notably hesperidin, which is abundant in the fruit's pulp and peel, and limonoids, which contribute to their phytochemical profile. These compounds are found in concentrations varying by variety, with hesperidin levels in mandarin types like tangerines reaching up to several milligrams per gram in peel extracts, though edible portions provide lower but significant amounts.

Health Benefits and Risks

Tangerines provide significant health benefits primarily through their rich content of vitamin C, a potent antioxidant that helps neutralize free radicals and supports collagen synthesis, thereby reducing the risk of scurvy in cases of deficiency. This nutrient also contributes to immune function by enhancing white blood cell activity and barrier integrity in the body.
The dietary fiber in tangerines, particularly soluble fiber like pectin found in the pulp and peel, promotes healthy digestion by slowing gastric emptying, improving bowel regularity, and potentially lowering cholesterol absorption in the intestines. Regular consumption of such fiber-rich citrus fruits has been associated with reduced risk of digestive disorders, including constipation and diverticular disease.
Limonoids, bioactive compounds concentrated in tangerine peels, exhibit potential anticancer properties by inducing apoptosis in tumor cells and inhibiting cancer cell proliferation, as demonstrated in studies from the 2010s using animal models and cell lines. For instance, limonin and nomilin, key limonoids in citrus including tangerines, have shown chemopreventive effects against colon and breast cancers through modulation of signaling pathways like NF-B.
Carotenoids such as -cryptoxanthin in tangerines support immune health by acting as provitamin A precursors, which bolster mucosal immunity and T-cell responses, potentially reducing infection susceptibility. These compounds also contribute to eye health by accumulating in the retina to protect against oxidative damage and age-related macular degeneration, with higher intakes linked to improved visual acuity in observational studies.
Despite these benefits, tangerines' natural acidity from citric acid can exacerbate heartburn or gastroesophageal reflux in sensitive individuals by relaxing the lower esophageal sphincter and increasing stomach acid production. Pesticide residues on tangerine peels pose a potential health risk, including neurotoxicity and endocrine disruption, though levels are typically below regulatory limits when fruits are washed; peeling mitigates exposure. Allergic reactions to tangerines are rare but possible, often involving oral allergy syndrome or cross-reactivity with pollen, manifesting as itching or swelling in affected individuals.
For adults, a recommended intake of 1-2 tangerines daily aligns with general fruit guidelines, providing about 1-2 cups of fruit toward the Dietary Guidelines' target while minimizing risks from excess acidity.

Uses

Culinary Applications

Tangerines are commonly consumed fresh, either whole or segmented, due to their sweet-tart flavor and easy-to-peel skin. They are frequently added to fruit salads, green salads, and desserts during their peak winter season, providing a juicy, vibrant contrast to other ingredients.
In processed forms, tangerines are transformed into juices for beverages, marmalades and jams for spreads, and candied peels for baking decorations and confections. Their essential oils, derived from the peels, are used to infuse flavor into baked goods like cakes and cookies, enhancing citrus notes without overpowering sweetness.
Tangerines feature prominently in various cultural dishes, such as Moroccan tagines where their juice and segments add a bright, tangy balance to spiced meats and vegetables. In Chinese New Year traditions, tangerines symbolize prosperity and good fortune, often exchanged as gifts and incorporated into festive meals to invoke abundance. Modern fusion recipes highlight tangerine glazes on meats, like spicy tangerine beef or citrus-glazed chicken, blending Asian-inspired sweetness with savory proteins.
For optimal ripeness and flavor retention, tangerines can be stored at room temperature for up to one week or refrigerated for two weeks, ideally in a mesh bag to allow air circulation and prevent moisture buildup.

Other Uses

Tangerine peel oil, derived from Citrus reticulata, is widely utilized in the perfume and cosmetics industries for its fresh, citrusy fragrance and skin-conditioning properties. The oil primarily consists of limonene, a terpene compound comprising 38.1% to 95.8% of its composition, which contributes to its aromatic profile and acts as a solvent and fragrance fixative. These oils are incorporated into fragrances, lotions, and deodorants, with safety assessments confirming their use in cosmetics at concentrations ensuring non-sensitizing and non-irritating formulations, provided 5-methoxypsoralen levels remain below 0.0015%.
Tangerine peel waste can be fermented to yield bioethanol. Additionally, microbial fuel cells using tangerine waste as a substrate have generated electricity, achieving peak voltages of 1.191 V and power densities up to 475.32 mW/cm through anaerobic digestion by bacteria like Serratia fonticola, highlighting its viability for sustainable bioenergy applications.
In traditional Chinese medicine, tangerine peel, known as chen pi or Citri Reticulatae Pericarpium, has been employed for over 2,000 years to support digestion, as documented in ancient texts like the Shennong Ben Cao Jing. It tonifies the spleen, promotes qi circulation, dispels dampness, and alleviates symptoms such as bloating, nausea, and poor appetite by modulating gut microbiota and enhancing gastrointestinal motility via compounds like hesperidin, which increases acetylcholine and motilin levels while reducing substance P and vasoactive intestinal peptide. Modern applications include supplements derived from tangerine peel bioflavonoids, such as hesperidin, nobiletin, and tangeretin, which provide antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects; these are used to protect against gastric ulcers and support overall digestive health through mechanisms like improved insulin sensitivity and reduced oxidative stress.
Tangerine trees (Citrus reticulata) are prized in ornamental landscaping for their evergreen foliage, which maintains dark green leaves year-round, providing consistent aesthetic appeal in gardens and urban settings. Their fragrant white star-shaped blooms, appearing in clusters from late winter to mid-spring, emit a sweet citrus scent that enhances outdoor environments, making them suitable for hedges, patio plantings, or container growth in suitable climates.
Culturally, tangerines hold significant symbolism, particularly during Lunar New Year celebrations, where they represent good fortune, prosperity, and abundance due to linguistic associations"j" for luck and "chng" for success in Mandarinalong with their round shape evoking wholeness and vitality. Traditionally offered in pairs or with attached leaves and stems to signify fertility and longevity, they are exchanged as gifts to wish recipients a bountiful year, a custom rooted in Qing Dynasty practices of placing oranges near children for protection. In art and literature, tangerines often symbolize hope and resilience; for instance, Ellsworth Kelly's 1964 lithograph Tangerine captures the fruit's elegant form in minimalist style, while Edward Bloor's novel Tangerine (1997) uses the fruit as a motif for perseverance amid adversity in Florida's citrus groves.
