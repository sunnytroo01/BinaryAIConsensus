 Michael Slovis is an American cinematographer and television director, best known for his innovative visual storytelling as the director of photography on the AMC series Breaking Bad (20082013).
Born in New York City, he grew up in Plainfield, New Jersey, where Slovis developed an early interest in photography through his mother's Kodak Tourist camera and learned film processing from his grandfather. He earned a B.S. in Professional Photographic Illustration from the Rochester Institute of Technology in 1976 and later attended film school at New York University. Slovis began his professional career in 1981 as a camera operator on various films, transitioning to roles as a set electrician and gaffer before becoming a cinematographer in 1995 with independent features like Party Girl.
Throughout his career, Slovis has contributed to numerous acclaimed television series, including CSI: Crime Scene Investigation (c. 20052007), for which he won a Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Cinematography for a Series in 2006, Fringe (20082009), The Leftovers (20142017), and New Amsterdam (20182023), where he also served as an executive producer and directed the series finale. His work on Breaking Bad earned him four Primetime Emmy nominations for Outstanding Cinematography for a Single-Camera Series (2010, 20122014), with the show's distinctive use of 35mm film and bold lighting techniques elevating its narrative impact. Slovis has also directed episodes of shows such as Ed, Castle, Better Call Saul, Chicago Fire, Chicago P.D., Game of Thrones, and recently The Walking Dead: The Ones Who Live (2024), joining the American Society of Cinematographers in 2010. His contributions span over four decades, emphasizing organic, story-driven visuals that have influenced modern television cinematography.

Early life and education

Early life

Michael Slovis was born in New York City, New York, USA, in 1955. His family relocated to Plainfield, New Jersey, during his early childhood, where he grew up amid the suburban environment of the area.
In 1967, Plainfield experienced significant racial tensions and riots that mirrored broader urban unrest across the United States, leading to widespread property damage and social upheaval. During this period, Slovis's parents, concerned for his safety amid the volatile social climate of the late 1960s, eventually decided to withdraw him from school. This decision reflected the family's efforts to navigate escalating racial conflicts.
Slovis developed an early interest in visual arts through photography, beginning as a child with his mother's Kodak Tourist folding camera. His grandfather further nurtured this passion by teaching him how to process and develop film in a homemade darkroom in the basement. By his teenage years, this hobby had evolved into more serious pursuits; at age 16, he became a finalist in the New Jersey State Teen Arts Festival with a notable photograph, marking an early recognition of his talent in visual media. These formative experiences laid the groundwork for his future career in cinematography.

Education

Michael Slovis earned a Bachelor of Fine Arts (BFA) in Professional Photographic Illustration from the Rochester Institute of Technology (RIT) in 1976. The program's curriculum focused on preparing students for careers in visual communications, with an emphasis on developing creative concepts and executing them through photography; key courses covered design principles, color theory, lighting techniques, studio and location photography, and art direction. During his undergraduate studies, a professor identified patterns in Slovis's work that aligned with cinematic storytelling and guided him toward cinematography as a specialization.
Following his time at RIT, Slovis pursued graduate education at New York University's Tisch School of the Arts, where he earned a Master of Fine Arts (MFA) in Film from the Maurice Kanbar Institute of Graduate Film and Television in 1979. The MFA program provided intensive training in filmmaking disciplines, including specialized coursework and practical experiences in cinematography that honed skills in visual composition, lighting for narrative impact, and collaborative production. These studies built directly on his foundational photographic training, deepening his technical proficiency in creating compelling visual narratives.

Professional career

Early career

Michael Slovis entered the film industry in the early 1980s, initially working as a set electrician and gaffer on various productions in New York City. One of his early roles was as a gaffer on the 1990 television movie Murder in Black and White, directed by E.W. Swackhamer. He continued in lighting positions through the decade, including serving as gaffer on the 1991 feature film At Play in the Fields of the Lord, a drama set in the Amazon rainforest directed by Hctor Babenco.
By the early 1990s, Slovis transitioned to camera operation roles, contributing to both independent and commercial projects to gain hands-on experience in the competitive industry landscape of the era. Notable credits include camera operator on the 1993 romantic comedy The Night We Never Met, directed by Warren Leight, and on the 1994 stand-up special Martin Lawrence: You So Crazy. He also operated the camera for the 1996 thriller Passion, a made-for-TV film starring Joanne Whalley. These positions on major and minor productions allowed him to build technical expertise amid the challenges of inconsistent work and evolving film technologies during the 1980s and 1990s.
In 1995, Slovis made the shift to cinematographer, earning his first full credit as director of photography on the independent comedy Party Girl, directed by Daisy von Scherler Mayer and starring Parker Posey, which premiered at the Sundance Film Festival. This marked the beginning of his work on low-budget features, including the Disney Channel original Halloweentown (1998), a family fantasy directed by Duwayne Dunham, and the teen comedy The Thirteenth Year (1999), also for Disney. These early cinematography assignments on independent and television films honed his visual storytelling skills before larger television opportunities.

Breakthrough in television cinematography

Michael Slovis's breakthrough in television cinematography came with his appointment as director of photography for CSI: Crime Scene Investigation during seasons 5 through 7, from 2004 to 2007. This role marked a significant elevation in his career, transitioning from independent films and earlier TV work to a high-profile procedural drama on CBS. Over the course of approximately 60 episodes, Slovis contributed to the show's distinctive visual aesthetic, emphasizing glossy, high-key lighting that highlighted the forensic examinations central to each storyline. This approach not only illuminated detailed crime scene investigations but also established a clean, clinical look that underscored the scientific precision of the Las Vegas forensics team, setting a standard for the genre's visual clarity.
Slovis's innovations in these projects included strategic use of even, bright illumination to mimic laboratory conditions in forensic sequences, fostering narrative tension by contrasting the sterile evidence analysis with the gritty realism of crime sites. This technique influenced visual storytelling in procedurals by prioritizing clarity in multi-element scenes, differing from the more static multi-camera setups common in sitcoms of the era, and allowing for deeper integration of visuals with plot progression.

Breaking Bad collaborations

Michael Slovis joined Breaking Bad as director of photography starting with the second season in 2009, shooting 55 episodes through the series finale in 2013. Recommended by director Adam Bernstein, Slovis was initially hesitant but was persuaded by his wife after reviewing the first season, ultimately becoming a key collaborator with creator Vince Gilligan in defining the show's visual language.
Slovis developed a cinematic style for Breaking Bad by shooting on 35mm film, a deliberate choice by AMC to evoke a textured, film-noir aesthetic despite the HDTV broadcast format. This medium imposed practical limits, such as a daily footage cap of 11,000 feet, which encouraged intentional framing and composition, while allowing for high-contrast, upscaleable images that aged well in post-production. Drawing from Sergio Leone's spaghetti Westerns and neo-realist influences, the visual approach treated Albuquerque as a character, using wide-angle lenses to capture both expansive desert vistas and claustrophobic interiors, desaturated colors for a gritty palette, and symbolic lighting to underscore themes of moral decay.
Notable techniques included high-contrast chiaroscuro lighting, such as the stark black-and-white sequences in the season 2 premiere "Seven Thirty-Seven," which contrasted sharply with the desaturated hues of everyday scenes. In desert sequences spanning episodes from the pilot era to the finale "Felina," symbolic elements like rays of sunlight piercing dark rooms in "Gliding Over All" (season 5, episode 8) highlighted tensions between domestic normalcy and violence, while cracked earth in "Ozymandias" (season 5, episode 14) evoked the protagonist's downfall, referencing Percy Shelley's poem.
Slovis extended his contributions to the Breaking Bad universe through spin-offs. For Better Call Saul, he directed the early season 2 episode "Bali Ha'i" in 2016, maintaining continuity in visual style with the parent series.

Directing and recent work

Slovis transitioned from his role as director of photography to directing during the later seasons of Breaking Bad, helming several key episodes that showcased his command of visual storytelling. His directorial debut on the series came with the season 3 episode "Kafkaesque" in 2010, followed by "Cornered" in season 4 (2011), and season 5 episodes "Live Free or Die" (2012) and "Confessions" (2013). These credits allowed him to blend his cinematographic expertise with narrative direction, contributing to the show's acclaimed tension and character focus.
After Breaking Bad concluded, Slovis extended his collaboration in the shared universe by directing episodes of its prequel Better Call Saul. Notable among these was the season 1 episode "Five-O" (2015), which delved into the backstory of character Mike Ehrmantraut, and season 2's "Bali Ha'i" (2016), praised for its emotional depth and subtle visual cues. His work on the series maintained the stylistic continuity established in Breaking Bad while adapting to the prequel's more introspective tone.
In the post-Breaking Bad era, Slovis diversified his directing career across multiple genres, with a significant focus on medical dramas. He served as a producing director on New Amsterdam from 2018 to 2023, overseeing more than a dozen episodes, including the series finale "How Can I Help?" in 2023, which provided emotional closure to the ensemble cast's arcs. This dual role underscored his ongoing influence in television visuals beyond the crime genre.
Into the 2020s, Slovis continued as an executive producer and director on projects like The Calling (2022present), where he directed at least one episode of the Peacock crime drama, adapting his precise framing to explore moral dilemmas in investigations. As of November 2025, his work reflects a sustained commitment to character-driven storytelling in prestige television, with no announced retirement.

Personal life

Family

Michael Slovis has been married to Maria Heritier since at least the mid-1980s. The couple resides in Montclair, New Jersey, where they have maintained their home for over 40 years as of 2025. Heritier, a fan of the series pilot, played a pivotal role in encouraging Slovis to accept the cinematography position on Breaking Bad in 2008, despite his initial reluctance after extensive prior travel for work.
Slovis and Heritier have three children: two sons and a daughter, all of whom were in their 20s as of 2012. Throughout his demanding career in television cinematography, particularly during the intensive shoots for Breaking Bad in New Mexico, Slovis has credited his family with providing essential support, allowing him to balance long absences from home with quality time during off-seasons. In interviews, he has expressed a deep appreciation for family life, noting that returning home to his wife and children remains a primary source of fulfillment amid professional demands.

Residence and community involvement

Michael Slovis resides in Montclair, New Jersey, where he has lived with his wife, Maria, for over three decades as of the mid-2010s.
Slovis has engaged with local educational institutions through speaking engagements and mentorship activities. In May 2014, as an alumnus of the Rochester Institute of Technology (RIT), he visited the campus to deliver a talk titled "Behind the Lens," discussing his cinematography on series like Breaking Bad and CSI: Crime Scene Investigation, followed by a tour of the School of Film and Animation and interactions with students on lighting techniques. He has also been a frequent speaker at Montclair State University, including presentations in 2014 and 2016 where he shared clips from his work on Breaking Bad and Game of Thrones to illustrate filming processes, and a 2022 event focused on his directing and pre-visualization techniques (following a postponed spring talk).
In 2025, Slovis taught a directing workshop as part of the Montclair Film Festival's programs.
Following the 2013 conclusion of Breaking Bad, Slovis has maintained a suburban lifestyle in Montclair, enjoying local pursuits like reading in his backyard and visits to the Jersey Shore, while continuing professional travel for television projects that often require time in Los Angeles or other locations. This arrangement allows him to balance high-profile Hollywood commitments with the stability of his New Jersey home base.

Directing

Michael Slovis's directing career spans over two decades, beginning with his debut on the 2001 television film Spirit, a supernatural drama starring Elisabeth Moss. Throughout the 2000s, he directed select episodes of procedural and comedy series, expanding into prestige cable dramas in the 2010s. By the late 2010s and 2020s, Slovis took on more extensive directing roles, helming multiple episodes per season on network medical dramas and franchise spin-offs, while occasionally overlapping with his cinematography duties on shared projects. To date, he has directed more than 50 television episodes across various genres, with no feature films to his credit as of 2025.

Awards and recognition

Primetime Emmy Awards

Michael Slovis first received Primetime Emmy recognition for his cinematography on CSI: Crime Scene Investigation, winning the Outstanding Cinematography for a Single-Camera Series award in 2006 for the episode "Gum Drops." He was nominated again in 2007 for the episode "Built to Kill, Part 1." This victory and nomination highlighted his ability to capture the procedural drama's forensic intensity through meticulous lighting and framing, competing against notable entries like 24 and The Sopranos.
Slovis's work on Breaking Bad earned him four nominations in the same category in 2009, 2010, 2012, and 2013, reflecting his innovative visual style that elevated the series' narrative tension with techniques such as wide desert landscapes, symbolic color palettes, and dynamic tracking shots. These nominations recognized episodes that exemplified his cinematic approach, often competing against high-profile dramas like Mad Men and Game of Thrones. In 2014, he secured a second win for the episode "Granite State," praised for its stark isolation motifs and subtle emotional depth in depicting Walter White's decline.

Other honors and contributions

In addition to his Primetime Emmy Awards, Slovis has been recognized for his visual artistry through membership in the American Society of Cinematographers since 2010, reflecting his standing among peers in the field.
Slovis has actively contributed to film education through speaking engagements and workshops, sharing insights on lighting and visual storytelling. In May 2014, as an alumnus, he visited the Rochester Institute of Technology (RIT), where he collaborated with students in the School of Film and Animation to demonstrate practical lighting techniques for narrative scenes. On September 27, 2022, he delivered a presentation at Montclair State University's Film Forum Speaker Series, discussing his directing and cinematography work with a focus on pre-visualization processes. These sessions highlight his role in mentoring emerging filmmakers on translating conceptual visions into compelling visuals.
Slovis has further influenced the industry through interviews that elucidate his approach to visual storytelling, emphasizing how cinematography enhances dramatic tension in television. In a 2010 Los Angeles Times profile, he described his method of using shadows and composition to convey character psychology in Breaking Bad, underscoring the medium's potential for cinematic depth. Similarly, a 2012 IndieWire discussion detailed his evolution of the show's aesthetic from intimate character studies to expansive desert landscapes, establishing a template for prestige television visuals.
His broader impact is evident in industry legacy mentions, particularly for shaping the look of spin-off series. A 2016 Hollywood Reporter article on the Better Call Saul debut praised Slovis's cinematography for maintaining Breaking Bad's innovative style while introducing subtler tonal shifts, influencing subsequent AMC productions through 2025.
