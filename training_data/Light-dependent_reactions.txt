Light-dependent reactions
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The light-dependent reactions, also known as the light reactions, constitute the initial phase of photosynthesis, wherein light energy absorbed by pigments in the thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts is converted into chemical energy stored in ATP and NADPH, while simultaneously splitting water molecules to release oxygen as a byproduct. These reactions occur exclusively in the presence of light and take place within the thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts in eukaryotic photosynthetic organisms such as plants and algae, and within thylakoid membranes in prokaryotes such as cyanobacteria. The primary function of these reactions is to generate the high-energy carriers ATP and NADPH, which are essential for powering the subsequent light-independent reactions (Calvin cycle) that fix carbon dioxide into organic molecules.
Central to the light-dependent reactions are two photosystems: Photosystem II (PSII) and Photosystem I (PSI), large protein-pigment complexes embedded in the thylakoid membrane that capture photons and initiate electron transfer. In PSII, light absorption by chlorophyll a (P680 reaction center) excites electrons, which are passed to a primary electron acceptor, creating an electron deficiency that is replenished by the photolysis of water, yielding protons (H), electrons, and O (with two water molecules producing one O molecule). These electrons then flow through an electron transport chain involving carriers such as plastoquinone, the cytochrome bf complex, and plastocyanin, ultimately reaching PSI. At PSI, light absorption by chlorophyll a (P700 reaction center) re-energizes the electrons, which reduce NADP to NADPH via ferredoxin.
The electron transport process establishes a proton gradient across the thylakoid membrane, with H ions accumulating in the thylakoid lumen due to water splitting and active pumping by the cytochrome bf complex. This electrochemical gradient drives chemiosmosis, where protons flow back into the stroma through ATP synthase enzymes, powering the phosphorylation of ADP to ATP. Both non-cyclic (linear) electron flow, which produces ATP, NADPH, and O, and cyclic electron flow around PSI, which generates additional ATP without NADPH or O, contribute to energy production, with the former yielding approximately 11.5 ATP and one NADPH per pair of electrons.
Antenna complexes in each photosystem, comprising hundreds of pigment molecules including chlorophyll a, chlorophyll b, and carotenoids, enhance light capture by funneling excitation energy to the reaction centers via resonance energy transfer. Chlorophylls absorb primarily in the blue-violet and red wavelengths, reflecting green light, which accounts for the characteristic color of photosynthetic tissues. Overall, the light-dependent reactions not only provide the reducing power and ATP for carbon fixation but also represent a fundamental mechanism for converting solar energy into usable chemical forms, sustaining nearly all life on Earth through oxygenic photosynthesis.

Overview

Definition and Role

The light-dependent reactions represent the initial phase of photosynthesis, comprising a series of biochemical processes that harness light energy to propel electron flow from electron donorssuch as water in oxygenic photosynthesisto the acceptor NADP, thereby generating ATP through photophosphorylation and, in oxygenic cases, releasing molecular oxygen as a byproduct. These reactions convert solar energy into chemical energy, establishing a proton gradient across the thylakoid membrane that drives ATP synthesis via chemiosmosis.
In their functional role, the light-dependent reactions supply the essential reducing agent NADPH and high-energy molecule ATP, which power the light-independent reactions of photosynthesis, including carbon fixation in the Calvin cycle. This energy capture occurs through two photosystems connected by an electron transport chain, ensuring efficient transfer of electrons to produce the necessary cofactors for subsequent biosynthetic pathways.
Distinct from the light-independent reactions, which utilize ATP and NADPH to incorporate CO into organic compounds without direct light involvement, the light-dependent reactions are strictly dedicated to photochemical energy transduction and do not participate in carbon assimilation. For oxygenic photosynthesis, the net stoichiometry of these reactions can be summarized by the equation:
2H2O+2NADP++nADP+nPiO2+2NADPH+nATP
2 \mathrm{H_2O} + 2 \mathrm{NADP^+} + n \mathrm{ADP} + n \mathrm{P_i} \rightarrow \mathrm{O_2} + 2 \mathrm{NADPH} + n \mathrm{ATP}
2H2O+2NADP++nADP+nPiO2+2NADPH+nATP
where $ n \approx 3 $ in non-cyclic flow, with cyclic flow contributing additional ATP to meet the variable demands of carbon fixation.

Location and Occurrence

The light-dependent reactions primarily occur in the thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts found in plants, algae, and cyanobacteria, where these specialized structures facilitate the capture of light energy to drive electron transport. In cyanobacteria, these reactions are similarly localized within thylakoid membranes, which are prokaryotic analogues to chloroplast thylakoids, enabling oxygenic photosynthesis in these ancient microbes.
These reactions are universal to oxygenic photosynthesis, which is performed exclusively by cyanobacteria and photosynthetic eukaryotes derived from cyanobacterial endosymbionts, such as plants and algae, across diverse terrestrial and aquatic habitats. In contrast, anoxygenic variants of the light-dependent reactions take place in intracytoplasmic membranes of proteobacteria, including purple nonsulfur bacteria, where these invaginations of the cytoplasmic membrane house the photosynthetic apparatus. For green sulfur bacteria in the phylum Chlorobi, the reactions occur at the cytoplasmic membrane, to which large light-harvesting complexes called chlorosomes are attached, adapting these organisms to strictly anaerobic, sulfide-rich environments.
In low-light aquatic environments, such as deep ocean waters or shaded coastal zones, organisms performing light-dependent reactions employ accessory pigments within their light-harvesting complexes to enhance absorption of available wavelengths, thereby optimizing energy capture for ATP and NADPH production in these resource-limited niches. This adaptation underscores the reactions' dependence on light, restricting their occurrence to phototrophic organisms capable of accessing solar or environmental illumination.

Core Components

Photosynthetic Pigments

Photosynthetic pigments are the molecules responsible for absorbing light energy during the light-dependent reactions, enabling the conversion of solar energy into chemical forms. These pigments are embedded in the thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts in plants, algae, and cyanobacteria, where they capture photons across different wavelengths of the visible spectrum to maximize light utilization. The core pigment, chlorophyll a, is essential for all oxygenic photosynthesis and consists of a porphyrin ring with a central magnesium ion, a long phytol tail for membrane anchoring, and a methyl group at the C7 position of the macrocycle. In vivo, chlorophyll a absorbs light primarily at peaks around 680 nm in photosystem II and 700 nm in photosystem I, corresponding to red light that drives electron excitation.
Chlorophyll b, an accessory pigment found in plants and green algae, complements chlorophyll a by broadening the absorption range into the blue-violet region (400-500 nm), thus enhancing overall light capture under varying environmental conditions. Structurally similar to chlorophyll a but with a formyl group (-CHO) at the C7 position instead of a methyl group, chlorophyll b exhibits absorption maxima at approximately 453 nm and 642 nm in solution, shifting slightly in protein-bound forms.
Carotenoids, another class of accessory pigments ubiquitous in photosynthetic organisms, absorb light in the blue-green spectrum (400-550 nm) and transfer this energy to chlorophyll molecules while also providing photoprotection against excess light-induced damage, such as photooxidation of chlorophyll. These lipid-soluble pigments, including -carotene and xanthophylls, feature conjugated polyene chains that enable their light-harvesting and antioxidant functions, with absorption bands typically between 400 and 500 nm peaking around 450-480 nm.
In cyanobacteria and some algae, phycobilins function as water-soluble accessory pigments attached to proteins in phycobilisomes, absorbing light in the green to orange range (500-650 nm) to support photosynthesis in low-light or shaded aquatic habitats where red and blue light are filtered out. Key phycobilins include phycoerythrin (absorbing ~490-570 nm), phycocyanin (~550-650 nm), and allophycocyanin (~650 nm), with their linear tetrapyrrole chromophores covalently bound to apoproteins for efficient energy transfer.
These pigments are organized into antenna complexes, such as the light-harvesting complexes (LHCs) in plants, which cluster hundreds of chlorophyll and carotenoid molecules around reaction centers to optimize photon capture and directional energy funneling via Frster resonance energy transfer. In plant LHCs, the efficiency of this energy transfer process is very high.

Reaction Centers

Reaction centers are integral membrane protein complexes that serve as the site of primary charge separation in photosynthesis, converting light energy into a transmembrane electrochemical gradient. These complexes are embedded in the thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts in oxygenic phototrophs or in intracytoplasmic membranes of anoxygenic bacteria, consisting of heterodimeric core subunits that coordinate pigments and cofactors in a highly organized manner. In photosystem II (PSII), the core is formed by the D1 and D2 proteins, while in photosystem I (PSI), it is the PsaA and PsaB subunits, each featuring multiple transmembrane helices that position the reaction center components for unidirectional electron flow.
Central to the structure of these reaction centers is a pair of chlorophyll a molecules functioning as the primary electron donor, designated P680 in PSII and P700 in PSI due to their absorption maxima at 680 nm and 700 nm, respectively. Adjacent to this special pair is the primary electron acceptor, pheophytin a, a magnesium-free derivative of chlorophyll a that accepts the excited electron. In PSII and PSI of oxygenic phototrophs, the special pair is situated near the lumenal side of the thylakoid membrane, with acceptors oriented toward the stromal side. This topology is inverted relative to anoxygenic bacterial reaction centers, where the donor side faces the periplasm. Crystal structures reveal that these components are arranged in a pseudosymmetric fashion, optimizing the spatial separation of charges.
Upon light absorption, the excited special pair (P*) rapidly donates an electron to pheophytin, forming the charge-separated state P Pheo within 13 picoseconds in PSII and approximately 100 femtoseconds in PSI, thereby generating an oxidized donor and reduced acceptor. This initial separation is followed by secondary transfers that further stabilize the charges, driven by a steep redox potential gradientfrom approximately +1.2 V for P680/P680 to -0.4 V for the terminal acceptors in PSIpreventing back-reactions and initiating the linear electron transport chain. The efficiency of this process approaches 100% quantum yield due to the precise tuning of pigment energies and protein environments.
Photosynthetic reaction centers are categorized into two evolutionary types based on their terminal electron acceptors: Type II centers, exemplified by PSII in plants and algae and the reaction centers in purple bacteria, feature a branched chain culminating in quinone molecules (QA and QB) that support two-electron reduction. In contrast, Type I centers, such as PSI in oxygenic organisms and those in green sulfur bacteria, utilize a linear chain ending in iron-sulfur clusters (FX, FA, FB) capable of one-electron transfers to ferredoxin. Despite these differences, both types share a conserved core motif derived from a common ancestor, ensuring ultrafast charge separation on picosecond timescales across diverse photosynthetic lineages.

Mechanism in Oxygenic Photosynthesis

Photosystem II

Photosystem II (PSII) is a multi-subunit protein complex located in the thylakoid membranes of oxygenic photosynthetic organisms, where it initiates the light-dependent reactions by absorbing photons to drive charge separation and the oxidation of water. The core reaction center of PSII is formed by the transmembrane heterodimer of D1 (PsbA) and D2 (PsbD) proteins, which coordinate the special pair of chlorophyll molecules known as P680, the primary electron donor, along with the primary electron acceptor pheophytin and the bound plastoquinone molecules QA and QB. Surrounding this core are inner light-harvesting complexes (CP47 and CP43) and outer antenna proteins that funnel excitation energy to the reaction center, enabling efficient light capture across a broad spectrum. The overall structure of PSII, resolved at high resolution, reveals a dimeric organization with a total molecular mass of approximately 350 kDa per monomer, essential for its role in non-cyclic electron transport.
Central to PSII's function is the oxygen-evolving complex (OEC), a catalytic cofactor embedded on the lumenal side near the D1 protein, comprising a MnCaO cluster ligated by amino acid residues from D1, D2, and the CP43 subunit. This cluster performs the critical water-splitting reaction, oxidizing two water molecules to produce molecular oxygen, four protons, and four electrons according to the stoichiometry $ 2 \mathrm{H_2O} \rightarrow \mathrm{O_2} + 4 \mathrm{H^+} + 4 e^- $. The process occurs through the Kok cycle, a sequence of five redox states (S to S) advanced by each light-induced charge separation in the reaction center; the S state is the dark-stable reduced form, S to S are semi-stable oxidized intermediates, and the transient S state facilitates O-O bond formation and O release before returning to S. Spectroscopic and structural studies confirm that the MnCa cluster undergoes conformational changes during these S-state transitions, with calcium stabilizing the cluster and enabling substrate water binding via ligating waters and amino acid side chains.
In the electron transfer pathway, light excitation promotes an electron from P680 to form the charge-separated state P680 Pheo, with the electron rapidly reducing pheophytin (Pheo) in about 1 picosecond, followed by sequential transfer to QA (a one-electron acceptor) in 200 picoseconds and then to QB (a two-electron gate) over tens to hundreds of microseconds. QA, bound to the D2 protein, serves as an intermediate that prevents back-reactions, while QB, associated with the D1 protein, accepts two electrons and two protons from the stromal side to form mobile plastoquinol (QH), which leaves the QB site and contributes to the proton gradient while delivering electrons to the plastoquinone pool for non-cyclic flow. This linear electron transport from water to plastoquinone establishes the reducing power and proton motive force for subsequent photosynthetic processes.
PSII is susceptible to photoinhibition under high light conditions, primarily due to oxidative damage to the D1 protein from reactive oxygen species and the highly oxidizing P680, which can lead to impaired electron transfer and reduced quantum efficiency. To counteract this, a dynamic repair cycle ensures PSII longevity through the selective degradation of damaged D1 via proteolysis and its rapid resynthesis and reassembly into the reaction center, with the half-life of D1 ranging from minutes under stress to hours in moderate light. This turnover mechanism, involving chaperones and assembly factors, maintains PSII activity and prevents accumulation of photodamaged complexes, highlighting the balance between damage and repair in sustaining oxygenic photosynthesis.

Cytochrome b6f Complex

The cytochrome b6f complex is a dimeric transmembrane protein embedded in the thylakoid membrane of chloroplasts, structurally homologous to the cytochrome bc1 complex in respiratory chains, sharing a conserved core of redox-active centers but featuring plant-specific elements such as an additional heme c_n and a bound chlorophyll a molecule. It comprises four main subunits per monomercytochrome b6 (containing two b-type hemes, b_H and b_L), cytochrome f (with a c-type heme), the extrinsic Rieske iron-sulfur protein (harboring a cluster), and subunit IValong with smaller subunits like PetG and PetN that stabilize the dimer interface. This architecture positions the complex as a central hub for linear electron flow, receiving reduced plastoquinol from Photosystem II and transferring electrons to plastocyanin en route to Photosystem I.
Central to its function is the Q-cycle mechanism, which enhances proton translocation efficiency during plastoquinol oxidation. At the Qo site on the lumenal (p-side) domain, plastoquinol (PQH) binds and undergoes oxidation, releasing two protons directly into the thylakoid lumen; the bifurcated electrons follow divergent pathsone via the high-potential chain through the Rieske cluster to cytochrome f and then to plastocyanin (PC, Cu), and the other via the low-potential chain across the two b hemes to the Qi site on the stromal (n-side) domain, where it reduces a plastoquinone molecule. This cyclic electron bifurcation effectively doubles the proton yield per electron transferred, as the second turnover completes quinone reduction to PQH at Qi, consuming two stromal protons while translocating an additional two to the lumen. The net stoichiometry for two PQH molecules processed (one net oxidized) transfers two electrons to plastocyanin and translocates four protons to the lumen, with the Q-cycle contributing the extra two beyond the initial deprotonation.
The simplified overall reaction for plastoquinol oxidation coupled to plastocyanin reduction via the Q-cycle is:
PQHX2+2PC(CuX2+)+2HXstroma+PQ+2PC(CuX+)+4HXlumen+
\ce{PQH2 + 2 PC(Cu^{2+}) + 2 H^{+}_{stroma} -> PQ + 2 PC(Cu^{+}) + 4 H^{+}_{lumen}}
PQHX2+2PC(CuX2+)+2HXstroma+PQ+2PC(CuX+)+4HXlumen+
This process underscores the complex's role in building the proton motive force, contributing up to two-thirds of the transmembrane pH gradient essential for ATP synthesis through photophosphorylation. By coupling electron shuttling to vectorial proton movement, the cytochrome b6f complex optimizes energy conversion in oxygenic photosynthesis, with regulatory mechanisms like lumenal pH feedback fine-tuning its activity to prevent over-reduction of downstream components.

Photosystem I

Photosystem I (PSI) functions as the terminal component in the linear electron transport chain of oxygenic photosynthesis, capturing light energy to drive electron transfer from plastocyanin to ferredoxin, thereby reducing NADP to NADPH for use in the Calvin cycle. The core structure of PSI is a heterodimeric reaction center composed of the PsaA and PsaB subunits, which together bind approximately 100 chlorophyll a molecules forming the antenna system, the primary donor P700 (a chlorophyll a dimer), and a series of electron acceptors including the chlorophyll a molecule A, the phylloquinone A, and iron-sulfur clusters F, F, and F_B. Associated with this core is ferredoxin-NADP reductase (FNR), a peripheral enzyme that catalyzes the final reduction step, often docking transiently to the stromal side of PSI to efficiently accept electrons from ferredoxin.
In the linear pathway, light absorption excites P700 to P700*, initiating rapid electron transfer: the excited electron moves from P700* to A within picoseconds, then to A (phylloquinone) in about 30 nanoseconds, followed by the iron-sulfur clusters F (interpolypeptide), and then F and F_B (on PsaC), reaching soluble ferredoxin in microseconds. Ferredoxin subsequently transfers the electron to FNR, which uses it to reduce NADP to NADPH via a two-electron mechanism involving FAD as a prosthetic group. This sequence ensures unidirectional electron flow with minimal back-reactions, supported by the decreasing redox potentials along the chain from plastocyanin (+0.36 V) through P700 (+0.49 V) to NADP (-0.32 V).
PSI also enables cyclic electron flow, where reduced ferredoxin donates electrons back to the plastoquinone pool or directly to the cytochrome bf complex, bypassing NADP reduction and generating additional ATP through proton translocation without net NADPH production. This pathway, mediated in part by proteins like PGR5, helps balance the ATP/NADPH ratio required for carbon fixation and contributes to the proton gradient established by upstream electron transport components.
The electron transfer in PSI achieves a remarkably high quantum yield, approaching 100% for charge separation per photon absorbed by the reaction center, with the overall linear flow to NADPH requiring two photons per electron (one at each photosystem). This efficiency underscores PSI's role in minimizing energy loss and maximizing the conversion of solar energy into chemical reductant.

Variations in Anoxygenic Photosynthesis

Purple Bacteria

Purple bacteria, encompassing both purple sulfur bacteria (e.g., Chromatium) and purple non-sulfur bacteria (e.g., Rhodobacter sphaeroides), conduct anoxygenic photosynthesis through a Type II reaction center known as P870, a special pair of bacteriochlorophyll molecules that absorbs light maximally at 870 nm. This single photosystem facilitates cyclic electron transport, where light excitation of P870 leads to electron transfer to an accessory bacteriochlorophyll, then to bacteriopheophytin, and subsequently to quinone acceptors (Q_A and Q_B) within the reaction center. The electron then cycles back to the oxidized P870 via the ubiquinone pool, the cytochrome bc1 complex, and soluble cytochrome c2, generating a proton motive force across the intracytoplasmic membrane for ATP synthesis without net NADPH production in the core cycle. Unlike oxygenic systems, this process does not evolve oxygen, as water is not used as an electron donor.
Electron donors in purple bacteria vary by species and environment; purple sulfur bacteria oxidize hydrogen sulfide (HS) to elemental sulfur (S), while purple non-sulfur bacteria typically use organic compounds such as acetate or succinate, with some capable of utilizing HS, hydrogen gas, or thiosulfate. These donors provide electrons that enter the transport chain upstream of the reaction center, often via sulfide:quinone oxidoreductase or other dehydrogenases, enabling the reduction of NAD to NADH through reverse electron flow driven by the proton motive force. This process supports carbon fixation via the Calvin-Benson-Bassham cycle in autotrophic modes, without oxygen production.
Light harvesting in purple bacteria is mediated by integral membrane complexes containing bacteriochlorophylls and carotenoids. The core light-harvesting complex LH1 encircles the P870 reaction center, absorbing primarily at 870 nm (B875 band), while peripheral LH2 complexes, present in many species under low-light conditions, feature bacteriochlorophylls absorbing at 800 nm (B800) and 850 nm (B850). These complexes, composed of and apoproteins, efficiently funnel excitation energy to the reaction center with near-unity quantum efficiency, adapting to light intensities through regulated expression of LH2. Bacteriochlorophyll a (absorbing 800960 nm) predominates in most species, though bacteriochlorophyll b (8351040 nm) occurs in some, enabling utilization of near-infrared light.
The photosynthetic apparatus of purple bacteria serves as a key model for anoxygenic photosynthesis on early Earth, where anaerobic conditions and abundant reduced sulfur compounds like HS prevailed before the rise of oxygenic photosynthesis around 2.4 billion years ago. Their Type II reaction center shares structural homology with Photosystem II, providing insights into the evolutionary precursors of oxygenic systems. This ancient pathway likely dominated microbial ecosystems, cycling sulfur and carbon without contributing to atmospheric oxygenation.

Green Sulfur Bacteria

Green sulfur bacteria, such as those in the phylum Chlorobi, perform anoxygenic photosynthesis using a single type I reaction center known as P840, which consists of a special pair of bacteriochlorophyll a molecules that absorb light maximally at 840 nm to initiate charge separation. This photosystem drives linear electron transport without the involvement of a quinone intermediate in the core chain, distinguishing it from type II systems. Electrons excited in P840 are transferred through a series of acceptors, including A (a chlorophyll a-670 molecule), A (phylloquinone), and iron-sulfur clusters (F, F, F), ultimately reducing ferredoxin. The reduced ferredoxin then serves as a potent reductant, transferring electrons to NAD via an RNF-type oxidoreductase or directly supporting CO reduction in autotrophic metabolism.
The primary electron donor in these bacteria is hydrogen sulfide (HS), which is oxidized to elemental sulfur (S) according to the half-reaction HS S + 2 H + 2 e, often mediated by a separate menaquinone-involved pathway for sulfide oxidation rather than the reaction center itself. This process avoids oxygen production and enables growth in anoxic, sulfide-rich environments like stratified lakes or sediments. The generated NADH fuels carbon fixation through the reductive tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, supporting autotrophic growth without external organic carbon sources.
For efficient light harvesting in low-light conditions, green sulfur bacteria employ large antenna complexes called chlorosomes, which contain self-assembled aggregates of bacteriochlorophyll c or e (along with minor pigments like chlorophyll a and carotenoids) to capture photons across a broad spectrum and funnel energy to the P840 reaction center via the Fenna-Matthews-Olson (FMO) protein. This adaptation allows these bacteria to thrive at irradiances as low as 0.11 mol photons m s, far below levels required by oxygenic phototrophs. The type I reaction center architecture in green sulfur bacteria shares evolutionary homology with photosystem I in oxygenic photosynthesis, reflecting an ancient origin for such linear electron flow mechanisms.

Cyanobacteria

Certain cyanobacteria, such as Oscillatoria limnetica, exhibit facultative anoxygenic photosynthesis in anoxic, sulfide-rich environments like stratified lakes or microbial mats, where they employ hydrogen sulfide (HS) as an alternative electron donor to photosystem I (PSI), bypassing photosystem II (PSII) to avoid oxygen production. This sulfide-driven mode, known as type I anoxygenic photosynthesis, transfers electrons from HS via flavocytochrome c or sulfide:quinone oxidoreductase into the electron transport chain, ultimately reducing NADP to NADPH through PSI, while generating ATP via cyclic electron flow around PSI.
A distinctive feature enabling this variation is the presence of phycobilisomes, massive extrinsic antenna complexes attached to the stromal side of thylakoids, which capture light primarily in the red and far-red wavelengths (620680 nm) through phycobiliproteins like phycocyanin and allophycocyanin. These structures funnel excitation energy to PSI with high efficiency under low-oxygen conditions. Regulation includes state transitions, where phycobilisomes reversibly associate with PSI in response to the redox state of the plastoquinone pool, balancing energy distribution during fluctuating illumination or sulfide exposure. Complementary chromatic acclimation optimizes antenna function by altering phycobilisome compositionupregulating phycoerythrin under green light or phycocyanin under red lightvia light-sensing phytochrome-like proteins, aiding adaptation to dominant wavelengths in aquatic or soil habitats.
This anoxygenic capability is environmentally induced and reversible; cyanobacteria quickly reinstate PSII and oxygenic photosynthesis upon return to oxic conditions, highlighting their versatility in sulfur-cycling ecosystems. The photosystems in cyanobacteria exhibit strong homology to those in chloroplasts, underscoring their role as progenitors in endosymbiotic evolution.

Evolutionary and Historical Aspects

Historical Discoveries

In the late 18th century, foundational observations linked light to the gaseous exchanges in plants during what would later be understood as photosynthesis. Dutch physician Jan Ingenhousz demonstrated in 1779 that only the green parts of plants release oxygen gas in sunlight, while in darkness or shade they consume oxygen and release carbonic acid, establishing light's essential role in oxygen production. Building on this, Swiss pastor and naturalist Jean Senebier showed in 1782 that plants absorb fixed air (carbon dioxide) and release purified air (oxygen) specifically under the influence of sunlight, with the process occurring more efficiently in brighter light.
The 20th century brought experimental advances isolating the light-dependent aspects of photosynthesis from carbon fixation. In 19371939, British biochemist Robin Hill discovered the "Hill reaction," in which isolated chloroplasts from spinach, when illuminated in the presence of an artificial electron acceptor like ferricyanide but without carbon dioxide, evolved oxygen gas, confirming that the light reactions could proceed independently to produce oxygen. This breakthrough separated the photochemical oxygen evolution from the reductive carbon assimilation, paving the way for subcellular studies of photosynthesis.
Further insights into the efficiency of light utilization came from American plant physiologist Robert Emerson in 1957, who observed the "enhancement effect": the rate of photosynthesis in algal cells increased beyond the sum of individual rates when illuminated simultaneously with short-wavelength red light (around 650 nm) and longer-wavelength far-red light (beyond 680 nm), indicating that two distinct light-harvesting processes were cooperating rather than a single one. This phenomenon, initially termed the "two-light effect," suggested the involvement of two photosystems operating in series to optimize electron transport.
In the 1960s, Dutch biophysicist Louis N. M. Duysens proposed the existence of two linked photochemical systems in oxygenic photosynthesis, based on fluorescence and absorption studies of algae and isolated chloroplasts. His 1961 work demonstrated that excitation energy from one pigment system (later identified as Photosystem I) could transfer to another (Photosystem II) under certain conditions, supporting a linear electron flow model where the two systems work sequentially, with cytochrome complexes bridging them. These findings, complemented by spectroscopic analyses of bacterial chromatophores, refined the concept of dual photosystems.
By the 1970s, biochemical techniques enabled the isolation of functional Photosystem II and Photosystem I complexes from thylakoid membranes, allowing direct assays of their activities. For instance, detergent-based fractionation yielded Photosystem II-enriched vesicles capable of water oxidation and oxygen evolution, while Photosystem I particles demonstrated NADP+ reduction, confirming their distinct roles in the electron transport chain. These isolations provided the foundation for detailed mechanistic studies of the light-dependent reactions.

Evolutionary Origins

The light-dependent reactions of photosynthesis originated in anoxygenic forms approximately 3.5 billion years ago, with early bacterial lineages developing Type I and Type II reaction centers to harness light energy using electron donors other than water. These primordial systems likely evolved from a common ancestral protein, enabling efficient charge separation in diverse microbial ecosystems under anoxic conditions. Phylogenetic analyses indicate that Type II centers, characteristic of purple bacteria (Proteobacteria), diverged early, while Type I centers, found in green sulfur bacteria (Chlorobi), represent a parallel lineage adapted to low-light environments.
The transition to oxygenic photosynthesis occurred around 3.0 to 2.4 billion years ago, marking a pivotal innovation where Photosystem II (PSII) integrated the oxygen-evolving complex (OEC), a Mn4CaO5 cluster capable of water oxidation. This fusion likely arose through gene duplication of the D1 and D2 proteins in an ancestral homodimeric Type II center, endowing cyanobacteria with the ability to use abundant water as an electron source and releasing oxygen as a byproduct. The emergence of this oxygenic machinery contributed to the Great Oxidation Event (GOE) approximately 2.4 billion years ago, transforming Earth's atmosphere from anoxic to oxygen-rich and enabling aerobic life.
Subsequent divergences saw cyanobacterial photosystems incorporated into eukaryotic cells via endosymbiosis, where an ancestral cyanobacterium became the progenitor of chloroplasts around 1.5 billion years ago. This event preserved the dual Type I (PSI) and Type II (PSII) architecture, optimizing linear electron flow in oxygenic photosynthesis across plants and algae. Horizontal gene transfer further shaped pigment evolution, facilitating adaptations like bacteriochlorophyll diversification among anoxygenic bacteria.
Modern insights reveal quantum coherence as a key feature enhancing energy transfer efficiency in light-harvesting complexes, with wavelike exciton dynamics persisting for hundreds of femtoseconds even at physiological temperatures. Studies from the 2000s to 2020s, including those by Fleming and colleagues, demonstrate how this coherence minimizes energy loss, underscoring its likely ancient role in the evolutionary optimization of photosynthetic reaction centers.
