Larry Ramos
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Larry Ramos (April 19, 1942 April 30, 2014) was an American singer, guitarist, and banjo player of Filipino descent, renowned as the first Asian American to win a Grammy Award. Born Hilario D. Ramos Jr. in Waimea, Kauai, Hawaii, he began his career in music early, performing in local venues before moving to Southern California as a teenager.
Ramos achieved his breakthrough in 1962 when he joined the folk ensemble the New Christy Minstrels, contributing to their debut album Presenting the New Christy Minstrels, which earned the 1963 Grammy for Best Performance by a Chorus and marked him as a trailblazer for Asian Americans in the music industry. In 1967, he joined the pop-rock band the Association, replacing departing member Gary Alexander, where he served as a lead vocalist and multi-instrumentalist on their breakthrough hits, including co-lead vocals on "Windy" (1967), which reached number one on the Billboard Hot 100, and "Never My Love" (1967), another major chart-topper.
Throughout his over 65-year career in show business, Ramos performed across genres from folk to pop, collaborating with notable artists and groups while maintaining a legacy as a pioneer for Filipino and Asian American performers in mainstream American music. His contributions were later honored through tributes, including the 2025 documentary Along Comes Larry, The Larry Ramos Story, film screenings, and concerts celebrating his groundbreaking achievements.

Early life

Childhood in Hawaii

Hilario "Larry" Ramos Jr. was born on April 19, 1942, in Waimea, Kauai County, Hawaii. Of Filipino descent with traces of Chinese and Spanish ancestry, Ramos grew up in a multicultural family in this small plantation town, where his father, Larry Ramos Sr., owned a pool hall and dry cleaning business in Kauai.
Ramos displayed early musical talent, beginning to play the ukulele at age four under his father's guidance, who taught him his first song, "My Bonnie Lies Over the Ocean." By age five, he was performing professionally in local settings, and in 1950, at around age eight, he won a statewide ukulele competition that led to broader opportunities. This early prowess earned him local recognition as a child prodigy within Hawaiian communities, where ukulele playing was a cherished cultural tradition blending Native Hawaiian, Portuguese, and immigrant influences.
His childhood performances gained national attention in the early 1950s. In 1950, Ramos won a ukulele contest that led to an appearance on The Arthur Godfrey Show in New York City, showcasing his skills to a wide American audience. That same year, he made his film debut in the MGM musical Pagan Love Song, starring Esther Williams and Howard Keel, where he played ukulele and sang in a cameo role. He was discovered playing ukulele at his mother's gift shop at the Kauai Inn by a film casting director. These experiences highlighted his budding talent and integrated him into the broader entertainment landscape from his Hawaiian roots.

Move to the mainland and early influences

In 1952, at the age of 10, Larry Ramos and his family relocated from Waimea, Kauai, to Bell, a suburb of Los Angeles, California, drawn by the entertainment opportunities in Hollywood for their musically gifted children. The move marked a significant shift from the island's plantation life to the bustling mainland environment, where Ramos quickly adapted by continuing his performances, including appearances with Harry Owens and His Royal Hawaiians, playing ukulele and singing Hawaiian songs.
Ramos attended Bell High School, graduating in 1960, and later pursued studies in political science at East Los Angeles College and Cerritos College, balancing academics with his growing interest in music. Largely self-taught by earhaving never learned to read musiche expanded his skills beyond ukulele to include guitar, banjo, and cello, often practicing by listening to the radio and transitioning from Hawaiian tunes to rock and roll influences during his teenage years.
The vibrant California folk music scene of the 1950s profoundly shaped Ramos's early style, inspiring him to perform at local coffee houses and high school events with his band, The Defiants, which also secured TV spots. At age 13, he performed the role of the crown prince in a national tour production of The King and I opposite Yul Brynner, gaining initial exposure to professional theater. These experiences honed his versatility as a multi-instrumentalist and vocalist, setting the stage for his entry into the music industry.

Career

The New Christy Minstrels

Larry Ramos joined the New Christy Minstrels in 1962 after being discovered performing at a coffee house in South Gate, California, and auditioning for the group despite initial racial hesitation from producers, as he was the only non-white member. He debuted with the ensemble at The Troubadour in July 1962, quickly settling into a role as a guitarist, banjo player, and vocalist, providing backup harmonies and instrumental support in their large-ensemble folk style.
Ramos contributed to the group's vibrant folk music sound, characterized by harmonious choral arrangements and acoustic instrumentation, during their high-profile live performances throughout the early 1960s. The ensemble, founded by Randy Sparks, frequently appeared as the backup band on The Andy Williams Show, where Ramos's banjo solos and vocal blends helped showcase their energetic stage presence to national audiences. He participated in extensive touring, performing nearly every day for three years, which solidified the group's reputation as a leading folk act and allowed Ramos to emerge as one of its more popular members due to his standout instrumental and vocal talents.
A highlight of Ramos's tenure came in 1963 when the New Christy Minstrels won the Grammy Award for Best Performance by a Chorus for their debut album Presenting the New Christy Minstrels, marking Ramos as the first Asian American to receive the honor and breaking barriers in the predominantly white music industry of the era. This achievement underscored the group's innovative blend of traditional folk with contemporary choral elements, with Ramos's contributions on tracks like the banjo-driven medleys enhancing their recordings and live sets.
Ramos remained with the New Christy Minstrels through 1965, contributing to several albums and performances that captured the folk revival's peak, before departing in January 1966 to spend more time with his growing family and pursue other musical opportunities. His exit highlighted the demanding dynamics of the large ensemble's schedule but paved the way for his later successes in pop music.

The Association

In April 1967, Larry Ramos joined The Association as a replacement for guitarist and vocalist Jules Alexander, who had departed the band to study meditation in India. Ramos, coming from the New Christy Minstrels, brought his expertise as a multi-instrumentalist, serving as the group's lead guitarist, banjo player, and a key vocalist until his departure in 1975. His addition helped stabilize the lineup during a period of rapid success, allowing the band to maintain its elaborate vocal arrangements and live performances.
Ramos quickly became integral to The Association's sound, providing co-lead vocals on the chart-topping single "Windy," which held the No. 1 position on the Billboard Hot 100 for four weeks in 1967, and co-lead vocals on "Never My Love," a No. 2 hit that same year. His tenor voice enriched the band's renowned six-part harmonies, blending seamlessly with the ensemble's sunshine pop style, while his banjo and guitar work added rhythmic texture to tracks on albums like Insight Out and Birthday. The group toured relentlessly across the United States and internationally during the late 1960s, headlining venues like the Hollywood Bowl and performing at festivals, which fostered tight-knit dynamics among members but also strained schedules amid their commercial peak. As the 1970s progressed, The Association experimented with more rock-infused and introspective material, reflecting broader shifts in popular music, though Ramos's instrumental contributions remained a constant in their evolving repertoire.
Ramos left The Association in 1975 to prioritize raising his family, amid the band's transition to a heavier touring emphasis and lineup changes. He rejoined for a 1979 reunion filmed for an HBO special, Then and Now, performing classics like "Windy" and "Cherish" at the Coconut Grove in Los Angeles. Thereafter, Ramos participated in intermittent tours, recordings, and live shows with the group through the 1980s and beyond, often co-leading alongside Russ Giguere as the band adapted its harmonious pop foundations to nostalgic revivals and occasional new material.

Personal life and later years

Family and marriage

Larry Ramos married Helene Ramos in 1964, and their union lasted 50 years until his death in 2014.
The couple had three children together: twin daughters Tracy and Stacy, born in 1965, and a son, Larry Ramos III. Ramos also helped raise Helene's two children from a previous marriage, stepson Terry and stepdaughter Keli, forming a blended family of five children.
The family resided in San Clemente, California, where Ramos balanced the demands of his music career with family responsibilities, often prioritizing time at home over extended tours. After the birth of his twin daughters, Ramos returned from a tour to be present but faced prolonged separations, which led him to make family his central focus in life decisions. His daughter Tracy later announced his passing on social media, reflecting the close family bonds they maintained.
Ramos's family life emphasized love and support, with Helene providing stability during his travels, and the couple celebrating milestones like their 50th anniversary shortly before his health declined.

Health challenges and death

In 2011, Ramos suffered a heart attack on August 31, which marked the beginning of a significant health decline that persisted for the next three years. Despite this, he continued performing with The Association, though his condition increasingly limited his activities.
In 2013, Ramos was diagnosed with metastatic melanoma, a diagnosis he chose to keep private during his lifetime. He underwent treatment for the cancer over the following year, but the illness severely impacted his ability to perform, leading to his retirement from the stage after a farewell concert with the band on February 24, 2014, at the Blue Fox Theatre in Grangeville, Idaho, where proceeds benefited the Relay for Life charity. Ramos spent his final years in Clarkston, Washington, near the Idaho border, supported by his family, including his wife of over 50 years.
Ramos died on April 30, 2014, at a hospital in Clarkston at the age of 72, after a year-long battle with metastatic melanoma. His daughter announced his passing via social media, stating that while his illnesses had been challenging, his death was peaceful and he was surrounded by family.
Bandmates paid immediate tributes to Ramos following his death. Russ Giguere, co-founder of The Association and Ramos's business partner for nearly three decades, described him as "the epitome of the word gentleman" who was "kind, generous, funny and a true friend," adding that the group was "devastated beyond words" and that Ramos would be "sorely missed."

Legacy and recognition

Awards and pioneering achievements

Larry Ramos achieved a historic milestone in 1963 when he became the first Asian American and Filipino American to win a Grammy Award as a member of The New Christy Minstrels. The group received the Grammy for Best Performance by a Chorus for their debut album, Presenting the New Christy Minstrels, released on Columbia Records, which showcased their folk ensemble style and peaked at number 19 on the Billboard 200.
This victory marked a significant breakthrough for Asian Americans in mainstream American music, as Ramos's win predated broader recognition of diverse performers in the industry during an era dominated by white artists. His success with the Grammy highlighted the potential for Filipino American talent to gain national acclaim, paving the way for future generations in folk and pop genres.
During his tenure with The Association, Ramos contributed to the band's multiple Grammy nominations, reflecting their peak commercial success in the late 1960s. The group earned nods in 1967 for Best Performance by a Vocal Group for "Windy" and Best Contemporary Album for Insight Out, as well as in 1968 for Best Performance by a Vocal Group for "Never My Love" and Best Contemporary Album for Birthday. These nominations underscored the band's innovative harmony-driven sound and chart-topping hits, though they did not result in additional wins.
Posthumously, Ramos was honored with the Rock Justice Award in 2019, accepted by his children on behalf of The Association's surviving members, recognizing his enduring contributions to rock and pop music. This accolade affirmed his pioneering role in integrating diverse voices into American popular music.

Cultural impact and recent tributes

Larry Ramos's pioneering presence as a Filipino American musician in the predominantly white pop-folk scene of the 1960s served as a vital symbol of representation, inspiring generations of Asian American artists to pursue mainstream success. As the only person of color in groups like The New Christy Minstrels and The Association, Ramos broke racial barriers during the Civil Rights era, offering visibility to underrepresented communities in American music. Musicians such as Black Eyed Peas member Apl.de.ap and Journey frontman Arnel Pineda have cited his trailblazing career as a direct influence, while peers like musician Gerald Ishibashi recalled Ramos's impact: "There were a lot of kids, like me, growing up as an Asian American, who remember seeing him and saying, Oh my God, theres an Asian American in a mainstream band. I want to be just like him." Ramos himself acknowledged this role, stating, "I know I opened doors for a lot of Orientals, Asians and Filipinos."
His early mastery of the ukulele further contributed to the folk revival movement and broader appreciation for diverse musical traditions in the United States. This foundation not only popularized Hawaiian instrumentation like the ukulele within folk ensembles but also highlighted multicultural influences, promoting diversity in American popular music during a time of limited ethnic representation.
Following his death in 2014, Ramos received renewed posthumous recognition, particularly for his 1963 Grammy win as the first Asian American recipient. Articles from 2023 to 2025, including features in AsAmNews and Tatler Asia, revisited his milestone, emphasizing its enduring significance for Asian representation in music awards. In 2025, the documentary Along Comes Larry: The Larry Ramos Story, directed by Rick Quan, world premiered at the Asia Society Texas Center in Houston on June 27, followed by screenings at the Hawaii International Film Festival in October, chronicling his journey from ukulele prodigy to Grammy winner and underscoring his overlooked legacy. The film screened at events like the Asia Society Texas Center's tribute concert in June 2025, featuring performances by surviving members of The Association, who honored Ramos as a foundational influence on their harmonious sound.
Media coverage in Hawaiian and Filipino communities amplified these tributes, with outlets like Hawaii News Now and KHON2 highlighting the documentary's role in reclaiming Ramos's story for local audiences, while Positively Filipino covered the Asia Society event as a celebration of his Filipino heritage. These efforts have sparked discussions on his contributions to ethnic diversity in the music industry, ensuring his influence resonates in contemporary cultural narratives.

Discography

With the New Christy Minstrels

Larry Ramos joined the New Christy Minstrels as a founding member in 1962, providing lead and harmony vocals, as well as playing guitar and banjo on their recordings during the height of the folk music revival.
The group's debut album, Presenting the New Christy Minstrels (Columbia, 1962), featured Ramos's contributions and peaked at No. 19 on the Billboard 200, staying on the chart for two years; it earned the 1963 Grammy Award for Best Performance by a Chorus.
Subsequent albums during Ramos's tenure included the live recording The New Christy Minstrels in Person (Columbia, 1963), which captured their energetic performances and peaked at No. 30 on the Billboard 200; Ramblin' (Columbia, 1963), a gold-certified release that reached No. 15 on the Billboard 200; Today (Columbia, 1964), peaking at No. 9; Land of Giants (Columbia, 1964), which reached No. 48; Cowboys and Indians (Columbia, 1965), peaking at No. 62; and Chim Chim Cher-ee and Other New Orleans Jazz and Folk Songs (Columbia, 1965), which climbed to No. 47 on the Billboard 200 and received a Grammy nomination for Best Performance by a Chorus. Ramos's multi-instrumental and vocal work added depth to these folk, jazz-influenced, and thematic collections, reflecting the era's blend of traditional and contemporary sounds.

Key singles from this period highlighted the group's commercial success, with Ramos often sharing lead vocals. "Green, Green" (1963, b/w "Denver"), co-written by founder Randy Sparks, became their signature hit, reaching No. 14 on the Billboard Hot 100 and exemplifying the upbeat folk style of the time. Other notable releases included "Saturday Night" (1963, b/w "Sowin' on the Mountain"), peaking at No. 29 on the Hot 100; "Today" (1964, b/w "The Cotton Fields"), hitting No. 17 on the Hot 100 and No. 4 on the Adult Contemporary chart; and "This Land Is Your Land" (1962, b/w "Don't Cry, Suzanne"), which charted at No. 93. "Chim Chim Cher-ee" (1965, from the Disney film Mary Poppins), reached No. 81 on the Hot 100.
For compilations, the 1966 Greatest Hits (Columbia) collected many of these tracks featuring Ramos, reaching No. 76 on the Billboard 200.

With the Association

Larry Ramos joined The Association in early 1967 as guitarist and vocalist, replacing Jules Alexander and helping transition the group's sound from folk harmonies to a more pop-rock orientation with psychedelic influences. His multi-instrumental skills and tenor voice became integral to the band's signature layered harmonies, particularly on their breakthrough album Insight Out, which achieved gold status and marked their commercial peak. Ramos contributed guitar throughout and shared lead vocals on key tracks, enhancing the album's blend of upbeat pop and introspective ballads.
The album Insight Out (1967) featured two major singles showcasing Ramos's vocal prominence: "Windy," where he co-led with Russ Giguere, reaching #1 on the Billboard Hot 100 for four weeks and earning gold certification, and "Never My Love," co-led with Terry Kirkman, peaking at #2 on the same chart and becoming one of the most-played songs of the year. Ramos also provided prominent harmonies on other tracks like "Sometime," solidifying his role in the band's evolving production style that incorporated studio experimentation and rock elements.
On Birthday (1968), Ramos delivered lead vocals on tracks such as "Come On In" and "Barefoot Gentleman," while contributing guitar and backing vocals across the record, which continued the pop-rock shift with more mature lyrical themes. The album's single "Everything That Touches You," featuring Ramos in the harmony stack, climbed to #10 on the Billboard Hot 100, underscoring the band's sustained hit-making ability. Ramos's involvement extended to The Association (1969), where he led on "Yes, I Will" and "Love Affair," adding rhythmic guitar to the self-titled effort's eclectic mix of country-folk and rock influences.
By Stop Your Motor (1971), Ramos's contributions included lead vocals on "Bring Yourself Home" and his composition "It's Gotta Be Real," reflecting a harder rock edge amid the band's commercial challenges. He remained with the group until 1975, recording five studio albums in total and participating in numerous singles that collectively sold millions.
Ramos rejoined for reunion efforts starting in 1979, including the HBO special The Association Reunion and subsequent live performances and recordings in the early 1980s with core members like Jim Yester and Ted Bluechel. These reunions preserved the group's pop-rock harmony style, though no major new studio album emerged from this period; Ramos continued touring sporadically with various lineups into the 2000s.
