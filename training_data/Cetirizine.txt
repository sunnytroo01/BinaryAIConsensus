Cetirizine
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Cetirizine (/strzin/ se-TIR-a-zeen) is a second-generation H1 antihistamine medication that selectively blocks histamine H1 receptors to alleviate symptoms of allergic conditions such as sneezing, runny nose, itchy or watery eyes, and itching of the nose or throat, as well as treating chronic idiopathic urticaria (hives) in adults and children aged 6 months and older. It is the active metabolite of hydroxyzine and is characterized by minimal penetration into the central nervous system, resulting in reduced sedation compared to first-generation antihistamines, with a half-life of approximately 8.3 hours and primary excretion via urine.
Available in oral forms including tablets, chewable tablets, capsules, orally disintegrating tablets, and syrup, as well as intravenous administration for acute urticaria in medical settings, cetirizine typically begins working within one hour and provides relief for up to 24 hours when taken once daily, with or without food. It is indicated for seasonal and perennial allergic rhinitis starting from age 2 years and 6 months, respectively, and is generally well-tolerated, though common side effects include drowsiness, dry mouth, headache, and fatigue.
Precautions include avoiding use in individuals with severe kidney or liver impairment without dose adjustment, and caution with alcohol or other central nervous system depressants due to potential additive drowsiness. A notable risk involves rare but severe itching (pruritus) upon discontinuation after long-term use (three months or more), prompting an FDA-required warning on product labels. Interactions with other sedatives or certain antidepressants should be monitored, and it is not recommended during pregnancy or breastfeeding without medical advice.

Medical uses

Indications

Cetirizine is indicated for the relief of symptoms associated with seasonal allergic rhinitis (hay fever), such as sneezing, nasal itching, rhinorrhea, and ocular symptoms like tearing and redness due to allergens including pollen from trees, grasses, and weeds. It is also approved for the treatment of perennial allergic rhinitis symptoms triggered by indoor allergens like dust mites, animal dander, and molds, from 6 months of age. Seasonal allergic rhinitis is approved from 2 years of age. Additionally, cetirizine addresses uncomplicated skin manifestations of chronic idiopathic urticaria, including hives, itching, and associated discomfort, from 6 months of age. Cetirizine is also indicated for allergic conjunctivitis and pruritic allergic dermatoses.
Clinical evidence from randomized controlled trials supports cetirizine's efficacy in allergic rhinitis, with daily doses of 10 mg achieving 40-50% reductions in total symptom scores compared to placebo across seasonal and perennial forms. Compared to other second-generation antihistamines such as fexofenadine, cetirizine is similarly effective for symptoms including sneezing, nasal itching, and rhinorrhea; however, fexofenadine-pseudoephedrine combinations provide superior relief for nasal congestion due to the added decongestant. Cetirizine may offer slightly faster or more sustained relief in some studies but is associated with mild drowsiness more frequently than fexofenadine. In urticaria, multiple placebo-controlled studies have shown cetirizine to be superior in alleviating wheal formation, erythema, and pruritus, often with response rates exceeding 70% in symptom control.
Compared to first-generation antihistamines like diphenhydramine, cetirizine's second-generation profile results in minimal central nervous system penetration, enabling daytime administration without substantial sedation or cognitive impairment.
Off-label applications include mitigating reactions from insect bites, serving as an adjunct to epinephrine in anaphylaxis prevention to control histamine release.
For these indications in adults and children over 6 years, dosing typically starts at 5-10 mg once daily, adjusted by age and symptom severity; pediatric patients aged 6 months to 5 years receive 2.5 mg once daily, with detailed regimens outlined in the dosage and administration section.

Dosage and administration

Cetirizine is administered orally, independent of food intake, with water. It is available in forms such as tablets, chewable tablets, capsules, orally disintegrating tablets, and syrup.
The recommended dose for adults and children older than 12 years is 10 mg once daily. For children aged 612 years, 5 mg twice daily or 10 mg once daily. For children aged 26 years, 2.5 mg twice daily or 5 mg once daily. For children aged 6 months to 2 years, 2.5 mg once daily, which may be increased to 2.5 mg twice daily (maximum 5 mg per day) if needed.
Dose adjustments are necessary in certain populations. In elderly patients over 65 years, initiate with 5 mg daily due to potential increased sensitivity, though the full 10 mg may be used if tolerated, with safe use documented up to 6 months or longer. For patients with renal impairment, the dose is reduced depending on creatinine clearance; for mild to moderate impairment (creatinine clearance 11 to 31 mL/min), reduce to 5 mg daily in those 12 years and older; cetirizine is not recommended in severe renal impairment (creatinine clearance below 11 mL/min) without dose adjustment and close monitoring. Similar reductions to 5 mg daily apply in moderate hepatic impairment.
The onset of action occurs within 20 to 60 minutes following oral administration, with antihistaminic effects persisting for at least 24 hours, allowing for once-daily dosing. The course of treatment is individual, usually 37 days for acute allergic conditions or longer as prescribed by a physician. Therapy duration varies by condition: short-term use suffices for acute allergic episodes, while long-term administration is appropriate for chronic allergic rhinitis or urticaria, with safety established in clinical studies up to 12 months or more in adults and children. It is recommended to consult a physician before use.

Available formulations

Cetirizine is primarily available in oral formulations to accommodate various age groups and preferences. Standard film-coated tablets containing cetirizine hydrochloride (also referred to as cetirizine dihydrochloride BP) as the active ingredient are offered in 5 mg and 10 mg strengths, suitable for adults and children aged 6 years and older; for example, the 10 mg strength is marketed in Bangladesh under the brand name Alatrol by Square Pharmaceuticals PLC. Liquid forms include an oral solution at 1 mg/mL (equivalent to 5 mg/5 mL) and a syrup at the same concentration, facilitating administration to infants and young children starting from 6 months of age. Chewable tablets provide options in 2.5 mg, 5 mg, and 10 mg strengths, designed for ease of use in pediatric patients aged 2 years and older. Other oral variants, such as capsules and orally disintegrating tablets, are also marketed in 10 mg strengths for rapid dissolution.
Combination products pair cetirizine with pseudoephedrine to address allergic rhinitis accompanied by nasal congestion. These extended-release formulations typically contain 5 mg of cetirizine hydrochloride with 120 mg of pseudoephedrine hydrochloride, available for adults and adolescents aged 12 years and older.
Cetirizine products should be stored at controlled room temperature between 20C and 25C (68F and 77F), with excursions permitted to 15C to 30C (59F to 86F), and protected from light and moisture to maintain stability. The typical shelf life is 2 to 3 years from the manufacturing date when stored properly, though specific expiration dates should be checked on packaging.
Following the expiration of its original patent on December 25, 2007, cetirizine has been widely available as a generic medication in multiple countries. It holds over-the-counter (OTC) status for adults and children aged 2 years and older in the United States since 2007, as well as in most European countries, Australia, and other regions, allowing non-prescription access for self-treatment of allergic conditions.

Contraindications and precautions

Contraindications

Cetirizine is contraindicated in patients with known hypersensitivity to cetirizine hydrochloride, any of its components, or hydroxyzine, as this may lead to severe allergic reactions including anaphylaxis.
Cetirizine is also contraindicated in patients with severe renal impairment (creatinine clearance <10 mL/min), during pregnancy, during the period of breastfeeding, and in children under 2 years of age (for tablets, under 6 years of age).
Relative contraindications include severe renal impairment, where cetirizine accumulation can occur due to reduced clearance; in patients with end-stage renal disease (creatinine clearance <10 mL/min), dose adjustment is required, such as reducing to 5 mg daily (or every other day in severe cases), even on dialysis, with close monitoring for adverse effects. In elderly patients, caution is advised due to potential decreased renal function, which may increase the risk of adverse effects, and dosing should be selected carefully with monitoring.
Cetirizine is not recommended for use in neonates or premature infants, as safety and efficacy have not been established in children under 6 months of age.

Precautions

If drowsiness occurs with cetirizine use, it is recommended to take the dose in the evening. Patients should avoid driving or operating machinery until they know how the medication affects them. If the drowsiness is bothersome, consult a doctor, who may suggest a less sedating alternative such as fexofenadine. Always read the medication leaflet or consult a pharmacist or doctor for personalized advice.

Use in special populations

Cetirizine is classified under the former FDA Pregnancy Category B, based on animal reproduction studies showing no fetal risk and inadequate human studies, though it may be used when the potential benefits justify the possible risks to the fetus. Observational studies, including a prospective cohort involving 228 known pregnancy outcomes with cetirizine exposure primarily in the first trimester, have reported no increased risk of major congenital malformations above background rates of 2-4%. A meta-analysis of multiple cohort studies similarly found no association between cetirizine exposure and adverse fetal outcomes, such as spontaneous abortion or preterm delivery.
During lactation, cetirizine is minimally excreted into breast milk, with an average relative infant dose of approximately 1.8-1.9% of the maternal weight-adjusted dose following a 10 mg daily intake. This low transfer level renders cetirizine generally compatible with breastfeeding for occasional or short-term use, though infants should be monitored for potential drowsiness or irritability.
In pediatric patients, cetirizine is approved for use starting from 6 months of age for conditions such as allergic rhinitis and chronic urticaria, with dosing scaled by age to minimize sedation: 2.5 mg once daily for children 6-23 months and 2.5 mg once or twice daily (up to 5 mg total) for ages 2-5 years. Clinical trials in over 1,000 children under 12 years demonstrated good tolerability, though somnolence occurs more frequently than in adults.
For geriatric patients, reduced renal clearanceapproximately 40% lower than in younger adultsnecessitates starting with a 5 mg daily dose to mitigate accumulation and potential adverse effects like confusion or dry mouth. Close monitoring is advised, as elderly individuals may exhibit heightened sensitivity to anticholinergic effects despite no overall increased incidence of serious adverse events in clinical studies.
Dose adjustments are required in renal impairment; for adults with creatinine clearance less than 30 mL/min, the dose should be reduced to 5 mg daily or every other day to account for prolonged half-life and decreased clearance. No dose adjustment is required in patients with hepatic impairment alone. However, in cases of concomitant hepatic and renal impairment, dose adjustment should follow renal guidelines. Use with caution in patients with severe hepatic impairment and monitor for adverse effects.

Adverse effects

Common adverse effects

The common adverse effects of cetirizine include somnolence, headache, dry mouth, and fatigue. The most common adverse effect associated with cetirizine is somnolence, reported in placebo-controlled clinical trials at rates of 11% for the 5 mg dose and 14% for the 10 mg dose, compared to 6% with placebo. This effect is dose-dependent and occurs less frequently than with first-generation antihistamines; for example, unlike promethazine, a first-generation antihistamine often used intentionally for its high sedating effects causing significant drowsiness and psychomotor impairment, cetirizine's sedation is mild and affects approximately 10-14% of users without major disruption for most, though patients should be advised to avoid activities requiring mental alertness, such as driving, until its impact is known.
Fatigue affects 2% to 6% of users, while dry mouth is observed in 2% to 5%, based on data from phase III trials involving adults. These symptoms are usually mild and transient, resolving with continued use or time. Headache and dizziness each occur in 1% to 3% of patients, often without leading to discontinuation.
Gastrointestinal effects, including nausea and abdominal pain, are reported in 1% to 2% of individuals in clinical studies, with placebo-adjusted rates confirming their association with the drug. In pediatric patients aged 6 to 11 years, the overall profile is similar but with a higher incidence of headache.
Cetirizine does not commonly cause weight gain and is not listed as a side effect in many major sources such as WebMD and RxList. However, some databases (e.g., Drugs.com) report "increased weight" and "increased appetite" as possible metabolic side effects, with frequency not reported, likely rare or based on post-marketing reports.
Management of these common effects typically involves dose reduction if symptoms persist, alongside supportive measures such as increased fluid intake for dry mouth or rest to alleviate fatigue. For patients experiencing significant somnolence, alternative second-generation antihistamines such as fexofenadine, which has a lower risk of causing drowsiness, may be considered. Patients should consult a healthcare professional for personalized recommendations and always read the medication leaflet. Discontinuation due to adverse effects is uncommon, occurring in less than 1% of trial participants.

Rare adverse effects

Cetirizine (Zyrtec) does not commonly cause drooling; typical side effects include drowsiness, dry mouth, fatigue, and headache. Increased salivation is reported rarely (frequency not known), which could potentially relate to drooling in some cases, but drooling itself is not listed as a side effect in major sources.

Serious adverse effects

Serious adverse effects associated with cetirizine are rare, typically occurring at incidences below 0.1% to 1% in clinical studies and more commonly identified through post-marketing surveillance. These events demand prompt medical evaluation, as they can lead to significant morbidity if untreated.
Cardiovascular effects include rare instances of tachycardia and severe hypotension, reported in post-marketing surveillance; patients with pre-existing cardiac conditions may require monitoring.
Neurological effects encompass seizures, particularly in predisposed individuals or overdose scenarios, and very rare hallucinations. These manifestations are infrequent but can be severe, with post-marketing data indicating agitation, aggressive reactions, and suicidal ideation in exceptional cases.
Hepatic effects involve elevated liver enzymes in 1% to 4% of patients in clinical trials, which are generally reversible upon drug discontinuation. Rare cases of liver function disorders, including cholestasis and hepatitis, have been documented in post-marketing surveillance.
Allergic effects in hypersensitive individuals may present as angioedema or severe rash, representing paradoxical hypersensitivity reactions. Anaphylaxis has been reported rarely post-marketing.
Post-marketing surveillance has captured isolated cases of agranulocytosis and thrombocytopenia, underscoring the need for hematologic monitoring in at-risk patients. 
Risk factors for these serious effects are elevated in elderly patients and those on polypharmacy, where reduced clearance and drug interactions may amplify susceptibility.

Discontinuation effects

Upon discontinuation of long-term cetirizine therapy, some patients experience a rebound phenomenon characterized by intense pruritus, or severe itching, which is not a true withdrawal syndrome but rather a hypersensitivity reaction. This effect has been documented in postmarketing surveillance, with the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) identifying 209 cases worldwide between April 2017 and July 2023, primarily following abrupt cessation after prolonged daily use. The pruritus typically manifests as widespread, intolerable itching that can significantly impair quality of life, sometimes accompanied by urticaria in about 20% of reported cases.
The onset of symptoms usually occurs within a few days of stopping the medication, with a median time of 2 days (range: 0.55 days) based on analysis of 91 cases. Duration varies but is often self-limited, with a median of 2 weeks (range: 1 day to 6 months) in 55 reported instances; however, symptoms can persist for months in rare cases, leading to serious outcomes such as disability in 48 reports or hospitalization in 3. The exact mechanism remains unknown, though it is theorized to involve prolonged high occupancy of H1 receptors by the antihistamine, potentially resulting in rebound hypersensitivity upon clearance of the drug from the system.
Risk factors include extended duration of use, with 92% of cases occurring after more than 3 months of daily therapy (median 33 months, range: 1 week to 23 years), and higher doses may contribute, though data are limited. The condition predominantly affects females (about 75% of cases) and individuals around median age 38 years (range: 671 years).
Management strategies focus on symptom relief and prevention of recurrence. Restarting cetirizine resolves pruritus in approximately 90% of cases, and subsequent gradual tapering (e.g., reducing dose over weeks) prevents rebound in about 38% of those attempts; adjunctive therapies may include topical antipruritics, corticosteroids, or short-term use of alternative antihistamines. Case reports and surveys, including a 2019 analysis of 146 events in the FDA Adverse Event Reporting System, indicate resolution without long-term sequelae in most patients, supporting a causal association with discontinuation rather than underlying disease progression. The FDA now requires labeling updates for cetirizine products to warn of this rare risk.

Drug interactions

Pharmacokinetic interactions

Cetirizine undergoes minimal hepatic metabolism, with approximately 50% to 60% of an administered dose excreted unchanged in the urine within 24 hours, primarily via glomerular filtration and active tubular secretion. This limited involvement of cytochrome P450 (CYP) enzymesestimated at less than 10% of metabolism through oxidative O-dealkylationresults in a low potential for pharmacokinetic interactions compared to first-generation antihistamines, which rely more heavily on hepatic CYP pathways. No pharmacokinetic interaction has been observed with azithromycin.
Among CYP3A4 inhibitors, ritonavir has been shown to significantly alter cetirizine pharmacokinetics. In a crossover study of healthy male volunteers, coadministration of ritonavir (600 mg twice daily) with cetirizine (10 mg once daily) increased the area under the curve (AUC) of cetirizine by 42% (from 3406 to 4840 gh/L) and decreased its apparent total body clearance by 29% (from 49.9 to 35.3 mL/min), with the elimination half-life prolonged by 53% (from 7.8 to 11.9 hours). In contrast, ketoconazole (400 mg once daily), another potent CYP3A4 inhibitor, does not produce clinically significant changes in cetirizine exposure or disposition when coadministered at standard doses.
Coadministration with theophylline (400 mg once daily) results in a modest 16% decrease in cetirizine clearance, though the mechanism remains unclear and the disposition of theophylline is unaffected. Etoricoxib may decrease the renal excretion rate of cetirizine, potentially leading to higher serum levels, but no major clinical interactions or issues have been reported. Food has no effect on the extent of cetirizine absorption (AUC), although it delays the time to maximum concentration (T_max) by approximately 1.7 hours and reduces peak concentration (C_max) by 23%. Overall, cetirizine's pharmacokinetic profile confers a favorable safety margin for concurrent use with most medications, with monitoring recommended primarily in cases involving potent inhibitors of renal secretion or when substantial exposure increases are anticipated, such as with ritonavir.

Pharmacodynamic interactions

Cetirizine, as a second-generation H1-receptor antagonist, exhibits pharmacodynamic interactions primarily through additive effects on central nervous system (CNS) depression and other physiological pathways, despite its relatively low penetration of the blood-brain barrier. These interactions occur at the receptor or tissue level rather than through alterations in drug metabolism or absorption.
Concurrent use of cetirizine with CNS depressants such as alcohol, benzodiazepines (e.g., lorazepam), or opioids (e.g., codeine) can potentiate sedation and psychomotor impairment. Although cetirizine causes less sedation than first-generation antihistamines, the additive CNS effects may increase drowsiness, dizziness, and impaired coordination, particularly in susceptible individuals. Patients are advised to avoid or limit alcohol intake during cetirizine therapy to mitigate these risks.
Cetirizine possesses minimal anticholinergic activity compared to older antihistamines, but when combined with anticholinergic agents like oxybutynin, there may be additive effects leading to exacerbated dry mouth, constipation, or urinary retention. These symptoms arise from enhanced blockade of muscarinic receptors, though cetirizine's contribution is limited. Monitoring is recommended in patients with predisposing conditions such as glaucoma or prostatic hypertrophy.
In patients receiving beta-blockers (e.g., metoprolol), cetirizine use for allergic conditions requires caution due to the potential for beta-blockers to mask early signs of anaphylaxis, such as tachycardia, thereby complicating timely recognition and management of severe allergic reactions. This interaction is not direct but stems from the physiological antagonism of beta-adrenergic responses during hypersensitivity events.
Combinations of cetirizine with other antihistamines (e.g., diphenhydramine) generally lack significant pharmacodynamic synergy at H1 receptors but should be avoided to prevent additive antihistaminic effects, increased sedation, or inadvertent overdose. No major antagonistic interactions have been reported among H1 antagonists.
Concurrent administration of cetirizine and fexofenadine (Allegra), both second-generation H1-receptor antagonists, is generally not recommended. No significant pharmacokinetic interactions exist between the two drugs, but combining them offers little additional therapeutic benefit and may increase the risk of adverse effects such as drowsiness, dry mouth, or dizziness due to additive pharmacodynamic effects. Patients should consult a healthcare provider before combining cetirizine (Zyrtec) and fexofenadine, particularly for severe allergies.
Concurrent administration of cetirizine and loratadine (Claritin), both second-generation H1-receptor antagonists, is generally not recommended. No significant pharmacokinetic interactions exist between the two drugs, but combining them offers little additional therapeutic benefit and may increase the risk of adverse effects such as drowsiness, dry mouth, dizziness, and other side effects due to additive pharmacodynamic effects and therapeutic duplication. If switching from one to the other, a washout period of at least 12-24 hours (depending on formulation) is advisable to minimize overlap and risks. Patients should consult a healthcare provider for personalized advice before combining or switching.
Concurrent administration of cetirizine and desloratadine, both second-generation H1-receptor antagonists, is generally not recommended. No significant pharmacokinetic interactions exist between the two drugs, but concurrent use represents therapeutic duplication and offers little additional therapeutic benefit while potentially increasing the risk of adverse effects such as drowsiness, dry mouth, dizziness, and other side effects due to additive pharmacodynamic effects. Patients should consult a healthcare provider before combining cetirizine and desloratadine or switching between them.
No significant pharmacokinetic or pharmacodynamic interactions have been reported between cetirizine and acetaminophen, zinc, or fenofibrate. No significant interactions exist between other pairs such as fenofibrate-cetirizine or acetaminophen-cetirizine. However, fenofibrate may moderately increase blood levels and effects of acetaminophen, potentially requiring dose adjustment or closer monitoring by a healthcare provider. No significant interactions were found involving zinc with the other drugs. Always consult a healthcare professional before combining medications.
Cetirizine has negligible effects on cardiac repolarization under normal dosing, with minimal risk of QT interval prolongation; coadministration with azithromycin shows no pharmacokinetic interaction and no significant QT interval changes in healthy volunteers, though both drugs may slightly prolong the QTc interval in some contexts. The combination is clinically safe for short courses, particularly in patients without a history of heart disease or other risk factors such as congenital long QT syndrome or electrolyte imbalances.30613-6/fulltext)

Overdose

Symptoms and signs

Symptoms of cetirizine overdose primarily involve central nervous system depression and mild anticholinergic effects, with severity correlating to the ingested dose. In mild cases, often involving 25 to 50 mg in adults, common manifestations include drowsiness, dry mouth, and tachycardia. These effects stem from the drug's antihistaminic and mild anticholinergic properties, which become exaggerated at supratherapeutic levels.
Overdoses at doses 50 mg can present with neurological and autonomic symptoms such as confusion, dizziness, fatigue, headache, malaise, mydriasis, restlessness, tremor, stupor, tachycardia, and urinary retention, reflecting enhanced central nervous system involvement and peripheral anticholinergic activity.
Life-threatening complications such as seizures, coma, and respiratory depression are uncommon with cetirizine due to its favorable safety profile compared to first-generation antihistamines. Fatalities from isolated cetirizine overdose are extremely rare, with limited case reports in the literature.
Children face heightened risks from overdose owing to their smaller body weight, which amplifies the effective dose per kilogram; symptoms have been observed in pediatric cases at relatively low absolute amounts, such as restlessness and irritability in an 18-month-old ingesting approximately 180 mg. Agitation, hyperthermia, hallucinations, and fixed dilated pupils have also been reported in young children following accidental ingestion.
The time course of symptoms generally begins within 1 to 2 hours of ingestion, with peak effects around 4 hours, aligning with the drug's pharmacokinetic profile of rapid absorption and a peak plasma concentration at about 1 hour post-dose.
Laboratory findings in cetirizine overdose lack specific diagnostic markers, though routine evaluations may reveal no abnormal blood chemistry or hematology in many cases; transient elevations in liver enzymes have been noted but resolve upon discontinuation. Electrocardiography is recommended to monitor for potential QT interval changes, although cetirizine is not associated with significant QT prolongation even at high doses.

Management

Management of cetirizine overdose focuses on supportive care, as no specific antidote exists, and the drug has a low toxicity profile in most cases. Initial assessment prioritizes the airway, breathing, and circulation (ABCs), with continuous monitoring of vital signs including heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, temperature, and electrocardiogram (ECG) to detect potential tachycardia, arrhythmias, or QT prolongation. A thorough history should be obtained to identify the ingested dose, time of ingestion, co-ingestants, and patient factors such as renal impairment, which can prolong cetirizine's half-life.
Decontamination measures are limited; administration of activated charcoal is recommended if the ingestion occurred within 1 hour and the patient is alert with an intact airway, but induction of emesis with ipecac is not advised due to risks of aspiration and delayed care. Gastric lavage may be considered in cases of massive recent ingestion after securing the airway, though it is rarely indicated for cetirizine alone.
Supportive care forms the cornerstone of treatment. Intravenous fluids should be administered to manage tachycardia or hypotension, while benzodiazepines such as lorazepam or diazepam are used for agitation or seizures. Hyperthermia, if present, requires cooling measures like evaporative cooling or antipyretics, and cardiac monitoring is essential for any ECG abnormalities. There is no role for physostigmine or other anticholinesterase agents in second-generation antihistamine overdoses like cetirizine due to minimal anticholinergic effects.
Enhancement of elimination is generally not effective; hemodialysis removes less than 10% of cetirizine due to its high plasma protein binding (93%) and large volume of distribution, though it may be considered in patients with severe renal failure and life-threatening symptoms.
Disposition depends on severity: asymptomatic or mild cases (e.g., ingestions below 150 mg in adults) may require 6-12 hours of observation in an emergency department or at home with poison control guidance, while severe cases involving altered mental status or cardiovascular instability warrant intensive care unit admission. Data from poison control centers indicate that most patients recover fully with supportive measures, often within hours to a day, as seen in cases of drowsiness resolving after rest and monitoring. Intentional overdoses necessitate psychiatric evaluation prior to discharge.

Pharmacology

Pharmacodynamics

Cetirizine is a selective antagonist and inverse agonist at peripheral histamine H1 receptors, where it stabilizes the inactive conformation of the receptor and inhibits histamine-mediated responses such as vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, and itching. By competitively binding to H1 receptors on effector cells like endothelial cells, smooth muscle cells, and immune cells, cetirizine prevents the activation of Gq-protein-coupled signaling pathways that lead to the release of intracellular calcium and subsequent physiological effects associated with allergic reactions. Its high affinity for the human H1 receptor, with a dissociation constant (Ki) of 6 nM, contributes to its potency, while it exhibits no significant binding affinity for H2, H3, or H4 histamine receptors or other major neurotransmitter receptors.
Due to its physicochemical properties, including moderate lipophilicity and recognition as a substrate by P-glycoprotein efflux transporters, cetirizine demonstrates minimal penetration across the blood-brain barrier, with less than 10% crossing into the central nervous system at therapeutic doses. This limited central H1 receptor occupancytypically around 10-30%accounts for its reduced sedative potential compared to first-generation antihistamines. Additionally, cetirizine displays weak antimuscarinic activity and some affinity for serotonin (5-HT) receptors, though these interactions are negligible at clinical concentrations and do not contribute meaningfully to its primary therapeutic effects.
Beyond H1 blockade, cetirizine exhibits anti-inflammatory properties independent of histamine antagonism, including the suppression of inflammatory cell recruitment (e.g., eosinophils and neutrophils) and the downregulation of pro-inflammatory cytokines and chemokines in allergic conditions. These effects are mediated through inhibition of pathways such as NF-B and reduced adhesion molecule expression on endothelial cells. The drug's pharmacodynamic profile supports a rapid onset of action, with significant H1 receptor inhibition occurring within 20-60 minutes of oral administration, and a sustained duration of effect lasting approximately 24 hours due to prolonged receptor occupancy.

Pharmacokinetics

Cetirizine is rapidly absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract after oral administration, achieving a bioavailability of 70% to 85%. The time to peak plasma concentration (Tmax) occurs within 0.5 to 1 hour, and the pharmacokinetics demonstrate linearity for doses up to 20 mg, with no significant accumulation upon multiple dosing. Food intake may delay absorption slightly but does not alter overall exposure.
The drug distributes widely in the body, with a volume of distribution of approximately 0.5 L/kg. Cetirizine is highly bound to plasma proteins (93%), primarily albumin, across a wide concentration range. Due to its hydrophilic nature and limited lipophilicity, cetirizine exhibits restricted entry into the central nervous system, contributing to its low sedating potential.
Metabolism of cetirizine is minimal, with less than 10% undergoing oxidative O-dealkylation to an inactive metabolite; cytochrome P450 enzymes, including CYP3A4, play only a minor role in this process. Approximately 50% of the dose is excreted unchanged in the urine.
Elimination occurs predominantly via renal excretion, with about 70% of the dose recovered in urine (including metabolites) and 10-13% in feces. The terminal elimination half-life in healthy adults averages 8 to 10 hours. In patients with renal impairment, the half-life prolongs to around 20 hours due to reduced clearance. In the elderly, clearance is decreased by approximately 40%, primarily reflecting age-related declines in renal function. Children exhibit faster elimination, with a half-life of about 6 hours, approaching adult values by age 12.

Chemistry

Chemical properties

Cetirizine has the molecular formula CHClNO and a molecular weight of 388.9 g/mol for the free base, while the commonly used dihydrochloride salt form has the formula CHClNO2HCl and a molecular weight of 461.82 g/mol.
The chemical structure of cetirizine is that of a piperazine derivative, specifically 2-piperazin-1-yl]ethoxy]acetic acid, which is a metabolite of hydroxyzine. It contains a chiral center at the carbon linking the phenyl and chlorophenyl groups but is administered as a racemic mixture.
Physically, cetirizine dihydrochloride appears as a white crystalline powder. It is freely soluble in water, with reported solubility values around 10 mg/mL in phosphate-buffered saline at pH 7.2. The compound exhibits pKa values of approximately 2.2 (carboxylic acid), 2.9 (conjugate acid of piperazine), and 8.0 (piperazine nitrogen).
Cetirizine dihydrochloride demonstrates good stability under neutral pH conditions and is resistant to base hydrolysis in 1 M NaOH as well as thermolysis at elevated temperatures with moderate humidity. However, it degrades under strong acidic or basic conditions and is sensitive to UV light, particularly in the presence of oxidants like chlorine.
Identification of cetirizine relies on characteristic spectroscopic data, including infrared (IR) absorption bands for the carboxylic acid (around 1710 cm), C-N stretch (1100-1300 cm), and aromatic C-H (3000-3100 cm), as well as nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) signals such as H NMR peaks at 7.2-7.4 ppm for aromatic protons and 3.5-4.0 ppm for methylene groups adjacent to oxygen and nitrogen. The logP value, indicating lipophilicity, is 2.8 (experimental octanol-water partition coefficient).

Synthesis

Cetirizine is synthesized through two primary routes: a multi-step process starting from piperazine derivatives and a more direct oxidation of hydroxyzine. The original industrial process, developed by UCB Pharma and patented in 1982, involves the alkylation of 1-piperazine with an omega-haloalkoxyacetic acid derivative, such as methyl (2-chloroethoxy)acetate, in the presence of an acid acceptor like sodium bicarbonate or triethylamine, followed by hydrolysis of the ester to the carboxylic acid using a mineral base such as sodium hydroxide in aqueous or hydroalcoholic media at 20100C. This route achieves high yields, typically 8091% for key steps, under mild conditions in inert solvents like toluene or dimethylformamide, avoiding harsh reagents and enabling scalable production.
An alternative synthesis begins with hydroxyzine, piperazin-1-yl]ethoxy]ethanol, as the starting material, leveraging its structural similarity to cetirizine. The process entails a two-stage oxidation of the terminal primary alcohol group (-CH2CH2OH) to the carboxylic acid (-CH2COOH): first, selective oxidation to the aldehyde using catalysts like TEMPO with sodium hypochlorite in ethyl acetate or methylene chloride at -10 to 30C for 0.55 hours, followed by further oxidation to the acid with hydrogen peroxide or sodium chlorite in a buffered organic solvent at 040C for 13 hours. The overall molar yield exceeds 80%, with reaction conditions maintained under mild, controlled temperatures to minimize side reactions and ensure high purity. The resulting cetirizine is then converted to the dihydrochloride salt by treatment with dry hydrogen chloride gas in acetone or butanone, followed by recrystallization.
Cetirizine is produced as a racemic mixture, with the chiral center at the benzhydryl carbon requiring no resolution step, as the (R)-enantiomer (levocetirizine) is addressed in separate syntheses. Post-2000, generic manufacturing has adopted simplified variants of these routes, often optimizing the piperazine alkylation for cost efficiency while retaining the patented core chemistry.

Society and culture

Brand names and availability

Cetirizine is marketed under several major brand names globally, including Zyrtec (commonly pronounced "ZIR-tek" , rhyming with "sir tech") in the United States as the original brand, Reactine in Canada, Zirtek in the United Kingdom, and Alatrol in Bangladesh. Alatrol, manufactured by Square Pharmaceuticals PLC, contains 10 mg of cetirizine hydrochloride (also referred to as cetirizine dihydrochloride BP) as the active ingredient per film-coated tablet and is used as an antihistamine for allergic conditions. Combination products, such as Zyrtec-D, which pairs cetirizine with pseudoephedrine for enhanced allergy and congestion relief, are also available in select markets like the US.
Generic versions of cetirizine became available in the United States following the expiration of the Zyrtec patent in 2007 and are sold over-the-counter for adults and children over age 2. In some countries, however, cetirizine remains available by prescription only, particularly for pediatric formulations or higher doses.
Cetirizine is included on the World Health Organization's Model List of Essential Medicines, reflecting its importance for treating allergic conditions, and is accessible in over 100 countries worldwide through low-cost generic options, often priced below $0.10 per dose. Syrup formulations are commonly used for pediatric patients across regions, providing age-appropriate dosing for children as young as 6 months in approved settings.
While nasal spray versions of cetirizine have been under development for targeted delivery in allergic rhinitis, they are not yet a standard commercial formulation. Occasional supply shortages of cetirizine products, including oral solutions, occurred in the 2020s due to manufacturing constraints, but these have largely been resolved by 2025 with increased production from multiple suppliers.

Development history

Cetirizine was developed by the Belgian pharmaceutical company UCB in the late 1970s and early 1980s as the primary active metabolite of hydroxyzine, a first-generation antihistamine, with the goal of creating a more selective H1-receptor antagonist with reduced central nervous system penetration. This metabolite approach aimed to improve efficacy for allergic conditions while minimizing sedative effects associated with earlier antihistamines. The compound, chemically known as 2-piperazin-1-yl]ethoxy]acetic acid, was first synthesized through oxidation of hydroxyzine, marking a key step in second-generation antihistamine innovation during that era.
Preclinical and early clinical development focused on confirming cetirizine's antihistaminic potency and pharmacokinetic profile, leading to phase I and II trials in the mid-1980s that demonstrated rapid onset and sustained relief from histamine-induced symptoms without significant cardiac effects. Phase III trials conducted throughout the 1980s, involving thousands of patients with seasonal allergic rhinitis and chronic urticaria, established its superior efficacy over placebo and comparable or better performance against first-generation agents like hydroxyzine, with symptom reduction rates exceeding 50% in key endpoints such as pruritus and wheal suppression. These multicenter, randomized, double-blind studies, primarily in Europe, supported regulatory submissions and highlighted cetirizine's once-daily dosing potential.
Cetirizine received its first regulatory approval in Belgium in 1987 under the brand name Zyrtec, marketed by UCB for the treatment of allergic rhinitis and urticaria in adults. In the United States, the FDA approved cetirizine hydrochloride in 1995 as a prescription medication for seasonal and perennial allergic rhinitis and chronic idiopathic urticaria, initially for adults and children aged 12 years and older, based on the accumulated European trial data and bridging U.S. studies. Subsequent pediatric extensions followed, with approval in 1996 for children aged 6 to 11 years and in 1998 for those aged 2 to 5 years, supported by pharmacokinetic and efficacy data from age-appropriate trials showing similar exposure and response profiles to adults. A major milestone occurred in 2007 when the FDA granted over-the-counter (OTC) status for cetirizine in the U.S., reflecting post-marketing surveillance confirming its safety profile and broad accessibility for self-treatment of mild allergies.
Further research in the late 1990s and early 2000s evolved from cetirizine's racemic structure, with studies isolating its active R-enantiomer, levocetirizine, which demonstrated approximately twice the H1-receptor affinity and potentially lower dosing requirements. These investigations, including enantiomer-specific pharmacokinetic analyses, paved the way for levocetirizine's approval in Europe in 2001 as a refined alternative for allergic conditions, building directly on cetirizine's established foundation.
Early in its development, cetirizine faced scrutiny over potential sedative effects, leading to its initial classification as prescription-only in many markets despite its peripheral selectivity; concerns stemmed from isolated reports of drowsiness in sensitive populations during phase III trials. However, subsequent long-term studies and meta-analyses in the 1990s and 2000s, including placebo-controlled assessments of somnolence via psychomotor testing, largely disproved significant central effects at standard 10 mg doses, with sedation incidence comparable to placebo (around 5-10%) and far below first-generation antihistamines. This evidence supported its OTC transition and reinforced cetirizine's role as a nonsedating option in clinical guidelines.

Legal and regulatory status

In the United States, according to current FDA labeling, there are no adequate and well-controlled studies of cetirizine in pregnant women, but animal reproduction studies have not shown fetal risk; it should be used during pregnancy only if clearly needed. The FDA approved cetirizine for over-the-counter (OTC) use in 2007 for individuals aged 2 years and older. It is not designated as a controlled substance under the Controlled Substances Act.
The European Medicines Agency (EMA) has authorized cetirizine for marketing in the European Union since 1987, with no scheduling as a controlled substance. Cetirizine is included on the World Health Organization's (WHO) Model List of Essential Medicines as a complementary medicine for the treatment of urticaria and allergic disorders, appearing on the 24th list published in 2025. Globally, cetirizine is not classified as a narcotic or controlled substance, though in some countries in Asia and Africa, it requires a prescription to ensure safe use, particularly for pediatric populations.
The original patent held by UCB Pharma for cetirizine expired in the European Union in 2003 and in the United States in 2007, enabling the approval and market entry of generic versions thereafter. Generic cetirizine has since been widely approved by regulatory agencies, including the FDA and EMA, under abbreviated pathways demonstrating bioequivalence to the reference product.
As of 2025, no new regulatory restrictions have been imposed on cetirizine worldwide, and its generic formulations continue to benefit from harmonized International Council for Harmonisation (ICH) guidelines, which facilitate streamlined approvals for equivalents across member regions.
