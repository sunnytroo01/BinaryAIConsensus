National Governors Association

1x
 The National Governors Association (NGA), founded in 1908 following a meeting of governors with President Theodore Roosevelt to address conservation issues, is the bipartisan organization representing the governors of the 50 U.S. states, five territories, and commonwealths. It functions as a collective voice for state leaders, promoting federalism, facilitating information exchange on best practices, and advocating bipartisan priorities with the federal government to enhance policy outcomes at state, national, and global levels. Through its Center for Best Practices, established in 1997, and Office of State-Federal Relations, created in 1967, the NGA provides technical assistance, management support, and resources to governors and their staffs, focusing on key policy areas including workforce development, education, infrastructure, health, and homeland security. The organization hosts annual Winter and Summer Meetings to foster collaboration and has driven initiatives such as skills-based hiring networks and entrepreneurship growth strategies, emphasizing practical, state-led solutions over federal overreach. While committed to nonpartisan cooperation, the NGA has experienced tensions in maintaining unity amid partisan divides on federal interventions, as evidenced by recent threats of withdrawal from some member states.

History

Founding and Early Years (19081940s)

The National Governors Conference, predecessor to the modern National Governors Association, originated from a White House conference convened by President Theodore Roosevelt on May 1315, 1908, in Washington, D.C., to address the conservation of natural resources amid growing concerns over resource depletion. All but one of the nation's governors attended the event, which featured Roosevelt's opening address, "Conservation as a National Duty," emphasizing the need for coordinated state and federal action to prevent waste of forests, water, and minerals. At the conference's conclusion, attendees resolved to establish a permanent bipartisan forum for governors to discuss interstate issues, share best practices, and influence national policy, marking the formal inception of the organization as a vehicle for collective gubernatorial action beyond conservation.
In its initial decade, the conference prioritized resource management and infrastructure, convening annual meetings to tackle topics such as highway development, agricultural standardization, and public health coordination, reflecting governors' roles in implementing progressive-era reforms at the state level. Proceedings from gatherings in 1910, 1911, and 1914 documented discussions on uniform state laws and federal-state partnerships, underscoring the body's evolution from ad hoc conservation advocacy to a platform for addressing economic and administrative challenges amid rapid industrialization. During World War I, the organization facilitated gubernatorial input on mobilization efforts, though its influence remained limited by the informal structure and focus on voluntary cooperation rather than binding resolutions.
The interwar period saw expanded scope amid economic turbulence, with 1920s meetings emphasizing transportation improvements and fiscal policy, while the Great Depression in the 1930s prompted debates on federal relief programs and state borrowing capacities, as governors navigated New Deal initiatives. Leadership rotated annually among sitting governors, fostering continuity without centralized bureaucracy. By the 1940s, World War II heightened the conference's relevance, as governors coordinated civil defense, rationing, and wartime production through joint declarations and federal liaisons, adapting to national emergencies that amplified state responsibilities in resource allocation and emergency management. This era solidified the organization's role in bridging state autonomy with federal imperatives, laying groundwork for postwar policy forums.

Mid-Century Reorganization and Expansion (1950s1970s)

During the 1950s, the National Governors' Conference (NGC) expanded its scope beyond domestic policy discussions to include international engagement, organizing delegations of governors for overseas travel to foster state-level diplomacy and observe foreign governance models. In 1959, the NGC led a group of 10 governors on a trip to the Soviet Union, followed by larger delegations, such as 28 governors to Europe in the early 1960s, reflecting governors' growing interest in global issues amid Cold War tensions and post-World War II economic recovery. These initiatives marked an early broadening of the organization's activities, enabling governors to represent state interests abroad independently of federal channels.
In the 1960s, amid the expansion of federal programs under the Great Society, the NGC strengthened its internal structure through standing committees addressing key areas such as human resources, natural resources, transportation, and governmental reorganization, which produced reports on state executive reforms and constitutional revisions. These committees facilitated coordinated responses to federal initiatives, including advocacy for revenue sharing to offset rising state costs from categorical grants, with annual meetings featuring executive sessions and task forces on topics like welfare and criminal justice. The period also saw the emergence of partisan governors' groupsthe Democratic Governors Association in 1961 and Republican Governors Association in 1963which complemented the NGC's bipartisan framework by enhancing party coordination without supplanting its policy focus.
The 1970s brought significant reorganization to assert greater independence and policy influence. In 1975, the NGC disaffiliated from the Council of State Governments to operate autonomously, allowing focused prioritization of governors' agendas over broader multistate collaboration. This structural shift culminated in the 1977 name change to the National Governors Association (NGA), signaling a transition from a periodic conference to a permanent advocacy body with enhanced Washington presence, dedicated staff for research, and dissemination of state policy recommendations to counter federal overreach. By decade's end, the NGA's expanded operations included winter meetings, dues adjustments for sustained funding, and positions on national issues like energy and rail reorganization, solidifying its role in federal-state relations.

Modern Developments and Bipartisan Initiatives (1980sPresent)

In the 1980s, the National Governors Association (NGA) navigated shifting federal-state dynamics under President Reagan's New Federalism agenda, advocating for greater state autonomy through consolidated block grants and reduced federal mandates. This period saw alternating partisan leadership, with Democratic Chair George Busbee (19801981) followed by Republican Richard Snelling (19811982), reflecting the organization's commitment to bipartisan consensus on devolving responsibilities like welfare and education to states.
The 1990s marked a high point for NGA's influence on national policy, particularly in welfare reform and education. Governors played a central role in the 1996 Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act, emphasizing work requirements and state flexibility, which reduced welfare rolls by over 50% nationwide by 2000. In education, the 1989 Education Summit in Charlottesville, Virginia, convened by NGA and President George H.W. Bush, established six national goals focusing on readiness, graduation rates, and literacy, laying groundwork for subsequent standards-based reforms. Chairs like Republican Tommy Thompson (19951996) advanced these efforts, bridging partisan divides to prioritize empirical outcomes over ideological mandates.
Entering the 2000s, NGA expanded its Center for Best Practices to disseminate state innovations in areas such as health care, technology, and workforce development, fostering cross-state learning without federal preconditions. Bipartisan initiatives persisted, exemplified by the joint development of the Common Core State Standards in 20092010 by NGA and the Council of Chief State School Officers, aimed at aligning curricula with labor market needs despite later controversies over implementation. Leadership alternated reliably, with figures like Republican Mike Leavitt (19992000) and Democrat Parris Glendening (20002001) emphasizing practical policy diffusion.
In the 2010s and 2020s, NGA addressed emerging challenges like infrastructure modernization and youth mental health, with recent chairs launching targeted initiatives such as Republican Asa Hutchinson's (20212022) push for K-12 computer science education and Democrat Phil Murphy's (20222023) focus on mental health access. The organization championed bipartisan responses to the 2021 Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, providing states with resources for implementation. Despite national polarization, including reports of some Democratic governors distancing themselves in 2025 amid federal tensions, NGA chairs like Republican Kevin Stitt and Democrat Wes Moore affirmed ongoing collaboration on economic mobility and job access in joint statements. On February 19, 2026, U.S. governors arrived in Washington for meetings led by the National Governors Association, seeking to promote bipartisan cooperation amid the Trump administration's partisan dynamics and a continuing partial government shutdown. This resilience underscores NGA's role as a forum for evidence-based, state-centric solutions amid partisan strains.

Organizational Structure

Membership Composition

The membership of the National Governors Association comprises the sitting governors of the 50 U.S. states and the governors of five U.S. territories and commonwealths: American Samoa, Guam, the Northern Mariana Islands, Puerto Rico, and the U.S. Virgin Islands. This structure yields a total of 55 members, all of whom participate irrespective of political party affiliation upon assuming office. Governors retain membership throughout their terms, with the association providing resources to support their policy and administrative needs.
The composition reflects a bipartisan framework, as the organization includes executives from both major parties without partisan restrictions on entry or participation. Leadership roles, such as the annual chair and vice chair, alternate between parties by custom to promote balanced representation; for the 20252026 term, the chair is Republican Governor Kevin Stitt of Oklahoma, and the vice chair is Democratic Governor Wes Moore of Maryland. As of early 2025, Republicans held 27 state governorships compared to 23 for Democrats, with territorial governors featuring a mix including Democrats in Guam and the U.S. Virgin Islands, an independent in American Samoa, a Republican-aligned New Progressive Party governor in Puerto Rico, and an independent in the Northern Mariana Islands. This distribution underscores the association's role in bridging partisan divides on issues like federal-state relations and interstate policy coordination.
While core membership is limited to governors, the NGA extends programmatic supportsuch as networking events and technical assistanceto governors' spouses and senior staff, enhancing operational effectiveness without conferring formal membership status. No formal dues or application process is required beyond holding the governorship, ensuring universal inclusion among eligible executives.

Leadership and Executive Operations

The National Governors Association (NGA) is governed by a nine-member Executive Committee, elected annually by attending member governors at the organization's Summer Meeting, which supervises overall operations and establishes policy priorities in collaboration with standing committees on legal affairs and finance. The committee comprises the Chair, Vice Chair, and seven additional governors, structured to include four members from the Chair's political party and five from the opposing party to maintain bipartisanship as mandated by the association's articles of incorporation. This body authorizes special committees or task forces as needed to address federal policy issues.
The Chair and Vice Chair lead the Executive Committee, with the Chair holding a one-year term and the Vice Chair succeeding to the Chair position the following year; the roles alternate annually between a Republican and a Democrat to ensure balanced representation. Elections occur via majority vote of attending governors, emphasizing collective decision-making among the 55 U.S. states, territories, and commonwealths. As of the 2025 Summer Meeting on July 26, Oklahoma Governor Kevin Stitt (Republican) was elected Chair for the 2025-2026 term, focusing initiatives on economic exceptionalism and state innovation, while Maryland Governor Wes Moore (Democrat) was elected Vice Chair and Chair-elect for 2026-2027, overseeing the NGA Center for Best Practices.
Day-to-day executive operations are directed by a professional staff of approximately 100, headquartered at 444 North Capitol Street NW, Suite 267, in Washington, D.C., and funded primarily through state dues that support lobbying, research, and administrative services. The staff operates through five principal offices: the Executive Director's office, Federal Relations, Management Consulting and Training, Communications, and Administration and Finance, with governors appointing Washington representatives to liaise on policy matters. Bill McBride has served as Executive Director since July 2019, managing strategic policy, federal advocacy, and organizational coordination; he announced his retirement effective December 31, 2025, with a successor search underway led by an external firm. Chief Operating Officer LeAnne Wilson oversees internal management, including finances, human resources, and operational efficiency, ensuring continuity in support for governors' offices from election through term end.

Committees, Centers, and Policy Divisions

The National Governors Association (NGA) structures its policy work through an Executive Committee and specialized task forces that address targeted governance challenges. The Executive Committee, comprising nine members elected annually, supervises overall operations and exercises authority over broad policy domains, including federalism, homeland security, federal budget, and tax policy. Complementing this, NGA maintains task forces such as the Education, Workforce and Community Investment Task Force, which covers labor markets, pre-K through postsecondary education, criminal justice, housing, and agriculture; the Economic Development and Revitalization Task Force, focusing on infrastructure, broadband access, energy, environment, and taxation; and the Public Health and Emergency Management Task Force, handling disaster response, cybersecurity, healthcare delivery, and immigration. These entities facilitate bipartisan deliberation among governors, enabling the development of state-level strategies and federal advocacy positions.
Additionally, the Council of Governors, distinct from internal NGA bodies, consists of ten membersfive from each major partyappointed by the U.S. President to two-year terms, with two serving as co-chairs. This council advises on national security, disaster preparedness, National Guard mobilization, cybersecurity threats, and federal resource allocation for emergencies. Established under statutory authority, it bridges state executives with federal defense and homeland security priorities, emphasizing coordination during crises like natural disasters or cyber incidents.
The NGA Center for Best Practices serves as the association's primary research and innovation hub, functioning as a nonpartisan entity dedicated to identifying and disseminating effective state policies. Organized into five specialized divisions as of 2019, it supports governors through data-driven analysis, technical assistance, and peer learning on emerging issues. These divisions include: the Economic, Human Services & Workforce Division, which examines employment programs, social safety nets, and economic mobility; the Education Division, addressing K-12 reforms, higher education access, and skills alignment; the Environment, Energy & Transportation Division, tackling sustainability, infrastructure resilience, and resource management; the Health Division, focusing on public health systems, care access, and behavioral health; and the Homeland Security & Public Safety Division, covering emergency management, cybersecurity, and law enforcement innovations. The center's work integrates with committee efforts by providing evidence-based resources, such as policy briefs on workforce pipelines or broadband expansion, to inform task force recommendations and state implementations.

Core Functions and Activities

Policy Development and Research

The National Governors Association (NGA) advances policy development through its Center for Best Practices, which serves as the organization's dedicated research and innovation arm, focusing on identifying, analyzing, and scaling effective state-level strategies to address national public policy issues. This center conducts applied research by convening governors, state policy leaders, and subject-matter experts to foster multi-state pilot programs and information-sharing initiatives, emphasizing practical, evidence-based solutions over theoretical advocacy. Unlike partisan think tanks, the center's work draws from empirical outcomes in diverse state contexts, prioritizing measurable impacts such as improved workforce training alignment or education system efficiencies.
Research activities encompass targeted analyses in key domains, including workforce development, K-12 education, health, and homeland security, where teams produce reports, policy briefs, and toolkits grounded in data from state implementations. For instance, in workforce policy, the center has examined how governors leverage the Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA) to redesign training programs, funding 30 states' efforts to expand skills-based hiring and remove credential barriers as of 20232025. A 2025 report, "Governors Reshaping Workforce Development," details how 15 states integrated employer-led data assessments to boost program completion rates by up to 20% in targeted sectors, using longitudinal employment data to validate causal links between policy changes and labor market outcomes.
Policy development often involves collaborative roadmaps and technical assistance, such as the 2025 "Let's Get Ready" initiative, which supports at least five governors in implementing cross-state outcome measurement for early childhood and workforce readiness, incorporating high-quality data dashboards to track progress beyond state lines. These efforts emphasize causal realism by linking interventions to verifiable metricslike reduced unemployment durations or higher skill attainmentwhile avoiding unsubstantiated claims from ideologically driven sources. The center's outputs, disseminated via state networks, have influenced federal-state alignments, as seen in shared best practices adopted by over 20 states for economic recovery post-2020 disruptions.

Meetings, Conferences, and Networking

The National Governors Association convenes governors biannually through its Summer and Winter Meetings, which function as key platforms for networking, policy deliberation, and bipartisan collaboration on national and state issues. These gatherings, held since the organization's founding in 1908 with annual meetings evolving into two per year by the modern era, enable direct exchange among the 50 state governors, territorial leaders, and their staffs.
The Winter Meeting, typically hosted in Washington, D.C., attracts the highest attendance and emphasizes coordination on federal-state relations and pressing national priorities, such as education innovation and workforce development. For example, the 2024 Winter Meeting took place February 22-24 in Washington, D.C., featuring sessions with experts like economist Emily Oster on data-driven policy. The 2025 Winter Meeting is scheduled for February, continuing this tradition of high-level discourse.
In contrast, the Summer Meeting rotates among host states and focuses on sharing state-level solutions to challenges like economic recovery and infrastructure. The 2024 event occurred July 11-12 in Salt Lake City, Utah, while the 2025 meeting is set for July 25-26 in Colorado Springs, Colorado. These sessions include plenaries, workshops, and networking opportunities tailored for governors, first spouses, and policy advisors.
Beyond flagship events, the NGA's membership team organizes supplementary conferences and staff-focused gatherings throughout the year to provide technical assistance and foster ongoing professional networks. Historical records document annual meetings dating back to at least 1964, with themes evolving to address contemporary issues like civil rights in the mid-20th century. Attendance and agendas reflect the association's role in facilitating interstate cooperation without formal policymaking authority.

Federal Advocacy and State-Federal Relations

The National Governors Association (NGA) engages in federal advocacy through its Office of Government Relations, which represents governors' perspectives in the development of federal policies by maintaining ongoing communication with members of Congress, the White House, and executive branch agencies. This office coordinates with governors' Washington, D.C., representatives and other state-federal liaison personnel to amplify state-level input on national legislation and regulations. NGA's efforts emphasize bipartisan priorities, such as enhancing emergency management capabilities, streamlining federal permitting processes, and providing flexibility in programs like Medicaid, SNAP, and TANF, as outlined in resolutions approved by governors.
In state-federal relations, NGA promotes a cooperative framework where states and the federal government collaborate to address shared challenges, viewing such partnerships as essential for effective governance and citizen welfare. The organization's Executive Committee holds primary jurisdiction over federalism-related matters, including homeland security and intergovernmental coordination, enabling governors to influence federal responses to state needs. Practical activities include providing legislative analysis, tracking federal grants to states, and offering guidance on policy implementation, which helps governors navigate federal mandates and secure resources.
Specific advocacy actions demonstrate NGA's role in shaping federal outcomes, such as governors' testimony before congressional committees on surface transportation reauthorization to advocate for state infrastructure priorities. Similarly, NGA has submitted formal recommendations to federal entities, including letters urging reforms to the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) to improve disaster response coordination between state and federal levels. At annual meetings, such as the 2025 Winter Meeting, governors approve targeted federal priorities for the upcoming Congress and administration, focusing on issues like federal funding stability and regulatory relief. These initiatives often involve multi-state task forces that align gubernatorial positions to exert collective pressure on federal policymakers.
NGA's federal engagement extends to facilitating direct dialogues between governors and federal officials, ensuring state innovations inform national policy while monitoring compliance with federalism principles. This work underscores a commitment to balanced intergovernmental relations, where states retain autonomy in implementation but seek federal support for capacity-building, as evidenced by coordinated advocacy for fiscal year 2026 appropriations in areas like infrastructure and workforce development.

Policy Influence and Impact

Major Policy Areas and Initiatives

The National Governors Association (NGA) addresses a range of policy domains through its Center for Best Practices and standing committees, emphasizing state-level innovations in areas such as education, workforce development, health care, infrastructure, and economic policy. These efforts often culminate in the annual Chair's Initiative, a bipartisan focus selected by the NGA chair to promote actionable solutions on national issues, with examples including education reform in the 1980s and skills-based hiring in recent years.
In education, the NGA has prioritized K-12 funding, teacher workforce development, and academic achievement, as evidenced by analyses of 2025 State of the State addresses where governors highlighted trends like increased investments in career and technical education and addressing chronic absenteeism. Major initiatives include the development of common core standards in collaboration with the Council of Chief State School Officers in 20092010, aimed at establishing consistent learning benchmarks across states, though implementation varied widely due to state autonomy.
Workforce development and economic policy represent core focuses, with programs supporting governors in skills-first hiring, regional economic clusters, and public-sector talent pipelines; for instance, the 20232025 initiatives launched communities of practice for skills-based public hiring in states like those participating in the Service-to-Career Pathways Policy Academy. The NGA's Opportunity Now program, stemming from earlier task forces, has funded scalable regional partnerships to align workforce training with employer needs, demonstrating measurable outcomes in job placement rates in participating states.
In health and human services, the NGA tackles family economic security, child welfare, and Medicaid reforms, including anti-poverty strategies and foster care improvements; initiatives have included webinars and policy academies on topics like juvenile justice and housing stability, often integrating data-driven best practices from peer states.
Infrastructure efforts emphasize funding mechanisms and public-private partnerships, with governors advocating for enhanced state investments; the NGA has tracked actions such as new revenue streams for transportation and broadband expansion, particularly post-2021 federal infrastructure legislation.
Additional areas include natural resources and public safety, addressed via committees on federalism and homeland security, where the NGA facilitates interstate coordination on energy policy and emergency response without endorsing uniform federal mandates. These initiatives underscore the NGA's role in fostering voluntary state cooperation rather than top-down directives, with empirical impacts measured through adopted best practices in over 50 states annually.

Contributions to Federalism and Interstate Cooperation

The National Governors Association (NGA) bolsters federalism by advocating for policies that maintain state decision-making authority amid federal initiatives, positioning governors as key intermediaries in national policymaking. Since its founding in 1908, NGA has enabled governors to lobby Congress and federal agencies collectively, ensuring legislation accounts for state fiscal health and autonomy rather than imposing unfunded mandates or preempting local innovations. This role is formalized through NGA's Office of Government Relations, which tracks federal grants, analyzes bills, and represents governors' perspectives to preserve balanced intergovernmental dynamics. In 2018, NGA outlined principles underscoring a cooperative state-federal framework essential for effective governance, rejecting one-size-fits-all federal approaches in favor of flexible state adaptations.
NGA advances interstate cooperation by convening governors to share operational strategies, develop model policies, and launch cross-state projects that address shared priorities without relying on federal directives. The Center for Best Practices, operational since the 1980s, disseminates evidence-based innovations across 14 policy domains, including workforce development and public health, fostering voluntary alignments among states to replicate successful programs. For example, in infrastructure, NGA's multi-state coordination efforts, such as the Infrastructure Coordinator Network established post-2021 federal legislation, link governor-appointed advisors to optimize resource allocation and public-private partnerships, enhancing efficiency in projects like highway funding distribution.
Early contributions included President Theodore Roosevelt's 1908 convening of governorsprecursor to NGA's formalizationto coordinate state-level conservation measures, marking an initial push for unified action on transboundary resources like forests and water. More recently, NGA's bipartisan task forces have facilitated agreements on issues like emergency management and economic recovery, enabling states to pool data and negotiate compacts that bypass federal bottlenecks, as seen in collective responses to supply chain disruptions. These mechanisms underscore NGA's emphasis on governors' collective leverage to drive pragmatic, state-led solutions over centralized impositions.

Empirical Outcomes and Measurable Effects

The National Governors Association (NGA), in collaboration with the Council of Chief State School Officers, led the development and promotion of the Common Core State Standards (CCSS) initiative, finalized in 2010 and adopted by 41 states by 2013. Empirical analyses of CCSS implementation, using data from the National Assessment of Educational Progress (NAEP), indicate small positive effects on mathematics achievement, ranging from 0.04 to 0.10 standard deviations (SD), particularly in fourth-grade scores (0.105 SD by 2013) and more modestly in eighth-grade (approximately 0.045 SD). No significant effects were observed in reading scores, attributable to non-parallel pre-treatment trends across states. Effects varied by implementation timing, with early adopters (20112013) showing relative gains compared to later ones (20142015), but no uniform impact nationwide due to differences in state capacity, resources, and fidelity of execution.
These mathematics gains were unevenly distributed, benefiting economically advantaged students more substantially while yielding no improvements or slight declines for disadvantaged subgroups, failing to narrow racial or socioeconomic achievement gaps overallfor instance, the White-Black gap in fourth-grade math narrowed by only 0.05 SD in some models. In non-tested subjects such as science and social studies, CCSS exposure correlated with declines in achievement of approximately 0.08 SD, equivalent to 2530% of a school year's learning loss, with disproportionately larger negative effects on Black, Hispanic, and low-income students. This unintended consequence stemmed from reduced instructional time and resources allocated to non-core areas, as evidenced by teacher surveys showing decreased weekly hours (e.g., probability drop of 0.201 for over 5 hours in non-targeted subjects) and inadequate materials.
Beyond education, rigorous causal evaluations of NGA-led initiatives remain limited, complicating attribution of broader outcomes to the organization's efforts amid confounding factors like state-specific implementations and economic conditions. For instance, NGA-facilitated gubernatorial advocacy contributed to the 1996 Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act (PRWORA), which replaced Aid to Families with Dependent Children with block-granted Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF) emphasizing work requirements. Post-enactment, TANF caseloads fell over 60%, from 12.2 million recipients in 1996 to about 5.3 million by 2000, alongside rises in employment rates for single mothers (from 60% to 75% participating in work activities) and modest poverty reductions among affected families. However, long-term studies attribute much of this decline to a strong economy and state innovations rather than federal mandates alone, with mixed effects on child well-being and persistent challenges in deep poverty.
In health policy, NGA's 2017 chair's initiative produced a roadmap for addressing the opioid crisis, including monitoring components for state-level evaluation, but subsequent national overdose deaths continued rising (from 42,000 in 2016 to over 100,000 by 2021), with limited evidence linking NGA recommendations to measurable reductions in state-specific mortality or treatment access improvements. Similarly, NGA's Center for Best Practices has supported workforce and sector strategy programs in over 30 states since the 2010s, aiming to align training with employer needs, yet peer-reviewed assessments of employment or earnings outcomes from these efforts are scarce, often relying on self-reported metrics rather than randomized controls. Overall, while NGA initiatives have influenced policy adoption, empirical data highlight challenges in demonstrating sustained, causal improvements, underscoring the difficulties of interstate coordination in achieving scalable effects.

Controversies and Criticisms

Internal Polarization and Attendance Declines

The National Governors Association has experienced increasing internal polarization, manifested in partisan-driven reductions in attendance at meetings and threats to withhold membership dues, straining its bipartisan framework. During the July 2023 summer meeting in Atlantic City, New Jersey, only three of the 26 Republican governors attended, with one departing after the first night and Utah Governor Spencer Cox compelled to participate as the incoming chair; by the closing remarks, fewer than half a dozen governors remained, predominantly Democrats. This low turnout reflected a broader trend among Republican governors favoring partisan alternatives like the Republican Governors Association and avoiding scrutiny over alignment with former President Trump, with states such as Florida and Texas ceasing participation entirely since around 2018.
Post-2024, polarization intensified from the Democratic side amid perceived federal encroachments under President Trump, prompting several governors to disengage. In July 2025, Minnesota Governor Tim Walz and Kansas Governor Laura Kelly announced they would cease paying NGA duesKelly effective by month's end and Walz starting in 2026citing the organization's insufficient opposition to actions including a January 2025 pause in federal funds, June 2025 National Guard deployments to California, and proposed congressional rescissions of state funding; both skipped the July 2025 meeting in Colorado Springs. Similarly, Illinois Governor J.B. Pritzker and California Governor Gavin Newsom threatened withdrawal in October 2025 unless the NGA condemned deployments like Texas National Guard units to Illinois, while Arizona Governor Katie Hobbs considered canceling membership on October 23, 2025, amid doubts over the group's effectiveness in safeguarding state sovereignty. 
These developments have exacerbated attendance declines, with Democratic frustrations centered on the NGA's requirement for consensus-based statements, which critics argue dilutes responses to partisan federal actions and fails to support affected states, as seen in limited assistance for governors testifying on National Guard issues. While the NGA, under chairs like Cox, has launched initiatives such as "Disagree Better" to foster cross-partisan dialogue, the pattern of selective participation underscores deepening partisan divides, mirroring national trends and challenging the organization's role in interstate cooperation.

Critiques of Federal Dependency and Overreach Facilitation

Critics from conservative and libertarian think tanks have argued that the National Governors Association (NGA) contributes to state dependency on federal funding by actively lobbying Congress for expanded grants and aid programs, which often include regulatory conditions that erode state autonomy. For instance, the Cato Institute's analysis contends that intergovernmental transfers, totaling over $700 billion annually in recent fiscal years, create fiscal incentives for states to align with federal priorities rather than local needs, fostering a cycle of dependency that distorts budgeting and accountability. This view posits that NGA's advocacy, such as its Federal Priorities Roadmap outlining requests for sustained funding in areas like infrastructure and workforce development, effectively prioritizes federal largesse over fiscal independence, enabling Washington to impose policy mandates via "strings attached" to grants.
The Heritage Foundation has specifically critiqued NGA-endorsed welfare reform proposals for embedding mechanisms that penalize state success in reducing dependency, such as automatic cuts in federal block grant funding when caseloads decline, thereby discouraging innovative state-level reductions in reliance on Washington. In this framework, NGA's promotion of "cooperative federalism"where states implement federal policies in exchange for fundsis seen not as partnership but as a vehicle for overreach, as evidenced by historical expansions like the Great Society programs, which multiplied conditional grants from fewer than 100 in 1960 to over 1,000 by the 1980s, binding states to national standards on education, health, and environment. Such arrangements, critics maintain, undermine the Tenth Amendment's reservation of powers to states by substituting federal incentives for genuine sovereignty, with NGA's bipartisan consensus often amplifying rather than resisting this dynamic.
Empirical data supports these concerns: Federal grants-in-aid rose from 26% of state revenues in 1980 to approximately 37% by 2020, correlating with increased federal regulatory preemption in state domains, according to analyses from federalism scholars. Detractors argue NGA facilitates this by framing federal aid as essential for state competitivenesse.g., in opposing cuts to programs like Medicaid expansionswithout sufficiently advocating for outright devolution or revenue-raising authority at the state level, thus perpetuating a system where governors prioritize securing funds over reforming entrenched dependencies. While NGA defends its role as bridging federal-state gaps to protect state interests, opponents counter that this lobbying entrenches a paternalistic model, where federal overreach is normalized through voluntary acceptance of aid, as articulated in Reagan-era addresses to the group highlighting grants' role in breeding "dependency."

Specific Policy Disputes and Partisan Tensions

In October 2025, partisan tensions within the National Governors Association escalated over President Donald Trump's authorizations of federal and out-of-state National Guard deployments to Democratic-led states, prompting threats from several Democratic governors to withdraw membership or withhold dues. California Governor Gavin Newsom and Illinois Governor JB Pritzker publicly stated on October 6 that they would pull out unless the NGA issued a formal condemnation of the deployments, which they described as unconstitutional encroachments on state sovereignty, including instances like Texas National Guard units sent to Illinois without local consent. This conflict highlighted fractures in the NGA's bipartisan framework, as Republican-led states supported the federal actions aimed at addressing urban unrest and border-related issues, while Democrats viewed them as partisan overreach.
Earlier in July 2025, Minnesota Governor Tim Walz and Kansas Governor Laura Kelly announced plans to cease paying NGA dues, citing the organization's insufficient response to Trump's policies perceived as infringing on gubernatorial control over state militias and law enforcement. These actions underscored deeper policy divergences, particularly on federalism, where Democratic governors argued for stricter limits on executive authority to deploy forces interstate without gubernatorial approval, contrasting with Republican governors' emphasis on national security priorities overriding state objections in crises. The NGA, historically neutral on such partisan flashpoints to preserve unity, faced criticism from departing Democrats for failing to prioritize state autonomy advocacy, potentially eroding its role as a cross-party forum.
The disputes extended to broader policy arenas, such as immigration enforcement and public safety, where NGA consensus broke down; for instance, Republican governors like Texas' Greg Abbott defended cooperative deployments as necessary for combating transnational crime, while opponents framed them as federal imposition exacerbating local resource strains without addressing root causes like sanctuary policies. Despite the NGA's "Disagree Better" initiativelaunched in 2023 to foster depolarization through joint events and videosthese events marked a rare institutional strain, with attendance risks and funding threats signaling potential long-term partisan balkanization absent unified stances on federal-state power balances.
