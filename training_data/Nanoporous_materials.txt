Nanoporous materials

 Nanoporous materials are solids containing pores with diameters typically ranging from 1 to 100 nanometers, which confer them with exceptionally high surface areasoften exceeding 1000 m/gand unique physicochemical properties such as tunable porosity and structural versatility. According to the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) classification, these pores are categorized as microporous (less than 2 nm), mesoporous (250 nm), and macroporous (greater than 50 nm, though still within the nanoscale regime for nanoporous contexts). They encompass a diverse array of organic, inorganic, and hybrid frameworks, including crystalline structures like zeolites and metal-organic frameworks (MOFs), as well as amorphous materials such as mesoporous silica and activated carbon.
These materials exhibit key properties that stem from their nanoscale architecture, including high adsorption capacity, selective permeability, mechanical robustness in some forms (e.g., ordered crystalline variants), and enhanced reactivity due to confined spaces within pores. Synthesis approaches have evolved to include bottom-up methods like sol-gel processes and self-assembly, as well as top-down techniques such as dealloying and plasma etching, enabling precise control over pore size, shape, and distribution.
Notable applications leverage these attributes across multiple fields: in catalysis for efficient reactant diffusion and product separation; in energy storage and conversion, such as supercapacitors and fuel cells; in environmental remediation for gas capture (e.g., CO) and water purification; and in biomedicine for targeted drug delivery, biosensing, and tissue scaffolds. The field has seen rapid progress since the 1990s discovery of ordered mesoporous silicas, driving innovations in scalable fabrication and multifunctional hybrids to address challenges in sustainability and healthcare.

Introduction

Definition and Characteristics

Nanoporous materials are porous solids featuring uniform or hierarchical pores with diameters ranging from 0.2 nm to 100 nm, setting them apart from coarser porous structures by their nanoscale confinement effects. This pore size regime yields extraordinarily high surface area-to-volume ratios, typically exceeding 100 m/g, as exemplified by mesoporous silica with values around 850 m/g. Such ratios arise from the intricate internal architecture, maximizing accessible surface for interactions at the molecular level.
These materials exhibit tunable porosity, allowing precise control over pore volume and interconnectivity to suit specific functions, alongside high adsorption capacity that enables efficient capture of gases or solutes. Enhanced reactivity is another hallmark, driven by the nanoscale confinement that alters molecular dynamics, promotes selective catalysis, and facilitates unique quantum effects in confined spaces. For instance, the restricted geometry can accelerate reaction kinetics by increasing collision frequencies on pore walls.
The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) classifies pores in these materials by size: microporous (0.22 nm), where strong adsorption potentials dominate; mesoporous (250 nm), offering balanced accessibility; and macroporous (50100 nm) within the nanoporous domain, emphasizing transport properties. Pore shapes vary, including cylindrical (favoring directional flow), spherical (enhancing uniform distribution), and slit-like (promoting layered adsorption), each influencing behaviors like diffusion rates and selectivity.

Historical Development

The discovery of zeolites in 1756 by Swedish mineralogist Axel Fredrik Cronstedt marked the initial recognition of nanoporous materials, as he observed certain aluminosilicate minerals effervescing and expanding upon heating water, leading him to coin the term "zeolite" from the Greek words for "boiling stone." These natural minerals featured microporous structures capable of selective adsorption, forming the basis for later nanoporous research, though systematic study of their porosity began in the early 20th century. By the 1930s, scientists like Richard M. Barrer explored their ion-exchange and molecular sieving properties, but the field advanced significantly with the first hydrothermal synthesis of a synthetic zeolite in 1948, when Barrer produced a chabazite-like material with controlled sorptive characteristics.
A pivotal shift occurred in 1992 with the synthesis of ordered mesoporous silicas, such as MCM-41, by researchers at Mobil Corporation including Charles T. Kresge, Markus E. Leonowicz, William J. Roth, James C. Vartuli, and James S. Beck, who employed liquid-crystal templating with surfactants to achieve uniform pores in the 210 nm range. This innovation enabled precise porosity control beyond the microporous limitations of zeolites, transitioning from naturally occurring minerals to engineered nanomaterials and sparking widespread adoption of surfactant-based methods throughout the 1990s.
The late 1990s introduced reticular chemistry, exemplified by the 1995 development of metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) by Omar M. Yaghi and Hao Li, who synthesized a cadmium-based framework with large, accessible pores for selective guest binding. Building on this, Yaghi's team reported covalent organic frameworks (COFs) in 2005, creating crystalline, all-organic porous structures through boronic acid condensation, which offered enhanced chemical stability and tunability.
In the 2020s, research has emphasized sustainable synthesis of nanoporous materials, including the use of industrial waste like coal fly ash to derive zeolites and mesoporous silicas via eco-friendly templating, reducing environmental impact while maintaining high porosity. This era's advancements culminated in the 2025 Nobel Prize in Chemistry, awarded to Omar Yaghi, Susumu Kitagawa, and Richard Robson for pioneering MOFs and related frameworks, underscoring their role in energy storage and separation technologies.

Classification

Size-Based Classification

Nanoporous materials are classified according to the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) guidelines based on their pore diameters, which dictate key functional properties such as molecular accessibility and transport dynamics. Microporous materials feature pores smaller than 2 nm, mesoporous materials have pores between 2 and 50 nm, and macroporous materials exhibit pores larger than 50 nm, with the nanoporous regime typically extending up to around 100 nm for macropores. This size-based categorization highlights how pore dimensions influence applications, from selective adsorption in small pores to enhanced diffusion in larger ones.
Microporous materials, with pore sizes below 2 nm, are predominantly exemplified by zeolites and activated carbons, which enable precise control over molecular interactions due to their constrained geometries. Zeolites, crystalline aluminosilicates, facilitate molecular sieving by allowing only molecules smaller than the pore aperture to enter, while activated carbons provide high surface areas for gas adsorption. These materials are particularly suited for shape-selective catalysis, where the pore structure restricts reactant orientation and product diffusion, enhancing reaction specificity in processes like hydrocarbon cracking.
Mesoporous materials, characterized by pore diameters of 2 to 50 nm, include well-ordered silicas such as MCM-41 and SBA-15, which offer tunable hexagonal or cubic pore arrangements with wall thicknesses that provide mechanical stability. The larger pores in these materials accommodate biomolecules and enable controlled release, making them advantageous for drug delivery systems where sustained therapeutic agent dispersion is required without pore blockage. For instance, MCM-41's uniform channels support high loading capacities for pharmaceuticals, while SBA-15's interconnected pores improve mass transport for larger payloads.
At the upper end of the nanoporous scale, macroporous materials with pore sizes from 50 to 100 nm, such as aerogels and anodic alumina, exhibit open, interconnected networks that facilitate rapid fluid flow and serve as scaffolds. Aerogels, including silica or alumina variants, combine low density with hierarchical openness, while anodic alumina membranes feature self-ordered hexagonal arrays of cylindrical pores. These structures are commonly employed as templates to fabricate hierarchical porous composites, guiding the assembly of smaller pores within larger frameworks to optimize multifunctionality.
Pore size distributions in nanoporous materials are typically determined using nitrogen adsorption-desorption isotherms at 77 K, with the Barrett-Joyner-Halenda (BJH) method applied for mesoporous regimes to model capillary condensation and evaporation. The BJH approach estimates pore diameters from the desorption branch, incorporating multilayer adsorption corrections. For cylindrical mesopores, an approximate relation for average pore diameter $ d $ is given by:
d=4VpA

where $ V_p $ is the pore volume and $ A $ is the surface area.
Hierarchical porous materials integrate multiple pore size regimessuch as micropores within mesopores or macro-mesoporous networksto synergistically enhance performance, combining selective accessibility with efficient transport pathways. This multiscale architecture mitigates diffusion limitations in single-scale systems, enabling superior outcomes in areas like energy storage and separation. For example, combining zeolite micropores with mesoporous supports amplifies catalytic efficiency, while organic frameworks like covalent organic frameworks (COFs) fit within the microporous category for rigid, crystalline sieving applications.

Composition-Based Classification

Nanoporous materials are categorized by their chemical composition into inorganic, organic, hybrid, and emerging bio-derived classes, each defined by unique bonding types, structural motifs, and stability characteristics that influence their applications.
Inorganic nanoporous materials, including zeolites and metal oxides such as silica, feature rigid frameworks built from metal-oxygen polyhedra, providing exceptional durability. Zeolites are crystalline aluminosilicates composed of tetrahedral units (T = Si or Al) linked by oxygen atoms to form open three-dimensional frameworks with inherent microporosity, where pore sizes are typically below 2 nm. These structures exhibit high thermal stability, often up to 1000C, due to their strong covalent and ionic bonds, enabling use in demanding environments like catalysis. A key metric for zeolites is framework density, defined as the number of T-atoms per nm, which ranges from approximately 10 to 20 in microporous types and governs the balance between framework rigidity and void space availability.
Organic nanoporous materials, such as polymers of intrinsic microporosity (PIMs) and hypercrosslinked polymers, achieve porosity through the steric hindrance of contorted, non-planar polymer backbones that resist close packing. PIMs are soluble, rigid polymers that form microporous solids upon solvent removal, offering superior flexibility and solution-processability over inorganic materials while maintaining structural integrity under ambient conditions, though with reduced thermal stability. Hypercrosslinked polymers, created by dense covalent crosslinking of precursors like polystyrene, yield amorphous nanoporous networks with tunable pore sizes below 2 nm, providing good chemical resistance and ease of synthesis but limited high-temperature endurance compared to inorganics.
Hybrid nanoporous materials integrate organic and inorganic elements for enhanced versatility, as seen in metal-organic frameworks (MOFs). MOFs consist of inorganic metal nodes or clusters coordinated to organic linkers, forming extended porous lattices where properties like bandgap can be precisely tuned by varying linker chemistry to enable applications in optoelectronics. Covalent organic frameworks (COFs), fully organic crystalline structures assembled from rigid molecular building blocks via covalent bonds, such as imine or boronate linkages, resulting in predictable topologies and moderate thermal stability up to around 500C, are classified under advanced organic materials.
An emerging subclass comprises bio-derived nanoporous carbons synthesized from sustainable biomass sources, like agricultural residues, through carbonization and activation processes that yield hierarchical pores while minimizing environmental impact, with significant advancements reported in the 2020s for energy storage.

Synthesis Methods

Templating Techniques

Templating techniques employ sacrificial templates to guide the formation of ordered nanopores in materials, enabling precise architectural control through directed assembly and subsequent template removal. These methods are categorized into hard and soft variants, each leveraging different template types to achieve uniform pore networks in the nano- to microscale regime.
Hard templating utilizes rigid, pre-formed nanostructures such as nanoparticles or colloidal assemblies as scaffolds. The process typically involves assembling the template into an ordered array, infiltrating it with a precursor solution via capillary action or impregnation, solidifying the precursor to form a composite, and then removing the template through thermal calcination, which leaves behind an inverse replica of the template's void space. For instance, ordered macroporous TiO has been fabricated by infiltrating titanium alkoxide precursors into colloidal crystals of silica spheres, followed by hydrolysis, drying, and calcination at temperatures up to 650C to etch away the silica template, resulting in interconnected macropores that mirror the original sphere packing.
Soft templating relies on dynamic, molecular-scale templates like surfactant micelles or block copolymers that self-organize under specific conditions. In this approach, the template molecules assemble into ordered phasessuch as cylindrical or lamellar structuresin the presence of inorganic precursors, which condense around them via hydrolysis and polymerization; the soft template is then extracted, often by solvent washing or mild heating, to reveal the porous framework. A foundational example is the synthesis of MCM-41 mesoporous silica, where cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) surfactant micelles self-assemble into hexagonal liquid crystal phases in an alkaline silicate solution, directing silicate hydrolysis and condensation to form walls around the micelles, with subsequent calcination at around 550C extracting the template and yielding uniform pores of approximately 3 nm.
Evaporation-induced self-assembly (EISA) extends soft templating to thin-film geometries, where a dilute sol containing surfactants and precursors is deposited on a substrate, and controlled solvent evaporation progressively concentrates the components, driving micelle formation and cooperative organization into mesophases that template pores typically ranging from 2 to 10 nm after surfactant removal.
These templating strategies offer significant advantages, including precise control over pore size, shape, and uniformity, which is challenging to achieve with non-templated routes, allowing for reproducible fabrication of hierarchically ordered structures. However, key challenges arise during template removal, as incomplete extraction or aggressive conditions can cause pore collapse or framework shrinkage; calcination effectively removes organic templates but requires temperatures above 500C that may induce defects, while acid etching provides a milder alternative for selective dissolution but demands careful pH control to preserve integrity.
Recent advancements in bio-templating have incorporated biological motifs, such as virus capsid-inspired designs, to create chiral nanoporous systems; for example, core-shell mesoporous silica nanoparticles with virus-like nanospikes have been synthesized in 2023 via epitaxial growth on silica cores, yielding ordered pores of 2.53.2 nm that enable chiral surface recognition through L-alanine modification.

Non-Templating Techniques

Non-templating techniques for the synthesis of nanoporous materials exploit inherent molecular or atomic rearrangements to generate pores, bypassing the need for removable scaffolds and enabling scalable production. These approaches leverage chemical reactions, diffusion processes, and physical phenomena to create controlled nanoporosity, often resulting in materials with high surface areas and tunable structures suitable for various applications. Unlike templating methods, they emphasize simplicity and inherent phase behaviors, though they may offer less precise pore size control in some cases.
The sol-gel process represents a foundational non-templating route for fabricating nanoporous oxides, particularly silica aerogels, through controlled hydrolysis and condensation of alkoxide precursors. Tetraethylorthosilicate (TEOS) serves as a common silica precursor, where acid- or base-catalyzed hydrolysis with water produces silanol groups (Si-OH), followed by condensation reactions forming a three-dimensional Si-O-Si network and releasing byproducts like ethanol. Pore formation arises from phase separation during the sol-to-gel transition, trapping solvent within the nanoscale network and yielding ultralight aerogels with porosity often exceeding 90%, corresponding to densities as low as 0.03 g/cm.
Dealloying involves the selective dissolution of a less noble metal from an alloy precursor, creating a bicontinuous nanoporous architecture of the more noble component without external templates. A prototypical example is the chemical or electrochemical etching of silver from gold-silver (Au-Ag) alloys, which produces nanoporous gold with interconnected ligaments and pores. Ligament sizes typically range from 10 to 50 nm, and their dimensions can be precisely tuned by adjusting the etching potential, which influences dissolution rates and surface passivation. This process is fundamentally driven by the Kirkendall effect, arising from asymmetric atomic diffusion rates that generate supersaturated vacancies and voids; the resulting vacancy flux $ J_v $ follows Fick's law as $ J_v = -D \nabla C $, where $ D $ is the diffusion coefficient and $ C $ is the concentration gradient.
Self-assembly of block copolymers offers a bottom-up non-templating strategy for nanoporous organic-inorganic hybrids, relying on microphase separation to dictate morphology. Diblock copolymers, such as polynorbornenedecaborane (PNB-b-PDB), segregate into thermodynamically stable domainscylindrical or lamellar structuresdue to the immiscibility of organic and inorganic blocks, guided by factors like the Flory-Huggins interaction parameter and block volume fractions. Selective etching or thermal pyrolysis then removes the organic block, converting the inorganic domains into a porous matrix with ordered mesopores around 20 nm in diameter, achieving surface areas up to 950 m/g in resulting ceramics like boron nitride. This method is particularly effective for hybrid materials where inorganic components provide mechanical stability.
Emerging green non-templating methods, such as sonochemical synthesis, harness ultrasound to induce porosity in polymers through cavitation without toxic reagents or high temperatures. High-frequency ultrasound (20 kHz1 MHz) generates transient bubbles that collapse, creating localized hotspots (~5000 K, ~1000 atm) and shear forces that disrupt polymer chains and promote rapid void formation. Advancements as of 2023 demonstrate this approach yielding biomass-derived nanoporous polymers with surface areas exceeding 3000 m/g, emphasizing energy efficiency and environmental compatibility for scalable production. More recent developments as of 2025 include on-surface synthesis techniques for atomically precise nanoporous graphene, enabling topological control without templates, and AI-driven design of metal-organic frameworks for optimized porosity.

Properties

Structural and Mechanical Properties

Nanoporous materials exhibit distinctive structural properties defined by their high porosity and extensive internal surface areas, which arise from the nanoscale void spaces within their frameworks. Total porosity () is calculated as = 1 - (_bulk / _skeleton), where _bulk represents the bulk density of the material and _skeleton the density of the solid skeletal component, often yielding values exceeding 90% in aerogels and foams. Interconnectivity of pores is characterized by tortuosity (), a dimensionless parameter greater than 1 that quantifies the extended path length through the pore network relative to a straight line, influencing transport phenomena and typically ranging from 1.5 to 3 in mesoporous structures.
Surface area and pore volume in nanoporous materials are quantified using nitrogen adsorption isotherms analyzed via the Brunauer-Emmett-Teller (BET) method for total surface area and the t-plot method for micropore contributions, with mesoporous variants like silica or carbon frameworks commonly achieving 5002000 m/g due to ordered pore arrays. These metrics highlight the materials' capacity for high void volumes while maintaining structural integrity at the nanoscale.
Mechanically, nanoporous materials display stiffness that scales with relative density according to the Gibson-Ashby model for open-cell foams, where the Young's modulus EEE follows E(/s)2E \sim (\rho^* / \rho_s)^2E(/s)2, with \rho^* as the effective density and s\rho_ss the solid density, reflecting bending-dominated deformation in low-density regimes. In nanoporous metals, surface elasticity contributes to enhanced elastic recovery, particularly for ligament sizes below 10 nm, due to surface stress effects that stiffen the nanoscale struts.
Under cyclic loading, aerogels demonstrate fatigue resistance with characteristic hysteresis in stress-strain curves, attributable to reversible pore collapse and recovery, allowing sustained performance over 100 cycles at strains of 40%, with testing up to 80% strain and minimal degradation.

Selectivity in adsorption is notably enhanced in zeolites through ion exchange, which modifies the cationic sites within the framework to preferentially bind specific molecules, such as achieving high HS uptake in Ag-exchanged zeolite X.
Chemical stability in nanoporous materials varies with composition and structure, influencing their reactivity under environmental conditions. Applications

Gas Storage and Separation

Nanoporous materials, particularly metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) and zeolites, have emerged as promising candidates for gas storage due to their high surface areas and tunable pore structures that facilitate physisorption. In hydrogen storage applications, MOFs such as HKUST-1 demonstrate significant uptake through physisorption mechanisms involving van der Waals interactions within the pores, achieving capacities around 7 wt% at 77 K and moderate pressures. This adsorption is primarily reversible and relies on the material's ability to accommodate hydrogen molecules in open metal sites and cavities, enabling potential use in clean energy systems. High surface areas, often exceeding 1500 m/g in these frameworks, enhance the interaction potential, though practical implementation requires optimization for volumetric efficiency.
For carbon dioxide capture, amine-functionalized mesoporous silicas, such as those based on MCM-41, offer high selectivity and capacity in post-combustion scenarios from flue gases containing 10-15% CO. These materials achieve adsorption capacities greater than 3 mmol/g at ambient temperatures and pressures, attributed to chemisorption via carbamate formation between CO and grafted amine groups like polyethyleneimine (PEI). The mesoporous structure (pore diameters 2-50 nm) provides ample space for amine loading while maintaining accessibility, outperforming traditional liquid amine scrubbers in energy efficiency and regenerability through temperature or pressure swings. This approach supports carbon capture and sequestration efforts by enabling selective removal of CO from nitrogen-rich streams.
In gas separation processes, zeolite membranes exploit size exclusion and molecular sieving to achieve high selectivities, such as O/N ratios exceeding 10 in mixed matrix configurations with low-silica LTA zeolites. These inorganic frameworks, with uniform micropores around 0.4-0.5 nm, preferentially permeate smaller O molecules (kinetic diameter 0.346 nm) over N (0.364 nm), facilitating air separation for oxygen enrichment or nitrogen production. Storage capacity in such systems is often quantified using excess adsorption, defined as $ q_{ex} = q_{total} - (V_p \cdot \rho_{gas}) $, where $ q_{total} $ is the total adsorbed amount, $ V_p $ is the pore volume, and $ \rho_{gas} $ is the bulk gas density; this metric accounts for the gas filling the void space without contributing to true adsorption.
Despite these advances, challenges persist in scaling nanoporous materials for practical applications, particularly for automotive hydrogen storage. Recent 2025 developments focus on fabricating scalable MOF pellets through mechanical densification of frameworks like UiO-67 and MOF-177, improving deliverable capacities by 14-42% while retaining structural integrity and targeting U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) system goals of 5.5 wt% gravimetric and 40 g/L volumetric hydrogen storage. These pellets address powder handling issues and enhance packing density, bringing physisorption-based systems closer to commercial viability for onboard fuel cell vehicles.

Catalysis and Sensing

Nanoporous materials play a pivotal role in catalysis by providing high surface areas and confined environments that enhance reaction rates and selectivity through active sites within pores. In fluid catalytic cracking (FCC), zeolites such as faujasite (Y-type) have revolutionized the process since their introduction in the 1970s, significantly boosting gasoline yields from around 40% to over 50% compared to amorphous silica-alumina catalysts, while also enabling higher conversion rates. This improvement stems from the uniform micropores of zeolites, which facilitate shape-selective cracking and reduce over-cracking to lighter gases. However, diffusion-limited rates within these narrow pores (typically 0.51 nm) can constrain reactant access to active sites, leading to concentration gradients that influence overall kinetics; for instance, Knudsen diffusion dominates in zeolites, slowing mass transport and necessitating hierarchical pore designs to mitigate this.
Nanoporous metals, exemplified by nanoporous gold (np-Au) derived from dealloying Au-Ag alloys, exhibit exceptional activity for oxidation reactions due to undercoordinated sites at ligament perimeters. In CO oxidation, np-Au outperforms supported Au nanoparticles by leveraging these perimeter interfaces, where atomic steps and twin boundaries act as low-coordination active sites, enabling full conversion at ambient temperatures (e.g., 100% activity at 20C) without precious metal supports. Studies indicate that the intrinsic activity per surface atom in np-Au can exceed that of nanoparticles by factors of 10100, attributed to residual silver or oxide impurities stabilizing reactive oxygen species at these interfaces. The bicontinuous nanoporous structure (pore sizes ~1050 nm) further aids by promoting efficient gas diffusion and preventing sintering, sustaining long-term stability under oxidative conditions.
In sensing applications, nanoporous materials transduce analyte binding into measurable signals via changes in optical, electrical, or mass properties, capitalizing on their high porosity for rapid response. Optical sensors based on mesoporous TiO films (pore diameters 250 nm) detect humidity through refractive index shifts induced by water adsorption, which alters the effective medium index and causes wavelength displacements in Fabry-Prot interferometers; for example, exposure to 5% ethanol (mimicking humidity effects) yields shifts of ~623 pm corresponding to n 2.4 10. This physisorption-driven mechanism ensures reversibility and fast equilibration (seconds), with the anatase phase of TiO enhancing sensitivity due to its hydrophilic surface hydroxyl groups.
Electrochemical sensors utilizing nanoporous carbon (e.g., ordered mesoporous carbon with ~310 nm pores) enable non-enzymatic glucose detection by providing conductive pathways and catalytic surfaces for direct oxidation. These materials achieve sensitivities around 5080 A mM cm in physiological ranges (015 mM), as seen in hybridized graphene-carbon architectures where edge-plane defects and - stacking boost electron transfer rates. The porous network minimizes diffusion barriers, allowing real-time monitoring with low detection limits (~M) and minimal interference from common interferents like ascorbic acid.
The turnover frequency (TOF) in confined nanoporous catalysis, defined as the reaction rate per active site (TOF = k , where k is the rate constant), is often amplified in one-dimensional pores by single-file diffusion, which prolongs reactant residence time near sites and suppresses desorption, effectively increasing local concentrations by up to 25 times compared to bulk conditions. This diffusion-programmed effect is particularly pronounced in zeolites or carbon nanotubes, where linear pore geometries enforce sequential molecule passage, enhancing selectivity for sequential reactions.
Emerging in 2025, AI-optimized covalent organic frameworks (COFs) are advancing VOC sensing for air quality monitoring by integrating machine learning with porous frameworks to predict and tune pore architectures for selective binding. These AI-driven designs, such as COF-on-MOF hybrids, achieve sub-ppm detection of VOCs (e.g., benzene, toluene) with humidity resistance, using colorimetric shifts or impedance changes for portable devices. Optimization algorithms refine linker selections to maximize adsorption isotherms, enabling real-time differentiation of VOC mixtures in urban environments.

Biomedical Applications

Nanoporous materials play a pivotal role in biomedical applications due to their high surface area, tunable pore sizes, and ability to facilitate controlled interactions with biological systems. In drug delivery, tissue engineering, and diagnostics, these materials enable targeted therapies while minimizing off-target effects, with biocompatibility enhanced through surface modifications. Their porous architecture allows for efficient loading and release of therapeutic agents, promoting integration with physiological environments.
In drug delivery, mesoporous silica nanoparticles (MSNs) are widely employed for the controlled release of anticancer drugs such as doxorubicin (DOX). These nanoparticles feature pH-responsive gates that trigger payload release in acidic tumor microenvironments, achieving up to 80% DOX release at pH 5.0 compared to minimal release (around 20%) at physiological pH 7.4, thereby improving therapeutic efficacy and reducing systemic toxicity. For tissue engineering, nanoporous hydroxyapatite foams serve as scaffolds for bone regeneration, where pore sizes in the 20-50 nm range enhance osteoblast adhesion, proliferation, and differentiation by mimicking the nanostructure of natural bone extracellular matrix. Nano-hydroxyapatite particles within these scaffolds, sized 20-80 nm, promote mesenchymal stem cell attachment and osteogenic activity, leading to improved bone tissue formation in vivo. These structures provide mechanical support while allowing nutrient diffusion and vascular ingrowth, essential for scaffold integration.
In diagnostics, porous gold substrates enable surface-enhanced Raman scattering (SERS) for ultrasensitive biomarker detection. These materials amplify Raman signals through plasmonic hotspots, achieving detection limits down to 10910^{-9}109 M for biomolecules like cytochrome c, facilitating early disease identification in clinical samples. The nanoporous architecture increases surface area for analyte adsorption, enhancing signal reproducibility and sensitivity.
Biocompatibility remains a key consideration for nanoporous materials in biomedical use, with silica-based implants recognized as generally safe (GRAS) by the FDA for certain applications, such as topical formulations approved in recent years. Toxicity concerns, including potential hemolysis or inflammation from silanol groups, are effectively mitigated through polyethylene glycol (PEG) coatings, which improve colloidal stability, reduce protein adsorption, and lower cytotoxicity in cellular and animal models.

Environmental and Energy Applications

Nanoporous materials play a crucial role in environmental remediation, particularly for water purification through the adsorption of heavy metals. Activated carbon, a widely used nanoporous material derived from various precursors, exhibits high adsorption capacities for contaminants like Pb ions, achieving up to 135 mg/g under optimized conditions such as varying dosages and contact times. Zeolites, another class of nanoporous aluminosilicates, function as efficient ion exchangers in water treatment, with NaX-type zeolites demonstrating a maximum adsorption capacity of 500 mg/g for Pb and nearly 99% removal efficiency within 10 minutes at neutral pH. These materials selectively bind heavy metal cations via ion exchange mechanisms, replacing them with less harmful ions like Na, thereby purifying wastewater from industrial effluents and enabling reuse in ecological systems.
In oil spill cleanup, covalent organic frameworks (COFs) offer superior selectivity for hydrocarbons over water due to their tunable hydrophobic pores. For instance, the porous covalent porphyrin framework PCPF-1 achieves exceptional uptake capacities of 14702590 wt% for saturated hydrocarbons (C5C8) and gasoline, with a water contact angle of 135 ensuring high selectivity that prevents water co-adsorption. Fluorine-functionalized COFs integrated into superhydrophobic sponges further enhance this performance, enabling selective adsorption of crude oil from aqueous environments with efficiencies exceeding 95% for hydrocarbons while maintaining reusability over multiple cycles. These properties make COFs promising for large-scale marine pollution mitigation, where rapid and selective hydrocarbon recovery minimizes ecological damage.
Shifting to energy applications, nanoporous carbon serves as a robust host matrix for silicon nanoparticles in lithium-ion battery anodes, addressing volume expansion issues and boosting capacity. By encapsulating Si nanoparticles within 3D nanoporous carbon derived from biomass, reversible capacities of up to 740 mAh/g are achieved after 200 cycles at 200 mA/g, with rate performance retaining 300 mAh/g at high currents of 5 A/g. This hosting strategy enhances electrochemical stability and lithium storage, increasing overall battery capacity beyond traditional graphite anodes (372 mAh/g) while improving cycle life.
In supercapacitors, nanoporous carbon electrodes leverage their high surface area to deliver capacitance values up to 300 F/g in aqueous electrolytes, governed by the equation $ C = \frac{\varepsilon A}{d} $, where $ C $ is capacitance, $ \varepsilon $ is the permittivity of the electrolyte, $ A $ is the effective pore surface area, and $ d $ is the ion diffusion layer thickness (typically ~0.11 nm). Activated carbons with optimized pore sizes (12 nm) maximize ion accessibility, yielding specific capacitances of 100300 F/g depending on electrolyte and pore distribution, thus enabling high-power energy storage with rapid charge-discharge rates.
Mesoporous TiO scaffolds are integral to perovskite solar cells, providing efficient electron transport layers that enhance device stability and efficiency. Phosphorus-doped mesoporous TiO achieves a power conversion efficiency of 23.96% in PSCs, surpassing undoped variants (21.15%) by improving charge extraction and reducing recombination, with devices retaining 85% efficiency after 600 hours of operation. This nanostructure facilitates perovskite infiltration and light harvesting, contributing to the field's progress, with certified single-junction efficiencies reaching 27.2% as of November 2025.
The sustainability of nanoporous materials is amplified through biomass-derived variants, which utilize agricultural waste to produce activated carbons with lower environmental footprints. Date-palm waste-derived nanoporous carbons, for example, offer CO uptake capacities of 4.21 mmol/g at 25C while reducing production-related CO emissions by up to 50% compared to synthetic petroleum-based counterparts, owing to renewable feedstocks and energy-efficient pyrolysis processes. These materials promote circular economy principles by valorizing biomass waste, minimizing landfill contributions, and enabling reusable adsorbents for long-term environmental and energy applications.
