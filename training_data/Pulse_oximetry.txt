Pulse oximetry
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Pulse oximetry is a non-invasive spectrophotometric technique that estimates the arterial oxygen saturation (SpO2) of hemoglobin in peripheral blood by transmitting red and infrared light through a pulsatile vascular bed, such as a fingertip, and analyzing the differential absorption ratios to distinguish oxygenated from deoxygenated hemoglobin.
The method relies on the Beer-Lambert law, isolating the pulsatile (arterial) component of light attenuation to compute saturation via the ratio of red-to-infrared absorbances, which correlates empirically with true SaO2 measured by co-oximetry.
Invented in 1974 by Japanese bioengineer Takuo Aoyagi at Nihon Kohden through the discovery of in vivo pulse-based oximetry, the technology was commercialized in the 1980s and rapidly adopted as a standard for real-time hypoxemia detection in anesthesia, critical care, and respiratory monitoring.
Its widespread implementation has demonstrably reduced perioperative hypoxia incidents and improved patient safety outcomes, establishing pulse oximeters as an essential, low-cost tool in clinical practice worldwide.
However, empirical studies reveal systematic overestimation of SpO2 during hypoxemia, particularly in individuals with darker skin pigmentation due to melanin-induced alterations in light scattering and absorption, resulting in higher rates of undetected (occult) hypoxemia compared to lighter-skinned patients.
This limitation, confirmed across multiple clinical validations and meta-analyses, underscores the need for confirmatory arterial blood gas analysis in high-risk scenarios and has prompted regulatory scrutiny, including FDA advisories on device accuracy disparities.
Additional challenges include reduced performance in low-perfusion states, motion artifacts, and dyshemoglobins like carboxyhemoglobin, though advancements in sensor design and algorithms continue to mitigate these issues.

Mechanism of Operation

Principle and Physics

Pulse oximetry relies on the spectrophotometric measurement of light absorption by hemoglobin in arterial blood, exploiting differences in the absorption spectra of oxyhemoglobin (HbO) and deoxyhemoglobin (Hb). HbO absorbs less red light at approximately 660 nm than Hb, which has a peak absorption in the red spectrum, while HbO absorbs more near-infrared light at around 940 nm compared to Hb. These wavelengths are emitted by light-emitting diodes (LEDs) in the oximeter probe, with a photodetector positioned opposite to capture transmitted light through a pulsatile vascular bed, such as a fingertip or earlobe.
The underlying physics follows the Beer-Lambert law, which quantifies light attenuation as $ A = \epsilon \cdot c \cdot l $, where $ A $ is absorbance, $ \epsilon $ the molar extinction coefficient specific to the hemoglobin species and wavelength, $ c $ the concentration, and $ l $ the path length. In tissue, total light attenuation includes constant (DC) components from non-pulsatile elements like venous blood, skin, and bone, and a variable (AC) pulsatile component synchronous with arterial blood flow. The ratio $ R = \frac{\mathrm{AC}{660}/\mathrm{DC}{660}}{\mathrm{AC}{940}/\mathrm{DC}{940}} $ isolates the arterial contribution and empirically correlates to oxygen saturation (SpO) via a calibration curve derived from human volunteer data under controlled hypoxia.
This ratio-based approach assumes minimal scattering and absorption by non-hemoglobin tissue at selected wavelengths, enabling non-invasive estimation without direct blood sampling. The pulsatile detection enhances specificity to arterial oxygenation, as the AC signal amplitude is typically 1-5% of the DC, requiring signal processing to filter noise from motion or low perfusion. Empirical validation confirms SpO accuracy within 2-4% of arterial saturation (SaO) under ideal conditions, though deviations arise from law violations like variable path lengths due to tissue heterogeneity.

Derived Physiological Measurements

Pulse oximeters utilize the photoplethysmographic (PPG) waveform generated during oxygen saturation measurement to derive additional parameters that assess peripheral perfusion and hemodynamic variability. These derived metrics, calculated from the pulsatile (AC) and non-pulsatile (DC) components of the PPG signal, provide insights into tissue perfusion and fluid status without requiring invasive procedures.
The perfusion index (PI) quantifies the relative strength of peripheral blood flow by computing the ratio of the AC component (reflecting arterial pulsations) to the DC component (reflecting static tissue and venous blood absorbance), expressed as PI = (AC/DC) 100%. Values typically range from 0.02% to 20%, with PI > 1.4 indicating adequate perfusion and PI < 0.4 signaling poor peripheral circulation, as observed in conditions like vasoconstriction or shock. Clinically, PI correlates with invasive measures such as cardiac index and has been used to detect early hypovolemia or guide vasopressor therapy in critically ill patients.
Another key metric is the pleth variability index (PVi), originally abbreviated as "PBI" by Masimo Corporation (e.g., in 2008 earnings calls), a noninvasive, continuous measurement from Masimo's rainbow SET pulse oximetry technology that assesses changes in the plethysmographic waveform to evaluate fluid responsiveness and perfusion status (ranging from 0 to 100). It evaluates respiratory-induced fluctuations in the PPG waveform amplitude to predict fluid responsiveness, particularly in mechanically ventilated patients. Defined as PVi = 100, where Pmax and Pmin are the maximum and minimum PPG amplitudes over a respiratory cycle, it ranges from 0 (no variability) to 100 (maximum variability). A PVi > 13-18% often predicts increased cardiac output following fluid bolus in preload-dependent states, though accuracy diminishes when baseline PI is low (<1.4), reflecting motion artifacts or low perfusion confounding the signal.
Advanced algorithms may also extract respiration rate (RR) from PPG waveform modulations caused by thoracic impedance changes during breathing, achieving accuracies comparable to capnography in stable patients (error <2 breaths/min). These derivations enhance pulse oximetry's utility in perioperative and intensive care monitoring but require signal quality validation, as low PI or arrhythmias can introduce errors exceeding 10-20% in variability estimates.

Clinical and Non-Clinical Applications

Medical Uses and Benefits

Pulse oximetry serves as a cornerstone for non-invasive monitoring of peripheral arterial oxygen saturation (SpO) and heart rate in diverse clinical environments, including intensive care units, emergency departments, and general wards. It enables continuous assessment of oxygenation without the need for arterial blood sampling, facilitating rapid identification of hypoxemiadefined as SpO below 90%which can precede clinical deterioration. In perioperative settings, it is a mandated standard by the American Society of Anesthesiologists for all anesthetics, providing quantitative evaluation of oxygenation to mitigate risks during induction, maintenance, and emergence from anesthesia. Studies indicate that intraoperative pulse oximetry contributes to reduced anesthesia-related complications by alerting providers to desaturation events that might otherwise go unnoticed.
For patients with respiratory pathologies, such as chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), asthma exacerbations, or acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), pulse oximetry supports titrated oxygen therapy and weaning from ventilatory support by tracking SpO trends in real time. During the COVID-19 pandemic, its application in home monitoring identified subclinical hypoxemia in discharged patients, enabling early rehospitalization and reducing mortality risks associated with silent desaturation; one program detected low oxygen levels in select cases without concurrent symptom worsening, averting potential complications. In postoperative recovery, continuous pulse oximetry has demonstrated a 12-fold increase in detecting SpO below 90% within the first hour compared to intermittent checks, underscoring its role in preventing undetected hypoxemic episodes.
In neonatal medicine, pulse oximetry screening, performed after 24 hours of life, detects critical congenital heart defects (CCHDs) by measuring pre- and post-ductal oxygen saturations; a positive screen prompts echocardiography for confirmation. This approach, recommended by the American Academy of Pediatrics, identifies up to 70% of previously undiagnosed CCHDs with high specificity, reducing early infant deaths from these conditions by 33%averting about 120 such fatalities annually in the United States alone. The test's benefits stem from its simplicity, low cost (under $50 per device in many settings), and non-invasiveness, allowing widespread implementation in newborn nurseries without procedural risks. Overall, pulse oximetry's advantages include portability for ambulatory use, immediate feedback for clinical decision-making, and proven efficacy in enhancing outcomes through proactive hypoxemia management across age groups and acuity levels.

Safety Considerations in Use

Pulse oximetry devices pose minimal direct risks to users, with complication rates significantly lower than invasive alternatives such as arterial blood gas sampling. Reported adverse events primarily involve localized skin trauma rather than systemic effects.
Prolonged or improper probe application can lead to skin integrity issues, including pressure necrosis, blanching, discoloration, lacerations, and cuts, particularly in patients with fragile skin such as neonates or those in intensive care settings. At least eight such incidents have been documented in healthcare advisories, often attributable to excessive pressure from tight probes or extended attachment times exceeding recommended durations. To mitigate these, guidelines emphasize rotating probe sites every 4-8 hours, ensuring loose but secure fit, and inspecting skin periodically for early signs of compromise.
Thermal burns represent a rare but serious hazard, typically arising from probe malfunction, overheating due to electrical faults, or prolonged exposure in low-perfusion states. Documented cases include second-degree burns and even gangrene in infants, with one report describing toe necrosis following days-long application in an ICU environment. Experimental data indicate probes remain safe on well-perfused skin up to 43C for at least 8 hours, beyond which burn risk escalates. Additional factors, such as concurrent photodynamic therapy, can exacerbate photosensitivity-related burns even with short attachment times. Electrical burns from current leakage are exceptionally uncommon in modern devices but have been noted in older nondisposable probes.
Adhesive components in disposable probes may cause minor skin irritation or allergic reactions in sensitive individuals, though this is infrequent and resolves upon removal. Safe use requires adherence to manufacturer specifications, including selecting the index or middle finger due to better peripheral blood flow for accurate readings, avoiding the thumb and little finger owing to poorer perfusion; choosing a finger without nail polish or artificial enhancements that could skew measurements or promote prolonged incorrect application; ensuring the finger is warm and stationary to minimize motion artifacts and perfusion-related errors; and immediate discontinuation if device malfunction (e.g., unusual warmth) is suspected. In pediatric or vulnerable populations, extra vigilance is warranted, with some authorities reporting up to seven burn cases linked to probes between 2015 and 2020. Overall, these risks are avertable through standard protocols, underscoring the device's favorable safety profile in clinical practice.

Limitations and Sources of Error

Inherent Technical Constraints

Pulse oximeters operate under the modified Beer-Lambert law, which posits that light attenuation through tissue is linearly proportional to the concentration of absorbing species, assuming negligible scattering and homogeneous media; however, biological tissues exhibit significant light scattering, necessitating empirical corrections that introduce systematic inaccuracies in absorbance calculations. This approximation limits precision, particularly in deriving pulsatile arterial components from composite signals encompassing arterial, venous, and static tissue absorption.
The technology fundamentally assumes hemoglobin exists solely in oxyhemoglobin (HbO) and deoxyhemoglobin (HHb) forms, calculating functional saturation (SpO) as HbO divided by the sum of HbO and HHb; dyshemoglobins such as carboxyhemoglobin (COHb) and methemoglobin (MetHb) violate this by exhibiting distinct absorption spectraCOHb mimics HbO at 660 nm, yielding falsely elevated SpO readings up to 100% despite hypoxemia in carbon monoxide poisoning, while MetHb causes bidirectional errors converging toward 85%. Standard two-wavelength devices cannot distinguish these, requiring multi-wavelength variants for total hemoglobin species measurement, though these remain calibration-dependent.
The ratio-of-ratios (R) methodR = (AC/DC) / (AC/DC), where AC and DC denote pulsatile and steady-state componentsrelies on empirical lookup tables calibrated against arterial blood gas (ABG) co-oximetry for SpO derivation, inherently propagating calibration errors with a root-mean-square deviation of approximately 2%, equating to 4% errors (two standard deviations) in about 5% of measurements under controlled conditions. This stems from non-linearities in the calibration curve, exacerbated at saturation extremes: accuracy degrades below 70% SaO due to sparse data points and the isobestic point convergence of absorption spectra, and above 97% where small denominator changes amplify relative errors.
Signal processing assumes perfect isolation of arterial pulsation, but incomplete separation of venous or capillary contributions introduces bias, with the device's inability to quantify absolute hemoglobin concentration or perfusion index deriving solely from relative ratios, precluding standalone anemia or hypovolemia detection. These constraints arise from the two-wavelength parsimony, trading specificity for simplicity, and persist despite signal averaging, as fundamental spectral overlaps cannot be resolved without additional emitters or detectors.

Physiological and Environmental Factors Impacting Accuracy

Low peripheral perfusion, characterized by reduced blood flow to extremities due to conditions such as shock, vasoconstriction, or hypothermia, impairs pulse oximetry accuracy by weakening the pulsatile arterial signal essential for distinguishing arterial from venous blood. Studies indicate that in low-perfusion states, including sepsis or cardiogenic shock, SpO readings become unreliable, with errors increasing as perfusion indices drop below critical thresholds.
Motion artifacts from patient movement introduce signal noise that disrupts photodetection, frequently causing erroneous readings or false alarms in clinical settings like anesthesia or intensive care. Advanced signal processing in modern devices mitigates but does not eliminate this issue, particularly during vigorous activity.
Dyshemoglobins, such as carboxyhemoglobin (COHb) in carbon monoxide poisoning or methemoglobin (MetHb) in methemoglobinemia, systematically bias readings because standard pulse oximeters operating at 660 nm and 940 nm wavelengths cannot differentiate these from oxyhemoglobin or deoxyhemoglobin. COHb leads to SpO overestimation by up to 10-20% or more, masking hypoxia, while MetHb causes readings to converge toward 85% regardless of true saturation.
Abnormal hemoglobin variants, including sickle cell hemoglobinopathies, alter light absorption properties and can result in SpO overestimation during desaturation events. Anemia affects accuracy primarily at low saturations below 50%, though functional saturation algorithms in some devices compensate by ratioing oxyhemoglobin to total functional hemoglobin.
Skin and core temperature variations influence vasomotor tone and perfusion; cold extremities (below approximately 33C) induce vasoconstriction, exacerbating low-perfusion errors, whereas elevated skin temperatures may lead to SpO overestimation through enhanced tissue scattering.
Postural changes impact measurement accuracy, particularly for heart rate; routine checks while standing are not recommended due to orthostatic effects on blood flow, which can introduce variations. Guidelines recommend sitting quietly for several minutes, ensuring the hand is warm, relaxed, and positioned below heart level to optimize accuracy. A lying-and-standing pulse oximetry test is employed diagnostically to detect position-related differences in heart rate and oxygen saturation levels.
Ambient light interference, particularly from fluorescent or sunlight sources, can penetrate sensors and alter detected intensities, leading to inaccurate ratios unless shielded probes are used. Electrical interference from nearby devices and extreme environmental temperatures further degrade performance by affecting sensor electronics or patient physiology indirectly.

Accuracy Disparities and Controversies

Skin Tone-Related Biases: Empirical Evidence

A 2020 retrospective cohort study of over 10,000 paired pulse oximetry (SpO2) and arterial blood gas (SaO2) measurements from hospitalized adults at the University of Michigan found that pulse oximeters overestimated oxygen saturation in Black patients compared to White patients, particularly during hypoxemia. Specifically, when true SaO2 was below 88%, SpO2 readings falsely indicated 92% (occult hypoxemia) in 11.7% of measurements from Black patients versus 3.6% from White patients, yielding an odds ratio of 3.06 (95% CI, 2.00 to 4.69). This discrepancy persisted after adjusting for confounders like age, sex, and comorbidities, suggesting a measurement bias rather than solely clinical differences.
Subsequent prospective studies have corroborated these findings in controlled and real-world settings. A 2024 evaluation of 11 fingertip pulse oximeters in healthy volunteers with induced hypoxemia (SaO2 down to 70%) demonstrated that devices exhibited greater root mean square error in participants with dark skin pigmentation, with biases exceeding FDA accuracy standards (Arms 3% for SaO2 70-100%) more frequently at low saturations; for instance, one model showed up to 4.5% overestimation in dark skin versus under 2% in light skin.00086-0/fulltext) Similarly, a 2023 systematic review of 35 studies involving over 2,000 participants reported consistent overestimation of SpO2 by 1-4% in individuals with dark skin tones relative to SaO2, with the effect amplifying below 85% saturation and varying by device type and probe placement.
Real-world data from critically ill patients further quantify the bias. In a March 2025 prospective study of over 1,000 intensive care unit admissionsthe largest of its kindpulse oximeters displayed skin pigment-dependent performance, with darker tones associated with 2-3% higher SpO2 errors during desaturation events compared to lighter tones, increasing the risk of undetected hypoxemia by up to 2.5-fold. A 2024 meta-analysis of wearable and clinical devices across skin pigmentation groups confirmed systematic inaccuracies, with dark skin showing the largest deviations (mean bias -1.5% to +3.2%) when benchmarked against co-oximetry, though all tones exhibited some error outside manufacturer claims. These results align with earlier volunteer studies, such as a 2005 investigation where dark skin reduced accuracy at SaO2 <80%, attributing errors to melanin interference with red light transmission without altering the device's algorithmic assumptions calibrated primarily on light-skinned populations.00086-0/fulltext) While magnitudes vary by device and perfusion, the empirical pattern indicates a directional bias toward overestimation in darker skin, potentially delaying interventions in clinical scenarios like COVID-19 pneumonia.

Regulatory Responses and Legal Challenges

In response to evidence of pulse oximeter inaccuracies across skin tones, particularly during the COVID-19 pandemic, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) issued draft guidance in November 2021 recommending that manufacturers test devices on individuals representing a range of skin pigmentations to address performance disparities. This followed studies, including one published in the New England Journal of Medicine in 2020, demonstrating that pulse oximeters overestimated oxygen saturation by up to 3 percentage points more frequently in Black patients compared to White patients, potentially delaying interventions. However, a 2024 analysis found low adherence to the FDA's 2013 precursor recommendation for diverse testing, with many cleared devices lacking validation across skin tones.
The FDA escalated its efforts in February 2024 with an executive summary on performance evaluation protocols, emphasizing the need for standardized testing to mitigate biases observed in clinical settings, such as higher variability in readings for darker skin. On January 6, 2025, the agency proposed updated recommendations requiring pulse oximeters to demonstrate non-disparate accuracydefined as mean bias and root mean square deviation within specified limits across light, medium, and dark skin tonespotentially affecting marketing authorization for non-compliant devices. The FDA noted that some existing devices might meet these criteria without modifications, but anticipated re-submissions from others, amid criticism from medical societies like the American Thoracic Society for prior regulatory leniency. State attorneys general and U.S. senators have urged stricter enforcement, highlighting delays in treatment for minority patients during COVID-19 outbreaks.
Legal challenges have targeted manufacturers for failing to disclose or mitigate known biases. In January 2024, a California physician filed a lawsuit against 12 companies, including Masimo and GE Healthcare, alleging negligent sale of inaccurate fingertip oximeters that overestimate saturation in darker-skinned individuals, violating consumer protection laws. A related class-action suit against retailers like Walgreens and CVS, alongside producers such as GE and Masimo, claims deceptive marketing of devices untested for skin tone variability, citing peer-reviewed evidence of clinical harm. Medtronic settled a similar lawsuit in November 2024 over its Nellcor oximeters' alleged inaccuracies in patients with darker pigmentation, without admitting liability, prompting device labeling updates. Separate class actions against wearable makers, including Apple for its smartwatch oximetry feature, accuse firms of omitting bias risks on labels despite internal awareness from studies predating commercialization. Researchers argue that ongoing litigation may drive compliance but insufficient without federal mandates, as manufacturers face reputational and liability risks from unaddressed discrepancies.

Device Types and Technological Variations

Professional Medical Devices

Professional medical pulse oximeters are Class II medical devices regulated by the FDA, requiring 510(k) premarket clearance to demonstrate safety and effectiveness for clinical monitoring of peripheral oxygen saturation (SpO) and pulse rate in healthcare settings such as hospitals, intensive care units, and operating rooms. These devices utilize transmission-based spectrophotometry, emitting red and infrared light through a digit, earlobe, or forehead to detect oxygenated and deoxygenated hemoglobin ratios, with sensors designed for attachment to medical equipment for continuous or spot-check use. Unlike consumer-grade models, professional oximeters undergo rigorous validation against arterial blood gas references, targeting accuracy within 2% in normoxemic ranges (85-100% SpO) and performing under low perfusion, motion, and diverse physiological conditions as per FDA guidance and ISO 80601-2-61 standards for essential performance.
Key technologies distinguish leading professional systems, including Masimo's Signal Extraction Technology (SET), which employs adaptive signal processing to separate arterial pulsatile signals from noise, achieving specified SpO accuracy of 1.5% root mean square (ARMS) during motionsuperior to competitors like Nellcor's OxiMax in some evaluations. Nellcor (Medtronic) devices, conversely, prioritize rapid stabilization of readings, with studies in newborns showing mean SpO errors below 1.1% across varied conditions, outperforming Masimo in low-perfusion scenarios. Other manufacturers such as Philips and GE Healthcare offer integrated modules with similar transmittance designs, often featuring alarms for hypoxemia thresholds and compatibility with electronic health records. Comparative analyses indicate hospital-grade oximeters generally exhibit lower bias and higher correlation (R 0.51-0.80) with reference oximetry than consumer devices, though performance varies by model and patient factors.
Regulatory evolution emphasizes equity in accuracy, with the FDA's 2025 draft guidance mandating expanded clinical studies (n 100 per skin tone category via spectrophotometric classification) to mitigate biases observed in darker skin tones, where professional oximeters have shown overestimation of SpO by up to 3-12% in hypoxemia per meta-analyses. Devices must include labeling on limitations like nail polish interference or poor circulation, and integration with multi-parameter monitors enables trending for critical care decisions, such as ventilator adjustments. Professional models thus support evidence-based hypoxia detection but require clinician interpretation alongside clinical context, as no oximeter substitutes for direct blood gas analysis in ambiguous cases.

Consumer-Grade and Wearable Oximeters

Consumer-grade pulse oximeters encompass fingertip clip-on devices intended for over-the-counter home use, while wearable oximeters integrate SpO2 monitoring into smartwatches, fitness trackers, and rings, such as the Apple Watch Series 6 or Fitbit Sense, primarily for wellness tracking during exercise or sleep. These devices typically employ reflectance photoplethysmography (PPG), where light emitters and detectors are positioned on the same side of the skin, contrasting with the transmission mode in professional medical oximeters that pass light through a digit or earlobe. This design facilitates continuous monitoring but introduces greater susceptibility to motion artifacts and ambient light interference; however, mainstream models such as the Apple Watch and Samsung Galaxy Watch typically limit SpO2 measurements to on-demand readings, occasional background checks, or during sleep rather than providing fully continuous all-day tracking, primarily to manage battery life and reduce errors from motion artifacts.
Accuracy evaluations reveal that consumer wearables achieve root mean square errors (RMSE) of approximately 4% in oxygen saturation measurements compared to arterial blood gas references under controlled conditions, deemed tolerable for non-clinical screening but inferior to medical-grade devices' typical RMSE below 2-3%. For instance, the Apple Watch Series 6 demonstrated no strong systematic bias against conventional pulse oximeters in adults, with mean differences around 0.8%, though performance degrades at saturations below 90%, where errors increase across all oximeters. Similarly, portable consumer fingertip models have shown reliability for home monitoring in resource-limited settings, with biases under 2% in normoxic conditions. However, systematic reviews highlight variability, with some devices failing to meet FDA standards for accuracy in dynamic scenarios like exercise.
Skin pigmentation impacts wearable accuracy, with multiple studies documenting overestimation of SpO2 by 2-4% in individuals with darker tones due to melanin's absorption of red and infrared light, potentially masking hypoxemia more severely than in lighter skin. This bias persists in reflectance-based wearables, though certain models exhibit reduced disparities through algorithmic corrections. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) classifies many consumer oximeters as Class II wellness devices exempt from premarket notification if not claiming medical diagnostic utility, unlike cleared medical-grade counterparts requiring 510(k) validation against clinical performance predicates. In 2021 and updated via 2025 draft guidance, the FDA cautioned against relying on these for critical decisions, emphasizing non-clinical bench testing limitations in diverse populations. Users are advised to corroborate readings with professional equipment, particularly for symptomatic individuals or those with chronic respiratory conditions.

Software-Based Implementations

Software-based pulse oximetry implementations leverage digital signal processing (DSP) algorithms to derive oxygen saturation (SpO) estimates from photoplethysmographic (PPG) signals, often using commodity hardware such as smartphone cameras rather than dedicated optoelectronic sensors. These systems compute the pulsatile (AC) and baseline (DC) components of red and infrared light absorption modulated by arterial blood flow, applying the ratio-of-ratios method: $ R = \frac{\mathrm{AC_{red}/DC_{red}}}{\mathrm{AC_{IR}/DC_{IR}}} $, followed by empirical calibration to SpO via equations like SpO 110 - 25R. Signal preprocessing includes filtering for motion artifacts, peak detection for AC extraction, and averaging to mitigate noise, typically implemented on microcontrollers or mobile processors.
A prominent approach utilizes smartphone cameras and LED flashes for contact or non-contact PPG acquisition, where a finger is placed over the camera lens to capture transmitted or reflected light variations. Consumer-facing mobile applications for Android and iOS platforms, such as "Pulsioxmetro - Monitor SPO2" on Google Play and "Oxgeno en Sangre Monitor" on the App Store, employ this technique to estimate SpO via PPG using the phone's camera and flash. These apps are not medical-grade, with accuracy varying (studies indicate 90-96% agreement in some cases but often lower reliability), and are unsuitable for clinical diagnosis or reliable monitoringdedicated pulse oximeters or devices with built-in sensors are recommended instead. In a 2022 clinical validation study involving induced hypoxemia with varied inspired oxygen fractions (FiO from 0.21 to 0.12), a smartphone-based system achieved SpO readings accurate to within 3% root mean square deviation (RMSD) down to 70% saturation compared to clinical-grade reference oximeters, meeting interim FDA/ISO standards for pulse oximetry at the time. Similar deep learning-enhanced algorithms process video frames to estimate SpO from subtle color channel fluctuations, addressing challenges like inconsistent illumination and low signal-to-noise ratios inherent to non-specialized sensors.
Advanced implementations incorporate machine learning (ML) or artificial intelligence (AI) for improved robustness, such as neural networks trained on diverse datasets to refine feature extraction from PPG waveforms or correct for physiological variabilities. A 2024 systematic review of AI models for pulse oximetry highlighted convolutional and recurrent neural networks achieving mean absolute errors below 2% in controlled settings, though performance degrades in low-perfusion or motion-heavy scenarios without hardware calibration. Open-source tools like ABOSA provide modular software for offline PPG analysis, computing SpO alongside respiration rate via time-domain and frequency-domain methods, validated against clinical data for accuracy in post-processing archived signals. These software frameworks enable integration with wearable data streams or telemedicine platforms but require validation against arterial blood gas references to ensure clinical reliability, as algorithmic assumptions about hemoglobin absorption spectra may falter under atypical conditions like dyshemoglobinemias.

Historical Development

Precursors and Invention

The measurement of blood oxygen saturation, or oximetry, originated with invasive techniques in the early 20th century, where blood samples were extracted from patients, analyzed spectrophotometrically, and often reinjected. These methods were cumbersome, risked infection, and provided only intermittent data. In 1937, German physiologist Karl Matthes developed the first in vivo oximeter using two wavelengths of light (red and green) applied to earlobes or nasal septa to estimate hemoglobin saturation, though it required individual calibration and was sensitive to motion and ambient light.
Further advancements came during World War II, when American physiologist Glenn Millikan created the first portable oximeter in 1942an ear-clip device using photoelectric cells to monitor oxygen levels in aviators under low-oxygen conditions at high altitudes. Millikan coined the term "oximetry" and improved on Matthes' design by incorporating eight wavelengths for more accurate readings of oxy- and deoxyhemoglobin, but these early devices measured average (non-pulsatile) saturation, assuming steady blood flow and still requiring calibration with arterial blood gas analysis. By the 1960s, multiwavelength ear oximeters, such as those refined by A.A. Shaw in 1964, allowed continuous monitoring in clinical settings like anesthesia but remained bulky, calibration-dependent, and limited to stationary use without isolating arterial pulsations from venous or tissue interference.
The core innovation of pulse oximetry emerged in 1972 from Japanese engineer Takuo Aoyagi at Nihon Kohden Corporation, who, while developing a noninvasive dye-dilution method for cardiac output measurement, observed that the ratio of pulsatile (AC) to non-pulsatile (DC) components of light absorption at red (around 660 nm) and infrared (around 940 nm) wavelengths specifically reflected arterial oxygen saturation (SpO), minimizing artifacts from tissue, venous blood, or dyes. This principle exploited the Beer-Lambert law and the distinct absorption spectra of oxy- and deoxyhemoglobin, enabling calibration-free, motion-tolerant estimates without blood sampling. Aoyagi filed a Japanese patent in 1974, and Nihon Kohden released the world's first pulse oximeteran ear model (OLV-5100)in 1975, marking the transition from average to pulse-based oximetry. Independent parallel work by Japan's Minolta Camera Company around the same period contributed to early competition, but Aoyagi's ratio technique formed the foundational algorithm still used today.

Commercialization and Widespread Adoption

The principle of pulse oximetry, discovered by Takuo Aoyagi in 1974 while working at Nihon Kohden in Japan, transitioned to commercial products in the early 1980s, primarily through U.S.-based firms that refined the technology for clinical reliability. Nihon Kohden had developed prototype devices in Japan by the late 1970s, but broader market entry occurred with Biox Technology's launch of the first standalone commercial pulse oximeter in 1981, designed for respiratory monitoring and later adapted for perioperative use. This device, acquired by Ohmeda (a BOC subsidiary) in 1984, emphasized non-invasive fingertip or earlobe sensors using red and infrared light to measure arterial oxygen saturation.
Nellcor, founded by anesthesiologist William New in 1981, released its N-100 model in 1983 after receiving U.S. FDA clearance, introducing proprietary signal processing to mitigate motion artifacts and low perfusion challenges that plagued early competitors. This innovation, including reflectance-based sensors and adaptive filtering, enabled more consistent readings during surgery, positioning Nellcor as a market leader; by the mid-1980s, its devices were integrated into anesthesia machines from major manufacturers like Datex-Ohmeda. Competition intensified with Japanese firms like Minolta Camera entering the U.S. market, but American companies dominated due to aggressive FDA approvals and clinical trials demonstrating reduced desaturation events.
Widespread adoption surged in anesthesiology by the late 1980s, driven by evidence from closed malpractice claims showing pulse oximetry prevented hypoxic brain damage; a 1987 analysis indicated it could avert up to 22,000 annual U.S. incidents of intraoperative hypoxia. Professional societies, including the American Society of Anesthesiologists, endorsed it as standard care for general anesthesia by 1987, leading to near-universal integration in operating rooms and recovery units; U.S. hospital adoption rates exceeded 90% within a decade. Expansion followed to intensive care, neonatal units, and emergency transport, with portable models like Nonin's Onyx 9500 fingertip device in 1995 facilitating pre-hospital use. The COVID-19 pandemic from 2020 further propelled global dissemination, with over 100 million consumer-grade units shipped annually by 2021, though regulatory scrutiny increased due to accuracy variances in non-clinical settings.

Modern Innovations and Standards Evolution

In recent years, innovations in pulse oximetry have focused on enhancing accuracy across diverse skin tones and physiological conditions through advanced algorithms and sensor designs. Medtronic's investigational Nellcor technology, designated under the FDA's Safer Technologies Program in May 2025, incorporates patient-specific factors such as skin pigmentation and sensor placement into oxygen saturation (SpO2) calculations to mitigate biases observed in traditional devices. Similarly, Masimo's Signal Extraction Technology (SET) employs adaptive signal processing to improve measurements during motion and low perfusion, demonstrating reduced error rates in clinical validations compared to conventional pulse oximetry. These developments build on multi-wavelength approaches and AI-driven predictive analytics, as explored by Medtronic, which aim to detect subtle variations in light absorption influenced by melanin content without relying solely on red and infrared LEDs.
Wearable and specialized applications represent another frontier, with GE HealthCare's collaboration on fetal pulse oximetry in May 2025 enabling real-time intrapartum monitoring to detect hypoxia earlier than Doppler ultrasound alone. Consumer-grade wearables have integrated refined photoplethysmography sensors for continuous monitoring, though empirical data indicate persistent challenges in low-signal environments. Innovations like these have been spurred by COVID-19-era revelations of SpO2 overestimation in darker skin tones, prompting redesigns that prioritize empirical validation over legacy assumptions about uniform light transmission.
Standards evolution has accelerated post-2020, with the FDA issuing draft guidance in January 2025 recommending clinical testing on at least 24 subjects per skin tone category (using Fitzpatrick or Monk scales) across SpO2 ranges of 70-100% to address pigmentation-related biases. This updates the 2013 FDA guidance and aligns partially with ISO 80601-2-61:2017, which lacks specific skin tone requirements but emphasizes essential performance; however, a 2025 medRxiv analysis found only 1 of 34 devices met anticipated FDA differential bias criteria, underscoring gaps in current ISO conformance. The FDA anticipates many marketed devices will comply without major redesigns, but labeling must now disclose performance by skin tone if disparities exceed 2-3% accuracy limits, reflecting causal links between melanin absorption and signal attenuation validated in controlled studies. Regulatory scrutiny has intensified, with a February 2024 FDA executive summary highlighting non-compliance in overtesting on light skin cohorts, driving toward causality-informed standards rather than historical precedents.
