Hamilton first formally presented quaternions as quadrinomial expressions with a real scalar and an imaginary vector part in a communication to the Royal Irish Academy on November 13, 1843, marking a breakthrough in hypercomplex numbers that influenced algebra, geometry, and physics.

This mapping avoids issues like gimbal lock inherent in Euler angle representations and allows smooth interpolation between rotations using spherical linear interpolation (slerp).
Beyond pure mathematics, quaternions have found extensive applications in fields requiring robust 3D rotation computations. In computer graphics and animation, they enable the interpolation of rotation sequences via quaternion curves, as introduced for keyframe animation, reducing computational overhead compared to 33 rotation matrices while preserving orthogonality. They are also employed in robotics, aerospace engineering for attitude control, and computer vision for pose estimation, where their compactness (four parameters versus nine for matrices) and resistance to numerical errors prove advantageous. Despite initial resistance due to their non-commutativity, quaternions' revival in the late 20th century underscores their enduring utility in modern computational geometry.

History

Invention and early development

Although the full quaternion algebra is attributed to the Irish mathematician William Rowan Hamilton, the French mathematician Olinde Rodrigues had independently described in 1840 a parametrization using four parameters equivalent to unit quaternions for representing rotations in three dimensions. Hamilton conceived the fundamental idea on October 16, 1843, while walking along the Royal Canal in Dublin with his wife on their way to a meeting of the Royal Irish Academy. In a moment of inspiration, Hamilton realized the non-commutative multiplication rule for the imaginary units i, j, and k, and he carved the equation i = j = k = ijk = 1 into the stone of Brougham Bridge (now known as Broom Bridge) to commemorate the discovery. This event marked a breakthrough after over a decade of Hamilton's efforts to develop a mathematical framework for three-dimensional space.
Hamilton's motivation stemmed from his desire to extend the algebra of complex numbers, which effectively handled two-dimensional geometry and rotations, to three dimensions in order to address geometric transformations and solve polynomial equations involving spatial quantities. He had struggled for years to define a consistent multiplication for ordered triples representing points in 3D space, analogous to complex numbers for 2D, but found that preserving key properties like a multiplicative norm required abandoning commutativity. This geometric and algebraic challenge drove Hamilton to formulate quaternions as a four-dimensional extension, incorporating a real part and three imaginary components.
Hamilton first announced his discovery in a preliminary paper, "On a New Species of Imaginary Quantities connected with a Theory of Quaternions," presented to the Royal Irish Academy on November 13, 1843, and published in the Proceedings of the Royal Irish Academy, volume 2, in 1844 (pages 424434). He elaborated on the system in a more comprehensive paper, "On Quaternions," presented on November 11, 1844, and also published in the Proceedings (volume 3). Hamilton's definitive exposition appeared in his 1853 book, Lectures on Quaternions, published by Hodges and Smith in Dublin, which systematically outlined the method based on lectures he had delivered at Trinity College since 1848.
The early reception of quaternions was mixed, with significant criticism centered on their non-commutative multiplication, which challenged prevailing algebraic norms. British mathematician Augustus De Morgan, in discussions around 1841, had asserted that commutativity was an essential property of any legitimate algebra, a view that Hamilton directly contested as he developed quaternions, leading to debates that highlighted the system's unconventional nature. Despite initial skepticism, quaternions gained proponents, including Scottish physicist Peter Guthrie Tait, who extensively developed their applications in vector analysis through works like his 1867 Elementary Treatise on Quaternions and integrated them into treatments of physical problems, such as electromagnetism.

Applications in physics and mathematics

In the late 19th century, quaternions found significant applications in physics, particularly through the work of James Clerk Maxwell and Peter Guthrie Tait. Maxwell initially explored quaternions for formulating electromagnetism, corresponding with Tait on their potential to express vector operations in his electromagnetic theory. In his 1865 paper "A Dynamical Theory of the Electromagnetic Field", he presented his theory in component form with around 20 equations. In his 1873 Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism, he included quaternion expressions for electromagnetic quantities and equations of the field (in Articles 618619), providing a compact alternative formulation. However, he ultimately favored a component-based vector approach in the Treatise to simplify calculations, and these quaternion forms were later reformulated into the modern four vector equations by Oliver Heaviside and others. Tait, a staunch advocate, extensively applied quaternions to electromagnetism and fluid dynamics, developing "quaternion vector analysis" as detailed in his 1867 Elementary Treatise on Quaternions, where he demonstrated their utility for describing rotational motions and strains in physical systems. Tait also extended quaternions to knot theory, inspired by Helmholtz's vortex theorems, using them to model atomic structures as knotted ether configurations in his 1885 Properties of Matter, influencing early ideas in topology and physics.
By the early 20th century, quaternions declined in popularity for physical applications, supplanted by the simpler vector calculus developed independently by Josiah Willard Gibbs and Oliver Heaviside. Gibbs's 18811884 Elements of Vector Analysis and Heaviside's 18851889 Electromagnetic Theory emphasized scalar and vector products without the scalar and vector components of quaternions, making computations more straightforward for electromagnetism and mechanics, as Heaviside critiqued quaternions as overly complex for practical engineering. This shift prioritized accessibility, leading to widespread adoption of vector methods in physics textbooks and diminishing quaternion use until mid-century.
Quaternions experienced a revival in the 20th century across diverse fields. In rigid body dynamics, they proved superior for representing rotations without singularities like those in Euler angles, gaining traction in aerospace engineering for attitude control systems by the 1960s, as explored in works like Altmann's 1986 Rotations, Quaternions, and Double Groups. In quantum mechanics, quaternions relate closely to Pauli matrices, which represent spin operators; the matrices _x, _y, _z correspond to the quaternion units i, j, k, facilitating the description of spin-1/2 particles in the Dirac and Pauli equations, as shown in Altmann's 1967 analysis linking quaternion algebra to spinor representations. Their modern resurgence in computer graphics began in the 1980s, with Ken Shoemake's 1985 SIGGRAPH paper introducing quaternion-based interpolation (slerp) for smooth 3D rotations in animation, avoiding gimbal lock and enabling efficient rendering in software like OpenGL.
In pure mathematics, quaternions influenced the study of algebras, notably through Adolf Hurwitz's 1898 theorem on composition algebras, which proved that the only finite-dimensional normed division algebras over the reals are the reals (dimension 1), complexes (2), quaternions (4), and octonions (8), establishing quaternions as the highest-dimensional associative example. This result, originally published in Nachrichten der Kniglichen Gesellschaft der Wissenschaften zu Gttingen, underscored quaternions' uniqueness in preserving multiplicative norms and inspired subsequent work in non-associative algebras and Lie groups.

Definition

Components and basis elements

A quaternion $ q $ is formally defined as an ordered quadruple of real numbers, expressed in the linear combination form $ q = a + bi + cj + dk $, where $ a, b, c, d \in \mathbb{R} $ are the components and $ i, j, k $ serve as the fundamental basis elements beyond the real unit 1, with each satisfying the square relation $ i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = -1 $. This structure was introduced by William Rowan Hamilton in 1843 as an extension of complex numbers, motivated by the need to represent three-dimensional rotations algebraically.
As a vector space over the real numbers $ \mathbb{R} $, the set of quaternions $ \mathbb{H} $ forms a four-dimensional space isomorphic to $ \mathbb{R}^4 $, equipped with the standard basis $ {1, i, j, k} $. Unlike ordinary vectors in $ \mathbb{R}^4 $ or matrices, quaternions are distinguished by their non-commutative multiplication rule, which endows $ \mathbb{H} $ with the structure of a division algebra rather than a mere vector space. The components $ a, b, c, d $ correspond directly to the coordinates with respect to this basis, allowing quaternions to be identified with elements of $ \mathbb{R}^4 $ for addition and scalar multiplication, but requiring the specialized quaternion product for other operations.
The component $ a $ is termed the scalar part of the quaternion, while $ bi + cj + dk $ constitutes the vector part, often denoted as $ \mathbf{v} = (b, c, d) $ in $ \mathbb{R}^3 $. This decomposition previews the geometric utility of quaternions, where the scalar aligns with rotational invariants and the vector encodes directional information, though full exploitation occurs through subsequent algebraic operations. Common notational conventions include boldface lettering (e.g., $ \mathbf{q} $) to denote quaternions as distinct entities, or the paired form $ q = a + \mathbf{v} $ to emphasize the scalar-vector split, with the understanding that $ \mathbf{v} $ is a pure imaginary quaternion (zero scalar part).

Geometric Applications

Scalar and vector parts

A quaternion $ q $ can be decomposed into its scalar part and vector part as $ q = \operatorname{Re}(q) + \operatorname{Im}(q) $, where $ \operatorname{Re}(q) $ is a real number and $ \operatorname{Im}(q) $ is a pure quaternion representing a vector in $ \mathbb{R}^3 $. The scalar part is given by $ \operatorname{Re}(q) = \frac{q + \bar{q}}{2} $, and the vector part by $ \operatorname{Im}(q) = \frac{q - \bar{q}}{2} $, where $ \bar{q} $ denotes the conjugate of $ q $. This decomposition highlights the algebraic structure of quaternions as an extension of complex numbers, with the scalar part behaving like a real component and the vector part capturing imaginary directions.
The scalar and vector parts exhibit linearity under addition: for quaternions $ q $ and $ r $, $ \operatorname{Im}(q + r) = \operatorname{Im}(q) + \operatorname{Im}(r) $ and $ \operatorname{Re}(q + r) = \operatorname{Re}(q) + \operatorname{Re}(r) $. Under multiplication, the scalar part is symmetric, satisfying $ \operatorname{Re}(q r) = \operatorname{Re}(r q) $, as it equals $ \operatorname{Re}(q) \operatorname{Re}(r) - \operatorname{Im}(q) \cdot \operatorname{Im}(r) $, where $ \cdot $ denotes the Euclidean dot product in $ \mathbb{R}^3 $. However, the vector part of the product $ q r $ does not simply equal the product of the individual vector parts, instead given by $ \operatorname{Re}(q) \operatorname{Im}(r) + \operatorname{Re}(r) \operatorname{Im}(q) + \operatorname{Im}(q) \times \operatorname{Im}(r) $, where $ \times $ is the cross product in $ \mathbb{R}^3 $.
Pure quaternions, those with vanishing scalar part ($ \operatorname{Re}(q) = 0 $), form a three-dimensional real vector subspace of the quaternions isomorphic to $ \mathbb{R}^3 $. The multiplication of two pure quaternions $ u $ and $ v $ yields $ u v = - (u \cdot v) + (u \times v) $, where the scalar part is the negative dot product and the vector part is the cross product, thus embedding vector algebra within quaternion multiplication. For such pure quaternions, the dot product relates to the scalar part of their product via $ u \cdot v = -\operatorname{Re}(u v) $.

Rotations in three-dimensional space

Unit quaternions, which have norm equal to one, offer a compact and efficient way to parameterize rotations in three-dimensional Euclidean space. A rotation by an angle around a unit axis vector u = (u_x, u_y, u_z) is represented by the unit quaternion q = \cos\frac{\theta}{2} + \sin\frac{\theta}{2} (u_x \mathbf{i} + u_y \mathbf{j} + u_z \mathbf{k}), where i, j, and k are the standard quaternion basis elements. To apply this rotation to a vector v , identify v as the pure quaternion (with zero scalar part) 0 + v_x i + v_y j + v_z k, and compute the rotated vector v' as the vector part of q v q, where q is the conjugate of q (since |q| = 1).

This expression decomposes v into components parallel and perpendicular to u, rotating the perpendicular part while leaving the parallel part fixed.
The mapping from the group of unit quaternions (isomorphic to SU(2)) to the rotation group SO(3) is a surjective group homomorphism known as the double cover, with kernel {1, -1}; thus, q and -q induce the same rotation, requiring a 720 rotation in quaternion space to achieve a full 360 rotation in physical space.
Compared to 33 rotation matrices, unit quaternions use only four parameters (with one constraint) instead of nine, reducing storage and computation while avoiding singularities like gimbal lock that plague Euler angle representations. They enable smooth interpolation between rotations via spherical linear interpolation (SLERP), which traces geodesics on the unit quaternion hypersphere for natural animation paths. These properties make unit quaternions widely adopted in computer graphics for object and camera animations, as well as in robotics for attitude control and path planning.

Analytic Functions

Exponential, logarithm, and powers

The exponential function for a quaternion $ q $ is defined by the power series
exp(q)=n=0qnn!,
\exp(q) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{q^n}{n!},
exp(q)=n=0n!qn,
which converges absolutely for all $ q \in \mathbb{H} $. This entire function extends the complex exponential to the quaternion algebra, preserving many analytic properties within suitable domains. For a quaternion expressed in polar form $ q = r u $ with $ r \geq 0 $ and $ |u| = 1 $, the exponential simplifies to
exp(q)=cosr+usinr.
\exp(q) = \cos r + u \sin r.
exp(q)=cosr+usinr.
This formula arises from the series expansion, leveraging the property that $ u^2 = -1 $ for unit pure quaternions $ u $.
The exponential map relates closely to unit quaternions, as it sends pure imaginary quaternions (those with zero real part) to the unit sphere in $ \mathbb{H} $, analogous to how the complex exponential maps the imaginary axis to the unit circle. Specifically, for a pure unit quaternion $ u $ and real $ \theta $, $ \exp(\theta u) $ lies on the 3-sphere $ S^3 $, parameterizing rotations in three-dimensional space.
The quaternion logarithm is defined for nonzero $ q \in \mathbb{H} $ as
logq=logq+uarg(q),
\log q = \log \|q\| + u \arg(q),
logq=logq+uarg(q),
where $ u $ is a unit pure quaternion and $ \arg(q) = \acos\left( \frac{\operatorname{Re}(q)}{|q|} \right) $. This principal branch captures the magnitude via the real logarithm and the direction via the argument, but the function is multi-valued due to the non-trivial topology of the unit sphere $ S^3 $ in the quaternion space, where the exponential map is a covering map with infinitely many preimages. The branches differ by $ 2\pi k u $ for integers $ k $, reflecting the $ 2\pi $-periodicity along each direction.
Powers of a nonzero quaternion $ q $ are defined for real exponents $ t $ using the exponential and logarithm: $ q^t = \exp(t \log q) $, accounting for the multi-valued nature of the logarithm by selecting an appropriate branch. For integer exponents $ n $, powers can alternatively be computed via repeated multiplication $ q^n = q \cdot q \cdots q $ ($ n $ times) or, for positive integers, using the binomial theorem on the decomposition $ q = a + b $ with $ a $ real and $ b $ pure, since $ b^2 $ is real and the expansion simplifies accordingly. This approach ensures consistency with the non-commutative multiplication, though care is needed for fractional or negative powers due to branch choices.

Square roots of quaternions

Unlike the complex numbers, where every non-zero element has exactly two square roots, the non-commutative structure of the quaternions leads to varying multiplicities depending on the element. For a non-zero quaternion $ q $, square roots always exist, but the number differs: exactly two if $ q $ is real and positive or non-real, and infinitely many if $ q $ is real and negative.
For $ q \neq 0 $ that is either real and positive or non-real, the two square roots lie in the commutative subalgebra generated by $ q $, which is isomorphic to the complex numbers. Write $ q = s + \mathbf{v} $, where $ s = \operatorname{Re}(q) \in \mathbb{R} $ and $ \mathbf{v} $ is the vector part with $ |\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{|q|^2 - s^2} $, and $ |q| = \sqrt{s^2 + |\mathbf{v}|^2} $ is the norm. One square root (the principal one) is given by
q=q+s2+vvqs2,
\sqrt{q} = \sqrt{\frac{|q| + s}{2}} + \frac{\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|} \sqrt{\frac{|q| - s}{2}},
q=2q+s+vv2qs,
with the other being its negative; this formula assumes $ |q| + s > 0 $, which holds unless $ q $ is real and non-positive. Equivalently, in compact form,
q=q+q2(q+Re(q)).
\sqrt{q} = \frac{|q| + q}{\sqrt{2(|q| + \operatorname{Re}(q))}}.
q=2(q+Re(q))q+q.
This explicit construction follows from solving the equation $ p^2 = q $ by decomposing into scalar and vector parts and equating components, yielding a quadratic for the scalar part of $ p $.
In general, computing square roots reduces to solving within the commutative 2-dimensional subalgebra generated by $ q $ (isomorphic to $ \mathbb{C} $), which contains exactly two roots. For real positive $ q > 0 $, the roots are simply the real numbers $ \pm \sqrt{q} $.
A special case occurs when $ q $ is real and negative, say $ q = -r $ with $ r > 0 $. Here, $ |q| + s = 0 $, so the above formula fails, and there are infinitely many square roots: any $ \sqrt{r} , \mathbf{u} $, where $ \mathbf{u} $ is a pure unit quaternion (i.e., $ \operatorname{Re}(\mathbf{u}) = 0 $ and $ |\mathbf{u}| = 1 $), forming the 2-sphere $ S^2 $ in the pure quaternion subspace. In particular, the square roots of $ -1 $ are precisely the pure unit quaternions, parametrizing all rotations in 3D space.
The zero quaternion has a unique square root, which is zero itself, as H\mathbb{H}H has no nonzero nilpotent elements. Uniqueness holds for zero, unlike the cases of real negative quaternions, reflecting the division algebra structure.

Number Theory Connections

Lagrange's four-square theorem

Lagrange's four-square theorem asserts that every natural number $ n $ can be expressed as the sum of four squares of integers, that is, $ n = a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2 $ for some $ a, b, c, d \in \mathbb{Z} $. This result was established by Joseph-Louis Lagrange in 1770 through a proof involving descent on the prime factorization of $ n $.
An elegant proof using quaternions was provided by Adolf Hurwitz in 1898, leveraging the algebraic structure of the quaternion ring to demonstrate the theorem. The foundation of this approach is Euler's four-square identity, discovered by Leonhard Euler in the 1740s and communicated in a 1749 letter to Christian Goldbach, which states that the product of two sums of four squares is again a sum of four squares. Explicitly, for integers $ a,b,c,d,e,f,g,h $,
(a2+b2+c2+d2)(e2+f2+g2+h2)=(aebfcgdh)2+(af+be+chdg)2+(agbh+ce+df)2+(ah+bgcf+de)2.
\begin{align*}
&(a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2)(e^2 + f^2 + g^2 + h^2) \\
&= (ae - bf - cg - dh)^2 + (af + be + ch - dg)^2 \\
&\quad + (ag - bh + ce + df)^2 + (ah + bg - cf + de)^2.
\end{align*}
(a2+b2+c2+d2)(e2+f2+g2+h2)=(aebfcgdh)2+(af+be+chdg)2+(agbh+ce+df)2+(ah+bgcf+de)2.
This identity arises from the multiplicativity of the quaternion norm: for quaternions $ p = a + bi + cj + dk $ and $ q = e + fi + gj + hk $, the norm $ N(p) = a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2 $ satisfies $ N(pq) = N(p) N(q) $.
The quaternion proof proceeds by considering the Hurwitz integers, the ring consisting of quaternions $ a + bi + cj + dk $ where $ a, b, c, d $ are all integers or all half-integers (with the property that the sum $ 2(a + b + c + d) $ is even), which forms a Euclidean domain under the norm. Since the ring is Euclidean, it is a principal ideal domain, and the norms of its elements generate all positive integers multiplicatively via the identity. To establish the theorem, it suffices to verify that every prime is a norm: 2 is represented as $ N(1+i) = 2 $, while for an odd prime $ p $, the pigeonhole principle applied to pairs of integers modulo $ p $ yields a nontrivial quaternion with norm divisible by $ p $, implying $ p $ itself is a norm after accounting for units of norm 1. Thus, every natural number, as a product of primes, is a product of such norms and hence a sum of four squares. Modern variants refine this using the geometry of numbers or class number computations in quaternion orders.

Quaternions as pairs of complex numbers

A quaternion $ q $ can be expressed as an ordered pair of complex numbers in the form $ q = z + w j $, where $ z = a + b i $, $ w = c + d i \in \mathbb{C} $, and $ j $ satisfies the relation $ j z = \bar{z} j $ for any $ z \in \mathbb{C} $, with the overline denoting complex conjugation.
