Tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) are a heterogeneous population of immune cells, primarily T lymphocytes, B cells, and natural killer (NK) cells, that infiltrate tumor tissues and constitute a key element of the antitumor immune response within the tumor microenvironment (TME). These cells migrate from peripheral blood into primary tumors, lymph nodes, and metastases, where they interact directly with cancer cells to exert cytotoxic effects, secrete cytokines, and modulate adaptive immunity.
The composition of TILs varies by cancer type and tumor stage but typically includes CD8+ cytotoxic T cells as the predominant effectors responsible for tumor cell lysis, CD4+ helper T cells that orchestrate broader immune activation, and immunosuppressive regulatory T cells (Tregs) expressing FoxP3, which can hinder antitumor activity. B cells within TILs contribute through antibody production and antigen presentation, while NK cells provide innate cytotoxicity independent of major histocompatibility complex recognition. Functional diversity among TILs is influenced by the TME, with exhaustion markers like PD-1 often indicating prior antigen exposure but reduced efficacy, necessitating strategies to rejuvenate their potency.
Prognostically, high TIL densityparticularly of CD3+ and CD8+ subsetscorrelates with favorable outcomes in multiple malignancies; for instance, meta-analyses show CD3+ TILs associated with improved overall survival (hazard ratio 0.58; 95% CI 0.430.78) and progression-free survival (HR 0.53; 95% CI 0.390.73) across solid tumors. In specific cancers like melanoma and ovarian carcinoma, elevated CD8+ TILs predict better survival, whereas Tregs may confer poorer prognosis in breast cancer but protective effects in colorectal cancer. Assessment of TILs via immunohistochemistry or advanced imaging, including machine learning-based spatial analysis, has become standardized for guiding clinical decisions.
In immunotherapy, TILs are harnessed through adoptive cell transfer, where tumor-resident lymphocytes are surgically resected, expanded in vitro with interleukin-2, and reinfused following lymphodepleting chemotherapy. In 2024, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved lifileucel (Amtagvi), the first TIL-based therapy, for adult patients with unresectable or metastatic melanoma who have previously received a PD-1 blocking antibody, and if BRAF V600 mutation positive, a BRAF inhibitor with or without a MEK inhibitor. This approach has yielded objective response rates up to 50% in advanced melanoma. TIL density also predicts responses to immune checkpoint inhibitors, such as anti-PD-1 therapies, in non-small cell lung cancer and melanoma, with ongoing trials exploring combinations to enhance efficacy in refractory solid tumors. Despite challenges like manufacturing complexity and T cell exhaustion, TIL-based approaches represent a personalized frontier in oncology.

Definition and Biology

Definition and Origin

Tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) are a heterogeneous population of immune cells, predominantly lymphocytes, that migrate from the peripheral blood into tumor tissues, where they reside and interact with malignant cells. These cells represent the adaptive immune system's response to cancer, primarily consisting of T cells and B cells that recognize tumor-specific antigens, though they may also include innate immune components such as natural killer (NK) cells. Unlike innate immune cells that provide immediate, non-specific defense, TILs from the adaptive arm undergo antigen-specific activation, enabling targeted cytotoxicity against tumors.
TILs originate from hematopoietic stem cells in the bone marrow, which differentiate into common lymphoid progenitors that mature into nave lymphocytes in the thymus or bone marrow before entering circulation. Upon encountering tumor antigens presented in tumor-draining lymph nodes, these nave cells become activated effector lymphocytes and are recruited to the tumor site through a multistep process involving chemokines and adhesion molecules. Key chemokines such as CXCL9 and CXCL10, secreted by tumor cells or stromal components, bind to the CXCR3 receptor on TILs, creating chemotactic gradients that direct their migration from peripheral blood into the tumor microenvironment (TME). Adhesion molecules further facilitate this recruitment: integrins like LFA-1 (lymphocyte function-associated antigen 1) and VLA-4 (very late antigen 4) on TILs interact with endothelial ligands such as ICAM-1 (intercellular adhesion molecule 1) and VCAM-1 (vascular cell adhesion molecule 1) to enable firm adhesion and extravasation across the vascular wall.
The tumor vasculature and extracellular matrix (ECM) play critical roles in modulating TIL infiltration, acting as both facilitators and barriers. Abnormal tumor blood vessels, characterized by irregular structure and leakiness, can enhance initial access but often express low levels of adhesion molecules, hindering efficient extravasation. The dense ECM, composed of collagen and proteoglycans, poses a physical obstacle, compressing vessels and limiting TIL penetration into tumor nests, thereby influencing the density and distribution of TILs within solid tumors.
TILs are distinguished from other tumor-associated immune cells by their specific localization within the tumor parenchyma, as opposed to circulating lymphocytes in the bloodstream or immune cells confined to the peritumoral stroma. While stromal immune cells contribute to the broader TME, TILs directly infiltrate the tumor tissue, enabling intimate contact with cancer cells and distinguishing them as key effectors of intratumoral immunity.

Cellular Composition and Function

Tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) comprise a heterogeneous population of immune cells that infiltrate solid tumors, primarily consisting of T lymphocytes, B cells, and natural killer (NK) cells. The main effectors are CD8+ cytotoxic T cells, which recognize tumor antigens presented by major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class I molecules via their T cell receptors (TCRs), leading to direct cytotoxicity through perforin and granzyme release. CD4+ helper T cells support this process by secreting cytokines such as interleukin-2 (IL-2) to amplify CD8+ responses and promote Th1 polarization. In contrast, regulatory T cells (Tregs), characterized by FOXP3 expression, suppress antitumor immunity by releasing transforming growth factor- (TGF-) and IL-10, thereby maintaining immune homeostasis but often hindering effective tumor control. B cells contribute through antibody production and antigen presentation, while NK cells provide innate cytotoxicity independent of MHC, targeting stressed tumor cells via activating receptors.
The functional roles of TILs center on antigen-specific recognition and effector mechanisms that induce tumor cell apoptosis. Upon TCR-MHC engagement, activated CD8+ and CD4+ T cells, along with NK cells, release pro-inflammatory cytokines like interferon- (IFN-) and tumor necrosis factor- (TNF-), which upregulate MHC expression on tumor cells and trigger Fas/FasL-mediated apoptosis. However, prolonged exposure to the tumor microenvironment often leads to TIL dysfunction, marked by upregulation of exhaustion markers such as programmed death-1 (PD-1) and T-cell immunoglobulin and mucin-domain containing-3 (TIM-3), which inhibit cytokine production and proliferative capacity. This exhaustion impairs the lymphocytes' ability to sustain antitumor responses, allowing tumor persistence despite initial infiltration.
TILs exhibit significant heterogeneity, distinguishing tumor-specific reactive cells from bystander populations that do not directly target the malignancy. Tumor-specific TILs, often enriched in high-mutation-burden tumors, recognize neoantigensmutated peptides unique to cancer cellsenabling precise antitumor activity; for instance, neoantigen-reactive CD8+ TILs display distinct transcriptomic signatures of expansion and dysfunction compared to bystanders derived from blood or non-tumor antigens. Bystander TILs, comprising viral- or self-antigen-specific clones, may infiltrate tumors but contribute minimally to cytotoxicity, highlighting the need to identify and expand reactive subsets for therapeutic efficacy. This variability influences the overall immune landscape within tumors.
A key concept in TIL biology is their involvement in cancer immunoediting, a dynamic process where immune cells shape tumor evolution across three phases: elimination, equilibrium, and escape. In the elimination phase, TILs such as CD8+ T cells and NK cells eradicate nascent tumor cells through cytotoxicity and IFN--mediated surveillance. During equilibrium, persistent TIL pressure selects for less immunogenic tumor variants, maintaining dormancy over extended periods. Ultimately, in the escape phase, tumors evade TIL control via mechanisms like antigen loss, immunosuppressive factors, or TIL exhaustion, leading to clinical progression. This immunoediting underscores the dual role of TILs in both tumor suppression and adaptation.

Detection and Analysis

Methods of Detection

Histological examination remains the cornerstone for detecting tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) in tumor samples, primarily through hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining and immunohistochemistry (IHC). H&E staining allows for the visual assessment of TIL density by identifying mononuclear cells within the tumor stroma and intraepithelial regions, typically evaluated at 200400x magnification on full sections or representative cores, while excluding necrotic areas, crush artifacts, and biopsy site reactions to ensure accuracy. IHC enhances specificity by targeting immune markers such as CD3 for total T cells and CD8 for cytotoxic T cells, using antibody-based staining on formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded tissues to quantify lymphocyte subsets and their spatial distribution.
Advanced techniques provide deeper insights into TIL composition and function beyond basic histology. Multiplex IHC or immunofluorescence (IF) enables simultaneous detection of multiple markers, such as CD3, CD8, and CD103 for activated T cells, on a single tissue section, facilitating the analysis of heterogeneous immune infiltrates while preserving spatial context. Flow cytometry, applied to enzymatically dissociated tumor samples, offers high-resolution phenotyping of viable TILs by measuring surface and intracellular markers in suspension, though it may alter cell representation due to dissociation artifacts. RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) of bulk or single-cell tumor samples infers TIL presence through gene expression signatures, including T cell receptor (TCR) sequencing to assess clonal diversity and antigen specificity, providing quantitative estimates of immune infiltration without direct visualization.
In vivo imaging techniques, such as multiphoton microscopy, enable real-time observation of TIL dynamics within living tumors by exploiting two-photon excitation for deep-tissue penetration and reduced phototoxicity, often tracking labeled CD8+ T cells and their interactions with other immune components in animal models. Complementing these, AI-based digital pathology analyzes digitized H&E or IHC slides using deep learning models, such as U-Net for stroma segmentation followed by Mask R-CNN for TIL detection, achieving high concordance (up to 95%) with pathologist scores and enabling automated, reproducible quantification across large cohorts. Recent advances include pan-cancer tools like TILScout, a deep-learning model for patch-level TIL scoring from whole slide images, improving scalability as of 2025.
Standardization is critical for reliable TIL detection, as outlined by the International Immuno-Oncology Biomarkers Working Group, which recommends reporting TILs as a continuous percentage of stromal (mononuclear cells in tumor-adjacent stroma) versus intraepithelial (within tumor nests) compartments on H&E slides, prioritizing average infiltration over hotspots and advocating digital tools for inter-observer consistency (intraclass correlation coefficient ~0.89 in breast cancer). These guidelines, derived from consensus efforts like the 2014 International TILs Working Group meeting, emphasize full-face sections for evaluation within the invasive tumor border and 1 mm margin, supporting cross-study comparability without predefined clinical thresholds.

Prognostic and Predictive Value

The presence of tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) serves as a significant prognostic indicator in various solid tumors, with high TIL density consistently associated with improved overall survival (OS). In melanoma, meta-analyses have demonstrated that elevated levels of CD3+, CD4+, CD8+, FOXP3+, and CD20+ TILs correlate with favorable OS outcomes, with hazard ratios (HRs) indicating reduced mortality risk (HR < 1). Similar prognostic benefits are observed in breast cancer, particularly triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC), where high TIL infiltration predicts better disease-free survival (DFS) and OS, supported by pooled HRs of approximately 0.65 for OS in meta-analyses focusing on CD8+ TILs. In colorectal cancer, generalized TIL density has been identified as a strong positive prognostic marker for survival, with meta-analytic evidence showing HRs below 1 across multiple studies. These associations underscore the role of TILs in reflecting an active antitumor immune response that enhances patient outcomes.
Beyond prognosis, TILs exhibit predictive value as biomarkers for response to immunotherapy, particularly immune checkpoint inhibitors. In non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), high TIL levels, including specific subsets like PD-1-expressing TILs, predict durable clinical benefit from PD-1 blockade, with patients showing increased progression-free survival (e.g., median 7.0 months vs. 1.9 months in low-TIL groups). Combining TIL assessment with PD-L1 expression further strengthens predictive accuracy for progression-free survival and OS in NSCLC treated with PD-1 inhibitors. This predictive utility highlights TILs' potential to guide personalized treatment decisions in immunogenic tumors.
Subtype-specific TIL compositions further refine prognostic and predictive insights, with cytotoxic CD8+ TILs generally linked to positive outcomes across cancer types. High CD8+ TIL density is associated with improved OS and DFS in breast, colorectal, and other cancers, as evidenced by meta-analyses reporting favorable HRs irrespective of tumor location. In contrast, elevated regulatory T cells (Tregs, often marked by FOXP3+) are correlated with poorer prognosis, exerting immunosuppressive effects that worsen survival in pancreatic, breast, and other malignancies, with meta-analyses indicating increased mortality risk (HR > 1).
Despite these findings, evidence on TILs' prognostic and predictive value is limited by inter-tumor variability and inconsistencies in assessment methods. Prognostic associations differ markedly across cancer types, with stronger effects in immunogenic tumors like melanoma and NSCLC compared to others like pancreatic cancer, complicating broad clinical application. Standardized scoring systems are essential to address this, such as those proposed for lung cancer akin to the International Association for the Study of Lung Cancer (IASLC) frameworks, which aim to reduce subjectivity in TIL quantification and enhance reproducibility.

Therapeutic Applications

Autologous TIL Therapy

Autologous tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte (TIL) therapy is a form of adoptive cell therapy that utilizes a patient's own TILs to combat cancer. The process begins with the surgical resection of a tumor, from which TILs are extracted through enzymatic digestion or mechanical dissociation of tumor fragments. These TILs are then expanded ex vivo in the laboratory using interleukin-2 (IL-2) and other growth factors to generate billions of tumor-reactive T cells, typically over a 3- to 4-week period. Following expansion, the patient undergoes lymphodepleting chemotherapy, such as cyclophosphamide and fludarabine, to create an immunosuppressive environment that enhances TIL engraftment and persistence. The expanded TILs are subsequently reinfused intravenously, often accompanied by high-dose IL-2 to further support their anti-tumor activity.
This therapy is primarily indicated for patients with advanced solid tumors, particularly unresectable or metastatic melanoma that has progressed after prior treatments like PD-1 inhibitors. In February 2024, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) granted accelerated approval to lifileucel (Amtagvi), the first autologous TIL product, for adult patients with unresectable or metastatic melanoma previously treated with a PD-1 blocking antibody and, if applicable, BRAF inhibitors with or without MEK inhibitors. While initial applications focused on melanoma due to its immunogenicity and TIL accessibility, ongoing investigations explore its use in other solid tumors such as cervical cancer and non-small cell lung cancer, with promising 2025 trial data including objective response rates in NSCLC and improved expansion methods for gynecologic malignancies like cervical cancer.
The therapeutic mechanism relies on the inherent tumor specificity of TILs, which are enriched for T cells recognizing patient-specific neoantigensmutated proteins unique to the tumor that arise from genomic alterations. During ex vivo expansion, these TILs maintain or enhance their cytotoxic potential, allowing them to traffic back to the tumor microenvironment upon reinfusion and mount a robust immune response against cancer cells expressing shared or private neoantigens. This approach leverages the polyclonal nature of TILs, enabling broad targeting of multiple tumor antigens without the need for prior antigen identification.
A key unique aspect of autologous TIL therapy is its fully personalized, non-engineered profile: it harnesses naturally occurring TILs directly from the patient's tumor without requiring genetic modification, distinguishing it from chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapies that involve ex vivo transduction to express synthetic receptors. This patient-specific derivation ensures TILs are already primed against the individual's tumor neoantigens, potentially reducing off-target effects while accommodating tumor heterogeneity.

Integration with Adoptive T Cell Transfer

Tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) are integrated into adoptive T cell transfer protocols through foundational principles that optimize their engraftment and function, such as lymphodepleting chemotherapy, which depletes endogenous lymphocytes to create a cytokine-rich environment that promotes TIL expansion and persistence post-infusion. This preconditioning, typically involving cyclophosphamide and fludarabine, enhances the homeostatic proliferation of transferred TILs by reducing competition for cytokines like interleukin-7 and interleukin-15, thereby amplifying antitumor responses in solid tumors.
Synergies between TILs and other adoptive T cell modalities, such as chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-T or T cell receptor (TCR)-T therapies, leverage the strengths of each to improve persistence and tumor infiltration, particularly in challenging solid tumor microenvironments. For example, TIL-derived CAR-T cells exhibit superior tumor infiltration and reduced exhaustion markers compared to peripheral blood-derived CAR-T cells, leading to enhanced survival in preclinical models of colorectal cancer. Similarly, combining TILs with TCR-T approaches can enrich for neoantigen-specific T cells, boosting specificity against intracellular tumor antigens that CAR-T may overlook. Bispecific antibodies further support TIL recruitment by simultaneously binding tumor-associated antigens and CD3 on T cells, facilitating TIL redirection and activation within the tumor site independently of MHC restriction.
Modifications to TILs during ex vivo expansion address key limitations like exhaustion, with PD-1 blockade enriching for tumor-reactive CD8+ T cells and improving their effector function upon adoptive transfer. In experimental settings, CRISPR-Cas9 editing enables precise genetic alterations, such as PD-1 knockout, to render TILs resistant to checkpoint inhibition and enhance their persistence and cytotoxicity against solid tumors. These edited TILs have shown feasibility in clinical manufacturing for gastrointestinal cancers, underscoring their potential to overcome immunosuppressive barriers.
In contrast to pure autologous TIL therapy, which relies on polyclonal, tumor-resident T cells primarily for solid tumors, engineered TIL variants introduce targeted specificity akin to CAR-T or TCR-T, potentially broadening applicability while facing unique hurdles in hematologic malignancies where TIL infiltration is limited by the bone marrow niche. Such integrations emphasize TILs' role as a versatile platform in adoptive therapies, prioritizing modifications that align with tumor-specific challenges over unselected expansions.

Historical Development

The concept of tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) emerged in the 1980s through pioneering work at the National Cancer Institute (NCI), where Steven A. Rosenberg and colleagues identified TILs within human tumors and demonstrated their potential for adoptive immunotherapy. In a seminal 1986 study, Rosenberg's team isolated TILs from melanoma tumors, expanded them ex vivo using interleukin-2 (IL-2), and showed their superior antitumor activity compared to lymphokine-activated killer cells in mouse models, marking the initial in vitro expansion demonstrations that laid the foundation for clinical translation. This observational pathology from tumor samples in the early 1980s highlighted TILs' presence and prognostic relevance in cancers like melanoma, shifting focus from mere histological observation to therapeutic exploitation.
Rosenberg played a central role in advancing adoptive immunotherapy, leading the transition from preclinical experiments to human applications by developing protocols for TIL isolation and reinfusion. The first clinical trials began in 1988, when Rosenberg and colleagues treated 20 patients with metastatic melanoma using autologous TILs expanded in vitro and administered with IL-2, achieving objective tumor regressions in some cases and establishing proof-of-concept for TIL therapy in humans. These early efforts at the NCI Surgery Branch pioneered the field, with Rosenberg's ongoing contributions emphasizing TILs' specificity for tumor antigens over non-specific immune cells.
During the 2010s, advancements in expansion protocols enhanced TIL therapy's feasibility and efficacy, evolving it toward standardized clinical use. A key development was the "young TIL" protocol introduced by Mark E. Dudley and colleagues in 2010, which simplified production by pooling lymphocytes from tumor fragments without individual clone selection, yielding CD8+-enriched populations with robust antitumor activity and reducing manufacturing complexity. This built on earlier rapid expansion methods, improving yield and applicability across more patients. The culmination came in 2024 with the FDA approval of lifileucel (Amtagvi), the first commercial TIL product for advanced melanoma, reflecting decades of refinement from experimental pathology to approved, standardized therapy.

Manufacturing and Administration Process

The manufacturing process for tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) begins with surgical resection of a patient's tumor, typically requiring 1.54 cm of accessible lesion to ensure sufficient TIL yield. The resected tumor is dissected under sterile conditions into 13 mm fragments or subjected to enzymatic digestion using collagenase to liberate TILs, followed by placement in culture media supplemented with interleukin-2 (IL-2) at 30006000 IU/mL to initiate selective outgrowth of T cells. This initial isolation phase, often termed pre-rapid expansion protocol (pre-REP), is conducted in good manufacturing practice (GMP)-compliant facilities and typically lasts 35 weeks, during which fragments are monitored, fed, and split every 34 days to reach approximately 60 10^6 TILs.
Following pre-REP, TILs undergo a rapid expansion protocol (REP) to generate the therapeutic dose of 10^910^11 cells, pioneered at the National Cancer Institute and now standardized across centers. In REP, pooled TILs are stimulated with anti-CD3 antibody (30 ng/mL) and high-dose IL-2 (3000 IU/mL), co-cultured with irradiated allogeneic peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) as feeder cells at a 1:200 ratio, often in gas-permeable flasks like G-Rex for scalability. This 1114 day phase achieves 1000- to 3000-fold expansion while maintaining tumor reactivity. Quality control during and post-expansion includes potency assays such as interferon- (IFN-) release (>200 pg/mL upon tumor co-culture), flow cytometry for CD3+ identity (>90%), viability (>70%), sterility, and endotoxin testing (<5 EU/kg), ensuring GMP compliance and release criteria. Expanded TILs are then cryopreserved in vapor-phase liquid nitrogen for storage.
Administration of TIL therapy follows a lymphodepleting regimen to enhance engraftment by depleting endogenous lymphocytes and regulatory T cells. Patients receive non-myeloablative chemotherapy with cyclophosphamide (60 mg/kg intravenously for 2 days) followed by fludarabine (25 mg/m intravenously for 5 days), starting 57 days before infusion. Approximately 24 hours after completing lymphodepletion, cryopreserved TILs are thawed and infused intravenously over 2030 minutes, targeting a total dose of 7.5 10^9 to 72 10^9 viable cells (as for approved lifileucel; general TIL doses range from 10^9 to 10^11 total cells). Post-infusion, patients receive high-dose IL-2 (600,000 IU/kg intravenously every 812 hours, up to 6 doses over 25 days) to support TIL proliferation and persistence.
The overall logistics demand centralized or on-site GMP facilities equipped for closed-system processing to minimize contamination risks, with manufacturing success rates of 90%98.8%. From tumor resection to product release, the timeline spans 2260 days, influenced by initial TIL growth kinetics and facility throughput, requiring patients to undergo surgery within 2 weeks of eligibility screening and remain near the treatment center for at least 30 days post-infusion for monitoring.

Clinical Trials and Efficacy

Clinical trials of tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte (TIL) therapy have demonstrated promising efficacy, particularly in advanced melanoma, with objective response rates (ORR) ranging from 30% to 50% in heavily pretreated patients. Early pivotal studies at the National Cancer Institute (NCI), including protocol NCI-9785, reported an ORR of approximately 50% in patients with metastatic melanoma, highlighting the potential for durable tumor regression following TIL infusion after lymphodepleting chemotherapy and interleukin-2 administration. These responses were observed even in patients refractory to prior therapies, establishing TIL as a viable option for advanced disease.
The registrational phase 2 C-144-01 trial evaluated lifileucel, an autologous TIL product, in 153 patients with unresectable or metastatic melanoma previously treated with anti-PD-1 therapy and, if BRAF V600 mutation-positive, targeted therapy. In this cohort, lifileucel achieved an investigator-assessed ORR of 36% (95% CI, 28.8-43.8), including 4% complete responses, with a median duration of response of 15.9 months among responders. Efficacy metrics from such trials underscore TIL's impact, with median progression-free survival (PFS) reaching 7.2 months compared to 3.1 months with ipilimumab in similar refractory populations, and durable responses observed in 20-30% of patients, some lasting beyond 5 years.
In February 2024, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved lifileucel (Amtagvi) as the first TIL therapy for adult patients with unresectable or metastatic melanoma previously treated with a PD-1 blocking antibody and, if applicable, targeted therapy. This approval was based on the C-144-01 results, marking a milestone for personalized adoptive cell therapy. Ongoing trials are expanding TIL applications to other solid tumors, including cervical and head/neck cancers; for instance, NCT01174121 investigates TIL combined with pembrolizumab in metastatic gastrointestinal cancers with HPV association, showing feasibility and preliminary antitumor activity. As of 2025, notable advancements include an April 2025 NCI phase 2 trial of neoantigen-specific TIL plus pembrolizumab in 91 patients with refractory metastatic GI cancers (primarily mismatch repair-deficient), achieving an ORR of approximately 24%; November 2025 interim data from Iovance for lifileucel in advanced NSCLC demonstrating promising efficacy in post-PD-1 refractory patients; and ASCO 2025 results from the GT101 trial in recurrent/metastatic cervical cancer, with early signs of activity. These updates, summarized at ASCO 2025, highlight TIL monotherapies and combinations for broader solid tumor applications.
Comparative data indicate TIL therapy's superiority over checkpoint inhibitors in refractory cases, with TIL reducing the risk of progression or death by 50% relative to ipilimumab in anti-PD-1-refractory melanoma, offering responses where immune checkpoint blockade fails. These outcomes position TIL as a complementary approach in the post-checkpoint inhibitor landscape, with ongoing studies exploring combinations to enhance efficacy across indications.

Challenges and Advances

Current Limitations

Despite promising clinical outcomes in select cancers, tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte (TIL) therapy faces significant biological hurdles that limit its efficacy. TILs often exhibit exhaustion, a state induced by chronic antigen exposure within the tumor microenvironment, characterized by upregulation of inhibitory receptors such as PD-1, LAG-3, and TIM-3, leading to diminished proliferation, cytokine production, and cytotoxic function. Senescence represents another barrier, where repeated stimulation causes replicative arrest, marked by loss of CD27/CD28 expression and secretion of immunosuppressive senescence-associated secretory phenotype factors like IL-6 and TGF-, further impairing TIL persistence and antitumor activity. Additionally, "cold" tumors with low immune infiltration pose a major challenge, as these exhibit sparse TIL presence due to factors such as low tumor mutational burden, MHC class I downregulation, and dense stromal barriers, resulting in insufficient TIL yield for effective therapy.
Technical limitations in TIL manufacturing further hinder scalability and reliability. The process is labor-intensive, requiring approximately 3 weeks of ex vivo expansion involving multiple manual steps like tumor digestion, culture monitoring, and rapid expansion protocols, which increase contamination risks and demand specialized facilities. Variability in expansion success is common, with improved methods achieving success rates of 80-90% in recent processes due to factors like tumor heterogeneity, necrotic tissue content, and donor serum inconsistencies, often failing to reach the minimum therapeutic threshold of 7.5 10^9 viable cells required for infusion in some cases.
Practical barriers restrict TIL therapy to a subset of patients. The high cost, exceeding $500,000 per treatment primarily due to complex manufacturing and hospitalization needs, limits accessibility and raises economic concerns for broader adoption. Moreover, the requirement for surgical tumor resectionat least 1.5 cm in diameterto procure sufficient TILs confines therapy to operable cases, excluding patients with unresectable or disseminated disease.
Safety concerns, particularly from supportive agents, add to the challenges. High-dose interleukin-2 (IL-2) administration post-infusion, essential for TIL expansion in vivo, frequently induces toxicities associated with capillary leak syndrome, such as hypotension and pulmonary edema, with any-grade capillary leak occurring in about 30% of patients and severe events in 8-10% necessitating intensive care monitoring. Off-target effects, such as autoimmune-like reactions from bystander T-cell activation against non-tumor antigens, have been reported, though rare, contributing to potential long-term immune dysregulation.

Emerging Therapies and Research Directions

Recent innovations in TIL therapy focus on enhancing specificity and accessibility through advanced selection techniques and alternative cell sources. Neoantigen-specific TIL selection utilizes single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) to identify and expand tumor-reactive T cells targeting patient-specific neoantigens, enabling more potent autologous therapies by isolating clones with stem-like memory phenotypes that exhibit improved persistence and antitumor activity. The 2024 FDA approval of lifileucel (Amtagvi) for advanced melanoma has improved accessibility, with expanded phase III trials in other solid tumors ongoing as of 2025. Efforts to develop allogeneic T cell therapies, such as engineered donor T cells expressing tumor-specific receptors, aim to create off-the-shelf products, circumventing the need for patient-specific tumor resection and reducing manufacturing time while maintaining graft-versus-tumor effects.
Combination strategies are emerging to overcome barriers to TIL infiltration and efficacy. Pairing TIL therapy with oncolytic viruses, such as engineered adenoviruses, promotes tumor lysis and chemokine release to recruit TILs into previously "cold" tumors, enhancing local immune activation and response rates in preclinical models of solid tumors. Integration with therapeutic vaccines, including neoantigen-targeted formulations, boosts TIL priming by expanding antigen-specific precursors in vivo prior to adoptive transfer, thereby increasing infiltration and durability in immunogenic cancers like melanoma. Additionally, artificial intelligence models, such as deep learning algorithms analyzing whole-slide images, predict TIL therapy responders by quantifying pretreatment TIL density and spatial distribution, aiding patient selection with up to 80% accuracy in correlating high TIL scores with improved progression-free survival.
Post-2023 advances highlight expanded applications and genetic enhancements. Phase II trials in HPV-associated cancers, including cervical malignancies, have demonstrated objective response rates of 30-44% with durable complete responses in select patients, underscoring TIL's potential beyond melanoma when combined with lymphodepletion. CRISPR/Cas9-mediated PD-1 knockout in TILs has shown preclinical efficacy in solid tumors, with edited cells exhibiting enhanced cytotoxicity and reduced exhaustion in patient-derived xenografts, and phase I trials ongoing in 2024-2025.
Looking ahead, scalable off-the-shelf T cell products from allogeneic sources, including induced pluripotent stem cell-derived T cells, promise broader accessibility by enabling immediate administration without personalization delays. Furthermore, integrating mRNA vaccines for TIL primingsuch as those encoding HPV E6/E7 antigenscould amplify neoantigen-specific responses prior to infusion, fostering synergistic effects in epithelial cancers and potentially transforming TIL into a versatile platform therapy.
