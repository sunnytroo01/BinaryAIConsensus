The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite and the fifth largest moon in the solar system. It orbits Earth at an average distance of approximately two hundred and thirty-nine thousand miles, close enough that it is the only celestial body that human beings have visited in person. The Moon has shaped life on Earth in ways both obvious and profound: it stabilizes Earth's axial tilt, which moderates the climate; it creates ocean tides through its gravitational pull; it slows Earth's rotation, lengthening our days; and it has served as a source of wonder, inspiration, and navigation for every human culture throughout history.

The Moon formed approximately 4.5 billion years ago, shortly after the formation of Earth itself. The leading theory of its origin, called the Giant Impact Hypothesis, proposes that a Mars-sized body, informally named Theia, collided with the young Earth in a catastrophic impact that melted much of Earth's surface and ejected a massive cloud of debris into orbit. This debris gradually coalesced under its own gravity to form the Moon. Evidence supporting this theory includes the Moon's relatively small iron core compared to its size, its lower density compared to Earth, the similar isotopic compositions of Earth and Moon rocks suggesting they share a common origin, and the high angular momentum of the Earth-Moon system.

The Moon is a rocky, airless world with a diameter of about twenty-one hundred miles, roughly one quarter of Earth's diameter. Its mass is about eighty-one times less than Earth's, giving it a surface gravity only about one sixth as strong. An astronaut who weighs one hundred and eighty pounds on Earth weighs only thirty pounds on the Moon, which is why the Apollo astronauts were able to bounce along the lunar surface in their heavy spacesuits with apparent ease.

The Moon has no atmosphere, no liquid water, and no magnetic field. Without an atmosphere to moderate temperature, the lunar surface experiences extreme temperature swings: up to two hundred and sixty degrees Fahrenheit in direct sunlight and minus two hundred and eighty degrees in shadow. Without a magnetic field, the surface is directly exposed to solar radiation and cosmic rays, making it hostile to unprotected life.

The Moon's surface is divided into two distinct types of terrain. The dark, relatively smooth areas are called maria, the Latin word for seas, because early astronomers thought they might be bodies of water. They are actually vast plains of basaltic lava that flooded ancient impact craters billions of years ago. The lighter, heavily cratered areas are called highlands or terrae, and they represent the Moon's original crust, formed from minerals that floated to the top of the magma ocean that covered the Moon shortly after its formation.

The Moon is covered in craters of every size, from microscopic pits to enormous basins hundreds of miles in diameter. These craters are the scars of asteroid and comet impacts accumulated over billions of years. On Earth, weathering, erosion, plate tectonics, and volcanic activity erase impact craters over time. The Moon has none of these processes, so its surface is a nearly perfect record of the bombardment history of the inner solar system. The South Pole-Aitken Basin, on the far side of the Moon, is one of the largest and oldest impact structures in the solar system, approximately fifteen hundred miles in diameter and five miles deep.

The Moon's surface is covered in a layer of fine, gray, powdery debris called regolith, created over billions of years by the grinding action of micrometeorite impacts. The regolith is typically three to sixty feet deep and contains fragments of rock, glass beads formed by the heat of impacts, and traces of solar wind particles embedded in the surface. Lunar regolith is extremely abrasive because there is no wind or water to smooth its edges. The sharp, angular dust particles caused significant problems for the Apollo astronauts, abrading their spacesuits, clouding their helmet visors, and irritating their lungs when tracked into the lunar module.

The Moon always shows the same face to Earth, a phenomenon called tidal locking. This occurs because the Moon's rotational period, the time it takes to spin once on its axis, is exactly equal to its orbital period, the time it takes to orbit Earth once. Both are approximately twenty-seven point three days. This means that while the near side of the Moon is familiar to every human being who has ever looked up at the night sky, the far side was completely unknown until the Soviet Luna 3 spacecraft photographed it in 1959. Contrary to popular usage, there is no permanent "dark side" of the Moon. Both sides receive equal amounts of sunlight. The far side is simply the side that faces away from Earth.

The Apollo program, which landed twelve human beings on the Moon between 1969 and 1972, remains one of the greatest achievements in the history of exploration. Apollo 11, on July 20, 1969, carried Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin to the lunar surface while Michael Collins orbited overhead. Armstrong's first step onto the Moon was watched by an estimated six hundred million people worldwide, the largest television audience in history at that time. His words, "That is one small step for man, one giant leap for mankind," became one of the most famous sentences ever spoken.

The Apollo missions brought back eight hundred and forty-two pounds of lunar rocks and soil samples, which have been studied continuously for over fifty years and have provided most of what we know about the Moon's composition, age, and geological history. Analysis of these samples confirmed that the Moon and Earth share a common origin, that the Moon's surface is ancient, with the youngest mare basalts dating to approximately three billion years ago, and that the Moon was once geologically active but has been essentially dormant for billions of years.

Recent discoveries have transformed our understanding of the Moon. Water ice has been detected in permanently shadowed craters near the lunar poles, where temperatures are so low that ice can persist indefinitely. This water, deposited by comet impacts over billions of years, could potentially be extracted and used for drinking water, oxygen generation, and rocket fuel by future lunar settlers, making the Moon's poles the most valuable real estate beyond Earth.

The Moon's influence on Earth extends far beyond its gravitational effects on the tides. The Moon stabilizes Earth's obliquity, the tilt of its rotational axis relative to its orbital plane, at approximately twenty-three point five degrees. Without the Moon, Earth's obliquity would vary chaotically over millions of years, producing extreme and unpredictable shifts in climate that might have prevented the development of complex life. Mars, which has no large moon, has experienced obliquity variations of up to sixty degrees, which may have contributed to the loss of its atmosphere and surface water.

The Moon is returning to the center of human space exploration. NASA's Artemis program plans to return astronauts to the lunar surface and establish a sustained human presence there, including a lunar space station called Gateway and a permanent base at the south pole. China has landed robotic spacecraft on the far side of the Moon and plans to establish its own lunar research station. Private companies are developing lunar landers and resource extraction technologies. The Moon, which seemed like a destination we had visited and left behind, is becoming a destination we are preparing to inhabit.

The Moon is more than a celestial body. It is a companion. It has lit our nights, measured our months, inspired our poetry, guided our sailors, puzzled our scientists, and received our footprints. It is the closest thing to another world that we can see with our naked eyes, hanging in the sky like a promise: you can reach beyond your home planet. You can walk on another world. You have done it before, and you will do it again.
