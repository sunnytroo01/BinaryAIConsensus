Armed Forces Reserve Medal
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Armed Forces Reserve Medal (AFRM) is a United States military decoration authorized by Executive Order 10163 on September 25, 1950, to recognize ten years of honorable and satisfactory service in the reserve components of the Armed Forces. Awarded to both officers and enlisted personnel, it honors cumulative qualifying reserve duty, including periods of active duty for training, annual training, and inactive duty points, provided the service meets standards of satisfactory performance and conduct. The medal's obverse bears a bronze 1-inch design featuring a flaming torch centered before a crossed powder horn and bugle, surrounded by a circle of thirteen stars and a laurel wreath, symbolizing readiness and historical militia traditions.
Subsequent ten-year increments of qualifying service are denoted by hourglass devices on the ribbon: a bronze hourglass for twenty total years, silver for thirty, gold for forty, and additional service beyond that indicated by numerals. In recognition of reserve mobilizations supporting contingency operations, an "M" device was authorized for wear on the ribbon, awarded to those involuntarily activated or voluntarily assigned in direct support of designated operations, regardless of overall reserve tenure. Approved operations for the "M" device are listed by the Department of Defense, encompassing post-1990 mobilizations such as those for Operations Desert Shield, Enduring Freedom, and others. The AFRM underscores the vital role of reservists in supplementing active forces, with eligibility extending to former members and Retired Reserve personnel meeting criteria.

History

Establishment and Initial Criteria

The Armed Forces Reserve Medal was established by Executive Order 10163, signed by President Harry S. Truman on September 25, 1950. This executive order created the medal and its accompanying appurtenances and devices to recognize honorable service specifically in the reserve components of the United States Armed Forces.
Initial eligibility required ten years of honorable service as a member of any reserve component, including the Coast Guard Reserve and Marine Corps Reserve, with the ten years completed within twelve consecutive years. Qualifying service encompassed participation in annual active-duty training and inactive-duty training as prescribed by the regulations of the respective military departments. Periods of service in the regular components of the Armed Forces, or those qualifying for the Naval Reserve Medal or Marine Corps Reserve Medal, were explicitly excluded.
The medal was limited to one award per individual, regardless of the number of reserve components served in; additional ten-year increments of qualifying service were recognized by attaching appropriate devices to the medal. Members eligible for both the Armed Forces Reserve Medal and the service-specific Naval Reserve Medal or Marine Corps Reserve Medal could elect to receive the Armed Forces Reserve Medal in lieu of the others.

Post-Cold War Amendments and Mobilization Recognition

Following the end of the Cold War in 1991, the criteria for the Armed Forces Reserve Medal underwent significant revision through Executive Order 13013, signed by President Bill Clinton on August 6, 1996. This amendment expanded eligibility beyond the original 10-year cumulative reserve service requirement established in 1950, authorizing the medal's award to reservists mobilized to active duty under a presidential selected reserve call-up or by order of the Secretary of Defense for contingency operations. The change reflected the increased reliance on reserve components for post-Cold War operations, such as those in the Balkans and initial responses to emerging global threats, distinguishing mobilization service from routine drilling.
The 1996 executive order introduced the bronze "M" device as a specific emblem for mobilization recognition, to be worn on the medal or suspension ribbon, with additional "M" numerals for each subsequent qualifying mobilization under unrelated call-ups. Qualifying mobilizations required at least 30 consecutive days of active duty, excluding annual training or active duty for training, and applied prospectively from August 1, 1996, excluding prior operations like the Gulf War despite reserve involvement therein. This device provided formal acknowledgment of involuntary or voluntary mobilizations for designated contingencies, such as Operations Joint Endeavor and Deny Flight, without crediting the time toward the standard 10-year service accumulation for the base medal.
Concurrently, Executive Order 13013 formalized the hourglass devicesbronze for the initial 10 years, silver for 20 years, and gold for 30 yearsto denote cumulative qualifying reserve service, replacing prior informal notations and ensuring longevity of service was visually represented on subsequent awards of the medal. These provisions emphasized peacetime readiness while carving out mobilization as a distinct category, limited to one base medal per individual but allowing multiple "M" devices; mobilization periods did not count toward hourglass increments unless separately qualifying as reserve service. Department of Defense Instruction 1348.33, updated post-1996, implemented these rules, designating specific operations for "M" eligibility and barring the device for voluntary active duty without mobilization orders.

Design and Symbolism

Medal Obverse and Reverse

The obverse of the Armed Forces Reserve Medal is a bronze disc measuring 1.25 inches (32 mm) in diameter, featuring a central design of a flaming torch positioned in front of a crossed powder horn and bugle, emblematic of readiness and vigilance in reserve service. This motif is encircled by thirteen stars and thirteen rays, representing the original thirteen colonies of the United States. The design is identical across all branches and was created by Thomas Hudson Jones of the U.S. Army Institute of Heraldry.
The reverse bears the inscription "ARMED FORCES RESERVE" arched around the upper rim, with the central design varying by reserve component to reflect branch-specific heraldry:

Army Reserve (Organized Reserve): A statue of the Lexington Minuteman atop a wreath, surrounded by thirteen stars.

Army National Guard: An eagle with reversed wings bearing two crossed fasces.

Air Force Reserve: An eagle displayed before a cloud formation on a wreath.

Navy Reserve: An eagle perched on an anchor in front of a sailing ship, derived from the Navy seal.

Marine Corps Reserve: An eagle atop a globe superimposed on an anchor.

Coast Guard Reserve: Crossed anchors enclosing a shield within an annulet.

These reverse designs incorporate traditional emblems of each service's reserve forces while maintaining the common inscription to denote the medal's unified purpose.

Ribbon and Wear Specifications

The ribbon of the Armed Forces Reserve Medal measures 1 3/8 inches in width and features a chamois (tan) background bisected by a 1/8-inch ultramarine blue center stripe, flanked on each side by 3/8-inch chamois sections. Symmetrically arranged bluebird stripes (1/16 inch each) and thin chamois pinstripes (1/32 inch) border the edges, creating a pattern of three narrow blue lines per side separated by chamois.
Devices are worn on both the suspension ribbon of the medal and the service ribbon bar. The hourglass device, indicating cumulative reserve service periods, is positioned as follows: a single bronze hourglass centered for 10 years; silver for 20 years; gold for 30 years; and a combination of bronze and gold for 40 years. The "M" device, denoting mobilization service, is centered on the ribbon when awarded alone or in combination with other devices. 
When multiple devices are authorized, placement prioritizes the wearer's perspective: hourglass(es) to the right, "M" device in the center, and any numeric devices (indicating multiple "M" awards, starting from the second) to the left. If no hourglass is authorized, the "M" device remains centered with numerals to the left. Regulations such as Army Regulation 670-1 specify that these devices are worn in accordance with uniform standards for all branches.

Eligibility Requirements

Reserve Service Accumulation

The Armed Forces Reserve Medal requires ten years of honorable and satisfactory service as a member of a Reserve Component of the Armed Forces for initial eligibility. Qualifying service is computed cumulatively from the date of first entry into a Reserve Component until transfer to the Retired Reserve, honorary Retired Reserve, or Standby Reserve Inactive List, encompassing active status membership such as drilled participation in the Selected Reserve of the Ready Reserve, including unit training assemblies and annual training periods. Active duty for training also contributes to accumulation, while full-time National Guard duty under Federal recognition qualifies similarly for Army or Air National Guard members.
Effective July 1, 1996, under Executive Order 13013, service in an inactive status became creditable toward the ten-year total only if immediately followed by active Reserve service eligible for retirement pay computation; inactive service performed exclusively to accrue retirement points does not qualify. Prior to August 1, 1979, all creditable service was restricted to active status, excluding any inactive periods. Periods of active duty beyond training (e.g., mobilization) interrupt Reserve service accumulation unless the interruption is less than one continuous year, in which case it may not fully exclude the prior period from computation.
Non-qualifying intervals include any time outside a Reserve Component, punitive discharges, desertion, absence without leave, or time lost under 10 U.S.C. 972 or 1173; service in the Inactive National Guard, Inactive Army Reserve, or solely for retirement credit accrual is similarly excluded. Eligibility determinations, verifying honorable and satisfactory performance, are handled by each service's personnel center, such as the U.S. Army Human Resources Command for Army Reserve members. Service performed after January 12, 1950, in any Reserve Component, including pre-1963 awards replaced by the AFRM, contributes to the total if otherwise qualifying.

Exclusions for Active Duty and Commissioned Officers

Service in the Regular Components of the Armed Forces, which constitutes full-time active duty, is excluded from the computation of qualifying service for the Armed Forces Reserve Medal, as the award recognizes honorable Reserve Component participation rather than active duty tenure. Such regular active duty service interrupts the required 12-consecutive-year period for accumulating the minimum 10 years of creditable Reserve service, though brief honorable active duty interruptions of less than 24 hours do not disqualify continuity. Qualifying Reserve service instead encompasses inactive duty training (such as weekend drills) and annual active duty for training periods, typically not exceeding two weeks, which earn retirement points toward the award's threshold of 50 points per year or 1,000 total points over 10 years. Extended active duty, including mobilizations beyond designated contingency operations eligible for the "M" device, generally does not count unless specifically tied to Reserve Component orders under Title 10 U.S.C. 12301(a), 12302, 12304, or 12306.
Commissioned officers of Reserve Components remain eligible for the medal, with their service creditable provided it meets the same Reserve participation standards as enlisted personnel or warrant officers. Time served by Reserve commissioned officers on the active duty list, such as in Active Guard/Reserve (AGR) roles, contributes to eligibility when performed under Reserve Component authorities, distinguishing it from non-creditable Regular Component commissioning. However, any prior commissioned service qualifying for other Reserve-specific medals, like the Naval Reserve Medal or Marine Corps Reserve Medal, is excluded from AFRM computation to prevent duplicate recognition for the same periods. Officers must maintain honorable service without disqualifying misconduct, ensuring the award reflects sustained Reserve commitment rather than active duty equivalents.

Award Devices

Hourglass Device for Cumulative Service

The Hourglass Device is an attachment authorized for wear on the Armed Forces Reserve Medal (AFRM) and its corresponding ribbon to denote completed 10-year periods of qualifying reserve service. It symbolizes the passage of time in reserve commitment, with the device awarded upon accumulation of each successive decade of honorable and satisfactory service in a U.S. Armed Forces Reserve Component.
The device features a 5/16-inch-high hourglass emblem, initially without additional numerals for the first three awards. A bronze hourglass is issued for the first 10 years of service, a silver hourglass for the second 10-year period (totaling 20 years), and a gold hourglass for the third (totaling 30 years). For each additional 10-year period beyond 30 years, a bronze hourglass with a superimposed Roman numeral "X" is authorized, allowing multiple such devices to reflect extended service. Eligibility is verified through service records, such as the ANG/USAFR Point Credit Summary (AF Form 526), confirming satisfactory participation without exclusions for active duty equivalents unless specified otherwise.
Wear specifications position the hourglass device centered on the AFRM suspension ribbon and service ribbon, with precedence over other attachments like the "M" device for mobilization. Multiple hourglass devices, when applicable, are worn in sequence from the recipient's right to left, ensuring visibility of cumulative achievement without overlapping the medal's core elements. This device underscores long-term reserve dedication, distinct from mobilization recognitions, and has been part of AFRM criteria since the medal's 1950 establishment by Executive Order 10163.

"M" Device for Contingency Mobilization

The "M" device, a bronze block letter measuring 1/4 inch (0.64 cm) in height, is authorized for attachment to the Armed Forces Reserve Medal to recognize reserve component members mobilized or serving on active duty in support of designated contingency operations. This device distinguishes mobilization service from standard reserve duty tracked by the hourglass device, with eligibility extending to those involuntarily ordered to active duty or who volunteer for such service under Title 10 U.S. Code provisions. Executive Order 13013, issued on October 30, 1996, established the criteria for this device, allowing its award for participation in operations designated by the Secretary of Defense.
Eligibility requires at least one day of qualifying active duty, regardless of prior reserve service accumulation toward the base medal's 10-year requirement. Multiple mobilization periods within the same designated contingency operation count as a single award of the "M" device, preventing duplication for related service increments. For recipients qualifying before completing 10 years of honorable reserve service, the medal may be worn with the "M" device but without the bronze hourglass until the service threshold is met. Subsequent unrelated mobilizations under separate presidential call-ups or distinct contingencies warrant additional "M" devices, denoted by Arabic numerals on the ribbon for multiples.
The device is positioned centered on the medal's suspension ribbon and service ribbon; when combined with hourglass or numeral devices, the "M" remains central, the hourglass to the wearer's right, and numerals to the left. Military departments determine individual eligibility based on verified orders and service records, ensuring awards align with official designations rather than self-reported participation. This framework, applied across Army, Air Force, Navy, Marine Corps, and Coast Guard reserves, underscores the device's role in denoting operational readiness contributions without inflating cumulative service credits.

Mobilization Qualifications

Definition of Qualifying Active Duty

Qualifying active duty for the Armed Forces Reserve Medal, particularly in the context of mobilization for the "M" device, encompasses reserve component service mobilized under specific statutory authorities in support of contingency operations. This is defined as active duty performed under orders specifying a period of not less than 30 consecutive days, executed pursuant to Title 10 U.S.C. 12301(a), 12301(d), 12302, 12304, or 12304a, or under Title 32 U.S.C. 502(f) when authorized by the secretary of the military department concerned and reimbursed by the federal government. Such duty excludes routine active duty for training (ADT) or annual training unless it meets the mobilization criteria, and it must contribute to retirement points while assigned to a reserve unit. 
The criteria emphasize involuntary activations under Title 10 U.S.C. provisions for full or partial mobilization (e.g., 12301(a), 12302, 12304), as well as voluntary service in designated contingencies on or after August 1, 1990, per Executive Order 13013. A single period of qualifying active duty cannot be credited toward multiple awards or devices, ensuring no double-counting of service days. These requirements apply uniformly across services, with eligibility verified through retirement point accounting systems requiring at least 50 points per anniversary year for overall medal qualification, though mobilization duty specifically triggers the "M" device for contingency support.

Designated Contingency Operations

Designated contingency operations for the Armed Forces Reserve Medal "M" device consist of U.S. military operations or contingencies explicitly approved by the Secretary of Defense, typically under Title 10 U.S.C. authorities such as section 12304 for mobilization. Reserve component members involuntarily mobilized or volunteering for active duty in direct support of these operations qualify for the "M" device, provided they complete at least 30 consecutive days or 45 non-consecutive days of qualifying service within the operation's timeframe. This distinguishes mobilization service from standard reserve duty accumulation for the base medal.
The Department of Defense's Office of the Deputy Assistant Secretary of Defense for Military Personnel Policy maintains and updates the official list of approved operations. Even if an operation lacks an official designation, deployments to the associated area of responsibility (AOR) in support thereof may qualify if aligned with the contingency's objectives. Multiple service periods within a single designated contingency count as one "M" device; subsequent qualifying mobilizations to distinct operations earn additional devices denoted by Arabic numerals (e.g., "2" for two operations).
Approved operations historically encompass major post-Cold War engagements, including Operation Desert Shield/Storm (Persian Gulf War, commencing August 2, 1990), Operation Restore Hope (Somalia, commencing December 5, 1992), Balkan stabilization missions such as Operations Joint Endeavor, Joint Guard, and Joint Forge (19952000), and Global War on Terrorism efforts like Operation Enduring Freedom (commencing September 11, 2001), Operation Iraqi Freedom (commencing March 19, 2003), Operation New Dawn (September 1, 2010December 15, 2011), and Operation Inherent Resolve (commencing June 15, 2014). Eligibility requires verification through personnel records confirming support to the operation, excluding routine training or non-contingency deployments.

Significance and Reception

Role in Recognizing Reserve Commitment

The Armed Forces Reserve Medal primarily recognizes the long-term commitment of individuals serving in the reserve components of the U.S. Armed Forces by awarding the medal upon completion of 10 years of honorable and satisfactory service. This includes active participation in required unit training assemblies, annual training periods, and other reserve obligations, which demonstrate readiness to support full-spectrum military operations without the structure of full-time active duty. The medal's criteria, established under Executive Order 10163 as amended, ensure that only those who maintain high standards of conduct and performance receive this distinction, thereby validating the reserves' role as a vital extension of the total force.
Through the attachment of hourglass devicesbronze for the initial 10 years, silver for 20 years, and gold for 30 yearsthe AFRM quantifies and honors cumulative service, symbolizing the passage of time dedicated to national defense. This progressive recognition incentivizes sustained involvement, acknowledging the sacrifices of reservists who balance military duties with civilian careers, family responsibilities, and community roles. Unlike awards tied to specific combat or mobilization events, the base AFRM focuses on peacetime and routine reserve participation, filling a critical gap in formal acknowledgment for this distinctive form of service commitment.
The medal's design, featuring an eagle perched on a scroll inscribed with "Armed Forces Reserve," further embodies the ethos of vigilance and preparedness inherent in reserve service. By providing a wearable emblem of achievement, it fosters a sense of professional identity and morale among recipients, reinforcing the strategic importance of the reserve components in maintaining a balanced and responsive military posture. Over time, the AFRM has become a benchmark for reserve longevity, with thousands awarded annually to reflect the collective dedication that underpins the operational flexibility of the U.S. defense establishment.

Debates on Award Dilution and Equity

Critics within military communities have argued that the Armed Forces Reserve Medal (AFRM) exemplifies broader concerns over award dilution in the U.S. armed forces, where the accumulation of service ribbons is perceived to diminish the prestige of more meritorious decorations. The AFRM's criteriarequiring 10 years of satisfactory reserve service within 12 consecutive years, often part-timehave been viewed by some as a relatively low threshold compared to the continuous demands of active duty, potentially contributing to "ribbon inflation" where service members display extensive arrays of awards for routine longevity rather than exceptional valor or achievement.
A specific point of contention is the absence of a direct active duty counterpart to the AFRM, leading to calls for its elimination to promote equity across components; for instance, in a 2005 discussion among naval aviation professionals, participants recommended scrapping the medal since "there is no equivalent award for 10 years active duty," suggesting instead that mobilization service be denoted via devices on other reserve ribbons. This highlights perceived unfairness, as active component personnel rely on awards like the Good Conduct Medal (typically for 3 years of honorable service) or higher decorations for career milestones, without a parallel longevity medal featuring cumulative devices like the AFRM's bronze, silver, or gold hourglasses.
Further fueling equity debates is the "M" device, awarded for mobilization in contingency operations regardless of prior reserve tenure, allowing even short-service reservists to qualify for the full medal upon brief active duty activationsuch as during post-9/11 operations where involuntary mobilizations were common. Proponents of stricter criteria argue this provision risks devaluing the award by conflating minimal mobilization with sustained reserve commitment, though official policy maintains it to recognize contingency contributions without diluting core eligibility standards. These views, while not resulting in policy changes, reflect ongoing tensions in military culture over balancing recognition for reserve sacrifices with preserving award selectivity amid expanded operations since the 1990s.

Notable Recipients

Recipients from Major Conflicts

During the Persian Gulf War (Operations Desert Shield and Desert Storm, 19901991), approximately 265,000 members of the National Guard and Reserve were mobilized, marking the largest reserve call-up since the Korean War. Of these, more than 35,000 Army Reserve soldiers from 626 units were activated, alongside over 20,000 Air Force Reservists and more than 21,000 Navy Reservists, many serving in theater or support roles. Qualifying reservists received the Armed Forces Reserve Medal with the "M" device for involuntary or voluntary mobilization on or after August 1, 1990, retroactively authorized in 1996 by Secretary of Defense William Perry to recognize contingency service.
In Operations Iraqi Freedom (20032011) and Enduring Freedom (20012014, primarily Afghanistan), reserve components experienced historic activation levels, with over 800,000 unique Guard and Reserve personnel deploying in support of these and related Global War on Terror missions. This surge contributed to the bulk of the estimated 282,000 reserve component members authorized for the "M" device across all post-1990 contingencies, often denoting multiple mobilizations indicated by numerals on the device. The award highlighted the operational integration of reservists, who filled critical combat, support, and sustainment roles, though eligibility required meeting basic reserve service criteria or specific mobilization thresholds under Executive Order 13013.
Recipients from these conflicts typically earned the medal through combined inactive-duty training points and active-duty mobilizations, with the "M" device distinguishing contingency service from routine reserve obligations. While individual combat valor is recognized via other decorations like the Bronze Star or Purple Heart, the AFRM with "M" underscores long-term commitment amid high-tempo operations that strained reserve families and employers. No comprehensive public tally isolates exact AFRM awards per conflict, but mobilization data indicate tens of thousands from the Gulf War and hundreds of thousands from Iraq and Afghanistan qualified, reflecting the reserves' evolution from strategic backups to integral warfighting elements.

High-Profile Military and Reserve Leaders

President Harry S. Truman, who served as the 33rd President of the United States from 1945 to 1953, received the first Armed Forces Reserve Medal on September 25, 1950, in recognition of his prior service in the Missouri Army National Guard during World War I and subsequent years in the Army Reserve until 1951. Truman's reserve commitment included artillery battery command and honorary colonelcy, reflecting early post-World War I reserve structures.
Major General William J. Donovan, founder and director of the Office of Strategic Services (OSS) during World War II, was awarded the Armed Forces Reserve Medal with one bronze hourglass device for over 10 years of reserve service, including his World War I infantry leadership and interwar reserve duties in the New York National Guard. Donovan's military career spanned active and reserve components, culminating in his promotion to brigadier general in 1943, underscoring the medal's applicability to leaders bridging wartime and reserve roles.
Other notable reserve leaders include Major General Mari K. Eder (USA, Ret.), who earned the medal with "M" and hourglass devices for cumulative reserve service alongside her active-duty roles in public affairs and command. Similarly, Major General David L. Harris, former Adjutant General of the Georgia National Guard, received the award with gold hourglass and "M" device, highlighting sustained reserve leadership in joint and state missions. These examples illustrate the medal's role in honoring flag officers who maintained reserve affiliations amid high-level responsibilities.
