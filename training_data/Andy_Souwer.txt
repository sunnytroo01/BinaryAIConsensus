 Andy Souwer, born on November 9, 1982, in 's-Hertogenbosch, Netherlands, is a retired Dutch kickboxer and mixed martial artist nicknamed "The Destroyer," renowned for his aggressive style, fast hand combinations, and devastating low kicks. He began training in kickboxing at age seven at Ling Ho Gym under coach John de Ling and made his professional debut at eight, amassing an impressive record of over 160 wins in approximately 180 bouts, including victories against elite opponents like Buakaw Banchamek, Masato Kobayashi, and Yodsanklai Fairtex.
Souwer's career highlights include two K-1 World MAX titles, won in 2005 and 2007, where he triumphed in grueling single-night tournaments in Tokyo, defeating Buakaw via decision in the 2005 final after two extra rounds and defeating Masato via majority decision in the 2007 final. He also captured four Shoot Boxing World Championships in 2002, 2004, 2008, and 2012, showcasing his versatility in rulesets that incorporate grappling and throws. Additional accolades encompass the It's Showtime 70MAX World Title in 2010, as well as championships from organizations like ISKA, WMTA, WKA, FIMC, and WPKA across various weight classes.
Beyond kickboxing, Souwer ventured into MMA with a 2-3 record and made a professional boxing debut in 2014, defeating Paata Varduashvili, though he primarily remained a striking specialist. His influence extends to coaching; he founded the Souwer Sport Institute and serves as a lead instructor, referee, judge, and commentator for events like KWU SENSHI, where he has been involved since at least 2023 and plans to conduct training camps in Varna, Bulgaria, in December 2025.

Early life and background

Childhood and family

Andy Souwer was born on November 9, 1982, in 's-Hertogenbosch, commonly known as Den Bosch, in the Netherlands. He was raised in this southern Dutch city, where he spent his early years in a typical urban environment, initially showing interest in outdoor play and team sports like soccer.
Souwer's family played a pivotal role in shaping his disciplined upbringing, particularly through his father's strict guidance. His father emphasized the importance of achievement and hard work, often intervening in Andy's activities to steer him toward structured pursuits, such as redirecting him from casual street play or soccer toward more rigorous endeavors. This parental influence instilled a strong sense of determination from a young age, setting the foundation for his later transition into martial arts training as a child.

Introduction to martial arts

Andy Souwer began his martial arts journey at the age of seven, enrolling in kickboxing classes at the Ling Ho Gym in Den Bosch, Netherlands. This early start was influenced by his friends and supported by his family's strong emphasis on discipline, with his father playing a pivotal role in ensuring consistent training despite initial reluctance. By age eight, Souwer had already entered his first amateur bout, marking the beginning of a rapid ascent in the sport.
Throughout his teenage years, Souwer progressed steadily through the Dutch amateur kickboxing circuits, securing the Dutch Junior Championship title.
Under the guidance of early coaches John and Deddy at Ling Ho Gym, Souwer trained alongside notable fighters like Albert Kraus, absorbing a rigorous regimen that emphasized Dutch-style kickboxing fundamentals. His initial training incorporated elements from Muay Thai, including knee strikes and clinch work, which enhanced his striking arsenal and adaptability against diverse opponents. By age 16, Souwer had advanced to A-class amateur competitions, setting the stage for his transition toward professional levels while amassing an impressive record of victories in youth divisions.

Professional kickboxing career

Early years and rise (19982004)

Andy Souwer made his professional kickboxing debut in 1998 at the age of 16, competing in an A-Class MTBN Title match under a Dutch promotion, where he secured a victory by points decision. This early success marked the beginning of his rapid ascent in the Dutch kickboxing scene, where he amassed a series of wins in local events, including multiple world titles across different organizations by the age of 18, often ending bouts with knockouts that showcased his aggressive striking style.
Souwer's first significant international exposure came in 2002 when he traveled to Japan for the Shoot Boxing S-Cup tournament, where he won the title. Building on this, he defended his S-Cup title in 2004. These victories against rising Asian talents established Souwer as a formidable lightweight contender on the global stage, highlighting his adaptability to hybrid rulesets through precise combinations and knockout power, while he continued to compete in Dutch circuits.

K-1 MAX dominance (20052009)

In 2005, Andy Souwer emerged as a dominant force in K-1 MAX by winning the World Championship tournament in Yokohama, Japan, defeating a field of elite competitors including Takayuki Kohiruimaki by decision in the quarterfinals, Gago Drago by unanimous decision in the semifinals, and reigning champion Buakaw Por. Pramuk by split decision in the final after five grueling rounds. This victory marked Souwer's first K-1 World MAX title and showcased his aggressive shootboxing style, characterized by high-volume combinations and relentless pressure. To prepare for the international stage, Souwer shifted his training to the renowned Mejiro Gym in Amsterdam under coach Andr Mannaart, where he honed his Dutch kickboxing techniques alongside top European talent, adapting to the three-round format and diverse opponents in K-1 events.
Following his 2005 triumph, Souwer aimed to defend his title but faced a setback in the 2006 K-1 World MAX Final, where he lost a second-round TKO to Buakaw Por. Pramuk after defeating Tsogto Amara by unanimous decision in the opening round and Masato Kobayashi by decision in the semifinals. This defeat motivated a strategic refinement in his approach, emphasizing better distance management and counterstriking during his continued training at Mejiro Gym. Souwer rebounded strongly in 2007, reclaiming the K-1 World MAX crown at the Nippon Budokan in Tokyo by defeating Gago Drago by knockout in the quarterfinals, Albert Kraus by majority decision in the semifinals, and forcing Masato's corner to stop the fight due to cuts in the second round of the final. These performances against perennial rivals solidified Souwer's reputation as a top technician in the 70kg division.
Throughout 2008 and 2009, Souwer maintained his competitive edge in K-1 MAX qualifying rounds, securing a fourth-round knockout over Mike Zambidis in the 2008 Final 16 to qualify for the Final 8, where he suffered a unanimous decision defeat to Albert Kraus in the quarterfinals, which spurred further tactical adjustments at Mejiro Gym to counter Kraus's boxing precision. In 2009, he advanced to the semifinals but lost a unanimous decision to Buakaw Por. Pramuk, demonstrating his resilience despite accumulating mileage from prior wars. These high-stakes bouts elevated Souwer's global profile, drawing widespread media attention from outlets covering K-1's golden era and expanding his fanbase across Europe and Asia, where his back-to-back titles positioned him alongside icons like Buakaw as a defining figure in lightweight kickboxing.

Later promotions and retirement (20102021)

Following his successes in K-1 MAX, Souwer continued competing in the It's Showtime promotion, where he captured the 70MAX World Title on June 30, 2012, defeating defending champion Chris Ngimbi by majority decision in the main event of It's Showtime 57 & 58 in Brussels, Belgium. This victory solidified his status as a top contender in the 70 kg division amid It's Showtime's merger with former K-1 assets to form Glory Kickboxing later that year. As the promotion evolved into Glory, Souwer transitioned into a coaching role, heading Team Souwer and training prominent Glory fighters such as Fraser Parsons and Robin van Roosmalen, while selectively taking fights in other organizations like Enfusion and the World Fighting League to maintain his competitive edge.
In the mid-2010s, Souwer faced increasing challenges from cumulative injuries and the physical toll of weight cutting for frequent bouts, including recurring shin and ankle issues that occasionally sidelined him. Despite these setbacks, he remained active, securing knockouts in Enfusion events, such as his 2014 stoppage of Sergio Wielzen, and continuing to represent Dutch kickboxing internationally. By 2018, Souwer, then 35 and contemplating retirement to focus on his Souwer Sport Institute gym, signed a multi-fight deal with ONE Championship, drawn back by the opportunity for high-profile "legacy" matches in Asia.
Souwer made his ONE Super Series debut on October 6, 2018, at ONE: Kingdom of Heroes in Bangkok, Thailand, where he dropped a controversial split decision to Anthony Njokuani in a three-round kickboxing bout marked by intense exchanges. He rebounded briefly with a unanimous decision victory over Zhang Chunyu on December 4, 2020, at ONE: Big Bang in Singapore, showcasing his technical boxing and low kicks to outpoint the Chinese fighter over three rounds. However, losses followed, including a second-round TKO to Yodsanklai Fairtex via punches at ONE: A New Era on March 31, 2019, in Tokyo, Japan.
Souwer's career concluded on October 15, 2021, at ONE: First Strike in Kallang, Singapore, in the quarterfinals of the ONE Featherweight Kickboxing World Grand Prix. Facing Marat Grigorian, he absorbed heavy pressure in the first round but succumbed to a TKO in the second after a leg kick damaged his shin, rendering him unable to continue at 2:26. Immediately after the defeat, Souwer announced his retirement in the cage, stating it was time to step away after over two decades in the sport and shift full focus to coaching at his gym and family life.

Other combat sports careers

Shootboxing

Shootboxing is a hybrid combat sport originating in Japan that blends striking arts like kickboxing and Muay Thai with elements of wrestling, permitting punches, kicks, knees, elbows, throws, and limited standing submissions while restricting prolonged ground grappling to three seconds before a stand-up. This ruleset emphasizes versatile stand-up fighting, where clinch exchanges can lead to takedowns scored as knockdowns if the opponent hits the ground first. Andy Souwer, with his foundational kickboxing skills, adapted effectively to shootboxing by incorporating throws to disrupt opponents' rhythm and enhance his close-range dominance, turning potential vulnerabilities into strategic advantages during high-stakes tournaments.
Souwer debuted in shootboxing at the 2002 S-Cup tournament, claiming the world title with a unanimous decision victory over Zheng Yuhao in the final after navigating a grueling eight-man bracket. He defended his prowess in 2004, securing back-to-back S-Cup wins by knocking out Hiroki Shishido in the second round of the final with a barrage of strikes following a clinch exchange. In 2008, Souwer captured his third title, defeating Edwin Kibus in the quarterfinals, Gago Drago via knees in the semifinals, and Kenichi Ogata by second-round head kick knockout in the championship bout. His fourth victory came in 2012, solidifying his status as a shootboxing elite through consistent tournament dominance.
Throughout his shootboxing tenure, Souwer faced formidable opponents such as Japanese technician Hiroki Shishido, whose resilient defense tested Souwer's finishing ability in the 2004 final, and hard-hitting Gago Drago, whom he outclinched in 2008 to advance. These encounters often unfolded as fast-paced narratives of striking flurries transitioning into throws, with Souwer's precision knees and uppercuts proving decisive in wearing down durable foes under the hybrid rules.
The demands of shootboxing refined Souwer's clinch proficiency and knee strikes, enabling seamless transitions from distance fighting to close-quarters control that later bolstered his kickboxing performances by improving endurance in prolonged exchanges.
Souwer participated in over a dozen shootboxing-specific bouts across his career, contributing to an overall professional record of 160 wins, 19 losses, and 1 draw, where his shootboxing success underscored a win rate exceeding 85% in hybrid ruleset competitions.

Mixed martial arts

Andy Souwer made his professional mixed martial arts debut in late 2015 under the Rizin Fighting Federation, a Japanese promotion known for its high-profile events blending striking and grappling disciplines. In his inaugural bout against Yuichiro Nagashima at Rizin Fighting World Grand Prix 2015: Iza no Mai on December 31, 2015, Souwer demonstrated his striking prowess by securing a first-round technical knockout via punches at 5:29, overwhelming his opponent with aggressive ground-and-pound after a takedown exchange. This victory highlighted his ability to adapt kickboxing fundamentals to MMA's multifaceted ruleset, though it was one of only two wins in his brief foray into the sport.
Over the next two years, Souwer competed in five MMA bouts, compiling an overall record of 2-3. His second win came against Massaro Glunder at World Fighting League MMA on May 27, 2017, where he claimed a third-round submission via armbar, showcasing improved ground control after transitioning from a dominant top position. However, losses to grappling-oriented fighters underscored the difficulties of evolving his pure striking background into a complete MMA skill set. For instance, he fell to a rear-naked choke from Daron Cruickshank in the first round (4:09) at Rizin 2 on September 25, 2016, and to an armbar by Kazuyuki Miyata in the first round (4:39) at Rizin 3 on December 29, 2016both opponents leveraging superior submission expertise to neutralize Souwer's stand-up advantages. His final MMA outing ended in a unanimous decision defeat to wrestler Akiyo Nishiura after three rounds at Rizin World Grand Prix 2017: Opening Round on October 15, 2017, where persistent takedown defense and ground pressure limited his offensive output.
Transitioning from kickboxing, Souwer faced notable challenges in integrating grappling into his game, as his losses often stemmed from vulnerabilities on the ground against specialists who capitalized on transitions from clinches or takedowns. To address this, he invested in targeted grappling training, building on his earlier shootboxing experiencewhich emphasized standing throws and clinch battlesto enhance his defensive wrestling and submission offense, as seen in his armbar finish against Glunder. Despite these efforts and appearances in prominent Rizin events, Souwer ultimately curtailed his MMA commitments after 2017, prioritizing his established dominance in striking arts over the broader demands of full-contact grappling integration.

Boxing

Andy Souwer ventured into professional boxing with a single bout on March 17, 2014, in Den Bosch, Netherlands, where he secured a technical knockout victory over Paata Varduashvili after the Georgian fighter retired in his corner at the end of the fourth round. This debut marked his only appearance in the professional boxing ranks, as documented in official records. Souwer did not pursue further bouts in boxing, maintaining his focus on kickboxing commitments during that period of his career.

Championships and accomplishments

Kickboxing titles

Andy Souwer established himself as one of the elite figures in 70 kg kickboxing through his conquest of major international titles, particularly in tournament formats that demand exceptional stamina and technical precision. His achievements highlight the rigorous hierarchy of the sport, where promotions like K-1 set the global standard for lightweight divisions, requiring victors to prevail in multiple bouts over a single evening, while European organizations such as It's Showtime and Enfusion emphasized sustained dominance through ranked defenses and high-stakes eliminations.
Souwer's crowning accomplishments came as a two-time K-1 World MAX champion in 2005 and 2007, titles that solidified his legacy in the sport's most revered lightweight grand prix, known for assembling top global talent and enforcing a grueling single-night bracket structure. In 2005, he advanced past Kozo Takeda via second-round TKO at the K-1 MAX Champions Challenge before securing the championship with a split decision over defending champion Buakaw Por. Pramuk in the Yokohama final, marking a breakthrough against one of Muay Thai's premier strikers.
Reclaiming the crown in 2007, Souwer dispatched Ole Laursen via first-round knockout in the opening round, followed by decision wins over Gago Drago in the quarterfinals and Albert Kraus in the semifinals, before stopping Masato with a second-round TKO in the Tokyo finala rematch victory that underscored his tactical evolution and power.
Beyond K-1, Souwer captured the It's Showtime 70MAX World Championship in 2012 by defeating Chris Ngimbi via unanimous decision at It's Showtime 57 & 58 in Brussels, a belt from a leading European promotion that bridged Dutch-style kickboxing with international contenders and elevated his status in the continent's competitive landscape.
In 2014, he added the Enfusion Live 70 kg World Title with a first-round knockout of Ardalan Sheikholeslam at Enfusion 22 in Groningen, Netherlands, affirming his prowess in Enfusion's tournament-heavy format that prioritizes aggressive, finish-oriented bouts among Europe's rising stars.
Souwer also secured the WFCA World Muay Thai Super Welterweight Championship around 2010, demonstrating his adaptability to full Muay Thai rules in a title that bridged kickboxing and Thai striking traditions within a respected sanctioning body.
His resume includes further accolades such as the ISKA World Super Welterweight Championship, alongside belts from the WMTA, WKA, WPKA, and FIMC, which collectively illustrate his versatility and sustained excellence across diverse kickboxing governing organizations.

Shootboxing titles

Andy Souwer is a four-time Shoot Boxing World Tournament (S-Cup) Champion, securing victories in 2002, 2004, 2008, and 2012.
The S-Cup tournaments followed an eight-man single-elimination format, typically contested over a single night at major venues in the Tokyo metropolitan area, such as Yokohama Cultural Gymnasium and Saitama Super Arena. These high-stakes events demanded rapid adaptation to shoot boxing's hybrid rules, which permitted throws and grappling alongside strikes, contrasting with Souwer's kickboxing background where he briefly adjusted his stand-up focus to incorporate takedown defenses.
Souwer's repeat appearances showcased his dominance against diverse international fields, including Japanese standouts like Hiroki Shishido and Kenichi Ogata, Thai muay Thai specialists, and fighters from China and Brazil, as he navigated semifinals and finals to claim each title. In the 2002 final, he defeated Cheng Yu-Hao by decision; in 2004, he knocked out Shishido in the second round; in 2008, he stopped Ogata early in the second; and in 2012, he outpointed Kem Sitsongpeenong in a majority decision. These successes, without formal defenses as tournament-based titles, highlighted his versatility in the throw-inclusive discipline.
Souwer's S-Cup triumphs significantly elevated his profile within Japanese promotions, establishing him as a premier foreign talent capable of thriving in shoot boxing's unique environment and paving the way for high-profile bouts in organizations like K-1.

Additional honors

Throughout his career, Andy Souwer earned multiple top rankings in the 70 kg division from prominent kickboxing organizations and media outlets. He was consistently placed in the top 10 by LiverKick during his peak years in the 2000s and early 2010s, reflecting his dominance in lightweight kickboxing. In addition, Souwer held a number three position in a 2012 K-1 MAX division ranking, underscoring his status among elite competitors like Masato and Giorgio Petrosyan.
Souwer is widely known by the media nickname "The Destroyer," a title originating from his relentless, high-pressure fighting style that dismantled opponents through aggressive combinations and unyielding forward movement. This moniker, also used interchangeably with "Souwer Power," became synonymous with his reputation as one of Dutch kickboxing's most formidable figures.
Beyond competitive accolades, Souwer received recognition for his promotional and charitable efforts in advancing the sport. In 2018, he served as an ambassador for Cambodia's Kingdom Fight Gym, auctioning a personal championship belt and donating a portion of his fight purse to fund free martial arts training and education for underprivileged youth, helping to develop the next generation of fighters. More recently, in 2025, he relaunched the World Kickboxing Series promotion to provide sponsorships, development opportunities, and global exposure for emerging amateur and professional talent, earning praise for expanding kickboxing's reach.

Fighting style and technique

Striking approach

Andy Souwer adopted an orthodox stance throughout his career, allowing him to deliver powerful strikes from a balanced position that emphasized mobility and forward movement. This foundation enabled him to incorporate elements from Muay Thai, particularly in his use of devastating low kicks to target opponents' legs and disrupt their balance, often setting up follow-up attacks. His knee strikes, drawn from Muay Thai techniques, were employed effectively in clinch situations and as jumping counters, adding a layer of unpredictability to his stand-up game.
Central to Souwer's striking philosophy was an aggressive pressure fighting style, characterized by relentless forward advancement and high-volume output to overwhelm adversaries, which earned him the moniker "The Destroyer." He favored rapid punching combinations influenced by boxing crossovers, using feints and jabs to close distance before unleashing hooks and crosses that transitioned seamlessly into leg kicks or knees. This approach reflected the Dutch kickboxing tradition of blending boxing precision with hybrid striking, allowing Souwer to maintain offensive dominance while minimizing exposure.
In the three-round formats common to K-1 MAX events, Souwer focused on pace control by inflicting early-round damage through leg kicks and body shots to accumulate points and weaken opponents' mobility for later exploitation. This strategic emphasis on initial aggression contributed to his success in securing multiple world titles, including two K-1 World MAX championships.

Strengths and notable tactics

Andy Souwer's technical strengths in kickboxing were prominently featured by his devastating leg kicks, which inflicted cumulative damage on opponents' mobility and often led to knockdowns or impaired defense over the course of fights. These low kicks, delivered with precision and power, were a hallmark of his Dutch-style approach, allowing him to dismantle taller or more durable fighters by targeting the thighs and calves repeatedly. Additionally, Souwer excelled in clinch work with sharp knees, particularly effective against taller opponents, where he used scissor and flying variations to close distance and deliver punishing strikes to the body or head under K-1 rules.
Among his notable tactics, Souwer frequently employed feints to set up powerful hooks, creating openings in opponents' guards during aggressive exchanges and transitioning seamlessly into combinations like the "Souwer Combo"a sequence of cross-hook, hook-cross, liver shot, and body kick. He also utilized teeps (push kicks) adeptly for distance management, feinting them to disrupt rhythm and set up follow-up knees or entries into the pocket, maintaining control in mid-range while pressuring forward. These tactics reflected his overall striking philosophy of relentless pressure combined with intelligent setups, blending Dutch aggression with Muay Thai elements for fluid adaptability.
Despite these assets, Souwer showed occasional vulnerability to counters in his later career, particularly due to his forward-pressing aggression, as seen in bouts where precise timing from counterpunchers like Buakaw Banchamek exploited his advances. This exposure became more evident post-2010, amid a grueling schedule of high-stakes tournaments.
Souwer's tactics evolved notably after 2010, shifting from the raw power shots of his early careermodeled after Ramon Dekkersto a more strategic emphasis on footwork, angle changes, and sustained combinations that conserved energy for later rounds. This maturation, highlighted by his 2010 It's Showtime 70MAX World Title win, incorporated better defensive adjustments and feint-heavy setups, allowing him to outmaneuver elite competition in endurance-testing Grand Prix formats.

Post-retirement activities

Coaching and gym ownership

Following his retirement from competitive kickboxing in 2021, Andy Souwer shifted his focus to coaching and gym ownership, establishing himself as a mentor for emerging martial artists. He owns and operates the Souwer Sports Institute in 's-Hertogenbosch, Netherlands, his hometown where he began training as a child, providing a dedicated space for kickboxing instruction and fighter development. At the institute, Souwer trains a team of aspiring fighters, emphasizing technical proficiency, physical conditioning, and mental resilience drawn from his championship experience.
Souwer's coaching curriculum centers on his proprietary 4-Pillar Systemattacking, countering, interfering, and strategic mindsetwhich integrates core Dutch kickboxing techniques with tactical drills and endurance training to build well-rounded competitors. This approach has been implemented through in-person sessions at the institute and extended internationally via seminars and training camps, including his role as lead instructor at SENSHI invitational camps in Bulgaria, where he works with skilled prospects from Europe and beyond. In 2018, he partnered with Cambodia's Kingdom Fight Gym to conduct workshops and inspire local talent, aiming to expand kickboxing's reach in Southeast Asia.
The growth of Souwer's coaching enterprise includes the launch of online training programs in 2025, offering modular video content for global students to follow his system remotely, alongside affiliate gym networks that adopt his methodology. His motivation for this transition stems from a desire to preserve his legacy by passing on decades of elite-level knowledge, helping the next generation avoid common pitfalls and achieve success in the sport.

Refereeing and event involvement

Following his retirement from competitive fighting, Andy Souwer has taken on significant roles in officiating kickboxing events, particularly with the SENSHI promotion. Starting in 2023, he began serving as a referee and judge for SENSHI fight nights, bringing his extensive experience to ensure fair and high-quality bouts. For instance, at SENSHI 19 on November 25, 2023, in Varna, Bulgaria, Souwer was among the legendary martial artists acting as referees and judges, overseeing 13 professional fights involving fighters from 16 countries.
Souwer continued this involvement into 2025, contributing to multiple SENSHI tournaments as a referee, judge, and commentator. At SENSHI 28 on September 13, 2025, in Varna, he was ringside alongside other K-1 icons, guiding the event's first Heavyweight Grand Prix and providing officiating support during the eight-man tournament. He is scheduled to officiate at SENSHI 29 on December 6, 2025, in Varna, where he will join fellow legends as a judge and referee for the promotion's final gala of the year. These roles highlight Souwer's transition to safeguarding the sport's integrity while mentoring participants directly.
In addition to officiating, Souwer has provided expert commentary on modern kickboxing through interviews tied to SENSHI events. In a September 2025 discussion with Sportal.bg following SENSHI 28, he praised the promotion's innovative format, stating that it "once again is doing something amazing for kickboxing as a whole" by reviving the intensity of one-night Grand Prix tournaments and inspiring new talent. Earlier, in a June 2025 bTV interview ahead of SENSHI 27, Souwer emphasized the mental and physical challenges of elite competition, drawing from his career to contextualize contemporary developments in the sport.
Souwer's event involvement extends to mentorship at SENSHI training camps, where he serves as a lead instructor for emerging fighters. As a board member of KWU SENSHI, he has led sessions at international camps, including the XIX International Summer Martial Arts Camp in July 2025 and the inaugural Invitational Training Camp in October 2025, both in Varna, Bulgaria, focusing on technique, sparring analysis, and mental preparation. He is scheduled to return for another camp in early December 2025, continuing to pass on his expertise to the next generation.
Beyond officiating and coaching, Souwer frequently appears at SENSHI events as a ceremonial figure and special guest, enhancing their prestige. At SENSHI 28, for example, he was honored ringside with other K-1 legends, symbolizing the promotion's connection to kickboxing's golden era while engaging with fans and fighters.

Professional records

Kickboxing and shootboxing record

Andy Souwer's professional record in kickboxing and shootboxing reflects a highly successful career spanning over two decades, with 160 wins, 98 by knockout or technical knockout, 19 losses, and 1 draw across 180 total bouts. This win rate of approximately 89% underscores his dominance in the 70 kg division, particularly in major promotions like K-1 and Shoot Boxing.
A breakdown of his victories shows a strong emphasis on finishes, with KO/TKOs accounting for about 61% of wins, while the rest came via unanimous or majority decision, highlighting both his knockout power and technical proficiency in longer exchanges. Losses were predominantly by decision (15) or late stoppage (4), often against elite competition, and the single draw occurred in a closely contested Shoot Boxing match.
Souwer demonstrated remarkable consistency through notable streaks, including a 10-fight winning run from 2004 to 2005 that propelled him to the K-1 World MAX championship. Another significant streak was a 7-win sequence between 2007 and 2008, securing multiple Shoot Boxing titles during that period.
His record evolved over time, with early years (20002005) featuring a higher KO rate of around 70%, driven by aggressive pressure fighting against regional opponents. Later bouts (2010 onward) saw a shift toward strategic decision wins at 55% KO rate, as he faced more defensively sound international stars in tournament formats. This adaptation contributed to sustained success despite increased competition levels.

Three of his fights ended in the first round, averaging approximately 4 minutes and 40 seconds for those encounters, while the other two extended into later rounds, highlighting variable durations influenced by grappling exchanges over prolonged striking.
