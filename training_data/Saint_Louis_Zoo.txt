 The Saint Louis Zoo, officially known as the Saint Louis Zoological Park, is a premier zoological institution located in Forest Park, St. Louis, Missouri, dedicated to conservation, education, and animal welfare. Founded in 1910 by the Zoological Society of St. Louis, it opened to the public in 1910 with initial exhibits and expanded following voter approval of a mill tax in 1916; it spans 90 acres and houses over 12,000 individual animals representing approximately 450 species, many of which are endangered or threatened.
One of the few major zoos in the United States offering free general admission year-round, the Saint Louis Zoo attracts millions of visitors annually through its immersive exhibits, such as the historic 1904 Flight Cagea pioneering aviary from the Louisiana Purchase Expositionand modern multi-species habitats like River's Edge and Primate Canopy Trails. The zoo is accredited by the Association of Zoos and Aquariums (AZA), with accreditation renewed in September 2025, and operates as part of the Metropolitan Zoological Park and Museum District, established in 1971, which provides public funding to support its operations alongside contributions from private memberships and donations.
Beyond exhibition, the Saint Louis Zoo emphasizes global conservation through initiatives like Species Survival Plans (SSPs), reproductive sciences including cryopreservation, and field projects to protect habitats for species such as cheetahs, rhinos, and amphibians. Educational programs, including camps, school outreach, and behind-the-scenes tours, engage visitors of all ages in wildlife stewardship, while facilities like the Endangered Species Research Center underscore its commitment to advancing scientific research for species preservation.

Overview

Location and facilities

The Saint Louis Zoo is situated in Forest Park, a 1,300-acre urban park in St. Louis, Missouri, occupying 90 acres in the park's southwest quadrant. This placement integrates the zoo seamlessly into the broader landscape of Forest Park, which serves as a central recreational hub in the city and is home to other major attractions, including the nearby St. Louis Art Museum, approximately one mile to the east. The zoo houses over 12,000 animals representing 450 species, many of which are endangered, across its expansive grounds designed for both wildlife habitation and visitor accessibility.
Key infrastructure enhances navigation and visitor experience within the zoo's layout, featuring extensive paved pathways that wind through themed zones and natural features such as ponds and streams. Among the standout facilities is the Emerson Zooline Railroad, a 1.5-mile narrow-gauge miniature railway that offers a narrated loop tour through tunnels and past animal exhibits, accommodating up to 80 passengers per train across multiple cars. Complementing this is the Mary Ann Lee Conservation Carousel, a two-story attraction with 64 hand-carved wooden figures depicting endangered species, promoting awareness of global conservation efforts while providing family-friendly rides.
In addition to its Forest Park location, the Saint Louis Zoo is developing WildCare Park, a 425-acre safari park and conservation center in north St. Louis County, Missouri, near the confluence of the Mississippi and Missouri Rivers. Construction milestones in 2025 include the arrival of the first southern white rhino herdfive individualsin late September, marking a significant step in breeding and habitat restoration for this threatened species, followed by the arrival of two reticulated giraffes in October. The facility is projected to open to the public in 2027, featuring expansive savanna habitats and nature trails to support species conservation and education.

Admission and attendance

The Saint Louis Zoo provides free general admission to visitors of all ages, a longstanding policy implemented since the zoo's founding in 1910 that underscores its commitment to public accessibility. This no-cost entry is made possible through substantial public funding from the Metropolitan Zoological Park and Museum District, a special taxing entity established in 1971 after approval by voters in St. Louis City and St. Louis County. The district levies property taxes at a rate of approximately 6.71 cents per $100 of assessed valuation, along with a portion of sales taxes, collectively supplying about 70% of the zoo's operating budgettotaling roughly $54 million in tax revenues for 2024.
Although core admission remains gratis, fees apply to select premium attractions to help offset operational costs. For instance, rides on the Emerson Zooline Railroad, a historic miniature train offering scenic tours of the grounds, cost $7.95 per person for unlimited daily access (with children under age 2 riding free). The interactive Stingrays at Caribbean Cove exhibit, where guests can touch cownose stingrays and small sharks in a saline pool, charges $4.95 per person (free for those under 2). Similarly, the Sea Lion Sound theater presentation, featuring trained California sea lions performing in an aquatic arena, is accessible via the zoo's Adventure Pass at $16.95, which bundles entry to multiple paid experiences including the above. These rates, current as of late 2025, reflect seasonal adjustments and are waived for zoo members.
The zoo's free-entry model drives robust attendance, averaging about 3.3 million visitors per year in the pre-pandemic era, positioning it among the most-visited cultural institutions in the United States. The COVID-19 pandemic led to temporary closures and reduced numbers, but recovery has been strong, with 2.9 million guests recorded in 2024a 9% increase from the prior year. For 2025, attendance is projected to build further toward pre-2020 levels, influenced by ongoing enhancements such as refreshed habitats and seasonal events that draw repeat and out-of-town visitors.
This high volume of visitors generates significant economic benefits for the St. Louis region, with the zoo contributing an estimated $200 million annually through direct spending on concessions, parking, and merchandise; indirect effects from tourism-related jobs in hospitality and transportation; and induced impacts from local supply chains supporting zoo operations.

History

Origins and early development

The origins of the Saint Louis Zoo trace back to the 1904 Louisiana Purchase Exposition, commonly known as the St. Louis World's Fair, held in Forest Park. The Smithsonian Institution commissioned the construction of the Flight Cage, a massive aviary designed by architect Frank L. Baker to showcase its bird collection; measuring 228 feet long, 84 feet wide, and 50 feet high, it was the world's largest enclosed aviary at the time. Following the fair's closure, local citizens campaigned to preserve the structure rather than see it dismantled and returned to Washington, D.C.; the City of St. Louis purchased it for $3,500about one-fifth of its original $17,500 construction costand relocated it slightly within Forest Park to serve as the foundation for a permanent zoological garden. This acquisition marked the zoo's conceptual beginning, transforming a temporary fair exhibit into a lasting public asset.
In the years immediately after the fair, efforts to formalize the zoo gained momentum. Park Commissioner Robert Aull established a makeshift zoological display in August 1905 adjacent to the Flight Cage, incorporating animals from the defunct Fairground Park Zoo, including monkeys, bears, and a camel; this informal attraction drew over 12,000 visitors in its first season and highlighted the potential for a dedicated institution. By 1910, growing public interest led to the formation of the Zoological Society of St. Louis, a nonprofit organization tasked with advocating for and overseeing the development of a comprehensive zoo. The society played a pivotal role in securing 77 acres of Forest Park land in 1913 specifically for the zoo's expansion. Further progress came in 1916 with voter approval of a property tax levy, providing the first dedicated public funding stream and enabling the construction of basic enclosures and pathways around the core Flight Cage exhibit.
Early development was hampered by funding constraints, as the zoo operated without a centralized tax district until later decades and depended heavily on city appropriations supplemented by private donations from philanthropists and civic groups. These resources supported modest initial animal acquisitions, prioritizing species suitable for educational displayssuch as birds in the Flight Cage and a variety of mammals in temporary pensto foster public appreciation for wildlife conservation and natural history. A significant milestone came in 1921 with the opening of the Bear Pits, innovative open-moat enclosures cast from local Mississippi River rock formations, which replaced barred cages and set a new standard for humane animal housing while attracting widespread acclaim. This period laid the groundwork for the zoo's evolution into a major institution, with continued expansions in the 1920s building on these foundations.

Mid-20th century expansions

The Saint Louis Zoo experienced significant growth during the 1920s, marked by the construction of several key facilities that expanded its capacity for diverse animal collections. The Bird House, featuring Spanish Revival architecture, opened in 1920 to house a variety of avian species. This was followed by the Bear Pits in 1921, which utilized rocky grottos crafted from Missouri River bluffs to provide naturalistic enclosures for bears, setting a new standard for zoo design. The Primate House, originally known as the Monkey House, opened on January 13, 1925, at a cost of $200,000, with its Spanish-style design accommodating a range of primates. The Reptile House, or Herpetarium, was completed in 1927, further enhancing the zoo's focus on specialized exhibits. These developments were primarily funded through civic contributions and public support, reflecting the community's commitment to establishing the zoo as a major cultural institution.
During the 1930s and 1940s, economic challenges from the Great Depression influenced but did not halt expansions, with several projects leveraging public works initiatives to improve animal care and visitor access. The Antelope House was built in 1935 as a Depression-era public works project, featuring expansive fields and walls mimicking local granite formations for hoofed mammals. Construction culminated in 1939 with the Ape House, providing dedicated space for great apes and marking the end of a major building phase amid fiscal constraints. The zoo maintained its free admission policy, established early as a public institution for education and amusement, ensuring broad accessibility during these years. The Stupp Memorial Pheasantry, focused on bird breeding and display, was added in the early 1940s as part of ongoing efforts to elevate animal welfare standards.
Post-World War II recovery spurred renewed investment in the 1950s and 1960s, emphasizing more naturalistic habitats and family-friendly features. The Sea Lion Arena, initially built as a lion arena in 1954, introduced performance spaces that engaged visitors with live animal demonstrations. The Elephant House, completed in 1957 with attached arenas and moated yards, allowed for expanded exhibits of large mammals in semi-natural settings. Construction on the Aquatic House began in 1961, providing modern facilities for aquatic species like walruses and seals, aligning with evolving standards for environmental enrichment. These enhancements contributed to rapid attendance growth, surpassing 1 million visitors annually by 1960, driven by the zoo's reputation as a free, accessible destination for families.

Late 20th century to present

In 1971, voters in St. Louis City and County approved the formation of the Metropolitan Zoological Park and Museum District (ZMD), a political subdivision that provided stable public funding through a property tax for the zoo, art museum, science center, and history museum, ensuring long-term financial support for operations and expansions. This funding mechanism enabled significant developments, including the opening of the Charles H. Yalem Children's Zoo in 1969, which offered interactive experiences for young visitors and emphasized educational engagement with animals.
During the 1980s and 1990s, the zoo shifted toward immersive habitats and conservation priorities, exemplified by the phased development of River's Edge, its first major immersion exhibit, which began in 1999 and expanded through the early 2000s to showcase species from multiple continents in naturalistic settings along a river theme. The Monsanto Insectarium (later renamed Bayer Insectarium) opened in 2000, providing a dedicated space for over 100 invertebrate species and highlighting biodiversity education. Concurrently, the zoo intensified efforts on endangered species conservation, including breeding programs for Asian elephants, whose population has dwindled to fewer than 50,000 in the wild due to habitat loss.
The 2000s and 2010s saw further habitat innovations, with the Jungle of the Apes indoor exhibit, originally built in 1987, receiving updates and animal transfers to enhance ape welfare and visitor immersion. Penguin & Puffin Coast debuted in 2010 as North America's only walk-through penguin exhibit, housing Humboldt, king, and gentoo penguins alongside puffins in a chilled, rocky coastal environment. In 2015, the $16 million McDonnell Polar Bear Point opened, featuring a 40,000-square-foot habitat with temperature-controlled pools and underwater viewing areas to simulate Arctic conditions for polar bears. The polar bear Kali, the exhibit's original resident since 2015, was transferred to another facility in March 2025 to support species management under U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service recommendations. In September 2025, the zoo announced that polar bear Kallik would transfer to the Oregon Zoo in late fall 2025 to support breeding recommendations under the Association of Zoos and Aquariums (AZA) Polar Bear Species Survival Plan.
Into the 2020s, the zoo continued modernizing with the $13 million Michael and Quirsis Riney Primate Canopy Trails opening in 2021, a 35,000-square-foot outdoor expansion on Historic Hill that includes elevated walkways, mesh tunnels, and eight new enclosures for lemurs and monkeys to promote natural behaviors. In 2023, the zoo introduced its first all-electric locomotive, named Mary Meachum after a prominent St. Louis abolitionist, as part of sustainability initiatives for the Zooline Railroad. Progress on the $230 million WildCare Park, a 425-acre off-site conservation center, advanced with the arrival of the first southern white rhino herd in September 2025; the facility, focused on breeding large carnivores and rhinos, is slated to open to the public in 2027. In October 2025, two giraffes, Moyo and Daniel, arrived at WildCare Park to join the rhino herd in acclimating to the site. Complementing these efforts, the zoo formed the Living Earth Collaborative in 2017 with Washington University in St. Louis and the Missouri Botanical Garden to advance biodiversity research and local conservation.

Leadership and directors

The leadership of the Saint Louis Zoo has evolved through dedicated directors who shaped its growth from a nascent public park feature into a global conservation leader. George P. Vierheller served as director from 1922 to 1962, overseeing significant animal acquisitions and infrastructure developments during the zoo's formative decades, including hands-on management during challenges like the 1960 labor strike where he personally fed animals. His long tenure solidified the zoo's commitment to free admission, established since its 1910 opening through public subsidy via the Metropolitan Zoological Park and Museum District.
R. Marlin Perkins succeeded Vierheller as director from 1962 to 1970, bringing expertise in herpetology and public outreach after starting at the zoo in the 1920s. He expanded educational programming and elevated the zoo's profile through television, hosting Mutual of Omaha's Wild Kingdom and fostering early conservation awareness. Perkins' era marked the zoo's initial alignment with emerging standards from the Association of Zoos and Aquariums (AZA), which accredited the institution in 1977.
Charles "Charlie" Hoessle directed the zoo from 1982 to 2002, following roles as reptile keeper since 1963 and founder of the education department in 1964. Under his leadership, the zoo advanced animal welfare and community engagement, including hosting the local Saint Louis Zoo Show from 1968 to 1978 and strengthening AZA compliance through rigorous animal care protocols. Hoessle's focus on immersive exhibits and volunteer programs laid groundwork for modern expansions.
Jeffrey P. Bonner served as president and CEO from 2002 to 2021, driving major habitat developments like the River's Edge immersion exhibit, which opened in 2002 and spans 13 acres across four continents to promote species coexistence. His tenure emphasized conservation, with the zoo earning repeated AZA accreditation for high standards in animal management and field initiatives.
Current leadership includes Dwight Scott as Dana Brown President & CEO since 2022, alongside Michael Macek as zoo director, prioritizing sustainability through projects like the 425-acre Saint Louis Zoo WildCare Park, set to open in 2027 as a safari and conservation center near the Mississippi and Missouri Rivers confluence. This initiative builds on the zoo's AZA leadership in species recovery, such as red wolf programs.
Governance involves the nonprofit Saint Louis Zoo Association's volunteer board of business and civic leaders, who collaborate on fundraising and strategy, complemented by the Metropolitan Zoological Park and Museum District's oversight board of eight members representing St. Louis City and County taxpayers. The zoo engages over 500 volunteers annually through programs like Zoo ALIVE, supporting education, conservation, and operations.

Exhibits and zones

Lakeside Crossing

Lakeside Crossing serves as the central hub of the Saint Louis Zoo, offering visitors an immersive introduction to aquatic life through interactive exhibits and demonstrations that emphasize marine behaviors and conservation awareness. Positioned near key pathways, this zone integrates educational encounters with relaxation spaces, including a grassy plaza for picnics and rest, while highlighting species like sea lions and stingrays to foster appreciation for ocean ecosystems.
A highlight of Lakeside Crossing is Stingrays at Caribbean Cove, an interactive touch pool located adjacent to the main pathways, where guests can observe and gently interact with cownose rays and southern rays gliding in a 17,000-gallon warm saltwater habitat. Small sharks, including bonnethead, brown-banded, and white-spotted bamboo varieties, share the pool, allowing visitors to touch the animals during supervised sessions that promote safe handling and learning about their natural foraging habits. Access to this exhibit requires a separate admission fee as part of the Zoo's Adventure Pass or individual ticket, distinct from the overall free entry policy.
Sea Lion Sound anchors the aquatic focus of the zone with its 1.5-acre habitat, featuring a 35-foot-long underwater viewing tunnel that provides close-up views of California sea lions and harbor seals swimming and interacting in a naturalistic environment. Opened in 2012, this $18 million exhibit includes an outdoor arena for daily demonstrations, where trainers showcase behaviors such as high jumps, powerful swims, and simulated hunting techniques to educate on pinniped agility and social dynamics. These shows, typically running seasonally from spring through fall, encourage visitor engagement by illustrating the animals' intelligence and conservation needs.
The zone also incorporates family-oriented features like a central fountain area surrounded by dining options, including the Lakeside Cafe for indoor-outdoor meals and quick-service spots like Ray's Snack Shack, creating a seamless blend of play and refreshment. Nearby, the Conservation Carousel integrates with Lakeside Crossing pathways, offering rides on hand-carved endangered species figures to extend the educational theme. In the 2020s, enhancements to accessibility have been implemented across the Zoo, including wheelchair-friendly paths and ramps in Lakeside Crossing to ensure inclusive experiences for all visitors.

River's Edge

River's Edge serves as the Saint Louis Zoo's inaugural immersion exhibit, immersing visitors in a simulated riverine ecosystem spanning continents through a meandering waterway and lush, naturalistic landscaping that highlights interactions between wildlife, plants, and human influences. Spanning approximately 10 acres, the zone features habitats mimicking floodplains, savannas, and forested riverbanks to house large mammals from Africa, Asia, and other regions, emphasizing biodiversity in riparian environments. Development occurred in multiple phases beginning in the late 1990s, with the initial Asia-focused section opening in 1999, followed by expansions through 2014 that added African and other continental elements.
Central to the Africa Nile area is the Anheuser-Busch Hippo Harbor within the Hippo House, a key exhibit dedicated to Nile hippos (Hippopotamus amphibius), where visitors can observe these semi-aquatic mammals through expansive underwater viewing windows. The design allows clear views of natural behaviors, such as submerged feeding and wallowing, which help regulate body temperature in their tropical habitat; symbiotic interactions are also visible, with small fish grazing on hippo skin and manure in the water. Nearby, the Africa Savanna section provides cheetahs (Acinonyx jubatus) and African wild dogs (Lycaon pictus) with expansive, floodplain-inspired enclosures featuring tall grasses, rocky outcrops, and water features to encourage hunting simulations and pack dynamics.
The Asia portion showcases the Asian elephant habitat, a multi-acre complex with outdoor yards equipped with a deep river channel, cascading waterfalls, and dense vegetation for foraging and dust bathing, complemented by indoor barns offering climate-controlled shelter and medical care facilities. Adjacent Red Panda Ridge provides elevated, arboreal enclosures for red pandas (Ailurus fulgens), these bamboo specialists from Himalayan forests, complete with climbing structures, hollow logs, and scent-marking opportunities to replicate their treetop lifestyle. Baird's tapirs (Tapirus bairdii), once housed in related riverine settings, contribute to the zone's representation of Central American floodplain species, though current placements emphasize naturalistic pools and shaded undergrowth for their shy, browsing habits.
In the 2020s, the zoo introduced targeted enhancements across River's Edge, including puzzle feeders and sensory enrichments to stimulate cognitive and physical activity among residents, such as dispersing food in novel devices to mimic wild foraging challenges. These updates align with broader welfare standards, promoting species-specific behaviors while supporting conservation efforts; for instance, the facility participates in breeding programs for Asian elephants to bolster wild populations threatened by habitat loss.

Discovery Corner

The Discovery Corner at the Saint Louis Zoo serves as an educational hub emphasizing interactive encounters with invertebrates and hands-on learning opportunities designed for young visitors. This zone highlights the diversity of small-scale wildlife through specialized exhibits that foster curiosity about natural ecosystems and animal behaviors.
The Bayer Insectarium, originally opened as the Monsanto Insectarium in 2000 and renamed in 2018, showcases over 100 species of insects and other invertebrates across more than 20 major exhibit areas. Key features include the Mary Ann Lee Butterfly Wing, a geodesic dome filled with tropical plants, a waterfall, and a pool that houses free-flying butterflies, moths, water striders, whirligig beetles, and turtles. Visitors can observe life cycles, such as silkworm metamorphosis, through displays and occasional behind-the-scenes tours where staff demonstrate invertebrate care, including feeding sessions for select species like tarantulas. These elements underscore the ecological roles of insects, with programs promoting their conservation.
Until its closure in October 2020 due to the COVID-19 pandemic, the Emerson Children's Zoo provided immersive, tactile experiences for children, including petting areas with goats, ponies, rabbits, and guinea pigs, as well as encounters with reptiles, amphibians, and birds. Activities encouraged direct interaction, such as brushing goats and pony rides, alongside educational play like fossil digging and gem panning, all within a 3.5-acre space near the Insectarium's seasonal butterfly elements. The site has since hosted the temporary Emerson Dinoroarus exhibit until 2023 and is now under construction for the $40 million Henry A. Jubel Foundation Destination Discovery, a multi-level family attraction set to open in 2026 with curated play zones, maker spaces, and nature-based adventures to inspire wildlife appreciation.
The Living World, opened in 1989 as a two-story educational facility at the zoo's North Entrance, features indoor exhibits that explore global ecosystems through life-size sculptures of marine life like sharks, squid, and stingrays in its rotunda. It includes classrooms, an auditorium, and resource centers supporting hands-on learning with specimens and interactive displays, such as touch tables for fossils and natural artifacts, aimed at building conceptual understanding of biodiversity. This venue hosts the zoo's broader STEM-focused programs for ages 3-12, integrating conservation themes through guided activities and events.

The Wild

The Wild zone at the Saint Louis Zoo immerses visitors in diverse habitats replicating forested and polar environments, emphasizing endangered species and their conservation needs. Developed primarily in the 2000s, this area features expansive exhibits designed to promote natural behaviors while educating guests on threats facing wildlife in the wild. Key attractions include primate habitats, polar bear enclosures, and coastal bird aviaries, all integrated with sustainable features like recycled materials and energy-efficient systems.
The Jungle of the Apes serves as the indoor centerpiece for great apes, housing troops of western lowland gorillas, chimpanzees, and Sumatran orangutans in environments mimicking their native tropical forests. This exhibit allows visitors to observe complex social interactions and foraging activities through multi-level viewing areas, with the apes accessing outdoor spaces in the adjacent Fragile Forest during warmer months. Opened in 1986 as part of early primate expansions, it underscores the zoo's commitment to species like the critically endangered Sumatran orangutan, whose wild populations have declined due to habitat loss.
Complementing the indoor facility, the Donn and Marilyn Lipton Fragile Forest provides a lush outdoor extension spanning landscaped areas with streams, vines, and climbing structures for orangutans, chimpanzees, and gorillas. Named for its donors and opened in 2005, this habitat highlights the fragility of rainforest ecosystems, enabling guests to witness family dynamics and territorial behaviors up close. The design facilitates seamless transitions between indoor and outdoor spaces, enhancing animal welfare while drawing attention to conservation efforts for these threatened primates.
Shifting to polar themes, the McDonnell Polar Bear Point offers a 40,000-square-foot expanse simulating Arctic seascapes, coasts, and tundra, complete with a 50,000-gallon dive pool visible through underwater viewing windows. Opened on June 6, 2015, the exhibit currently features the nearly three-year-old polar bear Kallu, who arrived in March 2025 alongside his twin brother Kallik following the transfer of longtime resident Kali to another facility. Kallik is scheduled to move to the Oregon Zoo in late fall 2025, supporting a national conservation initiative by the Association of Zoos and Aquariums to optimize breeding and genetic diversity for this vulnerable species, impacted by climate change and sea ice loss. The habitat includes a maternity den to encourage future reproduction, emphasizing the zoo's role in polar bear preservation.
Adjacent to the polar bear area, Centene Grizzly Ridge provides a 7,000-square-foot forested ridge habitat for two orphaned grizzly bear siblings, Huckleberry and Finley, rescued from Montana. Opened in September 2017, this enclosure incorporates a pond, waterfall, and 22-foot viewing walls to showcase the bears' playful and foraging routines in a naturalistic setting reminiscent of their North American wild habitats. Though not endangered, the exhibit raises awareness about human-wildlife conflicts that lead to such rescues, promoting habitat protection for grizzly populations.
The Penguin & Puffin Coast replicates rugged coastal cliffs and frigid waters for seabirds, featuring four penguin speciesHumboldt, king, gentoo, and rockhopperin open-air enclosures with waterfalls and nesting sites, alongside tufted and horned puffins. As North America's first such open-air penguin habitat when it debuted in 2003, the climate-controlled space uses specialized lighting to simulate seasonal changes, allowing dives into deep pools observable by visitors. These exhibits highlight conservation challenges for these species, many classified as vulnerable due to overfishing and pollution in their Southern Ocean and North Atlantic ranges.

Historic Hill

Historic Hill represents the foundational core of the Saint Louis Zoo, encompassing structures dating back to the early 20th century that highlight the institution's evolution from its informal origins in 1910. This zone preserves a blend of historic architecture and exhibits focused on avian, reptilian, and primate species, offering visitors an immersive glimpse into the zoo's heritage while prioritizing animal welfare through naturalistic habitats.
The centerpiece of Historic Hill is the 1904 World's Fair Flight Cage, originally constructed by the Smithsonian Institution as the world's largest single-unit aviary at the time, measuring 228 feet long, 84 feet wide, and 50 feet high. Repurposed after the fair, it now houses the Cypress Swamp exhibit, a recreated wetland environment featuring over 20 species of free-flying North American birds, including white ibis, great egrets, roseate spoonbills, and various cranes that forage and interact naturally amid cypress trees, shrubs, and aquatic plants. Visitors access the space via a winding floating bridge and elevated viewing pier, allowing close observation of the birds' behaviors without barriers.
Adjacent to the Flight Cage, the Charles H. Hoessle Herpetarium and the Bird House exemplify the zone's 1920s Spanish Revival architecture, characterized by Mediterranean-style stucco walls, red-tiled roofs, and integrated natural elements like vines and water features. The Herpetarium, renovated to support four distinct climatesmontane, temperate, tropical, and deserthouses more than 700 reptiles, amphibians, and crocodilians, including Komodo dragons like the recently arrived male Charlie, who occupies a specialized solarium enclosure designed for thermoregulation and enrichment. Notable residents also encompass venomous snakes from Ecuadorian rainforests, Asian pit vipers, and various frogs and turtles adapted to their respective habitats. In contrast, the Bird House features lushly planted aviaries showcasing global avian diversity, such as birds of paradise, toucans, and hummingbirds from tropical regions, alongside a walk-through aviary and shaded pavilion overlooking crane exhibits in the adjacent Bird Garden, which opened in 1996 to connect these historic spaces.
The Michael and Quirsis Riney Primate Canopy Trails, a 35,000-square-foot outdoor expansion opened on July 12, 2021, enhances the zone's primate offerings by linking to the original 1920s Primate House through elevated walkways and tunnels. This $13 million project provides immersive viewing for eight habitats housing lemurs, gibbons, spider monkeys, and other Old and New World primates, with climbing structures, rope bridges, and forested enclosures that promote natural behaviors like brachiation and foraging while exposing animals to sunlight and fresh air. The Primate House itself, preserved through a 1977 renovation, maintains its historic facade while featuring glass-fronted exhibits with rock work, trees, and foliage mimicking native environments for species like siamangs and colobus monkeys.
Preservation efforts in Historic Hill emphasize maintaining the architectural integrity of these early structures, as seen in targeted renovations that balance historical authenticity with modern animal care standards, ensuring the zone continues to educate visitors on biodiversity and conservation.

Red Rocks

Red Rocks is a savanna-inspired zone at the Saint Louis Zoo, featuring enclosures that mimic the rocky grasslands and open plains of Africa and Asia, housing both formidable predators and elegant herbivores in naturalistic settings. The area emphasizes multi-species habitats where animals interact in environments designed to promote natural behaviors, complete with simulated red granite boulders, shading trees, and integrated avian elements for ecological authenticity.
Central to the zone is Big Cat Country, which opened in May 1976 as part of the zoo's modernization efforts, providing three expansive yardseach approximately one-third of an acrewith boulders, pools, shrubs, and trees to replicate wild terrains. This exhibit showcases African lions (Panthera leo), Amur tigers (Panthera tigris altaica), and cheetahs (Acinonyx jubatus) in rocky, grassland landscapes equipped with viewing blinds that allow visitors to observe the cats without disturbing them. Recent additions, such as pumas in 2023, have expanded the diversity of felids, while ongoing habitat enhancements support breeding programs through advanced video monitoring systems.
Adjacent to Big Cat Country, the Antelope Habitatsdeveloped in the 1960s and expanded through the 1970s and 1980sfeature mixed-species enclosures simulating African savannas, where giraffes (Giraffa camelopardalis), plains zebras (Equus quagga), and addax (Addax nasomaculatus) roam together amid naturalistic rock formations and grassy expanses. These habitats, part of one of the nation's premier collections of hoofed mammals, foster interspecies dynamics while prioritizing animal welfare through spacious designs that encourage foraging and social interactions.
Enrichment programs in Red Rocks enhance animal well-being, particularly for the big cats, where keepers introduce scent trails using herbs and perfumes to stimulate hunting instincts and exploratory behaviors. Daily feeding demonstrations, such as giraffe feedings, provide educational opportunities for visitors to witness natural diets up close, while puzzle feeders and habitat manipulations across the zone promote physical and mental stimulation for all residents. These initiatives, integrated since the zone's expansions, underscore the zoo's commitment to conservation breeding and behavioral health.

Animals and conservation

Animal collection

The Saint Louis Zoo maintains a diverse animal collection comprising over 16,000 individuals from nearly 500 species and subspecies, with a significant portion consisting of endangered or threatened species critical to global conservation efforts. This inventory spans multiple taxonomic classes, including mammals such as big cats, primates, and ungulates; thousands of birds from various aviaries; reptiles and amphibians in specialized herpetariums; fish in aquatic displays; and thousands of invertebrates showcased in the Monsanto Insectarium. Representative examples of the collection's endangered mammals at the main Forest Park site include a herd of seven Asian elephants, including the calf Jet born in November 2024, a bachelor group of western lowland gorillas, and two black rhinos, while the affiliated WildCare Park now supports five southern white rhinos transferred in 2025.
Veterinary care is provided through an on-site hospital featuring advanced diagnostic capabilities, such as digital radiography, ultrasound, and collaborative access to MRI scanning for complex cases, ensuring proactive health management for all animals. The facility supports routine examinations, surgeries, and specialized treatments tailored to the needs of large species like elephants and rhinos. Recent breeding achievements highlight the zoo's success in reproduction programs, including the 2024 birth of an Asian elephant calf via artificial inseminationthe first at the institutionand the arrival of a Humboldt penguin chick in April 2025.
Husbandry practices emphasize animal welfare through climate-controlled enclosures that replicate natural conditions, such as temperature and humidity gradients for tropical species, and comprehensive behavioral enrichment programs involving novel objects, scents, and social interactions to stimulate cognitive and physical activity. These initiatives, integrated across exhibits like River's Edge for aquatic mammals and The Wild for primates, contribute to longer lifespans and successful breeding outcomes.

Conservation initiatives

The Saint Louis Zoo's conservation efforts are spearheaded by the WildCare Institute, which coordinates field projects across more than 15 countries to protect endangered species and their habitats. These initiatives emphasize in situ conservation, including research, community engagement, and anti-poaching measures. For instance, in Madagascar, the Center for Conservation in Madagascar focuses on reducing threats to threatened species such as radiated tortoises through health assessments, disease screening, and monitoring programs in collaboration with local rangers and the Turtle Survival Alliance. Similarly, the Center for Conservation in the Horn of Africa supports rhino protection efforts by funding anti-poaching patrols, habitat restoration, and education programs to safeguard black rhinos and other wildlife in partnership with organizations like the Grevy's Zebra Trust and Northern Rangelands Trust.
A key sustainability initiative is the #ByeToBags campaign, launched in 2016 by Zoo ALIVE teen volunteers to combat plastic pollution's impact on wildlife. The program encourages visitors to pledge switching to reusable bags, highlighting risks like ingestion by sea turtles and microplastic contamination in ecosystems. By December 2021, over 14,000 individuals had pledged, potentially diverting hundreds of thousands of single-use bags annually based on average usage estimates. It partners with The Ocean Project and aligns with Association of Zoos and Aquariums (AZA) sustainability guidelines to amplify broader environmental advocacy.
In 2017, the zoo co-founded the Living Earth Collaborative with Washington University in St. Louis and the Missouri Botanical Garden to advance urban ecology research and rewilding strategies. This interdisciplinary partnership integrates biodiversity science, focusing on local ecosystems like Forest Park to inform global conservation through studies on species interactions and habitat restoration.
The zoo contributes to breeding and reintroduction programs, achieving notable successes such as the release of over 10,000 captive-bred hellbenders into Missouri rivers by 2022, with further milestones including the first zoo-raised hellbenders reproducing in the wild confirmed in 2023, marking a historic milestone in amphibian recovery. In 2025, the WildCare Park began housing a herd of five southern white rhinos as part of the AZA Species Survival Plan, supporting metapopulation management to bolster wild populations through coordinated breeding and genetic diversity efforts in collaboration with the International Rhino Foundation. These programs extend to other species, including the reintroduction of Partula snailsonce extinct in the wildback to French Polynesia, with wild-born offspring and breeding confirmed in the wild in 2024, leading to a proposed IUCN downlisting from Extinct-in-the-Wild to Critically Endangered.
Annually, the WildCare Institute allocates funding for field equipment, staff support, and community programs, contributing to AZA grants like those for Cuban crocodile assessments, while educational outreach reaches thousands through initiatives such as World Okapi Day in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Overall, these efforts underscore the zoo's commitment to global species preservation, with impacts amplified through strategic partnerships and on-the-ground action.

Visitor amenities

Transportation and accessibility

The Emerson Zooline Railroad provides a primary on-site transportation option, offering a 1.5-mile narrated round-trip tour through the zoo with stops at four stations, allowing visitors to hop on and off throughout the day. The railroad, which has transported over 39 million passengers since its opening in 1963, operates daily year-round (weather permitting) generally from 10 a.m. to 4 p.m. during standard zoo hours, with tickets priced at $7.95 for all-day access and free for children under 2. In 2023, the zoo introduced an all-electric locomotive named the Mary Meachum, enhancing sustainability while maintaining the attraction's accessibility with wheelchair spaces and ramps.
Parking is available in two main lots (North and South) for a fee of $15 per vehicle on a first-come, first-served basis, with free parking offered to members at select levels; oversized vehicles over 24 feet are not permitted. Bicycle racks are provided at the zoo's entrances for visitors arriving by bike, though riding bicycles inside the grounds is prohibited. For those preferring public transit, the zoo is accessible via Metro bus lines (such as #90) and the Forest Park Metrolink station, with free weekend rides on the Forest Park Explorer shuttle connecting to the site.
Mobility aids are available for rent on a first-come, first-served basis, including manual wheelchairs at $11 per day, electric convenience vehicles (ECVs) at $35$45 per day, and strollers at $12$14 per day, to support visitors with varying needs. The zoo features ADA-compliant pathways throughout most areas, including ramps and accessible restrooms, along with Sensory-Inclusive certification through KultureCity, which includes designated quiet zones for neurodiverse individuals seeking reduced sensory input. Service animals are permitted in all public areas except specific exhibits like the Cypress Swamp and Bird House, in accordance with zoo policies.
Navigation is facilitated by the free STL Zoo Map mobile app, which provides interactive 3D maps, real-time location tracking, wait times for attractions, and highlights for accessibility features, available via QR code or download. As previews for the upcoming WildCare Park (set to open in 2027) continue into 2025 with animal acclimation and announcements of new species like scimitar-horned oryx, the zoo is incorporating enhanced accessibility planning for this 425-acre expansion, building on current standards.

Education and events

The Saint Louis Zoo provides extensive school programs designed to foster learning about wildlife and conservation, including educational zoo tours, classroom sessions, and outreach initiatives that accommodate groups from preschool through high school. These programs emphasize life sciences, animal adaptations, and biodiversity through hands-on activities, biofacts, and guided experiences, with options for virtual delivery to reach broader audiences. In 2024, Zoo educational learning experiences engaged 74,312 participants, many of whom were students on field trips or in structured sessions. The Zoo ALIVE (Active Leaders in Volunteer Education) program specifically recruits high school students as year-round volunteer educators, training them to lead interactive sessions on animal care and environmental topics.
Public events at the Zoo offer engaging, family-oriented activities that promote education alongside entertainment, such as the annual Boo at the Zoo, a non-scary Halloween celebration held on select October evenings with festive decorations, entertainers, and themed animal encounters. Wild Lights, presented by Commerce Bank, features millions of holiday lights illuminating Zoo pathways from November 28 through December 28, 2025, on select evenings, encouraging visitors to explore conservation themes through illuminated exhibits. Exclusive member nights, including Marlin Perkins Society events like the dedicated Boo at the Zoo evening on October 13, 2025, provide priority access and behind-the-scenes insights for supporters.
Adult education initiatives include specialized lectures and training opportunities focused on conservation, such as the Whitney and Anna Harris Conservation Forum, which hosts events like the November 4, 2025, presentation on cave ecosystems and their role in biodiversity preservation. While specific 2025 series on rhinos are not detailed, the Zoo regularly features talks on species like rhinos during events such as World Rhino Day, highlighting global protection efforts. Volunteer training supports over 1,000 dedicated individuals, including docents who undergo preparation to deliver interpretive talks, guided tours, and guest interactions on wildlife topics, contributing more than 74,000 service hours annually.
Digital outreach extends the Zoo's educational reach through virtual tours and programs conducted via Zoom, allowing remote participants to observe live animal feedings and discuss habitats in 30-minute sessions tailored for schools and groups. The Zoo produces podcasts, such as episodes from the Master Minds series featuring conversations on conservation medicine and wildlife health. Social media platforms amplify these efforts, with approximately 733,000 Facebook followers, 211,000 on Instagram, 109,000 on X (formerly Twitter), and 109,000 on TikTok as of November 2025, collectively surpassing 1 million users who engage with content on animal stories and conservation. This digital presence also integrates previews of the upcoming WildCare Park, sharing updates on arriving species like the first five white rhinos and two giraffes in October 2025 to build excitement for its 2027 opening as a conservation-focused safari site.
