Sabancaya
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Sabancaya is an active stratovolcano located in the Andes of southern Peru, rising to an elevation of 5,960 meters (19,554 feet) at coordinates 15.787S, 71.857W, approximately 70 kilometers northwest of Arequipa. It forms part of the Ampato-Sabancaya volcanic complex, situated between the older volcanoes Ampato to the southwest and Hualca Hualca to the northwest, and is the youngest and most active of the three, characterized by a summit crater containing an active lava dome.
The volcano's confirmed eruptive history dates back to 1750, with notable activity including a dome-building eruption from 1986 to 1988 that produced ash plumes and gas emissions affecting local livestock, though no human casualties were reported. Since late 2016, Sabancaya has been in a continuous eruptive phase that has featured frequent explosions (up to 50 per day at peak), phreatic activity, ash plumes rising 1-4 kilometers above the summit, sulfur dioxide emissions exceeding 2,000 tons per day, and intermittent thermal anomalies from the lava dome. This unrest began building in 2013 with increased seismicity, including volcano-tectonic earthquakes linked to magma migration along regional faults, leading to the ongoing eruption that prompted an orange alert level and a 12-kilometer exclusion zone enforced by Peruvian authorities; a notable explosion on September 13, 2025, produced an ash column over 5 km, but as of November 2025, activity has decreased to gas-and-steam plumes rising up to 1.3 km and near-daily thermal anomalies.
Geologically, Sabancaya features a large lava flow field covering about 60 square kilometers on its eastern flanks, with 39 individual lobes indicating relatively recent construction compared to its neighbors, and it remains capped by glaciers despite ongoing activity. Monitoring by the Instituto Geofsico del Per reveals persistent minor inflation near the complex and seismic patterns suggesting deep magma reservoir interactions, underscoring its significance as one of Peru's most hazardous volcanoes due to proximity to populated areas and potential for ashfall impacting aviation and agriculture.

Etymology and Exploration

Name Origin

The name Sabancaya derives from the Quechua language of indigenous Andean communities, where it translates to "tongue of fire," a descriptor likely inspired by the volcano's persistent fumarolic activity and visible emissions. This etymology underscores the cultural significance of the feature among Quechua-speaking peoples, who integrated natural phenomena into their linguistic traditions.
The name was historically used by local indigenous groups prior to European contact and was formally recorded in Spanish colonial documents starting in 1595 CE, marking its integration into written colonial records during the early years of Spanish rule in Peru. No major variations or misspellings of the name appear in verified early maps or literature, maintaining consistency as Sabancaya across historical sources.

First Ascent

The first modern ascent of Sabancaya, a stratovolcano rising to 5,960 meters in the Peruvian Andes, occurred in 1966, representing the initial documented climb to its main summit. This achievement came amid growing interest in Andean mountaineering, with the volcano's Quechua namemeaning "tongue of fire"evoking its potential for geothermal activity and spurring exploratory efforts.
Details on the specific participants, expedition leader, and precise route for the 1966 ascent remain sparsely recorded in mountaineering annals, though approaches typically originated from the Colca Valley region, involving multi-day treks across rugged terrain to establish base camps below the glacier-covered upper slopes. Climbers faced inherent challenges of high-altitude Andean volcanism, including thin air leading to altitude-related risks, unpredictable weather patterns with sudden storms, and unstable surfaces of loose ash and scree that complicated progress toward the summit ridge. No earlier indigenous or local ascents are documented in historical or ethnographic records for Sabancaya.
Subsequent to the main summit's conquest, the first complete ascent of all three summit peaks was accomplished in 1972, enabling a thorough traversal of the volcano's jagged crest and providing broader insights into its topographic features. These early climbs laid foundational observations of the summit craters and any contemporaneous fumarolic emissions, informing initial geological assessments of the volcano's active nature prior to its major reactivation in the late 1980s.

Geography

Regional Setting

Sabancaya is situated in the southern part of Peru's Arequipa Region, at coordinates 154712S 715127W, with an elevation of 5,960 m (19,554 ft). This positions the volcano within the high-altitude Andean cordillera, where it forms a prominent feature amid rugged terrain dominated by glaciated peaks and deep valleys.
The volcano is an integral component of the Ampato-Sabancaya volcanic complex, which lies in the Central Volcanic Zone (CVZ) of the Andesa 1,500 km-long segment extending from southern Peru through Bolivia to northern Chile. This zone represents one of four major volcanic arcs along the Andean margin, characterized by active stratovolcanoes resulting from oblique subduction processes. Sabancaya occupies the central position in the complex, flanked by the older Ampato to the southwest and Hualca Hualca to the northwest, contributing to a clustered alignment of volcanic edifices that span approximately 20 km.
Approximately 70 km northwest of the city of Arequipa, Sabancaya's location underscores its relevance to regional subduction zone dynamics, where the Nazca Plate subducts eastward beneath the South American Plate at rates of 6-7 cm per year, fueling magmatic activity across the CVZ. This tectonic regime not only drives volcanism but also influences broader geological provinces, including the adjacent Western Cordillera and the Altiplano plateau to the east.

Local Geomorphology

Sabancaya is a stratovolcano rising to a summit elevation of 5,960 meters, characterized by a composite structure built primarily through effusive and explosive activity during the Holocene. Its morphology features a central terminal cone composed largely of pyroclastic materials, surrounded by numerous blocky andesitic and dacitic lava flows that extend radially across the flanks. The upper edifice consists of multiple coalesced cones, with at least three summit craters identified, the most prominent being the currently active crater at approximately 5,800 meters elevation, which measures about 410 meters in diameter and hosts an ongoing lava dome.
The volcano's flanks are mantled in thick layers of Holocene lava flows, many of which exhibit lobate forms with well-defined fronts and pressure ridges, covering an area of roughly 68 square kilometers across 42 distinct flows. These are interbedded with pyroclastic deposits from Vulcanian and Plinian eruptions, including ash fallout layers that blanket the slopes. Glacial valleys, sculpted during the last glaciation, incise the flanks and channel many of these lava flows, while remnants of past glacial activity contribute to the rugged terrain. Adjacent to the main edifice, the Nevado Sabancaya represents a subsidiary cone within the broader Ampato-Sabancaya volcanic complex, featuring glaciated surfaces that integrate with the primary structure. The volcano's drainages, particularly on the northern and western sides, direct pyroclastic and fluvial materials toward the deeply incised Colca River valley, which reaches depths of up to 2.5 kilometers and facilitates ongoing erosion of the volcanic deposits.

Geology

Tectonic Context

Sabancaya volcano is situated in the Central Volcanic Zone (CVZ) of the Andes, where the eastward subduction of the oceanic Nazca Plate beneath the continental South American Plate along the Peru-Chile Trench generates the compressive forces and partial melting that fuel arc volcanism. This subduction process occurs at a convergence rate of approximately 6.7 cm per year, with variations up to 10 cm per year in the southern Peru segment, promoting the dehydration and fluxing of fluids from the downgoing slab into the mantle wedge, which lowers the solidus and initiates magma generation at depths of 100-150 km. The oblique convergence angle and the slab's dip angle of around 30 in this region further control the eastward migration of the volcanic arc to its current position, approximately 200-300 km inland from the trench.
Within the CVZ, which spans from southern Peru through Bolivia to northern Chile and hosts over 100 potentially active volcanoes, Sabancaya exemplifies the zonal pattern of volcanic distribution shaped by slab geometry. The subducting Nazca Plate's varying thickness and thermal structure lead to periodic along-arc variations in magma production, resulting in volcano spacing of roughly 50-100 km between major density peaks, as evidenced by spatial analyses of volcanic clusters. This spacing reflects deeper crustal diapirism and slab-induced mantle flow, concentrating magmatism in segments where the slab geometry allows efficient fluid transfer and partial melting, with Sabancaya positioned amid a cluster including Ampato and Hualca Hualca.
Regional fault systems exert significant control on magma pathways at Sabancaya by creating zones of weakness that facilitate ascent and eruption. These faults interact with volcanic processes through stress perturbations from magmatic inflation, promoting fault creep and seismicity that can open conduits for magma migration, as observed in the interplay between Sabancaya's unrest and nearby normal and strike-slip faults. Such volcano-tectonic coupling underscores how the broader structural framework of the Andean orogen modulates the volcano's activity.

Petrological Composition

Sabancaya's volcanic products are predominantly composed of andesitic to dacitic lavas and pyroclastics, with whole-rock silica contents ranging from 56.7 to 69.3 wt%. These rocks belong to the medium- to high-K calc-alkaline series, typical of subduction zone volcanism.
The mineral assemblage in these products features phenocrysts of plagioclase, clinopyroxene, amphibole, and biotite, comprising 30-60 vol.% of the rock, along with minor orthopyroxene, Fe-Ti oxides, and olivine (less than 2 vol.%). Olivine is primarily observed as destabilized crystals within mafic inclusions, indicating interaction with more primitive magmas.
Evidence for magma mixing is evident in the hybrid textures, such as disequilibrium features in phenocrysts from the 1990-1998 eruptions, and geochemical signatures including variations in trace element ratios like Sr/Y and La/Yb, as well as Sr-, Nd-, and Pb-isotopic compositions that suggest crustal contamination through assimilation-fractional crystallization (AFC) and mixing processes. These processes reflect repeated recharge of mafic magmas into a resident felsic reservoir, leading to the hybrid andesites erupted at Sabancaya.

Eruptive History

Prehistoric Activity

The AmpatoSabancaya volcanic complex, to which Sabancaya belongs as the youngest and active edifice, initiated its formation during the Middle Pleistocene. Major cone-building phases occurred between approximately 500,000 and 50,000 years ago, primarily constructing the basal edifice of the older Ampato volcano through effusive eruptions that produced thick sequences of andesitic to dacitic lavas. These phases involved at least two distinct stages dated to 450400 ka and 230200 ka, forming a compound stratovolcano that dominated the regional landscape before a prolonged period of quiescence.
In the late Pleistocene, volcanic activity resumed around 40 ka with the development of a dacitic cone on Ampato's southern flank, marked by significant explosive events. A prominent Plinian eruption during this period generated the Corinta ignimbrite, with tephra layers dispersed widely across southern Peru, extending up to several tens of kilometers from the source and providing key stratigraphic markers for regional paleovolcanology. Although precise dating places this event between 40 and 20 ka, similar large-scale explosive activity is inferred to have persisted into the early Holocene around 12,000 years BP, contributing to the complex's eruptive record through voluminous ash fallout and pumice deposits. The andesitic-dacitic composition of these materials facilitated the formation of thick, widespread tephra blankets that influenced local sedimentation and erosion patterns.
Holocene activity shifted northeastward to the Sabancaya edifice, where effusive and explosive eruptions built the current cone over the last 5,000 years. This period included sector collapses and associated debris avalanches, which sculpted the volcano's morphology, including the prominent horseshoe-shaped summit amphitheater open to the north. These gravitational failures, dated to the early to mid-Holocene, involved the mobilization of volcanic materials into hummocky avalanche deposits extending several kilometers downslope, altering the edifice's stability and contributing to its present geomorphic form. Stratigraphic evidence from lava flows overlying these deposits confirms their timing, with key units dated to around 5.2 ka and 4.1 ka via cosmogenic nuclide methods.

Historical Eruptions

The first documented eruptions of Sabancaya occurred in the mid-18th century, following prehistoric Vulcanian events in the late Holocene that produced localized ash fallout and set patterns for subsequent explosive activity. In 1750, the volcano produced ash plumes that deposited fallout on Arequipa, approximately 70 km southeast, marking the initial historical record of unrest. This activity continued intermittently through 1784, with additional ash emissions affecting the same region and demonstrating recurrent low-volume explosive behavior characteristic of the volcano. A smaller eruption was recorded in 1822, involving similar phreatic explosions and minor ash release, though with limited documented impacts.
Activity remained dormant until the late 20th century, when Sabancaya entered a prolonged eruptive phase from 1987 to 1998, characterized by Vulcanian explosions (Volcanic Explosivity Index 12) that generated ash plumes rising up to 5 km above the crater. These events triggered phreatic activity, pyroclastic flows, and lahars that flowed into the Colca Valley, 2326 km north, causing localized flooding and sediment deposition. Ashfall from the plumes reached Arequipa, leading to respiratory issues and reduced visibility, while in the Colca Valley, the eruptions damaged crops such as maize and quinoa, and resulted in livestock losses due to inhalation of fine ash and contaminated water sources. In response to escalating gas emissions and seismic swarms, authorities evacuated several villages within a 14 km radius in early 1992, displacing thousands of residents temporarily to mitigate risks from potential larger blasts. This period highlighted the volcano's threat to agricultural communities in the valley, with at least 20 fatalities attributed to lahars and landslides during the 1991 peak.

Ongoing Eruption (2016present)

The ongoing eruption of Sabancaya volcano commenced on November 6, 2016, marked by Vulcanian explosions that generated ash plumes rising up to 5 km above the crater. These initial events produced daily explosions, with incandescent blocks ejected up to 1.5 km from the summit, accompanied by thermal anomalies first detected in satellite imagery around the same time. The eruption style echoed the Vulcanian activity observed during the 19871998 period, featuring intermittent explosive pulses from a shallow magmatic conduit.
Throughout the eruption, persistent thermal anomalies have been recorded via satellite, with radiative power fluctuating between 0.5 and 25 MW, indicating ongoing lava dome extrusion and degassing. Sulfur dioxide emissions have varied significantly, peaking at up to 3,000 tons per day during periods of heightened activity, while block avalanches descended the northern and northeastern flanks, traveling up to 3.5 km in some instances. Ash plumes have commonly reached 24 km above the vent, dispersing tephra eastward and affecting regional air quality.
As of November 2025, the eruption remains active, with the Geophysical Institute of Peru elevating the alert to Orange level on September 13, 2025, due to increased volcanic activity. In 2025, explosions have occurred at variable rates, often 10-30 per day in recent months, generating gas-and-ash columns up to 3 km high and prompting continued pyroclastic flow monitoring. Sulfur dioxide fluxes have been at low levels, averaging around 130 tons per day as of mid-November 2025, with persistent thermal anomalies detected on the summit crater floor.
Cumulative effects include recurrent ashfall blanketing areas up to 30 km away, notably impacting Arequipa city through deposition on infrastructure and reduced visibility. This has disrupted aviation operations at Rodrguez Balln International Airport multiple times, leading to flight delays and cancellations, while agricultural lands in the Colca Valley suffered crop damage from ash accumulation. In response to early ashfall episodes, temporary evacuations affected communities in Yanque and Chivay, with over 200 residents relocated in 2016 to mitigate health risks from inhalation.

Monitoring and Hazards

Seismicity and Surveillance

The seismic monitoring of Sabancaya is primarily conducted through the collaborative efforts of the Observatorio Vulcanolgico del INGEMMET (OVI-INGEMMET) and the Observatorio Vulcanolgico del Sur del Instituto Geofsico del Per (OVS-IGP), with dedicated networks established since 2013 following the onset of renewed unrest. The IGP's permanent seismic network, operational since March 2019, consists of seven telemetered broadband and short-period stations surrounding the volcano, enabling real-time detection and location of seismic signals within approximately 50 km of the crater. These stations, supplemented by additional temporary and national network sensors during heightened activity, primarily record volcano-tectonic (VT) earthquakes indicative of brittle fracturing in the edifice and long-period (LP) events associated with fluid movement and degassing in the magmatic system.
During periods of unrest, Sabancaya exhibits elevated seismicity dominated by LP events, with typical daily counts ranging from 50 to 100, often correlating closely with explosion rates averaging around 50 per day as recorded since the 2016 eruption onset. VT events, including distal variants, occur in bursts of hundreds per day during pre-eruptive phases but decrease in frequency during ongoing explosive activity, where LP signals and explosion-related tremors prevail. In 2025, seismic activity included a swarm of over 500 volcano-tectonic (VT) earthquakes on 22-23 February, while as of November 2025, daily seismic events number around 25, reflecting moderate unrest. As of November 2025, the volcano continues in eruption with moderate activity, including gas-and-steam plumes rising up to 300 m above the vent and an average of 25 seismic events per day.
Surveillance is enhanced by integration of remote sensing data, including thermal anomalies detected via MODIS instruments on NASA's Aqua and Terra satellites, which track heat from lava dome extrusion and explosions, and SO emissions measured by the Ozone Monitoring Instrument (OMI) aboard the Aura satellite, with historical flux variations reaching up to 5,000 tons per day during eruptive peaks; as of November 2025, emissions average around 130 tons per day. Additionally, webcams operated by OVI-INGEMMET provide visual confirmation of ash plume heights, typically reaching 2-4 km above the crater during active phases, complementing seismic data for comprehensive activity assessment.

Volcanic Hazards

Sabancaya's eruptive activity, characterized by frequent explosions and lava dome extrusion, generates ballistic projectiles that can travel up to 5 km from the vent, posing risks to areas near the summit during heightened unrest. These ejecta, including rock fragments and bombs, result from the violent degassing and fragmentation of ascending magma.
Pyroclastic flows and surges represent another primary hazard, often triggered by the gravitational collapse of the unstable lava dome within the summit crater. These hot, fast-moving avalanches of ash, pumice, and gas can extend several kilometers down the volcano's flanks, particularly along steep ravines, endangering nearby settlements and infrastructure.
Lahars, or volcanic mudflows, frequently threaten drainages such as the Majes River, where they are initiated by the rapid melting of summit ice during eruptions or by heavy rainfall remobilizing loose volcanic debris. These flows can carry volumes of material sufficient to bury valleys and disrupt water supplies, agriculture, and transportation routes in downstream regions.
Ashfall from Sabancaya's plumes, which rise several kilometers and drift variably, can accumulate to thicknesses affecting agriculture, aviation, and health within radii encompassing Arequipa, impacting approximately 50,000 residents in vulnerable zones. Additionally, gas emissions in summit areas include carbon dioxide (CO2) at around 15 mol% and hydrogen sulfide (H2S) at 1.15 mol% in fumarolic mixtures, leading to hazards like asphyxiation in low-lying terrains and chronic respiratory issues from prolonged exposure. Seismicity often serves as an early indicator of escalating activity that could amplify these hazards.

Hazard Assessment and Mitigation

Hazard assessment for Sabancaya volcano involves detailed mapping and modeling to evaluate potential impacts from its ongoing eruptive activity. The Instituto Geolgico, Minero y Metalrgico (INGEMMET) produced updated hazard maps in 2020, which delineate zones of varying risk based on historical and modeled volcanic phenomena, including pyroclastic flows and ash dispersion. These maps identify high-risk areas within a 30 km radius of the summit for pyroclastic flows and surges, while ash fall poses threats up to 100 km downwind, affecting agriculture and infrastructure in the Colca Valley and beyond. Probabilistic scenarios model eruptions with Volcanic Explosivity Index (VEI) levels of 3 to 4, typical of Sabancaya's historical events, simulating plume heights up to 10 km and ash dispersion patterns influenced by prevailing winds. These models, developed by INGEMMET and IGP, predict ash fallout exceeding 10 cm in the Colca Canyon within hours of a VEI 4 event, informing evacuation planning and aviation restrictions. Alert levels range from Green (normal background activity) to Red (imminent major eruption), triggered by thresholds such as increased seismicity, gas emissions exceeding 2,000 tons per day of SO, or explosion frequencies above 20 per day; as of late 2025, Sabancaya remains at Orange, restricting access within 12 km.
Mitigation strategies emphasize proactive measures to reduce vulnerability in populated areas like the Colca Canyon. Community education programs, led by INGEMMET and local authorities, train residents in districts such as Chivay and Cabanaconde on recognizing eruption signs and implementing household protections, reaching over 5,000 participants annually through workshops and school curricula since 2017. Ash cleanup protocols, coordinated by Peru's National Civil Defense Institute (INDECI), involve rapid deployment of machinery and guidelines for safe removal to prevent respiratory issues and water contamination, applied during multiple ashfall events post-2016. International aid coordination, facilitated by organizations like USAID and the United Nations Office for Disaster Risk Reduction, has supported post-2016 recovery through funding for monitoring equipment and emergency supplies, aiding affected communities with over $2 million in assistance by 2020.

Environmental and Human Aspects

Climate and Glaciation

Sabancaya is situated in a high-altitude tropical climate characterized by marked seasonal variations, with a dry and cold season spanning May to October and a wet and warm season from November to April. Annual precipitation in the surrounding arid altiplano is meager, typically below 100 mm, primarily occurring as short-lived convective storms during the wet season. At the summit elevation of approximately 5,960 meters, temperatures range from -5C to 10C, influenced by diurnal fluctuations and the thin atmosphere, with frequent frosts and subzero conditions prevalent year-round.
The volcano's glacial history reflects broader Andean cryospheric dynamics, with glaciation peaking during the Little Ice Age (approximately 13001850 CE), when equilibrium lines reached 5,1005,200 meters and moraines formed at high altitudes near the summits. Glacial retreat has accelerated since the 1980s, driven by regional warming and compounded by recurrent volcanic eruptions that have melted ice through ash deposition and heat. Between 1986 and 2016, Sabancaya lost 77.9% of its glacier area at an average rate of -2.54% per year, resulting in the breakup of the former ice cap into fragmented patches; estimated ice cover was less than 1 km as of 2016, limited to small remnants on the eastern flank near the crater, with further retreat likely due to ongoing activity.
Rainfall variability at Sabancaya is modulated by the El Nio-Southern Oscillation (ENSO), where El Nio phases can intensify wet season precipitation through altered atmospheric circulation, leading to heightened lahar risks from remobilized volcanic deposits. Glacial melt from ongoing retreat further contributes to these hazards by increasing water availability for sediment-laden flows during heavy rains.

Vegetation and Biodiversity

The vicinity of Sabancaya, situated in the high Andes of southern Peru, falls within the Central Andean puna ecoregion, characterized by altitudinal zonation that shapes its vegetation patterns. Between approximately 4,000 and 5,000 meters above sea level, the dominant ecosystem is the puna grassland, featuring tussock-forming bunchgrasses such as Stipa ichu (ichu grass), Calamagrostis spp., and Festuca spp., alongside cushion plants adapted to harsh conditions, including species from genera like Azorella and Distichia. These formations create low-cover, resilient communities suited to the arid, high-elevation environment, with grasses forming dense tussocks that stabilize soil against erosion. Above 5,000 meters, nearing the volcano's summit at 5,960 meters, vegetation transitions to sparse pioneer communities dominated by lichens and mosses, which tolerate extreme exposure and nutrient-poor substrates. Climatic constraints, such as intense solar radiation and frost, further limit species distribution in these upper zones.
The fauna of Sabancaya's surroundings reflects the puna's biodiversity, supporting a range of high-altitude specialists in the Colca Canyon area. Mammals include Andean camelids such as wild vicuas (Vicugna vicugna) and domesticated alpacas (Lama pacos), which graze on the bunchgrasses, alongside rodents like the vizcacha (Lagidium spp.), a cliff-dwelling species that inhabits rocky outcrops. Avian life is highlighted by the Andean condor (Vultur gryphus), a soaring scavenger whose 3-meter wingspan makes it a keystone species in the canyon's thermals, preying on carrion from local herbivores. The Colca Canyon hosts several endemic species, including restricted-range birds like the Andean hillstar hummingbird (Oreotrochilus estella) and plants such as Mutisia arequipensis, contributing to the region's unique ecological mosaic amid the volcanic landscape.
Sabancaya's ongoing eruption since 2016 has notably impacted local vegetation through recurrent ashfall, which blankets grasslands and reduces photosynthetic capacity by blocking sunlight and altering soil chemistry. Remote sensing analyses indicate diminished biomass in affected puna areas, with ash deposits up to several centimeters thick smothering bunchgrasses and delaying regrowth, particularly within 20-30 km of the vent. Recovery studies post-2016 reveal variable resilience, with initial biomass reductions of 20-50% in heavily impacted zones, though pioneer species like Stipa ichu show partial rebound within 1-2 years via wind-dispersed seeds; however, repeated ash events prolong suppression, exacerbating aeolian remobilization on sparsely vegetated slopes. These effects underscore the vulnerability of the puna ecosystem to volcanic disturbances, with persistent activity as of 2025 continuing to affect recovery.

Access and Cultural Significance

Access to Sabancaya is primarily via the PE-34E highway from Arequipa to Chivay, a journey of approximately 160 km that takes 3-4 hours by vehicle, passing through the Salinas and Aguada Blanca National Reserve. Travelers reach the Patapampa pass at 4,910 m elevation, the highest point on the route, where views of the volcano are prominent and hiking trails begin. From Patapampa, a strenuous 5-7 hour hike leads to the base of Sabancaya, though summit attempts require permits from local authorities or the Instituto Geolgico Minero y Metalrgico (INGEMMET) due to the volcano's ongoing eruptive activity and associated hazards.
The reactivation of Sabancaya in 2016 has spurred tourism growth in the region, with guided day tours from Arequipa offering safe viewpoints of ash plumes and explosions, often integrated into broader Colca Canyon itineraries that include treks and condor sightings. These excursions, typically lasting 12-15 hours, have boosted local economies in communities like Chivay and Cabanaconde by creating jobs in guiding, transportation, and hospitality, contributing to sustainable development in the Caylloma province.
In Inca mythology, Sabancaya held significance as a site linked to fire deities and mountain spirits (apus), with its name deriving from the Quechua words "saban" (tongue) and "caya" (fire), translating to "tongue of fire" and reflecting perceptions of its eruptive nature. Eruptions, such as the one around 1470 CE, likely prompted capacocha rituals, including child sacrifices on nearby Ampato to appease the gods. Today, Quechua descendants in the Colca Valley continue offerings and rituals honoring Sabancaya's apu for protection and fertility, elements increasingly woven into ecotourism narratives to educate visitors on Andean cosmology.
