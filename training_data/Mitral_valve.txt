Mitral valve

 The mitral valve, also known as the bicuspid or atrioventricular valve, is a critical cardiac structure located between the left atrium and left ventricle of the heart, consisting of two leaflets that facilitate unidirectional blood flow by opening during diastole to allow oxygenated blood to pass from the atrium to the ventricle and closing during systole to prevent regurgitation. This valve is part of the heart's valvular apparatus, which ensures efficient pumping of blood throughout the circulatory system, and its proper function is essential for maintaining cardiac output and preventing conditions like heart failure.
The mitral valve's structure is a complex, dynamic unit comprising the mitral annulus, leaflets, chordae tendineae, and papillary muscles, all of which work in synchrony to support its role in the high-pressure left heart. The annulus is a fibrous, ovoid ring that contracts during systole to reduce the valve orifice area, while the anterior leaflet covers approximately one-third of the annulus and the posterior leaflet spans the remaining two-thirds, divided into three scallops (P1, P2, P3). The chordae tendineae, thin collagenous tendons, anchor the leaflets to the anterolateral and posteromedial papillary muscles in the left ventricle, preventing leaflet prolapse and ensuring coaptation during closure; these include primary chordae attached to leaflet edges and secondary ones to the ventricular surfaces. Histologically, the leaflets feature layered tissues: a dense collagenous fibrosa on the ventricular side for strength, an elastic atrialis layer for flexibility, and a central spongiosa of glycosaminoglycans for cushioning.
Functionally, the mitral valve's operation is integral to the cardiac cycle, where its closure generates the first heart sound (S1) and contributes to the left ventricular outflow tract's integrity, while its three-dimensional geometry adapts to ventricular contraction for optimal sealing. Embryologically, it develops from week 5 of gestation through the fusion of endocardial cushions into leaflets, with chordae and papillary muscles forming by weeks 8 to 15, highlighting its evolutionary adaptation for efficient left-sided heart performance. Disruptions in this apparatus, such as prolapse or stenosis, can lead to significant morbidity, underscoring the valve's clinical importance in cardiovascular health.

Anatomy

Leaflets

The mitral valve consists of two primary leaflets: the anterior leaflet, also referred to as the aortic or septal leaflet due to its continuity with the aortic valve, and the posterior leaflet, known as the mural leaflet for its position along the posterior ventricular wall. The anterior leaflet has a rectangular or trapezoidal shape, with a radial height of approximately 1824 mm and a circumferential attachment length of about 3 cm, making its surface area roughly 47 cm. In contrast, the posterior leaflet is crescent-shaped, with a shorter radial height of 1114 mm but a longer circumferential base of around 5 cm, resulting in a surface area of 23 cm; this configuration allows the anterior leaflet to be approximately twice as large overall and to cover about two-thirds of the valvular orifice during closure.
The posterior leaflet features distinctive scalloped edges, divided into three segments by indentations: the lateral P1 scallop adjacent to the anterolateral commissure, the central P2 scallop, and the medial P3 scallop near the posteromedial commissure. These scallops enhance flexibility and coaptation, with the rough zone (near the free edge) being broadest in the P2 segment. The anterior leaflet lacks such scallops and is subdivided descriptively into A1, A2, and A3 segments corresponding to the posterior scallops for surgical reference. The leaflets are connected to chordae tendineae, which provide essential support to prevent prolapse.
Histologically, the leaflets are thin (0.51 mm thick), largely avascular membranes covered by a continuous layer of endothelium on both atrial and ventricular surfaces, ensuring low thrombogenicity and facilitating nutrient diffusion. They exhibit a trilaminar structure: the fibrosa layer on the ventricular side, composed primarily of dense type I collagen fibers (about 74% of total collagen) aligned circumferentially for tensile strength; the central spongiosa layer of loosely arranged proteoglycans and glycosaminoglycans for shock absorption; and the atrialis layer with elastin fibers and type III collagen (24%) for elasticity. This composition balances rigidity and pliability, with the anterior leaflet featuring a thicker fibrosa relative to the more elastic posterior leaflet.
In normal adults, the mitral valve orifice measures approximately 34 cm in diameter, yielding a functional area of 46 cm during diastole, sufficient to accommodate peak left ventricular filling without obstruction. Variations in leaflet morphology occur in about 30% of individuals, primarily affecting the posterior leaflet with accessory scallops (e.g., between P3 and the inferoseptal commissure in 7% of cases) or fused scallops (e.g., P1P2 in 3%), leading to congenital asymmetries in size and shape. While the human mitral valve is characteristically bileaflet, comparative anatomy reveals differences in other mammals, such as more uniform leaflet heights in ovine and porcine models, though quadleaflet configurations are noted in select non-mammalian species. These human variations can influence surgical planning but rarely impair function.

Chordae tendineae

The chordae tendineae are tendinous cords that anchor the mitral valve leaflets to the papillary muscles, preventing leaflet prolapse into the left atrium during ventricular systole. These structures originate from the apices and sides of the anterolateral and posteromedial papillary muscles and insert onto the ventricular surfaces of the anterior and posterior leaflets, forming a supportive subvalvular apparatus. Their fan-like arrangement ensures balanced tension across the leaflets, with multiple cords diverging from each papillary muscle to multiple insertion points on the leaflets.
Chordae tendineae are classified based on their insertion sites and functions: marginal chordae attach to the free edges of the leaflets, basal chordae insert into the rough zone or belly of the leaflets, and strut chordae are thicker cords that provide primary support to the anterior leaflet. Marginal chordae, also known as primary chordae, are slender and numerous, ensuring precise alignment of leaflet edges during closure. Basal, or secondary, chordae connect to the midportion of the leaflets, while strut chordae, originating from the papillary muscle tips, are the most robust and resist excessive leaflet billowing. This classification reflects their specialized roles in maintaining leaflet geometry.
Composed primarily of densely packed collagen fibers surrounded by an elastin sheath, chordae tendineae exhibit high tensile strength, with individual cords capable of withstanding loads up to approximately 10-15 N before failure. The collagen core provides the primary structural integrity, allowing minimal elongation (typically less than 10%) under physiological pressures, while the elastin component contributes to flexibility. Biomechanically, these cords distribute systolic loads evenly across the leaflets, reducing localized stress concentrations through their redundant, fan-shaped configuration, which prevents catastrophic failure from damage to a single cord.
In normal anatomy, the chordae tendineae's integrity is crucial for competent valve closure, but pathological lengthening or rupture can disrupt this balance, serving as precursors to mitral regurgitation by allowing leaflet malcoaptation.

Papillary muscles

The papillary muscles are specialized muscular projections arising from the inner wall of the left ventricle, essential for maintaining the integrity of the mitral valve apparatus by providing dynamic tension to the chordae tendineae during cardiac contraction. These structures ensure proper coaptation of the valve leaflets, preventing regurgitation of blood into the left atrium.
There are two primary papillary muscles in the left ventricle: the anterolateral, which originates from the mid-to-apical region of the sternocostal (anterior) wall, and the posteromedial, which arises from the corresponding region of the diaphragmatic (inferior) wall. Both are typically conical or finger-like in shape, measuring approximately 2 to 3 cm in length with base diameters of 1 to 2 cm, though morphological variations such as truncation or bifurcation can occur. The papillary muscles serve as key insertion points for the chordae tendineae, which extend to the anterior and posterior leaflets of the mitral valve.
Innervation of the papillary muscles is provided via the left bundle branch of the cardiac conduction system, with electrical impulses propagating through Purkinje fibers to the base of these muscles, synchronizing their contraction with ventricular systole. This coordinated activation tensions the chordae tendineae, supporting efficient valve closure. Regarding vascular supply, the anterolateral papillary muscle benefits from a dual blood supply originating from the left anterior descending and left circumflex arteries, whereas the posteromedial papillary muscle relies primarily on the posterior descending artery, rendering it more susceptible to ischemic compromise.
Anatomical variations in the papillary muscles are not uncommon, with accessory or false papillae observed in approximately 10% of individuals, potentially altering the number or configuration of these structures while generally not impacting normal function.

Annulus

The mitral valve annulus is a dynamic fibrous ring situated at the atrioventricular junction, serving as the foundational support for the valve leaflets and contributing to overall valve stability and adaptability during the cardiac cycle. Its non-planar, saddle-shaped geometry features higher peaks in the anterior-posterior direction and lower troughs in the medial-lateral dimension, which optimizes stress distribution across the leaflets by reducing systolic strains, such as areal strain by up to 44% compared to a flat configuration. This hyperbolic paraboloid structure enhances mechanical efficiency and helps prevent excessive leaflet deformation.
Composed of a heterogeneous matrix rich in collagen types I and III, particularly concentrated in the septal region, the annulus also incorporates myocardial fibers predominantly in the lateral segments, enabling active contraction. During systole, it contracts by approximately 20-30%, with segmental variationssuch as 18% in lateral areas versus 4% in septalfacilitating valve closure and reducing the orifice area. In diastole, the annulus adopts a D-shaped profile, with a typical septo-lateral diameter of about 3 cm, which diminishes further during contraction to support efficient blood flow.
The annulus maintains structural continuity with adjacent cardiac components, including the aortic valve through the aorto-mitral fibrous curtain, which anchors it via fibrous trigones and influences left ventricular outflow dynamics. It also connects indirectly to the tricuspid annulus via shared fibrous elements in the anterior region, contributing to coordinated atrioventricular function. These interactions ensure integrated remodeling and stability across the valvular apparatus. The annulus provides basal support for the leaflets, anchoring their hinge points to maintain coaptation.
With advancing age, the annulus undergoes degenerative changes, including progressive calcification and dilation, which stiffen the structure and impair its contractile function, thereby predisposing to valvular dysfunction. These alterations, often linked to reduced cellularity and disorganized collagen, become more prevalent in the elderly and can compromise long-term valve competence.

Function

Role in the cardiac cycle

During diastole, the mitral valve opens passively due to the pressure gradient across the atrioventricular junction, with left atrial pressure (typically 4-12 mmHg) exceeding left ventricular pressure (near 0 mmHg in early diastole). This allows blood to flow from the left atrium into the relaxing ventricle, primarily during the rapid early filling phase (E wave) driven by the pressure difference, followed by a smaller contribution from atrial contraction (A wave).
In systole, ventricular contraction elevates left ventricular pressure above atrial pressure, causing the valve leaflets to coapt and seal the orifice, preventing regurgitation; this coaptation is maintained by tension in the chordae tendineae generated by synchronous contraction of the papillary muscles. The anterior and posterior leaflets, tethered by chordae tendineae to the papillary muscles, enable this precise motion to ensure efficient closure.
The mitral valve closes at the start of isovolumetric contraction, coinciding with the peak rate of ventricular pressure rise (dP/dt max), and reopens at the end of isovolumetric relaxation as ventricular pressure falls below atrial pressure; its closure produces the first heart sound (S1). In normal physiology, the mean transvalvular pressure gradient remains low at approximately 1.4 mmHg (range 0.6-2.7 mmHg), supporting unobstructed flow. Doppler assessment of mitral inflow typically shows an E/A velocity ratio of 0.75-1.5 in adults, reflecting balanced early and late diastolic filling.
Embryologically, the mitral valve develops from the fusion and remodeling of endocardial cushions in the atrioventricular canal, forming the leaflets that enable its cyclic function.

Hemodynamic regulation

The mitral valve plays a critical role in regulating left heart hemodynamics by maintaining an appropriate orifice area that facilitates efficient diastolic filling while minimizing resistance to blood flow from the left atrium to the left ventricle. In healthy adults, the normal mitral valve orifice area ranges from 4 to 6 cm, corresponding to an indexed area of approximately 3 to 5 cm/m body surface area, which supports adequate transvalvular flow without significant pressure gradients. This area can be calculated using the Gorlin formula: Area = (Flow / (44.3 mean gradient)), where flow is typically derived from cardiac output adjusted for the diastolic filling period.00431-8/pdf)
The valve's low resistance to flow in health, generally less than 0.5 mmHg/L/min, contributes to optimal diastolic filling compliance, allowing passive and active ventricular filling phases to occur with minimal energy loss. This low resistance ensures efficient ventricular-atrial coupling, where the valve prevents regurgitation during systole, thereby optimizing preload and supporting a typical stroke volume of approximately 70 mL per beat to maintain cardiac output. The integration of the mitral valve with left atrial reservoir function further enhances this process, as atrial contraction contributes 20% to 30% of total ventricular filling volume, augmenting end-diastolic volume without compromising forward flow.
Physiologic variations influence these hemodynamic parameters; for instance, aging leads to reduced left ventricular compliance, often resulting in E/A ratio reversal (where late diastolic filling exceeds early filling), which alters transvalvular flow patterns but remains within normal limits. During exercise, flow rates across the mitral valve can increase up to fivefold to meet elevated cardiac demands, reflecting the valve's adaptability in supporting augmented cardiac output while preserving low resistance and efficient coupling.

Clinical significance

Disorders

The mitral valve is susceptible to various pathological conditions that impair its structure and function, leading to significant hemodynamic consequences. These disorders encompass a range of etiologies, from inflammatory and degenerative processes to congenital malformations, each contributing to valvular dysfunction through distinct mechanisms such as fibrosis, calcification, or abnormal leaflet motion.
Mitral stenosis, characterized by narrowing of the valve orifice, is most commonly caused by rheumatic heart disease, where chronic inflammation leads to commissural fusion and thickening of the leaflets and chordae tendineae. In rheumatic mitral stenosis, the fusion predominantly affects the commissures, resulting in a funnel-shaped valve with restricted diastolic opening. Congenital forms are less frequent and include anomalies like the parachute mitral valve, where the chordae tendineae converge on a single papillary muscle, causing multilevel obstruction. Severity of mitral stenosis is often assessed using the Wilkins score, which grades the valve from 1 to 4 based on leaflet mobility, thickening, subvalvular apparatus thickening, and calcification, with higher scores indicating poorer suitability for certain interventions.
Mitral regurgitation involves incomplete valve closure, allowing backflow from the left ventricle to the left atrium during systole, and is classified as primary or secondary. Primary mitral regurgitation arises from intrinsic valve abnormalities, including degenerative changes, mitral valve prolapse, or infective endocarditis, where structural defects like leaflet perforation or vegetation directly impair coaptation. In contrast, secondary mitral regurgitation is functional, often due to ischemic heart disease or dilated cardiomyopathy, where ventricular remodeling displaces the papillary muscles and distorts the annulus without primary leaflet pathology. Severity of mitral regurgitation is assessed differently for primary and secondary forms. For primary MR, severe disease is typically defined by an effective regurgitant orifice area (EROA) 0.4 cm; for secondary MR, 0.2 cm, correlating with significant volume overload and adverse prognosis.
Mitral valve prolapse, a common disorder affecting up to 2-3% of the population, results from myxomatous degeneration of the valve apparatus, leading to redundant, thickened leaflets that billow into the atrium. Bileaflet involvement is more prevalent than isolated posterior leaflet prolapse, often associated with annular dilatation and chordal elongation. While generally benign, mitral valve prolapse carries a low risk of sudden cardiac death, estimated at less than 0.1% per year in the general affected population, though higher in subsets with ventricular arrhythmias. Recent research, including 2024 initiatives by the National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute (NHLBI), has highlighted genetic factors such as mutations in the FLNA gene, which disrupt cytoskeletal proteins and contribute to X-linked forms of prolapse with multisystem involvement.
Congenital anomalies of the mitral valve, occurring in approximately 0.2-0.4% of live births with congenital heart disease, include atrioventricular septal defects where a common atrioventricular junction leads to cleft mitral leaflets and regurgitation or stenosis. Double-orifice mitral valve, comprising about 1% of congenital mitral anomalies, features two separate orifices divided by a bridge of tissue, potentially causing obstruction or incompetence depending on the valve's form and associated defects.
Infective endocarditis preferentially affects the mitral valve due to its exposure to turbulent flow and endothelial damage sites, with bacterial adhesion often occurring on leaflets or the annular region. Mitral annular calcification exacerbates this risk by providing a nidus for vegetation formation, which can extend to form periannular abscesses through tissue invasion and inflammation. Such abscesses, particularly in the posterior annulus, may lead to conduction disturbances or rupture if untreated. Normal anatomical variations, such as annular asymmetry, may predispose certain individuals to accelerated calcification and subsequent complications.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis of mitral valve pathology begins with a thorough physical examination, which can reveal characteristic auscultatory findings and palpable signs indicative of stenosis or regurgitation. In mitral stenosis, a low-pitched mid-diastolic rumbling murmur, often following an opening snap, is best heard at the cardiac apex with the patient in the left lateral decubitus position, and may be accompanied by a palpable diastolic thrill at the apex in severe cases. For mitral regurgitation, a high-pitched holosystolic murmur is typically audible at the apex, radiating to the left axilla, and a systolic thrill may be palpable at the apex in moderate to severe disease. These findings, while suggestive, require confirmation through imaging and other modalities due to overlap with other cardiac conditions.
Echocardiography serves as the cornerstone for noninvasive assessment of mitral valve structure and function. Transthoracic echocardiography (TTE) is the initial modality of choice, enabling measurement of transvalvular pressure gradients via Doppler interrogation, with mean gradients exceeding 10 mmHg indicating severe stenosis. Transesophageal echocardiography (TEE) provides superior visualization of valve anatomy, particularly for identifying leaflet pathology, vegetation, or endocarditis, and is essential for procedural planning. Three-dimensional echocardiography enhances quantification of regurgitation severity, such as effective regurgitant orifice area (EROA) using the proximal isovelocity surface area (PISA) method, where an EROA greater than 0.4 cm signifies severe mitral regurgitation. Stress echocardiography, often with exercise or dobutamine, assesses functional changes in valve performance, detecting exercise-induced increases in regurgitation or gradients that correlate with symptom discrepancy.
Invasive cardiac catheterization is reserved for cases where noninvasive imaging is inconclusive or when concomitant coronary artery disease is suspected. It allows direct measurement of left atrial and ventricular pressures to calculate precise transvalvular gradients, confirming stenosis severity when echocardiography yields discordant results. Left ventriculography during angiography grades regurgitation on a scale of 1 to 4 based on left atrial opacification, with grade 3 or 4 indicating severe disease. Cardiac magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and computed tomography (CT) complement echocardiography in specific scenarios; CT excels at quantifying annular calcification, which can complicate interventions, while MRI assesses myocardial viability in ischemic mitral regurgitation through late gadolinium enhancement, identifying hibernating myocardium that may influence valve repair outcomes.
Laboratory evaluation includes biomarkers to gauge disease severity and associated heart failure. Elevated B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP) levels greater than 100 pg/mL correlate with increased left atrial pressure and worse prognosis in mitral regurgitation, serving as a marker of hemodynamic burden. Genetic testing is recommended in patients with mitral valve prolapse syndromes, such as those associated with connective tissue disorders like Marfan syndrome or mutations in genes including FLNA or DCHS1, to identify heritable forms and guide family screening.
Recent advancements in 2025 incorporate artificial intelligence (AI) to enhance echocardiographic interpretation, particularly for predicting mitral valve prolapse progression. AI algorithms applied to TTE videos achieve high accuracy in detecting prolapse, with deep learning models identifying subtle leaflet abnormalities and associating higher risk scores with adverse outcomes, as demonstrated in large cohort studies. These tools, trained on diverse datasets, improve early risk stratification beyond traditional metrics.

Treatment and management

Surgical interventions

Surgical interventions for mitral valve disease primarily involve open-heart procedures aimed at repairing or replacing the valve to address severe symptomatic regurgitation or stenosis. According to the 2020 ACC/AHA guidelines, surgery is indicated for patients with severe primary mitral regurgitation (stage D) who are symptomatic, or for asymptomatic patients with left ventricular dysfunction (ejection fraction 30%-60% or end-systolic dimension 40 mm), new-onset atrial fibrillation, or pulmonary hypertension (pulmonary artery systolic pressure >50 mm Hg). For severe mitral stenosis (valve area 1.5 cm, stage D) with unfavorable morphology for percutaneous intervention, surgical intervention is recommended, particularly in symptomatic patients. Preoperative diagnosis via echocardiography confirms the need for intervention by assessing valve pathology and hemodynamic severity. Repair is preferred over replacement when feasible, as it offers lower operative mortality (<1%), improved long-term survival, and reduced need for lifelong anticoagulation.
Mitral valve repair techniques for degenerative disease focus on preserving native tissue to restore valve competence. For posterior leaflet prolapse, quadrangular resection involves excising the diseased segment (typically P2 scallop) and approximating the edges, often combined with sliding plasty to reduce annular dimensions and prevent systolic anterior motion. Sliding plasty adjusts the height of adjacent leaflet segments to ensure tension-free closure, particularly useful in cases with annular dilation. These approaches achieve success rates exceeding 95% in degenerative cases at experienced centers, with 98% repair feasibility using standardized techniques.
When repair is not possible, mitral valve replacement uses prosthetic valves, with mechanical options preferred for younger patients due to superior durability. Mechanical valves, such as the St. Jude Medical bileaflet prosthesis, demonstrate excellent long-term performance, with reliable function observed over 20 years in mitral position follow-up studies. Bioprosthetic (tissue) valves offer an alternative for older patients or those avoiding anticoagulation, though their durability is limited to 10-15 years, with freedom from structural valve deterioration ranging from 58% to 93% at 15 years. Mechanical valves in the mitral position require lifelong anticoagulation with warfarin, targeting an international normalized ratio of 2.5-3.5 to prevent thromboembolism.
Surgical access typically employs median sternotomy for optimal exposure, though right mini-thoracotomy provides a less invasive open approach with comparable safety in low-risk patients. Operative mortality for isolated repair is under 1% in contemporary low-risk cohorts. Complications include paravalvular leak, occurring in 5-10% of replacements due to incomplete sealing, and stroke in 1-2% of cases, often linked to embolization during cardiopulmonary bypass. Recent registry data from 2023-2024 indicate strong long-term durability, with 5-year survival after replacement exceeding 75% in select groups and low reoperation rates for repairs.

Minimally invasive therapies

Transcatheter mitral valve repair (TMVR) represents a key minimally invasive approach for treating severe mitral regurgitation (MR) in high surgical risk patients, primarily through devices like the MitraClip system, which achieves edge-to-edge leaflet approximation to restore valve coaptation. This procedure typically reduces MR severity by at least two grades, achieving 2+ MR in over 90% of cases at 30 days post-procedure. The 2024 MATTERHORN randomized trial, comparing MitraClip transcatheter edge-to-edge repair (TEER) to surgical repair in patients with functional MR, demonstrated noninferiority in clinical efficacy at one year, with TEER showing superior safety through lower rates of major adverse events (17.7% vs. 31.5% at 30 days) and an estimated one-year survival rate approaching 80% in the TEER arm.
For patients unsuitable for repair, transcatheter mitral valve replacement offers full valve substitution using devices such as the Tendyne or Intrepid systems, targeted at high-risk individuals with prohibitive surgical risk (Society of Thoracic Surgeons score >8%). The Tendyne device, deployed via transapical access, has shown technical success in 94% of high-risk cases with severe MR and mitral annular calcification (MAC), achieving MR reduction to 1+ in 94% at discharge and freedom from heart failure hospitalization or all-cause mortality at 12 months in over 70% of patients. Similarly, the Intrepid TMVR system, accessed transfemorally or transapically, demonstrates feasibility in high-risk cohorts with severe symptomatic MR, yielding low 30-day mortality (around 7%) and sustained MR elimination in surviving patients at one year. In cases of calcific mitral stenosis with MAC, valve-in-MAC TMVR using balloon-expandable transcatheter valves provides an off-the-shelf option for high-risk patients, with procedural success rates exceeding 80% and reduced gradients post-implantation.
Robotic-assisted mitral valve repair, utilizing systems like the da Vinci, enables precise endoscopic interventions through small intercostal incisions, avoiding sternotomy and reducing perioperative morbidity in suitable high-risk candidates. Compared to conventional sternotomy, robotic approaches yield lower overall morbidity (approximately 5% vs. 15% in 2024 comparative studies), including reduced transfusion rates (under 10% vs. 20-30%) and shorter hospital stays, while maintaining high repair success rates over 95%.
The Lawrie technique, involving polymer artificial chordae implantation (neochordae), addresses mitral valve prolapse by replacing ruptured or elongated native chordae tendineae via minimally invasive off-pump transapical access, preserving the native leaflets. Multicenter trials up to 2025 report one-year procedural stability in over 90% of cases, with sustained MR reduction to 1+ and low reintervention rates (under 5%), particularly effective for isolated posterior leaflet prolapse in high-risk patients.
Post-procedure cardiac rehabilitation is integral, incorporating supervised exercise protocols that enhance functional recovery; a 2025 review recommends rehabilitation after mitral valve interventions, with phased programs typically starting 4-6 weeks post-procedure to improve cardiorespiratory fitness and quality of life, alongside reduced readmissions. Eligibility for these therapies centers on high surgical risk profiles, such as STS scores >8%, comorbid frailty, or anatomical unsuitability for open surgery, though complications like left ventricular outflow tract (LVOT) obstruction occur in about 5% of TMVR cases, necessitating pre-procedural CT screening for neo-LVOT area. In contrast to open surgical alternatives, these methods prioritize recovery in frail populations, with hybrid approaches emerging for complex anatomies.

Etymology and history

Etymology

The term "mitral valve" derives from the Latin word mitra, meaning a turban or mitre, the ceremonial headdress worn by bishops, owing to the valve's two cusps resembling the pointed, two-sided shape of a mitre when viewed in anatomical dissection. This descriptive nomenclature highlights the valve's bicuspid structure, distinguishing it from the tricuspid valve on the right side of the heart.
The English term "mitral valve" appeared in medical literature by the late 17th century, with the earliest recorded use in 1696.
An alternative designation, "bicuspid valve," emphasizes the valve's two cusps or leaflets and was used descriptively in early anatomical texts, including by Erasistratus in the 3rd century BC, who described it based on its form, and by Hieronymus Fabricius ab Aquapendente in 1603, reflecting the Latin roots bi- (two) and cuspis (point). Historically, it has also been called the left atrioventricular valve to denote its position between the left atrium and ventricle, underscoring its role in unidirectional blood flow.
The term "mitral" was applied by Andreas Vesalius in his 1543 treatise De humani corporis fabrica, where he explicitly likened the valve to a mitre. The terminology has remained stable in modern medical nomenclature, with no significant changes; for instance, congenital anomalies of the mitral valve are classified under ICD-10 code Q23.2 for congenital mitral stenosis. This persistence ensures consistency in clinical and anatomical contexts.

Historical developments

The earliest recognition of the atrioventricular (AV) valves, including the mitral valve, dates back to ancient Alexandria around 300 BC, when Herophilus conducted systematic human dissections and identified the structure and function of cardiac valves in preventing retrograde blood flow. In 1628, William Harvey's seminal work De Motu Cordis described the mitral valve's critical role in the systemic circulation, demonstrating through quantitative experiments that the heart acts as a pump directing blood unidirectionally through the valves, overturning Galenic theories of blood ebb and flow.
The 19th century advanced noninvasive assessment of mitral valve pathology with Ren Lannec's invention of the stethoscope in 1816 and his 1819 publication Trait de l'Auscultation Mdiate, which detailed the auscultation of heart murmurs associated with valvular stenosis and regurgitation, enabling clinical correlation of sounds to mitral disease. Concurrently, Rudolf Virchow's cellular pathology studies in the 1850s linked rheumatic fever to valvular inflammation and endocarditis, establishing the infectious-inflammatory etiology of mitral stenosis prevalent in young adults.
Surgical interventions began in the early 20th century, with Elliot Cutler's 1923 open commissurotomy at Brigham and Women's Hospital marking the first attempted repair for rheumatic mitral stenosis, though the patient survived only briefly; Henry Souttar's 1925 closed finger-fracture technique proved more enduring, allowing a patient to live 28 years post-procedure. Dwight Harken advanced the field in 1948 with the first successful closed mitral valvulotomy using a valvulotome, reducing operative mortality and enabling thousands of procedures for mitral stenosis during the rheumatic era. In the 1980s, Alain Carpentier's classification system for mitral regurgitation mechanisms and his "French correction" techniques standardized repair strategies, emphasizing leaflet preservation and annuloplasty for superior long-term outcomes over replacement.
Diagnostic innovations in the 1970s introduced two-dimensional echocardiography, pioneered by clinicians like Harvey Feigenbaum, which revolutionized mitral valve visualization by enabling real-time imaging of prolapse, regurgitation, and stenosis, supplanting invasive angiography. The transcatheter era emerged with the 2011 EVEREST II trial, demonstrating that percutaneous edge-to-edge repair using the MitraClip device was non-inferior to surgery in safety and efficacy for the primary composite endpoint at one year for patients with degenerative mitral regurgitation, with approximately 8% requiring subsequent surgery in the percutaneous group.
Recent milestones include the 2024 National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute (NHLBI) workshop on mitral valve prolapse, which convened experts to address gaps in pathogenesis, risk stratification, and therapies for this common disorder affecting up to 2% of the population. In 2025, the FDA approved the Tendyne transcatheter mitral valve replacement (TMVR) system for high-surgical-risk patients with severe mitral annular calcification or regurgitation, building on positive outcomes from trials like SUMMIT.
Insights into mitral valve embryology emerged in the 1940s through studies on endocardial cushion formation, where researchers like Patten, Kramer, and Barry observed that mesenchymal cushions derived from endocardial cells in the atrioventricular canal fuse to delineate the mitral leaflets, providing a foundational understanding of congenital defects like atrioventricular septal defects.
