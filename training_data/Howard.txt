Personal names

Given name

Howard is a masculine given name primarily used in English-speaking countries, derived from the Old English compound "hahweard," which translates to "high warden" or "chief warden," referring to a guardian or overseer of high status. Alternative etymologies trace it to the Norman French name "Huard" or "Houard," stemming from Germanic elements "*hug" (heart, mind) and "*hard" (brave, hardy), yielding meanings like "heart-brave" or "brave heart." It may also connect to the Old Norse "Hvarr," combining "h" (high) and "varr" (guardian), emphasizing "high guardian." These roots reflect its introduction to England following the Norman Conquest in 1066, initially as a personal name among Anglo-Norman elites before broader adoption.
The earliest records of Howard as a given name date to the late 11th century, appearing in the Domesday Book of 1086 as a tenant name, though its use as a distinct forename solidified in 13th-century English documents among nobility and commoners alike. By the medieval period, it gained traction through association with prominent families, such as the Howard earls and dukes of Norfolk, contributing to its enduring presence in British society. In the United States, immigration patterns from England and Ireland in the 19th century helped disseminate the name, with steady usage from the 1880s onward.
Cultural variations include the common diminutive "Howie," used affectionately in informal settings, particularly in American English. The name remains largely confined to English-speaking regions like the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, and Australia, with sporadic adoption in other European countries via English influence, but minimal global spread compared to more international names.
In terms of popularity trends, Howard entered U.S. Social Security Administration (SSA) records in 1880 at rank 44, with 357 male births that year. It rose steadily through the early 20th century, peaking during the 1920sa period influenced by cultural icons and traditional naming preferencesreaching rank 24 in 1920 with 7,446 births and maintaining top-30 status into the 1930s (e.g., rank 38 in the decade average, with over 51,000 total births). Post-World War II shifts toward shorter, more modern names led to a decline; by the 1950s, it fell out of the top 100, and usage continued dropping through the late 20th century. Recent SSA data shows a stabilization at lower ranks, with approximately 200-250 annual male births in the 2020s; for instance, 239 births in 2021 (rank 939) and 215 births in 2024 (rank 960, as of SSA data released May 2025). This represents about 0.01% of male births today, reflecting a vintage appeal rather than mainstream popularity. In 2023, there were 220 births (rank 950).

Surname

Howard is an English surname of multifaceted origins, primarily derived from the Norman personal name Huard or Houard, introduced to England following the Norman Conquest of 1066. This personal name stems from ancient Germanic elements hug ("heart, mind, spirit") and hard ("hardy, brave"), translating to "heart-brave" or "high guardian." An alternative occupational origin traces to Old English owu hierde, meaning "ewe-herd" or keeper of sheep, particularly ewes valued for milk production. Locational associations are less dominant but include derivations from places like Howarth in Lancashire, while variants such as Hayward (from "hay warden") and Howarth reflect similar phonetic evolutions in Middle English.
The surname's historical prominence is embodied in the noble Howard family, who rose to the dukedom of Norfolk in 1483 when John Howard was created the 1st Duke by King Richard III, inheriting the title through his mother's Mowbray lineage. This elevated the family to England's premier dukedom, with key figures including Thomas Howard, the 2nd Duke (14431524), who fought at the Battle of Bosworth and later led English forces to victory at Flodden in 1513, restoring the family's fortunes after temporary attainder. The Howards maintained their status through strategic marriages and service to the crown, navigating Tudor politics despite periods of imprisonment and execution, such as that of the 4th Duke in 1572.
In the 17th century, branches of the Howard family and others bearing the surname migrated to the American colonies, with early settlers like John Howard arriving in Virginia in 1622 and Samuel Howard in 1635, contributing to the establishment of communities in the New World. Globally, Howard ranks as the 1,179th most common surname, borne by approximately 1 in 16,115 people, with highest concentrations in English-speaking nations: the United States (264,826 bearers, approximately 0.086% of the population per 2010 census data), the United Kingdom (around 54,000, roughly 0.09%), and Australia (about 23,000, or 0.09%). Recent genetic and demographic studies confirm its persistence in these populations at frequencies of 0.10.2%.
As a mark of nobility, the Howard family's heraldry features a coat of arms blazoned as gules, a bend between six cross-crosslets fitchy argent, symbolizing their ancient lineage and often quartered with other noble arms in ducal achievements. This design, granted to early Howards and retained by the Dukes of Norfolk, underscores their enduring status as England's highest-ranking non-royal peers.

Howard Aiken (March 8, 1900 March 14, 1973) was an American physicist and computing pioneer best known for conceiving and developing the Harvard Mark I, the first large-scale automatic digital computer in the United States. Born in Hoboken, New Jersey, Aiken earned his PhD in physics from Harvard University in 1939 and partnered with IBM engineers to construct the electromechanical device, which spanned 50 feet and weighed nearly five tons when completed in 1944. The machine performed complex calculations for the U.S. Navy during World War II and laid foundational work for modern computing; Aiken later oversaw the development of improved versions, including the Harvard Mark II, at Harvard until his retirement in 1961.
Howard Ashman (May 17, 1950 March 14, 1991) was an American playwright, lyricist, and director whose collaborations with composer Alan Menken revitalized Disney's animated musicals in the late 1980s and early 1990s. Born in Baltimore, Maryland, Ashman earned an MFA from Indiana University and co-founded the WPA Theatre in New York, where he directed innovative productions like the off-Broadway hit Little Shop of Horrors in 1982. He joined Disney in 1988, penning lyrics for films such as The Little Mermaid (1989), including the Academy Award-winning "Under the Sea," and Beauty and the Beast (1991), which earned him a posthumous Oscar; Ashman died of AIDS-related complications at age 40, shortly before the latter film's release.
Howard Gardner (born July 11, 1943) is an American developmental psychologist renowned for his theory of multiple intelligences, which posits that human intelligence encompasses distinct modalities beyond traditional IQ measures. Born in Scranton, Pennsylvania, to Jewish immigrants who fled Nazi Germany, Gardner received his PhD from Harvard University in 1971 and has served as the John H. and Elisabeth A. Hobbs Professor of Cognition and Education there since 1986. His 1983 book Frames of Mind introduced the framework, identifying eight (later nine) intelligences like linguistic, logical-mathematical, and interpersonal, influencing educational practices worldwide by emphasizing diverse learning styles.
Howard Hughes (December 24, 1905 April 5, 1976) was an American aviator, aerospace engineer, and business magnate who achieved fame for his speed records, innovative aircraft designs, and Hollywood productions. Born in Humble, Texas, Hughes inherited the Hughes Tool Company at age 18 following his parents' deaths and used its wealth to enter filmmaking, producing and directing epics like Hell's Angels (1930), which featured groundbreaking aerial sequences. In aviation, he founded Hughes Aircraft Company in 1932, set the transcontinental air speed record in 1938, and completed a round-the-world flight in 91 hours in 1938; his H-4 Hercules "Spruce Goose," the largest wooden airplane ever built, flew once in 1947, symbolizing his ambitious engineering pursuits amid growing personal reclusiveness due to chronic pain and germaphobia.
Howard Schultz (born July 19, 1953) is an American businessman who transformed Starbucks from a regional coffee roaster into a global retail giant emphasizing community and premium experiences. Born in Brooklyn, New York, to a working-class family, Schultz grew up in public housing and earned a bachelor's degree in communications from (/page/Northern Michigan University) on a football scholarship. Joining Starbucks in 1982 as director of operations, he left to start his own coffee bar chain before acquiring Starbucks in 1987, expanding it to over 30,000 stores worldwide by focusing on employee benefits like health insurance and ethical sourcing; he served as CEO from 1987 to 2000 and again from 2008 to 2017.
Howard Zinn (August 24, 1922 January 27, 2010) was an American historian, author, and social activist whose works challenged traditional narratives of U.S. history by centering the perspectives of marginalized groups. Born in Brooklyn, New York, to immigrant Jewish parents, Zinn served as a bombardier in World War II before earning a PhD from Columbia University in 1958 and teaching at Spelman College, where he mentored civil rights leaders. His seminal 1980 book A People's History of the United States, which sold over 2 million copies, reframed events from the viewpoints of workers, women, and people of color, influencing activism and education; Zinn also participated in protests against the Vietnam War and nuclear weapons throughout his career at Boston University.

Historical Figures

Catherine Howard (c. 15231542) was the fifth wife of King Henry VIII of England, marrying him in July 1540 after serving as a lady-in-waiting to his fourth wife, Anne of Cleves; she was likely between 17 and 19 years old at the time of the marriage. As a member of the prominent Howard family, which held the title of Duke of Norfolk, her union elevated the family's influence at court, but it ended tragically when she was executed for adultery and treason in February 1542 at the Tower of London. Her brief queenship highlighted the volatile politics of the Tudor court and the Howard family's recurring ties to royal marriages.
John Howard (17261790) was an English philanthropist and social reformer renowned for his pioneering work in prison reform and public health. Appointed High Sheriff of Bedfordshire in 1773, he inspected prisons across England and Europe, documenting appalling conditions such as overcrowding, lack of sanitation, and failure to separate prisoners by gender or crime type in his influential 1777 book The State of the Prisons in England and Wales. His advocacy led to the Penitentiary Act of 1779, which aimed to establish humane prisons focused on rehabilitation through labor and reflection, and he died of typhus while aiding victims during a plague outbreak in Russia.

Politics

John Howard (b. 1939) served as the 25th Prime Minister of Australia from March 1996 to December 2007, the second-longest tenure after Robert Menzies, during which his Liberal-National Coalition government implemented economic reforms including the introduction of a goods and services tax in 2000. A lawyer by training and member of Parliament for Bennelong since 1974, Howard's administration emphasized fiscal conservatism, workplace deregulation, and support for the U.S.-led Iraq War in 2003, while navigating events like the 1999 republic referendum and the 2008 global financial crisis response. His policies on Indigenous affairs, including the 2007 Northern Territory intervention, remain debated for their impact on reconciliation efforts.

Arts and Entertainment

Robert E. Howard (19061936) was an American pulp fiction writer best known for creating the character Conan the Barbarian, whose adventures in the fictional Hyborian Age debuted in Weird Tales magazine in 1932 and influenced the sword-and-sorcery genre. Born in Peaster, Texas, and raised in Cross Plains, he produced over 300 stories across genres like fantasy, historical adventure, and boxing before his suicide at age 30, often drawing from his love of history and poetry. His works, including series featuring characters like Kull of Atlantis and Solomon Kane, have been adapted into comics, films, and games, cementing his legacy in speculative fiction.
Moe Howard (18971975), born Moses Harry Horwitz, was the leader and straight man of the Three Stooges, a seminal American comedy team that produced nearly 200 short films from 1934 to 1959, blending slapstick violence with vaudeville timing. Starting in silent films around 1910 and forming the Stooges with brothers Shemp and Curly (Jerry Howard), he managed the group's career through lineup changes and appeared in 25 feature films and TV episodes, embodying the authoritarian "boss stooge" archetype. His contributions helped define Hollywood's short-subject comedy era, with the Stooges' enduring popularity leading to revivals and honors like a Hollywood Walk of Fame star in 1983.
Ron Howard (b. 1954) is an American filmmaker and former child actor who gained fame as Opie Taylor on The Andy Griffith Show (19601968) before transitioning to directing, winning the Academy Award for Best Director for A Beautiful Mind (2001). Born in Duncan, Oklahoma, to actors Rance and Jean Howard, he starred in Happy Days (19741980) as Richie Cunningham and helmed blockbusters like Apollo 13 (1995), Rush (2013), and Solo: A Star Wars Story (2018), often focusing on real-life dramas through his production company Imagine Entertainment, co-founded in 1986. His work has grossed over $4 billion worldwide, earning him multiple Emmys and a 2009 Irving G. Thalberg Memorial Award.
Terrence Howard (b. 1969) is an American actor acclaimed for his portrayal of DJay in Hustle & Flow (2005), earning an Academy Award nomination for Best Actor and a Screen Actors Guild Award for his role in Crash (2004). Raised in Cleveland, Ohio, after early years in Chicago, he debuted in The Jacksons: An American Dream (1992) and later starred as Lucious Lyon in the Fox series Empire (20152020), blending music and drama while releasing his own album Shine Through It (2008). His filmography includes Iron Man (2008) as James Rhodes and The Butler (2013), showcasing his range in supporting roles across genres.
Bryce Dallas Howard (b. 1981) is an American actress and director known for her roles as Claire Dearing in the Jurassic World trilogy (20152022) and as Hilly Holbrook in The Help (2011), earning a Critics' Choice nomination. Daughter of director Ron Howard and raised in Westchester County, New York, she trained at the Stella Adler Conservatory and NYU's Tisch School before her breakout in Village of the Damned (1995) as a child, later directing episodes of The Mandalorian (20192023) and the film The Mandalorian & Grogu (upcoming 2026). Her advocacy for sustainable fashion and body positivity has complemented her on-screen presence in projects like Argylle (2024).

Sports

Ryan Howard (b. 1979) was a dominant first baseman for the Philadelphia Phillies, winning the National League Most Valuable Player Award in 2006 after hitting 58 home runs and driving in 149 RBIs, leading the team to a World Series title in 2008. Drafted in the fifth round out of Missouri State University in 2001, the St. Louis native set Phillies records for single-season home runs and holds the franchise mark for career RBIs with 1,194 over 13 MLB seasons, retiring in 2019 after earning three Silver Slugger awards. Post-retirement, he has invested in ventures like the Philadelphia Fusion esports team and authored children's books promoting perseverance.
Dwight Howard (b. 1985) is an American basketball center inducted into the Naismith Memorial Basketball Hall of Fame in 2025, who won the NBA Defensive Player of the Year award three consecutive times (20092011), the first player to achieve this, and was an eight-time All-Star during his 18-season NBA career spanning over 20 years including international play. Drafted first overall by the Orlando Magic in 2004 straight from Southwest Atlanta Christian Academy, the Atlanta native led the league in rebounds five times and blocks twice, contributing to the 2020 Lakers championship while earning All-NBA honors eight times and gold medals with Team USA at the 2008 and 2012 Olympics. Known as "Superman" for his athleticism, he continued playing after his NBA tenure, including with the Taoyuan Pauian Pilots in Taiwan's T1 League (20222023), Strong Group Philippines in the 2024 Dubai International Basketball Championship, and the Los Angeles Riot in the BIG3 league (2025).

Nicknamed Howie

The nickname "Howie" serves as an informal diminutive of the given name Howard, originating as a common shortening in 20th-century American English and extending to other English-speaking contexts. This affectionate form has been widely adopted in popular culture, particularly among public figures in entertainment, sports, and politics who are best known by it rather than their full names.
Howie Mandel, born Howard Michael Mandel on November 29, 1955, in Toronto, Canada, is a comedian, television host, and actor renowned for hosting the game show Deal or No Deal from 2005 to 2009. He has also served as a judge on America's Got Talent since 2010, leveraging his nickname in his high-energy, germaphobic persona that draws from his personal experiences. Mandel is a prominent advocate for obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) awareness, having publicly shared his diagnosis and partnered with organizations like the Anxiety and Depression Association of America (ADAA) to promote treatment and reduce stigma.
Howie Long, born Howard Matthew Moses Long on January 6, 1960, in Somerville, Massachusetts, is a former National Football League (NFL) defensive end who played primarily for the Los Angeles Raiders from 1983 to 1993, earning eight Pro Bowl selections and induction into the Pro Football Hall of Fame in 2000. After retiring, he transitioned to acting, appearing in films such as Broken Arrow (1996) alongside John Travolta, where his nickname became synonymous with his tough, charismatic on-screen presence. Long has since worked as a sports analyst for Fox Sports, further embedding "Howie" in his broadcasting career.
Howie Day, born Howard Kern Day on January 15, 1981, in Bangor, Maine, is an American singer-songwriter who rose to prominence in the early 2000s with his acoustic pop style. He is best known for the hit single "Collide" from his 2003 album Stop All the World Now, which peaked at number 20 on the Billboard Hot 100 and became a staple in radio play. Day's use of the nickname "Howie" has been central to his indie music branding, including during tours and album promotions.
Howie Hawkins, born Howard Gresham Hawkins III on December 8, 1952, in San Francisco, California, is an American labor activist and politician who served as the Green Party's presidential nominee in the 2020 United States election, receiving approximately 407,000 votes. A longtime environmental advocate and co-founder of the Green Party of New York, Hawkins has run multiple campaigns under his nickname, emphasizing ecosocialist policies in his political identity. His 2020 bid highlighted third-party challenges, with "Howie" often used in campaign materials and media coverage.
Internationally, Howie Severino, born Horacio Gorospe Severino on July 18, 1961, in Manila, Philippines, is a broadcast journalist, documentarian, and podcaster known for his investigative work with GMA Network since 2001. He has produced over 200 documentaries, earning awards for coverage of social issues like urban poverty and human rights, and frequently uses "Howie" in his on-air persona and public appearances. Severino's nickname underscores his approachable style in Filipino media, including hosting the news program i-Witness.

Fictional characters

In literature and comics

One prominent fictional character named Howard in comics is Howard the Duck, an anthropomorphic duck created by writer Steve Gerber and artist Val Mayerik for Marvel Comics. He first appeared in Adventure into Fear #19 in 1973, hailing from the planet Duckworld and stranded on Earth, where his cynical worldview and misadventures satirize American society, politics, and pop culture through absurd, countercultural narratives. Howard's series, which ran from 1976 to 1979, established him as a reluctant anti-hero and everyman figure, influencing later satirical works in the medium and earning a dedicated cult following for its blend of humor and social commentary.
In literature, Howard Roark serves as the protagonist of Ayn Rand's 1943 novel The Fountainhead, embodying the ideal of uncompromising individualism as a visionary architect who refuses to conform to traditional styles or societal pressures. Roark's character arc, spanning his expulsion from architecture school to the defense of his innovative designs, highlights themes of creative integrity and self-reliance, making him a seminal figure in objectivist philosophy and a symbol of the guardian of personal principles against collectivist forces. The novel's portrayal of Roark has had lasting cultural impact, inspiring discussions on ethics, innovation, and heroism in modern literature.
A lesser-known example appears in the graphic novel series Howard Lovecraft, created by writer Bruce Brown, with illustrations by Renzo Podesta for the first volume and subsequent artists for later entries, for Arcana Studio, beginning with Howard Lovecraft and the Frozen Kingdom in 2009. In this Lovecraftian-inspired tale, young Howard Lovecraft, a boy from Providence, Rhode Island, discovers the Necronomicon and embarks on interdimensional adventures to protect his family and world from cosmic horrors, drawing on the name's etymological ties to guardianship while exploring themes of inherited legacy and youthful resilience. The series, which includes sequels like Howard Lovecraft and the Kingdom of Madness (2013), reimagines eldritch mythos for younger audiences through Howard's everyman perspective as an ordinary child thrust into extraordinary, guardian-like roles.
Across these works, characters named Howard often embody tropes of the reluctant everyman or steadfast guardian, reflecting the name's Old English roots meaning "high guardian" or "brave heart," as seen in Howard the Duck's sardonic navigation of human absurdities, Roark's defense of his vision, and young Lovecraft's protection against otherworldly threats. These portrayals span satirical comics, philosophical novels, and horror-tinged graphic novels, illustrating the versatility of the name in print narratives pre-2025.

In film, television, and theater

One prominent fictional character named Howard in film is Howard Beale from the 1976 satirical drama Network, directed by Sidney Lumet. Portrayed by Peter Finch, who posthumously won the Academy Award for Best Actor, Beale is a veteran television news anchor who suffers a public on-air breakdown, delivering the iconic monologue "I'm as mad as hell, and I'm not going to take this anymore!" after learning of his impending dismissal. The character serves as a critique of media sensationalism and corporate greed, with Beale's rants boosting ratings until his unhinged persona is exploited by network executives; the performance and role were widely praised for their intensity, contributing to the film's three Oscars and enduring cultural impact.
In theater, Howard Wagner appears in Arthur Miller's 1949 Pulitzer Prize-winning play Death of a Salesman. As Willy Loman's young boss at the sales firm, Wagner inherits the company from his late father, whom Willy once admired, and represents the impersonal, ruthless side of modern business by callously firing the aging salesman despite their long history. Wagner's brief but pivotal scene underscores themes of obsolescence and the American Dream's betrayal, with the role often portrayed as detached and self-absorbed in stage productions.
Howard Cunningham is a central figure in the long-running American sitcom Happy Days, which aired on ABC from 1974 to 1984. Played by Tom Bosley, who earned three Emmy nominations for the role, Cunningham is the patriarchal head of a wholesome 1950s Milwaukee family, owning a hardware store and providing moral guidance to his children Richie and Joanie amid their adolescent adventures. As the archetypal sitcom father, his warm, level-headed demeanor contrasted with the era's flashy trends, helping the series become a cultural phenomenon that popularized nostalgia for mid-20th-century Americana.
In television, Howard Wolowitz is a key character in the CBS sitcom The Big Bang Theory, which ran from 2007 to 2019. Portrayed by Simon Helberg, who received four Emmy nominations, Wolowitz is an aerospace engineer at Caltech lacking a PhD, known for his overconfident yet awkward attempts at romance, distinctive wardrobe, and contributions to space missions like a stint on the International Space Station. Evolving from comic relief with mommy issues to a devoted husband and father, the character highlights themes of intellectual camaraderie and personal growth among the show's ensemble of scientists.
Howard the Duck features in the 1986 science fiction comedy film adaptation of the Marvel Comics character, directed by Willard Huyck and produced by George Lucas. Voiced by Chip Zien and physically performed by Ed Gale and Jordan Prentice, Howard is a cigar-smoking, anthropomorphic duck from the planet Duckworld who is teleported to Earth, where he teams up with aspiring rock musician Beverly Switzler (Lea Thompson) to thwart an alien invasion. Despite its basis in Steve Gerber's satirical comics, the film was a critical and commercial failure upon release, grossing only $37 million against a $37 million budget, but has since gained a cult following for its quirky humor and ahead-of-its-time effects. Howard the Duck has since appeared in the Marvel Cinematic Universe, with cameos in Guardians of the Galaxy (2014) and its sequel Guardians of the Galaxy Vol. 2 (2017), as well as in the animated series What If...? (2021, 2023), voiced by Seth Green.

Elsewhere

In the United Kingdom, the Howardian Hills form an Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty in North Yorkshire, designated in 1987 to preserve its rolling countryside, woodlands, and archaeological sites spanning 204 square kilometers (79 square miles). This landscape, nestled between the North York Moors National Park and the Vale of York, supports diverse habitats including limestone dales and ancient woodlands, managed for sustainable agriculture, recreation, and biodiversity conservation. Nearby, Castle Howard stands as a prominent Baroque estate in the same region, built from 1699 for the Howard family and encompassing extensive gardens, lakes, and follies that reflect 18th-century architectural grandeur.
In Canada, Howard Township in Ontario was an agricultural municipality in Kent County, known for its fertile soils and farming communities focused on crops like soybeans and corn, until its amalgamation into the Municipality of Chatham-Kent on January 1, 1998, as part of provincial restructuring that combined 23 entities to streamline services. The area remains a key part of southwestern Ontario's breadbasket, with over 70% of its land historically converted to farmland since European settlement. Further west, Howard Lake in British Columbia lies approximately 50 kilometers northeast of 100 Mile House, a small alpine lake at high elevation surrounded by coniferous forests, serving as a popular site for fishing, boating (with a 7.5 kW engine limit), and camping since its development as a recreation area.
Australia's Howard Springs, located in the Northern Territory about 30 kilometers southeast of Darwin, is a nature park established as the territory's first official reserve in 1957, featuring monsoon vine forests, waterfalls, and walking trails around thermal springs. The site holds significant World War II history, having served as rest and recreation camps for Australian and American servicemen, with a concrete weir built in 1944 by Royal Australian Engineers to create swimming pools that enhanced its recreational appeal.

Institutions and organizations

Educational institutions

Howard University, founded in 1867 in Washington, D.C., stands as a private historically Black research university and a premier historically Black college or university (HBCU) dedicated to advancing education and social justice. Chartered by the U.S. Congress and named for Major General Oliver Otis Howard, founder of the Freedmen's Bureau, it began operations in a single-frame building with a mission to eliminate racial and socioeconomic inequities through higher education. The institution has expanded significantly, now spanning a 256-acre urban campus with facilities including a 400-bed hospital, and has awarded more than 100,000 degrees while enrolling approximately 13,400 students from all 50 states, the District of Columbia, and over 70 countries as of 2024. As of fall 2024, Howard's undergraduate enrollment reached 11,519 students, reflecting an upward trend; the university achieved its strategic goal of 12,500 total students by 2024 and aims for 15,000 by 2030, supported by a 91% freshman retention rate as of 2021.
Central to Howard's legacy are its professional programs in law and medicine, which have shaped civil rights and healthcare equity. The Howard University School of Law, established in 1869 as the university's first professional school, evolved into a hub for legal activism under leaders like Dean Charles Hamilton Houston (19301935), who trained students as "social engineers" to combat injustice. Thurgood Marshall, who earned his LL.B. in 1933 as the top graduate, credited his Howard education for preparing him to argue landmark cases, including Brown v. Board of Education (1954), before becoming the first Black U.S. Supreme Court Justice in 1967. The College of Medicine, opened in 1868 with its inaugural class of eight students (seven Black), was among the earliest institutions to train African American physicians and endured the 1910 Flexner Report as one of only two surviving Black medical schools, emphasizing community health and research disparities.
Howard's impact extends to fostering diversity and leadership, ranking #88 among national universities and #17 for undergraduate teaching in the 2026 U.S. News & World Report edition. Its student body reflects broad representation, with roughly 70% African American graduate enrollment alongside 30% non-African American students, including substantial international cohorts, underscoring its role in producing Black professionals in medicine, law, engineering, and beyond.
Other notable U.S. institutions include Howard College, established in 1841 in Marion, Alabama, by the Alabama Baptist State Convention as a men's liberal arts college named for 18th-century English prison reformer John Howard. Relocating to Birmingham's East Lake suburb in 1887 amid social and economic shifts, it admitted its first women in 1896, gained accreditation in 1920, and moved to a modern Homewood campus in 1957 before renaming to Samford University in 1965 in honor of benefactor Frank Park Samford Sr., while preserving the Howard College of Arts and Sciences as its core undergraduate unit.
Various Howard High Schools highlight regional educational histories, particularly in desegregation efforts; for instance, Howard High School in Ellicott City, Maryland, operated within Howard County's segregated public school system until full desegregation in 1965, when the all-Black Harriet Tubman High School closed and students integrated into formerly white institutions like Howard High.
Internationally, the Howard Gardens College of Art in Cardiff, Wales, served as a key site for creative education from the mid-20th century, with a purpose-built facility opening in 1970 before merging in 1976 with Llandaff College of Technology and Cardiff College of Education to form the South Glamorgan Institute of Higher Education, later evolving into Cardiff Metropolitan University.

Other organizations

The Howard Hughes Medical Institute (HHMI), established in 1953 by aviator and industrialist Howard Hughes, operates as a leading nonprofit biomedical research organization and philanthropy dedicated to advancing fundamental research in the life sciences for the benefit of humanity. With consolidated diversified net assets of $25.6 billion as of fiscal year 2024, HHMI supports over 1,000 investigators at universities and medical schools across the United States through flexible, long-term funding that emphasizes investigator-driven science rather than specific projects. The institute has played a pivotal role in genomics research, providing key grants that enabled breakthroughs such as the development of CRISPR-Cas9 technologies and large-scale sequencing initiatives, while its investigators have contributed to more than 30 Nobel Prizes in Physiology or Medicine and Chemistry since 1978.
In the United Kingdom, the Howard League for Penal Reform, founded in 1866 as the Howard Association in honor of 18th-century prison reformer John Howard, is the world's oldest penal reform charity focused on creating less crime, safer communities, and fewer people in prison through advocacy, research, and policy influence. The organization campaigns vigorously against practices like solitary confinement, which it argues exacerbates mental health issues and recidivism, and has influenced landmark reforms such as the reduction of child imprisonment and improvements in women's prisons. As of 2025, the Howard League continues to submit evidence to parliamentary inquiries on prison overcrowding and justice system failures, maintaining its commitment to humane and effective penal policies.
Additionally, the Howard County Arts Council in Maryland, established to promote artistic expression, enriches local communities via programs like the biennial Art Howard County exhibit and the ARTsites outdoor sculpture initiative, which in 2025 featured public installations to enhance accessibility to contemporary art.

Other uses

Transportation

The USS Howard (DDG-83 is an Arleigh Burke-class guided-missile destroyer of the United States Navy, named in honor of Medal of Honor recipient Gunnery Sergeant Jimmie E. Howard. Commissioned on October 20, 2001, after being laid down on December 9, 1998, and launched on November 20, 1999, the vessel displaces approximately 9,200 tons fully loaded and achieves speeds exceeding 30 knots, equipped with advanced electronic warfare and undersea warfare capabilities. It has participated in multiple deployments, including a 2007 mission in the Persian Gulf where it helped secure Iraq's offshore oil terminals alongside coalition partners, and a 2017 operation in the Arabian Gulf involving maritime security and humanitarian efforts such as rescuing an entangled sea turtle. Since around 2020, the ship has been forward-deployed to Yokosuka, Japan, as part of Destroyer Squadron 15 in the U.S. Seventh Fleet, conducting patrols in the South China Sea and port visits including Otaru, Japan, in February 2025 to strengthen alliances.
An earlier USS Howard (DD-179) served as a Wickes-class destroyer during World War II, commissioned in 1920 and later converted for minesweeping duties as DMS-7, with a displacement of 1,154 tons and involvement in torpedo exercises and Pacific operations until decommissioning in 1946.
The H-1 Racer, an experimental single-engine monoplane designed by Howard Hughes and Richard Palmer and built by Glenn Odekirk in 1935, holds a prominent place in aviation history for setting speed records. Powered by a Pratt & Whitney R-1535 Twin Wasp Junior radial engine, the aircraft featured interchangeable wingsa short span of 25 feet (7.6 meters) for high-speed flights and a longer 31 feet 9 inches (9.7 meters) set for landingswith an overall length of 339 inches and construction using wood wings over an aluminum fuselage. On September 13, 1935, Hughes piloted it to a world landplane speed record of 352 mph (567 km/h) over Santa Ana, California, surpassing previous marks. In 1937, it established a transcontinental record by flying from Los Angeles to Newark in 7 hours, 28 minutes, and 25 seconds at an average 335 mph (535 km/h), demonstrating practical long-distance capabilities; the aircraft is preserved at the National Air and Space Museum's Udvar-Hazy Center.
The Howard is a traditional Chesapeake Bay skipjack, a V-bottomed deadrise sloop used for oyster dredging, built in 1909 at Deep Creek, Virginia, measuring 45 feet in length with two sails and a centerboard design. Homeported at Deal Island in Somerset County, Maryland, it remains one of approximately 35 surviving examples of this vessel type (as of 2025) and one of only 19 constructed before 1912, representing the last commercial sailing fleet in the United States; it was listed on the National Register of Historic Places in 1985 as part of the Chesapeake Bay Skipjack Fleet Multiple Property Documentation.
Howard Street in Baltimore has long functioned as a key transit corridor, initially hosting horse-drawn omnibus and streetcar lines from the 1840s that connected downtown to northern neighborhoods like Druid Hill Park. By the 1890s, electric streetcars operated along the route, facilitating retail and suburban growth in the surrounding commercial district. In the late 20th century, the street was converted to accommodate the Central Light Rail line starting in 1989, with tracks replacing the historic streetcar infrastructure while maintaining its role as the primary public transit artery northwest of the Inner Harbor.

Brands and media

Howard Johnson's, founded in 1925 by Howard D. Johnson as a soda fountain and ice cream parlor in Quincy, Massachusetts, evolved into one of America's first major restaurant chains by emphasizing consistent quality and 28 flavors of ice cream. The business expanded through franchising starting in 1935 and introduced motor lodges in 1953, combining restaurants and lodging to cater to highway travelers. At its peak in the mid-20th century, the chain operated over 1,000 locations, becoming a cultural icon symbolized by its distinctive orange roofs and steeples that dotted U.S. interstates, evoking family road trips and reliable roadside dining. Acquired by Wyndham Hotels & Resorts, the brand marked its 100th anniversary in 2025 with limited-edition commemorative items and new developments, including its first hotel in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia, while maintaining around 300 economy lodging properties globally.
Howard Industries, a Mississippi-based manufacturer of electrical equipment, was established in 1968 by Billy W. Howard Sr. after leaving General Electric, initially focusing on industrial products before expanding into power solutions. The company's Power Solutions division has become a leading producer of distribution transformers, with more than 10 million single-phase and three-phase units deployed across the U.S. and internationally, supporting electrical grid infrastructure. In 2025, Howard Industries announced a $236 million expansion across facilities in Clarke, Jones, and Simpson counties, enhancing transformer production and creating jobs in a region central to its operations. This growth underscores its status as a billion-dollar, family-owned enterprise with a global footprint in electrical manufacturing.
The Howard Stern Show, launched in 1983 and syndicated nationally in the 1980s, pioneered the shock jock format through provocative humor, celebrity interviews, and boundary-pushing discussions on topics like sex and politics, influencing modern talk radio. Stern's move to SiriusXM in 2006 via a landmark $500 million deal allowed uncensored content, solidifying his role as a media disruptor with a loyal audience. By late 2025, amid speculation about his contract's expiration at the end of the year, Stern returned from a summer hiatus with a prank-laden show that played on renewal rumors, though no extension has been confirmed as negotiations remain ongoing.
Howard the Duck, Marvel Comics' satirical anthropomorphic character created by Steve Gerber and Val Mayerik in 1973's Adventure into Fear #19, received its own series in 1976, blending absurdity and social commentary as Howard navigated human society from Cleveland, Ohio. The original run, illustrated by Gene Colan and others, achieved strong sales, with the debut issue printing around 275,000 copies and averaging about 200,000 per issue, reflecting its cult appeal amid 1970s counterculture. Revived in later series like Chip Zdarsky's 2015 volume, where Howard worked as a private investigator, the property has impacted Marvel's multiverse storytelling, celebrating its 50th anniversary in 2023 with tales of interdimensional adventures.
