Immunotherapy
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Immunotherapy is a medical treatment that harnesses or modifies the body's immune system to fight diseases, including cancer, allergies, autoimmune conditions, and certain infections. By either stimulating, suppressing, or redirecting immune responses, it aims to enhance the body's natural defenses against pathogens, abnormal cells, or overreactions to harmless substances. This approach contrasts with traditional treatments like chemotherapy or antibiotics by targeting the immune mechanisms directly, offering potential for long-term remission in responsive cases.
The history of immunotherapy spans over a century, beginning with early observations in the late 19th century when American surgeon William Coley discovered that bacterial infections could lead to tumor regression, leading him to develop "Coley's toxins"a mixture of heat-killed bacteria used to stimulate immune responses against cancer. Although initial enthusiasm waned due to inconsistent results and the rise of other therapies, the field revived in the late 20th century through advances such as the development of monoclonal antibodies in the 1970s and the identification of immune checkpoints starting in the 1980s. Key milestones include the 2018 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine awarded to James Allison and Tasuku Honjo for discovering cancer therapy through inhibition of negative immune regulation, which paved the way for checkpoint inhibitors. Today, immunotherapy has evolved into a cornerstone of modern medicine, with ongoing research expanding its applications beyond oncology.
Immunotherapy encompasses diverse types tailored to specific diseases and immune pathways. Monoclonal antibodies are lab-engineered proteins that mimic immune system components to target cancer cells or block harmful signals, such as rituximab for lymphoma. Immune checkpoint inhibitors, like pembrolizumab and nivolumab, release brakes on T-cells to unleash attacks on tumors, proving effective in melanoma, lung cancer, and more. Adoptive cell therapies, including CAR-T cell therapy, involve extracting, genetically modifying, and reinfusing a patient's T-cells to recognize and destroy cancer cells, with approvals for blood cancers like leukemia. Cancer vaccines and cytokines (e.g., interleukins or interferons) boost overall immune activity, while for allergies, allergen-specific immunotherapy (allergy shots or sublingual tablets) gradually desensitizes the immune system to triggers like pollen or dust mites, reducing symptoms of allergic rhinitis and asthma. In autoimmune diseases, certain immunotherapies modulate overactive responses, though many rely on immunosuppressive agents to prevent tissue damage.
Notable aspects include its potential for durable responses and fewer long-term toxicities compared to conventional treatments, though side effects like immune-related adverse events (e.g., inflammation in organs) require careful management. Approved for more than 30 cancer types and various allergic conditions, immunotherapy continues to advance through combination therapies and personalized approaches based on biomarkers like PD-L1 expression. Ongoing clinical trials explore its role in infectious diseases and transplantation, underscoring its versatility in precision medicine.

Overview

Definition

Immunotherapy is a therapeutic approach that modulates the immune system to treat or prevent disease by inducing, enhancing, or suppressing immune responses. This strategy leverages the body's natural defense mechanisms, either by stimulating immune activity against pathogens or tumors or by dampening overactive responses in conditions like allergies and autoimmune disorders. Unlike traditional treatments such as chemotherapy, which directly target diseased cells, immunotherapy aims to harness or restore the immune system's ability to recognize and combat threats more effectively.
The core principle of immunotherapy involves administering biological agents, such as monoclonal antibodies, vaccines, cytokines, or engineered immune cells, to alter immune function. For instance, in cancer treatment, it boosts the immune system's recognition of tumor cells, which often evade detection by mimicking normal cells or suppressing immune checkpoints. In contrast, for hypersensitivity disorders like allergies, allergen-specific immunotherapy gradually exposes patients to increasing doses of allergens to promote tolerance and reduce exaggerated immune reactions.
Immunotherapy encompasses a wide spectrum of applications, addressing immunodeficiencies through replacement therapies like intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) to bolster antibody production, as well as autoimmune diseases where it suppresses aberrant self-directed immunity, such as in multiple sclerosis or rheumatoid arthritis. Its versatility has made it a cornerstone in modern medicine, with ongoing research expanding its use to infectious diseases and transplant rejection prevention.

Historical Development

The roots of immunotherapy trace back to ancient observations of immune responses, but its formal development began in the late 18th century with Edward Jenner's 1796 introduction of the smallpox vaccine, which demonstrated the potential of harnessing the immune system to prevent disease through induced immunity. This milestone established the foundational principle of active immunization, influencing later therapeutic strategies.
In the field of cancer, the first targeted immunotherapeutic approach emerged in the 1890s when American surgeon William Coley observed tumor regressions in patients with concurrent bacterial infections and subsequently developed "Coley's toxins"a mixture of heat-killed bacteria administered to stimulate immune responses against sarcomas. By 1893, Coley reported the first successful treatment of an inoperable sarcoma patient using this method, marking the inception of cancer immunotherapy despite initial skepticism and limited adoption due to the rise of radiation and chemotherapy. Coley's work laid the groundwork for understanding immune activation in oncology, with retrospective analyses confirming durable remissions in some cases.
The mid-20th century saw renewed interest through the proposal of the cancer immunosurveillance hypothesis in 1957 by Frank Macfarlane Burnet and Lewis Thomas, positing that the immune system naturally monitors and eliminates nascent tumors, which spurred research into immune modulation for cancer control. This era also featured the discovery of interferons in 1957 by Alick Isaacs and Jean Lindenmann, antiviral proteins that became early cytokine-based immunotherapies tested in clinical trials by the 1960s. Concurrently, bacillus Calmette-Gurin (BCG), an attenuated tuberculosis vaccine, was explored for bladder cancer, leading to its FDA approval in 1990 as the first approved cancer immunotherapy based on intravesical administration to induce local immune responses.
Advancements accelerated in the 1970s with the development of monoclonal antibodies by Georges Khler and Csar Milstein in 1975, enabling targeted immune modulation and earning them the 1984 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine; this technology paved the way for antibody-based therapies like rituximab, approved in 1997 for non-Hodgkin lymphoma. The 1980s introduced cytokine therapies, highlighted by high-dose interleukin-2 (IL-2) approval in 1992 for metastatic renal cell carcinoma and in 1998 for metastatic melanoma, demonstrating objective response rates of up to 20% in refractory cases. The 1990s and 2000s focused on cellular approaches, including the 2010 FDA approval of sipuleucel-T, the first autologous cellular immunotherapy for prostate cancer, which improved overall survival by 4.1 months in phase III trials.
A paradigm shift occurred in the 2010s with immune checkpoint inhibitors, beginning with ipilimumab's 2011 FDA approval as the first CTLA-4 inhibitor for melanoma, achieving 20-30% long-term survival rates in advanced disease. This was followed by PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors like pembrolizumab (2014) and nivolumab (2014), which expanded immunotherapy to multiple cancers, with combination regimens showing synergistic effects. These breakthroughs culminated in the 2018 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine awarded to James P. Allison and Tasuku Honjo for their discoveries of CTLA-4 and PD-1 inhibition, respectively, transforming immunotherapy into a cornerstone of oncology with over 2,000 ongoing clinical trials by 2019. As of 2025, the number of clinical trials has grown to over 5,000, reflecting ongoing advancements. Recent extensions include bispecific antibodies and CAR-T cell therapies, such as axicabtagene ciloleucel approved in 2017 for lymphoma, reflecting the field's evolution toward personalized immune engineering.

Mechanisms of Immune Modulation

Activating Mechanisms

Activating mechanisms in immunotherapy encompass strategies designed to stimulate and enhance the immune system's response against diseases such as cancer and infections by targeting both innate and adaptive immune components. These approaches primarily work by promoting the recognition of antigens, amplifying effector cell functions, and overcoming immunosuppressive barriers within the tumor microenvironment. Key methods include the provision of co-stimulatory signals, cytokine administration, and activation of antigen-presenting cells (APCs), which collectively prime and expand anti-tumor immunity.
In the adaptive arm, T cell activation serves as a cornerstone, requiring three integrated signals for full effector function. The first signal involves T cell receptor (TCR) engagement with peptide-major histocompatibility complex (MHC) on APCs, initiating signaling cascades via kinases like LCK and ZAP-70. The second signal provides co-stimulation, predominantly through CD28 binding to B7-1 (CD80) or B7-2 (CD86) on APCs, which prevents T cell anergy and promotes IL-2 production and proliferation; in immunotherapy, agonists targeting alternative co-stimulatory receptors such as OX40 (CD134), 4-1BB (CD137), or GITR enhance this process by sustaining T cell survival and cytokine secretion. The third signal, delivered by cytokines like IL-12 or type I interferons, directs T cell differentiation into cytotoxic CD8+ effectors or helper CD4+ subsets, with clinical applications seen in high-dose IL-2 therapy that expands tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes.
Innate immune activation complements adaptive responses by rapidly mobilizing cells like dendritic cells (DCs), natural killer (NK) cells, and macrophages to initiate inflammation and antigen presentation. DCs, as primary APCs, are activated through pattern recognition receptors such as Toll-like receptors (TLRs) or the cGAS-STING pathway, which senses cytosolic DNA and triggers type I interferon production, enhancing cross-presentation of tumor antigens to T cells; synthetic agonists like poly-ICLC (TLR3) or ADU-S100 (STING) exemplify this in clinical trials for cancers like melanoma. NK cells are stimulated via activating receptors (e.g., NKG2D) recognizing stress ligands on tumor cells, augmented by cytokines such as IL-15, leading to direct cytotoxicity and IFN- release that bridges to adaptive immunity. Macrophages shift to an M1 pro-inflammatory phenotype through IFN- or TLR ligands, promoting phagocytosis and tumor cell lysis via antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity when combined with monoclonal antibodies.
These mechanisms often intersect in combination therapies, where innate activation amplifies adaptive responsesfor instance, STING agonists increase DC maturation and T cell priming, improving checkpoint inhibitor efficacy in preclinical models. However, challenges include off-target inflammation and dose-limiting toxicities, as observed in early IL-2 trials where vascular leak syndrome occurred at high doses. Ongoing research prioritizes tumor-selective delivery, such as nanoparticle-encapsulated agonists, to optimize activation while minimizing systemic effects.00331-X)

Suppressing Mechanisms

Suppressing mechanisms in immunotherapy refer to strategies that dampen or regulate overactive immune responses, primarily to treat autoimmune diseases, prevent transplant rejection, and alleviate allergic conditions. Unlike activating mechanisms that enhance immune effector functions, suppressing approaches target key pathways in T-cell activation, cytokine signaling, and regulatory cell induction to restore immune homeostasis. These mechanisms often involve small-molecule drugs, biologics, or antigen-specific therapies that inhibit proinflammatory signals while promoting tolerance, minimizing broad immunosuppression to reduce infection risks.
A primary suppressing mechanism is the blockade of T-cell activation through calcineurin inhibitors, such as cyclosporine and tacrolimus, which bind to cyclophilin or FKBP12, respectively, preventing the dephosphorylation of nuclear factor of activated T-cells (NFAT). This inhibits the transcription of interleukin-2 (IL-2) and other cytokines essential for T-cell proliferation and differentiation, effectively curtailing adaptive immune responses in conditions like rheumatoid arthritis and organ transplantation. Clinical studies demonstrate that tacrolimus achieves graft survival rates exceeding 90% at one year post-kidney transplant by this pathway, highlighting its potency in preventing rejection without completely ablating immunity.
Another key approach involves costimulatory signal blockade, exemplified by abatacept, a fusion protein that inhibits CD28-CD80/86 interactions between antigen-presenting cells and T cells. By preventing the second signal required for full T-cell activation, abatacept reduces synovial inflammation in rheumatoid arthritis, with trials showing American College of Rheumatology 20% improvement criteria met in up to 60% of patients. Complementing this, mTOR inhibitors like sirolimus target the mammalian target of rapamycin pathway, suppressing T-cell growth and promoting regulatory T-cell (Treg) expansion, which further enforces peripheral tolerance.
In antigen-specific suppressing immunotherapy, such as allergen immunotherapy for allergic rhinitis, mechanisms center on inducing immune tolerance via Treg generation and cytokine modulation. Gradual allergen exposure shifts the response from Th2-dominant (producing IL-4 and IL-13) to Treg-mediated suppression, increasing IL-10 and TGF- levels that inhibit effector cells and mast cell degranulation. Meta-analyses indicate sustained symptom reduction in 70-80% of patients after 3-5 years of sublingual therapy, underscoring the long-term tolerogenic effects. Similarly, in autoimmune settings, B-cell depletion with anti-CD20 monoclonal antibodies like rituximab eliminates autoantibody-producing cells, reducing pathogenic inflammation in diseases such as systemic lupus erythematosus.
Corticosteroids provide rapid suppression by binding glucocorticoid receptors, translocating to the nucleus to repress proinflammatory transcription factors like NF-B and AP-1, thereby inhibiting cytokine storms in acute autoimmune flares. This mechanism, while effective, is associated with side effects like osteoporosis, prompting combination therapies with steroid-sparing agents. Overall, these suppressing mechanisms balance efficacy and safety, with ongoing research focusing on pathway-specific targeting to enhance precision.

Cancer Immunotherapy

Checkpoint Inhibitors

Checkpoint inhibitors represent a cornerstone of cancer immunotherapy, targeting immune checkpointsmolecules that dampen T-cell activity to maintain immune homeostasis but are co-opted by tumors to evade immune surveillance. These monoclonal antibodies block inhibitory receptors such as CTLA-4 and PD-1 or their ligands, thereby reinvigorating cytotoxic T cells to recognize and destroy cancer cells. Unlike traditional chemotherapies, checkpoint inhibitors promote durable responses in a subset of patients by harnessing the adaptive immune system, leading to long-term tumor control in responsive cases. Their development has transformed outcomes in immunogenic cancers like melanoma and non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC).
The CTLA-4 pathway, one of the first targeted checkpoints, functions primarily during T-cell priming in lymphoid organs. CTLA-4, expressed on activated T cells and regulatory T cells, competes with the costimulatory receptor CD28 for binding to B7 ligands (CD80/CD86) on antigen-presenting cells, delivering a dominant inhibitory signal that suppresses T-cell proliferation and effector function. Blocking CTLA-4 with antibodies enhances T-cell activation and promotes antitumor immunity, as demonstrated in preclinical mouse models where anti-CTLA-4 treatment augmented rejection of immunogenic tumors. This concept was pioneered by James Allison and colleagues, with a seminal 1996 study showing that CTLA-4 blockade enhanced antitumor responses in vivo. Ipilimumab, a fully human anti-CTLA-4 monoclonal antibody, became the first checkpoint inhibitor approved by the FDA in March 2011 for unresectable or metastatic melanoma, based on a phase 3 trial demonstrating improved overall survival (median 10.1 months versus 6.4 months with gp100 vaccine alone).
The PD-1/PD-L1 axis operates mainly in peripheral tissues, including the tumor microenvironment, where chronic antigen exposure upregulates PD-1 on T cells, leading to exhaustion and reduced cytokine production. PD-1 binds to PD-L1 or PD-L2 on tumor or stromal cells, recruiting phosphatases that inhibit T-cell signaling pathways like PI3K/AKT. Inhibition of this pathway restores T-cell cytotoxicity and interferon- secretion, enabling tumor infiltration and clearance. Tasuku Honjo discovered PD-1 in 1992 as a gene induced during programmed cell death in T cells, later elucidating its role in immune inhibition. Clinical translation followed with nivolumab (anti-PD-1), approved by the FDA in December 2014 for melanoma and other cancers, supported by a phase 1 trial showing objective response rates of 28-41% across solid tumors. Pembrolizumab (anti-PD-1), approved shortly after in September 2014 for advanced melanoma, exhibited similar efficacy in a phase 1 study with 38% response rate and durable remissions. PD-L1 inhibitors like atezolizumab target the ligand to disrupt the interaction, offering an alternative approach approved for urothelial carcinoma in 2016.
Checkpoint inhibitors have expanded to over 50 indications across cancers, including FDA approvals for NSCLC, renal cell carcinoma, and Hodgkin lymphoma, often in combination regimens. For instance, nivolumab plus ipilimumab yields objective response rates up to 58% in melanoma, surpassing monotherapy, with 5-year overall survival rates of 52% in advanced cases. Predictive biomarkers like PD-L1 expression and tumor mutational burden guide patient selection, though not all tumors respond due to factors like low immunogenicity or resistance mechanisms. While effective, these therapies can induce immune-related adverse events, such as colitis or pneumonitis, in up to 20% of patients, necessitating vigilant management. Ongoing research explores novel checkpoints like LAG-3 and TIGIT to broaden applicability.

Adoptive Cell Therapies

Adoptive cell therapy (ACT) is a cornerstone of cancer immunotherapy, involving the extraction of a patient's immune cells, their ex vivo expansion or genetic modification to enhance tumor-targeting capabilities, and subsequent reinfusion to elicit a potent anti-tumor response. This approach leverages the specificity and cytotoxicity of immune effectors, such as T cells or natural killer (NK) cells, to selectively destroy malignant cells while minimizing damage to healthy tissues. Unlike passive immunotherapies like checkpoint inhibitors, ACT provides a tailored, high-dose infusion of activated cells that can persist and proliferate in vivo, offering durable remissions in certain hematologic malignancies.
The historical foundation of ACT traces back to the 1980s, when Steven Rosenberg and colleagues at the National Cancer Institute pioneered the use of tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs), demonstrating objective tumor regressions in patients with metastatic melanoma following adoptive transfer combined with interleukin-2 (IL-2). Early trials showed response rates of up to 34% in melanoma, establishing ACT as a viable strategy for solid tumors. Subsequent advancements in the 2000s focused on genetic engineering, leading to the development of chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cells, with the first FDA approval in 2017 for tisagenlecleucel (Kymriah) in pediatric acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL), achieving complete remission rates exceeding 80% in refractory cases.
Key modalities within ACT include TIL therapy, CAR-T cell therapy, and T-cell receptor (TCR)-engineered T cell therapy. TIL therapy isolates tumor-reactive T cells from resected tumors, expands them in culture with IL-2, and reinfuses them after lymphodepleting chemotherapy to reduce immunosuppression and enhance engraftment. It has shown particular efficacy in melanoma, with objective response rates of 50% or higher in select trials, including durable complete responses in 20-30% of patients. Recently, lifileucel (Amtagvi), an autologous TIL product, received FDA approval in 2024 for advanced melanoma, marking the first solid tumor ACT approval based on a 31.4% overall response rate in heavily pretreated patients. For gastrointestinal cancers with mismatch repair deficiency, neoantigen-specific TILs have induced responses in up to 40% of cases, highlighting its potential beyond melanoma.
CAR-T cell therapy genetically modifies patient-derived T cells to express a synthetic receptor fusing an antigen-binding domain (typically from antibodies) with T-cell signaling motifs, enabling recognition of surface antigens like CD19 on B-cell malignancies. Six CAR-T products are FDA-approved as of 2025, primarily for hematologic cancers: axicabtagene ciloleucel (Yescarta) and tisagenlecleucel for large B-cell lymphoma (response rates ~50-80%), brexucabtagene autoleucel (Tecartus) for mantle cell lymphoma, lisocabtagene maraleucel (Breyanzi) for follicular lymphoma, and idecabtagene vicleucel (Abecma) and ciltacabtagene autoleucel (Carvykti) targeting BCMA for multiple myeloma (response rates 70-98% in relapsed settings). These therapies have transformed treatment paradigms, with some patients achieving long-term remissions exceeding five years, though success in solid tumors remains limited due to antigen heterogeneity.
TCR-T cell therapy, an emerging variant, engineers T cells with recombinant TCRs to target intracellular antigens presented by major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules, broadening applicability to solid tumors expressing neoantigens or cancer-testis antigens like NY-ESO-1. Clinical trials have reported response rates of 20-50% in synovial sarcoma and melanoma, with durable responses in HPV-associated cancers. For instance, afamitresgene autoleucel (Tecelra), approved in 2024 for synovial sarcoma, achieved a 39% response rate. Unlike CAR-T, TCR-T can address a wider array of epitopes but risks off-target reactivity due to MHC restriction. NK cell-based ACT, often derived from peripheral blood or induced pluripotent stem cells, provides an off-the-shelf alternative with lower risk of graft-versus-host disease, showing promise in trials for acute myeloid leukemia with response rates around 50%.
Mechanistically, ACT cells exert cytotoxicity through perforin/granzyme release, Fas ligand-mediated apoptosis, and cytokine secretion (e.g., IFN-, TNF-) that remodel the tumor microenvironment. Pre-infusion lymphodepletion enhances efficacy by depleting regulatory T cells and competing sinks for homeostatic cytokines like IL-7 and IL-15, promoting ACT cell expansion up to 1,000-fold in vivo. Combination with checkpoint inhibitors or vaccines can further amplify responses by countering exhaustion.
Despite successes, ACT faces significant challenges, including severe toxicities like cytokine release syndrome (CRS, affecting 70-90% of CAR-T recipients, often grade 1-2 but potentially fatal) and neurotoxicity (ICANS), managed with tocilizumab and steroids. In solid tumors, barriers include poor trafficking due to immunosuppressive microenvironments, antigen escape, and manufacturing complexities, with TIL production taking 4-6 weeks and success rates below 50% for cell expansion. High costs (often exceeding $400,000 per treatment) and scalability issues limit accessibility.
Future directions emphasize allogeneic "off-the-shelf" products using CRISPR-edited cells to reduce costs and wait times, multi-antigen targeting to prevent escape, and integration with modalities like oncolytic viruses for solid tumors. Armored CAR-T cells secreting IL-12 or PD-1 blockers are in trials, showing improved responses in preclinical models. Ongoing phase III studies in non-small cell lung cancer and glioblastoma aim to extend ACT beyond blood cancers, with personalized neoantigen TCR-T therapies poised for broader adoption.

Cancer Vaccines

Cancer vaccines represent a cornerstone of cancer immunotherapy, designed to stimulate the immune system to recognize and destroy cancer cells by targeting specific tumor antigens. Unlike preventive vaccines, such as those against human papillomavirus (HPV) or hepatitis B virus, therapeutic cancer vaccines are administered to patients with existing malignancies to elicit de novo immune responses against tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) or tumor-specific antigens (TSAs), including neoantigens arising from mutations. These vaccines work by delivering antigens in forms that mimic natural infection or directly activate antigen-presenting cells, leading to the expansion of cytotoxic T cells and helper T cells capable of infiltrating and eliminating tumor cells.
The primary mechanisms of cancer vaccines involve antigen presentation via major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules to prime T cell responses, often enhanced by adjuvants that promote dendritic cell maturation and cytokine release, such as interleukin-2 or Toll-like receptor agonists. Vaccines can be categorized into several types based on their formulation: peptide or protein-based vaccines deliver purified antigen fragments; dendritic cell vaccines, like autologous tumor-loaded dendritic cells, directly present antigens to T cells; viral vector vaccines use modified viruses (e.g., adenovirus or poxvirus) to encode antigens; mRNA vaccines encode antigens in lipid nanoparticles for in vivo translation; and whole-cell or tumor lysate vaccines use irradiated tumor cells to provide a broad array of antigens. Each type aims to overcome immune tolerance in the tumor microenvironment, though efficacy depends on antigen immunogenicity and tumor escape mechanisms.
Several cancer vaccines have received U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approval, marking key milestones in clinical translation. Sipuleucel-T (Provenge), approved in 2010 for metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer, is an autologous dendritic cell vaccine prepared by culturing patient-derived cells with prostatic acid phosphatase antigen, resulting in a 22% reduction in mortality risk in phase III trials. Talimogene laherparepvec (T-VEC, Imlygic), approved in 2015 for advanced melanoma, is an oncolytic herpes simplex virus engineered to express granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF), which selectively replicates in tumor cells and induces local immune activation; phase III data showed a 32% improvement in durable response rates compared to GM-CSF alone. Bacillus Calmette-Gurin (BCG), approved in 1990 for non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer, functions as a live attenuated bacterial vaccine that triggers innate and adaptive immunity upon intravesical administration, achieving complete response rates of up to 70% in high-risk patients. These approvals highlight the potential of vaccines to extend survival in specific settings, though response rates remain modest without combination therapies.
Recent advances emphasize personalized neoantigen vaccines, leveraging next-generation sequencing to identify patient-specific mutations for tailored immunization. In a 2024 phase I trial for pancreatic cancer, an mRNA-based neoantigen vaccine (autogene cevumeran) induced T cell responses in 50% of patients, correlating with prolonged recurrence-free survival in responders, as reported in Nature. Similarly, a 2025 phase I study (AMPLIFY-201) of an amphiphile-peptide vaccine targeting mutant KRAS in 25 patients with pancreatic or colorectal cancers demonstrated immune activation with T-cell responses in 17 of 25 patients (68%) and improved recurrence-free survival in responders. Viral vaccines like RP1 (an oncolytic HSV-1 expressing GM-CSF) combined with nivolumab in the phase 1/2 IGNYTE trial yielded a 33% objective response rate in anti-PD-1 refractory advanced melanoma, with ongoing phase 3 evaluation in IGNYTE-3. As of November 2025, RP1 combined with nivolumab is under FDA review following BLA resubmission, with a target action date of April 2026. mRNA platforms, inspired by COVID-19 vaccines, have accelerated development; over 120 trials by 2024 evaluated mRNA cancer vaccines, with combinations showing enhanced tumor infiltration and survival benefits in non-small cell lung cancer and melanoma. These results underscore vaccines' role in converting immunologically "cold" tumors to "hot" ones when paired with checkpoint inhibitors.
Despite progress, cancer vaccines face significant challenges, including tumor heterogeneity leading to antigen loss, immunosuppressive microenvironments that dampen T cell function, and difficulties in selecting highly immunogenic neoantigens amid low mutation burdens in some cancers. Clinical response rates often hover below 20-30% in monotherapy settings, attributed to immune exhaustion and regulatory T cell dominance. Manufacturing complexities for personalized vaccines also limit scalability and increase costs. Future directions focus on combinatorial strategies, such as integrating vaccines with immune checkpoint inhibitors, adoptive cell therapies, or radiation to boost antigen release and efficacy; ongoing trials explore multi-antigen approaches and novel adjuvants to enhance durability. By 2025, experts anticipate broader adoption of mRNA and neoantigen vaccines, potentially transforming adjuvant therapy for high-risk cancers like melanoma and glioblastoma.

Oncolytic Virus Therapy

Oncolytic virus therapy is a form of cancer immunotherapy that utilizes viruses engineered or selected to selectively infect and destroy cancer cells through a process known as oncolysis. These viruses replicate within tumor cells, leading to their lysis and the release of tumor-associated antigens, which can then trigger a systemic immune response against the cancer. Unlike traditional chemotherapy, oncolytic viruses exploit the unique molecular defects in cancer cells, such as defective antiviral signaling pathways, to achieve tumor-specific replication while sparing healthy tissues. This dual actionnot only direct cell killing but also immune activationpositions oncolytic virotherapy as a promising strategy to convert immunologically "cold" tumors into "hot" ones that elicit robust T-cell responses.
The mechanisms of oncolytic virus therapy involve both direct and indirect effects on the tumor microenvironment. Directly, viral replication causes cell membrane rupture, releasing progeny viruses that propagate the infection to neighboring cancer cells and inducing immunogenic cell death pathways like apoptosis and necroptosis. Indirectly, the released antigens are presented to antigen-presenting cells, such as dendritic cells, promoting the activation of cytotoxic T lymphocytes and natural killer cells; additionally, many engineered viruses express immunostimulatory transgenes, such as granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF), to further amplify inflammation and recruit immune effectors. This immunomodulation can overcome tumor-induced immunosuppression by altering cytokine profiles and reducing regulatory T-cell activity, thereby enhancing the efficacy of concurrent therapies like checkpoint inhibitors.
A landmark example is talimogene laherparepvec (T-VEC), a genetically modified herpes simplex virus type 1 (HSV-1) that deletes viral genes for immune evasion (ICP34.5 and ICP47) and incorporates the GM-CSF gene to boost antigen presentation. Approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in 2015 as the first oncolytic virus therapy, T-VEC is indicated for advanced melanoma in patients with unresectable disease. In the phase III OPTiM trial, involving 436 patients, T-VEC demonstrated a durable response rate of 16.3% compared to 2.1% for subcutaneous GM-CSF, with an objective response rate of 26.4% versus 5.7%; median overall survival was 23.3 months versus 18.9 months, and 5-year survival reached 33.4%. Intratumoral injection of T-VEC has shown particular efficacy in accessible lesions, with responses observed in both injected and noninjected tumors, underscoring its abscopal immune effects.
Other oncolytic viruses in clinical use include reovirus (Reolysin), which exploits ras-transformed cancer cells' defective interferon signaling, and adenovirus-based H101, approved in China since 2005 for head and neck squamous cell carcinoma in combination with chemotherapy. Ongoing trials explore combinations, such as T-VEC with ipilimumab, where a phase Ib study reported an objective response rate of 28.3% in advanced melanoma, higher than ipilimumab monotherapy's 13%. These approaches highlight the versatility of oncolytic viruses across solid tumors, including glioblastoma and breast cancer, though challenges like rapid antiviral immunity, heterogeneous tumor penetration, and manufacturing scalability persist. Despite these hurdles, over 100 clinical trials are active as of 2025, focusing on engineering viruses for enhanced specificity and reduced off-target effects.

Suppressive Immunotherapies

Immunosuppressive Drugs

Immunosuppressive drugs are pharmacological agents designed to modulate or suppress immune system activity, forming a key component of suppressive immunotherapies aimed at controlling excessive or aberrant immune responses. These drugs are essential in preventing organ transplant rejection by inhibiting T-cell mediated allorecognition and in managing autoimmune disorders such as rheumatoid arthritis, systemic lupus erythematosus, and inflammatory bowel disease, where they reduce inflammation and autoantibody production. By targeting various stages of immune cell activation, proliferation, and effector function, they help maintain immune homeostasis without completely ablating the system's protective capabilities.
The major classes of immunosuppressive drugs include glucocorticoids, calcineurin inhibitors, antiproliferative agents, mTOR inhibitors, and biologic monoclonal antibodies, each acting through distinct molecular mechanisms. Glucocorticoids, such as prednisone and methylprednisolone, bind to intracellular receptors to translocate to the nucleus, where they inhibit transcription of pro-inflammatory genes like those encoding interleukins (IL-1, IL-6) and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-), thereby suppressing T-cell activation and cytokine storms. Calcineurin inhibitors, exemplified by cyclosporine and tacrolimus, form complexes with immunophilins (cyclophilin for cyclosporine and FKBP-12 for tacrolimus) that block the phosphatase activity of calcineurin, preventing dephosphorylation of nuclear factor of activated T-cells (NFAT) and subsequent IL-2 gene expression, which halts T-cell proliferation. Antiproliferative agents like azathioprine and mycophenolate mofetil interfere with DNA synthesis; azathioprine is metabolized to 6-mercaptopurine, inhibiting purine nucleotide synthesis in lymphocytes, while mycophenolate inhibits inosine monophosphate dehydrogenase, selectively depleting guanosine nucleotides in T- and B-cells. mTOR inhibitors, such as sirolimus (rapamycin) and everolimus, bind to FKBP-12 to allosterically inhibit the mammalian target of rapamycin (mTOR), disrupting cell cycle progression from G1 to S phase and reducing dendritic cell maturation. Biologic agents include interleukin-2 receptor antagonists like basiliximab, which bind CD25 to prevent T-cell expansion, and anti-CD20 antibodies like rituximab, which deplete B-cells via antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity and complement activation.
The introduction of cyclosporine in the early 1980s marked a pivotal advancement in suppressive immunotherapy, dramatically improving one-year survival rates for kidney transplants from around 50% to over 80% by providing more targeted T-cell suppression compared to earlier broad-spectrum agents like azathioprine and corticosteroids. Tacrolimus, approved in 1994, further refined this approach with superior efficacy in liver and kidney transplantation, showing comparable patient and graft survival to cyclosporine but with reduced rates of acute rejection in randomized trials. These drugs are often used in combination regimenssuch as tacrolimus with mycophenolate and low-dose steroidsto achieve synergistic immunosuppression while minimizing toxicity, a strategy that has expanded transplant viability to include heart, lung, and pancreas procedures.
Despite their efficacy, immunosuppressive drugs carry significant risks, including increased susceptibility to infections due to impaired pathogen clearance, nephrotoxicity from calcineurin inhibitors, and long-term malignancies from chronic immune suppression. Monitoring therapeutic drug levels, such as trough concentrations of tacrolimus (typically 5-15 ng/mL depending on the post-transplant phase), is crucial to balance efficacy and safety, often guided by pharmacokinetic assays to prevent under- or over-immunosuppression. Ongoing research focuses on personalized dosing based on genetic polymorphisms in drug-metabolizing enzymes like CYP3A5 to optimize outcomes in diverse patient populations.

Immune Tolerance Therapies

Immune tolerance therapies represent a class of suppressive immunotherapies designed to restore or induce immune tolerance, particularly in autoimmune diseases, organ transplantation, and allergic conditions, by promoting mechanisms that prevent or dampen aberrant immune responses against self-antigens or non-harmful foreign antigens. These approaches target the underlying loss of tolerance, aiming for long-term remission rather than symptomatic relief, through strategies such as antigen-specific modulation and enhancement of regulatory immune cells.
Central to these therapies is the activation or expansion of regulatory T cells (Tregs), which suppress effector T cell responses and maintain peripheral tolerance via mechanisms including cytokine secretion (e.g., IL-10, TGF-) and direct cell-cell contact. Tolerogenic dendritic cells (tolDCs), engineered or conditioned to present antigens in an immunosuppressive context, play a key role by inducing Treg differentiation and anergy in autoreactive T cells, as demonstrated in preclinical models of multiple sclerosis and rheumatoid arthritis. Other mechanisms involve central tolerance restoration, such as through thymic modulation, though peripheral strategies predominate due to their accessibility.
Prominent examples include tolerogenic vaccines, which deliver autoantigens via nanoparticles or liposomes to induce antigen-specific tolerance without systemic immunosuppression; for instance, PLG-based vaccines targeting myelin antigens have shown reduced disease severity in experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis models. In cellular therapies, ex vivo expanded Tregs, often FOXP3+, have been infused in clinical trials for type 1 diabetes, achieving transient increases in Treg function and delaying insulin dependence in some patients. Mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs) also contribute by secreting immunomodulatory factors that promote Treg expansion and inhibit pro-inflammatory cytokines, with phase I/II trials in graft-versus-host disease reporting improved tolerance rates of up to 70%.
Recent advances include chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapies targeting CD19 to deplete autoreactive B cells, showing sustained drug-free remission in over 80% of patients with systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) in phase II trials lasting up to 2 years as of 2025.
In transplantation, mixed chimerism induction via hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT) resets the immune system to accept donor organs, with non-myeloablative protocols achieving tolerance in 80% of kidney transplant recipients in select studies, avoiding lifelong immunosuppression. For allergies, sublingual or epicutaneous allergen immunotherapy builds tolerance through gradual exposure, with studies showing sustained unresponsiveness in approximately 50% of patients with peanut allergy after extended treatment. Emerging gene-edited approaches, such as CRISPR-modified Tregs, hold promise for durable tolerance but remain preclinical. Challenges include ensuring specificity to avoid broad immunosuppression and scalability for clinical use.

Allergen Immunotherapy

Allergen immunotherapy (AIT), also known as desensitization or hypo-sensitization, is a therapeutic approach that involves the controlled administration of increasing doses of allergens to induce immune tolerance in individuals with IgE-mediated allergic diseases. It represents the only disease-modifying treatment for allergies, targeting the underlying hypersensitivity rather than merely alleviating symptoms, and has been established as a causal therapy for conditions such as allergic rhinitis, allergic asthma, and Hymenoptera venom allergy. Introduced over a century ago, AIT was pioneered by Leonard Noon and John Freeman in 1911 through subcutaneous injections of pollen extracts to treat hay fever, marking the first successful application of specific immunotherapy for environmental allergens. Since its inception, AIT has evolved from rudimentary pollen extracts to standardized, evidence-based protocols, with seminal clinical trials demonstrating its potential to alter the natural course of allergic diseases by preventing progression from rhinitis to asthma and reducing new sensitizations.
The mechanisms of AIT involve complex immunomodulation, primarily shifting the immune response from a Th2-dominated, IgE-promoting state to a regulatory T-cell (Treg) and Th1-balanced profile that promotes tolerance. Early desensitization effects occur within hours of treatment through mast cell and basophil hypo-responsiveness, while long-term benefits arise from sustained changes, including increased production of allergen-specific IgG4 antibodies that block IgE-mediated effects, induction of IL-10 and TGF- secreting Tregs, and suppression of allergen-specific Th2 cells. These processes are supported by preclinical and clinical studies showing reduced basophil activation and eosinophil infiltration in the target tissues, with durable effects persisting for years after treatment cessation. AIT's mode of action is allergen-specific, requiring identification of clinically relevant triggers via skin prick tests or serum IgE assays to ensure targeted therapy.
Common routes of AIT administration include subcutaneous immunotherapy (SCIT), the traditional gold standard involving injections under the skin, and sublingual immunotherapy (SLIT), where allergen extracts are held under the tongue as tablets or drops. SCIT typically requires a build-up phase of weekly injections over 3-6 months followed by maintenance doses every 4-6 weeks for 3-5 years, while SLIT offers at-home administration with daily dosing, improving patient adherence. Alternative routes, such as oral immunotherapy for food allergies, local nasal immunotherapy, epicutaneous patches, and intralymphatic injections, are emerging but less standardized; for instance, epicutaneous delivery via patches has shown promise in reducing eczema severity in peanut-allergic children by promoting regulatory immune responses with minimal systemic exposure. Both SCIT and SLIT are effective for aeroallergens like grass pollen, house dust mites, and tree pollen, with meta-analyses indicating symptom reductions of 30-40% and medication use decreases of up to 50% compared to pharmacotherapy alone.
Efficacy of AIT is well-documented for IgE-mediated allergies, particularly in patients with moderate-to-severe symptoms inadequately controlled by allergen avoidance or medications, where it reduces rhinoconjunctivitis symptoms by 20-50% and asthma exacerbations by up to 60% in sensitized individuals. For Hymenoptera venom allergy, AIT achieves protection rates exceeding 90% against systemic reactions upon re-stinging, far surpassing symptomatic treatments. Safety profiles vary by route: SCIT carries a risk of systemic reactions in 0.1-0.2% of injections, necessitating administration in medical settings, whereas SLIT is associated with primarily local reactions like oral itching in 70-80% of patients but rare anaphylaxis (less than 0.1%). Contraindications include uncontrolled asthma (FEV1 <70% predicted) and beta-blocker use, with guidelines emphasizing patient selection based on confirmed sensitization and disease severity.
Indications for AIT encompass allergic rhinitis, conjunctivitis, and asthma triggered by inhalant allergens in both children and adults, as well as food allergies in select cases and insect venom hypersensitivity. It is recommended for patients over 5 years old with perennial or seasonal symptoms, where benefits include long-term remission lasting 7-12 years post-treatment and may reduce the risk of asthma onset in children with allergic rhinitis, with one study showing an incidence of 26% in treated patients versus 45% in untreated after 3 years. Evidence from systematic reviews supports its use in pediatric populations, with SCIT and SLIT demonstrating comparable efficacy to adults but with higher adherence for SLIT due to non-invasiveness.
Looking ahead, future directions in AIT emphasize personalized vaccines using recombinant hypoallergenic allergens or nanoparticle-based delivery to enhance safety and efficacy while shortening treatment duration to months rather than years. Emerging approaches, such as tolerogenic nanovaccines and adjuvanted formulations targeting dendritic cells, hold potential for broader application in food allergies and atopic dermatitis, with ongoing trials exploring combinations with biologics like anti-IgE antibodies to accelerate tolerance induction. These innovations aim to address current limitations in accessibility and duration, potentially expanding AIT's role in preventive allergy management.

Emerging Therapies

Helminthic Therapies

Helminthic therapies involve the controlled administration of helminth parasites or their derivatives to modulate the host immune system, primarily to dampen excessive inflammatory responses associated with autoimmune diseases, allergies, and inflammatory bowel disease (IBD). This approach draws from the hygiene hypothesis, which posits that reduced exposure to parasites in modern environments contributes to rising rates of immune-mediated disorders by impairing the development of regulatory immune mechanisms. Unlike traditional immunosuppressive drugs, helminthic therapies aim to restore immune balance through natural immunomodulatory pathways, often using non-pathogenic or self-limiting helminths in humans.
The immunomodulatory effects of helminths primarily occur through the induction of regulatory T cells (Tregs) and the promotion of anti-inflammatory cytokines such as interleukin-10 (IL-10) and transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-). Helminths trigger a shift from pro-inflammatory Th1 and Th17 responses to a Th2-dominated profile, which includes elevated levels of IL-4 and IL-13, while suppressing interferon-gamma (IFN-) production. Additionally, helminth-derived molecules, such as excretory-secretory products, interact with host pattern recognition receptors like Toll-like receptors (TLRs) to activate dendritic cells and macrophages in ways that favor tolerance over inflammation. In animal models, this leads to reduced tissue damage in conditions like experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis, mimicking multiple sclerosis.
A prominent example is the use of Trichuris suis ova (TSO), the eggs of the porcine whipworm, which do not establish chronic infection in humans but transiently colonize the gut to exert immunomodulatory effects. Early clinical trials demonstrated promise for IBD; a 2005 double-blind, placebo-controlled study (n=54) in patients with active ulcerative colitis (UC) found that TSO administration every two weeks led to clinical improvement in 43.3% of treated participants versus 16.7% in the placebo group, with reduced disease activity scores. However, a 2010 randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial (n=96) in grass pollen-induced allergic rhinitis found no significant symptom reduction with TSO treatment (P=0.87), despite inducing infection. Larger phase II trials for Crohn's disease, such as one involving 487 patients, failed to achieve statistical significance for remission induction (38.5-47.2% in TSO groups vs. 42.9% placebo) across various dosages.
Applications extend to other autoimmune conditions, including multiple sclerosis and type 1 diabetes, where helminth secretions have induced Treg expansion and protected against autoantigen-specific responses in preclinical models. For instance, Hymenolepis diminuta extracts ameliorated symptoms in rodent models of rheumatoid arthritis by elevating IL-10 and reducing pro-inflammatory cytokines. Safety profiles are generally favorable, with mild gastrointestinal side effects like transient diarrhea reported in up to 20% of participants, and no evidence of long-term parasitism from TSO. Despite these advances, challenges include regulatory hurdles for live organisms, potential immunogenicity, and inconsistent efficacy in diverse populations, prompting a shift toward purified worm-derived molecules like acetylcholinesterase inhibitors for more targeted therapies. Ongoing research focuses on synthetic analogs to harness these benefits without infection risks. As of 2025, helminthic therapies are being explored for diabetes mellitus, showing benefits in glycemic control via immune modulation, though regulatory concerns persist due to unregulated markets.

Microbiome-Based Therapies

Microbiome-based therapies represent an emerging class of immunotherapies that leverage the gut microbiota to modulate host immune responses, particularly in enhancing the efficacy of cancer treatments such as immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs). The gut microbiome influences antitumor immunity by producing metabolites like short-chain fatty acids and inosine, which activate dendritic cells and T cells, thereby promoting systemic immune priming and tumor infiltration by effector cells. Seminal studies have demonstrated that specific commensal bacteria, such as Bifidobacterium species, can restore responsiveness to anti-PD-L1 therapy in germ-free mice by enhancing CD8+ T cell function and cytokine production. Similarly, Akkermansia muciniphila abundance correlates with improved outcomes in PD-1 blockade for epithelial tumors, where oral supplementation in antibiotic-disrupted mice reactivates antitumor immunity through IL-12 secretion and Th1 responses.
Therapeutic strategies focus on reshaping the dysbiotic microbiome to favor immunogenic profiles. Fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) from responders to non-responders has shown promise in boosting ICI efficacy; for instance, in metastatic melanoma and non-small cell lung cancer patients, FMT increased objective response rates from 20-30% to over 50% in small cohorts by enriching for beneficial taxa like Ruminococcaceae. Probiotics, such as defined consortia including Bifidobacterium pseudolongum, have been tested in clinical trials, where administration prior to anti-PD-1 therapy elevated inosine levels, leading to improved progression-free survival in renal cell carcinoma cases. Prebiotics and dietary interventions, like high-fiber diets, promote the growth of Akkermansia and Faecalibacterium, correlating with reduced immune-related adverse events and enhanced ICI durability in observational studies. Conversely, broad-spectrum antibiotics disrupt these benefits, reducing response rates by up to 40% in retrospective analyses, underscoring the need for microbiome-sparing protocols.
Clinical evidence continues to evolve through ongoing trials, with phase II studies like NCT05502913 evaluating FMT combined with ICIs in advanced solid tumors for improved safety and efficacy compared to historical controls. In hepatocellular carcinoma, Akkermansia-enriched interventions extended median overall survival by 6 months when paired with sorafenib and PD-1 inhibitors. These approaches highlight the microbiome's role not only as a biomarkerwhere alpha diversity predicts 70% of ICI respondersbut as a tunable therapeutic target to overcome resistance. A 2025 meta-analysis reported an objective response rate of 43% (95% CI: 35-51%) with FMT combined with ICIs across advanced cancers, supporting its potential while emphasizing the need for standardized protocols. Future directions emphasize personalized modulation via multi-omics profiling to optimize outcomes across immunotherapy modalities.
