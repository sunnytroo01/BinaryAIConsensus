Milking
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Milking is the act of removing fluid from glands, most commonly milk from the mammary glands (udders) of lactating animals such as cows, goats, sheep, and others, for human consumption and dairy product processing, though the term also applies to extracting venom from animals like snakes for antivenom production. This practice, dating back to ancient civilizations, is fundamental to dairy farming and global food security, typically involving twice-daily sessions to optimize lactation and udder health, with more frequent milking potentially boosting yields. In modern dairy operations, machine milking using vacuum systems predominates, emphasizing hygiene and animal welfare to prevent issues like mastitis. Globally, the dairy sector supports the livelihoods of approximately 1 billion people, many smallholder farmers in developing countries.

Overview

Definition and Scope

Milking is the process of extracting milk from the mammary glands of female mammals, typically performed to harvest this nutrient-rich fluid for human consumption or other uses. In dairy production, it involves stimulating the release and collection of milk from lactating animals such as cows, goats, and sheep, ensuring the fluid is obtained hygienically to maintain quality.
The biological foundation of milking relies on lactation, the physiological process by which mammary glands synthesize and secrete milk in response to hormonal signals following pregnancy or parturition. Mammary glands, specialized exocrine structures in mammals, produce milk components like lactose, proteins, and fats under the influence of prolactin, which sustains milk synthesis, while oxytocin triggers the milk ejection reflex by contracting myoepithelial cells around the alveoli to release the milk. This let-down mechanism is essential for efficient extraction, as milk remains stored in the glands until stimulated.
The scope of milking extends beyond dairy applications to include the extraction of analogous glandular secretions from non-mammalian organisms, such as venom from snakes, where the process mimics milking by manually inducing the release of toxic fluids from venom glands for antivenom production. Common dairy animals illustrate varying production scales: dairy cows typically yield 20-30 liters of milk per day during peak lactation, goats average 2-4 liters daily over a 280-300 day period, and sheep produce 0.5-2 liters per day in specialized milking breeds. Over time, milking practices have evolved from manual methods to mechanized systems to accommodate these yields efficiently.

Historical Development

The practice of milking originated in the Neolithic period with the domestication of livestock in Southwest Asia, where evidence of animal milk use dates back to between 9000 and 6500 BCE, coinciding with the onset of farming in the region. Cattle, sheep, and goats were first domesticated in Mesopotamia, the area encompassing the Tigris-Euphrates river system, providing early sources for milk extraction alongside meat and other products. In ancient Egypt, similar developments occurred by the 7th millennium BCE, with sheep and goats kept in villages along the Nile Valley, and tomb paintings from later periods depicting cattle being milked, indicating established dairying practices.
During the medieval period in Europe (c. 5001500 CE), milking remained a manual process reliant on basic tools that enhanced efficiency and portability. Three-legged wooden stools, designed for stability on uneven barn floors, allowed milkers to sit comfortably while extracting milk by hand into shallow bowls or pails. Wooden pails, often carried using yokes for balance, were standard for collecting and transporting milk from barns to processing areas, as described in contemporary agricultural texts and farm inventories. These advancements reflected the growing importance of dairy in European feudal economies, where monasteries and manors managed herds for cheese and butter production.
The 19th century marked a pivotal shift toward mechanization, beginning with Anna Baldwin's patent for the first practical mechanical milker in 1879, a vacuum-based device that used suction through a rubber tube to draw milk directly from the udder, reducing labor and contamination risks. This invention laid the groundwork for broader adoption of machine milking after 1900, as improvements in vacuum technology and materials made devices more reliable for commercial dairy operations.
In the 20th century, electrification transformed milking systems, particularly from the 1930s onward, when rural power lines enabled the widespread use of electric vacuum pumps, making hand-cranked machines obsolete and standardizing automated extraction on farms. By the 1950s and 1960s, pipeline milking systems emerged, allowing milk to flow directly through overhead tubes from multiple cows to a central cooler, significantly increasing throughput and hygiene in larger herds.
Since the 1990s, the integration of robotics and artificial intelligence has revolutionized milking parlors, enabling fully automated systems where cows voluntarily enter stalls for milking. The Lely Astronaut, introduced commercially in 1995, exemplifies this era as the first robotic milker, using computer vision and sensors to attach teat cups, monitor udder health, and optimize feeding, with over 100 units sold by 1997. These innovations have continued to evolve, incorporating AI for predictive analytics on milk yield and animal welfare since the early 2000s. As of 2025, further advancements include DeLaval's VMS V300 robotic milker with flow-responsive technology for improved efficiency. The global milking robot market is projected to grow from $2.98 billion in 2024 to $3.39 billion in 2025.

Dairy Milking

Hand Milking Techniques

Hand milking involves manual extraction of milk from dairy animals, primarily cows, goats, and sheep, using tactile manipulation of the udder and teats to stimulate let-down and flow. The process relies on the milker's hands to mimic the natural suckling action, ensuring efficient drainage while minimizing animal discomfort. Traditional hand milking remains prevalent in small-scale and subsistence farming systems worldwide, where it serves as a low-technology alternative to mechanized methods.
Two primary techniques dominate hand milking: the stripping method and the full-hand method. In the stripping method, the milker grasps the teat between the thumb and forefingers at the base, then slides the hand downward toward the teat end to eject milk in streams; this is often used briefly at the start and end of a session to check for abnormalities like mastitis through foremilk inspection. Conversely, the full-hand method employs the entire hand, with the thumb and forefinger forming a ring around the teat base while the remaining fingers gently compress the teat walls sequentially toward the base, simulating a rolling motion to propel milk without sliding; this approach is preferred for the bulk of milking as it reduces teat irritation and promotes complete udder emptying.
Essential tools for hand milking include a low three-legged milking stool for stability, a clean stainless steel or hooded bucket placed between the milker's knees to catch milk and prevent contamination from splashes or dust, and cloths or disposable towels for hygiene. The setup begins with washing the udder and teats using warm water (around 38-40C) and mild soap to remove dirt and stimulate blood flow, followed by thorough drying to avoid bacterial entry; teats are then massaged gently from the udder base toward the tips to encourage let-down. Optimal positioning places the milker on the stool beside or slightly behind the animal, facing the rear quarters first since they hold a larger milk volume (up to 60% of total), with the animal secured in a stanchion or headlock for safety and to facilitate access without startling it.
A typical hand milking session lasts 5-10 minutes per animal, depending on udder size and milk volume, with skilled milkers averaging about 6 minutes to extract the bulk of the yield. Efficiency varies with technique proficiency; the full-hand method by experienced operators can yield 80-90% of available milk, though incomplete emptying risks up to 10-20% retention if stripping is over-relied upon, potentially leading to lower overall production.
Hand milking offers advantages such as minimal capital investment, making it accessible for resource-limited operations, and allows direct observation of the animal's health, including early detection of udder issues during the process. However, it is highly labor-intensive, limiting scalability to small herds of 5-20 animals per milker daily, and poses risks of repetitive strain injuries, particularly to the hands, wrists, and lower back from prolonged gripping and awkward postures.
Regional variations reflect local resources and cultural practices. In African nomadic herding systems, such as among Maasai or Borana pastoralists, milking often occurs outdoors twice daily with calves present to trigger let-down, using smoked gourds or woven vessels for collection and minimal udder cleaning, yielding 1-3 liters per animal amid low supplementary feeding. In contrast, European farmstead approaches on small traditional holdings emphasize seated milking in barns with hooded buckets and pre-washing routines for hygiene, supporting higher yields of 4-6 liters per session through better nutrition and structured routines.

Machine Milking Systems

Machine milking systems in dairy farming rely on vacuum technology to automate the extraction of milk from cows' udders, consisting of several core components designed for efficient and hygienic operation. The primary elements include a vacuum pump, which generates the necessary partial vacuum to draw milk; teat cups, comprising a flexible rubber liner inside a rigid shell that seals around the teat; and a pulsation system that cyclically alternates vacuum and atmospheric pressure in the shell to open and close the liner. The pulsation system typically operates at a 60:40 ratio, where 60% of the cycle applies vacuum for milk flow (compression phase) and 40% admits air to relax the teat and promote blood circulation.
These systems are categorized into three main types based on design and scale: bucket milkers, pipeline systems, and robotic milkers. Bucket milkers are portable units where milk collects directly into attached buckets, suitable for small herds or mobile use, with the vacuum pump and pulsator integrated into a single assembly. Pipeline systems, fixed installations in barns, transport milk through overhead or low-level tubes from teat cups to a central receiver, enabling simultaneous milking of multiple cows in parlors like herringbone or parallel setups. Robotic milkers represent autonomous systems where cows voluntarily enter stalls, and robotic arms attach teat cups, often incorporating advanced features like 3D cameras for precise placement.
In operation, teat cups are attached to the cow's teats, and vacuumtypically maintained at 40-50 kPais applied to initiate milk flow through the liners and tubes to a collection point, such as a bulk cooling tank where milk is rapidly chilled to preserve quality. The pulsation cycle ensures rhythmic extraction, while modern systems, particularly robotic ones, integrate sensors to monitor milk conductivity, flow rates, and color for early detection of mastitis, alerting operators to potential infections. As of 2025, advancements in machine learning algorithms enhance sensor data analysis for more precise mastitis prediction in automatic milking systems. Milk then flows via dedicated lines to insulated cooling tanks, maintaining temperatures below 4C to inhibit bacterial growth.
These systems significantly enhance efficiency in large-scale operations, with parlor-based setups processing over 100 cows per hour, far exceeding manual capacities. Compared to hand milking, machine systems reduce labor requirements by 50-70%, allowing farmers to manage larger herds with fewer workers.
Maintenance is critical to system performance and longevity, involving regular calibration of vacuum levels and pulsation parameters to ensure they remain within optimal ranges, thereby preventing excessive pressure that could cause teat end damage or incomplete milking. Technicians typically inspect and adjust components quarterly, checking for wear in liners and seals, and testing vacuum stability to avoid fluctuations that compromise milk yield or quality.

Hygiene and Animal Welfare

Hygiene protocols in dairy milking are essential to prevent contamination and ensure milk safety. Pre-milking teat disinfection, often using iodine-based dips, reduces bacterial load on the udder surface before attachment of milking equipment, thereby minimizing the risk of intramammary infections. Post-milking procedures include teat dipping with disinfectants to seal the teat canal and remove any residual bacteria or milk films that could harbor pathogens. Additionally, milk is rapidly cooled in bulk tanks to 4C immediately after milking to inhibit bacterial growth and maintain quality during storage.
Bacterial control measures focus on monitoring and limiting microbial contamination in raw milk. In the European Union, the standard plate count limit for bulk tank milk is set at 100,000 colony-forming units (CFU) per milliliter to ensure hygienic production. Somatic cell counts, an indicator of udder health and infection, must not exceed 400,000 cells per milliliter under EU regulations, as higher levels signal potential mastitis. Coliform testing plays a key role in detecting fecal contamination, prompting corrective actions like improved cleaning of milking equipment and cow hygiene to prevent pathogens such as E. coli from entering the milk supply.
Animal welfare considerations in dairy milking emphasize minimizing stress and physical discomfort to promote health and productivity. Cows are typically milked two to three times per day, aligning with their natural lactation rhythms to avoid overdistension of the udder. Gentle handling during milking, such as avoiding forceful restraint and ensuring calm environments, reduces cortisol levels and behavioral stress, which can otherwise impair immune function. The EU Directive 98/58/EC establishes minimum standards for farm conditions, requiring adequate space, freedom from pain, and inspection at least once daily to safeguard animal welfare in dairy operations.
Health monitoring is integral to both hygiene and welfare, with a focus on detecting and preventing conditions like mastitis. Early signs include udder swelling, heat, and abnormal milk appearance, which necessitate immediate isolation and treatment to prevent spread. Prevention strategies involve providing clean, dry bedding to reduce environmental bacteria exposure and balanced nutrition, including adequate selenium and vitamin E, to bolster udder immunity and tissue integrity.
Adhering to these hygiene and welfare practices directly influences milk yield and cow longevity. Poor hygiene can lead to mastitis outbreaks, reducing milk production by 10-20% in affected quarters due to inflammation and discarded milk. Conversely, enhanced welfare through stress reduction and health monitoring extends the productive lifespan of lactating cows, allowing more lactations per animal and improving overall herd efficiency.

Specialized Milking

Venom Milking Processes

Venom milking processes refer to the specialized techniques used to extract venom from venomous animals, primarily snakes and scorpions, for antivenom production, pharmaceutical research, and toxin studies. Pioneering systematic venom collection for antivenom in Brazil began in the mid-1890s under Vital Brazil, with the first effective antivenom serum produced in 1898 at the Bacteriological Institute of So Paulo, where researchers initiated controlled extractions to support serum therapy development. This marked a pivotal advancement in toxinology, transitioning from ad hoc collections to standardized procedures that ensured reliable venom supply for immunization processes.
The primary method for snakes involves manual milking, where a trained herpetologist restrains the animal, often after mild sedation with carbon dioxide to minimize stress, and stimulates the venom glands by applying gentle pressure near the fangs or encouraging a bite onto a collection surface. For certain elapid snakes, such as coral snakes (Micrurus corallinus), an intradermal injection of pilocarpine (10 mg/kg) approximately 10 minutes prior to extraction enhances gland secretion, increasing yield by up to 127%. In this process, the snake's head is held securely, and the fangs are positioned against a thin membrane, such as parafilm stretched over a glass beaker or petri dish, allowing venom droplets to be expressed and collected without direct contact. For scorpions, electrical stimulation is the preferred technique; a low-voltage current (typically 5-12 V) is applied across the telson (tail) using electrodes, prompting the animal to release venom from the stinger onto a glass slide or capillary tube. This method yields purer samples compared to manual squeezing, as it avoids tissue contamination from excessive handling.
Equipment for collection is simple yet precise to preserve venom integrity. Venom is captured on inert surfaces like glass slides for small droplets or synthetic membranes (e.g., parafilm or silicone sheets) over borosilicate glassware to prevent absorption or degradation. Immediately after extraction, samples are placed in ice baths (0-4C) to halt enzymatic activity, then transferred to microcentrifuge tubes or vials for short-term refrigeration. Long-term storage occurs at -20C in a freezer, where lyophilized (freeze-dried) venom remains stable for years, maintaining potency for downstream applications like antivenom immunization.
Species-specific considerations influence extraction protocols and yields. Elapids, such as cobras (Naja spp.), typically produce 100-500 mg of dry venom per milking, with averages ranging from 266 mg for the monocled cobra (Naja kaouthia) to 571 mg for the forest cobra (Naja melanoleuca), depending on factors like age, size, and prior stimulation. To prevent physiological stress and allow gland replenishment, elapids are milked no more than 1-2 times per month, often every 30-60 days under controlled husbandry. Viperids and scorpions follow similar frequency limits, with yields varying by species but emphasizing animal welfare to sustain long-term productivity.
Safety protocols are paramount due to the high risk of envenomation. Procedures are conducted only by trained herpetologists in biosafety level 2 facilities, using personal protective equipment including thick gloves, face shields, aprons, and snake hooks or tongs for restraint. Antivenom kits specific to the species being milked are kept immediately accessible, along with epinephrine for allergic reactions. Post-extraction, equipment is sterilized with 4% sodium hypochlorite, and collection sites on animals are treated with 0.12% chlorhexidine to prevent infection. Collected venom undergoes purity testing via techniques like high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) and sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) to detect contaminants such as salivary proteins or bacterial residues, ensuring suitability for medical use. These measures not only protect handlers but also maintain venom quality for therapeutic applications.

Other Non-Dairy Applications

In apiculture, bee venom is extracted using low-voltage electrical grids placed near hives, where worker bees sting the grid, depositing venom without losing their stingers or dying. This method typically yields approximately 0.1 mg of dry venom per bee, with collections often limited to short sessions to avoid stressing the colony. Royal jelly, a nutrient-rich secretion fed to queen larvae, is harvested manually by scooping it from artificial queen cells using small spatulas or suction tools after removing the larvae, ensuring minimal disruption to the hive.
Ethical sericulture has developed non-lethal approaches to silk production, known as ahimsa or peace silk, where silkworms complete metamorphosis into moths before cocoons are harvested, avoiding the traditional boiling process that kills pupae. This results in shorter, less continuous silk filaments compared to conventional methods, with yields of roughly 300-500 meters per cocoon cycle due to emergence holes pierced by moths.
In biotechnology, the production of proteins such as monoclonal antibodies from mammalian cell cultures in bioreactors serves as an analog to milking, where cells are continuously or periodically cultured and the secreted product is harvested from the medium without harming the cells. Perfusion systems, for instance, mimic repeated extraction by retaining cells while removing antibody-rich supernatant, enabling high-density cultures that produce grams per liter of therapeutic proteins like those used in cancer treatments.
Emerging techniques for extracting bioactive secretions from amphibians include gentle stimulation of skin glands in frogs such as Phyllomedusa bicolor, whose secretions contain dermorphin, a potent opioid precursor for painkillers far stronger than morphine. Traditional methods involve mild physical prompting or scraping to induce secretion release without injury, though innovations like custom stimulators are under exploration to improve yield and welfare in sustainable sourcing.
Since the 2010s, ethical debates in these fields have driven a broader shift toward non-lethal and sustainable extraction methods, emphasizing animal welfare and environmental impact in apiculture, sericulture, and biotechnology to replace harmful practices with scalable, humane alternatives.

Economic and Cultural Aspects

Industry Impact

The dairy industry plays a pivotal role in global agriculture, with milk production reaching 965.7 million metric tons in 2023 and nearly 979 million metric tons in 2024, according to the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO). India, the European Union, and the United States stand as the leading producers of total milk, contributing approximately 236 million, 160.8 million, and 102.7 million metric tons respectively in 2023, accounting for nearly half of the worldwide total. This scale underscores the sector's dominance in agricultural output, supporting food security and nutrition for billions. In 2024, India produced around 239 million metric tons, maintaining its lead, while the EU produced about 149 million metric tons and the US around 103 million metric tons (estimates).
Economically, the dairy sector sustains the livelihoods of over 600 million people globally, including farmers, processors, and distributors, through its extensive operations. The value chain extends from raw milk collection on farms to advanced processing into diverse products like cheese and yogurt, generating substantial revenue and fostering rural development in both developed and developing regions. In many countries, this chain drives export earnings and local economies, with integrated systems enhancing efficiency from production to consumer markets.
Adoption of advanced technologies, such as robotic milking systems, exemplifies the industry's investment in productivity, with individual units costing $185,000$230,000 but offering returns through reduced labor needs. These systems typically achieve payback periods of 5 to 10 years, depending on herd size and operational efficiency, by automating milking processes and minimizing manual intervention.
Beyond dairy, milking processes in non-dairy contexts, particularly venom extraction from snakes, support a pharmaceutical market valued at $1.18 billion in 2023, primarily for antivenom production. However, this niche faces significant supply chain challenges, including shortages due to limited venom sourcing, regulatory hurdles, and production constraints in regions with high snakebite incidence.
Sustainability remains a critical concern, as dairy milking contributes to resource-intensive practices, with the global water footprint averaging around 1,000 liters per liter of milk produced, encompassing irrigation for feed crops and on-farm usage. Efficient milking technologies and management strategies have enabled carbon footprint reductions, such as ~11% globally in emission intensity from 2005 to 2015 and 19% per unit of milk in the US from 2007 to 2017, by improving milk yield per cow and optimizing energy use in operations.

Cultural Significance

Milking has long served as a powerful symbol of nurturing and sustenance in various cultural traditions. In ancient Roman folklore, the goddess Rumina embodied this metaphor, protecting breastfeeding mothers and infants by ensuring the flow of milk, with her temple located near the sacred Ficus Ruminalis on the Capitoline Hill. Similarly, in India, the festival of Makar Sankranti honors cows through rituals that emphasize their role in providing nourishment, including bathing, decorating, and feeding them as acts of reverence tied to agricultural abundance and maternal care.
Traditional practices highlight milking's integration into communal and nomadic lifestyles. Among the Maasai people of East Africa, women traditionally handle the milking of cattle, a task central to their semi-nomadic pastoral economy where milk forms a staple of the diet, often fermented into products like kule naoto. In the United States, Amish communities persist with hand-milking methods, maintaining small-scale dairies of 40 to 70 cows without electricity or advanced machinery, preserving a way of life rooted in simplicity and self-sufficiency.
Historically, milking was predominantly a female domain across many societies, reflecting gendered divisions of labor where women managed dairy tasks from medieval European farms to 19th-century American homesteads. The advent of mechanization in the 20th century disrupted these roles, displacing women from dairy work in regions like rural France and pushing many toward urban opportunities or non-farm employment.
In modern media, milking appears as an emblem of rural idyll and family labor. Laura Ingalls Wilder's Little House series vividly depicts hand-milking as a daily chore on pioneer farms, underscoring themes of self-reliance and maternal provision in 19th-century American life. In art, 19th-century pastoral paintings, such as Jean-Franois Millet's milkmaid scenes and Aelbert Cuyp's Landscape with Maid Milking a Cow, romanticize the act as a serene, harmonious bond between humans and nature.
Since the 1970s, animal rights movements have reshaped cultural perceptions of industrial milking, critiquing factory farming practices through works like Peter Singer's Animal Liberation (1975), which highlighted ethical concerns over animal confinement and exploitation in dairy production. This activism has influenced public discourse, fostering veganism and welfare reforms that challenge the traditional view of milking as benign nurturing.
