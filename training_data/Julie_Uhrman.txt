Julie Uhrman
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Julie Uhrman is an American entrepreneur and sports executive who founded the OUYA video game console company in 2012 and co-founded Angel City FC, a professional women's soccer team in the National Women's Soccer League, in 2020.
Uhrman launched OUYA as an affordable, open-source Android-based console priced at $99, raising over $8.5 million through Kickstarter to enable independent developers to publish games without traditional publisher approval. Despite initial hype, OUYA achieved limited commercial success, criticized for subpar hardware performance, controller quality, and a lack of exclusive, high-quality games, leading to its operational wind-down by 2015.
Following OUYA, Uhrman served as executive vice president of over-the-top ventures at Lionsgate before pivoting to sports, where she co-founded Angel City FC with investors including Natalie Portman and venture capitalist Kara Nortman, raising over $250 million in private equity to establish what became the highest-valued women's professional sports franchise. The club debuted in 2022, emphasizing community engagement and female leadership, but has encountered financial strains, internal conflicts, and NWSL penalties, including a $200,000 fine and executive suspensions in 2024 for salary cap circumvention via undisclosed player payments.

Early Life and Education

Family Background and Upbringing

Julie Uhrman was born and raised in Los Angeles, California, where she spent her formative years immersed in the local culture and environment.
She grew up with her twin sister, with whom she shared a highly competitive dynamic, often striving to match or exceed activities typically associated with boys. This sibling rivalry fostered a tomboyish upbringing marked by physical challenges and sports participation, beginning with basketball as her initial athletic pursuit.
Uhrman's early exposure to creative processes stemmed from her family environment, including influences from her stepfather, though she has described herself as not inherently creative despite this backdrop. Limited public details exist regarding her parents' professions or specific family dynamics beyond these elements, reflecting a focus in available accounts on her personal development rather than extensive familial lineage.

Academic and Professional Preparation

Julie Uhrman graduated from Washington University in St. Louis in 1996 with a Bachelor of Science in Business Administration, holding a dual degree in finance and management. This educational foundation equipped her with core business acumen relevant to her subsequent entrepreneurial pursuits in technology and sports.
She later pursued advanced studies, earning a Master of Business Administration from UCLA Anderson School of Management. The MBA program emphasized strategic management and finance, aligning with her transition into executive roles in media, gaming, and venture development.
In her initial professional steps post-undergraduate, Uhrman worked in investment banking for several years, gaining experience in financial analysis and deal-making that informed her later business ventures. This period honed her skills in capital markets and corporate finance, providing practical preparation for founding tech startups and sports franchises.

Early Career

Media and Entertainment Roles

Uhrman began her professional career in the gaming and digital media sectors, holding executive positions that emphasized business development and distribution strategies. At IGN Entertainment, a prominent video game news and media platform, she served as Vice President of Digital Distribution, leading initiatives in digital content delivery, partnerships, and expansion of online gaming services during the mid-2000s. This role involved overseeing the transition of game-related media toward digital platforms, aligning with the growing demand for online access to reviews, downloads, and multimedia content.
She also occupied leadership positions at Vivendi Universal, where she contributed to executive functions in entertainment distribution, focusing on video games and home entertainment products under the company's broader media umbrella. Similarly, at GameFly, a subscription-based rental service for video games and DVDs, Uhrman held a key executive role that supported operational growth in physical and digital media rentals, capitalizing on the era's shift from retail purchases to accessible entertainment models. These experiences, spanning approximately a decade starting around 2003, provided foundational expertise in monetizing media assets amid technological disruptions like broadband adoption and early digital downloads.

Transition to Gaming and Tech

Following executive positions in broader entertainment distribution, including at Vivendi Universal, Uhrman shifted focus to the gaming sector around the early 2000s, leveraging her business development expertise in digital media. She joined IGN Entertainment, where she directed digital distribution strategies and business development initiatives aimed at expanding online game access and partnerships. This role immersed her in the evolving landscape of console and digital gaming, highlighting opportunities in accessible platforms amid rising mobile and online trends.
Subsequently, Uhrman took on leadership responsibilities at GameFly, a subscription-based game rental service, further deepening her involvement in consumer-facing gaming operations and supply chain logistics for physical and digital titles. These experiences, spanning over a decade in gaming media and distribution by 2012, equipped her with firsthand knowledge of developer challenges, market barriers for indie creators, and the demand for affordable hardware alternatives to dominant consoles. By 2011, amid frustrations with closed ecosystems controlled by major publishers, she began conceptualizing an open-source Android-based console to democratize game development and lower entry costs for players, marking her pivot to tech entrepreneurship. This transition reflected a causal shift from operational roles in content delivery to innovating hardware solutions, driven by observed gaps in platform accessibility rather than incremental media expansions.

Ouya Venture

Founding and Crowdfunding Success

Julie Uhrman founded Ouya, Inc. in 2012 with the goal of developing an affordable, open-source microconsole based on the Android operating system, designed to democratize access to TV gaming by allowing developers to create and distribute games without proprietary restrictions. The venture was co-founded with partners including Roy Bahat and designer Yves Bhar, leveraging Uhrman's prior experience in gaming media and digital distribution.
On July 10, 2012, Ouya launched a Kickstarter crowdfunding campaign seeking $950,000 to fund prototype development and initial production, promising backers an elegant $99 console with hackable hardware and a focus on indie game development. The campaign exceeded its goal within eight hours, generating widespread media attention for its potential to disrupt closed console ecosystems dominated by Sony, Microsoft, and Nintendo.
By the campaign's close on August 9, 2012, Ouya had raised $8,596,475 from 63,416 backers worldwide, marking it as one of the most successful Kickstarter projects at the time and the third-highest funded overall. This influx enabled rapid scaling, including commitments to ship units to backers by March 2013 and broader retail availability later that year. The crowdfunding triumph validated Uhrman's vision of community-driven innovation in gaming hardware, attracting further investment and partnerships.

Development Challenges and Launch

Following the Kickstarter campaign's conclusion in August 2012, Ouya Inc. faced hurdles in hardware finalization and production scaling. The team, led by Julie Uhrman, collaborated with designer Yves Bhar to refine the Android-based microconsole featuring a Nvidia Tegra 3 quad-core processor, 1 GB RAM, and 8 GB storage. Developers accessing prototype units reported hardware constraints limiting game complexity, with concerns over performance for TV-centric experiences.
A key development setback involved the controller, which exhibited defects like loose buttons due to imprecise molding. This necessitated a redesign, enlarging button holes to ensure proper fit, and contributed to delaying the retail launch from June 4 to June 25, 2013. Uhrman cited the fix as straightforward but essential for quality, alongside increased production to meet retailer commitments from partners like Target and Best Buy.
Kickstarter backers began receiving units on March 28, 2013, marking the initial distribution phase, though reports emerged of shipping discrepancies and minor delays for some recipients. The launch introduced the $99 console to retail shelves, emphasizing its open-source discoverability and developer access, but initial units revealed ongoing integration issues between the custom interface and Android ecosystem.

Commercial Failure and Criticisms

Despite its crowdfunding triumph, the Ouya console faltered commercially after launching on June 25, 2013, with widespread critiques targeting hardware and software shortcomings. Reviewers and users highlighted persistent lag, unreliable controllers prone to connectivity failures, and an underdeveloped user interface that undermined playability. The game library, while open to indie developers, suffered from a scarcity of high-quality exclusives, with many titles feeling unpolished or derivative of mobile apps, failing to differentiate Ouya from existing Android TV devices or established consoles.
Engagement metrics underscored the underperformance: by July 2013, 73 percent of Ouya owners had not purchased any paid games, reflecting limited consumer investment beyond the initial hardware buy-in. Kickstarter backers voiced frustrations over delayed or unfulfilled orders, substandard controller durability, and inadequate game selection, leading Ouya to extend store credit as remediation in August 2013. These issues contributed to sluggish sales, as evidenced by developer reports of modest unit revenuessuch as one title selling around 2,000 copies at $15 each shortly after launchinsufficient to sustain momentum against competitors.
Under Julie Uhrman's tenure as founder and CEO, Ouya grappled with execution missteps, including manufacturing delays and an advertising campaign perceived as alienating core gamers. By April 2015, Uhrman notified investors that the company had not met financial obligations, prompting her exit as CEO in July 2015. Hardware production ceased thereafter, with Ouya's software assets acquired by Razer Inc., which ended console support amid ongoing viability concerns. Uhrman later acknowledged multiple operational errors in post-mortem discussions, attributing them to the challenges of rapid scaling in a competitive market.

Angel City FC

Founding Vision and Structure

Angel City FC was founded on August 13, 2020, by entrepreneur Julie Uhrman, venture capitalist Kara Nortman, and actress Natalie Portman, with the explicit vision of creating a professional women's soccer club that transcended traditional sports franchises by integrating commercial success with social impact and community empowerment. Uhrman, serving as co-founder and president, articulated the core goal as building a "nexus between mission and capital," aiming to elevate women's sports through a model that prioritized long-term community relationships, fan engagement, and economic investment in underserved areas, rather than relying solely on on-field performance. This approach sought to position the club as a global brand "bigger than the game," with an audacious target of achieving the first billion-dollar valuation in women's sports history by fostering inclusive ownership and revenue streams beyond ticket sales.
The club's structure diverged from conventional sports ownership by establishing a decentralized investor base comprising over 100 minority stakeholders, including celebrities, athletes, and business leaders, to democratize equity and align incentives with diverse community interests. Uhrman championed this model to embed social responsibility directly into operations, notably through the "10% Model," which allocates 10% of the club's resources to impact initiatives like youth programs, girls' soccer access in low-income neighborhoods, and partnerships with local organizations for sustained community development. Unlike corporate-dominated franchises, this framework emphasized individual investor commitments to the mission, with Uhrman leading efforts to secure initial funding exceeding $100 million by 2022, drawn from high-profile backers such as Alexis Ohanian and Serena Williams, while maintaining operational control through the founding trio.
This founding architecture reflected Uhrman's prior experience in disruptive ventures, adapting crowdfunding-inspired equity distribution from her Ouya project to sports, but with rigorous governance to ensure fiscal discipline amid the NWSL's expansion. The structure facilitated rapid scaling, including the team's entry into the National Women's Soccer League in 2022 as an expansion franchise, while embedding accountability mechanisms for impact metrics, such as annual reporting on community investments, to validate the vision's causal links between capital infusion and tangible societal outcomes.

Achievements in Growth and Valuation

Angel City FC, co-founded by Julie Uhrman in 2020, achieved rapid growth upon entering the National Women's Soccer League (NWSL) in 2022, setting league records for attendance with an average of over 19,000 fans per game in its inaugural season and leading the league in two of its first three years. The club recorded four sellouts and surpassed attendance figures for some Major League Baseball and Major League Soccer teams in Los Angeles during that period, contributing to single-game gate revenues exceeding $1 million.
Under Uhrman's leadership as president and later CEO, the team generated $33 million in revenue in 2023, more than double that of its nearest NWSL competitor, driven by sponsorships, ticket sales, and merchandise. This financial performance supported broader league expansion, with NWSL average team valuations rising 57% to $104 million by 2024, amid record overall attendance surpassing 2 million fans league-wide that year.
In July 2024, an investment agreement valuing Angel City at $250 millionthe highest for any women's professional sports teammarked a pinnacle of its valuation growth, with subsequent Forbes estimates placing it at $280 million in 2025, topping NWSL franchises. Uhrman's strategy of prioritizing value-aligned investors and community-focused partnerships underpinned this ascent, positioning the club as a model for women's sports franchising despite operational challenges.

Financial Struggles, Controversies, and Ownership Transition

In 2024, Angel City FC reported record annual revenue of $35.4 million for a women's professional sports team, yet the club faced escalating financial demands for sustained growth and operations, prompting a search for additional capital and a new controlling owner. These pressures were compounded by internal mismanagement, including a major salary cap violation uncovered by the National Women's Soccer League (NWSL). The league's investigation revealed that the club had executed undisclosed "side letters" with five players signed in 2023, effectively circumventing salary cap rules by providing off-books compensation tied to performance incentives and other benefits.
The scandal resulted in severe penalties: a $200,000 fine, a three-point deduction from the 2024 standings, forfeiture of supplemental disciplinary allocation money, and suspensions for president Julie Uhrman and general manager Angela Hucles Mangano from all player-related transactions through the end of the season. This marked the second such infraction for the club, following a 2021 tampering violation that incurred lesser fines. Uhrman, as a key executive overseeing operations, bore responsibility for the non-disclosure, which the NWSL described as a deliberate breach undermining league integrity. The incident, reportedly tipped off by a former player, highlighted operational lapses amid rapid expansion.
Broader controversies stemmed from persistent board-level infighting and power struggles, as detailed in a Wall Street Journal investigation, which portrayed a fractured leadership marked by clashing egos, strategic disagreements, and interpersonal conflicts among co-founders and investors. Tensions reportedly peaked between Uhrman and investor Alexis Ohanian, with disputes over hiring decisions, spending priorities, and control influencing day-to-day governance. These dynamics, including resistance to a proposed male head coach hire in 2021 due to fan backlash, eroded cohesion and amplified perceptions of dysfunction despite the club's high-profile backing.
The combination of these issues culminated in an ownership transition announced on July 17, 2024, with media executive Willow Bay and her husband, Disney CEO Bob Iger, acquiring controlling interest in a deal valuing the club at $250 millionthe highest for any women's professional sports franchise. Bay assumed full control of the board and NWSL governance representation, while original co-founders including Uhrman, Natalie Portman, and Kara Nortman retained seats. The shift followed months of board squabbles and a formal process initiated in early 2024 to secure stable leadership and funding, addressing the instability that had hindered progress. Uhrman transitioned to CEO in February 2025, signaling continuity in executive roles post-transition, as the club added minority investors like NBA star Chris Paul later that year to bolster its structure.

Recognition and Broader Impact

Awards and Industry Influence

Uhrman has been recognized for her contributions to sports, technology, and entrepreneurship through various industry awards and honors. In 2025, she was named Innovator of the Year by the Los Angeles Business Journal for her leadership in elevating Angel City FC to the most valuable women's sports team globally. Previously, in 2024, she received the Brand Genius Award from Adweek, inclusion in the Inc. Female Founders 250 list, and recognition in the LA500 by the Los Angeles Business Journal. In 2023, the Los Angeles Parks Foundation honored her with the Rose Award for her community impact via Angel City FC.
Earlier accolades from her gaming career include designation as one of the 100 Most Creative People in Business by Fast Company and inclusion in Ad Age's Creative 50, reflecting her role in launching OUYA as an open-source Android console that raised over $8.5 million via Kickstarter in 2012. In sports business, Uhrman was named one of the Most Influential People in Sports Business in 2022 by the Sports Business Journal and one of the Ten Influencers of 2023 by SportsPro Media.
Her influence spans gaming and women's sports, where she pioneered innovative business models. With OUYA, Uhrman sought to democratize game development through affordable, hacker-friendly hardware, influencing crowdfunding strategies for consumer tech despite the venture's eventual sale to Razer in 2015 amid commercial underperformance. At Angel City FC, co-founded in 2020, she implemented a fan-ownership structure with over 25,000 equity members and secured a valuation exceeding $180 million by 2023, establishing a replicable blueprint for sustainable growth in women's professional leagues that emphasizes community investment over traditional billionaire ownership. This approach contributed to Angel City FC winning Sports Team of the Year at the 2023 Sports Business Journal Awardsthe first for a women's teamand has positioned her as a speaker at events like the MIT Sloan Sports Analytics Conference, where she advocates for mission-driven capital in sports. Uhrman has also served on award juries, such as Clio Sports in 2023, extending her impact in evaluating creative excellence in sports media.

Views on Entrepreneurship and Women's Sports

Uhrman describes a core tenet of entrepreneurship as requiring bravery and courage, where entrepreneurs perceive solutions amid problems and opportunities within challenges. She views entrepreneurial endeavors as inherently risky, involving the persuasion of investors, team members, and customers to realize a vision, as exemplified by her experiences founding Ouya and Angel City FC. In leadership, Uhrman prioritizes hiring skilled individuals and granting them autonomy and accountability to execute independently, fostering an environment of optimism, collaboration, and obstacle removal.
Applying these principles to women's sports, Uhrman co-founded Angel City FC in 2020 to demonstrate that such ventures can function as lucrative enterprises while advancing equitable recognition and compensation across genders. She advocates for a business model where mission and capital coexist, leading with passion and purpose to attain profitability, rather than treating investment in women's athletics as charitable. Uhrman positions Angel City not merely as a soccer club but as an organization generating broader societal impact, establishing elevated standards for player treatment, league operations, and community engagement.
Uhrman regards women's soccer as a vehicle for addressing gender and pay equity, leveraging the sport's platform to elevate player experiences and drive revenue through community investment and innovative sponsorships, such as allocating 10% of deals to local causes. By raising over $100 million in funding and achieving near-capacity attendance at BMO Stadium, she substantiates the viability of purpose-driven profitability in this sector, contrasting prior undervaluations of franchises at around $2 million with Angel City's trajectory toward $200 million.

Personal Life

Relationships and Family

Julie Uhrman was born and raised in Los Angeles, California, alongside her twin sister, Amy Uhrman (now Amy Longhi).
Uhrman was married to Kelly Jo Kirkpatrick until Kirkpatrick filed for divorce on November 29, 2023, citing irreconcilable differences after approximately 15 years of marriage. The couple shares custody of their two minor children, a son named Charlie (born circa 2011) and a daughter named Elle (born circa 2008), who were aged 12 and 15, respectively, at the time of the filing. Uhrman's involvement with Angel City FC has influenced her family, as the club inspired her son Charlie to take up soccer, while her daughter Elle participates in volleyball.

Public Persona and Interests

Julie Uhrman maintains a public image centered on competitive drive, entrepreneurial ambition, and advocacy for women's athletics. Born and raised in Los Angeles, she has frequently highlighted her early experiences in sports as formative, noting that at age nine, she and her twin sister pursued basketball through a local YMCA league flyer they brought home. This foundation in team sports underscores her persona as a hands-on competitor, having played basketball during her undergraduate years at Washington University in St. Louis while earning degrees in finance and management. Uhrman's enthusiasm for physical competition extends into her professional narrative, where she positions herself as embodying the "We are Angel City" ethos of her soccer club, reflecting a community-rooted, fan-first approach to leadership.
Her disclosed interests emphasize sportsmanship and civic leadership over leisure pursuits, with basketball as a recurring touchstone despite her later pivot to socceradmitting she never played the sport growing up but channeling her passion into building professional platforms for female athletes. Public appearances and interviews portray her as mission-oriented, prioritizing inclusive growth in underrepresented sectors like women's professional leagues, which aligns with her broader advocacy for virtues such as perseverance and collective achievement in athletics. This persona avoids overt personal disclosures, focusing instead on actionable impacts in gaming and sports ecosystems, where her ventures like Ouya and Angel City FC serve as extensions of her competitive ethos.
