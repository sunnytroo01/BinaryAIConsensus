World Netball
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 World Netball is the sole internationally recognised governing body for the sport of netball, responsible for promoting, regulating, and developing the game on a global scale.
Founded in 1960 as the International Federation of Womens Basketball and Netball Associations (IFWBNA) in Colombo, Sri Lanka, the organisation initially oversaw both netball and women's basketball before focusing exclusively on netball.
It later became known as the International Netball Federation (INF) and underwent a rebranding to World Netball in 2021, accompanied by a new strategic plan aimed at growing the sport's global participation and visibility.
Headquartered in London, United Kingdom, World Netball is affiliated with the Association of Recognised International Sports Federations (ARISF) and receives funding from the International Olympic Committee (IOC).
The organisation is governed by a Board of Directors, including a President, Vice President, Finance Director, and representatives from its five regional federations: Africa, Americas, Asia, Europe, and Oceania.
It boasts 65 full member nations and 12 associate members, with netball actively played in over 100 countries and supported by 76 national netball associations worldwide.
World Netball maintains official rankings for 52 national teams and organises major international events, including the Netball World Cup (biennially from 2027, previously every four years since 1963), the FAST5 Netball World Series, and the Netball World Youth Cup (held every four years; the 2025 edition was in Gibraltar).
Through initiatives like the NetballPass digital platform, which won the 'Best Fan Engagement Programme' at the 2024 Sports Business Awards, the organisation enhances fan interaction and accessibility.
Additionally, World Netball is committed to sustainability, targeting Net Zero emissions by 2040 as part of its broader mission to inspire participation and preserve the integrity of the sport.

Organisation

Headquarters

World Netball's headquarters is situated at Tomorrow, MediaCityUK, Salford Quays, Salford, Greater Manchester, England, M50 2AB. This location serves as the primary operational base for the international governing body of netball.
The organization relocated to these premises in 2021, transitioning to modern, carbon-neutral offices that align with its commitment to sustainable practices. This move from previous sites positioned World Netball in a vibrant media-centric district, home to major broadcasters and creative industries, thereby enhancing its visibility and access to global media networks for promoting the sport.
As the central hub, the headquarters oversees key administrative functions, including event planning for major international competitions and coordination with member nations across more than 100 countries. It houses a dedicated staff team responsible for day-to-day operations, supporting the execution of strategic initiatives and governance activities.
The facilities at MediaCityUK are equipped to facilitate digital operations, such as online resource management and virtual event hosting, alongside robust global communications infrastructure to engage with affiliates and stakeholders worldwide. This setup ensures efficient support for netball's growth and international outreach.

Governance

World Netball's governance is structured around key bodies that ensure accountability, strategic oversight, and global coordination of the sport. The supreme authority is the Congress, which serves as the general meeting of its membership and holds ultimate decision-making power on major policies, elections, and strategic directions. Ordinary Congress meetings occur biennially, typically preceding major events like the Netball World Cup or Netball World Youth Cup, with special meetings convened as required.
The most recent Congress was held virtually on July 10, 2025, attended by delegates from 49 full members and 3 associate members, where progress was reviewed, priorities were set, and board elections took place. Full members appoint up to two delegates each with voting rights, while associate members send one non-voting observer; nominations for positions must be endorsed by two full members and submitted 60 days in advance.
Overseeing day-to-day operations and strategy is the Board of Directors, which manages the organization's business and ensures compliance with governance standards. The Board, comprising the President, Vice-President, a Finance Director, additional independent directors, regional representatives, and an Athlete Director, meets at least twice annually or more frequently as needed to implement Congress decisions and drive netball's global growth.
The Articles of Association, updated in March 2023, outline the detailed roles, election processes, and operational frameworks, including term limits of four years (with a maximum of three terms) and requirements for secret ballots in elections. These articles also govern affiliations with regional federations, which must align with World Netball's regulations and elect regional directors to the Board, as well as international bodies such as the Association of IOC Recognised International Sports Federations (ARISF) and the International World Games Association (IWGA).
World Netball has been recognized by the International Olympic Committee (IOC) since 1995, enabling access to funding and alignment with Olympic standards, and it maintains full compliance with the World Anti-Doping Code as a signatory to the World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA) framework.
A significant recent development in governance was the appointment of Stacey Francis-Bayman as the first Athlete Director to the Board in July 2023 during Congress, enhancing athlete representation and fulfilling commitments to inclusive leadership structures. This role, appointed through an open process, underscores World Netball's evolving framework to prioritize athlete voices in strategic decisions. Governance activities are supported from the organization's headquarters in Manchester, United Kingdom, which serves as the operational hub.

History

Founding

World Netball was founded in 1960 as the International Federation of Women's Basketball and Netball Associations (IFWBNA) during a conference held in Colombo, Ceylon (now Sri Lanka). The inaugural meeting brought together representatives from five nationsAustralia, England, New Zealand, South Africa, and the West Indiesto address the need for standardized rules and international governance of netball, a sport that had evolved from basketball but developed distinct characteristics, particularly in women's play. This gathering stemmed from earlier discussions in 1957 during an Australian tour of England, where inconsistencies in rules across countries highlighted the urgency for a unified body. The IFWBNA's formation marked the first formal step toward separating netball's administration from basketball, emphasizing its focus on women's participation and promoting the sport as an independent discipline.
The primary purpose of the IFWBNA was to establish uniform rules for netball, ensuring consistency in gameplay and facilitating international competition among predominantly women's teams. At the 1960 conference, formal rules were adopted, including decisions on holding a full council meeting (congress) and a world tournament every four years, which laid the groundwork for global events. Key figures involved in the founding included representatives from the national associations of the initial members, though specific individuals such as Rena Stratford, who later served as honorary secretary of the evolving federation, played roles in early administrative efforts. These inaugural proceedings underscored the emphasis on standardization to support the sport's growth.
Early membership was restricted to the five founding Commonwealth nations, reflecting netball's deep roots in the British colonial legacy, where the sport had spread through educational and missionary networks in the early 20th century. This limited scope initially positioned the IFWBNA as a body governing a sport tied to English-speaking, Commonwealth-dominated regions, with South Africa's involvement highlighting the colonial ties despite emerging political tensions. The focus remained on fostering women's international netball without broader expansion at the outset.

Development

Following its founding in 1960 as the International Federation of Women's Basketball and Netball Associations, the organization underwent a significant renaming in 1971 to the International Federation of Netball Associations (IFNA), reflecting netball's separation from basketball and its growing independence as a distinct sport. In 2013, it rebranded to the International Netball Federation (INF) to align more closely with other international sports governing bodies. This evolution culminated in June 2021 with a further rebrand to World Netball, aimed at enhancing global branding and accessibility to broaden the sport's reach beyond traditional Commonwealth nations.
World Netball's membership has expanded dramatically since the 1960s, when it comprised around 10 founding nations primarily from the Commonwealth, such as Australia, England, New Zealand, and South Africa. By 2025, membership had grown to 77 full and associate members (65 full and 12 associate) across five regions, incorporating non-Commonwealth countries like the United States, Hong Kong, and several in Europe and Asia, which has diversified the sport's global footprint. Notably, the Africa region has seen rapid growth, increasing from just three active members in the early days to 21 by late 2025, including 13 full members like Kenya, Namibia, Zambia, and Zimbabwe, driven by targeted development programs and regional events.
Key milestones have marked this development, including International Olympic Committee (IOC) recognition in 1995, which provided access to funding and elevated netball's international profile. In 2004, World Netball integrated anti-doping measures by adopting its own rules aligned with the World Anti-Doping Code, ensuring compliance and integrity in competitions. Digital initiatives have further supported growth, such as the launch of the World Netball Rankings system in 2008 by statistician David Kendix, which objectively assesses team performances based on international matches, with annual updates including a significant revision in June 2025 to reflect recent results.
The organization has adeptly responded to challenges, including adaptations during the COVID-19 pandemic through event postponements, such as the Netball World Youth Cup 2021, and the creation of a COVID-19 Response Fund to provide financial aid to regional federations. At the 2023 Congress in Cape Town, Dame Liz Nicholl was re-elected as President, reinforcing stable leadership amid the Netball World Cup hosting. The 2025 virtual Congress saw Shirley Hooper re-elected as Vice President, alongside service awards recognizing contributions to the sport's expansion to individuals including Dr Grace Bryant OAM, Noeleen Dix AM, Pam Smith, and Wai Taumaunu. On July 31, 2025, netball was announced as one of the sports for the 2027 Commonwealth Youth Games in Malta, using a 7s format.

Membership

Regions

World Netball organizes its membership through five regional federations: Africa Netball, Americas Netball, Asia Netball, Europe Netball, and Oceania Netball, which collectively represent 78 members (66 full and 12 associate) as of November 2025. These federations serve as the primary agencies for regional governance, facilitating the implementation of World Netball's global policies while tailoring development strategies to local contexts.
Each regional federation is responsible for organizing continental events, delivering development programs, and establishing talent pathways to foster netball's growth. For instance, they provide grants, equipment, technical training in areas like coaching and umpiring, and opportunities for international competition to build capacity among member nations. The federations were historically formed to decentralize governance from the central body, enabling more targeted promotion of the sport in diverse geographic and cultural settings, which has supported overall membership expansion.
Africa Netball exemplifies this growth, expanding from three active nations in the early 2000s to 21 active nations by 2025, including 13 full members and 3 associates, alongside 5 emerging programs. This rapid development in a previously underrepresented region highlights the federations' role in addressing barriers to participation and infrastructure. Similarly, Asia Netball, with 13 full and 5 associate members, emphasizes grassroots integration through school-based programs to broaden access among youth. Europe Netball, comprising 13 full members (including recent addition Italy in 2025), prioritizes inclusion efforts via working groups that support emerging countries and promote equitable opportunities for diverse participants.
The remaining regions show varied scales: Americas Netball includes 19 full and 2 associate members across a vast area, focusing on strategic plans for competitions and training; Oceania Netball has 7 full and 2 associate members, leveraging historical events like the Pacific Games to strengthen regional ties. Across all regions, the 12 associate members represent nations in early stages of integration, underscoring ongoing efforts to expand netball's global footprint.

Members

World Netball comprises 66 full member associations and 12 associate member associations, totaling 78 members across five regional federations as of November 2025. Full members, which possess voting rights at Congress and full participation privileges in governance and events, represent established national netball bodies such as Australia, England, and Jamaica. Associate members, designed for emerging nations to support development, lack voting rights but access similar resources; examples include recent African additions like Liberia and Seychelles.
Admission to World Netball occurs through a formal application process outlined in the organization's Articles of Association. Prospective members submit written applications to the Board, including their constitution, details of officeholders, financial accounts, membership fees, and evidence of compliance with criteria such as exclusive governance of netball in their country, non-discrimination policies, solvency, and affiliation with a regional federation. The Board reviews applications, and approval requires a Special Resolution at Congress or via written resolution, with membership effective upon ratification. This process ensures alignment with World Netball's standards, including adherence to the World Anti-Doping Code.
Membership provides key benefits, including access to development grants, equipment and kit supplies, technical expertise from World Netball staff, and qualification pathways for international competitions such as the Netball World Cup and regional events. Full members additionally influence strategic priorities through voting at the annual Congress, while associates follow a structured pathway toward full status within five years. These supports aim to grow the sport globally, with membership expanding from 42 full and 4 associate members reported at the 2023 Congress to the current totals.
Members are distributed across regions, with representative examples highlighting the sport's reach. In Africa, full members include South Africa (Netball South Africa) and Malawi (Netball Association of Malawi), alongside associates like Botswana (Botswana Netball Association). The Americas feature full members such as Jamaica (Netball Jamaica) and Barbados (Barbados Netball Association), with associates including Venezuela. Asia's full members encompass Singapore (Netball Singapore) and Malaysia (Malaysia Netball Association), supported by associates like Bahrain (Bahrain Netball). Europe's full members comprise England (England Netball), Wales (Wales Netball), and Italy (Federazione Italiana Netball). Oceania includes full members New Zealand (Netball New Zealand) and Australia (Netball Australia), with associates such as Norfolk Island (Norfolk Islands Netball Association).

Competitions

Major events

The Netball World Cup serves as World Netball's flagship senior international competition, contested every four years since its inception and featuring the sport's top national teams in a round-robin and knockout format. The tournament, originally known as the World Netball Championships, began in 1963 in Eastbourne, England, where Australia claimed the inaugural title by winning all 10 matches. Australia has dominated the event, securing 12 of the 16 titles to date, including the most recent in 2023.
The 2023 Netball World Cup, hosted in Cape Town, South Africa from 28 August to 10 September, drew a record global audience, with cumulative linear TV viewership reaching 27.6 million across more than 90 countriesa 72% increase in broadcast hours from 817 in 2019 to 1,409. Australia defeated England 6145 in the final to claim their fifth consecutive championship, while Jamaica earned bronze. The event's success underscored netball's growing popularity, with live linear TV audiences more than doubling to 14.9 million compared to 7.3 million in 2019.
Qualification for the Netball World Cup typically grants automatic entry to the host nation and the top five teams in the World Netball rankings, with the remaining 10 spots filled by the top two finishers from each of five regional qualifiers held in the year prior to the tournament. Hosting rotates among member nations through a bidding process; Australia secured the 2027 rights in 2020 after submitting a formal bid, marking their fourth time staging the event. From 2027 onward, the tournament will shift to a biennial cycle, occurring every November starting with the 2029 edition, to align with calendar reforms and reduce reliance on multi-sport events. The 2027 edition in Sydney, scheduled for 25 August to 5 September, will introduce a Cup and Plate competition structure, with 16 teams divided into four pools before advancing to parallel knockout stages, alongside expansions like a dedicated men's tournament.
Netball's inclusion in the Commonwealth Games provides another premier senior competition, debuting as a demonstration sport at the 1990 Games in Auckland, New Zealand, before becoming a full medal event in 1998 in Kuala Lumpur, Malaysia. The biennial Games feature 12 top-ranked teams in a pool and knockout format, with Australia holding the most gold medals (12 of 16). The 2026 edition in Glasgow, Scotland, is set for 25 July to 2 August, qualifying the world's top 12 nations based on rankings as of a specified cutoff date, and will utilize the SEC Armadillo arena for all matches.
The Fast5 Netball World Series offers an annual fast-paced variant for elite senior teams, originating as the Fast Net World Series in Manchester, England, in 2009, where New Zealand won the inaugural title. Evolving into Fast5 in 2012, the event shortens quarters to 6 minutes, limits teams to five players, and incorporates power plays and super shots worth two or three points to heighten intensity. It now features nine women's teams from top-ranked nationsAustralia, England, Fiji, Jamaica, Malawi, New Zealand, Samoa, South Africa, and Ugandacompeting in a round-robin followed by semifinals and a final, with a men's division added in 2022. Australia has won the women's title three consecutive times (20222024), while New Zealand claimed the 2023 men's crown; the 2024 series concluded on 10 November in Christchurch, New Zealand.
World Netball maintains a rankings system updated periodicallyoften monthly or after major international matchesto reflect team performances, awarding points based on match outcomes relative to opponents' ratings (e.g., a win yields 50 points more than the opponent's rating). As of 1 September 2025, Australia leads with 5,308 points, followed by New Zealand (5,072) and Jamaica (4,602), influencing automatic qualification for events like the Netball World Cup and Commonwealth Games. These rankings, introduced in 2007 and refined over time, require teams to play at least eight matches for an official position and drive increased international fixtures to improve standings.

Youth and variants

World Netball supports youth development through dedicated international competitions that identify and nurture emerging talent under the age of 21. The flagship event is the Netball World Youth Cup, a biennial tournament for under-21 national teams that has been held since 1988, providing a platform for young athletes to compete at the highest level and transition toward senior international play.
The 2025 edition, hosted in Gibraltar from September 19 to 28, featured 20 teams divided into five pools during the initial stage, followed by a restructured format where the top eight teams advanced to quarterfinals, semifinals, and a final, while others competed in classification matches for positions 920; this included a new grouping for 9th12th and 13th20th placings to ensure competitive play across all levels. Australia emerged as champions, defeating New Zealand 6348 in the final to claim their fifth title overall, with New Zealand securing silver and England bronze after a 5535 win over South Africa. The tournament emphasized talent identification, with officials such as umpires Justin Barnes, Nathan Begley, and Tamara Buriani-Gennai appointed to oversee matches, highlighting World Netball's commitment to developing refereeing expertise alongside player pathways.
Previously, World Netball organized the World University Netball Championship from 2012 to 2018, an event for university-level players that fostered academic-athletic balance and international exposure; editions were held in Cape Town (2012), Nottingham (2016), and Kampala (2018), but it was discontinued thereafter and its objectives integrated into regional youth development programs to broaden accessibility.
Variant formats like Fast5 netball, a dynamic version with five players per side, shortened quarters, power plays, and multi-point shots, have been introduced in youth contexts to enhance engagement and skill development. Fast5 debuted at the Commonwealth Youth Games in 2023 in Trinidad and Tobago, contested by teams of athletes aged 1418 from across the Commonwealth, marking the first inclusion of netball in the event and providing a fast-paced introduction for young competitors.
To support grassroots growth, World Netball runs development tournaments including regional youth qualifiers for events like the Netball World Youth Cup, such as the 2024 Africa qualifiers in Tshwane, South Africa, and the Oceania qualifiers in Rarotonga, Cook Islands, which enable emerging nations like Zambia, Malaysia, and Papua New Guinea to qualify and build competitive experience. These initiatives, alongside challenges for novice associations, prioritize skill-building and inclusivity, ensuring pathways for underrepresented regions to participate in global youth netball.

Olympic aspirations

World Netball has pursued Olympic inclusion for netball since gaining recognition from the International Olympic Committee (IOC) as an international federation in 1995, which provided access to funding and resources but did not guarantee program inclusion. Despite this milestone, netball has not yet been added to the Olympic roster, with advocates citing its predominantly female participation and historical exclusion from major multi-sport events as barriers. A bid for demonstration status at the 2020 Tokyo Olympics failed primarily due to limited netball infrastructure and participation in the host nation Japan.
Following the Tokyo setback, World Netball shifted focus to the 2032 Brisbane Games, declaring its intention in August 2021 to collaborate with Netball Australia on a formal bid, leveraging Australia's strong netball heritage and the event's location. Progress accelerated in March 2025 when World Netball CEO Fiona Harold, alongside Netball Australia representatives, held a virtual meeting with Brisbane 2032 CEO Cindy Hook and Director of Sport Brendan Purcell to discuss inclusion strategies and alignment with Olympic priorities. By November 2025, the campaign had garnered over 80,000 petition signatures supporting netball's addition, with ongoing advocacy emphasizing the sport's potential to enhance the Games' appeal in a host country where it ranks among the top women's sports.
Netball's presence in other multi-sport events has bolstered its Olympic case, including participation in the World Games from 1985 to 1993, where it featured as a competitive sport across three editions before being discontinued. More consistently, netball has been a core event at the Commonwealth Games since its debut as a medal sport in 1998, with all major netball nations being Commonwealth members, providing a platform for high-level international competition and visibility.
World Netball's strategies for Olympic inclusion center on promoting gender equality, expanding global participation to over 20 million players across 85 member nations, and drawing parallels to sports like rugby sevens and softball that achieved inclusion through demonstrated growth and inclusivity. President Dame Liz Nicholl DBE has led 2025 advocacy efforts, highlighting netball's role in IOC gender parity goals and its potential to attract diverse audiences, as stated in public pledges and position papers.

Leadership

Presidents

The International Federation of Netball Associations (IFNA), later renamed World Netball, was founded in 1960 by representatives from Australia, England, New Zealand, South Africa, and the West Indies, with early leadership roles filled by figures from these founding nations, including Australian administrators who contributed to initial rule standardization and tournament organization. The first president was Margaret Cassidy from England, serving during the inaugural Netball World Championships in 1963.
Since the mid-1990s, World Netball presidents have been elected every four years at the biennial or quadrennial Congress by voting member federations, serving in both ceremonial capacitiessuch as representing the organization internationallyand strategic roles, including overseeing governance, development initiatives, and global advocacy for the sport.

Board

The Board of Directors of World Netball serves as the primary decision-making body between biennial Congress meetings, providing strategic oversight for the organization's operations, events, and development initiatives. It meets four times annually to address key policies and ensure alignment with global netball growth.
The board's composition emphasizes regional representation, expertise in finance and sports administration, and inclusion of athlete perspectives, reflecting post-2023 reforms aimed at enhancing gender balance and athlete involvement. It consists of a President, Vice-President, Independent Finance Director, Athlete Director, one Independent Director, and five regional directors representing Africa, Americas, Asia, Europe, and Oceania. As of November 2025, the Africa directorship is vacant following the resignation of Cecilia Molokwane on October 16, 2025.
