Strychnine poisoning

 Strychnine poisoning is a rare, acute toxic syndrome resulting from exposure to strychnine, a colorless, odorless alkaloid derived from the seeds of the Strychnos nux-vomica tree and historically employed as a stimulant and rodenticide, which exerts its lethal effects by competitively antagonizing postsynaptic glycine receptors in the spinal cord, thereby disinhibiting motor neurons and provoking violent, tetanic muscle contractions without loss of consciousness.
Symptoms manifest rapidly, typically 15 to 60 minutes post-exposure, commencing with anxiety, hyperreflexia, and facial muscle stiffness (risus sardonicus), escalating to generalized spasms triggered by sensory stimuli, culminating in opisthotonic posturing, respiratory compromise from diaphragmatic fatigue, and potential cardiovascular instability or rhabdomyolysis.
Primarily arising from intentional ingestion in suicidal acts, accidental rodenticide exposure, or adulteration of illicit drugs and herbal remedies, the condition demands immediate supportive therapy including benzodiazepine sedation, mechanical ventilation to safeguard airway patency, and correction of metabolic derangements, with survival contingent on early intervention to avert asphyxia, though severe instances may yield enduring neurologic sequelae. Regulatory curtailments since the mid-20th century have markedly diminished incidence, rendering fatalities exceptional in developed regions.

Pathophysiology

Mechanism of Toxicity

Strychnine exerts its toxic effects primarily as a competitive antagonist at postsynaptic glycine receptors (GlyRs) located in the spinal cord and brainstem. These receptors mediate inhibitory neurotransmission by allowing chloride ion influx upon glycine binding, hyperpolarizing neurons and suppressing excitatory signals. By binding to the orthosteric site with high affinity (Ki 0.0040.02 M), strychnine prevents glycine from activating the receptor, thereby blocking this inhibitory mechanism without altering receptor desensitization or internalization.
This antagonism leads to disinhibition of alpha motor neurons and interneurons, resulting in unchecked excitatory neurotransmission from upper motor pathways. Normally balanced by glycinergic inhibition, spinal reflex arcs become hyperexcitable, amplifying even minimal sensory stimuli into sustained, tetanic muscle contractions. The process manifests physiologically as rigid extensor spasms due to simultaneous activation of agonist and antagonist muscle groups, culminating in opisthotonos from axial muscle involvement and risus sardonicus from facial grimacing. Respiratory failure arises secondarily from diaphragmatic and intercostal muscle tetany, impairing ventilation without direct cardiotoxicity.
Strychnine is rapidly absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract and distributed to central nervous system tissues, crossing the blood-brain barrier efficiently due to its lipophilicity and low molecular weight (334 Da). Peak plasma concentrations occur within minutes to hours post-ingestion, with onset of toxic effects typically between 15 and 60 minutes, though delays up to 2 hours can occur with food co-ingestion or lower doses. The estimated minimal lethal oral dose in adults ranges from 30 to 120 mg (approximately 0.52 mg/kg body weight), based on case reports and toxicokinetic data, reflecting variability in individual metabolism via hepatic cytochrome P450 enzymes.

Etiology and Exposure

Sources of Strychnine

Strychnine is an indole alkaloid extracted from the seeds of the Strychnos nux-vomica tree, native to India and Southeast Asia. The seeds contain up to 1.5% strychnine by weight, along with related alkaloids like brucine.
Once employed in small doses as a stimulant in Western medicine until the early 20th century, strychnine's therapeutic uses have been abandoned worldwide due to its narrow therapeutic index and high toxicity. Today, it is registered solely as a restricted-use rodenticide in limited applications, such as underground baits for pocket gophers in the United States under Environmental Protection Agency oversight. Commercial production derives from natural extraction or synthesis, but availability is tightly controlled to prevent misuse in pest control products.
Human exposures leading to poisoning frequently involve unregulated or illegal formulations of strychnine-based rodenticides, often sourced from black-market pest control imports in regions with lax enforcement. Traditional herbal preparations incorporating Strychnos nux-vomica seeds or extracts, such as Southeast Asian supplements marketed for vitality or pain relief, have caused documented cases, with strychnine concentrations confirmed via serum and urine toxicology in affected individuals. Similarly, certain traditional Chinese medicines containing "maqianzi" (Strychni semen) have resulted in poisoning clusters, where the herb is used despite known risks.
In recent years, strychnine has emerged as an adulterant in illicit opioid supplies, particularly fentanyl street products analyzed between 2020 and 2025, likely added to bulk volume or mimic effects, though its inclusion defies typical cutting agent profiles due to enhanced lethality. Veterinary-grade strychnine formulations for animal pest control contribute to isolated human cases via accidental access or diversion, underscoring the predominance of ingestion from contaminated consumer or illicit goods over other origins.

Routes and Risk Factors

Strychnine primarily enters the body via oral ingestion, which accounts for the majority of poisoning cases due to its incorporation in rodenticides or adulterated substances. Secondary routes include inhalation of fine dust or aerosols, particularly during handling of powdered formulations, and dermal absorption from concentrated solutions or spills, though these are less common and typically require direct contact with mucous membranes or broken skin for significant uptake. Parenteral routes, such as intravenous injection, occur rarely but result in immediate systemic distribution when strychnine contaminates illicit drugs. Absorption across these pathways is rapid, with gastrointestinal uptake occurring within minutes, leading to peak plasma levels in under an hour and onset of effects shortly thereafter.
Key risk factors for strychnine poisoning encompass intentional exposure through suicidal or homicidal acts, where deliberate ingestion of pesticides or contaminated materials predominates in reported fatalities. Occupational hazards arise in agriculture and pest control settings, where workers handling strychnine-based rodenticides face elevated risks from accidental inhalation or dermal contact during bait preparation or application, though stringent regulations have reduced such incidents in regulated regions. Accidental nontarget ingestion poses dangers to children and animals mistaking baits for food, amplified by strychnine's bitter taste failing to deter curious explorers. An emerging vector involves drug adulteration, particularly in illicit opioids like fentanyl or heroin, where strychnine is added as a cutting agent, leading to poisoning via inhalation, injection, or nasal routes in substance users; case reports document severe outcomes from such contaminations as recently as 2025. 
The dose-response profile underscores vulnerability, with toxic effects manifesting from as low as 5-10 mg in adults, causing life-threatening spasms, while the probable lethal oral dose ranges from 1.5-2 mg/kg body weight (approximately 105-140 mg for a 70 kg individual), though variability in individual tolerance and route influences severity. Strychnine's first-order kinetics, with a half-life of 10-16 hours, contribute to prolonged risks even after minimal exposure.

Clinical Presentation

Symptoms in Humans

Symptoms of strychnine poisoning in humans typically manifest after a latent period of 15 to 60 minutes following oral ingestion, though this can extend to 10 to 120 minutes depending on dose and route of exposure. Initial signs include apprehension, restlessness, anxiety, and muscle twitching, often accompanied by hyperreflexia and stiffness in the neck and face.
These early symptoms rapidly progress to diffuse, involuntary muscle contractions and spasms, which are exacerbated by minimal external stimuli such as noise or touch, leading to violent, generalized convulsions characterized by opisthotonos (severe arching of the back). A hallmark feature is risus sardonicus, a grimacing expression resulting from sustained contraction of the facial muscles, while the patient remains fully conscious and alert, distinguishing strychnine toxicity from other convulsant poisonings like those involving barbiturates or benzodiazepines. Convulsions often involve cyanosis and apnea due to diaphragmatic and intercostal muscle rigidity, with episodes lasting seconds to minutes and recurring without intervening relaxation in severe cases.
Sustained muscle contractions can precipitate complications including rhabdomyolysis, hyperthermia, lactic acidosis, and metabolic disturbances from prolonged anaerobic metabolism and tissue breakdown. Death, when it occurs, is usually attributable to respiratory failure from asphyxia during apneic spasms or exhaustion of respiratory muscles, rather than direct cardiac or central nervous system depression.

Toxicity in Animals

Strychnine demonstrates acute toxicity in a wide range of animal species, with mammals and birds exhibiting particular sensitivity due to its rapid blockade of glycine receptors in the spinal cord, leading to uncontrolled convulsions. In dogs, the oral median lethal dose is approximately 0.75 mg/kg body weight, resulting in symptoms such as muscle rigidity, opisthotonus, and respiratory arrest within 10 to 120 minutes of ingestion. Cats show slightly higher tolerance, with an LD50 of about 2 mg/kg, though malicious or accidental exposures remain frequently fatal without intervention. Horses and other production animals experience similar tetanic spasms, often from nontarget bait access, underscoring the compound's broad mammalian lethality at low doses.
Birds display heightened vulnerability, with acute oral toxicity causing death in 1 to 5 hours via hyperthermia and exhaustive convulsions, amplifying ecological risks through secondary poisoning in raptors and scavengers that consume baited rodents or carcasses. In rodents, the primary targets, sublethal doses induce rapid-onset seizures that can foster bait-shy behavior, where survivors associate bait with illness and avoid subsequent exposures, though this does not mitigate direct fatalities or secondary transfer to predators. Wildlife nontarget incidents, including in predators, highlight cascading effects, as poisoned prey remains toxic to scavengers, perpetuating poisoning chains in ecosystems.
Veterinary case data from western Canada (20142023) illustrate these patterns, documenting 51 nontarget poisonings averaging 6 per year, predominantly in dogs (38 cases), horses (5), and wildlife (8), with most linked to illegal above-ground strychnine applications rather than approved burrow uses. Such incidents reveal species-specific variations: smaller mammals and birds succumb swiftly to minimal exposures, while larger species like horses may present with prolonged rigidity but variable outcomes based on dose and response time, emphasizing strychnine's role in unintended wildlife mortality over controlled pest management.

Diagnosis

Clinical Evaluation

Clinical evaluation of suspected strychnine poisoning begins with a detailed history focusing on potential exposure sources such as rodenticides, pesticides, or adulterated substances like herbal remedies (e.g., Maqianzi) or street drugs, alongside the timeline of symptom onset, typically 15 to 60 minutes post-ingestion. A history of intentional ingestion or accidental contact in agricultural or veterinary settings heightens suspicion, particularly when patients report initial apprehension, muscle twitching, or stiffness shortly after exposure.
Physical examination reveals hallmark signs of central disinhibition at the spinal level, including hyperreflexia, muscle fasciculations progressing to generalized spasms, and opisthotonic posturing with preserved consciousness, often described as "awake seizures" lacking post-ictal confusion. Spasms are readily triggered by minimal sensory stimuli such as noise, light, or touch, reflecting unopposed excitatory neurotransmission due to glycine receptor antagonism, while the absence of focal neurological deficits or analgesia requirements distinguishes the presentation. Patients remain lucid and in agony during episodes, with rapid escalation to respiratory compromise if unchecked.
Differentiation from mimics like tetanus relies on strychnine's acute onset without preceding wound infection, lack of persistent inter-spasm rigidity or prominent trismus, and sustained mental clarity absent in advanced tetanus; organophosphate poisoning, by contrast, features cholinergic excess (e.g., miosis, bradycardia, secretions) and early encephalopathy, not isolated hyperexcitable spasms. These bedside cues, grounded in the toxin's selective blockade of inhibitory glycinergic pathways sparing supraspinal function, guide presumptive diagnosis pending further confirmation.

Analytical Detection

Gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS), often preceded by solid-phase extraction, serves as the gold standard for confirmatory detection and quantification of strychnine in biological specimens such as blood, urine, and gastric contents. This technique provides high specificity and sensitivity, with limits of detection as low as 0.5 ng/mL in blood and limits of quantification around 5 ng/mL or 0.1 g/g in tissues. Presumptive screening methods like thin-layer chromatography on gastric aspirate or urine may suggest presence but require GC-MS confirmation to avoid false positives from structurally similar alkaloids.
Antemortem detection windows vary by specimen and dose; strychnine appears in urine within minutes of ingestion due to rapid absorption and partial renal excretion unchanged, but concentrations may fall below detectable limits in urine collected 12 days post-exposure. Blood levels peak early but decline quickly owing to distribution into tissues, rendering serum unreliable for late presentations without prompt sampling. Gastric contents remain the most reliable for recent ingestions, yielding quantifiable strychnine for hours post-ingestion. Toxicity correlates with blood concentrations exceeding 2 mg/L, which are associated with severe symptoms, while levels between 2 and 10 mg/L often prove lethal; symptomatic cases typically show 130 mg/L in blood or urine.
Postmortem analysis prioritizes liver and kidney tissues for reliability, as strychnine redistributes post-death and persists longer than in fluids; liver concentrations above 0.1 g/g confirm exposure, though low-dose cases challenge detection due to limits near 0.1 g/g. In formalin-fixed tissues, GC-MS still enables quantitation despite potential analyte loss. Recent forensic challenges arise in strychnine-adulterated illicit fentanyl, where trace amounts in biological samples from overdose cases necessitate ultrasensitive methods like GC-MS to distinguish intentional poisoning from unintentional low-level exposure. Such incidents, documented in 2023 across multiple U.S. sites, highlight specimen reliability issues in polydrug contexts, with strychnine detected alongside fentanyl in over 900 cases analyzed that year.

Treatment

Decontamination Procedures

In cases of acute strychnine ingestion, gastrointestinal decontamination aims to limit absorption of the rapidly absorbed toxin, which occurs primarily through the small intestine following ionization in gastric acid. Due to strychnine's quick onset of symptoms and potential for convulsions, procedures must prioritize airway protection to prevent aspiration.
Activated charcoal, administered at a dose of 1 g/kg orally or via nasogastric tube, is recommended if presentation occurs within 1-2 hours of ingestion and the airway is secured, as it adsorbs unabsorbed strychnine in the gut. Gastric lavage may be considered in early presentations (within 1 hour) under controlled conditions with endotracheal intubation to mitigate risks, supported by case reports demonstrating toxin removal when performed promptly. Multiple-dose activated charcoal has limited evidence for benefit given the toxin's rapid systemic distribution, with half-life estimates of 10-16 hours.
Emetics and ipecac are contraindicated due to the high risk of aspiration during strychnine-induced muscle spasms, which can precipitate respiratory compromise; public health guidance explicitly advises against inducing vomiting. Whole-bowel irrigation has negligible utility in most cases, as strychnine's pharmacokinetics favor swift absorption over sustained release, rendering it ineffective beyond initial decontamination efforts. Survival data from reported cases underscore better outcomes with early decontamination compared to delayed or absent interventions, though randomized trials are absent due to the rarity of exposures.

Symptomatic Control

Initial management of strychnine-induced spasms prioritizes high-dose intravenous benzodiazepines to enhance inhibitory neurotransmission via GABA_A receptor potentiation, countering the glycine receptor antagonism that causes neuronal hyperexcitability. Diazepam at doses of 0.5-1 mg/kg IV, repeated as needed, serves as first-line therapy due to its rapid onset and efficacy in suppressing tetanic contractions observed in clinical reports. This approach leverages dose-response data from poisoning cases, where escalating benzodiazepine administration correlates with spasm resolution before progression to refractory states, minimizing risks of rhabdomyolysis and acidosis from sustained muscle activity.
For refractory spasms unresponsive to benzodiazepines, barbiturates such as phenobarbital (15-20 mg/kg IV loading dose) provide deeper sedation by prolonging GABA-mediated chloride influx, or non-depolarizing neuromuscular blockers like vecuronium (0.1 mg/kg IV) are employed alongside mechanical ventilation to interrupt peripheral manifestations of central disinhibition. Depolarizing agents like succinylcholine must be avoided, as they risk exacerbating fasciculations and hyperkalemia in the context of strychnine's upregulation of nicotinic receptors, potentially precipitating cardiac arrest. Survival data from documented cases underscore the value of early paralysis and sedation, with patients achieving full recovery when instituted within hours of onset to prevent ventilatory failure.
Post-acute monitoring for spasm recurrence is essential, given strychnine's prolonged elimination half-life (10-16 hours) and potential for delayed re-emergence of hyperexcitability after initial control, necessitating continuous EEG or clinical observation in intensive care settings. This targeted symptomatic control directly addresses the causal mechanism of unchecked excitatory signaling, improving outcomes over historical conservative approaches by preempting secondary complications like hypoxia.

Supportive Care

Supportive care in strychnine poisoning focuses on stabilizing vital functions and addressing secondary complications arising from sustained muscle hyperactivity, as there is no specific antidote available. The toxin is primarily metabolized in the liver via microsomal enzymes and partially excreted unchanged by the kidneys, with a biological half-life of 10 to 16 hours, necessitating supportive measures to sustain the patient until natural elimination occurs. 
Airway protection is paramount due to the risk of respiratory compromise from diaphragmatic spasms and rigidity; endotracheal intubation and mechanical ventilation are indicated if protective reflexes are impaired or ventilation is inadequate. Acid-base imbalances, often resulting from lactic acidosis secondary to muscle contractions, require monitoring via arterial blood gases and correction with appropriate intravenous bicarbonate therapy if severe metabolic acidosis develops. 
Hyperthermia, induced by intense muscle activity, is managed through evaporative cooling techniques such as ice packs or cool mist, alongside environmental temperature control to prevent exacerbation of metabolic demands. Rhabdomyolysis, a potential sequela of prolonged spasms, is treated with aggressive intravenous fluid hydration to promote diuresis and prevent acute kidney injury, while monitoring creatine kinase levels and urine output. Succinylcholine should be avoided for paralysis during intubation due to risks of hyperkalemia in the context of rhabdomyolysis.
Patients require intensive care unit admission for close hemodynamic monitoring, typically for 24 to 48 hours, given the potential for recurrent spasms over this period corresponding to the toxin's half-life and clinical course. Empirical data indicate that survivors who navigate the acute phase beyond 24 hours generally recover fully without long-term neurological sequelae, provided complications are promptly addressed.

Prognosis and Outcomes

Factors Influencing Survival

The lethality of strychnine poisoning is primarily determined by the ingested dose, with a median lethal oral dose estimated at 1.5 mg/kg in humans, equivalent to approximately 100-140 mg for a 70 kg adult. Doses below this threshold may cause severe symptoms but permit survival with intervention, whereas exceeding itparticularly without rapid medical careresults in near-certain fatality due to unchecked glycine receptor antagonism leading to sustained convulsions and respiratory arrest. Case reports document survivals after ingestions up to 3750 mg when treatment commenced promptly, underscoring dose as a causal threshold modulated by physiological reserve and intervention speed.
Time from exposure to effective supportive measures critically influences outcomes, as delays beyond 30-60 minutes amplify risks of hypoxic brain injury from prolonged tetanic spasms compromising ventilation. Toxicology data indicate that early airway protection and sedation prevent the cascade of rhabdomyolysis, acidosis, and multi-organ failure, yielding survival in most cases where symptoms manifest within this window. Duration of respiratory compromise emerges as a key metric, with episodes exceeding 1-2 hours correlating to irreversible damage independent of initial dose.
Pre-existing comorbidities, such as cardiopulmonary disease, exacerbate mortality by reducing tolerance to hyperthermia and metabolic derangements from muscle hyperactivity, though empirical data remain limited to case series rather than large cohorts. In contrast to humans, animal outcomes show higher lethalitye.g., 60% mortality in canine casesattributable to delayed veterinary access and smaller body mass amplifying per-kg exposure effects. Human survival benefits from advanced care availability, with registries reporting near-complete recovery absent complications.
Survivors rarely experience long-term sequelae, with no evidence of chronic neurotoxicity or persistent glycine dysregulation; any residual effects stem from acute hypoxic insults rather than the toxin itself. Animal fatalities, conversely, preclude such assessments but highlight uniformly poor prognosis without analogous interventions.

In the United States, exposures have declined significantly, with the American Association of Poison Control Centers reporting only 66 human cases in 2014, including one fatality, reflecting sporadic occurrences rather than endemic patterns. This rarity stems from regulatory bans on strychnine as a pesticide and rodenticide in many developed nations since the mid-20th century, leading to a 63% drop in reported exposures over recent decades according to poison control surveillance data. However, isolated spikes persist, such as intentional suicides or unintentional exposure via adulterated substances, underscoring the poison's persistence in illicit channels.
In developing countries, incidence is comparatively higher due to ongoing agricultural use of strychnine-containing pesticides and limited access to alternatives, contributing to elevated rates of both accidental and suicidal ingestions. Pesticide-related suicides, including strychnine, account for a disproportionate share of poisoning deaths in low- and middle-income regions, though exact strychnine-specific figures are underreported amid broader toxicological challenges. Empirical trends show a post-regulatory decline in legal uses worldwide, yet illicit production and traditional medicine applications sustain cases, as evidenced by clusters in areas with weak enforcement. For instance, pediatric exposures predominate accidentally in such settings, often from household pesticide storage, contrasting with intentional adult cases elsewhere.

Accidental poisonings, conversely, skew toward children, often involving exploratory ingestion of baits or mislabeled products, accounting for roughly 85% of unintentional exposures in surveillance data. Historical Context

Origins and Early Applications

Strychnine, the principal alkaloid toxin from the seeds of the Strychnos nux-vomica tree native to Southeast Asia, entered European use centuries before its chemical isolation. Historical records document its application as a rodenticide as early as 1540, primarily to control rat infestations on merchant vessels and in urban settings across Europe, including Germany. The seeds, known as "poison nuts," were imported via trade routes and employed in baits due to their potent neurotoxic effects, which induce convulsions and rapid death in small mammals.
The pure compound was first isolated in 1818 by French chemists Pierre-Joseph Pelletier and Joseph Bienaim Caventou through extraction from S. nux-vomica seeds, enabling more precise dosing and study. This breakthrough prompted early 19th-century experiments positioning strychnine as a medicinal stimulant, with claims of benefits for cardiac, respiratory, and digestive functions, as well as tonics for fatigue and low vitality. Victorian-era physicians advocated minute doses to enhance muscle contractions and treat conditions like paralysis or neuralgia, often incorporating it into patent medicines such as nux vomica preparations.
However, empirical evidence of its narrow therapeutic indexwhere effective stimulant doses bordered lethal toxicityled to abandonment of widespread medicinal use by the late 19th century. Accidental poisonings from overdosed patent remedies, manifesting as violent convulsions and respiratory failure, underscored these risks, with documented cases highlighting strychnine's unreliability as a therapeutic agent. By the mid-20th century, applications consolidated toward pest control, leveraging its efficacy against rodents while minimizing human exposure through regulated formulations, though secondary risks from bait contamination persisted.

Regulatory Developments

In the United States, regulatory efforts began at the state level in the 1960s, with Michigan removing strychnine-treated peanut bait for mole control from the market in the late 1960s following numerous accidental pet poisonings. Federally, the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) imposed restrictions in the 1970s and 1980s, limiting strychnine to below-ground applications primarily for pocket gopher control by licensed applicators only, classifying it as a restricted-use pesticide to minimize nontarget exposures. The EPA's 1991 Reregistration Eligibility Decision further confined uses to buried baits, prohibiting above-ground applications and consumer products, which correlated with a decline in reported incidental human and domestic animal cases from unregulated baiting.
Internationally, strychnine faced escalating prohibitions, with the European Union banning its sale as a rodenticide in 2006 and recalling all supplies due to persistent risks of secondary poisoning in wildlife and pets. Canada announced the cancellation of all predacide uses in March 2024 after reevaluation found environmental risks unacceptable, aligning with broader trends in member states of conventions like the Rotterdam Convention, which flags highly hazardous pesticides for prior informed consent in trade. These measures aimed to curb illicit trade, yet data indicate only partial efficacy, as strychnine remains available in some agricultural contexts under strict protocols elsewhere.
Despite these restrictions, poisoning incidents persisted into the 2020s, underscoring enforcement challenges and gaps in global supply chain controls; for instance, a 2021 U.S. case involved illegal strychnine use to poison wolves on a Montana ranch, while Balkan wildlife studies documented ongoing vulture deaths from illicit baits through 2022. Such events, often tied to poaching or adulterated substances, reveal that while regulations reduced legal consumer exposuresevidenced by fewer EPA-reported domestic incidents post-1990snontarget wildlife and occasional human cases from black-market sources continue, arguing for enhanced international monitoring and stricter import bans to address residual risks.

Prevention and Mitigation

Regulatory Frameworks

In the United States, strychnine is classified as a restricted-use pesticide under the Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act (FIFRA), administered by the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), limiting its sale and application to certified applicators or those under their direct supervision for specific below-ground uses such as gopher control. Misuse, including application by uncertified individuals or for unapproved purposes, incurs penalties such as fines up to $50,000 per violation and potential criminal prosecution under FIFRA enforcement provisions. These restrictions have correlated with reduced accidental human exposures, though nontarget wildlife and domestic animal incidents persist, with diagnostic records indicating ongoing challenges in compliance monitoring.
In the European Union, strychnine-containing rodenticides have been prohibited since 2006 under Directive 98/8/EC, with broader import and use restrictions enforced via the REACH regulation, classifying it as a substance of very high concern due to its acute toxicity. United Nations frameworks, including the Rotterdam Convention on Prior Informed Consent for hazardous chemicals, further limit international trade by requiring notifications for exports to non-parties, aiming to prevent circumvention through developing markets. Compliance has effectively minimized legal uses, but illicit imports contribute to sporadic poisoning cases, underscoring enforcement gaps at borders.
Globally, regulations vary significantly: Australia maintains strict controls under state poisons acts, permitting strychnine only for targeted vertebrate pest management (e.g., mice or wild dogs) by licensed operators with mandatory training and baiting protocols, but prohibits broad-scale applications and unregistered products. In contrast, persistence in Asia and Africa stems from weaker enforcement and agricultural reliance, where inadequate pesticide laws allow illegal hunting and pest control uses, evidenced by widespread nontarget wildlife poisonings despite bans in 83% of African nations. Black-market circumvention is documented through unregulated trade routes, exacerbating poisoning rates in regions with limited tracking, as illegal supplies evade import controls.
Recent nontarget poisoning data, such as 51 confirmed cases in western Canada from 2014 to 2023 primarily affecting dogs and horses, have prompted calls for enhanced regulatory tracking, including mandatory reporting of bait deployments and post-application monitoring to quantify compliance impacts and reduce secondary exposures. Such measures could address persistent incidents despite restrictions, as evidenced by provincial cancellations of strychnine registrations due to environmental risks outweighing benefits.

Practical Safety Measures

Pesticides containing strychnine must be stored in locked cabinets within well-ventilated areas, separated from food, animal feed, and living spaces to prevent accidental access by children, pets, or nontarget wildlife. Original labeled containers should be used, maintained off the ground in cool, dry conditions between 40F and 100F to avoid degradation or spills that could lead to unintended exposure. Handlers require personal protective equipment including double-layered nitrile gloves, chemical-resistant goggles, protective clothing, and respiratory protection such as NIOSH-approved air-purifying respirators with N100 filters for dusty operations, with frequent glove inspections to mitigate skin or inhalation risks. 
For pest control, alternatives to strychnine baits include zinc phosphide formulations like ZP Rodent Oat Bait, which demonstrate comparable efficacy against ground squirrels and pocket gophers at lower costs, and anticoagulant options such as Rozol or Ramik, which pose reduced acute toxicity risks to nontarget species. Community-level deployment of tamper-resistant bait stations, spaced 10-50 feet apart depending on target rodents, confines access to intended pests while excluding children, pets, and wildlife, as evidenced by regulatory endorsements for their role in minimizing secondary poisonings. 
Public education emphasizes vigilance against strychnine contamination in herbal remedies, such as certain Chinese or Southeast Asian preparations derived from Strychnos nux-vomica, and adulterated street drugs like cocaine or heroin, where ingestion history warrants immediate suspicion of toxicity. Suspected exposures necessitate prompt reporting to poison control centers or emergency services for decontamination guidance, prioritizing airway management and avoidance of further ingestion to interrupt causal pathways to severe convulsions.
