 Skip Macdonald is an American film and television editor renowned for his work on the critically acclaimed AMC series Breaking Bad (20082013), its prequel spin-off Better Call Saul (20152022), and the feature film El Camino: A Breaking Bad Movie (2019). A member of the American Cinema Editors (ACE), Macdonald has earned multiple Primetime Emmy Awards for Outstanding Single-Camera Picture Editing for a Drama Series, including for the Breaking Bad series finale episode "Felina" in 2014 and the Better Call Saul finale "Saul Gone" in 2023. His editing style emphasizes precise pacing, tension-building, and collaboration with directors and showrunners like Vince Gilligan to maintain narrative tone and emotional depth.
Macdonald entered the industry in the late 1970s as a sound apprentice on the independent film The Great American Success Story (originally titled Ringer), starring Jeff Bridges and Bianca Jagger. He advanced through roles in sound editing and assistance on major productions such as Michael Cimino's Heaven's Gate (1980), where he handled cutting and Foley work, and later assisting on Nell (1994) and Primal Fear (1996). Transitioning to picture editing, he took over as editor on the television series Buffy the Vampire Slayer after the original editor departed, marking a pivotal shift in his career toward scripted television.
His involvement with the Breaking Bad universe began in its inaugural 2008 season, when he was recommended by a post-production producer and hired following an interview with series creator Vince Gilligan; he continued editing every season of both Breaking Bad and Better Call Saul, contributing to their distinctive montage sequences and time-jump structures. Beyond this franchise, Macdonald's credits include the pilot episode of Hell on Wheels (2011), episodes of Foundation (2021present), and other projects like Las Vegas. His career spans over four decades, highlighting a progression from sound work to Emmy-winning picture editing in high-profile drama series.

Early career

Assistant and department roles

Skip Macdonald attended university for one year before leaving to enter the film industry in the late 1970s, initially working in sound editing on projects such as the independent film The Great American Success Story (originally titled Ringer) and assisting on major productions like Heaven's Gate (1980), where he handled sound cutting and Foley work. He advanced through assistant sound editor roles in the 1980s on films including Things Are Tough All Over (1982), Dead Men Don't Wear Plaid (1982), The Man with Two Brains (1983), Cheech & Chong's The Corsican Brothers (1984), and Year of the Dragon (1985). Shifting to picture assistant editing roles in the late 1980s and 1990sa field that became his passionMacdonald built technical expertise in post-production workflows, including sound synchronization and nonlinear editing systems during the industry's transition from analog to digital tools.
In the early 1990s, he served as assistant film editor on projects like Harley Davidson and the Marlboro Man (1991), Perry Mason: The Case of the Killer Kiss (1993), and White Fang 2: Myth of the White Wolf (1994). Later in the mid-1990s, Macdonald worked in the editorial department on the psychological thriller Primal Fear (1996), as an assistant film editor. He also served as Lightworks assistant editor on the drama Nell (1994), directed by Michael Apted, gaining hands-on experience with pioneering nonlinear editing systems amid the shift toward digital platforms like Avid.
Macdonald's television contributions began in 1997 as an assistant editor on early episodes of Buffy the Vampire Slayer, including "Welcome to the Hellmouth," "School Hard," and later "Gingerbread" (1999), collaborating on the supernatural series' fast-paced storytelling and effects integration. Following the departure of the original editor, he transitioned to lead editor on 20 episodes of the series from 1997 to 1999, marking his breakthrough into picture editing for scripted television and honing skills in episodic post-production, from scene timing to character-driven narratives.
Through these late 1980s to late 1990s positions, Macdonald developed proficiency in editorial collaboration and technical aspects of post-production, laying the groundwork for advanced editing responsibilities in subsequent projects.

Breakthrough television projects

Macdonald's prominent lead editing roles on television built on his Buffy experience, with key assignments in the mid-2000s highlighting his expertise in dramatic and genre storytelling. He edited episodes of series such as The Chronicle (2001), Special Unit 2 (2001), Las Vegas (20052007), and Weeds ("Crush Girl Love Panic," 2006).
In 2007, Macdonald edited the MTV television movie Super Sweet 16: The Movie, a comedy-drama centered on teen rivalries and lavish parties, demonstrating his ability to handle fast-paced, character-driven narratives in a feature-length format for broadcast. That same year, he edited two episodes of the ABC prime-time soap opera Big Shots, including "Greatest AmeriMart Hero," contributing to the show's exploration of corporate intrigue and personal conflicts among high-powered executives. These assignments represented opportunities for independent creative input on episodic television.
A significant milestone came in 2011 when Macdonald edited the pilot episode of AMC's Hell on Wheels, a historical drama depicting the construction of the Transcontinental Railroad in the post-Civil War American West. Directed by David Von Ancken, the episode established the series' gritty tone through its blend of expansive action sequences and intimate character moments, with Macdonald's work helping to set the rhythmic foundation for the show's blend of Western genre conventions and historical realism. During this period, around 20112012, Macdonald became a member of the American Cinema Editors (ACE), an honorary society recognizing outstanding achievements in the field, which facilitated his deeper integration into Los Angeles' post-production networks.
The editing techniques refined on these projects, particularly in pacing complex genre elements like period authenticity and tension-building in Hell on Wheels, were instrumental in preparing Macdonald for subsequent high-profile series.

Breaking Bad universe

Breaking Bad

Skip Macdonald joined the production of Breaking Bad during its first season in 2008, brought on board after an interview with series creator Vince Gilligan.
His editing work gained early recognition with the season 3 premiere episode "No Ms," which earned a nomination for the Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Single-Camera Picture Editing for a Drama Series in 2010.
Macdonald's contributions emphasized a tense narrative style through innovative techniques, including montage sequences that accelerated the pacing of key processes, such as the chemistry lab builds for methamphetamine production, often synced with music to heighten urgency and transformation. He also crafted rhythm in action scenes by blending frenetic cuts with longer takes, creating suspense during high-stakes moments like confrontations and heists.
In handling emotional pacing, Macdonald favored single-shot coverage for dialogue to immerse viewers, while strategically cutting to non-speaking characters to reveal inner turmoil, particularly in the arcs of anti-hero Walter White and his partner Jesse Pinkman as their relationship evolved from mentorship to conflict.
Macdonald collaborated closely with creator Vince Gilligan and co-creator Peter Gould in an ego-free environment that fostered creative input across the production.
His detailed work intensified in seasons 4 and 5, where he shaped the escalating crime drama and character descents, culminating in the series finale "Felina," which won the Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Single-Camera Picture Editing in 2014. These efforts in the parent series influenced the editing approach in spin-offs like Better Call Saul.

Better Call Saul

Skip Macdonald began editing episodes of Better Call Saul from its first season in 2015, contributing to the prequel series' exploration of Saul Goodman's origins within the Breaking Bad universe. Over the course of all six seasons, he edited 32 episodes, helping to establish the spin-off's distinct narrative voice while connecting to the original series' timeline and characters. His work emphasized bridging moral ambiguities and character backstories, such as Jimmy McGill's transformation into Saul Goodman, through careful pacing that allowed legal and personal intrigues to unfold deliberately.
Macdonald's stylistic choices in Better Call Saul diverged from the more intense, hand-held cinematography of Breaking Bad, opting instead for locked-down camera setups, extended single takes, and wide shots to build subtle tension in con scenes and courtroom dramas. He incorporated black-and-white flashbacks to delve into character histories, enhancing the slower pacing suited to the series' focus on ethical dilemmas and interpersonal dynamics rather than high-stakes action. This approach highlighted non-verbal reactions and expressive performances, particularly from leads like Bob Odenkirk and Rhea Seehorn, to convey emotional depth without overt cuts.
Notable episodes under Macdonald's editing include season 3's "Expenses," which examined financial and relational strains, and season 4's "Something Stupid," featuring montages and time jumps that layered Jimmy's dual life as a lawyer and con artist.) In season 5's "Bagman," he managed a linear desert narrative between Saul and Mike Ehrmantraut, using long takes to heighten isolation and peril. His editing on the season 6 finale "Saul Gone" earned a 2023 Primetime Emmy nomination for Outstanding Single-Camera Picture Editing for a Drama Series, praised for its innovative time-jump structures that resolved the series' arcs while reflecting on past events.
Throughout the series, Macdonald adapted to the spin-off format by evolving the tone from an initially lighter, more colorful palette to a darker intensity mirroring Breaking Bad, all while preserving the vision of creators Vince Gilligan and Peter Gould. This progression allowed for comprehensive character development across multiple seasons, with his cuts supporting the deliberate buildup of tension in legal and criminal elements.

El Camino

Skip Macdonald served as the lead editor for the 2019 feature film El Camino: A Breaking Bad Movie, which extends the narrative arc of Jesse Pinkman immediately following the Breaking Bad series finale "Felina." In this role, he shaped the film's structure to provide emotional closure for Pinkman's character, focusing on his desperate escape and quest for freedom after years of trauma. Macdonald's editing bridged the television series to this cinematic epilogue by seamlessly incorporating elements that echoed the original show's tone and continuity, ensuring the film felt like a natural extension rather than a standalone project.
Macdonald employed distinctive techniques to enhance the film's pacing and tension, including the integration of flashbacks to delve into Jesse's past relationships and regrets, which were woven into the present-day action to heighten emotional depth. For chase sequences, such as the tense evasion at Todd's house involving fake DEA agents, he built rhythm by starting with slower, deliberate cuts to establish dread before accelerating the tempo to mirror Jesse's rising panic and urgency. Emotional closure was achieved through measured pacing in key dialogue scenes, like the interaction at the vacuum cleaner shop with Ed Galbraith, where longer takes allowed for subtle performances and cuts to silent observers to convey unspoken subtext and catharsis.
During production, Macdonald collaborated closely with director and writer Vince Gilligan, leveraging additional post-production time compared to the series to refine scene rhythms and shot selections for optimal impact. This partnership included the strategic use of archival Breaking Bad footage to maintain visual and narrative continuity across the universe, avoiding disruptions in the established aesthetic. These efforts ensured the film's editing preserved the intricate storytelling style of the original series.
El Camino premiered on Netflix on October 11, 2019, receiving critical acclaim for its editing, particularly in sustaining the suspenseful tension and character-driven intensity of Breaking Bad. Reviewers noted how Macdonald's work kept the high-stakes atmosphere intact, with seamless transitions that amplified the film's emotional and action elements without diluting the source material's legacy.

Later projects

Foundation

Skip Macdonald served as an editor for three episodes of the first season of the Apple TV+ series Foundation, which premiered in 2021 and adapts Isaac Asimov's foundational science fiction novels. These episodes included the pilot "The Emperor's Peace" (S01E01), "Upon Awakening" (S01E05), and "The First Crisis" (S01E09). The series' adaptation features a sprawling narrative spanning centuries, involving an ensemble cast portraying characters across multiple timelines, which required careful handling of nonlinear storytelling to maintain coherence and momentum.
The series received praise for its visual effects and editing, contributing to its reputation for stunning visual spectacle and immersive world-building. Critics highlighted the effective integration of effects that made the show's ambitious timelines feel fluid and engaging, with the pilot in particular lauded for setting a thrilling tone. This work marked an expansion of his television editing expertise into high-concept science fiction, distinct from his prior crime drama projects.

Alert: Missing Persons Unit and beyond

In 2024, Skip Macdonald edited two episodes of the Fox procedural drama Alert: Missing Persons Unit, a series starring Scott Caan and Dania Ramirez as Philadelphia police officers leading a specialized missing persons task force. His editing contributions emphasized tight pacing to heighten the urgency of investigations, balancing emotional family dynamics with high-stakes procedural action in each 42-minute episode. Macdonald also edited two episodes of the second season of Your Honor (2023). This work marked Macdonald's return to network television procedurals after the conclusion of the Breaking Bad universe.
Macdonald's editing extended to the 2025 Apple TV+ thriller series Pluribus, created by Vince Gilligan and starring Rhea Seehorn as a novelist navigating a transformed world. In this character-driven project, Macdonald focused on rhythmic cuts that amplified interpersonal tensions and thriller elements, collaborating with a returning team from Better Call Saul including writer/director Gordon Smith. The series premiered to critical acclaim for its blend of suspense and emotional depth, with Macdonald's cuts supporting Seehorn's lead performance.
As of November 2025, Macdonald maintains an active editing career based in Los Angeles, where he is a member of the American Cinema Editors (ACE). His ACE affiliation positions him for potential involvement in future high-profile television projects, continuing his emphasis on narrative-driven editing. Post-Breaking Bad universe, Macdonald's recent roles reflect an adaptation to hybrid streaming-network formats, integrating procedural structures with character-focused storytelling honed from earlier career experiences.

Filmography

Television editing credits

Skip Macdonald's television editing career began with contributions to Buffy the Vampire Slayer, where he edited 19 episodes from 1997 to 2000 across seasons 14, including "Wild at Heart" (season 4, episode 6), "Pangs" (season 4, episode 8), and "Choices" (season 4, episode 19), on The WB.
In 2011, he edited the pilot episode "Hell on Wheels" for the AMC series Hell on Wheels.
From 2008 to 2013, Macdonald edited 27 episodes of Breaking Bad across all five seasons on AMC, including the series finale "Felina" (season 5, episode 16).
He edited 29 episodes of Better Call Saul from 2015 to 2022 across all six seasons on AMC and Netflix, such as "Something Stupid" (season 4, episode 9).
In 2021, Macdonald edited selected episodes of season 1 of Foundation on Apple TV+, including the premiere "The Emperor's Peace".
For Alert: Missing Persons Unit (20232025) on Fox, he edited selected episodes across seasons 12, including "Bus 447" (season 2, episode 1, 2024).
In 2025, Macdonald edited two episodes of the Apple TV+ series Pluribus.
He also edited selected episodes of Las Vegas (20032008, NBC) and The Sopranos (2007, HBO).

Film editing credits

Skip Macdonald's feature film editing credits span a modest but notable range, beginning with an early contribution in the editorial department and culminating in a lead role on a high-profile sequel film that bridged his television expertise.
His first credited involvement in a theatrical feature came with Primal Fear (1996), where he worked as an assistant film editor under lead editor David Rosenbloom. Directed by Gregory Hoblit, this legal thriller starring Richard Gere and Edward Norton in a breakout performance relied on the editorial team's efforts to build suspense through tight pacing in courtroom and investigative sequences, contributing to the film's 129-minute runtime and its critical acclaim for narrative tension.
Macdonald's most prominent film editing credit is El Camino: A Breaking Bad Movie (2019), directed by Vince Gilligan, for which he served as the primary editor. This 122-minute Netflix feature continues the story of Jesse Pinkman (Aaron Paul) immediately after the Breaking Bad finale, with Macdonald leveraging his deep familiarity from editing over 40 episodes of the series to maintain stylistic continuity in pacing and tone. He emphasized the luxury of extended post-production time compared to television schedules, allowing for refined cuts that enhanced emotional depth without sacrificing momentumfor instance, in the tense Todd's house search scene, where overhead shots of Jesse's frantic exploration build to a rapid-cut reveal behind a mattress, heightening the stakes of his escape. Similarly, in the candy welder confrontation, Macdonald drew inspiration from classic Western standoffs like those in The Good, the Bad and the Ugly, using deliberate longer takes for dialogue to amplify gunslinger-like tension before a surprise weapon reveal. These choices helped pare down the assembly to preserve story integrity while delivering a cohesive cinematic experience that echoed the series' innovative editing style. This project represented a natural extension of Macdonald's television career into features, showcasing his ability to adapt episodic techniques to a standalone narrative.

Accolades

Primetime Emmy Awards

Skip Macdonald has earned eight Primetime Emmy Award nominations in the picture editing categories for drama series and limited series/movies, securing one win for his contributions to high-stakes narrative pacing in key episodes and films. These accolades, presented by the Television Academy, highlight his role in crafting tension and rhythm in finales and pivotal installments of acclaimed series.
His first nomination came in 2010 for Outstanding Single-Camera Picture Editing for a Drama Series for the Breaking Bad episode "No Ms," recognizing his assembly of the season premiere's layered emotional and suspenseful sequences. Macdonald received his breakthrough win in 2014 at the 66th Primetime Emmy Awards for the same category, editing the Breaking Bad series finale "Felina," where his precise cuts amplified the episode's climactic confrontations and resolutions, earning praise as a fitting capstone to the series' run.
He earned additional nominations in 2012 for Breaking Bad ("Face Off") and 2013 for Breaking Bad ("Dead Freight"). In 2017, Macdonald received two nominations for Outstanding Single-Camera Picture Editing for a Drama Series for Better Call Saul episodes "Mabel" (shared with Chris McCaleb) and "Witness" (shared with Kelley Dixon).
In 2020, he was nominated for Outstanding Single-Camera Picture Editing for a Limited Series or Movie for El Camino: A Breaking Bad Movie, lauded for maintaining the franchise's taut momentum in the feature-length sequel. His most recent nomination arrived in 2023 for Outstanding Single-Camera Picture Editing for a Drama Series on the Better Call Saul finale "Saul Gone," underscoring his skill in balancing introspection and high-tension closure in the prequel's concluding chapter.

American Cinema Editors Awards

Skip Macdonald is a member of the American Cinema Editors (ACE), an honorary society recognizing outstanding achievement in film and television editing, having earned the A.C.E. designation for his contributions to the craft. His involvement dates back to at least the early 2010s, with active participation in guild events, including presenting the Golden Eddie Filmmaker of the Year Award to Vince Gilligan in 2018 for work on Breaking Bad and Better Call Saul.
Macdonald's most notable ACE honors came from his editing on Breaking Bad, where he secured three consecutive Eddie Awards in the Best Edited One-Hour Series for Commercial Television category, a rare achievement highlighting his mastery of pacing and tension in dramatic storytelling. These wins validated his peer-recognized excellence in post-production, often overlapping with his Emmy-nominated episodes. No additional major ACE honors were reported through 2025.
