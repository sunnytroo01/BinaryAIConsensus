 A varnam is a structured musical composition in Carnatic music, serving as a foundational form that encapsulates the essence of a chosen raga through intricate melodic patterns, rhythmic complexity, and minimal lyrical content. It functions primarily as a pedagogical tool for training performers in raga delineation, voice control, and improvisation, while also acting as the opening piece in traditional concerts to warm up the artiste and establish the raga's characteristic phrases and ornaments. Composed typically in Telugu or Sanskrit, varnams emphasize technical precision and are rendered in multiple speeds to highlight gamakas (oscillations) and swara sequences.
The structure of a varnam is divided into two primary sections: the purvanga (first part) and the uttaranga (second part). The purvanga includes the pallavi (refrain), anupallavi (sub-refrain), and muktayi swara (concluding swara passage), focusing on the raga's core melodic framework with sahitya (lyrics) and solfa notations. The uttaranga comprises the charanam (stanza) followed by ettugada swaras or chitta swaras (any number of swara sets, often four to five), which allow for elaboration and demonstrate the performer's command over rhythm and melody. Varnams are set in common talas such as adi tala (8 beats) or ata tala (14 beats), and they exist in two main types: tana varnams, which prioritize musical notes and are suited for vocal concerts, and pada varnams, which integrate more sahitya for dance accompaniment. Examples include the famous viriboni varnam in Bhairavi raga by Pacchimiriyam Adiappayyar, showcasing vakra (zigzag) patterns and intricate phrasing.
Historically, varnams trace their roots to the 16th-century pedagogical exercises attributed to Purandara Dasa, the "father of Carnatic music," who developed graded compositions for systematic learning. The form evolved into its complex modern structure during the 18th and 19th centuries, with significant contributions from composers like the Tanjore Quartet (Chinnaiah, Sivanandam, Vativelu, and Sadasivam) and Veena Kuppayyar, who refined it for both music and Bharatanatyam dance. By the early 20th century, varnams were standardized as concert openers through the influence of performers like Ariyakudi Ramanuja Iyengar, solidifying their role in preserving raga grammar and fostering improvisational skills. Today, they remain essential for advanced training, with over 100 documented varnams in the repertoire, embodying the blend of composition (kalpita) and creativity (manodharma) central to Carnatic tradition.

Definition and Significance

Core Definition

A varnam is a fixed-composition musical form unique to Carnatic music, encapsulating the essential features of a raga, tala, and swara patterns within a structured sequence that includes pallavi, anupallavi, muktayi swara, charanam, and etugada swaras. This form serves as a comprehensive exposition of the raga's melodic contours, rhythm, and solfege syllables, presented in a balanced integration of musical notes and lyrical text.
The term "varnam" derives from the Sanskrit word "vara," meaning "color" or "description," symbolizing its role in vividly portraying and "coloring" the distinctive characteristics of a raga through varied swara combinations and rhythmic variations. As a pedagogical and performative piece, the varnam functions primarily as a vehicle for demonstrating technical proficiency in melody and rhythm, enabling performers to master voice modulation, precise swara rendition, and tala adherence across multiple speeds.
In distinction from other Carnatic forms such as the kriti, which emphasizes devotional lyrics and allows for extensive improvisation (manodharma), the varnam prioritizes swara elaboration and fixed patterns with minimal lyrical depth, making it a more rigorous exercise in technical execution rather than emotional expression. Similarly, unlike the geetam, a simpler introductory song focused on basic melody and rhythm without complex swara sequences, the varnam builds advanced skills through its elaborate structure and demands greater precision in raga depiction.

Role in Carnatic Music and Bharatanatyam

In Carnatic music concerts, the varnam serves as the traditional opening piece, establishing the foundational raga and tala for the performance while allowing the musician to warm up and demonstrate technical proficiency. This positioning enables the artist to explore the raga's melodic nuances through structured swara patterns and rhythmic variations, setting a deliberate pace and mood that engages the audience from the outset. By showcasing virtuosity in improvisation and precise execution, the varnam highlights the performer's command over complex elements, captivating listeners and building anticipation for subsequent compositions.
The varnam's intensity influences the overall concert structure, often leading into lighter, invocatory pieces such as a kriti in praise of Lord Ganesha, which provide contrast and ease the transition to more elaborate explorations like alapana or neraval. This sequencing balances the program's demands, preventing early fatigue while maintaining rhythmic and melodic continuity from the varnam's framework.
In Bharatanatyam, the varnam occupies a central position within the margam repertoire, functioning as the principal item that integrates nritta (pure dance), nritya (expressive dance), and natya (dramatic elements) to convey narrative depth. Through synchronized abhinayaencompassing facial expressions, gestures, and body movementsthe dancer interprets the sahitya (lyrics), evoking emotions like sringara (romantic love) or bhakti (devotion) to engage the audience in a vivid storytelling experience. This demands sustained stamina and improvisational skill, as the performer alternates between rhythmic footwork and interpretive sequences, making the varnam a benchmark for artistic competence.

History and Development

Origins in Carnatic Tradition

The varnam emerged in the late 17th century as a structured musical form within the Carnatic tradition of South Indian classical music, with the earliest known compositions attributed to the Karvetinagaram brothers, Govindasamayya and Kuvanasamayya, who were active between 1680 and 1710. These early varnams, referred to as chowka varnams in the treatise Sangita Sarvartha Sara Sangrahamu, represented an advancement in compositional complexity, integrating swaras (musical notes) and rhythmic patterns to showcase raga and tala proficiency. While not directly evolving from swarajatiswhich appeared later in the mid-18th century with composers like Merattur Virabhadrayyathe varnam built upon foundational elements of earlier vocal and dance-accompanying forms prevalent in the region during the Nayaka period (1530s1670s).
The form's development was deeply influenced by the devotional and temple music traditions of Tamil Nadu and Andhra Pradesh, where Carnatic music originated as a sacred practice tied to Hindu rituals and bhakti expressions in ancient South Indian temples. Compositions often invoked deities, reflecting the spiritual ethos of temple performances that emphasized rhythmic precision and melodic elaboration as offerings to the divine. This cultural milieu, rooted in texts like the Natyasastra and regional treatises, provided the devotional framework that shaped varnam's lyrical and interpretive depth, fostering its role in both musical and performative worship.
In the early 19th century, the Tanjore Quartetcomprising the brothers Chinnaiah, Sivanandam, Ponniah, and Vadiveluplayed a pivotal role in the systematization of varnams, codifying their structure for both music and dance while composing numerous examples in languages like Tamil, Telugu, and Sanskrit. Active primarily from 1824 to 1829 under royal patronage in Thanjavur and Travancore courts, they refined chauka and tana varnams, such as Ponniah's works dedicated to deities and rulers like Serfoji II, integrating them into formalized repertoires that enhanced technical training and artistic expression. Their efforts, informed by training under Muttuswami Dikshitar, helped establish varnams as a cornerstone of Carnatic pedagogy.
From its inception, the varnam served as a primary teaching tool within the guru-shishya parampara, the traditional teacher-disciple lineage of Carnatic music, enabling students to master raga bhava, tala intricacies, and vocal agility through repetitive practice of its swara and sahitya sections. This oral transmission method, emphasizing direct mentorship in temple and court settings, ensured the form's preservation and evolution as an foundational exercise before advancing to more improvisational compositions.

Evolution and Key Composers

In the 19th century, varnams underwent significant refinements, particularly in their technical complexity, with composers like Veena Kuppaiyer introducing intricate swara patterns that elevated the tana varnam's rhythmic and melodic demands. Kuppaiyer, active during the early to mid-1800s, composed numerous tana varnams that emphasized elaborate swara sequences, allowing performers to showcase virtuosity through precise execution of solfa syllables without sahitya interruptions, thus solidifying the form's role as a concert opener. These innovations built upon earlier foundations in the Carnatic tradition, adapting varnams for greater expressive depth while maintaining structural integrity.
Parallel developments in pada varnams focused on enhancing lyrical content, with Patnam Subramania Iyer (18451902) and Mysore Vasudevachar (18651961) making notable contributions by infusing sahitya with poetic nuance and emotional resonance. Iyer, a prolific composer of over 100 works including both tana and pada varnams, expanded the lyrical scope through devotional themes in Telugu and Sanskrit, creating pieces that balanced swara elaboration with meaningful narratives on divine grace. Similarly, Vasudevachar enriched pada varnams with melodic richness and lyrical beauty, composing around 200 songs that integrated chaste Telugu sahitya to deepen the interpretive layers for performers and audiences alike. Their efforts shifted emphasis toward pada varnams as vehicles for bhakti expression, influencing subsequent generations in lyrical craftsmanship.
By the 20th century, varnams adapted to modern concert formats, incorporating faster tempos and improvisational elements to suit extended performances and audience engagement. These changes, evident in urban music sabhas, allowed musicians to accelerate charana swaras up to 1.5 or double speed while adding kalpanaswaras for creative expansion, transforming the rigid structure into a dynamic platform for raga exploration. Such adaptations preserved varnams' pedagogical essence while aligning them with the evolving demands of professional recitals.
Regional variations further diversified varnams, with Andhra-style compositions dominated by Telugu sahitya reflecting the linguistic heritage of Telugu-speaking regions, as seen in works by composers like the Karvetinagaram brothers. In contrast, Tamil influences emerged prominently through the Tanjore Quartet's contributions in the early 19th century, introducing Tamil-texted varnams that emphasized narrative subtlety suited to dance contexts. This dichotomy highlighted varnams' adaptability to local cultural idioms, with Telugu forms prioritizing rhythmic precision and Tamil ones favoring expressive sahitya integration.

Types of Varnams

Tana Varnams

Tana varnams represent a swara-dominant category of varnams in Carnatic music, distinguished by limited sahitya primarily in the pallavi, anupallavi, and charanam, with swara sections (muktayi and ettugada swaras) rendered without lyrics, shifting the emphasis to intricate melodic patterns and rhythmic precision rather than textual elaboration in those parts. This structure makes them an ideal form for instrumental rendition on the veena or violin, where the focus on swara sequences allows performers to explore the raga's nuances without lyrical constraints in swaras, while also serving as a foundational exercise for vocalists to build technical proficiency. As compositions explicitly crafted to delineate the rga's subtletiesincluding ornamentation, characteristic phrases, and pitch movementsthey function as a practical gateway to rga mastery in performance and improvisation. The name "tana" reflects the form's resemblance to taanam, a rga elaboration using rhythmic syllables.
In terms of rhythmic framework, tana varnams are commonly set in talas such as Adi tala, comprising 8 beats divided as 4+2+2, or Ata tala with 14 beats structured as 5+5+2+2, providing a balanced canvas for swara development. For beginners, these varnams are frequently composed in approachable ragas like Mohanam, a pentatonic scale evoking simplicity and devotion, or Shankarabharanam, the melodic equivalent of the Western major scale, which facilitates early exposure to scalar movements and gamakas. This selection aids learners in grasping core rga features before progressing to more complex forms.
The structure creates a layered progression through pallavi, anupallavi, muktayi swara, charanam, and ettugada swaras, prioritizing technical execution over narrative content. Unlike pada varnams, which integrate sahitya for swara sections to enable expressive depth, tana varnams streamline the form to accentuate pure musicality.

Pada Varnams

Pada varnams represent a lyrical variant of the varnam form in Carnatic music, distinguished by the inclusion of sahitya (lyrics) across all structural components, including the charanam and swara sections, which facilitates detailed abhinaya (facial expressions and gestural interpretation) during Bharatanatyam performances. This comprehensive textual foundation enables dancers to convey nuanced emotions and narratives through synchronized movement and mime, making pada varnams a cornerstone of classical dance repertoire.
These compositions are commonly set in melodic frameworks like ragas Kalyani and Bhairavi, with Adi tala providing a steady rhythmic pulse that supports elaborate elaboration. Poetic themes often revolve around devotion to deities or romantic longing (sringara bhava), drawing from bhakti and courtly traditions to evoke profound sentiment.
The development of pada varnams for dance owes much to the Tanjore Quartetcomprising brothers Chinnayya, Ponnayya, Sivanandam, and Vadiveluin the early 19th century, who refined the form by integrating narrative elements akin to those in padams, such as extended dramatic pleas or devotional appeals. Performed at a slower tempo than tana varnams, this allows performers to delve into emotional depths, prioritizing expressive bhava over rapid rhythmic display. Unlike tana varnams, which emphasize swara proficiency with lyrics limited to main sections, pada varnams prioritize sahitya-driven storytelling for enhanced interpretive scope in dance.

Musical Structure

Primary Components

The primary components of a varnam in Carnatic music form a structured framework that delineates the raga and tala through a sequence of texted and swara-based sections, typically comprising the pallavi, anupallavi, muktayi swara, and charanam. These elements are rendered in a cyclic manner, with the pallavi often repeated after each major section to reinforce the composition's melodic and rhythmic identity. This architecture allows performers to explore the raga's phrases while adhering to the tala cycle, serving as both a pedagogical tool and a concert piece.
The pallavi functions as the opening refrain, introducing the raga and tala through a concise melodic line, usually spanning one or two avartas (cycles) of the tala and incorporating initial swara patterns set in the lower octave. It typically includes sahitya (lyrics) that encapsulate the thematic essence, such as devotion or description, and is repeated throughout the varnam to anchor the performance. In tana varnams, the pallavi's swaras emphasize rhythmic precision, often practiced at varying speeds to build technical proficiency.
Following the pallavi, the anupallavi serves as a melodic extension, presenting subtle variations in swara patterns that ascend to the upper octave and introduce additional raga characteristics, such as gamakas (ornamentations) and phrase modulations. This section, also texted with sahitya, typically mirrors the pallavi's length but expands the melodic range, providing a bridge to more elaborate explorations. It reinforces the raga's structure by highlighting key notes and oscillations, contributing to the overall cyclic reinforcement when the pallavi recurs.
The muktayi swara follows the anupallavi, serving as a swara-only passage that concludes the purvanga (first half) of the varnam. Typically spanning one avarta, it features melodic phrases adhering to the raga's motifs and resolves back to the pallavi, providing a transition without lyrical content and emphasizing technical control over swaras and rhythm.
The charanam constitutes the thematic core of the varnam, comprising one or more stanzas of sahitya that delve deeper into the lyrical content and raga elaboration, positioned in the uttaranga (second half) of the composition. In tana varnams, the sahitya is brief, and the charanam is followed by swara passages such as chitta swaras and culminating in ettugada swaras, focusing on intricate rhythmic patterns to delineate the raga's full scale. In contrast, pada varnams emphasize lyrical depth in the charanam, integrating expressive sahitya that aligns with dance interpretations, while still incorporating swaras for melodic variation. This section leads back to the pallavi to unify the varnam's raga identity.

Swara and Sahitya Integration

In varnams, swaras and sahitya are meticulously integrated to balance technical precision with expressive depth, ensuring that melodic sequences reinforce the lyrical themes without allowing deviation from the composed form. This fusion is evident in the ettugada swaras, which serve as concluding korvai sequences at the end of each major section, such as after the charanam or chittaswarams; these swara patterns progressively build rhythmic complexity through layered repetitions and accelerating tempos, culminating in intricate eduppus (starting points on the beat) that unify the raga's structure.
Particularly in pada varnams, synchronization between sahitya and swaras achieves rhythmic precision by aligning lyrical syllables directly with swara notations and talam beats, often rendering swara passages first in solfege (e.g., sa-ri-ga-ma) before repeating them with corresponding lyrics to highlight emotional nuances. This dual presentationswaras for nritta (abstract dance) and sahitya for abhinaya (expressive interpretation)ensures that each syllable lands precisely on matras (beat divisions), fostering a seamless flow that mirrors the devotee's plea or narrative in the text. For instance, the charana swaras are typically sung in groups, first purely as swaras and then with sahitya, matching phonetic stresses to melodic accents for heightened dramatic effect.
The muktayi swaras play a pivotal role in thematic transitions by bridging the purvanga (first half) and uttaranga (second half), providing a swara-only interlude after the anupallavi that resolves melodic phrases and prepares the shift to the charanam. These swaras maintain structural integrity by adhering strictly to the raga's characteristic motifs, avoiding any expansion that could disrupt the varnam's fixed architecture.
Unlike the ragam-tanam-pallavi, where performers engage in extensive neraval and kalpanaswaras for creative elaboration, varnams prohibit such improvisation to preserve their role as rigorous exercises in fidelity to the composer's intent, emphasizing exact replication of swara-sahitya patterns across multiple speeds. This restraint underscores the form's pedagogical value, where integration prioritizes discipline over spontaneity.

Performance and Practice

In Music Concerts

In Carnatic music concerts, varnams are typically performed as the opening piece, serving as a warm-up to demonstrate the artist's technical proficiency and establish the raga for subsequent compositions. The rendition begins with the purvanga section (pallavi, anupallavi, and muktayi swara) at a slow tempo, known as vilamba kala or first speed, which allows for clear enunciation of swaras and sahitya while emphasizing raga bhava through gamakas and subtle phrasing. This section is then repeated at a faster tempo, often double speed (madhyama kala), to showcase rhythmic precision and virtuosity, with the main artist leading swara sangatisvariations in note patternsthat the accompanists follow.
The uttaranga (charana and its swaras) follows directly at the faster tempo, integrating complex kanakku patternsmathematically structured swara sequencesthat highlight the performer's command over laya and tala without extensive improvisation. Vocal performances prioritize breath control and tonal purity, transitioning smoothly from deliberate clarity in the slow sections to agile execution in the fast ones, often concluding with a brief korvai to align with the tala cycle. Accompaniment includes the violin mirroring the main artist's melodic lines and the mridangam providing rhythmic support, with the latter entering after the initial pallavi to enhance the building intensity.
For instrumental adaptations, artists on the veena or violin adapt the vocal structure, employing string techniques such as meend (glides) and kampita (oscillations) to achieve swara precision and mimic vocal gamakas. The veena rendition, for instance, accentuates the tana passages with plucked strings for resonant depth, while the violin focuses on bow control for fluid transitions between speeds. These performances typically last 10-15 minutes, allowing the artist to warm up their range and engage the audience before proceeding to more elaborate ragam-tanam-pallavi or kritis.

In Dance Performances

In Bharatanatyam performances, pada varnams serve as a primary vehicle for padam-like storytelling, integrating narrative elements through a blend of nritta and nritya. The swara sections, consisting of melodic notes without lyrics, emphasize nritta, where dancers execute intricate pure dance sequences focused on rhythm and geometry, often accompanied by jatis to highlight technical prowess. In contrast, the sahitya portions feature explicit lyrics that enable nritya, allowing dancers to convey emotions and stories via abhinaya, including facial expressions and improvised sancharis that expand on the poetic content.
Choreography in varnam performances synchronizes mudrassymbolic hand gestureswith the lyrics to interpret and visualize the narrative, drawing from codified hastas outlined in ancient texts like the Abhinaya Darpana. These gestures often depict mythological themes, such as the devotee's longing for deities like Shiva or Krishna, portraying sringara (romantic love) or bhakti (devotion) rasas through metaphorical divine interactions. For instance, mudras may illustrate episodes from Hindu epics, where the dancer embodies the nayika (heroine) in a dramatic plea to her beloved god, enhancing the expressive depth of the performance.
Pada varnams composed by the Tanjore Quartet, including brothers Chinnayya, Ponnayya, Sivanandam, and Vadivelu in the early 19th century, hold a preferred place in Bharatanatyam repertoires due to their bhava-laden lyrics and architecturally balanced structure, which seamlessly support both musical and choreographic demands. These compositions, such as "Sami Niveyani" in raga Kalyani, exemplify emotional intensity and rhythmic complexity, making them staples for showcasing a dancer's interpretive skills.
Stage dynamics in varnam culminate in teermanam sequences, which feature concluding rhythmic patterns executed with vigorous footwork synchronized to the tala, often amplified by the sound of ghungroo (ankle bells). These sections demand precise korvaisinterlocking patterns of steps and syllablesto build intensity, transitioning from abstract nritta to a climactic resolution that tests the dancer's stamina and control.

Educational Importance

Training Melody and Rhythm

Varnams serve as essential exercises in Carnatic music education, enabling students to internalize the melodic structure of a raga through the systematic practice of ascending (arohana) and descending (avarohana) swara sequences. These patterns, often notated in sargam notation, allow learners to repeat characteristic raga phrases, mastering subtle pitch modulations and ornamentations (gamakas) that define the raga's essence, such as the six distinct variations of the tonic note (Sa) in Kalyani raga. By singing these swaras in structured sections like the purvanga and uttaranga, students develop a deep familiarity with raga permutations, as exemplified in beginner-friendly ragas like Mohanam.
In terms of rhythm, varnams provide rigorous training through complete tala cycles, typically in Adi tala (8 beats) or Ata tala (14 beats), where students learn to subdivide beats and execute korvai patternsrepetitive rhythmic motifs that conclude sections. These exercises, sung at increasing speeds (from vilamba to druta kala), cultivate precision in aligning swaras with tala beats, enhancing control over rhythmic complexity without sahitya in tana varnams. Such practice reinforces the integration of melody and rhythm, preparing students for improvisational elements like manodharma.
The pedagogical progression in varnam training begins with simpler compositions in elementary ragas, such as those in Mayamalavagowla scale, focusing on basic arohana/avarohana and single-speed rendering to build foundational accuracy. As proficiency grows, students advance to more intricate varnams in complex ragas, incorporating advanced gamakas and extended korvais, which hint at improvisation techniques like neraval and kalpanaswaras. This stepwise approach ensures gradual mastery, transitioning from rote swara exercises to fluid raga delineation.
For vocalists, varnams offer significant benefits in ear training by sharpening pitch recognition through aural repetition of raga phrases and gamaka variations, fostering intuitive raga identification. Additionally, the demands of modulating voice across swara clusters and speeds improve vocal control, enabling smoother execution of ornaments like kampita (oscillation) and jaru (glides), which are crucial for expressive singing. Specific teaching approaches, such as speed variations, further support these outcomes.

Pedagogical Techniques

In Carnatic music pedagogy, varnams are introduced after foundational exercises such as sarali and janta varisais, with teaching proceeding in a structured, incremental manner to build technical proficiency. The process typically begins with the pallavi swaras, allowing students to internalize the raga's melodic outline and rhythmic framework without the added complexity of lyrics. Once mastery of these swaras is achieved at varying speeds (vilamba, madhyama, and druta), sahitya is incorporated to align textual meaning with the melody, enhancing expressive delivery. Finally, the ettugada swaras (charana swaras) are taught, integrating the full composition while emphasizing returns to the pallavi for cohesion. This phased approach ensures gradual assimilation of the varnam's architecture, as outlined in standard instructional methods.
Notations play a crucial role in self-directed practice, with systems employing swara symbols (e.g., S, R, G for shadja, rishabha, gandhara) alongside tala indicators to document the composition's structure. These notations, often provided in lesson materials, facilitate repetition and error correction outside class sessions. Complementing this, audio recordings of guru demonstrations or model renditions enable students to mimic phrasing and tempo independently, reinforcing muscle memory for swara sequences. Such tools are particularly emphasized in beginner curricula to bridge oral tradition with accessible documentation.
Guru-shishya interaction remains central, with instructors demonstrating raga-specific gamakassubtle oscillations like kampita (shaking) or jaru (slides)and guiding the application of sangatis, which are melodic variations that elaborate on the base structure without altering the core swaras. This aural transmission, tailored to the varnam's raga (e.g., emphasizing jeeva swaras or key notes), cultivates nuanced interpretation and prevents generic renditions. Students receive personalized feedback during weekly sessions to refine these elements.
Assessment occurs through solo renditions, where students perform the varnam in full, demonstrating accuracy in swara alignment, gamaka execution, and rhythmic precision. Progression is evaluated from tana varnams, which prioritize swara complexity, to pada varnams, incorporating deeper sahitya expression; successful mastery signals readiness for advanced repertoire like kritis. This performance-based evaluation tracks overall development in melody and rhythm skills.

Notable Examples

Famous Tana Varnams

Tana varnams, emphasizing swara sequences over sahitya, include several enduring examples celebrated for their melodic clarity, rhythmic intricacy, and pedagogical value in Carnatic music tradition. One prominent composition is "Smi ninn koriyunnnu" in Sankarabharanam raga and Adi tala, composed by Veena Kuppaiyer.
Another widely acclaimed tana varnam is "vari bdhana" in Abhogi raga and Adi tala by Patnam Subramania Iyer.
The varnam "Ninnu kri" in Mohanam raga and Adi tala, attributed to Ramnad Srinivasa Iyengar.
These tana varnams owe much of their enduring fame to their frequent rendition in concerts and recordings by legendary artists such as M.S. Subbulakshmi, whose interpretations, including her acclaimed performance of "vari bdhana," have popularized them across generations and reinforced their role in both vocal and instrumental repertoires.

Famous Pada Varnams

One of the most celebrated pada varnams is "Vanajakshi" in Kalyani raga and Adi tala, composed by Ramnad Srinivasa Iyengar. This piece portrays Lord Krishna as the enchanting deer-eyed lover (vanajakshi), with sahitya rich in romantic imagery that evokes longing and devotion, making it ideal for expressive abhinaya in dance performances. The lyrics, such as "Vanajakshi ninne kori yunnadira," highlight themes of surrender to divine beauty, allowing dancers to explore subtle emotional nuances through facial expressions and gestures. Its melodic structure, with intricate swara patterns integrated into the sahitya, underscores the pada varnam's role in blending technical precision with narrative depth.
Another prominent example is "Mohamana" in Bhairavi raga and Rupaka tala, attributed to Ponnayya Pillai of the Tanjore Quartet. Known for its devotional lyrics addressing Lord Shiva as the enchanting one who captivates the heart ("Mohamana en meedil ni inda velaiyil"), this varnam features poignant sahitya that conveys bhakti and plea for grace, resonating deeply in interpretive dance sequences. The composition's rhythmic flow and emotional intensity have made it a staple for portraying sringara and karuna rasas, with the charanam elaborating on divine mercy.
"Sarasija Nabha" in Kambhoji raga and Kanda Ata tala, composed by Swati Tirunal, stands out for its elaborate charanam that expands on themes of obeisance and refuge at the feet of Vishnu ("Sarasija nabha ninnu sannuti jesenu ra"). The sahitya invokes compassion from the lotus-naveled lord, providing ample scope for rhythmic elaboration and expressive storytelling. This varnam's expansive structure, including detailed swara-sahitya interweaving, highlights the composer's mastery in creating pieces that balance melody, rhythm, and lyrical devotion.
These pada varnams hold significant cultural impact, forming the core of Bharatanatyam repertoires where they enable dancers to demonstrate nritta, nritya, and natya through their integrated swaras and sahitya. Frequently performed in margam sequences, they preserve Tanjore court traditions while inspiring modern fusions, such as contemporary interpretations blending classical elements with global rhythms to reach wider audiences. Their enduring popularity underscores the evolution of Carnatic music's narrative forms in sustaining South Indian performing arts heritage.
