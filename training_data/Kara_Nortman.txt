Kara Nortman
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Kara Nortman is an American venture capitalist and sports investor, serving as founder and managing partner of Monarch Collective, the first investment platform dedicated exclusively to women's sports. She is also co-founder of Angel City Football Club, a National Women's Soccer League (NWSL) team based in Los Angeles that rapidly achieved the status of the world's most valuable women's sports franchise through innovative financing and celebrity-backed ownership.
Nortman holds an AB in politics from Princeton University (class of 1997) and an MBA from Stanford University. Her early career included roles at Morgan Stanley, Microsoft, and Battery Ventures, followed by nearly seven years at IAC, where she co-headed the M&A group, served as SVP and general manager of Urbanspoon and Citysearch, and sat on the board of Hatch Labs, which incubated the dating app Tinder. She co-founded the children's e-commerce site Moonfrye before joining Upfront Ventures in 2014 as a partner, rising to managing partner over eight years focused on early-stage tech investments in companies such as Parachute Home, The Wing, and Qordoba.
A founding member of All Raise, a nonprofit advocating for gender diversity in venture capital, Nortman has advised the Women's National Soccer Team Players Association on pay equity issues. Her sports ventures emphasize profitability alongside purpose, but Angel City FC has encountered operational challenges, including reported board infighting, financial losses exceeding $50 million, and efforts to sell a controlling stake amid leadership tensions.

Early Life and Education

Family Background and Upbringing

Kara Nortman is the daughter of Donald Nortman, a nephrologist with a medical practice in Los Angeles, and Doreen Nortman, who worked as the office manager for her husband's practice. The family resided in Encino, California, a suburban neighborhood in the San Fernando Valley region of Los Angeles.
Nortman was raised in Encino, where her parents maintained their home during her childhood. This professional family environment, centered around her father's medical career, provided the backdrop for her early years in a stable, upper-middle-class setting typical of the area's demographics.

Academic Achievements

Kara Nortman received a Bachelor of Arts degree in politics from Princeton University in 1997. She later earned a Master of Business Administration from Stanford Graduate School of Business, completing the program from 2002 to 2004. No public records indicate additional academic honors, such as departmental prizes or distinctions, beyond these degrees.

Professional Career

Early Technology Roles

Nortman began her professional career after graduating from Princeton University in 1997 with a degree in politics. Her initial role was at Morgan Stanley, where she worked in private equity, becoming one of the few women in that group at the firm.
She subsequently interned at Microsoft in Redmond, Washington, gaining early exposure to software and technology operations more than two decades prior to 2021.
In 1999, Nortman joined Battery Ventures as an associate, engaging in venture capital focused on technology investments.
Prior to her involvement in venture capital firms like Upfront Ventures, Nortman spent nearly seven years at InterActiveCorp (IAC), starting in mergers and acquisitions where she co-headed the group. She later advanced to senior vice president and general manager of Citysearch, overseeing profit and loss responsibilities, and managed investments in mobile technologies including Match.com and Urbanspoon, while serving on the board for IAC's mobile portfolio companies.

Venture Capital Investments

In 2014, Kara Nortman joined Upfront Ventures, a Los Angeles-based early-stage venture capital firm, initially as a partner focused on enterprise software and cybersecurity investments. She advanced to managing partner and, in October 2020, was promoted to co-managing partner, overseeing the firm's investment strategy during a period of expansion in Southern California's tech ecosystem. Nortman's portfolio emphasized scalable SaaS platforms, data security solutions, and select consumer brands, reflecting her prior experience in mergers and acquisitions at IAC.
Key investments led by Nortman included Endgame, an endpoint security platform acquired by Elastic N.V. in October 2019 for $234 million, enhancing Elastic's capabilities in threat detection and response. Fleetsmith, a mobile device management tool for Apple ecosystems, was another success, acquired by Apple Inc. on June 24, 2020, to bolster enterprise deployment and security features. In cybersecurity, she backed Open Raven for data loss prevention and Britive for cloud identity governance, aligning with her advocacy for zero-trust architectures amid rising supply chain vulnerabilities. 
Nortman also pursued consumer-focused deals, such as Parachute Home, a direct-to-consumer bedding and home goods brand, and Time by Ping, a team scheduling software. In 2018, she participated in The Wing's $75 million Series C round for its women-only co-working spaces, joining the board alongside Sequoia Capital's Jess Lee; however, The Wing ceased operations in 2020 following financial challenges and internal controversies. Additional board roles included Writer, an AI-driven content platform, and Laurel, a women's health services provider.
During her tenure, Upfront Ventures under Nortman's leadership committed to seed and Series A rounds typically ranging from $1-5 million, prioritizing Los Angeles-based founders while drawing on her network for exits and follow-on funding. She departed Upfront in 2022 after eight years to shift toward sports investments, leaving a legacy of nine disclosed exits and ongoing portfolio growth in enterprise tech.

Transition to Sports-Focused Investing

In 2015, while attending a FIFA Women's World Cup match in Vancouver, Nortman searched nine stores for jerseys for her daughters but found none available, highlighting the underinvestment in women's sports infrastructure and merchandise. This experience sparked her interest in the sector, leading her to co-found Angel City FC, a National Women's Soccer League expansion team launched in 2022.
Having joined Upfront Ventures as a partner in 2014 and risen to co-managing partner by 2020, Nortman shifted her professional emphasis toward sports by July 2022, when she transitioned to a board partner role at the firm to prioritize her commitments with Angel City FC. This move marked her departure from active technology venture capital investing, allowing her to apply VC principlessuch as scalable branding, fan engagement, and revenue diversificationto emerging sports opportunities.
The pivot culminated in March 2023 with the launch of Monarch Collective, a $100 million investment fund co-founded with Jasmine Robinson, dedicated exclusively to women's sports teams, leagues, and related ventures. As managing partner, Nortman positioned the platform to capitalize on undervalued assets in the space, drawing on her prior successes in tech startups to target high-growth potential amid rising media rights and sponsorship deals. By 2025, the fund had expanded its commitments, including stakes in multiple NWSL franchises, reflecting Nortman's full reorientation toward sports as an asset class.

Sports Ownership and Investments

Involvement in Men's Professional Sports

Kara Nortman's sports investments have centered exclusively on women's professional leagues and teams, with no documented ownership stakes, equity positions, or operational roles in men's professional sports franchises as of October 2025. Her founding of Monarch Collective in 2023, which raised approximately $200 million by 2025 for targeted investments, explicitly prioritizes women's sports equity, including minority stakes in National Women's Soccer League (NWSL) clubs such as Angel City FC, San Diego Wave FC, and BOS Nation FC (formerly Boston Legacy FC).
This focus stems from Nortman's assessment of women's sports as an undervalued asset class, described by her as offering "arbitrage on sexism" due to lower entry valuations relative to growth potential, in contrast to the mature, high-barrier markets of men's leagues like the NBA, MLB, NFL, or MLS. While she has commented on multi-club ownership models linking men's and women's teamsadvocating for women's independence regardless of shared structuresNortman has pursued no such cross-gender investments herself. Her prior venture capital experience at Upfront Ventures involved tech startups, not men's sports entities.

Founding and Role in Angel City FC

Angel City Football Club (ACFC) was founded in 2020 as an expansion franchise for the National Women's Soccer League (NWSL), with the league awarding rights to a Los Angeles-based team on July 21, 2020. The club officially joined the NWSL on October 21, 2020, and began play in the 2022 season at BMO Stadium.
Kara Nortman, drawing on her venture capital background, co-founded ACFC alongside entrepreneur Julie Uhrman and actress Natalie Portman, establishing a novel ownership structure that prioritized diverse investors including celebrities, athletes, and corporations. This approach enabled the club to raise over $123 million in equity funding across multiple rounds before its debut, setting a record for women's sports franchises and valuing the team above $100 million pre-launch. 
As co-founder and original owner, Nortman played a key role in securing early investments, including from Alexis Ohanian's firm Seven Seven Six, and in pioneering sponsorship deals that exceeded $60 million, while emphasizing community impact through initiatives like $2.2 million in local contributions. Her efforts helped position ACFC as the world's most valuable women's soccer team by 2024, though controlling ownership later transitioned to Willow Bay and Bob Iger. 

Establishment of Monarch Collective

In March 2023, Kara Nortman and Jasmine Robinson established Monarch Collective as a growth equity investment firm dedicated exclusively to women's professional sports. The firm was launched with an initial target of raising $100 million to deploy in women's sports teams, leagues, and related revenue-generating assets, including media rights, licensing, and gaming. Positioned as the first private equity fund specifically targeting women's sports, Monarch Collective aimed to provide both capital and strategic advisory services to accelerate growth in the sector.
Nortman, leveraging her experience as co-founder and president of Angel City FC, partnered with Robinson, a Miami-based venture capitalist, based on their mutual belief that targeted investments in women's sports could influence broader cultural and societal shifts. The announcement was made publicly on March 27, 2023, via a Medium post authored by Nortman, coinciding with coverage in outlets such as Sportico and the Los Angeles Times. Legal structuring for the fund was handled by the law firm Sidley Austin, which advised on its formation as a U.S.-based vehicle.
Monarch Collective's establishment reflected a strategic pivot from Nortman's prior venture capital work at Upfront Ventures toward sports-specific opportunities, emphasizing equity ownership in undervalued women's leagues and franchises to capture emerging market potential. By mid-2023, the fund had begun securing commitments, later expanding beyond the initial $100 million target, though its foundational focus remained on long-term value creation in women's sports infrastructure.

Advocacy and Public Positions

Participation in Time's Up and Related Initiatives

Kara Nortman served as a board member of Time's Up, the organization launched in January 2018 by over 300 women in the entertainment industry to address systemic sexual harassment and promote workplace equality following high-profile allegations against figures like Harvey Weinstein.
In this capacity, Nortman participated in Time's Up initiatives extending beyond Hollywood, including cross-industry collaborations to advance gender equity. In April 2018, she publicly endorsed partnerships with Time's Up, highlighting the importance of cooperation among women across sectors to amplify impact. Her involvement connected technology and venture capital perspectives to the group's advocacy, aligning with Time's Up's expansion into legal and policy support for workplace reforms.
A key aspect of Nortman's participation involved supporting the U.S. Women's National Soccer Team (USWNT) in their March 2019 lawsuit against the U.S. Soccer Federation, which alleged gender discrimination in pay and working conditions despite the team's superior performance, including World Cup victories. Through Time's Up, Nortman collaborated with actress Natalie Portmanalso active in the organizationon efforts to back the USWNT Players Association, including hosting pay equity summits and providing resources for the litigation that settled in February 2022 with improved compensation structures.
This advocacy intersected with Nortman's later sports investments; discussions with Portman during Time's Up activities in late 2018 contributed to the ideation of Angel City FC, an NWSL expansion team announced in July 2020, emphasizing equitable investment in women's athletics as an extension of broader equality efforts.

Political Donations and Social Causes

Nortman has made political contributions exclusively to Democratic candidates and causes, as documented in federal campaign finance records. On October 22, 2024, she donated $3,300 to Kamala Harris's presidential campaign. Earlier, on January 2, 2020, Nortman contributed $250 to Fair Fight PAC, a political action committee founded by Stacey Abrams to promote voting rights and combat voter suppression, predominantly supporting Democratic efforts. Additionally, on November 12, 2020, she gave $500 to the Georgia Senate Victory Fund, a Democratic committee aimed at electing Democratic senators in Georgia during the state's pivotal runoff elections.
In 2016, Nortman was listed among top contributors from Beverly Hills to Democratic presidential campaigns, though specific amounts and recipients were not detailed in aggregated reports. These donations align with patterns among some Los Angeles-based venture capitalists favoring Democratic priorities such as electoral integrity and progressive policy agendas, contrasting with broader trends among sports team owners where Republican contributions predominate.
Beyond direct political giving, Nortman has supported social causes centered on gender equity in business and entrepreneurship. She co-founded All Raise in 2017, a nonprofit organization dedicated to increasing funding and leadership opportunities for women and nonbinary founders and investors in venture capital, addressing documented disparities where female-led startups receive less than 3% of VC dollars. This initiative emphasizes data-driven advocacy for merit-based inclusion, drawing on empirical evidence of underrepresentation rather than quotas. Her efforts in this area complement broader philanthropic focuses on empowering women through economic independence, though they remain distinct from her sports-related investments.

Controversies and Criticisms

Internal Conflicts at Angel City FC

Internal conflicts at Angel City FC emerged prominently among its founding investors and leadership, centering on disputes over operational management, personnel decisions, and executive temperament. Co-founders Kara Nortman and Julie Uhrman, the club's president, clashed with principal owner Alexis Ohanian, who criticized Uhrman's excessive spending to enhance competitiveness and her handling of a performance review, during which she reportedly cursed at him. Nortman consistently supported Uhrman's retention in her role, positioning herself against Ohanian's push for new leadership amid rising expenses and cash flow concerns despite attendance growth.
Tensions escalated in June 2024 when a law firm representing the board sent a letter to Nortman and Uhrman, accusing Nortman of violating a non-disclosure agreement by communicating with potential bidders during the ownership transition process, and alleging Uhrman breached fiduciary duties. Additional grievances included long-standing complaints from team officials about Uhrman's financial oversight and personnel choices, such as hiring her twin sister as chief marketing officer without board consultation and retaining her on payroll after dismissal.
Personnel disputes extended to the sporting director role, where Eniola Aluko, who joined in 2021, accused Uhrman of racial microaggressions and berating her during a 2022 meeting in London; Aluko departed in 2023 following her suggestion of an "amicable exit" for Uhrman, which was perceived as retaliatory. These issues, dating back several years per internal documents, contributed to a power vacuum after Ohanian ceded day-to-day control to a six-member board, ultimately prompting the sale of a controlling interest. In July 2024, Willow Bay and Bob Iger agreed to acquire majority ownership for $50 million, valuing the club at $250 million, with Ohanian planning continued involvement post-transaction.

Scrutiny of Advocacy Efforts

Nortman's involvement with Time's Up, where she served as a board member, drew scrutiny amid the organization's broader controversies, including allegations of mission drift and inadequate support for victims of workplace harassment. Launched in 2018 to combat sexual harassment following the #MeToo movement, Time's Up raised over $24 million but faced criticism for allocating a disproportionate share to executive salaries and overhead rather than direct aid, with reports indicating less than 1% of funds reached legal defense funds for accusers in its early years. The group dissolved operations in January 2023 after scandals, such as advising New York Governor Andrew Cuomo's team on responding to harassment allegations against him, which led to the resignation of key leaders and accusations of elitism and political partisanship that undermined its credibility. Critics argued that Time's Up prioritized high-profile advocacy and celebrity-driven initiatives over substantive, evidence-based reforms, reflecting a pattern where elite networks focused on symbolic gestures rather than measurable outcomes in addressing causal factors of workplace abuse.
Her advocacy for women's sports, channeled through Angel City FC's founding in 2020 and the launch of Monarch Collective in 2023a $250 million fund dedicated to women's sports investmentshas similarly faced questions about prioritizing branding and social messaging over competitive results. Despite raising over $150 million in equity for Angel City and attracting celebrity investors, the team has struggled on the field, posting losing records in its first three NWSL seasons (20222024) and finishing near the bottom of the league standings, prompting critiques that the emphasis on empowerment narratives and off-field activism overshadowed talent development and operational rigor. Observers noted that while attendance and media buzz surgedaveraging over 11,000 fans per home game in 2022the club's focus on "revolutionizing" women's sports through VC-style disruption yielded financial losses exceeding $30 million annually and internal discord, raising doubts about the sustainability of advocacy-led models that de-emphasize traditional metrics of sporting success.
These efforts have also intersected with Nortman's political donations, totaling over $100,000 to Democratic candidates and causes since 2018, including support for figures aligned with progressive social policies, though specific criticisms of her contributions remain sparse and tied to broader debates on VC influence in cultural institutions. Detractors in sports investment circles have questioned whether such alignments contribute to a perception of women's sports advocacy as ideologically driven, potentially alienating broader audiences and investors seeking apolitical, performance-based returns, though empirical data on fan engagement shows mixed results with high initial turnout tapering amid poor results. Overall, while Nortman's initiatives have elevated visibility for women's issues, the scrutiny highlights a tension between inspirational rhetoric and verifiable impacts, where causal links between advocacy inputs and long-term equity gains in sports or workplaces remain empirically underdeveloped.

Personal Life

Family and Relationships

Kara Nortman married Jake Elliot Blumenthal on January 8, 2007, at the New York City Municipal Building. The couple began dating in 2004 and courted for over two years without exchanging text messages, relying instead on phone calls and in-person communication. Blumenthal, a former vice president at the time of their wedding, has publicly expressed admiration for Nortman's professional achievements, including her role in founding Angel City FC.
Nortman and Blumenthal have three daughters and reside in Los Angeles, California. Nortman's parents include her father, a nephrologist with a practice in Los Angeles, and her mother, who serves as his office manager. No public details are available regarding siblings or extended family relationships.

Residences and Philanthropic Interests

Kara Nortman resides in Beverly Hills, California, within the Los Angeles metropolitan area, alongside her husband, Jake Blumenthal, and their three daughters. The family's primary residence is at 245 South Linden Drive, a property held in the Blumenthal Nortman Living Trust. 
Nortman's philanthropic efforts center on advancing gender equity in technology and venture capital. She is a founding member of All Raise, a nonprofit founded in 2017 by venture capitalists to close the funding gap for women-led startups and increase female representation among investors and founders. The organization provides resources, mentorship, and advocacy, having influenced over $10 billion in commitments to diverse founders by 2023 through pledges from participating firms.
