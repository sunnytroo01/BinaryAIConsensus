 Galois cohomology is the application of group cohomology theory to Galois groups, where for a field KKK and its algebraic closure K\overline{K}K, the absolute Galois group GK=\Gal(K/K)G_K = \Gal(\overline{K}/K)GK=\Gal(K/K) acts on modules MMM, yielding cohomology groups Hi(GK,M)H^i(G_K, M)Hi(GK,M) that encode information about extensions, fixed points, and obstructions in arithmetic geometry. Formally, the first cohomology group H1(G,M)H^1(G, M)H1(G,M) consists of 1-cocycles modulo 1-coboundaries, where a 1-cocycle is a map f:GMf: G \to Mf:GM satisfying f(gh)=f(g)+gf(h)f(gh) = f(g) + g \cdot f(h)f(gh)=f(g)+gf(h) for g,hGg, h \in Gg,hG, measuring derivations or torsors under the group action.
Developed in the mid-20th century, Galois cohomology builds on earlier work in group cohomology by mathematicians like Emmy Noether, who extended Hilbert's theorem to arbitrary Galois extensions, and was systematized by Jean-Pierre Serre in his seminal 1964 book Cohomologie galoisienne. Key advancements include John Tate's local duality theorem, which establishes perfect pairings Hi(K,M)H2i(K,M)Q/ZH^i(K, M) \times H^{2-i}(K, M^\vee) \to \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}Hi(K,M)H2i(K,M)Q/Z for local fields KKK and finite modules MMM, implying finiteness of these groups and vanishing in higher degrees. Higher cohomology groups, such as H2(GK,K)H^2(G_K, \overline{K}^\times)H2(GK,K), classify central simple algebras over KKK via the Brauer group, linking to class field theory where H2(Gp,Qp)Q/ZH^2(G_p, \overline{\mathbb{Q}}_p^\times) \cong \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}H2(Gp,Qp)Q/Z for ppp-adic fields.
In applications, Galois cohomology underpins the study of elliptic curves through Selmer groups, which are subgroups of H1(Q,E)H^1(\mathbb{Q}, E)H1(Q,E) imposing local conditions and bounding the Mordell-Weil rank via the Shafarevich-Tate group as its kernel. Globally, the Poitou-Tate exact sequence relates local and global cohomology, enabling Euler characteristic formulas and Euler systems for LLL-functions. These tools extend to non-abelian settings via cohomology of algebraic groups, influencing automorphic forms and the Langlands program.

Overview and Prerequisites

Basic Concepts in Group Cohomology

Group cohomology provides a framework for studying the algebraic structure of groups through homological methods, particularly via cochain complexes derived from group actions on modules. Central to this theory is the notion of a G-module, where G is a discrete group and M is an abelian group equipped with a left action of G, denoted $ g \cdot m $ for $ g \in G $ and $ m \in M $. This action satisfies $ (gh) \cdot m = g \cdot (h \cdot m) $ and $ e \cdot m = m $ for the identity $ e \in G $, making M a left module over the group ring $ \mathbb{Z}G $.
The cochain complex is constructed as follows: for each integer $ n \geq 0 $, the group of n-cochains $ C^n(G, M) $ consists of all functions $ f: G^n \to M $, forming an abelian group under pointwise addition. The coboundary operator $ \delta^n: C^n(G, M) \to C^{n+1}(G, M) $ is defined by

(nf)(g1,,gn+1)=g1f(g2,,gn+1)+i=1n(1)if(g1,,gigi+1,,gn+1)+(1)n+1f(g1,,gn)
for $ f \in C^n(G, M) $ and $ g_1, \dots, g_{n+1} \in G $. This operator satisfies $ \delta^{n+1} \circ \delta^n = 0 $, yielding a cochain complex $ (C^(G, M), \delta^) $. The cohomology groups are then $ H^n(G, M) = \ker(\delta^n) / \operatorname{im}(\delta^{n-1}) $ for $ n \geq 1 $, with $ H^0(G, M) = \ker(\delta^0) = M^G $, the subgroup of fixed points $ { m \in M \mid g \cdot m = m \ \forall g \in G } $. For $ n=1 $, the 1-cocycles $ Z^1(G, M) $ are the derivations $ f: G \to M $ satisfying $ f(gh) = f(g) + g \cdot f(h) $, while the 1-coboundaries $ B^1(G, M) $ are the inner derivations $ f(g) = g \cdot m - m $ for some fixed $ m \in M $, so $ H^1(G, M) \cong Z^1(G, M) / B^1(G, M) $.
In cases of trivial action (where $ g \cdot m = m $ for all $ g, m $), the theory simplifies, and normalized cochainsthose vanishing whenever any argument is the identity $ e $are often used to compute cohomology efficiently, as they form a subcomplex with the same cohomology as the full complex. This normalization, introduced to handle projective resolutions more tractably, preserves the essential structure while reducing computational complexity.
A concrete example illustrates these concepts: consider $ G = \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} = {e, \sigma} $ acting trivially on $ M = \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} $. Then $ H^0(G, M) = M = \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} $, and $ H^1(G, M) \cong \operatorname{Hom}(G, M) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} $, since the non-trivial homomorphism sends $ \sigma $ to the generator of $ M $, and there are no non-trivial inner derivations due to the trivial action. This non-trivial $ H^1 $ highlights how group cohomology captures extensions and symmetries beyond the fixed points.

Galois Groups and Fields

A Galois extension K/kK/kK/k of fields is an algebraic extension that is both normal and separable, meaning every irreducible polynomial in kkk with a root in KKK splits completely in KKK, and the minimal polynomials of elements in KKK over kkk have distinct roots. Equivalently, KKK is the splitting field over kkk of a separable polynomial. The Galois group Gal(K/k)\mathrm{Gal}(K/k)Gal(K/k), also denoted Autk(K)\mathrm{Aut}_k(K)Autk(K), consists of all field automorphisms of KKK that fix kkk pointwise. For a finite Galois extension, the order of Gal(K/k)\mathrm{Gal}(K/k)Gal(K/k) equals the degree .
The absolute Galois group Gk=Gal(k/k)G_k = \mathrm{Gal}(\bar{k}/k)Gk=Gal(k/k) is the Galois group of the algebraic closure k\bar{k}k of kkk over kkk, or more precisely, the separable closure to ensure separability. It carries the profinite Krull topology, defined as the inverse limit topology limGal(L/k)\varprojlim \mathrm{Gal}(L/k)limGal(L/k), where the limit runs over all finite Galois extensions L/kL/kL/k. This topology renders GkG_kGk a compact, totally disconnected Hausdorff topological group.
A GkG_kGk-module is a discrete abelian group MMM equipped with a continuous action of GkG_kGk, where the action is continuous with respect to the profinite topology on GkG_kGk and the discrete topology on MMM. Continuity holds if the stabilizer of each element in MMM is an open subgroup of GkG_kGk (of finite index). The action map GkMMG_k \times M \to MGkMM must be continuous with respect to the profinite topology on GkG_kGk and the discrete topology on MMM.
The absolute Galois group of the rationals, GQG_\mathbb{Q}GQ, exemplifies these structures as a profinite group that is compact and totally disconnected under the Krull topology. It contains dense subgroups generated by Frobenius elements Frobp\mathrm{Frob}_pFrobp, which are conjugacy classes in finite quotients corresponding to arithmetic progressions modulo primes ppp.
Infinite Galois theory establishes a bijection between the closed subgroups of GkG_kGk and the intermediate fields kLkk \subseteq L \subseteq \bar{k}kLk, where each closed subgroup HHH corresponds to its fixed field L=kHL = \bar{k}^HL=kH, and conversely, Gal(k/L)=H\mathrm{Gal}(\bar{k}/L) = HGal(k/L)=H. Finite subextensions correspond to open subgroups, while normal closed subgroups yield Galois subextensions over kkk.

Historical Development

Early Contributions

The foundations of Galois cohomology trace back to variste Galois's pioneering work in the 1830s on the solvability of polynomial equations by radicals. In his 1831 memoir, published posthumously in 1846, Galois introduced the Galois group as the automorphism group of the splitting field over the base field, demonstrating that the structure of this group determines whether the equation can be solved by radicals; however, his analysis did not involve cohomological methods.
In the 1890s, David Hilbert advanced the connections between Galois groups and arithmetic by studying reciprocity laws and unramified extensions in number fields. His 1897 report on algebraic number fields included Theorem 90, which asserts that for a cyclic Galois extension L/K with group G, the first cohomology group H(G, L^) vanishes, providing an early cohomological insight into norm equations. Hilbert's 12th problem, posed in his 1900 address to the International Congress of Mathematicians, called for the explicit construction of Hilbert class fieldsmaximal unramified abelian extensions whose Galois groups are isomorphic to the ideal class groupsmotivating much of the subsequent development in class field theory.
The 1920s saw significant progress through Teiji Takagi's completion of global class field theory. In his 1920 paper, Takagi proved the existence of class fields for number fields, establishing a bijection between finite abelian extensions and ray class groups via norms and conductors, thus resolving Hilbert's program for abelian cases. Concurrently, Emil Artin's 1924 paper explored Galois representations and L-functions attached to characters of Galois groups, while his 1927 reciprocity law generalized quadratic reciprocity to arbitrary abelian extensions, identifying the Frobenius automorphism with ideal classes to yield an explicit isomorphism between Galois groups and idelic quotients. These results linked abelian Galois groups directly to arithmetic ideals but left non-abelian extensions unresolved.
During the 1930s, Emmy Noether's 1933 generalization of Hilbert's Theorem 90 to arbitrary finite Galois extensions laid early cohomological groundwork by interpreting 1-cocycles for non-cyclic groups. Oswald Teichmller and Claude Chevalley began incorporating cohomological language into class field theory, shifting from explicit reciprocity constructions to abstract group-theoretic tools. Teichmller, in works around 19341936, interpreted higher cohomology groups like H(G, ) algebraically to analyze extension structures, revealing obstructions in non-abelian settings. Chevalley, building on his 1933 thesis, introduced ideles in 1936 to unify local and global reciprocity and employed explicit idempotent elements in the group ring to project onto invariant subspaces, facilitating cohomological computations of class groups. This reformulation highlighted persistent gaps in non-abelian class field theory, where no analogous reciprocity exists, paving the way for later axiomatic developments.

Modern Advancements

In the mid-20th century, Henri Cartan extended the framework of group cohomology to topological groups during his seminars at the cole Normale Suprieure, providing tools essential for handling profinite groups in Galois theory. This generalization, formalized in the 1956 monograph Homological Algebra co-authored with Samuel Eilenberg, enabled the computation of cohomology for continuous actions and influenced subsequent applications to infinite Galois groups.
John Tate's 1950 Princeton thesis revolutionized local class field theory by recasting it in cohomological terms, establishing duality theorems that pair cohomology and homology groups. A cornerstone result is Tate's local duality theorem, establishing perfect pairings Hi(k,M)H2i(k,M(1))Q/ZH^i(k, M) \times H^{2-i}(k, M^\vee(1)) \to \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}Hi(k,M)H2i(k,M(1))Q/Z for finite modules MMM, implying that H2(Gk,k)Q/ZH^2(G_k, \bar{k}^\times) \cong \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}H2(Gk,k)Q/Z for non-archimedean local fields kkk, providing a cohomological interpretation of the Brauer group linked to the reciprocity map from kk^\timesk. These ideas, further elaborated in Tate's later works, bridged abstract algebra and arithmetic duality.
Alexander Grothendieck's seminal 1957 paper "Sur quelques points d'algbre homologique," published in the Thoku Mathematical Journal, introduced derived categories and spectral sequences in homological algebra, setting the stage for tale cohomology as a generalization of Galois cohomology to schemes. This framework allowed cohomology to capture geometric invariants over arbitrary base fields, transforming the study of algebraic varieties. At the 1958 International Congress of Mathematicians in Edinburgh, Tate and Jean-Pierre Serre delivered influential talks on cohomological techniques in number theory and algebraic geometry, underscoring the field's rapid expansion.
In the 1980s and 1990s, Jean-Marc Fontaine pioneered p-adic Hodge theory, merging Galois cohomology with crystalline and de Rham cohomology to classify p-adic Galois representations and filtered modules. His constructions of period rings, such as BdRB_{\mathrm{dR}}BdR and BcrisB_{\mathrm{cris}}Bcris, provided a bridge between tale cohomology and Hodge theory over p-adic fields. More broadly, Galois cohomology has advanced into non-abelian settings, where pointed sets replace groups to handle non-abelian Galois actions, and into motivic cohomology, revealing deep ties to algebraic cycles. Unresolved challenges persist, notably the Bloch-Kato conjecture, which posits exact control of Galois cohomology classes by motivic cohomology and special L-values, remaining a focal point for ongoing research.

In Algebraic Geometry and Number Theory

Galois cohomology plays a pivotal role in descent theory within algebraic geometry, where the group $ H^1(G_k, \mathrm{GL}_n(\bar{k})) $ classifies isomorphism classes of $ k $-forms of the vector space $ \bar{k}^n $, corresponding to projective modules of rank $ n $ over the ring of integers in $ k $ or vector bundles on varieties over $ k $. In the context of Kummer theory, the cohomology group $ H^1(G_k, \mu_n) $ parametrizes cyclic covers of degree $ n $ of schemes over $ k $, arising from the exact Kummer sequence $ 0 \to \mu_n \to \mathbb{G}_m \xrightarrow{n} \mathbb{G}_m \to 0 $ in the tale topology, which yields $ H^1(X, \mu_n) \cong \mathrm{Pic}(X) $ for curves and links to the structure of line bundles. These tools enable the study of torsors and principal homogeneous spaces under Galois action, facilitating the reconstruction of geometric objects from their base change to $ \bar{k} $.
In arithmetic geometry, particularly for elliptic curves, Selmer groups provide a refined measure of the rational points via Galois cohomology. For an elliptic curve $ E $ over a number field $ k $, the $ n $-Selmer group $ \mathrm{Sel}n(E/k) $ is defined as a subgroup of $ H^1(G_k, E) $, consisting of classes that satisfy local conditions at each place $ v $ of $ k $, ensuring compatibility with the Kummer map from $ E(k)/nE(k) $. The Tate-Shafarevich group $ \mathrm{Sha}(E/k) $ is then the kernel of the natural map $ H^1(G_k, E) \to \prod_v H^1(G{k_v}, E) $, capturing the global obstructions to the Hasse principle beyond local solvability. The exact sequence $ 0 \to E(k)/nE(k) \to \mathrm{Sel}_n(E/k) \to \mathrm{Sha}(E/k) \to 0 $ links the Mordell-Weil rank to the finiteness of these cohomology groups, with $ \mathrm{Sel}_n(E/k) $ proven finite for elliptic curves over number fields.
The Bloch-Kato conjecture refines this framework by relating the corank of the $ p $-adic Selmer group $ \mathrm{Sel}_p(E/\mathbb{Q}) $ to the order of vanishing of the L-function $ L(E, s) $ at $ s=1 $, via a Tamagawa number formula that equates the algebraic rank (from $ E(\mathbb{Q}) \otimes \mathbb{Q}_p $) plus the corank of $ \mathrm{Sha}(E/\mathbb{Q}) $ to the analytic rank, adjusted by Tamagawa factors and regulators. For elliptic curves with complex multiplication (CM), Skinner and Urban resolved this conjecture using three-variable $ p $-adic L-functions and Hida families over anticyclotomic $ \mathbb{Z}_p $-extensions, establishing the formula under conditions of good ordinary reduction at $ p \geq 11 $ and irreducibility of the mod $ p $ Galois representation. Their proof links the characteristic ideal of the dual Selmer group to interpolated $ p $-adic L-functions via Eisenstein series, confirming the expected equality in the Iwasawa algebra for semistable CM curves.
tale cohomology extends Galois cohomology to schemes, where for a variety $ X $ over $ k $, the tale cohomology groups $ H^i_{\t}(X_{\bar{k}}, \mathbb{Q}\ell(j)) $ carry a continuous action of the absolute Galois group $ G_k $, and the continuous cohomology $ H^*(G_k, H^i{\t}(X_{\bar{k}}, \mathbb{Q}\ell(j))) $ with these $ \ell $-adic coefficients captures Galois representations associated to the fibers of sheaves on $ X{\bar{k}} $. A key comparison theorem asserts that for a nonsingular variety $ X $ over $ \mathbb{C} $, $ H^r_{\t}(X, \Lambda) \cong H^r(X^{\mathrm{an}}, \Lambda) $ for finite abelian coefficients $ \Lambda $, equating tale and singular cohomology via the Riemann existence theorem, which identifies finite tale covers of $ X $ with those of its analytification. This isomorphism preserves the Galois action, allowing arithmetic invariants like Frobenius eigenvalues to inform topological data.
As an illustrative example, consider an abelian variety $ A $ over $ \mathbb{Q} $. The Galois cohomology group $ H^1(G_\mathbb{Q}, A) $ appears in the descent exact sequence $ 0 \to A(\mathbb{Q})/n A(\mathbb{Q}) \to S^{(n)}(A/\mathbb{Q}) \to \mathrm{Sha}(A/\mathbb{Q}) \to 0 $, where $ S^{(n)}(A/\mathbb{Q}) $ is the $ n $-Selmer group, finite by results on abelian varieties over number fields. The finiteness of $ S^{(n)}(A/\mathbb{Q}) $ implies that $ A(\mathbb{Q})/n A(\mathbb{Q}) $ is finite for all $ n $, bounding the Mordell-Weil rank and establishing the finite generation of $ A(\mathbb{Q}) $ as $ A(\mathbb{Q})_{\mathrm{tors}} \oplus \mathbb{Z}^r $ for some $ r \geq 0 $. This descent perspective underscores how Galois cohomology constrains the arithmetic of abelian varieties, with $ \mathrm{Sha}(A/\mathbb{Q}) $ conjectured finite to pinpoint the rank precisely.
