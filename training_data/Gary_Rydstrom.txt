 Gary Rydstrom is an American sound designer, re-recording mixer, and film director best known for his pioneering contributions to cinematic audio at Skywalker Sound, where he has won seven Academy Awards in sound and sound editing categories for films including Terminator 2: Judgment Day (two awards, 1991), Jurassic Park (two awards, 1993), Titanic (1997), and Saving Private Ryan (two awards, 1998).
Born in Chicago, Illinois, on June 29, 1959, and raised in Elmhurst, Rydstrom graduated from the University of Southern California's School of Cinematic Arts before beginning his career in sound at Lucasfilm's Skywalker Sound in 1983 as a machine room operator, eventually rising to become Director of Creative Operations, Sound Designer, and Mixer.
Throughout his career, Rydstrom has innovated sound design for over 100 films, creating memorable effects such as the T-1000's liquid metal sounds in Terminator 2: Judgment Day and the dinosaur roars in Jurassic Park using animal recordings and creative layering techniques, while also mixing audio for projects like Star Wars: Episode I The Phantom Menace (1999), Ready Player One (2018), and Jurassic World Rebirth (2025).
In addition to sound work, Rydstrom has directed Pixar shorts including the Academy Award-nominated Lifted (2006) and Toy Story Toons: Hawaiian Vacation (2011), as well as the animated feature Strange Magic (2015), and supervised English dubs for Studio Ghibli films such as The Secret World of Arrietty (2010), From Up on Poppy Hill (2011), and The Wind Rises (2013). He has received Career Achievement Awards from the Cinema Audio Society and the Motion Picture Sound Editors for his lifetime contributions to the field.

Early life and education

Upbringing in Illinois

Gary Roger Rydstrom was born on June 29, 1959, in Chicago, Illinois.
His family soon relocated to the suburb of Elmhurst, Illinois, where Rydstrom grew up in a typical Midwestern environment that fostered his initial exposure to creative pursuits.
He attended and graduated from York Community High School in 1977, completing his secondary education in the local community.
From a young age, Rydstrom developed a strong fascination with filmmaking, particularly inspired by the silent comedies of Charlie Chaplin and Mack Sennett, which ignited his passion for the mechanics of movie creation during his teenage years in Illinois.
These early interests in visual storytelling laid the groundwork for his later exploration of sound as an integral element of film, prompting him to pursue formal studies at the University of Southern California.

Studies at USC

Gary Rydstrom enrolled at the University of Southern California (USC) School of Cinematic Arts. He graduated in 1981 with a degree in film production.
During his studies, Rydstrom emphasized sound design and editing within the film production curriculum, viewing audio not just as technical support but as a narrative tool to enhance storytelling. Key coursework included hands-on classes in cinematic techniques, where he explored audio manipulation and integration into visual media. In one notable project from the introductory production course (often referred to as 310 at USC), Rydstrom handled the sound mixing for a student film, collaborating closely with peer Tom Johnson, who served as lead mixer; this experience allowed him to experiment with layering everyday sounds to create immersive effects.
Rydstrom's time at USC was shaped by mentorship from faculty who emphasized collaborative filmmaking, teaching him to mentor peers and foster team dynamics essential for post-production roles. These interactions, including guidance from professors with ties to industry pioneers like George Lucas, ignited his passion for Hollywood sound work and highlighted the creative potential of audio in cinema. As Rydstrom later reflected, his USC education provided the foundational skills and network that made a career in sound design feasible.

Career

Beginnings at Skywalker Sound

Gary Rydstrom joined Skywalker Sound in 1983 as an operator in the machine room, marking the start of his professional career within George Lucas's Lucasfilm operations. The position came through a recommendation from one of his college professors at the University of Southern California, where Rydstrom had recently graduated with a foundation in film sound from his studies in cinematic arts.
Under the mentorship of acclaimed sound designer Ben Burtt, known for his pioneering work on Star Wars, Rydstrom immersed himself in the fundamentals of sound design. Burtt guided him in essential techniques, including Foley artistrycreating everyday sound effects through physical performanceand the innovative layering of audio elements to enhance cinematic storytelling. This hands-on learning emphasized the creative potential of sound beyond mere reproduction, shaping Rydstrom's approach to immersive audio environments.
In his initial years, Rydstrom handled assistant-level tasks on smaller-scale projects at the facility, gradually honing his expertise in sound editing and mixing. These foundational assignments allowed him to experiment with audio manipulation tools and collaborate within the collaborative atmosphere of Skywalker Sound, building proficiency in syncing effects with visuals and balancing mixes for theatrical impact.
By the late 1980s, Rydstrom had advanced from these entry-level positions to the role of supervising sound editor, overseeing teams and contributing more prominently to post-production workflows. This progression reflected his rapid assimilation of the facility's rigorous standards and his growing reputation for meticulous craftsmanship in sound. In 1998, he was appointed Director of Creative Operations for Skywalker Sound, overseeing the creative and technological direction for the facility.

Sound design achievements

Gary Rydstrom's sound design for Terminator 2: Judgment Day (1991) exemplified his innovative approach to sci-fi effects, layering everyday objects to create hyper-realistic metallic sounds for the T-1000's shape-shifting sequences. For instance, he combined a suction sound from opening a can of dog food with the spray of compressed air into a flour-water mixture to produce the viscous, evolving metallic bubbles as the liquid metal reformed. Similarly, grinding a pistachio nut on a metal plate yielded the crunch of a crushed skull underfoot, while shoving glass into a yogurt bucket simulated bullets impacting the T-1000's fluid body. These techniques, processed through the Synclavier sampler, enhanced the film's immersive intensity under director James Cameron's vision for amplified realism.
In Jurassic Park (1993), Rydstrom pioneered organic sound layering for prehistoric creatures, drawing from animal recordings to craft believable dinosaur vocalizations without relying on synthesis. The Tyrannosaurus rex roar blended a baby elephant's high-pitched trumpet from the San Francisco Zoo with low-frequency tiger growls and an alligator's bellow, while velociraptor screeches incorporated slowed horse whinnies, tortoise rasps, and African crane calls. Footsteps were achieved by layering sonic booms for deep rumbles and recordings of falling redwood trees for impacts, ensuring spatial depth that heightened suspense in Steven Spielberg's direction. This project marked a breakthrough in using field recordings to evoke extinct life, influencing subsequent creature sound design.
Rydstrom's work on Titanic (1997) shifted toward expansive environmental audio, where he contributed to re-recording mixing and effects design at Skywalker Sound, emphasizing the film's aquatic scale. He focused on amplifying water's presence through boom tracks and subwoofers more extensively than in any prior project, creating disorienting splashes and creaks that contrasted the ship's grandeur with its doom. Collaborating with Chris Boyes, Rydstrom layered hydrophone recordings and shipyard ambiences to make the sinking feel visceral and immediate, supporting James Cameron's historical epic.
For Saving Private Ryan (1998), Rydstrom designed battle sounds to immerse viewers in World War II chaos from a soldier's subjective viewpoint, layering thousands of individual effects for the Omaha Beach sequence. Bullets whizzing past combined ricochets off metal and wet thuds into flesh, while underwater penetrations used slowed, muffled impacts to convey drowning terror. He balanced explosive barrages with intimate details like ringing ears and labored breaths, drawing from archival recordings and foley to achieve raw authenticity in his ongoing partnership with Spielberg. This approach redefined war audio, prioritizing emotional immediacy over stylized action.
Rydstrom's contributions to Pixar's Finding Nemo (2003) highlighted his versatility in animation, using household items and manipulated recordings to animate underwater life. Nemo's injured fin flap came from a wet paper towel, while bubble pops were jacuzzi jets filtered for oceanic texture; a dental drill recording from his assistant provided the menacing dentist scene's whine. By placing microphones in aquariums and coastal caves, he captured sloshing water and whale gurgles, fostering a playful yet immersive seascape that complemented the studio's character-driven storytelling. His Pixar tenure, spanning multiple films, underscored a collaborative evolution from live-action effects to animated whimsy.
Throughout these projects, Rydstrom's long-term collaborations with Steven Spielbergon over a dozen films including Jurassic Park, Saving Private Ryan, War Horse (2011), Ready Player One (2018), and continuing with sound design for Jurassic World Rebirth (2025)and Pixar teams like Toy Story (1995) and Finding Nemo allowed him to refine techniques across genres, blending innovation with director-specific visions. His role expanded to re-recording mixing on more than 100 films, where he integrated designed effects into final mixes, as seen in credits for blockbusters grossing billions worldwide. This dual expertise solidified his influence on cinematic audio, prioritizing layered, source-based realism.

Directorial ventures

Gary Rydstrom made his directorial debut with the Pixar short film Lifted in 2006, a comedic animated piece centered on a bumbling teenage alien attempting to abduct a human during an exam supervised by his stern instructor, highlighting mishaps with spaceship controls for humorous effect. The film earned an Academy Award nomination for Best Animated Short Film, showcasing Rydstrom's ability to blend visual gags with rhythmic timing drawn from his sound design background.
Rydstrom continued directing Pixar shorts with Hawaiian Vacation in 2011, the inaugural entry in the Toy Story Toons series, where the toys orchestrate a makeshift Hawaiian getaway for Ken and Barbie in Bonnie's bedroom during winter break. The short, released alongside Cars 2, emphasized ensemble comedy with contributions from the Toy Story cast, including improvised lines from actors like Michael Keaton and Jodi Benson, while incorporating gag-driven romance inspired by surprise vacation tropes.
In 2008, Rydstrom was announced as director for Pixar's feature film Newt, a romantic comedy about the last two blue-footed newts on Eartha male and female forced together by scientists to prevent extinction but who initially despise each otherexploring themes of conservation and unlikely partnerships amid a perilous adventure. The project, originally slated for a 2011 release as Pixar's 14th feature, underwent extensive development including script rewrites before being canceled in 2011 due to creative challenges identified by Pixar's Brain Trust, with Rydstrom removed from the role during production for unspecified reasons.
Rydstrom's first feature-length directorial effort was Strange Magic (2015), a Lucasfilm animated jukebox musical fantasy inspired by A Midsummer Night's Dream, following a fairy princess's quest for a love potion that sparks an interspecies romance between a tomboyish fairy and a goblin king, featuring pop songs reimagined in a fairy-tale world. Conceived by George Lucas over 15 years earlier and primarily produced in Singapore, the film marked Rydstrom's transition to overseeing full visual narratives while co-writing the screenplay. It received mixed critical reception, praised for its animation and voice performances but critiqued for uneven pacing and song integration, earning a 19% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes and a 1.5/4 from Roger Ebert.
Beyond original projects, Rydstrom directed English-language dubs for several Studio Ghibli films, adapting scripts to preserve emotional timing and lip-sync while leveraging his sound expertise for natural delivery. These included Tales from Earthsea (2006), his first Ghibli collaboration; The Secret World of Arrietty (2010); From Up on Poppy Hill (2011); and The Wind Rises (2013), where he worked closely with Hayao Miyazaki to cast actors like Joseph Gordon-Levitt and ensure fidelity to the original's rhythm.

Awards and honors

Academy Awards

Gary Rydstrom is one of the most honored sound designers in Academy Awards history, with a total of 20 nominations in the Best Sound, Best Sound Mixing, and Best Sound Editing categories spanning from the 64th ceremony in 1992 to the 94th in 2022. He secured 7 wins, all in sound-related fields, highlighting his pivotal role in elevating cinematic audio through innovative effects and mixing techniques.
His first two Oscars came at the 64th Academy Awards in 1992 for Terminator 2: Judgment Day (1991), where he won both Best Sound (shared with Tom Johnson, Gary Summers, and Lee Orloff) and Best Sound Effects Editing (shared with Gloria S. Borders). These victories recognized Rydstrom's groundbreaking work on the film's robotic and action sequences, outcompeting nominees like Backdraft in a year noted for advances in digital sound integration.
At the 66th Academy Awards in 1994, Rydstrom achieved a rare double win for Jurassic Park (1993), earning Best Sound (shared with Gary Summers, Shawn Murphy, and Ron Judkins) and Best Sound Effects Editing (shared with Richard Hymns). The film's dinosaur roars and environmental ambiences, crafted at Skywalker Sound, marked a milestone in creature sound design, prevailing over strong contenders like Schindler's List. This was the first time since 1973 that a film won both sound categories, underscoring the technical evolution in post-production audio.
Rydstrom's subsequent wins included Best Sound for Titanic (1997) at the 70th Academy Awards in 1998 (shared with Tom Johnson, Gary Summers, and Mark Ulano), celebrated for its immersive underwater and orchestral layering amid competition from L.A. Confidential. In 1999, at the 71st ceremony, he triumphed again for Saving Private Ryan (1998) with Best Sound (shared with Gary Summers, Andy Nelson, and Ronald Judkins) and Best Sound Effects Editing (shared with Richard Hymns), lauded for the visceral realism of battle sequences that beat out The Matrix and influenced war film audio standards.

Other awards and recognitions

Rydstrom has earned five Golden Reel Awards from the Motion Picture Sound Editors (MPSE) for outstanding achievements in sound editing, including wins for his work on Jurassic Park (1993) in the category of Best Sound Editing - Sound Effects & Foley, and Saving Private Ryan (1998) for Best Sound Editing - Dialogue & Automatic Dialogue Replacement. He also received the MPSE Career Achievement Award in 2005, recognizing his lifetime contributions to the field of sound editing.
In addition to his MPSE honors, Rydstrom was awarded two Cinema Audio Society (CAS) Awards: the Career Achievement Award in 2004 for his overall impact on sound mixing, and the Outstanding Achievement in Sound Mixing for a Feature Film in 2013 for Les Misrables. He secured two BAFTA Awards for Best Sound, one in 1998 for Titanic and another in 1999 for Saving Private Ryan, highlighting his innovative approaches to immersive audio design in major blockbusters.
Rydstrom's versatility extends to music engineering, as evidenced by his 2023 Grammy Award for Best Engineered Album, Classical, shared for the album Philharmonia Fantastique performed by the Chicago Symphony Orchestra and conducted by Edwin Outwater. Other notable recognitions include the 2015 Hollywood Film Award for Sound on Bridge of Spies, acknowledging his precise re-recording mixing that enhanced the film's tense interrogations and period authenticity. He has also received nominations for Satellite Awards in the Best Sound category, such as for War Horse (2011), underscoring his consistent excellence across genres.

Legacy

Influence on sound design

Gary Rydstrom's innovative use of creative Foley techniques has significantly influenced modern sound design, particularly in crafting sounds for fantastical creatures in CGI-heavy films. In Jurassic Park (1993), he layered recordings of animal vocalizationssuch as baby elephant trumpets for the T. rex roar and horse whinnies combined with tortoise rasps for velociraptorsto create believable dinosaur audio that avoided generic roars, setting a standard for blending organic Foley with digital manipulation. This approach popularized the use of exaggerated, everyday sources to evoke realism in impossible scenarios, impacting subsequent projects like Pixar's animated features where similar Foley layering brought inanimate objects to life with personality-driven acoustics.
Rydstrom played a pivotal role in the evolution of digital sound during the 1990s and 2000s, bridging analog traditions with emerging technologies at Skywalker Sound. His work on films like Terminator 2: Judgment Day (1991) pioneered digital effects processing for hyperrealistic action sequences, while contributions to early 2000s projects advanced surround sound capabilities. By the early 2010s, Rydstrom created the first demonstration films for Dolby Atmos, utilizing object-based audio to enable precise placement of sounds in three-dimensional space, which helped establish industry standards for immersive mixing and influenced the adoption of Atmos in theatrical releases like Brave (2012).
In interviews, Rydstrom has articulated a philosophy of immersive audio storytelling that prioritizes emotional resonance over mere technical fidelity, viewing sound as an integral narrative driver equivalent to visuals in engaging audiences. He emphasizes designing audio to guide viewer attention and heighten tension, as seen in his layered mixes that integrate subtle environmental cues with dramatic effects to build immersion. This perspective has shaped sound design pedagogy and practice, promoting sound as a tool for psychological depth in cinema.
Rydstrom's pioneering status is widely acknowledged by peers at Skywalker Sound and beyond, who credit him with elevating the craft through innovative techniques that remain foundational. Successors like those continuing his legacy at the facility often reference his methods as benchmarks for blending creativity with technological advancement, solidifying his impact on the field's standards.

Mentorship and recent contributions

Rydstrom has played a significant role in mentoring emerging sound designers at Skywalker Sound, where he serves as Director of Creative Operations, fostering the next generation through hands-on guidance and internships. For instance, he supervised early interns, providing direct training in experimental sound techniques that shaped their careers in the industry.
In addition to his work at Skywalker Sound, Rydstrom has contributed to education by delivering guest lectures at institutions like the University of Southern California (USC) School of Cinematic Arts, his alma mater, where he shares insights on sound design principles and storytelling through audio. These sessions emphasize practical applications drawn from his extensive experience, inspiring students to innovate in cinematic sound.
Turning to recent projects, Rydstrom served as sound design consultant for the 2024 short film Make a Circle, collaborating on effects and mixing to enhance its narrative impact. In 2025, he contributed to Jurassic World: Rebirth as an additional sound designer and consultant for Skywalker Sound, focusing on crafting new dinosaur vocals; he provided key elements like a baby elephant recording repurposed for the T. rex roar, drawing on his iconic work from the original Jurassic Park.
As of 2025, Rydstrom remains active in advisory capacities, including serving on the advisory board for Northwestern University's Sound Arts and Industries program, where he offers strategic guidance on curriculum and industry practices. His ongoing involvement underscores a continued commitment to advancing sound design through both creative output and educational influence.
