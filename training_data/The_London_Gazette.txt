The London Gazette
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The London Gazette is the official public record and journal of the Government of the United Kingdom, first published on 15 November 1665 as a government-sanctioned periodical for disseminating news and proclamations. It holds the distinction of being the oldest continuously published newspaper in Britain, serving primarily to notify the public of statutory instruments, royal proclamations, military appointments, honours, company dissolutions, insolvencies, and other legally required announcements that carry official weight upon publication.
Originally issued as The Oxford Gazette during King Charles II's relocation to Oxford amid the Great Plague of London, the publication relocated with the court and adopted its current name upon return to the capital in early 1666, evolving from a weekly news sheet into a formalized gazette under royal authority. Its content has historically documented pivotal events, including battlefield despatches, coronations, currency reforms, and national crises, providing an unbroken archival record of British governance and societal changes over nearly 360 years. Today, managed by The Stationery Office, it encompasses the former Edinburgh Gazette and Belfast Gazette, ensuring comprehensive coverage across the United Kingdom while maintaining its role as the authoritative source for notices that trigger legal effects, such as peerage creations and public sector contract awards. Free digital access via its official website has democratized historical research, though its primary function remains the promulgation of binding public notices rather than journalistic reporting.

Historical Foundations

Origins and Founding in 1665

The Great Plague of 1665 prompted King Charles II to relocate the royal court from London to Oxford in October of that year, seeking to evade the outbreak that ultimately killed approximately 15% of London's population. This displacement created a need for an official channel to disseminate court news and government proclamations to the public and exiled officials, leading to the establishment of a periodical publication authorized by the crown.
The publication, initially titled The Oxford Gazette, was founded under the direction of Secretary of State Sir William Morrice and entrusted to journalist Henry Muddiman, who held a royal patent for news dissemination. Muddiman, previously involved in Restoration-era newsletters, produced the first issue on 7 November 1665, printed twice weekly from Oxford. The inaugural edition bore the imprint "Printed at Oxford by Leonard Lichfield Printer to the University, for Henry Muddiman," reflecting its official status without commercial advertising or sensationalism.
Early issues focused on verifiable official matters, including foreign intelligence relayed from the court, domestic proclamations, and public health data such as the Bills of Mortality tracking plague deaths. This format emphasized authoritative reporting over partisan or speculative content, establishing the periodical as a state-sanctioned record rather than a private venture.

Early Evolution and Name Change to London Gazette

The Oxford Gazette was established in 1665 amid the Great Plague ravaging London, which prompted King Charles II to relocate the royal court to Oxford for safety, necessitating a mechanism to disseminate court news to subscribers. The inaugural issue appeared on 7 November 1665, printed at the University Press and distributed twice weekly on Mondays and Thursdays via post, featuring official royal declarations, foreign intelligence, reports of natural disasters, and accounts of crimes to provide reliable information under strict post-Restoration press controls. Diarist Samuel Pepys described an early edition as "very pretty, full of newes, and no folly in it," reflecting its focus on factual content amid widespread censorship that suppressed competing publications.
As the plague abated by early 1666, killing approximately 15% of London's population, the court returned to the capital, prompting the gazette's relocation and rebranding. The first issue titled The London Gazettenumbered as the 24th overallwas published on 1 February 1666, marking the formal name change to align with the restored London base and solidifying its role as the primary authorized news outlet. This transition entrenched royal patronage, positioning it as the sole permitted newspaper and the earliest official journal of record for proclamations and public notices.
Subsequent early adaptations underscored operational resilience; following the destruction of printing premises at Baynard's Castle in the Great Fire of London on 3 September 1666, production shifted temporarily to the Savoy, where it delivered a concise eyewitness account of the conflagration's commencement. These developments evolved the publication from a plague-era expedient into a durable government-sanctioned periodical, emphasizing verifiable announcements over unsubstantiated reports.

Calendar Adjustments and Dates Before 1752

Prior to 1752, The London Gazette recorded all dates according to the Julian calendar, referred to as Old Style (O.S.), which was the official system in England at the time. This calendar featured a year length of 365.25 days, resulting in a gradual drift of approximately 1 day every 128 years relative to the solar year; by the early 18th century, it lagged 11 days behind the more accurate Gregorian calendar adopted elsewhere in Europe since 1582.
Under Old Style conventions in England, the legal and civil new year commenced on 25 March (Lady Day), rather than 1 January, a practice inherited from medieval traditions aligning with the Feast of the Annunciation. Thus, Gazette issues from 1 January to 24 March were dated to the preceding year; for instance, an entry dated 15 February 1666 actually fell within what modern usage would term early 1667. The publication adhered strictly to these contemporary norms without notation of alternatives, reflecting its role as an official record of government notices.
Modern researchers interpreting pre-1752 Gazette dates often apply dual datingsuch as 24 March 1709/10to bridge the gap between Old Style dating and the post-1752 New Style (N.S.) system, where the year starts on 1 January and accounts for the 11-day discrepancy. However, the original issues contain no such adjustments or annotations; dates appear as printed, preserving the historical context but requiring caution to avoid misaligning events with Gregorian equivalents. This approach ensures fidelity to primary sources while acknowledging the Julian system's limitations, which prompted the Calendar (New Style) Act 1750 to enforce the switch effective 14 September 1752 (O.S.), becoming 25 September (N.S.) after omitting the intervening days.

Operational Evolution

18th to 19th Century Developments

During the 18th century, The London Gazette solidified its role as the primary official journal "Published by Authority," shifting emphasis from general news to authoritative government proclamations, state papers, and legal notices. By the late 1700s, it ceased regular publication of foreign news, focusing instead on domestic official matters to maintain its status as a reliable record of executive actions. This evolution was facilitated by the lapse of the Licensing Act in 1695, which eased publishing restrictions and allowed for expanded content without censorship constraints.
A significant development was the introduction of insolvency notices on 5 June 1712, pursuant to Queen Anne's Act to Relieve Insolvent Debtors, marking the Gazette's growing utility for public legal announcements. Over the century, bankruptcy proceedings became standardly recorded in its pages to ensure public notification, reflecting the increasing commercialization and legal formalization of debt in British society. The Gazette also published key national proclamations, such as the 1707 Acts of Union uniting England and Scotland, and the 1717 suppression of piracy, underscoring its function in disseminating binding executive directives.
In military affairs, the Gazette emerged as the conduit for commanders' despatches, with notable examples including Vice-Admiral Collingwood's report on the Battle of Trafalgar on 6 November 1805, celebrating Lord Nelson's victory. This practice originated the term "gazetted" for official recognition and "Mentioned in Despatches" for gallantry, as field reports detailed achievements and casualties for public and archival record. Extraordinary editions, like the 1815 issue containing the Duke of Wellington's Waterloo despatch, highlighted its role in wartime communication.
The 19th century saw continued expansion of the Gazette's scope amid imperial growth and administrative reforms, with notices encompassing colonial appointments, honours, and statutory instruments. From 1889, all Gazettes were published under the superintendence of His Majesty's Stationery Office, standardizing production and ensuring authoritative dissemination. Its unwavering weekly format persisted, prioritizing legal precision over journalistic speed, as it chronicled events from the Napoleonic Wars to Victorian-era state functions without interruption.

20th Century and World Wars

Throughout the 20th century, The London Gazette maintained its function as the United Kingdom's official journal of record, disseminating government proclamations, military appointments, honours, and statutory notices under the management of His Majesty's Stationery Office, which had assumed responsibility for its publication in 1889. Its format evolved to include structured sections such as State Intelligence, Orders in Council, and Acts of Parliament, reflecting the expanding scope of official announcements amid growing administrative demands.
The Gazette played a pivotal role during the First World War, beginning with the notification of the state of war with Germany on 4 August 1914. It published the Defence of the Realm Act on 11 August 1914, which imposed wartime controls, and issued the first despatches from the front on 8 September 1914. Throughout the conflict, it recorded War Office communiqus, including officer commissions, promotions, and gallantry awards such as the Victoria Cross, alongside "Mentioned in Despatches" (MiD) citations honouring contributions from personnel in combat, medical, veterinary, and support roles, with hundreds of thousands of such recognitions documented. These entries provided the authoritative public record of military personnel actions, culminating in notices related to the Treaty of Versailles on 1 July 1919 and Peace Day on 11 July 1919.
In the Second World War, the Gazette similarly announced the declaration of war against Germany on 3 September 1939 and served as the conduit for official military updates. From 1941 to 1951, it serialized commanders-in-chief despatches detailing operations in theatres including France, Belgium, Norway, and the Middle East, alongside notices of promotions, commendations, and honours for service members. Military notices spanning 1939 to 1945 encompassed over 1.3 million indexed records of personnel actions, reinforcing the publication's status as an indispensable archive for verifying wartime contributions and awards. This period underscored the Gazette's resilience as a primary source amid heightened demand for timely official records, without fundamental alterations to its twice-weekly issuance pattern.

Digital Transition and Recent Adaptations

The London Gazette's transition to digital formats enhanced public access to its official notices and archives, with content made available online via dedicated service-specific sections on the official website, including improved search tools for categories such as insolvency, companies, and wills and probate. This shift incorporated cutting-edge technology to maintain its role as the UK's official public record while adapting to modern user needs for efficient retrieval of business-critical information. Daily issues are now primarily disseminated as PDFs, with printed copies available upon request, reflecting a move toward digital primacy while preserving statutory publication requirements.
Recent adaptations include the launch of the Gazette's Data Service, which supplies structured datasets on company details, insolvencies, and deceased estates to support data-driven decision-making, accessible under the Open Government Licence for free use of open data. The publication retained its contract with The Stationery Office (TSO) in 2012, ensuring continued operational stability under The National Archives' oversight. In response to contemporary events, supplements have addressed significant occurrences, such as detailed notices related to the Coronavirus Act 2020 during the COVID-19 pandemic and a special edition documenting Queen Elizabeth II's funeral proceedings in September 2022.

Publishing Mechanics

Current Format, Frequency, and Accessibility

The London Gazette is published primarily in digital format, with individual notices appearing online upon approval and complete daily editions available as downloadable PDFs via the official website. Printed hard copies of editions can be ordered upon request, though digital access predominates for official and public use.
Editions are produced each working day (Monday through Friday), excluding UK bank holidays, incorporating notices submitted and vetted from the preceding business day(s). Weekly supplements, including Ministry of Defence announcements on military promotions and honors every Tuesday, are integrated into the London edition. Extraordinary editions are issued as needed for urgent or high-profile events, such as royal proclamations.
Public accessibility is facilitated through the free-of-charge website at www.thegazette.co.uk, which offers searchable databases of current and historical notices, downloadable PDF editions, and advanced features like saved searches and notice alerts. Archives are digitized and searchable from 1752 onward, with full text from 1665 available in varying completeness; mobile-optimized interfaces and developer APIs enable programmatic access under open government licensing.

Production Process and Legal Requirements

The London Gazette is published daily from Monday to Friday by The Stationery Office (TSO) under the superintendence of His Majesty's Stationery Office (HMSO), a division of The National Archives. Notices are primarily submitted electronically through the official website, utilizing structured formats such as XML files for code-based uploads or web-based forms for manual entry, enabling automated processing and validation. Submitters must be verified individuals acting in an official capacity, with authority to establish a public record; submissions via email or post for the London edition require receipt by 11:30 a.m. at least two working days prior to desired publication to allow for review and scheduling.
During production, submitted notices are scrutinized for accuracy, statutory compliance, procedural adherence, and data protection standards, with users able to save drafts and track pending items via the online portal. Approved notices appear online firsttypically the day before the formal edition dateand the Gazette assembles content for digital release each working day, while physical printing occurs on a subscription or on-demand basis rather than routine mass distribution. This hybrid process ensures rapid accessibility while maintaining archival integrity, with no publications on UK public holidays and adjusted schedules announced in advance, such as skips around Easter 2025.
Legally, publication in The Gazette fulfills statutory mandates under various UK laws, rendering notices legally binding upon appearance; for instance, companies incorporated in England and Wales must gazette announcements for proceedings like winding-up resolutions, liquidator appointments, or dissolutions to achieve public notification and evidentiary status. Certain secondary legislation, including some Statutory Instruments and Special Procedure Orders, requires pre-laying or compliance notices in The Gazette to evidence fulfillment of publication duties, as outlined in government practice guides. Submitters bear responsibility for verifying all legal prerequisites, with The Gazette providing default timelines (e.g., claims periods of two months and one day post-publication) where specifics are omitted, ensuring notices serve as indisputable public records without which associated actions may lack validity.

Content and Notices

Types of Official Announcements

The London Gazette publishes a diverse array of official announcements mandated or authorized by statute, serving as the authoritative public record for governmental and public sector communications. These announcements encompass state notices, parliamentary notices, and other public sector notices, distinct from military honors or commercial matters. Over 450 notice types exist in total, with 288 required exclusively in The Gazette to fulfill legal obligations.
State notices include proclamations from the Crown, honours and awards declarations (excluding military-specific ones), Privy Council Office decisions, changes of name or arms, and immigration or asylum-related matters. These are typically placed by central government organizations and carry statutory weight under various acts.
Parliamentary notices cover proceedings from the UK Parliament, House of Lords, House of Commons, and devolved bodies like the Scottish Parliament, ensuring official dissemination of legislative actions such as bill progressions or procedural updates.
Other public sector notices span ecclesiastical matters (e.g., under Marriage Acts), public finance announcements from the Treasury or Bank of England, transport regulations, planning applications under the Town and Country Planning Act 1990, health and medicines controls, environmental pollution orders, and energy or agriculture sector directives. Many require publication in the Gazette as per specific legislation, providing legal notice to affected parties.
Public notices, a broader category, address statutory requirements in areas like road traffic under the Road Traffic Regulation Act 1984, railways, water resources, and fisheries, often involving public consultations or regulatory changes that must be advertised for transparency and due process.

Military Honors, Appointments, and Awards

The London Gazette has served as the primary official publication for announcing British military honors, appointments, and awards since the 17th century, providing verifiable public record of entitlements derived from such notifications. These announcements include gallantry medals for exceptional bravery, such as the Victoria Cross, awarded in recognition of extreme devotion to duty in the presence of the enemy, with citations detailing specific actions. Other notable gallantry awards published include the Distinguished Service Order for distinguished service in action, the Military Cross for acts of exemplary gallantry during active operations against the enemy, and the Distinguished Flying Cross for similar acts in aerial operations.
Appointments and promotions within the armed forces are routinely gazetted, encompassing commissions, honorary ranks, and advancements, often tied to meritorious service or operational needs. For instance, on 14 June 2025, the Gazette notified honorary appointments in His Majesty's Army, such as those approved by Buckingham Palace for distinguished individuals. Broader honours lists, including the King's Birthday Honours and New Year Honours, feature military divisions recognizing personnel for long service, leadership, and contributions, with 70 British Army members awarded in the 2025 Birthday Honours for such achievements. Appointments to orders like the Most Excellent Order of the British Empire are similarly approved and published, as in notifications for military recognition of service.
Non-medal distinctions, such as Mentioned in Despatches (MiD), are also recorded, signifying commendable service in action without formal decoration; during World War I, recipients were entitled to a certificate and a bronze oak leaf emblem, with hundreds of thousands of such entries cataloged. Historically, the Gazette has documented military awards from conflicts like the Crimean War onward, evolving into a comprehensive national record of armed forces achievements spanning over 350 years. These publications ensure legal and evidentiary validity, as entitlement to wear awards typically requires the official Gazette announcement.

Commercial, Insolvency, and Legal Notices

The London Gazette publishes commercial notices primarily concerning corporate entities registered under the Companies Act 2006, including incorporations, dissolutions, name changes, and special resolutions such as reductions of share capital or alterations to articles of association. These notices ensure public transparency for commercial transactions, with examples including the formation of entities like ZORIX LTD on 30 September 2025, detailing company numbers and registered addresses. Publication is often mandatory for proceedings like voluntary strike-offs, where a first Gazette notice signals intent to dissolve a company, allowing creditors a two-month window to object.
Insolvency notices form a significant category, governed by the Insolvency Act 1986 and associated rules, covering personal bankruptcies and corporate procedures such as creditors' voluntary liquidations, administrations, and company voluntary arrangements (CVAs). For corporate insolvency, multiple notices are requiredtypically five to six for liquidationsincluding appointments of liquidators, meetings of creditors, and final distributions, as seen in cases like the members' voluntary liquidation of YORK PLANES LLP (OC313468) involving non-trading limited liability partnerships. Administrations, aimed at rescuing viable businesses, mandate notices for appointment, progress reports, and distributions, with pre-pack sales requiring swift publication to preserve asset value. Winding-up petitions, triggered by debts exceeding 750, must be advertised at least seven business days before hearings to notify creditors.
Legal notices in the Gazette encompass statutory requirements beyond core commercial and insolvency matters, such as court-ordered petitions, administrative receiverships (now limited post-2002 Enterprise Act), and public declarations for creditor claims in estates or proceedings. These serve as the official public record for enforceability, with insolvency-related legal steps like compulsory liquidations via court orders published to initiate creditor involvement. Failure to gazette such notices can invalidate proceedings, underscoring the Gazette's role in causal chains of legal finality and public accountability.

Significance and Legacy

Role in British Governance and Law

The London Gazette functions as one of the official journals of record for the United Kingdom government, serving as a permanent public repository for statutory and non-statutory notices that hold legal standing. Published under the authority of the King's Printer through letters patent, it ensures the dissemination of critical governmental announcements, including royal proclamations, parliamentary proceedings, and state events, thereby facilitating transparency and accountability in public administration. Its publications are explicitly protected by the Documentary Evidence Act 1882, which deems copies printed under the superintendence of His Majesty's Stationery Office admissible as prima facie evidence in legal proceedings without further proof of authenticity. 
In British governance, the Gazette plays a pivotal role by documenting the granting of royal assent to legislation, such as the Act of Union 1707 and the European Union Referendum Act 2015, thereby formalizing the culmination of the legislative process across UK parliaments. It also publishes notices related to military appointments, honors, and despatches, which confer official recognition and have been integral to wartime governance since events like the Duke of Wellington's Waterloo dispatch in issue 17028 on 8 June 1815. Courts and legislative bodies frequently reference the Gazette as authoritative, with over 288 types of noticesranging from company insolvencies to planning permissionsmandated exclusively for publication therein to achieve legal effect and public notification. 
Legally, publication in the Gazette triggers binding consequences, such as the initiation of insolvency proceedings or the dissolution of entities, as required by statutes like the Insolvency Act 1986, ensuring that affected parties receive constructive notice through this centralized, tamper-resistant medium. This mechanism underscores its causal role in enforcing compliance and resolving disputes, as non-publication can invalidate actions, while its archival nature supports evidentiary use in litigation and administrative reviews. The Gazette's status as the Crown's newspaper thus embeds it deeply in the constitutional framework, prioritizing verifiable public record over ephemeral media.

The Concept of Being "Gazetted"

The term "gazetted" refers to the official publication of notices, appointments, promotions, honors, or legal proceedings in The London Gazette, which establishes their validity as public record under British law. This practice ensures that such announcements carry legal weight, as the Gazette serves as the authorized journal for state notifications, with publication conferring formal recognition that cannot be disputed without evidence to the contrary. For example, military promotions and awards are not effective until gazetted, typically in weekly Ministry of Defence supplements published every Tuesday.
Historically, the concept emerged from the Gazette's role since 1665 as the primary medium for disseminating royal proclamations and official acts, evolving into a requirement for certain events to achieve legal or ceremonial finality. In military contexts, officers' commissions were deemed unofficial until listed in the Gazette, a convention that persisted through conflicts like the Crimean War, where promotions for meritorious service were routinely gazetted, sometimes with delays of months. Similarly, during World War I, the volume of gazetted awards necessitated additional supplements due to high casualty and promotion rates.
Beyond the armed forces, being gazetted applies to civil and commercial matters, such as insolvency proceedings, where bankruptcy orders must be advertised in the Gazette to notify creditors and the public, with "to gazette" explicitly defined as publishing once therein. Peerages, knighthoods, and other honors in lists like the Order of the British Empire are also gazetted, marking their conferral. This uniformity underscores the Gazette's function as a neutral, verifiable archive, though delays in publicationcommon in wartimecould affect practical implementation. The process remains mandatory for specified notices under statutes like the Insolvency Act, ensuring transparency and legal enforceability without reliance on secondary media.

Genealogical and Historical Research Value

The London Gazette serves as a vital resource for genealogical research, particularly in tracing military lineages and aristocratic connections, due to its publication of official notices on commissions, promotions, and honors dating back to 1665. Researchers frequently uncover details such as ancestors' ranks, battle participations, and award citations, which provide verifiable evidence of service and social status otherwise absent from parish records. For instance, notices of New Year and Birthday Honours list recipients of orders like the Order of the British Empire, enabling family historians to link individuals to broader networks of influence and achievement. Bankruptcy and insolvency announcements further aid in reconstructing family estates and creditor relationships, offering insights into economic circumstances and inheritance disputes.
Its digitized archives, freely accessible online since encompassing over 350 years of continuous publication, facilitate targeted searches by name or date, with indexes available from 1829 onward for enhanced precision. This accessibility has proven instrumental in cases where traditional vital records falter, such as verifying foreign naturalizations or peerage successions recorded under Home Office mandates from 1782. Genealogical societies emphasize combining Gazette data with other repositories to corroborate findings, mitigating gaps in earlier, less indexed editions.
Historically, the Gazette functions as an unparalleled primary source for empirical reconstruction of governance, warfare, and societal shifts, embedding causal sequences of events through unaltered official dispatches. It chronicles pivotal occurrences like the Great Plague of 1665 and the Fire of London in 1666 via contemporaneous royal proclamations, providing unfiltered timelines absent in secondary narratives. Military despatches detail troop movements and victories, such as those from the Napoleonic Wars, offering quantitative data on engagements and casualties that underpin causal analyses of strategic outcomes. As the Crown's designated journal of record, its notices on treaties, parliamentary acts, and appointments yield direct evidence of institutional continuity and legal precedents, indispensable for verifying state actions against biased contemporary accounts.

Related Publications

Colonial and Overseas Gazettes

In the British Empire, colonial and overseas territories established government gazettes as official publications analogous to The London Gazette, serving to record and disseminate legislation, executive orders, judicial notices, appointments, and administrative announcements pertinent to local governance. These gazettes functioned as the authoritative legal record, with publication therein conferring official validity on enactments and notices, thereby ensuring transparency and public accessibility in remote administrations where alternative communication channels were limited. Their establishment typically followed the expansion of colonial control, with many commencing in the 19th century as territories formalized under Crown rule, such as the Gold Coast Gazette initiated around 1876 to cover legislative acts and government dispatches in present-day Ghana.
Content in these gazettes mirrored the structure of metropolitan counterparts but adapted to imperial peripheries, including proclamations from governors, ordinances on land tenure, taxation, and public health, as well as notices of bankruptcies, licenses, and military postings. For instance, the Official Gazette of British Guiana, published from the early 19th century onward, detailed colonial laws amending trade regulations and slavery-related measures until independence in 1966, providing a chronological archive of administrative evolution. In African protectorates, gazettes like those for Kenya (from 1899) and Nigeria (from 1887) documented treaties with local rulers, court rulings, and infrastructure developments, often in both English and vernacular languages to extend reach. Overseas domains, including crown colonies in the Caribbean and Pacific, issued similar periodicals; Barbados' Official Gazette, for example, has operated continuously since the 19th century, recording everything from hurricane relief ordinances to gubernatorial appointments.
These publications were produced under the oversight of colonial secretaries or governors, with print runs varying from weekly editions in settled colonies to irregular supplements in frontier areas, and they were archived in the Colonial Office records at The National Archives for imperial oversight. Their role extended beyond mere record-keeping to enforce legal presumptions of knowledgefailure to publish could invalidate measuresthus embedding gazettement as a cornerstone of colonial legality, distinct from but aligned with Westminster's practices. Upon decolonization, many transitioned into national gazettes, retaining their format; for example, Sierra Leone's gazette evolved from colonial origins in 1818 to post-independence continuity, preserving historical continuity in official notification systems. This proliferationspanning over 50 territoriesunderscored the Empire's decentralized administrative model, where local gazettes bridged metropolitan authority with on-ground realities.

Regional UK Counterparts

The Edinburgh Gazette functions as the official public record for Scotland, publishing notices related to Scottish parliamentary proceedings, statutory instruments, legal matters, and public appointments specific to the region. First issued on 29 October 1699 by authority of the Scottish Parliament, it predates the Acts of Union by eight years and has operated continuously as a twice-weekly publication on Tuesdays and Fridays. It records devolved government announcements, including those from the Scottish Government, alongside UK-wide notices applicable to Scotland, and is produced by The Stationery Office on behalf of His Majesty's Stationery Office.
The Belfast Gazette serves a parallel role for Northern Ireland, disseminating official notices concerning the Northern Ireland Assembly, local statutory rules, and regional public administration. It commenced publication on 6 June 1921, shortly after the partition of Ireland under the Government of Ireland Act 1920, and appears weekly on Mondays. Like its counterparts, it handles mandatory public disclosures such as insolvencies, honors, and military awards with Northern Irish relevance, ensuring legal notice to affected parties within the jurisdiction.
Wales lacks a distinct regional gazette; official notices for Welsh devolved matters, including Senedd Cymru legislation and executive actions, are instead published in The London Gazette or through dedicated legislative platforms like legislation.gov.uk. This arrangement reflects Wales's integration within the England and Wales legal framework for many purposes, without the separate gazette tradition established for Scotland and Northern Ireland.
