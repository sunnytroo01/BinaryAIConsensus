Meninges
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The meninges are three thin, protective membranes that surround the brain and spinal cord of the central nervous system, providing mechanical support, cushioning against trauma, and facilitating the circulation of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). These layers, collectively known as the dura mater (outermost), arachnoid mater (middle), and pia mater (innermost), envelop the central nervous system from the skull to the sacral region of the spine, separating it from surrounding bony structures while allowing passage for blood vessels and nerves.
The outermost layer, the dura mater, is a thick, tough, and fibrous connective tissue composed primarily of dense collagen, forming a durable barrier that adheres closely to the inner surface of the skull and vertebral canal. It consists of two sublayersthe periosteal layer, which is adherent to the bone, and the inner meningeal layerthat separate in certain regions to form dural venous sinuses for draining blood from the brain. Beneath the dura lies the arachnoid mater, a delicate, avascular, web-like membrane resembling a cobweb, with thin trabeculae extending to the pia mater and defining the subarachnoid space filled with CSF for shock absorption and nutrient transport. The innermost pia mater is a highly vascularized, transparent layer that closely adheres to the contours of the brain and spinal cord surfaces, supplying blood vessels that penetrate into the neural tissue.
Collectively, the meninges serve multiple critical functions beyond protection, including stabilizing the central nervous system against mechanical stress, supporting the vascular supply to the brain and spinal cord, and enabling the flow of CSF to nourish tissues, remove metabolic waste, and maintain intracranial pressure. The subarachnoid space between the arachnoid and pia layers is particularly vital, housing CSF that acts as a buoyant cushion and lymphatic-like drainage system via meningeal lymphatics. Disruptions to the meninges, such as inflammation (meningitis) or tumors (meningiomas), can compromise these roles, underscoring their essential contribution to neurological health.

Anatomy

Dura mater

The dura mater, the outermost layer of the meninges, is a thick, tough, and durable membrane composed primarily of dense fibrous connective tissue rich in collagen fibers and fibroblasts, providing structural support to the brain and spinal cord. It lacks extensive vascularization within its substance but receives nutrient supply via meningeal arteries that course along its surfaces. In the cranial cavity, the dura mater consists of two distinct layers: the outer periosteal layer, which adheres closely to the inner surface of the skull bones and functions as their periosteum, and the inner meningeal layer, which lies adjacent to the arachnoid mater and is responsible for forming dural reflections and partitions. These two layers are fused except at specific sites where they separate to form dural venous sinuses.
In the spinal region, the dura mater forms a single, continuous layer that extends inferiorly from the foramen magnum to approximately the level of the second sacral vertebra, where it tapers into the filum terminale. Unlike the cranial dura, the spinal dura does not have a separate periosteal layer; instead, it is separated from the vertebral column by the epidural space, a potential compartment filled with loose connective tissue, fat, and an internal vertebral venous plexus that aids in venous drainage. The spinal dura attaches superiorly at the foramen magnum and integrates with the surrounding structures at intervertebral foramina, through which spinal nerves exit, maintaining the integrity of the dural sac.
The meningeal layer of the cranial dura gives rise to several key partitions that divide the intracranial space and stabilize the brain: the falx cerebri, a sickle-shaped fold projecting into the longitudinal fissure between the cerebral hemispheres and attaching superiorly to the crista galli of the ethmoid bone; the tentorium cerebelli, a tent-like structure separating the cerebrum from the cerebellum and attaching along the petrous ridges of the temporal bones and the clinoid processes; the falx cerebelli, a smaller vertical fold in the posterior fossa between the cerebellar hemispheres; and the diaphragma sellae, a horizontal sheet covering the pituitary fossa and attaching to the anterior and posterior clinoid processes of the sphenoid bone. These partitions anchor the dura to the skull at specific bony landmarks, such as the clinoid processes and sphenoidal ridges, enhancing mechanical stability.
Dural venous sinuses are endothelial-lined channels formed by the separation of the periosteal and meningeal layers of the cranial dura, serving as the primary conduits for draining venous blood from the brain and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) absorbed via arachnoid granulations. Prominent examples include the superior sagittal sinus, which runs along the attached superior margin of the falx cerebri and collects blood from cerebral veins; the transverse sinuses, located along the posterior attachment of the tentorium cerebelli and draining laterally into the sigmoid sinuses; and the cavernous sinuses, situated on either side of the pituitary gland and receiving drainage from the ophthalmic veins and sphenoparietal sinus. These sinuses ultimately converge to form the internal jugular veins, facilitating the return of deoxygenated blood and CSF to the systemic circulation.
The blood supply to the dura mater arises from branches of the external and internal carotid arteries as well as the vertebral arteries. In the supratentorial region, the middle meningeal artery, a branch of the maxillary artery, provides the primary arterial supply, entering the cranium through the foramen spinosum and ramifying extensively over the dura. Additional contributions come from the anterior and posterior meningeal arteries (from the ethmoidal and ascending pharyngeal arteries, respectively) and accessory meningeal branches. For the infratentorial dura and spinal regions, supply is derived from branches of the vertebral arteries, such as the posterior meningeal artery, and occipital artery branches.

Arachnoid mater

The arachnoid mater is the middle layer of the three meninges enveloping the brain and spinal cord, forming a thin, delicate, and avascular membrane composed primarily of connective tissue without blood vessels or nerves. It lies immediately beneath the dura mater, to which it adheres loosely without any periosteal attachment, allowing for potential separation. This avascular structure consists of a superficial mesothelial layer and a central layer of tightly conjoined cells linked by junction proteins, providing a translucent barrier that separates the subdural space from the underlying subarachnoid space. The subdural space between the arachnoid mater and dura mater is a potential space containing only minimal serous fluid under normal conditions, facilitating slight gliding movement between the layers.
A key feature of the arachnoid mater are the arachnoid villi and granulations, which are microscopic to macroscopic projections that extend through the dura mater into the dural venous sinuses. These structures serve as the primary sites for the reabsorption of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) from the subarachnoid space into the venous bloodstream, regulating CSF volume and pressure. Arachnoid villi appear as small, finger-like outgrowths, while larger hypertrophied forms are known as arachnoid granulations, often visible as indentations on the inner surface of the skull.
Within the subarachnoid space, the arachnoid mater connects to the pia mater via numerous arachnoid trabeculae, which are delicate, web-like fibrous strands of connective tissue that traverse the CSF-filled compartment. These trabeculae provide structural support, preventing excessive collapse or distension of the space while allowing free circulation of CSF. In the cranial region, the arachnoid mater extends downward to form the walls of the basal cisternsenlarged CSF reservoirs at the brain baseand creates arachnoid sheaths that envelop the roots of cranial nerves as they exit the brain.
The spinal arachnoid mater forms a continuous cylindrical tube that surrounds the spinal cord, extending from the level of the second sacral vertebra (S2) upward to become confluent with the cranial arachnoid mater at the foramen magnum. This tubular extension maintains the subarachnoid space around the spinal cord, filled with CSF for cushioning and nutrient transport. Together with the pia mater, the arachnoid mater constitutes the leptomeninges, the thin inner meningeal complex in direct contact with the central nervous system.

Pia mater

The pia mater is the innermost of the three meningeal layers, consisting of a delicate, highly vascular membrane composed of fibrous tissue and endothelium that tightly adheres to the surfaces of the brain and spinal cord. It conforms closely to the contours of the cerebral gyri and sulci as well as the spinal cord's irregularities, providing direct coverage to the central nervous system without intervening spaces.
Structurally, the pia mater forms a thin, single layer primarily made up of fibroblasts embedded in a collagen-rich matrix, along with extensive networks of capillaries that collectively form the pia-vascular membrane. This composition includes a superficial epipial layer containing collagenous fibers and a capillary plexus, overlaid by an inner intimate layer that directly contacts the neural surface. Unlike the arachnoid mater, the pia lacks trabeculae and is separated from it by the subarachnoid space.
In the spinal region, the pia mater extends laterally as triangular projections known as denticulate ligaments, which anchor the spinal cord to the surrounding dura mater for stability. Perivascular spaces surrounding the penetrating arteries and veins within the pia mater are continuous with the Virchow-Robin spaces that extend into the brain parenchyma.
The pia mater facilitates nutrient supply to the underlying neural tissue by housing branches of the cerebral arteries and their extensive capillary networks, which deliver oxygenated blood directly to the brain and spinal cord surfaces. Along with the arachnoid mater, it constitutes the leptomeninges, a combined protective investment for the central nervous system.

Meningeal spaces and associated structures

The meningeal spaces are the intervals between the layers of the meninges and the surrounding bony structures, playing critical roles in containing cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), supporting vasculature, and providing anatomical barriers. These spaces include the epidural space, subdural space, and subarachnoid space, each with distinct characteristics and contents.
The epidural space lies between the dura mater and the inner surface of the skull or vertebral canal, functioning as a potential space filled with loose connective tissue, adipose tissue, and venous plexuses. In the cranial region, it primarily contains fat and blood vessels, while in the spinal region, it also houses the internal vertebral venous plexuses, known as Batson's plexus, which facilitate venous drainage and can serve as a route for metastatic spread.
Adjacent to the epidural space, the subdural space is a potential space between the dura mater and the arachnoid mater, normally devoid of content but capable of pathological expansion, such as in subdural hematomas where blood accumulates due to trauma or vascular rupture. This space is bounded by the meningeal layer of the dura and the arachnoid, with minimal natural separation in healthy individuals.
The subarachnoid space represents a true anatomical compartment between the arachnoid mater and pia mater, continuously filled with CSF that cushions the central nervous system. It contains delicate arachnoid trabeculaethin connective tissue strands connecting the arachnoid to the piaas well as cranial and spinal nerve roots and major blood vessels, including arteries and veins that supply the brain and spinal cord.
The arachnoid mater and pia mater together form the leptomeninges, a collective term for these delicate inner meningeal layers that create a permeable barrier enclosing the subarachnoid space and interfacing directly with the CSF and neural tissues. Unlike the tough dura mater, the leptomeninges are thin and avascular in parts, allowing for nutrient exchange and CSF circulation while providing a conduit for pathological processes like leptomeningeal metastases.
Within the subarachnoid space, the subarachnoid lymphatic-like membrane (SLYM), a recently identified fourth meningeal layer, forms a continuous mesothelial-like sheet that encases the cortical vasculature and separates the perivascular spaces from the broader subarachnoid compartment. Discovered through advanced imaging in mouse and human brains, SLYM expresses lymphatic markers such as Prox1 and podoplanin, facilitating glymphatic clearance by directing CSF flow around vessels and potentially aiding in waste removal from the brain parenchyma.
Enlarged regions of the subarachnoid space, known as cisterns, occur where the arachnoid and pia separate more widely, accommodating critical neurovascular structures. Notable examples include the cisterna magna at the posterior cranial fossa, which stores a large volume of CSF near the cerebellum and contains the vertebral arteries, and the chiasmatic cistern anterior to the brainstem, housing the optic chiasm and circle of Willis. These cisterns vary in size and location but consistently provide space for major vessels, nerves, and CSF reservoirs essential for surgical access and imaging.

Functions

Protective roles

The meninges serve as a multi-layered protective sheath for the central nervous system (CNS), absorbing mechanical shocks through the tensile strength of the dura mater and the hydraulic buffering provided by cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) in the subarachnoid space between the arachnoid and pia mater. This structure reduces the brain's effective weight from approximately 1,500 grams to 50 grams via buoyancy, minimizing stress on neural tissues and blood vessels during impacts. By adhering to the inner skull surface, the dura mater prevents direct brain-skull contact, thereby reducing friction and shear forces during head movements.
In addition to mechanical cushioning, the meninges form critical barriers that shield the CNS from pathogens and toxic substances. The arachnoid mater forms a barrier with tight junctions that limits the entry of harmful substances into the CSF, complementing the primary blood-CSF barrier at the choroid plexus. Leptomeningeal (pial) blood vessels exhibit blood-brain barrier properties with tight junctions. These barriers maintain a controlled microenvironment, preventing unregulated molecular exchange between blood and CSF spaces.
The meninges also facilitate immune surveillance, with resident macrophages and dendritic cells in the leptomeninges actively patrolling for pathogens and initiating defense responses. In the dura mater, mast cells enable rapid inflammatory reactions to potential threats, enhancing overall CNS protection without compromising parenchymal integrity.
Furthermore, the meningeal layers contribute to thermal insulation, buffering the brain against external temperature fluctuations through their composite thermal properties in conjunction with the skull and scalp. This regulation helps maintain stable neural function amid environmental variations.

Fluid and vascular dynamics

The subarachnoid space functions as the primary reservoir and circulation pathway for cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), which is continuously produced by the choroid plexus at a rate matching its absorption to maintain homeostasis. CSF flows from the ventricular system into the subarachnoid space, surrounding the brain and spinal cord to provide buoyancy and nutrient distribution. Absorption predominantly occurs through arachnoid granulations, specialized protrusions of the arachnoid mater that extend into the dural venous sinuses, facilitating bulk flow of CSF into the venous bloodstream via a pressure-dependent gradient. In adults, this absorption rate is approximately 0.30.4 mL/min, ensuring a total daily turnover of about 500 mL while the intracranial CSF volume remains stable at 90150 mL.
The vascular supply to the meninges supports their structural integrity and metabolic needs, primarily through arteries embedded in the dura mater. The middle meningeal artery, the largest dural branch, arises from the maxillary artery (a terminal branch of the external carotid artery) and supplies the dura overlying the cerebral convexities, calvaria, and adjacent bone. In the posterior fossa, the anterior and posterior meningeal arteries originate from the extracranial segment of the vertebral artery, providing blood to the dura of the posterior cranial fossa and clivus. Venous drainage occurs via dural sinuses, endothelial-lined channels within the dura that collect blood from cerebral veins, meningeal veins, and diploic veins before emptying into the internal jugular veins.
The glymphatic system, integrated with meningeal structures, enables dynamic exchange between CSF and brain interstitial fluid (ISF) for waste clearance. CSF enters perivascular spaces along arteries within the brain parenchyma, mixes with ISF driven by aquaporin-4 channels on astrocytic endfeet, and facilitates convective flow that removes solutes including beta-amyloid and tau proteinskey pathological aggregates in neurodegenerative diseases. This influx and efflux are supported by meningeal lymphatics and the subarachnoid lymphatic-like membrane (SLYM), a mesothelial barrier that divides the subarachnoid space and regulates fluid partitioning while permitting selective transport. Glymphatic clearance is markedly enhanced during sleep, when noradrenergic tone decreases and interstitial space volume expands by up to 60%, optimizing removal of neurotoxic proteins.
Meningeal lymphatic drainage provides an additional route for CSF and ISF efflux beyond arachnoid granulations. Conventional lymphatic vessels embedded in the dura mater, particularly along the superior sagittal sinus and base of the skull, absorb CSF-derived antigens, immune cells, and macromolecules, directing them to deep cervical lymph nodes for immune surveillance. These vessels were rediscovered in 2015 through intravital imaging in mice, revealing their direct continuity with extracranial lymphatics and functional role in brain fluid homeostasis.
The meninges contribute to intracranial pressure (ICP) regulation by creating compliant spaces that buffer volume changes in the brain-CSF-blood compartment. The subarachnoid space, filled with CSF, acts as a hydraulic cushion, allowing redistribution of fluid to accommodate fluctuations in cerebral blood volume or tissue mass while adhering to the Monro-Kellie doctrine. Normal adult ICP ranges from 7 to 15 mmHg in the supine position, maintained through balanced CSF production, circulation, and absorption, with meningeal compliance preventing rapid pressure elevations.

Development and histology

Embryonic origins

The meninges originate from mesenchymal tissues during early embryogenesis. The dura mater primarily derives from the paraxial and somatic lateral plate mesoderm, forming as the outermost layer surrounding the developing neural tube. In contrast, the arachnoid and pia mater, collectively known as the leptomeninges, arise from a combination of neural crest cells and mesoderm, beginning around 3-4 weeks of gestation. These origins reflect the meninges' role as a protective interface between the central nervous system and surrounding structures, with the primary meninxa mesenchymal sheathestablishing over the brain by the 5th week (Carnegie stage 15).
Recent single-cell RNA sequencing analyses of human fetal meninges from post-conception weeks 5 to 13 have revealed extensive cellular diversity, identifying 245 clusters across 8 superclasses, with fibroblasts (marked by FOXC1) comprising approximately half of the cells. These studies indicate that the meningeal layers develop concurrently through gene expression gradients rather than strictly sequentially, maturing by post-conception week 13. Meningeal precursors are induced shortly after neurulation, with the primary meninx forming around week 5. The leptomeninges become identifiable between weeks 6-7 (Carnegie stages 17-18), as the primary meninx differentiates into avascular arachnoid and vascularized pia layers. Neural crest cells migrate extensively during this period, contributing to pial melanocytes, which provide pigmentation, and to meningeal vasculature through pericytes and mural cells that support angiogenesis. This migration parallels central nervous system folding, ensuring coordinated envelopment of the expanding brain and spinal cord.
Meningeal development also influences adjacent structures, particularly skull ossification. The dura acts as a signaling center, releasing factors that guide calvarial bone formation at ossification centers, preventing premature fusion while promoting patterned growth. Disruptions in these processes lead to congenital anomalies. For instance, neural tube defects such as meningocele and myelomeningocele involve herniation of meninges through vertebral gaps, often due to failed closure around weeks 3-4. Rare conditions like acalvaria feature agenesis of the dura mater alongside absent calvarial bones, linked to abnormal ossification and sometimes craniosynostosis through disrupted dural signaling. Additionally, persistence of fetal venous structures, such as the falcine sinus, represents a remnant of embryonic drainage patterns that fails to regress postnatally.

Microscopic structure

The dura mater exhibits a microscopic structure characterized by dense irregular connective tissue, dominated by fibroblasts embedded within an extracellular matrix rich in type I collagen and elastin fibers. The outer periosteal layer includes osteoprogenitor cells and elongated fibroblasts oriented parallel to the skull's inner surface, while the inner meningeal layer features a CDH1+ sublayer expressing tight junction genes that contributes to barrier function and is lined by endothelium in the walls of dural venous sinuses, facilitating vascular passage. In standard hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining, the dura appears prominently eosinophilic owing to its compact collagenous composition.
The arachnoid mater comprises avascular fibroelastic connective tissue, covered externally by a layer of cuboidal epithelial-like arachnoid cap cells that form tight junctions via proteins such as VE-cadherin, establishing a selective barrier. Arachnoid trabeculae, delicate fibrous strands bridging to the pia mater, consist primarily of type III collagen supported by scattered fibroblasts.
The pia mater forms a delicate, thin investment of fibroblasts and endothelial cells that closely follows the brain's contours, incorporating a dense network of fenestrated capillaries to enable nutrient and gas diffusion into the underlying neural tissue. Immunohistochemical staining, such as for PDGFR-, highlights vascular pericytes associated with these pial capillaries, contributing to vessel stability and blood-brain barrier integrity.
Collectively, the leptomeninges (arachnoid and pia) are underpinned by a shared basement membrane composed of laminin, type IV collagen, nidogen, and proteoglycans, which anchors the layers to the brain parenchyma. Perivascular spaces within the leptomeninges harbor resident immune cells, including macrophages and T-lymphocytes, involved in local surveillance and response.
Arachnoid granulations, protrusions of the arachnoid into dural sinuses, feature a central core of collagen fibers encapsulated by arachnoid cells, with slit-like channels functioning as one-way valves to permit cerebrospinal fluid resorption while preventing reflux.

Clinical significance

Infections and inflammation

Infections and inflammation of the meninges, collectively termed meningitis, involve the inflammatory response of the leptomeninges to microbial invasion or non-infectious triggers, leading to significant morbidity if untreated. Bacterial meningitis, caused by pathogens such as Neisseria meningitidis and Streptococcus pneumoniae, is a medical emergency characterized by rapid progression and high mortality rates without prompt intervention. Viral meningitis, often due to enteroviruses, typically follows a milder, self-limited course, while fungal infections like those from Cryptococcus species predominantly affect immunocompromised individuals.
Common symptoms across these conditions include fever, severe headache, and nuchal rigidity, with classic meningeal signs such as Kernig's sign (pain on knee extension with hip flexed) and Brudzinski's sign (involuntary hip flexion on neck flexion) indicating irritation of the meninges. In bacterial cases, patients may also exhibit altered mental status, photophobia, and seizures due to increased intracranial pressure.
The pathophysiology begins with pathogens breaching the blood-CSF barrier, often via hematogenous spread or direct extension, triggering leptomeningeal inflammation through cytokine release and immune cell recruitment. This results in cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) pleocytosis, with bacterial meningitis typically showing >1000 white blood cells/L, predominantly neutrophils, alongside elevated protein and decreased glucose levels. In viral meningitis, pleocytosis is milder (<1000 cells/L) with lymphocytic predominance.
Aseptic meningitis, distinguished by negative bacterial cultures in CSF, arises from non-infectious causes such as autoimmune disorders like systemic lupus erythematosus or drug-induced reactions, particularly to nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as ibuprofen. These cases often present with a subacute onset and milder symptoms compared to bacterial forms, resolving with removal of the offending agent or immunosuppressive therapy.
Specific variants include tuberculous meningitis, caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis, which features thick basal exudates in the subarachnoid space that can obstruct CSF flow and lead to chronic inflammation. In neonates, Group B Streptococcus (Streptococcus agalactiae) is a leading cause, often acquired during birth and presenting with sepsis alongside meningeal involvement.
Diagnosis relies on lumbar puncture for CSF analysis, including cell count, glucose, protein, and Gram stain, which identifies bacteria in 60-90% of untreated bacterial cases. Imaging with computed tomography (CT) or magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is performed prior to lumbar puncture in select patients to rule out mass lesions and assess complications like hydrocephalus.
Treatment varies by etiology and aligns with the World Health Organization's 2025 guidelines on meningitis diagnosis, treatment, and care: bacterial meningitis requires immediate empirical intravenous antibiotics, such as ceftriaxone (2 g every 12 hours for adults), often combined with vancomycin to cover resistant strains, alongside adjunctive corticosteroids like dexamethasone to mitigate inflammation. Viral meningitis is managed supportively, with antivirals (e.g., acyclovir for herpes simplex) reserved for specific agents; fungal cases, such as cryptococcal, demand prolonged antifungal therapy with amphotericin B and flucytosine. Early intervention improves outcomes, emphasizing rapid diagnostic confirmation and targeted therapy.

Trauma, hemorrhage, and neoplasms

Traumatic injuries to the meninges often result in hematomas that accumulate blood in the meningeal spaces, leading to increased intracranial pressure and potential neurological deficits. Epidural hematomas typically arise from arterial bleeding, most commonly due to rupture of the middle meningeal artery following a temporal bone fracture in high-impact head trauma. These collections appear as lens-shaped hyperdensities on computed tomography (CT) scans, confined by the adherent dura mater. In contrast, subdural hematomas stem from venous bleeding, usually involving tearing of bridging veins that traverse the subdural space between the dura and arachnoid mater. They present as crescentic hyperdensities spanning the convexity of the brain on CT imaging, with acute forms developing rapidly after trauma and chronic forms evolving over weeks. Chronic subdural hematomas are particularly prevalent in the elderly due to age-related brain atrophy, which enlarges the subdural space and stretches the bridging veins, increasing their vulnerability to minor trauma or even spontaneous rupture.
Subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH) involves bleeding into the subarachnoid space, often originating from ruptured aneurysms at the circle of Willis, such as those in the anterior or posterior communicating arteries. This condition classically presents with a sudden, severe "thunderclap" headache, often accompanied by nausea, vomiting, and altered consciousness. The Fisher grading scale assesses the volume and distribution of subarachnoid blood on initial CT scans to predict the risk of complications, with higher grades (e.g., grade 3 or 4, indicating thick blood layers or intraventricular extension) correlating with increased likelihood of vasospasm. A major complication of aneurysmal SAH is cerebral vasospasm, which peaks in incidence between days 4 and 10 post-hemorrhage and can lead to delayed cerebral ischemia if untreated.
Neoplasms of the meninges primarily include meningiomas, which are typically benign tumors arising from arachnoid cap cells in the dura mater, accounting for about 30-40% of primary intracranial tumors. Most meningiomas are classified as World Health Organization (WHO) grade 1, exhibiting slow growth and low recurrence rates after resection, with common histological subtypes including meningothelial (syncytial pattern) and fibrous (spindle-shaped cells). They frequently occur along the cerebral convexity or parasagittal regions, causing symptoms through mass effect and compression of adjacent brain tissue, such as headaches, seizures, or focal neurological deficits depending on location. Other meningeal neoplasms include hemangiopericytomas (now reclassified under solitary fibrous tumors in WHO grading), which originate from pericytes surrounding meningeal capillaries and exhibit aggressive behavior with high rates of local recurrence (up to 70%) and distant metastasis. Leptomeningeal metastases, involving neoplastic spread to the pia and arachnoid mater, commonly arise from primary cancers such as breast or lung carcinoma, leading to multifocal leptomeningeal enhancement on MRI and symptoms like cranial nerve palsies or hydrocephalus.
Management of meningeal trauma and hemorrhage prioritizes rapid intervention to prevent herniation. Epidural and symptomatic subdural hematomas generally require surgical evacuation via craniotomy to remove the clot and control bleeding, with smaller asymptomatic lesions sometimes managed conservatively with serial imaging. For aneurysmal SAH, securing the aneurysm through endovascular coiling or microsurgical clipping is essential to prevent rebleeding, with coiling preferred for posteriorly located aneurysms due to lower procedural morbidity in select cases. Vasospasm in SAH is treated with induced hypertension, angioplasty, or intra-arterial vasodilators. Meningiomas are managed with surgical resection via craniotomy when symptomatic or growing, aiming for Simpson grade 1 removal (complete excision including dura) to minimize recurrence, while observation with serial MRI suffices for small, asymptomatic lesions. Aggressive neoplasms like hemangiopericytomas may require multimodality therapy, including gross total resection followed by adjuvant radiotherapy, though recurrence remains common. Leptomeningeal metastases are palliated with intrathecal chemotherapy, targeted systemic therapies (e.g., for HER2-positive breast cancer), and radiation, focusing on symptom control given the poor prognosis.

Comparative anatomy

In non-human vertebrates

In fish, the meninges are typically represented by a single primitive layer, often termed the meninx primitiva, which is thin, largely avascular, and lacks distinct differentiation into multiple layers such as a separate dura mater; the subarachnoid space is minimal, and cerebrospinal fluid is held between this layer and the brain surface. This simple structure aligns with the relatively uncomplicated brain organization in these basal vertebrates. In sharks, as cartilaginous fish, the meningeal covering similarly consists of a single undifferentiated membrane enclosing the central nervous system.
Amphibians and reptiles possess a two-layered meningeal system, with an outer tough pachymeninx (homologous to the mammalian dura mater) and an inner delicate leptomeninx combining features of the arachnoid and pia; recent histological studies suggest variation, with some reptiles exhibiting three distinct layers (pia mater, arachnoid mater, and dura mater) of varying distinctiveness. The choroid plexus for cerebrospinal fluid production is primarily located within the brain ventricles rather than extending into a prominent subarachnoid space. In reptiles such as crocodiles, the dura mater is connected to suboccipital muscles via a myodural bridge, facilitating biomechanical interactions between the neck musculature and spinal meninges.
Birds exhibit three distinct meningeal layers akin to those in mammals: an outer dura mater, middle arachnoid, and inner pia mater, though the overall arrangement is adapted to the avian skull's pneumatic structure, with dural extensions potentially interacting with air-filled sinuses for lightweight cranial support. The pia mater in birds is notably vascularized, contributing to efficient nutrient delivery and waste removal in the compact, high-metabolic brain.
Non-human mammals display meningeal structures closely resembling those in humans, with three layers providing robust protection, vascular support, and cerebrospinal fluid dynamics; however, variations occur, such as the presence of lymphatic vessels in the dura of nonhuman primates, enabling immune surveillance and fluid drainage similar to human counterparts. In large cetaceans like whales, the spinal dura is proportionally thicker to accommodate the elongated spinal cord and mechanical stresses of aquatic locomotion, while rodents often lack extensive dural venous sinuses, relying more on alternative cranial venous pathways. Across non-mammalian vertebrates, the simpler meningeal layering generally results in reduced reliance on cerebrospinal fluid for mechanical cushioning compared to the more elaborate systems in mammals.

Evolutionary perspectives

The meninges emerged as a key vertebrate innovation, derived primarily from neural crest cells that surround the developing neural tube, marking a departure from the simpler epithelial coverings found in invertebrate chordates. This origin coincides with the appearance of early vertebrates around 500 million years ago during the Cambrian period, when the neural crest evolved as a unique source of migratory cells contributing to protective structures around the central nervous system. In protochordates like amphioxus, lacking true meninges, basic mesenchymal sheaths provided rudimentary support, but the transition to vertebrates introduced a primary meninx that differentiated into specialized layers for enhanced protection and vascular integration.
As jawed vertebrates (gnathostomes) arose approximately 420 million years ago in the Silurian period, meningeal structure evolved from a single primitive meninx in agnathans and early fish to multi-layered configurations, correlating with expanding brain size and complexity. The leptomeninges (pia and arachnoid) emerged alongside neural crest contributions in early vertebrates, facilitating cerebrospinal fluid dynamics and nutrient exchange, while the dura mater developed as a robust outer barrier. In amniotes, which appeared around 310 million years ago, the dura's association with ossifying skull elements provided additional mechanical reinforcement, linking meningeal evolution to the terrestrial adaptations of tetrapods.
Key evolutionary events include the development of dural venous sinuses in therapsids, mammal-like reptiles from about 300 million years ago, which improved cerebral venous drainage essential for the higher metabolic demands of endothermy. Comparative studies reveal variations such as the absence or reduction of meningeal lymphatics in some fish lineages, contrasting with their expansion in mammals to support the glymphatic system, which clears waste from larger, more complex brains. Additionally, meningeal melanocytes derived from the neural crest appear in reptiles, contributing to pigmentation that may aid in visual camouflage or thermoregulation, though thicker meningeal layers across phylogeny present a trade-off by bolstering physical protection while potentially elevating susceptibility to infections like meningitis.
The high degree of conservation in meningeal structure and function across mammals underscores their role in supporting cognitive evolution, from enhanced neurovascular coupling to immune surveillance, adaptations that likely facilitated the diversification of mammalian brain architectures.
