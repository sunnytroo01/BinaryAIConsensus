 Institutional Shareholder Services Inc. (ISS) is a Rockville, Maryland-based firm founded in 1985 that serves as the world's leading provider of corporate governance research and proxy voting recommendations to institutional investors, including pension funds, mutual funds, and asset managers.
As part of the ISS STOXX group of companies, with principal ownership by Deutsche Brse AG alongside minority stakes held by Genstar Capital and ISS management, the firm delivers services encompassing proxy analysis, ESG data and ratings, and stewardship solutions aimed at promoting long-term value creation through informed shareholder engagement.
ISS's recommendations, which cover executive pay, board composition, and shareholder proposals, exert substantial influence on corporate voting outcomes due to the scale of clients it serves and the deference many investors pay to its guidance, with studies indicating high sensitivity among institutions to shifts in its positions.
The company has encountered notable controversies, including regulatory challenges over its classification as a proxy solicitor under SEC rules and state-level investigations into potential misleading practices and conflicts of interest stemming from its concurrent roles in advisory and consulting services, amid criticisms that it advances ESG priorities potentially at the expense of financial returns.

History

Founding and Early Development (19852000)

Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) was founded in 1985 by Robert A. G. Monks in Washington, D.C., with initial support from the Investor Responsibility Research Center, to assist institutional investors in fulfilling their fiduciary duties through informed proxy voting and corporate governance research. The firm's establishment aligned with the formation of the Council of Institutional Investors, marking the emergence of organized efforts to enhance shareholder influence amid growing scrutiny of corporate accountability.
In its early years, ISS provided proxy research and voting recommendations to pension funds, mutual funds, and other institutional clients, focusing on board elections, executive compensation, and shareholder proposals to promote long-term value creation. The 1988 U.S. Department of Labor "Avon Letter" played a pivotal role by affirming proxy voting as a core fiduciary responsibility, which increased demand for ISS's services and solidified its position in the nascent proxy advisory industry.
Throughout the 1990s, ISS expanded its influence by advocating for governance reforms, contributing to shareholder activism at major corporations such as American Express and Exxon, and benefiting from Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) initiatives under Chairman Richard Breeden that strengthened proxy access and voting rights. Key developments included the 1996 partnership between CalPERS and Relational Investors, which built on concepts of activist investing advanced by Monks and early collaborator Nell Minow through vehicles like the Lens Investment Management fund. By 2000, ISS had grown into the dominant provider of proxy advisory services globally, serving as a centralized resource for institutional shareholders navigating complex voting decisions.

Expansion and Ownership Changes (20012015)

In November 2006, RiskMetrics Group announced its acquisition of ISS for an undisclosed amount, with the transaction completed in January 2007, marking a significant ownership shift from prior stakeholders including Hermes Pensions Management and Warburg Pincus. This merger integrated ISS's proxy advisory expertise with RiskMetrics' risk analytics platform, enabling expanded offerings in governance risk assessment for institutional clients.
The acquisition facilitated ISS's growth in global operations, as the firm leveraged synergies to enhance its proxy research and voting recommendations amid post-Enron regulatory scrutiny on corporate governance. By the late 2000s, ISS had broadened its international footprint, providing services across multiple markets while maintaining its core focus on U.S. equities.
In June 2010, MSCI Inc. acquired RiskMetrics Groupincluding ISSfor $1.55 billion, incorporating ISS into MSCI's broader index and analytics ecosystem. This ownership change positioned ISS to integrate governance data with investment decision tools, though it later faced divestiture pressures as MSCI streamlined its portfolio.
By 2013, MSCI announced plans to sell ISS as a non-core asset, culminating in a July 2013 definitive agreement with Vestar Capital Partners for $364 million, completed on April 30, 2014. The sale to Vestar, a private equity firm, restored ISS's operational independence, with approximately 700 employees across 15 offices at the time, emphasizing proxy advisory and governance solutions. This transition supported further expansion in customized analytics amid growing institutional demand for voting support.

Acquisitions and Global Growth (2016Present)

In September 2017, Genstar Capital, in partnership with ISS management, acquired the company from Vestar Capital Partners for $720 million, providing capital for further expansion into ESG data and analytics services.
ISS pursued targeted acquisitions to bolster its research and data capabilities. In August 2016, it acquired iiWisdom's Interactive Governance Portal to enhance client access to governance analytics. In January 2017, ISS bought IW Financial, adding over 15 years of expertise in ESG ratings and custom research solutions to meet rising demand from investment managers. In January 2019, the firm acquired Strategic Insight, an investment data provider and publisher of Chief Investment Officer magazine, expanding its market intelligence offerings.
A pivotal shift occurred in November 2020 when Deutsche Brse Group announced the acquisition of an 81% majority stake in ISS for approximately 2.1 billion, with the transaction closing in February 2021 in partnership with Genstar and management; this valued ISS at around 2.6 billion enterprise value and positioned it for deeper integration into European financial markets. The ownership change facilitated synergies in ESG data and index products, with ISS contributing to Deutsche Brse's data and analytics segment, which saw net revenue growth of 22% in Q2 2024 partly driven by ISS operations.
Post-acquisition, ISS expanded globally through product integration and workforce scaling. In November 2023, ISS combined with STOXXanother Deutsche Brse assetto form ISS STOXX, creating a unified entity with over 3,400 professionals across 33 locations worldwide and enhancing ESG benchmarking and index services for institutional clients in Europe, Asia-Pacific, and beyond. This structure supported revenue acceleration in sustainable investing data, positioning ISS as a top-three global ESG provider amid 20% annual market growth in the sector.

Business Model and Operations

Core Services and Revenue Streams

Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) delivers core services centered on proxy voting and corporate governance research for institutional investors. Its flagship offering involves producing detailed proxy analyses and voting recommendations on agenda items such as board elections, executive compensation, shareholder proposals, and mergers, covering approximately 42,000 meetings and over 200,000 agenda items annually across 115 jurisdictions. These recommendations are generated using proprietary methodologies that evaluate governance practices, financial performance, and ownership structures, enabling clients to fulfill fiduciary duties in proxy voting. ISS also operates voting execution platforms like ProxyExchange, which facilitate secure, efficient ballot submission and reporting, with capabilities for handling over 1 million ballots per year.
Beyond proxy advisory, ISS provides governance analytics and benchmarking tools, including QualityScore, a quantitative assessment of corporate governance risks across pillars like board structure, shareholder rights, and compensation. The firm offers customized policy development, where clients can tailor voting guidelines to specific stewardship objectives, supported by scenario modeling and compliance reporting. For corporate clients, ISS Corporate Solutions delivers advisory on executive pay design, sustainability reporting, and governance program management, helping issuers benchmark against peers and engage with investors. Responsible investment services integrate ESG data into proxy research, with tools for evaluating sustainability disclosures and climate-related risks.
ISS generates revenue primarily through subscription-based access to its proxy research and voting platforms, charged to institutional investors based on factors such as assets under management or volume of meetings covered. Additional streams include licensing fees for analytics data and tools like QualityScore, as well as consulting fees from corporate advisory engagements. The firm serves more than 1,700 institutional clients worldwide, including major asset managers, with significant revenue contributions from entities like BlackRock and Vanguard Group. In 2020, ISS reported expected net revenues exceeding $280 million, reflecting growth in governance and ESG demand.

Client Base and Market Position

Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) primarily serves institutional investors seeking guidance on proxy voting, corporate governance research, and responsible investment solutions, including pension funds, mutual funds, asset managers, hedge funds, and public employee retirement systems. These clients, often managing trillions in assets, utilize ISS for objective recommendations to inform voting decisions at shareholder meetings.
ISS reports approximately 4,200 to 5,500 clients globally, encompassing leading institutional investors that depend on its impartial analytics and data services. In 2024, the firm supported around 2,000 clients in executing proxy votes across more than 51,500 shareholder meetings worldwide.
As the preeminent proxy advisory firm, ISS maintains a leading market position, holding an estimated 48% share of the U.S. proxy advice market for mutual funds as of 2021, advising on $26.8 trillion in assets from 144 fund families. Its closest competitor, Glass Lewis, commands about 42%, with the two firms collectively dominating over 90%and in some estimates up to 97%of the industry, forming a near-duopoly that influences the majority of institutional shareholder votes. This concentration has drawn scrutiny for potential impacts on corporate governance practices, though ISS's scale stems from its extensive research capabilities and established reputation since 1985. Smaller rivals, such as Egan-Jones, hold negligible shares.

Methodology for Proxy Recommendations

Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) generates proxy recommendations through a structured application of its benchmark voting policies, which are developed annually by a policy team drawing on empirical governance research, peer company benchmarks, regulatory developments, and feedback from institutional investor clients. These policies outline criteria for evaluating proposals across categories such as board elections, executive compensation, shareholder rights, and capital allocation, with recommendations typically favoring "for," "against," or "abstain" based on alignment with predefined governance standards. For instance, in board-related votes, ISS assesses director independence, tenure, and responsiveness to shareholder concerns using quantitative thresholds like a minimum of two-thirds independent directors for controlled companies.
The research process for individual recommendations involves analysts reviewing company proxy statements, SEC filings, and other disclosures to apply policy benchmarks on a case-by-case basis, incorporating both quantitative metricssuch as relative total shareholder return (TSR) for pay-for-performance evaluationsand qualitative factors like clawback provisions or peer group selection in compensation plans. In executive pay analyses, ISS employs models comparing realized pay to performance over multiple years, flagging concerns if payouts exceed benchmarks without justification tied to financial results. Policies are market-specific, with U.S. guidelines emphasizing pay alignment and board diversity metrics updated as of January 9, 2025, to reflect evolving practices like skills-based assessments over rigid diversity quotas.
ISS incorporates investor customization options, allowing clients to tailor policies via its Proxy Exchange platform, but benchmark recommendations adhere to default criteria designed for broad applicability, with transparency provided through published rationale in research reports accessible to companies post-publication. Updates to methodologies, such as 2025 enhancements to auditor independence evaluations focusing on non-audit fees exceeding 50% of total fees, stem from annual reviews incorporating academic studies on governance impacts and client surveys, though critics contend the process prioritizes certain ESG-integrated views over pure financial proxies.

Governance and Policy Framework

Proxy Voting Guidelines

Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) maintains benchmark proxy voting guidelines that deliver standardized recommendations to institutional investors on voting matters at shareholder meetings, including director elections, executive compensation approvals, and governance proposals. These guidelines form the foundation for ISS's proxy research reports and are designed to promote accountability, stewardship, independence, and transparency in corporate governance. Regional variations exist, with the U.S. policies serving as a primary example, covering analyses for U.S.-incorporated companies' meetings.
The guidelines undergo annual reviews, incorporating empirical data on governance outcomes, regulatory shifts such as SEC clawback rules, and input from investor consultations conducted via public comment periods. For the 2025 policy year, updates were announced on December 17, 2024, and apply to meetings held on or after February 1, 2025, with a February 25, 2025, amendment suspending the use of gender and racial/ethnic diversity metrics in director election recommendations due to reassessment of their application. This process ensures policies adapt to market practices, though critics have questioned the empirical basis for certain criteria amid broader scrutiny of proxy advisors' influence.
Core policy areas emphasize risk-adjusted governance aligned with long-term shareholder value:

Board of Directors: Votes case-by-case on elections, recommending against nominees if board independence falls below majority standards for the full board or key committees like audit or compensation; against directors with attendance below 75% at meetings; and against overcommitted directors (more than five public company boards generally, or CEOs on more than two external boards). Accountability factors include voting against chairs or committee members for unaddressed governance failures, such as adoption of stockholder-unapproved poison pills or maintenance of multi-class capital structures with disparate voting rights.

Executive Compensation: Case-by-case assessments of advisory "say-on-pay" votes, recommending against if prior shareholder support dipped below 70% without sufficient remediation, or if pay structures exhibit misalignment with performance metrics, including problematic practices like underwater option repricing without shareholder approval, multiyear guaranteed bonuses, or inadequate clawback provisions. Frequency defaults to annual say-on-pay votes unless a compelling rationale supports less frequent cycles.

Shareholder Rights and Defenses: Support for bylaws enabling shareholder action by written consent (where practicable) and special meetings at a 10-15% ownership threshold; case-by-case review of advance notice provisions, favoring 90-120 day windows; opposition to non-shareholder-approved poison pills or those exceeding one year without renewal; and against supermajority voting requirements for amendments unless justified by specific risks.

Audit and Risk Oversight: Generally recommend approval of auditor ratification absent red flags like non-audit fees exceeding audit fees or indications of compromised independence.

Capital and Restructuring: Case-by-case evaluations of stock issuances, authorizations, or recapitalizations, opposing excessive dilution (e.g., over 10% for non-financing purposes) or issuances lacking clear strategic need.

Institutional clients can override benchmark recommendations by customizing policies through ISS platforms like ProxyExchange, which facilitate tailored voting execution while maintaining a secure, efficient process. Benchmark policies also extend to international markets with adjustments for local regulations, such as European emphasis on one-share-one-vote structures.

Integration of ESG Factors

Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) incorporates environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors into its proxy voting recommendations primarily to assess risks to long-term shareholder value, evaluating company disclosures, board oversight, and alignment with international standards such as the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD) and the UN Principles for Responsible Investment (UNPRI). This integration occurs through case-by-case analyses in benchmark voting policies, where ESG considerations influence stances on director elections, executive compensation, and shareholder proposals, with guidelines updated annually to reflect evolving market practices and regulatory developments.
On environmental issues, ISS emphasizes climate accountability, recommending votes against the chairs of relevant board committees at significant greenhouse gas (GHG) emittersdefined by lists like the Climate Action 100+ Focus Groupif companies fail to provide TCFD-aligned disclosures on governance, strategy, risk management, metrics, and targets, or lack medium-term or net-zero-by-2050 reduction plans covering Scope 1 and 2 emissions. Proposals for sustainability reports, GHG emission reductions, or renewable energy investments receive case-by-case support based on disclosure quality, alignment with frameworks like the Paris Agreement, and potential controversies or peer benchmarks, aiming to mitigate transition risks without mandating specific outcomes unless material to value.
Social factors are integrated via evaluations of diversity, human rights, and labor practices; for instance, ISS votes against nominating committee chairs at Russell 3000 or S&P 1500 companies lacking women on the board (with exceptions for commitments to add within a year), while racial and ethnic diversity considerations were suspended effective February 25, 2025, pending further data. Human rights proposals, including reports on labor standards or supply chain risks, are assessed case-by-case, favoring disclosure and adherence to global norms unless companies demonstrate adequate performance and low controversy risk, with linkages to executive pay encouraged where social metrics demonstrably tie to financial outcomes.
Governance integration focuses on board composition, independence, and oversight, recommending against directors failing to address ESG-related risks, such as through insufficient transparency on political contributions or tax strategies, and supporting pay structures that incorporate ESG criteria only if aligned with performance metrics like return on equity over multi-year periods. ISS supplements these with tools like the Environmental & Social Disclosure QualityScore and Governance QualityScore, which benchmark company practices against peers but do not automatically dictate vote recommendations, instead informing customized analyses for clients. Overall, ESG assessments prioritize empirical evidence of risk materiality over prescriptive mandates, drawing on company-specific data, stakeholder engagement, and quantitative benchmarks to avoid overreach into non-financial activism.

Influence and Impact

Effects on Shareholder Voting Outcomes

Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) recommendations exert considerable influence on shareholder voting outcomes, as evidenced by multiple empirical analyses. Studies indicate that a negative ISS recommendation typically reduces shareholder support for proposals by 6 to 13 percentage points, with the effect varying by proposal type and investor class. This impact is particularly pronounced among institutional investors, including those managing passive index funds, who control the majority of shares, often delegate proxy voting, and align their votes with proxy advisors like ISS; for instance, ISS opposition has been associated with a 51 percentage point difference in institutional voting support compared to a mere 2 percentage point difference among retail investors.
In the context of say-on-pay votes, a 2016 analysis found that an adverse ISS recommendation leads to an average 25 percentage point drop in approval rates. For uncontested director elections, ISS against votes correlate with significantly lower tally percentages, often shifting outcomes from majority approval to failure thresholds under plurality rules. Broader examinations confirm that ISS advice shifts overall shareholder votes by 6% to 10%, driven largely by its role as a default guideline for asset managers handling trillions in assets.
The disparity in voting behavior underscores ISS's role in amplifying institutional preferences over individual shareholder input, as funds delegating proxy decisions frequently default to ISS benchmarks. When ISS opposes management, client opposition rises by approximately 20%. However, this influence does not uniformly produce "informed" outcomes; some research shows ISS recommendations occasionally steer votes away from evidence-based positions on governance metrics. Across proposal categories like executive compensation and board composition, ISS alignment boosts passage rates by 10 to 20 percentage points relative to dissenting advice.

Role in Shaping Corporate Practices

Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) shapes corporate practices by issuing proxy voting recommendations that institutional investors, managing trillions in assets, frequently follow, thereby pressuring companies to preemptively align governance, compensation, and policy proposals with ISS benchmarks to secure favorable vote outcomes. Controlling over 90% of the proxy advisory market alongside Glass Lewis as of April 2025, ISS's analyses on issues like board composition and shareholder proposals influence corporate decision-making, as firms adjust strategies to avoid "against" or "abstain" recommendations that correlate with higher failure rates in shareholder votes.
In executive compensation, ISS's emphasis on pay-for-performance alignment, using metrics like realizable pay calculated from proxy disclosures, prompts companies to restructure incentive plans, clawback policies, and equity grants to mitigate negative advice on say-on-pay resolutions. For the 2025 proxy season, firms that deviated from these models faced increased scrutiny, with ISS recommending against equity compensation plans deemed excessively costly, leading to revisions in burn rate calculations and performance vesting conditions to boost approval rates, which averaged near historic highs despite slight declines in overall support.
ISS also drives changes in board governance practices, recommending votes against directors or slates lacking independence, diversity, or relevant skills, which has compelled companiesparticularly recent IPOsto adopt staggered boards less frequently and enhance disclosure of board skills matrices. 2025 guideline updates heightened focus on board diversity and oversight of material risks, resulting in firms bolstering committees for audit, nominating, and compensation to align with these criteria and avert vote defeats.
Through integration of ESG factors into proxy evaluations, ISS encourages corporations to implement sustainability reporting, risk assessments, and board-level ESG oversight, influencing the proliferation of dedicated sustainability committees and disclosures on climate and social issues. This advisory role has accelerated ESG policy adoption, as companies respond to potential voting penalties by embedding these elements into governance frameworks, though such shifts reflect ISS's interpretive benchmarks rather than uniform empirical mandates.

Empirical Evidence of Effectiveness

Empirical studies demonstrate that ISS recommendations exert substantial influence on shareholder voting outcomes. A negative recommendation from ISS is associated with a reduction in support for director elections by approximately 19 percentage points. Similarly, in uncontested director elections, ISS recommendations shift between 6% and 10% of total shareholder votes, primarily through its role as an aggregator of governance-relevant information. For say-on-pay proposals, negative ISS recommendations correlate with 25 percentage points lower approval rates, underscoring the firm's sway over institutional investors who often delegate voting decisions.
ISS's influence extends to prompting changes in corporate practices, particularly executive compensation. Over half of companies receiving negative ISS recommendations on compensation adjust their plans in the following year to align with proxy advisor preferences. Approximately two-thirds of U.S. firms adapt executive pay structures based on ISS and Glass Lewis policies, often preemptively to avoid adverse voting outcomes. These adjustments reflect firms' responsiveness to ISS guidelines, which can shape governance decisions such as board composition and shareholder proposals.
Evidence on the informativeness of ISS recommendations for improving firm performance or governance quality remains mixed. Analyses of compensation assessments show that ISS "against" recommendations predict worse industry-adjusted accounting performance in subsequent periods, particularly for firms with non-December fiscal year-ends where ISS faces fewer resource constraints. However, such predictive power is absent or weaker during peak seasons, and major asset managers like BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street demonstrate superior ability to identify underperforming pay practices compared to ISS.
Some research indicates ISS indirectly enhances governance by fostering shareholder engagement. Firms receiving low say-on-pay support (below 70%)often tied to negative ISS viewsexhibit a 31% higher likelihood of disclosing engagement activities, with persistent increases in both the frequency and depth of investor interactions. This engagement correlates with positive abnormal stock returns of 2-3% for affected firms, suggesting potential alignment of practices with shareholder interests. Overall, while ISS demonstrably affects voting and behavior, studies lack consensus on net benefits to long-term governance or shareholder value, with findings highlighting both potential accountability gains and risks of formulaic adherence over nuanced evaluation.

Controversies and Criticisms

Conflicts of Interest and Dual Roles

Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) operates in dual capacities as a provider of proxy voting recommendations to institutional investors and as a seller of governance data and analytics services to corporate issuers, creating inherent conflicts of interest. In its proxy advisory role, ISS analyzes shareholder proposals and management recommendations, issuing vote guidance that influences how asset managers vote billions in shares, often swaying outcomes on governance matters such as board composition and executive compensation. Simultaneously, through its ISS Corporate Solutions division, the firm offers paid services to companies, including benchmarking tools, governance scoring, and preparation for proxy contests, which enable issuers to align their practices with ISS's own benchmarks and potentially secure favorable recommendations.
This duality incentivizes companies to purchase ISS's consulting services to mitigate risks of adverse voting advice, raising concerns that ISS's recommendations may be softened or tailored to favor paying clients rather than purely serving shareholder interests. For instance, SEC comment letters have highlighted that ISS's business model embeds a "foundational conflict," where revenue from issuer-paid governance productsreportedly generating significant portions of ISS's incomecould pressure the firm to recommend against non-clients or overlook deficiencies in paying ones. Critics, including U.S. senators and congressional witnesses, argue this structure undermines the independence expected of proxy advisors, as evidenced by hearings in 2025 where lawmakers noted ISS's consulting arm assists companies in "enhancing" alignment with ISS benchmarks, effectively creating a pay-to-play dynamic.
Regulatory scrutiny has intensified these issues, with the SEC adopting rules in 2022 under the Proxy Voting Advice framework to address conflicts, though implementation faced delays and litigation from ISS itself in 2025 over definitions of "solicitation." Despite firewalls and disclosure policiessuch as ISS's requirement to recuse analysts with prior consulting involvementthe firm acknowledges potential perceptions of bias but maintains that its proxy research remains independent, supported by compliance under the Investment Advisers Act. Empirical analyses, including academic reviews, suggest that while overt quid pro quo is rare due to reputational risks, the dual roles correlate with higher client retention and influence over corporate behavior, prompting calls for stricter separation or transparency mandates.

Allegations of Ideological Bias

Critics, including Republican state attorneys general and members of Congress, have accused Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) of embedding progressive ideological prioritiesparticularly environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors and diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) initiativesinto its proxy voting recommendations, allegedly at the expense of shareholder financial interests. Missouri Attorney General Andrew Bailey, in July 2025, publicly stated that ISS has "advanced radical ESG and DEI priorities that often run counter to shareholder value," recommending votes that prioritize non-financial agendas. Similarly, Florida Attorney General James Uthmeier launched an investigation in March 2025 into ISS and rival Glass Lewis for potential collusion and unlawful promotion of ESG policies, arguing these firms wield outsized influence over corporate governance without sufficient accountability to investors.
These allegations gained traction in congressional hearings, such as a May 2025 House Financial Services Committee session titled "Exposing the Proxy Advisory Cartel," where witnesses testified that ISS policies reflect "unfounded biases and presumptions," including a predisposition toward sustainability mandates that critics view as ideologically driven rather than empirically justified by returns data. Senate Banking Committee Republicans, led by Chairman Tim Scott, echoed these concerns in May 2025, questioning whether ISS's guidelines improperly steer votes toward political objectives, such as climate disclosures or board diversity quotas based on race and gender, which they argue lack robust evidence of enhancing long-term value. Earlier, in April 2024, Senators Bill Hagerty and Bryan Steil demanded ISS disclose how its voting services align with fiduciary duties, implying potential breaches through ideologically skewed advice.
Proponents of the bias claims point to patterns in ISS recommendations, such as frequent support for shareholder proposals on greenhouse gas emissions reductions or independent board chairs, which align more closely with advocacy group priorities than with majority investor preferences in empirical voting outcomes. An amicus brief filed in August 2025 in litigation involving ISS highlighted a "history of permitting undisclosed biases to impact voting recommendations," suggesting internal presumptions favor progressive governance reforms without transparent causal links to performance.
In October 2025, Tesla CEO Elon Musk criticized ISS and Glass Lewis for exerting outsized influence on shareholder votes without owning stock, describing them as "corporate terrorists" in the context of their recommendations against reinstating his performance-based compensation package, potentially worth up to $1 trillion in Tesla shares. Musk highlighted their sway over passive index funds, arguing that following such advice could undermine company leadership.
ISS has rebutted these accusations, maintaining that its guidelines are data-informed and client-customizable, with no "ideological stake" in outcomes, as stated in a January 2025 court filing asserting neutrality in shareholder resolutions. In response to mounting pressure, ISS announced in February 2025 that it would remove explicit DEI considerations, such as race and gender targets, from U.S. board voting recommendations, shifting emphasis toward skills-based assessments amid claims of overreach. Proxy advisory firms broadly defend against bias allegations by arguing their services enable informed, non-binding advice that clients can override, countering narratives of undue ideological influence.

Regulatory Challenges and Legal Disputes

Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) has faced significant regulatory scrutiny over its role as a proxy advisory firm, particularly regarding the classification of its voting recommendations under federal securities laws. In 2019, the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) issued guidance interpreting proxy advisory services, including those provided by ISS, as "solicitation" under Section 14(a) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, subjecting them to disclosure and liability requirements. ISS challenged this interpretation in a lawsuit filed in 2019, expanded after the SEC's 2020 rulemaking, arguing that its advice to clients does not constitute solicitation since clients independently seek the recommendations. On July 1, 2025, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit ruled in favor of ISS, holding that proxy advisory firms do not "solicit" votes when providing recommendations at clients' request, thereby vacating the SEC's rule on this point and limiting the agency's regulatory authority over such firms under proxy solicitation rules.
State-level regulations have also sparked disputes, with ISS contesting measures aimed at increasing transparency in non-financial voting advice. Texas Senate Bill 2337, effective September 1, 2025, requires proxy advisors to disclose when recommendations rely on nonpecuniary factors such as environmental, social, or governance (ESG) considerations not solely tied to financial interests. ISS, alongside Glass Lewis, filed suit in August 2025 to enjoin enforcement, claiming the law imposes undue burdens and conflicts with federal preemption. A federal court in Texas granted preliminary relief on August 30, 2025, blocking the Texas Attorney General from enforcing the law against ISS and Glass Lewis pending further proceedings, citing potential First Amendment and Commerce Clause violations.
Antitrust concerns have intensified amid ISS's dominant market position, where it and Glass Lewis collectively control approximately 97% of the proxy advisory market, influencing up to 40% of U.S. shareholder votes. In March 2025, Florida Attorney General James Uthmeier launched an investigation into ISS and Glass Lewis for potential antitrust violations, focusing on alleged collusion in adopting ESG-focused policies that may prioritize non-financial interests over shareholder value. U.S. Senator Bill Hagerty urged the Department of Justice and Federal Trade Commission in June 2025 to probe the firms for antitrust breaches, highlighting barriers to entry and uniform voting guidelines that stifle competition. The U.S. House Judiciary Committee initiated a related antitrust inquiry in March 2025, examining the duopoly's market power and practices such as standardized ESG criteria that critics argue distort corporate governance. These probes reflect broader debates over whether ISS's influence warrants heightened federal oversight, though no formal charges have been filed as of October 2025.

Recent Developments

Policy Updates and Adjustments (20242025)

In December 2023, Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) announced its benchmark policy updates for 2024, which generally applied to shareholder meetings on or after February 1, 2024, following an annual review process incorporating investor surveys, regulatory developments, and public feedback. Key adjustments included reinstating the "return on equity" (ROE) metric in evaluations for Japanese companies, reversing a prior suspension to better align with performance assessments in that market. Policies on board oversight were refined to emphasize case-by-case analyses for issues like climate accountability and executive compensation, with heightened scrutiny on pay-for-performance misalignment where realizable pay exceeded 25% over three-year grant values relative to total shareholder return. 
Further 2024 updates addressed shareholder proposals on governance topics, shifting certain recommendationssuch as those requiring independent board chairsto a case-by-case approach rather than blanket opposition, reflecting responsiveness to evolving investor priorities amid regulatory scrutiny. In sustainability-related guidelines, ISS clarified expectations for climate risk disclosure, recommending against directors at companies lacking credible transition plans aligned with Paris Agreement goals, though without mandating specific net-zero targets. These changes were informed by the 2024 Global Benchmark Policy Survey conducted from August to September 2023, which gauged investor views on topics like board diversity and ESG integration.
For 2025, ISS released benchmark policy updates on December 17, 2024, effective for meetings on or after February 1, 2025, with a narrower scope compared to prior years, focusing on three primary areas following a public comment period from November 18 to December 2, 2024. In the United States, the poison pill (shareholder rights plan) policy was codified to include explicit evaluation factors, such as voting against plans not approved by shareholders unless justified by demonstrable threats like activist pressures, aiming to balance takeover defenses with shareholder approval. Policies on special purpose acquisition companies (SPACs) were updated to recommend against extensions beyond two years without strong justifications, addressing concerns over prolonged de-SPAC processes and dilution risks.
Sustainability guidelines for 2025 included minor clarifications to advance disclosure standards without substantive shifts, such as indefinite suspension of certain racial or gender diversity quotas in favor of skills-based assessments, while maintaining votes against boards failing to oversee material climate risks. Globally, updates in regions like Europe and Asia incorporated local regulatory changes, such as French capital market reforms eliminating issuance discounts without preemptive rights, but avoided broad expansions in ESG mandates amid ongoing debates over their empirical impact on returns. These adjustments, totaling 41 across topics, were derived from internal reviews and the 2025 Policy Survey, signaling a more incremental approach potentially influenced by anti-ESG legislative pressures in the U.S.

Responses to Market and Political Pressures

In response to intensifying political scrutiny from Republican-led states and federal initiatives under the second Trump administration, Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) adjusted its U.S. benchmark voting policies on director elections. On February 11, 2025, ISS announced it would indefinitely halt consideration of gender, racial, or ethnic board diversity factors in vote recommendations for U.S. company directors, effective after February 25, 2025, citing evolving legal and regulatory developments including executive orders targeting diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) initiatives. This shift reversed prior guidelines that penalized boards lacking such diversity, reflecting pressure from anti-DEI litigation and state-level restrictions on ESG-related investing.
Market pressures, including client demands for customized voting frameworks amid reputational risks, prompted ISS to incorporate regulatory shifts into its December 17, 2024, announcement of 2025 benchmark policy updates, which assessed emerging trends like reduced emphasis on ESG metrics in executive compensation. During the 2025 U.S. proxy season, ISS withheld support for any environmental shareholder proposals, aligning with a broader decline in average support for environmental and social (E&S) resolutions, which fell amid heightened counter-ESG proposals from conservative activists. This outcome followed a surge in anti-ESG filings, representing 14.7% of proposals submitted by February 21, 2025, as institutional investors navigated state divestment mandates and federal antitrust probes into proxy advisory influence.
Further adaptations addressed conflicts over ESG/DEI integration in ongoing awards; ISS's 2025 Global Benchmark Policy Survey sought client input on whether removing such metrics from in-flight compensation plans should trigger adverse voting recommendations, signaling flexibility to mitigate backlash from asset managers facing political divestitures. These responses coincided with parallel industry moves, such as Glass Lewis's October 14, 2025, decision to phase out standardized benchmark recommendations by 2027 in favor of client-tailored policies, underscoring sector-wide recalibration to political and fiduciary pressures prioritizing shareholder value over ideological mandates. Empirical data from the season showed a sharp drop in total shareholder proposals and increased omissions under Rule 14a-8, attributing part of the trend to firms preempting advisory opposition amid regulatory hostility toward ESG-driven governance.
