 Commercial paper is a short-term, unsecured promissory note issued primarily by corporations to raise cash for current transactions and short-term liabilities, such as payroll or inventory financing. These instruments are typically sold at a discount to their face value and mature in one to 270 days, with an average maturity of about 30 days, allowing issuers to avoid the costs and delays of SEC registration under Section 3(a)(3) of the Securities Act of 1933. Issued in large denominationsoften a minimum of $100,000they are bought by institutional investors like money market funds, banks, and corporations seeking low-risk, liquid short-term investments.
The U.S. commercial paper market serves as a critical segment of the broader money market, facilitating efficient funding for high-credit-quality issuers while offering competitive yields to investors. As of November 12, 2025, total outstanding commercial paper amounted to $1.316 trillion, comprising nonfinancial ($335.8 billion), financial ($576.9 billion), and asset-backed ($398.1 billion) categories. Issuance occurs either directly by the borrower to investors or through dealer networks, with asset-backed commercial paper (ABCP) secured by pools of receivables or other assets to enhance creditworthiness.
Historically, the market has played a pivotal role in corporate liquidity, though it faced strains during financial crises, such as in 2008, when the Federal Reserve intervened to support issuance. Today, commercial paper remains unsecured for direct and dealer-placed types, relying on the issuer's credit ratingoften A-1/P-1 or higherfor appeal, and it exempts issuers from ongoing disclosure requirements, promoting its use for flexible, cost-effective borrowing.

Fundamentals

Definition and Purpose

Commercial paper is an unsecured, short-term promissory note issued primarily by corporations or financial institutions to raise funds for immediate financial needs. These notes typically mature in 1 to 270 days, with an average maturity of about 30 days, allowing issuers to meet short-term liabilities without the regulatory burdens of longer-term securities, as they are exempt from SEC registration under these conditions.
The primary purpose of commercial paper is to provide low-cost, efficient funding for operational expenses, such as payroll, inventory purchases, and accounts receivable, offering creditworthy entities an alternative to traditional bank loans that may carry higher interest rates or more restrictive terms. By tapping directly into the money markets, issuers can secure liquidity quickly and at competitive rates, supporting day-to-day business activities without long-term commitments.
Distinguished from longer-term bonds or secured loans, commercial paper functions as a negotiable instrument within the money markets, emphasizing its role in short-term, unsecured financing rather than capital structure enhancements or collateral-backed obligations. Typical issuers are large corporations, such as Apple and Microsoft, and financial institutions possessing strong short-term credit ratings, generally at least A-1 from Standard & Poor's or P-1 from Moody's, ensuring investor confidence in repayment.

Key Characteristics

Commercial paper is characterized by its short-term maturity, typically ranging from 1 to 270 days, with an average duration of about 30 days, designed to provide issuers with flexible, low-cost funding for immediate needs while qualifying for regulatory exemptions. This maturity limit is crucial under U.S. securities laws, as it allows commercial paper to be exempt from Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) registration requirements pursuant to Section 3(a)(3) of the Securities Act of 1933, provided the notes arise out of current transactions or the proceeds are used for current transactions, have a maturity at issuance of not more than 270 days, and are of a type not ordinarily purchased by the general public. Additionally, to leverage this exemption, commercial paper is typically sold to sophisticated institutional investors, ensuring the notes are of a type not ordinarily purchased by the general public.
A defining feature of commercial paper is its unsecured nature, meaning it is issued as a promissory note without any collateral backing, placing full reliance on the issuer's creditworthiness for repayment. This lack of security underscores the importance of the issuer maintaining a strong financial profile, as investors bear the risk of default without recourse to specific assets. To mitigate this risk and attract buyers, issuers must secure high short-term credit ratings, typically A-1+ from Standard & Poor's or P-1 from Moody's, the highest short-term ratings comparable to AAA in long-term ratings, which signal low default probability and are essential for market participation.
In terms of issuance structure, commercial paper is denominated in large increments to appeal to institutional investors, with a standard minimum face value of $100,000, though some programs allow smaller amounts down to $10,000 in certain cases. This high denomination threshold limits accessibility to retail investors and promotes high liquidity among money market funds and corporations. Regarding interest, commercial paper functions as a zero-coupon, discounted instrument: it is sold below its par value and redeemed at full face value upon maturity, with the difference representing the implicit yield rather than periodic coupon payments. For high-rated commercial paper, these yields are typically close to those of U.S. Treasury bills, though slightly higher due to credit risk, reflecting their high liquidity and strong credit quality. This discount mechanism simplifies transactions and aligns with the instrument's short-term focus, enabling issuers to avoid explicit interest expenses during the holding period.

Historical Development

Origins and Early Use

The term "commercial paper" originated from 19th-century merchant practices in Europe and the United States, evolving directly from earlier bills of exchange that facilitated trade by allowing sellers to draw payment promises from buyers. These instruments, initially known as two-name paper involving obligations from both buyer and seller, emerged as early as the 18th century to finance domestic commerce amid limited coinage. By the early 19th century, they had become essential for short-term credit in informal trading networks, where merchants issued promissory notes or bills to bridge cash flow gaps without relying on scarce specie.
In the United States, commercial paper saw widespread adoption during the 1830s and 1850s, particularly in agricultural trade sectors such as cotton, lumber, and grain, where banks and merchants used it to provide seasonal financing for harvests and shipments. This period's decentralized banking system, with regional interest rate variations, encouraged its use to exploit arbitrage opportunities, allowing borrowers in high-rate agricultural areas to secure funds more cheaply through paper discounted in financial centers like New York. Initially confined to domestic transactions, it supported credit sales among farmers and traders, filling voids left by unreliable local currencies and limited bank lending. For instance, promissory notes became a common money substitute in rural economies, enabling the purchase of goods like lumber on deferred payment terms.
A pivotal event underscoring commercial paper's vulnerabilities was the Panic of 1837, when liquidity crises revealed its role in amplifying financial instability; banks suspended specie payments and refused to redeem paper at full value, leading to widespread failures among merchant houses financing agricultural exports like cotton. Interest rates on prime commercial bills in New York surged from 12% in mid-1836 to as high as 30% by late that year, as specie drains and federal policies tightened money supply, highlighting the instrument's dependence on confidence in redemption. This panic, triggered partly by the Bank of England's halt on discounting U.S. commercial paper tied to Anglo-American trade, exposed how such instruments, while innovative for domestic agricultural finance, could propagate shocks across interconnected markets.
Prior to any regulatory oversight, commercial paper markets operated informally without standardization, relying heavily on banker acceptance to enhance negotiability and reduce risk through diversified obligors. Note brokers facilitated discounting for entities lacking direct bank access, creating ad hoc networks that circulated paper as a de facto currency in trade-heavy regions. These markets lacked a true secondary trading venue, with liquidity stemming from banks holding accepted paper as reserves, though this exposed holders to insolvency risks during downturns like 1837.

Modern Evolution

Following World War II, the commercial paper market experienced significant growth, particularly in the 1960s, driven by rising interest rates that exceeded the Federal Reserve's Regulation Q ceilings on bank deposits, prompting corporations to seek alternative short-term funding sources. Finance companies, such as General Motors Acceptance Corporation (GMAC), pioneered large-scale direct issuance during this period to finance consumer and business lending, bypassing traditional bank loans amid high corporate demand for cost-effective liquidity. By the mid-1960s, the number of issuers had surpassed 300, with outstanding volumes expanding rapidly as nonfinancial firms also entered the market to capitalize on favorable rates.
A major shock occurred in 1970 with the bankruptcy of Penn Central Railroad on June 21, the largest corporate failure in U.S. history at the time, which defaulted on $82 million in commercial paper. This event triggered a liquidity crisis, with nonfinancial commercial paper outstanding dropping by about 10% (around $3 billion) in the following weeks, as investors shunned the market. In response, the SEC issued Rule 17Ad-3 requiring prime commercial paper issuers (rated P-1 or A-1) to maintain 100% bank backup lines of credit equal to their outstanding paper, institutionalizing credit support and restoring confidence.
Regulatory developments further institutionalized the market, notably through the SEC's adoption of Rule 3a-1 under the Investment Company Act of 1940 on January 22, 1981, which provided a safe harbor exempting certain operating companies, including finance firms, from investment company status if no more than 45% of their assets (excluding government securities and cash items) consisted of securities other than government securities, securities of employee securities companies, majority-owned subsidiaries, or securities of controlled companies engaged in non-investment businesses. This facilitated finance companies' operations without full investment company regulation. The market's growth accelerated in the 1980s, fueled by deregulation, an expanding economy, and the rise of money market mutual funds (MMFs), which emerged in the 1970s and became major investors, holding significant portions of outstanding paper and driving annual growth rates exceeding 10% as they offered higher yields than capped bank deposits. By the end of the decade, commercial paper had become a cornerstone of short-term corporate funding, with outstanding amounts surpassing $200 billion.
The 2008 financial crisis highlighted the market's vulnerabilities, as Lehman Brothers' bankruptcy on September 15, 2008, triggered a sharp contraction in liquidity; investors fled short-term debt amid fears of further defaults, causing nonfinancial commercial paper outstanding to drop by approximately 10% in the following weeks and overall issuance to shrink by $300 billion in the month after the collapse. The Reserve Primary Fund "breaking the buck" due to Lehman exposures exacerbated the panic, leading to a freeze in funding for even high-quality issuers. In response to the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic, the Federal Reserve swiftly reestablished the Commercial Paper Funding Facility (CPFF) on March 17, 2020, under Section 13(3) of the Federal Reserve Act, enabling direct purchases of up to three-month commercial paper from eligible issuers to restore market function; the facility reached a peak outstanding of approximately $4.3 billion in June 2020, stabilizing rates and preventing a broader credit contraction for households and businesses.
Recent trends through 2025 reflect ongoing innovation and resilience, with the U.S. market size reaching approximately $1.3 trillion in outstanding commercial paper as of November 2025, supported by post-pandemic recovery and steady institutional demand. Digital issuance platforms have gained traction, leveraging blockchain for tokenized commercial paper to enable faster settlement and broader access; for instance, Guggenheim Treasury issued the first native digital commercial paper on the XRP Ledger in June 2025, achieving over $280 million in volume with near-instantaneous execution. Concurrently, ESG-linked or sustainable commercial paper has emerged as a growth area, tying proceeds to environmental, social, and governance objectives, with programs like those from RBC and increasing adoption in Europe and Asia since 2020, enhancing transparency and investor appeal in short-term funding.

Issuance and Market Mechanics

Issuance Process

The issuance of commercial paper begins with the issuer, typically a large corporation or financial institution, assessing its short-term funding needs, such as for payroll, inventory, or accounts payable, to determine the appropriate amount and maturity of the paper. Issuers then obtain a credit rating from established agencies like Standard & Poor's or Moody's, as a high investment-grade rating (e.g., A-1/P-1 or better) is essential to attract investors and ensure market access at favorable terms. Finally, the issuer selects the maturity, which ranges from 1 to 270 days but averages about 30 days, balancing liquidity needs with regulatory exemptions.
Placement of commercial paper occurs through two primary methods: direct issuance or dealer intermediation. In direct issuance, highly rated issuers, such as major finance companies, sell the paper straight to investors like money market funds, banks, or corporations, bypassing intermediaries to minimize costs. More commonly, issuers use dealersinvestment banks like Goldman Sachs or J.P. Morganwho purchase the entire issuance from the issuer and resell it to investors, providing distribution efficiency and market-making support.
Documentation for commercial paper is straightforward, consisting of a simple promissory note that outlines the issuer's unconditional promise to pay the face value at maturity, without any collateral or security interest. Due to its short-term nature and high-quality status, no formal indenture or registration with the SEC is required, as it qualifies for exemption under Section 3(a)(3) of the Securities Act of 1933 for notes maturing in nine months or less that arise from current transactions and are of prime quality.
To maintain ongoing funding, issuers frequently engage in rollover practices, repaying maturing paper by issuing new commercial paper, often on a daily or weekly basis through negotiations with dealers or direct sales. This continuous re-issuance allows issuers to roll over debt indefinitely, provided market conditions and credit quality remain supportive.
For indirect issuance via dealers, costs include a spread or fee charged by the intermediary, typically 1 to 6 basis points of the issuance amount, depending on maturity and issuer rating. This fee compensates dealers for distribution, inventory risk, and operational services, making commercial paper a cost-effective borrowing option compared to bank loans.

Market Participants and Trading

The primary issuers of commercial paper in the United States are large corporations seeking short-term funding for operational needs, with financial institutions comprising the largest share at approximately 46% of outstanding paper, followed by nonfinancial corporations at 24% and asset-backed issuers at 30%, as of November 2025. Examples include finance companies and banks, which issue to bridge funding gaps between longer-term assets and liabilities, while nonfinancial entities such as manufacturers and retailers use it for working capital like inventory or payroll. Foreign banks and corporations also participate significantly, often through U.S. subsidiaries to access dollar funding.
Investors in the commercial paper market primarily include institutional buyers focused on high-quality, short-term instruments, with prime money market mutual funds holding about 24% of the U.S. market as a key source of liquidity for issuers. Other major investors encompass corporations managing excess cash, banks, state and local governments, and institutional entities like pension funds and insurance companies, which collectively provide diversified demand and help maintain market stability. These investors prioritize credit-rated paper from established issuers to minimize risk, often holding notes to maturity given the instruments' brief durations.
Intermediaries play a central role in facilitating efficient distribution and monitoring, with dealer firms such as Goldman Sachs and Merrill Lynch handling around 90% of commercial paper placements by purchasing from issuers and reselling to investors, earning spreads of 1-6 basis points depending on maturity and issuer type. Rating agencies, including S&P Global Ratings and Moody's Investors Service, provide ongoing credit assessments that influence investor participation and pricing, ensuring transparency in this unsecured market.
Trading in commercial paper occurs predominantly in the over-the-counter (OTC) market through dealer networks, with electronic platforms like Bloomberg terminals used for quotes, order matching, and transaction execution to enhance efficiency. The secondary market exhibits limited liquidity due to the short maturitiestypically 1 to 270 days, averaging 30 dayswhich discourage resale, though it remains active for larger issues via dealer repurchases or master note agreements. Daily trading volume in the U.S. market averages $100-200 billion, primarily reflecting primary issuance but including some secondary activity, with all-to-all trading platforms not yet prevalent.

Credit Support Mechanisms

Backup Lines of Credit

Backup lines of credit serve as liquidity backstops for commercial paper programs, consisting of committed or uncommitted bank facilities that provide funds to issuers in the event they cannot roll over maturing paper into new issuances. These lines, often structured as revolving credit agreements or swing lines, ensure access to short-term borrowing at predetermined rates, such as prime or LIBOR plus a spread, to repay investors promptly. In the U.S., rating agencies like S&P Global mandate that such facilities cover at least 100% of the program's maximum outstanding amount to deem liquidity adequate, though highly rated issuers may secure partial coverage of 50% while others require full backing.
Issuers pay fees for these arrangements, including a commitment fee of typically 0.25% to 0.5% on the unused portion of the line, along with potential facility and usage fees if drawn heavily. These costs contribute 10 to 20 basis points to the effective borrowing rate for commercial paper programs, particularly for A-rated issuers who commonly maintain such lines to support ongoing rollover practices. By guaranteeing repayment during market disruptions, backup lines reduce perceived default risk for investors and enhance overall program stability, fulfilling rating agency requirements for non-restrictive covenants and maturities longer than the paper itself.
Prior to the 2008 financial crisis, these lines were often firm commitments with minimal conditions, but post-crisis regulatory changes, including stricter bank capital rules under Basel III, led to a shift toward more conditional structures incorporating performance covenants that could limit availability during stress. This evolution reflects banks' increased caution in extending unconditional liquidity support, balancing issuer needs with capital conservation requirements while still providing essential insurance against rollover failures.

Alternative Facilities

Asset-backed commercial paper (ABCP) represents a primary alternative to traditional bank lines of credit, where short-term unsecured promissory notes are issued by bankruptcy-remote special purpose vehicles (SPVs), known as conduits, to finance pools of underlying assets such as trade receivables, auto loans, leases, or securities. These conduits purchase diversified asset portfolios from originators, using the proceeds from maturing assets to repay investors, while liquidity facilitiesoften from sponsors or third partiesensure timely repayment of maturing paper even if asset cash flows are delayed. Unlike unsecured commercial paper, ABCP benefits from structural credit enhancements like overcollateralization or reserve accounts, reducing default risk and enabling issuance at lower yields for high-quality programs.
Letters of credit or guarantees from non-banks, such as insurance companies, provide another form of liquidity and credit support for commercial paper programs, particularly in ABCP structures. Financial guarantee insurers issue policies that enhance the creditworthiness of asset pools, covering potential shortfalls in cash flows and allowing conduits to achieve higher ratings without relying solely on bank commitments. For instance, funding agreement-backed notes in some programs are supported by the payment capacity of insurance providers, offering a cost-effective alternative for issuers seeking diversified backing. Multilateral development banks occasionally extend guarantees to commercial paper issuances in emerging markets, complementing private sector support by mitigating political or currency risks in infrastructure-related funding.
Central bank facilities serve as a lender-of-last-resort mechanism during market stress, exemplified by the U.S. Federal Reserve's Commercial Paper Funding Facility (CPFF). Launched in October 2008 amid the global financial crisis, the CPFF, with authority to purchase up to $1.8 trillion in highly rated unsecured and asset-backed commercial paper from eligible issuers through primary dealers, ultimately purchased approximately $335 billion, stabilizing short-term funding markets and preventing a broader credit freeze. Revived in March 2020 in response to COVID-19 disruptions, the program again bought commercial paper directly, supporting approximately $4.3 billion in purchases by its March 2021 expiration and ensuring liquidity for corporate operations, auto financing, and household credit.
Synthetic backups, such as credit default swaps (CDS) or total return swaps (TRS), offer derivative-based enhancements tied to commercial paper programs, transferring credit or market risk without traditional funding commitments. In ABCP conduits, a CDS can be issued to protect against defaults in underlying assets, with the swap counterparty reimbursing losses in exchange for premiums, thereby bolstering program ratings. TRS vehicles, meanwhile, synthetically replicate asset performance, allowing issuers to hedge liquidity mismatches while avoiding direct asset transfers.
These alternative facilities provide advantages over bank lines, including lower costs for high-rated issuers through diversified risk transfer and reduced reliance on banking sector balance sheets. The ABCP segment, in particular, has grown significantly, with global outstandings exceeding $375 billion in 2024; as of November 2025, U.S. asset-backed commercial paper outstanding stood at approximately $397 billion, driven by multi-seller programs that fund a broader range of assets and attract institutional investors seeking secured short-term yields.

Pricing and Yields

Yield Determination

Commercial paper yields are primarily determined using standardized formulas that account for the instrument's discount pricing and short-term maturity, reflecting conventions in money market trading. The most common method is the discount yield, which measures the annualized return based on the difference between the face value and purchase price relative to the face value itself. This yield is calculated as:
DiscountYield=(360DaystoMaturity)100
\text{Discount Yield} = \left \times \left( \frac{360}{\text{Days to Maturity}} \right) \times 100
DiscountYield=(DaystoMaturity360)100
This formula employs a 360-day year convention and expresses the yield as a percentage, focusing on the discount as a proportion of the face value.
An alternative measure is the money market yield, also known as the investment yield, which bases the return on the purchase price rather than the face value, providing a more accurate reflection of the investor's effective return. It is computed as:
MoneyMarketYield=(360DaystoMaturity)100
\text{Money Market Yield} = \left \times \left( \frac{360}{\text{Days to Maturity}} \right) \times 100
MoneyMarketYield=(DaystoMaturity360)100
This approach highlights the yield earned on the actual investment amount.
For comparability with longer-term bonds that use a 365-day year, the bond equivalent yield (BEY) adjusts the money market yield to a 365-day basis:
BEY=(365DaystoMaturity)100
\text{BEY} = \left \times \left( \frac{365}{\text{Days to Maturity}} \right) \times 100
BEY=(DaystoMaturity365)100
This formula facilitates direct comparisons across different fixed-income instruments.
In U.S. markets, commercial paper yields are conventionally quoted on a discount basis using the 360-day year, aligning with broader money market practices for Treasury bills and similar short-term securities.
To illustrate, consider a commercial paper with a $1,000,000 face value purchased for $995,000 and maturing in 90 days. The discount yield is:
DiscountYield=(36090)100=0.0054100=2%
\text{Discount Yield} = \left \times \left( \frac{360}{90} \right) \times 100 = 0.005 \times 4 \times 100 = 2\%
DiscountYield=(90360)100=0.0054100=2%
This example demonstrates the straightforward application of the discount yield formula in typical market scenarios.

Influencing Factors

The yield on commercial paper is significantly influenced by the credit quality of the issuer, with higher-rated issues commanding lower yields due to perceived lower default risk. For instance, issuers with top-tier ratings such as A-1+ typically offer yields 10 to 50 basis points lower than those with lower-tier ratings like A-2, reflecting investor preferences for safer credits. This differential arises because rating agencies assess factors including financial strength and liquidity, leading to tighter spreads for prime-rated paper over benchmarks.
Maturity length also plays a key role in determining yields, as longer tenors expose investors to greater interest rate risk and potential changes in market conditions. Commercial paper maturities range up to 270 days, but longer durationssuch as 90 to 180 daysgenerally require higher yields to compensate for the increased uncertainty over reinvestment and rate fluctuations. Shorter maturities, averaging around 30 days, thus trade at lower yields due to their reduced exposure to these risks.
Broader market conditions exert substantial pressure on commercial paper yields, often benchmarked against the federal funds rate, with spreads adjusting to prevailing liquidity and monetary policy. During periods of stress, such as the 2008 financial crisis, these spreads widened dramatically, reaching up to 300-400 basis points over the federal funds rateas investor caution intensified and funding became scarce. In normal conditions, yields track closely to the federal funds rate plus a modest risk premium, but liquidity crunches amplify the spread to reflect heightened systemic risks.
Supply and demand dynamics further shape yield levels, with high issuance volumes typically pushing yields upward to attract sufficient buyers in a competitive short-term funding environment. Conversely, strong investor appetite, particularly from money market funds which hold a significant portion of outstanding paper, tends to compress yields by increasing demand for high-quality issues. This interplay is evident in periods of robust corporate borrowing, where excess supply can elevate costs, while ample liquidity from institutional investors like money funds supports lower rates.
Economic indicators, including inflation and GDP growth, provide overarching context for yield movements, as rising economic expansion signals higher funding needs and potential rate hikes. Yields on commercial paper have historically ranged from approximately 0.04% to 6.7% annually between 2000 and 2025, correlating with periods of low inflation and subdued growth yielding tighter rates, while elevated inflation or robust GDP prompts higher yields to align with policy responses. For example, accelerating GDP growth often coincides with increased corporate issuance, driving yields up in tandem with broader economic activity. As of November 2025, 90-day AA financial commercial paper yields stand at approximately 3.9%.

Risks and Defaults

Associated Risks

Commercial paper, as an unsecured short-term debt instrument, exposes investors primarily to credit risk, where the issuer may fail to repay principal and interest at maturity due to financial distress. This risk is heightened because there is no collateral backing the obligation, leaving investors with limited recovery options in the event of default. While credit ratings from agencies such as Standard & Poor's or Moody's help mitigate this by restricting issuance to high-quality (typically A-1/P-1 rated) borrowers, the unsecured nature still leaves room for losses, particularly during economic downturns when issuer solvency weakens.
Liquidity risk arises from the challenges in rolling over maturing paper or selling it in the secondary market, especially under market stress when investor demand dries up. The commercial paper market relies heavily on continuous issuance to refinance obligations, often with maturities under 30 days, making issuers vulnerable to sudden freezes in funding availability. Secondary market trading is thin, amplifying illiquidity as holders may struggle to find buyers without incurring significant discounts. Backup lines of credit from banks can provide some mitigation by offering alternative funding sources during such periods.
Interest rate risk is generally minimal for commercial paper due to its very short maturities, which limit exposure to fluctuations in benchmark rates. However, upon rollover or reinvestment of maturing paper, issuers and investors face reinvestment risk if prevailing rates have fallen, potentially reducing yields or increasing borrowing costs. This aspect becomes more pronounced in environments of volatile short-term rates, though the overall impact remains contained compared to longer-term instruments.
Systemic risk in the commercial paper market stems from its interconnectedness with broader money markets, where disruptions can lead to contagion effects, such as runs on money market funds holding large amounts of paper. Events like market-wide freezes can propagate instability, as seen in historical stresses where reduced issuance strained liquidity across financial institutions and amplified credit concerns economy-wide. The market's role as a key funding channel for corporations and financial entities underscores its potential to contribute to wider financial instability if confidence erodes.
Operational risk involves potential errors or disruptions in the issuance process, such as administrative failures by the program sponsor or sudden rating downgrades that trigger yield spikes and market withdrawal. These issues can arise from reliance on dealer networks or covenant breaches in support facilities, leading to unexpected funding gaps. Proper contingency planning and robust administrative oversight are essential to manage such risks, though they remain inherent to the program's operational complexity.

Historical Defaults

One of the earliest significant defaults in the commercial paper market occurred in June 1970 when the Penn Central Railroad filed for bankruptcy with approximately $82 million in outstanding commercial paper. This event, the largest corporate bankruptcy in U.S. history at the time, triggered a liquidity crisis as investors lost confidence in nonfinancial issuers, leading to a sharp contraction in the market where outstanding nonfinancial commercial paper fell by over 9% within weeks, from $32 billion to $29 billion. The Federal Reserve intervened by urging banks to extend credit to support the rollover of maturing paper, averting a broader collapse, while the Securities and Exchange Commission responded with reforms, including requirements for commercial paper issuers to maintain backup lines of credit to enhance liquidity assurance.
The 2008 global financial crisis amplified these risks dramatically with the bankruptcy of Lehman Brothers on September 15, 2008, when the firm had approximately $5.7 billion in outstanding commercial paper across its enterprises. Lehman's default caused immediate turmoil in the money markets, as investors fled short-term instruments, resulting in a 17% drop in total commercial paper outstanding from $1.8 trillion to $1.5 trillion in the following weeks and breaking the buck in major money market funds holding Lehman paper. The Federal Reserve launched the Commercial Paper Funding Facility to purchase eligible paper, injecting over $300 billion in support to restore market function and prevent systemic contagion.
Post-2008 defaults remained rare, but notable incidents included the 2011 collapse of MF Global, which had issued about $1.3 billion in commercial paper shortly before its October bankruptcy filing, exacerbating concerns over broker-dealer stability and leading to temporary spikes in short-term funding costs. More recently, the 2023 regional banking crisis, marked by the failures of Silicon Valley Bank and Signature Bank, created scares in the commercial paper market through widened spreads in commercial paper-overnight index swap rates, though no outright defaults occurred among bank-issued paper, highlighting ongoing liquidity sensitivities.
Historically, commercial paper defaults have been infrequent, with Moody's data recording 126 issuer defaults totaling around $26.5 billion from 1972 to 2024, equating to an average annual default rate below 0.1% given the market's trillions in annual issuance. These events, while rare, often amplify systemic effects due to the instrument's role in short-term funding chains, but recovery rates average approximately 90%, benefiting from the short maturities that limit exposure duration.

Regulatory Environment

United States Regulations

In the United States, commercial paper is primarily regulated under the Securities Act of 1933 and the Investment Company Act of 1940, with oversight from the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) and the Federal Reserve, aimed at ensuring market liquidity and investor protection while facilitating short-term funding for issuers.
The cornerstone exemption for commercial paper issuance is found in Section 3(a)(3) of the Securities Act of 1933, which exempts from registration requirements any note, draft, bill of exchange, or banker's acceptance with a maturity of nine months or less (not exceeding 270 days) that arises out of a current transaction or the proceeds of which have been or are to be used for current transactions, provided it is of prime quality and issued by a U.S. or Canadian entity. This exemption applies only to negotiable commercial paper sold to sophisticated investors, such as money market funds or institutional buyers, without the need for a prospectus, thereby reducing issuance costs and enabling rapid market access. The SEC has interpreted "prime quality" to mean paper with the highest credit rating from nationally recognized statistical rating organizations (NRSROs), though issuers must avoid any promise of repurchase or guarantee to qualify.
For money market funds, which are major purchasers of commercial paper, SEC Rule 2a-7 under the Investment Company Act of 1940 imposes strict eligibility criteria to limit credit risk exposure. The rule requires funds to invest only in short-term securities, including commercial paper, that present minimal credit risks as determined by the fund's board of directors, with maturities not exceeding 397 days. Prior to 2014 amendments, this included commercial paper rated in the two highest short-term categories (A-1 by S&P or P-1 by Moody's) by NRSROs; however, following the Dodd-Frank Act's mandate to reduce reliance on ratings, Rule 2a-7 now prohibits direct references to NRSRO ratings, requiring funds to conduct independent credit assessments instead. Funds must also maintain minimum liquidity buffers25% in daily liquid assets and 50% in weekly liquid assetsto support redemptions, directly impacting their capacity to hold commercial paper.
The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act of 2010 introduced enhanced oversight of money markets, including commercial paper, by designating money market funds as potential systemic risks under the Financial Stability Oversight Council (FSOC) and mandating structural reforms to mitigate run risks. Title VIII of Dodd-Frank established a framework for supervising systemically important financial market utilities, indirectly bolstering commercial paper market stability through improved clearing and settlement oversight. Additionally, the Volcker Rule (Section 619 of Dodd-Frank) restricts banks from engaging in proprietary trading of commercial paper and limits their sponsorship of asset-backed commercial paper conduits classified as "covered funds," thereby curbing speculative issuance while preserving banks' ability to underwrite and market-make in traditional commercial paper programs.
The Federal Reserve plays a critical role in supporting commercial paper liquidity, particularly through its discount window, which provides collateralized loans to depository institutions using eligible assets such as high-quality commercial paper as pledged collateral to address short-term funding pressures. In times of market stress, the Fed can invoke Section 13(3) of the Federal Reserve Act to authorize emergency facilities, as demonstrated by the Commercial Paper Funding Facility (CPFF), first established in 2008 and reactivated in 2020, which directly purchases eligible commercial paper from issuers to restore market functioning when private funding dries up. The CPFF operates through a special purpose vehicle funded by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, accepting unsecured, asset-backed, and municipal commercial paper rated A-1/P-1 or equivalent, with terms up to three months, ensuring broad access for non-bank issuers under exigent circumstances.
Recent regulatory updates include the SEC's 2023 money market fund reforms, which eliminate the ability of fund boards to impose liquidity gates or fees tied to weekly liquid asset thresholds, aiming to prevent redemption runs that could spill over to commercial paper markets. These amendments, effective in phases through 2025 with full compliance by October 2024, raise minimum daily and weekly liquidity requirements to 25% and 50% of total assets, respectively, and mandate swing pricing for institutional non-government funds to allocate redemption costs more equitably, enhancing overall resilience without altering core commercial paper eligibility under Rule 2a-7. As of 2025, these reforms have contributed to a more stable funding environment, with no major further changes reported.

Global Variations

In the European Union, commercial paper with a maturity of less than 365 days is exempt from the prospectus requirements under the Prospectus Regulation, classifying it as a money market instrument rather than a transferable security requiring full disclosure. The European Central Bank provides liquidity support through programs like commercial paper purchases, which have been used to stabilize markets during crises, such as acquiring over 35 billion in the early stages of the COVID-19 pandemic to restore credit flow. The euro-denominated commercial paper market, focused on corporate issuers, had approximately 460 billion outstanding as of October 2025 (USD equivalent), though broader short-term European paper initiatives like the Short-Term European Paper (STEP) framework facilitate issuance across euro area currencies with total primary market volumes exceeding 1 trillion annually in recent years.
Canada's regulatory framework for commercial paper mirrors aspects of the U.S. model but operates under National Instrument 45-106, which exempts distributions of negotiable promissory notes or commercial paper maturing in one year or less from prospectus requirements, provided they meet conditions like non-convertibility into other securities. The Bank of Canada supports the market through facilities such as the Commercial Paper Purchase Program, launched in 2020 to purchase eligible commercial paper and alleviate funding strains, ensuring continued credit availability to issuers.
In the Asia-Pacific region, Japan's commercial paper market is regulated by the Financial Services Agency under the Financial Instruments and Exchange Act, treating short-term notes as dematerialized securities eligible for book-entry transfer systems operated by the Japan Securities Depository Center since 2003, with the Bank of Japan accepting them as collateral for monetary operations. China's commercial paper market has expanded significantly following 2015 monetary policy adjustments by the People's Bank of China, which liberalized interest rates and boosted trading volumes to over 100 trillion yuan that year, enabling non-bank financial institutions to issue and trade more freely in the interbank bond market. In Australia, the commercial paper market is overseen by the Australian Securities and Investments Commission and the Reserve Bank of Australia, with eligible corporate-issued paper under one year maturity accepted in domestic market operations, supporting a growing segment integrated into the broader wholesale funding landscape.
Emerging markets exhibit limited development of traditional commercial paper due to underdeveloped credit infrastructure and reliance on bank-dominated financing, though analogs exist such as Brazil's Certificados de Depsito Bancrio (CDBs), short-term bank deposit certificates regulated by the Central Bank of Brazil that function similarly for liquidity management but are primarily issued by financial institutions rather than corporates.
Efforts toward global harmonization include the International Organization of Securities Commissions' (IOSCO) principles for money market funds, which emphasize liquidity risk management and high-quality short-term instruments like commercial paper to enhance resilience, as outlined in their 2012 policy recommendations and subsequent reviews. Cross-border issuance faces challenges such as currency risk, where fluctuations in exchange rates can amplify costs and volatility for international investors in non-domestic commercial paper programs.
