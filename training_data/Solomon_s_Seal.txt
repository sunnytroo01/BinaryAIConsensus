 ''Solomon's seal'' is an ambiguous term referring to several distinct concepts, including a genus of plants, a legendary mystical symbol, and a decorative knot motif.
In botany, Solomon's seal (genus Polygonatum) comprises approximately 60 species of rhizomatous herbaceous perennials in the Asparagaceae family, native to temperate regions of the Northern Hemisphere. These plants are characterized by arching stems up to 5 feet (1.5 m) tall, alternate oval to elliptical leaves, dangling pairs of tubular greenish-white bell-shaped flowers blooming in spring, and blue-black spherical berries in summer.
These plants derive their common name from the distinctive circular scars on their thick, fleshy white rhizomes, which resemble the seals or impressions associated with the legendary King Solomon. The genus name Polygonatum, from Greek meaning "many knees," alludes to the segmented appearance of the rhizomes.
Notable species include the smooth Solomon's seal (P. biflorum), common in eastern U.S. forests and hardy in USDA zones 39, and the Japanese Solomon's seal (P. odoratum), valued for its variegated foliage in zones 48.
In legend, the Seal of Solomon is a signet ring or hexagram symbol attributed to King Solomon, used for magical protection and control over spirits. Solomon's knot is an ancient interlaced decorative pattern symbolizing eternity and unity.

Botany

Description and Etymology

Solomon's seal encompasses plants of the genus Polygonatum, herbaceous perennials in the Asparagaceae family characterized by their rhizomatous growth habit. These plants spread via horizontal underground rhizomes, forming dense colonies over time, and produce unbranched, arching stems that reach 1 to 6 feet (30 to 180 cm) in height, depending on the species. The stems bear alternate, smooth, lanceolate to elliptic leaves, 2 to 6 inches (5 to 15 cm) long, with prominent parallel veining and a slightly clasping base. In spring, small, tubular, greenish-white flowers dangle singly or in small clusters from the leaf axils, blooming for several weeks before giving way to rounded, blue-black berries in late summer. The rhizomes, thick and fleshy, feature distinctive rounded scars where previous stems have emerged and died back, contributing to the plant's unique appearance.
The common name "Solomon's seal" originates from these rhizome scars, which early observers likened to the impressions left by wax seals on documents or, more legendarily, to the ancient seal attributed to King Solomon. The genus name Polygonatum derives from Greek words meaning "many knees," referring to the jointed nature of the rhizomes. This nomenclature appears in European herbal texts from the 16th century onward, such as those by Otto Brunfels and Leonhart Fuchs, where the plant was valued for its form and purported virtues. The botanical features evoke the symbolic Seal of Solomon, a mystical emblem associated with wisdom and protection in biblical lore.

Species and Distribution

The genus Polygonatum, commonly known as Solomon's seal, encompasses approximately 60 species of rhizomatous perennial herbs in the family Asparagaceae, with the highest species diversity concentrated in eastern Asia, particularly China. These plants are predominantly native to the temperate zones of the Northern Hemisphere, spanning from boreal forests in North America and Europe to subtropical woodlands in Asia, though a few species have been introduced to regions outside their native ranges, such as parts of North America for P. odoratum. The genus exhibits a circumboreal distribution pattern, with species richness decreasing from east to west across Eurasia and into North America.
Key species include Polygonatum biflorum, a North American native characterized by smooth stems and arching growth, which is widespread in eastern woodlands from New Hampshire westward to southern Manitoba and southward to Texas. This species thrives in moist, deciduous forests, bottomland areas, and along streambanks, often forming colonies in the shaded understory. In Eurasia, P. odoratum stands out for its fragrant, nodding flowers, occurring natively from Europe across to East Asia, including China, Japan, Korea, and Mongolia, where it inhabits shady, moist forest floors and woodland edges. Another prominent European species, P. multiflorum, known for its historical medicinal value, is distributed across temperate Europe to northern Iran, favoring similar shaded forest habitats with well-drained, humus-rich soils. P. verticillatum, distinguished by its whorled leaf arrangement, ranges from European mountains to the montane and alpine zones of the Himalayas, preferring rocky, moist slopes and forest understories at elevations up to 3,000 meters.
Habitat preferences across the genus emphasize shaded, moist environments in forest understories, woodland margins, and ravines, where species like P. biflorum and P. odoratum contribute to ecosystem stability by providing ground cover, preventing soil erosion, and supporting herbivore food webs and pollinator activity. These niches typically feature neutral to acidic, humus-enriched soils with moderate drainage, allowing the plants to colonize disturbed edges while avoiding full sun exposure that could desiccate their rhizomes. In understory ecosystems, Polygonatum species enhance biodiversity by coexisting with ferns, trilliums, and shrubs, forming resilient layers that buffer against invasive species and aid in nutrient cycling through their deciduous foliage. Introduced populations, such as P. odoratum in North American gardens, occasionally naturalize in similar shaded sites but rarely disrupt native flora.

Cultivation and Uses

Solomon's seal (Polygonatum spp.) is a shade-loving perennial herbaceous plant well-suited to woodland or shade gardens. It thrives in USDA hardiness zones 3 to 9, depending on the species, with most varieties performing best in partial to full shade, receiving dappled sunlight or less than two hours of direct sun daily. The plant prefers organically rich, moist, well-drained soils high in humus, though it can adapt to drier shade conditions near tree roots once established. Propagation is most effectively achieved through division of the rhizomes in spring or fall, allowing the plant to spread slowly into colonies; seed propagation is possible but slower and less reliable.
These plants exhibit strong pest and disease resistance, with few reported issues from insects or deer, making them low-maintenance options for gardeners. They pair well as companion plants with hostas, ferns, bleeding hearts, and spring bulbs like daffodils and tulips, where the arching foliage of Solomon's seal can hide fading bulb leaves while providing textural contrast in shady borders or naturalized areas. Sustainable practices include mulching with organic matter to retain moisture and dividing clumps every few years to prevent overcrowding.
In ornamental horticulture, Solomon's seal serves as an excellent groundcover due to its gracefully arching stems, which bear attractive, alternate leaves and produce small, bell-shaped greenish-white flowers in late spring to early summer, often followed by showy blue-black berries in late summer. The foliage offers coarse texture and architectural interest in shade gardens, rain gardens, or woodland settings, with the flowers adding subtle fragrance. Popular cultivars include Polygonatum odoratum 'Variegatum', featuring leaves edged in white for brighter accents in dim areas, and P. odoratum var. pluriflorum 'Variegatum', selected for its superior ornamental foliage.
Historically, the rhizomes of Solomon's seal have been used in European herbal traditions as a poultice or tonic to treat inflammation, bruises, piles, and joint issues like rheumatism and gout, with the bruised roots applied topically for skin conditions such as black eyes. Native American communities employed the plant similarly, using rhizome preparations for wound healing, anti-inflammatory purposes, and as a general tonic for respiratory and gastrointestinal ailments. In Traditional Chinese Medicine, the rhizome, known as Yu Zhu (Polygonatum odoratum), acts as a yin-nourishing tonic to moisten the lungs and stomach, addressing dry cough, throat dryness, irritability, thirst, and lung yin deficiency, while also softening sinews to alleviate joint pain and spasms from fluid insufficiency.
Modern herbal preparations of Solomon's seal often include tinctures made from fresh or dried rhizomes, typically dosed at 1-4 ml in water or juice, to support joint health, tendon recovery, respiratory function, and musculoskeletal injuries by promoting tissue lubrication and reducing inflammation. These extracts are commonly combined in formulas for dry coughs, lung disorders, and conditions involving yin deficiency, with processed forms like stir-fried Yu Zhu enhancing spleen support.
The young shoots of Solomon's seal are edible when harvested in spring and boiled for about 10 minutes, serving as a vegetable substitute similar to asparagus or added to salads, a practice historically noted among Native Americans and early colonists. However, the mature berries are toxic if consumed raw, containing anthraquinones that can cause vomiting and diarrhea, though they provide low-toxicity food for birds. The rhizomes require cooking if used for food, such as boiling for 20 minutes to resemble potatoes.

Seal of Solomon

Legend and Origins

The legend of the Seal of Solomon, often depicted as a magical signet ring, draws its foundational inspiration from the biblical portrayal of King Solomon's extraordinary wisdom in 1 Kings 4:29-34, where God endows him with unparalleled insight and understanding surpassing that of all Eastern sages and Egyptian experts. This scriptural emphasis on Solomon's dominion over nature, animals, and profound knowledge laid the groundwork for later traditions attributing supernatural powers to him, though the Bible itself does not mention any ring.
The core myth of the ring emerges in the apocryphal Testament of Solomon, a pseudepigraphic text composed between the 1st and 5th centuries AD, which narrates how the archangel Michael delivers a ring to Solomon, engraved with the divine name, to aid in constructing the Temple in Jerusalem. In this account, Solomon uses the ring to summon and bind demons, forcing them to reveal their powers and submit to his command, thereby expanding the biblical theme of wisdom into a tale of magical authority over supernatural beings. The text portrays the ring as a direct gift from heaven, symbolizing God's endorsement of Solomon's rule.
Medieval Jewish texts, such as the Babylonian Talmud (Gittin 68a-b), further elaborate the legend, describing the ring's engraving as the "Seal of God" and recounting how the demon Asmodeus steals it after Solomon's downfall, leading to the king's temporary exile before its recovery. In Islamic tradition, the Quran (Surah 27:16-17) affirms Solomon's God-given control over winds, jinn, and birds, with medieval Arabic writings like those in Qia al-Anbiy (Stories of the Prophets) attributing the ringknown as Khatam Sulaymnas the instrument for subjugating jinn to perform labors such as quarrying stones for the Temple. The 14th-century Ethiopian epic Kebra Nagast incorporates a related motif, where Solomon bestows a signet ring upon the Queen of Sheba as a token of their son's royal lineage, linking it to the transfer of sacred authority alongside the Ark of the Covenant to Ethiopia. Christian medieval lore, influenced by these sources, similarly emphasizes the ring's role in Solomon's exorcisms and divine kingship.
Ascribed powers of the ring in these origins include commanding demons and jinn to labor on the Temple without iron tools, as detailed in the Testament of Solomon, where bound spirits haul massive stones and reveal hidden knowledge. It also granted protection against demonic assaults and the ability to heal through the subjugation of malevolent forces, with legends noting Solomon's use of it to thwart illnesses caused by evil spirits. In broader traditions, the ring ensured safeguarding from supernatural threats and facilitated communication with the unseen world. Various accounts describe its fate after Solomon's death: in Jewish lore, Asmodeus hurls it into the sea, where it is swallowed by a fish and later retrieved by fishermen, symbolizing the ephemeral nature of such power; Islamic variants suggest it was returned to God or hidden, while the Kebra Nagast implies its legacy endures through Solomon's descendants.

Forms and Interpretations

The Seal of Solomon has been depicted in various geometric forms across different traditions, with the most common being the hexagram, consisting of two interlocking equilateral triangles forming a six-pointed star. This hexagram, known as the Magen David in Jewish contexts, appears in medieval manuscripts such as the Leningrad Codex from 1008 CE. Alternative forms include the pentagram, a five-pointed star often enclosed in a circle, as described in the pseudepigraphic Testament of Solomon where it is engraved on Solomon's ring as a pentalpha for binding demons. In Islamic traditions, the Khtam Sulaymn is frequently represented as a hexagram or pentagram accompanied by a circle or square containing Arabic inscriptions, such as the Greatest Name of God or Quranic verses, used in talismanic art and architecture like mosque tilework.
Interpretations of these forms vary by cultural and religious context, emphasizing protection, authority, and harmony. In Jewish mysticism, the hexagram as the Magen David symbolizes the shield of divine protection against evil forces, representing the balance between mercy and judgment as well as the connection between the divine and human realms, and it has been employed on amulets since the medieval period. In Islamic esotericism, the Khtam Sulaymn signifies Solomon's God-given authority to command jinn and natural elements, serving as a talisman for warding off harm and invoking spiritual protection, often integrated into protective objects with inscribed scripts. Within Western occult traditions, the pentagram form, sometimes termed a pentacle when circled, is interpreted as a tool for elemental controlassociating its points with earth, air, fire, water, and spiritdrawing from Solomonic lore to harmonize opposing forces in rituals.
Variations of the Seal appear prominently in 17th-century grimoires, such as the Lesser Key of Solomon (Lemegeton Clavicula Salomonis), where a secret form of the Sealoften a hexagram or inscribed sigilis incorporated into evocation circles and protective vessels to bind and command spirits during magical operations. These depictions adapt the traditional hexagram or pentagram into complex ritual diagrams, emphasizing its role in ensuring the magician's safety and efficacy.

Cultural and Religious Significance

In Jewish tradition, the Seal of Solomon is employed in amulets to ward off the evil eye and demonic forces, drawing from ancient texts that attribute to King Solomon the power to command supernatural entities through a divine ring. This protective symbolism is rooted in works like the Testament of Solomon (1st3rd century CE), where the seal enables exorcism and control over demons to aid in temple construction, as described by historian Flavius Josephus in Antiquities of the Jews (8.4549). Such uses appear in Jewish magical papyri and handbooks like Sefer ha-Razim (5th6th century CE), emphasizing Solomon's role as a master exorcist.
Within Islam, the Seal of Solomon features in Sufi talismans as a symbol of dominion over jinn (spirits), reflecting Qur'anic accounts of Solomon's prophetic authority (Qur'an 34:12). Medieval Islamic texts, such as the Epistles of the Brethren of Purity (10th century), highlight its magical spells for protection and control, integrating it into esoteric practices that bridge prophetic wisdom and supernatural command. In Sufi contexts, it serves as a meditative emblem for invoking divine harmony and repelling malevolent influences.
In Christianity, particularly during the medieval period, the Seal of Solomon was incorporated into seals and amulets for exorcism rituals, as evidenced in the Testament of Solomon and its Christian recensions, which portray it as a tool for binding demons. Pilgrims to the Church of the Holy Sepulchre in the 4th century CE venerated a relic believed to be Solomon's ring, underscoring its role in protective devotion, as noted by traveler Egeria in her Itinerarium (37.7). This association extended to broader Christian symbolism of wisdom and divine authority, though its magical elements sometimes drew ecclesiastical caution.
The Ethiopian Orthodox Church regards the Seal of Solomon as a divine emblem of authority, legendarily bestowed by the archangel Michael as God's seal on a ring, central to national identity in the Kebra Nagast (14th century), which traces the Solomonic dynasty to this artifact. It symbolizes protection and legitimacy, appearing in imperial regalia like crowns and the Order of Solomon's Seal (established 1874), combining Christian crosses with the hexagram to represent spiritual dominion over demons and affirmation of biblical lineage.
In Western esotericism, the Seal of Solomon holds significance for invocation and ritual magic, prominently featured in the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn (late 19th century), where it is used in ceremonies to summon and bind spirits, influenced by grimoires like the Greater Key of Solomon (14th17th century). Modern Wicca adaptations incorporate it as a protective talisman in circles and spells, drawing from Solomonic traditions to balance elemental forces and enhance personal power, though often simplified from its original geometric forms.
As a protective symbol, the Seal of Solomon is commonly worn as jewelry, such as rings and pendants, to shield against evil spirits across Jewish, Islamic, and Christian communities, with historical examples including medieval amulets inscribed for personal safeguarding. It has been etched on buildings and artifacts for communal defense, like in Ethiopian ecclesiastical art to invoke divine warding. Due to its strong magical associations, the seal faced historical bans or restrictions; for instance, the Testament of Solomona key source for its exorcistic powerswas excluded from the biblical canon as apocryphal, reflecting early Christian concerns over sorcery. In medieval Europe, its use in talismans occasionally led to prohibitions by church authorities wary of occult practices.

Solomon's Knot

Design and Symbolism

The Solomon's knot is a geometric motif composed of two interlocking closed loops that form a continuous, endless band without beginnings or ends, creating a symmetrical pattern often rendered in a square or circular frame. Also known as the endless knot or Solomon's link, this design features interlaced rings that weave over and under each other, emphasizing seamless connectivity in its simplest four-sided form.
Symbolizing eternity and infinity through its unbroken structure, the Solomon's knot represents interconnectedness and the unity of opposites, frequently interpreted as marital fidelity and eternal love. In Celtic traditions, it evokes infinite bonds of affection and harmony between individuals, while early Christian contexts tie it to themes of resurrection and eternal life, as seen in motifs linked to baptism and salvation. Associated with King Solomon's wisdom, the knot illustrates the harmonious integration of dualities under divine insight.
Unlike the Seal of Solomon, which typically depicts a hexagram or star for magical protection, the knot serves as a decorative pattern rather than a literal ring, though lore sometimes conflates the two due to shared associations with Solomon's legendary signet.

Historical and Artistic Uses

The Solomon's knot, a motif of two interlocked loops forming an endless pattern, has roots tracing back to the Indus Valley Civilization around 2500 BC, where it appeared on clay seals and copper tablets in Harappa and Mohenjo-Daro, symbolizing immortality and continuity. This ancient design likely influenced later cultures through trade and migration, evolving under Bactrian and Achaemenid influences before spreading westward. In Roman art, the motif emerged in mosaics as early as the 1st century AD, often as a protective emblem known as the Hercules knot, representing an eternal cycle and warding off evil; examples include threshold mosaics in villas and public buildings, where it framed entrances to signify boundless protection. Byzantine artisans adapted it into textiles and church decorations during the 4th6th centuries, incorporating it into interlace patterns on Coptic tunics and mosaic floors, blending Christian iconography with pre-Christian magical symbolism for spiritual safeguarding.
During the medieval and Renaissance periods, the Solomon's knot proliferated in diverse artistic traditions. In Islamic art, particularly in Khorasan from the 10th13th centuries, it featured in Ghurid architecture such as the Minaret of Jam and on metalwork like incense burners, often paired with inscriptions invoking blessings; it drew from earlier Buddhist influences as one of the eight auspicious symbols, denoting eternity and harmony in geometric compositions. Christian manuscripts embraced the motif for its interlaced form, evoking divine interconnection; it appears in the 9th-century Book of Kells as part of elaborate knotwork borders, enhancing the illuminated Gospels with patterns symbolizing infinite faith. In heraldry, the knot served as a decorative charge in medieval European arms, representing fidelity and unity, though not as a primary emblem; it influenced noble badges and seals across England and Italy from the 12th century onward.
In modern contexts, the Solomon's knot persists as a versatile decorative element in jewelry, where it adorns pendants and rings to evoke enduring bonds, and in tattoos symbolizing personal eternity and protection. Architectural motifs incorporate it in contemporary designs inspired by historical patterns, such as facade interlaces in revivalist buildings. Its significance in Croatian Glagolitic heritage, where it integrates into interlace decorations (pleter) of 11th15th-century manuscripts and stone inscriptions, preserves this fusion of script and symbolism.

In Entertainment

Music

Solomon's Seal is the title of the fifth and final studio album by the British folk-jazz band Pentangle, released in June 1972 on the Reprise label. Recorded by the original lineup of Bert Jansch (guitar and vocals), John Renbourn (guitar and vocals), Jacqui McShee (vocals), Danny Thompson (double bass), and Terry Cox (drums), it marked the end of the group's initial incarnation before their breakup in 1973. The album blends traditional British folk songs with jazz improvisation and acoustic instrumentation, exemplified in tracks like the maritime ballad "Sally Free and Easy" (3:55), the medieval carol "The Cherry Tree Carol" (2:57), and the original instrumental "The Snows" (3:43), which highlight the band's signature interplay of guitar lines and rhythmic subtlety. Other notable songs include "High Germany," "The Bonny Boy," and "Prodigal Son," though the latter draws from earlier influences in their repertoire; the collection draws its name from the legendary Seal of Solomon, a mythical signet ring with magical powers sometimes associated with the pentagram symbol adopted by Pentangle.
Upon release, Solomon's Seal received mixed critical reception, with some reviewers noting its introspective tone as less commercially dynamic than prior works like Basket of Light (1969), contributing to the band's dissolution shortly after. However, it has since been recognized as a cornerstone of the British folk revival, praised for its elegant fusion of acoustic folk traditions with jazz elements and its role in elevating the genre during the early 1970s. The album's improvisational style, evident in live-like studio recordings, influenced subsequent acoustic ensembles and helped cement Pentangle's legacy as innovators in the folk-jazz crossover.
The album has endured through multiple reissues, including a 2004 Japanese remaster and a comprehensive 2025 2CD deluxe edition by Cherry Red Records, featuring a new stereo remaster, rare bonus tracks from BBC Radio's Sounds of the 70s session (live versions of five album songs), previously unreleased live performances from 1972 tours in Guildford and Birmingham, and soundtrack contributions like "When I Get Home" from the film On Any Day. These extras, compiled by Pentangle historian Colin Harper and including interviews with McShee, underscore the album's ongoing vitality, with the 2025 vinyl box set The Albums: 1968-1972 via Svart Records reintroducing it to new audiences and affirming its place in folk history.
Beyond Pentangle, the Seal of Solomon motifrooted in biblical and mystical lorehas inspired classical compositions, such as William Walton's 1938 motet "Set me as a seal upon thine heart," a choral setting of Song of Solomon 8:6-7 composed for the wedding of Ivor Guest and Lady Mabel Fox-Strangways, emphasizing themes of enduring love with its lyrical intensity and mixed choir arrangement. George Frideric Handel's 1748 oratorio Solomon (HWV 67) draws broadly from the king's legendary wisdom and biblical narratives, incorporating elements of Solomon's symbolic authority in scenes like the Queen of Sheba's arrival, though without direct reference to the seal itself.
In modern music, the Seal of Solomon appears as a motif evoking mysticism and power, notably in Two Steps from Hell's 2023 epic orchestral track "Seal of Solomon," composed by Nick Phoenix with vocals by Uyanga Bold, featured on the album Atlantis Ascending and used in trailers for its dramatic, otherworldly atmosphere symbolizing ancient protection and enigma. Similarly, Scottish folk artist Julie Fowlis collaborated on a 2025 orchestral piece titled "Solomon's Seal" with amon Doorley, Zo Conway, and John McIntyre, setting Michael Longley's poem to music for performance with the Ulster Orchestra, blending Celtic traditions with the symbol's themes of renewal and nature.

Literature

In Hammond Innes' 1980 thriller Solomon's Seal, protagonist Roy Slingsby, a property valuer and former naval officer, uncovers a collection of rare stamps from the Solomon Islands while appraising a decaying English estate, drawing him into a web of family intrigue, political unrest, and smuggling in the South Pacific. The narrative intertwines philatelic mystery with elements of cargo cults, ancestral curses, and island rebellion, evoking the mystical aura of Solomon's legendary artifacts through motifs of hidden legacies and forbidden knowledge. Themes of adventure dominate as Slingsby navigates treacherous seas and cultural clashes, blending high-stakes action with subtle undertones of supernatural dread tied to colonial exploitation.
Skyla Dawn Cameron's 2016 fantasy novel Solomon's Seal, the first in the Livi Talbot series, centers on disgraced heiress and single mother Olivia "Livi" Talbot, who accepts a lucrative commission to recover the mythical Seal of Solomona ring reputed to command demonsfrom an ancient site in Ethiopia. As Livi, a resourceful treasure hunter, confronts mercenaries, cryptic puzzles, and supernatural forces, the story explores themes of redemption and maternal resilience amid high-octane chases and magical confrontations. The seal serves as a pivotal plot device, embodying power over the occult while grounding the adventure in Talbot's personal stakes.
Short stories featuring Solomon's Seal often appear in occult anthologies, leveraging the symbol's esoteric connotations for tales of mysticism and the supernatural. In Robert W. Chambers' 1906 story "The Seal of Solomon," published in the collection The Tree of Heaven, a detective encounters astral projection and ghostly romance through the seal's influence, blending fin-de-sicle occultism with emotional intrigue. Similarly, Edward FitzGerald's 1852 fable "Solomon's Seal" reimagines Persian lore in a moral tale where King Solomon inscribes a ring with the phrase "This too shall pass," serving as a meditation on impermanence and wisdom that has influenced subsequent literary explorations of the motif.
The Seal of Solomon recurs as a potent artifact in adventure fiction, symbolizing hidden knowledge and control over otherworldly forces, a trope that influenced early exponents like H. Rider Haggard. In Haggard's 1885 novel King Solomon's Mines, the quest for the biblical king's lost diamond hoard evokes the seal's legendary power, establishing a template for treasure hunts infused with exotic mysticism and perilous trials that echoes in later works. This motif underscores themes of imperial ambition and arcane discovery, positioning the seal as a narrative catalyst for heroic journeys into the unknown.

Other Media

In video games, the Seal of Solomon features prominently in Solomon's Key, a 1986 puzzle-platformer developed and published by Tecmo for arcades and later the NES, where the protagonist Dana seeks the titular key to seal demons and restore order to a demon-infested world by collecting magical orbs and solving room-based puzzles. The game's mechanics revolve around using the seal's power to create blocks, cast spells, and control demonic entities, emphasizing strategic demon containment as a core gameplay element. More recently, in the 2023 mobile otome game What in Hell is Bad? developed by Withos, Solomon's Seals function as a premium in-game currency exclusively purchasable with real money, used for pulling limited-edition characters in gacha events within its demonic fantasy setting.
In film, the 2017 short film The Solomon's Seal, directed by Woosik Choi, delves into mystical and supernatural themes inspired by the legendary artifact's lore, portraying its discovery and enigmatic powers in a narrative blending historical mysticism with personal intrigue. On television, the Seal of Solomon appears in the 1973 episode "The Place of Solomon's Seal" from the BBC children's adventure series The Terracotta Horse, where it serves as a pivotal plot device in a quest involving ancient mysteries and exploratory challenges for young protagonists.
Broader media representations include comics like Ex Occultus: The Seal of Solomon (2010), an independent title where occult investigators Wakefield and Hollander pursue King Solomon's ancient ringbelieved to grant divine communication and control over supernatural forcesin a high-stakes fantasy adventure blending horror and historical elements.
