Filling-in
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Filling-in is a perceptual phenomenon in the visual system whereby the brain interpolates and completes missing or absent information across specific regions of the visual field, such as the physiological blind spot or artificial gaps, to produce a continuous and seamless representation of the surrounding color, brightness, texture, or motion. The concept has roots in early 19th-century observations, such as Troxler's fading effect, and has been extensively studied since the mid-20th century in relation to visual perception theories. This process compensates for inherent limitations in the retina and visual input, ensuring stable perception despite gaps caused by the blind spota roughly 6 diameter region lacking photoreceptors located about 15 temporal to the foveaor other scotomas.
Filling-in manifests in various forms, broadly classified by timing and dependency on stimuli. Instantaneous filling-in occurs rapidly, often within milliseconds, as seen in the blind spot where surrounding features propagate across the gap without noticeable delay, or in illusory contours like Kanizsa figures, where incomplete shapes induce perceived boundaries and surfaces. In contrast, delayed filling-in requires prolonged fixation, such as in the Troxler effect, where peripheral static stimuli fade and are replaced by background attributes after seconds of steady gaze. Other notable variants include neon color spreading, where a hue from line intersections diffuses into an enclosing region to form an illusory colored surface, and visual phantoms, in which low-contrast gratings extend dimly across a blank gap, mimicking real stimuli and even inducing aftereffects like motion perception. Filling-in can also be modal (creating a visible illusory surface) or amodal (perceiving continuity behind occluders without explicit filling).
The underlying mechanisms involve active neural processes in early visual cortex areas, including V1 and V2, where activity from surrounding regions may spread via horizontal connections (isomorphic model) or through attribute tagging (symbolic model), with both theories proposed to explain the phenomenon. This phenomenon highlights the brain's constructive role in vision, aiding object recognition, surface segmentation, and perceptual stability in complex scenes, with implications for understanding normal perception, illusions, and disorders involving visual completion. Studies as of 2024 further suggest multisensory integration can enhance filling-in for dynamic stimuli, expanding its scope beyond purely visual inputs.

Introduction

Definition and Basic Examples

Perceptual filling-in refers to the brain's process of interpolating missing visual information across regions of the visual field where sensory input is absent, such as blind spots, scotomas, or occlusions, thereby creating a seamless and continuous perceptual experience. This phenomenon compensates for inherent limitations in the visual system, ensuring that gaps in retinal input do not disrupt the overall perception of the environment.
A classic example is the filling-in at the physiological blind spot, located approximately 15 degrees temporal to the fovea and measuring approximately 5.5 degrees horizontally by 7.5 degrees vertically, where the optic nerve exits the retina and no photoreceptors are present. Here, the brain automatically completes the missing area with the color, texture, or brightness of the surrounding visual field, making the absence imperceptible during normal viewing. Another basic instance occurs in simple occlusion completion, where a partially hidden object, such as a continuous line or shape obscured by a foreground element, is perceived as unbroken and whole based on contextual cues from visible parts.
Filling-in plays a crucial role in maintaining perceptual stability by bridging sensory gaps, allowing the visual system to construct a coherent representation of the world despite incomplete input from the retina. This process was first notably observed in Troxler's fading, described in 1804, where sustained fixation on a peripheral stimulus causes it to fade and the surrounding background to fill in the space. Theoretical frameworks, such as isomorphic and symbolic models, later proposed mechanisms for how this interpolation occurs at neural and cognitive levels, respectively.

Historical Background and Significance

The phenomenon of filling-in in visual perception traces its roots to early 19th-century observations of perceptual fading. In 1804, Ignaz Paul Vital Troxler described the disappearance of stationary objects in the peripheral visual field during prolonged fixation, a process now known as Troxler fading, which highlighted the brain's tendency to interpolate missing information in stabilized retinal images. This laid foundational groundwork for understanding how the visual system completes incomplete scenes. Later, in 1955, Gaetano Kanizsa demonstrated illusory contours in subjective figures, interpreting them as a form of perceptual completion where the brain actively constructs boundaries and surfaces from fragmentary inducers, influencing subsequent research on amodal and modal completion. The concept gained renewed prominence in 1992 when Vilayanur S. Ramachandran popularized filling-in at the physiological blind spot through experiments showing seamless perceptual interpolation across this retinal gap, sparking widespread interest in the neural basis of such phenomena.
The 1990s marked a pivotal era in the development of theoretical models for filling-in, diverging into isomorphic and symbolic approaches. Isomorphic models posited active neural propagation of signals to fill gaps, supported by electrophysiological evidence from primate studies where neurons in striate cortex (V1) corresponding to the blind spot responded to stimuli presented outside it, suggesting a dynamic spread of activity akin to perceptual completion. In contrast, symbolic models, as articulated by Daniel C. Dennett in 1991, viewed filling-in as a passive inferential process where the brain ignores absences without explicit neural representation, treating perception as a high-level judgment rather than low-level interpolation, thereby challenging Cartesian notions of a unified conscious field. These opposing frameworks fueled debates on whether filling-in involves genuine neural rewriting or merely cognitive annotation, shaping the field's shift toward integrating neurophysiological and philosophical perspectives.
Filling-in holds profound significance for broader theories of visual perception and cognition. It illuminates mechanisms of consciousness by revealing how subjective experience arises from incomplete sensory data, as seen in philosophical analyses that use blind spot completion to question the veridicality of perceptual content and the boundaries between neural activity and awareness. In terms of neural plasticity, filling-in demonstrates adaptive reorganization in visual cortex, where prolonged exposure to scotomassuch as those in macular degenerationleads to perceptual stabilization through remapping, aiding functional recovery and highlighting the brain's capacity for experience-dependent changes. Furthermore, it underscores the role of active vision, where eye movements drive scene segmentation and object completion, enabling efficient environmental parsing beyond passive retinal input.
Early taxonomies of filling-in, particularly the modal/amodal distinction introduced by Pessoa, Thompson, and No in 1998which differentiated visible (modal) from inferred (amodal) completionsfaced critiques for oversimplifying diverse phenomena, as they failed to account for hybrid or context-dependent processes like boundary versus featural integration. This limitation prompted subsequent refinements, including proposals for multidimensional frameworks that better classify filling-in based on spatiotemporal dynamics and neural substrates, advancing more nuanced models of perceptual inference.

Traditional Types of Filling-in

Isomorphic Filling-in

Isomorphic filling-in is defined as the direct, retinotopic propagation of visual attributes, such as color, brightness, or texture, from surrounding regions into areas lacking sensory input, like the blind spot or a retinal scotoma, while preserving a one-to-one spatial mapping between the neural representation and the perceived image. This process creates a seamless perceptual surface by replicating surrounding features point-for-point into the deficient region, without requiring higher-level interpretation.
Key characteristics of isomorphic filling-in include its rapidity, often occurring within milliseconds, and its stimulus-driven nature, where it is automatically triggered by local visual cues such as edges or uniform surrounds. It primarily takes place in the early stages of visual processing, involving areas V1 and V2 of the visual cortex, where retinotopic organization allows for precise spatial correspondence. This low-level mechanism contrasts with symbolic filling-in, which relies on abstract, context-based inference rather than direct feature replication.
Classic examples demonstrate this process effectively. In the human blind spot, where the optic disc lacks photoreceptors, surrounding colors or patterns are perceived uniformly across the gap, with neurons in V1 firing as if stimulated despite the absence of input. Similarly, in cases of retinal scotomas caused by lesions, uniform surrounds lead to rapid completion of the missing area with the adjacent attributes, maintaining perceptual continuity.
The theoretical basis for isomorphic filling-in lies in neural lateral spread through short-range horizontal connections in V1, which facilitate the propagation of activity across retinotopic maps to complete boundaries and fill interiors. These connections enable stimulus-driven diffusion of signals, ensuring that the cortical representation matches the isomorphic structure of the visual field.

Symbolic Filling-in

Symbolic filling-in refers to a higher-level perceptual completion process in which the visual system infers and labels attributes for missing or occluded regions based on global contextual cues or prior knowledge, rather than through the propagation of local neural activity from surrounding areas. This interpretive mechanism treats the absent region as an extension of the surround's propertiessuch as color, brightness, or texturewithout generating a detailed, spatially explicit representation within the gap itself.
Key characteristics of symbolic filling-in include its relatively slower onset compared to bottom-up processes, strong influence from top-down cognitive factors like expectations and object knowledge, and involvement of mid-level visual areas such as V2 for boundary processing and the lateral occipital complex (LOC) for object-level interpretation. Unlike isomorphic filling-in, which relies on direct feature replication via neural spread in early visual cortex, symbolic filling-in produces no persistent retinotopic activation in the filled-in region, functioning instead as a passive attribution or "tagging" of inferred properties to maintain perceptual coherence.
Representative examples illustrate this process. In neon color spreading, a colored element at line intersections appears to diffuse illusorily into adjacent gaps, creating a transparent overlay perceived through contextual boundary cues rather than local color signals. The Craik-Cornsweet-O'Brien effect demonstrates brightness perception where a sharp luminance edge induces uniform lightness or darkness across extended uniform fields, interpreted symbolically as a computational labeling of surface properties based on edge statistics, without neural filling across the expanse. Amodal completion occurs when partially occluded shapes, such as a figure behind a barrier, are perceived as whole objects with hidden parts inferred from visible fragments and depth cues, relying on higher-level scene understanding.
Theoretically, symbolic filling-in aligns with Daniel Dennett's concept of perceptual "labeling" in Consciousness Explained, where the brain assigns attributes to regions without constructing a full internal model, emphasizing efficiency in object recognition by prioritizing interpretive shortcuts over exhaustive reconstruction. This approach supports rapid scene parsing and stable object perception in complex environments by leveraging contextual inference.
These traditional models remain debated, with neural evidence supporting isomorphic mechanisms in early visual areas for certain filling-in phenomena.

Modern Taxonomy and Phenomena

Instantaneous Filling-in

Instantaneous filling-in is a form of perceptual completion in which the visual system rapidly interpolates missing information across regions of visual space, occurring in less than 100 ms upon stimulus onset without the need for adaptation or prolonged fixation. This process ensures seamless perception despite physiological or stimulus-induced gaps, such as retinal blind spots or occlusions, by propagating surrounding visual attributes like color, texture, or brightness into the absent area. Unlike slower forms of filling-in, it depends on immediate neural mechanisms that prioritize perceptual continuity over explicit stimulus details.
Instantaneous filling-in is broadly divided into stimulus-dependent and stimulus-independent subtypes, based on whether specific configurational cues are required to initiate and bound the completion. In stimulus-dependent cases, the process is triggered by well-defined boundaries in the stimulus, allowing the visual system to infer and complete structures that are not physically present. A classic example is the perception of illusory contours in Kanizsa triangles, where three pacman-shaped inducers aligned in a triangular arrangement give rise to the immediate sensation of a fully formed white triangle, including its interior surface properties, despite the absence of actual lines or fills. Similarly, color from negative afterimages can fill into scotomas, spreading bounded by perceived edges to create a uniform colored surface where gaps exist, as demonstrated in experiments with artificial retinal defects.
Stimulus-independent instantaneous filling-in, by contrast, arises from intrinsic properties of the visual system without reliance on explicit boundary cues from the stimulus configuration. The natural blind spot, corresponding to the optic disc where retinal ganglion cell axons exit the eye, exemplifies this: when viewing uniform fields or textured backgrounds monocularly, the surrounding visual information completes the gap almost immediately, rendering the scotoma imperceptible and maintaining a continuous visual field. This rapid process also applies to small retinal lesions or pathological scotomas, where patients report no awareness of the defect as adjacent patterns or colors propagate into the area without delay.
A key property of instantaneous filling-in is its dependence on edge signals to guide the propagation of perceptual attributes into the completed region, ensuring that the filled area aligns with surrounding contours rather than expanding indefinitely. This edge-based mechanism operates without the perceptual fading or neural adaptation seen in delayed filling-in, allowing for immediate surface representation. In some instances, this aligns with the isomorphic model, where cortical activity directly reflects the unified perceived surface.

Delayed Filling-in

Delayed filling-in refers to the perceptual completion of missing visual information that occurs after a delay of several seconds or more, typically following sustained fixation, and is often mediated by neural adaptation processes. This phenomenon contrasts with more immediate forms of completion by requiring prolonged exposure for the visual system to interpolate or suppress features within blind or obscured regions. It can be categorized into stimulus-dependent and stimulus-independent subtypes, based on whether specific configurational cues from the stimulus are required to initiate the completion process.
In stimulus-dependent delayed filling-in, specific stimulus configurations, such as low contrast, indistinct edges, or dynamic elements in the surround, contribute to the gradual disappearance of a target after prolonged fixation. A classic example is Troxler fading, where a stationary peripheral target fades from perception during maintained central fixation, particularly when the target has indistinct edges or low contrast relative to its background. Another instance involves artificial scotomas created using gaze-contingent displays, in which a region of dynamic texture or noise is presented around a static target, leading to its perceptual filling-in over time; the rate of filling-in increases with greater eccentricity and boundary length of the scotoma. Motion-induced blindness provides a further example, where salient static targets embedded in a field of rotating or moving distractors intermittently disappear from awareness, with disappearance rates influenced by factors such as target eccentricity and perceptual grouping.
Stimulus-independent delayed filling-in occurs when the visual stimulus is stabilized on the retina, eliminating natural eye movements, leading to the uniform fading of the entire image. For instance, in experiments using retinal stabilization techniques, such as those involving oculometers to compensate for eye drifts, complex patterns or simple spots completely disappear after approximately 1-2 seconds of fixation, with the filling-in propagating from boundaries inward.
Key properties of delayed filling-in include the involvement of boundary adaptation, where neural representations of edges weaken over time, and surround inhibition, which suppresses activity in the region of the missing information by enhancing the influence of adjacent areas. These processes are reversible; the filled-in region reappears upon introduction of motion, a shift in attention, or transient stimuli that reactivate boundary signals. Some aspects of delayed filling-in may incorporate interpretive elements akin to symbolic filling-in, where higher-level perceptual organization aids in the completion.

Neural Mechanisms and Models

Isomorphic Model

The isomorphic model of filling-in describes an active neural process in which visual information is completed through the lateral spread of neural activity across retinotopic maps in early visual cortex, maintaining a point-to-point spatial correspondence between the perceived image and cortical representation. In this framework, activity originating from the borders of a visual gapsuch as a blind spot or scotomapropagates inward to fill the missing region, preserving the isomorphism between neural activation and the perceptual surface. This mechanism ensures that attributes like color, brightness, or texture are extrapolated continuously into the void, mimicking a diffusion-like process confined by perceptual boundaries.
Key mechanisms underlying the isomorphic model include short-range horizontal connections within primary visual cortex (V1) and secondary visual cortex (V2), which facilitate the lateral propagation of signals from edge detectors to interior neurons. These connections enable the completion of uniform surfaces by linking nearby receptive fields, while top-down modulation from higher visual areas can enhance or constrain the spread, integrating contextual cues to refine the filling-in. Computational models, such as the boundary contour system proposed by Grossberg and Mingolla, demonstrate how these interactions generate coherent perceptual boundaries that gate the diffusion of feature signals, preventing spillover across distinct regions.
Supporting evidence for the isomorphic model includes observations of persistent neural activity within retinotopic representations of blind spots and scotomas, where surrounding visual input elicits sustained responses in the deafferented cortical areas. This process applies particularly to localized gaps, allowing seamless perceptual continuity without conscious awareness of the deficit.
Despite its explanatory power for simple cases, the isomorphic model has limitations, as it struggles to account for filling-in over large gaps or in complex scenes that demand inferential processes beyond local propagation, where alternative mechanisms like symbolic labeling may dominate for non-retinotopic completions.

Symbolic Model

The symbolic model of filling-in describes a passive perceptual process in which missing regions in the visual field are labeled or tagged with attributes from the surrounding context through global computational mechanisms, without involving sustained neural activity or retinotopic firing within the completed areas. This approach emphasizes an economical, inferential strategy where the visual system assigns properties such as color, brightness, or texture to enclosed regions based on boundary information and contextual cues, akin to a symbolic representation rather than a spatially explicit one. As articulated by Dennett, this resembles "fame in the brain," where perceptual content gains prominence through distributed information processing without requiring a detailed, isomorphic neural map.
Mechanisms underlying the symbolic model likely involve higher-level inference rather than low-level propagation, potentially through subcortical amplification of surround signals or mid-level processing in areas like the lateral occipital complex (LOC). Reduced BOLD responses in early visual areas (V1 and V2) have been observed during certain completion tasks, such as Troxler fading, suggesting gaps may be effectively ignored while uniform attributes are assigned from the surround. Feedback from LOC to earlier areas could further support object-level labeling, enabling contextual integration without active filling across retinotopic space. This contrasts with the isomorphic model, which relies on active neural spread from boundaries.
Supporting evidence for the symbolic model includes the model accounting for amodal completion, where occluded shapes are inferred behind surfaces, and neon color spreading, where colored lines create an illusory tinted region bounded by invisible contours, both achievable through edge-based tagging without internal activation.
Despite its conceptual appeal, the symbolic model has limitations, particularly in explaining rapid or local filling-in phenomena that occur within milliseconds, which suggest more active neural processes. It has been critiqued for lacking precise neural specificity, as accumulating evidence from single-unit recordings and imaging shows activation in early visual areas during some completions, challenging the purely passive tagging hypothesis.

Experimental Evidence

Electrophysiological Studies in Animals

Electrophysiological investigations in animals have elucidated the neural underpinnings of perceptual filling-in by directly recording single-unit activity from the primary visual cortex (V1) during conditions that induce completion in regions lacking direct retinal input, such as the blind spot or artificial scotomas. These studies primarily utilize microelectrode arrays to probe neuronal responses in anesthetized or awake, behaving primates and other mammals, comparing activity under stimuli that promote filling-in (e.g., large uniform surrounds) versus non-filling conditions (e.g., isolated spots or gaps). Key observations reveal sustained neural firing in deafferented cortical zones driven by lateral inputs from adjacent areas, with preserved selectivity for stimulus features like orientation.
In primate studies, recordings from the V1 representation of the optic disk in macaque monkeys have demonstrated robust responses to visual stimuli surrounding the blind spot. For instance, in alert and anesthetized animals, neurons in this deafferented region fired action potentials when a moving bar traversed the blind spot, reflecting activation via dynamic receptive field surrounds rather than direct stimulation. These responses persisted for the duration of the surround stimulus, indicating active propagation of signals into the blind spot's cortical map. Orientation selectivity was maintained in these activations, mirroring properties of neighboring V1 neurons. No neural activity was elicited by stimuli confined strictly to the blind spot itself, underscoring the reliance on lateral spread for filling-in.
Additional primate recordings in awake, fixating monkeys confirmed sustained firing in blind spot V1 neurons to large, stationary disks or gratings encompassing the blind spot, with response strength scaling with surround size and contrast but independent of the blind spot's direct coverage. These findings highlight how V1 activity encodes surface properties for perceptual completion, with a subset of sampled neurons showing reliable surround-driven responses. Such persistent activity in deafferented zones aligns with isomorphic filling-in mechanisms.
In studies using retinal lesions to create chronic scotomas in macaque monkeys, multi-electrode recordings from V1 revealed early lateral spread of activity into lesioned regions, with persistent firing observed within hours to days post-lesion. Neurons in deafferented cortex maintained orientation selectivity during this phase, driven by inputs from intact retinal projections, though long-term reorganization was minimal. These acute responses to surround stimuli exemplify the neural correlates of delayed filling-in at artificial scotomas. Methods involved chronic implants in awake animals, allowing comparison of pre- and post-lesion activity under controlled visual presentations.

Psychophysical and Human Imaging Studies

Psychophysical experiments have demonstrated filling-in through paradigms that stabilize retinal images or induce perceptual disappearance. In gaze-contingent scotoma studies, an artificial blind spot is created by removing visual information from the region of fixation, leading to the perceptual filling-in of the scotoma with surrounding texture after prolonged viewing, as first observed in early stabilization experiments. This stabilization effect, where peripheral targets fade and are replaced by background, occurs within seconds and relies on sustained fixation, highlighting the role of neural adaptation in perceptual completion.
Recent psychophysical studies as of 2024 have shown that multisensory integration, such as auditory cues, can enhance filling-in at the natural blind spot for dynamic visual stimuli, suggesting broader integration beyond purely visual processes.
Delayed filling-in is evident in masking paradigms, where a target stimulus fades after its offset due to surrounding mask dynamics, with subjective reports indicating gradual spread of background features into the target region over 1-3 seconds. Perceptual reports in Troxler fading tasks, involving eccentric fixation on low-contrast targets, confirm this temporal progression, as participants describe the target dissolving into the homogeneous surround. Similarly, motion-induced blindness experiments show intermittent target suppression amid rotating patterns, where filling-in manifests as the background perceptually overwriting the static target, modulated by attention and contrast levels.
Functional MRI (fMRI) studies reveal neural correlates of filling-in in early visual cortex. In experiments using artificial scotomas, activation spreads retinotopically in V1 corresponding to the filled region, particularly for color filling-in, where signals in V1 exceed those in higher areas like V2 during the perceptual spread. For visual phantoms, fMRI shows enhanced activity in V1 and V2 retinotopic maps aligned with the occluded area, indicating automatic neural completion independent of attention.
The lateral occipital complex (LOC) contributes to amodal completion aspects of filling-in, where occluded shapes are perceived as continuous without luminance change. fMRI adaptation in LOC shows reduced responses to repeated amodally completed objects, indicating representation of the inferred whole form beyond retinal input. These methodsfMRI for spatial mapping, EEG for temporal dynamics, and subjective reports in stabilization taskscollectively provide non-invasive evidence of filling-in as an active perceptual process in humans.
