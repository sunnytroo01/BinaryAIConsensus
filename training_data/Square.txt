 A square is a regular quadrilateral in Euclidean geometry, defined as a polygon with four equal-length sides and four right angles of 90 degrees each. This makes it both equilateral (all sides congruent) and equiangular (all angles equal), distinguishing it as the only regular polygon with an even number of sides that is also a rectangle and a rhombus.
Key properties of the square include its diagonals, which are congruent in length, perpendicular to each other, and bisect the vertex angles and each other at right angles and at their midpoints. Each diagonal divides the square into two congruent 45-45-90 right triangles. The perimeter of a square with side length $ s $ is $ 4s $, while its area is $ s^2 $. These attributes contribute to the square's high degree of symmetry, belonging to the dihedral group $ D_4 $, with rotational symmetries of 90, 180, and 270 degrees, as well as four lines of reflection.
Historically, the square was formalized in ancient Greek mathematics, notably in Euclid's Elements (circa 300 BCE), where it serves as a foundational figure for theorems on congruence, similarity, and area. In modern applications, squares underpin concepts in coordinate geometry, vector spaces, and computer graphics, and they form the basis for square lattices in crystallography and tiling patterns that cover the plane without gaps or overlaps.

Definitions and Characterizations

Basic Definition

A square is a regular quadrilateral in Euclidean geometry, characterized by four equal-length sides and four right angles, each measuring 90 degrees. This definition establishes the square as both equilateral and equiangular, distinguishing it as a special case among polygons.
Unlike a rectangle, which has four right angles but opposite sides of equal length without requiring all sides to be equal, a square mandates uniformity in side lengths. Similarly, a rhombus features four equal sides but does not necessarily have right angles, allowing for non-perpendicular orientations. These distinctions highlight the square's unique balance of equality in both sides and angles.
Visually, a square appears as a flat, four-sided figure with straight edges meeting at precise corners, forming a stable and balanced shape often exemplified by a square with side length $ s $, where all edges measure $ s $. Squares exhibit rotational symmetry as a direct consequence of their equal sides and angles, enabling identical appearance under certain rotations.
In terms of geometric transformations, two squares are congruent if their corresponding sides are equal in length, preserving both shape and size through rigid motions like translation or rotation. All squares are similar, sharing identical angles and proportional sides regardless of scale, which underscores their uniform shape across different sizes.

Etymology and Historical Terminology

The word "square" in the context of geometry derives from the Latin quadratus, meaning "square" or "made square," which is the past participle of quadrare "to square" or "to make square." This Latin term stems from quattuor "four," reflecting the shape's four equal sides and angles, and entered English in the mid-13th century via Old French esquerre or esquarre, originally denoting a tool for measuring right angles before extending to the geometric figure itself.
In ancient mathematics, the square was referred to by terms emphasizing its four-angled structure, such as the Greek tetragnon (), meaning "four-angled" or "four-cornered," a compound of tetra- "four" and gnia "angle." Euclid, in his Elements (c. 300 BCE), formalized the square within Book I, defining it in Definition 22 as "that which is both equilateral and right-angled," and using tetragnon throughout to distinguish it from other quadrilaterals like the rhombus or oblong. Earlier civilizations also employed square shapes in practical measurements; Babylonian mathematicians around 2000 BCE compiled tables of squares for numbers up to 59 on clay tablets, aiding land surveys and calculations, while Egyptian texts from the Middle Kingdom (c. 2000 BCE) used squares to compute areas in administrative papyri for agriculture and construction.
Over time, the terminology evolved to encompass broader uses beyond the geometric shape, with "square" functioning as a verb from the 1530s to mean "to make square" or "to adjust to a right angle," and as a noun in algebra from the late 14th century to denote a "square number" or perfect square, such as 16 as the square of 4. Euclid's Elements marked a key milestone by integrating the square into axiomatic geometry, with propositions like I.46 demonstrating its construction on a given line segment, influencing subsequent mathematical nomenclature across cultures.

Geometric Properties

Sides, Angles, and Diagonals

A square is defined by four sides of equal length, with each pair of adjacent sides perpendicular to one another, forming a closed planar figure. This configuration ensures the boundary consists of straight line segments meeting at right angles, distinguishing the square from other quadrilaterals.
The interior angles of a square each measure exactly 90 degrees, resulting from the perpendicular adjacency of its sides. Consequently, the exterior angles, formed by extending one side beyond a vertex, also measure 90 degrees at each vertex, as the sum of an interior and adjacent exterior angle is 180 degrees.
The diagonals of a square are line segments connecting non-adjacent vertices, opposite corners of the figure. These diagonals are equal in length and bisect each other at a 90-degree angle, intersecting at the center of the square. Additionally, each diagonal bisects the two angles at the vertices it connects, dividing each 90-degree interior angle into two 45-degree angles, and they function as axes of symmetry for the square's geometric structure.

Area, Perimeter, and Diagonal Formulas

The perimeter of a square, which measures the total length around its boundary, is calculated as $ P = 4s $, where $ s $ is the length of each side. This formula arises directly from the square's four equal sides.
The area of a square, representing the space enclosed within its boundaries, is given by $ A = s^2 $. To derive this, divide the square into two congruent right-angled triangles by drawing one diagonal; each triangle has legs of length $ s $ serving as base and height, yielding an area of $ \frac{1}{2} s \cdot s = \frac{1}{2} s^2 $ per triangle, for a total area of $ 2 \times \frac{1}{2} s^2 = s^2 $.
The length of a diagonal $ d $, connecting two opposite vertices, is $ d = s \sqrt{2} $. This is obtained by applying the Pythagorean theorem to the right triangle formed by two adjacent sides and the diagonal:

The Pythagorean theorem is established as Proposition I.47 in Euclid's Elements.
For the unit square with $ s = 1 $, the perimeter is 4, the area is 1, and the diagonal is $ \sqrt{2} $. Under uniform scaling by a factor $ k > 0 $, linear measures such as the perimeter and diagonal scale proportionally by $ k $ (yielding $ P' = 4ks $ and $ d' = k \sqrt{2} $), while the area scales by $ k^2 $ (yielding $ A' = k^2 s^2 $).

Rotations maintain orientation, while reflections reverse it, but both types rigidly superimpose the square on its original position. Relation to Inscribed and Circumscribed Circles

A square admits both an inscribed circle, known as the incircle, and a circumscribed circle, known as the circumcircle. The incircle is tangent to all four sides of the square, with its center at the square's center of symmetry. For a square with side length $ s $, the radius $ r $ of the incircle is $ r = \frac{s}{2} $, derived directly from the geometry where the diameter equals the side length. The area of the incircle is $ \pi r^2 = \pi \left( \frac{s}{2} \right)^2 = \frac{\pi s^2}{4} $, which is $ \frac{\pi}{4} $ times the area of the square $ s^2 $.
The circumcircle passes through all four vertices of the square, also centered at the square's center. Its radius $ R $ is $ R = \frac{s \sqrt{2}}{2} $, obtained by halving the diagonal length $ d = s \sqrt{2} $, as the diagonal spans the diameter of the circumcircle. Thus, $ R = \frac{d}{2} $. The area of the circumcircle is $ \pi R^2 = \pi \left( \frac{s \sqrt{2}}{2} \right)^2 = \frac{\pi s^2}{2} $, or $ \frac{\pi}{2} $ times the square's area.
Among all rectangles inscribed in a fixed circle (sharing the same circumradius), the square achieves the maximum area. This follows from the constraint $ l^2 + w^2 = 4R^2 $ for length $ l $ and width $ w $, where the area $ A = l w $ is maximized when $ l = w = s = 2R / \sqrt{2} $, yielding $ A = 2 R^2 $.

Compass and Straightedge Construction

In classical Euclidean geometry, the construction of a square using only a compass and straightedge, given a line segment as its side, is a fundamental operation that demonstrates the existence of such a figure on any finite straight line. This method, which requires erecting perpendiculars, transferring lengths, and drawing parallels, ensures the resulting quadrilateral has four equal sides and four right angles. The procedure is rigorously outlined in Euclid's Elements, Book I, Proposition 46, where it serves as a building block for further geometric developments, such as applications in Books II, VI, and XIII.
To construct the square on a given straight line segment AB, proceed as follows:

At endpoint A, erect a perpendicular AC to AB. This is accomplished by setting the compass to a radius greater than half AB, drawing arcs centered at A and B to intersect at points equidistant from the line, and connecting those points to form the perpendicular (relying on Proposition I.11 for the right angle).

Along the perpendicular AC, mark point D such that AD equals AB. Transfer the length AB using the compass by placing one leg at A and adjusting the other to B, then replicating that opening from A along AC (per Proposition I.3).

Through D, draw line DE parallel to AB. This parallel is constructed by copying alternate angles or using corresponding segments with the compass and straightedge (as in Proposition I.31).

Through B, draw line BE parallel to AD, again using the parallel construction method. The intersection of BE and DE at point E completes quadrilateral ADEB as the square.

The validity of this construction is proven by verifying that ADEB is equilateral (all sides AB = BE = ED = DA by direct equality and parallelogram properties) and rectangular (angle BAD is right by construction, angle ADE is right via the properties of parallels and transversals per Proposition I.29, and opposite angles are equal per Proposition I.34). Thus, ADEB satisfies the definition of a square as a regular quadrilateral with equal sides and right angles. This approach requires approximately six circle drawings and four straight lines, though alternative methods using fewer steps exist while adhering to the same Euclidean postulates.
A key limitation in compass and straightedge constructions arises when the side length is not pre-given: starting from a unit length (rational), only squares with constructible side lengths can be formed, where constructible numbers are those generated by finite iterations of addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, and square roots over the rationals, yielding quadratic field extensions. Irrational lengths like 2 (the diagonal of a unit square) are constructible, but arbitrary irrationals, such as , cannot be achieved without incorporating the desired length as an initial element, as they do not lie in any such tower of quadratic extensions. This restriction underscores the algebraic foundations of Euclidean constructions, limiting them to numbers algebraic of degree a power of 2 over the rationals.

Applications

In Architecture, Art, and Design

In Islamic architecture, squares form the foundational elements of intricate geometric patterns used for tiling floors, walls, and windows, often interlaced with circles and stars to create repeating motifs that symbolize unity and infinity. These designs, prominent in mosques and madrasas from the 8th century onward, emphasize symmetry for aesthetic harmony and are evident in structures like the Alhambra in Granada, where girih tiles facilitate complex star patterns.
The square base has been a staple in monumental architecture for stability and symbolic representation of the earthly plane. The Great Pyramid of Giza, constructed around 25802560 BCE, features a nearly square base measuring approximately 230 meters on each side, providing a level foundation on uneven terrain and aligning with cardinal directions. In modern city planning, grid layouts based on square blocks promote efficient land use and navigation; William Penn's 1682 plan for Philadelphia divided the city into a rectangular grid of squares to foster orderly growth and communal spaces. Similarly, James Oglethorpe's 1733 design for Savannah, Georgia, incorporated a grid of wards centered around public squares, blending functionality with green areas for social interaction.
In art, squares underpin perspective techniques to simulate three-dimensional space on a two-dimensional surface. During the Renaissance, artists like Albrecht Drer employed square grids as underdrawings to ensure proportional accuracy in linear perspective, as detailed in his 1525 treatise Underweysung der Messung, where a grid of squares helps map objects receding to a vanishing point. Piet Mondrian's Neoplasticist works from the 1920s, such as Composition with Red, Blue, and Yellow (1930), reduce forms to intersecting black lines forming squares and rectangles filled with primary colors, embodying universal harmony through geometric abstraction.
Square-based modularity enhances adaptability in furniture design by allowing interchangeable components that maximize space efficiency. In mid-20th-century Scandinavian modernism, designers like Alvar Aalto used square modules in shelving systems, such as the 1933 Artek collection, to create customizable units that stack and reconfigure without waste. Early digital art relied on pixelsfundamental square units of color on raster displaysto build images, originating in the 1960s with NASA's color imaging systems and evolving into pixel art by the 1970s through limited-resolution computer graphics. This square grid structure, seen in pioneering works like those from the New York Institute of Technology's computer art lab, constrained yet inspired minimalist compositions in video games and algorithmic drawings.

Related Topics

Quadrature and Squaring the Circle

In mathematics, quadrature refers to the historical problem of constructing a square with the same area as a given polygon or curved figure using geometric methods.
The specific challenge of squaring the circle involves constructing, with compass and straightedge, a square whose area equals that of a given circle of radius $ r $, or r2\pi r^2r2. This task originated in ancient Greek mathematics as one of the three classical problems of antiquity, alongside angle trisection and cube duplication.
Early attempts date to the fifth century BCE, when Antiphon the Sophist proposed inscribing polygons within a circle and iteratively doubling the number of sides to approximate the circle's area, though his method incorrectly assumed the process could exhaust the circle's curvature in finitely many steps. Around 440 BCE, Hippocrates of Chios advanced the problem by demonstrating the quadrature of certain lunescrescent-shaped regions bounded by circular arcssuch as those formed by a semicircle and segments of circles with diameters equal to the semicircle's sides, showing these areas equal that of a right triangle. In the third century BCE, Archimedes provided a practical approximation by inscribing and circumscribing regular 96-gons around a circle, yielding bounds $ 3 \frac{10}{71} < \pi < 3 \frac{1}{7} $, which allowed construction of a near-equivalent square via polygonal areas.
The impossibility of exact compass-and-straightedge quadrature was established in 1882 by Ferdinand von Lindemann, who proved \pi is transcendentalmeaning it is not the root of any non-zero polynomial with rational coefficientsimplying no such algebraic construction exists, as constructible lengths are algebraic.
In modern mathematics, while exact geometric quadrature remains impossible, numerical methods approximate the circle's area for computational purposes, such as Monte Carlo integration, which estimates \pi by randomly sampling points in a square enclosing a unit circle and computing the ratio of points inside the circle.

Tiling, Packing, and Dissection

Squares form one of the three regular tessellations of the Euclidean plane, known as the square tiling or square grid, where congruent squares are arranged such that four meet at each vertex with 90-degree angles. This monohedral tiling uses a single prototile shapethe squareand covers the plane without gaps or overlaps, exhibiting translational, rotational, and reflectional symmetries. The tiling's vertex figure is itself a square, and it corresponds to the Schlfli symbol {4,4}, highlighting its uniform polyhedral properties in two dimensions.
In packing problems, squares achieve the maximum possible density of 1 when arranged in a square lattice to fill the entire plane without voids, as they perfectly tessellate the space. For finite containers, such as packing equal smaller squares into a larger square, the optimal arrangements minimize wasted space, with known configurations for small numbers like n=1 (trivial) or n=4 (2x2 grid). Packing equal squares into a circle is more challenging due to curvature, often requiring rotated placements to approach higher densities, though exact optima remain open for many n.
Dissection of squares involves cutting them into pieces that reassemble into other shapes, governed by the BolyaiGerwien theorem, which states that any two polygons of equal area, including squares, can be dissected into each other using finitely many polygonal pieces. A notable case is "squaring the square," the problem of tiling a square with smaller squares of unequal sizes and integer sides (perfect squared square); the simplest such tiling uses 21 distinct squares and was discovered by A. J. W. Duijvestijn in 1978 via computer search, proving it the lowest-order simple perfect example. For three-dimensional extensions involving cubic dissections, the Dehn invariant provides an obstruction, determining when polyhedra of equal volume, such as cubes derived from squares, can be dissected into each other, as resolved in Hilbert's third problem.

Squares in Other Geometries

In hyperbolic geometry, squares are regular quadrilaterals with four equal-length sides and four congruent interior angles, each measuring less than 90 degrees, as the total angle sum for any quadrilateral falls below 360 degrees due to the negative curvature of the space. This contrasts with Euclidean squares, where angles are exactly 90 degrees, and arises from the hyperbolic parallel postulate allowing more parallel lines through a point not on a given line. In the Poincar disk model, which represents the hyperbolic plane as the interior of a unit disk with hyperbolic lines as circular arcs orthogonal to the boundary circle, such squares can be constructed using geodesic segments of equal length, and their vertices lie within the disk. Tilings of the hyperbolic plane with these squares are feasible, permitting five or more squares to meet at a vertex, satisfying the condition that the angle defect allows infinite tessellations without gaps or overlaps, as governed by the inequality $ \frac{2}{k} + \frac{2}{n} < 1 $ for $ n $-gons with $ k $ meeting at each vertex.
Spherical geometry, characterized by positive curvature on the surface of a sphere, defines squares as quadrilaterals bounded by four great circle arcs of equal length, with interior angles exceeding 90 degrees and a total angle sum greater than 360 degrees, reflecting the spherical excess proportional to the enclosed area. For a small spherical square near the sphere's surface, the angles approach 90 degrees, but larger ones exhibit increased angular measure. These properties stem from the geometry's parallel postulate, where great circles (the "lines") always intersect, leading to no true parallels and forcing angles to bulge outward compared to planar figures. The dihedral group symmetries of Euclidean squares adapt here to rotations and reflections preserving great circles.
In taxicab geometry, also known as Manhattan geometry, the metric $ d((x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2)) = |x_1 - x_2| + |y_1 - y_2| $ measures distances along axis-aligned paths, transforming familiar shapes: the unit circle appears as a diamond (a square rotated 45 degrees relative to the coordinate axes) with vertices at $ (\pm 1, 0) $ and $ (0, \pm 1) $. Consequently, "squares" in this geometry are equilateral and equiangular figures under the taxicab metric, manifesting as diamonds in the Euclidean embedding, where sides follow the metric's grid-like paths and right angles are preserved in the L1 norm sense. This redefinition highlights how the geometry prioritizes horizontal and vertical movements, altering perimeter and area calculationsfor example, the taxicab perimeter of such a square doubles the Euclidean diagonal length.
The concept of a square extends to higher dimensions as the n-dimensional hypercube, with the 4-dimensional case known as the tesseract serving as the direct analog. A tesseract consists of 8 cubic cells (3D faces), 24 square faces (2D faces), 32 edges, and 16 vertices, each vertex connecting to 4 edges, mirroring the connectivity progression from square (4 edges per vertex in 2D) to cube (3 edges per vertex in 3D). Its volume is given by side length $ s $ raised to the fourth power, $ V = s^4 $, and it exhibits hyperoctahedral symmetry, generalizing the square's dihedral group $ D_4 $. Projections of the tesseract into 3D space reveal nested cubes connected by edges, illustrating its extra-dimensional structure without violating lower-dimensional embedding constraints.
