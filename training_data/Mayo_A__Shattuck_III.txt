 Mayo A. Shattuck III is an American businessman specializing in investment banking and energy, known for leading Constellation Energy Group as its Chairman, President, and CEO from 2001 to 2012, during which he oversaw the company's expansion and its $11 billion merger with Exelon Corporation. Born around 1954 in Hingham, Massachusetts, Shattuck earned a B.A. from Williams College and an M.B.A. from Stanford University before advancing through senior roles at Deutsche Bank, including Global Head of Investment Banking, Global Head of Private Banking, and Chairman and CEO of Deutsche Bank Alex. Brown, from which he resigned in September 2001. Following the Constellation-Exelon merger, he served as Executive Chairman of Exelon until 2012 and non-executive Chairman until his retirement from the board in 2022.
Shattuck's tenure at Constellation emphasized contrarian strategies that positioned the firm as a top performer in the competitive energy sector amid deregulation and market volatility, culminating in the transformative acquisition by Exelon that created one of the largest U.S. utilities. He currently chairs the board of Gap Inc., where he also leads the Audit and Finance Committee, and serves as a director at Capital One Financial Corporation, drawing on his extensive experience in finance and corporate governance. Shattuck has also contributed to philanthropy, co-chairing the Johns Hopkins University capital campaign.

Early Life and Education

Early Life

Mayo A. Shattuck III was born in Boston, Massachusetts, in 1954, to Mayo A. Shattuck Jr. and Jane Shattuck. He grew up in Hingham, Massachusetts, a community with longstanding family connections, as both his father and grandfather were local residents.
Shattuck's upbringing occurred in a household shaped by civic involvement and business influences, with his family maintaining ties to the Boston investment community. His mother, Jane, born to Swedish immigrants in Worcester in 1926, married his father in 1947 and focused on raising the family in Hingham while engaging in local volunteerism. This environment, rooted in New England traditions of commerce and community leadership, provided early exposure to financial and entrepreneurial principles without formal professional pursuits at the time.

Education

Shattuck earned a Bachelor of Arts degree from Williams College in 1976. At the college, he demonstrated leadership by serving as president of the student government in his senior year and captaining the tennis team.
He then pursued graduate studies at Stanford Graduate School of Business, receiving a Master of Business Administration in 1980 and graduating as an Arjay Miller Scholar, an honor recognizing distinction in the program. This combination of a liberal arts undergraduate foundation and advanced business training equipped him with analytical skills and economic insights essential for navigating complex financial markets.

Professional Career

Investment Banking Roles

Shattuck joined Alex. Brown & Sons in November 1985 as head of its corporate finance division in the San Francisco office, where he focused on technology sector deals amid the firm's growing emphasis on emerging high-growth industries. His rapid ascent positioned him as a key figure in the firm's expansion into Silicon Valley startups, contributing to Alex. Brown's reputation as a lead underwriter for initial public offerings in technology. In 1986, shortly after his arrival, the firm co-underwrote Microsoft's IPO, which raised $61 million and marked an early bet on software innovation. By 1992, under his oversight, Alex. Brown led the AOL IPO, raising $16.5 million and capitalizing on the potential of online services.
Promoted to president, chief operating officer, and director in 1991, Shattuck drove operational growth, with the firm valued at approximately $150 million at that time and anticipating participation in 140 IPOs and follow-on offerings by 1995. Revenue surged 31 percent in 1996 alone, reflecting expanded deal volumes and client acquisition in equity underwriting, where Alex. Brown ranked as the sixth-largest U.S. stock underwriter by dollar volume entering 1997. This period solidified the firm's prowess in tech-driven markets, broadening its institutional and corporate client base beyond traditional sectors.
In September 1997, Alex. Brown merged with Bankers Trust in a $1.7 billion transaction, enhancing global capabilities in private wealth management and corporate finance. Following Deutsche Bank's 1999 acquisition of Bankers Trust, Shattuck assumed the role of Chairman of Deutsche Bank Alex. Brown, while also serving as global head of investment banking and private banking, managing integration efforts across U.S. operations, including oversight of approximately 400 brokers. He advocated for preserving the Alex. Brown brand during the transition, though cultural melding with the German parent proved challenging. Shattuck resigned from these positions in September 2001.

Leadership in the Energy Sector

Shattuck assumed the role of President and Chief Executive Officer of Constellation Energy Group, Inc., effective November 1, 2001, and was elected Chairman of the Board in July 2002. Under his leadership, the company operated a generation portfolio dominated by nuclear assets, which comprised over 60% of output and included approximately 3,869 megawatts from facilities such as the two-unit Calvert Cliffs plant in Maryland and the two-unit Nine Mile Point facility in New York, plus the single-unit Ginna plant. This focus emphasized nuclear's high capacity factorsoften exceeding 90%providing dispatchable baseload power essential for grid stability, in contrast to the intermittency of renewables requiring backup capacity and storage to achieve equivalent reliability.
During the 2008-2009 period, Constellation encountered severe headwinds from the global financial crisis and energy market volatility, including sharp declines in natural gas prices that devalued hedging contracts tied to power sales. The company reported a $1.4 billion net loss for the fourth quarter of 2008equivalent to $7.75 per sharedriven by costs from terminating a proposed joint venture with MidAmerican Energy Holdings and broader hedging ineffectiveness. Constellation's stock price plummeted, closing at $22.30 on February 18, 2009, after an 8.1% single-day drop, amid fears of liquidity strains that nearly precipitated bankruptcy. Shattuck acknowledged personal accountability for inadequate risk oversight in volatile commodity markets and steered the firm through survival via a $1 billion equity infusion from EDF Group in late 2008, preserving nuclear operations without federal bailout.
Shattuck orchestrated the merger of Constellation with Exelon Corporation, announced in April 2011 and finalized on March 12, 2012, in a $7.9 billion all-stock transaction that created the nation's largest nuclear fleet with over 20 reactors and roughly 19,000 megawatts of capacity. He transitioned to Executive Chairman of the combined Exelon, later becoming non-executive Chairman, roles that amplified economies of scale in fuel procurement, maintenance, and regulatory compliance, thereby bolstering nuclear plants' cost-competitiveness and uptime amid fluctuating demand. This consolidation empirically improved resilience to price swings, as larger portfolios diversify revenue streams and leverage fixed nuclear operating costsestimated at 60-70% capacity utilization breakevenagainst renewables' higher system integration expenses for intermittency mitigation. Shattuck retired as Exelon's Chairman on April 26, 2022, following oversight of the entity's strategic realignments, including the eventual spin-off of Constellation's generation assets.

Post-Executive Board Roles

Following the March 2012 merger of Constellation Energy Group with Exelon Corporation, Mayo A. Shattuck III assumed the role of Executive Chairman of Exelon's board of directors, providing transitional leadership during the integration of the two entities. In February 2013, he shifted to non-executive Chairman, relinquishing day-to-day executive responsibilities while retaining oversight of board governance and strategic direction.
Shattuck's tenure as non-executive Chairman, spanning from 2013 to April 26, 2022, emphasized enhanced board independence and alignment with shareholder interests amid evolving regulatory and market pressures in the utility sector. He presided over board deliberations on enterprise risk management, including nuclear operations and competitive generation assets, contributing to the framework for separating Exelon's regulated utility businesses from its merchant energy operations.
A pivotal achievement under Shattuck's chairmanship was the oversight of Exelon's February 2022 spin-off of its competitive energy generation business as an independent Constellation Energy Corporation, which streamlined corporate structure and focused Exelon on regulated transmission and distribution utilities. This transaction, valued at over $20 billion in market capitalization for the spun-off entity, reflected board-guided strategic realignment to mitigate risks from volatile wholesale energy markets while complying with federal antitrust conditions imposed during the original merger. His leadership ensured continuity in governance practices, including annual evaluations of executive compensation tied to performance metrics such as safety, reliability, and financial returns.

Public Policy and Advocacy

Energy and Economic Policy Views

Shattuck has consistently advocated for nuclear power as a critical source of reliable, low-emissions baseload energy, emphasizing its empirical advantages in capacity factors and operational stability over intermittent renewables or fossil fuel alternatives. During his tenure as CEO of Constellation Energy from 2001 to 2009, the company, which operated one of the largest nuclear fleets in the United States, pursued expansions including plans to construct new reactors at the Calvert Cliffs site in Marylandthe first such U.S. build in over three decades if realizedhighlighting nuclear's role in meeting steady demand without subsidies distorting market signals for alternatives like wind or solar, which require backups and yield lower utilization rates. Constellation's nuclear assets under Shattuck demonstrated high performance, with the fleet contributing to the company's competitive edge in deregulated markets by providing dispatchable power that avoids the variability and emissions variability of gas peakers or subsidized intermittent sources.
Shattuck criticized regulatory overreach that distorts energy markets, particularly in cases where political interventions override economic incentives and private investment decisions. In 2006, Maryland regulators imposed stringent conditions on Constellation's proposed $11 billion merger with FPL Group, including mandates on power plant investments and rate structures that created excessive uncertainties, prompting Shattuck to terminate the deal as the "risks and uncertainties were too significant to overcome." This episode exemplified how state-level political pressures, such as demands for concessions unrelated to core merger economics, hinder efficient capital allocation and long-term planning in competitive energy sectors, contrasting with freer market dynamics that Constellation leveraged for growth.
On broader economic policy, Shattuck supported deregulation to foster private innovation and market-driven efficiency in energy production and trading. Constellation's expansion into competitive, deregulated wholesale markets under his leadershipyielding significant growth despite early industry volatility post-Enronunderscored his view that reducing government barriers enables firms to optimize assets like nuclear plants through voluntary partnerships, such as the 2009 nuclear collaboration with lectricit de France (EDF), rather than relying on protected monopolies or heavy-handed subsidies. He testified before Maryland's Public Service Commission in 2009 on the feasibility of new nuclear builds, arguing for policies that align costs with market realities over punitive oversight, positioning nuclear as economically viable when unburdened by excessive intervention. This free-market orientation extended to critiques of trading shakeouts, where Shattuck anticipated consolidation favoring disciplined players over speculative excess, promoting innovation through risk-managed competition.

Broader Civic Engagement

Shattuck served as chairman of the board of visitors for the University of Maryland, Baltimore County (UMBC) from approximately 2004 to 2014, guiding institutional priorities in a region-dependent civic context. In recognition of his contributions to public service, UMBC awarded him an honorary Doctor of Public Service degree.
As a national trustee for The First Tee, Shattuck supported initiatives fostering youth character development through golf programs, emphasizing life skills alongside economic accessibility in underserved communities. His involvement reflected a commitment to nonpartisan civic structures promoting individual agency over centralized oversight, aligning with data showing such programs correlate with improved youth outcomes in metrics like graduation rates and employability.
In Baltimore, Shattuck pursued broader public service opportunities upon relocating for professional roles, viewing them as extensions of market-oriented leadership to enhance local vitality amid urban economic pressures. He was considered for the CEO position at the U.S. Olympic Committee in 2001 but opted against it, prioritizing operational demands over expanded national administrative duties. Critics of such pro-business civic profiles argue for greater regulatory emphasis to mitigate inequality, though empirical evidence from similar engagements links them to sustained regional GDP growth without proportional increases in oversight burdens.

Controversies and Criticisms

Business Performance Challenges

During Shattuck's tenure as CEO of Constellation Energy Group from 2001 to 2012, the company encountered significant operational challenges, particularly in its energy trading and hedging activities, which were exposed during the 2008 financial crisis. In August and September 2008, Constellation faced a severe liquidity crisis exacerbated by volatile commodity prices and tightened credit markets, resulting in a nearly 75% drop in its stock price from around $100 per share at the start of the year to the low $20s. This downturn was linked to the firm's aggressive integration of power generation operations with speculative trading strategies, which failed to adequately hedge against rapid swings in natural gas and power prices, leading to mark-to-market losses and inability to roll over short-term debt.
The crisis culminated in a $1.4 billion net loss for the fourth quarter of 2008, or $7.75 per share, compared to a $258 million profit ($1.42 per share) in the same period of 2007, prompting Shattuck to publicly accept responsibility for insufficient oversight in exiting a proposed merger with MidAmerican Energy and broader risk exposures. Critics attributed these outcomes to internal mismanagement, including over-reliance on complex hedging portfolios that amplified vulnerabilities rather than mitigating them, as evidenced by the company's need for emergency capital infusions and dividend cuts to preserve cash. Into 2009, first-quarter results showed continued weakness with a swing to losses tied to divestiture charges and lingering merger fallout, though defenders, including Shattuck, emphasized external factors like the Lehman Brothers collapse and unprecedented credit dislocation as primary drivers, arguing that Constellation's physical asset base provided a foundation for eventual stabilization without bankruptcy.
Executive compensation under Shattuck also drew scrutiny amid these performance shortfalls, with shareholders rejecting his 2010 pay packagevalued at $15.7 million, including base salary, incentives, and equityin a May 2011 advisory vote, where only 38.6% supported it due to perceived misalignment with shareholder returns. Influential proxy advisors cited pay-for-performance disconnects, noting the increase from $10.9 million in 2009 despite the prior year's stock collapse and losses, though benchmarks against peers in the merchant energy sector were not uniformly lower, as many faced similar market headwinds. Proponents countered that Shattuck's 2008 pay cutfrom prior levels to reflect crisis impactsand subsequent equity-heavy structure tied rewards to long-term value creation, such as post-2009 debt reduction and asset sales that positioned the firm for its 2012 merger with Exelon. This debate highlighted tensions between short-term metrics like stock volatility and structural reforms implemented under Shattuck, including enhanced weekly risk reviews to address delegation gaps exposed by the hedging breakdowns.

Regulatory and Deal-Related Disputes

In 2006, Constellation Energy Group, under CEO Mayo A. Shattuck III, announced a proposed merger with FPL Group valued at approximately $10.8 billion, aimed at creating a larger entity with enhanced capabilities for electricity generation and transmission, including potential investments in new capacity to meet growing demand. The deal faced significant opposition from the Maryland Public Service Commission (PSC), which scrutinized its potential impact on Baltimore Gas and Electric (BGE) customers amid concurrent proposals for a 72% electricity rate increase by BGE to fund infrastructure and generation upgrades. Shattuck cited "risks and uncertainties" from ongoing political and judicial battles in Maryland, including legislative overrides of gubernatorial vetoes on rate stabilization measures, as reasons for termination on October 25, 2006, after months of limbo since May.
Pro-market analyses argued that such regulatory interventions, prioritizing short-term consumer rate protection over long-term supply reliability, discouraged mergers that could have accelerated new generation capacitypotentially including nuclear expansionsamid rising U.S. energy needs, with Constellation's fleet already demonstrating nuclear safety through zero core damage accidents at plants like Calvert Cliffs since 1975. Critics of the rejection, including energy executives, contended that Maryland's focus on rate caps ignored causal links between underinvestment and future blackouts, as evidenced by similar regulatory hurdles delaying capacity additions elsewhere. In contrast, consumer advocates and PSC rationales emphasized preventing cost shifts from the merger onto ratepayers, viewing it as a safeguard against monopolistic pricing in a deregulated market, though subsequent rate stabilization laws overrode initial hikes without addressing the lost synergies.
During the 2011-2012 Exelon-Constellation merger, valued at about $7 billion in stock, Shattuck negotiated amid Maryland PSC demands for concessions to secure approval, including commitments to develop 285-300 megawatts of new in-state generation (primarily renewables) and adherence to rate stabilization settlements to limit BGE customer impacts. Exelon initially resisted conditions tying the deal to extended operations at the Calvert Cliffs nuclear plant, arguing they exceeded standard merger reviews, but ultimately agreed to long-term supply assurances, enabling PSC approval in August 2011 and closure in March 2012. These concessions facilitated sustained nuclear output, with Calvert Cliffs providing baseload power at low marginal costs and a safety record including no radiation releases beyond licensed limits, countering environmental groups' anti-nuclear concerns that often amplified rare incidents despite empirical data showing nuclear's lower fatality rate per terawatt-hour than fossil fuels. Post-merger outcomes included preserved energy supply reliability in Maryland, though pro-market observers critiqued the process for injecting political conditions that raised transaction costs and delayed integration, potentially hindering broader nuclear relicensing efforts.

Board Affiliations and Directorships

Key Corporate Boards

Shattuck has served as a director of Gap Inc. since 2002 and as Chairman of its Board of Directors, with leadership in the Audit and Finance Committee focusing on financial oversight and risk management. During his tenure, Gap navigated retail sector challenges, including a 2022 executive reshuffle amid sales declines, where Shattuck continued as lead independent director to ensure governance continuity. Gap's stock price, adjusted for splits and dividends, traded around $20$30 per share in 2002 and hovered near $20 as of late 2025, reflecting stagnant long-term returns amid e-commerce shifts and brand repositioning efforts, though specific board-driven pivots like cost controls are not directly attributed in public records.
As a director of Capital One Financial Corporation since October 30, 2003, Shattuck contributes to the Compensation Committee and Governance and Nominating Committee, drawing on his investment banking and executive experience for strategic and risk-related decisions. Under his board service, Capital One expanded from a subprime-focused lender to a diversified financial institution with assets exceeding $400 billion by 2025, coinciding with stock price growth from approximately $25 (split-adjusted) in 2003 to over $200, driven by acquisitions like ING Direct in 2012 and regulatory adaptations post-2008 crisis, though firm performance metrics emphasize overall management execution rather than isolated directorial input.
Shattuck joined the board of Hut 8 Corp., a digital infrastructure company specializing in high-performance computing and energy-intensive operations, as an independent director in 2023. His role aligns with expertise in capital markets and energy-adjacent sectors, supporting governance during Hut 8's merger with US Bitcoin Corp. in November 2023, which expanded its data center footprint; the company's shares experienced volatility typical of crypto-related firms, rising from merger-related lows but remaining below pre-merger peaks as of 2025, with no verifiable causal links to Shattuck's specific contributions. These directorships underscore Shattuck's focus on financial services and retail governance outside energy utilities.

Philanthropic Contributions

Major Initiatives and Organizations

Shattuck served as president of the Alex. Brown & Sons Charitable Foundation from 1991 to 1997, overseeing the distribution of its approximately $10 million corpus to Baltimore-area cultural, health, and community institutions, including grants that supported local infrastructure and programs aimed at enhancing civic engagement and economic vitality in underserved neighborhoods. By 2002, the foundation had allocated an additional $11 million specifically for city initiatives, prioritizing attachments to Baltimore's heritage and long-term community stewardship over short-term aid, with recipients selected to foster sustainable growth rather than dependency.
As a national trustee of The First Tee, Shattuck has backed the nonprofit's expansion of character-building programs that integrate golf instruction with life skills training for youth, reaching over 2 million participants annually across chapters by emphasizing values like resilience and goal-setting, which correlate with improved academic performance and employability in longitudinal studies of similar interventions. These efforts target inner-city and rural communities with low-cost access to facilities, such as the junior golf center in Baltimore where Shattuck participated in its 2000s opening, aligning private funding with evidence-based youth development that promotes self-reliance and economic mobility over redistributive models lacking such targeted outcomes.
Through the Shattuck Family Foundation, established in Baltimore, Shattuck has directed multimillion-dollar grants to educational institutions, including a seven-figure gift to The Bryn Mawr School in 2023 for student programs enhancing critical thinking and leadership, and funding for a women's leadership initiative at Indiana University of Pennsylvania that equips participants with professional skills shown to boost career advancement and earnings potential. These contributions reflect a focus on human capital investment, where private philanthropy enables direct support for merit-based advancement, yielding higher causal efficacy in mobility outcomes compared to broad state interventions, as evidenced by program evaluations demonstrating sustained participant gains in education and employment metrics.
His role as a trustee of Johns Hopkins University since at least the early 2000s, including as vice chair of the Johns Hopkins Medicine Board, has involved stewardship of major capital campaigns, such as the $4.5 billion "Rising to the Challenge" effort concluded in 2018, which allocated over $610 million to student aid and $725 million to faculty endowments, correlating with increased research output and accessibility that drive regional innovation and health improvements. This engagement prioritizes empirical advancements in medicine and education, where donor-supported initiatives have demonstrably accelerated discoveries and enrollment diversity without relying on inefficient public funding mechanisms.

Personal Life

Family and Residences

Mayo A. Shattuck III married Molly George, a former Baltimore Ravens cheerleader, in 1997. The couple separated in early 2014 after 16 years of marriage, with their divorce finalized in November 2014. Shattuck and Molly Shattuck have at least one son together, Spencer Shattuck, who later pursued entrepreneurial ventures including ownership of Towson Watch Company. Shattuck has five children in total, including Mayo IV, Katie, Wyatt, and Lillian.
During their marriage, the Shattucks resided in a historic eight-bedroom Georgian-style mansion in Baltimore's Roland Park neighborhood, which Molly Shattuck listed for sale in 2020 at $4.9 million. This Baltimore residence reflected Shattuck's professional ties to the region through Constellation Energy. Following the divorce, Shattuck relocated his primary residences to Florida, including a property at 11090 Turtle Beach Road in North Palm Beach and a renovated mansion in Jupiter's Bears Club community, which he sold in 2022 for $15 million.
