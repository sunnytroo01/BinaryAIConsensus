Chlorhexidine
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Chlorhexidine is a synthetic cationic bisbiguanide compound with broad-spectrum antimicrobial properties, effective against Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria, fungi such as Candida albicans, and certain viruses. It is included on the World Health Organization's List of Essential Medicines. Its chemical formula is CHClN, featuring two p-chlorophenyl guanide units connected by a hexamethylene bridge, and it is most commonly employed in salt forms like gluconate or digluconate for enhanced water solubility and stability at physiological pH.
As a topical antiseptic, chlorhexidine disrupts microbial cell membranes by binding to negatively charged phosphate groups, leading to increased permeability, leakage of intracellular contents, and eventual cell death, with bactericidal effects at concentrations of 2% or higher. It exhibits substantivity, allowing prolonged adherence to oral tissues and sustained antibacterial action for up to 12 hours, which distinguishes it from other antiseptics like povidone-iodine. In medical and dental applications, it is widely used for preoperative skin preparation to reduce surgical site infections, wound cleansing, and as an oral rinse (typically 0.12% chlorhexidine gluconate) effective as an adjunct to mechanical oral hygiene for reducing plaque accumulation and gingival inflammation. The American Dental Association grants its Seal of Acceptance to certain chlorhexidine products for reducing plaque and gingivitis when used as directed. In prescription-strength 0.12% chlorhexidine gluconate form, it is used as a short-term adjunct to professional dental treatment for periodontitis, although mouthwash alone is insufficient for treating periodontitis.
Despite its efficacy, chlorhexidine can cause common side effects such as extrinsic tooth staining, altered taste sensation, oral mucosal irritation, and increased supragingival calculus formation; these are generally reversible upon discontinuation. Rare but serious allergic reactions, including anaphylaxis, have been reported, prompting FDA warnings for immediate discontinuation and medical attention upon symptoms like hives, swelling, or difficulty breathing; it is contraindicated in those with known hypersensitivity. Safety in pregnancy (Category B), lactation, and children under 18 remains unestablished, and its environmental persistence raises concerns about aquatic toxicity.

Uses

Human Medicine

Chlorhexidine serves as a broad-spectrum antiseptic in human medicine, primarily employed for preventing and treating infections across various clinical settings. It exhibits strong antimicrobial activity against Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria, certain fungi, and enveloped viruses by disrupting their cell membranes and causing precipitation of cellular components, though it is ineffective against bacterial spores. In 2023, chlorhexidine accounted for approximately 912,485 prescriptions in the United States, reflecting its widespread clinical utility.
In surgical contexts, chlorhexidine is a cornerstone for pre-operative skin disinfection to minimize surgical site infections (SSIs). Various formulations are employed depending on the specific purpose. Alcoholic solutions, such as 2% chlorhexidine gluconate in alcohol or 0.5% chlorhexidine gluconate in 70% isopropyl alcohol (often tinted pink to improve visibility of the applied area and ensure complete coverage), are recommended for skin preparation immediately before incision and for surgical hand scrubs, offering faster-acting antimicrobial effects due to the alcohol base and persistent activity lasting several hours. In contrast, aqueous solutions such as 0.2% chlorhexidine gluconate are typically used for general skin cleansing, wound irrigation, or other non-surgical applications. Guidelines recommend using alcoholic chlorhexidine preparations for skin preparation, applied via scrubbing or painting. Preoperative bathing or showering with 4% chlorhexidine solution the night before and morning of surgery further reduces bacterial colonization, with randomized trials demonstrating a 40% relative risk reduction in SSIs compared to povidone-iodine alternatives. For instance, a landmark multicenter trial involving over 1,000 patients showed chlorhexidine-alcohol preparation lowered SSI rates to 9.5% versus 16.1% with povidone-iodine.
Chlorhexidine gluconate 4% (commonly sold as Hibiclens) is frequently recommended as an antiseptic body wash to help treat and prevent bacterial folliculitis by reducing skin bacteria. It is often used for mild cases or as adjunct therapy, applied diluted on affected areas or as a full-body wash. For severe, persistent, or non-bacterial folliculitis, consult a healthcare provider, as oral or topical antibiotics may be required.
For oral health, chlorhexidine mouthwashes and gels are standard for managing gingivitis and periodontitis by reducing plaque accumulation and gingival inflammation. Prescription-strength chlorhexidine gluconate 0.12% (e.g., Peridex or PerioGard) is the most effective antiseptic mouthwash for periodontitis (advanced gum disease), commonly prescribed for short-term use to reduce bacteria, plaque, and inflammation as an adjunct to required professional dental treatment, since mouthwash alone is not sufficient for periodontitis. Chlorhexidine mouthrinse (typically 0.12% chlorhexidine gluconate) is effective as an adjunct to mechanical oral hygiene for reducing plaque and gingivitis. Benefits include significant reduction in plaque accumulation and gingival inflammation. The American Dental Association (ADA) grants its Seal of Acceptance to certain chlorhexidine products for reducing plaque and gingivitis when used as directed. For over-the-counter options, essential oil-based mouthwashes like Listerine Antiseptic have the ADA Seal of Acceptance for helping prevent and reduce gingivitis and supragingival plaque. Clinical trials have established that 0.12% to 0.2% chlorhexidine rinses, used twice daily for 1-2 weeks as an adjunct to mechanical plaque control, significantly decrease plaque index scores by 50-60% and gingival bleeding by up to 40% over placebo or saline. Cochrane reviews support its efficacy in specific contexts (e.g., short-term plaque control and in certain patient populations), but note side effects like staining and taste disturbance; no single Cochrane review focuses solely on general benefits and side effects for routine use. In certain regions, 0.05% chlorhexidine solutions (ready-to-use, requiring no dilution) are employed for oral rinses or mouth baths, particularly for conditions such as gingivitis, stomatitis, or post-tooth extraction care. Typical instructions involve placing 510 ml of the solution in the mouth, holding for 13 minutes (without active rinsing for baths), then spitting out (do not swallow), applied 23 times daily, preferably after meals and after preliminary rinsing with water. Use is recommended only as directed by a physician. Common side effects include extrinsic tooth staining, altered taste sensation, oral mucosal irritation, and increased supragingival calculus formation; these are generally reversible upon discontinuation. Avoid contact with eyes. Chlorhexidine is also used to prevent alveolar osteitis (dry socket) after tooth extractions, with rinses or gels reducing incidence by up to 58% compared to placebo. Additionally, pre-procedural rinses with 0.2% chlorhexidine minimize bacterial aerosols generated during dental procedures, reducing contamination risk for patients and staff. In intensive care units, chlorhexidine-based oral care protocols, involving 0.12% gel applied every 6-8 hours to the oral mucosa, have been shown to reduce ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP) incidence by 30-65% in mechanically ventilated patients, primarily by decreasing oral bacterial load and aspiration risk, as evidenced by meta-analyses of over 3,000 ICU cases.
Despite its established antimicrobial activity in the oral cavity and proven efficacy in reducing bacterial load for dental and respiratory indications, there is no reliable evidence that chlorhexidine mouthwash prevents sexually transmitted infections (STIs) in the throat following oral sex. A single-arm clinical pilot trial found that twice-daily gargling with 0.2% chlorhexidine for 6 days failed to eradicate oropharyngeal gonorrhea in asymptomatic men who have sex with men (0% efficacy; 95% CI 0%-56.1%). Evidence regarding mouthwashes in general for reducing the risk of oral STIs, including gonorrhea, chlamydia, herpes, HIV, HPV, and syphilis, remains weak and inconclusive, with no significant preventive effect demonstrated for most STIs. While chlorhexidine is effective against certain oral pathogens, it is not indicated or effective for post-exposure prophylaxis of oral STIs.
Chlorhexidine also plays a key role in wound care and device-related infection prevention. In wound management, irrigation with 0.05-0.2% solutions cleanses contaminated sites and inhibits bacterial growth, supporting healing in chronic or surgical wounds by reducing bioburden without delaying tissue repair. For urinary catheter maintenance, meatal cleaning with 0.1% chlorhexidine before insertion and during routine care lowers catheter-associated urinary tract infection rates by up to 94%, outperforming saline in randomized studies of hospitalized patients. Neonatal umbilical cord care involves daily application of 4% chlorhexidine to the stump, which meta-analyses of trials in low-resource settings indicate reduces sepsis risk by 23-32% and all-cause mortality by 20% compared to dry cord care, particularly in high-infection environments.
In endodontics, chlorhexidine (typically 0.2-2%) is used as an irrigant during root canal procedures to disinfect the canal system. It effectively eliminates residual bacteria in infected pulp spaces, with in vitro and clinical studies showing comparable antimicrobial efficacy to sodium hypochlorite against common endodontic pathogens like Enterococcus faecalis, while providing substantivity for up to 12 weeks post-treatment. In gynecology, chlorhexidine vaginal preparations (2-4% solutions or gels) treat bacterial vaginosis and prevent postoperative infections. Vaginal cleansing with 4% chlorhexidine before cesarean sections or hysterectomies reduces surgical site infections by 50-60%, as demonstrated in prospective trials, and topical gels improve symptoms in vaginitis cases more effectively than placebo, targeting biofilms without disrupting normal flora excessively.

Veterinary Medicine

Chlorhexidine is extensively applied topically in veterinary medicine to manage skin infections, cleanse wounds, and treat ear conditions in dogs, cats, and livestock such as cattle. In dogs and cats, solutions of 2% to 4% chlorhexidine gluconate are commonly used twice daily for superficial pyoderma or wound irrigation, while ear flushes employ diluted concentrations of 0.05% to 0.2% to minimize ototoxicity risks. For livestock, similar topical preparations aid in wound care and infection control, with application frequencies adjusted based on lesion severity and animal size to ensure efficacy without irritation.
As a disinfectant, chlorhexidine plays a critical role in veterinary surgical procedures, where 2% to 4% solutions, often combined with alcohol, are used for skin preparation to reduce microbial load prior to incision and for sterilizing instruments between uses. In dairy farming, post-milking teat dips containing 0.35% chlorhexidine with emollients prevent bovine mastitis by reducing new intramammary infections from major pathogens like Streptococcus species by 50% and minor pathogens such as coagulase-negative staphylococci by 49% to 65%. In aquaculture, chlorhexidine gluconate at 50 to 100 mg/L inhibits oomycete pathogens like Saprolegnia parasitica and S. australis, controlling saprolegniasis in cultured fish through bath treatments without observed toxicity at minimum inhibitory concentrations.
Chlorhexidine exhibits broad-spectrum activity against gram-positive bacteria (e.g., Staphylococcus aureus) and select gram-negative bacteria (e.g., Escherichia coli, Pseudomonas aeruginosa), with bactericidal effects at 0.1% within 15 seconds, though it is less effective against most fungi, spores, and viruses. In animals, it shows moderate antifungal efficacy against species like Malassezia pachydermatis in canine atopic dermatitis and Fusarium isolates at concentrations of 8 to 32 mg/L. Dosing requires species-specific adjustments, such as lower dilutions for cats to avoid toxicity and higher volumes for larger livestock, ensuring concentrations remain below 4% for skin applications.
In cats, chlorhexidine use carries specific risks, including post-surgical respiratory distress linked to aerosolized particles from scrubs on surgical sites or endotracheal tubes if not thoroughly rinsed. The European Medicines Agency (EMA) guidelines restrict chlorhexidine to topical applications in food-producing animals, noting low residue levels in milk (<50 g/L) and tissues, with no maximum residue limits (MRLs) required due to its inclusion in Annex II of Regulation (EEC) No 2377/90 and an acceptable daily intake (ADI) of 0.005 mg/kg body weight/day.

Pharmacology

Mechanism of Action

Chlorhexidine, a cationic bisbiguanide antiseptic, exerts its antimicrobial effects primarily through electrostatic interactions with negatively charged components on microbial cell surfaces. Its positive charge allows rapid adsorption and binding to phosphate and sulfate groups in bacterial cell walls, facilitating penetration into the cytoplasmic membrane via passive diffusion. This binding disrupts the lipid bilayer integrity, increasing membrane permeability and resulting in the leakage of essential intracellular contents, such as potassium and phosphorus ions, which compromises cellular homeostasis.
The antimicrobial activity of chlorhexidine is concentration-dependent. At low concentrations, it acts bacteriostatically by reversibly inhibiting key enzyme activities, such as those involved in cellular metabolism, without immediate cell death; this effect can be reversed upon removal of the agent. At higher concentrations, it becomes bactericidal through irreversible precipitation of cytoplasmic proteins and other phosphorylated compounds, including ATP and nucleic acids, leading to coagulation and ultimate cell lysis. The membrane disruption process involves initial adsorption to the cell surface, followed by insertion into the phospholipid layer, formation of complexes with membrane lipids, and creation of transient pores that allow efflux of low-molecular-weight solutes; this can be conceptually illustrated as a sequential model where cationic molecules aggregate at the negatively charged outer layer, destabilize the bilayer core, and trigger cascading leakage until cellular integrity fails.
Chlorhexidine demonstrates broad-spectrum activity against Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria, as well as yeasts and enveloped viruses such as HIV and hepatitis B, due to its ability to interact with diverse negatively charged microbial envelopes. However, its efficacy is limited against mycobacteria, owing to their waxy cell walls that impede penetration, and it is ineffective against bacterial spores, which possess impermeable outer layers resistant to disruption. These interactions with phospholipids and proteins underpin its selective disruption of microbial cells while sparing most host tissues, which lack the same density of negatively charged sites.

Pharmacokinetics

Chlorhexidine exhibits minimal systemic absorption when applied topically or orally, with bioavailability typically less than 1% due to its poor penetration through intact skin and gastrointestinal tract. In oral rinses, approximately 30% of the dose is retained in the mouth, but systemic levels remain low, with peak plasma concentrations around 0.206 g/g occurring 30 minutes after a 300 mg dose. This retention contributes to its substantivity on oral mucosa, where chlorhexidine persists at microgram per milliliter levels for up to 12 hours post-rinsing, providing prolonged local effects.
Distribution of chlorhexidine is primarily limited to skin and mucous membranes, where it binds strongly to proteins such as albumin in serum and saliva, as well as to dental pellicle and mucosal surfaces. It does not penetrate deeply into intact skin layers, restricting its spread to superficial tissues and preventing significant entry into deeper systemic circulation. This protein binding aligns with its mechanism of action, facilitating adhesion to negatively charged bacterial membranes and host tissues.
Chlorhexidine undergoes no significant metabolism in the body and is excreted primarily unchanged. Following oral ingestion, over 90% is eliminated via feces, with less than 1% appearing in urine; any absorbed fraction follows similar routes through biliary and renal excretion.
Chlorhexidine exhibits substantivity in oral applications lasting approximately 8-12 hours, due to its gradual release from bound sites. Pharmacokinetics can be influenced by formulation, such as the gluconate salt, which enhances solubility and retention in aqueous environments, and by pH, with optimal binding and release occurring between 5.5 and 7.0.
Repeated use of chlorhexidine may lead to accumulation in tissues like skin and oral mucosa due to its strong binding affinity, resulting in prolonged local antimicrobial effects beyond single applications.

Side Effects and Safety

Adverse Effects

Chlorhexidine can cause various local adverse effects upon topical application, including skin irritation, dryness, and, less commonly, allergic contact dermatitis. Skin irritation and dryness are frequently reported with prolonged use of chlorhexidine solutions or gels, often manifesting as mild erythema or xerosis that resolves upon discontinuation. Allergic contact dermatitis, a type IV hypersensitivity reaction, occurs rarely, with a prevalence of 0.471% in patch-tested populations, and is characterized by eczematous rashes at the site of application. Immediate-type (IgE-mediated) skin reactions, such as urticaria, are even less common but can precede more severe systemic responses.
In oral applications, such as mouthwashes, chlorhexidine commonly leads to extrinsic tooth staining, altered taste sensation, oral mucosal irritation, dry mouth, mucosal desquamation, and increased supragingival calculus formation, particularly with prolonged use. These side effects are generally reversible upon discontinuation. Extrinsic staining of teeth and restorations is the most frequent side effect, due to its substantivity allowing prolonged adherence to oral surfaces; clinical testing shows a measurable increase in facial anterior stain in 56% of users after six months of 0.12% rinses, compared to 35% in controls. Altered taste (dysgeusia or hypogeusia) is a common effect, often resolving after treatment cessation. Dry mouth (xerostomia) may also occur. Mucosal desquamation or superficial peeling of the oral epithelium is reported with extended use, presenting as white, sloughing patches that may cause discomfort. Patients should avoid contact with the eyes during use of oral chlorhexidine preparations, as accidental exposure can cause significant ocular irritation, eye pain, corneal edema, keratitis, ulceration, and potentially permanent visual impairment.
Systemic adverse effects are uncommon but can arise from accidental ingestion, aspiration, or high-dose exposure, including nausea, vomiting, respiratory distress, and anaphylaxis. Ingestion of small amounts typically causes transient gastrointestinal upset like nausea and vomiting. Overdose from ingesting chlorhexidine gluconate 0.15% mouthwash is uncommon due to poor systemic absorption of chlorhexidine, but large amounts swallowed (especially in children) can cause symptoms primarily from the alcohol content in many formulations. Symptoms include alcohol intoxication signs such as slurred speech, sleepiness, staggering or stumbling walk. Chlorhexidine itself rarely causes systemic toxicity. Emergency help is advised if a child 10 kg swallows >4 ounces or shows these symptoms. Aspiration risks include chemical pneumonitis leading to respiratory distress. Anaphylaxis, a severe IgE-mediated reaction, has been documented in 52 cases worldwide from 1969 to 2015, with symptoms such as hypotension, wheezing, and angioedema occurring within minutes of skin or mucosal contact; 26 of these were life-threatening, including two fatalities, and additional cases have been reported since 2015 indicating a rising trend. The incidence of anaphylaxis is estimated at 0.78 per 100,000 exposures, often linked to perioperative use.
Ototoxicity is a serious concern when chlorhexidine contacts the middle ear during surgical procedures, potentially causing sensorineural hearing loss. Animal studies demonstrate cochlear hair cell damage and vestibular dysfunction with concentrations as low as 0.05%, particularly when combined with alcohol, leading to high-frequency hearing thresholds shifts. In humans, two case reports and limited clinical data indicate permanent deafness following inadvertent middle ear exposure during tympanoplasty or mastoidectomy, with toxicity dependent on concentration and contact time with the round window membrane. Such reactions underscore the need for caution in ear surgery, where safer antiseptics are preferred.
Long-term use of chlorhexidine in dental applications may promote increased supragingival calculus formation and exacerbate oral microbiome imbalances. Calculus deposition rises due to reduced plaque disruption over time. Prolonged exposure also shifts the oral microbiota toward higher levels of lactate-producing bacteria, potentially contributing to periodontal progression, though clinical impact varies.

Contraindications and Interactions

Chlorhexidine is contraindicated in patients with a known hypersensitivity to chlorhexidine or other biguanides, as this can lead to severe allergic reactions including anaphylaxis. It must also be avoided in direct contact with the eyes, ears, or meninges, where it poses risks of serious and permanent injury, such as corneal damage, deafness, or neurotoxicity.
Relative contraindications include use during pregnancy and breastfeeding, classified as FDA Pregnancy Category B based on animal studies showing no fetal harm but with limited human data; it should be employed only if the potential benefits justify the risks. In neonates, particularly premature infants or those under 2 months, chlorhexidine requires caution due to potential skin irritation or chemical burns, though it is applied with care for umbilical cord care to prevent infections.
Chlorhexidine's antimicrobial activity is inactivated by anionic agents, such as those found in soaps, toothpastes, and natural detergents, which form insoluble salts and reduce its efficacy. In particular, common anionic surfactants like sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS) in many toothpastes can bind to chlorhexidine and diminish its antimicrobial effectiveness. Fluoride-free toothpastes may still cause interference if they contain anionic ingredients such as SLS, although interactions specifically with fluoride salts (e.g., sodium fluoride) are less consistently reported, with some studies indicating compatibility and no reduction in clinical efficacy. To minimize potential interactions and maintain chlorhexidine's effectiveness, it is recommended to rinse the mouth thoroughly with water after brushing to remove residual toothpaste before using chlorhexidine mouthwash, or to separate brushing and chlorhexidine use by at least 30 minutes (or longer, depending on guidelines), or to use them at different times of day. Its effectiveness is also diminished in high pH environments, where precipitation or altered ionization can occur, limiting its use in alkaline formulations. In combined therapies, potential interactions with other antiseptics or antibiotics may alter outcomes; for instance, concurrent use with iodine-based products like povidone-iodine requires evaluation for compatibility, as they are often alternatives rather than synergists in preventing infections. For patients with renal impairment, no specific monitoring is typically required, given that excretion occurs primarily via feces with minimal urinary involvement (less than 1%).

Chemistry

Properties

Chlorhexidine is a symmetric cationic bisbiguanide compound with the chemical formula CX22HX30ClX2NX10\ce{C22H30Cl2N10}CX22HX30ClX2NX10 and a molar mass of 505.45 g/mol. Its structure consists of two 4-chlorophenyl rings linked by a hexamethylene bridge to two biguanide groups, forming a linear molecule approximately 1.5 nm in length.
The base form of chlorhexidine appears as a white to off-white crystalline powder. It is sparingly soluble in water, with a solubility of about 0.08 g/100 mL at 20C, but its salts such as gluconate and acetate exhibit significantly higher solubility, enabling aqueous formulations. The biguanide groups confer basic properties, with pKa values of approximately 10.8 and 11.3, indicating protonation at physiological pH.
Chlorhexidine is stable in neutral pH conditions (around 58) but decomposes in alkaline environments above pH 8, where the free base may precipitate from solution. Spectrally, it exhibits UV absorption with a maximum at 254 nm, useful for analytical detection. In practice, it is most commonly employed as chlorhexidine digluconate, supplied as a 20% w/v aqueous solution for formulation purposes.

Synthesis

Chlorhexidine was originally synthesized in the 1940s by chemists at Imperial Chemical Industries (ICI) in the United Kingdom as part of research into bisbiguanide compounds for potential antimalarial applications. The compound, known initially as a derivative in the Hibitane series, was patented by ICI in 1954 (filed 1951) and first marketed in 1954 under the trade name Hibitane for antiseptic use. The seminal description of its synthesis appeared in a 1956 publication by F. L. Rose and G. Swain, detailing the formation of the symmetric bisbiguanide structure.
The original synthesis proceeds via a two-step condensation to build the biguanide moieties. In the first step, hexamethylenediamine reacts with two equivalents of sodium dicyanamide (cyanoguanidine) in aqueous solution at elevated temperature (around 100110C) to form 1,6-hexamethylenebis(dicyandiamide) as the key intermediate, typically in yields exceeding 80%. This intermediate then undergoes nucleophilic addition with two equivalents of 4-chloroaniline hydrochloride in a high-boiling solvent such as -ethoxyethanol (cellosolve), heated under reflux at 130140C for 24 hours. The reaction involves the displacement and cyclization to form the diguanide units, yielding chlorhexidine dihydrochloride after cooling, filtration, and recrystallization from aqueous acetic acid (melting point 258260C). Chlorination is inherent in the use of the p-chloroaniline precursor, with no separate coupling step required due to the symmetric design.
Modern industrial production largely follows this route but optimizes for scalability and purity, often using continuous flow reactors for the intermediate formation and employing solvents like n-butanol or water-ethanol mixtures to enhance efficiency. The bis-dicyandiamide intermediate is generated via SN2-type displacements of dicyanamide ions on protonated hexamethylenediamine, achieving overall yields of 7090% on a multi-ton scale. Post-synthesis, the chlorhexidine base is liberated and purified by acidification, then converted to the water-soluble gluconate salt by neutralization with gluconic acid in aqueous medium, facilitating formulation for medical applications. This salt formation improves solubility from <0.01% (free base) to over 20% in water, with minimal impurities due to rigorous crystallization steps.

Society and Culture

Brand Names

Chlorhexidine is marketed under various brand names globally, depending on the formulation and intended use, with many products available as topical antiseptics, oral rinses, or surgical scrubs. In the United States, prominent brands include Hibiclens, a 4% chlorhexidine gluconate skin cleanser used for preoperative skin preparation, general antisepsis, and commonly as an antiseptic body wash to treat and prevent bacterial folliculitis by reducing skin bacteria, and Peridex or PerioGard, which are 0.12% oral rinses prescribed for gingivitis treatment. Bactoshield is another U.S. brand offered as a surgical scrub solution containing 4% chlorhexidine gluconate for hand and skin disinfection in healthcare settings.
In the United Kingdom, Corsodyl is a widely used brand for chlorhexidine mouthwash at 0.2% concentration, available for managing oral infections and plaque, while Savlon is marketed as an antiseptic lotion combining chlorhexidine with other agents for wound care and skin disinfection, commonly found in households. Hydrex is a brand offering a 0.5% chlorhexidine gluconate in 70% isopropyl alcohol solution tinted pink, used primarily for surgical skin preparation and hand disinfection. The pink tint improves visibility of the applied area to ensure complete coverage and reduce confusion with other clear fluids in clinical settings. In Australia, Savlon follows a similar formulation and availability as in the UK. Internationally, variations include Septodin in India, a dental gel formulation of chlorhexidine for periodontal applications. Other global brands encompass Curasept for oral care in the United Arab Emirates and Elugel for topical use in Argentina. In Brazil, chlorhexidine digluconate 0.12% is commonly used as an antiseptic mouthwash for the control of bacterial plaque, gingivitis, and halitosis, and is available in pharmacies under various commercial names, including Riohex Gard (RioQumica), Septhex Gard (Prolink), Needs Gluconato de Clorexidina, Clinexidin (Dentalclean), Indahex, Periodental (Dentrat), Assepmed, Duxidina, Marclorhex, and generic versions labeled as Digluconato de Clorexidina 0.12%.
Common formulations of chlorhexidine products include 0.12% oral rinses for dental use, 2-4% topical solutions or gels for skin application, and 20% concentrates intended for dilution prior to use in surgical or cleansing protocols. These concentrations are standardized across many brands to ensure efficacy against bacteria while minimizing irritation.
Chlorhexidine has been available in generic forms since the expiration of key patents in the 1970s, leading to widespread production by multiple manufacturers and reduced costs. In the U.S., generic chlorhexidine gluconate oral rinse, for instance, became available following the approval of Peridex's generic equivalents in the 1990s, contributing to its status as the 270th most prescribed medication in 2023 with over 900,000 prescriptions. Market leaders vary by region; in North America, companies like 3M (Peridex) and Mlnlycke (Hibiclens) dominate topical and oral segments, while in Europe and the UK, GlaxoSmithKline (Corsodyl) holds significant share in mouthwashes. In Asia, including India, local producers like Cipla contribute to the generic market for dental gels and solutions.
Availability as over-the-counter (OTC) or prescription varies by country and formulation. In the United States, oral rinses like Peridex require a prescription due to potential side effects such as tooth staining with prolonged use, while topical solutions like Hibiclens are OTC for consumer purchase. In the UK and Australia, both oral mouthwashes (e.g., Corsodyl) and topical lotions (e.g., Savlon) are generally available OTC. In Canada, topical antiseptics at 2-4% concentrations are non-prescription, but higher-strength or oral products may need professional oversight. In India, dental gels like Septodin are typically OTC for routine oral care.

Regulatory Status

Chlorhexidine has been included on the World Health Organization's Model List of Essential Medicines since 1979, recognized as an essential antiseptic for various applications, including neonatal care and surgical preparation. It is classified under the Anatomical Therapeutic Chemical (ATC) system with codes A01AB03 for stomatological preparations (dental use) and D08AC02 for antiseptics and disinfectants used topically.
In the United States, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) addressed chlorhexidine in its over-the-counter (OTC) topical antimicrobial drug products review, with the 1994 Tentative Final Monograph establishing conditions for its use in surgical hand scrubs and healthcare personnel handwashes, classifying it as generally recognized as safe and effective for these indications when meeting specified formulation and labeling requirements. Subsequent FDA actions, including the 2017 final rule on healthcare antiseptics, deferred certain efficacy claims for chlorhexidine products pending additional data but maintained monograph status for approved uses.
The European Medicines Agency (EMA) evaluates chlorhexidine for veterinary use, recommending no maximum residue limits (MRLs) for topical applications in food-producing animals due to negligible residue levels in edible tissues and products like milk, as supported by pharmacokinetic studies showing concentrations below detection limits post-application.
Regulatory guidelines have imposed restrictions on chlorhexidine use in neonates, with agencies like the FDA and CDC advising against routine full-body bathing in preterm or low-birth-weight infants under 2 months due to potential risks of skin irritation and limited safety data from post-2010 studies, though it remains approved for targeted applications such as umbilical cord care.
Globally, chlorhexidine is promoted for accessible use in low-resource settings, included in UNICEF supply kits as a 7.1% digluconate gel (equivalent to 4% chlorhexidine) for umbilical cord care to prevent infections in newborns.
