 Trigonometry is a branch of mathematics that studies the relationships between the sides and angles of triangles, primarily through the use of six fundamental functions: sine, cosine, tangent, cosecant, secant, and cotangent. These functions are defined as ratios of the sides of a right-angled triangle, with sine equal to the opposite side over the hypotenuse, cosine equal to the adjacent side over the hypotenuse, and tangent equal to the opposite side over the adjacent side. Alternatively, sine and cosine can be defined using the unit circle, where a point on the circle at angle from the positive x-axis has coordinates (cos , sin ), providing a basis for extending these functions to all real numbers and modeling periodic phenomena.
The term "trigonometry" originates from the Greek words trignon (triangle) and metron (measure), reflecting its focus on measuring triangular elements. Its development began in ancient civilizations, with the Babylonians introducing the division of the circle into 360 degrees before 300 B.C.E. to facilitate astronomical calculations. Greek astronomer Hipparchus of Nicaea (c. 190120 B.C.E.) advanced the field by creating the first known trigonometric tables based on chord lengths in a circle, treating trigonometry as an applied science for astronomy. In the 2nd century C.E., Claudius Ptolemy compiled the Almagest, which included a comprehensive table of chords at half-degree intervals and proofs of key identities like the sum and difference formulas, solidifying trigonometry's role in spherical geometry and celestial navigation. Later contributions, such as those by Indian mathematicians like Aryabhata in the 5th century C.E., introduced sine tables that influenced global developments.
Trigonometry's applications extend far beyond triangles, underpinning fields like physics, engineering, astronomy, and computer science. In astronomy and navigation, spherical trigonometry calculates positions of stars and planets on the celestial sphere, as used by Ptolemy in his Geography. Engineering applications include surveying land, designing structures, and analyzing forces in mechanics, while in physics, trigonometric functions describe waves, oscillations, and periodic motion. Within mathematics, trigonometry is essential for calculusenabling the study of derivatives and integrals of periodic functionsand for linear algebra and statistics, where it aids in vector analysis and data modeling. Modern uses also encompass computer graphics for rendering 3D models and signal processing in electronics.

Fundamentals

Definition and Basic Concepts

Trigonometry is a branch of mathematics concerned with the relationships between the sides and angles of triangles, as well as the angular relationships in planar and three-dimensional figures. The term "trigonometry" derives from the Greek words trigonon, meaning "triangle," and metron, meaning "measure," reflecting its origins in measuring triangular figures. More broadly, trigonometric concepts extend to modeling periodic phenomena, such as waves and oscillations, through functions that repeat at regular intervals.
Trigonometry is broadly divided into plane trigonometry, which applies to triangles formed on a flat, Euclidean surface where the sum of angles in any triangle is exactly 180 degrees, and spherical trigonometry, which deals with triangles on the surface of a sphere, where angles sum to more than 180 degrees due to the curvature. Plane trigonometry forms the foundation for most introductory studies, while spherical trigonometry is essential for applications involving globes or celestial navigation.
Understanding trigonometry requires familiarity with prerequisite concepts from geometry, including angles and triangles. Angles can be measured in degrees, where a full circle is 360 degrees, or in radians, where a full circle is 22\pi2 radians, providing a more natural unit for calculus-based applications. Triangles are classified by their angles as acute (all three angles less than 90 degrees), obtuse (one angle greater than 90 degrees), or right (one angle exactly 90 degrees). In a right triangle, key terms include the hypotenuse, the longest side opposite the right angle; the opposite side, relative to a specific acute angle; and the adjacent side, the leg forming the angle with the hypotenuse.
For visualization, consider a right triangle ABC with right angle at C: side AB is the hypotenuse, side BC (opposite angle A) is the opposite side to angle A, and side AC is the adjacent side to angle A. Such labeled diagrams illustrate how angles and sides interrelate, setting the stage for exploring trigonometric ratios.

Historical Development

Ancient and Classical Contributions

The origins of trigonometry trace back to ancient Mesopotamian and Egyptian civilizations, where practical geometric techniques laid foundational groundwork for angular measurements, primarily in astronomy and surveying. In Mesopotamia, the Babylonians employed a sexagesimal (base-60) system to divide the circle into 360 degrees, facilitating calculations of angles and arcs for celestial observations, as evidenced in cuneiform tablets dating to around 1800 BCE. Egyptian surveyors, known as harpedonaptai or "rope stretchers," used knotted ropes to construct right angles via the 3-4-5 Pythagorean triple, essential for reestablishing land boundaries after Nile floods, a method described in texts like the Rhind Papyrus from circa 1650 BCE. These precursors emphasized empirical geometry over abstract theory, focusing on proportional relationships in triangles without formalized trigonometric functions.
The systematic development of trigonometry emerged in ancient Greece, with Hipparchus of Nicaea (c. 190120 BCE) widely regarded as the field's founder due to his creation of the first known chord tables. Working in Rhodes, Hipparchus compiled a table of chordslengths of straight lines subtending arcs in a circlefor astronomical computations, enabling precise predictions of planetary positions and eclipses. His tables, covering angles in increments of 7.5 degrees up to 180 degrees with linear interpolation for intermediate values, were derived from geometric propositions and used a circle of radius 60 units, aligning with Babylonian influences. This innovation shifted trigonometry from ad hoc calculations to a tabular method, primarily serving Hellenistic astronomy.
Building on Hipparchus's work, Claudius Ptolemy (c. 100170 CE) advanced trigonometric tables in his seminal astronomical treatise, the Almagest, composed around 150 CE in Alexandria. Ptolemy expanded the chord table to include values for every half-degree from 0.5 to 180 in a 360 circle of radius 60, incorporating interpolation techniques and geometric proofs for accuracy. His table, detailed in Book I, supported spherical astronomy, including calculations for the sun's position and planetary motions, and remained the standard reference for over a millennium. Ptolemy's approach integrated chords with the unit circle concept, though still oriented toward observational data rather than general geometry.
In ancient India, trigonometric concepts evolved independently within astronomical contexts, with significant contributions from mathematicians like Aryabhata (476550 CE) and Brahmagupta (c. 598668 CE). Aryabhata introduced the earliest known sine table in his Aryabhatiya (499 CE), listing values of jyathe Sanskrit term for chord half-lengths, equivalent to the modern sine functionfor angles at 345' intervals up to 90, computed using geometric approximations and difference methods. This table, embedded in Hindu cosmology and planetary models, emphasized sine ratios for eclipse predictions and timekeeping. Brahmagupta, in his Brahmasphutasiddhanta (628 CE), refined these by developing second-order interpolation formulas to estimate intermediate sine values from the tabulated data, enhancing precision in astronomical calculations. Indian trigonometry, like its Greek counterpart, prioritized astronomy, using jya and related functions without the modern sine's abstraction, and laid groundwork for later refinements.

Medieval and Modern Advancements

During the Islamic Golden Age, scholars built upon ancient Greek and Indian astronomical traditions, significantly advancing trigonometry as a tool for precise calculations. Al-Battani, a 9th-century Syrian astronomer, refined Ptolemy's chord tables by introducing more accurate trigonometric values and developing methods for spherical trigonometry, which enhanced the precision of solar and lunar eclipse predictions. Al-Khwarizmi, working around the same period, contributed treatises on sundials and astrolabes that incorporated early trigonometric procedures, including the use of sines and tangents derived from Indian sources to solve problems in timekeeping and celestial positioning.
In the 10th century, Abu al-Wafa al-Buzjani further innovated by explicitly defining the sine function in its modern formdistinct from the chordand compiling the first comprehensive tables of sines and tangents at intervals of 15 arcminutes, which facilitated more efficient astronomical computations. These developments marked a transition from empirical chord-based methods to a more algebraic approach, laying groundwork for trigonometry's broader application beyond astronomy.
The European Renaissance revived and expanded these Islamic contributions through scholarly translations and original works. In the 15th century, Regiomontanus (Johann Mller) authored De triangulis omnimodis (1464), the first systematic European treatise on plane and spherical trigonometry, which organized trigonometric rules into a cohesive framework and emphasized their utility for surveying and navigation. This was followed in 1551 by Georg Joachim Rheticus's Canon doctrinae triangulorum, which provided the earliest printed tables of all six trigonometric functions (sine, cosine, tangent, cotangent, secant, cosecant) with notable precision for the time.
In the 17th and 18th centuries, Leonhard Euler systematized trigonometry by treating functions as analytic expressions of real and complex variables, introducing modern notations like sin(x) and deriving infinite series expansions for sine and cosine in his Introductio in analysin infinitorum (1748). Euler also connected trigonometry to complex numbers through his formula $ e^{i\theta} = \cos \theta + i \sin \theta $, revealing deep links between exponential and periodic functions without delving into proofs here.
By the 19th century, trigonometry had evolved into a general mathematical discipline, exemplified by the standardization of the radian as an angle measure by James Thomson in the 1870s; he proposed it in 1873 examination questions as the arc length subtended by a radius, offering a natural unit independent of degrees for calculus applications. This progression shifted trigonometry from specialized astronomical tables to a foundational tool in analysis and geometry.

Unit Circle and Standard Values

The unit circle is defined as the circle centered at the origin (0,0) with a radius of 1, providing a geometric framework for defining trigonometric functions for any angle in standard position. For an angle measured counterclockwise from the positive x-axis, the coordinates of the point where the terminal side intersects the unit circle are (cos , sin ), allowing direct evaluation of sine and cosine as the y- and x-coordinates, respectively. This representation extends the right-triangle definitions of trigonometric ratios to all angles, including those greater than 90 or negative, by standardizing the hypotenuse to 1.
Key points on the unit circle correspond to standard angles, such as 0 (0 rad) at (1, 0), 30 (/6 rad) at (3/2, 1/2), 45 (/4 rad) at (2/2, 2/2), 60 (/3 rad) at (1/2, 3/2), and 90 (/2 rad) at (0, 1), with values in other quadrants determined by symmetry and signs. These points form the basis for memorizing standard trigonometric values, as the coordinates yield exact expressions for sine, cosine, and tangent without approximation.
Standard values are derived from special right triangles scaled to fit the unit circle. For 30 and 60, consider an equilateral triangle with side length 2; drawing an altitude bisects it into a 30-60-90 right triangle with sides 1 (opposite 30), 3 (opposite 60), and 2 (hypotenuse). Scaling the hypotenuse to 1 gives sin 30 = 1/2, cos 30 = 3/2, sin 60 = 3/2, and cos 60 = 1/2. For 45, an isosceles right triangle with legs of length 1 has hypotenuse 2; scaling yields sin 45 = cos 45 = 2/2.
To determine the signs of trigonometric functions in each quadrant, the mnemonic ASTC ("All Students Take Calculus") is commonly used: all functions are positive in Quadrant I, sine and cosecant in Quadrant II, tangent and cotangent in Quadrant III, and cosine and secant in Quadrant IV. This aids in assigning correct signs to values from the first quadrant when evaluating angles in other quadrants.
Reference angles facilitate finding trigonometric values for non-standard angles by reducing them to acute angles (0 to 90) in the first quadrant. Numerical approximations of trigonometric functions are essential for computational devices such as calculators and software, where precomputed tables are impractical for arbitrary inputs. These methods enable efficient evaluation without relying solely on lookup tables, balancing accuracy and computational cost. Key approaches include series expansions, iterative algorithms, and preprocessing techniques to handle the periodic and bounded nature of trigonometric functions.

Error analysis is critical in floating-point implementations, governed by standards like IEEE 754, which specifies formats such as single (32-bit) and double (64-bit) precision but does not mandate exact accuracy for transcendental functions like sine and cosine. Modern libraries support high-precision trigonometric computations beyond standard floating-point. For GPU-accelerated environments, CUDA implementations like CAMPARY enable multiple-precision arithmetic, allowing custom high-precision trigonometric evaluations in parallel, though standard CUDA math APIs focus on IEEE 754-compliant double precision with errors below 3 ULPs for sine and cosine.

Applications

Geometry and Surveying

Trigonometry plays a foundational role in geometry and surveying by enabling the measurement of distances, angles, and elevations across terrestrial landscapes. Triangulation, a core technique, involves dividing large areas into networks of triangles and using angular measurements from known baselines to determine unknown positions. This method relies on the law of sines to compute side lengths from measured angles, allowing surveyors to map extensive regions without directly measuring every distance. Historically, ancient Greek scholar Eratosthenes applied principles akin to triangulation around 240 BCE to estimate the Earth's circumference by measuring the angle of the sun's rays at two distant locations and using the known distance between them.
Surveying instruments such as theodolites and total stations integrate trigonometric functions to achieve precise angular and distance measurements. A theodolite measures horizontal and vertical angles with high accuracy, essential for establishing control points in triangulation networks. Total stations combine theodolite capabilities with electronic distance measurement (EDM), computing positions in real-time using trigonometry. For elevation determination, surveyors employ the tangent function, where the vertical rise is calculated as rise = run \times \tan \theta, with \theta representing the measured angle and run the horizontal distance. This trigonometric leveling is particularly useful in rugged terrain, providing elevations relative to benchmarks.
In coordinate geometry for surveying, polar coordinatesconsisting of a distance (r) and angle (\theta) from a reference pointare converted to Cartesian coordinates using sine and cosine: x = r \cos \theta and y = r \sin \theta. This transformation allows integration of field measurements into planar mapping systems, facilitating the plotting of boundaries and features. Cadastral surveying, which delineates property lines, relies on these conversions to establish legal boundaries from angular and linear data. The U.S. Public Land Survey System (PLSS), established in the 19th century, exemplifies trigonometry's role in large-scale mapping by using rectangular surveys adjusted with angular corrections to divide public lands into townships and sections.
Modern applications extend trigonometric principles to advanced technologies like GPS and LiDAR. GPS positioning uses trilateration, an extension of plane trigonometry to spherical geometry, where distances from multiple satellites intersect to determine a receiver's location on Earth's surface. In LiDAR surveying, trigonometric computations process laser pulse returns to generate 3D point clouds, converting slant-range distances and scan angles into Cartesian coordinates for terrain modeling and volume calculations. These methods enhance accuracy in cadastral updates and infrastructure planning, building directly on foundational trigonometric tools.

Physics and Engineering

Trigonometric functions are fundamental in physics for resolving vectors into orthogonal components, enabling the analysis of forces and motions in multiple dimensions. For a vector such as a force F\vec{F}F with magnitude FFF directed at an angle \theta to the horizontal axis, the horizontal component is Fx=FcosF_x = F \cos \thetaFx=Fcos and the vertical component is Fy=FsinF_y = F \sin \thetaFy=Fsin. In the study of waves and oscillations, trigonometry models periodic phenomena central to many physical systems. In electrical engineering, phasors represent alternating current (AC) circuit elements as rotating vectors in the complex plane, with voltage and current phasors related by phase angles derived from trigonometric ratios of resistance, inductance, and capacitance, facilitating impedance calculations and power analysis without time-domain integration.
Optics relies on trigonometric relations to describe light propagation and interaction with matter. In engineering applications, trigonometry supports structural and signal analysis. Truss structures, common in bridges and roofs, are analyzed by resolving member forces at joints using sine and cosine of inclination angles, ensuring static equilibrium under loads via the method of joints. Fourier analysis decomposes complex periodic signals into sums of sine and cosine terms of harmonic frequencies, as in f(t)=a0+n=1(ancos(nt)+bnsin(nt))f(t) = a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (a_n \cos(n \omega t) + b_n \sin(n \omega t))f(t)=a0+n=1(ancos(nt)+bnsin(nt)), enabling frequency-domain processing in communications and vibration control.
Representative examples illustrate these principles. The period of a simple pendulum for small angles approximates T=2L/gT = 2\pi \sqrt{L/g}T=2L/g by substituting sin\sin \theta \approx \thetasin into the torque equation, valid when <10\theta < 10^\circ<10 and linking gravitational restoration to angular displacement. In beam deflection, geometric methods like the moment-area theorem integrate curvature to find rotation angles, incorporating trigonometric functions to relate slope changes to applied moments and support conditions in cantilever or simply supported designs.
As of 2025, trigonometric computations remain integral to AI-driven robotics, particularly in inverse kinematics for path planning, where joint angles are solved from end-effector positions using cosine rules and arc tangents to generate collision-free trajectories in manipulators.

Astronomy and Navigation

Trigonometry plays a pivotal role in astronomy and navigation by enabling precise calculations on spherical surfaces, such as Earth's globe and the celestial sphere. Spherical trigonometry extends plane trigonometry to great circles, which represent the shortest paths between points on a sphere and are crucial for determining routes in navigation and mapping stellar positions. In celestial navigation, trigonometry allows mariners and aviators to determine position by measuring angles between celestial bodies and the horizon using a sextant, which captures altitudes essential for solving the navigational triangle. For instance, the altitude of Polaris provides a direct estimate of latitude in the Northern Hemisphere: the observer's latitude \phi approximates the measured altitude hhh of Polaris, adjusted by a small correction (typically under 1) due to its 0.7 offset from the north celestial pole, yielding h\phi \approx hh. Triangular sailings, including the plane sailing approximation for short distances, employ trigonometric identities to compute differences in latitude and departure (east-west distance) from course, speed, and time, treating the Earth as flat over small regions while incorporating spherical corrections for longer voyages.
Astronomical applications leverage trigonometry for measuring cosmic distances and orbital parameters. Trigonometric parallax exploits the Earth's orbital baseline of 2 AU (diameter) to gauge nearby stellar distances: the parallax angle ppp (half the apparent shift observed six months apart) relates to distance ddd in parsecs by d=1/pd = 1 / pd=1/p, where ppp is in arcseconds, derived from the small-angle approximation sinpp\sin p \approx psinpp (in radians) in the right triangle formed by the baseline and line of sight. Historical and modern examples illustrate trigonometry's enduring impact. Ancient Greek astronomer Hipparchus (c. 190120 BCE) pioneered chord tablesearly trigonometric toolsfor compiling the first comprehensive star catalog of approximately 850 stars, using chords of circle arcs to tabulate positions and enable predictions like precession. In space exploration, the Voyager missions (1977present) relied on trigonometric calculations for trajectory planning, including gravity-assist flybys where angular deflections from planetary encounters were modeled using spherical geometry to optimize paths across the solar system. Contemporary astrometry advances this legacy through the ESA's Gaia satellite, which continued observations beyond its initial extended mission with fuel expected to deplete around 2026 after more than 12 years of operation; it measures trigonometric parallaxes for over 2 billion stars with microarcsecond precision, enabling distance reductions and 3D galactic mapping via ongoing data releases.
