2010

1x
 2010 was a common year starting on Friday of the Gregorian calendar, following 2009 and preceding 2011, and it was marked by profound global challenges including catastrophic natural disasters and environmental crises alongside major sporting events. A magnitude 7.0 earthquake struck Haiti on January 12, devastating Port-au-Prince and surrounding areas, resulting in over 220,000 deaths and affecting 3.5 million people. In April, the Deepwater Horizon oil rig exploded in the Gulf of Mexico, triggering the largest marine oil spill in U.S. history, which released millions of barrels of oil and caused extensive ecological damage.
The year also saw significant political developments, such as the U.S. midterm elections in November, where Republicans regained control of the House of Representatives amid economic discontent following the Great Recession. Culturally, 2010 hosted the XXI Olympic Winter Games in Vancouver from February 12 to 28, featuring competitions in ice hockey, figure skating, and alpine skiing, with the United States topping the medal count. Additionally, South Africa became the first African nation to host the FIFA World Cup in June and July, drawing global attention to the tournament won by Spain. These events underscored a year of resilience amid tragedy, with ongoing recovery efforts and international responses shaping discourse on disaster preparedness and environmental policy.

Events

JanuaryMarch

On January 12, a magnitude 7.0 earthquake devastated Haiti near Port-au-Prince, killing over 220,000 people and injuring hundreds of thousands in the immediate aftermath. International organizations responded swiftly, with groups like Oxfam deploying emergency aid for water, shelter, and medical needs, while the U.S. military coordinated logistics and flights at Haiti's request to facilitate humanitarian deliveries. The Pan American Health Organization also mobilized rapidly to support health infrastructure amid collapsed facilities and widespread injuries.
On January 27, Apple unveiled the iPad tablet device, introducing a new category of portable computing that quickly dominated the market and reshaped consumer electronics.
The Vancouver Winter Olympics commenced on February 12 with an opening ceremony blending indigenous cultural performances, multimedia spectacles, and symbolic elements, culminating in Wayne Gretzky lighting the cauldron.
On March 20, Iceland's Eyjafjallajkull volcano initiated a fissure eruption under the glacier, prompting the evacuation of around 500 nearby residents and signaling potential broader disruptions from ash and meltwater.

AprilJune

On April 3, Apple launched the first-generation iPad in the United States, a touchscreen tablet that popularized portable multimedia consumption and app ecosystems, selling over 300,000 units on its first day and signaling a shift toward slate-form-factor devices.
The Deepwater Horizon semisubmersible drilling rig exploded on April 20 in the Gulf of Mexico, killing 11 crew members and causing the rig to sink two days later, which initiated an uncontrolled oil release from the Macondo well; early attempts to activate the blowout preventer and deploy containment domes failed, allowing millions of barrels to spill over subsequent weeks.
Thailand's United Front for Democracy Against Dictatorship, known as the red shirts, intensified anti-government protests in Bangkok starting in March, but April saw heightened tensions with skirmishes between protesters and security forces, culminating in a military operation from May 13 to 19 that dispersed fortified camps amid arson and gunfire, leaving at least 90 dead and prompting accusations of excessive force from both sides.
The U.S. financial markets underwent the Flash Crash on May 6, when a large sell order in E-mini S&P 500 futures triggered algorithmic trading cascades, causing the Dow Jones Industrial Average to plummet about 9%nearly 1,000 pointsin under 30 minutes before largely rebounding by day's end, exposing risks in automated systems and liquidity provision.

JulySeptember

In July 2010, WikiLeaks released the Afghan War Diary, comprising over 91,000 classified U.S. military documents from 2004 to 2009 that detailed incidents in the Afghanistan conflict, highlighting unreported civilian casualties and operational challenges.
Russia faced severe wildfires amid a prolonged heatwave starting in late July, exacerbated by dry conditions that led to widespread forest and peat fires, resulting in over 55,000 excess deaths primarily from heat-related causes and smoke inhalation, with Moscow particularly shrouded in haze. The fires emitted approximately 10 Tg of carbon monoxide, surpassing 85% of Russia's annual anthropogenic CO emissions, and affected millions through evacuations and agricultural losses.
Heavy monsoon rains triggered devastating floods in Pakistan beginning in late July, submerging one-fifth of the country and impacting around 20 million people, with over 1,700 deaths, 7 million left homeless, and widespread destruction of homes, farmland, and infrastructure. The humanitarian crisis prompted international aid, including U.S. efforts that evacuated over 21,000 individuals and delivered millions of pounds of supplies by early October.
Throughout the summer, primaries for the U.S. midterm elections intensified, featuring Tea Party-backed challengers who upset establishment candidates in several states, signaling growing voter discontent and setting the stage for Republican gains in November.
Early manifestations of the European debt crisis persisted with Greece's bailout implementation, with the 110 billion package agreed in May including initial disbursements that month to address immediate liquidity needs amid ongoing fiscal strains.

OctoberDecember

On October 13, the rescue operation for 33 miners trapped in the San Jos copper-gold mine near Copiap concluded successfully after 69 days underground, following a collapse on August 5 that buried them at a depth of about 2,300 feet. The effort involved multiple drilling teams and international support, including specialized capsules like the Fenix series to extract each miner individually through a narrow borehole, marking one of the longest underground survivals in mining history.
In the United States, the November 2 midterm elections resulted in significant Republican gains, with the party securing control of the House of Representatives by flipping 63 seats from Democrats, driven by voter dissatisfaction with economic conditions and the Tea Party movement's influence. Democrats retained a slim majority in the Senate after Republicans netted six seats, reflecting a broader ideological shift among independents toward conservatism.
The European sovereign debt crisis intensified in late 2010, particularly with Ireland's banking sector collapse prompting an EU-IMF bailout package of 85 billion announced on November 28 to stabilize its economy and prevent contagion to other eurozone members. This escalation highlighted vulnerabilities in peripheral economies, leading to heightened market volatility and policy responses aimed at bolstering the euro's stability.
Tensions on the Korean Peninsula escalated on November 23 when North Korea shelled Yeonpyeong Island with around 170 artillery rounds, targeting South Korean military positions and civilian areas in response to perceived provocations during drills. The attack killed two South Korean marines and two civilians, prompting retaliatory fire from Seoul and international condemnation, though it did not lead to broader conflict.
WikiLeaks initiated the full release of Cablegate on November 28, publishing over 250,000 classified U.S. diplomatic cables obtained from the State Department, which revealed candid assessments of foreign leaders and policy deliberations. The disclosures, coordinated with media partners for redaction of sensitive details, sparked global diplomatic fallout and debates over transparency versus national security.

Politics

United States

In March 2010, President Barack Obama signed the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act into law as Public Law 111-148. Key provisions expanded Medicaid eligibility to adults with incomes up to 133 percent of the federal poverty level, established health insurance exchanges for purchasing coverage, and imposed an individual mandate requiring most Americans to obtain health insurance or face penalties. The legislation also introduced premium tax credits to subsidize coverage for households earning between 100 and 400 percent of the federal poverty level, aiming to reduce uninsured rates and control costs.
On January 21, 2010, the Supreme Court issued its 5-4 decision in Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission, ruling that corporate and union independent expenditures on political speech constituted protected First Amendment activity and could not be restricted. The ruling overturned prior limits on such spending, enabling corporations, unions, and other groups to fund electioneering communications without direct coordination with candidates, which spurred the rise of super PACs and amplified spending by outside entities in subsequent campaigns. 
The Tea Party movement emerged as a significant conservative force in 2010, driven by opposition to federal spending, taxes, and the health care overhaul, with its supporters disproportionately wealthier, more educated, white, male, Republican, and married compared to the general public. This grassroots insurgency propelled victories in Republican primaries, such as Rand Paul's Senate nomination in Kentucky, and shaped the party's platform emphasizing fiscal conservatism.
These dynamics culminated in the November 2010 midterm elections, where Democrats lost control of the House, with Republicans netting 63 seats to shift from minority to majority status. Republicans gained 6 Senate seats, narrowing the Democratic majority but not overturning it, amid voter backlash against the administration's policies and energized conservative turnout influenced by the Tea Party.

International

In the United Kingdom, the general election on 6 May resulted in a hung parliament, with the Conservative Party, led by David Cameron, securing the most seats but falling short of a majority; subsequent negotiations led to a coalition government with the Liberal Democrats under Nick Clegg, marking the first such arrangement since 1945. The coalition agreement emphasized fiscal austerity and political reforms, with Clegg serving as deputy prime minister.
Ukraine's presidential election in January and February saw Viktor Yanukovych, leader of the Party of Regions, defeat incumbent Prime Minister Yulia Tymoshenko in a runoff on 7 February, securing approximately 49% of the vote amid international observation deeming the process largely fair. Yanukovych's victory represented a shift toward pro-Russian policies, reversing aspects of the 2004 Orange Revolution's legacy.
In Kyrgyzstan, widespread protests erupted in April against President Kurmanbek Bakiyev's government over corruption, rising utility prices, and authoritarianism, culminating in the storming of government buildings in Bishkek on 7 April and forcing Bakiyev to flee the capital. Opposition leaders established an interim government under Roza Otunbayeva, leading to Bakiyev's formal resignation and exile later that month amid fears for his safety.
Australia's federal election on 21 August produced a hung parliament, with the incumbent Labor Party under Julia Gillard winning 72 seats and the Liberal-National Coalition led by Tony Abbott winning 73 seats in the House of Representatives, requiring support from independents to form government. After negotiations, Gillard secured a minority Labor government with backing from key independents, focusing on stability amid economic debates.

Economy

Global Recovery

In the United States, recovery efforts from the 2008 financial crisis advanced with the passage of the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act, signed into law by President Barack Obama on July 21, 2010, to enhance financial regulation, increase transparency, and mitigate systemic risks that contributed to the downturn. This legislation established mechanisms like the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau and imposed stricter oversight on large financial institutions to prevent excessive risk-taking.
In Europe, the Eurozone responded to emerging sovereign debt pressures by initiating stability mechanisms, including the European Financial Stabilisation Mechanism formalized through Council Regulation (EU) No 407/2010 on May 11, 2010, which provided a framework for financial assistance to preserve monetary union stability. Complementing this, the European Financial Stability Facility was incorporated in June 2010 to offer loans and support to member states facing fiscal difficulties, marking an early step toward coordinated crisis management.
China's economic expansion accelerated during the recovery phase, with its nominal GDP reaching $5.8786 trillion in 2010, surpassing Japan's $5.474 trillion and positioning China as the world's second-largest economy behind the United States. This milestone reflected robust growth driven by export recovery and domestic stimulus, contrasting with slower rebounds in advanced economies.
Unemployment trends in major economies highlighted uneven progress, with rates remaining elevated amid structural adjustments; in the United States, the jobless rate hovered near 9-10% throughout 2010 after peaking at 10.1% in late 2009, signaling a protracted labor market rebound despite stabilizing output. Similar patterns emerged in other developed nations, where high unemployment persisted as firms cautiously rehired amid lingering credit constraints and demand weakness.

Corporate and Financial

The May 6, 2010, Flash Crash triggered a rapid plunge in U.S. stock indices, with the Dow Jones Industrial Average dropping nearly 1,000 points in minutes before recovering, largely attributed to interactions between high-frequency trading algorithms and a large sell order. In response, the SEC and CFTC's joint report analyzed the event and led to regulatory reforms, including the implementation of single-stock circuit breakers to halt trading in volatile securities and limits on stub quotes to enhance market stability.
The SEC filed fraud charges against Goldman Sachs in April 2010 for its role in structuring and marketing the Abacus 2007-AC1 collateralized debt obligation, alleging misrepresentations to investors about the involvement of hedge fund Paulson & Co., which selected underlying assets while betting against the deal. Goldman settled the charges in July without admitting or denying wrongdoing, agreeing to pay $550 million, including $300 million to harmed investors, marking one of the largest SEC settlements at the time.
Toyota encountered a severe recall crisis amid reports of unintended acceleration, leading to the recall of nearly 8 million vehicles in the U.S. for issues including floor mats interfering with accelerators and sticking pedals. The company temporarily halted sales of several models, faced congressional scrutiny, and substantial fines and settlements as investigations confirmed mechanical defects in some cases.
BP assumed primary corporate liability for the Deepwater Horizon oil spill, announcing it would cover all cleanup and response costs without capping at the Oil Pollution Act's $75 million limit, with early estimates exceeding $12.5 billion. By late 2010, BP had incurred over $10 billion in direct response expenses and established a $20 billion escrow fund for claims, reflecting heightened accountability under U.S. regulations.

Science and Technology

Discoveries

In May 2010, researchers from the Neanderthal Genome Project announced the completion of the first fully sequenced Neanderthal genome, derived from three female specimens found in Vindija Cave, Croatia, revealing genetic similarities to modern humans outside Africa and evidence of interbreeding.
Advancements in induced pluripotent stem (iPS) cell technology progressed toward clinical applications, with studies demonstrating improved reprogramming efficiency and potential for disease modeling, such as in cardiovascular regeneration, building on earlier methods to generate patient-specific cells for autologous therapies.
NASA's Kepler space telescope confirmed its first five exoplanets in January 2010Kepler-4b through Kepler-8bwith radii ranging from Neptune-sized to Jupiter-sized, demonstrating the telescope's capability in transit photometry and paving the way for detecting smaller exoplanets, including those in habitable zones.
In December 2010, a NASA-funded study reported the isolation of GFAJ-1, a bacterium from Mono Lake, California, purportedly capable of substituting arsenic for phosphorus in its DNA and other biomolecules under phosphate-starved conditions, igniting scientific debate over the biochemistry of life and alternative biochemistries, though subsequent analyses questioned the findings' validity.

Innovations

Apple introduced the first-generation iPad tablet on April 3, 2010, following its announcement on January 27, featuring a 9.7-inch multi-touch display designed for web browsing, email, and media consumption, which popularized the tablet form factor. Later that year, on June 24, Apple released the iPhone 4 smartphone, incorporating a high-resolution Retina display, front-facing camera for video calls, and improved antenna design, enhancing mobile computing capabilities.
Microsoft launched Kinect for the Xbox 360 on November 4, 2010, a motion-sensing accessory that enabled controller-free gaming through full-body tracking and voice commands, transforming interactive entertainment by integrating users directly into gameplay.
Google expanded Street View in 2010 by adding imagery from Brazil, Ireland, and Antarctica, achieving coverage across all seven continents and advancing panoramic street-level mapping technology.
Nissan debuted the Leaf electric vehicle in late 2010, with production beginning in Japan in October and initial sales in select markets, marking it as an early mass-produced, affordable battery-electric car aimed at everyday commuting.

Natural Disasters and Environment

Geological Events

On January 12, a magnitude 7.0 earthquake struck Haiti along the Enriquillo-Plantain Garden strike-slip fault, where tectonic plates slid horizontally, exacerbating structural collapses in poorly constructed buildings reliant on unreinforced masonry.
The February 27 Maule earthquake in Chile measured magnitude 8.8, involving subduction zone thrusting with fault slip reaching 15 meters, which deformed the seafloor and prompted widespread tsunami warnings across the Pacific basin.
In April, Iceland's Eyjafjallajkull volcano underwent a subglacial eruption, ejecting ash primarily composed of andesitic glass (57-58% SiO) with sharp, abrasive particles across a broad size spectrum.
August rains in China's Zhouqu county triggered a massive debris flow, where extreme monsoon precipitation saturated loose geological materials in steep terrain, initiating rapid mass movement down valleys.

Human-Caused Incidents

On April 20, 2010, the Deepwater Horizon semi-submersible drilling rig exploded in the Gulf of Mexico, approximately 41 miles off the Louisiana coast, due to a failure in the cementing process and a malfunctioning blowout preventer that failed to seal the well. The explosion killed 11 workers and led to an uncontrolled release of oil from the Macondo well, with an estimated 4 million barrels spilling into the Gulf over 87 days before containment efforts succeeded. Initial attempts to cap the well, including the deployment of a containment dome in late April and a top-kill procedure in May using heavy drilling mud to counteract the flow, failed due to technical complications such as ice crystal formation and overpowering pressure. A capping stack was ultimately installed on July 15, 2010, halting the release after multiple prior interventions proved ineffective.
In the mining sector, the Upper Big Branch Mine disaster occurred on April 5, 2010, when a coal dust explosion ripped through the underground workings of the Performance Coal Company's mine in Montcoal, West Virginia, killing 29 of the 31 miners present and injuring the survivors. The blast originated approximately 980 feet underground from a spark generated by mining equipment striking rock, which ignited accumulated methane gas and propagated via coal dust suspended in the air, exacerbated by ventilation and safety system deficiencies.
Similarly, on August 5, 2010, a tunnel collapse at the San Jos copper-gold mine in Chile's Atacama Desert trapped 33 miners about 2,300 feet underground after unstable rock formations gave way, severing access to the surface and initial escape routes. The incident stemmed from structural instabilities and inadequate reinforcement in the aging mine shafts, leaving the workers isolated in a shelter chamber with limited supplies for the initial period of entrapment.

Sports

Winter Sports

The 2010 Winter Olympics took place in Vancouver, British Columbia, Canada, with competition venues spread across a 120-kilometer zone including facilities in downtown Vancouver and Whistler, such as the Whistler Sliding Centre for luge and bobsleigh events. The United States topped the medal table with 37 medals overall, while host nation Canada set a Winter Games record with 14 gold medals. A notable tragedy occurred during pre-competition training when Georgian luger Nodar Kumaritashvili, aged 21, lost control of his sled at high speed, crashed into a metal pole, and died from his injuries; investigations attributed the incident to human error rather than track design flaws.
In professional ice hockey, the Chicago Blackhawks clinched the Stanley Cup by defeating the Philadelphia Flyers 4 games to 2 in the Finals, marking their first championship since 1961.
The Winter X Games XIV, held in Aspen, Colorado, featured standout performances including Icelandic snowboarder Halldor Helgason's gold medal in snowboard Big Air with two perfect runs of 50 points each, securing gold and making history as the first Icelander to win at the event.

Summer Events

The 2010 FIFA World Cup, hosted by South Africa from June 11 to July 11, marked the first time the tournament was held on the African continent and featured the distinctive sound of vuvuzelas, plastic horns producing a persistent buzzing noise that dominated broadcasts and drew both criticism and cultural embrace. Notable upsets included the United States earning a 1-1 draw against England after goalkeeper Robert Green's error allowed a goal, and Ghana's late penalty victory over Serbia. Spain claimed the title with a 1-0 extra-time win over the Netherlands in the final.
The Commonwealth Games took place in Delhi, India, from October 3 to 14, amid significant organizational challenges including infrastructure delays and safety concerns, yet proceeded with competitions across 17 sports. Australia dominated the medal standings, topping the table with strong performances in athletics, swimming, and cycling despite the disruptions.
In cycling, the Tour de France concluded with Alberto Contador initially declared the winner, but he tested positive for clenbuterol during the event, leading to a two-year ban in 2012 and the stripping of his title, with Andy Schleck retroactively awarded victory; Contador attributed the positive test to contaminated meat. The incident underscored ongoing doping controversies in professional cycling.

Culture

Film and Media

Toy Story 3 and Inception marked major box office achievements in 2010, with the Pixar animated sequel topping global earnings at over $1 billion, making it the year's highest-grossing film. Inception, Christopher Nolan's complex sci-fi thriller released in July, followed as one of the top performers with $839 million worldwide, praised for its innovative narrative on dream infiltration.
The Social Network, directed by David Fincher and released on October 1, depicted the founding of Facebook by Mark Zuckerberg amid legal disputes, earning acclaim for its portrayal of tech innovation and interpersonal conflicts.
The prior year's Avatar exerted ongoing influence in 2010, spurring cinema chains to invest in 3D projection upgrades to meet surging demand for immersive viewing experiences.
Television faced upheaval with Conan O'Brien's exit from The Tonight Show on January 22, after NBC's plan to shift the program to a later slot sparked public backlash and a $45 million settlement for O'Brien and his team.

Music and Literature

Kanye West released his fifth studio album, My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy, on November 22, 2010, which became one of the most critically acclaimed albums of the decade. Arcade Fire issued their third studio album, The Suburbs, on August 2, 2010, garnering significant attention for its thematic exploration of suburban life. Lady Gaga's The Fame Monster era continued to peak in popularity throughout 2010, driven by the ongoing Monster Ball Tour and hits like "Bad Romance."
In literature, the death of J.D. Salinger on January 27, 2010, at age 91, prompted widespread reflections on his legacy, particularly the enduring influence of his novel The Catcher in the Rye, which had shaped generations since its 1951 publication. Salinger's reclusive life and masterful short stories further cemented his status as a pivotal figure in 20th-century American literature, with his passing marking the end of an era for admirers of his cynical yet poignant voice.

People

Births

Notable births in 2010 included children of celebrities and royals, entering the public eye due to their parents' prominence.
In entertainment, Vida Alves McConaughey was born on January 3 to actors Matthew McConaughey and Camila Alves. Faith Margaret Kidman-Urban arrived on December 14 as the daughter of actress Nicole Kidman and musician Keith Urban.
Among royalty, Savannah Phillips was born on December 29 to Peter Phillips, a grandson of Queen Elizabeth II, and Autumn Kelly, marking a notable addition to the British extended royal family.

Deaths

J.D. Salinger, the reclusive author renowned for The Catcher in the Rye which captured adolescent alienation and became a cornerstone of 20th-century American literature, died on January 28, 2010, at age 91 from natural causes at his New Hampshire home.
Alexander Haig, a four-star general and forceful political figure who served as U.S. Secretary of State under President Reagan and as NATO Supreme Allied Commander, died on February 20, 2010, at age 85 from complications of a staphylococcal infection.
Gary Coleman, the child actor who gained fame as Arnold Jackson on the sitcom Diff'rent Strokes and symbolized 1980s television's focus on family dynamics amid social issues, died on May 28, 2010, at age 42 from a brain hemorrhage following a fall.
Leslie Nielsen, the versatile actor celebrated for deadpan comedic roles in films like Airplane! and The Naked Gun series that parodied disaster movies and spy thrillers, died on November 28, 2010, at age 84 from complications of pneumonia.
