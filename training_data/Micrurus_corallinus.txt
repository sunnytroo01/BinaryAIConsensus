Micrurus corallinus
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Micrurus corallinus is a highly venomous species of elapid snake in the genus Micrurus, belonging to the family Elapidae, known commonly as the painted coral snake or Brazilian coral snake. It is a medium-sized, semifossorial serpent characterized by its slender body, smooth glossy scales, and distinctive tricolored ring pattern consisting of broad red bands separated by narrower black and yellow or white rings that encircle the body, with a black head featuring a white or yellow nuchal band. Adults typically reach a snout-vent length of up to 950 mm, making it one of the more prominent coral snakes in its range.
Native to the Atlantic Forest ecoregion of South America, M. corallinus is distributed across southeastern Brazil (including states such as Rio de Janeiro, So Paulo, Minas Gerais, Esprito Santo, Bahia, Santa Catarina, and Rio Grande do Sul), northeastern Argentina (Misiones province), and eastern Paraguay, generally at elevations from sea level to 500 m. It inhabits subtropical moist forests, littoral zones, and areas with leaf litter, where its terrestrial to subfossorial lifestyle allows it to forage in soil and under vegetation. The species is oviparous, with females laying clutches of 313 eggs during the mid-wet season (DecemberJanuary), and clutch size correlates positively with maternal body size.
As a specialist predator, M. corallinus primarily feeds on elongate, fossorial or semifossorial prey such as amphisbaenians, caecilians, lizards, and other snakes, employing slow searching behavior and its proteroglyphous fangs to deliver potent neurotoxic venom. This venom, which includes three-finger toxins, phospholipases A, and other components, exhibits high lethality (LD of approximately 0.35 mg/kg in mice intraperitoneally) and causes presynaptic neuromuscular blockade leading to paralysis and potential respiratory failure in envenomations. Despite its potency, bites on humans are rare (accounting for about 0.78% of snakebites in Brazil) due to the snake's non-aggressive nature and reclusive habits.
Classified as Least Concern on the IUCN Red List, M. corallinus faces localized threats from deforestation and human persecution but benefits from occurrence in protected areas across its range. No subspecies are recognized, and its taxonomy remains stable under the original description by Merrem in 1820.

Taxonomy

Etymology

The genus name Micrurus derives from the Ancient Greek words mikros (meaning "small") and oura (meaning "tail"), alluding to the characteristically short tail relative to body length observed in species of this genus. This nomenclature was introduced by Johann Georg Wagler in 1824 when he established the genus to accommodate certain elapid snakes previously classified under Elaps.
The specific epithet corallinus originates from the Latin word corallium (meaning "coral"), referring to the species' distinctive red-banded pattern that resembles coral. The species was originally described by Blasius Merrem in 1820 under the binomial Elaps corallinus in his work Tentamen Systematicum Reptilium, with the current combination Micrurus corallinus established upon the genus's creation. Common names such as "painted coral snake" in English and "cobra-coral pintada" in Portuguese similarly evoke these vivid coral-like color associations.

Classification

Micrurus corallinus belongs to the kingdom Animalia, phylum Chordata, class Reptilia, order Squamata, suborder Serpentes, family Elapidae, genus Micrurus, and species corallinus.
The species was originally described as Elaps corallinus by Merrem in 1820, with subsequent synonyms including others such as Elaps corallinus (Wied, 1820; Dumril & Bibron, 1854; Boulenger, 1886), Micrurus corallinus (Amaral, 1925; Welch, 1994; Giraudo & Scrocchi, 2002; Wallach et al., 2014; Nogueira et al., 2019).
There are no recognized subspecies, and M. corallinus is treated as a monotypic species. Historically, it was sometimes considered part of broader species complexes within Micrurus, but morphological and genetic analyses have confirmed its distinct status.
Recent taxonomic updates, including genetic studies post-2020, support its separation from closely related species such as M. ibiboboca, with the latter's complex revised to include three new species in 2024 based on phylogenetic and morphological data. As of 2025, it remains confirmed as a distinct monotypic species in the Reptile Database.

Description

Morphology

Micrurus corallinus possesses a slender, cylindrical body typical of many elapid snakes. Adults commonly attain total lengths of 6585 cm, though the maximum recorded length is 98 cm.
The head is only slightly wider than the neck and lacks a distinct separation from the body, while the eyes are small with round pupils. The head is black with a white or yellow nuchal band.
Dorsal scales are smooth and arranged in 15 rows at midbody; ventral scales number 217229, the anal plate is divided, and subcaudal scales range from 3241.
The tail is short, representing approximately 46% of total length, and terminates in a pointed tip, featuring 38 alternating black and red bands tipped in black.
Sexual dimorphism manifests primarily in tail proportions and scalation, with males exhibiting relatively longer tails and a higher count of subcaudal scales compared to females.

Coloration

Micrurus corallinus exhibits a distinctive tricolored banding pattern that encircles the entire body, consisting of broad red rings alternating with narrow black rings and even narrower white or yellow rings. The sequence follows a red-black-yellow-black-red arrangement, in which the red rings adjoin the yellow or white rings, creating a clear separation between red and black elements. This configuration provides high visual contrast and serves as an aposematic signal to deter predators. The black rings are arranged as monadssingle, uninterrupted bandsunlike the triad patterns seen in many other coral snake species.
Typically, the species features 1119 broad red rings separated by 1527 black rings with white or yellow interspaces, contributing to a total of approximately 5070 rings along the body and tail. The red rings often display a cinnabar to burgundy hue, with scales at their edges sometimes tipped in black for added definition, while the yellow or white rings are usually one scale wide and the black rings two to three scales wide. This banding formula enhances the snake's conspicuous appearance in its forested habitat.
Juveniles closely resemble adults in pattern but possess slightly narrower bands overall, potentially aiding in camouflage during early growth stages before full aposematic development.
The vivid coloration functions primarily as an aposematic warning of the snake's potent venom, prompting avoidance by predators such as birds and mammals. M. corallinus acts as a Batesian model for mimicry complexes, where non-venomous colubrids like Erythrolamprus aesculapii imitate its pattern to gain protection from attacks. Field studies confirm that this high-contrast banding effectively reduces predation risk, with predators showing learned avoidance of similar patterns.

Distribution and habitat

Geographic range

Micrurus corallinus is native to South America, with its primary distribution centered in southeastern Brazil, where it inhabits the Atlantic Forest from the state of Rio Grande do Sul in the south to Bahia in the north, including intermediate states such as Santa Catarina, Paran, So Paulo, Rio de Janeiro, Esprito Santo, Minas Gerais, Rio Grande do Norte, and Mato Grosso do Sul. The species also occurs in northeastern Argentina, particularly in Misiones Province, and in eastern Paraguay. Its range extends westward to eastern Paraguay and northeastern Argentina, and eastward to the Atlantic coast.
The elevational range of M. corallinus is typically from sea level to 500 m. Historical records from Uruguay exist but are considered unreliable based on a 2005 review, with no confirmed presence there. Recent observations include new confirmed sightings in Paraguay documented in a 2020 study, expanding known localities within the Atlantic Forest ecoregion but not indicating broader range extension.
Compared to historical distributions, the species' range may have contracted in peripheral areas due to ongoing deforestation in the Atlantic Forest and associated habitats, though its core distribution in southeastern Brazil remains relatively stable. No significant range expansion has been reported since 2020.

Habitat preferences

Micrurus corallinus primarily inhabits humid tropical forests, with a strong association to the Atlantic Forest biome in South America. This species favors environments characterized by dense vegetation and high moisture levels within the Atlantic Forest biome.
The snake is highly cryptozoic and semifossorial, spending much of its time in microhabitats such as leaf litter layers on the forest floor, under fallen logs, and within loose soil burrows. It also seeks diurnal and nocturnal shelter in rotten wood or beneath stones, consistently avoiding open or exposed areas in favor of concealed, shaded spots. These preferences support its foraging behavior on the moist ground, where it hunts elongated prey like amphisbaenians and caecilians.
This coral snake thrives in warm, humid climates typical of its range, with average temperatures between 20C and 30C and annual rainfall exceeding 1,500 mm, often distributed throughout the year in its core habitats. It is generally found at low elevations up to approximately 500 m, reflecting its adaptation to lowland tropical conditions. M. corallinus exhibits sensitivity to habitat fragmentation, showing a marked preference for continuous, intact forest cover to maintain its fossorial lifestyle and foraging efficiency.

Behavior

Activity patterns

Micrurus corallinus exhibits a primarily fossorial lifestyle, spending much of its time burrowed underground, with surface activity occurring mainly at dusk and dawn, though it can be active both diurnally and nocturnally. In cooler weather, individuals may shift to more crepuscular or diurnal patterns to optimize thermoregulation. This snake moves slowly and deliberately, often using its head to probe the soil as it forages or navigates burrows.
Seasonal activity peaks during the wet season from October to March in southeastern Brazil, when increased rainfall facilitates surface foraging and reproductive behaviors. During the dry season, activity levels decrease significantly, with individuals retreating underground to conserve energy and avoid desiccation.
The species is solitary throughout most of its life, interacting with others only during the brief mating period, and no territorial behaviors have been documented.

Defensive strategies

Micrurus corallinus exhibits a predominantly non-aggressive disposition, favoring evasion or concealment over confrontation when encountering potential threats. This species typically seeks refuge in leaf litter, burrows, or under cover, relying on its secretive habits to avoid detection by predators or humans. Bites occur almost exclusively when the snake is handled, cornered, or provoked, and envenomations by the genus Micrurus account for less than 1% of reported snakebites in Brazil, underscoring their low propensity for aggression.
A prominent defensive tactic employed by M. corallinus is the tail display, where the snake inverts and curls its tail to mimic the appearance of its head, presenting an enlarged black subcaudal tip as a false head to divert attacks. This deimatic behavior, which includes twitching or "jabbing" motions resembling a strike, was documented in field observations on 27 November 2021 in Paraguay, where an individual maintained the display for approximately four minutes while concealing its true head in leaf litter before retreating into a log crevice. Such tail displays are widespread in the genus Micrurus and serve to confuse predators, leveraging the snake's similar head and tail patterning for effective mimicry.
In addition to behavioral evasion, M. corallinus utilizes visual defenses through its striking tricolor banding, which functions primarily as aposematism to warn visual predators of its venomous nature. This bold red, black, and white pattern signals danger and may deter attacks before they occur, as evidenced by studies on mimicry systems in Neotropical forests where M. corallinus serves as a model species for non-venomous mimics. While the coloration is overtly warning, it can also facilitate camouflage by blending with the dappled light and leaf litter in forested habitats, enhancing passive avoidance. Its largely nocturnal activity further aids evasion by limiting encounters during periods of heightened predator activity.

Ecology

Diet

Micrurus corallinus is primarily an ophiophagous predator, specializing in elongate fossorial vertebrates. Its diet consists mainly of snakes (52%), amphisbaenians such as Leposternon microcephalum (21%), lizards (15%), and caecilians (12%), with occasional consumption of other prey including conspecific cannibalism. Analysis of stomach contents from specimens and literature up to 2021 confirms this composition, with earlier studies (19972017) showing higher proportions of amphisbaenians (approximately 70%). Blind snakes including Liotyphlops species have also been recorded.
As an ambush predator, M. corallinus forages by probing burrows and soil with its snout to locate prey, often capturing it underground before dragging it to the surface. Prey is swallowed whole, typically head-first, though tail-first ingestion occurs in about 40% of cases involving amphisbaenians due to their fossorial habits. Ingestion times are prolonged, averaging 42 minutes for an adult swallowing an amphisbaenian roughly half its body length, reflecting adaptations to handling elongate prey.
Dietary patterns show seasonal variation, with increased consumption of surface-active prey during the wet season, when heavy rainfall mobilizes fossorial items like amphisbaenians and caecilians onto the litter layer. In captivity, M. corallinus refuses fish-based diets, even elongate species like eels, underscoring its specialization on terrestrial elongate vertebrates. Digestive adaptations include a capacity for slow processing of such prey, minimizing energy expenditure on frequent meals in its cryptozoic lifestyle. 

Reproduction

Micrurus corallinus is oviparous, with females laying eggs in clutches following internal fertilization during the mating season. Mating occurs primarily in spring, from October to November in southeastern Brazil, aligning with the onset of the rainy season and increased activity levels. Although male-male combat rituals have been documented in some congeners, no such behaviors have been observed in M. corallinus, which exhibits a pattern of black rings arranged in monads consistent with species lacking this agonistic display.
Females undergo vitellogenesis from September to December, leading to oviposition between late spring and early summer (November to January). Clutch sizes range from 2 to 12 eggs, with an average of approximately 4.5 eggs per clutch, and this variation positively correlates with female body size (snout-vent length). Eggs are elongated and laid in concealed sites such as leaf litter or burrows, reflecting the species' semifossorial habits.
Incubation lasts 78 to 93 days under natural conditions in the subtropical Atlantic Forest, with hatchlings emerging from late summer to early autumn (February to May). Neonates measure 177197 mm in snout-vent length (average 184 mm) and weigh 2.022.76 g (average 2.40 g) at hatching, displaying the adult coloration pattern but with brighter red bands; they are independent and venomous immediately upon emergence.
Sexual maturity is reached at around 18 months of age for both sexes, with females maturing at a snout-vent length of about 450500 mm and males slightly smaller. Fecundity is moderate, typically limited to one clutch per reproductive season, though some studies suggest potential for a second in favorable conditions. Recent efforts in captive breeding (20192023) highlight challenges, including low adaptation to enclosure conditions, dietary issues, and high mortality rates among juveniles, resulting in limited successful reproductions despite advances in semen collection and maintenance protocols.

Venom

Composition

The venom of Micrurus corallinus is delivered through a pair of short, fixed proteroglyphous fangs located at the front of the upper jaw. The typical yield is 25 mg of dry weight per extraction, with an average of 3 mg, though this can vary based on specimen size and milking conditions.
The venom proteome is dominated by neurotoxic proteins, primarily three-finger toxins (3FTx), including alpha-neurotoxins that target nicotinic acetylcholine receptors, and phospholipases A2 (PLA2), which disrupt cell membranes and neuromuscular transmission. Minor components include L-amino acid oxidases, true venom lectins, snake venom metalloproteinases (SVMP), natriuretic peptides, Kunitz-type protease inhibitors, nerve growth factors (NGF), and waprins, with trace levels of cardiotoxins (also within the 3FTx family) and hyaluronidases that facilitate venom spread. Transcriptomic analyses confirm high expression of 3FTx and PLA2 genes in the venom gland, accounting for over 80% of toxin transcripts.
The venom exhibits high potency, with an intraperitoneal LD50 of 0.09 mg/kg in mice (1825 g). Recent gene expression studies using sublethal doses in murine models have identified inflammatory peptides and pathways, evidenced by upregulation of over 2,400 differentially expressed genes at the injection site, including acute-phase response factors linked to leukocyte infiltration and edema.
Venom composition demonstrates ontogenetic stability, with negligible shifts in toxin profiles between juveniles and adults, as proteomic surveys show consistent dominance of 3FTx and PLA2 across life stages. Geographic variation is minimal, with venomics of populations from southeastern Brazil revealing low inter-population differences in protein abundance and enzymatic activities.

Effects on prey and humans

The venom of Micrurus corallinus induces rapid neuromuscular blockade in prey through both presynaptic and postsynaptic mechanisms, primarily by inhibiting acetylcholine release at motor nerve endings and blocking nicotinic acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction. This presynaptic inhibition, mediated by phospholipases A2, reduces evoked neurotransmitter release, while postsynaptic three-finger toxins competitively bind to receptors, leading to flaccid paralysis. The resulting respiratory paralysis, due to diaphragm failure, typically causes death within a few hours in small mammals, immobilizing prey such as amphibians and reptiles efficiently.
In humans, M. corallinus bites are rare, comprising less than 1% of reported ophidian envenomations in Brazil, where the genus Micrurus accounts for approximately 1% of the roughly 27,000 annual snakebite cases. Bites by M. corallinus are rare and typically occur on the lower limbs during rural activities. Initial symptoms include local pain and swelling, which a 2025 study demonstrated are more pronounced and inflammatory than previously recognized, involving significant gene expression changes and histopathological alterations at the injection site. Systemic neurotoxicity manifests as descending paralysis with cranial nerve involvement, such as ptosis, diplopia, dysphagia, and dysarthria, progressing to generalized weakness without local necrosis or significant tissue damage. Untreated envenomations carry a high fatality risk due to respiratory failure, though exact rates for M. corallinus are not precisely quantified in literature.
Treatment relies on polyvalent antivenom produced by the Butantan Institute, raised against M. corallinus and M. frontalis venoms, administered intravenously to neutralize neurotoxins, combined with supportive measures like mechanical ventilation for respiratory support. With prompt antivenom administration and supportive care, the prognosis for M. corallinus envenomations is generally favorable, with fatalities being rare when treated appropriately. Early hospitalization is critical, as neurotoxic symptoms can onset within 12 hours and progress rapidly without intervention.

Conservation

Status

Micrurus corallinus is classified as Least Concern on the IUCN Red List of Threatened Species, an assessment conducted on 14 November 2014 with no subsequent updates recorded as of 2025. The species is considered common across its range, occurring in a variety of habitats including primary and secondary forests, plantations, and rural gardens, though population size remains unknown and data on trends are limited.
Population estimates are unavailable globally, but the species is believed to be stable overall, with no major threats identified and local persistence noted in core Atlantic Forest habitats despite data deficiencies in some areas. Its fossorial habits contribute to resilience in fragmented landscapes, and recent field observations confirm ongoing presence in protected regions without evidence of significant declines.
Micrurus corallinus is not included in the appendices of the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES). In Brazil, where the majority of its range occurs, the species is protected under national environmental legislation administered by the Brazilian Institute of Environment and Renewable Natural Resources (IBAMA), which regulates the collection, trade, and handling of native wildlife.

Threats

The primary threat to Micrurus corallinus is habitat loss due to deforestation in the Atlantic Forest, where approximately 88% of the original vegetation has been destroyed through agricultural expansion, logging, and urbanization. This extensive deforestation fragments the species' range, isolating subpopulations in remnant forest patches, as the snake relies on intact leaf litter in subtropical moist forests and cannot persist in cleared areas. In Paraguay, where the species occurs in highly fragmented Atlantic Forest habitats, studies highlight how such fragmentation exacerbates isolation and reduces connectivity for forest-dependent reptiles.
Direct collection poses a minor threat, primarily through captive breeding for venom extraction used in antivenom production, as M. corallinus is one of the key species hyperimmunized in Brazil to develop serum against coral snake envenomations. Roadkill also contributes to mortality, particularly along highways bordering forested edges in southeastern Brazil, where surveys have documented instances of the species among herpetofaunal casualties.
Climate change presents an emerging risk, with projections indicating drier conditions and altered precipitation patterns in parts of the Atlantic Forest that could indirectly affect M. corallinus by reducing availability of its fossorial prey, such as amphisbaenians and small snakes. Additionally, pesticide runoff from agricultural activities contaminates the food chain, posing ecotoxicological risks to snakes by accumulating in prey and affecting reproduction and survival. As a habitat specialist with no major natural predators, the species remains vulnerable to these cumulative pressures despite its current IUCN Least Concern status.
