Boot!
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 A boot is a type of footwear designed as a fitted covering for the foot, usually extending above the ankle and often part of the lower leg, typically made from durable materials such as leather or rubber to provide protection, support, and insulation in various environments. Boots originated in prehistory as essential protective gear, with evidence from cave paintings around 14,000 BCE depicting fur-lined versions and archaeological finds from approximately 3600 BCE in Armenia revealing leather constructions stuffed with grass for structure and warmth. Their development accelerated in ancient civilizations, where Mesopotamian cultures around 3000 BCE introduced stylized raised-tip designs symbolizing status, influencing styles across Persia, Greece, and Rome, including the Roman calceusa half-boot with a hard sole used by soldiers and civilians alike.
Throughout the Middle Ages, European boots evolved from Roman influences into high, supple-leather huese styles strapped to the thigh, while Crusaders adopted exaggerated pointed toes from Eastern designs, prompting sumptuary laws to regulate extravagance by social class. In the Americas, indigenous groups developed practical variants like soft-leather moccasins for plains tribes and tall, insulating kamiks from caribou hide among the Inuit to withstand Arctic conditions as low as -55F. The Renaissance brought ornate, embroidered forms with heelsemerging in the 16th century initially for men as symbols of power, such as red-heeled variants echoing Byzantine traditions.
The Industrial Revolution transformed boot production through innovations like the 1809 sole-tacking machine and the 1830 sewing machine, enabling mass manufacturing and diversification into specialized types, including the waterproof Wellington boot introduced in the early 19th century for hunting and outdoor pursuits. Today, boots encompass a wide array of formsfrom military and work variants with steel toes for safety, to fashion-forward high-heeled designs and athletic models using synthetic materialsserving functional roles in protection and mobility while continuing to signify cultural, occupational, and stylistic identities across global societies.

Background

Band formation and history

The Thing was formed in February 2000 by Swedish saxophonist Mats Gustafsson (playing alto, tenor, baritone, and slide saxophones, along with live electronics), Norwegian bassist Ingebrigt Hker Flaten (bass and live electronics), and Norwegian drummer Paal Nilssen-Love, initially convening for a series of concerts in Scandinavia and to record their debut album of Don Cherry compositions on the Crazy Wisdom label. The trio's name derives from a piece by the jazz trumpeter Don Cherry, whose tunes formed the core of their early repertoire, reflecting a tribute to his influential role in free jazz.
From the outset, The Thing established strong ties to the vibrant Scandinavian jazz scene, performing at local venues and festivals while drawing on the region's improvisational traditions that bridged Nordic folk elements with avant-garde experimentation. Their early collaborations extended internationally, including appearances at events like the Kongsberg Jazzfestival in Norway and partnerships with artists such as Joe McPhee, whose involvement in a 2001 quartet recording expanded their sound beyond Cherry's material to incorporate broader free jazz standards.
Beginning as a collaborative side project focused on high-energy interpretations of jazz classics, The Thing evolved into a dedicated core ensemble by the mid-2000s, emphasizing a raw, unpredictable live sound that fused free jazz with punk and garage rock influences, as heard in covers of artists like PJ Harvey and The Sonics. This progression from tribute band to innovative power trio informed their studio work, with releases like Garage (2004) serving as building blocks for their distinctive, boundary-pushing style that prioritized intense collective improvisation.

Preceding works and influences

Prior to Boot!, The Thing had established a robust discography that highlighted their maturation as a free jazz trio, beginning with their self-titled debut album in 2000 on Crazy Wisdom, which captured early improvisations inspired by Don Cherry's compositions. This was followed by Garage in 2004 on Smalltown Supersound, a pivotal release blending raw free jazz with rock-infused energy and covers like "Have Love Will Travel" by The Sonics, signaling their genre-crossing tendencies. In 2006, Action Jazz continued this trajectory on the same label, emphasizing high-octane collective improvisation and live performance dynamics. By 2009, Bag It!also on Smalltown Supersounddemonstrated a shift toward more structured yet explosive sessions, incorporating electronics and covers from sources like The Ex, while foreshadowing greater artistic autonomy through self-production elements. These releases underscored the band's progression from label-dependent output to a desire for independence, culminating in their formation of The Thing Records around 2013 for enhanced creative control.
The Thing's sound drew heavily from free jazz pioneers, particularly John Coltrane's spiritual intensity and modal explorations, as well as Duke Ellington's compositional sophistication and ensemble interplay, which informed their repertoire of standards and originals. Saxophonist Mats Gustafsson's involvement in parallel projects amplified these influences, infusing noise rock and punk aestheticscharacterized by abrasive textures and relentless drivefrom his Fire! ensemble and orchestra, where he pushed boundaries with amplified reeds and collective chaos. This fusion of jazz heritage with experimental rock edges shaped Boot!'s aggressive yet nuanced approach, building on the trio's prior works to prioritize unfiltered expression.
The decision to launch The Thing Records marked a conceptual turning point, announced in September 2013 as a means to bypass traditional label constraints and directly engage fans, with Boot! serving as the inaugural release in 2013 and symbolizing their full independence from imprints like Smalltown Supersound.

Recording and production

Studio sessions

The recording sessions for Boot! took place over three consecutive days, from February 11 to 13, 2013, at Grand Sport Studio in Oslo, Norway. The sessions were engineered by Martin Siewert, with mixing later handled by Siewert and band member Mats Gustafsson in April 2013 at Garnison7 in Vienna. Grand Sport Studio was selected for its intimate analog setup, which aligned well with the trio's improvisational style and allowed for capturing raw, unpolished performances.
The atmosphere during these sessions was notably intense, reflecting the deep rapport built by the band through over 600 live concerts prior to the recording. This high-energy environment facilitated live-like takes that emphasized the interplay between Gustafsson's saxophones, Ingebrigt Hker Flten's electric bass, and Paal Nilssen-Love's drums, with minimal post-production to maintain the music's spontaneous vitality. The compressed three-day timeline enabled on-site development of both originals and re-workings of standards, such as John Coltrane's "India" and Duke Ellington's "Heaven," underscoring the band's boundary-pushing approach as their first release on The Thing Records.

Composition and arrangement

The album Boot! consists of six tracks, with two jazz standards, three originals composed collectively by the trio of Mats Gustafsson, Ingebrigt Hker Flaten, and Paal Nilssen-Love, and one composition by Flaten. The standards include John Coltrane's "India" and Duke Ellington's "Heaven," the latter arranged by Gustafsson. The band originals are "Reboot," "Boot!," and "Epilog," while Flaten contributed "Red River."
Gustafsson played a central role in the arrangement process, particularly in reinterpreting Ellington's "Heaven"originally a quiet, melodic pieceas a platform for free improvisation, incorporating heavy riffs that build tension and risk structural collapse under their intensity. In the originals, such as "Reboot," the trio employed collaborative riff-trading, where Gustafsson's saxophone lines interlock with Flaten's electric bass and Nilssen-Love's drumming to create dynamic shifts from rigid pounding to quieter interludes, fostering a shared improvisational dialogue. These arrangements were finalized during intensive studio sessions in February 2013 at Grand Sport Studio in Oslo.
The album's improvisational techniques emphasize spontaneous development without fixed scores, starting from recurring motifslike the repeated, octave-lowered saxophone patterns in "India," which echo Coltrane's meditative dronesand escalating to chaotic peaks of free-for-all intensity, as seen in the track's transition from sludgy unisons to savage collective outbursts. In "Epilog," the trio explores stretched saxophone and bass tones that gradually evolve into funky rhythmic breaks amid distortion and meltdown, highlighting a minimalist approach in post-production to preserve the raw, live energy of their interplay.

Musical style and content

Genre characteristics

Boot! exemplifies the Scandinavian free jazz tradition, infused with elements of noise rock and punk, characterized by aggressive low-end propulsion from baritone saxophone, electric bass, and forceful drumming that creates a visceral, high-volume intensity. The album's core sound draws from the trio's punk-garage ethos, blending raw improvisation with rock's rhythmic drive to produce a "bludgeoning physicality" that reinterprets jazz standards in a modern, sludgy context.
Instrumentation centers on Mats Gustafsson's expansive saxophone arsenalincluding bass, baritone, tenor, and soprano variantswhich delivers overblown blasts, hoarse honks, and dissonant snarls, often mimicking a "maddened-elephant" ferocity. Ingebrigt Hker Flaten's electric bass functions like a guitar, generating metallic vamps and grinding riffs that anchor the low end with doomy, riff-driven grooves. Paal Nilssen-Love's drumming provides propulsive, rumbling momentum, evoking chaotic punk energy with crunching rock beats, frantic percussion, and building steam under the horns.
Key sonic traits include interlocking riffs that scaffold transitions from structured motifssuch as unisons and pounding groovesto unrestrained free improvisation, incorporating white noise effects like electric drone, fuzz, and skree for a "blunt-force kick" of bombast and meditative release. These elements evoke heightened energy, with tonal contrasts of quiet squawks yielding to savage free-for-alls, all while retaining a deconstructivist edge rooted in orthodox jazz traditions.
The album's exclamatory title, Boot!, underscores its raw, visceral attitude, extending the trio's history of high-volume performances that maintain form amid intensity, as seen in reinterpretations of works by John Coltrane and Duke Ellington.

Thematic elements and innovations

The album Boot! delves into themes of space, texture, and poetic dialogue, realized through vivid color and tone in the trio's interplay. Saxophonist Mats Gustafsson, bassist Ingebrigt Hker Flaten, and drummer Paal Nilssen-Love craft expansive sonic landscapes where instruments converse in urgent, improvised exchanges, evoking a Babel-like musical conversation that elevates disparate elements into a raucous whole. This dialogue manifests in tracks like "Reboot," where rigid pounding gives way to quiet saxophone squawks and pummeling grooves, creating a ritualistic texture that sways mystically with quick bass slices. Similarly, "Heaven," a reinterpretation of Duke Ellington's standard, features circling themes between bass and baritone sax amid scattershot cymbal hiss, blending droning exploration with aching squeals to heighten poetic tension.
A central tension between restraint and chaos permeates the album, with gradual builds leading to unhinged peaks that underscore low-spectrum intensity without sacrificing form. In the title track "Boot!," long and hoarse honks from Flaten and Gustafsson establish a grinding, doomy tempo, as Nilssen-Love gradually builds steam, mirroring the meditative restraint of John Coltrane's "Psalm" before erupting into punk-garage euphoria. Tracks like "Red River" introduce threatening noise rock vibes, channeling the heavy blues rock of Black Sabbath through Flaten's compositional voice, which anchors the trio's dynamic with distorted electric bass functioning more as guitar than traditional upright. This emphasis on rumbling depths and tectonic shiftsfrom sparse, controlled motifs to explosive free-for-allsmaintains a gritty, subterranean force, distinguishing Boot!'s textural depth.
Innovations in Boot! arise from the fusion of jazz standards with noise rock, exemplified by the opener "India," a sacrilegious yet sacred reworking of Coltrane's 1963 piece, lowered in octaves with loose, creaky unisons that drop into a savage free-for-all, recasting spiritual jazz as raw, sludgy stoner rock. Blending experimental Dutch punk influences like those of The Ex with free jazz improvisation in "Reboot," using heavy riffs as scaffolding for bombastic excursions. The self-release on the band's newly formed Thing Records enabled this uncompromised vision, allowing the trio to refine their extensive vocabulary of skree and pummel into bludgeoning physicality paired with heady free jazz chops, free from external constraints. Flaten's innovations within the trio dynamic are particularly evident in "Red River," where his bass drives noise rock aggression, innovating on his compositional style to bridge jazz legacies with rock's liberating energy.

Release

Formats and distribution

Boot! was released on November 12, 2013, through The Thing Records, a label founded by the band itself.
The album was made available in several formats, including compact disc (CD) packaged in a cardboard mini-LP gatefold sleeve, digital download, and a limited vinyl edition consisting of a 12-inch LP. There was no involvement from a major record label, reflecting the band's commitment to a DIY approach in production and release.
Distribution occurred primarily through independent channels, with digital and physical copies offered directly via the band's Bandcamp page for global accessibility, bypassing traditional distributors. As the inaugural release on The Thing Records, it was also available through specialized jazz outlets such as Trost Records and Forced Exposure, targeting free jazz enthusiasts in Europe and North America.

Promotion and launch

The Thing adopted an independent, band-led promotional strategy for Boot!, leveraging their established presence in the avant-garde jazz scene rather than traditional big-budget advertising. As the inaugural release on their newly founded label, The Thing Records, the album was previewed through digital platforms including Bandcamp, where full streaming was made available alongside high-quality downloads, and YouTube, featuring an early clip of the track "India" uploaded on November 5, 2013, to generate anticipation ahead of the November 12 release date. This approach emphasized direct fan engagement and relied heavily on word-of-mouth within the jazz and experimental music networks, building on the trio's reputation for high-intensity performances.
Launch events were integrated with the band's active touring schedule across Europe in late 2013, aligning the album's rollout with live demonstrations of its raw, energetic sound. A key performance occurred at London's Caf Oto on November 3, 2013, where the trio promoted both the new label and Boot!, delivering a set that opened with a blistering rendition of John Coltrane's "India" to showcase the album's fusion of free jazz ferocity and rock-infused dynamics. The tour, described by saxophonist Mats Gustafsson as "exploding" in intensity and reception, extended to multiple European cities, including stops that previewed tracks like "Boot!" and highlighted the trio's improvisational prowess tied to the record's themes of unbridled power and innovation. No formal release party was documented, but these concerts served as de facto launch platforms, reinforcing the album's connection to the band's visceral live energy.
Media outreach focused on jazz and alternative music publications, with interviews underscoring the milestone of self-releasing on The Thing Records and the album's emphasis on raw, evolving sonic experimentation. In a November 11, 2013, interview with The Quietus, Gustafsson discussed the relaxed three-day studio sessions in Oslo that captured the trio's hardest-edged sound to date, likening it to a "heavy beast" that stripped back to essentials while provoking through friction and dynamics, directly tying into their history of intense live collaborations. Free snippets and digital previews were offered via Bandcamp to further hype the project, allowing fans to experience the album's molten bass saxophone and electric bass grooves without cost, fostering organic buzz in the niche jazz community.

Reception

Critical reviews

Upon its 2013 release, Boot! received widespread critical acclaim for refining The Thing's signature intensity while blending jazz standards with originals, positioning the album as an evolution in the trio's discography.
Thom Jurek of AllMusic praised the album's integrative approach, which blurs lines between rock, funk, jazz, free improvisation, and noise, culminating in a "breathtaking sphere where a Babel-like musical conversation takes place, elevating all of its singular elements into a rough, raucous, glorious whole." He highlighted reinterpretations like John Coltrane's "India" as a "stomping, grinding, slow-burning" opener and Duke Ellington's "Heaven" as a swinging yet boundary-pushing venture, noting how the trio maintains cohesion amid sonic aggression.
In DownBeat, Shaun Brady awarded four stars, commending the album's "slow-grind ferocity" and "blunt-force kick to the gut" through low-end sonics, with Mats Gustafsson's saxophone writhing over feedback, Ingebrigt Hker Flaten's distorted bass threatening to "crack foundations," and Paal Nilssen-Love's driving rhythms. Brady emphasized the trio's mastery of intensity, juggling "sheer belligerent force" with precise control to avoid chaos, as heard in tracks like "Reboot" and "Epilog."
Mark Corroto of All About Jazz described the trio as "rock stars" capable of making Coltrane and Ellington "smile," underscoring their sacrilegious yet sacred take on "India" and overall punk-jazz energy. Similarly, Ludovic Hunter-Tilney in the Financial Times lauded the "enjoyably abrasive" instrumentals, including an energetic Stooges-style cover of Coltrane's "India" marked by blaring saxophone and clattering drums.
Across these reviews, common themes emerged of acclaim for the album's sonic aggression tempered by disciplined form, innovative fusions of standards like "India" and "Heaven" with originals such as "Red River," and the trio's ability to channel raw power without descending into disorder.

Commercial performance and legacy

Boot! achieved niche success within the free jazz and improvisational music scenes following its 2013 release on The Thing Records, the band's newly established independent label in collaboration with Trost Records. As a limited-run vinyl and CD pressing typical of indie jazz imprints, it did not chart on mainstream music lists but garnered strong demand through specialized outlets like Bandcamp and Squidco, reflecting interest from avant-garde enthusiasts. The album's visibility was further boosted by festival performances, helping sustain its circulation in European and North American improv circuits without broader commercial penetration.
In terms of legacy, Boot! marked a pivotal shift for The Thing toward self-directed production, enabling greater artistic autonomy and bolder experimentation in their catalog, as evidenced by subsequent releases like the 2015 album Shake, which built on its energetic reinterpretations of jazz standards. The record contributed to the ongoing Scandinavian free jazz revival by exemplifying the trio's fusion of punk-infused intensity with canonical influences like John Coltrane and Duke Ellington, receiving positive reviews including from Pitchfork and influencing later musicians in the genre, such as drummer Graham Costello, who cited its baritone saxophone-driven power as a key inspiration. While it received no major awards, Boot! solidified The Thing's reputation for independence and enduring appeal in avant-garde communities, with tracks occasionally featured in compilations and reissues highlighting the band's evolution.
