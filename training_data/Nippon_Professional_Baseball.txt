 Nippon Professional Baseball (NPB) is the highest level of professional baseball in Japan, operating as a structured league with 12 teams divided equally between the Central League and the Pacific League. Founded in 1936 with an initial roster of seven teams, it represents the pinnacle of organized baseball in the country, featuring a regular season of 143 games per teamconsisting of intraleague matchups, interleague play typically in May and June, and concluding in Septemberthat begins in late March, along with an All-Star Game in July, followed by postseason playoffs. The league's championship is decided by the Japan Series, a best-of-seven contest between the winners of the two leagues, usually held in October or November, crowning the annual NPB champion.
The origins of professional baseball in Japan trace back to 1872, when American educator Horace Wilson introduced the sport to students at Kaisei Academy in Tokyo, sparking its growth as a popular amateur and collegiate pastime. The professional era emerged in the 1930s, influenced by barnstorming tours from Major League Baseball (MLB) stars like Babe Ruth, which inspired the formation of the first pro teams; the Yomiuri Giants, one of the league's flagship franchises, were established in 1934 following such a tour. By 1936, the Japanese Baseball League launched as a single entity with seven clubs, but wartime disruptions led to a hiatus, and the league reemerged in 1950 as NPB with its distinctive two-league format to foster competition and regional rivalries. Over the decades, the structure has evolved, including the addition of teams to reach the current 12such as the Tohoku Rakuten Golden Eagles in 2004and the introduction of the Climax Series in 2007, a playoff system that includes wild-card berths to determine league representatives for the Japan Series.
NPB's Central League teams include the Yomiuri Giants (Tokyo Dome, founded 1934), Hanshin Tigers (Hanshin Koshien Stadium, 1935), Chunichi Dragons (Vantelin Dome Nagoya, 1936), Yokohama DeNA BayStars (Yokohama Stadium, 1949), Hiroshima Toyo Carp (Mazda Stadium, 1949), and Tokyo Yakult Swallows (Jingu Stadium, 1950). The Pacific League comprises the Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks (PayPay Dome, 1938), Orix Buffaloes (Kyocera Dome Osaka, 1936), Hokkaido Nippon-Ham Fighters (ES CON Field Hokkaido, 1946), Chiba Lotte Marines (ZOZO Marine Stadium, 1950), Saitama Seibu Lions (Belluna Dome, 1950), and Tohoku Rakuten Golden Eagles (Rakuten Mobile Park Miyagi, 2004). The Yomiuri Giants hold the record for most Japan Series titles with 22, underscoring their dominance, while the league as a whole has produced international success, including Japan's victories in the World Baseball Classic in 2006, 2009, and 2023, as well as Olympic gold in 2020. Beyond domestic play, NPB serves as a vital talent feeder to MLB, with prominent alumni such as Ichiro Suzuki, who debuted with the Orix BlueWave before starring in Seattle, and Shohei Ohtani, who rose through the Hokkaido Nippon-Ham Fighters to become a two-way sensation in Los Angeles.

History

Origins and early development

Baseball was introduced to Japan in 1872 by American educator Horace Wilson, who taught the game to his students at Kaisei Academy (now the University of Tokyo) in Tokyo as a means of promoting physical education and Western ideals. The sport quickly gained traction among the educated elite, spreading through academic institutions where it became a symbol of modernization during the Meiji era. By the early 20th century, intercollegiate rivalries, particularly between Keio University and Waseda University, had elevated baseball to a national pastime, with their annual games drawing massive crowds and fostering a competitive culture that emphasized discipline and teamwork.
As amateur play flourished, calls for professionalization grew amid economic pressures and public demand in the 1930s. In 1936, the Japanese Baseball League (JBL) was established as Japan's first professional circuit, comprising seven teams: the Tokyo Giants (Dai Tokyo), Osaka Tigers, Hanshin Club, Tokyo Seagulls, Nagoya Golden Dolphins, Koraku Spiders, and Nankai Club. The league's inaugural season began in April, marking a shift from university-dominated baseball to a structured professional framework supported by media mogul Matsutaro Shoriki, who owned the Tokyo Giants and leveraged newspaper promotion to boost attendance. The fall season concluded with the Osaka Tigers as champions based on league standings.
A pivotal figure in the JBL's early success was Victor Starffin, a Russian-born pitcher who became the first foreign professional player in Japanese baseball history when he joined the Tokyo Giants in 1936 at age 19. Immigrating to Japan as a child, Starffin dominated the 1930s with his exceptional fastball and control, posting a 2.09 ERA over his first three seasons and leading the Giants to multiple titles; he won 20 or more games in six of his first seven years, amassing 96 victories by 1942 and earning the nickname "The Emperor's Pitcher" for his cultural assimilation and on-field prowess.
World War II profoundly disrupted the JBL, as escalating military demands and resource shortages led to a truncated 1944 season of only about 35 games per team before full suspension in 1945 due to Allied bombings and player enlistments. The league resumed operations in 1946 under the Allied occupation, with reforms imposed by U.S. authorities emphasizing democratization and anti-militarism, including bans on corporate team names to promote public accessibility and the integration of returning soldiers. This postwar revival laid the groundwork for further institutional growth, though initial seasons were marked by logistical challenges and reduced rosters.

Establishment of NPB and postwar expansion

The reorganization of Japan's professional baseball landscape culminated in 1950 with the formation of Nippon Professional Baseball (NPB), emerging from the Japanese Baseball League (JBL), which had operated since 1936 but suspended activities during World War II. Influenced by the U.S. occupation authorities following Japan's defeat, who promoted baseball as a means of cultural reconstruction and democratization, the league adopted a structure modeled after Major League Baseball's dual-league system. This led to the division of the JBL's franchises into the Central League and Pacific League, initially comprising 15 teamseight in the Central League and seven in the Pacific Leagueto foster competition and expansion in the postwar era.
The inaugural NPB season began with the Central League featuring teams such as the Yomiuri Giants, Chunichi Dragons, Shochiku Robins, Osaka Tigers, Kokutetsu Swallows, and Hiroshima Carp, while the Pacific League included the Mainichi Orions, Nankai Hawks, Nishitetsu Clippers, Hankyu Braves, Tokyu Flyers, and Daiei Stars. The leagues' champions met in the first Japan Series, where the Pacific League's Mainichi Orions defeated the Central League's Shochiku Robins 4 games to 2, marking the start of NPB's postseason tradition and signaling the league's viability amid postwar economic recovery.
Early expansion efforts were tempered by financial challenges, leading to contractions through mergers in the 1950s. For instance, the Central League's Nishi-Nippon Pirates, which had joined as a new franchise in 1950, merged with the Pacific League's Nishitetsu Clippers after just one season in 1951 to form the Nishitetsu Lions, reflecting the strain on smaller-market teams. Further instability arose from postwar economic hardships, with several franchises facing deficits; after the 1957 season, the Pacific League's Daiei Unions merged with the Mainichi Orions to form the Daimai Orions in 1958, reducing redundancy and costs. These adjustments, driven by corporate owners' need for sustainability, ultimately stabilized NPB at 12 teamssix per leagueby 1958, laying the groundwork for long-term growth without further major disruptions until later decades.

Dynasties and key eras (1960s1990s)

The 1960s marked a transformative era for Nippon Professional Baseball (NPB), highlighted by the Yomiuri Giants' unprecedented V9 dynasty from 1965 to 1973, during which they secured nine consecutive Japan Series titles under manager Tetsuharu Kawakami. This dominance was fueled by a powerhouse lineup featuring first baseman Sadaharu Oh, who amassed a career-record 868 home runs over his tenure with the Giants, and third baseman Shigeo Nagashima, whose charismatic play captivated national audiences. The dynasty not only established the Giants as the preeminent force in NPB but also propelled the league's popularity, with attendance surging as television broadcasts of their games became must-see events, drawing millions and solidifying baseball's status as Japan's premier sport.
Amid this growth, the 1960s and 1970s saw structural changes through team relocations and expansions that expanded NPB's footprint and fan base. The league maintained its 12-team structure after adding the sixth teams to each circuit by 1958, but relocations like the Taiyo Whales' move from Kawasaki to Yokohama in 1978renaming them the Yokohama Taiyo Whalesbrought professional baseball closer to urban centers and boosted regional rivalries. This period's fan-driven popularity boom was evident in record crowds at Korakuen Stadium, where Giants games often exceeded 50,000 attendees, reflecting broader economic prosperity and the sport's integration into everyday culture.
Key events underscored the era's intense fan culture, including the 1964 riot at Nishinomiya Stadium, where thousands of Hanshin Tigers supporters stormed the field and clashed with police following a controversial umpire call in a game against the Hiroshima Carp, resulting in arrests and property damage. Similarly, the origins of the "Curse of the Colonel" emerged in 1985 after the Tigers' Japan Series victory, when ecstatic fans in Osaka hurled a KFC Colonel Sanders statue into the Dotonbori River during celebrations, an act later blamed for the team's subsequent 38-year championship drought. A milestone in this period came on September 3, 1977, when Oh hit his 756th career home run against the Yakult Swallows' pitcher Isao Yagyu, surpassing Hank Aaron's then-major league record and symbolizing NPB's global prowess.
The 1980s shifted dominance to the Pacific League's Seibu Lions, dubbed the "Invincible Seibu" for their seven Japan Series titles between 1982 and 1992, including back-to-back wins in 19821983, 19861987, and 19901992. Under managers like Tatsuro Hirooka and Akira Ogi, the Lions' success relied on a formidable rotation anchored by left-hander Kimiyasu Kudo, who debuted in 1982 and contributed to eight of those championships as a player before adding more as a manager later. This era exemplified strategic pitching and disciplined hitting, with the Lions posting league-best records like 8640 in 1983, further elevating NPB's competitive intensity. The 1990s began to see early player defections to Major League Baseball, exemplified by pitchers like Hideo Nomo in 1995, signaling NPB's growing international influence.

Modern reforms and recent developments (2000s2025)

In 2004, Nippon Professional Baseball faced a significant crisis when the Osaka Kintetsu Buffaloes and Orix BlueWave announced plans to merge due to mounting financial losses, threatening to reduce the number of teams from 12 to 11. This proposal sparked widespread fan opposition and led to the first players' strike in NPB history, which lasted two days on September 1819 and canceled 12 games. The dispute was resolved through negotiations that preserved the 12-team structure by expanding with a new franchise, the Hokkaido Nippon-Ham Fighters, relocating from Tokyo and debuting in 2004.
To enhance fan engagement and address competitive imbalances between the Central and Pacific Leagues, NPB introduced interleague play in 2005, pitting teams from each league against one another in a mid-season series of 18 games per team. This format aimed to increase rivalries and attendance, particularly benefiting the Pacific League by exposing its teams to broader audiences. Building on this momentum, the Climax Series playoff system was launched in 2007, allowing the top three teams from each league to compete in a postseason tournament for Japan Series berths, thereby injecting drama into the regular season's conclusion and promoting parity.
The 2010s and 2020s brought further adaptations amid external pressures, including the COVID-19 pandemic, which delayed the 2020 season start to June 19 and shortened it to 120 games per team from the standard 143, with strict no-spectator protocols initially in place. The 2021 season faced ongoing disruptions, such as postponed games and limited crowds, but maintained a full schedule overall while prioritizing health measures. Concurrently, digital streaming expanded significantly, with platforms like Pacific League TV enabling global access to Pacific League games and attracting around 490,000 North American viewers in 2024 alone, signaling a shift toward international outreach.
Recent on-field success has highlighted shifting dynamics, with the Hanshin Tigers capturing the 2023 Japan Series title after a 38-year drought by defeating the Orix Buffaloes 43. In 2024, the Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks secured the Pacific League pennant but fell to the Yokohama DeNA BayStars in the Japan Series, marking DeNA's first championship in 26 years. The 2025 season saw the SoftBank Hawks clinch the Pacific League with an 87524 record, advancing to the Japan Series where they rallied to defeat the Hanshin Tigers 41 for their first title since 2019. Attendance has rebounded robustly, reaching a record 27 million fans league-wide in 2025, surpassing pre-pandemic figures from 2019 and averaging over 31,500 per game.
Amid these developments, NPB has addressed online harassment to protect players and sustain positive fan engagement. NPB maintains social media guidelines prohibiting defamatory or harassing content targeting players, staff, or the organization, with measures including reporting violations and requesting content removal. In 2023, NPB appealed to fans to avoid posting or spreading slander and to uphold proper conduct online. In 2025, the Japan Professional Baseball Players Association introduced AI-based monitoring to detect abusive posts on social media during the Climax Series and Japan Series, enabling reports to platforms and coordination with teams.

League Organization

Governance and structure

Nippon Professional Baseball (NPB) operates as a commissioner-led organization encompassing 12 professional teams, evenly split between the Central League (CL) and the Pacific League (PL), with each league featuring six franchises. This dual-league structure, established in 1950, fosters intra-league competition while allowing for interleague play and a unified postseason.
The Commissioner's Office, headed by Sadayuki Sakakibara since his appointment in December 2022, holds primary responsibility for league governance, including rule enforcement, dispute resolution, scheduling oversight, and maintaining the integrity of the sport. Sakakibara, former president and chairman of Toray Industries Inc. and chairman of Kansai Electric Power Co., succeeded Atsushi Saito and has focused on international expansion and operational modernization during his tenure.
Complementing the commissioner's authority is the Japan Professional Baseball Players Association (JPBPA), the official union representing all NPB players since its formation in 1965. The JPBPA engages in collective bargaining on issues such as salaries, contracts, working conditions, and player mobility, including negotiations over the posting system for transfers to Major League Baseball. It also advocates for player welfare, such as health benefits and retirement provisions, ensuring a balanced relationship between players and club owners.
A notable distinction in league rules pertains to the designated hitter (DH), which the Pacific League has utilized since 1975 to allow a batter to substitute for the pitcher in the lineup. The Central League, traditionally adhering to National League-style rules without a DH, unanimously approved its adoption starting in the 2027 season, aligning both leagues and enhancing offensive play across NPB. Each team competes in a 143-game regular season, balancing intra-league and interleague matchups to determine playoff qualifiers.
NPB's financial framework emphasizes competitive parity without a salary cap, relying instead on a structured amateur draft system and revenue mechanisms from central sources like broadcasting rights. The annual draft, held in October, begins with a lottery for the first round where all 12 teams can select from eligible high school, college, and independent league players, followed by a waiver-style allocation in later rounds to prevent talent hoarding by wealthier clubs. This system, introduced in 1965, helps distribute emerging talent evenly and supports long-term balance.

Seasons, scheduling, and interleague play

The Nippon Professional Baseball (NPB) regular season spans from late March to early October, typically commencing around March 28 and concluding by October 5, with each of the 12 teams playing 143 games over the course of approximately six months. This schedule accommodates a mix of day and night games, reflecting Japan's urban viewing habits and work schedules, while the annual All-Star series interrupts play in July, featuring exhibition games between top players from the Central League (CL) and Pacific League (PL) over two to three days, usually the third week of the month. The CL and PL operate as separate circuits within NPB, but their schedules align to facilitate balanced competition across the season.
Interleague play, pitting CL teams against PL opponents, was introduced in 2005 to boost fan interest and cross-league rivalries, marking a significant evolution in NPB's format. Interleague play began on May 6, 2005. Currently, each team plays 18 interleague gamesthree against each of the six rivals from the opposite leaguescheduled in a concentrated block from mid-May to early June, allowing for focused matchups without disrupting intra-league play. Since 2013, NPB has maintained a more balanced overall schedule, equalizing the number of home and away games within and across leagues to ensure fairness in travel and rest.
Scheduling in NPB includes provisions for weather disruptions and game resolutions, with rainouts typically rescheduled at the end of the regular season to avoid mid-season imbalances, though persistent rain may lead to official postponements announced hours before first pitch. Tie games, a distinctive NPB feature, occur if no run is scored after up to three extra innings (extending play to a maximum of 12 innings), at which point the game ends in a draw rather than continuing indefinitely, preserving player health and schedule integrity. For the 2025 season, minor adjustments were made to the calendar, including built-in breaks from June 23-26 and July 22-25 to accommodate the All-Star festivities and potential international commitments, such as preparations for upcoming World Baseball Classic qualifiers, though no direct overlap with events like the Premier12 (held in late 2024) impacted the core schedule.
Broadcasting plays a central role in NPB's visibility, with national deals enabling widespread access; NHK provides free-to-air coverage of select high-profile games, including All-Star events and key regular-season matchups, while J Sports holds rights to extensive live telecasts, particularly for Pacific League games and interleague series, reaching subscribers via cable and streaming. Attendance remains robust, reflecting strong fan engagement; through September 30, 2025, NPB recorded a total of 26,742,631 spectators across games, averaging 31,536 per contestan increase of 465 from 2024 and the highest cumulative figure in league history to that point.

Player agreements and systems

The Nippon Professional Baseball (NPB) utilizes an annual amateur draft, instituted in 1965, as the primary mechanism for acquiring young domestic talent and promoting competitive balance among its 12 teams. Eligible participants include recent high school graduates, university and college players, and athletes from corporate industrial leagues, who enter the draft pool without prior professional contracts. The draft conference occurs each October, with the first round employing a nomination-lottery system: all teams simultaneously nominate their preferred player; if a player receives a single nomination, that team secures negotiation rights, but multiple nominations trigger a random lottery among the interested clubs to determine the winner. Losing teams then renominate from the remaining eligible players, repeating the lottery process until every team has selected a first-round pick. Subsequent rounds shift to a snake-draft format, proceeding in reverse order of the prior season's final standings and alternating direction each round, starting with the worst-performing team in the interleague standings. This structure ensures equitable access to top prospects while allowing teams to pass on later selections if desired.
Foreign players, classified as non-Japanese nationals without Japanese citizenship, are integral to NPB rosters but subject to strict quotas to prioritize domestic development. As of 2025, each team may register up to four foreign players on its active 25-man game-day roster, limited to no more than three pitchers or three position players among them; the 70-man reserve roster allows up to five foreigners overall. These restrictions apply only to the active and reserve lists, with no salary caps imposed, enabling teams to pursue high-caliber international talent through open bidding. Violations, such as fielding an excess of foreigners, result in penalties like ejections or fines, as seen in rare instances during postseason play. This system, refined over decades, balances global appeal with the league's emphasis on nurturing Japanese players.
The posting system facilitates transitions from NPB to Major League Baseball (MLB), bridging the two leagues since its inception in 1998 through a bilateral agreement. Under the framework revised in 2012, an NPB club may post a playertypically after the Japan Serieswith MLB teams then having 45 days to negotiate a contract; if signed, the NPB team receives a release fee scaled by the MLB deal's value (e.g., 20% of the first $25 million plus 17.5% thereafter for contracts over $50 million). This compensates the originating club for development costs and prevents unilateral departures. A prominent example is pitcher Yoshinobu Yamamoto's 2023 posting by the Orix Buffaloes, which yielded over $50 million to the club upon his signing with the Los Angeles Dodgers, highlighting the system's role in high-stakes player mobility. Players under 25 or with fewer than six NPB seasons remain subject to MLB's international signing pools if posted early, but established stars like Yamamoto negotiate as free agents.
NPB player contracts operate under a reserve system that limits mobility, with domestic free agency unavailable until a player accrues eight or nine years of service timenine for high school draftees and eight for college or industrial league entrantsto retain talent within the league. After six years, players gain eligibility for salary arbitration, where an independent committee reviews performance metrics like batting average, ERA, and Wins Above Replacement to mediate disputes between player and club offers, often resulting in significant raises for standouts. Rookies and early-career players sign standardized one-year deals with modest base pay, supplemented by performance bonuses, while veterans command multi-year guarantees. The average annual salary across NPB in 2025 stands at approximately 50 million, reflecting steady growth amid economic pressures and union negotiations for earlier free agency rights.
Player development occurs primarily through NPB's affiliated farm system, comprising minor league teams in the Eastern League (primarily for Pacific League affiliates) and Western League (primarily for Central League affiliates), where prospects hone skills in shorter seasons of about 100-120 games. These farm clubs, operated by NPB franchises, focus on refining amateur draftees and rehabilitating injured players, with promotions based on performance rather than rigid levels like MLB's. This setup supports a pipeline from draft to stardom, exemplified by multi-year farm stints for future aces like Yamamoto before their NPB debuts.

Teams

Central League franchises

The Central League of Nippon Professional Baseball comprises six franchises, each with deep roots in Japanese baseball history dating back to the league's formation in 1950. These teams represent major urban centers and have collectively won all 76 Central League pennants awarded through the 2025 season. The Yomiuri Giants hold the most titles with 39, followed by the Hanshin Tigers with 10, the Chunichi Dragons with 9, the Hiroshima Toyo Carp with 8, the Tokyo Yakult Swallows with 8, and the Yokohama DeNA BayStars with 2.
The Yomiuri Giants, based in Tokyo and founded in 1934 as the Great Japan Tokyo Baseball Club, are the oldest franchise in NPB and the league's most successful team. Owned by the Yomiuri Shimbun media conglomerate, the Giants have dominated the Central League with 39 pennants, including a record nine consecutive from 1961 to 1969. Their iconic orange-and-black uniforms and massive fanbase at Tokyo Dome have made them Japan's "national team," though recent years have seen a shift toward rebuilding, finishing third in the 2025 regular season with a 70-69-4 record.
The Hanshin Tigers, established in 1935 in Nishinomiya and playing at Hanshin Koshien Stadium, are known for their passionate "Yellow Tide" supporters and a history of dramatic highs and lows. With 10 Central League titles, their most recent coming in 2025, the Tigers clinched the 2025 pennant with an 85-54-4 mark, advancing to the Japan Series before falling to the Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks in five games. Outfielder Koji Chikamoto emerged as a standout, extending his club record with 10 consecutive hits in the Japan Series and earning the Golden Spirit Award for his contributions on and off the field.
Founded in 1936 as the Nagoya Golden Dolphins in Nagoya, the Chunichi Dragons have a storied legacy tied to the Chunichi Shimbun newspaper, evolving through names like Chubu Nippon before settling on their current moniker in 1954. They have secured 9 Central League championships, with notable victories in 1974ending the Giants' V9 dynastyand 2007, when they won their second Japan Series title after a 53-year drought. In 2025, the Dragons finished fourth with a 63-76-4 record, focusing on young talent development at Vantelin Dome Nagoya.
The Hiroshima Toyo Carp, originally the Hiroshima Carp since entering the league in 1950, adopted their full name in 1968 and play at Mazda Stadium in Hiroshima. Symbolizing resilience for a city recovering from the atomic bombing, the Carp broke a 25-year title drought with their first Central League pennant in 1975 and have since added seven more, including three straight from 2016 to 2018. Owned by the Toyo Industries (now Mazda) group, they ended the 2025 season fifth at 59-80-4, emphasizing community ties and fan engagement.
The Tokyo Yakult Swallows, tracing their origins to 1950 as the Kokutetsu Swallows (National Railway), underwent multiple rebrandings before becoming the Yakult Swallows in 1974 under the Yakult Honsha beverage company. Based at Meiji Jingu Stadium, they have captured 8 Central League titles, highlighted by five Japan Series wins from 1978 to 2021, including a dramatic 2021 championship after 20 years. The Swallows placed sixth in 2025 with a 56-83-4 record, relying on a mix of veterans and prospects in their compact Tokyo ballpark.
The Yokohama DeNA BayStars, founded in 1950 as the Taiyo Whales in Shimonoseki before relocating to Yokohama in 1978, were acquired by DeNA Co. in 2012 and rebranded accordingly. With only 2 Central League pennants (1960 and 1998), they play at Yokohama Stadium and notched a franchise milestone by winning the 2024 Japan Series after 31 years. In 2025, they finished second with a 71-66-6 record, showcasing offensive firepower led by import players and domestic stars.
Within the Central League, the most storied rivalry is between the Yomiuri Giants and Hanshin Tigers, often dubbed the "Japan Series before the Series" due to their intense, high-stakes matchups that draw massive crowds and embody Tokyo-Osaka regional pride. This feud, fueled by the teams' historical dominance and fanbase fervor, has produced memorable clashes since the 1930s, including the Tigers' 2023 sweep of the Giants in the Climax Series en route to their Japan Series title.

Pacific League franchises

The Pacific League of Nippon Professional Baseball (NPB) features six franchises, each with deep roots in Japanese corporate culture and regional identities, competing in a circuit historically viewed as the underdog to the more tradition-bound Central League due to fewer overall Japan Series titles (37 for PL versus 38 for CL through 2024). These teams, primarily owned by major corporations, emphasize aggressive scouting, international talent acquisition, and the use of the designated hitter rule, which has given the league a more offense-oriented style since its adoption in 1975. The 2025 season highlighted the league's competitiveness, with the Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks clinching the pennant and advancing to win the Japan Series. Key intra-league rivalries, such as the geographic and stylistic clash between the Hawks and Hokkaido Nippon-Ham Fightersoften dubbed the "Pacific rivalry" for their battles over northern versus southern dominanceadd intensity to the schedule.

Stadiums and franchise relocations

Nippon Professional Baseball (NPB) teams play in a variety of stadiums across Japan, ranging from historic open-air venues to modern domed facilities. The Tokyo Dome, home of the Yomiuri Giants since 1988, is one of the league's most prominent venues with a baseball capacity of 46,000, featuring a fully enclosed air-supported roof that allows for year-round play regardless of weather. Hanshin Koshien Stadium, the Hanshin Tigers' home since 1937, opened in 1924 and holds 47,000 spectators; it is renowned for its ivy-covered outer walls and the world's last all-dirt professional infield, including basepaths, which contribute to its unique playing conditions and cultural significance in Japanese baseball. The Fukuoka PayPay Dome, occupied by the Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks since 1993, accommodates 38,500 fans and features Japan's only retractable-roof dome, enabling outdoor-like atmosphere when weather permits while protecting against rain.
Franchise relocations have occasionally reshaped NPB's geographic footprint, often driven by efforts to build regional fanbases and address urban market challenges. The Hokkaido Nippon-Ham Fighters relocated from Tokyo to Sapporo in 2004, moving from the Tokyo Dome to the Sapporo Dome (now primarily a multi-purpose venue) to tap into Hokkaido's untapped market and foster local identity; this shift marked the first major team move to northern Japan in decades. Similarly, the Tohoku Rakuten Golden Eagles were established as an expansion franchise in 2005, based in Sendai from inception rather than relocating from Tokyo, filling a Pacific League void in the Tohoku region; the team later symbolized regional recovery after the 2011 Great East Japan Earthquake by resuming play and community efforts in the affected area.
Recent developments emphasize modernization and sustainability amid aging infrastructure, with the average NPB stadium age hovering around 40 years. Notable upgrades include the installation of advanced LED floodlights at ZOZO Marine Stadium, home of the Chiba Lotte Marines, enhancing visibility and energy efficiency during night games in recent years. While no entirely new stadiums opened between 2005 and 2022, the Es Con Field Hokkaido debuted in 2023 as the Fighters' new home, incorporating high-tech features like a retractable roof. Looking ahead, NPB's 2025 Green Baseball Project introduces league-wide sustainability initiatives, such as renewable energy adoption and waste reduction at venues, to align with environmental goals while preserving the sport's traditions.

Competition Format

Regular season structure

The regular season of Nippon Professional Baseball (NPB) consists of 143 games per team, played over approximately six months from late March to late October, with each team facing its league opponents 25 times and interleague rivals 18 times. Games follow the standard structure of nine innings, with the home team batting last in each inning, and the team with the most runs at the end declared the winner. If the score is tied after nine innings, extra innings are played to resolve the outcome, but ties are permitted if the game remains deadlocked after the 12th inning, a rule in place since 2001 to prevent excessively long contests.
Each NPB team maintains a 70-player organizational roster, from which a 28-player active roster is selected for match days, allowing flexibility in lineup construction while limiting the number of available substitutes. Substitutions are unlimited in number, enabling managers to adjust for injuries, matchups, or strategy without penalty, though position players cannot re-enter once removed. Mound visits by coaches or managers are unlimited but must be conducted efficiently to maintain game flow, with no formal cap like those in other professional leagues.
To promote a brisk pace, NPB enforces a 30-second interval between batters and other timing guidelines, though it has not adopted a strict pitch clock as of the 2025 season, unlike Major League Baseball. This contributes to an average game duration of about three hours for nine-inning contests, with recent seasons showing times around 3 hours and 7 minutes in 2023. A mercy rule allows games to end early if one team leads by 10 or more runs after seven innings (or five innings in cases affected by weather or other disruptions), ensuring lopsided matches do not extend unnecessarily; this threshold was confirmed as standard entering 2025 without reported tweaks.
NPB emphasizes traditional statistics in evaluating player performance, with batting average (hits divided by at-bats) serving as the primary measure of offensive success and earned run average (ERA, earned runs allowed per nine innings) as the key pitching metric. Complete gameswhere a starting pitcher completes the full nine innings (or more)are more common in NPB than in MLB, reflecting a cultural preference for pitcher endurance and strategic depth, with multiple pitchers achieving 20 or more in a single season historically. These metrics, tracked meticulously by the league, highlight NPB's focus on fundamental skills over advanced analytics in regular-season play.

Postseason playoffs and Climax Series

The Climax Series, NPB's postseason playoff system, determines each league's representative for the Japan Series by pitting the top three regular-season finishers against one another in a two-stage elimination tournament. Introduced by the Pacific League in 2004 as the Pacific League Playoffs to inject excitement into the postseason and broaden participation beyond the regular-season champion, the format was adopted league-wide in 2007 when the Central League implemented an identical structure, unifying it under the Climax Series name. This shift marked a departure from NPB's prior postseason reliance on regular-season winners alone, aiming to extend competition into October and reward strong overall performance.
In each league, the first-place team earns a bye to the Final Stage, while the second- and third-place teams compete in the First Stagea best-of-three series hosted entirely at the second-place team's home stadium, with the winner advancing after securing two victories. The Final Stage then features a best-of-six matchup (first to four wins) between the First Stage winner and the first-place team, who receive an automatic one-win advantage to reflect their regular-season dominance, effectively starting the series at 1-0. Scheduling occurs in mid-to-late October, with games 1, 2, and 6 (if necessary) at the first-place team's venue and games 3 through 5 at the challenger's; ties are possible in individual games, and if the series ends 3-3, the first-place team advances on tiebreaker. Home-field arrangements favor higher seeds consistently, though the overall playoff hosting rotates annually between leagues for the Japan Series berth.
The Climax Series has played a pivotal role in enhancing competitive parity across NPB, enabling lower-seeded teams to challenge dominants and preventing regular-season leaders from coasting unchallenged. Since its full adoption in 2007, 38 Climax Series have been contested (19 per league through 2025), with notable upsets underscoring its impact; for instance, the Hokkaido Nippon-Ham Fighters claimed the inaugural Pacific League title in 2006 by sweeping the Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks 3-0 in the pre-unified format's Final Stage, marking their breakthrough after years of mid-pack finishes. More recently, in the 2025 Central League Final Stage, the Hanshin Tigers defended their top seed's advantage by sweeping the Yokohama DeNA BayStars 4-0 (accounting for the 1-0 lead), securing their Japan Series spot and exemplifying how the system sustains high-stakes drama. This structure has fostered broader fan engagement and roster depth, as teams vie for top-three positioning rather than solely the pennant.

Japan Series and champions

The Japan Series is the annual championship series of Nippon Professional Baseball (NPB), pitting the winners of the Central League (CL) and Pacific League (PL) Climax Series against each other in a best-of-seven playoff to determine the Japan champion. The series typically spans late October to early November, following the regular season and intra-league postseason. Since 2007, it has followed a best-of-seven format, expanded from the previous best-of-six structure to heighten competition and align with international standards.
Inaugurated in 1950 as the first cross-league postseason matchup in modern Japanese professional baseball, the Japan Series has been contested 76 times as of 2025. The Yomiuri Giants hold the record for most championships with 22 titles, reflecting their historical dominance in the CL. Overall, both leagues have secured 38 victories each in the all-time tally.
The 2025 Japan Series featured the PL's Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks against the CL's Hanshin Tigers, with the Hawks prevailing 41 to claim their 12th title. This victory marked a strong postseason performance for the Hawks, who had advanced directly as PL regular-season winners before sweeping their Climax Series opponent. The series Most Valuable Player (MVP) award, first presented in 1950 to honor the standout contributor, went to infielder Hotaka Yamakawa of the SoftBank Hawks for his clutch hitting and defensive plays.

Individual awards and honors

The Most Valuable Player (MVP) award in Nippon Professional Baseball recognizes the player who has contributed most significantly to their team's success during the regular season, with one recipient selected per league annually by a panel of sportswriters and baseball experts. The award originated in the pre-NPB Japanese Baseball League in 1937 but has been presented separately for the Central and Pacific Leagues since 1958, following the two-league format established in 1950. Notable recipients include Sadaharu Oh, who earned a record nine Central League MVPs between 1964 and 1977, all with the Yomiuri Giants, highlighting his dominance as a first baseman and home run hitter.
The Best Nine award honors the top performer at each position in both leagues, selected by votes from newspaper, television, and radio reporters based on overall offensive and defensive contributions during the season. Established in 1947shortly after NPB's formationit recognizes nine players in the Central League and ten in the Pacific League to accommodate the designated hitter rule. The award emphasizes balanced excellence rather than single-category dominance, with ties resolved by additional voting; for example, in 2004, Greg LaRocca and Masahiro Araki shared the Central League second base honor.
The Golden Glove award, sponsored by the Mitsui Group and originally called the Diamond Glove Award until its renaming in 1985, celebrates outstanding defensive skills at each position, with one winner per spot in each league determined by votes from experienced baseball journalists. Introduced in 1972 to parallel MLB's version, it covers pitchers, catchers, infielders, and three outfielders, prioritizing metrics like fielding percentage, assists, and putouts alongside qualitative assessments of range and arm strength. Multiple winners are common for elite defenders; for instance, Tomoyuki Sugano has secured the Central League pitching award four times as of 2024.
The Eiji Sawamura Award, NPB's highest individual pitching honor since 1947, is bestowed annually on the league's top pitcher regardless of league affiliation, named after Eiji Sawamura, a pioneering hurler who died in World War II. Selection by a committee considers a combination of criteria such as at least 15 wins, a 2.50 ERA or better, 10 complete games, 200 innings pitched, 150 strikeouts, and a .600 winning percentage, though flexibility allows for exceptional overall impact even if not all benchmarks are met. The award is occasionally withheld if no pitcher qualifies (as in 2024) or shared in tied cases (like 1966). Yoshinobu Yamamoto captured it consecutively from 2021 to 2023 with the Orix Buffaloes, posting ERAs under 1.70 each year and leading in strikeouts, before departing for Major League Baseball. In 2025, Hiromi Ito of the Hokkaido Nippon-Ham Fighters received the award for his outstanding performance.
The Rookie of the Year award identifies the most promising first-year player in each league, limited to Japanese nationals and selected since 1950 by baseball writers based on statistical performance, potential, and team impact. Unlike other honors, it may go unawarded in seasons lacking a clear standout (such as 1963 in both leagues), and ties can result in multiple recipients. The award has launched careers of future stars, with pitchers historically dominating; for example, the inaugural 1950 winners were pitchers Nobuo Oshima (Central League) and Atsushi Aramaki (Pacific League).

Hall of Fame inductees

The Japanese Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum was established in 1959 in Tokyo to honor outstanding contributions to baseball in Japan, initially located adjacent to Korakuen Stadium before relocating to the Tokyo Dome in 1988. The museum preserves artifacts and history while the Hall recognizes lifetime achievements through annual elections conducted by specialized committees, including the Sportswriters Committee, Expert Committee, Players Division, and Special Committee for umpires and other contributors.
Inductees are selected in three main categories: players, executives and managers (under experts), and umpires, with candidates typically requiring at least five years of retirement and eligibility after 10 or more years of service in professional baseball. Election demands a minimum of 75% approval from voters in the relevant committee, ensuring only those with exceptional impact are enshrined; as of 2025, the Hall includes 222 members.
Among notable player inductees, Sadaharu Oh was honored in 1994 for his record-setting career with the Yomiuri Giants, including 868 home runsthe most in Japanese professional baseball historyand nine Japan Series titles as both player and manager. Ichiro Suzuki joined in 2025 after receiving 323 of 349 votes (92.6%) in his first year of eligibility, recognized for his pre-MLB tenure with the Orix BlueWave where he amassed 1,278 hits, seven consecutive Pacific League batting titles from 1994 to 2000, and two Japan Series championships.
The 2025 class also featured reliever Hitoki Iwase, inducted as a player for his Japan record of 407 saves over 17 seasons with the Chunichi Dragons, including five league titles; third baseman Masayuki Kakefu, elected as an expert for his key role in the Hanshin Tigers' 1985 Japan Series win and multiple batting honors; and umpire Hiroya Tomizawa, selected via the Special Committee for officiating 3,775 games and pioneering umpire training exchanges with Major League Baseball.

Records and Statistics

Single-season and career batting records

Nippon Professional Baseball (NPB) batting records, tracked since the inception of the Japanese Baseball League in 1936, highlight the league's emphasis on contact hitting and opportunistic offense, though smaller ballpark dimensions compared to Major League Baseball have historically contributed to elevated power numbers. These records reflect the evolution of the game in Japan, where players often prioritize consistency and speed alongside power, influenced by the league's 143-game schedule and hitter-friendly environments.
In single-season achievements, Randy Bass set the benchmark for batting average with a .389 mark in 1986 while playing for the Hanshin Tigers, a figure that underscores the potential for high-average seasons in NPB's compact venues. For power, Wladimir Balentien established the home run record with 60 in 2013 for the Tokyo Yakult Swallows, surpassing the previous mark of 55 held by Sadaharu Oh since 1964; this outburst, aided by favorable park factors at Jingu Stadium, remains the league's pinnacle for long balls in one year.
Career records emphasize longevity and accumulation, with Isao Harimoto amassing 3,085 hits over 30 seasons from 1959 to 1989, primarily with the Nishitetsu Lions and other Pacific League teams, a total that stands as the all-time NPB leader and reflects his disciplined approach at the plate. Sadaharu Oh holds the career home run crown with 868 from 1959 to 1980, all with the Yomiuri Giants, a global professional baseball record that benefited from Tokyo's Korakuen Stadium but was built on consistent excellence across 2,786 games.

Single-season and career pitching records

In Nippon Professional Baseball (NPB), single-season pitching records highlight the endurance and dominance of early-era pitchers, often under challenging conditions including wartime play. The most wins in a single season is held by Victor Starffin, who recorded 42 victories in 1939 while pitching for the Yomiuri Giants, a mark achieved with a 1.73 ERA over 52 starts and 403 innings. Starffin followed this with 38 wins in 1940, tying for the second-highest single-season total and underscoring his role as a foundational figure in Japanese professional baseball. Another notable single-season achievement is the most strikeouts, set by Yutaka Enatsu with 401 in 1974 for the Hankyu Braves, surpassing MLB benchmarks like Sandy Koufax's 382 from 1965.
The lowest earned run average (ERA) in a single season is held by Hideo Fujimoto with 0.73 in 1943 for the Tokyo Kyojingun during wartime play, reflecting the era's abbreviated schedules and defensive contexts. Postwar records are higher, with the lowest being 0.98 by Minoru Murayama in 1970 for the Nishitetsu Lions. Modern examples include Cody Ponce's 1.89 in 2024 for the Hokkaido Nippon-Ham Fighters, but wartime figures set the all-time benchmark. No-hitters represent another pinnacle of single-season excellence, with NPB recording 108 total no-hitters through the 2025 season, including one combined effort on April 18, 2025, by Tatsuya Imai and Kaima Taira of the Saitama Seibu Lions against the Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks.
Career pitching records emphasize longevity and consistency, led by Masaichi Kaneda, who amassed 400 wins from 1950 to 1969 primarily with the Kokutetsu Swallows (now the Tokyo Yakult Swallows), the highest total in NPB history and achieved with a 2.34 ERA over 4,705 innings. Kaneda also holds the career strikeouts record with 4,490, leading the league 10 times and demonstrating his strikeout artistry across 19 seasons. Tetsuya Yoneda ranks second in wins with 352 from 1956 to 1977, mostly for the Hankyu Braves, highlighting the durability required in NPB's demanding schedules.
ERA leadership is awarded annually to pitchers meeting qualification thresholds (at least one inning per team game), a tradition dating to 1950 when Nobuo Osima won the Central League title with a 2.03 mark for the Shochiku Robins. Since then, the award has recognized pitchers like Kaneda (three times) and more recent standouts such as Yoshinobu Yamamoto, who captured multiple titles before departing for MLB. In 2025, Hiroto Saiki of the Hanshin Tigers claimed the Central League ERA crown with a 1.55 figure over 157 innings in 24 starts, contributing to the Tigers' pennant-winning campaign. Among active pitchers as of late 2025, Kenta Maeda stands as a notable returnee to NPB after his MLB tenure, holding 81 career NPB wins from 20082015 with the Hiroshima Toyo Carp and poised to add to his ledger in 2026.

Team and league-wide records

Team records in Nippon Professional Baseball highlight dominant performances by individual franchises during the regular season. The Nankai Hawks set the benchmark for most wins in a single season with 99 victories in 1955, a mark that remains unmatched despite the league's expansion to 143 games per team in modern eras. Similarly, the longest winning streak stands at 18 consecutive victories, first achieved by the 1954 Nankai Hawks and later matched by teams including the 2001 Osaka Kintetsu Buffaloes. These streaks underscore the potential for sustained excellence within NPB's grueling schedule, often driven by strong pitching rotations and timely hitting.
League-wide milestones reflect the sport's growing popularity and historical significance. The 2025 regular season established a new attendance record with 27,040,286 total fans across 858 games, averaging 31,515 per contest and surpassing previous highs amid increased fan engagement post-pandemic. Earlier, the 2013 season drew scrutiny for an apparent surge in home runs league-wide, totaling over 1,800 combined in both leagues, fueled by rumors of altered ball specifications that led to official investigations and apologies from NPB leadership. Key historical events include the first perfect game, pitched by Hideo Fujimoto of the Yomiuri Giants on June 28, 1950, against the Nishi Nippon Pirates, marking a rare feat in the league's early years.
The Japan Series, NPB's championship, has showcased competitive balance in recent decades, with 11 different teams claiming titles since 2000including the Yomiuri Giants, Tokyo Yakult Swallows, Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks, Seibu Lions, Chiba Lotte Marines, Chunichi Dragons, Hokkaido Nippon-Ham Fighters, Tohoku Rakuten Golden Eagles, Orix Buffaloes, Hanshin Tigers, and Yokohama DeNA BayStarscompared to fewer repeat winners in prior eras. This diversity, spanning both leagues, highlights evolving strategies and talent distribution, with the 2024 BayStars' upset victory over the Hawks and the 2025 Hawks' triumph over the Tigers exemplifying the postseason's unpredictability. As of 2025, the series has been contested 76 times, accumulating hundreds of games that form the cornerstone of NPB lore.

International and Cultural Impact

Expatriate players in NPB

Expatriate players have played a significant role in Nippon Professional Baseball (NPB) since the league's early days, bringing diverse talents and influencing the game's development. The first foreign player to appear in NPB was Victor Starffin, a Russian-born pitcher who debuted in 1936 with the Nishinomiya Baseball Club (now part of the Hanshin Tigers organization). Starffin achieved remarkable success, amassing 303 wins and 83 shutouts over his career, records that remain unmatched in NPB history, and becoming the first foreign-born inductee into the Japanese Baseball Hall of Fame in 1960. His accomplishments highlighted the potential for international talent to thrive despite initial skepticism toward non-Japanese players.
The integration of expatriates evolved amid concerns over team balance and national identity, leading to quota restrictions. In the 1950s, teams began importing players without limits, but the average per team rose to over five by the early 2000s, prompting regulations; quotas evolved, starting with no limits in the 1950s, capping at 3 in 1952, reducing to 2 from 1966-1980, increasing to 3 in 1981, and reaching 4 on the active roster since 1998 (with limits of no more than 3 pitchers or position players since 2002)a rule unchanged as of 2025. This system aims to preserve opportunities for Japanese players while allowing strategic imports, typically from the Americas, to fill specialized roles like power hitting or relief pitching.
Notable expatriates have left lasting legacies, often succeeding where Major League Baseball (MLB) careers faltered due to NPB's emphasis on contact and discipline. American first baseman Randy Bass, playing for the Hanshin Tigers from 1983 to 1987, won the Central League MVP award in 1985 and the Triple Crown in 1985 with 54 home runs, one shy of Sadaharu Oh's single-season record amid controversy over intentional walks. Venezuelan outfielder Alex Ramirez, active from 2001 to 2013 across multiple teams, became the first foreign-born player to reach 2,000 hits in NPB (finishing with 2,034) and won two Central League MVPs (2008, 2009); he later made history as the first non-Japanese manager, leading the Yokohama DeNA BayStars from 2016 to 2020. In 2025, Dominican infielder Maikel Franco has emerged as a standout for the Tohoku Rakuten Golden Eagles, posting a .237 batting average and 7 home runs in 101 games during the season, exemplifying the ongoing contributions of Latin American imports.
Foreign players have won at least 15 MVP awards across NPB history, underscoring their impact despite the quotas. Approximately 40-50 non-Japanese players remain active on NPB rosters in 2025, spread across the 12 teams, often as key contributors in offense or bullpens.
Culturally, expatriates have faced integration challenges, including language barriers, rigid training regimens differing from MLB's power-focused style, and occasional fan prejudice rooted in post-war nationalism. Early imports like Starffin navigated xenophobia, while modern players adapt to group-oriented team dynamics and smaller ballparks favoring precision over raw power. Successes, such as the Hiroshima Toyo Carp's 1991 Central League championship bolstered by American outfielder Dave Roberts' .301 average and 31 home runs, demonstrate how foreign talent can elevate franchises and foster mutual respect. These contributions have gradually normalized international participation, with some expatriates even transitioning via the posting system to MLB opportunities, though such reverse flows remain rare.

International competitions and national team

Nippon Professional Baseball (NPB) players form the backbone of Japan's national baseball team, known as Samurai Japan, which has achieved remarkable success in international competitions since the team's formal establishment in 2006. The roster predominantly features active NPB athletes, supplemented by select Major League Baseball (MLB) players of Japanese origin, enabling a seamless integration of domestic talent with global experience. This structure has allowed Samurai Japan to dominate major tournaments organized by the World Baseball Softball Confederation (WBSC), showcasing the depth and skill cultivated within NPB leagues.
Japan's triumphs in the World Baseball Classic (WBC), a premier international event held every four years since 2006, highlight NPB's global influence. Samurai Japan secured its first WBC title in 2006 by defeating Cuba 10-6 in the final, followed by a repeat victory in 2009 with a 5-3 win over South Korea. The team's third championship came in 2023, capping an undefeated run with a dramatic 3-2 victory over the United States in the final at LoanDepot Park in Miami, where NPB stars like Yoshinobu Yamamoto and Munetaka Murakami played pivotal roles alongside MLB talents such as Shohei Ohtani. These wins underscore Japan's status as the most successful WBC nation, with three titles out of five tournaments.
In Olympic baseball, Samurai Japan has earned four medals across its participations from 1992 to 2020, reflecting the sport's intermittent inclusion in the Games. The team claimed bronze medals in 1992 at Barcelona and 2004 at Athens, a silver in 1996 at Atlanta, and its first gold in 2020 at Tokyo (held in 2021), defeating the United States 2-0 in the final at Yokohama Baseball Stadium with standout pitching from NPB's Masato Morishita and a home run by Murakami. Although baseball returned as a demonstration sport in earlier Olympics like 1984 and 1988, no medals were awarded then, and the sport was absent from the 2024 Paris Games. These achievements, driven largely by NPB pitchers and hitters, affirm Japan's prowess on the Olympic stage.
The WBSC Premier12, introduced in 2015 as a top-tier tournament for the world's leading baseball nations, has further elevated Samurai Japan's profile. Japan won the inaugural 2015 edition, defeating the Dominican Republic 8-3 in the final, and defended the title in 2019 with a 9-0 shutout of South Korea, led by NPB manager Hirokazu Ibata. In the 2024 Premier12, hosted partly in Japan, the team advanced undefeated through the group and super rounds but fell 4-0 to Chinese Taipei in the Tokyo Dome final, ending a 27-game international winning streak and settling for silver. This result, while a setback, reinforced Japan's consistent contention for global supremacy.
Samurai Japan's success is epitomized by figures like Shohei Ohtani, who before his full-time MLB career with the Los Angeles Angels, honed his two-way skills in NPB with the Hokkaido Nippon-Ham Fighters and represented Japan in international play, including the 2017 WBC. Post-MLB transition, Ohtani's participation in the 2023 WBC final at-batstriking out Mike Troutsymbolized the bridge between NPB foundations and international stardom. As of 2025, Samurai Japan holds a total of 12 major international titles across WBC, Premier12, Olympics, and other WBSC events like the World Baseball Cup, solidifying NPB's role in elevating Japan to the world's top-ranked baseball nation.

Economic aspects and popularity

Nippon Professional Baseball (NPB) generates revenue primarily through ticket sales, broadcasting rights, and merchandise, with the league's total revenue estimated at approximately $1.3 billion in recent years. Ticket sales contribute significantly, projected at around US$265.88 million in 2025, reflecting strong fan engagement despite economic fluctuations. Broadcasting deals, including local and digital streaming platforms like Pacific League TV and Rakuten TV, add over 20 billion yen annually, bolstering financial stability. Merchandise sales, including jerseys and team gear, further support income streams, though exact figures vary by team popularity.
The league faced severe financial challenges following Japan's asset price bubble burst in the early 1990s, which triggered a prolonged recession affecting corporate owners of NPB teams. This economic downturn exacerbated operational costs, leading to mergers in the 2000s, such as the 2004 combination of the Orix BlueWave and Osaka Kintetsu Buffaloes due to mounting debts. A brief players' strike in September 2004, lasting two days and canceling 12 games, highlighted these tensions over league contraction. By 2025, NPB has shown recovery, driven by record attendance and expanded media rights, though precise total revenue figures for the year remain around 180-200 billion amid ongoing post-pandemic adjustments.
NPB enjoys immense popularity, with total annual attendance reaching a record high in 2025, exceeding 25 million fans across the season and averaging over 31,000 per game. Regional fanbases underscore this appeal: the Yomiuri Giants draw national support through their Tokyo base and media presence, while the Hanshin Tigers command fierce loyalty in Osaka, often filling Koshien Stadium to capacity with averages above 41,000. This grassroots enthusiasm sustains the league's cultural footprint.
To protect players, staff, managers, coaches, umpires, and their families from defamation, threats, and insults on social media, NPB, the 12 clubs, and the Japan Professional Baseball Players Association (JPBPA) have adopted strict countermeasures. These include legal actions such as requesting sender information disclosure from SNS providers and cooperating with law enforcement, while urging fans to avoid spreading defamatory content and to maintain proper etiquette in online posts that provide positive support. The JPBPA operates a countermeasures team supported by advisory lawyers to assist players, including through information disclosure procedures, settlements, and filing complaints where appropriate. In 2025, the JPBPA introduced an AI-based system to automatically detect slanderous posts targeting players during the Climax Series and Japan Series.
Media integrations enhance NPB's reach, notably through anime like the long-running series Major, which chronicles a young pitcher's journey inspired by professional baseball aspirations and aired from 2004 to 2010. In 2025, digital growth has accelerated via official apps for live stats and streaming, alongside esports tie-ins such as the Professional Baseball Spirits video game series, fostering younger audiences. SoftBank Group, owner of the Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks, provides substantial sponsorship, having invested billions since acquiring the team in 2004 for 20 billion. Post-2020, NPB has advanced gender diversity through initiatives like the Baseball Federation of Japan's equality programs, promoting women in coaching and front-office roles to broaden inclusivity.
