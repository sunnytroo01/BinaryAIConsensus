 Arsenic is a chemical element with the symbol As and atomic number 33, classified as a metalloid that appears as a steel-gray, brittle, crystalline semimetal in its most stable allotrope, gray arsenic, which tarnishes in air and oxidizes rapidly when heated to form arsenous oxide. It occurs naturally in the Earth's crust at an average concentration of about 1.5 to 2 parts per million, primarily in sulfide minerals and as a trace component in rocks, sediments, and groundwater, often mobilized into the environment through geological processes and human activities like mining.
Inorganic arsenic compounds are highly toxic, with acute exposure causing gastrointestinal distress, hemorrhage, and neurological effects, while chronic exposure via drinking water or food is linked to skin lesions, cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and cancers of the skin, lung, bladder, and liver. Despite its hazards, arsenic has industrial applications, including as gallium arsenide in semiconductors, in lead-acid battery alloys, and historically in pesticides, wood preservatives, and medicines like arsenic trioxide for treating acute promyelocytic leukemia, though many uses have been curtailed due to health risks.
Global production of arsenic, mainly as a byproduct of nonferrous metal smelting rather than primary mining, has declined with regulatory restrictions, but it remains essential in specialized high-tech sectors while posing ongoing environmental and public health challenges in regions with naturally elevated groundwater levels, such as parts of South Asia and the Americas.

Physical and Chemical Properties

Physical characteristics

Arsenic exists primarily as a solid metalloid at standard temperature and pressure, with the thermodynamically stable allotrope being gray arsenic, a steel-gray, brittle, crystalline material that tarnishes slowly in moist air. This form exhibits semimetallic properties, including poor electrical conductivity (resistivity of approximately 333 nm at 20C) and low thermal conductivity, distinguishing it from true metals. Gray arsenic has a density of 5.73 g/cm and adopts a rhombohedral crystal structure, where each arsenic atom is bonded to three others in a puckered, layer-like arrangement similar to that in black phosphorus.
Under ambient pressure, gray arsenic sublimes at 614C rather than melting, though it melts at 817C when subjected to elevated pressure (around 28 atm). Arsenic also manifests in less stable allotropes: yellow arsenic, a waxy, molecular solid composed of As tetrahedra with a density of 1.97 g/cm and melting point near -13C, which is highly reactive and light-sensitive; and black arsenic, a metastable orthorhombic form with layered structure analogous to black phosphorus, exhibiting anisotropic properties. These allotropes can be interconverted under specific conditions, such as heating or pressure, but gray arsenic predominates in natural and commercial samples due to its lower energy state. Native arsenic deposits often appear as gray nodules or masses with a metallic luster when freshly exposed.

Isotopic composition

Arsenic consists of a single stable isotope, ^{75}As, which accounts for 100% of its natural abundance on Earth and has an atomic mass of 74.921595(6) u. This monoisotopic composition results in a standard atomic weight of 74.921595(6) for the element.
In total, 32 radioactive isotopes of arsenic have been synthesized and characterized, spanning mass numbers from 60As to 92As. These isotopes primarily decay via beta minus (), electron capture (EC), or beta plus () emission, with shorter-lived ones exhibiting neutron emission in some cases. The longest-lived among them are ^{73}As (half-life 80.30(6) days, decaying by EC to ^{73}Ge) and ^{74}As (half-life 17.77(2) days, with 66% EC/ to ^{74}Ge and 34% to ^{74}Se). Other notable isotopes include ^{71}As (half-life 65.30(7) hours, EC/ to ^{71}Ge) and ^{77}As (half-life 38.79(5) hours, to ^{77}Se).

Chemical reactivity and bonding

Arsenic, as a group 15 metalloid, predominantly forms covalent bonds, leveraging its five valence electrons to achieve octet configurations through sharing rather than ionic transfer, though some polar character arises from its electronegativity of 2.18 on the Pauling scale. In compounds, it commonly exhibits oxidation states of -3, +3, and +5, with +3 being stable due to the inert pair effect stabilizing the 4s electrons, while +5 involves promotion to utilize all valence electrons. Bonding often involves sp hybridization, enabling tetrahedral geometries in species like AsH (arsine) or trigonal pyramidal structures with a lone pair, analogous to phosphine but with longer, weaker bonds owing to arsenic's larger atomic radius.
In its elemental gray allotrope, the most stable form under standard conditions, arsenic adopts a rhombohedral crystal structure (space group R3m) where each atom is covalently bonded to three neighbors via As-As single bonds of approximately 2.51 length, forming distorted puckered layers resembling those in black phosphorus, with interlayer van der Waals interactions accounting for its anisotropy and semimetallic conductivity. This catenation propensity allows extended As-As chains or rings in some compounds, though less extensive than in phosphorus due to weaker bonding energies around 180-200 kJ/mol per bond.
Elemental arsenic displays low reactivity at ambient temperatures, remaining unaffected by water, dilute acids, or alkalis, but it oxidizes slowly in moist air to arsenic trioxide (AsO) and ignites in oxygen at elevated temperatures to yield AsO and, under forcing conditions, AsO. With halogens, it reacts vigorouslyfluorine forms both AsF and AsF, while chlorine, bromine, and iodine yield trihalides AsX (X = Cl, Br, I) under controlled heating, with the trihalides featuring pyramidal molecular structures due to the lone pair. Strong oxidizing acids like concentrated nitric acid dissolve it to produce arsenic acid (HAsO) in the +5 state, reflecting its amphoteric tendencies in oxides and ability to act as a reductant. Arsenic also forms intermetallic arsenides with active metals, such as NaAs, where it accepts electrons to achieve the -3 state, underscoring its versatility in redox chemistry.

Chemical Compounds

Inorganic arsenic compounds

Inorganic arsenic compounds consist of arsenic bonded directly to elements other than carbon, such as oxygen, hydrogen, halogens, or sulfur, and are classified primarily by oxidation states of +3 (trivalent, arsenites) and +5 (pentavalent, arsenates). Trivalent forms demonstrate greater acute toxicity than pentavalent ones due to enhanced bioavailability and interference with cellular enzymes, while both contribute to carcinogenic risks through mechanisms like DNA damage and oxidative stress.
Arsenic trioxide (AsO), a key trivalent oxide, manifests as white or transparent glassy amorphous lumps or crystalline powder with limited water solubility (approximately 1.2 g/100 mL at 20C). It sublimes at 193C and boils at 465C, historically serving as a pigment, preservative, and poison, with modern application in targeted therapy for acute promyelocytic leukemia via induction of apoptosis in cancer cells.
Arsenic acid (HAsO), the pentavalent oxyacid, forms a clear, colorless aqueous solution that is noncombustible yet corrosive to metals and acutely toxic by ingestion, with a median lethal dose in rodents around 2050 mg/kg. Its salts, including sodium arsenate, were used in herbicides and insecticides until regulatory restrictions in the late 20th century due to environmental persistence and bioaccumulation.
Arsine (AsH), a hydride compound, is a colorless, flammable gas with a garlic-like odor detectable above 0.5 ppm, produced unintentionally in metalworking or semiconductor manufacturing through reduction of arsenic impurities. It inhibits cellular respiration by binding hemoglobin, leading to rapid hemolysis and potential fatality at concentrations exceeding 50 ppm for 30 minutes.
Halides such as arsenic trichloride (AsCl) appear as colorless to yellow oily liquids that fume in air, hydrolyze to arsenious acid, and were employed in organic synthesis and glass production, though their volatility and corrosivity limit contemporary use. Most inorganic arsenic compounds present as white, odorless solids with densities from 1.9 to over 5 g/cm, underscoring their prevalence in mining byproducts and industrial effluents.
Exposure to inorganic arsenic, primarily via contaminated water or occupational inhalation, induces acute symptoms including nausea, diarrhea, and hypotension, with chronic low-level intake (e.g., >0.01 mg/kg/day) linked to hyperpigmentation, keratosis, neuropathy, and elevated risks of lung, bladder, and skin cancers, as evidenced by epidemiological studies in regions with geogenic contamination. Regulatory bodies classify these compounds as Group 1 carcinogens, with permissible exposure limits set at 10 g/m for air and 10 ppb for drinking water.

Organoarsenic compounds

Organoarsenic compounds are organic substances containing at least one direct arsenic-carbon bond, distinguishing them from inorganic arsenic species. The arsenic atom's ability to form bonds with one to five organic substituents allows for diverse molecular architectures, including trivalent and pentavalent forms.
Simple alkyl derivatives, such as trimethylarsine ((CH)As), exhibit volatility and pyramidal geometry similar to phosphines, with arsenic adopting a lone pair in the trivalent state. More complex examples include arsenobetaine (CH)AsCHCOO, a zwitterionic compound prevalent in marine organisms like fish and shellfish, where it constitutes the majority of arsenic content and displays low mammalian toxicity due to rapid excretion. 
Historically, organoarsenic compounds found medical applications, notably arsphenamine (Salvarsan), developed by Paul Ehrlich in 1909 for treating syphilis through targeted action on spirochetes, marking an early chemotherapeutic success despite eventual replacement by penicillin. In agriculture, compounds like roxarsone and arsanilic acid served as feed additives for poultry and swine to enhance growth, control dysentery, and prevent coccidiosis from the mid-20th century until regulatory phase-outs in the 2010s over residue and cancer risk concerns. 
Toxicity varies by structure; pentavalent organoarsenicals like arsenobetaine are generally less acutely toxic than trivalent or inorganic forms, though chronic exposure to certain species, such as methylated arsenicals in food, prompts ongoing risk assessments for bioaccumulation and genotoxicity. Industrially, some organoarsenicals functioned as herbicides, insecticides, and fungicides, leveraging arsenic's disruption of microbial metabolism, but usage declined with safer alternatives. During World War I, vesicant agents like lewisite (dichloro(2-chlorovinyl)arsine) exemplified weaponized organoarsenic compounds, causing severe blistering via alkylation of biological thiols.

Natural Occurrence and Commercial Production

Geological distribution

Arsenic is present in the Earth's crust at an average concentration of 1.5 to 2 parts per million (ppm), making it a relatively scarce element compared to more abundant crustal components like silicon or aluminum. This distribution reflects its chalcophile affinity, leading to concentration in sulfide-rich environments rather than widespread dispersion. Geological processes such as magmatism, hydrothermal circulation, and sedimentation control its primary modes of occurrence, with higher abundances linked to tectonic activity at plate margins.
The element primarily exists in mineral forms rather than native state, with arsenopyrite (FeAsS), realgar (AsS), and orpiment (AsS) being the most prevalent arsenic sulfides. These minerals form mainly in hydrothermal and magmatic ore deposits, including epithermal veins and porphyry systems associated with gold, copper, and base metals. Native arsenic, occurring as a brittle, steel-gray elemental allotrope, is uncommon and restricted to specific hydrothermal veins, often intergrown with silver-cobalt-nickel arsenides in mesothermal or epithermal settings. Arsenic also substitutes into other sulfides like pyrite and sphalerite, enhancing local concentrations in volcanogenic massive sulfide deposits.
In sedimentary contexts, arsenic accumulates in fine-grained rocks such as shales, clays, and phosphate deposits, as well as iron and manganese oxides, through weathering and diagenetic processes in reducing environments. Concentrations can reach tens to hundreds of ppm in these lithologies, particularly in Cenozoic basins where groundwater interaction mobilizes arsenic from eroded sulfides. Metamorphic rocks host arsenic via alteration of primary sulfides, with arsenopyrite persisting under greenschist to amphibolite facies conditions. Igneous rocks, including pegmatites, contain minor arsenic in arsenate minerals or as trace impurities. Overall, arsenic's geological distribution correlates with sulfide mineralization and oxidative weathering cycles, resulting in patchy, elevated levels in orogenic belts and sedimentary basins rather than uniform crustal homogeneity.

Extraction and refining processes

Arsenic is commercially produced almost exclusively as a by-product of smelting ores containing other nonferrous metals, such as copper, lead, cobalt, gold, and zinc, rather than from dedicated arsenic mining operations. During the roasting or smelting of arsenical sulfide ores like arsenopyrite (FeAsS), the arsenic content oxidizes to arsenic trioxide (As2O3), which volatilizes due to its low boiling point of approximately 465 C and is captured as fine dust in flue gases, baghouses, or electrostatic precipitators downstream from the furnaces. This recovery process typically yields crude As2O3 containing impurities such as silica, sulfur, and trace metals from the host ores.
Refining of arsenic trioxide begins with purification of the crude product through sublimation in specialized furnaces, where the As2O3 is heated to vaporize and then condensed, exploiting its volatility to separate it from non-volatile contaminants; this may be repeated for higher purity grades exceeding 99%. For elemental arsenic metal, purified As2O3 undergoes carbothermic reduction in reverberatory or electric furnaces at temperatures around 600800 C, following the reaction 2 As2O3 + 3 C 4 As + 3 CO2, with the arsenic vapor subsequently condensed and cast into ingots. This reduction step produces arsenic metal with purity levels suitable for applications like lead-acid battery alloys and semiconductors.
Global production of arsenic trioxide reached an estimated 46,000 metric tons in 2023, predominantly from China (38,000 tons) and Morocco (5,000 tons), with smaller outputs from Russia, Belgium, and Bolivia; arsenic metal output was approximately 1,000 tons, almost entirely from China. No primary arsenic production has occurred in the United States since 1985, with imports supplying demand primarily for legacy uses before regulatory restrictions on chromated copper arsenate (CCA) wood preservatives reduced volumes. Emerging hydrometallurgical methods, such as acid leaching of flue dusts followed by precipitation or scorodite formation for stabilization, are under development to handle high-arsenic residues but remain secondary to pyrometallurgical recovery in commercial practice.

Historical Context

Pre-modern discovery and uses

Arsenic compounds, such as orpiment (AsS) and realgar (AsS), were recognized and employed in ancient civilizations including Mesopotamia, Egypt, China, and Greece from at least 3000 BCE. These yellow and red minerals served primarily as pigments for paints, dyes, and illuminated manuscripts due to their vivid colors, with orpiment dubbed "gold paint" in antiquity. Realgar found use in Roman painting as sandarach and in Chinese traditions as "masculine yellow."
In early metallurgy, arsenic was intentionally alloyed with copper to create arsenical bronze, a harder material than pure copper, with evidence of deliberate production emerging around 3500 BCE in regions like the Near East and spreading to Egypt by the Middle Kingdom (circa 2000 BCE). This alloy improved tool and weapon durability before the dominance of tin bronze, though its toxic fumes posed health risks to smiths.
Medicinally, arsenic sulfides were applied externally for skin conditions, snakebites, and as antiparasitics; Hippocrates prescribed realgar and orpiment for ulcers and abscesses around 400 BCE, while Dioscorides documented their use in the 1st century CE. In ancient China, arsenic minerals appeared in texts like the Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing (circa 200 BCE) for treating carbuncles and parasites, with roots tracing to earlier practices. Arsenic trioxide, derived from copper smelting, functioned both as a therapeutic agent and poison as early as 2000 BCE.
Elemental arsenic was first isolated in 1250 CE by Albertus Magnus, a German alchemist, through heating orpiment with soap, yielding the pure metalloid and enabling further experimentation. In medieval Europe, arsenic gained notoriety as a discreet poisonodorless and tasteless in compounds like arsenious oxideearning nicknames like "inheritance powder" for its role in assassinations, including reputed uses by figures such as Nero. Despite its toxicity, small doses were explored as stimulants in Renaissance medicine, foreshadowing later pharmaceutical applications.

Industrial era advancements

During the 19th century, arsenic production expanded dramatically as a byproduct of smelting non-ferrous metals such as copper, lead, gold, and nickel, with arsenic trioxide recovered from flue dusts, speiss, and sludges generated in roasting processes. Refining advancements included sublimation techniques to volatilize and purify arsenic trioxide, enabling commercial-scale output that met surging industrial demands; by the mid-1800s, enormous quantities were produced in Europe, particularly in mining regions like Cornwall, England, where arsenic residues from tin and copper extraction were processed into marketable forms.
Key innovations in arsenic applications drove further production growth, including the widespread adoption of copper arsenite pigments like Scheele's green, discovered in 1775 but industrialized in the 19th century for textiles, wallpapers, and paints due to its vivid color stability. In 1867, Paris green (copper acetoarsenite) was developed as an effective insecticide, applied extensively against pests such as the Colorado potato beetle, marking a pivotal advancement in agricultural pest control and increasing arsenic's role in supporting industrial-era farming productivity.
Arsenic trioxide also advanced glass manufacturing by acting as a decolorizing agent to neutralize iron impurities, producing clearer optical and container glass on a large scale from the early 1800s onward. Additionally, elemental arsenic was refined via distillation for use in alloys and semiconductors precursors, though initial industrial focus remained on compounds; these developments positioned arsenic as a critical material linking mining outputs to diverse manufacturing sectors between 1870 and 1914. Despite toxicity awareness emerging by the late 19th century, unregulated production and use persisted, reflecting the era's prioritization of industrial utility over health risks.

Practical Applications

Materials science and alloys

Arsenic has been employed in metallurgy since the prehistoric era, notably in arsenical bronze, an early copper-arsenic alloy that enhanced copper's hardness, elasticity, and castability compared to pure copper. This alloy emerged around 5000 BC on the Iranian plateau and spread widely in the Near East and Europe by the late 4th millennium BC, often containing 1-6% arsenic by deliberate addition or from arsenic-rich ores. Arsenical bronze facilitated the production of stronger tools and weapons, though its use declined after circa 2000 BC in favor of tin bronze, potentially due to arsenic's toxicity during smelting and handling, which caused chronic poisoning among artisans.
In modern materials science, elemental arsenic serves primarily as an alloying agent in lead-based systems, particularly for lead-acid batteries, where trace additions (0.05-0.25%) to lead-antimony grids improve mechanical strength, refine grain structure, and suppress corrosion by forming protective oxide layers. This role persists in automotive and industrial batteries, including those for electric vehicles, despite shifts toward antimony-free alloys due to environmental regulations. Arsenic also alloys with copper to produce deoxidized arsenical copper (e.g., alloy UNS C14200 with ~0.3% arsenic), which resists hydrogen embrittlement and is used in plumbing and electrical components for its improved fluidity and strength. 
Additional applications include hardening lead alloys for ammunition, where arsenic enhances tensile strength and reduces creep; solders for electronics and plumbing, improving wetting and joint integrity; and bearings, acting as an anti-friction additive in lead or bronze matrices to minimize wear under high loads. These uses leverage arsenic's metalloid properties to refine microstructure and elevate performance metrics like hardness (e.g., increasing Brinell hardness in lead alloys by 20-30% at low concentrations), though toxicity concerns have prompted ongoing substitution efforts with safer alternatives like calcium or silver in battery grids.

Agricultural and pest control

Arsenic compounds, particularly inorganic forms like lead arsenate and copper acetoarsenite (Paris Green), were historically prominent in agricultural pest control as insecticides starting in the late 19th century. Lead arsenate emerged around 1892 as a stable spray for fruit orchards, targeting pests such as the codling moth in apples and cherries, with the U.S. Department of Agriculture endorsing its use across millions of acres by the early 20th century. Paris Green, introduced in the 1860s, effectively controlled Colorado potato beetles but proved phytotoxic and environmentally persistent. From 1920 to 1980, the U.S. agricultural sector consumed the majority of domestic arsenic production for formulating such insecticides, herbicides, and cotton desiccants.
Herbicidal applications included sodium arsenite for non-selective weed eradication in crops like vineyards and fallow fields, often applied to dormant vegetation to minimize direct crop damage, though restrictions limited its use on actively growing plants. Calcium arsenite and similar As(III) compounds served as defoliants and desiccants in cotton production. Orgoarsenic compounds, such as roxarsone (4-hydroxy-3-nitrophenylarsonic acid), were incorporated into poultry and swine feeds from the 1940s as growth promoters, coccidiostats, and pigmentation enhancers, with U.S. usage peaking before voluntary manufacturer withdrawal in June 2011 due to evidence of conversion to carcinogenic inorganic arsenic in animal tissues.
Regulatory actions progressively curtailed these practices amid documented health risks, including acute poisoning from spray drift and chronic exposure via residues. Lead arsenate applications declined after the 1940s with the advent of DDT and organophosphates, leading to its full U.S. ban for insecticidal purposes on August 1, 1988, due to bioaccumulation and lack of viable mitigation. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration rescinded approvals for roxarsone and two other arsenicals (nitarsone and arsanilic acid) in October 2013, effectively prohibiting 97% of arsenic-based animal feed additives following studies linking them to elevated inorganic arsenic in meat and environmental discharge. Cacodylic acid, an arsenical herbicide used on cotton and turf until the 1980s, faced cancellation for most non-agricultural sites by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency due to groundwater contamination risks.
Today, inorganic arsenic pesticides are prohibited in U.S. agriculture, with global trends mirroring this shift toward synthetic alternatives deemed less persistent and toxic. Legacy contamination persists in former orchard soils, where lead arsenate residues exceed safe thresholds decades post-ban, necessitating remediation for residential conversion. Limited organoarsenic uses, such as in some veterinary contexts outside major markets, continue under scrutiny, but agricultural reliance has virtually ceased due to superior efficacy and safety profiles of modern pesticides.

Pharmaceutical and therapeutic uses

Arsenic compounds have been employed therapeutically for millennia, initially in traditional Chinese medicine for treating conditions such as syphilis, psoriasis, and leukemia, with arsenic trioxide documented as a remedy for acute promyelocytic leukemia (APL) as early as the 1970s in cohort studies showing response rates up to 73% in chronic myeloid leukemia patients. In the 19th and early 20th centuries, arsenicals like Fowler's solution (potassium arsenite) were used for syphilis, rheumatism, and skin disorders, while arsphenamine (Salvarsan), the first modern chemotherapeutic agent, revolutionized syphilis treatment after Paul Ehrlich's development in 1909, achieving cure rates exceeding 90% in controlled trials before penicillin supplanted it. These applications stemmed from arsenic's antimicrobial and antiproliferative effects, though high toxicity limited widespread adoption and led to its decline with safer alternatives.
The resurgence of arsenic in modern oncology centers on arsenic trioxide (ATO), an inorganic arsenical approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in 2000 for induction of remission and consolidation in adults with APL refractory to or relapsed after all-trans retinoic acid (ATRA) and anthracycline chemotherapy, based on phase II trials demonstrating complete remission in 70% of such patients with median durations exceeding 15 months. In 2018, the FDA expanded approval to include first-line treatment of newly diagnosed low-risk APL (white blood cell count <10 10^9/L) in combination with ATRA, supported by randomized trials showing 97% event-free survival at 2 years versus 83-93% with ATRA plus chemotherapy, with reduced hematologic toxicity and no chemotherapy requirement. ATO's mechanism involves targeted degradation of the PML-RAR fusion oncoprotein in APL cells, inducing differentiation, autophagy, and apoptosis at micromolar concentrations, as elucidated in preclinical models and confirmed in clinical pharmacodynamic studies measuring PML nuclear bodies. This regimen achieves molecular remission in over 95% of low-risk cases, with overall survival rates approaching 99% at 2 years in international consortia data.
Beyond APL, investigational uses of ATO include combination therapies for other leukemias and solid tumors, such as hepatocellular carcinoma and lung cancer, where it inhibits tumor growth via reactive oxygen species generation and phase separation disruption in membraneless organelles, though phase III evidence remains limited and toxicity profiles including QT prolongation, differentiation syndrome, and hepatotoxicitynecessitate cardiac monitoring and dose adjustments in 20-30% of patients. Veterinary applications, such as roxarsone for poultry growth promotion, were phased out by the FDA in 2015 due to inorganic arsenic residues, underscoring regulatory scrutiny over non-essential uses. Homeopathic arsenic preparations lack robust clinical evidence for conditions like poisoning or seizures, with randomized trials showing no superiority over placebo. Overall, ATO's narrow therapeutic window exemplifies arsenic's dual-edged role, confined primarily to APL due to superior efficacy against this subtype's genetic aberration.

Semiconductors and electronics

Arsenic serves as a key n-type dopant in silicon semiconductors, substituting for silicon atoms in the lattice to provide donor electrons that enhance electrical conductivity. Typical doping concentrations range from 10^{15} to 10^{20} atoms per cubic centimeter, enabling the fabrication of transistors and integrated circuits with controlled resistivity. This application leverages arsenic's group V valence electrons, which release one electron per atom into the conduction band upon ionization.
High-purity arsenic (99.9999% or greater) is primarily consumed in the production of III-V compound semiconductors, notably gallium arsenide (GaAs), where it forms the pnictide component in a zincblende crystal structure. GaAs possesses a direct bandgap of 1.42 eV at room temperature, facilitating efficient radiative recombination for optoelectronic devices, and an electron mobility of approximately 8500 cm/Vsover five times that of siliconallowing operation at frequencies exceeding 100 GHz. These properties stem from the covalent bonding between gallium and arsenic, yielding lower effective masses for charge carriers compared to group IV materials.
GaAs-based devices dominate high-frequency electronics, including monolithic microwave integrated circuits (MMICs) for radar and satellite communications, where power amplifiers achieve efficiencies above 50% at millimeter-wave bands. In optoelectronics, GaAs enables infrared light-emitting diodes (LEDs) emitting at 870 nm and laser diodes for fiber-optic telecommunications, with historical development tracing to single-crystal growth in 1955 for early photocells and commercial ICs by the 1980s. Photovoltaic applications utilize GaAs multi-junction solar cells, attaining efficiencies over 30% under concentrated sunlight due to superior radiation resistance and absorption in the 300900 nm spectrum. Other arsenide compounds, such as indium arsenide (InAs), support even higher mobilities (up to 40,000 cm/Vs) for low-noise amplifiers and quantum devices.
Arsenic's volatility during epitaxial growth processes like metal-organic chemical vapor deposition (MOCVD) necessitates precursors such as arsine (AsH) or trimethylarsine, which decompose to supply arsenic atoms while minimizing defects like antisite disorders that degrade performance. Despite advantages, GaAs fabrication involves handling toxic arsenic species, prompting engineering controls to limit worker exposure below 5 g/m. Ongoing research explores arsenide alloys for 5G/6G infrastructure and terahertz detectors, sustaining demand for refined arsenic feedstocks.

Other industrial applications

Arsenic trioxide serves as a fining agent and decolorizer in glass manufacturing, where it oxidizes iron impurities that otherwise impart a green tint, enabling production of clearer, colorless glass for optical and specialty applications. This use, established in the 19th century, persists in limited quantities for high-purity glass, with global consumption estimated at under 1% of total arsenic production as of 2020, primarily due to substitution with alternatives like antimony amid environmental regulations.
In ceramics, arsenic compounds such as arsenic trisulfide function as opacifiers, creating opaque white glazes by scattering light through fine particle dispersion during firing. Historical applications date to ancient glazing techniques, but modern usage is curtailed by toxicity, with arsenic content in consumer ceramics now regulated below 100 ppm in the European Union since 2013 to minimize leaching risks.
Arsenic sulfides, including realgar (AsS) and orpiment (AsS), have been utilized as pigments in industrial paints and enamels for their vibrant red and yellow hues, respectively, owing to their high refractive indices and stability in certain matrices. These applications peaked in the early 20th century for non-consumer products like marine coatings but have largely been replaced by synthetic azo pigments since the 1970s due to arsenic's carcinogenic properties, with residual use confined to restoration of historical artifacts.
In pyrotechnics, compounds like copper acetoarsenite (Paris green) produce intense blue colors through arsenic's volatile emission during combustion, a technique documented in fireworks formulations from the 19th century. Current industrial production is restricted; U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission regulations since 2006 prohibit arsenic in consumer fireworks, limiting it to professional displays where emissions are controlled, with arsenic content typically below 2% by mass fraction.

Biological and Biochemical Roles

Microbial interactions

Microorganisms mediate key transformations in the arsenic biogeochemical cycle, including oxidation of arsenite (As(III)) to arsenate (As(V)), reduction of arsenate to arsenite, and methylation to organoarsenic compounds, thereby regulating arsenic mobility and speciation in soils, sediments, and aquifers. These processes occur via specific enzymes and genes, such as arsenite oxidase (Aio) for oxidation and arsenate reductase (ArsC) or respiratory reductase (Arr) for reduction, enabling microbes to adapt to arsenic-rich environments or utilize it in respiration. In anoxic conditions, dissimilatory reduction predominates, mobilizing arsenic from sediments into groundwater, as observed in arsenic-contaminated aquifers of the Bengal Delta.
Arsenite-oxidizing bacteria convert the more toxic and mobile As(III) to less bioavailable As(V), facilitating its precipitation with iron oxides and aiding natural attenuation in contaminated sites. Notable species include Alcaligenes faecalis, Herminiimonas arsenicoxydans, and Thiomonas arsenitoxydans, which employ the aioBA gene cluster encoding the Aio enzyme for chemolithoautotrophic growth on arsenite. In arsenic-rich deltaic aquifers, dominant genera such as Acidovorax, Hydrogenophaga, Albidiferax, Bosea, and Polymorphum (primarily Proteobacteria) drive this oxidation, linking it to iron cycling and organic matter degradation under nutrient-limited, anoxic conditions.
Arsenate-reducing bacteria transform As(V) to As(III) either for detoxification via cytoplasmic reduction and efflux or as an electron acceptor in anaerobic respiration, often exacerbating arsenic release in reduced environments like rice paddies or mine tailings. Examples include Shewanella sp. ANA-3 and Sulfurospirillum barnesii using the arrAB genes for respiratory reduction, and sulfate-reducing bacteria consortia employing arsC for resistance-linked reduction. This process, documented since early studies on dissimilatory arsenate respiration, increases arsenic solubility and bioavailability in groundwater systems.
Microbial methylation sequentially adds methyl groups to As(III), producing monomethylarsonate (MMA), dimethylarsinate (DMA), and volatile trimethylarsine (TMA), which can escape soils into the atmosphere, thereby diminishing local arsenic burdens. The arsM gene encodes arsenite S-adenosylmethionine methyltransferase (ArsM), active in species like Rhodopseudomonas palustris and Paenibacillus strains, with fungi such as Humicola sp. also contributing in aerobic soils. In rice paddy ecosystems, this volatilization balances with demethylation, influencing arsenic uptake by crops, though anaerobic methylators remain less characterized. Overall, these interactions underscore microbes' dual role in arsenic detoxification and mobilization, critical for bioremediation strategies in contaminated environments.

Metabolism in higher organisms

In mammals, including humans, inorganic arsenic absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract or other routes undergoes biotransformation primarily in the liver, involving sequential reduction and oxidative methylation to produce less bioaccumulative metabolites. Inorganic arsenate (AsV) is reduced to arsenite (AsIII) via enzymes such as arsenate reductase, often utilizing glutathione (GSH) as a cofactor; AsIII is then methylated by arsenic (+3 oxidation state) methyltransferase (AS3MT), which transfers methyl groups from S-adenosylmethionine (SAM), yielding monomethylarsonous acid (MMAIII), subsequently oxidized to monomethylarsonic acid (MMAV), and further methylated to dimethylarsinous acid (DMAIII) and dimethylarsinic acid (DMAV). This pathway facilitates urinary excretion, with DMAV comprising up to 70-80% of urinary arsenic in humans following acute exposure, enabling clearance of over 90% of the absorbed dose within 48-72 hours.
The process exhibits a duality: pentavalent methylated species like MMAV and DMAV are generally less toxic and more water-soluble, promoting renal elimination and reducing tissue retention, whereas trivalent intermediates (MMAIII and DMAIII)generated during reduction steps involving GSH S-transferase omega-1 (GSTO1)possess high thiol reactivity, enabling binding to vicinal cysteines in proteins and enzymes, which disrupts cellular functions and amplifies oxidative stress via reactive oxygen species production. Historically viewed as detoxifying, methylation is now recognized as potentially activating arsenic toxicity, particularly in chronic low-dose scenarios where trivalent metabolites correlate with genotoxicity and carcinogenesis in tissues like the bladder.
Interspecies and intraspecies variations influence efficiency: humans and rodents (e.g., rats, mice) typically achieve high dimethylation (DMA excretion >60% of dose), but non-human primates like marmosets and chimpanzees exhibit negligible methylation capacity, resulting in inorganic arsenic accumulation in liver and slower clearance. In humans, genetic polymorphisms in AS3MT affect methylation ratios, with efficient methylators (e.g., certain Asian populations) showing lower inorganic arsenic retention but potentially higher exposure to toxic trivalent forms; nutritional factors like folate and selenium modulate SAM availability and GSH levels, while age-related differencessuch as higher DMA percentages in childrenalter susceptibility. Organic arsenicals, such as arsenobetaine from marine sources, undergo minimal metabolism in mammals and are excreted unchanged, contrasting with inorganic forms.

Potential essentiality debates

Studies in various animal species have provided evidence suggesting arsenic may function as an essential trace element at low concentrations. In goats and minipigs fed diets with arsenic levels below 35 ng/g, offspring exhibited reduced birth weights (by 13%), impaired reproduction (e.g., fewer kids per goat: 0.96 versus 1.4 in controls), and lower survival rates, effects that were mitigated by supplementation to 350 ng/g. Similar findings occurred in rats, where growth was depressed on diets under 50 ng/g, and supplementation with 0.5-2 g/g restored normal development. Chicks showed impaired growth on 20-35 ng/g diets, particularly under stress, with benefits from 1-2 g/g arsenic. These observations indicate potential roles in reproduction, growth, and stress response, though critics argue the effects may stem from overall dietary inadequacies rather than specific arsenic deficiency.
Proposed mechanisms involve arsenic influencing methionine metabolism, particularly under conditions like pregnancy, lactation, or nutrient stress. Arsenic deprivation in rats and hamsters lowers plasma taurine levels and hepatic concentrations of polyamines, alongside reduced activity of S-adenosylmethionine decarboxylase, an enzyme key to spermidine and spermine synthesis. Dietary requirements for growth in chicks and rats are estimated at approximately 25 ng/g, extrapolating to a possible human intake of 12 g/dayaligning with typical global dietary levels of 12-40 g from sources like fish, grains, and cereals.
For humans, however, essentiality remains unestablished, with no documented deficiency syndromes despite arsenic's environmental ubiquity in food, water, and air. No specific biochemical processes requiring arsenic, such as enzymatic catalysis or structural roles, have been identified, unlike established trace elements. Animal-derived estimates suggest adequacy from normal diets, rendering human needs moot and complicating controlled deficiency studies. The debate persists due to these indirect lines of evidence, but predominant scientific assessments conclude against proven essentiality, emphasizing toxicity risks over potential benefits at trace levels.

Toxicity Mechanisms

Molecular and cellular effects

Inorganic arsenic primarily exerts toxicity through its trivalent (arsenite, As(III)) and pentavalent (arsenate, As(V)) forms, which disrupt key cellular processes at the molecular level. As(III) exhibits high affinity for sulfhydryl (-SH) groups in proteins, binding to vicinal thiols and inhibiting enzymes such as pyruvate dehydrogenase (PDH) and -ketoglutarate dehydrogenase, which are critical for the tricarboxylic acid cycle and oxidative phosphorylation. This inhibition occurs via interaction with lipoic acid cofactors or dithiols, reducing acetyl-CoA production from pyruvate and impairing ATP synthesis, with 50% inhibition of purified PDH requiring approximately 18 M As(III). As(V), structurally analogous to phosphate, competes in phosphorylation reactions, forming unstable arsenylated intermediates that uncouple substrate-level phosphorylation and deplete cellular phosphate pools. Both species contribute to mitochondrial dysfunction by depleting cellular energy reserves and altering membrane potential.
Arsenic exposure induces oxidative stress by generating reactive oxygen species (ROS), including superoxide and hydrogen peroxide, through redox cycling between As(III) and As(V) or interference with antioxidant enzymes like glutathione peroxidase and superoxide dismutase. This ROS overproduction leads to lipid peroxidation, protein carbonylation, and thiol depletion, exacerbating enzyme inhibition and signaling disruptions in pathways such as MAPK and NF-B. At the cellular level, these effects trigger apoptosis via activation of caspase cascades and mitochondrial cytochrome c release, while also promoting necrosis in high-dose scenarios due to ATP depletion. Arsenic further impairs proteostasis by binding to chaperone proteins and ubiquitin ligases, accumulating misfolded proteins.
Genotoxic effects arise indirectly from ROS-mediated DNA strand breaks, base modifications, and chromosomal aberrations, rather than direct alkylation, with arsenic inhibiting DNA repair by binding to zinc finger domains in proteins like PARP-1 and XPA. This disruption promotes genomic instability, including aneuploidy and micronucleus formation, contributing to clastogenic outcomes observed in exposed cells. Epigenetic alterations, such as global hypomethylation via depletion of S-adenosylmethionine through arsenic's interference with one-carbon metabolism, further amplify cellular dysregulation. These molecular perturbations collectively underlie arsenic's cytotoxicity across cell types, with dose-dependent shifts from adaptive responses at low levels to overt damage at higher exposures.

Dose-response relationships

The dose-response curve for inorganic arsenic toxicity demonstrates nonlinear characteristics, with thresholds evident for noncancer endpoints such as skin lesions and cardiovascular effects, while carcinogenicity exhibits potential sublinearity at low exposures due to efficient biomethylation detoxifying mechanisms that saturate at higher doses. Acute oral exposure in rodents yields LD50 values ranging from 15 to 293 mg As/kg body weight in rats and 11 to 150 mg/kg in other species, primarily manifesting as gastrointestinal hemorrhage, hepatic necrosis, and cardiovascular collapse. In humans, single oral doses exceeding 2 mg As/kg can be fatal, with reported lethal ranges of 22121 mg/kg linked to multiorgan failure, though survival has occurred with supportive care at lower acute thresholds around 13 mg/kg.
Chronic low-dose exposure reveals a no-observed-adverse-effect level (NOAEL) of approximately 0.0008 mg As/kg/day for early dermal effects like hyperpigmentation, derived from epidemiological studies in Taiwan populations consuming arsenic-contaminated water; the U.S. EPA reference dose (RfD) of 0.0003 mg As/kg/day incorporates an uncertainty factor to account for human variability and lacks of carcinogenicity data. Liver enzyme elevations and peripheral neuropathy emerge at LOAELs of 0.006 mg As/kg/day, while skin lesions consistently appear above drinking water concentrations of 50100 g/L (equivalent to ~0.00140.0028 mg As/kg/day for a 70 kg adult consuming 2 L/day), supporting a threshold model where adaptive methylation reduces trivalent arsenic retention below saturation points.
For carcinogenicity, benchmark dose (BMD) modeling from cohort studies indicates a BMD10 (dose associated with 10% excess risk) for bladder cancer around 42 g/L in drinking water, with lower confidence limits suggesting nonlinearity and possible thresholds above 50 g/L, challenging linear no-threshold extrapolations used in some regulatory assessments. Animal studies corroborate reduced sensitivity compared to humans, with chronic NOAELs up to 0.6 mg As/kg/day in rats showing no hematological effects, but human epidemiology underscores interindividual variability influenced by nutrition and genetics, where low doses (<10 g/L) show no consistent excess risk in meta-analyses.

Human Health Impacts

Acute poisoning symptoms

Acute arsenic poisoning typically manifests within minutes to hours following ingestion of a high dose, often exceeding 100 mg of inorganic arsenic in adults, leading to severe gastrointestinal distress as the primary initial presentation. The hallmark symptoms include intense nausea, profuse vomiting, watery diarrhea resembling rice-water stools, and cramping abdominal pain, which can result in rapid dehydration and electrolyte imbalances. These gastrointestinal effects stem from arsenic's disruption of mucosal membranes and inhibition of cellular respiration, causing hemorrhagic enteritis in severe cases.
Cardiovascular complications emerge shortly after gastrointestinal onset, characterized by tachycardia, hypotension, and potentially cardiogenic shock due to myocardial depression and vascular permeability changes. Neurological symptoms may include headache, paresthesia or numbness in the extremities, muscle cramps, confusion, encephalopathy, and seizures, reflecting arsenic's interference with sulfhydryl groups in neuronal enzymes. A metallic or garlic-like odor on the breath or in urine can occur, attributable to volatile arsenic metabolites such as dimethylarsine.
Other systemic effects encompass facial edema, fever, hepatic transaminase elevation indicating liver injury, acute kidney injury from tubular necrosis, and bone marrow suppression leading to pancytopenia. Inhalation or dermal exposure to arsenic compounds may primarily cause respiratory irritation, cough, and pulmonary edema rather than dominant GI symptoms, though systemic toxicity can follow absorption. Without prompt intervention such as chelation therapy, progression to multiorgan failure and death can occur within 24-48 hours, with mortality rates historically approaching 100% in untreated severe cases prior to modern supportive care.

Chronic exposure outcomes

Chronic arsenic exposure, primarily through contaminated drinking water exceeding 1050 g/L over years to decades, manifests as arsenicosis, a condition characterized by dermatological changes including diffuse hyperpigmentation, spotted pigmentation, and hyperkeratotic lesions on palms and soles. These skin alterations, observed in endemic regions like Bangladesh and West Bengal where millions have been affected since the 1980s due to tubewell irrigation, precede more severe sequelae and serve as early biomarkers. Gastrointestinal disturbances such as abdominal pain and diarrhea may persist, alongside hematological abnormalities including anemia, leukopenia, and thrombocytopenia.
Non-malignant systemic effects include peripheral vascular disease, exemplified by Blackfoot disease in Taiwan's artesian wells with arsenic levels up to 1,800 g/L until remediation in the 1970s, leading to gangrene and limb amputation. Cardiovascular outcomes encompass ischemic heart disease and hypertension, with epidemiological data from Chile's Region II (exposed via mining-related water contamination peaking in the 1950s1970s) showing elevated risks even after exposure cessation. Diabetes mellitus type 2 risk increases, as evidenced by cohort studies in Taiwan linking urinary arsenic metabolites to impaired glucose metabolism. Neurological impacts feature symmetric peripheral neuropathy with sensory loss and painful paresthesia, attributed to axonal degeneration confirmed in histopathological examinations. Hepatic involvement ranges from elevated enzymes to portal hypertension and cirrhosis, while renal effects include proteinuria and chronic kidney disease.
Carcinogenic outcomes are well-established for skin (squamous and basal cell), lung, and bladder, with relative risks escalating dose-dependently; for instance, in Bangladesh, skin cancer incidence correlates with water arsenic above 100 g/L over 10+ years. Lung cancer emerges as the leading cause of arsenic-attributable mortality, supported by occupational and environmental cohorts, while associations with liver, kidney, and prostate cancers show consistent but less robust human evidence. In utero and early-life exposure heightens developmental risks, including cognitive deficits and growth stunting, as documented in longitudinal studies from exposed populations. These effects arise via oxidative stress, DNA damage, and epigenetic alterations, though individual susceptibility varies with genetics and nutrition.

Therapeutic benefits versus risks

Arsenic trioxide (ATO), an inorganic arsenic compound, has demonstrated substantial therapeutic efficacy in treating acute promyelocytic leukemia (APL), a subtype of acute myeloid leukemia characterized by a t(15;17) translocation leading to PML-RAR fusion protein expression. In clinical trials, ATO monotherapy achieved complete remission rates of 72-90% in relapsed or refractory APL patients, with mechanisms involving degradation of the PML-RAR oncoprotein, induction of differentiation, and apoptosis in leukemic cells. When combined with all-trans retinoic acid (ATRA), ATO yields event-free survival rates exceeding 97% at 2 years for low-risk APL and overall survival rates of 95-99% across risk groups, outperforming traditional ATRA-chemotherapy regimens in reducing hematologic toxicity and infections while maintaining high cure rates. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration approved ATO in 2000 for relapsed/refractory APL and expanded indications to frontline low-risk cases by 2018, reflecting its role as a standard-of-care component due to targeted cytotoxicity sparing normal hematopoietic cells at therapeutic doses of 0.15 mg/kg daily.
Beyond APL, arsenic compounds show investigational promise in other malignancies, including chronic myeloid leukemia, multiple myeloma, and solid tumors like lung and gastric cancers, where preclinical and early-phase trials indicate anti-angiogenic effects, inhibition of tumor cell proliferation, and synergy with chemotherapeutics via reactive oxygen species generation and hedgehog pathway modulation. However, these applications lack phase III validation and widespread approval, with outcomes limited by variable bioavailability and overlapping toxicity profiles. Historical uses, such as Fowler's solution (potassium arsenite) for syphilis and psoriasis in the 19th-20th centuries, provided symptomatic relief but were abandoned due to inconsistent efficacy and high cumulative toxicity risks, underscoring arsenic's narrow therapeutic index.
Therapeutic risks stem from arsenic's dose-dependent cytotoxicity, manifesting as acute side effects including QT interval prolongation (affecting up to 40% of patients, with torsades de pointes in <5%), electrolyte imbalances (hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia), and differentiation syndrome (fever, dyspnea, pulmonary infiltrates in 20-25% of ATRA-ATO recipients, manageable with steroids). Chronic exposure during prolonged regimens elevates risks of peripheral neuropathy, hepatotoxicity, and secondary malignancies, though incidence remains low (<10%) with monitoring and dose adjustments; pediatric trials confirm safety akin to adults when thiamine supplementation mitigates encephalopathy risks. In APL therapy, benefits eclipse risks for eligible patients, as untreated disease mortality approaches 100% from coagulopathy and hemorrhage, whereas ATO-ATRA cures most without alkylating agents' leukemogenic potential. For non-APL indications, toxicity often precludes net benefit absent superior alternatives, demanding rigorous risk-benefit assessment prioritizing empirical remission data over anecdotal or underpowered studies.

Environmental Dynamics

Natural versus anthropogenic sources

Arsenic enters the environment primarily through natural geological processes, including the weathering and erosion of arsenic-bearing minerals such as arsenopyrite (FeAsS), realgar (AsS), and orpiment (AsS), which are prevalent in volcanic rocks, hydrothermal deposits, and certain sedimentary formations. The average crustal abundance of arsenic is approximately 1.52 mg/kg, with higher concentrations (up to 100 mg/kg or more) in sulfide ores and volcanic materials, leading to baseline releases via dissolution in surface and groundwater, soil leaching, and airborne dust from wind erosion. Volcanic emissions and geothermal activity contribute volatile arsenic species like arsine (AsH) and particulate forms, with global natural atmospheric fluxes estimated at 1025 10 g As/year, predominantly from rock weathering (about 70% of natural inputs) and biogenic methylation by microorganisms in soils and sediments. These processes establish a persistent natural background, responsible for elevated geogenic arsenic in groundwater aquifers, as observed in regions like the Bengal Basin where reductive dissolution of arsenic-rich iron oxides mobilizes up to 15 mg/L in drinking water without human intervention.
Anthropogenic sources mobilize arsenic through industrial extraction and energy production, with primary contributions from non-ferrous metal smelting (especially copper and gold ores containing 0.11% arsenic as impurities), coal combustion (releasing 0.550 mg/kg from high-arsenic coals in regions like China), and legacy agricultural applications such as arsenical pesticides (e.g., lead arsenate) and poultry feed additives (e.g., roxarsone, phased out in the U.S. by 2015). Mining operations, including ore processing and tailings disposal, account for substantial point-source releases; for instance, global arsenic production as a by-product reached about 60,000 metric tons annually around 2006, with waste streams contaminating soils and waters at concentrations exceeding 1,000 mg/kg near sites. Atmospheric emissions from these activities total 1738 10 g As/year, driven largely by smelters (4050%) and fossil fuel burning, while diffuse releases occur via wastewater from electronics manufacturing and legacy wood preservatives like chromated copper arsenate (CCA).
Comparatively, anthropogenic atmospheric emissions now exceed natural background fluxes by a factor of approximately two globally, perturbing the arsenic cycle through enhanced deposition and localized hotspots, though natural processes dominate baseline crustal mobilization and remote areas remain largely unaffected by human inputs. This disparity arises because industrial processes concentrate and accelerate arsenic release from ores that would otherwise weather slowly over geological timescales, with anthropogenic additions estimated to contribute over 80% of recent environmental loading in industrialized regions, as evidenced by isotopic signatures distinguishing human-sourced arsenic (e.g., from smelting) from geogenic forms. While natural sources provide a steady, widespread flux integrated into ecosystems, anthropogenic releases amplify risks through rapid, high-concentration pulses, necessitating targeted monitoring to disentangle origins in contaminated sites.

Fate and transport in ecosystems

Arsenic exists in ecosystems primarily as inorganic species arsenite (As(III)) and arsenate (As(V)), with the latter dominating under oxidizing conditions and exhibiting greater adsorption to mineral surfaces such as iron oxides, thereby reducing mobility in soils and sediments. As(III) prevails in reducing environments, where it demonstrates lower affinity for sorption sites, higher solubility, and increased potential for leaching into groundwater or surface waters. pH further modulates this behavior: at low pH (<7), As(V) adsorption is enhanced, while As(III) mobility rises under alkaline conditions, influencing transport from soils to aquatic systems.
In soils, arsenic partitioning favors solids over solution due to strong binding with clays, organic matter, and hydrous metal oxides, limiting wind erosion or runoff transport unless disturbed by anthropogenic activity; however, reductive dissolution of iron oxides in anoxic zones can remobilize sorbed arsenic. Aquatic fate involves precipitation as arsenopyrite or adsorption to sediments under sulfidic conditions, serving as long-term sinks, though resuspension or redox shifts can reintroduce it to the water column.
Microbial processes significantly alter arsenic speciation through methylation, where bacteria and fungi convert inorganic forms to mono-, di-, and trimethylarsenic compounds, some of which volatilize as gases like trimethylarsine, facilitating atmospheric escape from soils and waters. This biomethylation, prevalent in anaerobic sediments and flooded paddies, enhances evasion from aquatic sinks but introduces volatile arsenic to the air, with methylation efficiency varying by microbial community and substrate availability.
Atmospheric transport of arsenic occurs via aerosol-bound particulates from soil dust or industrial emissions, or as methylated vapors, enabling long-range deposition; global modeling indicates that emissions from Asia contribute approximately 40% of arsenic wet and dry deposition to the Arctic and North America. Hydrological pathways, including surface runoff during precipitation events and subsurface leaching, redistribute arsenic across watersheds, with velocities dictated by local hydrology and exacerbated by erosion in contaminated areas. Overall, redox gradients and microbial activity drive dynamic cycling, with oxidizing surface layers promoting retention and reducing subsurface zones favoring mobilization.

Bioaccumulation and food chain effects

Arsenic bioaccumulates in primary producers and lower trophic levels, particularly in aquatic algae, aquatic plants, and terrestrial crops exposed to contaminated water or soil, but it generally does not biomagnify in food webs due to biotransformation into organic species and excretion in higher organisms. In aquatic systems, phytoplankton and periphyton assimilate dissolved inorganic arsenic, achieving bioconcentration factors up to 10,000 relative to surrounding water, with subsequent trophic transfer to zooplankton and benthic invertebrates via grazing, though assimilation efficiencies decline with increasing organism complexity. Higher predators, such as fish, exhibit biodilution, where arsenic concentrations decrease by one to two orders of magnitude per trophic level, attributed to metabolic conversion to arsenobetaine and other methylated forms that are readily eliminated.
In terrestrial food chains, rice serves as a prominent vector for arsenic bioaccumulation, with flooded paddy conditions reducing soil redox potential and enhancing arsenate mobility, leading to uptake via phosphate transporters and accumulation in grains at concentrations of 0.10.5 mg/kg in contaminated regions like the Bengal Basin. This plant-level retention transfers inorganic arsenic to human consumers, exacerbating exposure in staple-dependent diets, though livestock fed contaminated rice show limited further accumulation due to ruminant detoxification pathways. Soil properties, including pH and organic matter, modulate bioavailability, with acidic, low-iron soils promoting higher plant uptake compared to neutral, organic-rich profiles.
Marine food chains demonstrate similar patterns, with arsenic concentrating in shellfish and crustaceans (up to 10100 g/g wet weight in species like mussels from polluted harbors) through direct filtration of seawater, but predatory fish maintain lower tissue levels (typically <1 g/g) via speciation shifts to non-toxic organics, limiting human health risks from seafood relative to rice or groundwater. Experimental studies confirm trophic transfer factors below unity for total arsenic in freshwater webs, underscoring no net magnification despite initial bioaccumulation. Overall, these dynamics highlight arsenic's persistence at basal levels while mitigating escalation in top consumers, influencing exposure assessments focused on primary vectors rather than apex predators.

Exposure Assessment and Mitigation

Primary exposure pathways

The primary pathways of human exposure to arsenic are ingestion and inhalation, with dermal absorption contributing to a lesser extent due to limited skin permeability, particularly for inorganic forms. Ingestion occurs predominantly through consumption of contaminated drinking water and food, where inorganic arsenic leaches into groundwater from natural geological sources or anthropogenic activities like mining and pesticide use. In regions such as South Asia, groundwater arsenic concentrations exceeding 10 g/Lthe World Health Organization guidelineaffect over 140 million people, making water the dominant chronic exposure route, often amplified by its use in food preparation and irrigation of crops like rice, which bioaccumulate arsenic at levels up to 0.21.0 mg/kg in polished grains.
Inhalation represents a significant pathway in occupational settings, where workers in smelting, mining, or pesticide production inhale arsenic trioxide dust or fumes at concentrations that can reach 110 mg/m without controls, leading to respiratory uptake of 5080% of inhaled particles. Ambient air exposure is minimal globally, typically below 0.01 g/m, but localized spikes occur near industrial sites or from burning arsenic-laden coal or wood. Seafood consumption introduces organic arsenic species like arsenobetaine, which are metabolized and excreted rapidly with low toxicity, contributing negligibly to inorganic arsenic burden compared to terrestrial staples.
Dermal exposure, such as through bathing in contaminated water or soil contact, results in absorption rates under 6% for trivalent arsenic and even lower for pentavalent forms, rendering it non-primary except in scenarios of prolonged contact with high-concentration solutions. Children face elevated risks from hand-to-mouth soil ingestion in contaminated areas, potentially adding 0.0010.01 mg/kg/day in hyper-exposed locales. Overall, exposure assessments prioritize water and diet for population-level interventions, as evidenced by epidemiological data linking 90% of chronic cases to these vectors in endemic regions.

Regulatory standards and controversies

The World Health Organization established a provisional guideline value of 10 g/L for arsenic in drinking water in 1993, designated as provisional due to practical achievability in monitoring and treatment rather than a strict health-based threshold, though concentrations above this level pose risks of skin lesions, cancers, and cardiovascular effects from chronic exposure. The United States Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) set the maximum contaminant level (MCL) at 10 g/L (10 ppb) in 2001 under the Safe Drinking Water Act, with compliance required by public water systems starting January 23, 2006, replacing the prior 50 g/L standard; this reflects a health-based goal of zero but balances feasibility, as epidemiological data link even lower chronic exposures to increased bladder and lung cancer risks. The European Union adopted a similar 10 g/L limit via Directive 98/83/EC in 1998, enforceable across member states for public supplies.
For occupational exposure, the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) enforces a permissible exposure limit (PEL) of 0.010 mg/m as an 8-hour time-weighted average for inorganic arsenic compounds, classifying them as carcinogens requiring engineering controls, respirators, and medical surveillance; this limit stems from 1970s rulemaking based on lung cancer risks observed in smelter workers. The National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH) recommends a more stringent ceiling of 0.002 mg/m not to be exceeded in any 15-minute period, viewing inorganic arsenic as an occupational carcinogen with no safe threshold.
In food, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has not established binding maximum levels for inorganic arsenic in most products like rice but conducts risk assessments and issues guidance, such as advising variety selection and water rinsing to reduce exposure in infant rice cereals, where levels can contribute significantly to early-life intake; FDA monitoring shows U.S. rice often has lower inorganic arsenic than imports. The Codex Alimentarius Commission sets international guidance, including 0.2 mg/kg for inorganic arsenic in polished rice (adopted 2014), while the EU enforces stricter limits like 0.2 mg/kg for white rice and 0.1 mg/kg for rice-based baby foods since 2016.
Controversies surrounding arsenic regulations center on the adequacy of the 10 g/L drinking water threshold, with critics arguing it remains too high given evidence of genotoxic effects and cancers at levels as low as 5 g/L in Taiwanese and Chilean studies, potentially underprotecting vulnerable populations; proponents of the standard cite cost-benefit analyses showing disproportionate treatment expenses for small rural systems without proportional health gains. In the U.S., post-2006 implementation revealed persistent exceedances in over 700 systems serving millions, raising equity concerns for low-income communities reliant on groundwater, though EPA data indicate overall reductions in population exposure. For food, debates intensified over rice after 2012 Consumer Reports testing highlighted elevated inorganic arsenic in U.S. products, prompting FDA assessments but no mandates, with industry emphasizing total diet context and natural soil variability over isolated limits. Regulatory bans include the EPA's voluntary phase-out of chromated copper arsenate (CCA) for residential wood preservatives by 20032004 due to dermal absorption and child exposure risks from playground equipment, shifting use to industrial applications. The FDA suspended approvals for roxarsone and related organoarsenic feed additives in poultry in 2011 after finding they metabolize to potentially carcinogenic inorganic forms, leading to permanent withdrawal by 2015 amid animal health claims lacking robust evidence against efficacy alternatives. Earlier, the EPA restricted most inorganic arsenic pesticides by the 1980s following ecological and human toxicity data from agricultural runoff.

Remediation technologies and efficacy

Adsorptive processes using iron oxide-based media, such as granular ferric hydroxide or modified iron sands, effectively remove arsenic from groundwater and drinking water by forming surface complexes with arsenate (As(V)) and, to a lesser extent, arsenite (As(III)) after pre-oxidation. These technologies achieve removal efficiencies of 80-95% or higher, reducing concentrations from influent levels of 50-100 g/L to below the U.S. EPA maximum contaminant level (MCL) of 10 g/L in pilot and full-scale demonstrations, particularly at neutral pH and with low competing anions like phosphate. Limitations include reduced efficacy for As(III) without oxidation (e.g., via chlorination or permanganate) and media exhaustion requiring replacement every 6-24 months depending on water arsenic levels and flow rates.
Coagulation-filtration, often employing ferric or aluminum salts, co-precipitates arsenic as insoluble hydroxides or adsorbs it onto flocs, yielding 80-90% removal under optimal conditions of pH 6-7 and coagulant doses of 10-30 mg/L as Fe. Full-scale municipal plants have demonstrated effluent arsenic below 10 g/L from influents up to 50 g/L, though efficiency drops at higher pH (>8) or with silica interference. Reverse osmosis membrane filtration provides robust removal (>90%) across arsenic species, consistently achieving effluents below 5 g/L from 56 g/L influents in point-of-use systems, but generates 20-50% reject brine requiring disposal and incurs higher energy costs.
Advanced nanomaterials, including metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) like MIL-53(Al), offer adsorption capacities up to 105-300 mg/g for As(V) in batch tests, with selectivities >90% even in multi-contaminant matrices, outperforming traditional activated alumina (5-50 mg/g). However, scalability remains limited by high costs ($200+/kg) and potential leaching in real-world applications, as evidenced by lab-scale efficiencies of 95-99% that decline in continuous-flow pilots due to clogging. Agricultural waste-derived adsorbents, such as modified rice husks, provide cost-effective alternatives with capacities of 0.1-5 mg/g and up to 99% removal in low-arsenic waters, though regeneration is challenging and performance varies with organic matter content.
For arsenic-contaminated soils, solidification/stabilization (S/S) with Portland cement or lime binders immobilizes arsenic, reducing Toxicity Characteristic Leaching Procedure (TCLP) extract concentrations below 5 mg/L in 84% (37/44) of full-scale applications, including reductions from 5,000-40,000 mg/kg total arsenic to <0.5 mg/L leachate after oxidation pretreatment. Costs range $60-290/ton, but long-term remobilization risks exist under acidic conditions or after 5-6 years. Electrokinetic remediation applies low-voltage fields to mobilize ionic arsenic in low-permeability soils like clays, achieving >88% removal (from >250 mg/kg to <30 mg/kg) in one full-scale project over 1-2 m depths, with costs of $50-270 per cubic yard; efficacy depends on soil pH (optimal 4-7) and electrolyte enhancements.
Soil washing with acids or chelants extracts arsenic from fine fractions, but data show limited success (no quantifiable removals in 9 applications), with costs of $65-400/ton and risks of incomplete removal in aged, sorbed forms. Phytoremediation using hyperaccumulators like Pteris vittata extracts shallow arsenic (<1 m), with bioconcentration factors up to 265 in bench tests, though full-scale biomass yields only stabilize rather than fully remediate, requiring safe disposal of contaminated plants. Overall efficacy across technologies varies with arsenic speciation (As(V) more amenable than As(III)), soil mineralogy, and redox conditions, necessitating site-specific pilots; integrated approaches combining oxidation and adsorption enhance outcomes by 20-50% in combined water-soil systems.

Recent Developments

Advances in medical applications

Arsenic trioxide (ATO), an inorganic arsenical, has been a frontline therapy for relapsed or refractory acute promyelocytic leukemia (APL) since its FDA approval in 2000, inducing differentiation and apoptosis in promyelocytic cells via degradation of the PML-RAR fusion protein. Recent multicenter randomized trials, such as one comparing ATO to realgar-indigo naturalis formula in non-high-risk APL patients, have demonstrated comparable efficacy with reduced hospitalization needs when using oral formulations, achieving complete remission rates exceeding 95% in induction phases. Oral ATO combined with all-trans retinoic acid (ATRA) and ascorbic acid has shown promise in newly diagnosed APL, with phase II trials reporting 42-day induction cycles yielding high response rates while minimizing intravenous administration.
Advances in drug delivery systems have addressed ATO's limitations, including poor bioavailability and off-target toxicity. Nanoparticle-encapsulated ATO formulations enhance tumor-specific accumulation via the enhanced permeability and retention effect, improving efficacy against solid tumors like glioma and reducing systemic exposure; preclinical studies report up to 10-fold increases in cellular uptake compared to free ATO. Emerging orally bioavailable arsenicals, including next-generation derivatives, are under investigation for hematologic malignancies beyond APL, such as myelodysplastic syndromes (MDS), where they promote apoptosis in aberrant stem cells without excessive myelosuppression.
Combination regimens represent a key frontier, synergizing ATO with chemotherapeutic agents, vitamins (e.g., ascorbic acid analogs), and plant-derived compounds to overcome resistance and broaden indications. For instance, ATO-ATRA-chemotherapy protocols in pediatric APL intensification therapy have yielded event-free survival rates over 90% at five years, per 2025 analyses, while ATO paired with histone deacetylase inhibitors targets multiple myeloma via ROS-mediated pathways. In solid tumors, including lung and prostate cancers, ATO modulates inflammatory pathways like NF-B to inhibit proliferation, with phase I/II trials from 2020-2023 showing partial responses in 20-30% of refractory cases. An umbrella review of systematic reviews confirms ATO's edge in enhancing complete remission and recurrence-free survival across leukemias, though long-term cardiac monitoring remains essential due to QT prolongation risks. These developments underscore arsenic's paradoxical role, balancing potent antineoplastic effects against inherent toxicity through refined pharmacokinetics and targeted synergies.

Environmental remediation innovations

Recent advancements in arsenic remediation have emphasized nanotechnology, biological processes, and novel adsorbents to address contamination in water and soil, driven by the limitations of conventional methods like precipitation and filtration, which often generate secondary waste or require high energy inputs. Nanotechnology-based approaches, such as bio-based nanomaterials and magnetite nanocomposites, enable selective adsorption of arsenic species through high surface area and functionalized surfaces, achieving removal efficiencies exceeding 95% in groundwater applications under controlled conditions. For instance, superparamagnetic iron oxide nanoparticles (SPIONs) facilitate rapid arsenic capture via magnetic separation, reducing processing times compared to traditional sorbents. Similarly, reusable nanofibers incorporating arsenic-converting compounds streamline removal by integrating oxidation and adsorption in a single step, as demonstrated in laboratory trials converting soluble arsenite to adsorbable arsenate.
Biological remediation innovations leverage microorganisms and plants for in situ treatment, minimizing environmental disruption. Phytobial remediation, combining hyperaccumulator plants like Pteris vittata with arsenic-tolerant microbes, enhances uptake and transformation of arsenic in rhizospheres, with field studies reporting up to 70% reduction in soil bioavailability over growth cycles. Genetically engineered bacteria expressing arsenite oxidase genes promote biovolatilization or bioprecipitation, converting toxic arsenic forms into volatile organoarsines or insoluble sulfides, though scalability remains challenged by microbial stability in native environments. Biochar derived from agricultural waste, modified with iron oxides, immobilizes arsenic in soils by reducing mobility and leaching, with applications showing 80-90% immobilization rates in contaminated farmlands as of 2025.
Advanced chemical and hybrid methods further innovate remediation efficacy. Amino acid-derived ionic liquids enable soil washing with extraction efficiencies of 60-85% for arsenic, selectively desorbing it without excessive disruption to soil structure, as evaluated in 2025 studies on moderately contaminated sites. A 2025 chemical process treats arsenic-laden sludge from water treatment by alkali washing to liberate arsenic, followed by stabilization into non-toxic compounds, potentially repurposing waste for construction materials while preventing re-release. Modified natural zeolites, impregnated with multivalent metals like iron or aluminum, adsorb arsenic anions from water with capacities up to 50 mg/g, outperforming unmodified variants in low-concentration scenarios typical of drinking water sources. These innovations prioritize cost-effectiveness and sustainability, yet require site-specific validation to account for matrix effects like pH and co-contaminants influencing performance.
