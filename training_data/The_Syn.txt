The Syn
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The Syn is an English rock band formed in London in 1965 through the merger of two local groups, The Selfs and a band previously known as High Court, evolving from rhythm and blues roots into mod and psychedelic rock. Best remembered among collectors for its brief original tenure and two singles released on Deram Records"Created by Clive" b/w "Grounded" in June 1967 and "Flowerman" b/w "14 Hour Technicolor Dream" in September 1967the band featured key members who later shaped progressive rock history.
The original lineup centered on vocalist and guitarist Steve Nardelli, bassist Chris Squire, guitarist Peter Banks, keyboardist Andrew Pryce Jackman, and drummers including Gunnar Hkonarson, Ray Steele, and Chris Allen, with the group performing at iconic venues like The Marquee Club alongside emerging acts such as Pink Floyd, The Who, Cream, and Jimi Hendrix. By late 1967, The Syn disbanded as Squire and Banks transitioned to Mabel Greer's Toyshop, a direct precursor to the progressive rock band Yes, where both became founding members.
A short-lived reunion occurred in 1974 under the name Narsiquijack involving Nardelli, Squire, and Jackman, but the band reformed more substantially in 2004, led by Nardelli alongside original drummer Martyn Adelman and Banks, adopting a progressive rock style influenced by Yes's sound. This iteration released the studio album Syndestructible in 2005, followed by Armistice Day in 2006, with subsequent lineups incorporating musicians like guitarist Francis Dunnery, keyboardist Tom Brislin, and bassist Gerard Johnson.
Post-reunion, The Syn continued to explore psychedelic and progressive elements, issuing archival compilations such as Original Syn (1967-1969): The Lost Album in 2005 and Flowerman: Rare Blooms from The Syn 1965-69 in 2021, which collected unreleased recordings from their formative years and highlighted their influence on British rock's evolution. Despite limited commercial success in their original era, The Syn's legacy endures through its connections to Yes and its role in London's vibrant 1960s music scene.

History

Formation and early years (19651966)

The Syn originated in late 1965 from the amalgamation of two north London rhythm and blues groups: The Selfs, featuring vocalist and guitarist Steve Nardelli, and the original Syn, which had evolved from the band High Court with guitarist John Painter. The merger was sparked by a head-to-head "battle of the bands" competition at the Hampstead Country Club, where the two outfits competed and subsequently decided to join forces.
To complete the lineup, Nardelli and Painter recruited bassist Chris Squire, keyboardist Andrew Pryce Jackman, and drummer Martyn Adelman from The Selfs, establishing the band's initial configuration focused on R&B covers and mod-influenced sounds reflective of London's vibrant mid-1960s club scene. The name "The Syn" was proposed by Painter, drawing from the group's interest in creating a distinctive, synthesized sonic identity amid the era's beat music trends.
The band quickly immersed itself in the London music circuit, securing early residency spots and support slots at key venues that shaped the mod and R&B landscape. Performances in late 1965 and early 1966 included appearances at the Ricky Tick Club in Guildford, where they shared bills with emerging acts, honing their energetic stage presence amid influences from groups like The Who and The Small Faces. By mid-1966, The Syn had built a following through regular gigs at clubs such as the Marquee, supporting established performers and experimenting with original material rooted in the scene's raw, danceable energy.

Psychedelic period (1967)

In 1967, The Syn shifted from their mod and R&B roots toward psychedelic rock, embracing the era's burgeoning counterculture trends and experimenting with more experimental songwriting and arrangements in the studio. This evolution was facilitated by their signing to Decca's progressive Deram Records imprint, where they recorded tracks that incorporated psychedelic elements such as swirling keyboards, Eastern-influenced melodies, and introspective lyrics reflecting flower power themes. The band's original lineupvocalist Steve Nardelli, bassist Chris Squire, guitarist Peter Banks, keyboardist Andrew Jackman, and drummer Gunnar Hkonarsoncollaborated on these sessions, with producer Vic Smith overseeing the recordings that highlighted their growing ambition beyond straightforward pop structures.
The Syn's first Deram single, "Created by Clive" backed with "Grounded," was released on June 23, 1967, showcasing a whimsical, Carnaby Street-style A-side with harpsichord flourishes and a more grounded, rhythmic B-side influenced by their beat origins. Despite its catchy, novelty appeal, the single achieved only minor airplay and sales in the UK, failing to chart amid competition from established acts. Their second single, "Flowerman" paired with "14 Hour Technicolour Dream," followed on September 1, 1967, delving deeper into psychedelia with the A-side's pastoral imagery of urban escape and the B-side's nod to the era's all-night happenings, complete with tape effects and modal guitar lines. Like its predecessor, it garnered limited commercial success, receiving praise from underground tastemakers but little mainstream traction.
A key live milestone came on January 24, 1967, when The Syn supported The Jimi Hendrix Experience at London's Marquee Club, exposing them to innovative guitar techniques and psychedelic stagecraft that further inspired their sonic explorations. Internally, however, strains emerged as Squire and Banks developed stronger interests in progressive structures and longer-form compositions, leading to creative tensions with the band's pop-oriented direction; Squire's frequent tardiness to rehearsals and gigs exacerbated these issues.
Several unreleased tracks from this period, including demos like "Merry-Go-Round" and alternate takes of single material, captured their studio improvisations and were later rediscovered on the 2021 compilation Flowerman: Rare Blooms from The Syn 1965-69, offering insight into their transitional sound.

Disbandment and individual pursuits (19682003)

Following the release of their second single "Flowerman" in September 1967, The Syn disbanded by the end of the year due to the commercial failure of both singles on Deram Records and the departure of bassist Chris Squire and guitarist Peter Banks, who joined the short-lived group Mabel Greer's Toyshop as a precursor to forming Yes.
Squire played a central role in Yes's formation in July 1968, enlisting Banks from Mabel Greer's Toyshop and vocalist Jon Anderson to complete the initial lineup alongside drummer Bill Bruford and keyboardist Tony Kaye. Banks contributed guitar to Yes's first two albums, the self-titled Yes (1969) and Time and a Word (1970), before departing in 1970 amid creative differences.
Vocalist and rhythm guitarist Steve Nardelli left music for a business career, eventually becoming a director of the P3 Group of eco-focused companies while continuing to compose and produce music in collaboration with former bandmate Andrew Pryce Jackman. Keyboardist Jackman focused on session work as an arranger and composer for British popular music acts, including orchestral contributions to Chris Squire's solo debut Fish Out of Water (1975), until his death from a heart attack on August 16, 2003, at age 57.
Drummer Martyn Adelman and original lead guitarist John Painter both exited the music scene shortly after the band's dissolution, with limited public records of their subsequent pursuits. Sporadic archival interest in The Syn's 1967 recordings grew in the 1970s and 1990s among progressive rock collectors, driven by Squire and Banks' prominence in Yes, resulting in unofficial bootlegs and preparations for official reissues.

Reunion and core revival (20042015)

The reunion of The Syn was sparked by a 2003 interview conducted by vocalist Steve Nardelli with bassist Chris Squire to commemorate the band's 40th anniversary, during which the two discussed reviving the group, an idea further motivated by the sudden death of original keyboardist Andrew Pryce Jackman from a heart attack on August 16, 2003. In Jackman's memory, Nardelli coordinated the release of the archival compilation The Original Syn 19652004 in 2005 on Umbrello Records, which included three newly recorded tracks featuring Nardelli, original guitarist Peter Banks, drummer Martyn Adelman, and keyboardist Gerard Johnson. However, Banks departed the project shortly thereafter due to creative differences, paving the way for Squire's full involvement in the band's core revival.
The revived lineup centered on Nardelli (vocals), Squire (bass), Johnson (keyboards), Paul Stacey (guitar), and Jeremy Stacey (drums), producing the band's first full studio album of original material, Syndestructible, released in November 2005 on Umbrello Records. This progressive rock effort blended the band's mod and psychedelic roots with modern symphonic elements, highlighted by extended tracks such as "City of Dreams" (9:38) and "Golden Age" (8:07), alongside shorter pieces like the instrumental opener "Breaking Down Walls" (0:51). Squire's distinctive Rickenbacker bass tone anchored the sound, evoking Yes influences while reworking Syn-era themes, including a re-recorded version of the 1967 single "Flowerman."
The album's release was supported by live performances, beginning with a debut show at London's Marquee Club on November 15, 2005. A UK tour followed in January 2006, featuring Alan White (drums, from Yes) replacing Jeremy Stacey and Shane Theriot (guitar) on second guitar for select dates, with setlists emphasizing Syndestructible material interspersed with 1960s medleys. The band expanded its reach with an appearance at the RoSFest progressive music festival in Sarasota Springs, New York, on May 13, 2009, where they performed tracks from Syndestructible alongside later material from the 2009 album Big Sky.
The revival faced significant setbacks with the deaths of key figures. Original guitarist Peter Banks, who had contributed to the early reunion recordings, died on March 7, 2013, at age 65 from heart failure amid ongoing health issues. Squire's passing on June 28, 2015, at age 67 from acute erythroid leukemia marked the end of the core revival era, as he had been central to the band's post-2004 activities. These losses profoundly impacted the group's momentum, shifting focus away from the classic lineup configuration.

Evolving lineups and recent activities (2016present)

Following the death of bassist Chris Squire in June 2015, vocalist and founder Steve Nardelli assumed leadership of The Syn, steering the band toward collaborative projects that honored its progressive rock roots. In March 2016, the band released the studio album Trustworks through Umbrello Records, a collaborative effort with Swedish progressive rock group Moon Safari; Nardelli provided lead vocals, while Moon Safari members including Jonas Reingold (bass, additional guitars, keyboards) and Pontus kesson (guitars) contributed instrumentation, resulting in a blend of melodic prog elements and atmospheric soundscapes. The album's vinyl edition followed in August 2016, marking a transitional phase in the band's evolving lineup centered on Nardelli's vision without a fixed touring ensemble.
In October 2021, Cherry Red Records' Grapefruit imprint issued the compilation Flowerman: Rare Blooms From The Syn 1965-69, featuring 14 tracks of previously unreleased or rare material from the band's formative psychedelic era, including demos and outtakes with original members like Squire and guitarist Peter Banks. This archival release underscored The Syn's enduring influence as a precursor to Yes, compiling sessions that captured their mod-to-psychedelia shift.
The band has maintained a low-profile presence in the late 2010s and 2020s, with no major tours or new studio recordings announced as of 2025; activities have centered on legacy preservation through reissues and occasional virtual initiatives, such as exploratory "virtual band" concepts discussed in prog community updates. Nardelli has continued to reflect on The Syn's history in interviews, emphasizing its connections to progressive rock pioneers and the impact of lost members like Squire.

Band members

Original and classic lineup

The original and classic lineup of The Syn formed in 1965 through the merger of school friends' group High Court with The Selfs, a rhythm and blues outfit, establishing the band's foundation in mod and R&B sounds that evolved toward psychedelia. This core ensemble, active until 1967, featured vocalist and guitarist Steve Nardelli as the founder, alongside bassist Chris Squire, guitarist John Painter, keyboardist Andrew Pryce Jackman, and drummers Martyn Adelman (initially), Gunnar Hkonarson, Ray Steele, and Chris Allen; guitarist Peter Banks joined in 1966, adding a dynamic edge before the band's initial disbandment. Their collective contributions shaped The Syn's early harmonic richness and instrumental interplay, with several members later influencing progressive rock via Yes.
Steve Nardelli served as lead vocalist and rhythm guitarist from 1965 to 1967, acting as the band's founder and primary songwriter alongside Jackman. His ambitious compositions, such as the psychedelic rock opera "Flowerman," blended mod influences with emerging psychedelic elements, driving the group's shift from R&B covers to original material characterized by layered vocal harmonies. Nardelli's guitar work provided rhythmic support, complementing the band's harmony-based sound that emphasized melodic interplay.
Chris Squire played bass from 1965 to 1967, bringing a distinctive, melodic style rooted in Tamla Motown R&B that foreshadowed his innovative approach in Yes. At just 16, his brilliant Rickenbacker bass lines co-developed The Syn's foundational sound, offering syncopated counterpoint and prominent melodic roles that elevated the band's rhythmic drive beyond standard mod fare. Squire's contributions included backing vocals, enhancing the harmonic texture in tracks like early demos.
John Painter handled rhythm guitar from 1965 to 1966, contributing to the band's initial mod R&B framework through solid chord progressions and supportive harmonies. As an early member from Nardelli's school group, Painter's playing focused on rhythmic stability and vocal backing, helping establish the ensemble's tight, harmony-driven live performances before Banks' arrival. His role emphasized the group's cover-oriented phase, providing the backbone for Squire's bass prominence.
Peter Banks, joining as lead guitarist in 1966 and staying through 1967, introduced experimental leads that infused progressive elements into The Syn's sound. Replacing Painter, Banks' innovative guitar work, featuring sparkling tones and interplay with keyboards, added psychedelic flair to singles like "Grounded," bridging the band's R&B roots with more adventurous structures. His contributions highlighted dynamic solos and textural depth, influencing the group's brief evolution toward complexity.
Andrew Pryce Jackman provided keyboards from 1965 to 1967, incorporating classically trained orchestral elements that enriched the band's arrangements with harpsichord and organ swells. As a key composer, he wrote originals like "Love You" and envisioned psychedelic rock operas, driving progressive influences through lush, arrangement-heavy textures that contrasted the rhythm section's drive. Jackman's work laid early groundwork for symphonic prog, evident in demos featuring piano and backing vocals.
Martyn Adelman delivered drums from 1965 until mid-1966, supplying a solid backbeat that anchored the band's mod R&B origins and live energy. Transitioning from The Selfs, his steady, punchy style supported the harmonic and bass interplay, maintaining propulsion in covers and originals alike without overshadowing the melodic focus. Adelman's rhythmic foundation proved essential for the group's cohesive performances during their active years; he was replaced by Gunnar Hkonarson, followed by Ray Steele and Chris Allen.

Reunion-era and later members

The reunion of The Syn in 2004 was spearheaded by vocalist and guitarist Steve Nardelli and bassist Chris Squire, both founding members from the band's 1960s era, marking a revival focused on progressive rock explorations. Guitarist Peter Banks, another original member, participated in initial 2003-2004 rehearsals and recordings during this period but departed before the 2005 album. Keyboardist Gerard Johnson joined the core group from 2004, contributing to the band's sound through his work on keyboards and production. Drummer Alan White, known for his long tenure with Yes, provided drums for the 2006 tour, though his involvement was limited by ongoing commitments to Yes.
The debut reunion album, Syndestructible (2005), expanded the lineup for studio work with guitarist Paul Stacey and drummer Jeremy Stacey (Paul's brother), who added layered guitar textures and dynamic drumming to tracks like "Einstein's Question." Live shows supporting the album in 2006 featured a touring ensemble of Nardelli on vocals and guitar, Squire on bass, Johnson on keyboards, guitarist Shane Theriot, and White on drums, emphasizing the band's progressive leanings in performances across North America.
By the late 2000s, the lineup shifted significantly for the album Big Sky (2009) and subsequent live appearances, incorporating guitarist Francis Dunnery (also handling bass), keyboardist Tom Brislin, guitarist Brett Kull, and drummer Paul Ramsey, who provided a solid rhythmic foundation for the band's evolving sound. This configuration performed at events like RoSFest in 2009, where Dunnery's guitar work and Brislin's keyboard arrangements highlighted influences from the original psychedelic roots while advancing progressive structures.
Following Squire's death in 2015 and Banks' earlier exit, Nardelli remained the creative anchor, leading the band through its post-reunion phase with guest and session contributors. The 2016 album Trustworks, a collaboration with Swedish prog group Moon Safari, utilized various session players including vocalists and multi-instrumentalists Simon kesson, Pontus kesson, Mikael Israelsson, Daniel Brenna, and Fredrik Hermansson, alongside producer Jonas Reingold on bass, guitars, and keyboards, to craft intricate, symphony-like compositions. Since 2016, The Syn has been largely inactive, with no new recordings or tours as of 2025, and Nardelli as the primary figure. These changes reflected The Syn's adaptability, blending veteran presence with fresh talents to sustain its legacy.

Musical style and influences

Roots in mod and psychedelia

Formed in late 1965 in North London, The Syn emerged as an R&B-oriented mod band, drawing heavily from the British beat scene and performing covers of Tamla Motown hits such as "Heatwave" and "I'll Keep On Holding On." Their early sound emphasized driving rhythms, tight harmoniesoften provided by bassist Chris Squire and keyboardist Andrew Pryce Jackman, both former choirboysand a youthful, amphetamine-fueled energy reminiscent of contemporaries like The Who and The Small Faces. Gigs at venues like Kingsbury County School showcased this mod style, blending American soul influences with the sharp, mod fashion and scooter culture of 1960s London youth.
By 1967, The Syn underwent a marked psychedelic shift, moving away from R&B covers toward original compositions infused with the era's experimental ethos. This evolution was evident in singles like "Flowerman," which featured florid, flower-power lyrics satirizing suburban life, and the B-side "14 Hour Technicolour Dream," inspired by the iconic London be-in at Alexandra Palace. Signed to Deram Records, the band incorporated theatrical elements in performances at the Marquee Club, where they once opened for The Jimi Hendrix Experiencean event attended by The Beatlesreflecting their immersion in the London underground scene. Key influences included The Beatles' Revolver for its studio innovations and Hendrix's bold experimentation, pushing The Syn toward psychedelic guitar rock with dynamic arrangements.
Instrumentally, Squire's melodic, riff-driven bass lines provided a foundational pulse, as heard in tracks like the freakbeat-psychedelic hybrid "Grounded," while Jackman's use of the organ added lush, atmospheric textures that enhanced the band's emerging psychedelic depth. These signatures, combined with guitarist Peter Banks' contributions, distinguished The Syn within the mod-to-psychedelia transition, foreshadowing the progressive tendencies of members' later work without fully departing from their 1960s roots.

Progressive evolution and legacy ties

Following their reunion, The Syn's music evolved to incorporate greater progressive complexity, blending the band's psychedelic roots from the 1960s with extended compositions and intricate arrangements characteristic of progressive rock. The 2005 album Syndestructible exemplifies this fusion, featuring tracks with layered instrumentation, dynamic shifts, and prominent bass lines played on Chris Squire's signature Rickenbacker, which added a resonant, driving foundation to the sound. Critics noted the album's dual nature, balancing accessible rock structures with progressive depth, positioning it as a modern extension of early prog ideals.
Subsequent releases further advanced this trajectory, introducing symphonic textures and collaborative elements that enriched the band's palette. On the 2009 album Big Sky, the lineup including Francis Dunnery and Tom Brislin incorporated orchestral flourishes and melodic progressions to create expansive, forward-looking tracks. This work was hailed as a pinnacle of the band's progressive maturation, earning acclaim for its innovative blend of harmony-driven rock and symphonic ambition, and was voted the best progressive album of 2009 by Progressive Music magazine.
The Syn's legacy is deeply intertwined with the origins of progressive rock, particularly as a precursor to Yes, where founding members Chris Squire and Peter Banks honed techniques like intricate bass-guitar interplay and experimental song structures that later defined the genre. Early Syn recordings are credited by critics with proto-progressive innovations, such as harmonic experimentation and rhythmic complexity, influencing the London prog scene of the late 1960s.
In the years following Squire's death in 2015, The Syn paid tribute through Trustworks (2016), a collaborative effort with Moon Safari that emphasized orchestral progressive elements, including sweeping arrangements and multi-part vocal harmonies evoking Squire's foundational contributions. The band continued this evolution with releases such as Illusion in 2023, maintaining symphonic and progressive flair in their ongoing work as of 2025. This underscores the band's enduring ties to prog's evolution, blending nostalgic reverence with contemporary symphonic ambition.

Discography

Studio albums

The Syn's studio albums, released following the band's 2004 reunion, blend progressive rock elements with psychedelic influences, often featuring collaborations with notable musicians from the genre. These recordings were primarily overseen by vocalist and founder Steve Nardelli, who guided production and incorporated guest artists to expand the band's sound.
Syndestructible (2005) marked the band's first full-length studio album in nearly four decades, comprising 10 tracks that revive their progressive psychedelic style with modern production. Released in the UK on Umbrello Records on October 31, 2005, it includes extended compositions like "Cathedral of Love" (8:58) and "City of Dreams" (9:38), emphasizing melodic hooks and symphonic textures. Produced by Paul Stacey, the album features contributions from Yes bassist Chris Squire on bass and vocals, alongside drummer Alan White, highlighting ties to the band's Yes connections.
Armistice Day (2006) combines new studio recordings with live and acoustic performances, serving as a transitional release that bridges the band's reunion era. Issued by Umbrello Records, it features seven tracks including the title song (3:59), "Silent Revolution" (7:59), and a live version of "Cathedral of Love" (8:48), with contributions from Chris Squire and Paul Stacey. The album explores themes of peace and reflection, blending prog arrangements with intimate arrangements.
Big Sky (2009), a collaborative progressive rock effort, showcases extended suites and conceptual depth across 11 tracks, including the title track (5:57) and the multi-part "Life Is a Puzzle" sequence. Released on Umbrello Records in 2009, it incorporates guest performers such as vocalist Dorie Jackson, guitarist Brett Kull, and drummer Paul Ramsey from echolyn, under Nardelli's direction, resulting in a blend of mod-prog experimentation and orchestral arrangements. The album's thematic focus on existential themes is evident in pieces like "Universal Witness" (4:47) and the closing "In the Court of the Crimson Queen" (7:06).
Trustworks (2016), a 9-track tribute album created in the wake of Chris Squire's 2015 passing, integrates symphonic elements through a collaboration with Swedish progressive band Moon Safari. Released on Umbrello Records, it features Nardelli's production oversight and guest musicians including Moon Safari's Fredrik Hermansson on keyboards, with highlights like "Trustworks" (5:53) and "Lucifer Hesitating" (5:23) exploring themes of reflection and revolution. The album's orchestral swells and layered harmonies pay homage to the band's legacy while introducing fresh arrangements.
Illusion (2023), the band's most recent studio effort, comprises five tracks centered on an extended title piece and reinterpretations of earlier material. Released on September 15, 2023, by Cherry Red Records, it includes the 14-minute epic "Illusion," a re-recorded "Grounded 2004" (5:05), and a cover of Yes's "Time and a Word" (4:59), produced by Nardelli with contributions from returning collaborators. The album delves into introspective and symphonic prog, marking a continuation of the band's evolving sound.

Compilations and live releases

The Syn's compilations primarily focus on archival material from their 1960s era, gathering demos, singles, and unreleased tracks that highlight their mod and psychedelic roots. In 2005, the band released Original Syn 19652004, a two-CD set compiling their early singles, home demos, and rehearsal recordings from the mid-1960s, including tracks like "Grounded" and "Flowerman" alongside previously unheard material featuring future Yes members Chris Squire and Peter Banks. This collection, issued by Umbrello Records, provides a comprehensive retrospective of the band's pre-reunion output, with extensive liner notes detailing their formation and evolution.
Building on this archival interest, Flowerman: Rare Blooms from The Syn 1965-69 was released in 2021 by Grapefruit Records, a Cherry Red imprint. This single-CD anthology assembles all surviving 1960s recordings, including the band's Deram singles, pre-Syn tracks by related acts like The Selfs, and post-Syn demos involving Squire and Banks, such as "Merry-Go-Round" and "The Gangster Opera." Accompanied by a 5,000-word essay, rare photos, and a foreword by Phil Collins, it emphasizes the band's underground London scene contributions and ties to progressive rock pioneers.
Live releases capture the band's reunion performances, showcasing their progressive evolution. The Syn Live Rosfest, recorded at the 2009 RoSFest in Glenside, Pennsylvania, was issued in 2015 as a CD with a bonus DVD by Umbrello Records. The set features a lineup including Steve Nardelli on vocals, Francis Dunnery on guitar, and Tom Brislin on keyboards, performing tracks like "14 Hour Technicolour Dream" and "New Reality" from their Big Sky album, blending 1960s classics with newer material. The DVD includes additional footage and interviews, documenting the band's dynamic stage presence during their U.S. tour.
The band has also appeared on various-artist progressive rock compilations, contributing tracks that bridge their historical and modern phases, though specific charity efforts like ProgAid in 2009 remain less documented in primary discographies.

Singles and EPs

The Syn released two singles during their original 1960s incarnation on the Deram label, both showcasing their mod and psychedelic influences but failing to achieve commercial chart success. Their debut single, "Created by Clive" backed with "Grounded," was issued in June 1967 (catalog DM 130) as a 7-inch vinyl 45 RPM release in the UK. The A-side featured a psychedelic pop arrangement with ornate orchestration, while the B-side offered a harder mod-style track; neither entered the UK Singles Chart. The follow-up, "Flowerman" with B-side "14 Hour Technicolour Dream," followed in September 1967 (catalog DM 145), also on 7-inch vinyl. This release captured underground acclaim for its freakbeat and psychedelic elements, particularly the dreamy A-side evoking the era's counterculture, though it too did not chart and remained a collectors' favorite among 1960s British rock enthusiasts.
Following the band's reunion in the mid-2000s, they issued their first post-reformation single in October 2005 on Umbrello Records (catalog UMBRCD002), formatted as a CD single that functions as a three-track EP. Titled Cathedral of Love, it led with the title tracka melodic progressive rock piece from their forthcoming album Syndestructiblepaired with remastered versions of the 1967 B-sides "Fourteen Hour Technicolor Dream" and "Grounded" for historical context. Released digitally and on CD in limited quantities, it did not chart but served as a promotional bridge to the band's evolved sound, blending reunion-era material with archival tracks. No additional standalone EPs were released, though promotional materials tied to Syndestructible occasionally featured select tracks from the album in limited-edition formats for industry and fan distribution.

Video releases

The Syn's video releases center on live concert footage from their 2000s reunion era, documenting performances that blend their psychedelic roots with progressive rock elements. These DVDs and streaming videos capture key moments in the band's revival, featuring founding member Steve Nardelli alongside collaborators like the late Chris Squire.
The debut video release, Syndestructible Tour 2006, was issued by Umbrello Records in 2006 as a 120-minute NTSC DVD of the band's live set at the Somerville Theatre in Boston, Massachusetts, during their tour for the album Syndestructible. Recorded with the lineup of Nardelli on vocals and guitar, Squire on bass, Paul Stacey on guitar and keyboards, and Adam Robinson on drums, the concert includes 13 tracks such as "Breaking Down Walls," "Cathedral of Love," "Flowerman," and "City of Dreams," interspersed with an interlude story by Squire about Jimi Hendrix. The production emphasizes the band's dynamic stage presence and high-energy delivery of reunion material alongside 1960s classics.
In 2015, Umbrello Records followed with Live Rosfest, a hybrid CD/DVD edition preserving the band's full May 1, 2009, performance at RoSFest in Glenside, Pennsylvania's Keswick Theatre. The 70-minute DVD footage showcases a set of 12 songs, including "14 Hour Technicolour Dream," "Grounded," "Big Sky," and "Universal Witness," performed by Nardelli, Squire, Francis Dunnery on guitar, and Simon Phillips on drums. Bonus content comprises two documentaries: a 20-minute band retrospective titled The Syn in the 21st Century with interviews, and The Making of Big Sky, detailing the creation of their 2011 album. This release underscores the group's enduring appeal at progressive festivals and their technical prowess in a live setting.
A notable archival video emerged in 2018 via a limited-edition vinyl reissue of Syndestructible, which included an exclusive streaming link to The Syn: Live and Raw at the Marquee 2005. This footage documents the band's raw album launch concert at London's Marquee Club on November 26, 2005, featuring the original reunion quartet of Nardelli, Squire, Banks (on guitar), and Alan White (on drums). The unpolished video highlights intimate performances of tracks like "Syndestructible" and early singles, offering insight into the emotional reunion and promotional energy of the event.
Official promotional videos for select tracks have also been produced and shared online, such as the clip for "Big Sky" from the 2011 album of the same name, which uses stylized visuals to promote the song's thematic depth. These shorter videos, often uploaded to platforms like YouTube, support the band's digital presence and tie into their live releases by excerpting studio or performance elements.
