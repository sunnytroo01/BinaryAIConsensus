Rainforests are the most biologically diverse ecosystems on Earth. Covering approximately six percent of the planet's land surface, they are home to more than half of all known plant and animal species. A single hectare of tropical rainforest can contain over seven hundred species of trees, more than the total number of tree species found in all of North America. The diversity is so extreme that in many rainforests, it is possible to walk for miles without encountering two individuals of the same tree species. Rainforests are the crown jewels of the living world, and their destruction represents one of the greatest biological losses in the history of life on Earth.

Tropical rainforests are found in a band around the equator, in regions that receive at least eighty inches of rainfall per year distributed relatively evenly throughout the year. The largest tropical rainforest is the Amazon, which covers approximately two point one million square miles across nine countries in South America, with the majority in Brazil. The Congo Basin rainforest in Central Africa is the second largest. The Southeast Asian rainforests, spanning Indonesia, Malaysia, and other countries, are the third.

The structure of a tropical rainforest is organized into distinct vertical layers, each with its own microclimate, light level, and community of organisms. The emergent layer consists of the tallest trees, which rise above the main canopy to heights of one hundred and fifty to two hundred feet and are exposed to the strongest winds and most intense sunlight. The canopy layer, at approximately sixty to one hundred feet, forms a continuous roof of interlocking branches and leaves that intercepts most of the sunlight and rainfall. The understory, beneath the canopy, receives only about five percent of the sunlight and is home to shade-tolerant plants, small trees, and a wealth of climbing vines and epiphytes, plants that grow on other plants without being parasites, including orchids, ferns, and bromeliads. The forest floor is the darkest layer, receiving less than two percent of the sunlight, and is covered in a thin layer of decomposing leaves, branches, and fallen fruit.

Despite the extraordinary lushness of the vegetation, rainforest soils are surprisingly poor. Most of the nutrients in a rainforest ecosystem are stored in the living biomass, the trees, plants, and animals, rather than in the soil. When leaves, branches, and organisms die and fall to the forest floor, they are decomposed rapidly by the warm, moist conditions and the abundant fungi and bacteria, and the nutrients are immediately absorbed by the shallow root systems of the surrounding trees. This rapid nutrient cycling means that if the forest is cleared, the soil loses its fertility within a few years, which is why slash-and-burn agriculture in rainforest regions produces diminishing returns and drives farmers to clear more forest in a destructive cycle.

Rainforests play a critical role in the global climate system. They absorb approximately two billion tons of carbon dioxide per year through photosynthesis, acting as a massive carbon sink that helps to moderate the greenhouse effect. The Amazon alone produces approximately six percent of the world's oxygen, earning it the nickname "the lungs of the Earth," although this is somewhat misleading because the forest also consumes a similar amount of oxygen through respiration and decomposition. The more important climate function of the Amazon is its role in the water cycle: the forest releases enormous quantities of water vapor through transpiration, which forms clouds that produce rainfall not only over the forest itself but also over agricultural regions thousands of miles away.

The biodiversity of rainforests is almost incomprehensibly rich. The Amazon rainforest contains approximately ten percent of all species on Earth, including approximately forty thousand plant species, thirteen hundred bird species, three thousand species of freshwater fish, over four hundred species of amphibians, and an unknown number of insect species, with estimates ranging from two and a half million to thirty million. Many of these species have never been scientifically described, and new species are discovered at a rate of approximately one per day.

Rainforest species have evolved extraordinary adaptations. Poison dart frogs produce toxins in their skin so potent that indigenous peoples have used them to coat the tips of their blowgun darts for hunting. The Brazil nut tree depends on a single species of orchid bee for pollination, and the bees depend on a specific orchid for their mating fragrance, creating a chain of interdependence that illustrates the delicate web of relationships that sustains rainforest ecosystems. Strangler figs begin their lives as seeds deposited by birds in the canopy of a host tree, send roots down to the ground, and gradually envelope and kill the host tree, creating a hollow fig tree that stands where the host once was.

Deforestation is the most immediate threat to the world's rainforests. An area of tropical rainforest roughly the size of a football field is destroyed every six seconds, primarily for cattle ranching, soy cultivation, palm oil production, and logging. The Amazon has lost approximately seventeen percent of its forest cover in the past fifty years. Scientists have warned that if deforestation reaches a tipping point of approximately twenty to twenty-five percent, the remaining forest may not be able to generate enough rainfall to sustain itself, triggering a cascade of dieback that could convert much of the Amazon from rainforest to savanna, releasing billions of tons of stored carbon into the atmosphere and accelerating climate change dramatically.

Indigenous peoples have lived in and managed rainforests sustainably for thousands of years. Their traditional knowledge of the forest, its plants, its animals, its medicines, and its ecological processes represents an invaluable cultural and scientific resource. Studies have consistently shown that indigenous-managed territories have lower rates of deforestation than surrounding areas, demonstrating that the most effective way to protect rainforests is often to protect the rights and territories of the people who have been their stewards for millennia.

The rainforest is not just a collection of trees. It is a living system of almost unimaginable complexity, a community of millions of species that have co-evolved over millions of years into a web of interdependence so intricate that removing a single strand can cause the entire fabric to unravel. Protecting the world's remaining rainforests is not just an environmental priority. It is a moral obligation to the future of life on Earth.
