Symmetry breaking
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Symmetry breaking in physics is a fundamental concept describing scenarios where the governing equations of a system possess a symmetryinvariance under certain transformationsbut the physical states or solutions do not fully exhibit that symmetry. There are two primary forms: explicit symmetry breaking, in which an external influence or term in the Lagrangian directly violates the symmetry, altering the equations of motion (e.g., applying a force to bend a symmetric rod in a specific direction), and spontaneous symmetry breaking (SSB), where the system's Hamiltonian or Lagrangian remains symmetric, but the ground state selects a configuration that breaks the symmetry, leading to degenerate vacua related by the symmetry group. The concept was pioneered in the 1960s through studies of superconductivity and the Goldstone theorem.
SSB is ubiquitous across physics, manifesting in condensed matter systems such as ferromagnets, where the Heisenberg model Hamiltonian is rotationally invariant, yet the ground state aligns all spins in a particular direction, producing macroscopic magnetization. In superconductors and superfluids, it underlies phenomena like the Meissner effect and Bose-Einstein condensation, breaking U(1) gauge symmetry in superconductors or global U(1) phase symmetry in superfluids to enable zero-resistance currents and coherent quantum states. In particle physics, SSB plays a central role in the Standard Model through the Higgs mechanism, where the electroweak symmetry is spontaneously broken by the Higgs field's non-zero vacuum expectation value, granting mass to the W and Z bosons while preserving the photon's masslessness, consistent with the short range of the weak force. This mechanism, proposed in 1964 and experimentally confirmed by the 2012 discovery of the Higgs boson at CERN, resolves the mass problem for fundamental particles without violating gauge invariance. Beyond these, symmetry breaking influences cosmology, for example through phase transitions in the early universe that contribute to the formation of cosmic structures.

Introduction

Definition and basic concept

Symmetry breaking in physics refers to a situation where the observed laws of nature or the equilibrium state of a physical system do not possess all the symmetries inherent in the underlying Lagrangian or Hamiltonian that governs the system. This phenomenon arises when the full symmetry group of the theory is reduced to a subgroup in the actual physical realization, leading to asymmetric outcomes despite symmetric fundamental principles. For instance, while the equations describing a system may be invariant under certain transformations, the stable states or solutions selected by the system may break this invariance.
A key distinction exists between global and local symmetries in the context of symmetry breaking. Global symmetries involve transformations that are uniform across all space and time, such as rotations or translations applied identically everywhere, and their breaking typically results in conserved quantities or phase transitions. In contrast, local symmetries, also known as gauge symmetries, allow parameters to vary from point to point in spacetime, imposing stricter constraints on the theory and often requiring mediating fields to preserve consistency, as seen in fundamental interactions like electromagnetism. Breaking local symmetries can lead to massive particles and short-range forces, distinguishing them from the long-range effects of unbroken gauge symmetries.
An intuitive analogy for symmetry breaking is a pencil balanced precariously on its tip, which possesses full rotational symmetry in its upright position but spontaneously loses this symmetry upon falling, choosing a particular direction due to infinitesimal perturbations. This illustrates how symmetric initial conditions can evolve into asymmetric states without any explicit bias in the governing rules. Symmetry breaking can occur explicitly, through terms in the theory that directly violate the symmetry, or spontaneously, where the symmetry is hidden in the ground state selection.
Symmetry breaking plays a crucial role in understanding why the universe exhibits apparent asymmetries, such as the dominance of matter over antimatter, despite the fundamental laws being largely symmetric. It provides a framework for how symmetric theories can produce asymmetric physical realities, from the structure of crystals to the large-scale features of cosmology, without requiring asymmetries in the core principles themselves.

Historical development

The foundational link between symmetries and conservation laws in physics was established by Emmy Noether in her 1918 paper, where she proved that every continuous symmetry of the action of a physical system corresponds to a conserved quantity, providing the mathematical basis for later explorations of symmetry breaking. This theorem highlighted how symmetries underpin physical laws, setting the stage for understanding scenarios where ground states fail to respect those symmetries despite the underlying Lagrangian being invariant.
In the mid-20th century, the concept of symmetry breaking gained traction in condensed matter physics, particularly through studies of superconductivity. Philip W. Anderson's 1963 analysis demonstrated how gauge symmetry is spontaneously broken in superconductors to explain the Meissner effect and the generation of effective masses for photons, marking a pivotal application of the idea in many-body systems. Building on this, Yoichiro Nambu in 1960 drew an analogy between superconductivity and particle physics, proposing that spontaneous symmetry breaking in quantum fields could generate massless excitations akin to Cooper pairs, thus extending the notion to relativistic quantum field theory.
The 1960s saw rapid theoretical advancements. In 1961, Jeffrey Goldstone formulated his theorem, asserting that continuous global symmetries, when spontaneously broken, lead to massless scalar bosons (now known as Goldstone bosons) in the spectrum of the theory. This result posed challenges for gauge theories, as massive gauge bosons seemed incompatible with massless modes. These issues were resolved independently in 1964 by Franois Englert and Robert Brout, Peter Higgs, and Gerald Guralnik, Carl Hagen, and Tom Kibble, who proposed the Higgs mechanism: spontaneous breaking of a local gauge symmetry via a scalar field vacuum expectation value endows gauge bosons with mass while absorbing Goldstone modes.
Subsequent milestones integrated these ideas into unified theories. In 1967, Steven Weinberg and Abdus Salam developed the electroweak theory, incorporating the Higgs mechanism to break SU(2) U(1) symmetry and unify weak and electromagnetic interactions, predicting the Higgs boson. Experimental validation came in 2012 when the ATLAS and CMS collaborations at the Large Hadron Collider discovered a particle consistent with the Higgs boson at 125 GeV. This breakthrough earned Franois Englert and Peter Higgs the 2013 Nobel Prize in Physics for their theoretical predictions of the mechanism.

Types of Symmetry Breaking

Explicit symmetry breaking

Explicit symmetry breaking occurs when the Lagrangian of a physical theory includes terms that directly violate the symmetry of the underlying equations, resulting in asymmetries manifest in the fundamental laws themselves. This contrasts with spontaneous symmetry breaking, where the laws remain symmetric but the ground state selects an asymmetric configuration. In quantum field theory, such violations are often introduced through parameters like particle masses that do not commute with the symmetry transformations.
A canonical mathematical example arises in the Dirac Lagrangian for fermions, where a mass term $ \mathcal{L}_m = m \bar{\psi} \psi $ explicitly breaks chiral symmetry. Here, $ \psi $ represents the Dirac spinor, and the nonzero mass $ m $ mixes left- and right-handed components, preventing independent transformations under the chiral group $ SU(N_f)_L \times SU(N_f)_R $, where $ N_f $ is the number of flavors. The nonzero mass $ m $ explicitly breaks the classical chiral symmetry, mixing left- and right-handed components and giving the pions a small mass (instead of being massless Goldstone bosons). Quantum mechanically, a chiral anomaly further violates the conservation of the axial current, enabling the decay $ \pi^0 \to \gamma \gamma $.
In particle physics, explicit breaking is exemplified by the violation of parity (P) and chirality in the weak interaction, stemming from its vector-minus-axial-vector (V-A) structure in the Fermi theory. This asymmetry implies that weak processes distinguish between left- and right-handed particles, directly embedding non-conservation into the interaction Lagrangian. The historical discovery came in 1956, when Tsung-Dao Lee and Chen-Ning Yang proposed that parity might not hold in weak decays, motivated by puzzles like the - problem in kaon decays. Their hypothesis was experimentally confirmed in 1957 by Chien-Shiung Wu and collaborators, who observed asymmetric beta emission from polarized cobalt-60 nuclei at near-absolute-zero temperatures, showing electrons preferentially emitted opposite the nuclear spin direction.
Explicit breaking also plays a key role in flavor physics, where differences in quark masses violate the approximate $ SU(3) $ flavor symmetry among up, down, and strange quarks. Although quantum chromodynamics treats these light quarks nearly equally, the strange quark mass ($ m_s \approx 95 $ MeV) exceeds the up ($ m_u \approx 2.2 $ MeV) and down ($ m_d \approx 4.7 $ MeV) masses, introducing terms in the Lagrangian that split hadron multiplets and explain patterns like the "eightfold way" deviations. These mass differences generate explicit chiral symmetry breaking, contributing to phenomena such as the unequal lifetimes of charged and neutral kaons.
The consequences of explicit breaking include small but measurable asymmetries, such as the neutron electric dipole moment induced by CP violation in the Standard Model. In the quark sector, the complex phases in the Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa matrix explicitly violate CP symmetry, predicting a neutron dipole moment on the order of $ 10^{-32} $ ecm, though current limits (as of 2020: $ |d_n| < 1.8 \times 10^{-26} $ ecm) probe beyond-Standard-Model physics. This explicit CP term in the Lagrangian underscores how such violations yield direct, albeit tiny, observables in low-energy systems.

Spontaneous symmetry breaking

Spontaneous symmetry breaking occurs in physical systems where the fundamental laws, described by a symmetric Lagrangian or Hamiltonian, admit multiple degenerate ground states, and the system selects one of these states through thermal or quantum fluctuations, resulting in a ground state that lacks the original symmetry. This mechanism is vividly illustrated by the "Mexican hat" potential, where the potential energy landscape features a circular valley of minima, and fluctuations drive the system to settle in one direction around the valley, breaking rotational symmetry.
A key concept in spontaneous symmetry breaking is the order parameter, which is a measurable quantity that is zero in the symmetric phase but acquires a non-zero value in the broken phase, signaling the emergence of order. For example, in ferromagnets below the Curie temperature, the magnetization serves as the order parameter, distinguishing the spontaneously magnetized state from the symmetric, unmagnetized one. Mathematically, consider a complex scalar field \phi with the potential V()=22+4V(\phi) = -\mu^2 |\phi|^2 + \lambda |\phi|^4V()=22+4 where 2>0\mu^2 > 02>0 and >0\lambda > 0>0. This potential has a minimum at a non-zero vacuum expectation value =v=2/\langle \phi \rangle = v = \sqrt{\mu^2 / \lambda}=v=2/, leading to a vacuum state that breaks the underlying U(1) symmetry of the Lagrangian while the Lagrangian itself remains invariant.
Unlike explicit symmetry breaking, where symmetry-violating terms are directly present in the action, spontaneous breaking arises solely from the choice of vacuum, preserving the symmetry of the fundamental equations. This phenomenon applies to both global symmetries, such as U(1) phase symmetry in superconductivity, and local (gauge) symmetries, although the latter requires the additional Higgs mechanism to consistently describe the breaking without massless Goldstone modes in the spectrum. As a consequence, spontaneous symmetry breaking generates effective masses for particlessuch as gauge bosonswithout introducing explicit mass terms in the original symmetric theory. According to Goldstone's theorem, this breaking of continuous global symmetries generally produces massless Nambu-Goldstone bosons corresponding to the broken generators.

Theoretical Foundations

Mathematical formalism in quantum field theory

In quantum field theory, a Lagrangian or action $ S $ possesses a symmetry under a Lie group $ G $ if it remains invariant under infinitesimal transformations $ \delta \phi = i \epsilon^a T^a \phi $, where $ T^a $ are the generators of $ G $. Spontaneous symmetry breaking arises when the ground state, or vacuum, is not invariant under the full group $ G $, but only under a subgroup $ H \subset G $, characterized by the vacuum expectation value (VEV) of the scalar fields acquiring a nonzero value that selects a particular direction in the field space. This breaking pattern $ G \to H $ is determined by the minima of the effective potential, which governs the stability of the vacuum.
The path integral formulation provides a natural framework for analyzing symmetry breaking. The generating functional is given by
Z=Dexp(iS+id4xJ(x)(x)),

where $ J $ is an external source. In the absence of sources ($ J = 0 $), the partition function $ Z $ encodes the vacuum structure. Symmetry breaking manifests through a nonzero VEV $ \langle \phi \rangle = v \neq 0 $ in the effective potential $ V_{\text{eff}}(\phi) $, obtained via the Legendre transform of the connected generating functional $ W = -i \ln Z $, with $ V_{\text{eff}}(\phi) = -\frac{1}{V} W + J \phi $ at the stationary point $ \frac{\delta W}{\delta J} = \phi $. This VEV breaks the symmetry if it lies away from the origin, leading to a degenerate set of vacua related by $ G $. In finite volume, quantum tunneling between these vacua can restore the symmetry, but in the infinite volume limit, the system selects a broken phase.
Renormalization plays a crucial role in the consistency of symmetry-broken theories. The running of couplings under the renormalization group flow is influenced by the breaking pattern, as the VEV introduces mass scales that alter the beta functions. For explicit breaking, small perturbations to the Lagrangian, such as mass terms, introduce "soft" violations that do not drastically alter the low-energy spectrum but can stabilize the vacuum. The Ward-Takahashi identities, which enforce current conservation in symmetric theories, are modified in the broken phase: for a conserved current $ J^\mu $, the identity becomes $ \partial_\mu J^\mu = \Delta $, where $ \Delta $ represents either an anomaly or the explicit breaking term. These identities ensure that the theory remains consistent under renormalization, preserving relations between Green's functions even when symmetries are softly violated.
A notable mechanism for radiative symmetry breaking is the Coleman-Weinberg effect, where quantum corrections at one loop induce a nontrivial minimum in the effective potential for a classically massless theory. The one-loop contribution to the effective potential is

In low dimensions, such as two spatial dimensions, the Mermin-Wagner theorem prohibits continuous spontaneous symmetry breaking at finite temperature due to infrared divergences from long-wavelength fluctuations, necessitating the infinite volume limit in quantum field theory to realize the broken phase stably and avoid restoration via tunneling. This theorem highlights the dimensional dependence of symmetry breaking in QFT.
The broken generators corresponding to $ G/H $ lead to Goldstone modes, massless excitations that parametrize the flat directions of the potential.

Goldstone's theorem and its implications

Goldstone's theorem asserts that in a quantum field theory with a continuous global symmetry that is spontaneously broken, for each broken generator of the symmetry group, there exists a massless scalar particle known as a Nambu-Goldstone boson. This theorem, originally conjectured by Jeffrey Goldstone and rigorously proved by Goldstone, Abdus Salam, and Steven Weinberg between 1960 and 1962, provides a general mechanism linking symmetry breaking to the spectrum of particles.
The derivation of the theorem relies on the structure of current algebra in quantum field theory. Consider a continuous global symmetry with conserved charge $ Q $ associated to a broken generator. The action of this charge on a field $ \phi $ that transforms under the symmetry yields the commutator $ \sim i \partial_\mu \phi(x) $, implying that the vacuum expectation value breaks the symmetry. In the two-point correlation function of the current, this leads to a pole at zero momentum, corresponding to a massless mode; any mass term for such a mode would violate the Ward identity derived from the symmetry, as it would introduce a non-zero vacuum expectation value inconsistent with the broken phase. These Goldstone modes exhibit a linear dispersion relation, characteristic of gapless excitations:

where $ \omega $ is the energy, $ k $ the wave number, and $ c $ a speed parameter determined by the theory, ensuring propagation without an energy gap.
In theories with unbroken global symmetries, the Goldstone bosons remain exactly massless, manifesting as light degrees of freedom in the low-energy effective theory. However, for spontaneously broken local (gauged) symmetries, the theorem's implications differ: the would-be Goldstone modes are absorbed by the gauge bosons through the Higgs mechanism, rendering the gauge bosons massive while eliminating the massless scalars from the physical spectrum. Exceptions arise when the symmetry is discrete, yielding no Goldstone bosons since continuous generators are absent, or when the breaking is explicit rather than spontaneous, resulting in pseudo-Goldstone bosons with small but non-zero masses. A prime example is the chiral symmetry breaking in quantum chromodynamics (QCD), where the pions emerge as pseudo-Goldstone bosons due to explicit breaking by quark masses; their squared mass follows the Gell-MannOakesRenner relation, $ m_\pi^2 \propto m_q \Lambda_\mathrm{QCD} $, with $ m_q $ the light quark mass and $ \Lambda_\mathrm{QCD} $ setting the strong interaction scale.

Applications and Examples

In particle physics

In particle physics, symmetry breaking plays a central role in the Standard Model, particularly through the electroweak symmetry breaking mechanism, which breaks the SU(2)_L U(1)_Y gauge symmetry to the U(1)_EM electromagnetic symmetry via the Higgs doublet field. This spontaneous breaking generates masses for the W and Z bosons while leaving the photon massless, explaining the weak force's short range and the observed particle masses without violating gauge invariance. The Higgs field acquires a nonzero vacuum expectation value (VEV) at the potential's minimum, parameterized by the Mexican hat-shaped Higgs potential V() = -|| + ||, where the VEV v 246 GeV is determined from the Fermi constant G_F via v = (2 G_F)^{-1/2}.
The W boson mass arises directly from this mechanism as m_W = (g v)/2, where g is the SU(2)_L coupling constant, providing a quantitative link between the symmetry breaking scale and electroweak observables. The 2012 discovery of the Higgs boson at approximately 125 GeV by the ATLAS and CMS experiments at the LHC confirmed this spontaneous symmetry breaking, as the particle's properties matched Standard Model predictions for the Higgs excitation.
Another key example is chiral symmetry breaking in quantum chromodynamics (QCD), where the approximate SU(3)_L SU(3)_R symmetry of the light quarks is spontaneously broken, leading to the formation of quark condensates and explaining the small masses of pions as pseudo-Goldstone bosons. This breaking is dynamically generated through strong interactions at low energies, as modeled in the NambuJona-Lasinio framework, which captures the essence of quark pairing analogous to superconductivity and accounts for the internal structure of nucleons.
Explicit symmetry breaking manifests in CP violation within the Standard Model, primarily through the irreducible phase in the CabibboKobayashiMaskawa (CKM) matrix that parametrizes quark mixing in weak interactions. This phase enables processes that distinguish matter from antimatter, satisfying one of the Sakharov conditionsalong with baryon number violation and departure from thermal equilibriumnecessary for the observed cosmic matter-antimatter asymmetry.
Beyond the Standard Model, symmetry breaking extends to proposed extensions like supersymmetry (SUSY), where SUSY is spontaneously broken at a high scale to avoid conflict with experimental null results for superpartners, often mediated by soft breaking terms in the minimal supersymmetric Standard Model (MSSM). In grand unified theories (GUTs), a larger gauge symmetry such as SU(5) or SO(10) is broken at an energy scale around 10^{16} GeV, unifying the strong, weak, and electromagnetic forces while predicting phenomena like proton decay, though unobserved thus far. Ongoing experiments continue to probe flavor symmetries and their potential breaking, seeking hints of new physics.

In condensed matter physics

In condensed matter physics, symmetry breaking manifests in many-body systems where microscopic symmetries of the Hamiltonian lead to macroscopic phases with reduced symmetry, often through collective phenomena at finite temperatures. These transitions are typically second-order phase transitions, described by order parameters that acquire nonzero expectation values below a critical temperature, selecting a preferred direction or state from degenerate possibilities. This framework, rooted in Landau's phenomenological theory of phase transitions, explains diverse ordered states in solids and liquids, from magnetism to superfluidity, without invoking relativistic effects dominant in particle physics.
A classic example is ferromagnetism, where below the Curie temperature TcT_cTc, the system undergoes spontaneous breaking of the continuous rotational SO(3) symmetry of spin interactions, resulting in a net magnetization along a spontaneously chosen direction. In the Heisenberg model of interacting spins, the ground state degeneracy allows thermal fluctuations to select an alignment, leading to long-range magnetic order. The low-energy excitations in this broken-symmetry phase are magnons, which are quantized spin waves acting as Goldstone modes corresponding to the broken rotational generators. This concept was first developed in Felix Bloch's 1930 theory of spin waves, which explained the temperature dependence of magnetization in ferromagnets through these collective deviations from perfect alignment.
Superconductivity provides another paradigm, involving the spontaneous breaking of U(1) gauge symmetry associated with charge conservation, driven by the formation of Cooper pairs of electrons with opposite momenta and spins. Below the critical temperature, the superconducting order parameter \psi, representing the pair wavefunction, develops a nonzero magnitude, selecting a phase that breaks the symmetry. In the presence of the electromagnetic field, the would-be Goldstone mode from phase fluctuations is absorbed via the Anderson-Higgs mechanism, gapping it into the plasma frequency and enabling the Meissner effect of perfect diamagnetism.
Liquid crystals exemplify breaking of both translational and rotational symmetries, transitioning from isotropic fluids to ordered phases like the nematic state, where molecules align along a director field n\mathbf{n}n without positional order. In the nematic phase, the continuous O(3) rotational symmetry is spontaneously broken to O(2), with n\mathbf{n}n (a headless vector, nn\mathbf{n} \equiv -\mathbf{n}nn) serving as the order parameter that orients the molecular axes preferentially. This alignment arises from anisotropic intermolecular interactions, leading to macroscopic birefringence and fluidity. A more recent development involves topological insulators, where symmetry-protected edge states emerge from band structures robust against perturbations, often relying on time-reversal symmetry for protection in non-interacting systems. These helical edge modes, with spin-momentum locking, conduct without backscattering in the bulk gapped phase. In some realizations, such as magnetic topological insulators, explicit breaking of time-reversal symmetry via doping or proximity effects induces a mass gap in the edges while preserving topological invariants, enabling applications like quantum anomalous Hall effects. This connects symmetry considerations to robust quantum transport at material boundaries.

In cosmology and other fields

In cosmology, symmetry breaking plays a pivotal role during the early universe's phase transitions, where high temperatures restore symmetries that later break as the universe cools. The electroweak phase transition occurs around 100 GeV, marking the spontaneous breaking of SU(2)_L U(1)_Y symmetry into the electromagnetic U(1) symmetry, while the quantum chromodynamics (QCD) chiral phase transition happens at approximately 150 MeV, restoring quark confinement and breaking chiral symmetry. First-order phase transitions, such as those potentially in the electroweak sector, can facilitate baryogenesis by providing out-of-equilibrium conditions amplified by explicit CP violation, generating the observed baryon asymmetry. Andrei Sakharov outlined the necessary conditions for baryogenesis in 1967, requiring baryon number violation, C and CP violation, and departure from thermal equilibrium, often realized through spontaneous symmetry breaking (SSB) in non-equilibrium settings like these transitions.
Inflationary cosmology further illustrates symmetry breaking, addressing issues like the excess of magnetic monopoles predicted by grand unified theories (GUTs). In GUT models, the breaking of a unified symmetry group, such as SU(5), at high energies produces monopoles whose density would overclose the universe unless diluted by rapid expansion. Cosmic inflation, proposed by Alan Guth in the early 1980s, resolves this through exponential growth driven by a scalar inflaton field, effectively breaking scale invariance in the early universe's power spectrum while suppressing monopole relics. Guth's original "old inflation" model used a false vacuum decay mechanism driven by a scalar field in a supercooled state, achieving exponential expansion before reheating via bubble nucleation, though it encountered issues like the graceful exit problem resolved in subsequent slow-roll models.
Beyond cosmology, symmetry breaking manifests in diverse fields. In chemistry, the Jahn-Teller effect causes explicit breaking of orbital symmetry in transition metal complexes, where degenerate electronic states lead to geometric distortions that lower the system's energy, as seen in octahedral Cu(II) ions elongating along the z-axis. In biology, the prevalence of left-handed chirality in amino acids and right-handed DNA may trace to parity violation in weak interactions, introducing a tiny energy difference (~10^{-17} eV) between enantiomers that could bias molecular self-assembly during prebiotic evolution. In mathematics and dynamical systems, the breaking of Lie group symmetries, such as SO(n) or SU(n), often triggers bifurcations, where symmetric equilibria become unstable, leading to asymmetric attractors or heteroclinic cycles in equivariant vector fields.
