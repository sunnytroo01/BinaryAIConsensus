Fluconazole

1x
 Fluconazole is a synthetic triazole antifungal agent used to treat and prevent various fungal infections, including mucosal and systemic candidiasis, cryptococcal meningitis, and prophylaxis in immunocompromised patients. It belongs to the class of azole antifungals and is available in oral, intravenous, and in some markets topical formulations, with high bioavailability exceeding 90% when taken orally.
The drug functions as a fungistatic agent by selectively inhibiting the fungal cytochrome P450 enzyme 14--demethylase, which disrupts ergosterol biosynthesis in the fungal cell membrane, leading to increased permeability and impaired growth of susceptible fungi such as Candida species and Cryptococcus neoformans. Fluconazole is indicated for conditions like vaginal candidiasis (typically a single 150 mg dose), oropharyngeal and esophageal candidiasis (200 mg loading dose followed by 100 mg daily), systemic Candida infections including candidemia, and acute cryptococcal meningitis (400 mg loading dose followed by 200 mg daily for at least 1012 weeks). Off-label uses include treatment of endemic mycoses such as blastomycosis, histoplasmosis, and coccidioidomycosis. It is primarily excreted unchanged by the kidneys, with a plasma half-life of approximately 30 hours, allowing for once-daily dosing.
Common adverse effects include nausea, headache, abdominal pain, diarrhea, and elevated liver enzymes, while serious risks involve hepatotoxicity, QT interval prolongation, and rare severe skin reactions like Stevens-Johnson syndrome. Contraindications include hypersensitivity to azoles and concurrent use with certain drugs that prolong the QT interval, such as terfenadine or cisapride. High doses of fluconazole during the first trimester of pregnancy have been associated with an increased risk of congenital malformations; it was formerly classified as FDA Pregnancy Category D, though it is generally considered safe during lactation with minimal transfer to breast milk. Developed in the 1980s and approved by the FDA in 1990, it remains a cornerstone therapy for fungal infections due to its favorable pharmacokinetic profile and broad-spectrum activity against yeasts.

Medical uses

Indications

Fluconazole is approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of vaginal candidiasis, particularly as a single-dose therapy for uncomplicated cases caused by Candida species. It is also indicated for oropharyngeal and esophageal candidiasis, often in immunocompromised patients such as those with HIV. Additional FDA-approved uses include systemic candidiasis, encompassing candidemia, disseminated infections, and pneumonia in patients with normal or impaired white blood cell counts; candiduria (urinary tract infections due to Candida); peritonitis; and acute cryptococcal meningitis. For prophylaxis, fluconazole is indicated to reduce the incidence of candidiasis in high-risk patients, such as those undergoing bone marrow transplantation with heavy cytotoxic chemotherapy or radiation, and in HIV-positive individuals at risk for oropharyngeal candidiasis.
Off-label applications of fluconazole extend to other fungal infections, including maintenance therapy for cryptococcal meningitis, blastomycosis, coccidioidomycosis, histoplasmosis, and dermatophytosis, as well as prevention of fungal infections in neonates, HIV/AIDS patients, and in patients receiving broad-spectrum antibiotic therapy to prevent candidiasis. It is commonly used off-label for cutaneous manifestations of endemic mycoses and onychomycosis (toenail fungal infections), where once-weekly dosing is employed. For the off-label treatment of onychomycosis, patients should consult a healthcare professional before starting treatment to ensure proper diagnosis and management. A fungal scraping test, such as potassium hydroxide (KOH) preparation or culture, should be performed to confirm the diagnosis and identify the pathogen, as misdiagnosis can lead to ineffective therapy. Baseline liver function tests are recommended due to the risk of hepatotoxicity, particularly with prolonged courses of treatment. Self-treatment should be avoided owing to risks such as the development of antifungal resistance, incomplete cure leading to recurrence, or potential harm from adverse effects without medical supervision.
Dosing regimens vary by indication; for uncomplicated vaginal candidiasis, a single 150 mg oral dose is standard, while complicated cases may require 150 mg every 72 hours for three doses. For oropharyngeal candidiasis, treatment typically begins with 200 mg on the first day followed by 100 mg daily; esophageal candidiasis uses 200-400 mg daily. Systemic infections, including candidemia, often involve a 400-800 mg loading dose followed by 200-400 mg daily, with higher doses for cryptococcal meningitis maintenance at 200 mg daily after initial induction.
Fluconazole demonstrates high efficacy in treating mucosal candidiasis, with clinical cure rates of 80-90% and mycological eradication rates around 86% in comparative studies against topical agents like clotrimazole. In systemic candidiasis, it serves effectively as step-down oral therapy following intravenous amphotericin B in stable patients with susceptible isolates, reducing colonization and superficial infection rates.
As of 2025, the Infectious Diseases Society of America (IDSA) and global guidelines recommend fluconazole as an alternative initial therapy for non-neutropenic candidemia in stable, non-critically ill patients when azole resistance is unlikely, with a loading dose of 800 mg followed by 400 mg daily for susceptible Candida species. It is also endorsed as first-line oral consolidation therapy after echinocandin induction in invasive candidiasis cases without recent azole exposure.

Topical formulations

Fluconazole cream (typically 0.5% or 1% fluconazole) is a topical antifungal formulation used to treat superficial fungal skin infections, such as dermatomycosis. It is not a standard FDA-approved product in many countries, where fluconazole is primarily available as an oral medication; topical versions appear in research studies and some regional markets. The composition includes fluconazole as the active ingredient in a cream base. Side effects are generally mild and local (e.g., potential skin irritation), with clinical studies indicating good safety and no major adverse events reported in trials comparing it to other topical antifungals.

Resistance and management

Resistance to fluconazole in fungal pathogens, particularly Candida species, arises through several key mechanisms that impair the drug's ability to inhibit ergosterol biosynthesis. Mutations in the ERG11 gene, which encodes the target enzyme lanosterol 14-demethylase, reduce the binding affinity of fluconazole to the enzyme, thereby decreasing its inhibitory effect. Overexpression of efflux pumps, such as CDR1 and CDR2 in Candida albicans, actively expels the drug from fungal cells, lowering intracellular concentrations. Additionally, biofilm formation in C. albicans and non-albicans species like C. glabrata creates a protective matrix that limits drug penetration and promotes persister cells with reduced susceptibility.
Prevalence of fluconazole resistance has been increasing globally, especially among non-albicans Candida species. Candida krusei exhibits intrinsic resistance, while C. glabrata shows acquired resistance rates up to 15% in clinical isolates, driven by efflux pump upregulation. Surveillance data from bloodstream infections indicate overall resistance rates of 5-10% in Candida isolates as of 2025, with higher figures in intensive care unit settings where up to 54% of isolates may be resistant, particularly C. glabrata and emerging pathogens like C. auris.
Risk factors for developing fluconazole resistance include prior exposure to azoles, which selects for resistant strains through prolonged selective pressure, and extended therapy durations that allow mutational adaptation. Immunocompromised patients, such as those in ICU environments or undergoing hematopoietic stem cell transplantation, face elevated risks due to frequent prophylaxis and underlying host vulnerabilities. Environmental factors, including the widespread agricultural use of azole fungicides, contribute to cross-resistance by exposing fungi to similar triazole structures, fostering mutations that transfer to clinical settings.
Management of fluconazole-resistant candidiasis emphasizes antifungal susceptibility testing to guide therapy, with Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute (CLSI) breakpoints defining isolates with minimum inhibitory concentrations (MICs) 8 g/mL as susceptible, 16-32 g/mL as susceptible dose-dependent, and 64 g/mL as resistant for most Candida species. For resistant cases, switching to echinocandins like caspofungin is recommended as first-line therapy due to their efficacy against azole-resistant strains. Combination therapy with amphotericin B may be employed in severe infections to enhance fungal clearance, while antifungal stewardship programs promote judicious use to curb resistance emergence through de-escalation and prophylaxis optimization.
Recent developments as of 2025 highlight the World Health Organization's fungal priority pathogens list, which classifies fluconazole-resistant Candida auris as a critical priority due to its multidrug resistance and nosocomial spread. Studies have explored dose escalation to 800 mg/day for partially resistant (susceptible dose-dependent) strains, showing improved outcomes in candidemia when MICs are between 16-32 g/mL, though this approach requires monitoring for toxicity.

Safety profile

Contraindications

Fluconazole is contraindicated in patients with known hypersensitivity to fluconazole, other azole antifungals, or any of its excipients, as severe allergic reactions, including anaphylaxis and Stevens-Johnson syndrome, have been reported in such cases. Coadministration with terfenadine, cisapride, pimozide, or other drugs known to prolong the QT interval and increase the risk of torsades de pointes is also absolutely contraindicated due to the potential for life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias. Concurrent use with medications that can cause QT prolongation or torsades de pointes, such as certain antiarrhythmics or antipsychotics, is similarly prohibited to avoid additive effects on cardiac repolarization.
Relative contraindications include severe hepatic impairment classified as Child-Pugh C, where fluconazole use requires careful risk-benefit assessment due to the potential for exacerbated hepatotoxicity, though no specific dose adjustment is mandated. High-dose or prolonged fluconazole therapy during pregnancy, particularly in the first trimester, is associated with risks of teratogenic effects such as craniofacial abnormalities, skeletal defects, and congenital anomalies resembling Antley-Bixler syndrome. The FDA advises avoiding use unless benefits outweigh risks, based on case reports and observational data showing increased malformation risks with chronic high-dose exposure (400-800 mg/day). As of 2025, updates in some regions, including advice for women of childbearing potential to use effective contraception during treatment, highlight ongoing concerns based on post-marketing pregnancy outcome data. Fluconazole is excreted into breast milk at concentrations similar to maternal plasma levels (approximately 98%). Use during breastfeeding requires consideration of the developmental and health benefits to the infant against potential risks, such as gastrointestinal disturbances or rash; monitoring the infant is recommended. Single low doses are generally considered low risk.
Special monitoring is essential for patients with a history of cardiac conditions at risk for QT interval prolongation, as fluconazole can exacerbate this and lead to serious arrhythmias. Prolonged high-dose therapy also warrants monitoring for adrenal insufficiency, as fluconazole may inhibit steroidogenesis and cause hypocortisolism. The FDA includes boxed warnings for hepatotoxicity, emphasizing discontinuation if signs of liver injury appear, particularly in patients with underlying hepatic disease.

Adverse effects

Fluconazole is generally well tolerated, with most adverse effects being mild and transient. Common side effects occurring in more than 1% of patients include nausea (reported in 3-7% of cases across clinical trials), headache (approximately 2%), abdominal pain (around 3%), diarrhea (2%), and rash (1-2%). These gastrointestinal and dermatological symptoms are more frequently observed in patients receiving multiple doses compared to single-dose therapy.
Serious adverse effects, though less common, require prompt attention. Hepatotoxicity manifests as elevated alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST) levels in 1-5% of patients, with rare instances of fulminant hepatic failure, particularly in those with underlying conditions like AIDS or malignancy. QT interval prolongation is dose-dependent, with increased risk at doses exceeding 400 mg/day, potentially leading to torsades de pointes in susceptible individuals. Severe cutaneous reactions, such as toxic epidermal necrolysis or Stevens-Johnson syndrome, occur in less than 0.1% of cases. Anaphylaxis is rare but has been reported in patients with hypersensitivity.
Incidence data from clinical trials and post-marketing surveillance indicate that fewer than 1% of patients discontinue therapy due to adverse effects, though rates can reach 1.5% in broader populations. Adverse events are more prevalent in elderly patients, with higher rates of rash, vomiting, and diarrhea, and in HIV-infected individuals, where overall incidence may exceed 20%.
Monitoring recommendations include baseline and periodic liver function tests to detect hepatotoxicity early, electrocardiography (ECG) in patients with cardiac risk factors or on high doses, and vigilant observation for skin rashes or allergic symptoms.
With prolonged use, fluconazole carries risks of reversible adrenal suppression and rare bone marrow suppression, including leukopenia, neutropenia, or thrombocytopenia. Updated pharmacovigilance data as of 2025 highlight a low but persistent risk of arrhythmias, particularly in polypharmacy settings involving other QT-prolonging agents.

Drug interactions

Cytochrome P450 interactions

Fluconazole is a moderate inhibitor of the cytochrome P450 enzymes CYP2C9 and CYP3A4, a strong inhibitor of CYP2C19, and a weak inhibitor of CYP1A2. This inhibition reduces the metabolism of co-administered drugs that are substrates of these enzymes, leading to elevated plasma concentrations and potential toxicity. The effect persists for several days after fluconazole discontinuation due to its long half-life of approximately 30 hours.
Key interactions include those with warfarin, where CYP2C9 inhibition increases its anticoagulant effect, raising the international normalized ratio (INR) and bleeding risk; pharmacokinetic studies show a 2- to 5-fold increase in warfarin area under the curve (AUC). Sulfonylureas such as glipizide, also metabolized by CYP2C9, can cause severe hypoglycemia due to prolonged exposure. Benzodiazepines like midazolam, substrates of CYP3A4, experience delayed clearance and extended sedation. Statins including simvastatin, dependent on CYP3A4, face heightened myopathy risk, with case reports documenting rhabdomyolysis following co-administration. Cyclosporine levels rise via CYP3A4 inhibition, increasing nephrotoxicity potential and necessitating monitoring.
Clinical management involves dose reductions for interacting drugs, such as halving the warfarin dose upon fluconazole initiation, followed by INR monitoring. Therapeutic drug monitoring is recommended for immunosuppressants like cyclosporine, and high-dose fluconazole (>400 mg/day) should be avoided with sensitive CYP3A4 substrates. Fluconazole with ritonavir-containing antivirals like nirmatrelvir/ritonavir may result in synergistic CYP3A4 inhibition; avoidance or close monitoring is advised to prevent excessive drug accumulation.

Other interactions

Fluconazole exhibits pharmacodynamic interactions with other medications that prolong the QT interval, such as antiarrhythmics like amiodarone and certain antifungals including voriconazole, leading to an additive risk of torsades de pointes. Clinical evidence from post-marketing surveillance and case reports indicates that this combination can exacerbate cardiotoxicity, particularly in patients with underlying cardiac conditions or electrolyte imbalances. Similarly, concurrent use with amphotericin B may lead to antagonism in antifungal efficacy; caution is advised due to amphotericin B's nephrotoxicity.
In patients with renal impairment, fluconazole clearance is significantly reduced, necessitating dose adjustments to 50% of the normal dose when creatinine clearance is less than 50 mL/min to prevent accumulation and toxicity. For hepatic disease, fluconazole should be used with caution, as it can contribute to liver injury; regular monitoring of liver function tests is recommended to detect early hepatotoxicity.
Fluconazole has no significant interactions with food, allowing administration with or without meals. However, alcohol consumption may exacerbate the risk of hepatotoxicity when combined with fluconazole, as both can strain liver function. Additionally, fluconazole should be avoided or used cautiously in patients with hypokalemia, as low potassium levels can worsen QT prolongation and increase arrhythmia risk.
Beyond pharmacodynamic effects, fluconazole can increase theophylline serum concentrations by approximately 21% in area under the curve, potentially leading to toxicity; the mechanism remains unclear but warrants monitoring of theophylline levels. Rifampin decreases fluconazole exposure by 23%, which may indirectly heighten the risk of contraceptive failure in patients using oral contraceptives, as rifampin's induction effects counteract fluconazole's tendency to elevate estrogen and progestin levels.
Management of these interactions includes correcting electrolyte imbalances like hypokalemia to mitigate QT risks and considering alternative antifungals when combining with polyenes such as amphotericin B to avoid compounded nephrotoxicity.

Pharmacology

Pharmacodynamics

Fluconazole exerts its antifungal activity by selectively inhibiting the fungal cytochrome P450 enzyme lanosterol 14-demethylase, also known as CYP51 or Erg11p, which is encoded by the ERG11 gene. This inhibition blocks the conversion of lanosterol to ergosterol, an essential component of fungal cell membranes, resulting in the depletion of ergosterol and the accumulation of toxic 14-methyl sterols that disrupt membrane integrity and increase cellular permeability.
The drug is primarily fungistatic against most susceptible fungi, such as Candida species and Cryptococcus neoformans, meaning it inhibits growth rather than killing the organisms outright, though its activity can be concentration-dependent with prolonged post-antifungal effects observed at higher doses.
Fluconazole demonstrates a broad spectrum of activity against various yeasts and some dimorphic fungi, including most Candida species (e.g., C. albicans and C. parapsilosis), Cryptococcus neoformans, Histoplasma capsulatum, and Blastomyces dermatitidis, but it shows reduced efficacy against C. glabrata and no activity against C. krusei, Aspergillus species, or Zygomycetes. Resistance can develop via mutations in ERG11 (encoding CYP51), overexpression of ERG11, or upregulation of efflux pumps (e.g., MDR, CDR genes).
Minimum inhibitory concentrations (MICs) for susceptible Candida isolates typically range from 0.25 to 2 g/mL. Breakpoints (CLSI M60, as of 2020) vary by species; for C. albicans, C. parapsilosis, C. tropicalis: susceptible 2 g/mL, intermediate 4 g/mL, resistant 8 g/mL; for C. glabrata: susceptible dose-dependent 32 g/mL, resistant 64 g/mL.
A key pharmacodynamic advantage is fluconazole's excellent penetration into the cerebrospinal fluid, achieving concentrations of 70-90% of simultaneous serum levels, which supports its utility in treating fungal meningitis.

Pharmacokinetics

Fluconazole is characterized by excellent oral absorption, with a bioavailability exceeding 90% that remains unaffected by food intake or gastric pH. Following oral administration, peak plasma concentrations are typically achieved within 1 to 2 hours, and the pharmacokinetics are comparable between oral and intravenous routes.
The drug distributes widely throughout the body, with a volume of distribution of approximately 0.8 L/kg, consistent with its high water solubility and low plasma protein binding (11-12%). Fluconazole achieves good tissue penetration, including concentrations in cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) that reach about 80% of simultaneous plasma levels, as well as high levels in saliva, sputum, and urine. Metabolism is minimal, with approximately 11% of the dose metabolized primarily via CYP3A4 to minor metabolites (e.g., fluconazole N-oxide and a hydroxylated glucuronide), which are excreted in urine; the majority (>90%) of the drug is eliminated unchanged.
Elimination occurs predominantly via renal excretion, with approximately 80% of the dose recovered unchanged in urine and a mean plasma clearance of 0.23 mL/min/kg. In individuals with normal renal function, the elimination half-life is around 30 hours (range 20-50 hours), supporting once-daily dosing. However, half-life prolongs significantly in renal impairment, reaching up to 98 hours in anuric patients, necessitating dose adjustments for creatinine clearance (CrCl) <50 mL/mintypically reducing the maintenance dose by 50%, with the full dose administered after each hemodialysis session. Pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic indices, such as an area under the curve to minimum inhibitory concentration (AUC/MIC) ratio >25, correlate with therapeutic efficacy in candidemia.
In special populations, clearance is reduced in the elderly (half-life ~46 hours) due to age-related decline in renal function, requiring CrCl-based adjustments. Neonates exhibit prolonged half-life (46-74 hours) from immature renal clearance, with dosing starting at a loading dose of 25 mg/kg followed by 9-12 mg/kg daily, adjusted by gestational age. No major pharmacokinetic differences are noted between sexes.

Chemistry

Structure and properties

Fluconazole, chemically known as 2-(2,4-difluorophenyl)-1,3-bis(1H-1,2,4-triazol-1-yl)propan-2-ol, features a structure with a central tertiary alcohol linked to a 2,4-difluorophenyl group and two 1H-1,2,4-triazole rings, contributing to its antifungal properties through coordination with fungal enzymes. Its molecular formula is C13H12F2N6OC_{13}H_{12}F_{2}N_{6}OC13H12F2N6O, and the molecular weight is 306.27 g/mol.
As a white crystalline powder, fluconazole exhibits a melting point of 138140 C and is moderately hydrophilic with a logP value of 0.4, which facilitates its oral absorption. Fluconazole is slightly soluble in water (approximately 110 mg/mL, pH-dependent) due to the polar triazole rings, though solubility increases in the sodium salt form used for intravenous preparations, allowing concentrations up to 2 mg/mL in formulations. The pKa of the conjugate acid of the triazole is approximately 2.0, indicating that it is a very weak base, which influences its ionization in acidic environments.
Fluconazole demonstrates stability in aqueous solutions across a pH range of 48, as seen in its reconstituted oral suspensions and injectable formulations, where the pH is maintained between 4.0 and 6.5 without significant degradation. In solid form, it is sensitive to light and heat, prompting recommendations for light-protected storage in amber containers to prevent photodegradation during handling or infusion. The sodium salt variant enhances compatibility with intravenous vehicles.
Analytical characterization of fluconazole commonly employs UV spectroscopy, with maximum absorption at 260 nm, allowing for straightforward quantification in pharmaceutical quality control. High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) with UV detection at 210260 nm is standard for purity assessment, separating impurities such as degradation products effectively.
As of 2025, fluconazole's core structure and properties remain unchanged, though its synthesis has gained attention in green chemistry for improved efficiency through eco-friendly chromatographic methods and reduced solvent use in analog production.

Synthesis and formulation

Fluconazole is synthesized through multi-step processes that typically involve the formation of key intermediates from readily available starting materials. One common industrial route begins with the reaction of 1,3-dichloroacetone with 1H-1,2,4-triazole in the presence of a base such as potassium carbonate to form 1,3-bis(1H-1,2,4-triazol-1-yl)acetone. This intermediate then undergoes nucleophilic addition with the Grignard reagent derived from 1-bromo-2,4-difluorobenzene, specifically 2,4-difluorophenylmagnesium bromide, to yield the tertiary alcohol structure of fluconazole after hydrolysis and purification. An alternative multi-step pathway utilizes epichlorohydrin, which is first opened with 1H-1,2,4-triazole to form an epoxypropyl triazole intermediate; this epoxide is then reacted with additional triazole under basic conditions to construct the 1,3-bis-triazolyl propanol framework, incorporating the 2,4-difluorophenyl group via a subsequent Grignard addition. Optimized processes, including continuous flow Grignard reactions, achieve overall yields exceeding 80% by minimizing side reactions and improving selectivity in the addition steps.
The original synthesis was developed by Pfizer in the 1980s, as detailed in their foundational patent US 4,404,216, which described the epoxide ring-opening route with reported yields around 44% in early lab-scale preparations. Following the patent expiry in 2004, generic manufacturers adopted improved routes emphasizing efficient Grignard handling and purification to reduce costs, while maintaining the achiral nature of the final product, which exhibits antifungal activity without requiring stereoselective resolution.
Pharmaceutical formulations of fluconazole are designed for oral and intravenous administration to suit various clinical needs. Oral tablets are available in strengths of 50 mg, 100 mg, 150 mg, and 200 mg, providing immediate-release delivery for systemic absorption. Oral suspensions are formulated at concentrations of 10 mg/mL and 40 mg/mL, often as powder for reconstitution with water, facilitating pediatric or dysphagic patient use. Intravenous solutions are prepared at 2 mg/mL in sterile, preservative-free formulations for hospital settings requiring rapid onset.
Manufacturing considerations ensure product stability and safety across formulations. Intravenous production follows aseptic processing guidelines to maintain sterility, using sodium chloride or dextrose as diluents without bacteriostatic agents. Tablet formulations commonly incorporate excipients such as lactose monohydrate as a diluent, along with microcrystalline cellulose and magnesium stearate for compressibility and lubrication. Reconstituted oral suspensions require refrigeration and should be used within 2 weeks to preserve potency, as beyond this period, microbial growth or degradation may occur.
As of 2025, generic production of fluconazole has surged in Asia, driven by expanding markets in China and India, with annual global output exceeding 3,500 million units predominantly from generic sources. Recent bioequivalence studies have validated new Asian-manufactured generics against reference standards, confirming comparable pharmacokinetics and supporting their approval in international markets.

History and development

Discovery and preclinical studies

Fluconazole was developed by Pfizer in the early 1980s as part of a triazole antifungal research program initiated in 1978. The effort, led by chemist Ken Richardson and his team at Pfizer's Sandwich Research Station in Kent, United Kingdom, initially explored imidazole derivatives but shifted to triazoles to reduce metabolic degradation and enhance pharmacokinetic profiles, such as achieving high blood levels through polar structures. The compound, chemically 2-(2,4-difluorophenyl)-1,3-bis(1H-1,2,4-triazol-1-yl)propan-2-ol, was first synthesized in 1981 as a water-soluble bis-triazole alternative to earlier imidazoles like ketoconazole, which suffered from limited solubility and bioavailability issues. A patent for fluconazole was filed in the United Kingdom in 1981 (GB 2098719A), with the U.S. patent granted in 1983 based on a 1982 filing.
The preclinical rationale centered on creating a broad-spectrum antifungal with improved safety and efficacy over existing agents, particularly for treating superficial and systemic infections caused by opportunistic fungi like Candida species. In vitro evaluations revealed fluconazole's potent activity against Candida albicans and other yeasts, surpassing that of flucytosine due to its selective inhibition of fungal cytochrome P450-dependent 14-demethylase, which disrupts ergosterol biosynthesis in fungal cell membranes. This mechanism offered greater selectivity for fungal over mammalian enzymes, reducing potential hepatotoxicity associated with imidazoles. Structure-activity relationship studies from 1984 to 1986 optimized the difluorophenyl and bis-triazole moieties for balanced lipophilicity, ensuring metabolic stability and oral absorbability without the estrogenic or anti-androgenic side effects seen in prior azoles like ketoconazole, which strongly inhibited steroidogenesis.
Key preclinical studies confirmed fluconazole's promise in animal models. In mice with systemic candidiasis, oral administration demonstrated high efficacy, protecting against lethal infections at low doses, while superficial infection models in normal and immunosuppressed rodents showed comparable activity to intravenous dosing. Toxicity assessments indicated a wide therapeutic index, with oral LD50 values exceeding 1.3 g/kg in rats and 1.4 g/kg in mice, far higher than effective antifungal doses. In rabbits, fluconazole exhibited excellent cerebrospinal fluid penetration, achieving concentrations of 6080% of simultaneous serum levels following intravenous or oral administration, supporting its potential for central nervous system infections. These findings, combined with favorable pharmacokinetics including a long half-life and low protein binding, positioned fluconazole as a leading candidate for clinical advancement.

Clinical trials and approvals

In the late 1980s and early 1990s, fluconazole underwent pivotal phase III clinical trials demonstrating its efficacy in treating serious fungal infections, particularly in immunocompromised patients. A multicenter, randomized trial comparing oral fluconazole (100 mg daily) to ketoconazole (200 mg twice daily) for esophageal candidiasis in HIV-infected patients showed endoscopic cure rates of 91% with fluconazole versus 52% with ketoconazole after 3 weeks of therapy, highlighting fluconazole's superior tolerability and effectiveness. For cryptococcal meningitis in AIDS patients, a randomized comparative trial of high-dose fluconazole (400 mg daily) versus itraconazole (200 mg twice daily) reported clinical response rates of 68% and 70%, respectively, with overall mortality at 5.5% during induction therapy. These trials established fluconazole as a well-tolerated oral alternative to intravenous amphotericin B for systemic infections. Fluconazole came into commercial use in 1988.
Fluconazole received initial U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approval on January 29, 1990, for oral tablets in the treatment of serious systemic Candida infections, including candidemia, disseminated candidiasis, and pneumonia in immunocompromised patients. Approvals were expanded between 1990 and 1992 to include oropharyngeal and esophageal candidiasis, vaginal candidiasis, and prophylaxis against candidiasis in bone marrow transplant recipients. In Europe, the European Medicines Agency (EMA) granted marketing authorization in 1990 for similar indications, facilitating broad adoption across the continent. Globally, fluconazole was included in the World Health Organization (WHO) Model List of Essential Medicines, recognizing its critical role in treating fungal infections in resource-limited settings.
Post-approval developments included the expiry of the original U.S. patent on July 29, 2004, which enabled the widespread availability of generic formulations and significantly reduced costs. In 2024, FDA labeling was updated based on NIH-funded research to include specific dosing recommendations for preterm and term infants, enhancing its use in pediatric prophylaxis against invasive candidiasis. As of 2025, ongoing clinical trials are evaluating fluconazole in combination therapies, such as with flucytosine or novel agents like minocycline, to address antifungal-resistant infections in HIV-associated cryptococcosis and candidemia. Key post-approval data from randomized trials demonstrated fluconazole's non-inferiority to amphotericin B as step-down therapy for candidemia in stable patients with susceptible isolates, a strategy endorsed by the Infectious Diseases Society of America (IDSA) guidelines.
Early clinical trials in AIDS patients with cryptococcal meningitis also spotlighted access controversies, as high costs and patent protections initially limited fluconazole availability in developing countries, where the disease burden was highest; this prompted advocacy efforts and voluntary licensing agreements by the manufacturer to provide the drug at reduced or no cost in low-income regions.

Society and culture

Brand names and generics

Fluconazole is marketed under the primary global brand name Diflucan by Pfizer, which remains the most widely recognized proprietary formulation. Other notable brand names include Forcan by Cipla in India, Fluka by Roche in select markets, and Afungil in Latin American countries such as Mexico and Argentina.
Generic versions of fluconazole became widely available following the expiry of the original US patent held by Pfizer in 2004, enabling multiple manufacturers to produce bioequivalent alternatives. Key generic producers include Teva Pharmaceuticals, Mylan (now part of Viatris), Sandoz (a Novartis division), Aurobindo Pharma, and Sun Pharmaceutical Industries, with over 100 generic formulations distributed globally across various regulatory jurisdictions.
Under the Diflucan brand, fluconazole is available in multiple formulations, including oral tablets (50 mg, 100 mg, 150 mg, and 200 mg), oral suspension (10 mg/mL and 40 mg/mL), and intravenous injection (2 mg/mL), designed for both systemic and localized antifungal therapy. Generic equivalents are required to demonstrate bioequivalence to these branded forms, typically meeting FDA standards with a 90% confidence interval for area under the curve (AUC) and maximum concentration (Cmax) within 80-125% of the reference product.
The introduction of generics has significantly reduced fluconazole's cost, dropping from approximately $10 per dose for branded Diflucan to less than $1 per dose for generics in many markets, thereby improving accessibility in low-income settings and resource-limited regions. As of 2025, generics dominate the market, driven by ongoing entries from manufacturers offering standard and specialized formulations, while branded Diflucan retains a niche for premium or institutional use.

Availability and legal status

Fluconazole is classified as a prescription-only medication in the majority of countries worldwide, requiring a healthcare provider's authorization for purchase and use. However, exceptions exist for specific indications; in the United Kingdom, single-dose fluconazole (150 mg) has been available over-the-counter for the treatment of vaginal candidiasis in women aged 16 and over since December 2017, following approval by the Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA). In the United States, fluconazole remains strictly prescription-only, with no over-the-counter availability approved by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) as of 2025. However, licensed physicians on telemedicine platforms can prescribe fluconazole (Diflucan) for yeast infections, such as vaginal candidiasis, following an online consultation. Services such as Walgreens Virtual Healthcare, PlushCare, and GoodRx Care offer this option, where providers review symptoms and medical history, and issue prescriptions if appropriate, which are sent electronically to pharmacies for pickup or delivery.
Regarding legal status, fluconazole is not designated as a controlled substance under international drug conventions, such as those administered by the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime, due to its low potential for abuse. In India, it is listed under Schedule H of the Drugs and Cosmetics Rules, 1945, mandating a prescription for dispensing. Globally, fluconazole has been included on the World Health Organization (WHO) Model List of Essential Medicines since 1995, specifically for treating priority fungal infections including cryptococcosis in HIV/AIDS patients, candidemia, and other invasive mycoses in immunocompromised individuals.
Pricing and access to fluconazole vary significantly by region, with generic versions enabling low-cost availability in developing countries, where a 150 mg dose typically costs between $0.10 and $0.50 through international procurement mechanisms like the WHO's Global Fund. In sub-Saharan Africa, persistent supply chain disruptions, including procurement delays and stockouts, have challenged access, particularly for HIV-related opportunistic infections, despite improvements from generic competition. Pfizer's Diflucan Partnership Program, launched in 2000, donated millions of doses for cryptococcal meningitis and esophageal candidiasis in resource-limited settings, influencing the development of affordable generics; the program concluded in 2010 as generics became widely available.
Regulatory frameworks support broad availability through generic approvals. The FDA has granted Abbreviated New Drug Application (ANDA) approvals to over 50 generic manufacturers since the original Diflucan patent expiration in 2004, facilitating competition and price reductions. In Europe, the European Medicines Agency (EMA) authorized fluconazole in 1990 via a centralized procedure, with harmonized generics following patent expiry. As of 2025, WHO prequalification includes pediatric oral suspension formulations of fluconazole, ensuring quality-assured supply for children in low- and middle-income countries.
Equity concerns highlight disparities in access; prior to widespread generic entry, branded Diflucan in the United States cost over $100 per dose, burdening uninsured patients, though current generic prices have dropped to under $5 per dose. Fluconazole's inclusion on the WHO Essential Medicines List underscores its critical role in global health equity, particularly for managing HIV-associated cryptococcosis in high-burden regions like sub-Saharan Africa, where it remains a cornerstone of treatment protocols despite ongoing access barriers.
