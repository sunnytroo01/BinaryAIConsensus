Volkswagen emissions scandal

 The Volkswagen emissions scandal, commonly referred to as Dieselgate, centered on the Volkswagen Group's deployment of software-based defeat devices in approximately 11 million diesel vehicles worldwide, enabling these vehicles to detect laboratory emissions testing conditions and activate full pollution controls only during such tests, while otherwise operating in modes that emitted up to 40 times the permitted nitrogen oxide (NOx) levels under real-world driving scenarios.
This deception was uncovered in 2014 through on-road emissions testing conducted by researchers at West Virginia University, who employed portable measurement systems to evaluate light-duty diesel vehicles and identified stark discrepancies between lab-certified performance and highway operation, particularly in Volkswagen models exceeding NOx limits by 15 to 40 times. The findings prompted scrutiny by the California Air Resources Board and the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), culminating in a September 2015 notice of violation accusing Volkswagen of Clean Air Act breaches via undisclosed auxiliary emissions control devices that manipulated engine parameterssuch as steering angle, throttle position, and dynamometer-specific patternsto distinguish testing from normal use.
The scandal implicated diesel engines from Volkswagen, Audi, and Porsche brands, affecting around 590,000 vehicles in the U.S. alone from model years 2009 to 2016, with broader global reach spanning Europe and other markets where regulatory standards varied. Volkswagen admitted the software's existence in late 2015, leading to the resignation of CEO Martin Winterkorn and triggering extensive recalls, vehicle buybacks, and software modifications where feasible, though many units could not be retrofitted to comply without performance trade-offs.
Financial repercussions included criminal penalties exceeding $2.8 billion in the U.S., civil settlements up to $14.7 billion for consumer redress and environmental mitigation, and total costs surpassing $30 billion globally by 2017, encompassing fines, legal fees, and stock value erosion. The episode highlighted engineering trade-offs in diesel technology, where costlier hardware solutions like selective catalytic reduction systems were bypassed in favor of programmable evasion to meet stringent U.S. standards while preserving fuel efficiency and power, ultimately eroding trust in the automotive industry's self-reported compliance data.

Overview

Core Events and Timeline

The Volkswagen emissions scandal emerged from the installation of software-based defeat devices in diesel engines, first deployed in model year 2009 vehicles sold in the United States, enabling the cars to detect emissions testing conditions and reduce nitrogen oxide (NOx) emissions only during those tests. These devices affected 2.0-liter diesel engines in Volkswagen and Audi models from 2009 to 2015, as well as certain Porsche and Audi vehicles with 3.0-liter engines from 2014 to 2016. On-road emissions were measured at 10 to 40 times the permitted NOx levels under U.S. standards.
In early 2013, researchers at West Virginia University's Center for Alternative Fuels, Engines & Emissions (CAF) initiated real-world emissions testing on diesel vehicles, including Volkswagen models, under a grant awarded in 2012. By May 2014, the study revealed significant discrepancies between lab-certified and on-road NOx emissions for Volkswagen diesels, prompting the International Council on Clean Transportation (ICCT) to share findings with regulators. This led the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) and California Air Resources Board (CARB) to investigate, confirming the presence of illegal defeat devices by late 2014.
On September 18, 2015, the EPA issued a Notice of Violation to Volkswagen AG, Audi AG, and Volkswagen Group of America, Inc., alleging Clean Air Act violations for circumventing emissions standards in approximately 482,000 vehicles. Volkswagen initially denied wrongdoing but admitted on September 22, 2015, that the software affected about 11 million vehicles worldwide. The following day, September 23, 2015, CEO Martin Winterkorn resigned, stating he was unaware of the manipulations but accepting responsibility for the lapse.
Subsequent investigations expanded the scope, with CARB issuing a similar notice on September 20, 2015, and further revelations in November 2015 confirming defeat devices in larger 3.0-liter engines. By June 28, 2016, Volkswagen agreed to a partial settlement including buybacks, modifications, and fines exceeding $15 billion for U.S. 2.0-liter vehicles. The scandal prompted global recalls and regulatory probes, ultimately costing Volkswagen over 30 billion euros in fines, settlements, and recalls.

Scope of Affected Vehicles and Emissions Discrepancy

The Volkswagen emissions scandal involved defeat devices installed in approximately 11 million diesel vehicles worldwide, enabling the software to detect laboratory testing conditions and adjust engine parameters to artificially reduce nitrogen oxide (NOx) emissions during certification tests. This figure encompassed vehicles sold primarily in Europe (about 8 million) and the United States (nearly 500,000), with additional impacts in other markets including Canada, Australia, and Asia. The affected models spanned Volkswagen, Audi, and Porsche brands, focusing on turbocharged direct injection (TDI) diesel engines.
In the United States, the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) identified violations in roughly 482,000 light-duty 2.0-liter TDI vehicles from model years 2009 to 2015, including the Volkswagen Jetta, Golf, Beetle, and Passat, as well as the Audi A3 TDI. Separate issues affected heavier 3.0-liter V6 TDI engines in approximately 107,000 vehicles, such as the Volkswagen Touareg (2009-2016), Audi A6, A7, A8, Q5, and Q7 (2014-2016), and Porsche Cayenne (2013-2016). These encompassed passenger cars and SUVs marketed as "clean diesel" for their purported fuel efficiency and low emissions.
The emissions discrepancy centered on NOx, a key pollutant regulated under standards like the U.S. Tier 2 Bin 5 limit of 0.043 grams per mile and Euro 5/6 equivalents. In controlled tests, affected vehicles complied with these limits, but real-world on-road testing by West Virginia University and the International Council on Clean Transportation revealed NOx outputs 10 to 40 times higheroften exceeding 1 gram per miledue to the defeat device deactivating full emissions controls outside test cycles. This gap stemmed from the software's cycle-recognition algorithm, which optimized performance and emissions reduction only when dynamometer conditions (specific speeds, loads, and durations) mimicked official protocols, otherwise prioritizing power over pollution control. Independent verification post-scandal confirmed these vehicles failed to achieve compliant NOx levels without the cheat, contributing to excess pollution estimated at hundreds of thousands of tons annually across affected fleets.

Technical and Regulatory Context

Advantages and Engineering of Diesel Engines

Diesel engines function through compression ignition, in which intake air is compressed to elevate its temperature and pressure, prompting auto-ignition upon direct injection of fuel without requiring spark plugs, unlike spark-ignition gasoline engines that compress a premixed air-fuel mixture before ignition. This process adheres to the Diesel thermodynamic cycle, characterized by isentropic compression, constant-pressure heat addition during fuel injection, isentropic expansion, and constant-volume heat rejection. Typical compression ratios in diesel engines range from 14:1 to 25:1, far exceeding the 8:1 to 12:1 ratios in gasoline Otto-cycle engines, which permits more efficient conversion of fuel energy to mechanical work by extracting greater expansion from combustion gases.
The elevated compression ratios and leaner air-fuel mixtures in diesel combustion yield thermal efficiencies of 30% to 45%, surpassing the 20% to 35% of gasoline engines and translating to 20% to 35% superior fuel economy under real-world conditions. Contributing to this is the higher energy density of diesel fuel, which delivers about 15% more energy per unit volume than gasoline, enabling extended range per tank. Diesel engines also generate substantially higher torque output, especially at low engine speeds, owing to the sustained high-pressure combustion phase and inherent design for heavier loads, which enhances drivability in vehicles equipped with turbochargers to boost air intake and power without proportional fuel increases.
Modern diesel engineering incorporates high-pressure common-rail fuel injection systems, operating at up to 2,000 bar to atomize fuel precisely for complete combustion and reduced soot, alongside turbocharging or supercharging to improve volumetric efficiency and offset the naturally lower power density from lean operation. These features underpin diesel's advantages in longevity and load-bearing capacity, as the absence of throttling losses and robust constructionnecessitated by extreme cylinder pressuresallow operation at lower RPMs with less wear compared to gasoline counterparts. In passenger vehicle applications, such as those pursued by Volkswagen for European and North American markets, diesel powertrains provided a pathway to comply with fuel efficiency mandates like the U.S. Corporate Average Fuel Economy standards through inherent economy gains over gasoline alternatives.

NOx Emissions Control Technologies

Diesel engines produce elevated NOx emissions due to high-temperature combustion in lean (excess oxygen) conditions, necessitating specialized control strategies distinct from stoichiometric gasoline engines. NOx control primarily involves in-cylinder techniques to suppress formation and exhaust aftertreatment to convert NOx post-combustion. These methods achieve compliance with stringent standards like the U.S. EPA Tier 2 Bin 5 limit of 0.07 g/mi NOx for light-duty diesels, though effectiveness varies with operating conditions, fuel quality, and system integration.
Exhaust gas recirculation (EGR) serves as the principal in-cylinder NOx mitigation approach, recirculating a portion of exhaust gas into the intake to dilute the air-fuel mixture, thereby reducing combustion temperatures and oxygen concentration, which inhibits NOx formation. High-pressure EGR loops exhaust from after the turbocharger back to the intake manifold, while low-pressure systems route from downstream of aftertreatment; cooled EGR variants further lower intake temperatures for enhanced efficacy. EGR can reduce engine-out NOx by 40-90%, but it concomitantly elevates particulate matter (PM), hydrocarbons (HC), and carbon monoxide (CO) emissions, while potentially increasing fuel consumption and requiring precise control to avoid excessive soot buildup.
Selective catalytic reduction (SCR) represents a dominant aftertreatment technology, injecting ammonia precursors like urea (decomposing to NH3) into the exhaust upstream of a catalyst, where NOx reacts to form nitrogen and water via reactions such as 4NH3 + 4NO + O2 4N2 + 6H2O. Vanadium-based or zeolite catalysts operate effectively across broad temperature windows (200-500C), achieving NOx conversion efficiencies up to 90-95% under optimal conditions, particularly when paired with upstream diesel oxidation catalysts to optimize NO2/NO ratios. In light-duty diesel passenger cars, SCR enables compliance with ultra-low NOx mandates but requires infrastructure for diesel exhaust fluid (DEF) refilling and controls to prevent ammonia slip or secondary emissions like ammonium salts at low temperatures.
Lean NOx traps (LNT), also known as NOx adsorbers, temporarily store NOx on alkali earth metal sites during lean operation, followed by periodic rich exhaust pulses (every 1-2 minutes) that desorb and reduce NOx over precious metal catalysts akin to three-way converters. LNT systems deliver 70-90% NOx reduction in light-duty applications, suitable for Euro 5/6 and U.S. Tier 2 standards, but suffer from sulfur poisoning necessitating desulfation cycles and thermal limitations above 400-500C, restricting heavy-duty use. Often combined with EGR for engine-out NOx preconditioning or SCR for cold-start augmentation, LNT declines in standalone favor due to real-driving emission challenges and higher operational complexity.
Integrated systems combining EGR with SCR or LNT optimize the NOx-brake specific fuel consumption tradeoff, allowing higher engine-out NOx for efficiency gains while ensuring tailpipe compliance; for instance, moderate EGR reduces NOx sufficiently for aftertreatment sizing without excessive PM penalties. Such synergies proved essential for diesel passenger cars targeting markets with disparate standards, though real-world variability in temperature, load, and sulfur content can degrade performance absent robust calibration.

Disparities Between US and EU Standards

The United States Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) imposed stringent Tier 2 emissions standards for light-duty vehicles starting in model year 2004, requiring nitrogen oxides (NOx) emissions not to exceed 0.07 grams per mile (g/mi) for diesel passenger cars under Bin 5 certification, a common compliance bin for higher-emitting vehicles. In comparison, the European Union's Euro 5 standards, applicable to new diesel vehicles from September 2009, permitted NOx up to 180 milligrams per kilometer (mg/km), equivalent to approximately 0.29 g/mi after unit conversion. Euro 6, introduced in September 2014, tightened this to 80 mg/km or about 0.13 g/mi, still less restrictive than the US limit.
These disparities meant US regulations demanded more effective NOx aftertreatment, such as full-time selective catalytic reduction (SCR) systems with urea (AdBlue) injection to achieve near-zero tailpipe NOx, whereas EU standards allowed reliance on less efficient lean NOx traps (LNT) or partial SCR, which sufficed for lab compliance but faltered in real-world conditions. Volkswagen's EA189 diesel engines, targeted for both markets, employed defeat devicessoftware detecting lab testingto suppress emissions temporarily, but the cheat's necessity was amplified in the US due to the tighter NOx cap, where compliant real-world operation would have required costlier, urea-intensive SCR without shortcuts.
EU testing under the New European Driving Cycle (NEDC) was criticized for its predictability and leniency, enabling manufacturers to optimize for lab artifacts rather than robust on-road performance, a vulnerability less exploited in the US Federal Test Procedure (FTP-75) combined with stricter limits. Post-scandal revelations indicated Volkswagen also manipulated EU-market vehicles, yet exceedances relative to Euro limits were smaller (often 4-10 times on-road vs. US's 15-40 times), delaying widespread detection until International Council on Clean Transportation (ICCT) on-road testing in 2013-2014 highlighted global discrepancies, with US-focused scrutiny by West Virginia University exposing the full scale. The US's rigorous certification, including onboard diagnostics mandating continuous monitoring, contrasted with Europe's initial absence of real-driving emissions (RDE) requirements until 2017, underscoring regulatory gaps that permitted higher baseline pollution in Europe.

Development and Deployment of Defeat Devices

Origins in VW's Engineering Decisions

In the early 2000s, Volkswagen sought to expand its diesel passenger car sales in the United States, leveraging the fuel efficiency advantages of diesel engines to improve corporate average fuel economy (CAFE) compliance and compete with gasoline vehicles, but faced stringent NOx emission limits under EPA Tier 2 standards (0.043 g/mi for Bin 5 certification). Diesel engines inherently produce higher NOx due to high combustion temperatures, requiring advanced aftertreatment; VW engineers opted for exhaust gas recirculation (EGR) combined with lean NOx traps (LNT) on its 2.0-liter EA189 TDI engines rather than selective catalytic reduction (SCR) systems, which inject urea-based diesel exhaust fluid (DEF) to chemically reduce NOx. 
SCR was rejected primarily due to added costs, space constraints for the urea tank in compact vehicles, licensing requirements from Mercedes-Benz (which held key patents), and customer maintenance burdens from periodic DEF refills, which could undermine the marketed simplicity of "clean diesel" technology. LNT systems, which store and periodically regenerate NOx by richening the exhaust, proved insufficient for sustained real-world compliance without frequent regenerations that degraded fuel economy and performancetrade-offs unacceptable for VW's performance-oriented tuning goals. To bridge this gap, engineers tuned engines for optimal power, torque, and efficiency in normal operation (yielding up to 40 times the permitted NOx), then developed software to detect laboratory testing conditions via parameters like steering angle, acceleration patterns, and dynamometer operation. 
The defeat device software originated from an Audi-developed "acoustic function" introduced in 1999 for the 3.0-liter V6 diesel, which used pilot fuel injections to quiet engine noise during steady-state operation but inadvertently raised emissions; this code was repurposed and refined by VW starting in 2006 for the EA189 engine family to enable test-mode activation of aggressive EGR and other controls, ensuring certification while preserving road performance. This adaptation was approved internally amid pressure to launch U.S.-spec diesels by the 2009 model year without hardware overhauls, reflecting a prioritization of market competitiveness over full-cycle compliance. Subsequent generations, including the 3.0-liter V6 TDI, incorporated similar cycle-beating logic, with software switching to "dyno mode" upon recognizing EPA or California Air Resources Board (CARB) test protocols.

Mechanism and Functionality of the Software

The defeat device consisted of software embedded in the engine control unit (ECU) of affected Volkswagen diesel vehicles, which detected laboratory emissions testing conditions and selectively activated emissions reduction measures only during those tests. This software monitored real-time vehicle parameters, including steering wheel angle, vehicle speed profiles, throttle position, distance traveled, and time since engine start, to identify patterns matching standardized test cycles such as the U.S. FTP-75 urban dynamometer driving schedule. When these conditions alignedtypically involving fixed steering (no wheel movement on a dynamometer), consistent low-speed accelerations, and closed throttle periods indicative of lab testingthe ECU switched to a compliance mode that optimized nitrogen oxide (NOx) controls.
In compliance mode, the software commanded higher exhaust gas recirculation (EGR) rates to dilute combustion temperatures and reduce NOx formation, alongside adjustments to fuel injection timing, air-fuel ratios, andwhere equippedurea dosing in selective catalytic reduction (SCR) systems to enhance NOx conversion efficiency. These interventions ensured NOx output remained below regulatory thresholds during the approximately 20-minute test duration, but at the expense of reduced engine efficiency, lower power output, and increased fuel consumption compared to unrestricted operation. Upon detecting a deviation from test conditionssuch as steering input or speed variations outside the cyclethe software deactivated or relaxed these controls within seconds, reverting to a normal driving mode that prioritized fuel economy and performance by minimizing EGR and allowing higher NOx emissions, often 10 to 40 times the certified limits.
The code supported recognition of multiple test profiles, up to 10 in some implementations, and was disguised in diagnostic logs under misleading labels like "acoustic condition" to evade scrutiny during routine maintenance or calibration reviews. This functionality rendered the emissions controls ineffective under real-world conditions reasonably expected during normal vehicle operation, violating U.S. Clean Air Act definitions of an auxiliary emission control device (AECD) as a prohibited defeat device. Independent reverse-engineering of ECU firmware from models like the Jetta, Golf, and Passat confirmed the presence of these circumvention algorithms in approximately 400 out of 900 analyzed software versions, affecting 2.0-liter and 3.0-liter diesel engines from model years 2009 to 2015.

Early Internal Warnings and Oversight Failures

In 2006, as Volkswagen developed a new diesel engine for the U.S. market, engineers identified challenges in meeting the stricter 2007 emissions standards without compromising vehicle performance and fuel efficiency. To address this, they adapted software originally from Audiexplicitly marked as unsuitable for U.S. usethat functioned as a defeat device, activating full emissions controls only during laboratory testing while deactivating them under normal driving conditions. Internal objections were raised by engineers regarding the legality and ethical implications of this approach, but supervisors overruled them, opting to proceed with production and issuing instructions to avoid detection.
Similar concerns persisted into 2007, with engineers again highlighting risks associated with the software, yet management decisions prioritized market expansion and cost avoidanceeschewing more compliant but complex technologies like selective catalytic reduction (SCR) systems used by competitorsleading to the routinization of the defeat device. By 2011, at least one engineer explicitly warned company leadership about "possibly illegal" practices tied to emissions testing evasion, a caution that was disregarded amid ongoing pressure to achieve regulatory compliance without additional hardware costs. Suppliers, including Bosch, which provided foundational software elements, also issued warnings about potential misuse, further underscoring early awareness of improprieties that were not acted upon.
These episodes reflected broader oversight failures rooted in a corporate culture tolerant of rule-bending, where procedural lapses enabled misconduct rather than isolated rogue actions. Volkswagen's subsequent internal review, led by chairman Hans Dieter Ptsch, attributed the scandal to a "whole chain" of systemic errors originating as early as 2005, including a mindset that accommodated deviations from legal standards and inadequate checks on engineering decisions driven by aggressive U.S. market goals. Management's emphasis on rapid diesel rolloutwithout sufficient ethical or compliance scrutinyexacerbated these issues, as evidenced by later admissions that top executives bore ultimate responsibility despite limited direct involvement.

Discovery and Initial Exposure

ICCT Independent Testing (2013-2014)

!
In 2013, the International Council on Clean Transportation (ICCT), a nonprofit research organization focused on reducing vehicle emissions, commissioned the West Virginia University Center for Alternative Fuels, Engines, and Emissions (WVU CAFEE) to perform independent on-road testing of light-duty diesel passenger vehicles available in the United States. The study aimed to assess real-world emissions performance using portable emissions measurement systems (PEMS), which measure pollutants like nitrogen oxides (NOx) directly from the tailpipe during actual driving on highways and urban routes, in contrast to controlled laboratory dynamometer tests used for regulatory certification.
The testing targeted three vehicles: a 2012 Volkswagen Jetta TDI, a 2013 Volkswagen Passat TDI, and a 2012 BMW X5 diesel equipped with selective catalytic reduction (SCR) as a comparative model compliant with U.S. standards. Results indicated that the Volkswagen vehicles exhibited NOx emissions far exceeding U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) limits under on-road conditions; specifically, the Jetta produced NOx levels 15 to 35 times above the standard during highway cycles, while the Passat showed emissions up to 20 times the limit, depending on the test route and conditions. In contrast, the BMW X5's emissions remained within regulatory bounds, suggesting effective aftertreatment under real-world operation.
The ICCT released the report titled "In-use emissions testing of light-duty diesel vehicles in the United States" in May 2014, documenting these discrepancies and attributing them to potential shortcomings in emissions control systems during non-standard driving. Preliminary findings were promptly shared with the EPA and California Air Resources Board (CARB) in early 2014, prompting confirmatory laboratory tests by regulators that replicated the lab-on-road gaps and escalated investigations into possible defeat devicessoftware algorithms designed to detect and alter engine performance during testing. This independent verification provided critical empirical evidence challenging Volkswagen's claims of "clean diesel" compliance, marking a pivotal step toward exposing systematic emissions manipulation.

EPA Notice of Violation and Regulatory Escalation (2015)

On September 18, 2015, the United States Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) issued a Notice of Violation (NOV) under the Clean Air Act to Volkswagen AG, Audi AG, and Volkswagen Group of America, Inc., alleging that the companies had installed defeat devices in approximately 482,000 light-duty diesel vehicles equipped with 2.0-liter engines from model years 2009 to 2015. The affected models included Volkswagen Jetta, Golf, Passat, and Beetle sedans, as well as Audi A3 sedans, which emitted up to 40 times the permitted levels of nitrogen oxides (NOx) during real-world driving conditions due to software that detected emissions testing cycles and temporarily optimized performance to pass lab tests while disabling full emissions controls otherwise. This action was coordinated with the California Air Resources Board (CARB), which simultaneously notified Volkswagen of parallel violations under California's emissions standards, marking the first joint federal-state enforcement step in what became a multi-agency probe.
The NOV charged Volkswagen with multiple Clean Air Act prohibitions, including manufacturing, selling, and installing parts designed to bypass emissions requirements, as well as failing to disclose the defeat devices during certification processes. It halted sales of the non-compliant models and exposed Volkswagen to civil penalties potentially exceeding $37,500 per vehicle, totaling billions in fines, alongside requirements for vehicle recalls or fixes. In response, Volkswagen acknowledged irregularities in its software on September 22, 2015, but initially resisted full disclosure, prompting EPA threats on September 3prior to the NOVto withhold certification for 2016 model-year diesels unless compliance was demonstrated.
Regulatory escalation intensified in late 2015 with the EPA's second NOV on November 2, targeting approximately 85,000 3.0-liter diesel vehicles (model years 2009-2015) in models like the Volkswagen Touareg, Audi Q7, Q5, A6, A8, and Porsche Cayenne, which employed similar defeat mechanisms for higher NOx emissions. This expanded the scope to over 567,000 vehicles overall, drawing in the Department of Justice for civil and potential criminal inquiries, while CARB pursued state-level enforcement, culminating in coordinated demands for remediation plans and amplifying international scrutiny under stricter U.S. standards compared to Europe. The actions underscored systemic failures in self-certification oversight, with EPA and CARB emphasizing that on-road emissions far exceeded lab results, validating independent testing discrepancies.

Role of Intelligence Agencies in Detection

The initial detection of Volkswagen's defeat devices in diesel engines was achieved through independent emissions testing commissioned by the International Council on Clean Transportation (ICCT), conducted by researchers at West Virginia University between May 2013 and early 2014, which revealed NOx emissions up to 40 times higher than certified levels during real-world driving. This prompted regulatory scrutiny by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) and California Air Resources Board (CARB), culminating in a notice of violation issued to Volkswagen on September 18, 2015. No public records or official investigations attribute any role to intelligence agencies, such as the CIA or NSA, in identifying the software manipulation.
Speculation about intelligence agency involvement has occasionally surfaced in commentary on automotive software vulnerabilities, with experts noting that agencies like the NSA have capabilities to detect anomalous code in vehicles, akin to broader cybersecurity practices. However, such discussions remain hypothetical and unlinked to the empirical evidence from ICCT testing or EPA audits, which relied on on-road and dynamometer measurements rather than signals intelligence or covert operations. Post-exposure criminal probes involved the FBI in arrests, such as that of Volkswagen engineer Oliver Schmidt on January 7, 2017, but these occurred after public disclosure and focused on enforcement, not initial detection.

Volkswagen's Immediate Responses

Admissions and Executive Actions (2015)

On September 3, 2015, Volkswagen disclosed to the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) that it had installed software in its 2.0-liter diesel vehicles sold in the United States from model years 2009 to 2015, designed to detect when the cars were undergoing emissions testing and adjust engine performance to meet standards only under those conditions. This admission followed internal discussions and came amid escalating regulatory pressure after independent testing revealed discrepancies.
The EPA issued a public Notice of Violation on September 18, 2015, accusing Volkswagen of installing defeat devices in approximately 482,000 vehicles, circumventing Clean Air Act requirements by falsifying NOx emissions data during official tests while allowing up to 40 times higher emissions in real-world driving. Volkswagen confirmed the use of such software on September 22, 2015, stating that around 11 million diesel vehicles worldwide were equipped with the manipulative programming, primarily affecting 2.0- and 3.0-liter engines. In a video statement that day, CEO Martin Winterkorn expressed being "endlessly sorry" for the breach of trust but maintained he was unaware of the specifics.
Winterkorn resigned as CEO on September 23, 2015, citing the need to enable a "fresh start" for the company amid the crisis, while reiterating his shock at the events and denying personal knowledge of the wrongdoing. His departure was framed as taking responsibility for the scandal's impact, though he emphasized his commitment to Volkswagen's customers and employees. The supervisory board accepted the resignation and appointed Matthias Mller, previously head of the Audi brand, as interim CEO to lead remediation efforts. These actions marked Volkswagen's initial executive response, prioritizing leadership transition to address the fallout from the admitted emissions manipulations.

Scope Expansion to CO2 and Larger Engines

On November 2, 2015, the United States Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) issued a notice of violation determining that engine management software in Volkswagen vehicles equipped with 3.0-liter V6 TDI diesel engines contained illegal defeat devices designed to evade NOx emissions standards during testing. These engines powered higher-end models including the Volkswagen Touareg, Audi A6, A7, A8, Q5, Q7, and Porsche Cayenne, spanning model years 2009 through 2016 and affecting roughly 95,000 vehicles in the US market. By late November 2015, Volkswagen informed the EPA that the software manipulation extended across these model years, confirming the presence of cycle-beating algorithms similar to those in the smaller 2.0-liter engines. The revelation broadened the scandal's scope from compact and mid-size passenger cars to luxury SUVs and sedans, prompting additional regulatory scrutiny from the California Air Resources Board and international authorities.
Concurrently in November 2015, Volkswagen self-reported irregularities in CO2 emissions certification tests affecting approximately 800,000 diesel and petrol vehicles sold mainly in Europe, distinct from the NOx defeat devices as they involved procedural manipulations rather than real-time software detection of test conditions. These discrepancies stemmed from overstated fuel efficiency claims achieved through tactics such as under-declaring vehicle curb weight, using non-standard tires for lower rolling resistance, or incorrect aerodynamic measurements during type-approval processes under the New European Driving Cycle (NEDC). Initial internal audits estimated excess CO2 emissions ranging from 10 to 40 grams per kilometer per vehicle, potentially exposing Volkswagen to fines of up to 0.095 per gram under EU regulations, with total liabilities projected in the billions of euros. The disclosure followed heightened oversight post-NOx revelations, as Volkswagen cooperated with Germany's Kraftfahrt-Bundesamt (KBA) to verify test data across its lineup.
Subsequent independent verifications by KBA in early 2016 confirmed systematic deviations in reported CO2 values for affected models, though Volkswagen revised its estimates downward to around 36,000 vehicles by March 2016 after re-testing, attributing the reduction to refined methodologies excluding minor procedural variances. This phase highlighted vulnerabilities in Europe's less stringent type-approval regime compared to US on-road testing, where CO2 manipulations did not trigger equivalent defeat-device prohibitions but still violated consumer protection laws on fuel economy labeling. By mid-2016, the CO2 probe contributed to broader corporate governance reforms at Volkswagen, including enhanced compliance monitoring for all emissions-related certifications.

Initial Recall Plans and Communication Strategies

On September 20, 2015, Volkswagen CEO Martin Winterkorn issued a statement acknowledging that the company's U.S.-sold diesel vehicles violated environmental standards due to unauthorized software adjustments, and he ordered an external investigation while apologizing for breaching public trust. Two days later, on September 22, the company publicly admitted installing defeat devices in approximately 11 million diesel vehicles worldwide, primarily those with EA189 engines, to manipulate NOx emissions tests. Winterkorn followed with another apology video, pledging to "get to the bottom" of the issue and attributing the misconduct to individual actions rather than systemic policy, a framing later contradicted by evidence of broader internal knowledge.
Initial recall plans centered on software reprogramming to align real-world emissions with regulatory limits without hardware modifications, targeting nearly 500,000 2.0-liter diesel vehicles in the U.S. and millions more in Europe. Volkswagen proposed voluntary recalls in Europe, including 8.5 million vehicles, but German authorities rejected these on October 15, 2015, deeming the fixes insufficient to fully eliminate the cheat mechanisms, which prompted plans for mandatory interventions and further testing. In the U.S., the EPA mandated a recall of the affected vehicles by late September 2015, with Volkswagen committing to customer notifications and service center updates starting in early 2016 under new CEO Matthias Mller, who assumed the role after Winterkorn's resignation on September 23 amid the unfolding crisis.
Communication efforts emphasized transparency and remediation, including halting social media marketing on September 18, 2015, to avoid amplifying the scandal, alongside press releases detailing the software fix timelines and a "goodwill" compensation program offering affected U.S. owners $1,000 in vouchers and service perks as an interim measure before full recalls. However, the strategy faced criticism for initially downplaying executive involvementMller described the issue as isolated rogue engineeringand for delays in owner notifications, which regulators like the EPA scrutinized for adequacy in ensuring compliance. These approaches aligned legal defenses with public messaging, prioritizing admission of technical irregularities over broader accountability to mitigate reputational damage.

Remediation and Legal Proceedings

Vehicle Fixes, Buybacks, and Compensation

In the United States, the primary remediation for affected 2.0-liter diesel vehicles involved a $14.7 billion settlement announced on June 28, 2016, covering approximately 475,000 model year 2009-2015 Volkswagen and Audi vehicles equipped with defeat devices. This agreement mandated buybacks or lease terminations for "Generation 1" vehicles (pre-2015 models), as engineering assessments determined that hardware retrofits could not achieve U.S. NOx standards without substantial performance degradation or reliance on non-compliant modes. Owners opting for buybacks received the vehicle's appraised pre-scandal value plus compensation ranging from $5,100 to $10,000, with final settlement approval granted by U.S. District Judge Charles Breyer on October 25, 2016. By March 2018, Volkswagen had repurchased about 335,000 such vehicles, equating to roughly 71% of the eligible U.S. fleet, with the remainder processed through similar channels.
For "Generation 2" 2.0-liter models (2013-2015), eligible owners could choose fixes instead of buybacks, involving EPA- and CARB-approved software recalibrations to eliminate defeat device functionality and optimize emissions controls. These updates adjusted engine mapping, exhaust gas recirculation, and selective catalytic reduction systems, often supplemented by hardware additions like a downstream NOx sensor for real-time closed-loop monitoring, enabling compliance during on-road operation without the prior test-mode cheating. Independent testing post-fix confirmed NOx reductions to legal limits, though some vehicles experienced minor power losses of 5-10 horsepower and reduced fuel efficiency. A separate $1.2 billion settlement in February 2017 addressed 3.0-liter diesel vehicles (about 83,000 units, including Volkswagen Touareg and Audi Q7 models from 2009-2016), offering buybacks, fixes via software and hardware upgrades (e.g., enhanced particulate filters and oxidation catalysts), or cash payments up to $16,114.
Internationally, remediation emphasized fixes over buybacks due to less stringent NOx standards, with Volkswagen deploying software updates across millions of European vehicles starting in 2016 to recalibrate injection timing and urea dosing in SCR systems. Compensation varied by jurisdiction; for instance, a May 2024 settlement in Italy provided up to 1,100 per owner for over 60,000 affected vehicles, totaling 54 million, focusing on consumer protection claims rather than mass buybacks. In Germany, a January 2024 court ruling rejected software-only fixes for certain models, mandating costlier hardware retrofits to ensure verifiable compliance, highlighting ongoing disputes over fix efficacy. These measures, while addressing legal NOx exceedances, did not retroactively compensate for prior excess emissions' environmental impacts, with U.S. funds partly allocated to mitigation projects like electric vehicle infrastructure.

US Settlements and Fines

In June 2016, Volkswagen agreed to a partial settlement with U.S. regulators and consumers addressing the sale of approximately 475,000 2.0-liter diesel vehicles equipped with defeat devices, committing up to $14.7 billion in total remedies. This included $10.03 billion for vehicle buybacks, lease terminations, and cash payments to owners (up to $10,000 per vehicle plus trade-in value adjustments), alongside $2 billion for environmental mitigation projects to offset excess nitrogen oxide (NOx) emissions and $2.7 billion to promote zero-emission vehicle infrastructure. The settlement received preliminary approval in July 2016 and final court approval on October 25, 2016, by U.S. District Judge Charles Breyer in the Northern District of California.
For the affected 3.0-liter diesel vehicles (about 83,000 units), Volkswagen reached a separate settlement in December 2016, requiring fixes or buybacks, a $225 million fund for nationwide NOx reduction projects, and $603 million in consumer restitution. The Federal Trade Commission enforced consumer protections under this agreement, mandating full compensation for deceptive "clean diesel" marketing, with Volkswagen and related entities ultimately repaying over $9.5 billion to U.S. car buyers by July 2020.
Government penalties included a $2.8 billion criminal fine following Volkswagen's guilty plea on January 11, 2017, to three felony counts: conspiracy to defraud the U.S., obstruction of justice, and Clean Air Act violations. This plea, accepted by a Michigan federal court on April 21, 2017, formed part of a broader $4.3 billion package that also incorporated a $1.5 billion civil penalty. Additionally, Volkswagen paid $1.45 billion in civil penalties specifically for Clean Air Act breaches, distributed among federal, state, and tribal authorities. These U.S. actions, enforced by the Department of Justice, Environmental Protection Agency, and California Air Resources Board, totaled approximately $20 billion in fines, settlements, and remediation by 2017, with no major additional U.S. penalties reported through 2025.

European Investigations and Outcomes

Following the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency's notice of violation in September 2015, Germany's Federal Motor Transport Authority (KBA) launched an investigation into Volkswagen's diesel engines, focusing on the EA189 type used in approximately 2 million vehicles in Germany and 8.5 million across Europe. The KBA verified that software updates could mitigate excess NOx emissions to comply with Euro 5 and Euro 6 standards, approving these fixes in June 2016 without requiring hardware changes or widespread buybacks, unlike in the United States. These updates involved reprogramming engine control units to adjust injection timing and exhaust gas recirculation, though independent tests later indicated potential trade-offs in fuel economy and performance.
German public prosecutors in Braunschweig initiated a criminal probe in October 2015, raiding Volkswagen's headquarters and examining executive involvement in tolerating fraudulent type-approval certificates. This culminated in a 1 billion fine against Volkswagen AG in June 2018 for market manipulation related to the emissions cheating, which the company accepted without legal challenge, effectively closing the administrative proceedings. Subsidiary Audi AG faced an 800 million penalty in October 2018 for similar violations in its diesel models, while supplier Bosch was fined 90 million in May 2019 for developing and providing the defeat device software used across Volkswagen Group brands. These fines, totaling over 1.8 billion for the Volkswagen Group in Germany, represented the primary regulatory financial penalties in Europe, significantly lower per vehicle than U.S. equivalents given the scale of affected cars.
At the European Union level, direct enforcement for the defeat devices remained fragmented across member states, with the European Commission prioritizing antitrust scrutiny over emissions fraud. In July 2021, the Commission imposed 875 million in fines on Volkswagen, BMW, and Daimler (now Mercedes-Benz Group) for colluding from 2006 to 2014 to avoid competition in developing selective catalytic reduction systems for NOx control, delaying cleaner technology adoption. This action addressed systemic delays in emissions tech but did not directly penalize the software manipulation, highlighting enforcement gaps in EU-wide vehicle certification.
National consumer protection efforts yielded civil settlements rather than regulatory mandates, such as Volkswagen provisioning 830 million in 2019 for claims from hundreds of thousands of German diesel owners, with 620 million disbursed by April 2020 following court rulings affirming rights to compensation for deceived purchases. Similar out-of-court agreements occurred elsewhere, including 193 million for 91,000 UK claimants in May 2022 and 50 million for 60,000 Italian owners in May 2024, reflecting piecemeal remediation amid varying legal standards across Europe. Overall, European outcomes emphasized prosecutorial fines and software fixes over comprehensive vehicle replacements, constrained by less stringent NOx limits under Euro standards compared to U.S. EPA requirements.

Recent Criminal Convictions (Up to 2025)

On May 26, 2025, the Braunschweig Regional Court in Germany convicted four former Volkswagen executives of fraud for their roles in manipulating diesel emissions tests during the scandal. The court determined that the managers had concealed excess nitrogen oxide emissions from regulators, enabling the sale of non-compliant vehicles and constituting particularly serious fraud. Two of the executives received prison sentences, with one, former engineer Hanno Jelden, sentenced to two years and seven months for aggravated fraud linked to vehicle sales. The other sentences included suspended terms, marking the first criminal convictions of senior Volkswagen personnel in the emissions affair after nearly a decade of investigations.
These convictions stemmed from a trial examining the deployment of defeat devices in Volkswagen's EA189 diesel engines, which allowed vehicles to pass regulatory tests while emitting up to 40 times the permitted NOx levels in real-world driving. Prosecutors argued the executives knowingly participated in the deception, prioritizing sales targets over compliance despite internal awareness of the software's illegality as early as 2006. The defendants had pleaded not guilty, claiming limited involvement, but the court rejected these defenses based on evidence of coordinated efforts across engineering and management levels.
Separately, the high-profile trial of former Volkswagen CEO Martin Winterkorn, accused of fraud, false advertising, and market manipulation for allegedly approving the cheating software and misleading stakeholders, began on September 3, 2024, but was suspended on July 1, 2025, due to his deteriorating health. Winterkorn, indicted in both Germany and the United States, faces potential penalties including up to 10 years in prison if convicted, though proceedings remain halted as of October 2025. No additional criminal convictions of Volkswagen executives occurred in 2023 or 2024 in major jurisdictions, with prior U.S. cases, such as the 2017 sentencing of engineer Oliver Schmidt to seven years for Clean Air Act violations, predating the "recent" timeframe.

Consequences

Financial and Corporate Impacts on VW

The revelation of the emissions cheating on September 18, 2015, triggered an immediate plunge in Volkswagen's stock price, with shares dropping approximately 32% within a week, erasing around 16 billion to 27 billion in market capitalization. This initial shareholder value loss reflected investor reactions to the scandal's scope, affecting roughly 11 million vehicles worldwide, and contributed to lasting damage to the company's brand reputation. 
Volkswagen initially provisioned 6.7 billion in October 2015 to cover recall and other costs, but cumulative expenses escalated dramatically. By March 2020, the company reported total diesel scandal-related outflows of 31.3 billion euros ($34.69 billion), encompassing fines, settlements, and buybacks. By 2025, these economic costs exceeded 34 billion USD for fines, settlements, vehicle fixes, and lawsuits, including a 4.3 billion USD criminal fine paid to the U.S. Department of Justice. Key U.S. settlements included a $14.7 billion agreement in June 2016 for 2.0-liter diesel vehicles, covering consumer compensation, vehicle fixes or buybacks, and environmental mitigation, plus an additional $1.5 billion in civil penalties for environmental, customs, and financial claims. Under Federal Trade Commission orders, Volkswagen and Porsche repaid over $9.5 billion to affected U.S. car buyers by July 2020. These provisions contributed to a 2015 after-tax loss of $1.53 billion for the group, alongside executive accountability measures culminating in criminal convictions and imprisonments by 2025.
On the corporate front, CEO Martin Winterkorn resigned on September 23, 2015, citing shock at the events and assuming responsibility for the misconduct, though denying personal knowledge of the defeat devices. He was succeeded by Matthias Mller, former Porsche CEO, who prioritized crisis management and compliance reforms. U.S. CEO Michael Horn also resigned in 2016 amid ongoing probes. The scandal exposed governance weaknesses, including a dominant supervisory board structure and insufficient internal controls, prompting calls for structural changes to enhance oversight and ethical standards, though implementation details varied in effectiveness across reports. Long-term, these pressures accelerated Volkswagen's pivot toward electric vehicles, with investments redirected from diesel programs, though total market value recovery remained partial by 2025 amid persistent legal overhangs.

Health Claims: Empirical Evidence and Methodological Critiques

Studies estimating health impacts from excess nitrogen oxides (NOx) emissions in Volkswagen diesel vehicles equipped with defeat devices have primarily relied on atmospheric chemistry-transport models to simulate pollutant dispersion and secondary aerosol formation, coupled with epidemiological concentration-response functions to project outcomes such as premature mortality, respiratory illnesses, and hospital admissions. Broader estimates attribute thousands of premature deaths to excess pollution from scandal-affected vehicles, particularly in Europe. In the United States, a 2015 analysis projected 59 premature deaths (95% confidence interval: 10150), approximately 31 cases of chronic bronchitis, 34 cases of non-fatal heart attacks, 12,200 cases of acute bronchitis in children under 18, and 390,000 days of work loss due to respiratory symptoms, integrated over vehicle sales from 2008 to 2015. These projections assumed excess NOx emissions of up to 40 times federal standards, translated into elevated fine particulate matter (PM2.5) and ground-level ozone via photochemical reactions, with health effects valued at roughly $450 million in social costs.
In Europe, a 2017 Massachusetts Institute of Technology study estimated that excess NOx from Volkswagen vehicles sold in Germany alone would cause about 1,200 premature deaths continent-wide, primarily through ozone and nitrate particulate formation affecting populations downwind. Broader assessments of defeat-device-equipped diesels (including non-Volkswagen models) have attributed up to 10,000 premature deaths in 2013 to excess NOx contributions to PM2.5 and ozone, with 70% of impacts occurring transboundary due to prevailing winds. A quasi-experimental study exploiting spatial variation in U.S. county-level Volkswagen diesel registrations found associations between higher pre-scandal exposure and 1.52% increases in low birth weight and infant mortality rates, particularly in disadvantaged areas.
These estimates, however, face methodological limitations inherent to integrated assessment modeling, including assumptions of linearity in pollutant-health dose-response relationships derived from high-exposure cohorts (e.g., urban smog episodes or occupational settings), which may overestimate risks at the low, marginal concentrations relevant to Volkswagen's excess emissions amid declining baseline air quality. Chemical transport models like GEOS-Chem, used in several analyses, incorporate uncertainties in emission inventories, meteorology, and secondary pollutant yields (e.g., nitrate PM formation efficiency varying by temperature and VOC/NOx ratios), yet sensitivity tests often yield wide confidence intervals reflecting parameter variability rather than direct validation against observed health data. Attribution challenges persist, as Volkswagen's affected U.S. fleet of approximately 500,000 light-duty diesels represented a minor fraction of total transportation NOxestimated at under 5% even accounting for 1040-fold exceedancesdiluting isolable signals amid co-trending reductions from other sources like heavy-duty engines and power plants. 
Empirical verification remains elusive, with no detectable post-recall shifts in national morbidity or mortality metrics directly traceable to Volkswagen fixes, underscoring the models' reliance on counterfactual simulations over causal observational data; for instance, while excess NOx contributed to ozone precursors, real-world PM2.5 benefits from diesels' inherently lower direct black carbon emissions (versus gasoline) are unaccounted for in scandal-specific assessments, potentially netting positive health trade-offs in urban settings. Organizations like the International Council on Clean Transportation, which initiated emissions testing leading to the scandal, have produced expansive impact extrapolations, but their advocacy orientation warrants scrutiny for emphasizing upper-bound scenarios without equivalent weighting of model conservatism. Overall, while excess NOx posed quantifiable air chemistry perturbations, the projected health burdens hinge on extrapolative chains with high epistemic uncertainty, contrasting with the absence of forensic epidemiological evidence linking specific Volkswagen vehicles to excess disease incidence.

Environmental Assertions vs. Net CO2 Benefits of Diesel

Diesel engines in Volkswagen vehicles involved in the emissions scandal offered lower carbon dioxide (CO) emissions compared to equivalent gasoline-powered models due to their higher thermal efficiency and fuel economy advantages, typically ranging from 20% to 50% better miles per gallon. This efficiency translated to reduced CO output per mile driven, with lifecycle analyses indicating diesel vehicles can lower utilization-phase emissions by approximately 24% relative to gasoline counterparts. For instance, studies confirm that for the same driving distance, diesel passenger cars emit less CO owing to thermodynamic advantages like higher compression ratios and leaner operation.
Environmental assertions surrounding the scandal emphasized the health and air quality impacts of excess nitrogen oxides (NOx) emissions, which Volkswagen's defeat devices allowed vehicles to exceed U.S. standards by up to 40 times during real-world driving. Quantified estimates suggest the affected vehicles contributed between 237,000 and 949,000 tonnes of additional NOx annually worldwide, contributing to smog formation, acid rain, and respiratory issues. A 2017 MIT study attributed around 1,200 premature deaths in Europe to excess NOx from Volkswagen diesels sold in Germany alone, highlighting localized human health costs through ozone and particulate matter formation.
However, these NOx-focused critiques often overlooked the net CO reductions from diesel adoption, as the scandal-enabled proliferation of efficient diesels displaced less efficient gasoline vehicles, potentially averting higher global greenhouse gas emissions in the absence of viable electric alternatives at the time. While NOx emissions pose acute regional risks, diesel's CO efficiency provided a measurable climate benefit, with European data showing diesel cars maintaining a CO advantage, albeit narrowing to about 1.5% by 2010 due to improving gasoline technologies. Post-scandal shifts away from diesel toward gasoline or hybrids may have inadvertently increased fleet-average CO emissions in regions where electrification lagged. Empirical lifecycle assessments reinforce that diesel's lower well-to-wheel CO footprint versus gasoline holds, particularly pre-2015 when battery electric vehicles comprised less than 1% of sales.

Consumer and Market Effects, Including Resale Values

The Volkswagen emissions scandal, revealed on September 18, 2015, led to immediate erosion of consumer confidence in the company's diesel vehicles, particularly the TDI models marketed as "clean diesel" with low emissions, contributing to a broader loss of public trust in the diesel industry. The scandal exemplified asymmetric information, as Volkswagen concealed the defeat devices that enabled vehicles to pass emissions tests while emitting higher pollutants in real-world conditionsa fact unknown to buyers, regulators, and competitorsleading to adverse selection in vehicle markets akin to Akerlof's "market for lemons," where inability to discern quality eroded trust and precipitated price drops in affected used cars. Owners reported diminished trust in Volkswagen's environmental claims, prompting widespread dissatisfaction and class-action lawsuits alleging deceptive marketing. In the United States, affected consumers of 2.0-liter diesel vehicles from model years 2009-2015 faced options for vehicle fixes, buybacks, or lease terminations as part of a $14.7 billion settlement approved in October 2016, which included compensation for diminished value and extended warranties.
Resale values for Volkswagen TDI diesel vehicles plummeted following the disclosure, with Kelley Blue Book data indicating a decline more than three times faster than for comparable non-diesel Volkswagen models in the immediate aftermath. A June 2016 analysis estimated the median loss at $1,483 per vehicle, equivalent to nearly 8% of projected non-scandal value, driven by increased supply from owners seeking to offload affected cars and reduced demand due to stigma. In Europe, similar depreciation occurred, with affected vehicles experiencing rapid value drops that outpaced remedies like software updates, leaving consumers with ongoing losses upon resale. Academic studies confirmed a surge in used Volkswagen diesel listings post-scandal, reflecting owners' efforts to mitigate further depreciation amid heightened scrutiny of emissions performance.
Market-wide, the scandal accelerated the decline of diesel vehicle adoption, especially in the US where Volkswagen suspended sales of new diesel models by late 2015 and ceased marketing them entirely by 2016, eliminating a segment that had comprised about 25% of its US sales. Diesel market share contracted sharply as consumers shifted toward gasoline and emerging electric alternatives, compounded by reputational spillover affecting other German manufacturers with an estimated 105,000 fewer vehicle sales valued at $5.2 billion in the US through 2019. Globally, the event undermined diesel's appeal in markets like Europe, where pre-scandal diesel registrations had hovered around one-third of total vehicles in countries such as Germany, leading to policy reevaluations and slower sales recovery for affected brands.

Industry-Wide Ramifications

Practices at Other Manufacturers

Following the Volkswagen scandal, investigations by agencies including the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) and European regulators uncovered emissions manipulation software at other manufacturers, though often less extensive than Volkswagen's cycle-detection defeat devices. These practices typically involved auxiliary emission control strategies that optimized performance for laboratory test cyclessuch as the Worldwide Harmonized Light Vehicles Test Procedure (WLTP) or older New European Driving Cycle (NEDC)while allowing higher real-world NOx outputs, sometimes crossing into illegal territory by undisclosed means of circumventing controls.
Daimler AG (Mercedes-Benz parent) faced significant penalties for installing software classified as defeat devices in BlueTEC diesel vehicles. In September 2020, the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ) and EPA announced a $1.5 billion settlement with Daimler for violating the Clean Air Act through software that disabled or reduced emissions controls outside test conditions in approximately 250,000 diesel cars and vans sold in the U.S. from model years 20092016, leading to excess NOx emissions estimated at up to 16 times legal limits in real driving. The software manipulated selective catalytic reduction (SCR) systems, including AdBlue injection, to meet lab standards while prioritizing fuel efficiency and performance on roads, affecting models like the Mercedes E250 BlueTEC. Daimler neither admitted nor denied wrongdoing but agreed to emissions fixes, buybacks, and compliance audits.
Fiat Chrysler Automobiles (FCA, now Stellantis) settled related claims in 2019 for $800 million with U.S. states and the EPA over undisclosed software in Ram 1500 and Jeep Grand Cherokee diesels (model years 20142019) that could interpret driving conditions to limit emissions controls, potentially functioning as an undeclared defeat device under EPA rules. The software adjusted EGR rates and SCR dosing based on detected parameters mimicking test cycles, resulting in real-world NOx exceeding certification limits by factors of 915 times, though FCA maintained it complied with disclosure requirements for auxiliary controls.
In Europe, probes by Germany's Federal Motor Transport Authority (KBA) and others identified similar software in vehicles from BMW, Renault, Peugeot-Citron (PSA), and others, often termed "illegal switch-off devices" for reducing urea injection during non-test conditions. By 20172019, KBA approved software updates for over 5 million vehicles across multiple brands after finding cycle-beating algorithms that cut emissions by 2050% in tests versus roads, though not all qualified as outright defeat devices under U.S.-style illegality. Ongoing UK class-action suits filed in 2025 against Mercedes, Ford, Renault-Nissan, and PSA allege manipulation in up to 10 million diesel vehicles via test-recognizing software, seeking compensation for 1.61.8 million claimants on grounds of deceptive compliance. These cases highlight systemic industry reliance on software exploits amid stringent NOx caps (e.g., Euro 6 limits of 0.08 g/km), where empirical road testing post-scandal revealed widespread discrepancies, though penalties remained lower than Volkswagen's due to varying regulatory thresholds and self-reported fixes.

Shifts in Diesel Adoption and EV Push

The Volkswagen emissions scandal, revealed in September 2015, contributed to a marked decline in diesel vehicle adoption across major markets, eroding consumer confidence in diesel technology's environmental claims. In Europe, where diesel engines powered over 50% of new passenger car registrations in 2015, the market share fell to approximately 32% by 2019, driven partly by public backlash against the scandal's revelations of widespread NOx exceedances. This drop accelerated amid urban diesel bans in cities like Paris and London, alongside stricter EU CO2 fleet standards that incentivized alternatives, though pre-existing trends toward petrol and hybrids also played a role.
In the United States, diesel passenger cars represented a smaller segment, comprising about 3% of light-duty vehicle sales prior to the scandal, but the affair nearly eliminated VW's diesel offerings, which had accounted for up to 25% of its U.S. sales. Overall U.S. diesel sales, already waning due to higher upfront costs and fuel infrastructure limitations, peaked at around 490,000 units annually before 2015 and continued downward, with the scandal prompting buybacks of nearly 500,000 affected VW vehicles and halting new compliant diesel introductions.
The scandal prompted Volkswagen to pivot aggressively toward electric vehicles (EVs) as a reputational and strategic recovery measure. In 2016, VW outlined "Together Strategy 2025," committing over 35 billion to electrification, including development of 30 new EV models under the ID. badge and expansion of battery production facilities in Europe and North America. This shift was framed internally as a necessity after diesel became "radioactive" in public perception, with leadership under CEO Herbert Diess emphasizing EVs to meet tightening global emissions regulations and recapture market leadership.
By 2023, VW had launched multiple ID. series vehicles, such as the ID.3 and ID.4, aiming to position itself as a top global EV producer by 2025, though execution faced hurdles including slower-than-expected adoption and competitive pressures from Tesla and Chinese manufacturers. The company's U.S. settlements funded Electrify America, a $2 billion charging network intended to bolster EV infrastructure, yet as of 2024, VW acknowledged its core brand's diminished competitiveness amid EV market volatility. Industry-wide, the scandal indirectly hastened regulatory pushes for electrification, though diesel's persistence in commercial fleets underscores limits to the EV transition tied solely to passenger car NOx concerns.

Regulatory Changes and Compliance Burdens

In the United States, the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) responded to the Volkswagen scandal by intensifying enforcement against defeat devices, prohibiting software that alters emissions performance based on driving conditions. This included more aggressive auditing of certification data and expanded use of confirmatory testing to verify compliance with Tier 2 NOx standards, which limit emissions to 0.07 grams per mile for light-duty diesels. The California Air Resources Board (CARB) collaborated similarly, imposing stricter oversight that delayed vehicle approvals and required manufacturers to submit detailed engineering justifications for emissions controls.
In the European Union, regulators accelerated the rollout of the Worldwide Harmonized Light Vehicles Test Procedure (WLTP) in September 2017, replacing the less representative New European Driving Cycle (NEDC) with a protocol simulating varied real-world conditions, resulting in approximately 20-30% higher reported fuel consumption and CO2 emissions for many models. Concurrently, Real Driving Emissions (RDE) testing was mandated from 2017 onward, using portable emissions measurement systems (PEMS) to assess NOx and particulate matter on public roads, initially with a conformity factor of 2.1 that tightened to 1.5 by 2021 under Euro 6d standards. These measures included mandatory third-party verification and end-of-line emissions checks to prevent type-approval manipulation.
Compliance burdens escalated across jurisdictions, as automakers invested in advanced aftertreatment systems like selective catalytic reduction (SCR) with diesel exhaust fluid to meet tightened NOx limitsoften 80-90% reductions from pre-scandal baselineswhile navigating prolonged certification timelines. RDE testing alone added costs of 10,000-50,000 per vehicle prototype due to PEMS equipment and data analysis requirements, contributing to industry-wide delays in model launches and estimated annual compliance expenditures exceeding 1 billion for major European manufacturers by 2019. In the US, similar enhancements led to retrofit programs and software validations costing Volkswagen over $10 billion for affected 2.0-liter engines alone, with ripple effects prompting other firms to allocate billions more for independent audits and hardware upgrades. Globally, these reforms spurred adoption of WLTP/RDE variants in markets like Japan and China, amplifying development pressures and accelerating transitions away from non-compliant diesel architectures toward costlier alternatives.

Controversies and Debates

Monkeygate: Animal Testing Backlash

In January 2018, revelations emerged that Volkswagen, in collaboration with other German automakers through the European Research Group on Environment and Health in the Transport Sector (EUGT), had funded experiments exposing macaque monkeys to diesel exhaust fumes to assess potential health impacts. The specific Volkswagen-commissioned study, conducted in 2014 at the Lovelace Respiratory Research Institute in Albuquerque, New Mexico, involved 10 monkeys sealed in exposure chambers and subjected to fumes from a 1999 VW Beetle and a newer diesel model for approximately four hours each, with the animals provided cartoons as a distraction to reduce stress. These tests aimed to demonstrate reduced toxicity in modern diesel emissions compared to older technology, amid industry efforts to rehabilitate diesel's image following the 2015 emissions cheating scandal.
The disclosures, first reported by German outlet Stuttgarter Zeitung, triggered widespread condemnation from animal rights organizations and ethicists, who decried the experiments as cruel and scientifically questionable, arguing that primate testing for automotive emissions lacked necessity given alternative modeling methods and failed to yield peer-reviewed publications. PETA labeled the procedures "cruel" and called for accountability, while broader criticism highlighted the irony of such tests occurring after Volkswagen's admission of using defeat devices to falsify emissions data in over 11 million vehicles. Public outrage amplified under the moniker "Monkeygate," with media coverage emphasizing the ethical breach and questioning the industry's reliance on animal models for regulatory defense rather than transparent emissions reductions.
Volkswagen responded by suspending its chief lobbyist, Thomas Steg, on January 30, 2018, and issuing a statement accepting responsibility, asserting that the decision to conduct the tests was made by a "small group" within EUGT and did not align with the company's values or practices at the time. The firm distanced itself by noting the experiments predated current leadership reforms post-Dieselgate and emphasized that EUGT, dissolved in 2017, had operated independently, though internal reviews confirmed Volkswagen's funding contribution of around 630,000 to the broader research initiative. Other involved manufacturers, including BMW and Daimler, also initiated internal investigations and personnel actions, underscoring the controversy's industry-wide scope but centering scrutiny on Volkswagen due to its prior scandal. No criminal charges directly stemmed from the animal tests, but the episode fueled debates on ethical standards in automotive research and the credibility of industry-funded studies defending diesel technology.

Critiques of US EPA Enforcement and Standards

Critics of the US Environmental Protection Agency's (EPA) approach argue that the Tier 2 emission standards, phased in from 2004 to 2009, imposed unrealistically stringent nitrogen oxide (NOx) limits of 0.07 grams per mile on light-duty diesel vehicles, far below levels feasible without compromising engine performance and fuel efficiency. These standards, combined with certification tests based on fixed lab cycles, created incentives for manufacturers like Volkswagen to deploy software-based defeat devices, as compliant technologies such as urea-based selective catalytic reduction systems added thousands of dollars in costs and reduced drivability. Economists contend that this regulatory framework effectively sidelined diesel's inherent advantages in carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions and fuel economydiesels achieve up to 30% better efficiency than gasoline equivalentsprioritizing NOx reductions at the expense of broader environmental and economic trade-offs, including higher overall vehicle prices and reduced market viability for efficient powertrains.
The EPA's enforcement mechanisms drew scrutiny for failing to detect Volkswagen's cheating prior to 2015, despite mandatory certification processes; independent testing by West Virginia University researchers in 2013 revealed real-world NOx emissions 10 to 40 times above certified levels, prompting EPA action only after external alerts. A 2018 EPA Office of Inspector General report highlighted deficiencies in the agency's light-duty diesel compliance program, including the absence of formal risk assessments, performance metrics, and procedures for integrating on-road or remote sensing data, which allowed sophisticated defeat devicescapable of sensing test conditionsto evade detection during standard FTP-75 dynamometer testing. The report noted that light-duty diesels represented a minor fleet share (less than 3% of sales), reducing scrutiny, and recommended enhanced controls like randomized testing protocols, which the EPA subsequently implemented to bolster fraud detection.
Comparisons to European standards underscore critiques of the EPA's idealism over practicality; while US NOx caps equated to roughly 0.043 grams per kilometerstricter than the EU's Euro 6 limit of 0.080 grams per kilometer for dieselsthe EPA's lab-centric approach overlooked real-world discrepancies more pronounced in Europe due to less rigorous cycle simulations pre-2017 RDE rules. Detractors argue this disparity reflects a US regulatory emphasis on absolute pollutant minimization without sufficient cost-benefit analysis, exacerbating the scandal's fallout by mandating buybacks and fixes that eliminated viable low-CO2 options from the market, potentially inflating net greenhouse gas emissions through shifts to less efficient alternatives. The resulting settlements, exceeding $15 billion in the US for fixes, consumer compensation, and penalties, have been characterized by some analysts as punitive overreach, though Volkswagen's deliberate deception mitigated defenses of regulatory excess.

Broader Questions on Regulatory Realism vs. Idealism

The Volkswagen emissions scandal underscores fundamental tensions in environmental regulation between idealism, which prioritizes absolute reductions in specific pollutants like nitrogen oxides (NOx) through uniform, stringent standards, and realism, which accounts for technological trade-offs, compliance costs, and broader environmental metrics such as carbon dioxide (CO2) efficiency inherent to diesel engines. In 1999, U.S. regulators mandated identical NOx limits for diesel and gasoline engines under Tier 2 standards, disregarding diesels' higher NOx output from elevated combustion temperatures required for their 20-30% superior fuel economy and lower CO2 emissions compared to gasoline counterparts. This idealistic equivalence forced diesel manufacturers to retrofit expensive aftertreatment systems, such as lean NOx traps (LNT) or urea-based selective catalytic reduction (SCR), amid feasibility challenges for compact passenger vehicles where space constraints limited SCR adoption without costly redesigns.
Technical realism highlights the inherent diesel NOx dilemma: lower combustion temperatures curb NOx but elevate particulate matter, while higher temperatures optimize efficiency at the expense of NOx, rendering single-pollutant targets like the EPA's 2004 requirement of 0.07 grams per milea 94% reduction from prior levelsdifficult without performance penalties or added complexity. Volkswagen engineers, confronting these constraints, deemed full compliance infeasible within budget and timelines for U.S. market entry, opting instead for software defeat devices that activated controls only during lab tests like the Federal Test Procedure (FTP-75), which idealized steady-state conditions unrepresentative of real-world variability. Empirical models from industry analysis show that each 1% tightening of emissions standards elevates misconduct risk by 1.72%, amplified by competitive pressures where rivals could sidestep diesels altogether, as seen with Mazda and Honda abandoning U.S. diesel plans due to analogous hurdles.
From a causal perspective, idealistic regulations risk unintended consequences by prioritizing lab purity over on-road efficacy and economic viability, as LNT systemschosen by Volkswagen for packaging reasonsdegraded fuel economy in independent tests, undermining diesels' CO2 edge without proportionally advancing air quality. Compliance costs, including SCR's higher upfront expenses and urea logistics, contributed to Volkswagen's post-scandal penalties exceeding $30 billion by 2022, raising questions about whether such burdens justify marginal NOx gains when lifecycle assessments reveal diesels' net CO2 benefits in non-electrified fleets. Realist approaches advocate cost-benefit analyses incorporating multi-pollutant trade-offs and real-driving emissions protocolsadopted post-scandal in Europe via RDE testingto foster genuine innovation rather than evasion, though mainstream regulatory bodies have historically favored expansive mandates that expand bureaucratic reach without rigorous feasibility validation. This disconnect, evident in divergent U.S.-Europe standards where laxer European NOx limits enabled compliant sales until 2015 revelations, illustrates how idealism can distort markets toward alternatives like battery electrics, whose supply-chain emissions and grid dependencies remain empirically underexplored relative to optimized diesels.

Reactions

Political Statements and Policy Shifts

In response to the Volkswagen emissions scandal revealed on September 18, 2015, German Chancellor Angela Merkel described it as a "dramatic event" but asserted that it would not cause lasting damage to Germany's reputation as an automaking nation. On November 3, 2015, she urged Volkswagen to provide full transparency in investigating and resolving the software manipulation used to evade emissions tests. During a March 8, 2017, parliamentary inquiry, Merkel testified that her government had received no prior warnings about the cheating from federal agencies or Volkswagen, stating she learned of it through media reports, and rejected characterizations of the episode as a systemic government failure. By June 2017, amid ongoing fallout, Merkel softened her prior defense of diesel technology, condemning emissions cheats and acknowledging risks to economic growth, while in August 2017 criticizing executive bonuses at affected firms as insensitive given the scandal's impact.
In the United States, the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) and Department of Justice (DOJ) spearheaded enforcement, announcing on June 28, 2016, a settlement requiring Volkswagen to pay up to $14.7 billion for 2.0-liter diesel vehicles, including consumer compensation, vehicle buybacks or modifications, and environmental mitigation projects to offset excess nitrogen oxide emissions estimated at 175 times legal limits in real-world conditions. A separate DOJ agreement in January 2017 imposed an additional $4.3 billion in penalties, including a guilty plea to conspiracy and obstruction charges, with funds directed toward further buybacks, infrastructure investments, and zero-emission vehicle promotion. The Federal Trade Commission enforced $9.5 billion in repayments to affected U.S. buyers by 2020, addressing deceptive "clean diesel" marketing. UK MPs on the Transport Select Committee, in April 2016, accused their government of "phenomenal complacency" toward Volkswagen's actions, criticizing Transport Minister Robert Goodwill for prioritizing the company's reputation over rigorous enforcement.
The scandal catalyzed policy adjustments, particularly in the European Union, where it exposed flaws in laboratory-based emissions testing and prompted the accelerated adoption of real-world driving emissions (RDE) protocols under the Worldwide Harmonised Light Vehicle Test Procedure (WLTP), effective for new vehicle types from September 2017 and all registrations by 2021, aiming to close loopholes exploited by defeat devices. EU authorities enhanced consumer protection coordination via the Consumer Protection Cooperation network, mandating compensation for affected owners and fining non-compliant manufacturers, though critics noted the reforms granted the European Commission additional oversight powers without fundamentally overhauling the self-certification model reliant on industry data. In the U.S., while no wholesale regulatory overhaul ensued, the EPA expanded scrutiny of diesel certifications and defeat device prohibitions under the Clean Air Act, leading to investigations of other manufacturers and reinforcing recall mandates, with Volkswagen required to retire or retrofit over 500,000 vehicles by 2017. Over the longer term, Dieselgate contributed to accelerated shifts toward electrification mandates, including EU CO2 targets that by 2025 positioned manufacturers near compliance thresholds via electric vehicle adoption, though enforcement gaps persisted in Europe compared to U.S. penalties exceeding $30 billion across settlements.

Expert and Industry Commentaries

His 2014 ICCT-commissioned study with West Virginia University researchers revealed Volkswagen diesel vehicles emitting 5 to 35 times the permitted NOx levels under real-world driving conditions compared to lab tests, prompting EPA investigations that confirmed software manipulation in approximately 500,000 U.S. vehicles from model years 2009 to 2015.
Luann Lynch, a professor at the University of Virginia's Darden School of Business, attributed the scandal to a "dangerous triad" of pressure, opportunity, and rationalization within Volkswagen's corporate culture. She highlighted intense top-down demands from former CEO Martin Winterkorn to achieve over 10 million annual vehicle sales, an autocratic environment stifling dissent, and engineers' rationalization of cheating via software from supplier Boschburied in 100 million lines of codeciting prior lenient penalties like a $120,000 fine in the 1970s as justification.
Environmental scientists emphasized health risks from excess NOx emissions. Professor James Longhurst of the University of the West of England described Volkswagen's admission of installing devices to disable emission controls as a direct threat to public health, urging all manufacturers to disclose similar practices amid evidence of widespread real-world exceedances of Euro standards. Dr. Jo Barnes, from the same institution's Air Quality Management Resource Centre, linked diesel NOx to approximately 10,000 premature deaths annually in London alone, advocating government-led compensation schemes if European prevalence mirrors U.S. findings.
Industry executives from Volkswagen acknowledged responsibility while downplaying intent. Incoming CEO Matthias Mller stated in January 2016, "We didn't lie," but conceded the company had "let down customers, authorities, regulators," prioritizing trust restoration through transparency and fixes. A Volvo executive later revealed in 2016 that Volkswagen's defeat device tactics were an "open secret" in the industry, suggesting competitors were aware but did not challenge them publicly. The ICCT noted Dieselgate's exposure of systemic defeat device use beyond Volkswagen, influencing global shifts to real-driving emissions tests and accelerating diesel-to-electric transitions among manufacturers facing fines and regulatory overhauls.

Media Narratives and Public Opinion Polls

Initial media coverage of the Volkswagen emissions scandal, which broke publicly on September 18, 2015, following the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency's violation notice, framed the issue primarily as a case of deliberate corporate cheating through defeat devices that enabled diesel vehicles to emit up to 40 times the permitted nitrogen oxide (NOx) levels during real-world driving. Outlets such as the BBC highlighted the scandal's implications for the broader automotive industry, portraying Volkswagen's actions as a betrayal of consumer trust and environmental standards, with reports emphasizing the affected 482,000 vehicles in the U.S. alone. This narrative dominated headlines, focusing on executive accountabilitysuch as the resignation of CEO Martin Winterkorn on September 23, 2015and potential health risks from excess NOx, though empirical data on direct casualties remained limited to modeled estimates rather than confirmed incidents. Coverage often attributed the scandal to a culture of aggressive cost-cutting and regulatory evasion at Volkswagen, with less initial scrutiny on the feasibility of meeting stringent U.S. standards using European diesel technology without such measures.
Over time, media narratives evolved, with some analyses reframing "Dieselgate" from a Volkswagen-specific crisis to an industry-wide challenge by 2018, incorporating discussions of similar noncompliance at other manufacturers and the inherent difficulties of low-NOx diesel compliance under disparate U.S. and European regulations. Academic reviews noted a shift toward frames of moral responsibility and systemic regulatory failure, though mainstream reporting retained a predominant emphasis on Volkswagen's culpability, including secondary stories on related controversies like animal testing ("Monkeygate"). Social media amplified public outrage, with Twitter mentions surging and initial spikes in Volkswagen followers (up 225% in the first two days), but platforms also hosted misinformation narratives questioning the scandal's severity or regulatory motives. Sources like the BBC and Guardian, which lean toward environmental advocacy, prioritized pollution impacts in their framing, potentially underemphasizing evidence that on-road emissions testing gaps predated Volkswagen's actions and affected competitors, as later investigations revealed.
Public opinion polls reflected a sharp decline in trust toward Volkswagen and diesel vehicles post-scandal. A What Car? survey conducted in October 2015 among 2,000 UK drivers showed the proportion likely or very likely to purchase a diesel car of any brand falling from 58% pre-scandal to 43% afterward, with Volkswagen-specific intent dropping even more precipitously due to perceived deceit in "clean diesel" marketing. Similarly, a Guardian poll from October 15, 2015, indicated that 86% of Volkswagen owners expressed concern over their vehicles' environmental impact, while 83% worried about diminished resale value amid buyback uncertainties. Brand health metrics deteriorated rapidly; a September 2015 analysis by Brandwatch reported Volkswagen's overall public perception score plummeting, with sentiment analysis showing a surge in negative associations tied to deception and emissions fraud. In Germany, where Volkswagen's home market buffered some backlash, surveys like those referenced in Quora discussions (drawing from Forsa polls) noted a temporary dip in favorability but quicker recovery compared to the U.S., attributed to national pride and economic stakes, though global trust erosion persisted, contributing to a 30% drop in Volkswagen's U.S. sales in late 2015. Long-term polls, such as Euroconsumers' 2021 survey, found 40% of affected owners reporting performance issues like increased fuel use, sustaining skepticism toward diesel reliability.
