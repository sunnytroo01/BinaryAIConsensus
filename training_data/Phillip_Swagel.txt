 Phillip Swagel is an American economist serving as the director of the Congressional Budget Office (CBO), the nonpartisan agency that provides Congress with objective analyses of budgetary and economic issues. Appointed in June 2019 and reappointed for a second term in 2023, Swagel oversees the preparation of CBO's cost estimates for legislation, long-term budget projections, and economic forecasts.
Swagel earned an A.B. in economics from Princeton University and a Ph.D. in economics from Harvard University. His career spans academia and government service, including professorships at the University of Maryland's School of Public Policy, Northwestern University, and the University of Chicago Booth School of Business. In government, he served as Assistant Secretary for Economic Policy at the U.S. Department of the Treasury from 2006 to 2009, where he advised on responses to the financial crisis, and as chief of staff and senior economist at the White House Council of Economic Advisers under President George W. Bush. Earlier roles included economist positions at the International Monetary Fund and the Federal Reserve Board. Swagel is also a non-resident scholar at the American Enterprise Institute.
Under Swagel's leadership, the CBO has produced analyses that have faced partisan scrutiny, particularly regarding projections on major legislative proposals such as tax reforms and spending bills, prompting defenses of the agency's methodological rigor and independence amid accusations of bias from both Democrats and Republicans. These interactions highlight the CBO's role as a fiscal scorekeeper in a politically charged environment, where its estimates influence debates on deficit impacts and policy outcomes.

Early Life and Education

Academic Background

Swagel earned an A.B. in economics from Princeton University in 1987. He subsequently attended Harvard University for graduate studies, where he received both a master's degree and a Ph.D. in economics in 1993. His doctoral research focused on international economics, aligning with his later professional emphasis on macroeconomic policy and fiscal analysis.

Early Career

Initial Professional Roles

Following receipt of his Ph.D. in economics from Harvard University in 1993, Phillip Swagel commenced his professional career as an economist at the Federal Reserve Board. In this role, he engaged in economic research and analysis supporting monetary policy formulation.
Swagel later transitioned to the International Monetary Fund (IMF), serving as an economist focused on international economic issues. His work at the IMF included authoring working papers on subjects such as the effects of globalization on wages in advanced economies and union behavior in relation to industry rents and optimal trade policies. These positions established his expertise in macroeconomic policy and international economics prior to his entry into U.S. government service.

Treasury Department Service

Key Positions and Responsibilities

Phillip Swagel served as Assistant Secretary for Economic Policy at the U.S. Department of the Treasury from December 11, 2006, to January 20, 2009. In this capacity, he advised Secretary Henry Paulson on all facets of economic policy, encompassing domestic fiscal and monetary matters, international economic relations, and responses to emerging financial stresses.
Swagel's core responsibilities involved overseeing the analysis of macroeconomic trends, including projections on growth, inflation, and employment, as well as evaluating policy options for tax, spending, and regulatory reforms. He directed the Office of Economic Policy, which provided technical support for Treasury initiatives on housing finance, energy markets, and trade negotiations, ensuring data-driven inputs into executive decision-making. This included coordinating with the Council of Economic Advisers and Federal Reserve on joint assessments of economic indicators, such as GDP revisions and labor market data released quarterly during his tenure.
Additionally, Swagel represented the Treasury in interagency working groups on international finance, contributing to G7 and IMF discussions on global imbalances and exchange rate policies between 2007 and 2008. His role extended to briefing congressional committees on economic outlooks, with testimonies emphasizing evidence-based forecasts over speculative narratives, such as in July 2008 presentations on borrowing costs and fiscal sustainability.

Role in the 2008 Financial Crisis

Phillip Swagel served as Assistant Secretary for Economic Policy at the U.S. Department of the Treasury from December 2006 to January 2009, during which he advised Secretary Henry Paulson on economic analysis and policy responses to the escalating financial crisis. In this role, Swagel contributed to early 2008 proposals aimed at addressing credit market disruptions, including the Treasury's March 2008 Blueprint for Financial Markets Regulatory Reform, which outlined high-level reforms to enhance oversight of financial institutions and reduce systemic risks. His office analyzed macroeconomic impacts, such as the housing market downturn and frozen interbank lending, providing data-driven inputs that informed decisions on interventions like the Federal Reserve's facilitated rescue of Bear Stearns in March 2008 and the conservatorships of Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac in September 2008.
Swagel's involvement intensified in the fall of 2008 amid the failures of Lehman Brothers and the near-collapse of American International Group (AIG). As a key economic advisor and member of the TARP Investment Committee, he helped shape the Emergency Economic Stabilization Act of 2008, enacted on October 3, which authorized $700 billion for the Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP) to stabilize the banking system. Initially designed to purchase troubled mortgage-backed securities, TARP's implementation under Swagel's analytical oversight shifted toward direct capital injections into banks; for instance, on October 14, 2008, nine major institutionsincluding JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, and Goldman Sachsreceived $125 billion in preferred stock purchases to bolster liquidity and confidence. Swagel later emphasized that political constraints, such as the House of Representatives' initial rejection of TARP on September 29, 2008, delayed responses and exacerbated market volatility, underscoring institutional limits on executive action without congressional buy-in.
In post-crisis reflections, Swagel highlighted legal and fiscal barriers that prevented preemptive rescues, such as the inability to force private debt-for-equity swaps at firms like Lehman Brothers absent statutory authority, which contributed to its bankruptcy filing on September 15, 2008. He argued that TARP's flexibility, despite initial opposition, proved effective in averting deeper economic contraction by enabling targeted equity investments rather than asset purchases alone, though he noted ongoing challenges from housing foreclosures that Congress prioritized for TARP allocation in late 2008. These efforts, informed by Swagel's macroeconomic modeling, helped restore interbank lending and supported broader Federal Reserve actions, marking a pivot from ad hoc interventions to systemic stabilization by late October 2008.

Academic and Advisory Career

University of Maryland and Research

Swagel joined the University of Maryland School of Public Policy in 2010 as a professor of international economic policy. In this capacity, he directed the Thomas Schelling Distinguished Visitor Series, which brings economists and policymakers to campus for lectures and discussions on public policy issues. He held the position until June 2019, when he departed to assume the directorship of the Congressional Budget Office. During his tenure at Maryland, Swagel contributed to the school's emphasis on applied economics, integrating his prior government experience into teaching and advising on topics such as domestic and international economic policy.
Swagel's research at Maryland built on his earlier work in international economics, emphasizing empirical analysis of trade, financial stability, and macroeconomic policy. His publications include examinations of political instability's impact on per capita GDP growth across 113 countries from 1950 to 1982, finding a negative correlation that persisted after controlling for other factors. Another key contribution, co-authored with N. Gregory Mankiw, analyzed the politics and economics of offshore outsourcing, arguing that such trade in services enhances efficiency despite short-term adjustment costs for workers. He also explored the Balassa-Samuelson effect's role in cross-country inflation differences, using panel data to estimate its contribution to price level variations.
Further research addressed fiscal and demographic pressures, such as the expansion of welfare states in aging populations, where Swagel and co-authors modeled how intergenerational transfers influence public spending growth in OECD countries. His work on financial crises, including constraints on policy responses during the 20072009 downturn, drew from institutional economics to critique legal and political barriers to effective intervention. These studies, often published in peer-reviewed journals like the Journal of Public Economics and Brookings Papers on Economic Activity, amassed thousands of citations and informed policy debates on globalization and market reforms. Swagel's approach privileged data-driven models over ideological priors, consistently highlighting causal links between trade openness and long-term growth.

Think Tank Affiliations and Publications

Swagel has served as a non-resident scholar at the American Enterprise Institute (AEI), a think tank focused on free enterprise and limited government, where his work has emphasized financial market reforms, international trade, and fiscal policy. In this capacity, he contributed to AEI's economic research, including the 2006 working paper "The Politics and Economics of Offshore Outsourcing", co-authored with N. Gregory Mankiw, which analyzes the welfare effects of outsourcing using a gravity model of trade and argues that such activities enhance global efficiency despite short-term domestic disruptions. Other AEI-affiliated outputs include op-eds such as "Still Too Big to Fail?" (date not specified in sources), which defends the economic contributions of large banks in facilitating investment and job creation, countering post-crisis regulatory skepticism.
He has also held a visiting scholar position at the Milken Institute, engaging in discussions on economic policy and finance through events and analyses. Publications linked to this affiliation are less prolific but align with his broader research on growth and innovation, often incorporating data-driven assessments of policy impacts.
Swagel's think tank work underscores empirical approaches to topics like trade liberalization and financial stability, with outputs typically grounded in econometric evidence rather than ideological advocacy, though AEI's conservative orientation has shaped the framing of debates on government intervention.

Congressional Budget Office Directorship

Appointment and Leadership

Phillip Swagel was appointed as the tenth Director of the Congressional Budget Office (CBO) on May 15, 2019, through a joint selection process by the chairs and ranking members of the House and Senate Budget Committees, without requiring Senate confirmation. He succeeded Keith Hall, whose term had ended on January 3, 2019, but who served in a temporary capacity until Swagel's arrival. Swagel's initial four-year term ran from June 3, 2019, to January 3, 2023.
On July 27, 2023, bipartisan House and Senate leadership reappointed Swagel for a second term, extending his directorship until January 3, 2027. This reappointment reflected confidence in his management of the agency's nonpartisan mandate amid fiscal debates, including analyses of major legislation such as the American Rescue Plan Act of 2021 and the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022.
As director, Swagel has prioritized maintaining the CBO's independence and objectivity, emphasizing rigorous economic modeling and transparent methodologies to support Congress in evaluating budget proposals. He has overseen updates to the agency's forecasting tools and staff expansions to address complex issues like long-term entitlement projections and dynamic revenue effects. In public statements, Swagel has defended the CBO against partisan critiques, underscoring its commitment to empirical data over political influence. His leadership style, described as low-key, has focused on steady institutional reliability during periods of high legislative activity and economic uncertainty, including the COVID-19 response and subsequent recovery efforts.

Major Analyses and Projections

During Swagel's tenure as CBO Director, the agency produced annual baseline projections in its Budget and Economic Outlook reports, forecasting federal deficits, revenues, outlays, and macroeconomic indicators under current law assumptions. The January 2025 edition projected a $1.9 trillion federal budget deficit for fiscal year 2025, equivalent to 6.2 percent of GDP, with deficits averaging 5.8 percent of GDP over the 20252035 period amid rising interest costs and mandatory spending. Real GDP growth was forecasted to moderate to 1.9 percent in 2025 following 2.3 percent in 2024, then average 1.8 percent annually through 2035, driven by slower labor force growth and productivity gains. Federal debt held by the public was projected to reach 118 percent of GDP by 2035, exceeding its historical peak.
The March 2025 Long-Term Budget Outlook extended projections to 2055, estimating average annual real GDP growth of 1.6 percent from 2025 onward, constrained by demographic aging and federal borrowing crowding out private investment. Annual deficits were forecasted to climb to 7.3 percent of GDP by 2055, nearly double the 3.9 percent historical average, pushing debt to levels that could exceed 180 percent of GDP under extended baselines, raising risks of fiscal instability without policy changes. Swagel highlighted these trajectories in public statements, stressing the need to reduce deficits to sustainable levels to avoid higher interest rates and reduced economic flexibility.
CBO also assessed the accuracy of prior projections, including a September 2025 analysis of its 2018 forecasts for the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, which evaluated revenue and growth effects against actual outcomes. Updates to pandemic-era analyses, such as a June 2025 review, estimated that extended emergency spending would add $3.3 trillion to public debt by 2034 relative to the January 2025 baseline. These projections incorporated dynamic scoring where applicable, accounting for macroeconomic feedback, and were updated quarterly to reflect legislative changes and economic data.

Responses to Fiscal Challenges

Under Swagel's directorship, the Congressional Budget Office (CBO) issued annual budget and economic outlooks that projected escalating federal deficits and debt, attributing much of the strain to rising mandatory spending on entitlements, interest costs, and post-pandemic fiscal expansions. In the January 2025 Budget and Economic Outlook, CBO forecasted a $1.9 trillion deficit for fiscal year 2025, growing to $2.7 trillion by 2035, with federal debt held by the public reaching 118 percent of GDP by that yearsurpassing previous highs and driven by outlays exceeding revenues amid slower revenue growth relative to spending. Swagel emphasized in accompanying statements that these trends reflect an "unusual and very challenging fiscal situation," warning that unchecked deficits would compound interest burdens, projected to exceed $1 trillion annually by the mid-2030s.
Swagel responded to immediate fiscal pressures, such as debt limit constraints and short-term cash flow risks, by providing Congress with detailed monthly deficit updates and projections of borrowing needs. For instance, in March 2025 testimony, he highlighted the urgency of stabilizing deficits at sustainable levelsimplicitly below 3 percent of GDP long-termto avoid default risks, noting that extraordinary measures could deplete Treasury's cash by August without legislative action. CBO's analyses under his leadership also incorporated macroeconomic feedback effects, estimating that higher debt could crowd out private investment and elevate interest rates by 0.5 percentage points over the decade, further worsening primary deficits.
In addressing long-term sustainability, Swagel's tenure saw the release of the March 2025 Long-Term Budget Outlook, projecting federal debt climbing to 166 percent of GDP by 2055 under current policies, with annual deficits averaging 6.3 percent of GDPfar above historical normsand net interest consuming 6.4 percent of GDP by mid-century. He attributed this trajectory primarily to demographics-driven entitlement growth, with Social Security and Medicare outlays rising from 10.5 percent of GDP in 2025 to 12.4 percent by 2055, outpacing revenue increases. Swagel advocated for Congress to use CBO's tools, including options for deficit reduction, to inform reforms, stressing in public remarks that fiscal adjustments must prioritize spending restraint given revenue's limited upside from economic growth alone.
CBO under Swagel also scrutinized legislative proposals exacerbating deficits, such as estimating that permanent extensions of 2017 tax cuts without offsets would add $4.5 trillion to deficits over 20252034, while tariff hikes post-January 2025 were projected to modestly reduce primary deficits by $200300 billion through revenue gains offset partially by economic drag. These assessments aimed to equip lawmakers with evidence-based scoring, though Swagel noted in hearings the challenges of dynamic effects and baseline assumptions in capturing full fiscal risks.

Economic Policy Views

Fiscal Sustainability and Deficits

Swagel has emphasized that the United States faces an unsustainable fiscal trajectory due to persistently large budget deficits that drive rising federal debt levels, increasing economic risks over time. In his role as director of the Congressional Budget Office (CBO), he has overseen projections showing the federal deficit reaching $1.9 trillion in fiscal year 2025, or 6.2 percent of gross domestic product (GDP), and expanding to $2.7 trillion by 2035, equivalent to 6.1 percent of GDP, with federal debt held by the public climbing to 122 percent of GDP by the latter year. These deficits stem primarily from growth in mandatory spending on entitlements like Social Security and Medicare, amid an aging population and rising healthcare costs, compounded by elevated net interest payments on the debt, which Swagel identifies as a near-term fiscal risk exacerbated by higher interest rates and accumulating borrowing.
To address this, Swagel advocates for policies that stabilize the debt-to-GDP ratio through gradual fiscal adjustments, arguing that credible plans enhance economic flexibility, support private-sector growth, and allow room for productivity-enhancing investments without abrupt austerity. In a 2014 report co-authored with economist Janice Eberly for the Peter G. Peterson Foundation, he outlined a "fiscal balancing act" that reconciles existing federal commitments with necessary investments, warning that without such measures, net interest costsprojected to rise from 1.3 percent of GDP in 20132014 to over 3 percent by 2024would crowd out other priorities and undermine long-term sustainability. He has noted positive developments, such as the Fiscal Responsibility Act of 2023, which improved the 10-year budget outlook by roughly $1.4 trillion net after accounting for other changes, but maintains that broader reforms are essential to curb entitlement expansions and prevent deficits from averaging levels far above historical norms of around 3 percent of GDP.
Swagel has publicly urged policymakers to reduce deficits to sustainable levels, stating in March 2025 that "we have to get the deficit to a level that's sustainable" to mitigate risks like higher interest burdens that restrict federal and private-sector options. While acknowledging factors like recent immigration surges boosting labor force growth and revenuesadding an estimated 5.2 million workers by 2034he stresses that demographic pressures and policy inaction will otherwise lead to deficits consuming a growing share of the budget, potentially reaching 7.3 percent of GDP by 2055 under extended baselines. His analyses prioritize empirical projections over optimistic assumptions, cautioning against complacency despite the absence of an immediate crisis.

Tax Policy and Economic Growth

As director of the Congressional Budget Office, Phillip Swagel has overseen analyses concluding that the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) produced limited positive effects on economic growth, insufficient to offset the legislation's revenue losses. In a July 2024 Senate hearing, Swagel testified that the TCJA "had some positive effects on GDP, but by far it did not pay for itself," with actual real GDP growth in 20182019 averaging about 0.3 percentage points above CBO's pre-TCJA baseline projections, yet failing to generate enough additional tax receipts to cover the cuts.
Swagel has recognized that lower marginal tax rates under such policies enhance incentives for labor supply and saving, potentially elevating productivity and output. Extending TCJA's individual provisions, for example, would encourage work and investment through reduced personal income tax rates, though CBO models indicate these dynamic feedbacks yield only partial revenue recovery. In dynamic scoring exercises, which incorporate macroeconomic responses, CBO under Swagel's leadership estimated that proposed tax reforms like H.R. 1 could boost average annual potential GDP growth by 0.08 percentage points from 2025 to 2029 via increased labor supply, tapering to 0.03 percentage points thereafter.
These assessments highlight trade-offs: while tax reductions can stimulate short-term activity and investment certaintyparticularly if made permanent to avoid fiscal cliffspersistent deficits from unoffset revenues elevate debt, crowding out private capital and dampening long-term growth. CBO projections under extension scenarios foresee federal debt reaching 214% of GDP by 2054, amplifying interest costs and reducing fiscal space for future growth-enhancing policies. Swagel's emphasis on these empirical dynamics underscores a view that tax policy must balance incentive effects against unsustainable borrowing, rather than relying on growth to fully self-finance cuts.

Entitlements and Government Spending

Swagel has long emphasized the fiscal pressures posed by entitlement programs such as Social Security, Medicare, and Medicaid, which he identifies as primary drivers of long-term federal spending growth and deficits. In a 2013 analysis, he argued that without reforms, these programs would lead to unsustainable debt levels, projecting that national debt could begin rising uncontrollably within five years absent action. He advocated specific adjustments, including altering Social Security's benefit indexation to reflect slower wage growth, increasing Medicare deductibles and co-payments for higher-income beneficiaries, and gradually raising the Medicare eligibility age to align with life expectancy gains, framing these as measures to preserve the safety net for low-income dependents while ensuring progressive burden-sharing.
As director of the Congressional Budget Office (CBO) from 2019 to 2024, Swagel oversaw projections underscoring entitlements' role in fiscal deterioration, with mandatory spendingdominated by Social Security and Medicareexpected to rise significantly as a share of GDP due to demographic shifts and healthcare costs. In testimony and public statements, he highlighted Social Security's trust fund depletion projected for 2034 and Medicare's Hospital Insurance Trust Fund exhaustion by 2033, noting these timelines necessitate "difficult decisions" to avert benefit shortfalls, though a growing economy provides a window for deliberate action rather than immediate crisis response. He described U.S. entitlement programs collectively as integral to the nation's "challenging fiscal trajectory," warning that unchecked growth would exacerbate deficits alongside rising net interest costs.
Swagel's rationale for entitlement reform centers on generational fairness, contending that delaying adjustments unfairly burdens future workers and taxpayers while current beneficiaries receive unreformed benefits. He has critiqued past administrations, including Obama's, for insufficient fiscal restraint that failed to address entitlement expansions, arguing that such inaction diverts resources from productive investments like education and infrastructure. In this view, reforming entitlements is essential not only for solvency but to enable broader government spending priorities without compromising economic stability.

Controversies and Criticisms

Financial Crisis Policies

Phillip Swagel served as Assistant Secretary for Economic Policy at the U.S. Department of the Treasury from December 2006 to January 2009, where he contributed to the formulation of crisis responses, including analysis supporting the Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP), enacted on October 3, 2008, which authorized up to $700 billion for purchasing troubled assets and later recapitalizing banks to avert systemic collapse. Initially designed to buy mortgage-backed securities, TARP's pivot to direct equity investments in financial institutionssuch as $125 billion injected into nine major banks on October 28, 2008drew criticism for deviating from the original intent and effectively nationalizing aspects of the banking sector, raising concerns over government overreach and dilution of shareholder rights without adequate transparency on asset valuations.
Critics, including economists skeptical of interventionist approaches, argued that TARP and related measures like the $85 billion AIG bailout on September 16, 2008, created moral hazard by shielding executives and investors from failure, potentially encouraging future risk-taking while imposing costs on taxpayers through warrants and dividends that yielded uneven returns. Swagel defended these actions as essential to restore lending and prevent a depression, noting in retrospect that Treasury's early reluctance to propose credit market interventions in 2008 stemmed from political constraints and underestimation of subprime exposure's breadth, which delayed decisive steps until after Lehman Brothers' bankruptcy on September 15, 2008.
Further controversy arose over internal Treasury dynamics, with Swagel later citing "chronic disorganization" in vetting options like asset purchases as early as March 2008, where proposals underwent over a dozen iterations without implementation, exacerbating market panic. The extension of TARP funds to non-financial sectors, such as $17.4 billion for automakers in December 2008, was faulted for prioritizing political expediency over market discipline, propping up "zombie firms" and distorting credit allocation rather than allowing orderly resolutions. Swagel maintained that such measures, despite flaws, mitigated broader economic fallout, as evidenced by TARP's ultimate profitability of approximately $15 billion in recovered funds by 2014, though detractors contended this obscured opportunity costs and long-term distortions in financial incentives.

CBO Methodologies and Partisan Accusations

The Congressional Budget Office (CBO), under Director Phillip Swagel from 2019 to 2025, employed standardized methodologies for projecting federal budgets and scoring legislation, including baseline estimates that assume current laws remain unchanged and incorporate demographic, economic, and technical factors. For major fiscal legislation, CBO conducted dynamic analyses incorporating macroeconomic feedback effects, such as how tax cuts or spending changes might influence GDP growth, employment, and revenues, as mandated by congressional rules since 2015 for bills with budgetary impacts exceeding 0.25% of GDP. Swagel emphasized transparency in these processes, with CBO publishing detailed assumptions and models, including overlapping generations models for long-term debt effects on entitlements like Social Security.
Partisan accusations against CBO's methodologies intensified during Swagel's tenure, particularly from Republicans critiquing analyses of tax reforms and spending cuts. In June 2025, following CBO's dynamic estimate of H.R. 1 (the "One Big Beautiful Bill Act"), which projected increased public debt to 124% of GDP by 2034 due to modest growth offsets from tax reductions, Republican lawmakers and the White House accused the agency of underestimating economic expansion and ignoring "record savings" from deregulation. President Trump claimed CBO was "run by Democrats," despite Swagel's Republican background and prior Treasury service under George W. Bush, arguing its static-like assumptions biased results against pro-growth policies. Swagel responded in congressional testimony and interviews, asserting that methodologies adhered to statutory requirements, used peer-reviewed models, and avoided policy advocacy, while noting CBO's historical accuracy in revenue forecasting.
Critics, including House Republicans, also targeted CBO's 2024 assessments of the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) extensions, alleging overly conservative growth projections failed to capture supply-side effects, prompting calls for expanded dynamic scoring to better reflect behavioral responses. In contrast, Democratic criticisms were less prominent under Swagel, though some historical precedents from the 1990s involved challenges to CBO's independence during deficit debates; defenders across parties, including in a July 2025 analysis, upheld CBO's nonpartisan hiring based on expertise rather than affiliation. Swagel maintained that such attacks risked eroding the agency's role as an impartial referee, with methodologies subject to bipartisan oversight and periodic review.

Bibliography

Major Works and Contributions

Swagel's academic research has focused on international economics, fiscal policy, and labor market dynamics, with over 30 publications garnering more than 1,500 citations. Key works include his 2002 paper "Tax Burden and Migration: A Political Economy Theory and Evidence," co-authored with Assaf Razin and Efraim Sadka, which examines how tax policies influence migration patterns and welfare state sustainability, earning 388 citations. In 2001, he contributed to NBER Working Paper No. 8405, "The Aging Population and the Size of the Welfare State," analyzing demographic pressures on public spending.
A prominent contribution to trade and outsourcing debates is his 2006 NBER Working Paper No. 12398, "The Politics and Economics of Offshore Outsourcing," which critiques political responses to globalization's labor shifts following the 2004 Economic Report of the President, arguing that such outsourcing enhances efficiency despite short-term disruptions. Swagel's policy-oriented writings include "The Financial Crisis: An Inside View" (2009), published in Brookings Papers on Economic Activity, detailing the U.S. Treasury's response to the 20072009 crisis from his vantage as Assistant Secretary for Economic Policy. He later expanded on institutional barriers in "Legal, Political, and Institutional Constraints on the Financial Crisis Policy Response" (2015), in the Journal of Economic Perspectives, highlighting delays in bailout measures due to legal and partisan factors.
As director of the Congressional Budget Office since June 2019, Swagel has overseen nonpartisan analyses of fiscal projections, including the 2025 Budget and Economic Outlook, which incorporates updated models for deficits, entitlements, and revenue under varying policy scenarios. His earlier policy roles, such as at the International Monetary Fund (19972001) and Council of Economic Advisers (20032006), informed empirical contributions to sovereign debt and exchange rate studies, though specific IMF reports remain internal. These works underscore Swagel's emphasis on evidence-based policy amid demographic and global economic shifts.
