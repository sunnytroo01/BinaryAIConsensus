Electricity is the flow of electric charge, and it is the lifeblood of modern civilization. Without electricity, there would be no lights, no computers, no phones, no internet, no refrigerators, no air conditioning, no medical equipment, no factories, no trains, and no modern economy. The average American uses approximately thirty kilowatt-hours of electricity per day, enough to power about thirty thousand one-hundred-watt light bulbs for one hour. Global electricity generation exceeds twenty-eight thousand terawatt-hours per year, produced by a vast infrastructure of power plants, transmission lines, transformers, and distribution networks that constitutes the largest machine humanity has ever built.

Electricity is a fundamental property of matter. All matter is made of atoms, and atoms contain positively charged protons in the nucleus and negatively charged electrons orbiting the nucleus. When electrons are free to move through a material, they constitute an electric current. Materials that allow electrons to flow easily, like copper, silver, and aluminum, are called conductors. Materials that resist the flow of electrons, like rubber, glass, and plastic, are called insulators. Semiconductors, like silicon and germanium, fall between conductors and insulators and can be engineered to control the flow of electricity with extraordinary precision, which is the basis of all modern electronics.

Electric current is measured in amperes. Voltage, measured in volts, is the force that pushes electrons through a circuit, analogous to the pressure in a water pipe. Resistance, measured in ohms, is the opposition to current flow, analogous to the friction in a pipe. These three quantities are related by Ohm's Law: voltage equals current times resistance. This simple equation is the foundation of all electrical engineering and circuit design.

The generation of electricity at a large scale became possible with Michael Faraday's discovery of electromagnetic induction in 1831. Faraday demonstrated that moving a magnet through a coil of wire induces an electric current in the wire. This principle is used in virtually every power plant in the world: a turbine spins a magnet inside a coil of wire, converting mechanical energy into electrical energy. The mechanical energy that spins the turbine can come from many sources: steam produced by burning coal, natural gas, or nuclear fuel; falling water in a hydroelectric dam; wind turning the blades of a wind turbine; or steam produced by concentrating sunlight.

Thomas Edison and Nikola Tesla engaged in a famous rivalry over the best way to distribute electricity. Edison championed direct current, or DC, in which electrons flow in one direction. Tesla championed alternating current, or AC, in which electrons reverse direction many times per second. Tesla's AC system won because alternating current can be easily transformed to different voltages using transformers: electricity is generated at moderate voltage, stepped up to high voltage for long-distance transmission, which reduces energy loss, and then stepped down to low voltage for safe use in homes and businesses. Today's electrical grid uses AC at a frequency of sixty hertz in North America and fifty hertz in most of the rest of the world.

Solar electricity is generated by photovoltaic cells, which convert sunlight directly into electricity using the photovoltaic effect discovered by Edmond Becquerel in 1839. When photons of light strike a semiconductor material, typically silicon, they knock electrons loose from their atoms, creating an electric current. Solar cell efficiency has improved from approximately six percent in the 1950s to over twenty-five percent for commercial panels today, and the cost of solar electricity has fallen by approximately ninety-nine percent since 1976, making it the cheapest source of new electricity in most of the world.

Wind electricity is generated by wind turbines, which use the kinetic energy of moving air to spin a rotor connected to an electric generator. Modern wind turbines can have rotor diameters exceeding five hundred feet and generate up to fifteen megawatts of electricity, enough to power approximately five thousand homes. Offshore wind farms, located in shallow coastal waters where winds are stronger and more consistent than on land, represent one of the fastest-growing sources of electricity globally.

Batteries store electrical energy chemically and release it on demand. Lithium-ion batteries, which power virtually every smartphone, laptop, and electric vehicle, work by shuttling lithium ions between two electrodes through an electrolyte. When the battery discharges, lithium ions flow from the anode to the cathode, releasing electrical energy. When the battery charges, the process reverses. Battery technology is improving rapidly, with energy density increasing and costs decreasing by approximately eighty-five percent over the past decade. Grid-scale battery storage is becoming increasingly important as solar and wind electricity, which are intermittent, become larger shares of the energy mix.

The electrical grid is one of the most remarkable engineering achievements of the twentieth century. It connects millions of generators to billions of loads across thousands of miles, maintaining a precise balance between electricity supply and demand in real time. If supply exceeds demand, the frequency of the AC current rises. If demand exceeds supply, the frequency falls. Grid operators must constantly adjust generation to match consumption, a task that becomes more complex as the share of variable renewable energy increases. Smart grids, which use digital communication and control technologies to manage electricity flows more efficiently, are being developed to meet this challenge.

Electricity is invisible, silent, and taken for granted until it disappears. A power outage instantly reminds you how dependent modern life is on a continuous supply of electricity. Food spoils. Communication stops. Transportation halts. Work ceases. Heating or cooling fails. Medical equipment shuts down. The 2003 Northeast blackout in the United States and Canada, which affected fifty-five million people for up to two days, demonstrated the cascading consequences of electrical grid failure.

The future of electricity is being shaped by three simultaneous transformations: decarbonization, as fossil fuel generation is replaced by renewable sources to address climate change; decentralization, as rooftop solar panels and local batteries enable households and businesses to generate and store their own electricity; and digitalization, as smart meters, sensors, and software optimize the generation, distribution, and consumption of electricity in real time.

Electricity is not just a technology. It is the nervous system of civilization. Every advancement in human welfare over the past century and a half, from antibiotics to air travel to artificial intelligence, has been enabled by the reliable, affordable, and abundant supply of electrical power. Understanding electricity is understanding the physical foundation on which modern life is built.
