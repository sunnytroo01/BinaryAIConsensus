Ficciones
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Ficciones is a landmark collection of short stories written by Argentine author Jorge Luis Borges and first published in 1944 by Editorial Sur in Buenos Aires. The book combines eight stories from Borges's earlier 1941 volume El jardn de senderos que se bifurcan with six new tales in a second section titled Artifices, totaling fourteen stories in the original edition; a 1956 revised edition added three more stories to the second part, bringing the total to seventeen. Translated into English in 1962 by multiple translators including Alastair Reid and Anthony Kerrigan, it introduced Borges's intricate, metaphysical narratives to a global audience.
The collection is structured into two parts, with the first exploring invented worlds, infinite libraries, and paradoxical authorship through stories like "Tln, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius," "The Library of Babel," "Pierre Menard, Author of the Quixote," and "The Garden of Forking Paths." The second part delves into memory, betrayal, and destiny in tales such as "Funes, the Memorious," "Death and the Compass," "The Secret Miracle," and, in the expanded edition, "The South" and "The Sect of the Phoenix." Borges's style blends essayistic precision with fantastical elements, often incorporating mirrors, labyrinths, and infinite regressions to probe philosophical questions about time, identity, reality, and the limits of human knowledge.
Ficciones is celebrated for pioneering a "new narrative" in Latin American literature, influencing magical realism and postmodern fiction through its intellectual depth and innovative form. It contributed to Borges's international acclaim, including the 1961 Formentor Prize, and remains a cornerstone of 20th-century speculative literature, admired for its economy, irony, and exploration of existential themes. The work's enduring impact is evident in its frequent anthologization and adaptation, underscoring Borges's role as a transformative figure in world literature.

Background

Publication History

Ficciones originated as a collection of eight short stories titled El jardn de senderos que se bifurcan, published in late 1941 by Editorial Sur in Buenos Aires. This initial volume marked a pivotal moment in Jorge Luis Borges's career, assembling pieces that had appeared earlier in literary journals like Sur.
In 1944, Borges expanded the collection into the full Ficciones, incorporating the eight stories from El jardn de senderos que se bifurcan and adding a new section, Artificios, with six additional stories, for a total of fourteen. Published again by Editorial Sur in Buenos Aires, this edition was dedicated to Esther Zemborain de Torres Duggan, a close friend and literary collaborator of Borges. The work emerged during World War II, a period when Argentina remained neutral, fostering a vibrant literary scene influenced by Surrealism and marked by Borges's close collaboration with Adolfo Bioy Casares on joint projects like detective fiction under pseudonyms.
By 1956, Ficciones underwent further revision in an edition published by Emec Editores, which added three stories"The End," "The Sect of the Phoenix," and "The South"to the Artificios section, expanding the total to seventeen stories. These stories had previously appeared in Sur magazine in the early 1950s. This version solidified the collection's structure and contributed to its international recognition, including the 1962 English translation.

Editions and Translations

The 1956 expanded edition of Ficciones, published by Emec Editores in Buenos Aires, augmented the original collection by adding three stories to the Artificios section"El fin," "La secta del Fnix," and "El Sur"which had previously appeared in Sur magazine in the early 1950s. This revision transformed the volume into a more comprehensive anthology of Borges's early fiction, totaling seventeen stories, and solidified its status as a cornerstone of his oeuvre.
The first complete English translation of Ficciones appeared in 1962 from Grove Press in New York, edited and introduced by Anthony Kerrigan, with key contributions from multiple translators including Donald A. Yates (who rendered "The Library of Babel") and James E. Irby (responsible for "The Garden of Forking Paths" and others). An earlier glimpse into Borges's work in English came with the 1948 translation of the individual story "The Garden of Forking Paths" by Anthony Boucher, published in Ellery Queen's Mystery Magazine.
Subsequent editions have sustained Ficciones' accessibility across languages and markets, including the 1974 Penguin Classics edition, which featured the established English translations in a widely distributed paperback format. In the 2020s, reprints such as those from New Directions have included new forewords emphasizing Borges's centennial influence on global literature, often revisiting the philosophical depth of the original texts.
Translating Ficciones into English and other languages involves significant challenges, particularly in capturing Borges's precise, allusive style, his invented linguistic constructs, and the dense interplay of philosophical ideas. For instance, in "Tln, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius," the fictional planet's languagedevoid of nouns and composed solely of verbs and adjectives to reflect an idealist worldviewrequires translators to convey abstract conceptual shifts without losing the story's pseudo-encyclopedic tone. Borges's frequent use of scholarly footnotes, blending real and fabricated references, further complicates fidelity, as does the need to preserve nuanced allusions to metaphysics and infinity that underpin the narratives. These elements demand translators balance literal accuracy with the evocative ambiguity central to Borges's prose.

Contents

Structure of the Collection

Ficciones is organized as a short story collection divided into two primary sections. The first section, titled El jardn de senderos que se bifurcan (The Garden of Forking Paths) in the original Spanish, encompasses eight stories originally compiled from the 1941 publication of the same name. The second section, Artificios (Artifices), includes six stories newly assembled for the 1944 edition, resulting in a total of fourteen pieces. This structure presents the narratives as a cohesive yet labyrinthine whole, with the parts named after key stories within them to underscore their interconnected design.
In the 1956 revised edition, three additional stories"El fin" ("The End"), "La secta del Fnix" ("The Sect of the Phoenix"), and "El Sur" ("The South")were incorporated into the Artificios section, expanding the collection to seventeen stories. These additions, previously published in literary magazines between 1952 and 1953, were retrofitted to maintain the volume's unified framework, drawing from Borges's ongoing explorations in periodicals like Sur. The overall format adheres to a traditional short story anthology, but with innovative elements such as prologues to each part that frame the content as deliberate constructs.
Borges's prologues highlight the artificiality of the works, likening the first section's stories to an "artificial garden" or "labyrinthine house" for reader navigation, while the second emphasizes their fabricated nature to challenge perceptions of authenticity. The stories interconnect thematically through shared motifs, such as labyrinths and infinite regressions, without rigid chronological or narrative sequencing, fostering a sense of an expansive, illusory universe. This thematic weaving is amplified by the presentation of many pieces as pseudo-nonfictional formsencyclopedia excerpts, literary reviews, or scholarly fragmentsthat deliberately blur the lines between invention and documented reality.
Extensive footnotes and fabricated bibliographic references further enhance this blurring, creating an illusion of scholarly depth and verifiability while satirizing academic conventions. These elements, appearing throughout the collection, serve not merely as annotations but as integral components that extend the fictional worlds and reinforce interconnections across stories, such as recurring allusions to invented texts or historical forgeries. In physical editions, the book adopts a compact, unadorned format typical of mid-20th-century literary volumes, prioritizing the textual intricacies over visual embellishments.

List of Stories

Ficciones comprises seventeen short stories, divided into two main parts added together in the 1944 edition, with some stories originally appearing in earlier publications or periodicals between 1935 and 1944.

Part One: The Garden of Forking Paths

Tln, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius: The narrator and a friend discover references to a fictional region called Uqbar in an altered encyclopedia volume, which leads them to uncover details about Tln, an invented planet created by a secret society whose idealistic philosophy begins to reshape reality on Earth.
The Approach to Al-Mu'tasim: The narrator reviews a detective novel by the fictional Indian author Mir Bahadur Ali, in which a law student murders a rival during a riot and seeks spiritual redemption by pursuing a shadowy figure named Al-Mu'tasim, gradually approaching him through encounters with increasingly enlightened individuals; the story first appeared in 1936 as part of Historia de la eternidad.
Pierre Menard, Author of the Quixote: In 1939, French writer Pierre Menard undertakes to rewrite select passages from Cervantes's Don Quixote not by copying but by recreating them through historical and stylistic transposition, resulting in text identical to the original yet interpreted as more profound and relevant to modern times.
The Circular Ruins: An aging man arrives at a ruined temple in a jungle to dream a son into perfect existence through successive refinements of his nightly visions, only to discover upon the temple's fiery destruction that he himself is the creation of another dreamer.
The Lottery in Babylon: The narrator recounts the evolution of Babylon's Company, which expands a simple lottery into a omnipotent system dictating every life event from birth to death through chance drawings, enforcing a fatalistic society where uncertainty reigns supreme.
An Examination of the Work of Herbert Quain: The narrator analyzes the oeuvre of the obscure Irish writer Herbert Quain, highlighting his experimental novel April March, which features a narrative with multiple possible endings and influences other fictional works, including a reference to a dream-created man.
The Library of Babel: In an endless universe composed of hexagonal galleries filled with identical volumes containing every possible combination of letters, the librarians of the Library of Babel search futilely for meaning amid chaos, leading to cycles of mania, suicide, and cultish belief in a hidden "Total Book."
The Garden of Forking Paths: During World War I, Chinese-German spy Dr. Yu Tsun races to transmit the location of an British artillery park to Berlin before his capture, arriving at the home of Stephen Albert, who deciphers his grandfather's labyrinthine novel as a metaphor for branching timelines.

Part Two: Artifices

Funes the Memorious: In 1887, the narrator meets Ireneo Funes, a young Uruguayan whose horseback accident grants him superhuman perception and memory, allowing him to perceive and recall every detail of the universe but rendering him immobile and isolated in time.
The Form of the Sword: An English settler in a remote Uruguayan house shows the narrator a facial scar from his past as Irish revolutionary Moon, recounting how he betrayed his comrade Duncan during the independence struggle, earning the mark that symbolizes his treachery.
Theme of the Traitor and the Hero: In 1929, Kilpatrick descendant Ryan investigates the 1824 assassination of Irish leader Fergus Kilpatrick, uncovering through dreams and documents that Kilpatrick orchestrated his own death as a traitor to inspire national unity, with the plot echoing Shakespeare's Julius Caesar.
Death and the Compass: Erik Lnnrot, a rationalist detective, pursues the murderer of Yarmolinsky through three symbolically placed crimes in the Villa of the Tetrarch, interpreting them via the Kabbalah only to be trapped and killed in a diamond-shaped labyrinth by criminal Red Scharlach.
The Secret Miracle: In March 1939, Bohemian playwright Jaromir Hladk petitions God for time to complete his unwritten verse tragedy The Enemies before his Nazi execution; granted a year in frozen time, he revises the final scene before the fatal shot resumes.
Three Versions of Judas: Swedish theologian Nils Runeberg publishes escalating heretical tracts arguing that the eternal redemption requires a supreme sacrifice, positing Judas Iscariotnot Jesusas the true incarnate God who betrays for divine necessity, leading to his institutionalization.
The End: In 1942 Buenos Aires, a one-armed gaucho performs a guitar ballad from Martn Fierro in a general store, sparking a duel with the paralyzed bartender Recavarren, who kills him in self-defense, concluding the epic cycle with fatal inevitability.
The Sect of the Phoenix: An anonymous commentator describes a timeless, worldwide secret society whose obscene ritualthe "Secret"ensures its endurance across generations, likened to eternal practices among Jews, gypsies, and Masons but immune to eradication.
The South: Librarian Dahlmann endures a fever from a fight, then travels south to his ranch, where an innocuous knife challenge from gauchos escalates into a deadly duel, fulfilling a mythic confrontation under the indifferent stars.

Style

Narrative Techniques

Borges frequently employs first-person narration in Ficciones, often through a character named "Borges" or other unreliable sources that blend elements of autobiography with pure invention, thereby destabilizing the boundary between author and narrator. In stories such as "Funes, el memorioso," the narrator explicitly identifies as Borges, drawing on personal anecdoteslike visits to relatives or encounters with enigmatic figuresto infuse the fiction with an air of authenticity while subtly undermining it through hyperbolic or impossible details. This technique, evident also in "El jardn de senderos que se bifurcan," where a Chinese spy recounts his confession, creates a subjective lens that invites readers to question the narrator's reliability and the veracity of the events described.
A hallmark of Borges' approach is the pseudo-scholarly tone, where stories are framed as translations, literary reviews, or discoveries of lost manuscripts, lending an erudite veneer to the invented narratives. For instance, "Pierre Menard, autor del Quijote" is presented as a bibliographic review cataloging the fictional author's works, complete with fabricated editions and scholarly annotations that mimic academic discourse. Similarly, "El acercamiento a Almotsim" adopts the form of a critical analysis of a nonexistent Indian novel, citing apocryphal sources and philosophical digressions to construct a labyrinth of intertextual references. This framing device, as seen in "Tln, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius" with its invented encyclopedia entries, enhances the illusion of historical or intellectual legitimacy while exposing the constructed nature of knowledge itself.
Borges' narratives often eschew linearity, embracing fragmentation and temporal manipulation to evoke infinite possibilities and disrupt conventional storytelling. In "El jardn de senderos que se bifurcan," the plot unfolds through branching paths that represent diverging futures, allowing multiple outcomes to coexist in a non-chronological structure that mirrors the labyrinthine novel within the story. Such techniques fragment the progression of events, as in "La biblioteca de Babel," where the endless hexagonal galleries defy sequential exploration, compelling readers to navigate a disoriented, eternal space.
Footnotes serve as a key mechanism in Borges' arsenal, expanding or subverting the main text to build layers of verisimilitude and irony. In "Pierre Menard, autor del Quijote," footnotes detail the protagonist's habits and unpublished works, feigning scholarly rigor while highlighting the absurdity of reauthoring Cervantes. Likewise, in "El acercamiento a Almotsim," a pivotal footnote redefines the narrative's resolution, suggesting the seeker's guide is the divine figure himself and thus complicating the story's closure with meta-commentary. These annotations not only enrich the fictional world but also parody academic excess, drawing readers into a recursive interplay between text and paratext.

Literary Devices

Borges employs intertextuality as a core literary device in Ficciones, creating a web of references to both real and invented texts, authors, and philosophies that underscores the artificiality of narrative construction. In stories such as "Tln, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius," he fabricates an encyclopedia entry from a nonexistent volume that gradually reshapes reality, blending historical figures like Berkeley with fictional ones to question the boundaries of knowledge and invention. Similarly, "The Approach to Al-Mu'tasim" alludes to a fabricated Indian novel that influences its readers profoundly, parodying the chain of literary influence while citing actual mystical traditions. This device not only enriches the intellectual texture but also invites readers to navigate a labyrinth of sources, real and imagined.
Irony and parody further amplify the collection's self-reflexive quality, with Borges mocking established literary forms and conventions to reveal their absurdities. A prime example is "Pierre Menard, Author of the Quixote," where the titular character's verbatim reproduction of Cervantes' Don Quixote is presented as an original masterpiece, ironically subverting notions of authorship, originality, and textual fidelity through exaggerated scholarly apparatus. In "The South," subtle ironic reversals between dream and reality parody the realist tradition, transforming mundane events into metaphysical enigmas without overt resolution. These techniques highlight Borges' playful critique of literature's pretensions, employing parody to expose the constructed nature of meaning.
Paradox and oxymoron permeate Ficciones, challenging logical coherence and exploring the limits of human cognition through contradictory concepts. The infinite Library in "The Library of Babel" embodies this, an oxymoronic universe of hexagonal galleries holding every possible bookthus containing all truths and falsehoodsyet rendering search futile and existence absurd. In "The Garden of Forking Paths," time branches infinitely like a paradoxical labyrinth, where a single moment encompasses all outcomes, defying linear causality. Such devices create intellectual tension, using logical impossibilities to probe deeper philosophical inquiries within concise narratives.
Borges' economy of language enhances these devices, favoring sparse, precise prose laden with allusions over descriptive excess, which amplifies the artificial and cerebral tone of the stories. Avoiding naturalistic details, he packs dense intellectual content into brief forms, as in "Funes the Memorious," where the protagonist's total recall paradoxically cripples perception, conveyed through minimal yet evocative phrasing. This stylistic restraint rejects traditional novelistic verbosity, prioritizing conceptual clarity and reader engagement with abstract ideas.

Themes

Philosophical Concepts

In Jorge Luis Borges's Ficciones, the story "Tln, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius" delves into idealism and solipsism by depicting a fictional world, Tln, where inhabitants adhere to a Berkeleyan philosophy positing that reality exists only as perceptions in minds, with no independent material objects. This idealist framework manifests as a collective idealism that repudiates solipsism, where the planet's metaphysics denies the external world's autonomy, leading to a gradual encroachment on Earth as Tln's ideas infiltrate and reshape tangible reality through encyclopedic propagation. Borges draws explicitly from George Berkeley's A Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge (1710), using Tln to illustrate how sustained belief can fabricate existence, blurring the boundary between subjective thought and objective fact.
The collection further examines time and eternity through non-linear conceptions that challenge chronological progression. In "The Garden of Forking Paths," Borges presents time as a labyrinthine structure of branching possibilities, where every decision spawns parallel realities, rejecting linear causality in favor of an eternal, multifaceted existence that encompasses all outcomes simultaneously. This view aligns with metaphysical explorations of eternity as an infinite web of instants, allowing for the coexistence of contradictory events without resolution. Similarly, "The Theologians" portrays time as cyclical and redemptive, where historical figures endure eternal recurrence through theological disputes that loop across ages, emphasizing eternity not as endless duration but as repetitive patterns that transcend individual mortality.
Borges interrogates identity and authorship in "Pierre Menard, Author of the Quixote," where the protagonist re-creates portions of Cervantes's Don Quixote verbatim, yet produces a philosophically distinct work due to differing historical and interpretive contexts. This paradox underscores that a text's identity derives not solely from its words but from the author's situated perspective, challenging notions of originality and fixed meaning in literature. By equating Menard's effort with Cervantes's, Borges critiques traditional authorship as an illusion, suggesting that reinterpretation by later minds inherently alters the essence of any creation.
The theme of infinity and the infinite reaches its zenith in "The Library of Babel," which envisions a boundless archive containing every possible arrangement of letters, embodying the paradox of total knowledge amid universal chaos. This hexagonal cosmos, with its 410-page books in a finite alphabet, generates an effectively infinite repository that includes all truths but drowns them in incomprehensible gibberish, highlighting the epistemological futility of seeking meaning in boundless multiplicity. Borges thus probes infinity as both exhaustive and nullifying, where the aspiration for complete understanding confronts the vertigo of endless, indifferent permutations.

Motifs and Symbols

In Jorge Luis Borges's Ficciones, recurring motifs and symbols such as labyrinths, mirrors, doubles, books, libraries, and games deepen the collection's exploration of intellectual complexity, often evoking themes of infinity and human limitation. These elements function not merely as decorative imagery but as structural devices that mirror the intricate, paradoxical nature of reality and fiction themselves. Scholars have noted how Borges weaves these symbols to create a tapestry of interconnected ideas, where everyday objects become emblems of profound existential inquiries.
The labyrinth stands as one of the most prominent motifs in Ficciones, symbolizing the bewildering complexity of existence and the multiplicity of choices within it. It represents a maze-like structure of paths that defy linear progression, embodying the disorientation of navigating unknown intellectual or narrative spaces. As Ethan Weed observes, the labyrinth in Borges's work functions as a "labyrinth of symbols and ideas" without a discernible plan accessible to the wanderer, highlighting the perplexity inherent in decision-making and interpretation. This motif underscores the infinite branching possibilities of thought and action, reinforcing the collection's emphasis on non-deterministic structures.
Mirrors and doubles recur as symbols of duality and infinite replication, capturing the tension between self and other, reality and its reflection. Mirrors evoke an endless regression of images, signifying the illusory nature of identity and the proliferation of alternate selves. In Borges's fiction, they often provoke a sense of otherness and isolation, as the reflected image challenges the viewer's sense of uniqueness. Doubles extend this symbolism to embody internal conflict and multiplicity, where characters confront versions of themselves that blur the boundaries between life and death, original and copy. This duality amplifies the intellectual depth by questioning the stability of perception and existence.
Books and libraries emerge as central symbols of boundless knowledge and entrapment, portraying vast repositories that both promise and withhold understanding. The library motif, in particular, depicts an infinite archive containing every possible combination of words, akin to a universe of information that imprisons its seekers in futility. It symbolizes the paradox of total accessibilityholding solutions to all queriesjuxtaposed against the chaos of incomprehensibility, where no catalog or order renders meaning elusive. As analyzed in scholarly work, this image reflects a solipsistic confinement, where the pursuit of truth becomes an eternal, disorienting quest within structured yet anarchic confines.
Games and lotteries serve as motifs for the interplay between chance and predestination, illustrating deterministic systems masquerading as randomness. The lottery, in particular, symbolizes a cosmic mechanism that governs all outcomes, from fortune to ruin, suggesting a universe where fate is both arbitrary and inexorably controlled by unseen forces. This emblem critiques the illusion of free will, portraying life as a rigged game where every event is preordained, yet perceived through the veil of probability. Such symbols reinforce the collection's probing of causality, where chance emerges as a profound intellectual construct.

Reception and Legacy

Initial Critical Reception

Upon its publication in 1944 by Editorial Sur, Ficciones was acclaimed by the Argentine literary group associated with the magazine Sur, where several of its stories had first appeared between 1939 and 1942, for its groundbreaking innovations in short fiction that transcended Borges's earlier Ultraist poetry phase. Critics within this circle viewed the collection as a pivotal breakthrough, blending philosophical inquiry with narrative experimentation in stories like "Tln, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius," establishing Borges as a central figure in Argentine modernism.
Internationally, the 1962 English translation published by Grove Press propelled Ficciones to global prominence, catapulting Borges into widespread recognition and solidifying his reputation for metaphysical fiction comparable to that of Franz Kafka and James Joyce in its probing of reality, infinity, and textual illusion. Adolfo Bioy Casares, Borges's lifelong friend and frequent collaborator, enthusiastically endorsed the collection in their private discussions and joint projects, praising its intellectual depth and stylistic precision as exemplary of innovative prose. The work's influence was further evidenced by multiple editions throughout the 1940s to 1970s, reflecting steady sales and growing readership in both Spanish and translated versions.
Despite Ficciones' profound impact, Borges faced repeated Nobel Prize in Literature nominations from the 1960s onward but was ultimately snubbed each time, a decision later attributed to political controversies and the Swedish Academy's preferences, even as the collection laid the groundwork for his enduring acclaim, culminating in its 1999 inclusion among Le Monde's 100 Books of the Century.

Modern Interpretations and Influence

Since the late 1990s, scholarly interpretations of Ficciones have increasingly incorporated postcolonial frameworks, examining how Borges's narratives challenge colonial legacies and hybrid identities in Latin American literature. For instance, a 2021 dissertation from Duquesne University analyzes Borges's works, including stories from Ficciones, as hermeneutically provocative for philosophy of communication, highlighting their role in deconstructing power structures and cultural hybridity. Similarly, a 2021 study explores Jewishness and postcoloniality in Borges's fiction, positioning his portrayals of marginal identities as allegories for Argentine postcolonial artists navigating European influences.
In the digital age, interpretations of infinity in Ficciones have drawn parallels to artificial intelligence and vast data landscapes, particularly through "The Library of Babel." A 2023 arXiv preprint titled "Borges and AI" argues that Borges's infinite library anticipates the hallucinatory outputs of language models, offering insights into how AI blurs fiction and reality in ways that echo the story's themes of exhaustive possibility. Complementing this, a 2024 essay in The Scholarly Kitchen posits "The Library of Babel" as a metaphor for navigating AI-generated information's veracity challenges, emphasizing its relevance to contemporary information overload.
Ficciones has profoundly shaped postmodern literature and science fiction, influencing narrative experimentation with reality and simulation. Borges's metafictional techniques in the collection are credited with pioneering postmodern intertextuality and unreliable narration, as noted in a 2014 BBC Culture analysis of his impact on 20th-century writing. In science fiction, this extends to television; the 2020 book Borges and Black Mirror details how episodes like "White Christmas" and "Hated in the Nation" adapt Borges's motifs of simulated worlds and ethical dilemmas from stories such as "Tln, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius." Additionally, Norwegian author Karl Ove Knausgrd has praised "Tln, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius" as the greatest short story ever written, citing its philosophical depth in a 2019 interview that underscores its enduring influence on contemporary fiction.
While direct full adaptations of Ficciones remain rare, its motifs have indirectly inspired visual media. Christopher Nolan's 2010 film Inception draws on Borges's concepts of infinite realities and dream layers, particularly from "The Garden of Forking Paths," as explored in a 2021 academic paper analyzing mirrors and consciousness in both works. More recently, the 2024 feature film Borges and Me, directed by Marc Turtletaub and starring Alan Cumming, echoes motifs of labyrinthine identity and blindness from Ficciones through its biographical lens on the author's life.
Recent scholarship addresses lingering gaps in Ficciones' legacy within Latin American studies. Furthermore, 2023 publications have linked Borges's infinite regressions to quantum physics analogies in literature; William Egginton's The Rigor of Angels connects Borges's ideas in Ficciones to Heisenberg's uncertainty principle, portraying the collection as a literary precursor to quantum interpretations of reality and free will.
