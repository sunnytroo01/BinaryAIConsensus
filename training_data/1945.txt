1945

1x
 1945 was the final year of the Second World War, culminating in the unconditional surrender of Nazi Germany on May 8known as Victory in Europe (VE) Dayafter Adolf Hitler's suicide on April 30, and the formal capitulation of Imperial Japan on September 2 aboard the USS Missouri in Tokyo Bay, following the United States' atomic bombings of Hiroshima on August 6 and Nagasaki on August 9.
The year began with Allied conferences shaping the postwar order, including the Yalta meeting in February among Winston Churchill, Franklin D. Roosevelt, and Joseph Stalin to discuss Germany's division and the establishment of the United Nations, followed by the Potsdam Conference in July and August under Harry S. Truman, Churchill (later Clement Attlee), and Stalin to address Japan's surrender terms and European reconstruction. Intense Pacific campaigns, such as the Battle of Okinawa ending in June, preceded the atomic strikes that prompted Japan's decision to end hostilities, avoiding a costly Allied invasion projected to cause massive casualties on both sides. In Europe, Soviet and Western Allied forces liberated Nazi concentration camps, revealing the Holocaust's scale, while the deaths of Roosevelt on April 12 and Mussolini on April 28 underscored the era's profound leadership transitions.
These events not only terminated the global conflict that had claimed over 70 million lives but also initiated the Cold War divide, with the United States and Soviet Union emerging as superpowers amid the onset of nuclear deterrence and decolonization pressures. The year's military and diplomatic milestones defined the transition from total war to a bipolar world, influencing international institutions like the United Nations, chartered on October 24.

Events

January

In January 1945, Allied forces pressed their advantages across multiple fronts in the final stages of World War II. On the Western Front, U.S. and British troops concluded the Battle of the Bulge through counteroffensives that forced German retreats, depleting Nazi reserves and ending the engagement on January 25. Concurrently, a secondary German operation, Nordwind, launched on January 3 in Alsace-Lorraine, aimed to divert Allied resources but gained limited ground against U.S. defenses.
The Eastern Front saw the Soviet Red Army initiate the VistulaOder Offensive on January 12, deploying approximately 2 million troops from bridgeheads along the Vistula River in Poland. This rapid advance liberated Warsaw by January 17 and Krakw shortly thereafter, pushing German forces back over 300 miles to the Oder River by month's end and positioning the Soviets for the assault on Berlin. As Soviet units approached, SS guards at Auschwitz began evacuating the camp complex on January 17, forcing nearly 60,000 prisoners on death marches westward into Germany; those unfit to marchabout 7,000were left behind. Soviet forces entered Auschwitz on January 27, liberating the remaining survivors and exposing the scale of Nazi atrocities, including gas chambers and crematoria.
In the Pacific theater, the U.S. Sixth Army, under General Douglas MacArthur, invaded Luzon in the Philippines on January 9, landing 150,000 troops at Lingayen Gulf with minimal initial opposition but facing subsequent Japanese counterattacks. This operation aimed to recapture Manila and sever Japanese supply lines, marking a key step in the liberation of the archipelago. Elsewhere, British forces in Burma completed the occupation of Akyab Island on January 4, while Chinese and Allied troops linked the Ledo Road supply route on January 27, enhancing overland logistics to China.
On January 20, U.S. President Franklin D. Roosevelt was inaugurated for his fourth term in a brief wartime ceremony, emphasizing continued resolve against the Axis powers. In Hungary, a provisional government signed an armistice with the Soviet Union, United States, and Britain on the same day, effectively ending organized resistance there.

February

The Yalta Conference convened from February 4 to 11 in the Crimean resort town of Yalta, where U.S. President Franklin D. Roosevelt, British Prime Minister Winston Churchill, and Soviet Premier Joseph Stalin coordinated strategies for defeating Nazi Germany and shaping the postwar order. Agreements reached included the division of Germany into four occupation zones (with France receiving one), the establishment of a United Nations organization with a Security Council veto for permanent members, Soviet entry into the war against Japan three months after Germany's defeat in exchange for territorial concessions in Asia, and commitments to free and unfettered elections in liberated Eastern European countries, particularly Poland, whose borders were adjusted to include German territories east of the Oder River. 
On the Eastern Front, Soviet forces captured Budapest on February 13 following a two-month siege that resulted in heavy casualties on both sides, with German and Hungarian defenders suffering approximately 38,000 killed and 138,000 captured, while Soviet losses exceeded 80,000. This victory facilitated the Red Army's Vistula-Oder Offensive, which by late February brought Soviet troops to the Oder River, within 40 miles of Berlin, threatening the German heartland.
In the Western Allies' air campaign, British Royal Air Force bombers conducted raids on Dresden starting the night of February 13, followed by U.S. Army Air Forces daylight attacks on February 14 and 15, dropping over 3,900 tons of high-explosive and incendiary bombs that ignited a firestorm devastating the city's historic center and causing an estimated 22,700 to 25,000 civilian deaths, though Dresden served as a rail and communications hub supporting German troop movements. 
U.S. forces initiated the Battle of Manila on February 3, with American and Filipino troops entering the Philippine capital and engaging Japanese defenders in intense house-to-house fighting that lasted until early March, resulting in over 100,000 Japanese deaths and widespread destruction of the city, including cultural sites. In the Pacific, the Battle of Iwo Jima commenced on February 19 when approximately 70,000 U.S. Marines landed on the volcanic island under heavy Japanese fire from entrenched positions, securing beachheads amid casualties that would total nearly 7,000 Marines killed and 20,000 wounded by the campaign's end, with the island's airfields enabling P-51 fighter escorts for B-29 Superfortress bombers targeting Japan. 
On the Western Front, U.S. First and Ninth Armies launched Operation Grenade on February 23, crossing the Roer River after flooding subsided, advancing toward the Rhine River and capturing key industrial areas in the Ruhr Valley.

March

The Battle of Iwo Jima, which began on February 19, continued into March with intense fighting as U.S. Marines secured the island against entrenched Japanese defenders. By March 26, the island was declared secure, though pockets of resistance persisted; U.S. forces suffered approximately 26,000 casualties, including nearly 7,000 killed, while Japanese losses exceeded 20,000 dead.
On the Eastern Front, German forces launched Operation Spring Awakening from March 6 to 15 in western Hungary, aiming to recapture oil fields and relieve Budapest, but the offensive collapsed under Soviet counterattacks, marking Adolf Hitler's last major initiative.
In Western Europe, U.S. First Army troops captured Cologne on March 6 amid the ongoing Ruhr encirclement. On March 7, the U.S. Ninth Armored Division unexpectedly seized the intact Ludendorff Bridge over the Rhine at Remagen, enabling the first Allied crossing and accelerating the advance into Germany despite German demolition attempts.
The most destructive single air raid of the war occurred on the night of March 910, when 334 U.S. B-29 Superfortresses conducted low-level firebombing of Tokyo under Operation Meetinghouse, dropping incendiaries that ignited firestorms killing an estimated 80,000100,000 civilians and destroying 16 square miles of the city.
Soviet forces, following their JanuaryFebruary VistulaOder Offensive, consolidated positions along the Oder River in March, enlarging bridgeheads and preparing further advances toward Berlin.
British and Canadian forces under Operation Plunder crossed the Rhine on March 2324 near Rees and Wesel, supported by airborne Operation Varsity, breaching the last major natural barrier to Germany's industrial heartland.
In the China-Burma-India theater, Chinese forces of the U.S.-supported X Force retook Lashio on March 67, reopening key supply routes. The Royal Air Force employed the 22,000-pound Grand Slam earthquake bomb for the first time on March 14 against the Bielefeld viaduct, disrupting German logistics. The last V-1 flying bomb struck Britain on March 29.

April

On April 1, United States forces initiated Operation Iceberg, launching the Battle of Okinawa, a major amphibious assault aimed at capturing the island as a staging point for the planned invasion of Japan's home islands. The operation involved over 1,800 ships and faced fierce Japanese resistance, including kamikaze attacks that inflicted heavy casualties on Allied naval forces.
On April 7, the Imperial Japanese Navy's battleship Yamato, the world's largest battleship displacing over 72,000 tons, was sunk during Operation Ten-Go, a desperate suicide mission to attack Allied forces off Okinawa. Spotted by U.S. submarines and aircraft, Yamato and its escorting destroyers were overwhelmed by approximately 386 American carrier-based aircraft, resulting in the loss of the battleship after multiple torpedo and bomb hits caused it to capsize and explode; over 2,400 Japanese sailors perished in the engagement.
In Europe, Allied forces continued their advance into Germany. On April 11, U.S. Army units liberated Buchenwald concentration camp near Weimar, encountering over 20,000 surviving prisoners amid evidence of systematic extermination and slave labor. The following day, April 12, U.S. President Franklin D. Roosevelt died of a cerebral hemorrhage at the Little White House in Warm Springs, Georgia, while in his fourth term; Vice President Harry S. Truman was sworn in as president hours later.
Soviet forces captured Vienna on April 13, further eroding Axis control in Central Europe. The Battle of Berlin commenced on April 16 with a massive Soviet offensive involving over 2.5 million troops and 6,250 tanks, encircling the German capital after breaching defenses at the Seelow Heights despite heavy losses.
By April 18, German forces in the Ruhr Pocket surrendered to Allied troops, yielding approximately 370,000 prisoners and marking the collapse of organized resistance in western Germany. On April 25, U.S. and Soviet armies linked up at the Elbe River near Torgau, effectively cutting Germany in two and hastening the Nazi regime's end.
Italian communist partisans captured and executed Benito Mussolini, along with his mistress Clara Petacci and several ministers, on April 28 near Lake Como as he attempted to flee to Switzerland; their bodies were subsequently displayed upside down in Milan. The same day, U.S. forces liberated Dachau concentration camp, revealing further atrocities including mass graves and emaciated survivors.
On April 30, Adolf Hitler committed suicide by gunshot in his Berlin Fhrerbunker as Soviet troops closed in, with his body burned alongside that of Eva Braun; this act precipitated the final collapse of Nazi leadership and the unconditional surrender of German forces days later.

May

On May 2, the Battle of Berlin concluded with the surrender of the city's garrison to Soviet forces, following intense urban combat that resulted in over 80,000 Red Army casualties and the deaths of approximately 300,000 Berliners amid the ruins. Soviet troops raised their flag over the Reichstag building, symbolizing the fall of Nazi Germany's capital.
May 3: British RAF aircraft sank the German liner Cap Arcona and other vessels in the Bay of Lbeck, inadvertently killing around 8,000 concentration camp prisoners who had been evacuated onto the ships.
May 4: German forces in northern Germany, the Netherlands, and Denmark surrendered en masse to Field Marshal Bernard Montgomery at Lneburg Heath.
May 5: United States Army's 11th Armored Division liberated the Mauthausen concentration camp in Austria, where nearly 200,000 prisoners had been held since 1938, with at least 95,000 deaths attributed to brutal conditions including forced labor in granite quarries. In Prague, Czech partisans initiated an uprising against remaining German troops.
May 7: At 2:41 a.m. in Reims, France, General Alfred Jodl signed the unconditional surrender of all German armed forces to the Allies, effective at 11:01 p.m. that day; the document stipulated cessation of hostilities across all fronts. Concurrently, the Red Army captured the besieged city of Breslau after 82 days.
May 8: Designated Victory in Europe Day (VE Day), Allied leaders announced the end of hostilities in Europe following the German surrender; in Berlin, Field Marshal Wilhelm Keitel signed a second instrument for Soviet satisfaction. Celebrations erupted worldwide, including street parties and parades in London where Prime Minister Winston Churchill addressed crowds from Whitehall and the Royal Family appeared on the Buckingham Palace balcony; similar festivities occurred in New York and Paris, though the war against Japan persisted. President Harry S. Truman described the moment as "a solemn but glorious hour."
In the Pacific theater, fighting continued on Okinawa, where U.S. troops, upon hearing of VE Day, maintained operations amid heavy casualties from Japanese resistance.

June

The Battle of Okinawa, the largest amphibious assault in the Pacific Theater of World War II, concluded on June 22, 1945, after 82 days of intense fighting, with U.S. forces declaring organized Japanese resistance ended following the suicide of Admiral Minoru ta on June 17 and the death of Lieutenant General Mitsuru Ushijima on June 22. The campaign, part of Operation Iceberg, involved over 60,000 U.S. troops landing on April 1 and facing fanatical Japanese defenses, including kamikaze attacks that sank or damaged dozens of Allied ships; total U.S. casualties exceeded 49,000, including more than 12,500 killed, while Japanese losses surpassed 100,000 dead, with most troops fighting to the death rather than surrendering. This victory secured airfields for potential operations against Japan's home islands but highlighted the high cost of island-hopping, influencing Allied decisions toward atomic bombing and blockade strategies to force surrender without a full invasion.
On June 5, the United States, United Kingdom, Soviet Union, and France issued the Berlin Declaration, formally assuming supreme authority over all Germany as a single economic unit, vesting legislative, executive, and judicial powers in the Allied Control Council, and affirming Germany's unconditional surrender while prohibiting any German peace treaties without Allied consent. This declaration marked the legal framework for Allied occupation and denazification, dividing Germany into four zones but treating it as undivided for reparations and reconstruction purposes pending a future peace settlement.
The United Nations Charter was signed on June 26, 1945, by representatives of 50 nations in San Francisco's (/page/War Memorial Opera House), concluding the United Nations Conference on International Organization that began April 25; the document established the UN's structure, purposes for maintaining peace, and principles of sovereign equality among members, entering into force October 24 after ratification by the five permanent Security Council members and a majority of signatories. Ratified amid wartime momentum, the Charter reflected compromises on veto powers for major Allies and aims to prevent future aggressions through collective security, though its effectiveness would later depend on superpower cooperation.
Other developments included the U.S. initiation of Operation Paperclip, covertly recruiting over 1,600 German scientists and engineers, including Wernher von Braun, to leverage Nazi technical expertise for American postwar programs despite their prior involvement in forced labor and weapons development; this began gaining momentum in June following initial approvals in May. In the Pacific, sporadic kamikaze strikes continued, such as the sinking of the USS William D. Porter on June 10 off Okinawa, underscoring persistent Japanese desperation tactics.

July

On July 16, 1945, the United States conducted the Trinity test, the world's first detonation of a nuclear weapon, at the Alamogordo Bombing and Gunnery Range in New Mexico. The plutonium implosion device, code-named "Gadget," exploded at 5:29 a.m. local time with a yield of approximately 21 kilotons of TNT, creating a fireball and shockwave that confirmed the feasibility of atomic bombs for use against Japan. This event, part of the Manhattan Project, marked the dawn of the nuclear age and directly influenced subsequent decisions on deploying the weapon.
The Potsdam Conference commenced on July 17, 1945, in Potsdam, Germany, bringing together U.S. President Harry S. Truman, British Prime Minister Winston Churchill (later replaced by Clement Attlee following the UK general election), and Soviet Premier Joseph Stalin to negotiate postwar arrangements for Europe and the ongoing war against Japan. Key discussions addressed the administration of defeated Germany, including its demilitarization, denazification, and division into occupation zones, as well as reparations and the prosecution of war criminals. On July 24, Truman informally informed Stalin of the successful atomic bomb test, describing it as a weapon of extraordinary power, though without technical details; Stalin, already aware through espionage, responded noncommittally.
The conference produced the Potsdam Agreement on July 26, outlining principles for German governance, and the Potsdam Declaration on the same day, issued by the U.S., UK, and China, demanding Japan's unconditional surrender and warning of "prompt and utter destruction" if refused, setting the stage for the atomic bombings. Tensions arose over Soviet expansion in Eastern Europe and differing visions for Poland's borders, foreshadowing Cold War divisions, yet the Allies reaffirmed unity against Japan.
On July 30, 1945, the USS Indianapolis, having delivered components of the atomic bombs to Tinian, was torpedoed and sunk by the Japanese submarine I-58 in the Philippine Sea, resulting in 880 men entering shark-infested waters; only 316 survived after four days, marking one of the worst naval disasters in U.S. history due to delayed rescue efforts. This incident underscored the perils of Pacific operations even as Japan's defeat loomed.

August

On August 2, the Potsdam Conference concluded after negotiations among the United States, the Soviet Union, and the United Kingdom on postwar arrangements for Europe and Asia, issuing the Potsdam Declaration demanding Japan's unconditional surrender. The declaration warned of "prompt and utter destruction" if Japan did not comply, though it omitted specific mention of the emperor's status.
On August 6, the United States dropped the atomic bomb "Little Boy" on Hiroshima from the B-29 bomber Enola Gay, detonating at an altitude of approximately 1,900 feet over the city center and destroying about 70% of its buildings. The explosion killed an estimated 66,000 people immediately, with total fatalities reaching 140,000 by the end of 1945 due to blast effects, burns, and radiation. Hiroshima, a military and industrial hub with over 250,000 residents, had been selected as a target to maximize psychological impact on Japanese leadership.
On August 8, the Soviet Union declared war on Japan, honoring its Yalta Agreement commitment, and launched Operation August Storm, invading Japanese-occupied Manchuria with over 1.5 million troops, 5,000 tanks, and 3,700 aircraft against the depleted Kwantung Army. The offensive, coordinated across multiple fronts, rapidly overwhelmed Japanese defenses, capturing key cities and leading to the surrender of approximately 600,000 Japanese troops by mid-August.
On August 9, a second atomic bomb, "Fat Man," was dropped on Nagasaki, killing about 39,000 immediately and causing total deaths of up to 74,000 by year's end, though the intended target of Kokura was spared due to cloud cover. That same day, Soviet forces continued advances in Manchuria, isolating Japanese holdings in Korea and Sakhalin.
Facing these shocks, Japan's Supreme War Council deadlocked, but Emperor Hirohito intervened on August 10, directing acceptance of the Potsdam terms with the caveat that his sovereignty be preserved. After Allied clarification on August 12 that the emperor's role would be subject to the supreme commander's authority, Japan agreed unconditionally on August 14. Hirohito announced the surrender to the Japanese people via radio broadcast on August 15 (known as V-J Day in Allied nations), marking the effective end of World War II hostilities. The formal instrument of surrender was signed on September 2 aboard the USS Missouri.

September

!float-right
On September 2, 1945, representatives of the Empire of Japan signed the instrument of surrender aboard the USS Missouri in Tokyo Bay, formally ending World War II. The ceremony, attended by Allied commanders including General Douglas MacArthur, who acted as supreme commander, marked the unconditional capitulation following the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki and the Soviet declaration of war. This event concluded hostilities that had begun in 1939 in Europe and 1941 in the Pacific, with Japan agreeing to Allied occupation and demilitarization.
Immediately following the surrender, General MacArthur assumed command of the Supreme Commander for the Allied Powers (SCAP), initiating the occupation and reconstruction of Japan. Allied forces began disarmament of Japanese military units and repatriation efforts, while preparations for war crimes trials commenced, targeting leaders like former Prime Minister Hideki Tojo. On September 11, Tojo attempted suicide by gunshot upon arrest by American forces but survived and was later prosecuted.
In Norway, Vidkun Quisling, the collaborationist prime minister during the Nazi occupation, was sentenced to death for treason on September 24, exemplifying the Allied pursuit of justice against Axis collaborators. These proceedings underscored the shift from wartime operations to postwar accountability across Europe and Asia. Meanwhile, in Canada, Soviet embassy cipher clerk Igor Gouzenko defected on September 5, revealing extensive espionage networks that accelerated emerging Cold War tensions, though the full implications unfolded later.

October

On October 8, Rudolf Hess, Adolf Hitler's former deputy, was transferred from British custody to Nuremberg, Germany, to face impending war crimes charges as part of Allied efforts to prosecute Nazi leadership.
Preparations for the International Military Tribunal advanced rapidly, with the indictment against 24 high-ranking Nazi officialsincluding Hermann Gring, Rudolf Hess, and Joachim von Ribbentroplodged with the tribunal on October 18 in Berlin, and German-language copies served to defendants on October 19. U.S. prosecutors, including a team of female lawyers led by figures such as Mary Margaret McKay, had relocated to Nuremberg by early October to compile evidence of atrocities, setting the stage for the trial's opening on November 20.
The establishment of the United Nations marked the month's most pivotal development in global governance. On October 24, the UN Charter entered into force after ratification by the five permanent members of the Security CouncilChina, France, the Soviet Union, the United Kingdom, and the United Statesalong with 24 other nations, totaling 29 signatories and fulfilling the threshold for activation. This formalized the organization as a successor to the failed League of Nations, aimed at preventing future conflicts through collective security and diplomacy, with headquarters later established in New York.
In Southeast Asia, the Indonesian National Revolution gained momentum against returning Dutch colonial forces. British troops, tasked with disarming Japanese forces and maintaining order post-surrender, entered Java in early October, but encountered growing resistance from Indonesian nationalists seeking to enforce their August 17 independence declaration. On October 29, Indonesian fighters in Makassar, Celebes (Sulawesi), attacked Dutch positions and seized a local radio station to broadcast pro-independence messages, escalating clashes that foreshadowed larger battles like Surabaya in November.
Elsewhere, post-war repatriations and reconstructions proceeded amid demobilization. Allied forces continued overseeing the occupation of Japan and Germany, with millions of troops shipping home; for instance, U.S. heavyweight boxing champion Joe Louis was honorably discharged from the Army on October 1 after serving in exhibition bouts to boost morale. In Europe, General Dwight D. Eisenhower visited The Hague on October 2 to inspect liberated areas and coordinate relief efforts. These events reflected the shift from wartime mobilization to stabilizing fractured societies and addressing colonial transitions.

November

On November 10, intense fighting broke out in Surabaya, Indonesia, between pro-independence nationalists and Dutch colonial forces seeking to restore pre-war authority following Japan's defeat, marking a pivotal escalation in the Indonesian National Revolution with thousands of casualties on both sides and galvanizing anti-colonial sentiment across the archipelago.
The International Military Tribunal for the Far East had not yet convened, but in Europe, the Nuremberg trials opened on November 20 in the Palace of Justice, prosecuting 24 senior Nazi leadersincluding Hermann Gring, Rudolf Hess, and Joachim von Ribbentropfor planning aggressive war, war crimes, and crimes against humanity under the London Charter of 1945, with proceedings led by Allied prosecutors and judges from the US, UK, USSR, and France. The trial's evidence, drawn from captured German documents and witness testimonies, exposed the systematic nature of Nazi atrocities, resulting in 19 convictions by October 1946, though critics later debated the victors' justice aspect given the exclusion of Soviet crimes.
Elsewhere, Charles de Gaulle assumed the presidency of France's provisional government on November 13, steering national reconstruction amid economic dislocation and political fragmentation after four years of occupation. In aviation milestones, the Lockheed P-80 Shooting Star completed its maiden flight on November 1 near Burbank, California, becoming the first US operational jet fighter and signaling the shift to jet propulsion in military aircraft post-war. These developments underscored the era's focus on accountability, decolonization struggles, and technological reconfiguration amid global demobilization.

December

On December 4, the United States Senate voted 65-7 to approve U.S. participation in the United Nations, authorizing the president to appoint representatives and providing for U.S. membership in the organization established by the UN Charter signed in June 1945. President Harry S. Truman signed the United Nations Participation Act into law on December 20, enabling full U.S. engagement in the UN's organs and agencies, including commitments to maintain armed forces for collective security under Article 43 of the Charter.
The Moscow Conference of Foreign Ministers convened from December 16 to 26, bringing together U.S. Secretary of State James F. Byrnes, British Foreign Secretary Ernest Bevin, and Soviet Foreign Minister Vyacheslav Molotov to negotiate postwar arrangements, including peace treaties for Italy, Bulgaria, Hungary, Romania, and Finland, as well as the establishment of a Far Eastern Advisory Commission. The conference addressed German reparations, with the Soviet Union securing agreement on $10 billion in total reparations (half from its zone), though disputes over industrial dismantling persisted, foreshadowing tensions in Allied occupation policy.
General George S. Patton Jr., commander of the U.S. Third Army during the Allied advance into Germany, died on December 21 in Heidelberg, Germany, from injuries sustained in a low-speed automobile accident on December 9 near Mannheim, where his staff car collided with an Army truck. The official cause was pulmonary edema and congestive heart failure secondary to a broken neck and other trauma; Patton, aged 60, was buried among his troops at the Luxembourg American Cemetery.
On December 19, the Second Austrian Republic was formally re-established with the passage of the Austrian State Treaty preparatory measures, restoring sovereignty after seven years of Nazi annexation (Anschluss), though full Allied occupation continued until 1955.
The International Monetary Fund (IMF) and International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (World Bank) formally commenced operations on December 27, following ratification by sufficient member states under the Bretton Woods Agreement of July 1944, aimed at stabilizing international exchange rates and financing postwar reconstruction. These institutions marked the institutionalization of multilateral economic cooperation to prevent the competitive devaluations and trade barriers of the interwar period.
The Nuremberg International Military Tribunal, ongoing since November 20, conducted proceedings throughout December, including examinations of Nazi economic exploitation and concentration camp operations, with defendants like Hermann Gring and Rudolf Hess present as prosecutors presented evidence from captured documents and witness testimonies. By month's end, the trial had established precedents for individual accountability in aggressive war and crimes against humanity, though debates over retroactive justice and victors' tribunal legitimacy emerged among legal scholars.

Date unknown

Anne Frank and her sister Margot died from typhus in the Bergen-Belsen concentration camp in early 1945, shortly before British forces liberated the facility on April 15. The precise dates of their deaths are unknown due to the absence of records amid the camp's overcrowding, disease outbreaks, and administrative collapse; survivor testimonies and postwar investigations place Anne's death sometime between late February and early March, while official Dutch records list March 31 as a provisional date. Margot succumbed slightly earlier to the same epidemic. Their father, Otto Frank, was the sole survivor of the family, later publishing Anne's diary compiled from notebooks she maintained while in hiding from 1942 to 1944. The lack of exact documentation reflects broader challenges in verifying individual fatalities during the final months of Nazi camp operations, where typhus epidemics claimed tens of thousands of lives without systematic registration.

Spotted by U.S. submarines and aircraft, Yamato and its escorting destroyers were overwhelmed by approximately 386 American carrier-based aircraft, resulting in the loss of the battleship after multiple torpedo and bomb hits caused it to capsize and explode; over 2,400 Japanese sailors perished in the engagement.
In Europe, Allied forces continued their advance into Germany. On April 11, U.S. Army units liberated Buchenwald concentration camp near Weimar, encountering over 20,000 surviving prisoners amid evidence of systematic extermination and slave labor. The following day, April 12, U.S. President Franklin D. Roosevelt died of a cerebral hemorrhage at the Little White House in Warm Springs, Georgia, while in his fourth term; Vice President Harry S. Truman was sworn in as president hours later.

Italian communist partisans captured and executed Benito Mussolini, along with his mistress Clara Petacci and several ministers, on April 28 near Lake Como as he attempted to flee to Switzerland; their bodies were subsequently displayed upside down in Milan. The same day, U.S. forces liberated Dachau concentration camp, revealing further atrocities including mass graves and emaciated survivors.
On April 30, Adolf Hitler committed suicide by gunshot in his Berlin Fhrerbunker as Soviet troops closed in, with his body burned alongside that of Eva Braun; this act precipitated the final collapse of Nazi leadership and the unconditional surrender of German forces days later.

May

On May 2, the Battle of Berlin concluded with the surrender of the city's garrison to Soviet forces, following intense urban combat that resulted in over 80,000 Red Army casualties and the deaths of approximately 300,000 Berliners amid the ruins. Soviet troops raised their flag over the Reichstag building, symbolizing the fall of Nazi Germany's capital.
May 3: British RAF aircraft sank the German liner Cap Arcona and other vessels in the Bay of Lbeck, inadvertently killing around 8,000 concentration camp prisoners who had been evacuated onto the ships.
May 4: German forces in northern Germany, the Netherlands, and Denmark surrendered en masse to Field Marshal Bernard Montgomery at Lneburg Heath.
May 5: United States Army's 11th Armored Division liberated the Mauthausen concentration camp in Austria, where nearly 200,000 prisoners had been held since 1938, with at least 95,000 deaths attributed to brutal conditions including forced labor in granite quarries. In Prague, Czech partisans initiated an uprising against remaining German troops.
May 7: At 2:41 a.m. in Reims, France, General Alfred Jodl signed the unconditional surrender of all German armed forces to the Allies, effective at 11:01 p.m. that day; the document stipulated cessation of hostilities across all fronts. Concurrently, the Red Army captured the besieged city of Breslau after 82 days.
May 8: Designated Victory in Europe Day (VE Day), Allied leaders announced the end of hostilities in Europe following the German surrender; in Berlin, Field Marshal Wilhelm Keitel signed a second instrument for Soviet satisfaction. Celebrations erupted worldwide, including street parties and parades in London where Prime Minister Winston Churchill addressed crowds from Whitehall and the Royal Family appeared on the Buckingham Palace balcony; similar festivities occurred in New York and Paris, though the war against Japan persisted. President Harry S. Truman described the moment as "a solemn but glorious hour."
In the Pacific theater, fighting continued on Okinawa, where U.S. troops, upon hearing of VE Day, maintained operations amid heavy casualties from Japanese resistance.

June

The Battle of Okinawa, the largest amphibious assault in the Pacific Theater of World War II, concluded on June 22, 1945, after 82 days of intense fighting, with U.S. forces declaring organized Japanese resistance ended following the suicide of Admiral Minoru ta on June 17 and the death of Lieutenant General Mitsuru Ushijima on June 22. The campaign, part of Operation Iceberg, involved over 60,000 U.S. troops landing on April 1 and facing fanatical Japanese defenses, including kamikaze attacks that sank or damaged dozens of Allied ships; total U.S. casualties exceeded 49,000, including more than 12,500 killed, while Japanese losses surpassed 100,000 dead, with most troops fighting to the death rather than surrendering. This victory secured airfields for potential operations against Japan's home islands but highlighted the high cost of island-hopping, influencing Allied decisions toward atomic bombing and blockade strategies to force surrender without a full invasion.
On June 5, the United States, United Kingdom, Soviet Union, and France issued the Berlin Declaration, formally assuming supreme authority over all Germany as a single economic unit, vesting legislative, executive, and judicial powers in the Allied Control Council, and affirming Germany's unconditional surrender while prohibiting any German peace treaties without Allied consent. This declaration marked the legal framework for Allied occupation and denazification, dividing Germany into four zones but treating it as undivided for reparations and reconstruction purposes pending a future peace settlement.
The United Nations Charter was signed on June 26, 1945, by representatives of 50 nations in San Francisco's (/page/War Memorial Opera House), concluding the United Nations Conference on International Organization that began April 25; the document established the UN's structure, purposes for maintaining peace, and principles of sovereign equality among members, entering into force October 24 after ratification by the five permanent Security Council members and a majority of signatories. Ratified amid wartime momentum, the Charter reflected compromises on veto powers for major Allies and aims to prevent future aggressions through collective security, though its effectiveness would later depend on superpower cooperation.
Other developments included the U.S. initiation of Operation Paperclip, covertly recruiting over 1,600 German scientists and engineers, including Wernher von Braun, to leverage Nazi technical expertise for American postwar programs despite their prior involvement in forced labor and weapons development; this began gaining momentum in June following initial approvals in May. In the Pacific, sporadic kamikaze strikes continued, such as the sinking of the USS William D. Porter on June 10 off Okinawa, underscoring persistent Japanese desperation tactics.

July

On July 16, 1945, the United States conducted the Trinity test, the world's first detonation of a nuclear weapon, at the Alamogordo Bombing and Gunnery Range in New Mexico. The plutonium implosion device, code-named "Gadget," exploded at 5:29 a.m. local time with a yield of approximately 21 kilotons of TNT, creating a fireball and shockwave that confirmed the feasibility of atomic bombs for use against Japan. This event, part of the Manhattan Project, marked the dawn of the nuclear age and directly influenced subsequent decisions on deploying the weapon.
The Potsdam Conference commenced on July 17, 1945, in Potsdam, Germany, bringing together U.S. President Harry S. Truman, British Prime Minister Winston Churchill (later replaced by Clement Attlee following the UK general election), and Soviet Premier Joseph Stalin to negotiate postwar arrangements for Europe and the ongoing war against Japan. Key discussions addressed the administration of defeated Germany, including its demilitarization, denazification, and division into occupation zones, as well as reparations and the prosecution of war criminals. On July 24, Truman informally informed Stalin of the successful atomic bomb test, describing it as a weapon of extraordinary power, though without technical details; Stalin, already aware through espionage, responded noncommittally.
The conference produced the Potsdam Agreement on July 26, outlining principles for German governance, and the Potsdam Declaration on the same day, issued by the U.S., UK, and China, demanding Japan's unconditional surrender and warning of "prompt and utter destruction" if refused, setting the stage for the atomic bombings. Tensions arose over Soviet expansion in Eastern Europe and differing visions for Poland's borders, foreshadowing Cold War divisions, yet the Allies reaffirmed unity against Japan.
On July 30, 1945, the USS Indianapolis, having delivered components of the atomic bombs to Tinian, was torpedoed and sunk by the Japanese submarine I-58 in the Philippine Sea, resulting in 880 men entering shark-infested waters; only 316 survived after four days, marking one of the worst naval disasters in U.S. history due to delayed rescue efforts. This incident underscored the perils of Pacific operations even as Japan's defeat loomed.

August

On August 2, the Potsdam Conference concluded after negotiations among the United States, the Soviet Union, and the United Kingdom on postwar arrangements for Europe and Asia, issuing the Potsdam Declaration demanding Japan's unconditional surrender. The declaration warned of "prompt and utter destruction" if Japan did not comply, though it omitted specific mention of the emperor's status.
On August 6, the United States dropped the atomic bomb "Little Boy" on Hiroshima from the B-29 bomber Enola Gay, detonating at an altitude of approximately 1,900 feet over the city center and destroying about 70% of its buildings. The explosion killed an estimated 66,000 people immediately, with total fatalities reaching 140,000 by the end of 1945 due to blast effects, burns, and radiation. Hiroshima, a military and industrial hub with over 250,000 residents, had been selected as a target to maximize psychological impact on Japanese leadership.
On August 8, the Soviet Union declared war on Japan, honoring its Yalta Agreement commitment, and launched Operation August Storm, invading Japanese-occupied Manchuria with over 1.5 million troops, 5,000 tanks, and 3,700 aircraft against the depleted Kwantung Army. The offensive, coordinated across multiple fronts, rapidly overwhelmed Japanese defenses, capturing key cities and leading to the surrender of approximately 600,000 Japanese troops by mid-August.
On August 9, a second atomic bomb, "Fat Man," was dropped on Nagasaki, killing about 39,000 immediately and causing total deaths of up to 74,000 by year's end, though the intended target of Kokura was spared due to cloud cover. That same day, Soviet forces continued advances in Manchuria, isolating Japanese holdings in Korea and Sakhalin.
Facing these shocks, Japan's Supreme War Council deadlocked, but Emperor Hirohito intervened on August 10, directing acceptance of the Potsdam terms with the caveat that his sovereignty be preserved. After Allied clarification on August 12 that the emperor's role would be subject to the supreme commander's authority, Japan agreed unconditionally on August 14. Hirohito announced the surrender to the Japanese people via radio broadcast on August 15 (known as V-J Day in Allied nations), marking the effective end of World War II hostilities. The formal instrument of surrender was signed on September 2 aboard the USS Missouri.

September

!float-right
On September 2, 1945, representatives of the Empire of Japan signed the instrument of surrender aboard the USS Missouri in Tokyo Bay, formally ending World War II. The ceremony, attended by Allied commanders including General Douglas MacArthur, who acted as supreme commander, marked the unconditional capitulation following the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki and the Soviet declaration of war. This event concluded hostilities that had begun in 1939 in Europe and 1941 in the Pacific, with Japan agreeing to Allied occupation and demilitarization.
Immediately following the surrender, General MacArthur assumed command of the Supreme Commander for the Allied Powers (SCAP), initiating the occupation and reconstruction of Japan. Allied forces began disarmament of Japanese military units and repatriation efforts, while preparations for war crimes trials commenced, targeting leaders like former Prime Minister Hideki Tojo. On September 11, Tojo attempted suicide by gunshot upon arrest by American forces but survived and was later prosecuted.
In Norway, Vidkun Quisling, the collaborationist prime minister during the Nazi occupation, was sentenced to death for treason on September 24, exemplifying the Allied pursuit of justice against Axis collaborators. These proceedings underscored the shift from wartime operations to postwar accountability across Europe and Asia. Meanwhile, in Canada, Soviet embassy cipher clerk Igor Gouzenko defected on September 5, revealing extensive espionage networks that accelerated emerging Cold War tensions, though the full implications unfolded later.

October

On October 8, Rudolf Hess, Adolf Hitler's former deputy, was transferred from British custody to Nuremberg, Germany, to face impending war crimes charges as part of Allied efforts to prosecute Nazi leadership.
Preparations for the International Military Tribunal advanced rapidly, with the indictment against 24 high-ranking Nazi officialsincluding Hermann Gring, Rudolf Hess, and Joachim von Ribbentroplodged with the tribunal on October 18 in Berlin, and German-language copies served to defendants on October 19. U.S. prosecutors, including a team of female lawyers led by figures such as Mary Margaret McKay, had relocated to Nuremberg by early October to compile evidence of atrocities, setting the stage for the trial's opening on November 20.
The establishment of the United Nations marked the month's most pivotal development in global governance. On October 24, the UN Charter entered into force after ratification by the five permanent members of the Security CouncilChina, France, the Soviet Union, the United Kingdom, and the United Statesalong with 24 other nations, totaling 29 signatories and fulfilling the threshold for activation. This formalized the organization as a successor to the failed League of Nations, aimed at preventing future conflicts through collective security and diplomacy, with headquarters later established in New York.
In Southeast Asia, the Indonesian National Revolution gained momentum against returning Dutch colonial forces. British troops, tasked with disarming Japanese forces and maintaining order post-surrender, entered Java in early October, but encountered growing resistance from Indonesian nationalists seeking to enforce their August 17 independence declaration. On October 29, Indonesian fighters in Makassar, Celebes (Sulawesi), attacked Dutch positions and seized a local radio station to broadcast pro-independence messages, escalating clashes that foreshadowed larger battles like Surabaya in November.
Elsewhere, post-war repatriations and reconstructions proceeded amid demobilization. Allied forces continued overseeing the occupation of Japan and Germany, with millions of troops shipping home; for instance, U.S. heavyweight boxing champion Joe Louis was honorably discharged from the Army on October 1 after serving in exhibition bouts to boost morale. In Europe, General Dwight D. Eisenhower visited The Hague on October 2 to inspect liberated areas and coordinate relief efforts. These events reflected the shift from wartime mobilization to stabilizing fractured societies and addressing colonial transitions.

November

On November 10, intense fighting broke out in Surabaya, Indonesia, between pro-independence nationalists and Dutch colonial forces seeking to restore pre-war authority following Japan's defeat, marking a pivotal escalation in the Indonesian National Revolution with thousands of casualties on both sides and galvanizing anti-colonial sentiment across the archipelago.
The International Military Tribunal for the Far East had not yet convened, but in Europe, the Nuremberg trials opened on November 20 in the Palace of Justice, prosecuting 24 senior Nazi leadersincluding Hermann Gring, Rudolf Hess, and Joachim von Ribbentropfor planning aggressive war, war crimes, and crimes against humanity under the London Charter of 1945, with proceedings led by Allied prosecutors and judges from the US, UK, USSR, and France. The trial's evidence, drawn from captured German documents and witness testimonies, exposed the systematic nature of Nazi atrocities, resulting in 19 convictions by October 1946, though critics later debated the victors' justice aspect given the exclusion of Soviet crimes.
Elsewhere, Charles de Gaulle assumed the presidency of France's provisional government on November 13, steering national reconstruction amid economic dislocation and political fragmentation after four years of occupation. In aviation milestones, the Lockheed P-80 Shooting Star completed its maiden flight on November 1 near Burbank, California, becoming the first US operational jet fighter and signaling the shift to jet propulsion in military aircraft post-war. These developments underscored the era's focus on accountability, decolonization struggles, and technological reconfiguration amid global demobilization.

December

On December 4, the United States Senate voted 65-7 to approve U.S. participation in the United Nations, authorizing the president to appoint representatives and providing for U.S. membership in the organization established by the UN Charter signed in June 1945. President Harry S. Truman signed the United Nations Participation Act into law on December 20, enabling full U.S. engagement in the UN's organs and agencies, including commitments to maintain armed forces for collective security under Article 43 of the Charter.
The Moscow Conference of Foreign Ministers convened from December 16 to 26, bringing together U.S. Secretary of State James F. Byrnes, British Foreign Secretary Ernest Bevin, and Soviet Foreign Minister Vyacheslav Molotov to negotiate postwar arrangements, including peace treaties for Italy, Bulgaria, Hungary, Romania, and Finland, as well as the establishment of a Far Eastern Advisory Commission. The conference addressed German reparations, with the Soviet Union securing agreement on $10 billion in total reparations (half from its zone), though disputes over industrial dismantling persisted, foreshadowing tensions in Allied occupation policy.
General George S. Patton Jr., commander of the U.S. Third Army during the Allied advance into Germany, died on December 21 in Heidelberg, Germany, from injuries sustained in a low-speed automobile accident on December 9 near Mannheim, where his staff car collided with an Army truck. The official cause was pulmonary edema and congestive heart failure secondary to a broken neck and other trauma; Patton, aged 60, was buried among his troops at the Luxembourg American Cemetery.
On December 19, the Second Austrian Republic was formally re-established with the passage of the Austrian State Treaty preparatory measures, restoring sovereignty after seven years of Nazi annexation (Anschluss), though full Allied occupation continued until 1955.
The International Monetary Fund (IMF) and International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (World Bank) formally commenced operations on December 27, following ratification by sufficient member states under the Bretton Woods Agreement of July 1944, aimed at stabilizing international exchange rates and financing postwar reconstruction. These institutions marked the institutionalization of multilateral economic cooperation to prevent the competitive devaluations and trade barriers of the interwar period.
The Nuremberg International Military Tribunal, ongoing since November 20, conducted proceedings throughout December, including examinations of Nazi economic exploitation and concentration camp operations, with defendants like Hermann Gring and Rudolf Hess present as prosecutors presented evidence from captured documents and witness testimonies. By month's end, the trial had established precedents for individual accountability in aggressive war and crimes against humanity, though debates over retroactive justice and victors' tribunal legitimacy emerged among legal scholars.

Deaths

January

In January 1945, Allied forces pressed their advantages across multiple fronts in the final stages of World War II. On the Western Front, U.S. and British troops concluded the Battle of the Bulge through counteroffensives that forced German retreats, depleting Nazi reserves and ending the engagement on January 25. Concurrently, a secondary German operation, Nordwind, launched on January 3 in Alsace-Lorraine, aimed to divert Allied resources but gained limited ground against U.S. defenses.
The Eastern Front saw the Soviet Red Army initiate the VistulaOder Offensive on January 12, deploying approximately 2 million troops from bridgeheads along the Vistula River in Poland. This rapid advance liberated Warsaw by January 17 and Krakw shortly thereafter, pushing German forces back over 300 miles to the Oder River by month's end and positioning the Soviets for the assault on Berlin. As Soviet units approached, SS guards at Auschwitz began evacuating the camp complex on January 17, forcing nearly 60,000 prisoners on death marches westward into Germany; those unfit to marchabout 7,000were left behind. Soviet forces entered Auschwitz on January 27, liberating the remaining survivors and exposing the scale of Nazi atrocities, including gas chambers and crematoria.
In the Pacific theater, the U.S. Sixth Army, under General Douglas MacArthur, invaded Luzon in the Philippines on January 9, landing 150,000 troops at Lingayen Gulf with minimal initial opposition but facing subsequent Japanese counterattacks. This operation aimed to recapture Manila and sever Japanese supply lines, marking a key step in the liberation of the archipelago. Elsewhere, British forces in Burma completed the occupation of Akyab Island on January 4, while Chinese and Allied troops linked the Ledo Road supply route on January 27, enhancing overland logistics to China.
On January 20, U.S. President Franklin D. Roosevelt was inaugurated for his fourth term in a brief wartime ceremony, emphasizing continued resolve against the Axis powers. In Hungary, a provisional government signed an armistice with the Soviet Union, United States, and Britain on the same day, effectively ending organized resistance there.

February

The Yalta Conference convened from February 4 to 11 in the Crimean resort town of Yalta, where U.S. President Franklin D. Roosevelt, British Prime Minister Winston Churchill, and Soviet Premier Joseph Stalin coordinated strategies for defeating Nazi Germany and shaping the postwar order. Agreements reached included the division of Germany into four occupation zones (with France receiving one), the establishment of a United Nations organization with a Security Council veto for permanent members, Soviet entry into the war against Japan three months after Germany's defeat in exchange for territorial concessions in Asia, and commitments to free and unfettered elections in liberated Eastern European countries, particularly Poland, whose borders were adjusted to include German territories east of the Oder River. 
On the Eastern Front, Soviet forces captured Budapest on February 13 following a two-month siege that resulted in heavy casualties on both sides, with German and Hungarian defenders suffering approximately 38,000 killed and 138,000 captured, while Soviet losses exceeded 80,000. This victory facilitated the Red Army's Vistula-Oder Offensive, which by late February brought Soviet troops to the Oder River, within 40 miles of Berlin, threatening the German heartland.
In the Western Allies' air campaign, British Royal Air Force bombers conducted raids on Dresden starting the night of February 13, followed by U.S. Army Air Forces daylight attacks on February 14 and 15, dropping over 3,900 tons of high-explosive and incendiary bombs that ignited a firestorm devastating the city's historic center and causing an estimated 22,700 to 25,000 civilian deaths, though Dresden served as a rail and communications hub supporting German troop movements. 
U.S. forces initiated the Battle of Manila on February 3, with American and Filipino troops entering the Philippine capital and engaging Japanese defenders in intense house-to-house fighting that lasted until early March, resulting in over 100,000 Japanese deaths and widespread destruction of the city, including cultural sites. In the Pacific, the Battle of Iwo Jima commenced on February 19 when approximately 70,000 U.S. Marines landed on the volcanic island under heavy Japanese fire from entrenched positions, securing beachheads amid casualties that would total nearly 7,000 Marines killed and 20,000 wounded by the campaign's end, with the island's airfields enabling P-51 fighter escorts for B-29 Superfortress bombers targeting Japan. 
On the Western Front, U.S. First and Ninth Armies launched Operation Grenade on February 23, crossing the Roer River after flooding subsided, advancing toward the Rhine River and capturing key industrial areas in the Ruhr Valley.

March

The Battle of Iwo Jima, which began on February 19, continued into March with intense fighting as U.S. Marines secured the island against entrenched Japanese defenders. By March 26, the island was declared secure, though pockets of resistance persisted; U.S. forces suffered approximately 26,000 casualties, including nearly 7,000 killed, while Japanese losses exceeded 20,000 dead.
On the Eastern Front, German forces launched Operation Spring Awakening from March 6 to 15 in western Hungary, aiming to recapture oil fields and relieve Budapest, but the offensive collapsed under Soviet counterattacks, marking Adolf Hitler's last major initiative.
In Western Europe, U.S. First Army troops captured Cologne on March 6 amid the ongoing Ruhr encirclement. On March 7, the U.S. Ninth Armored Division unexpectedly seized the intact Ludendorff Bridge over the Rhine at Remagen, enabling the first Allied crossing and accelerating the advance into Germany despite German demolition attempts.
The most destructive single air raid of the war occurred on the night of March 910, when 334 U.S. B-29 Superfortresses conducted low-level firebombing of Tokyo under Operation Meetinghouse, dropping incendiaries that ignited firestorms killing an estimated 80,000100,000 civilians and destroying 16 square miles of the city.
Soviet forces, following their JanuaryFebruary VistulaOder Offensive, consolidated positions along the Oder River in March, enlarging bridgeheads and preparing further advances toward Berlin.
British and Canadian forces under Operation Plunder crossed the Rhine on March 2324 near Rees and Wesel, supported by airborne Operation Varsity, breaching the last major natural barrier to Germany's industrial heartland.
In the China-Burma-India theater, Chinese forces of the U.S.-supported X Force retook Lashio on March 67, reopening key supply routes. The Royal Air Force employed the 22,000-pound Grand Slam earthquake bomb for the first time on March 14 against the Bielefeld viaduct, disrupting German logistics. The last V-1 flying bomb struck Britain on March 29.

On August 8, the Soviet Union declared war on Japan, honoring its Yalta Agreement commitment, and launched Operation August Storm, invading Japanese-occupied Manchuria with over 1.5 million troops, 5,000 tanks, and 3,700 aircraft against the depleted Kwantung Army. The offensive, coordinated across multiple fronts, rapidly overwhelmed Japanese defenses, capturing key cities and leading to the surrender of approximately 600,000 Japanese troops by mid-August.
On August 9, a second atomic bomb, "Fat Man," was dropped on Nagasaki, killing about 39,000 immediately and causing total deaths of up to 74,000 by year's end, though the intended target of Kokura was spared due to cloud cover. That same day, Soviet forces continued advances in Manchuria, isolating Japanese holdings in Korea and Sakhalin.
Facing these shocks, Japan's Supreme War Council deadlocked, but Emperor Hirohito intervened on August 10, directing acceptance of the Potsdam terms with the caveat that his sovereignty be preserved. After Allied clarification on August 12 that the emperor's role would be subject to the supreme commander's authority, Japan agreed unconditionally on August 14. Hirohito announced the surrender to the Japanese people via radio broadcast on August 15 (known as V-J Day in Allied nations), marking the effective end of World War II hostilities. The formal instrument of surrender was signed on September 2 aboard the USS Missouri.

September

!float-right
On September 2, 1945, representatives of the Empire of Japan signed the instrument of surrender aboard the USS Missouri in Tokyo Bay, formally ending World War II. The ceremony, attended by Allied commanders including General Douglas MacArthur, who acted as supreme commander, marked the unconditional capitulation following the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki and the Soviet declaration of war. This event concluded hostilities that had begun in 1939 in Europe and 1941 in the Pacific, with Japan agreeing to Allied occupation and demilitarization.
Immediately following the surrender, General MacArthur assumed command of the Supreme Commander for the Allied Powers (SCAP), initiating the occupation and reconstruction of Japan. Allied forces began disarmament of Japanese military units and repatriation efforts, while preparations for war crimes trials commenced, targeting leaders like former Prime Minister Hideki Tojo. On September 11, Tojo attempted suicide by gunshot upon arrest by American forces but survived and was later prosecuted.
In Norway, Vidkun Quisling, the collaborationist prime minister during the Nazi occupation, was sentenced to death for treason on September 24, exemplifying the Allied pursuit of justice against Axis collaborators. These proceedings underscored the shift from wartime operations to postwar accountability across Europe and Asia. Meanwhile, in Canada, Soviet embassy cipher clerk Igor Gouzenko defected on September 5, revealing extensive espionage networks that accelerated emerging Cold War tensions, though the full implications unfolded later.

October

On October 8, Rudolf Hess, Adolf Hitler's former deputy, was transferred from British custody to Nuremberg, Germany, to face impending war crimes charges as part of Allied efforts to prosecute Nazi leadership.
Preparations for the International Military Tribunal advanced rapidly, with the indictment against 24 high-ranking Nazi officialsincluding Hermann Gring, Rudolf Hess, and Joachim von Ribbentroplodged with the tribunal on October 18 in Berlin, and German-language copies served to defendants on October 19. U.S. prosecutors, including a team of female lawyers led by figures such as Mary Margaret McKay, had relocated to Nuremberg by early October to compile evidence of atrocities, setting the stage for the trial's opening on November 20.
The establishment of the United Nations marked the month's most pivotal development in global governance. On October 24, the UN Charter entered into force after ratification by the five permanent members of the Security CouncilChina, France, the Soviet Union, the United Kingdom, and the United Statesalong with 24 other nations, totaling 29 signatories and fulfilling the threshold for activation. This formalized the organization as a successor to the failed League of Nations, aimed at preventing future conflicts through collective security and diplomacy, with headquarters later established in New York.
In Southeast Asia, the Indonesian National Revolution gained momentum against returning Dutch colonial forces. British troops, tasked with disarming Japanese forces and maintaining order post-surrender, entered Java in early October, but encountered growing resistance from Indonesian nationalists seeking to enforce their August 17 independence declaration. On October 29, Indonesian fighters in Makassar, Celebes (Sulawesi), attacked Dutch positions and seized a local radio station to broadcast pro-independence messages, escalating clashes that foreshadowed larger battles like Surabaya in November.
Elsewhere, post-war repatriations and reconstructions proceeded amid demobilization. Allied forces continued overseeing the occupation of Japan and Germany, with millions of troops shipping home; for instance, U.S. heavyweight boxing champion Joe Louis was honorably discharged from the Army on October 1 after serving in exhibition bouts to boost morale. In Europe, General Dwight D. Eisenhower visited The Hague on October 2 to inspect liberated areas and coordinate relief efforts. These events reflected the shift from wartime mobilization to stabilizing fractured societies and addressing colonial transitions.

November

On November 10, intense fighting broke out in Surabaya, Indonesia, between pro-independence nationalists and Dutch colonial forces seeking to restore pre-war authority following Japan's defeat, marking a pivotal escalation in the Indonesian National Revolution with thousands of casualties on both sides and galvanizing anti-colonial sentiment across the archipelago.
The International Military Tribunal for the Far East had not yet convened, but in Europe, the Nuremberg trials opened on November 20 in the Palace of Justice, prosecuting 24 senior Nazi leadersincluding Hermann Gring, Rudolf Hess, and Joachim von Ribbentropfor planning aggressive war, war crimes, and crimes against humanity under the London Charter of 1945, with proceedings led by Allied prosecutors and judges from the US, UK, USSR, and France. The trial's evidence, drawn from captured German documents and witness testimonies, exposed the systematic nature of Nazi atrocities, resulting in 19 convictions by October 1946, though critics later debated the victors' justice aspect given the exclusion of Soviet crimes.
Elsewhere, Charles de Gaulle assumed the presidency of France's provisional government on November 13, steering national reconstruction amid economic dislocation and political fragmentation after four years of occupation. In aviation milestones, the Lockheed P-80 Shooting Star completed its maiden flight on November 1 near Burbank, California, becoming the first US operational jet fighter and signaling the shift to jet propulsion in military aircraft post-war. These developments underscored the era's focus on accountability, decolonization struggles, and technological reconfiguration amid global demobilization.

December

On December 4, the United States Senate voted 65-7 to approve U.S. participation in the United Nations, authorizing the president to appoint representatives and providing for U.S. membership in the organization established by the UN Charter signed in June 1945. President Harry S. Truman signed the United Nations Participation Act into law on December 20, enabling full U.S. engagement in the UN's organs and agencies, including commitments to maintain armed forces for collective security under Article 43 of the Charter.
The Moscow Conference of Foreign Ministers convened from December 16 to 26, bringing together U.S. Secretary of State James F. Byrnes, British Foreign Secretary Ernest Bevin, and Soviet Foreign Minister Vyacheslav Molotov to negotiate postwar arrangements, including peace treaties for Italy, Bulgaria, Hungary, Romania, and Finland, as well as the establishment of a Far Eastern Advisory Commission. The conference addressed German reparations, with the Soviet Union securing agreement on $10 billion in total reparations (half from its zone), though disputes over industrial dismantling persisted, foreshadowing tensions in Allied occupation policy.
General George S. Patton Jr., commander of the U.S. Third Army during the Allied advance into Germany, died on December 21 in Heidelberg, Germany, from injuries sustained in a low-speed automobile accident on December 9 near Mannheim, where his staff car collided with an Army truck. The official cause was pulmonary edema and congestive heart failure secondary to a broken neck and other trauma; Patton, aged 60, was buried among his troops at the Luxembourg American Cemetery.
On December 19, the Second Austrian Republic was formally re-established with the passage of the Austrian State Treaty preparatory measures, restoring sovereignty after seven years of Nazi annexation (Anschluss), though full Allied occupation continued until 1955.
The International Monetary Fund (IMF) and International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (World Bank) formally commenced operations on December 27, following ratification by sufficient member states under the Bretton Woods Agreement of July 1944, aimed at stabilizing international exchange rates and financing postwar reconstruction. These institutions marked the institutionalization of multilateral economic cooperation to prevent the competitive devaluations and trade barriers of the interwar period.
The Nuremberg International Military Tribunal, ongoing since November 20, conducted proceedings throughout December, including examinations of Nazi economic exploitation and concentration camp operations, with defendants like Hermann Gring and Rudolf Hess present as prosecutors presented evidence from captured documents and witness testimonies. By month's end, the trial had established precedents for individual accountability in aggressive war and crimes against humanity, though debates over retroactive justice and victors' tribunal legitimacy emerged among legal scholars.

Nobel Prizes

Physics

The Nobel Prize in Physics for 1945 was awarded to Wolfgang Pauli, professor at the Swiss Federal Institute of Technology in Zrich, for the discovery of the exclusion principle, also known as the Pauli principle. Pauli formulated the principle in 1925, proposing that no two electrons in an atom could possess identical sets of quantum numbers, thereby introducing a fourth quantum number to account for electron spin and orbital configurations. This insight resolved longstanding anomalies in atomic spectra, particularly for helium and alkali metals, and provided a causal foundation for the discrete energy levels and shell structures observed in atoms.
The exclusion principle extends to all fermions, forbidding identical quantum states for particles with half-integer spin, which underpins the stability of atomic matter and the periodic table's arrangement of elements. Pauli's derivation relied on empirical data from spectroscopy rather than direct experimentation, demonstrating how theoretical reasoning could unify disparate observations in quantum theory. The 1945 award, announced in November amid post-war recovery, highlighted the principle's enduring impact on quantum mechanics, despite the Nobel Committee's wartime delays in ceremonies. Pauli's work complemented earlier atomic models, such as Bohr's 1921 theory, by enforcing statistical restrictions that prevent electron collapse into the nucleus.

Chemistry

The Nobel Prize in Chemistry for 1945 was awarded to Artturi Ilmari Virtanen, a Finnish biochemist, for his research and inventions in agricultural and nutrition chemistry, particularly his development of a method for preserving fodder that maintained its nutritional value. The prize, delayed due to World War II, was formally presented on December 10, 1946, in Stockholm.
Virtanen's key innovation, known as the AIV silage system introduced in 1928 and refined through the 1930s, involved treating silage with dilute acids such as hydrochloric or sulfuric acid to inhibit microbial decomposition and enzymatic breakdown of proteins into non-nutritive forms. This process preserved up to 90% of the fodder's protein content, enabling year-round feeding of high-quality feed to livestock and reducing reliance on imported concentrates, which proved vital for Finland's agricultural self-sufficiency amid wartime shortages. His method stemmed from earlier studies on nitrogen metabolism in plants and animals, including demonstrations of symbiotic nitrogen fixation by root nodule bacteria in legumes and the role of cozymase (later identified as NAD) in fermentations.
The Nobel Committee highlighted Virtanen's contributions as advancing practical biochemistry with direct economic impact, contrasting with more theoretical chemistry awards; his work built on empirical field trials at the State Institute of Agricultural Research in Helsinki, where he served as director from 1926. Virtanen, born January 15, 1895, in Helsinki, had earned a Ph.D. from the University of Helsinki in 1919 and later expanded his research to include butter preservation techniques using lactic acid bacteria. He declined a similar award in 1943 to avoid wartime publicity but accepted the 1945 prize, sharing no portion with others. His inventions influenced global fodder preservation practices, though adoption varied due to the handling of acids.

Physiology or Medicine

The Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for 1945 was awarded jointly to Sir Alexander Fleming, Ernst Boris Chain, and Sir Howard Walter Florey "for the discovery of penicillin and its curative effects in various infectious diseases." Fleming, a Scottish bacteriologist, first identified penicillin's antibacterial properties in September 1928 at St. Mary's Hospital in London, observing that a contaminant mold (Penicillium notatum) secreted a substance inhibiting Staphylococcus growth in a petri dish left open overnight. He named the compound penicillin and published preliminary findings in 1929, demonstrating its lytic effects on bacteria in vitro, but efforts to purify and apply it clinically stalled due to the substance's instability, low concentration, and challenges in extraction.
Chain, a German-born biochemist, and Florey, an Australian pathologist, advanced the work at the University of Oxford's Dunn School starting in 1938. Chain developed methods to purify and concentrate penicillin, identifying it as a non-protein with specific bactericidal action against gram-positive bacteria while sparing host tissues. Florey led animal trials from 1940, proving penicillin's efficacy in treating streptococcal and other infections in mice, with survival rates exceeding 90% in infected groups versus near-total mortality in controls; human trials followed in February 1941, successfully curing severe infections like puerperal sepsis and wound sepsis untreatable by prior methods. Their collaborative 1940 paper in the British Journal of Experimental Pathology established penicillin's therapeutic potential, shifting medicine from symptomatic treatment to causal elimination of pathogens.
Wartime urgency drove production scaling; initial yields yielded only milligrams, insufficient for broad use, prompting Florey's 1941 mission to the United States, where partnerships with firms like Pfizer adapted corn steep liquor-based deep-tank fermentation, boosting output to thousands of liters by 1943 and enabling Allied military deployment against battlefield infections. This innovation produced over 2.3 million doses by D-Day in June 1944, drastically reducing gas gangrene and sepsis mortality from 8-10% to under 1%. The prize announcement on October 25, 1945, by the Karolinska Institute recognized the interdependent contributionsFleming's observation, Chain's chemical isolation, and Florey's physiological validationdespite debates over credit, with Florey and Chain's systematic development proving decisive for clinical impact. Penicillin's specificity, targeting bacterial cell wall synthesis without host toxicity, marked the advent of modern antibiotics, saving an estimated 200 million lives by 2000 through derivatives and inspiring antimicrobial research.

Literature

The Nobel Prize in Literature for 1945 was awarded by the Swedish Academy to Chilean poet Gabriela Mistral (pseudonym of Lucila Godoy Alcayaga) "for her lyric poetry which, inspired by powerful emotions, has made her name a symbol of the idealistic aspirations of the entire Latin American world." The announcement occurred on November 15, 1945, with the award ceremony held on December 10, 1945, in Stockholm, Sweden, as was customary for Nobel Prizes in Literature. Mistral, born on April 7, 1889, in Vicua, Chile, became the first Latin American laureate in the category, recognizing her contributions amid a year marked by the end of World War II and global shifts toward postwar reconstruction.
Mistral's early life as a rural schoolteacher shaped her literary output, which began after personal tragedy, including a failed romance culminating in her lover's suicide in 1909; this event influenced her themes of profound emotion, motherhood, and social justice. Her breakthrough collection, Desolacin (1922), established her as a voice for Latin American identity, blending indigenous and European influences with advocacy for education and women's rights; subsequent works like Ternura (1924) and Tala (1938) expanded her focus on universal human struggles, nature, and spiritual longing. By 1945, Mistral had served as a diplomat and educator, including consul positions in Europe and the Americas, which broadened her perspective on hemispheric solidarity, aligning with the prize's emphasis on her symbolic role in Latin American idealism.
The Swedish Academy's selection highlighted Mistral's emotional depth over stylistic innovation, distinguishing her from contemporaries like T.S. Eliot, who was nominated but not chosen that year; her win underscored a preference for poetry evoking collective aspiration in a war-weary world, rather than experimental modernism. Critics have noted that her accessible, fervent stylerooted in Spanish-language traditionsprioritized moral and cultural uplift, reflecting her lifelong commitment to pedagogy and anti-fascist sentiments during the 1930s Spanish Civil War. Mistral accepted the prize in person, delivering a speech on the redemptive power of poetry, and used the 121,251 Swedish kronor award to support educational initiatives in Latin America. Her recognition cemented her legacy as a bridge between personal lyricism and regional advocacy, influencing subsequent Latin American writers.

Peace

The Nobel Peace Prize for 1945 was awarded to Cordell Hull, who served as United States Secretary of State from 1933 to 1944, with the announcement made on November 12, 1945. The Norwegian Nobel Committee cited Hull's "indefatigable work for international understanding" and his "pivotal role in establishing the United Nations," recognizing his contributions to postwar planning amid World War II. Hull had previously been nominated multiple times in the late 1930s for fostering hemispheric cooperation through the Good Neighbor Policy toward Latin America and for negotiating reciprocal trade agreements aimed at reducing economic barriers and promoting stability.
Hull's efforts toward the United Nations involved chairing key preparatory conferences, including the 1944 Dumbarton Oaks meetings where foundational structures for the organization were outlined, and advocating for its charter to prevent future global conflicts by institutionalizing collective security. As the longest-serving U.S. Secretary of State, he emphasized diplomacy over isolationism, helping to shift American policy from non-intervention to active multilateralism, though he resigned in November 1944 due to health issues before the UN's formal creation. The award, valued at approximately 140,000 Swedish kronor at the time, underscored the Committee's view of Hull as the "father of the United Nations," a designation reflecting his persistence in wartime negotiations despite personal frailties.
While praised for these diplomatic achievements, Hull's legacy includes criticisms regarding his restrictive stance on Jewish immigration during the Holocaust. In 1939, as the MS St. Louis carried over 900 Jewish refugees denied entry to Cuba and the U.S., Hull's State Department policies, aligned with broader quotas and security concerns, contributed to the ship's return to Europe, where over 250 passengers later perished under Nazi persecution. Historians have noted this as emblematic of Hull's prioritization of domestic political pressures and administrative caution over humanitarian imperatives, though defenders argue his focus remained on broader strategic diplomacy to counter Axis aggression. The 1945 prize, awarded amid the war's end and no Peace Prize given in 19401944 due to Nazi occupation of Norway, highlighted institutional continuity but also drew implicit contrasts to other nominees, such as Joseph Stalin, whose expansionist actions post-Yalta belied peace advocacy.

Scientific and Technological Milestones

Nuclear Developments

The Trinity test, conducted on July 16, 1945, at 5:29 a.m. local time, represented the culmination of the United States' Manhattan Project, achieving the world's first nuclear detonation. The test involved an implosion-type plutonium device known as the "Gadget," suspended 100 feet above a steel tower at the Alamogordo Bombing Range in New Mexico, approximately 210 miles south of Los Alamos. This design compressed a subcritical mass of plutonium-239 using conventional high explosives to initiate a supercritical chain reaction, a novel mechanism requiring empirical validation due to uncertainties in the implosion symmetry and neutron initiation. The explosion yielded approximately 18.6 kilotons of TNT equivalent, vaporizing the tower and creating a crater 1,000 feet wide filled with radioactive trinitite glass formed from fused desert sand.
Prior to the test, a dress rehearsal on May 7, 1945, detonated 108 tons of high explosives laced with radioactive tracers to calibrate instrumentation, confirming the setup's readiness. J. Robert Oppenheimer directed the effort at Los Alamos, overseeing scientists who resolved technical challenges like lens-shaped explosive charges and polonium-beryllium neutron initiators. The success validated the plutonium bomb's viability, paving the way for its deployment in the Fat Man device, while the uranium-based Little Boy design proceeded without testing due to its simpler gun-type mechanism. Observations from distant bunkers and aircraft recorded the fireball's ascent to 40,000 feet, shock waves shattering windows 125 miles away, and an electromagnetic pulse disrupting monitoring equipment.
This detonation not only confirmed the feasibility of controlled nuclear fission as a weapon but also initiated the Atomic Age, demonstrating energy release from 21 kilotons of TNTequivalent to 21,000 tons of conventional explosivesthrough the fission of mere pounds of plutonium. The test's data informed subsequent refinements in nuclear weapon design, though it exposed civilians downwind to fallout, with ranchers reporting ashfall and health effects traceable to radioactive iodine-131. No parallel advancements occurred in Axis programs; Germany's uranium project collapsed by April 1945, lacking sufficient heavy water or enriched uranium. The event underscored the Manhattan Project's scale, involving over 130,000 personnel and $2 billion in costs, achieving in secrecy what international efforts had deemed improbable.

Medical and Other Advances

In 1945, U.S. penicillin production scaled dramatically, with monthly output 40 to 50 times greater than at the program's outset, culminating in over-the-counter availability in pharmacies starting March 15. This wartime-driven expansion, coordinated by the War Production Board, released the antibiotic for civilian use and reduced infection-related deaths among soldiers and the public.
Researchers at Massachusetts General Hospital refined the Pap smear method that year, enhancing its reliability for early cervical cancer detection through improved cytological staining and analysis techniques. The first dedicated cervical cancer screening clinic opened in Massachusetts in 1945, marking the transition from experimental validationproven effective by 1941to routine clinical application.
Beyond medicine, the ENIAC (Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer) became operational in November 1945 as the first programmable, general-purpose electronic digital computer, executing its inaugural classified calculation on December 10. Developed at the University of Pennsylvania for U.S. Army ballistic computations, it utilized 18,000 vacuum tubes and performed 5,000 additions per second, enabling complex numerical simulations previously infeasible by mechanical means.
In household technology, Raytheon filed the inaugural patent for microwave-based cooking on October 8, 1945, following engineer Percy Spencer's observation that a magnetronused in radarmelted a candy bar via dielectric heating of water molecules. This serendipitous finding, rooted in wartime radar research, presaged commercial microwave ovens like the 1947 Radarange, which accelerated food preparation by agitating molecular vibrations rather than conventional conduction.

Controversies and Historical Debates

Strategic Bombing Campaigns

In the European theater, Allied strategic bombing campaigns against Germany persisted into early 1945, aiming to disrupt transportation networks, industrial production, and support advancing Soviet forces on the Eastern Front. The bombing of Dresden from February 13 to 15 involved over 1,200 RAF and USAAF heavy bombers dropping high-explosive and incendiary bombs, creating a firestorm that destroyed approximately 6.5 square kilometers of the city center and killed an estimated 22,700 to 25,000 civilians. This operation, part of broader efforts to impede German reinforcements to the east, highlighted the shift toward area bombing tactics despite debates over its military necessity given Germany's impending collapse.
Subsequent raids, such as the March 18 attack on Berlin by over 1,100 USAAF bombers, further strained Luftwaffe defenses but inflicted limited additional strategic damage as German industry was already crippled. Overall, the 1945 phase of the Combined Bomber Offensive resulted in the near-total devastation of German urban areas, contributing to the collapse of morale and logistics, though precise attribution of causal impact remains contested due to overlapping ground advances.
In the Pacific theater, the United States Army Air Forces escalated low-altitude incendiary bombing against Japanese cities following the relocation of B-29 Superfortress bases to the Marianas. Under Major General Curtis LeMay, the XXI Bomber Command conducted Operation Meetinghouse on the night of March 9-10, deploying 334 B-29s to drop 1,665 tons of incendiaries on Tokyo, igniting a firestorm that razed 16 square miles and caused approximately 100,000 deaths, surpassing the single-night toll of later atomic bombings. This raid, targeting densely packed wooden structures to maximize fire damage, demonstrated the efficacy of incendiary tactics against Japan's vulnerable urban-industrial base, with subsequent attacks on over 60 cities displacing millions and severely hampering war production.
These campaigns, while accelerating Japan's economic disintegration and civilian suffering, have sparked ongoing debates regarding proportionality and the blurring of military-civilian distinctions in total war, with empirical assessments affirming their role in weakening resolve without sole credit for surrender. Allied planners justified the intensity as causally necessary to avoid costlier invasions, supported by post-war surveys documenting halved Japanese aircraft production by mid-1945.

Yalta Conference and European Division

The Yalta Conference convened from February 4 to 11, 1945, at the Livadia Palace in Yalta, Crimea, bringing together U.S. President Franklin D. Roosevelt, British Prime Minister Winston Churchill, and Soviet Premier Joseph Stalin to coordinate the final stages of World War II in Europe and outline postwar arrangements. The leaders addressed the division of Germany into four occupation zonesadministered by the United States, United Kingdom, Soviet Union, and Francewith Berlin similarly partitioned, alongside commitments to Germany's demilitarization, denazification, democratization, and payment of reparations primarily to the Soviets. 
Central to the discussions was the future of Eastern Europe, particularly Poland, where Stalin secured recognition of the Soviet-backed Lublin Committee as the provisional government, with promises of free and unfettered elections to include non-communist parties, though the eastern border was shifted westward using the Oder-Neisse line at Poland's expense. The Declaration on Liberated Europe pledged that liberated territories would establish interim governments broadly representative of democratic elements, holding free elections as soon as possible, and fostering economic recovery through sovereign rights and self-expression. In exchange for these political concessions, Stalin agreed to Soviet entry into the war against Japan within three months of Germany's defeat and support for the United Nations' structure, including veto power for permanent Security Council members.
Roosevelt's visibly deteriorating healthmarked by fatigue and a gaunt appearancemay have contributed to the Allies' acquiescence to Soviet demands, as he prioritized securing Stalin's Pacific commitment over firmer guarantees for Eastern European sovereignty. Post-conference, Stalin systematically violated the electoral pledges; in Poland, elections in January 1947 were rigged through intimidation, arrests, and ballot stuffing, installing a communist regime, while similar manipulations occurred in Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria, and Czechoslovakia, where Soviet forces, already occupying these areas by war's end, suppressed opposition. These actions entrenched Soviet control over Eastern Europe, creating a buffer zone of satellite states under Moscow's influence.
The Yalta accords effectively legitimized the de facto division of Europe along lines dictated by military advances, with the Red Army's position enabling Stalin to impose one-party rule eastward of a demarcation roughly following Elbe River lines, while Western Allies governed the west. This bifurcation, later termed the Iron Curtain by Churchill in his March 1946 Fulton speech, sowed the seeds of the Cold War by institutionalizing ideological spheresdemocratic capitalism in the West versus Soviet communism in the Eastdespite Roosevelt's optimistic address to Congress portraying Yalta as a triumph of cooperation. Critics, including U.S. conservatives, later decried the conference for naively trusting Stalin's word without enforceable mechanisms, arguing it betrayed principles of self-determination and enabled totalitarian expansion.

Atomic Bombings

The atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki in August 1945 marked the first and only combat use of nuclear weapons, culminating the Manhattan Project's efforts to develop fission bombs during World War II. President Harry S. Truman authorized their deployment after Japan's rejection of the Potsdam Declaration's demand for unconditional surrender on July 26, 1945, aiming to force capitulation and avert the anticipated high casualties of Operation Downfall, the planned Allied invasion of the Japanese home islands. Estimates for Downfall projected 250,000 to over 1 million Allied casualties, alongside millions of Japanese deaths, based on fierce resistance observed in battles like Okinawa.
On August 6, 1945, the B-29 bomber Enola Gay dropped "Little Boy," a uranium-235 gun-type fission bomb with an explosive yield of approximately 15 kilotons, detonating 1,900 feet above Hiroshima at 8:15 a.m. local time. The blast and ensuing firestorm destroyed about 70% of the city's buildings and killed an estimated 70,000 people instantly, with total fatalities reaching 90,000 to 140,000 by the end of 1945 due to burns, injuries, and acute radiation sickness.
Three days later, on August 9, the B-29 Bockscar released "Fat Man," a plutonium-239 implosion-type bomb yielding about 21 kilotons, which exploded over Nagasaki after a targeting error diverted it from the primary site of Kokura. The detonation killed around 40,000 people immediately and caused 60,000 to 80,000 total deaths by year's end, compounded by the city's valleys channeling the blast wave.
The bombings, combined with the Soviet Union's declaration of war and invasion of Manchuria on August 8, shattered Japan's war leadership, leading Emperor Hirohito to intervene on August 10 and accept Potsdam terms, announcing surrender on August 15, 1945. While some postwar analyses, often from academics skeptical of U.S. motives, claim Japan was nearing surrender due to conventional bombing and naval blockade alone, primary documents reveal military hardliners' resolve for homeland defense, including arming civilians for attrition warfare, underscoring the bombs' causal role in prompting unconditional capitulation. Long-term effects included thousands more deaths from radiation-related illnesses, though empirical data on cancer spikes remains contested, with survivor cohorts showing elevated leukemia rates but debated solid tumor links attributable solely to radiation versus other wartime factors.

Soviet Actions and Expansions

The Red Army launched the Battle of Berlin on April 16, 1945, deploying over 2.5 million troops, 6,250 tanks, and 7,500 aircraft against German defenses in the city's outskirts. By April 25, Soviet forces had encircled Berlin after linking up with Allied troops to the west, initiating a brutal urban assault that included heavy artillery barrages exceeding 2 million shells. The battle concluded on May 2, 1945, with the surrender of the German garrison, following Adolf Hitler's suicide on April 30; Soviet casualties in the Berlin fighting alone reached approximately 80,000 killed, part of broader 1945 Eastern Front losses exceeding 600,000 dead or missing. German military deaths numbered around 50,000, with civilian fatalities estimated between 20,000 and 125,000 amid the destruction. 
In the wake of Berlin's fall, Soviet forces occupied eastern Germany and much of Eastern Europe, facilitating the rapid imposition of communist control despite assurances at the February 1945 Yalta Conference for free elections in liberated territories. Stalin's regime supported local communists in establishing provisional governments, as seen in Romania and Bulgaria where Soviet troops aided takeovers even before Germany's May 8 surrender. In Poland, the Soviets installed the Lublin Committee as the basis for a communist-dominated government, marginalizing non-communist Polish exiles and rigging subsequent processes to exclude opposition. The July-August Potsdam Conference affirmed occupation zones but could not reverse Soviet faits accomplis, with Moscow extracting reparations and deporting ethnic Germans and others, displacing millions under harsh conditions.
Soviet occupation involved widespread atrocities, including mass rapes estimated to affect up to 2 million German women, often gang rapes accompanied by killings and deportations to labor camps. Soviet authorities punished some perpetratorsover 4,000 officers and enlisted menbut the scale overwhelmed enforcement, reflecting orders from commanders like Marshal Zhukov to unleash vengeance after years of German invasions. These actions solidified Soviet dominance, creating a buffer zone of satellite states by late 1945, though full communist consolidation required further manipulations into 1947-1948.
On August 8, 1945, the Soviet Union declared war on Japan, honoring Yalta commitments, and invaded Japanese-held Manchuria the next day with 1.5 million troops, 5,500 tanks, and 3,700 aircraft in Operation August Storm. The offensive shattered the Japanese Kwantung Army, capturing 594,000 prisoners by August 20, with Soviet losses minimal at around 12,000 dead, hastening Japan's surrender on September 2. Post-invasion, Red Army units committed rapes and looting against Japanese and local civilians in Manchuria, mirroring European patterns, while occupying territories until 1946 and influencing communist gains in China. This expansion secured Soviet Pacific influence, including the Kuril Islands and southern Sakhalin, formalized in Allied agreements.

Cultural and Social Developments

Arts and Literature

In literature, Gabriela Mistral of Chile received the Nobel Prize in Literature on December 10, 1945, recognized for her lyric poetry inspired by powerful emotions, making her the first Latin American and first woman from the Americas to win the award. Her works, such as Desolacin (1922) and Ternura (1924), emphasized themes of motherhood, nature, and social justice, drawing from her experiences as a teacher and diplomat.
Notable publications included George Orwell's Animal Farm, released on August 17, 1945, in the United Kingdom, a novella allegorizing the Russian Revolution and critiquing Stalinist totalitarianism through farm animals overthrowing their human owner. Evelyn Waugh's Brideshead Revisited, serialized earlier but published as a novel in 1945, explored Catholic themes and aristocratic decline in interwar Britain. Bestsellers dominated U.S. charts, with Kathleen Winsor's Forever Amber topping The New York Times fiction list for much of the year, selling over 2 million copies amid controversy for its depiction of a Restoration-era courtesan. Other releases included E.B. White's children's book Stuart Little and Astrid Lindgren's Pippi Longstocking in Sweden, introducing whimsical, independent child protagonists.
Cinema in 1945 reflected wartime exhaustion and emerging postwar realism. American films like Billy Wilder's The Lost Weekend, released September 29, portrayed alcoholism's grip on a writer, earning the Academy Award for Best Picture in 1946. Michael Curtiz's Mildred Pierce, premiered September 28, depicted a mother's sacrifices amid family strife, winning Joan Crawford an Oscar for Best Actress. European neorealism gained traction with Roberto Rossellini's Rome, Open City, released September 26 in Italy, using non-professional actors to document Nazi occupation's brutality in Rome. Marcel Carn's Children of Paradise, completed in 1945 despite wartime constraints, premiered in December as a poetic tribute to 19th-century French theater, featuring Arletty and Jean-Louis Barrault.
Music events marked transitions from wartime austerity. Rodgers and Hammerstein's Carousel premiered on April 19 at New York's Majestic Theatre, adapting Liliom into a musical about redemption and labor struggles, with hits like "If I Loved You." Benjamin Britten's opera Peter Grimes, first performed June 7 at Sadler's Wells in London, introduced a psychological drama of an outcast fisherman, influencing modern opera with its 20th-century idiom. Popular U.S. charts featured "Sentimental Journey" by Les Brown and Doris Day, topping Billboard for nine weeks from its March release, evoking homecoming amid VE and VJ Days.
Visual arts saw resumed activity post-liberation. The Whitney Museum's Annual Exhibition of Contemporary American Painting, held November 27, 1945, to January 10, 1946, showcased over 400 works by 267 artists, highlighting abstract and social realist styles amid wartime recovery. Painter Pierre Bonnard died January 23, leaving a legacy of intimate domestic scenes in Post-Impressionist style. German Expressionist Max Pechstein perished June 29, his woodcuts and prints having critiqued prewar society. Allied forces recovered looted art, with U.S. Monuments Men cataloging thousands of pieces from Nazi repositories by year's end.

International Institutions and Social Shifts

The United Nations Charter was signed on June 26, 1945, in San Francisco by representatives from 50 nations, establishing the organization to foster international cooperation, maintain peace, and address economic and social issues in the wake of World War II. The Charter entered into force on October 24, 1945, upon ratification by China, France, the Soviet Union, the United Kingdom, and the United States, the permanent members of the Security Council, thereby creating structures like the General Assembly and Security Council to mediate disputes and promote collective security. This development represented a deliberate effort to supplant the ineffective League of Nations with a more robust framework, though its effectiveness was immediately tested by emerging U.S.-Soviet tensions.
Complementing the UN, the Bretton Woods financial architecture advanced in 1945; the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (IBRD, precursor to the World Bank) achieved formal existence on December 27, 1945, when their Articles of Agreement were signed by initial members, with the IMF focused on exchange rate stability and the IBRD on post-war lending for infrastructure. These institutions aimed to prevent the economic chaos of the interwar period by facilitating currency convertibility and reconstruction loans, drawing on U.S. economic dominance evidenced by its holding of two-thirds of global gold reserves at war's end.
The International Military Tribunal for the Far West opened on November 20, 1945, in Nuremberg, Germany, indicting 24 senior Nazi leaders for aggression, war crimes, and crimes against humanity under the London Charter of August 1945, marking the first use of permanent international criminal prosecution and codifying individual responsibility over state sovereignty in atrocities. The proceedings, conducted by judges from the U.S., UK, France, and Soviet Union, relied on extensive documentation including Nazi records, resulting in 19 convictions by October 1946 and establishing legal norms that influenced subsequent tribunals, though critics noted victors' justice given the Allies' own wartime actions went unexamined.
Socially, 1945 initiated massive demographic upheavals, with over 40 million Europeans displaced by combat, ethnic cleansings, and border redraws, including the forced expulsion of 12-14 million Germans from Eastern territories under Potsdam agreements, which reduced minority populations but inflicted hardship on millions through disease, starvation, and violence. Holocaust disclosures, via Allied liberations of camps like Auschwitz (January 27) and Dachau (April 29), exposed systematic extermination of six million Jews and others, galvanizing international revulsion and seeding human rights frameworks, as evidenced by early UN preparatory work on genocide prevention. In Allied nations, demobilization of 16 million U.S. troops alone spurred family formations, with U.S. marriage rates surging 30% from 1945 levels and birth rates rising to initiate the baby boom, while rationing's end unleashed pent-up demand for consumer goods like automobiles and appliances. These shifts underscored a transition from total war mobilization to peacetime normalcy, though persistent gender role expectations pushed many wartime female workers back to domestic spheres amid economic reconversion.
