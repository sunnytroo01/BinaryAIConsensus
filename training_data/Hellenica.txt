 The Hellenica (Ancient Greek: , lit. 'Greek Matters') is a historical work by the Athenian writer and soldier Xenophon (c. 430354 BC), composed in the early fourth century BC as a continuation of Thucydides' History of the Peloponnesian War, covering Greek events from 411 BC to the Battle of Mantinea in 362 BC. Divided into seven books, it details the conclusion of the Peloponnesian War, Sparta's brief hegemony, the Corinthian War, and Thebes' rising power, drawing on Xenophon's personal military experiences while presenting a relatively annalistic and less interpretive narrative than its predecessor. Xenophon's account, informed by his oligarchic sympathies and favoritism toward Sparta, serves as a key primary source for the period, though its selective focus and omissions reflect the author's exile from Athens and alignment with Spartan interests rather than exhaustive impartiality.

Authorship and Context

Xenophon's Background and Perspective

Xenophon was born circa 430 BC in Athens to a wealthy equestrian family in the deme of Erchia, during the early stages of the Peloponnesian War. As a young Athenian, he received a traditional elite education and served as a hoplite in the Athenian forces, gaining firsthand military experience in the conflict against Sparta. Influenced by the philosopher Socrates, whom he knew personally and later portrayed in dialogues like the Memorabilia, Xenophon's writings reflect Socratic emphasis on virtue, leadership, and moral inquiry.
In 401 BC, at approximately 30 years old, Xenophon joined the mercenary force of Cyrus the Younger, the Persian prince's bid to seize the throne from his brother Artaxerxes II, prompted by a recommendation from his Boeotian friend Proxenus and after consulting Socrates, who advised seeking Delphi's oracle. Following Cyrus's defeat at the Battle of Cunaxa, Xenophon emerged as a key leader in the retreat of the surviving Greek "Ten Thousand" mercenaries, an ordeal he chronicled in his Anabasis. This experience marked a turning point; accused of medism (favoring Persia) and oligarchic sympathies, he faced exile from democratic Athens around 399 BC after Socrates' execution.
Sparta provided refuge, granting Xenophon an estate at Scillus near Olympia, where he resided for about 20 years, writing extensively and aligning politically with Spartan interests. He participated in Spartan campaigns, including the Battle of Coronea in 394 BC alongside King Agesilaus II, whom he greatly admired. After Sparta's defeat at Leuctra in 371 BC, Xenophon returned to Athens, regaining citizenship, and continued his literary work until his death around 354 BC.
Xenophon's perspective in the Hellenica, composed as a continuation of Thucydides' history from 411 to 362 BC, is shaped by his disillusionment with Athenian democracy's excessessuch as the trial and execution of the Arginusae generalsand his preference for disciplined, hierarchical governance exemplified by Sparta. His narrative often portrays Spartan leaders like Agesilaus favorably, emphasizing their piety, strategic acumen, and adherence to oaths, while downplaying Spartan overreach or defeats, such as omitting the refounding of Messene after Leuctra, which undermined Spartan hegemony. This pro-Spartan tilt, evident in sympathetic justifications for Spartan interventions and criticism of Athenian demagogues, stems from his personal benefits from Spartan patronage and broader admiration for their constitution's stability over Athens' volatility. Scholars note his "ethical history" approach prioritizes moral lessons from human agency and leadership, viewed through a practical commander's lens rather than detached analysis, reflecting Socratic influences on personal virtue amid political chaos.

Relation to Thucydides' History

Xenophon's Hellenica commences its account in 411 BC, aligning precisely with the abrupt termination of Thucydides' History of the Peloponnesian War, which concludes amid the Sicilian expedition's aftermath and the Athenian oligarchic coup. Thucydides' narrative ends without resolution, leaving the Peloponnesian War's final phases undocumented in his text, a gap that Xenophon fills by resuming events from the same year, including the Spartan naval resurgence under Lysander. This chronological seam has led most scholars to interpret Hellenica as an intentional continuation, with Xenophon adopting a similar third-person, objective style to bridge the unfinished history.
Despite the continuity in subject matter and temporal starting point, Xenophon's approach diverges markedly from Thucydides' methodical emphasis on causation, speeches, and analytical depth. Thucydides prioritizes explanatory rigor, often embedding extended debates and psychological insights to elucidate motivations, whereas Xenophon employs a more annalistic, summary-driven structure, compressing events into yearly segments with fewer orations and less interpretive commentary. This shift results in Hellenica functioning less as a seamless extension and more as an independent chronicle, though textual allusions to Thucydidessuch as echoed phrasing in battle descriptions and political analysessuggest deliberate emulation. Some ancient traditions and modern hypotheses posit that Xenophon may have accessed Thucydides' unpublished notes or drafts to initiate his work, given their shared access to elite Athenian-Spartan circles, but direct evidence remains speculative and unverified.
Manuscript traditions further underscore the perceived linkage, as medieval codices frequently juxtapose the two histories, treating Hellenica as a de facto sequel despite Xenophon's silence on Thucydides in the text itself. This editorial convention reflects an early recognition of complementary coverage, spanning from the Archidamian War's onset through the Battle of Mantineia in 362 BC. However, scholarly consensus tempers claims of strict continuity, noting Xenophon's pro-Spartan sympathies and omission of Thucydidean themes like imperial overreach, which introduce biases absent in the predecessor. Both authors share interests in diplomatic documents and military tactics, yet Xenophon's briefer treatment prioritizes narrative flow over Thucydides' forensic dissection of power dynamics.

Composition and Structure

Date of Writing and Purpose

The Hellenica was composed in stages during the fourth century BCE, after Xenophon's exile from Athens circa 399 BCE, with the narrative extending to the Battle of Mantinea in 362 BCE, necessitating completion of the later books (Books 57) afterward. Scholarly analysis places initial drafting of Books 12 potentially in the 380s BCE, following key events like the end of the Corinthian War, while allusions to ongoing developments in Book 6 (e.g., 6.4.37) indicate revisions or additions in the 350s BCE, between approximately 357 and 353 BCE. This piecemeal approach reflects Xenophon's residence at Scillus in the Peloponnese, where he had access to Spartan records and participants, though exact chronology remains debated due to lack of direct authorial statements.
Xenophon's explicit purpose, inferred from the work's structure and content, was to extend Thucydides' unfinished History of the Peloponnesian War, which terminated mid-sentence in 411 BCE, into a systematic chronicle of Greek interstate conflicts and power dynamics through 362 BCE. Beyond mere factual record-keeping, the text emphasizes evaluative themes of political and military leadership, portraying success as tied to virtues like piety, self-control, and strategic foresight, often through speeches and character assessments that serve didactic ends. This moral framing distinguishes it from Thucydides' more analytical detachment, aligning with Xenophon's broader oeuvre, which prioritizes practical wisdom for elites over comprehensive causation or impartiality.

Division into Books and Narrative Breaks

Xenophon's Hellenica is transmitted in seven books, a division evident in the earliest surviving manuscripts and papyri, though likely added by later editors rather than by Xenophon himself, as book divisions in Greek historiography often postdate the original composition. The first two books cover the period from 411 BC, resuming Thucydides' unfinished account of the Peloponnesian War, through its conclusion in 404 BC and the immediate aftermath up to 403 BC, including the rule of the Thirty Tyrants in Athens and the restoration of democracy. Books 37 then narrate subsequent events, from Spartan hegemony and the Corinthian War (401387 BC) to the Theban ascendancy and the Battle of Mantinea in 362 BC, with a shift toward a more summary, year-by-year structure in places.
Scholars identify several narrative discontinuities that suggest the work may have been composed or revised in stages, rather than as a single continuous draft. One prominent break occurs at Hellenica 2.3.10, immediately after the Spartan victory at Aegospotami in 405 BC, where the detailed, Thucydidean-style engagement with events gives way to briefer treatment; this has led to theories that Xenophon initially wrote up to this point as a direct continuation of Thucydides before expanding the narrative later, possibly after his return from exile or in response to evolving political circumstances. Another seam appears between Books 2 and 3, marking the transition from the war's end to the era of Spartan dominance, with a abrupt shift from focused political intrigue to broader military annals, potentially reflecting interruptions in Xenophon's writing amid his involvement in events like the campaign of Cyrus the Younger in 401 BC. These breaks contrast with the seamless resumption of Thucydides' storyline at the outset, underscoring Xenophon's intent to provide a unified Greek history (Hellenica meaning "Greek affairs") while adapting to historiographical challenges like source gaps and personal biases.

Reliability and Sources

Xenophon's Eyewitness Accounts and Limitations

Xenophon provided eyewitness testimony for select events in the Hellenica, particularly those involving Spartan military campaigns during the Corinthian War (395387 BC), where he served as a cavalry commander under King Agesilaus II after his exile from Athens around 399 BC. His participation included the Spartan operations in Asia Minor from 396 to 395 BC and the Battle of Coronea in 394 BC, where Spartan forces, numbering approximately 6,000 hoplites, defeated a Boeotian-led alliance including Thebans and Athenians despite being outnumbered. These accounts feature granular details on troop movements, such as the Spartan phalanx's advance against superior numbers and the exploitation of terrain advantages, indicative of direct observation from the front lines.
Xenophon's firsthand involvement extended to the aftermath of the Persian Expedition in 401 BC, referenced in Hellenica Book 3, where he describes his role in the trial of the Greek generals following the Battle of Cunaxa, defending against charges of misconduct during the retreat of the Ten Thousand. This personal stake allowed for vivid depictions of logistical challenges and leadership decisions, though cross-referenced with his separate Anabasis for fuller context.
However, Xenophon's eyewitness capacity was geographically and temporally constrained, limiting his direct knowledge to Spartan-aligned activities primarily between 399 and 394 BC; he resided in exile at Scillus near Olympia thereafter, absent from pivotal clashes like the Battle of Leuctra in 371 BC or the Battle of Mantinea in 362 BC. For earlier events concluding the Peloponnesian War (411403 BC), his youtharound 2027 years oldlikely restricted him to Athenian perspectives without combat involvement, relying on oral reports or public records rather than personal experience.
Even in witnessed episodes, limitations arose from his embedded position within Spartan forces, potentially biasing tactical assessments toward allied viewpoints while underemphasizing adversaries' intentions or internal dissent; numerical estimates, such as enemy strengths at Coronea exceeding 14,000, may reflect approximations rather than precise counts. Memory lapses over decades and selective emphasis on virtuous leadershipfavoring figures like Agesilausfurther compromised objectivity, as later scholarly analyses note discrepancies with archaeological or epigraphic evidence from non-Spartan sites.

Biases, Omissions, and Pro-Spartan Leanings

Xenophon's personal circumstances contributed to perceptions of pro-Spartan partiality in the Hellenica. An Athenian aristocrat exiled after the Peloponnesian War, he received sanctuary in Sparta, including an estate at Scillus granted by the Spartan state, and developed close ties with King Agesilaus, for whom he fought at the Battle of Coronea in 394 BC. These connections, alongside his admiration for Spartan discipline and oligarchic stability, led scholars to identify a tendency to portray Spartan leaders and policies more sympathetically than those of rivals like Athens or Thebes. 
Manifestations of this leaning include selective detail in military narratives and omissions of events unfavorable to Sparta. For instance, Xenophon provides extensive coverage of Spartan campaigns in Asia Minor and Greece, emphasizing Agesilaus's tactical acumen, while underreporting Theban strategic innovations or the formation of the Second Athenian Confederacy around 377 BC, which challenged Spartan hegemony. He omits the refoundation of Messene in 369 BC after Theban liberation, focusing instead on Athenian errors, and gives minimal attention to figures like Pelopidas before key battles, potentially to avoid highlighting anti-Spartan agency. Such choices result in unequal treatment, with Spartan setbacks like the defeat at Leuctra in 371 BC attributed to divine retribution for impiety rather than systemic overextension or leadership errors. 
However, Xenophon's account is not uniformly uncritical, incorporating rebukes of Spartan flaws that undermine claims of naive partisanship. He depicts the seizure of the Theban Cadmea in 382 BC as an act of impiety initiating Sparta's downfall, links internal conspiracies like Cinadon's plot around 398 BC to elite-periokoi tensions, and portrays leaders such as Lysander and Agesilaus as ambitious to the point of harming Spartan interests, as in Lysander's hubris at Haliartus in 395 BC. These elements suggest a moral framework where Sparta exemplifies virtues like discipline but succumbs to vices such as greed and oath-breaking, contributing causally to its hegemony's collapse by 362 BC. 
Scholarly assessments debate the primacy of bias versus deliberate narrative structure. Traditional critiques emphasize pro-Spartan distortion, citing Xenophon's silences on Spartan atrocities or rival successes as evidence of partiality shaped by elite affiliations. Others argue omissions, such as the Arcadian Megalopolis foundation in 368367 BC, serve didactic purposesexemplifying virtue through Spartan loyalty or folly in democratic Athensrather than ideological favoritism, though his access to Spartan sources likely amplified positive portrayals. This tension reflects Xenophon's prioritization of aristocratic moral lessons over exhaustive chronology, informed by his firsthand observations but constrained by loyalties.

The Hellenica Oxyrhynchia (HO) consists of papyrus fragments discovered at Oxyrhynchus in Egypt, preserving an anonymous Greek history covering events from approximately 410 BC to 396 BC, including the final stages of the Peloponnesian War and the early Corinthian War. Likely composed in the early 4th century BC, possibly by an Athenian author such as Cratippus, the work exhibits a concise, unadorned style focused on factual narration of military and political developments, with minimal rhetorical embellishment or emotional pathos. 
In comparison to Xenophon's Hellenica, which spans 411362 BC and adopts a more continuous narrative with inserted speeches and moral evaluations, the HO provides an independent, often divergent account of overlapping events, serving as a key source for assessing Xenophon's selectivity and potential pro-Spartan biases. For instance, both works describe the Battle of Sardis in 395 BC, but Xenophon (Hellenica 3.4.2024) emphasizes Spartan tactical decisions and leadership, while the HO (chapter 11) highlights different causal factors, such as terrain and troop dispositions, presenting a less partisan analysis of Agesilaus's victory. Xenophon's eyewitness participation in some campaigns lends credibility to his details, yet the HO's variations suggest access to alternative traditions, potentially challenging Xenophon's omissions, such as fuller Athenian perspectives on the Ionian War's conclusion. Scholars note that Xenophon's coverage is more comprehensive overall, but the HO's sparseness avoids the former's occasional annalistic structure and Spartan favoritism, offering corroboration where aligned, as in broad outlines of the Elean War's chronology.
Among other contemporary or near-contemporary histories, the HO aligns with the broader genre of Hellenica works, including possible continuations by figures like Androtion or Ephorus, though surviving fragments limit direct comparisons. Later syntheses, such as Diodorus Siculus's Bibliotheca historica (1st century BC), draw on lost sources akin to the HO and Xenophon, occasionally preserving details absent in both, like expanded diplomatic contexts for the Corinthian War's outbreak, but these reflect 4th-century BC historiographical traditions rather than verbatim contemporaries. The HO's value lies in its relative neutrality compared to Xenophon's evaluative tone, providing empirical checks against the latter's moral judgments, though its fragmentary nature precludes comprehensive evaluation of either author's overall reliability.

Content Overview

Books 12: Conclusion of the Peloponnesian War (411403 BC)

Books 1 and 2 of Xenophon's Hellenica resume the narrative of the Peloponnesian War from the point where Thucydides' history abruptly ends in 411 BC, amid the Athenian oligarchic revolution and ongoing naval operations in the Hellespont. Xenophon details the fluctuating fortunes of the Athenian and Spartan fleets, emphasizing key naval battles, Persian involvement, and leadership changes that culminate in Athens' defeat. The account prioritizes military engagements and strategic decisions, with limited attention to broader diplomatic contexts beyond Sparta's alliances.
In Book 1, Xenophon chronicles events from 411 to 406 BC. Following the Spartan victory at Eretria, the Athenian fleet under Thrasybulus and Theramenes secures a win at Cynossema in late 411 BC, boosting morale despite the recent oligarchic turmoil in Athens. Subsequent clashes at Abydos in 411 BC see initial Spartan gains reversed by Athenian reinforcements, highlighting the vulnerability of Spartan admiral Mindarus. The decisive Athenian triumph at Cyzicus in 410 BC, led by Alcibiades, results in the capture of numerous Spartan ships and the death of Mindarus, severely weakening Sparta's position and prompting reliance on Persian funding from Pharnabazus. Athenian operations extend to recapturing Chalcedon and Byzantium in 408 BC after sieges, though internal politics lead to Alcibiades' exile following the inconclusive battle of Notium in 407 BC, where Lysander's deception exploits Athenian overconfidence. Book 1 concludes with the Spartan resurgence under Callicratidas, culminating in the Battle of Arginusae in 406 BC, where Athens' larger fleet inflicts heavy Spartan losses despite a storm complicating rescue efforts for survivors.
Book 2 shifts to the war's endgame from 406 to 403 BC. The Athenian assembly's controversial execution of the Arginusae generals for failing to recover the dead and shipwrecked sets a precedent for political retribution amid desperation. Lysander's second command enables the catastrophic Athenian defeat at Aegospotami in 405 BC; by luring the Athenian fleet ashore without defenses, he captures or destroys nearly all 180 triremes, leaving Athens blockaded and starved. Athens surrenders in early 404 BC after the walls' demolition, installing the pro-Spartan oligarchy of the Thirty Tyrants, led by Critias, who execute opponents and confiscate property to consolidate power with Spartan backing. Resistance emerges under Thrasybulus, who seizes Phyle in late 404 BC, rallying exiles and defeating the Thirty at Piraeus in 403 BC, forcing their flight to Eleusis. Spartan mediation under King Pausanias imposes a settlement reconciling democrats and moderates, restoring the democracy while granting amnesty to most oligarchs except the leading Thirty, marking the war's close.

Books 34: Corinthian War and Spartan Hegemony (401388 BC)

Books 3 and 4 of Xenophon's Hellenica chronicle Sparta's campaigns to enforce its hegemony from roughly 401 BC to the late 380s BC, beginning with punitive actions against Elis and internal threats before escalating into the broader Corinthian War against a coalition of Greek states backed by Persia. The narrative opens with the Spartan-Eleian War (402400 BC), initiated when Elis barred Spartans from the Olympic Games and supported anti-Spartan elements; King Agis II invaded, ravaging Eleian territory over multiple seasons until Elis capitulated, surrendering border regions, allies, and control of Olympia to Sparta. Xenophon emphasizes Spartan logistical superiority and Eleian disarray, portraying the victory as a restoration of traditional Peloponnesian order under Spartan oversight.
Internal Spartan stability is then tested by the conspiracy of Kinadon in 399 BC, as Xenophon describes: Kinadon, a disenfranchised Spartiate, plotted with perioikoi, helots, and neodamodeis to overthrow the ruling homoioi, exploiting grievances over wealth disparities and imperial burdens; the plot was exposed through a sting operation ordered by the ephors, leading to mass executions and reinforcing Sparta's secretive security apparatus. This episode precedes King Agesilaus II's Asian expedition (396395 BC), where he raided Persian territories with Pharnabazus's funding, aiming to liberate Ionian Greeks but ultimately recalled amid rising tensions in Greece.
The Corinthian War erupts in 395 BC, with Xenophon attributing its origins to Theban aggression against pro-Spartan Locris and Corinthian resentment over Spartan garrisons and tribute demands; Thebes allied with Corinth, Athens, and Argos, while Persia covertly funded the coalition to counter Spartan expansion. Sparta dispatched Lysander to Boeotia, but he fell at the Battle of Haliartus (summer 395 BC), where Theban forces ambushed his army during a siege, killing Lysander and several hundred Spartans despite a tactical withdrawal. Xenophon highlights Lysander's overconfidence and the coalition's numerical edge, framing the loss as a setback rather than a strategic reversal.
In 394 BC, Spartan arms reasserted dominance on land: at the Battle of Nemea (late summer), a Spartan-led force of approximately 6,000 hoplites under Aristodemus defeated a larger allied army (over 20,000), inflicting heavy casualties through disciplined phalanx maneuvers and exploiting allied disunity, with the Argive right wing routing early. Simultaneously or shortly after, Agesilaus, returning from Asia, clashed with Thebans and allies at Coronea (August 394 BC), where his 7,000-strong army prevailed in fierce close-quarters fighting, breaking the Theban left despite personal wounds to Agesilaus; Xenophon praises Agesilaus's leadership and piety, noting over 600 enemy dead versus lighter Spartan losses. However, naval fortunes reversed at Cnidus (394 BC), where Conon's 100+ triremes, bolstered by Persian funds and Pharnabazus's squadron, annihilated the Spartan fleet under Peisander (Lysander's brother-in-law), sinking or capturing nearly all 170 Spartan ships and restoring Athenian sea power.
Book 4 extends the stalemated Corinthian front into the 390s BC, detailing raids, fortifications, and asymmetric engagements around the Isthmus; a standout episode is the Battle of Lechaeum (ca. 390 BC), where 600 Spartan hoplites and peltasts under Praxitas and Aristarchus routed 600 Athenian marines led by Kabrias, killing over 200 through hit-and-run tactics that exposed hoplite vulnerabilities to light troops on open ground. Xenophon depicts ongoing attritionSpartan blockades of Corinth's long walls, Athenian fleet rebuilding under Conon (who reconstructed the Athenian walls by 393 BC with Persian aid), and failed siegesculminating in war weariness that paved the way for Persian-mediated talks. Though the King's Peace of 386 BC, imposing autonomy on Greek cities and ceding Asia Minor to Persia under Spartan enforcement, falls into Book 5, Books 34 underscore the limits of Spartan hegemony through overextension, coalition resilience, and Persian opportunism, with Xenophon's battle accounts emphasizing generalship and moral factors like discipline over sheer numbers.

Books 57: Decline of Sparta and Theban Ascendancy (388362 BC)

Books 57 of Xenophon's Hellenica chronicle the gradual erosion of Spartan hegemony following the Corinthian War, beginning with naval campaigns in the Aegean around 388 BC and concluding with the inconclusive Battle of Mantinea in 362 BC. Xenophon emphasizes Sparta's efforts to enforce the King's Peace (also known as the Peace of Antalcidas), a Persian-mediated treaty ratified in 387 BC that dissolved prior alliances and nominally placed all Greek city-states under Persian oversight while affirming Spartan preeminence in enforcing autonomy. This peace, dictated by Artaxerxes II, required the disbandment of the Boeotian League and other confederacies, but Sparta's aggressive interventionssuch as the campaign against Olynthus from 382 to 379 BC, where Spartan forces under Eudamidas and Polybiades subdued the Chalcidian league after sieges and battlesprovoked widespread resentment. Xenophon details how Spartan harmosts and kings like Agesilaus II extended influence into Arcadia and Thessaly, yet these actions alienated allies and fueled opposition from Thebes and a resurgent Athens.
Tensions escalated in 378 BC when the Spartan commander Sphodrias, bribed by Theban exiles, attempted a night raid on the Piraeus, failing but prompting Athens to rebuild its Long Walls and ally with Thebes against Sparta. Xenophon recounts Sparta's punitive expeditions into Boeotia, including the Battle of Tegyra in 375 BC, where Theban Sacred Band forces under Pelopidas ambushed and defeated a larger Spartan contingent led by Gorgoleon and Theopompus, marking an early sign of Theban resurgence despite Xenophon's minimization of the event's significance. Subsequent Spartan incursions, such as Agesilaus's victory at Coronea in 394 BC (though predating this period, echoed in ongoing conflicts), gave way to stalemates, with Book 5 closing on the eve of confrontation as Spartan king Cleombrotus I invaded Boeotia in winter 371 BC. Throughout, Xenophon attributes Spartan setbacks to treachery among allies and Persian duplicity rather than inherent weaknesses in Spartan strategy or manpower shortages, reflecting his admiration for Lacedaemonian discipline.
Book 6 centers on the catastrophic Spartan defeat at the Battle of Leuctra on July 6, 371 BC, where approximately 6,000 Thebans under Epaminondas faced 10,000 Spartans and allies commanded by Cleombrotus I. Xenophon describes how the Thebans deepened their left-wing phalanx to 50 shields deep and employed an oblique advance to concentrate force against the Spartan right, where Cleombrotus fell amid chaos; Spartan losses included 400 of their 700 Spartiates (elite citizen-soldiers), crippling their citizenry's breeding pool. This battle, per Xenophon, stemmed from Spartan internal discordCleombrotus's rash invasion against Agesilaus's counseland Theban violations of the peace, though he underemphasizes Epaminondas's tactical innovations compared to later historians. The aftermath saw Thebes assert dominance: Pelopidas secured Thessaly by 369 BC, while Epaminondas led invasions of the Peloponnese in 369 and 368 BC, allying with Arcadian dissidents to found Megalopolis and, critically, refound Messene in 369 BC using helot descendants, which halved Sparta's territory and revenue from klaroi (allotments). Xenophon laments Sparta's isolation, noting failed pleas to Persia and defections, yet praises Agesilaus's defensive stands, such as repelling Thebans at Sellasia.
Book 7 narrates the fragmented coalitions against Theban overreach, with Sparta rebuilding alliances alongside Athens, Elis, and Phocis amid Arcadian infighting and Sacred Wars in Thessaly. Epaminondas's third Peloponnesian campaign in 362 BC culminated in the Battle of Mantinea on July 4, where a Theban-Argive-Arcadian-Messenian force of about 30,000 clashed with a Spartan-Athenian-Elean coalition numbering similarly; Epaminondas again used oblique tactics, routing the Spartan right but dying from a javelin wound after breaking the center. Xenophon reports heavy casualties on both sidesover 1,000 Thebans and comparable foesbut no decisive victor, with Sparta holding its lines under Agesilaus. The narrative ends abruptly, with Xenophon moralizing on Greece's failure to unite under a stable leader post-Epaminondas, implying divine disfavor or human folly amid persistent factionalism; his pro-Spartan lens critiques Theban "tyranny" while excusing Lacedaemonian rigidity, as critiqued in modern analyses for selective omissions like full Spartan casualty figures from auxiliary troops. These books underscore causal chains of overextension and alliance fragility, with Sparta's decline tied empirically to demographic losses (fewer than 1,000 Spartiates by 371 BC) and economic disruption from Messenian independence.

Analytical Themes

Narrative Style and Historiographical Innovations

Xenophon's narrative style in the Hellenica employs plain Attic prose, characterized by simplicity, directness, brevity, and vivid detail in recounting military and political events chronologically from 411 to 362 BC. This episodic structure focuses on leaders' actions and outcomes, often assuming reader familiarity with prior events like those in Thucydides' History of the Peloponnesian War. Unlike Thucydides' analytical depth and causal explanations, Xenophon's approach is more descriptive and conversationalized, prioritizing ethical achievements through firm storylines and punchy conclusions.
Speeches form a key element, frequently rendered in direct discourse to reveal characters' moral qualities and motivations, adapting Thucydidean techniques but emphasizing speaker-appropriate propriety over rhetorical elaboration. Digressions, anecdotes, and sudden reversals (peripeteiai)marked by first-person interventionsinterrupt chronology to highlight virtues like piety, obedience, and self-control, as seen in accounts of Theban exiles' liberation (5.4.212) or Jason of Pherae's maneuvers. These devices infuse emotional content and dramatic contrast, drawing from Herodotean influences while infusing Socratic moral judgment.
Historiographically, Xenophon innovates by seamlessly continuing Thucydides without proem or recapitulation, treating the Hellenica as an extension to form a continuous record, though shifting to paradigmatic exempla over strict factual reconstruction. He subordinates completeness to didactic purpose, selectively omitting events (e.g., details of the Second Athenian Confederacy) to foreground moral paradigms, praising figures like Agesilaus for virtue while condemning impiety, as in divine retribution at Leuctra (6.4.78). This moralizing focus, integrating history with behavioral models, prefigures Hellenistic trends and distinguishes Xenophon from Thucydides' emphasis on reliability and political causation.

Role of Speeches and Rhetorical Devices

Xenophon employs speeches in the Hellenica primarily to delineate character traits, ethical motivations, and deliberative processes among leaders, thereby illuminating the causal factors behind historical outcomes rather than providing verbatim transcripts. These orations, often rendered in direct discourse, serve a didactic function by exemplifying virtues such as self-control (sphrosyn), piety, and strategic prudence, which Xenophon views as pivotal to effective governance and military success. Scholars observe that this approach reflects Xenophon's selective historiography, where speeches are crafted to prioritize moral exemplification over exhaustive detail, contrasting with Thucydides' more expansive, analytically probing compositions.
Rhetorical devices in these speeches are understated yet purposeful, favoring simplicity and naturalness over ornate elaboration, in line with Xenophon's broader stylistic restraint. Common techniques include gnomic maxims distilling practical wisdom, antithetical contrasts between virtue and vice, and appeals to pathos through evocations of communal solidarity or divine favor, which reinforce themes of leadership accountability. For example, speeches by figures like Procles of Phlius emphasize pragmatic counsel amid Athenian follies, using rhetorical questions and direct address to underscore the perils of demagoguery and the merits of moderation. This restrained rhetoric aligns with Xenophon's ethical framework, where persuasive speech demonstrates a speaker's moral authority rather than mere sophistic flair.
The integration of such elements advances Xenophon's narrative by embedding causal realism: speeches reveal how rhetorical efficacyor its absenceinfluences events, such as alliances forged or battles swayed by appeals to honor and fear. Analyses highlight that Xenophon's pro-Spartan sympathies subtly shape these portrayals, privileging orators who embody Spartan-like discipline, though he maintains consistency in using speeches to critique broader Greek failings like factionalism. Ultimately, these devices transform the Hellenica into a moral lens on history, guiding readers toward discerning patterns of success rooted in character-driven persuasion.

Military Strategy, Leadership, and Tyranny

Xenophon's Hellenica details numerous military campaigns and battles, portraying Greek interstate conflicts as characterized by continuous warfare rather than decisive resolutions, with strategies often revolving around sieges, raids, and opportunistic engagements rather than grand maneuvers. For instance, Agesilaus' campaigns in Asia Minor from 396 to 394 BC employed tactical raids to weaken Persian satraps, culminating in a victory at Sardis in 395 BC through disciplined infantry charges and effective scouting, demonstrating Xenophon's emphasis on mobility and surprise over static confrontations. Rational battle-planning is balanced with military divination, as leaders consult omens before engagements, yet Xenophon underscores human effort and preparation as complementary to divine favor.
Leadership in Hellenica is depicted as requiring moral virtues such as piety, self-control, and relentless training to inspire troop loyalty and achieve success. Agesilaus exemplifies these qualities, training his forces rigorously and demonstrating obedience to Spartan customs, which Xenophon credits for his early victories and personal endurance during retreats. Similarly, commanders like Dercylidas persuaded cities to defect through reasoned persuasion rather than coercion, maintaining alliances via justice and effort (Hellenica 4.8.4-6). In contrast, leaders failing in selflessness, such as Mnasippus, alienated their troops through harsh treatment, leading to defeat and death at Corcyra in 373 BC (6.2.20-23). Xenophon illustrates strategies for army cohesion under stress, including emotional appeals, deception, and equitable rewards, as seen in Teleutias' naval revival through displays of personal commitment (5.1.14-24).
Tyranny emerges as the antithesis of effective leadership, with Xenophon portraying tyrants as impious figures whose hubris invites divine retribution and societal backlash. The Thirty Tyrants in Athens (404-403 BC), led by Critias, are shown executing democratic opponents and confiscating property, their rule collapsing due to internal violence and exile resistance, ultimately restored democracy through Thrasybulus' intervention. Jason of Pherae, initially rising through disciplined mercenary forces (6.1.5-13), degenerates into tyranny marked by impiety, meeting assassination as punishment (6.4.19-32). Xenophon uses these narratives proscriptively to contrast tyrannical excessgreed, injustice, neglect of pietywith virtuous rule, embedding moral causality where poor leadership precipitates downfall, influencing later historiographical topoi.

Political Realism and Moral Judgments

Xenophon's Hellenica depicts Greek politics through a realist lens, emphasizing the pursuit of power, strategic prudence, and the contingencies of leadership amid shifting alliances and hegemonies, while subordinating these to an underlying moral framework that privileges virtues such as piety, self-control, and justice. Unlike Thucydides' more analytical detachment, Xenophon's narrative integrates Socratic-influenced evaluations of character, where leaders' success or failure often hinges on alignment with ethical norms rather than mere expediency, as seen in his selective omissions and structuring of events to highlight moral causation over chronological exhaustiveness. 
Moral judgments in the Hellenica frequently manifest implicitly through outcomes attributed to divine retribution against impiety, such as the Spartans' seizure of the Cadmea in Thebes (382 BC), condemned as hubristic and leading to their defeat at Leuctra (371 BC) as a form of nemesis. Similarly, the Corinthian massacre (392 BC) draws explicit rebuke for its sacrilege, underscoring Xenophon's view that violations of piety undermine political stability, even for dominant powers like Sparta. These assessments reflect a causal realism where moral failings precipitate decline, contrasting with purely power-based explanations and informing Xenophon's pro-Spartan yet critical stance on their overreach.
Evaluations of individual leaders blend realist prudence with moral scrutiny; Agesilaus, for instance, exemplifies effective governance through pious restraint and tactical foresight, as in his Asian campaigns (396394 BC), where obedience to oracles and disciplined command secure loyalty and victories. In contrast, Jason of Pherae (c. 380 BC) initially displays pragmatic counsel against rash Theban aggression but succumbs to tyrannical excess, hinted at by his handling of Delphi's treasures, culminating in assassination as moral corruption erodes his rule. Such portrayals prioritize leaders who cultivate sophrosyne (self-mastery) and devotion to followers, yielding willing obedience over coerced dominance.
The pervasive role of violence in the Hellenica further illustrates this interplay, with Xenophon critiquing illegitimate bia (force) and hybris in stasis and imperialism, as in Spartan interventions that desensitize actors to atrocities like the Aegospotami executions (405 BC), yet he reserves legitimate violence for state-ordered justice. Narratives of internecine strife, such as Phlebasian loyalty amid betrayals (c. 368 BC), highlight moral resilience against chaotic power grabs, reinforcing that prudence divorced from virtue invites failure. This framework underscores Xenophon's didactic intent, using history to caution against the perils of amoral realpolitik.

Religion, Piety, and Divine Causality

In Xenophon's Hellenica, piety toward the gods is depicted as a foundational virtue for leaders, fostering a reciprocal relationship whereby divine favor aids success in war and politics while impiety invites retribution. Xenophon portrays effective commanders as those who routinely perform sacrifices, consult oracles and seers, and heed divine signs, thereby securing the gods' goodwill before undertakings. This theological framework underscores his belief in divine causality, where historical outcomes reflect the gods' justice rather than chance or solely human agency, though such interventions are invoked judiciously, often to explain pivotal reversals or to reinforce moral lessons.
King Agesilaus II of Sparta exemplifies Xenophon's ideal of pious leadership. In Books 34, Xenophon details Agesilaus' accession guided by the Delphic Oracle's response to Lysander's query (3.3.14), followed by meticulous pre-campaign rituals, including sacrifices for favorable omens before his 396 BC invasion of Asia Minor (3.4.34). Xenophon attributes Agesilaus' early victories, such as at Sardis, to this devotion, presenting piety as instrumental in rallying troops and averting disasters, in contrast to leaders who neglect it. Such practices align with Xenophon's broader Socratic-influenced view that eusebeia (piety) ensures divine assistance, as echoed in his emphasis on Agesilaus avoiding impious acts to maintain harmony with the gods.
Conversely, Xenophon links impiety with downfall, particularly among tyrants and overreaching powers. In digressions on figures like the Theban Euphron (5.4.112), he draws explicit ties between tyrannical behaviormarked by sacrilege and disregard for oathsand subsequent ruin, implying divine punishment as causal. The Spartan defeat at Leuctra in 371 BC (6.4) features omens like an earthquake and lightning, which Xenophon notes but attributes authentically to divine displeasure rather than manipulation by Theban leaders (6.4.78), critiquing impious hybris as a contributing factor in Sparta's hegemony's collapse. This pattern reflects Xenophon's conviction that violations of religious norms disrupt the divine-human order, leading to inexorable consequences, as seen in the gods' sporadic but decisive interventions amid human strife.

Scholarly Reception and Debates

Ancient and Byzantine Evaluations

Dionysius of Halicarnassus praised Xenophon's choice of noble and significant historical subjects in the Hellenica, while characterizing his prose as charming and pure, though deficient in grandeur and vigor when compared to predecessors like Herodotus or Thucydides. Plutarch relied extensively on the Hellenica as a primary source for his Parallel Lives, particularly in biographies such as Agesilaus and Pelopidas, where he cited Xenophon's accounts of Spartan campaigns and Theban maneuvers, indicating its perceived utility for detailed military and political narrative. Later fourth-century historians like Theopompus of Chios responded to Xenophon's work by composing a rival Hellenica that emphasized different emphases, such as moral critiques of leaders, suggesting awareness of Xenophon's selective focus on Spartan perspectives and potential omissions of broader ethical dimensions.
In Byzantine scholarship, the Hellenica enjoyed preservation through manuscript copying and inclusion in educational curricula, valued for its Attic Greek purity and as a stylistic model amid the Macedonian Renaissance's revival of classical texts. Photius, in his Bibliotheca, referenced Xenophon's historical writings approvingly in contexts of stylistic imitation, aligning them with ideals of clear and unadorned prose that influenced later Byzantine authors. The Suda lexicon cataloged the Hellenica among Xenophon's major compositions, affirming its status as a canonical continuation of Thucydides' history from 411 to 362 BC, though usage remained selective, often prioritizing Xenophon's ethical and rhetorical elements over comprehensive factual scrutiny. This reception reflected broader Byzantine preferences for works blending narrative accessibility with moral instruction, despite occasional critiques of Xenophon's perceived partisanship toward Sparta.

Modern Criticisms of Accuracy and Tendentiousness

Modern scholars have frequently criticized Xenophon's Hellenica for historical inaccuracies, including chronological discrepancies and factual errors in military and diplomatic events. For instance, Xenophon's account of the Corinthian War (Books 34) contains inconsistencies in dating key battles and alliances, such as the imprecise timing of Spartan engagements against the coalition of Athens, Thebes, Corinth, and Argos around 395387 BC, which modern reconstructions from epigraphic evidence and Diodorus Siculus correct to earlier occurrences. Similarly, his treatment of the Battle of Cnidus in 394 BC omits critical details about Persian naval contributions, understating Conon's role in restoring Athenian sea power, as corroborated by later Hellenistic inscriptions. These lapses stem partly from Xenophon's reliance on personal recollections and second-hand reports rather than systematic archival research, leading to a narrative prone to compression and oversight. 
Critics also highlight tendentious omissions that skew the historical record, often attributed to Xenophon's pro-Spartan sympathies. He devotes disproportionate attention to Spartan campaigns under Agesilaus while neglecting the formation of the Second Athenian Confederacy in 377 BC, a pivotal anti-Spartan alliance that reshaped Greek power dynamics, mentioning it only peripherally despite its inscriptional documentation. This selective focus extends to downplaying Spartan atrocities, such as the harsh garrisoning of Thebes or the refoundation of Messene in 369 BC after Leuctra, events that underscored Sparta's hegemonic overreach and are detailed in Pausanias and archaeological finds. Scholars argue this reflects Xenophon's admiration for Spartan austerity and leadership, inherited from his oligarchic Athenian background and exile experiences, resulting in an ideologically driven historiography that favors moral exemplars over comprehensive causality. 
Further tendentiousness appears in Xenophon's portrayal of Theban ascendancy (Books 57), where he attributes Spartan decline primarily to moral failings like impiety and hubris rather than structural military weaknesses, as evidenced by the tactical innovations at Leuctra in 371 BC under Epaminondas, which Diodorus and archaeological analyses emphasize as decisive. This interpretive lens, prioritizing piety and divine retribution, aligns with Xenophon's broader Socratic-influenced ethics but compromises neutrality, prompting accusations of didactic bias over empirical fidelity. While some defend these choices as reflective of contemporary Greek historiographical norms, the consensus views Hellenica as less reliable than Thucydides for causal analysis due to such authorial predispositions. 

Defenses of Xenophon's Causal Insights and Value

Scholars defend Xenophon's causal insights in the Hellenica by emphasizing his focus on human agency, particularly the virtues and vices of leaders, as primary drivers of political and military outcomes, rather than abstract forces or excessive divine intervention. Vivienne Gray contends that Xenophon's narrative employs characterization to illustrate causal patterns, where successes stem from prudent leadership and piety, while failures arise from hubris and moral lapses, as seen in his portrayal of Sparta's post-war overextension leading to its defeat at Leuctra in 371 BC. This approach aligns with causal realism by linking specific decisionssuch as Agesilaus's campaigns in Asiato broader consequences like Theban resurgence, providing readers tools to evaluate historical contingencies through character-driven analysis.
Xenophon's value as a historian is upheld for his eyewitness perspective and technical expertise, which lend credibility to his accounts of tactics and logistics absent in predecessors like Thucydides. Having served as a mercenary commander and advisor to Spartan king Agesilaus II during the campaigns of 396394 BC, Xenophon offers detailed, plausible reconstructions of battles, such as the Corinthian War engagements, corroborated by later sources like Diodorus Siculus. Leo Strauss highlights Xenophon's historiographical subtlety, arguing that the Hellenica's composition teaches political wisdom by contrasting Spartan discipline's initial efficacy with its erosion due to imperial greed, eschewing overt moralizing for implicit lessons in prudence. These elements counter accusations of superficiality, positioning the work as a deliberate continuation of Thucydidean inquiry into power dynamics, enriched by Xenophon's Socratic influences on ethical causation.
Critics' claims of pro-Spartan bias are mitigated by defenses noting Xenophon's even-handed exposure of flaws across poleis, including Athenian and Theban errors, with his omissions often reflecting source limitations rather than deliberate distortion. Gray further argues that Xenophon's "charm" in storytellingneat resolutions tying actions to outcomesserves didactic purposes, fostering reader discernment of causal chains without fabricating events, as verified against epigraphic evidence like the King's Peace treaty of 386 BC. In reappraisals, such as those in the Cambridge Companion, Xenophon's corpus demonstrates historiographical innovation by integrating moral evaluation with empirical detail, preserving invaluable records of fourth-century BC interstate relations where alternatives like the Hellenica Oxyrhynchia fragments prove fragmentary and less insightful on leadership causality. Thus, the Hellenica retains enduring value for understanding how personal agency shaped Greek hegemony's shifts, prioritizing verifiable patterns over ideological narratives.

Related Works

Hellenica Oxyrhynchia as Alternative Account

The Hellenica Oxyrhynchia comprises fragments of an anonymous Greek history discovered in the Oxyrhynchus papyri excavations in Egypt, with principal fragments (P.Oxy. 5.842) published in 1906 by Bernard Grenfell and Arthur Hunt. Additional papyri, including PSI 1304 (1934) and Cairo fragments, extend the surviving text to approximately 900 lines, primarily from the late first to third centuries AD copies. The work covers Greek affairs from circa 411 BC (continuing Thucydides) to around 387 BC, encompassing the final phase of the Peloponnesian War, Spartan hegemony, and the Corinthian War's onset.
As an alternative to Xenophon's Hellenica, which narrates 411362 BC with a pro-Spartan emphasis, the Oxyrhynchia offers an independent, often contrasting narrative, likely composed by a fourth-century BC author familiar with Asia Minor and Persian affairs. Scholars value it for filling gaps in Xenophon's account, such as detailed military logistics and synchronistic dating, while exhibiting a style blending Thucydidean analysis with Herodotean breadth. Unlike Xenophon's selective omissionsattributable to his Spartan sympathies and personal tiesthe Oxyrhynchia provides fuller tactical descriptions, suggesting greater neutrality on Spartan shortcomings.
Notable divergences include the Corinthian War's origins: Xenophon attributes it chiefly to local dissatisfactions, whereas the Oxyrhynchia highlights Persian agent Timocrates' distribution of 50 talents in 395 BC to Theban and other leaders, catalyzing anti-Spartan alliancesa detail Xenophon minimizes. Similarly, on the Battle of Sardis (395 BC), the Oxyrhynchia (ch. 11) depicts Spartan commander Lysander's forces outmaneuvered by Pharnabazus' Persian cavalry in a more humiliating rout than Xenophon's brief, sanitized version (Hellenica 3.4.2024), emphasizing tactical errors like inadequate scouting. These contrasts enable historiographical cross-verification, with modern analyses often preferring the Oxyrhynchia's detail for events like Iphicrates' operations or Elean War chronology, despite both sources' limitations as non-contemporary.
The Oxyrhynchia's potential as a response to Xenophon underscores differing agendas: Xenophon's moralizing and oligarchic lens versus the former's focus on causation and reliability assessment of reports. Diodorus Siculus (15th book) likely drew from it independently of Ephorus, affirming its circulation and credibility among later historians. Authorship debatesfavoring figures like Cratippusremain unresolved, but its fragments' preservation highlights its role in challenging Xenophon's dominance as the primary post-Thucydidean source.

Other Histories Titled Hellenica or Covering Similar Periods

Anaximenes of Lampsacus (c. 380320 BC), a rhetorician and historian associated with Alexander the Great, composed a Hellenica in twelve books that traced Greek history from the mythological origins of the gods to the death of Epaminondas at the Battle of Mantinea in 362 BC. This scope overlapped extensively with Xenophon's narrative, providing an alternative account of the Corinthian War (395387 BC), Spartan hegemony, and Theban resurgence, though only fragments and citations by later authors like Athenaeus and Plutarch survive.
Theopompus of Chios (c. 380after 315 BC), another fourth-century BC historian, wrote a Hellenica in twelve books explicitly as a continuation of Thucydides' History of the Peloponnesian War, commencing around 411 BC and extending into the early phases of Macedonian ascendancy. Known for its moralistic tone and emphasis on decline, including critiques of Greek character flaws, Theopompus' work survives in scattered fragments preserved in authors such as Plutarch and Polybius, offering insights into events like the King's Peace of 387 BC and the Sacred War.
Cratippus of Athens, active in the late fifth and early fourth centuries BC, is attested as an early continuator of Thucydides with an eighth book extending the History to cover the war's conclusion and immediate aftermath, though no substantial fragments remain and its precise title as Hellenica is inferred from the historiographical tradition. These lost or fragmentary Hellenica reflect a competitive genre among fourth-century historians, often structured annalistically or thematically to rival Xenophon's version, with differences in emphasis on causation and bias noted by ancient critics like Dionysius of Halicarnassus.
Beyond strictly titled Hellenica, later compilations like Diodorus Siculus' Bibliotheca historica (c. 6030 BC) cover the same era in Books 1416, detailing the Ionian War's end in 404 BC, the Thirty Tyrants' regime, Spartan imperialism until 371 BC, and Theban victories at Leuctra, primarily derived from Ephorus of Cumae's fourth-century history. Ephorus' own universal history (c. 405330 BC), spanning from the Persian return of the Heracleidae to Philip II's death in 336 BC across thirty books, provided a comprehensive framework for these events, prioritizing political and military causality over personal anecdotes, though it survives mainly through Diodorus' excerpts. Such works, while not contemporaneously composed, preserve alternative perspectives on the period's power shifts, often highlighting economic factors and diplomatic maneuvers absent or understated in Xenophon.
