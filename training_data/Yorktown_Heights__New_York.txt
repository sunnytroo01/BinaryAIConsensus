 Yorktown Heights is a census-designated place (CDP) in the town of Yorktown, Westchester County, New York, United States, serving as one of the town's primary business hamlets and encompassing a land area of approximately 0.91 square miles. Situated in northern Westchester County about 35 miles north of New York City, it features a suburban landscape of rolling hills within the broader 40-square-mile town that includes residential neighborhoods, parks, and open spaces.
The area shares in the town's rich history, first settled in 1683 and playing a strategic role during the American Revolutionary War, where the Pines Bridge crossing of the Croton River was guarded by the 1st Rhode Island Regiment, an integrated unit that included African American soldiers. The town of Yorktown was incorporated in 1788 and named after the pivotal Revolutionary War victory at Yorktown, Virginia, with Yorktown Heights developing as a key commercial center featuring historic sites such as the Yorktown Heights Railroad Station, which anchored the community in the late 19th century. Today, the CDP is home to several preserved landmarks tied to colonial and Revolutionary eras, including structures like the Hyatt House and Lanes Tavern, contributing to the town's designation of 14 historic sites overall.
As of 2023, Yorktown Heights has a population of 2,038 residents, with a median age of 45.2 years and a diverse racial composition that includes 66% White and 33% Hispanic or Latino individuals. The community boasts a high median household income of $138,750, reflecting its affluent suburban character, and benefits from the town's excellent school system and proximity to recreational areas like Teatown Lake Reservation and Hilltop Hanover Farm.

History

Early Settlement and Colonial Period

The area now known as Yorktown Heights began its European settlement in 1683, when English colonists, many originating from Connecticut amid border disputes between the colonies, established initial farms and mills along the Croton River as part of the expansive Van Cortlandt Manor. Stephanus Van Cortlandt, a prominent New York official, initiated these purchases by acquiring tracts from Native American sachems, including Pewemind and Oskewan of the Kitchawank tribe, a branch of the Wappinger Confederacy that had inhabited the region for centuries. These early interactions involved negotiated land transfers to facilitate settlement, though tensions over territory persisted as European expansion displaced indigenous communities. The settlers focused on clearing forested land for agriculture, leveraging the fertile soils near the river for sustenance and trade.
The original land grants forming the basis of Yorktown's colonial development were consolidated under the Van Cortlandt Manor patent issued by King William III in 1697, encompassing over 86,000 acres in northern Westchester County and extending influences from Connecticut's "Oblong" region. This manor system structured land distribution, with tenant farmers leasing plots from the Van Cortlandt family while retaining rights to improvements like homes and orchards. Prior to 1700, Native American relations were primarily transactional, centered on deeds that acknowledged tribal use rights but prioritized colonial expansion, as evidenced by Van Cortlandt's multiple purchases from local Lenape groups to legitimize claims under English law.
The early economy of the settlement revolved around agriculture, with wheat serving as a staple crop exported to New York City markets and apples cultivated for cider production and local consumption, reflecting broader Hudson Valley practices introduced by English and Dutch colonists. Mills along the Croton River processed grain into flour, supporting self-sufficient farmsteads that dotted the landscape with stone walls and wooden structures. This agrarian foundation persisted through the mid-18th century, laying groundwork for the area's strategic role during the Revolutionary War.

Revolutionary Era and 19th Century Growth

During the American Revolutionary War, the area now known as Yorktown Heights served as a strategically vital location in Westchester County's "Neutral Ground," the contested zone between British-held New York City and Patriot forces in the Hudson Highlands. The Pines Bridge crossing over the Croton River was a key supply and communication route for the Continental Army, enabling the transport of provisions and troops while disrupting British foraging expeditions. Local Westchester County militia units initially guarded the bridge and surrounding posts, participating in skirmishes to protect Patriot lines from Loyalist raiders known as the "Cowboys" led by Col. James DeLancey.
A notable event occurred on May 14, 1781, when DeLancey's Loyalist forces launched a surprise attack on the Pines Bridge guard post, resulting in the deaths of Col. Christopher Greene of the 1st Rhode Island Regiment, Maj. Ebenezer Flagg, and several enlisted men, including African American and Native American soldiersan incident remembered as the Massacre at Pines Bridge. In early 1781, the 1st Rhode Island Regiment, an integrated unit, had been deployed to guard the defenses, with the unit continuing to secure the route through late 1781 as French and American forces marched south toward the decisive victory at Yorktown, Virginia. Local militia from Westchester, including figures like John Odell, contributed to broader regional efforts, such as patrols and support for major actions like the Battle of White Plains in 1776, through 1783.
Following the war, the Town of Yorktown was officially incorporated in 1788, named in honor of the 1781 surrender of British forces at Yorktown, Virginia, which effectively ended major hostilities. The local economy, rooted in farming since colonial times, began a gradual diversification in the early 19th century toward small-scale manufacturing, with operations like gristmills for grain processing and tanneries for leather production appearing along streams to harness water power. These ventures supplemented agricultural output, processing local crops and livestock byproducts for regional markets.
The arrival of rail infrastructure marked a turning point in growth. The Yorktown Heights Railroad Station, constructed in 1877 as part of the New York, Westchester & Putnam Railway's line (with regular service commencing in 1881), became the hamlets commercial hub, surrounded by stores, a hotel, and services that spurred trade in farm goods and manufactured items. This connectivity contributed to steady population expansion, from approximately 1,600 residents in the town around 1800 to about 2,400 by 1900, reflecting broader suburbanization trends in Westchester County.

20th Century Development and Modern Changes

Following World War II, Yorktown Heights underwent significant suburbanization as the area transitioned from its rural roots, with the construction of numerous housing developments to accommodate growing families seeking proximity to New York City. This period marked a shift toward residential expansion, including single-family homes and early cooperative apartments, driven by improved roadways and the broader postwar housing boom in Westchester County. The local railroad station, a key 19th-century link built in 1877, closed on May 29, 1958, due to declining ridership on the Putnam Division line, reflecting the increasing reliance on automobiles for suburban commuting.
The arrival of the IBM Thomas J. Watson Research Center in 1961 catalyzed a major population boom and transformed Yorktown Heights into a technology-oriented suburb. Designed by architect Eero Saarinen and serving as IBM Research headquarters, the facility attracted scientists, engineers, and their families, employing over 1,500 people by the mid-1980s and stimulating local economic activity through associated residential construction. This influx contributed to rapid demographic growth, with the broader Town of Yorktown's population increasing 14 percent from 28,028 in 1970 to 31,988 in 1980, bucking countywide trends of stagnation. In recognition of its distinct community identity amid this expansion, Yorktown Heights was officially designated a census-designated place (CDP) by the U.S. Census Bureau in 1980.
As of 2025, Yorktown Heights continues to experience residential growth amid Westchester County's broader development pressures, including proposals for mixed-use projects such as a nearly 50-acre commercial and multifamily development at 3000 Old Yorktown Road, which has sparked community opposition over infrastructure strains. The town's Heritage Preservation Commission actively works to balance this expansion by protecting historic sites, such as the restored Yorktown Heights Railroad Station listed on the National Register of Historic Places since 1981, through advocacy, education, and policy recommendations to maintain the area's suburban character. These efforts align with countywide initiatives like Westchester 2025, which emphasize sustainable growth while preserving environmental and cultural assets.

Geography

Location and Physical Features

Yorktown Heights is a hamlet and census-designated place in the Town of Yorktown, situated in northern Westchester County, New York, approximately 40 miles north of Midtown Manhattan. Its geographic coordinates are 411615N 734639W. The area encompasses 0.92 square miles of land, with no water bodies included in its boundaries.
The terrain of Yorktown Heights is characterized by rolling hills and dense woodlands, typical of the Appalachian foothills in the region, with elevations ranging from about 200 feet to around 500 feet above sea level in the hamlet proper. Notable natural elevations include Turkey Mountain, which rises to 831 feet and offers views of surrounding landscapes. The area lies in close proximity to the Croton River and the New Croton Reservoir, contributing to its varied topography of valleys and ridges.
Yorktown Heights shares boundaries with adjacent hamlets within the Town of Yorktown, including Shrub Oak to the north and Crompond to the east, forming part of a broader suburban-rural mosaic. Environmental features such as northern hardwood forests and scattered wetlands enhance its ecological diversity and support a mix of residential development amid preserved natural areas.

Climate and Environmental Conditions

Yorktown Heights experiences a humid continental climate (Kppen classification Dfa), characterized by four distinct seasons with cold, snowy winters and warm, humid summers. According to 1991-2020 normals from the National Centers for Environmental Information (NCEI), the area has an annual average high temperature of 60.1F and low of 38.7F, with total annual precipitation measuring 50.30 inches and snowfall averaging 39.5 inches. These conditions reflect the broader northeastern U.S. pattern, where precipitation is evenly distributed throughout the year, supporting lush vegetation but also contributing to occasional flooding risks.
Seasonal variations are pronounced, with July featuring an average high of 83.0F and humid conditions that can lead to discomfort, while January averages a high of 35.5F and low of 16.7F, often resulting in freezing temperatures and significant snow accumulation. The location in the Hudson Valley introduces a moderating microclimate influenced by the nearby Hudson River, which helps temper extreme cold in winter and excessive heat in summer compared to more inland areas, fostering slightly milder conditions overall. This valley effect also enhances local fog and dew formation, particularly in low-lying areas.
Environmental conditions in Yorktown Heights are shaped by its suburban position approximately 40 miles north of New York City, leading to air quality challenges from regional pollutants transported northward, including ozone and fine particulates that result in moderate to unhealthy levels on certain days. The area receives a "D" grade for ozone pollution from the American Lung Association's State of the Air 2025 report, highlighting the impact of urban emissions on downwind suburbs like Westchester County. Conservation efforts focus on preserving local biodiversity amid these pressures; the Town of Yorktown's Conservation Board oversees natural resource protection through land use reviews and open space initiatives, while the Climate Smart Community designation supports habitat enhancement and native plant programs at sites like Hilltop Hanover Farm to bolster ecological resilience as of 2025.

Government

Town Governance Structure

The Town of Yorktown, encompassing the hamlet of Yorktown Heights, operates under a standard New York town government structure with a supervisor as chief executive and a legislative town board. The supervisor oversees daily administration and serves as the chief fiscal officer, while the five-member boardcomprising the supervisor and four elected council membersexercises legislative powers, including adopting the annual budget, enacting local laws, and regulating zoning and land use in accordance with New York Town Law.
As of November 2025, the town supervisor is Ed Lachterman, a Republican first elected in a 2024 special election and reelected to a full two-year term on November 4, 2025, ending December 31, 2027. The current town board includes council members Sergio Esposito (Republican, deputy supervisor), Luciana Haughwout (Republican), Patrick Murphy (Republican), and Susan Siegel (Democrat). Council members are elected to staggered four-year terms, with two seats contested in each biennial election alongside the supervisor's race.
In the November 4, 2025, election, incumbent Sergio Esposito retained his seat as the top vote-getter in a four-way contest for two board positions, while Democratic challenger Ilan D. Gilbert secured the second seat after a late count of early votes overtook incumbent Luciana Haughwout; Esposito and Gilbert are scheduled to be sworn in on January 1, 2026, joining holdover council members Murphy and Siegel.
Yorktown Heights functions as the town's administrative center, housing Yorktown Town Hall at 363 Underhill Avenue, but as an unincorporated hamlet, it has no independent government and is fully administered by the town board. Community input is facilitated through appointed advisory committees, such as the Zoning Board of Appeals and Planning Board, which provide recommendations on local matters without separate hamlet-level authority.

The annual budgeting cycle involves public hearings and adoption by the Town Board, ensuring fiscal responsibility amid rising operational costs, with revenues allocated to support departments like police, fire, and public works.

Academically, YCSD demonstrates high performance, achieving a 95% four-year graduation rate for the class of 2023, exceeding state averages. Facilities enhancements underscore the district's commitment to modern education, with voters approving a $57 million capital bond in February 2024 to fund renovations such as upgraded science labs, technology-integrated classrooms, and expanded instructional spaces to accommodate enrollment and programmatic demands. Transportation

Road Networks and Access

Yorktown Heights is primarily served by U.S. Route 202, which functions as the main east-west artery through the hamlet, providing connectivity to surrounding areas in Westchester County. This route, concurrent with New York State Route 35 along Crompond Road, intersects with NY 118 (Saw Mill River Road) at the commercial center of Yorktown Heights, facilitating local traffic flow and access to nearby communities like Peekskill to the west. The intersection at US 202, NY 35, and NY 118 serves as a key junction for both through traffic and local destinations.
For north-south travel, the Taconic State Parkway lies approximately one mile east of central Yorktown Heights, with Exit 17A providing direct access to US 202 and NY 35. This limited-access parkway offers efficient connectivity southward toward New York City, with typical drive times ranging from 50 to 70 minutes to Manhattan under normal conditions, making it a preferred route for commuters avoiding urban congestion.
Local roadways, maintained by the Town of Yorktown's Highway Department, include approximately 200 miles of town roads and bridges, supporting residential and commercial access throughout the area. Underhill Avenue, a prominent local road, leads directly to the Yorktown Town Hall at 363 Underhill Avenue and connects to major routes like NY 118. In late 2023, a $4.1 million paving project began on a three-mile stretch of US 202/NY 35 from the Taconic State Parkway to the NY 118 intersection, with an expected completion by May 2024, involving resurfacing, milling, and ADA-compliant curb ramp improvements to enhance traffic flow and safety.
Regional access is further bolstered by proximity to Interstate 87 (New York State Thruway), located about 10 miles east, which connects to the Mario Cuomo Bridge and broader interstate networks, benefiting commuters traveling to the Bronx or beyond.

Public Transit Options

Public transit in Yorktown Heights primarily relies on the Westchester County Bee-Line Bus System, which operates several local routes serving the area and connecting to key destinations within the county. Route 15 provides direct service from Yorktown Heights to White Plains, with stops along major roads like Route 202 and Route 118, operating weekdays and limited weekends for commuters and shoppers. Additional routes, such as the 12 and 16, offer connections to nearby towns like Mahopac and Peekskill, facilitating travel to regional hubs without personal vehicles. Fares are $2.75 for a single ride, with options for reduced rates via MetroCard for seniors and disabled riders. As of November 2025, the Bee-Line system is transitioning to the OMNY contactless payment system, with MetroCard sales ending on December 31, 2025, while still accepting MetroCards for reduced fares until then.
For rail access, residents must travel to the nearest Metro-North Railroad Harlem Line station at Cortlandt, approximately 6 miles west of Yorktown Heights, reachable by bus or short drive. From Cortlandt, hourly trains reach Grand Central Terminal in Manhattan in about 1 hour, with fares ranging from $12 to $19 depending on peak or off-peak times. Yorktown Heights lacks its own active rail station, as the historic Yorktown Heights Railroad Station on the former Putnam Division closed to passenger service on May 29, 1958, due to declining ridership amid rising automobile use.
Local shuttle services are limited, primarily consisting of the county's RideConnect paratransit program, which offers door-to-door rides for seniors and individuals with disabilities within Yorktown and surrounding areas, bookable in advance for a nominal fee. Ridesharing options like Uber and Lyft have grown in availability, providing on-demand service throughout Yorktown Heights and to nearby stations, though wait times can vary outside peak hours.
As of 2025, the Bee-Line system continues its sustainability efforts with a fleet that includes over 100 hybrid-electric buses introduced since 2023, aimed at reducing emissions on routes serving Yorktown Heights, though full electrification remains in planning stages countywide.

Landmarks and Recreation

Historic Landmarks

Yorktown Heights features several preserved historic structures that reflect its colonial and Revolutionary War heritage. The town has designated 14 landmarks through its Landmarks Preservation Commission, encompassing early homes, churches, and public buildings that highlight the area's settlement and role in American history. These sites include examples of Dutch Colonial architecture and industrial remnants such as old mills and forges, which supported local agriculture and craftsmanship from the 18th century onward.
Lanes Tavern, built circa 1782, exemplifies early hospitality and Revolutionary-era significance with its Dutch architectural features, including gabled roofs and stone foundations. Located at 620 Underhill Avenue, it operated as a public house that likely accommodated travelers and soldiers during the conflict, contributing to the region's neutral ground status in Westchester County.
The First Presbyterian Church at 2880 Crompond Road stands as a key religious landmark, with its congregation founded in 1730 and the initial meeting house erected in 1738 on a 220-acre tract. The structure served as an arsenal, barracks, and gathering place for patriots before British forces burned it in 1779; the current building, completed in 1840, represents the third iteration.
Preservation efforts in Yorktown Heights are led by the Landmarks Preservation Commission, which protects these sites through designations and guidelines to prevent demolition or alteration. The Yorktown Historical Society supports ongoing initiatives, including virtual and guided tours of the 14 landmarks to educate residents on their heritage. Additionally, historical markers denote Revolutionary War sites, such as French Hill, where the French army encamped in 1781 en route to Yorktown, Virginia, emphasizing the area's strategic importance.

Notable People

Figures in Arts and Literature

Susan Faludi, born in 1959 in Queens, New York, was raised in Yorktown Heights and emerged as a prominent feminist author and journalist. Her seminal work, Backlash: The Undeclared War Against American Women (1991), earned the 1992 Pulitzer Prize for Explanatory Journalism and critiqued societal resistance to women's rights in the post-second-wave feminist era. Faludi's subsequent books, including Stiffed: The Betrayal of the American Man (1999) and In the Darkroom (2016), a memoir exploring her father's transgender identity, further solidified her influence in literary nonfiction, drawing on personal and cultural analysis to address gender dynamics.
Dave Matthews, born January 9, 1967, in Johannesburg, South Africa, moved to Yorktown Heights at age two where his father worked for IBM, and he attended local schools including Brookside Elementary before moving away in eighth grade. He is the lead vocalist and guitarist for the Dave Matthews Band, formed in 1991, which has sold over 38 million CDs and DVDs worldwide as of 2023, known for hits like "Crash into Me" and albums such as Under the Table and Dreaming (1994). Matthews has also pursued solo work, acting in films like Where the Red Fern Grows (2003), and philanthropy through the Bama Works Fund supporting community initiatives.
Steve Cohen, born February 1, 1971, in Yonkers, New York, grew up in Yorktown Heights and Chappaqua, developing an early interest in magic that shaped his career as a renowned illusionist specializing in close-up parlor performances. Known as the "Millionaires' Magician," Cohen has entertained elite audiences, including celebrities and world leaders, through his signature show Chamber Magic at the Lotte New York Palace Hotel in Manhattan since 2004, where he performs interactive mind-reading and sleight-of-hand routines in an intimate setting. His work blends traditional magic techniques with psychological insight, as detailed in his book Win the Crowd (2005), and he has contributed to preserving magic history through lectures and media appearances.
Yorktown Heights has also nurtured talents in visual arts and literature, such as children's book author and illustrator Marisabina Russo, a longtime resident whose works like The Big Brown Box (1992) and Under the Table (1997) explore family themes through autobiographical narratives and watercolor illustrations, enriching the local cultural scene with readings and workshops. These figures, alongside community events featuring local filmmakers and performers, highlight the area's role in fostering creative expression tied to broader artistic traditions.
