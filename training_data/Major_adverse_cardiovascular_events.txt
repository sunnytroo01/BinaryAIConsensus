Major adverse cardiovascular events
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) are a composite clinical endpoint widely used in cardiovascular research to assess the safety and efficacy of therapeutic interventions, typically comprising nonfatal myocardial infarction, nonfatal stroke, and cardiovascular death. This three-point MACE definition provides a concise summary of severe cardiovascular outcomes that signify substantial morbidity and mortality risks.
The term MACE originated in the 1990s, initially applied to evaluate in-hospital complications following percutaneous coronary interventions, evolving from earlier concepts like "adverse cardiac events" in the 1980s. Over time, it has become a cornerstone in cardiovascular outcomes trials (CVOTs), particularly for antidiabetic agents, where regulatory bodies such as the U.S. Food and Drug Administration require demonstration of cardiovascular safety through non-inferiority against MACE. In broader applications, MACE helps quantify the impact of risk factors, treatments, and procedures on long-term prognosis in populations with conditions like coronary artery disease or type 2 diabetes.
While the classic three-point MACE is prevalent, definitions exhibit significant variation across studies, reflecting differences in study design, population, and data sources. Common expansions include a four-point MACE adding ischemia-driven revascularization or a five-point version incorporating hospitalization for unstable angina or heart failure; however, systematic reviews indicate that only about 8.6% of observational studies using administrative databases adhere strictly to the three-point model. The most frequently reported components in such analyses are acute myocardial infarction and stroke (15.5% of studies), followed by acute myocardial infarction, stroke, and all-cause death (13.8%). This heterogeneity can complicate comparisons between trials and underscores the need for standardized reporting and validation of outcome definitions.
MACE endpoints are particularly valuable for powering clinical trials efficiently, as they aggregate rare but impactful events to detect meaningful differences in cardiovascular risk reduction. For instance, in trials of glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists and sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 inhibitors for type 2 diabetes, reductions in MACE have highlighted benefits primarily against atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, though they may overlook heart failure outcomes not captured in the core definition. Despite these advantages, limitations include potential dilution of treatment effects when components vary in clinical severity and the exclusion of non-cardiovascular deaths or less severe events, prompting calls for more nuanced, context-specific endpoints in future research. Overall, MACE remains a critical tool for advancing evidence-based strategies to mitigate the global burden of cardiovascular disease.

Definition and Components

Definition

Major Adverse Cardiovascular Events (MACE) is an acronym referring to a standardized composite outcome measure employed primarily in cardiovascular clinical trials to evaluate the efficacy and safety of interventions. This endpoint bundles multiple severe cardiovascular outcomes into a single metric, allowing researchers to capture a broader spectrum of treatment effects while enhancing the statistical power of studies, particularly when individual event rates are low. By aggregating these events, MACE provides a clinically relevant assessment of overall cardiovascular risk reduction or harm associated with therapies such as antiplatelet agents or revascularization procedures.
MACE is distinct from related composites such as Major Adverse Limb Events (MALE), which focuses on peripheral vascular complications like amputation or revascularization in patients with peripheral artery disease, and Major Adverse Cardiac and Cerebrovascular Events (MACCE), which typically incorporates revascularization procedures in addition to core cardiovascular events. Common components of MACE often include cardiovascular death, nonfatal myocardial infarction, and nonfatal stroke, though exact definitions can vary by study protocol.

Individual Components

The standard components of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) are cardiovascular death, non-fatal myocardial infarction, and non-fatal stroke, forming the classical three-point MACE endpoint used in most cardiovascular clinical trials to assess therapeutic safety and efficacy. These components are selected because they represent severe, clinically significant outcomes that capture a broad spectrum of ischemic cardiovascular risk without overlapping substantially.
Cardiovascular death is defined as death attributable to a cardiovascular cause, including fatal acute myocardial infarction, sudden cardiac death, death due to heart failure, stroke, or other cardiovascular mechanisms such as pulmonary embolism or cardiovascular procedural complications, excluding non-cardiovascular causes like hemorrhage or infection. Non-fatal myocardial infarction is characterized by acute myocardial injury with clinical evidence of acute myocardial ischemia, evidenced by a rise and/or fall of cardiac troponin values above the 99th percentile upper reference limit accompanied by at least one of the following: symptoms of acute myocardial ischemia, new ischemic ECG changes, development of pathological Q waves, imaging evidence of new loss of viable myocardium or new regional wall motion abnormality, or identification of an intracoronary thrombus by angiography or autopsy, as per the Fourth Universal Definition of Myocardial Infarction. Non-fatal stroke is defined as an acute episode of focal cerebral, spinal, or retinal dysfunction caused by intracranial or extracranial vascular pathology, such as ischemia or hemorrhage, lasting at least 24 hours or until death (with non-fatal cases resolving without mortality), excluding events due to trauma, infection, or metabolic disturbances, and confirmed by clinical examination, imaging, or autopsy.
Variations in MACE components occur across studies, particularly in observational research or expanded endpoints, where four-point MACE may include hospitalization for unstable angina (defined as ischemic chest pain at rest with dynamic ECG changes or transient troponin elevation without infarction) in addition to the standard three, and five-point MACE (sometimes termed MACE+) may further incorporate hospitalization for heart failure (evidenced by symptoms like dyspnea with objective evidence of cardiac dysfunction and elevated natriuretic peptides). These expansions aim to broaden the endpoint's sensitivity to coronary or heart failure-related morbidity but can introduce heterogeneity in trial interpretations.
In clinical trials, MACE components are adjudicated by independent Clinical Endpoint Committees (CECs) to ensure consistency and objectivity, reviewing source documentation such as medical records, ECGs, imaging, laboratory results, and autopsy reports against predefined criteria from guidelines like the Fourth Universal Definition for myocardial infarction or the 2017 Cardiovascular and Stroke Endpoint Definitions. This blinded process minimizes bias, classifies ambiguous cases (e.g., undetermined cause of death as cardiovascular if no non-cardiovascular etiology is identified), and standardizes event verification, with high inter-adjudicator agreement for major events such as stroke or infarction.

Historical Development

Origin in Clinical Trials

The concept of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) emerged in the mid-1990s as a composite endpoint to assess short-term, in-hospital outcomes following percutaneous coronary interventions (PCI), capturing events such as death, myocardial infarction, and the need for urgent revascularization to evaluate the net clinical impact of these procedures. This approach addressed the limitations of single-event endpoints in early PCI trials, where individual adverse outcomes occurred infrequently, by combining related ischemic and procedural complications into a more robust measure of efficacy and safety.
By the late 1990s and early 2000s, MACE gained prominence in larger cardiovascular trials evaluating antiplatelet and thrombolytic therapies for acute coronary syndromes. A seminal example is the Clopidogrel in Unstable Angina to Prevent Recurrent Events (CURE) trial (2001), which used a primary composite endpoint of cardiovascular death, nonfatal myocardial infarction, or strokenow recognized as the core 3-point MACEto demonstrate the benefits of dual antiplatelet therapy with clopidogrel plus aspirin over aspirin alone, reducing event rates from 11.4% to 9.3% at 12 months. This trial highlighted MACE's utility in detecting treatment effects across multiple related endpoints, thereby enhancing statistical power and clinical relevance in studies of acute myocardial infarction and unstable angina.
The adoption of MACE offered key advantages in trial design, particularly by aggregating low-incidence events to increase overall event rates, which reduced the required sample sizes and trial duration while providing a comprehensive assessment of cardiovascular risk reduction. In 2008, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) issued guidance promoting the use of standardized composite endpoints, including MACE, to evaluate cardiovascular safety in new antidiabetic therapies amid growing concerns over risks such as those associated with rosiglitazone. These efforts laid the groundwork for MACE's widespread use in subsequent trials, emphasizing its role in balancing sensitivity to treatment effects with interpretability.

Evolution of Criteria

A standardized three-point MACE definition, comprising cardiovascular death, nonfatal myocardial infarction, and nonfatal stroke, was outlined in the U.S. Food and Drug Administration's 2008 guidance for evaluating cardiovascular safety in diabetes drug trials. This standardized definition aimed to capture clinically significant outcomes while balancing trial feasibility and statistical power, influencing its adoption across cardiovascular research. This evolution extended MACE's application beyond PCI to broader cardiovascular outcomes trials, particularly for antidiabetic agents, following the FDA's 2008 guidance.
During the 2010s, MACE criteria evolved to incorporate additional components, reflecting advances in understanding multifactorial cardiovascular risk and the need for more comprehensive endpoints in diverse patient populations. For instance, trials in heart failure began including hospitalization for heart failure alongside cardiovascular death, as seen in the 2014 PARADIGM-HF trial, where the primary endpoint was a composite of these two events to better assess therapeutic impacts on morbidity and mortality. Similarly, revascularization proceduressuch as coronary artery bypass grafting or percutaneous coronary interventionwere added to broader four- or five-point MACE definitions in some studies, particularly those evaluating antithrombotic therapies or acute coronary syndromes, to account for recurrent ischemic events. Updated clinical guidelines have increasingly emphasized long-term outcomes, such as reinfarction, rehospitalization for acute coronary syndromes, and repeat revascularization, in assessments of cardiovascular events, emphasizing long-term management strategies.
Post-2020, ongoing debates have centered on the inclusivity of mortality components within MACE, particularly whether to prioritize cardiovascular-specific death or include all-cause mortality to capture broader treatment effects. A 2021 systematic review of cardiovascular outcome trials highlighted this variability, with 15 studies using all-cause mortality and 9 employing cardiovascular death in their MACE composites, underscoring challenges in adjudication and relevance to real-world outcomes. The COVID-19 pandemic further prompted refinements in MACE application during 20202023 trials, where protocols accounted for infection-related cardiovascular risks, such as myocarditis or thrombosis, by enhancing surveillance for pandemic-attributable events without fundamentally altering core definitions, as evidenced in analyses of ongoing cardiovascular studies. These adaptations aimed to isolate treatment effects amid elevated baseline risks, though they introduced complexities in event attribution.

Risk Factors and Epidemiology

Risk Factors

Risk factors for major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) are broadly categorized into non-modifiable and modifiable types, with the latter often linked to lifestyle and environmental influences that contribute to atherosclerosis, thrombosis, and endothelial dysfunction. These factors increase the susceptibility to MACE components such as myocardial infarction and stroke by promoting plaque formation and vascular inflammation.
Non-modifiable risk factors include advancing age, where the risk of cardiovascular events approximately doubles every decade after age 40 due to cumulative vascular wear and reduced physiological reserve. Male sex confers a higher baseline risk compared to females, particularly before menopause, as evidenced by earlier onset of events in men. A family history of premature cardiovascular diseasedefined as onset before age 55 in male relatives or 65 in female relativeselevates risk through shared genetic and environmental exposures. Genetic predispositions, such as familial hypercholesterolemia, further amplify vulnerability, with untreated cases associated with a 10- to 20-fold increase in premature atherosclerotic events due to severely elevated LDL cholesterol levels.
Modifiable risk factors predominate in the causal pathway to MACE and include smoking, which doubles to quadruples the risk by inducing oxidative stress, platelet activation, and arterial damage. Hypertension, particularly with systolic blood pressure exceeding 140 mmHg, heightens MACE likelihood by promoting arterial stiffness and left ventricular hypertrophy, with each 20 mmHg increment roughly doubling risk over a decade. Diabetes mellitus imparts a 2- to 4-fold elevated risk through hyperglycemia-induced endothelial dysfunction and accelerated atherogenesis. Dyslipidemia, characterized by LDL cholesterol above 160 mg/dL, fosters plaque buildup, with reductions in LDL levels correlating to 20-50% lower event rates depending on age. Obesity, defined by a BMI greater than 30 kg/m, approximately doubles MACE risk via systemic inflammation and metabolic dysregulation.
Lifestyle contributors such as physical inactivity and diets high in saturated fats exacerbate these risks by worsening dyslipidemia and insulin resistance, accounting for a substantial portion of preventable MACE. Emerging factors like chronic kidney disease independently heighten MACE risk through uremic toxins and vascular calcification, associated with an eight- to tenfold increase in cardiovascular mortality. Air pollution exposure, especially to fine particulate matter (PM2.5), increases event rates via oxidative stress and autonomic imbalance, with long-term exposure associated with a 23% increased risk of ischemic heart disease per 10 g/m increase. Psychosocial stress further amplifies susceptibility, potentially doubling cardiac responses to environmental triggers like pollution through heightened sympathetic activation.

Incidence and Prevalence

Major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) exhibit varying incidence rates across populations, with global estimates indicating an annual incidence of approximately 3-5% in high-risk groups such as individuals with established coronary artery disease (CAD). For instance, in a large cohort of patients with atherosclerotic disease, the 1-year MACE incidence was 2.1%, rising to 9.1% over 4 years, reflecting an average annual rate of about 2.3% in this population. In broader analyses of CAD patients, the annualized MACE rate has been reported as high as 4.7 per 100 person-years, underscoring the elevated burden in those with prior vascular disease.
Prevalence of MACE is notably higher among older adults in Western countries, where cumulative lifetime risks accumulate significantly with age. In North America, the age-standardized 10-year incidence of cardiovascular disease eventsa proxy for MACE componentsreaches 13.7% for men and 7.4% for women, with lifetime risks escalating to over 30-50% for major cardiovascular outcomes by age 70 when factoring in recurrent events and mortality. Regional variations highlight disparities, particularly in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), where MACE rates are elevated due to higher baseline risks; for example, South Asian populations show 2-4 times greater odds of acute myocardial infarction (a key MACE component) compared to Western groups, as evidenced by the INTERHEART study, with recent immigrant data confirming persistently higher age-standardized incidence rates of up to 20-30% greater for acute events. Approximately 80% of global cardiovascular deaths, many attributable to MACE, occur in LMICs, amplifying the prevalence in these settings.
Trends in MACE incidence demonstrate a decline in high-income countries over recent decades, attributed to improved risk factor management and interventions, though rising in aging demographics. In 22 high-income countries, the age- and sex-standardized incidence of cardiovascular events fell by 19% from 2000 to 2019, with U.S. data showing an approximately 30% reduction in age-adjusted cardiovascular mortality rates from 2000 to 2020. Conversely, in LMICs and aging populations worldwide, incidence is increasing due to demographic shifts and uneven access to care. Projections indicate a 90% increase in global cardiovascular prevalence from 2025 to 2050, driven largely by LMICs. In specific subgroups, such as patients post-percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI), the 1-year MACE rate typically ranges from 5-10%, with observational studies reporting 10.4-10.8% in unselected cohorts, driven primarily by myocardial infarction and revascularization needs.

Prediction and Assessment

Risk Stratification Tools

Risk stratification tools are validated algorithms and scoring systems designed to estimate an individual's probability of experiencing major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE), aiding clinicians in identifying high-risk patients for targeted interventions. These tools integrate clinical, demographic, and laboratory variables to quantify short- or long-term risks, often using probabilistic models or point-based systems. Widely adopted examples include the Framingham Risk Score for primary prevention in asymptomatic populations, and acute coronary syndrome (ACS)-specific scores like GRACE and TIMI, which focus on prognostic assessment during hospitalization.
The Framingham Risk Score, updated in 2008, predicts the 10-year risk of incident cardiovascular disease, including MACE components such as coronary death, myocardial infarction, and stroke, in apparently healthy adults. It incorporates age, sex, total and high-density lipoprotein cholesterol levels, systolic blood pressure, hypertension treatment status, diabetes, and current smoking status into a logistic regression-based probability model that categorizes risk as low (<10%), intermediate (10-20%), or high (>20%). This update expanded the endpoint to general cardiovascular disease and improved calibration for diverse populations compared to prior versions.
For patients with ACS, the GRACE (Global Registry of Acute Coronary Events) Score estimates in-hospital and 6-month mortality risk, serving as a key tool for guiding triage and management decisions. Developed from a multinational registry of over 100,000 patients, it uses a points system based on eight variables: age, heart rate, systolic blood pressure, serum creatinine, Killip classification, cardiac arrest at admission, ST-segment deviation on electrocardiogram, and elevated cardiac enzymes. Scores are stratified into low (<109 points), intermediate (109-140), and high (>140) risk categories, with high-risk patients facing up to a 10-fold increase in mortality compared to low-risk ones. The model demonstrates strong discriminatory ability (c-statistic >0.80) across STEMI and NSTEMI presentations.
The TIMI (Thrombolysis in Myocardial Infarction) Risk Score specifically targets patients with unstable angina or non-ST-elevation myocardial infarction (UA/NSTEMI), predicting 14-day risk of death, myocardial infarction, or urgent revascularization as composite MACE. It assigns one point each to seven independent predictors: age 65 years, 3 risk factors for coronary artery disease, known coronary stenosis 50%, ST-segment deviation 0.5 mm, 2 anginal events within 24 hours, aspirin use in the preceding 7 days, and elevated cardiac markers, yielding a total score from 0 to 7. Higher scores correlate with exponentially increasing event rates, from 4.7% for scores of 0-2 to 40.9% for scores of 5-7, enabling rapid bedside risk assessment with high predictive accuracy (c-statistic 0.65-0.70). This score has been externally validated in multiple trials and supports decisions on invasive versus conservative strategies.
Advancements in machine learning have enhanced MACE risk stratification by incorporating vast datasets, including electronic health records and imaging, to capture nonlinear variable interactions beyond traditional scores. As noted in recent American Heart Association scientific statements, AI/ML models outperform conventional tools like the Pooled Cohort Equations in predicting cardiovascular events, with applications in personalized risk assessment for diverse populations. For instance, ensemble algorithms integrating clinical variables, biomarkers, and genomics achieve c-statistics exceeding 0.85 for 10-year MACE forecasts, aligning with guideline recommendations for refined primary and secondary prevention. These approaches, while promising, require prospective validation to ensure generalizability across healthcare settings.

Biomarkers and Diagnostics

Cardiac biomarkers play a central role in detecting and assessing the risk of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE), particularly through the measurement of high-sensitivity troponin (hs-cTn) for myocardial infarction (MI). Hs-cTn assays are the preferred biomarker for identifying myocardial injury, with diagnostic thresholds set at the 99th percentile upper reference limit (URL) of a healthy population, typically varying by assay and sex (e.g., 14 ng/L for some assays). Levels exceeding this threshold indicate acute injury and are associated with increased MACE risk, enabling rapid rule-out or confirmation in clinical settings.
B-type natriuretic peptides, including brain natriuretic peptide (BNP) and N-terminal pro-BNP (NT-proBNP), are key for evaluating heart failure risk as a component of MACE. Elevated NT-proBNP levels greater than 125 pg/mL signal abnormal cardiac stress and are linked to higher MACE incidence, serving as a prognostic indicator in both acute and chronic settings. These markers help stratify patients for heart failure-related events, with values below this threshold offering high negative predictive value for ruling out significant risk.
Inflammatory markers provide insight into vascular inflammation contributing to MACE. High-sensitivity C-reactive protein (hs-CRP) levels above 2 mg/L are associated with elevated risk of vascular events, including MI and stroke, independent of traditional risk factors. Lipoprotein(a) , a genetically determined lipoprotein, acts as a causal risk factor for atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease when elevated, promoting thrombosis and plaque formation that heighten MACE likelihood. Levels are largely inherited, making Lp(a) a stable marker for lifelong genetic risk assessment.
Imaging diagnostics enhance MACE evaluation by visualizing structural and functional abnormalities. Coronary computed tomography angiography (CCTA) quantifies plaque burden, with higher volumes of non-calcified or mixed plaques strongly predicting future MACE in stable coronary artery disease patients. Echocardiography measures left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF), where values below 40% indicate systolic dysfunction and confer significantly increased MACE risk, such as recurrent heart failure or death. Carotid ultrasound assesses intima-media thickness (IMT), with greater IMT reflecting subclinical atherosclerosis and correlating with higher MACE rates, including coronary events.
Emerging technologies, such as artificial intelligence (AI)-enhanced electrocardiography (ECG), enable detection of subtle ischemia patterns not apparent on standard readings, aiding MACE risk prediction. Validated models from recent studies demonstrate AI-ECG's ability to identify ischemic changes with high accuracy, improving prognostic assessment in ambulatory and acute settings. These tools integrate with biomarkers and imaging for more precise risk stratification.

Prevention and Management

Primary Prevention

Primary prevention of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) focuses on strategies to mitigate risk in individuals without established cardiovascular disease (CVD), emphasizing lifestyle, pharmacologic, and public health interventions alongside routine screening. Lifestyle modifications form the cornerstone of these efforts, with smoking cessation demonstrating rapid benefits; quitting reduces the excess cardiovascular risk by approximately 50% within one year of abstinence. Adopting a Mediterranean diet, rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, fish, and olive oil, has been shown to lower MACE incidence by about 30% in high-risk populations, as evidenced by the PREDIMED trial involving over 7,000 participants followed for nearly five years. Regular aerobic exercise, such as brisk walking or cycling for at least 150 minutes per week at moderate intensity, is associated with a 20-30% reduction in cardiovascular mortality risk, supporting endothelial function and reducing inflammation.
Pharmacologic approaches target modifiable risk factors in select high-risk individuals. Statins, such as rosuvastatin 20 mg daily, reduce MACE by 44% in primary prevention among those with elevated C-reactive protein but normal LDL cholesterol, per the JUPITER trial of over 17,000 participants. Antihypertensive therapy aims to maintain blood pressure below 130/80 mmHg in adults with hypertension and elevated CVD risk, thereby decreasing stroke and coronary events by addressing vascular strain. Low-dose aspirin (75-100 mg daily) may be considered for primary prevention in adults aged 40-59 years with a 10-year CVD risk greater than 10%, balancing a modest reduction in ischemic events against bleeding risks, according to 2022 USPSTF guidelines.
Public health measures complement individual actions by addressing broader environmental factors. Annual influenza vaccination reduces the risk of cardiovascular events by 15-45% in the short term, particularly during peak flu seasons, by preventing infection-related inflammation and thrombosis. Improvements in air quality, such as reducing fine particulate matter (PM2.5) exposure through regulatory policies, can lower ischemic heart disease mortality by up to 16% globally, as air pollution exacerbates atherosclerosis and acute events.
Screening protocols enable early identification of risks to guide prevention. Guidelines recommend initiating lipid panels every 4-6 years starting at age 20 for adults without known dyslipidemia, assessing total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, and triglycerides to inform lifestyle or pharmacologic interventions. Blood pressure checks should begin at the same age during routine visits, with more frequent monitoring for those with family history or other risks, facilitating timely management to avert progression to hypertension.

Secondary Prevention and Interventions

Secondary prevention strategies for patients who have experienced a major adverse cardiovascular event (MACE) focus on reducing the risk of recurrence through targeted pharmacotherapy, procedural interventions, and lifestyle programs. These approaches are recommended by major guidelines for individuals with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD), such as post-acute coronary syndrome (ACS) or myocardial infarction (MI).
Antiplatelet therapy is a cornerstone of secondary prevention, typically involving dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) with aspirin and a P2Y12 inhibitor for 12 months following an ACS event to minimize recurrent ischemic events. The Clopidogrel in Unstable Angina to Prevent Recurrent Events (CURE) trial demonstrated that adding clopidogrel to aspirin reduced the composite endpoint of cardiovascular death, nonfatal MI, or stroke by 20% in patients with non-ST-elevation ACS. More recent evidence from the PLATO trial supports the use of ticagrelor over clopidogrel, showing a 16% relative risk reduction in the same composite endpoint when combined with aspirin. After the initial 12 months, transition to lifelong single antiplatelet therapy with aspirin alone is standard to balance thrombotic risk reduction with bleeding concerns.
Lipid management emphasizes aggressive lowering of low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL-C) using high-intensity statins, such as rosuvastatin 20-40 mg daily, aiming for an LDL-C target below 70 mg/dL in very high-risk patients with prior MACE. The 2018 AHA/ACC cholesterol guidelines recommend this approach for secondary prevention in ASCVD patients to achieve at least a 50% reduction from baseline LDL-C. For those with refractory hyperlipidemia despite maximal statin therapy, PCSK9 inhibitors like evolocumab are indicated; the FOURIER trial reported a 20% reduction in MACE (cardiovascular death, MI, stroke, hospitalization for unstable angina, or coronary revascularization) with evolocumab added to statins.
Revascularization procedures, including percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stenting for obstructive coronary artery disease (CAD) and coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) for multivessel or left main disease, are key interventions to restore myocardial perfusion and improve prognosis in post-MACE patients. The 2021 ACC/AHA/SCAI revascularization guideline endorses PCI as a means to alleviate symptoms and reduce ischemic events in appropriately selected cases with significant stenoses, while CABG is preferred for complex multivessel disease to enhance long-term survival.
Cardiac rehabilitation programs, involving supervised exercise training, risk factor education, and behavioral counseling, are integral to secondary prevention and have been shown to lower recurrent MACE risk by 20-30%. A 2011 meta-analysis of randomized trials in post-MI patients found exercise-based cardiac rehabilitation reduced all-cause mortality by 20% and cardiovascular mortality by 26%. These structured interventions promote adherence to lifestyle modifications and pharmacotherapy, further mitigating residual risk after an initial event.

Limitations and Future Directions

Criticisms of MACE

One major criticism of the major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) endpoint is its treatment of disparate clinical outcomes as equivalent, which dilutes the overall significance by lumping irreversible events like cardiovascular death with more recoverable ones such as nonfatal stroke. For instance, while death is final, approximately 65% of stroke survivors achieve functional independence in daily activities within six months, highlighting the unequal impact on patient prognosis and quality of life. This equal weighting ignores the varying severity and long-term consequences of components, potentially misleading interpretations of treatment benefits in clinical trials.
Heterogeneity in MACE definitions across studies further undermines its reliability, as variations in included components and diagnostic criteria lead to incomparable results and inconsistent event rates. A review of cardiovascular trials found substantial differences in MACE compositions, with no consensus on key elements like myocardial infarction subtypes or revascularization inclusion. Similarly, an analysis of observational studies reported wide variability in diagnostic codes and components, complicating cross-trial comparisons and reducing the endpoint's validity for pooled analyses.
MACE's focus on "hard" eventssuch as death, myocardial infarction, and strokeoverlooks "soft" outcomes like recurrent angina or quality-of-life impairments, limiting its ability to capture the full spectrum of cardiovascular burden. This narrow emphasis prioritizes quantifiable, severe incidents while neglecting patient-centered measures, such as symptom recurrence or functional recovery, which may be more relevant to daily living and treatment adherence. As a result, trials using MACE may undervalue interventions that mitigate milder but frequent events, skewing assessments of net clinical benefit.
Regulatory bodies, including the FDA, have highlighted risks associated with composite endpoints like MACE, particularly the potential for masking differential treatment effects across components. In guidance on multiple endpoints, the FDA recommends component-specific analyses to avoid scenarios where benefits in one area (e.g., reduced mortality) are offset by harms in another (e.g., increased nonfatal events), ensuring clearer evaluation of safety and efficacy. This critique underscores the need for hierarchical or weighted approaches in cardiovascular trials to better reflect clinical priorities.

Alternative Endpoints

In response to limitations in traditional MACE definitions, such as their potential to overlook safety concerns like bleeding or to undervalue patient perspectives, researchers have developed alternative endpoints that incorporate broader clinical or patient-oriented measures.
Net Adverse Clinical Events (NACE) extends MACE by including major bleeding risks alongside ischemic outcomes, providing a more balanced assessment of net benefit in interventions where antithrombotic therapy heightens hemorrhage potential. Typically defined as a composite of all-cause death, myocardial infarction, stroke, and major bleeding (often per Bleeding Academic Research Consortium criteria), NACE has been widely adopted in percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) trials since the early 2010s to evaluate therapies like dual antiplatelet regimens. For instance, in a 2020 analysis of ticagrelor versus clopidogrel post-PCI, NACE rates were 15.1% and 14.6% at one year, respectively, highlighting its utility in capturing both efficacy and safety trade-offs.
Major Adverse Cardiovascular and Cerebrovascular Events (MACCE) builds on MACE by adding repeat revascularization as a component, making it particularly suitable for surgical and hybrid revascularization studies where procedural durability is key. This endpoint, comprising death, myocardial infarction, stroke, and revascularization, is common in coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) trials, where event rates often reflect long-term graft patency and symptom recurrence. In the EXCEL trial evaluating PCI versus CABG for left main coronary artery disease, the five-year MACCE rate was 19% in the CABG arm, underscoring its role in comparing procedural outcomes over extended follow-up.
Patient-centered outcomes represent a shift toward composites that integrate hard clinical events with health status and quality-of-life metrics, prioritizing what matters most to individuals in cardiovascular trials. These endpoints often combine death, myocardial infarction, stroke, and heart failure hospitalizations with validated instruments like the Kansas City Cardiomyopathy Questionnaire (KCCQ), which assesses symptoms, physical limitations, and quality of life on a 0-100 scale. In heart failure trials during the 2020s, such as the CHIEF-HF study of canagliflozin, the primary outcome was the 12-week change in KCCQ total symptom score, with a mean improvement of 5.8 points versus placebo, demonstrating enhanced relevance for regulatory and clinical decision-making. Similarly, hierarchical composites in trials like HEART-FID rank death, hospitalizations, and KCCQ changes to better reflect patient priorities.
Emerging alternatives emphasize simpler, more robust endpoints like all-cause mortality or cause-specific death in precision medicine approaches, which tailor interventions based on genetic, biomarker, or phenotypic profiles to avoid composite ambiguities. This trend aligns with pragmatic trial designs favoring verifiable outcomes over adjudicated composites to accelerate evidence generation in targeted therapies. Recent reviews (as of 2024) highlight ongoing criticisms of arbitrary composite endpoints in atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease trials and the increasing adoption of hierarchical win ratio methods to address MACE limitations by prioritizing clinically important outcomes.
