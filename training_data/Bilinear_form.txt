Basic Concepts

Definition

A bilinear form on a vector space VVV over a field FFF is a mapping B:VVFB: V \times V \to FB:VVF that is linear in each argument when the other is held fixed.
This bilinearity is expressed by the following axioms: for all scalars a,bFa, b \in Fa,bF and all vectors u,v,wVu, v, w \in Vu,v,wV,
B(au+bv,w)=aB(u,w)+bB(v,w),B(u,av+bw)=aB(u,v)+bB(u,w).
\begin{align}
B(au + bv, w) &= a B(u, w) + b B(v, w), \\
B(u, av + bw) &= a B(u, v) + b B(u, w).
\end{align}
B(au+bv,w)B(u,av+bw)=aB(u,w)+bB(v,w),=aB(u,v)+bB(u,w).
These conditions ensure additivity and homogeneity in each variable separately. The set of all such bilinear forms on VVV is denoted Bil(V)\mathrm{Bil}(V)Bil(V), which itself forms a vector space under pointwise addition and scalar multiplication. More generally, one may consider bilinear maps B:VWFB: V \times W \to FB:VWF between two vector spaces, but the standard case focuses on V=WV = WV=W.
Typically, VVV is taken over the real numbers R\mathbb{R}R or complex numbers C\mathbb{C}C, with no additional structure such as a norm imposed at the outset. The concept assumes only the algebraic framework of a vector space, allowing bilinear forms to generalize structures like the dot product without requiring metric properties. The notion of bilinear forms traces back to the late 18th century in Joseph-Louis Lagrange's implicit use of matrices to study quadratic forms, and the term was introduced in the context of multilinear algebra during the early 20th century as vector spaces were formalized.

Representation

Coordinate representation

In finite-dimensional vector spaces over a field $ F $, bilinear forms admit a concrete coordinate representation with respect to a chosen basis. Let $ V $ be an $ n $-dimensional vector space with basis $ { e_1, \dots, e_n } $. For a bilinear form $ B: V \times V \to F $, the associated matrix $ A = (a_{ij}){1 \leq i,j \leq n} $ is defined by $ a{ij} = B(e_i, e_j) $. This matrix fully determines $ B $, as the bilinearity ensures that for any $ u = \sum_{i=1}^n u_i e_i $ and $ v = \sum_{j=1}^n v_j e_j $, the value is $ B(u, v) = \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n u_i a_{ij} v_j = \mathbf{u}^T A \mathbf{v} $, where $ \mathbf{u} = (u_1, \dots, u_n)^T $ and $ \mathbf{v} = (v_1, \dots, v_n)^T $ are the coordinate column vectors.
To compute the matrix $ A $, evaluate $ B $ directly on the basis vectors: the $ (i,j) $-entry is simply $ B(e_i, e_j) $. This yields a straightforward procedure, as only $ n^2 $ evaluations are needed, after which the form on arbitrary vectors follows from expanding in coordinates and applying bilinearity. For instance, in the standard basis of $ F^n $, the matrix entries correspond to the coefficients in the expression $ B(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = \sum_{i,j} a_{ij} u_i v_j $.
The representation changes under a basis transformation. Suppose $ { f_1, \dots, f_n } $ is a new basis, and let $ P $ be the invertible change-of-basis matrix whose $ k $-th column consists of the coordinates of $ f_k $ with respect to the old basis $ { e_i } $. Then, the coordinate vectors transform as $ \mathbf{u} = P \mathbf{u}' $ and $ \mathbf{v} = P \mathbf{v}' $, where primes denote new coordinates. Substituting gives $ B(u, v) = (P \mathbf{u}')^T A (P \mathbf{v}') = {\mathbf{u}'}^T (P^T A P) \mathbf{v}' $, so the new matrix is $ A' = P^T A P $. This congruence transformation preserves the bilinear form up to the choice of basis.
Such matrix representations rely on the finite dimensionality of $ V $, which ensures a finite basis and thus a finite matrix. In infinite-dimensional vector spaces, a similar coordinate approach is possible using a Hamel basis, leading to an infinite matrix with entries $ B(e_i, e_j) $, but these are typically unwieldy due to the lack of finite support in expansions, and practical treatments often defer to operator-theoretic or kernel representations instead.

Matrix properties

The rank of a bilinear form $ B $ on a finite-dimensional vector space $ V $ over a field $ F $ is the rank of its matrix representation $ A $ with respect to any basis of $ V $. This quantity, denoted $ \operatorname{rank}(B) $, is independent of the choice of basis and equals the dimension of the image of the associated linear map $ \phi_B: V \to V^* $, where $ V^* $ is the dual space of $ V $ and $ \phi_B(v)(w) = B(v, w) $ for all $ v, w \in V $. In the symmetric case with $ \operatorname{char} F \neq 2 $, the rank also corresponds to the number of nonzero entries in a diagonal matrix obtained by congruence transformation.
Two matrices $ A $ and $ B' $ represent the same bilinear form on $ V $ with respect to different bases if and only if $ B' = P^T A P $ for some invertible matrix $ P $; matrices related in this manner are said to be congruent. Congruence preserves essential properties of the bilinear form, such as its rank, and provides a framework for classifying forms up to change of basis. For symmetric bilinear forms over the real numbers, Sylvester's law of inertia further refines this classification: the inertia of the form, expressed as the triple $ (p, q, r) $ where $ p $ is the number of positive eigenvalues of $ A $, $ q $ the number of negative eigenvalues, and $ r $ the multiplicity of the zero eigenvalue (with $ p + q + r = \dim V $), remains invariant under congruence. The signature of the form is the pair $ (p, q) $, which captures the "indefiniteness" structure.
Symmetric bilinear forms admit diagonalization by congruence over fields of characteristic not equal to 2, meaning there exists a basis of $ V $ in which the matrix representation is diagonal. This holds in particular over algebraically closed fields, such as the complex numbers, where the diagonal entries can often be normalized to 1 for nondegenerate forms. For nondegenerate bilinear forms, the associated matrix $ A $ has full rank, implying $ \det A \neq 0 $. In the real symmetric nondegenerate case, the sign of the determinant is (-1)^q, where q is the number of negative eigenvalues (positive if q even, negative if q odd), but the signature provides a more complete invariant than the determinant alone.

Symmetry types

Bilinear forms are classified into symmetry types based on their behavior under interchange of arguments. A bilinear form B:VVFB: V \times V \to FB:VVF on a vector space VVV over a field FFF is symmetric if B(u,v)=B(v,u)B(u, v) = B(v, u)B(u,v)=B(v,u) for all u,vVu, v \in Vu,vV. In terms of matrix representation with respect to a basis, a symmetric bilinear form corresponds to a symmetric matrix AAA, satisfying AT=AA^T = AAT=A.
A bilinear form is skew-symmetric (or antisymmetric) if B(u,v)=B(v,u)B(u, v) = -B(v, u)B(u,v)=B(v,u) for all u,vVu, v \in Vu,vV. The associated matrix representation is then skew-symmetric, with AT=AA^T = -AAT=A. Skew-symmetric forms satisfy B(u,u)=0B(u, u) = 0B(u,u)=0 for all uVu \in VuV, but the converse holds only over fields of characteristic not equal to 2.
An alternating bilinear form is defined by the condition B(u,u)=0B(u, u) = 0B(u,u)=0 for all uVu \in VuV. Over fields of characteristic not equal to 2, every alternating form is skew-symmetric, but the converse is false in characteristic 2, where skew-symmetric forms need not vanish on the diagonal. For non-degenerate alternating forms over the real numbers, non-trivial examples exist only when the dimension of VVV is even.
For a non-degenerate skew-symmetric bilinear form on a finite-dimensional even-dimensional vector space, the Pfaffian provides a polynomial invariant that serves as a square root of the determinant of the associated skew-symmetric matrix. Specifically, if AAA is the skew-symmetric matrix representing the form, then Pf(A)2=det(A)\operatorname{Pf}(A)^2 = \det(A)Pf(A)2=det(A).

Reflexivity and orthogonality

A bilinear form $ B $ on a vector space $ V $ over a field $ K $ is called reflexive if $ B(u, v) = 0 $ for all $ v \in V $ implies $ u = 0 $, and symmetrically, if $ B(u, v) = 0 $ for all $ u \in V $ implies $ v = 0 $. This condition ensures that the form captures the full structure of the space without collapsing any nonzero vectors to zero under orthogonalization. In finite-dimensional spaces, reflexivity is equivalent to the standard notion of non-degeneracy for bilinear forms.
Bilateral non-degeneracy, or reflexivity, requires that both the left kernel $ { u \in V \mid B(u, v) = 0 \ \forall v \in V } $ and the right kernel $ { v \in V \mid B(u, v) = 0 \ \forall u \in V } $ are trivial, i.e., equal to $ {0} $. This bilateral condition distinguishes reflexive forms from those that may be degenerate on one side only, ensuring a balanced duality between the two arguments. In coordinate terms, if $ V $ is finite-dimensional with basis $ {e_i} $, the matrix representation $ A $ of $ B $ satisfies $ A_{ij} = B(e_i, e_j) $, and reflexivity holds if and only if $ \det A \neq 0 $.
Given a reflexive bilinear form $ B $, two vectors $ u, v \in V $ are orthogonal, denoted $ u \perp v $, if $ B(u, v) = 0 $. For a subspace $ S \subseteq V $, the orthogonal complement is the set $ S^\perp = { w \in V \mid B(u, w) = 0 \ \forall u \in S } $. Reflexivity implies that $ \dim S + \dim S^\perp = \dim V $ in finite dimensions, and $ S \cap S^\perp = {0} $ if $ S $ is non-degenerate as a restriction of $ B $.
For a non-degenerate symmetric bilinear form over the real numbers, the spectral theorem guarantees an orthogonal basis of eigenvectors, allowing $ V $ to decompose as an orthogonal direct sum of eigenspaces: $ V = \bigoplus_{\lambda \in \sigma(B)} E_\lambda $, where $ E_\lambda = { u \in V \mid B(u, v) = \lambda \langle u, v \rangle $ for some inner product, but adapted to the form's geometry, with $ E_\lambda \perp E_{\mu} $ for $ \lambda \neq \mu $. This decomposition highlights the form's role in classifying quadratic structures via eigenvalues.
In the context of duality induced by a reflexive bilinear form $ B: V \times V \to K $, which identifies $ V $ with its dual $ V^* $ via $ v \mapsto B(v, \cdot) $, the orthogonal complement $ S^\perp $ of a subspace $ S \subseteq V $ is precisely the annihilator of $ S $ under this pairing. Thus, reflexivity ensures the duality map is an isomorphism, preserving annihilator properties such as $ (S^\perp)^\perp = S $ for finite-dimensional $ V $.

In continuum mechanics, particularly for hyperelastic materials, ellipticity conditions on the tangent bilinear form distinguish between strong and weak variants. Strong ellipticity requires the bilinear form to be positive definite on the entire space of admissible symmetric tensor fields, ensuring the acoustic tensor has positive eigenvalues for all wave directions and guaranteeing material stability under arbitrary infinitesimal deformations. Weak ellipticity, in contrast, demands positivity only along rank-one directions (corresponding to plane waves), which is a necessary but insufficient condition for full stability and aligns with the Legendre-Hadamard condition but permits loss of ellipticity in higher-rank perturbations.

For real symmetric bilinear forms, the associated quadratic form QQQ admits a classification via its signature, defined as the pair (p,q)(p, q)(p,q) where ppp is the number of positive eigenvalues, qqq the number of negative eigenvalues (with the rest zero), of the diagonalized form under Sylvester's law of inertia. Tensor product relations

Bilinear forms on a vector space VVV over a field FFF can be understood through their connection to tensor products in multilinear algebra. Specifically, for finite-dimensional VVV, the space of bilinear forms Bil(V,V;F)\mathrm{Bil}(V, V; F)Bil(V,V;F) is naturally isomorphic to the tensor product of the dual space with itself, VVV^* \otimes V^*VV. Under this isomorphism, a bilinear form BBB corresponds to an element iiiVV\sum_i \phi_i \otimes \psi_i \in V^* \otimes V^*iiiVV, where B(v,w)=ii(v)i(w)B(v, w) = \sum_i \phi_i(v) \psi_i(w)B(v,w)=ii(v)i(w) for all v,wVv, w \in Vv,wV. In a basis {ej}\{e_j\}{ej} for VVV with dual basis {ei}\{e_i^*\}{ei} for VV^*V, the bilinear form expands as B=i,jB(ei,ej)eiejB = \sum_{i,j} B(e_i, e_j) e_i^* \otimes e_j^*B=i,jB(ei,ej)eiej.
This tensor product perspective aligns with the universal property of the tensor product. A bilinear form B:VVFB: V \times V \to FB:VVF is equivalently a linear map from the tensor product VVV \otimes VVV to FFF, and thus an element of the dual space (VV)(V \otimes V)^*(VV). The universal property ensures that Hom(VV,F)Bil(V,V;F)\mathrm{Hom}(V \otimes V, F) \cong \mathrm{Bil}(V, V; F)Hom(VV,F)Bil(V,V;F), providing a basis-free characterization that extends the algebraic structure of bilinear forms.
For bilinear forms B:VWFB: V \times W \to FB:VWF on distinct finite-dimensional spaces VVV and WWW, the tensor isomorphism becomes Bil(V,W;F)VW\mathrm{Bil}(V, W; F) \cong V^* \otimes W^*Bil(V,W;F)VW, with B(v,w)=ii(v)i(w)B(v, w) = \sum_i \phi_i(v) \psi_i(w)B(v,w)=ii(v)i(w). This induces adjoint maps: BBB defines a linear map VWV \to W^*VW by v(wB(v,w))v \mapsto (w \mapsto B(v, w))v(wB(v,w)), and dually WVW \to V^*WV by w(vB(v,w))w \mapsto (v \mapsto B(v, w))w(vB(v,w)). These adjoints embed bilinear forms into the space of linear maps involving duals, facilitating connections in representation theory and duality.
Bilinear forms represent the case k=2k=2k=2 of multilinear forms, which are linear maps on kkk-fold products VkFV^k \to FVkF, or elements of (Vk)(V^{\otimes k})^*(Vk). The universal property generalizes: the tensor product VkV^{\otimes k}Vk linearizes kkk-linear maps, so Bil(V,V;F)\mathrm{Bil}(V, V; F)Bil(V,V;F) is the special case where the multilinear form is separately linear in two arguments. This embedding highlights bilinear forms as building blocks in higher multilinear algebra.
In infinite-dimensional settings, such as Banach spaces, care is required with tensor products. The algebraic tensor product VWV \otimes WVW exists but is generally not complete with respect to natural norms, so continuous bilinear forms may require completed tensor products (e.g., projective or injective) to ensure the isomorphisms hold in the topological category. For Hilbert spaces, no single completion satisfies the universal property for all continuous bilinear maps to Hilbert spaces.

Generalizations

Distinct vector spaces

A bilinear form on distinct vector spaces VVV and WWW over a field FFF is a function B:VWFB: V \times W \to FB:VWF that is linear in each argument separately. Specifically, for all v1,v2Vv_1, v_2 \in Vv1,v2V, wWw \in WwW, and F\lambda \in FF, B(v1+v2,w)=B(v1,w)+B(v2,w)B(v_1 + v_2, w) = B(v_1, w) + B(v_2, w)B(v1+v2,w)=B(v1,w)+B(v2,w) and B(v1,w)=B(v1,w)B(\lambda v_1, w) = \lambda B(v_1, w)B(v1,w)=B(v1,w), and similarly for linearity in the second argument. Unlike the case where V=WV = WV=W, bilinear forms on distinct spaces exhibit asymmetries in their non-degeneracy properties. A bilinear form BBB is left non-degenerate if the induced map VWV \to W^*VW, defined by v(wB(v,w))v \mapsto (w \mapsto B(v, w))v(wB(v,w)), is injective, meaning that if B(v,w)=0B(v, w) = 0B(v,w)=0 for all wWw \in WwW, then v=0v = 0v=0. In the non-square case, left non-degeneracy does not imply right non-degeneracy, and vice versa, particularly when dimVdimW\dim V \neq \dim WdimV=dimW or in infinite dimensions. However, if both conditions hold and VVV, WWW are finite-dimensional with equal dimension, then BBB induces an isomorphism VWV \cong W^*VW.
In linear algebra, bilinear forms between distinct spaces arise in applications such as pairing linear maps. For instance, consider V=Hom(U,X)V = \mathrm{Hom}(U, X)V=Hom(U,X) and W=Hom(X,U)W = \mathrm{Hom}(X, U)W=Hom(X,U) for vector spaces UUU and XXX; the trace form B(f,g)=Tr(fg)B(f, g) = \mathrm{Tr}(f \circ g)B(f,g)=Tr(fg) defines a bilinear map VWFV \times W \to FVWF, which is non-degenerate under suitable conditions on dimensions.

This bilinear form B:VVFB: V^* \times V \to FB:VVF is non-degenerate in both directions when VVV is finite-dimensional, establishing a natural duality.

Module extensions

In the more general setting of modules over a commutative ring RRR, a bilinear form on RRR-modules MMM and NNN is an RRR-bilinear map B:MNRB: M \times N \to RB:MNR, meaning BBB is additive in each argument and satisfies B(rm,n)=rB(m,n)=B(m,rn)B(rm, n) = r B(m, n) = B(m, rn)B(rm,n)=rB(m,n)=B(m,rn) for all rRr \in RrR, mMm \in MmM, and nNn \in NnN. This extends the vector space case by incorporating the ring structure, where linearity holds with respect to the module actions rather than field scalar multiplication.
Unlike over fields, where the dual module M=\HomR(M,R)M^\vee = \Hom_R(M, R)M=\HomR(M,R) often allows straightforward isomorphisms via non-degenerate forms, no such general duality isomorphism exists for arbitrary modules over rings, complicating the identification of bilinear forms with elements of MNM^\otimes NMN. Non-degeneracy, defined as the associated maps MNM \to N^\veeMN and NMN \to M^\veeNM (given by m(nB(m,n))m \mapsto (n \mapsto B(m,n))m(nB(m,n)) and similarly) being isomorphisms, becomes harder to characterize due to torsion elements; for instance, in modules with 2-torsion, skew-symmetric forms may fail to be alternating, affecting properties like the dimension of isotropic subspaces.
For free modules such as M=N=RnM = N = R^nM=N=Rn, bilinear forms reduce to those represented by nnn \times nnn matrices over RRR, with the standard basis yielding B(ei,ej)=aijB(e_i, e_j) = a_{ij}B(ei,ej)=aij for the matrix (aij)(a_{ij})(aij). On abelian groups, viewed as Z\mathbb{Z}Z-modules, examples include the form B:QQQB: \mathbb{Q} \times \mathbb{Q} \to \mathbb{Q}B:QQQ given by B(r,s)=rsB(r, s) = rsB(r,s)=rs. For torsion groups, the tensor product Z/nZZZ/mZ=0\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \otimes_\mathbb{Z} \mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z} = 0Z/nZZZ/mZ=0 if gcd(n,m)=1\gcd(n,m)=1gcd(n,m)=1, illustrating issues with torsion in module tensor products, which bilinear forms help study.
Sesquilinear forms extend this framework over rings AAA with involution :AA\sigma: A \to A:AA, defined as biadditive maps s:VVAs: V \times V \to As:VVA on a right AAA-module VVV satisfying s(xa,yb)=(a)s(x,y)bs(xa, yb) = \sigma(a) s(x, y) bs(xa,yb)=(a)s(x,y)b, making sss linear in the second argument and anti-linear (via \sigma) in the first; this generalizes Hermitian forms over complexes, where \sigma is complex conjugation.
In algebraic geometry, bilinear forms appear on coherent sheaves over varieties, such as non-degenerate symmetric forms on Ulrich sheaves on a projective variety XPdX \subset \mathbb{P}^dXPd, which correspond to real symmetric determinantal representations of XXX and link to properties like hyperbolicity.
