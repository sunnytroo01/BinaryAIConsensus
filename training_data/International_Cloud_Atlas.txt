International Cloud Atlas
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The International Cloud Atlas is the authoritative reference manual published by the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) for the standardized classification and description of clouds and other meteorological meteors, enabling consistent global observations and reporting in meteorology. First established in 1896 following recommendations from the International Meteorological Conference in Munich, it originated from early 19th-century efforts by scientists such as Jean-Baptiste Lamarck and Luke Howard to systematize cloud nomenclature, evolving through international commissions and photographic advancements to become a cornerstone of atmospheric science. The atlas serves as part of WMO-No. 407, the Manual on the Observation of Clouds and Other Meteors, with legal status under WMO Technical Regulations for member states.
The classification system organizes clouds into 10 basic generaCirrus (Ci), Cirrocumulus (Cc), Cirrostratus (Cs), Altocumulus (Ac), Altostratus (As), Nimbostratus (Ns), Stratocumulus (Sc), Stratus (St), Cumulus (Cu), and Cumulonimbus (Cb)grouped by formation levels: high (above 6 km for Ci, Cc, Cs), middle (27 km for Ac, As), and low (below 2 km for Ns, Sc, St), while Cu and Cb can span multiple levels. Further refinements include species (e.g., fibratus for Ci, calvus for Cb), varieties (e.g., translucidus, opacus), supplementary features (e.g., virga, incus), accessory clouds (e.g., pileus, flumen), and other meteors such as hydrometeors (rain, snow), lithometeors (dust, haze), photometeors (halos, rainbows), and electrometeors (lightning, sprites). This hierarchical structure, based on appearance, height, and formation processes, supports aviation safety, weather forecasting, and climate research by providing precise observational codes and abbreviations.
Key historical milestones include the 1956 edition in two volumes (text and plates), revised in 1975 and 1987, which incorporated 224 high-quality images and expanded coverage of meteors. The 2017 edition marked a major modernization, introducing new classifications like the volutus species for roll clouds, supplementary features such as asperitas (undulating waves) and murus (wall cloud), and special clouds including flammagenitus (pyrocumulus from fires) and homogenitus (contrails from aircraft). Now fully digitized as an interactive web platform hosted by the Hong Kong Observatory on behalf of WMO, it features searchable image galleries, climate-zone filters, and multilingual translations in the six official UN languages, enhancing accessibility for meteorologists, educators, and the public worldwide.

History

Origins of Cloud Classification

The systematic classification of clouds began in the early 19th century, marking a shift from poetic or descriptive accounts to scientific nomenclature. In 1802, French naturalist Jean-Baptiste Lamarck published the first known classification, dividing clouds into categories based on their altitude and form, though his system did not achieve widespread adoption.
Independently, in 1803, British pharmacist and amateur meteorologist Luke Howard introduced a more enduring framework in his "Essay on the Modifications of Clouds," identifying three primary generaCirrus (wispy, high-altitude filaments), Cumulus (puffy, heaped masses), and Stratus (layered sheets)along with transitional forms like Cirro-stratus and Cumulo-stratus. Howard's use of Latin terms, inspired by Linnaean taxonomy, provided a standardized, universal language that facilitated consistent observation and communication among scientists.
Throughout the 19th century, cloud nomenclature evolved from these foundational descriptions toward greater precision, incorporating species and varieties to account for shapes, altitudes, and developmental stages, as meteorologists refined Howard's scheme through observational studies. This progression emphasized systematic categorization over subjective artistry, laying the groundwork for international collaboration by the late 1800s.
A significant advancement came with the integration of photography in cloud studies. In 1879, Swedish meteorologist Hugo Hildebrandsson, director of the Uppsala Observatory, pioneered its use by publishing an atlas of 16 photographic plates illustrating key cloud types, enabling more objective documentation and comparison across observers.
In the 1890s, Hildebrandsson and French meteorologist Lon Teisserenc de Bort further refined the classification, expanding on Howard's genera with detailed subtypes and emphasizing altitude-based layering, which helped standardize terminology ahead of broader international efforts.

Establishment and Early International Efforts

The establishment of the International Cloud Atlas marked a pivotal step in international meteorological cooperation, driven by the need for standardized cloud observation across borders. In 1894, a special international committee, under the auspices of the International Meteorological Committee, met in Uppsala to develop a uniform system of cloud classification and nomenclature, addressing inconsistencies in earlier national efforts. This committee, comprising experts from various countries including Hugo Hildebrandsson, John Abercromby, and Lon Teisserenc de Bort, played a crucial role in harmonizing terminology and visual representations, laying the groundwork for global comparability in weather reporting.
The first edition of the International Cloud Atlas was published in 1896 by the International Meteorological Committee, featuring 28 colored plates that illustrated principal cloud forms along with definitions and instructions for observation in French, German, and English. This publication, resulting from recommendations at the 1891 International Meteorological Conference in Munich, was widely adopted by meteorological services worldwide, facilitating consistent data collection and exchange. It represented the first truly international effort to codify cloud types, building on but transcending individual 19th-century classifications by emphasizing collaborative standardization.
Subsequent editions refined and expanded this foundation. The 1910 reprint maintained the core structure without major alterations. Further refinements were pursued by the International Commission for the Study of Clouds, established in 1921 in London under the International Meteorological Organization, which oversaw subsequent editions. By 1932, the International Commission issued International Atlas of Clouds and of States of the Sky, Volume I: General Atlas, which incorporated photographic plates for greater accuracy and detailed definitions to support practical observation. The 1939 edition, published under the International Meteorological Organization (the predecessor to the World Meteorological Organization), further enhanced this with 174 platesincluding 101 ground-based photographs, 22 aerial views, and 51 depictions of sky typesaccompanied by explanatory notes and schematic drawings, solidifying the atlas as a comprehensive reference for international meteorology.

Editions

Pre-WMO Editions (18961939)

The pre-WMO editions of the International Cloud Atlas, published between 1896 and 1939 under the oversight of the International Meteorological Committee (later the International Meteorological Organization, or IMO), established a standardized visual and descriptive framework for cloud classification to support consistent global meteorological observations. These physical volumes evolved from basic illustrations of cloud genera to more comprehensive treatments incorporating sky states and supplementary phenomena, reflecting ongoing international collaboration among meteorologists.
The inaugural 1896 edition, titled International Cloud Atlas, featured 28 coloured plates illustrating the principal cloud genera, accompanied by detailed definitions, descriptions, and practical instructions for cloud observation in French, German, and English. This publication directly resulted from recommendations at the 1891 International Meteorological Conference in Munich, which sought to enable comparable cloud reporting across nations by adopting a unified nomenclature derived from earlier 19th-century classifications. The plates combined chromolithographs of photographs and paintings to depict cloud forms, emphasizing their characteristic appearances and heights.
The 1910 edition served as a second printing of the 1896 atlas without substantive amendments, retaining the 28 plates and core content to meet ongoing demand for the standardized reference. It maintained the focus on basic genera while reinforcing the established system for international use, though minor refinements in explanatory text addressed practical observation challenges noted in early implementations.
By the 1932 edition, titled International Atlas of Clouds and of States of the Sky, the atlas underwent significant expansion under the International Commission for the Study of Clouds, incorporating detailed assessments of sky coverage and introducing codes for quantifying cloud amount (e.g., oktas representing eighths of sky coverage). Volume I, the General Atlas, provided updated definitions for cloud genera, species, and varieties, alongside sections on sky types to aid in synoptic reporting, thereby enhancing the utility for weather forecasting.
The 1939 edition, a revised version of the 1932 work titled International Atlas of Clouds and of Types of Skies, formalized its structure into three volumes under IMO auspices, granting it official status as the authoritative reference for cloud and sky observations. Volume I (General Atlas) contained textual explanations and 174 plates, including 101 ground-based cloud photographs, 22 aerial views, and 51 depictions of sky types, with 31 plates rendered in grey and blue tones for clarity; each plate included schematic drawings highlighting key features. Volume II comprised the full collection of illustrative plates, while Volume III addressed special clouds and associated meteorological phenomena (meteors), such as precipitation forms and optical effects, to provide a holistic guide for observers. This edition's comprehensive format, with its emphasis on visual accuracy and coded descriptions, solidified the atlas's role in pre-war meteorology.

Mid-20th Century Editions (19561987)

Following the establishment of the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) in 1950, the International Cloud Atlas underwent significant revisions to incorporate post-World War II advancements in meteorological observation and photography. The 1956 edition marked the first major update under WMO auspices, published in two volumes to separate textual descriptions from visual aids, thereby reducing production costs and enabling easier translations into multiple languages. Volume I provided comprehensive definitions, classifications of clouds and other meteors, and guidance on observations, while Volume II featured 224 plates illustrating clouds and meteors, including both black-and-white and color photographs sourced internationally. This edition refined cloud classification by eliminating outdated "cloud families," adjusting genera, species, and varieties, and introducing the concept of "mother-cloud" for dependent formations; it also expanded meteor categories into hydrometeors, lithometeors, photometeors, and electrometeors. Additionally, Part V introduced detailed instructions and pictorial guides for coding cloud amounts and types, standardizing reporting for synoptic meteorology.
The 1975 edition revised Volume I as a standalone text manual titled Manual on the Observation of Clouds and Other Meteors, approved by WMO's Executive Council to reflect evolving scientific understanding and observational practices. This update restructured content into three parts: meteor definitions and classification, cloud-specific details (including appearance, genera, species, varieties, and observation methods), and non-cloud meteors. Key enhancements included a broader definition of clouds as hydrometeors, the merger of mist and fog into a single category with a 1,000-meter visibility threshold, and refined descriptions tailored to polar and mountainous regions. Supplementary features such as virgaprecipitation trails evaporating before reaching the groundwere formally integrated into hydrometeor classifications, aiding precise identification in diverse climates. Appendices on etymology, historical bibliography, and nomenclature further supported standardized terminology.
Complementing the textual revisions, the 1987 edition of Volume II focused on enhancing visual representation through updated photography, resulting in 196 plates (161 in color and 35 in black-and-white) that emphasized real-world examples of cloud varieties and associated meteors. Building on the 1956 plates, this version deleted 26 black-and-white and 8 color images deemed obsolete, replacing them with 41 new color plates selected from global submissions to ensure broader geographical and typological coverage. The plates illustrated high-, middle-, and low-level cloud genera (e.g., cumulonimbus with anvil, altocumulus lenticularis), species, varieties, and supplementary features, alongside meteors like halos, rainbows, and virga. Each plate included explanatory legends detailing formation contexts, altitudes, and observational notes, promoting consistency in meteorological training and reporting. This edition, edited by experts and finalized by the WMO Secretariat, underscored the shift toward color imagery for improved accuracy in identifying subtle cloud characteristics.

2017 Digital Edition

The 2017 edition of the International Cloud Atlas marked a significant transition from traditional print formats to a fully digital, web-based resource, approved by the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) President in January 2017 and publicly released on March 23, 2017, coinciding with World Meteorological Day. This edition built upon the foundational classifications from prior print versions while incorporating modern technology to enhance accessibility and usability for meteorologists worldwide.
A key innovation was the addition of 12 new cloud types and features, reflecting advancements in observation and recognition of previously under-classified phenomena. These include the new species volutus, a rare, elongated, tube-shaped roll cloud; five new supplementary featuresasperitas (dramatic, undulating wave-like formations), cauda (a tail-like appendage often associated with arcus clouds), cavum (circular or elliptical holes in cloud layers), fluctus (wave-like Kelvin-Helmholtz instabilities), and murus (wall-like shelf clouds); the new accessory cloud flumen (a funnel-shaped feature linked to roll clouds); and five special cloud types resulting from specific generative processescataractagenitus (clouds formed by waterfalls), flammagenitus (pyrocumulus from fires), homogenitus (anthropogenic clouds such as contrails), silvagenitus (from forest transpiration), and homomutatus (clouds modified by human influence). Additionally, contrail cirrus was formally recognized as a variety of cirrus under homogenitus. These additions expanded the atlas's scope to include human-influenced and rare natural formations, aiding in more precise weather reporting and climate studies.
The digital structure transformed the atlas into an interactive online manual, organized into main sections such as definitions of meteorological phenomena, detailed cloud classifications, descriptions of other meteors, observation guidelines, an image viewer, glossary, and supplementary information. The image viewer serves as a core feature, allowing users to search and filter over 600 high-resolution photographs and videos by cloud type, altitude, or other criteria, with options for metadata viewing, side-by-side comparisons, and time-lapse sequences to illustrate dynamic formations. This interactivity facilitates global collaboration and education, making the atlas a dynamic tool rather than a static reference.
Officially designated as WMO-No. 407, the 2017 edition holds authoritative legal status as the international standard for cloud and meteor classification, maintained and updated by the WMO to ensure consistency in meteorological observations across member states.

Post-2017 Updates

Since its launch in 2017, the International Cloud Atlas has not seen a major full edition but has instead incorporated incremental updates through its online platform at cloudatlas.wmo.int. These enhancements focus on maintaining the atlas as a living digital resource, enabling timely additions to cloud classifications and supporting global meteorological observation standards. No new cloud genera, species, or features have been introduced as of November 2025, with updates centered on expanding the image database.
Ongoing digital improvements have bolstered the atlas's utility, including an expanded image database featuring thousands of high-resolution photographs and videos for cloud identification. User contributions play a key role, with meteorologists, researchers, and the public submitting images and observations through WMO channels to enrich the gallery and validate new features. The platform's responsive design ensures mobile accessibility, facilitating real-time reference in field observations and education worldwide. In 2020, translations into all six official United Nations languages were completed, enhancing global accessibility.
The website is maintained by the Hong Kong Observatory on behalf of the WMO, ensuring regular technical updates and multilingual support in six languages to promote international adoption. These efforts keep the atlas aligned with contemporary meteorological needs without requiring printed revisions.

Content and Classification

Principles of Cloud Classification

Cloud classification in the International Cloud Atlas follows a hierarchical system that categorizes clouds primarily by their altitude, visual characteristics, and underlying formation processes, enabling standardized observation and analysis across meteorological contexts. Altitude divides clouds into high-level (typically above 6 km or 20,000 ft), middle-level (27 km or 6,50023,000 ft), and low-level (below 2 km or 6,500 ft) groups, reflecting the vertical structure of the atmosphere and associated temperature regimes that influence cloud composition, such as ice crystals in high clouds versus water droplets in lower ones. Appearance encompasses shape and internal structure, distinguishing between layered (stratiform), heaped (cumuliform), or wave-like forms, while dynamics account for convective or stable processes driving cloud development, such as upward motion in cumulus formations or subsidence in stratus layers. This tripartite approach ensures that classifications capture both observable traits and physical mechanisms, facilitating predictions of weather patterns.
At the core of this system are ten basic genera, which represent the primary cloud types defined by combinations of altitude and morphology, providing a foundational taxonomy for all cloud observations. Examples include Cirrus (high-level, detached clouds in the form of white, delicate filaments or patches) and Cumulus (low-level, dense, dome-shaped clouds with a flat base, often indicating fair weather). Each genus is mutually exclusive, meaning an observed cloud belongs to only one, though transitional forms may occur without formal classification. This structure, established to promote international consistency, allows meteorologists to identify clouds based on their predominant features without requiring advanced instrumentation.
The nomenclature employs Latin terms to ensure precision and universality, organizing clouds into genera, species, varieties, and supplementary features. Genera form the broadest category (e.g., Cumulonimbus for thunderstorm clouds), while species denote specific structures within a genus, such as fibratus for fibrous, thread-like arrangements in Cirrus. Varieties describe arrangements or opacity levels, like intortus for twisted or entangled filaments, and features highlight accessory elements, including praecipitatio for visible precipitation streams from clouds. This layered Latin system, supplemented by accessory clouds (e.g., pileus caps) and special cases, refines identification while maintaining simplicity for global use.
For practical observation, the atlas incorporates codes to quantify sky conditions, notably totality codes that assess cloud coverage in oktasa unit dividing the celestial dome into eight equal parts. For instance, 8 oktas denotes overcast skies with complete obscuration, while 0 oktas indicates clear conditions; intermediate values (e.g., 4 oktas for half-covered) guide reporting in synoptic meteorology. These codes, integrated with genus notations, support automated weather stations and international data exchange, emphasizing the atlas's role in operational forecasting.

Cloud Types and Features

The International Cloud Atlas organizes cloud classification into a hierarchical system comprising ten genera, further subdivided into species, varieties, and supplementary features or accessory clouds, enabling precise identification based on form, structure, and altitude. This structure applies to tropospheric clouds, with genera primarily grouped by height: high-level (above 6 km), middle-level (27 km), low-level (below 2 km), and those exhibiting significant vertical development. These altitude ranges are approximate and vary by latitude, with higher levels in tropical regions (e.g., high-level 513 km) and lower in polar areas (e.g., middle-level 24 km in temperate). Each genus represents a broad category of cloud appearance and behavior, while species denote specific shapes or internal structures, varieties describe arrangements or transparency levels, and supplementary elements capture attached or accompanying phenomena.
High-level clouds include cirrus (Ci), delicate and feathery white filaments or patches formed of ice crystals; cirrocumulus (Cc), small white globular masses or ripples in a thin layer; and cirrostratus (Cs), a transparent whitish veil that may cover the entire sky. Middle-level clouds consist of altocumulus (Ac), composed of white or gray layers or lenses arranged in ripples or rounded masses; and altostratus (As), a fibrous grayish sheet or layer that uniformly obscures the sun. Low-level clouds encompass nimbostratus (Ns), a thick dark layer producing continuous rain or snow; stratocumulus (Sc), large gray or white rolls or patches often in a layer; stratus (St), a uniform gray fog-like layer near the surface; and cumulus (Cu), detached white or gray puffy heaps with flat bases and domed tops, typically indicating fair weather. Clouds with vertical development include cumulonimbus (Cb), massive towering heaps extending through multiple levels, often associated with thunderstorms and heavy precipitation.
Species provide finer distinctions within genera, describing characteristic forms such as humilis in cumulus, representing small, flat-based clouds with little vertical growth; congestus in cumulus, featuring well-developed vertical towers resembling a cauliflower; and calvus or capillatus in cumulonimbus, marked by anvil-shaped tops with fibrous or hair-like extensions due to upper-level shear. Other examples include fibratus for cirrus with fine hair-like fibers and castellanus for altocumulus with turret-like protuberances indicating instability. These species help meteorologists assess cloud dynamics and associated weather, such as the towering growth signaling convective activity in congestus or the anvil formation in capillatus indicating mature storm systems.
Varieties refine the classification by noting arrangements or optical qualities, applicable across multiple genera; for instance, undulatus denotes wave-like undulations in the cloud layer, radiatus describes parallel bands radiating outward from a common point, and virgaas a supplementary featureindicates pendant streaks of precipitation evaporating before reaching the ground. Other varieties include translucidus for thin layers allowing the sun to shine faintly through and opacus for dense, opaque forms blocking the sky. Accessory clouds, which merge with or attach to the main cloud, comprise pannus, ragged shreds beneath a layer cloud resembling a veil; and pileus, a small cap-like cloud atop a developing cumulus or cumulonimbus formed by moist air rising over the main updraft. Additional supplementary features include arcus, a dense horizontal roll at the leading edge of a thunderstorm gust front; tuba, a funnel- or tube-shaped cloud pendant from the base, potentially indicating rotation; and praecipitatio, visible precipitation streams reaching the surface.
Recent integrations into the atlas, from the 2017 edition onward, include asperitas as a supplementary feature characterized by chaotic, wave-like undulations in the cloud base, often resembling an upside-down undulatus variety, and volutus as a new species for elongated, horizontal roll clouds with a tubular appearance, typically detached and low-level. These additions, proposed by the international cloud community and validated by the World Meteorological Organization, enhance the atlas's ability to describe rare but increasingly observed formations linked to atmospheric waves or shear.

Other Meteorological Phenomena

The International Cloud Atlas classifies meteorological meteors beyond clouds into four primary categories: hydrometeors, lithometeors, photometeors, and electrometeors, providing standardized definitions, observational descriptions, and codes for international meteorological reporting. These classifications, refined across editions including the 1939 volume, emphasize observable characteristics to aid in weather observation and forecasting.
Hydrometeors encompass phenomena consisting of liquid or solid water particles that are suspended in the atmosphere, falling through it, raised by wind from the Earth's surface, or deposited on surfaces. Key examples include rain, defined as precipitation of water droplets larger than 0.5 mm diameter falling from clouds such as nimbostratus or cumulonimbus; snow, comprising ice crystals or aggregates precipitated from clouds; and hail, consisting of balls or irregular lumps of ice greater than 5 mm in diameter formed in strong updrafts. Observational codes for these, as per World Meteorological Organization (WMO) standards, assign rain intensities from 60 (slight) to 69 (heavy), snow from 70 to 79, facilitating synoptic reporting. Surface deposits like dewwater droplets formed by condensation on objects during clear nightsand frost, ice crystals deposited directly from water vapor when temperatures are at or below 0C, are also classified, though without specific numeric codes and instead noted under present weather conditions.
Lithometeors involve non-aqueous solid particles, typically mineral or organic, that are suspended in the air or raised by wind, affecting visibility and air quality. Prominent types include haze, a suspension of dry fine particles causing an opalescent sky appearance; dust and sand, particles raised by wind from arid surfaces leading to reduced visibility; and smoke, fine particles from combustion sources that disperse variably depending on wind and stability. These are observed in phenomena such as dust storms, where particles are lifted to significant heights, and are described without dedicated codes but integrated into visibility and present weather reports to denote atmospheric turbidity.
Photometeors are optical effects produced by the reflection, refraction, diffraction, or interference of light from the Sun or Moon interacting with atmospheric particles. Examples encompass halos, such as the common 22 ring formed by ice crystals refracting light around the Sun or Moon; rainbows, multicolored arcs resulting from sunlight refraction and reflection in raindrops with a primary bow radius of 40-42; coronas, colored diffraction rings around the Sun or Moon caused by uniform water droplets; and mirages, illusory displacements of distant objects due to temperature-induced refraction gradients in air layers. These phenomena, lacking specific observational codes, are documented through visual descriptions and plates in the Atlas to support aeronautical and maritime meteorology.
Electrometeors refer to manifestations of atmospheric electrical activity, often visible or audible, associated with charged particles and discharges. Lightning is characterized as a sudden electrostatic discharge producing a visible flash, typically occurring within or between cumulonimbus clouds and the ground during thunderstorms; thunder follows as the acoustic shock from rapid air heating and expansion along the lightning channel. Tornadoes, violently rotating air columns extending from cumulonimbus clouds to the surface, represent severe weather events capable of winds exceeding 100 m/s and widespread destruction, though they also involve hydrometeor elements like precipitation. No unique codes apply, but they are reported under thunderstorm descriptors in WMO protocols.
Distinct phenomena in the Atlas include specialized events illustrated with plates since the 1939 edition, such as waterspoutsrotating columns of air and water droplets over water surfaces, akin to tornadoes but often weakerand dust devils, localized whirling columns of dust and debris raised by intense surface heating on land. These are defined by their funnel-shaped structures and transient nature, with waterspouts classified partly as hydrometeors due to entrained spray, and dust devils as lithometeors, providing observers with visual references for identification in the 1939 and subsequent volumes.

Impact and Legacy

Adoption in Meteorology

The International Cloud Atlas serves as the authoritative reference for cloud classification and observation, mandated for use by all 193 World Meteorological Organization (WMO) member states and territories in their meteorological practices. This standardization is enshrined in the WMO Technical Regulations (WMO-No. 49), requiring adherence to the Atlas's definitions for coding clouds in SYNOP reports, which facilitate global weather data exchange through codes such as CL (low clouds), CM (middle clouds), and CH (high clouds). For instance, these codes assign numerical values to cloud generae.g., CL=1 for Cumulus humilisensuring consistent reporting of cloud types, heights, and amounts across international networks.
In aviation meteorology, the Atlas integrates directly into METAR (Meteorological Aerodrome Report) and TAF (Terminal Aerodrome Forecast) codes, where cloud layers are reported using abbreviations like FEW (few), SCT (scattered), BKN (broken), and OVC (overcast) based on oktas and heights derived from Atlas classifications. This supports flight safety by providing pilots with reliable data on significant clouds, such as Cumulonimbus, limited to operationally relevant layers below 1,500 meters or the highest minimum sector altitude. For climate monitoring, the Atlas enables comparable cloud observations worldwide, contributing to long-term datasets analyzed by bodies like the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change for trends in cloud cover and radiative forcing.
Historically, the Atlas's adoption since the 1956 editionfollowing WMO's establishment in 1950has transformed global meteorology by standardizing observations that were previously inconsistent, allowing seamless international data sharing and collaborative forecasting. In education and training, WMO guidelines reference the Atlas for certifying surface weather observers, with resources like the online identification guide used in professional programs to teach cloud recognition and coding.

Modern Developments and Recognition

In recent years, the International Cloud Atlas has fostered greater public engagement through collaborations with organizations like the Cloud Appreciation Society (CAS), which advocated for the inclusion of new cloud types such as asperitas in the 2017 digital edition based on member-submitted observations. This partnership highlighted the role of amateur cloudspotters in advancing global cloud nomenclature, with CAS competitions providing key photographic evidence for atlas updates and their Cloudspotters app enabling users to submit photos for verification by experts.
The atlas continues to contribute to scientific research on climate change, particularly through standardized classification of phenomena like contrail cirrus (Cirrus homogenitus), which form from persistent aircraft condensation trails and influence radiative forcing. Studies leveraging these definitions have quantified contrail cirrus's role in global warming, estimating it as a significant non-CO2 contributor to aviation's climate impact. Additionally, the inclusion of flammagenitus cloudsformed by intense wildfireshas aided monitoring efforts, allowing meteorologists to track pyrocumulonimbus development and associated firestorm risks.
The atlas received formal recognition in 2025 when the International Meteorological Organization (IMO) Legacy Collection, including its first edition, was included in UNESCO's Memory of the World Register, underscoring its historical and ongoing value as a foundational meteorological document. The digital edition's innovative online format, featuring searchable images and multimedia, has been praised for modernizing access to cloud classification standards.
Looking ahead, the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) maintains ongoing updates to the atlas to reflect evolving observations. Emerging research explores AI integration for automated cloud classification, with tools like the AI-Driven Cloud Classification Atlas (AICCA) using machine learning to analyze satellite imagery and group cloud patterns efficiently. Such advancements promise to enhance real-time monitoring while preserving the atlas's authoritative framework.
