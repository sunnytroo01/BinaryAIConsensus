 Intersection theory is a branch of algebraic geometry that studies the intersections of subvarieties within an ambient variety, providing tools to define and compute intersection multiplicities, cycles, and products in structures such as Chow groups or cohomology rings. It formalizes the notion of how two subvarieties "meet," accounting for both transverse and non-transverse cases through refined invariants like the intersection product, which endows the Chow ring with a ring structure.
The theory originated in classical enumerative geometry during the 19th century, with foundational results such as Bzout's theorem, which states that two plane curves of degrees d1d_1d1 and d2d_2d2 intersect in exactly d1d2d_1 d_2d1d2 points, counting multiplicity and points at infinity, under generic conditions in projective space. Pioneering work by mathematicians like Chasles, Schubert, and Zeuthen addressed enumerative problems, such as determining the number of conics tangent to five given conics (3,264) or cubics tangent to nine lines (33,616), laying the groundwork for rigorous intersection calculus. In the 20th century, the theory was modernized through the development of algebraic cycles and rational equivalence by Chow in the 1940s, followed by Fulton's comprehensive scheme-theoretic framework in the 1980s, which eliminated reliance on classical tools like the moving lemma and extended the theory to singular varieties.
Key concepts include Chow groups, which classify cycles modulo rational equivalence, and the intersection product, enabling computations via pushforwards and pullbacks under morphisms; these structures connect algebraic geometry to topology through isomorphisms with cohomology rings on smooth projective varieties. Notable applications span enumerative geometrysuch as counting lines on cubic surfaces (27 lines) or conics through pointsmoduli spaces of curves, singularity theory, and even string theory via Gromov-Witten invariants. The theory's influence persists in contemporary research, including equivariant and motivic extensions, underscoring its role in bridging classical and modern algebraic geometry.

Overview

Definition and motivation

Intersection theory provides a mathematical framework for quantifying the intersections of geometric objects, such as subvarieties in algebraic geometry or submanifolds in topology, by assigning invariants like intersection numbers or classes in associated rings. This approach addresses the need to measure how these objects "meet" in a rigorous way, extending beyond simple point counting to account for geometric complexities like tangencies and higher-dimensional overlaps.
The motivation for intersection theory stems from longstanding problems in enumerative geometry, where one seeks to count solutions to geometric conditionssuch as the number of plane curves of a given degree passing through specified pointsbut encounters issues with multiplicities when intersections are not transverse. In topology, similar challenges arise in defining linking numbers or self-intersections of cycles in manifolds, necessitating invariants that capture topological intersections independently of embeddings. These invariants enable the computation of global properties, such as degrees of maps or Euler characteristics, through local intersection data.
A basic prerequisite for understanding intersection theory includes knowledge of smooth manifolds from differential topology, singular homology from algebraic topology, and algebraic varieties from algebraic geometry, as intersections are often analyzed within these structures. For a simple illustration, consider two distinct lines in the Euclidean plane, which intersect transversely at a single point; their intersection number is defined to be 1, reflecting the minimal transverse case without multiplicity.
In specific contexts, realizations like the topological intersection form on homology groups or the Chow ring for algebraic cycles provide concrete tools for these computations.

Historical development

Intersection theory originated in 19th-century enumerative geometry, addressing problems of counting intersections of algebraic curves and surfaces, with foundational results such as Bzout's theorem and pioneering work by Chasles, Schubert, and Zeuthen on problems like the number of conics tangent to five given conics. Topological aspects were pioneered by Henri Poincar in his 1895 paper "Analysis Situs," where he introduced the concept of intersection numbers for cycles on manifolds, providing a topological measure of how submanifolds intersect, which laid the groundwork for understanding duality in homology. This was further developed in his 1904 supplement, where he refined these ideas to establish Poincar duality, linking intersection numbers to the topology of closed orientable manifolds.
In the 1920s and 1930s, Solomon Lefschetz advanced the topological aspects of intersection theory through his work on homology and fixed-point theorems. Lefschetz's 1923 fixed-point theorem generalized Poincar's ideas, using intersection numbers to count fixed points of maps on manifolds via traces on homology groups. He also introduced the intersection form on the middle homology of 4-manifolds in the 1930s, a bilinear form capturing self-intersection properties that became central to topological intersection theory.
Building on classical foundations, the mid-20th century saw further developments in algebraic intersection theory, beginning with Andr Weil's contributions in the 1940s. In his 1946 "Foundations of Algebraic Geometry," Weil developed intersection multiplicities and reciprocity laws for divisors on algebraic varieties over arbitrary fields, bridging classical projective geometry with modern algebraic structures. Jean-Pierre Serre built on this in the 1950s with his introduction of coherent sheaves in "Faisceaux algbriques cohrents" (1955), enabling intersection products via sheaf cohomology and refining multiplicity computations for non-transverse intersections. Around the same time, Wei-Liang Chow and others developed the Chow ring in the early 1950s, a structure formalizing intersections of cycles modulo rational equivalence on projective varieties.
Alexander Grothendieck's work in the 1960s revolutionized the field by reformulating intersection theory in the language of schemes and K-theory. Starting in the late 1950s, Grothendieck introduced algebraic K-theory to study coherent sheaves and their intersections, culminating in the Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch theorem (1958), which provided a universal transformation for pushforwards in intersection settings. His scheme-theoretic approach, detailed in foundational texts from the early 1960s, unified arithmetic and geometric intersections, while his 1966 International Congress of Mathematicians talk outlined the standard conjectures on algebraic cycles, positing homological equivalence as sufficient for intersection properties and motivating the theory of motives.
The modern synthesis of intersection theory emerged in the 1980s, with William Fulton's 1984 book "Intersection Theory" serving as a comprehensive reference that integrated topological and algebraic developments into a general framework for arbitrary schemes. Fulton's treatment encompassed refined intersection products, excess intersection formulas, and applications to blow-ups, drawing on Grothendieck's foundations. Subsequent advances in the 1980s and 2000s incorporated motives and derived categories, with Grothendieck's standard conjectures influencing higher K-theory and motivic cohomology, though many remain open.

Topological intersection theory

Basic intersection numbers

In topological intersection theory, the basic intersection number provides a way to quantify the algebraic intersection of cycles in a compact oriented manifold. To define it, one first requires the cycles to intersect transversally. Two submanifolds $ \alpha $ and $ \beta $ of a manifold $ M $ intersect transversally if, at every intersection point $ p \in \alpha \cap \beta $, the tangent spaces satisfy $ T_p \alpha + T_p \beta = T_p M $. This condition ensures that the intersection is a submanifold of the expected dimension $ \dim \alpha + \dim \beta - \dim M $. For cycles representing homology classes in singular homology, transversality can be achieved by perturbing one cycle slightly via a homotopy, as generic perturbations place intersections in general position, resulting in a finite set of isolated points when $ \dim \alpha + \dim \beta = \dim M $.

In three-dimensional manifolds, the linking number provides a special case of the intersection number. A representative example occurs with two closed curves on the torus $ T^2 = S^1 \times S^1 $. Consider the meridional curve $ \alpha $ (wrapping once around the first factor) and the longitudinal curve $ \beta $ (around the second factor); they intersect transversally at one point with positive local sign, yielding $ \cdot = 1 $. More generally, for curves of types $ (p, q) $ and $ (r, s) $ (winding $ p $ times meridionally and $ q $ longitudinally, etc.), the intersection number equals the determinant $ ps - qr $, which coincides with the degree of the induced map from the curves' parametrizations to $ S^1 \times S^1 $. This illustrates how intersection numbers capture winding relations on surfaces.

Extensions and applications

Enumerative geometry

Enumerative geometry employs intersection theory to solve problems of counting the number of geometric objects, such as curves or surfaces, that satisfy specified incidence conditions like passing through given points or intersecting given subvarieties in projective space. Classical results often rely on the intersection product in the Chow ring to compute these numbers rigorously, assuming general position of the conditions. For instance, the number of conics passing through 5 general points in the projective plane P2\mathbb{P}^2P2 is 1; this follows from the fact that the parameter space of conics is P5\mathbb{P}^5P5, and the 5 point conditions correspond to 5 general hyperplane sections whose intersection has degree 1. Similarly, in P3\mathbb{P}^3P3, the number of lines intersecting 4 general lines is 2, computed as the degree of the product of 4 copies of the Schubert class 1,0\sigma_{1,0}1,0 in the Chow ring of the Grassmannian Gr(2,4)\mathrm{Gr}(2,4)Gr(2,4).
The assumption of general position is justified by Bertini's theorem, which states that for a projective morphism with irreducible fibers, the general member of the family intersects a fixed ample divisor properly and with the expected dimension. In the context of intersection theory, this ensures that the imposed conditions lead to transverse intersections of the expected codimension. A key generalization is the Kleiman-Bertini theorem, which extends Bertini to the intersection of two families of subvarieties: for general members of ample families over a projective scheme, their scheme-theoretic intersection has the expected dimension and is equidimensional. This theorem underpins many enumerative computations by guaranteeing that generic choices yield the predicted counts without excess components or multiplicities.
Modern enumerative geometry extends these classical counts using Gromov-Witten invariants, which are defined as virtual intersection numbers on the moduli space Mg,k(X,)\overline{\mathcal{M}}_{g,k}(X,\beta)Mg,k(X,) of stable maps from genus-ggg curves with kkk marked points to a projective variety XXX of class \beta. These invariants generalize classical intersection numbers by incorporating virtual fundamental classes to handle non-transverse or obstructed situations, particularly for pseudoholomorphic curves in symplectic geometry and algebraic stable maps. For example, the Gromov-Witten invariant pt3d10,d(P2)\langle \mathrm{pt}^{3d-1} \rangle_{0,d}(\mathbb{P}^2)pt3d10,d(P2) counts the number of rational degree-ddd curves in P2\mathbb{P}^2P2 passing through 3d13d-13d1 general points, yielding the Kontsevich-Manin numbers NdN_dNd, which satisfy recursive relations derived from degeneration to nodal curves. In P3\mathbb{P}^3P3, the analogous invariant pt2d0,d(P3)\langle \mathrm{pt}^{2d} \rangle_{0,d}(\mathbb{P}^3)pt2d0,d(P3) counts rational degree-ddd curves through 2d2d2d general points, with computations often involving localization on toric varieties or relations to quantum cohomology.
Such invariants refine classical enumerative problems through quantum corrections, particularly in the context of mirror symmetry and string theory, where higher-genus contributions account for worldsheet instantons of genus greater than zero. These higher-genus Gromov-Witten invariants modify the classical counts by including terms that reflect multiple covers and higher topology, providing a more complete picture of curve enumerations in Calabi-Yau manifolds used in physical models. For instance, on quintic threefolds, genus-zero invariants count rational curves through points, while genus-one terms offer corrections essential for matching mirror symmetry predictions.
Classical Schubert calculus on Grassmannians also facilitates enumerative counts involving higher-degree objects via the Chow ring structure, which is generated by Schubert classes with relations given by the Pieri and Giambelli formulas. An illustrative computation arises in determining the number of lines in P3\mathbb{P}^3P3 intersecting 4 general degree-ddd curves, which is 2d42d^42d4; this is the intersection number of the classes d1,0d \sigma_{1,0}d1,0 (the class for intersecting a degree-ddd curve) raised to the fourth power in A(Gr(2,4))A^*(\mathrm{Gr}(2,4))A(Gr(2,4)). This approach extends to more complex incidences, linking linear spaces to higher-degree curves through pullbacks and pushforwards in intersection theory.

Connections to other fields

Intersection theory, originally developed in algebraic geometry and topology, finds significant extensions in symplectic geometry through the theory of Gromov-Witten invariants. These invariants are defined by counting pseudoholomorphic curves in a symplectic manifold, providing a refinement of the algebraic intersection numbers when the manifold admits a compatible Khler structure. In particular, J-holomorphic curves, which are holomorphic with respect to an almost complex structure compatible with the symplectic form, allow for the computation of symplectic invariants that generalize and sometimes correct the enumerative counts from algebraic geometry.
In arithmetic geometry, intersection theory is generalized to arithmetic varieties over number fields, incorporating both Archimedean and non-Archimedean places through arithmetic Chow groups. These groups combine algebraic cycles with metrics at infinite places and measures at finite places, enabling the definition of intersection products that yield arithmetic intersection numbers. A key application arises on abelian varieties, where the Nron-Tate height pairing serves as an intersection form on the Mordell-Weil group, pairing points via a quadratic form that regulates their arithmetic height and connects to the Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture. This pairing extends classical intersection theory by accounting for the arithmetic geometry over the ring of integers.
The motivic perspective further unifies these developments by embedding intersection theory into Voevodsky's triangulated categories of motives over a field, where motives of varieties are constructed using correspondences and transfers. In this framework, intersection products correspond to tensor products of motive objects, preserving homological and numerical equivalences. The standard conjectures on algebraic cycles posit that numerical equivalence coincides with homological equivalence in these categories, implying deep relations between intersection multiplicities and motivic cohomology groups.
Connections to algebraic K-theory are established via Adams operations on the Grothendieck group K0(X)K_0(X)K0(X) and the Chern character, which maps K0(X)QK_0(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}K0(X)Q isomorphically to the rational Chow ring A(X)QA^*(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}A(X)Q. The Adams operations k\psi^kk act as power operations compatible with the Chern character, allowing intersection-theoretic data to inform K-theoretic invariants and vice versa.
An illustrative example occurs in tale cohomology of arithmetic surfaces, where l-adic intersection numbers provide finite-place contributions to arithmetic intersection pairings, complementing the Archimedean components and facilitating computations in the arithmetic Chow groups.
