Insulin-like growth factor
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Insulin-like growth factors (IGFs) are a family of structurally related peptide hormones that mediate many of the effects of growth hormone (GH) on growth, development, and metabolism, primarily through endocrine, paracrine, and autocrine actions. The two main members, IGF-I and IGF-II, are single-chain polypeptides with approximately 70 and 67 amino acids, respectively, sharing about 50% sequence homology with proinsulin and featuring three disulfide bonds that confer structural similarity to insulin. IGF-I, predominantly produced in the liver in response to GH stimulation, peaks during puberty and promotes postnatal linear growth, tissue differentiation, and anabolic processes such as protein synthesis and glucose uptake. In contrast, IGF-II is largely GH-independent, playing a critical role in fetal development and maintaining adult stem cell populations, with higher circulating levels than IGF-I in humans.
The IGF system encompasses not only the ligands but also specific receptors and six high-affinity binding proteins (IGFBPs 16) that regulate bioavailability and activity. The primary receptor, IGF-1R, is a tyrosine kinase that binds both IGF-I and IGF-II with high affinity, activating downstream pathways like PI3K/AKT (for cell survival and metabolism) and MAPK/ERK (for proliferation and differentiation). IGF-II also interacts with the mannose-6-phosphate/IGF-II receptor (IGF-2R), which acts as a scavenger to degrade excess ligand without signaling, and there is cross-talk with the insulin receptor. IGFBPs, particularly IGFBP-3 (the most abundant in circulation), form stable complexes with IGFs and the acid-labile subunit (ALS), extending their half-life from minutes to hours and modulating tissue delivery while exerting IGF-independent effects on cell signaling.
Physiologically, the IGF axis is essential for normal somatic growth, with deficiencies leading to dwarfism (e.g., Laron syndrome) and excesses causing overgrowth (e.g., acromegaly). Dysregulation is implicated in numerous pathologies: elevated IGF-I levels correlate with increased cancer risk (e.g., prostate and breast), while low levels associate with cardiovascular disease, osteoporosis, and insulin resistance. Therapeutically, recombinant IGF-I is used for GH insensitivity syndromes, and IGF-1R inhibitors are under investigation for oncology, highlighting the system's translational potential.

Molecular Structure and Types

IGF-1

Insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1), also known as somatomedin-C, was identified in the 1970s as a key mediator of growth hormone effects and was purified and sequenced from human plasma by Rinderknecht and Humbel in 1978. This 70-amino-acid single-chain polypeptide features three intramolecular disulfide bridges that stabilize its structure, with a calculated molecular weight of 7,649 daltons. IGF-1 exhibits approximately 50% sequence homology with insulin, reflecting its membership in the insulin family of peptides.
The IGF1 gene, which encodes IGF-1, is located on the long arm of human chromosome 12 at position 12q23.2 and spans over 80 kb of genomic DNA, comprising six exons subject to alternative splicing that generates multiple isoforms. The primary transcript produces a pre-pro-IGF-1 precursor, which undergoes post-translational processing: the N-terminal signal peptide (residues 1-48 in the precursor) is cleaved during translocation into the endoplasmic reticulum, yielding pro-IGF-1 consisting of the mature IGF-1 sequence fused to a C-terminal E-peptide domain. Subsequent proteolytic cleavage removes the E-peptide, liberating the mature 70-amino-acid IGF-1 peptide.
In circulation, IGF-1 primarily exists bound to insulin-like growth factor-binding proteins (IGFBPs), forming either binary complexes (approximately 40-50 kDa) with various IGFBPs or, more commonly postnatally, ternary complexes (approximately 150 kDa) that include IGFBP-3 and the acid-labile subunit (ALS). These complexes extend IGF-1's half-life and regulate its bioavailability, with over 75% of circulating IGF-1 incorporated into the ternary form in adults.

IGF-2

Insulin-like growth factor 2 (IGF-2) is a polypeptide hormone that shares structural homology with insulin and IGF-1, but is distinguished by its imprinted expression and predominant role in early development. It was first isolated in 1976 and sequenced in 1978 by Rinderknecht and Humbel, and later identified as somatomedin A, a class of growth factors mediating somatotrophin effects, distinct from somatomedin C (IGF-1). IGF-2 exhibits partial sequence similarity to proinsulin, approximately 45% in the B and A domains, contributing to its insulin-like properties while maintaining distinct functions.
The mature IGF-2 protein consists of a 67-amino acid polypeptide chain with three intramolecular disulfide bridges that stabilize its structure, similar to other insulin family members, and has a molecular weight of approximately 7.5 kDa. It is encoded by the IGF2 gene located on the short arm of human chromosome 11 at position 11p15.5. In contrast to the non-imprinted, growth hormone-dependent circulating form of IGF-1, IGF-2 undergoes genomic imprinting, where it is expressed primarily from the paternal allele and silenced on the maternal allele through epigenetic mechanisms. This imprinting is regulated by the imprinting control region (ICR) shared with the adjacent H19 gene, involving DNA methylation that restricts enhancer access to the maternal IGF2 promoter.
The IGF2 gene produces multiple isoforms through alternative promoter usage and splicing, generating at least six distinct transcripts that all include exons 8-10 encoding the mature protein, with variations arising from differential inclusion of upstream exons such as exon 5. One notable isoform is "big-IGF-2," a proform retaining the 89-amino acid E-domain peptide, which is aberrantly produced in certain tumors due to altered processing and exhibits increased bioavailability compared to the mature form.
IGF-2 demonstrates high evolutionary conservation across vertebrates, with the primary amino acid sequence showing 57% identity between human and teleost fish orthologs, reflecting strong purifying selection to preserve its core structure and function. This conservation underscores its ancient origins and essential involvement in early embryogenesis among diverse species.

Biosynthesis and Regulation

GH/IGF-1 Axis

The GH/IGF-1 axis was first conceptualized in 1957 by Salmon and Daughaday, who identified a growth hormone-dependent serum factor, termed sulfation factor, that mediated sulfate incorporation into cartilage in vitro, laying the foundation for understanding IGF-1 as an intermediary in GH action.
Growth hormone (GH), secreted in a pulsatile manner from the anterior pituitary gland, binds to the growth hormone receptor (GHR) on hepatocytes in the liver, initiating the Janus kinase-signal transducer and activator of transcription (JAK-STAT) signaling pathway that transcriptionally induces IGF-1 expression and secretion. The liver accounts for approximately 75% of circulating systemic IGF-1, while other tissues contribute through autocrine and paracrine mechanisms, allowing local IGF-1 production independent of endocrine delivery.
Negative feedback in the axis is maintained by IGF-1, which inhibits GH release at the hypothalamic level by suppressing growth hormone-releasing hormone (GHRH) secretion and enhancing somatostatin tone, thereby modulating pituitary GH output.
IGF-1 levels exhibit dynamic changes across the lifespan, peaking during puberty to support growth spurts and progressively declining thereafter, a process known as somatopause that contributes to reduced anabolic activity in aging. Circulating IGF-1 bioavailability is further modulated by IGF-binding proteins (IGFBPs), which bind the majority of IGF-1 to regulate its free fraction and half-life.

Regulation of IGF-2 Expression

The human IGF2 gene is transcribed from five distinct promoters, designated P0 through P4, which enable tissue-specific and developmental stage-dependent expression patterns. These promoters drive the production of multiple mRNA transcripts encoding the same IGF-2 protein, with P0 being placental-specific and active during fetal development, P1 unique to humans and active in certain adult and embryonic tissues, while P2, P3, and P4 show varying activity across fetal and adult contexts. In particular, promoter P3 predominates in the adult liver, contributing to the majority of circulating IGF-2 levels postnatally, whereas P2 and P4 are more prominent in fetal tissues such as the choroid plexus and leptomeninges.
Genomic imprinting tightly regulates IGF2 expression through epigenetic mechanisms centered on the imprinting control region (ICR), also known as ICR1 or the H19 differentially methylated domain (DMD), located upstream of the H19 gene adjacent to IGF2 on chromosome 11p15.5. This ICR is methylated on the paternal allele, permitting IGF2 transcription from that chromosome, while the unmethylated maternal ICR recruits the insulator protein CTCF, which blocks enhancer access and silences the maternal IGF2 allele in most somatic tissues. The differential methylation at the ICR is established during gametogenesis and maintained throughout development, ensuring monoallelic paternal expression. Seminal studies in mouse models first demonstrated this imprinting of Igf2, revealing that disruption of the paternal allele leads to growth deficiency, confirming the gene's essential role in embryonic development.
During fetal development, IGF2 expression is elevated and regulated by factors such as placental lactogen (human chorionic somatomammotropin) and insulin, which stimulate transcription primarily through promoters P0, P2, P3, and P4 in tissues like the liver and placenta. This high-level expression supports rapid fetal growth, but IGF2 levels decline sharply postnatally in most tissues, coinciding with a shift toward promoter P3 usage in the liver and overall reduced transcription, potentially due to changes in hormonal milieu and epigenetic remodeling.
In pathological conditions, dysregulation of IGF2 imprinting often manifests as loss of imprinting (LOI), resulting in biallelic expression and overexpression that promotes tumorigenesis. This LOI is frequently linked to hypomethylation of the maternal ICR, allowing ectopic activation of the maternal allele, and has been observed in various cancers, including colorectal tumors, Wilms' tumors, and hepatocellular carcinomas, where it correlates with increased cell proliferation. For instance, in sporadic colorectal neoplasia, LOI of IGF2 occurs in peripheral blood leukocytes as an early event, conferring a heritable risk factor for malignancy.

Receptors and Signaling Pathways

IGF Receptors

The insulin-like growth factor 1 receptor (IGF1R) is a heterotetrameric transmembrane tyrosine kinase composed of two extracellular -subunits responsible for ligand binding and two membrane-spanning -subunits that contain the intracellular kinase domains, with the subunits linked by disulfide bridges. The receptor exhibits high binding affinity for both IGF-1 (Kd 0.11 nM) and IGF-2 (Kd 0.52 nM), while showing substantially lower affinity for insulin (50- to 100-fold reduced compared to IGF-1). This specificity enables IGF1R to primarily mediate the mitogenic and anabolic effects of IGFs upon ligand binding to the -subunits, which induces conformational changes leading to -subunit autophosphorylation.
In contrast, the IGF-2 receptor (IGF2R), also known as the cation-independent mannose-6-phosphate receptor (CI-MPR), is a monomeric single-pass transmembrane protein lacking any tyrosine kinase activity or intrinsic signaling capacity. It binds IGF-2 with high affinity to facilitate its rapid internalization and lysosomal degradation, thereby acting as a clearance mechanism to limit excessive IGF-2 signaling through IGF1R without transducing downstream signals itself. Evolutionarily, IGF2R derives from the ancient mannose-6-phosphate receptor family, retaining a dual function in trafficking mannose-6-phosphate-tagged lysosomal enzymes alongside IGF-2 regulation, a role conserved across vertebrates but with varying imprinting patterns.
Cross-talk occurs between the IGF system and the insulin receptor (IR), where IGF-1 binds the IR-A splice variant with moderate affinity at elevated concentrations, potentially activating hybrid IGF1R/IR complexes that preferentially respond to IGFs over insulin. IGF1R is ubiquitously distributed across mammalian tissues, with elevated expression in skeletal muscle, brain, liver, and adipose tissue, particularly during fetal development where levels are highest before declining postnatally. IGF2R shows broader but more restricted expression, with prominent levels in the liver, placenta, and brain, supporting its roles in systemic IGF-2 homeostasis and local enzyme delivery. IGF-binding proteins can influence receptor access by sequestering circulating IGFs, thereby modulating the availability of free ligands for binding to IGF1R and IGF2R.

Downstream Signaling Mechanisms

Upon binding to the IGF-1 receptor (IGF-1R), a receptor tyrosine kinase, insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1) triggers a conformational change leading to autophosphorylation of intracellular tyrosine residues, primarily at positions Y1131, Y1135, and Y1136 in the activation loop. This phosphorylation creates docking sites for adaptor proteins such as insulin receptor substrates (IRS-1 and IRS-2) and Src homology 2 domain-containing transforming protein (Shc), which initiate downstream cascades. Similarly, IGF-2 activates IGF-1R and the insulin receptor isoform A (IR-A) through analogous autophosphorylation mechanisms, recruiting the same adaptors to propagate signals, though IGF-2 exhibits higher affinity for IR-A, influencing mitogenic responses in fetal and tumor contexts. In contrast, the IGF-2 receptor (IGF-2R), also known as the cation-independent mannose-6-phosphate receptor, lacks intrinsic kinase activity and primarily facilitates IGF-2 internalization and lysosomal degradation without direct signaling contributions.
The canonical downstream pathways of IGF signaling center on the phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3K)/Akt and mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK)/extracellular signal-regulated kinase (ERK) cascades, which mediate survival, proliferation, and metabolic effects. In the PI3K/Akt pathway, IRS proteins recruit PI3K to generate phosphatidylinositol (3,4,5)-trisphosphate (PIP3), activating Akt (also known as protein kinase B), which phosphorylates targets like Bad, caspase-9, and mammalian target of rapamycin (mTOR) to inhibit apoptosis and promote protein synthesis and cell growth. The MAPK/ERK pathway, initiated via Shc-Grb2-SOS-Ras activation, leads to sequential phosphorylation of Raf, MEK, and ERK, culminating in nuclear translocation to regulate transcription factors such as MYC and ELK-1 for cell proliferation and differentiation. These pathways are conserved for both IGF-1 and IGF-2 signaling through IGF-1R and IR-A, with PI3K/Akt emphasizing metabolic and anti-apoptotic roles, while MAPK/ERK drives mitogenic outcomes, as evidenced in neural stem cell self-renewal and tumorigenesis.
Non-canonical mechanisms expand IGF signaling diversity, involving G-protein coupling, nuclear translocation, and crosstalk with other pathways. For instance, IGF-1R can engage G subunits and Src kinase for cell cycle regulation, or -arrestin and receptor for activated C kinase 1 (RACK1) to activate signal transducer and activator of transcription 3 (STAT3), promoting anchorage-independent growth. Nuclear signaling occurs via small ubiquitin-like modifier (SUMO)ylation of IGF-1R, enabling importin--mediated translocation to influence transcription factors like LEF1/TCF. Mitochondrial effects involve Akt and glycogen synthase kinase-3 (GSK-3) to modulate apoptosis, while phospholipase C (PLC)/protein kinase C (PKC) activation regulates gap junctions, and Wnt/-catenin crosstalk stabilizes -catenin for adhesion and gene expression. IGF-2 signaling shares these non-canonical routes via IGF-1R/IR-A but is modulated by IGF-2R-mediated clearance, preventing excessive activation in physiological settings like fetal development. Overall, these mechanisms yield context-specific outcomes, including neuroprotection in brain cells via PI3K/Akt and calcineurin/NFAT, bone maintenance through mTOR, and pathological proliferation in cancers.

IGF-Binding Proteins

Functions of IGFBPs

Insulin-like growth factor binding proteins (IGFBPs) play a central role in controlling the bioavailability of insulin-like growth factors (IGFs) by binding approximately 99% of circulating IGF-1 and IGF-2, which sequesters them from immediate receptor interaction and extends their plasma half-life from about 10 minutes for free IGFs to 12-15 hours for bound forms. This binding prevents rapid degradation and maintains a reservoir of IGFs, modulating their availability for tissue-specific actions.
IGFBPs also facilitate the transport and delivery of IGFs across biological barriers, such as the endothelium, while the ternary complex formed by IGFBP-3, IGF, and the acid-labile subunit (ALS) restricts passage to larger vessels and prevents renal clearance, thereby sustaining systemic IGF levels. This complex, whose formation is stimulated by growth hormone (GH), accounts for over 75% of circulating IGFs and ensures prolonged circulation without excessive filtration. In contrast, smaller IGFBP-IGF binary complexes enable targeted delivery to peripheral tissues.
The actions of IGFBPs on IGF signaling are dual: they primarily inhibit IGF bioactivity by sequestering IGFs and blocking their access to IGF receptors, but they can also promote IGF effects through conformational changes that enhance receptor affinity upon cell surface association or by reducing binding affinity in specific contexts. These modulatory effects allow IGFBPs to fine-tune IGF-dependent processes like cell proliferation and survival at local sites.
Beyond IGF modulation, certain IGFBPs exert independent biological effects; for instance, IGFBP-3 can induce apoptosis through nuclear localization and interaction with intracellular targets like nuclear receptors, independent of IGF binding. Similarly, IGFBP-1 influences cell migration by engaging integrins, such as 51, via its RGD motif, promoting motility in processes like tissue remodeling. These actions highlight IGFBPs' roles as multifunctional regulators.
The functions of IGFBPs are further regulated by limited proteolysis, primarily mediated by enzymes such as a disintegrin and metalloproteinases (ADAMs) and matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs), which cleave IGFBPs to release bioactive IGFs and thereby control local IGF availability.93835-X) This enzymatic degradation reduces IGFBP affinity for IGFs, providing a mechanism to dynamically adjust IGF signaling in response to physiological needs.

Major IGFBPs and Specificity

The insulin-like growth factor binding protein (IGFBP) family comprises six classical high-affinity members (IGFBP-1 through IGFBP-6) that modulate IGF bioavailability, along with IGFBP-7, which exhibits substantially lower affinity for IGFs. These proteins share a conserved structural architecture, consisting of a structured N-terminal domain, a flexible central linker region, and a C-terminal domain with thyroglobulin type-1 folds, stabilized by multiple disulfide bonds formed by conserved cysteine residues.
IGFBP-1 is predominantly synthesized in the liver, where its production is suppressed by insulin and elevated under fasting conditions such as starvation or stress. Structurally, it features the typical three-domain organization, with post-translational phosphorylation influencing its IGF binding. It binds IGF-1 with high affinity, preferentially over IGF-2, thereby regulating acute IGF-1 availability in metabolic contexts.
IGFBP-2 is expressed ubiquitously across tissues, with particularly high levels in cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) and prominent roles during fetal development, where it supports growth and differentiation processes. Its structure includes an RGD motif in the linker region that facilitates integrin interactions, and it maintains high-affinity binding to both IGF-1 and IGF-2, though slightly favoring IGF-2. Circulating IGFBP-2 levels are inversely associated with insulin resistance, serving as a biomarker for metabolic sensitivity.
IGFBP-3 is the most abundant IGFBP in circulation, accounting for 75-80% of IGF-bound forms, and is primarily liver-derived with induction by growth hormone (GH). It possesses the canonical domain structure, including sites for nuclear localization, and forms a stable ternary complex with IGFs and the acid-labile subunit (ALS) to extend IGF half-life. Beyond binding, IGFBP-3 exerts pro-apoptotic effects through nuclear translocation and interaction with cellular receptors, independent of IGFs in some contexts.
IGFBP-4 is ubiquitously expressed in various tissues and features the standard IGFBP fold, rendering it susceptible to proteolytic cleavage by pregnancy-associated plasma protein-A (PAPP-A), which limits its inhibitory potential. It binds both IGF-1 and IGF-2 with comparable high affinity and plays key roles in bone homeostasis, modulating osteoblast and osteoclast activities to influence skeletal growth and maintenance.
IGFBP-5 and IGFBP-6 display more tissue-specific expression patterns, notably in bone and brain, with structures analogous to other family members but distinct linker regions enabling unique interactions. IGFBP-5 binds IGF-1 and IGF-2 with high affinity and associates with the extracellular matrix to potentiate local IGF actions in osteogenic contexts. In contrast, IGFBP-6 shows a marked preference for IGF-2 (over 100-fold higher affinity than for IGF-1) and contributes to brain and bone regulation, including inhibition of angiogenesis and neuronal processes.
IGFBP-7, while structurally related with an IGFBP-like N-terminal domain, binds IGFs with much lower affinity (~100-fold reduced compared to classical IGFBPs) and is considered part of the broader IGFBP superfamily rather than a high-affinity modulator.

Physiological Roles

Growth and Development

Insulin-like growth factors (IGFs), particularly IGF-1 and IGF-2, are essential mediators of cellular proliferation, differentiation, and organ development across life stages, orchestrating anabolic processes that support tissue expansion and maturation. In fetal development, IGF-2 predominantly drives placental and embryonic growth by promoting trophoblast proliferation and nutrient transport, ensuring adequate fetal nourishment. Disruption of the Igf2 gene in mice results in fetuses that are approximately 60% the weight of wild-type controls at birth, highlighting IGF-2's non-redundant role in embryonic size regulation.
Following birth, IGF-1 takes precedence in postnatal growth, primarily facilitating linear skeletal elongation through enhanced proliferation and hypertrophy of chondrocytes within the epiphyseal growth plates of long bones. In Igf1-null mice, postnatal body weight remains severely restricted at 30-40% of normal levels, demonstrating IGF-1's critical endocrine and paracrine contributions to somatic growth. These growth-promoting actions of IGF-1 are mediated in part through activation of the PI3K signaling pathway, which supports cell cycle progression.
In the central nervous system, IGF-1 fosters brain development by stimulating neurogenesis in the subventricular zone and promoting myelination of axons during critical postnatal periods, thereby aiding neuronal connectivity and function. Human cohort studies have identified a modest positive association between circulating IGF-1 levels in childhood and intelligence quotient (IQ), with a correlation coefficient of approximately 0.2, suggesting IGF-1's influence on cognitive maturation.
IGFs also exhibit organ-specific effects essential for development; IGF-1 induces muscle hypertrophy by activating satellite cell proliferation and fusion into myofibers, while concurrently supporting bone formation through osteoblast differentiation and mineralization. In contrast, IGF-2 contributes to the maturation of visceral organs, with high fetal expression in the kidney promoting nephron formation and branching morphogenesis, and in the liver driving hepatocyte expansion and glycogen storage capacity.
Throughout the lifespan, IGF-1 concentrations progressively decline with advancing age, impairing tissue maintenance and regenerative capacity in multiple organs, which may underlie reduced muscle mass and bone density observed in senescence. This age-related diminution underscores IGF-1's sustained role in preserving structural integrity beyond developmental phases.

Metabolic Regulation

Insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1) plays a key role in glucose homeostasis by stimulating the translocation of glucose transporter type 4 (GLUT4) to the cell membrane, thereby enhancing glucose uptake in insulin-sensitive tissues such as skeletal muscle and adipose tissue. This effect mirrors insulin's action, particularly at higher IGF-1 concentrations, where it exerts insulin-mimetic properties that promote hypoglycemia through increased peripheral glucose utilization. Additionally, IGF-1 suppresses hepatic and renal gluconeogenesis, reducing glucose production and contributing to overall glycemic control.
In lipid metabolism, IGF-1 promotes lipogenesis in adipocytes by upregulating lipid synthesis pathways and inhibiting lipolysis, which supports fat storage and adipocyte differentiation. Meanwhile, IGF-2 influences fetal fat deposition by enhancing adipogenesis and lipid accumulation in developing adipose tissues, thereby regulating prenatal energy reserves.
IGF-1 enhances protein metabolism in skeletal muscle by increasing amino acid uptake and activating the mechanistic target of rapamycin (mTOR) pathway, which drives protein synthesis and anabolic processes. Regarding energy balance, low circulating IGF-1 levels are associated with increased adiposity, reflecting impaired metabolic regulation and higher fat mass accumulation. Insulin sensitivity, as measured by the Homeostatic Model Assessment for Insulin Resistance (HOMA-IR), is inversely related to IGF-1 levels, with higher IGF-1 correlating to lower HOMA-IR values and improved metabolic efficiency.
Nutritional factors such as exercise and protein intake further amplify IGF-1-mediated anabolism; resistance training and higher dietary protein consumption elevate IGF-1 levels, enhancing muscle protein synthesis and overall metabolic responsiveness.

Clinical Significance

Associated Diseases

Dysregulation of insulin-like growth factor (IGF) signaling is implicated in various growth disorders. Laron syndrome, a rare genetic condition caused by mutations in the growth hormone receptor (GHR) gene, results in growth hormone insensitivity, leading to severely low circulating IGF-1 levels and profound short stature, typically 4 to 10 standard deviations below the mean. Patients exhibit elevated growth hormone but minimal IGF-1 production due to impaired hepatic signaling, contributing to skeletal and muscular underdevelopment without increased cancer risk, unlike typical growth hormone excess states. As of 2024, studies confirm reduced risk of age-related diseases like cancer and cardiovascular disease in Laron syndrome patients. In contrast, acromegaly arises from excessive growth hormone secretion, often due to pituitary adenomas, causing elevated IGF-1 levels that promote overgrowth; when occurring before epiphyseal closure, it manifests as gigantism with extreme tall stature. High IGF-1 in acromegaly also drives comorbidities such as arthropathy and metabolic disturbances.
Aberrant IGF expression contributes to oncogenesis in several cancers. Elevated circulating IGF-1 levels are associated with increased risk of prostate and breast cancers, with meta-analyses indicating an odds ratio of approximately 1.4 to 1.5 for the highest versus lowest quartiles. This risk stems from IGF-1's mitogenic effects on epithelial cells, promoting proliferation and inhibiting apoptosis. Overexpression of IGF-2, often due to loss of imprinting at the 11p15 locus, is a hallmark of Wilms tumor, a pediatric kidney malignancy, where biallelic IGF-2 expression drives nephrogenic cell proliferation. Similarly, Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome, characterized by imprinting defects at the same locus leading to IGF-2 overexpression, predisposes individuals to Wilms tumor and other embryonal cancers through enhanced fetal growth signaling.
In metabolic diseases, altered IGF levels influence disease progression. Low serum IGF-1 is consistently observed in patients with type 2 diabetes and correlates with insulin resistance and impaired glucose tolerance, potentially exacerbating glycemic control. However, diminished IGF-1 may confer protection against certain complications, such as axon injury in diabetic peripheral neuropathy, by modulating nerve fiber vulnerability. Mutations or hypomethylation in the IGF-2 gene underlie some cases of Silver-Russell syndrome, resulting in intrauterine and postnatal growth restriction due to reduced IGF-2-mediated anabolic effects. Paternally inherited IGF-2 variants further highlight its role in severe growth failure beyond prenatal stages.
IGF dysregulation intersects with aging and neurodegeneration. Reduced IGF-1 signaling, as modeled by daf-2 mutations in nematodes, paradoxically extends lifespan by mimicking caloric restriction and enhancing stress resistance, a finding central to genetic studies of aging. Cynthia Kenyon's 2010 review underscores how insulin/IGF-1 pathway inhibition delays age-related decline across species. In humans, low IGF-1 levels are linked to accelerated aging phenotypes, though Laron syndrome patients with lifelong IGF-1 deficiency show reduced age-related diseases like cancer. Regarding neurodegeneration, impaired IGF-1 signaling disrupts amyloid-beta clearance in Alzheimer's disease models; blocking IGF-1 receptor in adult neurons reduces amyloid pathology and preserves synaptic function. Recent work demonstrates that targeted degradation of the IGF-1 receptor homolog DAF-2 at life's end extends Caenorhabditis elegans lifespan, suggesting late-life IGF modulation as a longevity strategy.
Alterations in IGF-binding proteins can modulate free IGF bioavailability in disease states, such as elevated IGFBP-1 reducing free IGF-1 in growth disorders or diabetes.

Therapeutic Applications

Mecasermin, a recombinant form of insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1), is approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the long-term treatment of growth failure in pediatric patients aged 2 years and older with severe primary IGF-1 deficiency, a condition characterized by insufficient endogenous IGF-1 production despite normal growth hormone levels. This therapy addresses growth failure associated with mutations in the IGF1 gene or defects in post-receptor IGF-1 signaling pathways, leading to short stature and metabolic issues. Clinical studies have demonstrated that subcutaneous administration of mecasermin at doses of 0.04 to 0.12 mg/kg twice daily can increase height velocity by approximately 2-3 cm/year in eligible children, though treatment requires careful monitoring for hypoglycemia and other adverse effects due to its insulin-mimetic properties.
Growth hormone (GH) therapies, such as somatropin, indirectly enhance IGF-1 levels by stimulating hepatic and local IGF-1 production, providing therapeutic benefits in conditions like Turner syndrome and Prader-Willi syndrome where IGF-1 signaling is impaired. In Turner syndrome, a chromosomal disorder affecting females, somatropin treatment is FDA-approved to improve final adult height, with meta-analyses showing an average height gain of 4-7 cm when initiated early in childhood. For Prader-Willi syndrome, an imprinting disorder involving GH resistance and low IGF-1, somatropin boosts linear growth, body composition, and motor function, with long-term studies indicating sustained improvements in lean body mass and reduced fat mass at doses of 0.24-1 mg/m/day. These therapies underscore the IGF-1 pathway's role in mediating GH's anabolic effects, though they do not directly replace IGF-1 in cases of primary deficiency.
In oncology, inhibitors targeting the IGF-1 receptor (IGF1R), such as the monoclonal antibody figitumumab, were developed to block IGF-1-driven tumor proliferation but encountered setbacks in phase III trials during the 2010s. Figitumumab, which binds to IGF1R and inhibits ligand-induced signaling, failed to improve overall survival when combined with erlotinib in non-small cell lung cancer patients or with carboplatin-paclitaxel in advanced nonadenocarcinoma subtypes, leading to trial terminations around 2010-2014 due to lack of efficacy and increased toxicity. Despite these failures, interest in IGF1R modulation persists into the 2020s, with newer combinations like the monoclonal antibody ganitumab paired with chemotherapy or immunotherapy showing preliminary promise in phase II trials for sarcomas and other IGF1R-overexpressing cancers, though a phase III trial in Ewing sarcoma failed to meet event-free survival endpoints in 2023.
For IGF-2-related pathologies, antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs) represent an emerging strategy to correct imprinting disorders by modulating IGF2 expression at the epigenetic level. IGF-2 modulation also holds potential for fetal growth restriction (FGR), where reduced IGF2 contributes to intrauterine growth impairment; animal studies using IGF-2 analogs or gene therapy have demonstrated rescue of placental and fetal growth phenotypes in models of growth restriction, suggesting a role for targeted IGF-2 supplementation.
IGF-1 mimetics are under investigation for anti-aging applications, particularly in sarcopenia, where age-related muscle loss correlates with declining IGF-1 signaling. Post-2020 clinical trials have explored agents that mimic or enhance IGF-1 activity to promote muscle anabolism; for instance, a phase II study of the selective androgen receptor modulator (SARM) MK-0773 reported modest increases in thigh muscle volume (approximately 1-2%) in older adults after 12 weeks, though functional improvements were limited. Ongoing research into direct IGF-1 receptor agonists, such as modified peptides, has shown in preclinical models enhanced muscle protein synthesis and strength gains, with early human trials post-2020 indicating potential for sarcopenia management when combined with resistance exercise, albeit with cautions for oncogenic risks. As of 2025, no IGF-1 mimetics have received regulatory approval for sarcopenia. These developments highlight IGF-1 mimetics' promise in countering frailty, supported by evidence of improved lean mass in GH/IGF-1 axis-targeted interventions.
