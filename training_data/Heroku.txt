Heroku
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Heroku is an AI platform as a service (AI PaaS) that enables developers to build, deploy, and scale applications in the cloud using managed containers and integrated data services, allowing focus on code rather than infrastructure management.
Founded in 2007 by Orion Henry, James Lindenbaum, and Adam Wiggins as a platform initially targeted at Ruby developers, Heroku pioneered the PaaS model by simplifying application deployment through Git-based workflows and dyno containers for runtime execution. The company was acquired by Salesforce in 2011 for $212 million, becoming a subsidiary that integrates with Salesforce's ecosystem for enhanced data synchronization and enterprise features.
Heroku supports a wide range of programming languages and runtimes, including Ruby, Node.js, Java, Python, PHP, Scala, and Go, alongside over 200 add-ons for services like databases (e.g., Heroku Postgres) and messaging (e.g., Apache Kafka). Its architecture emphasizes the full application lifecyclebuild, run, manage, and scalewith automated operations, monitoring, and security controls tailored for modern, cloud-native workloads.
In recent years, Heroku has evolved to incorporate AI capabilities, providing primitives for machine learning models and automated developer experiences while maintaining its core as a fully managed, container-based platform for deploying production-ready applications. On February 6, 2026, Heroku announced its transition to a sustaining engineering model focused on stability, security, reliability, and support rather than introducing new features, while continuing to invest in areas such as enterprise-grade AI solutions.

History

Founding and Early Development

Heroku was founded in June 2007 by James Lindenbaum, Adam Wiggins, and Orion Henry as a Y Combinator-backed startup aimed at simplifying the deployment and management of Ruby on Rails applications. The founders, experienced Ruby developers, sought to address the complexities of traditional server management by creating a platform that allowed developers to push code directly to the cloud without configuring infrastructure. This initial vision centered on Ruby-focused hosting, enabling rapid prototyping and deployment for web applications built with frameworks like Rails.
The platform entered a private beta in late 2007, quickly gaining traction among Ruby developers for its innovative approach to app hosting. By the end of 2008, Heroku had scaled to support over 20,000 applications in beta, including enterprise and Web 2.0 projects, but encountered growing pains from rapid expansion and technical limitations in the original architecture. In response to these challenges, including financial pressures from sustaining growth without a commercial model, the team undertook a three-month rewrite of the platform almost from scratch. This effort culminated in a relaunch in early 2009, shifting emphasis to developer ease-of-use and preparing for commercial viability.
Key innovations from this period included the adoption of Git-based deployment, which streamlined code pushes to the platform, and the introduction of "dynos"lightweight, isolated Linux containers that served as scalable units for running applications. The commercial launch in April 2009 marked the first paid tier, with dynos billed at $0.05 per hour beyond a free initial instance, supporting Ruby on Rails apps and fostering growth to handle thousands of production workloads. By mid-2009, these features had solidified Heroku's position as a pioneer in cloud PaaS, prioritizing simplicity over manual server orchestration.

Acquisition and Expansion

In December 2010, Salesforce acquired Heroku for approximately $212 million in cash, marking a significant milestone that integrated the platform into Salesforce's broader cloud ecosystem while preserving Heroku's operational independence and developer-focused culture. The deal, announced on December 8 and completed in January 2011, aimed to extend Salesforce's reach into the developer community by leveraging Heroku's innovative PaaS model for building and scaling applications.
Post-acquisition, Heroku accelerated its expansion by broadening language support to attract a wider developer base. Building on its initial Ruby focus, the platform added official support for Node.js in late 2010, followed by Clojure and Java in 2011, and Python around the same period. By the mid-2010s, this grew to include PHP in 2014, Go and Scala in 2015, enabling polyglot application development across diverse stacks.
Heroku's growth surged during this era, with over four million applications created on the platform by 2015, reflecting its increasing adoption for rapid deployment and scaling. Key integrations like Heroku Connect, launched in May 2014, enhanced synergy with Salesforce by enabling bidirectional data synchronization between Heroku Postgres databases and Salesforce objects, streamlining hybrid application architectures. To accommodate enterprise demands, Heroku evolved its offerings with features such as buildpacksintroduced in 2012 for customizable deployments of languages and frameworksand expanded paid tiers, including the Heroku Enterprise plan in 2015, which provided advanced security, compliance, and scalability options. These developments solidified Heroku's position as a versatile PaaS within the Salesforce portfolio through the early 2020s.

Challenges and Recent Milestones

In April 2022, a threat actor gained unauthorized access to a Heroku database, downloading stored OAuth tokens used for GitHub integrations and potentially affecting up to 4,000 customer accounts by also accessing hashed passwords in a separate internal database. This breach prompted Heroku to revoke all affected tokens, force password resets for impacted users, and implement enhanced security measures, including credential rotations, restricted database permissions, re-encryption of sensitive data, and advanced threat detection tools to prevent future unauthorized access.
Later that year, in November 2022, Heroku discontinued its free product plans, including hobby-tier dynos and databases, citing significant abuse such as cryptocurrency mining and fraud that increased operational costs and degraded service for legitimate users. The decision sparked widespread controversy and backlash from the developer community, leading many usersparticularly hobbyists and small projectsto migrate to alternative platforms like Render, Railway, and Vercel due to the loss of free tiers and rising costs. This shift transitioned remaining users to paid options like eco dynos, which offer a cost-effective alternative starting at $5 per month for multiple apps, aiming to sustain platform reliability while supporting developer needs.
Amid these challenges, Heroku achieved notable milestones in the 2020s, including sustained growth with developers creating over 65 million apps on the platform since its inception, serving billions of requests daily by 2023. The company also emphasized sustainability, leveraging Salesforce's net-zero greenhouse gas emissions achievement since 2017 to deliver a carbon-neutral cloud for Heroku operations, ensuring environmentally responsible infrastructure for customers.
A key modernization milestone began with the March 2024 announcement at KubeCon Paris of Heroku's replatforming onto Kubernetes, followed by its official platinum membership in the Cloud Native Computing Foundation in June 2024. This initiative advanced with the introduction of the Fir platforma Kubernetes-based, cloud-native stackin pilot in December 2024 and general availability in April 2025, enhancing scalability for containerized workloads while preserving developer simplicity. In December 2024, Heroku also launched a pilot for Heroku AI, integrating AI capabilities into the platform. Additional 2025 updates included expanded .NET buildpack support and deprecations of older stacks like Heroku-20 in April 2025, reflecting ongoing evolution.
In February 2026, Heroku announced its transition to a sustaining engineering model focused on stability, security, reliability, and support. The company emphasized maintaining quality and operational excellence rather than introducing new features, stating that Heroku remains an actively supported, production-ready platform. There are no changes for customers paying via credit card in the Heroku dashboard, including no alterations to pricing, billing, service, or day-to-day usage, with core platform functionality unaffected. New Enterprise Account contracts will no longer be offered, while existing Enterprise subscriptions and support contracts will continue to be honored and may renew. Heroku is focusing its product and engineering investments on delivering long-term customer value, particularly in enabling organizations to build and deploy enterprise-grade AI in a secure and trusted way.

Background

Etymology and Naming

The name "Heroku" is a portmanteau derived from "heroic," symbolizing the empowerment of developers to achieve feats of software deployment with ease, and "haiku," representing the brevity and elegance of concise, poetic code. This combination was intentionally chosen by the founding team during the platform's inception in 2007 to evoke a sense of heroic simplicity in application development.
The "haiku" element draws inspiration from the Japanese poetic form, reflecting the cultural roots of Rubythe programming language central to Heroku's early designand the minimalist philosophy of its creator, Yukihiro Matsumoto, often called Matz, whose approach emphasized elegant, succinct solutions. The founders, including Adam Wiggins, consulted native Japanese speakers to ensure the name resonated appropriately without unintended connotations, and Matz later endorsed its alignment with Japanese aesthetics. This nod to Japanese minimalism underscores Heroku's initial focus on streamlining Ruby-based web applications, aligning with the team's background in Ruby development.
Throughout its history, Heroku has experienced no formal trademark disputes or name changes, maintaining the original branding since its launch.

Company Philosophy and Overview

Heroku's core philosophy centers on enabling developers to focus on writing code rather than managing infrastructure, encapsulated in the belief that "great apps come from inspired and productive developers." This approach is embodied in its container-based Platform as a Service (PaaS) model, which facilitates rapid deployment, management, and scaling of applications without the need for server provisioning or maintenance. By prioritizing developer experience through principles of utility, simplicity, elegance, and quality, Heroku aims to create an ecosystem that minimizes friction and maximizes productivity.
As a cloud platform, Heroku hosts applications written in a wide range of programming languages, including Ruby, Node.js, Java, Python, Go, PHP, Scala, Clojure, and .NET, among others supported via community buildpacks. It serves a diverse audience from individual developers and startups to large enterprises and digital agencies, handling over 65 billion requests daily across more than 65 million apps created globally as of 2025. Acquired by Salesforce in 2011, Heroku operates as a key business unit within the Salesforce Platform, integrating seamlessly into the broader developer cloud ecosystem.
A cornerstone of Heroku's operational principles is the promotion of the Twelve-Factor App methodology, first articulated by Heroku co-founder Adam Wiggins in 2011 to guide the development of portable, scalable, and maintainable software-as-a-service applications. This methodology emphasizes best practices such as using declarative formats for setup, treating backing services as attached resources, and executing apps as one or more stateless processes, fostering agility in modern cloud environments. Heroku further underscores its commitment to the developer community through extensive open-source contributions, including over 5,400 buildpacks and ongoing support for projects like the Twelve-Factor App, which was fully open-sourced in 2024 to benefit the wider software ecosystem.

Technical Architecture

Core Components and Dynos

Dynos serve as the fundamental unit of execution in Heroku's platform, functioning as ephemeral, lightweight Linux-based containers that encapsulate and run application processes in isolation. Each dyno is managed entirely by Heroku, providing a secure environment where developers specify commands via a Procfile to define process types, ensuring that applications execute without direct access to underlying hardware. This design promotes portability and simplicity, as dynos can be dynamically provisioned, restarted, or terminated without persistent state, relying instead on external services for data storage.
Heroku supports three primary dyno types to accommodate diverse workloads: web dynos, which handle incoming HTTP traffic and route requests to the application; worker dynos, designed for executing background tasks such as queue processing or data synchronization without direct web exposure; and one-off dynos, which are short-lived instances used for ad-hoc administrative operations like database migrations or debugging sessions via the heroku run command. These types allow developers to compose an application's "formation" by scaling specific processes independently, with one-off dynos running alongside regular formations but exiting upon task completion.
As of 2025, Heroku operates two platform generations: the legacy Cedar (Common Runtime) and the new Fir generation. In Cedar, the dyno grid forms the backbone of the runtime infrastructure, distributing application dynos across a dynamic network of serversprimarily AWS EC2 instancesto enhance fault tolerance and enable seamless horizontal scaling. The Dyno Manager automatically handles redistribution during failures, minimizing downtime. In the Fir generation, dynos are orchestrated using Kubernetes on AWS Graviton (ARM) processors, providing more flexible sizing and improved isolation without the legacy cell-based organization.
HTTP traffic routing and load balancing in Heroku are managed through Router 2.0, the current routing layer generally available since November 2024, which directs requests to web dynos using a random selection algorithm for even distribution. Incoming traffic, whether via Heroku's subdomain (e.g., app.herokuapp.com) or custom domains, is load-balanced across available web dynos, with support for HTTP, HTTPS, and HTTP/2 protocols; routers also enforce security features like request timeouts at 30 seconds to prevent resource exhaustion. In Cedar apps, the slug compilation process packages the application during deployment: source code is fetched, dependencies resolved, the runtime environment configured, and outputs compiled into a portable "slug"a read-only archive that dynos download and execute, optimizing for consistency and reducing deployment times. Fir apps instead use Cloud Native Buildpacks to produce OCI-compliant container images for execution.
Resource allocation for dynos is standardized by size tiers, measured in equivalent units of RAM and CPU to facilitate predictable performance and billing; for instance, a standard-1X dyno provides 512 MB RAM and one unit of CPU share, while larger performance-M dynos offer up to 2.5 GB RAM and enhanced CPU allocation for compute-intensive tasks (in Cedar; Fir uses distinct sizing like dyno-1c-0.5gb). Developers select sizes based on application needs, with vertical scaling achieved by upgrading dyno types to access more resources. To optimize costs for low-traffic applications, Heroku implements sleep modes on Eco dynos, automatically idling them after 30 minutes of inactivityscaling web dynos to zerowhile waking them on the next request, though this introduces a brief startup delay of a few seconds.

Deployment and Scaling Mechanisms

Heroku facilitates application deployment through a streamlined Git-based workflow, where developers push code from their local repository to a Heroku remote using the command git push heroku main. This process begins with prerequisites such as installing Git and the Heroku CLI, creating a Heroku app via heroku create, and adding the Heroku remote to the local Git repository with heroku git:remote -a appname. Upon execution, the pushed code is received by Heroku's build system, which transforms the repository into a deployable artifact ready for execution on dynos. The artifact is then distributed to one or more dynos, enabling the application to run without requiring manual server configuration.
Central to this deployment is the use of buildpacks, which are scripts that detect the application's language or framework and automate the transformation of source code into an executable artifact. Buildpacks handle key tasks such as dependency resolution by installing required libraries and packages, environment setup by configuring runtime variables, and compilation of assets like assets precompilation in Ruby on Rails apps. Heroku supports both classic buildpacks, which follow a traditional script-based model with detect, compile, and release phases, and cloud native buildpacks, which align with the Cloud Native Buildpacks specification for containerized environments (required for Fir apps). For non-standard applications, developers can customize buildpacks by creating multi-buildpack setups or forking official ones to include bespoke dependency management or environment configurations, ensuring flexibility across diverse tech stacks.
The release process on Heroku encapsulates deployment outcomes, creating a new release each time code is pushed, config vars are updated, or add-ons are modified, which includes the deployable artifact, configuration settings, and process definitions. Config vars serve as environment-specific key-value pairs for storing sensitive data like API keys, persisting across deploys and restarts while triggering a new release upon changes, thus restarting the app to apply updates. Process types and commands are defined in a Procfilea simple text file at the app rootspecifying how dynos execute tasks, such as web: bundle exec puma -C config/puma.rb for web servers or worker: bundle exec sidekiq for background jobs, allowing clear separation of application components. Buildpacks contribute to releases by generating a release script that runs post-deployment tasks, like database migrations. For reliability, Heroku supports rollbacks via heroku rollback v10, which reverts to a prior release by restoring its artifact and config vars, creating a new release in the process without affecting the Git repository.
Scaling on Heroku occurs horizontally by adjusting the number of dynos for a process type using the heroku ps:scale command, such as heroku ps:scale web=5 to run five web dynos or heroku ps:scale worker=0 to pause workers, enabling rapid load distribution across instances. Vertical scaling involves resizing dyno types to larger sizeslike from standard-1x to performance-mfor increased CPU and memory, executed via heroku ps:type web=(/page/Performance)-m, though this requires zero-downtime strategies for production apps and is limited to available dyno sizes. For dynamic adjustment, Heroku's native autoscaling automatically varies web dyno counts based on performance metrics, including throughput (requests per second), response time (e.g., targeting under 200ms), or error rates, configured through the dashboard or API with minimum and maximum dyno limits to balance cost and responsiveness.

Products and Services

Core Platform Features

Heroku provides robust support for multiple programming languages through its platform, enabling developers to deploy applications in pre-configured environments without manual setup of dependencies or runtimes. The officially supported languages include Ruby, Node.js, Java, Python, PHP, Go, Scala, Clojure, and .NET, each backed by curated buildpacks that handle compilation, dependency resolution, and runtime provisioning. For instance, Ruby applications leverage MRI or JRuby interpreters, while Java uses various JDK versions, ensuring compatibility with popular frameworks like Rails, Express, Spring Boot, Django, Laravel, and ASP.NET Core. This official support includes regular updates for security patches and the latest stable releases, allowing seamless polyglot development where multiple languages can coexist in a single application via multi-buildpack configurations.
The Heroku Command Line Interface (CLI) serves as a primary tool for developers to interact with the platform directly from the terminal, offering commands for app creation, deployment, scaling, and maintenance. Users can install the CLI to manage resources such as provisioning dynos, viewing real-time logs with heroku logs --tail, running one-off processes via heroku run, and configuring environment variables. Complementing the CLI is the Heroku Dashboard, a web-based interface that provides visual oversight of applications, including monitoring of performance metrics like response times and error rates, real-time log streaming, and centralized app configuration such as domain management and resource allocation. The dashboard also facilitates collaboration by allowing team members to access app insights and settings securely.
Heroku's add-on marketplace enhances platform capabilities by integrating over 200 third-party and first-party services through standardized APIs, enabling effortless attachment of functionalities like logging, monitoring, and caching without custom code. Developers can provision add-ons such as Papertrail for log aggregation, New Relic for application performance monitoring, or Redis for caching directly from the dashboard or CLI, with automatic configuration of credentials and environment variables for seamless API access. This marketplace architecture promotes modularity, as add-ons handle their own scaling and maintenance while providing data via Heroku's resource attachment system.
Security is embedded in Heroku's core platform through features like Automated Certificate Management (ACM), which provisions and renews free TLS certificates for all custom domains, ensuring HTTPS encryption by default. API access is secured via OAuth 2.0, allowing granular authorization for third-party integrations and user actions without exposing credentials. Heroku maintains compliance with SOC 2 Type 2 standards for controls related to security, availability, and confidentiality, as well as GDPR requirements for data protection and privacy in the European Union.

Databases and Add-ons

Heroku provides managed database services as add-ons to support data persistence and querying needs for applications deployed on its platform. These services include Heroku Postgres, a fully managed relational database based on PostgreSQL, and Heroku Key-Value Store, a Valkey-based (Redis-compatible) in-memory datastore. Both are provisioned through the Heroku add-on marketplace and integrate seamlessly with applications via configuration variables, enabling developers to focus on code rather than infrastructure management.
Heroku Postgres offers a range of plans across Essential, Standard, Premium, Private, and Shield tiers, catering to applications from small prototypes to high-traffic production environments. Essential plans provide basic relational storage with up to 32 GB storage, 0 Bytes cache RAM, and support for PostgreSQL extensions like pgvector, while Premium and higher tiers scale to terabyte-level storage and enhanced performance characteristics based on CPU, RAM, and I/O configurations. As of May 2025, Heroku Postgres supports version 17 of PostgreSQL, providing enhanced query performance, better write throughput, and simplified upgrade processes. Key features include read-only follower databases for horizontal read scaling, which replicate data asynchronously to handle query loads without impacting the primary instance; point-in-time recovery enabled through write-ahead logging (WAL) archived off-premise every 60 seconds via WAL-E for disaster recovery; and Data Clips, a tool for executing SQL queries directly in a web interface and sharing results as public or private links for collaboration. The service includes automatic health checks, daily logical backups retained for 7 days on standard plans, and high availability with automatic failover to standby replicas in case of primary instance failure.
Heroku Key-Value Store serves as an in-memory data store compatible with Redis open-source software, optimized for caching, session management, real-time analytics, and message queuing in applications. It supports persistence options such as RDB snapshots for point-in-time backups and append-only file (AOF) logging for durability, configurable via CLI commands to balance performance and data safety. Plans range from hobby-level instances with 25 MB storage to premium configurations with multi-GB RAM and high availability clustering, including automatic failover and replication across regions. The service integrates with Heroku's metrics dashboard for monitoring key performance indicators like memory usage, command throughput, and eviction rates, allowing developers to set policies for memory eviction and connection timeouts.
The Heroku add-ons ecosystem comprises over 200 third-party and first-party services available through the Elements Marketplace, extending application functionality with specialized tools for monitoring, communication, logging, and more. Examples include New Relic for application performance monitoring with transaction tracing and alerting; SendGrid for scalable email delivery and transactional messaging; and Papertrail for centralized log aggregation and search. Add-ons are provisioned directly via the Heroku CLI with commands like heroku addons:create, which instantly attaches the service to an app and handles billing through the platform.
Integration of databases and add-ons occurs through automatically injected environment variables, such as DATABASE_URL for Heroku Postgres credentials or REDIS_URL for Key-Value Store connections, enabling apps to access services without hardcoding sensitive data. Many add-ons, particularly databases, feature built-in automatic backupssuch as daily snapshots for Postgresand failover mechanisms to ensure data durability and uptime, with logs and metrics streamed to Heroku's unified observability tools for troubleshooting.
Shared Heroku add-ons provide benefits for local app backups through real-time shared state across instances, requiring no manual data synchronization. During failover, applications retain full functionality, including reads, writes, and job processing. Upon unsuspension and return to Heroku, automatic synchronization restores the current state.

Enterprise and Specialized Offerings

Heroku Teams enable collaborative development by providing role-based access controls, allowing administrators to assign specific permissions to developers for managing applications within an organization. This includes features for app sharing, where teams can collaborate on a shared collection of applications, and centralized billing management to monitor and allocate resource usage across the entire group. These capabilities support unlimited teams with up to 500 users each, facilitating scalable collaboration without the overhead of individual account management.
Heroku Enterprise extends these team functionalities with advanced governance tools tailored for larger organizations, including Private Spaces that offer network-isolated, dedicated runtime environments for enhanced privacy and performance. These spaces allow deployment in user-specified geographic regions and support secure connections via Site-to-Site VPN, PrivateLink, and stable outbound IPs. Additional features encompass Single Sign-On (SSO) integration with providers like Okta and Microsoft Active Directory, comprehensive audit logs for tracking user activity, and compliance certifications such as HIPAA, PCI-DSS, ISO, and SOC to meet regulatory requirements for sensitive data handling.
Heroku Connect facilitates seamless integration for enterprise users by enabling bi-directional synchronization between Heroku Postgres databases and Salesforce data models, using a point-and-click interface to map and sync standard and custom objects without custom coding. This service supports read-only or read/write modes, handles high-volume transactions exceeding 65 billion daily, and includes options like Heroku Shield Connect for compliant handling of protected health information (PHI) and personally identifiable information (PII). As a managed add-on, it leverages Heroku Postgres for efficient data storage while exposing Postgres data as External Objects in Salesforce for real-time business processes.
Specialized plans within Heroku Enterprise address high-security and compute-intensive needs, such as Shield Spaces, which provide isolated environments with enhanced trust controls like encryption, strict TLS enforcement, and dedicated logging for HIPAA- and PCI-compliant applications in regulated sectors like healthcare and finance. For AI and machine learning workloads, Heroku offers GPU support through its Managed Inference and Agents service, allowing deployment of large modelssuch as OpenAI's gpt-oss-120b with 120 billion parameterson single 80GB GPUs via an OpenAI-compatible API, eliminating infrastructure management overhead. These offerings ensure secure, scalable solutions for mission-critical applications.

Innovations and Future Directions

Replatforming Initiatives

In March 2024, at KubeCon + CloudNativeCon Europe in Paris, Heroku announced its replatforming initiative to transition from the legacy Cedar architecture to a Kubernetes-based infrastructure, aiming to enhance orchestration capabilities and introduce support for Open Container Initiative (OCI) containers. This shift addressed limitations in the older Cedar runtime, such as proprietary networking constraints, by adopting Kubernetes as the unified runtime for greater operational efficiency and integration with modern cloud-native ecosystems.
The next phase of this initiative culminated in the launch of the Fir generation platform in early 2025, with general availability announced on April 14, 2025, featuring Fir Private Spaces that enable Kubernetes-native deployments and provide enhanced isolation for applications within dedicated network environments. Fir Private Spaces build on Kubernetes to abstract complex orchestration, allowing developers to deploy applications using familiar Heroku commands while benefiting from containerized isolation and regional data residency. This generation succeeds Cedar by consolidating multiple legacy runtimes into a single, scalable Kubernetes foundation, initially rolled out in pilot form in December 2024.
Key benefits of the replatforming include improved scalability, supporting up to hundreds of dynos per space with flexible sizing options and IPv6-enabled networking for high-throughput workloads. The adoption of OCI images ensures portability across compliant platforms, reducing vendor lock-in, while compatibility with cloud-native tools like Cloud Native Buildpacks and OpenTelemetry facilitates automated containerization, observability, and seamless integration with Kubernetes ecosystems, including potential use of Helm for package management. These enhancements maintain Heroku's developer-centric modelsuch as one-command deploymentswhile enabling advanced features like ARM processor support via AWS Graviton for cost-optimized performance.
The migration path emphasizes a gradual rollout to minimize disruption, with public beta phases beginning in late 2024 through pilot access for Fir Private Spaces, ensuring backward compatibility for existing dyno-based applications during the transition from Cedar. Users can migrate apps from Cedar Private Spaces to Fir with minimal downtime using guided processes that preserve dyno configurations and support hybrid environments during the shift. This approach allows existing dynoslightweight, isolated containers from Heroku's core architectureto continue operating alongside new Kubernetes-native workloads until full adoption.

Emerging Technologies and Updates

On October 8, 2025, Heroku launched a pilot program for Heroku Vibes, an AI-powered agent that enables developers to build and deploy applications using natural language descriptions, integrating tools for code generation, debugging, and model deployment directly within the platform. This initiative extends Heroku's PaaS capabilities to support AI inference and agentic applications, allowing seamless incorporation of large language models into workflows.
On October 14, 2025, Heroku introduced the next generation of Heroku Postgres, featuring an architectural overhaul with an Advanced Tier designed for high-scale performance and flexibility in vertical and horizontal scaling. This update supports complex data pipelines and AI workloads by providing enhanced capabilities for data-intensive applications, with general availability planned for early 2026.
Other notable updates include the public beta release of .NET support in December 2024, enabling developers to build and deploy C#, F#, and Visual Basic applications on the platform, which achieved general availability by April 2025. Heroku's cloud-native buildpacks have evolved to incorporate Open Container Initiative (OCI) standards and automate containerization, streamlining deployments in the Fir generation platform launched in 2025. Heroku's eco dynos continue to offer cost-efficient, shared-hour options for lightweight applications.
Looking ahead, the general availability of Fir features, including expanded Private Spaces and cloud-native integrations, positions Heroku for broader adoption in modern development environments. Deeper synergies with Salesforce AI, such as enhanced integrations with Agentforce and Data Cloud, are enabling cross-cloud intelligence for building custom AI applications as of 2025.
