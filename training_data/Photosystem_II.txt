Photosystem II
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Photosystem II (PSII) is a multi-subunit protein-pigment complex that functions as a water-plastoquinone oxidoreductase in oxygenic photosynthesis, catalyzing the light-dependent splitting of water molecules to produce molecular oxygen, protons, and electrons. Located in the thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts in plants and algae, as well as in cyanobacteria, PSII operates within the stacked grana domains to capture solar energy and initiate the photosynthetic electron transport chain. This process generates the highly oxidizing conditions necessary for water oxidation, marking PSII as the sole biological source of atmospheric oxygen on Earth.
The core structure of PSII consists of heterodimeric reaction center proteins D1 and D2, flanked by inner light-harvesting antennas CP43 and CP47, along with over 20 additional subunits including small membrane-intrinsic proteins and extrinsic oxygen-evolving complex (OEC) subunits such as PsbO, PsbP, and PsbQ in higher plants. Pigments within PSII include approximately 105 chlorophyll a molecules, chlorophyll b in plants, -carotenes, xanthophylls, and a central MnCaO cluster in the OEC that facilitates the four-electron oxidation of two water molecules. Recent high-resolution cryo-electron microscopy and X-ray crystallography studies, such as those achieving 1.9 resolution, have revealed the precise arrangement of these components, highlighting the dimeric nature of PSII supercomplexes associated with peripheral light-harvesting complexes (LHCII).
Functionally, PSII absorbs light primarily at wavelengths around 680 nm, exciting electrons from the P680 reaction center chlorophyll pair, which are then transferred to plastoquinone (QB) while the oxidized P680+ drives water oxidation via the S-state cycle of the OEC, as described by Kok's clock model. This electron flow reduces plastoquinone to plastoquinol, which carries protons into the thylakoid lumen, establishing a proton gradient essential for ATP synthesis, while electrons proceed to Photosystem I for NADPH production. PSII's efficiency is regulated by dynamic assembly factors like Psb27 and protective mechanisms against photodamage, including D1 protein turnover, underscoring its role in sustaining photosynthetic productivity under varying light conditions.
The emergence of PSII approximately 2.43.5 billion years ago revolutionized Earth's biosphere by enabling oxygenic photosynthesis, which supports aerobic life and global carbon cycling. Advances in structural biology, including femtosecond X-ray free-electron laser studies of the MnCaO cluster, continue to elucidate the mechanistic details of water oxidation, with implications for artificial photosynthesis and sustainable energy technologies.

Overview and Historical Context

Discovery and Early Research

The discovery of oxygen evolution in photosynthesis traces back to the late 18th century, when Jan Ingenhousz demonstrated that green plants release oxygen gas specifically in the presence of sunlight, distinguishing this process from mere plant respiration. His experiments, detailed in Experiments upon Vegetables (1779), established that only the green parts of plants perform this light-dependent gas exchange, laying the groundwork for understanding photosynthetic oxygen production. Building on this, Theodor Engelmann's 1882 experiments used motile, oxygen-seeking bacteria to map the wavelengths of light that drive oxygen release in algal filaments, revealing that red and blue light are most effective for photosynthesis while green light is least so. This action spectrum confirmed the role of chlorophyll in light absorption and oxygen evolution, providing early evidence of wavelength-specific photosynthetic activity.
A major breakthrough came in 1937 with Robin Hill's isolation of functional chloroplast fragments from spinach leaves, which evolved oxygen upon illumination in the presence of the electron acceptor ferricyanide, independent of carbon dioxide fixation. This "Hill reaction" demonstrated that oxygen production is a light-driven process localized to chloroplasts, separating the oxygen-evolving phase of photosynthesis from the carbon assimilation phase. The reaction highlighted the existence of an electron transport chain in isolated thylakoids, setting the stage for dissecting the light reactions.
By the mid-20th century, evidence emerged for two distinct light reactions in oxygenic photosynthesis. In 1957, Robert Emerson and colleagues observed the "Emerson enhancement effect" in Chlorella cells, where the quantum yield of photosynthesis increased when shorter-wavelength light (below 680 nm) was combined with longer-wavelength far-red light (above 680 nm), exceeding the efficiency of either alone. This phenomenon indicated the cooperation of two photosystems: one absorbing preferentially at longer wavelengths (later identified as Photosystem I) and another at shorter wavelengths (Photosystem II, responsible for oxygen evolution). Fluorescence studies by Louis Duysens in 1961 further confirmed the existence of these two systems in chloroplasts, showing that light absorbed by Photosystem II induced chlorophyll fluorescence that was quenched by excitation of Photosystem I, implying sequential electron transfer between them. In 1960, Robin Hill and Fay Bendall proposed the Z-scheme model, suggesting that the two photosystems operate in series, with PSII providing electrons to PSI via an electron transport chain, unifying the enhancement effect and fluorescence observations.
The 1960s and early 1970s saw advances in characterizing Photosystem II's oxygen-evolving mechanism through biochemical and spectroscopic approaches. Andr Jagendorf and collaborators developed methods for fractionating chloroplasts in the 1960s, isolating thylakoid membranes enriched in Photosystem II activity and demonstrating light-dependent electron transport specific to oxygen evolution without interference from Photosystem I. In 1970, Bessel Kok proposed the "oxygen clock" model based on flash-induced oxygen yield oscillations in algae, revealing a four-step cycle (S-states) in the water-oxidation process driven by Photosystem II, where four successive light quanta accumulate charges before O release. This model provided a quantitative framework for the periodicity of oxygen production, confirming Photosystem II's central role in catalytic water splitting.

Role in Photosynthetic Electron Transport

Photosystem II (PSII) functions as the water-plastoquinone oxidoreductase, initiating the light-dependent reactions of oxygenic photosynthesis by oxidizing water to molecular oxygen and reducing plastoquinone (PQ) to plastoquinol (PQH). This process absorbs light primarily at approximately 680 nm through its reaction center chlorophyll pair (P680), driving non-cyclic electron flow in the Z-scheme, where electrons are extracted from water and transferred to the PQ pool. In this scheme, PSII operates in series with photosystem I (PSI), providing low-potential electrons that PSI further energizes to reduce NADP to NADPH, while the proton gradient generated across the thylakoid membrane powers ATP synthesis via ATP synthase.
The coupling between PSII and PSI occurs through the mobile PQ pool, the cytochrome bf complex, and plastocyanin, enabling efficient electron transport and balancing the excitation energy between the two photosystems. Reduced PQH diffuses to cytochrome bf, where it undergoes oxidation via the Q-cycle, contributing protons to the lumen and electrons to plastocyanin, which then delivers them to PSI. This linear electron flow ultimately yields approximately 2.5 ATP and 2 NADPH per O evolved, supporting the Calvin-Benson cycle for carbon fixation. PSII handles roughly 50% of the total light absorption in oxygenic photosynthesis under balanced conditions, achieving a quantum yield of up to 90% for photochemistry, with four photons required per O molecule producedtwo to reduce one PQ to PQH and two waters to yield one O.
The emergence of PSII enabled oxygenic photosynthesis, fundamentally transforming Earth's atmosphere during the Great Oxidation Event approximately 2.4 billion years ago by accumulating atmospheric O, which facilitated the rise of aerobic respiration and complex life. Under environmental stress, such as high light intensity, PSII is prone to photoinhibition, where excess absorbed energy generates reactive oxygen species like singlet oxygen, damaging the D1 protein and reducing photosynthetic efficiency. To mitigate this, PSII is regulated through state transitions, which adjust the distribution of light-harvesting complexes (LHCII) between PSII and PSI based on the redox state of the PQ pool; phosphorylation of LHCII under reducing conditions promotes its migration to PSI, alleviating over-excitation of PSII and preventing further damage.

Molecular Architecture

Core Reaction Center Complex

The core reaction center complex of Photosystem II (PSII) forms the minimal functional unit responsible for photochemical charge separation and is embedded in the thylakoid membrane as a heterodimeric assembly. At its heart lies the D1 (PsbA) and D2 (PsbD) protein heterodimer, which serves as the reaction center and coordinates key redox-active cofactors, including the primary electron donor P680 (a chlorophyll a dimer), the primary electron acceptor pheophytin, and a non-heme iron located between the quinone binding sites QA and QB. These subunits each feature five transmembrane helices and are essential for binding the electron transport chain components that enable the initial light-induced charge separation. Flanking the D1/D2 heterodimer are the inner antenna proteins CP47 (PsbB) and CP43 (PsbC), which each possess six transmembrane helices and coordinate approximately 13-16 chlorophyll a molecules and 2 -carotenes per subunit, facilitating energy transfer to the reaction center while contributing to structural integrity.
Additional small subunits stabilize the core assembly and support cofactor interactions. Cytochrome b559, composed of the (PsbE) and (PsbF) subunits, forms a heterodimeric heme-binding unit that aids in protecting the reaction center from photooxidative damage and participates in quinone binding at the QB site during assembly. Similarly, the PsbI subunit, a single-transmembrane helix protein, enhances core stability, modulates quinone occupancy at QB, and is crucial for the maturation of the PSII complex. Together, these components form a monomeric unit of approximately 350 kDa, which dimerizes in the membrane to create the functional PSII core.
High-resolution crystallographic studies have elucidated the atomic-level organization of the PSII core. The structure at 1.9 resolution, determined from Thermosynechococcus vulcanus, reveals the dimeric arrangement with D1/D2 at the center, CP47 and CP43 extending outward, and cytochrome b559 and PsbI positioned peripherally, all oriented such that the dimer interface involves interactions between CP47 monomers across the stromal side. This dimeric organization predominates in the stacked grana regions of thylakoid membranes, promoting efficient packing and stability. The core dimer binds roughly 70-80 chlorophylls in total (35-40 per monomer), though the full PSII unit associates with 200-300 chlorophylls when including peripheral antennas.
Lipids integral to the thylakoid membrane, particularly monogalactosyldiacylglycerol (MGDG), are vital for maintaining the structural and functional stability of the PSII core complex. MGDG surrounds the transmembrane helices of D1, D2, CP47, and CP43, preventing dissociation of subunits and preserving the dimeric conformation under varying environmental conditions. Depletion of MGDG leads to impaired PSII assembly and reduced photostability, underscoring its role in lipid-protein interactions that sustain core integrity.

Peripheral Antenna Complexes

The peripheral antenna complexes of Photosystem II (PSII) serve as the primary site for light absorption in higher plants, capturing photons across a broad spectrum and funneling excitation energy to the core reaction center with high efficiency. These complexes consist of integral membrane proteins that bind chlorophylls and carotenoids, enabling the extension of the light-harvesting capacity beyond the intrinsic core antennas CP43 and CP47. The peripheral antennas are dynamically associated with the PSII core, allowing adjustments to varying light conditions through mechanisms such as state transitions and photoprotection.
The major component of the peripheral antenna is the light-harvesting complex II (LHCII), which forms heterotrimeric structures composed of proteins encoded by the nuclear Lhcb1, Lhcb2, and Lhcb3 genes. Each LHCII monomer binds 12-14 chlorophyll a and b molecules, along with xanthophyll carotenoids such as lutein, neoxanthin, and violaxanthin, which facilitate energy funneling toward the PSII core by optimizing pigment-pigment interactions. These trimers associate loosely or tightly with the PSII supercomplex, contributing up to half of the total chlorophyll in the thylakoid membrane and enabling rapid adaptation to light fluctuations through phosphorylation-mediated mobility.
In addition to LHCII, three minor antenna proteinsCP29 (encoded by Lhcb4), CP26 (Lhcb5), and CP24 (Lhcb6)function as monomeric complexes that bridge the major antennas to the core. These proteins each bind 6-8 chlorophylls and specific carotenoids, playing critical roles in photoprotection by stabilizing the antenna structure under stress and facilitating energy transfer to inner antennas. CP29 and CP26, in particular, are essential for state transitions, where LHCII phosphorylation promotes their detachment or reattachment to balance excitation between PSII and PSI, preventing over-reduction of the electron transport chain. CP24 further modulates antenna interactions in the grana stacks, enhancing overall photosynthetic efficiency by fine-tuning energy distribution.
Excitation energy from the peripheral antennas is transferred to the core via Frster resonance energy transfer (FRET), a non-radiative process driven by dipole-dipole interactions between chlorophyll molecules. In LHCII, energy migrates rapidly through the trimer before transferring to CP47 and CP43 with efficiencies exceeding 90%, as determined by spectroscopic measurements and theoretical modeling. Exciton dynamics simulations reveal that this transfer occurs on picosecond timescales, with xanthophylls aiding in directing flow and preventing energy loss, ensuring near-quantitative delivery to the reaction center under optimal conditions.
The supramolecular organization of peripheral antennas integrates them into PSII supercomplexes, typically comprising a dimeric core with 2-4 LHCII trimers and the minor antennas, as visualized in high-resolution cryo-electron microscopy structures. For instance, the C2S2M2 supercomplex includes two strongly bound and two moderately bound LHCII trimers flanking the core, resolved at approximately 3 resolution. More recent structures, such as those at 2.8 from spruce, confirm the precise positioning of minor antennas like CP24 and CP29, which stabilize LHCII association through protein-protein contacts and lipid bridges. A 2025 cryo-EM structure of the intact C2S2-type PSII-LHCII supercomplex from Arabidopsis thaliana at 2.44 resolution further elucidates the active conformation and atomic details of this organization. This architecture optimizes energy funneling while allowing disassembly for repair.
Under high light, peripheral antennas engage in non-photochemical quenching (NPQ) to dissipate excess energy as heat, primarily through the xanthophyll cycle involving violaxanthin de-epoxidation to zeaxanthin in LHCII and minor complexes. Zeaxanthin binding alters chlorophyll interactions, promoting quenching sites that reduce excitation pressure on PSII and prevent photodamage, with NPQ capacity reaching up to 4-5 fold induction in vivo. This mechanism, activated by lumen acidification, is particularly pronounced in LHCII trimers, where lutein and zeaxanthin quench triplet states, safeguarding the antenna from reactive oxygen species.

Oxygen Evolution Mechanism

Oxygen-Evolving Complex Structure

The oxygen-evolving complex (OEC) of Photosystem II features a Mn4CaO5 cluster arranged in a distorted cubane geometry, consisting of three manganese ions (Mn1, Mn2, and Mn3) and one calcium ion forming the core, with a fourth dangling manganese ion (Mn4) linked via oxygen bridges. This configuration is ligated by seven amino acid residues, including five from the D1 protein and two from the CP43 protein, which provide carboxylate and imidazole side chains to coordinate the metal ions.
Key ligands coordinating the Mn4CaO5 cluster include aspartate residues D1-Asp170 and D1-Asp342, which bridge Mn ions and Ca in the cubane; glutamate residues D1-Glu189 and CP43-Glu354, contributing to linkages to Mn1 and Mn3; the histidine residue D1-His332, which directly binds Mn4; and the C-terminal carboxylate of D1-Ala344. The redox-active tyrosine residue D1-Tyr161 (TyrZ), hydrogen-bonded via D1-His190, serves as the immediate electron donor to P680+ and facilitates proton-coupled electron transfer to the OEC. These ligands position water-derived oxygens at the cluster's periphery, facilitating substrate binding during catalysis.
Bicarbonate (HCO3-) or CO2 binds near the Mn4 site of the OEC, modulating the redox potential of the cluster and enhancing electron transfer efficiency, as evidenced by FTIR difference spectroscopy detecting vibrational changes upon bicarbonate depletion and restoration.
High-resolution structural studies have evolved from an initial X-ray crystallography model at 3.0 resolution revealing the basic Mn-Ca cluster arrangement (Loll et al., 2005), to a radiation-damage-free XFEL structure of the dark-stable S1 state at 1.95 resolution, which clarifies the open cubane conformation with precise Mn-Mn distances ranging from 2.7 to 3.3 and confirms the dangling Mn4 linkage.
The redox-inactive Ca2+ ion within the OEC stabilizes the Mn4CaO5 cluster by maintaining its structural integrity against photo-induced damage, as substitution with other ions disrupts assembly and reduces oxygen evolution rates.

Water Oxidation Cycle

The water oxidation cycle in photosystem II, known as the Kok cycle, involves sequential oxidation states of the oxygen-evolving complex (OEC), termed S-states (S to S), where each state advances by the removal of one electron from water substrates upon absorption of a photon. The cycle begins in the dark-stable S state and progresses as follows: light-induced oxidation drives S S S S, with the transient S state facilitating O release and relaxation back to S, completing the cycle and oxidizing two water molecules to produce one O molecule, four protons, and four electrons. This process requires exactly four photons per O evolved, establishing the minimum quantum requirement for water splitting in oxygenic photosynthesis.
Redox changes in the OEC during the Kok cycle primarily involve the manganese ions, which cycle through mixed oxidation states of Mn(III) and Mn(IV). In S, the configuration is typically three Mn(III) and one Mn(IV); advancement to S oxidizes one Mn(III) to Mn(IV), yielding two Mn(III) and two Mn(IV); S features one Mn(III) and three Mn(IV); and S has all four Mn(IV). These transitions are probed by electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectroscopy, where the S state exhibits a characteristic multiline signal at g 2, arising from a multinuclear Mn(IV)-centered spin system with hyperfine interactions from the four Mn ions. The S state shows distinct EPR signals, including a broad g = 2 feature, indicative of further structural or redox adjustments prior to O formation.
Substrate water binding and incorporation occur primarily during the S S and S S transitions, with the two water-derived oxygens coordinating to the OEC via terminal ligands or oxo-bridges on Mn or Ca ions. The exact mechanism of O-O bond formation remains debated, with models proposing either nucleophilic attack by a Ca-bound hydroxide on a Mn(IV)=O oxo group or radical coupling between an Mn(IV)-oxyl (Mn(IV)-O) and a Mn(III)-oxo (Mn(III)=O). Recent spectroscopic evidence supports the radical coupling pathway, where oxyl/oxo intermediates form in S, leading to O release in S without requiring external water insertion until cycle reset. As of 2025, time-resolved XFEL studies have provided further insights into structural dynamics during S-state transitions, highlighting the role of water channels like Cl-1 in facilitating O-O bond formation, as seen in mutated PSII complexes with altered kinetics.
The Kok cycle manifests experimentally as a period-four oscillation in O yield under saturating single-turnover flashes, first observed in kinetic studies of algal suspensions. This oscillation arises from the accumulation of S-states, with maximal O evolution on the fourth flash from S, but it is damped by inefficiencies: "misses" (failed state advances, ~10%) due to incomplete charge stabilization and "double-hits" (simultaneous two-state advances, ~20%), as quantified in the Joliot-Kok kinetic model. These factors lead to probabilistic state distributions and potential over-reduction risks, where back-reactions (e.g., electron return from reduced TyrZ to the OEC) can occur if proton release lags, destabilizing higher S-states and reducing efficiency.

Electron Transfer Processes

Primary Charge Separation

Primary charge separation in Photosystem II (PSII) is initiated by the absorption of a photon by the special pair of chlorophyll molecules, denoted as P680, consisting of Chl_D1 and Chl_D2 located in the D1 and D2 subunits of the reaction center. This excitation promotes P680 to its singlet excited state, P680, a delocalized excitonic state that spans the core pigments including accessory chlorophylls and the primary acceptor pheophytin (Pheo_D1). The excited state energy is rapidly transferred from the peripheral antenna complexes to P680 in a process that precedes the charge separation, ensuring efficient trapping within the reaction center.
From P680*, electron transfer occurs primarily to Pheo_D1, forming the initial charge-separated radical pair P680 Pheo_D1. This ultrafast process follows two coexisting pathways: a kinetically favored fast route involving the accessory chlorophyll Chl_D1 as the initial donor, with transfer times of approximately 163 fs (60% contribution) and 2.0 ps (40% contribution), and a slower pathway directly from the P_D1 P_D2 pair on timescales of 185 ps (62%) and 4.2 ns (38%). Accessory chlorophylls Chl_D1 and Chl_D2, along with -carotenes, facilitate excitonic delocalization over the reaction center pigments, lowering the energy barrier for charge separation in accordance with the energy gap law, which dictates that electron transfer rates increase exponentially with decreasing energy gaps between donor and acceptor states. The charge separation spans an effective distance of about 20 from the donor to Pheo_D1, as evidenced by electrochromic shifts in nearby pigment absorption spectra induced by the local electric field changes.
The radical pair P680 Pheo_D1 has a lifetime of approximately 100200 ns at physiological temperatures, during which the electron is transferred to the primary quinone acceptor Q_A to prevent deleterious recombination and stabilize the charge separation. This lifetime is modulated by the redox state of Q_A and protein conformational dynamics. Femtosecond transient absorption spectroscopy reveals the rise of the Pheo_D1 anion signal at around 455 nm within 1 ps, confirming the primary electron acceptance by Pheo_D1, while two-dimensional electronic spectroscopy at ambient and low temperatures (e.g., 77 K) resolves the delocalized excitonic couplings and unidirectional charge transfer pathway, ruling out significant contributions from alternative acceptors like Pheo_D2. This unidirectionality arises from a higher activation energy barrier in the D2 branchnearly twice that of the D1 branchdue to subtle differences in the protein environment and pigment embedding. These spectroscopic techniques have established that charge separation occurs with near-unity quantum efficiency, underscoring its role as the foundational step in photosynthetic electron transport.

Secondary Electron Acceptors and Donors

Following primary charge separation in Photosystem II (PSII), the electron on the reduced pheophytin (Pheo) is rapidly transferred to the tightly bound plastoquinone QA, occurring with a time constant of approximately 200 ps, thereby stabilizing the charge-separated state. Subsequently, the semiquinone anion QA reduces the exchangeable plastoquinone QB through two sequential one-electron transfers: the first within 200400 s to form QB, and the second on a similar timescale after another photon absorption, leading to the doubly reduced and protonated plastoquinol QBH in about 1 ms overall. This double reduction of QB is facilitated by protonation from nearby amino acid residues, such as histidine and aspartate, which stabilize the semiquinone intermediate and prevent charge recombination.
On the donor side, the highly oxidizing cation radical P680 is reduced by the redox-active tyrosine residue TyrZ (located at position 161 on the D1 protein) in approximately 30 ns, generating a neutral TyrZ radical that serves as an intermediary. The TyrZ radical is then reduced by the oxygen-evolving complex (OEC) on a microsecond timescale (501000 s, depending on the S-state), thereby linking the donor-side electron transfer to the advancement of the OEC's catalytic cycle.
These electron transfers are thermodynamically driven by the redox potentials of the involved cofactors: P680/P680* +1.2 V, QA/QA 150 mV, and QB/QB +90 mV, creating a favorable gradient that promotes unidirectional flow toward plastoquinol formation and minimizes back-reactions. The reduction of QB to QBH is tightly coupled to the uptake of two protons from the stromal side of the membrane via protonatable residues and water channels, which contributes to vectorial proton translocation and lumen acidification, thereby enhancing the proton motive force for ATP synthesis.
Under conditions of high light or environmental stress, an over-reduced plastoquinone pool can impose back-pressure on PSII by slowing QB reduction and QBH exchange (turnover time 20 ms), which limits overall electron flux and protects the system from photodamage; this regulatory effect is commonly quantified through measurements of chlorophyll a fluorescence yield, where increased fluorescence indicates QA accumulation.

Regulation and Inhibitors

Assembly and Repair Mechanisms

The assembly of Photosystem II (PSII) involves coordinated biogenesis of its multi-subunit structure, with most subunits encoded by the nuclear genome and a subset by the chloroplast genome. Nuclear-encoded subunits, such as the extrinsic proteins PsbO, PsbP, and PsbQ, are synthesized in the cytosol and imported into chloroplasts via the TOC/TIC translocon system before being routed to the thylakoid membrane through the Sec or SRP-dependent pathways. Chloroplast-encoded core subunits, including D1 (PsbA), D2 (PsbD), CP47 (PsbB), and CP43 (PsbC), are translated on thylakoid-bound ribosomes and inserted into the membrane via the YidC/Alb3 insertase, a member of the Oxa1 superfamily that facilitates co-translational integration and folding. This coordination ensures stoichiometric assembly, beginning with the formation of the PSII core complex in the unstacked stroma lamellae regions of the thylakoid membrane, where intermediate subcomplexes like the D1-D2-cyt b559 reaction center assemble prior to peripheral antenna integration.
A critical aspect of PSII maintenance is the rapid turnover of the D1 protein, which has a half-life of approximately 30 minutes under strong illumination due to photodamage from prolonged over-oxidation of the reaction center chlorophyll P680^+. This damage arises when electron donation from the oxygen-evolving complex (OEC) lags behind charge separation, leading to reactive oxygen species formation and oxidative modifications in the D1 transmembrane helices. Recent cryo-electron microscopy studies as of 2024 have provided structural insights into the early stages of the PSII repair cycle, including the mechanisms of D1 degradation and subunit exchange. The damaged D1 is selectively degraded by the chloroplast FtsH protease complex, an ATP-dependent metalloprotease that performs endoproteolytic cleavage, enabling replacement with newly synthesized D1 to restore PSII function and prevent net photoinhibition.
Chaperone proteins play essential roles in stabilizing assembly intermediates and protecting the OEC during catalytic cycles. Psb27, a nuclear-encoded extrinsic protein, binds to the lumenal side of PSII intermediates, facilitating OEC maturation by stabilizing the Mn4Ca cluster and preventing manganese ion release during S-state transitions in the water oxidation cycle. Similarly, the small transmembrane subunit PsbI associates with the reaction center to maintain structural integrity, aiding in the coordination of extrinsic subunits and shielding the OEC from destabilization during repeated S0S4 advancements. These chaperones ensure efficient repair by transiently binding to damaged or assembling PSII, promoting subunit exchange without full disassembly.
The assembly of the functional OEC is light-dependent, culminating in photoactivation where inorganic cofactors (Mn^2+ and Ca^2+) are incorporated into the apo-PSII form through a redox process driven by charge separation. This requires illumination with at least two single-turnover flashes: the first oxidizes Mn^2+ to Mn^3+ and binds the initial cluster, while the second advances the system to the S2 state, forming the stable Mn4CaO5 core capable of water oxidation. This stepwise photoactivation synchronizes OEC biogenesis with PSII core assembly, ensuring catalytic competence only under light conditions.
Under environmental stresses such as high light or UV radiation, PSII repair mechanisms are upregulated to counteract accelerated photodamage, involving enhanced protein turnover and antioxidant defenses. The Clp protease complex, a stromal ATP-dependent serine protease, contributes to quality control by degrading misfolded PSII subunits and aggregates formed during stress-induced disassembly, complementing FtsH activity. Concurrently, reactive oxygen species (ROS) scavenging is bolstered, with the extrinsic PsbO subunit playing a protective role by stabilizing the OEC against oxidative inactivation and facilitating proton channeling to mitigate ROS accumulation at the donor side. These responses maintain PSII efficiency by prioritizing repair over de novo assembly in stressed conditions.

Pharmacological Inhibitors

Pharmacological inhibitors of Photosystem II (PSII) primarily target specific sites within the complex to disrupt electron transport, oxygen evolution, or structural integrity, serving both as herbicides and research tools. These compounds are classified based on their binding locations and mechanisms, with many acting at the acceptor side, donor side, or through non-specific denaturation.
QB-site inhibitors, such as the urea herbicide DCMU (3-(3,4-dichlorophenyl)-1,1-dimethylurea, also known as diuron) and triazine herbicides like atrazine and terbuthylazine, bind competitively to the QB pocket on the D1 protein, preventing plastoquinone occupancy and blocking electron transfer from QA to QB. Certain phenolic QB-site inhibitors, such as bromoxynil, bind near the QB pocket and exhibit effects on QA kinetics, including acceleration of QA reoxidation, which disrupts stable charge separation and promotes back-reactions. This inhibition halts the reduction of the plastoquinone pool, leading to oxidative stress and cessation of photosynthesis in target plants. Binding affinities are high, with I values of approximately 72 nM for DCMU/diuron and 110 nM for terbuthylazine in pea thylakoid assays measuring DCPIP photoreduction. These inhibitors are seminal in herbicide development, with over 80 commercial compounds in this class classified under Herbicide Resistance Action Committee Groups 5 and 6.
Donor-side inhibitors target the oxygen-evolving complex (OEC), disrupting water oxidation. Ammonia and fluoride (F) anions bind to manganese ligands in the MnCaO cluster, stabilizing the S state and inhibiting S-state transitions required for O release, with ammonia models indicating replacement of a terminal water ligand on Mn. Hydroxylamine acts reductively, destroying the Mn cluster and lowering S-states, thereby decoupling oxygen evolution from downstream electron transfer. These inhibitors are valuable for isolating donor-side reactions in spectroscopic studies.
Non-specific inhibitors, such as high salt concentrations (e.g., 2 M NaCl or CaCl), release or destabilize extrinsic subunits like PsbO and PsbV, reducing oxygen evolution by impairing OEC stability and Ca/Cl cofactor retention without directly affecting the core reaction center. Alcohols, including ethanol or 2-propanol at moderate concentrations, can similarly denature these extrinsic proteins during isolation procedures, leading to loss of PSII activity.
These inhibitors have broad applications in agriculture, where PSII-targeting herbicides dominate weed control, accounting for a substantial market share (e.g., triazines and ureas among the most used globally). In research, they enable fluorescence assays, such as measuring the F/F ratio (maximum quantum yield of PSII), where DCMU blocks variable fluorescence to quantify photodamage or efficiency. Inhibitor-resistant mutants also inform evolutionary studies of PSII adaptations. While effective, their use highlights the need for rotation to mitigate resistance, as repair mechanisms partially counteract damage from blocked electron flow.
