Axis mundi
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 The axis mundi, a term derived from Latin meaning "world axis," refers to a sacred cosmological pillar or center that connects the heavens, earth, and underworld, symbolizing the point where the divine irrupts into the profane world and establishes the ordered cosmos. This concept breaks the homogeneity of undifferentiated space, transforming it into a consecrated, habitable realm oriented toward the transcendent by providing a fixed point of communication between human and divine planes. Popularized in comparative mythology by Mircea Eliade in the mid-20th century, the axis mundi manifests across diverse cultures through symbols such as mountains, trees, ladders, temples, and pillars, each serving as a ritual focus for worship, cosmogonic renewal, and shamanic ascent. For instance, in Mesoamerican traditions, pyramids like those at Tenochtitlan functioned as artificial mountains embodying the axis mundi, linking terrestrial life to celestial deities through sacrifices and ceremonies. Similarly, sacred trees or poles in Indigenous Australian and Native American mythologies, such as the Achilpa's cosmic pole, replicate the world's creation by anchoring the territory to the gods' domain. In broader religious contexts, structures like the Kaaba in Islam or Mount Meru in Hindu-Buddhist cosmology exemplify this archetype, underscoring its universal role in orienting sacred space and facilitating transcendence.

Etymology and Conceptual Foundations

Etymology

The term axis mundi derives from Latin, where axis refers to a pivot, axle, or central line, and mundi is the genitive form of mundus, meaning the world or universe, thus literally translating to "axis of the world" or "world pillar."
In scholarly contexts, the phrase gained prominence in the mid-20th century through the work of historian of religions Mircea Eliade, who employed it extensively in his 1957 book The Sacred and the Profane to describe a universal symbol in comparative mythology that connects cosmic realms, building on ancient Indo-European notions of a structured cosmic order.
The concept links to earlier linguistic traditions, such as the Sanskrit skambha, meaning "prop" or "pillar" from the root skambh ("to support"), which in Vedic hymns like those of the Atharvaveda denotes a cosmic support upholding the universe. Similarly, the Greek omphalos, from omphalos ("navel"), symbolizes the world's central navel or origin point, as in the Delphic oracle stone, paralleling the axial motif in Indo-European cosmologies.

Definition and Cosmological Role

The axis mundi, derived from the Latin phrase meaning "world axis," represents a sacred center or vertical axis that links the celestial (heaven), terrestrial (earth), and chthonic (underworld) realms, functioning as the world's navel or point of origin in mythological cosmologies. This archetype manifests as a fixed point of sanctity, detaching a specific territory from the homogeneous expanse of profane space and rendering it qualitatively distinct through ritual or divine revelation. As Mircea Eliade describes, it serves as the "central pivot of the earth or of the entire cosmos," enabling the structured apprehension of the world as a cosmos rather than undifferentiated chaos.
In cosmological narratives, the axis mundi plays a pivotal role as a stabilizing element amid the primordial flux of creation myths, where it establishes order by connecting disparate cosmic planes and facilitating the flow of spiritual energy between them. It acts as a conduit for divine communication, allowing transcendence from the earthly to the heavenly and underworldly domains, often symbolized as a universal pillar that supports the heavens and grounds the earth. Eliade emphasizes this function, noting that "the three cosmic levelsearth, heaven, underworldhave been put in communication this communication is sometimes expressed through the image of a universal pillar, axis mundi." Through this linkage, the axis mundi not only reactualizes the foundational events of creation but also ensures ongoing cosmic equilibrium, preventing relapse into formless disorder.
As an archetypal construct, the axis mundi exhibits remarkable invariance across diverse cultural traditions, serving as an immutable fixed point within the ever-shifting cosmic flux and providing existential orientation for human experience. It orients sacred space by emanating cardinal directions from its center, transforming disoriented profane territory into a habitable, meaningful domain aligned with divine patterns. This enduring quality underscores its role in religious worldviews, where proximity to the axis mundi equates to nearness to the gods, fostering a sense of centeredness and purpose amid universal impermanence.

Symbolic Representations

Natural Symbols

Natural symbols of the axis mundi manifest in unaltered elements of the physical landscape, embodying the primordial linkage between heaven, earth, and the underworld while underscoring the sacred's irruption into everyday existence. As Mircea Eliade describes, these formssuch as trees, mountains, and dynamic features like rivers or smokestructure the cosmos vertically, serving as fixed points of orientation and renewal in mythological frameworks across cultures.
Trees and plants prominently symbolize this vertical axis through their organic growth, roots delving into subterranean depths, trunks anchoring the terrestrial plane, and branches aspiring toward the celestial. The world tree archetype, recurrent in global traditions, encapsulates endless regeneration and cosmic unity, with Eliade noting that "the cosmos was imagined in the form of a gigantic tree; the mode of being of the cosmos, and first of all its capacity for endless regeneration, are symbolically expressed by the life of the tree." In Norse mythology, Yggdrasil, the vast ash tree, exemplifies this as the central pillar sustaining the nine interconnected worlds: its three roots extend to the wells of Urdarbrunnr (fate), Mmisbrunnr (wisdom), and Hvergelmir (underworld origins), while its branches spread over Asgard and the upper realms, binding the entire Norse cosmos. Likewise, the Bodhi Tree in Buddhism represents a pivotal axis of enlightenment, where Siddhartha Gautama meditated beneath its shade to attain nirvana, transforming the site into a conduit between mundane suffering and transcendent wisdom, as its sacred saplings propagate this spiritual linkage worldwide.
Mountains and peaks, as towering natural elevations, further incarnate the axis mundi by ascending from earth to touch the divine, often designated as the world's navel or origin point. Eliade emphasizes their universal symbolism, stating that "the mountain is a symbol of the universe," linking paradisiacal heights to earthly foundations. Mount Meru holds this role preeminently in Hindu-Buddhist cosmology, depicted as the golden, axial mountain at the universe's core, encircled by seven concentric rings of continents and oceans, with its summit housing the gods of Trayastrimsa and serving as the cosmic pivot where creation unfolds from primordial waters like Lake Anavatapta. In Tibetan traditions, Mount Kailash embodies a comparable sacred elevation, identified as the terrestrial form of Meru and Shiva's eternal abode, its pyramidal form channeling spiritual energies to unite the profane world with higher realms through pilgrimage circuits that circumambulate its base.
Additional natural forms, including rivers, vines, and smoke columns, evoke the axis mundi's dynamic ascent and descent, facilitating ritual or visionary traversal of cosmic layers. The Ganges River in Hindu lore descends as a celestial stream from the heavens via Shiva's matted locks, purifying the earth and acting as a vertical conduit that merges divine essence with human life, its flow symbolizing the axis from cosmic source to terrestrial renewal. Vines, with their twisting upward growth, mirror shamanic pathways in various indigenous contexts, akin to ladders bridging realms. Smoke columns, observed in Central Asian rituals, rise through yurt openings as ephemeral axes, enabling souls or shamans to ascend to the sky world, with Eliade observing their role in signifying passage to other dimensions.

Anthropogenic Symbols

Anthropogenic symbols of the axis mundi encompass human-constructed or modified artifacts designed to embody the cosmic center, often replicating the verticality of natural forms to establish a sacred connection between realms. These intentional creations serve as focal points for spiritual orientation, transforming ordinary spaces into hierophanies where the divine intersects the profane. Unlike spontaneous natural features, such symbols reflect deliberate cultural engineering to manifest the world axis in built environments.
In architectural contexts, ziggurats represent prominent examples of axis mundi symbolism, functioning as artificial mountains that link heaven, earth, and underworld. The Mesopotamian Etemenanki ziggurat in Babylon, dedicated to Marduk, was explicitly named "House of the Foundation of Heaven and Earth," embodying the axis mundi as a staged platform ascending toward the divine. This seven-tiered structure symbolized the cosmic order, with its summit serving as a temple where deities descended to the human realm. Similarly, pillars and ladders in ancient architecture, such as those in temple complexes, evoked the vertical pillar upholding the cosmos.
Totem poles among Indigenous peoples of the Pacific Northwest exemplify carved vertical forms as axis mundi, integrating ancestral narratives and spiritual hierarchies into wooden pillars. These poles, often erected at village entrances or clan houses, depict interconnected figuresanimals, spirits, and humansclimbing or stacked along a central axis, mirroring the world tree that bridges multiple worlds. Crafted from cedar trees, they not only commemorate histories but also orient communities toward the sacred center.
Domestic and ritual objects further extend axis mundi symbolism into everyday and ceremonial life, rendering the sacred portable and accessible. The hearth, as a central household feature in various cultures, acts as a microcosmic axis mundi, channeling fire as a conduit between earthly sustenance and celestial origins. In ancient Indo-European traditions, the hearth's smoke rising skyward symbolized the pillar linking domestic space to the divine. Maypoles, rooted in Germanic and Celtic festivals, serve as temporary axes during seasonal rites, with ribbons and dances encircling the pole to invoke fertility and cosmic renewal. Biblical Jacob's Ladder, envisioned as a visionary ladder with angels ascending and descending, represents a portable axis mundi, connecting patriarchal exile to divine promise.
Crosses and staffs embody the axis mundi in religious iconography, emphasizing vertical mediation through crafted symbols. The Christian cross, particularly when erected on elevated sites like Mount Calvary, functions as a vertical connector between human suffering and heavenly redemption, its intersection of horizontal and vertical arms delineating the cosmic crossroads. In shamanic traditions, staffs and drums incorporate world-tree motifs, with the drum's frame often painted as a tree trunk pierced by the axis, facilitating ecstatic journeys across realms. These objects, held or struck in ritual, personalize the world pillar for individual spiritual traversal.

Historical and Cultural Examples

In Eurasian Mythologies

In Norse mythology, Yggdrasil stands as the cosmic ash tree at the center of the universe, forming the axis mundi that interconnects the nine realms of existence. Its vast branches extend upward to support the heavens and Asgard, the realm of the gods, while its three roots extend to Niflheim at the Hvergelmir well, Jotunheim at the Mmisbrunnr well, and Asgard at the well of Urd, anchoring the structure across cosmic layers. This tree is not merely a static pillar but a living entity, sustained by the labors of creatures like the squirrel Ratatoskr, who carries messages along its trunk, and the deer that graze its boughs, symbolizing the dynamic interplay between divine and natural forces. A pivotal narrative involves Odin, the Allfather, who sacrificed himself by hanging from Yggdrasil for nine nights and days, pierced by his own spear, to attain esoteric wisdom, including the knowledge of runes; this act underscores the tree's role as a conduit for shamanic initiation and divine insight.
In Hindu and Buddhist cosmologies, Mount Meru emerges as the golden mountain serving as the world's central axis, piercing through the layers of the universe and linking the earthly realm to the divine abodes above. Positioned at the heart of Jambudvipa, the southern continent inhabited by humans, Meru is encircled by seven concentric rings of golden mountains and four vast continents, with its towering peak supporting the heavens and its base rooted in the cosmic ocean. In Hindu traditions, as described in texts like the Puranas, Meru is the abode of gods such as Brahma and Shiva, embodying the sacred center where creation unfolds and ritual pilgrimages converge to affirm cosmic order. Buddhist accounts, drawing from the Abhidharmakosha and similar works, portray Merualso called Sumeruas the axis around which the sun and moon revolve, flanked by continents like Purvavideha and Aparagodaniya, and topped by realms of devas, reinforcing its function as the pivot of samsara and enlightenment paths. This mountain's layered structure, with terraces of jewels and mythical trees, mirrors the hierarchical cosmos, where ascents symbolize spiritual progression from base desires to transcendent purity.
Greek mythology positions Mount Olympus as the exalted seat of the Olympian gods, functioning as a celestial axis that bridges the mortal world below with the divine ether above. Homer's Iliad describes Olympus as a snow-capped peak shrouded in clouds, where Zeus presides over the pantheon in halls of bronze and gold, issuing decrees that govern earthly affairs and serving as the immutable center of cosmic authority. This mountain, often depicted with gates that open to reveal the gods' assembly, embodies the vertical hierarchy from human strife to immortal harmony, with figures like Hermes traversing it as a messenger between realms. In Mesopotamian traditions, Babylonian ziggurats, such as the Etemenanki in Babylon dedicated to Marduk, replicate this linking function through their stepped pyramid form, designed as artificial mountains to connect the earthly plane with the heavenly abode of the gods. These structures, built with seven tiers symbolizing planetary levels, facilitated rituals where priests ascended to commune with deities, positioning the temple summit as the point where divine will descended to human society. Inscriptions and archaeological evidence from sites like Ur reveal ziggurats as the cosmic navel, where the god's presence manifested, ensuring fertility, justice, and order across the flat earth disk encircled by waters.

In American Indigenous Traditions

In Mesoamerican cosmologies, the ceiba tree, known as the yax che' or "green tree," serves as a central axis mundi, symbolizing the vertical pillar that links the earthly realm with the 13 layers of the heavens above and the 9 levels of the underworld below. This sacred tree, with its extensive roots penetrating Xibalba (the underworld) and its branches extending into the celestial domain, represents the pathway for divine communication and the cyclical renewal of creation, as depicted in Classic Maya art such as the sarcophagus lid of K'inich Janaab' Pakal at Palenque. The ceiba's structure embodies the quadripartite division of the cosmos, where its trunk marks the navel of the world, facilitating rituals and pilgrimages that reaffirm cosmic order.
Among the Aztecs, the teponaztli, a horizontal slit drum often carved from a single piece of wood, functioned within ritual contexts as a sonic embodiment of the cosmic axis, its resonant tones invoking the harmony between the earthly plane and the divine realms. Paired with the vertical huehuetl drum, the teponaztli symbolized the horizontal axis of the universe, contributing to ceremonies that reenacted the creation myth and maintained the balance of the five cosmic directions centered on the axis mundi at Tenochtitlan. These instruments, integral to festivals honoring deities like Tezcatlipoca, produced interlocking rhythms believed to bridge human actions with the primordial sounds of the gods' emergence from the waters of creation.
In North American Indigenous traditions, the Lakota Sioux regard the Black Hills (Paha Sapa) as the sacred center of the universe, functioning as a natural axis mundi that connects the physical world with the spiritual domains of Wakan Tanka, the Great Spirit. This elevated landscape, encompassing sites like Harney Peak (now Black Elk Peak), serves as the heart of Lakota cosmology, where vision quests and the Sun Dance rituals occur to align human life with cosmic forces, its rocky formations echoing the vertical pillar that sustains creation. Similarly, among the Hopi, the sipapua small hole in the floor of kivasrepresents the point of emergence from the underworld (Tuwaqachi), acting as a symbolic axis mundi through which ancestral clans ascended to the present Fourth World, enabling ongoing ceremonial access to subterranean ancestors and celestial guidance.
South American Amazonian traditions feature the yag vine (Banisteriopsis caapi), central to shamanic practices among groups like the Shipibo and Tukano, where its ingestion induces visions of an ascending ladder connecting the terrestrial realm to multiple heavenly layers. In these ayahuasca ceremonies, the vine manifests as a luminous pathway or cosmic vine, allowing shamans to traverse the axis mundi between the three-tiered universeearth, sky, and underworldfacilitating healing, prophecy, and communion with spirit beings in a process that mirrors the ecological interconnectedness of the rainforest.

In Other Global Traditions

In African traditions, the Dogon people of Mali envision the axis mundi through their cosmology centered on Amma's cosmic egg, a primordial structure that encapsulates the universe's origins and divides into elements like air, earth, fire, and water before unfolding into creation via spiral vibrations. This egg, known as aduno talu, links the dualistic unity of the cosmos, with the Sigui ceremonyheld every 60 years to commemorate Sirius's cyclereenacting its segmentation and renewal, incorporating a central pillar-like umbilical cord symbolized by the Hogon leader as the social and cosmic center. Similarly, among the Yoruba of West Africa, the palm tree (ope) functions as the axis mundi, bridging the realms of heaven (run), earth (Ay), and ancestors through its vertical structure: roots anchoring the terrestrial, trunk ascending to the divine, and fronds evoking the sky. In If divination, the tree's 16 nuts (ikin) serve as oracular tools embodying the deity runmila, who ascends and descends via the palm to maintain cosmic order, while its nuts and chain (pl) facilitate communication between human destiny and spiritual forces.
In Oceanic cultures, sacred platforms and genealogical frameworks embody the axis mundi. For the Hawaiian people, the lelea raised wooden altar or offering tower within heiau templesserves as a conduit linking the realm of light and life (ao) to the afterlife and darkness (p), enabling sacrifices of animals, fruits, or humans to invoke chiefly mana (spiritual power) and ancestral connections. Positioned in ritual complexes like those on Mokumanamana Island, aligned with the Tropic of Cancer and solstices, the lele reinforces cosmological balance, calibrating seasonal cycles and voyaging networks that tie human society to divine origins in the mythical west. Among the Mori of New Zealand, whakapapagenealogical layeringlinks cosmic epochs from the void (te kore) and night (te p) to the world of light (te ao mrama), tracing descent from primordial gods like Rangi (sky) and Papa (earth) through ancestors to all living entities. This relational framework integrates human identity with natural and spiritual realms, organizing tribal knowledge and obligations across layered realities through oral narratives that bind the universe's evolution.
In Jain cosmology, an outlier in South Asian traditions, Jambudvpa represents the central continent of the middle world (madhyaloka), the sole domain for human birth and spiritual liberation under the trthakaras, encircled by oceans and subsidiary lands in a vast, tiered universe. At its core stands Mount Meru, the axial pillar (axis mundi) supporting the cosmological structure, rising as the golden summit where heavens, earth, and hells converge in a multi-layered mandala of existence, symbolizing the path from mundane cycles to enlightenment. This vertical axis, detailed in texts like the Tiloyapannatti, underscores Jambudvpa's role as the universe's navel, distinct from surrounding realms by its accessibility to jinas and the ethical pursuit of non-violence (ahis).

Interpretive Perspectives

In Comparative Religion and Mythology

In comparative religion, the concept of the axis mundi has been analyzed as a universal symbol that structures human experience of the cosmos across diverse traditions. Mircea Eliade, in his seminal work The Sacred and the Profane, describes the axis mundi as a hierophanya manifestation of the sacred within the profane realmthat transforms undifferentiated, chaotic space into an oriented world. This central point, often symbolized by a mountain, tree, or pillar, connects the three cosmic levels of heaven, earth, and underworld, rendering the environment meaningful and habitable by establishing a fixed center amid homogeneity. Eliade argues that such symbols are not arbitrary but reflect an archetypal human need to irrupt the sacred into everyday existence, thereby consecrating space and time.
From a structuralist perspective, Claude Lvi-Strauss examines mythological systems as governed by binary oppositions, where the axis mundi functions as a mediating vertical axis between fundamental dualities such as heaven and earth. In works like The Structural Study of Myth, Lvi-Strauss posits that myths resolve cultural contradictions through such mediations, with the world axis serving as a structural element that bridges oppositional realms to maintain cosmic and social order. This approach highlights how the axis mundi, as a recurrent motif, embodies the logical operations of the human mind in processing vertical hierarchies inherent to cosmological narratives.
Comparative reconstructions in Indo-European studies reveal a proto-myth of the world pillar, evident in Proto-Indo-European (PIE) linguistic and mythological reflexes across descendant traditions. Scholars like Jaan Puhvel identify this as a shared archaic motif, reconstructed from terms and narratives such as the Vedic skambha (supporting pillar) in the Rigveda, which upholds the universe, paralleling later forms like the Norse Yggdrasil. This PIE world pillar symbolizes cosmic stability and the axis linking realms, suggesting a common ancestral cosmology among Indo-European speakers dating to the Bronze Age. Such reconstructions underscore the axis mundi's role in unifying diverse mythologies under a single structural paradigm. However, these approaches have faced criticism for oversimplifying cultural differences and assuming universal archetypes without sufficient historical evidence.

In Shamanic and Ritual Contexts

In shamanic traditions, the axis mundi serves as a central conduit for practitioners to traverse the cosmological layers, facilitating ascent to celestial realms or descent into the underworld during ecstatic journeys. Among Siberian Tungusic and Altaic shamans, the world tree embodies this axis, with initiates ritually climbing its branchesoften represented by a birch pole in ceremoniesto access the upper world for divination or soul retrieval, a practice documented as essential to their ecstatic techniques. Similarly, in Norse contexts interpreted through a shamanic lens, Odin's self-sacrifice by hanging on Yggdrasil for nine nights mirrors initiatory ordeals, where the tree functions as an axis mundi enabling passage between realms to gain esoteric knowledge, reflecting broader Indo-European shamanic motifs of vertical navigation. These traversals underscore the axis as a dynamic pathway, activated through drumming, chanting, or symbolic ascent to bridge profane and sacred domains.
Ritually, the axis mundi orients ceremonies by establishing a sacred center, guiding soul journeys for healing and communal harmony. In Siberian practices, shamans invoke the world tree during healing rites to journey downward for retrieving lost souls or upward for negotiating with sky spirits, thereby restoring balance in the tripartite cosmos of earth, heaven, and underworld. Among the Maya, ball courts functioned analogously as ritual portals to Xibalba, the underworld, where the ballgame reenacted mythic contests between life and death forces, with the court's central axis symbolizing the passage for offerings or sacrificial victors to mediate cosmic renewal and fertility. Such functions highlight the axis's role in shamanic healing, where orientation along this vertical line ensures efficacious interaction with spiritual entities, often culminating in communal rituals that reinforce social and ecological equilibrium.
The axis mundi exhibits symbolic ambivalence in shamanic rites, acting as both a connector across worlds and a formidable barrier enforcing vertical hierarchy and initiatory trials. While it enables transcendence, access is guarded by mythical sentinelssuch as bird spirits on the world tree's branches in Siberian loredemanding rigorous preparation to surmount, thus delineating sacred from profane and novice from adept. This duality emphasizes ritual hierarchy, where the axis's connective power is tempered by its role as a threshold, compelling participants to confront peril in ascent or descent, as seen in the perilous stakes of Mayan ballgame rituals symbolizing life's precarious balance. In these contexts, the axis mundi, akin to Eliade's notion of hierophany, manifests the sacred irruption into the ritual space, yet its barrier aspect underscores the disciplined vertical order of shamanic cosmology.

Modern Interpretations and Applications

In Contemporary Spirituality and Architecture

In contemporary architecture, skyscrapers such as Taipei 101 in Taiwan have been interpreted as embodying the axis mundi through their vertical form, which connects earth and sky while incorporating feng shui principles for harmony and stability. The building's pagoda-inspired tiers and central damper system have been seen as symbolizing a stabilizing cosmic pillar, drawing on traditional motifs to mitigate seismic forces and evoke prosperity in a modern urban context. Similarly, modern feng shui-influenced pagodas and towers, like those in Shanghai's Jin Mao Tower, align with cardinal directions to channel positive energy, positioning the structure as a contemporary world axis that integrates ancient geomancy with high-rise design. University campaniles at historic campuses serve as symbolic knowledge centers, their bell towers rising as vertical axes that orient academic communities toward enlightenment, much like sacred pillars in ritual spaces.
In contemporary spirituality, the axis mundi manifests in New Age practices through world tree meditations, where participants visualize a central cosmic tree to access higher realms and foster inner balance. These guided sessions, often rooted in shamanic influences adapted for modern wellness, emphasize the tree as a bridge between personal consciousness and universal energy, promoting healing and interconnectedness. Eco-spirituality further adapts this symbol by viewing ancient trees as living axes mundi, their towering forms representing resilience and the vital link between earthly ecosystems and spiritual vitality in environmental rituals. Events like Burning Man 2026, themed "Axis Mundi," invite participants to construct temporary cosmic trees in the desert, symbolizing communal connection to heaven, earth, and the underworld through art and collective experience.
Modern pilgrimages to sites like Mount Fuji in Japan continue to honor its sacred role, with annual climbs blending Shinto reverence and secular tourism to seek purification and cosmic alignment. These journeys, regulated since the Edo period but surging in popularity today, position the volcano as a sacred center where climbers ritually ascend to connect with divine energies. In civic contexts, Liberty Trees in American town squares evoke the axis mundi during public rituals, such as Revolutionary War commemorations, where the elm or pole stands as a unifying symbol of freedom and communal grounding.

In Psychology, Art, and Literature

In Jungian psychology, the axis mundi serves as a profound archetype symbolizing the Self, the unifying center of the psyche that integrates conscious and unconscious elements to achieve wholeness. Carl Jung and subsequent depth psychologists interpreted this symbol as representing the psyche's core, often depicted through images like trees or mandalas that bridge opposing realms, facilitating the individuation process. This concept is elaborated in Jung's The Archetypes and the Collective Unconscious, where the Self emerges as the regulating principle harmonizing psychic opposites.
In literature, the axis mundi motif appears in modern fantasy narratives as a connective axis between worlds, embodying renewal and cosmic order. J.R.R. Tolkien employs the White Tree of Gondor in The Lord of the Rings as such a symbol, linking the mortal realm to divine origins through its descent from the sacred Trees of Valinor and signifying hope amid decline. Similarly, C.S. Lewis's wardrobe in The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe functions as a portal, transitioning characters from the mundane world to the enchanted realm of Narnia, evoking mythological thresholds that unite disparate realities.
Artistic representations of the axis mundi in the twentieth and twenty-first centuries often evoke surreal transitions and cosmic centrality, drawing on psychological depths. Salvador Dal's surrealist works, such as The Elephants (1948), feature elongated, pillar-like elephant legs supporting obelisks, alluding to ancient symbols of vertical connection between earthly and divine spheres, inspired by Bernini's elephant-obelisk sculpture. In contemporary installations, artists like Rafael Trelles explore the motif through mythic trees as portals in his 2021 exhibition Axis Mundi at October Gallery, using layered paintings to depict interdimensional links. Likewise, Jrgen Partenheimer's Axis Mundi (1997/2014), a bronze sculpture of stacked cubes in ultramarine blue shades installed in London's Sculpture in the City in 2016, symbolizes a luminous bridge between realms, emphasizing transcendence in urban space.
