Dichloromethane
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Dichloromethane (DCM), also known as methylene chloride, is a colorless, volatile liquid organic compound with the chemical formula CHCl and a molecular weight of 84.93 g/mol. It possesses a sweet, ethereal odor and boils at approximately 40 C, rendering it useful as a low-boiling solvent in industrial applications such as paint and coating removal, metal degreasing, and extraction processes in pharmaceuticals and chemical manufacturing. First synthesized in 1840 by French chemist Henri Victor Regnault through chlorination of chloromethane, it is now primarily produced industrially via direct chlorination of methane or hydrochlorination of methanol, with global production historically reaching hundreds of thousands of tons annually, though usage has declined due to regulatory scrutiny. Despite its efficacy, dichloromethane is classified as a probable human carcinogen by agencies including the EPA and IARC, with acute exposure risks including neurotoxicity, dizziness, and cardiac sensitization from its metabolism to carbon monoxide, prompting restrictions like the 2019 EPA ban on its use in consumer paint strippers.

Properties

Physical properties

Dichloromethane is a colorless, volatile liquid with an ether-like odor detectable at concentrations as low as 250 ppm. It has a molecular formula of CHCl and a molar mass of 84.93 g/mol.
The compound melts at -97 C and boils at 40 C under standard pressure. Its density is 1.325 g/mL at 25 C, and it exhibits a refractive index of 1.42 at 20 C (589 nm). Dichloromethane has limited solubility in water, approximately 13.2 g/L at 25 C, but is miscible with most organic solvents. Its vapor pressure is 584 hPa at 25 C, corresponding to a relative vapor density of 2.93 compared to air. The dynamic viscosity is 0.42 mPas at 25 C.
These properties render dichloromethane useful as a low-boiling solvent in various applications, though its volatility necessitates careful handling to prevent inhalation exposure.

Chemical properties

Dichloromethane (CHCl) is a geminal dihalide featuring two chlorine atoms attached to the same carbon, conferring a significant dipole moment of approximately 1.60 D due to the electronegative chlorines and the resulting asymmetric electron distribution. This polarity renders it a versatile polar aprotic solvent, capable of dissolving a range of polar and nonpolar substances without participating in hydrogen bonding as a donor. Under standard ambient conditions (room temperature and pressure), dichloromethane demonstrates high chemical stability, showing minimal reactivity with water or most common organic solvents, though trace stabilizers like 2-methyl-2-butene are often added during production to prevent decomposition. 
Despite its general inertness, dichloromethane exhibits vigorous reactivity with certain substances. It reacts exothermically with alkali and alkaline earth metals such as lithium, sodium, and potassium, potentially generating heat and flammable hydrogen gas. Strong bases, including potassium tert-butoxide, can deprotonate it to form dichlorocarbene (:CCl), a reactive intermediate used in synthesis, while concentrated aqueous alkali may lead to hydrolysis yielding formaldehyde and HCl. Additionally, it is incompatible with strong oxidizers (e.g., permanganates, peroxides), which can provoke violent reactions or decomposition, and it slowly corrodes aluminum, particularly at elevated temperatures above room temperature. 
Thermal decomposition occurs above 600C, primarily yielding phosgene (COCl), hydrogen chloride (HCl), and carbon monoxide (CO), posing hazards in fire scenarios despite its nonflammable nature under normal conditions (flash point none; autoignition temperature >1000C). In the presence of moisture and light, especially with amines or under UV exposure, it may form trace hydrochloric acid, necessitating storage in airtight, light-resistant containers to maintain integrity. These properties underscore its utility in controlled chemical environments while highlighting risks from incompatible materials or extreme conditions.

Natural Occurrence

Sources and detection

Dichloromethane (CHCl) is present in trace quantities in certain natural environments, though its global emissions are overwhelmingly dominated by anthropogenic sources. Oceanic production by phytoplankton represents one identified biogenic pathway, with marine emissions contributing to atmospheric burdens. Macroalgae, wetlands, and volcanic emissions have also been cited as minor natural contributors, alongside biomass burning. Estimates place natural emissions at roughly 70,000 metric tons annually, primarily from seawater processes and combustion of organic matter, but these constitute a small fraction relative to industrial outputs exceeding hundreds of thousands of tons yearly.
Conflicting assessments exist regarding the significance of these sources; some authoritative reviews assert no substantial natural occurrence, attributing all detected environmental levels to human activity and dispersive use. This discrepancy may stem from challenges in distinguishing pristine biogenic signals from widespread contamination, as dichloromethane's volatility facilitates long-range atmospheric transport from industrial regions.
Detection in natural matrices such as seawater, air, and soils relies predominantly on chromatographic techniques for high sensitivity and specificity. Gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS) and gas chromatography with electron capture detection (GC-ECD) are standard methods, achieving detection limits as low as 0.11 ng per injection or parts-per-trillion in air samples. These approaches involve sample preconcentration via solid-phase microextraction or purge-and-trap, followed by separation and quantification, enabling monitoring of background atmospheric levels around 0.17 g/m in remote areas. Emerging biosensors, such as bioluminescent bacterial reporters, offer potential for field-deployable detection but remain less validated for routine environmental surveys compared to instrumental methods.

Synthesis and Production

Industrial methods

The primary industrial methods for producing dichloromethane are direct chlorination of methane and hydrochlorination of methanol.
In the direct chlorination process, methane reacts with chlorine gas via a free-radical mechanism at high temperatures, typically around 400500 C, to form a mixture of chloromethanes including monochloromethane, dichloromethane, chloroform, and carbon tetrachloride, along with hydrogen chloride as a byproduct. The reaction proceeds stepwise, with selectivity for dichloromethane controlled by the methane-to-chlorine ratio and reaction conditions; the product mixture is separated by fractional distillation. This method, one of the oldest commercial routes, relies on thermal initiation and is conducted in large-scale reactors to handle the exothermic radical chain reactions.
The hydrochlorination of methanol route first involves reacting methanol with hydrogen chloride (often recycled from the chlorination step) over a catalyst such as zinc chloride to produce monochloromethane and water. Monochloromethane is then chlorinated under similar free-radical conditions as the direct methane process to yield dichloromethane and regenerate hydrogen chloride. This two-step approach offers advantages in selectivity and integration with HCl recovery, reducing waste and enabling continuous operation in modern facilities. Both methods produce dichloromethane in high volumes, with global output exceeding hundreds of thousands of metric tons annually, primarily for solvent applications.

In the laboratory, dichloromethane is commonly synthesized via the reduction of chloroform (trichloromethane) using zinc dust and concentrated hydrochloric acid, a method historically employed for both preparative and early commercial production. Evolution of hydrogen gas occurs, and the dichloromethane layer separates due to its lower density and immiscibility with the aqueous phase. After reaction completion (monitored by cessation of gas evolution or distillation testing), the organic layer is separated, dried over a desiccant such as calcium chloride, and purified by fractional distillation under reduced pressure to yield dichloromethane boiling at 39.6C. This approach contrasts with industrial processes, which favor high-temperature chlorination of methane or methyl chloride to produce a mixture fractionated for dichloromethane, unsuitable for small-scale lab work due to equipment demands and explosive risks. Proper ventilation and handling are essential, as the reaction liberates toxic HCl fumes and involves volatile, carcinogenic reagents.

Applications

Industrial and commercial uses

Dichloromethane serves primarily as an industrial solvent in applications requiring effective dissolution of substances, including adhesives, paint and coating removal products, pharmaceuticals, and metal cleaning operations. It is also used for deep cleaning offset press cylinders in industrial printing operations, effectively removing inks and residues from components such as blankets and rollers, though its application is limited by toxicity concerns and regulatory restrictions. In paint stripping, it is blended into formulations to remove coatings from surfaces, with particular use in aviation for safety-critical and corrosion-sensitive components of aircraft and spacecraft, where its efficacy outweighs alternatives in specialized contexts.
In pharmaceutical manufacturing, dichloromethane functions as a process solvent for drug synthesis and extraction, leveraging its volatility and solvency for intermediates and active ingredients. It is also applied in polyurethane foam production as a blowing agent to create flexible foams used in upholstery and insulation. Additionally, in food processing, it extracts caffeine from coffee beans and tea leaves, as well as flavors from spices and hops, due to its selective solvency properties.
Commercially, dichloromethane appears in aerosol products as a solvent, flammability suppressant, and viscosity thinner, enhancing formulation stability in sprays for cleaning and degreasing. In electronics and photographic film production, it aids in cleaning and processing steps, though usage has declined with regulatory scrutiny. Its role in these sectors persists where no equally effective substitutes exist, supported by ongoing risk assessments balancing utility against health concerns.

Laboratory and specialized uses

In organic chemistry laboratories, dichloromethane serves as a versatile aprotic solvent for reactions requiring a low-boiling, non-flammable medium that dissolves a wide array of non-polar and moderately polar organic compounds, such as hydrocarbons, halides, and esters, while remaining immiscible with water to enable phase separations. Its dipole moment of 1.60 D facilitates selective solvating of electrophiles in processes like nucleophilic substitutions and carbonyl additions. Laboratories employ it in specialized syntheses, including the preparation of organometallics or as a medium for chromatography column elutions, where its volatility (boiling point 39.6 C) allows rapid evaporation post-reaction.
A primary application involves liquid-liquid extractions, where dichloromethane extracts lipophilic analytes from aqueous matrices in analytical protocols, such as isolating pesticides or pharmaceuticals from biological fluids; for example, dichloromethane-methanol blends (2:1 v/v) yield high recovery rates (>95%) for total lipids from diverse samples like algae or animal tissues, outperforming chloroform-based methods in some fatty acid profiling assays. In nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy, deuterated dichloromethane (CDCl) functions as a standard solvent for H and C spectra of air-sensitive or thermally labile compounds, providing a clean baseline with minimal proton signals (residual CHCl at 5.30 ppm) and compatibility with temperatures from -50 C to 50 C.
Specialized uses extend to instrument maintenance and calibration in research settings, such as flushing high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) systems or dissolving standards for mass spectrometry calibration, leveraging its low viscosity (0.43 cP at 20 C) and inertness toward metals. In forensic and pharmaceutical labs, it aids in the isolation of alkaloids or steroids via selective partitioning, though alternatives are increasingly adopted due to its metabolic conversion to carbon monoxide in vivo.

Chemical Reactivity

Key reactions

Dichloromethane exhibits limited reactivity toward many common reagents owing to the geminal positioning of its chlorine atoms, which sterically hinders nucleophilic substitution at the methylene carbon and precludes facile carbocation formation. This inertness permits its widespread use as an aprotic solvent in organic reactions where other halides might participate.
Hydrolysis of dichloromethane in neutral or weakly acidic water occurs via nucleophilic attack by water, displacing chloride to ultimately yield formaldehyde and hydrochloric acid (CHCl + HO HCHO + 2HCl), but the process is exceedingly slow under ambient conditions, with a minimum reported half-life of about 18 months.
Treatment with strong bases, such as potassium tert-butoxide, enables deprotonation to form the dichloromethyl anion (CHCl), which rapidly eliminates chloride ion to generate chlorocarbene (:CHCl). This -elimination pathway, pioneered by Closs and Closs in 1959, facilitates in situ cyclopropanation of alkenes to produce chlorocyclopropanes.
Soft nucleophiles like tertiary amines and phosphines can engage dichloromethane in substitution reactions. Tertiary amines form chloromethyl quaternary ammonium salts through an SN2-like Menshutkin process (RN + CHCl RNCHCl Cl), often under mild heating in dichloromethane solvent itself. Similarly, triphenylphosphine and other phosphines yield alkylated phosphonium salts, with reaction rates influenced by nucleophile basicity and sterics. Secondary amines may produce formamidines or other derivatives via sequential substitutions. These transformations underscore dichloromethane's occasional role as a C1 building block in organophosphorus and nitrogen chemistry.

Stability and handling

Dichloromethane exhibits chemical stability under recommended storage conditions, remaining largely undecomposed in closed containers at room temperature and in the absence of moisture. It is less prone to degradation than related chlorocarbons like chloroform or carbon tetrachloride under ordinary ambient conditions. Thermal decomposition occurs at high temperatures, typically above 700C, yielding hydrogen chloride, carbon monoxide, and phosgene as primary products, with reaction rates accelerating in the presence of oxygen or methane.
For safe handling, dichloromethane should be used exclusively in well-ventilated areas or fume hoods to minimize inhalation risks from its volatile vapors, which can form explosive mixtures with air under certain conditions. Personal protective equipment, including chemical-resistant gloves, safety goggles, and appropriate respirators certified for organic vapors, is required during manipulation. Skin contact must be avoided by immediate washing with soap and water if exposure occurs, and contaminated clothing should be removed promptly.
Storage necessitates tightly sealed, compatible containerssuch as glass or stainless steelin a cool, dry, well-ventilated location away from direct sunlight and heat sources to prevent pressure buildup or slow decomposition. Incompatible materials include strong oxidizing agents, alkalies, chemically active metals (e.g., aluminum, magnesium, titanium), and amines, which can lead to violent reactions or corrosion; aluminum containers must be strictly avoided. Secondary containment is advised to mitigate spill risks, and separation from foodstuffs or feedstuffs is mandatory.

Health Effects

Exposure pathways and metabolism

Dichloromethane (DCM), also known as methylene chloride, enters the human body primarily through inhalation of its vapors, which is the dominant exposure route owing to the compound's high volatility (vapor pressure of 353 mmHg at 20C) and its use in paints, adhesives, and degreasing agents. Occupational exposures can reach levels of 11,400 ppm in air, while general population intake via ambient air is estimated at 33309 g/day. Inhalation leads to rapid absorption through the alveolar membranes into the bloodstream, with 7075% of inhaled DCM absorbed during initial exposure and steady-state blood levels achieved within 24 hours at concentrations up to 300 ppm. Dermal exposure occurs via passive diffusion through the skin, particularly during prolonged contact or when vapors are trapped by clothing, though absorption rates are slower and contribute less to overall body burden compared to inhalation; no precise quantitative human data exist, but measurable uptake has been observed in volunteer studies. Ingestion represents a minor pathway, typically from contaminated water or food, with gastrointestinal absorption modeled to equate to inhalation doses such as 16 mg/kg/day from chronic 300 ppm air exposure; acute oral doses of 9,00018,000 mg/kg have caused unconsciousness within 1.5 hours in case reports.
Once absorbed, DCM distributes rapidly to lipophilic tissues including the liver, kidneys, brain, lungs, and adipose tissue due to its low molecular weight and high lipid solubility. Metabolism occurs predominantly in the liver via two parallel pathways, with the relative contribution depending on dose and individual genetic factors such as polymorphisms in CYP2E1 and GSTT1 genes. The oxidative pathway, mediated by mixed-function oxidase enzymes (primarily CYP2E1), converts DCM to carbon monoxide (CO) and carbon dioxide (CO) through an unstable formyl chloride intermediate; this route predominates at low exposure levels but saturates at airborne concentrations of 200500 ppm. The conjugative pathway, catalyzed by glutathione S-transferase theta 1-1 (GSTT1-1), forms a glutathione conjugate that yields formaldehyde (a reactive intermediate further metabolized to formate and CO); this pathway exhibits linear kinetics up to 5,000 ppm but is less active in humans than in rodents, particularly mice, contributing minimally at low doses. CO production from the oxidative pathway elevates carboxyhemoglobin levels (up to 9% after acute high exposure), impairing oxygen transport, with effects persisting for hours post-exposure. Human metabolism qualitatively mirrors that in animals, though species differences in GST activity influence metabolite profiles and toxicity thresholds.
Elimination primarily involves exhalation of unchanged DCM and metabolites, with 7075% excreted via the lungs within 68 hours following low-level inhalation (50200 ppm); urinary excretion accounts for trace amounts within 48 hours. No bioaccumulation occurs with repeated low-level oral exposure, but high-dose saturation of metabolic pathways can prolong half-lives and elevate CO burdens. Children may experience heightened vulnerability due to greater surface area-to-body weight ratios and immature detoxification systems, potentially amplifying dermal and inhalational uptake relative to adults.

Acute toxicity

Dichloromethane exerts acute toxicity primarily as a central nervous system (CNS) depressant following inhalation exposure, with symptoms manifesting within minutes to hours, including headache, dizziness, lightheadedness, drowsiness, slurred speech, decreased alertness, and slowed reaction times. Higher concentrations can induce respiratory tract irritation, noncardiogenic pulmonary edema, unconsciousness, coma, seizures, or respiratory arrest, potentially leading to death from asphyxiation or cardiac arrhythmias. This CNS depression arises from direct solvent effects, compounded by dichloromethane's metabolism via cytochrome P450 2E1 to carbon monoxide, which elevates carboxyhemoglobin levels and impairs oxygen delivery, mimicking carbon monoxide poisoning. In humans, concentrations as low as 1,000 ppm may cause lightheadedness and altered consciousness, while lethal exposures have been estimated at up to 168,000 ppm in confined spaces.
Acute oral toxicity is low, with animal LD50 values ranging from 1,410 to 3,000 mg/kg body weight, though ingestion irritates mucous membranes and may lead to gastrointestinal distress, aspiration pneumonia, or systemic absorption exacerbating CNS and hypoxic effects. Dermal exposure causes skin irritation or chemical burns upon prolonged contact with liquid, and while absorption occurs, it contributes less to systemic toxicity than inhalation unless extensive. Human case reports document fatalities from acute inhalation in occupational settings, such as paint stripping in bathtubs or glue application in enclosed areas, where inadequate ventilation allowed rapid buildup; for instance, OSHA investigations identified 74 occupational deaths from 1990 to 2014, many involving prior unrecognized overexposure symptoms. Survivors of severe acute episodes risk permanent brain or cardiac damage due to hypoxia.

Chronic toxicity and carcinogenicity

Chronic exposure to dichloromethane via inhalation or ingestion in experimental animals has been associated with hepatotoxicity, nephrotoxicity, and central nervous system effects, including behavioral changes and reduced motor activity at concentrations exceeding 500 ppm. In humans, occupational studies suggest potential neurobehavioral impairments, such as deficits in attention and visuomotor performance, following prolonged low-level exposure, though confounding factors like co-exposures limit causal attribution. Liver enzyme elevations and histopathological changes have been observed in rodents after chronic dosing, with no-observed-adverse-effect levels (NOAELs) around 50-100 mg/kg/day in oral studies. Reproductive toxicity, including reduced fertility and developmental delays, occurs in animals at high inhaled concentrations (e.g., >1,000 ppm), but human data indicate no consistent effects at occupational levels below permissible exposure limits.
Regarding carcinogenicity, the National Toxicology Program (NTP) lists dichloromethane as reasonably anticipated to be a human carcinogen, based on sufficient evidence from rodent bioassays demonstrating dose-related increases in lung and liver tumors in mice (via both inhalation and gavage routes) and mammary gland neoplasms in female rats. The International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) classifies it as Group 2B (possibly carcinogenic to humans), citing limited human evidence from occupational cohorts showing associations with brain, lung, and liver cancers, alongside strong mechanistic data involving DNA-reactive metabolites like S-(chloromethyl)glutathione. The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) deems it likely to be carcinogenic to humans by all exposure routes, primarily extrapolating from animal potency data estimating lifetime cancer risks of 2-6 x 10^{-7} per g/m for inhalation. Epidemiological studies report elevated risks for non-Hodgkin lymphoma and multiple myeloma among exposed workers, but results are inconsistent due to small sample sizes and exposure misclassification; no definitive human carcinogenicity has been established independent of animal findings.

Regulatory Status

United States regulations

The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), under the Toxic Substances Control Act (TSCA), finalized a risk management rule for methylene chloride on April 30, 2024, effective July 8, 2024, prohibiting its manufacture (including import), processing, distribution in commerce, and most commercial and industrial uses due to determinations of unreasonable risk from acute lethality, central nervous system effects, and carcinogenicity. This rule bans all consumer uses outright and extends prior restrictions on paint and coating removal products, with prohibitions on distribution for such purposes applying to manufacturers and processors immediately and to retailers by February 7, 2025; certain ongoing industrial uses, such as in pharmaceutical manufacturing and laboratory activities, are permitted subject to a Workplace Chemical Protection Program (WCPP) requiring exposure controls to maintain airborne concentrations below specified limits, along with hazard communication, recordkeeping, and downstream notification. 
The Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) addresses occupational exposures via 29 CFR 1910.1052, which sets a permissible exposure limit (PEL) of 25 parts per million (ppm) as an 8-hour time-weighted average, a short-term exposure limit (STEL) of 125 ppm for 15 minutes, and classifies methylene chloride as a potential occupational carcinogen, mandating initial and periodic exposure monitoring, engineering and administrative controls to reduce exposures below the PEL, use of personal protective equipment including respirators when necessary, employee training, medical surveillance for exposed workers, and emergency response plans. 
The Food and Drug Administration (FDA) deems any cosmetic containing methylene chloride as an ingredient adulterated and subject to seizure under 21 CFR 700.19, effectively prohibiting its use in cosmetics since 1987. In contrast, FDA permits residual methylene chloride in food from extraction processes, such as in spice oleoresins at levels not exceeding 50 parts per million (ppm) or in hop extracts at 2.2%, provided it complies with good manufacturing practices under 21 CFR 173.255 and 21 CFR 172.515. 
Under the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (CERCLA), releases of methylene chloride exceeding 1,000 pounds trigger immediate reporting to the National Response Center. It is also designated a hazardous constituent under the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA), subjecting characteristic or listed wastes containing it (e.g., F002 spent solvent wastes) to hazardous waste management requirements including generation, transportation, treatment, storage, and disposal regulations in 40 CFR Parts 260-279. For drinking water, EPA has established a maximum contaminant level (MCL) of 5 micrograms per liter (g/L) under the Safe Drinking Water Act.

International regulations and bans

Under the European Union's Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and Restriction of Chemicals (REACH) regulation, dichloromethane is restricted for use in paint stripping, prohibited from being placed on the market or used as a substance or in mixtures in any concentration after August 6, 2018. This builds on earlier phased restrictions, including a ban on supply to the general public after December 6, 2010, and on professional use after June 6, 2012, though member states may grant time-limited derogations for specific professional applications where alternatives are unavailable. The restriction targets risks from inhalation and dermal exposure during stripping activities, informed by assessments of acute neurotoxicity and potential carcinogenicity.
Dichloromethane is also prohibited in cosmetic products under EU Cosmetics Regulation (EC) No 1223/2009, listed in Annex II as a substance that must not be present in any cosmetic formulation, regardless of concentration, due to its volatility, irritancy, and classification as a possible human carcinogen (IARC Group 2B). Previously permitted in limited concentrations (up to 35% in nail products under Annex III), it was moved to the prohibited list following safety reviews by the Scientific Committee on Consumer Safety highlighting systemic toxicity risks.
No comprehensive international bans exist under global treaties; dichloromethane is not listed in Annex A, B, or C of the Stockholm Convention on Persistent Organic Pollutants, nor in Annex III of the Rotterdam Convention for prior informed consent on trade, reflecting its non-persistent nature and lack of severe bioaccumulation despite toxicity concerns. Similarly, it falls outside the Montreal Protocol's controls on ozone-depleting solvents. Restrictions vary by jurisdiction, with some countries like Sweden imposing national bans on its sale in chemical products for certain consumer applications, exceeding EU minima. In Canada, it is designated a toxic substance under environmental laws but not fully banned, subject instead to workplace exposure limits and reporting requirements. Australia regulates discharges and imports but permits industrial uses with controls.

Environmental Considerations

Fate in the environment

Dichloromethane (DCM) exhibits high volatility, with a vapor pressure of 349 mm Hg at 20C, leading it to partition predominantly to the atmosphere from surface waters and soils following release. Its Henry's law constant facilitates rapid volatilization, with estimated half-lives for evaporation from water ranging from 3 to 5.6 hours under quiescent conditions. In aquatic environments, DCM's water solubility of approximately 13 g/L at 20C allows limited dissolution, but partitioning coefficients indicate minimal sorption to sediments or soils, with a log Koc of 1.4, promoting mobility and further evasion to air rather than persistence in solid phases.
In the atmosphere, DCM degrades primarily through reaction with hydroxyl radicals, with an estimated half-life of 53 to 127 days, or approximately 4 to 6 months globally. Photolysis contributes minimally due to DCM's absorption spectrum, while direct hydrolysis is negligible in the gas phase. This oxidative pathway yields phosgene and other intermediates, ultimately forming carbon monoxide, hydrogen chloride, and water, limiting long-term atmospheric persistence.
Abiotic degradation in water proceeds slowly via hydrolysis, with a half-life of about 1.5 years at neutral pH, rendering it insignificant compared to volatilization. Biodegradation occurs under specific anaerobic conditions, such as methanogenic environments, where certain bacteria utilize DCM as a substrate, but rates are generally low in aerobic waters and soils, with complete mineralization reported only in adapted microbial consortia over extended periods. Water table fluctuations in soils can enhance anaerobic biodegradation by fostering suitable redox conditions, though overall microbial transformation remains secondary to physical removal processes.
In soils, DCM's low octanol-water partition coefficient (log Kow of 1.251.3) and weak sorption promote leaching potential in unsaturated zones, but high volatility typically results in atmospheric transfer before significant groundwater contamination. Persistence in soil is thus brief, with half-lives dominated by evaporation rather than degradation, and no substantial accumulation in biota due to rapid dissipation and metabolic clearance.

Ecological toxicity and impacts

Dichloromethane exhibits low to moderate acute toxicity to aquatic organisms, primarily through narcotic mechanisms rather than specific biochemical interactions. For fish, 96-hour LC50 values range from 13.2 mg/L in rainbow trout embryos (Oncorhynchus mykiss) to 193 mg/L in fathead minnows (Pimephales promelas) and 220502 mg/L in sheepshead minnows (Cyprinodon variegatus). Invertebrate sensitivity varies, with 48-hour LC50 values for Daphnia magna of 27 mg/L in first instar juveniles and up to 220 mg/L in adults, and 108.5 mg/L for marine grass shrimp (Palaemonetes pugio). Data on algae are limited and inadequate for robust assessment, though available evidence suggests lower sensitivity compared to fish or crustaceans. 
Chronic exposure effects are less studied but indicate potential sublethal impacts at lower concentrations. In freshwater nematodes (Panagrellus redivivus), 4-day exposure to 0.9 g/L inhibited larval moulting, and 0.849 g/L induced lethal mutations. Bioaccumulation potential is negligible, with calculated bioconcentration factors (BCF) in freshwater fish of 0.82.3, reflecting its high volatility and low lipophilicity (log Kow 1.25), which limit partitioning into tissues.
Terrestrial and sediment impacts include inhibition of soil microbial activity, with 10 g/g reducing enzymatic processes and decreasing ATP content in biomass (including fungi and aerobic bacteria) by 8085%. In freshwater sediments, a 7-day EC50 of 15.6 mg/g was reported for inhibition of CO2 production. Despite these localized effects at elevated concentrations, dichloromethane is not classified as persistently toxic or bioaccumulative under marine pollution conventions, and environmental levels from typical releases rarely exceed thresholds for ecological harm due to rapid atmospheric dispersion and hydrolysis. Ecosystem-wide disruptions are unlikely, as observed concentrations in biota (e.g., oysters and clams) remain below toxic thresholds.
