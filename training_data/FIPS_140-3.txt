FIPS 140-3
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 FIPS 140-3 is a U.S. Federal Information Processing Standard (FIPS) that establishes security requirements for cryptographic modules designed to protect sensitive information in computer and telecommunication systems used by federal agencies. Published by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) on March 22, 2019, it became effective six months after approval by the Secretary of Commerce, on September 22, 2019.
This standard supersedes FIPS 140-2 in its entirety, while FIPS 140-2 validations remain active until September 21, 2026, and aligns with international benchmarks by adopting the core requirements of ISO/IEC 19790:2012 for security requirements for cryptographic modules and ISO/IEC 24759:2017 for test requirements, with modifications specified in the NIST Special Publication (SP) 800-140 series. It defines four increasing security levels (1 through 4) to accommodate diverse applications and environments, ranging from basic module testing at Level 1 to stringent physical and environmental protections at Level 4.
The standard applies to all federal departments and agencies that rely on cryptographic-based security systems, ensuring moduleswhether hardware, software, firmware, or hybridmeet rigorous criteria across areas such as ports and interfaces, roles and services, physical security, and self-tests. Compliance is validated through the NIST Cryptographic Module Validation Program (CMVP), a joint effort with the Canadian Centre for Cyber Security, which began accepting FIPS 140-3 submissions in September 2020 and promotes the use of certified modules as a procurement metric for secure equipment. Validated modules are listed for five years, supporting the protection of sensitive but unclassified information and facilitating secure federal operations.

Overview

Purpose

FIPS 140-3 is a U.S. Federal Information Processing Standard (FIPS) published by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) on March 22, 2019, that defines security requirements for validating the cryptographic modules used to protect sensitive information in federal systems. As the successor to FIPS 140-2, it establishes a framework to ensure these modules meet rigorous criteria for safeguarding data confidentiality, integrity, and availability.
The primary goal of FIPS 140-3 is to mandate minimum security standards for cryptographic modules, thereby guaranteeing adequate protection of sensitive data through their secure design, implementation, and operation within federal environments. This standard addresses essential aspects of module security, including cryptographic algorithms for encryption and authentication, key management practices to handle cryptographic keys securely, physical security measures to prevent tampering, and requirements for the operational environment to mitigate risks from surrounding systems.
FIPS 140-3 aligns with overarching U.S. government cybersecurity policies, particularly those under the Federal Information Security Modernization Act (FISMA) of 2014, which requires federal agencies to implement validated cryptographic solutions for protecting unclassified federal information and information systems. The standard is enforced via the Cryptographic Module Validation Program (CMVP), a joint NIST-Canadian Centre for Cyber Security initiative that tests and certifies compliant modules.

Scope and Applicability

FIPS 140-3 specifies security requirements for cryptographic modules utilized within security systems that protect sensitive but unclassified information in computer and telecommunication systems. It applies to implementations of cryptographic modules, including hardware components or modules, software programs or modules, firmware modules, or any hybrid combinations thereof, provided they incorporate federally approved cryptographic algorithms. These modules must perform cryptographic operations to ensure the confidentiality, integrity, and authenticity of sensitive data processed by federal systems.
The standard is mandatory for all U.S. federal agencies employing cryptographic-based security systems to safeguard sensitive information, as required under the Information Technology Management Reform Act of 1996 (Section 5131). It targets federal agencies, as well as their contractors and vendors who supply cryptographic solutions for government use, ensuring that such modules meet validated security criteria before deployment. Private sector and commercial organizations may voluntarily adopt FIPS 140-3 for similar purposes, though it is not required outside federal contexts.
Exclusions encompass non-cryptographic modules, general-purpose computing hardware or software lacking dedicated cryptographic functions, and modules not involved in handling sensitive unclassified information. Cryptographic modules approved for classified use under separate government programs are permitted as alternatives to FIPS-validated modules for unclassified sensitive data. As of April 1, 2022, FIPS 140-3 became mandatory for new validation submissions in federal systems processing sensitive data, marking the end of acceptance for new FIPS 140-2 validations. Existing FIPS 140-2 validations will remain valid until September 22, 2026, after which they will be moved to historical status.
The requirements derive from international standards such as ISO/IEC 19790:2012 and ISO/IEC 24759:2017, with modifications provided by the NIST Special Publication (SP) 800-140 series to address specific test needs for the Cryptographic Module Validation Program (CMVP). This framework ensures consistent applicability without extending to non-security-related computing elements.

History and Development

Origins and Evolution

The development of the FIPS 140 series originated in 1991, when the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) initiated efforts to establish standardized security requirements for cryptographic modules in response to escalating digital threats and the need for consistent protection of sensitive information in federal systems. This foundational work addressed the growing reliance on cryptography for securing electronic data transmission and storage amid emerging vulnerabilities in computing environments. NIST, tasked with advancing information security standards, collaborated with government and industry stakeholders to create a framework that would ensure interoperability and reliability across cryptographic implementations. Development efforts began with public drafts in 2007 and 2009, but progressed slowly with a pause after a 2012 draft, before resuming with a 2015 request for information on adopting international standards.
The series evolved through successive versions, beginning with FIPS 140-1, published on January 11, 1994, which specified four security levels for cryptographic modules and emphasized physical and logical protections. FIPS 140-2 followed on May 25, 2001, expanding requirements for module ports, interfaces, and key management while incorporating feedback from industry implementations and alignments with evolving international standards; it underwent multiple revisions, including change notices in 2002 and subsequent updates to annexes on approved algorithms, remaining the active standard until its successor's adoption in 2019. NIST's leadership in this evolution was reinforced by the Clinger-Cohen Act of 1996, which mandated the development of federal information technology standards to promote efficient and secure IT acquisition and management.
Leading to FIPS 140-3, NIST resumed its revision process following a 2015 request for information on international standards alignment, culminating in the final publication on March 22, 2019. This version marked a significant shift by aligning closely with the international standard ISO/IEC 19790:2012 for security requirements of cryptographic modules, adopting its core structure while introducing U.S.-specific modifications through the NIST Special Publication 800-140 series to address national priorities such as enhanced testing for quantum-resistant algorithms and operational environments. These updates reflected ongoing industry input and harmonization efforts to maintain the standard's relevance in a rapidly advancing technological landscape.

Transition from FIPS 140-2

The transition to FIPS 140-3 from FIPS 140-2 was initiated with the approval of FIPS 140-3 on March 22, 2019, followed by its announcement in the Federal Register on May 1, 2019, and an effective date of September 22, 2019. FIPS 140-2 validation submissions were accepted until April 1, 2022, after which the Cryptographic Module Validation Program (CMVP) ceased processing new FIPS 140-2 certificates, though exceptions applied for modules already in progress. Legacy FIPS 140-2 validated modules remain active for use in existing systems until their individual five-year sunset dates or September 21, 2026, whichever occurs first, at which point they are moved to the historical list.
Key differences in FIPS 140-3 include its adoption of the ISO/IEC 19790:2012 structure as the core framework for security requirements, promoting international harmonization while incorporating NIST-specific modifications via Special Publication 800-140 series documents. The standard removes outdated requirements from FIPS 140-2, such as certain physical security tests for tamper resistance at higher levels that were deemed less relevant for modern modules, and streamlines testing for environmental failure protection. Additionally, FIPS 140-3 mandates enhanced self-tests for approved cryptographic algorithms, including pair-wise consistency checks for key generation and continuous random number generator health tests, to better ensure operational integrity. In 2024, NIST approved the first post-quantum cryptographic standards (FIPS 203 for ML-KEM, FIPS 204 for ML-DSA, and FIPS 205 for SLH-DSA), enabling their inclusion in FIPS 140-3 validated modules as of 2025.
NIST provides transition guidance through its Implementation Guidance (IG) documents for FIPS 140-3, which clarify testing interpretations and module compliance; for example, IG updates address transitions for specific algorithms and module types, building on prior FIPS 140-2 guidance like section 1.19 in earlier IGs for role-based authentication transitions. These documents, maintained by the CMVP, help vendors map existing FIPS 140-2 implementations to the new standard's requirements.
As of 2025, all new federal agency procurements mandate the use of FIPS 140-3 validated cryptographic modules to meet security standards under the Federal Information Security Modernization Act. To facilitate faster approvals amid growing demand, the CMVP introduced a voluntary interim validation option in May 2024 for modules submitted before January 1, 2024; these receive a two-year certificate upon review, allowing provisional use while full validation proceeds, after which the interim status can be upgraded.
The transition has presented challenges, including increased compliance costs for vendors due to updated documentation and testing needs, as well as requirements for cryptographic module testing laboratories (CMTLs) to obtain accreditation under the updated standard, which involves additional training and process alignments. These factors have extended validation timelines for some modules, prompting NIST to emphasize proactive planning in its guidance.

Security Requirements

Cryptographic Module Specifications

A cryptographic module is defined as the set of hardware, software, firmware, or any combination thereof that implements security functions, including cryptographic algorithms and key management, and is contained within a cryptographically defined boundary. This boundary encloses all components necessary to perform controlled cryptographic processing, such as encryption, decryption, and key generation.
Cryptographic modules are categorized by their implementation type. Implementation types include hardware (dedicated physical devices), software (programs or libraries), firmware (software embedded in hardware), and hybrid forms combining these elements.
The security requirements of FIPS 140-3 cover eleven areas: cryptographic module specification, module ports and interfaces, roles, services, and authentication; finite state model; physical security; operational environment; cryptographic key management; electromagnetic interference/electromagnetic compatibility; self-tests; design assurance; and mitigation of other attacks.
Core specifications for a cryptographic module encompass several key elements to ensure secure operation. Ports and interfaces define the physical and logical entry and exit points for data, control input, and status output, which must be clearly documented to prevent unauthorized access. Critical security parameters (CSPs), such as private keys and authentication data, require stringent protection mechanisms to mitigate risks of compromise. Modules must support defined roles, including the cryptographic officer role for initialization and management tasks, and the user role for operational functions, with services accessible only after role authentication where required. Operations are classified as authenticated (requiring role verification) or unauthenticated (for public services like key generation without secrets).
Physical and logical boundaries delineate the module's secure perimeter. Physical boundaries involve tamper-evident or tamper-resistant enclosures to detect or prevent unauthorized physical access, while logical boundaries in software or firmware rely on access controls and isolation techniques. Modules must also incorporate environmental failure protection to handle conditions like power fluctuations, temperature extremes, or electromagnetic interference that could lead to security breaches.
FIPS 140-3 mandates that cryptographic modules employ only NIST-approved algorithms and key lengths, as specified in NIST Special Publication 800-131A, to ensure robust security. Examples of such approved algorithms include the Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) for symmetric encryption and SHA-3 for hashing. Non-approved algorithms are permitted only in non-security functions outside the validated boundary.

Security Levels

FIPS 140-3 defines four progressive security levels that provide increasing assurances for cryptographic modules, ranging from basic functionality to protection against sophisticated threats in hostile environments. These levels apply to various module types, such as hardware, software, and hybrid implementations, with requirements building cumulatively to ensure higher levels encompass all prior specifications plus additional enhancements. The selection of a security level depends on the anticipated threat model and operational environment of the module.
Level 1 offers the lowest assurance, focusing on basic functional testing to verify that the module employs only approved cryptographic algorithms and performs them correctly. It requires production-grade equipment with no specific physical security mechanisms required. No specific authentication is required (implicit authentication may be used), suitable for controlled environments with low risk of unauthorized access.
Level 2 builds on Level 1 by introducing identity-based operator authentication to restrict access to cryptographic services and keys. Physical security is enhanced with tamper-evident protections, such as seals or coatings, to detect unauthorized attempts at module disassembly or modification. This level addresses threats from opportunistic attackers in office-like settings, requiring evidence of tampering but not active response.
Level 3 requires identity-based authentication for all roles, providing stronger operator verification through mechanisms like multi-factor methods. Physical security advances to tamper-resistant enclosures with active detection and response, including zeroizationautomatic erasure of cryptographic keys and sensitive dataupon detected breaches. This level suits environments with moderate physical threats, incorporating environmental failure protection to mitigate risks from power fluctuations or temperature extremes.
Level 4 represents the highest assurance, mandating identity-based authentication with multi-factor requirements for roles. It demands robust physical security through hardened casings and active tamper detection/response systems that zeroize data in real-time. Environmental protection is comprehensive, defending against extreme conditions like voltage surges, electromagnetic interference, or rapid temperature changes, making it appropriate for high-risk, unattended deployments.

Validation Process

Cryptographic Module Validation Program

The Cryptographic Module Validation Program (CMVP) is a joint initiative between the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) under the U.S. Department of Commerce and the Canadian Centre for Cyber Security (CCCS) under Canada's Communications Security Establishment, established on July 17, 1995, to validate cryptographic modules for conformance to Federal Information Processing Standards (FIPS). This partnership ensures a coordinated approach to cryptographic security assurance across both nations, promoting the use of rigorously tested modules in federal systems and beyond.
The CMVP administers the overall validation process for cryptographic modules, maintains the official lists of active, in-process, and historical validations, and accredits Cryptographic and Security Testing Laboratories (CSTLs) through NIST's National Voluntary Laboratory Accreditation Program (NVLAP). These accredited labs perform independent testing to verify module compliance, while the program oversees certificate issuance and revocation to uphold security integrity. As of September 2025, the CMVP has historically validated over 5,000 modules since its inception, with more than 1,000 remaining active on the list; under FIPS 140-3, individual validations are active for five years post-certification, after which modules must be revalidated or transitioned. FIPS 140-2 active modules can be used until September 21, 2026, after which they are moved to the historical list.
Structurally, the program features policy management by the joint NIST-CCCS authority, which defines operational guidelines, and technical oversight via the Derived Test Requirements (DTR) specified in NIST Special Publication (SP) 800-140 series. These include SP 800-140A for CMVP documentation requirements, SP 800-140B for security policies, SP 800-140C for vendor responsibilities, and SP 800-140D for approved cryptographic algorithms, ensuring consistent and adaptable validation criteria. The program receives annual policy revisions to incorporate advancements, such as support for quantum-resistant algorithms like ML-DSA, ML-KEM, and SLH-DSA, which have been integrated into validations as of firmware releases in 2025. Through this framework, the CMVP validates the security requirements of FIPS 140-3, providing a trusted metric for cryptographic module procurement and deployment.

Testing and Certification Procedures

The testing and certification procedures for FIPS 140-3 compliance begin with the vendor submitting comprehensive documentation on the cryptographic module, including specifications, interfaces, self-tests, zeroization methods, and protections for authentication data. An accredited Cryptographic and Security Testing (CST) laboratory then conducts conformance testing in accordance with the Derived Test Requirements (DTRs) outlined in NIST Special Publication 800-140, which adapt and extend the testing methodologies from ISO/IEC 24759. This process ensures the module meets the security requirements across physical, operational, and cryptographic aspects without relying on external inputs for pass/fail determinations.
Key testing areas include validation of algorithm implementations to confirm approved modes and security methods are correctly executed, such as through the Cryptographic Algorithm Validation Program (CAVP). Self-tests are rigorously evaluated, encompassing power-up (pre-operational) tests for software/firmware integrity, critical functions, and bypass capabilities, as well as conditional self-tests for cryptographic algorithms and pairwise consistency during key generation. Bypass checks verify that data output is inhibited during unauthorized or error states to prevent insecure operation, while key zeroization tests ensure sensitive security parameters (SSPs) and unprotected SSPs are securely erased and rendered irretrievable upon command or tamper detection.
CST laboratories performing these tests must be accredited by the National Voluntary Laboratory Accreditation Program (NVLAP) under NIST Handbook 150, which aligns with the general requirements of ISO/IEC 17025 for testing and calibration laboratories. More than 20 such NVLAP-accredited labs operate worldwide to handle FIPS 140-3 validations.
Modules may undergo modular validation, where individual components like hardware and software are tested separately, or full module testing for integrated systems; in either case, any non-approved modifications to the validated configuration will void the certification.
The overall timeline typically involves 3-6 months for lab testing, followed by 1-3 months of review by the Cryptographic Module Validation Program (CMVP) to coordinate and finalize results. In 2024, an interim validation option was introduced for modules submitted before January 1, 2024, granting provisional certification status for up to two years to address processing backlogs, with full validation required thereafter.

Compliance and Recognition

Approval Process

Following the completion of testing by an accredited Cryptographic and Security Testing (CST) laboratory, the laboratory submits a comprehensive validation report to the Cryptographic Module Validation Program (CMVP). The CMVP then conducts a thorough review phase, assigning two independent reviewersone serving as the primary point of contactto examine the report for full compliance with FIPS 140-3 requirements. If the review confirms adherence to all applicable security levels and specifications, the CMVP issues an official validation certificate.
The issued certificate details key elements of the validated module, including its name, version number, security level (ranging from 1 to 4), the originating vendor, and a unique validation number assigned by the CMVP. These certificates are publicly posted on the NIST CMVP website's validated modules list, serving as the authoritative record of compliance and enabling federal agencies to verify module suitability.
Certificates remain valid for five years from the issuance date, though interim validationstypically granted during transitional periodsare valid for only two years. Maintenance of certification requires revalidation for any significant changes, such as software updates, patches, or modifications to the module's configuration, as these alterations invalidate the prior FIPS status until new testing and approval are obtained.
FIPS 140-3 approval is mandatory for cryptographic modules used in U.S. federal government systems, ensuring adherence to national security standards for sensitive data protection. Non-compliance, including failure to revalidate after changes or during standard transitions, results in delisting from the CMVP's active validated modules list, as seen with certain FIPS 140-2 modules removed post-2022 due to unaddressed vulnerabilities or expired support.
Vendors bear ongoing responsibilities to maintain certification integrity, including providing documented evidence of secure production processessuch as tamper-evident manufacturing and supply chain controlsand robust cryptographic key management practices, like secure generation, storage, and destruction of keys within the module. These obligations are outlined in the module's non-proprietary security policy, which must be submitted and affirmed during validation to demonstrate compliance with FIPS 140-3's operational security requirements.

International Aspects

FIPS 140-3 achieves international harmonization by deriving its core security requirements from the ISO/IEC 19790:2012 standard for entity authentication and key management in cryptographic modules, along with ISO/IEC 24759:2017 for testing methodologies. In February 2025, these standards were updated to ISO/IEC 19790:2025 and ISO/IEC 24759:2025, which may influence future revisions of FIPS 140-3 to maintain alignment. This alignment enables broader global applicability, as the standard supports evaluations under international frameworks like Common Criteria (ISO/IEC 15408), which is managed in the U.S. by the National Information Assurance Partnership (NIAP) to facilitate cross-border product security assessments. By basing FIPS 140-3 on these ISO standards, it promotes consistency in cryptographic validation worldwide, reducing redundancies for vendors seeking compliance in multiple regions.
Mutual Recognition Arrangements (MRAs) extend FIPS 140-3 validations beyond U.S. borders, particularly through the joint operation of the Cryptographic Module Validation Program (CMVP) by NIST and the Canadian Centre for Cyber Security (CSEC). This partnership ensures automatic acceptance of validations by federal agencies in both countries for protecting sensitive information. Similar recognition exists with the UK and Australia, where FIPS-validated modules are accepted for government use, often alongside Common Criteria certifications under mutual agreements that treat FIPS 140 as a de facto international benchmark. These arrangements streamline cross-border validations, allowing certified modules to support secure communications and data protection in allied nations without additional testing.
The global impact of FIPS 140-3 is evident in its adoption by non-U.S. entities to enhance supply chain security, as the standard's rigorous validation of cryptographic modules helps mitigate risks in international trade and technology procurement. In the European Union, it influences standards under eIDAS 2.0, where FIPS 140-3 certified hardware, such as hardware security modules (HSMs), supports qualified trust services for electronic signatures and seals by aligning with EU requirements for secure key generation and storage. Approximately half of cryptographic suppliers in the EU market utilize NIST/FIPS standards like FIPS 140-3 to meet regulatory demands for digital identity and data protection.
