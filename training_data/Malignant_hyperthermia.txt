Malignant hyperthermia

1x
 Malignant hyperthermia (MH) is a rare, inherited pharmacogenetic disorder of skeletal muscle that presents as a life-threatening hypermetabolic response to potent volatile anesthetic gases (such as halothane, isoflurane, sevoflurane, and desflurane) and the depolarizing muscle relaxant succinylcholine. This reaction is triggered in genetically susceptible individuals, leading to uncontrolled release of calcium ions in muscle cells, which causes sustained muscle contractions, rapid elevation of body temperature (often exceeding 105F or 40.6C), tachycardia, hypercapnia, and metabolic acidosis. Without immediate intervention, MH can progress to rhabdomyolysis, disseminated intravascular coagulation, cardiac arrhythmias, renal failure, and death, with a mortality rate of approximately 3-5% even with treatment.
The condition arises primarily from mutations in the RYR1 gene, which encodes the ryanodine receptor 1 protein responsible for calcium release in skeletal muscle, accounting for the majority of cases; less commonly, mutations in the CACNA1S gene are implicated. MH susceptibility follows an autosomal dominant inheritance pattern, meaning a single copy of the mutated gene from one parent confers a 50% risk of inheritance to offspring, though up to 50% of cases may involve unidentified genetic factors or de novo mutations. It occurs in approximately 1 in 5,000 to 50,000 individuals receiving triggering anesthetics, with higher incidence in children (1 in 30,000) compared to adults (1 in 100,000), and is more prevalent in certain populations, such as those of Northern European descent. Rarely, MH-like episodes can be induced by intense physical exertion or heat exposure in susceptible individuals without anesthesia.
Early recognition is critical, with initial signs including unexplained increases in end-tidal CO, tachycardia, and muscle rigidity during or shortly after anesthesia induction. Diagnosis of susceptibility typically involves genetic testing for known mutations or the caffeine-halothane contracture test on muscle biopsy, though these are not always conclusive. Treatment centers on immediate discontinuation of all triggering agents (volatile anesthetics and succinylcholine) and halting the procedure, calling for help including the MHAUS hotline at 1-800-644-9737, rapid administration of IV dantrolene at an initial dose of 2.5 mg/kg (repeating as needed until symptoms resolve, up to 10 mg/kg total or more), hyperventilation with 100% oxygen at high flows, active cooling if core temperature exceeds 39C (such as cold IV fluids and ice packs, stopping at below 38C), and supportive care in an intensive care setting to manage complications like hyperkalemia and acidosis. Individuals with known MH susceptibility should wear medical alert bracelets and inform healthcare providers before any procedure requiring anesthesia, opting for non-triggering alternatives like propofol or regional techniques. Ongoing research focuses on improving genetic screening and preventive strategies through organizations like the Malignant Hyperthermia Association of the United States (MHAUS).

Background

Definition and Overview

Malignant hyperthermia (MH) is a rare, inherited pharmacogenetic disorder characterized by a potentially life-threatening hypermetabolic reaction in skeletal muscle, triggered by specific anesthetic agents that disrupt calcium regulation within muscle cells. This condition arises from genetic mutations that impair the normal control of calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum, leading to uncontrolled muscle contractions and metabolic derangements upon exposure to triggers. Primarily inherited in an autosomal dominant pattern with variable penetrance, MH susceptibility is most commonly associated with mutations in the RYR1 gene, which encodes the ryanodine receptor 1 protein essential for calcium handling in skeletal muscle.
The primary triggers for MH are potent volatile inhalational anesthetics, such as halothane, isoflurane, sevoflurane, and desflurane, as well as the depolarizing muscle relaxant succinylcholine. These agents provoke a rapid cascade of events in susceptible individuals, but non-triggering anesthetics, including intravenous agents like propofol, opioids, nitrous oxide, and non-depolarizing muscle relaxants (e.g., rocuronium), do not elicit the response and are considered safe alternatives. In rare cases, non-anesthetic stressors such as extreme heat or intense exercise may precipitate MH-like episodes in susceptible people, though anesthesia remains the predominant context.
Clinically, MH typically manifests with a rapid onset during or shortly after general anesthesia, featuring signs of hypermetabolism such as tachycardia, hypercapnia, and rising body temperature, which can escalate to severe complications including rhabdomyolysis, acidosis, and cardiac arrest if not promptly treated with dantrolene. The condition is potentially fatal without intervention, but early recognition and management have significantly improved outcomes. The incidence of MH episodes is estimated at approximately 1 in 5,000 to 50,000 exposures to triggering anesthetics, with higher rates observed in pediatric populations (about 1 in 30,000) compared to adults (about 1 in 100,000), reflecting the underlying prevalence of susceptibility in the general population.

Historical Development

The initial recognition of malignant hyperthermia (MH) as a distinct clinical entity occurred in 1960, when Australian physician Michael Denborough reported a family history of sudden perioperative deaths under general anesthesia, tracing back to 10 fatalities across three generations, all linked to a hereditary susceptibility triggered by anesthetic agents. This landmark case was first described in a letter to The Lancet in 1960 and detailed in the British Journal of Anaesthesia in 1962, highlighting a pattern of unexplained hyperthermic reactions during surgery, prompting further investigation into familial anesthetic risks. Earlier hints of similar events appeared in the early 20th century, including unpublished accounts of intraoperative deaths in 1915 and 1919 within a single family, characterized by rigidity and fever, though these were not systematically connected to a genetic syndrome at the time.
During the 1970s, MH gained formal recognition as a pharmacogenetic disorder through international collaboration. The first international workshop on MH, convened in Toronto in 1971, brought together clinicians and researchers to define the syndrome's clinical features, triggers like volatile anesthetics and succinylcholine, and its association with muscle abnormalities. Concurrently, studies linked MH susceptibility to underlying myopathies, notably central core disease (CCD), a congenital muscle disorder first associated with MH in reports from the early 1970s, where biopsy findings revealed core-like structures in skeletal muscle fibers of affected individuals. A pivotal therapeutic advance came in 1975, when Gordon Harrison demonstrated in susceptible swine models that dantrolene sodium, a muscle relaxant inhibiting calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum, effectively halted MH crises, laying the groundwork for its clinical adoption.
The late 1970s and 1980s saw the establishment of support structures and genetic insights. The Malignant Hyperthermia Association of the United States (MHAUS) was founded in 1981 by affected families and medical advocates to promote education, research, and crisis management resources, significantly enhancing patient safety and awareness. Genetic mapping advanced in the 1980s and 1990s, with the ryanodine receptor 1 (RYR1) gene on chromosome 19q13.1 identified as the primary locus for MH susceptibility; linkage studies in 1989 confirmed its role, followed by the discovery of causative mutations, such as Arg614Cys, in 1991 and 1993, establishing MH as a channelopathy of the skeletal muscle calcium release mechanism. By the 2010s, MH had become integrated into global anesthesia safety protocols, with organizations like the European Malignant Hyperthermia Group (EMHG) and MHAUS issuing updated guidelines for non-triggering anesthesia techniques, mandatory dantrolene stocking in operating rooms, and routine screening in high-risk populations, reducing mortality from near 80% pre-1975 to under 5%.

Etiology and Pathogenesis

Genetic Causes

Malignant hyperthermia susceptibility is primarily caused by mutations in the RYR1 gene, which encodes the ryanodine receptor 1, a calcium release channel in skeletal muscle located on chromosome 19q13.2. These mutations account for 70-80% of cases, with over 400 distinct variants identified as causative or associated with the condition.
Secondary genes contribute less frequently, including CACNA1S, which encodes the alpha-1 subunit of the dihydropyridine receptor (a voltage-gated calcium channel) on chromosome 1q32, implicated in approximately 1% of cases. Rare involvement has also been reported with STAC3, encoding a protein that interacts with the ryanodine receptor and calcium channel in excitation-contraction coupling, typically in recessive forms linked to congenital myopathies with MH susceptibility.
The disorder follows an autosomal dominant inheritance pattern, where a single mutated allele increases susceptibility, though with incomplete and variable penetrance estimated between 24% and 86% depending on the mutation and population studied. De novo mutations in RYR1 occur but are rare, representing a small fraction of cases without family history.
Genotype-phenotype correlations reveal that certain RYR1 mutations cluster in hotspots, such as the C-terminal domain (residues 4631-4991), which are associated with higher MH risk and more severe susceptibility compared to variants in other regions like the N-terminal or central domains. Incomplete penetrance is influenced by environmental modifiers, including the specific anesthetic trigger and patient factors like age or stress, leading to variable clinical expression even among carriers of the same mutation.

Pathophysiological Mechanisms

Malignant hyperthermia (MH) arises from genetic mutations that render the ryanodine receptor type 1 (RyR1), a calcium release channel in the sarcoplasmic reticulum of skeletal muscle, hypersensitive to triggering agents such as volatile anesthetics and succinylcholine. These mutations, primarily in the RYR1 gene, lower the activation threshold of the channel, resulting in excessive and uncontrolled release of calcium ions (Ca) into the myoplasm upon exposure to triggers. This aberrant calcium signaling disrupts normal excitation-contraction coupling, leading to a hypermetabolic crisis in skeletal muscle.
The surge in intracellular calcium concentration () initiates a cascade of events beginning with prolonged activation of the contractile apparatus, where elevated binds to troponin C, exposing myosin-binding sites on actin and promoting excessive actin-myosin cross-bridging. This sustained muscle contraction accelerates ATP hydrolysis by myosin ATPase, rapidly depleting cellular energy stores and causing a compensatory surge in oxygen consumption and carbon dioxide production. The inefficient cycling of calciummarked by inadequate reuptake into the sarcoplasmic reticulum due to overwhelmed SERCA pumpsfurther uncouples excitation from contraction, generating excessive heat through futile metabolic activity and potentially elevating core body temperature to 42C or higher.
Metabolically, the hypermetabolic state precipitates rhabdomyolysis through muscle fiber breakdown, releasing myoglobin into the bloodstream and causing myoglobinuria, which can lead to acute kidney injury. Concurrently, ATP depletion impairs ion homeostasis, resulting in hyperkalemia from potassium efflux and a mixed respiratory and metabolic acidosis due to lactic acid accumulation from anaerobic glycolysis and hypercapnia. These derangements collectively drive the life-threatening systemic effects of MH, emphasizing the central role of dysregulated calcium handling in skeletal muscle.

Animal Models

Animal models have been instrumental in elucidating the mechanisms of malignant hyperthermia (MH), a pharmacogenetic disorder primarily associated with mutations in the RYR1 gene encoding the skeletal muscle ryanodine receptor. These models replicate key aspects of MH susceptibility, such as exaggerated calcium release in response to triggers like volatile anesthetics and heat stress, allowing for controlled studies of pathogenesis and interventions.
The porcine model, particularly in Pietrain pigs, was the first recognized system for MH-like reactions, emerging in the 1960s as "porcine stress syndrome," characterized by sudden death during transport or anesthesia due to stress-induced hyperthermia and muscle rigidity. Early observations linked this syndrome to exposure to halothane, mimicking human MH crises, with Pietrain and related breeds showing heightened susceptibility. The seminal identification of the causative RYR1 mutation, c.1843C>T (p.R615C), equivalent to human p.R614C, confirmed its role in defective calcium handling and established the model as a cornerstone for MH research.
Mouse models, generated via targeted RYR1 knock-in mutations, provide precise genetic recapitulation of human MH variants and demonstrate variable penetrance reflective of clinical heterogeneity. The heterozygous p.G2435R knock-in mouse, corresponding to the common human p.G2434R variant, exhibits MH susceptibility upon exposure to halothane or heat, with elevated resting myoplasmic calcium levels, rapid temperature rises, and lethality that parallels human episodes. Other knock-in strains, such as p.Y524S, show similar hypermetabolic responses, while heterozygous configurations highlight incomplete penetrance, aiding studies of disease modifiers. RYR1 knockout (null) mice, or "dyspedic" models, lack skeletal muscle excitation-contraction coupling, underscoring the receptor's essential role in calcium release without anesthetic triggers.
Additional models in other species address rare mutations and broaden mechanistic insights. In dogs, a heterozygous RYR1 p.V547A mutation causes autosomal dominant MH, with susceptible animals displaying rigidity and hyperthermia upon halothane challenge, offering a large-animal parallel to human physiology. Zebrafish models, such as the ryr1b^{Y522S} mutant (mi340 strain), replicate MH-associated myopathies through impaired muscle contraction and calcium dysregulation, facilitating high-throughput drug screening for rare variants. These diverse models collectively validate RYR1's centrality in MH calcium handling defects.
These animal models have proven utility in validating MH triggers, including volatile anesthetics like halothane and depolarizing agents like succinylcholine, as well as non-pharmacological stressors such as heat, which induce consistent hypermetabolic crises across species. They have been pivotal in confirming dantrolene's efficacy as a calcium release inhibitor, reversing symptoms in porcine and murine crises, and informing its clinical use as the primary antidote. However, limitations persist, including species-specific differences in drug metabolism and calcium channel kinetics, which can alter trigger sensitivity and therapeutic responses compared to humans, necessitating cautious extrapolation.

Clinical Features

Signs and Symptoms

The clinical manifestations of malignant hyperthermia (MH) typically emerge during or shortly after exposure to triggering anesthetic agents, progressing from subtle early indicators to severe, potentially fatal systemic involvement if untreated. Early signs, often appearing within 30 to 60 minutes of trigger initiation, include masseter muscle spasm following succinylcholine administration, unexplained tachycardia with heart rates exceeding 100 beats per minute, and a progressive rise in end-tidal CO concentration above 55 mmHg despite hyperventilation efforts.
As the episode advances, progressive symptoms intensify, featuring a rapid hyperthermia with core temperature increases exceeding 1C every 5 minutes, widespread skeletal muscle rigidity beyond the masseter, mottled or patchy skin discoloration, and excessive diaphoresis due to heightened metabolic activity.
Late complications arise from unchecked hypermetabolism and include ventricular arrhythmias, profound hypotension, disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) triggered by endothelial activation and tissue damage, and acute renal failure resulting from myoglobinuria and rhabdomyolysis.
Atypical presentations can complicate recognition, such as delayed onset occurring several hours postoperatively or abortive episodes characterized by mild, self-resolving symptoms like isolated tachycardia or low-grade hyperthermia without full progression. These variations arise from the underlying dysregulation of intracellular calcium handling in skeletal muscle but do not alter the need for vigilant monitoring in at-risk individuals.

Acute Diagnosis

Acute diagnosis of malignant hyperthermia (MH) during anesthesia requires heightened clinical vigilance for early signs, including the triad of hypercapnia, tachycardia, and muscle rigidity, which signal a hypermetabolic crisis in susceptible individuals exposed to triggering agents like volatile anesthetics. Capnography is the primary monitoring tool, detecting an unexplained elevation in end-tidal CO2 concentration as the earliest and most sensitive indicator, often rising despite increased minute ventilation to maintain normocapnia. This prompt recognition is critical, as delays can escalate the crisis, with tachycardia reflecting sympathetic activation and rigidity indicating sustained muscle contraction.
Immediate laboratory tests confirm the diagnosis and guide urgency. Arterial blood gas analysis typically shows a mixed respiratory and metabolic acidosis, with pH <7.25, PaCO2 >60 mmHg with controlled ventilation, and base excess more negative than -8 mEq/L; hyperkalemia exceeding 6 mEq/L is also frequent due to muscle breakdown. Post-event evaluation includes serum creatine kinase levels, which rise above 20,000 U/L in most cases, peaking 12-24 hours after onset and signifying rhabdomyolysis. These findings, combined with clinical context, distinguish MH from mimics.
The Larach clinical grading scale, introduced in 1994 and refined in subsequent updates, provides a structured approach to assess MH probability during an adverse event. It assigns points to indicators such as persistent hypercapnia (15 points for PaCO2 >60 mmHg), muscle rigidity (15 points), rapid temperature increase to >38.8C (15 points), acidosis (10 points for pH <7.25), and elevated creatine kinase (15 points for >20,000 U/L after succinylcholine), yielding a total score that categorizes likelihood from "almost never" to "almost certain." This tool aids objective evaluation without relying solely on subjective judgment.
Differential diagnosis excludes conditions like sepsis, thyroid storm, or pheochromocytoma, which may present with similar hyperthermia and tachycardia, but MH is differentiated by its acute onset during or shortly after exposure to specific anesthetics and the absence of infection or endocrine markers. Contextual factors, such as normal preoperative status and rapid response to cessation of triggers, further support MH over these alternatives.

Susceptibility Testing

In Vitro Contracture Testing

The in vitro contracture test (IVCT), also known as the caffeine-halothane contracture test (CHCT) in North America, serves as the gold-standard diagnostic method for confirming susceptibility to malignant hyperthermia (MH) following a suspected episode. This functional assay evaluates the abnormal contractile response of skeletal muscle fibers to specific triggering agents, providing direct evidence of MH vulnerability in the ryanodine receptor 1 (RYR1)-related pathway. Performed exclusively in specialized laboratories, such as those affiliated with the European Malignant Hyperthermia Group (EMHG) or the North American Malignant Hyperthermia Registry (NAMHR), the test requires fresh muscle tissue and adheres to standardized protocols to ensure reliability.
The procedure begins with the excision of a small muscle biopsy, typically 1-2 grams, under non-triggering anesthesia to avoid precipitating an MH event; common sites include the vastus lateralis muscle in the thigh (per EMHG protocol) or the gluteus maximus in North American centers. The fresh tissue is immediately transported in a physiological solution like Krebs-Ringer buffer and must be tested within 5 hours to maintain viability. In the laboratory, thin muscle strips (approximately 15-25 mg) are dissected and mounted in an organ bath system, where they are sequentially exposed to incremental concentrations of halothane (starting at 0.11 mmol/L up to 0.44 mmol/L, equivalent to about 2% vapor) and caffeine (0.5-32 mmol/L). A sustained contracture of 0.2 g (or 2 mN) or greater at diagnostic thresholdssuch as 2 mmol/L caffeine or 0.44 mmol/L halothaneclassifies the sample as MH-susceptible (MHS), with subtypes including MHSh (halothane-sensitive), MHSc (caffeine-sensitive), or MHE (equivocal).
IVCT demonstrates high diagnostic accuracy, with a sensitivity of approximately 99% (95% CI: 94.8-100%) and specificity of 94% (95% CI: 89.2-96.5%) when applied to patients surviving fulminant MH episodes and unrelated low-risk controls, primarily detecting RYR1 variants. Testing is recommended 5-12 months after an acute MH event to allow muscle recovery and minimize false positives from residual damage, though it is suitable for individuals aged 4 years and older (preferably 10 years) without recent triggering agent exposure. The procedure carries minor risks associated with biopsy, including infection, bleeding, or pain at the site, and may require trigger-free general anesthesia in some cases, but complications are rare due to the outpatient setting.
Despite its precision, IVCT has notable limitations: it is invasive, costly (around $5,000), and available only at a limited number of certified centers worldwide (e.g., three in the United States and several in Europe), necessitating patient travel. False negatives can occur in cases of non-RYR1 MH susceptibility or atypical presentations, and the test cannot be performed on mailed samples due to the need for fresh tissue. The EMHG guidelines, last comprehensively updated in 2015, stress standardized diagnostic pathways integrating IVCT with clinical history and genetic counseling to optimize patient referral and interpretation.

Genetic Testing

Genetic testing for malignant hyperthermia (MH) susceptibility primarily involves molecular analysis of the RYR1 and CACNA1S genes, which harbor the majority of known causative variants. Targeted sequencing focuses on RYR1 hotspots, such as exons 39, 44-46, where pathogenic mutations cluster, alongside full sequencing of CACNA1S to identify variants in the dihydropyridine receptor region. Next-generation sequencing (NGS) panels enable comprehensive screening of these genes and occasionally STAC3, offering higher resolution for detecting rare variants compared to traditional Sanger sequencing.
The diagnostic yield of genetic testing identifies causative mutations in approximately 50-70% of individuals confirmed MH susceptible by in vitro contracture testing (IVCT). A negative genetic test does not exclude MH susceptibility, particularly in cases with a strong family history, as many causative variants remain undiscovered due to genetic heterogeneity.
Guidelines from the European Malignant Hyperthermia Group (EMHG) and Malignant Hyperthermia Association of the United States (MHAUS), updated through 2025, recommend initiating genetic testing with the index case (the individual with a suspected MH event) to identify a pathogenic variant before proceeding to cascade testing of at-risk relatives. Identified variants are classified using adapted American College of Medical Genetics and Genomics (ACMG) criteria, designating them as pathogenic or likely pathogenic if they meet thresholds for segregation, functional impact, and population frequency. Once a causative variant is confirmed in the index case, targeted testing for that specific variant in family members provides a definitive diagnosis if positive, avoiding invasive biopsies.
Challenges in genetic testing include the frequent identification of variants of unknown significance (VUS), which require additional functional studies or family segregation analysis for interpretation and may lead to uncertain risk stratification. As of 2025, advancements in pharmacogenomics, including broader NGS adoption and integration with electronic health records, have reduced costs and improved access, facilitating preoperative screening in high-risk populations.

Diagnostic Criteria

The diagnosis of malignant hyperthermia (MH) susceptibility relies on standardized criteria that integrate clinical, laboratory, and genetic evidence to classify episodes and identify at-risk individuals. The Larach Clinical Grading Scale, developed in 1994, provides a point-based system to assess the likelihood of an MH event during or after anesthesia, assigning scores from 0 to over 100 based on key manifestations. This scale evaluates factors such as respiratory acidosis (15 points for end-tidal CO >55 mm Hg or PCO >60 mm Hg), muscle rigidity (15 points for generalized or severe masseter rigidity), muscle breakdown (15 points for serum CK >20,000 U/L, myoglobinuria, or hyperkalemia >6 mEq/L), metabolic acidosis (10 points for base deficit >8 mEq/L or pH <7.25), temperature increase (15 points for rapid rise to >38.8C), cardiac involvement (3 points for unexplained tachycardia or arrhythmias), and other indicators like rapid reversal with dantrolene (5 points) or elevated resting CK (10 points), with family history adding 15 points if consistent with autosomal dominant inheritance. Scores are interpreted qualitatively: 0 indicates almost never (rank 1), 3-9 unlikely (rank 2), 10-34 possible (rank 3), 35-49 likely (rank 4), and 50 almost certain (rank 6, skipping rank 5 in some adaptations). A score >35 is generally considered indicative of likely MH, aiding post-event classification without requiring invasive testing.
The European Malignant Hyperthermia Group (EMHG) criteria complement the Larach scale by focusing on susceptibility diagnosis, combining family history, in vitro contracture test (IVCT) results, and genetic findings to designate individuals as MH susceptible (MHS). An individual is classified as MHS if IVCT demonstrates abnormal contracture responses to halothane (threshold 0.44 mmol/L) or caffeine (threshold 2.0 mmol/L), or if a known pathogenic RYR1 or CACNA1S mutation is identified through targeted genetic testing in families with confirmed MH history. Family history alone prompts investigation but does not confirm susceptibility without confirmatory testing; negative IVCT classifies as MH normal (MHN). These criteria emphasize a stepwise diagnostic pathway, starting with clinical suspicion and progressing to biopsy-based IVCT as the gold standard, with genetics used for predictive testing in mutation-known families.
Post-2005 refinements to the Larach scale incorporated explicit weighting for hyperthermia (>38.8C awarding 15 points) and elevated CK levels within muscle breakdown scoring, enhancing specificity for perioperative events while maintaining the original framework. By 2025, updates introduced genetic probability scoring adapted from ACMG guidelines, evaluating RYR1 and CACNA1S variants for pathogenicity using evidence like population frequency, computational predictions, and functional studies to assign likelihoods of MH causation. Overall probability assignments integrate these elements: almost certain (>97% likelihood) for high Larach scores (50) with positive IVCT or confirmed mutation; likely (75-97%) for scores of 35-49 combined with supportive family history or likely pathogenic variant; and possible (25-75%) for intermediate scores (10-34) with equivocal genetics or history alone. These thresholds guide clinical management, such as non-triggering anesthesia precautions, without overdiagnosing based on isolated features.

Management

Prevention Measures

Prevention of malignant hyperthermia (MH) primarily involves identifying susceptible individuals and implementing trigger-free anesthesia protocols. Preoperative screening is essential and includes a detailed inquiry into the patient's personal and family history of anesthesia-related complications, such as unexplained fever, muscle rigidity, or prior MH episodes. If a history suggestive of MH susceptibility is identified, patients should be referred to an MH expert or biopsy center for further evaluation, and anesthesia planning should prioritize non-triggering agents. For high-risk cases, pre-operative genetic counseling is recommended to discuss testing options and inheritance risks, given the autosomal dominant pattern with variable penetrance.
In suspected or confirmed MH-susceptible patients, total intravenous anesthesia (TIVA) is the preferred approach, utilizing agents such as propofol for induction and maintenance, opioids like fentanyl or remifentanil for analgesia, and non-depolarizing muscle relaxants such as rocuronium, while strictly avoiding volatile anesthetics and succinylcholine. Propofol-based TIVA has been demonstrated to be safe and effective in MH-susceptible individuals without triggering the syndrome.
Preparation of the anesthesia workstation is critical to eliminate residual triggering agents. Recommended methods include flushing the machine with high fresh gas flows (at least 10 L/min of 100% oxygen) for 20 to 60 minutes, disconnecting and removing vaporizers if feasible, replacing the breathing circuit with a new disposable one, and changing the CO2 absorbent to prevent rebound accumulation of volatiles. Alternatively, activated charcoal filters (e.g., Vapor-Clean) can be applied after a brief 90-second flush, reducing volatile agent concentrations to below 5 ppm within minutes and maintaining safety for up to 12 hours. Dedicated "vapor-free" machines or ICU ventilators unexposed to volatiles provide additional options in equipped facilities.
Institutional protocols enhance preparedness through standardized procedures, such as clearly labeling MH-susceptible patients in electronic medical records to alert perioperative teams during future encounters. The Malignant Hyperthermia Association of the United States (MHAUS) maintains a 24/7 emergency hotline (1-800-MH-HYPER or 1-800-644-9737) for real-time consultation on prevention and crisis management. Education for perioperative staff, including regular drills and awareness programs, is vital; a 2025 SIAARTI survey in Italy revealed significant gaps in simulation training and heterogeneous preparedness practices among anesthesiologists, underscoring the need for mandatory simulation-based education and standardized operating procedures to improve prevention.

Acute Treatment Protocols

Upon suspicion of a malignant hyperthermia (MH) episode, the priority actions for the surgical team (including anesthesia providers) in managing an acute MH crisis are:

Immediately discontinue all triggering agents (volatile anesthetics and succinylcholine) and notify the surgeon to halt the procedure as soon as possible.

Call for help (including institutional assistance and the MHAUS Hotline at 1-800-644-9737) and retrieve the MH cart/dantrolene.

Hyperventilate with 100% oxygen at high flows (e.g., 10 L/min) to flush anesthetics and reduce ETCO2.

Administer IV dantrolene rapidly at an initial dose of 2.5 mg/kg, repeating as needed until symptoms resolve (e.g., decreased ETCO2, reduced rigidity).

Initiate active cooling if core temperature >39C (e.g., cold IV fluids, ice packs), stopping at <38C.

If surgery must continue, switch to intravenous non-triggering anesthetics. Additional personnel should be summoned without delay.
Hyperventilation with 100% oxygen at high flow rates exceeding 10 L/min is essential to flush out triggering agents, correct hypercapnia, and support oxygenation; activated charcoal filters may be applied to the anesthesia circuit to adsorb residual volatiles.
For cooling, if the core temperature exceeds 39C (or is rising rapidly), multiple methods should be employed simultaneously, including ice packs to the axillae, groin, and neck, cold intravenous fluids, and gastric or peritoneal lavage with chilled solutions, while avoiding reliance on surface cooling alone due to the risk of peripheral vasoconstriction impairing heat dissipation. Cooling efforts should cease once the temperature falls below 38C to prevent hypothermia.
Additional steps include treating hyperkalemia, acidosis, and dysrhythmias. Hyperkalemia, a common and life-threatening complication, requires prompt treatment if serum potassium exceeds 5.9 mEq/L or if electrocardiographic changes are present; this includes administration of intravenous calcium chloride (10 mg/kg) or calcium gluconate (10-50 mg/kg) to stabilize cardiac membranes, followed by insulin with glucose infusion and sodium bicarbonate (1-2 mEq/kg) if metabolic acidosis is severe (pH <7.2). Dysrhythmias should be managed according to advanced cardiac life support protocols, with attention to reversible causes such as hyperkalemia and acidosis. Acidosis is further managed through aggressive hyperventilation and bicarbonate supplementation as needed, alongside liberal intravenous fluids to maintain urine output above 2 mL/kg/hour, promoting myoglobin clearance and preventing renal injury; diuretics such as mannitol may be considered if oliguria develops.
Continuous monitoring is critical throughout the acute phase, including end-tidal CO2, electrocardiography, core body temperature, arterial blood gases, serum electrolytes (particularly potassium and calcium), creatine kinase levels, urine output, and coagulation parameters to detect complications like disseminated intravascular coagulation. Once the patient is hemodynamically stable, with resolving hyperthermia, normalizing end-tidal CO2, and controlled metabolic derangements, transfer to an intensive care unit for at least 24 hours of observation is recommended.
Recent updates in MH management, as reflected in 2024 guidelines from the Malignant Hyperthermia Association of the United States (MHAUS), emphasize multidisciplinary team preparation and the integration of rapid-response tools, such as quick-dissolving formulations for supportive therapies, to enhance efficacy during crises.

Role of Dantrolene

Dantrolene serves as the primary pharmacotherapy for malignant hyperthermia (MH), acting as a postsynaptic muscle relaxant that specifically targets the hypermetabolic crisis by inhibiting calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum. It functions as an antagonist at the ryanodine receptor type 1 (RyR1) in skeletal muscle, thereby reducing the uncontrolled efflux of calcium ions into the myoplasm during an MH episode triggered by volatile anesthetics or succinylcholine. This inhibition diminishes sustained muscle contractures, ATP hydrolysis, and subsequent heat production, effectively halting the cascade of hypermetabolism. The intravenous (IV) formulations, such as Dantrium or Revonto (lyophilized powder requiring 60 mL sterile water per 250 mg vial) and Ryanodex (injectable suspension requiring only 5 mL sterile water per 250 mg vial for faster reconstitution), are preferred for acute MH treatment due to their rapid onset and reliability in emergency settings, as opposed to oral forms which are slower to act.
For acute management, the initial dose of dantrolene is 2.5 mg/kg administered as a rapid IV push, with repeat doses of 1 mg/kg every 5-10 minutes as needed until symptoms reverse, up to a total cumulative dose of 10 mg/kg, though higher amounts may be required in severe cases. For postoperative maintenance to prevent recrudescence, dosing continues at 1 mg/kg IV every 4-6 hours for at least 24-48 hours. Oral dantrolene prophylaxis, typically 4-8 mg/kg/day divided into 3-4 doses for 1-2 days preoperatively in confirmed high-risk patients, remains controversial and is generally not recommended due to risks of muscle weakness and lack of proven benefit in preventing MH onset.
Administration of IV dantrolene requires reconstitution of the lyophilized powder with sterile water without bacteriostatic agents (or minimal dilution for Ryanodex), followed by immediate use to avoid precipitation, and infusion via a large-bore IV line to ensure rapid delivery during a crisis. Patients must be monitored for potential side effects, including skeletal muscle weakness, hepatotoxicity (more common with prolonged oral use), drowsiness, and rare instances of anaphylaxis or respiratory depression.
Introduced in 1975 following demonstration of its efficacy in porcine models, dantrolene dramatically reduced MH mortality from approximately 80% in the 1960s to less than 5% in modern settings with prompt administration. Recent reviews as of 2024-2025 affirm its continued efficacy as the sole specific antidote, though challenges such as supply chain disruptions in certain regions can hinder timely access and contribute to poorer outcomes.

Outcomes and Public Health

Prognosis and Complications

Prior to the introduction of dantrolene in the 1970s, mortality from malignant hyperthermia (MH) episodes approached 70-80%, often due to rapid progression to multi-organ failure. With prompt diagnosis and treatment, contemporary mortality rates have declined to 1-5% in high-resource settings, though rates remain higher (up to 10-18%) in cases of delayed intervention, comorbidities, or limited access to care. Early availability of dantrolene and supportive measures in equipped centers further improves survival, as evidenced by North American registry data showing reduced complications with earlier dantrolene administration.
Individuals susceptible to MH face near-certain recurrenceapproaching 100%upon re-exposure to triggering agents like volatile anesthetics or succinylcholine, underscoring the need for genetic or contracture testing to confirm susceptibility. Following an episode and confirmatory testing, future anesthetics using non-triggering agents (e.g., total intravenous anesthesia) are considered safe, with no reported recurrences in properly managed cases.
Acute complications during MH episodes include cardiac arrest, which occurs in approximately 4% of reported cases and carries a high risk of death if not reversed swiftly, and renal failure due to rhabdomyolysis and myoglobinuria, affecting about 7% of patients and necessitating dialysis in approximately 15% of those with renal dysfunction. Chronic sequelae in survivors may involve persistent myopathy, such as muscle weakness or cramps, reported in up to 30% of cases, alongside psychological trauma including anxiety and post-traumatic stress, which can endure for years. Factors like peak body temperature above 41.6C and delayed dantrolene administration independently elevate complication risks by 2-3 fold.

Epidemiology

Malignant hyperthermia (MH) susceptibility in the general population is estimated to occur in approximately 1 in 3,000 to 1 in 10,000 individuals in North America and parts of Europe, as determined by in vitro contracture testing (IVCT), which assesses muscle biopsy responses to triggering agents. In contrast, prevalence appears lower in Asian populations, with estimates suggesting rates around 1 in 20,000 or less based on genetic and clinical studies in regions like Japan and Korea. Within families affected by MH, carrier rates are substantially higher; for instance, second-degree relatives such as aunts or uncles have about a 25% chance of carrying the susceptibility trait due to autosomal dominant inheritance patterns.
The incidence of clinical MH episodes during anesthesia is reported to range from 1 in 10,000 to 1 in 50,000 procedures in adults and approximately 1 in 15,000 in children, though these figures are likely underreported owing to abortive or unrecognized cases that resolve without full manifestation. Children account for over half of reported episodes, often occurring during common pediatric procedures such as ear, nose, and throat (ENT) surgeries. Demographically, MH susceptibility shows no clear sex bias overall, affecting males and females equally in most population studies, though some reports note a slight male predominance in clinical events. Certain populations exhibit elevated risk due to genetic founder effects, notably in Quebec's Abitibi-Tmiscamingue region, where historical migration patterns have concentrated MH-linked variants.
Recent trends indicate stable incidence rates globally, but mortality has declined significantlyfrom up to 80% prior to widespread dantrolene use to less than 10% in well-equipped settingsthanks to improved awareness and rapid intervention protocols. In low- and middle-income countries, however, mortality remains as high as 70% due to limited dantrolene availability and protocols, highlighting the need for global public health initiatives to enhance access. Global registries, such as the European Malignant Hyperthermia Group (EMHG) database, continue to track cases from 2023 to 2025, revealing regional variations in reporting and awareness; for example, higher recognition in Europe contrasts with underdiagnosis in parts of Asia and low-resource areas.

Special Topics

Malignant Hyperthermia in Animals

Malignant hyperthermia (MH) susceptibility occurs naturally in various animal species, most notably in pigs, dogs, and horses, where it manifests as a pharmacogenetic disorder triggered by volatile anesthetics or stress. In pigs, MH is commonly known as porcine stress syndrome (PSS), an autosomal recessive condition caused by a mutation in the RYR1 gene, leading to sudden death, muscle rigidity, and poor meat quality under stress or anesthesia. This mutation has been reported in breeds such as Landrace, with carrier frequencies up to 35% in some populations, prompting selective breeding programs to reduce its incidence. Dogs, particularly Greyhounds and Labrador Retrievers, exhibit MH episodes characterized by rapid increases in body temperature, hypercapnia, and tachycardia during anesthesia with agents like halothane. Horses experience MH rarely, primarily in Quarter Horses due to an autosomal dominant RYR1 mutation, with triggers including halogenated anesthetics and extreme exercise, resulting in hyperthermia and muscle stiffness.
Clinical signs in affected animals mirror those in humans, including hyperthermia, muscle rigidity, tachypnea, and metabolic acidosis, often induced by inhalant anesthetics such as halothane or isoflurane, or depolarizing agents like succinylcholine. In pigs, PSS episodes may also arise from transport stress or high ambient temperatures, leading to sudden collapse and elevated creatine kinase levels. Veterinary diagnosis relies on clinical presentation during anesthesia and genetic testing for RYR1 variants, with the condition confirmed postmortem in fatal cases through histopathology showing rhabdomyolysis.
Management in veterinary practice emphasizes avoidance of triggering agents, using non-depolarizing muscle relaxants and total intravenous anesthesia protocols instead of volatile inhalants. Dantrolene, a ryanodine receptor antagonist, is the cornerstone of acute treatment, administered intravenously at 25 mg/kg to halt the hypermetabolic crisis and prevent recurrence. In breeding programs, particularly for pigs, genetic screening identifies and eliminates PSS carriers to eradicate susceptible lines from commercial herds. These naturally occurring cases in animals provide valuable insights into human MH therapy, as canine RYR1 variants studied in 2024 have revealed novel missense mutations contributing to fatal episodes, enhancing cross-species understanding of pathophysiology and treatment efficacy.

Current Research Directions

Recent advancements in the genetics of malignant hyperthermia (MH) have leveraged next-generation sequencing (NGS) panels to uncover novel variants associated with susceptibility. A 2025 Japanese study employing targeted NGS on 338 individuals from 247 families identified pathogenic variants in 118 families (48.2%), primarily in RYR1 (29.8%) and CACNA1S (6.5%), alongside novel variants in genes such as STAC3, CASQ1, ATP2A1, ASPH, HRC, and TRPV1 (25.3% combined), indicating a potential polygenic basis for MH beyond traditional loci. These findings enhance risk stratification, particularly in families with positive caffeine-induced contracture tests or high clinical grading scores, where RYR1 variants predominated at 42-56%.
Gene editing technologies, including CRISPR-Cas9, are being investigated to mitigate RYR1 mutations in preclinical models. A 2024 study demonstrated that CRISPR-Cas9-mediated allele deletion of dominant RYR1 variants in cellular models restored normal calcium handling by eliminating leaky channels, providing proof-of-concept for targeted therapies in MH-susceptible individuals. Complementing this, prime editing achieved up to 59% correction of the MH-linked T4709M RYR1 mutation in patient-derived human myoblasts via RNA-delivered editors, highlighting its precision for myopathy-related disorders including MH.
Diagnostic research is shifting toward non-invasive biomarkers to improve early detection. Presentations at the 2025 European Malignant Hyperthermia Group (EMHG) meeting revealed metabolomic and lipidomic signatures in skeletal muscle from MH-susceptible individuals, featuring elevated amino acids (e.g., alanylglycine), acylcarnitines, and carnosine, which may contribute to the development of non-invasive screening methods, potentially including blood-based metabolite profiles, avoiding the need for muscle biopsy.
Therapeutic development extends beyond dantrolene with RyR1-specific agents and genetic interventions. Azumolene, a dantrolene analog, inhibits RyR1-coupled store-operated calcium entry in skeletal muscle, offering equipotent suppression of hypermetabolic responses in MH models and potential advantages in solubility and administration. Preclinical trials of gene silencing via prime editing and CRISPR approaches are advancing, with demonstrated restoration of calcium homeostasis in RYR1-mutant cells, paving the way for allele-specific therapies.
Public health efforts underscore the need for enhanced preparedness through surveys and training. The 2025 SIAARTI survey of 395 Italian anesthetists found that only 49% of centers maintain MH protocols, despite 31% having encountered cases, revealing deficiencies in pharmacogenomic screening and dantrolene availability that necessitate standardized national guidelines. A 2025 bibliometric analysis of global MH literature (1975-2024) identified pharmacogenomicsespecially RYR1 variant characterizationas a leading hotspot, alongside simulation training for multidisciplinary crisis response, with EMHG guidelines emphasizing regular drills to reduce mortality.
