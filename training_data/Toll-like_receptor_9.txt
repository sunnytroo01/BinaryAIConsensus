Toll-like receptor 9

 Toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9) is an intracellular pattern recognition receptor (PRR) belonging to the Toll-like receptor family, which is essential for innate immunity by detecting unmethylated CpG dinucleotide motifs in microbial DNA, thereby initiating immune responses against bacterial and viral pathogens. Discovered in 2000, TLR9 is predominantly expressed in immune cells such as plasmacytoid dendritic cells, macrophages, and B cells, where it resides in endolysosomal compartments to sense foreign DNA. Upon ligand binding, TLR9 undergoes proteolytic cleavage and dimerization, triggering downstream signaling that promotes the production of proinflammatory cytokines and type I interferons to orchestrate antimicrobial and antiviral defenses.
The human TLR9 protein consists of 1032 amino acids, featuring an extracellular leucine-rich repeat (LRR) domain of approximately 800 amino acids with 26 LRRs for ligand recognition, a single transmembrane helix, and an intracellular Toll/interleukin-1 receptor (TIR) domain for signal transduction. A distinctive Z-loop between LRR14 and LRR15 is crucial for its maturation, as TLR9 requires endosomal processing by proteases like cathepsins to generate a functional form capable of binding ligands. It recognizes not only stimulatory CpG-containing DNA but also inhibitory DNA (iDNA) sequences through two cooperative binding sites, with crystal structures revealing how these interactions induce conformational changes for receptor activation.
In terms of signaling, ligand-bound TLR9 recruits the adaptor protein MyD88 via TIR-TIR interactions, assembling a myddosome complex that activates kinases such as IRAK4 and IRAK1, ultimately leading to the nuclear translocation of NF-B and interferon regulatory factors (IRFs) for transcriptional regulation of genes encoding tumor necrosis factor- (TNF-), interleukin-6 (IL-6), IL-12, and interferon- (IFN-). This pathway is tightly regulated and localized to endosomes, facilitated by the chaperone protein Unc93B1, which ensures proper trafficking and prevents aberrant activation by self-DNA.
Beyond pathogen defense, TLR9 dysregulation contributes to various pathologies, including autoimmune diseases like systemic lupus erythematosuswhere self-DNA complexes activate TLR9and chronic inflammatory conditions. It is also involved in cancer, positioning it as a promising therapeutic target for immunomodulatory drugs such as CpG oligodeoxynucleotides used in vaccine adjuvants and oncology. Advances as of 2020 highlight its roles in sterile inflammation and cardiometabolic disorders, underscoring the receptor's broader influence on host physiology.

Discovery and Structure

Historical Discovery

The discovery of the Toll-like receptor (TLR) family emerged in the late 1990s, inspired by the identification of the Toll protein in Drosophila melanogaster as a critical regulator of antifungal immunity. In 1997, researchers cloned a human homolog of Drosophila Toll, later designated TLR4, which was shown to activate NF-B and induce inflammatory responses upon ligand binding, marking the entry of TLRs into mammalian innate immunity studies. This was followed in 1998 by the linkage of TLR4 mutations to lipopolysaccharide (LPS) hyporesponsiveness in mice, establishing TLRs as pattern recognition receptors for microbial components.
TLR9 was identified in 2000 as the specific receptor for unmethylated CpG motifs in bacterial and viral DNA, a breakthrough that expanded the TLR family's role in nucleic acid sensing. In seminal experiments, expression of TLR9 in human embryonic kidney cells conferred responsiveness to CpG-containing oligodeoxynucleotides (ODNs), triggering NF-B activation and cytokine production, while TLR9-deficient mice exhibited no cellular or cytokine responses to CpG DNA, confirming its essential function. Concurrently, genomic analyses revealed the gene structure of TLR9, alongside TLR7 and TLR8, highlighting evolutionary conservation across mammals and tissue-specific expression patterns that underscored its specialization in endosomal compartments.
These findings positioned TLR9 as a pivotal bridge between innate and adaptive immunity by enabling rapid detection of microbial DNA and orchestration of downstream immune activation. The broader impact of TLR discoveries, including TLR9, was recognized with the 2011 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine, divided, with one half jointly awarded to Bruce A. Beutler and Jules A. Hoffmann for their discoveries concerning the activation of innate immunity, and the other half to Ralph M. Steinman for his discovery of the dendritic cell and its role in adaptive immunity.

Molecular Structure

Toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9) is a type I transmembrane glycoprotein belonging to the Toll-like receptor family, characterized by an extracellular ectodomain, a single transmembrane helix, and an intracellular Toll/interleukin-1 receptor (TIR) domain. The ectodomain consists of 25 leucine-rich repeats (LRRs), spanning about 800 amino acids, which form a horseshoe-shaped structure responsible for ligand recognition. The transmembrane domain anchors the receptor in the endosomal membrane, while the TIR domain, comprising a central -sheet flanked by five -helices, facilitates downstream signaling interactions.
Upon ligand binding in endolysosomal compartments, TLR9 undergoes homodimerization, transitioning from a pre-formed monomeric or dimeric state to an active "m"-shaped dimeric conformation that brings the TIR domains into proximity for signaling initiation. This dimerization involves the ectodomains closing around the ligand, with the concave surfaces of the LRRs engaging specific motifs, and is enhanced by proteolytic processing. The Z-loop, a flexible region between LRR14 and LRR15, is cleaved in the endolysosome, removing 3040 amino acids to generate a mature, active form capable of efficient oligomerization.
Post-translational modifications play crucial roles in TLR9 maturation and function. N-linked glycosylation occurs at multiple sites in the ectodomain, influencing proper folding, trafficking from the endoplasmic reticulum through the Golgi to endosomes, and ligand binding specificity. Proteolytic cleavage of the Z-loop is essential for activation, as uncleaved forms exhibit reduced responsiveness.
Structural insights into TLR9 have been provided by X-ray crystallography and cryo-electron microscopy studies from the 2010s onward. The crystal structure of the unliganded ectodomain (PDB: 3WPB) reveals a monomeric horseshoe conformation, while ligand-bound forms (e.g., PDB: 3WPC) demonstrate a symmetric 2:2 dimer with CpG DNA, highlighting two distinct binding sites: one in the N-terminal region (LRRNTLRR10) and another in the C-terminal region (LRR20LRR22) that confer specificity for unmethylated CpG motifs. A 2018 crystal structure of mouse TLR9 in complex with CpG and inhibitory DNAs (PDB: 5ZLN) further revealed species-specific interactions in the binding sites. These structures elucidate how the receptor distinguishes agonistic from inhibitory DNA through conformational changes in the binding pocket.

Expression and Regulation

Cellular and Tissue Distribution

Toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9) is primarily expressed in various hematopoietic cells of the immune system, with prominent expression in plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDCs), B cells, macrophages, and monocytes. These cell types play central roles in innate immunity, where TLR9 facilitates the recognition of unmethylated CpG DNA motifs from pathogens. Lower levels of TLR9 expression are detected in non-immune cells, such as epithelial cells and fibroblasts, suggesting a broader but less dominant presence outside professional antigen-presenting cells.
In terms of tissue distribution, TLR9 exhibits high expression in lymphoid organs, including the spleen, lymph nodes, and bone marrow, which are rich in immune cells like B cells and pDCs. This pattern aligns with its role in immune surveillance. Additionally, TLR9 expression is inducible in extralymphoid tissues such as the liver, lung, and intestine during infectious or inflammatory conditions, allowing for localized responses to microbial challenges.
Species-specific variations influence TLR9 distribution; in humans, expression is largely restricted to pDCs and B cells, whereas in mice, it extends more broadly to macrophages and conventional dendritic cells. Human pDCs, in particular, display elevated TLR9 levels compared to their murine counterparts, contributing to robust type I interferon production.
Recent investigations have uncovered surface expression of TLR9 on certain immune cells, including neutrophils, B cells, and erythrocytes, challenging the conventional understanding of its exclusive endosomal localization. This surface form enables rapid extracellular sensing of DNA ligands and modulates innate immune activation in contexts like infection and anemia.

Regulatory Mechanisms

The human TLR9 gene is located on chromosome 3p21.2, spanning approximately 4.6 kb and consisting of two exons. The promoter region of TLR9 contains binding sites for transcription factors such as NF-B and interferon regulatory factors (IRFs), which mediate its transcriptional activation in response to inflammatory signals.
Transcriptional regulation of TLR9 expression is influenced by various cytokines and environmental factors. Type I interferons (IFNs), including IFN- and IFN-, upregulate TLR9 expression in B cells and dendritic cells, enhancing nucleic acid sensing during viral infections. In contrast, glucocorticoids downregulate TLR9 transcription in macrophages and other innate immune cells, suppressing excessive inflammatory activation as part of their immunosuppressive effects. Additionally, microRNAs (miRNAs) post-transcriptionally repress TLR9; for instance, miR-34a directly targets the 3' untranslated region (3'UTR) of TLR9 mRNA, reducing its expression and dampening TLR9-mediated signaling in tumor-associated macrophages.
Post-transcriptional mechanisms further control TLR9 activity. Alternative splicing of the TLR9 transcript generates isoforms, such as TLR9-A and TLR9-B in humans, which differ in their ectodomains and exhibit varying ligand-binding affinities and responsiveness to CpG DNA motifs. These variants can modulate the threshold for TLR9 activation in different cell types, influencing innate immune responses to microbial DNA. Lysosomal trafficking of TLR9, essential for its localization to endosomal compartments where it encounters ligands, is tightly regulated by the chaperone protein UNC93B1. UNC93B1 facilitates the exit of TLR9 from the endoplasmic reticulum and its delivery to lysosomes, ensuring proper maturation and signaling competence; mutations in UNC93B1 impair this process and abolish TLR9 responses.
Pathological dysregulation of TLR9 often involves altered expression levels linked to genetic and epigenetic factors. In autoimmune diseases like systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), hyperexpression (overexpression) of TLR9 in B cells and plasmacytoid dendritic cells drives aberrant self-DNA recognition, promoting autoantibody production and disease progression. In cancer, epigenetic silencing of TLR9 via promoter hypermethylation has been observed in certain tumors, such as cervical and head and neck cancers, reducing its expression and potentially contributing to immune evasion by impairing anti-tumor innate responses.

Ligands and Activation

Natural and Microbial Ligands

Toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9) primarily recognizes unmethylated cytosine-phosphate-guanine (CpG) motifs abundant in the DNA of bacteria and viruses, distinguishing them from vertebrate DNA, which is typically methylated at these sites. This recognition initiates innate immune responses against microbial pathogens.
TLR9 detects DNA from both Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria, as well as from various DNA viruses, including herpes simplex virus (HSV) and murine cytomegalovirus (MCMV). For instance, HSV-1 and HSV-2 activate TLR9 through their unmethylated CpG-containing genomes, leading to interferon-alpha production in plasmacytoid dendritic cells. Similarly, MCMV DNA engages TLR9 to promote cytokine secretion and antiviral defenses in immune cells. Additionally, malarial hemozoin, a heme polymer derived from Plasmodium falciparum, serves as a non-DNA agonist for TLR9, enhancing innate immune activation during malaria infection.
Under sterile inflammatory conditions, endogenous DNA can also ligate TLR9, contributing to sterile inflammation. Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA), which contains unmethylated CpG motifs resembling bacterial DNA, is released from damaged cells and acts as a danger-associated molecular pattern (DAMP) to stimulate TLR9. In autoimmune contexts, self-nuclear DNA complexed with autoantibodies forms immune complexes that deliver self-DNA to TLR9-expressing cells, promoting aberrant B-cell activation.
TLR9 also recognizes inhibitory DNA (iDNA) sequences through cooperative binding sites, which prevent receptor activation and dimerization.
Optimal activation of TLR9 requires specific binding arrangements, such as hexameric CpG motifs (e.g., PuPuCGPyPy), which facilitate receptor dimerization by bridging two TLR9 molecules. This process occurs in a pH-dependent manner within acidic endolysosomal compartments (pH 5.06.5), where conformational changes enable stable ligand-receptor interactions.

Synthetic Modulators

Synthetic modulators of Toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9) encompass artificially designed agonists and antagonists that either activate or inhibit receptor signaling to modulate immune responses. These compounds are primarily developed to harness or suppress TLR9's role in innate immunity, with agonists mimicking microbial DNA to stimulate protective responses and antagonists blocking excessive activation in inflammatory conditions.
The most prominent agonists are synthetic CpG oligodeoxynucleotides (ODNs), classified into A, B, and C types based on their structural features and immunostimulatory profiles. Class A ODNs, characterized by phosphodiester backbones and poly-G tails, primarily induce interferon-alpha (IFN-) production in plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDCs) while weakly activating B cells. Class B ODNs, with phosphorothioate backbones and a single CpG motif, potently stimulate B cell proliferation and cytokine secretion, such as interleukin-6 (IL-6), without significant IFN- induction. For instance, CpG-ODN 2006, a prototypical B-class agonist, drives nave B cell activation and differentiation via TLR9 engagement in endosomes. Class C ODNs combine features of A and B classes, promoting both IFN- secretion from pDCs and B cell responses. Preclinical studies demonstrate that these agonists, particularly class A and C, robustly induce IFN- in human pDCs, enhancing antiviral and antitumor immunity in mouse models.
Design principles for CpG-ODNs focus on sequence motifs optimized for species-specific TLR9 recognition, such as PuPuCGPyPy (where Pu denotes purine and Py pyrimidine), which forms the core immunostimulatory element. For human TLR9, the optimal motif is GTCGTT, while murine TLR9 prefers GACGTT, influencing agonist potency across species. To enhance endosomal targeting and bioavailability, nanoparticle delivery systems, such as poly(ethylene glycol)-b-poly(lactic acid) conjugates, encapsulate CpG-ODNs, improving uptake by immune cells and reducing off-target effects in preclinical settings.
Antagonists include immunomodulatory oligonucleotides (IMODs) and emerging small molecules that prevent CpG binding or downstream signaling. IMODs like IMO-3100, a dual TLR7/TLR9 antagonist, inhibit TLR9 activation by competing for ligand binding in endosomes, suppressing pro-inflammatory cytokine production in autoimmune models. Guanine-phosphate-guanine (GpG) motifs in ODNs act as competitive inhibitors, blocking TLR9 responses to CpG agonists. Recent structure-guided designs leverage the leucine-rich repeat (LRR) domains of the TLR9 ectodomain to develop small-molecule antagonists, such as quinazoline-based CPG-52364 and dihydropyrrolopyrimidines, which stabilize inactive receptor dimers with nanomolar potency. In 2025 studies, covalent reversible dual TLR2/TLR9 antagonists targeting the TIR domain locus demonstrated sub-micromolar inhibition of TLR9-induced inflammation without off-target activation. Preclinical data show these antagonists, including thiostrepton formulations, reduce cytokine storms and improve survival in sepsis models by dampening TLR9-mediated inflammation.

Signaling and Function

Intracellular Signaling Pathways

Upon ligand binding in endosomal compartments, Toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9) initiates intracellular signaling primarily through the MyD88-dependent pathway. TLR9's Toll/interleukin-1 receptor (TIR) domain recruits the adaptor protein MyD88, which oligomerizes to form the Myddosome complex with interleukin-1 receptor-associated kinases (IRAK) 4 and IRAK1/4. This complex activates TRAF6, an E3 ubiquitin ligase, leading to K63-linked polyubiquitination of TRAF6 and subsequent activation of the kinase TAK1. TAK1 then phosphorylates the IKK complex (comprising IKK, IKK, and NEMO), resulting in the release and nuclear translocation of NF-B subunits, which drive transcription of proinflammatory cytokines such as IL-6 and TNF-. Parallel activation of mitogen-activated protein kinases (MAPKs), including ERK1/2, p38, and JNK, promotes the formation of the transcription factor AP-1, further enhancing cytokine production.
In plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDCs), TLR9 signaling diverges to preferentially activate the interferon regulatory factor 7 (IRF7) pathway, culminating in type I interferon (IFN) production. MyD88 recruitment in pDCs assembles a complex including TRAF6, IRAK4, IRAK1, and IKK, which phosphorylates IRF7, enabling its dimerization and nuclear translocation to induce IFN- and IFN- expression. This pathway is spatially regulated, as endosomal retention of the TLR9-MyD88-IRF7 complex sustains IFN responses, distinct from the rapid degradation in other cell types. Post-translational modifications, such as S-palmitoylation, further regulate TLR9 signaling in DCs. Divergent TIR domain structures in TLR9 influence specific gene activation profiles. Signaling occurs in lysosome-related organelles marked by LAMP2.
Several negative regulators prevent excessive TLR9 activation to maintain immune homeostasis. SIGIRR acts as an inhibitory adaptor that sequesters MyD88, IRAK, and TRAF6, attenuating NF-B activation in response to TLR ligands, including those for TLR9. SOCS1, induced by TLR9 stimulation via Egr-1 and cytokines, inhibits signaling by promoting ubiquitination and degradation of the adaptor Mal/TIRAP, thereby reducing NF-B-driven cytokine production such as TNF- and IL-6. Endocytosis is essential for TLR9 signaling, with dynamin-dependent internalization trafficking the receptor to acidic endosomes where ligand-induced maturation occurs; disruption of this process impairs downstream activation.
Quantitative studies reveal dose-dependent activation of these pathways. For instance, CpG oligodeoxynucleotides (CpG-ODN), synthetic TLR9 ligands, induce NF-B activation in a dose-dependent manner at concentrations up to 5 M. High doses of CpG-ODN can stimulate tolerogenic responses. Kinetic modeling of TLR9 signaling in endosomal compartments highlights spatiotemporal dynamics, such as MyD88 oligomerization rates and IRF7 phosphorylation delays, underscoring the role of compartmental pH in pathway efficiency.

Key Functional Roles in Immunity

Toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9) plays a pivotal role in antigen presentation by enhancing the expression of major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class II molecules on dendritic cells (DCs), thereby facilitating the priming of CD4+ T cells. Upon recognition of unmethylated CpG DNA motifs, TLR9 activation in DCs promotes their maturation, leading to upregulated MHC class II surface expression and efficient peptide loading for T cell activation. Additionally, TLR9 contributes to cross-presentation of exogenous antigens on MHC class I molecules, enabling the activation of CD8+ T cells, particularly through mechanisms involving phagosomal delivery in antigen-presenting cells. This dual enhancement of MHC class I and II presentation underscores TLR9's importance in bridging innate recognition to adaptive CD4+ and CD8+ T cell responses.
Recent findings indicate that TLR9 can also be expressed on the cell surface of certain immune cells, such as neutrophils and B cells, where it senses extracellular DNA to modulate innate responses without endosomal trafficking.
In cytokine orchestration, TLR9 signaling biases immune responses toward a Th1 phenotype by inducing interleukin-12 (IL-12) production in DCs and other antigen-presenting cells. Stimulation with CpG-containing DNA ligands triggers robust IL-12p70 secretion, which drives the differentiation of naive T cells into IFN--producing Th1 effectors, essential for combating intracellular pathogens. Furthermore, TLR9-mediated activation of B cells modulates regulatory T cell (Treg) function, often by skewing B cell-Treg interactions to limit suppressive activity and promote effector responses. Co-activation of TLR9 with other receptors in B cells enhances IL-17 secretion and alters immunoglobulin modulation in Treg co-cultures, thereby fine-tuning immune balance.
TLR9 supports barrier immunity in mucosal sites such as the gut and lung epithelia by distinguishing commensal DNA from pathogenic motifs to maintain tolerance while mounting responses against invaders. In the intestinal epithelium, TLR9 engagement with gut flora-derived DNA promotes homeostasis by limiting Treg conversion and adjuvant-like effects that prevent excessive inflammation. Similarly, in lung epithelia, TLR9 expression on bronchial cells senses microbial DNA, contributing to localized tolerance to commensals while enabling rapid pathogen detection and response.
Recent insights highlight TLR9's involvement in the formation of tertiary lymphoid structures (TLS), ectopic lymphoid aggregates that sustain long-term immunity through organized antigen presentation and B cell maturation. Activation by TLR9 agonists, such as inhaled CpG oligonucleotides, induces TLS development in lung tissues, remodeling the microenvironment to support persistent T and B cell interactions for enhanced immune surveillance.

Physiological Roles

In Innate and Adaptive Immunity

Toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9) plays a pivotal role in innate immunity by recognizing unmethylated CpG motifs in viral and bacterial DNA, triggering rapid production of type I interferons (IFN-/) primarily in plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDCs). This IFN response establishes an antiviral state in infected cells and activates natural killer (NK) cells to enhance cytotoxicity against virus-infected targets, as demonstrated in murine cytomegalovirus (MCMV) infection models where TLR9 signaling coordinates cytokine secretion for NK cell-mediated viral clearance. Additionally, TLR9 activation promotes dendritic cell (DC) maturation, upregulating MHC class II and costimulatory molecules to facilitate initial pathogen containment and antigen presentation.
In bridging innate and adaptive immunity, TLR9 provides essential costimulatory signals for T cell priming through upregulation of CD80 and CD86 on DCs and B cells, enabling effective CD4+ and CD8+ T cell activation and proliferation in response to DNA-containing pathogens. For B cells, TLR9 ligation by CpG motifs directly induces proliferation, differentiation, and immunoglobulin class-switch recombination to IgG, amplifying humoral responses without requiring T cell help in some contexts.
TLR9 engages in crosstalk with other endosomal TLRs, such as TLR7, to synergize IFN- production in pDCs during viral infections, enhancing overall innate detection of nucleic acids. This property underscores TLR9's importance as a vaccine adjuvant for DNA antigens, where CpG oligodeoxynucleotides boost antigen-specific T and B cell responses, promoting Th1-biased immunity and improved pathogen clearance.
Experimental evidence from TLR9-deficient mouse models highlights its necessity for effective immune bridging, showing impaired NK cell activation, delayed viral clearance in DNA virus infections like MCMV, and diminished antibody production against T-dependent antigens.

In Immunological Memory and Heart Health

Toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9) plays a significant role in establishing immunological memory by enhancing the development and persistence of memory T and B cells. In CD8+ T cell responses, TLR9 activation through ligands like CpG oligodeoxynucleotides promotes the production of IL-12 and IFN-, which provide critical signal 3 cytokines that prolong T cell division, suppress Tcf1 expression to favor effector differentiation, and ultimately support the formation of long-lived central memory CD8+ T cells capable of robust recall responses.30136-0) This process is particularly evident in lymphoid tissues, where TLR9 sensing of microbial or self-DNA sustains germinal center (GC) reactions, increasing GC B cell numbers and facilitating affinity maturation and class switching for durable humoral memory. Furthermore, recent investigations have linked TLR9 signaling to the generation of long-lived plasma cells, as BCR-mediated uptake of TLR9 ligands drives B cell proliferation and differentiation into plasmablasts that home to survival niches in the bone marrow, ensuring sustained antibody production independent of ongoing antigenic stimulation.
In the context of heart health, TLR9 exhibits protective functions against cardiovascular stressors by modulating sterile inflammation and cellular responses in cardiac tissues. During myocardial ischemia-reperfusion injury, TLR9 sensing of released mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) can attenuate damage through non-canonical pathways that enhance cardiomyocyte energy metabolism via PI3K/Akt activation, reducing infarct size and preserving cardiac function in preclinical models. Similarly, TLR9 limits pathological cardiac hypertrophy in cardiomyocytes by regulating metabolic adaptations that counteract pressure overload-induced remodeling, as evidenced by studies showing that TLR9 deficiency exacerbates hypertrophy markers and ventricular dysfunction. In atherosclerosis, TLR9 exerts anti-atherosclerotic effects by reducing foam cell formation in macrophages and modulating endothelial inflammation; for instance, TLR9 agonists decrease lesion development in ApoE-deficient mice by skewing macrophage polarization toward an anti-inflammatory phenotype and limiting oxidized LDL uptake, thereby stabilizing plaques.
These dual roles of TLR9 highlight its context-dependent contributions to immunity and cardiac protection: in lymphoid tissues, it drives memory formation through adaptive immune enhancement, while in the heart, it controls sterile inflammation to mitigate tissue damage and promote resilience against cardiometabolic insults, as summarized in recent reviews on its endogenous ligand-mediated effects. This protective modulation underscores TLR9's potential as a therapeutic target for balancing long-term immune surveillance with cardiovascular homeostasis.

Pathological Involvement

In Autoimmune and Inflammatory Diseases

Toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9) plays a pivotal role in autoimmunity by recognizing self-DNA complexes, which trigger excessive type I interferon (IFN) production and contribute to disease pathogenesis in systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE). In SLE, immune complexes containing self-DNA activate plasmacytoid dendritic cells via TLR9, leading to IFN- storms that amplify autoantibody production and systemic inflammation. Similarly, in rheumatoid arthritis (RA), TLR9 engagement by self-DNA promotes proinflammatory cytokine release from synovial cells, exacerbating joint inflammation and tissue damage. Certain TLR9 polymorphisms have been associated with SLE susceptibility in some populations by altering receptor function and immune tolerance to self-nucleic acids.
In inflammatory conditions, TLR9 amplifies responses to microbial DNA from dysbiotic microbiota, contributing to chronic gut and skin inflammation. In inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), TLR9 signaling in response to bacterial DNA from altered gut microbiota enhances Th17 cell differentiation and mucosal inflammation, particularly in ulcerative colitis models. For psoriasis, TLR9 activation by self-DNA/LL-37 complexes promotes keratinocyte proliferation and IL-23/IL-17 axis-driven inflammation, linking dysregulated innate sensing to plaque formation. In sterile inflammation, such as gout, uric acid crystals facilitate neutrophil extracellular trap (NET) release, where associated DNA activates TLR9 to perpetuate IL-1-mediated joint inflammation.
TLR9 exhibits a dual role in multiple sclerosis (MS), where it promotes Th1 responses that may drive neuroinflammation while also limiting disease severity in experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis (EAE) models. In EAE, TLR9-deficient mice develop more severe symptoms with heightened IL-6 and IL-23 production, indicating a regulatory function that restrains pathogenic Th17 responses. Recent 2024 studies highlight TLR9's involvement in long COVID-associated inflammation, where persistent activation by circulating free mitochondrial DNA correlates with elevated IL-6 levels and prolonged systemic symptoms in post-acute sequelae of SARS-CoV-2 infection (PASC).
Clinically, TLR9 expression is elevated in the synovial fluid of arthritis patients, particularly in RA, where it is upregulated on monocyte subsets and correlates with disease activity and proinflammatory cytokine output.

In Infectious Diseases and Cancer

Toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9) plays a critical role in the innate immune response to viral infections by recognizing unmethylated CpG DNA motifs from pathogens such as hepatitis B virus (HBV), human papillomavirus (HPV), and Epstein-Barr virus (EBV). In HBV infection, TLR9 detects intracellular viral double-stranded DNA, triggering immune activation that limits viral replication and promotes clearance. Similarly, TLR9 contributes to antiviral defense against HPV by sensing viral DNA in infected cells, thereby enhancing cytokine production and immune cell recruitment to control infection and prevent progression to related cancers. For EBV, TLR9 is essential for the innate recognition of viral DNA by plasmacytoid dendritic cells (pDCs), leading to type I interferon secretion that restricts viral spread.
In bacterial infections, particularly sepsis, TLR9 exhibits context-dependent effects. During meningococcal sepsis, TLR9 signaling enhances bacterial clearance and host survival by promoting proinflammatory responses in immune cells. However, in polymicrobial sepsis models, excessive TLR9 activation drives hyperinflammation and tissue damage, contributing to higher mortality, as evidenced by improved outcomes with TLR9 inhibition. For oncogenic viruses like Merkel cell polyomavirus (MCPyV), persistent viral DNA is sensed by TLR9 to initiate immune surveillance, but viral large T antigen downregulates TLR9 expression, facilitating immune evasion and tumor development in Merkel cell carcinoma.
Specific viral mechanisms further highlight TLR9's involvement in pathogenesis. In HPV infections, the E6 and E7 oncoproteins from high-risk types abolish TLR9 expression and function by repressing its promoter activity, impairing innate antiviral signaling and promoting viral persistence. For HBV, viral particles impair TLR9-mediated interferon-alpha production in pDCs, though certain integrations may alter expression dynamics to favor chronicity. In EBV-associated lymphomas, such as Burkitt's lymphoma, TLR9 activation by CpG motifs suppresses viral lytic reactivation, limiting tumor progression, while EBV proteins like BGLF5 downregulate TLR9 to evade detection.
In cancer, TLR9 exerts a dual role, balancing antitumor immunity against protumor inflammation. Antitumor effects arise from TLR9-mediated activation of dendritic cells (DCs), which enhances antigen cross-presentation and T-cell priming; for instance, in breast cancer, TLR9-stimulated pDCs inhibit tumor cell growth via interferon-alpha, while in melanoma, TLR9 agonists promote DC maturation to elicit cytotoxic responses. Conversely, chronic TLR9 signaling in the tumor microenvironment fosters protumor effects through sustained inflammation; recent studies indicate that in triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC), dysregulated TLR9 contributes to aggressive subtypes, with low expression linked to poor survival in some cohorts.
Non-viral mechanisms amplify TLR9's protumor potential. Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA), released from damaged cells, acts as an endogenous TLR9 ligand, driving neutrophil extracellular trap formation and metastatic progression in lung cancer models by upregulating chemokines like IL-8 and CXCL1. In colorectal cancer, TLR9 hyperexpression correlates with poor tumor differentiation, increased invasion, liver metastasis, and reduced patient survival, highlighting its role in disease aggressiveness.

Therapeutic Applications

Targeting Strategies

Targeting strategies for Toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9) aim to modulate its activity to harness or suppress immune responses in disease contexts, leveraging its role in recognizing unmethylated CpG DNA motifs. Agonists activate TLR9 to boost innate and adaptive immunity, while antagonists inhibit it to mitigate excessive inflammation. These approaches exploit TLR9's endosomal localization and signaling through pathways like NF-B and IRF7, with delivery systems enhancing specificity and efficacy.
Agonist strategies primarily utilize synthetic CpG oligodeoxynucleotides (ODNs) to mimic bacterial DNA and stimulate TLR9, promoting cytokine production and antigen presentation for enhanced vaccination. For instance, CpG ODNs such as 1018 serve as adjuvants in cancer vaccines by activating plasmacytoid dendritic cells and inducing type I interferons, thereby amplifying antitumor T-cell responses. In oncology, combining CpG ODNs with immune checkpoint inhibitors like anti-PD-1 antibodies synergizes TLR9 activation with T-cell unleashing, reshaping the tumor microenvironment to favor effector immune cells over regulatory ones. This combination has been explored in preclinical models to overcome immunotherapy resistance in solid tumors.
Antagonist approaches focus on blocking TLR9 to dampen aberrant immune activation in autoimmune conditions. DNA-based inhibitors, such as immune modulatory oligonucleotides (IMOs) like IRS954 and IMO-3100, competitively bind TLR9 and prevent ligand-induced signaling, reducing interferon-alpha production in systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) models. Similarly, modified oligodeoxynucleotides with 2'-O-methylribonucleotide substitutions act as selective TLR9 antagonists, suppressing cytokine release in lupus-prone cells without activating other TLRs. For inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), antibody-based targeting, including neutralizing anti-TLR9 monoclonal antibodies, has shown potential to alleviate colitis by inhibiting microbial DNA-triggered inflammation in the gut mucosa.
Delivery innovations address TLR9's intracellular localization by facilitating endosomal uptake and reducing off-target effects. Liposomal encapsulation of CpG ODN agonists enables targeted delivery to antigen-presenting cells, enhancing vaccine potency while minimizing systemic toxicity, as demonstrated in tumor models where liposome-bound CpG increased dendritic cell maturation. Exosome-based systems offer biocompatibility for encapsulating TLR9 modulators, promoting cell-specific trafficking via natural vesicle pathways, though applications remain emerging for both agonists and antagonists. Recent advances include structure-activity relationship-optimized small molecule inhibitors like ETI41 and ETI60, which bind TLR9's Site I with nanomolar potency to block downstream signaling in autoimmune diseases such as psoriasis and SLE.
The rationale for these strategies stems from TLR9's dual-edged role: agonists amplify immunity against infections and cancer by driving proinflammatory responses, whereas antagonists curtail autoimmunity by limiting self-DNA sensing and chronic inflammation.

Clinical Developments and Challenges

As of November 2025, no therapies directly targeting Toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9) have received full regulatory approval for clinical use. However, several TLR9 agonists, such as the CpG oligodeoxynucleotide nelitolimod (formerly SD-101), remain in advanced clinical development, particularly for hematologic malignancies and solid tumors. Nelitolimod, administered via intratumoral injection, was evaluated in a phase 1/2 trial for untreated indolent non-Hodgkin lymphoma, demonstrating safety and induction of systemic immune responses when combined with low-dose radiation, with an objective response rate of 27.6%.
Clinical trials of TLR9 agonists have highlighted variable efficacy depending on the treatment regimen. Monotherapy approaches, such as intratumoral nelitolimod or other CpG-based agents, have generally underperformed in solid tumors, with objective response rates often below 25% due to limited systemic antitumor immunity. In contrast, combination therapies have yielded more promising results; for instance, a phase 2 trial of nelitolimod with pembrolizumab in anti-PD-1 treatment-nave advanced melanoma reported objective response rates of 76% (2 mg dose) and 49% (8 mg dose). Another example is the phase II trial of vidutolimod (CMP-001) with nivolumab in high-risk resectable stage III melanoma, which demonstrated a 55% major pathologic response rate (less than 10% viable tumor cells) based on 2024 data.
Despite these advances, TLR9 modulation faces significant challenges in clinical translation. Systemic administration of TLR9 agonists frequently induces toxicity, including cytokine storms characterized by elevated levels of proinflammatory cytokines like TNF- and IL-6, which can lead to severe inflammatory responses and dose-limiting adverse events. Off-target activation, particularly by microbial DNA mimics, may disrupt gut microbiota homeostasis, potentially exacerbating inflammatory conditions through unintended immune stimulation. Patient stratification remains a hurdle, as TLR9 polymorphismssuch as rs352139influence treatment responses; for example, certain variants are associated with reduced remission rates in rheumatoid arthritis patients on TNF- inhibitors, complicating trial design and efficacy prediction.
Looking ahead, future directions emphasize personalized approaches leveraging TLR9 expression levels for patient selection, as higher tumor TLR9 expression correlates with poorer prognosis in diffuse large B-cell lymphoma and may guide agonist therapy. As of November 2025, nanoparticle-encapsulated TLR9 modulators are in preclinical development for autoimmune diseases like systemic lupus erythematosus, aiming to improve targeted delivery, reduce systemic exposure, and mitigate toxicity while enhancing endosomal specificity.

Molecular Interactions

Protein-Protein Interactions

Toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9) primarily signals through direct protein-protein interactions mediated by its Toll/interleukin-1 receptor (TIR) domain, which recruits adaptor and effector proteins to initiate innate immune responses. The core adaptor MyD88 binds to the TIR domain of TLR9 via a TIR-TIR interaction upon endosomal ligand recognition, forming a signaling complex essential for NF-B and IRF7 activation. This interaction has been characterized through studies on TIR domain constructs. In certain cellular contexts, Mal (also known as TIRAP) serves as a co-adapter, facilitating MyD88 recruitment to TLR9 by bridging TIR domains, though this role is less dominant compared to plasma membrane TLRs.
For proper subcellular localization, TLR9 interacts with the chaperone protein UNC93B1, which binds to the receptor in the endoplasmic reticulum and escorts it to endolysosomal compartments where ligand sensing occurs. This interaction is critical for TLR9 maturation and positioning, as UNC93B1 mutations impair trafficking and reduce responsiveness to CpG DNA. Downstream effectors recruited via the MyD88 complex include IRAK4 and IRAK1, which bind to the death domain of MyD88 and undergo phosphorylation to activate the kinase cascade. Subsequently, TRAF6 associates with the complex to mediate K63-linked polyubiquitination of downstream targets like NEMO, enabling IB kinase activation. Additionally, CD14 functions as a coreceptor by directly interacting with TLR9 and facilitating ligand delivery, enhancing cellular uptake and responsiveness to bacterial CpG DNA.
Regulatory interactions fine-tune TLR9 activity to prevent excessive inflammation. Although TLR9 signaling is predominantly MyD88-dependent, it exhibits a minor engagement with TRIF (TIR-domain-containing adapter inducing IFN-), which can contribute to type I interferon production through cross-talk in specific infection models. SARM1 acts as a negative regulator by inhibiting adaptor recruitment and dampening TIR domain-mediated signaling, thereby limiting TLR9-driven proinflammatory responses. Recent interactome analyses, including proximity labeling and mass spectrometry approaches, have highlighted the AP-3 complex as a direct binder involved in TLR9 sorting to LAMP1-positive lysosomes, ensuring compartmentalized signaling and preventing aberrant activation. This interaction directs TLR9 to lysosome-related organelles distinct from conventional endosomes, as confirmed by AP-3 knockdown studies that disrupt lysosomal trafficking and cytokine induction.

Interaction Networks and Implications

Toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9) serves as a central hub in protein-protein interaction networks, primarily through its adaptor protein MyD88, which facilitates connections to pathways regulating inflammasome activation and autophagy. Bioinformatics analysis from the 2025 STRING database reveals a highly connected TLR9 network demonstrating strong clustering around innate immune signaling components, including MyD88 as a high-confidence direct interactor. Recent studies also indicate that TLR9 activation regulates S-palmitoylation of immune signaling proteins in dendritic cells, adding a layer of post-translational regulation to its interactome.
The TLR9-MyD88 complex primes the NLRP3 inflammasome by inducing NF-B-dependent transcription of NLRP3 and pro-IL-1, thereby linking endosomal recognition to cytosolic inflammasome assembly and IL-1 maturation. Additionally, TLR9 signaling promotes autophagy via divergent MAPK pathways, including p38/JNK activation, which enhances autophagosome formation and lysosomal degradation to modulate immune homeostasis. These connections underscore TLR9's role in balancing inflammatory and degradative processes.
In broader implications, TLR9 exhibits crosstalk with endosomal TLR7 and TLR8, where co-activation amplifies antiviral immunity through synergistic type I interferon production and dendritic cell maturation. Positive feedback loops involving interferon further amplify these networks, as TLR9-induced IFN-/ upregulates IRF7 expression, sustaining prolonged IFN responses and reinforcing antiviral signaling.
Pathologically, in cancer, the TLR9-TRAF6 axis activates STAT3 signaling, promoting tumor cell survival, stemness, and propagation, as observed in prostate and glioma models where this pathway enhances oncogenic potential. In autoimmunity, TLR9 networks are potentiated by Fc receptors, enabling immune complexes to synergize with TLR9 for heightened B cell activation and autoantibody production in diseases like systemic lupus erythematosus.
Therapeutically, network pharmacology targets shared hubs such as IRAK kinases downstream of TLR9-MyD88, with IRAK4 inhibitors disrupting signaling across multiple TLRs to mitigate excessive inflammation in autoimmune and oncologic contexts.
