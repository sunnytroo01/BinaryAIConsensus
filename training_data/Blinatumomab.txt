Blinatumomab
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Blinatumomab, sold under the brand name Blincyto, is a bispecific monoclonal antibody immunotherapy classified as a T-cell engager (BiTE) used primarily to treat CD19-positive B-cell precursor acute lymphoblastic leukemia (BCP ALL) in adults and pediatric patients aged one month and older. It works by simultaneously binding to the CD19 antigen on the surface of malignant B cells and the CD3 receptor on cytotoxic T cells, thereby redirecting the patient's T lymphocytes to recognize and destroy leukemia cells through perforin-mediated lysis and cytokine release. Administered via continuous intravenous infusion over 28-day cycles, blinatumomab has demonstrated efficacy in various stages of ALL, including relapsed or refractory disease, minimal residual disease (MRD)-positive cases, and as consolidation therapy following multi-phase chemotherapy.
Developed by Amgen Inc., blinatumomab received accelerated FDA approval in December 2014 for the treatment of Philadelphia chromosome-negative relapsed or refractory BCP ALL in adults and children, marking it as the first BiTE therapy approved for oncology. Subsequent expansions included full FDA approval in June 2023 for MRD-positive BCP ALL to reduce relapse risk, and in June 2024 for consolidation in newly diagnosed CD19-positive, Philadelphia chromosome-negative BCP ALL, based on clinical trials showing significant improvements in overall survival (OS) and relapse-free survival (RFS). For instance, in the E1910 trial, 3-year OS reached 84.8% with blinatumomab versus 69% with chemotherapy alone, while the 20120215 trial reported 5-year OS of 78.4% versus 41.4%. In the European Union, the European Medicines Agency granted conditional marketing authorization in November 2015, converted to full authorization in June 2018, for relapsed or refractory CD19-positive B-precursor ALL, with further expansion in January 2025 to include consolidation therapy for newly diagnosed Philadelphia chromosome-negative cases in adults and children aged one month and older. Blinatumomab's orphan drug designation underscores its role in addressing unmet needs in this rare, aggressive hematologic malignancy.
While highly effective, blinatumomab treatment requires careful monitoring due to potential adverse effects, including cytokine release syndrome (CRS), neurologic toxicities such as encephalopathy, and cytopenias like neutropenia and thrombocytopenia, which occur in over 20% of patients. Ongoing research explores its applications in combination therapies and other CD19-positive malignancies, building on its established mechanism to enhance T-cell-mediated antitumor activity.

Clinical uses

Indications

Blinatumomab is approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for the treatment of relapsed or refractory CD19-positive B-cell precursor acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) in adults and pediatric patients aged 1 month or older. It is also indicated for the treatment of CD19-positive B-cell precursor ALL in first or second complete remission with minimal residual disease (MRD) at levels greater than or equal to 0.1% in adults and pediatric patients. In June 2024, the FDA expanded approval to include blinatumomab as consolidation therapy for CD19-positive Philadelphia chromosome-negative B-cell precursor ALL in adults and pediatric patients aged 1 month or older following induction and consolidation chemotherapy.
Efficacy in relapsed or refractory settings was demonstrated in the phase 3 TOWER trial, a randomized study comparing blinatumomab to standard-of-care chemotherapy in adults, which showed a median overall survival of 7.7 months with blinatumomab versus 4.0 months with chemotherapy (hazard ratio 0.71, 95% CI 0.55-0.93, p=0.01). In Philadelphia chromosome-positive relapsed or refractory ALL, the phase 2 ALCANTARA trial reported a complete remission or complete remission with partial hematological recovery rate of 36% (95% CI 22-51) in adults, with 88% of responders achieving MRD negativity. For pediatric patients, the phase 3 Children's Oncology Group AALL1331 trial in first-relapse B-cell ALL showed improved 2-year disease-free survival of 61% with blinatumomab versus 50% with chemotherapy (P = .089) in low-risk patients overall, with a significant benefit in the bone marrow extramedullary relapse subgroup (73% vs 54%; P = .015).
Patient selection requires confirmation of CD19-positive disease status, as blinatumomab targets CD19 on B-cell precursors. It is not recommended for patients with active central nervous system (CNS) involvement, as clinical trials excluded those with uncontrolled CNS leukemia due to limited safety data. In frontline settings, blinatumomab is used in combination with chemotherapy for consolidation in Philadelphia chromosome-negative cases, based on trials like ECOG-ACRIN E1910, which reported 3-year overall survival of 84.8% with blinatumomab added to chemotherapy versus 69% with chemotherapy alone in adults (hazard ratio 0.42, p=0.001).

Dosage and administration

Blinatumomab is administered as a continuous intravenous infusion using a portable infusion pump, with dosing regimens varying by indication, patient age, and body weight. For the treatment of relapsed or refractory B-cell precursor acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) in adults weighing 45 kg or more, the first cycle consists of 9 mcg per day on days 1 through 7, followed by 28 mcg per day on days 8 through 28, with each cycle including a 14-day treatment-free interval after the 28-day infusion period; up to two induction cycles may be followed by up to three consolidation cycles. In pediatric patients weighing less than 45 kg for the same indication, cycle 1 dosing is 5 mcg/m per day (maximum 9 mcg per day) on days 1 through 7, then 15 mcg/m per day (maximum 28 mcg per day) on days 8 through 28, with subsequent cycles at 15 mcg/m per day (maximum 28 mcg per day) for 28 days followed by a 14-day break.
For minimal residual disease (MRD)-positive B-cell precursor ALL, adults receive 28 mcg per day continuously for 28 days in the induction cycle, followed by up to three consolidation cycles with the same dosing and a 14-day treatment-free interval between cycles. Pediatric patients for MRD-positive ALL follow the 15 mcg/m per day (maximum 28 mcg per day) regimen for 28 days per cycle, also with 14-day breaks. In consolidation therapy for first remission B-cell precursor ALL, dosing aligns with the 28 mcg per day for adults or 15 mcg/m per day for pediatrics, administered over 28 days per cycle with 14-day intervals, up to four cycles integrated with multiagent chemotherapy. Additional continued therapy cycles beyond consolidation may use the same target dose but with an extended 56-day treatment-free interval.
Preparation involves reconstituting the lyophilized powder from 38.5 mcg (delivering 35 mcg) single-use vials with 3 mL of preservative-free sterile water for injection to yield a 12.5 mcg/mL solution, which is then further diluted in a preservative-free 0.9% sodium chloride infusion bag pre-coated with 5.5 mL of intravenous solution stabilizer. The prepared solution is infused continuously over 24 hours at a constant flow rate via a dedicated line with a 0.2-micron in-line filter, using non-DEHP-containing tubing and bags; alternative 48-hour or 7-day infusion preparations are available for outpatient use with specific volume and rate adjustments. Hospitalization is recommended for the first 9 days of cycle 1 in relapsed/refractory settings (or first 3 days for MRD-positive or consolidation) and the first 2 days of cycle 2 to monitor for initial reactions.
Premedication with a corticosteroid, such as dexamethasone 20 mg intravenously for adults or 5 mg/m (maximum 20 mg) for pediatrics, is required 1 hour prior to the first dose of each cycle and before any step-up dose to reduce the risk of cytokine release syndrome. The step-up dosing in cycle 1starting at the lower rate before escalating to the target dosehelps mitigate infusion-related reactions. For toxicities, interrupt blinatumomab for grade 3 or higher events and restart at the step-up dose (9 mcg/day for adults or 5 mcg/m/day for pediatrics) once resolved to grade 1 or better, with escalation back to target dose after 7 days if tolerated; permanent discontinuation is advised for grade 4 events or unresolved grade 3 toxicities. Supportive measures include prophylactic anti-seizure medication in patients with seizure history and close monitoring of infusion site and vital signs during therapy.

Pharmacology

Mechanism of action

Blinatumomab is a recombinant single-chain bispecific monoclonal antibody engineered as a bispecific T-cell engager (BiTE), consisting of two single-chain variable fragments (scFv) connected by a flexible glycine-serine linker, with a total molecular weight of approximately 55 kDa. One scFv arm specifically binds to the CD19 antigen, a surface marker expressed on B-cell precursors and mature B cells (excluding plasma cells and stem cells), with high affinity (dissociation constant of approximately 10 M). The other scFv arm targets the CD3 subunit of the T-cell receptor complex on cytotoxic CD8 and CD4 T cells, with moderate affinity (dissociation constant of approximately 10 M). This dual binding redirects endogenous T cells to CD19-positive target cells, independent of major histocompatibility complex presentation or costimulatory signals.
Upon binding, blinatumomab brings T cells into close proximity with CD19-expressing cells, forming a cytolytic immunological synapse that mimics natural immune recognition. This interaction activates T cells, upregulating activation markers such as CD25 and CD69, and triggers their proliferation and expansion, even at low effector-to-target ratios. Activated T cells then mediate serial killing of target cells through multiple pathways: release of perforin, which forms pores in the target cell membrane, allowing granzymes to enter and activate caspases for apoptosis induction; engagement of Fas ligand (FasL) on T cells with Fas receptors on target cells, promoting extrinsic apoptosis; and secretion of proinflammatory cytokines including interferon- (IFN-), tumor necrosis factor- (TNF-), interleukin-2 (IL-2), IL-6, and IL-10. These processes enable one T cell to lyse multiple target cells sequentially, amplifying the cytotoxic response at picomolar concentrations of blinatumomab.
The specificity of blinatumomab confines its effects to CD19-positive B lineage cells, sparing other hematopoietic or non-hematopoietic cell types that lack CD19 expression. This targeted engagement results in rapid and profound depletion of CD19 B cells, with peripheral B-cell counts decreasing by 50% within 1 hour, 90% within 4 hours, and becoming undetectable within 2 days of continuous infusion initiation. Transient B-cell aplasia serves as a pharmacodynamic biomarker of blinatumomab activity, reflecting complete elimination of normal and malignant CD19 B cells, which underpins its application in treating CD19-positive acute lymphoblastic leukemia.

Pharmacokinetics

Blinatumomab is administered exclusively via continuous intravenous infusion, achieving complete bioavailability of 100%. Steady-state serum concentrations are reached within approximately 24 hours of initiation, remaining stable throughout the infusion period.
The drug exhibits a low volume of distribution, mean 5.27 L (SD 4.37) in adults (or 4.14 L/m (SD 3.32) in pediatrics), reflecting confinement primarily to the plasma compartment and limited extravascular distribution due to its large molecular weight as a bispecific antibody construct.
Blinatumomab undergoes no significant hepatic metabolism via cytochrome P450 enzymes and is instead degraded proteolytically into small peptides and amino acids through catabolic pathways involving the reticuloendothelial system and potentially T-cells. Elimination occurs primarily via this proteolysis, with negligible urinary excretion (less than 0.2%). The terminal half-life is approximately 2.1 hours (mean 2.20 hours, SD 1.34) in adults and approximately 2.1 hours (mean 2.14 hours, SD 1.44) in pediatric patients. Clearance averages 3.10 L/hour (SD 2.94) in adults (or 1.65 L/hour/m (SD 1.62) in pediatrics), independent of renal or hepatic function.
In special populations, no dosage adjustments are necessary for renal or hepatic impairment, as pharmacokinetics remain comparable to those in patients with normal function; moderate renal impairment may show slightly higher clearance, but variability precludes clinical significance. Parameters are unaffected by age, body weight (within pediatric ranges), sex, race, or acute lymphoblastic leukemia disease status. The short half-life necessitates continuous infusion to sustain effective concentrations for T-cell engagement.

Adverse effects

Common adverse effects

Blinatumomab treatment is associated with a range of common adverse effects, primarily occurring in clinical trials of patients with relapsed or refractory B-cell precursor acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL). In the pivotal TOWER trial involving adults, the most frequent adverse events (all grades, >20%) included pyrexia (61%), neutropenia (38%), anemia (32%), thrombocytopenia (29%), and headache (29%). These effects are generally manageable and often resolve with supportive care or temporary dose adjustments.
Hematologic effects are among the most prevalent, reflecting the drug's impact on bone marrow function. Febrile neutropenia occurred in 26% of patients (25% grade 3 or higher), anemia in 32% (16% grade 3 or higher), thrombocytopenia in 29% (21% grade 3 or higher), and neutropenia in 38% (35% grade 3 or higher). These typically manifest during the treatment cycles and may require dose interruptions for grade 2 or higher events, with monitoring of complete blood counts recommended.
Gastrointestinal effects include nausea (18%, 1% grade 3 or higher), constipation (18%), and diarrhea (18%, 1% grade 3 or higher), often linked to the infusion process. Symptomatic management with antiemetics or laxatives is usually sufficient, and premedication with dexamethasone as per dosing guidelines can help mitigate nausea and related symptoms.
General effects encompass pyrexia (61%, 6% grade 3 or higher), fatigue (20%, 3% grade 3 or higher), chills (17%), and peripheral edema (12%, 1% grade 3 or higher). Headache was also common (29%, 6% grade 3 or higher). These infusion-related reactions predominate in the first week of treatment and are addressed through antipyretics, hydration, and supportive measures, with most resolving without long-term sequelae.
In pediatric patients, the adverse effect profile is similar to adults, but with potentially higher rates of cytokine release syndrome (up to 20%) and infections due to underlying disease and immune status. Data from trials such as 20120215 show common events like pyrexia (over 80%), anemia, and thrombocytopenia at rates comparable to adults.

Serious adverse effects

Blinatumomab treatment is associated with cytokine release syndrome (CRS), a potentially life-threatening condition resulting from T-cell activation and subsequent cytokine release upon engagement of CD3-positive T cells with CD19-positive B cells. In clinical trials for relapsed or refractory B-cell precursor acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL), CRS occurred in 15% of patients, with grade 3 or higher events in approximately 5%. Symptoms typically include fever, hypotension, hypoxia, headache, nausea, and elevated transaminases, with median onset on day 2 of infusion and resolution within 5 days following intervention. CRS is graded according to American Society for Transplantation and Cellular Therapy (ASTCT) criteria, with management involving interruption of therapy for grade 3 events and permanent discontinuation for grade 4; supportive care includes tocilizumab for interleukin-6 blockade or corticosteroids such as dexamethasone (8 mg every 8 hours for grade 3). In the TOWER trial, CRS incidence was 14% (3% grade 3). Recent data from consolidation settings (e.g., E1910 trial) report CRS in 16%.
Neurologic toxicities represent another serious risk, potentially encompassing immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS) and manifesting as encephalopathy, seizures, tremor, confusion, or word-finding difficulties. These events occurred in approximately 65% of patients across clinical trials, with grade 3 or higher severity in about 13%, and a median onset within the first 2 weeks of treatment. Risk factors include high disease burden and prior neurologic conditions, with monitoring recommended via clinical assessment and electroencephalogram (EEG) if seizures are suspected. Management entails interrupting blinatumomab for grade 3 events, permanent discontinuation for grade 4 or recurrent seizures, and supportive measures such as corticosteroids or anticonvulsants. In the phase 3 TOWER trial, neurologic events of grade 3 or higher affected 9% of blinatumomab-treated patients.
Infections are heightened due to B-cell aplasia and associated neutropenia, with serious infections reported in 25% of patients in clinical trials, including sepsis (incidence around 20%), pneumonia, and opportunistic fungal infections. Prophylaxis with antimicrobial agents, such as trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole for Pneumocystis jirovecii and acyclovir for herpesviruses, is recommended, alongside vigilant monitoring of neutrophil counts. In the TOWER trial, infections occurred in 28% of patients, with grade 3 or higher events in 34%.
Other serious adverse effects include tumor lysis syndrome (TLS), observed in 1% of patients in the TOWER trial (all grade 3), which can lead to acute renal failure, cardiac arrhythmias, or seizures due to rapid tumor cell destruction; preventive measures such as hydration and uric acid-lowering therapy (e.g., allopurinol) are essential, particularly in patients with high tumor burden. Prolonged neutropenia, often grade 3 or higher, persists beyond the expected recovery period and requires laboratory monitoring with potential treatment interruption. Overall, serious adverse reactions occurred in 79% of patients in the TOWER trial.
Risk mitigation strategies for these effects include a step-up dosing regimen starting at 9 mcg/day for the first 7 days of cycle 1 to reduce CRS incidence, followed by 28 mcg/day, with mandatory hospitalization during the first 9 days of cycle 1 and the first 2 days of cycle 2 for relapsed/refractory ALL patients. Premedication with dexamethasone (e.g., 20 mg 1 hour prior to infusion) and close monitoring for early signs of toxicity are advised, with permanent discontinuation required for grade 4 CRS, neurologic events, or other life-threatening reactions.

History

Development

Blinatumomab, originally designated MT103, was developed by the German biotechnology company Micromet AG in the late 1990s to early 2000s as the inaugural bispecific T-cell engager (BiTE) antibody construct targeting CD19 on B cells and CD3 on T cells. This innovation built upon foundational research into bispecific antibodies conducted in the 1980s, which explored their potential to redirect cytotoxic T cells against tumor targets. Micromet's BiTE platform, pioneered by researchers including Peter Baeuerle and Patrick Kufer, aimed to create small, engineered antibody fragments capable of transiently linking T cells to malignant B cells without requiring Fc receptor engagement or additional costimulatory signals.
Preclinical studies validated the BiTE concept for blinatumomab through in vitro co-culture assays, where it induced potent, antigen-specific lysis of CD19-positive tumor cells by unstimulated human T cells at picomolar concentrations, demonstrating selective cytotoxicity without off-target effects on CD19-negative cells. In vivo, mouse xenograft models of human B-cell malignancies, including acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) and non-Hodgkin lymphoma (NHL), showed that blinatumomab mediated tumor regression via T-cell infiltration and B-cell depletion, establishing proof-of-concept for its efficacy in these indications. These findings highlighted the molecule's ability to activate polyclonal T-cell responses, paving the way for clinical translation.
Early clinical development began with phase 1 trials in the 2000s, first in relapsed or refractory NHL. In a dose-escalation study of 38 patients, continuous intravenous infusion of blinatumomab established a maximum tolerated dose (MTD) of 60 g/m/day, with rapid and profound B-cell depletion observed across doses; notably, complete responses occurred in 4 of 6 heavily pretreated patients at the lowest effective dose of 0.015 mg/m/day, indicating activity in refractory disease. A subsequent phase 1 trial in relapsed or refractory B-precursor ALL enrolled 21 patients, confirming safety and pharmacokinetics similar to NHL, with an MTD of 60 g/m/day after step-up dosing; key observations included sustained T-cell activation, B-cell aplasia, and antileukemic responses in minimal residual disease-positive cases.
In January 2012, Amgen acquired Micromet for approximately $1.16 billion, integrating blinatumomab into its oncology pipeline and providing resources to advance later-stage trials. This transaction accelerated the drug's progression from early proof-of-concept to broader evaluation in B-cell malignancies.

Regulatory approvals

Blinatumomab received accelerated approval from the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) on December 3, 2014, for the treatment of adults with relapsed or refractory Philadelphia chromosome-negative B-cell precursor acute lymphoblastic leukemia (B-ALL). This initial approval was based on results from a single-arm phase 2 trial demonstrating complete remission rates. In July 2017, the FDA converted this to full approval and expanded the indication to include pediatric patients aged 1 month and older with the same condition, supported by data from the phase 3 TOWER trial showing improved overall survival compared to standard chemotherapy. On March 29, 2018, the FDA granted accelerated approval for blinatumomab in adults and children with minimal residual disease (MRD)-positive B-cell precursor ALL following frontline or relapsed chemotherapy, based on a phase 2 single-arm study evaluating MRD response and relapse-free survival. This accelerated approval was converted to full approval on June 21, 2023. In June 2024, the FDA further expanded approval to include blinatumomab as consolidation therapy for adult and pediatric patients aged 1 month and older with CD19-positive, Philadelphia chromosome-negative B-ALL in first or second complete remission, primarily supported by the phase 3 E1910 trial demonstrating superior disease-free survival when added to multi-agent chemotherapy.
In the European Union, the European Medicines Agency (EMA) granted conditional marketing authorization for blinatumomab on November 23, 2015, for adults with relapsed or refractory Philadelphia chromosome-negative CD19-positive B-precursor ALL. This was converted to full marketing authorization on June 18, 2018, and the indication was expanded on August 29, 2018, to include pediatric patients aged 1 year and older with the same relapsed or refractory condition. The EMA approved blinatumomab for MRD-positive B-precursor ALL in adults on January 22, 2019, as monotherapy for the treatment of adults with minimal residual disease-positive B-precursor ALL, based on the same phase 2 trial data used for the FDA indication. On January 29, 2025, the European Commission approved blinatumomab for use in the consolidation phase of treatment for adult patients with Philadelphia chromosome-negative CD19-positive B-precursor ALL in first complete remission, drawing from the E1910 trial results.
Blinatumomab also received regulatory approvals in other regions, including conditional approval by Health Canada on January 12, 2016, for adults with relapsed or refractory B-precursor ALL, later expanded to pediatric patients. In Australia, the Therapeutic Goods Administration approved blinatumomab on November 9, 2015, initially for adults with the relapsed or refractory indication.
Following the initial FDA approval, the prescribing information was updated to include black box warnings for cytokine release syndrome (CRS) and neurological toxicities, including immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome, due to observed severe events in clinical trials; these warnings were further refined in subsequent label updates, such as in 2017 and 2019, to incorporate management guidance based on post-marketing experience.

Society and culture

Names and formulations

Blinatumomab is the international nonproprietary name (INN) and United States Adopted Name (USAN) for this bispecific monoclonal antibody. It is a bispecific T-cell engager (BiTE) constructed from single-chain variable fragments (scFvs) targeting CD3 and CD19.
The drug is marketed under the brand name Blincyto by Amgen Inc., and as a biologic product, no generic versions are currently available.
Blinatumomab is formulated as a lyophilized powder for intravenous injection, supplied in single-use glass vials, each containing 35 mcg of blinatumomab (with excipients totaling approximately 110 mg powder). Higher doses require multiple vials. Each vial is accompanied by an intravenous solution stabilizer diluent for reconstitution and preparation of infusion bags; the vial strengths support dosing regimens typically administered over 28-day cycles. Unopened vials must be stored refrigerated at 2C to 8C (36F to 46F) in their original packaging, protected from light, and not frozen.
Blinatumomab has received orphan drug designations in the United States and the European Union for various indications in acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL), reflecting its targeted use in this rare condition, and it is available exclusively as a prescription medication with no over-the-counter formulation.

Economics

Blinatumomab's pricing in the United States reflects its status as a specialized biologic therapy for acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL). The wholesale acquisition cost (WAC) for a single 35 mcg vial is approximately $5,427, with a standard 28-day induction cycle requiring around 17 vials, resulting in a per-cycle cost of roughly $92,000. For patients undergoing multiple cyclestypically up to four or five in consolidation or maintenance settingsthe annual treatment cost can exceed $200,000, though actual out-of-pocket expenses vary with insurance and discounts. In consolidation phases, costs may be higher due to repeated dosing regimens over several months.
Reimbursement for blinatumomab is generally available through major U.S. public programs. It is covered under Medicare Part B as an outpatient infusion drug, subject to standard deductible and coinsurance, and similarly under Medicaid fee-for-service plans for eligible indications. Amgen offers patient assistance through the Amgen SupportPlus program, including the Safety Net Foundation, which provides the drug at no cost to qualifying uninsured or underinsured patients based on income and residency criteria. In the European Union, value-based pricing discussions have influenced access, with health technology assessments in countries like Ireland estimating net cost offsets from reduced hospitalizations, though overall pricing remains premium due to orphan drug status.
Global sales of blinatumomab reached $581 million in 2023 and $819 million in 2024, according to Amgen's financial reports, driven by expanded use in relapsed/refractory and minimal residual disease-positive ALL. As of Q3 2025, year-to-date sales reached $1.146 billion, reflecting continued growth from broader adoption and recent regulatory approvals for broader indications. Cost-effectiveness analyses for blinatumomab in ALL demonstrate gains in quality-adjusted life years (QALYs), typically 2-5 additional QALYs versus standard chemotherapy, but with incremental cost-effectiveness ratios (ICERs) ranging from $110,000 to $150,000 per QALY gained in U.S. and international models, often exceeding common thresholds in resource-constrained evaluations.
The high cost of blinatumomab poses significant access barriers in low- and middle-income countries, where implementation studies highlight challenges in infrastructure for continuous infusion and affordability, limiting adoption despite efficacy in B-cell ALL. In the U.S., orphan drug exclusivity for recent indications (e.g., consolidation therapy approved in 2024) provides market protection until approximately 2031. In the European Union, exclusivity expires on November 25, 2027. These protections support pricing stability but delay generic or biosimilar competition.
