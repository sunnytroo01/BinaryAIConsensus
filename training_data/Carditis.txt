 Carditis is the inflammation of the heart, which can involve the heart muscle (myocardium), the inner lining of the heart chambers and valves (endocardium), or the outer sac surrounding the heart (pericardium). This condition arises as the body's immune response to infection, injury, or other triggers, potentially impairing the heart's ability to pump blood effectively and leading to complications such as arrhythmias, heart failure, or even death if untreated.
The primary types of carditis include endocarditis, which affects the endocardium and often the heart valves; myocarditis, targeting the myocardium and reducing pumping efficiency; and pericarditis, involving the pericardium and causing sharp chest pain. These forms may occur individually or together as pancarditis, particularly in conditions like rheumatic fever, where inflammation spans multiple heart layers. Causes commonly stem from infectionssuch as viruses (e.g., those causing COVID-19), bacteria (e.g., Lyme disease), or parasitesor from autoimmune disorders, certain medications, trauma, or environmental exposures like toxins, or rarely mRNA COVID-19 vaccines. Risk factors include underlying immune system weaknesses, recent infections, or intravenous drug use, with viral causes being the most frequent in developed regions.
Symptoms of carditis vary by type and severity but often include chest pain (which may worsen with lying down or breathing), shortness of breath, fatigue, fever, and irregular heartbeats. For instance, pericarditis typically presents with pain that improves when leaning forward, while myocarditis may cause swelling in the legs or abdomen due to fluid retention. Diagnosis involves a combination of physical exams, blood tests for infection markers or inflammation (e.g., elevated troponin levels), electrocardiograms (ECGs) to detect rhythm abnormalities, echocardiograms for structural assessment, and sometimes cardiac MRI or biopsy for confirmation.
Treatment focuses on addressing the underlying cause and managing symptoms, often starting with medications such as antibiotics or antivirals for infections, anti-inflammatory drugs like corticosteroids or colchicine for autoimmune-related cases, and pain relievers. In severe instances, procedures like pericardiocentesis to drain excess fluid, pacemaker implantation for conduction issues, or even heart transplantation may be necessary. Early intervention is crucial, as many cases resolve with treatment, though recovery can take weeks to years, and some may lead to chronic heart conditions. Carditis remains relatively rare but underscores the importance of prompt medical attention for heart-related symptoms, especially post-infection.

Overview

Definition

Carditis is defined as inflammation of the heart, encompassing any of its layers or surrounding tissues, including the endocardium (the inner lining), myocardium (the muscular middle layer), or pericardium (the outer sac). This condition represents the heart's inflammatory response to various insults, potentially impairing its structure and function.
The term "carditis" originates from New Latin, derived from the Greek "kardia" (heart) combined with the suffix "-itis" (inflammation), first appearing in medical usage around the late 18th century. This etymology reflects its focus on inflammatory processes affecting cardiac tissue.
Historically, carditis gained recognition in 19th-century medical literature as a complication of infectious diseases, notably rheumatic fever, with early comprehensive descriptions linking it to post-streptococcal sequelae by the 1880s. Physicians such as Walter Butler Cheadle detailed the clinical spectrum of acute rheumatic fever, including carditis, in 1889, marking a pivotal moment in its identification as a distinct entity. This era's observations laid the groundwork for understanding carditis beyond isolated heart involvement, emphasizing its ties to systemic infections.

Classification

Carditis is classified primarily according to the anatomical structures of the heart affected, encompassing inflammation of the endocardium (inner lining), myocardium (muscle layer), pericardium (outer sac), or all layers collectively. This anatomical basis distinguishes endocarditis, myocarditis, pericarditis, and pancarditis as the main categories, with further subdivision by etiology and duration to reflect clinical presentation and management.
Endocarditis involves inflammation of the endocardium, particularly the heart valves, leading to potential valvular dysfunction. Myocarditis targets the myocardium, impairing the heart's pumping ability. Pericarditis affects the pericardium, often causing sharp chest pain exacerbated by breathing or lying down. Pancarditis, a more extensive form, simultaneously involves all three layers and is exemplified in conditions like acute rheumatic fever.
Subtypes of carditis are delineated as infective or non-infective based on etiology, and as acute or chronic based on duration and progression. Infective forms arise from direct microbial invasion, such as bacterial or viral pathogens, whereas non-infective variants stem from autoimmune responses or other systemic processes. Acute carditis typically presents with rapid onset and severe symptoms over days to weeks, while chronic forms evolve over months to years, potentially leading to fibrosis or scarring.
Representative examples include rheumatic carditis, a non-infective pancarditis triggered by an immune reaction following streptococcal infection, often acute in initial episodes but leading to chronic valvular damage. Viral myocarditis, an infective acute subtype, commonly results from enteroviruses like Coxsackie B, causing myocardial inflammation without necessarily affecting other layers.
Overlaps occur when multiple layers are involved, such as combined endo-myocarditis in rheumatic fever or certain bacterial infections, where endocardial and myocardial inflammation coexist, complicating diagnosis and treatment.

Causes

Infectious Causes

Infectious carditis arises from microbial pathogens that invade cardiac tissues or elicit immune responses leading to inflammation of the endocardium, myocardium, or pericardium. Bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites, and rickettsiae represent the primary infectious etiologies, with the specific site of involvement depending on the pathogen's tropism and host factors. These infections account for a significant proportion of acute and chronic heart inflammation cases worldwide.
Bacterial causes predominate in infective endocarditis, where pathogens adhere to and colonize damaged or prosthetic heart valves, forming vegetations that can embolize or cause valvular dysfunction. Viridans group streptococci, Staphylococcus aureus (including methicillin-resistant strains), and Enterococcus species are the most common culprits, responsible for the majority of cases in both native and prosthetic valves. Borrelia burgdorferi, the causative agent of Lyme disease, can lead to Lyme carditis involving myocardial inflammation and atrioventricular conduction abnormalities, occurring in approximately 1% of Lyme disease cases in endemic regions. In contrast, group A beta-hemolytic Streptococcus (Streptococcus pyogenes) triggers rheumatic carditis as a post-infectious complication of acute rheumatic fever, without direct bacterial invasion of the heart; this occurs primarily in children and young adults following pharyngitis in regions with poor access to antibiotics.
Viral infections are the leading cause of myocarditis, often resulting from cardiotropic viruses that directly infect cardiomyocytes or provoke cytotoxic T-cell responses. Coxsackievirus B (an enterovirus), adenovirus, parvovirus B19, and severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) are among the most frequently identified agents, with parvovirus B19 implicated in both acute myocarditis and chronic dilated cardiomyopathy through persistent infection, and SARS-CoV-2 linked to myocarditis in COVID-19 patients via direct viral effects and hyperinflammation. These viruses typically enter via the bloodstream after a primary respiratory or gastrointestinal infection, causing focal or diffuse myocardial inflammation.
Fungal pathogens rarely cause carditis but pose a severe risk in immunocompromised patients, those with indwelling catheters, or post-cardiac surgery. Candida species, particularly C. albicans, and Aspergillus species are the predominant fungi, leading to endocarditis with high mortality due to rapid tissue invasion and embolization; Candida accounts for over 50% of fungal endocarditis cases.
Parasitic infections contribute to myocarditis in endemic areas, with Trypanosoma cruzi being the most significant, causing Chagas disease and chronic cardiomyopathy through direct myocardial invasion and autoimmune mechanisms; it affects millions in Latin America and is now global due to migration and transfusions. Other parasites like Toxoplasma gondii can also involve the heart, particularly in immunocompromised hosts.
Rickettsial infections, though uncommon, include Q fever endocarditis caused by Coxiella burnetii, a zoonotic pathogen acquired via inhalation of contaminated aerosols from livestock; it often presents as culture-negative endocarditis in patients with preexisting valvular disease and has a mortality rate of up to 25% if untreated.
Pathogenic mechanisms vary by agent but generally involve either direct tissue invasion or immune-mediated damage. In bacterial, viral, fungal, parasitic, and rickettsial carditis, pathogens directly infect cardiac cells, releasing toxins and eliciting cytokine storms that amplify inflammation. Rheumatic carditis exemplifies post-infectious autoimmunity via molecular mimicry, where streptococcal M protein shares epitopes with cardiac myosin, leading to cross-reactive antibodies and T-cell infiltration of the myocardium and valves.

Non-Infectious Causes

Non-infectious carditis encompasses a range of inflammatory conditions affecting the heart's layerssuch as the myocardium, pericardium, or endocardiumtriggered by immune dysregulation, toxic exposures, or idiopathic processes rather than microbial pathogens. These etiologies often involve autoimmune responses, hypersensitivity reactions, or direct cellular toxicity, leading to symptoms like chest pain, arrhythmias, or heart failure. Unlike infectious forms, non-infectious carditis typically requires targeted immunosuppression or cessation of the offending agent for management.
Autoimmune disorders represent a major category of non-infectious carditis. Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) frequently causes lupus carditis through immune complex deposition, resulting in myocarditis, pericarditis, or valvular involvement; clinical studies report pericarditis in up to 25% of patients, while autopsy findings indicate pericardial involvement in 43-83% of cases. Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is associated with rheumatoid pericarditis, a fibrinous inflammation affecting up to 50% of RA cases on postmortem examination, though clinically apparent in only 1-2%, often linked to disease severity and rheumatoid factor positivity. These conditions highlight the role of systemic autoimmunity in targeting cardiac tissues via autoantibodies and complement activation.
Toxic and iatrogenic factors contribute significantly to non-infectious carditis. Chemotherapy agents like doxorubicin induce cardiotoxicity through oxidative stress and DNA damage, manifesting as acute pericarditis or arrhythmias in up to 11% of patients during initial cycles, with cumulative doses exceeding 300 mg/m increasing chronic risk. Radiation therapy for thoracic malignancies can provoke acute pericarditis within months of exposure due to microvascular endothelial disruption, progressing to constrictive pericarditis in 1-2% of cases if more than 30% of the heart receives high doses. Hypersensitivity reactions, often to medications such as antibiotics or anticonvulsants, lead to eosinophilic myocarditis characterized by peripheral eosinophilia and myocardial infiltrates without fibrosis, typically resolving upon drug withdrawal.
Idiopathic and hypersensitivity-related forms include giant cell myocarditis, a rare, aggressive entity with multinucleated giant cells and T-cell infiltration, affecting young adults and carrying a high mortality rate of up to 70% without immunosuppression. Vaccine-associated myocarditis, notably with mRNA COVID-19 vaccines, occurs rarely at rates of 1-10 per 100,000 doses, predominantly in adolescent males, involving transient immune-mediated inflammation without long-term sequelae in most cases. Post-viral autoimmunity may also trigger persistent carditis via molecular mimicry, where prior infection initiates an aberrant immune response against cardiac antigens.
Rare causes include metabolic derangements like uremic pericarditis in end-stage renal disease, driven by toxin accumulation and inflammation, occurring in 6-10% of dialysis patients and presenting with hemorrhagic effusions. Traumatic carditis, such as contusio cordis from blunt chest impact, induces myocardial contusion and inflammation, potentially leading to ventricular fibrillation in susceptible individuals during the vulnerable repolarization phase. These uncommon triggers underscore the diverse non-microbial pathways to cardiac inflammation.

Pathophysiology

General Mechanisms

Carditis, encompassing inflammation across various cardiac layers, is fundamentally driven by an inflammatory cascade initiated by diverse triggers such as infections, autoimmune responses, or toxins, which activate pattern recognition receptors like Toll-like receptors on resident cardiac cells, leading to the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines including TNF- and IL-6. These cytokines amplify the response by recruiting and activating immune cells, promoting a self-perpetuating cycle of inflammation that can affect myocardial, pericardial, or endocardial tissues. Neutrophils and lymphocytes predominate in the early infiltration phase, with neutrophils contributing to acute damage through reactive oxygen species and proteases, while lymphocytes, particularly CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, sustain the response via cytokine production and antigen-specific targeting.
The ensuing tissue damage manifests as localized edema from increased vascular permeability induced by cytokines, followed by myocyte or pericardial cell necrosis due to direct cytotoxic effects and proteolytic activity of infiltrated immune cells. Over time, this progresses to fibrosis as activated fibroblasts deposit extracellular matrix in response to transforming growth factor- (TGF-) signaling, potentially impairing cardiac contractility, conduction, or compliance depending on the affected region. Such damage disrupts normal cardiac architecture, leading to functional deficits like reduced ejection fraction or valvular incompetence.
Systemic effects extend beyond the heart, involving activation of the complement systemparticularly the classical and alternative pathwayswhich opsonizes pathogens or damaged cells and generates anaphylatoxins like C3a and C5a that further recruit inflammatory cells. Endothelial dysfunction arises concurrently, characterized by upregulated adhesion molecules (e.g., ICAM-1, VCAM-1) on vascular endothelium, facilitating leukocyte extravasation and promoting a prothrombotic state that can exacerbate ischemia. Recent studies as of 2025 also implicate complement dysregulation, including mitochondrial injury, as a key driver in viral myocarditis progression. These processes contribute to broader hemodynamic instability, including hypotension from vasodilatory cytokines.
The progression of carditis typically unfolds in stages, beginning with acute inflammation dominated by innate immunity and cytokine storms, which may resolve with clearance of the trigger or evolve into subacute phases marked by adaptive immune dominance and persistent infiltration. In unresolved cases, chronic remodeling ensues, involving maladaptive hypertrophy, ongoing fibrosis, and scar formation that can culminate in dilated cardiomyopathy or restrictive physiology, influenced by genetic predispositions and unresolved autoimmunity. While much evidence derives from myocarditis models, these mechanisms apply broadly to carditis forms, with variations by layer and etiology.

Type-Specific Effects

In endocarditis, inflammation primarily targets the endocardium, leading to the formation of vegetationsmasses composed of fibrin, platelets, and infecting microorganismsthat adhere to the heart valves, particularly along their edges. These vegetations disrupt normal valve function by interfering with leaflet coaptation, resulting in valvular regurgitation where blood flows backward through the affected valve during diastole or systole. For instance, in aortic valve endocarditis, new or worsening regurgitation is a hallmark endocardial effect due to vegetation-induced damage or abscess formation.
Myocarditis involves direct inflammatory injury to the myocardium, the heart's muscular layer, causing myocyte damage and apoptosis that impair contractility and lead to reduced systolic function. This loss of contractile tissue can progress to dilated cardiomyopathy, with the heart's pumping efficiency compromised as inflammation triggers fibrosis and remodeling. Additionally, myocardial inflammation disrupts electrical conduction pathways, predisposing patients to arrhythmias such as ventricular tachycardia or fibrillation, which may arise acutely or persist in the chronic phase due to residual scarring.
Pericarditis affects the pericardium, the sac-like membrane surrounding the heart, where inflammation causes exudative effusionaccumulation of fluid in the pericardial spacethat increases intrapericardial pressure. In severe cases, this effusion compresses the cardiac chambers, leading to cardiac tamponade, a life-threatening condition that restricts diastolic filling and reduces cardiac output, often manifesting as hemodynamic instability. The inflammatory process in the pericardium typically involves fibrinous or serous exudates, which can adhere layers together if unresolved, though tamponade represents the acute mechanical consequence of unchecked effusion.
Pancarditis, a simultaneous inflammation of all three cardiac layers, occurs in conditions like acute rheumatic fever, where an autoimmune response to group A Streptococcus triggers widespread endocardial, myocardial, and pericardial involvement. This multi-layer process often begins with valvulitis in the endocardium, forming Aschoff bodies and fibrinous vegetations that evolve into valvular scarring and stenosis over repeated episodes, while myocardial inflammation contributes to transient dysfunction and pericardial effusion adds risk of tamponade. The resulting chronic valvular damage, particularly to the mitral and aortic valves, stems from progressive fibrosis across layers, underscoring the diffuse nature of the inflammatory cascade in rheumatic pancarditis.

Signs and Symptoms

Common Symptoms

Carditis, encompassing inflammation of the heart's various layers, commonly manifests through patient-reported symptoms that reflect cardiac irritation and impaired function. Chest pain is a hallmark symptom, often described as sharp and pleuriticworsening with deep breaths or lying downin cases involving the pericardium, or as a dull, oppressive pressure mimicking angina when the myocardium is affected. Fatigue and dyspnea on exertion are also prevalent, arising from reduced cardiac output and oxygen delivery, with patients noting progressive tiredness during routine activities.
Systemic symptoms frequently accompany these cardiac complaints, including fever and malaise, which may precede or coincide with heart involvement, particularly in infectious etiologies. Palpitations, perceived as fluttering or racing heartbeats, result from arrhythmias induced by inflammation. In advanced cases, peripheral edemaswelling in the legs, ankles, or abdomensignals fluid retention due to congestive heart failure.
The onset of symptoms varies by the acuity of carditis; acute forms often present suddenly with intense chest pain, fever, and shortness of breath, sometimes following a viral illness, while chronic carditis tends to develop insidiously with persistent low-grade fatigue and exertional dyspnea over weeks to months.
In pediatric patients with rheumatic carditis, symptoms may be less specific and include irritability and poor feeding, especially in infants, reflecting underlying heart failure and reduced tolerance to exertion during meals.

Physical Signs

Physical examination findings in carditis primarily involve abnormalities detected through auscultation, assessment of vital signs, and inspection for signs of hemodynamic compromise, reflecting involvement of the pericardium, myocardium, or endocardium.
Auscultation often reveals new valvular murmurs indicative of regurgitation, such as a holosystolic murmur at the apex radiating to the left axilla due to mitral involvement, or a diastolic murmur at the left sternal border from aortic regurgitation; these commonly occur, as valvulitis is the predominant manifestation of carditis in acute rheumatic fever. For endocardial involvement, particularly in infective endocarditis, additional peripheral signs may include Osler's nodes (tender nodules on fingers or toes), Janeway lesions (nontender erythematous macules on palms or soles), splinter hemorrhages under the nails, Roth spots (retinal hemorrhages with pale centers), and petechiae on the conjunctivae or skin. A pericardial friction rub, a high-pitched, scratchy sound best heard at the left sternal border during inspiration, may be present in a significant proportion of patients with pericardial involvement, signaling acute inflammation of the pericardium. In cases of significant pericardial effusion, heart sounds may become muffled or distant, accompanied by increased cardiac dullness to percussion.
Vital signs commonly show tachycardia disproportionate to the degree of fever, a hallmark of myocardial inflammation across various etiologies. Hypotension may develop in severe cases, particularly when carditis progresses to heart failure or tamponade.
Additional findings include jugular venous distension and hepatomegaly, which arise in congestive states due to right heart involvement or overall cardiac decompensation. Subtle auscultatory signs such as an S3 or S4 gallop rhythm, producing a triple cadence, suggest myocardial dysfunction and are associated with ventricular filling abnormalities in inflammatory cardiomyopathies.

Diagnosis

The diagnosis of carditis is guided by current clinical guidelines, such as the 2025 European Society of Cardiology (ESC) Guidelines for the management of myocarditis and pericarditis.

Clinical Evaluation

The clinical evaluation of suspected carditis commences with a comprehensive history and physical examination to guide etiology, risk stratification, and diagnostic planning. This initial assessment integrates patient-reported symptoms, such as chest pain, dyspnea, or fatigue, with physical findings to raise suspicion for specific forms like myocarditis, pericarditis, or endocarditis.
History taking focuses on recent infections, including viral upper respiratory or gastrointestinal illnesses that may precede acute myocarditis or pericarditis, and bacterial pharyngitis suggestive of rheumatic carditis. Inquiry into autoimmune history is essential, as conditions like systemic lupus erythematosus or sarcoidosis can trigger non-infectious carditis through immune-mediated mechanisms. Drug exposure must be explored, particularly hypersensitivity reactions to medications such as penicillin or immune checkpoint inhibitors in autoimmune myocarditis. Travel history to endemic regions, such as Latin America for Trypanosoma cruzi in Chagas cardiomyopathy or Africa for schistosomiasis-related pericarditis, helps identify parasitic causes.
Risk assessment evaluates modifiable and non-modifiable factors to prioritize high-risk etiologies. Intravenous drug use is a key risk for right-sided infective endocarditis, often involving Staphylococcus aureus. Immunosuppression from conditions like HIV, chemotherapy, or organ transplantation increases susceptibility to opportunistic infections, including fungal or viral carditis.
Differential diagnosis requires distinguishing carditis from mimics presenting with overlapping features like fever, chest pain, or hemodynamic instability. Acute coronary syndrome must be ruled out in older patients or those with cardiovascular risk factors, while pneumonia or pulmonary embolism should be considered in cases with respiratory symptoms or hypoxia.
For suspected rheumatic carditis, the 2015 revised Jones criteria from the American Heart Association provide a structured clinical framework, requiring evidence of antecedent group A streptococcal infectionestablished via history of recent pharyngitis or positive throat cultureplus two major manifestations or one major and two minor manifestations. Carditis serves as a major criterion, clinically manifest by new or changed valvular regurgitation murmurs on examination, though subclinical forms may require further evaluation.

Laboratory and Imaging Tests

Laboratory tests play a crucial role in diagnosing carditis by identifying markers of inflammation, infection, and myocardial injury. Blood tests often reveal elevated levels of C-reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), which indicate systemic inflammation commonly associated with pericarditis and myocarditis. Cardiac biomarkers such as troponin I or T are frequently elevated in myocarditis, elevated in over 50% of biopsy-confirmed cases, signaling myocardial damage, while creatine kinase-MB (CK-MB) may also rise to reflect similar injury. For infective causes, particularly endocarditis, blood cultures are essential to identify causative pathogens in the bloodstream, guiding targeted antibiotic therapy. A complete blood count may show leukocytosis suggestive of infection or anemia as a complication of endocarditis.
Electrocardiography (ECG) is a fundamental noninvasive test that detects electrical abnormalities in carditis. In pericarditis, ECG often shows widespread ST-segment elevation and PR depression, mimicking acute myocardial infarction but without reciprocal changes. Myocarditis may present with nonspecific findings such as sinus tachycardia, low-voltage QRS complexes, atrioventricular blocks, or arrhythmias, with a sensitivity of approximately 47% for detecting inflammation-related changes. Endocarditis-related ECG changes are less specific but can indicate conduction disturbances or ischemia if valvular involvement affects coronary flow. These patterns help differentiate carditis from other cardiac conditions, though results must be interpreted alongside clinical evaluation to guide further testing.
Imaging modalities provide structural and functional insights to confirm and characterize carditis. Echocardiography, particularly transthoracic and transesophageal variants, is pivotal for visualizing vegetations on heart valves in endocarditisa major criterion in the modified Duke criteria (updated 2023)pericardial effusions in pericarditis and myocarditis, and impaired ventricular function or wall motion abnormalities in myocarditis. Chest X-ray can reveal cardiomegaly or pulmonary congestion secondary to heart failure in advanced cases. Cardiac magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) serves as a gold standard noninvasive tool for myocarditis, detecting myocardial edema via T2-weighted sequences and late gadolinium enhancement for necrosis or fibrosis, per the updated Lake Louise criteria (2018). In pericarditis, MRI identifies pericardial thickening or inflammation, while computed tomography (CT) assesses pericardial calcification or constriction and evaluates extracardiac complications like abscesses in endocarditis.
Invasive procedures are reserved for cases requiring definitive diagnosis. Endomyocardial biopsy, obtained via cardiac catheterization, is the gold standard for confirming myocarditis through histopathological evidence of inflammatory infiltrates and myocyte necrosis, as defined by the Dallas criteria, though it is used selectively due to a low complication rate of less than 1%. This approach is particularly valuable when noninvasive tests are inconclusive or when specific etiologies, such as viral or autoimmune causes, need histological verification.

Treatment

Acute Management

Acute management of carditis prioritizes hemodynamic stabilization and addresses life-threatening complications while targeting the underlying etiology. Supportive measures include supplemental oxygen to maintain saturation above 92%, intravenous fluids for hypotension without evidence of fluid overload, and antiarrhythmic agents such as beta-blockers or amiodarone for tachyarrhythmias to prevent deterioration into ventricular fibrillation or tachycardia.
Etiology-specific interventions are initiated promptly based on suspected cause. For bacterial endocarditis, empirical intravenous antibiotics like vancomycin (15-20 mg/kg every 8-12 hours) combined with gentamicin or ceftriaxone are recommended until blood cultures guide targeted therapy, typically for 4-6 weeks. In viral myocarditis, routine antiviral therapy is not recommended due to limited evidence, but agents such as ganciclovir may be considered in cases of confirmed cytomegalovirus or herpesvirus involvement. For immune-mediated forms like giant cell myocarditis, high-dose corticosteroids (e.g., methylprednisolone 1 g/day for 3 days) followed by oral prednisone are used, often with immunosuppressive agents like cyclosporine.
Pericardial involvement with tamponade requires urgent pericardiocentesis under echocardiographic guidance to relieve pressure and prevent cardiac arrest, with surgical drainage indicated for recurrent or loculated effusions.
Patients with fulminant carditis, characterized by rapid-onset severe heart failure, cardiogenic shock, or arrhythmias, warrant intensive care unit admission for continuous monitoring, inotropic support (e.g., dobutamine), and potential mechanical circulatory assistance like intra-aortic balloon pump or extracorporeal membrane oxygenation if refractory hypotension persists. A multidisciplinary team, including cardiologists and infectious disease specialists, coordinates care to optimize outcomes during this critical phase.

Long-Term Therapy

Long-term therapy for carditis focuses on preventing recurrence, managing chronic sequelae such as heart failure or valvular damage, and addressing underlying autoimmune or inflammatory processes to improve quality of life and reduce morbidity. In cases of autoimmune myocarditis, immunosuppressive agents like corticosteroids and intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) are employed to modulate the immune response and mitigate ongoing myocardial inflammation. High-dose corticosteroids, often combined with IVIG, have been used in pediatric acute myocarditis to target inflammatory pathways, demonstrating safety without significant long-term side effects in select cohorts. However, meta-analyses indicate that IVIG does not consistently improve survival rates in children with acute myocarditis, though it may offer tentative benefits in viral cases. For immune checkpoint inhibitor-associated myocarditis, intravenous corticosteroids serve as first-line immunosuppression, with potential escalation to corticosteroid-sparing agents for chronic inflammatory cardiomyopathy.
Secondary prevention is critical for rheumatic carditis to avert recurrent acute rheumatic fever and progression of rheumatic heart disease. Prophylactic antibiotics, typically intramuscular benzathine penicillin G administered every 3-4 weeks, are recommended to eradicate group A streptococcal infections and reduce recurrence risk by up to 70% in high-risk patients. Duration of prophylaxis varies by age and carditis severity: for those with carditis but no residual valve disease, it extends for 10 years or until age 40, whichever is longer. Clinical trials confirm that adherence to this regimen significantly slows latent rheumatic heart disease progression in children and adolescents.
In patients developing heart failure as a sequela of carditis, angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors are a cornerstone of therapy to reduce afterload, prevent remodeling, and improve ejection fraction. For myocarditis-induced heart failure, ACE inhibitors like enalapril are initiated once stabilized, lowering the incidence of nonfatal cardiac events and supporting long-term ventricular function. These agents are particularly beneficial in reducing peripheral vascular resistance and enhancing cardiac output in post-inflammatory cardiomyopathy.
Surgical interventions address structural complications in chronic cases. Valve replacement is indicated for persistent infective endocarditis with severe valvular destruction or regurgitation, involving excision of infected tissue and prosthetic implantation to restore hemodynamics and eradicate residual infection. In constrictive pericarditis arising from prior pericardial inflammation, pericardiectomy remains the definitive treatment, with complete removal of the pericardium yielding symptom relief in most patients and operative mortality rates of 5-10%. Early surgical referral is advised for New York Heart Association class III or IV symptoms to optimize outcomes.
Lifestyle modifications complement pharmacotherapy by minimizing cardiac stress and preventing secondary insults. Activity restrictions, including avoidance of competitive sports or strenuous exercise for 3-6 months post-acute phase, allow myocardial recovery and reduce arrhythmia risk in myocarditis survivors, followed by a gradual return to exercise once medically cleared. Vaccination against preventable etiologies, such as influenza and pneumococcal vaccines, is emphasized for carditis patients to lower infection-related exacerbation risks, aligning with guidelines for those with underlying heart disease. A heart-healthy diet, such as a balanced Mediterranean-style diet rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean proteins, and healthy fats, low in sodium and saturated fats, alongside stress management techniques and ensuring adequate sleep (7-9 hours per night), further supports long-term cardiac stability. Management of cardiovascular risk factors, including control of blood pressure and cholesterol levels, avoidance of smoking, and limiting alcohol intake, is also essential.

Prognosis and Complications

Prognosis

The prognosis of carditis varies significantly depending on the specific type, severity, and underlying etiology, with many cases achieving full recovery through supportive care. For pericarditis, the most common form of carditis, the outlook is generally excellent, with over 90% of patients achieving full recovery within weeks to months and low mortality (<1%) when treated promptly; recurrence occurs in 15-30% but is often manageable. Infective endocarditis carries a more serious prognosis, with in-hospital mortality rates of 15-30% even with antibiotics and surgery, and long-term 5-year survival around 60-70% influenced by factors like age, comorbidities, and vegetation size.
In viral myocarditis, the most common subtype of myocarditis, recovery rates are typically high, ranging from 80% to 90% in mild to moderate cases, particularly when inflammation resolves spontaneously or with early intervention. In contrast, fulminant myocarditis carries a more guarded outlook, with historical mortality rates exceeding 50%, though recent advancements in mechanical circulatory support have reduced in-hospital mortality to as low as 5% in specialized centers.
Key factors influencing outcomes include early diagnosis and the underlying cause, as prompt recognition allows for timely supportive measures that improve survival. Infectious etiologies, such as viral infections, generally confer better prognosis compared to autoimmune forms like giant cell myocarditis, which historically result in 90% rates of death or transplantation without targeted immunosuppression.
Long-term, a subset of patients with myocarditis faces an elevated risk of progression to dilated cardiomyopathy, affecting up to 30% of biopsy-proven cases, particularly those with persistent left ventricular dysfunction.
As of 2025, studies on post-2020 vaccine-related myocarditis, including longer-term follow-ups, confirm favorable outcomes with >95% resolution of symptoms and very low mortality (around 1%) compared to traditional viral myocarditis (11%), while less severe viral cases show 5-year survival rates of 80% to 83%. Biopsy-proven cases show 5-year survival around 56% for lymphocytic myocarditis, highlighting the impact of histological confirmation on risk stratification.

Potential Complications

Carditis, encompassing inflammation of the heart's endocardium, myocardium, or pericardium, can lead to a range of secondary cardiac complications if untreated or severe. One primary concern is the development of heart failure, which arises from impaired ventricular function due to myocardial inflammation or valvular dysfunction in conditions like rheumatic carditis or infective endocarditis. In myocarditis, acute inflammation may progress to dilated cardiomyopathy, reducing the heart's pumping efficiency and causing symptoms such as dyspnea and fatigue. Similarly, valvular involvement in rheumatic carditis often results in regurgitation or stenosis, exacerbating left ventricular strain and contributing to congestive heart failure.
Arrhythmias represent another significant cardiac sequela, frequently triggered by inflammatory damage to the conduction system. In acute myocarditis, irritation of myocardial tissue can provoke ventricular or supraventricular arrhythmias, including atrial fibrillation, which may lead to hemodynamic instability. Rheumatic carditis similarly predisposes patients to arrhythmias through scarring of the cardiac conduction pathways, with atrial fibrillation being particularly common in chronic valvular disease. Valvular stenosis, a hallmark of long-term rheumatic carditis, further compounds this risk by promoting atrial enlargement and irregular rhythms.
Systemic complications extend beyond the heart, notably embolism from vegetations in infective endocarditis. These friable masses on heart valves can dislodge, causing ischemic events in distant organs such as the brain (stroke) or kidneys, with embolization occurring in up to 50% of cases. Constrictive pericarditis, arising from chronic pericardial inflammation, impairs diastolic filling by forming a rigid fibrous layer around the heart, leading to elevated venous pressures and signs of right heart failure.
Chronic scarring from unresolved inflammation contributes to restrictive cardiomyopathy, particularly following viral or idiopathic myocarditis. Fibrotic replacement of myocardial tissue stiffens the ventricles, hindering relaxation and filling without significantly altering systolic function, often mimicking constrictive pericarditis clinically. In rare instances, severe myocarditis can precipitate sudden death due to ventricular fibrillation, stemming from acute arrhythmogenic foci or profound myocardial dysfunction. These complications underscore the need for vigilant monitoring, as their onset can modulate overall prognosis in carditis patients.

Epidemiology

Incidence and Prevalence

Carditis, which includes inflammatory conditions affecting the heart such as myocarditis, endocarditis, pericarditis, and rheumatic carditis, remains relatively rare in the general population, with an overall annual incidence of approximately 50 cases per 100,000 population worldwide when combining major subtypes. This rarity is underscored by global estimates indicating that the combined burden of these subtypes affects a small fraction of individuals annually, though rates are notably higher in developing countries where rheumatic fever contributes significantly to the prevalence, impacting an estimated 55 million people globally, predominantly in low- and middle-income regions.
Among the specific types, myocarditis has an age-standardized incidence rate (ASIR) of approximately 16.2 cases per 100,000 population in 2021, reflecting a range of 10 to 20 cases per 100,000 based on varying regional reports. Pericarditis has an estimated incidence of 20 to 30 cases per 100,000 population annually. Infective endocarditis showed an ASIR of 13.8 cases per 100,000 in 2019, higher than traditional estimates of 3 to 10 cases per 100,000 globally. These figures highlight the low but persistent occurrence of carditis subtypes, with total incident cases for myocarditis alone reaching 1.32 million in 2021.
Trends in carditis incidence show a marked decline in rheumatic carditis following the widespread introduction of antibiotics in the mid-20th century, which contributed to a significant reduction in acute rheumatic fever and its cardiac sequelae in developed countries by the 1980s. Conversely, viral myocarditis cases have risen post-COVID-19, with infection-related incidence rates increasing by at least 15-fold compared to pre-pandemic levels of 1 to 10 cases per 100,000, reaching approximately 150 cases per 100,000 among individuals infected with COVID-19 according to surveillance data.
Geographically, parasitic forms of carditis are more prevalent in tropical regions, particularly in Central and South America, where Chagas disease infects 7 to 8 million people and leads to cardiac involvement in up to 30% of cases, manifesting as myocarditis or cardiomyopathy. Similar patterns occur with other tropical parasites like those causing cysticercosis and endomyocardial fibrosis, which affect millions in endemic areas and contribute to higher regional burdens.

Risk Factors and Demographics

Carditis encompasses inflammation of various heart layers, with risk profiles varying by type. Rheumatic carditis primarily affects children and adolescents aged 5 to 15 years, particularly in low- and middle-income countries and among marginalized communities where access to healthcare is limited. Nonwhite ethnicities face a higher risk due to socioeconomic and environmental factors. In contrast, infective endocarditis, often degenerative in nature, predominantly impacts older adults, with a median age around 68 years and a slight male predominance (approximately 68% of cases). Myocarditis shows a higher incidence in young adult males, while pericarditis more commonly occurs in middle-aged adults, also with male skew.
Key risk factors for carditis include immunosuppression, such as in patients with HIV, organ transplants, or those on immunosuppressive therapy, which increases susceptibility to infectious and autoimmune forms like endocarditis and myocarditis. Intravenous drug use is a major predisposing factor for infective endocarditis by facilitating bacterial entry into the bloodstream. Poor dental hygiene contributes to endocarditis risk through transient bacteremia from oral infections. Autoimmune diseases, including rheumatoid arthritis, systemic lupus erythematosus, and rheumatic fever, elevate the likelihood of pericarditis, myocarditis, and endocarditis via immune-mediated inflammation.
Emerging data post-2021 indicate a rare association between mRNA COVID-19 vaccines and myocarditis or pericarditis, primarily affecting adolescent and young adult males after the second dose, though the overall risk remains low compared to infection-related cases. Socioeconomic factors, such as residence in low-income areas with overcrowding, undernutrition, and limited healthcare access, heighten the risk of rheumatic carditis by promoting streptococcal infections and delaying treatment. These disparities underscore the role of social determinants in vulnerable populations.
