Ductility
 Ara Eve Leo Sal 
1x
 Ductility is a fundamental mechanical property of materials, defined as the ability of a solid to undergo substantial plastic deformation under tensile stress prior to fracture, allowing it to be drawn into wires or elongated without breaking. This property distinguishes ductile materials, which exhibit large strains before failure, from brittle ones that rupture with little to no deformation.
In materials science, ductility is closely related to but distinct from malleability, the latter referring to deformation under compressive forces to form thin sheets, whereas ductility specifically involves tensile loading. Common examples of highly ductile materials include metals such as mild steel, copper, aluminum, and gold. These properties make ductility crucial for manufacturing processes such as wire drawing and extrusion, which involve tensile or combined stresses, and forging, which is primarily compressive and relies more directly on malleability though ductility still contributes indirectly by helping prevent cracking.
Ductility is typically measured during standardized tensile tests, where specimens are pulled until failure, and key metrics include percent elongationcalculated as the increase in length relative to the original gauge lengthand percent reduction in area, which assesses the narrowing (necking) at the fracture point. High values in these measures indicate superior ductility, often in the range of 20-30% elongation or higher for many engineering metals, though values vary widely depending on alloy, heat treatment, and testing conditions. Factors influencing ductility encompass microstructure, temperature (which generally increases with increasing temperature, though some materials like BCC metals exhibit a ductile-to-brittle transition at low temperatures), strain rate, and alloying elements, with many advanced materials engineered to achieve a balance between ductility and strength to avoid catastrophic failure.
The importance of ductility in engineering cannot be overstated, as it provides critical safety margins by permitting visible deformation as a warning before rupture, particularly in load-bearing structures like bridges, vehicles, and pipelines. In contrast, low ductility can lead to sudden brittle fractures under impact or stress concentrations, underscoring its role in enhancing overall toughness and reliability in applications from aerospace to consumer products.

Fundamentals of Ductility

Definition and Basic Concepts

Ductility is defined as the ability of a material, particularly metals and alloys, to undergo significant plastic deformation under tensile stress prior to fracture, allowing it to be drawn into wires without breaking. This property distinguishes ductile materials from those that fail abruptly, as plastic deformation involves a permanent change in shape that exceeds the material's elastic limit, where elastic deformation would instead allow recovery to the original form upon stress removal. In essence, ductility enables materials to absorb energy through extensive elongation or necking before rupture, contributing to their toughness in applications requiring formability.
The concept of ductility has roots in ancient metallurgy, where civilizations such as the Egyptians and Mesopotamians observed the exceptional deformability of native metals like gold and copper, using them to craft intricate jewelry and artifacts as early as 4000 BCE due to their ease in drawing and hammering. The term "ductility" itself, derived from the Latin ductilis meaning "capable of being led or drawn," entered scientific usage in the 19th century amid advancements in materials testing and metallurgical analysis, formalizing observations of tensile behavior in industrial contexts. Exemplifying high ductility are pure metals such as gold and silver, which can be elongated to extreme lengthsgold, for instance, can be drawn into wires finer than human hairwhile materials like cast iron exhibit low ductility, fracturing with minimal deformation due to its brittle microstructure.
At the atomic level, ductility in crystalline materials arises from the activation of slip systems, where dislocationslinear defects in the crystal latticemove along specific planes and directions under applied stress, facilitating coordinated shear that accommodates plastic strain without catastrophic failure. Materials with multiple available slip systems, such as face-centered cubic metals like copper and aluminum, demonstrate enhanced ductility because these systems allow deformation in various orientations, distributing strain evenly across the lattice. This dislocation-mediated mechanism underscores why ductility is inherently linked to a material's capacity for energy absorption during deformation, though it can vary with temperature in ways that influence overall behavior.

Distinction from Other Mechanical Properties

Ductility refers to a material's ability to undergo significant plastic deformation under tensile stress before fracturing, typically demonstrated by processes like wire drawing, where metals such as copper can be elongated into thin wires without breaking. In contrast, malleability describes the capacity for plastic deformation under compressive forces, as seen in hammering gold into thin sheets, allowing shape change perpendicular to the applied force rather than along it. While both properties involve plastic flow and are prominent in metals, ductility emphasizes elongation in tension, whereas malleability focuses on lateral spreading under compression, with gold exemplifying high levels of both.
Brittleness, the opposite of ductility, is characterized by minimal plastic deformation prior to sudden fracture, resulting in little to no warning before failure, as observed in glass which shatters abruptly under stress. Brittle materials, such as ceramics, exhibit fracture without appreciable yielding, leading to clean, planar breaks, unlike the necking and dimpled surfaces typical of ductile failure in steels. This distinction is critical in applications where predictable deformation is needed, as brittle behavior can cause catastrophic failure under impact.
Toughness measures a material's ability to absorb energy up to fracture, represented by the area under the stress-strain curve, combining strength and ductility to quantify resistance to crack propagation. While ductility specifically gauges the extent of deformation capacity before rupture, toughness integrates this with the material's load-bearing ability, so a highly ductile but low-strength polymer may have lower toughness than a moderately ductile high-strength steel. For instance, metals like aluminum achieve high toughness through balanced ductility and yield strength, enhancing safety in structural designs.
Ductility serves as a quantitative indicator of the degree of plastic deformation a material can sustain under tension prior to failure, distinguishing it from broader plasticity, which encompasses any permanent shape change under various stresses. In metals, plasticity arises from dislocation motion enabling extensive deformation, but ductility specifically highlights the uniform and localized elongation before necking and fracture. This measure is essential for forming operations, where high ductility allows deep drawing without tearing.
Although hardness reflects resistance to surface indentation and often correlates with yield strength, it typically inversely relates to ductility, as harder materials like tempered martensitic steels exhibit reduced elongation. Strength, defined by the maximum stress a material withstands, can coexist with high ductility in advanced alloys through microstructural engineering, such as in nanotwinned copper, overcoming traditional trade-offs for enhanced performance. However, increasing strength via work hardening generally diminishes ductility, requiring careful alloy design to balance these properties for engineering reliability.

Quantification of Ductility

Standard Measures and Tests

Tensile testing serves as the primary standard method for quantifying ductility in metallic materials, involving the application of uniaxial tensile stress to a specimen until fracture occurs. This procedure, detailed in ASTM E8/E8M-24, measures the material's ability to undergo plastic deformation before breaking and is widely adopted for its reproducibility and relevance to engineering applications.
The key metrics derived from tensile testing include percent elongation and reduction in area, both expressed as percentages to indicate the extent of deformation. Within percent elongation, a distinction is made between uniform elongation, which occurs before the onset of necking and represents stable deformation, and total elongation, which includes post-necking extension and provides a broader assessment of ductility. This differentiation is crucial for understanding localized versus global behavior during testing.
For qualitative evaluation, particularly in scenarios where quantitative tensile data is impractical, bend tests are employed to assess ductility by bending a specimen to a specified angle or radius and inspecting for cracks or fractures. Guided bend tests, as outlined in ASTM E290-22, are commonly used for welds and sheet materials to verify soundness and resistance to deformation-induced failure.
In sheet metals, the Erichsen cupping test provides a specialized measure of formability and ductility by forming a hemispherical cup through a spherical punch until failure, with the depth of the cup (Erichsen value) indicating stretchability. This method, standardized under ISO 20482, is essential for predicting deep-drawing performance in automotive and aerospace components.
Interpretation of these measures typically considers values exceeding 15% elongation or reduction in area as indicative of ductile behavior in metals, enabling safe plastic deformation in service. For instance, annealed copper often exhibits around 40-60% elongation, reflecting its high ductility for electrical wiring, while structural steels range from 15-30%, balancing strength and formability in construction applications. Test results can vary slightly with temperature and specimen geometry, influencing apparent ductility values.

Effects of Sample Geometry and Testing Conditions

The geometry of tensile specimens significantly influences the measured ductility, particularly through variations in gauge length and cross-sectional shape. In standard tensile testing, elongationa primary measure of ductilityis calculated based on the change in gauge length, but longer gauge lengths often result in lower reported percent elongation values due to non-uniform strain distribution during necking, where deformation localizes away from the gauge section. For instance, studies on metallic materials have shown that total elongation increases as gauge length decreases, with ratios of gauge length to width affecting the uniformity of strain and thus underestimating intrinsic ductility in elongated samples. Similarly, the cross-sectional shape affects stress distribution; round specimens typically provide more uniform deformation and higher apparent ductility compared to flat ones, which can exhibit reduced elongation due to edge effects or gripping-induced stress concentrations. Notched specimens, often used to simulate stress raisers, further diminish measured ductility by promoting localized cracking and early fracture, lowering values by up to 20-30% in sensitive alloys relative to unnotched counterparts.
Testing conditions, including strain rate and atmospheric environment, introduce additional variability in ductility assessments. At low strain rates, such as those below 10^{-3} s^{-1}, many face-centered cubic metals like aluminum exhibit enhanced ductility due to increased time for dislocation motion and recovery, allowing greater plastic deformation before failure; conversely, high rates can promote adiabatic heating or twinning, reducing elongation in body-centered cubic metals. In oxidizing atmospheres, such as air at elevated temperatures, surface oxide layers form rapidly, embrittling the material and decreasing ductilityfor example, in titanium alloys, oxidation during testing can reduce elongation by over 50% by inducing microcracks at the oxide-metal interface. These effects highlight the need for controlled inert environments in precise measurements.
Size effects become prominent in micro- or nanoscale specimens, where smaller dimensions often yield higher ductility owing to a reduced density of defects like dislocations or inclusions. In thin films, for instance, metallic layers below 1 m thickness in materials such as copper or gold can demonstrate elongations up to 20% in supported configurations, attributed to constrained grain growth and fewer internal flaws that impede deformation, though typically lower than bulk values. This "smaller is ductiler" trend arises from statistical sampling of defect-free volumes, though it reverses in ultra-small scales due to surface constraints.
Standardization through guidelines like ASTM E8 for metals and ISO 6892-1 mitigates these influences, specifying precise specimen dimensionssuch as a 4:1 gauge length-to-diameter ratio for round barsto ensure reproducible results and minimize inter-laboratory variability, which can otherwise exceed 15% in elongation measurements. Improper geometry, such as non-standard gauge lengths, has been shown to lead to inconsistent material rankings; for example, in steel comparisons, varying cross-sections altered ductility assessments enough to misclassify alloys for structural applications. Adherence to these standards thus enables reliable quantification across diverse testing setups.

Mechanisms and Influencing Factors

Microstructural Mechanisms

Ductility in crystalline materials arises primarily from the plastic deformation enabled by the motion of dislocations, linear defects in the crystal lattice that allow atoms to shear past one another without breaking atomic bonds. Dislocation glide, the primary mechanism at lower temperatures, involves the movement of dislocations along specific slip planes under applied shear stress, while climb, which requires atomic diffusion, enables dislocations to bypass obstacles by moving perpendicular to the slip plane. The initiation of slip on a given system occurs when the resolved shear stress reaches a critical value, known as the critical resolved shear stress (CRSS), as described by Schmid's law, which relates the effective shear stress on a slip system to the orientation of the applied load.
The ductility of metals is strongly influenced by the number and activatability of slip systems, defined by combinations of slip planes and directions. Face-centered cubic (FCC) metals, such as aluminum, possess 12 non-parallel slip systems on {111} planes in <110> directions, facilitating multi-directional deformation and high ductility due to low lattice friction. In contrast, body-centered cubic (BCC) metals like iron have up to 48 possible slip systems, including {110}<111>, {112}<111>, and {123}<111>, but exhibit lower ductility because of a higher Peierls stressthe intrinsic lattice resistance to dislocation motionarising from the more open atomic structure and core asymmetry of screw dislocations.
At the microstructural level, ductile fracture proceeds through a sequence of void formation and evolution: voids nucleate at sites of stress concentration, such as inclusions or particle-matrix interfaces, through decohesion or particle cracking; these voids then grow by plastic straining of the surrounding matrix, elongating under triaxial stress; and finally, adjacent voids coalesce via internal necking or shear banding, forming a crack that propagates to produce characteristic dimpled fracture surfaces observed in scanning electron microscopy. This micromechanical process, first modeled analytically by McClintock in 1968, underscores the role of local plasticity in energy dissipation before final failure.
Grain boundaries play a dual role in ductility by acting as barriers to dislocation motion, which enhances strength according to the Hall-Petch relation: the yield strength y=0+kd1/2\sigma_y = \sigma_0 + k d^{-1/2}y=0+kd1/2, where ddd is the average grain diameter, 0\sigma_00 is a friction stress, and kkk is a constant reflecting boundary strengthening efficiency. However, grain refinement beyond a critical size can diminish ductility by reducing the mean free path for dislocation accumulation and multiplication, or by promoting crack initiation along boundaries under high stress concentrations. This relation, originally established by Hall in 1951 and Petch in 1953 through experiments on mild steel and iron, highlights the trade-off between strength and deformability in polycrystalline metals.
In non-metallic materials, ductility mechanisms differ due to the absence of dislocations in many cases. For polymers, plastic deformation and ductility stem from the viscoelastic sliding of long, entangled molecular chains past one another, where entanglements act as temporary cross-links that allow reptation-like motion and strain hardening without fracture, as evidenced in studies of glassy and rubbery polymers under tensile loading. In ceramics, intrinsic brittleness is mitigated in materials like partially stabilized zirconia through stress-induced martensitic phase transformation from tetragonal to monoclinic structure, which generates compressive stresses and volume expansion (approximately 4%) around crack tips to arrest propagation and enhance toughness, a phenomenon first identified in phase analysis by Garvie and Nicholson in 1972.

Environmental and Compositional Influences

The ductility of metals and alloys is profoundly influenced by their compositional elements, which can either enhance or impede plastic deformation through interactions with the crystal lattice. Interstitial alloying elements such as carbon and nitrogen in steels increase strength via solid solution strengthening but typically reduce ductility by pinning dislocations and promoting brittleness, particularly at higher concentrations where they distort the lattice and hinder cross-slip. In contrast, substitutional elements like nickel improve ductility in steels by stabilizing the austenitic phase and facilitating easier dislocation motion, thereby enhancing toughness without significantly compromising strength. These effects arise from solute-dislocation interactions that modulate the stacking fault energy and overall deformability.
Impurities and inclusions further modify ductility by acting as stress concentrators or weak points in the microstructure. In steels, sulfur impurities form low-melting-point iron sulfide (FeS) inclusions at grain boundaries, leading to hot shortnessa condition where the material becomes brittle and prone to cracking during hot working due to intergranular fracture. Similarly, non-metallic oxide inclusions, such as alumina or silicates, serve as nucleation sites for voids during tensile deformation, accelerating ductile fracture by initiating microcracks that propagate under load.
Heat treatments alter ductility by reshaping the microstructure through recovery and recrystallization processes. Annealing promotes recrystallization in deformed metals, replacing strained grains with equiaxed, low-dislocation-density structures that restore ductility and reduce hardness, making the material more formable for subsequent processing. Conversely, work hardening, or strain hardening, accumulates dislocations during cold deformation, increasing strength but decreasing ductility as the material becomes more resistant to further plastic flow and susceptible to localized necking.
Environmental exposures can degrade ductility through chemical interactions that embrittle the material over time. Hydrogen embrittlement in pipeline steels occurs when atomic hydrogen diffuses into the lattice from sour gas environments, reducing ductility by promoting brittle fracture modes and causing up to 90% loss in elongation, which poses risks in high-pressure natural gas transport. In marine applications, corrosion fatigue diminishes the effective ductility of structural alloys like stainless steels under cyclic loading in saline conditions, as pitting and crack initiation from chloride attack lower the fatigue endurance limit and accelerate failure.
Recent advancements in advanced materials, particularly advancements in high-entropy alloys (HEAs) in the 2020s, have achieved notable strength-ductility synergy through severe lattice distortion induced by multi-principal elements. In HEAs such as those based on CoCrNi or refractory systems, the cocktail-like composition creates local chemical fluctuations that elevate the Peierls stress for dislocation glide while maintaining high work-hardening rates, enabling tensile strengths exceeding 1 GPa alongside elongations over 50%. This lattice distortion mechanism, often tuned via thermomechanical processing, circumvents traditional trade-offs and supports applications in extreme environments.

Temperature-Dependent Behavior

Ductile-to-Brittle Transition

The ductile-to-brittle transition temperature (DBTT) is defined as the temperature below which a material's fracture mode shifts from ductile, characterized by dimpled rupture involving significant plastic deformation, to brittle cleavage fracture with minimal plasticity. This transition is particularly pronounced in body-centered cubic (BCC) metals, where the material's ability to deform plastically diminishes rapidly as temperature decreases.
DBTT is typically measured using impact tests such as the Charpy or Izod methods, which involve striking a notched specimen with a pendulum and recording the energy absorbed during fracture. By plotting absorbed energy against temperature, the DBTT is identified as the point where there is a sharp drop in energy absorption, often corresponding to a 50% reduction from the upper-shelf ductile value. These tests reveal a sigmoid-shaped curve, with high energy absorption above the DBTT indicating ductile behavior and low absorption below it signaling brittle failure.
The underlying mechanism involves reduced dislocation mobility at low temperatures, which limits plastic deformation and promotes unstable crack propagation via transgranular cleavage. In BCC metals like ferritic steels, this is exacerbated by the limited mobility of dislocations at low temperatures, despite a larger number of possible slip systems (48) compared to the 12 in face-centered cubic (FCC) structures, making it harder to accommodate strain without fracture. At temperatures below the DBTT, screw dislocations in BCC lattices exhibit particularly low mobility due to high lattice friction (Peierls stress), favoring cleavage over dislocation-mediated blunting of crack tips.
Several factors influence the DBTT. Grain size refinement lowers the DBTT by increasing grain boundary density, which enhances crack deflection and requires higher stresses for propagation, thereby promoting ductility at lower temperatures. Conversely, neutron radiation in nuclear reactors raises the DBTT through embrittlement, where defect clusters and solute segregation impede dislocation motion, shifting the transition to higher temperatures and reducing fracture toughness.
A historical example is the 1912 sinking of the RMS Titanic, where the ship's hull steel exhibited brittle fracture upon impact with an iceberg in the cold North Atlantic waters around -2C. Analysis of recovered steel samples showed a DBTT of approximately 0C to 32C, influenced by high manganese sulfide inclusions and coarse grain structure, leading to cleavage-dominated failure rather than ductile tearing.

High-Temperature Ductility

At elevated temperatures, typically above approximately half the absolute melting point (0.5 TmT_mTm), many metals and alloys exhibit significantly enhanced ductility compared to room-temperature behavior. This improvement arises primarily from thermal activation, which increases the mobility of dislocations within the crystal lattice, enabling extensive plastic deformation through mechanisms like climb and cross-slip. As a result, materials can undergo substantial straining without necking or fracture, contrasting with the reduced ductility often observed near the ductile-to-brittle transition temperature at lower regimes.
A key manifestation of this high-temperature ductility is superplasticity, observed in fine-grained alloys where uniform elongations exceeding 100%and often reaching 400% or moreare achieved under specific conditions of slow strain rates and temperatures around 0.5 TmT_mTm. In these materials, grain boundary sliding dominates, accommodated by diffusional processes, allowing the microstructure to deform homogeneously without cavitation. However, limits to this enhanced ductility emerge above the recrystallization temperature, where dynamic recovery and recrystallization soften the material, potentially leading to intergranular rupture if strain rates are too high or grain growth occurs.
The underlying deformation mechanisms at high temperatures include diffusional creep, such as Nabarro-Herring creep, where atomic diffusion through the lattice relieves stress, and dislocation creep, involving the glide and climb of dislocations under applied load. While these processes promote ductility, environmental factors like oxidation can degrade it by forming brittle oxide layers at grain boundaries, reducing effective elongation and promoting premature failure. In practical applications, this high-temperature ductility is leveraged in hot working processes like forging, where elevated temperatures (e.g., 9001200C for steels) enhance formability and reduce cracking risks. Similarly, nickel-based superalloys in gas turbine components maintain sufficient ductility up to 1000C, enabling reliable performance under combined thermal and mechanical loads.

Engineering Applications and Importance

Role in Material Selection and Design

In material selection for engineering applications, ductility serves as a critical criterion, particularly for components requiring formability and impact resistance. High ductility is prioritized in materials for sheet metal forming, such as automotive body panels, where it allows extensive plastic deformation without cracking during stamping or bending processes. For energy absorption in crash structures, ductile materials are selected to enable progressive deformation and dissipate kinetic energy, reducing the risk of sudden failure. To balance ductility with high strength, advanced alloys like dual-phase steels are commonly chosen; these consist of a ferrite-martensite microstructure that provides tensile strengths exceeding 590 MPa while maintaining elongations of 20-30%, making them ideal for lightweight automotive designs.
Design practices incorporate ductility into safety assessments to prevent catastrophic brittle failures, often through factors of safety that account for material deformability under overload. Engineering codes, such as those in the ASME Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code, specify minimum elongation values in material specifications to ensure adequate ductility; for instance, carbon steel forgings like SA-105 require at least 30% elongation in a 2-inch gage length to qualify for pressure-containing applications. This integration helps designers apply a margin that reflects the material's ability to yield plastically before fracture, enhancing overall system reliability.
While metals dominate selections due to their inherent ductility, polymers like high-density polyethylene (HDPE) are favored for piping systems where flexibility and toughness are essential for withstanding ground movements and impacts without leaking. In composites, fiber-reinforced variants with toughened polymer matrices, such as modified epoxy or polypropylene, are selected to improve post-yield behavior and prevent delamination in load-bearing structures. Ductility also dictates manufacturing feasibility: it enables bulk deformation processes like extrusion and rolling for metals, where materials can be shaped into complex profiles at room temperature or elevated temperatures, whereas low-ductility materials are restricted to casting to avoid defects.
In aerospace applications, titanium alloys such as Ti-6Al-4V are selected for their retained ductility at elevated temperatures, offering elongations above 10% up to 400C, which supports forging of airframe components while resisting fatigue. For infrastructure, ductile iron pipes replace brittle cast iron in water distribution networks because their nodular graphite microstructure provides 10-18% elongation compared to less than 1% in gray cast iron, improving resistance to bursting under pressure surges.

Ductility in Failure Analysis and Safety

In failure analysis, distinguishing between ductile and brittle fracture modes is essential for understanding the root causes of structural failures. Ductile overload typically manifests as necking followed by rupture, involving extensive plastic deformation that absorbs significant energy and provides visible warnings such as elongation or bulging before complete separation. In contrast, brittle failure occurs with minimal plastic deformation and low energy absorption, leading to sudden, catastrophic propagation without prior deformation indicators. Ductile failures are generally safer than brittle ones because the observable deformation allows for timely detection and intervention, reducing the risk of unanticipated collapse.
Fractography plays a critical role in analyzing these failure modes by examining fracture surfaces to identify the operative mechanisms. In ductile fractures, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) reveals characteristic dimples formed through the coalescence of microvoids during plastic flow, indicating void growth and shear lip formation at the edges. Brittle fractures, however, display flat, granular, or cleavage-like surfaces with little to no plastic zone, often showing river patterns or intergranular features under SEM. These techniques enable forensic engineers to reconstruct loading conditions and material behavior, guiding improvements in future designs.
Safety protocols in engineering incorporate minimum ductility specifications to avert brittle failures, with regulations mandating thresholds based on tensile elongation or impact toughness. For instance, the American Petroleum Institute (API) 5L standard for oil and gas pipelines requires minimum elongation percentagessuch as 22% for grades up to X52 in diameters over 141.3 mmto ensure sufficient ductility under operational stresses. In nuclear applications, periodic testing addresses degradation from neutron embrittlement, which shifts the ductile-to-brittle transition and reduces fracture toughness; surveillance programs involve extracting and testing Charpy specimens from reactor pressure vessels at intervals to monitor shifts exceeding 50100C, triggering maintenance if limits are approached.
Historical incidents underscore the consequences of inadequate ductility and the evolution of safety measures. During World War II, over 1,100 Liberty ships experienced brittle fractures, primarily in welded hulls at low temperatures around 0C, where the steel's high ductile-to-brittle transition temperature (above -20C) led to crack initiation at stress concentrations and rapid propagation across the structure. These failures, resulting in at least 10 total losses, highlighted the perils of using high-sulfur steels prone to embrittlement; subsequent investigations drove the adoption of low-transition-temperature steels with improved weldability and fracture toughness requirements in naval architecture.
Risk mitigation strategies focus on enhancing and monitoring ductility to prevent in-service degradation. Heat treatments such as normalizationheating steel to 850950C followed by air coolingrefine grain structure and reduce residual stresses, improving ductility for carbon steels. For ongoing assessment, acoustic emission (AE) monitoring detects ductility loss in real-time by capturing ultrasonic waves from microvoid formation, dislocation motion, or crack initiation during load-bearing, enabling predictive maintenance in structures like bridges or pressure vessels before macroscopic failure occurs.
