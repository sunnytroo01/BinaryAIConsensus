Vomeronasal organ

 The vomeronasal organ (VNO), also known as Jacobson's organ, is a paired chemosensory structure located bilaterally on the nasal septum in the nasal cavity of many vertebrates, specialized for detecting nonvolatile chemical cues such as pheromones that mediate social, reproductive, and defensive behaviors. It consists of a tubular or duct-like organ with a crescent-shaped lumen, lined by a vomeronasal sensory epithelium containing bipolar receptor neurons with microvillar apical surfaces, as well as supporting cells and a nonsensory epithelium that secretes mucus to facilitate stimulus access. These sensory neurons express specific G-protein-coupled receptors (V1R and V2R families) and transduction channels like TRPC2, enabling detection of pheromones at low concentrations (around 10 M) through a phospholipase C signaling pathway distinct from the main olfactory system's cAMP-based mechanism.
Anatomically, the VNO is enclosed in a cartilaginous or bony capsule and connected to the nasal cavity via a narrow duct, with vascular cavernous tissue that expands to "pump" fluids and enhance stimulus delivery to the sensory epithelium. Axons from the vomeronasal receptor neurons bundle into the vomeronasal nerve, projecting directly to the accessory olfactory bulb (AOB) in the brain, which relays signals to the amygdala, hypothalamus, and other limbic structures without thalamic intermediation, allowing rapid behavioral responses. In mammals such as rodents, cats, and many primates, the VNO is well-developed and functional from embryonic stages, playing a critical role in intraspecific communication by processing pheromones that trigger mating, aggression, territorial marking, and parental care. For instance, in mice, VNO activation influences reproductive status synchronization and sexual behavior via segregated neural pathways for apical (V2R-expressing) and basal (V1R-expressing) epithelial zones.
First described in detail by Danish anatomist Ludvig Levin Jacobson in 1813 across various mammals including rodents, horses, and dogs, the VNO's sensory function was confirmed in the mid-20th century through studies on pheromones and behavioral responses. It is absent in fish, birds, and some reptiles but present in most amphibians, reptiles, and mammals, with evolutionary persistence linked to its role in chemical communication. In humans, the VNO develops early in embryogenesis from the olfactory placode, appearing as bilateral pits or tubes on the nasal septum, but undergoes regression in adulthood, lacking functional sensory neurons, neural connections to the brain, and intact receptor genes (most of which are pseudogenes). While vestigial structures are observable in about 25-30% of adult human nasal septa, evidence indicates no active chemosensory role, with any residual presence potentially tied to developmental or endocrine functions rather than olfaction. This contrasts with functional VNOs in non-human primates like lemurs and New World monkeys, highlighting the evolutionary loss in catarrhine primates, including humans, amid reliance on the main olfactory system for social cues.

Anatomy and Structure

Gross anatomy

The vomeronasal organ (VNO), also known as Jacobson's organ, is a paired auxiliary chemosensory structure located bilaterally on the lower nasal septum in most vertebrates, positioned ventral to the main olfactory epithelium. It is typically enclosed within a bony or cartilaginous capsule in mammals, forming a blind-ended tube or pouch that opens into the nasal or oral cavity via the vomeronasal duct. This duct facilitates access of chemical stimuli to the organ's fluid-filled lumen, which is lined by specialized epithelium and supported by vascular and glandular tissues. In many mammals, the vomeronasal duct connects secondarily to the oral cavity through the incisive or nasopalatine duct, allowing stimuli from both nasal and oral sources.
Gross morphology varies significantly across vertebrate classes. In mammals such as rodents, the VNO is elongated and tubular, measuring approximately 2-5 mm in length with a crescent-shaped cross-section, as seen in mice where the lumen length averages about 2 mm. Reptiles, including snakes, exhibit a more pit-like or elongated form, often integrated into the palate with ducts opening directly into the oral cavity to aid in tongue-based stimulus delivery; for instance, in small snakes like the worm-like snake, the organ spans around 0.6 mm. Amphibians display a simpler integration into the nasal cavity floor without a distinct capsule, while in some mammals like carnivores and ungulates, the organ includes accessory cavernous tissues for vascular expansion.
In primates, the VNO is often vestigial or absent, appearing as a rudimentary pit or cyst on the anterior nasal septum without functional connections, contrasting with its robust presence in rodents and reptiles. The organ's sensory neurons project via the vomeronasal nervea bundle of thin, unmyelinated axons that travels along the nasal septum to the accessory olfactory bulbforming a key macroscopic connection in species where the system is developed. These variations in size, shape, and connectivity reflect evolutionary adaptations to diverse chemosensory needs across vertebrates.

Microscopic structure

The microscopic structure of the vomeronasal organ centers on its sensory epithelium, a pseudostratified neuroepithelium organized into a bistratified arrangement that includes apical supporting cells, intermediate microvillar sensory neurons, and basal stem cells. Supporting cells, also known as sustentacular cells, form the uppermost layer and provide structural support, metabolic maintenance, and barrier functions to the underlying neurons. Microvillar sensory neurons, the primary chemosensory elements, extend dendrites with microvilli into the organ's lumen to detect chemical cues, while basal stem cells serve as progenitors for neuronal turnover and regeneration.
This epithelium exhibits a distinct zonation, dividing the sensory neurons into apical and basal layers with specialized receptor expression. The apical zone contains neurons expressing vomeronasal type 1 receptors (V1Rs), which are tuned to lipophilic, volatile pheromones, and co-express the Gi2 protein. In contrast, the basal zone harbors neurons expressing vomeronasal type 2 receptors (V2Rs), adapted for water-soluble, peptide-based cues, and associated with the Go protein. This zonal organization ensures segregated processing of diverse chemical signals; in mice, there are over 150 functional V1R genes and approximately 120 functional V2R genes.
Associated with the epithelium are glandular structures, primarily tubuloacinar vomeronasal glands that line the organ's lumen and secrete a phlegm-like, seromucous fluid rich in neutral and acidic mucins. These glands empty via short ducts directly into the lumen, where the viscous secretion dissolves non-volatile stimuli, facilitating their access to sensory microvilli and aiding in post-detection purging.
Recent single-cell transcriptomic analyses of the mouse vomeronasal organ have uncovered novel cell types, including intermediate progenitors that express transcription factors like Ascl1, Neurod1, and Neurog1, bridging the transition from stem cells to mature neurons. These studies reveal diverse expression patterns, such as enriched endoplasmic reticulum-related genes in Go-positive basal neurons, highlighting subcellular specializations and potential new subclasses of progenitors that diversify neuronal maturation.

Neural pathways

The vomeronasal sensory neurons in the vomeronasal epithelium extend axons that form the vomeronasal nerve, which passes through the cribriform plate of the ethmoid bone to reach the accessory olfactory bulb (AOB), distinct from the projections of the main olfactory system to the main olfactory bulb. This afferent pathway ensures segregated processing of pheromonal cues. Within the AOB, these axons terminate in spherical glomeruli, which serve as the primary synaptic sites between first-order vomeronasal neurons and second-order projection neurons.
The AOB exhibits a zonal organization, with glomeruli divided into anterior (rostral) and posterior (caudal) regions corresponding to the apical and basal layers of the vomeronasal epithelium, respectively. Neurons expressing V1R receptors project to the anterior AOB, forming 1530 smaller glomeruli per receptor type in conserved areas, while V2R-expressing neurons target the posterior AOB, innervating 610 glomeruli per type with similar spatial consistency. Mitral cells, the principal output neurons, extend dendrites to multiple glomeruli of the same receptor type, converging divergent inputs into homotypic signals, whereas tufted cells connect to individual glomeruli; both reside in the mitral cell layer and relay processed information centrally.
Efferent projections from the AOB travel ipsilaterally via the accessory olfactory tract to key limbic structures, including the medial amygdala and posteromedial cortical amygdaloid nucleus, which integrate pheromonal signals for social processing. These pathways extend to the bed nucleus of the stria terminalis, providing reciprocal connections, and to hypothalamic nuclei, such as the medial preoptic area, for modulating innate behaviors like reproduction. Unlike the main olfactory system, the vomeronasal pathway lacks direct inputs to the neocortex, instead relying on subcortical routing through the amygdala and hypothalamus to enable rapid, reflexive responses without conscious perception.

Function

Pheromone detection

The vomeronasal organ (VNO) detects pheromones, which are chemical signals released by conspecifics that elicit specific behavioral or physiological responses, such as alarm, aggregation, sexual attraction, or kinship recognition. Alarm pheromones, for example, trigger defensive or stress responses in rodents, as seen with 2,5-dimethylpyrazine released by stressed male rats, which increases anxiety and hyperthermia in recipients. Aggregation pheromones, often carried by major urinary proteins (MUPs) in mice, promote social clustering and territorial marking. Sex pheromones facilitate reproductive behaviors, including exocrine gland-secreting peptide 1 (ESP1) that induces lordosis in female mice exposed to male cues. Kinship signals, such as major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class I peptides, enable recognition of genetic relatedness, influencing mate choice and pregnancy block (Bruce effect) in mice by detecting unfamiliar male odors.
Unlike the main olfactory system, which primarily processes volatile small-molecule odorants from the environment, the VNO specializes in non-volatile cues, including larger peptides and proteins that require direct contact or dissolution in fluid for detection. This specialization arises from the VNO's apical and basal epithelial zones, where vomeronasal receptors (V1Rs and V2Rs) bind these ligands, enabling discrimination of social signals over general scents. For instance, urinary pheromones in rodents, such as MUP20 (darcin), are non-volatile proteins that attract females to male territories by binding V2R receptors in the basal zone.
In intraspecific communication, the VNO mediates critical social interactions, particularly in rodents where it processes urinary and glandular pheromones to drive mate attraction and reproductive synchronization. Female mice, for example, accelerate puberty upon exposure to male urinary pheromones via VNO activation, enhancing fertility in social groups. This contrasts with the main olfactory system's role in learned odor discrimination, as the VNO's pathways project directly to the accessory olfactory bulb and hypothalamic nuclei, eliciting innate, stereotyped responses to social cues without prior experience.

Signal transduction

The signal transduction in the vomeronasal organ (VNO) converts pheromone binding into neuronal depolarization through a G-protein-coupled cascade in vomeronasal sensory neurons (VSNs). This process primarily involves two receptor families: vomeronasal type 1 receptors (V1Rs) and type 2 receptors (V2Rs), each expressed in distinct VSN subpopulations and coupled to specific G-proteins that activate downstream effectors.
V1Rs, located in the apical layer of the VNO neuroepithelium, are seven-transmembrane G-protein-coupled receptors tuned to volatile and small-molecule pheromones, coupling to the G-protein subunit Gi2 via its subunits to stimulate phospholipase C 2 (PLC2). This activation generates diacylglycerol (DAG) and inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate (IP3), with DAG gating the transient receptor potential canonical 2 (TRPC2) ion channel to permit influx of cations such as Na and Ca, leading to membrane depolarization and action potential firing. Meanwhile, V2Rs, expressed in basal-layer VSNs, detect peptide and proteinaceous ligandsoften transported by vomeronasal pheromone-binding proteins like major urinary proteins (MUPs)and couple to Go, similarly activating PLC2 to produce DAG and IP3 for TRPC2-mediated Ca influx and depolarization.
The TRPC2 channel serves as a critical accessory molecule in both pathways, functioning as a non-selective cation channel directly modulated by DAG and essential for the transduction response; genetic knockout of Trpc2 in mice eliminates pheromone-evoked Ca signals and electrical activity in VSNs, resulting in substantial neuronal degeneration (approximately 75% loss of V2R-expressing neurons and 50% loss of V1R-expressing neurons by adulthood) and profound deficits in pheromone-mediated behaviors. IP3 further amplifies the signal by mobilizing Ca from intracellular stores via IP3 receptors, enhancing excitability, though TRPC2 remains the primary conduit for sensory transduction. These mechanisms ensure rapid, specific conversion of chemical stimuli into neural signals, distinct from main olfactory transduction that relies on cyclic nucleotide-gated channels.
Recent advances in single-cell RNA-sequencing have elucidated subtype-specific expression profiles in the mouse VNO, revealing that V1R-expressing neurons exhibit largely monoallelic receptor choice regulated stochastically without dedicated transcription factors, while V2R-expressing neurons frequently co-express a common V2R alongside a specific one, driven by deterministic factors like Tfap2e and Atf5. These studies highlight molecular diversity in signaling components, such as variable TRPC2 and G-protein levels across neuronal clusters, underscoring the VNO's capacity for fine-tuned pheromone discrimination.

In Non-Human Animals

Presence across species

The vomeronasal organ (VNO) is a chemosensory structure present across a wide range of vertebrate taxa, particularly those reliant on terrestrial environments for detecting non-volatile chemical cues. It is ubiquitous in reptiles and amphibians, where it supports olfaction in diverse ecological contexts, and in most mammals, though its presence varies by lineage. In reptiles such as snakes and lizards, the VNO is well-developed and often paired with specialized delivery mechanisms; for instance, snakes employ their forked tongues to transport airborne or substrate-bound odorants directly to the VNO openings in the roof of the mouth, enabling stereoscopic chemosensation. The organ is absent in birds and fish, reflecting adaptations to aerial and aquatic lifestyles that diminish the need for vomeronasal-mediated detection. Similarly, it is lacking in aquatic mammals, including whales (cetaceans), where evolutionary shifts to fully marine habitats have eliminated the structure, as evidenced by the absence of VNO-associated anatomical features in dissected specimens.
Among mammals, the VNO remains functional and prominent in many terrestrial species, such as rodents, cats, and dogs, where it facilitates pheromone detection integral to social and reproductive interactions. It is also prominent in large mammals such as elephants and horses, aiding in social and reproductive signaling. However, it is vestigial or entirely absent in Old World non-human primates, including apes, correlating with enhanced reliance on the main olfactory system and visual cues in these lineages. Recent anatomical studies in pinnipeds highlight ongoing degeneration in semi-aquatic mammals; for example, in harbor seals (Phoca vitulina), the VNO has been modified into a secretory structure producing acidic mucus, with supporting evidence of genetic losses in vomeronasal signaling pathways. In contrast, the VNO persists robustly in wild cricetid rodents, as demonstrated by ontogenetic analyses showing consistent structural maturation from embryonic stages through adulthood in species like Calomys expulsus.
Phylogenetically, the distribution of the VNO aligns with transitions to terrestrial chemosensory demands, being retained or elaborated in lineages where detecting pheromones on solid substrates or in air is advantageous, while degenerating in aquatic or highly visual-adapted groups. This pattern underscores its role as an accessory olfactory system adapted to specific environmental pressures across vertebrates.

Role in behavior and reproduction

The vomeronasal organ (VNO) plays a pivotal role in mediating innate social and reproductive behaviors in animals through its detection of pheromones, which are processed via projections from the accessory olfactory bulb to the amygdala and hypothalamus, influencing the expression of aggression, mating, and parental care. These neural pathways enable rapid, stereotyped responses to conspecific chemical cues, distinct from learned olfactory processing.
In rodents, VNO signals modulate aggression by promoting territorial defense; for instance, detection of major urinary proteins (MUPs) via basal vomeronasal sensory neurons activates Go-coupled pathways that enhance male-male aggression through hypothalamic outputs. Similarly, VNO-mediated pheromone detection drives mating behaviors, such as lordosis in female mice triggered by exocrine gland-secreting peptide 1 (ESP1) binding to V2Rp5 receptors, facilitating reproductive readiness via amygdalar integration. For parental care, VNO cues from pups, including those recognized through non-canonical vomeronasal receptors, promote retrieval and nursing in virgin and lactating females by suppressing infanticidal tendencies and activating hypothalamic circuits; ablation or genetic disruption of these pathways reduces pup retrieval efficiency in virgin females from 92% to 58%.
VNO pheromones exert profound effects on reproductive physiology, including estrous cycle synchronization in grouped female mice exposed to male urine, known as the Whitten effect, which aligns ovulation timing to enhance fertility through vomeronasal-dependent chemosignals. In juvenile females, exposure to adult male pheromones accelerates puberty onsetthe Vandenbergh effectvia VNO detection, advancing vaginal opening by several days and dependent on intact vomeronasal function, as lesions abolish this acceleration.
Across species, the VNO influences context-specific behaviors; in cats, it detects pheromones involved in territorial marking, where urine spraying and cheek-rubbing deposit chemical signals for social and boundary communication, with inflammation of the vomeronasal epithelium linked to disrupted aggression patterns. In snakes, such as pit vipers, the VNO is essential for detecting predators, as suturing its ducts eliminates defensive and tracking responses to predator scents, underscoring its role in chemosensory survival.
Experimental evidence from VNO ablation in rodents consistently demonstrates disrupted social behaviors; surgical removal or genetic knockout (e.g., Trpc2/ mice) severely impairs male mating performance, reducing mount frequency and ejaculation rates, while also diminishing territorial aggression against intruders. Peripubertal ablation further abolishes pheromone-induced preferences for opposite-sex odors and heightens pro-social investigation, confirming the VNO's necessity for normative socio-sexual integration during development.

In Humans

Anatomical remnants

In adult humans, the vomeronasal organ persists primarily as a vestigial structure, often appearing as a small pit, duct, or cul-de-sac on the anteroinferior nasal septum, typically measuring 2-10 mm in length and 1-2 mm in width. These remnants are frequently blind-ended and lack a clear connection to sensory epithelium, serving no apparent olfactory role. Prevalence varies across studies, with estimates indicating presence in approximately 50-70% of adults, often unilaterally (predominantly on the left side), though rates can reach up to 92% in some populations without septal pathology.
During embryonic development, the vomeronasal organ emerges around the 8th gestational week (approximately 37-44 days post-fertilization) as bilateral epithelial thickenings within the nasal pits, homologous to the functional organ in other mammals. It progresses through stages of morphogenesis, featuring pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium, supporting cells, and serous glands by mid-gestation (around 16-18 weeks), but undergoes significant regression in late fetal life due to neuronal degeneration and epithelial atrophy. By birth, the organ has largely regressed, leaving behind subtle remnants such as the Jacobson's pit or canal in the nasal septum.
Histological examinations of these adult remnants reveal occasional non-sensory pseudostratified columnar epithelium with microvilli-bearing cells and scattered glands, but consistently show an absence of functional neurons, vomeronasal nerve fibers, or connections to the accessory olfactory bulb.
Imaging modalities like computed tomography (CT) and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) can visualize these septal pits or ducts as small depressions or tubular structures near the anterior nasal spine, providing non-invasive evidence of remnants; however, detection is inconsistent, with visibility influenced by individual anatomy and population differences.

Evidence of function and controversies

The debate on the functionality of the human vomeronasal organ (VNO) hinges on conflicting evidence regarding its sensory role in pheromone detection, with proponents citing developmental and physiological responses while opponents emphasize genetic and anatomical deficiencies. Putative vomeronasal nerves have been observed in human fetuses, including neuron-like cells in the VNO epithelium containing immunoreactivity for G-protein subunits such as Go and Gi2 as early as 5 months gestation, suggesting potential early sensory development. Electrophysiological studies further support limited function, demonstrating that exposure to the putative pheromone androstadienone elicits vomeronasal potentials and EEG changes in women, correlating with improved mood, reduced nervousness, and autonomic alterations like decreased cortisol levels.
Counterarguments highlight the VNO's likely vestigial status in adults due to molecular impairments. In humans, the majority of genes for vomeronasal receptors V1R and V2R are pseudogenes, with over 50 V1R pseudogenes identified and only a few potentially intact but non-expressed sequences, preventing effective pheromone binding. Similarly, the TRPC2 gene, crucial for signal transduction in the VNO, is a non-functional pseudogene, disrupting the ion channel necessary for neuronal depolarization. Anatomically, humans lack an accessory olfactory bulb to relay VNO signals to the brain, further isolating any remnant structure from central processing. These features indicate that any observed responses to odorants may occur via the main olfactory epithelium rather than a dedicated vomeronasal pathway.
A 2025 review in the Romanian Journal of Rhinology consolidates morphological, histological, and neurological data, concluding no conclusive neural pathway exists from the human VNO to hypothalamic or limbic centers, reinforcing its non-sensory role while noting persistent uncertainties in developmental regression. The controversy extends to volatile odor detection, where some studies attribute pheromone-like effects to mainstream olfaction, but without direct VNO involvement, as genetic evidence precludes specialized vomeronasal chemoreception.
Clinical implications remain speculative and unproven, with potential links proposed to disorders like hyposmia in Kallmann syndromewhere olfactory placode defects cause gonadotropin-releasing hormone migration failures and smell lossbut no evidence confirms VNO-specific contributions beyond general olfactory impairment. Surgical removal or damage during septoplasty shows no measurable effects on behavior or olfaction, underscoring the organ's negligible role.

Associated Behaviors

Flehmen response

The Flehmen response is a characteristic behavior observed in various mammals, involving a distinctive facial expression where the upper lip is curled upward, the mouth is opened, and the head is often tilted to expose the duct leading to the vomeronasal organ (VNO). This expression is commonly seen in ungulates such as horses and goats, felids like domestic cats and big cats, and to a lesser extent in rodents through analogous investigative postures.
The primary function of the Flehmen response is to actively deliver non-volatile pheromones and other chemical stimuli to the VNO by pumping air or fluid into the organ, enabling prolonged contact with substances that require solubilization for detection. Recent studies (as of 2024) have refined understanding of this vomeronasal pumping, highlighting the role of smooth muscle cells in facilitating fluid flow independent of vascular mechanisms. During the behavior, stimuli from the environment or oral cavity are transported via the nasopalatine ducts to the VNO lumen, where vascular and muscular mechanisms involving constriction and dilation of the cavernous walls facilitate the flow of fluid across the sensory epithelium.
This behavior is elicited by chemosensory detection of pheromones, often through initial nasal or oral investigation, and typically endures for several seconds to allow adequate stimulus processing.
Representative examples include stallions performing the Flehmen response after sniffing a mare's urine to evaluate her estrous status, which aids in reproductive decision-making. In felids, cats exhibit the response when investigating urine marks deposited by conspecifics, allowing assessment of social or territorial information embedded in the pheromones.

Other pheromonal behaviors

In territorial mammals such as wolves, the vomeronasal organ (VNO) facilitates scent marking and counter-marking behaviors, where individuals deposit pheromones to assert dominance or overwrite rivals' scents. For instance, wild wolves respond to conspecific rival marks by overmarking them, spending more time investigating and covering these scents compared to familiar or non-rival odors, which helps maintain territory boundaries and social hierarchy. This VNO-mediated detection allows wolves to distinguish and react specifically to competitor pheromones, as their functional VNO processes non-volatile chemical cues from urine and glandular secretions.
Prey species exhibit VNO-dependent avoidance responses to predator odors, enabling rapid evasion of threats through alarm pheromone detection. In mice, for example, the VNO is essential for recognizing and eliciting defensive behaviors, such as freezing or flight, in response to odors from predators like cats or snakes, with ablation of the VNO abolishing these innate reactions. These responses are triggered by sulfated steroids and other embedded pheromones in predator scents, processed exclusively via vomeronasal sensory neurons, highlighting the organ's role in survival-oriented chemosensory avoidance across mammalian prey.
Kin recognition in vertebrates relies on VNO input to discriminate familiar relatives, supporting nepotistic behaviors like parental care or inbreeding avoidance. In sheep, the VNO mediates maternal recognition of neonatal offspring odors during nursing, with ewes showing preferential bonding and rejection of non-kin lambs when the organ is intact; surgical removal disrupts this discrimination, leading to indiscriminate acceptance. This involves processing individual chemical cues, such as those from lamb wool after grooming, to facilitate recognition of familiar offspring. While insects achieve nestmate discrimination through antennal chemoreceptors detecting cuticular hydrocarbons, vertebrate kin recognition often involves the VNO for processing such cues.
In reptiles, the VNO enhances feeding behaviors by assessing food palatability and tracking prey cues, integrating chemical information for foraging efficiency. Snakes and lizards use tongue-flicking to deliver non-volatile prey odors to the VNO, which evaluates palatability factors like freshness or toxicity, guiding prey selection and strike decisions in species such as garter snakes. This vomerolfactory processing distinguishes edible from aversive stimuli, with VNO ablation impairing prey location and consumption rates.

Evolution and Development

Evolutionary origins

The vomeronasal organ (VNO) first emerged in early tetrapods approximately 350400 million years ago during the Devonian period, coinciding with the transition from aquatic to terrestrial environments. This evolution is linked to the need for detecting non-volatile pheromones and other chemical cues from substrates on land, which were less effective in water-dissolved forms. A precursor to the VNO, involving specific gene expressions in olfactory epithelia, likely predated the teleost-tetrapod split, but the fully developed organ became exclusive to tetrapods as they adapted to terrestrial life.
The diversification of vomeronasal receptors reflects adaptations across vertebrate lineages. In amphibians and reptiles, the V2R gene family expanded significantly, with amphibians like Xenopus tropicalis possessing over 330 V2R genes tuned to water-soluble peptides, while V1R repertoires remained small (e.g., ~21 in X. tropicalis). Reptiles, particularly squamates such as snakes, showed large V2R expansions (109216 genes) but few V1Rs (~4), supporting detection of environmental pheromones in diverse habitats. In mammals, the V1R family proliferated extensively (e.g., ~239 in mice), shifting emphasis toward volatile small molecules like steroids, while V2R numbers varied but often contracted compared to earlier tetrapods.
Independent losses of the VNO occurred in several lineages, driven by ecological shifts. In cetaceans, full aquatic adaptation led to VNO degeneration, evidenced by inactivating mutations in ancV1R and TRPC2 genes, relaxed purifying selection ( = 1.09 for ancV1R), and anatomical absence, likely as a trade-off with echolocation development in toothed whales. Birds lost the VNO early in their evolution, with no detectable V1R, V2R, or TRPC2 genes, attributed to olfactory reduction favoring visual and auditory senses essential for flight and navigation. In higher primates, including catarrhines, the VNO declined due to visual dominance, such as trichromatic vision, reducing reliance on pheromonal cues.
Genetic evidence confirms pseudogenization of VNO-related genes in humans and Old World monkeys around 23 million years ago, in the common ancestor of catarrhines. This involved disruptive mutations in TRPC2 (e.g., a stop codon) and V1R receptors, rendering the transduction pathway nonfunctional and leaving only pseudogenes (e.g., 5 V1R ORFs in humans). The process began shortly before the hominoid-Old World monkey divergence, contrasting with functional systems in New World monkeys and strepsirrhines.

Embryonic development

The vomeronasal organ (VNO) arises during early vertebrate embryogenesis from the placodal ectoderm of the developing nasal region. In mammals, induction begins with the formation of bilateral epithelial thickenings in the medial wall of the olfactory pit, derived from the olfactory placode. In humans, these primordia appear around embryonic day 37, with invagination occurring between days 37 and 43 to initiate VNO formation. In mice, a model for mammalian development, the VNO epithelium evaginates from the middle septal region around embryonic day 11, forming a kidney-shaped structure.
Morphogenesis proceeds through invagination of the placodal epithelium, leading to the development of a tubular duct and a medial sac-like chamber by the end of the first trimester in humans. By week 8 of gestation (approximately embryonic day 56), the VNO epithelium differentiates into sensory and non-sensory regions, with the sensory neuroepithelium lining the medial wall and exhibiting early neuronal markers. This process involves mesenchymal-epithelial interactions that shape the organ's crescent-shaped lumen and establish its connection to the nasal cavity via the vomeronasal duct. In mice, similar invagination and differentiation occur rapidly post-induction, with the duct patent by embryonic day 14.
Genetic regulation of VNO development relies on key signaling pathways that pattern the epithelium and establish zonation between apical and basal sensory neuron subtypes. Bone morphogenetic protein 4 (BMP4) signaling, mediated through Smad4, promotes maturation of basal vomeronasal sensory neurons (VSNs) and their axonal targeting to the accessory olfactory bulb, while also influencing epithelial zonation by restricting respiratory epithelium expansion. Sonic hedgehog (Shh) signaling, acting via Gli3 repressors in its absence, contributes to ventral patterning of the olfactory placode and VNO specification, with cross-talk between Shh and BMP4 domains ensuring proper neurogenic area formation. Recent transcriptomic studies (as of 2024) have revealed novel co-expression patterns of vomeronasal receptors in the mouse VNO, further elucidating zonation and maturation processes.
In humans, the VNO undergoes postnatal regression, characterized by apoptosis and degeneration of sensory neurons, leading to a vestigial structure lacking functional innervation by adulthood. This process begins in late fetal stages with reduced epithelial complexity after week 19 and continues postnatally, resulting in replacement of neuroepithelium by respiratory-like epithelium.

History of Research

Discovery and early studies

The vomeronasal organ was first systematically described in 1813 by Danish anatomist Ludvig Levin Jacobson, who identified it as a distinct structure within the nasal capsule of horses and several other mammals during dissections. Jacobson detailed its anatomical position on the nasal septum, noting its tubular form and connection to the nasal cavity, though he did not speculate on its function beyond its sensory potential. His monograph, published in Danish, emphasized the organ's presence across species but absence in humans and higher primates, marking the initial recognition of its comparative anatomy.
In the mid-19th century, advances in microscopy enabled more detailed histological examinations, with German anatomist Albert von Klliker providing the first comprehensive description of the organ's structure in mammals, including humans. In 1877, Klliker observed the organ's pseudostratified epithelium lined with sensory cells in fetal and adult specimens, confirming its role as a chemosensory apparatus distinct from the main olfactory epithelium. These findings built on Jacobson's work by revealing the organ's innervation and vascularization, suggesting involvement in detecting dissolved chemical stimuli rather than airborne odors.
Early 20th-century research intensified debates over the organ's presence and significance in humans, particularly through the studies of Swedish anatomist Ivan Broman. In 1920, Broman published detailed observations affirming the vomeronasal organ's existence in adult human nasal septa, describing its morphology and fluid-filled lumen as adapted for aqueous chemical detection, which he termed a "water olfactory organ." His work popularized the eponym "Jacobson's organ" in scientific literature, resolving earlier uncertainties and sparking comparative studies across vertebrates. Broman's assertions countered prevailing views of the organ as vestigial in primates, influencing subsequent anatomical surveys.
Functional hypotheses emerged in the mid-20th century through behavioral observations in reptiles, particularly snakes, where the organ was linked to chemical signaling. Studies in the 1950s demonstrated that snakes use tongue-flicking to transport substrates to the vomeronasal organ, enabling detection of conspecific cues that influence mating and predation behaviors. These findings proposed the organ's specialization for non-volatile chemical signals, a concept later formalized as pheromone detection following the term's introduction in 1959. Such early investigations laid the groundwork for understanding the vomeronasal system as a dedicated pathway for intraspecific communication in many species.

Modern research advances

In the 1970s and 1980s, electrophysiological studies in golden hamsters provided key evidence for the vomeronasal organ's (VNO) responsiveness to pheromones, demonstrating neural activation in response to conspecific urinary and vaginal secretions that correlated with mating behaviors. For instance, recordings from the accessory olfactory bulb revealed specific firing patterns elicited by female hamster vaginal fluid, distinct from main olfactory inputs, underscoring the VNO's role in sexual arousal. By the 1990s, advanced techniques like chronic electrode implants in the VNO capsule captured vascular pump activation and electrical changes during pheromone exposure, confirming fluid uptake and sensory transduction in awake, behaving animals.
The molecular era began with the cloning of vomeronasal receptors in the mid-1990s, identifying the V1R family as a novel class of G-protein-coupled receptors expressed exclusively in the VNO's apical sensory neurons. Subsequent genomic analyses of the mouse in the early 2000s expanded this to over 100 V1R genes clustered on chromosomes 6, 7, and 13, revealing evolutionary dynamics of gene duplication and diversification tailored to pheromone detection. Parallel efforts uncovered the V2R family in basal VNO neurons, structurally related to calcium-sensing and taste receptors, with initial identification in 1997 followed by comprehensive repertoire mapping of approximately 120-200 genes in mice and rats by 2005.
Genetic knockouts in the 2000s further validated the VNO's behavioral functions, particularly through disruption of the TRPC2 ion channel essential for VNO signal transduction. TRPC2-null mice exhibited profound deficits in pheromone-evoked responses, including impaired sexual mounting, aggression toward intruders, and maternal pup retrieval, without affecting main olfactory-mediated behaviors. These mutants demonstrated that VNO inputs are critical for innate sociosexual circuits, as knockout males showed indiscriminate mounting toward both sexes and reduced ultrasonic vocalizations during courtship.
Recent advances from 2020 to 2025 have leveraged single-cell transcriptomics to map VNO cellular diversity, identifying distinct neuronal subtypes with unique gene expression profiles for pheromone processing in mice. For example, a 2024 study revealed differential endoplasmic reticulum environments among VSN subtypes, potentially influencing receptor trafficking and signaling specificity. Concurrently, investigations into stem cell dynamics showed that CXCR4 expression in basal progenitors regulates VNO tissue homeostasis and neuronal turnover, preventing age-related degeneration. In humans, where VNO functionality remains debated, a 2025 review highlighted its vestigial yet enigmatic status, with morphological remnants but pseudogenized receptors suggesting residual or non-pheromonal roles. Additionally, single-cell atlases from the Stowers Institute uncovered potential novel sustentacular-like cells in the mouse VNO, expanding understanding of non-neuronal support for sensory maintenance.
